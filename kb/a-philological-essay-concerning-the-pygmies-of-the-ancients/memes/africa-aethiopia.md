# Africa (Aethiopia)

## Overview

Africa — known to the ancients as Aethiopia or Libya — was the primary geographic setting for classical pygmy accounts. Edward Tyson, in his 1699 _Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_, identified Africa as the crucial theater where ancient pygmy legends and natural history converged.

Tyson's most important evidence came from Africa: the chimpanzee, whose anatomy he knew firsthand from dissection, was an African ape. If ancient pygmy accounts described chimpanzees, Africa was where the identification had to work.

## Ancient African Pygmy Traditions

### Homer and Herodotus

[[homer|Homer]]'s pygmies were geographically unspecific, but [[herodotus|Herodotus]] placed them firmly in Africa. His account of the Nasamonian expedition describes travelers crossing the Sahara to encounter small-statured people — a report Tyson treated as the earliest credible ethnographic account of African pygmies.

### Aristotle's Egyptian Marshes

[[aristotle|Aristotle]] located the pygmy-crane conflict in the marshes of Upper Egypt. The cranes, fleeing the Pygmies' attacks, migrated to Egyptian wetlands — a detail that gave geographic precision to Homer's vague mythical geography. (011-010)

### The African Ape Connection

Tyson observed that Africa was home to "several races of" pygmy peoples in the ethnographic sense — short-statured human populations. (003-012) But critically, it was also the habitat of the chimpanzee, which Tyson had dissected and which he proposed as the true identity of the ancient Pygmy.

He cited Stanley's account: "In Darkest Africa" contained descriptions of creatures that might correspond both to human pygmies and to apes, illustrating the very ambiguity Tyson argued the ancients had faced. (003-032)

## Tyson's African Evidence

Tyson's analysis of Africa drew on multiple lines of evidence:

| Evidence Type | Source | Finding |
|--------------|--------|---------|
| Classical texts | Herodotus, Aristotle, Pliny | Pygmies consistently placed in African interior |
| Anatomical | Chimpanzee dissection | African ape matches ancient physical descriptions |
| Ethnographic | de Quatrefages, Stanley | Real pygmy human populations exist in Africa |
| Geographic | Contemporary maps | Ape range overlaps with ancient pygmy locations |

Tyson also noted that "pigmy blacks, Asiatic or African, differ in this part of the" anatomy from ordinary African populations — a nuanced observation distinguishing between different African peoples. (001-143) He observed that African pygmy groups "differ in one important respect from the ordinary African negro" (001-144), demonstrating his attention to intra-continental human variation.

## Africa vs. India

Tyson's essay treated Africa and [[india|India]] as parallel but distinct cases. Both regions had ancient pygmy traditions. Both had small-statured human populations. But Africa had the chimpanzee — the ape Tyson knew best — making it the stronger test case for his argument.

The African evidence was central to Tyson's conclusion. If "the Pygmy described in this work was, as a matter of fact, a chimpanzee" (001-043), then Africa was where the ancient accounts and modern anatomy converged most decisively.
