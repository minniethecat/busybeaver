# M. J. Walhouse

M. J. Walhouse was a nineteenth-century British ethnographer and colonial administrator in India whose observations of the dwarf tribes of the Nilgherry Hills (Nilgiris) provided [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] with ethnographic evidence of diminutive human populations in South Asia. His detailed accounts of the [[kurumbas|Kurumbas]] and Irulas — tribes of notably short stature dwelling in the mountain fastnesses of southern India — appear among the modern testimonies cited in Tyson's *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*.

## The Kurumbas and Irulas

In Section 2 of his essay, Tyson introduces Walhouse's testimony to describe the dwarf races inhabiting the Nilgherry Hills, alongside the [[todas|Todas]] and Badagas. Walhouse documented two distinct but related tribal groups of diminutive stature:

> They inhabit, says Walhouse, the most secluded, densely wooded fastnesses of the mountain slopes. (002-122–002-126)

This description of the Kurumbas and Irulas as forest-dwelling peoples of the Nilgherry slopes establishes their habitat as one of extreme seclusion — a common characteristic of pygmy and pygmoid populations noted by numerous nineteenth-century ethnographers.

## The Cromlech Worship

Walhouse further recorded a distinctive cultural practice that linked the Kurumbas to the prehistoric monuments of the region:

> "The Kurumbas of Nulli," says Walhouse, "one of the wildest Nilgherry declivities, come up annually to worship at one of the dolmens on the table-land above, in which they say one of their old gods resides." (002-128–002-140)

This account of annual worship at megalithic dolmens reveals a fascinating continuity between the diminutive forest tribes and the prehistoric builders of the stone monuments. Walhouse notes that although the Kurumbas were popularly associated with the aboriginal builders of these cromlechs, they had no legitimate claim to such distinction. Nevertheless, their ritual use of these ancient structures provides insight into the cultural integration of dwarf tribes within the broader ethnographic landscape of southern India.

## Significance in Tyson's Argument

Walhouse's evidence appears in Tyson's catalogue of modern dwarf races, contributing to the broader argument that diminutive human populations exist across multiple continents. His testimony concerning the Nilgherry tribes helps establish the Indian subcontinent as one of the principal regions where dwarf races have been documented, alongside accounts from [[africa|Africa]] and [[asia|East Asia]].
