# Fable and Fabulous Ethnography

## Overview

The ancient genre of fabulous ethnography—descriptions of wondrous peoples at the edges of the known world—forms the literary context within which the pygmy legend developed. Edward Tyson's essay engages critically with this tradition, seeking to separate plausible observation from literary invention.

## The Tradition

Classical authors from Herodotus onward included descriptions of marvelous races in their ethnographic writings. Pliny the Elder's _Natural History_ catalogued dozens of such peoples: Pygmies, Cynocephali (dog-headed men), Blemmyae (headless men with faces in their chests), and many others. These accounts circulated widely and were repeated by subsequent authors with varying degrees of skepticism.

## Tyson's Critical Stance

Tyson acknowledged that "most of the great and learned Men would seem to decry this Story as a Fiction and mere Fable." However, he insisted that "there is something of Truth" underlying even the most fabulous accounts. His innovation was to treat fables not as lies to be dismissed but as distorted reports requiring systematic analysis.

## Distinguishing Fable from Observation

Tyson developed implicit criteria for distinguishing fabulous embellishment from factual observation:
- Consistency across independent sources
- Concordance with known natural history
- Anatomical plausibility (based on his dissection experience)
- Absence of obvious literary tropes (such as the "marvels of the East" formula)
