Alfred Cort Haddon (1855–1940) was a British anthropologist and ethnologist who began his career as a zoologist before turning to the study of human cultures. A professor at Cambridge and a leading figure in the development of British anthropology, Haddon is thanked by Bertram C.A. Windle in the preface to the 1894 edition of Tyson's essay as one of the scholars who assisted in preparing the annotated text.

## Acknowledgment in the Preface

Windle's preface names Haddon among the distinguished scholars who contributed to the edition: "I have to thank Professors Sir William Turner, Haddon, Schlegel, Brinton" (P03-012). Haddon's inclusion in this list places him among the leading anthropologists and ethnologists of the late nineteenth century who found Tyson's seventeenth-century essay relevant to contemporary debates about human diversity.

## Field Research in Ireland

Haddon's most specific contribution concerns a location in Ireland. Windle writes that "my friend Professor Haddon, who has investigated this district, tells me that" (002-034) a certain area contained evidence relevant to the discussion of diminutive human types. The reference is elaborated later: "In Ireland, in Innisbofin, co. Galway, Professor Haddon" (007-047) provided observations about the physical anthropology of the region's inhabitants.

Haddon's Irish craniological studies gave him firsthand knowledge of human physical variation in the British Isles, making him a natural authority for Windle to consult on the question of whether any European populations might correspond to the "little men" of ancient accounts.

## Anthropological Significance

Haddon represents the transition in Pygmy studies from classical philology to modern anthropology. Where Tyson relied on textual analysis of Greek and Latin sources, Haddon — and Windle's use of him — exemplifies the late-Victorian turn toward field observation, craniometry, and ethnographic comparison as methods for evaluating the ancient testimony about diminutive peoples.
