Edwin Sidney Hartland (1848–1927) was a British folklorist and solicitor who made significant contributions to the systematic study of fairy tales and folklore in the late nineteenth century. His major work, *The Science of Fairy Tales* (1891), applied comparative methodology to the analysis of fairy-tale motifs and their diffusion across cultures.

Hartland is one of the few scholars personally acknowledged in the essay's prefatory material. The prefatory note records gratitude to "Sidney Hartland, for their kindness in reading" portions of the work (P03-017), indicating his direct involvement as a scholarly advisor to the project.

## Contributions to the Essay

Hartland's assistance to the essay is both editorial and substantive. In Section 6, Tyson notes that "Mr. Hartland points out to me" a specific folk-tale parallel recorded in the *Folk Lore Record* (006-072). This intervention suggests Hartland actively directed the author's attention to relevant comparative material from the periodical literature of the emerging folklore studies discipline.

In Section 7, Hartland's *Science of Fairy Tales* is cited as an authority on a specific folkloric motif (007-096), while Section 9 draws on the same work for a parallel instance (009-053). These citations embed the essay within the contemporary scholarly discourse on fairy traditions.

## Significance in the Essay's Methodology

Hartland represents the network of late-Victorian folklore scholars whose work provided Windle with both comparative data and methodological models. The essay's approach — collecting instances of diminutive supernatural beings from diverse national traditions and comparing them systematically — mirrors the comparative method Hartland advocated in *The Science of Fairy Tales*.

The direct acknowledgment in the preface, combined with the in-text citations, suggests Hartland's role as more than a passive source: he functioned as a scholarly interlocutor whose "attention" guided the essay's engagement with specific folkloric materials. In Section 9, Windle notes that his "attention was called by Mr. Hartland" to a particular point (009-064), further confirming this active advisory relationship.

## Key Work Cited

- **_The Science of Fairy Tales_ (1891)** — Hartland's systematic treatment of fairy-tale motifs using comparative folklore methodology, cited at multiple points in the essay as an authoritative reference on fairy traditions and their classification.
