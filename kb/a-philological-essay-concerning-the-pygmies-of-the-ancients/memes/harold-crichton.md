Mr. Harold Crichton Browne was a British explorer of West Africa whose firsthand investigation of reported dwarf tribes in the region adjoining the Soos (Soussou) district provided Tyson with a skeptical counterpoint to the more enthusiastic claims of dwarf-discoverers.

## Investigation of West African Dwarf Reports

In Section 3 of the essay, Tyson discusses reports of dwarf populations in West Africa. Crichton Browne appears as an investigator whose findings cast doubt on the existence of a distinct dwarf tribe in the region near the Soos:

> These dwarfs have been alluded to by Harris and Dönnenburg, but Mr. Harold Crichton Browne, who has explored neighbouring districts, is of opinion that there is no such tribe, and that the accounts of them have been based upon (003-117)

Tyson's quotation is unfortunately truncated, but the thrust is clear: Crichton Browne, having personally explored the districts adjacent to the areas where dwarf tribes were reported, concluded that the accounts were exaggerated or mistaken. The phrase "have been based upon" suggests that Crichton Browne believed the reports stemmed from misidentification — perhaps of individuals of short stature rather than a distinct tribal population — or from confusion with neighbouring groups.

## Role in Tyson's Argument

Crichton Browne's skepticism is significant for understanding Tyson's method. Throughout the essay, Tyson does not simply accumulate positive evidence for the existence of pygmy populations; he also engages with counter-arguments and skeptical assessments. Including Crichton Browne's negative findings alongside the positive reports of Harris and Dönnenburg allows Tyson to present a balanced evaluation of the evidence, acknowledging that not all claims of dwarf tribes withstand scrutiny.

This even-handedness, characteristic of the philological method, strengthens rather than weakens Tyson's overall argument. By conceding that some reports are unreliable, he enhances the credibility of those he does accept — a rhetorical strategy familiar from classical historiography and early modern scientific writing alike.
