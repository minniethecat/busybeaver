Fridtjof Nansen (1861–1930) was a Norwegian explorer, scientist, and diplomat who later won the Nobel Peace Prize for his humanitarian work. In the 1880s, he conducted pioneering ethnographic research among the Inuit (Eskimo) of Greenland, documenting their physical characteristics, material culture, and oral traditions.

Tyson cites Nansen six times in his essay, drawing on the explorer's first-hand observations of the Greenland Eskimo as comparative evidence in the pygmy debate.

## Nansen's Greenland Observations

Tyson first invokes Nansen in Section 4, noting the average height of the Eskimo as documented by the explorer. He records that "Nansen says of the Eskimo of the East Coast of Greenland" that "their mean height is five feet three inches" (004-039). This measurement places the Greenland Eskimo within the range of populations that 19th-century anthropologists associated with pygmy or diminutive types.

## The Ignerssuit

In Section 8, Tyson provides a more detailed account drawn from Nansen's work, introducing a specific group the explorer documented:

> "Dr. Nansen, speaking of the Ignerssuit (plural of Ignersuak, which means 'a great fire'), says that, according to the Eskimo, they live underground..."
> (008-001)

Nansen reported that the Greenland Eskimo believed these underground-dwelling Ignerssuit emerged at night to hunt and could only be killed by a blow to the forehead (008-001). The explorer also recorded that some Eskimo believed these beings lived "under the water" (008-021), reflecting the fluid boundary between land and sea in Arctic cosmology.

## The Inorutsit

In Section 9, Tyson returns to Nansen for another category of diminutive beings:

> "Thus Dr. Nansen tells us that amongst the Eskimo is found a belief in a kind of giant people who live underground, and are called Inorutsit."
> (009-114)

Nansen recorded that these underground giants "according to the Eskimo belief, live inside the rocks" (009-139) and that local traditions told "how the underground or inland people stole a woman from the coast people" (009-147). These narratives of subterranean races — sometimes small, sometimes giant — provided Tyson with ethnographic parallels to the ancient accounts of Pygmies and their legendary conflicts.

## Significance for Tyson's Argument

Nansen's first-hand observations served a specific purpose in Tyson's essay: they provided contemporary, empirically documented evidence that beliefs in underground or diminutive peoples were not merely literary fictions but living traditions among indigenous peoples. By citing a respected explorer-scientist like Nansen rather than only folklorists and classical authors, Tyson strengthened his case that pygmy-like traditions had a basis in ethnographic reality.

Nansen's Greenland accounts also allowed Tyson to draw parallels between Arctic and tropical pygmy traditions, suggesting that the phenomenon of "little people" beliefs was geographically universal rather than confined to any single region.
