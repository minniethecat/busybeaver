# John George Garson

John George Garson (1854–1932) was a British anthropologist and osteologist who contributed to the comparative study of human skeletal remains in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. His examination of ancient inhabitants of the Orkney Islands, with particular attention to stature, was cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] in Section 8 of his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients* as part of the osteological evidence for historically diminutive populations.

## Orkney Osteology and Stature

In Section 8, Tyson continues his survey of physical-anthropological evidence for small-statured races, turning to Garson's analysis of skeletal material from the Orkney archipelago north of Scotland:

> And Dr. Garson, treating of the osteology of the ancient inhabitants of the Orkneys, says that the female skeleton which he examined was about five feet two inches in height, i.e., about the mean height of the existing races of England. (008-113)

Garson's finding that an ancient Orkney female measured approximately five feet two inches — equivalent to the average stature of nineteenth-century English women — served Tyson's argument by demonstrating that even in relatively recent prehistoric contexts, human stature could fall within ranges that modern observers would consider unremarkable. This normalized the existence of populations of modest height, weakening the objection that pygmy-sized peoples were biologically implausible.

## Context in the Osteological Argument

Garson's Orkney data appears in Section 8 alongside evidence from multiple disciplines. Tyson had already presented the stature findings of [[rahon|Dr. Rahon]] on Neolithic European populations, and Garson's work on a specific insular British population extended this osteological baseline to the northern periphery of Europe. The Orkney evidence demonstrates that Tyson cast a wide geographic net, assembling stature data from France, the Mediterranean, and the North Atlantic to substantiate his claim that diminutive populations were a widespread feature of prehistoric Europe rather than a regional curiosity.
