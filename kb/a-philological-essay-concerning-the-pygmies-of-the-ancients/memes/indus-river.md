# Indus River

## Overview

The Indus River appears in Tyson's essay in two distinct contexts: as a classical geographic marker for pygmy populations in ancient sources, and as the home of modern ethnic groups whose physical characteristics Windle evaluates in relation to the pygmy question.

## In Ancient Geography

The Indus formed the eastern boundary of the Persian Empire and marked the limit of Alexander's conquests. To Greek and Roman geographers, the Indus valley was a remote region at the edge of the known world.

Classical sources placed pygmies near the Indus. Windle records the ancient testimony: "after the nation of the Prusians...is found the Indus" (004-058), noting that "these Pigmies may be" the same people described by other authors under different names.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

The Indus connection to pygmy traditions operates at two levels.

Classically, the Indus region was one of several locations in India to which ancient authors assigned pygmy populations. Windle situates this within Tyson's broader survey of pygmy geography, where India generally was the primary locus of ancient pygmy reports alongside Africa.

In modern ethnology, Windle examines the populations of the Indus delta. "The Kols inhabit the delta of the Indus and the neighbourhood of" (002-156) the river, alongside the Jats and Brahouis. These groups are evaluated for their stature and physical characteristics relative to the pygmy archetype.

## Tyson's Discussion

Windle's treatment of the Indus evidence reflects the broader structure of Tyson's geographic argument. Classical sources placing pygmies near the Indus are presented as part of the accumulated testimony of antiquity, which Tyson subjects to critical scrutiny.

The Indus evidence connects the classical pygmy tradition to the Indian subcontinent, complementing the African pygmy reports of Herodotus and Aristotle. Windle uses the Indus populations to illustrate the transition from ancient mythological geography to modern physical anthropology.

Windle also references the Indus in the context of ancient exploration. The phrase "nostro tempore, quo nulla Mundi pars est, quam Nautarum Industria non" (013-032) — "in our time, when there is no part of the world which the industry of sailors has not reached" — reflects the transformation in geographic knowledge between the classical era and Tyson's own time.

The Indus delta populations are not claimed to be pygmies in the strict sense, but rather represent points on a continuum of human stature variation. The region exemplifies the methodological challenge at the heart of Tyson's essay: distinguishing between ancient mythological geography and the observable distribution of human physical types.
