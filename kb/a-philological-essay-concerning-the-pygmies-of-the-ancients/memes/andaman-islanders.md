## Overview

The Andaman Islanders (Mincopies) are the Negrito inhabitants of the Andaman Islands in the Bay of Bengal, one of the most frequently cited modern populations in Tyson's comparative survey of diminutive races. Their significance for the pygmy question is threefold: they exemplify the insular distribution pattern of small-statured populations; their physical characteristics (short stature, dark skin, distinctive hair form) match the classical descriptions of pygmy races; and their isolated geographical position makes them a natural experiment in human variation uncomplicated by recent admixture with taller populations. Tyson draws on the Andamanese evidence particularly in Section 2, where he surveys the global distribution of diminutive races.

## Ancient Sources

The Andaman Islands were unknown to classical geography, lying far to the east of the *oikoumene* familiar to Greek and Roman writers. However, the Andamanese indirectly illuminate the ancient pygmy tradition by providing a living instance of the kind of population that classical authors could only describe at second hand. The "Mincopies of the Andaman Islands offer a" particularly instructive comparison because, like the pygmies of Homer and Aristotle, they are an insular Negrito population of markedly short stature whose physical characteristics would strike a Mediterranean observer as distinctive and exotic (001-132). The Andamanese thus serve as a hermeneutic bridge: by studying a living Negrito population, Tyson's contemporaries could better evaluate the plausibility of the ancient descriptions.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson places the Andamanese within his systematic survey of diminutive populations in Section 2. "South of the Andaman Islands are the Nicobars," whose inhabitants display similar physical characteristics, and the chain of Negrito populations extends from the Andamans through the Malay Peninsula to the Philippines and beyond (002-021). This distribution pattern is methodologically significant because it demonstrates that diminutive stature is not a single anomalous trait but a recurring adaptation across insular Southeast Asia.

Tyson reports the anthropometric data available to him: the average stature of Andamanese males falls within the range that De Quatrefages classified as "pigmy" — below five feet two inches. The physical description emphasizes not only stature but also skin colour ("little black Men") and hair form, traits that distinguish the Negrito populations from their taller neighbours in India and Southeast Asia.

The Andamanese evidence bears directly on the most contentious question in the pygmy debate: whether diminutive populations represent a primitive ancestral type (the "autochthonous race" hypothesis) or a secondary adaptation to insular environments (the "insular dwarfing" hypothesis). Tyson does not adjudicate between these alternatives — the data available to him were insufficient for such a determination — but his careful description of the Andamanese distribution provides the empirical foundation on which later anthropologists would build both theories.

## Modern Perspective

Modern genetics has confirmed the deep antiquity of the Andamanese populations. The Andaman Islanders are among the earliest modern human populations to have reached South Asia, with genetic isolation extending back tens of thousands of years. Their short stature is now understood as part of a broader adaptive pattern shared with other Negrito and "pygmy" populations in Africa and Southeast Asia — not an ancestral primitive type but a convergent adaptation to tropical forest and insular environments. The Andamanese have suffered catastrophic population decline since Tyson's day; several of the ten original tribal groups are now extinct, and the surviving populations number in the hundreds, making the ethnographic record that Tyson's contemporaries compiled an irreplaceable scientific resource.
