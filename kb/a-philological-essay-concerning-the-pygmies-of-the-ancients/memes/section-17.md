# Section 17

[Footnote A: _Job Ludolphus in Comment, in Historiam Æthiopicam_, p.m.

[017-001]71.]

 
[Footnote B: _[[philostratus|Philostratus]]. Icon_. lib. 2. p.m. 817.]

 
[Footnote C: _Plutarch. in vita Q. Sertorij_.]

[017-002]But to return to our _[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_; tho' most of the great and learned Men

[017-003]would seem to decry this Story as a Fiction and mere Fable, yet there is

[017-004]something of Truth, they think, must have given the first rise to it, and

[017-005]that it was not wholly the product of Phancy, but had some real

[017-006]foundation, tho' disguised, according to the different Imagination and

[017-007]_Genius_ of the _Relator_: 'Tis this that has incited them to give their

[017-008]several Conjectures about it. _Job Ludolphus_ finding what has been

[017-009]offered at in Relation to the _Pygmies_, not to satisfie, he thinks he can

[017-010]better account for this Story, by leaving out the _Cranes_, and placing in

[017-011]their stead, another sort of Bird he calls the _Condor_. I will give you

[017-012]his own words: _Sed ad Pygmæos_ (saith [A] _Ludolphus_) _revertamur;

[017-013]fabula de Geranomachia Pygmæorum seu pugna cum Gruibus etiam aliquid de

[017-014]vero trahere videtur, si pro Gruibus_ Condoras _intelligas, Aves in

[017-015]interiore_ Africa _maximas, ut fidem penè excedat; aiunt enim quod Ales

[017-016]ista vitulum Elephanti in Aerem extollere possit; ut infra docebimus. Cum

[017-017]his Pygmæos pugnare, ne pecora sua rapiant, incredibile non est. Error ex

[017-018]eo natus videtur, quod primus Relator, alio vocabulo destitutus, Grues pro

[017-019]Condoris nominârit, sicuti_ Plautus _Picos pro Gryphilus_, & Romani _Boves

[017-020]lucas pro Elephantis dixere_.

 
[Footnote A: _Job Ludolphus Comment, in Historiam suam Æthiopic_. p. 73.]

[017-021]'Tis true, if what _[[juvenal|Juvenal]]_ only in ridicule mentions, was to be admitted

[017-022]as a thing really done, that the _Cranes_ could fly away with a _Pygmie_,

[017-023]as our _Kites_ can with a Chicken, there might be some pretence for

[017-024]_Ludovicus's Condor_ or _Cunctor_: For he mentions afterwards[A] out of

[017-025]_P. Joh. dos Santos_ the _Portuguese_, that 'twas observed that one of

[017-026]these _Condors_ once flew away with an Ape, Chain, Clog and all, about ten

[017-027]or twelve pounds weight, which he carried to a neighbouring Wood, and

[017-028]there devoured him. And _Garcilasso de la Vega_[B] relates that they will

[017-029]seize and fly away with a Child ten or twelve years old. But _Juvenal_[C]

[017-030]only mentions this in ridicule and merriment, where he saith,

[017-031]Adsubitas Thracum volucres, nubemque sonoram

[017-032]Pygmæos parvis currit Bellator in armis:

[017-033]Mox impar hosti, raptusque per aera curvis

[017-034]Unguibus à fævâ fertur Grue.

 
[Footnote A: _Job Ludolphus ibid_. pag. 164.]

 
[Footnote B: _Garcilasso de la Vega Royal Comment_, of Peru.]

 
[Footnote C: _Juvenal Satyr_. 13 _vers_. 167.]

[017-035]Besides, were the _Condors_ to be taken for the _Cranes_, it would utterly

[017-036]spoil the _Pygmæomachia_; for where the Match is so very unequal, 'tis

[017-037]impossible for the Pygmies to make the least shew of a fight. _Ludolphus_

[017-038]puts as great hardships on them, to fight these _Condors_, as _Vossius_

[017-039]did, in making them fight _Elephants_, but not with equal Success; for

[017-040]_Vossius_'s _Pygmies_ made great Slaughters of the Elephants; but

[017-041]_Ludolphus_ his _Cranes_ sweep away the _Pygmies_, as easily as an _Owl_

[017-042]would a _Mouse_, and eat them up into the bargain; now I never heard the

[017-043]_Cranes_ were so cruel and barbarous to their Enemies, tho' there are some

[017-044]Nations in the World that are reported to do so.

[017-045]Moreover, these _Condor_'s I find are very rare to be met with; and when

[017-046]they are, they often appear single or but a few. Now _[[homer|Homer]]_'s, and the

[017-047]_Cranes_ of the Ancients, are always represented in Flocks. Thus

[017-048]_Oppian_[A] as I find him translated into Latin Verse:

[017-049]_Et velut Æthiopum veniunt, Nilique fluenta

[017-050]Turmalim Palamedis Aves, celsoeque per altum

[017-051]Aera labentes fugiunt Athlanta nivosum,

[017-052]Pygmæos imbelle Genus, parvumque saligant,

[017-053]Non perturbato procedunt ordine densæ

[017-054]Instructis volucres obscurant aëra Turmis._

[017-055]To imagine these _Grues_ a single Gigantick Bird, would much lessen the

[017-056]Beauty of _Homer's Simile_, and would not have served his turn; and there

[017-057]are none who have borrowed Homer's fancy, but have thought so. I will only

[017-058]farther instance in _Baptista Mantuan_:

[017-059]_Pygmæi breve vulgus, iners Plelecula, quando

[017-060]Convenere Grues longis in prælia rostris,

[017-061]Sublato clamore fremunt, dumque agmine magno

[017-062]Hostibus occurrit, tellus tremit Indica, clamant

[017-063]Littora, arenarum nimbis absconditur aër;

[017-064]Omnis & involvit Pulvis solemque, Polumque,

[017-065]Et Genus hoc Hominum naturâ imbelle, quietum,

[017-066]Mite, facit Mavors pugnax, immane Cruentum._

[017-067][Footnote: A _Oppian lib. I. de Piscibus_.]

[017-068]Having now considered and examined the various Opinions of these learned

[017-069]Men concerning this _Pygmaeomachia_; and represented the Reasons they give

[017-070]for maintaining their Conjectures; I shall beg leave to subjoyn my own:

[017-071]and if what at present I offer, may seem more probable, or account for

[017-072]this Story with more likelyhood, than what hath hitherto been advanced, I

[017-073]shall not think my time altogether misspent: But if this will not do, I

[017-074]shall never trouble my head more about them, nor think my self any ways

[017-075]concerned to write on this Argument again. And I had not done it now, but

[017-076]upon the occasion of Dissecting this _[[apes|Orang-Outang]]_, or _wild Man_, which

[017-077]being a Native of _Africa_, and brought from _Angola_, tho' first taken

[017-078]higher up in the Country, as I was informed by the Relation given me; and

[017-079]observing so great a Resemblance, both in the outward shape, and, what

[017-080]surprized me more, in the Structure likewise of the inward Parts, to a

[017-081]_Man_; this Thought was easily suggested to me, That very probably this

[017-082]_Animal_, or some other such of the same _Species_, might give the first

[017-083]rise and occasion to the Stories of the _Pygmies_. What has been the

[017-084][Greek: proton pheudos], and rendered this Story so difficult to be

[017-085]believed, I find hath been the Opinion that has generally obtained, that

[017-086]these _Pygmies_ were really a Race of _little Men_. And tho' they are only

[017-087]_Brutes_, yet being at first call'd _wild Men_, no doubt from the

[017-088]Resemblance they bear to _Men_; there have not been wanting those

[017-089]especially amongst the Ancients, who have invented a hundred ridiculous

[017-090]Stories concerning them; and have attributed those things to them, were

[017-091]they to be believed in what they say, that necessarily conclude them real

[017-092]_Men_.

[017-093]To sum up therefore what I have already discoursed, I think I have proved,

[017-094]that the _Pygmies_ were not an _Humane Species_ or _Men_. And tho'

[017-095]_Homer_, who first mentioned them, calls them [Greek: andres pygmaioi],

[017-096]yet we need not understand by this Expression any thing more than _Apes_:

[017-097]And tho' his _Geranomachia_ hath been look'd upon by most only as a

[017-098]Poetical Fiction; yet by assigning what might be the true Cause of this

[017-099]Quarrel between the _Cranes_ and _Pygmies_, and by divesting it of the

[017-100]many fabulous Relations that the _Indian Historians_, and others, have

[017-101]loaded it with, I have endeavoured to render it a true, at least a

[017-102]probable Story. I have instanced in _Ctesias_ and the _Indian Historians_,

[017-103]as the Authors and Inventors of the many [[fable|Fables]] we have had concerning

[017-104]them: Particularly, I have Examined those Relations, where Speech or

[017-105]Language is attributed to them; and shewn, that there is no reason to

[017-106]believe that they ever spake any Language at all. But these _Indian

[017-107]Historians_ having related so many extravagant Romances of the _Pygmies_,

[017-108]as to render their whole History suspected, nay to be utterly denied, that

[017-109]there were ever any such Creatures as _Pygmies_ in _Nature_, both by

[017-110]_[[strabo|Strabo]]_ of old, and most of our learned men of late, I have endeavoured

[017-111]to assert the Truth of their _being_, from a _Text_ in _[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_; which

[017-112]being so positive in affirming their Existence, creates a difficulty, that

[017-113]can no ways be got over by such as are of the contrary Opinion. This

[017-114]_Text_ I have vindicated from the false Interpretations and Glosses of

[017-115]several Great Men, who had their Minds so prepossessed and prejudiced with

[017-116]the Notion of _Men Pygmies_, that they often would quote it, and misapply

[017-117]it, tho' it contain'd nothing that any ways favoured their Opinion; but

[017-118]the contrary rather, that they were _Brutes_, and not _Men_.

[017-119]And that the _Pygmies_ were really _Brutes_, I think I have plainly proved

[017-120]out of _[[herodotus|Herodotus]]_ and _Philostratus_, who reckon them amongst the _wild

[017-121]Beasts_ that breed in those Countries: For tho' by _Herodotus_ they are

[017-122]call'd [Greek: andres agrioi], and _Philostratus_ calls them [Greek:

[017-123]anthropous melanas], yet both make them [Greek: theria] or _wild Beasts_.

[017-124]And I might here add what _Pausanias_[A] relates from _Euphemus Car_, who

[017-125]by contrary Winds was driven upon some Islands, where he tells us, [Greek:

[017-126]en de tautais oikein andras agrious], but when he comes to describe them,

[017-127]tells us that they had no Speech; that they had Tails on their Rumps; and

[017-128]were very lascivious toward the Women in the Ship. But of these more, when

[017-129]we come to discourse of _Satyrs_.

 
[Footnote A: _Pausanias in Atticis_, p.m. 21.]

[017-130]And we may the less wonder to find that they call _Brutes Men_, since

[017-131]'twas common for these _Historians_ to give the Title of _Men_, not only

[017-132]to _Brutes_, but they were grown so wanton in their Inventions, as to

[017-133]describe several Nations of _Monstrous Men_, that had never any Being, but

[017-134]in their own Imagination, as I have instanced in several. I therefore

[017-135]excuse _Strabo_, for denying the _Pygmies_, since he could not but be

[017-136]convinced, they could not be such _Men_, as these _Historians_ have

[017-137]described them. And the better to judge of the Reasons that some of the

[017-138]Moderns have given to prove the Being of _Men Pygmies_, I have laid down

[017-139]as _Postulata's_, that hereby we must not understand _[[dwarfs|Dwarfs]]_, nor yet a

[017-140]Nation of _Men_, tho' somewhat of a lesser size and stature than ordinary;

[017-141]but we must observe those two Characteristicks that _Homer_ gives of them,

[017-142]that they are _Cubitales_ and fight _Cranes_.

[017-143]Having premised this, I have taken into consideration _Caspar Bartholine

[017-144]Senior_ his _Opusculum_ _de Pygmæis_, and _Jo. Talentonius_'s Dissertation

[017-145]about them: and upon examination do find, that neither the Humane

[017-146]Authorities, nor Divine that they alledge, do any ways prove, as they

[017-147]pretend, the Being of _Men Pygmies_. St. _Austin_, who is likewise quoted

[017-148]on their side, is so far from favouring this Opinion, that he doubts

[017-149]whether any such Creatures exist, and if they do, concludes them to be

[017-150]_Apes_ or _Monkeys_; and censures those _Indian Historians_ for imposing

[017-151]such Beasts upon us, as distinct Races of _Men_. _Julius Cæsar Scaliger_,

[017-152]and _Isaac Casaubon_, and _Adrian Spigelius_ utterly deny the Being of

[017-153]_Pygmies_, and look upon them as a Figment only of the Ancients, because

[017-154]such little Men as they describe them to be, are no where to be met with

[017-155]in all the World. The Learned _Bochartus_ tho' he esteems the

[017-156]_Geranomachia_ to be a Fable, and slights it, yet thinks that what might

[017-157]give the occasion to the Story of the _Pygmies_, might be the _Nubæ_ or

[017-158]_Nobæ_; as _Isaac Vossius_ conjectures that it was those _Dwarfs_ beyond

[017-159]the Fountains of the _Nile_, that _Dapper_ calls the _Mimos_, and tells

[017-160]us, they kill _Elephants_ for to make a Traffick with their Teeth. But

[017-161]_Job Ludolphus_ alters the Scene, and instead of _Cranes_, substitutes his

[017-162]_Condors_, who do not fight the _Pygmies_, but fly away with them, and

[017-163]then devour them.

[017-164]Now all these Conjectures do no ways account for _Homer's Pygmies_ and

[017-165]_Cranes_, they are too much forced and strain'd. Truth is always easie and

[017-166]plain. In our present Case therefore I think the _Orang-Outang_, or _wild

[017-167]Man_, may exactly supply the place of the _Pygmies_, and without any

[017-168]violence or injury to the Story, sufficiently account for the whole

[017-169]History of the _Pygmies_, but what is most apparently fabulous; for what

[017-170]has been the greatest difficulty to be solved or satisfied, was their

[017-171]being _Men_; for as _Gesner_ remarks (as I have already quoted him) _Sed

[017-172]veterum nullus aliter de Pygmæis scripsit, quàm Homunciones esse_. And the

[017-173]Moderns too, being byassed and misguided by this Notion, have either

[017-174]wholly denied them, or contented themselves in offering their Conjectures

[017-175]what might give the first rise to the inventing this Fable. And tho'

[017-176]_Albertus_, as I find him frequently quoted, thought that the _Pygmies_

[017-177]might be only a sort of _Apes_, and he is placed in the Head of those that

[017-178]espoused this Opinion, yet he spoils all, by his way of reasoning, and by

[017-179]making them speak; which was more than he needed to do.
