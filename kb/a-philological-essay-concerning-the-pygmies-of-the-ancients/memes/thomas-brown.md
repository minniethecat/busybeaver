# Thomas Browne

## Biography

Sir Thomas Browne (1605–1682) was an English Physician, Author, and polymath. He is best known for his _Pseudodoxia Epidemica_ (also _Enquiries into Vulgar and Common Errors_), first published in 1646.

This wide-ranging work subjected popular beliefs and classical authorities to skeptical scrutiny. This made Browne a natural interlocutor for Edward Tyson's own philological investigation of the pygmy question.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

In _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_, Tyson engages with Browne's analysis at several key junctures. Most significantly, Browne had offered a skeptical reading of Aristotle\'s testimony on pygmies.

Tyson quotes Browne's assessment of Aristotle's rhetorical strategy:

> "Where indeed (saith he) Aristotle plays the Aristotle; that is, the wary and evading asserter; for tho' with non est fabula he seems at first to confirm it, yet at last he claps in, sicut aiunt, and shakes the belief he placed before upon it."
> (013-125)

Browne further noted that [[scaliger]] had not translated Aristotle's initial affirmation, "perhaps supposing it surreptitious" (013-129). Tyson disagrees, arguing that had Scaliger known the passage to be spurious, he would have remarked on it explicitly (013-131).

Tyson also draws on Browne's discussion of the Scriptural testimony regarding pygmies. Browne identified the passage in Ezekiel as a "second Testimony" deduced from Holy Scripture (016-002). Tyson refers readers to [[ludolphus]]'s commentary for further satisfaction (015-168).

In the essay's opening survey of dwarf populations, Tyson references a later "Browne" who explored districts adjoining the Soos river in search of diminutive peoples (003-118).

## Key Works Cited by Tyson

- **_Pseudodoxia Epidemica_ (Enquiries into Vulgar and Common Errors)** — Book 4, on the pygmy question and Aristotle\'s testimony; also cited for Scriptural exegesis of the Ezekiel passage.
