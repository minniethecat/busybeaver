Robert Henry Codrington (1830–1922) was an Anglican missionary and pioneering ethnographer whose decades of work in the Melanesian islands produced some of the most important anthropological documentation of Pacific cultures in the nineteenth century. His observations of Melanesian beliefs about diminutive supernatural beings provided Tyson with comparative evidence from a region largely unknown to classical and European traditions.

## The Vuis of Melanesia

Tyson draws upon Codrington's account of Melanesian "Vuis" — supernatural beings occupying an ambiguous position between spirits and fairies — in his discussion of cross-cultural traditions of "little people" associated with natural features:

> Speaking of these beings, Mr. Codrington says, "There are certain Vuis having rather the nature of fairies. The accounts of them are vague, but it is argued that they had never left the islands before the introduction of" (007-066)

The term "Vuis" (more commonly rendered "Vui" in Codrington's published works) referred to a class of beings in Melanesian belief that inhabited specific locations — stones, pools, and forest clearings — and possessed powers that could affect human affairs. Their ambiguous status — neither fully divine nor fully mortal — placed them in a category comparable to European fairies, a parallel Tyson was eager to exploit.

The Melanesian evidence enters Tyson's argument at a crucial point: he is assembling cross-cultural data to support the proposition that beliefs in diminutive supernatural beings associated with natural features are universal, and that such universality may point to a common origin — possibly in memories of pre-existing populations of small-statured peoples. Codrington's Melanesian material, collected through direct observation and linguistic competence rather than through interpreters or secondary sources, carried exceptional authority within the late-Victorian anthropological community.

## Ethnographic Legacy

Codrington's works — particularly *The Melanesians: Studies in their Anthropology and Folk-lore* (1891) and *The Melanesian Languages* (1885) — remain foundational texts in Pacific anthropology. His meticulous documentation of Melanesian social organisation, religious belief, and the concept of *mana* (which he introduced to Western scholarship) established standards of ethnographic rigour that influenced a generation of fieldworkers. For Tyson, Codrington's authority as a first-hand observer of Melanesian life made his testimony about fairy-like beings especially valuable.
