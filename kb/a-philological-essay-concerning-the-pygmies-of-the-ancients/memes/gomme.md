Sir George Laurence Gomme (1853–1916) was a British folklorist and public servant who served as Clerk of the London County Council. He simultaneously made significant contributions to the study of folklore and ethnology.

A founding figure in the Folk-Lore Society, Gomme developed an influential theoretical framework. He linked folk traditions to the survival of pre-Aryan cultural practices among the populations of the British Isles.

## The Non-Aryan Substratum Theory

Gomme's central theoretical contribution is the proposition that folk customs and beliefs represent cultural survivals from conquered non-Aryan peoples. These peoples, he argued, were subjugated by Aryan invaders in prehistory.

In Section 2, Tyson notes that "Mr. Gomme has drawn attention to this and other similar customs" associated with "the mysterious stone" (002-140). Gomme interprets such customs in "the interesting remarks which he makes upon the influence of conquered non-Aryan races upon their Aryan subduers" (002-141).

The theory posits that folk traditions encode the memory of racial displacement and cultural stratification in prehistoric Europe. It provides a mechanism by which historical realities enter folk memory.

The theory is revisited in Section 3, where Tyson cites "evidence in favour of Mr. Gomme's theory, to which attention has already been called" (003-136). This embeds Gomme's framework within the essay's discussion of archaeological evidence for prehistoric populations (003-136). The footnote references the *Anthropological Memoirs* (003-137).

## Relationship to the Pygmy Question

Gomme's theory bears directly on the essay's central question in two ways. First, it models how the physical traits of conquered peoples — including small stature — might be preserved in folk memory. Second, his methodology of identifying cultural survivals parallels the essay's own comparative procedure.

Gomme does not address the pygmy question directly. But his theoretical apparatus supports Tyson's broader argument that folk traditions contain recoverable historical content. His work aligns with Nutt's "distorted recollection" thesis while providing the specific mechanism of conquest and cultural submergence.

## Scholarly Context

Gomme's work belongs to late-Victorian folklore studies, where ethnological and racial theories combined with the classification of folk customs. His theory of non-Aryan survivals reflects the period's tendency to read folk traditions as encoded history.

This tendency was shared, with different emphases, by MacRitchie, Nutt, and Hartland. Gomme's particular contribution is the emphasis on conquest and cultural stratification as the mechanism by which historical realities enter folk memory.
