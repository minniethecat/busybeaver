## Overview

Wales contributes two specific sites to Tyson's survey of diminutive-race folklore: the Bryn-yr-Ellyllon (Fairy Hill) near Mold, and the Crwben-yr-Ellyl (Elf's Hollow Tree) in the park of Sir Robert Vaughan. (005-120, 007-121) These Welsh traditions provide Celtic evidence that fits within MacRitchie's broader thesis that European fairy lore preserves the memory of a prehistoric diminutive population.

Together with evidence from Scotland, Ireland, and Brittany, the Welsh material reinforces the Celtic-speaking regions as a particularly rich zone of pygmy-related folklore in Tyson's geographic survey.

## Bryn-yr-Ellyllon: The Fairy Hill

Tyson cites the Bryn-yr-Ellyllon, or Fairy Hill, near Mold in North Wales, as one of several British sites where fairies were believed to dwell in mounds. (005-120) He lists it alongside similar sites in England — a green mound in Pulborough, Bussers Hill in the Scilly Isles — as evidence for the widespread British tradition of mound-dwelling fairies.

The Welsh name itself is revealing: _bryn_ (hill) + _yr_ (the) + _ellyllon_ (elves/fairies) — "the Hill of the Elves." The preservation of a specific, named topographic feature associated with fairy habitation suggests a tradition anchored in local geography rather than abstract mythology.

The Bryn-yr-Ellyllon belongs to the broader pattern of fairy-mound traditions that Tyson, following MacRitchie, interprets as folk memories of the burial mounds and underground dwellings of a prehistoric diminutive race.

## Crwben-yr-Ellyl: The Elf's Hollow Tree

The second Welsh site is a celebrated oak: "in Wales, where there was formerly, in the park of Sir Robert Vaughan, a celebrated old oak-tree, named Crwben-yr-Ellyl, or the Elf's Hollow Tree." (007-121)

This tree, like the lime-tree haunts of Germanic Elves (007-118), represents the association of fairy beings with specific, named trees — a motif found across Northern European folklore. The hollow of the tree is imagined as the dwelling-place of the small beings, consistent with the troglodyte characterization of diminutive races that runs from Herodotus through to modern pygmy accounts.

The oak's location in the park of Sir Robert Vaughan — a named seventeenth-century Welsh landowner — gives the tradition a specific historical anchor, distinguishing it from vague folkloric generalities.

## Welsh Context in MacRitchie's Theory

MacRitchie's pygmy-fairy theory, which Tyson extensively cites, drew on evidence from across the Celtic world. Wales contributed to this theory through its megalithic monuments (cromlechs and chambered tombs), its fairy-mound traditions, and its linguistic evidence — the Welsh _ellyll_ (elf) cognate with other Celtic fairy terms.

Tyson's broader argument places the Welsh evidence alongside that from Scotland (Pictish "Pechs" as fairies), Ireland (Brugh of the Boyne, Innisbofin), and Brittany (Carnac, Locmariaquer) to establish a continuous Celtic zone of diminutive-race traditions that cannot be explained as isolated local inventions.
