# Julius Kollmann

**Julius Kollmann** (1834–1918) was a German anatomist and anthropologist who served as Professor at the University of Basle. In Tyson's [[A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients]], Kollmann is cited for his discovery and analysis of a Neolithic pygmy population at Schaffhausen, Switzerland—a finding that provided important anatomical evidence for the prehistoric existence of small-statured peoples in Europe (004-001–004-008).

## The Schaffhausen Discovery

Kollmann's most significant contribution to the pygmy question was his description of Neolithic interments at Schaffhausen, where the remains of two distinct racial types were found buried side by side. Tyson records this finding in a footnote to [[section-4]], noting that the discovery was made while the essay was already in press:

> "[004-001]described a group of Neolithic pigmies as having existed at Schaffhausen. The adult interments consisted of the remains of full-grown European types and of small-sized people. These two races were found interred side by side under precisely similar conditions, from which he concludes that they lived peaceably together, notwithstanding racial difference." (004-001–004-005)

## Stature and Comparison

The stature of the Schaffhausen pygmies was estimated at approximately three feet six inches—a figure Kollmann compared to the [[veddahs]] of Ceylon (004-006–004-007). This comparison to a living small-statured population was methodologically significant, as it situated the Neolithic remains within a broader comparative framework:

> "[004-006]Their stature (about three feet six inches) may be compared with that of the Veddahs in Ceylon. Prof. Kollmann believes that they were a distinct species of mankind." (004-006–004-008)

Kollmann's conclusion that the Schaffhausen pygmies constituted a **distinct species** of mankind was a bold taxonomic claim that placed him within the polygenist tradition of 19th-century anthropology—the view that human races represented separately created species rather than varieties of a single species.

## Significance for Tyson's Essay

Kollmann's work serves several functions in Tyson's argument:

1. **Anatomical evidence**: The Schaffhausen remains provided direct physical evidence—as opposed to textual or folkloric testimony—for the existence of diminutive peoples in prehistoric Europe.

2. **Peaceful coexistence**: The fact that full-sized and small-sized individuals were interred together under identical conditions suggested cooperative rather than hostile relations, a point relevant to classical traditions about [[pygmies]] engaging in warfare with [[cranes]].

3. **Comparative anthropology**: By linking the Schaffhausen remains to living populations like the Veddahs, Kollmann's work connected European prehistory with contemporary ethnography, reinforcing Tyson's global perspective on human diminutive variation.

Tyson places Kollmann's findings alongside those of [[dr-rahon]], [[professor-boyd-dawkins]], and other contemporary anthropologists to build a cumulative anatomical case for the widespread prehistoric presence of small-statured human populations.
