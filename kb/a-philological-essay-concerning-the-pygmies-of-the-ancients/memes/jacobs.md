Joseph Jacobs (1854–1916) was a British folklorist, historian, and literary scholar, best remembered today as the editor of the influential fairy tale collections *English Fairy Tales* (1890) and *Celtic Fairy Tales* (1892). In Tyson's essay, Jacobs appears as the most articulate summarizer of the MacRitchie theory — so clear, in Tyson's estimation, that he defers to Jacobs' formulation directly.

## Summarizer of the MacRitchie Theory

In Section 8, Tyson is about to expound MacRitchie's controversial theory that European fairy traditions preserve memories of an aboriginal dwarf race. Rather than paraphrase the theory himself, Tyson praises Jacobs' rendering:

> Such in essence is Mr. MacRitchie's theory, which has been so admirably summarised by Mr. Jacobs in the first of that series of fairy-tale books which has added a new joy to life, that I shall do myself the pleasure of quoting his statement in this place. (008-054)

The "series of fairy-tale books" refers to Jacobs' landmark collections — *English Fairy Tales*, *Celtic Fairy Tales*, *Indian Fairy Tales*, and *More English Fairy Tales* — published between 1890 and 1894. These books, edited with scholarly apparatus and comparative notes, had made Jacobs the most visible folklorist in the English-speaking world by the time Tyson composed his essay. Jacobs' endorsement of the MacRitchie theory, or at least his willingness to present it clearly in a widely circulated publication, gave the hypothesis a prominence it might not otherwise have achieved.

Tyson's decision to quote Jacobs rather than MacRitchie directly reveals both the literary quality of Jacobs' prose and the essay's strategy of drawing upon recognised scholarly authorities. Jacobs' status as a folklorist and his editorial position at the head of a popular series lent weight to the MacRitchie hypothesis even for readers skeptical of its more radical claims.

## Scholarly Contributions

Beyond his fairy tale collections, Jacobs made significant contributions to Anglo-Jewish history (*The Jews of Angevin England*, 1893), literary studies, and the nascent discipline of folklore. He edited the journal *Folk-Lore* and was a leading figure in the Folk-Lore Society, the institutional centre of British folklore studies. His comparative method — tracing tale types and motifs across linguistic and cultural boundaries — paralleled Tyson's own philological approach to the pygmy question.
