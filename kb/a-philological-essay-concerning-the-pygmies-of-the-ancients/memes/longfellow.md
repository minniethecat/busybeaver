## Biography

Henry Wadsworth Longfellow (1807–1882) was one of the most popular American poets of the nineteenth century. His narrative poems drew extensively on American history and Native American legend. "The Song of Hiawatha" (1855), written in trochaic tetrameter, wove together Ojibwe legends collected by [[henry-rowe-schoolcraft|Henry Rowe Schoolcraft]] into an epic romance.

## The Hiawatha reference in Tyson

In [[section-9|Section 9]], Tyson discusses the convergence of fairy lore and pygmy traditions across cultures. He notes that Longfellow's "Hiawatha" contains motifs paralleling European pygmy-fairy traditions, as observed by the folklorist [[leland|Charles Godfrey Leland]].

> people, the fairies, the pigmies," in the pages of Longfellow's "Hiawatha." (009-055)

Tyson cites this connection as part of a broader argument—advanced by [[macritchie|David MacRitchie]] and others—that European fairy beliefs may preserve folk memories of aboriginal pygmy populations.
