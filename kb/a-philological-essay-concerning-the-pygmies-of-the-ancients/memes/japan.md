## Overview

Japan provides compelling East Asian evidence for the prehistoric existence of diminutive peoples. The Ainu traditions of the Koro-puk-guru — small, pit-dwelling people who preceded the Ainu in the Japanese archipelago — closely parallel the European fairy-mound and pygmy traditions. (002-062)

These traditions establish Japan as a key node in the global distribution of pygmy-related folklore. The Koro-puk-guru ("people who live in holes") share the troglodyte characterization found in classical pygmy accounts.

## In Ancient Geography

Japan was entirely unknown to classical Greek and Roman geography. Its relevance to the pygmy question is exclusively modern and ethnographic, drawing on the work of missionaries and anthropologists, particularly John Batchelor, who documented Ainu language and folklore in the late nineteenth century.

The country "now inhabited by the Niphonians, or Japanese" (002-063) was previously occupied by the Ainu. These Ainu in turn displaced or absorbed an even earlier population of small stature.

The Japanese case offers a well-documented sequence of population replacements: Koro-puk-guru → Ainu → Japanese. Each incoming group pushed the previous inhabitants to the margins. Folklore preserved memories of the displaced peoples as small, primitive, cave-dwelling beings.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

The Koro-puk-guru tradition connects to the pygmy legend through multiple parallels. The name itself — "hole-dwelling people" — matches the troglodyte characterization of pygmies in classical sources. These beings were said to be small, to live underground or in pits, and to emerge only at night to trade with the Ainu.

The Ainu traditions record that Yezo (Hokkaido) was previously "the home of the Ainu, a white, hairy" people (002-064), who in turn inherited the land from the even earlier Koro-puk-guru. This sequence of displacement closely parallels the European pattern identified by MacRitchie.

The essay notes that "the pigmies beyond Japan" (004-145) are mentioned in ancient sources, suggesting that the Japanese archipelago marked the eastern boundary of the known pygmy-inhabited world.

Whether this refers to actual pygmy populations in the Kuril Islands or is a geographic formula for "the farthest east," the reference establishes Japan as a waypoint in the circum-Asian distribution of small-statured peoples.

The Japanese evidence supports the broader theoretical framework: that pygmy-like populations once occupied a continuous range across southern and eastern Asia before being displaced by later migrations. Japan preserves in its folklore the same pattern of diminutive aboriginal peoples found at the western end in African pygmy accounts.

## Tyson's Discussion

Tyson does not discuss Japan directly — the Japanese evidence was unknown to European scholarship in 1699. The Japanese material enters the essay through Windle's editorial apparatus and late-nineteenth-century anthropological sources.

The methodological significance of the Japanese case is that it demonstrates convergence: traditions of small, displaced, hole-dwelling aboriginal peoples arise independently in regions (Japan, northern Europe, central Africa) that could not have influenced one another.

This convergence strengthens the argument that such traditions reflect a genuine prehistoric reality rather than cultural diffusion of a single myth.
