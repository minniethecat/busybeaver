# Samuel Purchas

**Samuel Purchas** (c. 1577–1626) was an English travel writer and compiler, best known for his monumental collection of travel narratives, _Purchas his Pilgrimes_ (1625). In Tyson's [[A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients]], Purchas is cited as the source through which [[andrew-battell]]'s firsthand account of the [[matimbas]]—a pygmy-like people of Loango—was transmitted to later scholarship (003-089).

## Purchas his Pilgrimes

_Purchas his Pilgrimes_ (full title: _Hakluytus Posthumus, or Purchas his Pilgrimes_) was a four-volume compilation of travel accounts, continuing the work of [[richard-hakluyt]]. Published in 1625, it gathered narratives from English and European explorers, merchants, and missionaries across Africa, Asia, and the Americas. The work served as a major repository of ethnographic knowledge in early modern England.

Purchas's methodology was that of a compiler rather than a firsthand observer—he collected, edited, and published the accounts of others. His volumes preserved numerous descriptions of foreign peoples that might otherwise have been lost, including accounts relevant to the pygmy question.

## Battell's Account of the Matimbas

The most significant passage for Tyson's essay appears in Purchas's transcription of [[andrew-battell]]'s observations from the Loango region of West Africa (003-088–003-094):

> "[003-089]in 1625, in the work entitled 'Purchas his Pilgrimes,' in the following terms:—'To the north-east of Mani-Kesock are a kind of little people called Matimbas; which are no bigger than boyes of twelve yeares old, but very thicke, and live only upon flesh, which they kill in the woods with their bows and darts. They pay tribute to Mani-Kesock, and bring all their elephants' teeth and tayles to him.'"

This account situates the Matimbas geographically in Loango (present-day [[congo]] region) and describes their physical stature, diet, hunting methods, and political relations—all of which figure in Tyson's broader argument for the historical reality of pygmy peoples in Africa.

## Significance for the Pygmy Question

Purchas's role in the pygmy debate is that of a crucial transmitter. Battell's original observations, made during his captivity in Angola and Loango (c. 1590–1610), reached later scholars through Purchas's compilation. Tyson draws on this account as part of his cumulative case for the existence of diminutive peoples across multiple continents, connecting the classical tradition of [[pygmies]] with early modern ethnographic reports.

The Matimbas account in Purchas serves Tyson's argument in [[section-3]] alongside descriptions from [[du-chaillu]], [[henry-morton-stanley]], and other African explorers, demonstrating that reports of small-statured peoples in Africa were not isolated fantasies but recurring observations across different regions and centuries.
