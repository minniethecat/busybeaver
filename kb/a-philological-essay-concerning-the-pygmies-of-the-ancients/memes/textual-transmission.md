## Overview

Textual Transmission addresses the material philological processes — copying, editing, translating, annotating — by which classical accounts of pygmy races reached Tyson's desk in 1699. Every ancient text that Tyson cites exists not as an unmediated witness to antiquity but as the product of a long chain of scribes, editors, translators, and commentators, each of whom introduced variations, corrections, and errors. The concept is methodologically fundamental because it reminds us that the "ancient sources" for pygmy races are not transparent windows onto classical reality but constructed artifacts of the textual tradition. Philology — the discipline that reconstructs and evaluates this tradition — is therefore an indispensable tool for the pygmy investigator.

## Ancient Sources

The transmission history of the principal pygmy texts illustrates the problem. Homer's *Iliad* survived through a manuscript tradition extending from the Alexandrian editors (third–second centuries BC) through Byzantine copyists to the first printed editions of the Renaissance. The Homeric scholia — marginal annotations in medieval manuscripts — preserve variant readings and ancient interpretations that sometimes differ significantly from the received text. When Tyson quotes "Homer. Iliad. lib. 3. ver. 4" (010-098), he is not reading Homer directly but a specific Renaissance edition that may incorporate Alexandrian editorial decisions, Byzantine scribal errors, and humanist philological corrections.

Pliny's *Natural History* presents a different but equally complex transmission history. Unlike Homer, whose text was carefully preserved through a continuous scholarly tradition, Pliny's encyclopaedia survived in a smaller number of medieval manuscripts, some of which contain significant textual corruptions. The measurements that Pliny reports for pygmy stature — two cubits, one cubit and a half — may reflect not Pliny's original figures but subsequent scribal alterations, a possibility that Tyson acknowledges when he notes that numbers are particularly vulnerable to corruption in manuscript transmission.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson was acutely aware of the textual-transmission problem, though he did not thematize it as a separate concept in the modern sense. His practice — comparing multiple editions, consulting scholia and commentaries, weighing variant readings — reveals a scholar who understood that the classical pygmy tradition is not a single stable text but a dynamic and contested textual field.

The question of Homer's meaning is a case in point. The Homeric scholiast — "reviewing the Homerical Fight of the Cranes and Pygmies" — had already noted that the passage might refer to apes rather than human pygmies, an interpretation that reached Tyson through the accumulated commentary tradition (010-143). "Homeri Ilias Latino Carmine reddita ab Helio Eobano Hesso" — the Latin verse translation of the *Iliad* by Helius Eobanus Hessus (1540) — is one of several intermediary texts through which Tyson accessed the Homeric original, each with its own interpretive inflections.

Tyson's treatment of the "Translation" question (11 instances in the corpus) reflects his awareness that translation is never a neutral act. The rendering of Greek *Pygmaioi* as Latin *Pygmaei* and English *Pygmies* carries a set of assumptions about what kind of entity is being named — a people, a species, a mythical category — that the original Greek may not fully determine. "Therefore be mistaken in rendering" Greek measurements into English, Tyson warns, because the choice of equivalent units embeds assumptions about the magnitude of the figures being translated (011-056).

## Modern Perspective

The textual-transmission question that Tyson addressed implicitly has become a major subfield of classical scholarship. Modern critical editions of Homer, Pliny, and Aristotle incorporate evidence from papyri, medieval manuscripts, and indirect quotations that were unavailable to Tyson. The discovery of new manuscript witnesses has in several cases confirmed readings that Tyson suspected were corrupt, and in other cases revealed corruptions that he accepted as genuine. The larger methodological point — that the classical pygmy tradition is a textual construction as much as an ethnographic report — has become a foundational principle of classical reception studies.
