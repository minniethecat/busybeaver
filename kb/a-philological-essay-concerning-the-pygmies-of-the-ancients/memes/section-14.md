# Section 14

[014-001]For I am not of _Gesner_'s mind, _Sed veterum nullus_ (saith he[A])

[014-002]_aliter de Pygmæis scripsit, quàm Homunciones esse_. Had they been a Race

[014-003]of _Men_, no doubt but _[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_ would have informed himself farther

[014-004]about them. Such a Curiosity could not but have excited his Inquisitive

[014-005]_Genius_, to a stricter Enquiry and Examination; and we might easily have

[014-006]expected from him a larger Account of them. But finding them, it may be, a

[014-007]sort of _[[apes|Apes]]_, he only tells us, that in such a place these _[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_

[014-008]live.

 
[Footnote A: _Gesner. Histor. Quadruped._ p.m. 885.]

[014-009][[herodotus|Herodotus]][A] plainly makes them _Brutes_: For reckoning up the _Animals_

[014-010]of _[[libya|Libya]]_, he tells us, [Greek: Kai gar hoi ophies hoi hypermegathees,

[014-011]kai hoi leontes kata toutous eisi, kai hoi elephantes te kai arktoi, kai

[014-012]aspides te kai onoi hoi ta kerata echontes; kai hoi kynokephaloi

[014-013](akephaloi) hoi en toisi staethesi tous ophthalmous echontes (hos dae

[014-014]legetai ge hypo libyon) kai agrioi andres, kai gynaikes agriai kai alla

[014-015]plaethei polla thaeria akatapseusta;] i.e. _That there are here prodigious

[014-016]large Serpents, and Lions, and Elephants, and Bears, and Asps, and Asses

[014-017]that have horns, and Cynocephali,_ (in the Margin 'tis _Acephali_) _that

[014-018]have Eyes in their Breast, (as is reported by the Libyans) and wild Men,

[014-019]and wild Women, and a great many other wild Beasts that are not fabulous._

[014-020]Tis evident therefore that _Herodotus_ his [Greek: agrioi andres, kai

[014-021]gynaikes agriai] are only [Greek: thaeria] or wild Beasts: and tho' they

[014-022]are called [Greek: andres], they are no more _Men_ than our

[014-023]_Orang-Outang_, or _Homo_ _Sylvestris_, or _wild Man_, which has exactly

[014-024]the same Name, and I must confess I can't but think is the same Animal:

[014-025]and that the same Name has been continued down to us, from his Time, and

[014-026]it may be from _[[homer|Homer]]'s_.

 
[Footnote A: _Herodot. Melpomene seu_ lib. 4. p.m. 285.]

[014-027]So _[[philostratus|Philostratus]]_ speaking of _Æthiopia_ and _Ægypt_, tells us,[A] [Greek:

[014-028]Boskousi de kai thaeria hoia ouch heterothi; kai anthropous melanas, ho

[014-029]mae allai aepeiroi. Pygmaion te en autais ethnae kai hylaktounton allo

[014-030]allaei.] i.e. _Here are bred wild Beasts that are not in other places; and

[014-031]black Men, which no other Country affords: and amongst them is the Nation

[014-032]of the Pygmies, and the_ BARKERS, that is, the _Cynocephali._ For tho'

[014-033]_Philostratus_ is pleased here only to call them _Barkers_, and to reckon

[014-034]them, as he does the _Black Men_ and the _Pygmies_ amongst the _wild

[014-035]Beasts_ of those Countreys; yet _Ctesias_, from whom _Philostratus_ has

[014-036]borrowed a great deal of his _Natural History_, stiles them _Men_, and

[014-037]makes them speak, and to perform most notable Feats in Merchandising. But

[014-038]not being in a merry Humour it may be now, before he was aware, he speaks

[014-039]Truth: For _Cælius Rhodiginus's_[B] Character of him is, _Philostratus

[014-040]omnium qui unquam Historiam conscripserunt, mendacissimus._

 
[Footnote A: _Philostratus in vita Apollon. Tyanæi_, lib. 6. cap. 1. p.m.

[014-041]258.]

 
[Footnote B: _Cælij Rhodigini Lection. Antiq._ lib. 17. cap. 13.]

[014-042]Since the _Pygmies_ therefore are some of the _Brute Beasts_ that

[014-043]naturally breed in these Countries, and they are pleased to let us know as

[014-044]much, I can easily excuse them a Name. [Greek: Andres agrioi], or

[014-045]_Orang-Outang_, is alike to me; and I am better pleased with _Homer_'s

[014-046][Greek: andres pygmaioi], than if he had called [Greek: pithaekoi]. Had

[014-047]this been the only Instance where they had misapplied the Name of _Man_,

[014-048]methinks I could be so good natur'd, as in some measure to make an Apology

[014-049]for them. But finding them, so extravagantly loose, so wretchedly

[014-050]whimsical, in abusing the Dignity of Mankind, by giving the name of _Man_

[014-051]to such monstrous Productions of their idle Imaginations, as the _Indian

[014-052]Historians_ have done, I do not wonder that wise Men have suspected all

[014-053]that comes out of their Mint, to be false and counterfeit.

[014-054]Such are their [Greek: Amykteres] or [Greek: Arrines], that want Noses,

[014-055]and have only two holes above their Mouth; they eat all things, but they

[014-056]must be raw; they are short lived; the upper part of their Mouths is very

[014-057]prominent. The [Greek: Enotokeitai], whose Ears reach down to their Heels,

[014-058]on which they lye and sleep. The [Greek: Astomoi], that have no Mouths, a

[014-059]civil sort of People, that dwell about the Head of the _Ganges_; and live

[014-060]upon smelling to boil'd Meats and the Odours of Fruits and Flowers; they

[014-061]can bear no ill scent, and therefore can't live in a Camp. The [Greek:

[014-062]Monommatoi] or [Greek: Monophthalmoi], that have but one Eye, and that in

[014-063]the middle of their Foreheads: they have Dog's Ears; their Hair stands an

[014-064]end, but smooth on the Breasts. The [Greek: Sternophthalmoi], that have

[014-065]Eyes in their Breasts. The [Greek: Panai sphaenokephaloi] with Heads like

[014-066]Wedges. The [Greek: Makrokephaloi], with great Heads. The [Greek:

[014-067]hyperboreoi], who live a Thousand years. The [Greek: okypodes], so swift

[014-068]that they will out-run a Horse. The [Greek: opiothodaktyloi], that go with

[014-069]their Heels forward, and their Toes backwards. The [Greek: Makroskeleis],

[014-070]The [Greek: Steganopodes], The [Greek: Monoskeleis], who have one Leg, but

[014-071]will jump a great way, and are call'd _Sciapodes_, because when they lye

[014-072]on their Backs, with this _Leg_ they can keep off the Sun from their

[014-073]Bodies.

[014-074]Now _[[strabo|Strabo]]_[A] from whom I have collected the Description of these

[014-075]Monstrous sorts of _Men_, and they are mentioned too by _Pliny, Solinus,

[014-076]Mela, Philostratus_, and others; and _Munster_ in his _Cosmography_[B] has

[014-077]given a _figure_ of some of them; _Strabo_, I say, who was an Enemy to all

[014-078]such fabulous Relations, no doubt was prejudiced likewise against the

[014-079]_Pygmies_, because these _Historians_ had made them a Puny Race of _Men_,

[014-080]and invented so many Romances about them. I can no ways therefore blame

[014-081]him for denying, that there were ever any such _Men Pygmies_; and do

[014-082]readily agree with him, that no _Man_ ever saw them: and am so far from

[014-083]dissenting from those Great Men, who have denied them on this account,

[014-084]that I think they have all the reason in the World on their side. And to

[014-085]shew how ready I am to close with them in this Point, I will here examine

[014-086]the contrary Opinion, and what Reasons they give for the supporting it:

[014-087]For there have been some _Moderns_, as well as the _Ancients_, that have

[014-088]maintained that these _Pygmies_ were real _Men_. And this they pretend to

[014-089]prove, both from _Humane Authority_ and _Divine_.

 
[Footnote A: _Strabo Geograph._ lib. 15. p.m. 489. & lib. 2. p. 48. _&

[014-090]alibi_.]

 
[Footnote B: _Munster Cosmograph._ lib. 6. p. 1151.]

[014-091]Now by _Men Pygmies_ we are by no means to understand _[[dwarfs|Dwarfs]]_. In all

[014-092]Countries, and in all Ages, there has been now and then observed such

[014-093]_Miniture_ of Mankind, or under-sized Men. _Cardan_[A] tells us he saw one

[014-094]carried about in a Parrot's Cage, that was but a Cubit high.

[014-095]_Nicephorus_[B] tells us, that in _Theodosius_ the Emperour's time, there

[014-096]was one in _Ægypt_ that was no bigger than a Partridge; yet what was to be

[014-097]admired, he was very Prudent, had a sweet clear Voice, and a generous Mind;

[014-098]and lived Twenty Years. So likewise a King of _Portugal_ sent to a Duke

[014-099]of _Savoy_, when he married his Daughter to him, an _Æthiopian Dwarf_ but

[014-100]three Palms high.[C] And _Thevenot_[D] tells us of the Present made by the

[014-101]King of the _Abyssins_, to the _Grand Seignior_, of several _little black

[014-102]Slaves_ out of _Nubia_, and the Countries near _Æthiopia_, which being

[014-103]made _Eunuchs_, were to guard the Ladies of the _Seraglio_. And a great

[014-104]many such like Relations there are. But these being only _Dwarfs_, they

[014-105]must not be esteemed the _Pygmies_ we are enquiring about, which are

[014-106]represented as a _Nation_, and the whole Race of them to be of the like

[014-107]stature. _Dari tamen integras Pumilionum Gentes, tam falsum est, quàm quod

[014-108]falsissimum_, saith _Harduin_.[E]

 
[Footnote A: _Cardan de subtilitate_, lib. 11. p. 458.]

 
[Footnote B: _Nicephor. Histor. Ecclesiiast._ lib. 12. cap. 37.]

 
[Footnote C: _Happelius in Relat. curiosis_, No. 85. p. 677.]

 
[Footnote D: _Thevenot. Voyage de Levant._ lib. 2. c. 68.]

 
[Footnote E: _Jo. Harduini Notæ in Plinij Nat. Hist._ lib. 6. cap. 22. p.

[014-109]688.]

[014-110]Neither likewise must it be granted, that tho' in some _Climates_ there

[014-111]might be _Men_ generally of less stature, than what are to be met with in

[014-112]other Countries, that they are presently _Pygmies_. _Nature_ has not fixed

[014-113]the same standard to the growth of _Mankind_ in all Places alike, no more

[014-114]than to _Brutes_ or _Plants_. The Dimensions of them all, according to the

[014-115]_Climate_, may differ. If we consult the Original, _viz. Homer_ that first

[014-116]mentioned the _Pygmies_, there are only these two _Characteristics_ he

[014-117]gives of them. That they are [Greek: Pygmaioi] _seu Cubitales_; and that

[014-118]the _Cranes_ did use to fight them. 'Tis true, as a _Poet_, he calls them

[014-119][Greek: andres], which I have accounted for before. Now if there cannot be

[014-120]found such _Men_ as are _Cubitales_, that the _Cranes_ might probably

[014-121]fight with, notwithstanding all the Romances of the _Indian Historians_, I

[014-122]cannot think these _Pygmies_ to be _Men_, but they must be some other

[014-123]_Animals_, or the whole must be a Fiction.

[014-124]Having premised this, we will now enquire into their Assertion that

[014-125]maintain the _Pygmies_ to be a Race of _Men_. Now because there have been

[014-126]_[[giants|Giants]]_ formerly, that have so much exceeded the usual Stature of _Man_,

[014-127]that there must be likewise _Pygmies_ as defective in the other extream

[014-128]from this Standard, I think is no conclusive Argument, tho' made use of by

[014-129]some. Old _Caspar Bartholine_[A] tells us, that because _J. Cassanius_ and

[014-130]others had wrote _de Gygantibus_, since no Body else had undertaken it, he

[014-131]would give us a Book _de Pygmæis_; and since he makes it his design to

[014-132]prove the Existence of _Pygmies_, and that the _Pygmies_ were _Men_, I

[014-133]must confess I expected great Matters from him.

 
[Footnote A: _Caspar. Bartholin. Opusculum de Pygmæis._]

[014-134]But I do not find he has informed us of any thing more of them, than what

[014-135]_Jo. Talentonius_, a Professor formerly at _Parma_, had told us before in

[014-136]his _Variarum & Reconditarum Rerum Thesaurus_,[A] from whom he has

[014-137]borrowed most of this _Tract_. He has made it a little more formal indeed,

[014-138]by dividing it into _Chapters_; of which I will give you the _Titles_; and

[014-139]as I see occasion, some Remarks thereon: They will not be many, because I

[014-140]have prevented my self already. The _first Chapter_ is, _De Homuncionibus

[014-141]& Pumilionilus seu Nanis à Pygmæis distinctis_. The _second Chapter, De

[014-142]Pygmæi nominibus & Etymologia_. The _third Chapter, Duplex esse Pygmæorum

[014-143]Genus; & primum Genus aliquando dari_. He means _Dwarfs_, that are no

[014-144]_Pygmies_ at all. The _fourth Chapter_ is, _Alterum Genus, nempe Gentem

[014-145]Pygmæorum esse, aut saltem aliquando fuisse Autoritatibus Humanis, fide

[014-146]tamen dignorum asseritur_. 'Tis as I find it printed; and no doubt an

[014-147]Error in the printing. The Authorities he gives, are, _Homer, Ctesias,

[014-148]Aristotle, Philostratus, Pliny, [[juvenal|Juvenal]], Oppian, Baptista Mantuan_, St.

[014-149]_Austin_ and his _Scholiast. Ludovic. Vives, Jo. Laurentius Anania, Joh.

[014-150]Cassanius, Joh. Talentonius, Gellius, Pomp. Mela_, and _Olaus Magnus_. I

[014-151]have taken notice of most of them already, as I shall of St. _Austin_ and

[014-152]_Ludovicus Vives_ by and by. _Jo. Laurentius Anania_[B] ex Mercatorum

[014-153]relatione tradit (saith _Bartholine_) eos _(sc. Pygmæos) in

[014-154]Septentrionali Thraciæ Parte reperiri, (quæ Scythiæ est proxima) atque ibi

[014-155]cum Gruibus pugnare_. And _Joh. Cassanius_[C] (as he is here quoted)

[014-156]saith, _De Pygmæis fabulosa quidem esse omnia, quæ de iis narrari solent,

[014-157]aliquando existimavi. Verùm cum videam non unum vel alterum, sed complures

[014-158]Classicos & probatos Autores de his Homunculis multa in eandem fere

[014-159]Sententiam tradidisse; eò adducor ut Pygmæos fuisse inficiari non ausim._

[014-160]He next brings in _Jo. Talentonius_, to whom he is so much beholden, and

[014-161]quotes his Opinion, which is full and home, _Constare arbitror_ (saith

[014-162]_Talentonius_)[D] _debere concedi, Pygmæos non solùm olim fuisse, sed nunc

[014-163]etiam esse, & homines esse, nec parvitatem illis impedimenta esse quo

[014-164]minùs sint & homines sint._ But were there such _Men Pygmies_ now in

[014-165]being, no doubt but we must have heard of them; some or other of our

[014-166]Saylors, in their Voyages, would have lighted on them. Tho' _Aristotle_ is

[014-167]here quoted, yet he does not make them _Men_; So neither does _Anania_:

[014-168]And I must own, tho' _Talentonius_ be of this Opinion, yet he takes notice

[014-169]of the faulty Translation of this Text of _Aristotle_ by _Gaza_: and tho'

[014-170]the parvity or lowness of Stature, be no Impediment, because we have

[014-171]frequently seen such _Dwarf-Men_, yet we did never see a _Nation_ of them:

[014-172]For then there would be no need of that _Talmudical_ Precept which _Job.

[014-173]Ludolphus_[E] mentions, _Nanus ne ducat Nanam, ne fortè oriatur ex iis

[014-174]Digitalis_ (in _Bechor_. fol. 45).
