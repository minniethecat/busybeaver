## Identity

Dr. Falkenstein (fl. late nineteenth century) was an anthropologist and photographer active in African exploration and ethnography. His work combined anthropometric measurement with photographic documentation—an emerging scientific methodology in the late Victorian period. Biographical details are sparse; he is known primarily through his field observations cited in anthropological literature.

## Stature measurement in Tyson

In [[section-3|Section 3]], Tyson presents Falkenstein's anthropometric data as part of the evidence for diminutive human populations in Africa. Falkenstein measured and photographed an adult male whose stature was recorded at four feet six inches (approximately 137 cm)—a figure consistent with the pygmy stature ranges Tyson was documenting.

> Falkenstein, photographed and measured an adult male whose stature was four feet six inches. (003-104)

This measurement appears alongside other stature data from [[krapf|Johann Ludwig Krapf]] and [[stanley|Henry Morton Stanley]], building the cumulative case for the existence of pygmy-sized populations across different regions of Africa.
