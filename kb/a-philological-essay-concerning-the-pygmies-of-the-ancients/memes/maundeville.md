# Sir John Mandeville

Sir John Mandeville was a fourteenth-century English travel writer whose fantastical and influential account, *The Travels of Sir John Mandeville*, circulated widely in medieval Europe. His descriptions of pygmy races in the islands near Java and in the land of the Great Khan provide some of the most vivid medieval testimony to the belief in diminutive peoples of the East.

## Mandeville's Pygmy Accounts

Edward Tyson devotes substantial attention to Mandeville's testimony in his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*. Mandeville provides two distinct accounts of little peoples in Asia.

### The Mouthless Dwarfs of the Isles

Tyson first cites Mandeville's account of an insular race of mouthless dwarfs near Java (004-095):

> Writing of isles in the neighbourhood of Java, Maundeville says, "In another yle, ther ben litylle folk, as dwerghes; and thei ben to so meche as the Pygmeyes, and thei han no mouthe, but in stede of hire mouthe, thei han a lytylle round hole; and whan thei schulle eten or drynken, thei taken thorghe a pipe or a penne or suche a thing, and sowken it in, for thei han no tongue, and therefore thei speke not, but thei maken a maner of hissynge, as a Neddre dothe." (004-095–004-102)

Tyson acknowledges the fantastical nature of this description, noting that if one strips away "the characteristic Maundevillian touches with regard to the mouth and tongue," the account may refer to actual insular races of the region (004-103–004-105).

### The Pygmies of the Great Khan's Land

Mandeville's second account describes pygmies dwelling near a great river identified by a commentator as the Yangtze-Kiang (004-106–004-108):

> "That ryvere gothe thorge the lond of Pigmaus, where that the folk ben of litylle stature, that ben but 3 span long, and thei ben right faire and gentylle, aftre here quantytees, bothe the men and the women. And thei maryen hem, whan thei ben half zere of age and getten children. And thei lyven not, but 6 zeer or 7 at the moste." (004-112–004-116)

Mandeville further records that these pygmies were skilled artisans — "the beste worcheres of gold, sylver, cotoun, sylk" (004-117–004-118) — and that they waged war with the birds of the country (004-118–004-119), an echo of the [[homeric-geranomachia-simile|Homeric Geranomachia]].

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson notes that this passage "incorporates the Homeric tale of the battles between the Pigmies and the Cranes, and is adorned with a representation of such an encounter" (004-132–004-134). He raises the question of whether Mandeville's dwarfs correspond to the Siao-Jin of the Shan-hai-King, the legendary pygmy race of Chinese tradition (004-134–004-137).

Tyson also connects Mandeville's account to the Catalan map of the world (A.D. 1375), which records "little men who are but five palms in height" northwest of Cathay (004-138–004-142), and suggests that [[paulus-jovius|Paulus Jovius]] may have drawn upon similar sources when describing pygmies beyond Japan (004-143–004-145).

## Legacy

Mandeville's *Travels* remained influential well into the early modern period. His conflation of Homeric myth, Chinese legend, and traveller's tales exemplifies the syncretic nature of medieval pygmy lore, which Tyson sought to disentangle through philological and ethnographic analysis.
