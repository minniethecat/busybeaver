## Overview

China enters Tyson's essay in two distinct registers: as part of Indo-China, a region identified as the primordial homeland of Negrito populations, and as a test case for the modern disappearance of pygmy-like peoples from formerly inhabited territories. (001-121) The observation that "China no longer, so far as we know, contains any representatives of this race" (002-046) is methodologically significant — it acknowledges that the absence of contemporary evidence does not refute historical presence, a principle central to Tyson's evaluation of ancient pygmy accounts.

## In Ancient Geography

Classical authors knew China only dimly, through the silk trade and fragmentary reports. The Seres (silk-people) of Greek and Roman geography were understood to inhabit the far east of Asia, but no classical source placed pygmies in China proper. The Chinese material in the essay is therefore entirely modern, drawn from nineteenth-century anthropology and the historical records of Chinese civilization itself.

Of particular importance is the Chinese textual record, which documents the displacement of aboriginal peoples — including, potentially, diminutive races — by the expanding Han civilization. "In the twenty-third century B.C., the civilised Bak tribes" (002-061) displaced earlier inhabitants, a process that may have eliminated or absorbed pygmy-like populations. The "small area which the Chinese dominion" (002-059) originally covered, contrasted with its later expansion, implies a long history of contact with and displacement of aboriginal peoples.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

China's connection to the pygmy question is primarily negative evidence — the disappearance of pygmy populations from regions where they presumably once existed. "China no longer, so far as we know, contains any representatives" (002-046) of the Negrito race, yet Indo-China was identified by de Quatrefages as part of the original Negrito homeland. This gap between historical distribution and modern absence supports the broader argument that pygmy populations have been displaced and absorbed by later-arriving peoples across much of their former range.

The essay also notes that "some sort of marine plant or seaweed" (002-054) was used by immigrant Chinese populations, suggesting the material culture differences that would have distinguished aboriginal from incoming populations. The Chinese empire (004-137) is mentioned as a comparator for the geographic scale over which pygmy-related traditions are distributed.

## Tyson's Discussion

Tyson does not engage with China as a primary locus of pygmy geography. The Chinese evidence is deployed by Windle and de Quatrefages to establish the former extent of Negrito populations across southern and eastern Asia. The essay notes the Chinese case as part of a broader pattern: wherever agricultural civilizations expanded, aboriginal hunting and gathering populations — often of smaller stature — were displaced, absorbed, or exterminated.

The methodological point is that absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. China, where written records extend back millennia, provides a particularly well-documented case of aboriginal displacement that can serve as a model for understanding similar but less well-recorded processes elsewhere.
