## Overview

England occupies a special place in Tyson's essay not through classical geography but through the fairy-lore traditions of the British Isles. The "good people," pixy-houses, and underground dwellings recorded across England provide MacRitchie with some of his strongest evidence that European fairy beliefs encode historical memories of an aboriginal pygmy race. (005-084) The Arthurian landscape — groves, mounds, and stone structures — forms the physical backdrop against which these traditions are mapped.

## In Ancient Geography

England, as the Roman province of Britannia, was known to classical geographers but is not directly associated with pygmies in ancient sources. Its relevance to Tyson's argument is archaeological and folkloric rather than classical. The essay draws on nineteenth-century British folklore collections to establish a dense texture of fairy-belief across the English countryside. (005-102) From Devon pixy-houses to the broader pattern of "good people" traditions, England offers a well-documented case study of the small, hidden, mound-dwelling beings that MacRitchie identifies as folk memories of a conquered pygmy population. (007-050)

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

England's connection to the pygmy question rests on three pillars of the MacRitchie thesis. First, the pixy or piskie tradition of Devon and Cornwall describes small, cave-dwelling beings who emerge at night, avoid iron, and inhabit stone structures — all traits MacRitchie attributes to displaced aboriginal populations. "In England the Pixy-house of Devon is in a stone" (007-050) directly parallels the Scottish and Irish fairy-mound accounts.

Second, the "good people" terminology — a euphemistic name for fairies used across England — mirrors the respectful avoidance-naming found in cultures describing neighboring diminutive peoples. "The country is consecrated to the 'good people'" (005-102) reflects the same pattern of propitiatory naming that anthropologists documented among peoples living alongside pygmy groups.

Third, English fairy-lore shares structural features with Scandinavian, German, and Celtic traditions, forming a continuous north-European complex that MacRitchie takes as evidence of a widely distributed pre-historic small race. The English evidence is particularly valuable because it is among the best-documented in European folklore studies. (007-119)

## Tyson's Discussion

Tyson himself, writing in 1699, does not directly discuss England as a pygmy location. The English material enters the essay through Windle's editorial additions and through the broader scholarly apparatus that connects Tyson's classical philology to nineteenth-century anthropology. The English translations referenced in the essay — "the Italian, Low Dutch, and English translators" (016-012) — remind us that the essay itself participates in an English scholarly tradition of evaluating classical claims about exotic peoples.

The English fairy evidence serves a methodological function: it demonstrates that traditions of small, hidden peoples are not confined to the distant lands described by ancient authors but exist in the heart of modern Europe. This proximity strengthens the argument that such traditions have a basis in observed reality rather than travelers' fables.
