## Overview

The Congo-Gaboon region of Central Africa furnishes Tyson with some of his most detailed modern evidence for the existence of diminutive races. The area between the Gaboon (Gabon) River and the Congo River was home to multiple pygmy populations — the Akka, Batwa, Wambutti, Akoas, and M'Boulous — whose physical measurements, hunting practices, and trade relations were documented by nineteenth-century explorers. (003-086)

Tyson also preserves a remarkable classical-modern bridge: Vossius's account of Central African "Nanos" (dwarfs) who hunt elephants rather than cranes, and whose ivory trade connects ancient pygmy legends to contemporary economic reality. (016-161)

## Central African Pygmy Populations

Tyson draws on Stanley's account of the region between the Ngaiyu and Ituri rivers:

> _Among the Balessé, between Ipoto and Mount Pisgah, and inhabiting the land between the Ngaiyu and Ituri rivers, a region equal in area to about two-thirds of Scotland, are the Wambutti, variously called Batwa, Akka, and Bazungu. These people are under-sized nomads, dwarfs or pigmies, who live in the uncleared virgin forest, and support themselves on game, which they are very expert in catching. They vary in height from three feet to four feet six inches._ (003-025)

This description is significant for its precision: the Wambutti are not mythical but documented inhabitants of a specific, measurable territory, with recorded height ranges and observable economic practices. Their use of poisoned arrows to kill elephants and expertise in trapping (003-031) mark them as skilled hunters integrated into a regional economy.

## The Gaboon and Ashangoland Populations

Du Chaillu's measurements provided further documentation. "Between the Gaboon and the Congo, in Ashangoland, a male of which measured four feet six inches, has been described by Du Chaillu." (003-086)

The Akoas of the Gaboon district, "now apparently extinct" (003-074), represent a cautionary case: a pygmy population that vanished within living memory. A male of this tribe, photographed and measured by the French Admiral Fleuriot de l'Angle, "was about forty and his stature four feet six inches." (003-076)

The M'Boulous, inhabiting the coast north of the Gaboon River, were described by M. Marche as "probably the primitive race of the country." (003-079) Their numbers were rapidly diminishing under pressure from larger neighboring tribes: "In 1860 they were not more than 3000; in 1879 they were much less numerous." (003-083) This documented decline reinforces Tyson's broader thesis that diminutive races everywhere are being displaced by larger populations.

## The Elephant-Ivory Trade

Tyson preserves Vossius's reconciliation of classical and modern pygmy accounts through the elephant trade. Vossius notes that while the ancients spoke of pygmies fighting cranes, the modern reality is that Central African dwarfs hunt elephants:

> _Certum quippe est ultra_ Nili _fontes multos reperiri_ Nanos, _qui tamen non cum Gruibus, sed cum Elephantis perpetuum gerant bellum. Præcipuum quippe Eboris commercium in regno magni_ Macoki _per istos transigitur Homunciones._ (016-164)

"It is certain that beyond the sources of the Nile many Dwarfs are found, who however wage perpetual war not with Cranes but with Elephants. For the principal commerce of ivory in the kingdom of the great Macoko is carried on by these little men." The ivory passes from the pygmy hunters to the Jagi, "who sell them to those of _Congo_ and the _Portuguese_." (016-161)

This trade network — pygmy hunters → Jagi middlemen → Congo and Portuguese markets — demonstrates that the Central African pygmies were not isolated curiosities but active participants in a commercial system extending to the Atlantic coast.

## Significance for Tyson's Argument

The Congo evidence is central to Tyson's philological project. He uses it to demonstrate that: (1) diminutive races of documented, measurable stature exist in Central Africa; (2) their hunting practices (elephants rather than cranes) represent a rational adaptation of the classical pygmy-war motif; and (3) their integration into the ivory trade shows they are real economic actors, not mythological beings.
