Justus Mathias Thiele (1795–1874) was a Danish writer, librarian, and folklorist. He is best known for his pioneering collection of Danish folk legends, *Danske Folkesagn* (Danish Folk Legends), first published in four volumes between 1818 and 1823. His work was among the earliest systematic attempts to record and preserve the oral folk traditions of Denmark.

## Thiele in the Fairy-Pygmy Theory

Thiele appears in Tyson's essay indirectly, through the citations of Thomas Keightley, the Irish folklorist whose *Fairy Mythology* (1828) supplied much of the comparative folkloric evidence for Tyson's argument. In Section 6, Tyson, drawing on Keightley, relates a Danish legend about a hill inhabited by diminutive supernatural beings:

> "Quoted by Keightley (p. 9), from Thiele, i. 118."
> (006-043)

The Danish legend preserved by Thiele described a hill that appeared small on the outside but was believed to house a community of diminutive supernatural beings within. This motif — the hidden fairy dwelling concealed within an ordinary-looking landscape feature — was one that Keightley and, following him, Tyson, connected to the ancient accounts of Pygmies (006-042, 006-043).

## Thiele's Danish Legends in Section 7

In Section 7, Tyson returns to Keightley's citations of Thiele in the context of fairy tribute and the relationship between fairies and trees:

> "Keightley, p. 92, quoting from Thiele."
> (007-145)

This passage forms part of a broader discussion in which Tyson surveys comparative evidence from Germanic, Celtic, and Scandinavian folklore. Thiele's Danish material, alongside the work of Jacob Grimm on Germanic traditions, helped establish the cross-cultural pattern of fairy beliefs (007-143, 007-144).

Together, these sources suggested that fairy traditions might represent folk memories of encounters with real pygmy-sized peoples — a key element in Tyson's euhemerist theory of the Pygmies.

## Significance for the Pygmy Debate

Thiele's contribution to the pygmy debate is modest but characteristic of Tyson's comparative methodology. By citing Keightley's references to Thiele, Tyson anchors his folkloric argument in documented sources.

The evidence from Thiele showed that the belief in diminutive subterranean beings extended beyond the British Isles to Scandinavia. This comparative breadth strengthened the case that fairy legends preserve genuine folk memories rather than being pure literary inventions.

## Legacy

Thiele's *Danske Folkesagn* remains a foundational text in Danish folklore studies. His method of collecting oral traditions directly from informants anticipated the fieldwork-based approach that would later become standard in folkloristics.

For Tyson's essay, Thiele serves as a scholarly link — from Danish oral tradition, through Keightley's comparative mythology, to Tyson's philological argument about the Pygmies of the ancients.
