Albertus Magnus (c. 1200–1280), also known as Saint Albert the Great, was a German Dominican friar, philosopher, and natural scientist. One of the most learned figures of the medieval period, he wrote extensively on theology, natural philosophy, and the Aristotelian corpus. His work *De Animalibus* (On Animals), a massive commentary on Aristotle's zoological writings, was among the most important medieval contributions to natural history.

Albertus occupies a central and recurring role in Tyson's essay. His theory — that the Pygmies were a species of ape — is one of the principal positions against which Tyson argues throughout the work, appearing in no fewer than four separate sections.

## The Ape Theory

In Section 12, Tyson introduces Albertus's theory with a mixture of admiration and criticism. Albertus had the insight to connect Pygmies with apes — a connection Tyson himself entertains — but then, in Tyson's view, ruined his argument with an extravagant claim:

> "But _Albertus Magnus_, who was so lucky as to guess that the _Pygmies_ were a sort of _Apes_; that he should afterwards make these _Apes_ to _speak_, was very unfortunate, and spoiled all; and he do's it, methinks, so very awkwardly, that it is as difficult almost to understand his Language as his _Apes_."
> (012-157)

Tyson's criticism is pointed: Albertus's talking apes are both implausible and poorly argued. The wit of Tyson's remark — that Albertus's language is as hard to understand as his apes' — reflects his broader rhetorical strategy of using humor to weaken opposing positions.

## The Difficulty of Albertus's Position

In Section 13, Tyson acknowledges that a narrower version of Albertus's claim — that Pygmies were simply apes, without the added assertion of speech — might have gained more traction:

> "Had _Albertus_ only asserted, that the _Pygmies_ were a sort of _Apes_, his Opinion possibly might have obtained with less difficulty, unless he could have produced some Body that had heard them talk."
> (013-001)

This passage reveals the nuance in Tyson's position. He does not dismiss the ape connection outright — indeed, much of his own argument concerns the relationship between Pygmies, apes, and humans — but he insists that the evidence must be evaluated rigorously, and that extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.

## Albertus Among the Skeptics

Later in the essay, Albertus appears as one link in a chain of skeptical opinion. In Section 15, Tyson reports the critique of Albertus by later scholars, particularly the French physician Jean Riolan (referenced as "Bartholine" in context), who considered Albertus's opinion absurd:

> "_Albertus Magnus's_ Opinion he thinks absurd, that makes them part Men part Beasts; they must be either one or the other, not a _Medium_ between both."
> (015-094)

This objection — that a creature cannot be halfway between human and animal — is a recurring theme in early modern debates about the boundaries of humanity, and Albertus's theory served as a focal point for these discussions.

## Albertus on Pygmy Lifespan

Tyson also records Albertus's observation about the short lifespan of the Pygmies:

> "..._Vitæ autem longitudo anni arciter octo ut_ Albertus _refert._"
> (015-131)

This report — that Pygmies lived only about eight years — was drawn from Albertus's reading of ancient sources and passed down through later authors such as Cardan (015-132). The brief lifespan, if true, would have significant implications for the development of pygmy culture, language, and social organization — a point Tyson addresses elsewhere in the essay.

## Final Reference in Section 17

In the concluding section, Tyson returns to Albertus one last time, noting that Albertus was frequently quoted by later authors on the pygmy question:

> "_Albertus_, as I find him frequently quoted, thought that the _Pygmies_..."
> (017-176)

This final mention underscores Albertus's enduring influence on pygmy discourse. Whether accepted or rejected, his ape theory shaped the terms of debate for centuries after his death.
