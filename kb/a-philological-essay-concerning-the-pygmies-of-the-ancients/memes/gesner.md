Conrad Gesner (1516–1565) was a Swiss physician, naturalist, and bibliographer whose monumental *Historiae Animalium* (1551–1558) is considered one of the foundational works of modern zoology. Often called the "father of bibliography," Gesner compiled and synthesized classical and contemporary knowledge about the animal kingdom.

Tyson cites Gesner four times in his essay, consistently grouping him among the learned men who interpreted Aristotle's account of Pygmies as describing a race of diminutive human beings.

## Gesner on the Pygmies

Gesner is cited by Tyson as an authority who held that no ancient author wrote of the Pygmies in any other way than as "Homunciones" (little men). Tyson records Gesner's dictum:

> "Sed veterum nullus aliter de Pygmæis scripsit, quàm Homunciones esse."
> (014-001)

This claim — "No one of the ancients wrote of the Pygmies otherwise than that they are Little Men" — forms part of Tyson's broader argument. He notes that Gesner, along with Aldrovandus and other learned men, cited Aristotle's *History of Animals* in support of the view that Pygmies were human rather than simian.

Tyson, however, takes issue with Gesner's formulation. He argues that several ancient writers — most notably Herodotus — did *not* treat Pygmies as human beings but rather as brutes or animals. In this respect, Gesner serves in Tyson's text as a representative of the scholarly mainstream whose views Tyson seeks to challenge with a more careful reading of the classical sources.

Tyson also records Gesner's note on Pygmy lifespan, drawn from Albertus Magnus: "Vitæ autem longitudo anni circiter octo ut Albertus refert" — their lifespan is about eight years (015-130). This detail, repeated across multiple Renaissance natural histories, reflects the persistence of medieval lore in early modern zoological writing.

## Key Work Cited by Tyson

Tyson references Gesner's *Historiae Animalium*, specifically the volume on quadrupeds, cited as "Gesner. Histor. Quadruped. p.m. 885" (014-008). This encyclopedic work drew on Aristotle, Pliny, and medieval bestiaries.

Gesner's discussion of the Pygmies appears in the context of his broader treatment of human-like creatures and monstrous races, a standard topic in Renaissance natural history.
