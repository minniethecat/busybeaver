George Coffey (1857–1916) was an Irish archaeologist and curator of antiquities at the Royal Irish Academy and later the National Museum of Ireland. He was a leading authority on Irish prehistoric monuments, particularly the great passage tombs of the Boyne Valley, and his careful studies of these sites provide Tyson with an important counterpoint to the MacRitchie mound-dweller hypothesis.

## Coffey's Archaeological Work in the Essay

Coffey enters Tyson's discussion in Section 5 during the evaluation of MacRitchie's theory that certain Irish mounds were prehistoric dwellings. Tyson cites Coffey's study of the Brugh of the Boyne (Newgrange), noting that "Mr. Coffey in a most careful study" has examined this site and reached conclusions different from MacRitchie's — interpreting the Brugh as a burial monument rather than a former dwelling-place (005-168).

The essay further invokes Coffey's authority on the stone basins found within Irish passage tombs. Where MacRitchie regards the nature of these basins as doubtful, Coffey considers them to have served as "a receptacle for urns" (005-173). Tyson also records that "Mr. Coffey also quotes" an Irish poetic tradition stating that the legendary figures associated with Cruachan "did not bury at Cruachan" (005-182), evidence that bears on MacRitchie's dwelling-place interpretation.

In Section 6, Tyson notes that Coffey's observations extend figuratively to broader questions of how prehistoric monuments relate to later folk traditions, observing that "Mr. Coffey states, in connection with" certain Irish sites, evidence that complicates a simple dwelling-place reading (006-006).

## Significance in the MacRitchie Debate

Coffey's role in the essay is specific but structurally important. He provides the archaeological counter-evidence that allows Tyson to maintain a critical distance from MacRitchie's mound-dweller theory. By juxtaposing Coffey's burial interpretation against MacRitchie's dwelling hypothesis, the essay models the methodological principle of weighing competing expert interpretations rather than simply marshaling supporting evidence.

## Key Work Cited

- **"On the Tumuli and Inscribed Stones at New Grange, Dowth, and Knowth"** — Coffey's study of the Boyne Valley passage tombs, cited by Tyson as the authoritative archaeological analysis of these sites and their original function.
