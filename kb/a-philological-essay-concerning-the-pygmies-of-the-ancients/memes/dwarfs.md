# Dwarfs

## Overview

Dwarfs (often spelled "dwarfs" or "dwarfes" in Tyson's 1699 text) are a recurring category of small-statured peoples in ancient ethnography. Edward Tyson, in _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_, systematically examines dwarf races as part of his broader investigation into whether the ancient Pygmies were mythical, historical, or zoological. (001-085)

Tyson distinguishes between various categories of diminutive peoples: the Homeric Pygmies, African dwarf tribes reported by travelers, and apes misidentified as small humans. His discussion of dwarfs provides crucial context for understanding the broader ancient discourse on human variety. (001-088)

## Classical Sources

Ancient authors described dwarf races in various locations. Ctesias of Cnidus wrote of dwarfs in India, while Pomponius Mela placed them in Africa. (002-078) Aristotle noted that certain peoples were naturally "dwarfish" in stature, the latter especially so in comparison with Greeks. (002-122)

Accounts of dwarf men were of great antiquity and struck ancient writers as remarkable natural phenomena rather than purely mythical inventions. (003-013)

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson examines reports of nomadic dwarfs who lived in specific African regions, noting the ethnographic details provided by ancient authors. (003-026) He discusses accounts of dwarf tribes, including a notable passage about the queen of a dwarf people. (003-041)

Crucially, Tyson describes the behavior attributed to dwarf tribes: "As soon as a party of dwarfs makes its appearance near a" neighboring territory, they provoke conflict—a pattern reminiscent of the Homeric geranomachia. (003-061)

Tyson assembles evidence to demonstrate that a tribe of dwarfs was indeed known to antiquity, and that their existence need not be dismissed as pure fable. (003-114) His anthropometrical approach—comparing descriptions of stature across sources—represents an early scientific methodology. (001-104)

## Relation to Pygmies

The relationship between dwarfs and Pygmies is central to Tyson's argument. He questions whether the two categories refer to the same phenomenon or whether ancient authors distinguished between different types of small-statured peoples. His conclusion—that many accounts of Pygmies and dwarfs actually describe apes—unifies the discussion under a natural-historical framework.
