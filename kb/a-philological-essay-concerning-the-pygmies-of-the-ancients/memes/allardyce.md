# Dr. Allardyce

Dr. Allardyce was a British physician of good standing practising in Cheltenham during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. His principal significance for the history of pygmy studies lies not in his own scholarly contributions but in his familial connection to [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]]: Allardyce married Tyson's granddaughter, and through this marriage the famous "Pygmie" skeleton — the anatomical specimen that had inspired Tyson's *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients* — passed into his custody and ultimately into institutional preservation.

## The Pygmy Skeleton as Dowry

In the introduction to his essay, while tracing the provenance of the anatomical specimen that serves as the work's centerpiece, Tyson records the remarkable fate of the pygmy skeleton after his own death:

> Tyson's granddaughter married a Dr. Allardyce, who was a physician of good standing in Cheltenham. The "Pygmie" formed a somewhat remarkable item of her dowry. Her husband presented it to the Cheltenham Museum, where it was fortunately carefully preserved until... (001-045)

The passage of the pygmy skeleton as a marital dowry item, and its subsequent donation to the Cheltenham Museum, represents a critical link in the chain of custody that ensured the specimen's survival. Without Allardyce's decision to place the skeleton in an institutional collection, the anatomical evidence at the heart of Tyson's philological investigation might well have been lost. The Cheltenham Museum's careful preservation of the specimen ultimately enabled its transfer to the Natural History Museum at South Kensington, where it remained available for examination by later generations of anatomists and anthropologists.

## Role in the Provenance Chain

Allardyce occupies a modest but indispensable position in the documentary and material history of Tyson's essay. While he contributed no original research to the pygmy question, his actions as custodian and donor bridged the gap between Tyson's private anatomical collection and the public museum system of nineteenth-century Britain. The fact that the pygmy skeleton survived the vagaries of private inheritance — passing from anatomist to granddaughter to son-in-law to municipal museum — testifies to the recognition, even among non-specialists, that Tyson's "Pygmie" was an object of enduring scientific interest.
