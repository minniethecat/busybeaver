Dame Rapson is a figure from Hungarian folklore, mentioned in fairy tales
recorded in Magyar oral tradition. She appears as the owner of a castle
near which caves were said to be inhabited by fairies. (006-077)
Tyson cites these Hungarian legends as part of his broader survey of
diminutive supernatural beings across European folklore traditions,
drawing parallels between fairy lore and ancient accounts of pygmies.

## In Hungarian Folklore

In the Magyar fairy tradition, caves near Dame Rapson's castle at
Borza-vára were identified as haunts of fairies. The local population
attributed supernatural phenomena to these cave-dwelling beings,
including the cold "Nemere" wind, which was said to blow when the fairy
in the nearby Almás cave felt cold. (006-077) During an outbreak of
plague, the inhabitants ascribed the epidemic to the displeasure of
these cave-dwelling spirits.

The folklore further describes the fairy palace at Tartod and Dame
Rapson's own residence as illuminated by three diamond balls, each as
large as a human head, suspended from the ceiling—a motif of
supernatural splendor common in Central European fairy narratives.
(006-122) Tyson compares these glittering subterranean palaces to the
dwelling-places attributed to fairies and pygmy-like beings across
various cultures.

## Tyson's Comparative Method

Tyson includes the Dame Rapson tales within his philological survey of
diminutive beings in world mythology. The Hungarian fairy caves serve as
one example among many—alongside Irish, Norse, and Indian traditions—of
the widespread belief in small, supernatural cave-dwelling races. Tyson
uses these parallels to argue that many "pygmy" reports in ancient
authors may reflect encounters with similarly diminutive populations,
filtered through the lens of folkloric embellishment. (006-077) (006-122)
