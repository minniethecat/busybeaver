Mr. Macdonald was a folklorist and observer of African traditions whose testimony on water and river spirits appears in Section 7 of Tyson's essay. His observations contributed to the comparative framework through which Tyson examined beliefs in diminutive supernatural beings across different cultural traditions.

## African Water Spirits

Tyson introduces Macdonald's observations in the context of a broader discussion of water-dwelling supernatural beings, drawing comparative connections between European, Polynesian, and African traditions. Macdonald's testimony concerns the spirits of African waterways:

> "The spirits most commonly met with in African mythology," says Mr. Macdonald, "are water or river spirits, inhabiting deep pools where there are strong eddies and under-currents. Whether they are all even seen now-a-days it is difficult to determine, but they must" (007-167)

The passage, though Tyson's quotation is truncated mid-sentence, clearly establishes the pervasiveness of belief in water-dwelling spirits across African traditions. These beings, associated with dangerous natural features — deep pools, strong eddies, and under-currents — represented the immanence of supernatural agency within the natural landscape.

## Comparative Significance in Tyson's Argument

Macdonald's African water spirits are presented alongside similar beings from other cultural traditions: Tregear's Maori and Polynesian "elves, little tiny people, mostly dwellers in water, coming ashore to sleep," and European traditions of water-dwelling fairies and nixies. Tyson's strategy in Section 7 was to accumulate cross-cultural evidence for a consistent pattern of belief in diminutive supernatural beings associated with natural features — water, stones, mounds — that might preserve dim memories of aboriginal populations or, alternatively, represent universal products of the human imagination confronted with the mysterious in nature.

Macdonald's contribution illustrates the essay's reliance upon informants whose direct contact with non-European traditions provided data otherwise inaccessible to the armchair scholar. The brevity of Tyson's reference to Macdonald, and the absence of fuller bibliographic detail, suggests that Macdonald's observations may have reached Tyson through intermediary sources or personal communication rather than through widely circulated publications.
