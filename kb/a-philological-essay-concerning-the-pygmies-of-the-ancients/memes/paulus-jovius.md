Paolo Giovio (1483–1552), Latinized as Paulus Jovius, was an Italian historian, physician, and bishop of Nocera. A prominent figure of the Italian Renaissance, he wrote extensively on contemporary history and collected a famous gallery of portraits at his villa on Lake Como.

Tyson cites Giovio five times in his essay, drawing on his report of Pygmies in the Far East — a geographical tradition distinct from the better-known African accounts.

## Pygmies Beyond Japan

Giovio's most distinctive contribution was his claim that Pygmies existed in East Asia. Tyson records:

> Paulus Jovius docet Pygmæos ultra Japonem esse.
> (015-060)

This assertion — "Paulus Jovius teaches that there are Pygmies beyond Japan" — placed Pygmies at the farthest known reaches of Asia, far from the African and Indian locations discussed by most other authorities.

## A Northern Account

The work cited by Tyson is Giovio's *De Muscovitarum Legatione*, a treatise on a diplomatic mission to Russia (015-114). This text contained information about East Asian peoples gathered from Russian diplomatic sources.

Tyson records that Giovio was listed by Bartholine alongside Varchius, Johannes Tinnulus, and several other modern authorities who were "of the same mind" regarding the reality of Pygmies (015-110).

## Tyson's Assessment

Tyson approaches Giovio's Far Eastern Pygmies with cautious reserve. He notes that "those Pygmies that Paulus Jovius describes, tho' they dwell at a great distance from Africa, and he calls them Men, yet are so like" other accounts of diminutive peoples (018-084).

Tyson hypothesizes that Giovio may have been "alluding to the Koro-puk-guru" — an indigenous people of northern Japan — drawing on Pomponius Mela's earlier geographical traditions (004-143). This conjecture represents Tyson's attempt to reconcile Giovio's unusual geographical placement with known ethnographic realities.
