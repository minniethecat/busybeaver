# Joseph Ritson

Joseph Ritson (1752–1803) was an English antiquary and compiler whose collections of traditional folklore, particularly *Fairy Tales*, provided Edward Tyson with comparative material for his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*. Ritson's documentation of fairy beliefs in the British Isles served Tyson's broader argument that traditions of diminutive supernatural beings might preserve folk memories of actual pygmy races.

## Ritson in Tyson's Essay

Tyson draws upon Ritson's work at several points in his discussion of fairy lore and its possible connection to historical pygmy populations.

### Fairy Habitations and Tumuli

In his survey of fairy beliefs across Britain, Tyson cites Ritson's testimony regarding the association of fairies with ancient burial mounds in the Bishopric of Durham (005-110):

> Ritson mentions that the "fairies frequented many parts of the Bishopric of Durham." There is a hillock or tumulus near Bishopton, and a large hill near Billingham, both of which used in former time to be "haunted by fairies." (005-110–005-114)

Tyson connects these fairy-haunted mounds to the "hill-inhabiting fairies" tradition found across the British Isles (005-121), noting that Ferry-hill itself derives its name from "Fairy-hill" (005-114–005-115).

### Quotation and Source Material

Ritson's *Fairy Tales* is explicitly credited by Tyson as the source for a quotation used earlier in the essay, with the footnote recording: "The quotation is taken from Ritson, *Fairy Tales*, P. 4" (004 Footnote A). Tyson also references Ritson in connection with the fairy traditions of the Scilly Isles, citing page 62 of Ritson's work (005 Footnote E).

In a discussion of Willey How, a sepulchral barrow in Yorkshire associated with fairy activity, Tyson notes Ritson's citation of Aubrey's *Natural History of Surrey* (007 Footnote C). This chain of scholarship — from Aubrey through Ritson to Tyson — illustrates the antiquarian network upon which Tyson drew for his comparative folklore analysis.

## Significance for Tyson's Argument

Ritson's compilation of fairy lore served Tyson's central thesis: that the widespread European belief in fairies, elves, and "little people" might represent the degraded folk memory of encounters with diminutive races. By documenting the specific locations, place-names, and traditions associated with fairy habitation, Ritson provided empirical grounding for Tyson's speculative anthropology.

Tyson's use of Ritson also reflects the broader eighteenth-century antiquarian interest in folklore as a source of historical evidence — a methodology that anticipated later developments in ethnography and cultural anthropology.
