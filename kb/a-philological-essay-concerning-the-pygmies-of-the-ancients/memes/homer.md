# Homer

## Biography

Homer (c. 8th century BC) is the earliest and most influential literary source on Pygmies in the Western tradition. His two epics, the _Iliad_ and the _Odyssey_, form the cornerstone of ancient Greek literature. Edward Tyson, in his 1699 _Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_, identifies Homer as "the first who made mention of the Pygmies." (010-086)

## Homer's Pygmy Account

The pygmy reference appears in Book III of the _Iliad_, embedded in a simile comparing the Trojan army's advance to the seasonal migration of cranes:

> "So when inclement winters vex the plain
> With piercing frosts, or thick-descending rain,
> To warmer seas the cranes embodied fly,
> With noise and order, through the midway sky;
> To Pigmy nations wounds and death they bring,
> And all the war descends upon the wing."
> (005-001)

This passage established the [[geranomachia]] — the annual war between Pygmies and cranes — as the defining pygmy narrative in classical literature.

## The Geranomachia Tradition

Homer's simile became the seed for an entire mythological tradition. The image of diminutive warriors battling migrating birds captured the ancient imagination. [[aristotle|Aristotle]] later treated the crane-migration as a natural-historical fact, while [[pliny-the-elder|Pliny]] incorporated it into his encyclopedic catalog.

Tyson observed that "with Homer's Pigmies, who were, as will be seen, a totally different race" from later accounts — suggesting that Homer's mythical Pygmies had become confused with real peoples in subsequent retellings. (004-066)

## Tyson's Interpretation

Tyson approached Homer's account with characteristic philological care. He did not dismiss it as pure invention, nor accept it as literal truth. Instead, he proposed that "that which, alluded to by Homer, has become confused" represented a genuine natural phenomenon — possibly apes — filtered through poetic imagination. (004-162)

This reading allowed Tyson to reconcile Homer's authority with empirical natural history. The poet had observed something real; the mythical framing was a product of his art, not deliberate fabrication.

## Works Cited by Tyson

- _Iliad_ (Book III) — the geranomachia simile
- _Odyssey_ — referenced indirectly through the broader epic tradition

## Significance

Homer's pygmy account is significant for several reasons. It is the earliest surviving reference to Pygmies in Greek literature. It established the narrative template that [[herodotus|Herodotus]], [[aristotle|Aristotle]], and [[pliny-the-elder|Pliny]] would later elaborate.

It provided Tyson with the philological anchor for his argument that ancient pygmy accounts had a basis in natural observation.
