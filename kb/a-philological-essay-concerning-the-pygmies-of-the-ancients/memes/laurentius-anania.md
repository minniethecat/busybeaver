Jo. Laurentius Anania (Giovanni Lorenzo Anania, fl. late 16th century) was an Italian geographer whose work on world geography was cited by later scholars as an authority on the location of the ancient Pygmies. Tyson encounters Anania through the Danish scholar Thomas Bartholin, who quotes him alongside numerous other classical and Renaissance authorities.

## The Bartholine Citation

Tyson's direct source for Anania is Bartholin, who lists him among an extensive catalogue of authorities on Pygmies: "Homer, Ctesias, Aristotle, Philostratus, Pliny, Juvenal, Oppian, Baptista Mantuan, St. Austin and his Scholiast. Ludovic. Vives, Jo. Laurentius Anania, Joh. Cassanius, Joh. Talentonius, Gellius, Pomp. Mela, and Olaus Magnus" (014-148). This catalogue demonstrates that Anania was considered part of the standard scholarly apparatus on the Pygmy question in the seventeenth century.

## Anania's Geographical Account

From Bartholin's quotation, Tyson records Anania's placement of the Pygmies: "Joh. Laurentius Anania ex Mercatorum relatione tradit eos (sc. Pygmæos) in Septentrionali Thraciæ Parte reperiri, (quæ Scythiæ est proxima) atque ibi cum Gruibus pugnare" (014-152). According to Anania's report — drawn from merchants' accounts — the Pygmies were located in northern Thrace, close to Scythia, where they fought battles with cranes. This geographic placement differs from the more common African and Indian locations given by most ancient authorities.

## Tyson's Assessment

Tyson treats Anania's evidence with characteristic balance. While including him in the scholarly catalogue, Tyson observes that "tho' Aristotle is here quoted, yet he does not make them Men; So neither does Anania" (015-096). This remark places Anania among those authorities who, like Aristotle, described diminutive beings without necessarily endorsing the full mythological narrative of a separate race of miniature humans. Tyson cites Anania's work from "the end of the first treatise of his Geography" (*prope finem tractatus primi suæ Geograph.*) (014-152, footnote B).
