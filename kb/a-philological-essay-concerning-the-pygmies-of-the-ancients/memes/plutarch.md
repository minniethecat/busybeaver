Plutarch (c. 46–c. 119 AD), a Greek biographer and philosopher from Chaeronea in Boeotia, is one of the most widely read authors of classical antiquity. His *Parallel Lives*, pairing Greek and Roman statesmen, has been a foundational text of Western biography for nearly two millennia.

Tyson cites Plutarch three times in his essay, each time drawing on the biographer's *Life of Sertorius* — a Roman general who campaigned in Spain and whose story involves an unusual animal companion.

## Plutarch on Pygmy Stature

Tyson invokes Plutarch in Section 16 during a discussion of whether the ancient Pygmies were defined primarily by their diminutive size or by other characteristics. He writes:

> "The Pygmies were for their lowness of Stature: For Plutarch tells us, that Serbonius employed them in his Army, and placed them in the Front of the Battle, as Bulwarks to receive the Enemy's Charge."
> (016-200)

This tactical use of short-statured soldiers — positioning them in the front rank not despite but because of their height — serves Tyson's broader argument that the ancients understood "Pygmy" primarily as a term of physical description rather than a fabulous or mythological category.

## The White Hind of Sertorius

Tyson returns to Plutarch immediately afterward in connection with one of the most famous stories from the *Life of Sertorius*:

> "The Story of his white Hind, which Plutarch likewise relates."
> (016-205)

In Plutarch's narrative, the Roman general Quintus Sertorius kept a white hind (female deer) that he claimed was a gift from the goddess Diana and which brought him divine messages. The reference appears in a footnote citing "Plutarch in vita Q. Sertorii" (017-001).

Tyson's use of Plutarch here is indirect — he invokes the biographer not for explicit commentary on Pygmies but for contextual illustration. The Sertorius story serves as a classical parallel to Tyson's larger theme: the way ancient authorities used accounts of unusual beings and animals to support particular claims.

## Plutarch's Authority in the Essay

Plutarch's standing as a careful biographer rather than a writer of marvels made him a more credible source for Tyson than authors like Ctesias or Aelian. By citing Plutarch alongside other respected classical authorities, Tyson built his case that the ancient evidence for Pygmies was rooted in observation rather than pure invention.

## Key Works Cited by Tyson

- **_Life of Sertorius_** (from *Parallel Lives*) — Cited in Sections 16–17 of Tyson's essay, specifically the account of Sertorius's white hind and his use of short-statured soldiers in Spain.
