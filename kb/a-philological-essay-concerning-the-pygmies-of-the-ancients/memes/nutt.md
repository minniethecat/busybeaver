Alfred Trübner Nutt (1856–1910) was a British publisher, folklorist, and Celtic scholar who played a central role in the professionalization of folklore studies in late-Victorian England. As head of the publishing firm David Nutt, he oversaw the publication of numerous important works in folklore, mythology, and Celtic studies, including the journal *Folk-Lore*. His own scholarly work focused on the Celtic origins of the Grail legend and the methodology of comparative folklore.

## The Distorted-Recollection Theory

Nutt's most significant contribution to the essay is his formulation of the theory that fairy traditions represent "distorted recollections of alien and inimical races" (009-076). This thesis, which Tyson quotes directly with approval, posits that the "little people" of European folklore are not wholly imaginary beings but rather folk memories of encounters between invading populations and displaced indigenous peoples — whose small stature and subterranean dwellings became exaggerated and mythologized over generations.

The theory occupies an intermediate position between MacRitchie's more literal identification of fairies with specific prehistoric races and the purely mythological interpretation. Tyson invokes Nutt's authority in Section 8, where he states that "nor is it necessary, as they have been fully dealt with by Mr. Nutt, whose opinion on this point is worthy of all attention" (008-095). This remark, appearing in the context of a discussion about fairy origins, signals the essay's substantial reliance on Nutt's scholarly framework.

## Scholarly Standing

Nutt's standing in the essay extends beyond his own theory. As the publisher of the Folk-Lore Society's journal and a respected figure in the scholarly community, his citation by Tyson carries institutional weight. The essay positions Nutt alongside Hartland and Gomme as part of the network of British folklorists whose work defined the late-nineteenth-century discourse on tradition and race. A footnote in Section 3 records "London, Nutt, 1891" (003-122), marking the publication details of a scholarly work, further indicating Nutt's centrality to the essay's bibliographic world.

## Relation to Other Theorists

Nutt's distorted-recollection model complements and partially critiques MacRitchie's mound-dweller theory. While MacRitchie emphasized the archaeological evidence for actual small-statured races, Nutt focused on the psychological and social processes by which historical encounters are transformed into supernatural narratives. Tyson deploys both theorists eclectically, drawing on Nutt's framework when addressing the symbolic dimension of fairy beliefs and on MacRitchie's when examining the material evidence.
