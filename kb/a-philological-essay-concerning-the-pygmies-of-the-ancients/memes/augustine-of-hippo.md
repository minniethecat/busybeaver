# Augustine of Hippo

## Biography

Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD) was a Roman Theologian, Philosopher and Bishop of Hippo Regius in North Africa. One of the most influential figures in Western Christianity, his works — particularly _De Civitate Dei_ (The City of God) — shaped medieval theology and philosophy for centuries. His discussion of monstrous races in _De Civitate Dei_ Book 16 became an important reference point in early modern debates about the existence of pygmies as a distinct race of men.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

In Edward Tyson's _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699), Augustine appears as an authority whom several earlier writers — including [[aldrovandus]] and [[bartholine]] — had claimed as supporting the existence of pygmies as a race of men (016-035). Tyson firmly rejects this reading of Augustine.

Tyson shows that Augustine, far from endorsing the human status of pygmies, posed the skeptical question _An ex propagine Adam vel filiorum Noe, quædam genera Hominum Monstrosa prodierunt?_ — whether monstrous races of men could have descended from Adam or Noah's sons (016-037). Augustine lists the Pygmies, Sciopodes, and Cynocephali among the peoples described by "Indian Historians," but refuses to accept their testimony uncritically.

Tyson quotes Augustine's decisive conclusion at length:

> "Nam & Simias & Cercopithecos & Sphingas, si nesciremus non Homines esse, sed Bestias, possent isti Historici de sua Curiositate gloriantes velut Gentes Aliquas Hominum nobis impunitâ vanitate mentiri."
> (016-046)

Tyson interprets this as Augustine arguing that if we did not already know that apes, cercopitheci, and sphinxes are beasts and not men, historians might with impunity deceive us by presenting them as races of men. Tyson sees Augustine as an ally in his own argument that pygmies were apes, not humans (016-053).

In the final section of the essay, Tyson returns to Augustine to clinch his case: Augustine doubts whether any such creatures exist, and if they do, "concludes them to be _Apes_ or _Monkeys_," censuring those Indian historians for imposing on their readers (017-147).
