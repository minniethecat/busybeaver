## Biography

John Milne (1850–1913) was a British geologist, seismologist, and ethnologist
who spent much of his career in Japan. While best known as a pioneer of
seismology, Milne also made significant contributions to the study of the
Ainu people and the prehistory of the Japanese archipelago. His linguistic
and ethnographic work on the indigenous populations of northern Japan brought
European scholarly attention to the legends of the Koro-puk-guru, a
diminutive race said to have preceded the Ainu in the Japanese islands.

Milne's fieldwork in Hokkaido and his engagement with Ainu oral traditions
placed him among the first Western scholars to systematically document the
Koro-puk-guru legends, which held particular interest for anthropologists
investigating the global distribution of pygmy folklore.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tyson cites Milne's interpretation of the term "Koro-puk-guru" in Section 2
of the essay, within a comprehensive survey of pygmy-like peoples and legends
across Asia. The Koro-puk-guru — literally "men occupying excavations" or
"pit-dwellers" according to Milne's etymology — are legendary little people
spoken of by the Ainu of northern Japan (002-075).

Tyson notes an alternative etymology offered by the scholar Basil Hall
Chamberlain, who interpreted the name as "dwellers under burdocks,"
associating it with a legend in which the diminutive Koro-puk-guru would
hide under the great leaves of the burdock plant (_koro_) when the Ainu
approached (002-076–002-081). According to Ainu tradition, the Koro-puk-guru
were eventually exterminated by the Ainu with wooden clubs, at which they
raised their eyes to heaven and wept aloud (002-082–002-083).

Milne's "pit-dwellers" interpretation frames the Koro-puk-guru as a
subterranean-dwelling race, which links them typologically to other
underground-dwelling pygmy legends from European folklore. This
comparative dimension strengthens Tyson's argument that pygmy legends
represent a global phenomenon with recurring motifs.
