Isaac Casaubon (1559–1614) was a French-Swiss classical scholar, humanist, and one of the most learned men of his age. Renowned for his editions of Greek and Latin texts, including works by Aristotle, Athenaeus, and Strabo, Casaubon was a central figure in the late Renaissance republic of letters and a rigorous critic of textual and historical claims.

Tyson cites Casaubon in his essay as a representative of the learned skeptical tradition — scholars who, on the basis of philological and rational inquiry, denied the existence of the Pygmies altogether.

## Casaubon's Denial of the Pygmies

In Section 13, Tyson surveys the opinions of scholars who rejected the Pygmies as a historical reality. Casaubon features prominently among them. Following the testimony of Julius Caesar Scaliger, who called the Pygmies mere fictions of the ancients, Tyson introduces Casaubon's withering assessment:

> "And _Isaac Casaubon_ ridicules such as pretend to justifie them: _Sic nostra ætate_ (saith he) _non desunt, qui eandem de Pygmæis lepidam fabellam renovent; ut qui etiam è Sacris Literis, si Deo placet, fidem illis conentur astruere. Legi etiam Bergei cujusdam Galli Scripta, qui se vidisse diceret. At non ego credulus illi, illi inquam Omnium Bipedum mendacissimo._"
> (013-017)

Casaubon's rhetoric is sharp and dismissive. He notes with disdain that even in his own time there were those who attempted to revive "that charming little fable" of the Pygmies — some even appealing to Sacred Scripture to lend it credibility. He further recounts reading the writings of a certain Frenchman (Bergeus) who claimed to have seen Pygmies himself, but Casaubon declares himself unwilling to believe "the most mendacious of all two-legged creatures."

## Casaubon and the Editions of Aristotle

In the same section, Tyson expresses surprise that Casaubon, despite his scholarly diligence, failed to notice certain textual problems in the transmission of Aristotle's zoological works:

> "But I wonder that neither _Casaubon_ nor _Duvall_ in their Editions of _Aristotle_'s Works, should have taken notice of these Mistakes of _Gaza_."
> (013-105)

This passage reveals Tyson's own philological engagement with the classical sources. While respecting Casaubon's authority as a textual critic, Tyson identifies what he considers an oversight in Casaubon's editorial work — the failure to note translation errors by Theodore Gaza that, in Tyson's view, distorted Aristotle's account of the Pygmies and contributed to centuries of confusion.

## Casaubon Among the Total Deniers

In the final section of the essay, Tyson returns to Casaubon as part of the tradition of total denial. He groups Casaubon with Julius Caesar Scaliger and Adrian Spigelius as scholars who rejected the Pygmies outright:

> "_Julius Cæsar Scaliger_, and _Isaac Casaubon_, and _Adrian Spigelius_ utterly deny the Being of _Pygmies_, and look upon them as a Figment only of the Ancients."
> (017-152)

These three figures — Scaliger the philosopher-physician, Casaubon the classical scholar, and Spigelius the anatomist — represented different disciplinary approaches that converged on the same skeptical conclusion. That such learned authorities could deny the Pygmies' existence entirely testifies to the difficulty of the question and the ambiguity of the ancient evidence.

## Significance in Tyson's Essay

Casaubon serves as Tyson's most formidable skeptical opponent. Unlike some other authorities Tyson dismisses, Casaubon's scholarly credentials were unimpeachable, and his arguments required serious engagement rather than mere refutation. Tyson's response to Casaubon is characteristically balanced: he acknowledges the force of Casaubon's skepticism while pointing to gaps in his philological analysis (particularly regarding Aristotle's text) that may have led him to an overly dismissive conclusion. The careful navigation of Casaubon's authority exemplifies Tyson's broader method — honoring the weight of learned opinion while reserving the right to question its conclusions through fresh engagement with the sources.
