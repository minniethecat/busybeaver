Paul Topinard (1830–1911) was a French anthropologist and physician who served as secretary-general of the Société d'Anthropologie de Paris and succeeded Paul Broca as the leading figure in French physical anthropology. His major work, *Éléments d'anthropologie générale* (1885), was a standard textbook of anthropological method, covering craniometry, anthropometry, and racial classification.

Like Hartland, Topinard is personally acknowledged in the essay's prefatory note for "their kindness in supplying" materials to the author (P03-013), indicating his direct contribution of anthropometric data or publications to the project.

## Anthropometric Authority

Topinard's primary function in the essay is to supply authoritative data on human physical variation, particularly stature. In Section 1, "According to Topinard, the races of the" world exhibit measurable differences in average height that provide a scientific framework for evaluating historical reports of pygmy-sized peoples (001-090). This invocation establishes that the question of diminutive human races is not merely legendary but falls within the scope of physical anthropology.

In Section 3, Tyson returns to Topinard as "Professor Topinard, whose authority upon this point" is undisputed, to buttress a specific argument about the physical plausibility of small-statured populations (003-152). Section 4 further draws on anthropometric data "quoted by Topinard," citing the extremes of human stature variation that Topinard's compilations had documented (004-028).

## Role in the Essay's Argument

Topinard represents the scientific dimension of the essay's evidentiary strategy. Where MacRitchie supplies the folkloric-archaeological theory, Hartland the comparative folklore data, and Tylor the cultural-anthropological framework, Topinard provides the physical-anthropological baseline: measurable, published data on human stature variation against which the ancient pygmy accounts can be assessed. His presence in the essay reflects the interdisciplinary character of late-Victorian anthropological inquiry, which saw no firm boundary between folklore studies and physical anthropology.

## Key Work Cited

- **_Éléments d'anthropologie générale_ (1885)** — Topinard's comprehensive treatise on anthropological method, cited in the essay as an authoritative source for anthropometric data on human stature variation across populations.
