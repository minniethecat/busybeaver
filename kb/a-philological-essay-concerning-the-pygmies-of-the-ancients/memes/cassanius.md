# J. Cassanius

J. Cassanius (Johannes Cassanius) was an early modern scholar, probably Italian, who authored a treatise on giants, *De Gigantibus* (On Giants), during the seventeenth century. Though little is known of his biography, Cassanius occupies a notable position in the scholarly lineage traced by Edward Tyson in his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*, as one of the few early modern authors to have written systematically about diminutive human races.

## Cassanius in the Scholarly Tradition

Tyson encounters Cassanius through the work of [[caspar-bartholine|Caspar Bartholine]], the Danish physician who compiled a treatise on pygmies (*Opusculum de Pygmæis*). Bartholine explains his motivation for writing on pygmies by reference to Cassanius's prior work on the opposite subject:

> Old Caspar Bartholine tells us, that because J. Cassanius and others had wrote de Gygantibus, since no Body else had undertaken it, he would give us a Book de Pygmæis. (014-129–014-133)

This passage reveals the intellectual symmetry that early modern scholars perceived between giants and pygmies — the two extremes of human stature — and explains why Cassanius's work on giants served as a catalyst for Bartholine's complementary study.

## The Question of Pygmy Reality

In Bartholine's survey of authorities on the existence of pygmies, Cassanius appears in a catalogue of writers who treated the subject. The list includes Homer, [[ctesias|Ctesias]], [[aristotle|Aristotle]], [[philostratus|Philostratus]], [[pliny-the-elder|Pliny]], [[juvenal|Juvenal]], and numerous other ancient and early modern authorities (014-147–014-150). Cassanius's inclusion among such a distinguished intellectual lineage testifies to his reputation in seventeenth-century learned circles.

However, Bartholine quotes Cassanius as expressing skepticism about the pygmy tradition:

> And Joh. Cassanius (as he is here quoted) saith, De Pygmæis fabulosa quidem esse omnia, quæ de iis narrari solent. (014-155–014-156)

This Latin passage — "Concerning the Pygmies, all things that are commonly related about them are indeed fabulous" — positions Cassanius among the skeptics who regarded pygmy accounts as mythological rather than historical. His treatise *De Gigantibus* (cited in Tyson's footnotes as *Joh. Cassanius libello de Gygantibus*, p. 73) (015 Footnote C) thus represents the skeptical pole of early modern pygmy-giant scholarship.

## Significance

Cassanius exemplifies the type of early modern polymath whose work bridged classical erudition and emerging critical methodology. His willingness to challenge received tradition — labelling the pygmy narratives as *fabulosa* — anticipated the more systematic skepticism of later figures like Tyson and [[buffon|Buffon]]. Yet, like those later skeptics, his dismissal would ultimately be overturned by the empirical discoveries of nineteenth-century explorers in [[africa|Africa]] and [[asia|Asia]].
