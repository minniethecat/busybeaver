Professor Sir William Boyd Dawkins (1837–1929) was a British geologist and archaeologist whose work on prehistoric Britain and the antiquity of human occupation made him one of the most influential figures in late Victorian archaeology. His report on the excavation of a Welsh barrow enters Tyson's essay as a striking archaeological illustration of how prehistoric burial monuments became entangled with fairy and ghost traditions — a process central to the MacRitchie hypothesis.

## The Bryn-yr-Ellyllon Barrow

Tyson cites Boyd Dawkins for a single but significant archaeological case: "A barrow called Bryn-yr-Ellyllon (Fairy-hill), near Mold, was said to be haunted by a ghost clad in golden armour which had been seen to enter it" (005-161). The Welsh name of the site — literally "Hill of the Fairies" or "Goblin Hill" — already encoded the folkloric association between the prehistoric monument and supernatural beings. The local tradition held that a spectral figure in golden armour haunted the mound.

The barrow was excavated in 1832, and the findings proved remarkable. Boyd Dawkins reported that the excavation uncovered a Bronze Age burial containing what came to be known as the Mold Gold Cape — a spectacular ceremonial gold garment that provided a material basis for the "golden armour" of local legend. The discovery that a real prehistoric burial lay beneath the fairy-associated mound exemplified the process by which folk memory of ancient burial sites could transform into traditions of supernatural beings.

## Relevance to the MacRitchie Hypothesis

Boyd Dawkins' evidence enters Tyson's essay at a critical juncture in the discussion of MacRitchie's theory that European fairy traditions preserve memories of an aboriginal dwarf race whose burial mounds dot the landscape. Tyson cites the Bryn-yr-Ellyllon case to demonstrate that "this fact is still more clearly shown" — namely, the reality that "no corporeal tenant had ever occupied" what appeared to be a dwelling-place, and that the site was in fact a burial monument rather than a habitation (005-158).

This interpretation cuts against MacRitchie's dwelling-place theory. Where MacRitchie saw prehistoric houses, Boyd Dawkins' archaeology revealed tombs. The fairy connections of such sites, from Tyson's perspective, derived not from memories of living dwarf inhabitants but from the universal human tendency to associate ancient burial places with supernatural presences.

## Archaeological Significance

Beyond his role in the MacRitchie debate, Boyd Dawkins was a towering figure in British prehistoric archaeology. His works *Cave Hunting* (1874) and *Early Man in Britain* (1880) established the deep antiquity of human presence in the British Isles. His geological expertise informed his archaeological interpretations, and his professional standing lent weight to the Bryn-yr-Ellyllon evidence as cited by Tyson.
