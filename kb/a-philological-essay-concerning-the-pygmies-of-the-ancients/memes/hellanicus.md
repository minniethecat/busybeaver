# Hellanicus of Lesbos

Hellanicus of Lesbos (c. 490–405 BC) was one of the early Greek logographers — the forerunners of Herodotus and Thucydides — who sought to record the genealogies, ethnographies, and histories of the known world. In his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*, [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] quotes the geographer [[strabo|Strabo]] to place Hellanicus among the ancient authors whose accounts of India were considered fabulous — a judgment that bears directly on the reliability of classical pygmy testimony.

## Strabo's Censure

Tyson invokes Hellanicus in Section 11 of his essay, where he examines the credibility of ancient writers on India, the traditional homeland of the pygmies. Quoting Strabo, Tyson reports the geographer's harsh judgment:

> That one may sooner believe Hesiod, and Homer, and the Tragick Poets speaking of their Hero's, than Ctesias and Herodotus and Hellanicus and such like. (011-066–011-078)

Strabo's comparison — preferring the mythical testimony of epic poets over that of historians like Hellanicus — is a devastating indictment of early Greek prose writing on Eastern subjects. The geographer classed Hellanicus among those who wrote of India with more imagination than accuracy.

## Theopompus's Boast

Tyson deepens this portrait by quoting a second passage from Strabo, in which the historian Theopompus claims superiority in the art of inserting fables into historical narrative:

> That he could foist in Fables into History, better than Herodotus and Ctesias and Hellanicus, and all that have wrote of India. (011-079–011-090)

The passage reveals that among Greek intellectuals of the fourth century BC, Hellanicus was already regarded as a byword for the fabulous in ethnography — a reputation that would colour later assessments of classical testimony concerning [[pygmies|Pygmies]] and other marvels of the East.

## Hellanicus and the Pygmy Question

Although Tyson does not quote Hellanicus directly on the subject of pygmies, his inclusion in the catalogue of discredited Indian historians is significant for the broader argument of the essay. If writers like [[ctesias|Ctesias]], Hellanicus, and [[megasthenes|Megasthenes]] were unreliable on Indian matters generally, then their testimony on pygmy races — however vivid or detailed — must be weighed with scepticism. Tyson's philological method requires precisely this discrimination between credible ethnographic observation and the fabulous tradition of Graeco-Roman India-writing.
