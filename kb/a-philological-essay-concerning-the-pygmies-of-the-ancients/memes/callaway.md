# Henry Callaway

Henry Callaway (1817–1890) was an English Anglican missionary who served in South Africa and became the first Bishop of St. John's, Kaffraria. His pioneering ethnographic studies of Zulu religious beliefs, particularly his documentation of ancestor worship and the Zulu conception of the subterranean spirit world, were cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] in Section 9 of his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients* as comparative evidence for the widespread folkloric motif of an underground realm inhabited by diminutive spirit-beings.

## Ancestor Worship and the Underground World

In Section 9, which examines the connection between pygmy legends and beliefs about an underworld inhabited by the dead, Tyson draws upon Callaway's authoritative account of Zulu ancestor religion:

> Dr. Callaway has already drawn attention to this point in connection with the ancestor-worship of the Amazulu. He says, "It may be worth while to note the curious coincidence of thought among the Amazulu regarding the Amatongo or Abapansi, and that of the..." (009-086)

The Amatongo (ancestral spirits) and Abapansi (literally "those below") of Zulu belief inhabit a subterranean realm that closely parallels the underground dwellings attributed to pygmies and dwarf-spirits in the folklore of [[europe|Europe]], [[asia|Asia]], and the Pacific. Callaway's observation of this "curious coincidence of thought" provided Tyson with evidence that the association between diminutive beings and the underground world was not a culturally specific motif but a near-universal feature of human religious imagination.

## The Comparative Value of Callaway's Zulu Material

Callaway's work occupies a distinctive position in Tyson's comparative survey. While most of the ethnographic testimonies assembled in the essay come from Asia and Europe, Callaway's Zulu data extends the geographic range of the underground-dwarf motif to sub-Saharan [[africa|Africa]]. This African instance is particularly significant because it emerges from a religious system entirely independent of the Indo-European and East Asian traditions that furnish the bulk of Tyson's evidence. The convergence between Zulu beliefs about the Abapansi and European fairy-lore about subterranean dwarfs thus strengthens Tyson's argument that these parallel traditions reflect a shared human experience of actual diminutive populations, rather than cultural diffusion.

## Legacy

Callaway's *Religious System of the Amazulu* (1870) remains a foundational text in the study of African traditional religion. His careful transcription of Zulu informants' own accounts, with facing Zulu-English text, set a methodological standard for missionary ethnography that influenced later fieldworkers. While Tyson's use of Callaway focuses narrowly on the underground-dwarf motif, Callaway's broader documentation of Zulu cosmology, ritual, and oral narrative constitutes one of the most important nineteenth-century records of southern African religious thought.
