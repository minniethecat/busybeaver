Isaac Vossius (1618–1689) was a Dutch classical scholar and geographer, son of the celebrated humanist Gerardus Joannes Vossius. His work *De Nili aliorumque stuminum Origine* (*On the Origin of the Nile and Other Rivers*) is one of the principal modern scholarly sources that Tyson engages with in his essay. Vossius serves as one of Tyson's most important interlocutors — a learned contemporary whose skepticism about the ancient Pygmy tradition Tyson both respects and systematically refutes.

## Vossius's Skeptical Position

Tyson summarizes Vossius's fundamental challenge: "Isaac Vossius seems to unsettle all, and endeavours utterly to ruine the whole Story: for he tells us, If you travel all over Africa, you shall not meet with either a Crane or Pygmie" (016-129). Vossius expressed astonishment that Aristotle treated the Pygmy-crane war as factual rather than fabulous: "*Se mirari Aristotelem, quod tam seriò affirmet non esse fabellam, quæ de Pygmæis & Bello, quod cum Gruibus gerant, narrantur*" (016-132).

Tyson's response is sharp. He points out that Vossius attributed to Aristotle a claim the philosopher never made: "one would wonder more at Vossius, that he should assert this of Aristotle, which he never said" (016-135). This factual error, Tyson argues, undermines Vossius's credibility: "since Vossius is so mistaken in what he relates of Aristotle; where he might so easily have been in the right, 'tis not unlikely but he may be more out in what he alledges of Africa" (016-136).

## Vossius's Concession and the Nani

Despite his initial rejection of the Pygmy tradition, Tyson records that Vossius "upon second Thoughts did admit of Pygmies" (016-154). Drawing on Dapper's account of Africa, Vossius identified a group he called *Nani* (dwarfs) who killed elephants. Tyson quotes Vossius's own words: "*Attamen ut solent fabellæ non de nihilo fingi & aliquod plerunque continent veri, id ipsum quoque hic obtinet*" — "Yet as fables are not usually invented from nothing and generally contain some truth, the same holds here" (016-162).

However, Tyson rejects Vossius's identification of these *Nani* with the Pygmies of the Ancients. While Ludolphus criticized Vossius "for calling them Nani" (016-178), Tyson is more generous, suggesting "he may only mean a sort of Men in that Country of less Stature than ordinary" (016-178). But he concludes: "I see no reason why Vossius should take these Men for the Pygmies of the Ancients, or think that they gave any occasion or rise to that Story" (016-182).

## The Elephant-Hunting Pygmies

Tyson also discusses Vossius's account of Pygmies hunting elephants. He notes with dry humor that Vossius's Pygmies "made great Slaughters of the Elephants" (017-040), contrasting this improbable claim with Ludolphus's alternative vision in which the birds sweep away the Pygmies effortlessly. The juxtaposition highlights what Tyson sees as the fundamental problem with Vossius's approach: he wavers inconsistently between skepticism and credulity, rejecting the classical tradition only to embrace an equally dubious modern substitute.

## Vossius's Conjecture on the Nubæ

A further dimension of Vossius's argument is his identification of the Pygmies with the Nubæ (or Nobæ), whom the geographer Dapper called the *Mimos* — a people reported "beyond the Fountains of the Nile" (017-158). Vossius "conjectures that it was those Dwarfs" who gave rise to the Pygmy legend. This conjecture forms part of the broader early modern effort to locate a real-world referent for the ancient Pygmy tradition — an effort Tyson subjects to rigorous philological scrutiny.
