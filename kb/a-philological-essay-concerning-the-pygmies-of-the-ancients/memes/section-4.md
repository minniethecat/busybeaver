# Section 4

[Footnote A: _Jour. Ethn. Soc._, 1869-70, p. 455.]

 
[Footnote B: Since these pages were printed, Prof. Kollmann, of Basle, has

[004-001]described a group of Neolithic pigmies as having existed at Schaffhausen.

[004-002]The adult interments consisted of the remains of full-grown European types

[004-003]and of small-sized people. These two races were found interred side by

[004-004]side under precisely similar conditions, from which he concludes that they

[004-005]lived peaceably together, notwithstanding racial difference. Their stature

[004-006](about three feet six inches) may be compared with that of the Veddahs in

[004-007]Ceylon. Prof. Kollmann believes that they were a distinct species of

[004-008]mankind.]

[004-009]Dr. Rahon,[A] who has recently made a careful study of the bones of

[004-010]pre-historic and proto-historic races, with special reference to their

[004-011]stature, states that the skeletons attributed to the most ancient and to

[004-012]the Neolithic races are of a stature below the middle height, the average

[004-013]being a little over five feet three inches. The peoples who constructed

[004-014]the Megalithic remains of Roknia and of the [[caucasus|Caucasus]], were of a stature

[004-015]similar to our own. The diverse proto-historic populations, Gauls, Franks,

[004-016]Burgundians, and Merovingians, considered together, present a stature

[004-017]slightly superior to that of the French of the present day, but not so

[004-018]much so as the accounts of the historians would have led us to believe.

 
[Footnote A: _Recherches sur les Ossements Humaines, Anciens et

[004-019]Préhistonques. Mém. de la Soc. d'Anthrop. de Paris_, Sér, ii. tom. iv.

[004-020]403.]

[004-021]It remains now to deal with two races whose physical characters are of

[004-022]considerable importance in connection with certain points which will be

[004-023]dealt with in subsequent pages, I mean the Lapps and the Innuit or Eskimo.

[004-024]The Lapps, according to Karonzine,[A] one of their most recent describers,

[004-025]are divisible into two groups, Scandinavian and Russian, the former being

[004-026]purer than the latter race. The average male stature is five feet, a

[004-027]figure which corresponds closely with that obtained by Mantegazza and

[004-028]quoted by Topinard. The extremes obtained by this observer amongst men

[004-029]were, on the one hand, five feet eight inches, and on the other four feet

[004-030]four inches. As, however, in a matter of this kind we have to deal with

[004-031]averages and not with extremes, we must conclude that the Lapps, though a

[004-032]stunted race, are not pigmies, in the sense in which the word is

[004-033]scientifically employed.

 
[Footnote A: _L'Anthropologie_, ii. 80.]

[004-034]The Innuit or Eskimo were called by the original Norse explorers

[004-035]"Skraelingjar," or dwarfs, a name now converted by the Innuit into

[004-036]"karalit," which is the nearest approach that they are able to make

[004-037]phonetically to the former term. They are certainly, on the average, a

[004-038]people of less than middle stature, yet they can in no sense be described

[004-039]as Pigmies. Their mean height is five feet three inches. Nansen[A] says of

[004-040]them, "It is a common error amongst us in [[europe|Europe]] to think of the Eskimo as

[004-041]a diminutive race. Though no doubt smaller than the Scandinavian peoples,

[004-042]they must be reckoned amongst the middle-sized races, and I even found

[004-043]amongst those of purest breeding men of nearly six feet in height."

 
[Footnote A: _Eskimo Life_, p. 20.]

[004-044]II.

[004-045]The _raison d'être_ of [[edward-tyson|Tyson]]'s essay was to explain away the accounts of

[004-046]the older writers relating to Pigmy races, on the ground that, as no such

[004-047]races existed, an explanation of some kind was necessary in order to

[004-048]account for so many and such detailed descriptions as were to be found in

[004-049]their works. Having now seen not merely that there are such things as

[004-050]Pigmy races, but that they have a wide distribution throughout the world,

[004-051]it may be well to consider to which of the existing or extinct races, the

[004-052]above-mentioned accounts may be supposed to have referred. In this task I

[004-053]am much aided in several instances by the labours of De Quatrefages, and

[004-054]as his book is easily accessible, it will be unnecessary for me to repeat

[004-055]the arguments in favour of his decisions which he has there given.

[004-056]Starting with [[asia|Asia]], we have in the first place the statement of Pliny,

[004-057]that "immediately after the nation of the Prusians, in the mountains where

[004-058]it is said are pigmies, is found the Indus." These Pigmies may be

[004-059]identified with the Brahouis, now Dravidian, but still possessing the

[004-060]habit, attributed to them by Pliny, of changing their dwellings twice a

[004-061]year, in summer and winter, migrations rendered necessary by the search

[004-062]for food for their flocks. The same author's allusion to the "Spithamæi

[004-063][[pygmies|Pygmæi]]" of the mountains in the neighbourhood of the Ganges may apply to

[004-064]the Santals or some allied tribe, though Pliny's stature for them of two

[004-065]feet four inches is exaggeratedly diminutive, and he has confused them

[004-066]with [[homer|Homer]]'s Pigmies, who were, as will be seen, a totally different

[004-067]people.

[004-068]Ctesias[A] tells us that "Middle [[india|India]] has black men, who are called

[004-069]Pygmies, using the same language as the other Indians; they are, however,

[004-070]very little; that the greatest do not exceed the height of two cubits, and

[004-071]the most part only of one cubit and a half. But they nourish the longest

[004-072]hair, hanging down unto the knees, and even below; moreover, they carry a

[004-073]beard more at length than any other men; but, what is more, after this

[004-074]promised beard is risen to them, they never after use any clothing, but

[004-075]send down, truly, the hairs from the back much below the knees, but draw

[004-076]the beard before down to the feet; afterward, when they have covered the

[004-077]whole body with hairs, they bind themselves, using those in the place of a

[004-078]vestment. They are, moreover, apes and deformed. Of these Pygmies, the

[004-079]king of the Indians has three thousand in his train; for they are very

[004-080]skilful archers." No doubt the actual stature has been much diminished in

[004-081]this account, and, as De Quatrefages suggests, the garment of long

[004-082]floating grasses which they may well have worn, may have been mistaken for

[004-083]hair; yet, in the description, he believes that he is able to recognise

[004-084]the ancestors of the Bandra-Lokh of the Vindhya Mountains. Ctesias' other

[004-085]statement, that "the king of India sends every fifth year fifty thousand

[004-086]swords, besides abundance of other weapons, to the nation of the

[004-087]Cynocephali," may refer to the same or some other tribe.

 
[Footnote A: The quotation is taken from Ritson, _Fairy Tales_, P. 4.]

[004-088]De Quatrefages also thinks that an allusion to the ancestors of the Jats,

[004-089]who would then have been less altered by crossing than now, may be found

[004-090]in [[herodotus|Herodotus]]' account of the army of Xerxes when he says, "The Eastern

[004-091]Ethiopians serve with the Indians. They resemble the other Ethiopians,

[004-092]from whom they only differ in language and hair. The Eastern Ethiopians

[004-093]have straight hair, while those of Lybia are more woolly than all other

[004-094]men."

[004-095]Writing of isles in the neighbourhood of Java, Maundeville says,[A] "In

[004-096]another yle, ther ben litylle folk, as dwerghes; and thei ben to so meche

[004-097]as the Pygmeyes, and thei han no mouthe, but in stede of hire mouthe, thei

[004-098]han a lytylle round hole; and whan thei schulle eten or drynken, thei

[004-099]taken thorghe a pipe or a penne or suche a thing, and sowken it in, for

[004-100]thei han no tongue, and therefore thei speke not, but thei maken a maner

[004-101]of hissynge, as a Neddre dothe, and thei maken signes on to another, as

[004-102]monkes don, be the whiche every of hem undirstondethe the other."

 
[Footnote A: Ed. Halliwell, p. 205.]

[004-103]Strip this statement of the characteristic Maundevillian touches with

[004-104]regard to the mouth and tongue, and it may refer to some of the insular

[004-105]races which exist or existed in the district of which he is treating.

[004-106]A much fuller account[A] by the same author relates to Pigmies in the

[004-107]neighbourhood of a river, stated by a commentator[B] to be the

[004-108]Yangtze-Kiang, "a gret ryvere, that men clepen Dalay, and that is the

[004-109]grettest ryvere of fressche water that is in the world. For there, as it

[004-110]is most narow, it is more than 4 myle of brede. And thanne entren men azen

[004-111]in to the lond of the great Chane. That ryvere gothe thorge the lond of

[004-112]Pigmaus, where that the folk ben of litylle stature, that ben but 3 span

[004-113]long, and thei ben right faire and gentylle, aftre here quantytees, bothe

[004-114]the men and the women. And thei maryen hem, whan thei ben half zere of age

[004-115]and getten children. And thei lyven not, but 6 zeer or 7 at the moste. And

[004-116]he that lyveth 8 zeer, men holden him there righte passynge old. Theise

[004-117]men ben the beste worcheres of gold, sylver, cotoun, sylk, and of alle

[004-118]such thinges, of ony other, that be in the world. And thei han often tymes

[004-119]werre with the briddes of the contree, that thei taken and eten. This

[004-120]litylle folk nouther labouren in londes ne in vynes. But thei han grete

[004-121]men amonges hem, of oure stature, that tylen the lond, and labouren

[004-122]amonges the vynes for hem. And of the men of oure stature, han thei als

[004-123]grete skorne and wondre, as we wolde have among us of Geauntes, zif thei

[004-124]weren amonges us. There is a gode cytee, amonges othere, where there is

[004-125]duellynge gret plentee of the lytylle folk, and is a gret cytee and a

[004-126]fair, and the men ben grete that duellen amonges hem; but whan thei getten

[004-127]ony children, thei ben als litylle as the Pygmeyes, and therefore thei ben

[004-128]alle, for the moste part, alle Pygmeyes, for the nature of the land is

[004-129]suche. The great Cane let kepe this cytee fulle wel, for it is his. And

[004-130]alle be it, that the Pygmeyes ben litylle, zit thei ben fulle resonable,

[004-131]aftre here age and connen bothen wytt and gode and malice now." This

[004-132]passage, as will be noted, incorporates the Homeric tale of the battles

[004-133]between the Pigmies and the Cranes, and is adorned with a representation

[004-134]of such an encounter. Whether Maundeville's dwarfs were the same as the

[004-135]Siao-Jin of the Shan-hai-King is a question difficult to decide; but, in

[004-136]any case, both these pigmy races of legend inhabited a part of what is now

[004-137]the Chinese Empire. The same Pigmies seem to be alluded to in the rubric

[004-138]of the Catalan map of the world in the National Library of Paris, the date

[004-139]of which is A.D. 1375. "Here (N.W. of Catayo-Cathay) grow little men who

[004-140]are but five palms in height, and though they be little, and not fit for

[004-141]weighty matters, yet they be brave and clever at weaving and keeping

[004-142]cattle." If such an explanation may be hazarded, we may perhaps go further

[004-143]and suppose that Paulus Jovius may have been alluding to the

[004-144]Koro-puk-guru, when, as [[pomponius-mela|Pomponius Mela]] tells us, he taught that there were

[004-145]Pigmies beyond Japan. In both these cases, however, it is well to remember

[004-146]that there is a river in Macedon as well as in Monmouth, and that it is

[004-147]hazardous to come to too definite a belief as to the exact location of the

[004-148]Pigmies of ancient writers.

 
[Footnote A: _Maundeville_, p. 211.]

 
[Footnote B: _Quart. Rev._, 172, p. 431.]

[004-149]The continent of Africa yielded its share of Pigmies to the same writers.

[004-150]The most celebrated of all are those alluded to by [[aristotle|Aristotle]] in his

[004-151]classical passage, "They (the Cranes) come out of [[scythia|Scythia]] to the Lakes

[004-152]above [[egypt|Egypt]] whence the Nile flows. This is the place whereabouts the

[004-153]Pigmies dwell. For this is no fable but a truth. Both they and the horses,

[004-154]as 'tis said, are of a small kind. They are [[troglodytes|Troglodytes]] and live in

[004-155]caves."

[004-156]Leaving aside the crane part of the tale, which it has been suggested may

[004-157]really have referred to ostriches, Aristotle's Pigmy race may, from their

[004-158]situation, be fairly identified with the Akkas described by Stanley and

[004-159]others. That this race is an exceedingly ancient one is proved by the fact

[004-160]that Marriette Bey has discovered on a tomb of the ancient Empire of Egypt

[004-161]a figure of a dwarf with the name Akka inscribed by it. This race is also

[004-162]supposed to have been that which, alluded to by Homer, has become confused

[004-163]with other dwarf tribes in different parts of the world.
