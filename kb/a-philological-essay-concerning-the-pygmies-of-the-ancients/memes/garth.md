Sir Samuel Garth was an English physician and poet, a contemporary of
Edward Tyson and a fellow of the Royal College of Physicians. He is
mentioned in the biographical preface to Tyson's _Philological Essay
Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ in connection with his
best-known literary work, _The Dispensary_ (1699)—a satirical poem
about the rivalry between physicians and apothecaries.

## Connection to Tyson's Circle

The reference to Garth appears in a biographical note describing the
original of "Carus"—a character in _The Dispensary_ who is identified
as "not very flatteringly described in Garth's 'Dispensary.'"
This mention situates Garth within the London medical and literary
circles of the late seventeenth century that included Tyson himself.

_The Dispensary_, published in the same year as Tyson's essay (1699),
was one of the most celebrated mock-heroic poems of its era. It
satirized the College of Physicians' campaign to establish a dispensary
providing medicines to the poor, a controversy in which both Garth and
the physician satirized as "Carus" were active participants.

The biographical notice's mention of Garth thus provides context for
the intellectual and professional milieu from which Tyson's
philological work emerged at the close of the seventeenth century.
