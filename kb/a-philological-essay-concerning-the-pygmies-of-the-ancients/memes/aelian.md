# Claudius Aelianus (Aelian)

## Biography

Claudius Aelianus (c. 175–235 AD), commonly known as Aelian, was a Greek author and rhetorician who wrote during the Roman imperial period.

Though a native Italian born in Praeneste, he mastered Greek to such a degree that he was called "honey-tongued" (*meliglossos*). He chose to write exclusively in Greek.

His *De Natura Animalium* (On the Nature of Animals) spans 17 books and preserves fragments of earlier Greek writers whose original works are now lost.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Aelian is cited by Tyson as one of the classical authorities who transmitted the Pygmy legend. Athenaeus, quoting Philochorus, and Aelian both relayed stories about the transformation of Pygmies. (010-150)

Tyson notes a specific disagreement in the sources: while some accounts claimed Diana transformed a Pygmy woman into a crane, Aelian instead attributed the transformation to Juno. (010-156)

Aelian's testimony was part of a broader transmission chain from Greek sources. His accounts, along with those of Pliny, Solinus, Mela, and Philostratus, were ultimately derived from the same earlier Greek authorities. (012-014)

## Key Works Cited by Tyson

- **De Natura Animalium** (*Historia Animalium*, On the Nature of Animals) — Aelian's principal work, a compilation of natural history in 17 books, frequently cited by Renaissance and early modern scholars
