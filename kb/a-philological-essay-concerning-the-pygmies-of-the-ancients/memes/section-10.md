# Section 10

[Footnote A: Nansen, _ut supra_, p. 262.]

 
[Footnote B: Dorset, _Omaha Sociology. American Bureau of Ethnology_, iii.

[010-001]211.]

 
[Footnote C: Stevenson, _Religious Life of Zuni Child. American Bureau of

[010-002]Ethnology_, v. 539.]

[010-003]It is hardly necessary to allude to those spirits which animistic ideas

[010-004]have attached amongst other objects and places, to trees and wells. They

[010-005]are fully dealt with in Dr. Tylor's pages, and must not be forgotten in

[010-006]connection with the present question.

[010-007]To sum up, then, it appears as if the idea, so widely diffused, of little,

[010-008]invisible, or only sometimes visible, people, is of the most complex

[010-009]nature. From the darkness which shrouds it, however, it is possible to

[010-010]discern some rays of light. That the souls of the departed, and the

[010-011]underground world which they inhabit, are largely responsible for it, is,

[010-012]I hope, rendered probable by the facts which I have brought forward. That

[010-013]animistic ideas have played an important part in the evolution of the idea

[010-014]of fairy peoples, is not open to doubt. That to these conceptions were

[010-015]superadded many features really derived from the actions of aboriginal

[010-016]races hiding before the destroying might of their invaders, and this not

[010-017]merely in these islands, but in many parts of the world, has been, I

[010-018]think, demonstrated by the labours of the gentleman whose theory I have so

[010-019]often alluded to. But the point upon which it is desired to lay stress is

[010-020]that the features derived from aboriginal races are only one amongst many

[010-021]sources. Possibly they play an important part, but scarcely, I think, one

[010-022]so important as Mr. MacRitchie would have us believe.

[010-023]A PHILOLOGICAL ESSAY

[010-024]Concerning the PYGMIES, THE CYNOCEPHALI, THE SATYRS and SPHINGES OF THE

[010-025]ANCIENTS,

[010-026]Wherein it will appear that they were all either APES or MONKEYS; and not

[010-027]MEN, as formerly pretended.

[010-028]By [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] M.D.

[010-029]A Philological Essay Concerning the PYGMIES OF THE ANCIENTS.

[010-030]Having had the Opportunity of Dissecting this remarkable Creature, which

[010-031]not only in the _outward shape_ of the Body, but likewise in the structure

[010-032]of many of the Inward Parts, so nearly resembles a Man, as plainly appears

[010-033]by the _Anatomy_ I have here given of it, it suggested the Thought to me,

[010-034]whether this sort of _Animal_, might not give the Foundation to the

[010-035]Stories of the _[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_ and afford an occasion not only to the _Poets_,

[010-036]but _Historians_ too, of inventing the many [[fable|Fables]] and wonderful and merry

[010-037]Relations, that are transmitted down to us concerning them? I must

[010-038]confess, I could never before entertain any other Opinion about them, but

[010-039]that the whole was a _Fiction_: and as the first Account we have of them,

[010-040]was from a _Poet_, so that they were only a Creature of the Brain,

[010-041]produced by a warm and wanton Imagination, and that they never had any

[010-042]Existence or Habitation elsewhere.

[010-043]In this Opinion I was the more confirmed, because the most diligent

[010-044]Enquiries of late into all the Parts of the inhabited World, could never

[010-045]discover any such _Puny_ diminutive _Race_ of _Mankind_. That they should

[010-046]be totally destroyed by the _Cranes_, their Enemies, and not a Straggler

[010-047]here and there left remaining, was a Fate, that even those _Animals_ that

[010-048]are constantly preyed upon by others, never undergo. Nothing therefore

[010-049]appeared to me more Fabulous and Romantick, than their _History_, and the

[010-050]Relations about them, that _Antiquity_ has delivered to us. And not only

[010-051]_[[strabo|Strabo]]_ of old, but our greatest Men of Learning of late, have wholly

[010-052]exploded them, as a mere _figment_; invented only to amuse, and divert the

[010-053]Reader with the Comical Narration of their Atchievements, believing that

[010-054]there were never any such Creatures in Nature.

[010-055]This opinion had so fully obtained with me, that I never thought it worth

[010-056]the Enquiry, how they came to invent such Extravagant Stories: Nor should

[010-057]I now, but upon the Occasion of Dissecting this _Animal_: For observing

[010-058]that 'tis call'd even to this day in the _Indian_ or _Malabar_ Language,

[010-059]_[[apes|Orang-Outang]]_, i.e. a _Man_ of the _Woods_, or _Wild-men_; and being

[010-060]brought from _Africa_, that part of the World, where the _Pygmies_ are

[010-061]said to inhabit; and it's present _Stature_ likewise tallying so well with

[010-062]that of the _Pygmies_ of the Ancients; these Considerations put me upon

[010-063]the search, to inform my self farther about them, and to examine, whether

[010-064]I could meet with any thing that might illustrate their _History_. For I

[010-065]thought it strange, that if the whole was but a meer Fiction, that so many

[010-066]succeeding Generations should be so fond of preserving a _Story_, that had

[010-067]no Foundation at all in Nature; and that the _Ancients_ should trouble

[010-068]themselves so much about them. If therefore I can make out in this

[010-069]_Essay_, that there were such _Animals_ as _Pygmies_; and that they were

[010-070]not a _Race_ of _Men_, but _Apes_; and can discover the _Authors_, who

[010-071]have forged all, or most of the idle Stories concerning them; and shew how

[010-072]the Cheat in after Ages has been carried on, by embalming the Bodies of

[010-073]_Apes_, then exposing them for the _Men_ of the Country, from whence they

[010-074]brought them: If I can do this, I shall think my time not wholly lost, nor

[010-075]the trouble altogether useless, that I have had in this Enquiry.

[010-076]My Design is not to justifie all the Relations that have been given of

[010-077]this _Animal_, even by Authors of reputed Credit; but, as far as I can, to

[010-078]distinguish Truth from Fable; and herein, if what I assert amounts to a

[010-079]Probability, 'tis all I pretend to. I shall accordingly endeavour to make

[010-080]it appear, that not only the _Pygmies_ of the Ancients, but also the

[010-081]_Cynocephali_, and _Satyrs_ and _Sphinges_ were only _Apes_ or _Monkeys_,

[010-082]not _Men_, as they have been represented. But the Story of the _Pygmies_

[010-083]being the greatest Imposture, I shall chiefly concern my self about them,

[010-084]and shall be more concise on the others, since they will not need so

[010-085]strict an Examination.

[010-086]We will begin with the Poet _[[homer|Homer]]_, who is generally owned as the first

[010-087]Inventor of the Fable of the _Pygmies_, if it be a Fable, and not a true

[010-088]Story, as I believe will appear in the Account I shall give of them. Now

[010-089]_Homer_ only mentions them in a _Simile_, wherein he compares the Shouts

[010-090]that the _Trojans_ made, when they were going to joyn Battle with the

[010-091]_Græcians_, to the great Noise of the _Cranes_, going to fight the

[010-092]_Pygmies_: he saith,[A]

[010-093][Greek: Ai t' epei oun cheimona phygon, kai athesphaton ombron

[010-094]Klangae tai ge petontai ep' okeanoio rhoaon

[010-095]'Andrasi pygmaioisi phonon kai kaera pherousai.] i.e.

[010-096]_Quæ simul ac fugere Imbres, Hyememque Nivalem

[010-097]Cum magno Oceani clangore ferantur ad undas

[010-098]Pygmæis pugnamque Viris, cædesque ferentes._

 
[Footnote A: _Homer. Iliad_. lib. 3. ver. 4.]

[010-099]Or as _Helius Eobanus Hessus_ paraphrases the whole.[A]

[010-100]_Postquam sub Ducibus digesta per agmina stabant

[010-101]Quæque fuis, Equitum turmæ, Peditumque Cohortes,

[010-102]Obvia torquentes Danais vestigia Troës

[010-103]Ibant, sublato Campum clamore replentes:

[010-104]Non secus ac cuneata Gruum sublime volantum

[010-105]Agmina, dum fugiunt Imbres, ac frigora Brumæ,

[010-106]Per Coelum matutino clangore feruntur,

[010-107]Oceanumque petunt, mortem exitiumque cruentum

[010-108]Irrita Pigmæis moturis arma ferentes._

 
[Footnote A: _Homeri Ilias Latino Carmine reddita ab Helio Eobano Hesso_.]

[010-109]By [Greek: andrasi pygmaioisi] therefore, which is the Passage upon which

[010-110]they have grounded all their fabulous Relations of the _Pygmies_, why may

[010-111]not _Homer_ mean only _Pygmies_ or _Apes_ like _Men_. Such an Expression

[010-112]is very allowable in a _Poet_, and is elegant and significant, especially

[010-113]since there is so good a Foundation in Nature for him to use it, as we

[010-114]have already seen, in the _Anatomy of the Orang-Outang_. Nor is a _Poet_

[010-115]tied to that strictness of Expression, as an _Historian_ or _Philosopher_;

[010-116]he has the liberty of pleasing the Reader's Phancy, by Pictures and

[010-117]Representations of his own. If there be a becoming likeness, 'tis all that

[010-118]he is accountable for. I might therefore here make the same _Apology_ for

[010-119]him, as _Strabo_[A] do's on another account for his _Geography_, [Greek:

[010-120]ou gar kat' agnoian ton topikon legetai, all' haedonaes kai terpseos

[010-121]charin]. That he said it, not thro' Ignorance, but to please and delight:

[010-122]Or, as in another place he expresses himself,[B] [Greek: ou gar kat'

[010-123]agnoian taes istorias hypolaepteon genesthai touto, alla tragodias

[010-124]charin]. _Homer_ did not make this slip thro' Ignorance of the true

[010-125]_History_, but for the Beauty of his _Poem_. So that tho' he calls them

[010-126]_Men Pygmies_, yet he may mean no more by it, than that they were like

[010-127]_Men_. As to his Purpose, 'twill serve altogether as well, whether this

[010-128]bloody Battle be fought between the _Cranes_ and _Pygmæan Men_, or the

[010-129]_Cranes_ and _Apes_, which from their Stature he calls _Pygmies_, and from

[010-130]their shape _Men_; provided that when the _Cranes_ go to engage, they make

[010-131]a mighty terrible noise, and clang enough to fright these little _Wights_

[010-132]their mortal Enemies. To have called them only _Apes_, had been flat and

[010-133]low, and lessened the grandieur of the Battle. But this _Periphrasis_ of

[010-134]them, [Greek: andres pygmaioi], raises the Reader's Phancy, and surprises

[010-135]him, and is more becoming the Language of an Heroic Poem.

 
[Footnote A: _Strabo Geograph_. lib. 1. p.m. 25.]

 
[Footnote B: _Strabo_ ibid. p.m. 30.]

[010-136]But how came the _Cranes_ and _Pygmies_ to fall out? What may be the Cause

[010-137]of this Mortal Feud, and constant War between them? For _Brutes_, like

[010-138]_Men_, don't war upon one another, to raise and encrease their Glory, or

[010-139]to enlarge their Empire. Unless I can acquit my self herein, and assign

[010-140]some probable Cause hereof, I may incur the same Censure as _Strabo_[A]

[010-141]passed on several of the _Indian Historians_, [Greek: enekainisan de kai

[010-142]taen 'Omaerikaen ton Pygmaion geranomachin trispithameis eipontes], for

[010-143]reviewing the _Homerical_ Fight of the _Cranes_ and _Pygmies_, which he

[010-144]looks upon only as a fiction of the Poet. But this had been very

[010-145]unbecoming _Homer_ to take a _Simile_ (which is designed for illustration)

[010-146]from what had no Foundation in Nature. His _Betrachomyomachia_, 'tis true,

[010-147]was a meer Invention, and never otherwise esteemed: But his _Geranomachia_

[010-148]hath all the likelyhood of a true Story. And therefore I shall enquire now

[010-149]what may be the just Occasion of this Quarrel.

 
[Footnote A: _Strabo Geograph_. lib. 2. p.m. 48.]

[010-150]_Athenæus_[A] out of _Philochorus_, and so likewise _Ælian_[B], tell us a

[010-151]Story, That in the Nation of the _Pygmies_ the Male-line failing, one

[010-152]_Gerana_ was the Queen; a Woman of an admired Beauty, and whom the

[010-153]Citizens worshipped as a Goddess; but she became so vain and proud, as to

[010-154]prefer her own, before the Beauty of all the other Goddesses, at which

[010-155]they grew enraged; and to punish her for her Insolence, Athenæus tells us

[010-156]that it was _Diana_, but _Ælian_ saith 'twas _Juno_ that transformed her

[010-157]into a _Crane_, and made her an Enemy to the _Pygmies_ that worshipped her

[010-158]before. But since they are not agreed which Goddess 'twas, I shall let

[010-159]this pass.

 
[Footnote A: _Athenæi Deipnosoph_. lib. 9 p.m. 393.]

 
[Footnote B: _Ælian. Hist. Animal_. lib. 15. cap. 29.]

[010-160]_[[pomponius-mela|Pomponius Mela]]_ will have it, and I think some others, that these cruel

[010-161]Engagements use to happen, upon the _Cranes_ coming to devour the _Corn_

[010-162]the _Pygmies_ had sowed; and that at last they became so victorious, as

[010-163]not only to destroy their Corn, but them also: For he tells us,[A] _Fuere

[010-164]interiùs Pygmæi, minutum genus, & quod pro satis frugibus contra Grues

[010-165]dimicando, defecit._ This may seem a reasonable Cause of a Quarrel; but it

[010-166]not being certain that the _Pygmies_ used to sow _Corn_, I will not insist

[010-167]on this neither.

 
[Footnote A: _Pomp. Mela de situ Orbis_, lib. 3. cap. 8.]

[010-168]Now what seems most likely to me, is the account that _Pliny_ out of

[010-169]_Megasthenes_, and _Strabo_ from _Onesicritus_ give us; and, provided I be

[010-170]not obliged to believe or justifie _all_ that they say, I could rest

[010-171]satisfied in great part of their Relation: For _Pliny_[B] tells us, _Veris

[010-172]tempore universo agmine ad mare descendere, & Ova, Pullosque earum Alitum

[010-173]consumere_: That in the Spring-time the whole drove of the _Pygmies_ go

[010-174]down to the Sea side, to devour the _Cranes_ Eggs and their young Ones. So

[010-175]likewise _Onesicritus_,[B] [Greek: Pros de tous trispithamous polemon

[010-176]einai tais Geranois (hon kai Homaeron daeloun) kai tois Perdixin, ous

[010-177]chaenomegetheis einai; toutous d' eklegein auton ta oa, kai phtheirein;

[010-178]ekei gar ootokein tas Geranous; dioper maedamou maed' oa euriskesthai

[010-179]Geranon, maet' oun neottia;] i.e. _That there is a fight between the_

[010-180]Pygmies _and the_ Cranes (_as_ Homer _relates_) _and the_ Partridges

[010-181]_which are as big as_ Geese; _for these_ Pygmies _gather up their Eggs,

[010-182]and destroy them; the_ Cranes _laying their Eggs there; and neither their

[010-183]Eggs, nor their Nests, being to be found any where else_. 'Tis plain

[010-184]therefore from them, that the Quarrel is not out of any _Antipathy_ the

[010-185]_Pygmies_ have to the _Cranes_, but out of love to their own Bellies. But

[010-186]the _Cranes_ finding their Nests to be robb'd, and their young Ones prey'd

[010-187]on by these Invaders, no wonder that they should so sharply engage them;

[010-188]and the least they could do, was to fight to the utmost so mortal an

[010-189]Enemy. Hence, no doubt, many a bloody Battle happens, with various success

[010-190]to the Combatants; sometimes with great slaughter of the _long-necked

[010-191]Squadron_; sometimes with great effusion of _Pygmæan_ blood. And this may

[010-192]well enough, in a _Poet's_ phancy, be magnified, and represented as a

[010-193]dreadful War; and no doubt of it, were one a _Spectator_ of it, 'twould be

[010-194]diverting enough.
