## Overview

Travellers' Accounts is the methodological category for evaluating firsthand and secondhand reports from explorers, merchants, and voyagers as evidence for the existence and nature of pygmy races. Tyson's essay repeatedly confronts the epistemological problem of travel literature: how to distinguish reliable observation from exaggeration, honest mistake from deliberate fabrication. This issue is particularly acute for the pygmy question because so many of the ancient and early modern reports that describe diminutive races — from Ctesias's Indian pygmies to Stanley's Akka — reach European scholarship through the medium of travel narrative, a genre notorious for its mixture of authentic ethnography and sensational invention.

## Ancient Sources

The problem of travellers' credibility is as old as the pygmy question itself. Megasthenes, the Seleucid ambassador to the Mauryan court (c. 300 BC), reported Indian pygmies whom Pliny later incorporated into his *Natural History*; the difficulty, as Tyson notes, is that Megasthenes "mounts the Pygmies for this expedition" in a passage that may describe military service rather than ethnological observation (001-073). The distinction between honest mistake and deliberate deception is central: Tyson observes that many ancient accounts of pigmy tribes arose from "the mistakes of travellers who had taken apes for men," a diagnostic error that recurs across centuries of travel literature from Hanno's *gorillai* to the orang-utan debates of Tyson's own time (001-073).

Pliny's encyclopaedic method itself presents a methodological caution, since he compiled travellers' reports without independent verification. When he reports that African pygmies "live in caves" and construct their dwellings "of mud mixed with feathers and eggshells," the reader cannot distinguish Pliny's own credulity from the original observer's accuracy (005-108). The ancient testimony that reaches Tyson is thus doubly mediated: first through the traveller's perception, then through the compiler's selection and paraphrase.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson's treatment of travellers' accounts exemplifies his philological method. Rather than simply accepting or rejecting travel reports wholesale, he applies a multi-factor evaluation: the traveller's proximity to the events described, the internal consistency of the account, corroboration by independent witnesses, and the absence of motives for fabrication. When evaluating Stanley's reports of the Akka pygmies in Central Africa, Tyson notes that "the traveller who approaches within their range" faces both practical difficulty and the risk of projecting expectations onto ambiguous observations (006-164). He adds the caution that "the travellers hesitated about" accepting what they saw when the physical evidence contradicted their expectations — a remark that underscores the psychological dimension of traveller testimony (007-135).

Most revealing is Tyson's meta-methodological observation that "if you travel all over Africa, you shall find" pygmy races widely distributed, but that "who has travelled all Africa?" — an ironic admission that total verification is impossible and that scholarly judgment must operate under conditions of incomplete evidence (016-130, 016-138). This passage crystallizes the central tension of the essay: the desire for positive knowledge about pygmy races is perpetually frustrated by the limitations of travellers' testimony as a class of evidence.

Tyson's own conclusion is cautious. He does not reject travellers' accounts in principle — to do so would eliminate most of the evidence for pygmy races — but insists that each report be weighed against physical, philological, and comparative-anatomical evidence. The traveller who mistakes an ape for a pygmy, or who repeats a native informant's legend as an eyewitness observation, commits errors that only systematic method can detect.

## Modern Perspective

Modern anthropology has formalized the critique that Tyson pioneered. The "armchair anthropologist" tradition that culminated in Sir James Frazer's *Golden Bough* relied uncritically on travellers' and missionaries' reports; the professionalization of fieldwork in the twentieth century — particularly through Bronisław Malinowski's insistence on extended participant observation — was in part a response to the very problems of travellers' testimony that Tyson identified in 1699. The distinction between etic and emic description, the use of multiple independent informants, and the requirement that ethnographic claims be falsifiable all represent institutional solutions to the epistemic challenge that Tyson confronted with the tools of Renaissance philology.
