# Lucian of Samosata

## Biography

Lucian of Samosata (c. 125–180 AD) was a Greek Satirist and Rhetorician of Syrian birth. He was known for his witty and skeptical dialogues that ridiculed superstition, religious fraud, and the credulity of his contemporaries.

His _Verae Historiae_ (True History) is a parody of travelers' tales and fantastical histories. In it, the narrator professes to tell the truth while recounting impossible voyages to the moon and inside a whale's belly.

Tyson would cite this work approvingly for its ancient precedent of skepticism toward fabulous ethnography.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Lucian appears in Tyson's essay not as a source of testimony about pygmies, but as an ancient witness to the unreliability of those who claimed to describe them. His target was [[ctesias]], whose _Indica_ Tyson considered one of the most unreliable ancient accounts of India.

Tyson quotes Lucian's devastating verdict on Ctesias:

> "What he neither saw himself, nor ever heard from any Body else."
> (012-027)

This ancient skepticism gives Tyson's own critique a long and respectable pedigree. Lucian, in short, "will not believe a word he saith" (012-026).

Tyson also notes that Lucian's _Verae Historiae_ is the source from which he draws this critical principle (012-043). The distinction Lucian draws — between eyewitness testimony, second-hand report, and sheer invention — becomes the epistemological standard Tyson applies throughout his essay.

In Section 15, Tyson returns to Lucian's criterion when assessing the relative reliability of authorities. He judges that while [[olaus-magnus]] may have written "some things by hear-say," he is not "so fabulous as _Ctesias_, who (as _Lucian_ tells us) writes what he neither saw himself, or heard from any Body else" (015-017).

Tyson's invocation of Lucian serves a strategic purpose. By showing that even an ancient pagan satirist recognized Ctesias's unreliability, Tyson undercuts any argument that his own skepticism is merely modern prejudice against classical authorities. The ancients themselves knew whom to trust and whom to doubt.
