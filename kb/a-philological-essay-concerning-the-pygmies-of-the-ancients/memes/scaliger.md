Julius Caesar Scaliger (1484–1558) was an Italian-born scholar who spent most of his career in France, renowned for his commentaries on Aristotle and his combative scholarly style. In the Pygmy debate, Scaliger represents the skeptical position — Tyson introduces him as one who "makes them to be only a Fiction of the Ancients" (013-014). Scaliger's dismissive treatment of the Pygmy tradition forces Tyson to engage with the most serious classical objection to the reality of Pygmies.

## Scaliger's Skepticism and Tyson's Response

The crux of Scaliger's argument rested on a textual-critical point about Aristotle. Tyson observes that "Scaliger in translating this Text of Aristotle, omits both these words" (013-116), referring to the passage in the *Historia Animalium* that describes the Pygmies' battle with the cranes. Tyson questions whether Scaliger had any justification for this omission: "without giving us his reason for it, if he had any" (013-121).

Tyson then drives the point home: "And therefore Scaliger (saith he) hath not translated the words" — Scaliger simply left them out (013-129). Tyson speculates that "had Scaliger known it to be surreptitious, no doubt but he would have told us" (013-131), suggesting that Scaliger's silence on the textual question undermines his own skeptical conclusion. The passage in question, which Scaliger translated as *Pusillum genus, ut aiunt, ipsi atque etiam Equi* (013-137), was part of the Aristotelian corpus that Tyson and his allies considered authentic.

Tyson returns to Scaliger in Section 17, grouping him among those who denied that Pygmies were a distinct race of men — "Julius Cæsar Scaliger" is listed alongside other skeptics (017-151). This placement in the final section of the essay shows that Scaliger's objection remained a live issue throughout Tyson's survey.
