# Aristotle

## Biography

Aristotle (384–322 BC) was a Greek philosopher and naturalist whose works span logic, metaphysics, ethics, politics, and natural history. His _Historia Animalium_ is among the earliest systematic works of zoology in the Western tradition. Edward Tyson drew extensively on Aristotle's authority in _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699).

## Aristotle's Pygmy Account

Unlike [[homer|Homer]]'s poetic depiction, Aristotle treated the Pygmies within the framework of natural history. In his _Historia Animalium_, he discussed the crane-migration that featured centrally in the pygmy legend.

Tyson quotes Aristotle's observation that cranes migrate to escape the Pygmies' attacks:

> "For _Aristotle_ tells us, That the _Cranes_, to avoid the Pygmies, flie to the Marshes above _Egypt_."
> (011-010)

This detail — that cranes fled to Egyptian marshes — provided geographic specificity missing from Homer's account. It allowed Tyson to connect the pygmy legend to the known distribution of great apes in [[africa-aethiopia|Africa]].

## Troglodytic Detail

Aristotle also reported that the Pygmies dwelled in caves — a detail Tyson found highly significant. "Aristotle's Pigmy race may, from their troglodytic habits, be identified with apes," Tyson argued, noting that cave-dwelling was characteristic of the great apes known to 17th-century naturalists. (004-157)

This troglodytic detail became a key piece of evidence in Tyson's broader argument. If the ancient "Pygmies" were cave-dwellers of small stature, they matched the profile of chimpanzees more closely than that of any known human population.

## Tyson's Use of Aristotelian Authority

Aristotle's authority was crucial to Tyson's argument. As "the master of them that know" (in Dante's phrase), Aristotle commanded respect across early modern scholarship. By showing that even Aristotle treated Pygmies as a real subject for inquiry, Tyson undermined the view that pygmy accounts were pure fable.

Tyson noted that "Pliny, Herodotus, Aristotle, and other writers" formed a consistent tradition of serious ancient engagement with the pygmy question. (001-083) The weight of these authorities, Tyson argued, demanded explanation — not dismissal.
