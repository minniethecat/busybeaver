Pausanias (c. 110–180 AD) was a Greek traveler and geographer whose *Description of Greece* (Ἑλλάδος Περιήγησις) remains one of the most valuable sources for the topography, monuments, and local traditions of ancient Greece. Writing in the tradition of the *periegetes* (guidebook writer), Pausanias combined firsthand observation with the collection of local myths and historical accounts.

Tyson cites Pausanias in the final section of his essay, where he draws upon Pausanias's account of wild men encountered on remote islands as part of a broader discussion of half-human, half-bestial creatures in ancient literature.

## The Account from Euphemus Car

In Section 17, Tyson discusses various ancient reports of wild, sub-human races and their relationship to the Pygmy question. Within this context, he introduces Pausanias's account of a strange encounter at sea:

> "And I might here add what _Pausanias_ relates from _Euphemus Car_, who by contrary Winds was driven upon some Islands, where he tells us there inhabited wild Men, but when he comes to describe them, tells us that they had no Speech; that they had Tails on their Rumps; and were very lascivious toward the Women in the Ship. But of these more, when we come to discourse of _Satyrs_."
> (017-124)

The account, which Pausanias drew from the otherwise unknown Euphemus of Caria, describes a crew driven off course by adverse winds to islands inhabited by savage humanoids. These creatures, according to the report, lacked the faculty of speech, possessed tails, and behaved with uncontrolled lust toward the women aboard the ship.

## Relation to Satyr Lore

Tyson explicitly defers fuller discussion of this passage to his treatment of Satyrs, noting the thematic connection between Pausanias's wild islanders and the broader tradition of half-human creatures:

> "[Footnote A: _Pausanias in Atticis_, p.m. 21.]"
> (017-129)

The footnote reference situates the account within Pausanias's first book, which covers Attica, and indicates Tyson's direct familiarity with the text. By citing Pausanias alongside other ancient discussions of wild men and satyrs, Tyson contextualizes the Pygmy question within the wider classical discourse on boundary creatures — beings that straddle the line between human and animal.

## Pausanias's Place in Tyson's Argument

Tyson's use of Pausanias serves a comparative function. The wild men of Pausanias's account — speechless, tailed, and sexually unrestrained — represent a category distinct from the Pygmies, who are consistently described as articulate, socially organized, and fully human in everything except stature. By juxtaposing these accounts, Tyson reinforces his argument that the Pygmies should not be conflated with the monstrous or semi-bestial races of ancient legend.
