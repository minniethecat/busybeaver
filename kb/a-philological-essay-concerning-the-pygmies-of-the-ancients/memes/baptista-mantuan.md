Baptista Mantuanus (1447–1516), also known as Battista Mantovano or Battista Spagnoli, was an Italian Carmelite friar and one of the most celebrated Neo-Latin poets of the Renaissance. His *Bucolica* (Eclogues) were widely read across Europe and used as a school text in Elizabethan England. Tyson cites Mantuan twice in his essay — once among Bartholin's catalogue of authorities, and again for a vivid Latin passage on Pygmy-crane warfare.

## Mantuan in the Scholarly Catalogue

Tyson first encounters Mantuan through Bartholin's list of classical and Renaissance authorities on the Pygmy question: "Homer, Ctesias, Aristotle, Philostratus, Pliny, Juvenal, Oppian, Baptista Mantuan, St. Austin and his Scholiast" (014-148). The inclusion of a poet in this scholarly list reflects the Renaissance habit of treating classical and humanist verse as legitimate testimony on natural-historical questions.

## The Pygmy-Crane Battle Verse

In Section 17, Tyson returns to Mantuan for a poetic illustration of the Homeric simile of Pygmies and cranes. After declaring that "there are none who have borrowed Homer's fancy, but have thought so," Tyson offers "a farther instance in Baptista Mantuan" (017-058), quoting the poet's Latin verse:

> "Pygmæi breve vulgus, iners Plelecula, quando / Convenere Grues longis in prælia rostris, / Sublato clamore fremunt, dumque agmine magno / Hostibus occurrit, tellus tremit Indica, clamant / Littora, arenarum nimbis absconditur aër; / Omnis & involvit Pulvis solemque, Polumque" (017-058)

The passage — describing the Pygmies as a short-statured folk who raise a clamour when the long-beaked cranes gather for battle, while the Indian shore trembles and dust obscures the sky — is a direct poetic elaboration of the Homeric simile from *Iliad* 3. Tyson's quotation of Mantuan demonstrates the enduring literary appeal of the Pygmy-crane motif from antiquity through the Renaissance.

## Place in Tyson's Argument

Mantuan's verse, while not bearing on the factual question of Pygmy existence, illustrates a point Tyson makes throughout the essay: the literary tradition of Pygmies is rich and continuous, but it must be distinguished from empirical observation. By quoting Mantuan alongside Homer and Oppian, Tyson traces the poetic lineage of the motif while reserving his natural-historical judgment.
