# Apes (as Pygmies)

## Overview

The identification of apes—specifically the chimpanzee ("Orang-Outang")—as the true referent of ancient pygmy accounts is the central thesis of Edward Tyson's _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699). Tyson argued that classical descriptions of diminutive, tool-using, humanlike creatures in Africa and Asia were not mythical but derived from real encounters with great apes. (001-057)

Tyson was uniquely positioned to make this argument: he had dissected a chimpanzee in 1698 (published as _Orang-Outang, sive Homo Sylvestris_, 1699) and was the foremost comparative anatomist of his generation. (001-014)

## The Thesis

Tyson confronts the problem that many learned men dismissed pygmy stories as fable. He counters with a natural-historical hypothesis: "the execution [of this essay] will show" that what ancients called Pygmies were apes. (001-057) He was aware of travelers' reports "who had taken apes for men" and, significantly, "nor was he without" his own anatomical evidence to support the identification. (001-073)

The argument draws on classical texts, traveler accounts, and—crucially—Tyson's firsthand anatomical knowledge of primate morphology. (004-078)

## Classical Descriptions

Ancient authors described creatures "in shape and appearance like men" that inhabited remote regions. (005-060) These accounts, Tyson argues, are consistent with ape behavior and morphology. The descriptions of pygmies as tool-using, bipedal, and possessing humanlike faces all align with what was known of great apes.

## Anatomical Evidence

Tyson's dissection of the chimpanzee provided him with detailed comparative data. He could demonstrate both the similarities (bipedal posture, facial features, tool use) and differences (foot structure, dentition, lack of true speech) between apes and humans. This anatomical perspective was unprecedented in classical scholarship and represents one of the earliest applications of comparative anatomy to a philological question.

## Implications

Tyson's thesis has broader implications for the history of science. It represents an early attempt to reconcile classical texts with empirical observation—a methodology that would become central to the Enlightenment. By arguing that mythological accounts had a natural-historical basis, Tyson contributed to the demythologization of ancient ethnography without dismissing it as mere fiction.
