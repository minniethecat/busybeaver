Edward Tregear (1846–1931) was a New Zealand scholar and civil servant whose work on Maori mythology and Polynesian comparative linguistics made him a significant figure in late nineteenth-century anthropology. His documentation of Maori traditions concerning supernatural little people — especially water-dwelling elves — provided Edward Tyson with Pacific-region evidence for the near-universal distribution of pygmy-like beings in world folklore.

## Maori Water-Elves and Wood-Spirits

Tregear's most important contribution to Tyson's argument concerns the Ponaturi, the water-dwelling supernatural beings of Maori tradition. Tyson records that "the Maories also have their Water-Pigmies, the Ponaturi, who are, according to Mr. Tregear, elves, little tiny people, mostly dwellers in water, coming ashore to sleep" (007-165). This description placed the Ponaturi within a global pattern of water-associated dwarf or pygmy beings, a motif Tyson found significant for tracing the diffusion of pygmy lore.

The essay also references Maori wood-elf traditions documented by Tregear. The "te tini ote hakuturi," or "the multitude of the wood-elves," were little people "who put the chips all back into the tree Rata had felled and stood it up again, because he had not paid tribute to Tane" (007-144). This narrative — where supernatural little people punish a human who fails to perform proper ritual observance — exemplifies the moral dimension often present in pygmy folklore.

## Comparative Mythology and Diffusion

Tregear's scholarship was distinguished by its comparative approach, tracing mythological parallels across Polynesian, Melanesian, and Southeast Asian cultures. His contributions to the Journal of the Anthropological Institute, cited by Tyson (007-145), placed Maori pygmy-lore within a broader Austronesian mythological context. This comparative perspective aligned with Tyson's own methodology of demonstrating the wide distribution of pygmy beliefs.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tregear's evidence served Tyson's argument that pygmy-like beings appear in the mythologies of geographically distant and culturally unrelated peoples, suggesting either independent invention from similar psychological sources or ancient diffusion. The Ponaturi — water-dwelling, nocturnal, diminutive, and feared — share structural features with European water-elves and African water-spirits, strengthening the case for a universal human tendency to populate marginal spaces (forests, waters) with imagined small peoples.

## Key Works Cited

- **"The Maori-Polynesian Comparative Dictionary"** (1891) — Tregear's magnum opus, tracing linguistic and mythological connections across the Pacific.
- **Papers in the Journal of the Anthropological Institute** — Containing his detailed accounts of Maori supernatural beings, including the Ponaturi and hakuturi.
