## Overview

Negrito Populations, in the terminology adopted by Tyson from the French anthropologist Armand de Quatrefages, are the diminutive, dark-skinned, woolly-haired aboriginal peoples of South and Southeast Asia. De Quatrefages considered them the purest surviving representatives of the original "Negrito type" — a pigmy black population that he believed had once occupied a continuous area stretching from India through Indo-China to the archipelagoes of the western Pacific, before being fragmented and partially absorbed by later waves of "yellow or white races" (001-121). Tyson devotes the first major section of his ethnographic survey to these populations, starting with the Andaman Islanders and working outward through the Nicobars, Malay Peninsula, and Philippines. His treatment reveals the essay's dual character as both a work of classical philology and a contribution to late-nineteenth-century physical anthropology.

## Ancient Sources

The Negrito populations of Southeast Asia have no direct counterpart in classical literature. The ancient authors whom Tyson discusses — Homer, Aristotle, Pliny, Ctesias — placed their pygmies in India and Africa, not in the Malay Peninsula or the Philippine archipelago. Tyson implicitly acknowledges this gap by beginning his survey of Negrito populations with a modern anthropological framework rather than with ancient textual evidence. The connection he draws is typological rather than historical: the Andaman Islanders, Semang, and Aeta represent the kind of diminutive, dark-skinned population that, he suggests, may have given rise to the ancient reports of Indian pygmies. The "black Men" whom Ctesias described "in the middle of India" (001-155) could, on this view, be understood as an ancient witness to the same Negrito populations that modern anthropology was documenting.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson organizes his survey of Negrito populations geographically, beginning with "the Mincopies of the Andaman Islands" as "a convenient starting-point" (001-132). He draws extensively on the detailed ethnographic work of Mr. E.H. Man and the anatomical studies of Dr. G.E. Dobson, both published in the Journal of the Anthropological Institute. The Andaman Islanders are presented as the best-documented case: they are described as having a mean male stature of about four feet ten inches, dark skin, and woolly hair — physical characteristics that distinguish them from both the ordinary African Negro and from the taller Malay populations that surround them (001-132).

From the Andamans, Tyson proceeds south to the Nicobar Islands, whose aboriginal inhabitants he describes as similar in physical type but more modified by mixture with Malay populations (002-021). He then moves to the Malay Peninsula, where the Semang are identified as the indigenous Negrito population, living alongside — and increasingly displaced by — the taller, Malay-derived Tagals (002-027). The survey continues through the Philippines, where the Aeta (spelled "Aetas" or "Aëtas" in Tyson's sources) represent the Negrito type at its easternmost extension.

Throughout this geographical survey, Tyson maintains a consistent comparative framework. For each population, he records stature, skin color, hair type, cranial measurements, and social customs, using these data points to support or challenge the claim that Asian Negritos and African Pygmies represent a single racial type. The De Quatrefages radiation model — "some going east, some west, have given rise to the black populations of Melanesia and Africa" (001-143) — provides the theoretical scaffolding, though Tyson is characteristically cautious about endorsing it without reservation.

A notable feature of Tyson's analysis is his attention to the cultural as well as physical characteristics of Negrito populations. He notes their technologies (dugout canoes, bows and arrows), their social organization (monogamous family units), and their religious beliefs — treating these not as mere ethnographic curiosities but as data points relevant to the question of whether these populations represent a single ancestral type or the product of convergent adaptation to similar environments.

## Modern Perspective

The Negrito populations that Tyson surveyed remain a subject of active anthropological and genetic research. Modern studies confirm the distinctiveness of Andaman Islanders, Semang, and Philippine Aeta as among the earliest modern human populations to have settled in Southeast Asia, with genetic divergence dates estimated at 25,000–50,000 years before present. Importantly, however, genetic evidence does not support De Quatrefages's theory of a Negrito radiation from South Asia westward to Africa. African Pygmies and Asian Negritos represent independent evolutionary trajectories; their shared small stature is now understood as convergent adaptation to tropical forest environments rather than evidence of a common ancestral pygmy population. Tyson's survey, while limited by the scientific tools of his era, remains valuable as one of the earliest systematic comparative treatments of these populations in English.
