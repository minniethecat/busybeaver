# Section 6

[006-001]"Behold the sidhe before your eyes:

[006-002]It is manifest to you that it is a king's mansion,

[006-003]Which was built by the firm Dagda;

[006-004]It was a wonder, a court, a wonderful hill."

 
[Footnote A: _Tumuli at New Grange. Trans. Roy. Irish Academy_, XXX. 1.]

[006-005]But certain of the expressions in this are evidently to be taken

[006-006]figuratively, since Mr. Coffey states, in connection with this and other

[006-007]quotations, that their importance consists in that they establish the

[006-008]existence at a very early date of a tradition associating Brugh na Boinne,

[006-009]the burial-place of the kings of Tara, with the tumuli on the Boyne. The

[006-010]association of particular monuments with the Dagda and other divinities

[006-011]and heroes of Irish mythology implies that the actual persons for whom

[006-012]they were erected had been forgotten, the pagan traditions being probably

[006-013]broken by the introduction of Christianity. The mythological ancestors of

[006-014]the heroes and kings interred at Brugh, who probably were even

[006-015]contemporarily associated with the cemetery, no doubt subsequently

[006-016]overshadowed in tradition the actual persons interred there.

[006-017]Finally, it seems that the fairy hills may have been actual

[006-018]dwelling-places, fortified or not, of prehistoric peoples. Such were no

[006-019]doubt some of the Picts' houses so fully dealt with by Mr. MacRitchie,

[006-020]though Petrie[A] seems to have considered that many of these were

[006-021]sepulchral in their nature. Such were also the Raths of Ireland and

[006-022]fortified hills, like the White Cater Thun of Forfarshire.

 
[Footnote A: _Anthrop. Mems._, ii. 216.]

[006-023]The interior of the mound-dwellings, as described in the stories, is a

[006-024]point to which allusion should be made. Sometimes the mound contains a

[006-025]splendid palace, adorned with gold and silver and precious stones, like

[006-026]the palace of the King of Elfland in the tale of "Childe Rowland." In the

[006-027]Scandinavian mound-stories we find a curious incident, for they are

[006-028]described as being capable of being raised upon red pillars, and as being

[006-029]so raised when the occupants gave a feast to their neighbours. "There are

[006-030]three hills on the lands of Bubbelgaard in Funen, which are to this day

[006-031]called the Dance-hills, from the following occurrence. A lad named Hans

[006-032]was at service in Bubbelgaard, and as he was coming one evening past the

[006-033]hills, he saw one of them raised on red pillars, and great dancing and

[006-034]much merriment underneath."[A] This feature is met with in several of the

[006-035]stories collected by Keightley, and is made use of in Cruikshank's

[006-036]picture, which forms the frontispiece to that volume. Lastly, in a number

[006-037]of cases there is not merely a habitation, but a vast country underneath

[006-038]the mound. An instance of this occurs in the tale of John Dietrich from

[006-039]the Isle of Rügen. Under the Nine-hills he found "that there were in that

[006-040]place the most beautiful walks, in which he might ramble along for miles

[006-041]in all directions, without ever finding an end of them, so immensely large

[006-042]was the hill that the little people lived in, and yet outwardly it seemed

[006-043]but a little hill, with a few bushes and trees growing on it."[B]

 
[Footnote A: Quoted by Keightley (p. 9), from Thiele, i. 118.]

 
[Footnote B: Keightley, 178.]

[006-044]2. The haunts of the fairies may be in caves, and examples of this form of

[006-045]dwelling-place are to be met with in different parts of the world. The

[006-046]Scandinavian hill people live in caves or small hills, and the Elves or

[006-047]dwarfs of La Romagna "dwell in lonely places, far away in the mountains,

[006-048]deep in them, in caves or among old ruins and rocks," as Mr. Leland,[A]

[006-049]who gives a tale respecting these little people, tells us. A Lithuanian

[006-050]tale[B] tells "how the hero, Martin, went into a forest to hunt,

[006-051]accompanied by a smith and a tailor. Finding an empty hut, they took

[006-052]possession of it; the tailor remained in it to cook the dinner, and the

[006-053]others went forth to the chase. When the dinner was almost ready, there

[006-054]came to the hut a very little old man with a very long beard, who

[006-055]piteously begged for food. After receiving it, he sprang on the tailor's

[006-056]neck and beat him almost to death. When the hunters returned, they found

[006-057]their comrade groaning on his couch, complaining of illness, but saying

[006-058]nothing about the bearded dwarf. Next day the smith suffered in a similar

[006-059]way; but when it came to Martin's turn, he proved too many and too strong

[006-060]for the dwarf, whom he overcame, and whom he fastened by the beard to the

[006-061]stump of a tree. But the dwarf tore himself loose before the hunters came

[006-062]back from the forest and escaped into a cavern. Tracing him by the drops

[006-063]of blood which had fallen from him, the three companions came to the mouth

[006-064]of the cavern, and Martin was lowered into it by the two others. Within it

[006-065]he found three princesses, who had been stolen by three dragons. These

[006-066]dragons he slew, and the princesses and their property he took to the spot

[006-067]above which his comrades kept watch, who hoisted them out of the cavern,

[006-068]but left Martin in it to die. As he wandered about disconsolately, he

[006-069]found the bearded dwarf, whom he slew. And soon afterwards he was conveyed

[006-070]out of the cavern by a flying serpent, and was able to punish his

[006-071]treacherous friends, and to recover the princesses, all three of whom he

[006-072]simultaneously married."

 
[Footnote A: _Etrusco Roman Remains_, p. 222.]

 
[Footnote B: _Folk Lore Record_, i. 85. Mr. Hartland points out to me that

[006-073]this tale, being a Marchen, does not afford quite such good evidence of

[006-074]belief as actually or recently existing as a saga.]

[006-075]Amongst the Magyars,[A] also, in some localities caves are pointed out as

[006-076]the haunts of fairies, such as the caves in the side of the rock named

[006-077]Budvár, the cave Borza-vára, near the castle of Dame Rapson; another haunt

[006-078]of the fairies is the cave near Almás, and the cold wind known as the

[006-079]"Nemere" is said to blow when the fairy in Almás cave feels cold. On one

[006-080]occasion the plague was raging in this neighbourhood; the people ascribed

[006-081]it to the cold blast emanating from the cave; so they hung shirts before

[006-082]the mouth of the cave and the plague ceased.

 
[Footnote A: Jones and Kropf, _Folk Tales of the Magyars_, pp. xxxvi. _et

[006-083]seq_.]

[006-084]In a widely distant part of the world, the Battaks-Karo,[A] of the high

[006-085]ground north of Lake Toba in Sumatra, believe in three classes of

[006-086]mysterious beings, one of which closely corresponds with the fairies of

[006-087][[europe|Europe]]. The first group are called [[giants|Hantous]]; they are giants and dead

[006-088]Begous (i.e. definitely dead souls), who inhabit Mount Sampouran together

[006-089]with the second group. These are called Omangs; they are dwarfs who marry

[006-090]and reproduce their species, live generally in mountains, and have their

[006-091]feet placed transversely. They must be propitiated, and those making the

[006-092]ascent of Mount Sébayak sacrifice a white hen to them, or otherwise the

[006-093]Omangs would throw stones at them. They carry off men and women, and often

[006-094]keep them for years. They love to dwell amongst stones, and the Roumah

[006-095]Omang, which is one of their favourite habitations, is a cavern. The third

[006-096]group, or Orangs Boumans, resemble ordinary beings, but have the power of

[006-097]making themselves invisible. They come down from the mountains to buy

[006-098]supplies, but have not been seen for some time. Westenberg, from whom this

[006-099]information is quoted, regards the last class as being proscribed Battaks,

[006-100]who have fled for refuge to the mountains. Passing to another continent,

[006-101]the Iroquois[B] have several stories about Pigmies, one of whom, by name

[006-102]Go-ga-ah, lives in a little cave.

 
[Footnote A: _L'Anthropologie_, iv. 83.]

 
[Footnote B: Smith, _Myths of the Iroquois_. _American Bureau of

[006-103]Ethnology_, ii. 65.]

[006-104]3. The little people may occupy a castle or house, or the hill upon which

[006-105]such a building is erected, or a cave under it. Without dwelling upon the

[006-106]Brownies and other similar distinctly household spirits, there are certain

[006-107]classes which must be mentioned in this connection. The Magyar fairies

[006-108]live in castles on lofty mountain peaks. They build them themselves, or

[006-109]inherit them from giants. Kozma enumerates the names of about twenty-three

[006-110]castles which belonged to fairies, and which still exist. Although they

[006-111]have disappeared from earth, they continue to live, even in our days, in

[006-112]caves under their castles, in which caves their treasures lie hidden. The

[006-113]iron gates of Zeta Castle, which have subsided into the ground and

[006-114]disappeared from the surface, open once in every seven years. On one

[006-115]occasion a man went in there, and met two beautiful fairies whom he

[006-116]addressed thus, "How long will you still linger here, my little sisters?"

[006-117]and they replied, "As long as the cows will give warm milk."

[006-118]Like the interior of some of the mound-dwellings already mentioned, these

[006-119]fairy caves are splendid habitations. "Their subterranean habitations are

[006-120]not less splendid and glittering than were their castles of yore on the

[006-121]mountain peaks. The one at Firtos is a palace resting on solid gold

[006-122]columns. The palace at Tartod and the gorgeous one of Dame Rapson are

[006-123]lighted by three diamond balls, as big as human heads, which hang from

[006-124]golden chains. The treasure which is heaped up in the latter place

[006-125]consists of immense gold bars, golden lions with carbuncle eyes, a golden

[006-126]hen with her brood, and golden casks, filled with gold coin. The treasures

[006-127]of Fairy Helen are kept in a cellar under Kovászna Castle, the gates of

[006-128]the cellar being guarded by a magic cock. This bird only goes to sleep

[006-129]once in seven years, and anybody who could guess the right moment would be

[006-130]able to scrape no end of diamond crystals from the walls and bring them

[006-131]out with him. The fairies who guard the treasures of the Pogányvár (Pagan

[006-132]Castle) in Marosszék even nowadays come on moonlight nights to bathe in

[006-133]the lake below."[A] In Brittany, "a number of little men, not more than a

[006-134]foot high, dwell under the castle of Morlaix. They live in holes in the

[006-135]ground, whither they may often be seen going, and beating on basins. They

[006-136]possess great treasures, which they sometimes bring out; and if any one

[006-137]pass by at the time, allow him to take one handful, but no more. Should

[006-138]any one attempt to fill his pockets, the money vanishes, and he is

[006-139]instantly assailed by a shower of boxes on the ear from invisible

[006-140]hands."[B] In the Netherlands, the "Gypnissen," "queer little women,"

[006-141]lived in a castle which had been reared in a single night.[C] The Ainu

[006-142]have tales of the Poiyaumbe, a name which means literally "little beings

[006-143]residing on the soil" (Mr. Batchelor says that "little" is probably meant

[006-144]to express endearment or admiration, but one may be allowed to doubt

[006-145]this). The Ainu, who is the hero of the story, "comes to a tall mountain

[006-146]with a beautiful house built on its summit. Descending, for his path had

[006-147]always been through the air, by the side of the house, and looking through

[006-148]the chinks of the door, he saw a little man and a little woman sitting

[006-149]beside the fireplace."[D]

 
[Footnote A: _Folk Tales of the Magyars_, p. xxxviii.]

 
[Footnote B: Grimm, apud Keightley, 441.]

 
[Footnote C: _Testimony of Tradition_, p. 86.]

 
[Footnote D: _Folk Lore Journal_, vi. 195.]

[006-150]4. The little people or fairies occupy rude stone monuments or are

[006-151]connected with their building. In Brittany they are associated with

[006-152]several of the megalithic remains.[A] "At Carnac, near Quiberon," says M.

[006-153]De Cambry, "in the department of Morbihan, on the sea-shore, is the Temple

[006-154]of Carnac, called in Breton 'Ti Goriquet' (House of the Gories), one of

[006-155]the most remarkable Celtic monuments extant. It is composed of more than

[006-156]four thousand large stones, standing erect in an arid plain, where neither

[006-157]tree nor shrub is to be seen, and not even a pebble is to be found in the

[006-158]soil on which they stand. If the inhabitants are asked concerning this

[006-159]wonderful monument, they say it is an old camp of Cæsar's, an army turned

[006-160]into stone, or that it is the work of the Crions or Gories. These they

[006-161]describe as little men between two and three feet high, who carried these

[006-162]enormous masses on their hands; for, though little, they are stronger than

[006-163]giants. Every night they dance around the stones, and woe betide the

[006-164]traveller who approaches within their reach! he is forced to join in the

[006-165]dance, where he is whirled about till, breathless and exhausted, he falls

[006-166]down, amidst the peals of laughter of the Crions. All vanish with the

[006-167]break of day. In the ruins of Tresmalouen dwell the Courils. They are of a

[006-168]malignant disposition, but great lovers of dancing. At night they sport

[006-169]around the Druidical monuments. The unfortunate shepherd that approaches

[006-170]them must dance their rounds with them till cockcrow; and the instances

[006-171]are not few of persons thus ensnared who have been found next morning dead

[006-172]with exhaustion and fatigue. Woe also to the ill-fated maiden who draws

[006-173]near the Couril dance! nine months after, the family counts one member

[006-174]more. Yet so great is the cunning and power of these dwarfs, that the

[006-175]young stranger bears no resemblance to them, but they impart to it the

[006-176]features of some lad of the village."
