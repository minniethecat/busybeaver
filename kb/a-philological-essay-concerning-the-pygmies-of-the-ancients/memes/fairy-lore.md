## Overview

Fairy Lore and Pygmy Races is the theory, most fully formulated by David MacRitchie in the late nineteenth century, that the fairies, elves, trolls, and other "little people" of European folklore are not purely imaginary beings but distorted historical memories of aboriginal pygmy races who inhabited Northern Europe before the arrival of taller Aryan invaders. Tyson devotes the entirety of Section 8 to evaluating this theory, treating it as the most significant modern contribution to the question of whether historical pigmy races underlie European fairy traditions.

## Ancient Sources

The fairy-pygmy theory draws on no direct ancient literary sources for its core claims — it is fundamentally an archaeological and folkloric argument. However, the theory engages indirectly with the classical tradition through the general question of pygmy races. Homer's account of the Pygmies and their war with the cranes (001-075), Aristotle's discussion of troglodytes and small-statured peoples, and Pliny's catalogues of monstrous races all form the backdrop against which nineteenth-century scholars asked whether diminutive races had genuinely existed in Europe. The mounds, barrows, and chambered tombs that MacRitchie identified as fairy dwellings correspond to no ancient textual tradition; rather, the theory attempts to supply a historical-ethnological explanation for traditions that the ancients themselves treated as mythological.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson presents MacRitchie's theory through the summary provided by Joseph Jacobs: "Briefly put, Mr. MacRitchie's view is that the elves, trolls, and fairies represented in popular tradition are really the mound-dwellers, whose remains have been discovered in some abundance in the form of green hillocks, which have been artificially raised over a long and low passage leading to a central chamber open to the sky" (008-056). Tyson acknowledges the suggestive power of the theory — MacRitchie had shown that in several instances, traditions about trolls or "good people" had attached themselves to mounds which, on archaeological investigation, proved to be former residences of men of smaller build than modern Europeans (008-069).

However, Tyson mounts a sustained critique, enumerating seven objections. First, there is no osteological evidence for a genuinely pigmy race in northern Scotland — the Keiss skeletons examined by Huxley and the Orkney remains studied by Dr. Garson show statures within the normal range, with females averaging about five feet two inches (008-099). Second, the mounds associated with little people are often natural or sepulchral, not habitations, and similar legends have attached to mounds in Yorkshire and even in North America, where no pigmy races ever existed (008-132). Third, the folkloric transformation of simple mound dwellings into gorgeous underground palaces decked with gold and precious stones is difficult to explain by the historical-memory theory alone (008-140). Fourth, little people in folklore are associated not only with mounds but with caves, forests, mountains, moors, underwater dwellings, and even church altars (008-145). Fifth, the frequent association and even confusion of giants and dwarfs in folklore — both dwelling in the same mounds, as at the Wunderberg near Salzburg — resists monocausal explanation (009-062). Sixth, tales of little people exist in countries that never had pigmy races, most notably among the Algonquin and other Native American peoples (009-070). Seventh, the stunted races whom MacRitchie identifies as the originals of the fairy legend — such as the Eskimo — themselves have tales of still smaller people, suggesting that such narratives arise from universal imaginative patterns rather than specific historical memories (009-075).

## Modern Perspective

Tyson's conclusion — that MacRitchie's theory "cannot be accepted as a complete explanation of the fairy question" but that it has "gone far to show that one of these mythic elements, one strand in the twisted cord of fairy mythology, is the half-forgotten memory of skulking aborigines" (009-075) — has proved remarkably prescient. Modern folklore studies reject monocausal theories of fairy origins, recognizing instead that the fairy complex draws on diverse sources: distorted memories of conquered peoples, animistic beliefs about nature spirits, Christian demonology, and psychological archetypes. The specific claim that European fairy mounds represent Neolithic chambered tombs has received archaeological support, though the connection to an actual pygmy race remains unproven. Tyson's balanced treatment, which neither dismisses MacRitchie's evidence nor accepts his reductionism, exemplifies the essay's philological method at its best.
