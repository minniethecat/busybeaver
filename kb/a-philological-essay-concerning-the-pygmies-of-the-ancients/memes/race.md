## Overview

Race (Racial Classification) is the taxonomic framework that Tyson deploys throughout the essay to categorize human varieties and to evaluate whether pygmies constitute a distinct race, a stature variant within an existing race, or no natural kind at all. The term "race" appears 107 times in the corpus — more than almost any other analytical category — reflecting its centrality to Tyson's method. For Tyson, the question "do pygmies exist?" is inseparable from the prior question "what is a race, and how do we know one when we see one?" The essay is thus as much a treatise on the concept of race itself as it is an investigation of pygmies.

## Ancient Sources

The classical world lacked the modern biological concept of race, but it possessed a rich vocabulary of ethnic and physical classification. Pliny's catalogues of "monstrous races" — Pygmies, Cynocephali, Blemmyae, Sciopodes — represent an ethnographic rather than biological taxonomy, organizing human varieties by their deviation from Mediterranean norms. Aristotle's biological works distinguish between *genos* (kind) and *eidos* (species) in ways that would later influence Linnaean taxonomy. The Hippocratic tradition ascribed physical differences between populations to climate and geography rather than to inherent racial essence — an environmental-determinist framework that Tyson both inherits and critiques.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson's use of "race" is methodologically sophisticated for its period. He does not treat race as a fixed biological essence but as a provisional taxonomic category based on convergent physical characteristics: stature, skin colour, hair form, cranial shape, and geographical distribution. When he asks whether a reported population constitutes "a race of men," he means: do these people form a breeding population with distinctive and heritable physical characteristics that distinguish them from their neighbours? This operational definition — race as a statistical clustering of physical traits within a geographically defined population — anticipates aspects of modern population genetics.

The pygmy question sharpens the definitional problem. If pygmies are simply the short members of otherwise normal populations — "a Dwarfish Race of Men; that they speak the Indian Language" — then they do not constitute a distinct race but a stature variant (013-149). If, however, they form a self-reproducing population with distinctive physical characteristics beyond mere stature — hair form, skin colour, facial proportions — then they meet Tyson's criteria for racial distinctness. The Akka evidence is decisive here precisely because Stanley and Schweinfurth described not merely short individuals but a population with consistent non-stature physical markers.

Tyson's treatment of the Negro and Negrito populations illustrates the complexity of his racial taxonomy. He notes that "Negros and Negritos" differ not only in stature but in "the facial hair" — a diagnostic criterion that distinguishes racial categories from mere size variants (003-066). The "larger Negro tribes" that surround the pygmy populations provide a control group: if pygmies differ from their neighbours across multiple physical dimensions, they are a race; if they differ only in height, they are a variety within the larger race (003-081).

The most revealing passage for Tyson's concept of race is his discussion of "a lighter colour than the Negroes, with very long arms and woolly hair" — a description that catalogues multiple independent physical traits as racial markers (003-127). The method is additive: no single trait (stature, skin colour, hair form) is decisive, but the convergence of multiple traits establishes racial distinctness.

## Modern Perspective

Tyson's operational concept of race — a geographically delimited breeding population with distinctive heritable physical characteristics — is remarkably close to the modern biological species concept as applied to human populations. The term "race" itself has become scientifically problematic since the mid-twentieth century, as genetic studies have shown that within-group variation exceeds between-group variation for most human populations. However, the specific question that Tyson investigated — whether African and Southeast Asian "pygmy" populations represent independent evolutionary convergences or descent from a common ancestral pygmy race — has been answered by modern genomics: they are independent convergences, associated with different genetic pathways affecting the insulin-like growth factor axis. Tyson's caution about "ethnological equations" — the assumption that similar appearance implies common descent — has been vindicated.
