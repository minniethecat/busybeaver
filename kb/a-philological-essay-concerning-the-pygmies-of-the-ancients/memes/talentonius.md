Johannes Talentonius was a professor at Parma whose dissertation on Pygmies, contained in his *Variarum & Reconditarum Rerum Thesaurus* (Treasury of Various and Recondite Matters), served as one of [[bartholine|Caspar Bartholine]]'s principal sources. Tyson introduces him as "a Professor formerly at Parma" (014-135) and acknowledges that Bartholine "is so much beholden" to Talentonius's work (014-160).

## Talentonius's Core Argument

Tyson records Talentonius's central thesis: *debere concedi, Pygmæos non solùm olim fuisse, sed nunc etiam superesse* — that it must be conceded that Pygmies not only existed in antiquity but survive even now (014-162). This claim, going beyond the classical sources to posit the continued existence of Pygmies into the early modern period, represents one of the most forceful pro-Pygmy positions in the entire debate.

Tyson is characteristically measured in his assessment: "tho' Talentonius be of this Opinion, yet he takes notice" of the difficulties (014-168). Talentonius acknowledged that the accounts of Pygmies by ancient writers contained inconsistencies, but argued these could be reconciled through careful philological analysis.

## Influence on Bartholine and Later Debate

Talentonius's influence on the Pygmy debate was largely mediated through Bartholine, who drew heavily on the *Thesaurus*. Tyson notes that Bartholine listed Talentonius among his key authorities alongside "Cassanius, Gellius, Pomp. Mela, and Olaus Magnus" (014-150). Later in the essay, Tyson observes that "Talentonius and Bartholine think that what Ctesias" and other ancient writers described referred to actual diminutive peoples (015-165).

In Section 17, Tyson again references "Jo. Talentonius's Dissertation" (017-144) alongside Bartholine's *Opusculum*, confirming that Talentonius's treatise was one of the foundational texts of the early modern pro-Pygmy position that Tyson felt obliged to address in his comprehensive survey.
