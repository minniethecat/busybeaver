John Aubrey was an English antiquary, natural philosopher, and pioneer
of archaeological field observation. He is cited in the editorial
footnotes to Edward Tyson's _Philological Essay Concerning the
Pygmies of the Ancients_ as a source on English fairy lore, specifically
through his _Natural History of Surrey_. (007-060)

## Folklore Documentation

The reference to Aubrey appears in the apparatus of Tyson's Section 7,
which surveys fairy traditions across the British Isles as comparative
material for the pygmy question. Aubrey's _Natural History of Surrey_
(iii. 366) is quoted via Joseph Ritson (p. 106), the English antiquary
and compiler of fairy lore. (007-060)

This chain of citation—Tyson's editors citing Ritson citing
Aubrey—reflects the layered scholarly tradition through which
seventeenth-century antiquarian observations were transmitted to late
nineteenth-century folklore studies.

Aubrey's work is significant to Tyson's essay because it represents an
early attempt to document English folk beliefs about diminutive
supernatural beings using empirical field methods. Aubrey interviewed
local informants and recorded oral traditions about fairies, practices
that anticipated the systematic folklore collection methodologies of
the later nineteenth century.

His observations of barrows, standing stones, and other prehistoric
monuments—often attributed to fairies or pygmy-like beings in local
tradition—provided material for subsequent comparative studies of the
relationship between archaeological remains and fairy mythology.
