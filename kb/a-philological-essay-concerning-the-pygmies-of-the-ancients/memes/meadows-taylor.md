Philip Meadows Taylor (1808–1876) was a British author and administrator in India whose long residence in the Deccan gave him extensive knowledge of Indian antiquities and folklore. Tyson cites Taylor's firsthand observations of Indian cromlechs and their associated dwarf traditions as comparative evidence for the MacRitchie hypothesis.

## Cromlechs and Dwarf Traditions in India

In Section 7, discussing the association of megalithic monuments with dwarf traditions, Tyson quotes Taylor directly:

> Mr. Meadows Taylor, speaking of cromlechs in India, says, "Wherever I found them, the same tradition was attached to them, that they were Morie humu, or Mories' houses; these Mories having been dwarfs who inhabited the" (007-009)

The term "Morie humu" — meaning "Mories' houses" in the local vernacular — referred to megalithic burial structures that Taylor encountered across the Indian landscape. The tradition that these monuments were the dwellings of dwarfs paralleled the European folk belief that prehistoric barrows and cromlechs were inhabited by fairies or "little people," a correspondence central to MacRitchie's cross-cultural argument.

Taylor's account was particularly valuable for Tyson because it came from a direct observer rather than a compiler of secondary sources. Taylor's decades of residence in India, during which he studied local antiquities, lent authority to his testimony. The Indian evidence, alongside European and Melanesian parallels, reinforced the pattern: wherever megalithic monuments existed, local traditions often associated them with diminutive supernatural or semi-historical beings.

## Literary and Administrative Career

Taylor is best known as the author of *Confessions of a Thug* (1839), a sensational novel about the Thuggee cult that was widely read in Victorian Britain and helped shape British perceptions of India. His autobiographical work *The Story of My Life* (1877) documented his experiences in Indian administration and his antiquarian interests. His contributions to Indian archaeology and folklore studies, though less well-known than his fiction, provided valuable comparative data for scholars like Tyson who sought to trace global patterns in folklore and tradition.
