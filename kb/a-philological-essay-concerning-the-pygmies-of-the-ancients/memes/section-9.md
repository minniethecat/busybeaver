# Section 9

[009-001]Seah on enta geweorc, Looked on the giant's work,

[009-002]hû ða stân-bogan, how the stone arches,

[009-003]stapulinn-faeste, on pillars fast,

[009-004]êce eorð-reced the eternal earth-house

[009-005]innan healde. held within.

 
[Footnote A: _Folk Lore_, i. 130.]

 
[Footnote B: _Flint Chips_, p. 412.]

[009-006]The mounds have sometimes been made by giants and afterwards inhabited by

[009-007]dwarfs, as in the case of the Nine-hills, already alluded to. In others,

[009-008]they are at the same time inhabited by giants, dwarfs, and others, as in

[009-009]the story of the Dwarf's Banquet,[A] and still more markedly in the

[009-010]Wunderberg. "The celebrated Wunderberg, or Underberg, on the great moor

[009-011]near Salzburg, is the chief haunt of the Wild-women. The Wunderberg is

[009-012]said to be quite hollow, and supplied with stately palaces, churches,

[009-013]monasteries, gardens, and springs of gold and silver. Its inhabitants,

[009-014]beside the Wild-women, are little men, who have charge of the treasures it

[009-015]contains, and who at midnight repair to Salzburg to perform their

[009-016]devotions in the cathedral; giants, who used to come to the church of

[009-017]Grödich and exhort the people to lead a godly and pious life; and the

[009-018]great Emperor Charles V., with golden crown and sceptre, attended by

[009-019]knights and lords. His grey beard has twice encompassed the table at which

[009-020]he sits, and when it has the third time grown round it, the end of the

[009-021]world and the appearance of the Antichrist will take place."[B]

 
[Footnote A: Grimm ap. Keightley, 130.]

 
[Footnote B: Grimm ap. Keightley, 234.]

[009-022]In the folk-tales of the Magyars we meet with a still more remarkable

[009-023]confusion between these two classes of beings. Some of the castles

[009-024]described in these stories are inhabited by giants, others by fairies.

[009-025]Again, the giants marry; their wives are fairies, so are their daughters.

[009-026]They had no male issue, as their race was doomed to extermination. They

[009-027]fall in love, and are fond of courting. Near Bikkfalva, in Háromszék, the

[009-028]people still point out the "Lover's Bench" on a rock where the amorous

[009-029]giant of Csigavár used to meet his sweetheart, the "fairy of

[009-030]Veczeltetö."[A]

 
[Footnote A: _Folk Tales of the Magyars_, p. xxix.]

[009-031](6.) Tales of little people are to be found in countries where there never

[009-032]were any Pigmy races. Not to deal with other, and perhaps more debatable

[009-033]districts, we find an excellent example of this in North America. Besides

[009-034]the instances mentioned in the foregoing section, the following may be

[009-035]mentioned. Mr. Leland, speaking of the Un-a-games-suk, or Indian spirits

[009-036]of the rocks and streams, says that these beings enter far more largely,

[009-037]deeply, and socially into the life and faith of the Indians than elves or

[009-038]fairies ever did into those of the Aryan race.[A] In his Algonquin Legends

[009-039]the same author also alludes to small people.

 
[Footnote A: _Memoirs_, i. 34.]

[009-040]Dr. Brinton tells me that the Micmacs have tales of similar Pigmies, whom

[009-041]they call Wig[)u]l[)a]d[)u]mooch, who tie people with cords during their

[009-042]sleep, &c. Mr. L.L. Frost, of Susanville, Lassen County, California, tells

[009-043]us how, when he requested an Indian to gather and bring in all the

[009-044]arrow-points he could find, the Indian declared them to be "no good," that

[009-045]they had been made by the lizards. Whereupon Mr. Frost drew from him the

[009-046]following lizard-story. "There was a time when the lizards were little

[009-047]men, and the arrow-points which are now found were shot by them at the

[009-048]grizzly bear. The bears could talk then, and would eat the little men

[009-049]whenever they could catch them. The arrows of the little men were so small

[009-050]that they would not kill the bears when shot into them, and only served to

[009-051]enrage them." The Indian could not tell how the little men became

[009-052]transformed into lizards.[A] Again, the Shoshones of California dread

[009-053]their infants being changed by Ninumbees or dwarfs.[B]

 
[Footnote A: _Folk Lore Journal_, vii. 24.]

 
[Footnote B: Hartland, _ut supra_, p. 351.]

[009-054]Finally, every one has read about the Pukwudjies, "the envious little

[009-055]people, the fairies, the pigmies," in the pages of Longfellow's

[009-056]"Hiawatha."[A] It ought to be mentioned that Mr. Leland states that the

[009-057]red-capped, scanty-shirted elf of the Algonquins was obtained from the

[009-058]Norsemen; but if, as he says, the idea of little people has sunk so deeply

[009-059]into the Indian mind, it cannot in any large measure have been derived

[009-060]from this source.[B]

 
[Footnote A: xviii.]

 
[Footnote B: _Etrusco Roman Remains_, p. 162.]

[009-061](7.) The stunted races whom Mr. MacRitchie considers to have formed the

[009-062]subjects of the fairy legend have themselves tales of little people. This

[009-063]is true especially of the Eskimo, as will have been already noticed, a

[009-064]fact to which my attention was called by Mr. Hartland.

[009-065]For the reasons just enumerated, I am unable to accept Mr. MacRitchie's

[009-066]theory as a complete explanation of the fairy question, but I am far from

[009-067]desirous of under-estimating the value and significance of his work. Mr.

[009-068]Tylor, as I have already mentioned, states, in a sentence which may yet

[009-069]serve as a motto for a work on the whole question of the origin of the

[009-070]fairy myth, that "various different facts have given rise to stories of

[009-071]giants and dwarfs, more than one mythic element perhaps combining to form

[009-072]a single legend--a result perplexing in the extreme to the mythological

[009-073]interpreter."[A] And I think it may be granted that Mr. MacRitchie has

[009-074]gone far to show that one of these mythic elements, one strand in the

[009-075]twisted cord of fairy mythology, is the half-forgotten memory of skulking

[009-076]aborigines, or, as Mr. Nutt well puts it, the "distorted recollections of

[009-077]alien and inimical races." But it is not the only one. It is far from

[009-078]being my intention to endeavour to deal exhaustively with the difficult

[009-079]question of the origin of fairy tales. Knowledge and the space permissible

[009-080]in an introduction such as this would alike fail me in such a task. It

[009-081]may, however, be permissible to mention a few points which seem to impress

[009-082]themselves upon one in making a study of the stories with which I have

[009-083]been dealing. In the first place, one can scarcely fail to notice how much

[009-084]in common there is between the tales of the little people and the accounts

[009-085]of that underground world, which, with so many races, is the habitation of

[009-086]the souls of the departed. Dr. Callaway has already drawn attention to

[009-087]this point in connection with the ancestor-worship of the Amazulu.[B] He

[009-088]says, "It may be worth while to note the curious coincidence of thought

[009-089]among the Amazulu regarding the Amatongo or Abapansi, and that of the

[009-090]Scotch and Irish regarding the fairies or 'good people.' For instance, the

[009-091]'good people' of the Irish have assigned to them, in many respects the

[009-092]same motives and actions as the Amatongo. They call the living to join

[009-093]them, that is, by death; they cause disease which common doctors cannot

[009-094]understand nor cure; they have their feelings, interests, partialities,

[009-095]and antipathies, and contend with each other about the living. The common

[009-096]people call them their friends or people, which is equivalent to the term

[009-097]_abakubo_ given to the Amatongo. They reveal themselves in the form of the

[009-098]dead, and it appears to be supposed that the dead become 'good people,' as

[009-099]the dead among the Amazulu become Amatongo; and in funeral processions of

[009-100]the 'good people' which some have professed to see, are recognised the

[009-101]forms of those who have just died, as Umkatshana saw his relatives amongst

[009-102]the Abapansi. The power of holding communion with the 'good people' is

[009-103]consequent on an illness, just as the power to divine amongst the natives

[009-104]of this country. So also in the Highland tales, a boy who had been carried

[009-105]away by the fairies, on his return to his own home speaks of them as 'our

[009-106]folks,' which is equivalent to _abakwetu_, applied to the Amatongo, and

[009-107]among the Highlands they are called the 'good people' and 'the folk.' They

[009-108]are also said to 'live underground,' and are therefore Abapansi or

[009-109]subterranean. They are also, like the Abapansi, called ancestors. Thus the

[009-110]Red Book of Clanranald is said not to have been dug up, but to have been

[009-111]found on the moss; it seemed as if the ancestors sent it." There are other

[009-112]points which make in the same direction. The soul is supposed by various

[009-113]races to be a little man, an idea which at once links the manes of the

[009-114]departed with Pigmy people. Thus Dr. Nansen tells us that amongst the

[009-115]Eskimo a man has many souls. The largest dwell in the larynx and in the

[009-116]left side, and are tiny men about the size of a sparrow. The other souls

[009-117]dwell in other parts of the body, and are the size of a finger-joint.[C]

[009-118]And the Macusi Indians[D] believe that although the body will decay, "the

[009-119]man in our eyes" will not die, but wander about; an idea which is met with

[009-120]even in [[europe|Europe]], and which perhaps gives us a clue to the conception of

[009-121]smallness in size of the shades of the dead. Again, the belief that the

[009-122]soul lives near the resting-place of its body is widespread, and at least

[009-123]comparable with, if not equivalent to, the idea that the little people of

[009-124]Scotland, Ireland, Brittany, and [[india|India]] live in the sepulchral mounds or

[009-125]cromlechs of those countries. Closely connected with this is the idea of

[009-126]the underground world, peopled by the souls of the departed like the

[009-127]Abapansi, the widespread nature of which idea is shown by Dr. Tylor. "To

[009-128]take one example, in which the more limited idea seems to have preceded

[009-129]the more extensive, the Finns,[E] who feared the ghost of the departed as

[009-130]unkind, harmful beings, fancied them dwelling with their bodies in the

[009-131]grave, or else, with what Castrén thinks a later philosophy, assigned them

[009-132]their dwelling in the subterranean Tuonela. Tuonela was like this upper

[009-133]earth; the sun shone there, there was no lack of land and water, wood and

[009-134]field, tilth and meadow; there were bears and wolves, snakes and pike, but

[009-135]all things were of a hurtful, dismal kind; the woods dark and swarming

[009-136]with wild beasts, the water black, the cornfields bearing seed of snake's

[009-137]teeth; and there stern, pitiless old Tuoni, and his grim wife and son,

[009-138]with the hooked fingers with iron points, kept watch and ward over the

[009-139]dead lest they should escape."

 
[Footnote A: _Primitive Culture_, i. 388.]

 
[Footnote B: _Religious System of the Amazulu_, p. 226.]

 
[Footnote C: Nansen, _ut supra_, p. 227.]

 
[Footnote D: Tylor, _ut supra_, i. 431.]

 
[Footnote E: Tylor, _ut supra_, ii. 80.]

[009-140]It is impossible not to see a connection between such conceptions as these

[009-141]and the underground habitations of the little people entered by the green

[009-142]mound which covered the bones of the dead. But the underground world was

[009-143]not only associated with the shades of the departed; it was in many parts

[009-144]of the world the place whence races had their origin, and here also we

[009-145]meet in at least one instance known to me with the conception of a little

[009-146]folk. A very widespread legend in Europe, and especially in Scandinavia,

[009-147]according to Dr. Nansen, tells how the underground or invisible people

[009-148]came into existence. "The Lord one day paid a visit to Eve as she was busy

[009-149]washing her children. All those who were not yet washed she hurriedly hid

[009-150]in cellars and corners and under big vessels, and presented the others to

[009-151]the Visitor. The Lord asked if these were all, and she answered 'Yes;'

[009-152]whereupon He replied, 'Then those which are _dulde_ (hidden) shall remain

[009-153]_hulde_ (concealed, invisible). And from them the huldre-folk are

[009-154]sprung."[A] There is also the widespread story of an origin underground,

[009-155]as amongst the Wasabe, a sub-gens of the Omahas, who believe that their

[009-156]ancestors were made under the earth and subsequently came to the

[009-157]surface.[B] There is a similar story amongst the Z[=u]nis of Western New

[009-158]Mexico. In journeying to their present place of habitation, they passed

[009-159]through four worlds, all in the interior of this, the passage way from

[009-160]darkness to light being through a large reed. From the inner world they

[009-161]were led by the two little war-gods, Ah-ai-[=u]-ta and M[=a]-[=a]-s[=e]-we,

[009-162]twin brothers, sons of the Sun, who were sent by the Sun to bring this

[009-163]people to his presence.[C] From these stories it would appear that the

[009-164]underground world, whether looked upon as the habitation of the dead or

[009-165]the place of origination of nations, is connected with the conception of

[009-166]little races and people. That it is thus responsible for some portion of

[009-167]the conception of fairies seems to me to be more than probable.
