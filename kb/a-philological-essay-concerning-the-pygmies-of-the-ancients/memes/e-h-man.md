Edward Horace Man (1846–1929) was a British anthropologist and ethnographer whose work on the Andaman Islanders became a foundational reference in nineteenth-century comparative anthropology. His meticulous field observations — covering physical anthropology, social customs, language, and religious beliefs — were cited by Edward Tyson as authoritative evidence for understanding the Negrito populations of the Andaman Islands. Tyson acknowledges that "knowledge of the Andamanese blacks is extensive, thanks to the labours in particular of Mr. Man" (001-134).

## Physical Anthropology of the Andaman Islanders

Man's measurements of Andamanese stature provided key data for Tyson's discussion of human height variation. The essay records that "the average stature of the males of this race is four feet six inches, the height of a boy of ten years of age" (001-137). This diminutive stature placed the Andamanese among the shortest known human populations and made them a crucial comparative case for Tyson's broader argument about pygmy races. Man further observed that, "like children, the head is relatively large in comparison" with body size — a characteristic that echoed classical descriptions of pygmy proportions (001-138).

## Social Customs and Constancy

Man's ethnographic observations extended to social behaviour and moral character. Tyson approvingly quotes Man's assessment that Andamanese "men and women are models of constancy" (002-004). This observation supported Tyson's implicit argument that so-called primitive peoples possessed moral virtues that challenged contemporary European assumptions about savagery. The constancy Man described in marital relations stood in notable contrast to stereotypes about the promiscuity of uncivilised peoples.

## Religious Beliefs

Man also documented the religious conceptions of the Andaman Islanders in some detail. According to his informants, the Andamanese believe in a Supreme Deity of whom they say "although He resembles fire, He is invisible; that He was never born, and is immortal; that He created" all things (002-005). This relatively sophisticated monotheistic conception — involving an invisible, uncreated, immortal creator — was a significant ethnographic finding that challenged contemporary theories about the evolution of religious ideas from crude animism to monotheism.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Man's work served Tyson's argument in two distinct ways. First, it provided reliable anthropometric data establishing the existence of populations with mean statures well below the European norm. Second, and perhaps more importantly, Man's descriptions of Andamanese social sophistication — their marital fidelity and elevated religious conceptions — countered the prejudice that short-statured peoples were necessarily intellectually or morally inferior. This was central to Tyson's broader project of taking the ancient pygmy accounts seriously as descriptions of real human populations.

## Key Work Cited

- **"On the Aboriginal Inhabitants of the Andaman Islands"** — Published in the Journal of the Anthropological Institute, this study provided the most detailed nineteenth-century account of Andamanese physical anthropology, customs, and beliefs. Tyson drew on it through De Quatrefages' summary.
