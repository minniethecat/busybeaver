Theodorus Gaza (c. 1415–1475) was a Greek humanist and translator active in Italy during the Renaissance. A native of Thessalonica, he was one of the Greek scholars who brought classical learning to the Latin West after the fall of Constantinople. His Latin translations of Aristotle were widely used by early modern scholars, including Edward Tyson and his contemporaries.

## Gaza's Translation of Aristotle

Tyson's engagement with Gaza centres on a single passage in Aristotle's *Historia Animalium* (Book 8, Chapter 12), where Aristotle discusses the migration of cranes. Tyson presents the Greek text and then gives "Theodorus Gaza's Translation" (013-054). The passage in Greek describes small-statured beings — but Gaza's Latin version, Tyson charges, goes significantly beyond the original.

## Tyson's Critique

Tyson is sharply critical of Gaza's rendering. He writes that "Theodorus Gaza has been unjust in translating him, by foisting in, *Quo in loco pugnare cum Pygmæis dicuntur*, whereas there is nothing in the Text that warrants it" (013-085). Gaza inserted a reference to fighting cranes that Aristotle never mentioned. Furthermore, where Aristotle used the neutral Greek phrase for "a small race of both men and horses," Gaza's Latin — *Sed certè Genus tum Hominum, tum etiam Equorum pusillum* — makes them explicitly "men," a distinction Tyson considers consequential for the entire Pygmy debate.

Tyson further laments that "neither Casaubon nor Duvall in their Editions of Aristotle's Works, should have taken notice of these Mistakes of Gaza, and corrected them. And Gesner, and Aldrovandus, and several other Learned Men, in quoting this place of Aristotle, do make use of this faultly Translation" (013-095). In Tyson's view, Gaza's translation errors propagated through the entire Renaissance and early modern scholarly tradition, misleading generations of naturalists.

## Acknowledged by Later Scholars

Tyson notes that Gaza's mistranslation did not go entirely unnoticed. He observes that Johannes Talentonius, though generally favourable to the Pygmy account, "takes notice of the faulty Translation of this Text of Aristotle by Gaza" (014-092). However, most early modern authorities cited the passage through Gaza's Latin without consulting the Greek original, perpetuating the error.

## Place in the Scholarly Tradition

Gaza's role in the Pygmy question is indirect but significant: his widely-used Latin Aristotle provided the textual foundation on which much Renaissance natural history rested. Tyson's critique of Gaza is part of his broader argument that the ancient evidence for Pygmies as a distinct race of miniature men is far weaker than commonly believed — and that translation errors are partly to blame for the confusion.
