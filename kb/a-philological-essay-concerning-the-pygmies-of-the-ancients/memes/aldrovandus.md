Ulisse Aldrovandi (1522–1605) was an Italian naturalist and physician who established one of the first natural history museums in Europe. Often called the "father of natural history studies," his illustrated volumes on birds, insects, and other animals were among the most comprehensive works of Renaissance science.

Tyson cites Aldrovandi four times in his essay, positioning him among the learned men who grappled with — and ultimately rejected — the classical accounts of Pygmies.

## Denial of the Ape Pygmies

Tyson records that Aldrovandi "is so far from believing his Ape Pygmies ever spoke, that he utterly denies, that there were ever any such Creatures in being" (013-004). This sentence crystallizes Aldrovandi's position on both central questions of the Pygmy debate: whether the beings could speak, and whether they existed at all.

Aldrovandi's categorical denial on both counts placed him in opposition to those Renaissance scholars who entertained the possibility of a genuine race of Pygmy humans. The footnote cites "Ulys. Aldrovandi Ornitholog. lib. 20. p.m. 344."

## Correcting Aristotle

Tyson names Aldrovandi alongside Gesner as one of the learned men who made use of a particular reading of Aristotle's *History of Animals* concerning the Pygmies (013-107). These scholars interpreted Aristotle's description as referring to a diminutive human race rather than to any species of ape.

Tyson also lists Aldrovandi among "several, as Cardan, Budæus, Aldrovandus, Fullerus and others" who held particular views on the Pygmy question (015-119).

## Aldrovandi and Saint Augustine

Tyson questions the reasoning of certain scholars:

> But why Aldrovandus or Caspar Bartholine should bring in St. Austin as a Favourer of this Opinion of Men Pygmies, I see no Reason.
> (016-035)

Tyson argues that nothing in Augustine's writings justifies belief in Pygmy men. Aldrovandi thus appears as part of a scholarly tradition that Tyson believes misread the ancient authorities — even when Aldrovandi himself rejected Pygmy existence.
