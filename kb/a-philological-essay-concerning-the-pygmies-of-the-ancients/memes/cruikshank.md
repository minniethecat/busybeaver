# George Cruikshank

**George Cruikshank** (1792–1878) was a British illustrator and caricaturist, known for his prolific work in book illustration and social satire. In Tyson's [[A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients]], Cruikshank is cited for his artistic depiction of a fairy mound, which served as the frontispiece to [[thomas-keightley]]'s influential volume on fairy mythology (006-035–006-036).

## Connection to Fairy Lore

Tyson references Cruikshank in [[section-6]], which examines the fairy mythology of Northern Europe and its possible connections to ancient traditions about diminutive peoples. The passage describes fairy hills (mounds) across various European landscapes:

> "[006-034]This feature is met with in several of the stories collected by Keightley, and is made use of in Cruikshank's picture, which forms the frontispiece to that volume." (006-035–006-036)

The picture in question depicts a fairy hill raised on pillars, with dancing and merriment beneath—a motif drawn from Danish folklore (the Dance-hills of Bubbelgaard in Funen) and common across Germanic and Celtic fairy traditions.

## The Keightley Connection

Cruikshank's frontispiece appeared in [[thomas-keightley]]'s _Fairy Mythology_, a comprehensive 19th-century survey of European fairy beliefs. Keightley's work gathered folk traditions from across Europe and attempted to trace their origins, including the possibility that fairy beliefs preserved dim memories of encounters with small-statured peoples—a theory that Tyson explores at length in his essay.

## Significance for Tyson's Argument

Within Tyson's philological project, Cruikshank's illustration serves a dual function:

1. **Documentary evidence**: The frontispiece visually attests to the pervasiveness of fairy-hill imagery in European folklore, which Tyson connects to ancient traditions about subterranean or mound-dwelling diminutive races.

2. **Cultural transmission**: The collaboration between Keightley (folklorist) and Cruikshank (illustrator) exemplifies how fairy lore was collected, systematized, and disseminated in the 19th century—the intellectual milieu within which Tyson developed his pygmy thesis.

Tyson's [[section-6]] weaves together folklore, archaeology, and classical sources to argue that European fairy traditions may preserve genuine ethnographic memory of encounters with small-statured peoples, rather than being purely imaginary creations.
