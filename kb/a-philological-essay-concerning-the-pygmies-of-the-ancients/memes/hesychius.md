Hesychius of Alexandria was a Greek grammarian and lexicographer who compiled a comprehensive lexicon of rare and obscure Greek words, probably in the fifth or sixth century AD. His *Lexicon* is one of the most important surviving sources for the study of ancient Greek dialects, obscure vocabulary, and citations from lost works of classical literature.

## Hesychius and the Pygmy Question

Hesychius enters Tyson's essay in Section 16 through the arguments of Samuel Bochartus. Bochartus, seeking to locate the Homeric Pygmies in Africa, drew upon Hesychius's lexicon as linguistic evidence for his identification of the Pygmies with the Nubae of northeastern Africa:

> "The Authority of _Hesychius_, who saith, [Greek: Noboi Pygmaioi]."
> (016-061)

The Greek phrase recorded by Hesychius — *Noboi Pygmaioi* — is cited by Bochartus as the first of five reasons for equating the Pygmies with the Nubae. In the Hesychian gloss, *Noboi* (or *Nubae*) is equated with *Pygmaioi*, providing a lexical bridge between the two ethnonyms and allowing Bochartus to relocate the pygmy tradition from India and Thrace to the region south of Egypt.

## Tyson's Response

Tyson treats the Hesychian evidence with considerable caution. He acknowledges the lexical connection but questions whether it actually supports Bochartus's larger argument:

> "And if the [Greek: Noboi Pygmaioi] in _Hesychius_ are such _Pygmies_ as _Bochartus_ makes his _Nubæ, Quos tamen absit ut putemus staturâ fuisse Cubitali_, it will not do our business at all."
> (016-099)

Tyson's Latin quotation from Bochartus concedes that the Nubae were unlikely to have been of cubital (dwarf) stature. Tyson thus argues that even if the Hesychian gloss establishes an etymological connection between "Noboi" and "Pygmies," it does not prove that the Nubae possessed the diminutive stature characteristic of the Homeric Pygmies. The lexical evidence, in Tyson's view, is insufficient to resolve the geographical and anthropological questions at stake.

## Role in the African Theory

The Hesychian gloss played an important role in the early modern debate over the location of the Pygmies. Bochartus's use of Hesychius was part of a larger philological argument that sought to anchor the Pygmies in Africa rather than Asia. Hesychius's lexicon, with its preservation of rare dialect words and obscure ethnonyms, provided exactly the kind of linguistic data that seventeenth-century philologists prized for reconstructing ancient geography and ethnography.
