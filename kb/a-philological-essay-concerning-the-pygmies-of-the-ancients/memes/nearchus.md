# Nearchus

**Nearchus** (also Nearchos; c. 360–300 BC) was a Greek admiral and historian who served under [[alexander-the-great]]. He is best known for commanding the fleet that sailed from the Indus River to the Persian Gulf, and for writing a lost work on India (_Indica_). In Tyson's [[A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients]], Nearchus appears in a passage from [[strabo]] that critiques the reliability of classical writers on India (011-074).

## The Strabo Passage

In [[section-11]], Tyson quotes Strabo's assessment of Greek historians who wrote about India. Strabo ranked these writers by their degree of fabulousness:

> "[011-071]Megasthenaes, Onaesikritos te kai Nearchos, kai alloi toioutoi; i.e. All who have wrote of India for the most part, are fabulous, but in the highest degree Daimachus; then Megasthenes, Onesicritus, and Nearchus, and such like." (011-071–011-074)

Strabo's ranking places Nearchus in the second tier of unreliable writers—less fabulous than Daimachus (the worst offender) but still prone to including legendary material in his account of India.

## Role in Alexander's Expedition

Nearchus served as a companion (_hetairos_) of Alexander the Great and was appointed admiral of the fleet during the Indian campaign (c. 326–325 BC). His voyage from the Indus delta to the Euphrates, recorded in his _Indica_, was one of the great feats of ancient navigation. The work described the geography, peoples, and natural history of the regions along the Indian Ocean coast.

Although Nearchus's original text is lost, his observations were preserved through citations in later authors, particularly Strabo and [[arrian]], who drew on his accounts for their own geographical writings.

## Significance for the Pygmy Question

Nearchus's presence in Tyson's essay serves a methodological purpose. By quoting Strabo's critique of the Greek writers on India, Tyson demonstrates:

1. **Ancient awareness of source reliability**: Classical authors themselves recognized that some writers on distant lands were unreliable, showing critical discernment in their use of sources.

2. **The problem of fabulous India**: India was, in classical geography, a repository of marvels and monstrous races—including [[pygmies]]. Strabo's ranking of writers by veracity shows that the boundary between ethnography and myth was already contested in antiquity.

3. **Methodological caution**: Tyson uses this passage to frame his own careful sifting of classical testimonies about diminutive peoples, distinguishing credible reports from obvious fabrications.

Nearchus thus appears not as a direct source on pygmies but as part of Tyson's broader argument about the need for critical evaluation of classical authorities on distant peoples.
