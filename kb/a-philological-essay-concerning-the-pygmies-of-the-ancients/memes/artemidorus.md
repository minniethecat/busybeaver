Artemidorus of Ephesus (c. 100 BC) was a Greek geographer who wrote a comprehensive geographical work (*Geographoumena*) in eleven books, describing the known world from Iberia to India. His writings on India, though now lost, were preserved through the *Naturalis Historia* of Pliny the Elder, who cited Artemidorus as an authority on Indian geography and ethnography.

## Artemidorus in Pliny's Account

Tyson encounters Artemidorus through Pliny's discussion of the Calingae, a people of India said to have remarkably short lifespans. Pliny cites Artemidorus as a source for this claim, which Cardan had adduced as a parallel to the Pygmies:

> "instances out of _Artemidorus_ in _Pliny_, as a _Parallel_ in the _Calingæ_ a Nation in _India_, where the Women conceive when five years old, and do not live above eight."
> (015-128)

Tyson rejects this comparison, arguing that the Calingae account describes short-lived human beings, not a race of diminutive stature, and therefore does not support Cardan's argument that the Pygmies should be classed with apes rather than men.

## Tyson's Skeptical Assessment

Tyson's evaluation of Artemidorus is markedly critical. Rather than accepting his testimony at face value, Tyson dismisses it as belonging to a class of unreliable "romances" about India:

> "_Artemidorus_ being an _Indian Historian_, and in the same place telling other Romances, the less Credit is to be given to him."
> (015-134)

This dismissal is significant for understanding Tyson's methodology. When an ancient authority reports marvels about distant lands, Tyson applies a credibility test: if the same author relates multiple improbable tales, all of his testimony becomes suspect. Artemidorus, by including what Tyson considers fanciful material alongside his geographical observations, undermines his own reliability as a witness in the pygmy debate.

## Significance in the Pygmy Debate

Artemidorus occupies an ambiguous position in the classical tradition on India. His geographical work was respected enough to be used by both Pliny and Strabo, yet his inclusion of ethnographic marvels made him vulnerable to the skepticism of later critics like Tyson. His case illustrates a recurring problem in the pygmy debate: ancient authors on India frequently mixed genuine geographical knowledge with hearsay and legend, making it difficult for later scholars to separate credible testimony from fiction.
