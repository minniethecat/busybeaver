## Overview

Skeletal Evidence (Osteological Evidence) is the category of physical proof for pygmy races derived from excavated human remains — skeletons, skulls, and individual bones. Unlike the textual testimony of classical authors or the anecdotal reports of travellers, skeletal evidence offers material proof that can be measured, compared, and re-examined by independent investigators. Tyson treats osteology as the most reliable form of evidence for the pygmy question because "the bones testify" in ways that words cannot — a skeleton cannot exaggerate, misremember, or fabricate (003-144).

## Ancient Sources

The ancient world had no systematic osteology. However, the recovery of giant bones — in reality, the fossilized remains of Pleistocene megafauna — was occasionally reported in classical literature and interpreted as evidence for the former existence of giant races. The absence of corresponding "pygmy bones" in the ancient record is itself significant: it suggests that the classical tradition of pygmy races was not based on the discovery of anomalously small skeletons, in contrast to the giant tradition which was partially stimulated by fossil discoveries.

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson draws on three categories of osteological evidence. First, the **Keiss skeletons** from Caithness, Scotland, examined by Professor Thomas Henry Huxley. These remains, recovered from burial mounds associated with fairy traditions, showed statures within the normal modern range — "the skeletons attributed to the most ancient" inhabitants of Britain were not pygmies but people of ordinary height (004-011). Huxley's analysis that the Keiss skeletons showed "about five feet two inches" (for females) placed them below modern British averages but well within the normal human range, not in pygmy territory. Tyson cites this evidence to qualify MacRitchie's claim that the mound-dwellers were a distinct pygmy race.

Second, the **Neolithic skeletal evidence** from continental Europe. "The skeletons attributed to the most ancient" populations of Switzerland, France, and Germany showed statures consistently below modern norms, with the Schaffhausen group described as Neolithic pigmies. Tyson notes that "a careful study of the bones of" these populations reveals statures at the lower end of the modern range — significant but not extraordinary (004-009). The long bones in particular provide reliable stature estimates, and "the long bones, being little more than five" feet in estimated living stature, place these populations in the ambiguous zone between small-normal and genuinely pygmoid (003-160).

Third, the **barrow burials** associated with fairy traditions. "Found to contain the skeleton of a man wearing a golden coronal" — the Bryn-yr-Ellyllon barrow in Wales, excavated in 1832, yielded a skeleton of ordinary stature accompanied by high-status grave goods, contradicting the expectation that fairy mounds would contain pygmy remains (005-164). Tyson uses this evidence to argue that "not, in many cases, been habitations, but were natural or sepulchral" mounds — the fairy association is with the mound, not with the stature of its occupant (008-125).

Tyson's conclusion from the osteological evidence is characteristically nuanced: skeletal remains confirm the existence of populations somewhat shorter than modern Europeans in prehistoric Europe, but they do not confirm the existence of a genuinely pygmy race — a population with adult statures consistently below four feet six inches. The bone evidence narrows the range of plausible pygmy statures without closing the question.

## Modern Perspective

Tyson's osteological analysis has been superseded by a vastly richer fossil and skeletal record. The discovery of Homo floresiensis in 2003 — an adult hominin with a stature of approximately three feet six inches and a cranial capacity of 380 cc — provides the first genuine skeletal evidence for a pygmy-sized hominin population. The Flores "hobbits" inhabited Liang Bua cave from approximately 100,000 to 50,000 years ago, overlapping with modern humans in the region. Whether traditions of these diminutive hominins survived into the historical period and influenced the classical pygmy tradition remains an open and intensely debated question — one that Tyson, with his commitment to multiple converging lines of evidence, would have relished.
