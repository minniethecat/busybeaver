# Franz Boas

Franz Boas (1858–1942) was a German-American anthropologist and a foundational figure in modern American anthropology. Best known for his work on the Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest and his critique of scientific racism, Boas also produced a seminal ethnographic study of the Central Eskimo (Inuit), in which he documented dwarf legends cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] in his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*.

## The Uissuit — Arctic Dwarf Spirits

In Section 8 of his essay, where Tyson surveys dwarfish races and legends from northern latitudes, he invokes Boas's authority on Inuit dwarf traditions. Describing the spirit-beings of Eskimo folklore, Tyson writes:

> The spirits here alluded to appear to be the same as those described by Mr. Boas as Uissuit in his monograph on the Central Eskimo. (008-009–008-013)

Tyson elaborates on Boas's description of these beings:

> He describes them as "a strange people that live in the sea. They are dwarfs, and are frequently seen between Iglulik and Netchillik, where the Anganidjen live, an Innuit tribe whose women are in the habit of tracing rings around their eyes. There are men and women among them." (008-014–008-020)

The Uissuit, as documented by Boas, represent an Arctic variant of the worldwide motif of dwarf or fairy races — beings believed to inhabit liminal spaces (the sea, in this case) and to interact occasionally with human communities.

## Context in Comparative Mythology

Tyson's inclusion of Boas's Uissuit data reflects the essay's comparative method: by assembling dwarf legends from the Arctic (Boas), [[india|India]] ([[walhouse|Walhouse]]), [[japan|Japan]] ([[batchelor|Batchelor]]), and [[africa|Africa]], Tyson constructs a global ethnography of pygmy belief. Boas's testimony is particularly valuable because it comes from a rigorous ethnographic observer whose fieldwork standards were among the most exacting of the late nineteenth century.

## Legacy

Boas would go on to become the most influential American anthropologist of the early twentieth century, training a generation of scholars including [[alfred-kroeber|Alfred Kroeber]], [[ruth-benedict|Ruth Benedict]], and [[margaret-mead|Margaret Mead]]. His brief appearance in Tyson's essay, documenting a minor element of Eskimo folklore, is a footnote to a career that transformed the discipline of anthropology — yet it illustrates the breadth of ethnographic data that the pygmy question attracted from even the most serious scholars of the period.
