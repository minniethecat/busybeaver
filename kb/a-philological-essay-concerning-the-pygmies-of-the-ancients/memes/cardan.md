Geronimo Cardano (1501–1576), known in Latin as Hieronymus Cardanus and in English as Jerome Cardan, was an Italian Renaissance polymath — physician, mathematician, astrologer, and natural philosopher. Tyson cites Cardan frequently in his essay, primarily from *De Rerum Varietate* (*On the Variety of Things*) and *De Subtilitate* (*On Subtlety*). Cardan serves as one of the principal skeptical voices against the reality of the ancient Pygmies.

## Cardan's Skepticism

Tyson presents Cardan as a doubter who "tho' he allows there may be some Men much less than others, yet esteems the Story a Miracle, not a Nation: *Consequentiam ut firmam, admittit Cardanus, licet de Pygmæis hoc tantùm concedat haberi posse pro miraculo, non pro Gente*" (015-032). In other words, Cardan concedes individual dwarfs exist but rejects the idea of an entire race of miniature people.

Tyson notes that Cardan argued that the Pygmy story was geographically implausible. "Cardan therefore must be very much out in his Computation" is Tyson's rejoinder to Cardan's argument that the Pygmies' reported location would be impossible (013-094). The footnote specifies: "*Cardan de Rerum varietate*, lib. 8" (013-101).

## Cardan's Observations and Objections

Cardan is also cited as a witness to actual cases of small-statured individuals. Tyson records that "Cardan tells us he saw one" example of an "under-sized" person (014-093), drawn from *De Subtilitate* (014-108). This firsthand observation is used by Cardan to argue that while individual dwarfs exist, they do not constitute a separate nation of Pygmies.

In Section 15, Tyson engages systematically with Cardan's second objection, which concerns a logical problem in the Pygmy story: "what Cardan urges would not follow" from the premises (015-127). Tyson provides a substantial quotation from Cardan (015-096), treating his arguments with respect even while refuting them. The same passage notes that Cardan's authority may derive from earlier sources (015-132).

## Place in the Scholarly Debate

Cardan appears alongside other Renaissance and early modern scholars in Tyson's survey: "there are several, as Cardan, Budæus, Aldrovandus, Fullerus" (015-119). This grouping shows Cardan as part of a broader learned conversation about the reality of the Pygmies in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. His skepticism serves as a foil for Tyson's own more moderate position: neither accepting the ancient accounts uncritically, nor dismissing them wholesale as Cardan inclined to do.
