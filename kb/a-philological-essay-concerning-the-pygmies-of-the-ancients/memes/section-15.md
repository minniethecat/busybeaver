# Section 15

[Footnote A: _Jo. Talentionij. Variar. & Recondit. Rerum. Thesaurus._ lib.

[015-001]3. cap. 21.]

 
[Footnote B: _Joh. Laurent. Anania prope finem tractatus primi suæ

[015-002]Geograph._]

 
[Footnote C: _Joh. Cassanius libello de Gygantibus_, p. 73.]

 
[Footnote D: _Jo. Talentonius Variar. & recondit. Rerum Thesaurus_, lib. 3.

[015-003]cap. 21. p.m. 515.]

 
[Footnote E: _Job Ludolphi Comment. in Historiam Æthiopic._ p.m. 71.]

[015-004]I had almost forgotten _Olaus Magnus_, whom _Bartholine_ mentions in the

[015-005]close of this Chapter, but lays no great stress upon his Authority,

[015-006]because he tells us, he is fabulous in a great many other Relations, and

[015-007]he writes but by hear-say, that the _Greenlanders_ fight the _Cranes_;

[015-008]_Tandem_ (saith _Bartholine_) _neque ideo [[pygmies|Pygmæi]] sunt, si fortè sagittis &

[015-009]hastis, sicut alij homines, Grues conficiunt & occidunt._ This I think is

[015-010]great Partiality: For _Ctesias_, an Author whom upon all turns

[015-011]_Bartholine_ makes use of as an Evidence, is very positive, that the

[015-012]_Pygmies_ were excellent _Archers_: so that he himself owns, that their

[015-013]being such, illustrates very much that _Text_ in _Ezekiel_, on which he

[015-014]spends good part of the next _Chapter_, whose Title is, _Pygmæorum Gens ex

[015-015]Ezekiele, atque rationibus probabilibus adstruitur_; which we will

[015-016]consider by and by. And tho' _Olaus Magnus_ may write some things by

[015-017]hear-say, yet he cannot be so fabulous as _Ctesias_, who (as _Lucian_

[015-018]tells us) writes what he neither saw himself, or heard from any Body else.

[015-019]Not that I think _Olaus Magnus_ his _Greenlanders_ were real _Pygmies_, no

[015-020]more than _Ctesias_ his _Pygmies_ were real _Men_; tho' he vouches very

[015-021]notably for them. And if all that have copied this Fable from _Ctesias_,

[015-022]must be look'd upon as the same Evidence with himself; the number of the

[015-023]_Testimonies_ produced need not much concern us, since they must all stand

[015-024]or fall with him.

[015-025]The _probable Reasons_ that _Bartholine_ gives in the _fifth Chapter_, are

[015-026]taken from other _Animals_, as Sheep, Oxen, Horses, Dogs, the _Indian

[015-027]Formica_ and Plants: For observing in the same _Species_ some excessive

[015-028]large, and others extreamly little, he infers, _Quæ certè cum in

[015-029]Animalibus & Vegetabilibus fiant; cur in Humana specie non sit probabile,

[015-030]haud video: imprimis cum detur magnitudinis excessus Gigantæus; cur non

[015-031]etiam dabitur Defectus? Quia ergo dantur Gigantes, dabuntur & Pygmæi. Quam

[015-032]consequentiam ut firmam, admittit Cardanus,[A] licet de Pygmæis hoc tantùm

[015-033]concedat, qui pro miraculo, non pro Gente._ Now Cardan, tho' he allows

[015-034]this Consequence, yet in the same place he gives several Reasons why the

[015-035]_Pygmies_ could not be _Men_, and looks upon the whole Story as fabulous.

[015-036]_Bartholine_ concludes this _Chapter_ thus: _Ulteriùs ut Probabilitatem

[015-037]fulciamus, addendum Sceleton Pygmæi, quod_ Dresdæ _vidimus inter alia

[015-038]plurima, servatum in Arce sereniss._ Electoris Saxoniæ, _altitudine infra

[015-039]Cubitum, Ossium soliditate, proportioneque tum Capitis, tum aliorum; ut

[015-040]Embrionem, aut Artificiale quid Nemo rerum peritus suspicari possit.

[015-041]Addita insuper est Inscriptio_ Veri Pygmæi. I hereupon looked into Dr.

[015-042]_Brown_'s Travels into those Parts, who has given us a large Catalogue of

[015-043]the Curiosities, the _Elector_ of _Saxony_ had at _Dresden_, but did not

[015-044]find amongst them this _Sceleton_; which, by the largeness of the Head, I

[015-045]suspect to be the _Sceleton_ of an _[[apes|Orang-Outang]]_, or our _wild Man_. But

[015-046]had he given us either a figure of it, or a more particular Description,

[015-047]it had been a far greater Satisfaction.

 
[Footnote A: _Cardan. de Rerum varietate_, lib. 8. cap. 40.]

[015-048]The Title of _Bartholine_'s _sixth Chapter_ is, _Pygmæos esse aut fuisse

[015-049]ex variis eorum adjunctis, accidentibus_, &c. _ab Authoribus descriptis

[015-050]ostenditur_. As first, their _Magnitude_: which he mentions from _Ctesias,

[015-051]Pliny, Gellius_, and _[[juvenal|Juvenal]]_; and tho' they do not all agree exactly,

[015-052]'tis nothing. _Autorum hic dissensus nullus est_ (saith _Bartholine_)

[015-053]_etenim sicut in nostris hominibus, ita indubiè in Pygmæis non omnes

[015-054]ejusdem magnitudinis._ 2. The _Place_ and _Country_: As _Ctesias_ (he

[015-055]saith) places them in the middle of _[[india|India]]_; _[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_ and _Pliny_ at

[015-056]the Lakes above _Ægypt_; _[[homer|Homer]]_'s _Scholiast_ in the middle of _Ægypt_;

[015-057]_Pliny_ at another time saith they are at the Head of the _Ganges_, and

[015-058]sometimes at _Gerania_, which is in _Thracia_, which being near _[[scythia|Scythia]]_,

[015-059]confirms (he saith) _Anania's Relation_. _Mela_ places them at the

[015-060]_Arabian Gulf_; and _Paulus Jovius docet Pygmæos ultra Japonem esse_; and

[015-061]adds, _has Autorum dissensiones facile fuerit conciliare; nec mirum

[015-062]diversas relationes à_, Plinio _auditas._ For (saith he) as the _Tartars_

[015-063]often change their Seats, since they do not live in Houses, but in Tents,

[015-064]so 'tis no wonder that the _Pygmies_ often change theirs, since instead of

[015-065]Houses, they live in Caves or Huts, built of Mud, Feathers, and

[015-066]Egg-shells. And this mutation of their Habitations he thinks is very plain

[015-067]from _Pliny_, where speaking of _Gerania_, he saith, _Pygmæorum Gens_

[015-068]fuisse _(non jam esse) proditur, creduntque à Gruibus fugatos._ Which

[015-069]passage (saith _Bartholine_) had _Adrian Spigelius_ considered, he would

[015-070]not so soon have left _Aristotle's_ Opinion, because _Franc. Alvares_ the

[015-071]_Portuguese_ did not find them in the place where _Aristotle_ left them;

[015-072]for the _Cranes_, it may be, had driven them thence. His third Article is,

[015-073]their _Habitation_, which _Aristotle_ saith is in _Caves_; hence they are

[015-074]_[[troglodytes|Troglodytes]]_. _Pliny_ tells us they build Huts with Mud, Feathers, and

[015-075]Egg-shells. But what _Bartholine_ adds, _Eò quod Terræ Cavernas

[015-076]inhabitent, non injuriâ dicti sunt olim Pygmæi, Terræ filii_, is wholly

[015-077]new to me, and I have not met with it in any Author before: tho' he gives

[015-078]us here several other significations of the word _Terræ filij_ from a

[015-079]great many Authors, which I will not trouble you at present with. 4. The

[015-080]_Form_, being flat nosed and ugly, as _Ctesias_. 5. Their _Speech_, which

[015-081]was the same as the _Indians_, as _Ctesias_; and for this I find he has no

[015-082]other Author. 6. Their _Hair_; where he quotes _Ctesias_ again, that they

[015-083]make use of it for _Clothes_. 7. Their _Vertues and Arts_; as that they

[015-084]use the same Laws as the _Indians_, are very just, excellent Archers, and

[015-085]that the King of _India_ has Three thousand of them in his Guards. All

[015-086]from _Ctesias_. 8. Their _Animals_, as in _Ctesias_; and here are

[015-087]mentioned their Sheep, Oxen, Asses, Mules, and Horses. 9. Their various

[015-088]_Actions_; as what _Ctesias_ relates of their killing Hares and Foxes with

[015-089]Crows, Eagles, &c. and fighting the _Cranes_, as _Homer, Pliny, Juvenal_.

[015-090]The _seventh Chapter_ in _Bartholine_ has a promising Title, _An Pygmæi

[015-091]sint homines_, and I expected here something more to our purpose; but I

[015-092]find he rather endeavours to answer the Reasons of those that would make

[015-093]them _Apes_, than to lay down any of his own to prove them _Men_. And

[015-094]_Albertus Magnus's_ Opinion he thinks absurd, that makes them part Men

[015-095]part Beasts; they must be either one or the other, not a _Medium_ between

[015-096]both; and to make out this, he gives us a large Quotation out of _Cardan_.

[015-097]But _Cardan_[A] in the same place argues that they are not Men. As to

[015-098]_Suessanus_[B] his Argument, that they want _Reason_, this he will not

[015-099]Grant; but if they use it less or more imperfectly than others (which yet,

[015-100]he saith, is not certain) by the same parity of Reason _Children_, the

[015-101]_Boeotians_, _Cumani_ and _Naturals_ may not be reckoned _Men_; and he

[015-102]thinks, what he has mentioned in the preceding _Chapter_ out of _Ctesias_,

[015-103]&c. shews that they have no small use of Reason. As to _Suessanus_'s

[015-104]next Argument, that they want Religion, Justice, &c. this, he saith, is

[015-105]not confirmed by any grave Writer; and if it was, yet it would not prove

[015-106]that they are not _Men_. For this defect (he saith) might hence happen,

[015-107]because they are forced to live in _Caves_ for fear of the _Cranes_; and

[015-108]others besides them, are herein faulty. For this Opinion, that the

[015-109]_Pygmies_ were _Apes_ and not _Men_, he quotes likewise _Benedictus

[015-110]Varchius_,[C] and _Joh. Tinnulus_,[D] and _Paulus Jovius_,[E] and several

[015-111]others of the Moderns, he tells us, are of the same mind. _Imprimis

[015-112]Geographici quos non puduit in Mappis Geographicis loco Pygmæorum simias

[015-113]cum Gruibus pugnantes ridiculè dipinxisse._

 
[Footnote A: _Cardan. de Rerum varietate_, lib. 8. cap. 40.]

 
[Footnote B: _Suessanus Comment. in Arist. de Histor. Animal._ lib. 8.

[015-114]cap. 12.]

 
[Footnote C: _Benedict. Varchius de Monstris. lingua vernacula._]

 
[Footnote D: _Joh. Tinnulus in Glotto-Chrysio._]

 
[Footnote E: _Paulus Jovius lib. de Muscovit. Legalione._]

[015-115]The Title of _Bartholine's eighth_ and last _Chapter_ is, _Argumenta eorum

[015-116]qui Pygmæorum Historiam fabulosam censent, recitantur & refutantur._ Where

[015-117]he tells us, the only Person amongst the Ancients that thought the Story

[015-118]of the _Pygmies_ to be fabulous was _[[strabo|Strabo]]_; but amongst the Moderns

[015-119]there are several, as _Cardan, Budæus, Aldrovandus, Fullerus_ and others.

[015-120]The first Objection (he saith) is that of _Spigelius_ and others; that

[015-121]since the whole World is now discovered, how happens it, that these

[015-122]_Pygmies_ are not to be met with? He has seven Answers to this Objection;

[015-123]how satisfactory they are, the Reader may judge, if he pleases, by

[015-124]perusing them amongst the Quotations.[A] _Cardan_'s second Objection (he

[015-125]saith) is, that they live but eight years, whence several Inconveniences

[015-126]would happen, as _Cardan_ shews; he answers that no good Author asserts

[015-127]this; and if there was, yet what _Cardan_ urges would not follow; and

[015-128]instances out of _Artemidorus_ in _Pliny_,[B] as a _Parallel_ in the

[015-129]_Calingæ_ a Nation in _India, where the Women conceive when five years

[015-130]old, and do not live above eight._ _Gesner_ speaking of the _Pygmies_,

[015-131]saith, _Vitæ autem longitudo anni arciter octo ut_ Albertus _refert._

[015-132]_Cardan_ perhaps had his Authority from _Albertus_, or it may be both took

[015-133]it from this passage in _Pliny_, which I think would better agree to

[015-134]_Apes_ than _Men_. But _Artemidorus_ being an _Indian Historian_, and in

[015-135]the same place telling other Romances, the less Credit is to be given to

[015-136]him. The third Objection, he saith, is of _Cornelius à Lapide_, who denies

[015-137]the _Pygmies_, because _Homer_ was the first Author of them. The fourth

[015-138]Objection he saith is, because Authors differ about the Place where they

[015-139]should be: This, he tells us, he has answered already in the fifth

[015-140]Chapter. The _fifth_ and last Objection he mentions is, that but few have

[015-141]seen them. He answers, there are a great many Wonders in Sacred and

[015-142]Profane History that we have not seen, yet must not deny. And he instances

[015-143]in three; As the _Formicæ Indicæ_, which are as big as great Dogs: The

[015-144]_Cornu Plantabile_ in the Island _Goa_, which when cut off from the Beast,

[015-145]and flung upon the Ground, will take root like a _Cabbage_: and the

[015-146]_Scotland Geese_ that grow upon Trees, for which he quotes a great many

[015-147]Authors, and so concludes.

 
[Footnote A: _Respondeo._ 1. _Contrarium testari Mercatorum Relationem

[015-148]apud_ Ananiam _supra Cap. 4._ 2. _Et licet non inventi essent vivi à

[015-149]quolibet, pari jure Monocerota & alia negare liceret._ 3. _Qui maria

[015-150]pernavigant, vix oras paucas maritimas lustrant, adeo non terras omnes à

[015-151]mari dissitas._ 4. _Neque in Oris illos habitare maritimis ex Capite

[015-152]quinto manifestum est._ 5. _Quis testatum se omnem adhibuisse diligentiam

[015-153]in inquirendo eos ut inveniret._ 6. _Ita in terra habitant, ut in Antris

[015-154]vitam tolerare dicantur._ 7. _Si vel maximè omni ab omnibus diligentia

[015-155]quæsiti fuissent, nec inventi; fieri potest, ut instar Gigantum jam

[015-156]desierint nec sint ampliùs_.]

 
[Footnote B: _Plinij Hist. Nat._ lib. 7. cap. 2. p.m. 14.]

[015-157]Now how far _Bartholine_ in his Treatise has made out that the _Pygmies_

[015-158]of the Ancients were real _Men_, either from the Authorities he has

[015-159]quoted, or his Reasonings upon them, I submit to the Reader. I shall

[015-160]proceed now (as I promised) to consider the Proof they pretend from _Holy

[015-161]Writ_: For _Bartholine_ and others insist upon that _Text_ in _Ezekiel_

[015-162](_Cap. 27. Vers. 11_) where the _Vulgar_ Translation has it thus; _Filij

[015-163]Arvad cum Exercitu tuo supra Muros tuos per circuitum, & Pygmæi in

[015-164]Turribus tuis fuerunt; Scuta sua suspenderunt supra Muros tuos per

[015-165]circuitum._ Now _Talentonius_ and _Bartholine_ think that what _Ctesias_

[015-166]relates of the _Pygmies_, as their being good _Archers_, very well

[015-167]illustrates this Text of _Ezekiel_: I shall here transcribe what Sir

[015-168]_Thomas Brown_[A] remarks upon it; and if any one requires further

[015-169]Satisfaction, they may consult _Job Ludolphus's Comment_ on his _Æthiopic

[015-170]History_.[B]
