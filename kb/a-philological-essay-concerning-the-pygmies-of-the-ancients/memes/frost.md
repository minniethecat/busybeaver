L. L. Frost of Susanville, Lassen County, California, was a late nineteenth-century collector of Native American folklore whose field observations reached Edward Tyson's essay through the intermediary of the American anthropologist Dr. Daniel G. Brinton. Frost's contribution, though modest in volume, preserves a distinctive California Indian tradition of diminutive supernatural beings — the lizard-people — that enriched Tyson's catalogue of pygmy-like figures in world mythology.

## The Lizard-People of California

Frost's primary contribution to Tyson's essay is a single but evocative ethnographic anecdote. The essay records that "Mr. L.L. Frost, of Susanville, Lassen County, California, tells us how, when he requested an Indian to gather and bring in all the arrow-points he could find, the Indian declared them to be 'no good,' that they had been made by the lizards" (009-042). This seemingly puzzling response led Frost to probe further.

"Whereupon Mr. Frost drew from him the following lizard-story" (009-045). The Indian informant related a tradition in which "there was a time when the lizards were little" people-like beings — an origin myth that collapsed the categories of reptile and human in a manner characteristic of many indigenous mythologies. The arrow-points attributed to these lizard-beings were considered ritually contaminated, explaining the informant's initial reluctance to collect them.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Frost's lizard-story contributes to Tyson's broader argument in two respects. First, it demonstrates the wide geographic range of pygmy-like supernatural beings in Native American traditions, complementing the Micmac Wiguladumoch tales recorded by Brinton (009-040). Second, the conflation of diminutive humanoid beings with animal forms — here, lizards — illustrates the conceptual fluidity between human and non-human categories that Tyson finds characteristic of pygmy mythology across cultures. The lizard-people are at once small humans and reptiles, paralleling the ambiguous ontological status of pygmies in classical sources.

## Source and Transmission

Frost's observations reached Tyson through the filter of Dr. Daniel G. Brinton, the prominent American anthropologist who served as a key conduit for North American pygmy lore in the essay. This chain of transmission — from local informant through Frost to Brinton to Tyson — is representative of how nineteenth-century anthropological knowledge was assembled from widely scattered local observers.
