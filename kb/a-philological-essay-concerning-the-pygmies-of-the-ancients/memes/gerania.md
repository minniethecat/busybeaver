## Overview

Gerania is one of the many locations assigned to the pygmies by classical authors, notable for being the specific site where Pliny located the _Pygmæorum Gens_ — the nation of the Pygmies — before their supposed expulsion by cranes. (015-058, 015-067) Situated in Thrace, near Scythia, Gerania represents the European branch of classical pygmy geography, distinct from the African and Indian traditions.

The Gerania tradition is particularly significant because it anchors pygmy lore in a specific, named European location, and because Pliny's account of their disappearance from the site frames the pygmies as a historically displaced people.

## Classical Sources

Tyson summarizes the conflicting classical geographies of the pygmies: "Pliny at another time saith they are at the Head of the _Ganges_, and sometimes at _Gerania_, which is in _Thracia_, which being near _Scythia_, confirms (he saith) _Anania's Relation_." (015-058)

The multiplicity of classical locations — India, the Nile headwaters, the Arabian Gulf, and Thrace — is addressed by Tyson through a migration hypothesis: "as the _Tartars_ often change their Seats, since they do not live in Houses, but in Tents, so 'tis no wonder that the _Pygmies_ often change theirs, since instead of Houses, they live in Caves or Huts, built of Mud, Feathers, and Egg-shells." (015-063)

This is a rationalizing interpretation: the pygmies' apparent presence in multiple locations across the ancient world is explained not as mythology but as the mobility of a nomadic cave-dwelling people.

## Pliny's Testimony

The key Plinian passage cited by Tyson reads:

> _Pygmæorum Gens fuisse (non jam esse) proditur, creduntque à Gruibus fugatos._ (015-067)

"The nation of the Pygmies is reported to have existed (but no longer does), and they are believed to have been driven away by cranes."

This passage is of critical importance for Tyson's argument because it establishes that even in antiquity, the pygmies were regarded as a people who had once inhabited a specific location and then disappeared. The cranes — the pygmies' legendary adversaries — are the agents of their displacement. Pliny thus frames the Gerania pygmies as a historical population whose absence requires explanation, not as a purely mythological invention.

Bartholine remarks that had Adrian Spigelius "considered" this passage, "he would not so soon have left _Aristotle's_ Opinion, because _Franc. Alvares_ the _Portuguese_ did not find them in the place where _Aristotle_ left them." (015-069) The failure to find pygmies at a given location, in other words, does not disprove their historical existence — it may simply indicate that they had moved.

## Geographic Context

Thrace, the region containing Gerania, occupied the southeastern corner of Europe, bordering Scythia to the north and the Aegean Sea to the south. Its proximity to Scythia is significant because classical sources also associated pygmies with the far north. The Thracian location thus bridges the African/Indian pygmy traditions with the northern European ones.

The exact site of Gerania is not known with certainty. Its name (from Greek _geranos_, "crane") may indicate a place associated with migratory cranes, which would be consistent with the pygmy-crane mythology.

## Significance in Tyson's Argument

Gerania serves multiple functions in Tyson's philological essay: it demonstrates the geographic range of classical pygmy traditions extending into Europe; it provides a specific named location (not merely a vague region) where pygmies were said to have lived; and Pliny's "no longer exists" formulation supports Tyson's thesis that pygmies are a historically real but displaced and diminished population, not a fabulous invention.
