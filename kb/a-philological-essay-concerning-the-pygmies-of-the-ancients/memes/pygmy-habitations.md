## Overview

Pygmy Habitations encompasses the diverse forms of dwelling that ancient and modern sources attribute to diminutive races: caves, underground chambers, mounds, and huts constructed of mud, feathers, and eggshells. The topic occupies a substantial portion of Tyson's essay (Sections 5–9) because the physical remains of habitation — unlike the ambiguous testimony of travellers' reports — provide material evidence that can be archaeologically investigated. The dwellings question is central to the fairy-pygmy theory, since MacRitchie's argument that fairy mounds are the folk memory of prehistoric pygmy dwellings rests on the identification of specific habitation types with archaeological remains.

## Ancient Sources

The most vivid ancient description of pygmy dwellings comes from Pliny, who reports that the pygmies of India construct their houses "of mud mixed with feathers and eggshells" — an image that combines architectural specificity with fabulous exoticism. Tyson treats this account with the methodological caution characteristic of his approach: "according to the nature of their habitations" describes the diversity of dwelling types across regions, suggesting that no single model fits all pygmy populations (005-090). Pliny also records that pygmies "change their dwellings twice a year" — a detail that, if accurate, implies seasonal migration patterns consistent with tropical forest foragers (004-060).

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson's analysis of pygmy habitations is organized typologically. He identifies five categories of dwellings in the ancient and modern records. First, **mound-dwellings**: "fairies are found dwelling in mounds" of earth, which Tyson identifies with both natural hillocks and the artificial barrows and chambered tombs of Neolithic Europe (005-093). The interior of these mounds, "as described in the stories," contains "a long and low passage leading to a central chamber open to the sky" — an accurate description of megalithic passage graves (006-023). "This form of habitation is" attested across the Celtic and Germanic regions, from Scottish brochs to Irish sidhe-mounds (005-094).

Second, **cave-dwellings**: "accounts of the fairies may be in caves" (006-044). The Scandinavian hill people "live in caves or small hills," and similar traditions attach to the caves of the Pennine chain in England (006-046). Tyson notes that cave-dwellings are particularly associated with the troglodyte tradition — the ancient report that certain African populations "have no dwellings" except natural shelters (003-109).

Third, **forest habitations**: "little people may have their habitation in forests or trees" (007-097). This category is particularly significant because it matches the ethnographic reports of Central African pygmies, who construct temporary leaf shelters rather than permanent structures.

Fourth, **underground habitations**: "there is not merely a habitation, but a vast country underneath" the surface, a belief that Tyson connects to both the fairy tradition and the ancient Greek conception of subterranean peoples (006-037). The "underground habitations of the little people" entered through wells, caves, and hollow hills constitute a pan-Eurasian narrative type (009-141).

Fifth, **splendid subterranean palaces**: "fairy caves are splendid habitations. Their subterranean habitations" gleam with gold and precious stones — a folkloric embellishment that MacRitchie interpreted as the imaginative magnification of simple Neolithic dwellings (006-119). Tyson is sceptical of this reductionism: the transformation of "dwelling-places, fortified or not, of prehistoric peoples" into palaces decked with gold requires a theory of cultural memory more powerful than the evidence supports (006-018).

Tyson's typology serves a methodological purpose beyond mere description. By classifying habitations by type rather than by source, he makes visible the convergence between ancient literary accounts and modern archaeological and ethnographic evidence. The fact that "of these habitations fall into line with Mr. MacRitchie's" theory in some cases but not in others — that "not, in many cases, been habitations, but were natural or sepulchral" mounds — demonstrates that the habitation evidence cannot be reduced to a single historical source (008-125, 008-150).

## Modern Perspective

The typological approach that Tyson pioneered has been substantially confirmed by modern archaeology. The identification of megalithic passage graves with fairy mounds — once considered a speculative fancy — is now a standard interpretation in European prehistory. The seasonal dwellings of Central African forest foragers correspond to the "leaf-hut" type that Tyson classed under forest habitations. The most spectacular confirmation of the ancient tradition comes from the discovery of Homo floresiensis in the Liang Bua cave on Flores Island: an actual diminutive hominin species dwelling in a cave, associated with stone tools and evidence of controlled fire, in precisely the insular Southeast Asian region where traditions of "little people" have persisted into the modern era.
