Augustinus Suessanus was a Renaissance scholar who wrote a commentary on Aristotle's *Historia Animalium* (lib. 8, cap. 12). He is cited in Edward Tyson's essay as an authority who argued against the humanity of the Pygmies, contending that they lack reason, religion, and justice.

Tyson engages with Suessanus in Section 15, where he surveys the arguments of various scholars who denied that the Pygmies were human beings. Suessanus features alongside figures such as Cardan and Albertus Magnus in this critical examination of pygmy anthropology.

## Suessanus's Arguments Against the Pygmies

Tyson reports that Suessanus advanced two principal arguments against classifying the Pygmies as human. First, Suessanus argued that the Pygmies lacked reason (*Ratio*), a proposition Tyson refuses to concede:

> "_Suessanus_ his Argument, that they want _Reason_, this he will not Grant; but if they use it less or more imperfectly than others (which yet, he saith, is not certain) by the same parity of Reason _Children_, the _Boeotians_, _Cumani_ and _Naturals_ may not be reckoned _Men_."
> (015-098)

Tyson counters Suessanus's argument through *reductio ad absurdum*: if imperfect reason disqualifies the Pygmies from humanity, then the same logic would exclude children, the proverbially dull Boeotians, and those with intellectual disabilities — all of whom are unquestionably human.

Tyson further notes that the evidence from Ctesias (discussed in the preceding chapter) demonstrates that the Pygmies possess "no small use of Reason," undermining Suessanus's central claim (015-103).

## The Argument from Religion and Justice

Suessanus's second argument, as recorded by Tyson, was that the Pygmies lacked religion, justice, and other markers of civil society:

> "As to _Suessanus_'s next Argument, that they want Religion, Justice, &c. this, he saith, is not confirmed by any grave Writer; and if it was, yet it would not prove that they are not _Men_."
> (015-104)

Tyson responds that even if the Pygmies lacked these institutions — a claim unconfirmed by any reliable authority — this would not demonstrate their non-human status. He suggests that any such deficiency might result from their harsh living conditions: forced to dwell in caves to escape the attack of Cranes, the Pygmies may simply not have had the opportunity to develop complex social institutions (015-107).

## Tyson's Refutation Strategy

Tyson's treatment of Suessanus is characteristic of his broader rhetorical approach in Section 15. He presents Suessanus's arguments at length, then systematically dismantles them, often by showing that the logic employed would prove too much — that it would exclude from humanity groups universally acknowledged as human. This method allows Tyson to engage seriously with skeptical authorities while ultimately reinforcing his own conclusion that the Pygmies should be regarded as a diminutive race of human beings.
