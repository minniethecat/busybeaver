# Section 16

[Footnote A: Sir _Thomas Brown's Enquiries into Vulgar Errors_, lib. 4.

[016-001]cap. 11. p. 242.]

 
[Footnote B: _Comment. in Hist. Æthiopic._ p. 73.]

[016-002]The _second Testimony_ (saith Sir _Thomas Brown_) _is deduced from Holy

[016-003]Scripture; thus rendered in the Vulgar Translation_, Sed & [[pygmies|Pygmæi]] qui

[016-004]erant in turribus tuis, pharetras suas suspenderunt in muris tuis per

[016-005]gyrum: _from whence notwithstanding we cannot infer this Assertion, for

[016-006]first the Translators accord not, and the Hebrew word_ Gammadim _is very

[016-007]variously rendered. Though_ Aquila, Vatablus _and_ Lyra _will have it_

[016-008]Pygmæi, _yet in the_ Septuagint, _it is no more than Watchman; and so in

[016-009]the_ Arabick _and_ High-Dutch. _In the_ Chalde, Cappadocians, _in_

[016-010]Symmachus, Medes, _and in the_ French, _those of_ Gamed. Theodotian _of

[016-011]old, and_ Tremillius _of late, have retained the Textuary word; and so

[016-012]have the_ Italian, Low Dutch, _and_ English _Translators, that is, the Men

[016-013]of_ Arvad _were upon thy Walls round about, and the_ Gammadims _were in

[016-014]thy Towers._

[016-015]_Nor do Men only dissent in the Translation of the word, but in the

[016-016]Exposition of the Sense and Meaning thereof; for some by_ Gammadims

[016-017]_understand a People of_ Syria, _so called from the City of_ Gamala; _some

[016-018]hereby understand the_ Cappadocians, _many the_ Medes: _and hereof_

[016-019]Forerius _hath a singular Exposition, conceiving the Watchmen of_ Tyre,

[016-020]_might well be called_ Pygmies, _the Towers of that City being so high,

[016-021]that unto Men below, they appeared in a Cubital Stature. Others expound it

[016-022]quite contrary to common Acception, that is not Men of the least, but of

[016-023]the largest size; so doth_ Cornelius _construe_ Pygmæi, _or_ Viri

[016-024]Cubitales, _that is, not Men of a Cubit high, but of the largest Stature,

[016-025]whose height like that of [[giants|Giants]], is rather to be taken by the Cubit than

[016-026]the Foot; in which phrase we read the measure of_ Goliah, _whose height is

[016-027]said to be six Cubits and span. Of affinity hereto is also the Exposition

[016-028]of_ Jerom; _not taking_ Pygmies _for [[dwarfs|Dwarfs]], but stout and valiant

[016-029]Champions; not taking the sense of [Greek: pygmae], which signifies the

[016-030]Cubit measure, but that which expresseth Pugils; that is, Men fit for

[016-031]Combat and the Exercise of the Fist. Thus there can be no satisfying

[016-032]illation from this Text, the diversity, or rather contrariety of

[016-033]Expositions and Interpretations, distracting more than confirming the

[016-034]Truth of the Story._

[016-035]But why _Aldrovandus_ or _Caspar Bartholine_ should bring in St. _Austin_

[016-036]as a Favourer of this Opinion of _Men Pygmies_, I see no Reason. To me he

[016-037]seems to assert quite the contrary: For proposing this Question, _An ex

[016-038]propagine_ Adam _vel filiorum_ Noe, _quædam genera Hominum Monstrosa

[016-039]prodierunt?_ He mentions a great many monstrous Nations of _Men_, as they

[016-040]are described by the _Indian Historians_, and amongst the rest, the

[016-041]_Pygmies_, the _Sciopodes_, &c. And adds, _Quid dicam de_ Cynocephalis,

[016-042]_quorum Canina Capita atque ipse Latratus magis Bestias quàm Homines

[016-043]confitentur? Sed omnia Genera Hominum, quæ dicuntur esse, esse credere,

[016-044]non est necesse._ And afterwards so fully expresses himself in favour of

[016-045]the _Hypothesis_ I am here maintaining, that I think it a great

[016-046]Confirmation of it. _Nam & Simias_ (saith he) _& Cercopithecos, &

[016-047]Sphingas, si nesciremus non Homines esse, sed Bestias, possent isti

[016-048]Historici de sua Curiositate gloriantes velut Gentes Aliquas Hominum nobis

[016-049]impunitâ vanitate mentiri._ At last he concludes and determines the

[016-050]Question thus, _Aut illa, quæ talia de quibusdam Gentibus scripta sunt,

[016-051]omnino nulla sunt, aut si sunt, Homines non sunt, aut ex_ Adam _sunt si

[016-052]Homines sunt._

[016-053]There is nothing therefore in St. _Austin_ that justifies the being of

[016-054]_Men Pygmies_, or that the _Pygmies_ were _Men_; he rather makes them

[016-055]_[[apes|Apes]]_. And there is nothing in his _Scholiast Ludovicus Vives_ that tends

[016-056]this way, he only quotes from other Authors, what might illustrate the

[016-057]Text he is commenting upon, and no way asserts their being _Men_. I shall

[016-058]therefore next enquire into _Bochartus_'s Opinion, who would have them to

[016-059]be the _Nubæ_ or _Nobæ_. _Hos Nubas Troglodyticos_ (saith[A] he) _ad

[016-060]Avalitem Sinum esse Pygmæos Veterum multa probant._ He gives us five

[016-061]Reasons to prove this. As, 1. The Authority of _Hesychius_, who saith,

[016-062][Greek: Noboi Pygmaioi]. 2. Because _[[homer|Homer]]_ places the _Pygmies_ near the

[016-063]Ocean, where the Nubæ were. 3. _[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_ places them at the lakes of the

[016-064]_Nile_. Now by the _Nile Bochartus_ tells us, we must understand the

[016-065]_Astaborus_, which the Ancients thought to be a Branch of the _Nile_, as

[016-066]he proves from _Pliny, Solinus_ and _Æthicus_. And _Ptolomy_ (he tells us)

[016-067]places the _Nubæ_ hereabout. 4. Because _Aristotle_ makes the _Pygmies_ to

[016-068]be _[[troglodytes|Troglodytes]]_, and so were the _Nubæ_. 5. He urges that Story of

[016-069]_Nonnosus_ which I have already mentioned, and thinks that those that

[016-070]_Nonnosus_ met with, were a Colony of the _Nubæ_; but afterwards adds,

[016-071]_Quos tamen absit ut putemus Staturâ fuisse Cubitali, prout Poetæ fingunt,

[016-072]qui omnia in majus augent._ But this methinks spoils them from being

[016-073]_Pygmies_; several other Nations at this rate may be _Pygmies_ as well as

[016-074]these _Nubæ_. Besides, he does not inform us, that these _Nubæ_ used to

[016-075]fight the _Cranes_; and if they do not, and were not _Cubitales_, they

[016-076]can't be _Homer_'s _Pygmies_, which we are enquiring after. But the Notion

[016-077]of their being _Men_, had so possessed him, that it put him upon fancying

[016-078]they must be the _Nubæ_; but 'tis plain that those in _Nonnosus_ could not

[016-079]be a Colony of the _Nubæ_; for then the _Nubæ_ must have understood their

[016-080]Language, which the _Text_ saith, none of the Neighbourhood did. And

[016-081]because the _Nubæ_ are _Troglodytes_, that therefore they must be

[016-082]_Pygmies_, is no Argument at all. For _Troglodytes_ here is used as an

[016-083]_Adjective_; and there is a sort of _Sparrow_ which is called _Passer

[016-084]Troglodytes_. Not but that in _Africa_ there was a Nation of _Men_ called

[016-085]_Troglodytes_, but quite different from our _Pygmies_. How far _Bochartus_

[016-086]may be in the right, in guessing the Lakes of the _Nile_ (whereabout

[016-087]_Aristotle_ places the _Pygmies_) to be the Fountains of the River

[016-088]_Astaborus_, which in his description, and likewise the _Map_, he places

[016-089]in the Country of the _Avalitæ_, near the _Mossylon Emporium_; I shall not

[016-090]enquire. This I am certain of, he misrepresents _Aristotle_ where he tells

[016-091]us,[B] _Quamvis in ea fabula hoc saltem verum esse asserat Philosophus,

[016-092]Pusillos Homines in iis locis degere_: for as I have already observed;

[016-093]_Aristotle_ in that _Text_ saith nothing at all of their being _Men_: the

[016-094]contrary rather might be thence inferred, that they were _Brutes_. And

[016-095]_Bochart's_ Translation, as well as _Gaza's_ is faulty here, and by no

[016-096]means to be allowed, _viz. Ut aiunt, genus ibi parvum est tam Hominum,

[016-097]quàm Equorum_; which had _Bochartus_ considered he would not have been so

[016-098]fond it may be of his _Nubæ_. And if the [Greek: Noboi Pygmaioi] in

[016-099]_Hesychius_ are such _Pygmies_ as _Bochartus_ makes his _Nubæ, Quos tamen

[016-100]absit ut putemus staturtâ fuisse Cubitali_, it will not do our business at

[016-101]all; and neither _Homer's_ Authority, nor _Aristotle's_ does him any

[016-102]Service.

 
[Footnote A: _Sam. Bochart. Geograph. Sacræ_, Part. 1. lib. 2. cap. 23.

[016-103]p.m. 142.]

 
[Footnote B: _Bocharti Hierozoici pars Posterior_, lib. I. cap. II. p.

[016-104]76.]

[016-105]But this Fable of _Men Pygmies_ has not only obtained amongst the _Greeks_

[016-106]and _Indian Historians_: the _Arabians_ likewise tell much such Stories of

[016-107]them, as the same learned _Bochartus_ informs us. I will give his Latin

[016-108]Translation of one of them, which he has printed in _Arabick_ also:

[016-109]_Arabes idem_ (saith[A] _Bochartus_) _referunt ex cujusdam_ Græculi _fide,

[016-110]qui_ Jacobo Isaaci _filio_, Sigariensi _fertur ita narrasse_. _Navigabam

[016-111]aliquando in mari_ Zingitano, _& impulit me ventus in quandam Insulam_.

[016-112]_In cujus Oppidum cum devenissem, reperi Incolas Cubitalis esse staturæ, &

[016-113]plerosque Coclites. Quorum multitudo in me congregata me deduxit ad Regem

[016-114]suum. Fussit is, ut Captivus detinerer; & inquandam Caveæ speciem

[016-115]conjectus sum; eos autem aliquando ad bellum instrui cum viderem, dixerunt

[016-116]Hostem imminere, & fore ut propediem ingrueret. Nec multò post Gruum

[016-117]exercitus in eos insurrexit. Atque ideo erant Coclites, quod eorum oculos

[016-118]hæ confodissent. Atque Ego, virgâ assumptâ, in eas impetum feci, & illæ

[016-119]avolârunt atque aufugerunt; ob quod facinus in honore fui apud illos_.

[016-120]This Author, it seems, represents them under the same Misfortune with the

[016-121]_Poet_, who first mentioned them, as being blind, by having their Eyes

[016-122]peck'd out by their cruel Enemies. Such an Accident possibly might happen

[016-123]now and then, in these bloody Engagements, tho' I wonder the _Indian

[016-124]Historians_ have not taken notice of it. However the _Pygmies_ shewed

[016-125]themselves grateful to their Deliverer, in heaping _Honours_ on him. One

[016-126]would guess, for their own sakes, they could not do less than make him

[016-127]their _Generalissimo_; but our Author is modest in not declaring what they

[016-128]were.

 
[Footnote A: _Bochartus ibid_. p.m. 77.]

[016-129]Isaac Vossius seems to unsettle all, and endeavours utterly to ruine the

[016-130]whole Story: for he tells us, If you travel all over _Africa_, you shall

[016-131]not meet with either a _Crane_ or _Pygmie_: _Se mirari_ (saith[A] _Isaac

[016-132]Vossius_) Aristotelem, _quod tam seriò affirmet non esse fabellam, quæ de

[016-133]Pygmæis & Bello, quod cum Gruibus gerant, narrantur. Si quis totam

[016-134]pervadat_ Africam, _nullas vel Grues vel Pygmæos inveniet_. Now one would

[016-135]wonder more at _Vossius_, that he should assert this of _Aristotle_, which

[016-136]he never said. And since _Vossius_ is so mistaken in what he relates of

[016-137]_Aristotle_; where he might so easily have been in the right, 'tis not

[016-138]improbable, but he may be out in the rest too: For who has travelled all

[016-139]_Africa_ over, that could inform him? And why should he be so peremptory

[016-140]in the Negative, when he had so positive an Affirmation of _Aristotle_ to

[016-141]the contrary? or if he would not believe _Aristotle's_ Authority, methinks

[016-142]he should _Aristophanes's_, who tells us,[B] [Greek: Speirein hotau men

[016-143]Geranos kroizon es taen libyaen metachorae]. _'Tis time to sow when the

[016-144]noisy Cranes take their flight into_ [[libya|Libya]]. Which Observation is likewise

[016-145]made by _[[hesiod|Hesiod]], Theognis, Aratus_, and others. And _Maximus Tyrius_ (as I

[016-146]find him quoted in _Bochartus_) saith, [Greek: Hai geravoi ex Aigyptou ora

[016-147]therous aphistamenai, ouk anechomenai to thalpos teinasai pterygas hosper

[016-148]istia, pherontai dia tou aeros euthy ton Skython gaes]. i.e. _Grues per

[016-149]æstatem ex_ Ægypto _abscedentes, quia Calorem pati non possunt, alis

[016-150]velorum instar expansis, per aerem ad_ Scythicam _plagam rectà feruntur_.

[016-151]Which fully confirms that Migration of the _Cranes_ that _Aristotle_

[016-152]mentions.

 
[Footnote A: _Isaac Vossius de Nili aliorumque stuminum Origine_, Cap.

[016-153]18.]

 
[Footnote B: _Aristophanes in Nubibus_.]

[016-154]But _Vossius_ I find, tho' he will not allow the _Cranes_, yet upon second

[016-155]Thoughts did admit of _Pygmies_ here: For this Story of the _Pygmies_ and

[016-156]the _Cranes_ having made so much _noise_, he thinks there may be something

[016-157]of truth in it; and then gives us his Conjecture, how that the _Pygmies_

[016-158]may be those _Dwarfs_, that are to be met with beyond the Fountains of the

[016-159]_Nile_; but that they do not fight _Cranes_ but _Elephants_, and kill a

[016-160]great many of them, and drive a considerable Traffick for their teeth with

[016-161]the _Jagi_, who sell them to those of _Congo_ and the _Portuguese_. I will

[016-162]give you _Vossius's_ own words; _Attamen_ (saith[A] he) _ut solent fabellæ

[016-163]non de nihilo fingi & aliquod plerunque continent veri, id ipsum quoque

[016-164]que hìc factum esse existimo. Certum quippe est ultra_ Nili _fontes multos

[016-165]reperiri_ Nanos, _qui tamen non cum Gruibus, sed cum Elephantis perpetuum

[016-166]gerant bellum. Præcipuum quippe Eboris commercium in regno magni_ Macoki

[016-167]_per istos transigitur Homunciones; habitant in Sylvis, & mira dexteritate

[016-168]Elephantos sagittis conficiunt. Carnibus vescuntur, Dentes verô_ Jagis

[016-169]_divendunt, illi autem_ Congentibus & Lusitanis.

 
[Footnote A: _Isaac Vossius ibid_.]

[016-170]_Job Ludolphus_[A] in his _Commentary_ on his _Æthiopick History_ remarks,

[016-171]That there was never known a Nation all of Dwarfs. _Nani quippe_ (saith

[016-172]_Ludolphus_) _Naturæ quodam errore ex aliis justæ staturæ hominibus

[016-173]generantur. Qualis verô ea Gens sit, ex qua ista Naturæ Ludibria tantâ

[016-174]copiâ proveniant, Vossium docere oportelat, quia Pumiliones Pumiles alios

[016-175]non gignunt, sed plerunque steriles sunt, experientia teste; ut planè non

[016-176]opus habuerunt Doctores Talmudici Nanorum matrimonia prohibere, ne

[016-177]Digitales ex iis nascerentur. Ludolphus_ it may be is a little too strict

[016-178]with _Vossius_ for calling them _Nani_; he may only mean a sort of Men in

[016-179]that Country of less Stature than ordinary. And _Dapper_ in his History of

[016-180]_Africa_, from whom _Vossius_ takes this Account, describes such in the

[016-181]Kingdom of _Mokoko_, he calls _Mimos_, and tells us that they kill

[016-182]_Elephants_. But I see no reason why _Vossius_ should take these Men for

[016-183]the _Pygmies_ of the Ancients, or think that they gave any occasion or

[016-184]ground for the inventing this Fable, is there was no other reason, this

[016-185]was sufficient, because they were able to kill the _Elephants_. The

[016-186]_Pygmies_ were scarce a Match for the _Cranes_; and for them to have

[016-187]encountered an _Elephant_, were as vain an Attempt, as the _Pygmies_ were

[016-188]guilty of in _[[philostratus|Philostratus]]_[B] 'who to revenge the Death of _Antæus_,

[016-189]having found _Hercules_ napping in _Libya_, mustered up all their Forces

[016-190]against him. One _Phalanx_ (he tells us) assaulted his left hand; but

[016-191]against his right hand, that being the stronger, two _Phalanges_ were

[016-192]appointed. The Archers and Slingers besieged his feet, admiring the

[016-193]hugeness of his Thighs: But against his Head, as the Arsenal, they raised

[016-194]Batteries, the King himself taking his Post there. They set fire to his

[016-195]Hair, put Reaping-hooks in his Eyes; and that he might not breath, clapp'd

[016-196]Doors to his Mouth and Nostrils; but all the Execution that they could do,

[016-197]was only to awake him, which when done, deriding their folly, he gather'd

[016-198]them all up in his Lion's Skin, and carried them (_Philostratus_ thinks)

[016-199]to _Euristhenes_.' This _Antæus_ was as remarkable for his height, as the

[016-200]_Pygmies_ were for their lowness of Stature: For _Plutarch_[C] tells us,

[016-201]that _Q. Sterorius_ not being willing to trust Common Fame, when he came

[016-202]to _Tingis_ (now _Tangier_) he caused _Antæus's_ Sepulchre to be opened,

[016-203]and found his Corps full threescore Cubits long. But _Sterorius_ knew well

[016-204]enough how to impose upon the Credulity of the People, as is evident from

[016-205]the Story of his _white Hind_, which _Plutarch_ likewise relates.
