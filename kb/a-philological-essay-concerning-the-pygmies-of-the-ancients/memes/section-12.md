# Section 12

[012-001]_Ctesias_ was Physician to _Artaxerxes Mnemon_ as _[[diodorus-siculus|Diodorus Siculus]]_[A]

[012-002]and _[[strabo|Strabo]]_[B] inform us. He was contemporary with _Xenophon_, a little

[012-003]later than _[[herodotus|Herodotus]]_; and _Helvicus_ in his _Chronology_ places him

[012-004]three hundred eighty three years before _Christ_: He is an ancient Author,

[012-005]'tis true, and it may be upon that score valued by some. We are beholden

[012-006]to him, not only for his Improvements on the Story of the _[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_, but

[012-007]for his Remarks likewise on several other parts of _Natural History_;

[012-008]which for the most part are all of the same stamp, very wonderful and

[012-009]incredible; as his _Mantichora_, his _Gryphins_, the _horrible Indian

[012-010]Worm_, a Fountain of _Liquid Gold_, a Fountain of _Honey_, a Fountain

[012-011]whose Water will make a Man confess all that ever he did, a Root he calls

[012-012][Greek: paraebon], that will attract Lambs and Birds, as the Loadstone

[012-013]does filings of Steel; and a great many other Wonders he tells us: all of

[012-014]which are copied from him by _Ælian, Pliny, Solinus, Mela, [[philostratus|Philostratus]]_,

[012-015]and others. And _[[photius|Photius]]_ concludes _Ctesias_'s Account of _[[india|India]]_ with

[012-016]this passage; [Greek: Tauta graphon kai mythologon Ktaesias. legei t'

[012-017]alaethestata graphein; epagon hos ta men autos idon graphei, ta de par

[012-018]auton mathon ton eidoton. polla de touton kai alla thaumasiotera

[012-019]paralipein, dia to mae doxai tois mae tauta theasamenois apista

[012-020]syngraphein;] i.e. _These things_ (saith he) Ctesias _writes and feigns,

[012-021]but he himself says all he has wrote is very true. Adding, that some

[012-022]things which he describes, he had seen himself; and the others he had

[012-023]learn'd from those that had seen them: That he had omitted a great many

[012-024]other things more wonderful, because he would not seem to those that have

[012-025]not seen them, to write incredibilities_. But notwithstanding all this,

[012-026]_Lucian_[C] will not believe a word he saith; for he tells us that

[012-027]_Ctesias_ has wrote of _India_, [Greek: A maete autos eide, maete allou

[012-028]eipontos aekousen], _What he neither saw himself, nor ever heard from any

[012-029]Body else._ And _[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_ tells us plainly, he is not fit to be believed:

[012-030][Greek: En de taei Indikaei hos phaesi Ktaesias, ouk on axiopistos.][D]

[012-031]And the same opinion _A. Gellius_[E] seems to have of him, as he had

[012-032]likewise of several other old _Greek Historians_ which happened to fall

[012-033]into his hands at _Brundusium_, in his return from _Greece_ into _Italy_;

[012-034]he gives this Character of them and their performance: _Erant autem isti

[012-035]omnes libri Græci, miraculorum fabularumque pleni: res inauditæ,

[012-036]incredulæ, Scriptores veteres non parvæ authoritatis_, Aristeas

[012-037]Proconnesius, & Isagonus, & Nicæensis, & Ctesias, & Onesicritus, &

[012-038]Polystephanus, & Hegesias. Not that I think all that _Ctesias_ has wrote

[012-039]is fabulous; For tho' I cannot believe his _speaking Pygmies_, yet what he

[012-040]writes of the _Bird_ he calls [Greek: Bittakos], that it would speak

[012-041]_Greek_ and the _Indian Language_, no doubt is very true; and as _H.

[012-042]Stephens_[F] observes in his Apology for _Ctesias_, such a Relation would

[012-043]seem very surprising to one, that had never seen nor heard of a _Parrot_.

 
[Footnote A: _Diodor. Siculi Bibliothec_. lib. 2. p.m. 118.]

 
[Footnote B: _Strabo Geograph_. lib. 14. p. 451.]

 
[Footnote C: _Lucian_ lib 1. _veræ Histor_. p.m. 373.]

 
[Footnote D: _Arist. Hist. Animal._ lib. 8. cap. 28.]

 
[Footnote E: _A. Gellij. Noctes. Attic._ lib. 9. cap. 4.]

 
[Footnote F: _Henr. Stephani de Ctesia Historico antiquissimo disquisitio,

[012-044]ad finem Herodoti._]

[012-045]But this Story of _Ctesias_'s _speaking Pygmies_, seems to be confirm'd by

[012-046]the Account that _Nonnosus_, the Emperour _Justinian_'s Ambassador into

[012-047]_Æthiopia_, gives of his Travels. I will transcribe the Passage, as I find

[012-048]it in _Photius_,[A] and 'tis as follows:

 
[Footnote A: _Photij. Bibliothec._ cod. 3. p.m. 7.]

[012-049][Greek: Hoti apo taes pharsan pleonti toi Nonnosoi, epi taen eschataen ton

[012-050]naeson kataentaekoti toion de ti synebae, thauma kai akousai. enetuche gar

[012-051]tisi morphaen men kai idean echousin anthropinaen, brachytatois de to

[012-052]megethos, kai melasi taen chroan. hypo de trichon dedasysmenois dia pantos

[012-053]tou somatos. heiponto de tois andrasi kai gynaikes paraplaesiai kai

[012-054]paidaria eti brachytera, ton par autois andron. gymnoi de aesan hapantes;

[012-055]plaen dermati tini mikroi taen aido periekalypron, hoi probebaekotes

[012-056]homoios andres te kai gynaikes. agrion de ouden eped eiknynto oude

[012-057]anaemeron; alla kai phonaen eichon men anthropinaen, agnoston de pantapasi

[012-058]taen dialekton tois te perioikois hapasi, kai polloi pleon tois peri taen

[012-059]Nonnoson, diezon de ek thalattion ostreion, kai ichthyon, ton apo taes

[012-060]thalassaes eis taen naeson aporrhiptomenon; tharsos de eichon ouden. alla

[012-061]kai horontes tous kath' haemas anthropous hypeptaesan, hosper haemeis ta

[012-062]meiso ton thaerion.]

[012-063]_Naviganti à Pharsa Nonoso, & ad extremam usque insularum delato, tale

[012-064]quid occurrit, vel ipso auditu admirandum. Incidit enim in quosdam forma

[012-065]quidem & figura humana, sed brevissimos, & cutem nigros, totúmque pilosos

[012-066]corpus. Sequebantur viros æquales foeminæ, & pueri adhuc breviores. Nudi

[012-067]omnes agunt, pelle tantum brevi adultiores verenda tecti, viri pariter ac

[012-068]foeminæ: agreste nihil, neque efferum quid præ se ferentes. Quin & vox

[012-069]illis humana, sed omnibus, etiam accolis, prorsus ignota lingua, multoque

[012-070]amplius Nonosi sociis. Vivunt marinis ostreis, & piscibus è mari ad

[012-071]insulam projectis. Audaces minime sunt, ut nostris conspectis hominibus,

[012-072]quemadmodum nos visa ingenti fera, metu perculsi fuerint._

[012-073]'That _Nonnosus_ sailing from _Pharsa_, when he came to the farthermost of

[012-074]the Islands, a thing, very strange to be heard of, happened to him; for he

[012-075]lighted on some (_Animals_) in shape and appearance like _Men_, but little

[012-076]of stature, and of a black colour, and thick covered with hair all over

[012-077]their Bodies. The Women, who were of the same stature, followed the Men:

[012-078]They were all naked, only the Elder of them, both Men and Women, covered

[012-079]their Privy Parts with a small Skin. They seemed not at all fierce or wild;

[012-080]they had a Humane Voice, but their _Dialect_ was altogether unknown to

[012-081]every Body that lived about them; much more to those that were with

[012-082]_Nonnosus_. They liv'd upon Sea Oysters, and Fish that were cast out of

[012-083]the Sea, upon the Island. They had no Courage; for seeing our Men, they

[012-084]were frighted, as we are at the sight of the greatest wild Beast.'

[012-085][Greek: _phonaen eichon men anthropinaen_] I render here, _they had a

[012-086]Humane Voice_, not _Speech_: for had they spoke any Language, tho' their

[012-087]_Dialect_ might be somewhat different, yet no doubt but some of the

[012-088]Neighbourhood would have understood something of it, and not have been

[012-089]such utter Strangers to it. Now 'twas observed of the _[[apes|Orang-Outang]]_, that

[012-090]it's _Voice_ was like the Humane, and it would make a Noise like a Child,

[012-091]but never was observed to speak, tho' it had the _Organs_ of _Speech_

[012-092]exactly formed as they are in _Man_; and no Account that ever has been

[012-093]given of this Animal do's pretend that ever it did. I should rather agree

[012-094]to what _Pliny_[A] mentions, _Quibusdam pro Sermone nutus motusque

[012-095]Membrorum est_; and that they had no more a Speech than _Ctesias_ his

[012-096]_Cynocephali_ which could only bark, as the same _Pliny_[B] remarks; where

[012-097]he saith, _In multis autem Montibus Genus Hominum Capitibus Caninis,

[012-098]ferarum pellibus velari, pro voce latratum edere, unguibus armatum venatu

[012-099]& Aucupio vesci, horum supra Centum viginti Millia fuisse prodente se

[012-100]Ctesias scribit._ But in _Photius_ I find, that _Ctesias's Cynocephali_

[012-101]did speak the _Indian Language_ as well as the _Pygmies_. Those therefore

[012-102]in _Nonnosus_ since they did not speak the _Indian_, I doubt, spoke no

[012-103]_Language_ at all; or at least, no more than other _Brutes_ do.

 
[Footnote A: _Plinij Nat. Hist._ lib. 6. cap. 30. p.m. 741.]

 
[Footnote B: _Plinij. Nat. Hist._ lib. 7. cap. 2. p.m. 11.]

[012-104]_Ctesias_ I find is the only Author that ever understood what Language

[012-105]'twas that the _Pygmies_ spake: For _Herodotus_[A] owns that they use a

[012-106]sort of Tongue like to no other, but screech like _Bats_. He saith, [Greek:

[012-107]Hoi Garamantes outoi tous troglodytas Aithiopas thaereuousi toisi

[012-108]tetrippoisi. Hoi gar Troglodytai aithiopes podas tachistoi anthropon

[012-109]panton eisi, ton hymeis peri logous apopheromenous akouomen. Siteontai de

[012-110]hoi Troglodytai ophis, kai Saurous, kai ta toiauta ton Herpeton. Glossan

[012-111]de oudemiaei allaei paromoiaen nenomikasi, alla tetrygasi kathaper hai

[012-112]nukterides;] i.e. _These_ Garamantes _hunt the_ Troglodyte Æthiopians _in

[012-113]Chariots with four Horses. The_ Troglodyte Æthiopians _are the swiftest of

[012-114]foot of all Men that ever he heard of by any Report. The_ [[troglodytes|Troglodytes]] _eat

[012-115]Serpents and Lizards, and such sort of Reptiles. They use a Language like

[012-116]to no other Tongue, but screech like Bats._

 
[Footnote A: _Herodot. in Melpomene._ pag. 283.]

[012-117]Now that the _Pygmies_ are _Troglodytes_, or do live in Caves, is plain

[012-118]from _Aristotle_,[A] who saith, [Greek: Troglodytai de' eisi ton bion].

[012-119]And so _Philostratus_,[B] [Greek: Tous de pygmaious oikein men

[012-120]hypogeious]. And methinks _Le Compte_'s Relation concerning the _wild_ or

[012-121]_savage Man_ in _Borneo_, agrees so well with this, that I shall

[012-122]transcribe it: for he tells us,[C] _That in_ Borneo _this_ wild _or_

[012-123]savage Man _is indued with extraordinary strength; and not withstanding he

[012-124]walks but upon two Legs, yet he is so swift of foot, that they have much

[012-125]ado to outrun him. People of Quality course him, as we do Stags here: and

[012-126]this sort of hunting is the King's usual divertisement._ And _Gassendus_

[012-127]in the Life of _Peiresky_, tells us they commonly hunt them too in

[012-128]_Angola_ in _Africa_, as I have already mentioned. So that very likely

[012-129]_Herodotus's Troglodyte Æthiopians_ may be no other than our

[012-130]_Orang-Outang_ or _wild Man_. And the rather, because I fancy their

[012-131]Language is much the same: for an _Ape_ will chatter, and make a noise

[012-132]like a _Bat_, as his _Troglodytes_ did: And they undergo to this day the

[012-133]same Fate of being hunted, as formerly the _Troglodytes_ used to be by the

[012-134]_Garamantes_.

 
[Footnote A: _Arist. Hist. Animal._, lib. 8. cap. 15. p.m. 913.]

 
[Footnote B: _Philostrat. in vita Appollon. Tyanæi_, lib. 3. cap. 14. p.m.

[012-135]152.]

 
[Footnote C: _Lewis le Compte_ Memoirs and Observations on _China_, p.m.

[012-136]510.]

[012-137]Whether those [Greek: andras mikrous metrion elassonas andron] which the

[012-138]_Nasamones_ met with (as _Herodotus_[A] relates) in their Travels to

[012-139]discover _[[libya|Libya]]_, were the _Pygmies_; I will not determine: It seems that

[012-140]_Nasamones_ neither understood their Language, nor they that of the

[012-141]_Nasamones_. However, they were so kind to the _Nasamones_ as to be their

[012-142]Guides along the Lakes, and afterwards brought them to a City, [Greek: en

[012-143]taei pantas einai toisi agousi to megethos isous, chroma de melanas], i.e.

[012-144]_in which all were of the same stature with the Guides, and black_. Now

[012-145]since they were all _little black Men_, and their Language could not be

[012-146]understood, I do suspect they may be a Colony of the _Pygmies_: And that

[012-147]they were no farther Guides to the _Nasamones_, than that being frighted

[012-148]at the sight of them, they ran home, and the _Nasamones_ followed them.

 
[Footnote A: _Herodotus in Euterpe_ seu lib. 2. p.m. 102.]

[012-149]I do not find therefore any good Authority, unless you will reckon

[012-150]_Ctesias_ as such, that the _Pygmies_ ever used a Language or Speech, any

[012-151]more than other _Brutes_ of the same _Species_ do among themselves, and

[012-152]that we know nothing of, whatever _Democritus_ and _Melampodes_ in

[012-153]_Pliny_,[A] or _Apollonius Tyanæus_ in _Porphyry_[B] might formerly have

[012-154]done. Had the _Pygmies_ ever spoke any _Language_ intelligible by Mankind,

[012-155]this might have furnished our _Historians_ with notable Subjects for their

[012-156]_Novels_; and no doubt but we should have had plenty of them.

 
[Footnote A: _Plinij Nat. Hist._ lib. 10. cap. 49.]

 
[Footnote B: _Porphyrius de Abstinentia_, lib. 3. pag. m. 103.]

[012-157]But _Albertus Magnus_, who was so lucky as to guess that the _Pygmies_

[012-158]were a sort of _Apes_; that he should afterwards make these _Apes_ to

[012-159]_speak_, was very unfortunate, and spoiled all; and he do's it, methinks,

[012-160]so very awkwardly, that it is as difficult almost to understand his

[012-161]Language as his _Apes_; if the Reader has a mind to attempt it, he will

[012-162]find it in the Margin.[A]

 
[Footnote A: _Si qui Homines sunt Silvestres, sicut Pygmeus, non secundum

[012-163]unam rationem nobiscum dicti sunt Homines, sed aliquod habent Hominis in

[012-164]quadam deliberatione & Loquela, &c._ A little after adds, _Voces quædam

[012-165](sc. Animalia) formant ad diversos conceptus quos habent, sicut Homo &

[012-166]Pygmæus; & quædam non faciunt hoc, sicut multitudo fere tota aliorum

[012-167]Animalium. Adhuc autem eorum quæ ex ratione cogitativa formant voces,

[012-168]quædam sunt succumbentia, quædam autem non succumbentia. Dico autem

[012-169]succumbentia, à conceptu Animæ cadentia & mota ad Naturæ Instinctum, sicut

[012-170]Pygmeus, qui non, sequitur rationem Loquelæ sed Naturæ Instinctum; Homo

[012-171]autem non succumbit sed sequitur rationem._ Albert. Magn. de Animal. lib.

[012-172]1. cap. 3. p.m. 3.]
