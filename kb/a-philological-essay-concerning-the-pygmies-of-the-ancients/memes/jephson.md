# A. J. Mounteney Jephson

A. J. Mounteney Jephson (1858–1908) was a British explorer who served as one of [[stanley|Henry Morton Stanley]]'s officers during the Emin Pasha Relief Expedition (1887–1889). His observations of the pygmy peoples inhabiting the Ituri Forest region of Central Africa contributed significantly to European knowledge of these populations in the late nineteenth century.

## Discovery of the Central African Pygmies

Jephson's contribution to the pygmy question is documented in Section 3 of [[edward-tyson|Tyson]]'s essay, where Tyson surveys the accumulating nineteenth-century evidence for the existence of diminutive races in equatorial Africa. Tyson records Jephson's findings concerning the geographic distribution of these peoples:

> Jephson states that he found continual traces of them from 27° 30' E. long., a few miles above the Equator, up to the edge of the great forest, five days' march from Lake Albert. He also says that they are a hardy daring race, always ready for war, and are much feared by their neighbours. (003-057–003-060)

The region described by Jephson spans a substantial tract of what is now the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo, placing the pygmy populations within the vast Ituri rainforest system.

## Context in the Emin Pasha Expedition

Jephson's observations were made during the Emin Pasha Relief Expedition, the last major European expedition into the African interior. Alongside Stanley and other expedition members including [[schweinfurth|Georg Schweinfurth]], Jephson helped document the physical characteristics, territorial range, and social relations of the Akka and related pygmy groups. His description of them as "a hardy daring race, always ready for war" countered the contemporary stereotype of pygmy peoples as timid forest-dwellers.

## Relevance to Tyson's Argument

Tyson cites Jephson alongside other nineteenth-century observers to demonstrate the reality of diminutive human populations in Africa — evidence that supported the credibility of ancient Greek and Roman accounts of [[pygmies|Pygmies]]. The geographic precision of Jephson's report (longitude coordinates, distances from known landmarks) distinguished it from earlier travelers' tales and gave it scientific credibility in Tyson's view.
