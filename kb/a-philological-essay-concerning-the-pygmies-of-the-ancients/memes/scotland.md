# Scotland

## Overview

Scotland appears in Tyson's essay primarily through the lens of David MacRitchie's theory connecting fairy-lore to prehistoric dwarf races. Windle discusses the Scottish evidence extensively in Sections 5 through 9, examining the kitchen-middens, lake-dwellings, and the identification of the Picts as a diminutive race whose traditions survived in Highland folklore.

## In Ancient Geography

In antiquity, the region north of Hadrian's Wall was known as Caledonia to the Romans. However, the ancient sources cited by Tyson do not place pygmies in the British Isles directly — rather, the Scottish connection is a modern (nineteenth-century) scholarly construct through comparative folklore and archaeology. Windle notes that the region equal in area to about two-thirds of Scotland is inhabited by the Wambutti pygmy people in Africa (003-024), using the geographic comparison purely for scale.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

Scotland's pygmy connection derives from three strands of evidence discussed by Windle:

First, the fairy mounds and "Picts' houses" of the Scottish Highlands. Windle writes that "fairies are called 'Pechs' in Scotland" (008-067), a term etymologically linked to Picts, whom MacRitchie identifies as a historical dwarf race.

Second, the survival of Lapp-like traditions in the Scottish Highlands. Stories are told in the Highlands that "seem to be derived from the habits of Scotch tribes like the Lapps in our day" (008-042). The theory holds that "these Scotch traditions relate to the tribes who made kitchen-middens and lake-dwellings in Scotland, and that they were allied to Lapps" (008-052–053).

Third, the churches built by dwarfs. Windle mentions among examples of dwarf-built structures "the churches built by dwarfs in Scotland and Brittany" (007-022), connecting Scottish folklore to the broader European tradition of megalithic builders conceived as a diminutive race.

## Tyson's Discussion

Windle's treatment of Scotland is embedded in a critical evaluation of MacRitchie's theory that "the elves, trolls, and fairies" (008-058) of European folklore represent memories of an actual prehistoric dwarf population. Windle examines this theory across multiple chapters, noting that MacRitchie's view has been formulated to explain "the sepulchral mounds or 'fairy hills' in which, according to tradition, the fairies had their dwellings" — including mound-dwellings in "Scotland, Ireland, Brittany, and India" (009-124).

While Windle acknowledges the breadth of MacRitchie's evidence, he ultimately expresses reservations: "For the reasons just enumerated, I am unable to accept Mr. MacRitchie's theory" (009-065) that all fairy-lore derives from memories of a prehistoric dwarf race. Nevertheless, he grants that "Mr. MacRitchie has done good service in calling attention to the remarkable connection between the fairy-lore of Europe and the actual existence of dwarf races" (009-073).

Tyson also references Scotland in an unrelated context, citing reports of "_Scotland Geese_ that grow upon Trees" (015-146) — the barnacle goose myth — as an example of the credulity of early natural historians.
