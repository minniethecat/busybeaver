Hermann Schaaffhausen was a German anthropologist and anatomist, best
known for his early work on the original Neanderthal remains discovered
in 1856.

In the editorial apparatus of Edward Tyson's _Philological Essay
Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_, Schaaffhausen is cited as an
authority who compiled anthropometric data on African populations
relevant to the question of historical pygmy stature. (003-121)

## Anthropological Contribution

The reference to Schaaffhausen appears in a footnote to Tyson's Section
3, where the modern editors supplement Tyson's seventeenth-century
account with nineteenth-century anthropological data.

Schaaffhausen quoted an 1853 article from the _Morgenblatt_—a German
cultural periodical—in his own contribution to the _Archiv für
Anthropologie_ (1866, p. 166), which reported measurements and
descriptions of diminutive African populations encountered by European
travellers. (003-121)

This editorial citation demonstrates how Tyson's essay continued to
accumulate supporting evidence long after its original 1699 publication,
as later anthropologists like Schaaffhausen independently documented
the existence of short-statured peoples in regions corresponding to
the "pygmy" territories described by Homer, Herodotus, and Aristotle.
