Adrian Spigelius (1578–1625), also known as Adriaan van den Spiegel, was a Flemish anatomist and botanist who served as professor of anatomy at the University of Padua. His major work, *De Corporis Humani Fabrica*, was published posthumously in 1627.

Tyson cites Spigelius five times in his essay, positioning him as one of the most prominent modern skeptics who denied the reality of Pygmies.

## Spigelius the Skeptic

Tyson groups Spigelius with Isaac Casaubon as scholars who "utterly deny the Being of Pygmies, and look upon them as a Figment only of the Ancients" (017-152). Their skepticism was rooted in the argument that the Age of Discovery had failed to locate any genuine Pygmies.

As Tyson summarizes the objection: "since the whole World is now discovered, how happens it, that these" Pygmies have not been found (015-120)? This was one of the most powerful skeptical arguments against Pygmy existence.

## A Witness Examined

Spigelius produced what appeared to be compelling evidence: a witness who claimed to have examined "the very place, where the Pygmies were said to be" (013-023). However, the testimony proved less decisive than it first appeared.

Tyson, drawing on Bartholine's critique, notes that "had Adrian Spigelius considered" the full implications of a certain passage in Aristotle, "he would not so soon have left Aristotle's Opinion" (015-069).

## The Definition from Stature

Spigelius offered an etymological definition of the Pygmies:

> Hoc loco de Pygmæis dicendum erat, qui para pygonos dicti à statura, quæ ulnam non excedunt.
> (013-026)

The definition derives the name from the Greek *pygme* (cubit), claiming Pygmies do not exceed a cubit in height. The footnote cites "Adrian. Spigelij de Corporis Humani fabrica, lib. 1. cap. 7. p.m. 15."
