# Section 5

[005-001]"So when inclement winters vex the plain

[005-002]With piercing frosts or thick-descending rain,

[005-003]To warmer seas the cranes embodied fly,

[005-004]With noise and order, through the midway sky;

[005-005]To Pigmy nations wounds and death they bring,

[005-006]And all the war descends upon the wing."

[005-007]Attention may here be drawn to [[edward-tyson|Tyson]]'s quotation (p. 78) from Vossius as

[005-008]to the trade driven by the Pigmies in elephants' tusks, since, as we have

[005-009]seen, this corresponds with what we now know as to the habits of the

[005-010]Akkas.

[005-011]The account which [[herodotus|Herodotus]] gives of the expedition of the Nasamonians is

[005-012]well known. Five men, chosen by lot from amongst their fellows, crossed

[005-013]the desert of Lybia, and, having marched several days in deep sand,

[005-014]perceived trees growing in the midst of the plain. They approached and

[005-015]commenced to eat the fruit which they bore. Scarcely had they begun to

[005-016]taste it, when they were surprised by a great number of men of a stature

[005-017]much inferior to the middle height, who seized them and carried them off.

[005-018]They were eventually taken to a city, the inhabitants of which were black.

[005-019]Near this city ran a considerable river whose course was from west to

[005-020]east, and in which crocodiles were found. In his account of the Akkas, Mr.

[005-021]Stanley believed that he had discovered the representatives of the Pigmies

[005-022]mentioned in this history. Speaking of one of these, he says,[A]

[005-023]"Twenty-six centuries ago his ancestors captured the five young Nasamonian

[005-024]explorers, and made merry with them at their villages on the banks of the

[005-025]Niger." It may be correct to say that, at the period alluded to, the dwarf

[005-026]races of Africa were in more continuous occupancy of the land than is now

[005-027]the case, but such an identification as that just mentioned gives a false

[005-028]idea of the position of the Pigmies of Herodotus. De Quatrefages, after a

[005-029]most careful examination of the question in all its aspects, finds himself

[005-030]obliged to conclude, either that the Pigmy race seen by the Nasamonians

[005-031]still exists on the north of the Niger, which has been identified with the

[005-032]river alluded to by Herodotus, but has not, up to the present, been

[005-033]discovered; or that it has disappeared from those regions.

 
[Footnote A: _Op. supra cit._, ii. 40.]

[005-034][[pomponius-mela|Pomponius Mela]] has also his account of African Pigmies. Beyond the Arabian

[005-035]Gulf, and at the bottom of an indentation of the Red Sea, he places the

[005-036]Panchæans, also called Ophiophagi, on account of the fact that they fed

[005-037]upon serpents. More within the Arabian bay than the Panchæans are the

[005-038]Pigmies, a minute race, which became exterminated in the wars which it was

[005-039]compelled to wage with the Cranes for the preservation of its fruits. The

[005-040]region indicated somewhat corresponds with that which is assigned to the

[005-041]Dokos by their describer. In this district, too, other dwarf races have

[005-042]been reported. The French writer whom I have so often cited says, "The

[005-043]tradition of Eastern African Pigmies has never been lost by the Arabs. At

[005-044]every period the geographers of this nation have placed their River of

[005-045]Pigmies much more to the south. It is in this region, a little to the

[005-046]north of the Equator, and towards the 32° of east longitude, that the Rev.

[005-047]Fr. Léon des Avanchers has found the Wa-Berrikimos or Cincallès, whose

[005-048]stature is about four feet four inches. The information gathered by M.

[005-049]D'Abbadie places towards the 6° of north latitude the Mallas or

[005-050]Mazé-Malléas, with a stature of five feet. Everything indicates that there

[005-051]exist, at the south of the Galla country, different negro tribes of small

[005-052]stature. It seems difficult to me not to associate them with the Pigmies

[005-053]of Pomponius Mela. Only they have retreated farther south. Probably this

[005-054]change had already taken place at the time when the Roman geographer wrote;

[005-055]it is, therefore, comprehensible that he may have regarded them as having

[005-056]disappeared."

[005-057]Tyson (p. 29) quotes the following passage from [[photius|Photius]]:--"That Nonnosus

[005-058]sailing from Pharsa, when he came to the farthermost of the islands, a

[005-059]thing very strange to be heard of happened to him; for he lighted on some

[005-060](animals) in shape and appearance like men, but little of stature, and of

[005-061]a black colour, and thick covered with hair all over their bodies. The

[005-062]women, who were of the same stature, followed the men. They were all

[005-063]naked, only the elder of them, both men and women, covered their privy

[005-064]parts with a small skin. They seemed not at all fierce or wild; they had a

[005-065]human voice, but their dialect was altogether unknown to everybody that

[005-066]lived about them, much more to those that were with Nonnosus. They lived

[005-067]upon sea-oysters and fish that were cast out of the sea upon the island.

[005-068]They had no courage for seeing our men; they were frighted, as we are at

[005-069]the sight of the greatest wild beast." It is not easy to identify this

[005-070]race with any existing tribe of Pigmies, but the hairiness of their

[005-071]bodies, and above all their method of clothing themselves, leave no doubt

[005-072]that in this account we have a genuine story of some group of

[005-073]small-statured blacks.

[005-074]From the foregoing account it will be seen that it is possible with more

[005-075]or less accuracy and certainty to identify most of those races which,

[005-076]described by the older writers, had been rejected by their successors.

[005-077]Time has brought their revenge to [[aristotle|Aristotle]] and Pliny by showing that they

[005-078]were right, where Tyson, and even Buffon, were wrong.

[005-079]III.

[005-080]The little people of story and legend have a much wider area of

[005-081]distribution than those of real life, and it is the object of this section

[005-082]to give some idea of their localities and dwellings. Imperfect as such an

[005-083]account must necessarily be, it will yet suffice I trust in some measure

[005-084]to show that, like the England of Arthurian times, all the world is

[005-085]"fulfilled of faëry."

[005-086]In dealing with this part of the subject, it would be possible, following

[005-087]the example of Keightley, to treat the little folk of each country

[005-088]separately. But a better idea of their nature, and certainly one which for

[005-089]my purpose will be more satisfactory, can, I think, be obtained by

[005-090]classifying them according to the nature of their habitations, and

[005-091]mentioning incidentally such other points concerning them as it may seem

[005-092]advisable to bring out.

[005-093]1. In the first place, then, fairies are found dwelling in mounds of

[005-094]different kinds, or in the interior of hills. This form of habitation is

[005-095]so frequently met with in Scotch and Irish accounts of the fairies, that

[005-096]it will not be necessary for me to burden these pages with instances,

[005-097]especially since I shall have to allude to them in a further section in

[005-098]greater detail. Suffice it to say, that many instances of such an

[005-099]association in the former country will be found in the pages of Mr.

[005-100]MacRitchie's works, whilst as to the latter, I shall content myself by

[005-101]quoting Sir William Wilde's statement, that every green "rath" in that

[005-102]country is consecrated to the "good people." In England there are numerous

[005-103]instances of a similar kind. Gervase of Tilbury in the thirteenth century

[005-104]mentions such a spot in Gloucestershire: "There is in the county of

[005-105]Gloucester a forest abounding in boars, stags, and every species of game

[005-106]that England produces. In a grovy lawn of this forest there is a little

[005-107]mount, rising in a point to the height of a man." With this mount he

[005-108]associates the familiar story of the offering of refreshment to travellers

[005-109]by its unseen inhabitants. In Warwickshire, the mound upon which

[005-110]Kenilworth Castle is built was formerly a fairy habitation.[A] Ritson[B]

[005-111]mentions that the "fairies frequented many parts of the Bishopric of

[005-112]Durham." There is a hillock or tumulus near Bishopton, and a large hill

[005-113]near Billingham, both of which used in former time to be "haunted by

[005-114]fairies." Even Ferry-hill, a well-known stage between Darlington and

[005-115]Durham, is evidently a corruption of "Fairy-hill." In Yorkshire a similar

[005-116]story attaches to the sepulchral barrow of Willey How,[C] and in Sussex to

[005-117]a green mound called the Mount in the parish of Pulborough.[D] The fairies

[005-118]formerly frequented Bussers Hill in St. Mary's Isle, one of the Scilly

[005-119]group.[E] The Bryn-yr-Ellyllon,[F] or Fairy-hill, near Mold, may be cited

[005-120]as a similar instance in Wales, which must again be referred to.

 
[Footnote A: _Testimony of Tradition_, p. 142.]

 
[Footnote B: _Op. cit._, p. 56.]

 
[Footnote C: _Folk Lore_, ii. 115.]

 
[Footnote D: _Folk Lore Record_, i. 16 and 28.]

 
[Footnote E: _Ritson_, p. 62.]

 
[Footnote F: Dawkins, _Early Man in Britain_, p. 433.]

[005-121]The pages of Keightley's work contain instances of hill-inhabiting fairies

[005-122]in Scandinavia, Denmark, the Isle of Rugen, Iceland, Germany, and

[005-123]Switzerland. It is not only in [[europe|Europe]], however, that this form of

[005-124]habitation is to be met with; we find it also in America. The Sioux have a

[005-125]curious superstition respecting a mound near the mouth of the Whitestone

[005-126]River, which they call the Mountain of Little People or Little Spirits;

[005-127]they believe that it is the abode of little devils in the human form, of

[005-128]about eighteen inches high and with remarkably large heads; they are armed

[005-129]with sharp arrows, in the use of which they are very skilful. These little

[005-130]spirits are always on the watch to kill those who should have the

[005-131]hardihood to approach their residence. The tradition is that many have

[005-132]suffered from their malice, and that, among others, three Maha Indians

[005-133]fell a sacrifice to them a few years since. This has inspired all the

[005-134]neighbouring nations, Sioux, Mahas, and Ottoes, with such terror, that no

[005-135]consideration could tempt them to visit the hill.[A]

 
[Footnote A: Lewis and Clarke, _Travels to the Source of the Missouri

[005-136]River._ Quoted in _Flint Chips_, p. 346. The tale is also given in _Folk

[005-137]Lore, Oriental and American_ (Gibbings & Co.), p. 45.]

[005-138]The mounds or hills inhabited by the fairies are, however, of very diverse

[005-139]kinds, as we discover when we attempt to analyse their actual nature. In

[005-140]some cases they are undoubtedly natural elevations. Speaking of the

[005-141]exploration of the Isle of Unst, Hunt[A] says that the term "Fairy Knowe"

[005-142]is applied alike to artificial and to natural mounds. "We visited," he

[005-143]states, "two 'Fairy Knowes' in the side of the hill near the turning of

[005-144]the road from Reay Wick to Safester, and found that these wonderful relics

[005-145]were merely natural formations. The workmen were soon convinced of this,

[005-146]and our digging had the effect of proving to them that the fairies had

[005-147]nothing to do with at least two of these hillocks." The same may surely be

[005-148]said of that favourite and important fairy haunt Tomnahurich, near

[005-149]Inverness, though Mr. MacRitchie seems to think that an investigation,

[005-150]were such possible, of its interior, might lead to a different

[005-151]explanation.

 
[Footnote A: _Anthrop. Mems._, ii. 294.]

[005-152]In other cases, and these are of great importance in coming to a

[005-153]conclusion as to the origin of fairy tales, the mounds inhabited by the

[005-154]little people are of a sepulchral nature. This is the case in the instance

[005-155]of Willey How, which, when explored by Canon Greenwell, was found, in

[005-156]spite of its size and the enormous care evidently bestowed upon its

[005-157]construction, to be merely a cenotaph. A grave there was, sunk more than

[005-158]twelve feet deep in the chalk rock; but no corporeal tenant had ever

[005-159]occupied it.

[005-160]This fact is still more clearly shown in the remarkable case mentioned by

[005-161]Professor Boyd Dawkins. A barrow called Bryn-yr-Ellyllon (Fairy-hill),

[005-162]near Mold, was said to be haunted by a ghost clad in golden armour which

[005-163]had been seen to enter it. The barrow was opened in the year 1832, and was

[005-164]found to contain the skeleton of a man wearing a golden corselet of

[005-165]Etruscan workmanship.

[005-166]The same may be said respecting that famous fairy-hill in Ireland, the

[005-167]Brugh of the Boyne, though Mr. MacRitchie seems to regard it as having

[005-168]been a dwelling-place. Mr. Coffey in a most careful study appears to me to

[005-169]have finally settled the question.[A] He speaks of the remains as those of

[005-170]probably the most remarkable of the pre-Christian cemeteries of Ireland.

[005-171]Of the stone basins, whose nature Mr. MacRitchie regards as doubtful, he

[005-172]says, "There can be hardly any doubt but that they served the purpose of

[005-173]some rude form of sarcophagus, or of a receptacle for urns." Mr. Coffey

[005-174]quotes the account from the Leadhar na huidri respecting cemeteries, in

[005-175]which Brugh is mentioned as amongst the chief of those existing before the

[005-176]faith (i.e. before the introduction of Christianity). "The nobles of the

[005-177]Tuatha de Danann were used to bury at Brugh (i.e. the Dagda with his three

[005-178]sons; also Lugaidh, and Oe, and Ollam, and Ogma, and Etan the Poetess, and

[005-179]Corpre, the son of Etan), and Cremthain followed them, because his wife

[005-180]Nar was of the Tuatha Dea, and it was she solicited him that he should

[005-181]adopt Brugh as a burial-place for himself and his descendants, and this

[005-182]was the cause that they did not bury at Cruachan." Mr. Coffey also quotes

[005-183]O'Hartagain's poem, which seems to bear in Mr. MacRitchie's favour:--
