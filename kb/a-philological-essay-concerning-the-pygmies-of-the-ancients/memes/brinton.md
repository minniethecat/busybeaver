Daniel Garrison Brinton (1837–1899) was a distinguished American ethnologist and anthropologist who held professorships at the University of Pennsylvania and the Academy of Natural Sciences of Philadelphia. A pioneering figure in American anthropology, Brinton published extensively on Native American languages, mythology, and racial classification. His synthetic work *The American Race* (1891) was the first comprehensive classification of the indigenous peoples of the Americas.

## Direct Consultation by Tyson

Brinton is one of the few scholars personally consulted by Tyson during the preparation of the essay. The prefatory note acknowledges "Dr. Brinton" among those whom the author thanks for assistance (P03-012). More significantly, Section 3 records that Brinton was consulted on the specific question of whether any "pigmy race" existed in the Americas: "Dr. Brinton, the distinguished American ethnologist, to whom I applied for information on this point, has been good enough to write to me that, in his opinion" no such race was known to present knowledge (003-139). This personal correspondence, solicited specifically for the essay, demonstrates the seriousness with which Tyson pursued the comparative evidence.

Brinton's negative finding — that no "pigmy race" was known in the Americas — serves an important function in the essay's argumentative architecture. It establishes that while traditions of "little people" are widespread among Native American cultures, these do not correspond to any identifiable diminutive ethnic group. This distinction between folk tradition and ethnographic reality is central to the essay's overall treatment of the pygmy question.

## Micmac Evidence

In Section 9, Tyson reports additional information received directly from Brinton: "Dr. Brinton tells me that the Micmacs have tales of similar Pigmies, whom they call Wiguladumooch, who tie people with cords during their sleep, &c." (009-040). This Micmac tradition of diminutive nocturnal beings — their name untranslatable by Brinton — provides a specific Native American parallel to the European fairy motifs catalogued elsewhere in the essay. The detail emphasises the personal, consultative relationship between Tyson and Brinton, distinguishing Brinton's contributions from the merely literary sources that populate much of the essay's apparatus.

## Significance in the Essay's Methodology

Brinton's role in the essay is methodologically significant. Rather than relying solely on published ethnological literature, Tyson actively solicited expert opinion on a specific question from a leading authority. This practice — combining literary survey with personal correspondence — reflects the transitional state of late-nineteenth-century scholarship, in which the protocols of professional academic inquiry were still coalescing. Brinton's personal communication to Tyson is treated as evidence on par with published sources, a practice common in the period but marking the essay as pre-dating the more formalized citational norms of twentieth-century anthropology.
