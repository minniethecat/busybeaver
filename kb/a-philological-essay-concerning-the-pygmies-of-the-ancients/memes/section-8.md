# Section 8

[008-001]Dr. Nansen, speaking of the Ignerssuit (plural of Ignersuak, which means

[008-002]"great fire"), says that they are for the most part good spirits, inclined

[008-003]to help men. The entrance to their dwellings is on the sea-shore.

[008-004]According to the Eskimo legend, "The first earth which came into existence

[008-005]had neither seas nor mountains, but was quite smooth. When the One above

[008-006]was displeased with the people upon it, He destroyed the world. It burst

[008-007]open, and the people fell down into the rifts and became Ignerssuit and

[008-008]the water poured over everything."[A] The spirits here alluded to appear

[008-009]to be the same as those described by Mr. Boas as Uissuit in his monograph

[008-010]on the Central Eskimo. He describes them as "a strange people that live in

[008-011]the sea. They are dwarfs, and are frequently seen between Iglulik and

[008-012]Netchillik, where the Anganidjen live, an Innuit tribe whose women are in

[008-013]the habit of tracing rings around their eyes. There are men and women

[008-014]among the Uissuit, and they live in deep water, never coming to the

[008-015]surface. When the Innuit wish to see them, they go in their boats to a

[008-016]place where they cannot see the bottom, and try to catch them with hooks

[008-017]which they slowly move up and down. As soon as they get a bite they draw

[008-018]in the line. The Uissuit are thus drawn up; but no sooner do they approach

[008-019]the surface than they dive down headlong again, only their legs having

[008-020]emerged from the water. The Innuit have never succeeded in getting one out

[008-021]of the water."[A]

[008-022]8. Amongst habitations not coming under any of the above categories may be

[008-023]mentioned the moors and open places affected by the Cornish fairies, and

[008-024]lastly the curious residences of the Kirkonwaki or Church-folk of the

[008-025]Finns. "It is an article of faith with the Finns that there dwell under

[008-026]the altar in every church little misshapen beings which they call

[008-027]Kirkonwaki, i.e., Church-folk. When the wives of these little people have

[008-028]a difficult labour, they are relieved if a Christian woman visits them and

[008-029]lays her hand upon them. Such service is always rewarded by a gift of gold

[008-030]and silver."[A] These folk evidently correspond to the Kirkgrims of

[008-031]Scandinavian countries, and the traditions respecting both are probably

[008-032]referable to the practice of foundation sacrifices.

 
[Footnote A: Grimm ap. Keightley, p. 488.]

[008-033]IV.

[008-034]The subject of Pigmy races and fairy tales cannot be considered to have

[008-035]been in any sense fully treated without some consideration of a theory

[008-036]which, put forward by various writers and in connection with the legends

[008-037]of diverse countries, has recently been formulated by Mr. MacRitchie in a

[008-038]number of most interesting and suggestive books and papers. An early

[008-039]statement of this theory is to be found in a paper by Mr. J.F. Campbell,

[008-040]in which he stated, "It is somewhat remarkable that traditions still

[008-041]survive in the Highlands of Scotland which seem to be derived from the

[008-042]habits of Scotch tribes like the Lapps in our day. Stories are told in

[008-043]Sutherlandshire about a 'witch' who milked deer; a 'ghost' once became

[008-044]acquainted with a forester, and at his suggestion packed all her

[008-045]plenishing on a herd of deer, when forced to flit by another and a bigger

[008-046]'ghost;' the green mounds in which 'fairies' are supposed to dwell closely

[008-047]resemble the outside of Lapp huts. The fairies themselves are not

[008-048]represented as airy creatures in gauze wings and spangles, but they appear

[008-049]in tradition as small cunning people, eating and drinking, living close at

[008-050]hand in their green mound, stealing children and cattle, milk and food,

[008-051]from their bigger neighbours. They are uncanny, but so are the Lapps. My

[008-052]own opinion is that these Scotch traditions relate to the tribes who made

[008-053]kitchen-middens and lake-dwellings in Scotland, and that they were allied

[008-054]to Lapps."[A] Such in essence is Mr. MacRitchie's theory, which has been

[008-055]so admirably summarised by Mr. Jacobs in the first of that series of

[008-056]fairy-tale books which has added a new joy to life, that I shall do myself

[008-057]the pleasure of quoting his statement in this place. He says: "Briefly

[008-058]put, Mr. MacRitchie's view is that the elves, trolls, and fairies

[008-059]represented in popular tradition are really the mound-dwellers, whose

[008-060]remains have been discovered in some abundance in the form of green

[008-061]hillocks, which have been artificially raised over a long and low passage

[008-062]leading to a central chamber open to the sky. Mr. MacRitchie shows that in

[008-063]several instances traditions about trolls or 'good people' have attached

[008-064]themselves to mounds which long afterwards, on investigation, turned out

[008-065]to be evidently the former residence of men of smaller build than the

[008-066]mortals of to-day. He goes on further to identify these with the Picts--

[008-067]fairies are called 'Pechs' in Scotland--and other early races, but with

[008-068]these ethnological equations we need not much concern ourselves. It is

[008-069]otherwise with the mound traditions and their relation, if not to fairy

[008-070]tales in general, to tales about fairies, trolls, elves, &c. These are

[008-071]very few in number, and generally bear the character of anecdotes. The

[008-072]fairies, &c., steal a child; they help a wanderer to a drink and then

[008-073]disappear into a green hill; they help cottagers with their work at night,

[008-074]but disappear if their presence is noticed; human midwives are asked to

[008-075]help fairy mothers; fairy maidens marry ordinary men, or girls marry and

[008-076]live with fairy husbands. All such things may have happened and bear no

[008-077]such _a priori_ marks of impossibility as speaking animals, flying through

[008-078]the air, and similar incidents of the folk-tale pure and simple. If, as

[008-079]archaeologists tell us, there was once a race of men in Northern [[europe|Europe]]

[008-080]very short and hairy, that dwelt in underground chambers artificially

[008-081]concealed by green hillocks, it does not seem unlikely that odd survivors

[008-082]of the race should have lived on after they had been conquered and nearly

[008-083]exterminated by Aryan invaders, and should occasionally have performed

[008-084]something like the pranks told of fairies and trolls."[B] In the same

[008-085]place, and also in another article,[C] the writer just quoted has applied

[008-086]this theory to the explanation of the story of "Childe Rowland."

 
[Footnote A: _Journ. Ethnol. Soc._, 1869-70, p. 325.]

 
[Footnote B: _English Fairy Tales_, p. 241.]

 
[Footnote C: _Folk Lore_, ii. 126.]

[008-087]Mr. MacRitchie has, in another paper,[A] collected a number of instances

[008-088]of the use of the word _Sith_ in connection with hillocks and tumuli,

[008-089]which are the resort of the fairies. Here also he discusses the possible

[008-090]connection of that word with that of _Tshud_, the title of the vanished

[008-091]supernatural inhabitants of the land amongst the Finns and other "Altaic"

[008-092]Turanian tribes of Russia, as in other places he has endeavoured to trace

[008-093]a connection between the Finns and the Feinne. Into these etymological

[008-094]questions I have no intention to enter, since I am not qualified to do so,

[008-095]nor is it necessary, as they have been fully dealt with by Mr. Nutt, whose

[008-096]opinion on this point is worthy of all attention.[B] But it may be

[008-097]permitted to me to inquire how far Mr. MacRitchie's views tally with the

[008-098]facts mentioned in the foregoing section. I shall therefore allude to a

[008-099]few points which appear to me to show that the origin of the belief in

[008-100]fairies cannot be settled in so simple a manner as has been suggested, but

[008-101]is a question of much greater complexity--one in which, as Mr. Tylor

[008-102]says, more than one mythic element combines to make up the whole.

 
[Footnote A: _Journ. Roy. Soc. Antiq. Ireland_, iii. 367.]

 
[Footnote A: _Folk and Hero Tales from Argyleshire_, p. 420.]

[008-103](1.) In the first place, then, it seems clear, so far as our present

[008-104]knowledge teaches us, that there never was a really Pigmy race inhabiting

[008-105]the northern parts of Scotland.

[008-106]The scanty evidence which we have on this point, so far as it goes, proves

[008-107]the truth of this assertion. Mr. Carter Blake found in the Muckle Heog of

[008-108]the Island of Unst, one of the Shetlands, together with stone vessels,

[008-109]human interments of persons of considerable stature and of great muscular

[008-110]strength. Speaking of the Keiss skeletons, Professor Huxley says that the

[008-111]males are, the one somewhat above, and the other probably about the

[008-112]average stature; while the females are short, none exceeding five feet two

[008-113]inches or three inches in height.[A] And Dr. Garson, treating of the

[008-114]osteology of the ancient inhabitants of the Orkneys, says that the female

[008-115]skeleton which he examined was about five feet two inches in height, i.e.,

[008-116]about the mean height of the existing races of England.[B] There is no

[008-117]evidence that Lapps and Eskimo ever visited these parts of the world; and

[008-118]if they did, as we have seen, their stature, though stunted, cannot fairly

[008-119]be described as pigmy. Even if we grant that the stature of the early

[008-120]races did not average more than five feet two inches, which, by the way,

[008-121]was the height of the great Napoleon, it is more than doubtful whether it

[008-122]fell so far short of that of succeeding races as to cause us to imagine

[008-123]that it gave rise to tales about a race of dwarfs.

 
[Footnote A: Laing, _Prehistoric Remains of Caithness_, p. 101.]

 
[Footnote B: _Journ. Anthrop. Inst._, xiii. 60.]

[008-124](2.) The mounds with which the tales of little people are associated have

[008-125]not, in many cases, been habitations, but were natural or sepulchral in

[008-126]their nature. It may, of course, be argued that the story having once

[008-127]arisen in connection with one kind of mound, may, by a process easy to

[008-128]understand, have been transferred to other hillocks similar in appearance,

[008-129]though diverse in nature. It is difficult to see, however, how this could

[008-130]have occurred in Yorkshire and other parts of England, where it is not

[008-131]argued that the stunted inhabitants of the North ever penetrated. It is

[008-132]still more difficult to explain how similar legends can have originated in

[008-133]America in connection with mounds, since there never were Pigmy races in

[008-134]that continent.

[008-135](3.) The rude and simple arrangements of the interior of these mound

[008-136]dwellings might have, in the process of time, become altered into the

[008-137]gorgeous halls, decked with gold and silver and precious stones, as we

[008-138]find them in the stories; they might even, though this is much more

[008-139]difficult to understand, have become possessed of the capacity for being

[008-140]raised upon red pillars. But there is one pitch to which, I think, they

[008-141]could never have attained, and that is the importance which they assume

[008-142]when they become the external covering of a large and extensive tract of

[008-143]underground country. Here we are brought face to face with a totally

[008-144]different explanation, to which I shall recur in due course.

[008-145](4.) The little people are not by any means associated entirely with

[008-146]mounds, as the foregoing section is largely intended to show. Their

[008-147]habitations may be in or amongst stones, in caves, under the water, in

[008-148]trees, or amongst the glades of a forest; they may dwell on mountains, on

[008-149]moors, or even under the altars of churches. We may freely grant that some

[008-150]of these habitations fall into line with Mr. MacRitchie's theory, but they

[008-151]are not all susceptible of such an explanation.

[008-152](5.) The association of giants and dwarfs in certain places, even the

[008-153]confusion of the two races, seems somewhat difficult of explanation by

[008-154]this theory. In Ireland the distinction between the two classes is sharper

[008-155]than in other places, since, as Sir William Wilde pointed out, whilst

[008-156]every green rath in that island is consecrated to the fairies or "good

[008-157]people," the remains attributed to the giants are of a different character

[008-158]and probably of a later date. In some places, however, a mound similar to

[008-159]those often connected with fairies is associated with a giant, as is the

[008-160]case at Sessay parish, near Thirsk,[A] and at Fyfield in Wiltshire. The

[008-161]chambered tumulus at Luckington is spoken of as the Giant's Caves, and

[008-162]that at Nempnet in Somersetshire as the Fairy's Toot. In Denmark, tumuli

[008-163]seem to be described indifferently as Zettestuer ([[giants|Giants]]' Chambers) or

[008-164]Troldestuer (Fairies' Chambers).[B] In "Beowulf" a chambered tumulus is

[008-165]described, in the recesses of which were treasures watched over for three

[008-166]hundred years by a dragon. This barrow was of stone, and the work of

[008-167]giants.
