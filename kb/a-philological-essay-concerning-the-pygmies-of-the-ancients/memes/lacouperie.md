Albert Étienne Jean-Baptiste Terrien de Lacouperie (1844–1894) was a French orientalist and sinologist whose pioneering studies of ancient Chinese texts brought Chinese historical sources into the comparative discussion of early human populations. For Tyson, Lacouperie's research on the Bamboo Books annals provided a crucial link between East Asian historical records and the wider distribution of pygmy-type peoples across the ancient world.

## The Bamboo Books and the Tsiao-Yao

In Section 2 of the essay, Tyson surveys the present and historical distribution of Negrito and pygmy-type populations across Asia. After noting that modern China "no longer, so far as we know, contains any representatives of this type," he introduces Lacouperie's counter-evidence from ancient Chinese sources:

> Professor Lacouperie has recently shown that they formerly existed in that part of Asia. According to the annals of the Bamboo Books, "In the twenty-ninth year of the Emperor Yao, in spring, the chief of the Tsiao-Yao, or dark pigmies, came to court and offered as tribute feathers from the Mot." The Professor continues, "As shown by this entry, we begin with the semi-historic times as recorded in the 'Annals of the Bamboo Books'" (002-047)

The term "Tsiao-Yao" — which Lacouperie translated as "dark pigmies" — referred to a group of small-statured, dark-skinned people who paid tribute to the court of Emperor Yao, one of the legendary sage-kings of Chinese antiquity traditionally dated to the third millennium BC. The tribute of feathers from the "Mot" (possibly referring to a bird species or a region) was consistent with what was known of Negrito material culture across Southeast Asia and the Andaman Islands.

## Significance for Tyson's Argument

Lacouperie's evidence from the Bamboo Books — a chronicle text discovered in a tomb in AD 281 and containing annals from legendary times to the Zhou dynasty — served several purposes in Tyson's essay. First, it extended the documented range of pygmy-type peoples into East Asia, a region where they were no longer extant, reinforcing the pattern of retreat and marginalisation that characterised pygmy populations globally. Second, the semi-historical status of the Bamboo Books annals placed the Tsiao-Yao within a textual tradition rather than a purely mythological one, lending weight to the claim that these pygmy-type peoples were historical realities rather than legendary inventions.

Tyson's citation of Lacouperie also reflected the late-Victorian philological method that gave the essay its distinctive character: the conviction that ancient texts, properly interpreted, could yield ethnographic data as valuable as physical anthropology. Lacouperie's sinological expertise provided the linguistic and textual key that unlocked this Chinese evidence for a Western scholarly audience.

## Scholarly Legacy

Lacouperie was a controversial figure in his own time, known for bold diffusionist theories about the Western origins of Chinese civilisation that later scholarship largely rejected. His specific claim about the Tsiao-Yao, however, was grounded in a direct reading of the Bamboo Books text and did not depend on his broader theoretical framework. Modern scholarship continues to debate the identity of the groups described in early Chinese annals as dark-skinned and small-statured, with some researchers connecting them to Negrito populations known from archaeological and genetic evidence to have inhabited parts of southern China in prehistory.

## Key Works Cited

- **The Languages of China Before the Chinese** (1887) — Lacouperie's major work on the pre-Chinese linguistic landscape, including discussion of aboriginal populations displaced by Chinese expansion.
- **Catalogue of Chinese Coins** (1892) — His comprehensive numismatic catalogue for the British Museum.
