# Olaus Magnus

## Biography

Olaus Magnus (1490–1557) was a Swedish Archbishop, Historian, and Cartographer. He is best known for his _Historia de Gentibus Septentrionalibus_ (History of the Northern Peoples), published in 1555.

This encyclopedic work on the lands, peoples, and natural history of Scandinavia made him a widely cited authority in early modern Europe. His _Carta Marina_, a detailed map of the Nordic countries, was among the earliest and most influential cartographic representations of northern Europe.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Olaus Magnus enters the pygmy debate through [[bartholine]]'s _De Pygmæis_, where he is listed among authorities who had written on diminutive peoples. In Section 14, Tyson enumerates a long list of authors cited by Bartholine, including "Cassanius, Joh. Talentonius, Gellius, Pomp. Mela, and _Olaus Magnus_" (014-150).

In Section 15, Tyson returns to Olaus Magnus at greater length with a candid acknowledgment:

> "I had almost forgot _Olaus Magnus_, whom _Bartholine_ mentions in the close of this Chapter, but lays no great stress upon his Authority, because he tells us, he is fabulous in a great many other Relations."
> (015-004)

This double-edged acknowledgment — Bartholine cites Olaus but distances himself from his credibility — captures the ambiguous status of Olaus in the pygmy debate.

Tyson takes a measured view. While conceding that Olaus may "write some things by hear-say," he judges him less culpable than [[ctesias]], who "writes what he neither saw himself, or heard from any Body else" (015-016). This places Olaus in a middle tier of reliability.

Tyson is clear, however, that Olaus's Greenlanders were not real pygmies: "Not that I think _Olaus Magnus_ his _Greenlanders_ were real _Pygmies_, no more than _Ctesias_ his _Pygmies_ were real _Men_" (015-019). This distinction is central to Tyson's taxonomic argument throughout the essay.
