Olfert Dapper (1636–1689) was a Dutch physician and geographer who, despite never traveling outside the Netherlands, compiled one of the most influential seventeenth-century descriptions of Africa. His *Description de l'Afrique* (1686) became a standard reference work for European scholars.

Tyson cites Dapper five times in his essay, drawing on his observations of African peoples of unusually short stature and his account of the "Mimos" — a group inhabiting the region near the sources of the Nile.

## Dapper on African Peoples of Short Stature

Tyson notes that Dapper describes people "of less Stature than ordinary" living in a particular region of Africa (016-179). This observation appears in Tyson's broader survey of modern travelers' accounts that might corroborate the ancient reports of Pygmies.

Dapper's testimony is relayed second-hand through Isaac Vossius, who "takes this Account" from Dapper's work. Despite the indirect transmission, Tyson treats it as noteworthy evidence.

## The Mimos of the Upper Nile

One of Dapper's most distinctive contributions is his description of the "Mimos," a people living near the sources of the Nile. Tyson records:

> Dapper calls the Mimos, and tells us, they kill Elephants for to make a Traffick with their Teeth.
> (017-159)

This detail places Dapper's account among those describing small-statured Africans engaged in elephant hunting for the ivory trade.

## Taming Young Elephants

In another passage, Tyson draws on Dapper's report:

> the Indians take the Baris when young, and make them so tame, that they will do almost the work of domesticated animals.
> (018-129)

The footnote for this passage cites "Dapper Description de l'Afrique, p.m. 249" (018-150).

## Tyson's Regret

Tyson expresses regret that Dapper's *Description de l'Afrique*, published in 1686, "should have missed his" account of certain African Pygmy-related traditions available in earlier sources (003-101). This remark underscores Tyson's view that even the best modern compilations were incomplete.
