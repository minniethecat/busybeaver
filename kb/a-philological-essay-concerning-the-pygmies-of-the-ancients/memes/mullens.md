# Mullens

Mullens was a missionary or ethnographic observer who documented the customs and beliefs surrounding the Vazimba people of Madagascar during the nineteenth century. His observations concerning the ritual status of Vazimba tombs were cited by [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] in Section 3 of his *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*, as part of a discussion of possible pygmy populations in Madagascar and the western Indian Ocean.

## The Vazimba and Their Tombs

Tyson draws upon Mullens's testimony to describe the sacred character of Vazimba burial sites and their continuing significance in Malagasy religious practice. The Vazimba, described by earlier authorities such as [[rochon|Rochon]], were considered to be the aboriginal inhabitants of the island's central highlands:

> In idolatrous days, says Mullens, the Malagasy deified the Vazimba, and their so-called tombs were the most sacred objects in the country. (003-133–003-135)

This observation is significant because it documents the transition from ancestor veneration to outright deification — a pattern that Tyson recognized as common to diminutive aboriginal populations elsewhere. The "so-called tombs" phrase, as recorded by Mullens, suggests an ambiguity about whether these structures were actually sepulchral in nature, a point that echoes [[canon-greenwell|Canon Greenwell]]'s discovery of a cenotaph at Willey How.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Mullens's evidence served two functions in Tyson's argument. First, it corroborated the testimony of earlier writers like Rochon and [[oliver|Oliver]] that the Vazimba were of distinctly short stature, consistent with pygmy populations elsewhere. Second, it demonstrated the deep cultural imprint left by these aboriginal peoples — to the point of deification — which Tyson took as evidence of their antiquity and former dominance before displacement by taller, later-arriving populations. This pattern of displacement and subsequent mythologization of diminutive predecessors was, in Tyson's view, a recurring global phenomenon.
