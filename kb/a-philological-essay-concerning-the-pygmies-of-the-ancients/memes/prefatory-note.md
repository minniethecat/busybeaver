# PREFATORY NOTE

[P03-001]It is only necessary for me to state here, what I have mentioned in the

[P03-002]Introduction, that my account of the habits of the Pigmy races of legend

[P03-003]and myth makes no pretence of being in any sense a complete or exhaustive

[P03-004]account of the literature of this subject. I have contented myself with

[P03-005]bringing forward such tales as seemed of value for the purpose of

[P03-006]establishing the points upon which I desire to lay emphasis.

[P03-007]I have elsewhere expressed my obligations to M. De Quatrefage's book on

[P03-008]Pigmies, obligations which will be at once recognised by those familiar

[P03-009]with that monograph. To his observations I have endeavoured to add such

[P03-010]other published facts as I have been able to gather in relation to these

[P03-011]peoples.

[P03-012]I have to thank Professors Sir William Turner, Haddon, Schlegel, Brinton,

[P03-013]and Topinard for their kindness in supplying me with information in

[P03-014]response to my inquiries on several points.

[P03-015]Finally, I have to acknowledge my indebtedness to Professor Alexander

[P03-016]Macalister, President of the Anthropological Institute, and to Mr. E.

[P03-017]Sidney Hartland, for their kindness in reading through, the former the

[P03-018]first two sections, and the latter the last two sections of the

[P03-019]Introduction, and for the valuable suggestions which both have made. These

[P03-020]gentlemen have laid me under obligations which I can acknowledge, but

[P03-021]cannot repay.

[P03-022]BERTRAM C. A. WINDLE.

[P03-023]MASON COLLEGE,

[P03-024]BIRMINGHAM, 1894.
