## Biography

Little is known of Karonzine beyond what Tyson's citation reveals. The
reference identifies Karonzine as "one of their most recent describers" of
the Lapps (004-024), indicating an anthropologist active in the late
nineteenth century who had conducted systematic study of the Sami people
of northern Scandinavia. Given the timeframe of Tyson's essay (1890s) and
the context of the citation, Karonzine's work likely appeared in
German-language anthropological journals of the period.

The name Karonzine suggests Russian or Eastern European origin, which would
be consistent with the geographical scope of Lapp studies, as the Sami
territory spans across Norway, Sweden, Finland, and the Kola Peninsula of
Russia.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tyson discusses the Lapps (Sami people) in Section 4 of the essay as part
of a careful examination of races whose stature has at times been claimed
to fall within the pygmy range. Karonzine's work provides the most
up-to-date anthropometric data available at the time of writing, dividing
the Lapps into two groups: Scandinavian and Russian, the former being
considered purer than the latter (004-024–004-025).

The key finding, as reported by Karonzine, is that the average male stature
of the Lapps is five feet — approximately 152 cm (004-025–004-026). Tyson
uses this figure to make an important argumentative move: while five feet
is certainly below the European average, it does not qualify as pygmy
stature in the scientific sense. As the essay itself concludes:

> "we must conclude that the Lapps, though a stunted race, are not pigmies,
> in the sense in which the word is scientifically employed."
> (004-031–004-033)

Karonzine's data thus serves a crucial role in the essay's methodology:
it demonstrates that Tyson does not uncritically accept every report of
short-statured people as evidence for pygmies. Instead, he applies
discriminating criteria, distinguishing between genuinely diminutive
races (four feet six to eight inches) and merely "stunted" populations
whose stature, while below average, remains outside the pygmy category.
This methodological rigor strengthens the credibility of the cases
Tyson does accept.
