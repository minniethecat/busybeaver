# Brittany

## Overview

Brittany, the northwestern peninsula of France, enters Tyson's essay through its extraordinary concentration of megalithic monuments. Windle discusses the Carnac alignments and the dolmen of Locmariaquer (Manné-er-Hrock) as key exhibits in MacRitchie's argument that the megalith builders of Atlantic Europe were a dwarf race remembered in folklore as fairies.

## In Ancient Geography

Brittany was known to the Romans as Armorica, part of the Celtic fringe of Gaul. Like Scotland and Ireland, it was never associated with pygmies by classical authors. The Breton evidence is entirely archaeological and folkloric, introduced into the pygmy debate by nineteenth-century scholars who saw in its megaliths the handiwork of a vanished dwarf population.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

Several specific Breton sites and traditions are cited by Windle:

The Carnac alignments — thousands of standing stones arranged in parallel rows — are among the most famous megalithic monuments in Europe. MacRitchie interpreted these as the constructions of a dwarf race, and Windle notes that "in Brittany they are associated with" (006-151) fairy builders in local tradition.

The dolmen of Manné-er-Hrock at Locmariaquer provides a particularly vivid folkloric account. Windle records that in Brittany, "a number of little men, not more than a foot high" (006-133) were believed to inhabit the dolmen, dancing beneath it at the lake below. This tradition of diminutive megalith-builders exactly parallels the Scottish and Irish fairy beliefs.

Dwarf-built churches feature in Breton tradition as well. Windle mentions "the churches built by dwarfs in Scotland and Brittany" (007-022), placing the Breton evidence alongside Scottish parallels as described by MacRitchie.

## Tyson's Discussion

Windle's treatment of Brittany follows the same pattern as his discussion of Scotland and Ireland: he presents MacRitchie's evidence in detail while maintaining critical distance from the theory's broader claims. The Breton megaliths are "described by Mr. MacRitchie, as also the two following instances, taken from widely distant parts of the globe" (007-023–024), with Windle drawing attention to the fact that "in Brittany, the" same pattern of fairy-mound associations appears across "Scotland, Ireland, Brittany, and India" (009-124).

This cross-cultural consistency is, for Windle, the strongest argument in MacRitchie's favor — that similar traditions about mound-dwelling fairies attach to similar megalithic structures across geographically and culturally disparate regions. He treats this as a pattern requiring explanation, even if he ultimately rejects MacRitchie's conclusion that all fairy-lore represents racial memory of a single prehistoric dwarf population.
