# America

## Overview

America serves a specific function in Tyson's argument: as a counter-example demonstrating that legends of small-statured peoples do not necessarily indicate the historical existence of a true pygmy race. Windle observes that "in the great continent of America there does not appear to have ever been" a genuine pygmy population (003-138), despite the widespread presence of "little people" traditions among indigenous American cultures.

## In Ancient Geography

The Americas were unknown to the classical authors who first placed pygmies in Africa and Asia. Their relevance to the pygmy question is purely modern, arising from nineteenth-century ethnological comparisons. Windle draws on the work of American ethnologists including James Mooney (Cherokee and Sioux mythology), Franz Boas (Uissuit monograph), and Erminnie Smith (Iroquois myths) to survey the indigenous traditions.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

Windle identifies several Native American traditions that superficially resemble Old World pygmy legends:

The Sioux have a tradition of "little people" inhabiting mounds. Windle notes that "we find it also in America. The Sioux have a superstition" (005-124) about mound-dwelling spirits, a parallel to European fairy-mound beliefs.

The Yahgans of Tierra del Fuego, while short in stature, are specifically excluded by Windle from pygmy classification: their height, though below European averages, does not reach the extreme reduction characteristic of true pygmies such as the Akka. Windle cites a "distinguished American ethnologist" (003-140) who confirmed "no evidence of any pigmy race in America" (003-142).

The "little people" of Cherokee and Iroquois mythology share features with European fairy-lore — dwelling in mounds, association with stone structures — but Windle treats these as independent mythological developments rather than evidence of an actual dwarf population.

## Tyson's Discussion

Tyson's treatment of America is principally negative: the American case demonstrates that legends of small peoples can arise independently of any biological dwarf race. Windle references Tennessee stone grave traditions and Sioux mound superstitions as examples. The argument serves to caution against over-reading European fairy traditions as historical evidence — just as American "little people" legends do not prove a prehistoric American pygmy race, neither do European fairy beliefs necessarily prove MacRitchie's mound-dwelling dwarfs.

Windle also notes that "aborigines in America, and notably, in our case, in Tasmania" (003-010) were displaced by larger populations, drawing a parallel between the fate of indigenous peoples worldwide and the possible historical displacement of prehistoric dwarf races by taller invading populations.
