# India

## Overview

India appears throughout Edward Tyson's _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699) as a key geographic zone in the ancient pygmy tradition. Alongside [[africa-aethiopia|Africa]], India was one of the two primary regions where classical authors placed pygmy populations.

Tyson noted that "in particular, India and Indo-China first belonged" to the geographic range of small-statured peoples discussed by ancient authorities. (001-121)

## Ancient Sources on Indian Pygmies

### Ctesias and the Indica

[[ctesias|Ctesias]] of Cnidus, a Greek physician at the Persian court in the 5th century BC, wrote extensively about India in his _Indica_. Though the original work is lost, fragments preserved through [[photius|Photius]] describe pygmy peoples inhabiting the Indian interior. Tyson treated Ctesias' account cautiously, noting the mixture of genuine observation and traveler's embellishment.

### Pliny's Indian Pygmies

[[pliny-the-elder|Pliny]] placed pygmies in the mountains near the Ganges: "Pygmæi of the mountains in the neighbourhood of the Ganges may apply to" a real population, Tyson noted. (004-063) Pliny's geographic specificity — naming the Ganges region — distinguished his account from the vaguer Homeric tradition and allowed Tyson to correlate ancient descriptions with known ape habitats.

### Philostratus

[[philostratus|Philostratus]], in his _Life of Apollonius of Tyana_, discussed Indian pygmies in the context of the broader marvelous ethnography of the East. His account, though late (3rd century AD), preserved details from earlier sources.

## Tyson's Geographic Analysis

Tyson's treatment of India was methodologically careful. He recognized that Indian traditions about small-statured peoples might have a different basis than African accounts.

He discussed Indian legends recorded by de Quatrefages: "In some of the early Indian legends, De Quatrefages thinks" that references to diminutive beings reflected actual knowledge of pygmy or proto-pygmy populations. (002-098)

Tyson also noted populations "in the centre of India, amongst the Vindyah Mountains" that might correspond to ancient pygmy accounts. (002-112) The geographic distribution of these groups — stretching "almost across Central India into Behar and the" Gangetic plain — suggested to Tyson that the ancient Indian pygmy tradition had an empirical basis. (002-157)

## India vs. Africa

Tyson's comparative approach treated India and [[africa-aethiopia|Africa]] as parallel cases. Both regions had ancient pygmy traditions. Both had known populations of small-statured humans. And both were habitats of great apes (gibbons and orangutans in Asia; chimpanzees and gorillas in Africa).

This parallel allowed Tyson to argue that the same mechanism — ancient observers confusing apes with small humans — operated independently in both regions, strengthening his overall thesis.
