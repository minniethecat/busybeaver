Jean Louis Armand de Quatrefages de Bréau (1810–1892) was a French anthropologist and zoologist, professor at the Muséum National d'Histoire Naturelle in Paris. His comprehensive study *Les Pygmées* (1887), translated into English as *The Pygmies*, was the most thorough scientific treatment of pygmy populations available to Tyson at the time of writing.

Tyson cites De Quatrefages extensively throughout his essay, treating him as the preeminent modern authority on the anthropology of diminutive races. The French anthropologist's work serves as a bridge between classical accounts of Pygmies and late 19th-century physical anthropology.

## De Quatrefages on Pygmy Origins

Tyson opens his essay by engaging with De Quatrefages' theory of pygmy origins. In the Introduction, he notes that "De Quatrefages after a most careful examination of the question in all its aspects, finds himself obliged to conclude" that the Pygmies seen by the Nasamonians were a real African population (005-028).

Tyson cites De Quatrefages' work on the physical anthropology of pygmy populations, particularly his analysis of stature data collected by European researchers:

> "The average stature of various groups of Negritos, as measured by different observers and summarized in De Quatrefages' work..."
> (001-136)

De Quatrefages identified consistent physical characteristics across geographically dispersed diminutive populations, which Tyson uses to argue for the historical reality of a widespread pygmy race.

## The Negrito Theory

A central contribution De Quatrefages made to the pygmy question was his identification of the Negritos — small-statured, dark-skinned peoples of Southeast Asia and the Andaman Islands — as modern representatives of the ancient pygmy type. Tyson notes that De Quatrefages considered the existence and "pigmy Negrito characteristics" of these peoples as "established" (002-045).

De Quatrefages also traced evidence of prehistoric Negrito populations through early Indian legends. Tyson records that "in some of the early Indian legends, De Quatrefages thinks that he finds traces of these prehistoric connections between the indigenous Negrito tribes and their invaders" (002-098), including the account of services rendered to Rama.

## Tyson's Acknowledgment

Tyson is explicit about his intellectual debt to De Quatrefages. In Section 4, he writes:

> "I am much aided in several instances by the labours of De Quatrefages, and as his book is easily accessible, it will be unnecessary for me to repeat the arguments in favour of his decisions which he has there given."
> (004-053)

This candid acknowledgment reveals De Quatrefages as Tyson's most important single source. Tyson defers to De Quatrefages' authority on specific interpretive questions, such as the suggestion that "the garment of long floating grasses" worn by certain pygmy peoples "may have been mistaken for hair" in classical accounts (004-081).

De Quatrefages also speculated that "an allusion to the ancestors of the Jats" could be found in Herodotus' account of Xerxes' army (004-088), connecting modern ethnographic observation to ancient textual evidence.

## Key Work Cited by Tyson

- **_Les Pygmées_ / _The Pygmies_ (1887)** — A comprehensive anthropological survey of pygmy populations worldwide, drawing on measurements, traveler accounts, and classical sources. Tyson relies on this work throughout Sections 1–5 of his essay.
