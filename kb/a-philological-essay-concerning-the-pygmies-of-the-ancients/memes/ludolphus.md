Job Ludolphus (1624–1704) was a German orientalist and philologist, best known as the founder of Ethiopian studies in Europe. His *Commentarius in Historiam Æthiopicam* (Commentary on the History of Aethiopia) is one of Tyson's most frequently cited modern authorities, appearing in four sections of the essay. Tyson treats Ludolphus with a mixture of respect and critical scrutiny — citing his learned opinion while subjecting his arguments to rigorous examination.

## Ludolphus and the Pygmy Question

Ludolphus provided Tyson with one of the most vivid non-classical accounts of diminutive peoples. Tyson notes that "Ludolphus mentions, *Nanus ne ducat Nanam, ne fortè oriatur ex iis Digitales*" — a concern that dwarfs marrying dwarfs might produce ever-smaller offspring (014-173). This observation anchors Ludolphus's treatment of Pygmies within the broader context of Aethiopian ethnography.

Tyson observes that Ludolphus made "several Conjectures about it" (017-008), referring to the Pygmy question. However, Tyson also considers Ludolphus "a little too strict" in one of his arguments (016-177), particularly regarding the idea that Pygmies were merely a natural aberration rather than a distinct race: Ludolphus described them as *Naturæ quodam errore ex aliis justæ staturæ hominibus* (016-172).

## Key Arguments Critiqued by Tyson

Tyson engages with Ludolphus's reasoning across multiple sections. In Section 16, he introduces Ludolphus as a principal modern authority — "Job Ludolphus in his Commentary on his Æthiopick History" — and then proceeds to test Ludolphus's conclusions (016-170). In Section 17, Tyson returns to Ludolphus for the core argument: *Sed ad Pygmæos revertamur* (but let us return to the Pygmies), tracking how Ludolphus constructs his case (017-012).

A notable feature of Ludolphus's account is the role of cranes in Aethiopia. Tyson records that "Ludolphus his Cranes sweep away the Pygmies in their Expedition" (017-041), and that Ludolphus shifts the geographical scene from India to Africa (017-161). This relocation of the Pygmy-crane conflict was a significant contribution to early modern debates about the legend's geographical roots.
