Sir William Robert Wills Wilde (1815–1876) was an Irish surgeon, ophthalmologist, antiquarian, and writer on Irish folklore. He was the father of the celebrated author Oscar Wilde. In addition to his distinguished medical career — he founded St. Mark's Ophthalmic Hospital in Dublin — Wilde was a passionate student of Irish archaeology, folklore, and rural superstition.

## Wilde's Testimony on Irish Fairy Belief

Tyson cites Sir William Wilde in two separate sections of the essay, both times drawing on Wilde's first-hand observations of Irish folk belief to support the connection between fairy traditions and the ancient Pygmies. In Section 5, Tyson adduces Wilde's authority as evidence for the sanctity of fairy-associated places in Ireland:

> "I shall content myself by quoting Sir William Wilde's statement, that every green 'rath' in that country is consecrated to the 'good people.'"
> (005-101)

The "rath" — a circular earthen fortification of prehistoric origin — was universally regarded by the Irish peasantry as belonging to the fairies, or "good people." Wilde's observation provides Tyson with a striking example of how deeply the belief in diminutive supernatural inhabitants was embedded in the Irish rural landscape.

## Distinction Between Fairy and Giant Remains

In Section 8, Tyson returns to Wilde's authority, this time to underscore a crucial distinction in Irish folk archaeology between sites associated with fairies and those attributed to giants:

> "In Ireland the distinction between the two classes is sharper than in other places, since, as Sir William Wilde pointed out, whilst every green rath in that island is consecrated to the fairies or 'good people,' the remains attributed to the giants are of a different character."
> (008-155)

This observation is significant for Tyson's larger argument because it establishes that the Irish peasantry drew a clear categorical distinction between two classes of supernatural beings — fairies (small, associated with raths) and giants (large, associated with megalithic structures). If the fairy folklore were merely undifferentiated superstition, such a consistent distinction would be unlikely. Tyson uses this to argue that fairy beliefs may be distinct folk memories of encounters with a small-statured people, preserved alongside — but separate from — traditions about giants.

## Role in Tyson's Argument

Wilde's testimony serves a dual purpose in Tyson's essay. First, it provides first-hand, documented evidence of Irish fairy belief from a respected nineteenth-century scholar, lending credibility to the folkloric argument. Second, Wilde's observation about the fairy-giant distinction supports Tyson's euhemerist thesis: that fairy legends are not pure fantasy but distorted memories of real diminutive peoples. Wilde, as both a medical man and an antiquarian, carries the kind of dual authority that Tyson values throughout his essay.
