# Ireland

## Overview

Ireland occupies a central position in the fairy-pygmy debate documented in Tyson's essay. Windle draws extensively on Irish archaeology, folklore, and ethnography to evaluate MacRitchie's theory that fairy traditions preserve memories of a prehistoric dwarf race. The Brugh of the Boyne (Newgrange), the Raths, and the accounts of two distinct racial classes reported by Professor Haddon all contribute to the argument.

## In Ancient Geography

To classical authors, Ireland was known as Hibernia, a remote island at the edge of the known world. No ancient source places pygmies in Ireland — rather, the Irish evidence enters the pygmy discourse through nineteenth-century comparative folklore and archaeology. Windle references the "pre-Christian cemeteries of Ireland" (005-170), noting that Newgrange is "probably the most remarkable" of these ancient burial sites.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

Ireland provides some of the strongest archaeological and folkloric evidence for MacRitchie's theory.

The Brugh of the Boyne features prominently. Windle describes "that famous fairy-hill in Ireland, the Brugh of the Boyne" (005-166), an immense passage tomb regarded in Irish tradition as the dwelling of the fairy folk or Tuatha Dé Danann. The stone basins and cruciform chambers within the mound are cited by MacRitchie as evidence that these structures were habitations rather than mere tombs.

Professor Alfred Cort Haddon's fieldwork in Innisbofin, County Galway, provides ethnographic evidence. Windle reports that "in Innisbofin, co. Galway, Professor Haddon" (007-047) identified two physically distinct racial types among the island's population — a finding that MacRitchie's supporters interpreted as evidence of a historical dwarf population's partial survival into the modern era. Windle notes: "In Ireland the distinction between the two classes" (008-154) of population is well attested.

The Raths of Ireland — circular earthen fortifications — are compared by Windle to the Picts' houses of Scotland. "Such were also the Raths of Ireland" (006-021), he writes, suggesting a common pattern of prehistoric habitation across the Celtic regions.

## Tyson's Discussion

Windles's treatment of the Irish evidence is characteristically balanced. He presents MacRitchie's interpretation of sites like the Brugh of the Boyne at length, including O'Hartagain's poem "which seems to bear in Mr. MacRitchie's favour" (005-183). Yet he remains cautious about the broader theory, noting that MacRitchie "seems to regard it as having" (005-167) a significance that may exceed what the evidence warrants.

The Irish material is particularly important because it ties together the archaeological evidence of megalithic structures with living folk traditions about fairies as "the good people" — traditions that Windle examines across "Scotland, Ireland, Brittany, and India" (009-124), finding a consistent pattern of fairy-mound associations that, in his view, demands explanation even if MacRitchie's specific theory is not fully accepted.
