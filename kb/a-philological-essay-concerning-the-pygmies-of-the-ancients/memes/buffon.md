# Comte de Buffon

Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon (1707–1788), was one of the most influential naturalists of the eighteenth century. His monumental *Histoire Naturelle*, générale et particulière (1749–1788) sought to provide a comprehensive account of the natural world. In the context of Edward Tyson's *Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients*, Buffon represents the leading proponent of the ape-theory of pygmies — the position Tyson himself adopted and which Victorian discoveries ultimately refuted.

## Buffon and the Ape-Theory of Pygmies

Tyson lists Buffon among the prominent followers of his own opinion that the pygmies of ancient tradition were not diminutive humans but apes mistaken for men by unreliable travellers. In the introduction to his essay, Tyson identifies the interpretive tradition:

> Nor was he without followers in his opinion; amongst whom here need only be mentioned Buffon, who in his *Histoire des Oiseaux* explains the Homeric tale much as Tyson had done. (001-074–001-076)

Buffon's endorsement of the ape-theory carried considerable weight due to his stature as Europe's foremost natural historian. His *Histoire des Oiseaux* specifically addressed the Homeric account of the [[homeric-geranomachia-simile|battle between the Pygmies and the Cranes]], interpreting it as a garbled description of ape behaviour rather than a genuine ethnographic report of a diminutive human population.

## The Demolition of Buffon's Theory

The central irony of Tyson's essay is that its argument — carefully constructed over eighteen sections — was already being dismantled at the time of publication. Nineteenth-century African exploration, particularly the discovery of the [[akka|Akka]] and other Central African pygmy populations, definitively established the existence of diminutive human races. As the editors of Tyson's essay note:

> The discoveries, however, of this century have, as all know, re-established in their essential details the accounts of the older writers, and in doing so have demolished the theories of Tyson and Buffon. (001-077–001-078)

Tyson returns to this theme in his discussion of [[aristotle|Aristotle]] and [[pliny-the-elder|Pliny]], observing that "Time has brought their revenge to Aristotle and Pliny by showing that they were right, where Tyson, and even Buffon, were wrong" (005-077–005-078).

## Legacy

Buffon's role in the pygmy debate illustrates a recurring pattern in the history of science: the authoritative dismissal of traditional knowledge subsequently vindicated by new evidence. His *Histoire Naturelle* remains a landmark of Enlightenment science, but his position on pygmies stands as a cautionary example of the limits of armchair natural history when confronted with the diversity of human populations.
