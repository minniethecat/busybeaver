# Canon Greenwell

William Greenwell (1820–1918), known as **Canon Greenwell**, was a British clergyman, archaeologist, and antiquary who served as Canon of Durham Cathedral. He was one of the most accomplished field archaeologists of his generation, particularly noted for his systematic excavation of prehistoric burial mounds throughout northern England. His major work, *British Barrows* (1877), remains a foundational reference for the study of Bronze Age Britain.

## The Willey How Excavation

In Section 5 of his essay, [[edward-tyson|Edward Tyson]] cites Greenwell's investigation of a conspicuous burial mound in Yorkshire. Discussing the origin of fairy lore and its connection to prehistoric sepulchral monuments, Tyson notes:

> This is the case in the instance of Willey How, which, when explored by Canon Greenwell, was found, in spite of its size and the enormous care evidently bestowed upon its construction, to be merely a cenotaph. A grave there was, sunk more than nine feet below the surface. (005-155–005-157)

The discovery that this massive, carefully constructed mound was a cenotaph — an empty memorial rather than a tomb — was significant for Tyson's argument. It showed how such imposing prehistoric structures could fuel folk beliefs about the dwellings of supernatural beings, including the "little people" of fairy tradition.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tyson deploys Greenwell's excavation results to support his thesis that many legends of diminutive subterranean races — commonly identified by antiquarian writers with the [[pygmies|Pygmies of the Ancients]] — originated from misinterpretations of prehistoric burial mounds. Willey How, despite the "enormous care" of its builders, contained no human remains, suggesting to the surrounding population that it served as the dwelling of supernatural beings rather than as a grave. This anthropological reading of folklore anticipated later twentieth-century scholarship on the same theme.
