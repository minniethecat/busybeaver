## Overview

Ainu Pygmy Lore refers to the Koro-puk-guru tradition preserved by the Ainu people of northern Japan — a legendary race of diminutive pit-dwellers who are said to have inhabited Yezo (modern Hokkaido) and the Kurile Islands before the arrival of the Ainu themselves. The tradition is of particular significance to Tyson's argument because it represents one of the clearest cases in which a living people (the Ainu) preserve detailed oral traditions about an earlier pygmy population whose former existence is corroborated by archaeological evidence: pit-dwellings, stone arrow-heads, pottery, and other implements. The Koro-puk-guru tradition thus serves as a powerful analogue to the European fairy-pygmy theory, suggesting that folklore can indeed preserve historical memories of extinct diminutive populations.

## Ancient and Ethnographic Sources

The Koro-puk-guru tradition has no direct counterpart in classical literature — neither Greek nor Roman authors had any knowledge of the Japanese archipelago. However, the tradition enters the Western scholarly record through the Chinese geographical tradition. The Shan-hai-king (the canonical "Book of Hills and Seas") refers to the country of the Koro-puk-guru as the "Siao-jin-Kouo, or land of little men," distinguishing it carefully from the "Peh-min-Kouo, or land of white people," which Professor Gustav Schlegel identifies with the Ainu themselves (002-075). This Chinese distinction between "little men" and "white people" corresponds precisely to the Ainu oral tradition, in which the Koro-puk-guru are remembered as a distinct, pre-Ainu population.

Tyson's primary ethnographic source for the Koro-puk-guru is the work of A.H. Savage Landor, whose "Alone with the Hairy Ainu" provided detailed descriptions of the pit-dwellings, stone implements, and oral traditions of Yezo. Additional etymological and folkloric details are drawn from John Milne (who interpreted "Koro-puk-guru" as "men occupying excavations") and Basil Hall Chamberlain (who translated it as "dwellers under burdocks"). The Chinese annals of the Bamboo Books, as analyzed by Professor Lacouperie, further corroborate the existence of Negrito populations in East Asia, recording that "in the twenty-ninth year of the Emperor Yao, in spring, the chief of the Tsiao-Yao, or dark pigmies, came to court and offered as tribute feathers from the Mot" — a date corresponding to approximately 2048 B.C. (002-088).

## Tyson's Analysis

Tyson places the Koro-puk-guru tradition within the broader context of his worldwide survey of pygmy populations, treating it as the easternmost extension of the Negrito type. The passage on Japan and the Ainu appears at a crucial juncture in Section 2, immediately after Tyson's discussion of Chinese evidence for ancient Negrito populations and immediately before his transition to the Indian subcontinent — the hypothesized center of De Quatrefages's Negrito radiation.

Tyson's treatment is notable for its careful layering of evidence types. He distinguishes four distinct categories of evidence for the Koro-puk-guru: (1) the Ainu oral tradition itself, with its legend of dwarfs hiding under burdock leaves and their poignant dying cry — "Why were we made so small?" (002-081); (2) the archaeological remains — "pit-dwellings, stone arrow-heads, pottery, and other implements" — that give the tradition empirical grounding (002-075); (3) the Chinese textual tradition, which independently records a "land of little men" in the region; and (4) the linguistic evidence of the name itself, with its competing etymologies (pit-dwellers vs. burdock-dwellers) both pointing to a diminutive, subterranean population.

The chronological layering that Tyson documents is equally significant: the Negrito Koro-puk-guru occupied the land first; then came the Ainu, "a white, hairy under-sized race, possibly, even probably, emigrants from Europe" (002-075); then the Japanese (Niphonians) who displaced the Ainu in turn. This stratigraphy of peoples — each layer preserving traditions about its predecessors — provides a model for how historical memories of pygmy populations could be transmitted across millennia. The parallel with MacRitchie's European theory is unmistakable: just as the Ainu remember the Koro-puk-guru, so might the Celts and Teutons remember the Picts and other aboriginal mound-dwellers as fairies and elves.

Tyson also returns to the Koro-puk-guru briefly in Section 4, referencing Pomponius Mela and the ancient geographical tradition of pygmies in the far east (004-144). This recurrence in a later section, in a different argumentative context, suggests that Tyson regarded the Ainu tradition as one of his strongest pieces of evidence for the worldwide distribution of pygmy populations and for the reliability of folkloric memory as a source of ethnological data.

## Modern Perspective

Modern scholarship has generally confirmed the broad outlines of the Koro-puk-guru tradition while refining the details. Archaeological excavations in Hokkaido have documented a pre-Ainu culture, now known as the Jomon or post-Jomon period, characterized by pit-dwellings and stone implements of the type described by Savage Landor. However, the identification of this archaeological culture with an actual Negrito pygmy population has not been supported by skeletal evidence; the pre-Ainu inhabitants of Hokkaido appear to have been of normal stature. The Ainu oral tradition of Koro-puk-guru may thus represent a folkloric memory of cultural difference — the pit-dwelling lifestyle of the earlier inhabitants — rather than of physical difference in stature. Alternatively, some scholars have suggested that the tradition may refer to a small number of Negrito individuals who reached Japan through the island chains to the south. The question remains open, and the Koro-puk-guru tradition continues to be cited in comparative studies of pygmy folklore worldwide.
