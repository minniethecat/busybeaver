## Overview

The Ganges River, one of the great rivers of the Indian subcontinent, serves as a recurring geographic reference point in Tyson's discussion of Indian pygmy populations. Ancient authors placed pygmy tribes in the mountains near the headwaters of the Ganges, and modern ethnography finds diminutive populations — the Santals and other groups — in the river's basin. (002-154) The Ganges functions in the essay as both a specific geographic marker and a synecdoche for the Indian subcontinent as a whole.

## In Ancient Geography

Classical geographers knew the Ganges as the great river of eastern India, marking the boundary of the known world. Pliny and other Roman authors located various marvelous peoples — including pygmies — in the mountainous regions at its source. "Pliny at another time saith they are at the Head of the Ganges" (015-057) preserves the ancient association of pygmy tribes with the river's upper reaches. This geographic tradition was inherited from earlier Greek sources, including Ctesias and Megasthenes, who reported on the peoples of India following Alexander's campaigns.

The Ganges also served a schematizing function in ancient geography: lands beyond it were the realm of the fabulous, while the river itself marked a transitional zone between the known and the marvelous. Pygmies located "at the Head of the Ganges" occupied this liminal space — far enough to be exotic, but attested by authorities deemed credible.

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

The Ganges is linked to the pygmy legend through both ancient and modern evidence. In classical sources, the "Pygmaei of the mountains in the neighbourhood of the Ganges" (004-063) represent one of the primary Indian pygmy populations. Ctesias reportedly described these mountain pygmies as a distinct race dwelling in the highlands above the river.

Modern ethnography reinforces the ancient association. "In the east the Santals inhabit the basin of the Ganges" (002-154), and these tribal populations — while not pygmies in the strict sense — represent the kind of non-Aryan aboriginal peoples whose existence lent credibility to ancient reports of diminutive races in India. The Dravidian and pre-Dravidian populations of the Ganges basin provided nineteenth-century anthropologists with living comparisons for the pygmy accounts in classical texts.

The river also features as a waypoint in discussions of pygmy migrations and distributions. "A civil sort of people, that dwell about the Head of the Ganges" (014-059) suggests settled communities rather than the wild, troglodytic existence sometimes attributed to pygmy groups — an important distinction in Tyson's effort to separate credible reports from fabulous embellishments.

## Tyson's Discussion

Tyson treats the Ganges references with his characteristic philological care, distinguishing between the various classical sources that mention pygmies in connection with the river. He notes that Pliny's account shifts between locating pygmies at the Ganges headwaters and elsewhere, reflecting the composite nature of ancient geographic knowledge. (015-057) The essay weighs these classical testimonies against modern ethnographic reports from the Ganges basin, applying the same critical method to both ancient texts and contemporary travel accounts.
