Jacob Grimm (1785–1863), the elder of the Brothers Grimm, was a German philologist, jurist, and mythologist. While best known today for the fairy tale collection he compiled with his brother Wilhelm, his scholarly masterpiece was *Deutsche Mythologie* (Teutonic Mythology, 1835), a comprehensive study of Germanic pagan beliefs and folklore.

In Tyson's essay, Grimm appears exclusively as a source relayed through Thomas [[keightley]]'s *Fairy Mythology*. Nevertheless, Grimm's authority as a philologist and mythologist lent weight to the traditions Keightley compiled.

## Grimm in the Pygmy Debate

Tyson never cites Grimm directly; every reference to Grimm in the essay is mediated through Keightley, typically in footnotes with the formula "Grimm apud Keightley" (Grimm via Keightley). This citation pattern appears four times across Sections 6 through 9.

Grimm's *Teutonic Mythology* provided Keightley — and through him, Tyson — with a vast repository of Germanic and Scandinavian traditions about dwarfs, elves, and underground-dwelling little people. These traditions form the European core of Tyson's comparative argument.

## Specific Citations

Tyson draws on Grimm's authority for several specific folkloric motifs:

- **Foundation sacrifices**: Grimm documented the belief that buildings required a living being — often a dwarf or child — buried in the foundations, a practice Tyson connects to pygmy-like subterranean beings (008-032).
- **Underground dwellings**: Grimm's collection included widespread Germanic beliefs about dwarfs who lived inside hills and rocks, emerging only at night (009-021).
- **Dwarf-fairy continuity**: Grimm traced the conceptual link between the dwarfs of Norse mythology and the fairies of later European folklore, a connection central to Tyson's argument that fairy lore preserves memories of ancient pygmy races (006-149, 007-145).

## Significance

Grimm's importance to Tyson's essay is methodological as much as evidentiary. As the founder of scientific philology and comparative mythology, Grimm represented the scholarly tradition Tyson sought to emulate. By invoking Grimm — even at second hand through Keightley — Tyson positioned his own investigation of pygmy lore within the respected framework of 19th-century comparative folklore studies.

## Key Work Cited by Tyson

- **_Deutsche Mythologie_ / _Teutonic Mythology_ (1835)** — Cited via Keightley at specific pages (130, 230, 261, 441, 488). Tyson's reliance on Keightley's mediation rather than direct citation of Grimm suggests he worked primarily from the English folklorist's compilation.
