# Giants

## Overview

Giants occupy a significant place in Edward Tyson's _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699) as the natural contrasting category to the diminutive Pygmies and dwarfs. Tyson examines giant legends not merely as curiosities but as potential evidence for how ancient and medieval peoples interpreted anomalous skeletal remains. (008-152)

The association of giants and dwarfs in certain places forms a recurring pattern in ancient ethnography that Tyson treats as analytically significant. (008-152)

## Classical and Medieval Sources

Ancient and medieval sources abound with giant lore. Certain peoples called "Hantous" were described as giants, and their existence was taken seriously by ethnographers. (006-087) Kozma enumerates the names of giants and the traditions associated with them, providing a catalog of giant legends across cultures. (006-109)

The nightly dances of giants around their territory feature in several accounts, suggesting ritual or ceremonial practices attributed to these legendary beings. (006-163)

## Giants, Dwarfs, and Fossil Remains

Tyson identifies a crucial pattern: "the remains attributed to the giants are of a different character" than those attributed to dwarfs. (008-157) This suggests he was aware of what we would now call taphonomy—the processes by which remains are preserved and misinterpreted.

The association of "fairies" with giants appears in multiple traditions, indicating a common folkloric framework for explaining unusual landscape features and archaeological remains. (008-159) Tyson notes that caves and ancient structures were "sometimes been made by giants and afterwards inhabited by" dwarfs or other peoples, reflecting the layered nature of folk explanations. (009-006)

Some locations were described as "at the same time inhabited by giants, dwarfs, and others," suggesting complex traditions in which different-sized peoples coexisted in the same legendary geography. (009-008)

## Tyson's Analytical Approach

Tyson's treatment of giants demonstrates his methodological commitment to comparative analysis. By examining giant legends alongside pygmy and dwarf accounts, he sought patterns that might reveal a common natural-historical basis for all three categories. His approach anticipates modern anthropological interest in how human societies explain anomalous fossils and archaeological remains.
