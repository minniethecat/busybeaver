Aulus Gellius (c. 125–180 AD) was a Roman grammarian, antiquarian, and writer whose sole surviving work, *Noctes Atticae* (Attic Nights), is a miscellany of notes on grammar, philosophy, history, law, and antiquities compiled during his student years in Athens. The work preserves numerous fragments of lost authors and has long been valued as a source for ancient scholarship.

In Tyson's essay, Gellius is cited in three distinct contexts: as an authority on the credibility of Ctesias, as one of the ancient authors who wrote about the Pygmies, and as part of the broader tradition of pygmy testimony.

## Gellius on Ctesias and Ancient Historians

Tyson invokes Aulus Gellius in Section 12 when discussing the credibility of Ctesias, the Greek historian who provided one of the earliest and most detailed accounts of the Pygmies in India. Gellius, who encountered various Greek historical works during his travels, expressed skepticism about some of the more marvelous tales in ancient historiography:

> "And the same opinion _A. Gellius_ seems to have of him, as he had likewise of several other old _Greek Historians_ which happened to fall into his hands at _Brundusium_, in his return from _Greece_ into _Italy_."
> (012-031)

This passage situates Gellius within the skeptical tradition regarding Ctesias's reliability. Gellius acquired manuscripts of Greek historians at Brundusium (modern Brindisi) while returning from Greece to Italy, and his critical judgment of them parallels other ancient authorities who questioned Ctesias's veracity (012-033).

Tyson uses this reference to acknowledge the doubts surrounding Ctesias while maintaining that the existence of Pygmies does not depend solely on Ctesias's testimony.

## Gellius in the Catalogue of Pygmy Authorities

In Section 14, Tyson enumerates the many authorities who have written about the Pygmies, listing Gellius among a distinguished company of ancient and Renaissance scholars:

> "..._Cassanius, Joh. Talentonius, Gellius, Pomp. Mela_, and _Olaus Magnus_. I have taken notice of most of them already."
> (014-150)

This catalogue places Gellius alongside Pomponius Mela (the Roman geographer), Olaus Magnus (the Swedish historian), and other notable writers, underscoring the breadth of the pygmy tradition across centuries.

## Gellius on Pygmy Stature

In Section 15, Tyson references Gellius among the authorities who commented on the small stature of the Pygmies:

> "...their _Magnitude_: which he mentions from _Ctesias, Pliny, Gellius_, and _Juvenal_; and tho' they do not all agree exactly, 'tis nothing."
> (015-051)

Here Gellius appears as part of the converging testimony — alongside Pliny the Elder and Juvenal — regarding the diminutive size of the Pygmies. Tyson dismisses minor discrepancies among these sources, treating their general agreement as sufficient evidence.
