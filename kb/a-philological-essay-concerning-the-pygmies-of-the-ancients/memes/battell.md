## Biography

Andrew Battell (c. 1565–c. 1614) was an English traveler who spent nearly two
decades in Central Africa after being captured by the Portuguese and sent to
Angola. His observations of the peoples and regions of West-Central Africa,
recorded after his return to England, were published posthumously in Samuel
Purchas's influential travel collection.

Battell's account is notable as one of the earliest first-hand European
descriptions of the interior of the Loango region (in present-day Republic
of Congo). His reports on the physical characteristics of the tribes he
encountered provided early modern evidence of African peoples of unusually
small stature, long before the systematic anthropological studies of the
nineteenth century.

## Relevance to the Pygmy Question

Tyson cites Battell's account as part of his survey of historical and modern
testimonies concerning pygmy-sized peoples in Africa. Writing of the Loango
region, Tyson records Battell's description of a tribe called the Babonko
(003-088). Battell's original account, as reproduced in "Purchas his Pilgrimes"
(1625), describes them in the following terms:

> "To the north-east of Mani-Kesock are a kind of little people called Matimbas;
> which are no bigger than boyes of twelve yeares old, but very thicke, and live
> only upon flesh, which they kill in the woods with their bows and darts. They
> pay tribute to Mani-Kesock, and bring all their elephants' teeth and tayles to
> him."
> (003-090–003-094)

Battell's description is significant for several reasons. His comparison of
the Matimbas to "boyes of twelve yeares old" is a concrete size reference,
and his details about their hunting practices, tribute system, and social
isolation provide a relatively detailed early modern ethnographic sketch
(003-088–003-095). Tyson places this account alongside other reports from
the Loango and Congo regions, weaving Battell's seventeenth-century testimony
into the larger fabric of evidence for the historical existence of pygmy
peoples in Africa.
