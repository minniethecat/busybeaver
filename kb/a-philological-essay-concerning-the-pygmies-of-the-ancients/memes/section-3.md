# Section 3

[003-001]An examination of the present localisation of these remnants of the

[003-002]Negrito inhabitants shows how they have been split up, amalgamated with,

[003-003]or driven to the islands by the conquering invaders. An example of what

[003-004]has taken place may be found in the case of Borneo, where Negritos still

[003-005]exist in the centre of the island. The Dyaks chase them like wild beasts,

[003-006]and shoot down the children, who take refuge in the trees. This will not

[003-007]seem in the least surprising to those who have studied the history of the

[003-008]relation between autochthonous races and their invaders. It is the same

[003-009]story that has been told of the Anglo-Saxon race in its dealings with

[003-010]aborigines in America, and notably, in our case, in Tasmania.

[003-011]Turning from [[asia|Asia]] to a continent more closely associated, at least in

[003-012]popular estimation, with pigmy races, we find in Africa several races of

[003-013]dwarf men, of great antiquity and surpassing interest. The discoveries of

[003-014]Stanley, Schweinfurth, Miani, and others have now placed at our disposal

[003-015]very complete information respecting the pigmies of the central part of

[003-016]the continent, with whom it will, therefore, be convenient to make a

[003-017]commencement. These pigmies appear to be divided into two tribes, which,

[003-018]though similar in stature, and alike distinguished by the characteristic

[003-019]of attaching themselves to some larger race of natives, yet present

[003-020]considerable points of difference, so much so as to cause Mr. Stanley to

[003-021]say that they are as unlike as a Scandinavian is to a Turk. "Scattered,"

[003-022]says the same authority,[A] "among the Balessé, between Ipoto and Mount

[003-023]Pisgah, and inhabiting the land between the Ngaiyu and Ituri rivers, a

[003-024]region equal in area to about two-thirds of Scotland, are the Wambutti,

[003-025]variously called Batwa, Akka, and Bazungu. These people are under-sized

[003-026]nomads, dwarfs or pigmies, who live in the uncleared virgin forest, and

[003-027]support themselves on game, which they are very expert in catching. They

[003-028]vary in height from three feet to four feet six inches. A full-grown adult

[003-029]may weigh ninety pounds. They plant their village camps three miles around

[003-030]a tribe of agricultural aborigines, the majority of whom are fine stalwart

[003-031]people. They use poisoned arrows, with which they kill elephants, and they

[003-032]capture other kinds of game by the use of traps."

 
[Footnote A: In Darkest Africa, vol. ii. p. 92.]

[003-033]The two groups are respectively called Batwa and Wambutti. The former

[003-034]inhabit the northern parts of the above-mentioned district, the latter the

[003-035]southern. The former have longish heads, long narrow faces, and small

[003-036]reddish eyes set close together, whilst the latter have round faces and

[003-037]open foreheads, gazelle-like eyes, set far apart, and rich yellow ivory

[003-038]complexion. Their bodies are covered with stiffish grey short hair. Two

[003-039]further quotations from the same source may be given to convey an idea to

[003-040]those ignorant of the original work, if such there be, of the appearances

[003-041]of these dwarfs. Speaking of the queen of a tribe of pigmies, Stanley

[003-042]says,[A] "She was brought in to see me, with three rings of polished iron

[003-043]around her neck, the ends of which were coiled like a watch-spring. Three

[003-044]iron rings were suspended to each ear. She is of a light-brown complexion

[003-045]with broad round face, large eyes, and small but full lips. She had a

[003-046]quiet modest demeanour, though her dress was but a narrow fork clout of

[003-047]bark cloth. Her height is about four feet four inches, and her age may be

[003-048]nineteen or twenty. I notice when her arms are held against the light a

[003-049]whity-brown fell on them. Her skin has not that silky smoothness of touch

[003-050]common to the Zanzibaris, but altogether she is a very pleasing little

[003-051]creature." To this female portrait may be subjoined one of a male aged

[003-052]probably twenty-one years and four feet in height.[B] "His colour was

[003-053]coppery, the fell over the body was almost furry, being nearly half an

[003-054]inch long, and his hands were very delicate. On his head he wore a bonnet

[003-055]of a priestly form, decorated with a bunch of parrot feathers, and a broad

[003-056]strip of bark covered his nakedness."

 
[Footnote A: In Darkest Africa, vol. i. p. 345.]

 
[Footnote B: Ibid., ii. 40.]

[003-057]Jephson states[A] that he found continual traces of them from 270 30' E.

[003-058]long., a few miles above the Equator, up to the edge of the great forest,

[003-059]five days' march from Lake Albert. He also says that they are a hardy

[003-060]daring race, always ready for war, and are much feared by their

[003-061]neighbours. As soon as a party of dwarfs makes its appearance near a

[003-062]village, the chief hastens to propitiate them by presents of corn and such

[003-063]vegetables as he possesses. They never exceed four feet one inch in

[003-064]height, he informs us, and adds a characteristic which has not been

[003-065]mentioned by Stanley, one, too, which is very remarkable when it is

[003-066]remembered how scanty is the facial hair of the Negros and Negritos--the

[003-067]men have often very long beards. The southern parts of the continent are

[003-068]occupied by the Bushmen, who are vigorous and agile, of a stature ranging

[003-069]from four feet six inches to four feet nine inches, and sufficiently well

[003-070]known to permit me to pass over them without further description. The

[003-071]smallest woman of this race who has been measured was only three feet

[003-072]three inches in height, and Barrow examined one, who was the mother of

[003-073]several children, with a stature of three feet eight inches. The Akoas of

[003-074]the Gaboon district were a race of pigmies who, now apparently extinct,

[003-075]formerly dwelt on the north of the Nazareth River. A male of this tribe

[003-076]was photographed and measured by the French Admiral Fleuriot de l'Angle.

[003-077]His age was about forty and his stature four feet six inches.

 
[Footnote A: Emm Pasha, p. 367, et seq.]

[003-078]Flower[A] says that "another tribe, the M'Boulous, inhabiting the coast

[003-079]north of the Gaboon River, have been described by M. Marche as probably

[003-080]the primitive race of the country. They live in little villages, keeping

[003-081]entirely to themselves, though surrounded by the larger Negro tribes,

[003-082]M'Pongos and Bakalais, who are encroaching upon them so closely that their

[003-083]numbers are rapidly diminishing. In 1860 they were not more than 3000; in

[003-084]1879 they were much less numerous. They are of an earthy-brown colour, and

[003-085]rarely exceed five feet three inches in height. Another group living

[003-086]between the Gaboon and the Congo, in Ashangoland, a male of which measured

[003-087]four feet six inches, has been described by Du Chaillu."

[003-088]In Loango there is a tribe called Babonko, which was described by Battell

[003-089]in 1625, in the work entitled "Purchas his Pilgrimes," in the following

[003-090]terms:--"To the north-east of Mani-Kesock are a kind of little people

[003-091]called Matimbas; which are no bigger than boyes of twelve yeares old, but

[003-092]very thicke, and live only upon flesh, which they kill in the woods with

[003-093]their bows and darts. They pay tribute to Mani-Kesock, and bring all their

[003-094]elephants' teeth and tayles to him. They will not enter into any of the

[003-095]Maramba's houses, nor will suffer any one to come where they dwell. And if

[003-096]by chance any Maramba or people of Longo pass where they dwell, they will

[003-097]forsake that place and go to another. The women carry bows and arrows as

[003-098]well as the men. And one of these will walk in the woods alone and kill

[003-099]the Pongos with their poysoned arrows." It is somewhat surprising that

[003-100][[edward-tyson|Tyson]], who gives in his essay (p. 80) the account of the same people

[003-101]published at a later date (1686) by Dapper, should have missed his

[003-102]fellow-countryman's narrative. The existence of this tribe has been

[003-103]established by a German expedition, one of the members of which, Dr.

[003-104]Falkenstein, photographed and measured an adult male whose stature was

[003-105]four feet six inches.

[003-106]Krapf[A] states that in the south of Schoa, in a part of Abyssinia as yet

[003-107]unworked, the Dokos live, who are not taller than four feet. According to

[003-108]his account, they are of a dark olive colour, with thick prominent lips,

[003-109]flat noses, small eyes, and long flowing hair. They have no dwellings,

[003-110]temples, holy trees, chiefs, or weapons, live on roots and fruit, and are

[003-111]ignorant of fire. Another group was described by Mollieu in 1818 as

[003-112]inhabiting Tenda-Maié, near the Rio Grande, but very little is known about

[003-113]them. In a work entitled "The [[dwarfs|Dwarfs]] of Mount Atlas," Halliburton[B] has

[003-114]brought forward a number of statements to prove that a tribe of dwarfs,

[003-115]named like those of Central Africa, Akkas, of a reddish complexion and

[003-116]with short woolly hair, live in the district adjoining Soos. These dwarfs

[003-117]have been alluded to by Harris and Dönnenburg,[C] but Mr. Harold Crichton

[003-118]Browne,[D] who has explored neighbouring districts, is of opinion that

[003-119]there is no such tribe, and that the accounts of them have been based upon

[003-120]the examination of sporadic examples of dwarfishness met with in that as

[003-121]in other parts of the world.

 
[Footnote A: _Morgenblatt_, 1853 (quoted by Schaafhausen, _Arch. f.

[003-122]Anth._, 1866, p. 166).]

 
[Footnote B: London, Nutt, 1891.]

 
[Footnote C: _Nature_, 1892, ii. 616.]

 
[Footnote A: _Nature_, 1892, i. 269.]

[003-123]Finally, in Madagascar it is possible that there may be a dwarf race.

[003-124]Oliver[A] states that "the Vazimbas are supposed to have been the first

[003-125]occupants of Ankova. They are described by Rochon, under the name of

[003-126]Kunios, as a nation of dwarfs averaging three feet six inches in stature,

[003-127]of a lighter colour than the Negroes, with very long arms and woolly hair.

[003-128]As they were only described by natives of the coast, and have never been

[003-129]seen, it is natural to suppose that these peculiarities have been

[003-130]exaggerated; but it is stated that people of diminutive size still exist

[003-131]on the banks of a certain river to the south-west." There are many tumuli

[003-132]of rude work and made of rough stones throughout the country, which are

[003-133]supposed to be their tombs. In idolatrous days, says Mullens,[B] the

[003-134]Malagasy deified the Vazimba, and their so-called tombs were the most

[003-135]sacred objects in the country. In this account may be found further

[003-136]evidence in favour of Mr. Gomme's theory, to which attention has already

[003-137]been called.

 
[Footnote A: _Anthrop. Memoirs_, iii. 1.]

 
[Footnote B: _Jour. Anthrop. Inst._, v. 181.]

[003-138]In the great continent of America there does not appear to have ever been,

[003-139]so far as our present knowledge teaches, any pigmy race. Dr. Brinton, the

[003-140]distinguished American ethnologist, to whom I applied for information on

[003-141]this point, has been good enough to write to me that, in his opinion,

[003-142]there is no evidence of any pigmy race in America. The "little people" of

[003-143]the "stone graves" in Tennessee, often supposed to be such, were children,

[003-144]as the bones testify. The German explorer Hassler has alleged the

[003-145]existence of a pigmy race in Brazil, but testimony is wanting to support

[003-146]such allegation. There are two tribes of very short but not pigmy stature

[003-147]in America, the Yahgans of Tierra del Fuégo and the Utes of Colorado, but

[003-148]both of these average over five feet.

[003-149]Leaving aside for the moment the Lapps, to whom I shall return, there does

[003-150]not appear to have been at any time a really pigmy race in [[europe|Europe]], so far

[003-151]as any discoveries which have been made up to the present time show.

[003-152]Professor Topinard, whose authority upon this point cannot be gainsaid,

[003-153]informs me that the smallest race known to him in Central Europe is that

[003-154]of the pre-historic people of the Lozère, who were Neolithic troglodytes,

[003-155]and are represented probably at the present day by some of the peoples of

[003-156]South Italy and Sardinia. Their average stature was about five feet two

[003-157]inches. This closely corresponds with what is known of the stature of the

[003-158]Platycnemic race of Denbighshire, the Perthi-Chwareu. Busk[A] says of them

[003-159]that they were of low stature, the mean height, deduced from the lengths

[003-160]of the long bones, being little more than five feet. As both sexes are

[003-161]considered together in this description, it is fair to give the male a

[003-162]stature of about five feet two inches,[B] It also corresponds with the

[003-163]stature assigned by Pitt-Rivers to a tribe occupying the borders of

[003-164]Wiltshire and Dorsetshire during the Roman occupation, the average height

[003-165]of whose males and females was five feet two and a half inches and four

[003-166]feet ten and three-quarter inches respectively.
