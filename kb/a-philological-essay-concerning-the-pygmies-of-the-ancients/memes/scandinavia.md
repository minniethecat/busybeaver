## Overview

Scandinavia, the northern European region encompassing modern Norway, Sweden, Denmark, and Finland, appears throughout Tyson's essay as an important comparative reference point. It is invoked not as the homeland of pygmies in the classical sense, but as a modern analog: the Lapps (Sámi) of northern Scandinavia provide Tyson with a living example of a diminutive population whose stature and dwelling habits parallel the ancient pygmy accounts. (004-023) Scandinavia also features prominently in the fairy-mound traditions that MacRitchie marshals in support of his pygmy-fairy theory.

## In Ancient Geography

Ancient authors did not write extensively about Scandinavia under that name. The region lay beyond the Roman frontier, known only through fragmentary reports. Tyson's essay, however, draws upon modern ethnographic and folkloric sources rather than classical geographers for its Scandinavian references. The Scandinavian data enters the essay through nineteenth-century anthropology: measurements of Lapp stature, accounts of Scandinavian mound-stories, and descriptions of hill-dwelling peoples. (003-021) The Eddas and Norse mythology provide a textual layer that Tyson's contemporaries connected to pre-historic racial memories. (007-044)

## Connection to the Pygmy Legend

Scandinavia is significant to the pygmy legend through three intersecting lines of evidence. First, the Lapps themselves — described as "though no doubt smaller than the Scandinavian peoples" (004-041) — serve as a modern stunted population against which pygmy stature claims can be calibrated. Karonzine's measurements place the average Lapp male at a height that, while not pygmy-small, represents a notable reduction from the European mean. (004-024)

Second, Scandinavian folklore preserves stories of mound-dwelling, cave-inhabiting small peoples who closely resemble the fairy races of Celtic tradition. "Scandinavian mound-stories we find a curious incident" (006-027) describes underground dwellers who emerge only at night — a pattern MacRitchie explicitly links to aboriginal pygmy populations driven underground by invaders. The hill people "live in caves or small hills" (006-046), and the elves or fairies of Norse tradition occupy the same conceptual space as the "good people" of Irish and Scottish lore.

Third, the Scandinavian Necks — water-dwarfs — parallel the German Water-Dwarfs and the Celtic water-spirits, creating a pan-European distribution of small supernatural beings that MacRitchie interprets as folk-memories of a pre-historic diminutive race. (007-151)

## Tyson's Discussion

Tyson does not engage with Scandinavia as a primary geographic locus of the pygmy question in the same way he treats India or Africa. Rather, Scandinavian evidence appears in the essay through the editorial apparatus provided by Windle and the comparative anthropology of the late nineteenth century. The observation that "Scandinavian countries, and the traditions" (008-031) preserve pygmy-like folk memories supports the broader argument that the pygmy legend has a basis in observed reality rather than pure invention.

The case of the Lapps is particularly instructive because it demonstrates Tyson's methodological caution: "leaving aside for the moment the Lapps, to whom I shall return" (003-149) shows the author setting aside a complex case for separate treatment, unwilling to force it into a premature conclusion. When he does return to it, the Lapp evidence is weighed carefully against Eskimo and other Arctic populations. (004-031)
