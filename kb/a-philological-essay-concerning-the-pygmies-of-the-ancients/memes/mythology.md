# Mythology and Truth in Ancient Accounts

## Overview

Edward Tyson's _A Philological Essay Concerning the Pygmies of the Ancients_ (1699) engages with a fundamental methodological problem: how to extract historical truth from mythological accounts. Tyson's approach—neither credulously accepting nor dismissively rejecting ancient stories—represents a sophisticated early modern hermeneutics of classical mythology.

## Tyson's Hermeneutic Method

Tyson recognized that ancient accounts of Pygmies contained a mixture of observation, exaggeration, and literary invention. His method involved:
1. Collecting all classical references to Pygmies
2. Comparing accounts across authors for consistency
3. Identifying natural-historical explanations for seemingly mythical elements
4. Using comparative anatomy to test ancient descriptions against reality

This four-step method allowed Tyson to argue that the pygmy legend was not pure invention but had a basis in real encounters with apes.

## The Role of Homer

Homer's account of the geranomachia in the _Iliad_ was the most influential source for the pygmy legend. Tyson treats Homer's account not as pure fiction but as a poetic elaboration of ethnographic reports that reached the Greeks from Africa. The crane migration—a real natural phenomenon—provides the factual kernel around which the mythical battle narrative crystallized.

## Beyond Euhemerism

Tyson's approach goes beyond simple euhemerism (the theory that myths are distorted history). While he does seek a historical core, he also accounts for the literary and rhetorical dimensions of ancient texts. His philological expertise allows him to trace how a single Homeric passage generated centuries of elaboration and cross-referencing among later authors.
