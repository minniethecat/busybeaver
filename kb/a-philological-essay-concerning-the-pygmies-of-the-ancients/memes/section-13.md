# Section 13

[013-001]Had _Albertus_ only asserted, that the _[[pygmies|Pygmies]]_ were a sort of _[[apes|Apes]]_,

[013-002]his Opinion possibly might have obtained with less difficulty, unless he

[013-003]could have produced some Body that had heard them talk. But _Ulysses

[013-004]Aldrovandus_[A] is so far from believing his _Ape Pygmies_ ever spoke,

[013-005]that he utterly denies, that there were ever any such Creatures in being,

[013-006]as the _Pygmies_, at all; or that they ever fought the _Cranes_. _Cum

[013-007]itaque Pygmæos_ (saith he) _dari negemus, Grues etiam cum iis Bellum

[013-008]gerere, ut fabulantur, negabimus, & tam pertinaciter id negabimus, ut ne

[013-009]jurantibus credemus._

 
[Footnote A: _Ulys. Aldrovandi Ornitholog._ lib. 20. p.m. 344.]

[013-010]I find a great many very Learned Men are of this Opinion: And in the first

[013-011]place, _[[strabo|Strabo]]_[A] is very positive; [Greek: Heorakos men gar oudeis

[013-012]exaegeitai ton pisteos axion andron;] i.e. _No Man worthy of belief did

[013-013]ever see them_. And upon all occasions he declares the same. So _Julius

[013-014]Cæsar Scaliger_[B] makes them to be only a Fiction of the Ancients, _At

[013-015]hæc omnia_ (saith he) _Antiquorum figmenta & meræ Nugæ, si exstarent,

[013-016]reperirentur. At cum universus Orbis nunc nobis cognitus sit, nullibi hæc

[013-017]Naturæ Excrementa reperiri certissimum est._ And _Isaac Casaubon_[C]

[013-018]ridicules such as pretend to justifie them: _Sic nostra ætate_ (saith he)

[013-019]_non desunt, qui eandem de Pygmæis lepidam fabellam renovent; ut qui etiam

[013-020]è Sacris Literis, si Deo placet, fidem illis conentur astruere. Legi etiam

[013-021]Bergei cujusdam Galli Scripta, qui se vidisse diceret. At non ego credulus

[013-022]illi, illi inquam Omnium Bipedum mendacissimo._ I shall add one Authority

[013-023]more, and that is of _Adrian Spigelius,_ who produces a Witness that had

[013-024]examined the very place, where the _Pygmies_ were said to be; yet upon a

[013-025]diligent enquiry, he could neither find them, nor hear any tidings of

[013-026]them.[D] _Spigelius_ therefore tells us, _Hoc loco de Pygmæis dicendum

[013-027]erat, qui [Greek: para pygonos] dicti à statura, quæ ulnam non excedunt.

[013-028]Verùm ego Poetarum fabulas esse crediderim, pro quibus tamen_ Aristoteles

[013-029]_minimè haberi vult, sed veram esse Historiam._ 8. Hist. Animal. 12.

[013-030]_asseverat. Ego quo minùs hoc statuam, tum Authoritate primùm Doctissimi_

[013-031]Strabonis I. Geograph. _coactus sum, tum potissimùm nunc moveor, quod

[013-032]nostro tempore, quo nulla Mundi pars est, quam Nautarum Industria non

[013-033]perlustrarit, nihil tamen, unquam simile aut visum est, aut auditum.

[013-034]Accedit quod_ Franciscus Alvarez _Lusitanus, qui ea ipsa loca peragravit,

[013-035]circa quæ Aristoteles Pygmæos esse scribit, nullibi tamen tam parvam

[013-036]Gentem à se conspectam tradidit, sed Populum esse Mediocris staturæ, &_

[013-037]Æthiopes _tradit._

 
[Footnote A: _Strabo Geograph._ lib. 17. p.m. 565.]

 
[Footnote B: _Jul. Cæs. Scaliger. Comment. in Arist. Hist. Animal._ lib.

[013-038]8. § 126. p.m. 914.]

 
[Footnote C: _Isaac Causabon Notæ & Castigat. in_ lib. 1. _Strabonis

[013-039]Geograph._ p.m. 38.]

 
[Footnote D: _Adrian. Spigelij de Corporis Humani fabrica_, lib. 1. cap.

[013-040]7. p.m. 15.]

[013-041]I think my self therefore here obliged to make out, that there were such

[013-042]Creatures as _Pygmies_, before I determine what they were, since the very

[013-043]being of them is called in question, and utterly denied by so great Men,

[013-044]and by others too that might be here produced. Now in the doing this,

[013-045]_[[aristotle|Aristotle]]_'s Assertion of them is so very positive, that I think there

[013-046]needs not a greater or better Proof; and it is so remarkable a one, that I

[013-047]find the very Enemies to this Opinion at a loss, how to shift it off. To

[013-048]lessen it's Authority they have interpolated the _Text_, by foisting into

[013-049]the _Translation_ what is not in the Original; or by not translating at

[013-050]all the most material passage, that makes against them; or by miserably

[013-051]glossing it, to make him speak what he never intended: Such unfair

[013-052]dealings plainly argue, that at any rate they are willing to get rid of a

[013-053]Proof, that otherwise they can neither deny, or answer.

[013-054]_Aristotle_'s Text is this, which I shall give with _Theodorus Gaza's_

[013-055]Translation: for discoursing of the Migration of Birds, according to the

[013-056]Season of the Year, from one Country to another, he saith:[A]

 
[Footnote A: _Aristotel. Hist. Animal._ lib. 8. cap. 12.]

[013-057][Greek: Meta men taen phthinoporinaen Isaemerian, ek tou Pontou kaiton

[013-058]psychron pheugonta ton epionta cheimona; meta de taen earinaen, ek ton

[013-059]therinon, eis tous topous tous psychrous, phoboumena ta kaumata; ta men,

[013-060]kai ek ton engus topon poioumena tas metabolas, ta de, kai ek ton eschaton

[013-061]hos eipein, hoion hai geranoi poiousi. Metaballousi gar ek ton Skythikon

[013-062]eis ta helae ta ano taes Aigyptou, othen ho Neilos rhei. Esti de ho topos

[013-063]outos peri on hoi pigmaioi katoikousin; ou gar esti touto mythos, all'

[013-064]esti kata taen alaetheian. Genos mikron men, hosper legetai, kai autoi kai

[013-065]hoi hippoi; Troglodytai d' eisi ton bion.]

[013-066]_Tam ab Autumnali Æquinoctio ex Ponto, Locisque frigidis fugiunt Hyemem

[013-067]futuram. A Verno autem ex tepida Regione ad frigidam sese conferunt, æstus

[013-068]metu futuri: & alia de locis vicinis discedunt, alia de ultimis, prope

[013-069]dixerim, ut Grues faciunt, quæ ex Scythicis Campis ad Paludes Ægypto

[013-070]superiores, unde Nilus profluit, veniunt, quo in loco pugnare cum Pygmæis

[013-071]dicuntur. Non enim id fabula est, sed certe, genus tum hominum, tum etiam

[013-072]Equorum pusillum (ut dicitur) est, deguntque in Cavernis, unde Nomen

[013-073]Troglodytæ a subeundis Cavernis accepere._

[013-074]In English 'tis thus: 'At the _Autumnal Æquinox_ they go out of _Pontus_

[013-075]and the cold Countreys to avoid the Winter that is coming on. At the

[013-076]_Vernal Æquinox_ they pass from hot Countreys into cold ones, for fear of

[013-077]the ensuing heat; some making their Migrations from nearer places; others

[013-078]from the most remote (as I may say) as the _Cranes_ do: for they come out

[013-079]of _[[scythia|Scythia]]_ to the Lakes above _Ægypt_, whence the _Nile_ do's flow. This

[013-080]is the place, whereabout the _Pygmies_ dwell: For this is no _Fable_, but

[013-081]a _Truth_. Both they and the Horses, as 'tis said, are a small kind. They

[013-082]are _[[troglodytes|Troglodytes]]_, or live in Caves.'

[013-083]We may here observe how positive the _Philosopher_ is, that there are

[013-084]_Pygmies_; he tells us where they dwell, and that 'tis no Fable, but a

[013-085]Truth. But _Theodorus Gaza_ has been unjust in translating him, by

[013-086]foisting in, _Quo in loco pugnare cum Pygmæis dicuntur_, whereas there is

[013-087]nothing in the Text that warrants it: As likewise, where he expresses the

[013-088]little Stature of the _Pygmies_ and the Horses, there _Gaza_ has rendered

[013-089]it, _Sed certè Genus tum Hominum, tum etiam Equorum pusillum_. _Aristotle_

[013-090]only saith, [Greek: Genos mikron men hosper legetai, kai autoi, kai hoi

[013-091]hippoi]. He neither makes his _Pygmies Men_, nor saith any thing of their

[013-092]fighting the _Cranes_; tho' here he had a fair occasion, discoursing of

[013-093]the Migration of the _Cranes_ out of _Scythia_ to the _Lakes_ above

[013-094]_Ægypt_, where he tells us the _Pygmies_ are. Cardan[A] therefore must

[013-095]certainly be out in his guess, that _Aristotle_ only asserted the

[013-096]_Pygmies_ out of Complement to his friend _[[homer|Homer]]_; for surely then he

[013-097]would not have forgot their fight with the _Cranes_; upon which occasion

[013-098]only _Homer_ mentions them.[B] I should rather think that _Aristotle_,

[013-099]being sensible of the many [[fable|Fables]] that had been raised on this occasion,

[013-100]studiously avoided the mentioning this fight, that he might not give

[013-101]countenance to the Extravagant Relations that had been made of it.

 
[Footnote A: _Cardan de Rerum varietate_, lib. 8. cap. 40. p.m. 153.]

 
[Footnote B: _Apparet ergo_ (saith _Cardan_) Pygmæorum Historiam esse

[013-102]fabulosam, quod &_ Strabo _sentit & nosira ætas, cum omnia nunc fermè

[013-103]orbis mirabilia innotuerint, declarat. Sed quod tantum Philosophum

[013-104]decepit, fuit Homeri Auctoritas non apud illium levis.]

[013-105]But I wonder that neither _Casaubon_ nor _Duvall_ in their Editions of

[013-106]_Aristotle_'s Works, should have taken notice of these Mistakes of _Gaza_,

[013-107]and corrected them. And _Gesner_, and _Aldrovandus_, and several other

[013-108]Learned Men, in quoting this place of _Aristotle_, do make use of this

[013-109]faulty Translation, which must necessarily lead them into Mistakes. _Sam.

[013-110]Bochartus_[A] tho' he gives _Aristotle_'s Text in Greek, and adds a new

[013-111]Translation of it, he leaves out indeed the _Cranes_ fighting with the

[013-112]_Pygmies_, yet makes them _Men_, which _Aristotle_ do's not; and by

[013-113]anti-placing, _ut aiunt_, he renders _Aristotle_'s Assertion more dubious;

[013-114]_Neque enim_ (saith he in the Translation) _id est fabula, sed reverâ, ut

[013-115]aiunt, Genus ibi parvum est tam Hominum quàm Equorum. Julius Cæsar

[013-116]Scaliger_ in translating this Text of _Aristotle_, omits both these

[013-117]Interpretations of _Gaza_; but on the other hand is no less to be blamed

[013-118]in not translating at all the most remarkable passage, and where the

[013-119]Philosopher seems to be so much in earnest; as, [Greek: ou gar esti touto

[013-120]mythos, all' esti kata taen alaetheian], this he leaves wholly out,

[013-121]without giving us his reason for it, if he had any: And Scaliger's[B]

[013-122]insinuation in his Comment, _viz. Negat esse fabulam de his (sc. Pygmeis)_

[013-123][[herodotus|Herodotus]], _at Philosophus semper moderatus & prudens etiam addidit_,

[013-124][Greek: hosper legetai], is not to be allowed. Nor can I assent to Sir

[013-125]_Thomas Brown_'s[C] remark upon this place; _Where indeed_ (saith he)

[013-126]Aristotle _plays the_ Aristotle; _that is, the wary and evading asserter;

[013-127]for tho' with_ non est fabula _he seems at first to confirm it, yet at

[013-128]last he claps in,_ sicut aiunt, _and shakes the belief he placed before

[013-129]upon it. And therefore_ Scaliger (saith he) _hath not translated the

[013-130]first, perhaps supposing it surreptitious, or unworthy so great an

[013-131]Assertor._ But had _Scaliger_ known it to be surreptitious, no doubt but

[013-132]he would have remarked it; and then there had been some Colour for the

[013-133]Gloss. But 'tis unworthy to be believed of _Aristotle_, who was so wary

[013-134]and cautious, that he should in so short a passage, contradict himself:

[013-135]and after he had so positively affirmed the Truth of it, presently doubt

[013-136]it. His [Greek: hosper legetai] therefore must have a Reference to what

[013-137]follows, _Pusillum genus, ut aiunt, ipsi atque etiam Equi_, as _Scaliger_

[013-138]himself translates it.

 
[Footnote A: _Bocharti Hierozoic. S. de Animalib. S. Script. part.

[013-139]Posterior_. lib. 1. cap. 11. p.m. 76.]

 
[Footnote B: _Scaliger. Comment. in Arist. Hist. Animal._ lib. 8. p.m.

[013-140]914.]

 
[Footnote C: Sir _Thomas Brown_'s _Pseudodoxia_, or, _Enquiries into

[013-141]Vulgar Errors_, lib. 4. cap. 11.]

[013-142]I do not here find _Aristotle_ asserting or confirming any thing of the

[013-143]fabulous Narrations that had been made about the _Pygmies_. He does not

[013-144]say that they were [Greek: andres], or [Greek: anthropoi mikroi], or

[013-145][Greek: melanes]; he only calls them [Greek: pygmaioi]. And discoursing of

[013-146]the _Pygmies_ in a place, where he is only treating about _Brutes_, 'tis

[013-147]reasonable to think, that he looked upon them only as such. _This is the

[013-148]place where the_ Pygmies _are; this is no fable,_ saith Aristotle, as 'tis

[013-149]that they are a Dwarfish Race of Men; that they speak the _Indian_

[013-150]Language; that they are excellent Archers; that they are very Just; and

[013-151]abundance of other Things that are fabulously reported of them; and

[013-152]because he thought them _Fables_, he does not take the least notice of

[013-153]them, but only saith, _This is no Fable, but a Truth, that about the Lakes

[013-154]of_ Nile such _Animals_, as are called _Pygmies_, do live. And, as if he

[013-155]had foreseen, that the abundance of Fables that _Ctesias_ (whom he saith

[013-156]is not to be believed) and the _Indian Historians_ had invented about

[013-157]them, would make the whole Story to appear as a Figment, and render it

[013-158]doubtful, whether there were ever such Creatures as _Pygmies_ in Nature;

[013-159]he more zealously asserts the _Being_ of them, and assures us, That _this

[013-160]is no Fable, but a Truth_.

[013-161]I shall therefore now enquire what sort of Creatures these _Pygmies_ were;

[013-162]and hope so to manage the Matter, as in a great measure, to abate the

[013-163]Passion these Great Men have had against them: for, no doubt, what has

[013-164]incensed them the most, was, the fabulous _Historians_ making them a part

[013-165]of _Mankind_, and then inventing a hundred ridiculous Stories about them,

[013-166]which they would impose upon the World as real Truths. If therefore they

[013-167]have Satisfaction given them in these two Points, I do not see, but that

[013-168]the Business may be accommodated very fairly; and that they may be allowed

[013-169]to be _Pygmies_, tho' we do not make them _Men_.
