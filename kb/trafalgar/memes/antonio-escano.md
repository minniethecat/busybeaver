# Antonio Escaño

A Spanish rear-admiral and major-general who served as Gravina's second-in-command at Trafalgar, argued against the fleet's departure, and commanded at Cádiz after the battle.

---

## Identity

Antonio Escaño (c. 1750–1814) was a Spanish naval officer holding the dual rank of rear-admiral and major-general in the Armada Española. At Trafalgar, he was second-in-command of the Spanish squadron under Federico Gravina, aboard the flagship *Príncipe de Astúrias*. He was among the senior officers wounded during the battle.

Escaño acted as the senior Spanish naval commander at Cádiz in the battle's immediate aftermath. He had the authority to grant or deny permission for vessels to put to sea on rescue missions — a role that placed him at the center of the post-battle recovery effort.

## Role in the Narrative

Escaño participates in the council of war convened by Villeneuve aboard the *Bucentaure*. He attends alongside Gravina, Vice-Admiral Álava, Rear-Admiral Cisneros, Commodore Galiano, and Captain Churruca (008-018). The Spanish officers unanimously oppose sailing out to seek the English, arguing that the season is too advanced and that the fleet is better employed on the defensive within the bay.

When the fleet does sail and battle is joined, Escaño fights from the *Príncipe de Astúrias*, which endures a "terrific fire" from the *Defiance* and *Revenge*. The flagship's "sides riddled with balls" tell the story: Escaño is wounded alongside Gravina during the engagement, and the Spanish resolve to break off — the battle already lost — hoisting the signal to retire (013-015).

After the battle, Escaño commands at Cádiz. Don José María Malespina approaches him for permission to board a rescue vessel seeking his son. In the narrator's paraphrase, Malespina finds "the Admiral in command, my old friend Escaño," and "after some hesitation" Escaño grants the request (015-011). This hesitation shows Escaño balancing humanitarian impulse against military discipline in the chaos following the disaster.

## Historical Context

Escaño was a senior officer in the Spanish navy during the Napoleonic Wars. As Spain's alliance with France placed its fleet alongside the French at Trafalgar, officers like Escaño faced the tension between obedience to Madrid and their own military judgment. His opposition to Villeneuve's plan to sail — recorded in the novel and attested in historical sources — proved prescient when the combined fleet was destroyed on 21 October 1805.

His survival of the battle, despite being wounded alongside Gravina, allowed him to coordinate the Spanish naval recovery at Cádiz, making him one of the few senior Spanish commanders to emerge from Trafalgar with their reputation intact and their life preserved.
