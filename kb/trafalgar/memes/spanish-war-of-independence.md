# Spanish War of Independence

The 1808--1814 conflict in which the Spanish people rose against Napoleonic occupation -- a war of national resistance whose spirit was already visible in the defiant patriotism of the sailors who fought at Trafalgar.

---

## Overview

The Spanish War of Independence (1808--1814), also known as the Peninsular War, was the uprising of the Spanish nation against the French imperial occupation imposed by Napoleon Bonaparte. What began with the popular insurrection of the Dos de Mayo in Madrid spread across the entire Iberian Peninsula and drew in British forces under the Duke of Wellington, becoming the protracted struggle that contemporaries called "the Spanish ulcer" -- the wound that bled Napoleon's empire dry.

The war does not appear within the pages of *Trafalgar*, which ends in the winter of 1805, shortly after the battle. But it is the invisible horizon toward which the novel points. The narrator Gabriel de Araceli, looking back as an old man, closes his account with a promise: "my fate, which had taken me to Trafalgar, led me subsequently through many glorious and inglorious scenes" (017-027). Those subsequent scenes -- told across the later volumes of Galdos's *Episodios Nacionales* -- include the War of Independence, in which Gabriel himself fights.

---

## Background

The roots of the war lay in the same Franco-Spanish alliance that had sent the combined fleet to its destruction at Trafalgar. The disastrous Treaty of San Ildefonso bound Spain to French interests, and as the young artillery officer Malespina explains on the eve of battle, "our alliance with France has proved most disastrous" (006-030). The alliance was deeply unpopular among ordinary Spaniards. Marcial, the old sailor, gives voice to a widespread sentiment when he fumes: "If Napoleon must fight, let him fight alone" (006-029). Doña Francisca echoes the same frustration more bluntly: "Why should Spain be subject to his lordship's vagaries?" (006-029).

Napoleon's continental ambitions knew no restraint. The destruction of the combined fleet at Trafalgar ended his invasion plans for England, but it did not check his power on land. The narrator notes the bitter irony: while Spanish and French ships were being destroyed off Cape Trafalgar, "at that very time Napoleon had won a glorious victory on land" (016-028) at Ulm, and within weeks he would crush the Austrians and Russians at Austerlitz -- "the greatest of his many victories" (016-028). Trafalgar secured British naval supremacy, but it left Spain exposed and exhausted, its fleet shattered and its treasury drained by subsidies to France.

By 1807 Napoleon had concluded that the Spanish Bourbons were unreliable allies. Exploiting the internal divisions of the Spanish court, he forced the abdication of Charles IV and his son Ferdinand VII, installing his own brother Joseph Bonaparte on the Spanish throne. The occupation that followed was meant to be a pacification. It became a war.

---

## Events

The uprising exploded on 2 May 1808 -- the Dos de Mayo -- when the citizens of Madrid attacked the French garrison with knives, scissors, and whatever weapons came to hand. The French repression was savage, and Francisco Goya's later paintings and etchings would immortalize both the heroism of the *mamelucos* and the firing squads of the third of May. The rising spread rapidly from Madrid to the provinces. Provincial juntas organized resistance, and what the French had expected to be a simple dynastic transition became a nationwide insurrection.

The conflict evolved into a war of unprecedented character. Regular Spanish armies, reinforced by British expeditionary forces under Sir Arthur Wellesley (later the Duke of Wellington), fought pitched battles at Bailen, Talavera, and Salamanca. But alongside the conventional campaign there emerged something new: the *guerrilla* -- literally "little war" -- bands of irregular fighters who harassed French supply lines, ambushed couriers, and made the occupation of the countryside a nightmare. The term *guerrilla* entered the European military vocabulary from this conflict.

The tide turned decisively in 1812. Wellington's victory at Salamanca opened the road to Madrid, and Napoleon's disastrous invasion of Russia forced him to withdraw troops from Spain. In 1813, at the Battle of Vitoria, the French were routed, and by early 1814 Ferdinand VII was restored to the Spanish throne. The war that had begun with the people of Madrid facing French cavalry armed with scissors ended with the French empire in retreat across the Pyrenees.

---

## Aftermath

The war devastated Spain. Cities lay in ruins, the economy was shattered, and the American colonies, left to govern themselves during the chaos, would soon embark on their own wars of independence. Ferdinand VII, restored to power, repudiated the liberal constitution of 1812 that the Cortes of Cadiz had enacted in his name, ushering in a period of reactionary absolutism that would plague Spain for decades.

Yet the war also forged a new sense of national identity. The resistance to Napoleon demonstrated that sovereignty resided not in dynasties or treaties but in the popular will. The sailors of Trafalgar had shown the same spirit: Marcial, thumping his wooden leg as he recited his "patriotic list" of Spanish ships and guns (006-028); Churruca, ordering the flag nailed to the mast so that no one could strike it while he breathed (013-030); the Spanish crews who "held out bravely against these odds till two in the afternoon" (013-023). The difference was that at Trafalgar the patriotic spirit was harnessed to a doomed strategic cause -- the service of an emperor who would repudiate his Spanish allies at the first opportunity. In 1808, that same spirit found a cause worthy of its sacrifice: the defense of the nation itself.

---

## Significance

In the arc of the *Episodios Nacionales*, the Spanish War of Independence transforms the meaning of Trafalgar. What was, in 1805, a catastrophic defeat in a French war becomes, in retrospect, the prologue to a national awakening. The narrator's closing words -- "how much I have seen, well worthy of record" (017-027) -- point forward to a story in which the courage wasted at Trafalgar is redeemed in the defense of the fatherland.

The war also completes the moral arc of the novel's treatment of patriotism. At Trafalgar, men fought and died in a war they did not choose, for an alliance they did not trust, under an emperor they resented. Doña Francisca's exasperated question -- "why should Spain be subject to his lordship's vagaries?" (006-029) -- hung over every Spanish gun fired on 21 October 1805. The War of Independence answered that question: Spain was subject to no one's vagaries, and the same stubborn courage that had faced English broadsides at Trafalgar would drive French armies from Spanish soil.
