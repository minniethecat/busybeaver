## Overview

The **Santa Ana** was a Spanish first-rate ship of the line mounting 112 guns, laid down in 1784. At the Battle of Trafalgar she served as the flagship of Vice-Admiral [[Ignacio de Álava]] and was one of the most heavily engaged vessels in the combined Franco-Spanish fleet. As Marcial noted before the battle, the *Santa Ana* was among the largest ships in the fleet, carrying 120 guns by one count (006-027), though the authoritative account records her as a 112-gun three-decker (013-009).

The *Santa Ana* opened the engagement against Collingwood's division alongside [[HMS Royal Sovereign]] and endured one of the most sustained batterings of any ship in the battle (013-022). She was captured by the British, then recaptured in a bold counter-attack led by her wounded commander — one of the most honourable episodes of Trafalgar (014-006). The ship survived the battle by eleven years, ultimately sinking in the bay of Havana in 1816 for want of repairs (013-009).

## Design and Armament

The *Santa Ana* was a three-decked first-rate, one of the largest Spanish warships of her era. She carried 112 guns, placing her among the most powerful vessels in the combined fleet, exceeded only by the 140-gun [[Santísima Trinidad]] and the 118-gun [[Príncipe de Astúrias]] (006-027). Her broadside weight and three-deck layout made her a formidable opponent in close action, though like many Spanish first-rates she was designed as much for showing the flag as for fleet battle.

Her construction dated to 1784, making her twenty-one years old at Trafalgar — not exceptionally aged for a ship of the line of that period, but no longer in her prime. By contrast the *Rayo*, a fellow Spanish ship, had been afloat for nearly fifty-six years (015-008).

## At Trafalgar

At the outset of the battle, the *Santa Ana* was placed in the van of the combined fleet under Vice-Admiral Álava. When Villeneuve ordered the fleet to wear — a manoeuvre that reversed the order of battle — Álava's van became the rear, exactly where Collingwood's lee column struck (013-009).

The *Santa Ana* and [[HMS Royal Sovereign]] opened the firing as the two opposing columns met, and soon every ship in turn came into action (013-022). The *Santa Ana*, supported only by the French *Fougueux*, faced the *Royal Sovereign* and four other British ships. The fighting was described as terrific: the two great flagships fought with their masts almost entangled, exchanging fire for six hours (013-009).

The toll was catastrophic. Vice-Admiral Álava and his second, Gardoqui, were both wounded — Álava's injuries would later prove fatal. Five officers and ninety-seven sailors were killed, with more than one hundred and fifty wounded (013-009). By the end of the engagement the *Santa Ana* had lost all her masts and her rudder, though her hull remained fairly sound. She was forced to strike her colours and was taken as a British prize (013-009).

The English prize crew placed on board proved less courteous than those on the *Trinidad*: some officers were "stern and repellent, doing all they could to mortify" the captured Spaniards, provoking alarming murmurs of rebellion among the crew (013-009).

The night of 21 October brought a violent storm, and the *Santa Ana* — dismasted, rudderless, and unable to make any course — drifted at the mercy of wind and waves (015-008). Her situation, as the gale rose, was desperate; it seemed to stand to reason that she was in greater jeopardy than even the elderly *Rayo* (015-008).

Yet the *Santa Ana* was not finished. On 22 October, when within sight of Spanish ships that had come out of Cádiz to rescue captured vessels, Álava — suffering from multiple wounds — addressed a stirring harangue to his disheartened crew. In a sudden rush they disarmed the English prize crew, threw them into the lower deck, and hoisted the Spanish flag once more (014-005). This bold counter-attack, made on a dismasted ship with half her complement killed or wounded, was described as "one of the most honorable episodes of the battle of Trafalgar" (014-006).

The *Asís*, the *Montañes*, and the [[Rayo]] — ships that had got off with Gravina on the 21st — came to the *Santa Ana*'s assistance, and together with two French ships and a frigate fought off the British vessels attempting to re-take her (014-010). The recapture succeeded, but the ship was now completely disabled. The French frigate *Themis* took her in tow, a "child leading a giant," and with favourable winds she finally approached Cádiz (014-011).

## Aftermath

The *Santa Ana* survived the battle of Trafalgar by eleven years. She would have lived much longer, the narrator records, "if she had not gone to the bottom for want of repairs in the bay of Havana, in 1816" (013-009). Her service after Trafalgar was not without distinction, but the neglect that finally claimed her reflected the declining fortunes of the Spanish navy in the post-Napoleonic era.
