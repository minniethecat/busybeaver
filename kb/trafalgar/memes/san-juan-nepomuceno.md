# San Juan Nepomuceno

The Spanish 74-gun ship whose captain, Cosme Churruca, fought her so heroically at Trafalgar that even the British officers who captured her spoke of him with awe and reverence.

---

## Overview

The *San Juan Nepomuceno* was a Spanish 74-gun third-rate ship of the line, and the last ship in the rear of the combined Franco-Spanish fleet at Trafalgar. Under the command of Captain Cosme Damián Churruca — a distinguished navigator, hydrographer, and one of the finest officers in the Spanish navy — she was fitted and arranged to his own specifications under a special privilege, making her one of the best-finished ships in the fleet (008-017).

Her defense at Trafalgar was the most celebrated Spanish action of the battle. Positioned at the extreme end of the line, she was isolated and attacked by five English vessels under Collingwood's division (013-022). Churruca commanded with "gloomy calmness" (013-025), his crew fought with "terrible promptitude and accuracy" (013-023), and the ship did not strike until her captain was dead, her masts were gone, and half her company lay killed or wounded (013-029). The novel presents her story through the eyewitness account of Rafael Malespina, who served aboard her and was wounded in her defense (013-014).

## Design and Armament

The *Nepomuceno* mounted 74 guns in the classic two-deck configuration of a third-rate. She was listed among the Spanish ships of the line in Marcial's enthusiastic recitation of the fleet's strength alongside the *Trinidad*, *Príncipe de Astúrias*, and *Santa Ana* (006-027). Unlike most Spanish warships, she benefited from Churruca's personal supervision of her fitting — he "had had it planned and fitted to his own taste, under a special privilege, and had made it one of the finest ships in the Spanish fleet" (008-017).

Her crew included both seasoned sailors and new recruits, but under Churruca's leadership "the new recruits had learned their lesson in courage in no more than a couple of hours' apprenticeship" (013-023). Among those serving aboard her was the young officer Rafael Malespina, the betrothed of Doña Rosa, whose presence on the *Nepomuceno* was learned by the narrator Gabriel before the battle (009-013).

## At Trafalgar

The *Nepomuceno* was placed at the rear of the combined fleet's line — a position of particular danger since Nelson's plan of attack was designed to isolate and overwhelm the rear squadron. Even before sailing, Churruca had a presentiment of disaster. He had voted against putting to sea in the council of war on board the *Bucentaure*, arguing that the advanced season and the enemy's superiority made remaining in Cádiz the wiser course (008-019). On the 19th of October, he told his brother-in-law Apodaca: "Sooner than surrender my ship, I will blow her up or go to the bottom. That is the duty of every man who serves his king and country" (013-018). In a letter to a friend that same day he wrote: "If you hear that my ship is taken you will know that I am dead" (013-018).

On the morning of the battle, Churruca assembled the crew and soldiers on deck, had the chaplain pronounce absolution, and addressed them with words that combined religion and military duty: "My children all: In God's name I promise heavenly bliss to all who die doing their duty. If one of you shirks it he shall be shot on the spot" (013-020). The harangue "filled every man on board with enthusiasm" (013-021).

When the action began, the *Nepomuceno*, at the end of the line, was attacked by five English ships under Collingwood. Two passed on, leaving Churruca to face three (013-022). His crew held out "bravely against these odds till two in the afternoon, suffering terribly," and their defense "struck the English not merely with dismay but with astonishment" (013-023). Churruca directed the action with "gloomy calmness," economizing fire and trusting to careful aim so that "each ball did terrible havoc on the foe" (013-025).

The English were forced to bring up reinforcements — six ships against one. The *Dreadnought* came alongside at half a pistol-shot's distance, and the *Nepomuceno* was pounded by the combined fire of "six giants pouring balls and small shot into a vessel of 74 guns" (013-024). Yet the Spanish crew's courage seemed to mount with the danger: "our ship seemed positively to grow bigger in proportion to the desperate bravery of her defenders" (013-024).

Churruca's death decided the battle for the *Nepomuceno*. He went below to judge a line of fire and succeeded in dismasting one of the enemy ships, but as he returned to the quarterdeck a cannonball struck his right leg, nearly severing it at the thigh (013-026). Refusing to be carried below, he said "It is nothing — go on firing" (013-026). Only when he understood he must give up command did he yield, calling for his lieutenant Moyna — who was already dead — and then for the officer of the first battery, who assumed command (013-027). He ordered the flag nailed to the mast, declaring the ship should never surrender so long as he breathed (013-030).

The effect on the crew was immediate and devastating: "A sudden paralysis of soul and body seemed to have fallen on the crew; they all stood petrified and speechless" (013-028). Churruca died with "the calm resignation of a just man and the fortitude of a hero" (013-030).

When the ship finally struck, the six British captains who had subdued her quarreled over the honor of receiving the sword of the dead hero. The Spanish officer who had taken command answered: "To all. The *Nepomuceno* would never have surrendered to one" (013-031). The English officers gazed "with sincere emotion on the body of the hapless Churruca," and one remarked that "a man of such illustrious qualities ought never to be exposed to the risks of battle; he should be kept to live and serve the interests of science and navigation" (013-032).

## Aftermath

After her capture, the *Nepomuceno* was taken as a prize by the British. The narrative records that she "was left for many years at Gibraltar, where she was regarded as an object of veneration and curiosity" (016-025). Her wounded, including Malespina, were distributed among other British and captured ships for treatment (013-033). The ship's heroic reputation survived her defeat: the story of Churruca's death, and the reply of her officer that she would never surrender to one ship alone, became legendary in both Spain and Britain.
