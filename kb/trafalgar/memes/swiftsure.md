# HMS Swiftsure

A 74-gun third-rate ship of the line that served at both the Battle of the Nile and Trafalgar, earning a place among the Royal Navy's most experienced warships of the Napoleonic era.

---

## Overview

HMS *Swiftsure* was a 74-gun Elizabeth-class third-rate ship of the line, launched in 1787 at Bucklers Hard. She displaced approximately 1,612 tons and carried a peacetime complement of around 590 men, which would have been increased for battle conditions. At Trafalgar she was commanded by Captain William Gordon Rutherfurd and fought in the lee column under Vice-Admiral Cuthbert Collingwood.

The *Swiftsure* was one of the veteran ships of the fleet, having already distinguished herself at the Battle of the Nile in 1798. Her crew were experienced and her presence in the Trafalgar line added weight to Collingwood's attack on the rear of the combined Franco-Spanish fleet.

## Design and Armament

The Elizabeth class of 74-gun ships was designed by Sir Thomas Slade and represented the standard British two-decker of the late 18th century. The *Swiftsure* mounted 28 x 32-pounder guns on her lower gundeck, 28 x 18-pounders on her upper gundeck, and 14 x 9-pounders on her quarterdeck, with 4 x 9-pounders on the forecastle — a total of 74 guns. Her broadside weight was approximately 780 pounds.

The 74-gun ship was the workhorse of the Royal Navy, combining a heavy enough broadside to fight in the line of battle with the speed and handling to serve in reconnaissance and blockade duties. The *Swiftsure* was built at Bucklers Hard on the Beaulieu River, one of the key private shipyards supplying the Navy during the expansion of the fleet in the American Revolutionary War period.

## At Trafalgar

The *Swiftsure* was positioned in Collingwood's lee column at Trafalgar and engaged the rear of the combined Franco-Spanish line. The lee column was the first to make contact with the enemy, and Collingwood's ships bore the brunt of the initial fighting before Nelson's weather column broke through further north. The *Swiftsure* contributed to the overwhelming British firepower that overwhelmed the allied rear division.

The novel does not name the *Swiftsure* individually; the narrative focuses on the Spanish experience aboard the *Santísima Trinidad* and related vessels. However, the presence of the British lee column under Collingwood, which included the *Swiftsure* and other 74-gun ships, is attested in the depiction of the opening phase of the battle as the British ships approached in two parallel lines (010-013).

The *Swiftsure* survived the battle with moderate casualties and returned to Gibraltar for repairs, having done her duty in one of the most decisive naval engagements in British history.

## Earlier Service

The *Swiftsure* was present at the Battle of the Nile on 1 August 1798, where she played a significant role under Captain Benjamin Hallowell. During the night action in Aboukir Bay, the *Swiftsure* engaged the French line and helped to destroy or capture much of the French Mediterranean fleet. Hallowell later famously sent Nelson a coffin made from the mainmast of the French flagship *L'Orient*, which had exploded during the battle — a macabre gift that Nelson reportedly kept in his cabin.

## Aftermath

After Trafalgar the *Swiftsure* continued to serve in the Mediterranean and the Channel. She was eventually broken up in 1816, having outlived the Napoleonic Wars. Her career spanned nearly thirty years and encompassed two of the most celebrated British naval victories of the era.
