# Marcial

The one-legged veteran sailor known as "Medio-hombre" (Half-Man), whose decades of naval experience, fierce patriotism, and rough tenderness make him the moral center of the novel's battle narrative.

## Identity

Marcial — universally called by his nickname "Medio-hombre" — is an old Spanish sailor who has lost a leg in service to the Spanish navy. His wooden leg is both a physical reality and a badge of honor: he has given his body to his country and wears the evidence without complaint. He is uneducated, speaking in the rough dialect of the Andalusian coast, but possesses deep practical wisdom about the sea, ships, and combat.

As a character type, Marcial belongs to the tradition of the loyal retainer — he has served under Don Alonso for years and extends his protective loyalty to the young Gabriel. But Marcial is no mere sidekick; his voice, opinions, and worldview are distinct and forceful. He represents the common Spanish seaman: brave, fatalistic, fiercely patriotic, and sharp-tongued about officers he considers incompetent.

His nickname "Medio-hombre" — literally "half a man" — is both literal (he has half the limbs of a whole man) and deeply ironic. In courage, competence, and moral authority, Marcial is more than whole — he is the character who most fully embodies the novel's values of duty, skill, and honor.

## Role in the Narrative

Marcial is the novel's principal source of naval knowledge and battle experience. It is he who explains the workings of the ship, the tactics of combat, and the character of the enemy to the young narrator. His running commentary on the battle — the "rattle of the cannon" that he mimics by "thumping his wooden leg on the floor" (004-046) — makes the technical realities of naval warfare vivid and accessible.

In the household, Marcial serves as Don Alonso's confidant and ally. When Don Alonso resolves to rejoin the fleet despite his wife's objections, Marcial supports him, declaring to Doña Francisca that it is "my duty to join the fleet" (002-016). Together, the old officer and the old sailor form a partnership of shared experience that transcends class difference.

During the battle itself, Marcial directs the crew, loads and fires cannon — "He loaded the cannon as quickly as he was able" (011-027) — and provides the steadying presence that keeps the young Gabriel from panic. His physical limitation (he cannot go aloft or move quickly) becomes irrelevant in the context of his knowledge and moral authority. When the battle turns desperate, it is Marcial who maintains the crew's spirit, taking grim pride in the damage they inflict even as the ship founders around them.

Marcial's relationship with Gabriel is the novel's emotional core. The old sailor sees something of his younger self in the boy and trains him with rough affection. The moment when Gabriel fires a cannon for the first time — "Bravely firing cannon like a man!" (011-029) — is Marcial's triumph as much as the boy's.
