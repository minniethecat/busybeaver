# Toulon

A fortified Mediterranean seaport and the principal naval arsenal of France, serving as both the operational base for Vice-Admiral Villeneuve's fleet and the strategic anchor of French naval power in southern Europe during the Napoleonic Wars.

---

## Overview

Toulon occupies a sheltered natural harbour on the Mediterranean coast of Provence, protected by a peninsula to the south and fortified headlands commanding the approaches. By the time of the Trafalgar campaign in 1805, it was the most important French naval base outside the Atlantic ports of Brest and Rochefort. Its dry docks, ropewalks, and ordnance stores supported a fleet capable of challenging British maritime supremacy. Nelson's Mediterranean squadron maintained a close blockade of Toulon for nearly two years, from the renewal of hostilities in May 1803 until Villeneuve slipped out in March 1805, beginning the chain of maneuvers that ended at Cape Trafalgar.

## Geography

Toulon sits at the head of a deep, well-protected anchorage framed by the Cépet peninsula to the east and the Sicié cape to the west. The inner harbour is divided into two basins: a commercial port and the military dockyard known as the Arsenal, constructed under the direction of Vauban and Colbert during the reign of Louis XIV. The city is ringed by hills that provided natural defensive positions, fortified over centuries by successive French governments. Its position on the Provençal coast places it roughly equidistant from the Spanish naval base at Cartagena to the southwest and the Italian peninsula to the east, making it a strategically situated command centre for projecting French naval power into the western and central Mediterranean.

## Role in the Novel

Toulon appears in Benito Pérez Galdós's *Trafalgar* primarily as the origin point of French naval threat. In the narrator's earliest childhood memories, Toulon occupies a place on the crude mental map of a boy who mistook the names of foreign ports for separate islands: "this scheme of geography I had constructed on the basis of the names of the places from which the ships came" (001-009). For young Gabriel, Toulon was simply one among many exotic locations -- alongside Gibraltar, Port Mahon, Russia, and London -- from which vessels arrived at the Cádiz quay.

The port takes on more concrete significance through Doña Francisca's bitter recollections of her husband's career. Railing against the constant disruption of family life by naval dispatches, she laments how "suddenly another dispatch comes from Madrid, with orders to go to Toulon or Brest or Naples -- go here and go there" (002-011). Her complaint captures the reality of Franco-Spanish alliance: Spanish ships and officers were routinely diverted to French bases, subordinating Spanish interests to Napoleon's strategic priorities.

Don Alonso's own service record includes an engagement at Toulon. The text records that he "fought in the glorious engagements between the Anglo-Spanish fleet and the French before Toulon in 1793" (003-001), a reference to the Royalist uprising that briefly delivered the port to Anglo-Spanish forces during the early revolutionary wars. This battle formed part of the long counter-revolutionary conflict in Provence, in which a young artillery officer named Napoleon Bonaparte first rose to prominence by driving the allied fleet from the harbour -- an irony not lost on readers who know the later trajectory of both the man and the city.

The most significant mention of Toulon occurs during the council of war aboard the *Bucentaure* in October 1805. Commodore Churruca, arguing against Villeneuve's proposal to sail out and seek battle, warns his fellow officers that the English cannot indefinitely "blockade Toulon and Cartagena" simultaneously if the combined fleet remains in Cádiz (008-019). Churruca's assessment proved prescient: the difficulty of maintaining simultaneous blockades was a persistent pressure on British naval strategy throughout the war. Villeneuve's desperation to escape this very blockade -- and his fear of being relieved of command by Napoleon -- ultimately drove the fatal sortie that led to Trafalgar.

## Significance

Toulon occupies a distinctive position in the Trafalgar narrative as the hidden antagonist in the background: a place that shapes the strategic calculus of the battle without ever appearing as a direct setting in the novel. The fleet that Villeneuve eventually led to Cádiz had been built, provisioned, and manned in Toulon. Its escape from Nelson's blockade in March 1805, followed by the fruitless cruise to the West Indies and the inconclusive action off Cape Finisterre in July, consumed months of the campaign's timeline and exhausted both crews and supplies.

For the Spanish actors in Galdós's story, Toulon embodies a double grievance. It was, first, the operational hub from which the French admiral they distrusted had come and to which Napoleon's peremptory orders were dispatched. Second, it was the site of a joint Anglo-Spanish victory in 1793 -- a campaign in which Spain and Britain had been allies against revolutionary France, only a decade before they became mortal enemies at Trafalgar. This reversal of alliances, from coalition partners to adversaries, haunts the memory of veterans like Don Alonso and invests the name of the Provençal port with a melancholy resonance that the young narrator only dimly perceives.
