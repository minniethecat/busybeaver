## Overview

The Storm After Trafalgar was a violent southerly gale that rose during the night of 21--22 October 1805 and raged for several days, striking the battle-damaged fleets while they were still struggling with the wreckage of the engagement. Many of the prizes captured by the British were lost to the tempest; the Spanish attempted to recapture drifting dismasted vessels; and the combined losses from the storm rivaled those of the battle itself. In Galdos's novel, the storm is depicted through the eyes of [[gabriel-de-araceli]] and the dying [[marcial]], transforming a meteorological catastrophe into a harrowing coda to the battle.

## Background

The [[battle-of-trafalgar]], fought on 21 October 1805, left the combined Franco-Spanish fleet shattered. Eighteen ships of the line had been taken as prizes by the British, but most were dismasted, holed, and barely afloat. The British fleet under [[cuthbert-collingwood]], who had succeeded the slain [[horatio-nelson]], was itself badly battered and faced the immense challenge of securing the captured vessels while tending to thousands of wounded and prisoners. The Spanish survivors who had escaped the battle took shelter in Cadiz, but the relief was temporary. As the novel describes, the fleet was in a shattered condition and the storm rose on the very night after the engagement:

> "...the shattered condition of our ship and the dreadful storm that rose during the night of the 21st..."
> (013-009)

The Spanish flagship [[santa-ana]], which had fought [[royal-sovereign]] in one of the fiercest duels of the battle, was among the vessels barely holding together as the weather deteriorated.

## Events

### The Gale Rises

During the night of 21 October, a southerly gale began to blow up across the battle zone, catching both the British fleet and its prizes in a precarious state. The wind increased steadily, and by the morning of 22 October it had reached full fury:

> "...the storm seemed to be at its wildest on purpose to add to the terror of the ceremony."
> (012-037)

Gabriel de Araceli, still aboard his battered ship, describes how the storm was perceived as a supernatural addition to the horror of the preceding day. The ceremonial burial at sea of the dead took place even as the waves grew higher and the winds more violent.

The gale continued to strengthen through 22 October. Aboard the Spanish vessels that had escaped the battle, including the *San Francisco de Asis*, the *Montanes*, and the *Rayo* -- three ships that had got off relatively lightly from the combat -- the situation was increasingly desperate (014-006). These ships had come out from Cadiz with the intention of recapturing some of the prizes, described vividly:

> "The *Asis*, the *Montanes*, and the *Rayo* -- three ships that had got off lightly from the battle and had come out from Cadiz on the 22nd with a view to recapturing the prizes. The brave cripples rushed into the fray again, defying the storm."
> (014-006)

### The Spanish Sortie and Recapture

Under the command of Admiral [[federico-gravina]], several Spanish ships ventured out from Cadiz into the teeth of the gale on 22 October. Their mission was to retake the drifting prizes before they could be towed to [[gibraltar]] by the British. The sortie involved the *Rayo*, the *Montanes*, and the *San Francisco de Asis* (014-011). Gabriel witnessed the approach of these ships:

> "I saw them making towards us. A southerly gale was blowing up and it was clear that they were making an effort to be able to keep before the gale."
> (014-013)

The Spanish crews managed to recapture the [[santa-ana]], one of the most important prizes. The *Rayo* passed to leeward of the recaptured vessel, and Spanish sailors hauled across to take possession of the dismasted flagship (014-024). The transfer of the prize crew was arduous:

> "The passage, though short, was difficult, and the danger was great."
> (014-032)

### Loss of the Rayo

The *Rayo*, a 100-gun ship of the line, was not to survive the storm. Having participated in the recapture of the *Santa Ana*, the *Rayo* attempted to make for the safety of Cadiz, but the gale proved too strong:

> "The gale not only prevented her getting into Cadiz, but was driving her onto a lee shore."
> (015-044)

The *Rayo* was driven aground and wrecked on the coast near Cadiz with great loss of life. The ship's name -- meaning "Thunderbolt" in Spanish -- had been a source of pride, but as one character remarks bitterly:

> "Rayo is an unlucky name for this coast."
> (015-001)

### The Fate of the Prizes

As the storm continued through 22 and 23 October, the British struggled to hold onto their captured vessels. Many had to be cut loose and abandoned to the sea rather than risk the towing ships. The French and Spanish prisoners aboard the prizes posed an additional danger, with fears they might mutiny and attempt to recapture the vessels (012-034). Some Spanish sailors who had been taken prisoner later escaped when their captors' ships foundered.

The British succeeded in towing a handful of prizes into [[gibraltar]], including the *San Juan Nepomuceno* -- the ship of the fallen [[cosme-churruca]] (016-025). But the majority of the captured vessels were lost. Ships that had survived the fire of battle could not survive the wind and waves. Gabriel himself, taken as a prisoner, was carried off to [[gibraltar]] after the ordeal (016-027).

## Aftermath

The combined losses from the storm significantly altered the strategic calculus of Trafalgar. Of the eighteen prizes taken by the British during the battle, only four ultimately reached Gibraltar intact. Hundreds of wounded men, both British and Franco-Spanish, drowned when the dismasted hulks on which they lay foundered. The Spanish recapture of the *Santa Ana* was one of the few consolations for the defeated allied fleet.

The storm also claimed the *Rayo* and contributed to the foundering of several other Spanish ships that had survived the battle. On 23 October, additional Spanish vessels ventured out again:

> "These had gone out again on the 23rd to recapture the vessels making for Gibraltar; and of these, two, the San Francisco de Asis and the Principe de Asturias, as well as the Montanes, 80 guns, commanded by Alcedo, returned safely."
> (016-025)

The human cost of the storm deepened the tragedy immeasurably. Men who had survived the horror of the battle found themselves fighting a new and more implacable enemy. The sea claimed sailors and officers alike with no distinction between victors and vanquished.

## Significance

The Storm After Trafalgar is inseparable from the battle itself in both historical and literary terms. Historically, it robbed Britain of the full material fruits of its victory -- the prize ships that would have enriched the fleet and its officers. Napoleon's propaganda machine was able to blur the scale of the defeat by pointing to the final tally of ships actually brought into British ports.

In Galdos's novel, the storm serves a crucial narrative function. It extends the ordeal beyond the battle, denying the reader an easy resolution. Gabriel's survival through both battle and storm marks his passage from boyhood into a harder, more knowing maturity. The death of [[marcial]], the old sea-dog who had fought at [[battle-of-cape-st-vincent]] and embodied the spirit of the old Spanish navy, occurs not in the glory of combat but in the bleak aftermath of the gale -- a fittingly grim conclusion to a story in which heroism and futility are inseparable:

> "I doubt our getting into port with all these dead bodies on board."
> (015-023)

The storm thus becomes, in Galdos's hands, the final judgment on the folly of the engagement. The wind and sea do not discriminate among nations; they sweep away French, Spanish, and British alike, reducing the grand strategy of emperors and admirals to the elemental struggle of men against nature.
