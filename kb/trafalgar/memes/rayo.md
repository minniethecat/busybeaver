# Rayo

The *Rayo* — Spanish for "Thunderbolt" — was a 100-gun first-rate ship of the line of the Armada Española, one of the most powerful Spanish warships at the Battle of Trafalgar. She fought in the vanguard division under Vice-Admiral Álava, survived the battle, and was among the few Spanish ships to escape the disaster with her hull intact.

---

## Design and Armament

The *Rayo* was rated at 100 guns, placing her among the largest warships in the Spanish fleet — comparable to the *Santa Ana* (120 guns) and exceeded only by the four-decked *Santísima Trinidad* (140 guns). She is listed alongside "the *Santa Ana* 120, the *Rayo* 100, the *Nepomuceno*" (006-027) in a roll call of Spanish naval power.

Her name, meaning thunderbolt in Spanish, was a point of pride among her crew. When Marcial mockingly refers to a French officer as "that clever man that called you the *Rayo*" (009-020), he invokes the ship's fierce reputation. But the name also carried a darker significance — after the battle, a survivor grimly reflects that "*Rayo* is an unlucky name for this coast, where there are always storms at this season" (015-001), foreshadowing the ship's eventual fate.

## Role at Trafalgar

The *Rayo* served in the vanguard of the Combined Fleet, the forward division led by Vice-Admiral Álava aboard the *Santa Ana*. During the pre-battle discussions, the Spanish sailors debated the disposition of their forces, noting "the *Neptuno* and the *Rayo* which are in front" (010-005) — a position that placed them directly in the path of Nelson's advancing columns.

When the British attack shattered the Franco-Spanish line, the *Rayo* was one of the few ships that attempted to come to the aid of their comrades. "Only the *Rayo* and *Neptuno* came to our assistance" (012-008), a witness recalls, as the *Santa Ana* was battered from both sides. The *Rayo* and *Neptuno* "made every effort to help us, but in vain; for before they could reach us we were totally dismasted and our hull shot through" (011-014). This failed rescue attempt captures the chaos of Trafalgar. As a survivor recounted:

> "Only the Rayo and Neptuno came to our assistance; and they, after fighting with the greatest gallantry and losing many men, were obliged to sheer off." (012-008)

Even the best-intentioned maneuvers were rendered hopeless by the speed and violence of the British assault.

## Aftermath

Unlike many Spanish ships that were captured or destroyed, the *Rayo* was among "three ships that had got off to windward" (014-006) — surviving the battle with enough maneuverability to escape. Alongside the *Montañes* and the *San Francisco de Asís*, she had "supported" the battered *Santa Ana* during the fighting (014-011).

In the battle's aftermath, the *Rayo* participated in the grim work of rescue. As the shattered *Santa Ana* drifted helplessly, "the *Rayo* passed to leeward and we hailed her" (014-024) — Álava, wounded but still commanding, begged for assistance. "Boatloads were pulled off to the *Rayo*" (014-032), transferring survivors from the crippled flagship. The passage, though short, was treacherous — the sea had risen and the exhausted crews faced the double threat of drowning and capture by British patrols.

## Historical Fate

The historical *Rayo* was indeed an unlucky ship. Launched in 1749 as a 80-gun ship and later rebuilt to carry 100 guns, she survived Trafalgar only to be wrecked in the storm that followed the battle. After being re-captured by the Spanish from the British prize crews, she ran aground near Sanlúcar de Barrameda on 23 October 1805 and was destroyed — a fate that gives bitter weight to the sailor's premonition about her name.
