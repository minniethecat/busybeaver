# HMS Royal Sovereign

Collingwood's flagship at Trafalgar, the 100-gun first-rate that was the first British ship to engage the enemy line — her newly coppered hull carrying her into battle ahead of even Nelson's *Victory*.

---

## Overview

HMS *Royal Sovereign* was a 100-gun first-rate ship of the line, launched in 1786 and extensively refitted before Trafalgar. She was the flagship of Vice-Admiral Cuthbert Collingwood, Nelson's second-in-command, who had been given command of the leeward column in the British two-pronged attack. Collingwood had her hull freshly coppered before the battle, a decision that made her the fastest sailer in the fleet — and the first British ship to break the enemy line.

The novel introduces the *Royal Sovereign* through the eyes of the Spanish narrator, who observes the British approach from the deck of the *Trinidad*: "At the head of the other line was the *Royal Sovereign*, commanded by Collingwood" (010-013). The Spanish sailors, already awed by the sight of the *Victory* bearing down on them, see the second column advancing and understand that they face not one but two coordinated attacks — a tactical predicament from which there is no escape.

## Design and Armament

The *Royal Sovereign* was a three-decker carrying 100 guns: 28 × 32-pounders on the lower gundeck, 28 × 24-pounders on the middle, and 30 × 12-pounders on the upper deck, supplemented by carronades on the quarterdeck and forecastle. Her crew numbered approximately 850 men. Built at Plymouth Dockyard, she was one of the larger British first-rates and had served in the Channel Fleet and the Mediterranean before Trafalgar.

Her freshly coppered bottom, applied in the refit preceding Trafalgar, gave her a significant speed advantage. This was a deliberate choice by Collingwood, who knew that speed into action would be the decisive factor in the British attack. The result was that the *Royal Sovereign* reached the enemy line some fifteen minutes before any other ship in the leeward column — an isolated and exposed position that Collingwood accepted as the price of setting the example.

## At Trafalgar

The *Royal Sovereign* led the leeward column directly at the rear of the combined fleet. The narrator records that "a vessel in the rear had been the first to fire on the *Royal Sovereign*, commanded by Collingwood" (011-001), confirming that she was the target of the earliest Spanish defensive fire. While the *Royal Sovereign* carried on her fight with the Spanish *Santa Ana*, the *Victory* came down on the center. The battle ignited along the line as "the fire ran along from the *Santa Ana* in the middle, to each end of the line" (011-001).

The engagement between the *Royal Sovereign* and the *Santa Ana* was one of the most prolonged and brutal of the battle. The *Santa Ana*, a Spanish 112-gun first-rate under Vice-Admiral Alava, "only supported by the *Fougueux*, a Frenchman, had to fight the *Royal Sovereign* and four other English ships" (013-009). The narrator notes that despite the unequal odds, "one side suffered as much as the other, for Collingwood's ship was the first to retire and the *Euryalus* took her place" (013-009). The two great ships fought for six hours, their masts nearly entangled, exchanging fire at close range. By the time the *Santa Ana* was forced to surrender, she had suffered grievous casualties — both Alava and his second-in-command Gardoqui wounded, 97 sailors killed, and over 150 wounded (013-009).

The *Royal Sovereign*, though battered and forced to withdraw from close action, had done her work. The leeward column had broken through, and Collingwood had demonstrated the aggressive spirit upon which Nelson's battle plan depended. After Nelson was mortally wounded aboard the *Victory*, Collingwood assumed command of the entire fleet, directing the final stages of the battle and the subsequent storm operations from the *Royal Sovereign*.

## Aftermath

The *Royal Sovereign* survived Trafalgar, though heavily damaged. After repairs at Gibraltar, she returned to service in the Mediterranean and later the Channel Fleet, serving until 1841 — one of the longer-lived Trafalgar veterans. Collingwood, elevated to the peerage as Baron Collingwood, continued to command from her until his health failed and he died at sea in 1810. The *Royal Sovereign* was eventually broken up, her timbers dispersed, but her name and legacy remained: she had been the first ship into action at the decisive naval battle of the age.

The novelist himself acknowledges Collingwood's humanity after the battle: "Collingwood, in his narrative, does justice to this generosity on the part of my fellow-countrymen" (016-024), referring to the Spanish citizens of Cadiz who cared for the wounded of both sides without distinction.
