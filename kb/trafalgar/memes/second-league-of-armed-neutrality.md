# Second League of Armed Neutrality

The 1800-1801 alliance of Baltic powers, led by Russia under Tsar Paul I, that sought to protect neutral shipping rights against British naval dominance — an episode that revealed the tensions underlying Britain's blockade strategy and shaped the diplomatic landscape in which Trafalgar was fought.

---

## Overview

The Second League of Armed Neutrality was a coalition of northern European powers — Russia, Denmark-Norway, Sweden, and Prussia — formed in December 1800 to resist the British Royal Navy's practice of searching neutral vessels and seizing enemy goods. The League revived the principles of the First Armed Neutrality of 1780, asserting that neutral ships could freely trade with belligerent powers and that blockades must be physically effective to be legally binding.

The League's formation was directly stimulated by British naval aggression: the Royal Navy's interception of a Danish frigate in July 1800 and its seizure of the Swedish convoy in the same year convinced the Baltic powers that collective action was necessary. For Britain, which depended on naval dominance to prosecute its war against Napoleonic France, the League posed a grave threat — not merely in principle, but materially, since the Baltic was a critical source of timber, hemp, and other naval stores without which the Royal Navy could not function.

The crisis came to a head in early 1801, when a British fleet under Sir Hyde Parker and Horatio Nelson attacked Copenhagen, destroying much of the Danish fleet in the Battle of Copenhagen (2 April 1801). The assassination of Tsar Paul I in March 1801 — before news of Copenhagen reached Russia — effectively decapitated the League, which dissolved soon afterward. Though short-lived, the Second League demonstrated how British naval policy, in its uncompromising application, risked creating coalitions of neutral powers that could threaten the strategic foundations of British sea power.

## Background

The League emerged from a long-standing dispute over neutral rights at sea. Britain, fighting a life-and-death struggle against Revolutionary and Napoleonic France, insisted on its right to stop and search neutral vessels for contraband destined for French ports. The Baltic powers, whose merchant fleets carried a substantial share of European trade, rejected this claim and held that neutral flags protected cargo from seizure. The principle of "free ships, free goods," which Britain had never accepted, was at the core of the disagreement.

The immediate catalyst came in summer 1800. On 25 July, the British frigate HMS *Nemesis* intercepted the Danish frigate *Freya* in the English Channel and, after a brief engagement, escorted her into the Downs. Denmark protested; Britain refused to back down, and the incident pushed Copenhagen toward cooperation with Russia. That same year, a British squadron seized a Swedish merchant convoy in the Mediterranean. Prussia, its Baltic trade disrupted, joined the emerging coalition.

The diplomatic context was shaped by the broader war against France. The novel's characters discuss the French alliance in terms that reveal how diplomatic alignment was a matter of existential concern. The "villainous treaty of San Ildefonso" (004-041) bound Spain to France; other powers sought safety in armed neutrality. Tsar Paul I, increasingly estranged from his former British allies and drawn toward Napoleon, saw the League as both a principled defense of neutral rights and a means of asserting Russian influence in northern Europe.

## Events

The League was formalized by a convention signed at St. Petersburg on 16 December 1800 between Russia, Denmark-Norway, Sweden, and Prussia. The signatories agreed to close the Baltic to British warships, to convoy their merchant vessels with armed escorts, and to treat any attempt to search or seize their ships as a hostile act. An embargo on British goods in Russian ports followed immediately.

Britain's response was rapid and decisive. In March 1801, a fleet of 20 ships of the line under Admiral Sir Hyde Parker, with Vice-Admiral Horatio Nelson as second-in-command, sailed for the Baltic with orders to break the League by force. The fleet arrived off Copenhagen on 30 March. Nelson, characteristically impatient with Parker's caution, led the attack on 2 April in what became the Battle of Copenhagen — a savage engagement fought in confined waters against Danish shore batteries and moored ships. Nelson's controversial "turning a blind eye" to Parker's signal of recall occurred during this battle. The Danish fleet was largely destroyed, and Denmark agreed to an armistice.

But the decisive blow to the League had already fallen. Tsar Paul I had been assassinated in a palace coup on 23 March 1801 — eleven days before Copenhagen. His successor, Alexander I, was far more sympathetic to Britain and immediately abandoned his father's policy of armed neutrality. Sweden and Prussia, deprived of Russian backing, had no choice but to follow suit. The League was formally dissolved by a convention between Britain and Russia in June 1801.

## Aftermath

The collapse of the Second League removed the most serious diplomatic challenge to British naval dominance between the French Revolutionary Wars and the Trafalgar campaign. The Baltic remained open as a source of naval stores — a strategic necessity that Nelson's biographers and naval historians have often noted. Britain's willingness to attack a neutral capital to preserve its maritime rights established a precedent that would inform its conduct for the remainder of the Napoleonic Wars.

The episode also had a lasting effect on Nelson's reputation. Copenhagen demonstrated his capacity for independent command under fire and his willingness to take calculated risks when the strategic stakes demanded it. The experience of fighting in confined Baltic waters added to the tactical inventiveness he would later display at Trafalgar.

The Spanish experience of alliance politics during this period was quite different. The novel captures the bitterness of a nation bound by treaty to a war not of its own making: "This French alliance," Marcial remarks in the novel, "has proved most disastrous" (006-030). While the Baltic powers organized to resist interference with their interests, Spain — as shown in the novel — was drawn ever deeper into France's war against Britain, culminating in the destruction of the combined fleet at Trafalgar. The Second League's collapse and the Battle of Trafalgar both demonstrated, in different registers, the overwhelming force of British naval power when fully committed.

## Significance

The Second League of Armed Neutrality illuminates a crucial dimension of the strategic context surrounding Trafalgar: Britain's naval dominance was never absolute, but continuously contested by neutral powers whose interests diverged from those of the belligerents. The League revealed how quickly a coalition of neutrals could threaten to sever the supply lines that sustained the Royal Navy. That Britain's response was to send Nelson to literally shoot its way into Copenhagen underscores the ruthlessness with which British naval supremacy was maintained.

For the novel's world, the League serves as important historical context. The "blockade" of Cádiz that Gabriel and his shipmates endure (006-036) was part of a global system of British maritime control that the League had challenged and failed to break. The "villainous treaty of San Ildefonso" (004-041) that bound Spain to France's war was forged in the same diplomatic crucible that produced the League — a European state system in which neutrality was difficult to maintain and alliances carried mortal risks. Understanding the Second League helps explain why Britain could concentrate overwhelming force off Cádiz in 1805: the Baltic was quiet, the neutral powers subdued, and the Royal Navy could direct its full attention to the destruction of the combined fleet.
