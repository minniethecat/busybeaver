# Paquita

A young woman of Don Alonso's household circle whose presence in the Cádiz domestic scenes adds a dimension of youthful attachment and civilian normalcy to the novel's naval world.

---

## Identity

Paquita — likely a cousin, niece, or ward within Don Alonso's extended household — appears in the domestic scenes set in Cádiz before the fleet sails. She represents the world of civilian affections that naval service puts at risk: the young women who wait, the marriages that may never happen, the ordinary life interrupted by extraordinary events.

Her role is small but significant. She is mentioned in the context of family conversations about the fleet, and her presence adds weight to Doña Francisca's arguments against Don Alonso's departure. She is also, implicitly, a figure of romantic possibility — the young narrator Gabriel observes her with a boy's dawning awareness of feminine presence, though the novel is too much a war story to develop this thread.

## Role in the Narrative

Paquita appears as part of the domestic ensemble that surrounds Don Alonso's household. When Doña Francisca argues against rejoining the fleet, Paquita's presence — as a young woman whose future is bound up with the men who are leaving — strengthens the emotional force of the domestic case. She is a reminder that naval warfare is not only about ships and guns but about the human relationships it severs.

Her character is not developed in depth — she is a sketch rather than a portrait — but her function is clear: to personalize the cost of war, to give a face to the civilian world that watches the fleet sail and waits for its return.
