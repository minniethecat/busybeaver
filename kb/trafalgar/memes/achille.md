# Achille

A French 74-gun ship of the line destroyed in the most spectacular single catastrophe of the battle — the explosion of her magazine during the afternoon fighting.

---

## Overview

The *Achille* was a French Téméraire-class 74-gun ship of the line, launched in 1803 and among the newest major warships in the combined fleet at Trafalgar. Commanded by Captain Louis-Gabriel Deniéport, she was assigned to the rear (southern) division of the Franco-Spanish line. Her destruction was the most visually terrifying event of the battle: after catching fire during close action, her magazines detonated in a blast that momentarily illuminated the entire battlefield.

The explosion of the *Achille* is witnessed from the Spanish flagship *Trinidad* in the novel. The narrator describes "a tremendous explosion, louder than all the thousand guns of the fleet fired at once" (011-015) — a detonation so powerful that it rents the smoke and "reveals the whole panorama of the battle" (011-015). The *Achille*'s destruction encapsulates the arbitrary violence of naval warfare: "a noble ship of 74 guns and 600 men" reduced in seconds to "a myriad fragments" (011-016).

## Design and Armament

The *Achille* was a standard French 74-gun two-decker of the Téméraire class, the workhorse design of the French navy during the Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars. She carried 28 × 36-pounder long guns on her lower deck, 30 × 18-pounders on her upper deck, and 16 × 8-pounders plus carronades on her quarterdeck and forecastle. Her crew numbered approximately 600 men.

As a third-rate ship of the line, the *Achille* balanced firepower with maneuverability. She was not the largest or most heavily armed vessel in the combined fleet, but 74s formed the backbone of every Napoleonic-era battle line, prized for their combination of a powerful broadside with adequate sailing qualities.

## At Trafalgar

The *Achille* was stationed in the rear division of the combined fleet, on the southern side of the line. As the British attack developed and the center collapsed — Villeneuve's [[Bucentaure]] struck her colors, and the *Trinidad* was surrounded — the *Achille* became heavily engaged with British ships.

The catastrophe came in the afternoon. The narrator, aboard the [[Santísima Trinidad]], experiences it first through the senses: a flash so intense it "lighted up the two fleets" and a sound so loud it "paralyzed every man" (011-015). At first, the Spanish sailors could not identify which ship had exploded — they speculated it was the *Santa Ana*, the *Argonauta*, the *Ildefonso*, or the *Bahama* (011-016). Only later did they learn "it was a Frenchman, the *Achille*" (011-016).

The cause of the fire is not detailed in the novel, but historically the *Achille* caught fire when a British shell or heated wadding ignited her rigging or a stack of ready-use cartridges on deck. The flames spread quickly to the magazine. Of her 600 men, fewer than 200 survived — the British sent boats to rescue those who had leaped into the sea before the explosion.

In the novel's retrospective summary at Cádiz, the narrator confirms: "The *Achille* blew up, as I have said, in the midst of the action" (016-027).

## Aftermath

The destruction of the *Achille* was one of the most widely reported incidents of Trafalgar. The explosion was visible for miles and became a staple of battle paintings and eyewitness accounts. For the French navy, already reeling from the loss of Villeneuve's flagship and the general defeat, the *Achille*'s fate underscored the total nature of the disaster: the combined fleet had lost not only the battle but many of its finest ships and most experienced crews.
