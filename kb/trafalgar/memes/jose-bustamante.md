## Identity

José de Bustamante y Guerra (1759--1825) is a historical Spanish naval officer who appears by name in the novel through the recollections of [[marcial]]. Bustamante commanded the Spanish squadron of four frigates -- the *Medea*, *Fama*, *Mercedes*, and *Santa Clara* -- tasked with escorting treasure from the Americas to Cádiz in 1804. In real history, he later served as Governor of Montevideo and Captain General of Guatemala, but the novel references him solely for his role in the peacetime engagement that became known as the Action of 5 October 1804.

## Role in the Narrative

Bustamante appears in the novel only through Marcial's narration, as the old sailor recounts how he lost his leg. Marcial describes sailing from Montevideo aboard the *Fama* with a squadron conveying treasure -- gold, silver, and goods -- back to Spain (004-025). The squadron was intercepted off the Portuguese coast by four British frigates under Commodore Graham Moore, despite peace still officially prevailing between Britain and Spain.

Marcial's judgment of Bustamante is blunt and negative. He states that "the commander of our squadron, Don José Bustamante, was not very prompt" (004-028), implying that Bustamante's hesitation contributed to the Spanish defeat. The British demanded surrender before war had been declared; the Spanish refused and the engagement that followed was catastrophic.

> The English commodore sent a little whippersnapper officer, in a swallowtail coat, on board the Medea, who wasted no time in trifling but said at once that though war had not been declared, the commodore had orders to take us. (004-028)

The *Mercedes* blew up with heavy loss of life, the *Medea* and *Clara* struck their colors, and the *Fama* was captured after attempting to flee. Marcial lost his leg to a cannonball during this action and was taken to England as a prisoner (004-028).

## Historical Context

Bustamante was a career officer in the Spanish Royal Armada, born in Ontaneda, Cantabria, in 1759. The Action of 5 October 1804, also known as the Battle of Cape Santa Maria, took place in peacetime -- Britain feared that the treasure convoy from Montevideo would fund Napoleon's war efforts. Bustamante refused the British demand to surrender, reportedly declaring that Spanish frigates would defend their honor.

> We were sailing on quite confidingly, for no one expected to be trapped even by a heretic dog of a Moor, much less by an Englishman who does the polite thing in a Christian fashion. (004-012)

The engagement lasted only minutes: within ten minutes the *Mercedes* exploded, killing nearly all aboard. Bustamante, on board the flagship *Medea*, was overwhelmed by superior British forces and was taken prisoner to Plymouth, England. He was later fully exonerated by a Spanish court-martial. The unprovoked peacetime attack outraged Spain and directly precipitated the declaration of war against Britain in December 1804, which in turn set the stage for the [[participant::battle-of-trafalgar]].
