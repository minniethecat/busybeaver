# Doña Francisca

The formidable wife of Don Alonso, whose desperate efforts to prevent her husband from rejoining the fleet embody the domestic cost of naval warfare — the wives and families left behind when men go to sea.

---

## Identity

Doña Francisca is Don Alonso's wife, a woman of strong character and stronger opinions. Where her husband is gentle and philosophical, Francisca is practical and fiercely protective of her household. She views the navy not as a noble calling but as a rival that has repeatedly taken her husband from her and now threatens to take him again — perhaps for the last time.

Her opposition to Don Alonso's departure is not weakness or cowardice but clear-eyed realism. She understands, perhaps better than he does, what is at stake: his life, their life together, the domestic world they have built. Her arguments are not abstract but personal — she appeals to his age, his health, his obligations to her and to their household.

Doña Francisca is not unsympathetic to naval patriotism; she simply refuses to subordinate domestic love to it. In a novel dominated by male voices discussing duty, honor, and courage, her voice represents a different kind of morality — one grounded in care, relationship, and the preservation of life rather than its sacrifice.

## Role in the Narrative

Doña Francisca's role centers on her opposition to Don Alonso's decision to rejoin the fleet. She employs every weapon in her domestic arsenal — argument, tears, appeals to duty ("his young man cannot join the squadron" (006-016)), and even attempts to enlist Marcial's support. Her campaign fails — Don Alonso goes to sea — but her opposition gives voice to a perspective the novel takes seriously.

She represents the civilian world that naval warfare disrupts and destroys. Her fears are not irrational; they are prophetic. The novel does not mock her for trying to keep her husband safe; it honors the love that motivates her even as it admires the duty that compels him to leave.
