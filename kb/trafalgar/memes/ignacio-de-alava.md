# Ignacio María de Álava

Ignacio María de Álava was a Spanish vice-admiral who commanded the vanguard of the Combined Fleet at the Battle of Trafalgar. Flying his flag from the first-rate *Santa Ana*, he led the Spanish line into the battle and fought on despite severe wounds, embodying the doomed professionalism of the Spanish navy under French command.

---

## Role in the Narrative

Álava appears in the novel as one of the most respected Spanish commanders, mentioned alongside Churruca and Alcalá Galiano as an officer of genuine capability (002-009). Before the fleet sails from Cádiz, he is identified among the senior leadership: "the Vice-Admiral Álava, Rear-Admirals Escaño and Cisneros" (008-018). His flagship assignment is to the *Santa Ana* (009-020), and he leads the van — the forward division of six vessels that would bear the brunt of the British attack (009-024).

During the battle, Álava fights with distinction even as the Combined Fleet collapses around him. The narrator, recovering from his own injuries, glimpses a figure he concludes "must be Álava, and though suffering from severe wounds he was still directing the manoeuvres" (014-004). The admiral continues to command from his shattered flagship, and "in spite of his wounds, he gave his orders in a low but perfectly distinct voice" (014-014). In the battle's aftermath, the *Santa Ana* hails a nearby vessel and "Álava begged her to enquire of the English admiral whether he would give her leave to anchor" (014-024) — a moment that captures the shift from combat to survival. By this point, "Álava is very bad, most of the officers and crew being killed or wounded" (014-025).

> "The vanguard led by Álava consists of six vessels; the centre, which the French admiral commands, consists of twenty-one, all French; the rearguard consists of fourteen ships under Admiral Gravina." (009-024)

## Historical Context

The historical Ignacio María de Álava y Sáenz de Navarrete (1750–1817) was one of Spain's most experienced naval officers by 1805. He had served in campaigns across the Atlantic and the Mediterranean, and his appointment as second-in-command of the Spanish squadron reflected both seniority and professional reputation. At Trafalgar, his van division was positioned at the head of the Franco-Spanish line — the first to encounter Nelson's advancing columns.

Álava survived Trafalgar despite serious wounds, a testament to his resilience. The *Santa Ana*, a 112-gun first-rate, was one of the few Spanish ships to be recovered after the battle, and Álava's conduct under fire earned him continued respect in the post-Trafalgar Spanish navy. His fate contrasts with that of Churruca, who died commanding the *San Juan Nepomuceno*, and Gravina, who succumbed to wounds shortly after the battle.
