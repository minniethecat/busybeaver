## Overview

The Treaty of San Ildefonso was not a single document but two successive agreements — in 1796 and 1800 — that bound Spain to France in a military and political alliance against Great Britain. In the novel, Spanish characters refer to it repeatedly as the root cause of their fleet's tragic entanglement in the Trafalgar campaign: an alliance they did not want, a war that served only French ambition, and a commitment they could not escape without dishonor. The treaty is also called the "Treaty of Subsidies" (002-019) because of the financial tribute Spain was forced to pay Napoleon in a futile attempt to purchase neutrality.

## Background

After the execution of Louis XVI in 1793, Spain had joined the First Coalition against Revolutionary France, a war that ended in 1795 with the Treaty of Basle (006-032). That agreement, negotiated by the Spanish minister Manuel Godoy (the "Prince of Peace"), already tilted Spain toward accommodation with the French Republic. The first Treaty of San Ildefonso, signed in August 1796, formalized this realignment into an offensive-defensive alliance — effectively making Spain an enemy of Britain, its former ally. A second treaty in 1800 deepened the commitment, placing the Spanish fleet directly under Napoleon's strategic direction.

In the novel, Churruca's assessment of how the treaty came about is blunt:

> "This French alliance and that villainous treaty of San Ildefonso, which the astuteness of Bonaparte and the weakness of our government made a mere question of subsidies, will be the ruin of us and the ruin of our fleet if God does not come to the rescue, and afterwards will be the ruin of the colonies too and of Spanish trade with America."
> (004-041)

## Events

The treaty's consequences unfolded in stages, as recounted by the novel's characters:

**The Treaty of Basle pivot (1795).** Spain's peace with France after the War of the Pyrenees was the diplomatic gateway that led directly to the San Ildefonso agreements (006-032). Godoy, elevated to the title "Prince of Peace" for his role in the treaty, steered Spain away from the anti-French coalition.

**The first San Ildefonso treaty (1796).** The offensive-defensive alliance committed Spain and France to joint military action against any enemy — in practice, Britain. This was the arrangement that Churruca denounced as "villainous" and driven by Bonaparte's cunning (004-041).

**The second treaty and the subsidy trap (1800).** Napoleon, now First Consul, pressed Spain for renewed allegiance. Malespina provides the most detailed account of the diplomatic chain reaction:

> "The defeat might not have led to such serious consequences if the Spanish ministry had not signed the treaty of San Ildefonso with the French republic. That put us at the mercy of the First Consul, obliging us to support him in wars which had no aim or end but the furthering of his ambition. [...] We wished to remain neutral, for that treaty did not oblige us to take any part in the second war, but he insisted on our cooperation with so much determination that the King of Spain, to pacify him, agreed to pay him a subsidy of a hundred millions of reales — it was purchasing our neutrality with gold."
> (006-034)

The subsidy was meant to buy Spain out of active participation, but as Malespina explains, it failed: England seized four Spanish treasure frigates returning from America, and Madrid was forced to declare war and place its navy under French command.

## Aftermath

Once committed, Spain could not extricate itself. The combined Franco-Spanish fleet was placed under French admirals, culminating in Villeneuve's command at Trafalgar. The Spanish navy, once among the finest in Europe, was deployed for Napoleon's strategic goals — the Martinique feint, the plan to invade England — none of which served Spanish interests. In the novel, even the common sailors comprehended the injustice. Marcial, the veteran gunner, gave voice to their resentment on the eve of battle:

> "Whenever we have had to work with them they have got us into mischief and we have had the worst of it. Well — may God and the Holy Virgin del Cármen be on our side, and rid us of our French friends forever and ever, Amen."
> (009-025)

The treaty's ultimate harvest was the destruction of the combined fleet at Trafalgar, the deaths of Churruca, Nelson, and thousands of Spanish sailors, and the severing of Spain's connection to its American colonies.

## Significance

The Treaty of San Ildefonso functions in the novel as the political original sin from which the tragedy of Trafalgar flows. Every Spanish character who reflects on the war identifies it as the fatal diplomatic error: Churruca foresees ruin in chapter 4, Malespina narrates the chain of consequences in chapter 6, and Marcial curses it on the deck of the *Santísima Trinidad* the night before battle. The treaty epitomizes the broader theme of Spain as a victim of forces beyond its control — manipulated by Napoleon's strategic cunning, betrayed by the weakness of its own government, and dragged into a catastrophe that its naval officers foresaw but could not prevent.
