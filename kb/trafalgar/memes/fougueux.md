# Fougueux

A French 74-gun ship of the line that fought alongside the Spanish *Santa Ana* against overwhelming British forces, was captured, and perished in the storm after the battle.

---

## Overview

The *Fougueux* was a French Téméraire-class 74-gun ship of the line, launched in 1785 and thus a veteran of over two decades of service by the time of Trafalgar. Commanded by Captain Louis-Alexis Baudouin, she was assigned to the rear (southern) division of the combined Franco-Spanish line. The *Fougueux* is noted in the novel for her stalwart support of the Spanish flagship *Santa Ana* — positioned beside the 112-gun three-decker as the two ships fought Collingwood's *Royal Sovereign* and four other British vessels.

The *Fougueux*'s actions at Trafalgar are recorded only in passing — the novel's narrator, aboard the *Santísima Trinidad*, hears of her role secondhand — but her presence is significant. She was one of the French 74s that stood with the Spanish rear division and absorbed the full weight of Collingwood's lee column. Though captured, she would not survive the storm that followed the battle.

## Design and Armament

The *Fougueux* was a standard French 74-gun two-decker of the Téméraire class, the most numerous and successful ship-of-the-line design of the French navy during the Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars. She carried 28 x 36-pounder long guns on her lower deck, 30 x 18-pounders on her upper deck, and 16 x 8-pounders plus carronades on her quarterdeck and forecastle. Her crew numbered approximately 600 men.

Launched in 1785, the *Fougueux* was among the older French 74s in the combined fleet at Trafalgar — nearly two decades older than many of her Téméraire-class sisters present at the battle. Despite her age, she remained a capable fighting ship, and her presence in the rear division testifies to the French navy's continued reliance on the 74-gun two-decker as the backbone of the battle line.

## At Trafalgar

The *Fougueux* was stationed in the rear (southern) division of the combined fleet's battle line. When Collingwood's lee column struck the rear of the Franco-Spanish line, the *Fougueux* found herself beside the Spanish 112-gun *Santa Ana*, the flagship of Vice-Admiral Álava.

In the novel, this pairing is described in a single sentence rich with implication: "The *Santa Ana*, only supported by the *Fougueux*, a Frenchman, had to fight the *Royal Sovereign* and four other English ships" (013-009). The phrase "only supported by" suggests that the *Fougueux* was the *Santa Ana*'s sole companion against odds of five to two — Collingwood's flagship and four British ships pouring fire into the two vessels. The same passage testifies to the ferocity of the action: "in spite of their unequal strength one side suffered as much as the other, for Collingwood's ship was the first to retire and the *Euryalus* took her place" (013-009).

The course of the engagement saw both the *Santa Ana* and the *Fougueux* fight for hours — the novel records that the two great ships "whose masts were almost entangled, fired into each other for six hours" (013-009). Captain Baudouin was killed in the action, and the *Fougueux*, battered and dismasted, eventually struck her colors. The British took possession of her.

## Aftermath

The *Fougueux* was captured but not saved. The great storm that rose on the night of 21 October 1805 — the storm that doomed so many prizes, including the *Santísima Trinidad* — proved fatal to the *Fougueux* as well. Severely damaged from the six-hour pounding she had endured, and unable to make headway under prize crew, she foundered in the gale. Most of her surviving crew and the British prize crew aboard her were lost.

The *Fougueux* thus shares the fate of several captured Trafalgar prizes: won in battle, lost to the sea. Her story, though brief in the novel's telling, captures the essential tragedy of Trafalgar for the French navy — ships fought bravely, were taken, and then were claimed not by the enemy but by the implacable Atlantic.
