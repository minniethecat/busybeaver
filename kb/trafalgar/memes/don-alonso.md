# Don Alonso

The retired Spanish naval officer whose decision to rejoin the fleet for the Trafalgar campaign sets the novel's plot in motion, embodying the values of honor, duty, and personal sacrifice that define the old Spanish naval tradition.

## Identity

Don Alonso de Guzmán (spelled "Don Alonzo" in some editions) is a retired captain of the Spanish navy, a gentleman of advancing years who has served his country at sea for decades and now lives in comfortable retirement in Cádiz. He is the master of the young Gabriel, who serves in his household, and the husband of Doña Francisca, whose fierce opposition to his rejoining the fleet provides the novel's central domestic conflict.

Don Alonso represents a type of Spanish officer increasingly rare by 1805: the gentleman-amateur, brave and honorable but not necessarily expert in the new science of naval warfare. He is competent rather than brilliant, dutiful rather than ambitious. His decision to return to sea is driven not by careerism — he is retired, with nothing to gain — but by a conviction that his country needs him and that a gentleman does not sit at home while others fight.

His physical description emphasizes his age and dignity: he is not a young man, and his body bears the marks of previous service. His "trembling fingers" (006-003) tracing lines on navigational charts suggest both the precision of the trained officer and the physical toll of age and perhaps illness.

## Role in the Narrative

Don Alonso's most consequential act occurs before the novel's main action: his decision to "join the fleet" (002-016). This choice, made against his wife's desperate objections, brings Gabriel into the naval world and sets the entire plot in motion. His rationale is simple and absolute: "it is my duty" — a phrase that reverberates through the novel as the moral foundation of naval service.

During the battle preparations and the battle itself, Don Alonso provides the officer's perspective that complements Marcial's seaman's view. He explains naval tactics to Gabriel, interprets the admiral's signals, and maintains a calm dignity even as the situation deteriorates. His relationship with Marcial is one of the novel's warmest elements — the retired officer and the old sailor, separated by class but united by shared service and mutual respect.

Don Alonso also serves as a moral anchor. In a novel that depicts the chaos and horror of battle, his steady adherence to duty — not flamboyant heroism, but simple, unwavering commitment — represents the virtues that the Spanish navy, even in defeat, can claim as its own.
