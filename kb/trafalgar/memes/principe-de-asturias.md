# Principe de Asturias

The Spanish first-rate flagship of Admiral Gravina, commanding the reserve squadron at Trafalgar, whose shattered decks and dismasted hull became the symbol of dignified defeat when the signal to retreat was hoisted from her wreckage.

---

## Overview

The *Principe de Asturias* (Prince of Asturias) was a Spanish 112-gun first-rate ship of the line, launched in 1794 and among the most powerful warships in the Armada Espanola at the time of Trafalgar. As the flagship of Admiral Federico Gravina, she led the reserve squadron of twelve ships -- the strongest division of the combined fleet (009-024).

At Trafalgar, the *Principe de Asturias* fought at the center of the Spanish line and endured concentrated fire from multiple British ships. By the time resistance became hopeless, she was "with all her tackle cut, her masts overboard and her sides riddled with balls" (013-015). Both Gravina and his second-in-command, Antonio Escano, were wounded aboard her. The image of Gravina hoisting the signal to retire "on the stump of a mast" (013-015) is one of the novel's most poignant evocations of defeated courage.

Unlike many Spanish flagships at Trafalgar, the *Principe de Asturias* survived the battle and made it back to Cadiz, bringing her wounded admiral home to die five months later (016-025).

## Design and Armament

The *Principe de Asturias* was a first-rate three-decker, heavily armed with a mixed battery typical of Spanish capital ships of the period. Marcial, reciting his "patriotic list" to Don Alonso, rattles off her complement of 118 guns alongside the *Trinidad*, *Santa Ana*, and *Rayo* (006-027). Historical records give her nominal armament as 112 guns, which likely varied in practice depending on the campaign.

Her great size made her a formidable platform for close action, but also a conspicuous and slow-moving target. Marcial himself, watching the fleet depart Cadiz, points to her and says: "What a lump that Don Federico is!" (009-020) -- a remark that captures both the ship's bulk and the affection with which the old sailor regarded Gravina's command.

The *Principe de Asturias* carried a complement of over 1,000 officers and men. Her officers at Trafalgar included the fleet's most senior Spanish commanders: Gravina himself and Rear-Admiral Antonio Escano, the major-general of the Spanish navy (013-015).

## At Trafalgar

The *Principe de Asturias* sailed from Cadiz on 20 October 1805 as the flagship of the reserve squadron, the strongest of the four divisions into which Villeneuve organized the combined fleet (009-024). Gravina commanded twelve ships, positioned at the rear of the line -- though the reversal of the fleet's course before battle meant the reserve ended up at the front of the formation.

When the British attack began, the *Principe de Asturias* was heavily engaged. She "stood a terrific fire from the *Defiance* and the *Revenge*" (013-015). The French ship *Neptune* came to her aid with the *San Ildefonso* and *San Justo*, but British reinforcements -- the *Dreadnought*, *Thunderer*, and *Polyphemus* -- made resistance hopeless (013-015).

The flagship was reduced to a floating wreck: "with all her tackle cut, her masts overboard and her sides riddled with balls" (013-015). Both Gravina and Escano were wounded, and with the battle irretrievably lost, "Gravina hoisted the signal to retire on the stump of a mast" (013-015). This signal -- sent from the broken spar of a dismasted first-rate -- became one of the defining images of Trafalgar from the Spanish perspective. Other Spanish ships, including the dying *San Juan Nepomuceno*, attempted to follow her lead but could not: the *Nepomuceno* "was mortally wounded and could not move nor steer" (013-029).

The *Principe de Asturias* sailed off for Cadiz, followed by the *San Justo*, *San Leandro*, *Montanes*, and three others (013-015). The retreat was not a rout: the *Nepomuceno*'s surviving officers made "an attempt to follow the *Principe de Asturias* which had given the signal to retreat" (013-029), recognizing her as the rallying point for what remained of the Spanish squadron.

## Aftermath

The *Principe de Asturias* reached Cadiz safely, one of the few Spanish capital ships to survive both the battle and the storm that followed (016-025). Gravina, mortally wounded, was carried ashore. He died in March 1806, five months after the battle, leaving behind a reputation for courage and dignity preserved even in catastrophic defeat.

The ship's survival -- battered, dismasted, but afloat -- reflected the condition of the Spanish navy itself after Trafalgar: broken, but not annihilated. Gravina's retreat preserved what remained of the fleet, and his flagship became a symbol of honorable resistance against impossible odds.
