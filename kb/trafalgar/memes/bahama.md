# Bahama

A Spanish 74-gun ship of the line that fought under Brigadier Dionisio Alcalá Galiano at Trafalgar, one of the hardest-fighting vessels in the Combined Fleet's rear, captured by the British after her commander fell in action.

---

## Overview

The *Bahama* was a Spanish 74-gun ship of the line that formed part of the Combined Franco-Spanish Fleet at the Battle of Trafalgar. She was commanded by Brigadier [[Dionisio Alcalá Galiano]], a distinguished naval officer and cartographer known for his scientific expeditions before the war. At Trafalgar, the *Bahama* was positioned in the rear of the Combined Fleet's line — a position that, after Villeneuve's ill-advised reversal of course, left the reserve ships trailing and exposed.

The novel singles out the *Bahama* as one of the ships that fought hardest and longest: "I was not in the *Rayo* but in the *Bahama*, one of the ships that fought hardest and longest" (016-007). She was eventually captured by the British after Galiano was killed at his post on the quarterdeck, and foundered while under tow to Gibraltar (016-025).

## Design and Armament

The *Bahama* was a two-decked ship of the line carrying 74 guns, the standard armament for the workhorse capital ships of the Napoleonic era. Her broadside was arranged across two gun decks, and like all Spanish ships of the line, she was built for durability and heavy firepower, though the Spanish fleet at Trafalgar suffered from poorly maintained arsenals and crews of uneven quality.

Marcial's remarks before the battle note that the Spanish line was spread thin and that ships in the rear, including the *Bahama*, would struggle to support the centre when the British attack came: "how can the *San Juan* and the *Bahama* come up to support us from the rear — or the *Neptuno* and the *Rayo* which are in front" (010-005). This observation highlights both the tactical vulnerability of the Combined Fleet's formation and the *Bahama*'s position in the rear division.

## At Trafalgar

The *Bahama* was one of the reserve ships that, after the Combined Fleet reversed course, found itself at the rear of the line — "the reserve ships, which as I heard said, were the best, were hindmost of all" (010-004). When the British attacked in two columns, the *Bahama*, positioned too far from the centre to provide effective support, was forced to fight a largely isolated action.

Galiano prepared his crew with characteristic resolve. At noon he reviewed the crew, walked the gun-decks, and addressed them: "Gentlemen, you all know that our flag is nailed to the mast" (016-012). He then turned to the captain of marines, Don Alonso Butron, and declared: "No Galiano ever surrenders and no Butron should either" (016-012). These words, reported after the battle by a surviving crewman, capture the determination aboard the *Bahama* before the British attack.

The action was fierce from the outset. "The ships riddled us with broadsides to port and starboard. The wounded fell like flies from the very first" (016-014). Galiano was first struck by a splinter to the head but refused to leave the quarterdeck, continuing to direct his guns as if "we had been firing a salute at a review" (016-014). A spent ball knocked his telescope from his hand, making him laugh. But shortly afterwards, a cannonball struck him in the head, killing him instantly (016-014).

Galiano's death broke the spirit of the *Bahama*'s crew. As the survivor recounted: "The fight was not at an end, but all our heart in it was gone. When our beloved captain fell the officers covered his body that we men might not see it, but we all knew at once what had happened, and after a short and desperate struggle for the honor of our flag, the *Bahama* surrendered to the English" (016-015).

The British took the *Bahama* as a prize and attempted to tow her to Gibraltar, but she never reached port: "The *Bahama* had surrendered but went to pieces before she could be got into Gibraltar" (016-025). She foundered at sea with her dead commander aboard.

## Aftermath

After the *Bahama* surrendered, her surviving crew were transferred to other vessels. One crewman was taken aboard the *Santa Ana* and later made his way to shore, where he recounted the story of Galiano's death to the novel's narrator (016-016). The loss of the *Bahama*, along with the *San Juan Nepomuceno* and other Spanish ships, was a severe blow to what remained of the Spanish fleet.
