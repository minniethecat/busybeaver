# Redoutable

The French ship whose close-quarters duel with HMS Victory produced the most consequential single shot of the Trafalgar battle — the musket ball from her fighting tops that struck down Nelson.

---

## Overview

The *Redoutable* was a French 74-gun ship of the line, launched in 1791 and commanded at Trafalgar by Captain Jean-Jacques Lucas. Positioned in the center of the combined fleet's line, she found herself directly in the path of Nelson's *Victory* as the British weather column pierced the Franco-Spanish formation. Her engagement with the British flagship, at pistol-shot range, became one of the battle's defining encounters.

The *Redoutable*'s crew fought with exceptional determination. When the *Victory* opened fire on her, the French ship not only returned the broadside but repulsed the initial British assault — "The *Victory* fired first on the *Redoutable*, and being repulsed, came up to the windward of the *Trinidad*" (011-002). It was during this close action that a French marksman in the *Redoutable*'s fighting tops fired the shot that mortally wounded Nelson, striking him through the chest and lodging in his spine (012-030).

## Design and Armament

The *Redoutable* was a typical French 74-gun two-decker, the workhorse design of late-eighteenth-century naval warfare. She carried 28 x 36-pounders on her lower gundeck, 30 x 18-pounders on her upper gundeck, and 16 lighter guns (8-pounders and carronades) on her quarterdeck and forecastle. Her crew numbered approximately 650 men.

French 74s of this period were generally well-regarded for their sailing qualities, with designs favoring speed and maneuverability over the heavier scantlings of their British counterparts. The *Redoutable*'s relatively light construction, however, proved a vulnerability under sustained close-range fire from the 104-gun *Victory*.

## At Trafalgar

The *Redoutable* occupied a position in the center of the combined fleet's line, near the *Bucentaure* and the *Santísima Trinidad*. When Nelson's weather column struck, the *Victory* singled her out as its first target. The *Redoutable* absorbed the British flagship's opening broadside and returned fire with enough effect to repel the initial approach — the *Victory* was forced to shift target toward the *Trinidad* (011-002).

The duel resumed at close quarters, the ships so near that French marksmen in the *Redoutable*'s fighting tops could pick out individual figures on the *Victory*'s quarterdeck. From this elevated position, a musket ball struck Nelson as he walked the deck with Captain Hardy. The shot "pierced his chest and lodged in the spine" (012-030), and though he lingered for several hours, directing the battle to its conclusion, the wound proved fatal.

Despite inflicting this devastating loss on the British, the *Redoutable* could not withstand the *Victory*'s superior broadside weight. The French ship suffered enormous casualties and was battered into submission. Captain Lucas ultimately surrendered, and the *Redoutable* was taken as a British prize.

## Aftermath

The *Redoutable* did not survive the storm that followed Trafalgar. Already shattered by the *Victory*'s guns, she foundered in the heavy seas of the night of October 21, taking many of her surviving crew with her. Her brief but fierce resistance, and the singular consequence of her marksman's shot, secured her a lasting place in the history of the battle.
