# Rafael Malespina

Don Rafael Malespina was a Spanish artillery officer stationed in Cádiz, belonging to an old Mediterranean family, whose courtship of the young Rosario and eventual departure for the fleet form a significant subplot in the early chapters of *Trafalgar*.

---

## Role in the Narrative

Malespina enters the story as "an acting officer of artillery named Malespina, belonging to a family of Mediterranean origin" (005-002) who becomes a regular visitor to the household where the young narrator serves. He is described as "a fine-looking young fellow of very distinguished manners" (005-012), and his social standing makes him an attractive suitor — a fact that torments the adolescent Gabriel, who is himself enamored of Rosario.

The narrator's jealousy is immediate and visceral: "I need hardly add that I hated Malespina; I no sooner saw him come into the house than a burning flame of wrath seemed to scorch my soul" (005-016). Malespina's visits become a source of misery — when he fails to appear one day, the narrator notes his absence with hope, but the respite is brief. The old family retainer describes Malespina's father as "a very tall, dry, wooden-looking old gentleman" (005-014), a man of formal and imposing bearing.

The subplot reaches its crisis when Malespina arrives to announce his departure: "I have come to take leave of you" (006-011). He has been ordered to join the fleet as it prepares to sail, and though he is an artillery officer, "his battery was on board the *Trinidad*" (006-012) — the great Spanish flagship. The announcement stuns the assembled company, and his parting words on the eve of battle carry the weight of fatal premonition. In his final appearance, Malespina voices a rare political opinion:

> "I must admit," said Malespina, "that our alliance with France has been fatal to us." (006-030)

This sentiment echoes the broader Spanish discontent with Napoleon that pervades the novel.

## Historical Context

Artillery officers in the Spanish army of 1805 were drawn disproportionately from the nobility and the educated middle class — the artillery required mathematical training that set its officers apart from the infantry and cavalry. Malespina's Mediterranean family background and his status as "a man of birth and breeding" (005-013) are consistent with the corps' social profile. During the Trafalgar campaign, Spanish artillerymen served both ashore in the Cádiz fortifications and afloat on the warships, where their expertise with cannons was essential to manning the naval batteries.

The name Malespina evokes the noble Italian-Spanish Malaspina family, known for producing explorers and naval officers — Alessandro Malaspina's Pacific expedition of 1789–1794 was one of the most ambitious scientific voyages of the Spanish Enlightenment. While the novel's Malespina is a fictional character, his surname carries these historical resonances.
