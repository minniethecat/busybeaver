# San Agustín

A Spanish 74-gun ship of the line that fought at Trafalgar under Captain Don Felipe Cagigal, engaged in the outer ring of the battle around the surrounded *Trinidad* before being captured and ultimately claimed by the storm.

---

## Overview

The *San Agustín* was a Spanish 74-gun third-rate ship of the line, one of the fifteen Spanish vessels that formed part of the combined Franco-Spanish fleet at Trafalgar. Her commander at the battle was Captain Don Felipe Cagigal. The ship had seen earlier service in the Napoleonic Wars, including the ill-fated night action off Algeciras in 1801, where she sailed as part of a squadron escorting French ships to Cádiz (004-012).

At Trafalgar, the *San Agustín* fought in the center of the combined fleet's line, where she was one of the ships engaged at somewhat greater distance from the concentrated British assault that overwhelmed the *Trinidad* and *Bucentaure*. She was captured during the battle and subsequently went down in the storm that raged on the night of October 21-22, 1805.

## Design and Armament

The *San Agustín* was a 74-gun two-decker, the standard configuration for line-of-battle ships of the period. She carried a main battery of 24-pounder and 36-pounder cannon across two gundecks, with lighter guns on her quarterdeck and forecastle. Her crew numbered approximately 600-700 men, typical for a Spanish 74 of the era.

As a third-rate, the *San Agustín* represented the workhorse of fleet actions — powerful enough to stand in the line of battle, yet more maneuverable and economical to operate than the massive first-rates like the *Santísima Trinidad*. Spanish 74s were generally well-built ships, often constructed at the Havana dockyard from tropical hardwoods that made them notably resilient in combat.

## At Trafalgar

During the battle, the *San Agustín* was one of the ships that found itself in the outer ring of the engagement as the British columns struck the combined fleet's center. The narrator Gabriel, observing from the deck of the *Santísima Trinidad*, notes that "the *Agustín*, the *Héros*, and the *Leandro* were engaged at some distance from us where they had rather more sea-room" (011-007). This description places the *San Agustín* away from the most concentrated British assault, though still under fire and actively fighting.

The ship was captured by the British during the battle along with many others in the combined fleet. The novel's summary account confirms her fate: "the *San Augustín*, 80 guns, Don Felipe Cagigal, had been sunk" (016-025). The slight discrepancy between the reported 80 guns here and her likely 74-gun rating is consistent with the novel's occasional loose handling of armament figures.

In the aftermath, the survivors of the *Trinidad* — including Gabriel, Don Alonso, and Marcial — briefly mistook the captured *Santa Ana* for the *San Agustín* (013-007). The confusion underscores the chaos following the battle: "The *San Agustín* was sunk," Don Alonso corrects Marcial, confirming that her loss was already known to the Spanish officers (013-008).

## Earlier Service

Before Trafalgar, the *San Agustín* participated in the 1801 squadron that sailed from Algeciras to Cádiz to escort French vessels. Marcial recounts this operation in vivid detail, describing how six Spanish ships of the line including the *San Agustín*, along with the frigate *Sabina*, joined a French squadron in a night passage that ended disastrously when the British intercepted them, leading to the tragic nighttime collision between the *Real Cárlos* and *San Hermenegildo* (004-012).
