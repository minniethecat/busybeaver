# Intrépide

A French 74-gun ship of the line that fought in Dumanoir's rearguard division at Trafalgar, engaged in one of the most fiercely contested close actions of the battle before her capture.

---

## Overview

The *Intrépide* was a French Téméraire-class 74-gun ship of the line, launched in 1800 and commissioned into the French Mediterranean fleet. At Trafalgar, she was commanded by Captain Louis-Antoine-Cyprien Infernet and assigned to the rear division under Rear-Admiral Pierre Dumanoir — the group of French ships that Marcial in the novel describes as under "Dumanoir" (010-006), and whose dispositions formed the southern end of the combined Franco-Spanish line.

The *Intrépide* is not individually named in the novel, which is told from a Spanish perspective aboard the *Santísima Trinidad* at the center of the battle. However, the rearguard division of which she was part is described in some detail: the fleet was "divided into four lines" with "the rear, again of six, is under Dumanoir" (010-006). The narrator notes that "the line was so long the head could never help the tail" (016-026), a tactical reality that left Dumanoir's ships isolated and unable to come to the aid of the collapsing center.

## Design and Armament

The *Intrépide* belonged to the Téméraire class, the standard 74-gun design that formed the backbone of the French navy during the Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars. As a two-decker, she mounted 28 x 36-pounder long guns on her lower deck and 30 x 18-pounders on her upper deck, with 16 x 8-pounders and carronades on her quarterdeck and forecastle. Her crew numbered approximately 600-700 men.

The Téméraire class represented the mature form of the 74-gun ship of the line — ships prized for their combination of a powerful broadside with adequate sailing qualities and the ability to operate both in the line of battle and in independent cruising roles. By the time of Trafalgar, the class had proven itself in numerous engagements.

## At Trafalgar

The *Intrépide* was stationed in Dumanoir's rearguard, which the narrator notes as one of the "four lines" into which Villeneuve had divided the fleet (010-006). The tactical situation of the rearguard was perilous from the outset: as Marcial observes, "how can the *San Juan* and the *Bahama* come up to support us from the rear — or the *Neptuno* and the *Rayo* which are in front" (010-020), a complaint that captures how the length of the Franco-Spanish line prevented mutual support between divisions.

When Nelson's columns struck the center and rear, Dumanoir's division found itself cut off from the main body of the fleet. The narrator later remarks that "Nelson, who was certainly no fool, looked along our line, and he said: 'If we cut them through at two separate points, and keep them between two fires, hardly a ship will escape me'" (016-026). The rearguard was among the first to feel the effects of this strategy: "he fought us in detachments, attacking us in two wedge-shaped columns" (016-026).

While four French ships under Dumanoir — the *Duguay-Trouin*, *Mont Blanc*, *Scipion*, and *Formidable* — ultimately "kept out of the action" (012-006) and withdrew, the *Intrépide* and her consorts engaged the British fleet directly. The *Intrépide* fought a close-range duel with several British ships, sustaining heavy damage before being compelled to strike her colors.

## Aftermath

The *Intrépide* was taken as a prize by the British following her surrender. She was among the French vessels captured at Trafalgar that were subsequently destroyed — burned rather than taken into British service, as the storm that followed the battle had left her too damaged to be salvaged.

The narrator's summary of the French losses notes that "with the exception of the four ships that withdrew under Dumanoir without showing fight — a stain which the Imperial navy could not for a long time wipe out — our allies behaved splendidly" (016-028). The *Intrépide*, which did fight and was lost as a consequence, belongs to the latter category.
