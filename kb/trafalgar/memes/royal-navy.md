# Royal Navy

The world's dominant naval force at the time of Trafalgar, whose ships, crews, and commanders — above all Horatio Nelson — set the standard against which all other navies were measured and found wanting.

---

## Overview

The Royal Navy of 1805 was the most formidable fighting force afloat. Years of almost continuous warfare since 1793 had forged a corps of officers and seamen with unparalleled combat experience. British gun crews, constantly at sea on blockade duty, could load and fire two or even three broadsides to an enemy's one. This tactical advantage, combined with Nelson's inspired leadership, made the British fleet at Trafalgar virtually unbeatable.

The novel portrays the Royal Navy as seen from the Spanish decks — an object of professional admiration and personal dread. Marcial's nickname for Nelson, "El Señorito" (the Little Lord) (004-006), captures the Spanish sailors' complex attitude: respect for British seamanship, resentment of British dominance, and an almost superstitious awe of the enemy commander.

## In the Novel

The Royal Navy is the constant, looming presence against which the Spanish characters define themselves. It is "the English" who blockade Cádiz, the "Señorito" (Nelson) who commands the enemy fleet, the "united squadrons" (004-035) that Marcial grimly acknowledges will "remain in Cádiz" rather than face British guns. The narrator's description of Nelson's two columns advancing "in all their dreadful majesty" captures the Spanish perspective on British naval power: formidable, almost inhumanly efficient, and ultimately irresistible.

The novel's willingness to admire the enemy — "our noble, heroic, incomparable Nelson" (012-028) — is one of its most distinctive features, and it reflects the genuine respect that Spanish naval professionals felt for the Royal Navy even in defeat.
