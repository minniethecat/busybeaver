# Horatio Nelson

The British vice-admiral who led the attack at Trafalgar, a commander of extraordinary tactical brilliance and personal magnetism whose death at the moment of his greatest victory made him immortal.

---

## Identity

Horatio Nelson (1758–1805) was the preeminent naval commander of his age. By the time of Trafalgar, he had already lost an eye at Calvi and an arm at Santa Cruz de Tenerife — his body was a map of previous battles, and his physical frailty belied his indomitable will. He was loved by his crews with an intensity rare in any service, and his tactical innovations — particularly the perpendicular attack in two columns at Trafalgar — had transformed naval warfare.

Nelson's genius lay in combining tactical originality with inspirational leadership. He trusted his captains to exercise initiative (his famous "band of brothers"), and they repaid that trust with aggressive execution. His pre-battle memorandum for Trafalgar explicitly minimized reliance on signals: "he gave no signals but expected every captain to use his own judgment" (009-025). This was radical — and it worked.

His death at Trafalgar, struck by a musket ball fired from the French *Redoutable* as he walked his own quarterdeck, completed the Nelson legend. He died knowing the battle was won, his last words reportedly "Thank God I have done my duty." The Spanish narrator's description of "our noble, heroic, incomparable Nelson" (012-028) captures the remarkable fact that even his enemies admired him.

## Role in the Narrative

Nelson appears in the novel as a figure of almost supernatural stature, seen through the eyes of the Spanish characters. Marcial gives him the nickname "El Señorito" (the Little Lord) — "Nelson he called the Señorito, implying something more than a mere mortal" (004-006). This nickname captures the Spanish sailors' complex attitude toward their enemy: respect bordering on awe, resentment without hatred.

His tactical decisions shape the battle: the two-column attack, the concentration on the enemy center, the refusal to issue detailed signals. The Spanish characters observe these decisions with a mixture of professional admiration and personal dread, understanding that they face a commander whose methods they cannot counter.

Nelson's death, reported after the battle, produces a complex reaction in the Spanish narrator. Victory and defeat blur; what remains is the recognition of greatness, irrespective of nationality — "our noble, heroic, incomparable Nelson" (012-028).
