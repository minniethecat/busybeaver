# Patriotism

The love of country that impelled men to fight, and sometimes to die, for Spain in the naval wars of the Napoleonic era — a force both unifying and complicated by the divided loyalties of a nation at war with itself over its relationship to Revolutionary France.

---

## Definition

Patriotism in the context of Napoleonic-era Spain was a layered and sometimes contradictory sentiment. For naval officers, it meant loyalty to the Crown and the Spanish nation; for common seamen, it often meant loyalty to their ship, their comrades, and their immediate commander as much as to any abstract national idea. The novel explores patriotism not as a simple flag-waving emotion but as a complex of feelings: pride in Spanish naval traditions, resentment of British dominance, frustration with French alliance, and a deep attachment to specific places — Cádiz, the Andalusian coast, the familiar waters of home.

The narrator, looking back from old age, recalls the patriotism of his youth with both affection and irony, understanding that the cause for which he fought was, in strategic terms, profoundly misguided even as the courage it inspired was genuine.

## Historical Context

Spanish patriotism in 1805 was in crisis. The alliance with Napoleonic France was deeply unpopular among many Spaniards, who saw it as subordination to a revolutionary power that had overthrown its own monarchy. The naval war against Britain had been disastrous for Spain — the defeat at Cape St. Vincent in 1797, followed by Trafalgar in 1805, effectively destroyed Spanish sea power. Patriotic rhetoric, in this context, served partly to sustain morale in the face of strategic futility.

Yet patriotism also had a genuine popular basis. The Spanish resistance to Napoleon that would erupt in 1808 — the Dos de Mayo uprising and the guerrilla war — drew on the same reservoir of national feeling that animated the sailors at Trafalgar. The novel, written decades later by Galdós as part of his *Episodios Nacionales*, reflects this retrospective understanding: Trafalgar was a defeat, but the spirit of those who fought it foreshadowed the national resistance to come.

## Role in the Novel

Patriotism surfaces as both explicit declaration and implicit motivation. Characters speak of their duty to Spain in formal terms — "it is my duty to join the fleet" (002-016) — but the novel also shows patriotism operating through smaller, more personal channels: pride in one's ship, loyalty to one's commander, the refusal to be seen as less courageous than the enemy. When Spanish ships "held out bravely against these odds till two in the afternoon" (013-023), patriotism is the invisible force that keeps men at their guns.

The novel complicates simple patriotism by showing the humanity of the enemy. Nelson is described in terms of genuine admiration — "our noble, heroic, incomparable Nelson" (012-028) — and British courage is acknowledged without resentment. This is patriotism without chauvinism: love of country that does not require hatred of the Other.

## Significance

Patriotism in *Trafalgar* is the emotional infrastructure of the naval world. It is what converts strategic necessity into individual motivation, and what allows men to find meaning in defeat as well as victory. The novel's treatment of patriotism — nuanced, historically informed, free of jingoism — reflects Galdós's larger project of understanding Spanish national identity through its military history.
