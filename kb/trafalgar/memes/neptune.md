# Neptune

The French *Neptune* was an 80-gun ship of the line that fought at Trafalgar and was one of the few French vessels to survive the battle, only to be captured months later in the Caribbean.

---

## Overview

The *Neptune* was a French 80-gun two-decker of the *Bucentaure* class, launched in 1803 as part of Napoleon's ambitious naval reconstruction program. At Trafalgar, she served under Captain Esprit-Tranquille Maistral in the rear division of the combined fleet under Rear-Admiral Pierre Dumanoir le Pelley. While most of the French center and rear were overwhelmed by the British attack, the *Neptune* was among the handful of French ships that managed to disengage and escape the battle.

Her survival at Trafalgar, however, was only a temporary reprieve. In February 1806, while attempting to return to France from the West Indies, she was captured by a British squadron at the Battle of San Domingo — the last major naval engagement of the Trafalgar campaign.

## Design and Armament

The *Neptune* was a *Bucentaure*-class 80-gun ship of the line, designed by Jacques-Noel Sane, the most prolific French naval architect of the era. The class represented the standard French two-decker design of the Napoleonic period, carrying 30 x 36-pounder long guns on the lower deck and 32 x 24-pounders on the upper deck, with additional 18-pounders and carronades on the quarterdeck and forecastle. Her crew complement numbered approximately 700 officers and men.

Like her sister ship the *Bucentaure*, the *Neptune* was a well-balanced design — powerful enough to stand in the line of battle but more maneuverable than the larger three-deckers such as the Spanish [[Santísima Trinidad]]. The Sane 80-gun design was widely regarded as one of the finest two-decker types of its era, and the British later incorporated captured examples into the Royal Navy with minimal modification.

## At Trafalgar

The *Neptune* was stationed in the rear of the combined fleet's line, part of Dumanoir's division. When Nelson's two columns struck the center and rear, the rear division found itself detached from the main action. The *Neptune* had not been as heavily engaged as the ships in the center and was in a position to come to the aid of other vessels still fighting.

As the battle turned decisively against the combined fleet, the *Neptune* rendered assistance to Admiral Gravina's flagship, the *Principe de Asturias*, which was under heavy fire from the *Defiance* and the *Revenge*. The *Neptune*, described in the Spanish account as "a Frenchman, came to her assistance with the *San Ildefonso* and the *San Justo*" (013-015). However, the arrival of British reinforcements — the *Dreadnought*, the *Thunderer*, and the *Polyphemus* — made further resistance futile.

When Gravina hoisted the signal to retire and set course for Cadiz, the *Neptune* was among the ships that disengaged and followed. She was one of only a handful of French ships of the line to escape the battle without being captured or destroyed.

## Aftermath

The *Neptune* reached Cadiz safely after Trafalgar, but her career did not end there. In early 1806, she sailed for the West Indies as part of a French squadron. On 6 February 1806, at the Battle of San Domingo, a British squadron under Vice-Admiral Sir John Duckworth intercepted and engaged the French force. The *Neptune* was captured in the action, ending her service in the French navy.
