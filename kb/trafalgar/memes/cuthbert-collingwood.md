# Cuthbert Collingwood

Nelson's trusted second-in-command, whose ship the *Royal Sovereign* was the first to engage the combined fleet at Trafalgar, setting the example of aggressive attack that defined the British battle plan.

---

## Identity

Cuthbert Collingwood (1748–1810) was Nelson's closest friend and most reliable subordinate. They had served together for decades, and Nelson trusted Collingwood absolutely — when the two-column attack plan was devised, Nelson took command of the weather column and gave Collingwood the leeward column, an assignment that required both tactical skill and the confidence to act independently.

Collingwood's *Royal Sovereign* was the first British ship to engage at Trafalgar, breaking into the enemy line ahead of Nelson's *Victory*. Collingwood had had his ship's hull freshly painted and coppered before the battle, making it the fastest sailer in the fleet — and the first into action. After Nelson was shot, Collingwood assumed command of the fleet, directing the final stages of the battle and the subsequent storm operations with competence and humanity.

He was, by all accounts, a gentler man than Nelson — less flamboyant, less hungry for glory — but no less brave and perhaps a better administrator. His post-battle handling of the fleet and its prizes was exemplary, saving many lives that would otherwise have been lost in the storm that followed Trafalgar.

## Role in the Narrative

Collingwood appears in the novel as a presence at the edge of the action — the commander of the leeward division whose ships the Spanish characters glimpse through the smoke of battle. The "other line" that the narrator observes advancing toward the combined fleet (010-013) is Collingwood's column, and the Spanish sailors' awareness that they face not one but two coordinated attacks is part of the growing realization of their tactical predicament.
