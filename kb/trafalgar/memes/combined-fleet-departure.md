# Departure of the Combined Fleet

The departure of the combined Franco-Spanish fleet from Cádiz on October 19-20, 1805, was the final naval maneuver before the Battle of Trafalgar. The sailing was controversial, opposed unanimously by the Spanish commanders, but ordered by the French Admiral Villeneuve in a desperate bid to recover his reputation. Forty vessels -- thirty-two men-of-war, five frigates, and two brigantines -- cleared the bay over two days, steering southward toward their fateful encounter with Nelson's fleet.

---

## Overview

On October 19, 1805, the combined French and Spanish fleet under Admiral Pierre-Charles Villeneuve weighed anchor and sailed out of Cádiz bay. The departure had been ordered by Villeneuve against the unanimous advice of his Spanish counterparts, who favored remaining on the defensive within the bay (008-019). The fleet consisted of forty vessels of varying tonnage and proficiency, and it took several hours for all ships to clear the bay and form up at sea (009-019).

The fleet steered southward after nightfall on October 19, and by the afternoon of October 20 Villeneuve signaled for the ships to form five lines (010-001). By dawn on October 21, the fleet had sighted Nelson's thirty-three ships to windward, setting the stage for the decisive battle.

---

## Background

### The Council of War

On October 8, 1805, Villeneuve convened a council of war aboard the *Bucentaure* to discuss the fleet's course of action. The Spanish commanders -- including Vice-Admiral Álava, Rear-Admirals Escaño and Cisneros, Commodore Galiano, and Commodore Churruca -- attended alongside French Rear-Admirals Dumanoir and Magon (008-018).

Villeneuve expressed his desire to go out to sea and seek the English. The Spanish officers unanimously opposed this course:

> "Villeneuve having expressed his wish to go out to sea, we Spaniards unanimously opposed it. The discussion was warm and eager, and Alcalá Galiano and Magon exchanged such hard words that it must have come to a duel if we had not intervened to pacify them. Our opposition greatly annoyed Villeneuve, and in the heat of argument he even threw out certain insolent hints to which Gravina promptly retorted." (008-019)

Churruca argued that remaining in the bay would give them the advantage, forcing the English to maintain an unsustainable blockade. He cited the advanced season, the superiority of British armament and crews, and the inexperience of the combined fleet's men (008-019).

### Villeneuve's Desperation

Villeneuve's determination to sail was rooted in his increasingly precarious position. Napoleon had used harsh language toward him, and the threat of dismissal loomed. As Churruca explained to Don Alonso:

> "Villeneuve in fact is desperate; his sovereign has used harsh language to him, and the warning that he will be degraded from his command is prompting him to the maddest acts, in the hope of recovering his tarnished reputation, in a single day, by death or victory." (008-020)

The Spanish officers recognized they would be forced to obey despite their opposition. Churruca's grim assessment was that "we must go if Villeneuve orders it, but if the result is a disaster our opposition to his insane resolution stands on record as our acquittal" (008-020).

### Ships and Commanders

The combined fleet comprised both French and Spanish warships, with the Spanish contingent including some of the largest vessels afloat. The *Santísima Trinidad*, with its four decks of 140 guns, was the flagship of Rear-Admiral Baltasar Hidalgo de Cisneros and the largest warship in the world (009-002, 009-006). Other key vessels included the *Príncipe de Asturias* under Gravina, the *Bucentaure* under Villeneuve, the *Santa Ana* under Álava, and the *San Juan Nepomuceno* under Churruca (009-020).

## Events

### October 18: The Eve of Departure

On the evening of October 18, word spread through Cádiz and the fleet that the order to sail had been given. Don Alonso de Cisniega, aboard the *Santísima Trinidad*, spoke eagerly with Captain Uriarte and Rear-Admiral Cisneros about the impending departure (009-011). The narrator, a young cabin boy, overheard their conversation and realized that "the French admiral had ordered the fleets to put out to sea the next morning" (009-011).

In the forecastle, the veteran sailor Marcial held council with a knot of old sea-dogs, discoursing on the diplomacy and politics behind the war in a comical parody of a lecture he had overheard (009-013). All conversation on board turned toward the impending battle, and the mood among the veterans was one of grim anticipation.

### October 19: The Fleet Sails

The morning of October 19 dawned clear and beautiful:

> "The morning of the 19th dawned, the day I hailed so eagerly; indeed it had not yet dawned when I found myself at the stern of the vessel with my master, who wanted to look on at the working of the ship. After clearing the decks the business of starting the ship began. The huge topsails were hoisted, and the heavy windlass, turning with a shrill clatter, dragged the anchor up from the bottom of the bay." (009-015)

The scene was one of measured grandeur. The *Santísima Trinidad* moved out with a "slow and solemn advance which was only perceptible to those on board by watching the apparent motion of the merchantmen lying at anchor and the landscape beyond" (009-015).

Looking back, the narrator saw the full fleet taking shape:

> "And what a scene it was! Thirty-two men-of-war, five frigates, and two brigantines, Spanish and French together -- some in front, some behind, and some abreast of us -- were bursting into sail, as it were, and riding before the light breeze. I never saw a lovelier morning. The sun flooded those lovely shores with light; a faint purple tinge colored the sea to the east, and the chain of hills which bound the horizon on the side of the town seemed to be on fire in the sunrise." (009-016)

The ships did not all sail with equal speed; some got ahead while others lagged. Marcial commented on each vessel in turn, mocking the clumsy ones and encouraging the swift. He derided the *Rayo* ("which was the least manageable of all the fleet"), praised the *Santa Ana* under Álava, and scoffed at Dumanoir's French ship *Le Formidable* ("That Frenchman keeps a hairdresser to crimp the topsail") (009-020, 009-021).

### Cádiz Receding

As the fleet moved out, Cádiz unfolded like a panorama behind them. The narrator watched the white buildings and the morning bells ringing across the bay:

> "Now they seemed to me to ring gladly, and send good wishes after us -- I listened to them as if they were human voices bidding us Godspeed; then again they tolled sadly and dolefully -- a knell of misfortune; and as we sailed further and further away their music grew fainter till it was lost in space." (009-018)

By evening the sky clouded over, and Cádiz faded from view in the mist. The fleet steered southward into the gathering darkness (009-022).

### October 20: Formation and Advance

The following morning brought a stiff breeze, and the ships kept at some distance from each other. By noon the wind had moderated, and Villeneuve signaled for the fleet to form in five lines -- van, centre, rear, and two lines of reserve (010-001). The wind was from the southwest, and the fleet, catching the breeze on the starboard quarter, ran toward the Straits of Gibraltar.

During the night of October 20, a few lights were sighted, and by dawn on October 21, twenty-seven enemy ships were visible to windward, including three three-deckers. By eight o'clock all thirty-three of Nelson's vessels were in sight, forming two columns advancing in a wedge formation (010-001).

## Aftermath

The departure placed the combined fleet in a tactically disadvantageous position. Villeneuve ordered a wear of the fleet -- a complete reversal of course -- intended to keep Cádiz to leeward in case of disaster. This maneuver broke up the already imperfect line of battle, drawing fierce criticism from Marcial and other experienced hands aboard the *Trinidad* (010-002, 010-003). What had been the vanguard became the rear, and the best reserve ships were left hindmost. The line became "wider than the Milky Way," and the fleet found itself to leeward, allowing the English to "pick and choose where they will attack us" (010-005).

By noon on October 21, the decks had been cleared for action, sand spread to absorb blood, ammunition passed up from the storerooms, and the two British columns -- led by Nelson in the *Victory* and Collingwood in the *Royal Sovereign* -- closed in to deliver the battle that would define the age (010-009, 010-013).

On the 19th, Churruca had already foretold disaster. He told his brother-in-law Apodaca that "before going on board he had had a presentiment of the defeat of the allies, as he did our defeat" (013-018). His fatalism mirrored the broader Spanish conviction that the departure was a mistake forced upon them by French desperation.

---

## Significance

The departure of the combined fleet from Cádiz represents the final decision in a chain of command failures that led directly to Trafalgar. The Spanish commanders' unanimous opposition to sailing (008-019), Villeneuve's fragile mental state (008-020), and the structural weakness of the Franco-Spanish alliance all converged in this two-day operation.

In the novel, the departure serves as a dramatic turning point. Chapter 9, which narrates October 18-19, marks the transition from the political and domestic scenes of Cádiz to the epic scope of the battle itself. The narrator's childhood wonder at the *Santísima Trinidad* -- "that floating fortress of timber" (009-002) -- gives way to the sober reality of combat. The beauty of the fleet under sail, and the poignant image of Cádiz fading from view with its bells tolling "a knell of misfortune" (009-018), invests the departure with an elegiac quality that foreshadows the carnage to come.
