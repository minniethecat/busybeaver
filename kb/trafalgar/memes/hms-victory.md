# HMS Victory

Nelson's flagship at Trafalgar, the most famous warship in British naval history and the vessel from which the decisive attack of the battle was directed.

---

## Overview

HMS *Victory* was a 104-gun first-rate ship of the line, launched in 1765 and already forty years old by the time of Trafalgar. She had served as flagship in multiple campaigns and had been extensively refitted before Nelson hoisted his flag aboard her in 1803. At Trafalgar, she led the weather column of the British attack, with Nelson himself on her quarterdeck.

The novel depicts the *Victory* as seen from the Spanish decks — an object of awe and dread. Marcial's nickname for Nelson, "El Señorito" (the Little Lord), extends by implication to his flagship: the *Victory* is the vessel of the enemy commander, the ship "which was said to contain Nelson" (010-015), and therefore the symbolic heart of the British attack.

## Design and Armament

The *Victory* mounted 104 guns on three decks: 30 × 32-pounders on the lower gundeck, 28 × 24-pounders on the middle, and 30 × 12-pounders on the upper deck, plus carronades and quarterdeck guns. Her broadside weight was approximately 1,148 pounds — less than the *Trinidad*'s, but delivered with superior speed and accuracy by her highly trained crew.

Her construction was unusually robust, with oak hull planking up to two feet thick at the waterline. This heavy construction made her relatively slow — she was not the fastest ship in the fleet — but exceptionally resistant to enemy fire. At Trafalgar, she sustained heavy damage to her masts and rigging but her hull remained sound.

## At Trafalgar

The *Victory* led the weather column directly into the enemy line. The narrator observes her approach from the deck of the *Trinidad*: "At the head of the other line was the Victory, commanded by Nelson" (010-013). The Spanish sailors watch with a mixture of admiration and dread as the British flagship bears down on them.

The *Victory* passed under the *Trinidad*'s stern and delivered a raking broadside — "a broadside on my figurehead" (004-049) in one encounter — before engaging the French *Redoutable* at close range. It was during this engagement that Nelson was mortally wounded by a musket shot from the *Redoutable*'s fighting tops.

The *Victory* survived the battle, though heavily damaged, and returned to Gibraltar for repairs. Her commander had died, but his flagship had done what he asked of it — led the attack, broken the enemy line, and secured the victory.
