# Gabriel de Araceli

The young narrator of *Trafalgar*, a boy from the backstreets of Cádiz whose experience of the great naval battle transforms him from a child playing with toy ships into a man who has fired a cannon in earnest.

---

## Identity

Gabriel de Araceli — sometimes affectionately called "Gabrielillo" — is the first-person narrator of *Trafalgar*. Born in the "notorious quarter De la Viña" in Cádiz (001-003), he enters the story as a boy of about fourteen, an orphan or semi-orphan raised in the household of the retired naval officer Don Alonso. His name, de Araceli, suggests a connection to the Virgin of Araceli, a patron of sailors — fitting for a boy whose destiny lies at sea.

Gabriel belongs to the tradition of the naive narrator: his youthful perspective allows the novel to present the Battle of Trafalgar with fresh eyes, untainted by strategic knowledge or adult cynicism. He does not understand the political context of the war, the tactical decisions of the admirals, or even much about how ships work — he learns all this from Marcial and Don Alonso, and the reader learns with him.

His defining characteristic is his openness: to experience, to fear, to wonder, to the influence of the people around him. He is not a conventional hero — he is often frightened, sometimes confused — but his honesty about his own limitations makes him a trustworthy narrator. The old man who tells the story, looking back across decades, is wiser than the boy who lived it, and the gap between their perspectives is one of the novel's richest dimensions.

## Role in the Narrative

Gabriel's narrative arc is a classic coming-of-age story set against the backdrop of catastrophic war. He begins as a child whose understanding of naval warfare comes from play — his "squadrons of little ships, roughly hewn out of pitch-pine" (001-008) that fight mock battles. The boundary between play and reality blurs progressively until, standing on the deck of the *Santísima Trinidad*, he first sees "the three tiers of cannon with their threatening muzzles" (009-003) and understands that this is no game.

The battle transforms him. He experiences terror — "at times I felt desperately frightened, and would gladly have hidden myself at the very bottom of the hold" (011-006) — and discovers within himself a courage he did not know he possessed. The climactic moment comes when he loads and fires a cannon himself: "the roar of the cannon fired by my own hand produced in me a feeling of extraordinary excitement" (011-028). Marcial's proud cry — "Bravely firing cannon like a man!" (011-029) — marks Gabriel's symbolic transition from boy to man.

As narrator, Gabriel's retrospective voice frames the entire novel. He tells the story as an old man, his "white hairs which now cover my old head" (011-008) contrasting with the boy he once was. This double perspective — youthful experience filtered through aged wisdom — gives the novel its distinctive tone, at once immediate and reflective.
