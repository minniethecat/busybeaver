# Fifty-Ninth Street

## Overview

Fifty-Ninth Street marks the southern boundary of Central Park in Manhattan and, in the novel, serves as one of the few specific New York street references that grounds Brewster's world in a recognisable urban geography. The street appears in a brief but telling moment: a practical arrangement for urban transit amid Brewster's mounting financial drama.

## In the Novel

The reference appears early in the novel, during the period when Brewster is absorbing the implications of the Sedgwick will and the million dollars he must spend. A companion offers him transport (005-026):

> "My man is waiting at Fifty-ninth Street. If you'll come that far, I'll go with pleasure." (005-026)

This brief mention is characteristic of McCutcheon's approach to New York geography: specific enough to feel real, casual enough to suggest that the reader already knows the city's layout. Fifty-Ninth Street was, at the turn of the century, the southern edge of the increasingly fashionable Upper East Side residential district.

## Geographical Context

In 1902 Fifty-Ninth Street was a significant thoroughfare, with Central Park to the north and the expanding midtown commercial district to the south. The Plaza Hotel — opened in 1890 — stood at Fifth Avenue and Fifty-Ninth Street, anchoring the southwest corner of the park. The street's position made it a natural meeting point and transit corridor, exactly the kind of place where a man's driver might reasonably be found waiting.

## Significance

Fifty-Ninth Street represents the "ordinary New York" against which Brewster's extraordinary circumstances are measured. While his thoughts are consumed by millions of dollars and the Sedgwick challenge, the city's mundane geography — streets, corners, waiting carriages — continues unchanged. This juxtaposition of the ordinary and the extraordinary is a hallmark of the novel's comic tension.
