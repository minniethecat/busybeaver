# Luck and Misfortune

## Overview

Luck in *Brewster's Millions* operates with a perverse logic: every venture Brewster undertakes to lose money succeeds instead. The stock market rises when he bets on its fall. Investments made in haste prove unexpectedly sound. The novel's central comic irony is that a man trying to destroy a fortune finds himself pursued by good luck — which, for him, is the worst misfortune of all. This inversion of normal fortune raises the question: is Brewster's luck genuinely random, or is wealth itself possessed of a kind of malign intelligence that resists destruction?

## In the Novel

### The Perversity of Fortune

Brewster's year is defined by outcomes that consistently oppose his intentions. When he invests in Lumber and Fuel expecting a loss, the stock surges. When he backs prizefighters certain to lose, they win. The narrator captures Brewster's exasperation: "It's just my luck, that's all. Something told me all along that things would turn out this way" (033-020). Luck, for Brewster, is not random chance but an active force working against his designs.

The Stock Exchange misfortune is particularly instructive. Brewster's broker reports a devastating loss — but "devastating" for a normal investor means something quite different for a man trying to go broke: "He brought up the information two days after the Stock Exchange misfortune" (011-004). The word "misfortune" itself becomes ironic when applied to a man who needs to lose.

### The Unwinnable Bet

Brewster's attempts to lose money through gambling prove equally futile. He backs horses and fighters "which seemed to have least chance of winning" (020-036), yet they prevail. The universe, it seems, is betting against Brewster's bets.

This pattern suggests a deeper theme: that wealth, once acquired, develops its own momentum. Sedgwick built his fortune through shrewdness, but Brewster cannot destroy it through foolishness. The money fights back. The narrator notes that Brewster "rebelled against his desire to spend" (030-027) — as if the money itself had a will that opposed its own destruction.

### Fortune as Character Test

The novel uses luck to test character. Brewster's friends observe his reversals with a mixture of amusement and concern. His misfortune at being lucky becomes a social spectacle. Yet the test is genuine: can Brewster maintain his resolve when every force — including chance itself — seems arrayed against his goal?

The executor Swearengen Jones, watching from Montana, must ultimately judge whether Brewster's good luck is his own doing. If Brewster has genuinely tried to lose and been thwarted by chance, does he still meet the condition? The novel leaves this question suspended until its final pages.

## Key Passages

> "It's just my luck, that's all. Something told me all along that things would turn out this way." (033-020)

> "He brought up the information two days after the Stock Exchange misfortune." (011-004)

> "He backed horses which seemed to have least chance of winning. But he simply couldn't lose." (020-036)

> "He rebelled against his desire to spend the seventy dollars that still remained." (030-027)

> "The Lumber and Fuel misfortune" — $58,550 unexpectedly recovered. (031-018)
