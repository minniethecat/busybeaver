## Overview

The Rescue of Peggy is one of the most dramatic action sequences in the novel: during Brewster's Mediterranean yacht cruise, [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]] is seized by one of the Arab crewmen — a retainer of the sheik whose party had visited [[the-yacht-flora-belle|the yacht]] — and held at knifepoint in a small boat. Brewster and a young American sailor named Conroy leap into the sea to save her, with Conroy firing a deadly accurate shot that drops the abductor before both men plunge into the water to retrieve [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] as she sinks beneath the waves [025-001–026-031].

## Narrative

The crisis erupts at night. The Arab captor, one of Mohammed's "principal retainers" and a man who had drawn attention during the sheik's earlier visit, stands in a small craft holding [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] before him as a human shield (025-001). A "long, cruel knife" gleams in his hand, leaving no doubt about the "ghastly intention" of the threat (025-001). Brewster, watching from his own boat, cries out in agony: "For God's sake, don't kill her!" (025-001).

The situation is broken by the "sharp crack of a gun" from the stern of Brewster's boat. Conroy, the sailor whose life Brewster had saved in an earlier incident, fires an "unerring bullet" that strikes the Arab between the eyes (025-001). Death is instantaneous, but the Arab's body topples over the gunwale of his craft, plunging both himself and [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] into the sea.

Conroy, having anticipated this outcome, is already in the air. Brewster follows an instant later. The sailor's powerful strokes carry him to the spot first, but [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] has already slipped beneath the surface. Without hesitation, Conroy dives after her. Brewster prays "with all his soul" that one of them will reach her (025-001).

Below the surface, [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] is fighting for consciousness. She feels herself "going down and down, strangling, smothering, dying" when "something vise-like" seizes her arm and hauls her upward (025-001). Conroy and Brewster together support [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] until one of the boats reaches them, and she is pulled aboard to safety.

## Consequences

The rescue transforms the atmosphere on the *[[the-yacht-flora-belle|Flora Belle]]* from terror to jubilation: "Fear gave way to joy, and where despair had for a moment reigned supreme, there was now the most insane delight" (025-001). [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] is carried below to her berth, attended by [[dr-lotless|Dr. Lotless]] and "all the women on board" (025-001).

The event also cements the bond between Brewster and Conroy. When Brewster thanks the sailor, Conroy responds with characteristic modesty: "I saw a [[luck-and-misfortune|chance]] to drop the big nigger, and then it was up to me to get her out of the water" (025-001). The rescue repays Brewster's earlier act of saving Conroy's life, completing a narrative circle of mutual obligation and gratitude.

## Analysis

The Rescue of Peggy is notable as one of the few moments in the novel where Brewster acts with unambiguous physical courage and decisive action. Throughout most of the story, his heroism is financial — the courage to spend recklessly. Here, he plunges into dark water without hesitation, risking his life for [[peggy-gray|Peggy]].

The episode also reflects the novel's adventure-novel DNA. McCutcheon, writing in the tradition of late-Victorian popular fiction, punctuates the social comedy of Brewster's spending spree with set pieces of genuine peril. The Arab abduction, while reflecting the racial attitudes of the era, serves to test Brewster's character in a domain where money cannot help him — only courage can.
