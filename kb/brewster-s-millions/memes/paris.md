# Paris

## Overview

Paris serves as a shorthand for European luxury in Brewster's New York. Rather than a destination visited, Paris in the novel is a source — of chefs, automobiles, chauffeurs, and cultural cachet. Brewster's expenditures on Parisian imports represent the cosmopolitan dimension of his profligacy.

## In the Novel

Paris first enters the narrative through Brewster's domestic arrangements: "The chef came from Paris and his name was Detuit" (006-013), establishing the French capital as the source of culinary prestige. The chef's Parisian origin is a status marker, distinguishing Brewster's household from those of his merely wealthy peers.

Later, Brewster escalates his spending by importing automobiles: he begins "to import automobiles from Paris" (022-002). The scheme is characteristically excessive: "the Paris chauffeurs and machines were out of the question" in terms of practicality — but for Brewster's purposes, impracticality is precisely the point. "At noon the chauffeurs from Paris reported for duty, and five great French machines" arrive (022-031), marking the peak of his automotive extravagance.

The city is also invoked in social conversation: Peggy Gray mentions the possibility of joining "the Prestons in Paris" (021-030), hinting at the transatlantic mobility of the wealthy — precisely the mobility Brewster's spending may threaten.

## Historical Context

Paris at the turn of the twentieth century was the cultural capital of the Western world. The 1900 Exposition Universelle had drawn millions of visitors, and French cuisine, fashion, and automobiles represented the height of luxury in American society. McCutcheon's readers would have recognised the importation of a Parisian chef and French automobiles as markers of extreme wealth and cosmopolitan aspiration.
