## Overview

The novel opens not with a death or a legal document but with a dinner — a birthday celebration for [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]] thrown by his circle of bachelor friends, the self-styled "Little Sons of the Rich." It is a scene of comfortable, unexamined privilege: good food, good company, and the easy assumption that the world will continue to provide. The narrator observes that "birthdays were always occasions for feasting" among this set (001-005), establishing the novel's satirical eye before the plot has even begun.

The dinner is the calm before the storm. By its end, a letter from [[grant-and-ripley|the law firm]] of [[grant-and-ripley|Grant & Ripley]] has been delivered, and Brewster's life has been irrevocably altered. The gathering that began as a celebration of youth and friendship becomes, in retrospect, the last moment of Brewster's old life — the final evening before the million-dollar challenge transforms him from a carefree young man into a man pursued by the obligations of fortune.

## Narrative

The dinner is described in the novel's opening paragraphs. "The Little Sons of the Rich," a circle of young men of means, have gathered to celebrate "the twenty-fifth birthday of Mr. [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]]" (001-006). The atmosphere is convivial, the wine flows, and the easy camaraderie of old friends fills the room. The narrator establishes Brewster's character through the setting: he is at home among friends, comfortable in his place, expecting nothing more demanding than another evening of good fellowship.

The evening's trajectory changes with the arrival of a letter. The precise moment of delivery is not dramatized — McCutcheon's narrative economy skips from the dinner scene to Brewster's subsequent visit to [[grant-and-ripley|Grant & Ripley]]'s offices — but the dinner serves as the narrative fulcrum. Before it, Brewster was an ordinary young man of modest expectations. After it — after the letter is read — nothing will be ordinary again.

The dinner also introduces the novel's governing theme: the relationship between wealth and identity. Brewster's friends celebrate him for who he is, not what he has. The inheritance that arrives that night will test every one of those friendships, forcing Brewster to distinguish between those who love him and those who love his fortune.

## Consequences

The birthday dinner sets in motion every subsequent event in the novel:

1. **The inheritance**: The letter from [[grant-and-ripley|Grant & Ripley]], delivered during or immediately after the dinner, informs Brewster of his uncle's death and the terms of the will. This directly leads to the reading of the will (004-002) and the launch of the spending campaign.

2. **The social transformation**: Before the dinner, Brewster was one of many young men of comfortable means. After it, he is the subject of intense public interest — a man whose every expenditure is tracked, discussed, and judged by [[new-york-city|New York]] society.

3. **The dinner-party motif**: The birthday dinner inaugurates a recurring pattern in the novel: the dinner as theater of social display. Brewster's subsequent "ten big dinner parties" (010-005) are direct descendants of this first gathering, escalating in [[profligacy|extravagance]] until they become a burden rather than a pleasure.

4. **The test of friendship**: The friends gathered at the birthday dinner — [[joe-bragdon|Joe Bragdon]], "Subway" Smith, [[elon-gardner|Elon Gardner]] — become Brewster's inner circle throughout the ordeal. Their presence at the beginning establishes the baseline of loyalty against which all subsequent relationships are measured.

## Analysis

The birthday dinner functions as the novel's **Edenic prelude** — a scene of innocence before the fall into wealth. McCutcheon establishes the normal, the expected, the comfortable, so that the disruption of [[the-sedgwick-fortune|the inheritance]] registers with full force. The structural choice is deliberate: by beginning with celebration rather than with the death or the legal machinery, the novel foregrounds character over plot mechanics.

The "Little Sons of the Rich" as a name is itself satirical. These are not the heirs to industrial empires but the sons of comfortable families — men who have never had to work but who have also never commanded real power. Brewster's elevation from this circle into the realm of genuine wealth is a rise in fortune but also a fall from a kind of grace: the uncomplicated friendship of men who need nothing from each other.

The dinner also establishes the novel's **social taxonomy**. The friends are "Little Sons," not captains of industry. The dinner is "occasional," not institutional. The world of the dinner — intimate, male, convivial — is precisely the world that Brewster will lose as [[the-sedgwick-fortune|the inheritance]] forces him into ballrooms, bank offices, and the public eye.
