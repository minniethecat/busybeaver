## Overview

The New York Stock Exchange appears in Brewster's Millions as the setting for one of the novel's central ironies: [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]]'s deliberate attempt to lose money through stock speculation results instead in a substantial profit. In Chapter 10 ("Napoleon of Finance"), Brewster, seeking to accelerate the depletion of his inheritance, enters the [[speculation|stock market]] intending to lose. His purchase of ten thousand shares of [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]] Common inadvertently triggers a market panic that drives the stock's price upward, turning his intended loss into a windfall. The Exchange thus serves as a narrative device illustrating the perversity of Brewster's luck — the harder he tries to lose money, the more money he makes.

## Role in the Novel

Brewster's engagement with the Stock Exchange is presented as a calculated gamble. After "some days he stealthily watched the [[speculation|stock market]] and plied his friends with questions about values" (010-006). His research leads him to a counterintuitive conclusion: [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]] Common, a stock widely expected to decline, is the ideal vehicle for his purposes. "Casting aside all apprehension, so far as [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] was concerned, he prepared for what was to be his one and only venture on the Stock Exchange before the 23rd of the following September" (010-006).

The execution of the trade is entrusted to [[elon-gardner|Elon Gardner]], whose "face was the picture of despair when Brewster asked him to buy heavily in [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]]" (010-006). [[elon-gardner|Gardner]], unaware of Brewster's true motive — to lose, not to gain — sees only recklessness in the young man's strategy.

> "When Elon Gardner, acting for Montgomery Brewster, took ten thousand shares at 108¾ there was a mighty gasp on the Exchange, then a rubbing of eyes, then commotion. Astonishment was followed by nervousness, and then came the struggle." (010-014)

The market's reaction is precisely the opposite of what Brewster intends. "There had been little to excite the Stock Exchange for some weeks: nothing was active and the slightest flurry was hailed as an event" (010-014). Brewster's large purchase, in a moribund market, is interpreted by other traders as a signal of insider confidence. A buying frenzy ensues, driving [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]]'s price upward and earning Brewster a substantial profit — described elsewhere as one of his largest "setbacks" in the campaign to exhaust the million.

The Stock Exchange episode is referenced again in Chapter 11, where Brewster's despair "a day or two after the Stock Exchange misfortune" prompts him to try other avenues of loss, and in Chapter 15, where the ball's cost is seen as a way to overcome "the heavy handicap the Stock Exchange had imposed" on his spending race (015-023). The episode thus becomes a recurring plot point: the profit Brewster cannot avoid making haunts his subsequent efforts and raises the stakes for every expenditure that follows.

## Associated Characters

- **[[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]]** approaches the Exchange as an amateur, his strategy based on "constant reading and observation" rather than experience (010-006). His "cunning and craftiness of a general" in planning the trade proves counterproductive — the more carefully he tries to lose, the more successfully he gains.

- **[[elon-gardner|Elon Gardner]]** serves as Brewster's broker and financial conscience. His "despair" at executing the [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]] purchase represents the perspective of conventional financial wisdom, which cannot comprehend Brewster's inverted incentives (010-006).

- **[[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]]**, as executor of the will, looms over the Stock Exchange episode as the unseen auditor. Brewster's decision to risk the [[speculation|stock market]] is taken with "all apprehension, so far as [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] was concerned" set aside (010-006) — a reminder that every financial move is subject to [[swearengen-jones|the executor]]'s ultimate judgment.

The Stock Exchange episode exemplifies a pattern that runs throughout the novel: Brewster's attempts to lose money are consistently thwarted by a combination of market irrationality, misinterpretation of his motives, and sheer [[luck-and-misfortune|bad luck]] at being good at losing badly. The Exchange, as an institution, embodies the larger economic forces that resist individual control — a theme that McCutcheon plays for comic effect throughout Brewster's year-long ordeal.
