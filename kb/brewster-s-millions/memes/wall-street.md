# Wall Street

## Overview

Wall Street is the epicentre of New York's financial world at the turn of the twentieth century, and the setting for one of the novel's richest ironies: Montgomery Brewster, attempting to lose his inherited fortune through reckless investment, inadvertently makes money on the stock market. The "excitement in Wall Street" (010-006) caused by his operations transforms the Street from a mere backdrop into an active agent in the plot — the place where Brewster's competence as a businessman undermines his goal of profligacy.

## In the Novel

Wall Street is invoked as the standard of financial sophistication against which Brewster's affairs are measured. Mr. Grant, the lawyer, assures him that his inheritance has been "handled with all the skill of a Wall Street financier" (004-008), establishing the Street as the benchmark of financial acumen.

When Brewster begins his campaign of loss-making investments, the irony is immediate: his market moves "cause a little excitement in Wall Street" (010-006), suggesting that even his attempts at financial self-destruction carry a certain flair. "For some days he stealthily bought and sold" — and contrary to all his intentions, the market rewards him. The Street thus functions as a narrative antagonist: the more Brewster tries to fail, the more the impersonal mechanisms of finance conspire to make him succeed.

## Historical Context

By 1900, Wall Street was already established as the United States' preeminent financial centre. The New York Stock Exchange had moved to its Broad Street building in 1865, and the consolidation of American finance in lower Manhattan was well underway. J.P. Morgan and other financiers dominated the era's financial landscape. McCutcheon's readers would have understood Wall Street as synonymous with wealth, speculation, and the impersonal machinery of capital — exactly the associations the novel exploits for comic effect.
