# Loyalty and Friendship

## Overview

Amid the financial chaos of *Brewster's Millions*, the loyalty of Montgomery Brewster's friends provides a stable moral center. His circle — "Subway" Smith, Richard "Rip" Van Winkle, Joe Bragdon, and others — remain steadfast through his year of profligate spending. They do not understand his behavior, and at times they are alarmed by it, but they never abandon him. The novel suggests that friendship, unlike money, is a form of wealth that grows rather than diminishes through adversity.

## In the Novel

### The Brewster Circle

Brewster's friends form a distinct social unit within the novel. They are young men of varying means and temperaments, bound together by shared history rather than shared fortune. The narrator describes the group as representing "his best and truest friendships" and notes "the loyalty of these companions" as a defining feature of Brewster's life (018-016).

The friends serve various functions in Brewster's spending campaign. "Subway" Smith provides comic commentary and moral support. "Rip" Van Winkle offers steadiness and intervention when needed — at one point, "Rip Van Winkle interfered and peace was restored" during a confrontation (023-008). Joe Bragdon serves as private secretary, managing the logistics of Brewster's increasingly complex affairs.

### Loyalty Without Understanding

Crucially, Brewster's friends remain loyal without knowing his secret. They watch his "reckless extravagance" with concern (006-015) but do not withdraw their friendship. When Brewster confides news to them, it is "to Rip Van Winkle and Subway Smith" that he turns (023-006) — the inner circle who have earned his trust through their constancy.

This loyalty is tested by Brewster's behavior. His friends must defend him in society, explain him to others, and at times restrain him from his worst impulses. Yet they do so without the knowledge that would justify his actions. Their loyalty is, in a sense, blind — and therefore all the more valuable.

### Friendship vs. Fortune

The novel draws an implicit contrast between the wealth Brewster must destroy and the friendships he preserves. Money, in this world, is fickle — it accumulates against his will, resists spending, and ultimately vanishes. Friendship remains. The loyalty of "Subway" Smith and "Rip" Van Winkle represents a kind of capital that Sedgwick's conditions cannot touch.

In the novel's moral economy, friendship is the one asset Brewster is never asked to spend. It survives the year of profligacy intact, suggesting that the truest forms of wealth are those that no will can condition and no challenge can destroy.
