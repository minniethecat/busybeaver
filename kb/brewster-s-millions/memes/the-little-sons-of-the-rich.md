## Overview

"The Little Sons of the Rich" is the self-designated name of an informal social circle of wealthy young [[new-york-city|New York]] men who gather regularly at [[pettingills-studio|the studio]] of [[pettingill|Pettingill]], one of their number. The group makes its first appearance in the novel's opening chapter, "A Birthday Dinner," where nine members assemble to celebrate [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]]'s twenty-fifth birthday (001-002). Throughout the novel, the "Little Sons" function as a Greek chorus — simultaneously enabling, critiquing, and marveling at Brewster's year-long campaign to exhaust a million-dollar inheritance. Their nickname, with its ironic self-awareness, captures the peculiar position of young men born into wealth who affect a detachment from it while thoroughly enjoying its privileges.

## Role in the Novel

The "Little Sons of the Rich" are introduced at the novel's outset, gathered around "the long table in [[pettingill|Pettingill]]'s studio" for Brewster's birthday dinner (001-002). There are "nine of them present, besides Brewster," all "young, more or less enterprising, hopeful, and reasonably sure of better things to come" (001-002). Most bore names that "meant something in the story of [[new-york-city|New York]]" — a detail that establishes them as members of [[new-york-city|the city]]'s established families, distinct from nouveau riche arrivistes like the Californian Rodney Grimes (001-002).

At the birthday dinner, the group establishes its tone of affectionate mockery. When Brewster receives news of his grandfather's death and the million-dollar inheritance with its spending condition, the "Little Sons" are present as witnesses and initial commentators. Their reaction — a mixture of excitement, envy, and disbelief — frames the reader's introduction to [[the-sedgwick-fortune|the inheritance]] challenge.

Throughout Brewster's spending year, the group reconvenes for "old-time supper party" gatherings, where they dissect his latest [[profligacy|extravagance]]s with a blend of humor and concern (015-003). In Chapter 15 ("[[the-cut-direct|The Cut Direct]]"), they are particularly prominent: when Brewster unveils his plans for the Spanish costume ball, "even the 'Little Sons,' who were imaginative by instinct and reckless on principle, could not be quite acquiescent" (015-007). Their itemized objections — "$125,000" for the ball, "thirteen thousand dollars for flowers," "a Viennese orchestra for twenty-nine thousand" — serve as a narrative device to quantify Brewster's spending for the reader (015-008–015-016).

> "The 'Little Sons' looked at one another in consternation, while Brewster's indifference expressed itself in an unflattering comment upon his friend's vulgarity." (015-008)

The group's role shifts over the course of the novel. In the early chapters, they are enablers — "The 'Little Sons' took off their coats and helped [[pettingill|Pettingill]] in the work of preparation" for the ball (015-023). After Brewster's appendicitis and the ball's postponement, they become caretakers: "[[nopper-harrison|Harrison]] and his associates, almost frantic with fear for Brewster's life, managed to perform wonders" (015-035). By the novel's final act, when society has given Brewster the "cut direct," the "Little Sons" remain among the few who still associate with him, their loyalty outlasting the fair-weather friends who consumed his hospitality.

## Associated Characters

The membership of the "Little Sons" includes several recurring characters whose professions and personalities are sketched through dialogue and incident:

- **[[pettingill|Pettingill]]**, the artist in whose studio the group meets, serves as unofficial host and master of ceremonies. His studio, located above a French restaurant from which the suppers are catered (001-005), is the group's fixed meeting place.

- **[[nopper-harrison|Harrison]]** ("Nopper") functions as Brewster's general manager and conscience, expressing the most consistent alarm at his spending. It is [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]] who solemnly estimates the ball's cost at $125,000 (015-008) and who later oversees the practical arrangements of Brewster's affairs.

- **Bragdon** provides philosophical commentary — "When you lose your heart your sense of humor goes too" (015-004) — and is entrusted with the repair and return of the *Flitter* after [[the-yacht-flora-belle|the yacht]] cruise (029-004).

- **"Subway" Smith** earned his nickname from the group's running joke that "a man is known by the street that's named after him" (001-002). He delivers some of the sharpest observations about Brewster's situation, noting that "all that glory [is] wasted on two hundred rank outsiders" (015-017).

- **Van Winkle** and **[[elon-gardner|Elon Gardner]]** round out the circle. [[elon-gardner|Gardner]], more serious than the others, serves as Brewster's financial secretary and is devastated by the pace of his spending — "I've done my best to economize for you here at home … but nothing could possibly balance the [[profligacy|extravagance]]s" (029-016).

Together, the "Little Sons" represent a spectrum of responses to wealth: from [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]]'s anxious management to Bragdon's philosophical detachment to Subway Smith's satirical edge. Their collective presence provides the novel with a social context for Brewster's solitary spending ordeal, reminding the reader that his experiment unfolds within a community of peers who are by turns amused, alarmed, and ultimately loyal.
