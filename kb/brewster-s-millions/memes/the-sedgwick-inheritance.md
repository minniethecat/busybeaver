## Overview

The Sedgwick Inheritance is the foundational event of the novel, set in motion by the death of [[james-t-sedgwick|James T. Sedgwick]], [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]]'s uncle. Sedgwick, a reclusive millionaire who made his fortune in Western gold mines and ranches, died on September 24 in [[butte-montana|Butte, Montana]]. His last will named Brewster as sole heir to an [[inheritance|estate]] valued at approximately $7 million — but with an unprecedented condition attached (004-006) (004-018).

## Narrative

On September 30, shortly after celebrating his twenty-fifth birthday, [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]] is summoned to the law offices of [[grant-and-ripley|Grant & Ripley]]. [[mr-grant|Mr. Grant]] informs him that his uncle [[james-t-sedgwick|James T. Sedgwick]], long believed dead, had actually been alive in [[montana|the West]], amassing a substantial fortune through gold mines and ranches (004-004) (004-008). Sedgwick had never married; Brewster's mother was his only sister and closest known relative (004-014).

The will's terms are extraordinary. Brewster inherits the entire [[inheritance|estate]] — but only on condition that he first disposes of a $1 million [[inheritance|bequest]] within a single year. During that year, he is permitted no other worldly possession beyond the clothes on his back on the September 23 that marks his twenty-fifth birthday (004-019). Furthermore, Sedgwick's will dictates that Brewster must provide satisfactory evidence to [[swearengen-jones|the executor]], [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] of [[montana|Montana]], that he has spent the million dollars "satisfactorily" and without retaining any assets (004-020).

> The instrument went into detail in respect to this supreme condition. It set forth that Montgomery Brewster was to have no other worldly possession than the clothes which covered him on the September day that marked his twenty-fifth birthday. (004-019)

> There was also a clause in which he undertook to dictate the conduct of Montgomery Brewster during the year leading up to his twenty-sixth anniversary. He required that the young man should give satisfactory evidence to the executor that he had disposed of the million dollars "satisfactorily." (004-020)

[[mr-grant|Mr. Grant]] confirms the will's authenticity with a telegram from the Probate Court in Sedgwick's home county (004-024). Brewster, initially skeptical, is both exhilarated and overwhelmed — [[the-sedgwick-fortune|the inheritance]] is vastly larger than anyone anticipated. When the full extent of Sedgwick's wealth becomes clear, the figure settles at approximately seven million dollars (005-050).

## Consequences

The Sedgwick inheritance sets the entire plot in motion. Brewster's one-year spending obligation creates the central dramatic tension of the novel: he must squander an enormous sum without acquiring any lasting assets, all while maintaining the appearance of ordinary [[profligacy|extravagance]]. The condition specifically forbids him from simply [[charity|giving]] the money away or destroying it — he must *spend* it.

The emotional consequences are equally significant. Brewster's newfound wealth complicates his relationship with [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]], who finds his sudden fortune and erratic behavior deeply unsettling. The inheritance also attracts the attention of society figures, fortune-hunters, and financial advisors who each have their own designs on the [[the-sedgwick-fortune|Sedgwick millions]].

The will's executor, [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] of [[montana|Montana]], becomes a distant but ever-present arbiter of Brewster's conduct. The final reckoning, scheduled for Brewster's twenty-sixth birthday, looms over every spending decision in the narrative (034-039).

## Key Passages

> "You are both fortunate and unfortunate, Mr. Brewster," said Mr. Grant, after the young man had dropped into a chair in the office of Grant & Ripley the next day. (004-002)

> "The situation is so remarkable that you'll consider that a mild way of putting it when you've heard everything. I think you were told, in our note of yesterday, that you are the sole heir. Well, it may surprise you to learn that James Sedgwick died possessed of an estate valued at something like seven million dollars." (004-006)
