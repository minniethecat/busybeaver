## Overview

The reading of James T. Sedgwick's will at the law offices of Grant & Ripley is the narrative turning point that transforms Montgomery Brewster from a carefree young heir into a man racing against time. In this scene, Brewster learns the extraordinary terms of his uncle's will: he must spend one million dollars within one year, retaining no assets, in order to inherit the full seven-million-dollar estate (004-018, 004-019).

## The Scene

The day after his grandfather's death, Montgomery Brewster arrives at Grant & Ripley's office wearing a 「slightly bored expression」, having completely forgotten the existence of an uncle (004-002, 004-003). Mr. Grant, the senior partner, begins by describing the situation as both fortunate and unfortunate — a characterisation Brewster does not yet understand (004-005).

Mr. Grant reveals that James T. Sedgwick died possessed of an estate valued at almost seven million dollars, accumulated through gold mines and ranches in the Northwest after arriving in Montana from Australia in 1885 (004-006, 004-008). Brewster, as the son of Sedgwick's only sister, is the sole heir (004-014).

## The Condition

The will contains a 「supreme condition」: Montgomery Brewster must be penniless on his twenty-sixth birthday, September 23rd, owning nothing beyond the clothes on his back (004-019). He is to dispose of the one million dollars he has inherited from his grandfather — with no assets retained, no gifts that could be reclaimed, and no one told of the terms (004-018, 004-019). The executor, Swearengen Jones, is to verify compliance before releasing the seven-million-dollar inheritance.

## Brewster's Response

Initially stunned, Brewster quickly recovers his confidence. 「Wouldn't you exchange a million for seven millions, Mr. Grant?」 he asks, proposing to rid himself of the money by 「the simple process of destruction」(004-031, 004-033). Mr. Grant cautions him that spending a million dollars without violating the will's conditions may prove more difficult than he imagines — a warning that proves prescient (004-037).

## Significance

This scene establishes the novel's central dramatic premise. The reading of the will transforms what could have been a simple inheritance story into a comic race against time. The contrast between Brewster's breezy confidence and Mr. Grant's sober caution prefigures the complications that will follow.
