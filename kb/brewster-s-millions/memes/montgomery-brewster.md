# Montgomery Brewster

## Character Overview

Montgomery "Monty" Brewster is the protagonist of *Brewster's Millions*. At twenty-five years old, he is "tall and straight and smooth-faced" (001-003), a popular young man about New York whose life is transformed by two inheritances: one million dollars from his grandfather [[Edwin Peter Brewster]], and the conditional bequest of seven million dollars from his uncle [[James T. Sedgwick]]. The novel follows his year-long attempt to satisfy Sedgwick's extraordinary condition — that he arrive penniless on his twenty-sixth birthday, as defined in [[the-million-dollar-challenge]].

Brewster stands at the center of the novel's exploration of [[wealth-and-character]]. Unlike many wealthy protagonists, his task is not to acquire fortune but to divest it — a premise that inverts the conventional rags-to-riches narrative and forces both Brewster and the reader to question what money means when its purpose is to be destroyed.

## Role in the Novel

### The Inheritance

Brewster begins the novel as a young man of prospects rather than accomplishments. "Montgomery Brewster no longer had 'prospects.' People had troubled him with them for years" (002-002). His grandfather's death and the revelation of Sedgwick's will transform him from a man awaiting fortune into a man required to destroy it.

The will's conditions place Brewster in an impossible position: he must spend a million dollars without giving it away, maintain a spotless reputation, and tell no one the reason for his behavior. As [[Mr. Grant]], the lawyer, explains, "You understand that you are expected to be penniless a year from now?" (004-030). This secrecy requirement becomes the engine of the novel's comic tension — Brewster must appear profligate while remaining honorable, wasteful while being strategic.

The central irony of the challenge is articulated by Brewster himself in conversation with [[Swearengen Jones]]:

> "I don't mind being a pauper for a few hours on the 23rd of next September, if I can only look back and see that I have lived a millionnaire's life for a solid year. But I'll be hanged if I can see the object in living the life of a millionnaire and then ending it as a millionnaire. That's like working all your life to save money to die rich." (004-033)

### Character and Temperament

Brewster is presented as fundamentally decent but untested. His grandfather "had taken the boy to his own heart" (001-004) and provided for him, but the young man has never had to earn his own way. Sedgwick's challenge is designed to remedy this — to force Brewster to demonstrate the "shrewdness" and "enterprise" (004-020) that Sedgwick himself valued.

Throughout the novel, Brewster navigates the contradiction at the heart of his task: he must appear wasteful while being disciplined, profligate while temperate. His ability to maintain his character under this pressure is the novel's central moral question. His friend [[Nopper Harrison]] observes: "He's the most extravagant man I ever saw, and yet I'll swear he isn't enjoying it" (008-015). This tension between external appearance and internal reality makes Brewster a more complex protagonist than the comic premise might suggest.

### Relationships

Brewster's relationships are tested by the secrecy his challenge requires. His romance with [[Barbara Drew]] founders on her inability to understand his behavior: "It was impossible for him to share all his secrets with her" (009-004). His friendship with [[Peggy Gray]], by contrast, proves more resilient — she trusts his character even when his actions appear inexplicable.

His circle of male friends — [[Subway Smith]], [[Rip Van Winkle]], [[Elon Gardner]], [[Joe Bragdon]], and others — remain loyal despite their confusion, forming a comic chorus that observes and comments on Brewster's increasingly bizarre expenditures. Their loyalty is tested during events like [[the-yacht-cruise]] and the Florida expedition, where Brewster's spending reaches its most extreme.

### The Resolution

The novel's climax turns on whether Brewster has successfully disposed of the million. His final report to [[Swearengen Jones]] (031-018) details his expenditures with the precision of a ledger. The resolution tests not only his spending ability but his character — whether he has emerged from a year of [[profligacy]] with his integrity intact. The [[the-september-23rd-deadline]] marks the moment when the fate of both the million dollars and the seven-million-dollar inheritance is decided.

## Character Development Arc

Brewster's arc follows a pattern unusual for its time: he does not learn to value money more, but rather learns to value it less. At the novel's opening, he is a young man of conventional expectations — waiting for his inheritance, planning a comfortable life. By its close, he has passed through the fire of enforced wastefulness and emerged with a clearer sense of what matters.

His development can be traced through three phases:

1. **Inheritance and shock** (Chapters 1–4): Brewster receives the news of both bequests and grapples with the absurdity of his position. His initial reaction is disbelief, then a reluctant acceptance of the challenge.

2. **Profligate living** (Chapters 5–27): The longest phase, in which Brewster systematically spends the million. This period includes the renovation of [[fifth-avenue]], the yacht cruise to the Mediterranean, the Florida cottage, and countless smaller extravagances. Throughout, he must balance the appearance of genuine wastefulness with the underlying requirement of honorability.

3. **The final accounting** (Chapters 28–34): As [[the-september-23rd-deadline]] approaches, Brewster confronts the possibility of failure and its consequences. His ultimate success — or failure — depends not on his spending acumen alone but on forces beyond his control, including the loyalty of those who have benefited from his apparent profligacy.

## Narrative Function

As protagonist, Brewster serves several narrative functions simultaneously. He is the lens through which the reader experiences [[New York City]] high society at the turn of the century. He is the vehicle for McCutcheon's satire of wealth and social convention. And he is the moral center of a story that constantly tests the boundary between appearance and reality — a theme explored in [[appearance-and-reality]].

The structural genius of Brewster's situation is that it renders him simultaneously powerful and powerless. He controls a million dollars, yet cannot use it for his own benefit. He is surrounded by friends, yet cannot confide in them. This double bind generates the novel's comic energy while allowing McCutcheon to explore the theme of [[luck-and-misfortune]] without becoming preachy.
