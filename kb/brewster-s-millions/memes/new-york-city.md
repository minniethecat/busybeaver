## Overview

New York City at the turn of the twentieth century is the backdrop against which [[Montgomery Brewster]]'s financial odyssey plays out. The novel maps a geography of wealth and power: from the exclusive apartment buildings where Brewster takes up residence to the banking houses of [[Wall Street]], from the ballrooms of [[Fifth Avenue]] society to the humble rooms on Fortieth Street where [[Peggy Gray]] lives. McCutcheon's New York is a city of stark contrasts — a place where a man can spend forty thousand dollars in a single evening and where a modest teacher's daughter can hold the moral center of the story.

The city is more than scenery. It is an engine of social judgment, a theater where every expenditure is observed and evaluated. "It is not only New York that laughs at his quixotic expenditures," the narrator notes — but New York is the primary audience (016-038). The city's gaze follows Brewster from his first extravagant dinner to his final, penniless return.

## In the Novel

Brewster's relationship with New York unfolds in three movements.

### Arrival and Ascent

Upon receiving news of the Sedgwick inheritance, Brewster takes "the most expensive apartments to be found in New York City" at a rental of $23,000 per year (006-002) and begins his campaign of conspicuous consumption. His celebrated dinner parties become the talk of the town (006-023). The young heir's sudden prominence in New York society is met with a mixture of admiration and bemusement from established figures like [[Colonel Prentiss Drew]].

### Crisis and Loyalty

When a stock market panic triggers a run on the banks, the crisis centers on a Manhattan financial institution where Brewster has deposited funds (013-004). His decision not to withdraw his money — sacrificing a clear path to winning the inheritance — is a choice made in the pressure cooker of Wall Street, under the eyes of men whose respect he values. This moment crystallizes the tension between Brewster's public persona as a profligate and his private character as a man of [[loyalty-and-friendship]].

### Return and Reckoning

After the long yacht cruise, Brewster returns to New York transformed. "Four days out from New York, then three days, then two" — the countdown to arrival measures his dwindling fortune (029-012). His landing is not triumphant but humbling: "the most miserable man in New York," his former friends now seeing "a shadow of the once debonair young New Yorker" (029-017).

The city also provides the novel's domestic anchor. Peggy Gray's home on Fortieth Street — "her only possession" — stands in quiet contrast to Brewster's rented extravagance, a reminder that place can mean permanence rather than display (003-003).

## Historical Context

The New York of *Brewster's Millions* (published 1902) is the New York of the Gilded Age's final years. The city's population had swelled to nearly 3.5 million through immigration and consolidation (the five boroughs unified in 1898). The gap between the wealth of families like the Vanderbilts and Astors and the poverty of the tenements was at its most extreme.

The novel's references to specific locations anchor it in this world: the Yucatan Building where [[Grant and Ripley]] have their offices (005-055), the clubs where Brewster lunches with Colonel Drew (010-016), and the social circuit of dinners and balls that constituted "society." The very extravagance that Brewster pursues — the $3,000-a-plate dinners, the lavish apartments, the chartered yachts — reflects the actual spending patterns of Gilded Age elites, pushed to satirical extremes.

Fortieth Street, where Peggy Gray lives, was at this period a residential neighborhood undergoing transition as commercial development pushed northward. The contrast between Peggy's modest home and Brewster's luxury apartments maps the social geography of Manhattan: wealth concentrated below Fifty-ninth Street, with the truly fashionable addresses clustered near Fifth Avenue and Central Park.
