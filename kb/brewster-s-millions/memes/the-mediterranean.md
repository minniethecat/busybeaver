# The Mediterranean

## Overview

The Mediterranean Sea serves as the stage for the most ambitious phase of Montgomery Brewster's spending campaign. After exhausting the possibilities of New York City, Brewster takes his friends on a four-month cruise aboard the chartered yacht *Flora Belle*, intending to burn through his remaining fortune in European luxury. The Mediterranean represents both the cosmopolitan aspirations of American wealth and the existential gamble at the heart of Brewster's challenge.

## The Cruise Plan

Brewster's desire to escape New York and take his spending campaign international is first revealed in Chapter 17. After his second extravagant ball, he longed "for the time when he could take his few friends with him and sail away to the Mediterranean to escape the eyes and tongues of New York" (017-008). He impatiently urged [[oliver-harrison|Harrison]] to finalise the arrangements.

The yacht *Flora Belle* was chartered for four months, with a "picked crew of fifty men under command of Captain Abner Perry" (017-008). Even Harrison, normally complicit in Brewster's extravagance, protested: "I can't for my life figure how you can get out for less than a fortune, if we do everything you have in mind" (017-009). Brewster's response — "It's not in me to save money" (017-010) — encapsulated his approach to the cruise.

## The Riviera

The party landed on the French Riviera, where Brewster's spending reached new heights. On the pretext that the yacht required a thorough cleaning, he transferred his guests to "the hotel of a fascinating village which was near the sea and yet quite out of the world" (020-002). He engaged the entire first floor with "balconies overlooking the blue Mediterranean and a separate dining-room and salon" (020-002).

The local economy responded predictably: "The town quickly took on the appearance of entertaining a royal visitor, and a number of shops were kept open longer than usual in the hope that their owners might catch some of the American's money" (020-003). The hotel proprietor Philippe wept tears of joy, noting that August was normally dead — "*C'est magnifique! c'est superbe!*" (020-004).

## Crisis and Calm

The Mediterranean cruise was not without peril. After a severe storm damaged the *Flora Belle*, it was decided "to make for the Canary Islands, where repairs could be made and the voyage resumed" (028-041). But the wind died entirely, and "for a week the *Flitter* tossed about absolutely unable to make headway" (028-041).

With the September 23rd deadline approaching, Brewster faced the real possibility of failing the challenge — not from inability to spend, but from being stranded at sea: "Over one hundred thousand dollars would remain after he had settled the expenses of the cruise, and he was helplessly drifting in mid-ocean" (028-041).

## Significance

The Mediterranean represents both the pinnacle of Brewster's spending campaign and its most dangerous moment. On land, his spending was limited only by his imagination; at sea, he was at the mercy of wind and weather. The cruise embodies the central irony of the novel: that spending a million dollars in a year can be genuinely difficult when fate intervenes.
