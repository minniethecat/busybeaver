# The Reading of the Will
## Overview
The reading of [[james-t-sedgwick|James T. Sedgwick]]s will at the offices of [[grant-and-ripley|Grant & Ripley]] is the event that sets the entire plot in motion. [[mr-grant|Mr. Grant]] reveals that Sedgwick died with an [[inheritance|estate]] "valued at almost seven million dollars" (004-006) — but that Brewster can only inherit if he arrives penniless on his twenty-sixth birthday, having disposed of every penny of his grandfathers money.
## Key Moments
The wills conditions are extraordinary: Brewster must possess nothing but the clothes on his back (004-019), maintain temperate habits and a fair name (004-020), and arrive at the deadline "absolutely penniless" (004-018). Brewsters initial reaction — "I dont mind being a pauper for a few hours on the 23rd of next September" (004-033) — reveals his failure to grasp the challenges difficulty.
## Significance
This scene establishes the central dramatic premise and the moral framework of the novel. Sedgwicks hatred of [[edwin-p-brewster|Edwin Brewster]] (004-019) is revealed as the hidden engine of the challenge.
