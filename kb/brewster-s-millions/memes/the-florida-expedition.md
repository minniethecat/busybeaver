## Overview

The Florida Expedition represents a brief interlude of supposed recuperation that Brewster, characteristically, transforms into yet another spending opportunity. After the punishing pace of his [[new-york-city|New York]] campaign — the dinners, the [[speculation|stock market]] disaster, the De Mille ball — Brewster's health visibly deteriorates. "His color was bad, his eyes were heavy," and his physician [[dr-lotless|Dr. Lotless]] "advised a month at least" of recuperation in Florida (015-045). Brewster agrees, but the expedition to St. Augustine proves to be less a rest cure than a change of venue for his profligacy. The trip introduces the Ponce de Leon Hotel, brings the Drew and Gray families into closer proximity with Brewster, and sets in motion the ill-fated Viennese orchestra tour that will cost him over $56,000 (031-018).

## Narrative

The Florida expedition is born from medical necessity. [[dr-lotless|Dr. Lotless]], observing Brewster's deteriorating condition after weeks of relentless social expenditure, "advised a month at least" in Florida for recuperation (015-045). Brewster, ever alert to opportunities to spend money, agrees — not for his health, but because the trip promises new avenues for [[profligacy|extravagance]].

The party travels to St. Augustine, where a [[new-york-city|New York]] millionaire's winter cottage — "which had fallen to Brewster" — provides accommodation (016-002). The setting is described as "[[florida-cottage|the cottage]] of a [[new-york-city|New York]] millionaire which had fallen to Brewster. The owner had, for the time, preferred Italy to St. Augustine" (016-002). The acquisition of this property, whether through purchase or lease, represents another drain on Brewster's shrinking fortune.

The Drew family — Mrs. [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Prentiss Drew]] and Barbara — register at the same hotel (016-013), creating an uncomfortable proximity. [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]], meanwhile, is "in despair over [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]]'s attitude" (016-014), watching him continue his self-destructive spending even in what was supposed to be a period of rest.

It is during the Florida expedition that Brewster conceives the Viennese orchestra scheme. The idea had been planted earlier by [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]], who was "in communication with the manager of that Hungarian orchestra" (012-028), but the plan crystallizes in Florida. Brewster engages the orchestra for an American tour — an enterprise that proves to be one of his most reliably unprofitable ventures.

The orchestra's tour is a catastrophe from the start. "From the beginning, apparently, the concerts had been marked for disaster" (016-012). The musicians quarrel, the audiences are sparse, and the tour hemorrhages money. When the orchestra finally "straggled into [[new-york-city|New York]]" (017-006), Brewster's ledger records the toll: "Amount lost on concert tour | 56,382.00" (031-018).

## Consequences

The Florida expedition, while briefer and less dramatic than [[the-yacht-flora-belle|the yacht]]ing cruise, plants several important narrative seeds:

1. **The Viennese orchestra loss**: The $56,382 lost on the concert tour represents one of Brewster's largest single expenditures — a stroke of genuine financial incompetence that, ironically, brings him closer to his goal.

2. **The Barbara subplot intensifies**: The Drew family's presence in St. Augustine keeps the romantic tension alive. Barbara's continued coldness toward Brewster, juxtaposed with [[peggy-gray|Peggy]]'s quiet devotion, sharpens the novel's central romantic triangle.

3. **The health narrative**: Brewster's physical decline — his bad color, heavy eyes, and visible exhaustion — provides a physiological counterpoint to his financial dissolution. The million is killing him, literally as well as socially, and the Florida interlude only briefly arrests the decline.

4. **The pattern of displacement**: The Florida expedition establishes a pattern that will define the novel's middle section: Brewster's attempts to escape his circumstances (to Florida, to [[the-yacht-flora-belle|the yacht]], to Europe) only intensify them. Geography offers no refuge from the challenge.

5. **The financial accounting**: The final balance sheet entry for the expedition — "Florida trip, including medical attendance | 450.00" (031-018) — is deceptively modest. The real costs of the Florida interlude are captured in the orchestra losses and the St. Augustine property expenses that ripple through subsequent chapters.
