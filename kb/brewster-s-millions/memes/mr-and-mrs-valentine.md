# Mr. and Mrs. Valentine

## Overview

Mr. and Mrs. Valentine are a married couple who serve as "the official chaperon[s]" (018-016) aboard Montgomery Brewster's extravagant cruise on the *Flora Belle*. Their presence provides the voyage with a veneer of social respectability, though their own composure frays as the journey descends into storm and shipwreck. Mrs. Valentine is the more socially visible of the pair, while Mr. Valentine emerges as a voice of reason in the shipboard councils.

## Role in the Story

The Valentines first appear at a dinner party prior to the cruise, where "Colonel Drew took in Mrs. Valentine and his content was assured" (006-023), establishing Mrs. Valentine as a figure of social cachet. They join the cruise party as chaperons, bringing their daughter Mary (018-016).

During the voyage, both Valentines become witnesses to Brewster's increasingly strained circumstances. Mrs. Valentine is observant: she "had seen him as he stood revealed" during a moment of crisis (020-027), and later rides in a carriage with Peggy, her "curiosity abundant" about Brewster's situation (022-007). She is present when "the matter [is] settled" alongside Peggy and Brewster (022-030).

Mr. Valentine takes a more active role in the shipboard decision-making. When the passengers gather to discuss the crisis, he speaks with authority: "'It is very necessary, in fact,' said Mr. Valentine. 'We cannot proceed without the fullest understanding'" (026-018). His intervention is conciliatory rather than confrontational: "'We like you, Brewster'" (026-033). In retrospect, he "regretted that he had gone on the cruise" (028-028), a candid admission that underscores the voyage's toll on even its most proper passengers.

## Relationships

The Valentines are parents to Mary Valentine and serve as her social sponsors aboard the cruise (018-016). Mrs. Valentine socialises with Colonel Drew (006-023) and maintains close acquaintance with Peggy Gray (022-007, 022-030). Mr. Valentine positions himself as a mediator in the shipboard meetings, balancing concern for safety with respect for Brewster as host (026-018, 026-033). Both maintain a cordial but increasingly strained relationship with Brewster as their situation deteriorates.

## Significance

The Valentines embody the social norms that Brewster's experiment in profligate spending both depends upon and strains against. As official chaperons, they are the guarantors of propriety — the people whose presence makes the cruise socially permissible. Their gradual loss of composure mirrors the broader collapse of order aboard the *Flora Belle*, and Mr. Valentine's candid regret (028-028) serves as a quiet indictment of the venture's excess. Together, they represent the conventional world that Brewster risks alienating through his inheritance scheme.
