## Overview

Colonel Prentiss Drew is one of the most powerful men in New York — a railroad magnate whose name carries weight in both financial and social circles. As the father of Barbara Drew and a director of the bank where Brewster deposits his inheritance, Colonel Drew occupies a pivotal position in the novel: he is simultaneously a social gatekeeper, a financial adversary, and a measuring stick for Monty's success or failure.

Drew embodies the established wealth and power structure that the newly enriched Brewster must navigate. His initial warmth toward Monty cools noticeably as the young man's financial recklessness threatens the stability of the institutions Drew has built. The Colonel's arc traces the shift from paternalistic advisor to wary observer, and ultimately to someone who must reckon with the consequences of Brewster's spending on his own interests.

## Role in the Story

Colonel Drew first appears at the directors' meeting following Brewster's inheritance, where he extends avuncular advice to the young heir (002-008). His initial posture is that of a seasoned man of affairs guiding a novice — he tells Monty, "You have a fortune and, with judgment, you ought to be able to double it" (002-010).

The Colonel's relationship with Monty deepens through two channels: business and romance. On the business side, Drew is a director of the bank that holds Brewster's deposits, and Monty's reckless withdrawals during the stock market panic put Drew in an acutely uncomfortable position (013-004). On the personal side, Drew is Barbara's father, and Monty's courtship of Barbara creates obligations and expectations that complicate their financial dealings.

The climactic crisis comes when a run on the bank threatens to bring down Drew's institution. Brewster, despite his mandate to lose money, cannot bring himself to withdraw his $200,000 deposit — an act that would ruin Colonel Drew. This moment crystallizes the central moral tension of the novel: the obligation to one's social circle versus the demands of the inheritance challenge (013-006). Colonel Drew's quiet desperation during the bank run — "Brewster, it looks bad," he says, turning abruptly (013-021) — is one of the novel's most human moments, revealing the vulnerability beneath the magnate's commanding exterior.

After the crisis passes, the Colonel's regard for Monty shifts. The young man who seemed a frivolous spendthrift has demonstrated loyalty at a critical juncture, and Drew's subsequent interactions carry a new note of respect (018-004).

## Relationships

**Barbara Drew** — Colonel Drew's daughter is the object of Monty's romantic pursuit. The Colonel's affection for Barbara is evident in his indulgence of her social ambitions, including the magnificent ball he supports (015-002). His relationship with his daughter also shapes his view of Brewster: a father does not easily entrust his child to a man who seems determined to ruin himself.

**Montgomery Brewster** — The relationship between the Colonel and Monty is the novel's most complex cross-generational dynamic. Drew begins as a mentor figure ("Don't listen to any of them," he advises about those who would exploit Monty's wealth (002-012)), but becomes increasingly bewildered by Monty's extravagance. Their interactions at lunch, at dinner parties, and finally in the bank crisis trace an arc from paternalism through alarm to something approaching respect.

**Mrs. Valentine** — A fellow member of the social elite, Mrs. Valentine appears alongside Colonel Drew at Monty's celebrated dinner party (006-023). Their social circle is the arena in which Monty must prove himself.

**The Bank Directors** — Colonel Drew's relationships with his fellow directors, particularly during the panic, reveal him as a leader under pressure. His calm exterior masks deep concern as depositors flee (013-009), and his colleagues look to him for decisions that could make or break the institution.

## Significance

Colonel Drew serves three critical narrative functions. First, he is the **embodiment of established capital** — the old guard of wealth and power that Monty's inherited fortune places him in tension with. The railroad magnate represents what Monty might become (or fail to become) through the responsible stewardship of wealth.

Second, Drew provides the novel's most pointed **moral test**. When Brewster refuses to withdraw his funds from Drew's bank during the panic, sacrificing a clear path to winning the inheritance challenge, the moment affirms that personal loyalty outweighs financial calculation. Drew's bank is a proxy for the social fabric that wealth is meant to serve — and Monty's choice to protect it, even at his own expense, is the novel's central ethical statement (013-022).

Third, as Barbara's father, Drew is the **romantic gatekeeper** whose approval Monty must earn. The Colonel's journey from skepticism to acceptance mirrors the reader's own assessment of Brewster's character — and when Drew ultimately comes to respect the young man, it signals that Brewster has passed the tests that matter.

## Background

Colonel Drew's background as a "railroad magnate" (002-008) places him in the generation of industrialists who built America's infrastructure in the late nineteenth century. The title "Colonel" likely reflects either military service (perhaps in the Civil War, which would align with his presumed birth circa 1850–1855) or an honorific common among Southern gentlemen and industrialists of the period.

His presence on the boards of both a railroad and a Manhattan bank reflects the interconnected nature of Gilded Age finance, where transportation, banking, and industry were controlled by overlapping circles of the same elite families. Drew's social position — hosting dinners, attending balls, and moving effortlessly through New York's highest circles — is inseparable from his financial power.
