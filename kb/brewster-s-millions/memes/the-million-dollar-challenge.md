# The Million Dollar Challenge

## Overview

The million-dollar challenge is the engine that drives the entire plot of *Brewster's Millions*. James T. Sedgwick's will sets an extraordinary condition: Montgomery Brewster must spend every penny of his grandfather Edwin Peter Brewster's one-million-dollar inheritance within twelve months, ending on September 23rd — his twenty-sixth birthday — with no possessions beyond the clothes on his back. Only then will he inherit Sedgwick's own fortune of nearly seven million dollars.

## The Sedgwick Will

The will was read to Brewster by Mr. Grant of Grant & Ripley, the law firm representing the Sedgwick estate. It bequeathed everything to Montgomery Brewster, "son of Robert and Louise Sedgwick Brewster, his only nephew" (004-018), but with a condition that transformed what would have been a straightforward inheritance into a moral and practical puzzle.

The will stipulated that Brewster must have "no other worldly possession than the clothes which covered him on the September day named" (004-019). He was required to give "satisfactory evidence" to the executor, Swearengen Jones, "that he was capable of adapting himself" to the demands of the condition (004-020).

The motive was personal: James Sedgwick hated Edwin Peter Brewster. As the narrator explains, "it was more than apparent that he expected the young man to be enriched liberally by his enemy. It was to preclude any possible chance of the mingling of his fortune with the smallest portion of Edwin P. Brewster's" (004-019).

## The Arithmetic

The challenge can be expressed in simple terms: spend one million dollars in one year, end with nothing, and earn seven million. As Brewster put it to Mr. Grant: "Wouldn't you exchange a million for seven millions, Mr. Grant?" (004-031).

But the arithmetic concealed a trap. As Mr. Grant pointed out, it would be "no easy task to spend a million dollars without in some way violating the restrictions in your uncle's will, thereby losing both fortunes" (004-037). The rules prohibited Brewster from giving the money away, gambling it, or destroying it — he had to receive fair value for every dollar spent.

Brewster's initial confidence — "All I have to do is to cut a few purse strings and there is but one natural conclusion" (004-033) — gives way to the sobering realisation that spending a million dollars honestly in one year is genuinely difficult. "All his life had been spent in wondering how to get enough money to pay his bills, and it had not occurred to him that it might be as difficult to spend as to acquire wealth" (005-002).

## The Rules

The challenge operated under unwritten but enforceable constraints:
- Brewster could not simply give the money away
- He could not gamble it recklessly
- He could not destroy valuable property
- He must receive "value" for every expenditure
- By September 23rd, he must possess nothing beyond the clothes on his back

These constraints, enforced by the vigilant executor Swearengen Jones, transform what might have been a simple exercise in dissipation into a genuine test of character and ingenuity.

## Plot Function

The million-dollar challenge serves multiple narrative functions. Structurally, it provides the novel's central dramatic question: will Brewster succeed? Comedically, it generates the increasingly absurd spending schemes — from lavish balls to automobile fleets to Mediterranean cruises. Thematically, it interrogates what money means: can a million dollars be reduced to nothing while retaining moral integrity?

The challenge also creates dramatic irony: Brewster's friends see his spending as reckless profligacy, while the reader knows it is cold calculation. Every extravagant gesture — the French chef, the Florentine ball, the yacht — is an act of deliberate financial strategy, not mere waste.

## The Stakes

Failure means more than embarrassment. If Brewster fails, he will have spent his grandfather's million and forfeited Sedgwick's seven — leaving him with nothing but the memory of a year's indulgence. As he reflected during the becalmed cruise in the Atlantic: "He had given up all hope of the Sedgwick legacy and was trying to be resigned to his fate" (028-042).

## Historical Precedent

While McCutcheon's novel is fiction, the premise echoes real anxieties of the Gilded Age: the fear that inherited wealth corrupts, the suspicion that spending is morally suspect, and the faith that a test of character can separate the worthy from the unworthy heir. The challenge is, in essence, a moral trial dressed in financial terms.

## Associated Characters

- **Montgomery Brewster** — the challenger, bound by Sedgwick's condition
- **James T. Sedgwick** — the deceased uncle whose hatred of Edwin Brewster created the challenge
- **Edwin Peter Brewster** — the grandfather whose million-dollar bequest becomes the obstacle
- **Swearengen Jones** — the executor in Montana who alone judges whether Brewster has succeeded
- **Oliver Harrison** — Brewster's friend and financial manager, who "retained" himself to stand between Brewster and financial ruin
