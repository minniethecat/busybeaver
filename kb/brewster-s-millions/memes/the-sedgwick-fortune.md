## Overview

The Sedgwick Fortune is both the novel's premise and its central mystery. [[james-t-sedgwick|James T. Sedgwick]] — a man who left [[new-york-city|New York]] in obscurity and built a mining empire in [[montana|Montana]] — died possessed of an [[inheritance|estate]] valued at nearly seven million dollars (004-006). His last will and testament, handwritten on a scrap of paper and remarkably unspecific about the source of his wealth (004-008), bequeaths everything to his nephew [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]] — on one extraordinary condition.

The will stipulates that Brewster must first prove his ability to "dispose of" one million dollars. He must spend (or lose) the entire sum within one year, and at the end of that year he must have nothing to show for it — no property, no investments, no hidden assets. If he succeeds, he inherits the full seven million. If he fails, he gets nothing. This challenge — at once a test of character and a dark joke about the corrosive power of wealth — drives every event in the novel.

## Role in the Novel

**The will's revelation**: The news of Sedgwick's death and the terms of his will arrive through [[grant-and-ripley|Grant & Ripley]], [[grant-and-ripley|the law firm]] handling the [[inheritance|estate]] (003-044). The letter, received at Brewster's birthday dinner, transforms an evening of celebration into the beginning of an ordeal. The "impenetrable injunction of [[james-t-sedgwick|James Sedgwick]]" is "plainly discernible" — Brewster must take the million and dispose of it entirely (004-019).

**The spending campaign**: The fortune fuels Brewster's extraordinary campaign of expenditure: the apartments, the dinners, [[the-yacht-flora-belle|the yacht]], the ball, the reckless investments. But the will's conditions prove maddeningly difficult to satisfy. Every attempt to lose money — the lumber scheme, the [[speculation|stock market]] speculation — seems to generate more wealth. The fortune, in McCutcheon's satirical vision, has a perverse momentum of its own.

**The final reckoning**: In the novel's closing chapters, Brewster discovers that the fortune has dwindled to "a few paltry thousands" (029-012). In a final desperate act, he borrows money from [[joe-bragdon|Joe Bragdon]] to buy a marriage license — spending the last dollar at the last possible moment (030-027). [[swearengen-jones|Jones]]'s final accounting reveals that Brewster has succeeded: the million is gone, and the full seven million is his. But the fortune is no longer what matters most — Brewster has learned, through the ordeal of spending, what wealth cannot buy.

**The fortune's true source**: In a late revelation, [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] discloses that he personally assembled "all of [[james-t-sedgwick|Jim Sedgwick]]'s stuff" (034-039) — suggesting that [[swearengen-jones|the executor]] knew far more about the [[inheritance|estate]] than the will alone conveyed. The fortune's origins in [[montana|Montana]] mining connect it to the extractive economy of the American West, a world utterly remote from the [[new-york-city|New York]] drawing rooms where it is spent.

## Historical Context

Seven million dollars in 1900 would be equivalent to roughly $250 million today — a fortune large enough to place Sedgwick among the wealthiest men of his era, though not in the Vanderbilt or Rockefeller tier. The source of the wealth — [[montana|Montana]] mining — reflects the historical reality of the Copper Kings and silver barons who built vast fortunes from the mineral resources of the American West.

The novel's legal premise — a conditional [[inheritance|bequest]] requiring the heir to spend money rather than preserve it — plays on contemporary anxieties about inherited wealth. The Gilded Age saw the first generation of American fortunes pass from their founders to their heirs, and the question of whether inherited wealth corrupted character was a live cultural debate. McCutcheon's twist — that spending recklessly is harder than it looks — offers a comic critique of the very idea that wealth is easy come, easy go.
