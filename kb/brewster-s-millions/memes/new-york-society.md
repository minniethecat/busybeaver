# New York Society

## Overview

New York society at the turn of the twentieth century provides the stage on which *Brewster's Millions* plays out. It is a world governed by wealth, appearance, and the judgment of one's peers — a world in which Montgomery Brewster's million-dollar spending spree is simultaneously celebrated and condemned. The novel's society is a collective character: curious, fickle, and relentlessly attentive to the fortunes of its members. Brewster's challenge is not merely to spend money but to do so under the watchful gaze of a social world that measures worth in dollars.

## In the Novel

### The Social Stage

Brewster moves through a New York in which social standing is everything. He takes "the most expensive apartments to be found in New York City" at a rental of $23,000 (006-002), establishing himself as a figure of consequence. His spending attracts immediate attention from "the men and women whose names filled the society columns" (011-007). The society pages function as a kind of public scoreboard, recording who spends what and what it signifies.

The charity bazaar at the Astoria becomes a focal point for society display: "Everyone was interested in the charity bazaar at the Astoria. Society was there in all its glory" (011-007). At such events, wealth is performed rather than merely possessed. Brewster's participation in this world is both natural — he was born to it — and newly complicated by his secret mission.

### Judgment and Gossip

New York society in the novel is above all a tribunal of reputation. Brewster's friends are "worried" by his "reckless extravagance" (006-015). His behavior becomes the subject of dinner-table conversation and newspaper speculation. The social world judges without knowing the facts, and Brewster cannot defend himself without revealing the challenge.

This creates a dramatic irony: the reader knows that Brewster's wastefulness is calculated, but society sees only a young man squandering his inheritance. The gap between private motive and public perception drives much of the novel's tension. When Miss Drew "forgets" Brewster after his financial notoriety (012-033), it is society's judgment, not her own, that she reflects.

### The Cost of Spectacle

The novel suggests that New York society is both audience and antagonist. Brewster must perform extravagance for its sake — his spending must be visible to be believed — yet the same society that applauds his parties also condemns his profligacy. The social world is a double-edged sword: necessary for the challenge's legitimacy, but dangerous to Brewster's reputation and relationships.

The executor Swearengen Jones, watching from distant Montana, represents an alternative to New York values. Where society judges by appearances, Jones judges by facts and ledgers. The contrast between Montana plainness and New York sophistication runs through the novel as a quiet moral commentary.
