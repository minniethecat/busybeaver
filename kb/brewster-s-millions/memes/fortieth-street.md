# Fortieth Street

## Overview

Fortieth Street is the site of Mrs. Gray's boarding house, where Montgomery Brewster lives before receiving his grandfather's inheritance. A modest New York City address in a neighbourhood of "delicatessen shops and apartment houses" (003-002), Fortieth Street represents the world of ordinary middle-class respectability that Brewster leaves behind — and ultimately returns to.

## In the Novel

Fortieth Street is introduced in the opening pages as Brewster's residence: "Mrs. Gray lived in Fortieth Street. For years Montgomery Brewster had regarded her as his closest friend and advisor" (003-002). The property is described as "her only possession" (003-003), underscoring the financial modesty of the household.

After receiving news of his grandfather's death, Brewster "hastened, by electric car, to Fortieth Street and Broadway" (003-005), the journey itself marking his passage between worlds — from the financial district where the will is read to the domestic sphere where his emotional life resides.

The street reappears at the novel's conclusion, providing an emotional counterpoint to the extravagant settings of the middle chapters. "Up in Fortieth Street Peggy Gray was grieving her heart out" (029-021), anchoring the narrative's resolution in domestic emotion rather than financial spectacle. When Brewster's inheritance experiment concludes, "the old rooms in Fortieth Street were still open to him" (029-023), symbolising the enduring value of the relationships formed there over the transient wealth he has abandoned.

## Historical Context

Fortieth Street in Manhattan, circa 1900, was a residential thoroughfare in the rapidly developing Midtown area. The neighbourhood's character — "delicatessen shops and apartment houses" (003-002) — places it firmly in the middle-class residential belt of turn-of-the-century New York, a world away from the Fifth Avenue mansions that Brewster briefly inhabits as a millionaire. Today, West 40th Street runs through Midtown Manhattan, its 1900 boarding-house character long since replaced by commercial development.
