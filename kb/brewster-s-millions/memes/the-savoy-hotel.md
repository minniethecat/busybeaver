# The Savoy Hotel

## Overview

The Savoy Hotel in London stood at the summit of late-Victorian and Edwardian hotel luxury. Opened in 1889, it was the first London hotel lit entirely by electricity, with en-suite bathrooms on every floor — a radical innovation. By 1902, when *Brewster's Millions* was published, the Savoy had become synonymous with the highest tier of international hospitality.

## In the Novel

The Savoy does not appear as a physical setting in *Brewster's Millions*, but rather functions as a reference point for the kind of luxury Brewster inhabits and exceeds. After learning of his inheritance, Brewster "had gone to his own rooms, forgetting that he lived on Fifth Avenue" (005-018) — and the implied comparison between the wealthy New York lifestyle and the grand hotels of Europe is part of the novel's satire.

The hotel's reputation as a standard of luxury is implicitly invoked whenever Brewster's spending crosses into the territory of European-style extravagance. His importation of a French chef from Paris (006-013) and fleet of French automobiles (022-002) mirrors the Savoy's own cosmopolitan approach to luxury — notably its employment of Auguste Escoffier, the most famous French chef of the era.

## Historical Context

The Savoy Hotel was managed by César Ritz and employed Auguste Escoffier as head chef from 1890 to 1897. The Ritz-Escoffier partnership at the Savoy revolutionized hotel dining, making the hotel restaurant a social destination in its own right — not merely an amenity for guests. The Savoy's reputation for French cuisine, electric lighting, and modern plumbing set the template for luxury hotels worldwide.

McCutcheon's readers would have understood a reference to the Savoy as shorthand for the highest possible standard of accommodation and service, the kind of establishment where a "millionaire with million-dollar inclinations" (005-001) would naturally find himself at home.
