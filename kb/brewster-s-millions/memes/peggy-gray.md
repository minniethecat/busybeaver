# Peggy Gray

## Character Overview

Peggy Gray is the moral anchor of *Brewster's Millions* — a young woman whose loyalty to Montgomery Brewster does not waver even when his behavior becomes inexplicable to everyone around him. Unlike Barbara Drew, whose affection is mediated by social standing, Peggy's attachment is personal and unconditional. She and her mother live in modest circumstances on Fortieth Street, their "meager fortune" (013-027) a quiet counterpoint to the millions that dominate Brewster's life.

## Role in the Novel

### Steadfast Loyalty

Peggy has known Brewster for years before the novel's action begins. The narrator notes that "for years Montgomery Brewster had regarded her quiet, old-fashioned home as a refuge" (003-002). Their relationship predates his inheritance and is therefore immune to its fluctuations.

When Brewster's spending becomes notorious, Peggy does not withdraw. Unlike the society figures who judge by appearances, she trusts in the man she knows. Her loyalty is blind in the best sense — it does not require explanation because it is rooted in character rather than circumstance.

### Love and Rivalry

The novel sets up an implicit rivalry between Peggy and Barbara Drew for Brewster's affection. Barbara represents the world of society and spectacle; Peggy represents the world of personal connection and steadfastness. Brewster's thoughts "treacherously wandered off to Peggy Gray, and then everything was bright again" (025-021), suggesting that Peggy offers an emotional refuge that Barbara's world cannot provide.

Peggy's feelings for Brewster are complicated by pride. She and her mother have "meager fortune" and pride "alone stood between them and the acceptance of favors" (003-003). She will not accept charity, and Brewster knows that offering her money would wound her: "She'd hate me if I offered it" (003-035).

### The Novel's Moral Center

Peggy Gray represents what money cannot buy. In a novel organized around the destruction of wealth, Peggy's constancy suggests that the most valuable things — loyalty, love, character — are not financial assets. She is the novel's answer to the question posed by Sedgwick's challenge: what remains when the money is gone?

In the novel's resolution, Brewster's discovery that true worth lies outside the ledger is inseparable from his recognition of what Peggy has always represented.
