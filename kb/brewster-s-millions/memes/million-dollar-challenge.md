# The Million-Dollar Challenge

## Overview

The Million-Dollar Challenge is the central narrative premise of *Brewster's Millions*. It refers to the extraordinary condition in the last will of James T. Sedgwick: his sole heir, Montgomery Brewster, must spend his entire inherited fortune of one million dollars within a single year and arrive penniless on his twenty-sixth birthday in order to receive the far greater Sedgwick estate worth nearly seven million dollars.

The challenge is not merely financial — it is also a test of character. Sedgwick's will imposes strict behavioral conditions: Brewster must maintain a fair reputation, avoid serious dissipation, demonstrate business acumen, and neither donate nor give away money in ways that might later be returned to him (004-020). The challenge thus becomes a paradox: to gain enormous wealth, Brewster must first destroy a smaller fortune while remaining morally and socially upright.

## In the Novel

### The Will's Revelation

The challenge is first revealed to Brewster at the offices of Grant & Ripley, the law firm handling Sedgwick's estate. Mr. Grant informs him that James Sedgwick died possessed of an estate "valued at almost seven million dollars" (004-006), accumulated through gold mines and ranches in the Northwest. The will names Montgomery Brewster as sole heir — but with an extraordinary condition attached.

The "supreme condition" requires that on September 23rd, Brewster's twenty-sixth birthday, he must possess nothing beyond the clothes on his back: "no other worldly possession than the clothes which covered him on the September day named" (004-019). At nine o'clock that morning, the executor Swearengen Jones is to transfer the entire Sedgwick fortune to Brewster — but only if he has fully complied with every requirement of the will.

### The Motive: A Family Feud

Behind the challenge lies a bitter enmity. James Sedgwick despised Edwin Peter Brewster, Montgomery's grandfather, a wealthy banker. The will explicitly aims to prevent "any possible chance of the mingling of his fortune with the smallest portion of Edwin P. Brewster's" (004-019). Sedgwick anticipated that his nephew would receive money from the hated Brewster and constructed his will to force the young man to purge himself of that tainted inheritance before receiving the Sedgwick fortune.

The irony is that Edwin Brewster had indeed bequeathed exactly one million dollars to his grandson — the very sum that must now be destroyed (004-019).

### The Spending Campaign

Brewster initially views the challenge as straightforward: "Don't you suppose I can get rid of a million in a year? Great Scott, who wouldn't do it!" (004-033). But he soon discovers the difficulty. He cannot simply give the money away, for Sedgwick's conditions forbid "endowments" and require him to "give sparingly to charity" (004-020). He must genuinely consume the money — getting "his money's worth" for every expenditure.

As the year progresses, the challenge consumes Brewster's life. The spending becomes a public spectacle and a personal burden. His friend "Subway" Smith marvels at his profligacy: "he spends it like a Chicago millionaire" (006-002). The task that seemed simple becomes a grueling race against both the calendar and Brewster's own good fortune, as some of his investments unexpectedly prosper, returning money he has tried to lose.

### The Stakes

If Brewster fails, the seven-million-dollar estate will be "distributed among certain institutions of charity" (004-019). The alternative is stark: "Suppose I succeed in spending all but a thousand dollars before the 23rd of next September! I'd lose the seven millions and be the next thing to a pauper" (005-008). Brewster must walk a tightrope — spend too little and fail the condition; spend too much and miss the thousand-dollar threshold, ending up with nothing.

## Key Passages

> "It set forth that Montgomery Brewster was to have no other worldly possession than the clothes which covered him on the September day named. He was to begin that day without a penny to his name, without a single article of jewelry, furniture or finance that he could call his own or could thereafter reclaim." (004-019)

> "It was to preclude any possible chance of the mingling of his fortune with the smallest portion of Edwin P. Brewster's that James Sedgwick, on his deathbed, put his hand to this astonishing instrument." (004-019)

> "He required that the young man should give satisfactory evidence to the executor that he was capable of managing his affairs shrewdly and wisely — that he possessed the ability to add to the fortune through his own enterprise." (004-020)

> "Suppose I succeed in spending all but a thousand dollars before the 23rd of next September! I'd lose the seven millions and be the next thing to a pauper. That wouldn't be quite like getting my money's worth." (005-008)

## Analysis

The Million-Dollar Challenge operates as more than a plot device — it is a moral crucible that tests the nature of wealth and the character of those who pursue it. Sedgwick, a self-made man who built his fortune from nothing, constructs a test that forces his heir to experience both extremes of fortune: the apparent abundance of a million dollars, and the literal poverty of owning nothing but one's clothes.

The challenge inverts the conventional rags-to-riches narrative. Brewster does not climb from poverty to wealth; instead, he must descend from wealth to pennilessness to win an even greater fortune. The journey strips away his inherited assumptions about money and forces him to confront what it truly means to have "nothing."

Sedgwick's conditions also serve as a critique of inherited wealth. By requiring Brewster to demonstrate business capability and maintain temperate habits, the will imposes a meritocratic filter on what would otherwise be a simple inheritance. The fortune cannot be received passively — it must be earned through a year of disciplined destruction.
