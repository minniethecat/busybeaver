## Overview

Edwin Peter Brewster is the grandfather of Montgomery Brewster and the architect of the million-dollar challenge that drives the novel's plot. A wealthy man who never forgave his daughter for eloping, he took in his orphaned grandson but kept him at arm's length — only to reveal, after his death, a scheme of extraordinary complexity designed to test Monty's character.

## Role in the Story

Edwin Peter Brewster first appears in the narrative through backstory: he was the father of Monty's mother, who made a 「romantic runaway match」 with Monty's father in the 1870s (001-003). Brewster never forgave his daughter for the elopement, and his long relentlessness became the talk of the town. After both parents died, he took the boy into his own house and cared for him with what the narrator dryly calls 「affection」 (001-004).

On the night of Monty's twenty-fifth birthday, Edwin Peter Brewster suffers a sudden heart attack and dies (001-039). His will — signed on the day of his death — reveals that he possessed an estate valued at approximately seven million dollars (004-006, 004-008). The will stipulates that Monty must spend one million dollars within a year, keeping no assets, to inherit the remaining seven million (004-014). Brewster appointed James T. Sedgwick — a man he hated — as co-executor alongside Swearengen Jones, ensuring that Monty's actions would be scrutinised by an unsympathetic observer (004-014).

His influence extends beyond the grave through the challenge he designed. The September 23rd deadline, the prohibition on retaining assets, and the requirement that Monty tell no one of the terms all originate from Edwin Peter Brewster's elaborate scheme (004-018, 004-019).

## Relationships

Brewster's most significant relationship is with his grandson Monty — a bond marked by duty rather than warmth. His decision to make Monty his sole heir suggests a grudging recognition of family obligation. His relationship with James T. Sedgwick is defined by mutual hostility: Sedgwick is described as a man Brewster hated, and Brewster's choice to make him executor appears calculated to add difficulty to Monty's challenge (004-014).

## Significance

Edwin Peter Brewster functions as a posthumous puppet-master — his will transforms Monty's life from comfortable ease into a frantic race against time. His character embodies the novel's themes of wealth, control, and the testing of character. The elaborate conditions of his will suggest a man who, despite his coldness, believed his grandson capable of proving himself worthy of a great fortune.
