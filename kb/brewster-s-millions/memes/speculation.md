# Speculation

## Overview

Financial speculation — the buying and selling of stocks in hopes of profit — plays an ironic role in *Brewster's Millions*. Montgomery Brewster enters the stock market hoping to lose money, only to find that his speculative ventures turn unexpected profits. The Lumber and Fuel episode exemplifies the novel's comic logic: when Brewster tries to destroy his fortune, the market conspires to preserve it. Speculation thus becomes a metaphor for the perversity of wealth — money, once acquired, resists being spent.

## In the Novel

### The Lumber and Fuel Episode

Brewster's most dramatic encounter with speculation comes through Lumber and Fuel, a stock he targets for a losing investment. On the advice of his stockbroker friend Elon Gardner, he takes a large position, expecting the stock to decline. Instead, the market moves against him: "He had realized heavily in his investments which caused his friends and his broker to wonder" (011-002). The very act of investing — which for most people carries the risk of loss — becomes for Brewster a source of unwanted gain.

The narrator captures the absurdity: "Nobody looked for a sensation in Lumber and Fuel. It was a foregone conclusion" (010-014). Yet sensation is exactly what Brewster gets. The stock rises against all expectation: "In ten minutes' time Lumber and Fuel was up to 113" (010-031). What should have been a losing speculation becomes a winning one, complicating Brewster's challenge.

### The Paradox of Losing Money

Brewster's problem is that speculation, by its nature, can produce either gain or loss. The executor Swearengen Jones has explicitly forbidden "idiotic Board of Trade speculation" (005-055), recognizing that deliberate loss-making in the markets would violate the spirit of the challenge. Yet even when Brewster tries to invest prudently, his choices go awry — in the wrong direction.

His broker friend Gardner monitors the market obsessively: "stealthily he watched the stock market and plied his friends with questions" (010-006). The stock market becomes a second antagonist in the novel, as unpredictable and implacable as Swearengen Jones himself.

### Speculation and Character

The novel draws a distinction between Sedgwick's legitimate wealth — built through "shrewd, careful, and thrifty" management of mines and ranches (004-008) — and the speculative fever of Wall Street. Sedgwick's conditions require Brewster to demonstrate business capability, but the stock market offers only luck disguised as skill. The Lumber and Fuel episode suggests that speculation reveals character: Brewster's attempt to lose money through speculation shows him to be neither shrewd nor thrifty, but merely fortunate — and fortune, in this novel, is a fickle ally.
