## Overview

Dan DeMille is a prominent figure in Brewster's social circle, appearing first as the husband of Mrs. Dan DeMille — the "social mentor and utility" enlisted to guide Brewster through New York high society (006-022). He later becomes an integral part of the yacht cruise that forms the central adventure of the novel's second half.

## Role in the Story

Dan DeMille enters the narrative through his wife, who Brewster hires to manage his social affairs. Mrs. DeMille "handled her people without gloves" and arranged the seating at Brewster's dinner parties with "the skill of a general" (007-020) (006-023). Dan DeMille appears alongside her in these early social scenes.

When Brewster embarks on his extended yacht cruise aboard the *Flora Belle*, Dan DeMille is among the party, and his presence is noted with "surprise" by those who believed him unlikely to sail (018-017) (018-016). During the voyage, DeMille proves himself an active and often comic participant. He gathers in money during a gambling scene aboard the yacht (020-037), and later blames himself "relentlessly" for the danger that befalls Peggy Gray when she is swept overboard (025-015).

DeMille serves as chairman of an impromptu meeting among the passengers during the cruise, and acts as a spokesman for the group in confronting Brewster (026-009). When a conspiracy is uncovered among the passengers, DeMille cries out about it "with no anger in his voice" — more amused than offended (028-015). After the cruise returns to New York, DeMille asks the entire party to stay an extra day at his request (029-003).

In the novel's closing chapters, DeMille and his wife appear at the theater by Brewster's invitation (032-018), and attend Brewster's wedding to Peggy Gray without formal invitation (034-021). In the climactic final scene, DeMille clutches Joe Bragdon by the arm and exclaims, "Good heaven, it is so much easier to save a million than to make a million in New York" (034-027).

## Relationships

Dan DeMille's most defining relationship is with his wife, **Mrs. Dan DeMille**, who is the more socially dominant of the pair. She orchestrates their social engagements while Dan plays the role of the amiable, fashionable husband. Their marriage is portrayed as companionable and mutually supportive.

With **Montgomery Brewster**, DeMille has a friendly, somewhat mercenary relationship. He and his wife are among the many drawn into Brewster's orbit by the young man's wealth and entertaining lifestyle. DeMille acts as an intermediary between Brewster and the other guests on the yacht, and is trusted enough to be invited to key social events.

During the cruise, DeMille develops a particular dynamic with **Reginald Vanderpool**, who playfully calls out "Will you be good?" to DeMille as Brewster descends from the upper deck (027-002). His friendship with **Joe Bragdon** is evident in the final scene where the two men share the novel's closing observation about the difficulty of making — versus spending — a fortune (034-027).

## Significance

Dan DeMille functions as a representative of fashionable New York society that clusters around Brewster's wealth. Unlike some of the more mercenary hangers-on, DeMille is portrayed with warmth and humor — he is a social creature who genuinely enjoys the company and adventure that Brewster's fortune enables. His presence on the yacht cruise supplies comic relief and a viewpoint character through whom the reader experiences the increasingly surreal events of Brewster's spending spree. His final observation about the relative ease of losing versus making a million serves as an ironic coda to the novel's central conceit.
