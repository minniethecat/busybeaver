## Overview

The chartered steam-yacht is Brewster's most audacious indulgence — a floating palace that carries his spending spree from New York to the Mediterranean and back. Known alternately as the *Flitter* and the *Flora Belle*, the vessel belongs to Reginald Brown and costs Brewster $212,309.50 for the full cruise (031-018) — his single largest expense in the inheritance challenge. The yacht transforms from a pleasure craft into a theater of crisis: mutiny, kidnapping, and the desperate race back to New York before the deadline.

More than a setting, the yacht is a sealed world where Brewster's character is tested away from the judgments of New York society. At sea, his choices have immediate, physical consequences — and it is on the yacht that he proves his courage, his loyalty to Peggy, and his willingness to risk everything for those he loves.

## In the Novel

**Charter and departure**: The yacht enters Brewster's plans as an escape from the exhausting social round of New York dinners (012-036). He charters the *Flitter*, a steam-yacht belonging to Reginald Brown, and assembles a party of friends — Joe Bragdon, Elon Gardner, "Subway" Smith, Dr. Lotless, and others. The vessel sails "out of the harbor of New York" to cheers, "a merry crowd" embarking on what they imagine will be a pleasure cruise (018-015).

**Life at sea**: The yacht becomes a floating society, complete with its own band that "fairly outdid Sousa" (020-019). Brewster entertains French and Italian dignitaries aboard (024-037), and the vessel anchors at Naples, the Riviera, and other Mediterranean ports. But the expenses mount relentlessly: repairs, provisions, and the sheer cost of maintaining a private steamer consume the fortune at an alarming rate.

**The mutiny**: The cruise turns dark when the yacht is seized by mutineers — Arab crew members who had been taken aboard at a Mediterranean port. Brewster and Bragdon fight back with rifles (024-054), but Peggy Gray is kidnapped and carried away in a small boat (025-002). The sequence is the novel's most sustained action, transforming the pleasure yacht into a zone of genuine danger.

**The rescue and aftermath**: Brewster leads the rescue of Peggy, an act of physical courage that stands apart from his financial exploits. After the crisis, the yacht limps onward but the mood has changed. The repairs and the return voyage to New York cost Brewster an additional $40,000 (029-012), leaving him with only a few thousand dollars as the deadline approaches.

## Historical Context

Private steam-yachts were the ultimate status symbol of the Gilded Age. J.P. Morgan's *Corsair* (343 feet) and William K. Vanderbilt's *Valiant* set the standard for floating luxury. The *Flitter* — a fictional vessel — would have been a smaller but still imposing craft, likely 150–200 feet, with steam engines, a crew of twenty or more, and accommodations for a dozen guests. The charter cost of over $200,000 (equivalent to roughly $6–7 million today) reflects the genuinely astronomical expense of operating such vessels.

The yacht's itinerary — New York to the Mediterranean and back — follows the actual routes taken by wealthy Americans on the "Grand Tour" circuit. Naples, the French Riviera, and the Canary Islands were standard ports of call.
