## Overview

The Bank Run of Chapter 13 is the novel's most dramatic financial crisis and Brewster's finest moral hour. After a bank failure wipes out $100,000 of his inheritance — a loss he welcomes as progress toward his goal — the panic spreads to the Bank of [[new-york-city|Manhattan]] Island, where [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Prentiss Drew]], Barbara's father, serves as president (013-002). As depositors mob the tellers and the bank teeters on the edge of collapse, Brewster faces an agonizing choice: withdraw his remaining $200,000 to preserve his spending campaign, or deposit even more of his fortune to restore confidence and save the institution — knowing that if the bank fails, he loses everything and fails the challenge (013-024).

He chooses to save the bank, on one condition: "Barbara must never know of this" (013-026).

## Narrative

The crisis begins with a stroke of apparent luck for Brewster. "One of the banks in which his money was deposited failed and his balance of over $100,000 was wiped out. Mismanagement was the cause and the collapse came on Friday, the thirteenth day of the month. Needless to say, it destroyed every vestige of the superstition he may have had regarding Friday and the number thirteen" (013-002). Brewster had spread his money across five banks, "a transaction inspired by the wild hope that one of them might some day suspend operations and thereby prove a legitimate benefit to him" (013-003).

But the failure triggers a chain reaction. "This failure caused a tremendous flurry in banking circles. It was but natural that questions concerning the stability of other banks should be asked, and it was not long before many wild, disquieting reports were afloat" (013-004). Rumors soon target the Bank of [[new-york-city|Manhattan]] Island, whose president is [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Prentiss Drew]].

"When the bank opened for business on the Tuesday following the failure, there was a stampede of frightened depositors. Before eleven o'clock the run had assumed ugly proportions and no amount of argument could stay the onslaught" (013-005). Brewster, with [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]] and Bragdon, goes to the bank. His friends urge him to withdraw: "Get out every dollar of it, [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]], and don't waste a minute. It's a smash as sure as fate" (013-012).

Instead, Brewster is summoned to [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Drew]]'s private office. The proud old railroad magnate is "pacing the floor like a caged animal" (013-012). Drew assures Brewster his money is safe but confesses: "another check like Austin's, which we paid a few minutes ago, would cause us serious, though temporary, embarrassment" (013-015). At that moment, an official bursts in: "Oglethorp wants to draw two hundred and fifty thousand dollars" (013-017) — and the bank cannot pay.

[[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Drew]] turns to Brewster. "The other banks are afraid of a run and we can't count on much help from them. Now, I not only ask you to refrain from drawing out your deposit, but I want you to help us in this crucial moment" (013-021). Brewster asks what he can do. The Colonel's answer is staggering: "You can restore absolute confidence, [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]], my dear boy, by increasing your deposits in our bank" (013-023).

Brewster's mind races. "Could he afford to risk the loss of his entire fortune on the fate of this bank? What would [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] say if he deliberately deposited a vast amount of money in a tottering institution like the Bank of [[new-york-city|Manhattan]] Island?" (013-024). Then he remembers: the bank holds every dollar belonging to [[mrs-gray|Mrs. Gray]] and [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] — their entire "meager fortune" (013-026).

"I will do all I can, Colonel," said [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]], "but upon one condition." "That is?" "Barbara must never know of this." The Colonel's gasp of astonishment was cut short as [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]] continued. "Promise that she shall never know." (013-026)

## Consequences

The Bank Run episode is a turning point in the novel's moral architecture:

1. **Brewster's character vindicated**: Throughout the novel, Brewster appears to his friends as a reckless fool squandering a fortune. Here, behind closed doors, he acts with genuine heroism — risking his entire inheritance to save not only the Drew family's honor but the life savings of hundreds of depositors, including the Gray family.

2. **The secrecy clause**: Brewster's demand that Barbara never learn of his rescue is the novel's purest act of selfless love. He saves her father's bank not to win her favor but despite knowing she has already rejected him. The secrecy ensures the act cannot be used as leverage in their relationship.

3. **The spending paradox**: Saving the bank by depositing more money is, perversely, one of the few actions that could genuinely threaten Brewster's challenge. If the bank had failed, he would have lost everything and failed the will's terms. His willingness to take that risk, putting human obligation above the challenge, reveals the man beneath the [[profligacy|spendthrift]] mask.

4. **The Gray family saved**: Brewster's recollection that [[mrs-gray|Mrs. Gray]] and [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] have their savings in the bank adds a layer of personal stakes beyond the Drew family drama. His rescue preserves the financial security of the two women who represent his moral anchor throughout the novel.
