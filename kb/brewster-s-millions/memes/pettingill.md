## Overview

Paul Pettingill is an artist and member of Montgomery Brewster's social circle. His studio serves as the setting for the birthday dinner in Chapter 1 that launches the novel's central plot (001-002). Pettingill combines the roles of friend, decorator, and occasional comic relief throughout Brewster's spending ordeal.

## Role in the Story

Pettingill first appears at the birthday dinner held in his studio, where he introduces Montgomery Brewster to the assembled guests (001-013). His role expands significantly when Brewster inherits the Sedgwick fortune and begins his frantic spending campaign. Brewster specifically instructs his associates to "have Pettingill redecorate it from top to bottom," referring to his new apartment (005-072).

Pettingill, to his own "intense surprise," finds himself commissioned to transform Brewster's living quarters (006-003). Unable to believe anyone could afford such extravagance, he remarks to himself that Brewster cannot possibly "afford anything" (006-005). Nevertheless, "Pettingill and a corps of decorators" soon set to work (006-006). He designs "the curiously warm and brilliant atmosphere" of the rooms (006-024) and devises a "scheme for lighting the room" (006-025).

As Brewster's spending campaign continues, Pettingill remains part of the entourage, with "great ideas for doing" various projects (012-028). He accompanies Brewster on the yachting expedition and is among the party when the *Flora Belle* is wrecked (018-016). In the quieter interludes, "Pettingill wandered about as though he cared not which way the wind blew" (021-020).

## Relationships

Pettingill's closest relationship is with Montgomery Brewster, for whom he serves as both artist-decorator and loyal companion. He is connected to the broader social circle including Nopper Harrison, whom Brewster silences "with work and Pettingill with opportunities" (015-023), and "Subway" Smith, with whom he appears in social gatherings (018-016).

The novel notes that "Pettingill had once lost his heart" in a romantic context (021-003), and he is linked to characters including Mrs. Dan and Mary Valentine (021-022). Brewster later recalls that "Pettingill had spoken of the Prestons" (021-031), suggesting a wide social network.

## Significance

As an artist figure, Pettingill represents the bohemian side of Brewster's social world, balancing the financial and legal characters who dominate the inheritance plot. His role as decorator turns Brewster's spending mandate into visible, aesthetic excess — the apartment renovation being one of the most concrete expressions of Brewster's attempt to dispose of the million dollars. His presence on the *Flora Belle* during the shipwreck episode places him at the center of the novel's most dramatic set piece (018-016).

## Notes

Pettingill's full first name, "Paul," is explicitly given at (006-003). The novel uses his surname alone in most passages. His studio at the opening of the novel suggests he was already an established artist before Brewster's inheritance transformed their relationship into one of patron and decorator.
