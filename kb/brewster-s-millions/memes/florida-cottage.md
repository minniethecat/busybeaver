## Location and Acquisition

The Florida cottage was a winter estate in St. Augustine belonging to a New York millionaire who had preferred Italy that season (016-002):

> It was the cottage of a New York millionaire which had fallen to Brewster. The owner had, for the time, preferred Italy to St. Augustine, and left his estate, which was well located and lavishly equipped, in the hands of his friends (016-002).

Brewster leased the property for three months "at a fabulous rate per month" (016-002). The decision to travel to Florida followed medical advice; doctors determined Brewster "would best recuperate in Florida, and advised a month at least in the warmth" (015-045).

## Physical Setting

The estate was described as spacious and luxuriously appointed — a millionaire's winter retreat (016-002):

> Brewster's lease covered three months, at a fabulous rate per month. With Joe Bragdon installed as manager-in-chief, his establishment was transferred bodily from New York, and the rooms were soon as comfortable as their grandeur would permit (016-002).

The estate's grounds featured woods, a beach road, and great iron gates at the entrance (016-039). It operated in stark contrast to the nearby the Ponce de Leon hotel, where [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]] and her mother had taken rooms.

## Brewster's Household

Brewster's party at the cottage included [[dr-lotless|Dr. Lotless]] and his sister, [[subway-smith|"Subway" Smith]], and the [[mrs-gray|Grays]] — Peggy Gray and her mother (016-002). [[joe-bragdon|Joe Bragdon]] served as manager-in-chief, overseeing the transfer of Brewster's household from New York.

The cottage was equipped with Brewster's horses and a new automobile shipped from New York, though Brewster himself was restricted by Dr. Lotless's orders (016-002):

> Brewster was not allowed to take advantage of his horses and the new automobile which preceded him from New York, but to his guests they offered unlimited opportunities (016-002).

## Convalescence and Social Life

Dr. Lotless imposed a "rigid diet, with most discouraging restrictions" on Brewster during the convalescence period (016-002). Confined indoors, Brewster whiled away the hours playing cards — preferring piquet with [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]] to bridge, which he dismissed as the "pons asinorum" (016-002).

The relaxed atmosphere of the cottage became the backdrop for significant personal conversations. During one card game, Peggy raised the subject of Brewster's estrangement from Barbara Drew, asking whether it wouldn't be "pleasanter to have them here?" (016-002).

## Peggy and Barbara's Confrontation

The cottage served as the setting for a pivotal narrative sequence — Peggy Gray's drive with Barbara Drew through the woods and along the beach, during which Peggy directly challenged Barbara about her estrangement from Brewster.

Barbara defended her position, citing Brewster's reckless spending — "to my certain knowledge he has spent more than four hundred thousand dollars since last September" (016-025) — and the talk in New York clubs where "they are betting that he will be a pauper within a year" (016-027).

Peggy's loyalty to Brewster remained steadfast through the exchange. The conversation ended as they "turned in through the great iron gates" (016-039) of the estate, with Barbara failing to win Peggy's agreement.

## Expenses

The Florida sojourn proved costly. Harrison's financial summary recorded:

| Expense Item | Amount |
|-------------|--------|
| Special train to Florida | $1,000.00 |
| Cottage in Florida | (included in living expenses) |

The Florida trip as a whole — "including medical attention, the cottage and living expenses" — totaled $18,500 (018-002).

## Later References

After the Florida interlude, the cottage became a point of nostalgic contrast. During Brewster's European travels, one character reflected, "Can't you fancy living in palaces and longing for a thatched cottage?" (022-011), suggesting the simplicity of cottage life as an unrealized ideal.

During Brewster's absence in Florida, [[nopper-harrison|"Nopper" Harrison]] took control of certain domestic arrangements, altering the decorative scheme from "the Spain of Velasquez to France and Louis Quinze" (017-007).

## Significance in the Novel

The Florida cottage represents a transitional space in the narrative — between New York society and the Mediterranean cruise, between illness and recovery, and between Brewster's engagement to Barbara Drew and its dissolution. It is here that Peggy Gray's role as Brewster's most loyal defender becomes fully visible, and where the contrast between Peggy's steadfastness and Barbara's worldliness is most sharply drawn.
