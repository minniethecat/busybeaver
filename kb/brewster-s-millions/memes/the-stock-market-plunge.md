## Overview

The Stock Market Plunge is the novel's most darkly comic episode — and the clearest illustration of Brewster's peculiar predicament. Seeking to lose a substantial portion of his million dollars through reckless speculation, Brewster shorts [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]] stock, betting heavily that it will fall. Instead, his very action triggers a market panic that drives the stock to unprecedented heights, turning his intended loss into a staggering profit and deepening the irony of his financial curse.

## Narrative

In the weeks following the reading of the will, Brewster grows increasingly anxious about the pace of his spending. Conventional [[profligacy|extravagance]] — dinners, gifts, entertaining — is proving insufficient to burn through a million dollars in a single year. He turns to the [[speculation|stock market]], reasoning that [[wall-street|Wall Street]] offers the most efficient mechanism for losing money quickly (010-005).

Brewster approaches a broker with instructions to short [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]], a stock he believes is overvalued. The broker is incredulous: "Good heavens, [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]], you're joking. Lumber is going up every day" (010-007). But Brewster insists, and the broker executes a massive short position — approximately a hundred thousand dollars at risk, exclusive of commission (010-013).

The market's reaction is swift and perverse. News of the enormous short position — possibly leaked, possibly inferred from the volume of selling — triggers a buying frenzy. Other traders, assuming that someone with inside knowledge must be betting against the stock, rush to buy in anticipation of a short squeeze:

> In ten minutes' time Lumber and Fuel was up to 113 and soaring. The effect of his plunge had been exactly the opposite of what Brewster intended. (010-031) (010-024)

Brewster watches in mounting horror as the ticker tape reports ever-higher prices. His attempt to lose money has instead produced what may be the single most profitable trade of his career. The scene at the broker's office — Brewster's despair juxtaposed against the jubilation of ordinary speculators — is the novel's sharpest satire of the disconnect between conventional values and Brewster's inverted financial incentives.

> He watched the stock market stealthily and plied his friends with questions that would have aroused suspicion in less innocent minds. (010-006)

## Consequences

The [[the-lumber-and-fuel-episode|Lumber and Fuel]] episode deepens Brewster's predicament in several ways. Financially, the profit pushes him further from his goal of spending the entire million. Psychologically, it reinforces the maddening pattern that will define his year: every attempt to lose money somehow generates more of it. His friends and the public, unaware of his true motives, regard him as a financial genius — a reputation that makes it even harder to dispose of his wealth unobtrusively.

The episode also foreshadows Brewster's later, more desperate speculations. Having learned that [[wall-street|Wall Street]] cannot be relied upon to lose money, he must turn to ever more creative — and risky — spending schemes. The [[speculation|stock market]] plunge thus funnels him toward the [[the-yacht-cruise|Mediterranean cruise]] and other extravagant ventures that will dominate the novel's second half.

Thematicatically, this episode crystallizes McCutcheon's central irony: in a system built on the assumption that everyone seeks profit, a man who genuinely wishes to lose money finds the machinery rigged against him at every turn.

## Key Passages

> "Take my word for it Lumber can't go any higher. Think of the conditions. The stock is away above its actual value now." Brewster was arguing against his own interests with unintentional prescience. (010-011)

> "Good heavens, Monty," cried the broker, "you're joking. Lumber is going up every day. You might as well try to stop the tide." (010-007)

> Brewster, confident that the stock could go no higher, and already counting his paper losses with satisfaction, was stunned to see the ticker recording a fresh advance. The market, it seemed, had a perverse genius for frustrating his designs. (010-015)
