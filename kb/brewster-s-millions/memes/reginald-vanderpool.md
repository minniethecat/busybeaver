## Overview

Reginald Vanderpool, often called "Reggie" or "Reggy," is a young Englishman of leisure who joins Brewster's yacht cruise aboard the *Flora Belle*. He embodies the aristocratic idler type — charming, indolent, and perpetually amused by the absurdities of life aboard a millionaire's pleasure yacht. His comic theatricality and capacity for finding himself in trouble make him one of the more memorable minor characters.

## Role in the Story

Vanderpool makes his first appearance as one of the party on Brewster's yacht (018-016). He quickly establishes himself as a figure of entertainment, with his affectations of English aristocracy and his penchant for amateur theatrical performance. When masks are needed for an entertainment aboard ship, Vanderpool declares, "The moment I get a mask on, and you know, by Jove, I have a remarkable talent for disguise" (020-015).

His mischievous nature leads to one of the cruise's more dramatic episodes. Vanderpool requires rescue from "the stern arm of the law" after some escapade, requiring others to come to his aid (020-028). During a tense moment involving Brewster and the other passengers, Vanderpool shouts to DeMille, "Will you be good?" as Brewster descends from the upper deck (027-002).

Throughout the cruise, Vanderpool maintains his characteristic sangfroid. When dark rumors of conspiracy circulate among the passengers, he responds with his trademark amusement: "Just what I expected," he cries, seemingly unflappable in the face of intrigue (028-022). He is also noted for his unsuccessful but comical efforts at heroism — when passengers rush for the upper deck at one point, "Vanderpool was not the hero of the hour" (019-026).

## Relationships

Vanderpool's primary on-page relationship is with **Dan DeMille**, with whom he shares an easy, bantering rapport. The two men are often paired in scenes of comic relief during the cruise, and Vanderpool's teasing of DeMille — "Dan DeMille, you are quite foolish" — suggests the affectionate rivalry of social equals (027-010).

With **Montgomery Brewster**, Vanderpool maintains the cordial but detached relationship of a guest to his host. He is present for the major events of the cruise and participates in the group's various entertainments and crises, but remains a satellite figure orbiting Brewster's more intense personal drama.

He is part of the wider social circle that includes **Joe Bragdon**, **Dr. Lotless**, **"Rip" Van Winkle**, and **Peggy Gray** — all of whom are named together in the passenger list (018-016). This grouping places Vanderpool among what amounts to a floating house party of fashionable young people.

## Significance

Vanderpool is a comic type — the English aristocrat adrift in American waters, bringing with him the airs and pastimes of London club life. His presence on the yacht reflects the international flavor of Brewster's social experiment, drawing not just New York society but also European idlers into the millionaire's orbit. His perpetual amusement at the melodrama unfolding around him serves as a kind of deflationary device: when the other passengers grow earnestly concerned about conspiracy, betrayal, or danger, Vanderpool's laconic "Just what I expected" reminds the reader not to take any of it too seriously. He is the novel's embodiment of aristocratic insouciance, filtering the story's more melodramatic turns through a lens of comic detachment.
