# Wealth and Character

## Overview

*Brewster's Millions* is, at its core, a moral test. James Sedgwick's will does not simply require Montgomery Brewster to spend money — it requires him to demonstrate, through that spending, that he possesses the character to deserve a fortune. The behavioral conditions attached to the inheritance — temperance, fair dealing, business acumen — are not incidental obstacles but the heart of the test. The novel asks: does great wealth corrupt the character, or does strong character survive the temptations of wealth? Brewster's year of enforced profligacy is designed to answer that question.

## In the Novel

### Sedgwick's Moral Framework

The conditions James Sedgwick imposes on his heir reveal his own theory of character. He requires that Brewster "give satisfactory evidence to the executor that he was capable of managing his affairs shrewdly and wisely — that he possessed the ability to add to the fortune through his own enterprise" (004-020). This is not merely a test of spending but a test of competence. Sedgwick, a self-made man, believes that character is demonstrated through action — that a man who cannot manage money does not deserve it.

The behavioral requirements extend beyond finance. Brewster must "come to his twenty-sixth anniversary with a fair name and a record free from anything worse than mild forms of dissipation; that his habits be temperate" (004-020). The fortune must go to a man of sound reputation, not merely a successful spender.

### The Paradox of Virtuous Waste

The challenge places Brewster in a moral paradox. To win the fortune, he must behave wastefully. But Sedgwick's conditions require him to behave virtuously. He must be simultaneously a spendthrift and a gentleman — wasting money while preserving character.

This paradox is the engine of the novel's moral inquiry. Can a man spend a million dollars on himself and remain temperate? Can he be seen as wasteful by society while maintaining a "fair name"? The challenge tests not only Brewster's spending ability but his capacity to navigate the contradiction at the heart of Sedgwick's design.

### The Final Judgment

The executor Swearengen Jones, in his final assessment, must weigh character against expenditure. Brewster reports his spending in precise detail (031-018), but the real question is whether he has satisfied the moral conditions. The novel's resolution suggests that character survives the test — that Brewster emerges from his year of profligacy not corrupted but clarified.

The narrator's quiet observation that "he should come to his twenty-sixth anniversary with a fair name" (004-020) becomes the novel's moral thesis: wealth is not inherently corrupting, but it is a test that not everyone passes.

## Key Passages

> "He required that the young man should give satisfactory evidence to the executor that he was capable of managing his affairs shrewdly and wisely — that he possessed the ability to add to the fortune through his own enterprise." (004-020)

> "He should come to his twenty-sixth anniversary with a fair name and a record free from anything worse than mild forms of dissipation; that his habits be temperate." (004-020)

> "He should possess nothing at the end of the year which might be regarded as a visible or invisible asset." (004-020)

> "It was to preclude any possible chance of the mingling of his fortune with the smallest portion of Edwin P. Brewster's that James Sedgwick, on his deathbed, put his hand to this astonishing instrument." (004-019)
