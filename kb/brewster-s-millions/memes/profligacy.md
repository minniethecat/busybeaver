# Profligacy

## Overview

Profligacy — the reckless, extravagant expenditure of money — is the engine that drives the plot of *Brewster's Millions*. [[Montgomery Brewster]]'s [[inheritance]] challenge requires him not merely to spend a million dollars, but to do so with sufficient abandon that his friends and the executor [[Swearengen Jones]] deem his conduct genuinely wasteful. The novel explores profligacy as both a comic spectacle and a moral question: can a person of fundamentally temperate character sustain a year of deliberate waste?

Profligacy in the novel is distinguished from simple wastefulness. It is *performative* — Brewster must be seen to spend — and *constrained* — Sedgwick's will forbids outright gifts or endowments. The resulting paradox — spend everything, keep nothing, tell no one — transforms profligacy from a character flaw into a narrative mechanism. It is no accident that the novel's most memorable scenes are not moments of acquisition but of divestment: the extravagant dinners, the yacht cruise, the Florida cottage, each representing a calculated assault on the million-dollar inheritance.

## In the Novel

### The Scale of the Task

Brewster quickly realizes that spending a million dollars in a single year requires more than ordinary extravagance. His friends, observing his spending from the outside, are alarmed by its sheer scale. The narrator remarks on "Monty's reckless extravagance" and notes that his companions "were worried" (006-015). Even in a city accustomed to wealth, Brewster's spending attracts attention and concern.

The challenge is compounded by the fact that Brewster cannot simply give the money away. Sedgwick's will forbids endowments and requires him to "get his money's worth" for every expenditure (004-020). He must consume rather than donate, turning profligacy into something approaching a discipline. As Brewster himself reflects, he may need to "employ an army of spendthrifts to devise ways and means" for disposing of the fortune (005-014).

### Extravagance as Performance

Brewster's profligacy becomes a public performance. His friend [[Subway Smith]] observes that "he spends it like a Chicago millionaire" (006-002), and Brewster himself wonders whether his spending meets "the New York or the Montana standard of extravagance" (006-011). The reference to Montana is significant — Swearengen Jones, the Montana executor, will ultimately judge whether Brewster's spending was sufficiently wasteful.

The yacht cruise aboard [[the-yacht-flora-belle]] provides new scope for extravagance: "new opportunities for extravagance would present themselves" (018-003). Each new setting — [[New York City]] apartments, European travel, the yacht — offers fresh avenues for consumption, and Brewster pursues them all with the grim determination of a man working against his own interests.

### The Moral Dimension

Profligacy in the novel is not treated as simple vice. Brewster's deliberate waste troubles those who care about him. [[Barbara Drew]] sees "how his profligacy was troubling her and Mrs. Gray" (010-005). The spending creates a moral tension: Brewster must behave wastefully to win the greater fortune, yet his wasteful behavior alienates the people whose respect he values.

The novel thus poses an implicit question: is profligacy merely wasteful, or does it reveal something about [[wealth-and-character]]? Brewster's year of extravagance tests not only his ability to spend but also his ability to remain recognizably himself — temperate in habits if not in expenditure. His ultimate success depends on a paradox: that he can be profligate without becoming a profligate.

## Relationship to Other Themes

Profligacy intersects with several of the novel's other core concepts, forming a thematic web that gives the story its moral complexity.

### Profligacy and Inheritance

[[Inheritance]] provides the material for profligacy. Without [[Edwin Peter Brewster]]'s million-dollar bequest, there would be nothing to spend. Without [[James T. Sedgwick]]'s conditional seven-million-dollar fortune, there would be no reason to spend it. The novel's structure makes profligacy not a choice but a condition of advancement — a critique, perhaps, of how inherited wealth distorts normal economic behavior.

### Profligacy and Charity

The will's prohibition on charitable giving creates a distinct tension. Brewster cannot become a philanthropist; he must be a consumer. Yet his spending inevitably benefits others — the tradesmen who furnish his apartments, the crew of [[the-yacht-flora-belle]], the guests at his dinners. The novel raises, without fully resolving, the question of whether consumption can be a form of [[charity]] when its effect is to distribute wealth.

### Profligacy and Luck

Brewster's project is constantly threatened by [[luck-and-misfortune]]. Investments intended to fail succeed. Speculations meant to lose money turn profitable. The universe conspires against his profligacy, suggesting that wealth, once acquired, has a momentum of its own — a force that resists even the most determined attempts at divestment.
