# Appearance and Reality

## Overview

The gap between appearance and reality is the fundamental dramatic irony of *Brewster's Millions*. To all of New York society — his friends, his lover, the newspapers — Montgomery Brewster appears to be a young man recklessly squandering his inheritance. In reality, he is meticulously fulfilling the conditions of a secret legal contract. The novel derives much of its tension and comedy from this disjunction: Brewster cannot explain his actions to anyone, and so he must endure the judgment of a world that sees only the surface of his behavior.

## In the Novel

### The Necessary Deception

Brewster's inability to reveal the terms of Sedgwick's will is both a legal and dramatic necessity. If he could explain his spending, the challenge would lose its moral weight — and potentially its legal validity. The executor Swearengen Jones's letters from Montana serve as reminders that Brewster's conduct is being watched by someone who knows the truth. But in New York, Brewster is alone with his secret.

Even those closest to him cannot be told. The narrator notes that it was "impossible for him to share all his secrets with her" (009-004), referring to Barbara Drew. His silence creates misunderstandings that ripple through his relationships. His friends watch his "reckless extravagance" with growing alarm (006-015), unable to reconcile the Monty Brewster they know with the profligate they see.

### Society as Misreader

New York society functions in the novel as a collective misreader of Brewster's behavior. The society columns report his spending without context. His friends interpret his actions through the lens of normal morality — in which wastefulness is a vice — rather than the extraordinary morality of Sedgwick's challenge, in which wastefulness is a contractual duty.

This misreading is not innocent; it has consequences. Barbara Drew's affections cool when Brewster's reputation suffers. His business associates doubt his judgment. The gap between what Brewster is and what he appears to be becomes a source of mounting social pressure.

### The Final Revelation

The novel's resolution depends on the closing of this gap. When the deadline arrives and the truth can finally be told, appearance and reality converge. Those who judged Brewster harshly must reckon with their misreading. The narrative structure thus rewards the reader — who has known the truth all along — with the satisfaction of seeing society's false judgments corrected.

## Key Passages

> "It was impossible for him to share all his secrets with her, that is why he did not tell her the truth." (009-004)

> "Monty's reckless extravagance. They were worried." (006-015)

> "I am sorry I can't tell you about this," he said, so gravely that she forgave him. (032-031)

> "I defy him or anyone else to prove that I have not met the conditions." (031-019)
