## Overview

The charity bazaar at the Astoria Hotel was a two-day society fundraising event held during the winter of 1900–1901 (Chapter 11, "Coals of Fire"). It served as a public exhibition of [[new-york-city|New York]] [[new-york-society|high society]], with [[new-york-city|the city]]'s elite presiding over booths while the public paid admission to observe them. For [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]], the bazaar was both an opportunity to dispose of his inheritance through ostentatious [[charity|philanthropy]] at [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]]'s booth and the occasion of an unwelcome romantic rivalry with the newly arrived Californian millionaire Rodney Grimes (011-007–011-010).

## Narrative

The bazaar took place shortly after Brewster's costly automobile accident, in which his "Green Juggernaut" crashed through a subway excavation barricade, injuring three workmen whom he compensated at $4,000 each (011-006). The social calendar turned immediately to the charity bazaar at the Astoria, where "society was on exhibition, and the public paid for the privilege of gazing at the men and women whose names filled the society columns" (011-007).

Brewster directed his attention — and his money — to the booth presided over by [[barbara-drew|Miss Barbara Drew]], and "there seemed to be no end to his [[charity|philanthropy]]" (011-007). Over the two days and nights of the bazaar, his account-book recorded a "profit" — meaning expenditure — of nearly three thousand dollars (011-007).

> "At this time everyone was interested in the charity bazaar at the Astoria. Society was on exhibition, and the public paid for the privilege of gazing at the men and women whose names filled the society columns." (011-007)

Brewster's serenity was disrupted by the appearance of Rodney Grimes, a Californian "of immense wealth and unbounded confidence in himself," armed with letters of introduction to [[new-york-city|New York]] society (011-007). Grimes, aged fifty, had already amassed and lost several fortunes in mining and had been twice widowed — first marrying a cook in a mining camp, then a schoolteacher who had come to California "ready to marry the first man who asked her" (011-007). His romantic strategy with Barbara was the same "dash and impulsiveness" he had deployed in [[montana|the West]].

Grimes "vied with Brewster in patronizing Barbara's booth" and "rushed into the conflict with an impetuosity that seemed destined to carry everything before it" (011-008). Within ten days of his arrival in [[new-york-city|New York]], he had become "the most talked-of man in town" (011-008). Brewster, recognizing Grimes as "an obstacle, but not as a rival," resumed his attentions to Barbara with renewed vigor, dismissing the Californian as an impostor. He remarked to Barbara that Grimes's father "was honored by the V.C., on the coast in '49" — and when Barbara innocently asked if he meant the Victoria Cross, Brewster replied: "No, the vigilance committee" (011-009–011-010).

## Consequences

The charity bazaar reinforced two patterns central to Brewster's spending year. First, it demonstrated his strategy of using society events as vehicles for rapid expenditure: by channeling money through [[charity|philanthropy]] and [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]]'s booth, he could dispose of thousands of dollars while maintaining the appearance of generosity rather than profligacy. The bazaar's $3,000 "profit" followed immediately on the heels of the $12,000 paid out for the automobile accident victims, showing Brewster's ability to sustain a high burn rate across different categories of loss.

Second, the appearance of Rodney Grimes introduced a rival whose competitive presence would further inflate Brewster's spending on [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]] and on society events generally. Grimes's aggressive courtship — the same tactics that had won him two wives in California — pressured Brewster to maintain visibility at Barbara's side, which in turn required ever more lavish expenditure. This dynamic would persist through the winter season, culminating in the Spanish costume ball planned in Chapter 15 ("[[the-cut-direct|The Cut Direct]]") and the various entertainments that followed.

The bazaar also illustrates the novel's satirical treatment of [[new-york-city|New York]] society, in which the public literally paid admission to watch the rich perform charity — a spectacle that blurred the line between [[charity|philanthropy]] and entertainment.
