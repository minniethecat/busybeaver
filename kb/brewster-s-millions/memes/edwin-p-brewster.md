## Overview

Edwin Peter Brewster is the deceased patriarch of the Brewster family fortune, which he accumulated through the discovery and operation of three rich gold mines (004-008). Though dead before the novel's opening, his presence looms over the entire narrative: it is his million dollars — and the eccentric conditions attached to its inheritance — that set the story in motion.

## Role in the Story

Edwin Peter Brewster dies on the evening of his grandson Montgomery's twenty-fifth birthday celebration. The news arrives at the birthday dinner itself, when a messenger announces that "Mr. Brewster is dead" — an event that transforms the festive gathering into sober reflection (001-042).

His death sets the novel's plot in motion, but it is the peculiar nature of his will that defines the story's central conflict. The will, drafted by his legal representative Mr. Grant, reveals that Montgomery is to inherit one million dollars — but only on the condition that he spend every penny of it within one year, retaining no assets or property at the end (004-002). The will explicitly states that "Montgomery Brewster was to have no other worldly possessions than the million dollars," and that failure to dispose of the entire sum would forfeit the inheritance to the estate (004-019).

The will also spells out the family history: it records "the date of your birth, of the death of Robert Brewster and of Mrs. Brewster," establishing the grandfather's relationship to the full Brewster line (004-014). Montgomery is identified as "the son of Robert and [his mother]," making Edwin Peter his paternal grandfather (004-018).

Edwin Peter Brewster's reputation as a self-made industrialist colors how others perceive Montgomery. The birthday dinner toast celebrates "the grandson of old Edwin Peter Brewster," marking Monty's identity as inseparable from his grandfather's legacy (006-010). Late in the novel, after Montgomery has spent the entire fortune, the text reflects that "something had been done to obliterate Edwin Peter Brewster's legacy" — suggesting that the grandfather's life's work has been undone by the grandson's prodigality (029-020). And in a climactic declaration, Montgomery announces, "There is not one penny of Edwin Peter Brewster's money in my possession," marking the completion of his task (031-018).

## Relationships

Edwin Peter Brewster's key relationship is with his grandson, **Montgomery Brewster**. The grandfather's posthumous influence over Montgomery is absolute, enforced through the elaborate mechanism of the will. The relationship is characterized by absence — the old man is never a living presence in the novel — and by the curious blend of generosity and control embodied in the will's conditions. Edwin Peter's intentions remain ambiguous: is the spending condition a test of character, a cosmic joke, or a lesson about the burden of wealth?

He is also the father of **Robert Brewster**, whose death (along with that of Mrs. Brewster) is recorded in the will (004-014). This makes Montgomery the sole surviving heir of the Brewster line, concentrating the entire family fortune — and its attendant moral and psychological weight — on one young man's shoulders.

## Significance

Edwin Peter Brewster functions as the novel's originating force — a character who, despite never appearing alive on the page, drives every major plot development. His fortune is the engine of the story, and the conditions attached to it provide the dramatic tension. The grandfather represents the archetype of the self-made American industrialist whose wealth creates as many problems as it solves for the next generation. The novel's central irony — that being forced to spend a million dollars is a form of punishment — originates in Edwin Peter's eccentric testamentary vision, making him, in a structural sense, the true author of Brewster's ordeal.
