## Overview

Ellis is the footman employed in Montgomery Brewster's household. He serves as a recurring domestic presence, delivering messages between Brewster and the outside world, attending to callers, and providing continuity across the novel's various settings — from the birthday dinner in Chapter 1 to the final gathering a year later.

## Role in the Story

Ellis first appears at the birthday dinner, announcing himself to the assembled guests: "He says he is Ellis, from your grandfather's, sir" (001-020). Brewster, deep in the festivities, sends back facetious replies through Ellis to his grandfather's messengers (001-021), and at one point commands, "Tell Ellis to go to Halifax" (001-031). Despite the dismissive treatment, Ellis "strode firmly into the studio" (001-033) to deliver a message, and Harrison welcomes him for a nightcap (001-034).

Ellis continues his duties as footman throughout the novel. He calls Brewster at seven in the mornings (002-005) and handles practical errands such as taking Brewster's horse to the entrance of a building (005-022). The narrative notes that "Ellis, the footman, also found a music master" during the spending campaign (006-013) — a wry detail suggesting that even the domestic staff were caught up in Brewster's whirlwind of expenditure.

In the novel's closing, the narration observes that "Ellis did not deliver his message" on the night that mirrored the birthday dinner of a year before (032-021), creating a bookend that marks the arc of Brewster's transformation from carefree spendthrift to a man facing the consequences of his choices.

## Relationships

Ellis is employed by Montgomery Brewster, serving as footman and general domestic attendant (001-020). His interactions with Brewster's friends are limited to service encounters, though Oliver Harrison's warm greeting — "just in time for a nightcap, Ellis" (001-034) — suggests a degree of familiar rapport between the footman and Brewster's social circle.

## Significance

As a servant figure, Ellis anchors the novel's social texture in the realities of class and domestic service in early twentieth-century New York. His presence at both the opening birthday dinner and the final gathering a year later (032-021) provides structural symmetry. The detail that even Ellis found himself enriched during Brewster's spending campaign — securing a music master (006-013) — illustrates how Brewster's fortune-distribution effort rippled through all levels of society, from financiers to footmen.

## Notes

Ellis's first name is never given in the novel; he is always referred to by his surname alone, consistent with period conventions for referring to domestic staff. The novel uses his presence at both the opening and closing gatherings as a subtle structural device (001-020) (032-021).
