# Montana

## Overview

Montana in *Brewster's Millions* is less a physical location than a symbolic presence — the distant moral horizon against which Brewster's New York spending is measured. It is the home of Swearengen Jones, executor of Sedgwick's will, whose letters and telegrams from Butte provide the only external check on Brewster's conduct. The contrast between Montana plainness and New York sophistication runs through the novel as a quiet commentary on values: where New York judges by appearances, Montana judges by ledgers and facts.

## In the Novel

### The Distant Executor

Swearengen Jones's location in Montana is not accidental. James Sedgwick himself made his fortune in the West — gold mines and ranches in Montana and the Northwest. Jones, his chosen executor, inhabits the world that produced the Sedgwick fortune: a world of "shrewd, careful, and thrifty" values (004-008) far removed from the society columns of New York.

Jones's letters from Montana punctuate the novel like the voice of an absent judge. Each communication — "Swearengen Jones, Butte, Montana" (008-009) — reminds Brewster that his spending is being monitored from afar. The physical distance between Montana and New York mirrors the moral distance between Sedgwick's frontier ethos and Brewster's metropolitan profligacy.

### Montana as Moral Standard

Brewster himself frames his spending in terms of Montana expectations. Wondering whether his extravagance is sufficient, he asks himself: "Is this the New York or the Montana standard of extravagance?" (006-011). The question is revealing: New York extravagance is performative and social, while Montana extravagance — if such a thing exists — would be measured by different criteria entirely.

The Montana standard, as embodied by Jones, is one of plain dealing and honest accounting. Jones's final report — "To Swearengen Jones, Esq., Executor under the will of the late James T. Sedgwick of Montana" (031-018) — is a document of facts, not impressions. Where New York gossips, Montana tallies.

### Frontier Values vs. Metropolitan Values

The novel draws an implicit contrast between the world that created the Sedgwick fortune and the world in which Brewster must spend it. Sedgwick built his wealth through work — "within five years he was the owner of a huge ranch" (004-008). Brewster must destroy wealth through consumption. Montana represents the producer ethic; New York represents the consumer spectacle.

This contrast is never stated directly, but it suffuses Jones's correspondence. His terse telegrams from Butte carry the weight of a different moral order — one in which money is earned, not inherited, and in which a man's word matters more than his social standing.
