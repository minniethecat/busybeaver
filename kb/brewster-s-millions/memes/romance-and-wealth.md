# Romance and Wealth

## Overview

In *Brewster's Millions*, love and money are perpetually entangled. Montgomery Brewster's romantic life — his courtship of the society beauty Barbara Drew and his deeper connection to the loyal Peggy Gray — unfolds against the backdrop of his million-dollar spending challenge. The novel examines whether love can be disentangled from fortune, and whether a man who must destroy his wealth can also build a relationship. Brewster's romantic choices become another measure of his character under the extraordinary pressure of Sedgwick's conditions.

## In the Novel

### Barbara Drew: Society and Expectation

Barbara Drew belongs to the world of New York high society that Brewster inhabits. She presides over charity booths and fills the society columns (011-007). Their relationship begins as a "society acquaintance" (005-027) and develops into something more serious — but it is always framed by the expectations of their social world.

Barbara's feelings for Brewster are genuine but complicated by his changing circumstances. The narrator observes that "Barbara Drew loved Brewster, but she" struggled with the implications of his profligate year (009-019). Her love is tested by his behavior — behavior she cannot understand because she does not know the secret of the challenge. When Brewster's spending becomes notorious, "Miss Drew had forgotten him" (012-033), illustrating how quickly social regard can shift with perceived financial irresponsibility.

### Peggy Gray: Loyalty Without Condition

Peggy Gray represents a different kind of attachment — loyalty that does not depend on wealth or social standing. She and her mother have "meager fortune" (013-027), yet Peggy's concern for Brewster is personal rather than financial. The narrator notes that Brewster's thoughts "treacherously wandered off to Peggy Gray, and then everything was bright again" (025-021) — a suggestion that Peggy offers an emotional refuge from the pressures of the challenge.

Unlike Barbara, Peggy knows Brewster without the filter of his fortune. Her constancy provides a moral counterweight to the social world's fickleness. The novel suggests that true attachment survives the vicissitudes of wealth — that Peggy's loyalty, unlike Barbara's social affection, is not contingent on Brewster's financial standing.

### The Test of Love

The spending challenge functions as an inadvertent test of romantic character. Brewster cannot explain his behavior to either woman without violating the terms of the will. He must appear wasteful and irresponsible while actually being bound by strict conditions. The women in his life must judge him by appearances alone — and their judgments reveal their own characters.

The novel thus poses an implicit question: can love survive a year of apparent madness? Barbara's withdrawal and Peggy's steadfastness offer two answers. In the end, the challenge clarifies not only Brewster's financial position but his romantic one as well.
