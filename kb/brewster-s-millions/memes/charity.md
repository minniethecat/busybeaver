# Charity

## Overview

Charity occupies an ambiguous position in *Brewster's Millions*. In ordinary moral terms, giving money to the poor is virtuous. Yet Sedgwick's will explicitly forbids it as a means of disposing of the fortune — precisely because charity would be too easy. Brewster could simply donate the entire million to worthy causes and meet the condition overnight. By restricting charity, Sedgwick forces Brewster into genuine consumption rather than virtuous disposal. The novel thus raises an uncomfortable question: is the prohibition on charity a moral flaw in Sedgwick's design, or a necessary safeguard against a too-easy solution?

## In the Novel

### The Prohibition

The will's instructions on charity are precise and restrictive. Sedgwick requires that Brewster "give sparingly to charity" and make "no endowments" (004-020). The executor Swearengen Jones reinforces this with his own directives: "No indiscriminate giving away of funds. By that I don't mean that you can't give to charity. I mean that you can't give to anything that looks like a scheme to dispose of money" (005-055). The distinction is crucial: ordinary charitable impulse is permitted, but organized disposal through philanthropy is forbidden.

### The Temptation to Give

Brewster faces repeated temptations to solve his problem through giving. Early in the novel, the narrator notes that "requests for public or private charity were abundant" (002-005) — the millionaire is besieged by those seeking donations. His friend Peggy Gray's financial vulnerability presents a particularly acute temptation. Brewster reflects: "If I thought that this money would make any difference I would" (003-016), but he recognizes that direct gifts would violate the spirit of the challenge and potentially insult the recipient.

### The Moral Paradox

The novel's treatment of charity creates a moral paradox. Brewster must spend a million dollars, yet he cannot give it to those who need it. He must consume rather than share. The narrator captures this tension in a single poignant detail: Brewster's one charitable act during his year of spending — giving his clothing to a poor family — is described as "his only gift to charity and he excused his motive in giving" (030-002). Even this modest gesture requires self-justification.

The restriction on charity serves Sedgwick's purpose: it ensures that Brewster genuinely consumes the fortune rather than transferring it. But it also means that the year of profligacy benefits no one — the money vanishes without leaving any lasting good behind.
