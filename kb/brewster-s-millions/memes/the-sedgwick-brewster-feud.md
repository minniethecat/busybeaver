# The Sedgwick-Brewster Feud

## Overview

The Sedgwick-Brewster feud is the hidden engine of the novel's plot — a personal hatred so deep that it survived both antagonists and shaped the destiny of their mutual heir. James T. Sedgwick and Edwin Peter Brewster were brothers-in-law who became bitter enemies. Sedgwick's will, drafted on his deathbed, was designed with one overriding purpose: to ensure that not a single penny of Edwin Brewster's money would ever mingle with the Sedgwick fortune. This hatred, reaching from beyond the grave, is what transforms a simple inheritance into the million-dollar challenge.

## In the Novel

### The Origins of Hatred

The novel does not detail the precise origins of the feud, but its contours are clear from the will and from Mr. Grant's explanations. Sedgwick and Brewster were linked by family — Sedgwick's sister married Robert Brewster, making Montgomery Brewster the nephew of both men. Yet Sedgwick came to regard Edwin Brewster with consuming hatred. The narrator simply refers to "the man he hated, Edwin Peter Brewster" (004-019), leaving the specific grievances to implication.

### The Deathbed Instrument

Sedgwick's will was "signed on the day of his death, September 24th" (004-004), suggesting it was drafted in his final hours. The timing is significant: Sedgwick used his last act on earth to wage war against a man who, by then, was already dead. Edwin Brewster had predeceased him, yet Sedgwick's hatred was undiminished. The narrator describes the will as "this astonishing instrument" — a legal document that reads more like a declaration of war than a disposition of property (004-019).

### The Will as Weapon

The central condition of the will — that Brewster must be penniless of all Brewster money before receiving the Sedgwick fortune — is explicitly framed as an act of revenge. Sedgwick declared that "his heir should not receive the fortune if he possessed a single penny that had come to him, in any shape or form, from the man he hated" (004-019). The language is absolute: not a dollar, not a penny, not "in any shape or form."

The narrator plainly states the motive: "It was to preclude any possible chance of the mingling of his fortune with the smallest portion of Edwin P. Brewster's that James Sedgwick, on his deathbed, put his hand to this astonishing instrument" (004-019). The feud is not merely personal — it is financial, a determination to keep the two fortunes eternally separate.

### The Unwitting Heir

Montgomery Brewster, the grandson of one enemy and the nephew of the other, is caught between two dead men. He had no part in their quarrel, yet the feud determines the course of his life. Sedgwick's will presumes that Brewster might be "enriched liberally by his enemy" — a prescient assumption, since Edwin Brewster had indeed left his grandson one million dollars (004-019). The feud thus creates the novel's central dramatic irony: Brewster must destroy one inheritance to claim another, all because two old men could not forgive each other.
