# Pennilessness

## Overview

Pennilessness in *Brewster's Millions* is both a legal requirement and a philosophical state. The will of James Sedgwick demands that his heir arrive at his twenty-sixth birthday "absolutely penniless" — owning nothing beyond the clothes on his back. This condition transforms poverty from a misfortune to be avoided into a goal to be pursued. The novel repeatedly returns to the contrast between wealth and destitution, using Brewster's enforced march toward pennilessness to examine what it means to have nothing, and whether nothing is, in fact, the absence of everything.

## In the Novel

### Pennilessness as a Legal Condition

The will's language is precise and absolute. Brewster is "to have no other worldly possession than the clothes which covered him on the September day named. He was to begin that day without a penny to his name, without a single article of jewelry, furniture or finance that he could call his own or could thereafter reclaim" (004-019). This is not metaphorical poverty — it is a legally defined state of total divestment.

Mr. Grant underlines the gravity of the requirement: "You understand that you are expected to be penniless a year from now?" (004-030). Brewster's cavalier response — "I don't mind being a pauper for a few hours on the 23rd of next September" (004-033) — reveals his initial failure to grasp the difficulty of the task. Becoming penniless is not simply a matter of writing checks; it requires the systematic undoing of an entire financial life.

### The Difficulty of Having Nothing

As the year progresses, Brewster discovers that poverty is surprisingly hard to achieve. His investments unexpectedly prosper. Money he has tried to lose returns to him. The final days become a desperate scramble to dispose of the remaining dollars. The narrator captures the absurdity: Brewster "rebelled against his desire to spend the seventy dollars that still remained" (030-027) — the last stubborn remnant of a fortune.

In the novel's climax, Brewster faces the real possibility of his own pennilessness with dread. "The million is gone — all gone. I'm as poor as Job's turkey," he declares (032-002). The experience transforms him. What began as a legal technicality becomes an existential crisis.

### Pennilessness and Character

The novel suggests that pennilessness, paradoxically, reveals character more clearly than wealth. Stripped of his money, Brewster must confront who he is without it. His friend Gardner observes the moral dimension: "You are the next thing to being a poor man" (029-016). The line between being penniless and being poor is one the novel explores with care — pennilessness is a temporary legal state, while poverty is a permanent social condition. Brewster's brief encounter with the former gives him, and the reader, a glimpse of the latter.

The executor Swearengen Jones, in his final judgment, must determine whether Brewster has truly met the condition. The question is not only whether the money is gone, but whether Brewster has been transformed by its absence.
