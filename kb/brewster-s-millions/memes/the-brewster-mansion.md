## The Brewster Legacy

The Brewster mansion on Fifth Avenue represented the substantial wealth of the Brewster family. Upon old Mr. Brewster's death, the property was distributed according to his will (002-003):

> The house in Fifth Avenue went to a sister, together with a million or two, and the residue of the estate found kindly disposed relatives who were willing to keep it from going to the Home for Friendless Fortunes (002-003).

[[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]], the grandson and heir apparent to a greater fortune, did not inherit the house directly — it passed to a female relative while he awaited the outcome of his grandfather's unusual testamentary scheme.

## Physical Setting

The mansion was described in somber, imposing terms. After old Mr. Brewster's death, the house carried the weight of mourning (002-004):

> The gloom of death still darkened the Fifth Avenue place, and there was a stillness, a gentle stealthiness about the house that made him long for more cheerful companionship (002-004).

Montgomery Brewster stayed at the "gloomy old Brewster house in Fifth Avenue" in the weeks following his grandfather's death, paying only "two or three hurried visits to the rooms at Mrs. Gray's, where he had made his home" (002-004). The contrast between the cold formality of the Brewster mansion and the warmth of [[fortieth-street|Mrs. Gray's boarding house]] defined Brewster's domestic life.

## Brewster's Residence

The mansion became Brewster's temporary residence following his inheritance. He occupied the house in a liminal state — caught between his grandfather's death and the revelation of the [[million-dollar-challenge|million-dollar challenge]], between his old life as a young man of prospects and his new status as a wealthy heir.

After learning of his inheritance, Brewster's relationship to the Fifth Avenue mansion shifted. On one occasion, "after dinner he had gone to his own rooms, forgetting that he lived on Fifth Avenue" (005-018), suggesting both the grandeur of his new circumstances and his lingering emotional attachment to the simpler life at [[fortieth-street|Mrs. Gray's]].

## The Will and Its Consequences

It was at the gloomy old Brewster house that Montgomery Brewster received the first intimations of his grandfather's unusual will. [[ellis|Ellis]], the family footman, had come to [[pettingills-studio|Pettingill's studio]] during Brewster's birthday dinner to summon him (001-041):

> "He sent me here, sir, with a message from Mr. Brewster, wishing you many happy returns of the day, and also with the request that you come to see him at once, if you can, sir" (001-041).

But by the time the message reached Monty, his grandfather was already dead — "Mr. Brewster is dead, sir" came the news soon after (001-042). The Fifth Avenue mansion thus became the site where the legal machinery of the inheritance was set in motion.

## Social Significance

The Brewster mansion on Fifth Avenue placed Montgomery Brewster squarely within New York's elite geography. [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Prentiss Drew]], a neighbor, had "shown a fondness for young Mr. Brewster, and Monty had been a frequent visitor at his house" (002-008). This proximity to the Drew family — and to [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]] — made the Fifth Avenue address socially strategic.

The house also represented the solid, old-money respectability that Brewster's subsequent spending spree threatened to undermine. His friends and associates measured his current extravagance against the dignified backdrop of the family mansion.

## Later Period

As Brewster's inheritance challenge intensified, he spent less time at the Fifth Avenue mansion. The house remained in the possession of the sister who had inherited it, while Brewster's life became increasingly mobile — moving between New York, [[florida-cottage|Florida]], and the [[the-yacht-flora-belle|Mediterranean cruise]].
