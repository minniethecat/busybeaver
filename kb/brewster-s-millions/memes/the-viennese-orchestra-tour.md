## Overview

The Viennese Orchestra Tour is one of Brewster's most extravagant and ill-conceived spending schemes. Acting on a whim, he engages a Hungarian orchestra for the staggering sum of $29,000 and dispatches them on an American concert tour under the hapless management of [[elon-gardner|Elon Gardner]] (020-001). The tour is an unmitigated catastrophe: public indifference meets artistic temperament, the musicians quarrel incessantly, and [[elon-gardner|Gardner]] is driven to the brink of a nervous breakdown. The final accounting records a loss of $56,382 on the venture — making it one of the single largest drains on Brewster's fortune (031-018).

## Narrative

The orchestra was originally conceived as part of Brewster's plans for a spectacular ball at Sherry's, the famous [[new-york-city|New York]] restaurant. [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]], Brewster's trusted aide, had been "in communication with the manager of that Hungarian orchestra" and found the musicians "quite ready for a little jaunt across the water" (020-001). Alongside a military band for the promenade, the Viennese orchestra was to be the musical centerpiece of the evening.

[[joe-bragdon|Joe Bragdon]], Brewster's level-headed friend, captured the absurdity of the expense with characteristic dry wit: "With a Viennese orchestra for twenty-nine thousand! And yet they maintain that silence is golden" (020-001). The observation proved prophetic — the orchestra would generate far more noise than revenue.

When the ball plans were disrupted, the orchestra was not simply dismissed. Instead, Brewster doubled down on the investment by launching an American concert tour, with [[elon-gardner|Elon Gardner]] assigned the thankless task of managing the enterprise. The tour was "marked for disaster" from the start: "Public indifference had aroused the scorn of the irascible members of the orchestra, and there was imminent danger of a collapse in the organization" (020-001). [[elon-gardner|Gardner]] "lived in constant fear that his troop of quarrelsome Hungarians would finish their tour in a butchery" (020-001).

By the time the tour staggered to its conclusion, the orchestra "straggled into [[new-york-city|New York]], headed by [[elon-gardner|Elon Gardner]], a physical wreck" (020-001). The musicians were "quarreling with itself and everybody else, and driving poor [[elon-gardner|Gardner]] to the insane asylum" (020-001). The flowers ordered for the original ball, meanwhile, had "lost their bloom long ago" (020-001).

## Consequences

The financial toll of the orchestra tour was severe. The final balance sheet records a loss of $56,382 — nearly twice the original $29,000 engagement fee, reflecting the accumulated costs of the American tour, [[elon-gardner|Gardner]]'s expenses, and the general entropy of the enterprise (031-018). Brewster, however, took a perverse satisfaction in the disaster: "The 'profit' side of his ledger account was enlarged and in that there was room for secret satisfaction" — profit, in Brewster's inverted accounting, meaning successful expenditure (020-001).

The human cost was borne primarily by [[elon-gardner|Gardner]], who served as a cautionary example of what happened to those who helped Brewster execute his spending schemes. The orchestra tour also contributed to Brewster's growing reputation as a man whose money brought chaos wherever it went.

## Analysis

The Viennese Orchestra Tour is a quintessential Brewster scheme: grand in conception, indifferent to practicalities, and ultimately successful only in losing money. It reflects the novel's running joke that the surest way to waste a fortune is to combine high art with commercial enterprise — the musicians' contempt for their audience meets the audience's indifference to the music, and [[elon-gardner|Gardner]] is crushed between them.

The episode also showcases McCutcheon's gift for comic compression. In a few brief passages, he sketches a complete arc of disaster: the initial [[profligacy|extravagance]], the mounting chaos, the physical and financial wreckage. The orchestra tour is never shown directly — it is reported through Bragdon's quips, [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]]'s reports, and the cold finality of the balance sheet — and this narrative distance makes the disaster funnier than any direct description could.
