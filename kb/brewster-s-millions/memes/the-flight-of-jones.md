## Overview

The Flight of Jones is the pivotal crisis of the novel: [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]], [[swearengen-jones|the executor]] of [[james-t-sedgwick|James T. Sedgwick]]'s will and the man entrusted with Brewster's entire inheritance, vanishes without a trace. As attorneys [[grant-and-ripley|Grant & Ripley]] reveal to a stunned Brewster, [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] systematically liquidated every asset — stocks, bonds, mines, real [[inheritance|estate]] — and disappeared into the [[montana|Montana]] wilderness with his secretary sometime around the third of September [033-001–033-061]. The discovery plunges Brewster from the heady task of spending a million dollars into the abyss of sudden [[pennilessness|poverty]], with the one-year deadline still ticking.

## Narrative

The crisis begins late at night when Brewster, celebrating at a social gathering, receives an urgent telegram: "Come to the office immediately. Will wait all night for you if necessary. [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] has disappeared and there is absolutely no trace of him" (032-001). Brewster freezes, the words "[[swearengen-jones|Jones]] has disappeared" echoing with brutal finality (032-001). He rushes through the streets of [[new-york-city|New York]], so impoverished by his spending spree that he cannot even afford a streetcar fare, and runs six or seven blocks to the lawyers' office (033-001).

At the office, Ripley delivers the devastating details with "deadly intentness" (033-001). [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] was last seen in [[butte-montana|Butte, Montana]], on the third of the month. Telegrams went unanswered. The telegraph company reported he could not be found. The chief of police responded to inquiries by asking, in turn, if anyone in [[new-york-city|New York]] knew [[swearengen-jones|Jones]]'s whereabouts (033-001).

The scope of the disaster is methodically laid out: [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] began converting securities into cash around the first of the month. He visited safety deposit vaults and removed "every scrap of stock, every bond, everything of value" belonging to the Sedgwick [[inheritance|estate]] (033-001). He sold Brewster's mines and real [[inheritance|estate]] to a mysterious buyer named Golden — a man who, it later emerges, cannot be located and whose very existence is suspect (033-001).

The authorities in [[butte-montana|Butte]] believe [[swearengen-jones|Jones]]'s secretary — a man who came to [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] less than a year ago and in whom [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] placed "implicit faith" — has murdered the old man and fled with the fortune (033-001). Brewster, connecting the dots, theorizes that the secretary orchestrated the entire scheme: influencing [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] to liquidate everything, then disposing of him and vanishing with the proceeds.

## Consequences

The disappearance of [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] reshapes the entire trajectory of the novel. Brewster, who moments earlier had been agonizing over how to spend his remaining fortune, now faces the prospect of genuine [[pennilessness|poverty]]. The million dollars is gone — not spent in the profligate manner required by the will, but stolen. The cruel irony is that Brewster may have failed the terms of [[the-sedgwick-fortune|the inheritance]] not through insufficient spending, but through a crime beyond his control.

The event also introduces the mysterious figure of "Golden," the supposed purchaser of Brewster's properties who cannot be traced — a loose thread that creates narrative suspense and foreshadows the novel's resolution. Meanwhile, Brewster must grapple with the psychological whiplash of losing everything he had been trying so desperately to be rid of.

## Analysis

The Flight of Jones serves as the novel's darkest hour — the moment when the comic premise of enforced [[profligacy|extravagance]] collides with genuine criminal menace. Throughout the preceding chapters, Brewster's struggle has been fundamentally comic: the harder he tries to waste money, the more it seems to multiply. [[swearengen-jones|Jones]]'s disappearance transforms the narrative stakes from farce to potential tragedy.

The episode also crystallizes the novel's meditation on the meaning of wealth. Brewster, who has spent months treating money as a burden to be shed, suddenly confronts its absence. His inability to pay a streetcar fare — a detail McCutcheon includes with pointed irony — drives home the speed of his reversal (033-001). The million dollars that was a weight around his neck becomes, in its disappearance, the object of desperate longing.
