## Overview

The Prizefight is a financial misadventure in which [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]], flush with his grandfather's million-dollar inheritance and the pressure to spend it within a year, bets heavily on a San Francisco boxing match. Convinced that the underdog — a local amateur — would defeat the reigning champion, Brewster instructed his associate [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]] to place $3,000 in wagers on the presumed loser (009-002). The bet was entered into Brewster's ledger as profit before the fight even took place, reflecting his characteristic overconfidence (009-002). The final balance sheet later records the outcome tersely: "Prizefight misjudged | 1,000.00," confirming that Brewster's sporting instincts proved costly (031-018).

## Narrative

The episode unfolds on the afternoon of the prizefight, shortly after Brewster receives an acerbic telegram from [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] — [[swearengen-jones|the executor]] of his grandfather's will — remarking sardonically that he "could feed the whole State of [[montana|Montana]] for less than six thousand dollars" (009-035). Already irritated by the letter of the law governing his inheritance, Brewster vents his frustration and then turns his attention to the morning newspapers.

The papers are filled with accounts of the upcoming bout in San Francisco. Brewster immerses himself in the fight coverage, "revelling in the descriptions of 'upper cuts' and 'left hooks'" (009-002). He learns that the match is expected to be "quite one-sided": a local amateur is set to box a professional champion (009-002). Spotting what he believes to be a mispriced opportunity, Brewster persuades himself that [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] could not object to "such a display of sportsmanship" (009-002).

He telephones [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]] and instructs him to place several wagers on the amateur. [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]] soon reports back that he has placed three thousand dollars on Brewster's chosen fighter (009-002). Brewster is so confident of the outcome that he records the bets on the profit side of his ledger the moment he receives [[nopper-harrison|Harrison]]'s confirmation — treating the wager as though the winnings were already in hand (009-002).

## Consequences

The prizefight wager proved to be a miscalculation. The final accounting of Brewster's expenditures, rendered in the balance sheet submitted to [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]], lists a curt entry: "Prizefight misjudged | 1,000.00" (031-018). The discrepancy between the $3,000 bet and the $1,000 recorded loss suggests that some portion of the wager may have been recovered, or that the loss was consolidated with other entries. Regardless, the prizefight stands as one of the earliest examples of Brewster's pattern of impulsive financial decision-making — a pattern that would repeat throughout his year-long ordeal.

The same afternoon, Brewster telephoned [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]] and arranged to visit her. His behaviour in the preceding days had been marked by "uneasiness, supplemented by moroseness and occasional periods of irascibility" (009-002) — symptoms that Barbara, experienced in the study of men, recognized immediately. The prizefight thus functions as both a financial set piece and a narrative bridge to the developing romantic tension between Brewster and Barbara.

## Analysis

The prizefight represents Brewster's early, reckless phase of spending — when he still believed he could game the system by making speculative investments that might turn a profit. The irony is sharp: Brewster is required to lose money, yet his instinct is to gamble on a winner. When the bet fails, it ironically helps his cause by reducing his fortune, though not in a way he can claim credit for.

The episode also illustrates the tension between Brewster's self-image as a shrewd judge of character and the reality of his inexperience. He "cajoles himself into the belief" that [[swearengen-jones|Jones]] cannot object, revealing a pattern of self-deception that recurs throughout the novel (009-002). The prizefight is a minor financial event in the grand scheme of Brewster's million-dollar spending spree, but it is emblematic of his approach: impulsive, optimistic, and almost always wrong.
