## Overview

Christmas arrives as an unexpected crisis in Brewster's spending campaign. In Chapter 12 — titled "Christmas Despair" — the holiday season transforms from a time of celebration into a financial and emotional trial. Brewster has been spending lavishly to exhaust his inheritance, but Christmas presents pose a unique challenge: how to give generously without appearing to preserve his fortune, and how to navigate the social expectations of gift-[[charity|giving]] while remaining within the spirit of his uncle's will (012-001).

The chapter opens with Brewster's unlikely act of mercy toward a thief, but its emotional core lies in his Christmas preparations. Over two weeks, he mornings at Tiffany's and afternoons scouring antique dealers along Fourth and [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]s, purchasing gifts that "elaborately concealed their value" (012-033). The chapter closes with a devastating personal blow: [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]] returns his gift of pearls, and Brewster resolves to leave [[new-york-city|New York]] aboard a chartered yacht.

## Narrative

The chapter opens on an unexpected note of grace. Brewster has been summoned to police headquarters to identify the men who robbed him. He immediately recognizes Bill, one of the thieves, but instead of pressing charges, Brewster vouches for him: "Pass Bill, Captain. He was with me last night just before my money was taken, and he couldn't possibly have robbed me without my knowledge" (012-001). Outside, Bill returns Brewster's watch, and [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]] refuses the stolen money: "Keep it, Bill, you need it more than I do. It's enough to set you up in some other trade" (012-001). Bill, moved to gratitude, promises: "if ever I can do anything for you jes' put me nex'" (012-001).

The narrative then shifts to Brewster's social planning. He visits [[mrs-dan-demille|Mrs. Dan DeMille]] to discuss an upcoming ball and yachting cruise. [[mrs-dan-demille|Mrs. Dan]] reassures him: "give me until after Christmas and I'll have the finest favors ever seen" (012-031). But her reference to Christmas triggers a wave of anxiety in Brewster. "Never before had he looked upon presents as a calamity; but this year it was different" (012-032).

Brewster immediately telegraphs [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]], the will's executor, seeking guidance. [[swearengen-jones|Jones]]'s reply — delivered with "pleasing irascibility" — declares that "anyone with a drop of human kindness in his body would consider it his duty to give Christmas presents to those who deserved them" (012-032). His path cleared, Brewster embarks on a two-week spending spree.

"For two weeks his mornings were spent at Tiffany's, and the afternoons brought joy to the heart of every dealer in antiquities in Fourth and [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]s. He gave much thought to the matter in the effort to secure many small articles which elaborately concealed their value. And he had taste" (012-033). The result was a Christmas Eve bombardment: "many friends who would not have thought of remembering [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]] with even a card were pleasantly surprised on Christmas Eve" (012-033).

The emotional climax comes after Christmas. Brewster "fared very well in the matter of gifts, and for some days much of his time was spent in reading notes of profuse thanks, which were yet vaguely apologetic" (012-033). But [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]] had "forgotten him," and when they met after the holiday "her recognition was of the coldest" (012-033). The "beautiful pearls with which he asked for a reconciliation were returned with '[[barbara-drew|Miss Drew]]'s thanks'" — and Brewster, who "loved Barbara sincerely," was cut to the heart (012-033).

He turns to [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]] for comfort. She encourages him to try again with Barbara, though "it was a bit difficult for her to advise him to try again, but his happiness was a thing she had at heart" (012-033). Brewster, wounded, declares: "It's beastly unfair, [[peggy-gray|Peggy]]. I've really been white to her. I believe I'll chuck the whole business and leave [[new-york-city|New York]]" (012-034). When he announces his plan to "charter a yacht and sail away from this place for three or four months," [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] gasps — and then laughs: "I think you'll end in the poorhouse, [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]]" (012-034).

## Consequences

The Christmas Party chapter sets three major narrative threads in motion:

1. **The yachting cruise**: Brewster's impulsive decision to charter a yacht directly leads to the *Flitter* expedition (Chapters 16–29), the longest and most expensive episode of his spending campaign, which consumes over $210,000 (029-012).

2. **The breach with Barbara**: The returned pearls mark a decisive turn in Brewster's romantic life. Barbara's coldness deepens throughout the novel, culminating in her eventual engagement to a European aristocrat — news that reaches Brewster in Chapter 29 (029-020).

3. **The deepening of [[peggy-gray|Peggy]]'s role**: The Christmas chapter is the first time [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]] is explicitly shown as Brewster's emotional anchor. Her willingness to comfort him even as she advises him to pursue another woman establishes the quiet devotion that will prove decisive in the novel's final chapters.

The chapter also demonstrates a recurring pattern in Brewster's spending: his instinct for generosity repeatedly conflicts with the terms of the will. His gifts were thoughtful and tasteful — "elaborately concealed their value" — yet they were genuine expressions of affection, not the wasteful expenditures the challenge demanded. This tension between Brewster's natural character and the artificial role the will forces upon him defines the novel's central irony.
