# Inheritance

## Overview

Inheritance is the structural foundation of *Brewster's Millions*. The novel's entire plot turns on two inheritances: the one million dollars Montgomery Brewster receives from his grandfather Edwin Peter Brewster, and the seven-million-dollar estate bequeathed to him by his uncle James T. Sedgwick — but only on the extraordinary condition that he first rid himself of the Brewster fortune. Through this double inheritance, the novel examines wealth not as earned but as received, and the moral obligations that accompany unearned fortune.

## In the Novel

### Two Fortunes, One Heir

Montgomery Brewster is the beneficiary of two very different legacies. From his grandfather Edwin Brewster, a New York banker, he receives one million dollars — a substantial fortune by the standards of 1900. The narrator notes that "the banker had bequeathed one million dollars to his grandson" in what seemed a conventional inheritance (004-019).

The second inheritance is far larger and far stranger. James T. Sedgwick, who "died possessed of an estate valued at almost seven million dollars" (004-006), accumulated through gold mines and ranches, also names Brewster as his sole heir. But Sedgwick's will is not a simple bequest — it is an instrument of revenge against the dead Edwin Brewster.

### Inheritance as Vengeance

James Sedgwick's will is driven by hatred. The narrator explains that Sedgwick declared "that his heir should not receive the fortune if he possessed a single penny that had come to him, in any shape or form, from the man he hated, Edwin Peter Brewster" (004-019). The inheritance thus becomes a weapon in a feud that outlives both antagonists. Sedgwick could not prevent Edwin Brewster from leaving money to his grandson, but he could force Montgomery to choose: the million from his grandfather, or the seven million from his uncle.

The condition transforms inheritance from a gift into a test. Brewster is not simply given wealth; he must prove himself worthy of it by first demonstrating that he can live without any wealth at all — arriving "absolutely penniless upon the twenty-sixth anniversary of his birth" (004-018).

### The Moral Weight of Unearned Wealth

The novel probes the ethics of inheritance through its supporting characters. Mrs. Gray and Peggy, whose "meager fortune had been entrusted" to risky investments (013-027), represent those who lack the safety net of inherited wealth. Brewster's friends, observing his profligacy, question whether inherited money carries any moral weight at all. The challenge itself becomes a critique of passive inheritance — Sedgwick requires Brewster to demonstrate "the ability to add to the fortune through his own enterprise" (004-020), imposing a meritocratic condition on what would otherwise be an unearned windfall.
