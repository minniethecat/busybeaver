## Overview

The De Mille Ball, hosted by Mrs. De Mille and funded by [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]]'s seemingly inexhaustible fortune, is a lavish society event that serves as the backdrop for the strained romantic relations between Brewster and [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]]. After a falling-out, Barbara initially excludes Brewster from her cotillon, choosing Herbert Ailing to lead instead. [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Drew]], who has become fond of Brewster, intervenes and compels his daughter to extend an invitation (014-001). Brewster accepts, hoping for reconciliation, but Barbara receives him with "icy reserve" — setting the stage for one of the novel's most pointed social confrontations (014-001).

## Narrative

The rift between Brewster and Barbara had been brewing for some time before the ball. When [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Drew]] discovers that Brewster has been excluded from the cotillon guest list, he is incensed. "Why not [[montgomery-brewster|Monty Brewster]], Babs?" he demands (014-001). Barbara responds coolly that Brewster "has not been asked" (014-001). The Colonel, who knows that Brewster's money has made the entire affair possible, threatens to cancel the event: "There will not be any party, if [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]] is not asked" (014-001).

Barbara, weeping but obedient to her father's will, sends a reluctant invitation — but accompanies it with "a very cold and difficult little note of apology" (014-001). Brewster, however, reads the gesture optimistically: "He only saw in it a ray of hope that Barbara was relenting and was jubilant at the prospect of a reconciliation" (014-001).

The reunion at the ball proves disastrous. When Brewster seeks a private interview, Barbara receives him with "icy reserve" (014-001). His attempt at reconciliation is met with disdain. When he reminds her that she once told him she cared, she retorts: "When you are ready to treat me with respect I may listen to your petition" (014-001). The word "petition" stings Brewster, and his tact deserts him: "It's as much yours as mine. Don't throw the burden of responsibility on me, [[barbara-drew|Miss Drew]]" (014-001). Barbara excuses herself curtly, leaving Brewster humiliated.

## Consequences

The De Mille Ball marks a low point in the Brewster–Barbara romance. Rather than healing the breach, the forced invitation and the tense exchange at the ball deepen the misunderstanding on both sides. Brewster leaves feeling "hurt and insulted," while Barbara remains convinced that he has "imposed upon her disgracefully" (014-001).

The ball also illustrates the social dynamics that Brewster's fortune has created: [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Drew]]'s insistence on Brewster's invitation is driven not purely by affection for the young man, but by an awareness of who is funding the festivities. The tension between genuine feeling and financial dependency runs throughout the episode.

## Analysis

The De Mille Ball episode crystallizes the novel's treatment of pride and social performance. Both Barbara and Brewster are performing roles — she the wounded aristocrat, he the magnanimous suitor — and both performances fail. The ballroom, with its elaborate choreography of manners, becomes a stage on which genuine emotion cannot find expression.

The episode also showcases [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Drew]] as a mediating figure. Unlike the younger generation, he sees clearly that money and affection have become entangled in Brewster's courtship, and his pragmatic intervention — however heavy-handed — represents an older generation's approach to such entanglements.
