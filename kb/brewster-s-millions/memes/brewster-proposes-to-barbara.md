## Overview

The courtship of Barbara Drew by [[montgomery-brewster|Montgomery Brewster]] is the central romantic thread of the novel, running parallel to his financial ordeal. Brewster's proposal to Barbara represents the collision of two forces in his life: his genuine love for her and the ruinous financial terms of his uncle's will. The proposal is further complicated by the deep opposition of [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Prentiss Drew]], Barbara's father, who views Brewster's extravagant spending with suspicion and contempt.

## Narrative

Brewster's affection for [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]] is established early in the narrative. At the birthday dinner that opens the novel, Barbara is present among the guests, and Brewster's particular attention to her is noted by those in attendance (006-029). As his fortune grows — and with it the pressure to spend recklessly — Brewster finds himself drawn increasingly to Barbara, whose quiet dignity and lack of mercenary interest set her apart from the society women now pursuing him.

The courtship intensifies during the autumn social season. At the [[the-charity-bazaar|charity bazaar at the Astoria]], Brewster competes with a rival suitor for Barbara's attention, patronizing her booth with conspicuous generosity (011-007) (011-008). Yet Barbara is troubled by Brewster's transformation. His sudden wealth and the company he keeps raise questions she cannot answer:

> She was not insensible to the significance of his inquiry if she could only have divined its import. (009-003)

> The fervor of his half-whispered words took possession of her. She had expected protestations of love, but there was something in his manner that she could not fathom. (009-019)

The critical obstacle emerges in the person of [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Prentiss Drew]]. Barbara's father, a man of rigid principles and old-fashioned propriety, views Brewster's spending spree as evidence of moral dissolution. The Colonel's opposition crystallizes during a confrontation in which he forbids Barbara from continuing the relationship (013-026). Brewster, despite having nearly $200,000 at his disposal at that point, finds his wealth powerless against the Colonel's intransigence (013-006).

> He was Barbara's father. The proud old man would not be moved by the desperate appeal of the young spendthrift. (013-026)

Barbara, caught between filial duty and her feelings for Brewster, weeps after her father's decree but does not openly defy him (014-014). The proposal, when it finally comes, is shadowed by the knowledge that Brewster's financial situation — both its true nature and the public perception of it — makes their future together uncertain.

## Consequences

The proposal and its aftermath drive much of the emotional tension through the middle chapters of the novel. Barbara's partial estrangement from Brewster isolates him at the very moment he most needs emotional support. Her father's opposition forces Brewster to confront the social cost of his mandated [[profligacy|extravagance]]: the very spending required to secure [[the-sedgwick-fortune|the inheritance]] is what alienates him from the woman he loves.

This romantic complication intertwines with [[the-sedgwick-fortune|the inheritance]] plot when [[mrs-gray|Mrs. Gray]], Barbara's aunt, later appeals directly to Brewster regarding the pain his behavior causes Barbara (030-002) (030-003). The proposal thus becomes not merely a romantic milestone but a measure of Brewster's moral trajectory — whether he will choose love over lucre, or whether the will's iron condition will cost him everything that matters.

## Key Passages

> It was only toward the end of the evening that Montgomery Brewster found the opportunity to exchange a few words with Barbara Drew, and they were words that meant more to him than all the congratulations of the night. (006-029)

> For the first time he was beginning to feel the unpleasant pangs of jealousy, and they were not lessened by the evident pleasure Barbara took in the attentions of the tall young man. (008-004)

> Brewster walked to the club, elated by the memory of the moments he had spent with her and yet troubled by the shadow of a doubt that had been cast over his hopes. (009-031)
