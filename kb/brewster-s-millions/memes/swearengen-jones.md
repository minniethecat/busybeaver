# Swearengen Jones

## Character Overview

Swearengen Jones is the unseen but decisive presence in *Brewster's Millions*. From his base in Butte, Montana, he serves as executor of James T. Sedgwick's will — the man charged with determining whether Montgomery Brewster has satisfied the extraordinary conditions of his inheritance. Jones never appears in person during the novel's main action, yet his letters and telegrams punctuate the narrative like the voice of an absent judge, reminding Brewster that his spending is being watched and measured from afar.

## Role in the Novel

### The Distant Executor

Jones first enters the story through "a communication from Mr. Swearengen Jones of Montana, conveying the sad intelligence that your uncle, James T. Sedgwick, died on the 24th" (003-044). From that moment, he becomes the novel's moral arbiter — the one person who knows the truth of Brewster's challenge and will ultimately determine its outcome.

His location in Montana is significant. The novel contrasts Jones's frontier directness with New York sophistication. His letters are terse and factual: "Swearengen Jones, Butte, Montana" (008-009). He represents a world in which character is judged by actions rather than appearances.

### Judge of the Challenge

Jones's role is to determine whether Brewster has "in every particular, complied with the requirements of the will, to the full satisfaction of the said executor" (004-019). This grants him enormous discretionary power. He must judge not only whether the money has been spent but whether Brewster has maintained the character standards Sedgwick demanded — temperate habits, a fair name, business capability.

His instructions to Brewster, conveyed through the lawyers Grant & Ripley, reveal a pragmatic moralist. He forbids "idiotic Board of Trade speculation" and "indiscriminate giving away of funds" (005-055), but allows reasonable latitude. He is strict but not unreasonable — a fair judge rather than a cruel one.

### The Final Judgment

The novel's resolution depends on Jones's final assessment. Brewster's report "To Swearengen Jones, Esq., Executor under the will of the late James T. Sedgwick of Montana" (031-018) is the document on which everything turns. Jones's response — conveyed in the final chapter — determines whether Brewster's year of profligacy has been a success or a failure.

Jones's judgment represents the novel's final word on the relationship between wealth and character. As Sedgwick's chosen proxy, he embodies the values that built the fortune: shrewdness, thrift, and plain dealing. His verdict is the moral conclusion of the story.
