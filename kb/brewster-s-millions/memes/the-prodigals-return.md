## Overview

Chapter 29, "The Prodigal's Return," marks the emotional nadir of Brewster's year-long ordeal. After the *Flitter* is towed into Southampton by a freighter — a salvage operation costing $50,000 — Brewster and his party race back to [[new-york-city|New York]] aboard the fastest available liner (029-001). The chapter chronicles Brewster's final weeks: the disposal of his furniture and belongings, the public humiliation of his homecoming, and the grim satisfaction of reducing his fortune to $79.08 with two days remaining before the deadline (029-020).

The prodigal's return is not celebrated but condemned. "Friends of former days gave him the cut direct, clubmen ignored him or scorned him openly, women chilled him with the iciness of unspoken reproof, and all the world was hung with shadows" (029-018). Only [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]] and the "Little Sons of the Rich" remain loyal — and Brewster, for the first time, begins to tell [[peggy-gray|Peggy]] the truth.

## Narrative

The chapter opens with the *Flitter* in tow of the freighter *Glencoe*, whose thrifty Scotch captain extracts a $50,000 salvage fee from Brewster — a sum the young man welcomes as providential assistance (029-001). After stops along the English coast, the party reaches Southampton on August 28th. Brewster books passage on the fastest ship to [[new-york-city|New York]], can only secure space for twelve of his guests, and entrusts Bragdon with $15,000 to arrange the *Flitter*'s repairs and the remaining guests' return — extracting "a solemn promise that the entire amount would be used" (029-008).

On the crossing, Brewster pores over his accounts. "Going over the statistics carefully, he estimated that the cruise, including the repairs and return of [[the-yacht-flora-belle|the yacht]] to [[new-york-city|New York]], would cost him $210,000 in round figures. One hundred and thirty-three days marked the length of the voyage when reckoned by time and, as near as he could get at it, the expense had averaged $1,580 a day" (029-012). His fortune has "dwindled to a few paltry thousands."

The landing in [[new-york-city|New York]] is brutal. "Piles of newspapers lay scattered about the floor of the room in which they sat. Every one of them contained sensational stories of the prodigal's trip, with pictures, incidents and predictions. [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]] was pained, humiliated and resentful" (029-018). [[elon-gardner|Gardner]] delivers the grim summary: "The worst of it, [[montgomery-brewster|Monty]], is that you are the next thing to being a poor man."

What follows is a portrait of social ostracism. "With the condemnation of his friends ringing in his troubled brain, with the sneers of acquaintances to distress his pride, with the jibes of the comic papers to torture him remorselessly, Brewster was fast becoming the most miserable man in [[new-york-city|New York]]" (029-018). He is described as "almost a physical wreck, haggard, thin and defiant, a shadow of the once debonair young [[new-york-city|New York]]er, an object of pity and scorn" (029-018).

Yet Brewster presses on. "In absolute recklessness he gave dinner after dinner, party after party, all on a most lavish scale, many of his guests laughing at him openly while they enjoyed his hospitality" (029-018). Piece by piece, his possessions vanish: "At last the furniture began to go, then the plate, then all the priceless bric-a-brac" (029-020). The sale of his belongings yields $40,350, which he proceeds to spend as well.

In one of the chapter's most poignant moments, Brewster "implored the faithful Bragdon to steal the Boston terriers. He could not and would not sell them and he dared not give them away. Bragdon dejectedly appropriated the dogs" (029-021).

He learns, almost indifferently, "that [[barbara-drew|Miss Barbara Drew]] was to become a duchess before the winter was gone" (029-020) — a report that confirms the finality of their estrangement. And he faces the ultimate indignity: "Up in [[fortieth-street|Fortieth Street]] [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]] was grieving her heart out and he knew it" (029-021).

The chapter closes with Brewster taking a room in the old [[fortieth-street|Fortieth Street]] house, "though he quailed at the thought of making them a refuge, he faced the ordeal in the spirit of a martyr" (029-021). He has reduced the million to $79.08 and is preparing his final report to [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] — the receipts for every dollar spent, "faithfully and honorably kept" (030-002).

## Consequences

The Prodigal's Return chapter sets the stage for the novel's climax:

1. **The million nearly gone**: Brewster ends the chapter with only $79.08 and forty-eight hours remaining — a position that seems impossible to escape, setting up the final crisis of the [[million-dollar-challenge|spending challenge]].

2. **The social judgment**: The chapter provides the novel's most sustained portrait of public opinion. Brewster is branded a fool by society, yet the reader knows the truth: every apparent act of folly is calculated obedience to the will's terms.

3. **The loyalty of the few**: Against the backdrop of universal condemnation, the steadfastness of [[peggy-gray|Peggy Gray]], [[joe-bragdon|Joe Bragdon]], and the "Little Sons of the Rich" stands in sharp relief. Their loyalty, offered without understanding, is the novel's moral counterweight to Brewster's public disgrace.

4. **The Barbara arc closes**: The report of Barbara's impending marriage to a duke closes the romantic subplot that has run through the novel, clearing the way for Brewster's eventual recognition of [[peggy-gray|Peggy]]'s devotion.

5. **The final accounting**: Brewster's meticulous preservation of receipts — "thousands upon thousands of them" — represents his integrity within apparent dissolution. He has spent a million dollars, but he has done so honestly, and he can prove it.
