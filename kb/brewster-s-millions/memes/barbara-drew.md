# Barbara Drew

## Character Overview

Barbara Drew is the leading society figure among the younger set of *Brewster's Millions*. Beautiful, poised, and occupying a prominent position in New York's social world, she becomes the object of Montgomery Brewster's romantic attention. Her relationship with Brewster is shaped — and ultimately strained — by the secrecy surrounding his spending challenge. Barbara represents the social world that judges Brewster by appearances, and her shifting affections trace the arc of his public reputation.

## Role in the Novel

### Society Beauty

Barbara Drew moves at the center of New York society. She presides over the charity bazaar at the Astoria, where "the booth presided over by Miss Drew" (011-007) attracts the attention of the fashionable world. Her name appears in the society columns, and her social standing makes her a desirable match for a young man of Brewster's position.

### Romance and Estrangement

Brewster's interest in Barbara begins as "merely a society acquaintance" (005-027) but deepens over the course of the novel. Barbara, for her part, "loved Brewster" (009-019) — but her love is contingent on understanding him. When Brewster's spending becomes notorious and inexplicable, Barbara's affection wavers.

The narrator captures the fragility of society romance: "Miss Drew had forgotten him, and when they met again she was too generously grateful" (012-033). Barbara's feelings are real but shallow — they depend on Brewster maintaining the social standing that his spending challenge requires him to jeopardize.

### A Test of Character

Barbara Drew functions in the novel as a measure of Brewster's social standing. When he is in favor, she is attentive; when his reputation suffers, she withdraws. This is not presented as a moral failing so much as a consequence of her world — a world in which a man's worth is inseparable from his wealth and reputation.

The contrast with Peggy Gray is implicit but clear. Where Barbara's love is conditioned on social acceptability, Peggy's loyalty endures without explanation. Barbara is not a villain but a product of her environment — and her inability to love Brewster without understanding him reveals the limits of society romance.
