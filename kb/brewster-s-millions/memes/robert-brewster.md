## Overview

Robert Brewster is Montgomery Brewster's father, who died before the events of the novel alongside his wife, Mrs. Brewster. Though he never appears in the narrative as a living character, his position in the Brewster family line — as the son of the wealthy industrialist Edwin Peter Brewster and the father of the protagonist Montgomery — is central to the inheritance plot that drives the novel.

## Role in the Story

Robert Brewster is referenced almost exclusively in the context of his father's will. When Montgomery meets with the executor, Mr. Grant, the will recounts the family history: it specifies "the date of your birth, of the death of Robert Brewster and of Mrs. Brewster" (004-014). This legal recitation is the closest the novel comes to giving Robert and his wife any biographical detail — their lives are reduced to dates in a legal document.

Montgomery is identified in the will as "the son of Robert and [his mother]," establishing the direct patrilineal descent from Edwin Peter through Robert to Montgomery (004-018). This line of descent is crucial because it makes Montgomery the sole surviving male heir — the last link in the Brewster chain — and thus the sole recipient of the grandfather's eccentric bequest.

The death of both parents leaves Montgomery without immediate family at the novel's opening, a circumstance that amplifies his vulnerability to the grandfather's posthumous scheme. Without a father to advise him on the inheritance, Montgomery must navigate the million-dollar challenge entirely on his own.

## Relationships

Robert Brewster is the son of **Edwin Peter Brewster**, the wealthy industrialist whose fortune provides the novel's central plot device. The nature of the father-son relationship is never explored in the text — Robert exists primarily as a genealogical link, the means by which the Brewster fortune passes from one generation to the next.

As the father of **Montgomery Brewster**, Robert's early death has shaped the protagonist's life in ways the novel implies rather than states. Montgomery was raised to expect a conventional inheritance, presumably from his father's share of the family wealth. Robert's death — and the grandfather's decision to bypass the normal generational transfer in favor of his eccentric will — places Montgomery in a position of financial dependence on his grandfather that the father, had he lived, might have mediated or prevented.

## Significance

Robert Brewster is a figure defined by absence. His death, along with his wife's, creates the familial vacuum at the center of the novel — Montgomery Brewster is an orphan-heir, disconnected from the normal structures of family authority and inheritance. In a novel concerned with the moral weight of inherited wealth, Robert Brewster represents the missing middle generation: the father who might have provided guidance, context, or resistance to the grandfather's scheme. His absence ensures that Montgomery faces the million-dollar challenge alone, with no paternal figure to mediate between the dead patriarch's demands and the living heir's predicament.
