## Overview

Mr. Grant is the senior partner of Grant & Ripley, the New York law firm entrusted with executing the will of James T. Sedgwick (003-043). He serves as Brewster's primary point of contact with the legal machinery of the Sedgwick inheritance, delivering the extraordinary terms of the will and providing counsel — often with genial amusement — throughout Brewster's spending ordeal.

## Role in the Story

Grant first appears through correspondence: the law firm Grant & Ripley sends Brewster a letter requesting his immediate presence (003-043). When Brewster visits the office, Mr. Grant genially explains the situation, noting that "everyone who knew your uncle knew that he was peculiar" (004-004).

Grant reads the will aloud in his office (004-017), then watches with what appears to be professional patience as Brewster struggles to comprehend its terms. When Brewster asks "How do you like it?", Grant simply passes the will across the desk for Brewster to read himself (004-021). His manner is mild and occasionally bemused: when Brewster asks about spending a million to gain seven million, Grant mildly inquires, "and how do you purpose doing it?" (004-031) (004-032).

Throughout the narrative, Grant remains a steady presence. He "leaned forward slowly" (004-036) during key conversations and "smiled broadly" at Brewster's concern over telegraph charges (005-052). He coordinates with his partner, exchanging "quick, significant" glances (005-054), and communicates with Swearengen Jones in Montana on Brewster's behalf (005-006) (005-064).

## Relationships

Grant's professional relationship with Montgomery Brewster evolves from formal attorney-client interactions to something approaching amused guardianship. He communicates regularly with Swearengen Jones, the co-executor of the Sedgwick estate (005-006). His partnership with Mr. Ripley at Grant & Ripley forms the legal backbone of the novel's inheritance plot (005-054).

## Significance

Mr. Grant embodies the institutional, legal dimension of the Sedgwick fortune. Where Jones represents the moral test of the inheritance (observing from Montana whether Brewster acts honestly), Grant represents its legal machinery — the dry, procedural unfolding of James T. Sedgwick's eccentric testamentary wishes. His genial, professional demeanor creates a counterpoint to Brewster's increasingly frantic spending, and his recurring presence anchors the plot's central financial stakes in recognizable legal reality.

## Notes

Mr. Grant's first name is never provided in the novel; he is consistently referred to as "Mr. Grant." The law firm name "Grant & Ripley" suggests he is the founding or senior partner (003-043).
