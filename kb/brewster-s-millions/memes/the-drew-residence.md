## The Drew Household

The Drew residence was the New York home of [[colonel-prentiss-drew|Colonel Prentiss Drew]], a railroad magnate, and his daughter [[barbara-drew|Barbara Drew]]. Colonel Drew was a neighbor and associate of the Brewster family, and "had shown a fondness for young Mr. Brewster" (002-008). Monty was "a frequent visitor at his house," and Colonel Drew affectionately called him "my young friend" (002-008).

The household was one of the established centers of New York society, with both Colonel Drew and Barbara maintaining active social calendars. The residence served as a venue for formal dinners, afternoon calls, and the intricate social rituals of the upper class.

## Courtship at the Drew Residence

The Drew home became the setting for key moments in Brewster's courtship of Barbara. After the revelation of his inheritance, Brewster's social standing made him an even more welcome guest. He "had dined at the Drews the night before and Barbara had had more charm for him than usual" (004-002), marking one of the early high points of their romance.

The rhythm of dining at the Drew residence established the pattern of their developing relationship. On another occasion, Brewster "had dined at the Drews' the evening before and had had an awakening" (008-002), suggesting that dinners at the Drew household were often turning points in his emotional life. The formality of these occasions — the drawing room, the dining table, the post-dinner conversation — provided the backdrop against which Brewster and Barbara's relationship was measured.

## Social Functions

The Drew residence hosted significant society events. During one dinner party, Colonel Drew "took in Mrs. Valentine and his daughter sat next to Brewster" (006-023). These carefully orchestrated seating arrangements were instruments of social strategy. Across the table, Barbara could hear "Colonel Drew's voice — 'Brewster evidently offended some one'" (006-026), revealing how the gossip of the circle circulated even at formal dinners.

The residence was also the site of a notable incident involving a butler and a dinner card: "it took on a different color when the butler handed him a card which signified that he was to take Miss Drew in to dinner" (014-033), demonstrating the elaborate protocol governing social interaction at the Drew table.

## A Point of Departure

As the estrangement between Brewster and Barbara deepened, the Drew residence became a place of tension rather than warmth. On one occasion, "just before leaving his apartments for Miss Drew's home he received this response from faraway Montana" (009-012) — a letter from [[swearengen-jones|Swearengen Jones]] that complicated Brewster's evening visit.

The shift in the Drew residence's function — from welcoming social hub to site of strained formality — mirrored the arc of Brewster and Barbara's relationship across the novel.
