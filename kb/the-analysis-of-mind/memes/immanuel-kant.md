Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) was a German philosopher whose *Critique of Pure Reason* established the framework of transcendental idealism and whose classification of mental phenomena shaped subsequent psychology. In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell invokes Kant at several strategic points — not as an authority to be followed but as a representative of philosophical positions that require reexamination in light of behaviourist psychology and neutral monism.

## The Tripartite Division of Mind

Russell's most sustained reference to Kant concerns the classification of mental phenomena. Since Kant, psychology and philosophy have recognized three fundamental categories: knowledge, desire, and feeling (where "feeling" designates pleasure and discomfort). Russell acknowledges this framework but immediately subjects its first category to critique:

> [003-020] Since Kant it has been customary to recognize three great divisions of mental phenomena, which are typified by knowledge, desire and feeling, where "feeling" is used to mean pleasure and discomfort. Of course, "knowledge" is too definite a word.

Russell's reservation about "knowledge" reflects a central theme of his lectures: that what traditional philosophy calls knowledge — a relation between a knowing subject and a known object — is better analyzed as a collection of more primitive phenomena, including [[sensation]], [[belief]], and [[habit]]. The Kantian tripartition, while historically influential, is too coarse-grained for Russell's analytical purposes.

## Kant and Introspective Certainty

Russell also discusses Kant in the context of introspection's trustworthiness. The Cartesian-Kantian tradition held that knowledge of one's own mental states enjoys a special certainty unavailable to knowledge of the external world. Russell traces this view through British empiricism and finds its descendant in Kant:

> [006-003] Corresponding to the supposed duality of matter and mind, there are, in orthodox psychology, two ways of knowing what exists. One of these, the way of sensation and external perception, is supposed to furnish data for our knowledge of matter, the other, called "introspection," is supposed to furnish data for our knowledge of mental processes.

Russell engages this tradition through the lens of the American behaviourist critique. He grants that introspection provides knowledge of a kind — we can observe our own sensations, images, and feelings — but he rejects the claim that such knowledge is infallible or that it reveals a unified [[self]] standing behind mental phenomena. The Kantian "I think" that accompanies all representations is, for Russell, not a datum of introspection but a philosophical construction that needs to be dissolved.

## The Kantian "I Think"

Russell cites Kant's doctrine of the transcendental unity of apperception — the claim that the "I think" must be capable of accompanying all representations — in his discussion of consciousness. He treats Kant's position as particularly instructive because it represents the most rigorous attempt to establish the certainty of self-consciousness:

> [001-035] He does not believe in the supposed immediate certainty of thought. "Let the case be what it may in others," he says, "I am as confident as I am of anything that, in myself, the stream of thinking (which I recognize emphatically as a phenomenon) is only a careless name for what, when scrutinized, reveals itself to consist chiefly of the stream of my breathing."

The passage Russell quotes illustrates his own approach: replacing the Kantian transcendental subject with observable bodily and mental processes. For Russell, what Kant called the "I think" is better understood as the stream of sensations, images, and bodily feelings that constitute our mental life — no unitary self is required.

## Methodological Significance

Russell's treatment of Kant exemplifies his broader strategy in *The Analysis of Mind*. Kantian categories — the tripartite division of mind, the duality of matter and mind, the transcendental unity of apperception — serve as foils against which Russell develops his [[neutral-monism]]. Where Kant sought to establish the conditions of possible experience through transcendental argument, Russell pursues an empirical analysis that draws equally on philosophical logic and experimental psychology.

This ambivalent relationship to Kant reflects Russell's intellectual trajectory. Trained in the idealist tradition at Cambridge, Russell's early philosophical development was shaped by Kantian and Hegelian thought. By the time of *The Analysis of Mind*, he had broken decisively with idealism — but Kant's systematic ambition and his attention to the structure of mental life remained a model for Russell's own project, even as Russell rejected Kant's specific doctrines.
