## Definition

The subject–object duality is the philosophical doctrine that knowledge and consciousness involve an irreducible relation between a subject (the knower, the mind) and an object (the known, the external world). Russell identifies this dualism as a core assumption requiring scrutiny: "after mentioning the duality of subject and object, which is supposed to constitute knowledge" (001-033), he proceeds to question whether this relation is genuinely primitive or merely a reification of grammatical structure.

## Russell's Account

Russell's critique of the subject–object dualism runs through the entire book. He traces its origin to the grammatical habit of treating experience as requiring a bearer: "one of its 'terms' becomes the subject or bearer of the knowledge" (001-032). This reification, he argues, produces an unnecessary entity—the subject as a substantial self. [[Alexius Meinong|Meinong's]] "'act' is the ghost of the subject, or what once was the full-blown soul" (001-022); the philosophical tradition, having abandoned the soul, preserves its shadow in the form of a subject that supposedly performs acts of consciousness.

Russell turns to [[Knight Dunlap]] for an alternative: "Dunlap's view is that there is a dualism of subject and object, but that the subject can never be perceived" (006-012). On this view, introspection never reveals a subject—only objects of awareness. This observation leads Dunlap, and Russell after him, to conclude that the subject is "the consequence of his abandoning the dualism of thought and things" (006-009): if thinking is not fundamentally different in kind from its objects, the need for a special subjective pole disappears.

## Key Distinctions

### Subject–Object vs. Mind–Matter

Russell distinguishes the epistemological dualism of subject–object from the metaphysical dualism of mind–matter. "Corresponding to the supposed duality of matter and mind, there are... different sorts of duality" (006-003), including the subject–object distinction. His strategy is to dissolve both: by abandoning "the dualism of thought and things" (006-009), the subject–object problem is revealed as a pseudo-problem generated by a mistaken theory of knowledge.

### The Unobservable Subject

A key argument Russell deploys is the unobservability of the subject. If introspection "after abandoning the dualism of thoughts and things" (006-014) reveals only particular mental contents and never a substantial self, then the subject is an unnecessary postulate. Russell's neutral monism proposes that what we call "subject" and "object" are different logical constructions out of the same neutral stuff—sensations and images—rather than fundamentally different kinds of entity.

## Historical Context

The subject–object dualism has its roots in [[Rene Descartes|Descartes's]] cogito, which posited a thinking substance as the foundation of knowledge. [[Franz Brentano]] revived the concept through his notion of intentionality—the "act" of consciousness directed toward an object—which Russell directly engages. [[William James|James's]] radical empiricism, which considered "subject and object to be the same thing, but at different times" (006-010), provides Russell with an alternative framework that deeply informs his neutral monism.
