## Definition

Meaning, in Russell's behaviourist account, is not a mental entity or an abstract relation between words and things but a function of the habits governing the use of words in context. Russell approaches meaning through the lens of linguistic behaviour: "a set of habits in the use of words" (001-040) constitutes what it is for a speaker to understand or employ a term meaningfully. The apprehension of meaning, traditionally regarded as a paradigmatic mental act, is recast as a behavioural disposition.

## Russell's Account

Russell postpones full treatment of linguistic meaning until later in *The Analysis of Mind*, but his key commitments are stated early. He notes that spoken and written words are part of the "physical behaviour" (001-041) that provides the behaviourist's primary evidence. There is, he argues, "no reason — so Watson contends — to suppose that anything different occurs when we think in words from what occurs when we speak aloud" (001-041); the difference is one of degree, not of kind.

Russell's behaviourist treatment of meaning is closely tied to his analysis of the image-word relationship. He observes that "my apprehension of its meaning is mental" (001-050) is the commonsense view, but challenges it by asking what observable difference meaning-understanding makes to behaviour. The answer, for Russell, lies in the causal role of words in shaping action: words, like images, can serve as proximate causes of behaviour when embedded in the right habit structures.

The complexity of language is acknowledged: "the part played by words in all this is complicated" (003-017), especially regarding how words influence emotion — as "in rhetoric and poetry" (003-028) — and how they interact with desire and belief. This complexity, however, does not force Russell to abandon behaviourism; it merely indicates that the behavioural analysis of meaning must account for rich contextual and dispositional factors.

## Key Distinctions

Russell's approach distinguishes:
- **Meaning vs. mental act of understanding**: The latter is a traditional but dispensable notion; meaning is exhausted by use-habits plus contextual factors.
- **Words as behaviour vs. words as vehicles of thought**: Spoken words are behaviour; thinking-in-words is internalized behaviour, not a fundamentally different process.
- **Descriptive vs. emotive use of words**: Words convey information (descriptive) and influence emotion (rhetorical); both functions are explicable in behavioural terms, though the latter is more complex (003-028).
