The concept of [[Wish|wish]] occupies a pivotal position in [[Bertrand Russell|Russell's]] *The Analysis of Mind*, bridging his engagement with [[Sigmund Freud|Freudian]] psychoanalysis and his [[Behaviourism|behaviourist]] account of [[Desire|desire]]. Russell examines the Freudian theory of unconscious wishes as an explanation of apparently irrational behaviour, while simultaneously attempting to translate the concept of wish into the language of physiological causation and observable behaviour.

## Freud and Unconscious Wishes

Russell takes Freud's theory of unconscious wishes seriously, recognizing its explanatory power for phenomena that conscious psychology cannot account for:

> [001-047] …lish actions with unconscious wishes has been brought to light, …

He describes how Freud's work revealed that much of what we do is motivated by wishes we do not acknowledge — wishes that have been repressed because they are "abhorrent to our conscious selves" (003-002). This discovery, Russell argues, is one of the great achievements of psychoanalysis, comparable to the discovery of unconscious mental processes themselves (001-048).

## The Repression Mechanism

Russell examines the mechanism by which wishes become unconscious:

> [001-055] …ensor. They suppose that when wishes are repressed they are repr…

According to the Freudian account Russell presents, wishes are repressed when they conflict with the individual's conscious self-image. The "censor" — an internal psychological mechanism — prevents these wishes from reaching consciousness. Yet the repressed wishes continue to influence behaviour, producing symptoms, dreams, and apparently irrational actions (001-053, 001-054).

## The Physiological Basis of Wish

Russell attempts to ground the concept of wish in [[Physiology|physiology]], arguing that what Freud calls an "unconscious wish" can be understood as a physiological disposition:

> [001-057] …ical basis of the unfulfilled wish. Such 'wishes' need never hav…

He suggests that an unfulfilled wish is essentially a state of physiological tension or disequilibrium that tends toward a certain resolution. This tension can exist without ever having been conscious — it is a bodily state, not necessarily a mental one. The Freudian "unconscious" thus becomes, in Russell's hands, a description of physiological dispositions (001-051).

## Wish and Desire

Russell distinguishes wish from [[Desire|desire]] proper, while acknowledging their intimate connection:

> [003-029] …belief in the vanity of human wishes: the vain wishes are those …

A wish, for Russell, is a more diffuse and less behaviourally articulated state than a desire. Desires produce action cycles; wishes may remain purely dispositional. The "vanity of human wishes" consists in the fact that many wishes cannot be fulfilled — they produce tension without finding resolution in action (003-009, 003-010).

## Behaviorist Translation

Russell's project throughout is to translate mentalistic concepts like wish into behaviourist terms:

> [001-045] …se of saying to ourselves, "I wish that would happen." We theref…

He notes that when we say "I wish that would happen," we are reporting on a felt state of tension plus a cognitive representation of a desired outcome. The behaviourist can analyze this entirely in terms of physiological states and behavioural dispositions, without positing an irreducible mental act of "wishing" (001-018, 001-002).

## Wish in Relation to Belief and Action

Russell connects wish to his broader theory of [[Belief|belief]] and action:

> [003-029] …belief in the vanity of human wishes: the vain wishes are those …

A wish involves a representation of a state of affairs combined with a feeling-tone of attraction. This is structurally similar to belief, which involves a representation combined with a feeling of assent. The difference lies in the direction of fit: wishes aim to make the world match the representation; beliefs aim to make the representation match the world.
