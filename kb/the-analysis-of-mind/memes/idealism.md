[[Idealism]] is a major tradition in modern philosophy holding that reality is ultimately mental or that the objects of [[Knowledge]] are constituted by the [[Mind]] that knows them. Russell engages idealism throughout *The Analysis of Mind*, treating it as the principal opponent of his own [[Realism|realist]] position. The idealist tradition runs from Berkeley's subjective idealism through Kant's transcendental idealism to the absolute idealism of Hegel and [[Francis Herbert Bradley|Bradley]], and Russell argues that its lingering influence — particularly in the veneration of [[Consciousness]] and [[Experience]] — distorts both epistemology and psychology.

## The Idealist Theory of Knowledge

The defining claim of idealism is that the mind does not merely discover its objects but in some sense constitutes them. Knowledge, on this view, is not a relation between a knowing subject and an independent object; the object is, at least in part, a product of the knowing activity. Russell identifies this tendency in the idealist treatment of "experience":

> The difference between realism and idealism is expressed by this opposition of relations: in the one case, the mind is related to an object; in the other, the mind constitutes the object. The essential point is that in idealist theories the object of knowledge is not independent of the knowing. (001-024)

This constitutive view of knowledge leads idealists to treat the subject-object relation as internal rather than external — the knower and the known are not separable entities but moments within a larger unity.

> Modern idealism professes to be by no means certain that there is anything outside of experience, and it uses the word "experience" in a sense which makes it almost synonymous with "reality." The result is that the whole dispute between idealism and realism becomes verbal. (001-026)

## Russell's Critique

Russell's central charge against idealism is that it turns psychological categories into metaphysical absolutes. Terms like "consciousness," "experience," and "mind" are treated as primitive and all-explanatory, when in fact they are vague and in need of analysis themselves:

> A lingering influence of idealism is visible in the use of the word "experience." "Experience," like "consciousness," must be a product, not a part, of the primary stuff of the world. (001-036)

He argues that the idealist habit of treating consciousness as a fundamental category is a survival of religious and animistic thinking. The proper approach, in Russell's view, is to analyze mental phenomena into more basic constituents — sensations, images, and the causal laws that bind them — rather than positing a unified mental substance.

> The standpoint of materialism and idealism alike is only misleading. Both assume that there is one fundamental stuff — matter for the materialist, mind for the idealist. But neither "mind" nor "matter" is a genuine philosophical starting-point. (001-050)

## Coherence Theory of Truth

Idealism is closely associated with the coherence theory of truth, which holds that a belief is true not by correspondence to an independent fact but by its coherence with the total system of beliefs. Russell rejects this as circular: coherence presupposes logical laws whose own truth cannot be established by coherence without begging the question.

> Coherence as the definition of truth is advocated by idealists, particularly by those who follow Hegel. On this view, truth is a property of a whole system of propositions, and a single proposition is true in so far as it forms part of a coherent system. (013-031)

The coherence theory, Russell contends, conflates the *test* of truth with its *nature*. While coherence may serve as a fallible criterion, truth itself requires a relation to something outside the system of beliefs.

## Varieties of Idealism

| Variety | Key Figure | Core Claim | Russell's Objection |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subjective Idealism | Berkeley | Objects exist only as perceived; *esse est percipi* | Confuses the object of perception with the act of perceiving |
| Transcendental Idealism | Kant | We know appearances, not things-in-themselves, because the mind imposes forms on experience | Retains an unknowable noumenon that contradicts the idealist starting-point |
| Absolute Idealism | Hegel, Bradley | Reality is a single coherent whole; all separation is ultimately illusory | Makes truth depend on an inaccessible totality; denies the reality of relations |

Berkeley's subjective idealism is the most radical form. Russell notes that its arguments, though logically tight, rest on an equivocation:

> His arguments for subjective idealism turn on the assumption that what is immediately perceived is mental. But this assumption, when examined, proves to be without foundation. (003-020)

## Russell's Realist Alternative

Russell's own position — developed into neutral monism — is built on the rejection of the idealist starting-point. Instead of beginning with consciousness and asking how it can reach an external world, Russell begins with neutral particulars (sensations, images) that are neither mental nor physical in themselves but enter into mental or physical causal chains depending on their relations. This approach was shaped by the realist revolt against idealism in which Russell and [[George Edward Moore|Moore]] played leading roles:

> Empirically, this whole line of thought was directed against idealism. The aim was to show that knowledge is a direct relation to objects other than and independent of the knowing mind. (008-009)

The realist alternative treats the knowing relation as external and contingent: the object known is what it is regardless of whether anyone knows it, and knowledge is a natural phenomenon to be explained by the causal laws of psychology, not a metaphysical mystery requiring the mind to constitute its world.
