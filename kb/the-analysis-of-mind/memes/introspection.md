Russell's examination of introspection in Lecture VI of _The Analysis of Mind_ challenges one of the foundational assumptions of traditional philosophy and psychology: the idea that we have a special, privileged form of access to our own minds. Russell surveys behaviourist and psychological critiques, particularly the work of Knight Dunlap, and concludes that introspection is not a separate kind of knowledge but merely a form of observation directed at particular kinds of data. This conclusion is essential to his behaviouristic analysis of [[Consciousness|consciousness]] and [[Knowledge|knowledge]].

## The Traditional View of Introspection

The traditional view holds that there are two distinct sources of knowledge: sense-perception, which provides data about the physical world, and introspection, which provides data about the mental world. Russell summarizes this view at the outset:

> "One of these, as we saw in our first lecture, was the belief that there were two sorts of data, one furnished by the senses, the other by introspection." (006-014)

On this dualistic picture, introspection gives each person direct, incorrigible access to their own thoughts, feelings, and mental states -- a kind of access that external observation can never provide. Russell sets out to show that this picture is philosophically untenable.

## Knight Dunlap's Critique

Russell draws heavily on Knight Dunlap's article "The Case against Introspection" (Psychological Review, 1912), which he describes as the main source of the critique:

> "The criticism of introspection has been in the main the work of American psychologists. I will begin with an article called 'The Case against Introspection,' by Knight Dunlap." (006-005)

Dunlap's central argument is that introspection does not provide the kind of intersubjective verification that genuine scientific knowledge requires. As Russell summarizes: "the observation is of a single observer, and his introspection does not provide for the observation of the same fact by others" (006-008). This places introspection outside the methodology of natural science, which depends on publicly verifiable data.

Dunlap further argues that what passes for introspection is really retrospection -- the recollection of a past mental state rather than the simultaneous observation of a present one. When we attempt to observe our own mental processes, we are always a moment too late, and what we report is based on [[Memory|memory]], not direct inspection.

## The Behaviourist Challenge

Russell notes that the behaviourists have gone even further than Dunlap:

> "The behaviourists have challenged introspection even more vigorously than Knight Dunlap, and have gone so far as to deny that there is such a thing." (006-015)

The behaviourist position is that all genuine data about the mind must be obtainable through external observation of behaviour. If introspection provides data that cannot be verified behaviourally, then introspection is not a distinct source of knowledge at all but a confused way of talking about behavioural facts.

Russell finds the behaviourist position too extreme but endorses its methodological thrust. He argues that introspection should be reconceived as a kind of observation rather than as a special mental faculty.

## Reconceiving Introspection

Russell proposes a definition that preserves what is useful in the concept of introspection while abandoning its metaphysical baggage:

> "I should favour introspection being defined as the observation of the events which are connected with the nervous system in the way in which sensations are connected." (006-020)

On this view, introspection is not a separate channel of knowledge but simply the observation of data that happen to be causally connected to one's own body in a particular way. The data observed through introspection -- thoughts, images, feelings -- are "not different in kind from the data of external perception" (001-042). They are physical events in the brain, accessible to the subject because of their unique causal relationship to the subject's nervous system.

## Implications for the Analysis of Mind

Russell's critique of introspection serves his larger project of dismantling the dualistic separation between mind and matter. If introspection provides no special access to a non-physical mental realm, then the concept of [[Consciousness|consciousness]] as a private theatre must be abandoned. The data we gain through introspection are simply physiological events that we happen to observe from the inside.

This conclusion connects introspection to Russell's accounts of [[Sensation and Images|sensation-image]], [[Belief|belief]], and [[Knowledge|knowledge]]. Each of these, on his analysis, can be understood in terms of publicly observable behaviour and physiological processes, without appeal to a private mental realm accessible only through introspection.

Russell does not deny that we can report on our own mental states. But he insists that such reports are not fundamentally different from reports about external objects. Both are based on observation, both are fallible, and both are subject to the same standards of intersubjective verification.
