## Biography

William James (1842–1910) was an American philosopher and psychologist, one of the three founders of pragmatism alongside Charles Sanders Peirce and John Dewey. He held professorships at Harvard University in both psychology and philosophy. His major works include *The Principles of Psychology* (1890), *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902), *Pragmatism* (1907), and the posthumous *Essays in Radical Empiricism* (1912).

## Key Ideas

James's most important contribution to the philosophy of mind, for Russell's purposes, is his rejection of consciousness as an entity. In his essay "Does 'Consciousness' Exist?" (1904), James argued that consciousness is not a separate stuff but a function — the same neutral "pure experience" can be arranged in different patterns to yield either the mental or the physical. As Russell quotes him: "'Consciousness,' when once it has evaporated to this estate of pure diaphaneity, is on the point of disappearing altogether. It is the name of a nonentity, and has no right to a place among first principles" (001-028).

James's doctrine of radical empiricism holds that "the raw material out of which the world is built up is not of two sorts, one matter and the other mind, but that it is arranged in different patterns by its inter-relations, and that some arrangements may be called mental, while others may be called physical" (001-031). His thesis is that "if we start with the supposition that there is only one primal stuff or material in the world, a stuff of which everything is composed... philosophy might be set down as an attempt at a complete and consistent description of experience" (001-032).

James also denied the subject-object duality that was supposed to constitute consciousness: "EXPERIENCE, I BELIEVE, HAS NO SUCH INNER DUPLICITY" (001-033). He illustrated this with the analogy of paint: the same paint appears as a commodity in a paint-shop, but as a pictorial element in a painting — the difference is in the arrangement, not the substance (001-034).

## Russell's Engagement

Russell aligns himself explicitly with James on the rejection of consciousness: "My own belief... is that James is right in rejecting consciousness as an entity, and that the American realists are right in denying that the subject-object relation is fundamental" (001-037). James's influence runs throughout *The Analysis of Mind*: the neutral monism Russell advocates, the rejection of the mental "act," the treatment of sensation as common to mind and matter, and the broadly empiricist methodology all derive in significant part from James.

Russell does, however, diverge from James on the status of images. While James's radical empiricism treats all experience as fundamentally of the same kind, Russell insists that images belong "only to the mental world" (001-037) — a modification of James's pure experience doctrine.
