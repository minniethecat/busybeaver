## Definition

Association is the psychological counterpart to physical causation — the "mnemic" principle by which one mental occurrence reliably leads to another due to past contiguity rather than any present stimulus. Russell describes it as a psychological law parallel to physical laws: "the law of association is a psychological law" (001-037).

## Russell's Account

Russell treats association not as a fundamental law of mind but as a derived phenomenon explainable in terms of habit and neural pathways. He observes that "the broad fact of association, on the mental side, is that one mental occurrence tends to call up another according to certain rules" (004-009). Importantly, Russell distinguishes between simultaneous and successive association: beyond the association of co-occurring mental contents, "there is also association of successively aroused engrams" (004-018).

In Russell's analysis, images are intimately tied to association. An image is not caused directly by a stimulus but rather "caused by association with a sensation, not by a stimulus similar to the sensation" (006-004). Furthermore, "an image is occasioned, through association, by a sensation or another image" (008-030), making association the generative mechanism behind much of mental life.

## Key Distinctions

Russell draws several important distinctions regarding association:

- **Association vs. physical causation**: While physical causation operates through present stimuli and laws of physics, association operates through past history — what Russell calls "mnemic causation"
- **Habit vs. association**: Russell largely equates the two: "habit (or association) explains why the present feature leads us to expect the future event" (009-041). The line between habit and association is blurred throughout his analysis
- **Verbal association**: Language provides Russell's clearest example of association at work — "the word acquires, through association, some of the same causal efficacy as the object" (010-029), creating a reciprocal relationship where "there is a reciprocal association between it and the images of what it means" (010-043)

## Historical Context

The concept of association traces back to the British empiricists — Locke, Hume, and Hartley — who made the association of ideas central to their psychology. Russell's treatment is distinctive in its attempt to reduce association to a physiological-psychological mechanism grounded in habit and neural pathways, reflecting the influence of behaviourism (Watson) and the experimental psychology of his era.
