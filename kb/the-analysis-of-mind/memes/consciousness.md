## Definition

Consciousness, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, is the concept traditionally taken to be the defining characteristic of mind—"If there is one thing that may be said, in the popular estimation, to characterize mind, that one thing is 'consciousness'" (001-008). Russell's project in Lecture I is to refute the view that consciousness is a simple, ultimate entity or essence, arguing instead that it is a logical construction out of more primitive neutral components.

## Russell's Account

Russell opens his analysis by rejecting the theory—which he himself formerly held—that "the essence of everything mental is a certain quite peculiar something called 'consciousness'" (001-002). He divides his critique into two lines: (1) direct reasons from philosophical analysis, and (2) indirect reasons from comparative psychology and psychoanalysis (001-003–005).

Russell identifies three traditional views of consciousness that he finds inadequate. The first is **Brentano's intentionality thesis**: the view that every mental phenomenon is characterized by "intentional inexistence"—reference to an object (001-016–017). Russell formerly accepted this view but now rejects it, arguing that the reference of thoughts to objects is "derivative" rather than essential, consisting largely in beliefs about connections between mental contents and external things (001-023).

The second is **Meinong's act-content-object analysis**, which divides a thought into three elements: the act of thinking, the content (what distinguishes one thought from another), and the object (what the thought is about) (001-019–020). Russell criticizes the "act" as "unnecessary and fictitious"—"Empirically, I cannot discover anything corresponding to the supposed act; and theoretically I cannot see that it is indispensable" (001-022). The act, he suggests, is merely the ghost of the subject, the "I" in "I think."

The third view Russell engages is **William James's rejection of consciousness as an entity**. James argued that "consciousness" is not a separate stuff but a function—the same neutral "pure experience" arranged in different patterns yields both the mental and the physical (001-031–033). Russell aligns himself with James: "James is right in rejecting consciousness as an entity, and... the American realists are right in denying that the subject-object relation is fundamental" (001-037).

Russell also draws on **behaviorism** (John B. Watson) and **psychoanalysis** (Freud) to support his case. Behaviourism shows that "knowing" can be accounted for in terms of observable physical behavior without invoking an inner consciousness (001-041). Psychoanalysis demonstrates that desires can be wholly unconscious yet dominate action and belief, undermining the view that consciousness is essential to the mental (001-048).

Russell's positive position is **neutral monism**: "The stuff of which the world of our experience is composed is... neither mind nor matter, but something more primitive than either" (001-007). Mind and matter are both logical constructions out of the same neutral entities, which have "neither the hardness and indestructibility of matter, nor the reference to objects which is supposed to characterize mind" (001-050).

## Key Distinctions

Russell distinguishes several "ways of being conscious" catalogued in conventional psychology: perception, memory, ideas (in the Lockean sense, as opposed to impressions), and belief (001-010–013). These are not different manifestations of a single essence "consciousness," but distinct psychological phenomena with different analyses. Russell also distinguishes between consciousness as it applies to sensation (where he remains a realist) and as it applies to memory and thought (where he rejects realism) (001-025).

## Historical Context

The concept of consciousness as the essence of mind was central to post-Cartesian philosophy and to the Brentano school's development of descriptive psychology. Russell's position in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) represents his definitive break with the idealism and mentalism of his earlier work, influenced by James's radical empiricism, the American new realists, and Watson's behaviourism. The book marks Russell's turn toward a scientifically-grounded philosophy of mind.
