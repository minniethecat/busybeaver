## Definition

Voluntary movement, in Russell's behaviourist analysis, is one of three classes of bodily movement — alongside reflex and mechanical movements — defined not by an inner act of will but by the involvement of an idea or image of the result to be achieved. Russell writes that "the distinctive characteristic of a voluntary act is that it involves an idea of the end to be achieved" (014-016). This idea, itself a bodily occurrence (an image or word-habit), serves as part of the causal chain leading to the movement, distinguishing voluntary action from purely reflex responses.

## Russell's Account

Russell classifies bodily movements into three categories: "voluntary, reflex and mechanical" (002-007). This classification is made from the standpoint of external observation — Russell is careful to note that "to the outside observer" there may be no obvious distinction "between voluntary and reflex movements" (002-012). The physiologist cannot reliably tell them apart by looking at the movement itself; the difference lies in the causal history that produced it.

The key to Russell's analysis is the role of images and desires in voluntary action. A voluntary movement, unlike a reflex, is preceded by an image of the movement's outcome. "The distinctive characteristic of a voluntary act is that it involves an idea of the end to be achieved" — this idea is a present mental occurrence (an image or verbal representation) that forms part of the causal chain leading to the movement (014-016). Russell acknowledges, however, that the boundary between voluntary and reflex is not sharp: "there certainly" are intermediate cases where the classification is ambiguous (014-015).

Russell links voluntary movement closely with desire. "Efficacy in causing voluntary movements" is one of the characteristics by which we identify desires (012-030). A desire is, in part, a state that tends to produce voluntary movements directed toward its own satisfaction. This connection is crucial for Russell's behaviourist treatment of motivation: rather than postulating an inner "will" as a separate faculty, he analyses voluntary action as a causal sequence in which desires (themselves analysable as behavioural dispositions) produce images of goals, which in turn cause movements.

Russell also notes that some actions involve "something more than voluntary movement" — cases where belief, deliberation, and conscious choice enter the picture (014-018). The sort of case he has in mind is one where a person considers alternative courses of action before moving. This complexity does not, in Russell's view, require abandoning the behaviourist framework; it simply means the causal chain includes additional elements (beliefs, expectations, comparative images) between the initial stimulus and the final movement.

The distinction between voluntary and involuntary also plays a role in Russell's analysis of the feeling of reality. He observes that sensations have effects upon us "without our voluntary co-operation" — the external world impinges on us regardless of our will (009-059). This involuntary character of sensation is, for Russell, part of what gives sensation its distinctive feeling of reality, in contrast to images, which we can summon and dismiss more or less at will.

Russell connects voluntary movement to the broader mind–body relationship. In his neutral monist framework, the causal chain from brain event to image to voluntary movement is entirely physical — it involves no mysterious interaction between mental and material substances. "It is primarily this difference" in causal context — whether a brain event causes images or voluntary movements — that distinguishes the psychological from the merely physiological (015-027). Psychology, on this view, is the study of those causal chains that pass through images and mnemic phenomena on their way to voluntary action.

## Key Distinctions

- **Voluntary vs. Reflex Movements**: Voluntary movements involve an idea of the end; reflex movements occur automatically in response to a stimulus without the mediation of an anticipatory image (002-007, 014-016).
- **Voluntary vs. Mechanical Movements**: Mechanical movements are those determined purely by physics (e.g., a falling body); voluntary movements involve the organism's own causal contribution via images and desires (002-007).
- **Voluntary Movement vs. Deliberate Action**: Some actions involve belief and choice in addition to voluntary movement; these represent a higher degree of complexity rather than a different kind of event (014-018).
- **Voluntary vs. Involuntary Sensation**: Sensations occur without our voluntary co-operation, contributing to their felt reality; images are more amenable to voluntary control (009-059).

## Historical Context

Russell's analysis of voluntary movement in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) draws on the behaviourist psychology of John B. Watson, who sought to eliminate the concept of "will" from scientific psychology. Russell follows Watson in treating voluntary action as a causal sequence rather than an expression of free will, but he preserves a role for images and ideas — inner occurrences — that Watson's more radical behaviourism tended to dismiss. Russell's classification of movements into voluntary, reflex, and mechanical also reflects the influence of comparative psychology, where the distinction between instinctive and learned behaviour was a central topic of investigation.
