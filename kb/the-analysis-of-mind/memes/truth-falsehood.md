Russell's treatment of truth and falsehood in Lecture XIII of _The Analysis of Mind_ marks a deliberate departure from the psychological analysis that occupies the bulk of the book. Truth and falsehood, he argues, are not properties discoverable by inspecting mental states in isolation; they depend upon a relation between a belief and a fact that lies outside the believer's experience. Nevertheless, Russell insists that no analysis of mind can ignore the question of whether our beliefs yield [[Knowledge|knowledge]], just as no description of a chronometer can ignore its accuracy as a time-keeper (013-001).

## The Problem of Truth and Falsehood

Russell opens by acknowledging that the definition of truth and falsehood "lies strictly outside our general subject, namely the analysis of mind" (013-001). From a purely psychological standpoint, there may be different kinds of belief and different degrees of certainty, but "there cannot be any purely psychological means of distinguishing between true and false beliefs" (013-001). A belief is rendered true or false by its relation to a fact, which may lie outside the experience of the person entertaining the belief.

Despite this external character, Russell insists we cannot avoid the question. He writes:

> "We wish to believe that our beliefs, sometimes at least, yield KNOWLEDGE, and a belief does not yield knowledge unless it is true. The question whether our minds are instruments of knowledge, and, if so, in what sense, is so vital that any suggested analysis of mind must be examined in relation to this question." (013-001)

Russell divides the discussion into four parts: (I) a behaviouristic account of knowledge as accurate and appropriate response, (II) a critique of intrinsic criteria of truth such as self-evidence and coherence, (III) the problem of verifiability, and (IV) the formal definition of truth and falsehood in terms of correspondence to fact.

## The Behaviouristic Criterion: Accuracy and Appropriateness

Russell's first approach is to view a human being as an instrument making responses to stimuli. From the outside, we observe responses that display knowledge when they exhibit two characteristics: **accuracy** and **appropriateness** (013-001).

Accuracy is defined by two conditions:

> "An instrument is ACCURATE when it avoids the defects of the two boys and weather-cocks, that is to say, when—(a) It gives different responses to stimuli which differ in relevant ways; (b) It gives the same response to stimuli which do not differ in relevant ways." (013-001)

Russell illustrates with the example of a boy who always answers "four" regardless of the question — he fails condition (a). Another boy who answers randomly fails condition (b). A good instrument, like a person with knowledge, varies its response only when the stimulus varies in relevant ways.

However, accuracy alone is insufficient. A person who always believes falsely is just as "sensitive" an instrument as one who always believes truly. The difference is that "the one who always believed falsely would quickly come to a bad end" (013-001). This introduces **appropriateness** — the suitability of a response for realizing one's purpose. Russell concludes that the proportion of accuracy to appropriateness in defining knowledge is difficult, but "both enter in."

## Against Intrinsic Criteria of Truth

Russell devotes considerable attention to refuting two philosophical attempts to find an intrinsic mark of truth within beliefs themselves, without reference to external facts.

**Self-evidence** is the first candidate. Russell acknowledges that some beliefs carry a strong subjective feeling of certainty, but argues that this feeling is "usually a result of habit, and may lead us astray in circumstances which are unusual in ways of which we are unaware" (013-001). He concludes that "no form of self-evidence seems to afford an absolute criterion of truth" (013-001).

**Coherence** — the view that truth consists in membership of a consistent system of beliefs — is the second target. Russell's fundamental objection is logical: "no endeavour is made to show that we cannot form a consistent whole composed partly or wholly of false propositions, as in a novel" (013-001). A coherent fairy-tale, however elaborate, is not truth. Leibniz's conception of many possible worlds, Russell suggests, accords much better with modern logic.

## Truth as Correspondence to Fact

Russell's positive account holds that the truth or falsehood of a belief "consists in a relation to a certain fact, namely the objective of the belief" (013-001). This is a correspondence theory: a belief is true when it corresponds to a fact that makes it true, and false when no such fact exists.

This view builds on the analysis of [[Belief|belief]] developed in Lecture XII. Beliefs are the "vehicles of truth and falsehood" (012-001). They are propositional attitudes whose content can stand in a relation of correspondence or non-correspondence to reality. The judgment that constitutes belief takes the form of an image or word-complex accompanied by a feeling of assent, and it is this complex — not the isolated components — that is true or false.

Russell is careful to distinguish the psychological question (what kind of mental occurrence a belief is) from the logical question (what makes it true or false). The psychology of belief belongs to mental analysis; the definition of truth belongs to logic and epistemology. But the two are connected, because without an account of truth, the analysis of mind cannot address the question of whether our mental states yield knowledge.
