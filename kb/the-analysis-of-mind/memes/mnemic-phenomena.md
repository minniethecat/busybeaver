In Lecture IV of _The Analysis of Mind_, Russell introduces one of the most distinctive and consequential concepts in the book: **mnemic phenomena**. Drawing on the work of German biologist Richard Semon, Russell argues that what broadly distinguishes the behaviour of living organisms from dead matter is that an organism's response to a stimulus depends not only on the present stimulus and the present state of the organism, but also on its past history. This temporal spread of causation — where the past enters essentially into the causation of the present — is the foundation upon which Russell builds his distinction between psychological and physical causal laws.

## The Definition of Mnemic Phenomena

Russell defines mnemic phenomena with precision:

> "Following a suggestion derived from Semon ('Die Mneme,' Leipzig, 1904; 2nd edition, 1908), we will give the name of 'mnemic phenomena' to those responses of an organism which, so far as hitherto observed facts are concerned, can only be brought under causal laws by including past occurrences in the history of the organism as part of the causes of the present response." (004-005)

The key clarification is that Russell is not merely talking about past occurrences as part of a chain of causes. He means that, "in attempting to state the PROXIMATE cause of the present event, some past event or events must be included, unless we take refuge in hypothetical modifications of brain structure" (004-005). The peat-smoke example makes this vivid: the smell of peat-smoke recalls a former experience, and the same stimulus would not produce the same recollection in someone who did not share that experience. The cause of the recollection is both the present stimulus and the past occurrence — the recollection is therefore a mnemic phenomenon.

## Five Classes of Mnemic Phenomena

Russell provides five illustrations spanning the breadth of mental life:

**(a) Acquired Habits.** A cat that has learned to escape from a cage behaves differently on subsequent encounters. "The observable fact is that the stimulus of being in the cage produces differing results with repetition" (004-007). All habitual knowledge — including our ability to answer questions like "What is the capital of France?" — "consists of acquired habits, and comes under the head of mnemic phenomena" (004-007).

**(b) Images.** Images are "copies" of past sensations, and their occurrence depends on past experience as well as present stimuli. "It is generally believed that all images, in their simpler parts, are copies of sensations; if so, their mnemic character is evident" (004-008). This is significant because [[Sensation and Images|images]] play an essential role in thinking.

**(c) Association.** The broad fact of association — that experiencing something previously experienced tends to call up its original context — is "obviously a mnemic phenomenon" (004-009). Russell notes that there is no essential distinction between association and habit; both involve the dependence of present response on past experience.

**(d) Non-Sensational Elements in Perception.** When we perceive a familiar object like a penny, "much of what appears subjectively to be immediately given is really derived from past experience" (004-010). We seem to see a circular shape, not an elliptical one; the visual appearance is "filled out" with tactile expectations. "In that case there is a large mnemic element in all the common perceptions by means of which we handle common objects" (004-010).

**(e) Memory as Knowledge.** Definite knowledge of a past event — remembering something that happened to us — is caused both by a present stimulus and by the past occurrence itself. "Thus our remembering is caused by (1) the present stimulus, (2) the past occurrence" (004-014). It is therefore a mnemic phenomenon according to the definition.

## Semon's Mnemic Principles

Russell acknowledges his debt to Richard Semon, whom he calls "the best writer on mnemic phenomena known to me" (004-016). Semon formulated two mnemic principles:

> "The first, or 'Law of Engraphy,' states that the occurrence of an excitation in an organism produces a permanent alteration in its substance — an 'engram.' The second, or 'Law of Ecphory,' states that the reoccurrence of part of the original excitation tends to revive the whole complex." (004-019)

Russell accepts the phenomenological validity of these principles but resists the neurological hypothesis of engrams as brain modifications. His concern is with the observable fact that past occurrences enter into causal laws, regardless of whether this is mediated by brain changes or by direct causal connection across time.

## Mnemic Causation and the Distinction Between Sciences

The concept of mnemic phenomena is the linchpin of Russell's argument that psychology and physics are distinguished by their causal laws. "The essential characteristic of mnemic phenomena" (004-025) — that the causal unit spans time — is what separates psychological causation from physical causation. In physics, the proximate cause of an event can be found in the present state of the system. In psychology, the past must be included.

Russell uses "mnemic causation" to denote causal relations in which "the mnemic portion" — the past event — is an irreducible part of the proximate cause (004-026). This framework makes it possible to have purely psychological causal laws that take no account of the brain or the body, while remaining compatible with the possibility that brain modifications are the physical mechanism underlying mnemic phenomena. The concept thus preserves the autonomy of psychology without committing to dualism.
