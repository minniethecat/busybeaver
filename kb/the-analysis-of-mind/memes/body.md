## Definition

In Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, the body is the physical organism studied from the outside — the observable system of movements, habits, and physiological processes that behaviourists take as the proper object of psychological investigation. Russell does not treat body as a metaphysical substance opposed to mind, but as a convenient grouping of physical phenomena whose behaviour displays regularities that are partly physiological and partly psychological.

## Russell's Account

**Methodological Primacy of the Body.** Russell endorses the behaviourist methodological preference for the body: "our own body is easier to observe than that of other animals" (001-042), but the knowledge we gain from observing our own body is not fundamentally different in kind from our knowledge of others. The behaviourist "maintains that our knowledge of ourselves is no different in kind from our knowledge of other people" (001-042).

**Mechanical vs. Voluntary Movement.** Russell distinguishes mechanical bodily movements from voluntary ones. A mechanical movement "proceeds as if only dead matter were involved" (002-011), like stumbling or falling. Voluntary movements, by contrast, involve the higher nervous centres and display characteristics — purpose, persistence, adaptation to obstacles — that distinguish living bodies from dead matter (001-043). Russell notes that "a stone at the top of a hill may start rolling, but it shows no pertinacity in trying to get to the bottom" (001-043), whereas animals pursue goals with persistence.

**Habit and the Body.** Habit is "a characteristic of the body at least as much as of the mind" (004-032). Russell points out that association can be "a merely physical association, which is indistinguishable from physical habit" — the kind studied by Thorndike in animals, where a stimulus is associated with a bodily act without any mental intermediary: "there need not be anything mental, but merely a habit of the body" (004-009).

**Mnemic Phenomena.** The body is also the seat of mnemic causation — the influence of the past on present behaviour. Russell cites Semon's theory of engrams: material alterations in the body that embody past experience (004-032, 004-033). However, he cautions that the necessity of brain states for memory does not prove their sufficiency: "memory may require for its causation a past occurrence as well as a certain present state of the brain" (004-033).

## Key Distinctions

- **Body vs. dead matter**: Living bodies display purpose, adaptation, and mnemic phenomena that dead matter does not (001-043, 002-011).
- **Body vs. mind**: Russell rejects the Cartesian dualism of mind and matter as fundamentally different substances. Instead, "mind and matter are neither of them the actual stuff of reality, but different convenient groupings of an underlying material" (001-050). The body-mind distinction is methodological, not metaphysical.
- **Physiological habit vs. mental association**: Some habits are purely bodily (soldier's drill); others involve mental imagery. The boundary is one of degree, not kind (004-009).

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of the body reflects the influence of behaviourism ([[John B Watson]]), the mnemic theories of Richard Semon, and the psychophysical parallelism debates of the late 19th century. He positions himself against Cartesian dualism while also resisting reductive materialism, arguing instead for neutral monism: the view that both body and mind are constructed from the same neutral-stuff (001-050, 001-037).
