Perspective, in Russell's [[neutral-monism|neutral monist]] metaphysics, is a technical concept denoting the set of all sensory [[particulars]] available at a given place at a given time. It is one of the two fundamental ways of grouping the ultimate constituents of the world — the other being the "thing" or "piece of matter." Together, these two modes of classification form the structural basis of Russell's reconstruction of both [[mind]] and [[matter]] from neutral stuff.

## Perspectives as Fundamental Classification

Russell introduces the concept of perspective in [[Lecture V]] as an alternative to the common-sense classification of particulars into physical objects. Where common sense groups together all the appearances of a table — its look from different angles, its feel, the sound it makes when struck — and calls the collection "the table," Russell proposes that we can equally well group particulars by the place from which they are experienced. A perspective is the set of all particulars that would be perceived by an observer at a given point in space at a given moment.

> [007-001] In Lecture V we found reason to think that the ultimate constituents of the world do not have the characteristics of either mind or matter as ordinarily understood: they are not solid persistent objects moving through space, nor are they fragments of "consciousness." But we found two ways of grouping particulars, one into "things" or "pieces of matter," the other into series of "perspectives," each series being what may be called a "biography."

This dual classification is the key to Russell's neutral monism: the same neutral particulars can be grouped one way to yield the world of physical objects studied by [[physics]], and grouped another way to yield the biographies studied by [[psychology]]. Neither grouping is more fundamental than the other.

## The Laws of Perspective

The grouping of particulars into perspectives is governed by what Russell calls, in a generalized sense, the "laws of perspective." These include not only the ordinary laws of visual perspective — by which a circular coin appears elliptical when viewed from an angle — but all the systematic ways in which the appearance of things varies with the position of the observer.

> [007-002] The physical classification of particulars collects together all those that are aspects of one "thing." Given any one particular, it is found often (we do not say always) that there are a number of other particulars differing from this one in gradually increasing degrees. Those (or some of those) that differ from it only very slightly will be found to differ approximately according to certain laws which may be called, in a generalized sense, the laws of "perspective"; they include the ordinary laws of perspective, together with other laws governing changes in colour, sound, smell, and so on, according to the position of the observer.

These laws of perspective are what give structure to the flux of sensory experience. Without them, the appearances of things would vary randomly, and neither the concept of a persisting physical object nor the concept of a unified conscious subject could be constructed. The lawfulness of perspectival variation is what makes both physical science and [[perception|perceptual]] experience possible.

## Perspectives and Physical Objects

Russell's theory of [[matter]] is built upon the concept of perspectives. A physical object — a piece of matter — is defined not as a single existent thing that causes appearances, but as the collection of all those correlated particulars that common sense would regard as its appearances from different places.

> [005-019] According to the view that I am suggesting, a physical object or piece of matter is the collection of all those correlated particulars which would be regarded by common sense as its effects or appearances in different places. On this view, the star that a astronomer photographs, the star that he sees in the telescope, and the star that a man in the street sees with the naked eye, are all of them the star; but they are not all of them the same particular. They are different particulars, forming part of one system.

This has the radical consequence that the "real" object is not something behind or beyond its appearances but is simply the system of appearances themselves, ordered by the laws of perspective and [[physics|dynamics]]. The common-sense distinction between appearance and reality is replaced by the distinction between a single particular (an appearance from one perspective) and the whole system of correlated particulars (the physical object).

## Biographies as Series of Perspectives

Just as a physical object is a collection of particulars grouped by their mutual correlations across different perspectives, a [[mind]] or biography is a series of perspectives grouped by their temporal and causal relations. A biography is the set of all perspectives associated with a given body — or more precisely, with the place that the body occupies — over time.

> [015-015] Subjectivity, as a characteristic of mental phenomena, was considered in Lecture VII, in connection with the definition of perception. The whole set of particulars that occur at a given time, taken in connection with their intrinsic character, is called a "perspective"; taken throughout a period of time, a series of perspectives is called a "biography." Subjectivity is the characteristic belonging to the perspectives which constitute one biography, namely the characteristic of being grouped together by the fact that they all belong to the history of one body.

This reconstruction of the mental subject as a series of perspectives eliminates the need for a substantial [[self]] or soul. The unity of consciousness is not the unity of a single enduring substance but the unity of a series — like the unity of a film, which is not a single thing but a sequence of frames related by continuity and causation.

## The Privacy of Perspectives

An important feature of perspectives is their essential privacy. No two observers can share exactly the same perspective, because perspective is defined by place, and two bodies cannot occupy exactly the same place at the same time. This privacy is what gives rise to the appearance of a fundamental division between the mental and the physical.

> [006-024] That no sensation is ever completely public, results from differences of point of view. Two people cannot be at exactly the same place at the same time; consequently their sensations, even when caused by the same object, are never exactly alike. The appearances of the object from the two places differ according to the laws of perspective; and although the difference may be slight, it always exists.

However, Russell insists that this privacy is a matter of degree, not of kind. The difference between one perspective and another is a difference of spatial and temporal position, not a difference of ontological category. Both are composed of the same neutral stuff; they differ only in their relations.

## Perspectives and the Construction of Space

Russell extends the concept of perspective to address the problem of [[space]]. The space of common sense and physics is not a pre-existing container in which particulars are located; rather, it is constructed from the perspectival relations among particulars themselves. The laws of perspective define a quasi-spatial order among appearances that serves as the basis for the construction of physical space.

> [007-025] What it is that happens when a wave of light reaches a given place we cannot tell, except in the sole case when the place in question is a brain connected with an eye which is turned in the right direction. In that one case we know that there results a sensation of colour. But we know nothing of what goes on in intervening places; we only know the laws of grammar, i.e. of perspective. The star being situated in empty space, we have no reason to suppose that anything goes on there at all except the passage of waves.

This epistemological modesty — we know only our own perspective and the laws that relate it to others — is a defining feature of Russell's [[knowledge|epistemology]] and one of the motivations for his neutral monist metaphysics.
