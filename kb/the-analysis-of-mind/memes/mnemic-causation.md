## Definition

Mnemic causation is a form of causation in which the effect is not determined solely by the present stimulus but also by past events — specifically, the causal unit includes both the present occurrence and the past occurrences that shaped the organism's response. Russell defines it as causation "of that kind in which the proximate cause consists not merely of a present event, but of this together with a past event" (004-022). The term derives from Semon's "mneme" and designates cases where a response cannot be explained purely by present physical conditions without reference to the organism's history.

## Russell's Account

Russell introduces mnemic causation in Lecture IV as a provisional concept necessary for explaining behaviour that resists purely physical explanation (004-015). He argues that physiological changes — such as the effect of an injury on future blood circulation, or the learning of a motor skill — depend on the organism's past history in ways that make their causal unit "not one event at one time, but two events at two times" (015-012). The learning of speech provides a paradigm case: "Provisions which have been made in the structure of a bridge to allow for expansion and contraction are not instances of mnemic causation" (004-028), whereas modifications of behaviour that persist after the stimulus are.

Russell observes that mnemic causation is what led Bergson to deny that the brain stores memories in a physical manner (004-030). He further notes that it is "impossible that mnemic causation should exist in psychology but not in physics" (004-029), anticipating that all mnemic phenomena may ultimately be reducible to physical laws — a key tenet of his neutral monism.

## Key Distinctions

Russell distinguishes mnemic causation from ordinary physical causation on the grounds that "the causal unit is not one event at one time, but two events at two times" (015-015). Whereas physical causation relates present stimulus to present response, mnemic causation requires reference to the past. He also distinguishes mnemic causation from mnemic phenomena: the former is the causal mechanism; the latter are the observable effects — memory, habit, recognition — that display dependence on past history (004-026).

A further distinction runs between physical and psychological mnemic causation. The difference between a photographic plate retaining traces of light and a person remembering is, for Russell, one of degree rather than kind: "if we could distinguish images from sensations by their mnemic character, we could distinguish images from sensations without needing to invoke consciousness" (008-030).

## Historical Context

Russell adopts the term from Richard Semon, whose "Mneme" (1904) proposed that organic memory traces ("engrams") underlie both biological heredity and individual memory. Russell extends Semon's concept from biology into the philosophy of mind, using it to bridge behaviourist and introspectionist accounts of mental life. The concept also engages with Bergson's vitalism — Russell sees mnemic causation as a counter to Bergson's anti-mechanistic arguments about memory — and with Watsonian behaviourism, which Russell simultaneously admires and critiques for its refusal to countenance any unobservable causal mechanism.
