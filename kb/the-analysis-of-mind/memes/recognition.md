Recognition, in Russell's analysis of mind, is a fundamental cognitive act that bridges present [[sensation]] and past experience. It operates as a form of [[memory]]-knowledge, distinct both from mere habit and from the independent recollection of specific past events. Russell identifies recognition as playing a critical role in [[perception]], [[belief]], and the acquisition of [[knowledge]].

## Russell's Two Senses of Recognition

Russell distinguishes between two senses of the word "recognition." In the first sense, recognition means knowing the name of a thing or some other property of it — identifying what kind of thing it is. This is the sense involved when one hears a noise and says "tram": the present sensation is combined with a word or classification to form a judgment about what the sensation indicates.

> [012-014] We may take first recognition, in either of the forms "this is of such-and-such a kind" or "this has occurred before." In either case, present sensation is a constituent. For example, you hear a noise, and you say to yourself "tram." Here the noise and the word "tram" are both constituents of your belief; there is also a relation between them, expressed by "is" in the proposition "that is a tram."

In the second sense, recognition means knowing that we have seen something before — it involves [[knowledge]] about the past, not merely classification of the present. This second form of recognition is more closely tied to memory proper.

> [009-025] There is, however, another sense of the word, in which we mean by recognition, not knowing the name of a thing or some other property of it, but knowing that we have seen it before. In this sense recognition does involve knowledge about the Past.

## Recognition and the Feeling of Familiarity

A crucial feature of recognition is the feeling of familiarity that accompanies it. Russell notes that we often feel something in our sensible environment is familiar without having any definite [[recollection]] of previous occasions on which we have seen it. This feeling of familiarity falls short of full memory-knowledge but nevertheless informs our cognitive engagement with the world.

> [009-022] The sort of memory with which we are here concerned is the sort which is a form of knowledge. It is only knowledge of past events that can have any worth for the purposes of inference, and it is only this sort of memory that forms part of the data of science. But there is another sort of memory, which consists merely in the feeling of familiarity and recognition.

Russell observes that this feeling of familiarity — what he calls the "yes, yes" sensation — is something beyond mere recognition in the classificatory sense. It represents an affective dimension of memory that resists purely behaviorist explanation.

## Critique of the Behaviourist Account

Russell subjects the [[behaviourism|behaviourist]] account of recognition to critical scrutiny. On the behaviourist view, recognition consists in behaving in the same way on a second occasion as one did on the first. Russell acknowledges this as partially correct but identifies significant inadequacies.

> [009-027] A second point is that, when we recognize something, it was not in fact the very same thing, but only something similar, that we experienced on a former occasion. It is fairly clear that, whatever exactly recognition may be, it must consist in a reaction to something which is similar to something previously experienced, but which is not the identical thing.

The behaviourist account, Russell argues, fails to capture the cognitive richness of recognition — the way in which recognition involves a judgment that goes beyond mere behavioural repetition. Recognition is not simply behaving similarly; it is knowing that one is doing so.

## Recognition in Perception and Belief

Recognition plays a foundational role in [[perception]] and [[belief]] formation. Russell notes that most of our judgments of perception are in fact derived from recognition: when we say "this is a button" or "that is a tram," we are performing acts of recognition that classify present sensory data according to past experience.

> [013-029] The next thing to observe (although I do not personally doubt that most of our judgments of perception are true) is that it is very difficult to define any class of such judgments which can be known, by its intrinsic quality, to be always exempt from error. What is called sense or sensation is subject to errors of recognition, as when we say "this is a button" and find that it is a coat of paint.

The possibility of error in recognition-based judgments is significant: because recognition depends on similarity rather than identity, there is always a risk of misclassification. This epistemological vulnerability is one reason Russell treats recognition as a form of knowledge that is practically reliable but theoretically fallible.

## Recognition and Memory Systems

Within Russell's broader theory of [[memory]], recognition occupies a middle ground between two extremes. At one pole is pure habit-memory — the kind involved in knowing a lesson by heart, where no conscious reference to the past is required. At the other pole is independent [[recollection]] — the conscious calling to mind of a specific past event with its temporal context. Recognition lies between these, involving a judgment about the present informed by the past, but without the full temporal specificity of recollection.

The importance of recognition for Russell's project lies in its demonstration that mental phenomena cannot be reduced to either purely physiological processes or purely introspective data. Recognition is simultaneously a behavioural disposition, a cognitive judgment, and a felt quality of familiarity — illustrating the multi-dimensional character of mind that Russell's neutral monism seeks to accommodate.
