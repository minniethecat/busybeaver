David Hume (1711–1776), the Scottish empiricist philosopher, is a recurring reference in *The Analysis of Mind*. Russell draws on Hume's distinction between impressions and ideas as a precursor to his own distinction between [[sensation]] and [[images]], and he engages Hume's scepticism about the self as part of his critique of the notion of a unified conscious subject.

## Impressions and Ideas

Russell explicitly aligns his sensation–image distinction with Hume's impression–idea distinction. Hume defined impressions as "those perceptions which enter with most force and violence" and ideas as "the faint images of these in thinking and reasoning." Russell quotes this formulation at length:

> [008-021] Hume, who gives the names "impressions" and "ideas" to what may, for present purposes, be identified with our "sensations" and "images," speaks of impressions as "those perceptions which enter with most force and violence" while he defines ideas as "the faint images of these (i.e. of impressions) in thinking and reasoning."

However, Russell identifies a problem with Hume's criterion. Hume himself acknowledged that in certain cases — illness, madness, or extremely vivid imagination — ideas can approach the force of impressions, making the criterion of "liveliness" unreliable. Russell treats this as a definitional challenge that requires refinement:

> [008-023] I think Hume is right in holding that they should be ranked under distinct heads, with a peculiar name for each. But by his own confession in the above passage, his criterion for distinguishing them is not always adequate.

Russell retains the fundamental Humean distinction while seeking more reliable criteria for differentiating sensations from images, ultimately locating the difference in their causal laws rather than in their qualitative character.

## Images as Copies

Russell also endorses Hume's principle that simple images are copies of previous simple sensations. He quotes Hume's classic formulation:

> [008-042] Hume says: "Every simple idea has a simple impression, which resembles it; and every simple impression a correspondent idea."

While Russell notes that there are "difficulties in establishing Hume's principles, and doubts as to whether it is exactly true" (008-042) — Hume himself identified an exception — he affirms that in the main, images do function as copies of prior sensations, and that this characteristic is essential to our capacity for acting with reference to what is sensibly absent.

## Hume and Introspection

Russell situates Hume within the tradition of British philosophy that treats introspective knowledge as more certain than knowledge of the external world (006-028). This tradition — which runs from Locke through Berkeley to Hume — holds that we have privileged access to our own mental states. Russell rejects this claim categorically:

> [006-028] Our spontaneous, unsophisticated beliefs, whether as to ourselves or as to the outer world, are always extremely rash and very liable to error. The acquisition of caution is equally necessary and equally difficult in both directions.

Russell's rejection of introspective privilege is essential to his project of treating psychology as a science continuous with physics. Hume, despite providing Russell with crucial conceptual distinctions, represents the philosophical tradition whose epistemic assumptions Russell seeks to overturn.

## Hume and the Association of Ideas

Hume's empiricist account of the [[association]] of ideas — alongside [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[george-berkeley|Berkeley]] — is also part of the intellectual background Russell references when discussing the history of psychological concepts (001-012). Russell's own treatment of [[habit]], [[memory]], and the causal laws governing the succession of mental states builds on and transforms this empiricist tradition, recasting its insights in terms of [[mnemic-causation]] and physiological [[engram]] theory.
