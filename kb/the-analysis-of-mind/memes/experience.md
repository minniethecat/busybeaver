## Definition

Experience, in Russell's neutral monist framework, is the fundamental "stuff" of which the world is composed (001-007). It is neither intrinsically mental nor physical; rather, it is a neutral substratum that can be arranged or contextualised in different ways. Russell adopts the phrase "pure experience" from [[William James]], using it to denote this primitive, undifferentiated material that precedes the mind-matter distinction (001-032, 001-036).

## Russell's Account

Russell argues that the world of our experience is not divided into two radically different kinds of substance. Instead, a single portion of experience can function as a thought in one context and as a physical thing in another (001-034). The difference between a perception and the object perceived is not a difference of intrinsic nature but of the relations into which the given portion of experience enters. This view dissolves the traditional epistemological problem of how the mind can know an external world: if both knower and known are composed of the same neutral stuff, the gap between them is not ontological but relational.

Russell further distinguishes experience from instinct. Instinctive movements are independent of prior experience, occurring without learning (002-018, 002-019). By contrast, behaviour that is acquired or modified through exposure to past situations constitutes experience-based learning (002-017, 002-021). Experience thus serves as the mechanism by which instinct becomes adaptive: as Russell notes, instinct can "grow wiser through experience" (002-037).

## Key Distinctions

**Experience and the physical world.** Russell draws a sharp boundary: that which does not form part of any experience belongs only to the physical world (001-037). This criterion separates the domain of psychology (which deals with experienced events) from physics (which deals with inferred, unexperienced events). The physical world contains entities and processes that no one experiences, but these are constructed from the same neutral stuff as experience itself.

**Experience and desire.** Only experience can reveal what will actually satisfy a desire (001-046). Abstract reasoning alone cannot determine this; the test of satisfaction is empirical and experiential.

**Experience and instinct.** As noted above, instinct operates without prior experience, while acquired behaviour depends on it. The interplay between the two is central to Russell's account of learning and adaptation.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of experience draws directly on the radical empiricism of [[William James]], who coined the term "pure experience" to describe a primal, undifferentiated flux that is neither subjective nor objective. Russell integrates this Jamesian insight into his own neutral monist position, which he shares — with variations — with Ernst Mach and the American new realists. The concept forms a bridge between the empiricist tradition (which grounds knowledge in experience) and the monist ambition to reduce the world to a single kind of substance.

Russell's account also engages with behaviourist psychology, particularly in its treatment of the instinct-experience distinction. By analysing experience in terms of acquired behavioural modifications, Russell aligns his epistemology with the emerging behaviourist science of his day while preserving a central role for conscious [[Perception]].
