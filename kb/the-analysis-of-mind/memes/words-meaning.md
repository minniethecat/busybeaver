Russell devotes Lecture X of _The Analysis of Mind_ to a behaviouristic analysis of words and meaning, asking the question: "What is the relation called 'meaning'?" (010-001). Departing from the conventional view that meaning is a mental association between a sound and an idea, Russell argues that the meaning of a word is a causal law connecting its use to objects, images, and actions. This analysis extends his broader project of deconstructing [[Consciousness|consciousness]] into observable causal patterns and ties language directly to his accounts of [[Sensation and Images|sensation-image]] and [[Belief|belief]].

## Words as Physical Phenomena

Russell begins by insisting that a word, considered apart from its meaning, is a purely physical phenomenon. "The word 'Napoleon,' we say, means a certain person" (010-001), but considered merely as a sound or mark, it is no different from any other physical event. What makes a sound into a word is precisely the fact that it has meaning. As Russell writes:

> "Meaning is clearly what distinguishes a word from other noises." (010-003)

This physicalist starting point aligns with Russell's commitment to behaviourism. A word is not a vehicle for a mental entity; it is a physical event that enters into specific causal relations with other physical events, including the behaviour of speakers and hearers.

## Meaning as a Causal Law

The core of Russell's positive account is that meaning is a causal relation. He states this thesis directly:

> "The relation of a word to its meaning is of the nature of a causal law." (010-019)

A word means something not because a speaker has a mental image or concept associated with it, but because the word is causally connected to objects in the world and to actions in behaviour. The word "food" means food because its use is causally linked to the presence or pursuit of food. This causal law is a regularity in behaviour -- a [[Instinct and Habit|habit]] -- not a mental state.

Russell emphasizes that the causal relation between a word and its meaning does not require conscious awareness: "To say that a word has a meaning is not to say that those who use the word are conscious of the meaning" (010-017). This point reinforces his earlier arguments against the necessity of [[Introspection|introspection]] for understanding mental life.

## Against the Conventional View

Russell challenges the natural assumption "to think of the meaning of a word as something conventional" (010-004). While convention plays a role in fixing which sounds mean which things, the underlying relation of meaning itself must be explained in naturalistic, causal terms. Russell surveys non-linguistic forms of meaning -- gestures, cries, emotional expressions -- to show that meaning is not essentially tied to convention. "A large part of one of Wundt's volumes," he notes, "is concerned with the non-conventional ways of conveying meaning" (010-005).

## Words and Images

Russell connects the problem of meaning to his earlier analysis of images. The meaning of a word is often mediated by images: hearing the word "Napoleon" may call up an image of a man in a tricorn hat. Watson, the behaviourist, would argue that these images are themselves reducible to incipient speech-movements -- "inner speech" (010-022). Russell treats images more cautiously than Watson, but agrees that the essential relation is causal, not mental.

> "Thus the problem of the meaning of words is brought into connection with the problem of images." (010-037)

The upshot is that meaning is not a mysterious semantic relation but a causal chain: word to image, image to object, and back again -- all analysable in terms of the physiology and behaviour of the language user.

## Language as Habit

Russell's treatment of language fits squarely within his account of [[Instinct and Habit|instinct-habit]]. Linguistic competence is a matter of acquired [[Knowledge|knowledge]]-habits:

| Aspect of Language | Causal Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Word recognition | Stimulus (sound) triggers mnemic response |
| Word production | Habitual motor patterns (speech) |
| Word comprehension | Causal connection from word to image/action |
| Naming | Mnemic association between object and word |
| Narrative | Sequence of word-habits causally linked to past events |

This behaviouristic table shows that nothing essentially mental is required for language. A purely physical organism with the right causal wiring could exhibit all the linguistic behaviour we associate with meaning.
