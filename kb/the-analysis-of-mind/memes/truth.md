# Truth

**Truth**, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, is analysed as a property of [[belief|beliefs]] that consists in correspondence with fact — but understood behaviouristically, not as a mysterious relation between a proposition and a state of affairs. Russell rejects the [[idealism|idealist]] coherence theory, which defines truth as consistency within a system of beliefs, arguing that coherence is neither necessary nor sufficient for truth. He also criticizes the appeal to self-evidence as a criterion, since false beliefs can feel just as self-evident as true ones. Instead, Russell proposes that a belief is true when the events that would verify it actually occur — a causal-correspondence account that aligns with his broader [[behaviourism|behaviourist]] and neutral monist framework, and that distinguishes truth sharply from [[Knowledge|knowledge]], since one can have true beliefs without knowing them to be true. The theory also engages the pragmatist insights of [[William James]], who likewise sought to ground truth in practical consequences rather than abstract correspondence.

## The Formal Problem of Truth

Russell frames the problem of truth as a formal one: given that beliefs are the primary bearers of truth and falsehood, what does it mean to say that a particular belief is true? The question is not how we *discover* truth — that is a psychological or methodological question — but what truth *consists in*:

> "The definition of truth and falsehood, which is our topic today, is a formal problem. We are not asking how we can know whether a given belief is true, but what it means to say that it is true." (013-001)

Beliefs are characterized by the fact that they can be either true or false. This is a distinctive feature of belief that sets it apart from other mental phenomena such as sensation or desire:

> "Beliefs are the vehicles of truth and falsehood. A sensation is neither true nor false; a desire is neither true nor false. It is only when we come to belief that the distinction between truth and falsehood arises." (012-003)

The challenge, then, is to explain this distinction without appealing to unobservable entities — without positing "propositions" as abstract objects that beliefs somehow "correspond" to. Russell's behaviourism demands that the definition of truth be grounded in observable causal relations.

## Against Coherence and Self-Evidence

Russell considers two prominent alternatives to the correspondence theory and finds both wanting. The first is the coherence theory, associated with [[idealism]], which holds that truth consists in the internal consistency of a system of beliefs:

> "Coherence as a definition of truth has been advocated by idealists, who hold that a belief is true if it coheres with the rest of our beliefs and false if it does not." (013-031)

Russell's objection is twofold. First, coherence is not sufficient for truth: a fairy tale may be internally consistent while being entirely false. Second, coherence is not necessary for truth: scientific discoveries often contradict existing beliefs before being accepted, but they are true nonetheless. Coherence may serve as a *test* of truth in some contexts, but it cannot serve as its *definition*.

The second alternative is the appeal to self-evidence — the idea that certain beliefs carry an intrinsic mark of truth that can be directly perceived:

> "Self-evidence has often been taken as the guarantee of truth, but the difficulty is that a belief may seem self-evident and yet be false. Self-evidence is a psychological property, not a logical one." (013-024)

Russell acknowledges that some beliefs do feel self-evident, but argues that this feeling is no guarantee of their truth. The history of philosophy and science is replete with examples of propositions that once seemed self-evident — such as Euclidean geometry as the necessary structure of space — but turned out to be false.

## The Behaviouristic Account of Truth

Russell's positive account is a behaviouristic version of the correspondence theory. A belief, understood behaviouristically as a disposition to act in certain ways, is true if the events that would satisfy or verify those dispositions actually occur:

> "The truth or falsehood of a belief consists in the occurrence or non-occurrence of the events that would verify it. If I believe that it will rain, my belief is true if it rains; if I believe that there is food in the cupboard, my belief is true if, when I go to the cupboard, I find food there." (013-009)

This account has several distinctive features:

1. **Verification is behavioural**: truth is not a static correspondence between an abstract proposition and a fact, but a dynamic relation between an organism's expectations and its subsequent experiences.

2. **Truth is causal**: a true belief is one that is caused, ultimately, by the very fact it represents. The belief's content is linked to its truth-condition through a causal chain: "Truth results not from the intrinsic properties of a belief but from its relations to occurrences outside itself." (013-025)

3. **Falsehood is possible**: unlike sensations, which simply are what they are, beliefs can misrepresent. A false belief is one whose verification-events do not occur. Russell notes that falsehood itself is evidence of the sophistication of cognition: "Falsehood is evidence of knowledge, for it shows that the organism has expectations that go beyond the immediately given." (013-021)

4. **Truth is not identical to knowledge**: one can have a true belief without knowing it is true. Knowledge requires, in addition to truth, some form of justification or reliable causation.

## The Causal Structure of Truth

Russell elaborates the causal structure of truth by distinguishing between the *causes* of a belief and its *verification-conditions*:

> "The truth or falsehood of a belief depends upon the relation between the belief and the fact to which it refers. The difficulty lies in specifying what this relation is without presupposing a knowing subject." (013-022)

The solution lies in the concept of *reference* understood causally. A belief refers to the set of events that would verify it; these events are the belief's truth-condition. The belief is true if and only if those events actually occur. Crucially, this reference relation is not a mental act of "pointing" at an object — it is a causal-habitual relation built up through experience.

This yields the following structural analysis:

| Component | Role in Truth | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Belief (disposition) | The bearer of truth-value | Expectation that food is in the cupboard |
| Verification-events | What would make the belief true | Finding food upon opening the cupboard |
| Causal link | How the belief refers to its object | Past experiences linking cupboard-opening to food |
| Occurrence of events | The truth-maker | Food is actually in the cupboard |

Russell emphasizes that an "absolute criterion" of truth is not available — we have no infallible test that guarantees a belief is true. But this is a practical limitation, not a theoretical one:

> "There is no absolute criterion of truth, no mark by which a true belief can be infallibly distinguished from a false one. But this does not mean that the concept of truth is meaningless or that truth does not consist in correspondence with fact." (013-030)

## Truth and Belief

The relationship between truth and [[belief]] is fundamental to Russell's analysis. Truth is not a property of propositions in a Platonic heaven; it is a property of beliefs as psychological or behavioural occurrences. This naturalizes the concept of truth and integrates it into the broader project of the analysis of mind:

> "Beliefs, as we have seen, are the vehicles of truth and falsehood. Without belief, there would be no truth — not because truth depends on being believed, but because it is beliefs that are the sort of thing that can be true." (012-001)

This view has a significant consequence: truth and falsehood are not metaphysical primitives but artefacts of the cognitive architecture of organisms capable of belief. An organism that merely sensed its environment without forming expectations would encounter no truth or falsehood — only sensations:

- **Truth** is the property a belief has when the events it anticipates actually occur.
- **Falsehood** is the property a belief has when those events do not occur.
- **Knowledge** is, in part, the possession of true beliefs that are reliably caused by their objects.
- **Belief** is the psychological vehicle — the disposition to act — that carries truth-value.
