## Definition

Verification, in Russell's epistemology, is the process by which a belief or expectation is confirmed through the occurrence of the sensations it anticipates. Russell identifies expectation-based verification as the paradigm case: "One of the plainest cases of verification, perhaps ultimately the only strictly pure case, is the confirmation of an expectation" (013-036). Verification does not require access to a mind-independent reality; it operates entirely within experience, as a relation between an anticipatory state and a subsequent sensation that matches it.

## Russell's Account

Russell treats verification as central to his theory of truth and falsehood, developed in Lectures XII and XIII. His approach is pragmatic in method but not in metaphysics: verification is the test of truth, but truth itself is defined in terms of correspondence — specifically, correspondence between a belief-content and a fact, both of which are constituted from the same neutral particulars.

The paradigm of verification, for Russell, is the confirmation of an expectation. He describes the sequence: "We have first an expectation" of some future sensation, and then the sensation occurs as anticipated; this is what "constitutes the maximum of verification" (013-037). The expectation is a present occurrence (an image or word-habit), and the verifying sensation is a subsequent occurrence; the relation between them is one of similarity or structural correspondence. Russell generalizes: "I think all verification is ultimately of the above sort" (013-038).

This account has important implications. Verification does not require comparing a belief with a reality outside experience — a comparison that would be impossible, since we cannot step outside our own experience to check its correspondence with the world. Instead, verification is always a relation between two occurrences within experience: an anticipatory state (belief, expectation, memory) and a confirming (or disconfirming) sensation. The "truth of memory cannot be wholly pragmatic," Russell notes, because memory-belief carries its own intrinsic warrant — but verification through subsequent experience provides additional confirmation (009-019).

Russell extends the verification model beyond simple expectations to more complex beliefs. Even the verification of a judgment about an animal's emotional state — for example, whether a dog is inquisitive or terrified — involves expectations about its subsequent behaviour: "The verification of our judgment, so far as verification is possible, consists in the satisfaction or otherwise of the expectations" derived from our interpretation (003-008). In every case, verification operates through the satisfaction of expectations by subsequent sensations.

Russell's verificationism is thus closely tied to his broader causal theory. Beliefs are dispositions to behave in certain ways, and verification is simply the event of those dispositions being fulfilled. The causal chain from object to sensation to belief to expectation to confirming sensation is the complete story of how we know what we know. There is no need for a mysterious "correspondence" between mental states and an inaccessible external world — the correspondence is always between two sets of sensible occurrences linked by causal laws.

## Key Distinctions

- **Verification vs. Truth**: Verification is the experiential test of truth — the process by which we confirm a belief. Truth itself is the correspondence between a belief-content and a fact, which may hold independently of whether anyone verifies it (013-001).
- **Expectation-Based vs. Memory-Based Verification**: Expectation looks forward and is verified by a subsequent sensation; memory looks backward and its verification, where possible, involves corroboration by other memories or by physical records (009-019, 013-036).
- **Maximum vs. Partial Verification**: "Maximum verification" occurs when an expectation is directly followed by the anticipated sensation; partial verification occurs when the confirming evidence is indirect or inferential (013-037).

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of verification in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) anticipates themes that would later become central to logical positivism and the verification principle of the Vienna Circle. However, Russell's version is more psychological than logical: he is interested in the mental processes by which verification actually occurs, not in formulating a criterion of meaningfulness. His account builds on William James's pragmatist theory of truth — Russell quotes James's description of belief as "the sense of reality" — while rejecting James's reduction of truth to practical consequences. Russell's verificationism also reflects the influence of Einstein's operational definitions in physics, where concepts are defined by the procedures used to measure them.
