Ernst Mach (1838–1916) was an Austrian physicist and philosopher whose empiricist analysis of sensation and critique of Newtonian absolute space exerted a decisive influence on the development of logical positivism and early analytic philosophy. In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell explicitly cites Mach's *Analysis of Sensations* (1886) as a precursor to his own [[neutral-monism|neutral monist]] position.

## Sensations as the Common Ground

Russell's most direct reference to Mach appears in his lecture on [[sensation]] and [[images]], where he articulates the thesis that sensations are neither exclusively mental nor exclusively physical but constitute the intersection of the two:

> [008-012] Sensations are what is common to the mental and physical worlds; they may be defined as the intersection of mind and matter. This is by no means a new view; it is advocated, not only by the American authors I have mentioned, but by Mach in his Analysis of Sensations, which was published in 1886.

Russell's acknowledgment of Mach is significant for several reasons. Mach had argued, decades before Russell and [[william-james|William James]], that the basic elements of experience — colors, sounds, pressures, temperatures — are in themselves neutral. They become "physical" when studied in their relations to one another (as in physics) and "mental" when studied in their relation to the organism (as in psychology). This is precisely the position Russell develops in *The Analysis of Mind*, though Russell supplements it with a detailed account of how the same neutral elements can be organized into the causal structures we identify as minds and material objects.

## Machian Empiricism and Russell's Method

Mach's influence on Russell extends beyond the specific thesis of neutral sensations. Mach's general philosophical method — the elimination of unobservable metaphysical entities through analysis of the sensory basis of scientific concepts — is closely aligned with Russell's own analytical approach. Just as Mach sought to eliminate absolute space from physics by showing that all spatial relations can be reduced to relations among sensations, Russell seeks to eliminate the [[self]] and the subject-object relation from psychology by showing that [[consciousness]] can be analyzed into more primitive components.

Russell departs from Mach, however, in his willingness to retain theoretical entities that are not directly observable. While Mach was a strict phenomenalist who would admit only sensations into his ontology, Russell is comfortable postulating unobserved entities — including [[mnemic-causation|mnemic causes]], unconscious [[desire|desires]], and neural processes — provided they earn their keep by explaining observable regularities in mental life. This is Russell the logical analyst rather than Russell the strict empiricist.

## Mach and the Anti-Materialist Tendency in Physics

Russell also cites Mach in the context of the "anti-materialist tendency" in physics that he invokes in the preface to *The Analysis of Mind*. Mach's critique of Newtonian mechanics — particularly his argument that absolute space and absolute motion are unobservable and therefore scientifically illegitimate — contributed to the intellectual climate in which relativity theory developed. For Russell, this anti-materialist tendency in physics converges with the anti-mentalist tendency in behaviourist psychology: both suggest that the traditional categories of "mind" and "matter" are inadequate to the phenomena they purport to describe.

Mach's vision of a unified science grounded in the analysis of sensation thus serves as a methodological model for Russell's neutral monist project. Where Mach sought a physics purged of metaphysical assumptions, Russell seeks a psychology — and ultimately a metaphysics — in which the distinction between the mental and the physical is recognized as a pragmatic convenience rather than a reflection of fundamental ontological divisions.
