## Definition

Particulars are the concrete, sensory elements that Russell takes to be the fundamental constituents of physical objects. A particular is an entity that exists at a specific time and place and is sensed by a specific observer — "one of the set of correlated particulars which is the physical object of which (say) a table is the appearance" (005-029). Unlike universals, particulars are unique, unrepeatable occurrences in sensory experience.

## Russell's Account

Russell develops his theory of particulars primarily in Lecture V, "The Physical World." He argues that what we call a physical object — a table, for instance — is not a single enduring substance but a system of correlated particulars perceived by different observers from different perspectives. "Given a system of particulars which is a physical object," Russell writes, "I shall define that one of the system which is in a given place (say) as the 'appearance' of the object from that place" (005-022).

The correlation among particulars is essential to the construction of physical objects. Russell notes that "when a number of people at a table all have similar visual sensations, these different sensations are all particulars" (005-027), and it is their systematic correlation — not an underlying substance — that constitutes the table. This approach secures "neutrality by the equal representation of all possible points of view" (005-010), treating no single observer's perspective as privileged.

Russell also distinguishes between two fundamental ways of grouping particulars: "one way collects together the particulars which are appearances of the same object from different places; the other collects together the particulars which are appearances of different objects from the same place" (005-030). The former yields the physical object; the latter yields what Russell calls a "perspective" — the totality of things seen from a single viewpoint.

## Key Distinctions

- **Particulars vs. physical objects**: Physical objects are logical constructions from particulars; they exist as systems of correlated sense-data, not as independent substances
- **Particulars vs. universals**: Particulars are spatiotemporally unique; universals (like "redness") are repeatable across multiple instances
- **Actual vs. possible particulars**: Russell allows for unsensed particulars (sensibilia) that would be sensed if an observer occupied the relevant position — "concerned essentially with actual particulars, not merely with systems" (005-032)

## Historical Context

Russell's theory of particulars extends the empiricist tradition of Berkeley and Hume, who likewise treated objects as collections of sensory qualities. It also responds to the problem of perception raised by the New Realists: how different observers can perceive the "same" object. By reconstructing objects as systems of particulars, Russell eliminates the need for an unknowable thing-in-itself behind appearances.
