## Definition

Reference, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, denotes the directedness of mental states toward objects — what Brentano called the "intentional inexistence" of mental phenomena. Russell quotes Brentano's thesis directly: "mental phenomena have essential reference to objects, except possibly in the case of pleasure and pain" (001-018). However, Russell's own position is more nuanced: he questions whether this reference is genuinely essential or merely apparent, arguing that "the reference of thoughts to objects is not the simple, irreducible thing Brentano supposed it to be" (001-023).

## Russell's Account

Russell approaches reference through critical engagement with Brentano's intentionality thesis. While acknowledging that many mental states *seem* to be about something, he seeks a behaviorist and physiological explanation that does not rely on an irreducible mental relation: "I do not believe that mental phenomena have that intrinsic reference to objects which Brentano assigns to them" (001-037). Instead, Russell proposes that the appearance of reference can be explained through causal chains involving images, words, and bodily dispositions.

In his discussion of belief, Russell refines the concept of reference into what he calls "objective reference." He states: "to its objective I call the 'reference' or the 'objective reference' of the belief" (012-003), introducing a terminological distinction between the act of referring and the object referred to. This distinction is central to his analysis of propositional attitudes: "the objective reference of a belief is connected with the fact that it has a 'meaning'" (012-008).

Russell extends his treatment of reference to linguistic context, noting that "the power of acting with reference to what is sensibly absent is one of the things that constitute the superiority of what is called 'intelligence'" (008-042). This highlights the functional role of reference in adaptive behaviour — thinking about absent things enables planning and foresight.

## Key Distinctions

Russell distinguishes several aspects of reference:
- **Reference vs. content**: The content is the mental occurrence itself; the reference is its directedness toward an object (001-023). Russell follows (and partly criticizes) Meinong's analysis of "act, content, and object."
- **Objective vs. subjective reference**: Objective reference concerns the fact that a belief points to something in the world (012-003); subjective reference concerns the introspective feeling of aboutness.
- **Reference in belief vs. desire**: Both beliefs and desires exhibit reference, but Russell treats their referential structures as different — belief references facts, desire references goals (012-008).
- **Present vs. absent reference**: Reference to present objects (perception) differs from reference to absent objects (memory, imagination), the latter being "one of the things that constitute the superiority of what is called 'intelligence'" (008-042).

## Historical Context

Brentano's thesis of intentionality (that mental phenomena are distinguished from physical phenomena by their essential reference to objects) serves as Russell's starting point. Russell's critique is part of his broader project to reconcile the apparent directedness of thought with a neutral monist metaphysics that treats both mind and matter as constructions from the same neutral stuff. Meinong's theory of objects and the Austrian phenomenological tradition form the intellectual backdrop against which Russell develops his alternative account.
