## Definition

For Russell, emotion is one of the fundamental categories of mental phenomena, alongside [[Sensation-Image|sensation]], [[Memory-Image|image]], and [[Belief|belief]]. Each of these "may be a cause of a series of actions" (003-033), and emotion in particular is distinguished by its intimate connection to bodily states. Russell treats emotion not as a primitive irreducible quality of mind but as a complex phenomenon amenable to causal analysis.

## Russell's Account

Russell devotes sustained attention to emotion, culminating in an extended discussion of the James–Lange theory in his later lectures. He notes that emotions are "dependent upon past experience" (004-010) and that words exercise significant "influence upon our emotions, in rhetoric for example" (003-028). This highlights the learned and associative character of emotional responses.

A central thread in Russell's treatment is the relation between emotion and sensation. He observes that "a very intense emotion will often bring with it—especially in those who are not accustomed to them—bodily sensations" (008-027), and that images "may cause appropriate emotions and appropriate behaviour" (010-033). The causal chain—object → sensation → emotion → behaviour—foreshadows his endorsement of a physiological account of the emotions.

## Key Distinctions

### Emotion vs. Sensation

Russell distinguishes emotion from bare sensation partly by its complexity and partly by its causal history. While sensations arise from immediate stimulus, emotions incorporate "previous experience" (004-010) and associational learning. The difference "between mind and matter" (006-026) is, on his view, not a radical dualism but a difference in the causal laws governing desires, pleasures, pains, and emotions as against purely physical events.

### The James–Lange Theory

Russell presents what he calls "modern views on the causation of emotions" beginning with "what is called the James–Lange theory" (014-004). On this account, "the coarser emotions, grief, fear, rage, love" (014-005) are not causes of bodily changes but rather perceptions of those changes. Russell notes that experimental evidence showed "many of the usual marks of emotion were present" (014-007) even in animals with interrupted visceral feedback, complicating a purely peripheral account. He ultimately defends a modified version in which bodily sensations are integral but not exhaustive of emotional experience.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of emotion draws on the James–Lange theory, formulated independently by [[William James]] and Carl Lange in the 1880s. James's famous claim—that we do not run because we are afraid but are afraid because we run—challenged the commonsense view of emotion as a purely mental cause of bodily expression. Russell engages this tradition critically, acknowledging its insights while noting the experimental limitations discovered by later physiologists including [[Knight Dunlap]].
