## Definition

A relation, in Russell's philosophical vocabulary, is a way in which two or more entities stand toward one another. Russell treats relations not as derivative properties of independently existing substances but as ontologically fundamental: an entity is "constituted by relations" to other entities rather than by an intrinsic essence (001-022). This relational ontology is the methodological backbone of his neutral monism and his rejection of the subject-object duality.

## Russell's Account

Russell's treatment of relations pervades *The Analysis of Mind* and serves as a unifying methodological principle across his discussions of mind, matter, knowledge, and perception.

**(1) Relation to an object as the supposed mark of the mental.** Brentano held that "relation to an object is an ultimate irreducible characteristic of mental phenomena" (001-018). Every mental act is directed toward something — thought is always *of* something. Russell initially accepted this thesis but came to reject it: the reference of thoughts to objects is "derivative" and consists largely in beliefs and causal connections, not in an irreducible intentional relation (001-023).

**(2) The relation of content and object.** Meinong distinguished the content of a thought (a mental entity) from its object (typically an external entity). "The reference of thoughts to objects is not, I believe," Russell writes, "the simple direct essential thing that Brentano and Meinong represent it as being" (001-023). The relation between content and object is mediated by causal chains, linguistic conventions, and habitual associations — it is not a primitive metaphysical bond.

**(3) Relations as constitutive.** Russell's positive doctrine is that entities are constituted by "different patterns" of "inter-relations" (001-031). The very same neutral particulars can enter into different relational arrangements, yielding what we call "mind" in one pattern and "matter" in another. The stuff is the same; the patterns are what differ. "The stuff of which the world of our experience is composed is... neither mind nor matter, but something more primitive than either" (001-007).

**(4) The subject-object relation rejected.** Russell aligns himself with the American new realists in denying that "the relation of subject and object is fundamental" (001-037). The grammatical structure of subject-predicate sentences misleads us into positing a fundamental knowing-relation between a mental subject and an external object. Russell argues that this relation, like all others, is a construction out of more basic patterns of neutral particulars.

**(5) Relations in perception.** In perception, Russell notes, "we are in direct contact with objects, which may be, and usually are, outside our bodies" (001-026). This "direct contact" is not a mysterious mental relation but a causal-physical one, explicable in terms of light waves, neural impulses, and behavioral responses. Perception, like thought, involves no essentially mental relation.

**(6) Time relations and personal identity.** Russell extends the relational analysis to personal identity: "It is not only by time-relations that the parts of one biography are collected together" (007-009). A person is a collection of particulars unified by certain kinds of relations (mnemic, causal, spatial) — not by a substantial self that underlies them.

## Key Distinctions

- **Relation vs. Quality**: Traditional metaphysics treated qualities as intrinsic to substances and relations as derivative. Russell reverses this: relations are fundamental, and qualities emerge from relational patterns.
- **Internal vs. External Relations**: Though not explicitly thematized in *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell's broader philosophy distinguishes external relations (which do not affect the nature of the relata) from internal relations (which do). His atomism depends on the reality of external relations.
- **Causal vs. Intentional Relations**: Russell replaces Brentano's mysterious "intentional relation" with ordinary causal relations — the same kind that hold between physical events.
- **Subject-Object vs. Neutral Patterns**: The subject-object duality is replaced by the idea of neutral particulars arranged in different relational patterns.

## Historical Context

Russell's emphasis on relations was a cornerstone of his break with British idealism (Bradley, Green), which held that all relations are internal — that every relation affects the intrinsic nature of its terms. Russell, following G.E. Moore's early realism, insisted on the reality of external relations, which made his logical atomism possible. In *The Analysis of Mind*, this relational ontology is extended to the philosophy of mind: the mental-physical distinction is not a dualism of substances but a difference in relational patterns among neutral particulars. This move was influenced by William James's "radical empiricism" and the American new realists' rejection of the subject-object dichotomy.
