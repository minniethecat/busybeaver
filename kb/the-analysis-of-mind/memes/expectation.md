## Definition

Expectation is one of three fundamental kinds of belief identified by Russell, alongside memory and bare assent. It is the mental state of anticipating future events through images of expected sensations, accompanied by a characteristic belief-feeling. Russell writes that "some expectations may deserve to rank with memory as a separate form of belief" (009-018), and he ultimately accords it equal status: "three kinds of belief, namely memory, expectation and bare assent" (012-042). Expectation is distinguished from memory by its temporal direction — toward the future rather than the past — and from bare assent by its involvement of specific anticipatory imagery.

## Russell's Account

Russell develops his account of expectation primarily in Lectures IX through XII. Expectation, like memory, involves images, but these images point forward in time. When we wait for a train, we are "entertaining an image of a train, in the state of mind which may be expressed by 'there will be a train'" (012-045). The content is imagistic, but the attitude — the belief-feeling directed at that content — is future-oriented.

Russell draws a close parallel between memory and expectation: "Exactly similar remarks apply to expectations. Bare assent, memory and expectation are three kinds of belief all of which I shall regard as ultimate" (012-004). He argues that expectation cannot be reduced to memory plus inference, nor to bare assent about future-tense propositions. Instead, it is a primitive form of belief with its own distinctive phenomenology.

The functional role of expectation is central to Russell's account of behaviour. Expectation "brings us the image" before the actual sensation arrives (008-015), enabling anticipatory action. "Men and animals continually act with reference to the future" (003-008), and they "derive their expectations as to subsequent conduct" from past experience (003-008). Expectation bridges the gap between past learning and future-oriented behaviour.

## Key Distinctions

- **Expectation vs Memory**: Memory is accompanied by a feeling of pastness; expectation by a feeling of futurity. Both involve images and a belief-feeling, but their temporal direction differs (009-038). Russell distinguishes them as co-equal primitive belief types (012-042).
- **Expectation vs Bare Assent**: Bare assent involves no specific temporal imagery — it is the mental "yes" given to a proposition without past or future feeling. Expectation, by contrast, involves anticipatory images and a future-directed feeling (009-038).
- **Expectation vs Imagination**: Mere imagination-images "to which no assent is given" (012-040) lack the belief-feeling that characterises expectation. Expectation is imagination plus future-directed belief.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of expectation as a primitive form of belief is innovative within the empiricist tradition. Hume had analysed expectation in terms of custom and habit, reducing it to associative mechanisms. Russell preserves the associative basis — expectation arises through habitual associations — but insists that the belief-feeling accompanying future-directed images is psychologically irreducible. This reflects his broader project of taking introspective data seriously while grounding mental phenomena in physiological and behavioural facts.

## Criticism

Russell's claim that expectation is an "ultimate" form of belief raises a question he does not fully resolve: if expectation, memory, and bare assent are all ultimate, what unifies them as species of belief? The belief-feeling that they share seems to be the common element, but Russell's characterisation of this feeling remains vague — it is "a specific feeling which we may call the feeling of belief" (012-027). Whether this feeling is genuinely primitive or analysable into more basic components (perhaps involving dispositional and behavioural factors) is left open.
