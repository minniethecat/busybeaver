## Definition

Presentation (German: *Vorstellung*) is, in Russell's engagement with Brentano and Meinong, a fundamental mode of consciousness in which an object is simply presented to the mind — without the affirmation or denial characteristic of judgment, and without the pro/con attitude characteristic of desire. Russell states: "in presentation something is presented, in judgment something is affirmed or denied" (001-016). This defines presentation as the most basic and neutral mode of mental directedness, upon which more complex attitudes like judgment and desire are built.

## Russell's Account

Russell treats presentation primarily through the lens of Brentano's classification of mental phenomena. Brentano divided mental acts into three fundamental classes: presentations (*Vorstellungen*), judgments, and phenomena of love and hate (feelings and desires). Presentation is the foundational class because "in presentation something is presented" (001-016) — it is the simplest form of having an object before the mind.

However, Russell is critical of the traditional analysis of presentation into three components: act, content, and object. He notes that Meinong had "criticized the analysis of a presentation into act, content and object" (012-005), pointing toward a more economical account. Russell's own position tends toward collapsing this tripartite structure, arguing that the content is not a separate entity mediating between act and object but rather the presentation itself under a different description.

Russell also considers the role of language and imagery in presentation. He asserts that thought "does not need language or sensuous presentations" (011-014), pushing against the empiricist view that all thinking requires sensory images. This claim — that there can be "imageless thought" — is central to Russell's rejection of the traditional empiricist account of mental content, and it separates his view from both classical British empiricism and from certain strands of behaviorism that reduce thought to subvocal speech.

Russell also connects presentation to the origins of language, noting that written language "began as a direct pictorial representation of what was to be expressed" (010-005), suggesting that pictorial presentation is developmentally and functionally prior to linguistic expression.

## Key Distinctions

Russell distinguishes presentation from:
- **Judgment**: Presentation merely presents an object; judgment affirms or denies something *about* the object (001-016). Presentation is ontologically simpler.
- **Feeling and desire**: These involve a pro/con attitude toward the presented object, while presentation itself is neutral.
- **Linguistic expression**: Presentation can occur without language — "I assert rather that in proper thinking we do not need language or sensuous presentations" (011-014).
- **Pictorial imagery**: Early writing began as pictorial presentation (010-005), but mental presentation need not involve sensory images — a point on which Russell diverges from classical empiricism.

## Historical Context

Russell's analysis of presentation engages with the Brentano school's classification of mental phenomena. Brentano's tripartite division (presentation → judgment → love/hate) was refined by Meinong and others in the Austrian phenomenological tradition. Russell's critical engagement with this tradition — accepting the basic distinction while rejecting its ontological commitments — is characteristic of his broader project of reconstructing the philosophy of mind on neutral monist foundations.
