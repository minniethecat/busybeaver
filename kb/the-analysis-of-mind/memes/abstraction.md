[[Bertrand Russell|Russell]] addresses the problem of [[Abstraction|abstraction]] in Lecture XI of *The Analysis of Mind*, offering a [[Behaviourism|behaviourist]] alternative to the traditional philosophical doctrine. Rather than treating abstraction as a mysterious mental operation by which the mind extracts universal concepts from particular experiences, Russell proposes a physiological account grounded in [[Mnemic Causation|mnemic causation]] and the associative properties of words.

## The Traditional Doctrine of Abstraction

Russell briefly examines the classical empiricist account of abstraction before offering his critique:

> [011-006] …in defence of the doctrine of abstraction, and try if I can discover wh…

> [011-007] …posed that they are formed by abstraction, after the manner set forth i…

The traditional doctrine, associated with [[John Locke|Locke]] and the British empiricists, holds that general ideas are formed by abstracting common features from multiple particular experiences. We see many individual dogs, abstract their common properties, and thereby form the general idea of "dog." Russell finds this account both psychologically implausible and metaphysically suspect (011-008).

## Physiological Abstraction

Russell's alternative is what he calls "physiological abstraction":

> [011-008] …nt of a sort of PHYSIOLOGICAL abstraction. Mnemic homophony gives us, w…

The mechanism is mnemic — the same causal process by which past experience modifies present response. When we encounter multiple similar particulars, the nervous system registers their common features through a kind of physiological resonance. "Mnemic homophony" — the tendency of similar stimuli to evoke similar responses — produces a generalized response that functions as what philosophers call a "general idea."

This is not a mental operation performed by a [[Mind|mind]] on ideas, but a physiological process occurring in [[Nervous System|nervous tissue]]. The organism learns to respond to a class of stimuli rather than to individual particulars, and this class-responsive disposition is what the abstraction doctrine misdescribes as a "general idea" (010-050).

## Words and Abstraction

Russell emphasizes the role of [[Words and Meaning|words]] in enabling abstraction:

> [010-050] …words we are able to dwell on abstractions in a way which would otherwi…

Words provide a handle for abstract thought. The word "dog" becomes associated, through repeated experience, with a class of similar stimuli. When we hear or think the word, it evokes a generalized response — a disposition to behave appropriately toward any member of the class. Words thus make possible a kind of abstract thinking that would be impossible through imagery alone.

This account aligns with Russell's broader theory of [[Meaning|meaning]], in which the meaning of a word is understood through its causal role in behaviour rather than through a mental act of "intending" or "referring."

## Abstraction and General Ideas

Russell connects his analysis of abstraction to the problem of [[General Ideas and Thought|general ideas]]. A general idea, on his account, is not a special kind of mental entity — it is simply a word-governed disposition to respond to a class:

The philosophical puzzle about how a finite mind can "contain" an infinite class dissolves when we recognize that what the mind contains is not a representation of the class but a disposition to respond to its members. The word is the instrument of abstraction; the disposition is its physiological reality.

## Critique of Mental Acts

As throughout *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell's analysis of abstraction serves his broader anti-mentalist project. The traditional doctrine posits an irreducible mental act — the act of abstracting. Russell replaces this with:

1. A physiological process (mnemic modification of nervous tissue)
2. A linguistic instrument (the word as class-marker)
3. A behavioural disposition (the generalized response to class members)

No mental act is required. What appears to introspection as "abstract thinking" is, from the outside, a complex of physiological and behavioural processes organized around linguistic symbols.
