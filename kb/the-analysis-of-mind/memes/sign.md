## Definition

A sign, in Russell's vocabulary, is a sensible particular that indicates or stands for something beyond itself. Russell uses "sign" and "symbol" interchangeably, defining the relation: "that which is now sensible may be called a 'sign' or 'symbol,' and that of which it is a sign or symbol may be called its 'meaning'" (010-005). The sign-meaning relation is the foundational mechanism by which words, images, and even sensations come to have cognitive significance.

## Russell's Account

Russell's account of signs is developed primarily in Lecture X, "Words and Meaning," where he analyses how language functions as a system of signs, and extends the analysis to mental imagery.

**(1) Signs and sensations.** Even at the level of sensation, Russell finds a sign-function at work. "A familiar sensation is to us a sign of the things that usually go with it" (008-006). When we see smoke, it is a sign of fire; when we hear a familiar voice, it is a sign of the person speaking. Sensations function as signs "partly by being itself a sign of things that are correlated with it" (008-008). This associative sign-function is the primitive basis upon which more sophisticated linguistic meaning is built.

**(2) Words as signs.** Words are the paradigmatic signs. Russell analyses word-meaning as a relation between a sensible particular (the heard or seen word) and whatever the word designates. He notes that a word may be used as a sign in different ways: "a word may be used as a sign of the present existence of something sensible" (010-038) — as when we say "Hullo!" upon seeing someone — or as a sign of something absent, as in narrative or abstract discourse. The hearer may "only pay attention to the words, and not call up images of what the words mean" (010-032), showing that sign-use can proceed without accompanying imagery.

**(3) Images as signs.** Russell extends the sign analysis to mental images. "Images are not themselves 'memories,' but merely used as signs of the past event" (009-058). An image functions as a sign when, through association, it points beyond itself to an event no longer present. This allows Russell to analyse memory without positing a direct relation to the past: the image is a present sign whose meaning is a past occurrence. The transition from image-as-sign to memory-belief requires the additional element of the feeling of pastness.

**(4) The associative basis.** Russell grounds the sign-function in association, not in any intrinsic resemblance. "There is no more 'intentionality' or 'reference' in a memory-image... than there is between a signpost and the town to which it points" (012-007). The image points to the past, or the word points to its meaning, not because of any mental act of "intending," but because of "associations which make them signs possessed of meaning" (012-018). Signification is a causal, associative relation, not a mysterious mental property.

**(5) Generality of signs.** Russell addresses the problem of how signs can be general — how the word "dog" can apply to all dogs. He discusses Locke's view that "words become general by being made the signs of general ideas" (011-006) and Berkeley's counter-argument "denying the existence of abstract general ideas" (011-006). Russell's own solution, following his behaviourist leanings, emphasizes the uniformity of the stimulus-response habit rather than positing abstract entities as the meanings of general signs.

## Key Distinctions

- **Sign vs. Meaning**: The sign is the sensible particular; the meaning is what it indicates.
- **Natural Sign vs. Conventional Sign**: Smoke is a natural sign of fire; words are conventional signs.
- **Sign vs. Image**: A sign may be a word or an image; an image becomes a sign when used to indicate something beyond itself.
- **Sign-function vs. Intrinsic Meaning**: There is no intrinsic meaning — signs acquire meaning through associative history.

## Historical Context

Russell's sign-theory is part of a broader early-20th-century turn toward semiotics. His treatment echoes C.S. Peirce's triadic theory of signs (though Russell does not cite Peirce), and his analysis of words as signs parallels the behaviourist linguistics of Leonard Bloomfield. Russell's distinctive contribution is to extend the sign-analysis from language to mental imagery itself — making the sign a unifying concept that bridges the external (linguistic behaviour) and the internal (thought) in a behaviourist-compatible way.
