## Definition

Volition, in Russell's usage, refers to the supposed act of willing — the mental determination that is traditionally taken to initiate voluntary action. Russell does not give volition extended treatment in *The Analysis of Mind*, but the few references he makes are consistently deflationary. Volition is treated not as an irreducible mental faculty but as a name for the final stage in a causal chain of desire, belief, and habit — a stage that has no special metaphysical status and that is itself subject to scientific explanation.

## Russell's Account

Russell's scattered remarks on volition reflect his broader naturalistic project: to reduce apparently mysterious mental acts to observable patterns of behavior and their causal antecedents.

**(1) Volition and instinct.** Russell notes that in accounts of animal behavior, "instinct and volition enter upon equal terms" (002-009). This parity is significant: if volition is on equal footing with instinct — an obviously natural, biologically-grounded phenomenon — then volition itself is natural rather than supernatural. The distinction between instinctive and voluntary action is one of degree, not of kind.

**(2) Volition and the illusion of freedom.** Russell observes that "the more our so-called volitions and their causes are examined, the more they seem to be determined by antecedent conditions" (003-017). The experience of free choice, on Russell's view, is a phenomenological illusion: what feels like an uncaused act of will is actually the final link in a causal chain of desires, beliefs, and habits, all of which are shaped by past experience and present circumstances.

**(3) Volition in the causal order.** Russell classifies volitions alongside beliefs and desires as phenomena that "appeared to us to belong to psychology and not to physics on account of their mnemic character" (015-023). Volitions are psychological because they depend on past experience (mnemic causation), not because they involve a non-physical mental substance. This dependence on past history — what an organism has learned, desired, and believed — is what distinguishes a volition from a mere physical event, not any mysterious freedom from causality.

**(4) The criterion of voluntariness.** Russell raises the question of what distinguishes a "voluntary act" from a reflex (002-009). The criterion he suggests is behavioral rather than introspective: a voluntary act is one that varies appropriately with circumstances and is shaped by past experience, while a reflex is stereotyped and innate. The difference lies in the kind of causal history involved, not in the presence or absence of a special mental act of "willing."

## Key Distinctions

- **Volition vs. Instinct**: Instinct is innate and stereotyped; volition is acquired and flexible. But both are natural, causally-determined processes.
- **Volition vs. Desire**: Desire is the motivational state; volition is the final determination to act. Russell treats the former as more fundamental.
- **Volition vs. Reflex**: A voluntary act is shaped by learning and varies with circumstances; a reflex is innate and invariant.
- **Volition vs. Free Will**: Russell's deflationary account of volition is incompatible with traditional doctrines of free will as uncaused choice.

## Historical Context

The concept of volition has a long history in philosophy and psychology, from Augustine's analysis of the will through Descartes's treatment of the will as the mark of the mind's freedom. In the 19th century, the rise of deterministic science — particularly in physiology and neurology — put pressure on the traditional notion of volition as an uncaused mental act. The discovery that brain processes precede conscious awareness of decision (later confirmed by Libet's experiments in the 1980s) was anticipated by the naturalistic psychology Russell draws on. Russell's deflationary treatment of volition is consistent with his broader neutral monism: if mind and matter are made of the same neutral stuff, then volition cannot be a special mental act outside the causal order. It must be, like everything else, a pattern of events governed by natural laws.
