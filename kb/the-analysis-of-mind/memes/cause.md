## Definition

**Cause** designates the relation between two events such that the occurrence of the first (the cause) brings about or produces the second (the effect). In psychology, causal explanation is the project of identifying the conditions — physiological, behavioural, and environmental — that reliably produce mental phenomena. Russell approaches causation not as a metaphysical primitive but as a working instrument of scientific inquiry: a causal law is simply a generalisation that permits prediction and control of behaviour (003-009).

## Russell's Account

Russell's treatment of causation in *The Analysis of Mind* is shaped by his commitment to a behaviourist methodology grounded in physiology. He maintains that mental events must be understood through their observable antecedents, and he repeatedly emphasises that physiological study reveals the causes of conditions such as insanity (001-048). Insanity, he notes, is "considered physiologically, as caused by defects in the brain" (001-050), and he criticises approaches that treat it as though "a positive cause had to be assigned for its being" in purely mentalistic terms (001-052). The driving methodological principle is that causal explanations in psychology must account for behaviour without multiplying mental entities beyond necessity (003-009).

The most distinctive element of Russell's causal framework is his discussion of **mnemic causation**. Borrowing from Richard Semon, Russell introduces a special variety of causation in which the effect is markedly "disproportionate to the proximate cause" (002-015). In ordinary physical causation, the magnitude of an effect corresponds closely to the magnitude of its immediate stimulus. Mnemic causation, by contrast, characterises situations where past experience — stored as a physiological trace — dramatically amplifies or alters the response to a present stimulus. This concept is essential for explaining how [[Habit]] operates: through repetition, a particular response to a stimulus becomes increasingly probable, until eventually the stimulus "causes it alone to be performed" (002-025). The past history of the organism enters into the causal chain in a way that cannot be reduced to the current physical state alone, yet Russell insists that the mechanism remains physiological.

## Key Distinctions

Russell draws several important distinctions within the broad category of causation:

- **Proximate versus remote causes.** A proximate physiological cause (a neural event) may be the immediate trigger of behaviour, while a more remote psychological explanation may invoke past learning history, [[Memory]], or acquired dispositions. The relation between these levels is not one of competition but of complementary description.

- **Physiological versus psychological causation.** Russell treats physiological causation as ontologically fundamental: brain states cause behaviour. Psychological causation is a higher-level description of the same causal nexus, legitimate insofar as it tracks real regularities (001-048, 001-050).

- **Ordinary versus mnemic causation.** The most important distinction in the text. Ordinary causation links stimulus magnitude to response magnitude in a proportionate way; mnemic causation, by contrast, involves the cumulative modification of the organism such that past events enter into the determination of present response (002-015).

- **Causal explanation versus teleological explanation.** Russell rejects the view that behaviour must be explained by reference to future goals or purposes. A causal account proceeds from antecedent conditions; Russell argues that we can account for behaviour without invoking purposes when the causal antecedents are fully specified (003-009).

## Historical Context

Russell's emphasis on causal laws in psychology reflects the early twentieth-century shift toward scientific naturalism in the study of mind. The concept of mnemic causation was developed by the German biologist Richard Semon in *Die Mneme* (1904) and *Die mnemischen Empfindungen* (1909), and Russell's adoption of it places him in conversation with both the emerging behaviourist tradition of [[Behaviourism]] and the physiological psychology of his contemporaries. Russell's insistence that causal explanation in [[Psychology]] be anchored in physiology aligns with the programme of John B. Watson and the broader reaction against introspective methods, though Russell's philosophical sophistication — particularly his event-ontology and his neutral monism — distinguishes his account from simpler stimulus-response behaviourisms.
