Sigmund Freud (1856–1939), the Austrian founder of psychoanalysis, appears in *The Analysis of Mind* as the most influential theorist of the [[unconscious]] in Russell's era. Russell engages Freud's work seriously — acknowledging its empirical importance — while rejecting what he sees as the mythological trappings of Freudian metapsychology.

## The Empirical Contribution

Russell acknowledges without reservation the importance of psychoanalytic discoveries for the philosophy of [[mind]]. The Freudian demonstration that much of mental life operates outside conscious awareness is, for Russell, one of the forces driving the rejection of the traditional concept of [[consciousness]]:

> [001-047] Psycho-analysis, as every one knows, is primarily a method of understanding hysteria and certain forms of insanity; but it has been found that there is much in the lives of ordinary men and women which bears a humiliating resemblance to the delusions of the insane. The connection of dreams, irrational beliefs and foolish actions with unconscious wishes has been brought to light, though with some exaggeration, by Freud and Jung and their followers.

Russell does, however, register a reservation: he suggests that the psychoanalysts are "unduly narrow" in their emphasis on certain kinds of unconscious wishes, noting that wishes for honour and power are "equally operative and equally liable to concealment" (001-047). This criticism reflects Russell's broader theoretical concern — not with the existence of unconscious desires, but with the specific taxonomy psychoanalysis imposes.

## The Mythology of the Unconscious

Russell's most pointed criticism of Freud concerns the conceptual apparatus surrounding the unconscious — the "censor," repression, and the personification of unconscious mental life:

> [001-052] Freud and his followers, though they have demonstrated beyond dispute the immense importance of "unconscious" desires in determining our actions and beliefs, have not attempted the task of telling us what an "unconscious" desire actually is, and have thus invested their doctrine with an air of mystery and mythology which forms a large part of its popular attractiveness.

Russell objects to the picture of the unconscious as "a sort of underground prisoner, living in a dungeon" (001-052) — a separate consciousness suppressed by a Freudian censor. He diagnoses the popular appeal of this image in terms that anticipate later critiques of psychoanalytic rhetoric:

> [001-052] Most of us like the idea that we could be desperately wicked if only we let ourselves go. For this reason, the Freudian "unconscious" has been a consolation to many quiet and well-behaved persons.

## Russell's Alternative

Russell proposes to demystify the unconscious by treating it as a causal disposition rather than a hidden mental entity. An unconscious desire, on his view, is "merely a causal law of our behaviour" (001-053) — a tendency rather than an occult force. He also rejects Freud's claim that unconscious wishes are necessarily the product of repression:

> [001-053] It is not necessary to suppose, as Freud seems to do, that every unconscious wish was once conscious, and was then, in his terminology, "repressed" because we disapproved of it. On the contrary, we shall suppose that, although Freudian "repression" undoubtedly occurs and is important, it is not the usual reason for unconsciousness of our wishes. The usual reason is merely that wishes are all, to begin with, unconscious, and only become known when they are actively noticed.

This inversion — treating unconscious desire as the primitive form and conscious desire as derivative — is characteristic of Russell's broader project of building mental phenomena from simpler, non-mental components, an approach aligned with his [[neutral-monism]].

Russell also quotes with approval the behaviourist reinterpretation of the Freudian censor, citing the view that "one group of habits can 'down' another group of habits — or instincts" (001-055). By translating the censor from a metaphysical gatekeeper into ordinary behavioural inhibition, Russell aims to preserve Freud's empirical insights while discarding the mythological superstructure.
