## Definition

Realism, in the philosophical sense relevant to Russell's project, is the thesis that the objects of knowledge — whether of perception or thought — exist independently of the mind that knows them. Russell frames the issue explicitly: "I shall state the difference between realism and idealism in terms of this opposition of mental and extra-mental objects" (001-024). For the realist, what we perceive and think about is not constituted by our perceiving or thinking it; the object stands on its own. Russell distinguishes "two different kinds of realism, according as we make a thought consist of act and object, or of object only" (001-027), aligning himself with the latter, object-only variety.

## Russell's Account

Russell's relationship to realism is nuanced. He opens Lecture I by declaring: "I have been in the past a realist, and I remain a realist as regards knowledge" (001-025). Yet his realism is qualified — he remains realist about the objects of sensation and knowledge but rejects the act-object distinction that traditional realism inherited from Brentano. For Russell, the mental act is a fiction: "Empirically, I cannot discover anything corresponding to the supposed act" (001-022). What remains is the object alone, and the question becomes how objects — sensations, images, beliefs — are organised into the patterns we call mind.

The American new realists, including William James, provide Russell with crucial resources. These thinkers held that "the 'stuff' of the world is neither mental nor material, but a 'neutral stuff,' out of which both are constructed" (P01-002). Russell embraces this neutral monism: "I agree with the American realists that the world consists of neutral entities" (001-037). However, he parts company with them on the analysis of consciousness and belief, arguing that their treatment is insufficiently attentive to the complexities of mnemic causation and the role of images in mental life.

Russell's realism manifests practically in his methodology: he treats sensations as genuine constituents of the world, not as private mental episodes. "Sensations are what is common to the mental and the physical worlds; they may be defined as the intersection of mind and matter" (001-037). This is realism without dualism — objects are real, but they are neither mental nor physical in themselves.

## Key Distinctions

- **Realism vs Idealism**: Idealism holds that objects of knowledge are in some way constituted by or dependent upon mind. Modern idealism "professes to be by no means certain that there are physical objects which exist when we are not perceiving them" (001-026). Realism denies this dependence (001-024).
- **Act-Object Realism vs Object-Only Realism**: Traditional realism (Brentano, Meinong) retains both the mental act and its object. Russell's realism eliminates the act, retaining only the object organised by causal and mnemic relations (001-027).
- **Naive Realism vs Critical Realism**: Russell's position is a critical realism — he is realist about the objects of sensation but denies that this commits him to the full apparatus of common-sense physical objects. Physical objects are logical constructions out of sensations, not inferred entities behind them.

## Historical Context

The realism-idealism debate was central to early twentieth-century philosophy. British idealism ([[Francis Herbert Bradley]], T. H. Green) had dominated Anglophone philosophy in the late nineteenth century. Russell and [[George Edward Moore]] led the revolt against idealism at Cambridge around 1900. The American new realists (William James, Ralph Barton Perry, Edwin Holt) developed a parallel critique, and their neutral monism deeply influenced Russell. By the time of *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell was synthesising these realist currents with behaviourist psychology to produce a novel philosophical position.

## Criticism

Russell acknowledges a persistent argument for idealism that he does not claim to have conclusively refuted: the argument from the privacy of experience. If all we ever directly know are our own sensations, and physical objects are inferred from these, then the realist's claim to know mind-independent objects requires justification. Russell's response — that sensations are themselves neutral entities, neither mental nor physical — is more a reframing of the problem than a solution. He concedes that the argument for idealism in memory "is more akin to idealism" and that these arguments "do not, to my mind, disprove realism, but they do make it a somewhat precarious hypothesis" (001-027).
