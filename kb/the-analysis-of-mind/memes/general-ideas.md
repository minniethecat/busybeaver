In Lecture XI of _The Analysis of Mind_, Russell takes up the problem of general ideas — a problem with a distinguished philosophical pedigree stretching from Plato through Berkeley and Hume. The question is: given that we use abstract words intelligently, what must be assumed about the mental content that accompanies their use? Russell's answer is radically deflationary. Abstract thought does not require abstract mental contents. A particular image or word can function as a general sign without itself being general, just as a particular line drawn by a geometer can stand for all lines.

## The Logical Problem

Russell opens by stating the problem in logical terms. The mere fact that we use abstract words intelligently does not, by itself, entail anything about mental content:

> "It is clear that a sufficiently ingenious person could manufacture a machine moved by olfactory stimuli which, whenever a dog appeared in its neighbourhood, would say, 'There is a dog,' and when a cat appeared would throw stones at it. The act of saying 'There is a dog,' and the act of throwing stones, would in such a case be equally mechanical." (011-003)

Correct speech, Russell argues, affords no better evidence of mental content than any other set of biologically useful movements. All that is inferable from language is that two instances of a universal may cause the utterance of two nearly identical instances of the same word. "Thus, so far as logic is concerned, we are entirely free to adopt any theory as to general ideas which empirical observation may recommend" (011-003).

## Berkeley's Critique of Abstract Ideas

Russell aligns himself with Berkeley and Hume's empiricist critique of abstract ideas. He quotes Berkeley at length:

> "I deny that I can abstract from one another, or conceive separately, those qualities which it is impossible should exist so separated; or that I can frame a general notion, by abstracting from particulars in the manner aforesaid." (011-005)

Berkeley argued that while we can imagine particular things and use them to represent other particulars of the same kind, we cannot form an "abstract general idea" — an idea of a triangle that is neither equilateral nor scalene, neither red nor blue. Russell, following Berkeley, holds that "I do not deny absolutely there are general ideas, but only that there are any ABSTRACT general ideas" (011-007).

## The Image Theory of General Thought

Russell's positive account is that a particular image becomes general by functioning as a sign. He explains:

> "An idea which, considered in itself, is particular, becomes general by being made to represent or stand for all other particular ideas of the same sort." (011-010)

A vague image of a dog may function as a general idea of "dog" if it is associated with the word "dog" and thereby acquires the same behavioural effects that exposure to any particular dog would have. The generality lies not in the intrinsic character of the image but in its causal role — its capacity to guide behaviour appropriately across a class of particulars. Russell writes that "a word embodies a general idea when its effects are different according to the general nature of the object concerning which it gives information" (011-010).

This account coheres with Russell's broader behaviouristic and causal framework. Just as [[Desire and Feeling|desire]] is analysed as a causal law of behaviour rather than a mental substance, so general ideas are analysed as causal dispositions acquired through association and habit. The image of a dog functions generally not by being abstract but by having been associated, through past experience, with the class of dogs.

## Imageless Thinking

Russell briefly addresses the Würzburg school's concept of "imageless thinking," the idea that some thought occurs without any sensory or imaginal content. He notes that "similar remarks apply to the general idea of 'imageless thinking'" (011-015) — that is, what appears to be thought without images may simply be thought whose images are so faint or fleeting as to escape introspection. Russell is sceptical but open-minded: he does not rule out imageless thought but sees no compelling evidence for it.

More important to Russell is that whether or not there is imageless thinking, the functional account of general ideas stands. The essential feature of general thought is that it enables behaviour appropriate to classes of objects, not just to particular individuals. This capacity depends on the associative structure of the mind — the [[Mnemic Phenomena|mnemic]] connections built up through past experience — not on the presence of abstract mental entities.

## The Place of General Ideas in Russell's System

The analysis of general ideas completes a crucial piece of Russell's project. If thought can be general without requiring abstract mental contents, then the analysis of mind can proceed without postulating a special faculty of abstraction that would set humans apart from animals. The difference between human and animal cognition becomes one of degree — the richness and flexibility of associative networks — rather than one of kind.

This conclusion is important for [[Neutral Monism|neutral monism]] as well. If the stuff of thought is no different in kind from the stuff of sensation — particular images and sensations organized by causal laws — then there is no need to postulate a special mental substance to account for abstract thought. The mind is built from the same neutral materials as the physical world, distinguished only by the causal laws under which those materials are organized.
