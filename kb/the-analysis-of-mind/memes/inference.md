## Definition

Inference, in Russell's analysis, is the cognitive process by which one belief is derived from another, or from sensory evidence. Russell treats inference not as a self-evident rational operation but as an empirical phenomenon subject to error: "anything that passes for knowledge of other people, or 'remembers' anything, is an inference, and in fact a very doubtful one" (001-039). His approach to inference is naturalistic — it is a psychological process that must be studied through behaviour and causal relations, not through logical introspection.

## Russell's Account

Russell's discussion of inference is woven throughout *The Analysis of Mind*, appearing most prominently in his treatments of knowledge of other minds, memory, and belief. In Lecture I, he introduces the problem by noting that our knowledge of other people's mental states is always inferential: "the inference that other people have something similar to our own stream of feeling is shown in this behaviour" (001-041). This inference, while practically indispensable, is "precarious" — and Russell notes that philosophers "anxious to avoid precarious inferences, have gradually discovered more and more" that can be known directly (001-040).

The tension between direct knowledge and inference is a recurring theme. In Lecture II, Russell cautions against inferences that "go beyond the observable facts" and insists they "are to be avoided with the utmost care" in scientific psychology (002-006). When studying other minds, we observe bodily movements and physiological processes, but "we must not, to begin with, assume any knowledge of the minds of others" — the inference to mental states must be built gradually from correlation (002-006).

Russell distinguishes two sources of knowledge about the external world in Lecture VII: "(1) By inference from things within my biography; (2) By testimony of others, reported through language" (007-020). Both are inferential and both are fallible. Inference from within one's own biography relies on mnemic associations — past regularities projected into the future — while testimony adds a further layer of inference through the interpretation of language.

In his discussion of belief in Lecture XII, Russell explores the relationship between inference and truth. Some beliefs "are obtained by inference, while others are not" (015-020). A belief obtained by inference is only as secure as the inferential principles on which it rests. Russell considers the view — associated with traditional logic — that inference proceeds "according to self-evident principles of inference," but he subjects this claim to the same naturalistic scrutiny he applies to all mental phenomena (013-023). For Russell, the reliability of inference is an empirical question, not an a priori certainty.

Russell's treatment of inference in Lecture IX is particularly revealing: he contrasts knowledge obtained "by inference, not (so to speak) spontaneously" with knowledge that appears to come directly from sense experience (009-028). Much of what we take as direct knowledge of the external world is, on examination, the product of "elaborate calculation and inference" that has become habitual and unconscious (009-018).

## Key Distinctions

- **Inference vs. Direct Knowledge**: Much of what passes for direct knowledge — of other minds, of the past, of the external world — is actually inferential and therefore fallible (001-039–041).
- **Logical vs. Psychological Inference**: Traditional logic treats inference as governed by self-evident rules; Russell treats it as a psychological process whose reliability must be established empirically (013-023).
- **Conscious vs. Unconscious Inference**: Some inferences are deliberate and conscious; others, such as filling in what we hear from fragmentary sounds, occur "quickly and unconsciously" (008-006).
- **Inference from Biography vs. Inference from Testimony**: We infer external facts either from our own sensory history (mnemic inference) or from the reports of others, which requires interpreting language — itself an inferential process (007-020).

## Historical Context

Russell's naturalistic treatment of inference reflects the broader empiricist tradition stemming from Hume, who argued that causal inference rests on custom and habit rather than rational demonstration. Russell extends this sceptical approach to all forms of inference, including the inference to other minds and to the past. His view also engages with the logical positivist programme of reducing all knowledge to direct observation plus logical construction, though Russell is more cautious than the positivists about the security of inferential knowledge. The behaviourist context is important: if we can only observe behaviour, then our knowledge of others' mental states is always doubly inferential — from behaviour to mind, and from observed behaviour to inferred mental states (003-007).
