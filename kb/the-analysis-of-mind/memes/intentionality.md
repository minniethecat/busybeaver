## Definition

Intentionality is the property of mental phenomena whereby they refer to, are about, or are directed toward an object. Brentano, drawing on medieval scholastic terminology, characterised it as "the intentional (also the mental) inexistence of an object" — a relation to a content or direction toward an object that is exclusive to psychical phenomena (001-016). In Brentano's formulation: "This intentional inexistence is exclusively peculiar to psychical phenomena. No physical phenomenon shows anything similar" (001-017).

## Russell's Account

Russell devotes substantial attention to intentionality in Lecture I, treating Brentano's thesis as a central claim requiring refutation. He acknowledges his own prior allegiance to the view: "Until very lately I believed, as he did, that mental phenomena have essential reference to objects, except possibly in the case of pleasure and pain. Now I no longer believe this, even in the case of knowledge" (001-018).

Russell's rejection proceeds along two lines. First, he questions the act-content-object distinction elaborated by Brentano's successor [[Alexius Meinong]], who separated the mental act, the content occurring in the mind, and the external object (001-019). Russell finds the act itself "unnecessary and fictitious" — "Empirically, I cannot discover anything corresponding to the supposed act" (001-022).

Second, and more fundamentally, Russell argues that the reference of thoughts to objects is not a simple, direct, essential relation as Brentano and Meinong maintained. Instead, "it seems to me to be derivative, and to consist largely in beliefs" (001-023). The apparent aboutness of thought is, on Russell's analysis, constituted by networks of belief connecting present mental content with other elements — what one would see, feel, or what others report.

## Key Distinctions

Russell does not reject the *phenomenon* of thoughts having objects; he rejects the claim that this is an ultimate, irreducible characteristic of mind. His positive account treats object-directedness as analysable into:

- **Belief-based reference**: The connection between a thought and its object is mediated by beliefs about causal and associative links (001-023).
- **Image-propositions**: In later lectures, Russell suggests that even image-based thought has propositional content without requiring an intrinsic object-reference (008-009).
- **Neutral monism**: If both mind and matter are composed of neutral-stuff, then the supposed gulf between the mental (object-directed) and the physical (non-object-directed) dissolves (001-037).

## Historical Context

Brentano's intentionality thesis, presented in *Psychology from the Empirical Standpoint* (1874), was enormously influential across continental and analytic philosophy. It was developed by Meinong, Husserl, and the phenomenological tradition. Russell's engagement with Brentano in *The Analysis of Mind* represents a pivotal moment in analytic philosophy's turn away from intentionality as a primitive mental property toward behaviourist and functional accounts. Russell retains his interest in psychology "not so much for its own sake, as for the light that it may throw on the problem of knowledge" (001-018), but concludes that Brentano's apparent simplicity is "incapable of maintaining itself either against an analytic scrutiny or against a host of facts" (001-018).

## Criticism

Russell's main critical point is methodological: "there is no enemy to thinking so deadly as a false simplicity" (001-018). The intentionality thesis, he argues, offers an illusory simplicity that obscures the complex, derivative nature of reference. By treating aboutness as primitive, Brentano's view forecloses the analytical work needed to understand how thoughts come to be connected with objects. Russell's alternative — analysing reference in terms of beliefs, habits, and causal connections — is more demanding but, he contends, truer to the empirical facts.
