## Definition

Russell rejects the traditional view of mind as a unitary substance or subject of experience. Instead, he proposes that mind is a logical construction — a way of organizing certain events (sensations, images, beliefs, desires) according to their causal laws. He writes: "the stuff of which the world is made is, in my belief, neither mind nor matter, but something more primitive than either" (001-007).

## Russell's Account

Russell's analysis of mind proceeds by dismantling the traditional marks of the mental. One common characterisation — that mind is distinguished by consciousness — is rejected: "to characterize mind, that one thing is 'consciousness' is not sufficient" (001-008). Russell argues that consciousness is not an essential property of all mental phenomena; many mental processes (habits, mnemic causation, desire) occur without conscious awareness.

A central theme is the contrast between the psychological and the physical: "the distinction between mind and matter" is one that comes "not from the stuff of which they are made but from the kind of causal laws that govern them" (001-031). Physical events obey physical causal laws; mental events obey mnemic laws — causal regularities that depend on past history rather than present stimulus alone.

Russell further analyses mind through its contents. He identifies the "cognitive" elements as central: "the 'cognitive' elements in mind, and it is these that will occupy us most" (001-014). These include sensation, imagery, belief, memory, and desire. He examines each of these components in subsequent lectures, building toward a constructive account in which mind is the set of events arranged according to a particular biography or perspective.

An important aspect of Russell's method is his rejection of the "subject" or "act" in mental life. He writes that "an event that is happening in my mind is different when I think of one object rather than another" (001-019), but this difference lies in the content and relations of the event, not in a special act of a subject.

## Key Distinctions

- **Mind vs. matter**: Not a difference in substance but in causal laws — matter obeys physical causation; mind involves mnemic causation
- **Mind as substance vs. mind as construction**: Russell rejects the Cartesian thinking substance; mind is a collection of events unified by causal and temporal relations
- **Conscious vs. unconscious mental processes**: Russell acknowledges that much mental life is non-conscious, anticipating later developments in cognitive psychology

## Historical Context

Russell's neutral monist analysis of mind draws on William James's radical empiricism, Ernst Mach's neutral elements, and the behaviourist critique of introspection (John B. Watson). His position is a deliberate departure from the dualism of Descartes and the idealism of Bradley and the British Hegelians. The book also engages with Freud's unconscious and the emerging science of experimental psychology.

## Criticism

Russell's constructive account faces the challenge of explaining the unity of consciousness — why mental events belonging to one "biography" cohere in a way that events across biographies do not. His appeal to mnemic causation and temporal continuity may not fully account for the phenomenological unity of experience.
