## Definition

Language, in Russell's behaviourist analysis, is not a system of abstract symbols manipulated by a mind but a set of bodily habits — specifically, the acquired association between hearing or producing a word and the effects that word has on behaviour and imagery. Russell treats language as "a certain language-habit" (001-040), a practical skill learned through exposure and practice, comparable to understanding a game of cricket rather than performing a mental translation (010-017).

## Russell's Account

Russell devotes a substantial portion of Lecture X to the analysis of language and word meaning. His approach is resolutely behaviourist: understanding a word is not a mental act but a disposition to behave in certain ways when the word is encountered. "Understanding language is more like understanding cricket," he writes — it is a practical competence acquired through experience, not the inner grasp of a meaning (010-017). When you hear a sentence in a language you know, "you will seem to have grown a new set of bodily habits," and your behaviour, including your subsequent thoughts and speech, will be shaped by what you heard (008-006).

Russell insists that language, for all its complexity, does not fundamentally alter the nature of thought. "At bottom language does not" introduce a radically new kind of mental operation; it makes cognition "logically more complex" but does not create an entirely new category of mental phenomenon (002-030). This is consistent with his broader project of showing that all mental phenomena can be explained in terms of habits, images, and sensations, without appeal to an irreducible thinking subject.

The core of Russell's account of word meaning is causal and dispositional. A word is understood when its occurrence causes the appropriate effects in the hearer — effects that were established during the process of learning the language. "Understanding language" consists in the fact that "they have acquired" associations while "the language was being learnt" (010-021). These associations are mnemic phenomena: past exposures to the word shape present responses to it. Russell approvingly quotes Watson's account of the genesis of a "true language-habit" in which a child learns to use words not merely as substitutes for objects but as instruments with independent causal power (010-038–039).

Russell identifies several distinct uses of language. There is the "demonstrative" use, where language serves "to point out some feature in the present environment" (010-031). There is the narrative use, to describe or recall a memory-image. And there is the imaginative use, where words are "used to describe or recall a memory-image: to describe it when it already exists, or to recall it when the word exists as a habit and is able to cause the image to occur" (010-044). Words, in Russell's view, can function as tools for calling up images — they do not need to be accompanied by images to be meaningful, but they must have the capacity to produce appropriate effects, whether imaginal or behavioural.

Russell also addresses the relationship between language and generality. "All that is inferable from language is that two instances of a universal" are similar in some respect (011-003). The existence of general words does not, for Russell, imply the existence of abstract universals; it implies only that humans are disposed to respond similarly to similar stimuli. "Men who use language are able to abstract or generalize" in the sense that they respond to common features, but this ability does not require a special mental faculty (011-006). In fact, Russell notes that some thinkers maintain that abstraction "does not need language or sensuous presentations" at all — it may be a more primitive cognitive operation (011-014).

## Key Distinctions

- **Language vs. Thought**: Language makes thought "logically more complex" but does not introduce a fundamentally new category of mental operation (002-030).
- **Understanding vs. Mental Translation**: To understand language is to have acquired dispositions, not to perform an inner act of translation. Russell compares it to understanding cricket — a practical skill, not a mental operation (010-017).
- **Demonstrative vs. Narrative Use**: Language can point to features of the present environment or describe/recollect past and imagined occurrences (010-031, 010-044).
- **Word Meaning vs. Imagery**: A word's meaning is its causal efficacy — its ability to produce appropriate effects — not an accompanying mental image (010-027).

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of language in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) engages critically with Wilhelm Wundt's monumental work on language in his *Völkerpsychologie*, which Russell dismisses as "thrown away" because it fails to explain how language can be understood without invoking mentalistic assumptions (010-005). Russell draws instead on Watson's behaviourist account of language acquisition, adapting it to his neutral-monist framework. His analysis also reflects the influence of his own earlier work on logical form — though here the emphasis is psychological rather than logical. The discussion of language and generality engages with the long-standing philosophical problem of universals, which Russell treats sceptically, reducing general words to habits of response to similarities.
