## Definition

Desire, in Russell's analysis, is a phenomenon that appears prima facie mental but can be reduced to behavioural dispositions and causal laws. Russell proposes that "desire, like force in mechanics, is a convenient fiction for describing certain laws of behaviour" (001-046). He does not deny the experiential reality of desire but seeks to explain it without appealing to an irreducible mental substance — instead treating it as a behavioral pattern governed by causal regularities involving past history and present stimuli.

## Russell's Account

Russell introduces desire in the very first lecture as one of the phenomena "which might be called 'mental,' such as desire and pleasure and pain" (001-014). He approaches it through two complementary lenses: introspective awareness and behavioural observation. While we seem to have direct knowledge of our own desires through introspection, Russell argues that this access is less reliable than commonly supposed — "when we think of our own desires, most people believe that we can know them by introspection, but this is subject to the same limitations as our knowledge of other people's" (001-044).

A central theme in Russell's treatment is the role of unconscious desires, heavily influenced by Freud. He observes that "the importance of 'unconscious' desires in determining our actions and beliefs has been brought very much to the fore by psychoanalysis" (001-052). However, Russell diverges from Freud by reframing unconscious desire not as a repressed mental entity but as a causal disposition: "I believe an 'unconscious' desire is merely a causal law of our behaviour, namely, that we remain restless until something happens which causes us to cease to be restless" (001-053).

Russell also addresses desires we are ashamed to acknowledge — those "we should be ashamed of having" (001-045) — noting the human tendency toward self-deception about what we truly want, and connecting this to the psychoanalytic concept of repression and censorship.

## Key Distinctions

Russell distinguishes:
- **Conscious vs. unconscious desire**: A conscious desire is felt; an unconscious desire is a causal law of behaviour (001-053). The distinction maps onto introspective access, not ontological difference.
- **Desire vs. belief**: Both are propositional attitudes with effects on behaviour, but Russell treats them as "closely analogous" rather than identical (001-058).
- **Desire vs. purpose**: Russell warns against reading teleology into behavioural patterns, preferring mechanistic description wherever possible (003-015).
