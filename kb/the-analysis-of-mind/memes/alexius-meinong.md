[[Alexius Meinong]] (1853--1920) was an Austrian philosopher and a student of [[Franz Brentano]] at the University of Vienna. His most enduring contribution to philosophy is the [[act-content-object]] distinction in the analysis of thought, which [[Bertrand Russell]] engages with extensively in *The Analysis of Mind*. Meinong's [[theory of objects]] (*Gegenstandstheorie*) extended Brentano's [[intentionality]] thesis into a systematic ontology that could accommodate non-existent, impossible, and merely subsistent objects -- a position Russell first championed, then famously rejected through his theory of descriptions.

## Life and Work

Meinong studied under Brentano in Vienna from 1875 to 1878 and later taught at the University of Graz, where he founded the Graz School of experimental psychology and object theory in 1894. His intellectual project was to develop a general theory of objects that would be broader than metaphysics -- a science of everything thinkable, whether existent or not.

Meinong's principle of the "independence of Sosein from Sein" (the independence of an object's properties from its existence) became the defining slogan of his philosophy:

1. Objects can have determinate properties without existing
2. Thought can be directed toward objects that do not exist
3. A science of objects must study the "so-being" of objects regardless of their ontological status
4. The round square is round and square, even though it cannot exist

His major works include *On Assumptions* (1902), *On the Theory of Objects* (1904), and *On the Experiential Foundations of Our Knowledge* (1906) -- the latter cited directly by Russell.

## The Act-Content-Object Distinction

Meinong refined Brentano's intentionality thesis by distinguishing three elements in every mental phenomenon: the act, the content, and the object. Russell introduces this tripartite analysis in Lecture I:

> [001-020] "According to Meinong, we have to distinguish three elements in every presentation, namely, the act, or (as Meinong calls it) the psychical event, the content, and the object."

And he elaborates on the same page:

> [001-019] "His Austrian successor Meinong elaborated the doctrine in a number of works."

The three elements break down as follows:

- **Act** (`Akt`): The psychological event of thinking -- e.g., the act of *judging* that grass is green
- **Content** (`Inhalt`): The way the object is presented -- the "what" that mediates between act and object
- **Object** (`Gegenstand`): The thing thought about -- what the judgment is *about*, whether it exists or not

This tripartite structure allowed Meinong to explain how two people (or the same person at different times) can think about the same object through different acts and different contents. The object remains invariant while the psychological particulars vary.

Russell found this schema powerful enough to adopt it in his own early work. Speaking of the content in particular, he notes in Lecture IX:

> [009-015] "What, if we followed Meinong's terminology, we should call the 'object' of a belief, i.e., what is believed, is a proposition, and propositions may be true or false."

Here Russell uses Meinong's language to articulate his own emerging theory of propositional attitudes.

## Theory of Objects (*Gegenstandstheorie*)

Meinong's theory of objects is his most ambitious philosophical achievement. It holds that there is a realm of objects (*Gegenstaende*) that includes everything that can be thought about, regardless of whether it exists, subsists, or is even possible. The theory recognizes:

- **Concrete existent objects**: tables, chairs, mountains -- objects with *Sein* (being) in space and time
- **Abstract subsistent objects**: numbers, propositions, relations -- objects with *Sein* but not in space and time
- **Non-existent objects**: golden mountains, the present King of France -- objects that *are* objects of thought but lack *Sein*
- **Impossible objects**: round squares, married bachelors -- objects that *are* objects but cannot have *Sein* in any possible world

For Meinong, "there are objects of which it is true that there are no such objects." This paradoxical formulation captures his conviction that the domain of objects outstrips the domain of existents, and that a proper theory of thought must respect this fact.

Russell discusses Meinong's treatment of non-existent objects in Lecture XIII:

> [013-023] "Meinong in his book, 'Ueber die Erfahrungsgrundlagen unseres Wissens,' argues that we may know objects which do not exist, and which are not presented to us."

And later:

> [013-024] "Meinong meets this difficulty by saying that some objects, though they do not exist, have being of another kind, which he calls 'Bestand' or subsistence."

## Meinong's Influence on Russell

Russell's engagement with Meinong was deep and transformative. In *The Principles of Mathematics* (1903), Russell embraced a broadly Meinongian ontology, accepting that every denoting phrase corresponds to an object, including phrases that denote nothing real. Russell even described himself during this period as a "Meinongian."

The break came with "On Denoting" (1905), where Russell deployed the theory of descriptions to eliminate the ontological commitment to non-existent objects. A sentence like "The present King of France is bald" does not require a non-existent object to be meaningful; it is simply a complex quantificational claim. As Russell later reflected in these lectures:

> [001-022] "Meinong's 'act' is the ghost of the subject, or what once was the full-blooded soul."

This metaphor -- the "ghost of the subject" -- captures Russell's final verdict on the Meinongian act. In Russell's neutral monism, the observing subject dissolves into a bundle of sensations and images, and the act becomes a behaviorally explicable event rather than an irreducible mental occurrence. The same analysis applies to Meinong's content and object:

> [001-023] "It is not the direct essential thing that Brentano and Meinong represent it as being, namely the act and the reference to the object."

What Russell retains from Meinong is the recognition that thought has structure, that belief involves a relation to a propositional content, and that any adequate theory of mind must account for the objectivity of thought. What he rejects is the postulation of unobservable mental acts and non-existent objects as theoretical primitives.

## Russell's Criticism

Russell's criticism operates on two fronts: metaphysical parsimony and psychological verifiability.

**Metaphysically**, Russell objects to Meinong's bloated ontology. A theory that populates the world with impossible objects violates the principle of economy. Russell's theory of descriptions and his behavioral account of belief are designed to achieve the same explanatory work with fewer ontological commitments.

**Psychologically**, Russell argues that the "act" is unobservable. In Lecture I he treats it as a relic of the substantial soul:

> [001-022] "Meinong's 'act' is the ghost of the subject, or what once was the full-blooded soul. It is supposed that thoughts cannot just come and go, but need a person to think them."

For Russell, psychology should begin from observable data -- sensations and images -- and construct mental phenomena from these building blocks. The postulation of an "act" simply reifies the subject that neutral monism dissolves.

Despite these criticisms, Meinong's problems remain alive. How thought can be about what does not exist, how two people can think about the same thing, and how false belief is possible -- these are perennial puzzles. Meinong's radical solution, even where rejected, set the terms of debate for the next century of philosophy of mind and language.
