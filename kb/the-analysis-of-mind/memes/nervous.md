Russell repeatedly invokes the [[Nervous System|nervous system]] throughout *The Analysis of Mind* as the physiological foundation for [[Psychology|psychological]] explanation. In his [[Behaviourism|behaviourist]] framework, mental phenomena such as [[Habit|habit]], [[Memory|memory]], and [[Sensation and Images|sensation]] are understood not as irreducibly mental events but as processes dependent upon the properties of nervous tissue. The nervous system serves as the bridge between physical causation and the phenomena traditionally called "mental."

## Russell's Physiological Turn

Russell opens his analysis by positioning psychology firmly within the domain of physiological observation. The nervous system is not merely correlated with mental events — it is the substrate in which those events occur:

> [002-012] …ver that both depend upon the nervous system, and he may find that …

> [002-015] …hat a stimulus applied to the nervous system, like a spark to dynam…

Russell argues that the ordinary person, confronted with the dependence of thought and feeling on the brain, is already "materialistic" in practice. The task of scientific psychology is to follow this dependence systematically, reducing mental phenomena to their nervous correlates wherever possible (002-013).

## Nervous Tissue and Mnemic Causation

A central concept in Russell's analysis is [[Mnemic Causation|mnemic causation]] — the idea that past events can influence present responses through modifications of nervous tissue. This is not a mysterious mental causality but ordinary physical causation operating through altered physiological structures:

> [008-005] …terms of the peculiarities of nervous tissue, and these peculiariti…

> [008-030] …ome condition external to the nervous system itself and operating u…

The peculiarities of nervous tissue explain why a stimulus applied now can produce a response that depends on events long past. The nervous system is modified by experience, and these modifications — rather than an immaterial "mind" — account for the phenomena of learning, habit, and memory (004-017).

## Higher Nervous Centres and Thought

Russell distinguishes between simple reflex arcs and the complex operations of the "higher nervous centres," which he associates with belief, desire, and volition:

> [012-030] …ments as involving the higher nervous centres. I do not like to dis…

The higher nervous centres introduce a degree of complexity and integration that makes the resulting behaviour appear "intelligent" or "purposeful." Yet Russell insists this appearance does not require a non-physical cause — the complexity of nervous organization is sufficient (015-033, 015-040).

## Nervous System and Sense-Organs

The nervous system operates in concert with sense-organs to produce perception. A stimulus external to the nervous system initiates a chain of physiological events:

> [007-023] …ed by stimuli external to the nervous system (or at least to the br…

This stimulus-response arc forms the basis of Russell's account of perception. The sense-organ transduces physical energy, the nervous system conducts and transforms the signal, and the resulting response — whether behaviour or conscious experience — is the end-product of a purely physical chain (005-029, 006-004).

## Physiological Causation vs. Mental Causation

Russell returns repeatedly to the question of whether nervous processes involve a unique form of causation:

> [015-031] …rdinary physical causation in nervous tissue. This is the question …

> [015-040] …rdinary physical causation in nervous (and other) tissue.

His answer is unequivocal: there is no special "vital" or "mental" causation. The laws governing nervous tissue are the same physical and chemical laws that govern all other tissue. The appearance of a special mental causality arises only from the complexity of nervous organization, not from the intrusion of a non-physical agent (015-012, 015-015).

## Role in Neutral Monism

The nervous system plays a foundational role in Russell's [[Neutral Monism|neutral monism]]. If both mind and matter are constructed from the same neutral stuff, then the physiology of the nervous system explains how those constructions come to differ. The special properties of nervous tissue — its sensitivity, conductivity, and modifiability — account for why certain organizations of neutral stuff give rise to what we call "mind," while others give rise to what we call "matter" (009-027, 015-022, 015-027).
