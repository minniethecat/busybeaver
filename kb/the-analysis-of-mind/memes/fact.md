## Definition

A fact, in Russell's epistemology, is an observable occurrence that serves as a datum for inquiry. Russell takes "the apparent fact that we have KNOWLEDGE, that there is a phenomenon called 'knowing' about an object" as the fundamental starting point of his investigation (001-015). Facts are the raw material of both science and philosophy: "so much may be taken as fact, and as a datum for our inquiry" (001-008). Crucially, Russell does not treat facts as belonging to either the mental or the physical realm — they are neutral particulars available to any observer, and the distinction between psychological and physical facts is a matter of the causal laws we apply, not of ontology.

## Russell's Account

Russell's use of "fact" throughout *The Analysis of Mind* is deliberately non-technical and pragmatic. He takes facts to be what is observable and uncontroversial — the shared basis from which theorising begins. Early in Lecture I, he anchors his inquiry in "the fact that we have KNOWLEDGE" and treats this as a datum requiring explanation rather than denial (001-015). The philosopher's task is to account for the facts, not to explain them away.

Russell contrasts facts with inferences. Inferences "which go beyond the observable facts, are to be avoided with the utmost care" in scientific psychology (002-006). The distinction between fact and inference is methodological, not metaphysical: facts are what we can directly observe; inferences are what we hypothesise to explain the facts. This aligns with Russell's broader empiricist commitment to building knowledge from the ground up, with "the sensations through which" all knowledge is ultimately grounded serving as the foundation (015-022).

In Lecture II, Russell emphasises the practical importance of sticking to the facts when studying other minds. We observe "their movements, their physiological processes, and the sounds they emit" — these are facts (002-006). The inference that these movements express mental states similar to our own is a reasonable hypothesis, but it remains an inference, not a fact. Similarly, "the fact that instinct does not require prevision of the biological end which it serves" is an observable regularity, not a teleological explanation (002-043).

Russell's treatment of facts in the concluding lectures reveals the concept's role in his neutral monism. "Throughout these lectures," he writes, "the data of psychology do not differ in kind from the data of physics" (015-016). A fact is a fact regardless of which science studies it. The notion of "data" is "familiar throughout science," but Russell warns against conflating the epistemological concept of data (what is given to a knower) with the psychological concept (what is present in experience) (015-017–018). When Russell speaks of data in the epistemological sense, "I do not mean the things of which we are sure without any special knowledge — I mean the things which, when a science is well advanced, appear as affording grounds for other parts of the science" (015-020).

Facts also play a critical role in Russell's discussion of truth and belief. "Common sense and science hold that there are" facts that exist independently of what anyone believes about them (013-035). A belief is true when it corresponds to a fact, and false when it does not — but Russell is careful to note that this correspondence theory of truth does not require facts to be mysterious entities. A fact is simply what is the case, and what is the case is determined by the causal structure of the world.

## Key Distinctions

- **Fact vs. Inference**: Facts are directly observable; inferences go beyond the observable and are always corrigible (002-006). Much of what we take for knowledge is inferential rather than factual.
- **Fact vs. Belief**: A belief is a psychological state that may or may not correspond to a fact; truth consists in correspondence between belief and fact (013-035).
- **Psychological Data vs. Physical Data**: There is no difference in kind between the data of psychology and the data of physics — both are neutral particulars (015-016).
- **Epistemological Data vs. Psychological Data**: Epistemological data are the foundational propositions of a science; psychological data are the contents of immediate experience (015-017–020).

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of facts reflects the influence of both British empiricism and early analytic philosophy. The concept of facts as truth-makers comes from Russell's own logical atomism, developed with Wittgenstein before 1920, in which the world consists of atomic facts corresponding to true atomic propositions. In *The Analysis of Mind*, however, Russell uses "fact" in a broader, more pragmatic sense that is closer to the scientific usage of "data" — observable phenomena that constrain theory. This usage also reflects the influence of William James's radical empiricism, which took "pure experience" as the neutral foundation of both mind and matter. Russell's emphasis on sticking to observable facts and treating inferences as fallible is a methodological principle that would later become central to logical positivism.
