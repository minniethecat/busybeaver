John Locke (1632–1704) was an English philosopher whose *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* established the empiricist framework that dominated British philosophy for two centuries. In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell invokes Locke at key moments in his discussion of [[memory]], [[images]], and [[general-ideas]], situating Lockean empiricism as a precursor to his own analytical approach while criticizing its limitations.

## Locke's Theory of Ideas

Russell's primary engagement with Locke concerns the empiricist theory of ideas — the view that all mental content derives from experience and can be classified into impressions (from sensation) and ideas (from memory and thought). Russell traces this distinction through the British empiricist tradition:

> [001-012] From memory it is an easy step to what are called "ideas"—not in the Platonic sense, but in that of Locke, Berkeley and Hume, in which they are opposed to "impressions." You may be conscious of a friend either by seeing him or by "thinking" of him; and by "thought" you can be conscious of objects which cannot be seen, such as the human race, or physiology.

Russell's terminology in *The Analysis of Mind* follows this Lockean-Humean framework but with significant modifications. Where Locke and Hume used "idea" to cover a broad range of mental contents, Russell reserves "idea" (or "image") specifically for memory-images and imagination-images — mental contents that are copies of sensations but lack the vivacity and causal immediacy of sense-data. This refinement reflects Russell's behaviourist scruples: he wants a classification of mental phenomena that maps onto actual psychological processes rather than inherited philosophical categories.

## Abstract Ideas and Berkeley's Critique

Locke figures prominently in Russell's lecture on [[general-ideas]] and [[thought]]. Locke had claimed that the mind can form an abstract idea of a triangle in general — a triangle that is "neither Oblique nor Rectangle, neither Equilateral, Equicrural nor Scalenon, but all and none of these at once." Berkeley famously attacked this claim, arguing that we cannot form an image of a triangle without specifying its particular properties:

> [011-004] Locke having maintained that he could form an idea of triangle in general, without deciding what sort of triangle it was to be, Berkeley contended that this was impossible.

Russell largely sides with Berkeley on this point but develops the analysis further. Drawing on the behaviourist concept of word-mediated responses, Russell argues that what appears to be abstract thought is better explained as a learned [[habit]] of responding to words in particular ways. The word "triangle" acts as a stimulus that produces an appropriate response — such as drawing one, or forming a specific visual image — without requiring the mind to entertain a genuinely abstract entity.

## The Empiricist Legacy

Locke's significance for Russell lies in his role as the founder of a tradition that made mental content — rather than logical form or transcendental conditions — the primary object of philosophical analysis. Russell inherits this empiricist orientation while rejecting its atomistic assumptions. For Locke, ideas were simple, atomic units combined by association. For Russell, mental phenomena are complex processes involving [[sensation]], [[habit]], [[mnemic-causation]], and [[language]], none of which can be reduced to simple ideas.

Russell also departs from Locke in his treatment of the [[self]]. Locke's account of personal identity in terms of memory continuity is congenial to Russell's project of dissolving the substantial self. But Russell goes further, arguing that even the continuity of memory does not require a continuing subject — it requires only that present mental events have mnemic causal connections to past mental events.
