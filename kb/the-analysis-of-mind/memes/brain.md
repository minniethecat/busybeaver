## Definition

The brain, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, is the physical organ that serves as the primary locus of the physiological processes underlying mental phenomena. Russell does not offer a detailed neurological account but invokes the brain strategically: wherever a traditional philosopher would posit a mental substance or faculty, Russell appeals to modifications of brain tissue as the sufficient physical basis. The brain is, for Russell, the material anchor that allows him to treat mental phenomena as part of the causal order of nature.

## Russell's Account

Russell's references to the brain are instrumental rather than anatomical. He uses brain-related evidence to support three core arguments.

**(1) Brain and mental disorder.** Russell notes that "when a man's delusions are cured, for instance, by drugs, it is natural to suppose that the cause of the delusions is physical, and the cure is effected by a physical remedy" (001-048). The fact that "those who treat delusions look for a physical defect in the brain" (001-048) and that "insanity may therefore be caused by physical defects" — "as caused by defects in the brain" (001-050) — supports the view that mental phenomena are not autonomous but grounded in physical processes.

**(2) Brain and memory.** The most sustained brain-related argument concerns memory. Russell observes that "there are purely physical laws connecting a fact with the memory of it" and that the "connection of brain-lesions with loss of memory affords grounds for this hypothesis" (004-021). If damage to specific brain regions can destroy specific memories, then memory must be "embodied in modifications of the brain and nerves" (004-031). Russell makes the point vivid: "No one can be expected to see differences between the brain of a man who speaks English and the brain of a man who speaks French" (004-034) — yet those differences must exist, and they constitute the physical basis of linguistic memory.

**(3) Brain and habit.** Russell treats habit as "embodied in modifications of the brain and nerves" (004-031). When an organism acquires a habit, "the effect of the past event on the brain, making it a different instrument" (015-013) is the physiological reality behind the psychological description. Learning, on this view, is "modifying the structure of the brain, not directly" — but indirectly, through experience — and these modifications are the physical reality of what we call dispositions to behave (004-003).

**(4) Brain and sensation.** Russell distinguishes sensations (which have stimuli external to the nervous system) from images (which arise from within): "When a mental occurrence can be regarded as an appearance of an object external to the brain, however irregular... we can account for it causally by the stimulus from the object" (007-031). But some mental occurrences "have no stimulus external to the brain" (007-031) — these are images, and their causes "belong to the nerves and brain, being habits which they have acquired" (010-021).

**(5) Brain in comparative psychology.** Russell extends brain-based analysis across species: "where the higher animals are concerned," mental phenomena involve processes in the brain rather than in the peripheral nervous system alone (006-004). Even emotional expressions like rage and fear "gain expression without the brain" in decorticate animals (014-008), suggesting a layered organization where lower functions are distributed and higher functions are cortically dependent.

## Key Distinctions

- **Brain vs. Mind**: The brain is a physical organ; "mind" is a logical construction out of brain-involving events. Russell denies any non-physical mental substance.
- **Brain vs. Nervous System**: Russell sometimes uses "brain" loosely for the entire central nervous system; "the nerves and brain" (004-031) are treated as a continuous physiological substrate.
- **Brain causes vs. External causes of sensation**: Sensations have causes "external to the brain" (007-031); images and memories have causes internal to it.

## Historical Context

Russell's brain-centered approach to mind reflects the early-20th-century convergence of neurology and psychology. The discovery of localized brain functions (Broca, Wernicke), the study of memory loss through lesions (Ribot, Semon), and the emerging field of neurophysiology (Sherrington) all informed Russell's naturalism. Russell was not himself a neurologist, but he drew on the scientific consensus of his time to argue that mental phenomena are continuous with physical processes — a position that set him against both idealist philosophers and dualist psychologists. His invocation of brain evidence was strategic: if mental phenomena have discoverable physical causes, then the postulate of a non-physical mind is scientifically superfluous.
