## Overview

Analysis is the defining method of [[Bertrand Russell]]'s philosophical project. In *The Analysis of Mind*, analysis serves as the primary tool for clarifying psychological concepts by decomposing them into simpler constituents and examining their logical relations. The book's title itself announces this commitment: the mind, like [[matter]], is to be understood not through metaphysical speculation but through patient logical analysis.

Russell applies analysis to dissolve the apparent unity of [[consciousness]], to distinguish components within [[belief]], to separate [[memory]] from mere [[association]], and to clarify the relationship between [[sensation-image|sensation and images]]. In each case, the goal is not reduction but clarification.

> [001-004] Direct reasons, derived from analysis and its difficulties…

The difficulties of analysis are themselves instructive. When a concept resists decomposition — as consciousness long did — that resistance reveals something about its nature.

## Analysis vs. Synthesis

Russell contrasts philosophical analysis with rival approaches that emphasize synthesis or speculative system-building:

| Approach | Method | Example |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Analysis (Russell) | Decompose into simples | Breaking "belief" into propositional attitude + content |
| Synthesis (Idealism) | Build wholes from relations | [[idealism|Idealist]] monism of [[Francis Herbert Bradley\|Bradley]] |
| Psychoanalysis (Freud) | Uncover hidden motives | [[Sigmund Freud\|Freud's]] interpretation of [[dream\|dreams]] |
| Introspectionism | Direct self-observation | [[William James\|James']] stream of consciousness |

> [002-005] But when we come to the analysis and scientific understanding…

Russell's analysis differs from [[psychoanalysis]] in crucial respects. While psychoanalysis seeks hidden affective causes, Russell's analysis seeks logical structure. Both are "analytic," but they operate at different levels.

## Applications in The Analysis of Mind

### Dissolving Consciousness

The most striking application of Russell's analytic method is his treatment of [[consciousness]]. Rather than accepting consciousness as a fundamental mental substance, he analyzes it into a relation between a subject and an [[object]]. By questioning whether this relation exists at all, he opens the door to [[neutral-monism]].

> [006-006] …seems to me one which demands analysis of a somewhat destructive character…

### Decomposing Belief

[[Belief]] appears as a unified mental state, but analysis reveals it as a complex involving propositional content, an attitude (affirmation or denial), and behavioral dispositions. This decomposition allows Russell to treat belief naturalistically while respecting its logical structure.

### Clarifying Memory

> [001-011] …incidentally when we come to the analysis of memory.

[[Memory]] is distinguished from mere [[habit]] or [[association]] through analysis of its temporal reference. Memory-knowledge points to past events; mere behavioral repetition does not. Analysis makes this distinction explicit.

### Separating Components of Desire

> [003-032] We may now summarize our analysis of desire and feeling.

[[Desire-feeling|Desire]] is analyzed into a behavioral cycle (uneasiness → action → quiescence) plus a feeling-tone. This analysis allows Russell to treat desire in behaviorist terms while acknowledging the phenomenological residue that [[John B. Watson|Watson's]] strict behaviourism ignores.

## The Scope and Limits of Analysis

Russell is clear about what analysis can and cannot do:

- **Can**: Clarify concepts, expose logical relations, dissolve pseudo-problems born of linguistic confusion
- **Cannot**: Generate new empirical facts, replace experimental psychology, settle questions that require physiological investigation

The analysis of mind is philosophical, not physiological. It clarifies what we mean by mental terms; it does not reveal the neural mechanisms underlying mental processes. That latter task belongs to [[physiology]] and [[psychology]].
