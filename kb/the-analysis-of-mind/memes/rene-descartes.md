René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist whose [[dualism|dualistic]] account of mind and matter set the terms for centuries of philosophical debate. In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell engages Descartes as the paradigmatic opponent — the philosopher whose sharp separation between mental and physical substance created a problem that Russell's [[neutral-monism]] is designed to dissolve.

## Cartesian Dualism and Its Problems

Russell's most extended reference to Descartes appears in his discussion of the mind-body problem. Descartes held that mind (thinking substance) and matter (extended substance) are fundamentally different kinds of thing, making any causal interaction between them impossible. Russell summarizes the historical position:

> [001-050] The followers of Descartes held that mind and matter are so different as to make any action of the one on the other impossible. When I will to move my arm, they said, it is not my will that operates on my arm, but God, who, by His omnipotence, moves my arm whenever I want it moved.

The Cartesian solution — occasionalism, in which God mediates every apparent mind-body interaction — struck Russell as an admission of failure. If the only way to explain how a desire to raise one's arm results in the arm's rising is to invoke divine intervention, then the initial metaphysical premises must be wrong.

## Psycho-Physical Parallelism as Cartesian Legacy

Russell traces a direct line from Descartes's substance dualism to the modern doctrine of psycho-physical parallelism, which holds that mental and physical events run in parallel without causal interaction. He regards this as merely a secularized version of the Cartesian occasionalist position:

> [001-050] The modern doctrine of psychophysical parallelism is not appreciably different from this theory of the Cartesian school. Psycho-physical parallelism is the theory that mental and physical events each have causes in their own sphere, but run on side by side owing to the fact that every state of the brain coexists with a definite state of the mind, and vice versa.

Russell rejects this parallelism as having "no basis except in metaphysical theory" and as "very difficult to harmonize with obvious facts." His counterexample is deliberately ordinary: receiving a dinner invitation. The letter (a physical fact) causes a mental apprehension, which in turn causes a physical action (going to the right place at the right time). These everyday causal chains refute any theory that denies mind-body interaction.

## Neutral Monism as the Solution

Russell's alternative to Cartesian dualism is [[neutral-monism]] — the view that the fundamental constituents of reality are neither mental nor physical but neutral, and that minds and material objects are different logical constructions out of the same neutral stuff. From this perspective, the Cartesian problem dissolves:

> [001-050] Our world is to be constructed out of what the American realists call "neutral" entities, which have neither the hardness and indestructibility of matter, nor the reference to objects which is supposed to characterize mind.

Russell's neutral monism is designed precisely to avoid the impasse Descartes created. If sensations are the common ground of mental and physical descriptions, then the question of how mind can act on matter — or matter on mind — becomes a question about different causal laws governing the same underlying events under different descriptions, not about interaction between two incommensurable substances.

## The Cartesian Legacy in Russell's Thought

Descartes's influence on Russell extends beyond the mind-body problem. The Cartesian method of systematic doubt, the quest for certainty, and the privileging of first-person knowledge all inform Russell's approach — but Russell inverts each of these Cartesian commitments. Where Descartes sought certainty in the *cogito*, Russell finds only a stream of sensations, images, and bodily feelings. Where Descartes posited the thinking self as a substance, Russell argues that the [[self]] is a logical fiction. And where Descartes separated mind from matter, Russell seeks to show that "matter is not so material and mind not so mental as is generally supposed."

Russell's engagement with Descartes thus exemplifies his larger philosophical project: taking the problems inherited from the philosophical tradition and showing that they dissolve when we abandon the metaphysical assumptions that generated them. The Cartesian dualism that structured modern philosophy is, for Russell, not a problem to be solved but a framework to be abandoned.
