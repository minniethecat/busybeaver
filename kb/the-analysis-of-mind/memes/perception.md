## Definition

Perception, in Russell's neutral monism, is the actual lived experience in which a sensation — the non-mnemic core — is inseparably fused with contributions from past experience. Russell defines perception physically as "the appearance of the object from a place where there is a brain (or, in lower animals, some suitable nervous structure), with sense-organs and nerves forming part of the intervening medium" (007-014). Crucially, perception is not a precise notion: "we perceive things more or less, but always with a very considerable amount of vagueness and confusion" (007-029).

## Russell's Account

Russell develops his theory of perception in Lecture VII as a bridge between the physical and psychological standpoints. Before one can define either sensations or images, "it is necessary to consider this twofold classification in somewhat greater detail, and to derive from it a definition of perception" (007-001). The problem is that every particular relevant to physics is associated with two places — an active place (the object) and a passive place (the percipient): "in the case of a perception or photograph of a star, the active place is the place where the star is, while the passive place is the place where the percipient or photographic plate is" (007-012). This dualism, Russell insists, has nothing to do with any mind — "it exists in exactly the same sense if I am replaced by a photographic plate" (007-012).

Perceptions are distinguished from mere physical appearances by two mnemic characteristics: "(1) They give rise to mnemic phenomena; (2) They are themselves affected by mnemic phenomena" (007-015–016). That is, perceptions may be remembered, associated, influence habits, or give rise to images; and they are themselves different from what they would have been without past experience — as when a spoken sentence's effect depends on whether the hearer knows the language (007-017).

The core of Russell's analysis is the distinction between perception and sensation. "Theoretically, though often not practically, we can, in our perception of an object, separate the part which is due to past experience from the part which proceeds without mnemic influences out of the character of the object" (007-018). Russell defines sensation as the non-mnemic part, and perception as sensation plus mnemic additions: "the sensation is a theoretical core in the actual experience; the actual experience is the perception" (007-018). This means pure sensation is an analytical abstraction — what we actually experience in daily life is always perception, never raw sensation alone.

Russell further argues that all actual perception is "confused" to some degree, because the particular in question may be "a blend of two or more" objects (007-029). The notion of perception is inherently vague: a particular may count as a perception of an object when "it can be regarded as an appearance of an object external to the brain, however irregular, or even as a confused appearance of several such objects" (007-031). When a mental occurrence lacks sufficient connection to external objects, it cannot be called a perception. "But the distinction is one of degree, not of kind. Until this is realized, no satisfactory theory of perception, sensation, or imagination is possible" (007-031).

## Key Distinctions

- **Perception vs. Sensation**: Sensation is the theoretical non-mnemic core; perception is the actual experience, always shaped by past experience and habitual associations.
- **Perception vs. Image**: Images are centrally excited (their causes are in the brain); perceptions have an external stimulus traceable to objects outside the brain.
- **Active vs. Passive Place**: Every particular has both — the star's place (active) and the percipient's place (passive). This dualism is physical, not mental.
- **Regular vs. Irregular Appearances**: Regular appearances belong directly to the object's system; irregular ones involve the distorting influence of the intervening medium.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of perception in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) is part of his broader neutral monist project. By analysing perception in terms of physical appearances and mnemic causation, he eliminates the need for a "subject" or "act" of perceiving. The theory draws on Einstein's relativity (through the distinction between active and passive places), on Watson's behaviourism (external stimulus analysis), and on Mach's neutral elements. It represents Russell's attempt to give perception a fully scientific treatment without invoking the traditional apparatus of mind.
