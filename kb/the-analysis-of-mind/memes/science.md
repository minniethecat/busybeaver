## Definition

Science, in Russell's framework, is the methodical study of causal regularities in observable phenomena. Russell identifies physics as "the most fundamental science at present in existence" (P01-001), but he insists that psychology too must be conducted as a science: "Psychology as a science" is concerned with those regularities that involve mnemic causation — the influence of past experience on present occurrences — whereas physics abstracts away from such causal lines (001-038). The distinction between sciences is one of causal laws, not of subject matter: "everything that we hear or seen belongs equally to psychology and to physics" (001-037).

## Russell's Account

Russell's conception of science is pragmatic and anti-metaphysical. He is less interested in demarcating science from philosophy than in ensuring that psychology adopts the methodological standards of the natural sciences. His opening remarks in *The Analysis of Mind* identify "two different tendencies, one in psychology, the other in physics" — the psychological tendency, materialistic, seeks to explain mind in observable terms; the physical tendency, paradoxically anti-materialistic, derives matter from sense-data (P01-001). Russell's project is to reconcile these by showing that "a science of the mind" is possible without either reducing mind to matter or treating matter as a mere construct of mind.

Russell emphasises that science works with approximations and idealisations. Causal laws "have a definite place in science, in spite of not being exact" in the sense of unbreakable uniformities (005-006). The notion of cause itself "is one which modern science shows to be fundamentally erroneous" when understood as a necessary connection between events; science offers only functional relations and statistical regularities (005-001). "Science normally does" assume the continuity of causal processes, but this is a working hypothesis, not a metaphysical commitment (005-012).

In Lecture IV, Russell addresses the relationship between science and teleology. The study of living organisms introduces a different kind of causal law — mnemic causation — "that distinguishes sciences dealing with living organisms from physics" (004-015). But this is not a concession to vitalism: mnemic causation is still causation, and the study of it is still science. Russell insists that to ask "why" such causal sequences exist "is to ask a question which science cannot answer" — science can only describe the laws, not provide ultimate explanations (004-030).

Russell's scientific method extends to the study of introspection itself. "In the actual condition of science," some phenomena "cannot be brought under the causal laws" that govern publicly observable events, but this is a practical limitation, not a principled one (006-020). Private observations are legitimate data for science, just as in physical science, but they must be treated as corrigible and subject to cross-validation (006-028). "For purposes of science," Russell concludes, "it is justified practically" to assume the reality of the external world even if philosophical doubt cannot be fully dispelled (007-022).

In the concluding lecture, Russell returns to the theme of science as a unified enterprise. Psychology must be "a separate science" only in the sense that it attends to a distinct set of causal laws, not because its subject matter is ontologically distinct (015-013). In "the ideal structure of science," the data of psychology would be expressed in the same neutral language as the data of physics, with "the sensations through which" all knowledge is ultimately grounded serving as the common basis (015-022).

## Key Distinctions

- **Science vs. Philosophy**: Science deals with observable regularities and causal laws; philosophy, for Russell, clarifies concepts and integrates scientific findings, but the boundary is porous (001-036).
- **Physics vs. Psychology as Sciences**: Physics studies causal laws that abstract from mnemic influences; psychology studies causal laws that include the effects of past experience on present occurrences (001-038).
- **Exact vs. Approximate Laws**: Science does not demand exact universal laws; statistical regularities and approximations are scientifically legitimate (005-006).
- **Science vs. Metaphysics**: Science describes what happens and the laws governing it; it does not answer ultimate "why" questions about the nature of causation itself (004-030–031).

## Historical Context

Russell wrote *The Analysis of Mind* at a time when psychology was struggling to establish itself as an independent science distinct from philosophy. John B. Watson's behaviourism, published in 1913–1919, offered a model of psychology as an objective science based on publicly observable behaviour. Russell embraced much of this programme but resisted its eliminativism about mental content. He was also influenced by the physics of Einstein and the philosophy of Mach, both of which stressed the constructed nature of scientific concepts. Russell's vision of a unified science grounded in neutral particulars reflects his broader ambition — inherited from Leibniz and developed with Whitehead — of a single logical language for all scientific knowledge.
