Richard Semon (1859–1918) was a German zoologist and evolutionary biologist whose theory of mnemic causation, developed in *Die Mneme* (1904) and *Die mnemischen Empfindungen* (1909), serves as a crucial theoretical resource in *The Analysis of Mind*. Russell adopts Semon's terminology and conceptual framework — notably the "engram" and the laws of engraphy and ekphory — as the basis for his analysis of [[memory]], [[habit]], and the influence of past experience on present behaviour.

## Mnemic Phenomena

Russell introduces the concept of mnemic phenomena directly from Semon's work. A mnemic phenomenon is one whose proximate cause includes past events in the history of the organism, not merely present stimuli:

> [004-005] Following a suggestion derived from Semon ("Die Mneme," Leipzig, 1904; 2nd edition, 1908, English translation, Allen & Unwin, 1921; "Die mnemischen Empfindungen," Leipzig, 1909), we will give the name of "mnemic phenomena" to those responses of an organism which, so far as hitherto observed facts are concerned, can only be brought under causal laws by including past occurrences in the history of the organism as part of the causes of the present response.

Russell distinguishes mnemic causation from ordinary causal chains. In ordinary causation, the proximate cause of an event is entirely present at the time the event occurs. In mnemic causation, a past event must be cited as part of the cause. The smell of peat-smoke, for example, will cause a recollection in someone who has previously experienced it, but not in someone who has not — even though no observable brain-structural difference accounts for this.

## The Engram and Mnemic Principles

Russell provides a detailed summary of Semon's theoretical apparatus. The central concept is the "engram" — a hypothetical material modification in the organism that serves as the persistent trace of experience:

> [004-018] When two stimuli occur together, one of them, occurring afterwards, may call out the reaction for the other also. We call this an "ekphoric influence," and stimuli having this character are called "ekphoric stimuli."

Semon formulates two fundamental laws. The Law of Engraphy states that all simultaneous excitements in an organism form a connected complex that leaves behind a connected engram-complex (004-019). The Law of Ekphory states that the partial return of the original energetic situation operates ekphorically on the associated engram-complex — that is, a partial recurrence of the original stimulus tends to arouse the whole of the original reaction.

> [004-019] Semon formulates two "mnemic principles." The first, or "Law of Engraphy," is as follows: "All simultaneous excitements in an organism form a connected simultaneous excitement-complex, which as such works engraphically, i.e. leaves behind a connected engram-complex."

## Russell's Assessment

Russell's treatment of Semon is not uncritical. While he adopts Semon's descriptive framework, he notes that the engram remains "hypothetical, invoked for theoretical uses, and not an outcome of direct observation" (004-021). The observable facts are captured in the mnemic law itself — that the recurrence of part of a stimulus complex tends to arouse the whole of the original response — without requiring commitment to the existence of engrams.

Moreover, Russell extends Semon's insights in a direction Semon himself might not have anticipated. He observes that mnemic phenomena occur in physiology and even in plants — not exclusively in psychology — which suggests that the influence of past experience is a general biological phenomenon rather than a uniquely mental one:

> [004-032] Mnemic phenomena are just as much to be found in physiology as in psychology. They are even to be found in plants, as Sir Francis Darwin pointed out (cf. Semon, "Die Mneme," 2nd edition, p. 28 n.). Habit is a characteristic of the body at least as much as of the mind.

This observation supports Russell's [[neutral-monism|neutral monist]] framework: if mnemic causation operates across the mind–body divide, then mind and matter are best understood as different organizations of the same neutral stuff rather than fundamentally distinct substances.

Semon's work also provides Russell with concrete illustrations. Russell cites Semon's account of hunters deceiving stags by imitating mating calls, noting that older stags become progressively harder to deceive — a case of [[instinct]] growing wiser through accumulated experience (002-037).
