# Universals

## Definition

Universals are general concepts or properties — such as whiteness, justice, or triangularity — that apply across multiple particular instances. In Russell's analysis, the problem of universals is recast in psychological terms: how does the mind come to entertain general ideas, and what is the nature of our consciousness of the universal (011-016)?

## Russell's Account

Russell's treatment of universals in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) reflects his turn away from the Platonic realism of his earlier work. In *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912), he had defended the independent reality of universals.

By 1921, influenced by behaviourism and the neutral monist framework, he approaches universals as a problem about mental processes — specifically, about how general ideas are formed through [[Abstraction]] and made available through language.

The key mechanism is the use of general signs. Russell argues that the capacity to deal with universals depends on the use of words and other signs that apply indifferently to many particular instances (011-006).

A child learns the word "dog" not by grasping a universal dog-ness but by being conditioned to respond with that word to a range of similar but distinct particular animals. The sign is general; the mental process it enables is what we call thinking about universals.

Russell is attentive to the psychology of abstraction. Two instances of a universal, even when they differ greatly in their particular features, can trigger the same linguistic response (011-003). This sameness of response — not any mystical apprehension of a Platonic form — is what constitutes our grasp of the universal.

Abstraction is a behavioural and cognitive process, not a special faculty of intellectual intuition.

The question "whether there is a universal, called whiteness" is thus transformed (010-014). Instead of asking about the metaphysical status of whiteness, Russell asks about the psychological and linguistic mechanisms by which we group diverse particular sensations under a common term. The universal, on this account, is not an entity but a way of responding.

Russell also considers the role of language in making universals possible. A particular sensation or image is always of a specific shade, a specific shape, a specific moment. It becomes universal when it exists alongside a linguistic habit that treats it as representative of a class (011-017). The word supplies the generality that the image alone lacks.

## Key Distinctions

**Universals vs. Particulars.** [[Particulars]] are the unique, unrepeatable data of sensation — this patch of red, that sound, that momentary pain. Universals are what allow us to group particulars into kinds.

Russell's neutral monism complicates this distinction: if the ultimate stuff of the world is neutral particulars (sensations and images), then universals must be explained as constructions from particulars, not as additional entities.

**Universals and Images.** An image is always of a particular — a specific remembered face, a specific imagined sound. Yet images can function as general signs when they are used to stand for a class.

Russell suggests that a vague or schematic image of a dog may serve as a general sign for dogs, even though it remains, in its intrinsic character, a particular mental occurrence (011-017).

**Realism vs. Nominalism.** Traditionally, the problem of universals pitted realists (who held universals to be real entities) against nominalists (who held them to be mere names).

Russell's 1921 position is a sophisticated nominalism grounded in psychology and linguistics: general terms are real and important, but their generality comes from their use, not from their reference to abstract entities.

## Historical Context

The problem of universals has been central to Western philosophy since Plato and Aristotle. Russell's early work (especially *The Problems of Philosophy*, 1912) defended a form of Platonic realism about universals.

His 1921 treatment represents a significant shift toward nominalism and psychologism, influenced by his engagement with behaviourist psychology and the linguistic turn in philosophy.

This shift was not Russell's alone. The early twentieth century saw a widespread revolt against idealism and Platonism in Anglophone philosophy. Russell's neutral monism, combined with his behaviourist account of language, provided a naturalistic alternative to the traditional metaphysics of universals.

The position anticipates, in important respects, the later Wittgenstein's treatment of family resemblances and rule-following.

Russell's analysis of universals also engaged with the empiricist tradition. Hume had famously denied the existence of abstract ideas, arguing that what we call a general idea is really a particular idea associated with a general term.

Russell accepted the broad outlines of this account while providing a more detailed psychological mechanism — the behaviour-cycle of linguistic habit.
