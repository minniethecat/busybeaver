## Definition

A proposition is "a series of words (or sometimes a single word) expressing the kind of thing that can be asserted or denied" (012-022). Russell, however, extends the term beyond its usual logical sense. He argues that "we may extend the term 'proposition' so as to cover the image-contents of beliefs consisting of images" (012-023). On this expanded view, propositions are not exclusively linguistic; they are the content-vehicles of belief, whether expressed in words or constituted by interrelated images.

## Russell's Account

**Image-Propositions.** Russell contends that image-propositions are more fundamental than word-propositions: "It would seem that image-propositions are more primitive than word-propositions, and may well ante-date language" (012-024). A memory-image of a room with the window to the left of the door, when accompanied by the belief-feeling, constitutes a proposition whose content is the spatial arrangement depicted — without any words being involved.

**Belief and Consideration.** A central feature of propositions is that they are neutral between belief and mere consideration: "a proposition can be either believed or merely considered, and... the content is the same in both cases" (012-034). Russell gives the homely example: "We can expect an egg for breakfast, or merely entertain the supposition that there may be an egg for breakfast" (012-034). Since believing and considering have different effects on bodily movement, there must be some intrinsic difference between them — but it is not a difference in propositional content.

**Word-Propositions.** When a proposition is expressed in words, the words function as vehicles for the belief-content. The sentence "that all men are mortal" expresses a proposition, but so does the image of a specific mortal man. Russell's analysis thus decouples propositional content from linguistic form, anticipating later developments in cognitive science and the philosophy of mind.

**Relation to Logic.** Russell notes the connection to logic: "The content of a belief, when expressed in words, is the same thing (or very nearly the same thing) as what in logic is called a 'proposition'" (012-022). However, his psychological approach treats propositions primarily as mental occurrences — the actual contents before the mind when one believes or considers — rather than as abstract logical entities.

## Key Distinctions

- **Image-proposition vs. word-proposition**: The former is more primitive and may predate language; the latter is a later, more abstract development (012-024).
- **Proposition vs. belief**: The proposition is the *content*; belief is the *attitude* toward that content. The same proposition ("there will be an egg for breakfast") can be believed, disbelieved, or merely considered.
- **Proposition vs. judgment**: A judgment is the act of affirming a proposition; the proposition is what is affirmed.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of propositions in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) reflects his move away from the realist view of propositions he had held earlier (in *The Principles of Mathematics*, 1903) toward a more psychological, naturalistic account. This shift parallels his broader transition from Platonic realism to neutral monism. The idea that image-contents can serve as propositional vehicles was innovative and influenced later theories of non-linguistic thought.
