## Definition

Belief is, for Russell, "the central problem in the analysis of mind" (012-001). It is the most "mental" thing we do, seemingly the most remote from mere matter. A belief is a present occurrence in the believer, involving a complex content (what is believed) and standing in a relation of truth or falsehood to an "objective" — a fact outside the belief itself. Russell's analysis of belief is the pivot of his entire project: if belief can be analysed without positing consciousness as an entity, the case for neutral monism is made.

## Russell's Account

Russell structures his analysis around five requisites that any adequate theory of belief must satisfy:

**(1) Truth and falsehood as reference to fact.** "Just as words are characterized by meaning, so beliefs are characterized by truth or falsehood" (012-003). Truth or falsehood consists in relation to something outside the belief — what Russell calls its "objective" or "fact." "What makes a belief true or false I call a 'fact'" (012-003).

**(2) The believing vs. what is believed.** The act of believing is a constant character — whether one believes that Columbus crossed the Atlantic or that two and two are four, "the believing is just the same" (012-004). What differs is the content. Russell distinguishes memory-belief, expectation, and bare assent as forms of belief sharing the same feeling character.

**(3) Present occurrence.** "What is believed, and the believing, must both consist of present occurrences in the believer, no matter what may be the objective of the belief" (012-006). When you believe "Caesar crossed the Rubicon," the event is long past, but the believing and its content are happening now.

**(4) The gulf between content and objective.** There can be a wide gap between the content (present words or images) and the objective (a historical event, a mathematical truth). This gap, Russell notes, may initially make us sceptical about how beliefs can refer to anything beyond themselves (012-007).

**(5) Meaning as the bridge.** The objective reference of a belief depends on the fact that the constituents of its content have meaning. "If I say 'Caesar conquered Gaul,' a person who knows the meaning of the three words... knows as much as can be known about the nature of the objective" (012-008).

Russell further analyses the content of belief as always complex: "We believe that a certain thing has a certain property, or a certain relation to a certain other thing" (012-010). Even apparently simple beliefs like "God exists" are complex — what is believed is *that God exists*. A belief's content always involves at least one word or image, and may include sensations as constituents (012-013). For example, in recognition ("this is a tram"), a present sensation (the noise) is combined with a word or image ("tram") in a specific relation.

The analysis of belief parallels Meinong's act-content-object schema, but Russell argues the parallel is only superficial: "The objections to the act (in the case of presentations) are not valid against the believing in the case of beliefs, because the believing is an actual experienced feeling, not something postulated" (012-005). The believing — the feeling of assent — is an empirically discoverable constituent of experience.

## Key Distinctions

- **Believing vs. What is Believed**: The feeling of assent is constant across beliefs; the content varies.
- **Content vs. Objective**: The content is present and mental; the objective is (often) remote and factual.
- **Belief vs. Memory-Belief**: Memory-belief involves the additional feeling of pastness and is one species of belief.
- **Belief vs. Sensation**: Sensation is non-cognitive; belief is essentially cognitive and propositional.

## Historical Context

Russell's analysis of belief in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) builds on his earlier logical work but takes a psychological turn. The problem had occupied him since *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912) and *Theory of Knowledge* (1913 manuscript). The present treatment reflects his post-Wittgenstein turn toward a more behaviourist-influenced, psychologically-grounded approach, while retaining the logical rigour of his earlier analyses of propositions.
