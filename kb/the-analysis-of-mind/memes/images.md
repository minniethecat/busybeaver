## Definition

Images, in Russell's terminology, are mental contents distinguished from sensations by their physiological causation: sensations are "peripherally excited" (caused by stimulation of the sense-organs), while images are "centrally excited" — caused by processes within the brain without corresponding sensory stimulation (007-023). Russell adopts but modifies this distinction, noting that "all our store of images and memories, all our mental habits, are due to mnemic phenomena" (004-029). Images, along with sensations, constitute the "stuff" of mental life: "sensations (including images) supply all the 'stuff' of the mind" (003-020).

## Russell's Account

Russell devotes the entirety of Lecture VII to defining sensations and images, treating their distinction as foundational to his whole project. He rejects attempts to distinguish images from sensations by intrinsic properties such as faintness or vagueness, arguing instead for a causal criterion. "As regards privacy, all images, of whatever sort, belong with the mental world" (006-022) — but this privacy is not metaphysical; it flows from their central causation.

Russell engages with the behaviourist challenge to images. He notes that some behaviourists "go so far as to deny the existence of images" (006-015), but he demurs: "I think that they have been too hasty in this denial" (006-015). Knight Dunlap, for instance, "contends that images are really muscular contractions" (006-025), a view Russell finds insightful about the physiological correlates of imaging but insufficient to eliminate images as a distinct category of mental content.

The theoretical role of images in Russell's system is substantial: the mind "consists wholly of sensations and images" (006-004). Consciousness itself "practically consists of awareness of 'images,' visceral sensations, and sensations of the special senses" (006-014). Without images, Russell's account of memory, imagination, belief, and thought would collapse, since all these higher mental functions involve centrally excited content.

## Key Distinctions

- **Images vs Sensations**: Sensations are peripherally excited (caused by sense-organ stimulation); images are centrally excited (caused by brain processes) (007-023). This is a causal, not intrinsic, distinction.
- **Memory-Images vs Imagination-Images**: These "do not differ in their intrinsic nature" (009-037) but are distinguished by accompanying feelings — memory-images carry a feeling of pastness and a belief-feeling, while imagination-images do not (011-010).
- **Images vs Muscular Contractions**: Russell considers Dunlap's view that images reduce to muscular activity, accepting the correlation while maintaining that images are a genuine category of mental content (006-025).

## Historical Context

The sensation-image distinction was crucial to British empiricism: Hume distinguished impressions from ideas partly by vividness, and the associationist tradition relied heavily on the concept of mental imagery. Russell inherits this tradition but reworks the distinction in physiological terms, drawing on the emerging neuroscience of his day. The behaviourist critique of images — that they are unobservable and therefore unscientific — posed a serious challenge that Russell attempts to meet by granting the physiological correlates of imaging while insisting on the indispensability of images for psychological explanation.

## Criticism

Russell's causal criterion for distinguishing images from sensations faces a difficulty he acknowledges: in actual experience, the boundary between peripheral and central excitation is blurry. "Images, in the actual condition of experience, are often hard to distinguish from faint sensations" (006-020). The causal criterion, while theoretically clean, is not always applicable in introspective practice. Furthermore, Russell's insistence on images as real mental entities sits in tension with his neutral monism: if mind and matter are both constructed from neutral particulars, the sensation-image distinction should, in principle, be replaceable by a purely causal description.
