Materialism, as Russell discusses it in *The Analysis of Mind*, operates in two distinguishable senses: an old-fashioned metaphysical doctrine about the primacy of matter, and a methodological commitment to explaining mental phenomena through physical causation. Russell treats neither sense as tenable in full, but he acknowledges the evidential pull of the methodological form while ultimately proposing [[neutral-monism]] as a resolution.

## Two Senses of Materialism

Russell identifies a crucial ambiguity in the term. In its metaphysical sense, materialism is the doctrine that matter is the ultimate reality and mind merely a property of certain kinds of organised matter. Russell defines this position alongside its traditional opponent in his opening lecture on the mind–matter distinction:

> [001-006] Those who argue that matter is the reality and mind a mere property of protoplasm are called "materialists." They have been rare among philosophers, but common, at certain periods, among men of science. Idealists, materialists, and ordinary mortals have been in agreement on one point: that they knew sufficiently what they meant by the words "mind" and "matter" to be able to conduct their debate intelligently.

Russell's criticism of both camps is that they share a mistaken premise: the assumption that "mind" and "matter" are clear, well-defined terms capable of supporting the debate. In a second, more nuanced sense, materialism refers to the view that all mental phenomena are causally dependent upon physical phenomena:

> [015-031] One sense of materialism is the view that all mental phenomena are causally dependent upon physical phenomena in the above-defined sense of causal dependence. Whether this is the case or not, I do not profess to know.

On this causal question, Russell provisionally leans toward the materialistic answer, while reserving judgment about the ultimate status of mnemic causation.

## Materialism and Modern Physics

A key argument against old-fashioned materialism, in Russell's view, comes from developments in physics. The behaviourist psychologists of his day adopted materialistic methods, treating matter as more solid and indubitable than mind. But Russell observes that physics was simultaneously undermining the very concept of matter upon which materialism depends:

> [P01-001] On the one hand, many psychologists, especially those of the behaviourist school, tend to adopt what is essentially a materialistic position, as a matter of method if not of metaphysics. They make psychology increasingly dependent on physiology and external observation, and tend to think of matter as something much more solid and indubitable than mind. Meanwhile the physicists, especially Einstein and other exponents of the theory of relativity, have been making "matter" less and less material.

The physicists' world, Russell notes, consists of "events" from which matter is derived by logical construction — not of solid material substance. An old-fashioned materialism, he concludes, can receive no support from modern physics.

## Methodological Materialism in Psychophysiology

Despite rejecting metaphysical materialism, Russell acknowledges that the working methods of psychophysiology are materialistic in their explanatory direction. When precise causal laws are sought for mental phenomena, investigators are driven toward the nervous system:

> [015-033] It is such considerations that make students of psychophysiology materialistic in their methods, whatever they may be in their metaphysics. There are, of course, exceptions, such as Professor J. S. Haldane, who maintains that it is theoretically impossible to obtain physiological explanations of psychical phenomena.

Russell regards Haldane's position as a minority view and accepts that the bulk of expert opinion favours materialistic methods in practice, even while he remains agnostic about the ultimate question of whether psychology can be reduced to physics.

## Neutral Monism as Resolution

Russell's proposed resolution to the materialism–[[idealism]] debate is [[neutral-monism]], the view — drawn from [[william-james]] and the American new realists — that the "stuff" of the world is neither mental nor material but neutral:

> [P01-002] The view that seems to me to reconcile the materialistic tendency of psychology with the anti-materialistic tendency of physics is the view of William James and the American new realists, according to which the "stuff" of the world is neither mental nor material, but a "neutral stuff," out of which both are constructed.

This position allows Russell to accept the methodological pull toward physical explanation without committing to the metaphysical claim that matter is the fundamental reality. The practical upshot is a position that approximates materialism in some respects and its opposite in others:

> [001-050] In certain respects, the views which I shall be setting forth approximate to materialism; in certain others, they approximate to its opposite. On this question of the study of delusions, the practical effect of the modern theories, as Dr. Hart points out, is emancipation from the materialist method.
