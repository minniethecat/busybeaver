# Error

**Error**, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, is treated not merely as a failure of cognition but as a phenomenon that reveals the nature of [[belief]] and the conditions under which cognition operates. The very possibility of error distinguishes belief from simpler mental phenomena: [[sensation|sensation]] are neither true nor false, but beliefs can be mistaken. Russell argues that error arises from the gap between expectation and verification — a gap that is inherent in any cognitive system that goes beyond the immediately given. Against the [[idealism|idealist]] tendency to define truth in terms of coherence, Russell insists that error is a real phenomenon that cannot be defined away, and that acknowledging its possibility is essential to any adequate theory of [[knowledge]] and [[truth]].

## Error as Evidence of Cognitive Sophistication

Russell makes the striking claim that the capacity for error is itself evidence of cognitive advancement. An organism that merely responds reflexively to stimuli cannot be said to err — it simply acts. Error enters the picture only when an organism forms expectations that go beyond the immediately present:

> "Falsehood is evidence of knowledge, for it shows that the organism has expectations that go beyond the immediately given." (013-021)

This is because error presupposes representation: the organism must represent the world as being a certain way, and that representation must be capable of failing to match reality. Simple [[reflex]] movements lack this representational character; they respond to stimuli but do not *assert* anything about the world.

Russell draws an implicit contrast with [[behaviourism|behaviourist]] accounts that would reduce all behaviour to stimulus-response patterns: such accounts struggle to explain error, because a response is simply what it is — it cannot be "wrong" about anything. Belief, by contrast, is inherently normative: it aims at truth and is subject to correction when it misses.

## The Formal Structure of Error

Russell analyzes error in terms of the relationship between a belief and the events that would satisfy it. A belief is erroneous when the [[verification-conditions]] it anticipates fail to materialize:

> "The truth or falsehood of a belief consists in the occurrence or non-occurrence of the events that would verify it." (013-009)

This analysis yields a simple formal structure:

| Component | Role in Error |
|-----------|--------------|
| Belief (expectation) | Represents the world as being a certain way |
| Verification-events | What would need to happen for the belief to be correct |
| Non-occurrence | The events do not occur — the world is not as the belief represents it |
| Practical consequence | The believer's expectations are disappointed |

Crucially, error is not an intrinsic property of the belief-state itself. A belief and a true belief can feel identical to the believer. The difference lies in the belief's external relations — whether the events it anticipates actually occur:

> "Self-evidence has often been taken as the guarantee of truth, but the difficulty is that a belief may seem self-evident and yet be false. Self-evidence is a psychological property, not a logical one." (013-024)

## Sources of Error in Cognition

Russell identifies several systematic sources of error in human cognition:

**Hasty generalization**. The most common source of error is the tendency to form beliefs on the basis of insufficient evidence:

> "The acquisition of caution is a large part of education. The untrained mind rushes to conclusions on insufficient evidence." (006-028)

**Mnemic interference**. Past experience, operating through [[mnemic-causation]], can produce expectations that are appropriate to past situations but inappropriate to the present one. This is the mechanism by which [[habit]] can lead to error when circumstances change.

**Emotional and practical bias**. Russell notes that our beliefs are often shaped by what we wish to be true rather than by evidence:

> "Against such errors, the only safeguard is to be able to view our own beliefs with the same detachment with which we view the beliefs of others." (010-051)

**The absence of an infallible criterion**. Russell emphasizes that there is no mark by which a true belief can be infallibly distinguished from a false one:

> "There is no absolute criterion of truth, no mark by which a true belief can be infallibly distinguished from a false one." (013-030)

This means error is an ineliminable risk in any cognitive system. We can reduce the probability of error through [[science|scientific method]] and the systematic collection of evidence, but we can never eliminate it entirely.

## Error and the Theory of Knowledge

The possibility of error creates the central problem of epistemology: if beliefs can be false even when they feel certain, how can we distinguish [[knowledge]] from mere true belief? Russell's answer is that knowledge requires not merely truth but a reliable connection between belief and fact — a causal chain that makes the belief depend on its truth-condition:

> "Knowledge is a matter of the causal antecedents of a belief. A true belief that is caused by a lucky guess is not knowledge." (013-025)

Error thus serves as a kind of negative test for knowledge: a belief-forming process that frequently produces errors cannot yield knowledge, even when it occasionally produces true beliefs. This is why Russell emphasizes the importance of scientific method and systematic evidence-collection — they are techniques for reducing the error rate of our cognitive processes.

But Russell is careful not to demand certainty. We can have knowledge without being infallible:

> "We shall be wise if we aim at being wholly exempt from error only in a very small number of our beliefs." (013-029)

## Error and the Unreliability of Introspection

Russell extends his analysis of error to the domain of [[introspection]], challenging the common philosophical assumption that we cannot be mistaken about our own mental states:

> "This, however, would be an error — the sort of error that the theory of an inner sense is especially liable to." (007-008)

The claim that introspection provides infallible access to our own minds is itself an error — and one that Russell's behaviourist analysis is designed to correct. Just as we can be mistaken about the external world, we can be mistaken about whether a particular mental event is a sensation or an image, a memory or a fantasy. The sensation-image distinction is a causal one, not one that is transparent to introspection.
