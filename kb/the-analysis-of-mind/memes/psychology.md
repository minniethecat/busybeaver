## Definition

Psychology, as Russell approaches it, is the science that studies mental phenomena — not as the private data of an individual mind, but as observable occurrences in the physical world. Russell declares: "I am interested in psychology, not so much for its own sake, as for the light that it may throw on the problem of knowledge" (001-018). His aim is not to contribute to empirical psychology but to use its findings to dissolve traditional philosophical problems about mind and matter.

## Russell's Account

Russell opens *The Analysis of Mind* by situating psychology between two competing tendencies: "different tendencies, one in psychology, the other in physics" (P01-001). The psychological tendency, broadly materialistic, seeks to explain mind in terms of observable behaviour; the physical tendency, paradoxically anti-materialistic, treats matter as constructed from sense-data. Russell's project is to reconcile these two.

He acknowledges that psychology, as a science, is in its infancy: "in psychology we are hardly yet at the stage" where abstract scientific laws comparable to those in physics have been formulated (001-051). Traditional psychology has been hampered by its reliance on introspection and its assumption that mental states are inherently private. Russell traces two divergent tendencies in the history of psychology: "those who take mental phenomena as the data for psychology" and those who take behaviour and external observation as primary (001-015).

Russell's own methodology is explicitly behaviourist in its starting point. "If we begin our study of psychology by external observation," he argues, "we must not, to begin with, assume any knowledge of the minds of others" — we observe bodily movements and infer mental states only after a long process of correlation (002-006). He cautions, however, that behaviourism "is exceedingly misleading" if it denies the existence of images and other non-sensory mental contents (002-005). Russell's position is a qualified behaviourism: external observation is the proper starting point, but the full scope of psychology must also account for images, beliefs, desires, and feelings.

The relationship between psychology and physics is central to Russell's neutral monism. "Everything that we hear or seen belongs equally to psychology and to physics," he writes (001-037). The difference is not in the subject matter but in the causal laws applied: "Psychology as a science" is concerned with those regularities that involve mnemic causation — the influence of the past on present occurrences — whereas physics abstracts away from such causal lines (001-038). "The whole of psychology, we shall find, is included" within the physical world, once physics is understood broadly enough to encompass mnemic phenomena (005-028).

In Russell's framework, psychology is ultimately a branch of physics — not because the mental is reduced to the material, but because the physical world, properly understood, already contains all the ingredients of mental life. "The whole of psychology" can be expressed in terms of causal laws that apply to the same particulars that physics studies, but with attention to the mnemic and associative relations that physics ignores (005-028–029).

## Key Distinctions

- **Psychology vs. Physics**: Psychology studies the same particulars as physics but attends to mnemic causal lines (the influence of past experience on present occurrences), which physics abstracts away (005-028–029).
- **Scientific vs. Conventional Psychology**: Conventional psychology relies on introspection and assumes a knowing subject; scientific psychology, as Russell envisions it, begins with external observation and behaviour (001-009, 002-006).
- **Materialistic vs. Anti-materialistic Tendencies**: The materialistic tendency in psychology aligns with behaviourism and physics; the anti-materialistic tendency treats mental states as irreducibly private (P01-001–002, 001-015).
- **Behaviourist vs. Introspective Method**: Russell advocates beginning with external observation but rejects a behaviourism that denies images and other non-sensory mental occurrences (002-005–006).

## Historical Context

Russell's *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) responds directly to two movements in early twentieth-century psychology: John B. Watson's behaviourism, which Russell admired for its methodological rigour but found overly restrictive, and the introspective tradition derived from William James and Franz Brentano. Russell draws particularly on Brentano's concept of intentionality but strips it of its mentalistic implications, and on the comparative psychology of animals, which he cites as a valuable corrective to human-centred introspection (001-005). His approach also reflects the influence of Ernst Mach's neutral monism and the American New Realism, both of which sought to dissolve the mind–matter distinction.
