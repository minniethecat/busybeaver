## Definition

Pure experience is the term Russell adopts from William James to designate the fundamental "stuff" of reality before it is divided into subject and object, mind and matter. Russell invokes it in articulating his neutral monism: "if we call that stuff 'pure experience,' then knowing can easily be explained as a relation between different parts of experience" (001-032).

## Russell's Account

Russell uses "pure experience" to designate the neutral entities that, when arranged one way, constitute a mind, and when arranged another way, constitute a physical object. He notes that James first "used the phrase 'pure experience'" to refer to the primordial flux, and that "the use of the phrase 'pure experience' in both essays points to a living and evolving philosophical tradition" (001-036) — namely, the radical empiricism that Russell and the American New Realists shared.

Russell clarifies that the term is meant to encompass both sensory and imaginal occurrences without privileging either: he notes that some philosophers call these elements "'ideas' or 'thoughts,' and later, 'pure experience,' the latter term including both sensations and images" (006-010). This inclusiveness is essential to Russell's project: if all mental contents — sensations, images, beliefs, desires — are made of the same neutral stuff, then the sharp divisions traditional philosophy draws between them need to be re-examined in terms of causal relations rather than intrinsic nature.

The advantage of pure experience as a metaphysical primitive is that it dissolves the problem of how mind can know matter. If both knower and known are composed of the same neutral stuff, knowing becomes an external relation among elements of the same kind, rather than a mysterious transaction across an ontological divide.

## Key Distinctions

- **Pure experience vs. ordinary experience**: Ordinary experience is experience-as-classified (into subject/object, inner/outer); pure experience is the unclassified datum before these distinctions are drawn
- **Pure experience vs. sensation**: Sensation is pure experience considered in its causal relation to sense organs; pure experience is the broader category
- **Pure experience vs. consciousness**: Pure experience does not presuppose a subject who is conscious of it; consciousness is an additional relation

## Historical Context

William James introduced "pure experience" in his *Essays in Radical Empiricism* (1912), arguing that the knower and the known are both portions of experience. Russell adopted the term after his turn away from dualism around 1918–1919, integrating it with his own logical atomism. The concept also appears in the work of Ernst Mach (whose "elements" are comparable) and in the neutral monism of the American New Realists.
