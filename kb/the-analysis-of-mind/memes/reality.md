## Overview

Reality, for [[Bertrand Russell]], is not a metaphysical postulate but something encountered directly in [[sensation-image|sensation]]. His realism about the external world — that it exists independently of our minds and is knowable through perception — is a foundational commitment that shapes every aspect of *The Analysis of Mind*.

Russell distinguishes carefully between **reality as a metaphysical category** (what exists independently of mind) and the **feeling of reality** (a psychological phenomenon that accompanies certain beliefs and perceptions). This distinction is crucial to his analysis of [[belief]], [[memory]], and [[imagination]].

> [001-024] …except thoughts, and all the reality that we know is mental…

Russell's realism is nuanced: he rejects the idealist claim that all reality is mental, but also rejects naive materialism. His [[neutral-monism]] holds that the "actual stuff of reality" is neither mental nor physical but neutral.

## Metaphysical Reality

Russell maintains that there is a reality independent of our minds, and that [[sensation]] provides our most direct access to it. Unlike [[memory]] or [[imagination]], which involve images and may err, sensation confronts us with something genuinely external.

> [001-050] …neither of them the actual stuff of reality, but different convenient groupings…

| Domain | Relationship to Reality |
|--------|------------------------|
| Sensation | Direct access to external particulars |
| Perception | Interpretation of sensation, potentially corrigible |
| Memory | Reference to past reality, fallible |
| Imagination | No necessary connection to reality |
| Belief | Assertion about reality, truth-conditional |

This hierarchy explains Russell's epistemological priorities: he is a realist about sensation but more cautious about memory and belief. [[Truth]] consists in correspondence between belief-content and reality.

## The Feeling of Reality

A distinctive contribution of *The Analysis of Mind* is Russell's analysis of the **feeling of reality** as a psychological phenomenon distinct from actual reality. When we [[belief|believe]] something, we typically experience a feeling of conviction that the content of our belief is real.

> [009-059] The feeling of reality is a feeling akin to respect: it belongs PRIMARILY to whatever we regard as outside ourselves…

This feeling can attach to mental contents that do not correspond to reality:

- [[Dream|Dreams]] feel real while they occur
- Hallucinations carry a conviction of reality
- Vivid [[memory-image|memory-images]] may feel "more real" than faint sensations

> [008-028] …our absence of belief in the "physical reality" of images. When we are aware of an image, we do not normally believe it corresponds to a physical object.

Russell's point is that the *feeling* of reality is a psychological fact, not a guarantee of veridicality. This is why [[verification]] through behavior and consensus matters: it distinguishes cases where the feeling of reality aligns with actual reality from cases where it does not.

## Reality and Neutral Monism

Russell's realism culminates in [[neutral-monism]], the view that mind and [[matter]] are not two substances but two ways of organizing the same neutral particulars.

> [015-035] …neutral stuff, not the hypothetical material fiction, were the ultimate reality. I think that, if our scientific knowledge were complete, we should find that the ultimate constituents of the world are neither mental nor material.

This is a radical claim: the ultimate reality is not mental ([[idealism]]) nor physical ([[materialism]]) but neutral — the same elements that appear as sensations in one context appear as physical objects in another.

| Position | What is Ultimately Real |
|----------|-----------------------|
| Idealism | Mind / ideas |
| Materialism | Matter / physical particles |
| Dualism | Both mind and matter |
| **Neutral Monism** | Neutral particulars (sensations, images) |

## Reality in Science and Common Sense

Russell aligns his conception of reality with the scientific worldview. Scientific [[physics]] describes the structure of reality, but does not reveal its intrinsic nature. Common sense, meanwhile, treats as real whatever is vivid, consistent, and shared — criteria that Russell respects but subjects to logical scrutiny.

His realism thus navigates between two extremes: the idealist reduction of reality to mind, and the naive realist assumption that reality is exactly as it appears. Reality is independent of mind, but our access to it is mediated by sensation and refined by logical analysis.
