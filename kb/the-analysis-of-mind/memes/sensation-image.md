## Definition

In Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, sensation and images are the two basic ingredients of mental life. Sensations are the non-mnemic core of perception, independent of past experience; images are mental copies of past sensations, distinguished by their causal laws rather than by vividness or belief. Together they form the building blocks of Russell's neutral monism, in which "the stuff of the world... consists of innumerable transient particulars such as occur in seeing, hearing, etc." (008-011).

## Russell's Account

Russell defines sensation as "the non-mnemic elements in a perception" (008-005). Pure sensation is what remains after stripping away all contributions from habit, expectation, and past experience. This is harder than it seems: "so much interpretation, so much of habitual correlation, is mixed with all such experiences, that the core of pure sensation is only to be extracted by careful investigation" (008-006). Russell illustrates this with the example of hearing a foreign language — what seems like a pure auditory experience in one's native tongue reveals itself, in an unfamiliar language, to be largely filled in by inference (008-006).

Crucially, Russell denies that pure sensation is a form of knowledge. "A patch of colour is certainly not knowledge, and therefore we cannot say that pure sensation is cognitive" (008-008). Sensation is, rather, the intersection of mind and matter — "what is common to the mental and physical worlds" (008-012). This is a core tenet of neutral monism: a sensation, such as a patch of colour, "simply is that patch of colour, an actual constituent of the physical world, and part of what physics is concerned with" (008-008).

Images, by contrast, are defined by their causal laws. Russell considers and rejects two common criteria for distinguishing images from sensations: (1) lesser vividness, and (2) absence of belief in "physical reality" (008-016–020). Neither is universally reliable — dreams and hallucinations show that images can be vivid and believed. Russell settles on a causal criterion: images obey mnemic laws (laws involving past experience), whereas sensations obey only non-mnemic physical laws.

The distinction between sensation and image is fundamental to Russell's epistemology because it replaces the traditional mind-matter dualism. "The dualism is not primarily as to the stuff of the world, but as to causal laws" (008-001). Sensations and images are made of the same neutral stuff; what distinguishes them is how they behave causally.

## Key Distinctions

- **Sensation vs. Perception**: Perception includes sensation plus habitual associates — "images and expectations of their usual correlates" (009-002). Pure sensation is the non-mnemic core.
- **Sensation vs. Sense-datum**: If we dispense with the "subject" or "act," the distinction between sensation and sense-datum collapses. The sensation just is the patch of colour, not an awareness of it (008-008).
- **Image vs. Sensation (causal)**: Images have mnemic causes — their occurrence depends on past experience. Sensations are caused by present stimuli independent of the individual's history.

## Historical Context

Russell's view draws on Ernst Mach's *Analysis of Sensations* (1886), which similarly treated sensations as neutral elements common to mind and matter. The American new realists and William James's radical empiricism are also key influences. Russell's position in *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) represents his mature synthesis of empiricism, behaviourism, and neutral monism.
