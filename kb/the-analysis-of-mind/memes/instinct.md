## Definition

Russell defines an "instinctive" movement as "a vital movement performed by an animal which has not learnt it" (002-020). This negative criterion—not acquired through experience—is the hallmark of instinct. But Russell immediately complicates the picture: "many instincts mature gradually" (002-022), and it is often "impossible to judge what is instinctive in the behaviour of an isolated animal" (002-019) because the boundaries between maturation and learning are blurred.

## Russell's Account

Instinct occupies a foundational role in Russell's psychological theory. He notes early in the book that an organism "does at each stage because instinct gives it an impulse to do just that thing" (001-039), treating instinct as the primordial motive force from which more complex psychological phenomena develop. Instincts provide "no prevision of the biological end" which they serve (002-038) and are "only adapted to achieve the end" under "normal circumstances" (002-039)—when conditions deviate, instinctive behaviour can become maladaptive.

Russell reinforces the instinct-habit relationship through a dynamic of conflict and integration: "one group of habits can 'down' another group of habits—or instincts" (001-055), and "processes initiated by instinct often come to be performed better" through learning (002-040). This layering of acquired behaviour over instinctive foundations is central to Russell's account of mental development.

## Key Distinctions

### Instinct vs. Habit

"The broad distinction between instinct and habit is undeniable" (002-023), yet Russell takes care to expose its ambiguities. Instincts mature on a developmental timetable that may involve environmental triggering; habits, though acquired, can become so deeply engrained as to be indistinguishable from instincts in their automaticity. Russell's solution is to anchor the distinction in causal history rather than present phenomenology: an instinct is movement "which an animal performs without having learnt [it]" (002-020).

### The Fallibility of Instinct

Russell provides "examples of the mistakes made by instinct" (002-035), arguing that instinct is far from infallible. He quotes the case of a moth flying into a flame as "a good illustration of an instinct growing wiser through experience" (002-037)—the moth cannot learn, but the analogy serves to show why intelligence must supplement instinct in complex environments. The adaptive narrowness of instinct is what makes habit and learning necessary.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of instinct reflects the influence of [[William James]], who gave instinct a prominent place in his *Principles of Psychology*, and of the comparative psychology tradition that studied "the behaviour of animals, as regards instinct and habit" (002-030). Russell extends the animal data to humans, arguing that "what is true of animals... is equally true of men" (002-030), a continuity thesis that underpins his entire neutral-monist project. [[John B. Watson|Watsonian]] behaviourism, with which Russell was engaged at the time, similarly sought to minimise the role of instinct in human behaviour, though Russell takes a more balanced position.
