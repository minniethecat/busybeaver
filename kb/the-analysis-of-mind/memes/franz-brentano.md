[[Franz Brentano]] (1838--1917) was an Austrian philosopher and psychologist whose theory of [[intentionality]] became one of the most influential ideas in modern philosophy of mind. His central claim -- that every mental phenomenon is "directed toward an object" -- shaped the work of his students [[Alexius Meinong]] and [[Edmund Husserl]], and provided a critical foil for [[Bertrand Russell]]'s own analysis of [[mind]] and [[consciousness]] in these lectures. Brentano's *Psychology from the Empirical Standpoint* (1874) inaugurated a distinctively Austrian tradition in philosophical psychology that Russell engages with throughout the text.

## Life and Work

Brentano was born in Marienberg, Germany, into a family of Italian descent. He was ordained a Catholic priest in 1864 but left the church in 1873, shortly before his appointment as professor at the University of Vienna. His career was marked by a commitment to a rigorous, scientifically informed philosophy -- a project he called "descriptive psychology."

His major works and their significance:

| Work | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| *Psychology from the Empirical Standpoint* | 1874 | Foundation of intentionality thesis; the text Russell cites directly (001-015) |
| *The Origin of Our Knowledge of Right and Wrong* | 1889 | Ethics grounded in descriptive psychology |
| *The Classification of Psychical Phenomena* | 1911 | Appendix to *Psychology*, refining the intentionality criterion |
| *On the Several Senses of Being in Aristotle* | 1862 | Habilitation thesis on Aristotle's metaphysics |

Brentano's lectures in Vienna attracted an extraordinary generation of students, including Meinong, Husserl, Christian von Ehrenfels, and Sigmund Freud. His intellectual legacy runs through both analytic philosophy (via Russell and Moore) and the Continental phenomenological tradition (via Husserl).

## The Intentionality Thesis

Brentano's signature doctrine is that intentionality -- "the reference to a content, the direction toward an object" -- is the defining mark of the mental. Every belief, desire, perception, or emotion is *about* something; no physical phenomenon exhibits this kind of directedness.

Russell introduces the view early in Lecture I:

> [001-015] "The view of the 'act' as the essential element in every psychical occurrence was first set forth explicitly by the Austrian psychologist Brentano, whose 'Psychology from the Empirical Standpoint,' though published in 1874, is still influential and was the starting-point of a considerable school."

Brentano characterized this directedness as "intentional inexistence" -- the object of thought exists *in* the mental act, not necessarily in the external world. This allowed him to account for thoughts about non-existent objects (unicorns, golden mountains) without committing to their reality. Russell returns to Brentano's criterion in Lecture VIII:

> [008-009] "I shall begin with the view of Brentano, that 'we may define psychical phenomena by saying that they are phenomena which intentionally contain an object.'"

Russell then tests this definition against his own neutral monism, asking whether the intentional relation can survive the reduction of mind to a bundle of sensations and images.

## Brentano's Influence on Russell

Russell first encountered Brentano's work through his study of Meinong in the late 1890s. The Brentanian framework provided Russell with a systematic starting point for his own theory of judgment and belief. In Lecture I, Russell explicitly aligns himself with Brentano's approach while signaling his eventual departure:

> [011-016] "If we adopt the view of Brentano, according to which all mental data involve an act, or, as he calls it, a 'psychical direction towards an object,' we shall hold that, even when the object of a presentation does not exist, the presentation still has an object."

Russell's early multiple-relation theory of judgment (developed in *The Problems of Philosophy* and *Theory of Knowledge*) retains a modified Brentanian shape: judgment is a relation between a subject and the constituents of a proposition. But as Russell moved toward neutral monism, he came to regard the "act" itself as an unnecessary postulate.

## Russell's Critique

Russell's central criticism of Brentano is that "intentional reference" is not a uniquely mental property. Throughout *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell argues that the same directedness can be explained in terms of images, words, and behavioral dispositions -- all of which are constructed from sensations, the "neutral stuff" of his monism.

In Lecture I, Russell sets up the problem:

> [001-037] "I shall contend that the mental elements in an act of imagination or desire, though they involve psychic reference to objects which Brentano assigns to them, are not different in kind from sensations and images."

And in Lecture VI, he returns to the question:

> [006-006] "I spoke in the last lecture in connection with Brentano. In this view a single cognitive occurrence -- say, a belief -- involves an act of a certain kind and an object of a certain kind."

Russell concludes that once we properly analyze belief as a causal chain involving sensations, images, and words, the mysterious "intentional relation" Brentano posited dissolves. What remains is a naturalistic account of how organisms respond to their environment.

## Brentano's School

Brentano's students carried his project in divergent directions, forming two major philosophical traditions:

- **[[Alexius Meinong]]** (1853--1920): Developed the act-content-object distinction and the theory of objects (*Gegenstandstheorie*), which Russell subjects to sustained criticism in these lectures.
- **[[Edmund Husserl]]** (1859--1938): Radicalized intentionality into the core concept of phenomenology, developing a transcendental framework that Russell largely ignored.
- **Christian von Ehrenfels** (1859--1932): Extended Brentanian descriptive psychology to the study of *Gestalt qualities*, influencing Wertheimer and the Berlin School.
- **Kazimierz Twardowski** (1866--1938): Brought Brentano's method to Poland, founding the Lvov-Warsaw school of logic and analytic philosophy.
- **Sigmund Freud** (1856--1939): Though not a philosopher, Freud attended Brentano's lectures and absorbed his emphasis on unconscious mental acts.

The Brentano school thus represents one of the most consequential nodes in the history of philosophy, bridging nineteenth-century empiricism with both analytic philosophy and phenomenology.
