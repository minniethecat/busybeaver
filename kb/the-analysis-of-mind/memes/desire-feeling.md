Russell's analysis of desire and feeling in Lecture III of _The Analysis of Mind_ constitutes a radical departure from the common-sense view. Instead of treating desire as an introspectively accessible mental state directed at an absent object, Russell reconceives it as a causal law of behaviour. Feeling, in turn, is analysed not as a separate mental quality but in terms of the behavioural dispositions that accompany approach and avoidance. This analysis is central to his broader project of deconstructing [[Consciousness|consciousness]] into observable behavioural patterns.

## The Common-Sense View and Its Problems

The ordinary view treats desire as "an attitude towards something which is absent, but which is imagined" (003-001). On this view, a person who desires food has a mental state directed at food, which is not present. Russell identifies two major problems with this account.

First, the psycho-analytic discovery of unconscious desires challenges the idea that we have privileged introspective access to our own desires. Russell observes that "a man may be wholly dominated by a desire of which he is quite unconscious" (001-048). If desires can exist without conscious awareness, then defining desire in terms of conscious feeling becomes untenable.

Second, the common-sense view struggles to explain the behavioural efficacy of desire. Russell argues that desire, like force in mechanics, is best understood by its effects rather than by any intrinsic mental quality.

## Desire as a Causal Law

Russell proposes that desire be understood behaviouristically: an organism desires a state of affairs if it tends to act in ways that bring that state of affairs about. He writes:

> "I believe an 'unconscious' desire is merely a causal law of our behaviour, namely, that we remain restless until something happens." (001-053)

This formulation eliminates the need for a mental entity called "desire" and replaces it with a dispositional property of the organism. A person who desires food is simply a person whose behaviour is causally organized around obtaining food -- restlessness, searching, consumption -- until the food is obtained.

## The Role of Feeling

Feeling enters Russell's analysis not as a distinct mental substance but as the qualitative dimension of behavioural processes. He distinguishes between pleasure and pain as "feeling-tones" that accompany the satisfaction or frustration of desire:

> "Pleasure, on the one hand, is that feeling-tone which accompanies the satisfaction of desire; pain, on the other hand, is that feeling-tone which is directly opposed to the satisfaction of desire." (003-021)

Russell uses the example of hunger to illustrate the connection. When hungry, "we have first an uncomfortable feeling inside, producing a disinclination to sit still" (003-017). The discomfort generates restlessness, which leads to food-seeking behaviour, which upon success produces the feeling-tone of satisfaction. What appears introspectively as a sequence of feelings is, from the outside, a causal chain of stimuli and responses.

## Knowledge, Desire, and Feeling

Russell integrates desire and feeling into a tripartite classification alongside [[Knowledge|knowledge]]. In Lecture III, he writes of what is "signified by knowledge, desire and feeling" (003-020), treating these as the three fundamental categories of psychological analysis. Each is analysed in behaviouristic terms:

- Knowledge is a matter of appropriate response to stimuli
- Desire is a causal law governing an organism's tendency to seek certain outcomes
- Feeling is the qualitative accompaniment of desire's satisfaction or frustration

This tripartite framework provides the scaffolding for much of the book's subsequent analysis, including Russell's accounts of [[Belief|belief]], [[Emotions and Will|emotions-will]], and [[Memory|memory]].

## Unconscious Desires and Behaviourism

The phenomenon of unconscious desire provides Russell with a key argument for behaviourism. If the most important determinants of conduct can operate below the threshold of [[Consciousness|consciousness]], then the study of mind cannot be reduced to the study of conscious experience. Russell asserts that the importance of unconscious desires "has been brought very much to the fore in recent years by psycho-analysis" (001-047), and that this discovery forces a reconceptualization of mental life in causal rather than introspective terms.

Russell's conclusion is that the vocabulary of desire -- "wants," "wishes," "longings" -- picks out real causal patterns in behaviour, but that these patterns do not depend on any mysterious mental substance. Desire is simply a name for the observable regularity with which organisms pursue certain ends.
