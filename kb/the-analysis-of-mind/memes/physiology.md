Throughout *The Analysis of Mind*, [[Bertrand Russell]] repeatedly invokes [[Physiology|physiology]] as the proper scientific foundation for [[Psychology|psychology]]. His central methodological claim is that psychology should become "increasingly dependent on physiology and external observation" (P01-001), moving away from introspection toward an objective, biologically grounded science of mind. Physiology provides the causal framework within which mental phenomena must be understood.

## Psychology's Dependence on Physiology

Russell opens his lectures by declaring the dependence of psychology on physiology as both a methodological imperative and an established scientific trend:

> [P01-001] …ogy increasingly dependent on physiology and external observation, and…

This dependence is not merely programmatic — it reflects the actual state of scientific knowledge. The more we learn about the brain and nervous system, the less room there is for a purely mentalistic psychology. Russell sees this as a welcome development that aligns psychology with the natural sciences (001-012).

## The Reduction to Chemistry and Physics

Russell acknowledges that physiology itself is being reduced to more fundamental sciences:

> [002-013] …ly know in part how to reduce physiology to chemistry, but we know eno…

This reductionist chain — psychology → physiology → chemistry → physics — does not trouble Russell. He sees it as the natural progression of scientific explanation. The fact that we cannot yet fully explain mental phenomena in physiological terms is a practical limitation, not a philosophical one (004-029, 004-031).

## Physiology and the Problem of Consciousness

A key question for Russell is whether physiology can account for [[Consciousness|consciousness]] itself. He considers the disturbances of consciousness produced by physiological interventions — drugs, injury, fatigue — as evidence that consciousness is a physiological phenomenon:

> [004-021] … direct observation. No doubt physiology, especially the disturbances …

> [004-022] …that, in the present state of physiology, the introduction of the engr…

Russell argues that the correlation between physiological states and conscious states is so tight that it would be inexplicable if consciousness were non-physical. The evidence points toward consciousness being "just as much to be found in physiology as in psychology" (004-032).

## Nerve-Physiology and the Mind-Body Boundary

Russell blurs the traditional boundary between psychology and physiology:

> [010-042] …tinguish psychology (or nerve-physiology?) from physics is the law tha…

The parenthetical "(or nerve-physiology?)" is telling — Russell sees psychology as essentially nerve-physiology pursued from a different angle. The distinction between the two disciplines is methodological rather than ontological (006-004).

## Psychophysiology and Materialism

Russell addresses the concern that physiological psychology leads to [[Materialism|materialism]]:

> [015-033] …s that make students of psychophysiology materialistic in their method…

He distinguishes between methodological materialism — the practice of seeking physiological explanations — and metaphysical materialism — the doctrine that only matter exists. Russell endorses the former while rejecting the latter in favor of [[Neutral Monism|neutral monism]] (015-022, 015-027).

## Physiology of Emotion

Russell briefly discusses the physiology of emotion, drawing on the James-Lange theory:

> [014-009] …e functions of which, both in physiology and in connection with the em…

The bodily changes associated with emotion — heart rate, respiration, muscular tension — are physiological events. Russell's analysis suggests that [[Emotion|emotion]] is not a mental cause of these bodily changes but rather the perception of them, a position aligned with [[William James|William James's]] theory.
