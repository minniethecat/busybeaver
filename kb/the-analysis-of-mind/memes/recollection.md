Recollection, in Russell's analysis of [[memory]], is the conscious calling to mind of a specific past event, distinguished from both habit-memory and the mere feeling of familiarity that characterizes [[recognition]]. Drawing on the work of [[Henri Bergson]] and [[Richard Semon]], Russell treats recollection as the clearest instance of a [[mnemic causation|mnemic phenomenon]] — a response whose causal explanation requires reference to past occurrences in the history of the organism.

## Recollection as Mnemic Phenomenon

Russell adopts the concept of "mnemic phenomena" from Semon to describe responses that cannot be explained solely by present stimuli without reference to the organism's history. Recollection is the paradigm case of such a phenomenon: the present act of remembering is causally dependent on a past event, and no complete account of the act can be given in purely physiological terms that ignore this historical dependence.

> [004-023] The clearest instance of what I mean is recollection of a past event. Suppose you are asked an question which you have been asked before: "What is the capital of France?" You answer "Paris," not because you have any present sensation associated with Paris, but because, on the former occasion when you learned the fact, a certain connection was established in your brain which now operates again. The cause of your recollection, so far as hitherto observed facts are concerned, involves the past occurrence as part of the chain of causes.

This mnemic character means that recollection resists reduction to purely behaviourist explanation. While the behaviourist can describe the observable response, the causal explanation necessarily refers to a past event that is no longer physically present.

## Bergson's Distinction: Habit-Memory vs. Independent Recollection

Russell endorses [[Henri Bergson|Bergson]]'s crucial distinction between two fundamentally different forms of memory. The first is habit-memory — exemplified by knowing a lesson by heart — which requires no conscious reference to the past and consists simply in acquired behavioural dispositions. The second is independent recollection — the conscious recall of a specific past episode, such as remembering the occasion when one first read the lesson.

> [009-020] It is important not to confuse the two forms of memory which Bergson distinguishes in the second chapter of his "Matter and Memory," namely the sort that consists of habit, and the sort that consists of independent recollection. He gives the instance of learning a lesson by heart: when I know it by heart I am said to "remember" it, but this merely means that I have acquired certain habits; on the other hand, my recollection of (say) the second time I read the lesson while I was learning it is the recollection of a unique event which happened only once.

This distinction is critical for Russell's theory of mind because it separates the genuinely mental aspect of memory — the conscious reference to a past event — from the merely physiological aspect of habit formation. Only independent recollection carries the distinctive temporal awareness that marks memory as a form of [[knowledge]] about the past.

## Recollection and the Feeling of Pastness

A central phenomenological feature of recollection is the feeling that the recalled event belongs to the past. Russell distinguishes this feeling from the mere feeling of familiarity that accompanies [[recognition]]. When one recollects a specific past event, the content of the recollection includes not only the event itself but also a temporal定位 — a sense of "when" it occurred relative to the present.

> [009-033] Between memory-image and sensation there is an intermediate experience concerning the immediate past. For example, a sensation of hearing a noise may be followed, at a very short interval, by an image of a noise of very similar sound. This image, which may be called an "image of the immediate past," has a peculiar felt quality of pastness which distinguishes it from a sensation. But this felt quality of pastness is something quite different from the definite placing of an event in past time which is involved in true memory.

This "felt quality of pastness" is what elevates recollection above mere imagination: the recollected image is not merely similar to a past sensation but is experienced as being about the past. Russell treats this intentional directedness toward the past as a defining feature of memory-knowledge.

## Recollection and Truth

Russell identifies an important epistemological asymmetry in recollection: vague recollections have a greater likelihood of being true than precise ones. A precise recollection — with detailed imagery and specific temporal placement — is more likely to have been altered by imagination and inference than a vague impression that something occurred.

> [009-048] It follows from what has been said that a vague thought has more likelihood of being true than a precise one. Try to recollect the circumstances under which you last saw a certain friend. You probably can call to mind the room, the chairs, the other people present, and so on. Your recollection of these details may be approximately correct. But if you try to recollect exactly what your friend was wearing, you are more likely to be wrong than if you have only a vague general impression as against the mere recollection that I met a man.

This counter-intuitive principle — that vagueness can be epistemically virtuous — reflects Russell's broader commitment to the fallibility of memory and the pervasive role of inference and construction in what we take to be simple recall.

## Recollection in the Causal Theory of Memory

Within Russell's broader theory of [[mind]], recollection serves as a bridge between the [[physiology|physiological]] and the psychological. The causal chain that produces a recollection — from past event to brain modification to present image — is physical throughout, yet the resulting experience has a distinctively mental character: it is about something absent. This dual character — physical causation, mental content — exemplifies the neutral monist thesis that mind and matter are not fundamentally different substances but different arrangements of the same neutral [[particulars]].

> [015-013] Very similar considerations apply to memory, if taken as the essence of mind. A recollection is aroused by something which is similar to, but not identical with, something previously experienced. The present stimulus, together with the past occurrence and the resulting modification of the brain, brings about the recollection. The whole process, from start to finish, can theoretically be stated in terms of physics and physiology; yet the recollection itself, as an experience, has characteristics which we call "mental."
