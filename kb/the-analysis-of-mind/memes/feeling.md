## Definition

Feeling, in Russell's psychological taxonomy, is one of three fundamental classifications of mental phenomena, standing alongside [[Knowledge]] and desire. Russell uses the term broadly to designate the affective dimension of mental life — encompassing bodily sensations, emotional tones, and the qualitative "feel" that accompanies various cognitive states. Rather than treating feeling as a narrowly hedonic category, he extends it to include phenomena ranging from toothache and hunger to the sense of familiarity that attaches to certain memories (003-020).

## Russell's Account

Russell's treatment of feeling departs from the traditional mind-body dualism by treating feelings as irreducible components of experience rather than as epiphenomena of purely physical processes. He observes that bodily feelings — toothache, hunger, thirst, and the feeling of fatigue — form a distinct class of mental occurrences that resist reduction to either pure cognition or volition (006-022). These sensations are not merely physiological events but possess an intrinsic qualitative character that qualifies them as mental.

A central thesis in Russell's account is that the distinction between feeling and thinking is one of degree rather than kind. There is no sharp ontological boundary separating the two; instead, they lie on a continuum of mental activity (008-022). This gradualism reflects Russell's broader commitment to neutral monism, wherein mind and matter are constructed from the same underlying stuff differentiated only by the relations into which their constituents enter.

## Key Distinctions

**Feeling and Belief.** Russell classifies belief itself as a species of feeling — specifically, a feeling directed toward a propositional content. Belief is "one kind of feeling in relation to this content," meaning that the attitude of assent or conviction is fundamentally affective rather than purely intellectual (003-001).

**Feeling-Tone.** Within the category of feeling, Russell distinguishes between pleasant and unpleasant feeling-tones, which stand in direct opposition to one another (003-021). An uncomfortable feeling inside produces aversive behavior, illustrating how feeling-tone motivates action (003-017).

**The Feeling of Familiarity.** One of Russell's more distinctive observations concerns the role of feeling in epistemology: we distinguish trusted memory-images from mere imaginings by the "feeling of familiarity" that accompanies the former (009-009). This affective marker serves as a practical criterion for navigating one's own mental contents.

**Feeling of Unreality.** When elements of experience fail to cohere into a unified whole, the result is a characteristic feeling of unreality — an affective signal that integration has broken down.

## Historical Context

Russell's classification of feeling alongside knowledge and desire reflects the influence of eighteenth- and nineteenth-century faculty psychology, which typically divided the mind into cognition, affection, and conation. However, Russell strips this tripartite scheme of its metaphysical baggage. He does not posit feeling as a distinct mental substance or faculty; rather, he treats it as a descriptive category for grouping certain kinds of psychological occurrences.

His gradualism about the feeling-thinking distinction aligns with the empiricist tradition, particularly [[David Hume]] and [[William James]], both of whom questioned rigid boundaries among mental faculties. Russell's emphasis on bodily feelings also anticipates themes later developed in embodied cognition and phenomenology.
