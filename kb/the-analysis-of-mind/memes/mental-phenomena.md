At the end of _The Analysis of Mind_, Russell returns to the question from which he set out: what characterizes mind as opposed to matter? Lecture XV provides his culminating answer — one that rejects traditional introspective criteria in favour of a causal and relational account. The provisional suggestion from the outset was that "psychology and physics are distinguished by the nature of their causal laws, not by their subject matter" (015-001), and the final lecture systematically defends this thesis against rival views.

## Consciousness is Not the Essence of Mind

Russell begins by examining the most common candidate for the mark of the mental: [[Consciousness|consciousness]]. He reiterates the arguments from Lecture I against the view that consciousness is fundamental, but now he goes further — he attempts to define what consciousness is, precisely in order to show that it is too complex and derivative to serve as the distinguishing characteristic of mind.

Russell defines consciousness in terms of the meaning-relation developed in Lecture XI:

> "When a sensation is followed by an image which is a 'copy' of it, I think it may be said that the existence of the image constitutes consciousness of the sensation, provided it is accompanied by that sort of belief which, when we reflect upon it, makes us feel that the image is a 'sign' of something other than itself." (015-003)

This definition reveals consciousness to be a complex affair involving images, beliefs, and associative connections. Russell concludes that "consciousness is far too complex and accidental to be taken as the fundamental characteristic of mind" (015-003). Belief and images both enter into it, and belief itself is complex.

## Mnemic Causation as the Mark of the Mental

Russell turns next to characteristics of mental phenomena that arise out of mnemic causation. The capacity to act with reference to what is not sensibly present is one candidate for distinguishing mind:

> "The whole essence of the practical efficiency of 'thought' consists in sensitiveness to signs: the sensible presence of A, which is a sign of the present or future existence of B, enables us to act in a manner appropriate to B." (015-011)

Words are the supreme example — their effects as signs are prodigious, while their intrinsic interest as sensible occurrences is usually very slight. Crucially, this operation of signs may or may not be accompanied by consciousness. A familiar sign tends to operate directly through habit, without the intervention of consciousness, which "marks an imperfectly established habit" (015-011).

Russell identifies the power of acquiring experience as a central instance of mnemic causation: "the causal unit is not one event at one time, but two or more events at two or more times" (015-013). This temporal spread of causation — where the past history of the organism enters essentially into the causation of present responses — is what distinguishes psychological from physical causal laws.

## Neutral Monism and the True Metaphysic

The lecture culminates in a sketch of what Russell calls "that fundamental science which I believe to be the true metaphysic, in which mind and matter alike are seen to be constructed out of a neutral stuff" (015-001). This is [[Neutral Monism|neutral monism]] — the view that the ultimate constituents of the world are neither mental nor physical, but neutral entities that enter into both psychological and physical causal laws depending on how they are grouped.

Russell's position is neither materialism nor idealism. Materialism, the view that "all mental phenomena are causally dependent upon physical phenomena" (015-031), is rejected as going beyond the evidence. Instead, Russell proposes that mind and matter are logical constructions out of the same neutral particulars, distinguished only by the kinds of causal laws under which they are organized.

## The Causal Criterion

Russell's final answer is that no intrinsic mark universally distinguishes mental from physical phenomena. Images, which were initially assigned exclusively to psychology, "can only be defined causally" (015-003). The distinction between psychology and physics is a distinction between two ways of grouping the same neutral events — by their psychological causal laws (involving mnemic causation and association) or by their physical causal laws (involving the laws of physics).

This conclusion completes the arc of the book: from the critique of [[Consciousness|consciousness]] in Lecture I, through the analysis of [[Instinct and Habit|instinct-habit]], [[Desire and Feeling|desire-feeling]], [[Sensation and Images|sensation-image]], [[Memory|memory]], [[Belief|belief]], and [[Truth and Falsehood|truth-falsehood]], to this final dissolution of the mind-matter dichotomy into a unified neutral monism.
