# Privacy

## Definition

Privacy, in Russell's philosophy of mind, is the property by which certain occurrences — sensations, images, feelings — are directly accessible to only one observer: the subject whose mental life they constitute.

Russell argues that this privacy is not a metaphysical necessity but a contingent fact about physiology, and that it need form no bar to the scientific study of mind (008-037).

## Russell's Account

Russell treats privacy as one of the central objections that must be met by any naturalistic theory of mind.

If mental occurrences are inherently private — accessible only to the introspecting subject — then how can psychology be a public, objective science? Russell's answer is that privacy is real but misunderstood: it is a consequence of the causal pathways by which mental occurrences are produced, not a defining mark of their nature.

The fundamental distinction Russell draws is between privacy and publicity. The whole distinction of privacy and publicity, he argues, has been drawn too sharply (006-023). A sensation, considered in its intrinsic character, is neither private nor public — it is a neutral particular.

It becomes "private" when it is caused through pathways internal to one organism, and "public" when its causes involve the shared physical environment. But the sensation itself is the same kind of thing in both cases.

All images, Russell contends, are private in the sense that their causes are tied to the individual's own body and neural history (006-022). A memory-image of a face, unlike the percept of the face itself, cannot be shared — its causal pathway runs through one person's brain, not through the public world of light and objects.

Yet this privacy is physiological, not metaphysical. The image is private only because of its causal connection to a particular nervous system (008-035).

This physiological account of privacy has important consequences. If privacy is a matter of causal isolation rather than ontological difference, then it need not block scientific psychology. The privacy of mental data no more prevents their scientific study than the privacy of a person's digestive processes prevents the science of physiology (008-037).

The scientist cannot directly observe another's sensations, but can study their causes, correlates, and behavioural effects — which is sufficient for a causal science of mind.

Russell also addresses the privacy of introspective data. Introspection yields knowledge that is, in a practical sense, private — it is the privacy of introspective data that causes many philosophers to doubt whether psychology can be genuinely scientific (011-018). But Russell counters that this privacy is a matter of degree, not kind.

All observation involves causal pathways specific to the observer; introspection simply involves pathways that happen to end within the same organism. The epistemological difference is real but not fundamental.

## Key Distinctions

**Privacy vs. Subjectivity.** Privacy is an epistemic property — a limitation on who can access an occurrence. [[Subjectivity]] is an ontological property — a claim about the nature of the occurrence itself. Russell rejects the latter while accepting the former as a contingent physiological fact.

**Privacy and the Body.** The body is the boundary of privacy. Sensations caused by events outside the body are public (accessible, in principle, to multiple observers); images and feelings caused by events within the body are private (accessible only to the embodied subject).

This boundary, however, is fuzzy — proprioceptive sensations straddle it, and the body itself is both a public physical object and the seat of private experience.

**Privacy and Science.** Many philosophers have held that the privacy of mental states makes a scientific psychology impossible. Russell's response is that privacy reflects causal isolation, not ontological mystery — and causal isolation is a challenge for method, not a limit on knowledge.

## Historical Context

The problem of privacy has deep roots in modern philosophy. Descartes's cogito grounded knowledge in the privacy of first-person experience; the resulting mind-body problem was, in part, a problem about how private mental substances could interact with public physical substances.

Russell's neutral monism dissolves this problem by denying that privacy marks an ontological divide.

Russell's approach was influenced by the behaviourist movement in psychology (John B. Watson), which insisted that psychology study only publicly observable behaviour. Russell accepted the behaviourist emphasis on public methods but rejected the elimination of private mental occurrences.

His position is a nuanced one: mental occurrences are real and some are private, but privacy is a causal fact, not a metaphysical barrier.

This position also engaged with the emerging physiological psychology of figures like Cannon and Pavlov. If the emotions, traditionally the paradigm of private mental states, could be studied through their physiological correlates, then privacy was a practical limitation — not a principled impossibility — for psychology.

Russell's account of privacy thus served as a philosophical foundation for the new scientific psychology.
