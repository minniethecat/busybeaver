## Definition

Physics, in Russell's usage, is the science that deals with matter and its changes — "primarily such changes that physics deals with, i.e. it deals primarily with movements of matter" (005-027). Russell contrasts physics with psychology not to reify a dualism of substances, but to clarify the methodological differences between the two sciences. The ultimate aim of his analysis is to show that these differences are not fundamental: the same neutral stuff underlies both the subject matter of physics and the subject matter of psychology, and the apparent distinction is one of relational pattern, not of substance.

## Russell's Account

Russell's engagement with physics runs throughout *The Analysis of Mind* and serves two functions: it provides a model of scientific law, and it defines the boundary against which psychology must be measured.

**(1) The laws of physics as a model.** Russell takes the laws of traditional physics as the paradigm of exact causal law: "The laws of traditional physics, in the form in which they deal with the movements of matter, have the advantage of being comparatively simple and dependable" (005-009). He notes that even these laws are "known to be somewhere near the truth, but are not to be regarded as exact" (005-006) — a recognition of the provisional nature of all scientific knowledge.

**(2) Physics vs. psychology — a methodological distinction.** The key difference Russell identifies is one of standpoint, not of subject matter: "Physics treats of the general properties of pieces of matter... Psychology treats of the particular pieces of matter that are living bodies" (005-030). Physics abstracts away from the peculiarities of any particular piece of matter; psychology focuses on the special causal laws that govern living organisms, particularly mnemic phenomena where past history influences present behavior. "The difference between psychology and physics it is vital to understand" (005-019) — but it is a difference of causal pattern, not of underlying substance.

**(3) The convergence thesis.** Russell's ultimate position is that the laws of physics and psychology are not fundamentally different: "The stuff of which the world of our experience is composed is... neither mind nor matter, but something more primitive than either" (001-007). Both sciences are describing different arrangements of the same neutral particulars. Physics describes the patterns that are common to many perspectives; psychology describes the patterns that are peculiar to individual biographies.

**(4) Physics and matter.** Russell critically examines the concept of matter as defined by physics. "A piece of matter, according to the definition that I propose, is, as a first approximation, the set of all those particulars which would naturally count as its appearances" (005-018). This definition reduces "matter" to a logical construction out of sensed particulars — a view that aligns with the anti-materialistic tendency Russell observes in contemporary physics (P01-002).

**(5) Physics and perception.** Russell notes that "physics assumes the whole world of physics (including its unperceived portions)" (007-001) and that the standpoint of physics is not the only valid one. A complete account of the world must include the perspectives of individual perceivers — which is what psychology provides. But these perspectives are themselves part of the physical world, not something outside it.

## Key Distinctions

- **Physics vs. Psychology**: Physics deals with general patterns common to many pieces of matter; psychology deals with the special causal laws of living organisms. Both are sciences of the same neutral stuff.
- **Physics vs. Matter**: Physics is the science; matter is a logical construction out of the appearances that physics correlates. Russell denies that matter has the "hardness and indestructibility" traditionally attributed to it (001-050).
- **Causal Laws in Physics vs. Mnemic Laws**: Physical laws are (ideally) independent of the history of the system; psychological laws often depend essentially on past events.

## Historical Context

Russell wrote *The Analysis of Mind* at a time of revolutionary change in physics. Einstein's relativity (1915) and the early quantum theory had dismantled the Newtonian picture of matter as solid, indestructible particles. Russell draws on the "anti-materialistic tendency of physics" (P01-002), particularly as articulated by William James and the American new realists, to argue that the traditional dualism of mind and matter is scientifically obsolete. Physics itself, Russell argues, no longer supports the view of matter as fundamentally different from mind. Both are constructions from the same neutral particulars, differentiated only by the patterns of their interrelations.
