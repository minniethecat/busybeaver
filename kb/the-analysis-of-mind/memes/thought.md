## Definition

Thought, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, is a category of mental occurrence that serves as the primary test case for theories of consciousness. Rather than defining thought substantively, Russell analyzes it structurally — decomposing it into the supposed elements of act, content, and object — and concludes that thought does not require a non-physical mind. "By 'thinking' of him… by 'thought' you can be conscious of objects which cannot be seen, such as the human race, or physiology" (001-012). Thought is simply one among several "ways of being conscious," alongside perception, memory, and belief.

## Russell's Account

Russell's treatment of thought is primarily critical. He uses the analysis of thought as a lever to dismantle the traditional conception of mind.

**(1) The tripartite structure of thought.** Meinong analyzed a single thought into three components: the act of thinking, the content (what distinguishes this thought of St. Paul from another thought of St. Paul), and the object (St. Paul himself) (001-020). Russell describes this as "the analysis of a thought into act, content and object" (001-021) and subjects each element to scrutiny.

**(2) The act as superfluous.** Russell's central criticism is that the "act" is empirically undetectable and theoretically unnecessary. "The occurrence of the content of a thought constitutes the occurrence of the thought. Empirically, I cannot discover anything corresponding to the supposed act" (001-022). The act, he suggests, is a grammatical ghost — the shadow cast by the first-person pronoun.

**(3) The content as neutral.** The content of a thought, Russell argues, is not essentially mental. Drawing on American neorealism, he notes that "the same content, exactly the same content, in one group it figures as a thought, in another group as a thing" (001-034). The very same neutral entity can enter into different relations — in one context it is a mental content; in another, a physical object. This is central to Russell's neutral monism.

**(4) The object as external.** The object of a thought is typically something in the outer world — "the object is usually something in the outer world" (001-024) — and is not essentially mental. Even the reference of thoughts to objects, which Brentano took as the defining mark of the mental, Russell argues is "derivative" rather than primitive (001-023).

**(5) Thought without "mind."** Russell endorses a behaviourist conclusion: "There is no more need of 'thought' in this than there is when a man turns round in his sleep" (001-040), referring to the behaviour of animals and human beings in solving problems. Watson's argument — that behaviour can be accounted for without invoking consciousness — applies equally to thought. The word "thought" names a set of observable behavioral and neural patterns, not a ghostly inner process.

**(6) The immediacy claim.** Russell also addresses the Cartesian claim that thought enjoys immediate self-certainty. "Let the case be what it may," he quotes skeptically, the supposed "immediate certainty of thought" (001-035) dissolves under examination. What we take as immediate awareness of our own thinking is itself an inference from memory and linguistic habit.

## Key Distinctions

- **Thought vs. Perception**: In perception, we are "in direct contact with objects" (001-026); in thought, the relation to objects is mediated by content and belief.
- **Thought vs. Memory**: Memory involves a "certain essential relation to the past event" (001-027); thought may have no temporal relation to its object.
- **Thought vs. Language**: Russell, following Watson, treats thought as largely internalized speech — "there is no reason to suppose that anything more is involved" than subvocal verbal behavior (001-041).
- **Content vs. Object**: The content is the mental occurrence; the object is what the thought is about. Russell argues the content is not essentially mental.

## Historical Context

The analysis of thought in terms of act, content, and object derives from the Brentano school (Franz Brentano, Alexius Meinong, Kasimir Twardowski). Russell had engaged deeply with this tradition in his earlier work. His rejection of the framework in 1921 reflects the convergence of several influences: William James's rejection of consciousness as an entity, the American new realists' denial of the subject-object relation as fundamental, and John B. Watson's behaviourist reduction of thinking to subvocal speech. The chapter on thought in *The Analysis of Mind* is a pivotal locus of Russell's transition from idealism to a scientifically-grounded naturalism about the mental.
