## Definition

Subjectivity, in Russell's treatment, is not an irreducible property of a mental substance but a structural feature of how sensory appearances are organized. He defines it operationally: "subjectivity, i.e. of the 'passive' place in the sense defined in Lecture VII" (015-015). This definition ties subjectivity to the concept of a *perspective* — the set of all appearances at a given location in space-time — and makes clear that subjectivity is a matter of how data are grouped, not a mysterious inner quality.

## Russell's Account

Russell develops his account of subjectivity primarily through the physics of perspectives. He argues that "what may be called subjectivity in the point of view is not a peculiar kind of existence" (007-013) — it does not point to a distinct mental substance. Rather, subjectivity is an artifact of how sensory appearances are bundled: "this manner of grouping that subjectivity is defined" (015-039).

The key move in Russell's neutral monism is to treat subjective and objective as different *groupings* of the same neutral stuff, not different *kinds* of stuff. When appearances are grouped by the place from which they are perceived, we get a perspective — a subjective view. When the same appearances are grouped by the object they are appearances *of*, we get a physical object — an objective entity.

Russell acknowledges that subjective experience "is shot through and through with subjectivity" (011-018) in the context of relativistic physics, recognizing that even scientific observation cannot eliminate the perspectival element entirely. However, "thus the conception of subjectivity, i.e. of the 'passive' place" (015-015) is ultimately a technical notion, not an appeal to a ghost in the machine.

## Key Distinctions

Russell's account rests on the distinction between two modes of grouping:
- **Subjective grouping**: Grouping appearances by the passive place where they occur (a perspective). This yields the subjective — the "view from here."
- **Objective grouping**: Grouping the same appearances by the active object they are appearances *of*. This yields the physical — the "view from nowhere."

Subjectivity, in this framework, is not a problem to be eliminated but a structural fact about how data are organized. The "subjective" is simply "this manner of grouping" (015-039) — no more, no less.
