Tendency, in Russell's analysis of [[mind]] and [[behaviourism|behaviour]], is a dispositional concept that plays a central role in explaining [[desire]], [[habit]], [[volition]], and other psychological phenomena without positing immaterial mental entities. Russell treats tendency not as a mental substance or force but as a statistical regularity — a law-like pattern in an organism's behaviour that can be described in probabilistic terms.

## Tendency and the Materialist Programme

Russell situates the concept of tendency within the broader tension between materialistic and anti-materialistic approaches to [[psychology]]. The materialistic tendency in psychology seeks to explain mental phenomena in purely physical terms, while the anti-materialistic tendency resists such reduction. Russell's [[neutral-monism|neutral monism]] is presented as a resolution of this conflict.

> [P01-002] The view that seems to me to reconcile the materialistic tendency of psychology with the anti-materialistic tendency of physics is the view of William James and the American new realists, according to which the "stuff" of the world is neither mental nor material, but a "neutral stuff," out of which both are constructed. I have endeavoured in this work to develop this view in some detail as regards the phenomena with which psychology is concerned.

The concept of tendency itself exemplifies this reconciliation: a behavioural tendency is neither purely physical (it is not a specific neural state) nor purely mental (it is not a conscious intention), but a pattern observable at the level of the whole organism.

## Tendency in Habit Formation

Russell draws on [[John B. Watson|Watson]]'s behaviourist theory of habit formation to illustrate how tendencies emerge from random movements through a process of selection and reinforcement. When an animal makes random movements, only some of which lead to a successful outcome, the successful movements are gradually reinforced, creating a statistical tendency to repeat those movements.

> [002-025] Professor Watson ("Behavior," pp. 262-3) has an ingenious theory as to the way in which habit arises out of random movements. I think there is a reason why his theory cannot be regarded as alone sufficient, but it seems not unlikely that it is partly correct. Suppose, for the sake of simplicity, that there are just ten random movements which may be made by the animal—say, ten paths down which it may go—and that only one of these leads to food, or whatever else represents success in the case in question. In that case, the successful movement will tend to be repeated, and after a number of trials the tendency to repeat the successful movement will be approximately ninety-five per cent.

Russell's partial endorsement of Watson's theory is significant: he accepts that tendencies can be explained mechanistically through repetition and reinforcement, but he insists that this explanation is not complete. The conscious aspect of habit — the feeling of familiarity, the sense of ease — requires additional conceptual resources beyond pure behaviourism.

## Tendency and Desire

The relationship between tendency and [[desire]] is particularly important in Russell's psychology. On the behaviourist view that Russell critically examines, desire is nothing more than a tendency to act in a certain way — there is no inner "feeling" of desire that exists independently of the behavioural disposition. Russell acknowledges the explanatory power of this view while also noting its limitations.

> [003-027] But there is a further point which needs emphasizing, namely, that a belief that something is desired has often a tendency to cause the very desire that is believed in. It is this fact, I think, which makes psycho-analysis useful as a therapeutic method. When a patient becomes aware of a repressed desire, the desire often ceases to exist, or at any rate ceases to be harmful.

This recursive relationship between belief and desire — that believing one has a desire tends to produce or reinforce that desire — complicates any simple behaviourist reduction. The cognitive representation of a tendency can itself modify the tendency, introducing a level of reflexivity that behaviourism struggles to accommodate.

## Tendency as Statistical Law

Russell is careful to specify that tendencies are not deterministic laws but statistical generalizations. A tendency describes what an organism is likely to do under certain conditions, not what it must do. This probabilistic character is what distinguishes the psychological concept of tendency from the physical concept of causal necessity.

> [015-032] In considering the causal laws of psychology, the distinction between rough generalizations and exact laws is important. There are many rough generalizations in psychology, such as that anger tends to produce certain actions, or that hunger tends to produce certain others. These are not exact laws; they are statements of tendency, which hold true in the majority of cases but admit of exceptions.

Russell contrasts these statistical psychological tendencies with the exact laws that might be discovered at the level of [[physiology|nervous system]] processes. The tendency, as a psychological concept, operates at a higher level of abstraction than neural causation and cannot be reduced to it without loss of explanatory power.

> [015-033] If we wish to pass from the law of habit, stated as a tendency or average, to something more precise and invariable, we seem driven to the nervous system. There, it is to be presumed, what goes on is subject to the same exact causal laws as obtain in physics elsewhere. But this does not mean that the psychological law is false; it means only that it is not the whole truth.

## Tendency in the Psychology of Belief

Russell extends the concept of tendency to the analysis of [[belief]]. On the view he attributes to [[William James]], belief does not require any specific feeling or mental act; rather, the mere existence of images or representations, combined with behavioural tendencies, yields everything that is required for belief. The tendency to act upon a proposition — to behave as if it were true — is, on this account, what belief consists in.

> [012-039] If this view is correct, it follows (though James does not draw the inference) that there is no need of any specific feeling called "belief," and that the mere existence of images yields all that is required. The state of mind in which we merely consider a proposition, without believing or disbelieving it, will then appear as a sophisticated product, the result of some rival force adding to the image-proposition a positive feeling which may be called suspense or non-belief—a feeling which may be compared to that of keeping the hands off something which we are tempted to touch.
