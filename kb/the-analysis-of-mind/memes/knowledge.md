# Knowledge

**Knowledge**, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, is reconceived not as a mysterious relation between a mental subject and an object, but as a form of behavioural sensitivity grounded in habit and physiological disposition. This account departs sharply from the traditional epistemological framework, which treated knowledge as an irreducible act of [[consciousness]] directed toward an object. Russell, drawing on the [[behaviourism]] of [[John B. Watson]] and the radical empiricism of [[William James]], argues that knowledge is constituted by causal and mnemic relations in the nervous system rather than by an inner mental act of apprehending a proposition. The analysis connects directly to Russell's treatment of [[belief]] as the vehicle of truth and falsehood, and to the role of [[memory]] and [[sensation-image]] in providing the raw materials from which knowledge is built.

## The Traditional View and Its Problems

Russell begins by identifying the traditional conception of knowledge as a relation between a knowing subject and a known object. On this view, which Russell associates with [[idealism]] and act-psychology, knowledge requires a subject — a "mind" or "consciousness" — that stands over and against the world. This subject-object dichotomy, Russell argues, is a metaphysical assumption without empirical warrant:

> "It is the form which gives 'knowledge' in the strict sense, and it is this form that we shall be concerned to analyse." (001-013)

The difficulty with this traditional picture is that it posits an unobservable mental entity — the "subject" — and then appeals to introspection to justify its existence. Russell's neutral monism aims to eliminate this superfluous postulate. If knowledge can be explained in terms of habits, sensations, images, and causal relations among them, there is no need to invoke a substantial ego or transcendental subject as the bearer of knowledge.

Russell further observes that knowledge has often been treated as a mysterious and unique relation to the world, one that seems to bridge the gap between mind and matter:

> "Knowledge is a form of sensitivity, but it is a form which has been regarded, perhaps rightly, as mysterious." (001-015)

The task of the analysis of mind, then, is to demystify knowledge — to show how it can be constructed from non-cognitive, non-mental materials without presupposing the very thing to be explained.

## Knowledge as Habit: The Behaviourist Account

The central move in Russell's reconstruction of knowledge is the behaviourist thesis that knowledge consists in habits, not in inner mental acts. He endorses the claim that what we call "knowing" is, at bottom, a set of acquired dispositions to respond to stimuli in particular ways:

> "According to the behaviourist view, knowledge is a matter of habits, not of inner mental acts. The behaviourist denies that there is any such thing as a distinctively mental occurrence called 'knowing'." (001-041)

On this account, knowing how to ride a bicycle does not require the presence of any occurrent mental state; it consists in the possession of a complex set of sensorimotor habits. Russell extends this analysis to more abstract forms of knowledge, suggesting that even propositional knowledge — knowing *that* something is the case — can be understood in terms of dispositions to behave, including linguistic behaviour.

This behaviourist reconstruction has a significant implication: knowledge of ourselves is fundamentally no different in kind from knowledge of external objects:

> "Knowledge of ourselves is not fundamentally different from knowledge of external objects. Both are constituted by sensations and the habits they engender." (001-042)

Introspection, traditionally regarded as a privileged and infallible source of self-knowledge, loses its special epistemic status. Russell treats introspection as simply one causal pathway among others by which we acquire information about the world — including that portion of the world which happens to be our own body and its history:

> "Introspection, regarded as a separate source of knowledge, is a misleading concept. What we know of ourselves we know in the same way as what we know of other things." (001-038)

## Sensation, Images, and the Materials of Knowledge

Russell's constructive account traces knowledge back to its raw materials: sensations and images. Sensations provide the primary data for knowledge of the physical world:

> "Sensation furnishes the data for our knowledge of matter. Without sensation, there would be no knowledge of the outer world." (006-003)

But sensation alone is insufficient. Knowledge requires the capacity to retain, combine, and manipulate sensory traces — a function served by images. Russell emphasizes that all knowledge, both practical and theoretical, depends on our store of images:

> "All our knowledge, all our store of images and their combinations, is built up from the data of sensation by means of habit and association." (004-029)

This picture yields a stratified account of knowledge:

| Level | Material | Function |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Sensation | Sensory data from the environment | Provides raw input |
| Image | Faint copies of sensations, stored mnemonically | Enables memory and imagination |
| Habit | Acquired dispositions linking stimuli to responses | Constitutes knowing-how |
| Belief | Images combined with specific feelings or expectations | Carries propositional content |
| Knowledge | True beliefs grounded in causal relations to facts | The endpoint of the cognitive process |

The key point is that each level of this hierarchy can be explained without invoking a "subject" or "act of knowing." Knowledge emerges from the causal interplay of sensations, images, and habits — all of which are, in Russell's neutral monist framework, composed of the same neutral stuff.

## Knowledge and Desire

Russell draws an instructive parallel between knowledge and desire. Both are mental phenomena that have traditionally been conceived as relations between a subject and an object — knowledge relates the subject to a fact, desire relates the subject to a desired state of affairs. Yet both, Russell argues, can be reformulated in behaviourist terms:

> "Desire, like knowledge, is, of course, in one sense a mental phenomenon. But the behaviourist contends that it can be dealt with in terms of behaviour and physiological process." (001-044)

Russell groups knowledge, desire, and feeling together as the three typical mental phenomena that any theory of mind must account for:

> "Mental phenomena are typified by knowledge, desire, and feeling. Any adequate analysis of mind must explain how these three types of phenomenon arise from the materials provided by sensation and image." (003-020)

The parallel is instructive because it underscores the generality of Russell's approach: if desire can be analysed in terms of behaviour cycles (uneasiness, search, satisfaction), then knowledge can be analysed in terms of cognitive habits and sensitivity to evidential relations.

## Analysis and Definition

Russell distinguishes between knowledge as a mental fact — something that occurs in a mind — and knowledge as a behavioural or bodily characteristic. The latter, he suggests, is what survives his analysis:

> "The word 'knowledge' is sometimes used to denote a mental fact, sometimes to denote a bodily characteristic. It is the latter usage that the behaviourist emphasizes, and that we shall find to be the more fundamental." (004-007)

This reorientation has methodological significance. If knowledge is fundamentally a characteristic of an organism's behaviour and its causal relations to the environment, then epistemology becomes a branch of psychology — indeed, a branch of natural science — rather than a domain of a priori philosophical inquiry.

Russell also notes that knowledge has a temporal dimension: our knowledge of the past depends on mnemic causation, the capacity of past events to produce present effects (images, memories, habits) without being present themselves:

> "Definite knowledge of some past event is only possible through the operation of mnemic phenomena — images, memories, and habits that carry the past into the present." (004-011)

This mnemic dimension of knowledge reinforces the behaviourist picture: knowing is not a static relation but a temporally extended causal process, grounded in the plasticity of the nervous system.
