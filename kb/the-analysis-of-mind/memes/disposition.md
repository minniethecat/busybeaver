The concept of [[Disposition|disposition]] is essential to [[Bertrand Russell|Russell's]] [[Behaviourism|behaviourist]] project in *The Analysis of Mind*. A disposition is a latent capacity or tendency — something that exists not as an active occurrence but as a permanent modification of an organism that manifests under appropriate conditions. Russell uses the concept to analyze [[Belief|belief]], [[Memory|memory]], [[Desire|desire]], and [[Habit|habit]] without invoking irreducible mental acts.

## Disposition as Latent Capacity

Russell defines disposition by contrast with active occurrence. A disposition is a state that persists even when it is not manifesting:

> [004-027] … merely what may be called a "disposition," i.e. it is capable of being…

The key feature of a disposition is its latency. A piece of iron is disposed to rust even when dry; a trained dog is disposed to sit on command even when standing. The disposition is real — it is a structural property of the organism or object — but it is not a current event. Russell applies this same logic to mental phenomena (012-033).

## Dispositional Theory of Belief

Russell's most significant application of the disposition concept is to [[Belief|belief]]. He distinguishes between active belief and dispositional belief:

> [012-032] …ive belief, not the permanent disposition. Now, what are the occasions …

Active belief is the occurrent judgment — the mental event of assenting to a proposition. But most of our beliefs are not active at any given moment. I believe that Paris is the capital of France even when I am not thinking about it. This "permanent" aspect of belief, Russell argues, is best understood as a disposition — a modification of the organism that produces the active belief under appropriate circumstances.

The dispositional account allows Russell to explain belief without positing a [[Mind|mind]]-substance that "holds" beliefs. Beliefs are not stored in a mental repository; they are embodied in the [[Nervous System|nervous system]] as dispositions to behave and judge in certain ways (004-027).

## Disposition and Action

Russell extends the dispositional analysis to [[Desire|desire]] and volition:

> [012-033] …xists actively (not as a mere disposition) without producing any volunt…

A desire that does not lead to action is still real — it exists as a disposition. The dispositional account explains why we can have desires that never manifest in behaviour: the disposition is present but the triggering conditions are not met.

Similarly, [[Habit|habits]] are dispositions acquired through repetition. A habit is not a current event but a permanent modification of the organism that produces characteristic responses under appropriate stimuli — a concept closely related to [[Mnemic Causation|mnemic causation]].

## Disposition vs. Mental Act

Russell's appeal to dispositions is part of his broader rejection of mental acts. Where [[Franz Brentano|Brentano]] and [[Alexius Meinong|Meinong]] would posit an "act" of believing or desiring, Russell substitutes a disposition:

The mental act is replaced by a physiological disposition — a modification of nervous tissue that tends to produce certain behaviours and conscious occurrences under certain conditions. This is Russell's [[Neutral Monism|neutral monist]] solution: the "mind" is not a thing that acts but a collection of dispositions organized in a certain way.

## Philosophical Significance

The concept of disposition allows Russell to navigate between the Scylla of reductive materialism and the Charybdis of dualism. Dispositions are real — they are structural properties of physical systems — but they are not "material" in the sense of being current physical events. They occupy a middle ground that permits a scientifically respectable psychology without eliminating the phenomena that make mind distinctive.
