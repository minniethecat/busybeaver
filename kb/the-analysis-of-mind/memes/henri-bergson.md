Henri Bergson (1859–1941) was a French philosopher whose theories of creative evolution, duration, and intuition exerted considerable influence in the early twentieth century. In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell engages with Bergson critically, referencing his views on [[instinct]] and [[voluntary-movement]] while ultimately rejecting the Bergsonian approach to the mind–body problem.

## Bergson on Voluntary Action

Russell's most sustained engagement with Bergson concerns the question of whether the movements of living bodies are reducible to physical causation. Bergson held that voluntary action involves a distinctive kind of causation that cannot be captured by the laws governing dead matter. Russell uses Bergson's position as a foil for his own methodological neutrality:

> [001-051] When a man falls from a precipice or slips on a piece of orange peel, his body behaves as if it were devoid of life. These are the occasions that make Bergson laugh. But when a man's bodily movements are what we call "voluntary," they are, at any rate prima facie, very different in their laws from the movements of what is devoid of life.

Russell is sympathetic to Bergson's observation that living bodies appear to behave differently from dead matter, but he does not accept Bergson's conclusion that this difference is irreducible. Instead, Russell treats the question as an open empirical one, comparing psychology's developmental stage to the state of gas physics before Boyle's law.

## Bergson and the Fallibility of Instinct

Russell criticizes Bergson's treatment of [[instinct]], particularly his reliance on the entomological observations of Jean-Henri Fabre. Bergson had used Fabre's accounts of the Ammophila wasp — which allegedly stings its caterpillar prey with unerring precision — to argue for the extraordinary accuracy of instinct. Russell cites contrary evidence:

> [002-032] "According to Fabre's observations, which Bergson accepts, the Ammophila stings its prey EXACTLY and UNERRINGLY in EACH of the nervous centres."

Russell then quotes Drever's rebuttal, which shows that the Peckhams' observations contradicted Fabre's account: the wasp's sting is not unerring, the number of stings is not constant, and the larva survives regardless. Russell draws a broader methodological lesson:

> [002-034] This illustrates how love of the marvellous may mislead even so careful an observer as Fabre and so eminent a philosopher as Bergson.

## Methodological Significance

Russell's treatment of Bergson serves two functions in *The Analysis of Mind*. First, it illustrates his methodological principle that the laws governing living bodies should be studied independently, without "undue haste to subordinate them to the laws of physics" (001-051). Second, it demonstrates the danger of accepting extraordinary claims about instinct and mental phenomena without rigorous empirical verification.

Bergson's presence in the text is emblematic of Russell's broader strategy: engaging with influential contemporaries whose views he partially acknowledges while ultimately subordinating their insights to his [[neutral-monism|neutral monist]] framework. The Bergsonian vital impulse and intuition are replaced by Russell's account of [[mnemic-causation]] and the analysis of mental phenomena into sensations, images, and habits.
