## Definition

In Russell's analysis of mind, an "appearance" is the sensory data presented at a particular place by an object, distinct from the physical object itself. Russell defines it precisely: "I shall give the name of 'appearance of that object in that place'" to the data that constitute the sensible presence of an object at a given location (005-022). Appearances are not subjective illusions but the actual constituents of what we call physical objects — "the 'real' table underlying its appearances is, in any case, not itself perceived, but inferred" (005-011).

## Russell's Account

Russell uses the concept of appearances to dissolve the traditional distinction between appearance and reality. He observes that "the visual appearance is filled out with feeling of what we should touch if we put our hand where it seems to be" (004-010), illustrating how appearances are multimodal and actively constructed. The common-sense notion of a physical object is, for Russell, a logical construction from the system of its appearances: "common sense regards the 'real' table as the common cause of all the appearances which the table presents" (005-009).

Crucially, Russell notes that "when I speak of 'appearances,' I do so only for brevity" (005-018) — he is not reifying appearances as a separate ontological category but using the term as shorthand for the sensory data at a given perspective. The relationship between object and appearance is causal and perspectival, not representational: "the changes in appearances of an object which are due to the intervening medium can be distinguished from those due to the object itself" (005-028).

Russell treats "the whole system of appearances of a piece of matter" as a unified physical phenomenon (005-029). This holistic treatment of appearances anticipates his neutral monism, where neither mind nor matter is fundamental, and both are constructed from the same neutral stuff — sensory appearances being the primary data.

## Key Distinctions

Russell distinguishes:
- **Appearance vs. object**: The appearance is the sensory datum at a perspective; the object is the logical construction from the entire system of such appearances (005-009, 005-022).
- **Simultaneous appearances**: Appearances of different objects at a single place vs. appearances of a single object at different places (005-016–005-017) — the former defines a perspective, the latter defines the object.
- **Real vs. apparent change**: "Cases in which only certain appearances of the object change, while others, especially those in neighbouring places, do not" (005-024) indicate local conditions, while changes "where all, or almost all, the appearances of the object undergo a connected alteration" (005-025) indicate genuine object-level change.
