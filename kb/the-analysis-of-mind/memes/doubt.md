## Definition

Doubt is a mental attitude of suspended judgment — a state in which the mind neither fully assents to nor rejects a proposition. Russell treats doubt as one of the cognitive attitudes, alongside belief and perception, that must be accommodated in any adequate analysis of mind (003-020). Unlike simple sensation, which is non-cognitive, doubt is propositional: it takes a content that could be true or false, but withholds the feeling of assent that characterizes belief.

## Russell's Account

Russell does not devote a separate lecture to doubt, but the concept permeates his method. *The Analysis of Mind* is itself the product of "a long course of philosophic doubt" (007-022), and Russell repeatedly positions doubt as the engine of his neutral monist project.

**(1) Methodological doubt.** Russell's starting point is doubt about common-sense dualism — the idea that mind and matter are fundamentally different kinds of stuff. He doubts that we can know "of the existence of a world outside ourselves" by direct acquaintance, treating this as an inference and "in fact a very doubtful one" (001-039). The neutral monist position emerges precisely from doubting both the Cartesian certainty of consciousness and the materialist certainty of matter.

**(2) Doubt and belief.** Russell distinguishes doubt from both belief and disbelief. "Belief in the existence of things outside my own biography exists antecedently to evidence, and can only be destroyed, if at all, by a long course of philosophic doubt" (007-022). This frames doubt as the *erosion* of a pre-existing belief — not a positive state in its own right, but the result of weakening the feeling of assent through systematic questioning. For purposes of science, he argues, doubt is preferable to unwarranted certainty (007-022).

**(3) Practical vs. theoretical doubt.** Russell acknowledges a tension between theoretical doubt and practical certainty. We cannot "practically doubt that we got up this morning" (009-017), yet on theoretical grounds the certainty of memory-belief can be questioned. In practice, he notes, "there can be no doubt that both forms" — images and sensations as sources of memory — "exist" (009-021). Russell consistently distinguishes the practical necessity of belief from its theoretical justification.

**(4) Doubt as a cognitive attitude.** In his taxonomy of mental phenomena, Russell groups doubt alongside beliefs, perceptions, and "the understanding of concepts" (003-020). This places doubt in the class of propositional attitudes — mental states that take a content and adopt a stance toward it — distinguishing it from non-cognitive sensations and images.

## Key Distinctions

- **Doubt vs. Disbelief**: Disbelief is a positive rejection (a belief in the negation); doubt is the suspension of judgment.
- **Doubt vs. Sensation**: Sensation is non-cognitive and non-propositional; doubt is cognitive and propositional.
- **Methodological vs. Practical Doubt**: Methodological doubt serves philosophical analysis; practical doubt would paralyze daily life.
- **Doubt vs. Scepticism**: Scepticism is a settled doctrine; doubt is an occurrent mental attitude.

## Historical Context

Russell's use of doubt follows the Cartesian tradition of methodological scepticism, but with an important difference: where Descartes doubted in order to find an indubitable foundation (the *cogito*), Russell doubts the foundation itself. The very certainty of consciousness — Descartes's Archimedean point — is what Russell places under systematic doubt (007-022). This aligns Russell more closely with Hume's empiricist scepticism, though Russell's aim is constructive: to replace the doubted dualism with a viable neutral monism, not to end in sceptical paralysis.
