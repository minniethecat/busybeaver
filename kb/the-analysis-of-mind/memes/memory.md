## Definition

Memory, in Russell's analysis, is a form of knowledge distinguished from both sensation and perception. Unlike sensation, memory is genuinely cognitive — it involves beliefs about the past. Russell defines memory-belief as a present occurrence involving images accompanied by two distinctive feelings: familiarity (which grounds confidence in accuracy) and pastness (which locates the remembered event in time relative to the present). Crucially, memory must be analysed without positing a "subject" or "act" that directly apprehends the past.

## Russell's Account

Russell treats memory early in his analysis because "it seems to be involved in the fact that images are recognized as 'copies' of past sensible experience" (009-003). The analysis of memory is part of his broader project of analysing knowledge without the traditional apparatus of subject and object.

Russell first establishes that "everything constituting a memory-belief is happening now, not in that past time to which the belief is said to refer" (009-004). He notes the logical possibility that the world sprang into being five minutes ago with apparent memories — an uninteresting but analytically useful sceptical hypothesis that forces us to examine what, in the present, constitutes memory (009-004–005).

The core of Russell's positive account concerns images. Memory-images, he argues, arrive with two sorts of feelings: (1) familiarity, which grounds our trust in their accuracy, and (2) a feeling of greater or less remoteness, which allows temporal placement (009-009–013). The feeling of familiarity is "a feeling capable of degrees" (009-009); when some parts of a memory-image feel more familiar than others, we become critical of our memory's accuracy.

Russell distinguishes memory from mere habit. "Images without beliefs are insufficient to constitute memory; and habits are still more insufficient" (009-006). The behaviourist cannot eliminate memory because even recording observations requires trusting one's own memory — behaviourism implicitly presupposes what it tries to explain away.

If the traditional "subject" or "act" were retained, the problem of memory would be simple: remembering would be a direct relation between the present act and the past event (009-015). Russell's rejection of the subject makes the analysis harder but more honest. Memory must be constituted entirely out of present occurrences — images, feelings, and beliefs — without any mysterious direct access to the past.

## Key Distinctions

- **Memory vs. Habit**: Habit involves similar events at different times without belief; memory essentially involves belief about the past.
- **Memory vs. Perception**: Perception fills out sensation with habitual associates from past experience, but does not involve recollection of that past experience.
- **Memory-belief vs. Memory-image**: An image alone is not memory; it becomes memory only when accompanied by the appropriate belief ("this occurred") and feelings of familiarity and pastness.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of memory engages with the behaviourist challenge (Watson) while insisting on the irreducibility of knowledge. His analysis is part of the broader project of *The Analysis of Mind* — showing how apparently "mental" phenomena can be analysed without positing consciousness as an entity, while not reducing everything to mere behaviour.
