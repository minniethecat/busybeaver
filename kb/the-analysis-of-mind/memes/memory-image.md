## Definition

A memory-image, in Russell's analysis, is an image that occurs in the context of genuine memory: it is accompanied by a distinctive feeling of pastness and by a memory-belief — the conviction that the image refers to something actually experienced in the past. Russell writes that "memory-images, when they occur in true memory" are the carriers of our knowledge of the past (009-007). Unlike a mere imagination-image, the memory-image brings with it a specific kind of confidence: "the feeling of belief such as distinguishes memory-images from mere imagination-images" (009-035).

## Russell's Account

Russell devotes Lecture IX to the analysis of memory, with the memory-image serving as a central explanatory concept. His treatment is notable for approaching memory without assuming a knowing subject who compares a present image with a past occurrence. Instead, Russell analyses the difference between a memory-image and other kinds of images entirely in terms of the feelings and beliefs that accompany them.

The memory-image occupies an intermediate position between sensation and pure imagination. "Between memory-image and sensation there is an intermediate" experience — the memory-image is not as vivid or compelling as a sensation, but it carries more conviction than a mere imagination-image (009-033). The key differentiator is not the image's intrinsic quality but the "memory-belief" that accompanies it. "Memory-belief confers upon the memory-image something which we may call meaning"; it is this belief that refers the image to the past (009-042).

Russell identifies two essential characteristics of memory-images. The first is a feeling of familiarity: "the environment brings up the memory-image, but it does not explain the feeling of recognition" that accompanies it (009-041). This feeling is not itself explained by the image's accuracy — "confidence in the accuracy of a memory-image must, in fundamental cases, be based upon" something other than comparison with the original, since the original is by definition no longer present (009-009). The second characteristic is the feeling of pastness: the memory-image carries with it a "feeling of reality, related to the memory-image, and referred to the past by the memory-belief" (009-059).

Importantly, Russell argues that memory-images would not be said to occur at all without the accompanying memory-belief. "Memory-images would not be said to occur" in cases where one has an image of a past event but does not recognise it as such (009-058). The image alone is not sufficient for memory; what "concerns us" is the combination of image plus belief plus feeling of pastness. Russell uses the term memory as "a vague term, covering the memory-image and anything very like it" (009-065), underscoring that the concept is one of degree, not of kind.

Russell also explores the relationship between memory-images and abstract or general ideas. He describes how repeated experiences of similar objects can produce "an abstract memory-image of the different appearances" — a generic image that represents not a single past occurrence but a type (011-009). This abstract memory-image is "analogous to that in which a memory-image differs from an imagination-image" (011-010), suggesting that the mechanisms of memory and abstraction are continuous with each other.

On the question of animal memory, Russell is cautious but open. "There is no reason why memory-images, accompanied by that very simple and primitive feeling of pastness," should not occur in animals that lack language (012-024). This is consistent with his broader behaviourist approach: if memory-imagery is a bodily occurrence with characteristic causal properties, there is no need to invoke language or higher cognitive faculties to explain it.

## Key Distinctions

- **Memory-Image vs. Sensation**: Sensation belongs to the present, with an external stimulus; memory-images lack the vividness and the direct external cause of sensation (009-033).
- **Memory-Image vs. Imagination-Image**: The memory-image is distinguished from the imagination-image by the memory-belief — the feeling that the image refers to an actual past event (009-035, 009-042). Without this belief, the image would be mere imagination.
- **Memory-Image vs. Abstract Memory-Image**: An abstract memory-image is a generic or schematic image formed from multiple similar past experiences; a concrete memory-image refers to a single past occurrence (011-009).

## Criticism

Russell acknowledges a limitation in his account: the feeling of familiarity that accompanies memory-images is not itself explained by the causal theory. "The environment brings up the memory-image, but it does not explain the feeling of recognition" (009-041). This is one of the points where Russell's behaviourist framework encounters a phenomenon — the qualitative feel of familiarity — that resists purely causal analysis. He treats this feeling as a brute fact about certain images, not as evidence for a substantial self or subject.
