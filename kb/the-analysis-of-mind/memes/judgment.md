## Definition

Judgment is the mental act in which something is acknowledged or affirmed, as opposed to being merely presented or entertained. Brentano distinguished presentation from judgment: "in presentation something is presented, in judgment something is acknowledged or rejected" (001-016). In Russell's own account, judgment is a species of [[Belief]] — specifically, belief that takes propositional form and can be expressed in words or images.

## Russell's Account

Russell treats judgment primarily in connection with two topics: recognition and general ideas.

**Judgment in Recognition.** When we encounter something familiar, we may have a mere feeling of familiarity without any definite cognitive content. Recognition proper, however, involves a belief or judgment: "it is a belief or judgment, which the sense of familiarity is not" (009-025). This recognition-judgment can take the form "This has existed before" — a belief about the past that is more than mere familiarity yet less than full episodic memory (009-025).

**Judgment and General Ideas.** Russell appeals to judgment to explain how vague images become general concepts. A general idea, he argues, "is distinguished from a vague one by the presence of a judgment" (011-012). The judgment supplies the logical articulation that a mere image lacks: it specifies what the image is being used to represent and how it relates to other concepts.

**Judgments of Perception.** The most basic class of judgments are "judgments of perception" (012-015). When you hear a noise and think "tram," the noise (sensation) and the word or image "tram" are both constituents of the belief. These perceptual judgments add an interpretative element to bare sensation, linking present stimuli to past experience and future expectations.

## Key Distinctions

- **Judgment vs. mere consideration**: A proposition can be either believed or merely considered; the content is the same in both cases, but judgment involves an additional element — the feeling of assent — that distinguishes it from neutral contemplation (012-034).
- **Judgment vs. bare familiarity**: Familiarity is a non-cognitive feeling; judgment adds the cognitive dimension of affirming that something is the case (009-025).
- **Image-judgment vs. word-judgment**: Image-propositions are more primitive than word-propositions and "may well ante-date language" (012-024). Our most elementary beliefs, including those added to sensation to make perception, often remain at the image level.

## Historical Context

The analysis of judgment has been central to philosophy since Aristotle. Brentano's distinction between presentation and judgment was influential on early analytic philosophy. Russell's treatment in *The Analysis of Mind* reflects his broader project of reducing apparently mentalistic categories to combinations of simpler elements — sensations, images, and the belief-feeling — without positing a substantial self or consciousness that performs the judging.
