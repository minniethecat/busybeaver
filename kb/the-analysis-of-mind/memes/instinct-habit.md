Russell draws a fundamental distinction between **instinct** and **habit** as the two primary sources of human and animal behaviour in Lecture II of _The Analysis of Mind_. This distinction is central to his [[Behaviourism|behaviouristic]] account of [[Psychology|psychology]], which seeks to explain mental phenomena without appealing to non-physical entities. Instincts are innate, unlearned vital movements, while habits are acquired through experience and repetition. Russell argues that this distinction, though subject to borderline cases, is indispensable for understanding how minds develop from simple biological foundations.

## The Definition of Instinct

Russell defines an instinctive movement as "a vital movement performed by an animal the first time that it finds itself in a novel situation" (002-020). The movement must serve a biological purpose -- hunting, escaping, mating -- and must occur without prior learning. This definition is deliberately behaviouristic: it characterizes instinct by observable action rather than by introspective feeling or mental accompaniment.

Instincts need not be fully formed at birth. Russell notes that "many instincts mature gradually, and while they are immature an animal may act in a bungling manner" (002-022), making it difficult in practice to distinguish immature instinct from early learning. Nevertheless, the conceptual distinction remains clear.

## The Definition of Habit

A habit, by contrast, is a movement that is "learnt" or embodies a "habit" if it is "due to previous experience which that animal has had" (002-021). Habits arise through the repetition of actions that prove successful. Russell accepts the behaviourist account that "habit arises out of random movements" (002-025) -- an animal tries various actions, and those that lead to satisfaction are reinforced while others fade.

Russell observes that through practice, habits can become as automatic as instinct:

> "Habits become so automatic that they are performed without prevision of their end, just as if they were instinctive." (002-023)

This makes the boundary between instinct and habit blurry in adult organisms, though the distinction of origin -- innate versus acquired -- remains valid.

## Five Characteristics of Instinct

Russell enumerates five defining characteristics of instinct (002-038 through 002-042), which together provide a behaviouristic profile of innate action:

| Characteristic | Explanation |
|---|---|
| No prevision required | Instinct achieves its biological end without the animal foreseeing that end |
| Narrow adaptation | Instinct is adapted only to achieve ends that occur regularly in the species' environment |
| Habit takeover | Processes initiated by instinct often come to be performed better by acquired habits |
| Impulse supply | Instinct supplies the impulses that drive experimental or learned behaviour |
| Nascent fallibility | Instincts in nascent stages are easily misled and may produce maladaptive responses |

These five points collectively show that instinct is not the infallible inner guide posited by popular psychology. Russell explicitly criticizes the popular view: "The popular conception of instinct errs by imagining it to be infallible" (002-031). Instincts are rough biological adaptations that function adequately in the environment in which the species evolved but break down under novel conditions.

## The Continuity Between Instinct and Habit

Despite drawing a sharp conceptual distinction, Russell emphasizes the continuity between instinct and habit in actual behaviour. He gives the example of pecking in chicks:

> "The hen teaches her chicks to peck, but the impulse to peck is instinctive. Here we have a good illustration of an instinct growing wiser through experience." (002-037)

This continuity means that in the behaviour of complex organisms, most actions involve a blend of instinctive impulse and habitual refinement. The chick's pecking instinct provides the raw drive; experience and learning make the behaviour more precise and effective.

Russell extends this analysis to human beings, arguing that "what is true of animals, as regards instinct and habit, is equally true of men" (002-030). Human [[Knowledge|knowledge]] itself, on his account, is largely a matter of acquired habits rather than a separate mental substance.

## Relation to the Analysis of Mind

The instinct-habit distinction underpins Russell's broader project of analysing mind without appealing to [[Consciousness|consciousness]] as a fundamental category. If human behaviour can be explained through the interplay of innate instincts and acquired habits, then the appeal to an inner mental theatre becomes theoretically superfluous. Instinct provides the biological foundation; habit explains the acquisition of complex skills, language, and even thought.

This framework connects directly to Russell's accounts of [[Desire and Feeling|desire-feeling]], [[Belief|belief]], and [[Memory|memory]] -- each of which he analyses in terms of behavioural dispositions rather than introspectively accessible mental states.
