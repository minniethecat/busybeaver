## Definition

Evidence, in Russell's analysis of mind, is whatever justifies a belief — what turns a mere psychological occurrence into something properly called knowledge. Russell distinguishes two principal forms: the direct evidence of sense-experience, and the reflective evidence of logical coherence and self-evidence. "What sort of evidence is there?" (009-003) is the question that drives his inquiry into memory, belief, and the foundations of knowledge.

## Russell's Account

Russell treats evidence as the bridge between psychology (how beliefs arise) and epistemology (why beliefs are justified). His account in *The Analysis of Mind* is fragmentary but structured around several key themes.

**(1) Evidence and memory.** Russell raises the question of evidence most acutely in his discussion of memory. "What sort of evidence is there?" for memory-beliefs, he asks, noting that the image alone does not logically guarantee the past event (009-003). "It is not logically necessary to the existence of a memory-belief that the event remembered should have occurred" (009-004). This means evidence for memory must come from somewhere other than the mere feeling of familiarity.

**(2) Self-evidence.** In a sustained discussion in Lecture XIII, Russell examines self-evidence as one of two traditional criteria for knowledge: "(1) self-evidence, (2) mutual coherence" (013-022). He defines self-evidence as a feeling that accompanies certain beliefs — a felt obviousness. However, he is sceptical about its reliability: "Self-evidence must not consist merely in the fact that we believe a proposition" (013-024). The feeling of self-evidence can be explained by past experience and habit rather than by the truth of the proposition; "thus the feeling of self-evidence, in this case, seems explicable by the laws of association" (013-025). In logic and mathematics, self-evidence "lies merely in the fact that they are part of our psychological constitution" (013-026).

**(3) Evidence and animal behaviour.** Russell considers the behaviourist challenge to evidence-based accounts of mind. He quotes evidence suggesting "that birds do not LEARN to build nests by watching their parents" (002-022), and notes that "seeking food is not sufficient evidence from which to conclude that the animal has 'consciousness'" (003-016). This use of evidence cuts both ways — Russell uses it to argue that mentalistic inferences about animals are underdetermined by behavioural data.

**(4) Evidence in physiology.** Russell acknowledges that physiology provides evidence relevant to the analysis of mind, but cautions against over-interpretation. "No doubt physiology, especially the diseases of the brain, affords evidence as to the dependence of mind on the brain" (004-021), yet he warns that "so far as I am aware, [there is] no good evidence that every difference between mental states corresponds to a difference in brain states" (004-034).

## Key Distinctions

- **Evidence vs. Belief**: Belief is a psychological occurrence; evidence is the epistemic ground that may justify it.
- **Self-evidence vs. Coherence**: Two rival criteria for knowledge — one internal and psychological, the other external and logical.
- **Evidence vs. Cause**: A belief may be caused by habit or association without being supported by evidence.
- **Direct vs. Indirect Evidence**: Sense-experience provides direct evidence; memory and testimony provide indirect evidence.

## Historical Context

Russell's treatment of evidence in 1921 reflects his post-foundationalist epistemology. Having abandoned the Russellian sense-data foundationalism of *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912), he now treats evidence as a psychological feeling rather than a metaphysical guarantee. His scepticism about self-evidence aligns with the pragmatist tradition (James, Dewey) and anticipates later work in naturalized epistemology, where evidence is analysed as part of the causal order rather than as a transcendent justification.
