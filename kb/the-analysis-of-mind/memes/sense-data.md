## Definition

Russell introduces the sense-datum as a technical term to mark a distinction within the act of perception: the immediate object present to the senses is "physical, and may be called the sense-datum, to distinguish it from the sensation" (008-008). A sensation, on Russell's analysis, is a mental event; the sense-datum is what that event is *of*—the colour-patch, the sound, the texture—considered as an object rather than as an experience.

## Russell's Account

Russell deploys the sense-datum concept to resolve an ambiguity in traditional empiricism. When we speak of seeing a colour, we may mean either the physiological process of sensing or the colour itself as an object of awareness. Russell insists on keeping these apart: the sensation is a mental particular, while the sense-datum is the physical particular that sensation presents.

However, Russell does not treat this distinction as final. He acknowledges that "the reason for distinguishing the sense-datum from the sensation disappears" (008-009) once certain philosophical assumptions are abandoned—specifically, the assumption that sensation involves a subject acting upon a separate object. In his neutral monist framework, what we call sensation and what we call sense-datum may turn out to be different logical constructions out of the same neutral material, rather than fundamentally different kinds of entity.

Beyond visual and auditory sense-data, Russell notes that emotions can involve "changes in the viscera or by sensible objects" (014-013), extending the concept of the sense-datum into the domain of bodily awareness. This foreshadows his modified version of the James–Lange theory of [[Emotion|emotion]].

## Key Distinctions

### Sense-Datum vs. Sensation

The core distinction: the sense-datum is objective (the red patch, the noise), while the sensation is subjective (the experience of red, the hearing of the noise). Russell treats the sense-datum as "physical" (008-008) in a broad sense—it belongs to the world of objects, not to the private theatre of consciousness. But this objectivity is provisional; in the fuller neutral-monist account, both sense-data and sensations are composed of the same neutral particulars.

### Sense-Data and Perception

Russell's treatment of sense-data connects to his broader theory of [[Perception|perception]]. Whereas sensations "are subject to both kinds of laws" (001-037)—physical and psychological—sense-data are purely physical. The act of perceiving involves a causal chain: physical object → sense-datum → sensation → belief or knowledge. By isolating the sense-datum as the first link in the psychological chain, Russell provides an analysis that does not require the mind to reach out and grasp external objects directly.
