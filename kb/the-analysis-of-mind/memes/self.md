## Definition

The self, in Russell's neutral monism, is not a substantive entity but a logical construction. Russell rejects the traditional view — which he himself once held — that the essence of mind consists in "the theory that the essence of everything mental is a certain quite peculiar something called 'consciousness'" (001-002). The self, or subject, is the ghost that remains after consciousness is exorcised: "the 'act' is the ghost of the subject, or what once was the full-blooded soul" (001-022). Russell's analysis aims to show that "the apparent indubitability of self-knowledge is a mere illusion" and holds only "as against the knowledge of other things" (002-005).

## Russell's Account

Russell's treatment of the self unfolds across several lectures of *The Analysis of Mind*. In Lecture I, he introduces the problem by noting that the traditional dualism of subject and object is "supposed to be fundamental in the mental world" — the subject knowing, the object known (001-033). Russell's neutral monism dissolves this dualism: in a case of knowledge, the relation itself is a part of pure experience, and "one of its 'terms' becomes the subject or bearer of the knowledge, the other becomes the object known" (001-032).

The key move in Russell's argument is the rejection of immediate self-knowledge. While "some think we can know them by an immediate self-knowledge which does not depend upon observation of their bodily manifestations," Russell counters that this claim dissolves under scrutiny (001-044). In Lecture II, he argues that the apparent advantages of self-knowledge are largely real but specific: "so far as knowledge of detached facts is concerned, the advantage is on the side of self-knowledge as against external observation" (002-004). But this advantage is limited — "when we pass to the scientific understanding of the facts, the advantages on the side of self-knowledge become far less clear" (002-005).

Russell identifies "self-deception" as a central problem for any theory that treats the self as transparent to introspection. "What are commonly regarded as showing self-deception" in psycho-analysis demonstrates that we lack direct access to our own motives (003-002). He reframes self-deception in causal terms: "what is called self-deception arises through the fact that we mistake the imagined object of a desire for its real object" (003-030). The individual may be "quite unconscious" of desires that nonetheless govern behaviour, and "the man himself cannot tell you what he desires" until he faces "the facts about himself" (001-048).

Russell's conclusion is that the self is a derivative notion — a logical construction from particular mental occurrences, not a datum. The "stream of thinking" that Descartes took as indubitable is, for Russell, "a series of events, not a single persistent entity" (001-035). The self that remains after analysis is a convenient abbreviation for a bundle of causal relations among particulars, not a substance.

## Key Distinctions

- **Self as Subject vs. Self as Construction**: Traditional philosophy treats the self as the knowing subject standing over against objects; Russell treats it as a logical construction from particular occurrences (001-032–033).
- **Self-Knowledge vs. External Observation**: Self-knowledge provides better access to detached facts about one's own mental life, but external observation is superior for scientific understanding and generalisation (002-004–005).
- **Conscious vs. Unconscious Motives**: The self does not have transparent access to its own desires; unconscious motives, revealed through behaviour and psycho-analysis, are causal laws of our actions that we fail to acknowledge (001-047–048).
- **Self-Deception as Misdirected Desire**: What appears as self-deception is not a special faculty of the ego but a mismatch between the real object of a desire and the imagined object we attribute to ourselves (003-029–030).

## Historical Context

Russell's dissolution of the self draws on multiple sources. From David Hume, he inherits the bundle theory — the idea that the self is a collection of perceptions, not a substance. From William James, he takes the notion of the "stream of consciousness" as a series of events rather than a persistent ego. From Freudian psycho-analysis, he borrows the concept of unconscious motives, which undermines the Cartesian claim of privileged self-access. Russell's innovation is to combine these strands within the framework of neutral monism, where "the stuff of which the world of our experience is composed is, in my belief, neither mind nor matter, but something more primitive than either" — making both self and object constructions from the same neutral material (001-032).
