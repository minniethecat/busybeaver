# Time

**Time**, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, is not treated as a metaphysical absolute — as it is in the philosophies of [[Isaac Newton]] or [[Immanuel Kant]] — but as a dimension of [[consciousness|conscious]] experience whose structure is revealed by the analysis of [[memory]] and [[expectation]]. Russell's approach is resolutely empirical: instead of asking what time *is* in itself, he asks how temporal relations — past, present, future — manifest in the psychology of an organism. The answer draws on the same [[mnemic-causation|mnemic]] framework that underlies his accounts of memory, [[belief]], and [[learning]]: the past is that which operates through mnemic traces, the future is that toward which expectations are directed, and the present is the nexus of sensation and action.

## Time as a Psychological Problem

Russell explicitly distances himself from the metaphysical tradition in which time is treated as an absolute container or a form of intuition. He approaches time through the lens of what organisms actually do — how they are affected by the past and how they anticipate the future:

> "It is now time to pass on to other modern tendencies, which have arisen partly as a reaction against the excessive simplicity of earlier behaviourism." (001-037)

Though this passage is a rhetorical transition, it signals Russell's methodological commitment: the analysis of time, like the analysis of mind itself, must begin from observable behaviour and its causal conditions, not from a priori reflection.

Russell's analysis distinguishes three temporal dimensions as they appear in experience:

| Temporal Dimension | Psychological Correlate | Causal Basis |
|--------------------|------------------------|--------------|
| Past | [[Memory]] | Mnemic causation — past events leave traces that affect present behaviour |
| Present | [[Sensation]] | Current physical stimulus acting on sense-organs |
| Future | [[Expectation]] | Learned associations that project current patterns forward |

This tripartite structure is not derived from an abstract "intuition of time" but from the different causal relations that connect an organism to events at different temporal positions.

## Memory and the Sense of the Past

The sense of the past — our awareness that some events are behind us — is rooted in the special character of [[memory]] as a mnemic phenomenon. When we remember, we are not merely entertaining an image that happens to correspond to a past event; the image carries a feeling of pastness from its causal history:

> "Your dreams and memories and thoughts, of which no one else is directly aware, are mental facts." (006-003)

What distinguishes a [[memory-image]] from a mere [[imagination]] image is precisely that the memory-image is caused, through a continuous mnemic chain, by the past event it represents. The "pastness" of the memory is not an intrinsic quality of the image — an image is just an image — but a relational property grounded in the causal history of the organism. This means our sense of the past is fallible: we can have images that feel like memories but are not.

## Expectation and Orientation Toward the Future

If memory is the psychological correlate of the past, [[expectation]] is the correlate of the future. Russell analyzes expectation behaviouristically, as a disposition to act conditionally on anticipated events:

> "The first time a bird builds a nest, we can hardly suppose that it has an 'idea' of the nest it is going to build." (001-039)

This example illustrates Russell's rejection of the view that expectation requires explicit mental representation of future states. An organism can be oriented toward the future — can act in ways only intelligible in relation to future outcomes — without entertaining a propositional thought about that future. The bird's nest-building is future-directed because its behaviour is shaped by mnemic mechanisms selected to produce beneficial outcomes.

Russell extends this to human expectation, arguing that even our most sophisticated anticipations are continuous with simpler future-directed animal behaviours. The difference is one of complexity, not of kind.

## The Present as the Locus of Action

The present moment occupies a special position: it is the only point at which sensation and action meet — the only point where the organism is in direct causal contact with its environment. The present is not a durationless instant but a span of time unified by the fact that events within it can be directly causally related to current stimuli and actions — what Russell elsewhere calls the "specious present."

This concept dissolves a traditional puzzle: how can we be aware of temporal succession if consciousness is confined to an instantaneous present? The answer is that the "present" of experience is not instantaneous; it encompasses a span within which succession can be directly perceived.

## Time, Causation, and Mnemic Phenomena

The deepest theme is the connection between temporality and causation. The distinction between past and future is fundamentally causal: the past can affect the present (through mnemic traces), but the future cannot. This asymmetry gives time its direction. It is not a metaphysical postulate but an empirical generalization: events leave traces, traces decay, expectations can be disappointed — but the reverse never happens. We do not have memories of the future or expectations of the past. This causal asymmetry is the psychological foundation of our sense of temporal direction.

Russell integrates this with the analysis of [[mnemic-causation]]: time is intimately bound up with the fact that organisms carry their history within them, in the form of modified neural pathways and acquired dispositions. The past is present in the organism — not as a representation but as a causal trace.
