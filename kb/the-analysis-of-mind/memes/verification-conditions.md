Verification conditions, in Russell's analysis of [[truth]] and [[knowledge]], are the experiential criteria by which a [[belief]] or proposition is confirmed or disconfirmed. Russell identifies verification as the critical link between mere belief and warranted knowledge: a belief that cannot in principle be verified lacks the connection to [[sensation|sense-experience]] that distinguishes knowledge from groundless conviction.

## Verification as the Happening of the Expected

Russell offers a strikingly simple account of the core mechanism of verification: it consists, ultimately and perhaps solely, in the happening of something expected. When we hold a belief about the world, that belief generates expectations about future experience. If those expectations are fulfilled — if what we anticipated actually occurs — the belief is verified.

> [013-036] One of the plainest cases of verification, perhaps ultimately the only case, consists in the happening of something expected. You go to the station, expecting to find your friend on the platform; you see him, and say "there he is." Your belief, previously somewhat hesitant, now becomes confident. The sensation which you have when you see your friend is the verification of your belief that he would be on the platform.

This account places expectation at the heart of the cognitive economy. Beliefs are not static representations but dynamic states that project forward into anticipated experience. Verification is the moment when anticipation and actuality converge.

## The Structure of Verification

Russell provides a detailed theoretical account of the verification process, breaking it down into constituent elements. Verification involves a relationship between a present sensation and a prior expectation, mediated by the feeling of recognition or fit between the two.

> [013-037] But although the experience in question is common, it is not wholly easy to give a theoretical account of it. We have first an expectation, which is a belief about the future, and which consists of present words or images with a feeling of belief attached to them. Then we have a sensation, which may be called the "verifier." The sensation is felt to be what was expected; it fulfills the expectation. The relation of the sensation to the expectation is one which we feel, and which we express by saying "Yes, that is it."

The feeling of "Yes, that is it" — the affective experience of confirmation — is the subjective correlate of verification. This feeling is primitive; it cannot be reduced to anything more basic. It is the experiential signature of truth, the moment when belief becomes knowledge.

## Verification and the Primacy of Sense-Experience

All verification, Russell argues, ultimately traces back to [[sensation|sense-experience]]. Even the most abstract [[science|scientific]] theories and mathematical [[logic|logical]] derivations must, if they are to count as knowledge rather than mere formal systems, make contact with sensory verification at some point.

> [013-038] I think all verification is ultimately of the above sort. We verify a scientific hypothesis by deducing consequences which can be verified by observation. The observation, when it comes, consists of sensations, and is of the nature of an expected occurrence which happens. The whole fabric of science, in so far as it is verifiable, rests upon this simple relation of expectation and sensation.

This epistemological foundationalism places sensory experience at the base of the knowledge edifice. The entire structure of [[science]] — theories, laws, predictions — derives its epistemic authority from the simple act of sensory verification: expecting something and then experiencing it.

## Verification and the Definition of Truth

The concept of verification conditions is closely tied to Russell's theory of [[truth]]. A proposition is true, on the correspondence theory that Russell generally endorses, if it corresponds to a fact. But our access to facts is always mediated by sensory experience, and therefore our warrant for asserting a proposition's truth always depends on verification conditions.

This creates an important asymmetry: a proposition may be true without being verified (if the verification conditions happen not to be met, even though the proposition corresponds to the facts), but a proposition cannot be known to be true without verification. Verification conditions are thus epistemological rather than metaphysical: they concern our access to truth, not truth itself.

## Verification in Behaviourist Context

Russell also discusses verification in the context of [[behaviourism|behaviourist]] psychology. The behaviour of an animal provides a form of verification for our judgments about its mental states. When we judge that an animal is hungry, our judgment is verified by its subsequent behaviour — seeking food, eating eagerly, and so on.

> [003-008] We judge easily from the behaviour of an animal of a familiar kind whether it is hungry or thirsty, or pleased or displeased, or inquisitive or terrified. The verification of our judgment, so far as verification is possible, must be derived from the subsequent behaviour of the animal, including its responses to stimuli which we can control.

This behavioural verification is indirect — we cannot directly experience the animal's hunger — but it follows the same basic pattern: an expectation (the animal will eat if offered food) is fulfilled or disappointed by subsequent sensory experience (we observe it eating or refusing food).

## The Limits of Verification

Russell is sensitive to the limits of verification. Not all meaningful propositions are directly verifiable. Propositions about the past, about other minds, about unobservable entities in [[physics]], and about general laws all present challenges to the simple model of verification as the happening of the expected.

The role of [[inference]] becomes crucial here: many of our most important beliefs are verified only indirectly, through the verification of their logical consequences. The verification of a general law, for example, consists in the verification of its particular predictions. This indirectness does not invalidate the beliefs in question, but it does introduce fallibility — a chain of inference is only as strong as its weakest link.
