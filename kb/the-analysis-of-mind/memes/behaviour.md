## Definition

Behaviour, in Russell's analysis, refers to the observable bodily movements — including speech and writing — that constitute the primary evidence for psychological inquiry. Russell aligns himself with the behaviourist school in holding "that nothing can be known about mental events except by inference from behaviour" (001-038). Behaviour thus serves as the methodological foundation of a scientific psychology: what animals and humans *do* is publicly accessible, whereas what they *experience* is private and inferential.

## Russell's Account

Russell opens *The Analysis of Mind* by positioning behaviourism as a methodological starting point. He traces the behaviourist turn to comparative psychology: "those who were concerned with animal psychology were led to observe the behaviour of animals, in the hope that their behaviour would throw light on their mental processes" (001-039). Watson and his followers extended this approach to humans, arguing that "similar methods can be applied to human beings without the need of asking them to introspect" (001-040).

Russell's own stance is nuanced. He endorses the behaviourist method but not the metaphysical conclusion often drawn from it — that consciousness does not exist. "We are all willing to admit," he writes, "that there are characteristic differences between the behaviour of animals and the behaviour of human beings" (001-042). His neutral monism allows him to accept both the public evidence of behaviour and the private reality of experience, without reducing one to the other.

A key example Russell uses is the hungry animal: "A hungry animal is restless; it moves about until it finds food; then it eats and becomes quiet" (001-046). This behavioural cycle — restlessness, seeking, satisfaction, quiescence — captures the pattern that Russell identifies as the behavioural correlate of desire.

Russell also discusses semi-reflexive behaviour, such as "the act of running towards the train" (002-009), which occupies a middle ground between fully automatic reflex and deliberative action, illustrating the continuity between mechanical and purposive behaviour that his neutral monism requires.

## Key Distinctions

- **Behaviour vs. experience**: Behaviour is public and observable; experience is private. Russell uses behaviour as evidence for mental states without reducing the latter to the former.
- **Behaviour vs. physiology**: Behaviour comprises whole-organism actions; physiology concerns the internal mechanisms. Russell notes that "in all such cases the reduction of behaviour to physical laws can only be effected by including the past history of the organism" (002-014).
- **Animal vs. human behaviour**: Differences exist but are matters of degree, not kind. "From this fact it is a highly probable inference that there is no gap either in structure or in behaviour" (002-001) between humans and other animals.

## Historical Context

Russell's engagement with behaviourism reflects the intellectual climate of early 20th-century psychology. John B. Watson's "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It" (1913) had argued for the elimination of introspection as a scientific method. Russell, while sympathetic to Watson's methodology, maintained that the private data of consciousness could not simply be dismissed. His position represents a middle path — behaviourist in method, neutral monist in metaphysics — that distinguishes him from both Watsonian orthodoxy and introspectionist traditionalism. The concept of behaviour also connects to his broader philosophical commitments: if mental states are logical constructions out of neutral particulars, then behaviour provides the publicly available correlate that anchors such constructions.
