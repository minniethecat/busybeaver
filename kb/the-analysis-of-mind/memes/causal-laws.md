In Lecture V of _The Analysis of Mind_, Russell addresses the nature of causal laws and the distinction between those that govern psychology and those that govern physics. This distinction is central to his entire project: if psychology and physics are to be distinguished by their causal laws rather than by their subject matter, then the nature of causal laws themselves must be clarified. Russell uses the lecture to dismantle the traditional conception of cause and effect and to replace it with a more precise understanding grounded in modern science.

## Against Traditional Causation

Russell opens with a sweeping critique of the traditional conception of causation. The idea that a particular event A causes a particular event B, with some mysterious "necessary" connection between them, is "fundamentally erroneous" (005-001). Modern science replaces this with the notion of **laws of change** — differential equations that describe how a system evolves at each moment, rather than discrete cause-effect pairs.

Russell illustrates the problem with the example of arsenic poisoning:

> "Suppose, for example, that a man dies of arsenic poisoning, we say that his taking arsenic was the cause of death. But clearly the process by which he acquired the arsenic is irrelevant: everything that happened before he swallowed it may be ignored, since it cannot alter the effect except in so far as it alters his condition at the moment of taking the dose." (005-001)

As we trace the causal chain more precisely, both cause and effect must be "shortened indefinitely," until we arrive at differential equations that describe instantaneous rates of change. The traditional "A causes B" gives way to a mathematical description of how the state of a system is changing at each moment.

## Empirical Generalizations in Practice

Despite the theoretical superiority of differential equations, Russell acknowledges that the laws we actually know empirically retain the form of traditional causal laws — except that they are "not to be regarded as universal or necessary" (005-001). "Taking arsenic is followed by death" is a good empirical generalization with rare exceptions. Such rough generalizations have a definite place in science:

> "They are the data for more exact laws, and the grounds for believing that they are USUALLY true are stronger than the grounds for believing that the more exact laws are ALWAYS true." (005-001)

This pragmatic acknowledgment is important for psychology, where exact differential equations are rarely available. Psychological causal laws, like those governing [[Mnemic Phenomena|mnemic phenomena]], are empirical generalizations about how past experience shapes present behaviour — rough but indispensable.

## The Lack of Uniqueness in Causation

Russell also attacks the assumption that each event has a single THE cause. In practice, "cause" means "nearly invariable antecedent," and there are always many such antecedents:

> "Men leave a factory for dinner when the hooter sounds at twelve o'clock. You may say the hooter is THE cause of their leaving. But innumerable other hooters in other factories, which also always sound at twelve o'clock, have just as good a right to be called the cause." (005-001)

The attempt to single out one antecedent as THE cause depends on a notion of cause derived from will — and will, Russell will later argue, is not what it is generally supposed to be. The plurality of nearly invariable antecedents undermines the quest for unique causes.

## Psychological vs. Physical Causal Laws

With the traditional conception of causation clarified, Russell can address the distinction that is fundamental to his book. Psychological causal laws are distinguished from physical ones by the role of **mnemic causation** — the dependence of present responses on past history, not merely on the present state of the organism.

In physical causal laws, the proximate cause of a present event can in principle be found entirely in the present state of the system (as described by differential equations). In psychological causal laws, by contrast, the causal unit spans time: "two or more events at two or more times" (015-013) enter essentially into the causation. This temporal spread — exemplified by [[Memory|memory]], [[Instinct and Habit|habit]], and [[Association|association]] — is what makes psychology irreducible to physics, even if both sciences ultimately deal with the same neutral stuff.

## Neutral Monism and the Construction of Matter

Russell extends his analysis of causal laws into a constructive proposal about the nature of physical objects. Rejecting the inference to an unknowable "real" table behind its appearances, he proposes instead that "the table which is neutral as between different observers (actual and possible) is the set of all those particulars which would naturally be called 'aspects' of the table from different points of view" (005-001).

A piece of matter, on this view, is a logical construction out of particulars grouped by their causal correlations. The same neutral particulars, grouped differently, constitute both the physical object and the perceptions of it. This is the constructive side of [[Neutral Monism|neutral monism]] — the metaphysic that Russell endorses as the framework within which both psychological and physical causal laws find their place.
