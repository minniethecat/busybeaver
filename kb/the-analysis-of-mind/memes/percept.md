## Definition

A percept is the immediate object of a perceptual act — what perception delivers to consciousness. Russell distinguishes the percept from both the physiological process of perception and from the inferred physical object: "what I am speaking of now is perception, where, according to conventional psychology, we have a sensation followed by an interpretation" (001-010). The percept is the sensory core of perception, prior to interpretive elaboration.

## Russell's Account

Russell treats the percept as a key datum for his neutral monist analysis. He notes that it is a mistake to confuse the percept — the sensory presentation — with the physical object that common sense posits as its cause: "in order to eliminate the reference to our perceptions, which introduces an irrelevance" (005-012). The percept is what is actually given; the physical object is inferred.

Russell's treatment of the percept is closely tied to his analysis of perception as a psychological phenomenon. He observes that "our perception is made up of sensations and images" (006-006), situating the percept within a broader cognitive architecture that includes both sensory input and memorial elaboration. The percept is primarily sensory, but sensory data alone do not constitute perception — they must be organized and interpreted.

Russell draws a notable distinction between external and internal perception: "what might be called external perception, it may not reveal differences" (006-027) as reliably as internal perception (introspection). This challenges the traditional empiricist privileging of external sense-perception, suggesting that "introspection may be more trustworthy than 'external' perception" (006-028) in certain respects.

When Russell turns to the "psychology of perceptions, we observe that perception is not a simple act but involves a complex synthesis of sensations, images, and habitual expectations" (005-029). The percept — the object of perception — is thus not a raw sensory atom but a structured presentation shaped by past experience and cognitive habits.

## Key Distinctions

Russell distinguishes:
- **Percept vs. sensation**: Sensation is the raw sensory input; the percept is the organized object of perceptual awareness, already shaped by interpretation (001-010).
- **Percept vs. physical object**: The percept is what is given in experience; the physical object is a logical construction inferred from systems of percepts and appearances (005-012).
- **External vs. internal perception**: External perception delivers percepts of physical objects; internal perception (introspection) delivers percepts of mental states — and the latter may be "more trustworthy" in some respects (006-027, 006-028).
- **Percept vs. image**: Percepts arise from current sensory stimulation; images arise from memory and imagination, though both contribute to the perceptual experience (006-006).
