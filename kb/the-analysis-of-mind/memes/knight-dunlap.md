Knight Dunlap (1875–1949) was an American psychologist associated with the [[behaviourism|behaviourist]] movement. In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell devotes sustained attention to Dunlap's critique of introspection, using his 1912 article "The Case against Introspection" as a representative specimen of behaviourist arguments against introspective method.

## The Case Against Introspection

Russell opens his discussion of Dunlap by acknowledging the significance of the American behaviourist challenge to introspection. He quotes Dunlap's critique of G. F. Stout, one of the leading defenders of introspection at the time:

> [006-005] The criticism of introspection has been in the main the work of American psychologists. I will begin by summarizing an article which seems to me to afford a good specimen of their arguments, namely, "The Case against Introspection," by Knight Dunlap ("Psychological Review," vol xix, No. 5, pp. 404-413, September, 1912).

Dunlap's central argument against Stout was that the concept of a unified observing self — the "single observer" — was illicitly presupposed by introspective method. Russell grants the force of this criticism against theories that invoke a unitary subject, but he does not concede that introspection is therefore impossible:

> [006-008] The objections to any theory which brings in the single observer were considered in Lecture I, and were acknowledged to be cogent. In so far, therefore, as Stout's theory of introspection rests upon this assumption, we are compelled to reject it. But it is perfectly possible to believe in introspection without supposing that there is a single observer.

## The Awareness Paradox

The most philosophically significant aspect of Dunlap's position, for Russell, is Dunlap's claim that while knowing certainly exists, knowing can never itself be known: "I am never aware of an awareness" (006-012). Russell finds this claim paradoxical:

> [006-013] The statement "I am aware of a colour" is assumed by Knight Dunlap to be known to be true, but he does not explain how it comes to be known.

Russell argues that if we can make the statement "I am aware of a colour" — which Dunlap grants — then we possess knowledge that purely non-introspective beings would lack. Dunlap's position requires that this knowledge be purely inferential, but he provides no account of how such an inference could work. Russell concludes that "the fact (which he admits) that we know there is awareness, is all but decisive against his theory, and in favour of the view that we can be aware of an awareness."

## Dunlap on Images

Russell also engages Dunlap's specific views on mental imagery. Dunlap contended that what are called "images" are really muscular contractions — a position that would reduce introspective data to bodily sensations accessible to external observation. Russell notes the strategic significance of this move:

> [006-025] Opponents of introspection do not mean to deny the obvious fact that we can observe bodily sensations which others cannot observe. For example, Knight Dunlap contends that images are really muscular contractions, and evidently regards our awareness of muscular contractions as not coming under the head of introspection.

Russell's assessment of Dunlap is nuanced. He agrees with Dunlap that introspective data often consist of images, visceral sensations, and other bodily states rather than a direct apprehension of a mental "act." But he maintains — against Dunlap — that some of these data constitute instances of knowing, and that awareness of them is a form of legitimate introspection. Dunlap's critique thus serves to refine rather than to eliminate introspection in Russell's system.
