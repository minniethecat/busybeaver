## Definition

Existence, in the context of Russell's analysis of mind, is not a univocal concept. Russell inherits from Brentano the notion of "intentional (also the mental) inexistence of an object" (001-016) — the idea that the object of a mental act has a distinctive mode of being *within* the act itself. This is contrasted with the "existence of a world outside ourselves" (001-026), which common sense posits but which Russell treats as an inference in need of justification.

## Russell's Account

Russell's discussion of existence in *The Analysis of Mind* is shaped by two opposing forces: Brentano's intentional psychology and Russell's own neutral monism, which aims to dissolve the distinction between mental and physical existence.

**(1) Brentano's intentional inexistence.** Russell quotes Brentano's definition: "Every mental phenomenon is characterized by... the intentional (also the mental) inexistence of an object" (001-016). Brentano adds: "This intentional inexistence is exclusively peculiar to psychological phenomena. No physical phenomenon exhibits anything like it" (001-017). For Brentano, this is the defining mark of the mental: mental acts contain their objects in a way physical things do not.

**(2) Russell's critique.** Russell acknowledges the historical force of Brentano's criterion but resists its dualistic conclusion. "It cannot for a moment be doubted that we are right in believing that we have something in us called 'ideas' having reference to objects" — but the question is whether this reference is a fundamental feature of reality or something reducible (001-008). Russell's project is to explain intentional reference without positing a special kind of "mental" existence — to show that the sign-function and associative mechanisms can do the work Brentano assigned to intentional inexistence.

**(3) Existence of the external world.** Russell treats belief in the external world as an inference: "They think that they can know of the existence of a world outside themselves" (001-026). This belief, he argues, is prompted by certain experiences but is not directly given in sensation. The existence of physical objects is therefore not a datum but a construction — a logical posit justified by its explanatory power, not by direct acquaintance.

**(4) Existence of images.** Russell affirms the existence of images as an empirical fact: "We shall henceforth assume that the existence of images is admitted" (008-037). Unlike some radical behaviourists who would deny images altogether, Russell insists that the introspective evidence for imagery is too strong to dismiss. "I think that there cannot be any reasonable doubt that images exist" (006-015).

**(5) Existence in logic.** Russell's broader philosophical framework — the theory of descriptions from "On Denoting" (1905) — is implicitly at work. Existence, in this view, is not a predicate of individuals but a property of propositional functions. When Brentano speaks of the "inexistence" of the object in the mental act, Russell would analyse this as a logical construction, not a mysterious ontological status.

## Key Distinctions

- **Mental Inexistence vs. Physical Existence**: The object exists *in* the mental act (Brentano) vs. independently of it (common sense).
- **Existence vs. Subsistence**: Russell's earlier distinction (in *The Problems of Philosophy*) between being and existence is muted in 1921.
- **Existence as Inference vs. Existence as Datum**: Physical objects are inferred; sensations and images are given.
- **Existence vs. Truth**: To say "X exists" is to say the propositional function "x is X" is sometimes true.

## Historical Context

The concept inherits from Brentano's *Psychology from an Empirical Standpoint* (1874), where "intentional inexistence" was proposed as the criterion of the mental. Russell, who had studied Brentano and his student Meinong extensively, respects the problem Brentano identified but rejects the solution. Russell's own neutral monism — influenced by William James and the American New Realists — attempts to dissolve the dualism of mental existence vs. physical existence by positing a single neutral stuff that is neither intrinsically mental nor physical.
