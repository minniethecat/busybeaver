# Dream

**Dreams**, in Russell's *The Analysis of Mind*, are analysed as a special class of [[images]] that occur during sleep and are governed by [[mnemic-causation|mnemic]] rather than physical laws. Russell draws on [[Sigmund Freud]]'s work to emphasize that dreams are not random noise but are causally determined by the dreamer's past experiences and present physiological state. Dreams occupy a critical position in Russell's argument for the continuity between waking and sleeping cognition: the [[sensation-image]] distinction that operates in waking life is largely suspended during dreams, where images feel just as real as sensations. This blurring of the sensation-image boundary supports Russell's broader thesis that sensations and images differ in degree of causal dependence rather than in intrinsic quality, a cornerstone of his [[neutral-monism|neutral monist]] position.

## Dreams as Images

Russell classifies dreams as a species of [[images]], distinct from sensations primarily by their causal origins rather than their felt quality. While waking images are usually recognized as such — we know that a recalled face is not the person standing before us — dream images carry full sensory conviction:

> "The things we see in dreams when our eyes are shut must be images, yet they feel just as real as if they were sensations." (008-015)

This phenomenological reality of dream images poses a challenge for any theory that claims images and sensations are intrinsically distinguishable by their "faintness" or lack of vividness. Dreams show that images can be maximally vivid:

> "In the case of dreams, it FEELS just as real as if it were a sensation." (008-029)

The epistemic problem this creates — how do we know we are not dreaming right now? — is one Russell acknowledges as a traditional philosophical worry, though he treats it as a limiting case rather than a practical obstacle to knowledge.

## Mnemic Causation in Dreams

The most important theoretical point Russell makes about dreams concerns their causal structure. Unlike waking perception, which is partly governed by current physical stimuli, dreams are governed primarily by [[mnemic-causation]] — causation in which the effect depends on past experience stored in the organism:

> "Dreams, as Freud has shown, are just as subject to laws as the behaviour of waking life, but the laws are different." (008-003)

The "different laws" Russell refers to are precisely mnemic laws. In waking life, if you see a dog, the proximate cause is the physical presence of the dog reflecting light onto your retina. In a dream, if you see a dog, the cause is a chain that runs through past experiences (the mnemic trace) combined with current cerebral events — without any dog being physically present. This is the same kind of causation that produces [[memory]] and [[imagination]], but operating with less constraint from current stimuli.

Russell uses the dream as a paradigmatic case of mnemic causation precisely because the absence of relevant external stimuli is so clear. When you dream of a childhood home, there is no house in the bedroom; the image is caused entirely by neural processes that carry the effects of past experience.

## Dreams, Hallucinations, and Fever-Delirium

Russell groups dreams together with hallucinations and fever-delirium as cases where the distinction between sensations and images becomes practically indiscernible:

> "The cases of dreams and fever-delirium are as hard to distinguish from sensations as any image could be." (008-027)

This continuum has important theoretical implications:

| Phenomenon | Primary Cause | Sensation-Image Distinction |
|------------|--------------|----------------------------|
| Waking perception | External stimulus + mnemic contribution | Clear (sensations dominate) |
| Hallucination | Mnemic + abnormal cerebral state | Blurred |
| Dream | Mnemic (during sleep) | Suspended (images feel like sensations) |
| Fever-delirium | Mnemic + physiological disruption | Suspended |

The table illustrates Russell's key point: the sensation-image distinction is a matter of degree along a causal continuum, not a metaphysical divide between two fundamentally different kinds of mental entities. Dreams, by occupying the far end of this continuum, reveal the underlying unity of mental phenomena.

## The Connection to Irrational Beliefs

Russell draws a suggestive link between dreams, irrational beliefs, and insanity:

> "The connection of dreams, irrational beliefs and foolish actions with unconscious wishes... has been brought to light by Freud." (001-047)

This connection operates through the same mnemic mechanisms. Just as dream images are produced by past experiences operating through cerebral traces without the constraint of current reality-testing, so too irrational beliefs may arise when mnemic associations overpower the corrective influence of current sensation. Russell does not fully endorse Freudian theory, but he credits Freud with demonstrating that the same causal principles apply across sleeping and waking mental life — a finding that supports Russell's own thesis of psychological continuity.

## Dreams and the Privacy of Mental Events

Dreams also feature in Russell's argument for the privacy of mental events. Because dream images are caused by events within the dreamer's own nervous system — specifically, by cerebral processes that carry mnemic traces — they are inaccessible to external observers:

> "Your dreams and memories and thoughts, of which no one else is directly aware, are mental facts in a sense in which the movements of your body are not." (006-003)

This privacy is not a metaphysical mystery but a straightforward consequence of the causal localization of mnemic phenomena within individual brains. Dreams are private because their causes are private — a point that Russell considers obvious once [[mind]] is understood in causal rather than substantive terms.
