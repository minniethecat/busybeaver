## Definition

In traditional philosophy, matter is the substance composing the physical world — that which is extended in space, subject to mechanical laws, and devoid of consciousness. Russell challenges this conception on two fronts: first, by arguing that modern physics has rendered matter increasingly immaterial; and second, by proposing that both mind and matter are constructions from a more primitive neutral-stuff.

## Russell's Account

**The Illusory Solidity of Matter.** Russell opens *The Analysis of Mind* by observing the tension between behaviourist psychology, which "tend[s] to think of matter as something much more solid and indubitable than mind," and relativity physics, whose world "consists of 'events,' from which 'matter' is derived by a logical construction" (P01-001). The common-sense belief in "an impassable gulf between [mind and matter]" (001-006) is, Russell contends, undermined by both scientific developments.

**Neutral Monism.** Russell's positive doctrine — building on [[William James]] and the American realists — is that "the stuff of which the world of our experience is composed is, in my belief, neither mind nor matter, but something more primitive than either. Both mind and matter seem to be composite, and the stuff of which they are compounded lies in a sense between the two, in a sense above them both, like a common ancestor" (001-007). He approvingly quotes James's view that "the raw material out of which the world is built up is not of two sorts, one matter and the other mind, but that it is arranged in different patterns by its inter-relations" (001-031).

**Mind-Matter Interaction.** Against the Cartesian doctrine that mind and matter are too different to interact, and the psychophysical parallelist view that they run on separate tracks, Russell argues that everyday experience shows constant interaction: "I receive a letter inviting me to dinner: the letter is a physical fact, but my apprehension of its meaning is mental. ... In consequence of my apprehension of the meaning of the letter, I go to the right place at the right time; here we have an effect of mind on matter" (001-050). There is, he insists, "no necessity to make any such assumption" of causal independence, "which is very difficult to harmonize with obvious facts" (001-050).

**The Laws of Living Matter.** Russell questions whether the laws of physics are adequate to explain the behaviour of living bodies: "There is no cogent empirical reason for supposing that the laws determining the motions of living bodies are exactly the same as those that apply to dead matter" (001-051). While he does not assert this difference dogmatically, he leaves the question open, noting that voluntary movements are "at any rate prima facie, very different in their laws from the movements of what is devoid of life" (001-051).

## Key Distinctions

- **Matter as substance vs. matter as logical construction**: Traditional metaphysics treats matter as a substance; Russell, following developments in physics and philosophy, treats it as a construction from more fundamental elements.
- **Dead matter vs. living matter**: The behaviour of living bodies may be governed by different laws from those governing inanimate objects (001-051, 001-043).
- **Matter vs. neutral-stuff**: Both matter and mind are groupings of the same underlying stuff; neither is metaphysically prior (001-007, 001-031).

## Historical Context

Russell's views on matter reflect the convergence of several early 20th-century developments: Einstein's relativity theory, which dematerialised the physical world into events; James's radical empiricism; and the behaviourist challenge to mental substance. Russell draws on all three to argue for a position — neutral monism — that was neither idealism nor materialism, though it "approximate[s] to materialism" in some respects and "to its opposite" in others (001-050).
