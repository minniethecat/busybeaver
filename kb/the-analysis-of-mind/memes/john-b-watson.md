## Biography

John Broadus Watson (1878–1958) was an American psychologist, formerly of Johns Hopkins University, who founded the school of behaviourism. His landmark paper "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It" (1913) launched a revolution in psychology by arguing that the only admissible data for a scientific psychology are publicly observable behaviours, not private introspective reports.

## Key Ideas

Watson's behaviourism rests on the principle that "nothing can be known except by external observation" (001-038). Behaviourists "deny altogether that there is a separate source of knowledge called 'introspection,' by which we can know things about ourselves which we could never observe in others." They argue that psychology as a science is "only concerned with BEHAVIOUR, i.e. with what we DO; this alone, they contend, can be accurately observed" (001-038).

On this view, what we call "knowing" is simply a set of language-habits. Russell gives a crude illustration: when two children are asked "What is six times nine?", one says fifty-four and the other fifty-six. "The one child has acquired the habit of saying 'six times nine is fifty-four'; the other has not. There is no more need of 'thought' in this than there is when a horse turns into his accustomed stable" (001-040). What is called "knowing" is "a phenomenon exemplified in their physical behaviour, including spoken and written words" (001-041).

Watson also wrote on the psychology of wish fulfilment, applying behaviourist principles to Freudian concepts. Russell references Watson's 1916 article "The Psychology of Wish Fulfilment" in *The Scientific Monthly* (001-054).

## Russell's Engagement

Russell uses Watson's behaviourism as a valuable corrective to introspectionism, while not fully endorsing it. He acknowledges the behaviourist challenge to the Cartesian certainty of self-knowledge: "He maintains that our knowledge of ourselves is no different in kind from our knowledge of other people" (001-042). Russell concedes that "I believe the behaviourists somewhat overstate their case, yet there is an important element of truth in their contention, since the things which we can discover by introspection do not seem to differ in any very fundamental way from the things which we discover by external observation" (001-042).

Throughout *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell maintains a middle position: he adopts behaviourist methodology where it is illuminating (particularly in analysing knowledge and desire), but insists that some mental phenomena — particularly images and belief-feelings — require an analysis that goes beyond pure behaviourism. As he states in the memory lecture: "I do not myself believe that the analysis of knowledge can be effected entirely by means of purely external observation, such as behaviourists employ" (009-002).
