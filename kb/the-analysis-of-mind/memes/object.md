## Definition

In the Brentano-Meinong tradition that Russell critically engages, an "object" is the referent of a mental act — that which a thought is about. In Brentano's formulation, every mental phenomenon is characterized by "the intentional (or mental) inexistence of an object" (001-016). Russell himself, after abandoning his earlier acceptance of this view, comes to treat the object of thought not as a special mental entity but as "usually something in the outer world" (001-024), accessible through the ordinary causal relations of perception.

## Russell's Account

The concept of "object" enters Russell's analysis primarily through his critique of the act-content-object distinction inherited from Brentano and Meinong.

**(1) The tripartite analysis.** Meinong analyzed a thought into three elements: "the act, the content and the object" (001-021). The act is the thinking, the content is what distinguishes one thought of the same object from another, and the object is what the thought is about — for example, when thinking of St. Paul, St. Paul himself is the object (001-020). Russell acknowledges the descriptive appeal of this analysis but rejects it as ultimately metaphysical.

**(2) Russell's rejection of the act.** Russell argues that the "act" — the supposed mental activity of thinking — is "unnecessary and fictitious. Empirically, I cannot discover anything corresponding to the supposed act; and theoretically I cannot see that it is indispensable" (001-022). He suggests that the act is merely the ghost of the Cartesian subject, the residue of the grammatical "I" in "I think."

**(3) The object as external.** Crucially, Russell observes that Meinong's own view implies that the object of thought is typically not mental at all: "in the thought of a past event, for instance, the content is mental, but the object — namely, the past event — is not mental" (001-023). This observation undermines the thesis that reference to an object is the defining characteristic of mind, since the object is often something physical or past.

**(4) Object alone, without act.** Russell goes further than Meinong, maintaining that "a thought consists either of act and object alone, or of object alone" (001-025). He flirts with the idea that the content itself may be dispensable — that thought might be constituted purely by relations to objects without any mental intermediary. This is part of his broader neutral monist project: mind and matter are both logical constructions out of the same neutral stuff, and "the stuff of which the world of our experience is composed is... neither mind nor matter, but something more primitive than either" (001-007).

**(5) The subject-object duality rejected.** Russell follows William James and the American realists in denying that "the subject-object relation is fundamental" (001-037). The duality of subject and object "which is supposed to constitute knowledge" is, for Russell, a grammatical illusion — a projection of the subject-predicate structure of language onto the world.

## Key Distinctions

- **Object vs. Content**: The content is the mental occurrence (what is in the mind when one thinks); the object is what the thought is about, which may be physical, past, or abstract.
- **Object vs. Act**: The act is the supposed mental activity of thinking; the object is what is thought about. Russell denies the act exists.
- **Immanent vs. Transcendent Object**: Brentano's "intentional inexistence" treats the object as immanent in the mental act; Russell treats the object as transcendent — existing independently in the outer world.
- **Object in Perception vs. Object in Thought**: In perception, we are "in direct contact with objects" (001-026); in thought, the relation to objects is derivative, resting on beliefs and causal connections.

## Historical Context

The concept of the intentional object traces to medieval scholasticism and was revived in the 19th century by Franz Brentano as the defining mark of the mental. Brentano's student Alexius Meinong elaborated the tripartite act-content-object distinction. Russell, who studied Meinong's work closely in his early period, had initially accepted a version of this analysis. By 1921, however, under the influence of James's radical empiricism and Watson's behaviourism, Russell had come to see the entire framework as a relic of Cartesian dualism — a projection of grammar onto metaphysics.
