Russell addresses emotions and will in Lecture XIV of _The Analysis of Mind_, extending his behaviouristic framework to two of the most apparently mental phenomena. He argues that emotions are not inner mental states but complex physiological processes involving visceral changes, and that will is not a separate mental faculty but a form of the [[Desire and Feeling|desire-feeling]] behaviour cycle. This analysis completes his project of analysing all putatively mental phenomena in terms of observable causal patterns, without appeal to [[Consciousness|consciousness]] as a fundamental category.

## The Nature of Emotion

Russell opens his analysis by emphasizing the complexity of emotion:

> "It is clear that an emotion is essentially complex, and we have to inquire whether it ever contains any non-physiological material." (014-002)

An emotion, on Russell's view, is not a simple feeling but a composite of physiological events. He lists the "coarser emotions" -- "grief, fear, rage, love" (014-005) -- as primary objects of analysis. These emotions involve characteristic patterns of bodily change, facial expression, and behavioural tendency, each of which can be analysed without reference to any mental substance.

## The James-Lange Theory

Russell grounds his discussion in the James-Lange theory of emotion, which he describes as the starting point for modern views:

> "Modern views on the causation of emotions begin with what is called the James-Lange theory." (014-004)

According to this theory, the emotion is not the cause of bodily changes (as common sense supposes) but rather the perception of those bodily changes. We do not cry because we are sad; we are sad because we perceive ourselves crying. Russell accepts the broad thrust of this theory, which aligns with his behaviourism by locating emotion in physiological processes rather than in an inner mental realm.

Russell notes experimental evidence that supports this account. In experiments where "the viscera were cut off from all communication with the brain" (014-007), emotional behaviour was significantly altered, though not entirely eliminated. This suggests that visceral feedback is crucial to emotional experience but may not constitute its whole content.

## The Problem of Emotional Specificity

A challenge to the James-Lange theory is that similar physiological changes accompany different emotions. Russell acknowledges this difficulty:

> "Similar affections of the viscera may accompany dissimilar emotions, especially fear and rage." (014-010)

If both fear and rage involve accelerated heartbeat, flushed skin, and muscular tension, then the difference between the two emotions cannot be reduced to visceral sensation alone. Russell suggests that the difference lies partly in the pattern of the overall process -- its behavioural trajectory and its causal antecedents -- rather than in any single mental or physiological element.

This points toward a functional analysis of emotion: an emotion is distinguished not by how it feels but by what it does -- the behaviour it tends to produce and the situations that trigger it.

## Emotion as Process

Russell concludes that emotion is fundamentally a process rather than a state:

> "An emotion is essentially a process, and it will be only a certain pattern of causal connections that distinguishes one emotion from another." (014-014)

This process view has several important implications. First, it dissolves the apparent gulf between emotion and [[Instinct and Habit|instinct-habit]] -- emotions are simply complex instinctive patterns with characteristic physiological and behavioural components. Second, it connects emotion to the analysis of [[Belief|belief]], since emotional responses often depend on what an organism believes about its situation. Third, it grounds emotion in the same causal framework that Russell applies to all other mental phenomena.

## The Will as Desire-Behaviour Cycle

Russell's treatment of will is deliberately deflationary. Traditional philosophy treated the will as a distinct mental faculty -- the power of an agent to initiate action through a mental act of volition. Russell rejects this picture entirely. The will, on his analysis, is not a faculty but a form of the desire-behaviour cycle discussed in Lecture III.

When a person or animal "wills" to act, Russell reduces this to the operation of a [[Desire and Feeling|desire-feeling]]: "A hungry animal is restless until it finds food" (001-046). The restlessness is the causal mechanism of will. The "act of will" is simply the point in a behaviour cycle at which action is initiated, driven by the causal law of desire. No special mental act of volition is needed.

Russell identifies three classes of movement relevant to will:

| Movement Class | Relation to Will |
|---|---|
| Reflex | Automatic, not willed |
| Mechanical | Physically caused, not willed |
| Voluntary | Caused by desire-behaviour cycles |

Voluntary movements are distinguished from reflex and mechanical movements by their causal origin in a desire-behaviour cycle, not by the presence of a mental act of will (002-007). This allows Russell to maintain a fully naturalistic account of agency without appealing to mental causation.
