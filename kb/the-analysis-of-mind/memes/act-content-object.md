## Definition

The act-content-object distinction is a tripartite framework for analyzing mental phenomena, originating with Franz Brentano and elaborated by Alexius Meinong. Every mental state is said to involve three components: the *act* (the mental process itself, such as judging or desiring), the *content* (what the act presents or represents), and the *object* (the extra-mental thing the act is about). Russell summarizes this as the view that "thinking is the act of a person" and that "Meinong's 'act' is the ghost of the subject" (001-022) — a framework he ultimately rejects in favor of neutral monism.

## Russell's Account

Russell discusses Brentano's framework in the opening lecture. He presents Brentano as the representative of the view that mental phenomena are distinguished by their intentional reference to objects: "the direct essential thing that Brentano and Meinong represent it as being" — namely, the relation to an object — is what supposedly marks the mental as distinct from the physical (001-023). Russell notes Brentano's key thesis that "we may define psychical phenomena by saying that they are phenomena which intentionally contain an object" (008-009).

Brentano's concept of "intentional inexistence," derived from medieval scholasticism, holds that the object of thought exists *in* the mental act in a special way — "what the scholastics of the Middle Ages called the intentional (also the mental) inexistence of an object" (001-016). This "intentional inexistence is exclusively peculiar to mental phenomena" (001-017), serving as Brentano's criterion for demarcating the mental from the physical.

Russell's own position is critical. He acknowledges the descriptive accuracy of the framework — "Like Brentano, I am interested in psychology" (001-018) — but ultimately regards the act-content-object structure as reifying what are better understood as neutral particulars and their relations. His rejection of the act as a mental entity distinct from its object aligns with his broader neutral monist project.

## Key Distinctions

Within this framework, three elements are distinguished:
- **Act**: The mental process — judging, desiring, perceiving, remembering. Meinong treats this as the "actus" of a subject, though Russell notes it is "the ghost of the subject" once the subject itself is stripped away (001-022).
- **Content**: The mode of presentation or what is "before the mind" — that which distinguishes thinking-of-Brown from thinking-of-Smith when both acts are of the same type.
- **Object**: The extra-mental entity the act is directed toward — Brown himself, not the thought of Brown.

Russell observes that the distinction between content and object collapses under scrutiny, since "if we think of Smith or think of Brown, the act of thinking, in itself, is exactly the same in the two cases" (001-019) — the difference lies in the relation, not in an intermediary mental content.

## Historical Context

Brentano introduced the tripartite framework in *Psychology from an Empirical Standpoint* (1874), aiming to establish psychology as a science distinct from physiology. His student Meinong developed the theory further in his *Theory of Objects* (Gegenstandstheorie), extending intentional reference to non-existent objects. Russell studied both Brentano and Meinong intensively during his early philosophical development, and *The Analysis of Mind* represents his mature, critical engagement with their legacy — retaining the focus on intentionality while jettisoning the mental act as a distinct ontological category. The framework also influenced Edmund Husserl's phenomenology, though Russell's engagement with Husserl is more indirect.
