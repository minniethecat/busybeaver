## Definition

Russell defines habit as behaviour that is acquired rather than innate, "learnt" or embodying a pattern "due to previous experience" (002-021). Habit stands in contrast to [[Instinct|instinct]], the two forming a fundamental dichotomy in Russell's behaviourist-influenced analysis of mental life. The occurrence of habit marks the transition from purely physiological to genuinely psychological explanation.

## Russell's Account

Russell opens *The Analysis of Mind* by noting that "occurrences which we are in the habit of calling 'mental'" (001-001) constitute the very subject-matter of the book—a reflexive use of the term that signals its centrality. His analysis treats habit as extending beyond simple motor routines into the cognitive domain: "knowledge IS anything beyond the habits shown in this behaviour" (001-041) is presented as a view to be examined, while "our habitual knowledge is not distinguishable from physical habit" (004-007) in the case of a practised response.

Language itself is analysed as a habit-system. Russell observes that "the one child has acquired a language-habit" (001-040) while another has not, and that "one group of habits can 'down' another group of habits—or instincts" (001-055). The acquisition of "the habits of a civilized adult" (001-056) involves the gradual suppression and reorganisation of earlier patterns, with language emerging as "the most important outcome of linguistic habits" (001-058).

## Key Distinctions

### Habit vs. Instinct

"The broad distinction between instinct and habit is undeniable" (002-023), Russell asserts, but he immediately qualifies: many instincts "mature gradually" (002-022) and require environmental triggering, while habits can become so automatic as to appear innate. The criterion is causal history: instinctive movements are those "which an animal performs without having learnt them" (002-020), whereas habits "arise out of random movements" (002-025) through selection and reinforcement.

### Habit and Knowledge

Russell entertains but ultimately complicates the behaviourist reduction of knowledge to habit. A person responding correctly to a stimulus may display "merely a bodily habit" (004-007), yet knowledge properly so-called seems to require something more—a relationship to truth that pure habit cannot guarantee. This tension between the habitual and the epistemic runs through Russell's entire treatment.

## Historical Context

Russell's analysis of habit draws on the experimental tradition of "the acquisition of habits, [which] has been much studied in various animals" (002-024), as well as on the philosophical tradition descending from [[David Hume|Hume]], who treated custom and habit as the foundation of causal inference. Russell's neutral monism leads him to treat habit as a bridge concept between physiology and psychology: "what is true of animals, as regards instinct and habit, is equally true of men" (002-030).
