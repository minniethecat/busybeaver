## Overview

Cognition, in [[Bertrand Russell]]'s *Analysis of Mind*, serves as the umbrella category for mental processes involving [[knowledge]], [[recognition]], and [[judgment]]. Rather than treating cognition as the defining essence of [[mind]] — as the Cartesian and Brentanian traditions do — Russell analyzes it as one natural phenomenon among many, occurring alongside and interacting with [[habit]], [[instinct]], [[emotion]], and [[desire-feeling|desire]].

> [001-015] …regarded as typical of every form of cognition, and sometimes of mental life in general…

Russell resists the temptation to make cognition paradigmatic of the mental. Much of mental life — [[dream|dreaming]], [[emotion|emotional]] response, habitual action — involves little or no cognition in the strict sense.

## Cognition and the Mental

Russell challenges the Brentanian assumption that all mental acts are essentially cognitive — that is, directed toward an [[object]] in a way that constitutes knowledge. He argues that this over-intellectualizes the mind:

| Mental Phenomenon | Cognitive Component? | Non-Cognitive Component |
|-------------------|---------------------|------------------------|
| [[Belief]] | Propositional content | Feeling of conviction |
| [[Desire-feeling\|Desire]] | Awareness of goal | Bodily uneasiness, impulse |
| [[Memory]] | Reference to past | Feeling of familiarity |
| [[Emotion]] | Appraisal of situation | Physiological arousal |
| [[Habit]] | Minimal or none | Automatic repetition |

> [008-008] …regard a sensation as itself a cognition, and until lately I did so regard it…

Russell's evolving view on whether [[sensation-image|sensation]] itself is a cognition reflects his deepening commitment to [[neutral-monism]]. If sensations are neutral particulars — neither mental nor physical — then they are not *inherently* cognitive. Cognition arises when sensations are organized into beliefs and judgments, not at the level of raw sensory input.

## Recognition

Recognition occupies a central place in Russell's analysis of cognition. It bridges the gap between mere behavioral repetition ([[habit]]) and fully propositional [[knowledge]]:

> [009-024] …Recognition in this sense does not necessarily involve…

Russell distinguishes several senses of recognition:

| Type | Example | Cognitive Complexity |
|------|---------|---------------------|
| Behavioral recognition | Cat responds to familiar person | Minimal (habit-like) |
| Feeling of familiarity | "I've seen this before" | Moderate (affective component) |
| Conceptual recognition | "This is a [[Perception|percept]] of a cat" | High (involves classification) |

> [009-027] …our behaviourist views, that recognition consists in behaving in the same way…

Russell's behaviourist account of recognition treats it as a disposition to respond similarly to similar stimuli. This avoids positing a mysterious cognitive act, replacing it with observable patterns of [[behaviour]].

## Cognition and Knowledge

Russell's broader epistemology distinguishes between two forms of knowledge relevant to cognition:

| Type | Content | Cognitive Mechanism |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| Knowledge by acquaintance | Direct awareness of particulars | [[Sensation]] |
| Knowledge by description | Knowledge of objects via propositions | [[Judgment]], [[inference]] |

> [012-007] …united into a unity by the fact of cognition; hence when this unity is placed…

In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell focuses primarily on knowledge by acquaintance — our direct cognitive contact with sensations and [[memory-image|images]]. Knowledge by description, central to his earlier work on [[logic]] and language, recedes in importance as he attends to the psychological processes underlying cognition.

## The Limits of Cognitivism

Russell's analysis of cognition is notable for what it refuses to claim:

1. **Not all mental life is cognitive**: [[Instinct]], [[habit]], and [[emotion]] involve mental processes that are not primarily about knowledge
2. **Cognition does not require a cognitive subject**: The "I" that knows is a logical construction, not a substantial [[self]]
3. **Cognition is continuous with animal behavior**: The cognitive achievements of humans differ in degree, not kind, from those of other [[Animal|animals]]

This modesty about cognition aligns with Russell's naturalistic project. By refusing to treat cognition as a mystery, he makes it amenable to scientific investigation.
