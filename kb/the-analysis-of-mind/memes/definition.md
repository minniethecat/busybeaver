## Definition

Definition, in Russell's philosophical practice, is the precise specification of what a term means within a given theoretical framework. Russell does not theorize about definition as a topic in its own right; rather, he models it as a methodological tool. Throughout *The Analysis of Mind*, he offers definitions of key terms — "mental," "instinct," "desire," "mnemic phenomena," "matter," "perception," "image" — not as exercises in lexicography but as instruments for clarifying thought and exposing the ambiguities that generate philosophical confusion.

## Russell's Account

Russell's use of definition is performative rather than expository. He does not lecture on the nature of definition; he demonstrates its importance through his practice.

**(1) Definition as clarification.** Russell opens the book by noting that "the exact definition of the word 'mental' will, I hope, emerge as the lectures proceed" (001-001). This is characteristic: definitions are not given at the outset as axiomatic starting points; they emerge through analysis and are refined as the argument develops. A good definition is the endpoint of inquiry, not its precondition.

**(2) Stipulative definitions.** When Russell offers a definition, it is typically stipulative — he is specifying how he will use a term for the purposes of his analysis, not claiming to capture ordinary usage. Lloyd Morgan's "definition of 'instinctive behaviour'" is quoted and examined (002-017), and Russell acknowledges that such definitions are "framed for the purposes of" a particular theoretical approach (002-019). The value of a definition lies in its theoretical utility, not its fidelity to common sense.

**(3) Definition by causal criteria.** Russell's preferred method is to define mental phenomena by their causal relations rather than by intrinsic qualities. Images, for instance, are defined by their causes: they arise "without any stimulus external to the brain" (008-031). Sensations are defined by having external causes. This causal approach to definition reflects Russell's broader naturalism: mental phenomena are defined by their place in the causal order, not by their feel.

**(4) Definition and the exposure of confusion.** Russell uses definitions to expose philosophical error. He notes that "a definition is not sound if it only applies" to a subset of cases (008-023) — a badly framed definition can create the illusion that a category is more unified than it actually is. The attempt to define "consciousness" precisely, Russell argues, reveals that there is no single thing answering to the term.

**(5) Working definitions.** Russell adopts "the following definitions for describing the behaviour of animals" regarding desire (003-011), and emphasizes that these are operational: "I believe these definitions to be adequate also to human behavior" (003-015). A definition is validated by its explanatory power across domains, not by its intuitive appeal.

## Key Distinctions

- **Stipulative vs. Reportive Definition**: Russell's definitions are stipulative — they specify how a term will be used in his analysis — rather than reportive of ordinary meaning.
- **Causal vs. Qualitative Definition**: Russell prefers causal criteria (what causes a phenomenon? what effects does it have?) over qualitative criteria (what does it feel like?).
- **Emergent vs. Axiomatic Definition**: Definitions emerge through analysis and are refined as the argument develops; they are not fixed axioms given at the outset.

## Historical Context

Russell's methodological use of definition reflects his training in logic and mathematics, where precise definition is fundamental. His approach also reflects the influence of G.E. Moore's method of analysis — the careful dissection of concepts to expose their logical structure. In *The Analysis of Mind*, Russell extends this analytic method from logic to psychology, treating psychological concepts with the same rigor he had brought to mathematical ones. His causal approach to definition was influenced by the behaviourist emphasis on operational criteria: a concept is defined by the observable operations used to measure or detect it, not by an appeal to private introspection.
