## Overview

Behaviourism is a psychological school, of which "the protagonist is Professor John B. Watson, formerly of the Johns Hopkins University" (001-038). Its core methodological principle is that psychology as a science should concern itself only with publicly observable behaviour, not with private mental states accessible only through introspection. The school emerged in the early twentieth century as a reaction against introspectionist psychology and was influenced by comparative psychology — the study of animal behaviour.

## Key Figures

- [[John B. Watson]] — founder and principal advocate
- John Dewey — associated with behaviourism and co-founder of pragmatism (001-038)

## Russell's View

Russell engages with behaviourism primarily in Lecture I of *The Analysis of Mind*, where he treats it as one of the key influences supporting his rejection of consciousness as an entity. He describes the behaviourist position sympathetically: behaviourists "do not by any means deny that all sorts of things MAY go on in our minds: they only say that such things, if they occur, are not susceptible of scientific observation, and do not therefore concern psychology as a science" (001-038).

Russell finds behaviourism illuminating for the analysis of knowledge: "Thus what is called 'knowing,' in the sense in which we can ascertain what other people 'know,' is a phenomenon exemplified in their physical behaviour, including spoken and written words" (001-041). The behaviourist reduction of knowledge to language-habits provides a powerful critique of the Cartesian assumption that introspection yields special access to thought.

However, Russell does not fully endorse behaviourism. He notes that "the behaviourists somewhat overstate their case" (001-042) and, in the lecture on memory, explicitly states: "I do not myself believe that the analysis of knowledge can be effected entirely by means of purely external observation, such as behaviourists employ" (009-002). Russell's position is a middle way: he adopts behaviourist methodology for analysing publicly observable aspects of mind, while insisting that images and belief-feelings require a broader analysis.

## Key Doctrines

- **Methodological externalism**: only publicly observable behaviour is admissible as scientific data
- **Denial of introspection**: no separate source of knowledge about one's own mind
- **Language-habit account of knowledge**: "knowing" is a set of acquired verbal habits
- **Continuity with animal psychology**: human behaviour should be explained by the same principles as animal behaviour
