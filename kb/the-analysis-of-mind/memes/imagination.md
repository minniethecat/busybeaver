## Definition

Imagination, in Russell's analysis, is the mental process of forming images that lack the specific feeling of pastness and the belief-accompaniment characteristic of memory. While imagination-images and memory-images "do not differ in their intrinsic nature" (009-037), they are distinguished by "the fact that the images in question are accompanied by a belief-feeling and a specific feeling of pastness" in the case of memory, feelings that are absent in mere imagination (011-010). Russell draws on Hume's criterion: "The Humean distinction between images of imagination and images of memory is that the former are less vivid" (009-035).

## Russell's Account

Russell treats imagination primarily through its contrast with memory, devoting Lectures VIII through X to the distinction. He acknowledges the difficulty of separating them: "I am not at all sure that I could do what is required, as far as possible only in imagination" (008-034), referring to the challenge of purely imagining bodily movements without any actual motor impulse.

The key insight in Russell's account is that the difference between memory and imagination is not intrinsic to the images themselves but lies in their causal context and accompanying mental attitudes. Russell notes that we can entertain "mere imagination-images to which no assent is given" (012-040), and that "mere imagination does not involve consciousness of reality" (015-005). This is contrasted with memory, which carries a belief-feeling about the past.

Russell offers a genetic account: "the memory-image in the child and an imagination-image in the hearer" may be identical in content — what makes one memory and the other imagination is the causal history of how the image was produced (010-032). He distinguishes "the way of memory and the way of imagination" (010-034), grounding the difference in the mnemic causal chain.

## Key Distinctions

Russell draws three central distinctions involving imagination:

- **Memory vs Imagination**: Memory-images are accompanied by a feeling of pastness and a belief-feeling; imagination-images lack both (011-010). The images themselves may be intrinsically indistinguishable (009-037).
- **Imagination vs Perception**: Imagination involves kinesthetic sensations similar to but fainter than those in actual movement — "such as we have when we imagine moving an arm or, on a smaller scale, when we image a form" (008-034). Unlike perception, imagination is not constrained by current sensory input.
- **Pure Imagination vs Prototype-Based Imagination**: Russell questions whether a "pure imagination-image, without any prototype" truly exists, suggesting that imagination always recombines elements derived from past experience (010-046). "I can imagine a man with two heads, or the lower half of a centaur" (011-005), but these are novel combinations of familiar elements.

## Historical Context

The imagination-memory distinction traces to Hume's *Treatise of Human Nature* (1739), where vividness serves as the criterion. Russell, writing after the behaviourist revolution and drawing on the mnemic framework of [[Richard Semon]], recasts the distinction in causal-genetic terms rather than introspective ones. His account integrates the physiological psychology of his time: imagination, like memory, depends on mnemic phenomena, but the causal chain in imagination is more complex and less directly tied to a past sensory stimulus (004-010).

## Criticism

Russell acknowledges a difficulty in his account: the "way of memory and the way of imagination" distinction works for the producer of an image but is less clear for an external observer. When a hearer forms an image based on someone else's memory-description, the hearer's image is imagination while the speaker's is memory — yet both images may have identical content (010-032). This asymmetry challenges any purely intrinsic criterion for distinguishing the two, and Russell's appeal to causal history, while philosophically sophisticated, pushes the distinction outside the realm of direct introspective awareness.
