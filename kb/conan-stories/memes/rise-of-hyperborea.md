# Rise of Hyperborea

## Overview

The rise of Hyperborea was the founding of the first Hyborian kingdom, marking a pivotal transition in Hyborian history — the shift from nomadic tribalism to settled civilization. Howard describes it as "one of the few more dramatic events in history than the rise of the rude, fierce kingdom of Hyperborea, whose people turned abruptly from their nomadic life to rear dwellings of naked stone, surrounded by cyclopean walls" (017-020).

## Prelude

For fifteen hundred years, the Hybori people had spread across the north, growing into "a tall, tawny-haired, grey-eyed race, vigorous and warlike, and already exhibiting a well-defined artistry and poetism of nature" (017-015). They lived mainly by hunting, with southern tribes raising cattle, and their wars had been only against one another. A key development preceded Hyperborea's rise: "a tribe of that race has discovered the use of stone in building" (017-020).

## The Event

The tribe that discovered stone construction "soon abandoned their horsehide tents for stone houses, crudely but mightily built" (017-020). Protected by these structures, they grew strong. Howard marvels at the improbability of the achievement — "a race scarcely emerged from the polished stone age, who had by a freak of chance, learned the first rude principles of architecture" (017-020). The first Hyborian kingdom had "its beginning in a crude fortress of boulders heaped to repel tribal attack" (017-020).

## Aftermath

The rise of Hyperborea triggered a cascade of tribal migrations. Many clans, "defeated in the war, or refusing to become tributary to their castle-dwelling kinsmen, set forth on long treks that took them halfway around the world" (017-021). These drifts would spread the Hyborians across the western world, founding the kingdoms — Aquilonia, Nemedia, Koth, Ophir, Argos, and others — that would later dominate the Hyborian Age. Hyperborea itself would eventually be overthrown by a northern tribe, though the conquerors retained the old name (017-022).
