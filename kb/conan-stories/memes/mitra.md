Mitra is the primary deity of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] peoples, a god of light, truth, and righteousness whose worship dominates the kingdoms between [[aquilonia]] in the west and [[turan|Turan]] in the east. Unlike [[crom]], the grim and indifferent god of the [[cimmerians]], Mitra represents an organized, priest-led religion with moral teachings and ritual practices.

## Nature and Worship

Mitra is portrayed as a benevolent, if distant, god who expects righteousness from his followers. His worship involves temples, a priestly hierarchy, and established rituals—a stark contrast to the temple-less, prayer-less faith of [[crom|Crom]].

> "Mitra! grant that I be not too late!" [008-152]

The Mitraic faith is the official religion of [[aquilonia|Aquilonia]], Nemedia, and most other [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms. Its priests hold considerable social and political influence, and the name of Mitra is frequently invoked in oaths, blessings, and moments of desperation by characters throughout the corpus.

### Core Tenets

Mitraic doctrine, as revealed through the stories, includes:

- **Light over darkness**: Mitra is the god of light, and his followers associate him with truth, clarity, and moral righteousness
- **Protection of the innocent**: Mitra's followers are expected to defend the weak against evil and [[magic-in-the-hyborian-age|sorcery]]
- **Opposition to [[set|Set]]**: the Mitraic faith stands in direct opposition to the serpent-god [[stygia|Set]] of [[stygia|Stygia]], whose dark [[magic-in-the-hyborian-age|sorcery]] represents everything Mitra opposes
- **Priestly authority**: Mitra's priests serve as moral guides, judges, and occasionally political advisors

## Mitra vs. Crom

The theological contrast between Mitra and [[crom|Crom]] is one of the central philosophical tensions in Howard's [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]]:

| Aspect | Mitra | [[crom|Crom]] |
|--------|-------|------|
| Domain | Civilized [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms | [[cimmerians|Cimmerian]] north |
| Worship | Temples, priests, rituals | None—no prayer, no temple |
| Character | Benevolent, may answer prayers | Grim, indifferent, silent |
| Gift to men | Moral order, community | Strength to strive and slay |
| Relation to [[magic-in-the-hyborian-age|sorcery]] | Opposed | Unconcerned |
| Priesthood | Organized hierarchy | None |

This contrast maps onto Howard's broader theme of civilization versus barbarism. Mitra represents the order, ritual, and moral structure of civilization; [[crom|Crom]] represents the raw self-reliance and fatalism of the barbarian. Neither is presented as morally superior—Mitra's civilization breeds decadence and corruption, while [[crom|Crom]]'s barbarism can tip into cruelty and chaos.

## Historical Context

The [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] essay (Chapter 017) provides the most detailed account of Mitra's historical role:

> Mitra's worship spread with the Hyborian migrations from the north, displacing older, darker cults. By Conan's era, the Mitraic faith was the unifying religious framework of the western Hyborian world, much as the Christian Church unified medieval Europe—but without the same degree of centralized authority. [017-032 through 017-037]

The Mitraic religion functions as a civilizing force that binds together the disparate [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms through shared ritual and moral language, even as they war against one another politically.

## In the Stories

Mitra is invoked by characters across the social spectrum—from kings and priests to soldiers and commoners. Key appearances include:

- **Epimetreus the Sage** invokes Mitra's protection [002-045]
- **[[conan|Conan]]**, despite being a [[cimmerians|Cimmerian]] who follows [[crom|Crom]], occasionally swears by Mitra when in civilized lands [002-152, 002-283]
- **[[belit|Bêlit]]** acknowledges Mitra alongside other gods [003-080]
- **The priests of Mitra** in [[aquilonia|Aquilonia]] are referenced as political actors [006-119 through 006-156]

[[conan|Conan]]'s occasional use of Mitra's name in oaths—rather than [[crom|Crom]]'s—while traveling in [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms demonstrates his pragmatic adaptability: he respects local custom without abandoning his [[cimmerians|Cimmerian]] heritage.

## Significance

Mitra serves as the theological backbone of Howard's [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] civilization. Where [[crom|Crom]] embodies the philosophy of radical self-reliance, Mitra embodies the social contract—the belief that order, justice, and mutual obligation have value. The tension between these two worldviews runs through the entire [[conan|Conan]] corpus and is central to understanding the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]] as a coherent fictional world.
