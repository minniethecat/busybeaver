Tascela is the immortal princess of the [[zogar-sag|Tecuhltli]] and the most terrifying presence in Howard's novella **Red Nails**. Behind her youthful appearance lies a centuries-old [[stygia|Stygian]] sorceress who feeds on the vitality of young women to maintain her ageless beauty — a predator who has outlived dynasties and reduced an entire civilization to ashes through her insatiable hunger.

## The Woman Behind the Mask

Tascela is first introduced to [[arus|Valeria]] and [[conan]] by Prince Olmec at the Tecuhltli feast, but the introduction barely hints at what she truly is. To the untrained eye, she is simply a beautiful princess — dark-eyed, enigmatic, and disturbingly focused on Valeria:

> "This woman is Tascela," he said. "She is a princess of Tecuhltli." [012-034]

But the truth, revealed layer by layer through the story, is far more monstrous. Tascela is not Olmec's daughter or sister — she was the wife of Xotalanc himself, stolen by the Tecuhltli a half-century ago in the act that began the blood feud:

> "No. She is — Tascela! She was the wife of Xotalanc — the woman Tecuhltli stole, to start the feud." [012-102]

And even this is a half-truth. In reality, **Tascela predates the feud itself by centuries**. She is ancient — older than Xuchotl, older than the tribe that worships her. She was a girl in Stygia when a priest fell in love with her and granted her the secret of immortality:

> "I am old, so old I do not remember my childhood. But I was a girl once, and a priest of Stygia loved me, and gave me the secret of immortality and youth everlasting. He died, then — some said by poison. But I dwelt in my palace by the shores of Lake Zuad and the passing years touched me not." [015-057]

## The Price of Immortality

Tascela's youth is maintained through a ritual of vampiric transference — she drains the life essence from beautiful young women, absorbing their vitality into her own body:

> "She renews her life and her youth by the sacrifice of beautiful young women. That's one thing that has reduced the clan to its present state. She will draw the essence of Valeria's life into her own body, and bloom with fresh vigor and beauty." [016-014]

This revelation reframes the entire tragedy of Xuchotl. The blood feud is not merely a cycle of revenge — it is part of a larger ecology of death in which Tascela is the apex predator. The dwindling population of the city is not only the result of intertribal warfare but of a more insidious drain: the princess who consumes her own people, generation after generation, to feed her unnatural longevity.

## The Eyes of Tascela

Howard titles Chapter 15 "The Eyes of Tascela," and the choice is telling. Tascela's most potent weapon is not her sword arm (though she fights with lethal skill) but her gaze — a hypnotic, paralyzing force that can reduce even the fiercest warrior to a trembling captive:

> It was not fear of the dominant strength that lurked in the princess' limbs that made Valeria a helpless, quivering captive in her hands. It was the burning, hypnotic, terrible eyes of Tascela. [015-059]

Valeria, who has never feared another woman in her life, discovers a new kind of terror in Tascela's presence:

> Valeria had always been inclined to despise the other members of her sex; and it was overwhelming to encounter another woman who could handle her like a child. [015-047]

## A Predator in the Blood Feud

Tascela's relationship to the Tecuhltli-Xotalanc feud is one of cold indifference. Unlike her tribesmen, who rage and exult with each victory or defeat, Tascela views the conflict with the detachment of someone who has seen a hundred such wars come and go:

> While Olmec talked, Valeria felt the blazing eyes of Tascela fixed upon her. The princess seemed not to hear what Olmec was saying. Her expression, as he narrated victories or defeats, did not mirror the wild rage or fiendish exultation that alternated on the faces of the other Tecuhltli. The feud that was an obsession to her clansmen seemed meaningless to her. [012-077]

When the final battle erupts between the two tribes, Tascela fights — not out of loyalty, but because slaughter is simply another form of entertainment to a being who has outlived all conventional morality:

> Tascela was the only one of the Tecuhltli who had escaped without a wound. The princess was untouched, though she had fought as savagely as any. [014-044]

## Immortal Cruelty

Tascela's most disturbing quality is her complete transcendence of normal human emotion. She is not merely cruel — cruelty implies investment, passion, the desire to cause pain for its own sake. Tascela is beyond such engagement. Her actions — poisoning Olmec, binding Valeria for the ritual, casually disposing of those who have served her for decades — are performed with the detachment of someone for whom other beings are merely resources:

> Tascela was beyond the grip of habit, as she was beyond the touch of other human instincts and emotions. [015-054]

Olmec, who has served her for decades, finally admits the truth:

> "I've never been anything but a puppet in Tascela's hands." [016-012]

## Significance

Tascela is one of Howard's most memorable antagonists and a figure who transcends the conventions of pulp villainy. She is not a scheming sorcerer-king like [[zogar-sag|Thoth-amon]] or a brute warlord — she is something older, colder, and more fundamentally alien: a being who has lived so long that human life holds no more meaning for her than cattle hold for a butcher.

Her presence in "Red Nails" transforms what could have been a simple adventure story into a meditation on entropy, decay, and the horror of immortality without purpose. Xuchotl is her creation — not through direct action, but through the slow, corrosive effect of her existence on the society that shelters her. The blood feud is merely the most visible symptom of a deeper sickness: a people who have been slowly consumed, generation by generation, by the beautiful monster in their midst.
