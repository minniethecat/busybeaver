## Overview

Hanuman is a dark deity worshipped in the remote and decadent civilizations of the Black Kingdoms. Represented by a leering carven ape-mask and served by sinister priesthoods in ancient, vine-choked temples, Hanuman belongs to the same pantheon of shadowy gods that includes [[Set]] and [[Dagon]] — primordial entities whose worship persists in the forgotten corners of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] world. Unlike the widely-invoked gods of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] north, Hanuman's cult is intensely local, centered on specific ruined temple complexes where the god's idols still command the devotion — and the blood sacrifices — of those who remember the old ways.

## Worship and Shrines

Hanuman's primary shrine appears in the lost city of Alkmeenon, deep in the jungles of [[Keshan]]. The shrine is described as an ancient temple where the god's image leers from the darkness — a carven ape-mask set above a black stone altar [009-120]. The shrine is not merely a relic of a dead religion but an active site of worship, tended by priests who maintain the ancient rites and guard the temple's secrets.

> "Then follow me to Hanuman's shrine, and the gods have mercy on us!" [009-120]

The altar of Hanuman is constructed of black stone, following the pattern common to dark [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] deities (compare the black altars of [[Set]] and the stone temples of [[Dagon]]). Offerings are placed upon this altar by worshippers who approach the god through the temple's labyrinthine passages, guided by priests who alone know the way [009-129].

> "In the uncertain light Hanuman leered with his carven mask" — a description conveying the unsettling presence of the god's image [009-131]

### The Ape-God and His Nature

Hanuman's simian form distinguishes him among the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] deities. While [[Set]] is serpentine and [[Jhebbal Sag]] is a diffuse nature-spirit, Hanuman is specifically anthropoid — a monstrous, intelligent ape whose image suggests a disturbing proximity between the divine and the bestial. This positions Hanuman among the "ape-gods" occasionally referenced in Howard's work, beings that represent humanity's fear of its own animal origins.

Unlike the more abstract gods of civilization, Hanuman's physical representation is concrete and immediate. The carven mask is not a symbol standing for the god — it is the god's face, leering directly at the worshipper from the temple shadows. This immediacy of divine presence is characteristic of the older, pre-[[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] religious traditions that survive in the jungles and deserts of the south.

## Hanuman in the Black Kingdoms

Hanuman's cult is situated within the broader religious landscape of the Black Kingdoms, a region known for its ancient, decaying civilizations and the persistence of pre-[[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] religious practices. The Black Kingdoms — [[Keshan]], [[Punt]], and the lost cities of the interior — harbor temples to gods that the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms have long forgotten. Hanuman is representative of this pattern: a god whose worship survived the fall of the civilization that created it, maintained by isolated priesthoods in ruins that the outside world has forgotten.

The cult of Hanuman is not evangelistic; it does not seek converts or expand its influence. Instead, it endures — a remnant of an older religious order, waiting in the darkness of its temples for worshippers who remember the ancient rites. This endurance without expansion is characteristic of the dark gods of Howard's world: unlike [[Mitra]], whose worship spreads with civilization, the cults of gods like Hanuman survive precisely by avoiding the notice of the civilized world.

## Relationship to Other Deities

Hanuman exists within the theological framework of the Black Kingdoms and southern [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] world, which includes [[Set]] (the preeminent dark god of [[stygia|Stygia]]), [[Dagon]] (a [[dagon|fish-god]] of the ancient empires), and various unnamed ape-gods and beast-deities. These gods do not form a structured pantheon but rather a constellation of dark powers, each with its own domain and priesthood, occasionally overlapping in their spheres of influence.

The ape-god of Hanuman's shrine in Alkmeenon is described in terms that recall the broader tradition of simian deities in Howard's work — beings that embody a primordial, pre-human consciousness and demand worship from humans who have regressed to a state closer to the beasts than to the civilized peoples of the north.
