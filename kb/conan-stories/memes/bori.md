# Bori

## Overview

Bori is the deity of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] people in Robert E. Howard's fictional prehistory — a figure at the intersection of history, legend, and religion. Howard describes him as "some great chief, whom legend made even more ancient as the king who led them into the north, in the days of the great Cataclysm, which the tribes remember only in distorted folklore" (017-014). Bori represents the process by which a historical tribal leader became elevated to divine status across millennia of oral tradition.

## Origins and Legend

The origin of Bori is rooted in the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] migration following the [[Great Cataclysm]]. Howard places the emergence of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]]s — or Hybori — a thousand years after a lesser cataclysm, when "a curious race of intelligent, though stunted, aborigines" was conquered by fair-skinned invaders from the north (017-014). These conquerors swept away the aboriginal kingdom and "built their own huts over the ruins of a civilization they did not understand" (017-014).

It is in this context that Bori emerges:

> These people are called Hyborians, or Hybori; their god was Bori—some great chief, whom legend made even more ancient as the king who led them into the north, in the days of the great Cataclysm, which the tribes remember only in distorted folklore. (017-014)

The passage reveals several key aspects of Bori's significance: he was originally a mortal leader, his memory was transformed by legend into a divine figure, and his story is intertwined with the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] people's foundational migration narrative.

## Euhemeristic Interpretation

Howard presents Bori through a distinctly euhemeristic lens — interpreting a god as a deified historical figure. The phrase "their god was Bori—some great chief" (017-014) explicitly demythologizes the deity, suggesting that [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] religion originated in ancestor worship elevated to the level of divine cult. This treatment is consistent with Howard's broader approach to the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]] as fictional prehistory: even its gods have rational, historical origins.

The "distorted folklore" that the tribes remember further emphasizes the gap between historical reality and religious tradition. The [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]]s' own understanding of their god had become so transformed by time that they no longer recognized him as a mortal man.

## Bori in Hyborian Civilization

By the time of the mature [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms, Bori's cult appears to have been largely supplanted. The [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] nations of [[conan|Conan]]'s era worship [[Mitra]], the "gentle" god adopted by the civilized kingdoms, rather than the tribal deity of their ancestors. [[Crom]], the grim god of the [[Cimmerians]], represents a different religious tradition entirely — one rooted in [[cimmerians|Cimmerian]] descent from the [[Atlanteans]] rather than [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] origins.

The eclipse of Bori mirrors the broader trajectory of [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] civilization: from tribal wanderers with a chief-god to cosmopolitan kingdoms with an organized priesthood. Only in isolated provinces like Gunderland, where "the people keep no slaves" and preserve "pure [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] stock" (017-027), might traces of the old Bori-cult have survived.
