# Arus

## Overview

Arus was a Nemedian priest of Mitra whose missionary work among the Picts became one of the great ironies of Hyborian history. A "natural-born reformer," Arus ventured alone and unarmed into the Pictish Wilderness, determined to "modify the rude ways of the heathen by the introduction of the gentle worship of Mitra" (017-032). He succeeded beyond his wildest dreams — but in a direction that would have horrified him.

## Mission to the Picts

Against all expectation, Arus was not instantly killed upon arriving among the Picts. He was "fortunate in being thrown in with a chief of more than usual intelligence — Gorm by name" (017-034). Through a combination of genuine idealism and practical persuasion, Arus convinced Gorm to allow him to remain among the tribe unbutchered — "a case unique in the history of the race" (017-034).

Howard paints a vivid contrast between the two men: "Arus was the highest product of an innately artistic race, refined by centuries of civilization; Gorm had behind him a heritage of a hundred thousand years of screaming savagery — the pad of the tiger was in his stealthy step, the grip of the gorilla in his black-nailed hands" (017-034). In Gorm's mud-floored wattle hut, beneath rows of human skulls, Arus expounded the eternal truths of Mitra while the chief listened in inscrutable silence.

## The Irony of Progress

Arus was a practical man. Finding vast deposits of iron ore in the Pictish hills, he taught the natives to mine, smelt, and work the metal into implements — "agricultural implements, as he fondly believed" (017-036). He also instilled in Gorm a desire to see the civilized world and established contact between the Picts and the Hyborian kingdoms. The Picts now came and went freely into Aquilonia, learning armor-forging and sword-making from the very people Arus hoped they would emulate in peace (017-039).

The results were catastrophic. The Picts, armed with iron weapons and united under Gorm's leadership, burst upon the Bossonian frontiers "not clad in tiger-skins and brandishing copper axes as of yore, but in scale-mail, wielding weapons of keen steel" (017-042).

## Death and Legacy

Arus saw his mistake too late. "He had not touched the soul of the pagan, in which lurked the hard fierceness of all the ages" (017-041). He was brained by a drunken Pict while making a last effort to undo the work he had unwittingly done. Gorm, not without gratitude, caused the slayer's skull to be set atop the priest's cairn — "one of the grim ironies of the universe that the stones which covered Arus's body should have been adorned with that last touch of barbarity — above a man to whom violence and blood-vengeance were revolting" (017-042).
