## Overview

Ishtar is the [[shem|Shem]]itish goddess of love and fertility, one of the most widely invoked deities of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]]. Her name appears on the lips of warriors and sailors far beyond the borders of her native [[Shem]], used as an exclamation and oath by characters ranging from pirate crews to mercenary captains. Unlike the grim gods of the north like [[Crom]] or the dark deities of [[Stygia]], Ishtar represents a more accessible divinity — one concerned with human passion, love, and the generative forces of life.

## Worship and Cult

The worship of Ishtar was centered in the [[shem|Shem]]itish city-states, where her temples were important civic institutions. The [[shem|Shem]]ites recognized Ishtar alongside other deities including Ashtoreth, Derketo, and Adonis [003-087], forming a pantheon typical of the urban civilizations of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] south. Her cult spread beyond [[shem|Shem]] through trade routes and mercenary companies, and her name became a common oath throughout the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms.

> "I, by Ishtar!" — the characteristic oath of Ishtar's worshippers, used as a battle cry and exclamation [002-191]

In [[Khauran]], the worship of Ishtar was particularly well-established. The temple of Ishtar in the capital served as a major religious center until the events of *A Witch Shall Be Born*, when the usurper Constantius — installed by the witch Salome — abolished the worship of Ishtar and converted her temple into a shrine to other gods [006-156]. This act of religious persecution was part of the broader corruption of Khauran under the false Taramis.

### Devotional Practices

Ishtar's worshippers called upon her in moments of both crisis and passion. The characteristic invocation was simply her name — "Ishtar!" — used as an oath, a prayer, and a battle cry [002-073]. This direct, personal relationship between deity and worshipper was characteristic of [[shem|Shem]]itish religion, contrasting with the more formal, priest-mediated worship of gods like [[Set]] or [[Mitra]].

> "Oh, Ishtar, if you love true men, aid me now!" — a desperate prayer offered by a prisoner in Khauran's dungeons [006-190]

## In the Stories

Ishtar appears primarily as an invoked presence rather than an active participant in events. Her name punctuates the dialogue of numerous characters, serving as a marker of [[shem|Shem]]itish cultural identity and a reminder of the religious diversity of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] world. Characters who swear by Ishtar include the pirate crew of the *Tigress* [002-191], mercenaries fighting in the Black Kingdoms [007-114], and the people of Khauran defending their city against tyranny [006-190].

The goddess is never physically manifested — unlike the more tangible supernatural entities encountered in stories like *The Devil in Iron* or *The People of the Black Circle*. This reflects Howard's typically restrained treatment of the "good" deities of his world: they are real forces of order and life, but they act through their worshippers rather than through direct supernatural intervention.

## Ishtar Among the Hyborian Gods

Ishtar occupies an intermediate position in the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] theological landscape. She is neither a grim and distant god like [[Crom]] — who grants nothing to his worshippers but courage at birth — nor a dark and actively malevolent force like [[Set]]. She represents the more conventional, civilization-oriented deities of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] south: gods who are called upon for aid, who reward devotion, and whose worship is integrated into the fabric of urban life.

> "Some gods are strong to harm, others, to aid" — Conan's theological observation, which applies to the distinction between gods like Set and gods like Ishtar [003-080]
