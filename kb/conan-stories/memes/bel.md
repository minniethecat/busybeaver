## Overview

Bel is the [[shem|Shem]]itish god of thieves, a trickster deity born in ancient Shumir who occupies a unique position in the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] pantheon. Unlike the grim and demanding gods like [[Crom]] or the dark and fearsome deities like [[Set]], Bel is described with playful affection — "he went forth laughing, with curled beard and impish wise eyes, to steal the gems of the kings of old times" [003-087]. He is, in [[conan|Conan]]'s succinct assessment, "a good god" [003-080] — a deity who aids his worshippers rather than demanding their submission or sacrifice.

## Nature and Character

Bel's defining attribute is his role as god of thieves. This is not a dark or malevolent portfolio but a roguish one — Bel protects those who live by their wits and their skill at stealth. [[conan|Conan]], who learned of Bel during his years as a thief in [[Zamora]] [003-080], speaks of him with something approaching fondness, a stark contrast to the barbarian's typically cynical attitude toward gods.

> "Bel, god of thieves, is a good god. When I was a thief in Zamora I learned of him." — Conan's endorsement of the trickster deity [003-080]

Bel's character embodies the trickster archetype: clever, irreverent, and fundamentally on the side of the underdog. He steals from the powerful — "the gems of the kings of old times" — rather than oppressing the weak. This sets him apart from the hierarchical, priesthood-dominated cults of gods like [[Set]] or [[Mitra]], whose worship reinforces social structures rather than subverting them.

## Origins in Ancient Shumir

Bel's origins trace back to ancient Shumir, a civilization that predates even the [[shem|Shem]]itish city-states of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]]. "He was born in ancient Shumir, long, long ago" [003-087] — a detail that places Bel among the oldest gods of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] world, his worship stretching back to the dawn of urban civilization in the region.

> "Bel, too, is Shemitish, for he was born in ancient Shumir, long, long ago and went forth laughing, with curled beard and impish wise eyes, to steal the gems of the kings of old times." [003-087]

Despite his great antiquity, Bel's nature has not darkened with age — unlike gods like [[Set]], whose primordial origins manifest in a fearsome and malevolent character. Bel remains the laughing trickster, his impish nature apparently immune to the corruption that afflicts older and more powerful deities. This may reflect the essential nature of his domain: thievery requires wit and skill rather than terror, and a god of thieves who inspired fear would be a contradiction in terms.

## Bel Among the Shemitish Gods

Bel belongs to the [[shem|Shem]]itish pantheon alongside [[Ishtar]], Ashtoreth, Derketo, and Adonis [003-087]. Within this pantheon, he occupies a distinct niche — neither a goddess of love and fertility like [[ishtar|Ishtar]], nor a figure of cosmic significance like the more powerful deities. He is a specialist god, the patron of a particular profession and way of life, whose worshippers are defined by their craft rather than their nationality or social status.

This professional specialization is unusual among [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] deities. Most gods — [[Mitra]], [[Set]], [[Crom]] — claim the allegiance of entire peoples or kingdoms. Bel's worshippers, by contrast, are united by what they do rather than where they come from. A thief in [[Zamora]], a thief in [[Shem]], and a thief in the [[Hyborian Age|Hyborian kingdoms]] all fall under Bel's protection, regardless of their other religious affiliations.

## Conan's Relationship with Bel

[[conan|Conan]]'s familiarity with Bel dates to his youth as a thief in [[Zamora]] [003-080], a period of the [[cimmerians|Cimmerian]]'s life when he lived by stealth and cunning rather than by the sword. That [[conan|Conan]] — who famously trusts no god and relies on his own strength — speaks well of Bel is significant. The [[cimmerians|Cimmerian]]'s endorsement ("Bel, god of thieves, is a good god") represents one of the few positive theological statements in the entire [[conan|Conan]] corpus.

[[conan|Conan]]'s theological pragmatism — "Some gods are strong to harm, others, to aid" [003-080] — finds its most sympathetic expression in Bel. Gods who harm, like [[Set]], demand propitiation through fear; gods who aid, like Bel, earn their worshippers' loyalty through tangible benefit. For a practical man like [[conan|Conan]], the distinction between a god who demands and a god who gives is the only theological distinction that matters.
