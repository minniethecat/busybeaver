Techotl is a warrior of the [[zogar-sag|Tecuhltli]] tribe who first encounters [[arus|Valeria]] and [[conan]] in the labyrinthine city of Xuchotl in Howard's novella **Red Nails**. A man poised between desperate courage and paralyzing terror, Techotl serves as the newcomers' guide through the blood-feud that has consumed his people for generations.

## Encounter with Valeria

Techotl first appears when Valeria, wandering alone through the alien halls of Xuchotl, stumbles upon him standing over the body of a slain Xotalanc enemy. The encounter is tense and disorienting — Valeria has just destroyed a glowing skull of sorcerous origin, and Techotl's reaction reveals the superstition that governs life within Xuchotl's walls:

> "My name is Techotl. I am of Tecuhltli. I and this man who lies with his throat cut came into the Halls of Science to try and ambush some of the Xotalancas. But we became separated and I returned here to find him with his gullet slit. The Burning Skull did it, I know, just as he would have slain me had you not killed him." [011-102]

When Valeria shatters the sorcerous skull, Techotl is simultaneously awed and terrified — his reaction captures the dual nature of his character: a hardened warrior who nevertheless lives in constant fear of the supernatural forces that pervade Xuchotl:

> Techotl's hand was plucking at her arm and he was gibbering: "You have broken it! You have destroyed it! Not all the black arts of Xotalanc can rebuild it! Come away! Come away quickly, now!" [011-112]

## The Blood Feud

Techotl belongs to the Tecuhltli, one of the two surviving tribes locked in an endless war of extermination within the sealed city of Xuchotl. Their enemies, the Xotalancas, are marked by a white skull painted on the breast — a symbol that Techotl fears with an intensity bordering on the pathological.

When a party of Xotalanc warriors ambushes the group, Techotl fights with the ferocity of a man with nothing left to lose. Howard's description of the combat is unflinching:

> There were no challenges or war-cries. Like blood-mad tigers the men of Xotalanc sprang at the throats of their enemies. Techotl met them with the fury of desperation, ducked the swipe of a wide-headed blade, and grappled with the wielder, and bore him to the floor where they rolled and wrestled in murderous silence. [011-116]

The aftermath of the skirmish reveals the grim arithmetic of the blood feud — the "crimson nails" driven into a black pillar, a ritual tally of the dead:

> "Five dead dogs!" exclaimed Techotl, his flaming eyes reflecting a ghastly exultation. "Five slain! Five crimson nails for the black pillar! The gods of blood be thanked!" [011-127]

## Character Traits

### Fanatical Courage

Techotl's bravery is real but brittle — it draws not from confidence but from desperation. He fights because surrender is not an option; capture by the Xotalancas means a fate worse than death. His courage is the courage of the cornered animal, and it cracks repeatedly into tremors of terror when the supernatural intrudes.

### Superstitious Terror

Throughout "Red Nails," Techotl oscillates between two emotional poles: battle-fury and abject fear. The supernatural elements of Xuchotl — the Burning Skull, the sorcerous traps, the eerie silence of the halls — terrify him far more than any blade. His physical trembling at the approach of danger is described repeatedly:

> Again a shaking seized Techotl, and he clenched his teeth to keep them from chattering. [011-149]

> "Run!" yelped Techotl, a note of hysteria in his voice. [011-155]

### Tribal Loyalty

Despite his terror, Techotl never abandons his tribal allegiance. He guides Valeria and Conan to the Tecuhltli stronghold, introduces them to Prince Olmec with formal ceremony, and fights to the death for his people. His identity is inseparable from his tribe:

> Techotl said: "This is Tecuhltli!" [011-171]

## Guide and Herald

Techotl's narrative function in "Red Nails" is that of a guide and herald — he introduces the outsiders (and the reader) to the nightmare world of Xuchotl. His explanations of the city's layout, the blood feud's history, and the tribe's customs provide the exposition that makes the story's setting comprehensible:

> "They roam through the corridors at all hours," answered Techotl, "as do we. The halls and chambers of Xuchotl are open to all. Only the strongholds are guarded." [011-147]

His formal presentation of Conan and Valeria to Prince Olmec establishes the social hierarchy of the Tecuhltli and the precarious diplomatic tension within the doomed city.

## Significance

Techotl embodies the tragedy of Xuchotl — a man of genuine courage and loyalty whose potential is consumed by an endless, pointless blood feud. He fights brilliantly, guides faithfully, and trembles constantly, caught between the warrior he must be and the terrified man he cannot help being. His death (implied in later chapters) is one of many that marks the final dissolution of the Tecuhltli people.

Through Techotl, Howard illustrates the psychological cost of tribal warfare taken to its ultimate extreme — a world where every corridor is a battlefield, every shadow conceals an enemy, and victory means nothing because the war never ends.
