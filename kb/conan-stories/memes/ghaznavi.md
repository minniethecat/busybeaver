# Ghaznavi

Ghaznavi is the chief counselor to Jehungir Agha, a lord of [[Turan]], in Robert E. Howard's Conan story "The Devil in Iron" (published in *Weird Tales*, August 1934). He is the architect of the scheme to use [[Octavia]] as bait to lure [[Conan]] to the island of [[Xapur]], and his cunning makes him one of the more memorable secondary villains in the Conan corpus.

## Counselor to Jehungir Agha

Ghaznavi serves as Jehungir Agha's most trusted advisor. When Jehungir contemplates how to deal with the threat posed by Conan—who has become a pirate chief on the Vilayet Sea—he turns immediately to Ghaznavi:

> Jehungir bluntly demanded his counselor Ghaznavi. [004-013]

Ghaznavi is described as an older man who "tugged his gray-shot beard" in contemplation [004-016], a gesture that signals his deliberative and calculating nature. Unlike the impulsive Jehungir, Ghaznavi embodies the patient, strategic mind—a man who thinks before he speaks and weighs every variable before proposing a course of action.

## The Xapur Plan

When Jehungir asks for a strategy to eliminate Conan, Ghaznavi lays out the core problem: Conan has risen to prominence among the kozaks and pirates of the Vilayet:

> "A leader has risen among them," answered Ghaznavi. "You know whom I mean." [004-021]

He notes that the other kozaks are at least partially loyal to their new chief, making a direct assault risky. His solution is characteristically indirect—lure Conan to a place where he can be trapped:

> Ghaznavi spoke of an island near the mainland, "known as Xapur, the Fortified..." [004-029]

The plan is elegantly simple: use Octavia, the beautiful Nemedian captive, as bait to draw Conan to Xapur, where Jehungir's forces can ambush him. Ghaznavi explains his reasoning:

> "We will ask for a parley..." Jehungir listened with growing approval as Ghaznavi continued. [004-032]

When Jehungir expresses amazement at the scheme's audacity, Ghaznavi calmly elaborates the details [004-032]. The plan is built on Ghaznavi's assessment of Conan's psychology—the Cimmerian will not resist the chance to claim both a beautiful woman and a strategic advantage.

## Strategic Mind

Ghaznavi's intelligence is demonstrated by his ability to anticipate Conan's responses. When Jehungir questions whether the Cimmerian will fall for the trap, Ghaznavi counters with confidence:

> "The chances are all that he will." retorted Ghaznavi. [004-042]

He has studied Conan through the reports of Turanian intelligence, recognizing that the Cimmerian's boldness can be turned against him. Ghaznavi has considered what he terms Conan's "animal intuition"—the barbarian's instinct for danger—but concludes that clever bait will override it [004-063].

Jehungir is impressed enough by the plan to promise Ghaznavi a reward if it succeeds [004-048]. The counselor's position depends on delivering results, and the Xapur scheme represents his most ambitious gambit.

## Aftermath

The plan goes awry for reasons Ghaznavi could not have anticipated—the reawakening of [[Khosatral Khel]], the ancient iron demon whose fortress on Xapur was supposed to be an uninhabited ruin. Conan survives not because Ghaznavi's trap was poorly conceived, but because a force far beyond human scheming intervened.

Ghaznavi's ultimate fate is not explicitly detailed in Howard's text, but his status as Jehungir's counselor suggests he survived the Xapur debacle. The failure of his scheme would have been a significant blow to his standing at court, but his demonstrated cunning makes it likely he found ways to deflect blame onto other parties.

## Significance

Ghaznavi represents the Hyborian Age equivalent of the courtly schemer—the advisor who fights with words and plans rather than swords. His characterization draws on Howard's recurring interest in the conflict between civilization's cunning and barbarism's directness. Ghaznavi's elaborate trap should have worked against any normal adversary; it fails only because Conan is something more than a normal man, and because Xapur holds secrets that no human counselor could have predicted.

The character also demonstrates Howard's nuanced depiction of Turanian civilization. Ghaznavi is not a cartoonish villain but a capable strategist playing a dangerous game with high stakes. His failure is not due to incompetence but to the intrusion of the supernatural into what should have been a purely political calculation.
