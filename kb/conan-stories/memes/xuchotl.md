# Xuchotl

Xuchotl is the ancient, enclosed city that serves as the setting of Robert E. Howard's Conan novel "Red Nails" (serialized in *Weird Tales*, July–October 1936). Located deep in the jungles south of [[Stygia]], Xuchotl is a windowless, fortress-like structure built by a vanished pre-human race and now inhabited by the degenerate Tlazitlans, who are locked in a generations-long war of mutual annihilation within its walls.

## Geography

Xuchotl is situated in the trackless jungles of the far south, beyond the [[Black Kingdoms]] and the reach of any Hyborian civilization. The city itself is a single enormous structure—a vast enclosed edifice without windows or exterior openings, built from an unknown purple-black stone that glows faintly with internal phosphorescence:

> The city of Xuchotl rose from the jungle like a single monstrous jewel, its walls gleaming with a light that was no reflection of the sun.

The interior of Xuchotl is a labyrinth of chambers, halls, corridors, and great open spaces lit by the phosphorescent walls. The city is divided into two halves by a central wall, with each half controlled by one of the warring Tlazitlan factions. The architecture is cyclopean and alien, built on a scale that dwarfs human occupants—evidence of the pre-human race that originally constructed it.

The surrounding jungle is dense and hostile, effectively isolating Xuchotl from the outside world. No roads lead to the city; it can only be reached by cutting through miles of uncharted wilderness.

## Inhabitants

The current inhabitants of Xuchotl are the Tlazitlans, a human people who discovered the abandoned city generations ago and have since degenerated into a culture of ritualized warfare. They are divided into two factions that occupy opposite halves of the city, separated by a fortified wall. The war between these factions has been ongoing for so long that its original cause is forgotten.

[[Techotl]] and [[Tascela]] are the most prominent Tlazitlan characters. Techotl is a warrior of one faction who encounters Conan and [[Valeria]] at the city's edge. Tascela, the "witch" of the Tlazitlans, is an immortal sorceress who has preserved her youth through dark magic and whose machinations drive the story's central conflict.

The Tlazitlans are described as having degenerated physically and culturally. Their society has simplified to the point where the only meaningful activity is the war between factions. They speak a debased dialect and have forgotten most of the knowledge that their ancestors possessed.

## History

Xuchotl was built in a remote age by a non-human race whose identity is never fully revealed. The city's cyclopean architecture and self-luminous walls suggest a civilization with capabilities far beyond those of the Hyborian Age. At some point before the rise of human kingdoms, the original builders vanished, leaving Xuchotl empty.

The Tlazitlans discovered the city centuries before the events of "Red Nails." They took up residence within its walls, finding the enclosed structure a perfect fortress against the jungle's dangers. Over time, a schism divided the Tlazitlans into two factions, and the war between them became the defining feature of their existence. The conflict has persisted for so many generations that no living Tlazitlan remembers how or why it began.

Conan and Valeria arrive at Xuchotl while fleeing Pictish raiders in the jungle. Their presence catalyzes the final collapse of the Tlazitlan society, as Tascela's schemes, the inter-faction warfare, and the intervention of the two outsiders converge in the city's destruction.

## Appearances

Xuchotl appears exclusively in "Red Nails," where it serves as both the physical setting and a thematic symbol of the story's concerns with decadence, isolation, and the self-destructive nature of cyclic violence.

## Real-World Parallels

Xuchotl shares characteristics with the lost city trope common in pulp adventure fiction, drawing on real-world archaeological discoveries of Mesoamerican ruins and the popular fascination with "lost civilizations" in the early 20th century. The enclosed, lightless city with its degenerate inhabitants has parallels in Howard's other lost-city stories, including "The Devil in Iron" (with the fortress of Dagon on Xapur).
