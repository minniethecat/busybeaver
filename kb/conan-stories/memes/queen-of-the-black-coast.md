## Overview

**"Queen of the Black Coast"** is one of the most celebrated [[Conan]] stories by Robert E. Howard, first published in the May 1934 issue of *Weird Tales*. The story follows [[conan|Conan]]'s flight from [[argos|Argos]], his meeting with the legendary [[shem|Shem]]itish pirate queen [[Bêlit]], and their reign of terror along the Black Coast. The tale is notable for its poetic prose, its tragic romance, and its grim climax — an encounter with a winged horror dwelling among the ruins of a lost civilization.

> Between the yard and her white throat stretched a line of crimson clots that shone like blood in the gray light. [003-151]

## Plot Summary

### Escape from Argos

The story opens with [[conan|Conan]] fleeing the city guard of an [[argos|Argossean]] port. With pursuers closing in, he leaps from the pier onto the deck of the *Tigress*, a pirate galley commanded by [[belit|Bêlit]], the [[shem|Shem]]itish "Queen of the Black Coast." Rather than killing the intruder, [[belit|Bêlit]] is drawn to [[conan|Conan]]'s savage vitality and accepts him as her lover and co-captain [003-002].

> "I am Bêlit, queen of the black coast! Who are you?" [003-010]

### Reign of Terror

Together, [[conan|Conan]] and [[belit|Bêlit]] raid the coasts of the black kingdoms with a crew of black corsairs. Their partnership is one of genuine passion — [[belit|Bêlit]] declares that she has found in [[conan|Conan]] the one man worthy of her, and [[conan|Conan]], for the first time in his life, forms a deep emotional bond. Their raids bring them immense plunder and a fearsome reputation across the southern seas [003-020].

### The Winged Horror

The *Tigress* sails up a mysterious jungle river and discovers the ruins of an ancient pre-human city. Disembarking to explore, the crew is gradually picked off by a winged, bat-like creature that dwells among the crumbling stone. [[conan|Conan]] and [[belit|Bêlit]] find themselves trapped in a nightmare of attrition, as the unseen horror methodically hunts the pirates one by one through the dark jungle [003-133].

### Climax and Tragedy

The creature — implied to be a survivor of a primordial race — kills [[belit|Bêlit]], hanging her body from the yardarm of her own galley as a warning. [[conan|Conan]], consumed by grief and fury, tracks the winged horror to its lair in the ruined city and slays it in single combat. He then builds a funeral pyre for [[belit|Bêlit]], laying her among her plunder like a true queen, and watches the flames burn into the dawn [003-151][003-184].

> Like a true queen she lay, with her plunder heaped high about her: silks, cloth-of-gold, silver braid, casks of gems and golden coins, silver ingots, jeweled daggers and teocallis of gold wedges. [003-153]

## Setting

The story takes place along the **Black Coast** — the southwestern shores of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]], south of [[Kush]] and the black kingdoms, where jungle meets sea. The later portion of the story occurs in a nameless ruined city up a jungle river, a relic of a pre-human civilization predating even the [[thurian-age|Thurian Age]]. The geography of the region is deliberately vague, evoking the unmapped and primordial southern continent.

## Themes

- **Love and Death**: The story is one of Howard's most romantic works, juxtaposing [[conan|Conan]]'s genuine passion for [[belit|Bêlit]] against the constant presence of violence and mortality.
- **Primordial Horror**: The winged creature embodies the Lovecraftian theme of ancient, inhuman survivals from epochs before mankind — a horror that cannot be reasoned with or bargained with.
- **The Barbarian vs. Civilization**: [[conan|Conan]]'s vitality and directness contrast with the decadent, crumbling ruins of the pre-human city, continuing the series' central thematic tension.
- **Fate and Tragedy**: [[belit|Bêlit]]'s death — despite her power and [[conan|Conan]]'s strength — underscores the randomness of fate in Howard's universe.

## Publication

*Queen of the Black Coast* was first published in *Weird Tales*, May 1934. It is widely considered one of Howard's finest [[conan|Conan]] stories, noted for its poetic language and emotional depth. The story has been collected in numerous [[conan|Conan]] anthologies including *The Coming of [[conan|Conan the Cimmerian]]* (Del Rey, 2003).

## Connections

- [[Bêlit]] is referenced in later [[conan|Conan]] stories including *[[jewels-of-gwahlur|Jewels of Gwahlur]]*, where [[conan|Conan]]'s fame as "a chief of the black corsairs" precedes him.
- The Black Coast appears or is referenced in multiple [[conan|Conan]] tales, serving as the southern boundary of the known [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] world.
- The story establishes the template of [[conan|Conan]]'s tragic romantic relationships, echoed in his later encounters with characters such as [[valeria|Valeria]] of the [[Red Brotherhood]].
