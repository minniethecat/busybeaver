# Pictish Invasion of the Bossonian Marches

## Overview

The Pictish Invasion of the Bossonian Marches was the military campaign that shattered the centuries-old frontier between the civilized Hyborian kingdoms and the Pictish wilderness. Led by Gorm, the Pictish "chief of chiefs," the invasion marked the moment when the Picts transformed from a frontier nuisance into an existential threat to Hyborian civilization (017-040)(017-042).

## Prelude

The groundwork for the invasion was laid by the Nemedian priest Arus, who taught the Picts iron-working "as he fondly believed" for peaceful agricultural purposes (017-036). Under Gorm's leadership, the Picts used this knowledge to forge weapons. Gorm sent Pictish warriors to serve as mercenaries in Aquilonia's armies, where they returned with "good ideas of civilized warfare, and that contempt for civilization which arises from familiarity with it" (017-040). "Drums began to beat in the hills, gathering-fires smoked on the heights, and savage sword-makers hammered their steel on a thousand anvils" (017-040).

## The Invasion

The Picts burst upon the Bossonian frontiers "with fire and sword, not clad in tiger-skins and brandishing copper axes as of yore, but in scale-mail, wielding weapons of keen steel" (017-042). For years, the superior armaments and stubborn courage of the Bossonians held the invaders at bay, "aided, when necessary, by imperial Aquilonian troops" (017-043). Gorm, now past middle age, had waited decades for this moment (017-040).

The turning point came through "treachery from an unexpected source" (017-044). Contentions between the Aquilonians and their Bossonian auxiliaries — who felt they were treated with growing contempt by their imperial masters — led the Bossonians to withdraw their support. The frontier defenses, designed to work as an integrated system of Bossonian walls and Aquilonian cavalry, collapsed when the two components turned against each other (017-046).

## Aftermath

The broken frontier opened Aquilonia to conquest. The Pictish empire that emerged stretched across the entire western world. Gorm, who had "worn a corselet of silvered mail now, instead of the tiger-skin, but underneath he was unchanged — the everlasting barbarian, unmoved by theology or philosophy, his instincts fixed unerringly on rapine and plunder" (017-041), lived to see his life's work accomplished.
