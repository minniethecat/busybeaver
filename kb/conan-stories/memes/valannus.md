# Valannus

Valannus is the commander of Fort Tuscelan, the Aquilonian outpost on the Pictish frontier, in Robert E. Howard's Conan story "Beyond the Black River" (published in *Weird Tales*, May–June 1935). He is a young but competent officer who represents the doomed civilization attempting to hold the border against the Pictish wilderness. His death in the final assault on Fort Tuscelan is one of the story's most memorable moments.

## Commander of Fort Tuscelan

Valannus commands the undermanned fort that serves as Aquilonia's last line of defense against the [[Picts]] of the Black River. He is described as still a young man but well known along the frontier:

> Valannus was still a young man, well known on the frontier... [008-102]

His relationship with Conan is professional and respectful. He values Conan's skills as a scout, acknowledging that the Cimmerian is "of more use ranging the woods" than serving as a garrison soldier [008-018]. This pragmatic judgment reflects Valannus's understanding of frontier warfare—he knows that conventional military thinking has limits against the guerrilla tactics of the Picts.

## The Death of Tiberias

When Conan brings news of the merchant [[Tiberias]]'s mysterious death, Valannus is forced to confront the reality of supernatural forces on the frontier. Conan reports the discovery directly:

> "But listen, Valannus, we found Tiberias dead on the trail." [008-111]

Valannus reacts with shock, paling at the news [008-112]. He had not known Tiberias was traveling the trail alone—the merchant had been a fool to attempt the journey. When Conan presses him on the larger strategic implications, Valannus makes no reply, merely wiping his brow in consternation [008-114].

The confrontation with the supernatural visibly disturbs him:

> Valannus shuddered. Turning, he walked... [008-119]

Yet Valannus rallies. He speaks his thoughts aloud, weighing the strategic reality:

> "After all," said Valannus, as if speaking his thoughts... [008-120]

He decides to act on Conan's intelligence, even when it means acknowledging a threat that defies conventional military doctrine. Conan had spoken truly—no white man on the frontier had encountered anything like Zogar Sag's power before [008-176].

## The Fall of Fort Tuscelan

Valannus's strategic position is ultimately hopeless. He commands an undermanned outpost against an enemy whose numbers and supernatural resources far exceed anything the Aquilonian military can match. The fort's construction reflects Aquilonian expansionist policy rather than defensive prudence:

> "Valannus wanted a fort built at the mouth of the river..." [008-299]

Conan reflects that Valannus and men like him "have to obey the whims of their superiors" [008-300]—ambitious governors and kings who push the frontier forward without providing the resources to hold it.

When the Picts launch their final assault on Fort Tuscelan, Valannus fights to the last. He is killed in the desperate defense, standing among his men:

> "Valannus was killed among the last—in the final assault..." [008-407]

His death is not glamorous or triumphant. He dies, as so many frontier commanders did, holding a position that was never adequately supported. The men who survived could do nothing but die with him [008-315].

## Significance

Valannus is Howard's portrait of the tragic colonial officer—competent, dutiful, and doomed by forces beyond his control. He is not a villain; he is simply a man serving a system that has placed him in an impossible situation. His death alongside his garrison at Fort Tuscelan is one of Howard's most poignant depictions of the cost of empire.

The character serves as a foil for Conan—where the Cimmerian survives through primal instinct and battle prowess, Valannus dies because civilization's structures cannot hold against the wild. His fate underscores the story's central theme: that the Hyborian kingdoms' expansion into the Pictish wilderness is doomed, and the men who carry it out are sacrificed to political ambitions they cannot control.
