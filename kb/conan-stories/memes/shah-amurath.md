## Overview

Shah Amurath is a Turanian lord — specifically the Lord of Akif — who appears in *Iron Shadows in the Moon* as the cruel master of [[Olivia]] and the first antagonist [[Conan]] dispatches in the story. A noble of [[Turan]], the mightiest empire of the Hyborian Age, Shah Amurath embodies the refined cruelty of civilization in contrast to Conan's direct barbarism. His pursuit of the escaped Olivia brings him to the shores of the [[Vilayet Sea]], where Conan — a fugitive kozak and survivor of Shah Amurath's own massacre — kills him in a brief but brutal duel. Shah Amurath's death is the catalyst that throws Olivia and Conan together, setting the entire story in motion.

> "Shah Amurath, the great Lord of Akif! Oh, damn you, how I love the sight of you—you, who fed my comrades to the vultures, who tore them between wild horses, blinded and maimed and mutilated them—ai, you dog, you filthy dog!" — Conan's recognition of Shah Amurath [002-019]

## A Turanian Lord

Shah Amurath was a nobleman of [[Turan]], the empire ruled by King Yezdigerd from the port city of Aghrapur. As the Lord of Akif — a city of "purple gardens" — he commanded significant wealth and military power [002-043]. His station placed him among the Hyrkanian aristocracy that dominated the Vilayet Sea region, and he rode at the head of hosts that numbered in the thousands: a week before the story's opening, Shah Amurath had trapped a band of five thousand Free Companions with a force of fifteen thousand men near the Ilbars River [002-045].

His physical description marks him as a warrior of the ruling class: "tall, slender, but hard as steel," clad in light silvered mesh-mail that fit his form like a glove, with a gold-chased, dome-shaped helmet [002-002]. His eyes regarded others mockingly, and his laughter was described as "like the purr of a sword sliding from a silken sheath" [002-004]. He rode a tall, slender-legged desert stallion named Irem, which Olivia notes was the fastest horse west of Vilayet [002-005].

## The Cruel Master

Shah Amurath's relationship with Olivia defines his character. A desert trader, seeking to buy Shah Amurath's good will, had given Olivia to him as a gift at Akif [002-043]. In Shah Amurath's palace, Olivia endured sustained psychological torment. His words to her reveal a calculating, predatory mind:

> "You are strangely virile, Olivia. I wonder if I shall ever weary of you, as I have always wearied of women before. You are ever fresh and unsullied, in spite of me. Each new day with you brings a new delight." [002-007]

Shah Amurath's cruelty was not the explosive violence of a barbarian but the patient, methodical cruelty of a civilized sadist who took pleasure in his captive's suffering. Olivia later reflects that Shah Amurath — like her own father, the king — was a "civilized man," and from civilized men she had had only suffering [002-216]. He was the worst oppressor she had ever known.

## The Pursuit

When Shah Amurath returned from a successful military campaign against border raiders, a great celebration was held in his honor. In the drunkenness and rejoicing, Olivia seized her opportunity to escape, stealing a horse and fleeing the city [002-044]. Shah Amurath pursued her personally, outpacing his own vassals on his swift desert stallion Irem. By midday he had caught up with her, cornering her on a reed-fringed shore of the Vilayet Sea.

> "No, you will not drown, Olivia, daughter of confusion, for the marge is too shallow, and I can catch you before you can reach the deeps. You gave me a merry chase, by the gods, and all my men are far behind us." [002-004]

At the moment of his triumph, Shah Amurath found his path blocked by an unexpected obstacle: a naked, blood-caked giant emerging from the reeds — [[Conan]], survivor of the very massacre Shah Amurath had engineered a week before.

## The Duel with Conan

Shah Amurath recognized Conan immediately as a surviving kozak. "Kozak!" he ejaculated, recoiling. "I did not know a dog of you escaped! I thought you all lay stiff on the steppe, by Ilbars River" [002-015]. His reaction — recoiling, shouting "Keep back!" and watching Conan narrowly — betrays a man who understands that he faces a foe with every reason to hate him [002-018].

The duel was brief and one-sided. Conan charged with a maddened scream, driven by the accumulated fury of battle, massacre, torture, and hunger-haunted flight [002-019]. Their blades met in an instant of flashing steel — then Conan's broadsword crashed through Shah Amurath's saber and descended "terrifically on Shah Amurath's shoulder" [002-021]. The blow split his silvered mail, snapped his shoulder-bone with an audible crack, and sent blood spurting over the links of his hauberk. His saber slipped from nerveless fingers [002-021].

What followed was not combat but butchery. Conan's sword "rose and fell with the sound of a butcher's cleaver" as the Cimmerian vented his hatred upon Shah Amurath's body [002-024]. Even Olivia, who knew her tormentor deserved no mercy or pity, closed her eyes and pressed her hands over her ears to shut out the sight and sound of his death.

## Death and Legacy

Shah Amurath's death is swift, brutal, and final — a single paragraph that ends the life of a Turanian lord. His body is left on the shore as Conan takes Olivia away in a reed boat, knowing that Shah Amurath's "mailed dogs" would soon be close at hand [002-046].

Shah Amurath's legacy in the Conan stories is entirely catalytic: his cruelty toward Olivia drives her to escape; his pursuit brings her to the shore of the Vilayet; and his death at Conan's hands throws the barbarian and the Ophirean together. Without Shah Amurath, there is no meeting between Conan and Olivia, and no story. He serves as a representative of civilized evil — refined, calculating, and predatory — in contrast to the honest brutality of the barbarian who kills him.
