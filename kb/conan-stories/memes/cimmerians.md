# Cimmerians

## Overview

The Cimmerians are a barbarian race of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]], most famously represented by their son [[conan|Conan]]. They are the descendants of the ancient [[atlanteans|Atlanteans]], having climbed back from total savagery over thousands of years. Dwelling in the grey, gloomy hills north of [[aquilonia|Aquilonia]], the Cimmerians are ferocious savages untamed by the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] invaders, yet advancing rapidly through contact with civilization (017-023).

## Origins and Descent

The Cimmerians trace their lineage to the [[atlanteans|Atlanteans]], who after the [[great-cataclysm|Great Cataclysm]] were reduced to ape-like savagery — "sunk back into the squalling chaos of jungle-bestiality from which ages ago their ancestors so laboriously crawled" (017-012). Unlike their ancient rivals the [[picts|Picts]], who stagnated for millennia, the [[atlanteans|Atlanteans]] began the long climb upward, "completing the cycle of existence" and starting anew "unhelped and unhindered by human memories" (017-018). From these roots the Cimmerians eventually emerged as a distinct people.

## Physical Character and Culture

Physically, the Cimmerians are "tall and powerful, with dark hair and blue or grey eyes" (017-027). They have kept their bloodline pure through isolation, avoiding the racial mixing that transformed the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms. [[conan|Conan]] himself is described as a "Cimmerian" throughout the stories — black-haired, blue-eyed, towering in stature, with the wolfish instincts of his hill-country upbringing (002-035)(002-037).

The Cimmerians are a warlike people, living by the hunt and by raiding. Their country is described as a land of "barren, gloomy hills" where the "gloomy skies and drifting mists" bred a dour, fatalistic people. They worship [[crom|Crom]], a grim and distant god who cares little for mankind and whose name is invoked more often as an oath than in prayer (002-063)(002-069).

## Role in Hyborian History

Throughout the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]], the Cimmerians served as a constant pressure on the northern borders of the civilized kingdoms. They fought the [[picts|Picts]], the [[bossonian-marches|Bossonians]], and the [[nordheim|Nordheimir]] impartially, and "several times raided into [[aquilonia|Aquilonia]] itself" — though their wars were "less invasions than mere plundering forays" (017-031). [[aquilonia|Aquilonia]]'s attempt to subdue [[cimmeria|Cimmeria]] ended in defeat, one of the rare military failures of the empire, as the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] heavy cavalry proved helpless against the ferocious hand-to-hand fighting of the northern barbarians (017-045).

In the later history of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]], after the fall of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms and the [[picts|Pictish]] empire, the Cimmerians were among the Nordic races that swept southward before the advancing glaciers. They were destined, along with the Æsir and Vanir, to become the ancestors of modern European peoples (017-055).

## Religion

The Cimmerians worship [[crom|Crom]], a grim and distant god who dwells on a great mountain and cares little for the affairs of mortals. Unlike the elaborate temple hierarchies of the Hyborian kingdoms or the sorcerous priesthoods of [[stygia|Stygia]], Cimmerian religion is stark and minimal: a warrior invokes his god's name but does not pray to him. As Conan explains, "His communing with his god is on the battle field, with red blood flowing and his sword singing" (002-063). Howard presents Crom as reflecting the Cimmerian worldview itself — indifferent, powerful, and scornful of weakness. Cimmerian religion has no priests, no temples, and no ritual beyond the naming of newborns and the burial of the dead (002-069).

## The Cimmerian Character

Howard's portrait of the Cimmerians was shaped by his reading in nineteenth-century ethnography and by his own celebration of barbarism as a corrective to decadent civilization. The Cimmerians are not noble savages — they are brutal raiders, and their "plundering forays" into civilized lands are motivated by poverty and hunger rather than ideology (017-031). Yet Howard clearly admired their ferocity, their directness, and their immunity to the softening effects of civilization. Conan, as their exemplar, succeeds in the civilized world precisely because his Cimmerian upbringing gave him attributes — physical resilience, instinctual perception, moral simplicity — that civilization has bred out of its own citizens (017-023)(017-027). In this sense, the Cimmerians are central to Howard's overarching thesis: that civilization is not a permanent achievement but a fragile and cyclical phenomenon, repeatedly overthrown by the barbarian vitality it both despises and depends upon.
