# Gorulga

Gorulga is the high priest of [[Keshan]] and one of the primary antagonists in Robert E. Howard's Conan story "Jewels of Gwahlur" (published in *Weird Tales*, March 1935). He leads the expedition to recover the legendary Treasure of Gwahlur from the abandoned temple in the mountains, but his greed and his susceptibility to deception make him the story's most prominent victim of supernatural retribution.

## High Priest of Keshan

Gorulga is introduced as the spiritual leader of Keshan and the driving force behind the treasure expedition. When his priests and followers grow impatient with the delays in the search, Gorulga exercises his authority:

> With some impatience, the high priest Gorulga announced that before any decision could be made... [007-019]

His position is one of considerable power. The [[Keshani]] people revere their priesthood, and Gorulga commands both religious and political influence. Yet Conan quickly assesses that Gorulga is not the simple holy man he presents himself to be:

> No observer would for a moment have doubted that Gorulga was as subtle and devious as any politician. [007-045]

Conan also suspects that [[Thutmekri]], the Stygian emissary, has corrupted Gorulga—bribed him or offered him a share of the treasure in exchange for influence over the expedition [007-041].

## The Oracle Deception

Gorulga's expedition to the temple is driven by a mixture of religious fervor and material greed. He believes—or purports to believe—that the oracle Yelaya can be consulted and that the Treasure of Gwahlur rightfully belongs to the temple of Keshan.

When [[Muriela]], impersonating the oracle Yelaya, delivers false prophecies from the oracle chamber, Gorulga is the first to be deceived. He leads the parade of priests to the chamber:

> Conan saw Gorulga at the head of the parade... [007-137]

Gorulga's voice booms "eerily and hollowly in the great dark chamber" as he intones a chant [007-139]. He is performing the role of high priest with theatrical gravitas, unaware that the "oracle" speaking to him is a Corinthian dancing-girl hidden behind the dais.

When the false Yelaya speaks, Gorulga cries out in awe:

> Gorulga cried in the deep, rich resonance of a priest in the grip of religious passion... [007-142]

His eyes glitter "fanatically" [007-145]—Gorulga is not merely playing a role; he genuinely believes in the power of the oracle, and this belief makes him vulnerable to the deception.

## The Treasure and Death

Once the deception succeeds, Gorulga moves to claim the treasure. He leads his acolytes deeper into the temple, following the trail to the chamber of the bestial god where the Treasure of Gwahlur is hidden [007-191].

> Before this repugnant deity Gorulga and his ten acolytes knelt and began their ritual. [007-194]

The ceremony that follows is intended to consecrate the recovery of the treasure, but it instead awakens something ancient and malevolent. The true Yelaya—or whatever force animates the oracle's body—appears:

> "Yelaya!" screamed Gorulga, his brown features ashen. "Who—what—" [007-199]

Gorulga looks "like a shocked vulture" [007-202] as the terror of the situation overtakes him. He leads the flight from the chamber, jamming through the doorway with his priests [007-206].

But Gorulga's escape is short-lived. When he reaches for the treasure chest, the temple's guardian strikes:

> Gorulga, after lifting his arms aloft, reached a long arm into the recess and brought up a small brass chest. [007-239]

The guardian—a massive ape-like creature—crushes him:

> Gorulga screamed and fell backward, the chest flying from his hands. [007-242]

> A fist like a sledgehammer smashed down, and Gorulga's cries were stilled. Limp and broken, his body was flung aside... [007-243]

Gorulga dies clutching the treasure he came to claim. The chest, "still smeared with Gorulga's blood and clotted with his hair" [007-254], passes into Muriela's possession in the story's final act.

## Significance

Gorulga exemplifies a recurring character type in Howard's Conan stories: the religious authority whose piety is a mask for ambition. He is not entirely a fraud—his belief in the oracle appears genuine—but his willingness to use that belief for personal enrichment makes him complicit in his own destruction.

His death is fittingly ironic. The man who came to exploit the temple's secrets is killed by the very guardian he disturbed. The brass chest he clutched in his dying moments becomes a blood-stained trophy that passes from hand to hand, never truly belonging to anyone. Through Gorulga, Howard suggests that greed for ancient treasures inevitably brings ancient curses down upon the greedy.
