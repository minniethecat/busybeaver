## Overview

"Alafdhal" is the name by which [[Jungir Khan]], the satrap of Zamboula, is known during the period in which he is drugged into madness and held captive by the priest [[Totrasmek]]. The identity is a deliberate deception — "Alafdhal" is presented to [[Conan]] and the reader as a "poor captain of the guardsmen" [009-219], concealing his true status as the ruler of the city. The revelation of this dual identity is the central plot twist of *Shadows in Zamboula*.

> "I must now tell you the truth. I am not Zabibi. I am Nafertari. And he is not Alafdhal, a poor captain of the guardsmen. He is Jungir Khan, satrap of Zamboula." — The double revelation of identities [009-219]

## The False Identity

[[Zabibi]] first introduces the name "Alafdhal" to [[Conan]] when she seeks his help to rescue her lover from the cannibalistic Darfari in the Maul. She describes him as a man she loves, a victim of [[Totrasmek]]'s vengeance — the priest gave her a drug to mix with his wine, promising it would deepen his love, but instead it drove him into raving madness [009-094].

> "Why—Alafdhal. I am Zabibi, a dancing-girl. I have danced often before the satrap, Jungir Khan, and his mistress Nafertari..." — The carefully constructed cover story [009-094]

Throughout the early action, "Alafdhal" is treated as a helpless victim: unconscious, carried by [[Conan]] through the streets, and spoken of with genuine tenderness by the woman calling herself Zabibi. Her love for the man is unmistakably real — "She obviously loved Alafdhal sincerely" — even as the identity she presents is a fiction [009-113].

## The Reveal

The deception unravels when the woman reveals her own true identity as [[Nafertari]], the satrap's mistress. In the same breath, she exposes "Alafdhal" as [[Jungir Khan]] himself [009-219]. The implications are immediate: the man [[Conan]] has been carrying through the deadly streets of Zamboula is not a common soldier but the ruler of the city.

The satrap's ring — which [[Totrasmek]] had stolen, and which [[Conan]] recovers during the climax — serves as the physical proof of his true identity [009-202].

## Connection to Nafertari / Zabibi

The "Alafdhal" deception is inseparable from [[Zabibi]]'s own false identity as a mere dancing-girl. Both fabrications were necessitated by the political situation in Zamboula: with [[Jungir Khan]] drugged and helpless, and with [[Totrasmek]] wielding de facto control through his priesthood and the Darfari cannibals, revealing the satrap's true identity would have meant certain death. The fiction of "Alafdhal the captain" provided enough cover to seek help without triggering an immediate coup.
