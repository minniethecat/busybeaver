# Vendhya

Vendhya is an eastern kingdom of the Hyborian Age, ruled by the Devi (goddess-queen), in Robert E. Howard's Conan story "The People of the Black Circle" (serialized in *Weird Tales*, September–November 1934). It is the homeland of [[Yasmina]], the Devi who becomes Conan's companion and ally during his conflict with the [[Black Seers of Yimsha]].

## Geography

Vendhya occupies a region east of the Vilayet Sea, beyond the [[Himelian Mountains]] and the reach of the western Hyborian kingdoms. The kingdom's geography encompasses diverse terrain, from the fertile valleys of the Vendhyan heartland to the mountain passes that border the lands of [[Turan]] and the territories of the hill tribes. The opening of "The People of the Black Circle" places the kingdom's terrain at the meeting of starkly different landscapes:

> The king of Vendhya was dying. Through the hot, stifling night, the temple gongs and the conchs boomed their warning to the city of Ayodhya. [005-002]

The kingdom's position at the eastern edge of the Hyborian world places it at a cultural crossroads. Vendhya's trade connections extend both westward toward the Hyborian kingdoms and eastward toward the mysterious lands beyond, including [[Iranistan]] and the realms of the far east. The Vilayet Sea serves as Vendhya's primary maritime connection to the western powers.

## Inhabitants

The Vendhyans are a distinct people within the Hyborian world, described with cultural characteristics that draw on Howard's interpretation of Indian and Persian civilizations. Their society is hierarchical, dominated by a priestly caste and a warrior aristocracy that serves the Devi.

The Devi herself is both a political and religious figure—a goddess-queen whose authority is absolute within Vendhya's borders. Yasmina, the Devi during the events of "The People of the Black Circle," is described as young and beautiful but also politically astute and capable of decisive action. Her identity as goddess-queen is central to her character:

> I am Queen of Vendhya. [005-563]

Vendhyan culture includes a tradition of mysticism and sorcery distinct from the magic of the western Hyborian world. The conflict between Vendhyan sorcerers and the [[Black Seers of Yimsha]]—a rival magical order based in the remote mountains—drives much of the story's plot. [[Khemsa]], an acolyte of the Black Seers, is himself of Vendhyan origin.

The assassination of the Devi's predecessor by magical means sets the plot in motion:

> "The king of Vendhya was destroyed by magic," she said. [005-052]

## History

Vendhya's role in the Conan stories centers on the political and magical conflicts described in "The People of the Black Circle." The kingdom is threatened by the Black Seers of Yimsha, a sorcerous order that seeks to dominate Vendhya through magical means. The assassination of the previous Devi by the Black Seers sets the plot in motion, as Yasmina—the new Devi—seeks both vengeance and the destruction of the threat.

The Devi's forces include five thousand mounted warriors who prove decisive in the climactic battle against the forces of [[Secunderam]]:

> With a crash that shattered the stars, five thousand riders of Vendhya smote the hosts of Secunderam. [005-613]

Yasmina's quest brings her into contact with Conan, who has been captured by Vendhyan forces while leading a band of Afghuli hill tribesmen. The alliance between the Cimmerian barbarian and the Vendhyan queen drives the narrative, as they navigate the schemes of multiple antagonists including [[Kerim Shah]], [[Yezdigerd]], and the Black Seers themselves.

[[Ghaznavi]], the Turanian counselor whose scheme against Conan drives the plot of "The Devil in Iron," also has connections to the Vendhyan political sphere, reflecting the interconnected nature of Hyborian Age politics.

## Notable Locations

- **Ayodhya** — The Devi's capital city and the center of Vendhyan political power
- **The Himelian Mountains** — The mountain range that forms Vendhya's natural border with the western lands
- **Yimsha** — The mountain fortress of the Black Seers, located in the remote heights beyond Vendhya's borders

## Appearances

Vendhya appears or is referenced in "The People of the Black Circle," which constitutes the primary source for the kingdom's depiction in Howard's canon. It is also referenced in "The Devil in Iron" (via the character Ghaznavi's regional connections) and in Howard's essay "The Hyborian Age," which places Vendhya within the broader historical framework of the Hyborian world.
