# Aram Baksh

Aram Baksh is the innkeeper of a tavern in Zamboula who appears as the primary human antagonist in Robert E. Howard's Conan story "Shadows in Zamboula" (published in *Weird Tales*, November 1935). He operates a gruesome enterprise: luring travelers to his inn, only to sell them to the cannibal slaves who roam the streets of Zamboula by night. His treachery provides the story's central conflict.

## The House of Aram Baksh

Aram Baksh's tavern is notorious among those who know Zamboula's secrets. The opening of the story wastes no time in establishing the danger:

> "Peril hides in the house of Aram Baksh!" [009-002]

A Zuagir tribesman warns Conan explicitly, listing the horrors connected to the inn:

> "Many travelers have slept in the house of Aram Baksh, and never been seen or heard from again!" [009-007]

The warning is vivid and urgent: "devils walk the streets of Zamboula by night from the house of Aram Baksh!" [009-009] Conan, characteristically, listens but does not turn away. He makes his way toward the tavern regardless [009-021].

## The Innkeeper's Mask

Aram Baksh presents himself as an unremarkable innkeeper. His physical description is deliberately unthreatening:

> Aram Baksh came forward, walking so softly that his feet made no sound. [009-029]

The soft-footed movement is the first hint of his true nature—a predator who moves in silence. He is fat and bearded, with a bushy appearance that conceals a cruel intelligence. When Conan checks in, Aram leads him down a winding corridor to a room [009-035], lighting a bronze lamp and making small talk:

> "You will sleep soundly tonight, Cimmerian," said Aram, blinking over his bushy beard. [009-038]

The innkeeper's unctuous manner is a mask. He stands fingering something—presumably a weapon—while speaking to Conan [009-041], and bolts the door from the outside after leaving the room [009-054].

## The Slave Trade

The full scope of Aram Baksh's trade becomes clear through Conan's investigation. He is not merely an innkeeper who happens to lose guests; he is the key supplier of human victims to the cannibal slaves of Zamboula:

> "Such men as Aram Baksh sell these strangers to the cannibals..." [009-086]

The system is straightforward: Aram identifies wealthy or isolated travelers, sees them to their rooms, then opens the doors at night for the cannibals to enter. The slaves take their victims, and Aram collects his payment. The bodies are never found.

The cannibals confirm the arrangement when Conan intercepts them:

> Aram Baksh promised us a man, but he has not given us one." [009-235]

Conan, with dark amusement, tells them to wait—Aram Baksh will indeed give them a man [009-236].

## Confrontation and Death

Conan does not flee the inn. Instead, he turns the tables on Aram Baksh, entering the innkeeper's private chamber:

> Conan knew that Aram Baksh slept in a chamber that could be reached... [009-240]

The confrontation is swift and brutal. Conan awakens Aram with a hiss, holding a knife:

> "Aram Baksh!" hissed Conan. "The black devils have sent me for you!" [009-242]

Aram fights desperately, but Conan overpowers him. Blood spurts in the starlight [009-246]. The innkeeper makes "hideous noises, gasping and strangling" [009-246], and fights "like a wild man" [009-248]—but his soft, fat body is no match for the Cimmerian. Conan drags him out to the cannibal slaves, who cannot recognize their supplier in the "half-naked, bloody figure" that Conan throws to them [009-249].

The irony is complete: the man who sold travelers to cannibals becomes their meal himself.

## Significance

Aram Baksh is one of Howard's most effectively drawn minor villains. His evil is mundane rather than supernatural—he is a businessman who has found a profitable but monstrous trade. The contrast between his avuncular public persona and his private treachery makes him a more chilling antagonist than the overt monsters Conan faces in other tales.

"Aram Baksh" also serves as a commentary on the lawlessness of frontier cities like Zamboula—places where civilization's thin veneer has worn away and predators operate openly. The innkeeper's success depends on the indifference of the authorities and the silence of those who know his secret. In killing him, Conan does not merely dispatch a villain; he eliminates a symptom of the deeper corruption that makes such enterprises possible.
