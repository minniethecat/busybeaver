## Overview

Jhebbal Sag is an ancient nature deity of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]], venerated in primordial times as the lord of all beasts and animals. In the era when "beasts and men spoke one language" [008-219], Jhebbal Sag ruled supreme over the natural world. His worship has faded with the rise of civilization and newer gods, but his power lingers in scattered sacred groves and through his "children" — individuals like [[Zogar Sag]] who inherit fragments of his dominion over the animal kingdom.

## Origins and Primordial Worship

The worship of Jhebbal Sag dates to the earliest epochs of human memory, long before the rise of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms or even the pre-Cataclysmic civilizations. In that primeval age, humans lived in closer harmony with nature, and the boundary between the human and animal worlds was permeable. Jhebbal Sag was the unifying deity of that era, commanding the allegiance of all beasts [008-217].

> "That was long ago, when beasts and men spoke one language. Men have forgotten him; even the beasts forget. Only a few remember." [008-219]

As humanity developed agriculture, cities, and civilization, the worship of Jhebbal Sag declined. New gods — [[Mitra]], [[Set]], [[Ishtar]], [[Crom]] — claimed the allegiance of civilized peoples, while the beasts themselves forgot their ancient lord. Yet Jhebbal Sag was never fully extinguished; his power retreated into the deep forests and hidden groves where civilization could not reach.

## The Children of Jhebbal Sag

Those who inherit Jhebbal Sag's power are known as his "children." The most prominent of these in [[conan|Conan]]'s time is [[Zogar Sag]], the [[picts|Pictish]] shaman who terrorized the [[bossonian-marches|Bossonian Marches]]. [[zogar-sag|Zogar Sag]]'s name itself declares his lineage — "a child of Jhebbal Sag who slept in the grisly depths of the Black River" [008-387].

> "The Children of Jhebbal Sag" — a section heading in *Beyond the Black River* devoted to explaining the nature of these beings [008-189]

The children of Jhebbal Sag possess the ability to command animals and summon creatures from the depths of the forest. [[zogar-sag|Zogar Sag]]'s power extended to beasts that normal men could neither control nor comprehend — swamp demons, forest devils, and creatures that had not been seen by human eyes for millennia [008-166]. However, this power is not absolute: "He can't command all the animals. Only such as remember Jhebbal Sag" [008-217].

### Powers and Limitations

A child of Jhebbal Sag's authority extends to:
- Summoning and commanding forest creatures and predators
- Communicating with beasts in their own language
- Making the sacred sign of Jhebbal Sag, which carries terrifying supernatural authority [008-385]
- Brewing "war-magic" — sorcerous workings that poison water supplies and spread disease [008-240]

The limitations are significant:
- Only animals that still remember Jhebbal Sag can be commanded — generations of domestication and civilization have erased the ancient memory from many species [008-217]
- The power is geographically tied to sacred groves and ancient forests [008-223]
- The children are mortal and can be killed by conventional means, though they are protected by their bestial servants

## Sacred Sites

An ancient grove sacred to Jhebbal Sag exists somewhere in the forests of the [[pictish-wilderness|Pictish Wilderness]] [008-223]. The grove contains a peculiar symbol that strikes terror into those who recognize its significance — a sign that "only a priest of Jhebbal Sag dare make" [008-385]. These sites are remnants of the god's primordial worship, maintained by the few who still remember the old ways.

## Decline and Persistence

Jhebbal Sag represents a recurring theme in Howard's [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] world: the decline of elder powers and their replacement by newer, more anthropomorphic deities. The god's trajectory mirrors the broader historical pattern of the [[Hyborian Age]] itself — ancient powers fading but never fully extinguished, their remnants lurking in the margins of the civilized world.

> The Hyborian Age essay describes how the earliest humans "still live mostly by the hunt, and the more elemental forms of nature" [017-015], reflecting the worldview of primordial nature-worship in which Jhebbal Sag held sway.
