# Stygia

## Overview

Stygia is an ancient kingdom of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]], lying far to the south of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms along the great river Styx (or Nilus). It is one of the oldest continuous civilizations in Howard's fictional world, predating the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] ascendancy by millennia and described as having "never been invaded" — a rare distinction among Hyborian Age nations (017-026). Stygia is characterized by its serpent-worship, its dusky-skinned ruling class, and its reputation for dark [[magic-in-the-hyborian-age|sorcery]]. At the time of [[conan|Conan]], it "sleeps" — untouched by foreign invasion but no longer the expanding power it once was (017-023).

## Origins

Stygia was founded by the survivors of a mysterious pre-human civilization of the far south. After the [[lemurians|Lemurian]] slaves rose up and destroyed their eastern masters during the chaos of the Cataclysm, the survivors fled westward and "fell upon that mysterious pre-human kingdom of the south and overthrew it, substituting their own culture, modified by contact with the older one" (017-013). Remnants of the older, non-human race seemed to have survived their overthrow and were even worshipped afterward. This origin in conquest and cultural fusion — a human civilization built atop the ruins of something pre-human — gave Stygia its unique character: ancient, decadent, and carrying secrets older than the human race itself (017-013).

## Religion and Sorcery

The religion of Stygia centers on the worship of [[set|Set]], the [[set|Old Serpent]], a deity both feared and revered throughout the Hyborian world. [[set|Set]] is described as "the arch-demon of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]]s but god of the Stygians" (003-025). This duality — seen as demonic by other nations but divine by its own worshippers — underscores Stygia's role as a dark mirror of [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] civilization. Stygian priests are renowned for their sorcerous powers, wielding a magic described as "the shadow of darkness" and "the shadow of unreason" (005-026). Stygian sorcery is feared throughout the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] world, and the kingdom's magical reputation is a key source of its diplomatic deterrence — fewer nations dare to challenge a theocracy that commands supernatural forces (005-026).

Among the dark arts cultivated in Stygia are the teachings of the [[book-of-skelos|Book of Skelos]], and legends persist of Stygian sorcerers who extend their lives through unnatural means, becoming "set off from humanity" (005-026). The priesthood of [[set|Set]] holds immense temporal power as well, with the king himself subordinate to or at least deeply entangled with the religious hierarchy.

## People and Society

Stygian society is sharply stratified. The ruling classes are "tall and well made, dusky, straight-featured," while the lower classes are "a downtrodden, mongrel horde, a mixture of negroid, Stygian, [[shem|Shem]]itish, even [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] bloods" (017-027). This racial hierarchy reflects Stygia's history of conquest and enslavement, which stretches back to the kingdom's foundation. To the south of Stygia stretch the vast [[black-kingdoms|black kingdoms]] of the Amazons, the [[kushites|Kushites]], the Atlaians, and the empire of [[zembabwei|Zembabwei]] (017-027).

The Stygian people are described as haughty and inward-looking, conscious of their ancient lineage and contemptuous of the younger Hyborian kingdoms to the north. Their isolationism is both a strength — it has preserved Stygia intact for millennia — and a weakness, as it leaves them blind to changes in the balance of power across the Hyborian world.

## Stygia in the Stories

In **[[belit|Queen of the Black Coast]]**, [[conan|Conan]] and [[belit|Bêlit]]'s pirate crew sail along the coasts of Stygia, described as a land of ancient evil. The mention of "survivors of butchered Stygian ships" naming [[belit|Bêlit]] with curses indicates the reach of Stygian maritime power (003-067). A Stygian galley fleeing into an uncharted river leads to one of the story's key encounters — the discovery of a ruined city and its monstrous inhabitant (003-069).

In **[[the-hyborian-age-essay|The Hyborian Age]]**, Stygia is portrayed as a kingdom that successfully resisted [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] domination — though at least once a Stygian army was "defeated with great slaughter" by [[aquilonia|Aquilonian]] forces, and the king of Stygia sent tribute to divert invasion (017-045). In the essay's historical frame, the Stygian empire survived the Hyborian age and continued to exist as a decaying power while the Hyborian kingdoms rose and fell around it.

## Powers and Weaknesses

Stygia's strength derives from three sources: its ancient magical traditions, its geographic isolation behind desert and river barriers, and the fanaticism of its serpent-cult that unifies the ruling class around a single, fearsome deity. No foreign army has ever occupied the Stygian heartland (017-026). However, its weaknesses are equally structural: an ossified social hierarchy that stifles innovation, a dependence on sorcerous power that provokes fear and hatred from neighbors, and a cultural arrogance that underestimates the vitality of younger, more dynamic civilizations such as the Hyborian kingdoms (017-027). The kingdom is described as sleeping — not dead, but no longer growing — and this somnolence makes it vulnerable to the accelerating changes of the Hyborian Age's final centuries.
