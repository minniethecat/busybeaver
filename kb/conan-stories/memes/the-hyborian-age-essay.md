**The Hyborian Age** is Robert E. Howard's pseudo-historical essay that serves as the foundational worldbuilding document for the entire [[conan|Conan]] canon. Written as a scholarly chronicle of a lost prehistoric era, it traces the migrations of peoples, the rise and fall of empires, and the geological catastrophes that shaped the world in which [[conan|Conan]]'s adventures take place — from the sinking of [[great-cataclysm|Atlantis]] to the dawn of recorded history.

## Overview

Howard composed "The Hyborian Age" as a reference document for his own use, mapping out the history that underlies the [[conan|Conan]] stories. It was not published during his lifetime; it first appeared in the fanzine *The Phantagraph* in 1938, two years after his death. Despite its modest origins, the essay is indispensable to understanding the [[conan|Conan]] corpus — it is the Rosetta Stone that transforms scattered references to [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms, [[cimmeria|Cimmerian]] migrations, and [[turan|Hyrkanian]] invasions into a coherent historical narrative spanning millennia.

The essay is written in the voice of a historian chronicling a lost age — Howard affects the tone of a sober academic describing events attested only in fragmentary records, lending the fantastical material a gravitas that makes the [[conan|Conan]] stories feel like reports from a real past rather than inventions of pure fancy.

## Structure

The essay proceeds chronologically through several distinct epochs:

### The Pre-Cataclysmic Age

The earliest period, covering the Thurian continent and the civilizations of [[valusia|Valusia]], Commoria, Grondar, and Thule, dominated by the human kingdoms that preceded the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]]s. The sinking of [[atlanteans|Atlantis]] and Lemuria — the [[great-cataclysm|Great Cataclysm]] — ends this age and reshapes the world's geography.

### The Rise of the Hyborians

The [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]]s, a fair-haired, grey-eyed people of the north, descend from their original homelands in a great southward drift. The essay traces their migrations, the founding of their kingdoms, and their encounters with older races:

> "But tribes of the Hyborians were drifting south, and as they moved they drove before them the dark-skinned inhabitants." [017-016]

### The Hyborian Kingdoms

The golden age of [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] civilization, when the kingdoms of [[aquilonia]], [[nemedia|Nemedia]], [[brythunia|Brythunia]], [[koth|Koth]], [[ophir|Ophir]], [[argos|Argos]], and [[corinthia|Corinthia]] dominated the western world. This is the period immediately preceding [[conan|Conan]]'s era, and its political arrangements form the backdrop of his adventures.

### The Hyrkanian Invasions

A series of devastating cavalry invasions from the east that shattered the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] hegemony and reconfigured the political map. This section provides the crucial bridge between Howard's fantasy prehistory and the world of recorded antiquity:

> "Then again the Hyrkanians rode from the blue east. The withdrawal of the imperial legions from Zamora was their incitement. Zamora fell easy prey to their thrusts, and the Hyrkanian king established his capital in the largest city of the country." [017-050]

### The Pictish Ascendancy

Following the [[hyrkanians|Hyrkanian]] conquests, the [[cimmerians|Picts]] — descendants of the aboriginal inhabitants pushed westward by the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] drift — rise to dominance, overrunning the remnants of the [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]] kingdoms.

### The Transition to History

The essay concludes by tracing the evolution of [[hyborian-age|Hyborian Age]] peoples into the historical cultures of the ancient world. The [[cimmerians|Cimmerians]] become the historical [[cimmerians|Cimmerians]] of the Black Sea region; the [[nordheim|Nordheim]]r become the Norse; the [[picts|Picts]], driven to the fringes, become the historical [[picts|Picts]] of Caledonia.

## Thematic Significance

### Worldbuilding as Literature

"The Hyborian Age" demonstrates Howard's commitment to internal consistency at a level rare in pulp fiction. The essay provides a framework that makes the [[conan|Conan]] stories feel like windows into a world that exists independently of any single narrative — a quality that would later be recognized as essential to modern fantasy worldbuilding.

### Cyclical History

The essay embodies Howard's cyclical view of history: civilizations rise, flourish, decay, and are destroyed by barbarians who then become the next civilization. The [[hyborian-age|Hyborian]]s themselves were once barbarians who displaced earlier peoples; their kingdoms are in turn destroyed by [[hyrkanians|Hyrkanian]] barbarians; the [[hyrkanians|Hyrkanians]] are then challenged by newer waves from the east. There are no permanent empires in Howard's universe — only temporary arrangements of power awaiting the next cataclysm.

### Barbarism as Renewal

Throughout the essay, Howard's thesis is consistent: civilization breeds decadence, and only barbarian vigor can sweep away the rot and begin the cycle anew. The essay provides the theoretical foundation for [[conan|Conan]]'s personal journey from barbarian to king — a trajectory that enacts, in miniature, the grand historical cycles the essay describes.

## Legacy

"The Hyborian Age" has influenced generations of fantasy authors. Its technique — the in-world reference document, written in the voice of a chronicler within the fictional universe — became a standard tool of fantasy worldbuilding, from Tolkien's appendices to the glossaries of modern epic fantasy series. Howard, writing for pulp magazines at a penny a word, invented a technique that would become the gold standard of the genre.
