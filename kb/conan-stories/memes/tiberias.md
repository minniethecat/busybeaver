# Tiberias

Tiberias is a merchant of Velitrium who appears as a minor but plot-significant character in Robert E. Howard's Conan story "Beyond the Black River" (published in *Weird Tales*, May–June 1935). His death at the hands of [[Zogar Sag]]'s swamp demon sets the story's supernatural stakes in motion and serves as the first clear evidence that the Picts are backed by forces beyond mere human warfare.

## Death on the Trail

Tiberias is first encountered as a corpse on the forest trail outside Fort Tuscelan. Conan discovers his body after investigating a mysterious scream:

> Conan tested the corpse with his foot—"Tiberias, a merchant of Velitrium." [008-050]

The death is immediately strange. No Pict killed him—there are no arrow wounds or hatchet marks, which would be typical of Pictish ambushes. Instead, the nature of his death is inexplicable:

> "...And now Tiberias is dead. No Pict killed any ordinary merchant... Tiberias was a fool to start for Velitrium alone." [008-051]

The circumstances are suspicious. Tiberias had set out from Fort Tuscelan for the settlement of Velitrium, traveling the trail alone—a decision that, in hindsight, was fatally unwise.

## The Demon's Victim

[[Conan]]'s investigation reveals that Tiberias was killed by the swamp demon of [[Zogar Sag]]—a supernatural entity that the Pictish shaman commands. The body shows no conventional wounds:

> "Tiberias gave one scream, and then his death-wound was not visible." [008-058]

Conan, with characteristic grim humor, notes that even armed men fall to this creature:

> "Tiberias was an armed man," grunted Conan. [008-060]

The Cimmerian determines to prevent the demon from claiming Tiberias's head—a resolution that leads to the discovery of the sacrificial altar where the merchant's severed head has been placed [008-069].

## The Altar of Zogar Sag

Conan tracks the demon to its lair, where he finds the evidence of Zogar Sag's dark rituals:

> There was the head of the merchant Tiberias. Behind the altar was an idol... [008-184]

The severed head confirms that Tiberias was not merely killed but ritually sacrificed—part of Zogar Sag's effort to raise supernatural forces against the Aquilonian settlements along the Black River. The discovery is reported to the fort:

> "That's Tiberias," blurted one. "I recognize the head..." [008-100]

[[Valannus]], the commander of Fort Tuscelan, is briefed on the situation. Conan explains what he found, forcing Valannus to confront the reality that supernatural forces are arrayed against the fort [008-111].

## Aftermath

Tiberias's body no longer possesses its head when Conan recovers it [008-086], taken for the demon's grim trophy. The head had been placed at the altar "not far from that spot" where Zogar Sag conducted his ceremonies [008-332].

The death of Tiberias serves several narrative purposes. It provides the first incontrovertible evidence of supernatural menace—a threat that even the pragmatic commander Valannus cannot dismiss. It also establishes Zogar Sag as an antagonist who operates through proxy demons, extending the scope of his power beyond the physical Pictish warriors. For Conan and the defenders of Fort Tuscelan, Tiberias's fate is a warning that the frontier war has entered a darker phase.

## Significance

Tiberias is a classic Howard minor character: a civilian whose unfortunate death illuminates the danger of the setting. He never speaks in the narrative; his role is entirely posthumous. Yet his death is the inciting incident for the story's supernatural plotline, forcing the defenders of the Black River frontier to acknowledge that they face more than human enemies. Through Tiberias, Howard establishes the grim reality of life on the Pictish border—where a merchant's routine journey can end in demonic sacrifice.
