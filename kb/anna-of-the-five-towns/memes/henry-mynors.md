# Henry Mynors

Henry Mynors is the last surviving member of a once-prominent Bursley family, a self-made pottery manufacturer, and the man Anna Tellwright agrees to marry. Handsome, accomplished, and devout, he is the novel's most complete specimen of Five Towns manhood — and its most sustained critique of the fusion between piety and profit. His dual pursuit of Anna, for love and for her capital, makes him simultaneously romantic hero and morally ambiguous figure.

## Character Overview

Mynors is thirty years old at the novel's opening: "handsome, dark, accomplished, exemplary and prosperous," a man who has "walked for ten years circumspect and unscathed amid the glances of a whole legion of maids" (PN 001-045). He serves as morning superintendent of the Bursley Wesleyan Sunday-school and conductor of the men's Bible-class. His baritone voice is admired in the chapel orchestra.

The antique red mansion opposite Manor Terrace, now a school, was "the home of the Mynors family for two generations" (PN 002-002) — a lineage now "extinct in the district save for one member" (PN 002-002). This detail is telling: Mynors is the last of his line, a man who must rebuild family fortunes through marriage and commerce.

> "He had all the qualities both of a good business man and of a sincere Christian; he was shrewd, energetic, ambitious, and yet his soul was truly uplifted by the thought of the New Jerusalem." (PN 001-045)

## Background & Circumstances

Mynors was born into an influential Bursley family. His mother was a second cousin of Mrs Sutton, making him a frequent and favoured presence at the Sutton household — a connection that grants him access to Bursley's social elite.

He has built his own pottery works with fifteen hundred pounds and seeks a partner to invest a further two thousand. His works are equipped with "all the latest machinery and dodges" (PN 003-105), marking him as a representative of the new industrial efficiency that is transforming the Five Towns. The partnership arrangement with Ephraim Tellwright — proposed by Mynors himself as a gracious concession — binds the romantic and commercial dimensions of his relationship with Anna from the outset.

His financial ambition is explicit. He needs capital to expand, and Anna's inheritance of fifty thousand pounds — revealed late in the novel — transforms his courtship. "The fifty thousand danced a jig in his brain that night. A man of thirty, with a quarter of a million in prospect! It was intoxicating" (PN 011-220).

## Key Relationships

- **[[Anna Tellwright]]**: Mynors's courtship proceeds with methodical care. At their first significant encounter, he is "too much moved by the sight of her, and the situation, to continue" (PN 004-054). But his tenderness is inseparable from calculation. When she reveals her fortune, "his countenance shone with delight" (PN 011-220).

- **[[Beatrice Sutton]]**: Daughter of Alderman Sutton and a former romantic interest. Their easy intimacy — the whispered jokes, the shared familiarity — provokes Anna's instinctive resentment at the Sewing Meeting (PN 001-045). Mynors seems untroubled by the ambiguity.

- **[[Ephraim Tellwright]]**: In business negotiations, the miser outmanoeuvres Mynors with "unscrupulous cleverness" (PN 004-054). Mynors is "annoyed, but he gave no sign of his annoyance" — the self-control of a man who knows that displaying emotion is a commercial liability.

- **[[Titus Price]]**: Mynors's senior colleague in the Sunday-school. When Price's embezzlement surfaces, Mynors urges the matter be "kept quiet" to avoid "a frightful scandal" (PN 011-220). His instinct is institutional preservation, not justice.

- **[[Willie Price]]**: Mynors's treatment of Willie reveals his limitations. When Anna asks what will become of Willie after the business sale, the men's reassurances are vague. Mynors cannot see Willie as anything but an encumbrance to be managed.

## Role in the Narrative

Mynors is the catalyst for Anna's awakening. His attentions at the Sunday-school gate — a deviation from the "rule of a lifetime" — signal both determination and willingness to break convention for her (PN 002-009). He is a man of "compelling eyes" and "aquiline gaze" (PN 001-024), conscious of his power and adept in its deployment.

In the novel's commercial subplot, Mynors exemplifies the new entrepreneurial class: efficient, modernising, and morally flexible. He is a man for whom "the Doing was never suspended" (PN 001-068). When business and morality conflict, business prevails; Mynors rationalises rather than wrestles.

His most revealing moment comes late: learning of Anna's fortune, "his countenance shone with delight. His joy was almost childlike in its frankness" (PN 011-220). The fifty thousand transforms his affection into something closer to avarice — though Bennett is careful never to let Mynors become a villain. He is simply a man of his class and time.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Mynors is driven by three intertwined ambitions: commercial success, social standing, and a suitable marriage. His piety is genuine — he is "truly uplifted by the thought of the New Jerusalem" (PN 001-045) — but inseparable from the framework of respectability and networking that Wesleyanism provides in Bursley.

### Arc & Development

Mynors is a static character. From his first appearance teasing Agnes to his final raptures over the fifty thousand, he remains remarkably consistent: charming, competent, and incapable of the moral crisis that transforms Anna. This is not a flaw in characterisation but an argument: the system that produces Mynors does not want him to change.

### Thematic Significance

| Theme | How Mynors Embodies It |
|-------|----------------------|
| Religion and Commerce | Wesleyan piety as vehicle for social networking and business |
| Masculine Power | Benevolent in manner, structural in effect |
| Industrial Capitalism | The efficient, modernising entrepreneur who discards what cannot compete |
| Love and Calculation | Courtship as commercial transaction |

Mynors embodies the fusion of religion and commerce in the Wesleyan bourgeoisie. He is the man the Five Towns is designed to produce — and Bennett's critique is not that Mynors is a hypocrite, but that the system makes hypocrisy unnecessary. Piety and profit are not in conflict; they are two expressions of the same impulse toward respectability and accumulation.

## Critical Perspective

> Mynors is not a villain in any conventional sense. He is something more troubling: a good man whose goodness is entirely compatible with the exploitation of others. His delight at the fifty thousand is not greed but the natural response of a man formed by a culture that measures all things — including love — in pounds sterling.
