# The Autumn Bazaar

A large-scale fundraising event for the Wesleyan chapel building fund, the Autumn Bazaar is "a triumphant and unparalleled success" [013-067] that dominates the social life of Bursley in the novel's closing chapter. Organised principally by Mrs. Sutton, the bazaar is both a communal celebration and the background against which Anna and Henry's wedding preparations reach their conclusion.

## Organisation and Purpose

The bazaar is designed to raise money for the chapel building fund [013-055]. Henry Mynors, ever the practical businessman, is closely involved in the financial arrangements. Mrs. Sutton and Beatrice are among the principal organisers, and the event occupies the energies of Bursley's Wesleyan community in the weeks leading up to it.

## The Event

The bazaar fills the chapel hall with stalls and activities. Despite the formal prohibition on raffling, "the bazaar would be enlivened by an auction" [013-083] — a characteristically Bennett touch, in which Methodist propriety is observed in name while being circumvented in practice. At eleven o'clock, the bazaar is "closed to the public" [013-088] and the organisers count the takings. The lights are extinguished; "the bazaar was over" [013-098].

## Significance

### The Bazaar and the Wedding

The bazaar brackets the final chapter alongside the preparations for Anna and Henry's wedding. Anna is pulled between the two events — between her obligations to the chapel community and her new life as Henry's wife. Mrs. Sutton's preoccupation with the bazaar ("the imminent bazaar, which occupied Mrs. Sutton and her daughters almost to the exclusion of everything else" [013-065]) creates a backdrop of bustling communal activity against which Anna's more solitary journey toward marriage unfolds.

### Communal Triumph

The bazaar's success — "triumphant and unparalleled" — is presented as a collective achievement of the Wesleyan community, a testament to the organisational energy that Methodism channels into practical ends. It is also, in a quieter register, a valediction to the communal life that Anna is about to leave behind for the domestic sphere of the Priory.
