# Wesleyan Methodism

Wesleyan Methodism is the spiritual and social architecture of Arnold Bennett's *Anna of the Five Towns*, as pervasive in Bursley as the bottle-kilns on its skyline. It is at once the church of Anna Tellwright's inherited faith, the theatre of [[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]]'s ambition, the institution betrayed by [[titus-price|Titus Price]]'s embezzlement, and the crucible in which the novel's central moral crisis unfolds. More than a denomination, it is the conscience of a community — and the novel's most sustained examination of the distance between public piety and private conduct.

The [[five-towns|Five Towns]] in the late nineteenth century were a stronghold of Nonconformity, and Wesleyan Methodism — the original Methodist tradition tracing its lineage to John Wesley — held pride of place among the dissenting churches. In [[bursley|Bursley]], "the ancient home of the potter" (PN 001-049), the Wesleyan chapel and its adjoining Sunday-school, lecture-hall, and chapel-keeper's house formed an enclosed quadrangle that was the real civic centre of respectable life, rivalling the Town Hall itself. For families like the Tellwrights, the Suttons, and the Prices, membership in the Wesleyan Society was not only a theological commitment but a marker of social standing, a network of economic obligation, and a framework for moral judgment.

## The Wesleyan System: Chapels, Circuits, and Social Order

The institutional machinery of Wesleyan Methodism in the Five Towns was organised around a circuit system connecting multiple chapels across the contiguous towns. At the novel's opening, Ephraim Tellwright had recently migrated his membership from Hanbridge to Bursley, having "found the atmosphere of Hanbridge Wesleyan chapel rather uncongenial" (PN 002-007) after purchasing cottage property in Bursley. This migration was itself unusual — "the circuit was astounded by the strange and wilful act" (PN 002-007) — and signals the degree to which chapel membership, like property, was a marker of territorial identity and social allegiance.

The Bursley chapel complex, enclosed on three sides by "the various Connectional buildings" (PN 001-002), housed a complete parallel civic structure. The Sunday-school occupied the largest building; the chapel proper served as the worship space; the lecture-hall hosted weekday meetings; and the chapel-keeper's house maintained the physical plant. This spatial arrangement reflected the denomination's comprehensive reach into daily life. A member's standing was measured not merely by Sunday attendance but by participation across the full spectrum of Connectional activities: teaching in the Sunday-school, singing in the choir, attending Monday-night prayer meetings, contributing to circuit funds, and serving on building committees.

The social hierarchy encoded in this system was exacting. "Only two families of the Bursley Wesleyan Methodists kept a carriage" (PN 001-020), one of them being the Suttons. Mrs. Sutton's permission to "use her equipage on Sundays" represented a "license granted by Connexional opinion" (PN 001-020) — a delicate negotiation between wealth and the Nonconformist suspicion of display. The chapel was simultaneously the great leveller and the great sorter of Bursley society, bringing potters and manufacturers into the same pews while calibrating precisely who stood where in the community's estimation.

## The Revival: Piety, Performance, and Social Pressure

The religious revival that sweeps through Bursley early in the novel serves as both a genuine spiritual event and a revealing social mechanism. A professional revivalist is engaged, and the "historic chapel was crowded almost in every part" (PN 005-015). Under "the imperious hypnotism of the revivalist" (PN 005-022), Anna experiences a classic conversion crisis: "tortured by conscience, pulled this way by Christ and that by the devil" (PN 005-022). The episode dramatises the intense emotional pressure the Wesleyan system could exert on sensitive consciences, particularly young women.

Yet Bennett's narration also registers the theatrical quality of revival culture. The revivalist's plea — "Let the Holy Ghost descend upon us now" (PN 005-019) — is described with "restrained passion" (PN 005-019), a phrase that captures the studied, performative dimension of evangelical preaching. After the service, notices announce "that cabinet photographs of the revivalist could be purchased on application" (PN 005-042), a commercial coda that complicates the spiritual intensity of what has come before. The [[the-revival|Revival]] thus operates on multiple registers: as genuine religious experience, as social spectacle, and as a mechanism for reinforcing the Wesleyan community's shared identity through collective emotional discharge.

The revival's effects are notably uneven. While Anna is profoundly shaken, [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] remains entirely unmoved — "the potent revival in the Wesleyan chapel had produced no effect on the miser" (PN 006-017). This contrast between father and daughter crystallises one of the novel's central insights: that the Wesleyan system, for all its institutional power, could not compel genuine spiritual transformation. The forms of piety could be observed without the substance.

## The Sunday-School: Institution, Ambition, and Corruption

The Sunday-school is the novel's central institutional stage, and its building project drives the plot's financial crisis. "The building scheme about to be undertaken by the Wesleyan Methodist Society in Bursley" had as "its object to erect a new Sunday-school" (PN 004-002). This project, noble in purpose, becomes the vehicle for the novel's darkest revelations about the gap between institutional piety and private conduct.

[[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]] exemplifies the Sunday-school as a ladder of social and professional advancement. At thirty, he is "morning superintendent of the Sunday-school and conductor of the men's Bible-class" (PN 001-004), roles that give him authority, visibility, and access to the community's business networks. His rapid rise through the Sunday-school hierarchy parallels his commercial success: "owing mainly to his own strenuous application, he was what is termed 'a good Wesleyan,' preaching and teaching, and contributing handsomely to the funds of the circuit" (PN 002-005). For Mynors, Wesleyan service and capitalist enterprise are mutually reinforcing pursuits.

The Sunday-school system also defines the novel's female characters. [[anna-tellwright|Anna]] inherits her teaching role from family tradition; Beatrice Sutton is described as "active in the Wesleyan Sunday-school" (PN 001-073), having been accustomed to Sunday work from girlhood; and Mrs. Sutton remains "a pillar of the Sunday-school, which Sunday by Sunday she drove to in her carriage" (PN 007-061). The Sunday-school was one of the few public spheres in which Five Towns women could exercise authority, teach, and organise — a limited but genuine form of female agency within the patriarchal Wesleyan structure.

It is precisely the Sunday-school's centrality that makes [[titus-price|Titus Price]]'s betrayal so devastating. As superintendent, Price "had made passionate appeals for the new school and had always prayed with such sweet and wistful fervency at the Monday night prayer-meetings" (PN 011-101). When his embezzlement of building funds is exposed, the scandal threatens not merely a financial loss but the moral credibility of the entire Wesleyan enterprise. "It's an awful thing for the Sunday-school, and the whole society, too" (PN 011-080), a character laments, and the blow reverberates through "Methodist circles" with "almost greedy curiosity" (PN 011-102).

## Character Connections: The Wesleyan Spectrum

The novel's Wesleyan characters range across a spectrum from sincere devotion to hollow performance to outright hypocrisy:

| Character | Wesleyan Role | Relationship to the Institution |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------------------|
| [[henry-mynors\|Henry Mynors]] | Morning superintendent, Bible-class conductor, admired baritone | Embodiment of the successful Wesleyan — piety and profit fused |
| [[ephraim-tellwright\|Ephraim Tellwright]] | Circuit steward, treasurer of building fund | Inherited Wesleyanism as social credential; spiritually absent |
| [[titus-price\|Titus Price]] | Sunday-school superintendent | Pious surface concealing financial desperation and criminal fraud |
| [[anna-tellwright\|Anna Tellwright]] | Sunday-school teacher | Inherited member; experiences genuine religious feeling but questions institutional forms |
| [[willie-price\|Willie Price]] | Secretary of the Bible-class | The Wesleyan system's most innocent victim, crushed by his father's disgrace |
| Mrs. Sutton | Pillar of the Sunday-school | Represents older generation's settled, carriage-borne respectability |
| Beatrice Sutton | Sunday-school teacher | Active in Wesleyan social circuits; her engagement to Mynors conducted within its norms |

This spectrum allows Bennett to explore the institution from multiple angles without reducing it to any single judgment. Mynors's Wesleyanism is genuine but instrumentalised; Ephraim's is nominal but strategically maintained; Price's is a mask; Anna's is inherited but gradually interrogated. Only Willie Price, the stammering secretary of the Bible-class, seems to inhabit his Wesleyan identity without calculation — and he is the one who suffers most.

## The Wesleyan Moral Economy

The Wesleyanism of the Five Towns operated as much as a system of economic ethics as a theology of grace. The denomination's historic suspicion of debt, its emphasis on "contributing handsomely to the funds of the circuit" (PN 002-005), and its culture of public subscription for building projects — the "interminable alternation of debt-raising and new liability which keeps a Nonconformist community lively" (PN 002-005) — created a moral economy in which solvency was next to godliness. Economic respectability — paying one's debts, meeting one's pledges, keeping one's accounts — was inseparable from spiritual standing.

This fusion of finance and faith makes the [[price-embezzlement-scandal|Price Embezzlement Scandal]] doubly catastrophic. Price's crime is not merely a legal violation but a desecration of the entire moral order on which Wesleyan community life depended. When [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] demands of Price "Whose property is it, mine or thine?" (PN 006-114), he invokes the most fundamental distinction in the Wesleyan economic imagination — the boundary between honest possession and theft — and by extension the boundary between a reputable member and an outcast.

Anna's own moral development can be traced through her relationship to Wesleyan economic norms. Her first act of financial independence — demanding "some of my own money" (PN 010-038) from her father — is simultaneously a Wesleyan act of claiming what is rightfully hers and a rebellion against the domestic tyranny that Wesleyan patriarchy enabled. Later, when she offers to pay Price's debt secretly, she enacts a form of Christian charity that exceeds the institutional Wesleyanism of her upbringing, forgiving a debt that the system's own logic would demand be called in.

## Critical Perspective

> The Wesleyan chapel in Bennett's Five Towns fiction functions as what the Victorian sociologist might call a "total institution" — not in the carceral sense but in its comprehensive regulation of time, space, status, and moral judgment. Its power derives less from theological conviction than from the density of social relations it organises: who teaches whom, who sits where, who signs what cheque, and who is seen to do so.
