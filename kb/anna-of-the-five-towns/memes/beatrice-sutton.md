# Beatrice Sutton

Daughter of a prosperous Bursley family, Sunday-school teacher, and Anna Tellwright's friend, representing the easy confidence of those born to comfort in the Five Towns. (PN 001-073, PN 007-025)

## Character Overview

Beatrice Sutton is the daughter of the prosperous Mr. and Mrs. Sutton of Bursley. A Sunday-school teacher at the Wesleyan Methodist Chapel and a friend to Anna Tellwright, Beatrice represents the settled, confident world of the Five Towns middle class — a foil to Anna's awkwardness and isolation. She once held a romantic interest in Henry Mynors, and her ease in navigating the social world of chapel teas and bazaars highlights by contrast the constraints under which Anna lives. (PN 001-073, PN 007-025)

## Background & Circumstances

Beatrice belongs to one of Bursley's comfortable Methodist families. She teaches in the Sunday-school alongside the other young women of the chapel community. Her home is substantial enough to host social visits, and her bedroom — mentioned during a tea gathering — is a space of feminine domesticity that Anna observes with a mixture of curiosity and alienation. Beatrice's life is shaped by the rhythms of chapel society: Wednesday meetings, bazaar preparations, and the quiet courtship rituals of the Wesleyan young. (PN 007-021, PN 007-152)

## Key Relationships

- **[[anna-tellwright]]**: Her friendship with Anna is warm but marked by an asymmetry — Beatrice's easy social confidence throws Anna's shyness into relief. She is kind to Anna without fully understanding her. (PN 001-073)
- **[[henry-mynors]]**: Formerly a romantic interest; Beatrice's feelings for Mynors form a quiet subtext in the early social scenes. Her graceful acceptance of his eventual preference for Anna demonstrates the poise that Anna herself lacks. (PN 007-025)
- **[[mrs-sutton]]**: Her mother, part of the established Wesleyan matronage that shapes chapel society.

## Role in the Narrative

Beatrice appears primarily in the novel's social set-pieces — the chapel tea, the bazaar, the Sunday-school anniversary. Her narrative function is to establish the norms of Bursley's middle-class Wesleyan world: she is competent, pleasant, conventional, and entirely at ease. At the bazaar, she arranges her stall "radiant and gorgeous," the picture of a young woman who knows exactly how to occupy her appointed place. She is not a driver of plot but an essential part of the social texture against which the central drama unfolds. (PN 007-025, PN 009-126)

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Beatrice's motivations are those of her class and generation: to fulfill the expectations of chapel society, to marry well, to maintain the pleasant surface of Bursley life. She is not driven by passion or ambition but by the quiet desire to be good, agreeable, and approved.

### Arc & Development

Beatrice does not undergo significant change; she is a static character by design. Her constancy — her unchanging pleasantness — serves to measure the transformations happening in Anna. Where Anna's path leads toward renunciation and self-knowledge, Beatrice's path leads toward the expected consolations of her world.

### Thematic Significance

Beatrice embodies the theme of social ease and its absence. She is what Anna might have been had Anna grown up with warmth and money — and her presence in the novel reminds the reader of what Anna has been denied. She also represents the limited but real satisfactions of conformity to the Wesleyan middle-class ideal.
