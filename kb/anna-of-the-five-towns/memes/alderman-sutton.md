# Alderman Sutton

Alderman Sutton (also referred to as Mr. Sutton and, earlier, Councillor Sutton) is a prominent citizen of Bursley, the father of Beatrice Sutton and husband of Mrs. Sutton. A potter's valuer and commission agent by profession, he is a man of substantial wealth and genial temperament who figures prominently in the civic, social, and domestic life of the Five Towns.

## Profession and Wealth

Mr. Sutton is described as "a potter's valuer and commission agent" who "made money with facility in that lucrative vocation"[PN=001-020]. His professional success allows the Sutton family to live in considerable comfort. The Suttons are one of only two families among the Bursley Wesleyan Methodists who keep a carriage, the other being the Clayton Vernons[PN=001-020].

The Sutton residence is one of an "imposing row of four new houses, said to be the best planned and best built in the town, each erected separately and occupied by its owner"[PN=002-002]. Situated on Trafalgar Road at Bleakridge, opposite Manor Terrace where the Tellwrights live, the house is valued at 60 pounds a year[PN=002-002]. It is described as "Councillor Sutton's" at this point in the narrative, indicating his earlier role on the municipal council before his elevation to alderman.

## Character and Temperament

Alderman Sutton is characterised by an easy-going, genial disposition. He "beamed a rather absentminded goodwill on the entire company" at his wife's sewing-meeting tea, and seemed usually to exist "in a benignant dream"[PN=007-110]. When he greets Anna Tellwright at that gathering, he shakes hands with her "heartily, emerging for a moment from the benignant dream in which he seemed usually to exist"[PN=007-110].

He is a devoted father. The luxury of the Sutton drawing-room is "mainly due to Alderman Sutton's inability to refuse anything to his daughter, whose tastes lay in the direction of rich draperies, large or quaint chairs, occasional tables, dwarf screens, hand-painted mirrors, and an opulence of bric-a-brac"[PN=007-075]. This indulgent paternal affection has made Beatrice Sutton, by her own parents' admission, a somewhat spoiled only child.

## Cultural Interests

Unlike his wife, whose character remains untouched by wealth, Mr. Sutton has acquired certain cultivated habits. He "had joined the Five Towns Field Club and acquired some of the habits of an archaeologist"[PN=001-020]. His interest in archaeology and geology emerges during the Isle of Man holiday, where he makes "an interesting companion, full of facts about strata, outcrops, and breaks"[PN=010-140]. His one weakness, the narrator wryly notes, is "the habit of quoting extremely sentimental scraps of verse when walking by the seashore"[PN=010-140].

He is also a collector of pictures, acquired "gradually by Mr. Sutton at auction sales: it was commonly held that he had an excellent taste in pictures, and that his daughter's aptitude for the arts came from him, and not from her mother"[PN=007-075]. The gilt clock and side pieces on the mantelpiece were publicly presented to him by the directors of a local building society of which he had been chairman for many years[PN=007-075]. He and Henry Mynors share an interest in art, examining together a large photogravure of Sant's *The Soul's Awakening* which Mr. Sutton had recently bought[PN=007-137].

## Civic Role

Mr. Sutton is a figure of considerable civic standing. By the novel's middle, he has risen from councillor to alderman, and later becomes the prospective mayor of Bursley. An "important dinner at the mayor's house at Hillport" is held "in connection with Mr. Sutton's impending mayoralty"[PN=012-110]. His position in the community is such that the minister Mr. Banks readily accepts his hospitality and the entire female society of Bursley Wesleyanism attends his wife's sewing-meetings.

## Role at the Isle of Man

The Alderman plays an avuncular, genial role during the Isle of Man holiday. He walks "in a row down to the harbour, the Alderman giving an arm each to Beatrice and Anna"[PN=010-058]. During the mackerel-fishing expedition, he lies "on the foredeck, placidly smoking" while the others attempt to fish[PN=010-069]. In the afternoons, he and his wife "slept as usual, Mr. Sutton with a bandana handkerchief over his face"[PN=010-166]. When Anna and Henry depart Port Erin after their engagement, "the smiles of the Alderman and his wife as they waved Godspeed from the doorstep, filled Anna with a serene content"[PN=010-234].

After the engagement is announced, Alderman Sutton "added his felicitations to the rest, with that flattering air of intimate sympathy and comprehension which some middle-aged men can adopt towards young girls"[PN=012-028]. At the celebratory tea, he sits on Anna's left, with Henry Mynors on her right[PN=012-028].
