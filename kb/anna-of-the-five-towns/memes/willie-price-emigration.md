# Willie Price's Emigration

In the aftermath of his father Titus Price's suicide and the exposure of the embezzlement, Willie Price takes a cheap lodging and prepares to emigrate to Melbourne, Australia. His departure is one of the novel's quietest but most affecting resolutions — the extinction of the Price family's presence in Bursley and the dispersal of its remaining members.

## Background

Willie Price has been one of the novel's gentlest figures — a shy, physically awkward young man who bears his father's humiliation and his own social marginalisation without complaint. After the suicide of Titus Price and the sale of the manufactory, Willie is left with nothing: no business, no reputation, no place in the community that has destroyed his father.

## The Departure

"Before Christmas he was to sail for Melbourne" [012-149]. The brevity of Bennett's announcement is characteristic — Willie's fate is dispatched in a single subordinate clause, as if the narrative itself is hurrying him out of sight. He takes "a cheap lodging with the woman who had been called in on the night of Sarah's collapse" [012-149], a detail that links his departure to the earlier crisis of the servant's illness and underscores the compounding nature of the Prices' misfortunes.

The Priory — his childhood home — is emptied of its "rickety furniture," which is sent to auction in Hanbridge where "the frail sticks lost their identity in a medley of other sticks, and ceased to be" [012-149]. The erasure of the Price household is total: the furniture dissolved into the anonymous stream of commerce, the house taken over by Mynors, the son sailing to the far side of the world.

## Significance

### The Fate of the Weak

Willie Price's emigration is Bennett's most explicit treatment of the novel's Darwinian social economy. In a world where "only the strong survive," as one character puts it, Willie represents the gentle, the inept, the constitutionally unfit — those whom the Five Towns' commercial culture grinds down and spits out. His departure for Melbourne is not a fresh start but an expulsion.

### Anna's Response

Anna's attitude toward Willie — her sympathy, her refusal to judge him for his father's crimes, her quiet gift of money before his departure — is one of the novel's most affecting moral gestures. It is in her treatment of Willie that Anna most clearly distinguishes herself from the harder judgments of her community, and their final parting is freighted with an emotion the novel's restrained prose only hints at.
