# The Price Embezzlement Scandal

The financial ruin and moral collapse of [[Titus Price]], Sunday-school superintendent of the [[bursley|Bursley]] Wesleyan Society and a small pottery manufacturer, forms the novel's central crisis. Over a period of months spanning chapters 3 through 11, Price's long-concealed embezzlement of the Sunday-school building fund -- amounting to some four hundred pounds entrusted to him by the congregation -- is exposed through a relentless campaign of financial pressure orchestrated by [[Ephraim Tellwright]] and executed, unwittingly or otherwise, by his daughter [[Anna Tellwright]]. The scandal culminates in Price's forgery of bills of exchange, his public disgrace, and his suicide, leaving his son [[Willie Price]] to face ruin alone. The event entwines the novel's key themes -- the collision of [[wesleyan-methodism|religious profession]] with economic predation, the moral agency of [[Anna Tellwright]], and the inexorable logic of property in the [[five-towns]].

## Origins: The Sunday-School Fund

[[Titus Price]] occupied a position of immense trust within the [[bursley|Bursley]] Methodist community. As Sunday-school superintendent, he was responsible not only for the spiritual instruction of the children but also for the custody of the building fund -- monies collected over years from the congregation for the construction of a new Sunday-school hall. By the time the novel opens, Price had already diverted these funds to prop up his failing pottery works in Edward Street, a small, old-fashioned manufactory chained to debt and arrears of rent.

Price's pottery business was hopelessly insolvent. [[Ephraim Tellwright]], who owned the premises and held a mortgage on Price's equipment, had for years pursued a calculated strategy of financial control. His policy, as Bennett records with chilling precision, was "to squeeze the last penny out of Price without forcing him into bankruptcy" (PN 003-100). This was not vindictiveness but method -- Tellwright judged that a bankrupt Price would yield nothing, whereas a desperate Price might somehow pay. The Sunday-school fund, held by Price in a position of apparent trust, became the collateral damage of this calculus.

When [[Anna Tellwright]] first visits the Price works to collect overdue rent on her father's behalf, she is met with the squalor of Titus Price's circumstances. The works are described as "a frontier post of the town," small, old-fashioned, and barely operational. Price himself appears sickly and abject, pleading poverty in a voice that wavers between defiance and despair. He can produce no rent -- "not a cent," he admits -- but promises twenty pounds within a week (PN 003-113). Anna, though unsettled by the encounter, carries her father's demands back to him faithfully. She does not yet know that the money Price struggles to produce will come, ultimately, from the Sunday-school fund.

> "His policy was to squeeze the last penny out of Price without forcing him into bankruptcy. Such was the predicament where Anna now found herself enlisted as her father's agent." (PN 003-100)

## The Campaign of Financial Pressure

The systematic extraction of money from [[Titus Price]] unfolds over chapters 6 through 9, and [[Anna Tellwright]] becomes its unwilling but obedient instrument. [[Ephraim Tellwright]], having learned that Price has managed to produce twenty pounds toward the arrears, escalates his demands. He dispatches Anna with promissory notes requiring payment on the first Monday of each month, and he demands a second twenty pounds almost immediately -- a demand that Anna delivers to Price at the Sunday school itself.

The scene at Price's home on a rainy evening marks a turning point. [[Willie Price]] arrives at the Tellwright house, soaking wet, to plead his father's case. Anna receives him with what Bennett calls "an uncomplaisant tone," ushering him to the front parlour where [[Ephraim Tellwright]] sits waiting like a creditor in judgment (PN 006-115). Willie's embarrassment is palpable -- he blinks at the gaslight, stammers out his father's promise of future payment, and retreats into the rain without having softened Tellwright's resolve. Anna, watching him go, feels the first twinges of a conscience her father's system cannot accommodate, but she says nothing.

The rhythm of demand and partial payment continues for weeks. Titus Price scrapes together another twenty pounds on a Monday -- money almost certainly drawn from the Sunday-school fund he was supposed to be protecting (PN 006-142). Meanwhile, [[Henry Mynors]] and [[Ephraim Tellwright]] are finalizing their partnership to acquire Price's works, a transaction that depends on Price's continued desperation driving him to sell at the lowest possible price. The [[the-revival|revival]] at which Price weeps publicly for his sins is happening concurrently with this financial destruction -- a juxtaposition that Bennett handles with devastating understatement.

> "You insist on another twenty pounds on Monday?" (PN 006-128)

This single line, delivered by Willie Price to Ephraim Tellwright, captures the entire dynamic of the scandal: Tellwright's implacable demand, Price's helplessness, and Willie's role as the pathetic intermediary between his father's shame and Tellwright's power.

## The Forged Bills of Exchange

When [[Titus Price]] could no longer produce cash from the depleted Sunday-school fund, he turned to a more desperate expedient: forgery. Price drew bills of exchange on Mr. Sutton, the wealthy alderman and pillar of the [[wesleyan-methodism|Wesleyan]] community, endorsing them with Sutton's forged signature. These bills -- blue papers, each for thirty pounds -- were passed to [[Ephraim Tellwright]] as security against the mounting arrears of rent (PN 009-023). Price extracted a promise from Tellwright that the bills would not be presented to Sutton for payment, believing this would buy him time to make good on his debts.

Tellwright, predictably, broke his word. The moment Sutton's name appeared on negotiable paper in Tellwright's possession, the old man saw an opportunity to monetize the debt through a party with deeper pockets than Price could ever offer. He presented the bills directly to Sutton, who of course disclaimed the endorsements as forgeries. The exposure was immediate and catastrophic -- not merely proof that Price had stolen from the Sunday-school fund, but proof that he had committed a criminal offense carrying the penalty of imprisonment.

[[Anna Tellwright]] discovered the forgery when she found her father examining the blue bill of exchange in the kitchen. [[Ephraim Tellwright]] showed her the forged endorsement with a grim satisfaction that appalled her. He had, he explained, always suspected the bill was fraudulent, and he had held it precisely so that it could be used as leverage at the moment of maximum pressure. Anna protested -- she argued that Price had been driven to the forgery by Tellwright's own relentless demands -- but her father silenced her with a single devastating rejoinder: she herself had been the one who delivered the notes, who collected the payments, who sat in Willie Price's front parlour demanding money. She was, Ephraim Tellwright told her, an accomplice in the crime.

> "Thou'rt an accomplice in forgery. Thou sees the scamp on the scaffold, and thou say'st 'It serves him right.' But thou sent him there." (PN 011-220)

## The Suicide of Titus Price

The exposure of the forgery meant arrest, trial, and imprisonment for [[Titus Price]]. He did not wait for the law to take its course. On a Monday morning shortly after the bills were presented to Mr. Sutton, Titus Price was found dead -- he had hanged himself in his own workshop, the very pottery that [[Ephraim Tellwright]] had spent years trying to extract from him.

The news reached [[Anna Tellwright]] through [[Henry Mynors]], who arrived at the Tellwright house visibly shaken. "Our superintendent has committed suicide," he told Anna (PN 011-076). The phrase is striking: Mynors says "our superintendent," as though even in death Titus Price remained first and foremost an officer of the Wesleyan Society, a man defined by his institutional role rather than by his human desperation. The inquest returned the expected verdict -- suicide while of unsound mind -- and the Methodist community closed ranks, speaking of the tragedy only in hushed, embarrassed tones. Mynors, in a characteristic act of damage control, informed Anna that the trustees of the Sunday-school fund had decided not to pursue recovery of the embezzled money from [[Willie Price]], a gesture of mercy that also had the effect of containing the scandal.

For Anna, the suicide was a moral earthquake. She had walked to the penitent form at [[the-revival]] only weeks earlier, publicly declaring her submission to Christ. Now she confronted the fact that her own hands -- delivering notes, demanding payments, carrying her father's commands to a desperate man -- had helped drive that man to self-murder. The contradiction between the religion she professed and the economic role she performed became unbearable, and it structures her psychological development through the remainder of the novel.

> "Apart from any forgery, the Prices had swindled their creditors; even in dying, Titus Price had committed a direct and flagrant crime to serve as an excuse for sympathy." (PN 011-168)

## Aftermath: Willie Price Alone

The suicide of [[Titus Price]] did not end the scandal's consequences. [[Willie Price]], the son who had shuttled between his father and [[Ephraim Tellwright]] carrying impossible messages, was left alone to face the wreckage. The pottery works were sold to the [[Henry Mynors]]-[[Ephraim Tellwright]] partnership at a price far below their value. The creditors received nothing. The Sunday-school fund was never fully restored -- the congregation absorbed the loss in silence, preferring institutional dignity to public reckoning.

Willie Price himself disappeared from [[bursley]] shortly after his father's death. His last appearance in the novel shows him standing in the rain outside Anna's house, gazing at her window with what she perceives as "wistful reproach" (PN 008-100). He had loved [[Anna Tellwright]] in his inarticulate way, and she had been kind to him -- the one kindness she had shown any Price. But kindness cannot cancel complicity, and Anna watches him walk away knowing that whatever decency she possessed had been utterly insufficient to alter the outcome.

The scandal's structural function in the novel is to expose the hollowness of the moral claims made by [[wesleyan-methodism|Wesleyan Methodism]] in the [[five-towns]]. Titus Price was the Sunday-school superintendent, the man entrusted with the spiritual formation of Bursley's children. His weeping at [[the-revival]] was real enough -- Bennett does not suggest it was hypocrisy. But the same religious system that moved him to tears provided no mechanism for confronting the economic predation that destroyed him. [[Ephraim Tellwright]], the circuit treasurer turned landlord, could pray in meeting and squeeze a debtor to suicide in the same week without feeling any contradiction. It is this fusion of piety and predation -- not the embezzlement itself -- that constitutes the novel's deepest indictment.
