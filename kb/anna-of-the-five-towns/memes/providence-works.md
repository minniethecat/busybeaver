Providence Works is the pottery manufactory owned by Henry Mynors in Bursley. It represents the modern, efficiently managed industrial enterprise of the late Victorian Potteries.

## Description

The works are extensively described in the novel, particularly when Mynors shows Anna the operation (008-034). The manufactory embodies the industrial vigor of the Five Towns — its kilns, workshops, and warehouses representing the productive energy that drives the district's economy.

## Anna's Investment

A crucial development in the novel is Anna's decision to invest a significant portion of her inheritance — ten thousand pounds — in Providence Works as a silent partner. This investment, arranged through Ephraim Tellwright, creates a financial bond between Anna and Mynors that parallels and complicates their romantic relationship.

## Significance

Providence Works symbolizes the new generation of pottery manufacturing — efficient, commercially minded, and forward-looking. Unlike the older, more traditional potbanks, Mynors's works represents the rationalization of industry that characterized the late 19th century. It is both a workplace and a declaration of Mynors's ambition, making it a central symbol in the novel's exploration of industry, class, and marriage.
