# Reputation and Scandal

Reputation in the Five Towns is a social asset painstakingly accumulated and catastrophically lost. The novel's central scandal — Titus Price's forgery and suicide — demonstrates how the Wesleyan community both depends upon and polices the moral standing of its members, and how swiftly the "irreproachable respectability" of a Sunday-school superintendent can dissolve into disgrace.

## Definition

Within the social world of Bursley, reputation functions as a form of moral credit: a person's standing is established through public piety, financial probity, and institutional service within the Wesleyan chapel. A "good Wesleyan" (PN 002-005) is one whose private conduct never contradicts public profession.

Scandal arises when the hidden truth — embezzlement, forgery, moral weakness — breaks through the surface of respectability, exposing the gap between public performance and private reality (PN 011-101).

## Manifestations in the Novel

The Price scandal forms the moral centre of the novel's second half. Titus Price, a Sunday-school superintendent who "had always prayed with such sweet and wistful fervency at the Monday night prayer-meetings" (PN 011-101), is revealed as a forger and embezzler who has drained the building fund meant for the new Sunday-school.

The discovery forces his son Willie into complicity through a forged bill of exchange, and Titus's suicide follows the exposure (PN 011-076).

The scandal reaches beyond the Price family. Henry Mynors, as the "safe and profoundly respectable man" (PN 011-057), must navigate the fallout. Ephraim Tellwright, as treasurer of the building fund, becomes an accomplice through his decision to cover the loss rather than allow public disgrace: "Thou'rt an accomplice in forgery" (PN 011-220).

The Wesleyan community feels the blow collectively: "It's an awful thing for the Sunday-school, and the whole society, too" (PN 011-080).

Gossip is the mechanism by which scandal propagates. "In a town like Bursley, gossip flies and returns like a shuttlecock" (PN 008-052). Miss Dickinson's indiscreet conversation spreads the news of Price's difficulties, foreshadowing the public explosion to come (PN 007-061).

After the suicide, the news "became the theme of universal gossip immediately" (PN 012-058). The community's response is not simple sympathy but "almost greedy curiosity, beneath the cloak of righteous indignation" (PN 011-102).

Anna Tellwright's own situation is shaped by the threat of scandal. Her engagement to Mynors and her financial dealings with the Prices place her at risk of reputational damage.

The concern that something "would create such a frightful scandal" (PN 013-062) constrains what can be spoken openly, and the Price family's secret "was not to go" beyond a tight circle (PN 013-066). The management of scandal — containing it, hiding it, surviving it — becomes a central moral and practical question.

## Key Passages

> He was what is termed "a good Wesleyan," preaching and teaching, and contributing handsomely to the funds of the circuit. (PN 002-005)

> It was gradually perceived that Titus Price, the Methodist, the Sunday-school superintendent, who had made passionate appeals for the new school and had always prayed with such sweet and wistful fervency at the Monday night prayer-meetings, was a thief. (PN 011-101)

> "It's an awful thing for the Sunday-school, and the whole society, too. He, a prominent Wesleyan, a worker among us! An awful blow to the Methodist connection." (PN 011-080)

> The obese man, with his reputation in rags about him, was acutely conscious of the spectacle he presented. (PN 009-119)

## Character Connections

- **Titus Price**: The central figure of scandal — Sunday-school superintendent whose embezzlement and forgery destroy his reputation (PN 011-101)
- **Willie Price**: The son drawn into his father's crime; his reputation ruined by association (PN 011-173)
- **Ephraim Tellwright**: As treasurer, covers the loss rather than allow public disgrace, making himself an accomplice (PN 011-220)

- **Henry Mynors**: Embodies the "safe" reputation Price destroyed; prioritises appearances over mercy (PN 011-057)
- **Miss Dickinson**: The town gossip whose chatter spreads scandal through the community (PN 007-061)
- **Anna Tellwright**: Caught between duty and reputation; confronts the human cost of her father's rectitude (PN 012-147)

## Social / Economic Context

Scandal in the Five Towns is inseparable from money. Reputation is maintained through financial as well as moral rectitude: paying debts, contributing to chapel funds, keeping property in good order.

Titus Price's crime is specifically financial — embezzlement and forgery — because in a community where "the wages they earned" (PN 008-052) determine social standing, money and morality are inseparable. Ephraim Tellwright's willingness to hide the crime reflects an economic calculus as much as a moral one: the cost of disgrace is measured in more than reputation alone.
