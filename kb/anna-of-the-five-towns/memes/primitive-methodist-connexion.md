## Overview

The Primitive Methodist Connexion was a Methodist denomination formed by secession from Wesleyan Methodism in 1808. The split originated at Bursley, one of the Five Towns, where a faction advocating a return to primitive, revivalist Christianity broke away from the established Wesleyan circuit. Ephraim Tellwright's father, "a Bursley man, had fought in the fight which preceded the famous Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808 at Bursley" [225-P225].

## Historical Background

The secession of 1808 was one of several major ruptures in early 19th-century English Methodism. The Tellwright family belonged to "the Old Guard of Methodism," a faction characterised by "a tradition of holy valour for the pure doctrine" that resisted the Primitive Methodist departure [225-P225].

The Primitive Methodist movement emphasised camp meetings, open-air preaching, and a simpler, more emotional form of worship in contrast to the institutionalised Wesleyan circuit system. The secession at Bursley was a local manifestation of broader tensions within Methodism over the role of lay preachers and the centralisation of authority.

## Legacy

The 1808 Bursley secession was followed by further Methodist fractures, including the "Warren affrays of '28" and "the disastrous trouble of the Fly-Sheets in '49, when Methodism lost a hundred thousand members" — events in which Tellwright's father also participated [225-P225]. The Primitive Methodist Connexion grew into a significant denomination, eventually reuniting with other Methodist bodies in the Methodist Union of 1932.
