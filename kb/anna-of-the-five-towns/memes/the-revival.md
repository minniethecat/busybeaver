# The Revival

A week-long Wesleyan revival campaign that sweeps through Bursley Chapel in the autumn of the novel's opening, bringing the [[five-towns|Five Towns]] Methodist community to a pitch of spiritual intensity. Preceded by a Leaders' Meeting and a house-to-house visitation of scholars' parents, the revival is led by a visiting revivalist whose imperious hypnotism brings dozens of penitents to the Communion rail, including [[Anna Tellwright]] herself. The event serves as the novel's inciting incident -- directly precipitating Anna's engagement to [[Henry Mynors]], exposing the spiritual fault lines within the [[wesleyan-methodism|Wesleyan]] congregation, and casting an ironic shadow over the [[price-embezzlement-scandal|financial catastrophe]] that will engulf [[Titus Price]].

## Prelude: The Leaders' Meeting

The groundwork for the revival was laid at a special Teachers' Meeting of the [[bursley|Bursley]] Wesleyan Society, held ten days before the revival services began. [[Willie Price]] had mentioned this meeting to [[Anna Tellwright]] during their encounter at the Sunday school, and [[Henry Mynors]] had pressed her to attend -- a request that carried extraordinary weight given their still-developing relationship. At this meeting, held in a schoolroom adjoining the chapel, Mynors made the case for the revival in a cogent, businesslike address. His prayer for the unconverted was so pointed that Anna felt it aimed directly at her own spiritual condition. [[Mrs. Sutton]], the alderman's wife and a central figure in Bursley Wesleyan society, emerged as a commanding presence throughout these preparations.

The meeting was "one of the final preliminaries to a Revival -- that is, a revival of godliness and Christian grace -- about to be undertaken by the Wesleyan Methodist Society in Bursley" (PN 004-002). Mynors' prayer was "a cogent appeal for the success of the Revival," punctuated by Amens from around the room; "the atmosphere became suddenly fervent, emotional and devout" (PN 004-020). Bennett notes that "underneath the broad religious structure of the enterprise, and supporting it, there was a basis of individual diplomacy and solicitation. The town had been mapped out into districts, and each of these was being importuned, as at an election" (PN 004-023). Mynors called upon each teacher by name to pledge themselves to the home-visitation programme; when he reached Anna, the public request carried intimate undertones that marked a turning point in their relationship.

The Leaders' Meeting resolved on an ambitious plan: a week-long series of revival services led by a visiting evangelist, preceded by a systematic house-to-house visitation of every family with children enrolled in the Sunday school. Anna, despite her habitual reserve, was drawn into this visitation work. She found herself calling on the homes of the [[five-towns|Five Towns]] pottery workers, delivering invitations to parents who regarded the chapel with wary indifference. The work was exhausting and often thankless, but it drew Anna deeper into the communal life of the Society than she had ever ventured before.

## The Revival Services

The revival proper began on a Friday evening and ran for a full week, with services every night and additional afternoon meetings for women. The Rev. Reginald Banks, superintendent minister of the Bursley Wesleyan Circuit, presided over the opening service, his stout, pale-faced dignity lending institutional weight to proceedings. But the true force in the pulpit was the visiting revivalist -- a man of striking physical presence described by Bennett as tall, gaunt, and possessed of a voice that could drop to a whisper or rise to a thunderous plea without ever losing its grip on the congregation.

The revivalist had been heralded by extraordinary publicity: "the number of souls which he had snatched from eternal torment was precisely stated, and it amounted to tens of thousands. He played the cornet to the glory of God, and his cornet was of silver" (PN 004-002). [[Ephraim Tellwright]], with characteristic sardonic detachment, dismissed him as "th' old trumpeter" who "will turn the town upside down" (PN 004-004).

The chapel filled each night to overflowing. Bennett's description of the atmosphere is vivid: "the historic chapel was crowded almost in every part, and the congregation -- impressed, excited, eager -- sang the opening hymn with unprecedented vigour and sincerity" (PN 005-015). The gas lamps hissed, the room grew stiflingly hot, and the congregation sang with what Bennett calls "unprecedented vigour and abandon." On the broad balustrade of the pulpit lay the revivalist's silver cornet; when he seized it and joined the melody, "massive yet exultant, the tones rose clear over the mighty volume of vocal sound, an incitement to victorious effort" (PN 005-015). [[Titus Price]], the Sunday-school superintendent and a man already haunted by private financial desperation, sat near the front with tears streaming down his face -- a detail that would only acquire its full tragic resonance after the [[price-embezzlement-scandal]]. Mr. Sutton, the alderman, lent his substantial civic presence to the gathering, while [[Mrs. Sutton]] moved discreetly among the women, identifying those who seemed on the verge of conversion.

The revivalist's method was methodical yet overwhelming. He would preach for forty minutes on sin and redemption, his voice ranging from a confiding murmur to a clarion call. Then the congregation would sing a hymn, after which he would issue the call to the penitent form. Those who rose and walked to the Communion rail were met by class leaders who knelt beside them, praying in low, urgent voices. The pattern repeated nightly: hymn, sermon, hymn, appeal, prayer, another hymn, another appeal. By the third night, the atmosphere had become so charged that Anna found herself "oppressed by the closeness of the atmosphere and by a strange, growing apprehension," as Bennett records.

The revivalist himself was unapologetically theatrical. He declared to the congregation: "Friends and fellow-sinners, a lot of you, fools that you are, have come here tonight to hear me play my cornet. Well, you have heard me. I have played the cornet, and I will play it again. I would play it on my head if by so doing I could bring sinners to Christ. I have been called a mountebank. I am one. I glory in it. I am God's mountebank, doing God's precious business in my own way" (PN 005-021).

> "Little by little, yielding more and more to the imperious hypnotism of the revivalist, Anna gradually became obsessed by the idea of her own sinfulness." (PN 005-022)

## Anna's Spiritual Crisis

[[Anna Tellwright]] attended the revival in a state of acute spiritual discomfort. She had been raised in the hard, doctrinal Methodism of her father [[Ephraim Tellwright]], a man who had once been circuit treasurer and whose faith, if not extinguished, had long since hardened into a creed of property and discipline. Anna's religion was sincere but proud -- she had never, as [[Mrs. Sutton]] gently pointed out to her, "given her heart to Christ" in the public, emotional manner that [[wesleyan-methodism|Wesleyan Methodism]] demanded as the hallmark of true conversion.

On the second night of the revival, [[Mrs. Sutton]] approached Anna at her pew and asked quietly whether she was decided for Christ. It was a question Anna could not answer. She felt the weight of her own reserve, her incapacity for the public self-abandonment that conversion required. Mrs. Sutton's kindness was disarming: she told Anna that it was quite simple, that if she would yield herself, the blessing would come. Anna experienced this exchange not as comfort but as a sharp exposure of her spiritual pride -- a quality that Bennett suggests is inseparable from her Tellwright inheritance.

The crisis reached its peak on the third evening. Anna "gradually became oppressed by a vague sensation which was partly sorrow and partly an inexplicable dull anger -- anger at her own penitence. She felt as if everything was wrong and could never by any possibility be righted" (PN 005-022). During the final hymn, she rose and walked to the penitent form, kneeling at the rail as class leaders gathered around her. She experienced no dramatic emotional release -- her conversion, characteristically, was silent, inward, and marked more by resolution than by ecstasy. When she rose from her knees and returned to her pew, she saw [[Henry Mynors]] watching her from his seat near the organ, his face unreadable.

Anna was now "in the profoundest misery. The weight of her sins, of her ingratitude to God, lay on her like a physical and intolerable load, and she lost all feeling of shame, as a seasick voyager loses shame after an hour of nausea" (PN 005-024). The revivalist's words resonated: "The blood of Christ atones. The spirit of Christ is working among us. Let us engage in private prayer. Let us drive the devil out of this chapel" (PN 005-023). A moment of communal ecstasy followed when "a venerable class-leader came from the vestry, and, slowly climbing the pulpit stairs, whispered in the ear of the revivalist. The latter faced the congregation with a cry of joy. 'Lord,' he exclaimed, 'we bless Thee that seventeen souls have found Thee!'" (PN 005-027).

> "The blood of Christ atones," said the revivalist solemnly at the end of the hymn. "The spirit of Christ is working among us tonight." (PN 005-023)

## Immediate Consequences

Anna's conversion at the revival had swift and far-reaching consequences. On the walk home from chapel that very night, [[Henry Mynors]] joined her, and in the darkness of the Bursley street he proposed marriage. In the days leading up to this moment, Mynors had spoken with her after the Teachers' Meeting with "the most oblique delicacy, the most perfect assumption of equality between them," allowing her "to perceive his genuine and profound anxiety for her spiritual welfare. The atmosphere of the meeting was still round about him, the divine fire still uncooled" (PN 004-028). Anna, still dazed from the spiritual ordeal of the evening, accepted. The proposal and acceptance were characteristically undemonstrative -- Mynors was a practical man, and Anna a reserved woman -- but the revival had given them both the emotional permission to act on impulses they had long been nursing.

When Anna left the chapel on the night of her conversion, "Mrs. Sutton bent and kissed Anna affectionately, and then hurried away to offer her ministrations elsewhere" (PN 005-042). In the chapel porch, a notice announced "that cabinet photographs of the revivalist could be purchased on application, at one shilling each" (PN 005-042) -- a commercial detail that Bennett includes as a characteristic note of irony, the machinery of religious merchandising coexisting with the drama of souls saved.

The engagement transformed Anna's social position overnight. She was no longer merely [[Ephraim Tellwright]]'s daughter and household manager but the betrothed of one of the Five Towns' most promising young manufacturers -- a man already forming a partnership with her father to acquire [[Titus Price]]'s failing pottery works in Edward Street. The revival, which had begun as a collective spiritual exercise, had produced a thoroughly worldly outcome: an advantageous marriage alliance that meshed neatly with Ephraim Tellwright's property calculations.

The revival also exposed the moral contradictions that would come to define the novel's central drama. [[Titus Price]], the Sunday-school superintendent who wept openly during the services, was at that very moment embezzling funds from the Sunday-school building account -- a fact unknown to the congregation but already known to [[Ephraim Tellwright]], whose policy was to draw maximum payment from Price without forcing him into bankruptcy. Anna, fresh from her conversion, would soon be enlisted as her father's agent in this campaign of financial pressure, demanding rent from the weeping superintendent and dispatching promissory notes with a severity that sat uneasily beside her newly affirmed Christian commitment.

## Aftermath

When the revival concluded it had produced "a superb record of seventy saved souls" (PN 007-060). The event became a touchstone in Bursley conversation, discussed alongside the coming school-treat and the change of circuit ministers. For Anna, the spiritual aftermath was complex. The morning after her conversion she had resolved to tell her father what had happened but found the words impossible; she attended a prayer-meeting for revival workers at seven in the morning, only to find the chapel doors closed -- "She should have known that early morning prayers were never offered up in the chapel, but in the lecture-hall" (PN 006-018). Her spiritual awakening, so publicly enacted, was followed by private confusion and a persistent anxiety about whether her faith was genuine.

## Narrative Function

The revival serves as the novel's inciting incident, the event that transforms the diffuse situation of the opening chapters into a focused dramatic trajectory. Before the revival, Anna's relationships with [[Henry Mynors]], with the Prices, and with her own spiritual conscience are all potential energies, latent and uncommitted. After the revival, each of these relationships crystallizes into action: the engagement with Mynors, the financial pressure on [[Titus Price]], and the dawning awareness that her father's religion of property and her own religion of feeling may be incompatible.

The revival also exemplifies Bennett's characteristic narrative irony. An event that presents itself as purely spiritual -- a week of prayer, preaching, and conversion -- produces consequences that are almost entirely material: a marriage, a property deal, and a chain of financial demands that will end in a man's suicide. The gap between the revival's professed purpose and its actual outcomes is one of the novel's most devastating structural effects, and it anticipates the larger pattern by which the institutions of [[wesleyan-methodism|Wesleyan Methodism]] in the [[five-towns]] serve to ratify rather than challenge the economic order.
