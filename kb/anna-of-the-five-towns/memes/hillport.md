# Hillport

Hillport is the most fashionable residential district serving Bursley, described by Arnold Bennett as the "abode of the highest fashion" with "its own government and authority"[PN=002-002]. Situated on rising ground to the west of Bursley and Hanbridge, Hillport represents the social summit of the Five Towns — a place inhabited by the carriage-keeping elite, distinct from the merely respectable aspirations of Bleakridge.

## Location and Geography

Hillport lies to the west of Bursley and Hanbridge. From Toft End, "lying on the highest hill in the district, one had a panoramic view of Hanbridge and Bursley, with Hillport to the west, and all the moorland and mining villages to the north and northeast"[PN=003-099]. Its elevated position places it both physically and socially above the industrial centre of the Five Towns.

Hillport Church is a local landmark visible from considerable distances. From the garden wall of Manor Terrace, looking "over the grey fields of the valley up to Hillport," Anna can see "Hillport Church, dark and clear against the orange sky"[PN=002-009]. From her bedroom window at night, "to the far right across the fields the silhouette of Hillport Church could just be discerned on the ridge"[PN=006-003].

## Social Distinction

Bennett carefully distinguishes Hillport from Bleakridge in the novel's social geography. Whereas Bleakridge is "the pleasantest quarter of Bursley" within the municipal limits, Hillport "had its own government and authority"[PN=002-002]. To reside at the top of Trafalgar Road in Bleakridge "was still the final ambition of many citizens," but Hillport represents a higher tier altogether — the preserve of families who have transcended ordinary municipal ambition[PN=002-002].

The social gulf between Hillport and the rest of Bursley is illustrated by the carriage economy. Only two families among the Bursley Wesleyan Methodists keep a carriage: the Suttons and the Clayton Vernons. The Clayton Vernons, "boasting lineage and a large house in the aristocratic suburb of Hillport, gave to the society monetary aid and a gracious condescension"[PN=001-020].

## Hillport House and the Wilbraham Estate

Hillport House was the fine seat of William Wilbraham, J.P., "a famous magnate and benefactor of the Five Towns"[PN=002-006]. Wilbraham's "superb horses were constantly seen, winged and nervous, in the streets of Bursley and Hanbridge"[PN=002-006]. Upon his death, the *Staffordshire Signal* reported that the net value of his estate was sworn at fifty-nine thousand pounds — a sum that, when Anna read of it, "added a definite and startling significance to figures which had previously conveyed nothing to Anna except an idea of vastness"[PN=002-006].

The contrast between Hillport House and the Tellwrights' six-roomed abode in Manor Terrace becomes, for Anna, a catalyst for reflection on her father's wealth and miserliness. Bennett writes: "The crude contrast between the things of Hillport House and the things of the six-roomed abode in Manor Terrace gave food for reflection, silent but profound"[PN=002-006].

After Wilbraham's death, Henry Mynors acquires Hillport House, further cementing his social ascension. The house becomes known as "the Priory" — the home that Mynors and Anna will occupy after their marriage.

## The Mayor's Residence

The mayor of Bursley hosts important civic functions at his house at Hillport. An "important dinner at the mayor's house at Hillport" is held "in connection with Mr. Sutton's impending mayoralty"[PN=012-110]. This dinner occasions a significant narrative event: Beatrice Sutton, hurrying to attend it with her parents, asks Anna to visit the ailing Sarah Vodrey at Toft End in her mother's stead — an errand that leads to Anna's encounter with Willie Price and the deepening of her involvement in the Price family's tragedy.

## The Clayton Vernons

Mrs. Clayton Vernon is one of Hillport's most prominent residents. A figure of immense social authority within Bursley Wesleyanism, she "gave to the society monetary aid and a gracious condescension"[PN=001-020]. Her approval or disapproval carries weight in chapel affairs. She expects Anna to visit her at Hillport as a social duty, and Anna duly complies, though the visits are occasions of some nervousness for the younger woman.

When Mrs. Clayton Vernon is ill, Mrs. Sutton must go "up to Hillport before tea" to attend to her, treating the visit as a matter of some urgency[PN=001-018].

## Narrative Significance

Hillport functions in the novel as the visual and symbolic horizon of social aspiration. Anna repeatedly looks towards it from Manor Terrace and from Bleakridge, measuring her own constrained life against its promise of ease and elegance. The view of Hillport Church against the sky is the backdrop to her first conscious realisation that Henry Mynors loves her — a moment of emotional awakening that coincides with the contemplation of a world beyond her reach[PN=002-009].

When Anna, at the novel's close, visits the Priory (Hillport House) to check on workmen, she is "as red as the sun over Hillport"[PN=012-242] — the blush of a woman whose social trajectory has brought her, improbably, from the penury of Manor Terrace to the threshold of Hillport itself.

Hillport also serves as a geographical marker in the novel's wider topography. From Edward Street, the road climbs to Toft End, and from that height one sees "Hanbridge and Bursley, with Hillport to the west"[PN=003-099]. The industrial wastelands west of Bleakridge are contrasted with the ordered respectability of the Hillport ridge, creating a landscape that mirrors the social divisions of the Five Towns.
