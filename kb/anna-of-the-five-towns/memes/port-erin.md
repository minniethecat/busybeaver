# Port Erin

A small fishing village on the Isle of Man, described as "a lovely little fishing village" [010-011] where the Sutton family take their annual summer holiday. Unlike the more fashionable Llandudno, Port Erin is deliberately unpretentious — an escape from the social pressures and industrial landscape of the Five Towns.

## Location and Character

Port Erin is situated on the southern coast of the Isle of Man, approximately sixteen miles from Douglas — a journey that takes "sixty-five minutes" by the island's transport [010-083]. The village lies beneath the coastal heights; from the surrounding peaks one can see "peak after peak from Port Erin to Peel" [010-092]. Looking down, "Port Erin was hidden under their feet" [010-097] — a detail that captures the village's tucked-away quality.

The Suttons describe it as a place where "no one ever dresses" and note "it isn't like Llandudno" [010-017] — an explicit contrast to the more socially competitive Welsh resorts, underscoring Port Erin's appeal as a place of genuine relaxation rather than display.

## Significance in the Novel

### The Chapter 10 Excursion

Port Erin serves as the base for the novel's most extended episode of leisure — the mackerel-fishing expedition in Chapter 10. This is the only chapter set entirely outside the Five Towns, and the change of setting allows Bennett to explore his characters away from the constraints of chapel, commerce, and domestic routine.

The excursion to Port Erin brings together a cross-section of characters — Henry Mynors, Anna, Beatrice and Alderman Sutton, Willie Price — in a setting where the usual social hierarchies are temporarily suspended. The hired yacht *Fay*, the mackerel fishing, and the simple pleasures of the seaside create a rare interval of uncomplicated enjoyment.

### Escape and Return

Port Erin represents the possibility of escape from the Five Towns — a theme that resonates particularly with Anna, whose life is otherwise circumscribed by her father's authority and Bursley's expectations. Yet the return is inevitable: the chapter ends with the party heading back, and the brief freedom of Port Erin only sharpens the novel's prevailing sense of constraint.
