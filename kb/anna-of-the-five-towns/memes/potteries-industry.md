# The Potteries Industry

The Five Towns exist because of pottery. The manufacture of earthenware — cups, saucers, plates, and sanitary ware — is the economic foundation of Bursley, Hanbridge, and the surrounding towns. The industry shapes not only the landscape of kilns, canals, and manufactories but also the class structure, family fortunes, and moral character of Bennett's fictional world.

## The Industrial Landscape

The physical setting of the novel is inseparable from manufacturing. The "large manufactory with its corps of jacket-men" (PN 008-034) dominates Edward Street. The canal, essential for transporting both clay and finished goods, runs through the district. At the "gates of the manufactory opposite the chapel, a man in shirt-sleeves" (PN 006-027) stands as a constant reminder that industry and religion coexist in uneasy proximity. Anna's property includes "the earthenware manufactory in Edward Street occupied by Titus Price" (PN 003-045), making her literally a factory owner.

## Economic Structure

The pottery trade creates a distinctive class hierarchy. At the top sit the owners and valuers — Ephraim Tellwright made his fortune as a "potter's valuer" who had "exceptional opportunities of estimating" property values (PN 002-004). Below them are the tenant-manufacturers like Titus Price, who operate works they do not own. At the base are the workers — the "jacket-men" in the manufactories and the families in the "miserable quarter" of "infinitesimal cottages" (PN 003-100).

## Knowledge and Craft

Anna is "perfectly ignorant of the craft of pottery, and every detail of it" (PN 008-043), despite owning a manufactory. This ignorance is significant — it reflects the separation between capital and labor, owner and worker, that characterizes industrial capitalism. The novel's characters who work directly with clay are largely invisible; the story focuses on those who own, value, and trade in the products and properties of industry.

## Industry as Moral Force

Bennett portrays industry as a morally ambivalent force. It creates wealth but also the "grave disadvantages" (PN 003-045) of dilapidated properties and exploited tenants. It structures time — work rhythms, market cycles, property sales — but also produces the anxious speculation about "whether there's money in it" (PN 005-008). The manufacturing town is a place where people "couldn't help but make money" (PN 003-107) yet remain spiritually impoverished.
