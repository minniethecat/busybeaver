## Overview

The Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808 was a major schism in English Methodism that originated at Bursley, one of the Five Towns. The split resulted in the formation of the Primitive Methodist Connexion, a revivalist denomination that emphasised camp meetings, open-air preaching, and a return to what its adherents saw as primitive Christianity. The secession was "famous" in the history of Methodism in the Potteries [225-P225].

## The Bursley Conflict

The secession at Bursley was preceded by significant factional conflict within the local Wesleyan community. Ephraim Tellwright's father, "a Bursley man, had fought in the fight which preceded the famous Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808 at Bursley," identifying with the "Old Guard of Methodism" — those who opposed the Primitive Methodist departure [225-P225]. The Tellwright family had "a tradition of holy valour for the pure doctrine," placing them firmly in the anti-secession camp.

## Wider Methodist Fractures

The 1808 Bursley secession was the first of several major ruptures in early 19th-century Methodism. The Tellwright family's involvement in Methodist conflicts continued through "the Warren affrays of '28" and "the disastrous trouble of the Fly-Sheets in '49, when Methodism lost a hundred thousand members" [225-P225]. These successive conflicts reflected deep tensions within Methodism over authority, worship style, and the role of lay preachers.
