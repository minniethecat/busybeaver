# Self-Sacrifice and Renunciation

The moral framework — part Methodist theology, part Victorian domestic ideology — that taught the inhabitants of the Five Towns, particularly its women, to find virtue in the surrender of personal desire to duty, family, and God.

## Definition

Self-sacrifice and renunciation form a double doctrine in the world of the novel. Sacrifice involves the active giving up of something valued — money, love, autonomy — for a perceived higher good. Renunciation is the quieter, more pervasive state of having already surrendered one's claims.

The narrator observes with grim clarity that "a woman's life is always a renunciation, greater or less" (PN=014-024). This was not presented as tragedy but as the natural order, reinforced by the Methodist emphasis on submission, duty, and the mortification of selfish desire.

Anna Tellwright's stepmother found "satisfaction in the ideas of duty and sacrifice" (PN=002-009) — a satisfaction that was its own tyrannical reward.

## Manifestations in the Novel

Anna Tellwright is the novel's central figure of renunciation. Raised by a miserly father, she "had never any money of her own, not a penny" (PN=004-017), and her desires had been so thoroughly suppressed that she scarcely recognised them.

When she inherits wealth at twenty-one, the habits of self-denial are too deeply ingrained to be easily cast off. Her "sensitive and peremptory conscience" (PN=002-009) ensures that every impulse toward freedom is checked and reined in.

The grand renunciation scene occurs when Willie Price, the timid and vulnerable son of the disgraced Titus Price, silently surrenders his claim on Anna's sympathy and departs. Anna recognises "the grandeur of this superb renunciation on his part" (PN=003-058).

The victim of circumstance chooses to remove himself rather than burden another. Anna's own life trajectory toward marriage with Henry Mynors is itself a form of renunciation: she substitutes duty and respectability for passionate choice.

The broader society reinforces this ethic. "The worst tyrannies of her father" were made possible because of the daughter's "satisfaction in the ideas of duty and sacrifice" (PN=002-009).

Women were trained from childhood to accept that "what was permitted in childhood, was often sacrificed in maturity" (PN=011-037). The novel shows how this doctrine served patriarchal and economic power — Ephraim Tellwright's control over his daughters was legitimised by their own internalised duty.

## Key Passages

> "A woman's life is always a renunciation, greater or less. Hers by chance had been supreme." (PN=014-024) — The narrator's bleak summary of Anna's condition.

> "The worst tyrannies of her father were made possible only by reason of her own satisfaction in the ideas of duty and sacrifice." (PN=002-009) — The psychology of self-oppression: how the victim's own virtue enables the tyrant.

> "What was permitted in childhood, was often sacrificed in maturity." (PN=011-037) — The generational pattern of renunciation in Five Towns families.

## Character Connections

- **[[Anna Tellwright]]**: The exemplary figure of renunciation — a woman whose entire life is shaped by the ethic of duty, from her submission to her father to her pragmatic acceptance of Mynors's courtship (PN=014-024).

- **[[Ephraim Tellwright]]**: The beneficiary of his daughters' self-sacrifice, whose domestic tyranny relies on their internalised submission to duty (PN=002-009).

- **[[Willie Price]]**: The tragic embodiment of renunciation — a young man who gives up even the possibility of human connection, walking away from Anna in an act whose "grandeur" moves her deeply (PN=003-058).

- **[[Mrs. Tellwright (stepmother)]]**: A woman who has so fully internalised the doctrine of sacrifice that she finds genuine satisfaction in her own erasure (PN=002-009).

## Religious / Social Context

The ethic of self-sacrifice and renunciation in the Five Towns draws from two powerful sources. The first is Wesleyan Methodism, with its emphasis on personal holiness through self-discipline, the mortification of worldly desires, and submission to God's will.

Anna, though only "on the fringe of the Methodist society" (PN=004-020), had absorbed its moral logic thoroughly: "Imbued then with ideas of duty, submission, and systematic kindness" (PN=012-054).

The second source is the broader Victorian ideology of separate spheres, which assigned to women the role of moral guardian and selfless servant of family. In the industrial Potteries, where men's labour was tied to the brutal economics of the furnace and the workshop, women's sacrifice in the domestic sphere was naturalised as the necessary complement.
