# Price's Earthenware Manufactory

Price's Earthenware Manufactory, commonly called Price's Works, occupies a pivotal place in the economic and moral landscape of Arnold Bennett's *Anna of the Five Towns*. Located at the bottom of Edward Street at the eastern extremity of [[bursley|Bursley]], the manufactory is a decaying, ill-designed potbank whose physical dilapidation mirrors the financial and spiritual degradation of its operator, [[titus-price|Titus Price]]. Owned by the Tellwright estate and inherited by [[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]] on her twenty-first birthday, the works becomes a crucible in which the novel's themes of [[wealth-and-property|wealth and property]], moral hypocrisy, and the collision between industrial capitalism and Christian piety are tested.

The manufactory is far more than a backdrop. It is the site where Anna first exercises her economic agency, where the gap between Sunday-morning righteousness and Monday-morning reality becomes unbridgeable, and where the grim logic of [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]]'s property empire -- "squeeze the last penny out of Price without forcing him into bankruptcy" (PN 003-100) (PN 003-100) -- reaches its tragic conclusion. In the world of the [[five-towns|Five Towns]], Price's Works is a monument to the moral cost of industrial decline.

## Physical Condition and Layout

The works occupies the end of Edward Street, "a miserable quarter -- two rows of blackened infinitesimal cottages, and her manufactory at the end -- a frontier post of the town" (PN 003-100). Bennett's description is unsparing: the yard is "a small quadrangle paved with black, greasy mud," in one corner "a load of coal had been cast; in another lay a heap of broken saggars" (PN 003-102). Decrepit doorways lead to the various "shops" on the ground floor; those on the upper floor are reached by "narrow wooden stairs, which seemed to cling insecurely to the exterior walls" (PN 003-102). The office, the domain of Titus Price, is "the dirtiest that Anna had ever seen" -- a long narrow room with a bulging, blackened ceiling, a floor that "sank away in the middle till it was hollow like a saucer," and a "periodic muffled thud" from the engine below that shakes everything (PN 003-102).

The architecture itself is a study in industrial incompetence. The arched entrance for carts is placed "at the top of the steepest rise of the street, when it might as well have been at the bottom" -- a design flaw that [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] calculates "cost five pounds a year in horseflesh," which sum he notes "was the interest on a hundred" (PN 003-100). This observation, so characteristic of Tellwright's mentality -- converting every physical defect into its precise financial equivalent -- encapsulates the novel's treatment of the manufactory as a site where capital, labour, and material decay intersect.

Despite its decrepitude, the works is a functioning pottery. A packer in the packinghouse "was packing basins in a crate: with swift, precise movements, twisting straw between basin and basin, he forced piles of ware into a space inconceivably small" (PN 003-102) (PN 003-102). A girl in "clayey rags" carries a six-foot board laden with biscuit cups up the precarious exterior stairs -- a feat Anna watches with alarm, but "a man who stood in the yard did not even turn his head to watch it" (PN 003-119). These vignettes of skilled labour amid squalor convey the grim resilience of the pottery industry, even in its most marginal outposts.

## Ownership and Financial Structure

The manufactory is part of the property portfolio inherited by [[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]] from her maternal grandfather, coming to her on her twenty-first birthday alongside fifty thousand pounds in securities, shares, and real estate. Bennett lists it among "sundry properties in the Five Towns," noting that even as the list is read aloud, "Anna was a little alarmed to find herself the owner of this works; she knew that her father had had some difficult moments with [[titus-price|Titus Price]], and that the property was not without grave disadvantages" (PN 003-045).

The financial arrangements are a tangle of arrears and bad faith. Titus Price is five quarters in arrears on his rent, owing a total of 125 pounds. [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]]'s policy, before and after Anna's assumption of ownership, is calculated inertia: "He promised repairs in exchange for payment of arrears which he knew would never be paid, and his policy was to squeeze the last penny out of Price without forcing him into bankruptcy" (PN 003-100)" (PN 003-100). The property itself is virtually unsaleable: "the rent was a hundred a year, and the whole freehold would not have fetched eight hundred" (PN 003-100). Tellwright tells Anna bluntly: "Supposing ye sold owd Titus up? What then? D'ye reckon ye'd get a tenant for them ramshackle works? A thousand pound spent wouldn't 'tice a tenant" (PN 003-081).

This is the predicament Anna inherits. The works is a trap for its tenant and a liability for its owner -- a perfect Bennettian emblem of wealth that is no wealth, property that is no asset.

## Titus Price's Operation

[[titus-price|Titus Price]] himself is revealed, in the setting of his works, as a figure starkly different from the sleek Sunday-school superintendent. Anna's visit to Edward Street on her twenty-first birthday occasions a startling transformation: "Anna was astounded by the contrast between the Titus of Sunday and the Titus of Monday: a single glance compelled her to readjust all her notions of the man" (PN 003-100). She finds him standing on the weighing platform in shirtsleeves, smoking a pipe, "his puffed, heavy face, and general bigness and untidiness" giving "the idea of a vast and torpid male slattern" (PN 003-100). He has no cap; the sleeves of his shirt are rolled up, "exposing forearms covered with auburn hair" (PN 003-100) (PN 003-100). The superintendent who chastises schoolgirls in broadcloth is, on Monday, indistinguishable from the labourers he employs.

Price's business rhetoric mixes desperation with false promises. He tells Anna he "can't pay anything now -- no, not a cent. But I'll pay twenty pounds in a week" (PN 003-113) -- the same promise he made the previous week. He catalogues "ninety-and-nine alterations and repairs that must be done without the loss of a moment" (PN 003-115), blaming the building for his failure. But when Anna, acting on her father's instructions, insists on some payment, Price capitulates with theatrical reluctance: "I'll give ye ten pounds now, and I'll do what I can next week" (PN 003-117). Anna's silent response is the novel's verdict: "'Liar! You said you had nothing!' her unspoken thought ran" (PN 003-121): "'Liar! You said you had nothing!' her unspoken thought ran, and at the same instant the Sunday-school and everything connected with it grievously sank in her estimation" (PN 003-121).

The visit to Edward Street is, in effect, Anna's fall from innocence -- her first direct encounter with the duplicity that the [[wesleyan-methodist-society-bursley|Wesleyan Methodist Society]]'s prestige can mask.

## Willie Price and the Human Cost

If [[titus-price|Titus Price]] represents the moral corruption of the manufactory, his son [[willie-price|Willie Price]] embodies its human tragedy. Willie works alongside his father in the office, "casting up figures in an account book, and murmuring numbers to himself," wearing "an office coat, short at the wrists and torn at the elbows," with "a battered felt hat" thrust "far back over his head so that the brim rested on his dirty collar" (PN 003-102). Bennett describes him as "tall, thin, and ungainly in every motion," with "the look of a ninny" -- a young man whom "all the boys by a common instinct had combined to tease" and whom "the young paintresses continually made private sport of" (PN 003-102).

Yet Anna's perception of Willie is entirely different. She sees in his "blue eyes" only "simplicity and good intentions," and beside him she feels "old, sagacious, crafty: it seemed to her that someone ought to shield that transparent and confiding soul from his father and the intriguing world" (PN 003-102). Willie is the casualty of the manufactory's decline -- loyal, industrious, and incapable of the ruthlessness that survival in the pottery trade requires. When his father's financial ruin becomes inescapable, and Titus Price takes his own life by hanging, it is Willie who inherits the wreckage. The pathetic dignity of Willie Price, superintending the loading of crates onto railway lorries in the Edward Street yard, lingers as one of the novel's most affecting images.

## Symbolism and Thematic Function

Price's Earthenware Manufactory functions as a symbol of the broader industrial and moral decay that Bennett diagnoses in the [[five-towns|Five Towns]]. The works is "badly located, badly planned and badly constructed," its "faults defied improvement" (PN 003-100). This triple condemnation -- of location, design, and construction -- echoes the novel's implied verdict on the economic and spiritual order that the manufactory represents. Just as the building cannot be repaired, only demolished ("It wants pullin' down," Price tells Anna [PN=003-115]), so the system of debt, exploitation, and hypocrisy that it embodies cannot be reformed, only swept away (PN 003-100).

The manufactory also crystallises the novel's critique of the fusion between religious authority and economic power. Titus Price is simultaneously a debtor who breaks his promises and a Sunday-school superintendent who presumes to judge the morals of children. Anna's discovery of this contradiction -- "That he, debtor and promise-breaker, should have the effrontery to pray for the souls of children, to chastise their petty furtive crimes, was nearly incredible" (PN 003-077) -- is a crucial moment in her moral development. The works in Edward Street is the physical evidence that disproves the spiritual pretensions of the [[wesleyan-methodist-society-bursley|Wesleyan Methodist Society]].

Finally, the manufactory illustrates [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]]'s approach to property and people. Tellwright will not evict a tenant who cannot pay, not out of mercy, but because eviction would destroy the property's only value -- the modest rent it yields. He will not invest in repairs because the return would be negative. He maintains the status quo of slow decay because the alternatives are worse. This is the logic of [[wealth-and-property|wealth and property]] stripped of all sentiment: a calculus in which human beings -- Titus, Willie, the girl in clayey rags with her board of biscuit cups -- and a man who "did not even turn his head to watch it" (PN 003-119) -- are merely variables in an equation of profit and loss.

## Real-World Context

Price's Earthenware Manufactory is representative of the small, marginal potbanks that characterised the Staffordshire Potteries in the late 19th century. The real Stoke-on-Trent contained hundreds of such enterprises: family-run, undercapitalised, operating in antiquated premises, and perpetually vulnerable to fluctuations in demand and competition from larger, more efficient factories. Bennett, who was born in Hanley and worked in a solicitor's office that handled pottery business, knew this world intimately.

The architectural details of Price's Works -- the arched cart entrance, the yard, the packinghouse, the exterior stairways, the separate "shops" for different stages of production -- reflect the typical layout of a small Victorian pottery. The process of earthenware manufacture involved multiple specialised stages: slip-making, throwing, turning, biscuit-firing, glazing, and glost-firing. Each of these required distinct spaces, equipment, and skills. The "biscuit cups" carried by the girl on her board were ware that had been fired once but not yet glazed -- an intermediate state familiar to every pottery worker. The "saggars" in the yard were the fireclay containers in which ware was placed during firing to protect it from kiln gases. Bennett's description is technically precise without being obtrusive, grounding the novel's moral drama in the material realities of the pottery industry.
