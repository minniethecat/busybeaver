# Conscience and Duty

The concept of duty -- enforced by a "sensitive and peremptory conscience" (PN 002-009) -- forms the psychological backbone of *Anna of the Five Towns*. Characters are driven less by desire than by obligation, and the novel's central conflicts arise when personal feeling collides with perceived moral duty.

## The Wesleyan Moral Framework

Duty in [[bursley|Bursley]] is inseparable from [[wesleyan-methodism|Wesleyan Methodism]]. [[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]] embodies this fusion: he "took a certain satisfaction in the ideas of duty and sacrifice" (PN 002-009), viewing self-denial not as deprivation but as virtue. The [[sunday-school-education|Sunday-school]], prayer meetings, and circuit obligations create a structure in which every action has moral weight. [[anna-tellwright|Anna]]'s conscience is shaped by this same framework -- she "smote her for conniving at what she supposed to be wrong" (PN 009-119).

The [[wesleyan-methodist-society-bursley|Wesleyan Methodist Society of Bursley]] provided the institutional framework for this moral system. Class meetings, quarterly tickets, and the public confession of sin made conscience a communal rather than purely private matter. The revival scene in which Anna walks to the penitent form exemplifies this: her private spiritual crisis must be enacted publicly, before the eyes of the congregation, before it can be considered genuine. [[mrs-sutton|Mrs. Sutton]]'s gentle yet insistent questioning -- "Are you decided for Christ?" -- is the voice of a community that expects conscience to be visible, accountable, and conforming to established forms.

## Anna's Conscience

[[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]] is the novel's primary vehicle for exploring conscience. After learning of [[titus-price|Titus Price]]'s embezzlement, she experiences a "conscientious effort after honesty" (PN 009-119). Her internal conflict over confronting Price, her duty to her father's financial interests, and her unwilling complicity in the cover-up drive the novel's central moral drama. "The idea of duty ceased for a time to be paramount" (PN 011-002) only after the full weight of the crisis becomes clear.

Anna's conscience is inseparable from her relationship to [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] and the [[paternal-authority|paternal authority]] he represents. "The worst tyrannies of her father never dulled the sense of her duty to him" (PN 002-009) -- a statement that encapsulates the paradox at the heart of the novel's moral vision. Duty is at once oppressive and liberating, a prison and the only known path to moral coherence.

## The Price Scandal as Moral Crisis

[[titus-price|Titus Price]] represents the failure of conscience. "Tortured by conscience, pulled this way by Christ and that way by the devil" (PN 005-022), he embezzles building fund money while continuing to pray "with such sweet and wistful fervency" (PN 011-101). His exposure forces the Wesleyan community to confront the gap between public piety and private conduct. Anna's "unworthy attempt to appease her conscience" (PN 011-183) by visiting the disgraced Price family illustrates how conscience operates through social performance as much as inward conviction.

The [[price-embezzlement-scandal|embezzlement scandal]] and [[price-suicide|Price's subsequent suicide]] expose the limits of the Wesleyan moral framework. A system designed to produce righteous conduct instead produced a man whose "whole career was made up of dissimulations: religious, moral, and social" (PN 011-101). [[willie-price|Willie Price]]'s [[willie-price-emigration|emigration to Australia]] represents the collateral damage of this moral collapse: the innocent son who bears the shame his father created.

## Duty vs. Freedom

The novel questions whether duty liberates or imprisons. Anna sees "how miserably narrow, tepid, and trickling" (PN 002-009) the stream of duty-governed life can be, yet she cannot escape its claims. The "worst tyranny" of duty is that it makes characters complicit in their own constraint -- they choose what they are obligated to choose. The [[the-revival|Revival]] brings this tension to its sharpest point: Anna's conversion appears to be a free act of submission, yet it can equally be read as her capitulation to the community's expectations, the final surrender of a self that might have chosen differently.
