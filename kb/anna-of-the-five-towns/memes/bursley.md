# Bursley

Bursley is the primary setting of *Anna of the Five Towns* — a pottery town of "an antiquity of a thousand years" (PN 001-049), where every significant event of the novel unfolds. It is simultaneously a real place (Bennett's fictional name for Burslem, the oldest of the six Potteries towns), a social microcosm, and an argument about what industrial capitalism does to the human soul. Anna Tellwright's coming-of-age is inseparable from Bursley's grimy streets, its Wesleyan chapels, and the relentless accounting that governs both its commerce and its conscience.

## Overview

Bursley is one of the Five Towns — the contiguous pottery towns of Turnhill, Bursley, Hanbridge, Knype, and Longshaw (PN 001-050) — and serves as the novel's narrative centre. It is where Anna Tellwright has lived since her father Ephraim "settled in Bursley" (PN 001-045), where she attends the Wesleyan Methodist chapel, and where her social world unfolds.

The town is described as sedate and conservative, governed by "sedate elders of the borough" (PN 001-042). Yet beneath this placid surface, Bursley is a theatre of intense commercial and emotional drama: property dealings, inheritance, embezzlement, suicide, and marriage negotiations — all conducted within the narrow circumference of a town where everyone knows everyone's affairs.

> "Bursley, the ancient home of the potter, with an antiquity of a thousand years…" (PN 001-049)

## Location & Geography

Bursley sits at the centre of the Five Towns conurbation, linked to its neighbours by Trafalgar Road, which "under many aliases, runs through the Five Towns from end to end, uniting them" (PN 002-002). The town's most pleasant quarter is Hillport, "abode of the highest fashion of the town" (PN 002-002), situated on the heights above the industrial valley.

From the elevated vantage of Hillport one commands "a panoramic view of Hanbridge and Bursley, with Hillport to the west" (PN 003-099). The town's landmark is the "angel of the Town Hall spire" (PN 001-049), visible from the Manchester road as travellers approach — a gilded emblem of municipal aspiration above the bottle-kilns and smoke.

## Significance in the Novel

Bursley is not backdrop but determinant. The town's Wesleyan Methodist Society is "the sole form of municipal enterprise in Bursley" (PN 001-042) — an observation that captures the fusion of religious, social, and civic life that defines the community. The chapel is where courtships begin, reputations are made, and the social order is reinforced.

The town's commercial geography maps onto its moral geography. Trafalgar Road is the thoroughfare of respectable commerce; Edward Street houses the failing Price manufactory; Manor Terrace is the scene of domestic negotiation and paternal tyranny. Bennett's Bursley is a town where "the Doing was never suspended" (PN 001-068) — where idleness is sin and industry is religion.

> "Only two families of the Bursley Wesleyan Methodists kept a carriage — the Suttons and the Tellwrights. The Suttons' carriage was a landau, the Tellwrights' was a victoria." (PN 001-020)

This detail is characteristically Bennett: status is precise, comparative, and publicly legible. In Bursley, the carriage you keep is the person you are.

## Associated Characters

- **[[Anna Tellwright]]**: The protagonist, who has lived in Bursley since her father settled there. Her moral awakening is a struggle against the town's values.
- **[[Ephraim Tellwright]]**: The miser who "became a familiar object to Bursley" (PN 002-008), his fortune built on "a lot of cottage property in Bursley" (PN 002-007).
- **[[Henry Mynors]]**: Described as the "most influential man in Bursley" (PN 003-089) — the town's ideal of masculine success.
- **[[Titus Price]]**: Sunday-school superintendent whose earthenware manufactory operates in Bursley's Edward Street.
- **[[Willie Price]]**: The son of Titus Price, whose awkwardness makes him constitutionally unsuited to Bursley's commercial culture.
- **[[Beatrice Sutton]]**: Daughter of Alderman Sutton, representing Bursley's fashionable Hillport society.

## Thematic Resonance

Bursley is Bennett's laboratory for testing the effects of industrial capitalism on the human spirit. The town is prosperous but spiritually impoverished; its religion is sincere but entirely compatible with exploitation; its social bonds are warm but function as instruments of surveillance and control. Anna's ultimate crisis — the conflict between her charitable impulse toward Willie Price and her father's commercial logic — is a crisis manufactured by Bursley itself.

## Real-World Context

Bursley is Bennett's fictional name for Burslem, one of the six towns of Stoke-on-Trent and historically the centre of the English ceramics industry. Bennett, born in Hanley (Hanbridge in the novel), knew this landscape intimately. His fictionalisation is exact: the bottle-kilns, the canals, the Nonconformist chapels, and the social hierarchies of the Potteries are rendered with documentary precision. The omission of Fenton (the sixth town) from his "Five Towns" is a calculated simplification that has become part of the region's literary mythology.
