# Agnes Tellwright

Agnes Tellwright is Anna Tellwright's younger half-sister, the daughter of Ephraim Tellwright by his second wife. She is twelve years old when the novel opens, still a pupil at the Wesleyan Sunday-school and day-school in Bursley. Agnes is one of the few characters in the novel whose presence consistently brings warmth, affection, and a touch of girlish spontaneity into the austere Tellwright household.

## First Appearance

Agnes makes her first appearance in the novel's opening scene, at the Sunday-school prize-giving. Standing alone in the schoolyard, she is described as "a little girl of twelve years, dressed in a cream coloured frock, with a wide and heavy straw hat," quietly kicking her "foal-like legs against the wall" as she waits for her older sister Anna to emerge from the school[PN=001-002]. She has won a prize book and takes it from under her arm repeatedly to glance at its frontispiece "with a vague smile of satisfaction"[PN=001-002].

When Henry Mynors and Willie Price approach her, Mynors playfully questions whether she deserves the prize. Agnes scrutinises his "sparkling and vehement black eyes with the fearless calm of infancy" and decides firmly: "Yes, I do"[PN=001-005][PN=001-006]. This small moment reveals Agnes's self-possession and the directness that distinguishes her from her more diffident elder sister.

Mynors is, in Agnes's estimation, a perfect man: "His eyes, so kind and sincere, and that mysterious, delicious, inexpressible something which dwelt behind his eyes: these constituted an ideal for her"[PN=001-008]. She also feels a protective fondness for Willie Price, whose "simple, pale blue eyes had a wistfulness which made her feel towards him as she felt towards her doll when she happened to find it lying neglected on the floor"[PN=001-009].

## Family Background

Agnes is the product of Ephraim Tellwright's second marriage. After the death of his first wife (Anna's mother) from erysipelas, Tellwright, then in his fifties, married a young orphan girl some thirty years his junior who worked at the stationer's shop where he bought his newspaper[PN=002-006]. Anna, then eight years old, vividly remembered the arrival of this "wistful and fragile creature" and her own attempts to shield her from "the implacable harshness of her father's temper"[PN=002-006]. Agnes was born within a year, and her mother died of puerperal fever. Bennett notes that "in that year lay a whole tragedy, which could not have been more poignant in its perfection if the year had been a thousand years"[PN=002-006].

Agnes was thus raised by her father, her half-sister Anna, and a succession of housekeepers. By the time the novel opens, Anna, now in her early twenties, has become the de facto mistress of the household and Agnes's primary caregiver — a responsibility she discharges with quiet competence and deep affection.

## Character and Role

Agnes represents youthful innocence and unspoiled naturalness in a novel otherwise dominated by adult concerns of money, property, religion, and marriage. She skips around her sister's skirts as they walk home from chapel[PN=001-034], takes Mynors's arm with childish enthusiasm to show him the way[PN=001-041], and accepts her sister's authority with docile affection.

Despite her youth, Agnes exhibits moments of perceptiveness and emotional depth. When Anna departs for the Isle of Man, Agnes — who had been "laughing and chattering" — suddenly "burst into tears, sobbed passionately as though overtaken by some terrible and unexpected misfortune," as her sister's lips touch hers at the railway carriage[PN=010-049]. The narrator notes: "Tears stood also in Anna's eyes. The sisters had never been parted before"[PN=010-049]. This is one of the most emotionally direct moments in the novel.

During Anna's absence, Agnes serves as the "little housekeeper" at Manor Terrace — a prospect that pleases her almost as much as if she too were going to the Isle of Man[PN=009-111]. Anna brings her back a clothes-brush from the Isle of Man as a present, "long desired," which partially consoles Agnes for being sent to bed before she can see Mynors on the night of Anna's return[PN=011-040].

## Significance

Agnes Tellwright serves several narrative functions. She is a foil to her elder sister, embodying a childhood freedom and candidness that Anna, burdened by domestic responsibility and her father's emotional tyranny, has largely lost. Her presence humanises the Tellwright household, providing Anna with an outlet for the affection and nurturing impulse that find no other expression in that grim domestic economy. The bond between the two sisters — forged in shared loss and sustained through daily intimacy — is one of the novel's quiet emotional anchors.

She also represents the next generation of Five Towns womanhood. Unlike Anna, who was raised under the full force of Ephraim's miserly regime and whose spirit has been shaped by deprivation, Agnes has Anna as a buffer and protector. The novel leaves open the question of whether Agnes will grow into a woman like her sister — dutiful, self-sacrificing, and constrained — or whether the changing times and Anna's influence will allow her a freer path.
