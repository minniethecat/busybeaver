# Paternal Authority

The figure of the father in *Anna of the Five Towns* represents an almost absolute form of domestic power, rooted in property law, Wesleyan patriarchy, and the deferential culture of Victorian England. Ephraim Tellwright's control over his daughters — their money, their movements, their marriage prospects — is the novel's central dramatic constraint.

## Ephraim Tellwright: The Miser-Father

Ephraim embodies paternal authority in its most extreme form. Described "in plain terms, as a miser" (PN 002-006), he lives in "lodgings on thirty shillings a week" (PN 002-004) while controlling a fortune of fifty-nine thousand pounds. His authority over Anna is total: she has "never any money of her own, not a penny" (PN 004-017), despite being an heiress. The "shrewd miser of Manor Terrace" (PN 005-013) exercises a dominion that is simultaneously financial, psychological, and spatial — he owns the houses, controls the accounts, and determines what his daughters may know.

## The Inheritance of Power

Paternal authority in the novel is inherited as much as wealth. Ephraim's father was "a prominent Wesleyan Methodist" (PN 002-004) and circuit official, passing down both religious standing and property. Anna inherits not just her mother's eighteen thousand pounds but also her grandfather's Edward Street property and the paternalistic obligations that come with it. The novel traces how patriarchal power transmits across generations through legal instruments — wills, trusts, property deeds — that women cannot control.

## Silence and Secrecy

Ephraim's authority operates through withholding. He conceals the extent of Anna's fortune until she is twenty-one. He never discusses her mother. He gives commands rather than explanations. Anna learns that "it was torture to her to refer to her father's possessions" (PN 001-083), internalizing the taboo against questioning paternal control. The household at Manor Terrace is defined by what cannot be said.

## Challenge and Continuity

The novel explores whether paternal authority can be challenged. Anna's engagement to Mynors represents a potential transfer of control from father to husband, not liberation. When Anna finally confronts Price about his debt, she acts on her father's behalf, not her own initiative. The novel ends with Anna still under male authority — the question of whether she has achieved any genuine autonomy remains deliberately unresolved.
