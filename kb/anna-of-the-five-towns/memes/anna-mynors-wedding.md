# Anna & Mynors' Wedding

The marriage of Anna Tellwright to Henry Mynors is the novel's concluding event — the resolution toward which the narrative has been moving since their first walk to the Park in Chapter 1. The wedding brings together the major plot threads of inheritance, courtship, and patriarchal authority, and marks Anna's formal passage from her father's household to her husband's.

## The Engagement

Mynors proposes to Anna in the aftermath of the Price scandal and the Revival — the timing is significant, as Anna's moral courage in visiting the Price house and her steady conduct during the crisis have made her, in Mynors's eyes, a fitting partner. The engagement is announced and preparations begin, with Anna telling her father: "I must buy things for the wedding — clothes and things, father" [013-016].

## Preparations

The weeks before the wedding are marked by Ephraim Tellwright's characteristic tight-fistedness. When Anna asks for money for her trousseau, he grudgingly provides a sum that is, by his standards, generous but, by the standards of Anna's fortune, negligible. The purchase of furniture for the Priory — the house Mynors has taken as their marital home — is another site of negotiation, with Mynors setting aside "two hundred pounds for the purchase of furniture for the Priory — a modest sum; but he judged it sufficient" [013-065].

## The Ceremony

The wedding itself takes place without the detailed description Bennett gives to other communal events. The narrative focus is on the emotional dynamics surrounding the ceremony rather than its ritual particulars. Anna's state of mind — the mixture of relief, apprehension, and dutiful acceptance that has characterised her throughout — persists into the wedding day.

## Significance

### A Qualified Happy Ending

The wedding provides formal closure, but Bennett complicates the conventional "happy ending." Anna marries a good man — industrious, respectable, kind within his limits — but the marriage also represents a transfer of patriarchal control from father to husband. Anna's fortune, revealed on her twenty-first birthday, becomes Mynors's to manage. The novel's final sentence, famously, lingers on Anna's face at the wedding ceremony, leaving the reader to interpret what emotions lie beneath her composed exterior.

### The Price of Respectability

The wedding embodies the novel's ambivalent treatment of respectability. The marriage secures Anna's social position and material comfort, but it does so at the cost of the kind of passionate self-determination her grandfather's fortune might, in a different kind of novel, have made possible. The wedding is a triumph of the Five Towns' values — and a quiet tragedy of the same.
