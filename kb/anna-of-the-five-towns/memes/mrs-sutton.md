# Mrs. Sutton

Mrs. Sutton is one of the most respected women in the Wesleyan community of Bursley, the wife of Alderman Sutton and mother of Beatrice Sutton. Though she belongs to one of the wealthiest families in the Five Towns, she remains untouched by the pretensions of wealth, conducting herself with a plainness and simplicity that command universal respect. Her life is devoted to charity, the Sunday-school, and the spiritual welfare of those around her.

## Appearance and Manner

Mrs. Sutton is introduced in the novel's opening chapter as "a grey-haired woman with a benevolent but rather pinched face" who emerges "with much briskness from the girls' door" of the Sunday-school[PN=001-012]. She is short-sighted[PN=001-013] and her breathing is "short and quick"[PN=001-014], physical limitations that reflect the toll taken by a life of ceaseless activity on behalf of others.

Mrs. Sutton's bodily frame "had long ago proved inadequate to the ceaseless demands of a spirit indefatigably altruistic, and her continuance in activity was a notable illustration of the dominion of mind over matter"[PN=001-020]. Her health is fragile, yet she persists in her charitable rounds with determination. She uses her carriage not as a symbol of status but as "a contrivance on four wheels for enabling an infirm creature to move rapidly from place to place," and "when she got into it she had exactly the air of a doctor on his rounds"[PN=001-020].

## Character and Simplicity

Perhaps Mrs. Sutton's most striking quality is the way she has resisted the corrupting influence of wealth. Bennett emphasises that "neither husband nor wife had allowed riches to put a factitious gloss upon their primal simplicity. They were as they were"[PN=001-020]. The luxury of the Sutton drawing-room is attributable to Alderman Sutton's indulgence of their daughter Beatrice, not to any pretension on Mrs. Sutton's part. Mrs. Sutton "herself seemed scarcely of a piece with it"[PN=007-075]. Her character remains fundamentally unaltered by prosperity.

This simplicity is paired with enormous personal warmth. When Anna Tellwright first comes to a sewing-meeting at the Sutton house, Mrs. Sutton beckons her over and whispers maternally: "I thought I would like to have you near me, my dear"[PN=007-097]. She kisses Anna "almost passionately" when Anna agrees to consider the Isle of Man invitation, exclaiming: "Ah! my dear, I am glad, so glad. I did so want to have a word with you. You must 'lean hard,' as Miss Havergal says. 'Lean hard' on Him. Do not be afraid"[PN=007-130].

## Charitable Work

Mrs. Sutton's charitable activities are legendary in Bursley, though she attempts to conceal them. Her "charities were famous, notwithstanding her attempts to hide them"[PN=001-020]. She is a tireless visitor of the sick and elderly, as demonstrated when old Sarah Vodrey falls ill: Beatrice fears her mother would "take it into her head to do something stupid" like abandoning a formal dinner engagement to visit the sick woman personally[PN=012-110].

She is the organiser of the Sunday-school stall at the great Wesleyan Bazaar, "the greatest undertaking of its kind ever known in Bursley," and "pressed all her friends into the service"[PN=012-195]. Her housekeeping at the Isle of Man reveals the scale of her accustomed generosity: her purchases of food "seemed startlingly profuse to Anna," who calculates "that the housekeeping could not cost less than six pounds a week for the five" — while at Manor Terrace "three people existed on a pound"[PN=010-058].

## Spiritual Influence

Mrs. Sutton leads a weekly class for young women in the Wesleyan society. Henry Mynors suggests that Anna might join "Mrs. Sutton's class, which met on Monday nights," and Anna "found a well of spiritual inspiration in Mrs. Sutton's modest and simple yet fervent homilies"[PN=011-003]. Her piety is genuine and unostentatious. At the Isle of Man, during Beatrice's illness, she offers the "first and only word of religion, except grace at table, that Anna heard from the Suttons during her stay in the Isle of Man"[PN=010-220].

## Role in Anna's Life

Mrs. Sutton functions as a maternal figure for Anna, whose own mother died when she was a child. She extends the invitation for Anna to join the family on their Isle of Man holiday[PN=009-096], and it is she who negotiates with Ephraim Tellwright for his consent[PN=009-100]. When Anna embraces her in gratitude, Mrs. Sutton responds with simple warmth, kissing the child Agnes and naming her "the little housekeeper"[PN=009-111].

After Anna's engagement to Henry Mynors, Mrs. Sutton becomes deeply involved in the wedding preparations, though the imminent bazaar temporarily consumes her attention. It is decided that "the first week of the New Year, if indeed Mrs. Sutton survived the bazaar, should be entirely and absolutely devoted to Anna's business"[PN=012-220] — a comment that wryly acknowledges both her indispensability and her frailty.

## A Universal Regard

Mrs. Sutton commands a remarkable breadth of affection in the Five Towns. In his youth, Ephraim Tellwright himself "had known Mrs. Sutton as a young and charming girl, a vision that had stirred poetic ideas in hundreds of prosaic breasts, Tellwright's included." Bennett adds: "There was scarcely a middle-aged male Wesleyan in Bursley and Hanbridge who had not a peculiar regard for Mrs. Sutton, and who did not think that he alone truly appreciated her"[PN=009-081].
