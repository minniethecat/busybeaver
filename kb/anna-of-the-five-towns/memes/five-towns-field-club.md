# Five Towns Field Club

A social and sporting club drawing its membership from across the Five Towns. The Field Club appears in the novel primarily as a marker of Alderman Sutton's social position and his aspirational embrace of the habits and manners of the English country gentleman.

## Character and Membership

The Field Club is one of the institutions through which the prosperous middle class of the Five Towns signals its arrival. Alderman Sutton "had joined the Five Towns Field Club and acquired some of the habits of a sportsman" [001-020] — the phrasing is characteristically Bennettian, implying that the habits are acquired rather than innate, performance rather than nature.

In Chapter 7, at the sewing meeting, it is noted that Sutton's field-club membership — "which most of his acquaintances were resolved to forget" [007-087] — is among the traits that mark him as a figure of gently comic social pretension. The club membership, like the Sutton carriage and the aspirations toward Hillport, belongs to the apparatus of social climbing that Bennett observes with amused precision.

## Significance

The Field Club represents a particular strain of social aspiration in the Five Towns — the desire of successful manufacturers and businessmen to adopt the trappings of the landed gentry. Alderman Sutton, a successful building-society manager, cannot be a country gentleman, but he can join the club, affect the manner, and acquire the habits. The faint absurdity of the enterprise — the field sports of a potteries town — is part of Bennett's affectionate satire of provincial ambition.
