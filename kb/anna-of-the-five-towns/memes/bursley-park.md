# Bursley Park

The new municipal park of Bursley, a recently completed public amenity that features prominently in the opening chapter. The Park provides the setting for Anna's first socially significant encounter with Henry Mynors, and its terraced grounds and bandstand serve as the gathering place for Bursley's population on summer afternoons.

## Description

The Park is accessed by a "grand entrance" on the Moorthorne Road [001-042]. It "rose in terraces from the railway" [001-048], suggesting an ambitious landscaping effort that made the most of the sloping ground. A bandstand dominates the central area, visible to visitors as they approach [001-047].

Bennett's description is touched with gentle irony about provincial aspiration: "under all the circumstances the Park made a very creditable appearance" [001-048] — the qualifying phrase capturing both the genuine civic achievement and the awareness that Bursley's park, for all its merits, is not quite a metropolitan one.

## Significance in the Novel

### The Opening Scene

The Park is the destination of Anna's first significant outing in the novel. Agnes wants "to go up to the Park to hear the band play" [001-040], and it is here that Anna and Mynors walk together for the first extended time, observed — and deliberately not interrupted — by Beatrice Sutton, who diverts Agnes's attention [001-085].

On this particular afternoon, "it seemed, in the fierce heat, that the whole population was idle in the Park" [001-052]. The Park thus serves as Bennett's introduction to Bursley as a community — a place where the classes mingle, where courtship proceeds under the guise of hearing the band, and where the town's social life unfolds in the open air.

### Civic Pride and Social Display

The Park represents Bursley's self-conscious modernity. It is new — recently completed — and its terraces, bandstand, and "grand entrance" are objects of local pride. The opening discussion of the Park between Anna and Mynors ("So you have come to look at the Park," "They began to discuss the Park" [001-074, 001-081]) establishes it as the default topic of polite conversation, a shared civic reference point that bridges the gap between strangers.
