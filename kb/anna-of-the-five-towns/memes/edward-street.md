# Edward Street

Edward Street is a short, steep industrial street in [[bursley|Bursley]] where [[titus-price|Titus Price]] operates his earthenware manufactory. It is the locus of the novel's central financial plot: the property dealings, unpaid rents, and eventual forgery that drive the story towards its tragic climax.

## Overview

Edward Street represents the industrial underside of [[five-towns|Five Towns]] prosperity. It is "a short steep thoroughfare" (PN 003-099) located in a "miserable quarter -- two rows of blackened" dwellings and workshops (PN 003-100). [[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]] had "never been in Edward Street before" (PN 003-100) her father sent her to collect rent from [[titus-price|Titus Price]], marking it as a descent -- both physical and social -- into the realities that her sheltered domestic life has hidden from her.

The street leads into "a rough road across unoccupied land dotted with the mouths of abandoned pits: this road climbed up to Toft End, a mean annex of the town" (PN 003-099). The abandoned pitshafts that surround Edward Street prefigure the novel's dark conclusion, when [[willie-price|Willie Price]]'s departure is linked to an abandoned pit that serves as a repository for secrets never to be recovered. From Toft End, "lying on the highest hill in the district, one had a panoramic view of Hanbridge and Bursley, with Hillport to the west, and all the moorland and mining villages to the north and northeast" (PN 003-099) -- a view that contrasts the industrial blight below with the wider world beyond.

## Location and Geography

Edward Street sits in the lower, industrial part of Bursley. From its elevation, one could command a "panoramic view of Hanbridge and Bursley" (PN 003-099). The street is dominated by the [[price-manufactory|Price manufactory]], a potbank whose fortunes mirror the precariousness of Five Towns industry. The street is "miserable" in aspect, with blackened buildings testifying to decades of kiln smoke and industrial grime.

## Significance in the Novel

Edward Street is where the novel's romantic surface cracks to reveal the harsh economic realities beneath. [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] owns property on Edward Street, including the "earthenware manufactory in Edward Street occupied by Titus Price" (PN 003-045). Anna's first visit marks her reluctant entry into the world of business: "'Must I go to Edward Street?'" (PN 003-071) she asks her father. The street becomes a recurring site of anxiety -- Titus Price's desperate financial situation, culminating in forgery and [[price-suicide|suicide]], has its roots in the Edward Street works. Ephraim's obsession with the property -- "he had left his thoughts in Edward Street" (PN 009-119) -- and his final declaration that "Us'n have to do summat wi' Edward Street at last" (PN 011-090) shows how the street refuses to be ignored.

## Associated Characters

- **[[titus-price|Titus Price]]**: The Sunday-school superintendent and pottery manufacturer whose "miserable and forlorn undertaking in Edward Street" (PN 006-135) is the novel's central failing business.
- **[[willie-price|Willie Price]]**: Titus Price's son, who works alongside his father in the Edward Street works and bears the weight of its collapse.
- **[[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]]**: The landlord of the Edward Street property, whose relentless pursuit of rent drives the Prices towards ruin.
- **[[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]]**: Sent to Edward Street to collect rent, her visits there expose her to the reality of her father's business methods.

## Real-World Context

Edward Street corresponds to one of the many industrial streets in the real Burslem (Bursley). The potbanks of the Potteries were typically located on steep streets, with bottle-kilns dominating the skyline. Bennett's depiction of the "miserable quarter" captures the working conditions of the late-Victorian pottery industry.
