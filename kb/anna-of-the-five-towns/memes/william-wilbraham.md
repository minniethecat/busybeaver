William Wilbraham is a wealthy pottery magnate who resides at Hillport House, a great mansion overlooking the Five Towns. Though he never appears directly in the narrative, his presence looms over the novel's exploration of wealth and social status.

## Role in the Novel

Wilbraham is first mentioned when Ephraim Tellwright reveals to Anna the extent of her inheritance from her maternal grandfather. Tellwright uses Wilbraham as a benchmark, noting that even this great magnate left a fortune of only fifty thousand pounds:

> "Old William Wilbraham, the greatest pottery manufacturer that ever lived, only left fifty thousand pounds" (002-006).

This comparison serves a crucial narrative function — it makes Anna realize that her own fortune of fifty thousand pounds places her among the wealthiest individuals in the Five Towns, transforming her understanding of her social position.

## Hillport House

Wilbraham's residence, Hillport House, is described as a commanding mansion on the hill overlooking Bursley. It symbolizes the pinnacle of industrial success in the Potteries and serves as a material benchmark of the wealth Anna unexpectedly possesses. The house represents the social heights attainable through pottery manufacturing in Victorian England.

## Significance

As an offstage figure, Wilbraham represents the established industrial aristocracy of the Potteries — the generation that built the great pottery fortunes against which the novel's active characters measure themselves. His legacy provides the yardstick by which Tellwright, and through him Anna, understand the meaning of great wealth in the Five Towns.
