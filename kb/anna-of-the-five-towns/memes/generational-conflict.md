# Generational Conflict

The Five Towns at the turn of the century are caught between old and new ways. Ephraim Tellwright, with his chamois-leather moneybags and thirty-shilling lodgings, represents a generation whose accumulation is its own end; Henry Mynors, the "audaciously successful" commission agent, represents a generation for whom money is a means to social position.

## The Old Generation

Ephraim embodies the Potteries old guard — men who built fortunes through property and thrift, who "had no social ambitions" (PN 011-057), and whose authority over their children was absolute. His father was "a prominent Wesleyan Methodist" (PN 002-004) of the old school. The elder Tellwrights belong to a world where fortunes were made by "potter's valuers" with "exceptional opportunities of estimating" property (PN 002-004), and where money, once accumulated, was never spent.

## The Rising Generation

Mynors, Anna, Beatrice, and the younger Suttons inhabit a different world. Mynors "made money with facility in that lucrative line" (PN 001-020) of commission agency — a new kind of business built on trade rather than property. The younger generation attends bazaars, takes excursions to the Isle of Man, and dreams of "fifty ovens" (PN 008-042). Yet they remain bound by the old moral framework — the Wesleyan conscience that Ephraim's generation built.

## Anna Between Worlds

Anna is caught between generations. She inherits her grandfather's property and her father's moral inhibitions, but she is courted by Mynors, the man of the future. Her fifty thousand pounds is old money; her engagement is to new ambition. The novel leaves unresolved whether she can bridge the gap or will be crushed between them.

## Key Passages

> At twenty he was worth twenty thousand pounds, and he lived in lodgings on thirty shillings a week. (PN 002-004)

> Ephraim had no social ambitions — with all his meanness, he was above them. (PN 011-057)

> A "safe" and profoundly respectable man, at the same time audaciously successful. (PN 011-057)
