# Anna Tellwright

Protagonist of Arnold Bennett's _Anna of the Five Towns_ (1902), a young woman trapped between duty to her miserly father and her awakening to love and moral autonomy in the industrial Potteries.

## Character Overview

Anna Tellwright is introduced on the eve of her twenty-first birthday as a tall, sturdily built young woman with a grave, angular face and an austere, almost cloistered bearing. She is the elder daughter of Ephraim Tellwright, a wealthy miser of Bursley, and has been mistress of her father's household since the age of sixteen.

Bennett describes her as "the ascetic, the prosaic, the impassive" -- a woman whose life has been shaped by "ideas of duty and sacrifice" within the narrow confines of Wesleyan Methodism and her father's oppressive regime (PN 002-010).

Despite her outward severity, Anna possesses a "just and unshakable self-esteem which is fundamental in all strong and righteous natures" (PN 001-045). She is the moral centre of the novel, and her evolution from passive endurance to active moral choice constitutes its primary arc.

## Background and Circumstances

Anna was born around 1873, the only child of Ephraim Tellwright's first marriage. Her mother died of erysipelas when Anna was five, and a housekeeper was engaged (PN 002-004).

When Anna was eight, her father remarried a young orphan girl thirty years his junior. Agnes was born within a year, and the second Mrs Tellwright died of puerperal fever. "In that year lay a whole tragedy" (PN 002-004).

At sixteen, upon the housekeeper's death, Anna became "mistress of the household, with a small sister to cherish and control" (PN 002-004). The family inhabited a six-roomed house in Manor Terrace, Bleakridge, rented at thirty pounds a year.

The household ran on a weekly sovereign for housekeeping. Anna's only intellectual outlet was the Free Library. Unknown to most of Bursley, she was heiress to a fortune of fifty thousand pounds inherited from her mother.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Ephraim Tellwright]]**: The defining bond of Anna's life -- a "tyranny" that provokes hatred in her mind but a "disobedient" love in her heart. She is terrified of his wrath yet bound by blood that "nothing could break" (PN 002-004).

- **[[Agnes Tellwright]]**: Anna's half-sister and companion. Agnes's "passive fatalism characteristic of her years" provides a foil to Anna's moral seriousness (PN 011-220).

- **[[Henry Mynors]]**: The man who awakens Anna to love. Mynors is "handsome, dark, accomplished, exemplary and prosperous" (PN 001-045). His courtship transforms her, making her see "for the first time that she was loved" (PN 001-024).

- **[[Willie Price]]**: A tragic figure whom Anna protects by burning the forged promissory note, defying her father's demand for prosecution (PN 011-220).

## Role in the Narrative

Anna's trajectory is driven by two catalysts: her father's investment of her inheritance in Henry Mynors's pottery business, and Mynors's romantic pursuit of her.

At the novel's opening, she is a figure of passive endurance -- teaching Sunday-school and shrinking from community notice. The courtship proceeds through set pieces of Wesleyan life: the Park excursion, a sewing meeting, the Isle of Man trip.

Anna moves from a "trance" where she murmurs "He loves me" (PN 002-009) to formal engagement and the revelation of her fortune. The climax involves her moral awakening: she burns the forged note, telling Ephraim "It was mine, really" (PN 011-220).

Her father's furious denunciation cannot undo her act of moral independence. She has chosen conscience over obedience for the first time in her life.

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Anna is driven by a deeply internalised sense of duty, reinforced by the Wesleyan ethic of self-denial. Her love for Mynors introduces a competing motivation: the desire to escape the "miserably narrow, tepid, and trickling" stream of her existence (PN 002-009).

The tension between duty and desire animates her entire arc. Her burning of the forged note resolves it in favour of moral conscience over paternal obedience.

### Arc and Development

Anna begins the novel in near-total subservience. The arrival of love -- and the recognition that she, "the miser's shabby daughter," has drawn Mynors from his "strict orbit of virtuous custom" (PN 002-009) -- marks the first rupture in her psychological armour.

The crisis over Willie Price completes her development. By defying her father, Anna moves from passive sufferer to active moral agent. She does not escape her circumstances but transcends them through deliberate compassion.

### Thematic Significance

Anna embodies the central tension between materialist values (her father's miserliness, Mynors's ambition) and genuine Christian conscience. She is a study in female renunciation and the costs of patriarchal control.

## Critical Perspective

> Anna Tellwright has been read as a proto-feminist figure whose moral awakening parallels the broader social awakening of women in late-Victorian England.
