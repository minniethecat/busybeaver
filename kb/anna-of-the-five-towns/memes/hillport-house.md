Hillport House is the commanding mansion on the hill of Hillport, overlooking the Five Towns of the Potteries. Once the residence of the great pottery magnate William Wilbraham, it stands as a material symbol of the highest achievement possible in the district's industrial economy.

## Description

The house is situated atop the hill at Hillport, affording views over Bursley and the surrounding towns. Its elevated position physically embodies the social elevation achieved by the pottery aristocracy. After Wilbraham's death, the house remains a landmark against which other characters measure their own prosperity.

## Significance in the Novel

Hillport House serves as a crucial reference point when Ephraim Tellwright reveals Anna's inheritance. By comparing her fifty thousand pounds to the fortune left by Wilbraham — \"the greatest pottery manufacturer that ever lived\" — Tellwright uses the house and its late owner to calibrate Anna's understanding of her own wealth (002-006).

The house also represents the aspirational horizon of the Five Towns bourgeoisie. Henry Mynors, as a rising manufacturer, represents the next generation that might aspire to such establishments. The house thus anchors the novel's exploration of wealth, class mobility, and the material culture of industrial Victorian England.
