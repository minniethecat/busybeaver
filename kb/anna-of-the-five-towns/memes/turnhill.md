# Turnhill

One of the Five Towns of the Staffordshire Potteries, listed first in Bennett's enumeration at the opening of the novel: "Five contiguous towns — Turnhill, Bursley, Hanbridge, Knype, and Longshaw — united by a single winding thoroughfare" [001-050]. Turnhill occupies the north-western end of this urban chain.

## Significance in the Novel

Turnhill appears in the novel primarily in relation to Henry Mynors's early career. Before establishing himself as a successful pottery manufacturer in Bursley, Mynors "had worked for a large firm in Turnhill, as clerk, as traveller, and last as manager, living always quietly in lodgings" [005-013]. The decade Mynors spent in Turnhill represents his apprenticeship in commerce — the patient, steady climb from clerk to manager, saving capital and building the expertise that would eventually allow him to set up independently.

Late in the novel, when the Price manufactory is being sold as a going concern, Anna is told that "some people at Turnhill are buying it" [012-146]. This brief mention positions Turnhill as a source of outside capital, underlining the interconnectedness of the Five Towns' commercial life.

## The Five Towns Context

Turnhill is the first-named of the Five Towns in Bennett's famous enumeration, suggesting it occupies the geographical and symbolic starting point of the conurbation. While the novel is centred on Bursley, the other four towns appear intermittently — as places of work, as sources of competition, and as reminders that Bursley's dramas are part of a larger regional tapestry.
