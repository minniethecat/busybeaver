St. Luke's Square is the central marketplace and public square of Bursley, one of the principal public spaces of the Five Towns. It appears throughout *Anna of the Five Towns* as a focal point of commercial and social activity.

## Description

The Square is dominated by St. Luke's Church and surrounded by shops, businesses, and public buildings. Its market serves as the commercial heart of Bursley, where traders and townspeople gather for the buying and selling of goods. The area around the Square includes some of Bursley's most substantial commercial establishments.

## Significance

St. Luke's Square represents the civic and commercial center of Bursley life. It is the space where the town's various social strata intersect — where manufacturers, shopkeepers, laborers, and housewives all conduct their daily business. In Bennett's fictional geography of the Five Towns, St. Luke's Square occupies a position analogous to the market squares of the real Potteries towns (such as Stoke and Hanley) that served as the region's urban centers.

The Square's name, invoking the patron saint of the parish church, reflects the deep intertwining of religious and civic identity characteristic of Victorian English towns. It is a space where sacred and secular, commerce and community, intersect in the daily life of the Five Towns.
