The pottery industry is the economic backbone of the Five Towns in *Anna of the Five Towns*. The manufacture of ceramic goods — from fine china to sanitary ware — defines the region's economy, social structure, and physical landscape.

## Industrial Context

By the late 19th century, the Potteries (based on the real Stoke-on-Trent area) had become the world's center of ceramic manufacturing. The district's unique combination of local clays, coal for firing kilns, and canal/rail transport infrastructure made it the ideal location for pottery production on an industrial scale.

## In the Novel

The pottery industry is omnipresent in Bennett's novel, though often as background rather than foreground. Characters like Henry Mynors represent the new generation of pottery manufacturers — efficient, commercially minded, and ambitious. The physical landscape of kilns, manufactories, and potbanks shapes the visual world of the novel. Financial dealings in pottery-related stocks (the industries Anna inherits shares in) drive key plot developments.

The industry also shapes the novel's social themes: the wealth it generates (as represented by William Wilbraham), the labor it commands, and the class structure it produces. Bennett, himself the son of a solicitor in the Potteries, portrays the industry with an insider's understanding of its rhythms and hierarchies.
