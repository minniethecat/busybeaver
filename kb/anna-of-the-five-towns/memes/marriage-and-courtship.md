# Marriage and Courtship

In the [[five-towns|Five Towns]], marriage is primarily an economic and social transaction, conducted within the constraints of [[wesleyan-methodism|Wesleyan]] respectability. [[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]]'s courtship by [[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]] -- and her eventual engagement to him -- reveals how property, piety, and propriety govern romantic choice in Bennett's world.

## Courtship as Negotiation

[[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]]'s attention to [[anna-tellwright|Anna]] is inseparable from her inheritance. The community suspects "it was her money which was drawing Mynors from Beatrice Sutton" (PN 001-045). Anna's "wealth had diminished to a trifle" (PN 013-055) in the scale of her emotional life, yet it remains the invisible third party in every romantic exchange. Mynors is described as "wrecked in the sea of foolish marriage" (PN 010-002) only in passing -- the novel never directly questions whether he loves Anna or her fifty thousand pounds.

The courtship unfolds within the institutional framework of the [[wesleyan-methodist-society-bursley|Wesleyan Methodist Society]]. Conversations occur after services, at Teachers' Meetings, en route to and from the chapel gates. Mynors calls at [[manor-terrace|Manor Terrace]] "once a week, never on the same day" (PN 010-002) -- a method as calculated as any business strategy. The [[the-revival|Revival]] provides the emotional catalyst: in its aftermath, with "the divine fire still uncooled" (PN 004-028), Mynors presses his suit with an urgency that the ordinary rhythms of chapel life would not have permitted.

## The Marriage Market

The Five Towns marriage market operates on clear principles. Eligible men -- Mynors, the rising commission agent; the circuit preacher -- assess women by their piety, their property, and their family connections. [[beatrice-sutton|Beatrice Sutton]], with her carriage and her mother's social standing, was the presumed favorite before Anna's fortune was known. Anna herself, "a woman since seventeen" (PN 001-024), has been marriageable for four years without suitors because her wealth was hidden.

The [[sutton-family|Suttons]] represent the model of marriage as social institution. Their carriage, their pew, their annual trip to the [[isle-of-man|Isle of Man]] -- all reinforce the interconnection of marriage, property, and respectability. When Mrs. Sutton invites Anna on the Isle of Man excursion, she is effectively facilitating a courtship that will absorb Anna into the Sutton-Mynors orbit of prosperous Methodist respectability.

## Engagement as Constraint

Anna's engagement to Mynors is narrated less as liberation than as a new form of obligation. She "had long foreseen the possibility of this marriage" (PN 011-057) with something closer to resignation than joy. The engagement ring, the public announcement in chapel, the visits to relatives -- each ritual of courtship binds Anna more tightly to a future she has not chosen freely. Mynors's declaration that "I'm none for marriage and never was" (PN 012-085) (spoken of another matter) hints at the ambivalence beneath the conventional surface.

[[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]]'s approval of the match is characteristically financial. He sees in Mynors "a 'safe' and profoundly respectable man" (PN 011-057), and his consent is given in the same spirit with which he approves a property transaction. The engagement becomes another asset in the Tellwright portfolio, and Anna's emotional life is subsumed into the calculus of advantage.

## Love in an Industrial World

Bennett offers fleeting moments of genuine feeling -- Anna's recognition "for the first time that she was loved" (PN 001-025), the terrace "where love is kindling" (PN 001-050) -- but these moments are overwhelmed by social machinery. The novel asks whether love can survive in a world where everything, including marriage, has its price. The [[price-embezzlement-scandal|embezzlement scandal]] and [[price-suicide|Titus Price's suicide]] cast their shadow over the engagement, and the question of whether Anna truly loves Mynors or merely acquiesces to the inevitable remains one of the novel's central ambiguities.

## Key Passages

> From this terrace-height where love is kindling, embrace the whole town. (PN 001-050)
