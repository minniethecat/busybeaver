# The Priory

A red-brick eighteenth-century house in Toft End, Bursley, originally the home of Titus and Willie Price and later taken by Henry Mynors as the house he will share with Anna after their marriage. The Priory — sometimes called Priory House, after a legend that a priory once occupied the site [012-081] — passes from one family to another in a transaction that mirrors the larger financial and moral drama of the Price embezzlement.

## Architecture and Setting

The Priory is a solid, unpretentious house: "Built of red brick, towards the end of the eighteenth century, double-fronted, with small, evenly disposed windows, and a chimney stack at either side" [012-081]. It stands in the midst of Toft End, the "mean and struggling suburb" on the hillside east of Bursley [012-081]. A long, narrow garden runs parallel with the road, and a garden-gate opens onto a path flanked by rank bushes that "had been allowed to grow till they almost met across the narrow path leading to the front door" [012-115].

## Significance in the Novel

### The Price Era

Under Titus Price's ownership, the Priory is a house of secrets — its respectable facade concealing the financial ruin within. The house "belonged to Mr. Price, but was mortgaged up to the hilt" [012-070]. It is here that Anna makes her brave visit after the scandal breaks, arriving at the garden-gate on a "thick and mysterious night — such a night as death chooses" [012-115].

The Priory's "rickety furniture" is auctioned off in Hanbridge — "the frail sticks lost their identity in a medley of other sticks, and ceased to be" [012-149] — a quietly devastating image of a home dissolved.

### The Mynors Era

Henry Mynors takes the Priory less than a fortnight after Anna's visit to Price, announcing: "I've taken the Priory, subject to your approval" [012-108]. From that moment, Mynors "invariably referred to the place as the Priory" [012-108], reclaiming the name and the house for a new chapter. Bricklayers, plasterers, painters, and paperhangers descend upon the deserted house, "whistled and sang in it" [012-149], filling its rooms with the sound of renewal. Mynors sets aside two hundred pounds for furniture — "a modest sum; but he judged it sufficient" [013-065].

### A House as Symbol

The passage of the Priory from Price to Mynors is emblematic. Price, the failed and desperate man, loses his home; Mynors, the successful and upright man, acquires it at a favourable price and transforms it. The same walls that contained Price's guilt and ruin will house Mynors's marriage and future. Bennett offers no explicit commentary on this transaction, but the juxtaposition speaks volumes about the remorseless logic of property and respectability in the Five Towns.
