# Bleakridge

The most desirable residential quarter within the municipal limits of Bursley, "the pleasantest quarter" of the town (PN 002-002). Bleakridge is where the Tellwright family lives, at the top of Trafalgar Road, and it embodies the aspirational hopes of Bursley's prosperous citizens — a place to which one climbs, literally and metaphorically.

## Location and Character

Bleakridge occupies the height at the upper end of Trafalgar Road. Bennett describes its evolving status with characteristic precision: "to reside 'at the top of Trafalgar Road' was still the final ambition of many citizens, though the natural growth of the town had robbed Bleakridge of some of that exclusive distinction which it once possessed" (PN 002-002). It sits above the commercial centre, above Duck Bank, looking westward over the town toward the industrial landscape of kilns and chimneys.

Within its limits, Trafalgar Road underwent a distinct social geography on its ascent to Bleakridge. First came "a succession of manufactories and small shops; then, at the beginning of the rise, a quarter of a mile of superior cottages; and lastly, on the brow, occurred the houses of the comfortable — detached, semi-detached, and in terraces, with rentals from £25 to £60 a year" (PN 002-002). This gradient — from commerce through skilled labour to professional comfort — is a physical diagram of Bursley's class structure.

Bleakridge is distinct from Hillport, the "abode of the highest fashion" which has "its own government and authority" (PN 002-002). Bleakridge is aspirational but not aristocratic — it is the home of the solidly prosperous, not the carriage-keeping elite. The distinction was material and measurable: the Sutton carriage was a landau, the Tellwright carriage a victoria (PN 001-020); neither family kept horses for display alone.

The view from Bleakridge encompasses the full industrial panorama: "on the waste lands west of Bleakridge, long fields of burning ironstone glowed with all the strange colours of decadence" (PN 006-003). The proximity of beauty and industry — residential comfort and burning waste — is characteristic of the Five Towns. "The entire landscape was illuminated and transformed by these unique pyrotechnics of labour atoning for its grime, and dull, weird sounds, as of the breathings and sighings of gigantic nocturnal creatures, filled the enchanted air" (PN 006-003). Anna, Bennett tells us, "saw nothing there save the repulsive evidences of manufacture, had never seen anything else" (PN 006-003) — a reminder that the picturesque industrial sublime was invisible to those who lived inside it.

## The Tellwright Home

The Tellwright house in Manor Terrace was "of light yellow brick, two-storied, with a long narrow garden behind, and the rent £30" (PN 002-002). "Manor Terrace" itself was "a last reminder of the great farmstead which formerly occupied the western hill side" (PN 002-002) — Bennett's footnote on the erasure of an agrarian past by industrial housing.

Ephraim Tellwright's wealth could afford a more fashionable address, but his miserliness and his identity as a self-made man of business keep the family in Bleakridge — comfortable but not ostentatious, respectable but not grand. The rent of £30 a year compares pointedly with Alderman Sutton's house at £60 — exactly double — a difference that Bennett records with the precision of a valuer's report.

## The Mynors Connection

Exactly opposite Manor Terrace stood "an antique red mansion, standing back in its own ground — home of the Mynors family for two generations, but now a school, the Mynors family being extinct in the district save for one member" (PN 002-002). This detail is loaded: Henry Mynors, the "one member" who remains, is the last survivor of a family that once dominated Bleakridge. His return to prominence through business and courtship is, in part, a reconquest of natal territory.

## Social Geography of the Brow

The brow of Bleakridge mapped social standing with cartographic precision. Below Manor Terrace "lived the Wesleyan superintendent minister, the vicar of St. Luke's Church, an alderman, and a doctor" (PN 002-002) — the professional pillars of the community, arranged along the road in descending order of spiritual and temporal authority. "Somewhat higher up, still on the opposite side to Manor Terrace, came an imposing row of four new houses, said to be the best planned and best built in the town, each erected separately and occupied by its owner. The nearest of these four was Councillor Sutton's, valued at £60 a year" (PN 002-002).

## The Geography of Ambition

Bleakridge represents the summit of ordinary ambition in Bursley. Characters climb toward it — Anna "breasting the first rise towards Bleakridge" on her walk home (PN 004-025) — in journeys that are both physical and social. The fact that Bleakridge's exclusivity has been eroded by the town's growth mirrors the novel's concern with the fluidity and precariousness of social position in an industrialising society. Ephraim Tellwright "could remember the time when this part of it was a country lane, flanked by meadows and market gardens" (PN 002-002) — the entire suburb a monument to the speed with which industrial capitalism had transformed the landscape.

## Proximity to Industry

Bleakridge's situation, with its views of burning ironstone and distant kilns, embodies the intimate relationship between residential comfort and industrial production that defines the Five Towns. The pleasantness of Bleakridge is never entirely separate from the industry that funds it — one looks out from the parlour window onto the fires of the potteries. Toft End, "a mean annex of the town about half a mile east of Bleakridge" (PN 003-099), lies on the highest hill in the district, from which "one had a panoramic view of Hanbridge and Bursley, with Hillport to the west" (PN 003-099) — a reminder that elevation was no guarantee of gentility in the Potteries.
