# The Sunday-School

The Sunday-school is the beating heart of Wesleyan community life in Bursley, functioning simultaneously as a site of religious education, a mechanism of social control, and a stage upon which the major characters perform their public roles — teachers, superintendents, benefactors, and scholars alike.

## Definition

In late 19th century Nonconformist England, the Sunday-school was far more than a weekly hour of Bible instruction. It was the primary instrument of working-class and lower-middle-class education in literacy and morality, a key site of lay leadership, and a central pillar of chapel social organisation.

In *Anna of the Five Towns*, the Sunday-school provides the institutional framework for the novel's opening scene and becomes the symbolic battleground for its central moral conflict — the embezzlement of the new school building fund by its own superintendent (001-002, 004-002).

## Manifestations in the Novel

The novel opens at the Sunday-school itself: "the great doors at either end of the Sunday-school — boys from the right, girls from the left" (PN 001-002). This opening establishes the school as the novel's primary social stage. Henry Mynors presides as morning superintendent while the teachers — including Anna Tellwright and Beatrice Sutton — manage their classes.

The opening scene captures the hierarchy and ritual of the institution: prizes, attendance records, the Bible class for senior men, and the intricate social negotiations that happen in the school yard afterwards (001-004, 001-027).

Titus Price's position as Sunday-school superintendent gives him the authority and trust that make his betrayal so devastating. Anna is "a little startled by the sudden sight of the magnificent and stout superintendent" (PN 003-077).

His embezzlement of building funds exploits the trust placed in his office, and his exposure is felt as a wound to the entire institution: "It's an awful thing for the Sunday-school, and the whole society, too" (PN 011-080).

The new Sunday-school building project, undertaken by "the Wesleyan Methodist Society in Bursley" (PN 004-002), drives the plot. The visitation of parents of Sunday-school scholars (PN 004-002) brings Anna into closer contact with Mynors and initiates the teachers' meeting subplot.

The fundraising for the new school — including the Wesleyan Bazaar with its Sunday-school stall managed by Mrs. Sutton (PN 013-002) — threads through the novel's second half, a constant reminder of the institution that Price has betrayed.

## Key Passages

> The great doors at either end of the Sunday-school — boys from the right, girls from the left — were thrown open. (PN 001-002)

> Henry Mynors was the morning superintendent of the Sunday-school and conductor of the men's Bible class held in the lecture-hall on Sunday afternoons. (PN 001-004)

> The building scheme about to be undertaken by the Wesleyan Methodist Society in Bursley. Its object was to erect a new Sunday-school. (PN 004-002)

## Character Connections

- **Henry Mynors**: Morning superintendent, Bible class conductor, driving force behind the new school project (001-004, 002-005)
- **Titus Price**: Sunday-school superintendent whose embezzlement of the building fund exposes the institution to betrayal (003-045, 011-101)
- **Anna Tellwright**: A Sunday-school teacher; her participation reflects her inherited Wesleyanism (PN 003-045)

- **Beatrice Sutton**: Active teacher in the Sunday-school, accustomed to its rhythms and responsibilities (PN 001-073)
- **Agnes Tellwright**: A scholar, representing the student body; her attendance anchors Anna's involvement in the school (PN 011-092)
- **Mrs. Sutton**: "A pillar of the Sunday-school" (PN 007-061); organiser of the Sunday-school stall at the Bazaar (PN 013-002)

## Educational and Social Context

The Sunday-school in the Five Towns served both religious and practical functions. For working-class children, it provided basic literacy and moral instruction in a society where formal schooling was not yet universal.

The institution's social reach extended beyond the classroom: home visitation (PN 004-002), teachers' meetings (PN 004-010), fundraisers, and bazaars created a dense web of social obligation and connection.

The school also reproduced class hierarchies. "Only in such an organisation as a Sunday-school could hide the difference between the classes" (PN 004-020) — yet the novel shows that difference persisting beneath the surface. The Suttons' carriage, the Tellwrights' property, and the Prices' declining fortunes all play out against the egalitarian pretensions of the Sunday-school community.

When Titus Price falls, the blow is not merely to one man's character but to the institution itself. The Sunday-school that had been the locus of shared piety becomes the scene of a crime, and the community must reckon with its own complicity in trusting appearances over realities.
