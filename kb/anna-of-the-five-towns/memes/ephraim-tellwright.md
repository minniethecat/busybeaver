# Ephraim Tellwright

The fabulously wealthy miser of Bursley, whose fortune -- estimated at over sixty thousand pounds -- is matched only by his meanness of spirit and his tyrannical control over his daughters.

## Character Overview

Ephraim Tellwright is "one of the most extraordinary and most mysterious men in the Five Towns" (PN 002-004). To the public, he is a legend -- immense wealth hidden behind a shabby brown suit and a six-roomed house.

To his daughters, he is a tyrant whose moods govern every aspect of existence: "that sinister and formidable individuality, whom her mind hated but her heart disobediently loved" (PN 002-004).

He is a portrait of avarice raised to the level of a life philosophy, a man whose entire existence is organised around accumulating and retaining money.

## Background and Circumstances

Tellwright was born in Hanbridge, son of a prominent Wesleyan who fought in the Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808 and the Fly-Sheets controversy of 1849 (PN 002-005).

He inherited a small fortune and, working as a potter's valuer, augmented it through property investments. By thirty he was worth twenty thousand pounds, living in lodgings on a hundred pounds a year (PN 002-004).

He married twice. His first wife bore Anna and died of erysipelas. His second wife, a young orphan thirty years his junior, bore Agnes and died of puerperal fever within the year (PN 002-004).

Tellwright was circuit treasurer, class-leader, and local preacher in Hanbridge. But "he was not, in fact, much smitten with either the doctrinal or the spiritual side of Methodism" (PN 002-005). His real interest was "the finance of salvation."

When religious work ceased to amuse him, he retired, caring "naught for public opinion" (PN 002-005). His move to Bursley was a silent retort to a silent rebuke from Hanbridge chapel.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Anna Tellwright]]**: The central oppressive relationship. He keeps her in domestic servitude on a pound a week and controls her fortune absolutely. His wrath, when provoked, keeps "an awful silence for days together" (PN 002-009).

- **[[Agnes Tellwright]]**: The younger daughter, treated with a slightly softer hand. "I am terribly angry, but I am not angry with you. However, behave yourself" (PN 011-220).

- **[[Henry Mynors]]**: Tellwright respects Mynors as a businessman but outmanoeuvres him, insisting on six percent interest (PN 004-054). He tolerates the courtship so long as it does not threaten his control.

- **[[Titus Price]]**: Tellwright sends Anna to collect overdue rent. When Price's embezzlement is discovered, he demands prosecution with full righteous anger (PN 011-168).

## Role in the Narrative

Tellwright is the central antagonist, his villainy emerging from character rather than plot. His weekly sovereign, his silent breakfasts, the terror of his displeasure create the conditions from which Anna must escape.

His investment of Anna's money in Mynors's works sets the plot in motion. At the climax, his demand that Willie Price be prosecuted precipitates Anna's defiance.

His fury is apocalyptic: "Shame on thee, Anna Tellwright! Shame on thee for a shameless hussy!" (PN 011-220). But this explosion marks the limit of his power. Anna has seen "the limit of his anger" and knows she can endure it (PN 011-220).

## Character Analysis

### Motivations

Tellwright's ruling passion is money -- not for what it buys but for its own sake. Even his religion was financial: "the finance of salvation" was where "he rose supreme" (PN 002-005).

His control over his daughters extends the same instinct: Anna is property, and her money is his to deploy.

### Arc and Development

Tellwright does not change; his stasis is the point. His "grotesque and ambiguous grin" is permanent, like the shabby suit. After Anna's defiance, he simply reroutes -- giving the sovereign to Agnes instead. Tyrants do not repent; they adapt.

### Thematic Significance

Tellwright embodies industrial capitalism stripped of justification: accumulation without purpose, power without benevolence, methodism without faith. He is a study in how the love of money displaces all other loves, leaving a man who "existed within himself, unrevealed" (PN 002-005).

## Critical Perspective

> Tellwright has been compared to literary misers from Moliere to Dickens, but Bennett's creation is distinguished by ordinariness. He is a recognisable product of nonconformist industrial culture, not a caricature.
