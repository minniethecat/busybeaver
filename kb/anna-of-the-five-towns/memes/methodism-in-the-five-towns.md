Methodism in the Five Towns is the dominant religious and social force shaping the community depicted in *Anna of the Five Towns*. The Wesleyan Methodist Society of Bursley forms the central institutional framework for the novel's characters.

## Historical Context

The Potteries were a stronghold of Wesleyan Methodism in the 19th century. The denomination's emphasis on personal piety, social discipline, and community organization resonated with the industrial working and middle classes of the region. John Wesley himself had preached in the Potteries, and the area became one of Methodism's heartlands.

## Role in the Novel

Methodism provides the moral vocabulary and social structure within which the novel's drama unfolds. The chapel, Sunday-school, Bible classes, and revival meetings are not merely background but the actual arenas in which character is formed and tested. Anna's spiritual crisis during the revival, Mynors's position as a Sunday-school superintendent, and Ephraim Tellwright's history as a local preacher all demonstrate how thoroughly Methodism penetrates the life of the Five Towns.

The tension between genuine religious feeling and social convention — between the Revivalist's emotional preaching and the comfortable respectability of the Wesleyan establishment — is one of the novel's central themes. Bennett portrays Methodism with sympathy and irony, recognizing both its genuine moral force and its entanglement with class, commerce, and social ambition.
