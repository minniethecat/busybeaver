# Wesleyan Methodist Society of Bursley

The Wesleyan Methodist Society of Bursley stands at the heart of religious, social, and economic life in Arnold Bennett's *Anna of the Five Towns*. More than a congregation, it is a network of institutions -- the chapel, the Sunday-school, the circuit system, the class-meeting, the Teachers' Meeting, and the Revival campaign -- that together form the moral architecture of [[bursley|Bursley]] society. The Society shapes the rhythms of the week, polices the boundaries of respectability, and provides the arena within which characters such as [[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]], [[titus-price|Titus Price]], and [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] enact their public selves. It is also the institution through which [[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]] awakens to love, conscience, and the gap between profession and conduct.

The Society belongs to the Wesleyan branch of [[wesleyan-methodism|Methodism]] -- the established, centrist stream, distinct from the more working-class Primitive Methodists who had seceded from the Bursley society in the historic disruption of 1808. As Bennett narrates, the Tellwright family belonged "by birth to the Old Guard of Methodism," and Ephraim Tellwright's father had "fought in the fight which preceded the famous Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808 at Bursley" (PN 002-005). This institutional lineage gives the Society an identity that is at once spiritual and deeply political, woven into the dynastic rivalries of the [[five-towns|Five Towns]].

## The Chapel and Circuit

The physical centre of the Society is the Wesleyan chapel on Trafalgar Road -- "the historic chapel," as Bennett calls it, a building whose architecture and seating arrangements encode the social hierarchies of the town (PN 005-015). On Sunday evenings, the [[five-towns|Five Towns]] stream towards its doors: "Trafalgar Road is the long thoroughfare which, under many aliases, runs through the Five Towns from end to end, uniting them as a river might unite them" (PN 002-002). The chapel stands in a quadrangle flanked by the school and lecture-hall, forming an enclosed civic-religious precinct at the centre of Bursley.

The circuit system gives the Society its organisational reach. The Bursley circuit is part of a wider connexional network, and the circuit treasurer -- a role once held by [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] during his years at Hanbridge -- manages the finances of the entire regional operation. Bennett describes Tellwright's tenure in terms that reveal the bureaucratic soul of Methodist governance: "It was in the finance of salvation that he rose supreme -- the interminable alternation of debt-raising and new liability which provides a lasting excitement for Nonconformists" (PN 002-005). The circuit is, in Tellwright's hands, a "going concern" -- a phrase that captures the uneasy fusion of gospel and bookkeeping at the Society's core.

The chapel's interior has its own geography of rank. The "orchestra" or "singing-seat" is where [[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]] sits as "an admired baritone" (PN 002-009). The pews are allocated by invisible but rigid precedence: the Suttons are among "only two families of the Bursley Wesleyan Methodists" who keep a carriage (PN 001-020). The chapel-keeper is a functionary of modest station but indispensable ritual function. And down in the body of the chapel, the congregation sits in graded proximity to the pulpit, the revivalists, and the preacher.

## The Sunday-School

The Sunday-school is the Society's engine of moral formation and intergenerational continuity. It is here that the novel's web of relationships is most tangibly woven. [[titus-price|Titus Price]] serves as Sunday-school superintendent, a position of immense local prestige; [[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]] is the morning superintendent, his natural domain of cheerful authority. [[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]] herself is a teacher, though she carries the burden of being "not converted" -- technically, as Bennett puts it, "a lost creature" (PN 004-020) (PN 004-020).

The Sunday-school's routines structure the week. The annual prize-giving, which opens the novel, takes place in the schoolroom: Agnes Tellwright receives her prize, and it is here that Anna first exchanges significant words with Mynors. The Teachers' Meeting -- convened in "a large classroom, furnished with wooden seats, a chair and a small table" whose walls display "a few Biblical cartoons depicting scenes in the life of Joseph and his brethren" (PN 004-019) -- becomes the setting for Mynors's charismatic leadership and the launching of the Revival campaign (PN 004-019). It is at this meeting that Mynors calls upon each teacher by name to pledge themselves to the home-visitation programme: "Will you, Miss Dickinson? Will you, Miss Machin? Will you, Mrs. Salt? Will you, Miss Tellwright?" (PN 004-023). His address to Anna, publicly but with intimate undertones, marks a turning point in their relationship.

The irony that pervades the Sunday-school is captured in Anna's visit to Price's manufactory in Edward Street. Confronted with Titus Price's squalid workspace and financial dishonesty, she reflects: "The image of the sleek and stout superintendent of the Sunday-school, arrayed in his rich, almost voluptuous, broadcloth, offended her profoundly. That he, debtor and promise-breaker, should have the effrontery to pray for the souls of children, to chastise their petty furtive crimes, was nearly incredible" (PN 003-077). The Sunday-school is thus a stage on which the novel's central drama -- the collision between public piety and private conduct -- is performed.

## The Revival and the Revivalist

A major plot arc of the novel is organised around the Revival -- "a revival of godliness and Christian grace" -- undertaken by the Society with the engagement of a celebrated specialist revivalist (PN 004-002). The Revival structures the novel's spiritual geography: the town is "mapped out into districts" and canvassed "as at an election," revealing "the basis of individual diplomacy and solicitation" beneath "the broad religious structure of the enterprise" (PN 004-023). Mynors speaks of it as a campaign: "The affair was a campaign before it was a prostration at the Throne of Grace" (PN 004-023).

The revivalist himself is a figure of almost mythic dimensions. It is "got about that the preacher who had been engaged, a specialist in revivals, was a man of miraculous powers: the number of souls which he had snatched from eternal torment was precisely stated, and it amounted to tens of thousands. He played the cornet to the glory of God, and his cornet was of silver" (PN 004-002). Ephraim Tellwright, with characteristic sardonic detachment, calls him "th' old trumpeter" who "will turn the town upside down" (PN 004-004).

The Revival services themselves are intense affairs (PN 005-015). The historic chapel is "crowded almost in every part" (PN 005-015). The atmosphere becomes electric. A worshipper cries out: "Let us drive the devil out of this chapel" (PN 005-023). Yet the Revival's spiritual power coexists with, and is perhaps undermined by, its organisational machinery -- the canvassing, the district-mapping, the recruitment of teachers -- exposing Bennett's characteristic interest in the tensions between evangelical fervour and institutional management.

## Social Control and Moral Authority

The Wesleyan Methodist Society functions as Bursley's primary mechanism of social control. It polices the boundaries of permissible behaviour through a dense system of mutual surveillance: "The worst tyrannies of her father never dulled the sense of her duty to him" (PN 002-009), Anna reflects, and this sense of duty is inseparable from the Methodist ethos in which she has been raised. The Society's moral authority extends beyond the chapel walls into domestic life, business dealings, and courtship.

Bennett illustrates the Society's regulatory power most vividly through the figure of [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]]. Tellwright's service as circuit treasurer, class-leader, and local preacher "gave him an unassailable position within the central group of the society" (PN 002-005) -- a position he values not for its spiritual rewards but for its social immunity. When he loses interest in religious work and retires, the circuit is "astounded by this sudden defection" (PN 002-007); his withdrawal is "an inexplicable fall from grace" only because the Society cannot conceive of participation as anything other than a marker of standing.

The Society also regulates gender and courtship. Anna's relationship with [[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]] unfolds almost entirely within the institutional framework of the chapel and Sunday-school: their conversations occur after services, at Teachers' Meetings, en route to and from the chapel gates. Mynors himself -- "a pattern and exemplar of seemliness" whose "abandonment of habit and discretion at the bidding of a desire" shocks Anna -- is defined by his perfect conformity to the Society's expectations (PN 002-009). The chapel is not merely the setting for their romance; it is the condition of its possibility and the guarantor of its respectability.

## Real-World Context

The Wesleyan Methodist Society of Bursley is modelled closely on the real Wesleyan Methodist presence in the Staffordshire Potteries during the 19th century. Methodism was the dominant religious force in Stoke-on-Trent, and the Wesleyan branch was the largest and most established. The Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808, to which Bennett alludes through the Tellwright family history, was a genuine historical event: Primitive Methodism split from the Wesleyans over the use of camp meetings and a more populist, emotional style of worship, becoming especially strong among working-class communities in the Potteries.

The Warren affrays of 1828 and the Fly-Sheets controversy of 1849 -- both referenced in Bennett's description of the Tellwright family's Methodist pedigree -- were real schisms within Wesleyan Methodism. The Warren controversy centred on the autonomy of the Manchester district and the role of the laity in church governance. The Fly-Sheets affair involved anonymous pamphlets attacking the leadership of Jabez Bunting and the centralising tendencies of the Wesleyan Connexion. These disputes together lost Methodism a hundred thousand members, as Bennett notes (PN 002-005). By anchoring the Tellwright family in this history of doctrinal warfare, Bennett positions the Bursley Society within a broader narrative of Protestant institutional politics.

In the novel, the Society's financial operations -- the circuit accounts, the debt-raising, the quarterly rental of pews, the anniversary services with their chamois-leather moneybags -- reflect the real economic infrastructure of Victorian Nonconformity. Methodism was a property-owning, capital-raising enterprise as much as a spiritual movement; Bennett's portrayal of [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]] as a man for whom "the circuit was a 'going concern'" captures precisely this fusion of faith and finance.
