# Hanbridge

The largest of the Five Towns of the Potteries, Hanbridge is the commercial and demographic rival of Bursley. It is the original home of the Tellwright family, the seat of a prominent Wesleyan Methodist circuit, and the residence of the Sutton family. In Bennett's geographical scheme, it represents the triumph of scale — a larger, brasher, more commercial counterpart to Bursley's ancient identity.

## Overview

Hanbridge is one of the Five Towns — "Turnhill, Bursley, Hanbridge, Knype, and Longshaw" (PN 001-050) — that form the fictionalised Potteries in Arnold Bennett's *Anna of the Five Towns*. Bennett names it explicitly as the largest: "of these five Bursley is the mother, but Hanbridge is the largest" (PN 001-050). This single sentence establishes the rivalry that suffuses the novel's municipal consciousness — Bursley has antiquity and maternity; Hanbridge has size and commercial muscle.

The town's character is industrial and unapologetic. Bennett describes the Five Towns collectively as "mean and forbidding of aspect — sombre, hard-featured, uncouth; and the vaporous poison of their ovens and chimneys has soiled and shrivelled the surrounding country till there is no village lane within a league but what offers a gaunt and ludicrous travesty of rural charms" (PN 001-050). Hanbridge, as the largest town, stands as the exemplary specimen of this industrial sublime — a place where "the vast Doing which goes forward below" (PN 001-050) is most fully realised.

## Location and Geography

Hanbridge borders Bursley, and the two towns are the most prominently featured in the narrative. From the Moorthorne ridge, one has a panoramic view taking in both Hanbridge and Bursley, with Hillport to the west (PN 003-099). The town's far-stretching borders mark the transition from the rural approaches into the industrial heartland of the Potteries (PN 002-002). The "gold angel of the town-hall gleamed in the evening light" (PN 014-022) — a civic landmark visible across the valley.

## The Tellwrights' Hanbridge Origins

Hanbridge is central to the Tellwright family history. Ephraim Tellwright was "a native of Hanbridge" (PN 002-004), born into a Bursley family that had been prominent in the Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808. His father had "fought in the fight which preceded the famous Primitive Methodist Secession of 1808 at Bursley, and had also borne a notable part in the Warren affrays of '28, and the disastrous trouble of the Fly-Sheets in '49, when Methodism lost a hundred thousand members" (PN 002-004).

After inheriting a small fortune from his father and building his wealth through property investment, Tellwright lived "in lodgings on a total expenditure of about a hundred a year" until his sudden marriage at thirty-five to "the daughter of a wood merchant at Oldcastle" (PN 002-004). The couple "lived narrowly in a small house up at Pireford, between Hanbridge and Oldcastle. They visited no one, and were never seen together except on Sundays" (PN 002-004). Anna was born during this Hanbridge period.

Tellwright was, for many years, "what is termed 'a good Wesleyan,' preaching and teaching, and spending himself in the various activities of Hanbridge chapel. For many years he had been circuit treasurer" (PN 002-004). His religious work was administrative rather than spiritual — "in the finance of salvation that he rose supreme — the interminable alternation of debt-raising and new liability which provides a lasting excitement for Nonconformists" (PN 002-004). "To him the circuit was a 'going concern,' and he kept it in motion, serving the Lord in committee and over statements of account" (PN 002-004).

His eventual departure from Hanbridge to Bursley was loaded with unspoken tensions. Having "lost interest in his religious avocations; they had ceased to amuse him, the old ardour had cooled" (PN 002-004), Tellwright "purchased a lot of cottage property in Bursley" and migrated. Bennett notes that "perhaps the chief [reason] was that he found the atmosphere of Hanbridge Wesleyan chapel rather uncongenial. The exodus from it was his silent and malicious retort to a silent rebuke" (PN 002-004). The Hanbridge circuit, Bennett adds, was "astounded by this sudden defection of a class-leader, a local preacher, and an officer. It was an inexplicable fall from grace" (PN 002-004).

## Religious and Civic Life

The Hanbridge Methodist circuit is central to the religious life of the district. Henry Mynors, a leading figure in the community, was intimately connected with Hanbridge chapel activities. Mr. Banks, the revivalist, pointedly addressed Tellwright: "You used to be a pillar of the Hanbridge circuit — was it not so?" (PN 011-216) — a question that carried the weight of an accusation.

Hanbridge also features as a commercial centre. Bostock's "great shop at Hanbridge" (PN 007-026) supplied the latest fashions, and Brunt's at Hanbridge was "quite the fashion to go there now" (PN 007-035) for important purchases. The town was a destination for Bursley residents seeking goods and services beyond their own town's offerings.

## Social and Romantic Associations

The comparison between Bursley and Hanbridge forms part of the social texture of the novel. When Henry Mynors first converses with Anna at the Park, he asks "And how does Bursley compare with Hanbridge?" (PN 001-045), a question that reveals the competitive municipal consciousness of the Five Towns. Anna's hesitation in answering reflects both her provincial narrowness and her acute awareness of the social dynamics at play.

Mrs. Sutton, a resident of Hanbridge, was a figure of middle-aged admiration across both towns: "There was scarcely a middle-aged male Wesleyan in Bursley and Hanbridge who had not a peculiar regard for Mrs. Sutton, and who did not think that he alone truly appreciated her" (PN 010-043) — including, remarkably, Ephraim Tellwright himself.

In Anna's final moments of reflection on the Isle of Man, "her thoughts went back to Hanbridge and Bursley and her life there; and all the remembered scenes, bathed in the glow of a new ideal, seemed to lose their pain" (PN 012-070). Hanbridge endures in her memory as one of the twin poles of her existence, inseparable from Bursley and all that the two towns have meant.
