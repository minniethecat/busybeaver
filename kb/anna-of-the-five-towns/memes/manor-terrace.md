# Manor Terrace

Manor Terrace is the Tellwright family home in [[bursley|Bursley]], where [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]], his daughters [[anna-tellwright|Anna]] and [[agnes-tellwright|Agnes]], and their household live in an atmosphere of calculated parsimony. The name, Bennett notes, is "a last reminder of the rural past" (PN 002-002) in a landscape now dominated by industry.

## Overview

The Tellwright household at Manor Terrace is a six-roomed dwelling whose material conditions reflect Ephraim Tellwright's obsessive frugality. The house, of light yellow brick, was two-storied, with a long narrow garden behind, and the rent was thirty pounds a year -- modest even by Bursley standards. While the family possesses a fortune of fifty thousand pounds, "three people existed on a pound a week" (PN 010-139) at Manor Terrace. The house becomes a central stage for the novel's domestic scenes: Mynors's weekly visits, the family meals, and the tense encounters between father and daughter that define Anna's constrained life.

The interior of Manor Terrace reflects the household regime. The front parlour with its bay window is Anna's accustomed place on Sunday evenings, where she watches the world pass down [[trafalgar-road|Trafalgar Road]]. The domestic economy is managed by Anna alone, who keeps house for her father and sister with a competence that masks the emotional poverty of the arrangement. The "atmosphere of Manor Terrace afflicted" (PN 009-002) even those who visited -- a gloom that Ephraim himself had created yet remained incapable of recognizing as his own creation.

## Location and Geography

Manor Terrace is situated in [[bursley|Bursley]], its name evoking a pastoral past long since overtaken by potbanks and collieries. The house is described as a modest terrace dwelling -- Bennett notes the "surroundings of the six-roomed abode in Manor Terrace gave food for reflection" (PN 002-006) on the contrast between Tellwright's wealth and his living standard. The house is within walking distance of both the [[bursley-wesleyan-chapel|Wesleyan chapel]] on [[trafalgar-road|Trafalgar Road]] and the industrial quarter of [[edward-street|Edward Street]].

Exactly opposite Manor Terrace stood "an antique red mansion, standing back in its own ground -- home of the Mynors family for two generations, but now a school" (PN 002-002). Higher up on the opposite side came "an imposing row of four new houses, said to be the best planned and best built in the town" (PN 002-002), the nearest of which belonged to Councillor Sutton, valued at sixty pounds a year. This geography of housing -- the Tellwrights at thirty pounds, the Suttons at sixty, the Mynors mansion now a school -- encodes the social hierarchy of Bursley into the very street.

## Significance in the Novel

Manor Terrace embodies the central dramatic tension of the novel: immense wealth imprisoned within a deliberately impoverished domestic setting. While [[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim]] owns properties across the [[five-towns|Five Towns]], he chooses to live at the level of a labourer. The house becomes a symbol of Anna's confinement. [[henry-mynors|Mynors]] "called at Manor Terrace once a week, never on the same day" (PN 010-002), his calculated courtship unfolding in this cramped domestic theatre. The silence with which Anna and Mynors "walked back to Manor Terrace" (PN 011-202) after critical moments underscores the house's role as a place where words are suppressed.

## Associated Characters

- **[[anna-tellwright|Anna Tellwright]]**: The daughter who keeps house at Manor Terrace and whose emotional life is constrained within its walls.
- **[[ephraim-tellwright|Ephraim Tellwright]]**: The "shrewd miser of Manor Terrace" (PN 005-013) who dominates the household with his economic tyranny.
- **[[agnes-tellwright|Agnes Tellwright]]**: Anna's younger half-sister, also living under their father's regime at Manor Terrace.
- **[[henry-mynors|Henry Mynors]]**: Suitor to Anna, who "arrived at Manor Terrace on the next Saturday afternoon" (PN 012-060) for his regular visits during courtship.

## Real-World Context

Manor Terrace corresponds to a terrace house in Burslem (Bursley). In the late-Victorian Potteries, even wealthy industrialists and property owners might live in relatively modest terraced houses, though Tellwright's thrift is extreme even by local standards. Bennett uses the contrast between wealth and living conditions to expose the spiritual poverty at the heart of Five Towns materialism.
