# Mr. Lovatt

Richard Lovatt is the manager of the bank in Bursley, described as "probably the most influential man in Bursley." He appears in Chapter 3 when Anna Tellwright, at her father's instruction, comes to sign documents related to her inheritance and open her bank account.

## Character Overview

Mr. Lovatt is the gatekeeper of Bursley's financial establishment — a formal, businesslike figure who greets Anna with "a formal and rather fatigued politeness" [003-088]. His office, with its large leather armchair, large table, and large open book, conveys institutional solidity and the weight of commercial authority [003-088]. His manner is "high-pitched" and "perfunctory" [003-089], suggesting a man performing a well-worn professional script rather than exhibiting any genuine warmth.

## Role in the Narrative

Lovatt's single-scene appearance serves a crucial narrative function: it is through him that Anna first confronts the reality of her wealth. He hands her a chequebook, a deposit-book, and a passbook and informs her: "There is already a credit of four hundred and twenty pounds in your favour" [003-092] — a sum that astounds her yet leaves her unable to claim even the modest amount of ready money she desires without her father's consent [003-093].

Ephraim Tellwright refers to him familiarly as "Mester Lovatt," signaling a long-standing business relationship between the two men [003-052].

## Background & Circumstances

Lovatt wields significant influence in Bursley — the narrative flatly states he is "probably the most influential man in Bursley" [003-089]. As bank manager, he sits at the intersection of commerce and community power. The formal trappings of his office — the frosted glass door painted "Manager," the oversized furniture, the leather appointments — all reinforce his position within the town's economic hierarchy.

## Character Analysis

### The Face of Institutional Power

Lovatt represents the impersonal machinery of finance in the novel. His interaction with Anna encapsulates a central irony of her situation: she is legally wealthy yet practically powerless. Lovatt explains the banking instruments to her "in simple language" [003-093], but the explanation does nothing to bridge the gap between her technical ownership of capital and her complete lack of agency over it.

### Relationship to Ephraim Tellwright

The relationship between Lovatt and Ephraim Tellwright is one of mutual professional respect between men of commercial standing. Tellwright sends Anna to Lovatt as a matter of routine business — the transfer of property and the formalisation of accounts — and Lovatt carries out the instructions with institutional efficiency, not questioning the arrangement that leaves a twenty-one-year-old heiress so entirely under her father's control.
