# Isle of Man

The Isle of Man is a holiday destination that figures centrally in the latter half of *Anna of the Five Towns*. The Sutton family's annual excursion there, and Anna Tellwright's invitation to join them, becomes a symbol of escape, aspiration, and the possibility of a life beyond the industrial constraints of the Five Towns.

## The Invitation

The Isle of Man is first mentioned at Mrs. Sutton's sewing-meeting. Mrs. Sutton, having observed that Anna is "looking pale," says to her: "You want a holiday. We shall be going to the Isle of Man in August or September. Would your father let you come with us?"[PN=007-130]. This invitation, extended with characteristic maternal warmth, sets in motion a sequence of events that will transform Anna's life.

Mrs. Sutton later visits Manor Terrace to secure Ephraim Tellwright's formal consent. She tells Anna: "Mr. Sutton and myself and Beatrice are going to the Isle of Man soon for a fortnight or so, and we should very much like you to come with us"[PN=009-096]. It is also revealed that "Henry Mynors will most likely come with us to the Isle of Man"[PN=009-118], a detail that adds a layer of romantic possibility to the trip.

For Anna, the prospect of the journey becomes an obsession. Bennett writes: "The future, then, meant the Isle of Man. Anna dreamed of an enchanted isle and hours of unimaginable rapture"[PN=010-003].

## The Journey

The departure from Bursley is a moment of high emotion. Agnes, Anna's younger sister, accompanies the party to the station. "Agnes, far more excited than any of the rest, seized her straw hat, and slipping the elastic under her small chin, sprang into the cab, and found a haven between Mr. Sutton's short, fat legs"[PN=010-049]. The journey takes them "down the first hill, up Duck Bank, through the marketplace, and down the steep declivity of Oldcastle Street" to the station[PN=010-049]. Mynors, "admirably attired as a tourist," has secured tickets and a private compartment in the through-coach for Liverpool[PN=010-049].

The crossing from Liverpool brings the first sight of the island. "That is the Isle of Man," someone announces, and the moment is freighted with significance[PN=010-089].

## Port Erin

The Suttons go to Port Erin, "a lovely little fishing village" where they have established annual traditions[PN=010-010]. Beatrice explains: "All the fishermen know us there. Last year Henry hired a yacht for the fortnight, and we all went mackerel-fishing, every day; except sometimes Pa. Now and then Pa had a tendency to go fiddling in caves and things"[PN=010-010].

The party stays at familiar lodgings. Upon arrival, "a fire was burning in the parlour" and through the window "Anna had a glimpse of a deeply indented bay at the foot of cliffs below them, with a bold headland to the right. Fishing vessels with flat red sails seemed to hang undecided just outside the bay. From cottage chimneys beneath the road blue smoke softly ascended"[PN=010-098].

The daily rhythm of the holiday is leisurely. Mrs. Sutton "devoted herself to the attainment of absolute quiescence. She had come for a rest, and she achieved her purpose"[PN=010-140]. The Alderman, as noted, makes "an interesting companion, full of facts about strata, outcrops, and breaks," his sole weakness being "the habit of quoting extremely sentimental scraps of verse when walking by the seashore"[PN=010-140]. Anna "frankly enjoyed Anna's attention to him, and took pride in her society"[PN=010-140].

Beatrice, the "changing quantity in the domestic equation," has "frequent fits of petulance, particularly with Mynors; but her energy and spirits atoned well for these"[PN=010-140].

## Daily Life

The household economy of the holiday reveals the gulf between the Suttons' world and the Tellwrights'. Mrs. Sutton's food purchases seem "startlingly profuse to Anna, but gradually she became accustomed to the scale, and to the amazing habit of always buying the very best of everything, from beefsteak to grapes." Anna calculates "that the housekeeping could not cost less than six pounds a week for the five. At Manor Terrace three people existed on a pound"[PN=010-058].

A fishing expedition on Tom Kelly's yacht provides a set-piece of holiday camaraderie. The Alderman lies "on the foredeck, placidly smoking" while the others attempt to fish[PN=010-069]. They visit Tom Kelly's cabin, "partly excavated out of the rock behind the little quay," where the old fisherman "remarked with severity on their pallid faces, and insisted that a change of complexion must be brought about"[PN=010-058].

In the afternoons, "the Alderman and his wife slept as usual, Mr. Sutton with a bandana handkerchief over his face. The rest went out immediately; the invitation of the sun and the sea was far too persuasive to be resisted"[PN=010-166].

## Beatrice's Illness

The holiday takes an unexpected turn when Beatrice falls ill with influenza. Her temperature rises to 103 degrees, and the doctor diagnoses "a case of influenza, severe, demanding very careful treatment"[PN=010-202]. The household reorganises around the sickroom: "The sickroom became a mysterious centre round which everything revolved, and the parlour, without the alteration of a single chair, took on a deserted, forlorn appearance"[PN=010-202].

Anna rises to the occasion as nurse, and her competence during the crisis earns her the deep gratitude of the Suttons. She reflects that "she had been of real value to her friends in the last few days, had probably saved Mrs. Sutton from illness, had certainly laid them all under an obligation"[PN=010-220]. The illness also interrupts the developing romance between Anna and Mynors, creating a period of emotional suspension.

## The Engagement

Despite — or perhaps because of — the disruption caused by Beatrice's illness, the holiday culminates in Mynors's proposal to Anna. They leave Port Erin early, at half-past six in the morning to catch the Liverpool steamer at Douglas. The Alderman and his wife "waved Godspeed from the doorstep"[PN=010-234]. The day of their return journey is described as "an ecstasy, a bright expanse unstained" — for Anna, "a unique day, marking the apogee of her existence"[PN=010-234].

## Symbolic Significance

The Isle of Man functions in the novel as a geographical counterpoint to the Five Towns. It is a place of sea and sky rather than kilns and chimneys; of leisure rather than industry; of emotional expansiveness rather than the emotional constriction that characterises life under Ephraim Tellwright's roof. Anna's journey there marks her first real departure from the world of her father, and the engagement that takes place there seals her passage into a new life.

The island is also associated with class mobility. The ease and generosity of the Suttons' holiday spending — six pounds a week for food alone — contrasts starkly with the Tellwrights' domestic parsimony. Through her association with the Suttons and her engagement to Mynors, Anna is absorbed into a different economic order.

Religion, notably, is almost entirely absent from the holiday. The narrator observes that Mrs. Sutton's brief prayer during Beatrice's illness was "the first and only word of religion, except grace at table, that Anna heard from the Suttons during her stay in the Isle of Man"[PN=010-220]. The holiday represents a temporary liberation from the vigilant piety that governs life in Bursley.
