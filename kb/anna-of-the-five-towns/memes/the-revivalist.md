The Revivalist is an unnamed preacher who conducts a series of revival meetings in Bursley, profoundly affecting the religious life of the community and triggering a spiritual crisis in Anna Tellwright.

## Description and Methods

The Revivalist is distinguished by his use of a silver cornet to draw crowds and his emotionally charged preaching style. He arrives in Bursley as part of the broader Methodist revival movement that swept through industrial England in the 19th century. His meetings are held in the Wesleyan chapel and attract large, fervent congregations.

## Impact on Anna

The Revivalist's preaching has a powerful effect on Anna. His vivid descriptions of sin and damnation awaken in her a deep sense of spiritual inadequacy and fear. She becomes convinced of her own unworthiness and undergoes an intense period of religious anxiety (004-002 through 005-031).

## Narrative Significance

As an unnamed, charismatic figure, the Revivalist functions as a catalyst rather than a developed character. His role is to precipitate Anna's spiritual crisis — one of the novel's central psychological developments. Through this crisis, Bennett explores the tension between Methodism's emotional revivalism and the more restrained, established forms of worship represented by characters like Henry Mynors. The Revivalist embodies the disruptive, transformative power of popular evangelical religion in Victorian England.
