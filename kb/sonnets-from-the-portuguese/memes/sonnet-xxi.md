## Overview

Sonnet XXI of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is a plea for repetition. The speaker asks the be[[love-theme|love]]d to say "thou dost [[love-theme|love]] me" over and over, deflecting the charge of monotony with the analogy of the cuckoo's spring [[music-theme|song]] — each iteration is not wearisome but welcome, the sound of a returning season.

## Full Text

> Say over again, and yet once over again [021-001]
> That thou dost love me. Though the word repeated [021-002]
> Should seem a "cuckoo-song," as thou dost treat it, [021-003]
> Remember, never to the hill or plain, [021-004]
> Valley and wood, without her cuckoo-strain [021-005]
> Comes the fresh Spring in all her green completed! [021-006]
> Beloved, I, amid the darkness greeted [021-007]
> By a doubtful spirit-voice, in that doubt's pain [021-008]
> Cry, "Speak once more—thou lovest!" Who can fear [021-009]
> Too many stars, though each in heaven shall roll, [021-010]
> Too many flowers, though each shall crown the year? [021-011]
> Say thou dost love me, love me, love me—toll [021-012]
> The silver iterance!—only minding, Dear, [021-013]
> To love me also in silence with thy soul. [021-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXI [021-001]–[021-014]

## Analysis

### The Plea for Repetition

The sonnet opens with an imperative that is also a plea: "Say over again, and yet once over again / That thou dost [[love-theme|love]] me" [021-001]–[021-002]. The phrasing is deliberately excessive — "over again, and yet once over again" — performing the repetition it requests. The be[[love-theme|love]]d has apparently dismissed his own declarations as a "cuckoo-[[music-theme|song]]" [021-003], a term suggesting monotonous repetition (the cuckoo's two-note call is one of the simplest in nature). But the speaker revalues the comparison: the cuckoo's [[music-theme|song]] is not tedious but seasonal, each iteration a sign of returning spring.

### The Cuckoo Argument

The speaker's counter-argument occupies the remainder of the octave: "never to the hill or plain, / Valley and wood, without her cuckoo-strain / Comes the fresh Spring in all her green completed!" [021-004]–[021-006]. The logic is that the cuckoo's repetition does not diminish the spring but announces it — each "cuckoo" is a fresh declaration of seasonal renewal. By analogy, each repetition of "thou dost [[love-theme|love]] me" is not a worn-out echo but a new proof of [[love-theme|love]]'s continuing reality.

This is a characteristically [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] move: to take an apparent weakness (monotony, repetition) and transform it into a strength through recontextualisation. What the be[[love-theme|love]]d sees as a flaw — the need to hear "I [[love-theme|love]] you" repeatedly — the speaker reframes as the natural rhythm of [[love-theme|love]] itself.

### The Doubtful Spirit-Voice

The sestet shifts from argument to autobiography: "Be[[love-theme|love]]d, I, amid the darkness greeted / By a doubtful [[soul-theme|spirit]]-voice, in that doubt's pain / Cry, 'Speak once more—thou [[love-theme|love]]st!'" [021-007]–[021-009]. The "doubtful [[soul-theme|spirit]]-voice" is a haunting phrase — it suggests that the speaker has been visited by voices she cannot trust, perhaps the voices of depression, illness, or self-doubt that characterised her years of seclusion. Against these "doubtful" voices, the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice is a counter-spell: "Speak once more—thou [[love-theme|love]]st!"

### Stars and Flowers

The rhetorical questions that follow — "Who can fear / Too many stars, though each in heaven shall roll, / Too many flowers, though each shall crown the year?" [021-009]–[021-011] — extend the natural analogy. Stars and flowers are not feared for their abundance; each is individually beautiful and collectively glorious. Love's declarations should be received in the same [[soul-theme|spirit]].

### The Silver Iterance

The poem's climax is the triple repetition: "Say thou dost [[love-theme|love]] me, [[love-theme|love]] me, [[love-theme|love]] me—toll / The silver iterance!" [021-012]–[021-013]. The word "toll" suggests a bell — the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice is figured as a church bell ringing out a repeated note. "Silver iterance" is one of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s most beautiful coinages: "iterance" (iteration, repetition) modified by "silver," the metal of clarity, preciousness, and endurance.

### Love in Silence

The poem closes with a paradox: "only minding, Dear, / To [[love-theme|love]] me also in [[silence]] with thy [[soul-theme|soul]]" [021-013]–[021-014]. After fourteen lines of begging the be[[love-theme|love]]d to speak, the speaker acknowledges the final inadequacy of speech. Words are necessary but not sufficient; they must be grounded in the [[silence|silent]] [[love-theme|love]] of the [[soul-theme|soul]]. The sonnet that begins by demanding repetition ends by honoring [[silence]].

## Literary Context

Sonnet XXI engages with a venerable poetic problem: can language ever be adequate to [[love-theme|love]]? The *trobar clus* (closed style) of the troubadours deliberately made [[love-theme|love]] poetry obscure to protect its mysteries; the Petrarchan tradition oscillated between rhetorical elaboration and claims of inexpressibility. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] takes a middle path: language is necessary but insufficient, and its insufficiency is itself the subject of the poem.

The cuckoo as a figure of spring renewal has a long history in English poetry, most famously in the medieval lyric "Sumer is icumen in / Lhude sing cuccu!" [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s use of the cuckoo is more argumentative: it is not merely a seasonal marker but an analogy for [[love-theme|love]]'s need for renewal through repetition.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XXI uses ABBA ABBA CDCDCD. The octave's enclosed rhymes create a self-contained argumentative space, while the sestet's interlocking tercets open outward into the stars-and-flowers imagery.

Key prosodic moments:
- The opening line's excessive length — "Say over again, and yet once over again" [021-001] — performs the repetition it requests
- "cuckoo-[[music-theme|song]]" [021-003] and "cuckoo-strain" [021-005] bracket the octave's central argument with sonic echoes
- The triple "[[love-theme|love]] me, [[love-theme|love]] me, [[love-theme|love]] me" [021-012] is the poem's emotional core, a spoken incantation
- "toll / The silver iterance!" [021-012]–[021-013] uses enjambment to separate the verb from its object, mimicking the bell's pause between strokes

## Themes

### Repetition as Renewal

The poem's central argument is that repetition is not depletion but renewal. Each "I [[love-theme|love]] you" is not a worn copy of the first but a new event, as each cuckoo-call is not an echo but a fresh announcement of spring. This is a defence of [[love-theme|love]]'s need for constant verbal reassurance — and, by extension, a defence of [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]]'s own repetitive structure.

### Voice and Doubt

The "doubtful [[soul-theme|spirit]]-voice" [021-008] is a figure for the interior voices that undermine the speaker's confidence. Against these, the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice is a reality principle — a sound from outside the self that confirms the self's worth. The poem is thus also a document of the speaker's ongoing struggle with self-doubt.
