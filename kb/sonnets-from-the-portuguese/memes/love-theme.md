# Love (Theme)

## Definition

Love is the central and unifying theme of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850). The sequence traces an autobiographical arc — from the speaker's initial disbelief that she could be loved, through surrender and reciprocal devotion, to a [[dream|vision]] of love that transcends [[death-theme|mortality]]. The love portrayed is not merely romantic but encompasses [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transformation, creative inspiration, [[grief-theme|grief]], and a redefinition of selfhood through the beloved.

The sonnets draw on the Petrarchan tradition — in which the beloved is idealized and often unattainable — but subvert it: [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s speaker moves from passive longing to active, reciprocal love, culminating in [[sonnet-xliii|Sonnet 43]]'s declaration that love is measured not by loss but by fullness ("I love thee to the depth and breadth and height / My [[soul-theme|soul]] can reach" [043-001]).

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

Love pervades the entire sequence, appearing in over 25 of the 44 sonnets. The following selected passages trace its development.

### Early Sonnets: Love as Transformation and Awakening

The sequence opens with the speaker's astonishment at being loved. In [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]], the beloved's call transforms [[death-theme|death]] into love: "Guess now who holds thee!—'Death!' I said. But there, / The silver answer rang—'Not Death, but Love'" [001-013]. This pivotal moment establishes love as the force that rescues the speaker from solitude and [[death-theme|mortality]].

[[sonnet-vii|Sonnet VII]] traces the speaker's awakening to love as a transformative education: she "was caught up into love, and taught the whole / Of life in a new rhythm" [007-006]. Love here is not merely an emotion but a fundamental reordering of perception and being. The sonnet's closing line acknowledges the suddenness of this change — "this lute and [[music-theme|song]] loved yesterday" [007-012] — contrasting the speaker's past isolation with present fullness.

### The Nature of Love: Sonnets IX–X

[[sonnet-ix|Sonnet IX]] grapples with the speaker's unworthiness and the paradox of receiving love she feels she cannot reciprocate: "So to be lovers; and I own and grieve / That givers of such gifts as mine are, must / Be counted with the ungenerous" [009-008]. The closing lines assert the exclusivity and totality of her devotion: "Beloved, I only love thee! let it pass" [009-014].

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]] contains the sequence's most concentrated meditation on the nature of love. The poem opens with a declaration — "Yet, love, mere love, is beautiful indeed / And worthy of acceptation" [010-001] — and proceeds through a series of definitions: "And love is fire" [010-005]. The central lines dramatize love's power to transform the lover's perception: "I love thee—mark!—I love thee—in thy sight / I stand transfigured" [010-006]. The sonnet closes with the paradox that love at its most humble is love at its most powerful: "In love, when love the lowest: meanest creatures / Who love [[god|God]], [[god|God]] accepts" [010-010].

### Love as Reciprocal Union: Sonnets XXI–XXII

In the middle sonnets, love matures into a [[dream|vision]] of [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual union. [[sonnet-xxi|Sonnet XXI]] describes a love so intimate it needs no words: the speaker asks the beloved to "love me also in [[silence]] with thy [[soul-theme|soul]]" [021-014]. This [[silence|silent]] communion prefigures the imagery of [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]], where the lovers' [[soul-theme|soul]]s "stand up erect and strong" [022-001] as "isolate pure [[soul-theme|spirit]]s" [022-012] — a [[dream|vision]] of love as the union of two complete beings rather than the absorption of one into the other.

### Love, Grief, and Mortality: Sonnets XXXV–XXXVIII

The later sonnets confront the [[shadow]] side of love — [[grief-theme|grief]], fear, and [[death-theme|mortality]]. [[sonnet-xxxv|Sonnet XXXV]] treats [[grief-theme|grief]] as love's inseparable companion: "For [[grief-theme|grief]] indeed is love and [[grief-theme|grief]] beside" [035-011]. The speaker's vulnerability is raw: "I have grieved so I am hard to love — / Yet love me — wilt thou?" [035-012]. This passage acknowledges that love, once given, exposes the lover to loss.

[[sonnet-xxxvi|Sonnet XXXVI]]II uses sacra[[thought-imagery|mental imagery]]: love's "chrism" [038-010] anoints and consecrates the beloved, transforming earthly affection into something sacred. The final line — "My love, my own" [038-014] — completes the movement from doubt to possession.

### The Culmination: Sonnets XLIII–XLIV

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII ("How do I love thee? [[sonnet-xliii|Let me count the ways]]") is the sequence's rhetorical and emotional climax. The anaphoric "I love thee" [043-001] enumerated across eight dimensions — to the "depth and breadth and height" of the [[soul-theme|soul]] [043-002], with the "passion put to use / In my old [[grief-theme|grief]]s" [043-009], and finally "with the breath, / Smiles, [[tear-imagery|tears]], of all my life" [043-012] — defines love as the totality of lived experience. The closing line — "I shall but love thee better after [[death-theme|death]]" [043-014] — insists that love transcends [[death-theme|mortality]], fulfilling the promise made in the opening sonnet.

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIV brings the sequence full circle. The speaker addresses the beloved as the source of all her poetic flowers: "Beloved, thou hast brought me many flowers" [044-001]. The final image — roots left in the beloved's soil — suggests that love is the ground from which all creative and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual life grows [044-005].

## Literary Context

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s sonnets intervene in the Petrarchan love-sonnet tradition, which conventionally positions the male poet as supplicant and the female beloved as distant, [[silence|silent]] object. By adopting the female speaker's voice — one that moves from passive gratitude to active, reciprocal desire — the sequence subverts the tradition's gendered power dynamics. The autobiographical dimension (the poems chronicle [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]], 1845–1846) adds a layer of lived experience unusual in the sonnet tradition.

The sequence also participates in the Victorian preoccupation with the relationship between earthly and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual love. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s love is neither purely sensuous nor purely abstract: it is materially embodied ("I love thee to the level of everyday's / Most quiet need" [043-005]) yet ascends toward the divine. This synthesis of the physical and the transcendent reflects the influence of the Oxford Movement and Tractarian sacramental theology on [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s religious sensibility.

## Critical Reception

The love sonnets of Elizabeth Barrett Browning have been the subject of sustained critical attention since their publication. Angela Leighton, in *Elizabeth Barrett Browning* (1986), argues that the sequence's treatment of love is inseparable from its theological commitments: "the lover's voice in these sonnets borrows its authority from the devotional tradition, so that erotic and religious language become mutually reinforcing."

Dorothy Mermin, in *Elizabeth Barrett Browning: The Origins of a New Poetry* (1989), reads the love sonnets as a feminist reclamation of the Petrarchan tradition. Where the conventional Petrarchan sonnet positions the female beloved as silent object of male desire, Barrett Browning's speaker actively names, describes, and anatomises her own love — a radical reversal of the tradition's gendered gaze. The sequence's rhetorical strategies — enumeration ("How do I love thee? Let me count the ways"), paradox ("I love thee with the breath, / Smiles, tears, of all my life"), and direct address — constitute what Mermin calls "a poetics of mutual presence" [043-001, 043-012].

Marjorie Stone, in *Elizabeth Barrett Browning* (1995), situates the sonnets within the broader context of Victorian women's poetry, noting that Barrett Browning's willingness to write candidly about female desire was exceptional in an era when women poets were expected to confine themselves to domestic and religious themes. Stone reads the sonnet sequence as a deliberate intervention in literary history: by claiming the sonnet form — the most prestigious form of love poetry in the Western tradition — for a woman's voice, Barrett Browning challenged assumptions about who could speak, and how, about love.

The sonnets have also been read through the lens of disability studies. Barrett Browning's chronic illness and her self-representation as physically weak ("I lift my heavy heart up solemnly" [005-001]) inform the sequence's recurring tension between bodily limitation and emotional expansiveness. The speaker's love, which transcends physical constraint, becomes a model of agency achieved through, rather than despite, bodily vulnerability.

## Scholarly Consensus

Critical consensus holds that the *Sonnets from the Portuguese* represent the finest love sonnet sequence in English since the Renaissance. Their fusion of Petrarchan convention with autobiographical immediacy, their theological sophistication, and their feminist reclamation of a traditionally masculine genre have secured their place in the canon of Victorian poetry.
