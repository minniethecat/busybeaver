# Tear (Imagery)

## Definition

Tears in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* are not signs of weakness but the body's authentic language of emotional truth. The sequence opens with the speaker's past defined by [[grief-theme|grief]] — a life of tears that [[love-theme|love]] transforms but does not erase. Unlike the conventional Victorian association of tears with feminine fragility, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] presents weeping as a shared act: both the speaker and the be[[love-theme|love]]d weep, and tears become the medium through which their emotional honesty is proven. The tear is at once evidence of past [[grief-theme|suffering]] ("frequent tears" in [[sonnet-viii|Sonnet VIII]]) and present devotion.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

Tears appear across at least eight sonnets, concentrated in the early and middle sections.

### Tears as Past Grief

The early sonnets establish tears as the speaker's pre-existing condition. [[sonnet-viii|Sonnet VIII]] acknowledges "frequent tears" [008-010] as an ongoing reality, while [[sonnet-v|Sonnet V]] reveals how much "[[grief-theme|grief]] lay hid" beneath the speaker's surface. These are tears accumulated over years of illness and isolation — the emotional landscape from which [[love-theme|love]] must rescue her.

### Tears as Purification

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] draws on the Christian tradition of tears as [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual cleansing. The tear is not merely an expression of [[grief-theme|sorrow]] but a sacrament of the [[heart-imagery|heart]] — a visible sign of invisible transformation. When the speaker weeps, she does so not to manipulate but to witness: the tears testify to [[love-theme|love]]'s reality more authentically than any declaration.

### Tears and the Beloved

The be[[love-theme|love]]d is not exempt from tears. Coming from a mother's [[grief-theme|grief]] or divine witness, the tear's dignity is asserted: it belongs to [[god|God]] ["Ask [[god|God]] who knows!" [008-010]]. This theological framing elevates the tear from mere emotion to [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual testimony.

## Literary Context

The Victorian "[[religious-faith|religion]] of tears" — influentially discussed in scholarly treatments of Tennyson and Dickens — provides the cultural context for [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s tear imagery. Unlike the sentimental tradition that exploits tears for pathos, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s tears are dignified by their theological framing: they are seen by [[god|God]], and that seeing makes them meaningful.
