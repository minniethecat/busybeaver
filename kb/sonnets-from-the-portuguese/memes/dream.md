# Dream (Motif)

## Definition

The dream and vision motif in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* operates through a sustained contrast between imagined and real [[love-theme|love]]. The speaker's life before the be[[love-theme|love]]d was populated by "visions" [026-001] — imaginative, aesthetic, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual experiences that substituted for human relationship. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's arrival does not obliterate these dreams but fulfills and surpasses them: he proves to be what the visions "seemed" [026-009]. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] draws on the Platonic tradition of dreams as imperfect copies of reality and the Christian tradition of dreams as divine revelation, but she inverts both: in her sonnets, the dream is not the transcendent reality but the lesser one, and the be[[love-theme|love]]d — a mortal human being — is the fulfilment that puts "man's best dreams to shame" [026-014].

The motif appears across four sonnets (VI, XXIII, XXVI, XXX) and traces a movement from the transformation of solitary dreaming through the renunciation of [[death-theme|death]]-dreams to the climactic declaration of dreams' insufficiency before divine-human [[love-theme|love]].

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

### Dream as Transformed Solitude: Sonnet VI

The dream motif enters the sequence quietly in [[sonnet-vi|Sonnet VI]], embedded within the poem's meditation on the be[[love-theme|love]]d's encompassing presence. The speaker declares that even the widest geographical separation cannot sever her from the be[[love-theme|love]]d: "The widest land / Doom takes to part us, leaves thy [[heart-imagery|heart]] in mine / With pulses that beat double" [006-009]. The consequence is total transformation of interior life: "What I do / And what I dream include thee, as the wine / Must taste of its own grapes" [006-010]. The verb "dream" here encompasses the speaker's entire imaginative life — her thoughts, fantasies, and unconscious mental activity. To say that even her dreams "include" the be[[love-theme|love]]d is to assert that [[love-theme|love]] has penetrated the most private and autonomous region of the self. The simile of wine tasting "of its own grapes" [006-012] reinforces the claim: as wine cannot escape the flavour of the grape, so the speaker's dreaming mind cannot escape the presence of the be[[love-theme|love]]d. Solitude, even the solitude of sleep, has been permanently altered.

### Renouncing Dreams of Death: Sonnet XXIII

[[sonnet-xxiii|Sonnet XXIII]] introduces a darker register. The speaker, reading the be[[love-theme|love]]d's letter and marvelling at the depth of his feeling, undergoes a conversion: "Then my [[soul-theme|soul]], instead / Of dreams of [[death-theme|death]], resumes life's lower range" [023-008]. The phrase "dreams of [[death-theme|death]]" is deliberately ambiguous — it may refer to the speaker's habitual thoughts of [[death-theme|mortality]] (her chronic illness and isolation having made [[death-theme|death]] a constant imaginative companion), or to the escape from life that [[death-theme|death]] represented before [[love-theme|love]] arrived. Either way, the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[love-theme|love]] effects a reversal: the [[soul-theme|soul]] turns from [[death-theme|death]]-dreams to "life's lower range" [023-009], the ordinary, embodied, earthly existence that the speaker had previously transcended or fled. The adjective "lower" is not pejorative — it is honest. Life on earth is lower than heaven, lower than the visionary realm of [[death-theme|death]]-dreams. But the speaker chooses it: "I yield the grave for thy sake, and exchange / My near sweet view of Heaven for earth with thee" [023-013]. The exchange is asymmetrical — heaven for earth, [[death-theme|death]]-dreams for waking life — and the asymmetry is the point: [[love-theme|love]] makes the "lower" choice the right one.

### Visions Surpassed: Sonnet XXVI

[[sonnet-xxvi|Sonnet XXVI]] is the motif's definitive statement, a compressed autobiography of the speaker's imaginative life. She recalls a time when "visions" were her "company / Instead of men and women, years ago" [026-001], and she "found them gentle mates, nor thought to know / A sweeter [[music-theme|music]] than they played to me" [026-003]. The visions — imaginative projections, aesthetic ideals, [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual consolations — served as substitutes for human intimacy. But they proved insufficient: "their trailing purple was not free / Of this world's dust, their lutes did [[silence|silent]] grow" [026-005]. The visions' inadequacy is two-fold: they are contaminated by earthly imperfection ("this world's dust") and they are ultimately mute (their "lutes did [[silence|silent]] grow," 026-006). The turning point is the be[[love-theme|love]]d's arrival: "Then *thou* didst come — to *be*, / Be[[love-theme|love]]d, what they *seemed*" [026-008]. The italicized "*be*" and "*seemed*" are the sonnet's philosophical core: the visions had the *appearance* of fulfilment; the be[[love-theme|love]]d has the *reality*. The final couplet delivers the verdict: "[[god|God]]'s gifts put man's best dreams to shame" [026-014]. The word "shame" is harsh — the dreams are not merely less than the be[[love-theme|love]]d, they are embarrassed by the comparison. This is the motif's most radical claim: the real, given by [[god|God]] in the form of a human be[[love-theme|love]]d, so far exceeds human imagination that the imagination's best efforts are revealed as impoverished.

### Dream and Disillusion: Sonnet XXX

[[sonnet-xxx|Sonnet XXX]] returns to the dream motif in a mood of doubt. The speaker, seeing the be[[love-theme|love]]d's image "through my [[tear-imagery|tears]] tonight" [030-001] though she "saw thee smiling" earlier that day [030-002], questions the reality of her happiness: "Be[[love-theme|love]]d, dost thou [[love-theme|love]]? or did I see all / The glory as I dreamed, and fainted when / Too vehement [[light-imagery|light]] dilated my ideal / For my [[soul-theme|soul]]'s eyes?" [030-010]. The fear is that [[love-theme|love]] itself may be a dream — that the "glory" she perceived was not the be[[love-theme|love]]d's actual affection but her own "ideal" dilated by excessive [[light-imagery|light]]. The metaphor is optical: too much [[light-imagery|light]] (too much hope, too much desire) distorts perception, making the "ideal" — the dreamed-of be[[love-theme|love]]d — appear larger and brighter than reality. The sonnet does not resolve this doubt within itself; it ends with a question: "Will that [[light-imagery|light]] come again / As now these [[tear-imagery|tears]] come — falling hot and real?" [030-013]. The juxtaposition of "[[light-imagery|light]]" (the possibly dreamed glory) with "[[tear-imagery|tears]]" (the undeniably real) is the sonnet's epistemological gesture: [[tear-imagery|tears]] prove reality in a way that [[light-imagery|light]] cannot. The dream motif here registers the genuine uncertainty that [[shadow|shadows]] even reciprocated [[love-theme|love]] — the fear that one has imagined everything.

## Development Across the Sequence

The dream motif follows a trajectory from integration through renunciation to doubt. [[sonnet-vi|Sonnet VI]] establishes that the be[[love-theme|love]]d has colonized even the speaker's dreams, transforming solitary mental life into shared experience. [[sonnet-xxiii|Sonnet XXIII]] marks a willed choice: the speaker exchanges "dreams of [[death-theme|death]]" for earthly life with the be[[love-theme|love]]d. [[sonnet-xxvi|Sonnet XXVI]], the motif's apex, declares the be[[love-theme|love]]d a fulfilment so complete that "[[god|God]]'s gifts put man's best dreams to shame" [026-014]. [[sonnet-xxx|Sonnet XXX]], the motif's final appearance, reintroduces doubt: the fear that [[love-theme|love]] itself may be a dream, that the speaker's ideal has distorted her perception. This final turn prevents the motif from becoming triumphalist — the dream, even when surpassed, remains a live category, a [[shadow]] that [[love-theme|love]] must continually dispel.

## Literary Context

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s treatment of the dream motif engages the Platonic distinction between appearance and reality, most influentially articulated in the allegory of the cave (Republic, Book VII). In Plato's allegory, the prisoners see only [[shadow|shadows]] and dream they are real; the philosopher escapes the cave and sees the Forms in the sun's true [[light-imagery|light]]. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] inverts this structure: the visions she lived among were her cave-[[shadow|shadows]], and the be[[love-theme|love]]d — a mortal, embodied being — is the true [[light-imagery|light]]. This inversion is characteristically Victorian in its valuation of human relationship over abstract ideality, and characteristically [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] in its insistence that divine gifts arrive in human form.

The motif also participates in the Romantic tradition of the dream-vision, from Coleridge's "Kubla Khan" to Keats's "The Eve of St. Agnes." But where the Romantics often valorize the dream as a superior mode of perception — more intense, more truth-bearing than waking consciousness — [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] reverses the hierarchy: her dreams are pale [[shadow|shadows]] of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's reality. This reversal reflects her broader critique of Romantic solitude, shared with her husband [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]], whose dramatic monologues consistently expose the limitations of solitary consciousness.

The biblical resonance of "[[god|God]]'s gifts put man's best dreams to shame" [026-014] should not be overlooked. The verse echoes 1 Corinthians 2:9 ("Eye hath not seen, nor ear heard, neither have entered into the [[heart-imagery|heart]] of man, the things which [[god|God]] hath prepared for them that [[love-theme|love]] him"), transforming a Pauline promise of heaven into a statement about earthly [[love-theme|love]]. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s theological move — applying eschatological language to human relationship — is characteristic of the sequence's incarnational poetics.
