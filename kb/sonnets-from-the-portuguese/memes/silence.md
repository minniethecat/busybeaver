# Silence (Theme)

## Definition

Silence in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* functions as a paradoxical language — a mode of communication more powerful than speech. The motif appears across five sonnets (XIII, XX, XXI, XXII, XXVI) and traces an arc from defensive self-protection through longing absence to sacramental union. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s silences are never mere vacancy; they are charged with meaning: the silence of womanhood that "commends" [[love-theme|love]] without speaking it [013-009], the silence of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's absence that "sinks" into a solitary room [020-004], and the "deep, dear silence" [022-009] into which [[angel-imagery|angels]] would drop "some golden orb of perfect [[music-theme|song]]" [022-008]. The motif's culmination in [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]] — where the two [[soul-theme|soul]]s stand "face to face, silent, drawing nigh and nigher" [022-002] — transforms silence from a condition of restraint into the medium of the most complete intimacy imaginable.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

### The Silence of Womanhood: Sonnet XIII

The silence motif first appears in [[sonnet-xiii|Sonnet XIII]] as a gendered strategy of self-protection. The poem opens with the be[[love-theme|love]]d's request that the speaker "fashion into speech / The [[love-theme|love]] I bear thee" [013-001], a request she immediately refuses: "I drop it at thy feet. I cannot teach / My hand to hold my [[soul-theme|spirit]]s so far off / From myself — me" [013-005]. The refusal to verbalize [[love-theme|love]] is not a failure of eloquence but a positive assertion: "Nay, let the silence of my womanhood / Commend my woman-[[love-theme|love]] to thy belief" [013-009]. Here "silence" is the opposite of absence — it is a form of testimony, an embodied proof that surpasses "words, of [[love-theme|love]] hid in me out of reach" [013-008]. The sonnet closes with a striking oxymoron: "a most dauntless, voiceless fortitude" [013-013], in which the lack of voice becomes a form of bravery, and silence — far from being passive — is an active, willed stance.

### The Silence of Absence: Sonnet XX

[[sonnet-xx|Sonnet XX]] reverses the valence of silence, transforming it from chosen restraint into suffered deprivation. The speaker recalls the year before she knew the be[[love-theme|love]]d: "What time I sate alone here in the snow / And saw no footprint, heard the silence sink / No moment at thy voice" [020-003]. The verb "sink" personifies silence as something that descends and settles, a weight that deepens with each moment the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice does not break it. The silence here is not the meaningful silence of [[sonnet-xiii|Sonnet XIII]] but the empty silence of a world before [[love-theme|love]] — a silence measured in "chains" counted "link by link" [020-006]. The contrast with [[sonnet-xiii|Sonnet XIII]] is deliberate: where the earlier sonnet's silence was chosen and eloquent, this silence is imposed and mute.

### Silence as Spiritual Language: Sonnet XXI

[[sonnet-xxi|Sonnet XXI]] synthesizes the tension between speech and silence through the image of iteration. The speaker demands that the be[[love-theme|love]]d repeat his declaration of [[love-theme|love]] — "Say over again, and yet once over again / That thou dost [[love-theme|love]] me" [021-001] — acknowledging that the repeated word "Should seem a 'cuckoo-[[music-theme|song]]'" [021-003], a mechanical repetition. The argument for repetition is a natural one: just as spring is incomplete "without her cuckoo-strain" [021-005], so the speaker's happiness requires the be[[love-theme|love]]d's reiterated word. But the sonnet's final couplet introduces a qualification: the be[[love-theme|love]]d is to speak his [[love-theme|love]] repeatedly, "only minding, Dear, / To [[love-theme|love]] me also in silence with thy [[soul-theme|soul]]" [021-013]. The distinction is crucial: words are necessary but insufficient; the truest [[love-theme|love]] is the [[love-theme|love]] that operates beneath and beyond speech — a silent [[love-theme|love]] of the [[soul-theme|soul]] that words can accompany but never replace. The poem thus holds speech and silence in productive tension rather than opposition.

### The Deep, Dear Silence: Sonnet XXII

[[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]] is the sequence's definitive treatment of silence as [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual union. The poem en[[dream|visions]] the [[love-theme|love]]rs' [[soul-theme|soul]]s standing "erect and strong, / Face to face, silent, drawing nigh and nigher" [022-001]. The adjective "silent" is placed at the line's turning point, suspended between "face to face" and "drawing nigh" — it is the condition in which the [[soul-theme|soul]]s approach each other, not the barrier that keeps them apart. The silence is so rich, so complete, that the [[angel-imagery|angels]] themselves — who in the poem's cosmology "would press on us, and aspire / To drop some golden orb of perfect [[music-theme|song]] / Into our deep, dear silence" [022-007] — are figured as aspiring to contribute *to* the silence, not to break it. The phrase "deep, dear silence" [022-009] is the motif's emotional climax: silence is deep (spatially capacious, holding everything) and dear (intimately precious, be[[love-theme|love]]d). The sonnet's preference for earthly silence over [[angel-imagery|angel]]ic [[music-theme|song]] — "Let us stay / Rather on earth" [022-009] — asserts that human silence, shared between [[love-theme|love]]rs, is a greater good than the [[music-theme|music]] of heaven.

### The Silencing of Visions: Sonnet XXVI

The motif's final appearance in [[sonnet-xxvi|Sonnet XXVI]] is retrospective. The speaker recalls how her former [[dream|visionary]] companions — the "[[dream|visions]]" that were her "company / Instead of men and women" [026-001] — eventually proved insufficient: "their lutes did silent grow, / And I myself grew faint and blind below / Their vanishing eyes" [026-006]. Here "silent" marks the failure of an imaginative mode of life: the [[dream|visions]]' [[music-theme|music]] stops, and silence descends as loss. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's arrival reverses this silence: "Then *thou* didst come — to *be*, / Be[[love-theme|love]]d, what they *seemed*" [026-008]. The be[[love-theme|love]]d is not a new silence but a new *sound*: "Their shining fronts, / Their [[music-theme|song]]s, their splendours — better, yet the same" [026-009]. The [[dream|visions]]' lutes grew silent; the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[music-theme|song]]s endure. This final treatment ties the silence motif back to the [[music-theme|music]] motif: silence and [[music-theme|music]] are not opposites but phases in a single rhythm of loss and restoration.

## Development Across the Sequence

The silence motif traces a chiastic pattern. [[sonnet-xiii|Sonnet XIII]] presents silence as chosen, defensive, and gendered — the silence a woman must maintain to protect her interior life. [[sonnet-xx|Sonnet XX]] inverts this: silence becomes the mark of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's absence, a weight rather than a shield. [[sonnet-xxi|Sonnet XXI]] negotiates between the two, holding speech and silence in productive tension. [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]] resolves the tension into union: silence is no longer the condition of solitude but the medium of the most complete intimacy. [[sonnet-xxvi|Sonnet XXVI]], looking backward, confirms the resolution by contrasting the "silent" lutes of former [[dream|visions]] with the enduring [[music-theme|song]]s of the be[[love-theme|love]]d. The motif thus moves from defense through deprivation to sacramental presence.

## Literary Context

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s treatment of silence engages several literary and theological traditions. The "silence of my womanhood" [013-009] participates in the Victorian discourse of feminine virtue — the idea that a woman's worth is demonstrated by restraint rather than display — but subverts it by making silence a form of *strength* ("dauntless, voiceless fortitude," 013-013) rather than submission.

The "deep, dear silence" of [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]] draws on the mystical tradition of apophatic theology — the idea that the divine is best approached through negation, through what cannot be said. The silence between the [[love-theme|love]]rs' [[soul-theme|soul]]s echoes the "silent [[love-theme|love]]" of St. John of the Cross and the "cloud of unknowing" of medieval English mysticism. But [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to locate this mystical silence not in the [[soul-theme|soul]]'s relation to [[god|God]] but in the [[love-theme|love]]rs' relation to each other — a radical domestication of the mystical that is characteristic of her religious poetics more broadly.

The contrast between the silence of [[dream|visions]] and the [[music-theme|song]]s of the be[[love-theme|love]]d in [[sonnet-xxvi|Sonnet XXVI]] also echoes the Romantic critique of solipsistic imagination — Wordsworth's similar renunciation of "[[dream|visionary]]" solitude for human relationship in "Tintern Abbey" and *The Prelude*. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] shares with Wordsworth the conviction that the imagination, unaided by [[love-theme|love]], ultimately falls silent.
