# Death (Theme)

## Definition

Death in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* functions not as an end but as a counterpoint — the darkness against which [[love-theme|love]]'s [[light-imagery|light]] is measured. The sequence opens with death as the speaker's assumed identity ("'Death!' I said"), only to be answered by [[love-theme|love]]. Throughout the 44 sonnets, death appears as past [[grief-theme|grief]] (the "deathweights" of earlier [[grief-theme|sorrow]] in [[sonnet-ii|Sonnet II]]), as a liminal state from which [[love-theme|love]] rescues the speaker ([[sonnet-vii|Sonnet VII]]), and ultimately as the boundary that [[love-theme|love]] promises to cross ("I shall but [[love-theme|love]] thee better after death" [043-014]). Unlike the conventional Petrarchan treatment of death as the [[love-theme|love]]r's tragic fate, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s death is a passage, not a terminus.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

Death appears explicitly in seven sonnets, with related imagery (darkness, the grave, mortality) throughout the sequence.

### Death as the Speaker's Starting Point: Sonnets I–II

The sequence's opening sonnet stages death as the speaker's self-conception before [[love-theme|love]] intervenes: "Guess now who holds thee!—'Death!' I said. But there, / The silver answer rang—'Not Death, but Love'" [001-013]. This exchange frames the entire sequence as a movement from death to [[love-theme|love]], from [[silence]] to [[music-theme|song]].

[[sonnet-ii|Sonnet II]] deepens this association. The speaker describes "deathweights" placed upon her that would have prevented her from seeing the be[[love-theme|love]]d: "The deathweights placed there would have ended all" [002-007]. These deathweights are the accumulated [[grief-theme|grief]] and illness of her past life — barriers that [[love-theme|love]] overcomes.

### Death and Resurrection: Sonnets VII and XXII

In [[sonnet-vii|Sonnet VII]], the speaker recalls a state "of obvious death" from which [[love-theme|love]] raised her: she was "caught up into [[love-theme|love]], and taught the whole / Of life in a new rhythm" [007-005]. Death here is [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual and emotional rather than physical — a deadness of the [[soul-theme|soul]] that [[love-theme|love]] reverses.

[[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]] en[[dream|visions]] the [[love-theme|love]]rs united beyond death's reach. The closing image — "With darkness and the death-hour rounding it" [022-014] — places death as the horizon that frames but does not define their union. The [[soul-theme|soul]]s that "stand up erect and strong" [022-001] are not threatened by the approaching darkness.

### Death in the Later Sonnets: XXXIX and XLIII

Sonnet XXXIX acknowledges death and [[grief-theme|suffering]] as real forces, not mere metaphors: "Nor [[god|God]]'s infliction, nor death's neighbourhood" [039-010] names death alongside divine affliction as a genuine challenge to [[love-theme|love]]. Yet the sonnet does not resolve these threats — it accepts them as the condition of mortal [[love-theme|love]].

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII provides the sequence's definitive statement on death. The closing couplet — "I shall but [[love-theme|love]] thee better after death" [043-014] — transforms death from a threat into a promise. Love is not diminished by mortality but intensified beyond it. This is the theological resolution the sequence has been moving toward: [[love-theme|love]] as the one thing that death cannot touch.

## Literary Context

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s treatment of death draws on both the Petrarchan tradition and Christian eschatology. In Petrarch's *Canzoniere*, Laura's death is the central event — [[love-theme|love]] becomes eternally unconsummated mourning. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] reverses this: death is the pre-existing condition (the speaker's "deathweights") from which [[love-theme|love]] rescues her, and the future death the [[love-theme|love]]rs face together is not a loss but a transformation. This reversal reflects her engagement with Victorian theological debates about resurrection and the afterlife, influenced by her Congregationalist upbringing and her reading of liberal Anglican theology.
