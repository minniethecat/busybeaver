# Elizabeth Barrett Browning

## Biography

Elizabeth Barrett Browning (6 March 1806 – 29 June 1861) was one of the most celebrated English poets of the Victorian era. Born in County Durham to a wealthy family whose fortune derived from Jamaican sugar plantations, she was a precocious reader and writer, publishing her first major collection (*The Seraphim and Other Poems*) in 1838. By her late thirties, chronic illness had made her a near-recluse in her father's Wimpole Street house — a period of isolation that forms the biographical backdrop to [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]]'s opening movement from [[silence]] to [[music-theme|song]].

Her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] began in 1845 after he wrote to admire her poetry. Their correspondence — over 570 letters — led to a secret marriage in September 1846 and [[browning-courtship|elopement]] to Italy. They settled at Casa Guidi in Florence, where Barrett Browning wrote much of her major later work, including *Aurora Leigh* (1856). She died in Florence in 1861 and is buried in the English Cemetery there.

## Connection to the Sonnets

*[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* is the poetic record of Barrett Browning's [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]. Written between 1845 and 1846, the 44 sonnets trace her emotional journey from initial disbelief that she could be [[love-theme|love]]d — "Guess now who holds thee!—'Death!' I said" [001-013] — through surrender, reciprocal devotion, and finally a [[dream|vision]] of [[love-theme|love]] that transcends [[death-theme|mortality]]: "I shall but [[love-theme|love]] thee better after [[death-theme|death]]" [043-014].

The title's "Portuguese" refers to [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]'s pet name for her — "my little Portuguese" — based on her dark complexion and her earlier poem "Catarina to Camoens," written from the perspective of a Portuguese woman. The sonnets were initially presented as translations from Portuguese to obscure their autobiographical nature, though this fiction was transparent to most readers.

The sequence subverts the Petrarchan tradition by giving voice to a female speaker who moves from passive longing to active, reciprocal desire. Barrett Browning transformed the [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|sonnet form]] — conventionally used by male poets to address distant, idealised be[[love-theme|love]]ds — into a medium for a woman's declaration of [[love-theme|love]].

## References in the Text

Barrett Browning is present throughout the sequence as its implied speaker — the "I" whose voice [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]] record. Key passages that illuminate her self-presentation include:

- The opening sonnet's image of the speaker as one defined by [[death-theme|death]] and transformed by [[love-theme|love]]: "The silver answer rang — 'Not Death, but Love'" [001-013].
- The admission of past [[grief-theme|grief]]: "What a great heap of [[grief-theme|grief]] lay hid in me" [005-005] — alluding to her years of illness and isolation.
- The declaration of total devotion: "Be[[love-theme|love]]d, I only [[love-theme|love]] thee! let it pass" [009-014].
- The famous enumeration of [[love-theme|love]]'s dimensions in [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII, which has become one of the most widely recognised [[love-theme|love]] poems in the English language.

## Biographical Context

Born in 1806 at Coxhoe Hall, Durham, Elizabeth Barrett was the eldest of twelve children in a wealthy family whose fortune derived from Jamaican sugar plantations — a legacy of colonial exploitation that troubled her throughout her life. A precocious child, she read Homer in Greek by age eight and published her first volume of poetry, *The Battle of Marathon*, at fourteen.

Her life was shaped by chronic illness: a lung condition (possibly tuberculosis) and a spinal injury sustained in adolescence kept her largely confined to her room at 50 Wimpole Street, London, from her early thirties. Her father, Edward Moulton Barrett, exercised tyrannical control over his children, forbidding any of them to marry. This domestic imprisonment became the backdrop against which her romance with Robert Browning unfolded.

Barrett's poetry before *Sonnets from the Portuguese* was already distinguished by its engagement with social and political issues. *The Cry of the Children* (1843) protested child labour; *Aurora Leigh* (1856), her verse-novel, addressed the condition of women artists. The love sonnets, written during the most intensely personal period of her life, represent not a retreat from public concerns but an insistence that the personal — the life of the affections — is itself a legitimate subject for serious poetry.

## Poetic Style and Innovation

Barrett Browning's poetic voice combines intellectual rigour with emotional intensity. Her sonnets are characterised by:

- **Metaphysical conceits**: Extended comparisons that yoke together the spiritual and the sensual, as when she describes the beloved's kiss as a "chrism" — a sacramental anointing [038-010].
- **Rhetorical architecture**: Poems built on anaphora, enumeration, and paradox, creating a sense of argumentative momentum that mirrors the emotional trajectory from doubt to affirmation.
- **Theological depth**: A sustained engagement with Christian theology, particularly the Tractarian emphasis on the sacramental nature of material reality. Barrett Browning's love is never merely earthly: it participates in and points toward the divine.

These qualities distinguish her from her Victorian contemporaries. Where Tennyson cultivates melancholy and Arnold philosophical resignation, Barrett Browning offers what critic Isobel Armstrong calls "a poetry of affirmation that earns its optimism through honest confrontation with doubt."

## Legacy

Elizabeth Barrett Browning remains one of the most significant English poets of the nineteenth century. Her influence extends beyond the Victorian period: Virginia Woolf praised her in *The Common Reader*, and contemporary poets including Eavan Boland and Adrienne Rich have acknowledged her as a precursor. The *Sonnets from the Portuguese*, continuously in print since 1850, have been translated into more than thirty languages and adapted into music, theatre, and film. The closing line of Sonnet XLIII — "I shall but love thee better after death" [043-014] — is among the most quoted lines in the English language.
