# Browning Courtship (1845–1846)

## Overview

The courtship of Elizabeth Barrett and Robert Browning is among the most famous literary love stories of the 19th century. It began in January 1845 when Browning, then an obscure poet with a string of critical failures, wrote to Barrett — already one of England's most celebrated poets — to praise her work. Their correspondence, amounting to over 570 letters exchanged over twenty months, records the gradual transformation of mutual admiration into romantic love, leading to their secret marriage on 12 September 1846 and immediate elopement to Italy.

The courtship is the biographical occasion of *Sonnets from the Portuguese*, which Barrett Browning composed during this same period. While the sonnets are not literal transcriptions of the correspondence, their emotional arc — from disbelief through surrender to reciprocal devotion — maps closely onto the psychological trajectory of the letters.

## Key Events

### The First Letter (January 1845)

Robert Browning's first letter to Elizabeth Barrett, dated 10 January 1845, opened with: "I love your verses with all my heart, dear Miss Barrett... and I love you too." This declaration of love for both the poetry and the poet, delivered before they had ever met, sets the terms of the relationship that the sonnets would later record. Barrett's reply was characteristically self-deprecating, questioning what he could possibly see in her.

### Meeting and Correspondence (May 1845 – September 1846)

The couple met for the first time on 20 May 1845. Barrett was then 39, an invalid confined largely to her room in the Wimpole Street house of her domineering father, Edward Moulton-Barrett. Browning was 33 and lived independently. Over the next sixteen months, they exchanged letters at a rate that sometimes reached two or three per day. The correspondence covers poetry, philosophy, religion, and their growing emotional intimacy.

### The Secret Marriage (12 September 1846)

The couple were married secretly at St Marylebone Parish Church on 12 September 1846. Barrett's father had forbidden any of his children to marry, and the marriage was conducted without his knowledge — Barrett left her father's house a week later, with her maid and her dog Flush, to join Browning for the journey to Italy. Edward Moulton-Barrett never spoke to his daughter again.

### Elopement to Italy (September 1846)

The couple travelled through France to Italy, arriving in Pisa in October 1846 and settling permanently at Casa Guidi in Florence in 1847. Italy represented liberation — from Barrett's father's control, from the London literary establishment, and from the illness that had confined her. The move to Italy marks the biographical fulfilment of the sonnets' movement from death to life: the speaker who began the sequence defined by death ended it in a city of sunlight and creative vitality.

## Significance

The courtship and elopement transformed both poets' literary trajectories. Barrett Browning produced *Sonnets from the Portuguese*, *Casa Guidi Windows* (1851), and *Aurora Leigh* (1856) in Italy — her greatest works. Browning developed the dramatic monologue form in *Men and Women* (1855). Their marriage was by all accounts a deeply happy one, lasting until Elizabeth's death in 1861.

## Connection to the Sonnets

The courtship is the biographical substrate of *Sonnets from the Portuguese*, though the sonnets are not literal transcriptions of the correspondence. Key connections include:

- The opening sonnet's opposition between Death and Love (001-013) mirrors Barrett's own description of her isolated life before Browning's first letter
- The speaker's persistent self-deprecation and disbelief that she could be loved — "Can it be right to give what I can give?" (009-001) — echoes Barrett's initial letters questioning Browning's admiration
- The theme of silent communion in Sonnet XXI ("love me also in silence with thy soul" [021-014]) reflects the epistolary nature of their courtship, conducted primarily through written words
- The sequence's movement from darkness to light parallels the biographical arc from Barrett's Wimpole Street confinement to the couple's life in sunlit Florence

## Textual Evidence

Primary sources documenting the courtship include:

- **The Browning Letters**: Over 570 letters exchanged between January 1845 and September 1846, held at Wellesley College and Baylor University. Published in *The Letters of Robert Browning and Elizabeth Barrett Barrett, 1845–1846* (ed. Elvan Kintner, 1969).
- **Barrett Browning's diary and notebooks**: Record her emotional state during the courtship period, including her anxiety about her father's reaction.
- **Contemporary accounts**: Including letters from mutual friends such as John Kenyon and Mary Russell Mitford, who facilitated the couple's introduction.
