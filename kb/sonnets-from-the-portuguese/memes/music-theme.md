# Music (Theme)

## Definition

Music in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* functions as a multivalent metaphor for poetic vocation, emotional authenticity, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transcendence. The speaker identifies herself repeatedly as a singer or musician — "a poor, tired, wandering singer" [003-011], a "cricket" chirping against the be[[love-theme|love]]d's "mandolin" [004-011] — framing [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]] itself as a musical performance. Unlike the conventional Petrarchan conceit in which the be[[love-theme|love]]d's beauty inspires the poet's song, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s music metaphor encodes self-doubt: the speaker fears her "melancholy music" [011-008] is unworthy of its audience. The motif culminates in the image of mortal song absorbed into "the music of Heaven's undefiled" [033-007], where earthly imperfection yields to eternal harmony.

The metaphor appears across seven sonnets (III, IV, XI, XVII, XXVI, XXXIII, XLI), evolving from anxious self-deprecation through consolatory function to apocalyptic resolution.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

Music appears in seven of the forty-four sonnets, clustered in the early and middle portions of the sequence, with a powerful return in the final sonnet. The following passages trace its development.

### The Singer and the Musician: Sonnet III

The first appearance of the music metaphor establishes the speaker's anxious self-positioning. Addressing the be[[love-theme|love]]d as a "princely Heart" [003-001], she contrasts their stations: he is "a guest for queens to social pageantries" [003-006], while she declares herself "A poor, tired, wandering singer, singing through / The dark, and leaning up a cypress tree" [003-011]. The cypress — a funereal tree — links the act of singing with [[death-theme|mortality]] and [[grief-theme|grief]]. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's social elevation is figured in musical terms: he is a "chief musician" [003-009] whose performance belongs to the public sphere of "social pageantries" [003-006], while the speaker's song is solitary, nocturnal, and elegiac.

### Unworthy Music and the Golden Door: Sonnet IV

[[sonnet-iv|Sonnet IV]] intensifies the music metaphor through a spatial contrast between the be[[love-theme|love]]d's art and the speaker's humble song. The speaker asks how the be[[love-theme|love]]d can bear "To let thy music drop here unaware / In folds of golden fullness at my door" [004-007] — the "golden fullness" evoking both aesthetic richness and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual grace. The "door" at which the be[[love-theme|love]]d's music arrives is, the poem insists, unworthy: "this house's latch too poor / For hand of thine" [004-005]. The domestic architecture — "the casement broken in, / The bats and owlets builders in the roof" [004-009] — is juxtaposed with the cricket's chirp that "chirps against thy mandolin" [004-011]. The mandolin, an instrument of cultured music, contrasts with the cricket's involuntary, natural sound. Yet the sonnet closes not with rejection but with a demand for song: "thou must sing" [004-014], acknowledging that the be[[love-theme|love]]d's music has an obligation the speaker herself cannot discharge.

### Melancholy Music and Unworthiness: Sonnet XI

[[sonnet-xi|Sonnet XI]] presents the music metaphor at its most self-lacerating. The speaker describes her "weary minstrel-life" [011-005] — once ambitious ("to climb Aornus," 011-006) — now reduced to the bare capacity "To pipe now 'gainst the valley nightingale / A melancholy music" [011-007]. The nightingale, traditional symbol of poetic song, here becomes a rival whose natural superiority exposes the speaker's inadequacy. The melancholy quality of her music is intrinsic, not situational: it arises from the recognition that "I am not of thy worth nor for thy place" [011-010]. Yet even this admission of unworthiness becomes, paradoxically, a form of vindication: "because I [[love-theme|love]] thee, I obtain / From that same [[love-theme|love]] this vindicating grace" [011-011]. The music of inadequacy, freely offered, is redeemed by the [[love-theme|love]] that receives it.

### Medicated Music: Sonnet XVII

In [[sonnet-xvii|Sonnet XVII]] the music metaphor shifts from performance to healing. Addressing the be[[love-theme|love]]d as one capable of pouring "Antidotes / Of medicated music, answering for / Mankind's forlornest uses" [017-005], the speaker reconfigures music as a therapeutic agent. The phrase "medicated music" is striking: it combines aesthetic and pharmaceutical registers, suggesting that the be[[love-theme|love]]d's song has the power to cure despair. The speaker's own role, by contrast, is to be a passive vessel — "a hope, to sing by gladly? or a fine / Sad memory, with thy songs to interfuse? / A [[shadow|shade]], in which to sing — of palm or pine? / A grave, on which to rest from singing?" [017-011]. The progression from "sing" through "interfuse" to "rest from singing" traces a trajectory toward [[silence]], anticipating the music's dissolution into something beyond song.

### Sweeter Music: Sonnet XXVI

[[sonnet-xxvi|Sonnet XXVI]] offers the sequence's most explicit comparison between [[dream|visionary]] and human music. The speaker recalls a past in which "[[dream|visions]]" were her "company / Instead of men and women" [026-001], and she "nor thought to know / A sweeter music than they played to me" [026-003]. The word "sweeter" encodes a comparative judgment: the [[dream|visions]]' music was pleasurable, but not the *sweetest*. When the be[[love-theme|love]]d arrives, he proves to be what the [[dream|visions]] merely "seemed" [026-009]: "Their shining fronts, / Their songs, their splendours — better, yet the same" [026-009]. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's "songs" surpass the [[dream|visionary]] music not by being different in kind but by being "better, yet the same" — a fulfilment rather than a replacement. The sonnet closes with the assertion that "[[god|God]]'s gifts put man's best [[dream|dreams]] to shame" [026-014], identifying the be[[love-theme|love]]d's human music as a divine gift that supersedes the speaker's former imaginary consolations.

### Heaven's Undefiled Music: Sonnet XXXIII

[[sonnet-xxxiii|Sonnet XXXIII]] marks the apotheosis of the music metaphor. The speaker recalls the "clear / Fond voices" [033-005] of those now dead, "which, being drawn and reconciled / Into the music of Heaven's undefiled, / Call me no longer" [033-006]. Here music becomes the medium of eschatological transformation: the individual voices of the departed are "drawn" into a collective, purified harmony, their separate identities subsumed into divine music. The adjective "undefiled" contrasts this heavenly music with the melancholy, "medicated" music of the earlier sonnets — it is music free of self-doubt, illness, and [[death-theme|mortality]]. Yet the transformation carries loss: the voices "call me no longer" [033-008], and the speaker must turn to the living be[[love-theme|love]]d to continue the song.

### The Final Performance: Sonnet XLI

The final occurrence of the music metaphor, in [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LI, returns the sequence to its opening concerns but with a crucial reversal. The speaker thanks "all who have [[love-theme|love]]d me in their [[heart-imagery|heart]]s" [041-001] — those who "paused a little near the prison-wall / To hear my music in its louder parts" [041-004]. The "prison-wall" recalls the "cypress tree" of [[sonnet-iii|Sonnet III]]: both images situate the speaker's song at a threshold between confinement and freedom, life and [[death-theme|death]]. But where the early sonnets dwelt on the music's inadequacy, [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LI shifts the frame to gratitude. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's special role is singled out: "thou, who, in my voice's sink and fall / When the sob took it, thy divinest Art's / Own instrument didst drop down at thy foot / To harken what I said between my [[tear-imagery|tears]]" [041-007]. The image of the be[[love-theme|love]]d dropping his instrument — silencing his own superior music — to listen to the speaker's tear-choked voice is the sequence's most radical musical gesture: the listener's attentiveness, not the performer's skill, is what consecrates the song.

## Development Across the Sequence

The music motif traces an arc from self-doubt to grateful acceptance. In the early sonnets (III, IV, XI) the speaker's music is defined by anxious comparison: her song is melancholy, her instrument a cricket's chirp, her audience too exalted. The middle sonnets (XVII, XXVI) introduce the be[[love-theme|love]]d's music as a counter-force — medicated, sweeter, divine — that paradoxically does not [[silence]] the speaker but authorizes her voice. The late sonnet XXXIII transfigures music into a theological category: the dead are "reconciled / Into the music of Heaven's undefiled" [033-006]. [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LI, the motif's final appearance, returns to the speaker's own music but reframes it as worthy not because of its quality but because of the [[love-theme|love]] that has received it. This trajectory mirrors the sequence's broader movement from isolation to union, from doubt to affirmation.

## Literary Context

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s music metaphor participates in a long poetic tradition that figures poetry as song — a tradition stretching from the Homeric bard through the troubadours to the Romantic identification of poet with nightingale or Aeolian harp. Within the [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]] tradition, music typically belongs to the be[[love-theme|love]]d (Laura's song, Laura's voice) or to the natural world that echoes the poet's lament. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to make the *speaker* the musician, and to make the quality of her music — its melancholy, its inadequacy, its tear-choked stuttering — the explicit subject of poetic scrutiny.

The "medicated music" of [[sonnet-xvii|Sonnet XVII]] echoes the Renaissance concept of *musica humana* — the harmony that regulates the human body and [[soul-theme|soul]] — while anticipating the Victorian fascination with music as moral and therapeutic force (cf. George Eliot's "Daniel Deronda," 1876, and Walter Pater's dictum that "all art constantly aspires towards the condition of music"). [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s own deep musicality is well-documented: she was an accomplished pianist, and [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]'s poetry is notable for its musical forms and structures. The mandolin reference in [[sonnet-iv|Sonnet IV]] may allude to the [[robert-browning|Browning]]s' life in Italy, where such instruments were part of the soundscape of Florentine daily life.
