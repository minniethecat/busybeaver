# Sonnets from the Portuguese

## Overview

*Sonnets from the Portuguese* is a sequence of 44 [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s by [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], first published in 1850 in the second edition of her *Poems*. Written during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846), the sonnets trace an autobiographical arc from the speaker's initial disbelief that she could be [[love-theme|love]]d, through surrender and reciprocal devotion, to a [[dream|vision]] of [[love-theme|love]] that transcends [[death-theme|mortality]]. The title's reference to "the Portuguese" was a private joke — [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]'s pet name for Elizabeth was "my little Portuguese," a reference to her dark complexion and to her poem "Catarina to Camoens."

The sequence is among the most celebrated [[love-theme|love]] poetry in the English language. [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII ("How do I [[love-theme|love]] thee? [[sonnet-xliii|Let me count the ways]]") is one of the most frequently anthologised poems in English, and the sequence as a whole stands as the Victorian era's definitive poetic treatment of romantic [[love-theme|love]] as a [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual as well as emotional experience.

## Text

The sonnets follow the Petrarchan (Italian) form: fourteen lines of iambic pentameter, typically rhymed ABBA ABBA CDC DCD, divided into an octave (first eight lines) and a sestet (final six). [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] occasionally varies the sestet rhyme scheme. The Petrarchan form's built-in "turn" (volta) between octave and sestet suits the sequence's emotional logic: the octave often presents a state of feeling or a philosophical proposition, and the sestet complicates, reverses, or deepens it.

## Analysis

The sequence's structure is not linear but spiral — themes introduced in early sonnets ([[love-theme|love]] and [[death-theme|death]], the [[soul-theme|soul]]'s awakening, the speaker's unworthiness) are revisited with greater depth as the sequence progresses. The opening sonnet establishes the governing opposition between [[death-theme|death]] and [[love-theme|love]]: "The silver answer rang — 'Not Death, but Love'" [001-013]. Subsequent sonnets develop this opposition into a theology of [[love-theme|love]] in which the be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence is sacramental — a visible sign of invisible grace.

The middle sonnets (XX–XXX) explore the nature of reciprocal union, using the imagery of [[soul-theme|soul]]-communion to assert [[love-theme|love]] as the meeting of equals. The later sonnets (XXXV–XLIV) confront [[grief-theme|grief]] and [[death-theme|mortality]], culminating in [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII's enumeration of [[love-theme|love]]'s dimensions and [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIV's image of the be[[love-theme|love]]d as the soil from which all the speaker's creative life grows.

## Place in the Sequence

Each sonnet stands as a complete poem while contributing to the cumulative emotional narrative. Individual sonnets are not numbered in the original 1850 edition but are conventionally identified by Roman numerals (I–XLIV). The lack of titles within the sequence reinforces the sense of a continuous meditation — the sonnets are not discrete poems but movements within a single work.

## Literary Context

*Sonnets from the Portuguese* represents a significant intervention in the Petrarchan [[love-theme|love]]-sonnet tradition. The [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]] had conventionally positioned the male poet as supplicant before a distant, idealised female be[[love-theme|love]]d. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] reverses this dynamic — the speaker is female, the be[[love-theme|love]]d male, and the emotional trajectory moves from passive longing to active, reciprocal desire. This reversal, combined with the sequence's autobiographical grounding in a real, consummated [[love-theme|love]], distinguishes it from the entire prior sonnet tradition.

## Publication History

The sonnets were written in secret during 1845–1846, the period of Elizabeth Barrett's courtship with Robert Browning. Barrett did not show the poems to Browning until 1849, three years after their marriage. As she later recounted, she "slipped the packet into his pocket" during a visit to Bagni di Lucca, Italy.

Published in 1850 as part of the revised two-volume *Poems* (Chapman & Hall), the sonnets appeared under the deliberately misleading title *Sonnets from the Portuguese*. Barrett Browning chose this title to disguise the autobiographical nature of the work, hoping readers would assume the poems were translations from the Portuguese rather than original love poems to her husband. The ruse was transparent to those who knew the couple: Robert Browning's pet name for Elizabeth was "the Portuguese," a reference to her dark complexion and her earlier poem "Catarina to Camoens," which featured a Portuguese heroine.

The 1850 edition of *Poems* established Barrett Browning as one of the most important living English poets. The *Athenaeum* praised the sonnets as "the noblest of her works," and the volume went through multiple editions. By the time of her death in 1861, *Sonnets from the Portuguese* had become the most widely read love-sonnet sequence in the English language — a position it has never relinquished.

## Critical Heritage

The sonnets' reception has evolved across three phases. In the Victorian period, they were celebrated as a sublime expression of wifely devotion — a reading that domesticated their radicalism. In the mid-twentieth century, feminist critics including Angela Leighton and Dorothy Mermin recovered the sequence as a pioneering work of female authorship: by adopting the Petrarchan sonnet — the quintessential form of male-authored love poetry — and infusing it with a woman's voice and experience, Barrett Browning staged a quiet revolution in literary history [001-013].

More recent scholarship, notably by Marjorie Stone and Simon Avery, has situated the sonnets within broader Victorian debates about gender, religion, and the body. The sequence's fusion of erotic and devotional language — the beloved as both lover and Christ-figure — reflects the influence of Tractarian sacramental theology on Barrett Browning's imagination. Contemporary criticism reads the sonnets not as a retreat from the political engagement of Barrett Browning's earlier work (notably *Aurora Leigh*), but as an extension of it: the personal is, in these poems, always already political.

## Place in the Victorian Canon

Together with Tennyson's *In Memoriam* (1850) and Arnold's *Empedocles on Etna* (1852), *Sonnets from the Portuguese* defines the high-water mark of Victorian poetry at mid-century. Where Tennyson's elegy traces the long arc of grief and Arnold's poem dramatises philosophical despair, Barrett Browning's sonnets chart the trajectory of love from doubt to affirmation — offering what Mermin calls "the century's most sustained poetic argument for the redemptive power of human affection."
