## Full Text

> My own Beloved, who hast lifted me [027-001]
> From this drear flat of earth where I was thrown, [027-002]
> And in betwixt the languid ringlets blown [027-003]
> A life-breath, till the forehead hopefully [027-004]
> Shines out again, as all the angels see, [027-005]
> Before thy saving kiss! My own, my own, [027-006]
> Who camest to me when the world was gone, [027-007]
> And I who looked for only God, found *thee*! [027-008]
> I find thee: I am safe, and strong, and glad. [027-009]
> As one who stands in dewless asphodel [027-010]
> Looks backward on the tedious time he had [027-011]
> In the upper life—so I, with bosom-swell, [027-012]
> Make witness, here, between the good and bad, [027-013]
> That Love, as strong as Death, retrieves as well. [027-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXVII [027-001]–[027-014]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXVII of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is a triumphant declaration of salvation through [[love-theme|love]]. The speaker who "looked for only [[god|God]]" found instead the be[[love-theme|love]]d — and this finding is not a disappointment but a fulfillment.

The octave narrates the be[[love-theme|love]]d's rescue: lifting the speaker from the "drear flat of earth," breathing life into her, making her forehead shine "as all the [[angel-imagery|angels]] see." The sestet's classical reference to asphodel — the flower of the Elysian fields — elevates the personal experience to mythic scale. The final couplet asserts the central claim: "Love, as strong as Death, retrieves as well."

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXVII is a pivotal affirmation, consolidating the redemptive arc. Where earlier sonnets question [[love-theme|love]]'s power, this sonnet declares it — with biblical force — as strong as [[death-theme|death]] itself.
