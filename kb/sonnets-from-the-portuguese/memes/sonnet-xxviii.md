## Full Text

> My letters! all dead paper, mute and white! [028-001]
> And yet they seem alive and quivering [028-002]
> Against my tremulous hands which loose the string [028-003]
> And let them drop down on my knee tonight. [028-004]
> This said, he wished to have me in his sight [028-005]
> Once, as a friend; this fixed a day in spring [028-006]
> To come and touch my hand—a simple thing, [028-007]
> Yet I wept for it!—this—the paper's light— [028-008]
> Said, *Dear, I love thee*; and I sank and quailed [028-009]
> As if God's future thundered on my past. [028-010]
> This said *I am thine*—and so its ink has paled [028-011]
> With lying at my heart that beat too fast; [028-012]
> And this—O Love, thy words have ill availed, [028-013]
> If what this said I dared repeat at last! [028-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXVIII [028-001]–[028-014]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXVIII of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is one of the sequence's most intimate and dramatic poems. The speaker holds the be[[love-theme|love]]d's letters — "all dead paper, mute and white" — which come alive in her hands as she rereads them.

The octave establishes the scene: letters untied, dropped on her knee at night. The sestet quotes three climactic phrases from the letters, each more intimate: the offer of friendship, the declaration "Dear, I [[love-theme|love]] thee," and the surrender "I am thine." The physical detail of ink paled "with lying at my [[heart-imagery|heart]] that beat too fast" renders the speaker's emotional response tangible.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXVIII is the epistolary [[heart-imagery|heart]] of the sequence, dramatizing the letters that mediate the [[love-theme|love]]rs' relationship. It anchors [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]]' abstractions in material texts, grounding [[love-theme|love]] in words on paper.
