## Definition

The hand is the most frequently invoked body part in *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*.
Appearing in 13 distinct PN paragraphs across the 44 sonnets, the hand-image operates
across a spectrum of meanings: the gracious hand that gives, the trembling hand that
reveals vulnerability, the enclasped hands that signify union, the master-hand that
creates, and the writing hand that generates [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]] itself. Unlike more
abstract imagery such as [[heaven-imagery|heaven]] or [[thought-imagery|thought]],
the hand is irreducibly physical — it is the body's primary instrument of contact,
and its recurring presence in [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]] charts the sequence's movement from
isolation toward reciprocated touch.

## Occurrences

### The gracious hand: gift and time

The hand enters [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]] in its opening lines through the mediated figure
of [[theocritus|Theocritus]]:

> Who each one in a gracious hand appears / To bear a gift for mortals, old or
> young [001-001]

This is not the speaker's hand but a pastoral, mythological hand — the hand of the
personified year, bearing gifts in the Theocritean tradition. The hand as gift-bearer
establishes a motif that will run through the entire sequence: [[love-theme|love]] as something
given, held in the hand, offered and received.

### The hand that cannot reach

The early sonnets emphasize the hand's limitation — its incapacity to bridge the
distance between speaker and Be[[love-theme|love]]d:

> Our hands would touch for all the mountain-bars [002-012]

In [[sonnet-ii|Sonnet II]], the separation between the [[love-theme|love]]rs is figured as a physical barrier
that hands cannot cross. This image of hands that *would* touch but *cannot* recurs
in the sequence's first half, where the speaker's illness and self-doubt make physical
contact seem impossible.

> nor lift my hand / Serenely in the sunshine as before [006-005]

Here the hand's immobility signals the speaker's emotional paralysis. The hand that
cannot lift is the hand that cannot write, cannot reach, cannot give — the hand of
depression before [[love-theme|love]]'s intervention.

### The hand as marketplace

[[sonnet-xix|Sonnet XIX]] deploys the hand in an extended mercantile metaphor:

> The soul's Rialto hath its merchandise; / I barter curl for curl upon that mart
> [019-001]

The exchange of locks of hair — her curl for his — transforms the hand into an
instrument of commerce, but a commerce of [[love-theme|love]] in which the currency is the body
itself. The "barter" carries erotic charge precisely because it involves the hand's
intimacy with the be[[love-theme|love]]d's person.

### The hand that strikes / the hand that heals

[[sonnet-xx|Sonnet XX]] introduces the hand's double valence — it can wound or save:

> Struck by thy possible hand — why, thus I drink / Of life's great cup of wonder
> [020-008]

"Struck by thy possible hand" condenses two opposed meanings: the hand as agent of
blow (struck) and the hand as source of possibility (possible hand). The ambiguity
is deliberate — [[love-theme|love]] in these sonnets is always both wound and wonder, and the hand
is the body part that most directly embodies this duality.

### The trembling hand

> Can I pour thy wine / While my hands tremble? [023-008]

The trembling hand in [[sonnet-xxiii|Sonnet XXIII]] reveals the speaker's emotional state without
requiring her to name it. The hands that tremble as she pours wine register the
intensity of her feeling — [[love-theme|love]] as physical incapacity, the body's betrayal of
composure. The question form ("Can I...?") underscores her self-doubt even as
the trembling hands answer it.

### The enclosing hand of Love

> Shut in upon itself and do no harm / In this close hand of Love, now soft and warm
> [024-003]

[[sonnet-xxiv|Sonnet XXIV]] reverses the hand's earlier trajectory from limitation to enclosure —
but enclosure figured as protection, not imprisonment. The "close hand of Love" is
the hand that holds without crushing, that closes to protect rather than to possess.
The adjectives "soft and warm" domesticate the earlier, more threatening hand imagery.

### The hand that is touched

The pivotal hand-moment occurs in [[sonnet-xxviii|Sonnet XXVIII]], which narrates [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]'s
first request to meet:

> To come and touch my hand — a simple thing, / Yet I wept for it! [028-007]

"A simple thing" — the phrase both acknowledges and ironizes the hand's ordinariness.
A hand-touch is the most minimal of physical contacts, yet for a woman who has been
confined by illness and social convention, it carries the weight of consummation.
The speaker weeps not from sadness but from the overwhelming reality of contact
after so much imagined distance.

### Master-hands and instruments

> 'Neath master-hands, from instruments defaced — [032-013]

The "master-hands" of [[sonnet-xxxii|Sonnet XXXII]] introduce the hand as creative agency. The image
is of a [[music-theme|musician]] drawing perfect [[music-theme|music]] from a flawed instrument — the hand as the
agent of transformation, turning damage into beauty. This implicitly analogizes
[[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]'s role in the speaker's life: the hand that touches her also calls
forth her poetic voice.

### The hand as offering

> Lay thy hand on it, best one, and allow / That no child's foot could run fast as
> this blood [034-013]

In [[sonnet-xxxiv|Sonnet XXXIV]], the speaker offers herself entirely: "Lay thy hand on it." The hand
here is the hand of acceptance, the hand that receives what is given. The comparison
to a child's running foot underscores the urgency and vitality of the offering.

### Enclasped hands: union

> Lest these enclasped hands should never hold [036-010]

The "enclasped hands" of [[sonnet-xxxvi|Sonnet XXXVI]] image the [[love-theme|love]]rs' joined hands — the hand
finally achieving what [[sonnet-ii|Sonnet II]]'s "mountain-bars" prevented. Yet the fear remains
("Lest... should never hold"), suggesting that even in union, the hand's grip is
tenuous.

### The writing hand

> The fingers of this hand wherewith I write [038-002]

In [[sonnet-xxxvi|Sonnet XXXVI]]II, the hand reveals its ultimate identity within the sequence: the
writing hand. The sonnet narrates [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]'s first [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] — on the fingers "of
this hand wherewith I write" — conflating the erotic hand and the authorial hand.
The hand that is [[kiss-imagery|kiss]]ed is the hand that writes the sonnet recording the [[kiss-imagery|kiss]]. The
meta-poetic loop is complete: the hand is both the subject of the poem and the
instrument of its creation.

## Literary Context

### Hands in the Petrarchan tradition

The [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]] tradition from which [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]] draws is rich in hand imagery.
Petrarch's *Rime Sparse* dwells on Laura's hand, Della Casa's sonnets anatomize
the be[[love-theme|love]]d's fingers, and Renaissance poets generally treated the hand as a
synecdoche for the be[[love-theme|love]]d's unattainable body. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s innovation is to shift
the hand from the *be[[love-theme|love]]d's* hand (the traditional focus of male poets) to the
*speaker's* hand — a female hand that trembles, writes, reaches, and offers.
This transposition transforms the hand from an object of erotic contemplation
into a site of female agency and vulnerability.

### Victorian tactility

The Victorian period's complex codes governing physical touch — the handshake, the
arm-in-arm promenade, the g[[love-theme|love]]d hand — inform [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s hand imagery. The "simple thing"
of a hand-touch [028-007] was, in Victorian [[browning-courtship|courtship]], anything but simple; it was
a regulated gesture freighted with social meaning. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s sonnets navigate this
terrain by making the hand's smallest movements — trembling, reaching, clasping —
carry the weight of erotic and emotional revelation.
