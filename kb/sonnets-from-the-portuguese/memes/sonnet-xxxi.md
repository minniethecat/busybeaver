## Full Text

> all is said without a word. [031-001]
> I sit beneath thy looks, as children do [031-002]
> In the noon-sun, with souls that tremble through [031-003]
> Their happy eyelids from an unaverred [031-004]
> Yet prodigal inward joy. [031-005]
> Behold, I erred [031-005]
> In that last doubt! [031-006]
> and yet I cannot rue [031-006]
> The sin most, but the occasion—that we two [031-007]
> Should for a moment stand unministered [031-008]
> By a mutual presence. [031-009]
> Ah, keep near and close, [031-009]
> Thou dove-like help! [031-010]
> and when my fears would rise, [031-010]
> With thy broad heart serenely interpose: [031-011]
> Brood down with thy divine sufficiencies [031-012]
> These thoughts which tremble when bereft of those, [031-013]
> Like callow birds left desert to the skies. [031-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXXI [031-001]–[031-018]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXXI is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form (octave ABBA ABBA, sestet with varied pattern).

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXXI occupies its position within the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief through surrender to transcendent [[love-theme|love]].
