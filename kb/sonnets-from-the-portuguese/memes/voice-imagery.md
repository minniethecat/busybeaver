## Definition

Voice is the medium of utterance in *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* — the faculty
through which command is issued, [[love-theme|love]] is declared, [[grief-theme|grief]] is wept, and poetry is
sung. The sequence's 8 voice-instances trace a trajectory from external to internal,
from command to dialogue, from mastery to vulnerability. The voice that first
appears as a mysterious external command in [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]] ("a voice said in mastery")
becomes, by [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LI, the speaker's own voice breaking with sobs. This
trajectory mirrors [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]]'s broader movement from passivity to
agency, from being addressed to addressing.

Voice-imagery is intimately linked to the [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|sonnet form]] itself: a sonnet is
literally a "little [[music-theme|song]]" (Italian *sonetto*), a small vocal utterance. The
sonnet sequence's obsessive return to voice — who speaks, who listens, who
commands, who weeps — reflects its fundamental condition as a text that
records and recreates the sound of a woman thinking aloud about [[love-theme|love]].

## Occurrences

### The commanding voice

> And a voice said in mastery, while I strove: / "Guess now who holds thee!" —
> "Death!" I said. But there, / The silver answer rang: "Not Death, but Love."
> [001-012]

[[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]]'s climactic voice is the sequence's most dramatic acoustic event. It is
disembodied ("a voice"), authoritative ("in mastery"), and dialogical — it demands
a response, and receives one. The speaker's own voice ("'Death!' I said") answers
from within the [[dream|vision]], only to be corrected by the "silver answer." Three voices
intertwine in three lines: the commanding voice, the speaker's frightened response,
and the corrective "silver answer." The sonnet's acoustic architecture — question,
answer, correction — establishes voice as the primary medium of revelation.

The passage also establishes voice's intimacy with error: the speaker's "Death!"
is wrong, and voice corrects it. Throughout the sequence, voice will be the
faculty through which truths are spoken *to* the speaker, against her own
misapprehensions.

### The inner voice

> Of desolation! there's a voice within / That weeps as — thou must sing — alone,
> aloof [004-013]

[[sonnet-iv|Sonnet IV]] introduces the voice's interiorization. The "voice within" is distinct
from the external commanding voice of [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]] — it "weeps" rather than commands,
and it speaks not to the speaker but *within* her. The parenthetical "thou must
sing" sets the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[music-theme|singing]] against the inner voice's [[tear-imagery|weeping]]: external
[[music-theme|music]] vs. internal lament. The voice "within" is the voice of the speaker's own
[[grief-theme|sorrow]], her illness, and her fear of unworthiness — a voice that will gradually
be displaced as the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice gains authority.

### The voice that binds

> No moment at thy voice, but link by link / Went counting all my chains [020-005]

In [[sonnet-xx|Sonnet XX]], the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice is a binding force. The speaker hears "thy
voice" and immediately inventories her chains — the voice does not free her;
it makes her aware of her bondage. Yet the counting is also a form of worship:
"as if that so / They never could fall off at any blow" — the chains counted
at his voice become permanent, even desirable. Voice transforms constraint
into devotion.

### The doubtful voice

> By a doubtful spirit-voice, in that doubt's pain / Cry, "Speak once more —
> thou lovest!" [021-008]

[[sonnet-xxi|Sonnet XXI]] introduces the "doubtful [[soul-theme|spirit]]-voice" — a voice that hovers between
revelation and delusion, [[soul-theme|spirit]] and self. The demand "Speak once more — thou
[[love-theme|love]]st!" reveals the voice's deepest function in the sequence: it is the carrier
of the declaration of [[love-theme|love]]. The speaker needs to *hear* [[love-theme|love]] spoken, not merely
know it. The sonnet's repeated "[[love-theme|love]] me, [[love-theme|love]] me, [[love-theme|love]] me" [021-010] is itself
a voice demanding voice.

### The voice of vow

> I hear thy voice and vow / Perplexed, uncertain, since thou art out of sight
> [030-007]

In [[sonnet-xxx|Sonnet XXX]], the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice carries his vow — but the vow is "perplexed,
uncertain" because he is "out of sight." Voice without presence is incomplete,
even treacherous. The simile that follows — "As he, in his swooning ears, the
choir's amen" — likens the speaker to a worshipper hearing the sacred word
through a faint. The voice is real, the vow is true, but the medium (sound
without sight) is inherently unstable.

### Fond voices reconciled

> Fond voices, which, being drawn and reconciled / Into the music of Heaven's
> undefiled, / Call me no longer. [033-006]

[[sonnet-xxxiii|Sonnet XXXIII]] (numbered XXXI in some editions) presents voice's transcendence
and its cost. The "fond voices" — familiar, be[[love-theme|love]]d, intimate — are "drawn and
reconciled" into heavenly [[music-theme|music]], but this reconciliation [[silence]]s them: "Call me
no longer." The passage is the sequence's darkest reflection on voice: what if
the voices that once called to us, [[love-theme|love]]d us, are absorbed into a harmony so pure
that they cease to be individually audible? Heaven's [[music-theme|music]] comes at the cost of
particular voices.

### The voice that sinks and falls

> But thou, who, in my voice's sink and fall / When the sob took it, thy divinest
> Art's / Own instrument didst drop down at thy foot [041-007]

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LI returns voice to the speaker — but it is a voice in collapse. "My
voice's sink and fall / When the sob took it" describes the acoustic of
breakdown: the voice descending in pitch, interrupted by sobs. Yet the be[[love-theme|love]]d
receives even this broken voice as "thy divinest Art's / Own instrument," and
his response is to drop his own instrument — to stop [[music-theme|singing]], to stop playing,
and to listen. The voice that sinks and falls achieves what the confident,
commanding voice of [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]] could not: it moves the listener to [[silence]].

## Literary Context

### Voice and the female poet

For a Victorian woman poet, voice was a particularly charged concept. The
convention that women should be [[silence|silent]] in public — that a "lady's" voice
belonged to the domestic sphere — made every act of poetic publication an
act of transgression. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]] negotiates this constraint by making voice itself
a subject of her poetry. The sequence records the process by which a [[silence]]d
woman recovers her voice — but it is a voice marked by "sink and fall" [041-007],
by [[tear-imagery|weeping]], by doubt. The female poet's voice does not need to be stentorian to
be powerful; its very vulnerability is its authority.

### Voice in the Petrarchan tradition

Petrarch's *Rime Sparse* is obsessed with the sound of Laura's name ("L'aura"),
the pun that conflates the be[[love-theme|love]]d, the breeze, and poetic fame. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s voice-
imagery inverts this tradition: the voice that matters is not the be[[love-theme|love]]d's
melodious name but the speaker's own utterance — her counting, her crying,
her "sink and fall." Voice in Petrarch is an object of male desire; in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]],
it is the medium of female agency.
