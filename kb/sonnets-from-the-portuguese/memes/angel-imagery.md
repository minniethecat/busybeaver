# Angel (Imagery)

## Definition

The angel in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* is not a doctrinal assertion but a poetic figure for the be[[love-theme|love]]d's transformative power. Drawing on the Petrarchan tradition of the *donna angelicata* (the angelic lady who leads the [[love-theme|love]]r toward [[god|God]]), [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] reworks the convention: her be[[love-theme|love]]d is not a distant, unattainable ideal but a present, embodied being whose presence is itself angelic — mediating, revealing, and transforming. The angel appears in several sonnets as a figure of mediation between the human and the divine, between [[grief-theme|grief]] and joy.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

Angelic imagery appears across at least seven sonnets, clustering in the middle section where the sequence explores the nature of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's transformative presence.

### The Beloved as Angelic Mediator

The be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice and presence carry angelic qualities — not because the be[[love-theme|love]]d is literally an angel but because [[love-theme|love]] makes the be[[love-theme|love]]d a channel of divine grace. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s angels descend not to judge but to reveal: they bring the speaker from [[silence]] into [[music-theme|song]], from isolation into communion. The angelic figure thus functions as a metaphor for [[love-theme|love]]'s mediating power — the be[[love-theme|love]]d stands between the speaker and [[god|God]], making the divine accessible through human [[love-theme|love]].

### Petrarchan Convention Transformed

In the Petrarchan tradition, the angelic lady is unattainable — her beauty points toward heaven but cannot be possessed on earth. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] subverts this: her be[[love-theme|love]]d is attainable, and the angelic imagery describes not distance but proximity. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's angelic quality is not that he is remote but that he brings heaven close.

### Angel and Soul

The angel imagery intersects with the [[soul-theme|soul]] imagery throughout the sequence. Where the [[soul-theme|soul]] is the interior self, the angel is the external presence that awakens it. The be[[love-theme|love]]d does not replace the speaker's [[soul-theme|soul]] but calls it forth, much as the angel Gabriel called Mary to her vocation.

## Literary Context

The *donna angelicata* tradition originates in Dante's *Vita Nuova* and *Divine Comedy*, where Beatrice leads the pilgrim toward [[god|God]]. Petrarch adapted this into the *Canzoniere*, where Laura's [[death-theme|death]] makes her eternally angelic and eternally lost. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to make the angelic be[[love-theme|love]]d alive, present, and reciprocal — a Victorian Protestant re[[dream|vision]] of a Catholic medieval trope.
