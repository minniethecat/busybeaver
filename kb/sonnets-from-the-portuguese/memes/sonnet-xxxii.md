## Full Text

> The first time that the sun rose on thine oath [032-001]
> To love me, I looked forward to the moon [032-002]
> To slacken all those bonds which seemed too soon [032-003]
> And quickly tied to make a lasting troth. [032-004]
> Quick-loving hearts, I thought, may quickly loathe; [032-005]
> And, looking on myself, I seemed not one [032-006]
> For such man’s love! [032-007]
> —more like an out-of-tune [032-007]
> Worn viol, a good singer would be wroth [032-008]
> To spoil his song with, and which, snatched in haste, [032-009]
> Is laid down at the first ill-sounding note. [032-010]
> I did not wrong myself so, but I placed [032-011]
> A wrong on thee. [032-012]
> For perfect strains may float [032-012]
> ’Neath master-hands, from instruments defaced— [032-013]
> And great souls, at one stroke, may do and dote. [032-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXXII [032-001]–[032-016]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXXII is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form (octave ABBA ABBA, sestet with varied pattern).

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXXII occupies its position within the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief through surrender to transcendent [[love-theme|love]].
