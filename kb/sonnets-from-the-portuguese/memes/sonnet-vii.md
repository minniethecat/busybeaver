## Full Text

> The face of all the world is changed, I think, [007-001]
>  [007-002]
> Since first I heard the footsteps of thy soul [007-003]
>  [007-004]
> Move still, oh, still, beside me, as they stole [007-005]
>  [007-006]
> Betwixt me and the dreadful outer brink [007-007]
>  [007-008]
> Of obvious death, where I, who thought to sink [007-009]
>  [007-010]
> Was caught up into love, and taught the whole [007-011]
>  [007-012]
> Of life in a new rhythm. The cup of dole [007-013]
>  [007-014]
> God gave for baptism, I am fain to drink, [007-015]
>  [007-016]
> And praise its sweetness, Sweet, with thee anear. [007-017]
>  [007-018]
> The names of country, heaven, are changed away [007-019]
>  [007-020]
> For where thou art or shalt be, there or here; [007-021]
>  [007-022]
> And this—this lute and song—loved yesterday, [007-023]
>  [007-024]
> (The singing angels know) are only dear, [007-025]
>  [007-026]
> Because thy name moves right in what they say. [007-027]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet VII [007-001]–[007-027]

## Analysis

Sonnet VII is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The poem continues the sequence's exploration of [[love-theme|love]], devotion, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transformation.

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form (octave ABBA ABBA, sestet with varied pattern), employing [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s characteristic blend of intimate address and rhetorical elevation.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet VII occupies its position within the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief through surrender to transcendent [[love-theme|love]]. Each sonnet contributes a distinct emotional or rhetorical development to this larger narrative.

## Literary Context

Sonnet VII announces a world transformed. "The face of all the world is changed, I think" [007-001] — the qualifying "I think" is characteristically [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]: the speaker marks her own experience as subjective even as she declares its totality. This sonnet picks up the motif of [[light-imagery]] first introduced in the early sonnets and develops it into a sustained metaphor for [[love-theme|love]]'s perceptual effects.

The be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] — "a cup of dole / [[god|God]] gave for baptism" [007-005]–[007-007] — combines the sacramental with the sensory. The word "dole" carries its older meaning of portion or gift, but the resonance of [[grief-theme|sorrow]] (as in "doleful") is also present. The [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] that should be pleasure is mediated through [[grief-theme|grief]] and divine ordination. This double registration — pleasure and pain, gift and burden — is characteristic of the sequence's emotional complexity.

## Structure and Prosody

The sonnet's octave builds a cumulative case for transformation, listing the signs of change in the be[[love-theme|love]]d and the world. The sestet's shift at line 9 moves from observation to analysis, explaining the source of the change in terms of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] and the speaker's [[grief-theme|grief]].

The poem's sonic texture is notably liquid: "[[god|God]]'s gifts," "greeting," "[[grief-theme|grief]]" [007-005]–[007-010] create an alliterative chain that threads through the octave and into the sestet.

## Themes

### Transformation through Love

The central claim — that [[love-theme|love]] has changed how the world looks — is the first explicit statement in the sequence of [[love-theme|love]]'s transformative power. Earlier sonnets have described the speaker's unworthiness and the be[[love-theme|love]]d's splendour; here, the speaker claims that [[love-theme|love]] has actually altered perception itself. This anticipates the sequence's later, more confident declarations.

### Kiss as Sacrament

The [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] described as "a cup of dole / [[god|God]] gave for baptism" [007-005]–[007-007] merges erotic and religious imagery. The [[kiss-imagery]] throughout the sequence often carries this sacramental valence, and Sonnet VII is one of its earliest and most explicit deployments.
