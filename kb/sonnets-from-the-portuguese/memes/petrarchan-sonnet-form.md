# Petrarchan Sonnet Form

## Definition

The Petrarchan (or Italian) sonnet is a fourteen-line poem in iambic pentameter, divided into an octave (first eight lines, rhymed ABBA ABBA) and a sestet (final six lines, rhymed CDC DCD or CDE CDE). The form derives its name from the 14th-century Italian poet Petrarch (Francesco Petrarca), whose *Canzoniere* — 366 poems addressed to his be[[love-theme|love]]d Laura — established the sonnet as the pre-eminent form of European [[love-theme|love]] poetry. The Petrarchan sonnet's defining structural feature is the *volta* (turn): a shift in argument, perspective, or emotional register between the octave and sestet, typically occurring at line 9.

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s 44 sonnets adhere to the Petrarchan form with occasional variations in the sestet rhyme scheme. She exploits the volta's potential for emotional reversal — the octave often presents doubt or [[grief-theme|grief]], while the sestet resolves into affirmation or transcendence — making the form's structure mirror the sequence's larger movement from [[death-theme|death]] to [[love-theme|love]], [[silence]] to [[music-theme|song]].

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

All 44 sonnets in *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* use the Petrarchan form. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s handling of the form is notable for:

### The Volta as Emotional Pivot

The turn between octave and sestet often dramatises the sequence's core tension between past [[grief-theme|grief]] and present [[love-theme|love]]. [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]]'s octave establishes the speaker's [[death-theme|death]]-identified self, while the sestet introduces [[love-theme|love]]'s transforming answer. [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII's octave announces the counting of [[love-theme|love]]'s ways, while the sestet carries the enumeration into its transcendent conclusion: "I shall but [[love-theme|love]] thee better after [[death-theme|death]]" [043-014].

### Rhyme Scheme Variations

While the octave is consistently ABBA ABBA, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] varies the sestet rhyme scheme, most commonly using CDC DCD (the Italian pattern) but occasionally employing CDE CDE or CDCDCD. These variations are not random — tighter rhyme schemes often accompany moments of emotional intensity.

## Literary Context

The Petrarchan sonnet entered English poetry through Wyatt and Surrey in the 16th century but was largely displaced by the Shakespearean (English) sonnet (ABAB CDCD EFEF GG). The Romantic revival of the Petrarchan form — particularly in Wordsworth's sonnets — provided [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s immediate model. Her innovation lies in combining the Petrarchan form's emotional range with a distinctively Victorian, female voice: the sonnet that had for centuries been the vehicle of male desire becomes, in her hands, the medium of female agency and reciprocity.
