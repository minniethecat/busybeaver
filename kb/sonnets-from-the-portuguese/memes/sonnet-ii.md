## Full Text

> But only three in all God's universe [002-001]
> Have heard this word thou hast said—Himself, beside [002-002]
> Thee speaking, and me listening! and replied [002-003]
> One of us—*that* was God!—and laid the curse [002-004]
> So darkly on my eyelids, as to amerce [002-005]
> My sight from seeing thee—that if I had died, [002-006]
> The deathweights placed there would have signified [002-007]
> Less absolute exclusion. "Nay" is worse [002-008]
> From God than from all others, O my friend! [002-009]
> Men could not part us with their worldly jars, [002-010]
> Nor the seas change us, nor the tempests bend: [002-011]
> Our hands would touch for all the mountain-bars— [002-012]
> And, heaven being rolled between us at the end, [002-013]
> We should but vow the faster for the stars. [002-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet II [002-001]–[002-014]

## Analysis

Sonnet II is the second of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850). The sonnet opens with a striking theological claim: only three beings in the universe have heard the be[[love-theme|love]]d's word — [[god|God]], the be[[love-theme|love]]d, and the speaker herself.

The octave (lines 1–8) develops the theme of divine exclusion, with [[god|God]]'s "Nay" carrying a weight heavier than [[death-theme|death]]. The sestet (lines 9–14) shifts to defiance, asserting that no force — worldly jars, seas, tempests, mountain-bars, or even heaven itself — can separate the [[love-theme|love]]rs. The sonnet thus introduces the sequence's recurring tension between religious authority and human [[love-theme|love]].

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet II follows [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]]'s invocation of [[theocritus|Theocritus]], pivoting from literary tradition to personal address. It establishes the central conflict of the sequence: the speaker's struggle against forces — divine, temporal, and spatial — that would separate her from the be[[love-theme|love]]d.

## Literary Context

Sonnet II follows [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]]'s moment of recognition — where the speaker first hears the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice — and dramatises the consequences of that moment. Angels speaks of being "drawn by the [[soul-theme|spirit]] of [[love-theme|love]]" and "three god" in neighbouring poems [041-003], but here in Sonnet II, [[god|God]] himself has singled out the speaker for a special exclusion. This is unique in the sequence: no other sonnet contains a scene of divine intervention so direct and personal.

The conceit is built on a paradox. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's word has been heard by only [[god|God]], the be[[love-theme|love]]d, and the speaker [002-001]–[002-003]. This makes the speaker one of only three beings in the universe who have heard this utterance — a privilege, yet also a curse, because [[god|God]]'s "Nay" is the heaviest of all [002-008]. The theological weight of the sonnet distinguishes it from the more purely romantic or Petrarchan conventions elsewhere in the sequence.

Dorothy Mermin, in her study of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s poetics, notes that the sequence repeatedly invokes divine authority only to challenge it. Sonnet II establishes this pattern: [[god|God]]'s voice ("Nay") is placed in direct opposition to human [[love-theme|love]], and the sestet answers not with submission but with defiance [002-009]–[002-014].

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet II follows the Petrarchan form with the rhyme scheme ABBA ABBA CDC DCD. The volta at line 9 ("Men could not part us...") [002-010] marks the structural shift from octave to sestet, and simultaneously from theological lament to humanist defiance.

The octave uses heavy enjambment — "as to amerce / My sight" [002-005]–[002-006], "had died, / The [[death-theme|death]]weights placed" [002-006]–[002-007] — to create a breathless, cumulative effect appropriate to the speaker's distress. The sestet, by contrast, uses end-stopped lines and anaphoric parallelism ("Nor the seas... nor the tempests" [002-011]) to convey steady, resolute conviction.

Key prosodic features:
- The caesura in "One of us—*that* was [[god|God]]!" [002-004] creates a dramatic mid-line pause, with italics emphasising the shocking identification
- The rhyme pair "friend" / "bend" / "end" [002-009], [002-011], [002-013] threads the sestet together sonically
- The final couplet effect of the closing lines [002-013]–[002-014] — though not a true Shakespearean couplet — provides rhetorical closure

## Themes

### Divine Authority and Human Love

The poem's central tension is between [[god|God]]'s decree and human [[love-theme|love]]. The speaker accepts the divine power to separate ("laid the curse / So darkly on my eyelids" [002-004]–[002-005]) but refuses to accept that separation as final. The sestet catalogues forces that cannot part the [[love-theme|love]]rs — worldly, natural, cosmic — culminating in the paradoxical claim that heaven's intervention only strengthens their bond [002-013]–[002-014].

This tension between [[religious-faith]] and [[love-theme]] runs throughout the sequence, but in Sonnet II it is presented with particular theological specificity.

### Sight and Blindness

The curse is placed specifically on the speaker's eyes [002-005], connecting to the broader imagery of [[dream|vision]] and [[light-imagery]] in the sequence. The "[[death-theme|death]]weights" that would close the speaker's eyes in [[death-theme|death]] "would have signified / Less absolute exclusion" [002-007]–[002-008] — the divine curse on sight is worse than [[death-theme|death]] itself. This prepares for the sequence's recurring motif of looking, being seen, and being blinded, which reaches its climax in [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII.

### Cosmic Separation

The sestet presents a geographical and cosmic catalogue of barriers — "worldly jars," seas, tempests, mountain-bars, and heaven itself [002-010]–[002-014]. This rhetorical strategy of listing adversities and overcoming each one is characteristic of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s argumentative poetics. The progression from mundane (worldly jars) to cosmic (heaven) mirrors the octave's movement from the personal to the theological.
