## Full Text

> I think of thee!—my thoughts do twine and bud [029-001]
> About thee, as wild vines about a tree [029-002]
> Put out broad leaves, and soon there's nought to see [029-003]
> Except the straggling green which hides the wood. [029-004]
> Yet, O my palm-tree, be it understood [029-005]
> I will not have my thoughts instead of thee [029-006]
> Who art dearer, better! Rather, instantly [029-007]
> Renew thy presence; as a strong tree should, [029-008]
> Rustle thy boughs and set thy trunk all bare, [029-009]
> And let these bands of greenery which insphere thee [029-010]
> Drop heavily down—burst, shattered, everywhere! [029-011]
> Because, in this deep joy to see and hear thee [029-012]
> And breathe within thy shadow a new air, [029-013]
> I do not think of thee—I am too near thee. [029-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXIX [029-001]–[029-014]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXIX of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is one of the sequence's most be[[love-theme|love]]d and virtuosic sonnets. It opens with thoughts that "twine and bud" about the be[[love-theme|love]]d like wild vines about a tree — a metaphor of obsessive mental occupation.

But the speaker immediately rejects this: "I will not have my thoughts instead of thee / Who art dearer, better!" The sestet demands the be[[love-theme|love]]d's real presence, commanding the metaphorical palm-tree to shake off its vines. The final couplet delivers the sequence's most famous turn: "I do not think of thee—I am too near thee." Presence replaces thought; proximity renders reflection unnecessary.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXIX is a thematic peak, dramatizing the movement from meditation to presence, from absence to union. Its vine-and-tree metaphor is among the sequence's most memorable images.
