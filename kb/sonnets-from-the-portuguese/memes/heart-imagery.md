# Heart (Imagery)

## Definition

The heart in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* is the embodied register of [[love-theme|love]] — the physical organ that bears [[grief-theme|grief]]'s weight and [[love-theme|love]]'s [[light-imagery|light]]ness. Unlike the [[soul-theme|soul]] (which is [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual and eternal), the heart is mortal, vulnerable, and intensely physical. The sequence opens with the speaker's heart as an object she offers: "I lift my heavy heart up solemnly" [005-001]. Throughout [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]], the heart is lifted, broken, conveyed, and ultimately given. It is the site where [[love-theme|love]] is felt before it is understood, and its movements — heaviness, trembling, expansion — trace the speaker's emotional trajectory more directly than any abstract language of emotion.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

The heart appears in over fifteen sonnets, making it one of the sequence's most recurrent images.

### The Heavy Heart: Sonnets V and XI

The heart first appears as a burden: "I lift my heavy heart up solemnly" [005-001]. The gesture is liturgical — the speaker presents her heart as an offering, though it is weighed down by past [[grief-theme|grief]] ("What a great heap of [[grief-theme|grief]] lay hid in me" [005-005]). The heart's heaviness is not merely sadness but accumulated [[grief-theme|sorrow]] that [[love-theme|love]] must overcome.

[[sonnet-xi|Sonnet XI]] continues this register: the speaker is called "to bear the burden of a heavy heart" [011-004]. The heart's weight is both literal ([[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s chronic illness) and metaphorical (the emotional cost of loving after prolonged isolation).

### The Heart as Conveyance: Sonnets VI and XIII

In [[sonnet-vi|Sonnet VI]], the speaker describes her heart as inseparable from the be[[love-theme|love]]d: "what takes to part us, leaves thy heart in mine" [006-009]. The image suggests a mutual indwelling — the be[[love-theme|love]]d's heart resides within the speaker's, and separation is ontologically impossible.

[[sonnet-xiii|Sonnet XIII]] intensifies this image: "Lest one touch of this heart convey its [[grief-theme|grief]]" [013-014]. The heart is not merely a container of feeling but a transmitter — it can pass [[grief-theme|grief]] through touch alone. This suggests a nearly sacramental understanding of physical presence: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's touch would communicate the speaker's [[grief-theme|sorrow]] without words.

### The Heart's Unfolding: Sonnets VIII and XXXV

[[sonnet-viii|Sonnet VIII]] uses floral imagery to describe the heart's opening: the be[[love-theme|love]]d can "read the purple of thine heart, unstained, untold" [008-003]. The heart is a flower whose color reveals its inner state — "purple" suggesting both royalty and passion. That the heart is "unstained" and "untold" emphasises its purity and the unprecedented nature of its disclosure.

[[sonnet-xxxv|Sonnet XXXV]] returns to the heart's vulnerability. The speaker, hardened by [[grief-theme|grief]], asks: "Open thy heart wide, / And fold within the wet-winged" [035-013]. The heart is asked to be a shelter — to open and receive what is fragile. The image of "wet-winged" suggests a bird battered by rain, seeking refuge in the be[[love-theme|love]]d's heart.

## Literary Context

The heart's prominence in [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]] reflects the Victorian confluence of Romantic emotionalism and Tractarian sacramentalism. The heart as physical organ of feeling owes much to the Romantics (Keats's "heart high-[[grief-theme|sorrow]]ful and cloyed"), while the heart as site of divine encounter draws on the Catholic tradition of the Sacred Heart, mediated through [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s Broad Church sensibilities. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to insist on the heart's physicality — it is not a disembodied metaphor but a living organ whose heaviness, trembling, and opening are felt in the body.
