## Analysis

Sonnet IV is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850), composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]].

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form, with an octave (ABBA ABBA) and a sestet. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s voice blends intimate address with formal elevation, characteristic of the sequence.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet IV contributes to the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief and melancholy through surrender and reciprocal devotion to transcendent [[love-theme|love]].

## Literary Context

Sonnet IV continues the sequence's early argument about the [[love-theme|love]]rs' inequality, but shifts from [[sonnet-iii|Sonnet III]]'s emphasis on social difference to a contrast between the be[[love-theme|love]]d's public splendour and the speaker's private desolation. The be[[love-theme|love]]d is a [[music-theme|musician]] performing at "some palace-floor" [004-001], while the speaker occupies a ruined house with "bats and owlets builders in the roof" [004-019].

This architectural imagery is notable for its autobiographical resonance. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] was an invalid who rarely left her room at 50 Wimpole Street, while [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] moved freely in London society. The "casement broken in" [004-017] and the latch "too poor / For hand of thine" [004-009]–[004-010] are metaphors for the speaker's physical and social confinement. Margaret Forster's biography of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] notes that the poet's self-characterisation as a ruin was both psychologically accurate and strategically deployed — it allowed her to test [[robert-browning|Browning]]'s commitment by presenting her worst self first.

The poem is the first in the sequence to explicitly deploy the [[music-theme|music]] metaphor that will recur throughout: "high poems," "thy [[music-theme|music]]," "thy mandolin" [004-003], [004-014], [004-021]. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's art is refined and public; the speaker's is the "cricket" chirping against this mandolin [004-021] — small, natural, private. This contrast between two kinds of artistic voice is developed across the sequence and reaches its resolution in [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet IV follows the Petrarchan form (ABBA ABBA CDCDCD). The octave poses a series of rhetorical questions about the be[[love-theme|love]]d's condescension to visit a "poor" house [004-009]–[004-015]; the sestet turns imperative ("Look up" [004-017]) and catalogues the evidence of ruin.

The octave's syntax is notably complex. Lines [004-005]–[004-008] contain a subordinate clause embedded within a rhetorical question: the dancers break footing "from the care / Of watching up thy pregnant lips for more." This layered syntax mirrors the speaker's self-consciousness — she cannot simply state her unworthiness, she must perform it through rhetorical intricacy.

The sestet's final tercet breaks the descriptive catalogue with an imperative and a revelation: "Hush! call no echo up in further proof / Of desolation!" [004-023]–[004-024]. The dash in the final line — "as—thou must sing—alone, aloof" [004-027] — creates three breathless units that collapse the poem's accumulated distance into a single, isolated utterance.

### Key Prosodic Features

- The spondee "preg-nant lips" [004-007] draws attention to the be[[love-theme|love]]d's creative power
- The enjambment between [004-011]–[004-012] ("bear / To let") enacts the strain of asking the question
- The sestet's rhyme scheme CDCDCD produces a more insistent, less resolved rhythm than the Shakespearean variant
- The word "alone, aloof" [004-027] closes the poem on a double stress that isolates the speaker in sound as well as sense

## Themes

### Music and Art

The [[music-theme|music]] metaphor structures the entire poem. The be[[love-theme|love]]d is the "gracious singer of high poems" [004-003]; his [[music-theme|music]] is carried by a mandolin [004-021], an elegant and courtly instrument. The speaker's art, by contrast, is represented by a chirping cricket [004-021] — natural, unpolished, humble. This anticipates the sequence's broader meditation on art, fame, and the gendered politics of creative work. See also [[music-theme]].

### The Ruined House

The ruined house with broken windows, bats, and owls [004-017]–[004-019] is a complex figure. It represents the speaker's invalid body, her social isolation, and in a broader Romantic sense, the ruins of her former poetic self. The house that cannot properly receive its noble visitor mirrors the speaker who cannot believe herself worthy of [[love-theme|love]].

### Self-Diminishment

Sonnet IV is perhaps the most self-abasing poem in the first half of the sequence. The speaker characterises herself as a ruin, a cricket, a voice that weeps [004-025]. This rhetoric of self-diminishment has been read by feminist critics as both a genuine expression of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s insecurities and as a rhetorical strategy — by presenting the worst, she clears the ground for the genuine mutuality that develops later in the sequence.
