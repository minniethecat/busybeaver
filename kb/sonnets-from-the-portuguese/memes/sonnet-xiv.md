## Overview

Sonnet XIV of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is second only to [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII in its popularity and quotability. The poem advances a radical argument for unconditional [[love-theme|love]], cataloguing the perishable reasons one might be [[love-theme|love]]d — only to reject each in favour of a [[love-theme|love]] grounded in nothing but itself.

## Full Text

> If thou must love me, let it be for nought [014-001]
> Except for love's sake only. Do not say [014-002]
> "I love her for her smile—her look—her way [014-003]
> Of speaking gently—for a trick of thought [014-004]
> That falls in well with mine, and certes brought [014-005]
> A sense of pleasant ease on such a day,"— [014-006]
> For these things in themselves, Beloved, may [014-007]
> Be changed, or change for thee—and love so wrought [014-008]
> May be unwrought so. Neither love me for [014-009]
> Thine own dear pity's wiping my cheeks dry: [014-010]
> A creature might forget to weep, who bore [014-011]
> Thy comfort long, and lose thy love thereby. [014-012]
> But love me for love's sake, that evermore [014-013]
> Thou mayst love on through love's eternity. [014-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XIV [014-001]–[014-014]

## Analysis

### The Negative Catalogue

The octave opens with a conditional command that borders on paradox: "If thou must [[love-theme|love]] me, let it be for nought / Except for [[love-theme|love]]'s sake only" [014-001]–[014-002]. The word "nought" here means "nothing" — the speaker asks to be [[love-theme|love]]d for no reason at all, or rather for the only reason that is not a reason: [[love-theme|love]] itself.

The speaker then ventriloquises the be[[love-theme|love]]d's hypothetical protest, placing his anticipated words in quotation marks: "I [[love-theme|love]] her for her smile—her look—her way / Of speaking gently" [014-003]–[014-004]. This is a remarkable rhetorical move: the speaker pre-emptively denies the be[[love-theme|love]]d any of the conventional grounds for [[love-theme|love]]. Each item in the catalogue — smile, look, gentle speech, "a trick of thought / That falls in well with mine" [014-004]–[014-005] — is a perishable quality, subject to change or disappearance. The argument is relentlessly logical: if [[love-theme|love]] is based on mutable qualities, then when those qualities change, [[love-theme|love]] "so wrought / May be unwrought so" [014-008]–[014-009].

### The Rejection of Pity

The sestet's first movement adds pity to the catalogue of unacceptable motives. "Neither [[love-theme|love]] me for / Thine own dear pity's wiping my cheeks dry" [014-009]–[014-010] — the speaker acknowledges her own [[tear-imagery|tears]] (a persistent motif in the sequence) but refuses to let them become the basis of [[love-theme|love]]. The argument is again logical: if [[love-theme|love]] depends on the be[[love-theme|love]]d's need for comfort, then recovery would paradoxically destroy the [[love-theme|love]] it created. "A creature might forget to weep, who bore / Thy comfort long, and lose thy [[love-theme|love]] thereby" [014-011]–[014-012].

This rejection of pity-as-[[love-theme|love]] has biographical resonance. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett]] was an invalid when [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] first wrote to her; the idea that his [[love-theme|love]] might be charity rather than genuine attraction was a real anxiety underlying the sequence's persistent themes of unworthiness.

### The Imperative Conclusion

The closing couplet — "But [[love-theme|love]] me for [[love-theme|love]]'s sake, that evermore / Thou mayst [[love-theme|love]] on through [[love-theme|love]]'s eternity" [014-013]–[014-014] — resolves the negative argument into a positive command. "Love's eternity" is a deliberately paradoxical phrase: eternity is usually associated with the divine, but here it belongs to [[love-theme|love]] itself. The poem that began by rejecting every reason for [[love-theme|love]] ends by discovering the one reason that is not a reason — [[love-theme|love]] as self-grounding, [[love-theme|love]] as its own justification.

## Literary Context

Sonnet XIV participates in a philosophical tradition stretching back to Plato's *Symposium*, where [[love-theme|love]] is figured as desire for the eternal possession of the good. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to relocate this desire from the abstract to the personal: the eternity she seeks is not the transcendence of the physical but the permanence of a specific human [[love-theme|love]].

The poem also engages with Shakespeare's [[sonnet-xi|Sonnet 11]]6 ("Let me not to the marriage of true minds / Admit impediments"), which similarly argues for [[love-theme|love]]'s immutability. But where Shakespeare posits [[love-theme|love]] as an "ever-fixèd mark" that "looks on tempests and is never shaken," [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s speaker is more anxious: she knows [[love-theme|love]] *can* change — "these things in themselves, Be[[love-theme|love]]d, may / Be changed" [014-007]–[014-008] — and her argument is a plea against that possibility.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XIV employs a modified Petrarchan form. The octave is ABBA ABBA, but the sestet shifts to CDCDCD — a scheme that eliminates the closing couplet in favour of interlocking tercets. This creates a seamless forward momentum in the sestet that mirrors the poem's logical unfolding.

Key prosodic moments:
- The feminine ending of "nought" [014-001] softens the imperative, turning command into supplication
- The quoted speech in [014-003]–[014-006] is distinguished by [[light-imagery|light]]er, more conversational rhythms, contrasting with the poem's otherwise elevated register
- The enjambment across the octave-sestet boundary [014-008]–[014-009] ("[[love-theme|love]] so wrought / May be unwrought so") makes the volta less a turn than a continuation, enacting the argument's cumulative force

## Themes

### Love as Self-Grounding

The poem's central philosophical claim is that authentic [[love-theme|love]] must be its own foundation. Any external reason — beauty, charm, pity, need — makes [[love-theme|love]] contingent on something outside itself, and therefore vulnerable to loss. The phrase "for [[love-theme|love]]'s sake only" [014-002] is a tautology that is also the poem's deepest truth: [[love-theme|love]] is only secure when it refers to nothing beyond itself.

### Mutability and Permanence

The poem is [[shadow]]ed by the awareness of change. Every quality the speaker lists — smile, look, way of speaking, [[tear-imagery|weeping]] — is something that might alter or disappear. The goal is a [[love-theme|love]] so independent of mutable qualities that it can survive any transformation, continuing "through [[love-theme|love]]'s eternity" [014-014].
