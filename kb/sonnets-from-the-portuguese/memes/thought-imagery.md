## Definition

Thought is the cognitive engine of *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*. The sequence opens
with an act of thinking ("I thought once how [[theocritus|Theocritus]] had sung" [001-001]) and
closes with an act of giving back thoughts ("Take back these thoughts which here
unfolded too" [044-006]). Between these endpoints, thought oscillates between two
functions: it is the medium through which the speaker processes [[love-theme|love]] (thinking
*about* the be[[love-theme|love]]d), and it is the substitute that threatens to displace the
be[[love-theme|love]]d (thinking *instead of* the be[[love-theme|love]]d). This tension — thought as bridge vs.
thought as barrier — runs through all 13 instances and constitutes one of the
sonnet sequence's most sophisticated self-reflections on the act of writing [[love-theme|love]]
poetry.

The sequence's formal structure mirrors its thematic preoccupation: a sonnet is
itself a thought-unit, a 14-line cognitive gesture. Each sonnet records an act of
thinking, and the 44-sonnet sequence records the arc of a mind transforming itself
through sustained thought about [[love-theme|love]].

## Occurrences

### The inaugurating thought

> I thought once how Theocritus had sung / Of the sweet years [001-001]

The sequence begins not with feeling but with thinking. "I thought once how
[[theocritus|Theocritus]] had sung" — the act of cognition precedes the act of [[music-theme|song]]. The speaker
is not seized by passion; she is *thinking about* a dead Greek poet's representation
of time. This intellectual framing distinguishes [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s [[love-theme|love]] poetry from the
conventional amatory sonnet. Her speaker is a mind before she is a [[heart-imagery|heart]].

### Thought as error / thought corrected

> I thought the funeral shears / Would take this first, but Love is justified
> [018-011]

In [[sonnet-xviii|Sonnet XVIII]], thought is explicitly a site of error. The speaker "thought the
funeral shears" would cut off her lock of hair before [[love-theme|love]] could claim it. Love
proves her wrong — "Love is justified." The thought-habit of anticipating [[grief-theme|grief]]
is one [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]] must unlearn.

> Quick-loving hearts, I thought, may quickly loathe [032-005]

Again in [[sonnet-xxxii|Sonnet XXXII]], thought is the voice of self-doubt: "I thought" introduces
a theory about [[love-theme|love]] — that passion is inversely proportional to duration — that
the speaker then disproves through the sheer persistence of her own devotion.

### The beloved's thought in the letter

> Thy thought so in the letter. I am thine — [023-006]

[[sonnet-xxiii|Sonnet XXIII]]'s "Thy thought so in the letter" is one of the few instances where
thought is attributed to the be[[love-theme|love]]d rather than the speaker. His "thought" —
mediated through the written letter — precipitates her declaration of surrender:
"I am thine." Thought here is a transitive act: his thinking produces her belonging.

### Thought as substitute: the crisis of Sonnet XXIX

[[sonnet-xxix|Sonnet XXIX]] presents the sequence's most sustained meditation on the danger of
thought:

> I think of thee! — my thoughts do twine and bud / About thee, as wild vines
> about a tree [029-001]

The metaphor is botanical but the anxiety is existential. Thoughts "twine and bud"
about the be[[love-theme|love]]d with the same aggressive vitality as wild vines — they grow so
thickly that "soon there's nought to see / Except the straggling green which hides
the wood." Thought, in its generative excess, obscures its object. The more she
thinks about him, the less she sees him.

> I will not have my thoughts instead of thee / Who art dearer, better! [029-006]

This is the sequence's definitive statement on the problem of representation.
"Thoughts instead of thee" — thought as substitute, thought as idol — must be
rejected in favor of the actual be[[love-theme|love]]d. The sonnet demands presence: "Renew thy
presence." Writing about [[love-theme|love]] is necessary but dangerous; the poem must never
become more real than the person.

### Thought as burden

> I drop a grave thought — break from solitude [034-010]

In [[sonnet-xxxiv|Sonnet XXXIV]], thought is a weight that must be dropped. The "grave thought"
is both serious and sepulchral — thinking that has become leaden, that isolates
the thinker in solitude. Dropping it enables connection: "Yet still my [[heart-imagery|heart]] goes
to thee."

### The closing restitution

> Take back these thoughts which here unfolded too [044-006]

The final sonnet returns thought to its source. The speaker has "unfolded" her
thoughts across 44 sonnets, and now she offers them back to the be[[love-theme|love]]d. The
gesture recapitulates the sequence's central ambivalence: the thoughts *are* the
poems, and giving them back is both an act of humility (they were always his,
inspired by him) and an act of self-effacement (the poet disappears into the
be[[love-theme|love]]d at the sequence's end).

### Trembling thoughts

> These thoughts which tremble when bereft of — [031-013]

The truncated line from [[sonnet-xxxi|Sonnet XXXI]] — the thought itself breaks off, syntactically
incomplete — enacts the condition it describes. Thoughts "tremble" when separated
from their object, just as the poem trembles at the edge of its own incompleteness.

## Literary Context

### Romantic introspection and the lyric "I"

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]] inherits from the Romantics — particularly Wordsworth and Coleridge — a
tradition in which thought (reflection, memory, the "emotion recollected in
tranquility") is the primary material of poetry. But where Wordsworth's thought
tends toward the meditative and the resolved, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s thought is dynamic and
unresolved — it doubts itself, corrects itself, grows too thickly, and must be
pruned back. The sonnet sequence form enables this restless cognitive motion in
a way that the single lyric cannot.

### The Victorian crisis of introspection

The Victorian period's preoccupation with introspection — with the mind's
tendency to turn inward and feed on itself — [[shadow|shadows]] [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s thought-imagery.
Tennyson's "In Memoriam" (1850), published the same year as *Sonnets from the
Portuguese*, shares the same anxiety: thought as potentially pathological, the
mind as a space that can become a prison. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s answer — the be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence
as the antidote to excessive thought — is both psychologically acute and
structurally elegant.
