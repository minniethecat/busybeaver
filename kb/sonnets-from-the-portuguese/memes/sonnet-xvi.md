## Overview

Sonnet XVI of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is the sequence's great surrender poem. After fifteen sonnets of resistance, anxiety, and self-diminishment, the speaker at last accepts the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[love-theme|love]] — but she redefines surrender as elevation. Conquest, in this sonnet, is not defeat but liberation.

## Full Text

> And yet, because thou overcomest so, [016-001]
> Because thou art more noble and like a king, [016-002]
> Thou canst prevail against my fears and fling [016-003]
> Thy purple round me, till my heart shall grow [016-004]
> Too close against thine heart henceforth to know [016-005]
> How it shook when alone. Why, conquering [016-006]
> May prove as lordly and complete a thing [016-007]
> In lifting upward as in crushing low! [016-008]
> And as a vanquished soldier yields his sword [016-009]
> To one who lifts him from the bloody earth,— [016-010]
> Even so, Beloved, I at last record, [016-011]
> Here ends my strife. If *thou* invite me forth, [016-012]
> I rise above abasement at the word. [016-013]
> Make thy love larger to enlarge my worth. [016-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XVI [016-001]–[016-014]

## Analysis

### The Conquering Beloved

The sonnet opens with "And yet" [016-001], a conjunction that links it explicitly to the preceding sonnets of resistance. After the asymmetrical [[dream|vision]] of [[sonnet-xv|Sonnet XV]] and the conditions laid down in [[sonnet-xiv|Sonnet XIV]], the speaker now acknowledges that her resistance has been overcome — not by argument but by the be[[love-theme|love]]d's inherent nobility.

The be[[love-theme|love]]d is figured as a king: "more noble and like a king" [016-002]. The royal imagery extends through "Thy purple round me" [016-004], purple being the colour of imperial authority. The verb "fling" [016-003] is important — it suggests not the careful draping of a mantle but a sudden, almost violent enfolding. The be[[love-theme|love]]d does not ask the speaker to accept his [[love-theme|love]]; he throws it around her, and the gesture itself is the argument.

### The Transformed Heart

The consequence of this royal enfolding is a transformed [[heart-imagery|heart]]. The speaker's [[heart-imagery|heart]] will grow "Too close against thine [[heart-imagery|heart]] henceforth to know / How it shook when alone" [016-005]–[016-006]. The image is of two [[heart-imagery|heart]]s pressed together so intimately that the memory of solitary trembling is erased. This is not the merger of two into one (a conventional [[love-theme|love]]-poetry trope) but a proximity so close that the distinction between past and present self becomes imperceptible.

### Conquest as Elevation

The octave closes with a generalising couplet that is the poem's thesis: "Why, conquering / May prove as lordly and complete a thing / In lifting upward as in crushing low!" [016-006]–[016-008]. This is a radical redefinition of conquest. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's victory is not a defeat for the speaker but an elevation — the conqueror lifts the conquered rather than grinding them down. The word "lordly" [016-007] retains the aristocratic register while redirecting it from domination to beneficence.

### The Vanquished Soldier

The sestet develops the military metaphor through the figure of a "vanquished soldier" [016-009] who yields his sword "To one who lifts him from the bloody earth" [016-010]. The image is chivalric: the conqueror is also a rescuer, and the yielding of the sword is not an act of humiliation but of trust. The speaker "at last record[s]" [016-011] her surrender — the verb "record" suggests a formal, almost legal declaration, as if she is signing a treaty that ends a long war.

The closing couplet — "I rise above abasement at the word. / Make thy [[love-theme|love]] larger to enlarge my worth" [016-013]–[016-014] — transforms the poem's movement into a direct request. "Abasement" was the speaker's condition before surrender; "rising" is its consequence. The final line is both a plea and a statement of [[religious-faith|faith]]: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[love-theme|love]] has the power to expand the speaker's worth, and the speaker trusts him to exercise that power.

## Literary Context

Sonnet XVI draws on a rich tradition of military and erotic metaphor that stretches back to Ovid's *Amores*, where [[love-theme|love]] is figured as a kind of military service (*militat omnis amans* — "every [[love-theme|love]]r is a soldier"). In the Petrarchan tradition, the be[[love-theme|love]]d-lady is often figured as a fortress to be besieged or a conqueror who subdues the speaker. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] reworks this tradition by making conquest mutual and elevating: the be[[love-theme|love]]d conquers not to possess but to liberate.

The poem also anticipates the mutual surrender of [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]] ("When our two [[soul-theme|soul]]s stand up erect and strong"), where the speaker and be[[love-theme|love]]d face each other as equals. Sonnet XVI is the necessary precondition for that equality: the speaker must first accept being overcome before she can stand beside rather than beneath.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XVI uses a modified Petrarchan scheme: ABBA ABBA CDCDCD. The sestet's interlocking tercets, as in [[sonnet-xiv|Sonnet XIV]], create a seamless forward propulsion that mirrors the poem's movement toward surrender.

Key prosodic features:
- "And yet" [016-001] opens the poem with an unstressed-stressed pattern that reads as a continuation of a prior argument
- The heavy caesura in "And as a vanquished soldier yields his sword / To one who lifts him from the bloody earth,—" [016-009]–[016-010] creates a tableau-like stillness at the poem's dramatic centre
- The italicised *thou* in "If *thou* invite me forth" [016-012] is a typographic stress that distinguishes the be[[love-theme|love]]d from any other possible agent — only *this* be[[love-theme|love]]d can effect the transformation
- The final line's chiastic structure — "Make thy [[love-theme|love]] larger to enlarge my worth" [016-014] — enacts the reciprocity it describes: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's enlargement produces the speaker's enlargement

## Themes

### The Paradox of Surrender

The poem's central argument is that surrender to [[love-theme|love]] is not loss but gain. The speaker who has spent fifteen sonnets resisting, questioning, and qualifying [[love-theme|love]] now discovers that yielding is the only path to expansion. "I rise above abasement at the word" [016-013] — the very act of surrender ("the word" of invitation) is what enables rising.

### Worth and Its Source

The final line's concern with "worth" [016-014] echoes the "inner cost" of [[sonnet-xii|Sonnet XII]] [012-004]. Throughout the sequence, the speaker has been preoccupied with the question of what she is worth — whether she deserves the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[love-theme|love]], whether her own [[love-theme|love]] is valuable. Sonnet XVI offers an answer: worth is not intrinsic but relational, created and enlarged by [[love-theme|love]] itself.
