## Overview

Sonnet XV of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is a study in asymmetry. The speaker and the be[[love-theme|love]]d do not experience [[love-theme|love]] the same way: he looks at her with untroubled devotion, while she, even in [[love-theme|love]]'s embrace, cannot stop seeing "the end of [[love-theme|love]]" [015-011]. The poem's central image — a bee enclosed in crystal — is one of the most complex and haunting metaphors in the sequence.

## Full Text

> Accuse me not, beseech thee, that I wear [015-001]
> Too calm and sad a face in front of thine; [015-002]
> For we two look two ways, and cannot shine [015-003]
> With the same sunlight on our brow and hair. [015-004]
> On me thou lookest, with no doubting care, [015-005]
> As on a bee shut in a crystalline; [015-006]
> Since sorrow hath shut me safe in love's divine, [015-007]
> And to spread wing and fly in the outer air [015-008]
> Were most impossible failure, if I strove [015-009]
> To fail so. But I look on thee—on thee— [015-010]
> Beholding, besides love, the end of love, [015-011]
> Hearing oblivion beyond memory; [015-012]
> As one who sits and gazes from above, [015-013]
> Over the rivers to the bitter sea. [015-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XV [015-001]–[015-014]

## Analysis

### The Divided Gaze

The poem's governing conceit is stated in the third line: "we two look two ways" [015-003]. The speaker and the be[[love-theme|love]]d are physically present to each other — face to face — but mentally oriented in opposite directions. The be[[love-theme|love]]d looks at the speaker "with no doubting care" [015-005], his gaze untroubled by the knowledge of loss. The speaker, by contrast, looks "on thee—on thee—" [015-010], the repetition and dash registering an intensity that transforms into distance: even as she looks at him, she sees beyond him.

The plea "Accuse me not" [015-001] suggests that the be[[love-theme|love]]d has noticed the speaker's sadness and perhaps reproached her for it. The poem is her defence: she cannot help what she sees, and what she sees is [[love-theme|love]]'s ending.

### The Bee in Crystal

The central image of the octave — "as on a bee shut in a crystalline" [015-006] — is strikingly ambiguous. The be[[love-theme|love]]d looks at the speaker as one looks at a bee trapped in crystal. The image can be read in two directions: the crystal preserves and displays the bee, making it permanently visible and beautiful, but it also entombs it, preventing f[[light-imagery|light]]. "Sorrow hath shut me safe in [[love-theme|love]]'s divine" [015-007] — the word "safe" is the pivot of the ambiguity. Is the speaker safely preserved or safely imprisoned? The phrase "[[love-theme|love]]'s divine" suggests the former, but the verb "shut" leans toward the latter.

The lines that follow confirm the darker reading: "to spread wing and fly in the outer air / Were most impossible failure, if I strove / To fail so" [015-008]–[015-009]. F[[light-imagery|light]] — escape from the crystalline of [[grief-theme|sorrow]] — is impossible not because it cannot be attempted but because the attempt itself would be a failure. The speaker is not merely trapped; she is trapped in a condition where the very concept of freedom has become incoherent.

### The Bitter Sea

The sestet's concluding simile — "As one who sits and gazes from above, / Over the rivers to the bitter sea" [015-013]–[015-014] — shifts the perspective from enclosure to elevation. The speaker now looks down from a height, surveying a landscape of rivers flowing toward a "bitter sea." This is the view from "the end of [[love-theme|love]]" [015-011]: a terminal vista in which all currents converge on the same destination. The adjective "bitter" is the poem's final word, colouring everything that precedes it.

## Literary Context

Sonnet XV draws on the long poetic tradition of the *contemptus mundi* — the view from a height that reduces earthly life to insignificance. The rivers flowing to the bitter sea recall Ecclesiastes 1:7 ("All the rivers run into the sea; yet the sea is not full") and the classical *topos* of the *locus amoenus* viewed from a melancholic distance. But where the traditional *contemptus mundi* is a religious exercise in detachment from the world, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s speaker experiences it as unwanted knowledge — she sees the end of [[love-theme|love]] not because she wishes to but because she cannot help it.

The bee-in-crystal image has no obvious source in the sonnet tradition and is one of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s most original inventions. It belongs to the Victorian fascination with natural specimens preserved in glass — the display case, the terrarium, the bell jar — and shares with those objects the ambiguity of something at once preserved and captured.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XV employs the Petrarchan ABBA ABBA CDC DCD scheme. The volta at line 9 ("Were most impossible failure") is less a turn of argument than an intensification of the octave's established mood — the sestet does not resolve the problem but deepens it.

Key prosodic features:
- The repeated "on thee—on thee—" [015-010] uses the dash to create a stutter of attention, the speaker's gaze fixing and then unfixing on the be[[love-theme|love]]d
- The enjambment across [015-008]–[015-009] ("the outer air / Were most impossible failure") suspends the syntax across the volta, creating a moment of breathless hesitation
- The alliteration of "b" in "beholding, besides" [015-011] and "beyond" [015-012] creates a sonic current that carries the sestet forward
- The final line's heavy stresses — "Over the rivers to the bitter sea" [015-014] — slow the poem to a funeral pace

## Themes

### Asymmetry of Vision

The poem's deepest theme is the tragic inequality of perception. Two people look at each other, but they do not see the same thing. The be[[love-theme|love]]d sees a be[[love-theme|love]]d; the speaker sees "besides [[love-theme|love]], the end of [[love-theme|love]]" [015-011]. This asymmetry is not a failure of [[love-theme|love]] but its condition — the speaker's awareness of [[death-theme|mortality]] is precisely what makes her [[dream|vision]] different from the be[[love-theme|love]]d's.

### Sorrow as Protection

The line "[[grief-theme|sorrow]] hath shut me safe in [[love-theme|love]]'s divine" [015-007] articulates a paradox that runs through the entire sequence: [[grief-theme|sorrow]] is not opposed to [[love-theme|love]] but integral to it. The speaker's melancholy is not a flaw to be cured by [[love-theme|love]] but the medium through which [[love-theme|love]] is experienced. To be "shut safe" is to be simultaneously imprisoned and preserved.
