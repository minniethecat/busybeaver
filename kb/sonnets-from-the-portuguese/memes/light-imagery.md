# Light (Imagery)

## Definition

Light in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* is [[love-theme|love]]'s native element — the radiance that the be[[love-theme|love]]d brings into the speaker's world of [[shadow]] and [[silence]]. The sequence is structured around a movement from darkness to light, from the speaker's initial state of isolation ("a quiet, shielded place") to the illumination of reciprocal [[love-theme|love]]. Light imagery pervades [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]], drawing on biblical (Genesis's "Let there be light"), Petrarchan (the be[[love-theme|love]]d's luminous eyes), and Romantic (the inner light of consciousness) traditions. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s light is not merely visual but ontological — it reveals being itself.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

### Light as Revelation

Light in [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]] is fundamentally revelatory: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence makes visible what was hidden. The speaker's former life — illness, isolation, [[grief-theme|grief]] — was a kind of darkness, and [[love-theme|love]] is the light that dispels it. This is not merely metaphorical; [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s own biography (years as an invalid in her father's house) gives the imagery of emerging from darkness into light a literal dimension.

### The Beloved's Radiance

The be[[love-theme|love]]d himself is a source of light. His voice, his presence, his [[love-theme|love]] — all are described in terms of luminosity. This draws on the Petrarchan convention of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's radiant beauty but transforms it: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's light is not a distant star but a fire that warms and transforms.

### Light and Shadow

The sequence does not eliminate darkness but contains it within light's frame. [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]] pairs the radiant [[soul-theme|soul]]s with "darkness and the [[death-theme|death]]-hour rounding it" [022-014]. Light does not abolish [[shadow]] but defines its boundary, making darkness meaningful as the context that light illuminates.

## Literary Context

Light imagery in Victorian poetry draws on Romanticism's natural supernaturalism (Wordsworth's "light of setting suns") and the Christian tradition of Christ as *lux mundi*. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s distinctive contribution is to locate the sacred light not in nature or doctrine but in human [[love-theme|love]] — the be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence is theophanic, revealing [[god|God]] through human affection.
