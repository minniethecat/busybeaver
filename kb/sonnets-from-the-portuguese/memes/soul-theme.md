# Soul (Theme)

## Definition

The soul in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* is the innermost self — the site where [[love-theme|love]] takes root and from which it speaks. Unlike the [[heart-imagery|heart]] (which feels) or the body (which is mortal), the soul is presented as the dimension of the self capable of union with another and with the divine. The soul appears across the sequence as the agent of [[love-theme|love]]'s deepest movements: it is what the be[[love-theme|love]]d addresses ("the footsteps of thy soul" [007-002]), what the [[love-theme|love]]r offers ("Thy soul hath snatched up mine" [012-011]), and what outlasts [[death-theme|death]]. The soul's trajectory through [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]] traces the speaker's movement from isolated spiritual desolation to reciprocal union.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

The soul appears in more than ten sonnets, with concentrated clusters in the early and middle sections.

### The Soul's Awakening: Sonnets VI–VII

The first mention of the soul sets the tone for the entire sequence: "The uses of my soul, nor lift my hand / Serially" [006-005]. The speaker describes a state in which her soul was dormant, unused — until [[love-theme|love]] awakened it. [[sonnet-vii|Sonnet VII]] deepens this awakening: the speaker hears "the footsteps of thy soul" [007-002], an auditory image that suggests the soul announces itself not through sight but through sound, through presence felt before it is seen.

### The Soul's Union: Sonnets XII and XIX

[[sonnet-xii|Sonnet XII]] enacts the soul's most dramatic claim: "Thy soul hath snatched up mine all faint and weak" [012-011]. The verb "snatched" conveys both violence and rescue — the be[[love-theme|love]]d's soul does not gently persuade but seizes the speaker's soul from its weakness. The closing line's parenthetical "(O soul, we must be meek!)" [012-013] tempers this assertion with humility, acknowledging the audacity of claiming such union.

In [[sonnet-xix|Sonnet XIX]], the soul is figured as a marketplace: "The soul's Rialto hath its merchandise" [019-001]. This unexpected commercial metaphor suggests that the soul, like Venice's Rialto bridge, is a site of exchange — [[love-theme|love]] is transacted, bartered, given and received. The image complicates the sequence's spiritual register by grounding the soul in the material language of commerce.

### The Silent Soul: Sonnet XXI

[[sonnet-xxi|Sonnet XXI]] offers the sequence's most intimate rendering of soul-union. The speaker asks the be[[love-theme|love]]d to "[[love-theme|love]] me also in [[silence]] with thy soul" [021-014]. This [[silence|silent]] [[love-theme|love]] — beyond words, beyond touch — is the soul's native language. The sonnet presents soul-communion as more authentic than any spoken declaration.

### Two Souls Standing: Sonnet XXII

The sequence's most concentrated [[dream|vision]] of soul-union occurs in [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]]: "When our two souls stand up erect and strong" [022-001]. The souls are imaged as "isolate pure spirits" [022-012] — separate, complete, and equal. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to insist that union does not dissolve individuality; the two souls stand together precisely because each is whole. This [[dream|vision]] of [[love-theme|love]] as the joining of equals is the sequence's central spiritual claim.

## Literary Context

The soul's prominence in [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]] reflects [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s immersion in Victorian religious thought, particularly the Broad Church movement's emphasis on personal spiritual experience over doctrinal orthodoxy. The soul's capacity for direct communion — with the be[[love-theme|love]]d and with [[god|God]] — bypasses institutional mediation. This Protestant interiority is married to the Petrarchan tradition's language of spiritualized [[love-theme|love]], but [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] transforms the convention: the be[[love-theme|love]]d is not an unattainable ideal (as in Petrarch) but a real, present partner whose soul answers hers.
