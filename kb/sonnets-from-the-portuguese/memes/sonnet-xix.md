## Overview

Sonnet XIX of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) completes the hair-exchange diptych begun in [[sonnet-xviii|Sonnet XVIII]]. Where the preceding sonnet gave, this one receives: the speaker accepts a dark curl from the be[[love-theme|love]]d's forehead and treasures it as a "counterpart" to her own offering, placing it on her [[heart-imagery|heart]] as a talisman against [[death-theme|death]].

## Full Text

> The soul's Rialto hath its merchandise; [019-001]
> I barter curl for curl upon that mart, [019-002]
> And from my poet's forehead to my heart [019-003]
> Receive this lock which outweighs argosies— [019-004]
> As purply black, as erst to Pindar's eyes [019-005]
> The dim purpureal tresses gloomed athwart [019-006]
> The nine white Muse-brows. For this counterpart, [019-007]
> The bay-crown's shade, Beloved, I surmise, [019-008]
> Still lingers on thy curl, it is so black! [019-009]
> Thus, with a fillet of smooth-kissing breath, [019-010]
> I tie the shadows safe from gliding back, [019-011]
> And lay the gift where nothing hindereth, [019-012]
> Here on my heart as on thy brow, to lack [019-013]
> No natural heat till mine grows cold in death. [019-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XIX [019-001]–[019-014]

## Analysis

### The Soul's Marketplace

The sonnet opens with one of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s most striking conceits: "The [[soul-theme|soul]]'s Rialto hath its merchandise" [019-001]. The Rialto was Venice's great commercial exchange, a bridge lined with shops where merchants traded luxury goods. By figuring the [[love-theme|love]]rs' exchange of hair as commerce on "the [[soul-theme|soul]]'s Rialto," [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] elevates material transaction to [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual economy. The verb "barter" [019-002] suggests a pre-monetary exchange — not buying and selling but direct trading of equivalent value.

The lock the speaker receives "outweighs argosies" [019-004] — a classical term for large merchant vessels. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's single curl is worth more than entire ships of cargo, a hyperbolic valuation that asserts the supremacy of [[love-theme|love]]'s economy over the commercial world's.

### The Pindaric Comparison

The be[[love-theme|love]]d's hair is "purply black, as erst to Pindar's eyes / The dim purpureal tresses gloomed athwart / The nine white Muse-brows" [019-005]–[019-007]. This is an extraordinarily learned allusion. Pindar (c. 518–438 BCE) was the greatest of the Greek lyric poets, and [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] — a formidable classical scholar who read Greek from childhood — invokes him here as the ultimate arbiter of poetic beauty.

The effect is to place the be[[love-theme|love]]d's physical feature in the company of classical sublimity. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's dark curl is not merely handsome but worthy of comparison with the Muses themselves. The "bay-crown's [[shadow|shade]]" [019-008] — the laurel wreath of poetic achievement — is figured as still lingering on the be[[love-theme|love]]d's hair, a suggestion that his poetic gift is physically manifest.

### The Ritual Binding

The sestet enacts a ritual of preservation. The speaker uses "a fillet of smooth-[[kiss-imagery|kiss]]ing breath" [019-010] to "tie the [[shadow|shadows]] safe from gliding back" [019-011]. The "[[shadow|shadows]]" are the dark hairs, figured as mobile, potentially fugitive — they might "glide back" to the be[[love-theme|love]]d's forehead if not bound. The "fillet" (a ribbon or band) made of breath is an immaterial binding, the speaker's exhalation serving as the knot that secures the gift.

The lock is placed "Here on my [[heart-imagery|heart]] as on thy brow" [019-013], creating a spatial symmetry: what rested on the be[[love-theme|love]]d's forehead now rests on the speaker's [[heart-imagery|heart]]. The final line — "to lack / No natural heat till mine grows cold in [[death-theme|death]]" [019-013]–[019-014] — transforms the lock into a source of warmth, a living presence that will sustain the speaker's [[heart-imagery|heart]] until [[death-theme|death]] itself extinguishes it.

## Literary Context

The hair-exchange sonnets draw on a rich tradition of gift-giving in [[love-theme|love]] poetry. Catullus's *Carmen* 13 promises a perfume given by Venus and the Muses; Sidney's *Astrophil and Stella* features the exchange of tokens. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to make the exchange explicitly commercial in metaphor (the Rialto, barter, argosies) while insisting on its [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual value.

The Pindaric allusion is characteristic of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s classical learning. She read Pindar in the original Greek and published translations of his odes. Invoking him here is not mere ornamentation but a claim about the be[[love-theme|love]]d's poetic stature — and, by implication, her own.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XIX employs ABBA ABBA CDCDCD. The octave's enclosed rhymes create a self-contained space for the commercial metaphor, while the sestet's interlocking pattern enacts the binding and tying it describes.

Key prosodic features:
- "I barter curl for curl upon that mart" [019-002] uses alliteration (curl/curl) and internal consonance to mimic the balanced exchange
- The Pindaric passage [019-005]–[019-007] employs Latinate vocabulary ("purpureal," "athwart") to create an elevated classical register
- The enjambment across the final lines [019-013]–[019-014] ("to lack / No natural heat") suspends the syntax, delaying the mention of "[[death-theme|death]]" until the poem's final word

## Themes

### Economy and the Sacred

The poem's governing metaphor combines the commercial and the sacred. The Rialto is a place of trade, but it is "the [[soul-theme|soul]]'s Rialto" — a marketplace of the [[soul-theme|spirit]]. This fusion of economic and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual language is characteristic of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s Christian sensibility, in which material exchange can figure divine transaction.

### Warmth Against Death

Like many sonnets in the sequence, Sonnet XIX ends with the word "[[death-theme|death]]" [019-014]. But the tone here is not tragic. The lock of hair is figured as a source of "natural heat" — a living warmth that defers [[death-theme|mortality]]. Love, in this imagining, is a stay against the coldness of the grave.
