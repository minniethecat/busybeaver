## Analysis

Sonnet VI is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The poem continues the sequence's exploration of [[love-theme|love]], devotion, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transformation that [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] develops across the collection.

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form, with an octave establishing a premise or situation, followed by a sestet that develops, complicates, or resolves it. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s characteristic voice — at once intimate and rhetorically elevated — is present throughout, blending personal emotion with the formal demands of the sonnet tradition.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet VI occupies a specific position within the forty-four-sonnet arc. The sequence moves from the speaker's initial disbelief that she could be [[love-theme|love]]d (Sonnets I–VII), through surrender and reciprocal devotion (Sonnets VIII–XXVIII), to a [[dream|vision]] of [[love-theme|love]] that transcends [[death-theme|mortality]] (Sonnets XXIX–XLIV). Each sonnet contributes a distinct emotional or rhetorical development to this larger narrative.

## Literary Context

Sonnet VI ("Go from me") is the sequence's most direct rejection of the be[[love-theme|love]]d, and paradoxically, its most intimate confession. The opening imperative — "Go from me" [006-001] — is repeated in the poem's second line, establishing a pattern of rejection that the rest of the poem systematically undermines. The speaker cannot sustain the command, immediately qualifying it: "yet I feel that I shall stand / Henceforward in thy [[shadow]]" [006-005]–[006-006].

This sonnet marks a crucial pivot in the sequence. Sonnets I–V have dramatised unworthiness and divine exclusion; Sonnet VI begins the movement towards acceptance, but through the back door of paradox. The speaker both insists on separation and acknowledges its impossibility in a single breath. Angela Leighton notes that this doubleness — saying one thing and meaning another — is [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s characteristic rhetorical mode, derived from her deep reading of Shakespeare's sonnets.

The poem's spatial and sensory imagery deserves attention. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence transforms the speaker's world: "the wider air" and "the door / Of the individual life" [006-003]–[006-006] are metaphors for the expansion of self that [[love-theme|love]] brings. The "[[shadow]]" [006-006] that the speaker will stand in is protective rather than threatening — a marked shift from the curse-darkened eyes of [[sonnet-ii|Sonnet II]].

## Structure and Prosody

The Petrarchan form is deployed with particular subtlety in Sonnet VI. The octave's ABBA ABBA scheme creates a tight sonic envelope, while the sestet's more varied rhymes enact the loosening of the speaker's resistance.

Key prosodic moments:
- The trochaic substitution in "Go from me" [006-001] opens the poem with a stressed syllable that functions as a command
- The enjambment across [006-003]–[006-005] ("the wider air / But large; the door / Of the individual life") spans three lines, enacting the expansion it describes
- The near-oxymoron "thy [[shadow]]" as a place of standing [006-006] reverses the typical [[light-imagery|light]]/dark hierarchy of the sequence

## Themes

### Rejection as Intimacy

The poem's central paradox is that the speaker's rejection of the be[[love-theme|love]]d is the most intimate gesture possible. Only someone who knows themselves deeply affected can expend such rhetorical energy on denial. The repeated "Go from me" [006-001] acts as an apotropaic formula — a spell to ward off what is already inside.

### Threshold and Door

The image of the door [006-006] is a threshold figure that recurs in the sequence. The speaker stands at the boundary between her old self (invalid, isolated, grieving) and a new self opened by [[love-theme|love]]. The door of "the individual life" being "commanded" to stand open [006-006] suggests a violation that is also a liberation.
