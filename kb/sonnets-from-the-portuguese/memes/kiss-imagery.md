# Kiss (Imagery)

## Definition

The kiss in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* marks the intersection of the [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual and the physical. Throughout a sequence that often soars into theological abstraction — [[soul-theme|soul]]-union, divine [[love-theme|love]], eternal life — the kiss grounds [[love-theme|love]] in the body. It is mortal, tangible, and irreducibly physical. [[sonnet-xxxvi|Sonnet XXXVI]]II, the sequence's most sustained meditation on kissing, traces three kisses from the be[[love-theme|love]]d ("First time he kissed me, he but only kissed" [038-001]) through a rising scale of intimacy and significance — from the fingers to the forehead to, impliedly, the lips. The kiss is not a metaphor; it is an event, and its reality matters precisely because [[love-theme|love]] in these sonnets is never purely [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

The kiss appears in at least five sonnets, with [[sonnet-xxxvi|Sonnet XXXVI]]II as its fullest treatment.

### The First Kiss: Sonnet XXXVIII

[[sonnet-xxxvi|Sonnet XXXVI]]II provides the sequence's only narrative account of physical intimacy. The first kiss falls on the speaker's fingers — tentative, courtly, almost formal: "First time he kissed me, he but only kissed / The fingers of this hand wherewith I write" [038-001]. The kiss is self-consciously literary (the hand that writes is kissed) and self-consciously restrained.

The second kiss is more intimate: "The second passed in height / The first" [038-007], rising from hand to forehead. Each kiss is described as a chrism — a sacramental anointing — that consecrates rather than possesses. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's restraint honours the speaker's dignity even as the kisses grow more intimate.

### The Mutual Kiss: Sonnet XXXVI

[[sonnet-xxxvi|Sonnet XXXVI]] imagines a kiss that is fully reciprocal: "This mutual kiss drop down between us both / As a free-will offering" [036-011]. The kiss is not taken but given, not demanded but offered. This mutuality is the sequence's erotic and ethical ideal — [[love-theme|love]] as gift exchanged freely between equals.

### The Saving Kiss: Sonnet XXVII

In [[sonnet-xxvii|Sonnet XXVII]], the be[[love-theme|love]]d's kiss is figured as salvation: "Before thy saving kiss! My own, my own" [027-006]. The adjective "saving" carries theological weight — the kiss does what sacraments do, mediating grace through physical sign. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s Protestant imagination here flirts with Catholic sacramentalism: the kiss is not literally a sacrament, but it functions like one.

## Literary Context

The Victorian poetry of [[love-theme|love]] typically [[soul-theme|spirit]]ualises physical desire (see Coventry Patmore's *The Angel in the House*) or displaces it onto nature (see Tennyson's *Maud*). [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s treatment of the kiss is unusually direct for a mid-Victorian woman poet — not because it is explicit (it is not) but because it insists on the theological significance of physical touch. The kiss matters not despite its physicality but because of it.
