## Overview

Sonnet XIII of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is a sonnet about the impossibility of writing sonnets. The be[[love-theme|love]]d has asked the speaker to "fashion into speech" her [[love-theme|love]], but she finds language inadequate to the task. The poem dramatises this failure: the speaker tries to hold up a torch of articulation, drops it, and retreats into "dauntless, voiceless fortitude."

## Full Text

> And wilt thou have me fashion into speech [013-001]
> The love I bear thee, finding words enough, [013-002]
> And hold the torch out, while the winds are rough, [013-003]
> Between our faces, to cast light on each?— [013-004]
> I drop it at thy feet. I cannot teach [013-005]
> My hand to hold my spirits so far off [013-006]
> From myself—me—that I should bring thee proof [013-007]
> In words, of love hid in me out of reach. [013-008]
> Nay, let the silence of my womanhood [013-009]
> Commend my woman-love to thy belief— [013-010]
> Seeing that I stand unwon, however wooed, [013-011]
> And rend the garment of my life, in brief, [013-012]
> By a most dauntless, voiceless fortitude, [013-013]
> Lest one touch of this heart convey its grief. [013-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XIII [013-001]–[013-014]

## Analysis

### The Request and Its Refusal

The sonnet opens as a direct response to a request — apparently the be[[love-theme|love]]d has asked the speaker to articulate her [[love-theme|love]] in verse. The question "And wilt thou have me fashion into speech / The [[love-theme|love]] I bear thee" [013-001]–[013-002] is disingenuous, since the speaker is already doing precisely that by writing Sonnet XIII. The poem thus performs a paradox: it declares itself impossible while simultaneously existing.

The torch image [013-003] is the poem's central metaphor for articulation. To "hold the torch out" between two faces is to illuminate both, to make visible what would otherwise remain in darkness. But the winds are "rough" [013-003], and the task cannot be sustained. The speaker "drops it at thy feet" [013-005] — a gesture that is at once a failure and an offering. The torch, even fallen, lies before the be[[love-theme|love]]d.

### The Problem of Distance

The octave's second half explores the psychological difficulty of self-expression. The speaker "cannot teach / My hand to hold my [[soul-theme|spirit]]s so far off / From myself—me" [013-005]–[013-007]. The dash between "myself" and "me" is a graphic enactment of self-di[[dream|vision]]: the speaker is split between the self that feels and the self that must represent feeling in language. The [[love-theme|love]] is "hid in me out of reach" [013-008] — it exists, but at a depth that language cannot plumb.

### Silence as Argument

The sestet pivots from the failure of speech to the alternative of [[silence]]. "Nay, let the [[silence]] of my womanhood / Commend my woman-[[love-theme|love]] to thy belief" [013-009]–[013-010]. The repetition of "woman" marks a shift from the abstract [[love-theme|love]]r of the Petrarchan tradition to a specifically female speaker whose [[silence]] is not absence but presence — a "dauntless, voiceless fortitude" [013-013] that "rends the garment" of her life [013-012].

The final line — "Lest one touch of this [[heart-imagery|heart]] convey its [[grief-theme|grief]]" [013-014] — reveals what the [[silence]] protects. The speaker's [[heart-imagery|heart]] is so full of [[grief-theme|grief]] that even a "touch" would transmit it. The fortitude, then, is not for the speaker's own sake but for the be[[love-theme|love]]d's: she protects him from the full weight of her [[grief-theme|sorrow]].

## Literary Context

Sonnet XIII engages with the ancient *topos* of inexpressibility — the claim that language cannot capture feeling, even as language is used to make the claim. This figure appears in Dante's *Vita Nuova* ("my tongue is as one struck dumb") and in Sidney's *Astrophil and Stella* [[sonnet-i|Sonnet 1]] ("I sought fit words to paint the blackest face of woe"). [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to ground the inexpressibility not in the be[[love-theme|love]]d's transcendent beauty but in the speaker's own emotional complexity: what cannot be said is not praise of the other but the [[grief-theme|grief]] within the self.

The poem's balancing of speech and [[silence]] also reflects [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s own biographical situation. As an invalid and a poet, she had long experience of expressing herself through written words while physically secluded. The sonnet's negotiation between "fashioning into speech" and "voiceless fortitude" enacts the paradox of the reclusive writer whose words reach the world while her body does not.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XIII uses the Petrarchan ABBA ABBA CDC DCD scheme with notable rhythmic variations. The octave's first question — "And wilt thou have me fashion into speech" [013-001] — begins with an unstressed syllable, giving the line a conversational, almost breathless quality. The caesura after "I drop it at thy feet." [013-005] is the poem's physical and emotional turning point.

Key prosodic features:
- The heavy spondaic substitution in "Nay, let" [013-009] marks the volta with emphatic rejection
- The internal fragmentation in "myself—me" [013-007], where the dash creates a caesura that visibly splits the self on the page
- The enjambment across [013-011]–[013-012] ("I stand unwon, however wooed, / And rend the garment of my life") stretches the syntactic unit across the rhyme boundary, enacting the "rending" it describes

## Themes

### Language and Its Limits

The poem is a sustained meditation on the inadequacy of language to convey emotional truth. The speaker is asked to "fashion into speech" [013-001] her [[love-theme|love]], but the verb "fashion" implies artifice — language can only approximate, never reproduce, the feeling it represents. The dropped torch [013-005] is an admission of this limitation.

### Gender and Silence

The phrase "[[silence]] of my womanhood" [013-009] introduces a gendered dimension to the poem's concerns. In the Victorian context, female [[silence]] was both a social expectation and a literary convention. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] revalues [[silence]]: it is not submission but a form of "fortitude" [013-013], a willed withholding that protects both speaker and listener.
