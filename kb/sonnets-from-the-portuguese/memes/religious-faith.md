# Religious Faith

## Definition

Religious faith pervades [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* not as doctrinal assertion but as the assumed framework within which [[love-theme|love]] gains its fullest meaning. [[god|God]] appears throughout the sequence — as witness, as gift-giver, as the one who set the be[[love-theme|love]]d and speaker "between His After and Before" [017-002]. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s religiosity is characteristically Victorian in its fusion of romantic [[love-theme|love]] and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual devotion: the be[[love-theme|love]]d is not [[god|God]] but is [[god|God]]'s gift, and loving the be[[love-theme|love]]d becomes a form of worship. The sequence's famous closing claim — "if [[god|God]] choose, / I shall but [[love-theme|love]] thee better after [[death-theme|death]]" [043-013] — makes [[god|God]] the arbiter of [[love-theme|love]]'s eternity.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

[[god|God]] is explicitly named in over fifteen sonnets, appearing with particular density in the early and middle sections.

### God as Architect of Love: Sonnets II and XVII

The first mention of [[god|God]] sets the theological stakes: "only three in all [[god|God]]'s universe / Have heard this word" [002-001]. The rarity of the speaker's experience — being [[love-theme|love]]d — is framed within [[god|God]]'s cosmic order. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice is "from [[god|God]]" [002-009], positioning [[god|God]] as the ultimate source of [[love-theme|love]]'s power.

[[sonnet-xvii|Sonnet XVII]] provides the sequence's most explicit theology of [[love-theme|love]]'s origins: "[[god|God]] set between His After and Before" [017-002]. [[god|God]] is the temporal architect who placed the [[love-theme|love]]rs in history at the right moment to meet. The sonnet's logic is carefully non-idolatrous: the be[[love-theme|love]]d is not divine, but their meeting is divinely ordained. "[[god|God]]'s will devotes / Thine to such ends" [017-008] makes clear that [[love-theme|love]] serves purposes beyond itself.

### Love as Baptism: Sonnets VII–VIII

[[sonnet-vii|Sonnet VII]] uses baptismal imagery to describe the speaker's transformation through [[love-theme|love]]: what "[[god|God]] gave for baptism, I am fain to drink" [007-008]. Love is a sacrament — a visible sign of invisible grace — and the speaker's acceptance of [[love-theme|love]] is figured as receiving a divine gift.

[[sonnet-viii|Sonnet VIII]] extends the sacramental register. The speaker's frequent [[tear-imagery|tears]] are met with the reply: "Ask [[god|God]] who knows!" [008-010]. Human emotion is valid because [[god|God]] understands it; the be[[love-theme|love]]d need not explain or defend the speaker's [[tear-imagery|tears]] because [[god|God]] already witnesses them.

### The God Who Gives: Sonnets XXVI–XXVII

In [[sonnet-xxvi|Sonnet XXVI]], "[[god|God]]'s gifts put man's best [[dream|dreams]] to shame" [026-014]. The be[[love-theme|love]]d is a gift that exceeds anything the speaker could have imagined for herself. This theology of gift is central to the sequence: [[love-theme|love]] is not earned or deserved but given, and the appropriate response is gratitude — "I thank all who have [[love-theme|love]]d me in their [[heart-imagery|heart]]s, / With thanks and [[love-theme|love]] from mine" [041-001].

[[sonnet-xxvii|Sonnet XXVII]] completes the identification of the be[[love-theme|love]]d with divine gift: "And I who looked for only [[god|God]], found thee!" [027-008]. The exclamation is not blasphemous but devotional — finding the be[[love-theme|love]]d is finding [[god|God]]'s purpose, not replacing [[god|God]].

### Eschatology: Sonnets XLII–XLIII

Sonnet XLII images the speaker turning "to the white throne of [[god|God]]" [042-005], drawing on Revelation's imagery of final judgment. The gesture is one of surrender — not of [[love-theme|love]] but of the fear that [[love-theme|love]]'s imperfection renders it unworthy.

[[sonnet-xliii|Sonnet XLIII]]'s closing couplet subordinates [[love-theme|love]]'s eternity to [[god|God]]'s will: "if [[god|God]] choose, / I shall but [[love-theme|love]] thee better after [[death-theme|death]]" [043-013]. The conditional "if" is crucial — [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] does not claim to know [[god|God]]'s will but trusts that a [[god|God]] who gave [[love-theme|love]] will not let it perish. This is faith as hope, not certainty.

## Literary Context

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s religious language reflects her Congregationalist upbringing and her engagement with the Broad Church movement's theological liberalism. Unlike the Anglo-Catholic sacramentalism of the Oxford Movement (which emphasised ritual and institutional authority), [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s faith is interior, personal, and expressed through the language of individual conscience. The sonnets' fusion of romantic and religious discourse also draws on the tradition of the "religion of [[love-theme|love]]" in Dante and Petrarch, but [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s [[god|God]] is more intimate — less the remote judge of medieval Christianity than the benevolent witness of Victorian liberal Protestantism.
