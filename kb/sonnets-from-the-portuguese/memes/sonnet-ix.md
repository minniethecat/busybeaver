## Full Text

> Can it be right to give what I can give? [009-001]
>  [009-002]
> To let thee sit beneath the fall of tears [009-003]
>  [009-004]
> As salt as mine, and hear the sighing years [009-005]
>  [009-006]
> Re-sighing on my lips renunciative [009-007]
>  [009-008]
> Through those infrequent smiles which fail to live [009-009]
>  [009-010]
> For all thy adjurations? O my fears, [009-011]
>  [009-012]
> That this can scarce be right! We are not peers, [009-013]
>  [009-014]
> So to be lovers; and I own and grieve [009-015]
>  [009-016]
> That givers of such gifts as mine are, must [009-017]
>  [009-018]
> Be counted with the ungenerous. Out, alas! [009-019]
>  [009-020]
> I will not soil thy purple with my dust, [009-021]
>  [009-022]
> Nor breathe my poison on thy Venice-glass, [009-023]
>  [009-024]
> Nor give thee any love—which were unjust. [009-025]
>  [009-026]
> Beloved, I only love thee! let it pass. [009-027]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet IX [009-001]–[009-027]

## Analysis

Sonnet IX is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850), composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]].

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form, with an octave (ABBA ABBA) and a sestet. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s voice blends intimate address with formal elevation, characteristic of the sequence.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet IX contributes to the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief and melancholy through surrender and reciprocal devotion to transcendent [[love-theme|love]].

## Literary Context

Sonnet IX is the sequence's most explicit meditation on the ethics of receiving [[love-theme|love]] when one feels unable to reciprocate adequately. The opening question — "Can it be right to give what I can give?" [009-001] — establishes the sonnet as a moral inquiry framed in the language of gift and exchange. The speaker's gifts are "[[tear-imagery|tears]]," "sighs," and "infrequent smiles" [009-003], [009-005], [009-009]; she questions whether offering such diminished emotional currency to the be[[love-theme|love]]d constitutes a form of theft or unfairness.

The poem marks an ethical turn within the sequence. Where Sonnets II–V dramatised the speaker's unworthiness through mythological and spatial metaphors, Sonnet IX addresses the problem directly, in the language of right and wrong. The word "right" appears three times: "Can it be right" [009-001], "this can scarce be right" [009-013], and the implied "unjust" of "which were unjust" [009-025]. This insistent moral vocabulary is unusual for a [[love-theme|love]] lyric and signals [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s debt to Victorian religious discourse, in which [[love-theme|love]] and duty are inseparable categories.

The closing couplet — "Be[[love-theme|love]]d, I only [[love-theme|love]] thee! let it pass." [009-027] — is one of the sequence's most famous lines. After an entire sonnet arguing against [[love-theme|love]], the speaker abruptly concedes it. The exclamation "I only [[love-theme|love]] thee" contradicts the poem's entire rhetorical structure, and the half-line "let it pass" is a deliberate anti-climax, an exhausted surrender to feeling that reason cannot justify.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet IX follows the Petrarchan form. The octave poses a single extended question [009-001]–[009-011] about the rightness of the speaker's [[love-theme|love]]; the sestet develops the answer through a series of self-denials culminating in the paradoxical final couplet.

The octave's syntax is exceptionally fluid. The question that begins at line [009-001] runs through line [009-011] without a full stop, threading subordinate clauses ("To let thee sit," "and hear," "Through those infrequent smiles which fail to live") into a single breathless inquiry. This syntactic suspension enacts the speaker's hesitation — she cannot bring herself to complete the thought because completing it means accepting its conclusion.

The sestet's imagery becomes materially specific: "purple" [009-021], "dust" [009-021], "poison" [009-023], "Venice-glass" [009-023]. The speaker shifts from abstraction ("right," "peers") to concrete objects, as if grounding the moral argument in material reality will make it more compelling. Venice-glass — delicate, expensive Murano glassware — represents the be[[love-theme|love]]d's refinement and fragility; the speaker's "poison" would shatter it.

### Key Prosodic Features

- The opening question word "Can" [009-001] placed at the line's head creates immediate interrogative urgency
- The word "renunciative" [009-007] is prosodically excessive — four syllables where two would do — and its very length enacts the self-denial it describes
- The volta-like exclamation "Out, alas!" [009-019] breaks the argument with a cry of sudden emotional intensity
- The closing half-line "let it pass" [009-027] uses two unstressed syllables after the emphatic "I only [[love-theme|love]] thee" to create a dying fall

## Themes

### Love as Moral Obligation

The poem's central question is ethical, not emotional: is it right to give impoverished [[love-theme|love]]? The speaker conceptualises [[love-theme|love]] as a transaction in which the be[[love-theme|love]]d deserves a return proportionate to his gift. Since her own emotional resources are diminished by [[grief-theme|grief]] and illness, she cannot make that return — hence the [[love-theme|love]] is "unjust" [009-025]. This moral framing is distinctively Victorian and distinguishes [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s sequence from more purely Romantic [[love-theme|love]] poetry.

### Costly Signalling

The catalogue of the speaker's meagre gifts — [[tear-imagery|tears]] [009-003], sighs [009-005], infrequent smiles [009-009] — functions as a form of costly signalling. By naming how little she can give, the speaker demonstrates the very honesty that she claims disqualifies her from [[love-theme|love]]. The paradox is structural: if she were truly incapable of generous [[love-theme|love]], she would not care enough to refuse it.

### The Final Concession

"Be[[love-theme|love]]d, I only [[love-theme|love]] thee! let it pass." [009-027] is the sonnet's rhetorical climax and anti-climax simultaneously. After fourteen lines of argument against [[love-theme|love]], the speaker concedes its existence and requests that the concession itself be ignored. This self-contradictory gesture captures the central dynamic of the early sonnets: the speaker talks herself out of [[love-theme|love]] in order to ensure that any [[love-theme|love]] that survives her arguments is real.
