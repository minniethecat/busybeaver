## Definition

In *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, heaven operates on three distinct but overlapping
registers: as **cosmic space** (the physical sky through which stars "roll"), as
**theological destination** (the dwelling-place of [[god|God]] and [[angel-imagery|angels]], the afterlife of
the [[soul-theme|soul]]), and as **erotic counterweight** (that which must be set aside, exchanged,
or redefined in the presence of earthly [[love-theme|love]]). [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]] inherits
heaven-imagery from both the [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]] tradition — where the be[[love-theme|love]]d is
routinely figured as heavenly — and from her own dissenting Protestant background,
which emphasized the direct, unmediated relationship between the individual [[soul-theme|soul]]
and [[god|God]]. The sonnets' distinctive contribution is to place these two traditions in
productive tension: [[love-theme|love]] does not ascend to heaven so much as it brings heaven
down to earth.

## Occurrences

### Heaven as obstacle

> And, heaven being rolled between us at the end, / We should but vow the faster
> for the stars. [002-013]

[[sonnet-ii|Sonnet II]] introduces heaven as a spatial obstruction: it is "rolled between us"
like a curtain or a scroll. Yet the obstruction paradoxically strengthens the
[[love-theme|love]]rs' bond — "we should but vow the faster for the stars." The stars that
compose heaven become witnesses to, rather than impediments to, [[love-theme|love]]. This
reversal — obstacle transmuted into reinforcement — is characteristic of the
sonnet sequence's rhetoric: whatever separates the [[love-theme|love]]rs ultimately binds them
more tightly.

### Heaven displaced by the beloved

> The names of country, heaven, are changed away / For where thou art or shalt be,
> there or here [007-010]

[[sonnet-vii|Sonnet VII]]'s declaration is among the most radical in the sequence. Both "country"
and "heaven" — patriotism and piety, the two great loyalties of Victorian life —
are "changed away" in favor of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence. The reorientation is total:
the be[[love-theme|love]]d becomes the speaker's geography ("where thou art") and her eschatology
("or shalt be"). Heaven is not denied; it is redefined as wherever the be[[love-theme|love]]d is.

### Stars in heaven: the anxiety of abundance

> Too many stars, though each in heaven shall roll, / Too many flowers, though each
> shall crown the year? [021-010]

[[sonnet-xxi|Sonnet XXI]] shifts from theological to natural register. The stars rolling through
heaven become a figure for the [[love-theme|love]]r's repeated declarations — "Say thou dost [[love-theme|love]]
me, [[love-theme|love]] me, [[love-theme|love]] me" [021-010] — which, like stars, can never be too many. The
heavenly host is invoked not as object of worship but as rhetorical proof that
abundance does not diminish value.

### Heavenly dews: grace as natural process

> The lilies of our lives may reassure / Their blossoms from their roots, accessible
> / Alone to heavenly dews that drop not fewer [024-012]

[[sonnet-xxiv|Sonnet XXIV]]'s "heavenly dews" image merges the natural and the divine. The dews are
"heavenly" — they descend from above — but they function organically, nourishing
lilies through roots. Grace here is not a dramatic intervention but a steady,
unseen sustenance, "dropping not fewer." The image anticipates the sonnet
sequence's broader reconciliation of sacred and profane [[love-theme|love]].

### Heaven exchanged for earth

> I yield the grave for thy sake, and exchange / My near sweet view of Heaven for
> earth with thee! [022-016]

In [[sonnet-xxii|Sonnet XXII]], the speaker makes the sequence's most audacious theological claim.
She possesses a "near sweet view of Heaven" — proximity to the divine, earned
through [[grief-theme|suffering]] and illness — and she voluntarily *exchanges* it for "earth with
thee." The verb "exchange" is precise: this is a transaction, not a loss. Earth
*with* the be[[love-theme|love]]d is worth more than Heaven *without* him. The line inverts the
conventional Christian hierarchy of values and announces [[love-theme|love]]'s power to redefine
the cosmic order.

### The music of Heaven

> Fond voices, which, being drawn and reconciled / Into the music of Heaven's
> undefiled, / Call me no longer. [031-009]

[[sonnet-xxxi|Sonnet XXXI]] returns heaven to its [[music-theme|music]]al register — the heavenly choir, the
harmony of the blessed. But here the "[[music-theme|music]] of Heaven's undefiled" is a silencing
force: the reconciled voices "call me no longer," leaving the speaker in [[silence]]
"on the bier." Heaven, in this late-sonnet turn, is no longer displaced by [[love-theme|love]]
but figured as a realm of perfected sound against which earthly utterance —
including [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnets]] themselves — falls [[silence|silent]].

### The new Heavens

> The patient angel waiting for a place / In the new Heavens — because nor sin nor
> woe, / Nor God's infliction, nor death's neighbourhood [040-010]

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]L's "new Heavens" draw on Revelation 21:1 ("a new heaven and a new earth")
to project an eschatological horizon in which the speaker's [[grief-theme|suffering]] is finally
redeemed. The "patient [[angel-imagery|angel]]" waiting for placement in the new order mirrors the
speaker's own patience throughout the sequence — waiting for [[love-theme|love]], for health, for
recognition, for the transformation that only the be[[love-theme|love]]d (and [[god|God]]) can bring.

## Literary Context

### Petrarchan heaven vs. EBB's heaven

In the Petrarchan tradition, the be[[love-theme|love]]d is routinely elevated to heaven — Laura
is an [[angel-imagery|angel]]ic figure whose [[death-theme|death]] only confirms her celestial nature. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]] radically
revises this schema. Her speaker does not ascend to heaven to join the be[[love-theme|love]]d;
she descends from her "near sweet view of Heaven" [022-016] to join him on earth.
The direction of desire is downward, not upward — a movement that valorizes the
earthly, the bodily, and the present over the celestial, the [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual, and the
deferred.

### Dissenting theology

[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]] was raised in a dissenting (Congregationalist) tradition that emphasized the
priesthood of all believers and the direct accessibility of [[god|God]] without
ecclesiastical mediation. This theological formation shapes her comfort in
redefining heaven's relationship to earthly [[love-theme|love]] — she speaks of trading heaven
for earth not as a heretic but as one for whom the boundary between sacred and
profane [[love-theme|love]] has always been permeable.
