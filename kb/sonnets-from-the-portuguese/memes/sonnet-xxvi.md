## Full Text

> I lived with visions for my company [026-001]
>  [026-002]
> Instead of men and women, years ago, [026-003]
>  [026-004]
> And found them gentle mates, nor thought to know [026-005]
>  [026-006]
> A sweeter music than they played to me. [026-007]
>  [026-008]
> But soon their trailing purple was not free [026-009]
>  [026-010]
> Of this world’s dust, their lutes did silent grow, [026-011]
>  [026-012]
> And I myself grew faint and blind below [026-013]
>  [026-014]
> Their vanishing eyes. Then *thou* didst come—to *be*, [026-015]
>  [026-016]
> Beloved, what they *seemed*. Their shining fronts, [026-017]
>  [026-018]
> Their songs, their splendours—better, yet the same, [026-019]
>  [026-020]
> As river-water hallowed into fonts— [026-021]
>  [026-022]
> Met in thee, and from out thee overcame [026-023]
>  [026-024]
> My soul with satisfaction of all wants— [026-025]
>  [026-026]
> Because God’s gifts put man’s best dreams to shame. [026-027]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXVI [026-001]–[026-027]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXVI is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The poem continues the sequence's exploration of [[love-theme|love]], devotion, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transformation that [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] develops across the collection.

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form, with an octave establishing a premise or situation, followed by a sestet that develops, complicates, or resolves it. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s characteristic voice — at once intimate and rhetorically elevated — is present throughout, blending personal emotion with the formal demands of the sonnet tradition.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXVI occupies a specific position within the forty-four-sonnet arc. The sequence moves from the speaker's initial disbelief that she could be [[love-theme|love]]d (Sonnets I–VII), through surrender and reciprocal devotion (Sonnets VIII–XXVIII), to a [[dream|vision]] of [[love-theme|love]] that transcends [[death-theme|mortality]] (Sonnets XXIX–XLIV). Each sonnet contributes a distinct emotional or rhetorical development to this larger narrative.
