## Analysis

Sonnet XI is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The poem continues the sequence's exploration of [[love-theme|love]], devotion, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transformation.

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form (octave ABBA ABBA, sestet with varied pattern), employing [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s characteristic blend of intimate address and rhetorical elevation.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XI occupies its position within the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief through surrender to transcendent [[love-theme|love]]. Each sonnet contributes a distinct emotional or rhetorical development to this larger narrative.

## Literary Context

Sonnet XI continues [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]'s concessive argument with "And therefore" [011-001], building a chain of logical connectors ("Yet" in X, "And therefore" in XI) that makes these sonnets feel like a philosophical treatise in verse. The speaker reasons: if loving can be a form of merit, then the be[[love-theme|love]]d, who [[love-theme|love]]s, is deserving — regardless of the speaker's own worth.

The sonnet introduces a new register of humility: the speaker grants that the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[love-theme|love]] is not a mistake or a condescension but a genuine response to something real. "I [[love-theme|love]] thee to the depth and breadth and height / My [[soul-theme|soul]] can reach" — no, those are [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII's words. Sonnet XI is more modest: "I [[love-theme|love]] thee with a [[love-theme|love]] I seemed to lose / With my lost saints" [011-005]–[011-006], linking romantic [[love-theme|love]] to the speaker's childhood religious [[religious-faith|faith]].

## Structure and Prosody

The rhyme scheme is Petrarchan ABBA ABBA CDCDCD. The sestet's alternate rhymes create a gentler [[music-theme|music]] than the octave's envelope rhymes, appropriate to the poem's movement from argument to confession.

## Themes

### Love as Faith

The comparison of the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[love-theme|love]] to "my lost saints" [011-006] connects romantic [[love-theme|love]] with childhood religious devotion. The speaker's loss of [[religious-faith|faith]] and recovery of something analogous through [[love-theme|love]] is one of the sequence's deepest emotional currents, linking to the [[religious-faith]] theme elsewhere.

### Merit and Desert

The word "desert" [011-001] — meaning "that which is deserved" — continues the sequence's ethical vocabulary. The speaker asks not whether she deserves [[love-theme|love]] (the answer is clearly no) but whether [[love-theme|love]] itself can be a form of deserving. This is a characteristically [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] move: reframing a personal problem as a philosophical question.
