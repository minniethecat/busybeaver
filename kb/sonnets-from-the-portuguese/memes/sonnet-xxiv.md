## Full Text

> Let the world's sharpness like a clasping knife [024-001]
> Shut in upon itself and do no harm [024-002]
> In this close hand of Love, now soft and warm; [024-003]
> And let us hear no sound of human strife [024-004]
> After the click of the shutting. Life to life— [024-005]
> I lean upon thee, Dear, without alarm, [024-006]
> And feel as safe as guarded by a charm [024-007]
> Against the stab of worldlings, who if rife [024-008]
> Are weak to injure. Very whitely still [024-009]
> The lilies of our lives may reassure [024-010]
> Their blossoms from their roots, accessible [024-011]
> Alone to heavenly dews that drop not fewer; [024-012]
> Growing straight, out of man's reach, on the hill. [024-013]
> God only, who made us rich, can make us poor. [024-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXIV [024-001]–[024-014]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXIV of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) imagines [[love-theme|love]] as a protective enclosure. The world's sharpness is figured as a "clasping knife" that, held within Love's hand, closes harmlessly upon itself.

The octave establishes this image of sanctuary through the "click of the shutting" — a domestic, almost quotidian sound that seals the [[love-theme|love]]rs from "human strife." The sestet extends the metaphor with the lilies "growing straight, out of man's reach, on the hill" — an image of purity sustained by heavenly dew alone. The final line — "[[god|God]] only, who made us rich, can make us poor" — asserts the divine origin and protection of their [[love-theme|love]].

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXIV consolidates the protective and transcendent dimensions of [[love-theme|love]] that earlier sonnets have introduced separately. It follows the life-affirming [[sonnet-xxiii|Sonnet XXIII]] with an image of [[love-theme|love]] as sanctuary against the world.
