## Full Text

> Is it indeed so? If I lay here dead, [023-001]
> Wouldst thou miss any life in losing mine? [023-002]
> And would the sun for thee more coldly shine, [023-003]
> Because of grave-damps falling round my head? [023-004]
> I marvelled, my Beloved, when I read [023-005]
> Thy thought so in the letter. I am thine— [023-006]
> But—*so* much to thee? Can I pour thy wine [023-007]
> While my hands tremble? Then my soul, instead [023-008]
> Of dreams of death, resumes life's lower range. [023-009]
> Then, love me, Love! look on me—breathe on me! [023-010]
> As brighter ladies do not count it strange, [023-011]
> For love, to give up acres and degree, [023-012]
> I yield the grave for thy sake, and exchange [023-013]
> My near sweet view of Heaven for earth with thee! [023-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXIII [023-001]–[023-014]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXIII of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) begins with a startling hypothetical question: "Is it indeed so? If I lay here dead, / Wouldst thou miss any life in losing mine?" The speaker reads a letter from the be[[love-theme|love]]d and marvels at being so valued.

The octave's doubting questions give way to the sestet's transformative answer: the speaker's [[soul-theme|soul]], instead of [[dream|dreaming]] of [[death-theme|death]], "resumes life's lower range." The concluding couplet dramatizes the ultimate exchange — the speaker yields the grave and heaven itself for earthly life with the be[[love-theme|love]]d.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXIII is one of the sequence's most dramatic reversals, where the be[[love-theme|love]]d's written word pulls the speaker back from [[death-theme|death]]-obsession toward life. It demonstrates the sequence's epistolary dimension — many sonnets respond to the be[[love-theme|love]]d's letters.
