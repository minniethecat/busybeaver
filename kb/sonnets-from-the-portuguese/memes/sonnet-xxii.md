## Full Text

> When our two souls stand up erect and strong, [022-001]
>  [022-002]
> Face to face, silent, drawing nigh and nigher, [022-003]
>  [022-004]
> Until the lengthening wings break into fire [022-005]
>  [022-006]
> At either curved point—what bitter wrong [022-007]
>  [022-008]
> Can the earth do to us, that we should not long [022-009]
>  [022-010]
> Be here contented? Think. In mounting higher, [022-011]
>  [022-012]
> The angels would press on us, and aspire [022-013]
>  [022-014]
> To drop some golden orb of perfect song [022-015]
>  [022-016]
> Into our deep, dear silence. Let us stay [022-017]
>  [022-018]
> Rather on earth, Beloved—where the unfit [022-019]
>  [022-020]
> Contrarious moods of men recoil away [022-021]
>  [022-022]
> And isolate pure spirits, and permit [022-023]
>  [022-024]
> A place to stand and love in for a day, [022-025]
>  [022-026]
> With darkness and the death-hour rounding it. [022-027]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XXII [022-001]–[022-027]

## Analysis

Sonnet XXII is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The poem continues the sequence's exploration of [[love-theme|love]], devotion, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transformation that [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] develops across the collection.

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form, with an octave establishing a premise or situation, followed by a sestet that develops, complicates, or resolves it. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s characteristic voice — at once intimate and rhetorically elevated — is present throughout, blending personal emotion with the formal demands of the sonnet tradition.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet XXII occupies a specific position within the forty-four-sonnet arc. The sequence moves from the speaker's initial disbelief that she could be [[love-theme|love]]d (Sonnets I–VII), through surrender and reciprocal devotion (Sonnets VIII–XXVIII), to a [[dream|vision]] of [[love-theme|love]] that transcends [[death-theme|mortality]] (Sonnets XXIX–XLIV). Each sonnet contributes a distinct emotional or rhetorical development to this larger narrative.
