## Analysis

Sonnet III is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846). The poem continues the sequence's exploration of [[love-theme|love]], devotion, and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual transformation.

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form (octave ABBA ABBA, sestet with varied pattern), employing [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s characteristic blend of intimate address and rhetorical elevation.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet III occupies its position within the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief through surrender to transcendent [[love-theme|love]]. Each sonnet contributes a distinct emotional or rhetorical development to this larger narrative.

## Literary Context

Sonnet III extends the sequence's early meditation on the inequality between speaker and be[[love-theme|love]]d. If [[sonnet-ii|Sonnet II]] dramatises a vertical separation ([[god|God]] vs. the [[love-theme|love]]rs), Sonnet III maps inequality onto the horizontal plane of social difference: the be[[love-theme|love]]d is a "princely Heart" [003-001], a "guest for queens" [003-011], and "chief [[music-theme|musician]]" [003-017] while the speaker is a "poor, tired, wandering singer" [003-021] leaning against a cypress tree.

The poem opens with the word "Unlike" repeated twice, emphatically establishing the theme: "Unlike are we, unlike, O princely Heart!" [003-001]. This is followed by "Unlike our uses and our destinies" [003-003], enriching the initial exclamation with an explanation. The anaphora creates a rhetorical force that mirrors the speaker's distress.

Angela Leighton, in *[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]* (1986), identifies the "wandering singer" image as [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s self-portrait as poet — an artist working in obscurity, contrasted with [[robert-browning|Browning]]'s public career. The cypress tree, traditionally a symbol of mourning, links the speaker's poetic identity to [[death-theme|death]] and loss even before the poem's final couplet.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet III uses the Petrarchan form with a distinctive two-line gap pattern — blank lines in the text between each verse line — visually emphasising the fragmentation that the poem's content describes. This typographical feature is present in the 1850 *Poems* edition.

The octave (lines 1–8) develops the [[angel-imagery|angel]] metaphor: the [[love-theme|love]]rs' "ministering two [[angel-imagery|angels]]" [003-005] strike their wings against each other in surprise, a physical image of how mismatched the [[love-theme|love]]rs appear even to celestial beings. The sestet shifts register: from [[angel-imagery|angel]]ic perspective to the speaker's self-characterisation as a "wandering singer" [003-021] beneath a cypress tree.

The final couplet [003-025]–[003-027] is among the most striking in the sequence. "The chrism is on thine head — on mine, the dew" sets sacred anointment against natural moisture, crown against earth. "And Death must dig the level where these agree" is both a memento mori and an egalitarian assertion: only in the grave will the social and [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual gulf between them be closed.

### Key Prosodic Features

- The repeated "Unlike" [003-001] creates a spondaic opening that disrupts the expected iambic rhythm
- The dash in line [003-025] after "The chrism is on thine head" creates a dramatic pause before the deflating contrast "on mine, the dew"
- The rhyme "tree" / "agree" [003-023]–[003-027] bookends the sestet with the poem's most significant thematic word

## Themes

### Social Inequality

The poem's explicit subject is the difference in social standing between the [[love-theme|love]]rs. The be[[love-theme|love]]d is elevated — fit for queens and royal pageantry [003-011] — while the speaker presents herself as a marginal figure, wandering in darkness. This dramatises [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s real position: she was an established but reclusive poet, while [[robert-browning|Browning]] was a younger, socially mobile man about town.

### Angelology

The "ministering two [[angel-imagery|angels]]" [003-005] introduce the sequence's [[angel-imagery]] in concrete, almost comic terms: they "look surprise" and "strike athwart / Their wings in passing" [003-005]–[003-008]. The [[angel-imagery|angels]] function as objective witnesses to the mismatch, validating the speaker's sense of inadequacy from a divine perspective.

### The Cypress Tree

The cypress [003-023] is not accidental: in Victorian floral symbolism, cypress signifies mourning and [[death-theme|death]]. The speaker's self-portrait as a singer "leaning up a cypress tree" conflates poetic identity with [[death-theme|mortality]], preparing for the final couplet where Death becomes the great equaliser [003-027].
