## Overview

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII — universally known by its opening line, "How do I [[love-theme|love]] thee? Let me count
the ways" — is the forty-third poem in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *Sonnets from the
Portuguese* (1850) and the most anthologized [[love-theme|love]] sonnet in the English language. Written
during her secret [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] (1845–1846), the sonnet distills the
sequence's central concerns — the nature of [[love-theme|love]], the relationship between earthly and
divine devotion, and the transformation of personal [[grief-theme|suffering]] into poetic utterance —
into fourteen lines of extraordinary compression and rhetorical power.

The sonnet's opening question and its enumerative answer constitute one of the most
recognizable rhetorical structures in English poetry:

> How do I love thee? Let me count the ways. [043-001]

## Textual Analysis

### The sonnet's architecture

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII follows the Petrarchan (Italian) [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|sonnet form]]: an octave (lines 1–8) rhyming
ABBA ABBA, followed by a sestet (lines 9–14) rhyming CDCDCD. The volta (turn) occurs
at line 9, where the poem shifts from abstract, spatial metaphors of [[love-theme|love]] to the concrete,
embodied language of "old [[grief-theme|grief]]s," "childhood's [[religious-faith|faith]]," and "lost saints."

The octave presents five ways of loving, each introduced by the anaphoric "I [[love-theme|love]] thee":

| Way | Quotation | Dimension |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| 1 | "to the depth and breadth and height / My [[soul-theme|soul]] can reach" [043-001] | Spatial / [[soul-theme|spirit]]ual |
| 2 | "to the level of everyday's / Most quiet need, by sun and candle[[light-imagery|light]]" [043-001] | Temporal / domestic |
| 3 | "freely, as men strive for Right" [043-001] | Ethical / volitional |
| 4 | "purely, as they turn from Praise" [043-001] | Moral / self-effacing |
| 5 | "with the passion put to use / In my old [[grief-theme|grief]]s" [043-001] | Transformative / redemptive |

### Spatial and cosmic dimension

The poem's first metaphor reaches outward and upward — "depth and breadth and height"
[043-001] — a three-dimensional mapping of [[love-theme|love]] onto the cosmos. This spatial language
echoes the Pauline epistles (Ephesians 3:18–19: "the breadth, and length, and depth,
and height") and anchors the poem's erotic intensity in sacred rhetoric. The [[soul-theme|soul]]
"feeling out of sight / For the ends of Being and ideal Grace" [043-001] transforms
[[love-theme|love]] into a theological venture: the be[[love-theme|love]]d is approached through the language of
mystical union with [[god|God]].

### The domestic turn

The second way introduces a deliberate anticlimax: from cosmic "depth and breadth and
height" to "the level of everyday's / Most quiet need, by sun and candle[[light-imagery|light]]" [043-001].
This is the distinctively Victorian (and distinctively [[robert-browning|Browning]]) move — the sacralization
of domestic life. Love is not only sublime transport but also the quiet constancy of
daily companionship, symbolized by the alternation of "sun" (day) and "candle[[light-imagery|light]]"
(night).

### From grief to faith

> I love thee with the passion put to use / In my old griefs, and with my childhood's
> faith. [043-001]

The sestet traces [[love-theme|love]]'s genealogy in the speaker's own emotional history. "Old [[grief-theme|grief]]s"
refers to the chronic illness and loss that marked [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s life before meeting [[robert-browning|Robert]]
[[robert-browning|Browning]] — [[grief-theme|grief]] that is not discarded but *repurposed* ("put to use") as fuel for
[[love-theme|love]]. "Childhood's [[religious-faith|faith]]" and "lost saints" [043-001] evoke the ev[[angel-imagery|angel]]ical piety
of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]]'s upbringing, now redirected from religious devotion to human [[love-theme|love]] without
losing its intensity.

### The eschatological close

The sonnet ends with a daring theological claim:

> and, if God choose, / I shall but love thee better after death. [043-001]

This is not a conventional pious hope but a radical revaluation: [[love-theme|love]], not [[religious-faith|faith]], is
the virtue that survives the grave. The conditional "if [[god|God]] choose" preserves doctrinal
decorum while the main clause stakes a claim for human [[love-theme|love]]'s eternal dignity. It is
the logical culmination of a sequence that has repeatedly blurred the line between
erotic and divine devotion.

## Literary Significance

### Reception and canonization

[[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII has achieved an iconic status rare for any single sonnet. Its opening line
has entered the common language as a declaration of [[love-theme|love]], while the enumerative structure
("I [[love-theme|love]] thee... I [[love-theme|love]] thee...") has been endlessly imitated, parodied, and adapted.
The poem appears in wedding readings, Valentine's Day cards, and popular culture, where
it retains a direct emotional power that belies its complex theological and literary
architecture.

### Within the sonnet sequence

Within *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII functions as the sequence's lyrical
summit. The early sonnets trace the speaker's movement from melancholy and self-doubt
toward [[love-theme|love]]; the later sonnets reflect on [[love-theme|love]]'s challenges and endurance. [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]]LIII
sits at the apex, a moment of pure, unqualified affirmation in which all the sequence's
tensions — between past and present, [[grief-theme|grief]] and joy, earth and heaven, self and be[[love-theme|love]]d —
are temporarily resolved into enumeration and praise.

> I love thee—mark!—I love thee—in thy sight / I stand transfigured, glorified aright
> [010-006]

This earlier declaration from [[sonnet-x|Sonnet X]] anticipates the structural role of XLIII: a
pivotal moment of transfiguration in which the speaker moves from private feeling to
public declaration.

### The Petrarchan form

Like all 44 sonnets in the sequence, XLIII uses the Petrarchan (Italian) form, which
[[elizabeth-barrett-browning|EBB]] learned from studying the *Rime Sparse* of Petrarch (1304–1374). The Petrarchan
sonnet's di[[dream|vision]] into octave and sestet — problem and resolution, observation and
reflection — is ideally suited to XLIII's rhetorical strategy: the octave poses a
question and enumerates answers; the sestet deepens those answers through personal
history and theological speculation.
