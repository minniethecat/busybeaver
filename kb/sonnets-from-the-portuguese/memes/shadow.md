# Shadow (Motif)

## Definition

Shadow and shade form a recurring motif across [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, appearing in six of the forty-four sonnets (I, VI, XVII, XVIII, XIX, XXIX). The motif carries three distinct but interwoven meanings: first, shadow as the darkness of [[grief-theme|grief]] and [[death-theme|mortality]] cast across the speaker's life before [[love-theme|love]]'s arrival; second, the be[[love-theme|love]]d's shadow as a protective, habitable presence in which the speaker dwells; and third, shade as a poetic shelter — a space in which the speaker can sing without the exposure of full [[light-imagery|light]]. The motif's trajectory from the "shadow across me" of [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]] [001-009] to breathing "within thy shadow a new air" of [[sonnet-xxix|Sonnet XXIX]] [029-013] traces the speaker's movement from passive [[grief-theme|suffering]] to active inhabitation of [[love-theme|love]]'s overshadowing presence.

Unlike the conventional Petrarchan shadow (which typically signifies the be[[love-theme|love]]d's absence or the poet's melancholy), [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s shadows are places of *presence* — the be[[love-theme|love]]d's shadow is not what remains when he departs but the atmosphere in which the speaker lives when he is near.

## Occurrences in the Sonnets

### The Shadow of the Past: Sonnet I

The shadow makes its first appearance in [[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|the sonnet sequence]]'s opening poem as an image of retrospective [[grief-theme|grief]]. The speaker, reflecting on the "sweet, sad years, the melancholy years" of her own life [001-007], recalls how those years "by turns had flung / A shadow across me" [001-008]. The verb "flung" is violent — shadow here is not a gentle dimming but an assault, each year casting its darkness upon the speaker as if in succession. The shadow is immediately followed by the "mystic Shape" that "drew me backward by the hair" [001-010], linking the shadow of the past to the spectral presence of Death. The sonnet's final revelation — "Not Death, but Love" [001-014] — reframes the shadow retrospectively: what the speaker experienced as [[death-theme|death]]'s darkness was, unknown to her, [[love-theme|love]]'s approach.

### Dwelling in the Beloved's Shadow: Sonnet VI

[[sonnet-vi|Sonnet VI]] develops the shadow motif into the sequence's most sustained meditation on [[love-theme|love]] as habitation. The speaker, having been commanded "Go from me" [006-001], replies: "Yet I feel that I shall stand / Henceforth in thy shadow" [006-001]. The shadow here is neither threatening nor melancholy — it is a permanent condition of existence, the atmosphere in which the speaker now lives. The lines that follow elaborate the consequences: "Nevermore / Alone upon the threshold of my door / Of individual life, I shall command / The uses of my [[soul-theme|soul]]" [006-002]. To stand in the be[[love-theme|love]]d's shadow is to lose autonomous selfhood — the speaker can no longer exercise her [[soul-theme|soul]]'s "uses" independently, "nor lift my hand / Serenely in the sunshine as before" [006-005]. The contrast between "shadow" and "sunshine" is crucial: the sunshine of solitary life is serene but thin; the shadow of [[love-theme|love]] is darker but rich with shared existence, as the wine "Must taste of its own grapes" [006-012] and prayer to [[god|God]] includes "that name of thine" [006-013].

### The Shade as Poetic Shelter: Sonnet XVII

In [[sonnet-xvii|Sonnet XVII]], the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[music-theme|music]]al gifts are contrasted with the speaker's own role. After enumerating what the be[[love-theme|love]]d can do — pour "Antidotes / Of medicated [[music-theme|music]]" [017-005] — the speaker asks: "How, Dearest, wilt thou have me for most use?" [017-010]. The answer comes as a series of four images, each diminishing in vitality: "A hope, to sing by gladly? or a fine / Sad memory, with thy [[music-theme|song]]s to interfuse? / A shade, in which to sing — of palm or pine? / A grave, on which to rest from [[music-theme|singing]]?" [017-011]. The "shade" here is explicitly a place for [[music-theme|song]] — it shelters the speaker's voice without exposing it to full scrutiny. The alternatives — palm (triumph) or pine (mourning) — suggest that the quality of the shade (triumphal or elegiac) matters less than its function as a space in which the speaker can continue to make [[music-theme|music]].

### The Lock's Shade: Sonnets XVIII–XIX

Sonnets XVIII and XIX, the paired "lock of hair" sonnets, use shade in a more literal sense: the darkness of the exchanged locks. In [[sonnet-xviii|Sonnet XVIII]], the speaker's hair "only may / Now shade on two pale cheeks, the mark of [[tear-imagery|tears]]" [018-009] — the shade of hair on a grieving face, a physical darkness that mirrors the emotional darkness of "[[grief-theme|sorrow]]'s trick" [018-011]. In [[sonnet-xix|Sonnet XIX]], the be[[love-theme|love]]d's dark curl carries "The bay-crown's shade" [019-008] — the shadow of poetic laurels that still "lingers on thy curl, it is so black" [019-009]. The speaker performs a ritual act: "Thus, with a fillet of smooth-[[kiss-imagery|kiss]]ing breath, / I tie the shadows safe from gliding back" [019-010]. The plural "shadows" encompasses both the physical darkness of the hair and the symbolic darkness of poetic inheritance, [[grief-theme|grief]], and [[love-theme|love]] — all "tied safe" by the speaker's breath, an image of intimate preservation.

### A New Air in the Shadow: Sonnet XXIX

The motif's final appearance in [[sonnet-xxix|Sonnet XXIX]] reverses its initial valence. The speaker's thoughts of the be[[love-theme|love]]d are figured as entwining vines that threaten to obscure the be[[love-theme|love]]d himself: "my thoughts do twine and bud / [[About]] thee, as wild vines about a tree" [029-001]. She demands that the be[[love-theme|love]]d renew his presence directly, stripping away the vines of thought so that she may "breathe within thy shadow a new air" [029-013]. The shadow here, as in [[sonnet-vi|Sonnet VI]], is the be[[love-theme|love]]d's immediate presence — but now it is a source of "new air," fresh and life-giving. The poem's final line — "I do not think of thee — I am too near thee" [029-014] — completes the motif's arc: shadows, which in [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]] signified the intrusive darkness of the past, have become the medium of the most intimate proximity imaginable, a closeness so complete it dissolves the need for thought.

## Development Across the Sequence

The shadow motif follows a pattern of reversal. In the opening sonnet, shadow is invasive and melancholic — the darkness cast by years of [[grief-theme|grief]]. By [[sonnet-vi|Sonnet VI]], shadow has become a dwelling-place, the be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence so encompassing that the speaker "shall stand / Henceforth in thy shadow" [006-001]. The middle sonnets (XVII–XIX) develop the motif into a poetics of shelter and preservation — shade as the condition of [[music-theme|song]], shadow as that which can be "tied safe" from loss. [[sonnet-xxix|Sonnet XXIX]]'s "new air" [029-013] completes the transformation: shadow is no longer a diminishment of [[light-imagery|light]] but its own positive atmosphere, the air of [[love-theme|love]] itself.

This trajectory from threat to shelter mirrors the sequence's broader movement from [[death-theme|death]] to [[love-theme|love]], from solitude to union. The shadow that once "flung" itself across the speaker is, by the end, the atmosphere she breathes.

## Literary Context

The shadow motif has deep roots in the Petrarchan tradition. In Petrarch's *Canzoniere*, the be[[love-theme|love]]d Laura is frequently associated with shade and shadow — the laurel tree (*lauro*) under which the poet seeks shelter, the shadow of absence that falls when she departs. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] inherits this tradition but transforms it: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's shadow in her sonnets is not a sign of absence (as Petrarch's often is) but of overwhelming presence. Where Petrarch's shadows mark what has been lost, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s shadows mark what has been found.

The motif also engages the Platonic and Christian traditions of [[light-imagery|light]] and darkness. In Platonic thought, shadow is the imperfect copy of the Form — a lesser reality. In Christian mysticism, particularly the *via negativa*, the "cloud of unknowing" or "shadow of [[god|God]]" signifies divine presence too overwhelming to be perceived directly. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s shadows draw on both traditions: the shadow of [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]] participates in the Platonic shadow-as-diminishment, while the shadow of Sonnets VI and XXIX participates in the mystical shadow-as-overwhelming-presence. The sequence moves, in effect, from Plato to the mystics.
