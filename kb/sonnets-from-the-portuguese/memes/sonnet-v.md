## Analysis

Sonnet V is one of the forty-four [[petrarchan-sonnet-form|Petrarchan sonnet]]s in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850), composed during her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]].

The sonnet follows the Petrarchan form, with an octave (ABBA ABBA) and a sestet. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s voice blends intimate address with formal elevation, characteristic of the sequence.

## Place in the Sequence

Sonnet V contributes to the forty-four-sonnet arc tracing the speaker's movement from initial disbelief and melancholy through surrender and reciprocal devotion to transcendent [[love-theme|love]].

## Literary Context

Sonnet V is among the most dramatic and classically allusive sonnets in the sequence. It opens with a direct invocation of the Electra myth: the speaker "lifts her heavy [[heart-imagery|heart]]" as "once Electra her sepulchral urn" [005-001]–[005-003]. Electra, in Sophocles' tragedy, holds what she believes to be her brother Orestes' ashes — an urn she mistakes as containing his remains, though he lives. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] repurposes this image: the speaker carries not ashes of the be[[love-theme|love]]d but the ashes of her own [[grief-theme|grief]], and the be[[love-theme|love]]d's presence causes her to "overturn / The ashes at thy feet" [005-005]–[005-007].

This classical allusion serves multiple functions. It elevates the personal [[love-theme|love]] lyric to the dignity of tragedy, establishes [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s erudition (she read Greek fluently), and dramatises the risk the speaker is taking. As Dorothy Mermin observes, the Electra allusion introduces the idea that what appears dead may be alive — a metaphor for the speaker's emotional state throughout the early sonnets.

The poem marks a turning point. Unlike Sonnets II–IV, which catalogue the speaker's unworthiness, Sonnet V shifts to warning: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's proximity to the speaker's [[grief-theme|grief]] is dangerous. "Stand further off then! Go." [005-027] is the most direct rejection in the sequence so far, yet its very directness signals the growing intensity of feeling it denies.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet V follows the Petrarchan form. The octave (ABBA ABBA) presents the Electra simile and the overturning of the [[grief-theme|grief]]-urn; the sestet (CDCDCD or variant) draws out the consequences: either the be[[love-theme|love]]d tramples the ashes or the wind blows them up and they scorch him.

The poem's syntax is exceptionally violent for a [[love-theme|love]] sonnet. The verbs are physical and destructive: "overturn," "tread," "scorch," "shred" [005-005], [005-014], [005-025], [005-026]. The "red wild sparkles" [005-011] that "dimly burn" within the ashes suggest embers — [[grief-theme|grief]] is not dead but dormant, and contact with the be[[love-theme|love]]d risks rekindling it into a conflagration.

The octave's ABBA envelope rhymes create a sense of enclosure appropriate to the conceit of an urn — the poem's form mirrors the containment of [[grief-theme|grief]]. The sestet's shift to a more varied rhyme pattern corresponds to the poem's warning about what happens when that containment breaks.

### Key Prosodic Features

- The opening line's iambic pentameter is interrupted by "heavy [[heart-imagery|heart]]" [005-001] — two stressed syllables that enact the weight
- The enjambment at [005-013]–[005-015] ("If thy foot in scorn / Could tread them out to darkness utterly") spans three lines, creating a syntactic suspension that mirrors the poised threat
- The closing imperative "Stand further off then! Go." [005-027] uses three stressed monosyllables to end the poem with percussive finality

## Themes

### Classical Allusion

The Electra reference [005-003] connects the sonnet to Greek tragedy and specifically to Sophocles' *Electra*, in which the recognition scene between Electra and Orestes hinges on the urn. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s use of the image transforms it: her urn contains not another person's remains but her own [[grief-theme|grief]], and overturning it is an act of exposure rather than mourning.

### Grief as Fire

The "red wild sparkles" [005-011] burning through "ashen greyness" [005-013] present [[grief-theme|grief]] as latent fire rather than extinguished coal. This anticipates the sequence's development of [[fire-imagery]] in later sonnets. The warning — that the be[[love-theme|love]]d's laurels will not shield him from being scorched [005-021]–[005-026] — positions the speaker's emotional intensity as genuinely dangerous.

### The Rejection Trope

"Stand further off then! Go." [005-027] is the sequence's most explicit early rejection, but its very vehemence signals the opposite: the speaker is afraid not of the be[[love-theme|love]]d but of her own response to him. This pattern — rejection as self-protection — recurs in several later sonnets and contributes to the sequence's dramatic tension.
