## Overview

Sonnet XVII of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is addressed to "My poet" — the first explicit acknowledgment in the sequence that the be[[love-theme|love]]d is himself a writer. The speaker, having surrendered to [[love-theme|love]] in [[sonnet-xvi|Sonnet XVI]], now asks what use she can be to him, offering a catalogue of possible roles that descends from hope to grave.

## Full Text

> My poet, thou canst touch on all the notes [017-001]
> God set between His After and Before, [017-002]
> And strike up and strike off the general roar [017-003]
> Of the rushing worlds a melody that floats [017-004]
> In a serene air purely. Antidotes [017-005]
> Of medicated music, answering for [017-006]
> Mankind's forlornest uses, thou canst pour [017-007]
> From thence into their ears. God's will devotes [017-008]
> Thine to such ends, and mine to wait on thine! [017-009]
> How, Dearest, wilt thou have me for most use? [017-010]
> A hope, to sing by gladly? or a fine [017-011]
> Sad memory, with thy songs to interfuse? [017-012]
> A shade, in which to sing—of palm or pine? [017-013]
> A grave, on which to rest from singing? Choose. [017-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XVII [017-001]–[017-014]

## Analysis

### The Poet-Beloved

The sonnet opens with the direct address "My poet" [017-001], establishing that the be[[love-theme|love]]d is not merely a [[love-theme|love]]r but a fellow artist. [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]] was already an established poet when he began his correspondence with [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett]] in 1845, and this sonnet acknowledges that fact within the sequence's intimate address. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's poetic gift is figured as cosmic in scope: he can "touch on all the notes / [[god|God]] set between His After and Before" [017-001]–[017-002] — a temporal span that encompasses all of created time.

The imagery of the octave is [[music-theme|music]]al and medicinal. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's poetry is a "[[music-theme|melody]] that floats / In a serene air purely" [017-004]–[017-005], extracted from "the general roar / Of the rushing worlds" [017-003]–[017-004]. This is the Romantic poet's traditional labour — finding harmony in chaos. But [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] adds a therapeutic dimension: the be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[music-theme|music]] is "Antidotes / Of medicated [[music-theme|music]], answering for / Mankind's forlornest uses" [017-005]–[017-007]. Poetry here is not merely beautiful but healing.

### The Catalogue of Roles

The volta at line 9 — "[[god|God]]'s will devotes / Thine to such ends, and mine to wait on thine!" [017-008]–[017-009] — establishes the speaker's subordination to the be[[love-theme|love]]d's vocation. His work is divinely appointed; hers is to serve his. The question that follows — "How, Dearest, wilt thou have me for most use?" [017-010] — is remarkable for its humility, but the catalogue that follows is not merely self-abnegating.

The speaker offers four roles:
- **A hope** — "to sing by gladly" [017-011], a positive inspiration
- **A fine sad memory** — "with thy [[music-theme|song]]s to interfuse" [017-012], melancholy woven into art
- **A [[shadow|shade]]** — "in which to sing—of palm or pine" [017-013], the protective cover of [[love-theme|love]]
- **A grave** — "on which to rest from [[music-theme|singing]]" [017-014], the final resting place

The catalogue descends from life-affirming hope to [[death-theme|death]]-dealing grave, a progression that mirrors the sequence's larger arc from [[death-theme|death]]-[[shadow]]ed beginning to [[love-theme|love]]-transfigured end — but here in reverse. The single-word closure "Choose" [017-014] places the decision entirely in the be[[love-theme|love]]d's hands, an act of trust that is also an act of self-erasure.

## Literary Context

Sonnet XVII is unique in the sequence for explicitly naming the be[[love-theme|love]]d as a poet. The tradition of the poet addressing a fellow poet has classical precedents — Horace's epistles to Virgil, Dante's addresses to fellow *fedeli d'amore* — but [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s version is inflected by the particular dynamics of her relationship with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]]. She was the more famous poet when they met; his recognition came later. Her address to "My poet" is thus tinged with the generosities and anxieties of the established writer acknowledging the rising one.

The catalogue form — the [[love-theme|love]]r offering possible roles to the be[[love-theme|love]]d — echoes the *servitium amoris* (slavery of [[love-theme|love]]) tradition of Roman elegy, where the [[love-theme|love]]r-poet offers various services to the be[[love-theme|love]]d. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] transforms this tradition by making the service specifically poetic: the speaker's role is to enable the be[[love-theme|love]]d's art.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XVII employs ABBA ABBA CDCDCD, with the sestet's interlocking tercets creating a chain-like progression through the catalogue of roles. The enjambment throughout the octave — particularly across [017-003]–[017-004] and [017-006]–[017-007] — creates a flowing, [[music-theme|music]]al quality that enacts the "[[music-theme|melody]]" it describes.

Key prosodic moments:
- "My poet" [017-001] opens with a stressed syllable, the possessive pronoun claiming the be[[love-theme|love]]d as hers even as she subordinates herself to his art
- The alliteration in "strike up and strike off" [017-003] mimics the percussive action of [[music-theme|music]]al performance
- The final word "Choose" [017-014] is isolated by the caesura and the poem's close, a monosyllable that carries the weight of all that precedes it

## Themes

### Service and Vocation

The poem's central question — "How, Dearest, wilt thou have me for most use?" [017-010] — reframes [[love-theme|love]] as service. The speaker does not ask what the be[[love-theme|love]]d can do for her but what she can do for him. This reversal of the Petrarchan norm (where the lady is served) is characteristically [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]: [[love-theme|love]] is not reception but offering.

### The Descent into Silence

The catalogue's progression from hope to grave traces the speaker's movement toward self-dissolution. To be a "grave, on which to rest from [[music-theme|singing]]" [017-014] is to become the [[silence]] after the [[music-theme|song]] — the ultimate gift of the [[love-theme|love]]r to the poet.
