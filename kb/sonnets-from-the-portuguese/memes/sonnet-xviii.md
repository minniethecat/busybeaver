## Overview

Sonnet XVIII of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is the sequence's most intimate gift-poem. The speaker offers the be[[love-theme|love]]d a lock of her hair — a traditional [[love-theme|love]]-token in Victorian culture — but the gesture is freighted with personal history: the lock retains the [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] of the speaker's dead mother, making the gift a transmission of [[love-theme|love]] across generations.

## Full Text

> I never gave a lock of hair away [018-001]
> To a man, Dearest, except this to thee, [018-002]
> Which now upon my fingers thoughtfully [018-003]
> I ring out to the full brown length, and say: [018-004]
> "Take it." My day of youth went yesterday; [018-005]
> My hair no longer bounds to my foot's glee, [018-006]
> Nor plant I it from rose or myrtle-tree, [018-007]
> As girls do, any more: it only may [018-008]
> Now shade on two pale cheeks, the mark of tears, [018-009]
> Taught drooping from the head that hangs aside [018-010]
> Through sorrow's trick. I thought the funeral shears [018-011]
> Would take this first, but Love is justified: [018-012]
> Take it thou—finding pure, from all those years, [018-013]
> The kiss my mother left here when she died. [018-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XVIII [018-001]–[018-014]

## Analysis

### The Ritual of Giving

The sonnet opens with a declaration of exclusivity: "I never gave a lock of hair away / To a man, Dearest, except this to thee" [018-001]–[018-002]. The gift is unprecedented, and the speaker lingers over the physical gesture — "upon my fingers thoughtfully / I ring out to the full brown length" [018-003]–[018-004]. The verb "ring" suggests both the circular motion of winding hair and the ritual significance of a ring, another token of commitment.

### Age and Youth

The octave's second half is a meditation on aging. "My day of youth went yesterday" [018-005] — the line is wistful but not bitter. The speaker contrasts her present self with the girl she was: hair that once "bounds to my foot's glee" [018-006] now merely "[[shadow|shade]]s on two pale cheeks, the mark of [[tear-imagery|tears]]" [018-009]. The movement from vitality to stillness is the poem's emotional core: what is given is not the girlish curl of youth but the sober lock of a woman marked by [[grief-theme|sorrow]].

### The Funeral Shears

The volta introduces a darker register: "I thought the funeral shears / Would take this first" [018-011]–[018-012]. The speaker expected [[death-theme|death]] to claim her hair before [[love-theme|love]] did — an echo of the sequence's persistent association of [[love-theme|love]] with [[death-theme|mortality]]. "But Love is justified" [018-012]: [[love-theme|love]] has claimed what [[death-theme|death]] was expected to take, and the substitution is cause for celebration rather than mourning.

### The Mother's Kiss

The final line delivers the poem's most intimate revelation: the lock bears "The [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] my mother left here when she died" [018-014]. The gift is not merely the speaker's hair but a transmitted blessing — the mother's [[love-theme|love]], preserved in the physical trace of a [[kiss-imagery|kiss]], now passed to the be[[love-theme|love]]d. This layering of relationships (mother-daughter-be[[love-theme|love]]d) gives the sonnet a generational depth unusual in the [[love-theme|love]]-lyric tradition.

## Literary Context

The exchange of hair as a [[love-theme|love]]-token has a long history in both life and literature. In the Victorian period, hair jewelry and lockets containing a [[love-theme|love]]r's hair were common material expressions of affection and mourning. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s poem participates in this material culture while transcending it: the lock of hair is not merely a token but a palimpsest bearing the marks of the speaker's entire emotional history.

The hair-exchange sonnets (XVIII and XIX) form one of the sequence's paired structures, comparable to the diptychs in Petrarch's *Canzoniere* (such as sonnets 228–229 on Laura's [[death-theme|death]]). [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s innovation is to make the exchange explicitly reciprocal and physical.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XVIII uses ABBA ABBA CDCDCD. The octave's enclosed rhymes mirror the self-enclosure of the speaker's past — her hair, her [[grief-theme|sorrow]], her solitude — while the sestet's interlaced pattern enacts the opening outward toward the be[[love-theme|love]]d.

Key prosodic features:
- The parenthetical quotation — "Take it." [018-005] — is set off by the period, creating a dramatic pause within the octave's flow
- "My day of youth went yesterday" [018-005] uses internal rhyme (day/yesterday) to compress the passage of time into a single line
- The repeated "Take it" — first as command [018-005], then as invitation [018-013] — frames the gift with two different tonalities
- The final line's weight falls on "died" [018-014], a closure that is both mournful and, paradoxically, life-affirming

## Themes

### The Body as Archive

The lock of hair is a physical record of the speaker's life: it registered her youthful vitality, her adult [[grief-theme|sorrow]], her mother's [[kiss-imagery|kiss]], and now her [[love-theme|love]]. The body, in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s imagining, is not separate from the [[soul-theme|soul]] but its material archive — a surface on which [[love-theme|love]] and [[grief-theme|grief]] leave legible traces.

### Generational Transmission

The mother's [[kiss-imagery|kiss]] preserved on the hair creates a chain of [[love-theme|love]] across three generations: mother, daughter, be[[love-theme|love]]d. This is unusual for the sonnet tradition, which typically figures [[love-theme|love]] as an affair between two individuals abstracted from family history. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] roots her [[love-theme|love]] in a specific material inheritance.
