## Overview

Sonnet XII of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is a meditation on the origin and ownership of [[love-theme|love]]. The speaker, who in earlier sonnets has struggled with unworthiness, now acknowledges the magnitude of her feeling — but insists that even this [[love-theme|love]] is not her own achievement. It is the be[[love-theme|love]]d's example, mirrored back, that makes her capable of loving at all.

## Full Text

> Indeed this very love which is my boast, [012-001]
> And which, when rising up from breast to brow [012-002]
> Doth crown me with a ruby large enow [012-003]
> To draw men's eyes and prove the inner cost— [012-004]
> This love even, all my worth, to the uttermost, [012-005]
> I should not love withal, unless that thou [012-006]
> Hadst set me an example, shown me how, [012-007]
> When first thine earnest eyes with mine were crossed, [012-008]
> And love called love. And thus, I cannot speak [012-009]
> Of love even, as a good thing of my own. [012-010]
> Thy soul hath snatched up mine all faint and weak, [012-011]
> And placed it by thee on a golden throne— [012-012]
> And that I love, (O soul, we must be meek!) [012-013]
> Is by thee only, whom I love alone. [012-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XII [012-001]–[012-014]

## Analysis

### The Ruby Crown

The octave opens with the speaker contemplating her own [[love-theme|love]] — "this very [[love-theme|love]] which is my boast" [012-001] — and finding it magnificent. The image of a ruby crowning her as it rises "from breast to brow" [012-002]–[012-003] is one of the sequence's most visually striking self-descriptions. The ruby is "large enow / To draw men's eyes" [012-003]–[012-004], suggesting a [[love-theme|love]] so conspicuous and valuable that others cannot fail to notice it. The phrase "prove the inner cost" [012-004] acknowledges that this visible splendor is the external sign of an internal expenditure — [[love-theme|love]] has cost the speaker something, and the ruby is its receipt.

### The Beloved as Origin

Yet the octave's rhetorical movement is toward denial of ownership. The repeated "this [[love-theme|love]]" [012-001], [012-005] is twice qualified: first by the concessive "even" [012-005], and then by the crucial conditional — "I should not [[love-theme|love]] withal, unless that thou / Hadst set me an example, shown me how" [012-006]–[012-007]. The be[[love-theme|love]]d is not merely the object of the speaker's [[love-theme|love]] but its origin and tutor. The moment of first eye contact — "When first thine earnest eyes with mine were crossed" [012-008] — is the generative instant at which [[love-theme|love]] became possible. The phrase "[[love-theme|love]] called [[love-theme|love]]" [012-009] suggests a recognition scene: [[love-theme|love]] recognises itself in the be[[love-theme|love]]d's gaze and is, in that recognition, brought into being.

### The Golden Throne

The sestet shifts from self-description to a dramatic image of displacement. The be[[love-theme|love]]d's [[soul-theme|soul]] "hath snatched up mine all faint and weak" [012-011] and "placed it by thee on a golden throne" [012-012]. The verb "snatched" suggests a sudden, even violent appropriation — the speaker's [[soul-theme|soul]] does not ascend but is seized and repositioned. The throne is golden, implying worth and elevation, but the speaker's [[soul-theme|soul]] is described as "faint and weak" [012-011], a passive object rather than an active agent. The closing exhortation — "O [[soul-theme|soul]], we must be meek!" [012-013] — is a self-address that speaks through the be[[love-theme|love]]d to the speaker's own [[soul-theme|soul]], acknowledging that [[love-theme|love]] is received, not earned. The final line — "Is by thee only, whom I [[love-theme|love]] alone" [012-014] — returns agency to the be[[love-theme|love]]d as the sole source of the speaker's capacity to [[love-theme|love]].

## Literary Context

Sonnet XII participates in a long tradition of [[love-theme|love]] poetry that questions the origin of the [[love-theme|love]]r's feeling. The conceit that [[love-theme|love]] is taught by the be[[love-theme|love]]d — that one learns to [[love-theme|love]] by being [[love-theme|love]]d — has roots in the *dolce stil novo* of Dante and his circle, where the be[[love-theme|love]]d's gaze transmits a transformative power. [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] adapts this tradition to the Victorian sonnet sequence, replacing the medieval lady's remote perfection with the intimate reciprocity of her [[browning-courtship|courtship]] with [[robert-browning|Robert Browning]].

The sonnet's imagery of crowns and thrones also engages with the political language of the sonnet tradition. Shakespeare's [[sonnet-xi|Sonnet 11]]4 asks whether the [[love-theme|love]]r's eye "creates the thing it sees" or merely "receives"; [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]], by contrast, insists that the be[[love-theme|love]]d creates the [[love-theme|love]]r's very capacity to see. The "golden throne" [012-012] echoes the throne imagery of Petrarch's *Canzoniere* 366, where the be[[love-theme|love]]d is enthroned in the speaker's [[heart-imagery|heart]], but [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] inverts the convention: it is the speaker's [[soul-theme|soul]], not the be[[love-theme|love]]d, that is placed on the throne.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XII employs the Petrarchan rhyme scheme (ABBA ABBA CDC DCD) with characteristic [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] flexibility. The octave's enclosed rhymes create a sense of self-containment that mirrors the speaker's meditation on her own [[love-theme|love]], while the sestet's alternating pattern enacts the loosening and displacement described in its content.

Key prosodic moments:
- The initial spondee of "Indeed" [012-001] gives the opening a conversational but emphatic quality
- The enjambment across [012-003]–[012-004] ("a ruby large enow / To draw men's eyes") enacts the overflowing quality of the [[love-theme|love]] it describes
- The medial caesura in "And [[love-theme|love]] called [[love-theme|love]]. And thus, I cannot speak" [012-009] creates a syntactic hinge between the octave's argument and the sestet's surrender
- The parenthetical "(O [[soul-theme|soul]], we must be meek!)" [012-013] intrudes upon the closing couplet with a sudden drop in register — from elevated rhetoric to intimate admonition

## Themes

### Derivation and Originality

The poem's central paradox is that the speaker's greatest achievement — her [[love-theme|love]] — is not hers at all. This paradox is expressed through a rhetoric of negation and displacement: the speaker cannot speak of [[love-theme|love]] "as a good thing of my own" [012-010]. The be[[love-theme|love]]d is presented not as the recipient but as the source, the "example" [012-007] from which the speaker's [[love-theme|love]] is derived.

### Sight and Blindness

The moment of transformative eye contact — "When first thine earnest eyes with mine were crossed" [012-008] — is one of several such moments in the sequence. The crossing of eyes is figured as a generative act, producing [[love-theme|love]] where before there was only the potential for it. Compare the "mystic Shape" of [[sonnet-i|Sonnet I]] [001-010], which the speaker cannot see clearly, and the be[[love-theme|love]]d's face in [[sonnet-vii|Sonnet VII]], which is "too near" to be seen.
