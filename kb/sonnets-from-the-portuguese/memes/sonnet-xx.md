## Overview

Sonnet XX of [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]]'s *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]* (1850) is a sonnet of retrospective wonder. The speaker looks back to a time before [[love-theme|love]] and marvels that the be[[love-theme|love]]d existed in the world without her knowledge — a parallel existence so close and yet undiscovered. The poem's theological conclusion draws an analogy between [[religious-faith|faith]] in [[god|God]] and [[religious-faith|faith]] in [[love-theme|love]].

## Full Text

> Beloved, my Beloved, when I think [020-001]
> That thou wast in the world a year ago, [020-002]
> What time I sate alone here in the snow [020-003]
> And saw no footprint, heard the silence sink [020-004]
> No moment at thy voice, but link by link [020-005]
> Went counting all my chains as if that so [020-006]
> They never could fall off at any blow [020-007]
> Struck by thy possible hand—why, thus I drink [020-008]
> Of life's great cup of wonder. Wonderful, [020-009]
> Never to feel thee thrill the day or night [020-010]
> With personal act or speech—nor ever cull [020-011]
> Some prescience of thee with the blossoms white [020-012]
> Thou sawest growing! Atheists are as dull, [020-013]
> Who cannot guess God's presence out of sight. [020-014]

— [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Elizabeth Barrett Browning]], *[[sonnets-from-the-portuguese|Sonnets from the Portuguese]]*, Sonnet XX [020-001]–[020-014]

## Analysis

### The Snow-Scene of the Past

The octave is dominated by a single, sustained image: the speaker "sate alone here in the snow" [020-003], a figure of absolute isolation in a landscape devoid of human presence. There is "no footprint" [020-004] in this snow — no trace of another's passage — and the [[silence]] is unbroken by the be[[love-theme|love]]d's voice. The image is at once literal (Barrett was largely confined to her room during her years of illness) and metaphoric (the snow of emotional desolation).

The speaker "went counting all my chains" [020-006], a gesture of despairing self-inventory. The chains represent the constraints of illness, solitude, and hopelessness — but the speaker's counting of them is also an act of self-deception: she counts "as if that so / They never could fall off at any blow / Struck by thy possible hand" [020-006]–[020-008]. The word "possible" is crucial. Even before knowing the be[[love-theme|love]]d, the speaker dimly imagined that somewhere in the world existed a hand that could break her chains. She did not believe in it, but she could imagine it.

### The Cup of Wonder

The volta at "why, thus I drink / Of life's great cup of wonder" [020-008]–[020-009] shifts the poem from past to present, from desolation to astonishment. The image of drinking from a cup is sacramental — a secular Eucharist in which the speaker partakes of wonder itself. The repetition of "Wonderful" [020-009] at the line's end, followed by "Never to feel thee" [020-010], creates a hinge: the wonder lies not in what was experienced but in what was not experienced — the be[[love-theme|love]]d's parallel existence, invisible and unfelt.

### The Atheist Couplet

The closing couplet — "Atheists are as dull, / Who cannot guess [[god|God]]'s presence out of sight" [020-013]–[020-014] — is one of the most audacious in the sequence. The speaker compares her own pre-[[love-theme|love]] obliviousness to atheism: both are failures to perceive a presence that, while invisible, should be inferable from its effects. The logic is characteristically [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]: if [[love-theme|love]]'s miraculous arrival proves that it was always already present (the be[[love-theme|love]]d was "in the world a year ago" [020-002]), then [[god|God]]'s invisible presence might similarly be inferred from the miracle of existence itself.

## Literary Context

Sonnet XX belongs to a tradition of retrospective [[love-theme|love]] poetry that includes Shakespeare's [[sonnet-xxx|Sonnet 30]] ("When to the sessions of sweet [[silence|silent]] thought / I summon up remembrance of things past") and Wordsworth's "Surprised by Joy." The shared structure is one of temporal contrast: then was desolation, now is fullness, and the gap between them is the space of wonder.

The theological turn at the sonnet's close is characteristic of Victorian religious poetry, but [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s handling is distinctive. Where Tennyson might linger in doubt, [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]] uses the analogy to argue *for* [[religious-faith|faith]] — the [[love-theme|love]] she has experienced is proof that invisible presences are real.

## Structure and Prosody

Sonnet XX employs ABBA ABBA CDCDCD. The octave is a single, sustained sentence that winds through its eight lines without a full stop, enacting the chain-counting it describes. The volta at "why, thus I drink" [020-008] breaks the syntactic chain with a dash and a new direction.

Key prosodic features:
- "Be[[love-theme|love]]d, my Be[[love-theme|love]]d" [020-001] opens with an incantatory repetition that establishes the poem's tone of wonder
- The alliteration in "sate alone here in the snow / And saw no footprint" [020-003]–[020-004] creates a hush of sibilants
- The enjambment across [020-012]–[020-013] ("with the blossoms white / Thou sawest growing!") carries the syntax across the sestet's boundary
- The final line is end-stopped and declarative, closing the argument with conviction

## Themes

### Presence in Absence

The poem's deepest paradox is that the be[[love-theme|love]]d was present in the world even when the speaker could not feel him. This is a secular version of the theological problem of divine hiddenness: if [[god|God]] exists, why is His presence not felt? [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s answer — through the analogy of retrospective [[love-theme|love]] — is that unfelt presence is still presence, and that the proof lies in eventual recognition.

### Wonder as Response

The speaker's response to [[love-theme|love]]'s arrival is not gratitude or passion but wonder — "life's great cup of wonder" [020-009]. Wonder, in [[elizabeth-barrett-browning|Barrett Browning]]'s lexicon, is the primary religious emotion, the proper response to a world that exceeds one's capacity to comprehend it.
