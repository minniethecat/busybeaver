# Nampont

**Nampont** is the post-house town on the road from [[montreuil|Montreuil]] to [[amiens|Amiens]] where [[yorick|Yorick]] witnesses the celebrated "Dead Ass" episode — the lament of a poor German traveller for his deceased donkey. Occupying chapters 25 and 26 of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), Nampont provides the setting for one of the novel's most anthologised sentimental scenes and a key meditation on the nature of true fellow-feeling.

## 地理与文化背景

Nampont is a small staging-post on the [[calais|Calais]]-to-[[paris|Paris]] road, approximately halfway between [[montreuil|Montreuil]] and [[amiens|Amiens]]. In the 1760s, the post-house system divided the journey into stages of roughly fifteen to twenty miles each, with fresh horses obtained at each stop. As a transit point rather than a destination, Nampont is typical of the anonymous way-stations that punctuate [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey — places where the narrative pauses between one town and the next, and where chance encounters acquire disproportionate significance.

## 在小说中的角色

Nampont is the stage for the novel's most concentrated example of Sterne's sentimental method. Unlike [[calais|Calais]], [[paris|Paris]], or [[versailles|Versailles]], Nampont has no political or cultural weight of its own; its significance derives entirely from what happens there. The episode follows a structural pattern Sterne employs throughout the novel: a physical mishap ([[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s horse bolts) forces the journey to halt, compelling an unplanned stop during which a stranger's story transforms the interruption into a revelation.

The Nampont sequence is also the pivot between two of the novel's early set-pieces: it follows the comic frustrations of [[montreuil|Montreuil]] and the hiring of [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], and it immediately precedes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s arrival at [[amiens|Amiens]] and his emotional reverie about [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]]. The dead ass episode thus functions as an emotional keystone, deepening the novel's register before it returns to the mixture of comedy and pathos that characterises the journey.

## The Dead Ass Scene

The episode unfolds with characteristic Sternean indirection. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s misadventure with a frightened horse forces the party to halt at the Nampont post-house. While waiting for fresh horses, [[yorick|Yorick]] — seated in his post-chaise — observes a small crowd gathered around an elderly man on a stone bench. What follows is an extended set-piece of sentimental narration:

> — And this, said he, putting the remains of a crust into his wallet — and this should have been thy portion, said he, hadst thou been alive to have shared it with me. — I thought, by the accent, it had been an apostrophe to his child; but 'twas to his ass, and to the very ass we had seen dead in the road, which had occasioned La Fleur's misadventure. The man seemed to lament it much; and it instantly brought into my mind Sancho's lamentation for his; but he did it with more true touches of nature.

The mourner recounts how he had set out from his cottage in [[germany|Germany]] with his donkey as his "patient partner." He had made a vow to walk to [[spain|Santiago de Compostela]] if heaven spared his youngest son from smallpox; heaven accepted, the son lived, and the old man undertook the pilgrimage with his donkey bearing his burdens. The donkey died on the road to Nampont.

> Thou hast one comfort, friend, said I, at least, in the loss of thy poor beast; I'm sure thou hast been a merciful master to him. — Alas! said the mourner, I thought so when he was alive; — but now that he is dead, I think otherwise. — I fear the weight of myself and my afflictions together have been too much for him, — they have shortened the poor creature's days, and I fear I have them to answer for. — Shame on the world! said I to myself. — Did we but love each other as this poor soul loved his ass — 'twould be something.

[[la-fleur|La Fleur]] offers the mourner money, but the old man refuses: "it was not the value of the ass — but the loss of him." The mourner relates a story of how he and the donkey were separated for three days crossing the Pyrenees, and how the animal had sought him as diligently as he had sought it.

## The Postilion Episode

Immediately after the dead ass scene, Sterne stages a comic counterpoint that amplifies the sentiment through violent contrast. The postilion, utterly indifferent to the mourner's grief, lashes his horses and sets off at a furious gallop:

> The concern which the poor fellow's story threw me into required some attention; the postilion paid not the least to it, but set off upon the pavé in a full gallop.

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s appeals for a gentler pace only provoke the postilion to gallop harder. The result is a miniature essay on emotional mismanagement: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental mood is shattered by the jolting ride, and he falls into frustration — first with the postilion, then with himself. "He'll go on tearing my nerves to pieces," [[yorick|Yorick]] complains, "till he has worked me into a foolish passion, and then he'll go slow that I may enjoy the sweets of it."

The episode concludes with [[yorick|Yorick]] falling asleep, [[nature|Nature]] having provided "one sweet lenitive at least for evils," and waking to the word "[[amiens|Amiens]]" — the very town where his "poor lady" [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]] is to come.

## 叙事意义

The Nampont episode exemplifies Sterne's distinctive treatment of [[sentiment|sentiment]]: the object of sympathy — a dead donkey — is deliberately humble, yet the emotion it occasions is profound. By having [[yorick|Yorick]] observe the scene from his chaise rather than participate in it directly, Sterne creates a layered perspective in which the reader watches [[yorick|Yorick]] watching the mourner, and is invited to reflect on the very act of watching suffering from a comfortable seat. This self-conscious framing is a hallmark of Sterne's sentimentalism, which never quite loses sight of its own artifice.

The mourner's final words — confessing that he fears he overburdened the donkey "with the weight of myself and my afflictions" — reverse the expected direction of sympathy. Instead of simply pitying the mourner, the reader is led to recognise a deeper truth: that grief, however genuine, can distort its own object. The mourner's self-reproach complicates the sentimental tableau, introducing a note of moral psychology that lifts the scene above mere pathos.
