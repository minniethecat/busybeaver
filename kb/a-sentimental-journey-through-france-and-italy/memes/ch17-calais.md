## CALAIS.

[017-001]I never finished a twelve guinea

[017-002]bargain so expeditiously in my life: my time seemed heavy, upon

[017-003]the loss of the lady, and knowing every moment of it would be as

[017-004]two, till I put myself into motion,—I ordered post horses

[017-005]directly, and walked towards the hotel. 

[017-006]Lord! said I, hearing the town clock strike four, and

[017-007]recollecting that I had been little more than a single hour in

[017-008]Calais,— 

[017-009]—What a large volume of adventures may be grasped within

[017-010]this little span of life by him who interests his heart in every

[017-011]thing, and who, having eyes to see what time and chance are

[017-012]perpetually holding out to him as he journeyeth on his way,

[017-013]misses nothing he can fairly lay his hands on! 

[017-014]—If this won’t turn out something,—another

[017-015]will;—no matter,—’tis an assay upon human

[017-016]nature—I get my labour for my pains,—’tis

[017-017]enough;—the pleasure of the experiment has kept my senses

[017-018]and the best part of my blood awake, and laid the gross to

[017-019]sleep. 

[017-020]I pity the man who can travel from Dan to Beersheba, and cry,

[017-021]’Tis all barren;—and so it is: and so is all the

[017-022]world to him who will not cultivate the fruits it offers. I

[017-023]declare, said I, clapping my hands cheerily together, that were I

[017-024]in a desert, I would find out wherewith in it to call forth my

[017-025]affections:—if I could not do better, I would fasten them

[017-026]upon some sweet myrtle, or seek some melancholy cypress to

[017-027]connect myself to;—I would court their shade, and greet

[017-028]them kindly for their protection.—I would cut my name upon

[017-029]them, and swear they were the loveliest trees throughout the

[017-030]desert: if their leaves wither’d, I would teach myself

[017-031]to mourn; and, when they rejoiced, I would rejoice along with

[017-032]them. 

[017-033]The learned Smelfungus travelled from Boulogne to

[017-034]Paris,—from Paris to Rome,—and so on;—but he

[017-035]set out with the spleen and jaundice, and every object he

[017-036]pass’d by was discoloured or distorted.—He wrote an

[017-037]account of them, but ’twas nothing but the account of his

[017-038]miserable feelings. 

[017-039]I met Smelfungus in the grand portico of the

[017-040]Pantheon:—he was just coming out of it.—’ Tis

[017-041]nothing but a huge cockpit , [580] said he:—I

[017-042]wish you had said nothing worse of the Venus of Medicis, replied

[017-043]I;—for in passing through Florence, I had heard he had

[017-044]fallen foul upon the goddess, and used her worse than a common

[017-045]strumpet, without the least provocation in nature. 

[017-046]I popp’d upon Smelfungus again at Turin, in his return

[017-047]home; and a sad tale of sorrowful adventures had he to tell,

[017-048]“wherein he spoke of moving accidents by flood and field,

[017-049]and of the cannibals that each other eat: the

[017-050]Anthropophagi:”—he had been flayed alive, and

[017-051]bedevil’d, and used worse than St. Bartholomew, at every

[017-052]stage he had come at.— 

[017-053]—I’ll tell it, cried Smelfungus, to the

[017-054]world. You had better tell it, said I, to your

[017-055]physician. 

[017-056]Mundungus, with an immense fortune, made the whole tour; going

[017-057]on from Rome to Naples,—from Naples to Venice,—from

[017-058]Venice to Vienna,—to Dresden, to Berlin, without one

[017-059]generous connection or pleasurable anecdote to tell of; but he

[017-060]had travell’d straight on, looking neither to his right

[017-061]hand nor his left, lest Love or Pity should seduce him out of his

[017-062]road. 

[017-063]Peace be to them! if it is to be found; but heaven itself,

[017-064]were it possible to get there with such tempers, would want

[017-065]objects to give it; every gentle spirit would come flying upon

[017-066]the wings of Love to hail their arrival.—Nothing would the

[017-067]souls of Smelfungus and Mundungus hear of, but fresh anthems of

[017-068]joy, fresh raptures of love, and fresh congratulations of their

[017-069]common felicity.—I heartily pity them; they have brought up

[017-070]no faculties for this work; and, were the happiest mansion in heaven

[017-071]to be allotted to Smelfungus and Mundungus, they would be so far

[017-072]from being happy, that the souls of Smelfungus and Mundungus

[017-073]would do penance there to all eternity!
