## MONTREUIL.

[020-001]As La Fleur went the whole tour of

[020-002]France and Italy with me, and will be often upon the stage, I

[020-003]must interest the reader a little further in his behalf, by

[020-004]saying, that I had never less reason to repent of the impulses

[020-005]which generally do determine me, than in regard to this

[020-006]fellow;—he was a faithful, affectionate, simple soul as

[020-007]ever trudged after the heels of a philosopher; and,

[020-008]notwithstanding his talents of drum beating and

[020-009]spatterdash-making, which, though very good in themselves,

[020-010]happened to be of no great service to me, yet was I hourly

[020-011]recompensed by the festivity of his temper;—it supplied all

[020-012]defects:—I had a constant resource in his looks in all

[020-013]difficulties and distresses of my own—I was going to have

[020-014]added of his too; but La Fleur was out of the reach of every

[020-015]thing; for, whether ’twas hunger or thirst, or cold or

[020-016]nakedness, or watchings, or whatever stripes of ill luck La Fleur

[020-017]met with in our journeyings, there was no index in his

[020-018]physiognomy to point them out by,—he was eternally the

[020-019]same; so that if I am a piece of a philosopher, which Satan now

[020-020]and then puts it into my head I am,—it always mortifies the

[020-021]pride of the conceit, by reflecting how much I owe to the

[020-022]complexional philosophy of this poor fellow, for shaming me into

[020-023]one of a better kind. With all this, La Fleur had a small

[020-024]cast of the coxcomb,—but he seemed at first sight to be

[020-025]more a coxcomb of nature than of art; and, before I had been

[020-026]three days in Paris with him,—he seemed to be no coxcomb at

[020-027]all.
