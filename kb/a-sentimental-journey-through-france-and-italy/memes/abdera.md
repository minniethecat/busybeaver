# Abdera

**Abdera**, an ancient Greek city on the coast of Thrace, appears in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) not as a stop on [[yorick|Yorick]]'s itinerary but as the setting of an interpolated classical allegory titled "A FRAGMENT" — a tale about the transformative power of art upon a debased populace.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

Sterne places "A FRAGMENT" between the [[montreuil|Montreuil]] and [[the-remise-door|The Remise Door]] chapters, immediately after a passage in which [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects on the relationship between passion and generosity (Ch. 18, A Fragment). The fragment tells of Abdera, which "notwithstanding Democritus lived there, trying all the powers of irony and laughter to reclaim it, was the vilest and most profligate town in all Thrace" — a place so consumed by "poisons, conspiracies, and assassinations" that "there was no going there by day — 'twas worse by night."

The city's transformation comes not through philosophy — Democritus, the laughing philosopher, had failed — but through art. When Euripides' *Andromeda* is performed at Abdera, the "tender strokes of nature" in Perseus's speech ("O Cupid! prince of gods and men!") seize the entire population. "The fire caught — and the whole city, like the heart of one man, open'd itself to [[love|Love]]" (Ch. 18, A Fragment). The apothecaries can sell no hellebore; the armourers forge no weapons; "[[friend|Friendship]] and [[virtue|Virtue]] met together, and kiss'd each other in the street; the golden age returned, and hung over the town of Abdera."

The Abdera passage functions as a miniature manifesto for Sterne's sentimental project. Where Democritus's rational mockery could not reform a single Thracian town, a single performance of Euripides transformed it utterly. The fragment thus anticipates the novel's broader argument: that feeling, not reason, is the engine of moral improvement — a claim [[yorick|Yorick]] embodies throughout his journey.
