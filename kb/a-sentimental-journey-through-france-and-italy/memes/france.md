# France

**France** is the principal geographical setting of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), serving as both the physical terrain of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s travels and the cultural counterpoint to his English identity. The novel opens with the iconic declaration — "They order, said I, this matter better in France" (Ch. 1, [[calais|Calais]]) — establishing the kingdom as a land of putative superiority in matters of manners, feeling, and social intercourse.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

France functions in the novel on three interconnected levels:

**A Stage for [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al Encounter**: The French landscape — from the port of [[calais|Calais]] to the streets of [[paris|Paris]] and the road to [[versailles|Versailles]] — provides the backdrop for [[yorick|Yorick]]'s series of spontaneous, emotionally charged meetings with strangers. Each encounter tests his capacity for "sensibility" — the refined feeling that Sterne both celebrates and gently satirizes.

**A Mirror for English Identity**: Throughout his journey, [[yorick|Yorick]] measures French customs against English habits. The French "sublime" that "professes more than it performs" (Ch. 31, [[paris|Paris]]) becomes a recurring theme — a culture of grand gestures, theatrical bows, and elaborate courtesy that alternately charms and perplexes the English traveler.

**A [[travel|Journey]] Without [[italy|Italy]]**: Although the full title promises *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through France and [[italy|Italy]]*, the novel never reaches [[italy|Italy]]. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s travels remain confined to France — a structural irony that places the French experience at the absolute center of the work. The journey through France *is* the journey; [[italy|Italy]] exists only as an unattained destination, a horizon of expectation that recedes as the narrative deepens.

## 文化对比 (Cultural Contrast)

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s France is a kingdom of paradoxes. The French display elaborate courtesy — yet the [[the-monk|monk]] at [[calais|Calais]] is refused alms. The French profess refined sensibility — yet the dead ass at [[nampont|Nampont]] exposes the limits of public compassion. Through these contrasts, Sterne builds a portrait not of France as a fixed national character, but of the traveler's own shifting perceptions, colored by mood, fatigue, and the unpredictable chemistry of human encounter.

After drinking "the King of France's health" and reflecting that "the Bourbon is by no means a cruel race" (Ch. 1, [[calais|Calais]]), [[yorick|Yorick]] rises "an inch taller for the accommodation" — a physical manifestation of the benevolent disposition that France, at its best, inspires in him.
