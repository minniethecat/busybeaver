# The Translation

**The Translation** is an episode in the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), in which [[yorick|Yorick]] attends the opera and encounters a kindly old French officer. The officer's silent courtesy — removing his spectacles upon [[yorick|Yorick]]'s arrival — becomes the occasion for a digression on the art of "translating" gestures, looks, and bodily movements into plain words, a skill [[yorick|Yorick]] claims to have perfected through long habitude.

## 概要

[[yorick|Yorick]] finds himself at the [[paris|Paris]] [[the-opera|Opéra]], seated in a box with an old French officer whom he instantly warms to — not only for the man's personal courtesy but because he reminds [[yorick|Yorick]] of his late friend Captain Tobias Shandy, "the dearest of my flock and friends." When [[yorick|Yorick]] sits down, the officer removes his spectacles and puts them away — a gesture that [[yorick|Yorick]] immediately "translates" as: "Here's a poor stranger come into the box — he seems as if he knew nobody; and is never likely, was he to be seven years in [[paris|Paris]], if every man he comes near keeps his spectacles upon his nose: 'tis shutting the door of conversation absolutely in his face."

This leads [[yorick|Yorick]] into a broader reflection on his gift for reading the silent language of human interaction. Walking the streets of [[london|London]], he "goes translating all the way" (035-036), capable of constructing twenty different dialogues from a circle where not three words have been spoken. He calls this facility a "short hand" for rendering "the several turns of looks and limbs with all their inflections and delineations, into plain words," and declares: "There is not a secret so aiding to the progress of sociality, as to get master of this short hand."

The episode then shifts to a personal anecdote: [[yorick|Yorick]] recounts an encounter at Martini's concert in Milan, where he and the Marquisina di F— repeatedly collided at a doorway, each trying to let the other pass. Their mutual embarrassment and reciprocal apologies culminate in [[yorick|Yorick]] escorting her to her coach — and, after six failed attempts to part, stepping in and being carried home with her. He calls the Marquisina encounter "a vile translation" of his intentions, but concludes that "the connexion which arose out of the translation gave me more pleasure than any one I had the honour to make in [[italy|Italy]]."

## 主题分析

- **Translation as Social Art**: The episode elevates the reading of gestures to a moral and social skill. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s "short hand" for converting looks and limbs into words is presented not as mere cleverness but as the foundation of sociality itself. The French officer might as well have spoken his welcome aloud, but his silent gesture proves more eloquent.

- **[[courtesy|Courtesy]] and Connection**: The old French officer's gesture — removing his spectacles — is a small but powerful act of welcome. It parallels the novel's recurring theme that minor courtesies open the door to human connection, as seen in the [[courtesy|The Pulse]] episode with the [[the-grisset|grisette]] and in the officer's later wisdom during [[the-rose|The Rose]] episode.

- **Intertextuality**: The naming of Captain Tobias Shandy directly connects *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* to Sterne's earlier novel *Tristram Shandy*, reinforcing the shared fictional universe in which [[yorick|Yorick]] exists. The Marquisina digression, set in Milan, extends the novel's geographical reach beyond [[france|France]].
