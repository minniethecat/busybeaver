# The Bastile

**The Bastile** (Bastille) is the [[paris|Paris]] prison-fortress that looms over [[yorick|Yorick]]'s stay in the French capital in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Though [[yorick|Yorick]] never actually enters it, the threat of the Bastile drives the novel's passport plot and provides the occasion for one of its most characteristic passages of sentimental philosophy.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, the Bastille was the most notorious symbol of arbitrary royal power in [[france|France]] — a fortress-prison where the king could confine anyone without trial by means of a *lettre de cachet*. [[stranger|Foreigner]]s without proper papers were particularly vulnerable. For Sterne's English readers, the Bastille represented everything that distinguished French absolutism from English liberty. Its physical bulk — "its towers — fill up the fosse — unbarricade the doors" — made it both a tangible threat and a metaphor for confinement itself.

## 在小说中的角色

The Bastile enters the novel through a series of escalating warnings and philosophical responses, functioning as both a plot mechanism and a thematic device:

**[[the-landlord|The Landlord]]'s Warning (Ch. 39)**. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s [[paris|Paris]] landlord warns him that without a passport, "you'll be sent to the Bastile or the Chatelet au moins" (039-005). When [[yorick|Yorick]] dismisses the threat — "the King of [[france|France]] is a good natur'd soul" — the landlord counters that "nobody could oppose the king of [[france|France]]." [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] whispers the same warning in his ear.

**[[eugenius|Eugenius]]'s Foresight (Ch. 40)**. Before [[yorick|Yorick]] even left [[england|England]], his friend [[eugenius|Eugenius]] had predicted this danger. [[yorick|Yorick]] recalls joking that "before I have been three days in [[paris|Paris]], I shall take care to say or do something or other for which I shall get clapp'd up into the Bastile, and that I shall live there a couple of months entirely at the king of [[france|France]]'s expense" (040-003). [[eugenius|Eugenius]]'s dry reply — "really I had forgot that resource" — underscores the gulf between [[yorick|Yorick]]'s insouciance and real danger.

**The Philosophical Dismantling (Ch. 40)**. Alone in his hotel room, [[yorick|Yorick]] performs the novel's most celebrated act of philosophical reframing. "As for the Bastile; the terror is in the word," he tells himself. "Make the most of it you can — the Bastile is but another word for a tower; and a tower is but another word for a house you can't get out of" (040-005). With "nine livres a day, and pen and ink, and paper, and patience," he reasons, a man might do very well within — and emerge "a better and wiser man than he went in" (040-006). This passage is the quintessence of Sterne's sentimental method: reduce an object of terror to its constituent parts, and the terror dissolves — or at least becomes manageable.

**The Road to [[versailles|Versailles]] (Ch. 43)**. The Bastile's symbolic weight returns on the road to [[versailles|Versailles]]. [[yorick|Yorick]] chastises himself: "Go to the [[duc-de-c|Duc de C—]] with the Bastile in thy looks; my life for it, thou wilt be sent back to [[paris|Paris]] in half an hour with an escort" (043-003). The Bastile here becomes a test of character: approach power with fear, and power will confirm your fear; approach it with gaiety, and it may relent.

**[[the-passport|The Passport]]'s Resolution (Ch. 46–50)**. At [[versailles|Versailles]], the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] assures [[yorick|Yorick]] he has "no apprehensions" regarding the Bastile (046-004). The passport is obtained through court connections, and the threat evaporates — having served its narrative purpose and generated some of the novel's richest philosophical meditation.
