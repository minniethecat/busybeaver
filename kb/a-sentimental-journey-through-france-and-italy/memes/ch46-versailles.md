## VERSAILLES.

[046-001]I found no difficulty in getting

[046-002]admittance to Monsieur le Count de B—. The set of

[046-003]Shakespeares was laid upon the table, and he was tumbling them

[046-004]over. I walk’d up close to the table, and giving

[046-005]first such a look at the books as to make him conceive I knew

[046-006]what they were,—I told him I had come without any one to

[046-007]present me, knowing I should meet with a friend in his apartment,

[046-008]who, I trusted, would do it for me:—it is my countryman,

[046-009]the great Shakespeare, said I, pointing to his works— et

[046-010]ayez la bonté , mon cher ami , apostrophizing his

[046-011]spirit, added I, de me faire cet

[046-012]honneur-là .— 

[046-013]The Count smiled at the singularity of the introduction; and

[046-014]seeing I look’d a little pale and sickly, insisted upon my

[046-015]taking an arm-chair; so I sat down; and to save him conjectures

[046-016]upon a visit so out of all rule, I told him simply of the

[046-017]incident in the bookseller’s shop, and how that had

[046-018]impelled me rather to go to him with the story of a little

[046-019]embarrassment I was under, than to any other man in

[046-020]France.—And what is your embarrassment? let me hear it,

[046-021]said the Count. So I told him the story just as I have told

[046-022]it the reader. 

[046-023]—And the master of my hotel, said I, as I concluded it,

[046-024]will needs have it, Monsieur le Count, that I shall be sent to

[046-025]the Bastile;—but I have no apprehensions, continued

[046-026]I;—for, in falling into the hands of the most

[046-027]polish’d people in the world, and being conscious I was a

[046-028]true man, and not come to spy the nakedness of the land, I scarce

[046-029]thought I lay at their mercy.—It does not suit the

[046-030]gallantry of the French, Monsieur le Count, said I, to show it

[046-031]against invalids. 

[046-032]An animated blush came into the Count de B—’s cheeks as

[046-033]I spoke this.— Ne craignez rien —Don’t

[046-034]fear, said he.—Indeed, I don’t, replied I

[046-035]again.—Besides, continued I, a little sportingly, I have

[046-036]come laughing all the way from London to Paris, and I do not

[046-037]think Monsieur le Duc de Choiseul is such an enemy to mirth as to

[046-038]send me back crying for my pains. 

[046-039]—My application to you, Monsieur le Count de B—

[046-040](making him a low bow), is to desire he will not. 

[046-041]The Count heard me with great good nature, or I had not said

[046-042]half as much,—and once or twice said,— C’est

[046-043]bien dit . So I rested my cause there—and

[046-044]determined to say no more about it. 

[046-045]The Count led the discourse: we talk’d of indifferent

[046-046]things,—of books, and politics, and men;—and then of

[046-047]women.—God bless them all! said I, after much discourse

[046-048]about them—there is not a man upon earth who loves them so

[046-049]much as I do: after all the foibles I have seen, and all the

[046-050]satires I have read against them, still I love them; being firmly

[046-051]persuaded that a man, who has not a sort of affection for the

[046-052]whole sex, is incapable of ever loving a single one as he

[046-053]ought. 

[046-054]Eh bien ! Monsieur l’Anglois , said

[046-055]the Count, gaily;—you are not come to spy the nakedness of

[046-056]the land;—I believe you;— ni encore , I dare

[046-057]say, that of our women!—But permit me to

[046-058]conjecture,—if, par hazard , they fell into your way,

[046-059]that the prospect would not affect you. 

[046-060]I have something within me which cannot bear the shock of the

[046-061]least indecent insinuation: in the sportability of chit-chat I

[046-062]have often endeavoured to conquer it, and with infinite pain have

[046-063]hazarded a thousand things to a dozen of the sex

[046-064]together,—the least of which I could not venture to a

[046-065]single one to gain heaven. 

[046-066]Excuse me, Monsieur le Count, said I;—as for the

[046-067]nakedness of your land, if I saw it, I should cast my eyes over

[046-068]it with tears in them;—and for that of your women (blushing

[046-069]at the idea he had excited in me) I am so evangelical in this,

[046-070]and have such a fellow-feeling for whatever is weak about them,

[046-071]that I would cover it with a garment if I knew how to throw it

[046-072]on:—But I could wish, continued I, to spy the nakedness of

[046-073]their hearts, and through the different disguises of customs,

[046-074]climates, and religion, find out what is good in them to fashion

[046-075]my own by:—and therefore am I come. 

[046-076]It is for this reason, Monsieur le Count, continued I, that I

[046-077]have not seen the Palais Royal,—nor the

[046-078]Luxembourg,—nor the Façade of the Louvre,—nor

[046-079]have attempted to swell the catalogues we have of pictures,

[046-080]statues, and churches.—I conceive every fair being as a

[046-081]temple, and would rather enter in, and see the original drawings

[046-082]and loose sketches hung up in it, than the Transfiguration of

[046-083]Raphael itself. 

[046-084]The thirst of this, continued I, as impatient as that which

[046-085]inflames the breast of the connoisseur, has led me from my own

[046-086]home into France,—and from France will lead me through

[046-087]Italy;—’tis a quiet journey of the heart in pursuit

[046-088]of Nature, and those affections which arise out of her, which

[046-089]make us love each other,—and the world, better than we

[046-090]do. 

[046-091]The Count said a great many civil things to me upon the

[046-092]occasion; and added very politely, how much he stood obliged to

[046-093]Shakespeare for making me known to him.—But à

[046-094]propos , said he;—Shakespeare is full of great

[046-095]things;—he forgot a small punctilio of announcing your

[046-096]name:—it puts you under a necessity of doing it

[046-097]yourself.
