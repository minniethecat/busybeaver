# The Sword

**The Sword** is a solemn ceremonial episode in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occurring in chapter 77 at [[rennes|Rennes]]. The ruined Marquis d'E—, unable to support his family through his sword (military service), surrenders the blade to the court under an ancient Breton law that preserves nobility through commerce, rebuilds his fortune in Martinico, and returns after nineteen years to reclaim it. [[yorick|Yorick]], by "an incident of good fortune which will never happen to any traveller but a Sentimental one," happens to be at Rennes for the reclamation — and witnesses a ceremony of such quiet dignity that it reduces him to envy.

## 概要

The chapter opens with a Sternean meditation on the fall of great houses: "When states and empires have their periods of declension, and feel in their turns what distress and poverty is." The house d'E— in Brittany has fallen into decay through the "indiscretions" of the Marquis's ancestors. The Marquis himself "had fought up against his condition with great firmness; wishing to preserve, and still show to the world, some little fragments of what his ancestors had been."

But he has two boys "who looked up to him for light." His sword — the instrument of military advancement — "could not open the way, — the mounting was too expensive, — and simple economy was not a match for it." Commerce is the only resource.

In any other French province, pursuing trade would destroy the family's nobility. But Brittany preserves an ancient law that allows a nobleman to engage in commerce and later reclaim his rank. The Marquis avails himself of it.

## The Surrender

When the states are assembled at [[rennes|Rennes]], the Marquis enters the court with his two boys. He pleads the right of the ancient Breton law, "which, though seldom claim'd, he said, was no less in force." Then, in the ceremony's central gesture, "he took his sword from his side: — Here, said he, take it; and be trusty guardians of it, till better times put me in condition to reclaim it" (045-022).

The president accepts the sword, sees it deposited in the archives, and departs. The next day, the Marquis and his entire family embark for Martinico.

## The Return

After "about nineteen or twenty years of successful application to business, with some unlook'd for bequests from distant branches of his house," the Marquis returns home "to reclaim his nobility, and to support it."

[[yorick|Yorick]], with characteristic self-deprecation, notes the coincidence of his presence: "It was an incident of good fortune which will never happen to any traveller but a Sentimental one, that I should be at [[rennes|Rennes]] at the very time of this solemn requisition."

## The Ceremony

The scene Sterne paints is a tableau of restrained emotion. The Marquis enters the court with his whole family: "he supported his lady, — his eldest son supported his sister, and his youngest was at the other extreme of the line next his mother; — he put his handkerchief to his face twice."

"There was a dead silence." The Marquis approaches within six paces of the tribunal, gives the Marchioness to his youngest son, and advances three steps before his family. He reclaims his sword.

The moment it is returned to him, "he drew it almost out of the scabbard: — 'twas the shining face of a friend he had once given up." He examines it attentively from hilt to point — "when, observing a little rust which it had contracted near the point, he brought it near his eye, and bending his head down over it, — I think — I saw a tear fall upon the place. I could not be deceived by what followed."

The Marquis speaks his only line: "I shall find some other way to get it off."

He returns the sword to its scabbard, makes a bow to the guardians of it, and walks out with his wife, daughter, and two sons following him. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s response closes the chapter: "O, how I envied him his feelings!" (045-059)

## 主题分析

- **The Sentimental Coincidence**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s presence at Rennes is the novel's most explicit statement of the sentimental traveller's unique fortune. Ordinary travellers see monuments; the sentimental traveller stumbles into human ceremonies. The coincidence is not mere luck but a function of sensibility — the sentimental eye finds what it is attuned to find.

- **The Object as Repository of Feeling**: Like the [[the-snuff-box|snuff box]] that carries the monk's courteous spirit, the sword carries the Marquis's family dignity through nineteen years of exile. When he draws it and sees "the shining face of a friend he had once given up," the sword is not a weapon but a memory. The rust near the point — and the tear that falls upon it — is Sterne's masterful way of registering the passage of time through a material detail.

- **Nobility and Commerce**: The chapter is one of the novel's rare engagements with political economy. The Breton law that permits a nobleman to trade without losing rank is presented without commentary, but the Marquis's story makes an implicit argument: true nobility resides in character, not in the accidents of fortune. The man who reclaims his sword with tears in his eyes is more noble than the man who never had to surrender it.

- **Envy as the Highest Compliment**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s closing exclamation — "O, how I envied him his feelings!" — inverts the usual economy of envy. He envies not the Marquis's wealth or rank but his capacity for feeling. In the novel's sentimental calculus, the man who can weep over a rusted sword is richer than any man who merely possesses one.
