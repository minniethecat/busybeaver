# Heart

The heart is the master symbol of *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*. Sterne uses the word nearly sixty times — more than any other abstract term — and in every register: physiological, moral, social, and metaphysical. "My heart smote me," [[yorick|Yorick]] writes; "the heart is for saving what it can"; "when the heart flies out before the understanding, it saves the judgment a world of pains." The heart is the novel's primary organ of knowledge — it feels what reason cannot deduce.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century's fascination with the heart was both medical and moral. Harvey's discovery of circulation (1628) had made the heart newly visible as a pump; the culture of sensibility made it newly visible as a moral organ. By Sterne's time, to speak of the "heart" was to invoke a whole anthropology: that true knowledge of others comes through feeling, not calculation; that morality is felt before it is reasoned; that the body, far from being an obstacle to virtue, is its primary medium.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sterne tracks the heart through its entire emotional repertoire. It "smites" with guilt, "flies out" with impulse, feels "pain" in sympathy, "rejoices" in another's good fortune, is "wrung with pity and fellow-feeling," and can be "locked up" when passion withdraws. The geography is precise: the heart is where feeling registers as bodily event.

The novel's social philosophy is built on this cardiac model. If the heart is the seat of authentic response, then truth between persons is a matter of hearts connecting — directly, pre-verbally, without the mediation of social form. The many scenes in which [[yorick|Yorick]]'s heart responds before his understanding can formulate a judgment are the novel's argument for the primacy of feeling over reason in human affairs.

## 关键段落

> "My heart smote me the moment he shut the door." (The Monk chapter)

> "When the heart flies out before the understanding, it saves the judgment a world of pains." (The Remise Door chapter)

> "The heart is for saving what it can." (The Snuffbox chapter)

> "I could have taken her in my arms — ridiculous enough to a torpid heart." (The Remise Door chapter)

> "But here my heart is wrung with pity and fellow-feeling." (The Sword chapter)

> "I rejoiced in my heart at the event." (The Translation chapter)
