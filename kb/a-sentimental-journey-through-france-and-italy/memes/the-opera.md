# The Opera

**The Opera** is the [[paris|Paris]] opera house where [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters [[the-old-french-officer|the old French officer]] and witnesses the dwarf episode — two of the most memorable scenes in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). As a setting, the opera functions as a theater within a theater: a space where [[paris|Paris]]ian society performs itself and where [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental method of reading human behavior is displayed at its most acute.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, the [[paris|Paris]] opera — likely the Opéra-Comique or the Académie Royale de Musique — was not merely a venue for musical performance but a central institution of French social life. Its parterre (the standing pit), loges (boxes), and surrounding passages constituted a vertical cross-section of [[paris|Paris]]ian society, from the aristocratic occupants of the upper boxes to the grisettes and abbés who populated the cheaper sections. For an English observer like [[yorick|Yorick]], the opera offered a concentrated display of French manners in all their refinement and "grossièrté."

## 在小说中的角色

The opera appears in three episodes, each illuminating a different facet of Sterne's sentimental vision:

**[[the-old-french-officer|The Old French Officer]] (Ch. 41)**. [[yorick|Yorick]] enters the opera and find himself seated beside an old French officer who is "reading attentively a small pamphlet, it might be the book of the opera, with a large pair of spectacles" (041-003). The moment [[yorick|Yorick]] sits down, the officer removes his spectacles, puts them into a shagreen case, and returns both "the book and them into his pocket together." [[yorick|Yorick]] half-rises and makes him a bow. The officer's gesture — wordlessly making himself available for conversation — is translated by [[yorick|Yorick]] into its sentimental meaning: "Here's a poor stranger come into the box — he seems as if he knew nobody; and is never likely, was he to be seven years in [[paris|Paris]], if every man he comes near keeps his spectacles upon his nose: — 'tis shutting the door of conversation absolutely in his face" (041-004). The episode exemplifies what [[yorick|Yorick]] has elsewhere called his [[london|London]]-honed art of "translating" looks and gestures "into plain words." The old officer — whom [[yorick|Yorick]] later identifies as a figure of "philanthropy" comparable to his beloved Captain Tobias Shandy — becomes one of the novel's quiet heroes of civility.

**[[the-dwarf|The Dwarf]] and the Parterre (Ch. 42)**. In one of the novel's most theatrically vivid passages, the parterre erupts with a cry of "*Haussez les mains, Monsieur l'Abbé!*" (042-002). The old officer explains that "some poor Abbé in one of the upper loges" has been discovered "planted perdu behind a couple of grisettes in order to see the opera," and the parterre is "insisting upon his holding up both his hands during the representation." [[yorick|Yorick]] is astonished: "Good God! said I, turning pale with astonishment — is it possible, that a people so smit with sentiment should at the same time be so unclean, and so unlike themselves, — *Quelle grossièrté!*" (042-003). The old officer offers a philosophical gloss: every nation has its "refinements and grossièrtés, in which they take the lead, and lose it of one another by turns." The dwarf episode thus becomes, in the officer's cosmopolitan framing, a lesson in comparative manners — the parterre's cruelty coexisting with, rather than contradicting, the French genius for sentiment.

**The Dark Passage (Ch. 68)**. Sterne returns to the opera in the novel's penultimate chapter. "There is a long dark passage issuing out from the Opera Comique into a narrow street," he writes; "'tis trod by a few who humbly wait for a *fiacre*, or wish to get off quietly o'foot when the opera is done" (068-005). At the end of the passage, a small candle burns — "more for ornament than use: you see it as a fixed star of the least magnitude; it burns, — but does little good to the world, that we know of" (068-005). It is in this dim passage that [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters two ladies standing with their backs against the wall, waiting for a carriage, and the novel's final sentimental encounter begins. The passage, with its single candle "of the least magnitude," becomes a metaphor for the entire sentimental project: a small light in a dark space, illuminating just enough for a human encounter to occur.
