# Boulevards

The **Boulevards** (*boulevards*) of [[paris|Paris]] appear in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) as the setting for [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s off-stage courtship of the petite demoiselle — a comic subplot that mirrors, in miniature, the social transactions of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own [[paris|Paris]] sojourn.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

The boulevards make two brief but connected appearances, both tied to [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s romantic entanglements. During [[yorick|Yorick]]'s passport negotiations with the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], ever resourceful, "had connected himself with the demoiselle upon the landing of the staircase" and "had contrived to make it do to win the maid to his" (Ch. 44, [[the-passport|The Passport]]). The family being in [[paris|Paris]] that day, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] had made a party with the demoiselle and "two or three more of the Count's household, upon the boulevards."

Sterne immediately follows this detail with an apostrophe to the French: "Happy people! that once a week at least are sure to lay down all your cares together, and dance and sing and sport away the weights of grievance, which bow down the spirit of other nations to the earth" (Ch. 44, [[the-passport|The Passport]]). The boulevards thus function as a synecdoche for French sociability — the tree-lined promenades where class distinctions relax, servants mingle with their betters' households, and the national genius for pleasure finds its weekly outlet.

The boulevards reappear later when [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] confesses he has wrapped the missing sheets of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s fragment around the stalks of a bouquet "which he had presented to the demoiselle upon the boulevards" (Ch. 44, The Fragment, and the Bouquet). The gift, given on the boulevards, becomes the occasion for [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s later discovery of his mistress's infidelity — the demoiselle having passed his gage d'amour to another suitor.

As a [[paris|Paris]]ian location, the boulevards belong to the novel's geography of everyday pleasure, standing in contrast to the grand interiors of [[versailles|Versailles]] and the solemnity of [[the-bastile|the Bastile]]. They represent the public yet intimate spaces where the city's social life unfolds.
