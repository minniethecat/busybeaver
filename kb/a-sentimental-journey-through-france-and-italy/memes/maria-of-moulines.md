# Maria of Moulines

**Maria of Moulines** is a young woman whom [[yorick|Yorick]] seeks out and encounters near [[moulines|Moulines]] in the climactic sentimental episode of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). First introduced in Sterne's earlier novel *Tristram Shandy*, Maria reappears here as a figure of distilled pathos — a "disordered maid" whose losses (her lover, her father, her goat, her senses) make her the novel's supreme object of sentimental compassion.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

Maria is discovered sitting beneath a poplar tree by a brook, dressed in white, her hair loose, a pale green riband across her shoulder with a pipe at its end. She has lost her lover (who proved faithless), her goat (which "had been as faithless as her lover"), and her father (who "had died of anguish, for the loss of Maria's senses"). Her mind unsettled by grief, she wanders the roads of Europe — having "stray'd as far as [[rome|Rome]]" and "travell'd over all Lombardy, without money." (063-041)

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s encounter with Maria is the novel's most concentrated fusion of pity, aesthetic pleasure, and self-conscious sentiment — a scene that both exemplifies and gently ironizes the cult of sensibility.

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Search

[[yorick|Yorick]], passing through the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]] during the vintage harvest, is reminded of "the poor Maria my friend, Mr. Shandy, met with near [[moulines|Moulines]]." Compelled by "an impulse," he goes half a league out of his road to the village where her parents live. Her aged mother tells him that Maria's father has died of anguish and that the girl wanders the roads — a report that makes even [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], "whose heart seem'd only to be tuned to joy," pass the back of his hand twice across his eyes. (Ch. 63, [[moulines|Moulines]])

### Under the Poplar

[[yorick|Yorick]] discovers Maria at "a little opening in the road leading to a thicket":

> She was sitting with her elbow in her lap, and her head leaning on one side within her hand: — a small brook ran at the foot of the tree. (Ch. 63, Moulines)

She holds a little dog named Sylvio on a string — the goat who was her companion in *Tristram Shandy* having proved unfaithful. "Thou shalt not leave me, Sylvio," she says, drawing the dog toward her. [[yorick|Yorick]] sits beside her, wipes her tears, and experiences "such undescribable emotions... as I am sure could not be accounted for from any combinations of matter and motion." (Ch. 63, [[moulines|Moulines]])

### God Tempers the Wind

Maria tells [[yorick|Yorick]] of her wanderings — to [[rome|Rome]], across the Apennines, through Lombardy and [[savoy|Savoy]] — sustained by a providential faith:

> — but God tempers the wind, said Maria, to the shorn lamb. (064-022)

This line, one of Sterne's most quoted, distills Maria's role as an emblem of suffering cushioned by faith. [[yorick|Yorick]] responds with a rhapsodic offer of shelter — "wast thou in my own land, where I have a cottage, I would take thee to it" — and the two weep together.

### The Walk to Moulines

Maria takes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s arm, and together, with her little dog following, "in that order we enter'd [[moulines|Moulines]]." The final image of the chapter lingers on her beauty and brokenness: "affliction had touched her looks with something that was scarce earthly; — still she was feminine." (Ch. 64, [[moulines|Moulines]])

### The Lingering Image

Maria's image haunts the remainder of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey. Even amidst the festivity of the vintage, he sees "Maria in the background of the piece, sitting pensive under her poplar" — a ghostly afterimage that the novel's closing chapters cannot dispel. (Ch. 65, The [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]])

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**The Object of [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: Maria is the novel's purest example of a character whose function is to elicit — and test — the spectator's capacity for feeling. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s response to her is partly genuine compassion, partly aesthetic appreciation of her pictorial qualities, and partly self-congratulation at his own sensitivity. Sterne leaves it to the reader to disentangle these threads.

**Madness and Femininity**: Maria's "disordered" mind, her white dress, her loose hair, and her pipe all belong to a recognizable 18th-century iconography of the madwoman as a figure of pathos and beauty. Her madness is presented as a heightening of femininity rather than its negation — "still she was feminine."

**The Shorn Lamb**: Maria's proverb — "God tempers the wind to the shorn lamb" — became one of the most quoted lines of the novel, encapsulating its theology of sentimental providence: suffering is real, but comfort mysteriously arrives.
