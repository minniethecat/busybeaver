# The Beautiful Grisette

**The Beautiful Grisette** is an unnamed Parisian shop-girl in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), appearing in [[the-gloves|The Gloves]] chapter. A glove seller whose silent exchange of glances with [[yorick|Yorick]] across a narrow counter becomes one of the novel's most celebrated set-pieces of erotic commerce, she embodies Sterne's fascination with the forms of communication that lie beyond language — the "combined looks of simple subtlety" that "all the languages of Babel set loose together, could not express."

## 概要

The encounter begins when [[yorick|Yorick]], having been fitted for gloves at another shop, enters the grisette's establishment to buy an additional pair. The beautiful grisette rises, reaches down a parcel from behind the counter, and unties it. [[yorick|Yorick]] advances to the side opposite her.

The gloves are all too large. "The beautiful grisette measured them one by one across my hand. — It would not alter their dimensions." She begs him to try a single pair "which seemed to be the least." She holds it open; his hand slips in. "It will not do, said I, shaking my head a little. — No, said she, doing the same thing."

The gloves will not do — but the transaction has already moved beyond commerce.

## The Silent Duel

What follows is one of the novel's most virtuosic passages of silent communication. Sterne interrupts the scene to deliver a theory of the look:

"There are certain combined looks of simple subtlety, — where whim, and sense, and seriousness, and nonsense, are so blended, that all the languages of Babel set loose together, could not express them; — they are communicated and caught so instantaneously, that you can scarce say which party is the infector."

The theory delivered, Sterne returns to the scene: the gloves will not do, so [[yorick|Yorick]] and the grisette, "folding our hands within our arms, we both lolled upon the counter — it was narrow, and there was just room for the parcel to lay between us."

The ensuing exchange is a ballet of glances:

"The beautiful grisette looked sometimes at the gloves, then sideways to the window, then at the gloves, — and then at me. I was not disposed to break silence: — I followed her example: so, I looked at the gloves, then to the window, then at the gloves, and then at her, — and so on alternately."

[[yorick|Yorick]] discovers he is outmatched: "I found I lost considerably in every attack: — she had a quick black eye, and shot through two such long and silken eyelashes with such penetration, that she look'd into my very heart and reins. — It may seem strange, but I could actually feel she did" (034-027).

## The Price of Sentiment

Defeated in the duel of glances, [[yorick|Yorick]] capitulates by taking up "a couple of the pairs next me, and putting them into my pocket." The grisette, he notes, "had not asked above a single livre above the price." He wishes she had asked more — and puzzles his brains "how to bring the matter about."

The grisette, mistaking his embarrassment, makes a speech that completes the sentimental circuit: "Do you think, my dear Sir, that I could ask a sous too much of a stranger — and of a stranger whose politeness, more than his want of gloves, has done me the honour to lay himself at my mercy?" (034-039)

[[yorick|Yorick]] counts the money into her hand, makes "a lower bow than one generally makes to a shopkeeper's wife," and departs.

## 主题分析

- **The Look Beyond Language**: The Beautiful Grisette scene is Sterne's most sustained meditation on non-verbal communication. The "combined looks of simple subtlety" that blend "whim, and sense, and seriousness, and nonsense" constitute a language more expressive than any verbal one. The scene dramatizes the novel's broader argument that sentiment operates in the space between words — in glances, gestures, and the charged silence between two bodies leaning on a narrow counter.

- **Erotic Commerce**: The glove shop is a theatre of eroticised transaction. The gloves that will not fit, the measuring of hands, the counter that is "just room for the parcel to lay between us" — every physical detail carries a double charge. The grisette's refusal to overcharge ("I wish'd she had asked a livre more") transforms commerce into something like courtship: both parties give more than is strictly required, and the surplus is the measure of sentiment.

- **The Grisette Type**: The grisette — a young Parisian working woman, typically in the garment or accessory trade — was a recognizable social type in eighteenth-century literature. She occupied the ambiguous space between respectability and availability. Sterne's beautiful grisette is neither seduced nor condemned; she is given a dignity that matches [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own. The scene is a miniature demonstration of the novel's ethics: every person, however humble, is a potential partner in the exchange of sentiment.
