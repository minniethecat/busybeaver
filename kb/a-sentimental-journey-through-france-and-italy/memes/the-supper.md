# The Supper

**The Supper** (chapters 67–68) is one of the most quietly luminous episodes in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Stranded by a broken carriage on the ascent of [[mount-taurira|Mount Taurira]], [[yorick|Yorick]] takes shelter in a peasant cottage and shares a humble meal that becomes, in Sterne's telling, a scene of uncommon grace.

## 叙事背景 (Narrative Context)

The episode begins with mechanical mishap — a horse losing its shoe on a flinty road. Practical necessity, not sentimental pilgrimage, drives [[yorick|Yorick]] to a roadside cottage:

> A shoe coming loose from the fore foot of the thill-horse, at the beginning of the ascent of mount Taurira, the postilion dismounted, twisted the shoe off, and put it in his pocket (067-001).

This interruption of travel by physical contingency is Sterne's characteristic method: the grand journey yields to the small occasion, and the small occasion reveals everything.

## 晚餐 (The Meal)

Inside the cottage, [[yorick|Yorick]] finds a peasant family at their evening meal. The scene is rendered with deliberate simplicity — brown bread, a modest dish, the family gathered around a rough table. Yet Sterne invests this humble domestic tableau with the weight of sacrament. The family's natural grace in sharing what little they have with a stranger transforms the cottage kitchen into something approaching a church.

The chapter's companion piece, "[[the-grace|The Grace]]" (Ch. 68), extends this meditation. The family's unselfconscious prayer before the meal — their "grace" — prompts [[yorick|Yorick]] to reflect on the difference between performed piety and lived virtue. The peasants do not perform their charity; they inhabit it.

## 与书中核心主题的关联 (Thematic Connections)

**[[grace|Grace]] and [[nature|Nature]]**: The supper scene distills Sterne's theology of everyday virtue. [[grace|Grace]] is not confined to church ritual but found in the natural hospitality of simple people. The episode echoes the earlier encounter with the monk while inverting it — here, charity flows not from institution to individual but from the unlettered poor to the cultivated traveler.

**The [[travel|Journey]]'s [[silence|Quiet]] Climax**: Following the emotional intensity of the [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] episode, the supper provides a restorative counterpoint. Where [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]'s grief exceeds any possible comfort, the peasant family's modest meal offers a sustainable model of human goodness — small, daily, undramatic.

**[[character|National Character]], Again**: The French peasant family, like [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] and [[the-barber|the Parisian barber]], exemplifies a natural social grace that Sterne consistently associates with French culture. But here the observation is stripped of satire: the family is neither comic nor quaint, but genuinely admirable.
