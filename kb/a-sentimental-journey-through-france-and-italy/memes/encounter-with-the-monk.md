# The Encounter with the Monk

**The Encounter with the Monk** is a key early episode in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occurring in the chapter "[[the-monk|The Monk]]" (ch02). It is one of the novel's most frequently analyzed scenes, dramatizing the central tension between sentiment and self-interest.

## Summary

Shortly after arriving in [[calais|Calais]], [[yorick|Yorick]] is approached by a Franciscan monk begging for his convent (002-002). Moved by an unexplained impulse of contrary humor, [[yorick|Yorick]] refuses to give him a single *sou* (003-033) — despite recognizing the monk's dignity, age, and grace. After the monk leaves, [[yorick|Yorick]] is struck by remorse and resolves to mend his behavior, eventually exchanging snuff-boxes with the monk in a later encounter.

## Themes

- **[[sentiment|Sentiment]] vs. caprice**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s "predetermination" not to give is an irrational humor — the opposite of true sentiment.
- **The body as moral register**: the Monk's appearance (tonsure, eyes, posture) communicates moral worth without words.
- **Repentance and social repair**: the snuff-box exchange in a later chapter partially restores the broken sympathy.
