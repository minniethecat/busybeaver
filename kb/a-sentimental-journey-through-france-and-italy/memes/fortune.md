# Fortune

Fortune in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the whimsical, unpredictable agency that arranges human encounters. More than mere luck, Sterne's Fortune is a character-like force — capricious, ironic, and ultimately benevolent — that drives the novel's episodic structure and reflects the sentimental conviction that human connection is divinely or naturally orchestrated.

## 定义与历史语境

The concept of Fortune descends from classical and medieval traditions — the Roman goddess Fortuna, the wheel of fortune, the tension between chance and providence. In the eighteenth century, the rise of the novel as a genre brought Fortune into narrative theory: plots driven by coincidence rather than causal necessity increasingly required philosophic justification. Sterne embraces Fortune openly, making chance encounter the very engine of his narrative method.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Fortune is personified from the novel's earliest chapters. [[yorick|Yorick]] marvels at "Fortune's whimsical doings; to take two utter strangers by their hands" — framing his encounter with [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]] as Fortune's design. The novel's entire structure — a series of apparently random encounters in inns, streets, and carriages — is presented as Fortune's arrangement rather than [[yorick|Yorick]]'s planning.

The servant [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] is described as one upon whom "Fortune had not smiled" — a gentle judgment that presents economic circumstance as Fortune's doing, neither merit nor fault. Throughout the novel, Fortune functions as a secular providence, a force that brings people together for sentimental purposes that reason alone could never arrange.

## 关键段落

> "Fortune's whimsical doings; to take two utter strangers by their hands." (The Remise Door chapter)

> "thank Fortune, continued she — you had reason — the heart thanked you." (Calais chapters)

> "Fortune had not smiled upon La Fleur." (Paris chapters)

> "It was an incident of good fortune which will never happen to me again." (Paris chapters)
