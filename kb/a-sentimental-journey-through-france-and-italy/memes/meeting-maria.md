# Meeting Maria

The **Meeting with [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]** at [[moulines|Moulines]] (chapters 63–65) is the most famous and emotionally charged episode in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). It marks the climax of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental journey and represents a remarkable act of intertextual borrowing: [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] first appeared in Sterne's earlier novel *Tristram Shandy* (Vol. IX, Ch. 24), and [[yorick|Yorick]] seeks her out as a conscious pilgrimage to a fictional character.

## 叙事背景 (Narrative Context)

The episode begins as [[yorick|Yorick]] travels through the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]], "the sweetest part of [[calais|France]]," during the vintage season when "[[nature|Nature]] is pouring her abundance into every one's lap." The landscape itself seems to demand emotional response:

> To pass through this with my affections flying out, and kindling at every group before me, — and every one of them was pregnant with adventures. (Ch. 63, Moulines)

Yet [[yorick|Yorick]] deliberately diverts from this abundance of possible encounters. Remembering the story of [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] that his "friend Mr. Shandy" had told — the grief-maddened girl who had lost her lover and wandered the roads with her goat — [[yorick|Yorick]] makes a pilgrimage "half a league out of the road" to find her.

## 相遇 (The Encounter)

[[yorick|Yorick]] finds [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] sitting under a poplar tree by a brook (063-041), her goat beside her. The scene is carefully composed as a tableau of pathos. When [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] comes "a little to herself," she remembers a "pale thin person of a man" who had sat between her and her goat two years before — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own fictional alter ego from *Tristram Shandy*. She produces a handkerchief marked with an "S" that she has kept, folded in vine leaves, ever since:

> She had since that, she told me, stray'd as far as Rome, and walk'd round St. Peter's once, — and return'd back; — that she found her way alone across the Apennines; — had travell'd over all Lombardy, without money, — and through the flinty roads of Savoy without shoes: — how she had borne it, and how she had got supported, she could not tell; — but God tempers the wind, said Maria, to the shorn lamb. (Ch. 64, Maria)

The image of [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] wandering shoeless across Europe — her pilgrimage of suffering surpassing even [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey of sentiment — is one of the most indelible in 18th-century fiction.

## 主题意义 (Thematic Significance)

### Sentiment and Its Limits

The [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] episode tests the boundaries of sentimental response. [[yorick|Yorick]] can pity [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]], sit beside her, and weep with her — but he cannot cure her. The encounter exposes both the power and the impotence of sensibility: feeling is genuine but insufficient.

### Intertextuality

Sterne's self-quotation from *Tristram Shandy* creates a unique fictional space. [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] exists in both novels, remembered across the boundary between texts as she is remembered across time within the story. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s pilgrimage to her is also the reader's — a recognition that fictional characters can haunt us as vividly as real ones.

### The Goat and the Handkerchief

The physical tokens of the scene — [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]'s goat, the handkerchief marked "S," the vine leaves — serve as relics of sentiment. Like the snuffbox and the dead ass earlier in the novel, these objects carry emotional weight beyond their material value, becoming tangible evidence of feeling.
