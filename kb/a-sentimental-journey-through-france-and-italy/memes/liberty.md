# Liberty

Liberty in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* functions on two levels: the grand political ideal — invoked in the famous starling episode — and the intimate personal freedom of the sentimental traveller. Sterne's treatment moves characteristically between satire and sympathy, never letting the abstraction of "Liberty" eclipse the concrete reality of constraint.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century made liberty a watchword of Enlightenment politics — from the American Revolution to the French philosophes. But the sentimental tradition added a psychological dimension: liberty was not merely freedom from political tyranny but freedom of the heart to feel, to sympathize, and to act on benevolent impulse. Sterne, writing in 1768 on the eve of revolutionary upheaval, captures liberty at the pivot point between abstract ideal and felt experience.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's most concentrated treatment of liberty occurs in the starling episode. [[yorick|Yorick]], hearing a caged bird cry "I can't get out," transforms the moment into a meditation on imprisonment and freedom: "Disguise thyself as thou wilt, still, Slavery! still thou art a bitter draught." He addresses Liberty herself as a goddess whom "all in public or in private worship" — yet whose language, like the starling's, remains untranslated in [[paris|Paris]].

Beyond this set-piece, liberty appears throughout as the condition of sentimental travel itself. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s freedom from itinerary, his openness to chance encounter, his refusal to be bound by patriotic prejudice — all express a liberty that is personal rather than political, a liberty of sensibility.

## 关键段落

> "Disguise thyself as thou wilt, still, Slavery! said I — still thou art a bitter draught; and though thousands in all ages have been made to drink of thee, thou art no less bitter on that account." (The Passport chapter)

> "The bird in his cage pursued me into my room; I sat down close to my table, and leaning my head upon my hand, I began to figure to myself the miseries of confinement." (The Passport chapter)

> "I could not sustain the picture of confinement which my fancy had drawn." (The Passport chapter)

> "The — had the credit of being more free than any people in Europe; but in their contracts, they are so many word-bound slaves — liberty, but not with nature, in their contracts." (Paris chapters)
