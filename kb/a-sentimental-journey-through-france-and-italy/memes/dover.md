# Dover

**Dover** is the English Channel port where [[yorick|Yorick]] boards the packet for [[calais|Calais]], launching the journey that gives Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768) its name. Mentioned at the novel's opening and revisited in retrospective passages, Dover is the geographical and symbolic threshold between [[england|England]] and [[france|France]] — the point where sentimental travel literally begins and where the consequences of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s impulsiveness first become visible.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, Dover was the principal embarkation point for English travelers crossing to [[france|France]]. The "one and twenty miles sailing" (001-001) from Dover to [[calais|Calais]] was the shortest Channel crossing, served by packet boats that carried mail, passengers, and the steady traffic of Grand Tour travelers. For 18th-century Englishmen, Dover was a familiar threshold — the last piece of English ground before the cultural disorientation of the Continent began. Sterne himself crossed from Dover to [[calais|Calais]] multiple times, and his rendering of the port carries the authenticity of direct experience.

## 在小说中的角色

Dover appears at three structurally significant moments in the novel, each marking a different register of the journey's meaning:

**The Opening Gambit (Ch. 1)**. The novel's famous opening — "They order, said I, this matter better in [[france|France]]. — You have been in [[france|France]]? said my gentleman" — unfolds in a [[calais|Calais]] post-house, but it looks backward across the Channel to Dover. The entire journey is precipitated by [[yorick|Yorick]]'s impulsive decision, made after debating "with myself, That one and twenty miles sailing, for 'tis absolutely no further from Dover to [[calais|Calais]], should give a man these rights" (001-002). He goes straight to his lodgings, packs "half a dozen shirts and a black pair of silk breeches," takes "a place in the Dover stage," and by three o'clock the next day is eating a fricasseed chicken "so incontestably in [[france|France]]" that, had he died of indigestion, "the whole world could not have suspended the effects of the *droits d'aubaine*" — and all his possessions, "even the little picture which I have so long worn," would have gone to the King of [[france|France]] (001-002). The passage establishes Dover as the point of no return — the place where the decision to travel becomes irrevocable.

**[[the-passport|The Passport]] Realization (Ch. 50)**. In one of the novel's most important retrospective passages, [[yorick|Yorick]] reveals a crucial fact he had previously omitted: "I had left [[london|London]] with so much precipitation, that it never enter'd my mind that we were at war with [[france|France]]; and had reached Dover, and looked through my glass at the hills beyond [[boulogne|Boulogne]], before the idea presented itself; and with this in its train, that there was no getting there without a passport" (050-001). Dover is thus the place where the journey's central bureaucratic crisis first dawns — the spot where [[yorick|Yorick]]'s characteristic impulsiveness collides with the hard facts of international borders. His solution, to beg passage in the Count de —'s suite as far as [[calais|Calais]], sets in motion the entire passport plot that will dominate the [[paris|Paris]] and [[versailles|Versailles]] chapters.

**[[the-starling|The Starling]]'s Capture (Ch. 55)**. The [[the-starling|starling]] that becomes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s personal emblem was caught at Dover. "Whilst the [[honour|Honour]]able Mr. — was waiting for a wind at Dover, it had been caught upon the cliffs, before it could well fly, by an English lad who was his groom" (055-005). The lad carries it "in his breast into the packet" and brings it to [[paris|Paris]], where he teaches it the four words — "I can't get out" — that will resonate through the remainder of the novel. The starling's Channel crossing from Dover to [[paris|Paris]] mirrors [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own, and its caged state in [[france|France]] comments silently on the English traveler's own condition abroad: free in name, but increasingly entangled in the nets of sentiment, obligation, and bureaucratic necessity.

**The Return Gaze (Ch. 17)**. In the [[the-coach-yard|Calais coach-yard]], [[yorick|Yorick]] wishes the Inquisitive [[travel|Travel]]ler and the Simple [[travel|Travel]]ler "a good passage to Dover" (017-005). The phrase, tossed off in passing, reminds the reader that Dover is not merely a point of departure but also the eventual point of return — the English shore to which [[yorick|Yorick]], and every English traveler, must ultimately come back.
