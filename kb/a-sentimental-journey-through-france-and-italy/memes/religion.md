# Religion

Religion in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is a persistent presence — not as doctrine but as a field of human encounter. Sterne, himself an Anglican clergyman, treats religion with characteristic doubleness: institutional religion is gently satirized, while the religion of the heart — expressed in charity, compassion, and spontaneous benevolence — is held up as the truest form of devotion.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century saw religion transformed by the rise of sentimentalism and the cult of sensibility. The "religion of the heart" — associated with Latitudinarian Anglicanism and Continental pietism — emphasized feeling over form, benevolence over dogma. For Sterne's generation, the test of true religion was not doctrinal orthodoxy but charitable action and sympathetic feeling. This shift made religion a natural subject for the sentimental novel.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's first religious figure is [[the-monk|the Franciscan]] monk who appears at [[calais|Calais]] — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s initial refusal to give alms, followed by self-reproach, establishes the pattern of religious feeling correcting religious failure. The monk episode frames charity as the authentic expression of religion, transcending Catholic–Protestant division.

Religion reappears throughout the novel in varied forms: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s midnight invocation of the "great Governor of [[nature|Nature]]"; his reflections on providence and "the trials of my virtue"; his imagined scene of "Religion mixing in the dance" — a vision that Catholic and Protestant alike could embrace. Sterne consistently subordinates doctrinal difference to shared sentiment.

## 关键段落

> "I was not without some thoughts of religion, to help my mind on to something better: in truth, I was not without some thoughts of her." (The Monk chapter)

> "In a word, I thought I beheld Religion mixing in the dance:—but, as I had never seen her so engaged, I kept my eye upon her." (The Grace chapter)

> "The old woman had made a more thorough convert of me to revealed religion, than all the Encyclopædia had said against it." (The Bourbonnois chapters)

> "I believe with as much religion as my heart could hold." (The Bourbonnois chapters)
