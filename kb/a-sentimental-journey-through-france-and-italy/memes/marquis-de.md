# Marquis d'E—

**The Marquis d'E—** is a Breton nobleman whose story forms an embedded narrative within the [[versailles|Versailles]] sequence of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*. Impoverished by the gradual decay of his ancient house, he invokes an old Breton law permitting nobles to engage in commerce without derogation, surrenders his sword at the parliament of [[rennes|Rennes]], emigrates to Martinico for nineteen years, and returns to reclaim his sword in a ceremony so moving that [[yorick|Yorick]] — who happens to be present — sees a tear fall upon the blade. Sterne introduces his story as a companion piece to that of the [[chevalier-de-st-louis|Chevalier de St. Louis]]: "the two stories reflect light upon each other, — and 'tis a pity they should be parted" (045-001).

## Role Overview

The Marquis d'E— never interacts directly with [[yorick|Yorick]]; he exists entirely within a story that Sterne inserts as a digression from the passport narrative. Yet this embedded tale — "THE SWORD. RENNES." — is one of the most emotionally charged passages in the novel, depicting a man who sacrifices his ancestral honour to provide for his children, and who recovers it through patient labour and improbable good fortune.

The Marquis's story serves as a structural counterpoint to the [[chevalier-de-st-louis|Chevalier de St. Louis]] episode. Both men are victims of fortune's reversals; both respond with dignity rather than complaint; both are ultimately restored through a combination of personal virtue and institutional recognition. Where the Chevalier's restoration comes from the king, the Marquis's comes from the ancient laws of [[brittany|Brittany]] — two different forms of benign authority validating individual worth.

## Appearances in the Narrative

### The Surrender of the Sword

Sterne begins with the decline of the house d'E— in [[brittany|Brittany]], a decay brought about by causes he declines to specify: "When states and empires have their periods of declension, and feel in their turns what distress and poverty is, — I stop not to tell the causes which gradually brought the house d'E—, in [[brittany|Brittany]], into decay" (045-001).

The Marquis "had fought up against his condition with great firmness; wishing to preserve, and still show to the world, some little fragments of what his ancestors had been." But the indiscretions of his forebears had put restoration beyond his power. He had two boys "who looked up to him for light; — he thought they deserved it." He had tried his sword — "it could not open the way, — the mounting was too expensive, — and simple economy was not a match for it: — there was no resource but commerce" (045-001).

In any other province of [[calais|France]], engaging in trade would have meant the permanent loss of noble status — "smiting the root for ever of the little tree his pride and affection wish'd to see re-blossom." But [[brittany|Brittany]] possessed an ancient provision that permitted this:

> "In Brittany, there being a provision for this, he avail'd himself of it; and, taking an occasion when the states were assembled at Rennes, the Marquis, attended with his two boys, entered the court; and having pleaded the right of an ancient law of the duchy, which, though seldom claim'd, he said, was no less in force, he took his sword from his side: — Here, said he, take it; and be trusty guardians of it, till better times put me in condition to reclaim it." (045-002)

The president of the parliament accepted the sword, staid a few minutes to see it deposited in the archives, and the Marquis departed.

### Exile and Return

The Marquis and his whole family embarked the next day for Martinico, where, after "about nineteen or twenty years of successful application to business, with some unlook'd for bequests from distant branches of his house," he returned home "to reclaim his nobility, and to support it" (045-003).

Sterne notes the extraordinary coincidence that brought him to [[rennes|Rennes]] at precisely the right moment: "It was an incident of good fortune which will never happen to any traveller but a [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al one, that I should be at [[rennes|Rennes]] at the very time of this solemn requisition: I call it solemn; — it was so to me" (045-004).

### The Reclamation Ceremony

The ceremony itself is described with the visual precision of a painting:

> "The Marquis entered the court with his whole family: he supported his lady, — his eldest son supported his sister, and his youngest was at the other extreme of the line next his mother; — he put his handkerchief to his face twice." (045-005)

A dead silence falls. The Marquis approaches within six paces of the tribunal, gives his wife to his youngest son, advances three steps before his family, and reclaims his sword. What follows is the story's emotional climax:

> "His sword was given him, and the moment he got it into his hand he drew it almost out of the scabbard: — 'twas the shining face of a friend he had once given up — he look'd attentively along it, beginning at the hilt, as if to see whether it was the same, — when, observing a little rust which it had contracted near the point, he brought it near his eye, and bending his head down over it, — I think — I saw a tear fall upon the place. I could not be deceived by what followed." (045-006)

The Marquis's words — "'I shall find,' said he, 'some other way to get it off'" (045-007) — transform the rust spot into a metaphor for the stain of time and circumstance, which can be polished away now that honour has been restored.

He returns the sword to its scabbard, bows to the guardians of it, and walks out with his wife, daughter, and two sons following. Sterne's final line — "O, how I envied him his feelings!" (045-009) — is one of the novel's most revealing moments of sentimental identification, the narrator envying not the Marquis's recovered nobility but the emotional experience of recovering it.

## Character and Themes

**The Commercial Nobleman**: The Marquis's story dramatizes a tension at the heart of eighteenth-century European society — the incompatibility of noble status with commercial activity. [[brittany|Brittany]]'s ancient law, allowing nobles to trade without derogation, is presented as a rare instance of legal wisdom that permits honour and commerce to coexist. The Marquis's willingness to trade his sword for his children's future makes him a hero of practical sentiment, valuing substance over symbol while trusting that the symbol can one day be recovered.

**Swords and [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: The sword functions throughout the story as a double symbol — of military honour (the ancestral weapon) and of personal identity (the "shining face of a friend"). When the Marquis examines the blade for rust, he is checking whether time has corroded not just the metal but the self that surrendered it. The tear that falls upon the rust spot is the story's supreme sentimental moment — not grief but recognition, the physical acknowledgment that the years of exile have left their mark but have not destroyed what was preserved.

**[[fortune|Fortune]] and [[fortune|Providence]]**: The Marquis's story, like the Chevalier's, is a narrative of restoration. But where the Chevalier is saved by the king's intervention, the Marquis is saved by his own labour ("successful application to business") supplemented by providential windfalls ("unlook'd for bequests from distant branches"). The combination of personal effort and external fortune creates a balanced moral economy: virtue is necessary but not sufficient; luck completes what effort begins.

**The [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Travel]]ler as Witness**: Sterne's presence at the ceremony — "an incident of good fortune which will never happen to any traveller but a [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al one" — elevates the Marquis's story from mere anecdote to shared emotional experience. [[yorick|Yorick]] is not merely a reporter of the Marquis's feelings; he envies them, desiring to participate in the emotional fullness of the moment. This vicarious participation — feeling through another's feeling — is the essence of sentimentalism.
