# The Husband

**The Husband** is a brief but memorable character in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*, who appears in the [[paris|Paris]] glove-shop episode. He emerges unexpectedly from a back parlour just as [[yorick|Yorick]] is counting the pulsations of his wife — the pretty *grisette* who serves behind the counter — and his entrance precipitates one of the novel's most acid social observations about the difference between [[london|London]] and [[paris|Paris]]ian marriages (033-001).

## Role Overview

The Husband exists in the narrative for scarcely a paragraph of action, yet he anchors one of Sterne's sharpest comparative essays on national character. He enters the shop while [[yorick|Yorick]], seated in his black coat, is feeling the *grisette*'s pulse with theatrical devotion. When his wife identifies him — "'Twas nobody but her husband" — he takes off his hat, bows to [[yorick|Yorick]], murmurs that [[yorick|Yorick]] does him too much honour, puts his hat back on, and walks out without the faintest trace of jealousy or interest (033-002).

This three-sentence performance is all the Husband does in the novel. But Sterne seizes upon it to launch a disquisition on the difference between English and French shopkeeping marriages, using the Husband as a specimen of a broader social phenomenon.

## Appearance

The Husband's sole appearance occurs in the [[paris|Paris]]ian glove-shop where [[yorick|Yorick]] has been purchasing gloves from — and feeling the pulse of — the beautiful *grisette* who serves him:

> "'Twas nobody but her husband, she said; — so I began a fresh score. — Monsieur is so good, quoth she, as he pass'd by us, as to give himself the trouble of feeling my pulse. — The husband took off his hat, and making me a bow, said, I did him too much honour — and having said that, he put on his hat and walk'd out." (033-002)

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s astonished reaction — "Good God! said I to myself, as he went out, — and can this man be the husband of this woman!" (033-003) — is the emotional pivot of the scene, transforming a minor domestic encounter into a meditation on the unnatural inversion of marital roles in [[paris|Paris]].

## Character and Themes

**The Emasculated Husband**: Sterne uses the Husband to illustrate his thesis that [[paris|Paris]]ian commerce has inverted the natural order of marriage. In [[london|London]], he argues, "a shopkeeper and a shopkeeper's wife seem to be one bone and one flesh," roughly equal in their endowments. But in [[paris|Paris]], "there are scarce two orders of beings more different," for the legislative and executive powers of the shop rest entirely with the women (033-004).

The Husband, by contrast, "seldom comes" to the shop at all: "in some dark and dismal room behind, he sits commerce-less, in his thrum nightcap, the same rough son of [[nature|Nature]] that [[nature|Nature]] left him" (033-004). The "thrum nightcap" — a cap made of coarse waste threads — is a devastating detail, suggesting a man reduced to the coarsest domestic existence while his wife, through "continual higgling with customers of all ranks and sizes," has been polished "like a brilliant" (033-005).

**The Stone Underfoot**: Sterne concludes the portrait with an uncharacteristically harsh judgment: "*Monsieur le Mari* is little better than the stone under your foot" (033-005). The Husband becomes less a character than a moral emblem — a warning about what happens when the social arts are ceded entirely to women. Yet the judgment is tempered by the narrator's immediate appeal: "Surely, — surely, man! it is not good for thee to sit alone: — thou wast made for social intercourse and gentle greetings" (033-006).

**[[character|National Character]] and Satire**: The Husband episode belongs to Sterne's running comparison between English and French manners. Where the English husband is his wife's equal partner, the French husband is a virtual cuckold by commercial arrangement — not through infidelity but through irrelevance. The satire cuts both ways: if the French system produces polished women and brutish men, the English system — Sterne implies — produces a duller but more honest domestic parity.
