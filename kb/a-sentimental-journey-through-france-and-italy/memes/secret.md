# Secret

The secret in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is not merely information withheld but the hidden structure of social life itself. Every character carries a secret; every encounter turns on what is not said. Sterne treats the secret as a principle of narrative energy — the unspoken draws the reader forward as surely as it draws [[yorick|Yorick]] through [[calais|France]]. The secret is at once a social tool, a psychological depth, and a comic device.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century inherited from the Renaissance a fascination with secrecy as the hidden knowledge of power — the *arcana imperii*, the secrets of state. But the century also democratized the secret: the rise of the novel made private life legible, and the secret life of ordinary people became a subject of intense interest. Sterne, a clergyman who understood the confessional, works the secret in both its grand and everyday registers — from the secrets of the French court to the secret of a beggar's success.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sterne's most explicit statement on the secret as social tool comes in [[paris|Paris]]: "There is not a secret so aiding to the progress of sociality, as to get master of this short hand, and to be quick in rendering the several turns of looks and limbs with all their inflections and delineations, into plain words" ([[paris|Paris]] chapters). The "short hand" is the art of reading the body — the secret language of gesture and glance that conveys more than speech. To master this secret is to master sociality itself.

The secret as hidden power appears in the story of the [[paris|Paris]] beggar, who "told every woman what he had to say in her ear, and in a way which had much more the air of a secret than a petition" ([[paris|Paris]] chapters). The beggar's success depends not on what he says but on how he says it — the form of the secret, the intimacy of the whisper, commands the charity that open petition would not. [[yorick|Yorick]] is obsessed with learning this secret: "a secret, I thought, which so soon and so certainly soften'd the heart of every woman you came near, was a secret at least equal to the philosopher's stone" ([[paris|Paris]] chapters).

The novel's final secret is the one [[yorick|Yorick]] masters "just in time to turn" an awkward situation — the comic secret that saves face and preserves delicacy. Throughout, Sterne suggests that the true secret is not a piece of information but a mode of relation: the intimacy created by the act of withholding and revealing.
