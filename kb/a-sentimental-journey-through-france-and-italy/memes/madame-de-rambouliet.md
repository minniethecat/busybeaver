# Madame de Rambouliet

**Madame de Rambouliet** is a [[paris|Paris]]ian woman who appears briefly but memorably in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). She is introduced as a model of correctness and purity — and then immediately reveals a frankness about bodily functions that both tests and confirms [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental philosophy. The entire episode occupies scarcely twenty lines, yet it has become one of the novel's most discussed passages. (037-001)

## Role Overview

Madame de Rambouliet enters the narrative after [[yorick|Yorick]] has been in [[paris|Paris]] about six weeks. She has done him "the honour to take me in her coach about two leagues out of town." On the return journey, she makes a request that the novel treats with elaborate delicacy — and Sterne, in an apostrophe to the reader, transforms what might have been a coarse moment into an emblem of natural innocence. (037-002)

She is the most concise demonstration of Sterne's method: take a trivial incident, surround it with the language of gallantry and classical allusion, and extract from it a sentimental truth about human nature.

## Appearances

### The Coach Ride

The entire encounter takes place within a single paragraph. Sterne first establishes Madame de Rambouliet's character with emphatic praise:

> "Of all women, Madame de Rambouliet is the most correct; and I never wish to see one of more virtues and purity of heart." (037-001)

He then records the incident without the slightest hint of embarrassment. On the return journey, "Madame de Rambouliet desired me to pull the cord. — I asked her if she wanted anything — *Rien que pour pisser*, said Madame de Rambouliet." (037-002)

The French phrase — "Nothing, only to piss" — is printed without translation, as if the foreign language provides a veil of decorum that English would strip away. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s response is immediate and characteristic: not a blush of embarrassment, but a theatrical elevation of the moment.

### The Apostrophe to the Reader

Sterne immediately turns from the narrative to address his audience directly:

> "Grieve not, gentle traveller, to let Madame de Rambouliet p—ss on. — And, ye fair mystic nymphs! go each one pluck your rose, and scatter them in your path, — for Madame de Rambouliet did no more." (037-003)

The dash after the first letter of "p—ss" enacts the very delicacy it pretends to mock — the word is both printed and suppressed. The image of the "fair mystic nymphs" scattering roses transforms the bodily function into a pastoral rite.

### The Dismount

[[yorick|Yorick]] hands Madame de Rambouliet out of the coach, and the moment becomes explicitly ceremonial:

> "I handed Madame de Rambouliet out of the coach; and had I been the priest of the chaste Castalia, I could not have served at her fountain with a more respectful decorum." (037-004)

The allusion to Castalia — the sacred spring at Delphi, associated with poetic inspiration and ritual purity — completes the elevation. A woman stepping out of a coach to relieve herself has become a priestess at a fountain, and [[yorick|Yorick]] her officiating clergyman.

## Character and Themes

**Natural Frankness and Polite Society**: Madame de Rambouliet embodies Sterne's argument that true politeness is not prudery. Her correctness and virtue are established first; her frank bodily request follows as proof that the most "correct" woman need not pretend the body does not exist. This is sentimentalism's answer to both French libertinism and English squeamishness.

**The Comic Sublime**: The passage is a masterclass in Sterne's characteristic mode — taking a low subject and treating it with high rhetoric until the gap between them becomes the source of both humour and philosophical insight. The Castalia allusion is simultaneously sincere and ridiculous, and Sterne means it to be both.

**Gender and the Gaze**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s handling of the moment — his respectful decorum, his refusal to let the episode become prurient — models the sentimental gentleman's ideal relation to female bodily existence. He sees without leering, assists without presuming.
