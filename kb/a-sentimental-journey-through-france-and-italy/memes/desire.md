# Desire

Desire in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the force that sets the traveler in motion and keeps him there. It operates on every scale — from the "strong desire" to visit a grave to the currents of erotic and social longing that run beneath the novel's most delicate encounters. Sterne treats desire not as a simple appetite to be satisfied but as a complex energy that, properly directed, fuels the entire sentimental enterprise.

## 定义与历史语境

Eighteenth-century thought was ambivalent about desire. Hobbes had made desire the motor of human action, and Mandeville had scandalized readers by tracing virtue itself to the desire for esteem. [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al philosophy sought to redeem desire by distinguishing between selfish appetite and benevolent longing — between the desire that consumes its object and the desire that reaches toward another person in an attitude of tender regard. Sterne's novel enacts this distinction through the figure of [[yorick|Yorick]], whose desires are constantly being refined from the physical to the sentimental.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The word "desire" appears throughout the novel in strikingly varied contexts. The most powerful cluster occurs in the meditation on the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] episode: "If [[nature|Nature]] has so wove her web of kindness, that some threads of love and desire are entangled with the piece, — must the whole web be rent in drawing them out?" This passage, one of the novel's most philosophically sophisticated moments, argues that desire cannot be extricated from love — that the sentimental and the physical are woven together in human nature.

The novel also treats desire as a narrative force. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s "strong desire to see where they had laid him" — the wish to visit Father Lorenzo's grave — is desire as pilgrimage, the longing to honor the dead and complete an unfinished sentimental transaction. The notary episode presents desire in comic register: "the notary was inflamed with a desire to begin," a bureaucratic desire that mirrors and parodies the novel's more tender longings.
