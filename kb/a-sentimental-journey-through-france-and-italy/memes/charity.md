# Charity

**Charity** is the central moral virtue examined in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). From the opening encounter with [[the-monk|the Franciscan]] monk at [[calais|Calais]] to the final scenes with [[meeting-maria|Maria at Moulines]], [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey is structured as a series of tests of charitable impulse — each encounter asking whether, how, and why we give to those who ask.

## 作为核心美德的慈善 (Charity as Central Virtue)

The novel opens with perhaps its most explicit moral examination. When a poor monk of the order of St. Francis enters [[yorick|Yorick]]'s room at [[calais|Calais]] to beg for his convent, [[yorick|Yorick]] initially refuses him — not from lack of generosity but from irritation at the monk's manner. The refusal haunts him:

> I had scarce uttered the words, when a poor monk of the order of St. Francis came into the room to beg something for his convent. No man cares to have his virtues the sport of contingencies. (002-004)

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s subsequent exchange of snuffboxes with the monk — an elaborate ritual of mutual gift-giving — becomes the novel's first demonstration that charity is not simply about the transfer of money but about the recognition of shared humanity. The monk's gift of his horn snuffbox, exchanged for [[yorick|Yorick]]'s tortoise-shell one, materializes the sentimental principle that true charity involves reciprocal feeling, not one-way condescension.

## 神学与社会批判 (Theology and Social Critique)

Sterne, himself a clergyman, embeds the question of charity within 18th-century debates about religious institutions. The begging monk represents institutionalized charity — the Church as intermediary between giver and receiver. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s initial resistance and eventual embrace of the monk dramatizes the tension between spontaneous benevolence (the sentimental ideal) and organized almsgiving (the Catholic reality).

The French landlord's assumption that Englishmen are "so generous" adds a satirical layer: charity becomes entangled with national stereotype, economic expectation, and the traveler's performance of virtue. [[yorick|Yorick]] constantly calculates the cost of his sentiments in *livres* — a running joke that undercuts any simple celebration of charitable feeling.
