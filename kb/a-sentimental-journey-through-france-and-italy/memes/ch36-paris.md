## PARIS.

[036-001]I had never heard the remark made

[036-002]by any one in my life, except by one; and who that was will

[036-003]probably come out in this chapter; so that being pretty much

[036-004]unprepossessed, there must have been grounds for what struck me

[036-005]the moment I cast my eyes over the parterre,—and that was,

[036-006]the unaccountable sport of Nature in forming such numbers of

[036-007]dwarfs.—No doubt she sports at certain times in almost

[036-008]every corner of the world; but in Paris there is no end to her

[036-009]amusements.—The goddess seems almost as merry as she is

[036-010]wise. 

[036-011]As I carried my idea out of the Opéra Comique with me,

[036-012]I measured every body I saw walking in the streets by

[036-013]it.—Melancholy application! especially where the size was

[036-014]extremely little,—the face extremely dark,—the eyes

[036-015]quick,—the nose long,—the teeth white,—the jaw

[036-016]prominent,—to see so many miserables, by force of accidents

[036-017]driven out of their own proper class into the very verge of

[036-018]another, which it gives me pain to write down:—every third

[036-019]man a pigmy!—some by rickety heads and hump

[036-020]backs;—others by bandy legs;—a third set arrested by

[036-021]the hand of Nature in the sixth and seventh years of their

[036-022]growth;—a fourth, in their perfect and natural state like

[036-023]dwarf apple trees; from the first rudiments and stamina of their

[036-024]existence, never meant to grow higher. 

[036-025]A Medical Traveller might say, ’tis owing to undue

[036-026]bandages;—a Splenetic one, to want of air;—and an

[036-027]Inquisitive Traveller, to fortify the system, may measure the

[036-028]height of their houses,—the narrowness of their streets,

[036-029]and in how few feet square in the sixth and seventh stories such

[036-030]numbers of the bourgeoisie eat and sleep together; but I remember

[036-031]Mr. Shandy the elder, who accounted for nothing like any body

[036-032]else, in speaking one evening of these matters, averred that

[036-033]children, like other animals, might be increased almost to any

[036-034]size, provided they came right into the world; but the misery

[036-035]was, the citizens of were Paris so coop’d up, that they had

[036-036]not actually room enough to get them.—I do not call it

[036-037]getting anything, said he;—’tis getting

[036-038]nothing.—Nay, continued he, rising in his argument,

[036-039]’tis getting worse than nothing, when all you have got

[036-040]after twenty or five and twenty years of the tenderest care and

[036-041]most nutritious aliment bestowed upon it, shall not at last be as

[036-042]high as my leg. Now, Mr. Shandy being very short, there

[036-043]could be nothing more said of it. 

[036-044]As this is not a work of reasoning, I leave the solution as I

[036-045]found it, and content myself with the truth only of the remark,

[036-046]which is verified in every lane and by-lane of Paris. I was

[036-047]walking down that which leads from the Carousal to the Palais

[036-048]Royal, and observing a little boy in some distress at the side of

[036-049]the gutter which ran down the middle of it, I took hold of his

[036-050]hand and help’d him over. Upon turning up his face to

[036-051]look at him after, I perceived he was about forty.—Never

[036-052]mind, said I, some good body will do as much for me when I am

[036-053]ninety. 

[036-054]I feel some little principles within me which incline me to be

[036-055]merciful towards this poor blighted part of my species, who have

[036-056]neither size nor strength to get on in the world.—I cannot

[036-057]bear to see one of them trod upon; and had scarce got seated

[036-058]beside my old French officer, ere the disgust was exercised, by

[036-059]seeing the very thing happen under the box we sat in. 

[036-060]At the end of the orchestra, and betwixt that and the first

[036-061]side box, there is a small esplanade left, where, when the house

[036-062]is full, numbers of all ranks take sanctuary. Though you

[036-063]stand, as in the parterre, you pay the same price as in the

[036-064]orchestra. A poor defenceless being of this order had got

[036-065]thrust somehow or other into this luckless place;—the night

[036-066]was hot, and he was surrounded by beings two feet and a half

[036-067]higher than himself. The dwarf suffered inexpressibly on

[036-068]all sides; but the thing which incommoded him most, was a tall

[036-069]corpulent German, near seven feet high, who stood directly

[036-070]betwixt him and all possibility of his seeing either the stage or

[036-071]the actors. The poor dwarf did all he could to get a peep

[036-072]at what was going forwards, by seeking for some little opening

[036-073]betwixt the German’s arm and his body, trying first on one

[036-074]side, then the other; but the German stood square in the most

[036-075]unaccommodating posture that can be imagined:—the dwarf

[036-076]might as well have been placed at the bottom of the deepest

[036-077]draw-well in Paris; so he civilly reached up his hand to the

[036-078]German’s sleeve, and told him his distress.—The

[036-079]German turn’d his head back, looked down upon him as Goliah

[036-080]did upon David,—and unfeelingly resumed his posture. 

[036-081]I was just then taking a pinch of snuff out of my monk’s

[036-082]little horn box.—And how would thy meek and courteous

[036-083]spirit, my dear monk! so temper’d to bear and

[036-084]forbear !—how sweetly would it have lent an ear to this

[036-085]poor soul’s complaint! 

[036-086]The old French officer, seeing me lift up my eyes with an

[036-087]emotion, as I made the apostrophe, took the liberty to ask me

[036-088]what was the matter?—I told him the story in three words;

[036-089]and added, how inhuman it was. 

[036-090]By this time the dwarf was driven to extremes, and in his

[036-091]first transports, which are generally unreasonable, had told the

[036-092]German he would cut off his long queue with his knife.—The

[036-093]German look’d back coolly, and told him he was welcome, if

[036-094]he could reach it. 

[036-095]An injury sharpen’d by an insult, be it to whom it will,

[036-096]makes every man of sentiment a party: I could have leap’d

[036-097]out of the box to have redressed it.—The old French officer

[036-098]did it with much less confusion; for leaning a little over, and

[036-099]nodding to a sentinel, and pointing at the same time with his

[036-100]finger at the distress,—the sentinel made his

[036-101]way to it.—There was no occasion to tell the

[036-102]grievance,—the thing told himself; so thrusting back the

[036-103]German instantly with his musket,—he took the poor dwarf by

[036-104]the hand, and placed him before him.—This is noble! said I,

[036-105]clapping my hands together.—And yet you would not permit

[036-106]this, said the old officer, in England. 

[036-107]—In England, dear Sir, said I, we sit all at our

[036-108]ease . 

[036-109]The old French officer would have set me at unity with myself,

[036-110]in case I had been at variance,—by saying it was a bon

[036-111]mot ;—and, as a bon mot is always worth something

[036-112]at Paris, he offered me a pinch of snuff.
