## THE CASE OF DELICACY.

[069-001]When you have gained the top of

[069-002]Mount Taurira, you run presently down to Lyons:—adieu,

[069-003]then, to all rapid movements! ’Tis a journey of

[069-004]caution; and it fares better with sentiments, not to be in a

[069-005]hurry with them; so I contracted with a voiturin to take his time

[069-006]with a couple of mules, and convoy me in my own chaise safe to

[069-007]Turin, through Savoy. 

[069-008]Poor, patient, quiet, honest people! fear not: your poverty,

[069-009]the treasury of your simple virtues, will not be envied you by

[069-010]the world, nor will your valleys be invaded by it.—Nature!

[069-011]in the midst of thy disorders, thou art still friendly to the

[069-012]scantiness thou hast created: with all thy great works about

[069-013]thee, little hast thou left to give, either to the scythe or to

[069-014]the sickle;—but to that little thou grantest safety and

[069-015]protection; and sweet are the dwellings which stand so

[069-016]shelter’d. 

[069-017]Let the way-worn traveller vent his complaints upon the sudden

[069-018]turns and dangers of your roads,—your rocks,—your

[069-019]precipices;—the difficulties of getting up,—the

[069-020]horrors of getting down,—mountains impracticable,—and

[069-021]cataracts, which roll down great stones from their summits, and

[069-022]block his road up.—The peasants had been all day at work in

[069-023]removing a fragment of this kind between St. Michael and Madane;

[069-024]and, by the time my voiturin got to the place, it wanted full two

[069-025]hours of completing before a passage could any how be

[069-026]gain’d: there was nothing but to wait with

[069-027]patience;—’twas a wet and tempestuous night; so that

[069-028]by the delay, and that together, the voiturin found himself

[069-029]obliged to put up five miles short of his stage at a little

[069-030]decent kind of an inn by the roadside. 

[069-031]I forthwith took possession of my bedchamber—got a good

[069-032]fire—order’d supper; and was thanking heaven it was

[069-033]no worse, when a voiturin arrived with a lady in it and her

[069-034]servant maid. 

[069-035]As there was no other bed-chamber in the house, the

[069-036]hostess,—without much nicety, led them into mine, telling

[069-037]them, as she usher’d them in, that there was nobody in it

[069-038]but an English gentleman;—that there were two good beds in it, and

[069-039]a closet within the room which held another. The accent in

[069-040]which she spoke of this third bed, did not say much for

[069-041]it;—however, she said there were three beds and but three

[069-042]people, and she durst say, the gentleman would do anything to

[069-043]accommodate matters.—I left not the lady a moment to make a

[069-044]conjecture about it—so instantly made a declaration that I

[069-045]would do anything in my power. 

[069-046]As this did not amount to an absolute surrender of my

[069-047]bed-chamber, I still felt myself so much the proprietor, as to

[069-048]have a right to do the honours of it;—so I desired the lady

[069-049]to sit down,—pressed her into the warmest

[069-050]seat,—called for more wood,—desired the hostess to

[069-051]enlarge the plan of the supper, and to favour us with the very

[069-052]best wine. 

[069-053]The lady had scarce warm’d herself five minutes at the

[069-054]fire, before she began to turn her head back, and give a look at

[069-055]the beds; and the oftener she cast her eyes that way, the more

[069-056]they return’d perplexd;—I felt for her—and for

[069-057]myself: for in a few minutes, what by her looks, and the case

[069-058]itself, I found myself as much embarrassed as it was possible the

[069-059]lady could be herself. 

[069-060]That the beds we were to lie in were in one and the same room,

[069-061]was enough simply by itself to have excited all this;—but

[069-062]the position of them, for they stood parallel, and so very close

[069-063]to each other as only to allow space for a small wicker chair

[069-064]betwixt them, rendered the affair still more oppressive to

[069-065]us;—they were fixed up moreover near the fire; and the

[069-066]projection of the chimney on one side, and a large beam which

[069-067]cross’d the room on the other, formed a kind of recess for

[069-068]them that was no way favourable to the nicety of our

[069-069]sensations:—if anything could have added to it, it was that

[069-070]the two beds were both of them so very small, as to cut us off

[069-071]from every idea of the lady and the maid lying together; which in

[069-072]either of them, could it have been feasible, my lying beside

[069-073]them, though a thing not to be wish’d, yet there was

[069-074]nothing in it so terrible which the imagination might not have

[069-075]pass’d over without torment. 

[069-076]As for the little room within, it offer’d little or no

[069-077]consolation to us: ’twas a damp, cold closet, with a half

[069-078]dismantled window-shutter, and with a window which had neither

[069-079]glass nor oil paper in it to keep out the tempest of the

[069-080]night. I did not endeavour to stifle my cough when the lady

[069-081]gave a peep into it; so it reduced the case in course to this

[069-082]alternative—That the lady should sacrifice her health to

[069-083]her feelings, and take up with the closet herself, and abandon

[069-084]the bed next mine to her maid,—or that the girl should take

[069-085]the closet, &c., &c. 

[069-086]The lady was a Piedmontese of about thirty, with a glow of

[069-087]health in her cheeks. The maid was a Lyonoise of twenty,

[069-088]and as brisk and lively a French girl as ever moved.—There

[069-089]were difficulties every way,—and the obstacle of the stone

[069-090]in the road, which brought us into the distress, great as it

[069-091]appeared whilst the peasants were removing it, was but a pebble

[069-092]to what lay in our ways now.—I have only to add, that it

[069-093]did not lessen the weight which hung upon our spirits, that we

[069-094]were both too delicate to communicate what we felt to each other

[069-095]upon the occasion. 

[069-096]We sat down to supper; and had we not had more generous wine

[069-097]to it than a little inn in Savoy could have furnish’d, our

[069-098]tongues had been tied up, till necessity herself had set them at

[069-099]liberty;—but the lady having a few bottles of Burgundy in

[069-100]her voiture, sent down her fille de chambre for a couple

[069-101]of them; so that by the time supper was over, and we were left

[069-102]alone, we felt ourselves inspired with a strength of mind

[069-103]sufficient to talk, at least, without reserve upon our

[069-104]situation. We turn’d it every way, and debated and

[069-105]considered it in all kinds of lights in the course of a two

[069-106]hours’ negotiation; at the end of which the articles were

[069-107]settled finally betwixt us, and stipulated for in form and manner

[069-108]of a treaty of peace,—and I believe with as much religion

[069-109]and good faith on both sides as in any treaty which has yet had

[069-110]the honour of being handed down to posterity. 

[069-111]They were as follow:— 

[069-112]First, as the right of the bed-chamber is in

[069-113]Monsieur,—and he thinking the bed next to the fire to be

[069-114]the warmest, he insists upon the concession on the lady’s

[069-115]side of taking up with it. 

[069-116]Granted, on the part of Madame; with a proviso, That as

[069-117]the curtains of that bed are of a flimsy transparent cotton, and

[069-118]appear likewise too scanty to draw close, that the fille de

[069-119]chambre shall fasten up the opening, either by corking pins,

[069-120]or needle and thread, in such manner as shall be deem’d a

[069-121]sufficient barrier on the side of Monsieur. 

[069-122]2dly. It is required on the part of Madame, that

[069-123]Monsieur shall lie the whole night through in his robe de

[069-124]chambre . 

[069-125]Rejected: inasmuch as Monsieur is not worth a robe de

[069-126]chambre ; he having nothing in his portmanteau but six shirts

[069-127]and a black silk pair of breeches. 

[069-128]The mentioning the silk pair of breeches made an entire change

[069-129]of the article,—for the breeches were accepted as an

[069-130]equivalent for the robe de chambre ; and so it was

[069-131]stipulated and agreed upon, that I should lie in my black silk

[069-132]breeches all night. 

[069-133]3dly. It was insisted upon and stipulated for by the

[069-134]lady, that after Monsieur was got to bed, and the candle and fire

[069-135]extinguished, that Monsieur should not speak one single word the

[069-136]whole night. 

[069-137]Granted; provided Monsieur’s saying his prayers might

[069-138]not be deemed an infraction of the treaty. 

[069-139]There was but one point forgot in this treaty, and that was

[069-140]the manner in which the lady and myself should be obliged to

[069-141]undress and get to bed;—there was but one way of doing it,

[069-142]and that I leave to the reader to devise; protesting as I do it,

[069-143]that if it is not the most delicate in nature, ’tis the

[069-144]fault of his own imagination,—against which this is not my

[069-145]first complaint. 

[069-146]Now, when we were got to bed, whether it was the novelty of

[069-147]the situation, or what it was, I know not; but so it was, I could

[069-148]not shut my eyes; I tried this side, and that, and turn’d

[069-149]and turn’d again, till a full hour after midnight; when

[069-150]Nature and patience both wearing out,—O, my God! said

[069-151]I. 

[069-152]—You have broke the treaty, Monsieur, said the lady, who

[069-153]had no more slept than myself.—I begg’d a thousand

[069-154]pardons—but insisted it was no more than an

[069-155]ejaculation. She maintained ’twas an entire

[069-156]infraction of the treaty—I maintain’d it was provided

[069-157]for in the clause of the third article. 

[069-158]The lady would by no means give up her point, though she

[069-159]weaken’d her barrier by it; for in the warmth of the

[069-160]dispute, I could hear two or three corking pins fall out of the

[069-161]curtain to the ground. 

[069-162]Upon my word and honour, Madame, said I,—stretching my

[069-163]arm out of bed by way of asseveration.— 

[069-164](I was going to have added, that I would not have trespassed

[069-165]against the remotest idea of decorum for the world);— 

[069-166]But the fille de chambre hearing there were words

[069-167]between us, and fearing that hostilities would ensue in course,

[069-168]had crept silently out of her closet, and it being totally dark,

[069-169]had stolen so close to our beds, that she had got herself into

[069-170]the narrow passage which separated them, and had advanced so far

[069-171]up as to be in a line betwixt her mistress and me:—
