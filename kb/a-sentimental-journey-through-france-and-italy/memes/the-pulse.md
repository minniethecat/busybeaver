# The Pulse

**The Pulse** is an episode from the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), in which [[yorick|Yorick]] enters a [[paris|Paris]] shop to ask directions and finds himself charmed by a beautiful [[the-grisset|grisette]]. What begins as a simple request for guidance to the [[the-opera|Opéra Comique]] escalates, through a cascade of small courtesies, into one of the novel's most playful and self-aware seduction scenes — culminating in [[yorick|Yorick]] taking the young woman's pulse.

## 概要

The episode opens with a hymn to small courtesies: "Hail, ye small sweet courtesies of life, for smooth do ye make the road of it! like grace and beauty, which beget inclinations to love at first sight: 'tis ye who open this door and let the stranger in." [[yorick|Yorick]] has been scanning shop windows "in search of a face not likely to be disordered by" his interruption, and settles on a glover's shop where a young woman sits working on a pair of ruffles.

When [[yorick|Yorick]] asks directions to the Opéra Comique, the [[the-grisset|grisette]] rises "with so cheerful a movement, and so cheerful a look" that [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects: "had I been laying out fifty louis d'ors with her, I should have said — 'This woman is grateful.'" She gives him elaborate directions — "turn first to your left hand, — mais prenez garde — there are two turns; and be so good as to take the second — then go down a little way and you'll see a church" — and repeats them three times with "the same goodnatur'd patience."

[[yorick|Yorick]] walks ten paces, forgets everything she said, and returns. "Is it possible!" she laughs. "'Tis very possible," he replies, "when a man is thinking more of a woman than of her good advice." She takes the compliment as "every woman takes a matter of right, with a slight curtsey," and then detains him — laying her hand on his arm — while she calls a boy to escort him.

Seated beside her in the shop, [[yorick|Yorick]] launches into an elaborate compliment about courtesy as "a part of the temperature," and then, touching her wrist, declares: "if it is the same blood which comes from the heart which descends to the extremes, I am sure you must have one of the best pulses of any woman (032-069) in the world." She offers her arm: "Feel it." [[yorick|Yorick]] applies his forefingers to her artery, and then apostrophizes his absent friend [[eugenius|Eugenius]]: "How wouldst thou have laugh'd and moralized upon my new profession! — and thou shouldst have laugh'd and moralized on." He concludes with amused self-vindication: "when my views are direct, [[eugenius|Eugenius]], I care not if all the world saw me feel it."

## 主题分析

- **Courtesies as Seduction**: The episode traces a careful escalatory arc — from asking directions, to forgetting them, to the hand on the arm, to sitting together, to feeling the pulse. Each step is justified by courtesy, yet the cumulative effect is unmistakably erotic.

- **Self-Aware Irony**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s address to [[eugenius|Eugenius]] provides a comic frame that acknowledges the absurdity of his "new profession" as pulse-feeler. The irony is both self-deprecating and self-justifying: he knows exactly what he is doing and finds it amusing.

- **[[sentiment|Sentiment]] Embodied**: The pulse-taking literalizes the novel's obsession with feeling. [[sentiment|Sentiment]] is not abstract — it is measured in the throbs of an artery, counted "one by one, with as much true devotion as if I had been watching the critical ebb or flow of her fever."
