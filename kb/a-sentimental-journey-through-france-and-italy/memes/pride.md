# Pride

Pride in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is a double-edged concept. Sterne satirises pride as one of the motives that send travellers abroad — "labouring with pride, curiosity, vanity, or spleen" — yet he also acknowledges a natural pride that is inseparable from human dignity. The novel's treatment of pride moves between comedy, social critique, and an almost tender recognition of its necessity.

## 定义与历史语境

Pride held an uneasy place in eighteenth-century moral discourse. Christianity condemned it as the deadliest of the seven sins, while Enlightenment philosophy began to rehabilitate it as "proper pride" or self-respect. Hume distinguished between "pride" (a legitimate pleasure in one's own character) and "vanity" (an excessive need for the admiration of others), while Mandeville scandalously argued that private vices produced public benefits. Sterne, himself a clergyman, draws on both traditions — his [[yorick|Yorick]] can mock pride and exhibit it in the same paragraph.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The preface opens with travellers "labouring with pride, curiosity, vanity, or spleen" — pride is the first-named motive, the original sin of the Grand Tour. When [[yorick|Yorick]] hears the internal voices of Hypocrisy, Meanness, and Pride warning him not to approach the begging monk, Pride's counsel — "or be any thing in it, but a lousy prebendary" — is a brilliant miniature of ecclesiastical vanity. The encounter produces one of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s most honest moments: he recognizes in himself the very pride he condemns.

Elsewhere, pride takes more sympathetic forms. [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects that it "mortifies the pride of the conceit" to see how much he owes to [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s natural philosophy — an admission of intellectual vanity. The Marquis de E—'s story turns on a father who would rather give up his sword than "smite the root for ever of the little tree his pride and affection wish'd to see re-blossom" — pride here is paternal love, not arrogance. Even [[yorick|Yorick]]'s refusal to explain himself to his landlord in [[paris|Paris]] is attributed to "my pride not suffering me to enter into any detail of the case."

## 关键段落

> "The first two include all those who travel by land or by water, labouring with pride, curiosity, vanity, or spleen." (The Preface, Calais)

> "Or be any thing in it, said Pride, but a lousy prebendary." (The Monk, Calais)

> "It always mortifies the pride of the conceit, by reflecting how much I owe to the complexional philosophy of this poor fellow." (Montreuil)

> "This was smiting the root for ever of the little tree his pride and affection wish'd to see re-blossom." (The Sword, Rennes)
