# Travel

**Travel** is the central organizing metaphor of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al Journey Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). The novel belongs to the 18th-century travel literature tradition, but Sterne subverts its conventions: where the Grand Tour promised cultural refinement through exposure to foreign lands, [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey delivers something more intimate — a series of encounters that measure the traveler's heart rather than his mileage.

## 作为文学形式的旅行 (Travel as Literary Form)

The 18th century was the great age of travel literature. From Defoe's *Tour thro' the Whole Island of Great Britain* (1724–27) to Smollett's *Travels through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1766) — the work Sterne explicitly satirizes — travel writing combined empirical observation with personal reflection. Sterne's innovation was to abandon the guidebook altogether:

> —'Tis known by the name of a *Sentimental Journey*, and if it does not take you from place to place, but rather from heart to heart, the title will not mislead you.

The novel's structure reflects this priority. Chapters are organized not by cities or distances but by encounters — the Monk at [[calais|Calais]], the Dead Ass at [[nampont|Nampont]], the fille de chambre in [[paris|Paris]], [[meeting-maria|Maria at Moulines]]. Geography recedes; sentiment advances.

## 旅行的主题 (Themes of the Journey)

### The Physical Journey

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s route follows a plausible 18th-century itinerary: [[dover|Dover]] → [[calais|Calais]] → [[montreuil|Montreuil]] → [[amiens|Amiens]] → [[paris|Paris]] → [[versailles|Versailles]] → [[moulines|Moulines]]. Each stop provides the occasion for a sentimental encounter, but Sterne is deliberately vague about practical details — distances, expenses, travel times — that conventional travel narratives would foreground. The subtitle "Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]" is itself a joke: [[yorick|Yorick]] never reaches [[italy|Italy]].

### The Inward Journey

What Sterne substitutes for geography is psychology. [[yorick|Yorick]] travels not to see monuments but to feel — to test his sensibility against the world. His encounters function as moral experiments: How should a feeling man respond to a begging monk? To a grieving father? To a mad girl? The journey's arc is not spatial but emotional.

### National Character

Travel provides Sterne's vehicle for comparing French and English manners. The barber's "ocean" of a wig-buckle, the French marquis's "tant pis / tant mieux," the landlord's assumption of English generosity — each encounter becomes a miniature essay on national difference, delivered through comedy rather than treatise.

## 与传统游记文学的对比 (Contrast with Travel Literature)

| Aspect | Conventional Travel Writing | Sterne's [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al Journey |
|--------|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| Focus | Monuments, customs, statistics | Encounters, feelings, moral tests |
| Narrator | Objective observer | [[feeling|Emotion]]ally involved participant |
| Structure | Geographic (city by city) | Episodic (encounter by encounter) |
| Purpose | Information, cultural education | Moral self-discovery |
| Tone | Factual, descriptive | Comic, tender, digressive |

Sterne's model proved enormously influential, shaping the Romantic travel narrative and the novel of sensibility for generations.
