# Tears

Tears in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* are the body's liquid testimony to the power of sympathy. They flow at encounters with strangers, at memories of the dead, at the spectacle of suffering and the recognition of kindness. Sterne treats tears not as weakness but as the physiological proof of a connected humanity — the sign that one heart has been reached by another. "I am as weak as a woman," [[yorick|Yorick]] confesses after weeping at the dead ass's grave; but this weakness is the novel's central strength.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century rehabilitated tears. Where earlier periods had associated weeping with feminine weakness or aristocratic affectation, the culture of sensibility elevated tears into a sign of moral refinement. Adam Smith, in *The Theory of Moral [[sentiment|Sentiment]]s* (1759), analyzed the mechanism by which we weep in sympathy with another's sorrow. Sterne, writing between the rise of sensibility and its first critiques, makes tears the novel's emotional currency — but he also introduces the possibility that they can be self-indulgent, even manipulative.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's first flood of tears comes at the grave of the dead ass, when [[yorick|Yorick]], having contemplated the animal's patient suffering, "burst into a flood of tears" ([[calais|Calais]], the Dead Ass). The tears are prompted by a dead animal — an object so lowly that the weeping registers both the reach of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sympathy and its potential absurdity.

Tears return in the [[paris|Paris]] chapters when [[yorick|Yorick]] recalls a dead friend: "my eyes gush out with tears. For his sake I have a predilection for the whole corps of veterans" ([[paris|Paris]], the Old Officer). Here tears are transitive — they move from the individual loss to a general benevolence toward all veterans. The tear is the hinge between private grief and public virtue.

The novel's most complex treatment of tears comes in the story of [[maria-of-moulines|Maria of Moulines]]. [[yorick|Yorick]] cannot "dry up the fountain of her tears" but finds "an exquisite sensation" in wiping them away ([[moulines|Moulines]], [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]). The tear here is ambivalent: it is genuine suffering, but it also supplies the sentimental traveller with his most intense pleasure. When the goat's tears "trickled down her cheeks," the animal's weeping completes the sentimental tableau.

## 关键段落

> "They all struck together so forcibly upon my affections, that I burst into a flood of tears — but I am as weak as a woman; and I beg the world not to smile, but to pity me." (Calais, the Dead Ass)

> "To see her weep! and, though I cannot dry up the fountain of her tears, what an exquisite sensation is there still left, in wiping them away from off the cheeks of the first and fairest of women." (Moulines, Maria)

> "My eyes gush out with tears. For his sake I have a predilection for the whole corps of veterans." (Paris, the Old Officer)

> "For, as she utter'd them, the tears trickled down her cheeks." (Moulines, Maria)
