# The Case of Delicacy

**The Case of Delicacy** is the final episode of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Stranded by a rockslide in [[savoy|Savoy]] on the road to [[turin|Turin]], [[yorick|Yorick]] is forced to share a bedchamber with a Piedmontese lady and her maid at a small roadside inn. The resulting negotiation — a mock treaty of conduct — and the ambiguous events of the night that follows constitute one of literature's most celebrated endings, in which Sterne leaves the novel suspended on a gesture and a dash.

## 概要

Having crossed [[mount-taurira|Mount Taurira]] and descended toward [[lyons|Lyons]], [[yorick|Yorick]] engages a voiturin to convey him safely through [[savoy|Savoy]] to [[turin|Turin]]. But between St. Michael and Madane, a fallen rock blocks the road. The peasants have been working all day to clear it, and by the time [[yorick|Yorick]]'s carriage arrives, two hours of labour remain. A wet and tempestuous night forces the voiturin to put up at "a little decent kind of an inn by the roadside," five miles short of the stage.

[[yorick|Yorick]] takes possession of the bedchamber, orders supper, and is "thanking heaven it was no worse" — when a second voiturin arrives carrying a Piedmontese lady and her servant maid. As there is no other bedchamber in the house, the hostess leads them into [[yorick|Yorick]]'s room, explaining that there are two good beds and a third in a small closet within.

The situation is exquisitely awkward. The lady is about thirty, with "a glow of health in her cheeks." The maid is a "brisk and lively" [[lyons|Lyon]]oise of twenty. The beds stand parallel, so close together that only a small wicker chair fits between them. The closet is "a damp, cold" space with a half-dismantled window — no fit lodging. [[yorick|Yorick]] declares he "would do anything in my power."

Over supper, fortified by the lady's bottles of Burgundy, they conduct a two-hour negotiation and produce a formal treaty:

> **Article I**: The lady concedes to take the bed next the fire, as Monsieur insists it is warmest.
> **Article II**: Monsieur shall lie the whole night in his *robe de chambre* (069-130) — rejected, as Monsieur owns no robe de chambre. Amended: he shall lie in his black silk breeches.
> **Article III**: After the candle and fire are extinguished, Monsieur shall not speak one single word. Granted, with the proviso that saying his prayers shall not be deemed an infraction.

One point is " forgot in this treaty" — how they are to undress and get to bed. This [[yorick|Yorick]] leaves "to the reader to devise; protesting as I do it, that if it is not the most delicate in nature, 'tis the fault of his own imagination."

In bed, neither can sleep. After a full hour past midnight, [[yorick|Yorick]] exclaims, "O, my God!" The lady protests: "You have broke the treaty, Monsieur" (069-152). [[yorick|Yorick]] insists it was merely an ejaculation and therefore covered by the third article. In the warmth of the dispute, "two or three corking pins fall out of the curtain." [[yorick|Yorick]], stretching his arm out of bed by way of asseveration —

But the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]], hearing words between them and fearing "hostilities would ensue," has crept silently from her closet in the dark, and advanced so far up the narrow passage between the beds as to be "in a line betwixt her mistress and me." So that when [[yorick|Yorick]] stretched out his hand, he "caught hold of the fille de chambre's —"

THE END.

## 主题分析

- **Delicacy as Social [[virtue|Virtue]]**: The episode is Sterne's most elaborate exploration of delicacy — the shared understanding that allows strangers to navigate morally hazardous situations without explicit rules. Both [[yorick|Yorick]] and the lady are "too delicate to communicate what we felt to each other" (069-094), yet their delicacy is precisely what enables their negotiation.

- **The Treaty as Social Contract**: The mock-legal treaty — complete with articles, provisos, and amendments — parodies diplomatic convention while suggesting that all social interaction rests on negotiated terms. The substitution of black silk breeches for a robe de chambre is a comic miniature of political compromise.

- **The Unfinished Ending**: The novel's final line — ending on a dash and the word "—" — is one of the most famous endings in English literature. By breaking off at the moment of maximum suggestiveness, Sterne forces the reader to complete the scene in imagination — and, in doing so, to confront their own delicacy (or lack of it). The ending is not a failure of closure but a deliberate narrative strategy that makes the reader complicit.

- **[[nature|Nature]] and Shelter**: The opening paragraphs of the episode return to the novel's pastoral register, praising the "sweet dwellings" of [[savoy|Savoy]]'s poor and the protective scantiness of nature. This framing contrasts the elemental dangers of the mountain road with the refined dilemmas of indoor conduct.
