## PARIS.

[057-001]La Fleur had left me something to

[057-002]amuse myself with for the day more than I had bargain’d

[057-003]for, or could have enter’d either into his head or

[057-004]mine. 

[057-005]He had brought the little print of butter upon a currant leaf:

[057-006]and as the morning was warm, and he had a good step to bring it,

[057-007]he had begg’d a sheet of waste paper to put betwixt the

[057-008]currant leaf and his hand.—As that was plate sufficient, I

[057-009]bade him lay it upon the table as it was; and as I resolved to

[057-010]stay within all day, I ordered him to call upon the traîteur , to bespeak my dinner, and leave me to

[057-011]breakfast by myself. 

[057-012]When I had finished the butter, I threw the currant-leaf out

[057-013]of the window, and was going to do the same by the waste

[057-014]paper;—but stopping to read a line first, and that drawing

[057-015]me on to a second and third,—I thought it better

[057-016]worth; so I shut the window, and drawing a chair up to it, I sat

[057-017]down to read it. 

[057-018]It was in the old French of Rabelais’s time, and for

[057-019]aught I know might have been wrote by him:—it was moreover

[057-020]in a Gothic letter, and that so faded and gone off by damps and

[057-021]length of time, it cost me infinite trouble to make anything of

[057-022]it.—I threw it down; and then wrote a letter to

[057-023]Eugenius;—then I took it up again, and embroiled my

[057-024]patience with it afresh;—and then to cure that, I wrote a

[057-025]letter to Eliza.—Still it kept hold of me; and the

[057-026]difficulty of understanding it increased but the desire. 

[057-027]I got my dinner; and after I had enlightened my mind with a

[057-028]bottle of Burgundy; I at it again,—and, after two or three

[057-029]hours poring upon it, with almost as deep attention as ever

[057-030]Gruter or Jacob Spon did upon a nonsensical inscription, I

[057-031]thought I made sense of it; but to make sure of it, the best way,

[057-032]I imagined, was to turn it into English, and see how it would

[057-033]look then;—so I went on leisurely, as a trifling man does,

[057-034]sometimes writing a sentence,—then taking a turn or

[057-035]two,—and then looking how the world went, out of the

[057-036]window; so that it was nine o’clock at night before I had

[057-037]done it.—I then began and read it as follows.
