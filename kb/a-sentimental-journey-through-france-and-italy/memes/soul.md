# Soul

The soul in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the invisible organ of genuine feeling — the part of a person that pities, burns with impatience, receives the "iron" of suffering, and reveals character that social performance cannot manufacture.

Sterne's narrator consistently distinguishes between outward manner and inward soul, finding the latter in servants, beggars, and strangers as readily as in the polite company of [[paris|Paris]].

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth-century concept of the soul stood at the intersection of theology, philosophy, and the emerging discourse of sensibility.

Orthodox Christianity located the soul as the immortal essence; Lockean empiricism made it the tabula rasa shaped by experience; and the culture of sensibility elevated it to the seat of moral feeling — the inner organ that sympathized with suffering before reason could intervene.

Sterne drew on all three traditions, but his fiction consistently privileges the sentimental soul: the capacity to be "touched" by another's distress as the truest measure of human worth.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sterne deploys "soul" across a wide social range, and the word's distribution itself makes an argument. [[yorick|Yorick]] pities the distressed lady "from my soul" — the phrase implying a depth of feeling beyond politeness.

[[la-fleur|La Fleur]] is introduced as "a faithful, affectionate, simple soul" — the adjective "simple" here functioning as praise, identifying an unstudied goodness that [[yorick|Yorick]]'s more sophisticated acquaintances lack. The poor tattered figure who withdraws his claim in the [[montreuil|Montreuil]] beggar scene acts from soul rather than calculation.

And in the novel's most piercing moment, when [[yorick|Yorick]] contemplates [[the-starling|the caged starling]], he sees "the iron enter into his soul" — the line collapsing the distance between bird and man, making the soul the receptor of suffering itself.

The satirical counterpoint comes through [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] and [[mundungus|Mundungus]], whose "souls" would do penance even in heaven because they lack the sentimental faculty. The soul, for Sterne, is defined not by its theological status but by its capacity for feeling.

## 关键段落

> "Melancholy! to see such sprightliness the prey of sorrow — I pitied her from my soul; and though it may seem ridiculous enough to a torpid heart, I could have taken her into my arms." (010-036)

> "He was a faithful, affectionate, simple soul as ever trudged after the heels of a philosopher." (020-006)

> "Did we but love each other as this poor soul loved his ass — 'twould be something." (025-057)

> "I saw the iron enter into his soul — I burst into tears — I could not sustain the picture of confinement which my fancy had drawn." (041-035)

> "The souls of Smelfungus and Mundungus would do penance there to all eternity." (017-067)
