## IN THE DESOBLIGEANT.

[006-001]It must have been observed by many

[006-002]a peripatetic philosopher, That nature has set up by her own

[006-003]unquestionable authority certain boundaries and fences to

[006-004]circumscribe the discontent of man; she has effected her purpose

[006-005]in the quietest and easiest manner by laying him under almost

[006-006]insuperable obligations to work out his ease, and to sustain his

[006-007]sufferings at home. It is there only that she has provided

[006-008]him with the most suitable objects to partake of his happiness,

[006-009]and bear a part of that burden which in all countries and ages

[006-010]has ever been too heavy for one pair of shoulders.

[006-011]’Tis true, we are endued with an imperfect power of

[006-012]spreading our happiness sometimes beyond her limits, but

[006-013]’tis so ordered, that, from the want of languages,

[006-014]connections, and dependencies, and from the difference in

[006-015]education, customs, and habits, we lie under so many impediments

[006-016]in communicating our sensations out of our own sphere, as often

[006-017]amount to a total impossibility. 

[006-018]It will always follow from hence, that the balance of

[006-019]sentimental commerce is always against the expatriated

[006-020]adventurer: he must buy what he has little occasion for, at their

[006-021]own price;—his conversation will seldom be taken in

[006-022]exchange for theirs without a large discount,—and this, by

[006-023]the by, eternally driving him into the hands of more equitable

[006-024]brokers, for such conversation as he can find, it requires no

[006-025]great spirit of divination to guess at his party— 

[006-026]This brings me to my point; and naturally leads me (if the

[006-027]see-saw of this désobligeant will but let me get

[006-028]on) into the efficient as well as final causes of

[006-029]travelling— 

[006-030]Your idle people that leave their native country, and go

[006-031]abroad for some reason or reasons which may be derived from one

[006-032]of these general causes:— 

[006-033]Infirmity of body, 

[006-034]Imbecility of mind, or 

[006-035]Inevitable necessity. 

[006-036]The first two include all those who travel by land or by

[006-037]water, labouring with pride, curiosity, vanity, or spleen,

[006-038]subdivided and combined ad infinitum . 

[006-039]The third class includes the whole army of peregrine martyrs;

[006-040]more especially those travellers who set out upon their travels

[006-041]with the benefit of the clergy, either as delinquents travelling

[006-042]under the direction of governors recommended by the

[006-043]magistrate;—or young gentlemen transported by the cruelty

[006-044]of parents and guardians, and travelling under the direction of

[006-045]governors recommended by Oxford, Aberdeen, and Glasgow. 

[006-046]There is a fourth class, but their number is so small that they would

[006-047]not deserve a distinction, were it not necessary in a work of

[006-048]this nature to observe the greatest precision and nicety, to

[006-049]avoid a confusion of character. And these men I speak of,

[006-050]are such as cross the seas and sojourn in a land of strangers,

[006-051]with a view of saving money for various reasons and upon various

[006-052]pretences: but as they might also save themselves and others a

[006-053]great deal of unnecessary trouble by saving their money at

[006-054]home,—and as their reasons for travelling are the least

[006-055]complex of any other species of emigrants, I shall distinguish

[006-056]these gentlemen by the name of 

[006-057]Simple Travellers. 

[006-058]Thus the whole circle of travellers may be reduced to the

[006-059]following heads :— 

[006-060]Idle Travellers, 

[006-061]Inquisitive Travellers, 

[006-062]Lying Travellers, 

[006-063]Proud Travellers, 

[006-064]Vain Travellers, 

[006-065]Splenetic Travellers. 

[006-066]Then follow: 

[006-067]The Travellers of Necessity, 

[006-068]The Delinquent and Felonious Traveller, 

[006-069]The Unfortunate and Innocent Traveller, 

[006-070]The Simple Traveller, 

[006-071]And last of all (if you please) The Sentimental Traveller,

[006-072](meaning thereby myself) who have travell’d, and of which I

[006-073]am now sitting down to give an account,—as much out of Necessity , and the besoin de Voyager , as any one in

[006-074]the class. 

[006-075]I am well aware, at the same time, as both my travels and

[006-076]observations will be altogether of a different cast from any of

[006-077]my forerunners, that I might have insisted upon a whole nitch

[006-078]entirely to myself;—but I should break in upon the confines

[006-079]of the Vain Traveller, in wishing to draw attention

[006-080]towards me, till I have some better grounds for it than the mere Novelty of my Vehicle . 

[006-081]It is sufficient for my reader, if he has been a traveller

[006-082]himself, that with study and reflection hereupon he may be able

[006-083]to determine his own place and rank in the catalogue;—it will be

[006-084]one step towards knowing himself; as it is great odds but he

[006-085]retains some tincture and resemblance, of what he imbibed or

[006-086]carried out, to the present hour. 

[006-087]The man who first transplanted the grape of Burgundy to the

[006-088]Cape of Good Hope (observe he was a Dutchman) never dreamt of

[006-089]drinking the same wine at the Cape, that the same grape produced

[006-090]upon the French mountains,—he was too phlegmatic for

[006-091]that—but undoubtedly he expected to drink some sort of

[006-092]vinous liquor; but whether good or bad, or indifferent,—he

[006-093]knew enough of this world to know, that it did not depend upon

[006-094]his choice, but that what is generally called choice , was

[006-095]to decide his success: however, he hoped for the best; and in

[006-096]these hopes, by an intemperate confidence in the fortitude of his

[006-097]head, and the depth of his discretion, Mynheer might

[006-098]possibly oversee both in his new vineyard; and by discovering his

[006-099]nakedness, become a laughing stock to his people. 

[006-100]Even so it fares with the Poor Traveller, sailing and posting

[006-101]through the politer kingdoms of the globe, in pursuit of

[006-102]knowledge and improvements. 

[006-103]Knowledge and improvements are to be got by sailing and

[006-104]posting for that purpose; but whether useful knowledge and real

[006-105]improvements is all a lottery;—and even where the

[006-106]adventurer is successful, the acquired stock must be used with

[006-107]caution and sobriety, to turn to any profit:—but, as the

[006-108]chances run prodigiously the other way, both as to the

[006-109]acquisition and application, I am of opinion, That a man would

[006-110]act as wisely, if he could prevail upon himself to live contented

[006-111]without foreign knowledge or foreign improvements, especially if

[006-112]he lives in a country that has no absolute want of

[006-113]either;—and indeed, much grief of heart has it oft and many

[006-114]a time cost me, when I have observed how many a foul step the

[006-115]Inquisitive Traveller has measured to see sights and look into

[006-116]discoveries; all which, as Sancho Panza said to Don Quixote, they

[006-117]might have seen dry-shod at home. It is an age so full of

[006-118]light, that there is scarce a country or corner in Europe whose

[006-119]beams are not crossed and interchanged with

[006-120]others.—Knowledge in most of its branches, and in most

[006-121]affairs, is like music in an Italian street, whereof

[006-122]those may partake who pay nothing.—But there is no nation

[006-123]under heaven—and God is my record (before whose tribunal I

[006-124]must one day come and give an account of this work)—that I

[006-125]do not speak it vauntingly,—but there is no nation under

[006-126]heaven abounding with more variety of learning,—where the

[006-127]sciences may be more fitly woo’d, or more surely won, than

[006-128]here,—where art is encouraged, and will so soon rise

[006-129]high,—where Nature (take her altogether) has so little to

[006-130]answer for,—and, to close all, where there is more wit and

[006-131]variety of character to feed the mind with:—Where then, my

[006-132]dear countrymen, are you going?— 

[006-133]We are only looking at this chaise, said they.—Your most

[006-134]obedient servant, said I, skipping out of it, and pulling off my

[006-135]hat.—We were wondering, said one of them, who, I found was

[006-136]an Inquisitive Traveller ,—what could occasion its

[006-137]motion.—’Twas the agitation, said I, coolly, of

[006-138]writing a preface.—I never heard, said the other, who was a Simple Traveller , of a preface wrote in a désobligeant .—It would have been better, said

[006-139]I, in a vis-a-vis . 

[006-140]— As an Englishman does not travel to see

[006-141]Englishmen , I retired to my room.
