# The Passport

**The Passport** is the central bureaucratic-comic narrative arc of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), unfolding across six chapters between [[paris]] and [[versailles]]. The affair begins with [[yorick|Yorick]]'s precipitous departure from [[london|London]] — he forgot [[england|England]] was at war with [[france]] — and culminates in one of the novel's most memorable scenes: [[yorick|Yorick]] pointing to his own name in *Hamlet* and declaring "*Me voici!*" to [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], who secures him a passport addressed to "the king's jester." The arc spans the entire [[paris|Paris]] section and is threaded through by [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s prevenancy, the caged [[the-starling|starling]]'s cry, and Sterne's meditation on identity, liberty, and the art of getting through locked doors by wit alone.

## 概要

### The Origin: London to Dover

[[yorick|Yorick]] left [[london|London]] with "so much precipitation, that it never enter'd my mind that we were at war with [[france|France]]" (Ch. THE PASSPORT, PARIS). The realisation struck only at [[dover]], when he "looked through my glass at the hills beyond [[boulogne|Boulogne]]" and understood "that there was no getting there without a passport." (039-008) Unwilling to return "no wiser than I set out," he begged the Count de ——, who had hired the packet, to take him in his suite. The Count could only help as far as [[calais]] — beyond that, [[yorick|Yorick]] must "make friends and shift for myself."

### The Paris Crisis

The crisis surfaces in [[paris|Paris]] when [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] reports that the Lieutenant de Police has been enquiring after [[yorick|Yorick]]. The master of the [[hotel-de-modene|hotel]] adds that "my passport had been particularly asked after" (039-026). When [[yorick|Yorick]] confesses he has none, the master "retired three steps from me, as from an infected person," while [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] "advanced three steps towards me, and with that sort of movement which a good soul makes to succour a distress'd one" (Ch. THE PASSPORT, PARIS).

The hotel-master delivers the ultimatum: without a passport, [[yorick|Yorick]] "will certainly be sent to the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] to-morrow morning." (039-051) [[yorick|Yorick]], with characteristic insouciance, replies that he has taken lodgings for a month and "I'll not quit them a day before the time for all the kings of [[france|France]] in the world." [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] whispers that "nobody could oppose the king of [[france|France]]."

Alone after supper, [[yorick|Yorick]]'s mind turns to a dialogue he had with [[eugenius]] before departing — a jest about getting "clapp'd up into the [[the-bastile|Bastile]]" and living "entirely at the king of [[france|France]]'s expense." [[yorick|Yorick]] now treats the threat seriously but philosophically: "the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] is but another word for a tower; — and a tower is but another word for a house you can't get out of." With "nine livres a day, and pen and ink, and paper, and patience," a man "may do very well within."

It is during this meditation that [[yorick|Yorick]] steps into the courtyard and hears a voice crying "I can't get out" — the caged [[the-starling|starling]]. The bird's mechanical repetition of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own predicament becomes one of the novel's most famous passages:

> "I can't get out — I can't get out," said the starling. — God help thee! said I, but I'll let thee out, cost what it will; so I turn'd about the cage to get to the door; it was twisted and double twisted. — The bird flew to the place where he was nearest, and thrusting his head through the trellis, pressed his breast against it, as if impatient. — I fear, poor creature! said I, I cannot set thee at liberty. — "No," said the starling; — "I can't get out — I can't get out," said the starling.
>
> (039-040)

The starling's cage becomes the emblem of all confinement — political, existential, and spiritual.

### The Versailles Resolution

[[yorick|Yorick]] journeys to [[versailles]] to seek [[count-de-b|Count de B—]]. Finding a set of Shakespeare upon the Count's table, he approaches with an unconventional introduction: "it is my countryman, the great Shakespeare, said I, pointing to his works — *et ayez la bonté, mon cher ami*, apostrophizing his spirit, added I, *de me faire cet honneur-là*" (Ch. THE PASSPORT, VERSAILLES).

[[yorick|Yorick]] tells the Count the story of his embarrassment. When the Count asks his name, [[yorick|Yorick]] — finding Shakespeare lying upon the table — takes up *Hamlet*, turns to the grave-diggers' scene, and lays his finger upon [[yorick|Yorick]]:

> I laid my finger upon Yorick, and advancing the book to the Count, with my finger all the way over the name, — *Me voici!* said I.
>
> (047-011)

The Count, like the English bishop before him, falls "into the same error" — confounding the living [[yorick|Yorick]] with the king's jester of eight hundred years ago. "*Et, Monsieur, est-il [[yorick|Yorick]]?*" cried the Count. "*Je le suis,*" said [[yorick|Yorick]]. "*Vous? — Moi, — moi qui ai l'honneur de vous parler, Monsieur le Comte.*" "*Mon Dieu!*" said the Count, embracing him, "*Vous êtes [[yorick|Yorick]]!*"

The Count departs abruptly, Shakespeare in pocket. [[yorick|Yorick]], left alone, reads *Much Ado [[About]] Nothing* and forgets "[[versailles|Versailles]], the Count, or the passport" entirely — a characteristic surrender to imagination that Sterne calls the "sweet pliability of man's spirit."

The Count returns with the passport. The [[duc-de-c|Duc de Choiseul]] had said: "*Un homme qui rit ne sera jamais dangereux*" — a man who laughs will never be dangerous. "Had it been for any one but the king's jester," the Count adds, "I could not have got it these two hours." (046-037) [[yorick|Yorick]] protests that he is not the king's jester — he jests "entirely at my own expense." But the passport is signed:

> As the passport was directed to all lieutenant-governors, governors, and commandants of cities, generals of armies, justiciaries, and all officers of justice, to let Mr. Yorick the king's jester, and his baggage, travel quietly along, — I own the triumph of obtaining the passport was not a little tarnish'd by the figure I cut in it.
>
> (047-011)

### Aftermath

The passport secured, [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects that "there is nothing unmix'd in this world." The affair's residues surface throughout the remainder of the [[travel|Journey]]: [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], during the Count's audience, had "connected himself with the demoiselle upon the landing of the staircase" — his own parallel conquest (Ch. THE FRAGMENT, PARIS). And the "one kindness in the affair of my passport" is recalled even in the novel's final chapter, [[the-case-of-delicacy]], as a debt of gratitude that colours [[yorick|Yorick]]'s final encounter.

## 主题分析

- **Identity as Performance**: The passport crisis is resolved not by documentation but by performance — [[yorick|Yorick]] points to a fictional character who shares his name, and the Count accepts the conflation. "There are two [[yorick|Yorick]]s" — the dead jester and the living traveller — but the passport is issued to neither: it is addressed to a third [[yorick|Yorick]], the one the Count believes in. Sterne suggests that identity in the world of nations and borders is always a kind of fiction, granted by those with the power to name.

- **[[the-bastile|The Bastile]] as State of [[mind|Mind]]**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s philosophical response to the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] threat — reducing the prison to "a house you can't get out of" — is not mere bravado. It is a method: "reduce them to their proper size and hue, she overlooks them." The starling's cage literalises this meditation. The bird cannot get out, and neither can the man without a passport. But the starling also cannot reduce the cage by philosophy. [[yorick|Yorick]] can — and does — by walking to [[versailles|Versailles]] and charming a Count with Shakespeare.

- **Laughter as Political Currency**: The [[duc-de-c|Duc de Choiseul]]'s maxim — "*Un homme qui rit ne sera jamais dangereux*" — is the arc's thesis. [[yorick|Yorick]] secures passage through a hostile state not by rank, wealth, or diplomatic credentials, but by being amusing. The passport is issued to "the king's jester" because laughter is the one credential that needs no verification. Sterne's satire of absolutist bureaucracy is gentle but pointed: the state fears seriousness more than wit.

- **The Servant and the Master**: [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s role in the passport arc is a structural mirror of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own. Just as [[yorick|Yorick]] secures his passport through social charm, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] secures his demoiselle "during the time I was taken up with my passport." The servant's "prevenancy — for there was a passport in his very looks" (Ch. THE LETTER) prefigures the master's own passport strategy: social fluency is itself a form of diplomatic credential.

- **Shakespeare as Intercessor**: The volume of Shakespeare on the Count's table is the arc's hinge. It provides the introduction ("my countryman, the great Shakespeare"), the identity proof ("I laid my finger upon [[yorick|Yorick]]"), and the waiting-room reading material (*Much Ado [[About]] Nothing*). Shakespeare is not merely referenced; he is mobilised as a character in the scene — the silent fourth party to the negotiation, whose name opens doors that [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own cannot.
