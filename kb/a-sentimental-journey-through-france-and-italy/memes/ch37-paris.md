## PARIS.

[037-001]It was now my turn to ask the old

[037-002]French officer “What was the matter?” for a cry of

[037-003]“ Haussez les mains , Monsieur

[037-004]l’Abbé !” re-echoed from a dozen different

[037-005]parts of the parterre, was as unintelligible to me, as my

[037-006]apostrophe to the monk had been to him. 

[037-007]He told me it was some poor Abbé in one of the upper

[037-008]loges, who, he supposed, had got planted perdu behind a couple of

[037-009]grisettes in order to see the opera, and that the parterre

[037-010]espying him, were insisting upon his holding up both his hands

[037-011]during the representation.—And can it be supposed, said I,

[037-012]that an ecclesiastic would pick the grisettes’

[037-013]pockets? The old French officer smiled, and whispering in

[037-014]my ear, opened a door of knowledge which I had no idea of. 

[037-015]Good God! said I, turning pale with astonishment—is it

[037-016]possible, that a people so smit with sentiment should at the same

[037-017]time be so unclean, and so unlike themselves,— Quelle

[037-018]grossièrté ! added I. 

[037-019]The French officer told me, it was an illiberal sarcasm at the

[037-020]church, which had begun in the theatre about the time the

[037-021]Tartuffe was given in it by Molière: but like other

[037-022]remains of Gothic manners, was declining.—Every nation,

[037-023]continued he, have their refinements and grossièrtés , in which they take

[037-024]the lead, and lose it of one another by turns:—that he had

[037-025]been in most countries, but never in one where he found not some

[037-026]delicacies, which others seemed to want. Le POUR et le CONTRE se trouvent en chaque

[037-027]nation ; there is a balance, said he, of good and bad

[037-028]everywhere; and nothing but the knowing it is so, can emancipate

[037-029]one half of the world from the prepossession which it holds

[037-030]against the other:—that the advantage of travel, as it

[037-031]regarded the sçavoir vivre , was by seeing a great

[037-032]deal both of men and manners; it taught us mutual toleration; and

[037-033]mutual toleration, concluded he, making me a bow, taught us

[037-034]mutual love. 

[037-035]The old French officer delivered this with an air of such

[037-036]candour and good sense, as coincided with my first favourable

[037-037]impressions of his character:—I thought I loved the man;

[037-038]but I fear I mistook the object;—’twas my own way of

[037-039]thinking—the difference was, I could not have expressed it

[037-040]half so well. 

[037-041]It is alike troublesome to both the rider and his

[037-042]beast,—if the latter goes pricking up his ears, and

[037-043]starting all the way at every object which he never saw

[037-044]before.—I have as little torment of this kind as any

[037-045]creature alive; and yet I honestly confess, that many a thing

[037-046]gave me pain, and that I blush’d at many a word the first

[037-047]month,—which I found inconsequent and perfectly innocent

[037-048]the second. 

[037-049]Madame do Rambouliet, after an acquaintance of about six weeks

[037-050]with her, had done me the honour to take me in her coach about

[037-051]two leagues out of town.—Of all women, Madame de Rambouliet

[037-052]is the most correct; and I never wish to see one of more virtues

[037-053]and purity of heart.—In our return back, Madame de

[037-054]Rambouliet desired me to pull the cord.—I asked her if she

[037-055]wanted anything— Rien que pour pisser , said Madame de

[037-056]Rambouliet. 

[037-057]Grieve not, gentle traveller, to let Madame de Rambouliet

[037-058]p—ss on.—And, ye fair mystic nymphs! go each one pluck your rose , and scatter them in your path,—for

[037-059]Madame de Rambouliet did no more.—I handed Madame de

[037-060]Rambouliet out of the coach; and had I been the priest of the

[037-061]chaste Castalia, I could not have served at her fountain with a

[037-062]more respectful decorum.
