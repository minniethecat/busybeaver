# Passion

Passion in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the wilder cousin of sentiment — less governed, more turbulent, and morally ambiguous. Where sentiment refines and socializes feeling, passion disrupts and exposes. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s cry — "Base passion! thy hand is against every man" — marks the distance between the raw force and the cultivated sensibility: passion is the enemy of sympathy when it serves the self alone.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century conducted a long debate over the moral status of the passions. Stoic and Christian traditions had long taught their suppression; the new philosophy of sentiment argued for their regulation rather than their elimination. Hume's "calm passions" and "violent passions" provided a taxonomy; Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral [[sentiment|Sentiment]]s* described how sympathy moderates passion into something socially usable. Sterne inherited this debate and dramatised it: his novel is full of characters in whom passion either breaks through decorum (revealing truth) or destroys it (revealing selfishness).

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sterne treats passion as a double-edged instrument. The monk's gentle rebuke provokes in [[yorick|Yorick]] a "base passion" he later regrets — the novel's most explicit lesson in how ungoverned feeling wounds. Yet elsewhere, passion is the sign of authentic emotion: the "foolish passion" into which [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s story works [[yorick|Yorick]] is also the proof that the story has touched him. The tension is structural: passion that breaks free of sympathetic discipline becomes cruelty; passion that is entirely suppressed leaves sentiment bloodless.

The novel's most intense passages — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s encounter with [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]], the scene at the inn, the final ambiguous episode — all turn on the question of whether passion can be trusted. Sterne's answer, characteristically, is: sometimes.

## 关键段落

> "Base passion! said I, turning myself about, as a man naturally does upon a sudden reverse of sentiment,—base, ungentle passion! thy hand is against every man." (The Monk chapter)

> "some interval betwixt one passion and another: whilst this interregnum lasts, I always perceive my heart locked up." (The Remise Door chapter)

> "he has worked me into a foolish passion, and then abuses me for it." (The Supper chapter)

> "mask your passions — tell me, what trespass is it that man should have them?" (The Case of Delicacy chapter)
