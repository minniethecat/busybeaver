# Chevalier de St. Louis

**The Chevalier de St. Louis** is a former French military officer whom [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters on the streets of [[versailles|Versailles]] selling pâtés from a basket — a man whose Croix de St. Louis hangs incongruously over his cook's apron, embodying one of the novel's most poignant reversals of fortune. His story, told with modest dignity, eventually reaches the king, who grants him a pension of fifteen hundred livres a year (044-007).

## Role Overview

The Chevalier appears in a single sustained passage during the [[versailles|Versailles]] sequence, but his significance extends beyond his brief presence. He represents what Sterne calls "a reverse in man's life" that "awakens a better principle than curiosity" (044-003) — a living emblem of the fragility of honour in a world where military service can leave a man without provision. His function in the narrative is to test [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental responsiveness: the encounter moves from curiosity to pity, and from pity to esteem, tracing the ascending scale of genuine sympathy that Sterne's novel celebrates.

Unlike many of the novel's characters who are defined by a single comic trait, the Chevalier is presented with unadorned seriousness. His dignity in misfortune makes him one of the most morally weighty figures in the entire journey.

## Appearances in the Narrative

### The Encounter

[[yorick|Yorick]] first notices the Chevalier while waiting in his remise in [[versailles|Versailles]]. Seeing a figure "standing with a basket on the other side of the street, as if he had something to sell," he bids [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] to enquire for the Count's hotel. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] returns "a little pale" with the news: it is "a Chevalier de St. Louis selling pâtés" (044-001).

[[la-fleur|La Fleur]] is unable to account for the phenomenon, but he insists on the evidence: "he had seen the croix set in gold, with its red riband, he said, tied to his buttonhole — and had looked into the basket and seen the pâtés which the Chevalier was selling; so could not be mistaken in that" (044-002).

[[yorick|Yorick]] is transfixed:

> "Such a reverse in man's life awakens a better principle than curiosity: I could not help looking for some time at him as I sat in the remise: — the more I look'd at him, his croix, and his basket, the stronger they wove themselves into my brain. — I got out of the remise, and went towards him." (044-003)

### The Man and His Basket

[[yorick|Yorick]] finds the Chevalier "begirt with a clean linen apron which fell below his knees, and with a sort of a bib that went half way up his breast; upon the top of this, but a little below the hem, hung his croix" (044-004). The description dwells on the meticulous cleanliness of the man's appearance:

> "His basket of little pâtés was covered over with a white damask napkin; another of the same kind was spread at the bottom; and there was a look of propreté and neatness throughout, that one might have bought his pâtés of him, as much from appetite as sentiment." (044-004)

Crucially, the Chevalier does not solicit custom: "He made an offer of them to neither; but stood still with them at the corner of an hotel, for those to buy who chose it without solicitation" (044-004). This dignified passivity — selling without hawking — is the first sign of the honour that survives in him despite his fallen circumstances.

[[yorick|Yorick]] notes that the Chevalier "was about forty-eight; — of a sedate look, something approaching to gravity" (044-005). Taking one of the pâtés into his hand, [[yorick|Yorick]] "begg'd he would explain the appearance which affected me" (044-005).

### The Chevalier's Story

The Chevalier tells his story "in a few words": the best part of his life had passed in military service, in which, after spending a small patrimony, he had obtained a company and the Croix de St. Louis. But at the conclusion of the last peace, his regiment was reformed and the whole corps left without any provision. He found himself "in a wide world without friends, without a livre, — and indeed, said he, without anything but this, — (pointing, as he said it, to his croix)" (044-006).

> "The poor Chevalier won my pity, and he finished the scene with winning my esteem too." (044-006)

The Chevalier adds that the king "was the most generous of princes, but his generosity could neither relieve nor reward everyone, and it was only his misfortune to be amongst the number." He mentions a "little wife, he said, whom he loved, who did the pâtisserie; and added, he felt no dishonour in defending her and himself from want in this way — unless [[fortune|Providence]] had offer'd him a better" (044-006).

This speech, with its refusal to blame the king, its tender reference to a wife, and its assertion that honest labour carries no dishonour, completes the portrait of a man whose nobility is moral rather than titular.

### The King's Pension

Sterne adds a coda to the story, occurring "about nine months after." The Chevalier usually took his stand near the iron gates leading to the palace, and "as his croix had caught the eyes of numbers, numbers had made the same enquiry which I had done" (044-007). He told them the same story, "always with so much modesty and good sense, that it had reach'd at last the king's ears." The king, hearing the Chevalier had been "a gallant officer, and respected by the whole regiment as a man of honour and integrity, — he broke up his little trade by a pension of fifteen hundred livres a year" (044-007).

The Chevalier's story concludes with quiet justice — the king recognizing what [[yorick|Yorick]] had already seen: that a man who wears his military cross over a cook's apron, and who sells pâtés without soliciting, has not lost the honour that the cross was meant to reward.

## Character and Themes

**The Reversal of [[fortune|Fortune]]**: The Chevalier embodies the precariousness of honour in a society where military service does not guarantee provision. His Croix de St. Louis — a mark of gallantry — hangs over an apron, creating a visual paradox that Sterne uses to interrogate the relationship between outward marks of honour and inward worth. The Chevalier passes the test: his dignity is unchanged by his circumstances.

**[[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[charity|Charity]]**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s response to the Chevalier traces the novel's model of genuine sympathy. He moves from curiosity ("I could not help looking") to engagement ("I got out of the remise, and went towards him") to pity ("the poor Chevalier won my pity") and finally to esteem ("he finished the scene with winning my esteem too"). This ascending scale — curiosity → pity → esteem — is the sentimental journey in miniature.

**[[honour|Honour]] Without Shame**: The Chevalier's assertion that he "felt no dishonour in defending her and himself from want in this way" is one of the novel's most morally assured moments. Unlike characters who are objects of satire or sentimental condescension, the Chevalier is presented as a moral equal — a man whose self-respect is so secure that it survives the indignity of selling pâtés on the street. His quiet dignity makes him a foil to the novel's less self-possessed figures.

**The Good King**: The king's intervention — hearing of the Chevalier's story through public report and acting to remedy his situation — provides a rare moment of institutional benevolence in a novel that is otherwise sceptical of power. The Chevalier's refusal to blame the king ("the most generous of princes") is validated by the pension, creating a closed loop of mutual recognition between honour and authority.
