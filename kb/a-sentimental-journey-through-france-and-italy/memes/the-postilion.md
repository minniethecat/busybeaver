# The Postilion

**The Postilion** is the carriage driver who conveys [[yorick|Yorick]] through several stages of his journey in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). He appears most memorably at [[nampont|Nampont]], where his relentless galloping after the emotional Dead Ass episode serves as a comic counterpoint to [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental absorption, and again on the road to [[turin|Turin]], where his mechanical competence contrasts with the narrator's philosophical detachment.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Postilion is a functional figure — a man who drives carriages for a living — but Sterne elevates him into a minor comic character through the interplay between his professional impassivity and [[yorick|Yorick]]'s hyper-sensitive temperament. He represents the practical, unreflective world of physical labor that the sentimental traveller both depends upon and is perpetually at odds with. His appearances bookend the journey: first in the early chapters around [[montreuil|Montreuil]] and [[nampont|Nampont]], and later on the approach to [[turin|Turin]]. (Ch. 9, [[montreuil|Montreuil]]; Ch. 27, [[nampont|Nampont]]; Ch. 70, Road to [[turin|Turin]])

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### Early Service at Montreuil

The Postilion first appears as a background figure in the early stages of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey. At [[montreuil|Montreuil]], it is the Postilion who leads out the horses as [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] takes his emotional leave of the young women of the town. He is already established as a constant but unremarked presence in the machinery of eighteenth-century travel. (018-006)

### The Gallop After Nampont

The Postilion's most vivid appearance comes immediately after the Dead Ass episode at [[nampont|Nampont]]. [[yorick|Yorick]], deeply moved by the poor German's lament for his dead ass, craves "grave and quiet movements." The Postilion, however, "paid not the least to it, but set off upon the pavé in a full gallop":

> "The concern which the poor fellow's story threw me into required some attention; the postilion paid not the least to it, but set off upon the pavé in a full gallop. The thirstiest soul in the most sandy desert of Arabia could not have wished more for a cup of cold water, than mine did for grave and quiet movements; and I should have had an high opinion of the postilion had he but stolen off with me in something like a pensive pace. — On the contrary, as the mourner finished his lamentation, the fellow gave an unfeeling lash to each of his beasts, and set off clattering like a thousand devils." (026-009)

The louder [[yorick|Yorick]] calls for slower pace, "the more unmercifully he galloped." The Postilion then "managed the point to a miracle": by the time they reach the foot of a steep hill, he has put [[yorick|Yorick]] out of temper entirely — at which point [[yorick|Yorick]] suddenly wants a "good rattling gallop" but finds the Postilion, now faced with a hill, unable to get his horses into even a trot. The comic timing is perfect, and Sterne uses it to illustrate how our moods are governed by forces as mechanical as a postilion's whip. (026-022)

### The Road to Turin

In the novel's closing chapters, the Postilion reappears on the approach to [[turin|Turin]]. When a shoe comes loose from one of the horses near Taurira, the Postilion dismounts, twists the shoe off, and sets about refastening it as well as he can. [[yorick|Yorick]], eager to press on, prevails upon him "to manage his point as he could," while the narrator resolves to manage his own. This late appearance reinforces the Postilion's role as the embodiment of practical necessity — the physical world that persists regardless of the traveller's sentimental states. (Ch. 70, Road to [[turin|Turin]])

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**The Unfeeling World**: The Postilion represents the indifferent physical world that sentimental consciousness must constantly negotiate. His galloping after the Dead Ass episode dramatizes the collision between inner feeling and outer reality — between [[yorick|Yorick]]'s need for quiet reflection and the relentless forward motion of travel itself.

**Mechanical Competence**: Despite (or because of) his emotional opacity, the Postilion is fundamentally competent. He manages the horses, fixes the loose shoe, and delivers the traveller to his destinations. Sterne offers no judgment on his character beyond the comic juxtaposition of his professional efficiency with [[yorick|Yorick]]'s psychological volatility.
