# Eliza Draper

**Eliza Draper** (1744–1778) is the unnamed but unmistakable beloved who haunts Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). She is never named in the novel's main text, but Sterne's biographers and his published *Letters to Eliza* confirm her identity: Elizabeth Draper, the young wife of a Bombay East India Company official, with whom Sterne conducted an intense romantic friendship in the year before his death. Within the novel, she is the absent woman whose picture [[yorick|Yorick]] wears against his breast, whose "pure taper" rekindles his sentimental flame, and whose memory is the final measure against which all other women in the Journey are set.

## 书中出现

Eliza appears in the novel not as a character but as a presence — invoked in apostrophe, recalled in moments of emotional intensity, and set in implicit comparison with every woman [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters. Her appearances form a kind of emotional bass line beneath the novel's surface narrative.

### The Picture

In the novel's opening pages, [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects on what he would have lost had the King of France seized his belongings: "even the little picture which I have so long worn, and so often have told thee, Eliza, I would carry with me into my grave, would have been torn from my neck! — Ungenerous! to seize upon the wreck of an unwary passenger." The miniature portrait worn against the skin establishes Eliza as the novel's tutelary spirit — physically absent but perpetually present.

### The Pure Taper

After the disappointment of an earlier love, [[yorick|Yorick]] confesses that he "lighted it up afresh at the pure taper of Eliza but about three months before, — swearing, as I did it, that it should last me through the whole journey" (027-036). The image of the taper — a slim candle giving a steady, pure flame — captures Eliza's role in the novel's emotional economy. She is not the journey's destination but its enabling condition: the flame that makes sentimental perception possible.

### Brussels and Heaven

In a passage of extraordinary intensity, [[yorick|Yorick]] swears "that I would not travel to Brussels, unless Eliza went along with me, did the road lead me towards heaven!" The conditional is characteristic — Eliza's presence transforms any journey, even the journey towards death, into something worth undertaking.

### The Letter and the Fly

In one of the novel's most self-reflexive moments, [[yorick|Yorick]] interrupts his narrative to note: "I wrote a letter to Eliza." The letter momentarily displaces the Journey itself — the act of writing to the beloved takes precedence over writing for the reader.

### The Final Comparison

In the novel's closing chapter, comparing the disordered Maria with his own heart, [[yorick|Yorick]] makes explicit what has been implicit throughout: "could the traces be ever worn out of her brain, and those of Eliza out of mine, she should not only eat of my bread and drink of my own cup, but Maria should lie in my bosom, and be unto me as a daughter."

The syntax is revealing. Eliza is not replaceable — even the hypothetical adoption of Maria as a daughter is conditional on Eliza's traces being "worn out" of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s mind. The beloved muse occupies a place no other woman can fill.

## 历史背景

Elizabeth Draper was born in 1744 in India. She married Daniel Draper, an East India Company official, at the age of fourteen. In 1767, she traveled to England for her health and met Sterne, then fifty-three and dying of tuberculosis, at the house of a mutual friend. Their relationship — whether it was consummated is unknown, but it was certainly passionate — produced Sterne's *Journal to Eliza* and a series of letters that are among the most remarkable documents of eighteenth-century sensibility.

Eliza returned to India in April 1767, three months before Sterne began writing *A Sentimental Journey*. The novel is, in a real sense, the aftermath of that separation — a journey undertaken in her absence, sustained by her memory. Sterne died in March 1768, less than a month after the novel's publication. Eliza died ten years later, in 1778, at the age of thirty-four.

## 主题分析

- **The Absent Beloved as Narrative Engine**: Eliza is the novel's structuring absence. She never appears, yet she is everywhere — in the picture against [[yorick|Yorick]]'s breast, in the taper that lights his flame, in the letters that interrupt his narrative, in the final comparison with Maria. Sterne's innovation is to make absence more powerful than presence: Eliza governs the Journey precisely because she is not on it.

- **The Biographical Secret**: By never naming Eliza in the novel's main text — her name appears only in direct address — Sterne creates a gap between public fiction and private knowledge. Contemporary readers who knew of Sterne's relationship with Mrs. Draper would read the Journey differently from those who did not. The novel thus operates on two levels: as a self-contained fiction and as a coded autobiographical document.
