# Truth

Truth in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is rarely straightforward. It is layered, qualified, and often held in deliberate tension with politeness. [[yorick|Yorick]] tells the truth — but not "the whole truth"; he speaks "in honest truth" and then immediately qualifies; he observes "the truth only of the remark" while leaving philosophical solutions untouched.

Sterne's novel does not reject truth but insists that human truth is always partial — shaped by circumstance, softened by courtesy, and revealed more through sentiment than through systematic reasoning.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century inherited a robust correspondence theory of truth from Locke and the empiricists: truth was the agreement of ideas with things. But the culture of politeness complicated this picture. Social life required discretion, tact, and the selective withholding of unwelcome facts.

Rousseau celebrated sincerity as the highest virtue; Chesterfield advised prudence as the highest social art. Sterne, characteristically, occupies the middle ground — neither the naive truth-teller nor the polished dissembler, but the sentimental narrator who negotiates between honesty and benevolence, acknowledging that the two do not always align.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's most revealing treatment of truth occurs early in [[calais|Calais]].

When the lady asks [[yorick|Yorick]] whether he had been conversing with the monk, he reports: "I told him the truth — but I did not tell him the whole truth; for I was as full as much restrained by the appearance and figure of the lady he was talking to." Truth here is not a binary (true/false) but a matter of degree: one can tell the truth without telling everything.

The withholding is not deceit but discretion — the truth trimmed to fit the company.

Sterne extends this epistemology to national character. Comparing the French and English expressions of gallantry, he notes "in honest truth, and upon a more candid revision of the matter, The French expression professes more than it performs." Truth requires "candid revision" — it is an achievement of reflection, not a given of first impressions.

At the novel's theological extreme, truth becomes absolute. The dying narrator invokes "the spirit of thy eternal truth" — a divine truth against which all human partiality is measured. But this absolute truth appears only in prayer; in social life, truth remains provisional, qualified, and irreducibly personal.
