# Character

Character is the great subject of Sterne's observation in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*. [[yorick|Yorick]] travels not to see sights but to read people — their faces, their gestures, their "characters" written in every line of feature and posture. The word appears across the novel in its full eighteenth-century range: as moral quality, as national type, as the unique impress of a life upon a face, and as the literary form of the "Character" sketch.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century inherited from Theophrastus and La Bruyère the tradition of the "Character" — a short prose sketch capturing a moral type. Addison and Steele's *Spectator* had popularized the form, making character-writing a central genre of periodical literature. Meanwhile, Locke's *Essay Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* had challenged the idea of innate character, making it the product of sensation and experience — something written *on* the mind rather than born *in* it. Sterne draws on both traditions: his characters are types and individuals at once, sketched with a few strokes yet alive with contradiction.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The preface announces the importance of character: Sterne's classification of travellers exists "to avoid a confusion of character," and the "fourth class" of Simple [[travel|Travel]]lers are defined by their lack of distinguishing traits. [[yorick|Yorick]] reads faces as texts — [[the-monk|the Franciscan]] monk's "thin, spare form" where "'twas neither elegant nor otherwise, but as character and expression made it so"; the [[calais|Calais]] lady's face that "wore the characters of a widow'd look." He trusts these readings absolutely: "from that single trait I knew his character as perfectly, and could rely upon it as firmly, as if he had served me with fidelity for seven years."

The longest sustained treatment comes in the chapter titled simply "CHARACTER" at [[versailles|Versailles]], where [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects on national types — "I think I can see the precise and distinguishing marks of national characters more in these nonsensical minutiæ than in the most important matters of state." The old French officer who befriends him at the theatre is introduced with: "I love the character, not only because I honour the man whose manners are softened by a profession which makes bad men worse." And when [[yorick|Yorick]] sits down at the peasant family's table "to invest myself in the character as speedily as I could," he shows that character is also performance — a role one chooses to inhabit.
