## CALAIS.

[010-001]This certainly, fair lady, said I,

[010-002]raising her hand up little lightly as I began, must be one of

[010-003]Fortune’s whimsical doings; to take two utter strangers by

[010-004]their hands,—of different sexes, and perhaps from different

[010-005]corners of the globe, and in one moment place them together in

[010-006]such a cordial situation as Friendship herself could scarce have

[010-007]achieved for them, had she projected it for a month. 

[010-008]—And your reflection upon it shows how much, Monsieur,

[010-009]she has embarrassed you by the adventure— 

[010-010]When the situation is what we would wish, nothing is so

[010-011]ill-timed as to hint at the circumstances which make it so: you

[010-012]thank Fortune, continued she—you had reason—the heart

[010-013]knew it, and was satisfied; and who but an English philosopher

[010-014]would have sent notice of it to the brain to reverse the

[010-015]judgment? 

[010-016]In saying this, she disengaged her hand with a look which I thought

[010-017]a sufficient commentary upon the text. 

[010-018]It is a miserable picture which I am going to give of the

[010-019]weakness of my heart, by owning, that it suffered a pain, which

[010-020]worthier occasions could not have inflicted.—I was

[010-021]mortified with the loss of her hand, and the manner in which I

[010-022]had lost it carried neither oil nor wine to the wound: I never

[010-023]felt the pain of a sheepish inferiority so miserably in my

[010-024]life. 

[010-025]The triumphs of a true feminine heart are short upon these

[010-026]discomfitures. In a very few seconds she laid her hand upon

[010-027]the cuff of my coat, in order to finish her reply; so, some way

[010-028]or other, God knows how, I regained my situation. 

[010-029]—She had nothing to add. 

[010-030]I forthwith began to model a different conversation for the

[010-031]lady, thinking from the spirit as well as moral of this, that I

[010-032]had been mistaken in her character; but upon turning her face

[010-033]towards me, the spirit which had animated the reply was

[010-034]fled,—the muscles relaxed, and I beheld the same

[010-035]unprotected look of distress which first won me to her

[010-036]interest:—melancholy! to see such sprightliness the prey of

[010-037]sorrow,—I pitied her from my soul; and though it may seem

[010-038]ridiculous enough to a torpid heart,—I could have taken her

[010-039]into my arms, and cherished her, though it was in the open

[010-040]street, without blushing. 

[010-041]The pulsations of the arteries along my fingers pressing

[010-042]across hers, told her what was passing within me: she looked

[010-043]down—a silence of some moments followed. 

[010-044]I fear in this interval, I must have made some slight efforts

[010-045]towards a closer compression of her hand, from a subtle sensation

[010-046]I felt in the palm of my own,—not as if she was going to

[010-047]withdraw hers—but as if she thought about it;—and I

[010-048]had infallibly lost it a second time, had not instinct more than

[010-049]reason directed me to the last resource in these

[010-050]dangers,—to hold it loosely, and in a manner as if I was

[010-051]every moment going to release it, of myself; so she let it

[010-052]continue, till Monsieur Dessein returned with the key; and in the

[010-053]mean time I set myself to consider how I should undo the ill

[010-054]impressions which the poor monk’s story, in case he had

[010-055]told it her, must have planted in her breast against me.
