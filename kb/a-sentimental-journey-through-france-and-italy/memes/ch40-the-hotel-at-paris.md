## THE HOTEL AT PARIS.

[040-001]I could not find in my heart to

[040-002]torture La Fleur’s with a serious look upon the subject of

[040-003]my embarrassment, which was the reason I had treated it so

[040-004]cavalierly: and to show him how light it lay upon my mind, I

[040-005]dropt the subject entirely; and whilst he waited upon me at

[040-006]supper, talk’d to him with more than usual gaiety about

[040-007]Paris, and of the Opéra Comique.—La Fleur had been

[040-008]there himself, and had followed me through the streets as far as

[040-009]the bookseller’s shop; but seeing me come out with

[040-010]the young fille de chambre , and that we walk’d down

[040-011]the Quai de Conti together, La Fleur deem’d it unnecessary

[040-012]to follow me a step further;—so making his own reflections

[040-013]upon it, he took a shorter cut,—and got to the hotel in

[040-014]time to be inform’d of the affair of the police against my

[040-015]arrival. 

[040-016]As soon as the honest creature had taken away, and gone down

[040-017]to sup himself, I then began to think a little seriously about my

[040-018]situation.— 

[040-019]—And here, I know, Eugenius, thou wilt smile at the

[040-020]remembrance of a short dialogue which passed betwixt us the

[040-021]moment I was going to set out:—I must tell it here. 

[040-022]Eugenius, knowing that I was as little subject to be

[040-023]overburden’d with money as thought, had drawn me aside to

[040-024]interrogate me how much I had taken care for. Upon telling

[040-025]him the exact sum, Eugenius shook his head, and said it would not

[040-026]do; so pull’d out his purse in order to empty it into

[040-027]mine.—I’ve enough in conscience, Eugenius, said

[040-028]I.—Indeed, Yorick, you have not, replied Eugenius; I know

[040-029]France and Italy better than you.—But you don’t

[040-030]consider, Eugenius, said I, refusing his offer, that before I

[040-031]have been three days in Paris, I shall take care to say or do

[040-032]something or other for which I shall get clapp’d up into

[040-033]the Bastile, and that I shall live there a couple of months

[040-034]entirely at the king of France’s expense.—I beg

[040-035]pardon, said Eugenius drily: really I had forgot that

[040-036]resource. 

[040-037]Now the event I treated gaily came seriously to my door. 

[040-038]Is it folly, or nonchalance, or philosophy, or

[040-039]pertinacity—or what is it in me, that, after all, when La

[040-040]Fleur had gone down stairs, and I was quite alone, I could not

[040-041]bring down my mind to think of it otherwise than I had then

[040-042]spoken of it to Eugenius? 

[040-043]—And as for the Bastile; the terror is in the

[040-044]word.—Make the most of it you can, said I to myself, the

[040-045]Bastile is but another word for a tower;—and a tower is but

[040-046]another word for a house you can’t get out of.—Mercy

[040-047]on the gouty! for they are in it twice a year.—But with

[040-048]nine livres a day, and pen and ink, and paper, and patience,

[040-049]albeit a man can’t get out, he may do very well

[040-050]within,—at least for a month or six weeks; at the end of

[040-051]which, if he is a harmless fellow, his innocence appears, and he

[040-052]comes out a better and wiser man than he went in. 

[040-053]I had some occasion (I forget what) to step into the

[040-054]court-yard, as I settled this account; and remember I

[040-055]walk’d down stairs in no small triumph with the conceit of

[040-056]my reasoning.—Beshrew the sombre pencil! said I,

[040-057]vauntingly—for I envy not its powers, which paints the

[040-058]evils of life with so hard and deadly a colouring. The mind

[040-059]sits terrified at the objects she has magnified herself, and

[040-060]blackened: reduce them to their proper size and hue, she

[040-061]overlooks them.—’Tis true, said I, correcting the

[040-062]proposition,—the Bastile is not an evil to be

[040-063]despised;—but strip it of its towers—fill up the

[040-064]fosse,—unbarricade the doors—call it simply a

[040-065]confinement, and suppose ’tis some tyrant of a

[040-066]distemper—and not of a man, which holds you in

[040-067]it,—the evil vanishes, and you bear the other half without

[040-068]complaint. 

[040-069]I was interrupted in the heyday of this soliloquy, with a

[040-070]voice which I took to be of a child, which complained “it

[040-071]could not get out.”—I look’d up and down the

[040-072]passage, and seeing neither man, woman, nor child, I went out

[040-073]without farther attention. 

[040-074]In my return back through the passage, I heard the same words

[040-075]repeated twice over; and, looking up, I saw it was a starling

[040-076]hung in a little cage.—“I can’t get

[040-077]out,—I can’t get out,” said the starling. 

[040-078]I stood looking at the bird: and to every person who came

[040-079]through the passage it ran fluttering to the side towards which

[040-080]they approach’d it, with the same lamentation of its

[040-081]captivity. “I can’t get out,” said the

[040-082]starling.—God help thee! said I, but I’ll let thee

[040-083]out, cost what it will; so I turned about the cage to get to the

[040-084]door: it was twisted and double twisted so fast with wire, there

[040-085]was no getting it open without pulling the cage to

[040-086]pieces.—I took both hands to it. 

[040-087]The bird flew to the place where I was attempting his

[040-088]deliverance, and thrusting his head through the trellis pressed

[040-089]his breast against it as if impatient.—I fear, poor

[040-090]creature! said I, I cannot set thee at

[040-091]liberty.—“No,” said the starling,—

[040-092]“I can’t get out—I can’t get out,”

[040-093]said the starling. 

[040-094]I vow I never had my affections more tenderly awakened; nor do I

[040-095]remember an incident in my life, where the dissipated spirits, to

[040-096]which my reason had been a bubble, were so suddenly call’d

[040-097]home. Mechanical as the notes were, yet so true in tune to

[040-098]nature were they chanted, that in one moment they overthrew all

[040-099]my systematic reasonings upon the Bastile; and I heavily walked

[040-100]upstairs, unsaying every word I had said in going down them. 

[040-101]Disguise thyself as thou wilt, still, Slavery! said

[040-102]I,—still thou art a bitter draught! and though thousands in

[040-103]all ages have been made to drink of thee, thou art no less bitter

[040-104]on that account.—’Tis thou, thrice sweet and gracious

[040-105]goddess, addressing myself to Liberty, whom all in public or in

[040-106]private worship, whose taste is grateful, and ever will be so,

[040-107]till Nature herself shall change.—No tint of words

[040-108]can spot thy snowy mantle, or chymic power turn thy sceptre into

[040-109]iron:—with thee to smile upon him as he eats his crust, the

[040-110]swain is happier than his monarch, from whose court thou art

[040-111]exiled!—Gracious Heaven! cried I, kneeling down upon the

[040-112]last step but one in my ascent, grant me but health, thou great

[040-113]Bestower of it, and give me but this fair goddess as my

[040-114]companion,—and shower down thy mitres, if it seems good

[040-115]unto thy divine providence, upon those heads which are aching for

[040-116]them!
