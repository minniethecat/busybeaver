## MOULINES.

[063-001]I never felt what the distress of

[063-002]plenty was in any one shape till now,—to travel it through

[063-003]the Bourbonnois, the sweetest part of France,—in the heyday

[063-004]of the vintage, when Nature is pouring her abundance into every

[063-005]one’s lap, and every eye is lifted up,—a journey,

[063-006]through each step of which Music beats time to Labour , and

[063-007]all her children are rejoicing as they carry in their clusters:

[063-008]to pass through this with my affections flying out, and kindling

[063-009]at every group before me,—and every one of them was

[063-010]pregnant with adventures.— 

[063-011]Just heaven!—it would fill up twenty volumes;—and

[063-012]alas! I have but a few small pages left of this to crowd it

[063-013]into,—and half of these must be taken up with the poor

[063-014]Maria my friend, Mr. Shandy, met with near Moulines. 

[063-015]The story he had told of that disordered maid affected me not

[063-016]a little in the reading; but when I got within the neighbourhood

[063-017]where she lived, it returned so strong into the mind, that I

[063-018]could not resist an impulse which prompted me to go half a league

[063-019]out of the road, to the village where her parents dwelt, to

[063-020]enquire after her. 

[063-021]’Tis going, I own, like the Knight of the Woeful

[063-022]Countenance in quest of melancholy adventures. But I know

[063-023]not how it is, but I am never so perfectly conscious of the

[063-024]existence of a soul within me, as when I am entangled in

[063-025]them. 

[063-026]The old mother came to the door; her looks told me the story

[063-027]before she open’d her mouth.—She had lost her

[063-028]husband; he had died, she said, of anguish, for the loss of

[063-029]Maria’s senses, about a month before.—She had feared

[063-030]at first, she added, that it would have plunder’d her poor

[063-031]girl of what little understanding was left;—but, on the

[063-032]contrary, it had brought her more to herself:—still, she

[063-033]could not rest.—Her poor daughter, she said, crying, was

[063-034]wandering somewhere about the road. 

[063-035]Why does my pulse beat languid as I write this? and what made

[063-036]La Fleur, whose heart seem’d only to be tuned to joy, to

[063-037]pass the back of his hand twice across his eyes, as the woman

[063-038]stood and told it? I beckoned to the postilion to turn back

[063-039]into the road. 

[063-040]When we had got within half a league of Moulines, at a little

[063-041]opening in the road leading to a thicket, I discovered poor Maria

[063-042]sitting under a poplar. She was sitting with her elbow in

[063-043]her lap, and her head leaning on one side within her

[063-044]hand:—a small brook ran at the foot of the tree. 

[063-045]I bid the postilion go on with the chaise to

[063-046]Moulines—and La Fleur to bespeak my supper;—and that

[063-047]I would walk after him. 

[063-048]She was dress’d in white, and much as my friend

[063-049]described her, except that her hair hung loose, which before was

[063-050]twisted within a silk net.—She had superadded likewise to

[063-051]her jacket, a pale green riband, which fell across her shoulder

[063-052]to the waist; at the end of which hung her pipe.—Her goat

[063-053]had been as faithless as her lover; and she had got a little dog

[063-054]in lieu of him, which she had kept tied by a string to her

[063-055]girdle: as I looked at her dog, she drew him towards her with the

[063-056]string.—“Thou shalt not leave me, Sylvio,” said

[063-057]she. I look’d in Maria’s eyes and saw she was

[063-058]thinking more of her father than of her lover, or her little

[063-059]goat; for, as she utter’d them, the tears trickled down her

[063-060]cheeks. 

[063-061]I sat down close by her; and Maria let me wipe them away as

[063-062]they fell, with my handkerchief.—I then steep’d it in

[063-063]my own,—and then in hers,—and then in mine,—and

[063-064]then I wip’d hers again;—and as I did it, I felt such

[063-065]undescribable emotions within me, as I am sure could not be

[063-066]accounted for from any combinations of matter and motion. 

[063-067]I am positive I have a soul; nor can all the books with which

[063-068]materialists have pester’d the world ever convince me to

[063-069]the contrary.
