## VERSAILLES.

[048-001]I could not conceive why the Count

[048-002]de B— had gone so abruptly out of the room, any more than I

[048-003]could conceive why he had put the Shakespeare into his

[048-004]pocket.— Mysteries which must explain themselves are not

[048-005]worth the loss of time which a conjecture about them takes

[048-006]up : ’twas better to read Shakespeare; so taking up

[048-007]“ Much Ado About Nothing ,” I transported myself

[048-008]instantly from the chair I sat in to Messina in Sicily, and got

[048-009]so busy with Don Pedro, and Benedict, and Beatrice, that I

[048-010]thought not of Versailles, the Count, or the passport. 

[048-011]Sweet pliability of man’s spirit, that can at once

[048-012]surrender itself to illusions, which cheat expectation and sorrow

[048-013]of their weary moments!—Long,—long since had ye

[048-014]number’d out my days, had I not trod so great a part of

[048-015]them upon this enchanted ground. When my way is too rough

[048-016]for my feet, or too steep for my strength, I get off it, to some

[048-017]smooth velvet path, which Fancy has scattered over with rosebuds

[048-018]of delights; and having taken a few turns in it, come back

[048-019]strengthened and refresh’d.—When evils press sore

[048-020]upon me, and there is no retreat from them in this world, then I

[048-021]take a new course;—I leave it,—and as I have a

[048-022]clearer idea of the Elysian fields than I have of heaven, I force

[048-023]myself, like Æneas, into them.—I see him meet the

[048-024]pensive shade of his forsaken Dido, and wish to recognise

[048-025]it;—I see the injured spirit wave her head, and turn off

[048-026]silent from the author of her miseries and dishonours;—I

[048-027]lose the feelings for myself in hers, and in those affections

[048-028]which were wont to make me mourn for her when I was at

[048-029]school. 

[048-030]Surely this is not walking in a vain shadow—nor does

[048-031]man disquiet himself in vain by it :—he oftener

[048-032]does so in trusting the issue of his commotions to reason

[048-033]only.—I can safely say for myself, I was never able to

[048-034]conquer any one single bad sensation in my heart so decisively,

[048-035]as beating up as fast as I could for some kindly and gentle

[048-036]sensation to fight it upon its own ground. 

[048-037]When I had got to the end of the third act the Count de

[048-038]B— entered, with my passport in his hand. Monsieur le

[048-039]Duc de C—, said the Count, is as good a prophet, I dare

[048-040]say, as he is a statesman. Un homme qui rit , said

[048-041]the Duke, ne sera jamais dangereux .—Had it been for

[048-042]any one but the king’s jester, added the Count, I could not

[048-043]have got it these two hours.— Pardonnez moi , Monsieur

[048-044]le Count, said I—I am not the king’s

[048-045]jester.—But you are Yorick?—Yes.— Et

[048-046]vous plaisantez ?—I answered, Indeed I did

[048-047]jest,—but was not paid for it;—’twas entirely

[048-048]at my own expense. 

[048-049]We have no jester at court, Monsieur le Count, said I; the

[048-050]last we had was in the licentious reign of Charles

[048-051]II.;—since which time our manners have been so gradually

[048-052]refining, that our court at present is so full of patriots, who

[048-053]wish for nothing but the honours and wealth of their

[048-054]country;—and our ladies are all so chaste, so spotless, so

[048-055]good, so devout,—there is nothing for a jester to make a

[048-056]jest of.— 

[048-057]Voilà un persiflage ! cried the Count.
