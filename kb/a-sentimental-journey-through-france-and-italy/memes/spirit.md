# Spirit

Spirit in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* names the invisible quality that animates conduct — a person's characteristic temper as it manifests in gesture, speech, and the capacity for sympathy.

Unlike the theological soul, which looks toward eternity, spirit in Sterne's usage is embodied and social: it can be "regulated" or "courteous," "gentle" or "injured." It is what [[yorick|Yorick]] reads in a face, what the monk's horn box calls up as a moral reminder, and what the peasants' dance at the novel's close reveals as an "elevation" beyond mere entertainment.

## 定义与历史语境

Eighteenth-century thought inherited multiple senses of "spirit." In classical and Christian philosophy, spirit (pneuma, spiritus) was the divine principle animating matter. In the empirical psychology of Locke and Hume, "spirits" were the animal spirits — fine fluids moving through the nerves, mediating between body and mind.

In the culture of politeness, "spirit" denoted a person's characteristic disposition or temper — what we would now call personality. Sterne moves fluidly among all three registers, but his distinctive contribution is to make spirit visible: it is always embodied in conduct, never merely asserted.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's most sustained meditation on spirit comes through [[the-monk|the monk of Calais]], whose "courteous spirit" [[yorick|Yorick]] invokes as a moral touchstone: "oft and many a time have I called up by it the courteous spirit of its owner to regulate my own, in the justlings of the world." The monk's horn box becomes a material conduit for spirit — an object that transmits moral quality across time and distance.

Spirit also governs the social encounters that structure the journey. When [[yorick|Yorick]] misreads a lady's character, he notes "the spirit which had animated the reply was fled — the muscles relaxed" — spirit here being the transient animation that makes a face legible. At the theater of human types in [[paris|Paris]], [[yorick|Yorick]] apostrophizes the spirit of a departed friend.

And at the novel's close, the peasant dance reveals "an elevation of spirit different from that which is the cause or the effect of simple jollity" — spirit raised beyond the ordinary, approaching the sacred.

The "sweet pliability of man's spirit" — "that can at once surrender itself to illusions, which cheat expectation and sorrow" — captures Sterne's anthropology in a phrase: the spirit's capacity for self-deception is also its capacity for happiness.
