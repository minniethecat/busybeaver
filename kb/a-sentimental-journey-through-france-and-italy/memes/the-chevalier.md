# The Chevalier de St. Louis

**The Chevalier de St. Louis** is a minor but memorable character in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), appearing in the [[the-passport|Passport]] chapter at [[paris|Paris]]. A decorated former officer fallen into poverty after his regiment was disbanded, the Chevalier supports himself and his wife by selling pâtés on the street — a reversal of fortune that so moves [[yorick|Yorick]] that he places the Chevalier's story immediately before [[the-sword|The Sword]] chapter, noting that "the two stories reflect light upon each other — and 'tis a pity they should be parted."

## 概要

[[yorick|Yorick]] encounters the Chevalier while searching for the Count de B—'s hotel in [[paris|Paris]]. He sends his servant [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] to ask a man standing with a basket for directions. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] returns "a little pale" and reports that the man is "a Chevalier de St. Louis selling pâtés."

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s first reaction is disbelief: "It is impossible, La Fleur." But [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] persists — he has seen the croix set in gold with its red riband tied to the man's buttonhole, and has looked into the basket and seen the pâtés. The detail of the decoration — the Cross of St. Louis, a prestigious military order — contrasted with the humble basket of pastries creates the chapter's central image of reversed fortune.

"Such a reverse in man's life awakens a better principle than curiosity," [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects. He leaves his carriage and approaches the man.

## The Chevalier's Appearance and Manner

Sterne paints the Chevalier with the same careful attention to dress and deportment he gives to the monk and other figures of quiet dignity. The Chevalier wears "a clean linen apron which fell below his knees, and with a sort of a bib that went half way up his breast; upon the top of this, but a little below the hem, hung his croix" (044-035). His basket is covered with a white damask napkin, and "there was a look of propreté and neatness throughout, that one might have bought his pâtés of him, as much from appetite as sentiment."

He does not solicit customers — "he made an offer of them to neither; but stood still with them at the corner of an hotel, for those to buy who chose it without solicitation." He is about forty-eight, "of a sedate look, something approaching to gravity."

## The Chevalier's Story

[[yorick|Yorick]], approaching "rather to the basket than him," lifts the napkin and takes a pâté, then asks the Chevalier to explain his circumstances.

The Chevalier tells his story "in a few words": the best part of his life had been spent in military service, in which "after spending a small patrimony, he had obtained a company and the croix with it." But at the conclusion of the last peace, his regiment was reformed — disbanded — "and the whole corps, with those of some other regiments, left without any provision."

He found himself "in a wide world without friends, without a livre, — and indeed, without anything but this" — pointing to his croix.

The story wins [[yorick|Yorick]]'s pity, and the Chevalier "finished the scene with winning my esteem too." He speaks of the king as "the most generous of princes" whose generosity "could neither relieve nor reward everyone," and counts himself simply among the unfortunate. He has "a little wife, whom he loved, who did the pâtisserie," and he feels "no dishonour in defending her and himself from want in this way — unless Providence had offer'd him a better."

## The Royal Pension

Sterne cannot resist providing a happy ending. About nine months after [[yorick|Yorick]]'s encounter, the Chevalier's fortunes change. He had taken his usual stand near the iron gates leading to the palace, and "as his croix had caught the eyes of numbers, numbers had made the same enquiry" that [[yorick|Yorick]] had made. The Chevalier told them the same story, "always with so much modesty and good sense."

The story reached the king's ears. Hearing that the Chevalier "had been a gallant officer, and respected by the whole regiment as a man of honour and integrity," the king "broke up his little trade by a pension of fifteen hundred livres a year" (044-079).

## 主题分析

- **Dignity in Reversal**: The Chevalier is a study in the preservation of dignity through the worst reversals of fortune. His clean apron, his damask napkin, his refusal to solicit — these are not merely details of dress but markers of character. He has lost everything but his self-respect, and he protects that with the same care he gives to his basket of pâtés.

- **The Paired Stories**: Sterne explicitly links the Chevalier's story to [[the-sword|The Sword]] that follows immediately: "the two stories reflect light upon each other, — and 'tis a pity they should be parted" (044-082). Both concern French noblemen brought low — the Chevalier by the peace that disbanded his regiment, the Marquis d'E— by ancestral indiscretions. Both respond with dignity rather than complaint. But where the Marquis reclaims his sword in a public ceremony, the Chevalier's restoration is quieter: a pension granted by a king who heard of his modesty through others.

- **The Sentimental Economics of Pâtés**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s observation that "one might have bought his pâtés of him, as much from appetite as sentiment" captures the novel's fusion of material and emotional economies. The pâtés are both commodity and symbol — objects of commerce that carry the story of the man who sells them. To buy a pâté from the Chevalier is to participate in his dignity, not merely to purchase a pastry.
