# Pity

Pity in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the moral engine of the sentimental mode. More active than mere sympathy and more tender than abstract benevolence, pity is the emotion that "wrung" [[yorick|Yorick]]'s heart with "fellow-feeling" — the affective bridge between strangers that makes moral action possible without the mediation of reason or duty.

## 定义与历史语境

Eighteenth-century moral philosophy was deeply occupied with pity's ethical status. Rousseau elevated *pitié* as the natural sentiment that preceded all reflection and made society possible; Adam Smith analyzed sympathy as the foundation of moral judgment; Hume identified pity as a passion that could be cultivated into virtue. Sterne's novel participates in this conversation by dramatizing pity as a lived experience — not a philosophical abstraction but a bodily and emotional event, "the only dangerous thing in the world" because it compels action against self-interest.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Pity makes its most concentrated appearance in the Monk episode and its reverberations through the novel. When [[yorick|Yorick]] refuses [[the-monk|the Franciscan]]'s request for alms, he commits what the novel presents as a sentimental sin — not cruelty but a failure of pity, a hardening of the heart that he spends the remainder of the journey attempting to repair. "I pitied the man," he says afterward, and the pity he failed to show becomes the standard against which subsequent encounters are measured.

The novel consistently pairs pity with danger: "which would have made pity the only dangerous thing in the world." This is Sterne's insight — true pity is costly. It opens the self to the claims of others and threatens the comfortable detachment of the traveler. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s education in pity is the novel's deepest moral arc.

## 关键段落

> "the true point of pity is, as they can be earn'd in the world." (The Monk episode)

> "I beg the world not to smile, but to pity me." (Calais chapters)

> "which would have made pity the only dangerous thing in the world." (Montreuil chapters)

> "I pity the man who can travel from Dan to Beersheba, and cry, 'Tis all barren." (The Snuffbox episode)

> "hand nor his left, lest Love or Pity should seduce him out of his road." (The Dwarf episode)

> "But here my heart is wrung with pity and fellow-feeling." (Nampont chapters, the Dead Ass)

> "I heartily pity them; they have brought up no faculties for this work." (The Supper episode)
