# Grace

Grace in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is a word Sterne uses in its richest double sense: the theological gift of divine favor, and the aesthetic quality of elegance in conduct and feeling. The novel moves between these meanings with characteristic ease, suggesting that earthly grace — the grace of a gesture, a bow, a kind word — is the visible trace of a higher grace at work in human affairs.

## 定义与历史语境

The double meaning of grace — sacred and secular — was particularly charged in the eighteenth century. In Christian theology, grace was unmerited divine favor, the foundation of salvation. In the language of manners and aesthetics, grace denoted ease, elegance, and natural refinement — qualities celebrated by Castiglione in *The Courtier* and absorbed into eighteenth-century ideals of polite conduct. Sterne's genius was to fuse the two, making the aesthetic grace of his characters a sign of their spiritual condition.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's most concentrated treatment of grace appears in the chapter titled "[[the-supper|The Grace]]," where [[yorick|Yorick]] describes a supper among peasants. The grace spoken before the meal becomes the occasion for a reflection that transforms a simple ritual into an emblem of cosmic harmony — "[[religion|Religion]] mixing in the dance." The supper's grace, in Sterne's rendering, is both a prayer and a performance, both sacred invocation and social art.

Elsewhere, grace appears as an attribute of character: the "simple grace" of a gesture, the "grace which followed" a meal, the grace with which a stranger offers assistance. In every instance, Sterne's prose traces the movement from outward form to inward quality.

## 关键段落

> "If the supper was to my taste,—the grace which followed it was much more so." (The Supper chapter)

> "In a word, I thought I beheld Religion mixing in the dance:—but, as I had never seen her so engaged, I kept my eye upon her — 'twas grace —" (The Grace chapter)

> "She did it with so simple a grace,—and such an air of modesty." (Calais chapters)

> "How smooth do ye make the road of it! like grace and beauty, ye pave the way before us." (Paris chapters)
