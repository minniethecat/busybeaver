## PARIS.

[039-001]When I got home to my hotel, La

[039-002]Fleur told me I had been enquired after by the Lieutenant de

[039-003]Police.—The deuce take it! said I,—I know the

[039-004]reason. It is time the reader should know it, for in the

[039-005]order of things in which it happened, it was omitted: not that it

[039-006]was out of my head; but that had I told it then it might have

[039-007]been forgotten now;—and now is the time I want it. 

[039-008]I had left London with so much precipitation, that it never

[039-009]enter’d my mind that we were at war with France; and had

[039-010]reached Dover, and looked through my glass at the hills beyond

[039-011]Boulogne, before the idea presented itself; and with this in its

[039-012]train, that there was no getting there without a passport.

[039-013]Go but to the end of a street, I have a mortal aversion for

[039-014]returning back no wiser than I set out; and as this was one of

[039-015]the greatest efforts I had ever made for knowledge, I could less

[039-016]bear the thoughts of it: so hearing the Count de —— had hired

[039-017]the packet, I begg’d he would take me in his suite.

[039-018]The Count had some little knowledge of me, so made little or no

[039-019]difficulty,—only said, his inclination to serve me could

[039-020]reach no farther than Calais, as he was to return by way of

[039-021]Brussels to Paris; however, when I had once pass’d there, I

[039-022]might get to Paris without interruption; but that in Paris I must

[039-023]make friends and shift for myself.—Let me get to Paris,

[039-024]Monsieur le Count, said I,—and I shall do very well.

[039-025]So I embark’d, and never thought more of the matter. 

[039-026]When La Fleur told me the Lieutenant de Police had been

[039-027]enquiring after me,—the thing instantly recurred;—and

[039-028]by the time La Fleur had well told me, the master of the hotel

[039-029]came into my room to tell me the same thing, with this addition

[039-030]to it, that my passport had been particularly asked

[039-031]after: the master of the hotel concluded with saying, He hoped I

[039-032]had one.—Not I, faith! said I. 

[039-033]The master of the hotel retired three steps from me, as from

[039-034]an infected person, as I declared this;—and poor La Fleur

[039-035]advanced three steps towards me, and with that sort of movement

[039-036]which a good soul makes to succour a distress’d

[039-037]one:—the fellow won my heart by it; and from that single

[039-038]trait I knew his character as perfectly, and could rely upon it

[039-039]as firmly, as if he had served me with fidelity for seven

[039-040]years. 

[039-041]Mon seigneur ! cried the master of the hotel; but

[039-042]recollecting himself as he made the exclamation, he instantly

[039-043]changed the tone of it.—If Monsieur, said he, has not a

[039-044]passport ( apparemment ) in all likelihood he has friends in

[039-045]Paris who can procure him one.—Not that I know of, quoth I,

[039-046]with an air of indifference.—Then certes , replied

[039-047]he, you’ll be sent to the Bastile or the Chatelet au

[039-048]moins .—Poo! said I, the King of France is a good

[039-049]natur’d soul:—he’ll hurt nobody.— Cela

[039-050]n’empêche pas , said he—you will certainly

[039-051]be sent to the Bastile to-morrow morning.—But I’ve

[039-052]taken your lodgings for a month, answer’d I, and I’ll

[039-053]not quit them a day before the time for all the kings of France

[039-054]in the world. La Fleur whispered in my ear, That nobody

[039-055]could oppose the king of France. 

[039-056]Pardi ! said my host, ces Messieurs Anglois sont des

[039-057]gens très extraordinaires ;—and, having both said

[039-058]and sworn it,—he went out.
