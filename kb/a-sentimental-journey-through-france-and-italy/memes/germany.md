# Germany

**Germany** appears in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) in two distinct contexts: first, as the homeland of the grieving mourner encountered at [[nampont|Nampont]]; second, as a segment of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s imagined return route from [[italy|Italy]].

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

Germany enters the novel first through the story of the poor mourner at [[nampont|Nampont]]. The man tells the assembled crowd that heaven had blessed him "with three sons, the finest lads in Germany" (Ch. 27, [[the-dead-ass|The Dead Ass]]). The phrasing is deliberate and affectionate — the sons are not merely "German" but "the finest lads in Germany," a paternal pride that makes the subsequent losses unbearably poignant. Two of the three sons die of smallpox within a week; the youngest falls ill, and it is to save this last child that the mourner makes his vow to walk to [[spain|St. Iago]] in [[spain|Spain]].

The mourner's home is in Franconia, the border region of the German lands from which he has walked all the way to [[spain|Spain]] and back. His journey — Franconia to [[spain|Santiago de Compostela]], and homeward again through [[france|France]] — spans the breadth of western Europe. When his ass dies at [[nampont|Nampont]], he is near the end of that immense circuit. Germany, in this reading, is the mourner's point of origin and his intended point of return: a homeland he may never reach.

The second mention is geographical rather than emotional. [[yorick|Yorick]], having resolved to meet [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]] at [[brussels|Brussels]], calculates the detour: "'tis only returning from [[italy|Italy]] through Germany to Holland, by the route of [[flanders|Flanders]], home" (Ch. 66, The [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]]). Here Germany is a transit space — a region to be passed through, not experienced. The speed of the phrase — "through Germany to Holland, by the route of [[flanders|Flanders]], home" — mimics the mental shorthand of a traveller plotting his return. Germany is a corridor, not a destination; a space of passage rather than of encounter.

These two Germanys — the mourner's homeland and [[yorick|Yorick]]'s transit corridor — mirror the novel's larger geography of presence and absence. The mourner's Germany is a place of family and attachment, left behind and yearned for; [[yorick|Yorick]]'s Germany is a name on a mental map, contemplated but never visited. Neither man is in Germany during the novel's action — it exists only in memory and intention.
