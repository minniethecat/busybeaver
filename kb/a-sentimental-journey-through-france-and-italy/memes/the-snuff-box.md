# The Snuff Box

**The Snuff Box** episode is a pivotal scene of reconciliation in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occurring in chapter 19 at [[calais|Calais]]. Having earlier refused charity to a poor Franciscan monk and delivered a harsh sermon on the difference between those who labour and those who beg, [[yorick|Yorick]] now encounters the same monk again — and the exchange of snuff boxes becomes the instrument of their mutual forgiveness. The episode is one of the novel's most celebrated set-pieces of sentimental morality.

## 概要

The chapter opens with [[yorick|Yorick]] and the lady from the Remise still together when "the good old monk was within six paces of us, as the idea of him crossed my mind." The monk advances "with a world of frankness" and, "having a horn snuff box in his hand, he presented it open to me" (011-006).

[[yorick|Yorick]] responds with an impulse of generosity: "You shall taste mine — said I, pulling out my box (which was a small tortoise one) and putting it into his hand." When the monk pronounces the snuff excellent, [[yorick|Yorick]] offers the entire box: "Then do me the favour, I replied, to accept of the box and all, and when you take a pinch out of it, sometimes recollect it was the peace offering of a man who once used you unkindly, but not from his heart."

The monk blushes "as red as scarlet" and protests that [[yorick|Yorick]] never used him unkindly. The lady joins in defence of [[yorick|Yorick]] — and a strange, pleasurable contention ensues: "I knew not that contention could be rendered so sweet and pleasurable a thing to the nerves as I then felt it." The trio fall silent, the monk calmly rubbing his horn box on his sleeve "till it had acquired a little air of brightness by the friction."

## The Exchange

The monk breaks the silence by proposing they exchange boxes. "In saying this, he presented his to me with one hand, as he took mine from me in the other, and having kissed it, — with a stream of good nature in his eyes, he put it into his bosom, — and took his leave."

The horn snuff box becomes one of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s most treasured possessions. He guards it "as I would the instrumental parts of my religion, to help my mind on to something better: in truth, I seldom go abroad without it; and oft and many a time have I called up by it the courteous spirit of its owner to regulate my own, in the justlings of the world."

## The Monk's Story

Sterne provides a brief but poignant backstory for the monk, whose history explains the depth of his gentleness. The monk's courteous spirit "had found full employment for his" till about his forty-fifth year, "when upon some military services ill requited, and meeting at the same time with a disappointment in the tenderest of passions, he abandoned the sword and the sex together (011-049), and took sanctuary not so much in his convent as in himself."

This passage — a miniature biography in two clauses — reveals that the monk, before taking orders, was a soldier and a lover, whose withdrawal from the world was not a flight from life but a retreat into interior peace.

## The Monk's Death

In one of the novel's most affecting passages, [[yorick|Yorick]] records a damp upon his spirits: "in my last return through [[calais|Calais]], upon enquiring after Father Lorenzo, I heard he had been dead near three months, and was buried, not in his convent, but, according to his desire, in a little cemetery belonging to it, about two leagues off."

[[yorick|Yorick]] visits the grave. "I had a strong desire to see where they had laid him, — when, upon pulling out his little horn box, as I sat by his grave, and plucking up a nettle or two at the head of it, which had no business to grow there, they all struck together so forcibly upon my affections, that I burst into a flood of tears."

The sentence collapses in self-correction: "— but I am as weak as a woman; and I beg the world not to smile, but to pity me."

## 主题分析

- **The Material Object as Moral Instrument**: The snuff box is the novel's supreme example of a material object functioning as a conduit of sentiment. It is not valuable — a simple horn box — but it becomes priceless through the transaction of forgiveness it embodies. [[yorick|Yorick]] treats it as a religious relic, calling up "the courteous spirit of its owner" whenever he takes a pinch. The object bridges the gap between physical gesture and moral transformation.

- **Reconciliation Without Confession**: The exchange achieves reconciliation without either party explicitly confessing fault. [[yorick|Yorick]] calls his box a "peace offering," the monk blushes and protests, the lady mediates — and the exchange of boxes resolves what words cannot. It is Sterne's demonstration that sentiment operates through gesture and symbol, not through argument or formal apology.

- **The Grave as Sentimental Test Site**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s visit to the monk's grave, where the horn box triggers a flood of tears, is a classic scene of eighteenth-century sensibility — the grave, the nettles that "had no business to grow there," the tangible relic that brings the dead man vividly to mind. The self-mocking coda ("I am as weak as a woman") is characteristically Sternean: the feeling is real, but the self-awareness is never suspended.
