# Madame de V—

**Madame de V—** is a [[paris|Paris]]ian woman of fashion whom [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters in the later chapters of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). She is at a threshold moment in the "three epochas" of a French woman's life — passing from coquetry toward deism — when [[yorick|Yorick]] intercepts her with a blend of gallantry and religious argument that, by his own account, postpones her deist phase by two years and earns him a place in her *coterie*. (048-001)

## Role Overview

Madame de V— appears in a sequence of [[paris|Paris]]ian salon episodes where [[yorick|Yorick]] moves through the city's intellectual circles. She is the most fully dramatized of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s religious disputants — a woman whose scepticism is treated not as a philosophical position to be refuted with logic, but as an emotional condition to be cured with sentiment. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s "unperverting" of her is the novel's most explicit demonstration of sentimentalism's reach into the domain of religious belief.

Unlike [[madame-de-rambouliet|Madame de Rambouliet]], whose encounter turns on a single bodily act, Madame de V— provides the occasion for an extended dialogue on faith, beauty, and the insufficiency of reason without feeling.

## Appearances

### The Three Epochas

Sterne introduces Madame de V— through a sociological observation about French women's life cycles:

> "There are three epochas in the empire of a French woman. — She is coquette, — then deist, — then *dévote*: the empire during these is never lost, — she only changes her subjects when thirty-five years and more have unpeopled her dominion of the slaves of love, she re-peoples it with slaves of infidelity, — and then with the slaves of the church." (048-002)

Madame de V— is situated precisely at the transition:

> "Madame de V— was vibrating betwixt the first of those epochas: the colour of the rose was fading fast away; — she ought to have been a deist five years before the time I had the honour to pay my first visit." (048-003)

The image of "vibrating" is quintessential Sterne — she is in a state of emotional and intellectual suspension, neither fully coquette nor fully deist, and therefore available for sentimental intervention.

### The Sofa Debate

Madame de V— places [[yorick|Yorick]] beside her on a sofa "for the sake of disputing the point of religion more closely." She tells him she believes nothing. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s reply is not theological but personal:

> "I told Madame de V— it might be her principle, but I was sure it could not be her interest to level the outworks, without which I could not conceive how such a citadel as hers could be defended; — that there was not a more dangerous thing in the world than for a beauty to be a deist." (048-004)

The military metaphor — "outworks," "citadel" — is deliberately playful, but the argument beneath it is serious: religious belief, [[yorick|Yorick]] insists, is not merely true but *useful* to a beautiful woman. It provides the "restraints" that protect her from men — including, he frankly admits, from himself:

> "I had not been five minutes sat upon the sofa beside her, but I had begun to form designs; — and what is it, but the sentiments of religion, and the persuasion they had excited in her breast, which could have check'd them as they rose up?" (048-005)

He takes her hand and expounds on human frailty: "We are not adamant, said I, taking hold of her hand; — and there is need of all restraints, till age in her own time steals in and lays them on us. — But my dear lady, said I, kissing her hand, — 'tis too — too soon." (048-006)

### The Credit of Unperverting

The outcome is reported with characteristic comic self-satisfaction:

> "I declare I had the credit all over Paris of unperverting Madame de V—. — She affirmed to Monsieur D— and the Abbé M—, that in one half hour I had said more for revealed religion, than all their Encyclopædia had said against it." (048-007)

The word "unperverting" is [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own coinage — a verb that does not exist in the language until he needs it. The triumph over the *Encyclopédie* — the great monument of French Enlightenment thought — is presented not as a theological victory but as a social one. [[yorick|Yorick]] is "listed directly into Madame de V—'s *coterie*," and she "put off the epocha of deism for two years." (048-008)

### The Coterie and the Count de Faineant

In Madame de V—'s circle, [[yorick|Yorick]] finds himself discoursing on "the necessity of a first cause" when the young Count de Faineant interrupts to adjust his *solitaire* — a necktie — advising him it should be "*plus badinant*" (more playful). The juxtaposition of cosmological argument with fashion advice is pure Sterne: the salon is a place where the sublime and the trivial share a sofa. (048-009)

## Character and Themes

**[[sentiment|Sentiment]]al Apologetics**: Madame de V—'s conversion is the novel's case study in sentimental religion. [[yorick|Yorick]] does not refute her deism with syllogisms; he appeals to her self-interest as a beautiful woman, her need for protection, her emotional susceptibility. [[religion|Faith]] is recommended not because it is true but because it is useful — and the fact that this argument succeeds is the novel's wry comment on the limits of rationalist unbelief.

**The Gallant Theologian**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s method — theology delivered while holding a woman's hand and kissing it — embodies the fusion of spiritual counsel and erotic gallantry that defines his character. He is at once preacher and seducer, and the ambiguity is deliberate.

**Vibration as State**: The word "vibrating" — applied to Madame de V—'s spiritual condition — echoes the novel's master metaphor of the "great Sensorium of the world." Her soul, like the nerves of a sentimental man, is a sensitive instrument responsive to the right touch. [[yorick|Yorick]] supplies that touch.
