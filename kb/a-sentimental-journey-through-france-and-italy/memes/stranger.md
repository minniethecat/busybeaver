# Stranger

The stranger is the fundamental social unit of *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*. Every encounter in the novel — from [[calais|Calais]] to the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnais]] — is an encounter with a stranger. Sterne treats the stranger not as a threat but as an opportunity: the unknown other is the blank page upon which sentiment can write its claims. The novel's entire moral argument rests on the proposition that strangers deserve our compassion not in spite of their strangeness, but because of it.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century was the great age of cosmopolitanism. Enlightenment thinkers from Montesquieu to Kant argued that moral obligation extended beyond the tribe, the nation, and the familiar. Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral [[sentiment|Sentiment]]s* (1759) built its ethical system on the capacity to sympathize with strangers — people we have never met and whose interests are not our own. At the same time, the Grand Tour threw young Englishmen into daily contact with French and Italian strangers, and the travel narrative became a laboratory for testing cosmopolitan ideals against actual encounters. Sterne's novel belongs to this moment: a sentimental cosmopolitanism tested in the inns, shops, and coaches of [[calais|France]].

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The word "stranger" appears at every crucial turn. The preface defines one class of travellers as those who "cross the seas and sojourn in a land of strangers." At [[calais|Calais]], [[yorick|Yorick]] marvels at how [[fortune|Fortune]] can "take two utter strangers by their hands — of different sexes, and perhaps from different corners of the globe — and in one moment place them together in such a cordial situation." The glove-shop episode turns on the shopkeeper's wife asking: "Do you think I could ask a sous too much of a stranger?" — and the question is rhetorical, for the stranger's vulnerability is precisely what commands her honesty.

In [[paris|Paris]], [[yorick|Yorick]] is repeatedly conscious of being a stranger. The French officer translates his appearance to the theatre audience: "Here's a poor stranger come into the box." Seeking a passport, [[yorick|Yorick]] describes himself as "an absolute stranger, and what is worse... an Englishman too." The secretary at [[versailles|Versailles]] gives him a two-hour wait, and [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects that "a stranger at [[paris|Paris]] should have the opportunities presented to him" — a sly parenthesis on tourism and commerce.

The novel's climax in the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnais]] returns to the stranger theme: "the compassion of a stranger, as he journeyeth on his way, now pours into thy wounds." The stranger's compassion is the novel's highest moral act — unforced, unexpected, and therefore proof of genuine benevolence.
