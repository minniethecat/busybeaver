# The Notary

**The Notary** is a [[paris|Paris]]ian legal official who appears as the central figure in a fragmentary manuscript that [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters in [[paris|Paris]] in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Disputed by his wife, dislodged by wind, and ultimately called upon to record a dying gentleman's extraordinary history, the Notary is a comic study in professional misfortune and the collision of domestic strife with legal solemnity.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Notary enters the novel as a character within a character — he is the subject of a manuscript fragment that [[yorick|Yorick]] reads aloud in his [[paris|Paris]] hotel. The fragment presents him as a man caught between the fury of his wife, the violence of the elements, and the dignity of his profession. His story unfolds in a single [[paris|Paris]] night, moving from a domestic quarrel to a bridge-crossing catastrophe to a somber chamber where he is called to transcribe a stranger's last testament. (058-001)

Sterne uses the Notary to explore the tension between professional formality and the chaos of lived experience. The notary's instruments — his parchment, inkhorn, and pen — are repeatedly juxtaposed against forces they cannot control: a wife's temper, the wind on [[pont-neuf|the Pont Neuf]], and the pathos of a dying man's story.

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Domestic Quarrel

The Notary first appears in the midst of a heated dispute with his wife: "the notary's wife disputed the point with the notary with too much heat." When she rises hastily in anger, the Notary — recognizing "a little fume of a woman" — attempts to defuse the situation with a mild reply: "I would go, answered he, to bed." His wife retorts, "You may go to the devil." With only one bed in the house and "the notary not caring to lie in the same bed with a woman who had but that moment sent him pell mell to the devil," he ventures out into the windy [[paris|Paris]] night. (Ch. 46, [[paris|Paris]])

### The Pont Neuf Incident

Crossing [[pont-neuf|the Pont Neuf]] — which Sterne's narrator interjects to praise as "the noblest, — the finest, — the grandest, — the lightest, — the longest, — the broadest" bridge ever built — the Notary instinctively claps his cane to his hat against the wind. The point of his cane catches the loop of a sentinel's hat and hoists it into the Seine. A boatman catches it, declaring " 'Tis an ill wind which blows nobody any good." The sentry, a Gascon, seizes the Notary's own castor in retaliation, leaving him bareheaded on the bridge. (Ch. 46, [[paris|Paris]])

### The Lamentation

Stripped of his hat, the Notary walks through the Rue de Dauphine and the faubourgs of St. Germain, lamenting his condition in a mock-heroic monologue:

> "Luckless man that I am! said the notary, to be the sport of hurricanes all my days: — to be born to have the storm of ill language levell'd against me and my profession wherever I go; to be forced into marriage by the thunder of the church to a tempest of a woman; — to be driven forth out of my house by domestic winds, and despoil'd of my castor by pontific ones!" (058-054)

### The Testament Chamber

As the Notary continues his lament through a dark passage, a voice calls for "the next notary." Being the nearest, he avails himself of the opportunity and is ushered into a large chamber hung with military relics — a pike, a breastplate, a sword, and bandoleer. An old gentleman lies in bed, supporting his head on his hand, with a taper burning beside him. The Notary sits, draws out his inkhorn and paper, and prepares to take the gentleman's last will and testament. (Ch. 46, [[paris|Paris]])

The gentleman reveals he has nothing to bequeath but "the history of myself, which I could not die in peace, unless I left it as a legacy to the world." He promises the profits to the Notary's house. Three times the Notary dips his pen into his inkhorn, inflamed with a desire to begin, as the old man invokes heaven to direct his tongue "by the spirit of eternal truth." Just as the dictation is about to commence — the fragment ends, and [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] informs [[yorick|Yorick]] that the remaining sheets have been given away in a bouquet. (058-107)

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**Professional Dignity and Domestic Chaos**: The Notary embodies the comic gap between professional gravitas and domestic vulnerability. He carries the instruments of legal authority — parchment, inkhorn, pen — yet cannot command peace in his own household. This tension between the formal and the personal is a recurring sentimental motif in Sterne's novel.

**The Accidental and the Providential**: The Notary's misadventures — his wife's fury, the wind on the bridge, the boatman's adage — raise the question of whether accidents are truly random or secretly directed. The narrative's repeated invocation of "wind" (domestic, pontific, meteorological) suggests a universe where forces beyond human control govern even the most dignified professionals.
