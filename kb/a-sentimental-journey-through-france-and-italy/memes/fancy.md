# Fancy

Fancy in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the creative power of the mind that fills the gaps between perception and reality. It is the faculty that transforms a shabby carriage into a vehicle of sentiment, a stranger's face into a goddess, and a simple rencontre into an episode of moral significance. Sterne treats fancy not as idle daydream but as the engine of sentimental experience — what the eye merely sees, fancy colours and completes.

## 定义与历史语境

In eighteenth-century thought, fancy occupied a middle ground between mere sense-perception and higher reason. Addison, in the *Spectator* papers on the "pleasures of the imagination," distinguished fancy as the power of combining and varying images received from the senses. Hobbes had cast it more darkly as "decaying sense"; the Romantics would later elevate it into the creative imagination. Sterne, writing at mid-century, works with fancy as it was understood by Locke and Addison — a lively, associational power, neither wholly trustworthy nor wholly to be distrusted, but essential to the texture of lived experience.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Fancy appears at the novel's very outset as the force that chooses [[yorick|Yorick]]'s vehicle. The désobligeant, an old carriage in the corner of the coach-yard, "hit my fancy at first sight" ([[calais|Calais]], Ch. 6) — not because of its comfort or value, but because fancy had already begun to weave a story around it. Moments later, fancy supplies the history of the carriage's imagined former owner, an "Innocent [[travel|Travel]]ler" who left it to Mons. Dessein's honour.

Fancy is equally active in shaping [[yorick|Yorick]]'s perceptions of people. When he glimpses the lady at the [[the-remise|Remise]] door, "long before we had got to the door of the [[the-remise|Remise]], Fancy had finished the whole head, and pleased herself as much with its fitting her goddess, as if she had dived into the Tiber for it" ([[calais|Calais]], [[the-remise-door|the Remise Door]]). The woman is unknown; fancy supplies the ideal. Throughout the novel, fancy operates as a collaborator with sentiment — sentiment supplies the moral impulse, fancy supplies the images, and together they produce the distinctive texture of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s experience.

Sterne also acknowledges fancy's dark side. In [[paris|Paris]], when [[yorick|Yorick]] imagines the [[the-bastile|Bastille]], "the confinement which my fancy had drawn" ([[paris|Paris]] chapters) terrifies him more than the reality could. Fancy can scatter rosebuds; it can also conjure dungeons.

## 关键段落

> "An old désobligeant in the furthest corner of the court, hit my fancy at first sight, so I instantly got into it, and finding it in tolerable harmony with my feelings, I ordered the waiter to call Monsieur Dessein." (Calais, In the Desobligeant)

> "Long before we had got to the door of the Remise, Fancy had finished the whole head, and pleased herself as much with its fitting her goddess, as if she had dived into the Tiber for it — but thou art a seduced, and a seducing slut." (Calais, the Remise Door)

> "The smooth velvet path, which Fancy has scattered over with rosebuds of delights." (Paris chapters)

> "I felt the laurels shake which fancy had woven." (Paris chapters)
