# Mundungus

**Mundungus** is one half of Sterne's satiric double-portrait of failed travellers. Where [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] represents the splenetic fault-finder who cannot enjoy anything, Mundungus represents the merely wealthy traveller who passes through the world untouched — "with an immense fortune" yet entirely empty-handed in sentiment (017-007).

## Role Overview

Mundungus appears in a single compressed passage, introduced immediately after [[yorick|Yorick]] dismisses [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] with the advice to consult his physician. The pairing is deliberate: Sterne sketches two opposite failures of travel, one born of spleen and the other born of wealth without engagement. Together they define the negative space against which [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental ideal takes shape.

## Journey

Mundungus "made the whole tour; going on from [[rome|Rome]] to [[naples|Naples]], — from [[naples|Naples]] to [[venice|Venice]], — from [[venice|Venice]] to Vienna, — to Dresden, to Berlin" (017-007). His itinerary is a grand sweep of the European Grand Tour, yet Sterne records not a single encounter, observation, or moment of human connection. He "travell'd straight on, looking neither to his right hand nor his left, lest [[love|Love]] or [[pity|Pity]] should seduce him out of his road" (017-007).

The indictment is devastating in its economy: Mundungus completed the entire tour "without one generous connection or pleasurable anecdote to tell of" (017-007). His wealth enabled movement but insulated him from every occasion for sentiment.

## Character and Themes

Mundungus embodies the failure of travel detached from sensibility. His "immense fortune" — the only attribute Sterne grants him — becomes not a resource for generosity but a barrier against it. The image of a traveller who looks "neither to his right hand nor his left" is the antithesis of [[yorick|Yorick]], who stops for every monk, every starling, every grieving woman by the roadside.

Sterne's final word on both failed travellers is eschatological: even heaven itself would fail to please them. "Were the happiest mansion in heaven to be allotted to [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] and Mundungus, they would be so far from being happy, that the souls of [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] and Mundungus would do penance there to all eternity!" (017-008). The incapacity for happiness is the ultimate sentimental tragedy — neither fortune on earth nor paradise beyond can compensate for a soul that has cultivated no faculties for joy.
