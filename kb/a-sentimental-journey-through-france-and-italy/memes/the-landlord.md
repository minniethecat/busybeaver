# The Landlord

**The Landlord** is the innkeeper at [[montreuil|Montreuil]] who first appears early in [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey, introducing the valet [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] to him in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Part businessman, part social facilitator, the Landlord's blend of provincial shrewdness and genuine warmth makes him a memorable minor figure in the opening stages of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s travels through [[calais|France]].

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Landlord of Montreuil operates at the threshold of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s French adventure. It is he who — noticing [[yorick|Yorick]]'s need for a servant — recommends [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], the young man who will accompany the narrator through the entire remainder of his journey. The Landlord thus serves as a catalyst for one of the novel's most significant relationships. He is presented with a mixture of affectionate satire (for his commercial instincts) and genuine appreciation (for his good nature and local standing). (Ch. 9, [[montreuil|Montreuil]])

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Servant Recommendation

[[yorick|Yorick]] arrives at [[montreuil|Montreuil]] having twice lost his portmanteau and gotten out in the rain. When the Landlord asks "if I wanted not a servant," [[yorick|Yorick]] realizes this is precisely what he needs. The Landlord recommends a "clever young fellow, who would be very proud of the honour to serve an Englishman." When [[yorick|Yorick]] asks why Englishmen in particular, the Landlord replies with characteristic frankness: "They are so generous, Monsieur." [[yorick|Yorick]] mentally notes this as "a livre out of my pocket this very night." (018-006)

The Landlord seizes the opportunity to correct [[yorick|Yorick]]'s French usage, explaining the distinction between *tant pis* and *tant mieux*. Sterne observes that these "two of the great hinges in French conversation" are essential knowledge for any traveller, and the Landlord delivers his lesson with the confidence of a man who knows his province thoroughly. (018-027)

### The Introduction of La Fleur

Having established his linguistic authority, the Landlord calls in [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], vouching for him with measured prudence: "as for his talents he would presume to say nothing, — Monsieur was the best judge what would suit him; but for the fidelity of [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] he would stand responsible in all he was worth." This carefully calibrated endorsement — modest about abilities, absolute about character — reveals the Landlord's practical wisdom. (018-042)

### The Morning Departure

The following morning, as [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] takes leave of half a dozen young women gathered around the chaise, the Landlord observes through the window: "The young fellow is beloved by all the town, and there is scarce a corner in [[montreuil|Montreuil]] where the want of him will not be felt: he has but one misfortune in the world — he is always in love." This final remark, equal parts fondness and exasperation, captures the Landlord's role as a knowing observer of local character. (021-017)

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**Commercial Candour**: The Landlord embodies a peculiarly Sternean virtue — the ability to be commercially self-interested and genuinely warm at the same time. His compliments to English generosity are transparently mercenary, yet his affection for [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] and his standing in the community are equally real.

**Provincial French [[life|Life]]**: Through the Landlord, Sterne sketches a miniature portrait of French provincial society — its inns, its social networks, its linguistic customs — before the novel moves to the more complex urban world of [[paris|Paris]].
