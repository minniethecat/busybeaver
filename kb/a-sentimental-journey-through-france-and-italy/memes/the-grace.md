# The Grace

**The Grace** is an episode near the end of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), immediately following "[[the-supper|The Supper]]." After the humble meal in a [[savoy|Savoy]]ard peasant cottage, the old man summons his family to their nightly dance — a domestic ritual of thanksgiving so pure that [[yorick|Yorick]] perceives [[religion|Religion]] itself mingling in the steps.

## 概要

When supper is over, the old peasant — father of the household that has welcomed [[yorick|Yorick]] with such simple generosity — gives a knock upon the table with the haft of his knife. It is the signal for the dance. The women and girls run into a back apartment to tie up their hair; the young men to the door to wash their faces and change their wooden sabots. In three minutes every soul is ready upon the little esplanade before the house.

The old man and his wife seat themselves on a sofa of turf by the door, placing [[yorick|Yorick]] between them. The old man, some fifty years past being "no mean performer upon the vielle," still plays well enough for the purpose. His wife sings "now and then a little to the tune," then intermits and joins her husband, as their children and grandchildren dance before them.

It is not until the middle of the second dance that [[yorick|Yorick]], observing pauses in the movements "wherein they all seemed to look up," fancies he can distinguish "an elevation of spirit different from that which is the cause or the effect of simple jollity." He puts it into words: "I thought I beheld *[[religion|Religion]]* mixing in the dance" (068-020). Fearing this is another illusion of his "eternally misleading" imagination, he is reassured when the old man explains: this has been their constant way all his life — after supper, he calls his family to dance and rejoice, "believing that a cheerful and contented mind was the best sort of thanks to heaven that an illiterate peasant could pay" (068-026).

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s reply — "Or a learned prelate either" — closes the chapter with characteristic sentiment: the peasant's instinctive piety is more authentic than the elaborate theology of churchmen.

## 主题分析

- **Dance as [[religion|Devotion]]**: The chapter presents bodily joy as a legitimate form of worship. The family's nightly dance is not entertainment but "the best sort of thanks to heaven" — a ritual of gratitude that needs no church, no liturgy, no priest. Sterne's radical suggestion is that a cheerful mind is prayer enough.

- **Illiterate [[religion|Piety]], Learned [[religion|Faith]]**: The old man's careful distinction — "an illiterate peasant" — sets up [[yorick|Yorick]]'s perfect rejoinder: "Or a learned prelate either." The grace that follows the supper is performed not in Latin but in dance steps; its theology is wordless but no less profound. The episode is one of the novel's most direct statements of sentimental religion.

- **The Family as Congregation**: The dance includes "children and grand-children" — three generations moving together in a rhythm older than any formal creed. The family, not the church, is the true site of worship in Sterne's vision, and the grace they perform is the grace they live.
