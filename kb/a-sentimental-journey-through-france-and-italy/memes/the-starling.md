# The Starling

**The Starling** is a caged bird whose plaintive cry — "I can't get out" — becomes one of the most resonant symbols in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*. [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters the starling during his journey, and so identifies with its predicament that he adopts the bird as the crest of his family coat of arms (042-001).

## Role Overview

The starling appears not as a character in the conventional sense, but as a powerful symbolic presence that crystallizes the novel's central preoccupation with liberty and confinement. Caught on the cliffs of [[dover|Dover]] before it could fly, taught four English words by a groom, and passed from owner to owner across Europe, the bird's repeated cry echoes the human longing for freedom that Sterne explores throughout the narrative. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s decision to place the starling on his coat of arms elevates the bird from a chance encounter to the novel's emblematic heart.

## Appearances

[[yorick|Yorick]] first hears the starling while travelling, when a voice repeating "I can't get out — I can't get out" draws his attention. Looking up, he sees the bird in a cage, and the famous exchange follows: "I can't get out, said the starling. — God help thee! said I, but I'll let thee out, cost what it will" (042-002). When [[yorick|Yorick]] attempts to open the cage door, the bird flutters to the side where the wires are, and finding no opening, repeats its complaint: "I can't get out, said the starling" (042-003).

The bird's history is then recounted: it was caught on the [[dover|Dover]] cliffs by an English lad serving as groom to the [[honour|Honour]]able Mr. —. The lad carried it in his breast across the Channel, bought a little cage for it in [[paris|Paris]], and during five months taught it four simple words — and no more (042-004). When the master departed for [[italy|Italy]], the lad gave the bird to the master of the hotel in [[paris|Paris]], where, its English song being unintelligible to the French, it was neglected. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] eventually purchased both bird and cage for [[yorick|Yorick]] for a bottle of Burgundy (042-005).

[[yorick|Yorick]] brought the starling back to [[england|England]], where it passed through the hands of multiple lords — from Lord A to Lord B to Lord C and "half round the alphabet" — before descending into the House of Commons and passing through as many commoners. Throughout its travels, the bird's desire to get *out* was matched by its owners' desire to get *in*, rendering it equally neglected in [[london|London]] as in [[paris|Paris]] (042-006).

## Character and Themes

The starling is Sterne's master-symbol for the theme of liberty — both physical and existential. The bird's inability to free itself mirrors the various forms of captivity that Sterne explores: social constraint, emotional confinement, and the invisible cages of custom and obligation. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s immediate sympathy — his vow to free the bird at any cost — enacts the sentimental imperative to respond to suffering, even when that suffering belongs to a creature of another species.

The contrast between the bird's desire to get *out* and the lords' and commoners' desire to get *in* creates a pointed social satire: those who already possess liberty seek entry into ever-more-exclusive circles, while the truly confined creature longs only for the freedom they take for granted. This inversion is characteristic of Sterne's ironic method.

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s adoption of the starling as his crest — with the defiant postscript, "And let the herald's officers twist his neck about if they dare" (042-007) — represents the novel's final word on liberty: it is not merely a political condition but a personal emblem, worn as identity.
