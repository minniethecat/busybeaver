## CALAIS.

[012-001]I had never quitted the

[012-002]lady’s hand all this time, and had held it so long, that it

[012-003]would have been indecent to have let it go, without first

[012-004]pressing it to my lips: the blood and spirits, which had suffered

[012-005]a revulsion from her, crowded back to her as I did it. 

[012-006]Now the two travellers, who had spoke to me in the coach-yard,

[012-007]happening at that crisis to be passing by, and observing our

[012-008]communications, naturally took it into their heads that we must

[012-009]be man and wife at least; so, stopping as soon as they

[012-010]came up to the door of the Remise, the one of them who was the

[012-011]Inquisitive Traveller, ask’d us, if we set out for Paris

[012-012]the next morning?—I could only answer for myself, I said;

[012-013]and the lady added, she was for Amiens.—We dined there

[012-014]yesterday, said the Simple Traveller.—You go directly

[012-015]through the town, added the other, in your road to Paris. I

[012-016]was going to return a thousand thanks for the intelligence, that Amiens was in the road to Paris , but, upon pulling

[012-017]out my poor monk’s little horn box to take a pinch of

[012-018]snuff, I made them a quiet bow, and wishing them a good passage

[012-019]to Dover.—They left us alone.— 

[012-020]—Now where would be the harm, said I to myself, if I

[012-021]were to beg of this distressed lady to accept of half of my

[012-022]chaise?—and what mighty mischief could ensue? 

[012-023]Every dirty passion, and bad propensity in my nature took the

[012-024]alarm, as I stated the proposition.—It will oblige you to

[012-025]have a third horse, said Avarice, which will put twenty livres

[012-026]out of your pocket;—You know not what she is, said

[012-027]Caution;—or what scrapes the affair may draw you into,

[012-028]whisper’d Cowardice.— 

[012-029]Depend upon it, Yorick! said Discretion, ’twill be said

[012-030]you went off with a mistress, and came by assignation to Calais

[012-031]for that purpose;— 

[012-032]—You can never after, cried Hypocrisy aloud, show your

[012-033]face in the world;—or rise, quoth Meanness, in the

[012-034]church;—or be any thing in it, said Pride, but a lousy

[012-035]prebendary. 

[012-036]But ’tis a civil thing, said I;—and as I generally

[012-037]act from the first impulse, and therefore seldom listen to these

[012-038]cabals, which serve no purpose, that I know of, but to encompass

[012-039]the heart with adamant—I turned instantly about to the

[012-040]lady.— 

[012-041]—But she had glided off unperceived, as the cause was

[012-042]pleading, and had made ten or a dozen paces down the street, by

[012-043]the time I had made the determination; so I set off after her

[012-044]with a long stride, to make her the proposal, with the best

[012-045]address I was master of: but observing she walk’d with her cheek half resting upon the palm

[012-046]of her hand,—with the slow short-measur’d step of

[012-047]thoughtfulness,—and with her eyes, as she went step by

[012-048]step, fixed upon the ground, it struck me she was trying the same

[012-049]cause herself.—God help her! said I, she has some

[012-050]mother-in-law, or tartufish aunt, or nonsensical old woman, to

[012-051]consult upon the occasion, as well as myself: so not caring to

[012-052]interrupt the process, and deeming it more gallant to take her at

[012-053]discretion than by surprise, I faced about and took a short turn

[012-054]or two before the door of the Remise, whilst she walk’d

[012-055]musing on one side.
