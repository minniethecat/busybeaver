# Moulines

**Moulines** is a small town in the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]] region of central [[france|France]], near which [[yorick|Yorick]] seeks out [[maria-of-moulines|the disordered maid]] [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] in one of the most celebrated sentimental episodes of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Occupying chapters 63 through 65, the Moulines sequence forms the emotional climax of the novel's closing movement.

## 地理与文化背景

Moulines (likely Moulins in the Allier department) lies in the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]] — "the sweetest part of [[france|France]]," as [[yorick|Yorick]] describes it, traveling through the region "in the heyday of the vintage, when [[nature|Nature]] is pouring her abundance into every one's lap, and every eye is lifted up" (063-001). The contrast between this pastoral abundance and [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]'s private suffering is central to the scene's effect. Moulines is not a grand city like [[paris|Paris]] or a seat of power like [[versailles|Versailles]] — it is an ordinary French town made extraordinary by the emotional drama that unfolds at its edge.

## 在小说中的角色

The Moulines sequence weaves together three strands: the landscape of the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]], the literary memory of [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] (established in Sterne's earlier novel *Tristram Shandy*), and the immediate experience of the encounter:

**The Approach (Ch. 63)**. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s approach to Moulines is drenched in a heightened sensibility. [[travel|Travel]]ing through the vintage, he feels his "affections flying out, and kindling at every group before me" — each one "pregnant with adventures" (063-001). But his thoughts are fixed on [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]], "my friend, Mr. Shandy, met with near Moulines" (063-002). The story of the "disordered maid" — read in *Tristram Shandy* — has affected him so deeply that he cannot resist detouring "half a league out of the road, to the village where her parents dwelt, to enquire after her" (063-003).

**The Encounter (Ch. 64)**. Half a league from Moulines, [[yorick|Yorick]] finds [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] sitting under a poplar tree beside a small brook — the same posture in which Mr. Shandy found her. She has a little dog on a string and a pipe in her hand, and her "melancholy" beauty transforms the pastoral scene into a tableau of exquisite sorrow. The encounter between [[yorick|Yorick]] and [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] — "I sat down close by her; and [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] let me wipe them [her tears] away as they fell, with my handkerchief" (064-005) — is the novel's most concentrated passage of pure sentiment, and one of the most parodied scenes in 18th-century literature.

**Entering Moulines (Ch. 65)**. After the encounter, [[yorick|Yorick]] and [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] walk together into Moulines: "in that order we enter'd Moulines" — [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] leading, [[yorick|Yorick]] following, united by a shared moment of feeling (065-003). The town itself barely appears in the text; it is the emotional threshold it represents — the boundary between solitary suffering and shared sentiment — that matters.
