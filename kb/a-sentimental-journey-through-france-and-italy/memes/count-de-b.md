# Count de B—

**Count de B—** is a French nobleman residing at [[versailles|Versailles]] whom [[yorick|Yorick]] visits to obtain a passport in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). A lover of Shakespeare and English books, possessed of cultivated manners and genuine good nature, the Count represents the ideal of enlightened French aristocracy — worldly, literate, and capable of the kind of sympathetic understanding that transcends national boundaries.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Count de B— enters the novel at a moment of practical crisis. [[yorick|Yorick]], travelling without a passport in a time of war between [[england|England]] and [[calais|France]], has been warned by his hotel landlord that he risks the [[the-bastile|Bastille]]. A chance discovery at a [[paris|Paris]] bookseller — that the Count de B— owns a set of Shakespeare — provides [[yorick|Yorick]] with the opening for an introduction. What follows is one of the novel's most extended social encounters: a conversation that moves from the practical matter of the passport to reflections on women, love, nakedness, and the purpose of travel itself. (Ch. 44, [[paris|Paris]]; Ch. 46, [[versailles|Versailles]] — "[[the-passport|The Passport]]")

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Bookseller's Discovery

[[yorick|Yorick]] first learns of the Count at a [[paris|Paris]] bookshop on the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]], where he goes to purchase Shakespeare's works. The bookseller tells him that a set on the counter belongs to the Count de B— and is to be sent back to [[versailles|Versailles]]. When [[yorick|Yorick]] asks "And does the Count de B— read Shakespeare?" the bookseller replies, "C'est un esprit fort — He loves English books! and what is more to his honour, Monsieur, he loves the English too." This discovery — a French aristocrat who appreciates English culture — gives [[yorick|Yorick]] his strategy for the passport. (Ch. 44, [[paris|Paris]])

### The Passport Interview at Versailles

[[yorick|Yorick]] presents himself at [[versailles|Versailles]] and gains admittance to the Count's apartment. The set of Shakespeares is laid upon the table. Rather than presenting a letter of introduction, [[yorick|Yorick]] points to Shakespeare's works and declares: "it is my countryman, the great Shakespeare — et ayez la bonté, mon cher ami, de me faire cet honneur-là." The Count smiles at this singular introduction, and seeing [[yorick|Yorick]] "a little pale and sickly," insists he take an armchair. (Ch. 46, [[versailles|Versailles]])

The Count listens with great good nature as [[yorick|Yorick]] explains his predicament — the lack of a passport, the landlord's warning about the [[the-bastile|Bastille]] — and responds with the reassuring "Ne craignez rien — Don't fear" (046-033). When [[yorick|Yorick]] speaks of the gallantry of the French, an "animated blush" comes into the Count's cheeks. (Ch. 46, [[versailles|Versailles]])

### The Conversation on Women, Love, and Travel

With the passport business settled, the Count leads the discourse through "indifferent things, — of books, and politics, and men; — and then of women." [[yorick|Yorick]] declares his universal love of the sex, insisting that "a man, who has not a sort of affection for the whole sex, is incapable of ever loving a single one as he ought." The Count, gaily playing devil's advocate, suggests that perhaps [[yorick|Yorick]] has come "to spy the nakedness of the land — ni encore, I dare say, that of our women!" (Ch. 46, [[versailles|Versailles]])

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s response is the novel's most explicit statement of purpose:

> "I could wish, continued I, to spy the nakedness of their hearts, and through the different disguises of customs, climates, and religion, find out what is good in them to fashion my own by: — and therefore am I come (046-072). It is for this reason, Monsieur le Count, that I have not seen the Palais Royal, — nor the Luxembourg, — nor the Façade of the Louvre... I conceive every fair being as a temple, and would rather enter in, and see the original drawings and loose sketches hung up in it, than the Transfiguration of Raphael itself." (Ch. 46, Versailles)

The Count responds with "a great many civil things" and expresses his obligation to Shakespeare for the introduction.

### The Passport and La Fleur's Bouquet

The Count provides [[yorick|Yorick]] with a passport, and later the same day [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] is dispatched back to the Count's hotel to retrieve the remaining sheets of a manuscript fragment that had been wrapped around a bouquet — the bouquet having passed from [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s mistress to one of the Count's footmen, to a sempstress, to a fiddler. The Count's household thus becomes entangled, however indirectly, in the fate of the Notary's fragment. (Ch. 47, [[paris|Paris]])

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**Enlightened Cosmopolitanism**: Count de B— embodies the ideal of the Enlightenment aristocrat — a man whose rank gives him access to power (the passport) but whose intellectual curiosity (Shakespeare, English books) and social grace transcend national prejudice. His "animated blush" at [[yorick|Yorick]]'s praise of French gallantry reveals a man genuinely moved by the good opinion of a foreigner.

**The Civilized Conversation**: The extended dialogue between [[yorick|Yorick]] and the Count is the novel's most sustained example of what eighteenth-century culture valued as "polite conversation" — a discursive, digressive, mutually respectful exchange that moves freely between the practical and the philosophical.
