# The Conquest

**The Conquest** is a brief but pivotal episode in the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), immediately following "[[the-temptation|The Temptation]]." Alone in his hotel room with the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]], [[yorick|Yorick]] achieves what he calls a "conquest" — not over the young woman, but over his own passions. The chapter is an extended apostrophe justifying the presence of desire in human nature and celebrating the virtue of self-governance.

## 概要

The chapter opens mid-scene — "Yes, — and then —" — with [[yorick|Yorick]] addressing an imagined tribunal of stoic philosophers who would condemn passion itself as trespass. His defence is both theological and naturalistic: "If [[nature|Nature]] has so wove her web of kindness, that some threads of love and desire are entangled with the piece, — must the whole web be rent in drawing them out?" He calls for the "great Governor of [[nature|Nature]]" to whip such stoics, and declares his own creed: "whatever is my danger, — whatever is my situation, — let me feel the movements which rise out of it, and which belong to me as a man, — and, if I govern them as a good one, I will trust the issues to thy justice; for thou hast made us, and not we ourselves" (052-012).

Having finished his address, [[yorick|Yorick]] raises the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]] by the hand and leads her out of the room. She stands by while he locks the door and puts the key in his pocket — a symbolic gesture of mastery over the temptation. Then, "the victory being quite decisive — and not till then," he presses his lips to her cheek, and leads her safely to the gate of the [[hotel-de-modene|hotel]].

The chapter, despite its brevity, is one of the novel's most important ethical statements. It establishes that sentiment and virtue are not opposites but complements: feeling deeply is a condition of acting rightly, not an impediment to it.

## 主题分析

- **[[passion|Passion]] and [[virtue|Virtue]]**: The chapter's central argument is that passion is not the enemy of virtue but its raw material. "Let me feel the movements which rise out of it, and which belong to me as a man" — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s prayer is for governance, not extirpation. The conquest is not the absence of desire but its right direction.

- **The [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al Stoic**: Sterne explicitly positions himself against the stoic tradition that would "argue down or mask your passions." The sentimental hero feels everything and governs himself nonetheless — a more demanding ideal than mere insensibility.

- **The Locked Door**: The gesture of locking the door and pocketing the key is richly symbolic. It represents the boundary between temptation and action, the moment of conscious self-restraint that transforms potential transgression into moral victory.
