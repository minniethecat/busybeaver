# Rue de Gueneguault

The **Rue de Gueneguault** is a street in the Saint-Germain quarter of [[paris|Paris]], near the [[hotel-de-modene|Hotel de Modene]] where [[yorick|Yorick]] lodges. In Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), it appears as the setting for one of the novel's most delicately rendered farewells — a scene that exemplifies [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental art of the parting glance.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

The Rue de Gueneguault appears in the [[paris|Paris]] chapters as the site of a quietly charged leave-taking:

**The Farewell Scene**: After an encounter with a young *fille de chambre*, [[yorick|Yorick]] walks with her through the streets of [[paris|Paris]]. When they reach "the turning up of the Rue de Gueneguault" (Ch. 45, [[the-fille-de-chambre|The Fille de Chambre]]), he stops to bid her adieu. The street corner becomes, in Sterne's hands, a theater of suspended emotion — the moment of parting drawn out with the exquisite attention to gesture and hesitation that defines the sentimental mode.

**The Geography of [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: The Rue de Gueneguault is, in itself, an unremarkable [[paris|Paris]]ian street. What makes it significant is Sterne's practice of anchoring emotional climaxes in precise, named locations. The specificity of the street name — unusual in a novel that often veils personal identities with dashes — lends the scene a documentary weight: this happened *here*, at this corner, on this street.

## 18 世纪背景 (18th-Century Context)

The Rue de Gueneguault (modern spelling: Rue Guénégaud) runs through the Faubourg Saint-Germain, the district where the [[hotel-de-modene|Hotel de Modene]] was located. In Sterne's time, this was a neighborhood of hotels, furnished lodgings, and the transient population of travelers — the perfect setting for the kind of brief, intense encounters between strangers that fuel the sentimental journey.
