## PARIS.

[058-001]— Now , as the notary’s

[058-002]wife disputed the point with the notary with too much

[058-003]heat,—I wish, said the notary, (throwing down the

[058-004]parchment) that there was another notary here only to set down

[058-005]and attest all this.— 

[058-006]—And what would you do then, Monsieur? said she, rising

[058-007]hastily up.—The notary’s wife was a little fume of a

[058-008]woman, and the notary thought it well to avoid a hurricane by a

[058-009]mild reply.—I would go, answered he, to bed.—You may

[058-010]go to the devil, answer’d the notary’s wife. 

[058-011]Now there happening to be but one bed in the house, the other

[058-012]two rooms being unfurnished, as is the custom at Paris, and the

[058-013]notary not caring to lie in the same bed with a woman who had but

[058-014]that moment sent him pell mell to the devil, went forth with his

[058-015]hat and cane and short cloak, the night being very windy, and

[058-016]walk’d out, ill at ease, towards the Pont Neuf. 

[058-017]Of all the bridges which ever were built, the whole world who

[058-018]have pass’d over the Pont Neuf must own, that it is the

[058-019]noblest,—the finest,—the grandest,—the

[058-020]lightest,—the longest,—the broadest, that ever

[058-021]conjoin’d land and land together upon the face of the

[058-022]terraqueous globe. 

[058-023][ By this it seems as if the

[058-024]author of the fragment had not been a Frenchman .] 

[058-025]The worst fault which divines and the doctors of the Sorbonne

[058-026]can allege against it is, that if there is but a capfull of wind

[058-027]in or about Paris, ’tis more blasphemously sacre

[058-028]Dieu’d there than in any other aperture of the whole

[058-029]city,—and with reason good and cogent, Messieurs; for it

[058-030]comes against you without crying garde d’eau , and

[058-031]with such unpremeditable puffs, that of the few who cross it with

[058-032]their hats on, not one in fifty but hazards two livres and a

[058-033]half, which is its full worth. 

[058-034]The poor notary, just as he was passing by the sentry,

[058-035]instinctively clapp’d his cane to the side of it, but in

[058-036]raising it up, the point of his cane catching hold of the loop of

[058-037]the sentinel’s hat, hoisted it over the spikes of the

[058-038]ballustrade clear into the Seine.— 

[058-039]—’ Tis an ill wind , said a boatman, who

[058-040]catched it, which blows nobody any good . 

[058-041]The sentry, being a Gascon, incontinently twirled up his

[058-042]whiskers, and levell’d his arquebuss. 

[058-043]Arquebusses in those days went off with matches; and an old

[058-044]woman’s paper lantern at the end of the bridge happening to

[058-045]be blown out, she had borrow’d the sentry’s match to

[058-046]light it:—it gave a moment’s time for the

[058-047]Gascon’s blood to run cool, and turn the accident better to

[058-048]his advantage.—’ Tis an ill wind , said he,

[058-049]catching off the notary’s castor, and legitimating the

[058-050]capture with the boatman’s adage. 

[058-051]The poor notary crossed the bridge, and passing along the Rue de

[058-052]Dauphine into the fauxbourgs of St. Germain, lamented himself as

[058-053]he walked along in this manner:— 

[058-054]Luckless man that I am! said the notary, to be the sport of

[058-055]hurricanes all my days:—to be born to have the storm of ill

[058-056]language levell’d against me and my profession wherever I

[058-057]go; to be forced into marriage by the thunder of the church to a

[058-058]tempest of a woman;—to be driven forth out of my house by

[058-059]domestic winds, and despoil’d of my castor by pontific

[058-060]ones!—to be here, bareheaded, in a windy night, at the

[058-061]mercy of the ebbs and flows of accidents!—Where am I to lay

[058-062]my head?—Miserable man! what wind in the two-and-thirty

[058-063]points of the whole compass can blow unto thee, as it does to the

[058-064]rest of thy fellow-creatures, good? 

[058-065]As the notary was passing on by a dark passage, complaining in

[058-066]this sort, a voice call’d out to a girl, to bid her run for

[058-067]the next notary.—Now the notary being the next, and

[058-068]availing himself of his situation, walk’d up the passage to

[058-069]the door, and passing through an old sort of a saloon, was

[058-070]usher’d into a large chamber, dismantled of everything but

[058-071]a long military pike,—a breastplate,—a rusty old

[058-072]sword, and bandoleer, hung up, equidistant, in four different

[058-073]places against the wall. 

[058-074]An old personage who had heretofore been a gentleman, and

[058-075]unless decay of fortune taints the blood along with it, was a

[058-076]gentleman at that time, lay supporting his head upon his hand in

[058-077]his bed; a little table with a taper burning was set close beside

[058-078]it, and close by the table was placed a chair:—the notary

[058-079]sat him down in it; and pulling out his inkhorn and a sheet or

[058-080]two of paper which he had in his pocket, he placed them before

[058-081]him; and dipping his pen in his ink, and leaning his breast over

[058-082]the table, he disposed everything to make the gentleman’s

[058-083]last will and testament. 

[058-084]Alas! Monsieur le Notaire , said the gentleman,

[058-085]raising himself up a little, I have nothing to bequeath, which

[058-086]will pay the expense of bequeathing, except the history of

[058-087]myself, which I could not die in peace, unless I left it as a

[058-088]legacy to the world: the profits arising out of it I bequeath to

[058-089]you for the pains of taking it from me.—It is a story so

[058-090]uncommon, it must be read by all mankind;—it will make the

[058-091]fortunes of your house.—The notary dipp’d his pen

[058-092]into his inkhorn.—Almighty Director of every event in my

[058-093]life! said the old gentleman, looking up earnestly, and raising

[058-094]his hands towards heaven,—Thou, whose hand has led me on

[058-095]through such a labyrinth of strange passages down into this scene

[058-096]of desolation, assist the decaying memory of an old, infirm, and

[058-097]broken-hearted man;—direct my tongue by the spirit of thy

[058-098]eternal truth, that this stranger may set down nought but what is

[058-099]written in that Book , from whose

[058-100]records, said he, clasping his hands together, I am to be

[058-101]condemn’d or acquitted!—the notary held up the point

[058-102]of his pen betwixt the taper and his eye.— 

[058-103]It is a story, Monsieur le Notaire , said the gentleman,

[058-104]which will rouse up every affection in nature;—it will kill

[058-105]the humane, and touch the heart of Cruelty herself with

[058-106]pity.— 

[058-107]—The notary was inflamed with a desire to begin, and put

[058-108]his pen a third time into his ink-horn—and the old

[058-109]gentleman, turning a little more towards the notary, began to

[058-110]dictate his story in these words:— 

[058-111]—And where is the rest of it, La Fleur? said I, as he

[058-112]just then enter’d the room.
