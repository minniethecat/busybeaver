## AMIENS.

[027-001]The words were scarce out of my

[027-002]mouth when the Count de L—’s post-chaise, with his

[027-003]sister in it, drove hastily by: she had just time to make me a

[027-004]bow of recognition,—and of that particular kind of it,

[027-005]which told me she had not yet done with me. She was as good

[027-006]as her look; for, before I had quite finished my supper, her

[027-007]brother’s servant came into the room with a billet, in

[027-008]which she said she had taken the liberty to charge me with a

[027-009]letter, which I was to present myself to Madame R— the

[027-010]first morning I had nothing to do at Paris. There was only

[027-011]added, she was sorry, but from what penchant she had not

[027-012]considered, that she had been prevented telling me her

[027-013]story,—that she still owed it to me; and if my route should

[027-014]ever lay through Brussels, and I had not by then forgot the name

[027-015]of Madame de L—,—that Madame de L— would be

[027-016]glad to discharge her obligation. 

[027-017]Then I will meet thee, said I, fair spirit! at

[027-018]Brussels;—’tis only returning from Italy through

[027-019]Germany to Holland, by the route of Flanders,

[027-020]home;—’twill scarce be ten posts out of my way; but,

[027-021]were it ten thousand! with what a moral delight will it crown my

[027-022]journey, in sharing in the sickening incidents of a tale

[027-023]of misery told to me by such a sufferer? To see her weep!

[027-024]and, though I cannot dry up the fountain of her tears, what an

[027-025]exquisite sensation is there still left, in wiping them away from

[027-026]off the cheeks of the first and fairest of women, as I’m

[027-027]sitting with my handkerchief in my hand in silence the whole

[027-028]night beside her? 

[027-029]There was nothing wrong in the sentiment; and yet I instantly

[027-030]reproached my heart with it in the bitterest and most reprobate

[027-031]of expressions. 

[027-032]It had ever, as I told the reader, been one of the singular

[027-033]blessings of my life, to be almost every hour of it miserably in

[027-034]love with some one; and my last flame happening to be blown out

[027-035]by a whiff of jealousy on the sudden turn of a corner, I had

[027-036]lighted it up afresh at the pure taper of Eliza but about three

[027-037]months before,—swearing, as I did it, that it should last

[027-038]me through the whole journey.—Why should I dissemble the

[027-039]matter? I had sworn to her eternal fidelity;—she had

[027-040]a right to my whole heart:—to divide my affections was to

[027-041]lessen them;—to expose them was to risk them: where there

[027-042]is risk there may be loss:—and what wilt thou have, Yorick,

[027-043]to answer to a heart so full of trust and confidence—so

[027-044]good, so gentle, and unreproaching! 

[027-045]—I will not go to Brussels, replied I, interrupting

[027-046]myself.—But my imagination went on,—I recalled her

[027-047]looks at that crisis of our separation, when neither of us had

[027-048]power to say adieu! I look’d at the picture she had

[027-049]tied in a black riband about my neck,—and blush’d as

[027-050]I look’d at it.—I would have given the world to have

[027-051]kiss’d it,—but was ashamed.—And shall this

[027-052]tender flower, said I, pressing it between my hands,—shall

[027-053]it be smitten to its very root,—and smitten, Yorick! by

[027-054]thee, who hast promised to shelter it in thy breast? 

[027-055]Eternal Fountain of Happiness! said I, kneeling down upon the

[027-056]ground,—be thou my witness—and every pure spirit

[027-057]which tastes it, be my witness also, That I would not travel to

[027-058]Brussels, unless Eliza went along with me, did the road lead me

[027-059]towards heaven! 

[027-060]In transports of this kind, the heart, in spite of the

[027-061]understanding, will always say too much.
