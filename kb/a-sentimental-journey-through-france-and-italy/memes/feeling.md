# Feeling

Feeling is the primitive term of Sterne's moral vocabulary — the unanalyzed, pre-reflective responsiveness to the world that makes sentiment, sympathy, and moral judgment possible. It is what [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] lacks and what [[yorick|Yorick]] possesses in abundance. When Sterne invokes the "Eternal Fountain of our feelings," he is naming the ultimate source of moral life in his fictional universe: a capacity so basic that it can only be traced to its divine origin, never explained.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth-century "culture of sensibility" placed feeling at the center of moral philosophy. Against Hobbes's portrait of human nature as essentially selfish, Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, and Hume argued that human beings possess an innate "moral sense" — a capacity to feel approval or disapproval in response to the actions of others. This moral sense was understood as analogous to the physical senses: just as the eye perceives color, the moral sense perceives virtue and vice. "Feeling" in this tradition is not mere emotion; it is a form of moral perception.

Sterne inherits this philosophical vocabulary but gives it a distinctively literary turn. In *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*, feeling is not a faculty to be analyzed but a quality to be displayed. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s feelings are his credentials as a narrator and as a moral being. The novel's central drama is not whether [[yorick|Yorick]] will do the right thing but whether his feelings will prove adequate to the situations he encounters — whether his sensibility will rise to the demands of the world.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Feeling appears in the novel in several distinct registers. The most fundamental is *fellow-feeling* — the capacity to feel what another feels, the sympathetic identification that bridges the gap between self and other. [[yorick|Yorick]] explicitly claims this virtue: "I am so evangelical in this, and have such a fellow-feeling for whatever is weak about them, that I would cover it with a garment if I knew how to throw it on." Fellow-feeling is the sentimental equivalent of Christian charity — a secularized agape that extends protection to the weak.

The second register is *aesthetic feeling* — the traveler's responsiveness to scenes, faces, and atmospheres. [[yorick|Yorick]] "envied him his feelings" when he observes a man weeping before the tomb of the two lovers, recognizing that the capacity for strong feeling is itself enviable. The French, [[yorick|Yorick]] notes, are "so renowned for sentiment and fine feelings" — feeling as a national characteristic, a cultural achievement.

The third register is *reflective feeling* — the narrator's ability to observe and comment on his own emotional responses. "I lose the feelings for myself in hers" describes the process by which sympathy dissolves the boundary between self and other, making one person's feelings the medium through which another experiences the world. This reflective capacity is what distinguishes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sensibility from mere emotionalism: he not only feels but understands what it means to feel.

The novel's final, culminating image of feeling is the "Eternal Fountain of our feelings" — a religious invocation that transforms feeling from a psychological capacity into a metaphysical principle. "Here I trace thee — and this is thy divinity which stirs within us." Feeling becomes the trace of the divine in human nature, the evidence that we are more than rational calculators.
