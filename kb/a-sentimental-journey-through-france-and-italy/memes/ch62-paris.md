## PARIS.

[062-001]We get forwards in the world, not

[062-002]so much by doing services, as receiving them; you take a

[062-003]withering twig, and put it in the ground; and then you water it,

[062-004]because you have planted it. 

[062-005]Monsieur le Count de B—, merely because he had done me

[062-006]one kindness in the affair of my passport, would go on and do me

[062-007]another, the few days he was at Paris, in making me known to a

[062-008]few people of rank; and they were to present me to others, and so

[062-009]on. 

[062-010]I had got master of my secret just in time to turn

[062-011]these honours to some little account; otherwise, as is commonly

[062-012]the case, I should have dined or supp’d a single time or

[062-013]two round, and then, by translating French looks and

[062-014]attitudes into plain English, I should presently have seen, that

[062-015]I had hold of the couvert [652] of some more

[062-016]entertaining guest; and in course should have resigned all my

[062-017]places one after another, merely upon the principle that I could

[062-018]not keep them.—As it was, things did not go much amiss. 

[062-019]I had the honour of being introduced to the old Marquis de

[062-020]B—: in days of yore he had signalized himself by some small

[062-021]feats of chivalry in the Cour d’Amour , and had

[062-022]dress’d himself out to the idea of tilts and tournaments

[062-023]ever since.—The Marquis de B— wish’d to have it

[062-024]thought the affair was somewhere else than in his brain.

[062-025]“He could like to take a trip to England,” and asked

[062-026]much of the English ladies.—Stay where you are, I beseech

[062-027]you, Monsieur le Marquis, said I.— Les Messieurs

[062-028]Anglois can scarce get a kind look from them as it

[062-029]is.—The Marquis invited me to supper. 

[062-030]Monsieur P—, the farmer-general, was just as inquisitive

[062-031]about our taxes. They were very considerable, he

[062-032]heard.—If we knew but how to collect them, said

[062-033]I, making him a low bow. 

[062-034]I could never have been invited to Mons. P—’s

[062-035]concerts upon any other terms. 

[062-036]I had been misrepresented to Madame de Q— as an esprit .—Madame de Q— was an esprit herself: she burnt with impatience to see me, and hear me

[062-037]talk. I had not taken my seat, before I saw she did not

[062-038]care a sous whether I had any wit or no;—I was let in, to

[062-039]be convinced she had. I call heaven to witness I never once

[062-040]opened the door of my lips. 

[062-041]Madame de V— vow’d to every creature she

[062-042]met—“She had never had a more improving conversation

[062-043]with a man in her life.” 

[062-044]There are three epochas in the empire of a French

[062-045]woman.—She is coquette,—then deist,—then dévote : the empire during these is never

[062-046]lost,—she only changes her subjects when thirty-five years

[062-047]and more have unpeopled her dominion of the slaves of love, she

[062-048]re-peoples it with slaves of infidelity,—and then with the

[062-049]slaves of the church. 

[062-050]Madame de V— was vibrating betwixt the first of those

[062-051]epochas: the colour of the rose was fading fast away;—she

[062-052]ought to have been a deist five years before the time I had the

[062-053]honour to pay my first visit. 

[062-054]She placed me upon the same sofa with her, for the sake of

[062-055]disputing the point of religion more closely.—In short

[062-056]Madame de V— told me she believed nothing.—I told

[062-057]Madame de V— it might be her principle, but I was sure it

[062-058]could not be her interest to level the outworks, without which I

[062-059]could not conceive how such a citadel as hers could be

[062-060]defended;—that there was not a more dangerous thing in the

[062-061]world than for a beauty to be a deist;—that it was a debt I

[062-062]owed my creed not to conceal it from her;—that I had not

[062-063]been five minutes sat upon the sofa beside her, but I had begun

[062-064]to form designs;—and what is it, but the sentiments of

[062-065]religion, and the persuasion they had excited in her breast,

[062-066]which could have check’d them as they rose up? 

[062-067]We are not adamant, said I, taking hold of her hand;—and

[062-068]there is need of all restraints, till age in her own time steals

[062-069]in and lays them on us.—But my dear lady, said I, kissing

[062-070]her hand,—’tis too—too soon. 

[062-071]I declare I had the credit all over Paris of unperverting

[062-072]Madame de V—.—She affirmed to Monsieur D— and

[062-073]the Abbé M—, that in one half hour I had said more

[062-074]for revealed religion, than all their Encyclopædia had said

[062-075]against it.—I was listed directly into Madame de

[062-076]V—’s coterie ;—and she put off the epocha

[062-077]of deism for two years. 

[062-078]I remember it was in this coterie , in the middle of a

[062-079]discourse, in which I was showing the necessity of a first cause, when the young Count de Faineant took me by the hand to

[062-080]the farthest corner of the room, to tell me my solitaire was pinn’d too straight about my neck.—It should be plus badinant , said the Count, looking down upon his

[062-081]own;—but a word, Monsieur Yorick, to the

[062-082]wise — 

[062-083]And from the wise , Monsieur le Count, replied I, making

[062-084]him a bow,— is enough . 

[062-085]The Count de Faineant embraced me with more ardour than ever I

[062-086]was embraced by mortal man. 

[062-087]For three weeks together I was of every man’s opinion I

[062-088]met.— Pardi ! ce Monsieur Yorick a autant

[062-089]d’esprit que nous autres .— Il raisonne

[062-090]bien , said another.— C’est un bon enfant ,

[062-091]said a third.—And at this price I could have eaten and

[062-092]drank and been merry all the days of my life at Paris; but

[062-093]’twas a dishonest reckoning ;—I grew ashamed of

[062-094]it.—It was the gain of a slave;—every sentiment of

[062-095]honour revolted against it;—the higher I got, the more was

[062-096]I forced upon my beggarly system ;—the better the coterie ,—the more children of Art;—I

[062-097]languish’d for those of Nature: and one night, after a most

[062-098]vile prostitution of myself to half a dozen different people, I

[062-099]grew sick,—went to bed;—order’d La Fleur to get

[062-100]me horses in the morning to set out for Italy.
