# The Riddle Explained

**The Riddle Explained** is the resolution chapter in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occurring in chapter 108 at [[paris|Paris]]. After the mysterious beggar from chapter 53 — the man who asked charity only from women and whispered a secret in each ear — had occupied [[yorick|Yorick]]'s mind as an unsolved riddle, he finally stumbles upon the answer: the beggar's secret is flattery. The chapter is one of the novel's characteristic anti-climaxes — a teasing mystery resolved with a single word that opens onto a wider meditation on human nature.

## 概要

The chapter opens with [[yorick|Yorick]] stepping hastily after a man in the Paris streets: "it was the very man whose success in asking charity of the women before the door of the hotel had so puzzled me; — and I found at once his secret, or at least the basis of it: — 'twas flattery."

This is the resolution of [[the-riddle|the riddle]] that had confounded [[yorick|Yorick]] outside the [[hotel-de-modene|Hôtel de Modène]]. For half an hour he had watched the beggar, unable to form "any system to explain the phenomenon" — why the man asked only women, why he whispered, why he was never refused. The answer, when it comes, is almost absurdly simple: flattery.

## The Apostrophe to Flattery

Sterne follows the revelation with one of his celebrated apostrophes — a direct address to an abstraction that transforms the discovery into a philosophical meditation:

"Delicious essence! how refreshing art thou to Nature! how strongly are all its powers and all its weaknesses on thy side! how sweetly dost thou mix with the blood, and help it through the most difficult and tortuous passages to the heart!" (061-006)

The passage is both a celebration of flattery's power and a knowing wink at its mechanism. [[yorick|Yorick]] does not condemn the beggar for using flattery — he marvels at the skill with which it is deployed. The poor man, "as he was not straiten'd for time, had given it here in a larger dose" — in the streets, he had condensed it for "the many sudden cases he had to do with."

The beggar's reward is modest: "two twelve-sous pieces." But Sterne's closing reflection is pointed: "they can best tell the rest, who have gained much greater matters by it."

## 主题分析

- **The Anti-Climax as Narrative Strategy**: The Riddle Explained is a masterclass in Sterne's anti-climactic art. A mystery that occupied an entire chapter and teased the reader's curiosity is resolved in a single sentence: "'twas flattery." The disproportion between the build-up and the payoff is deliberate — Sterne is less interested in the answer than in what the answer reveals about human susceptibility. The real riddle was never the beggar's secret; it was why we are so easily moved by what we know to be insincere.

- **Flattery as Universal Solvent**: The apostrophe to flattery — "Delicious essence!" — elevates a street beggar's trick into a principle of human nature. Flattery "mixes with the blood" and navigates "the most difficult and tortuous passages to the heart." The metaphor is physiological: flattery is not a lie we believe but a substance we absorb, bypassing the rational faculties entirely. The chapter thus connects to the novel's broader interest in the mechanisms of sentiment — the ways feeling operates beneath and beyond reason.

- **The Paired Chapters**: [[the-riddle|The Riddle]] and The Riddle Explained form one of the novel's most deliberate paired structures. The first chapter poses a question and refuses to answer it; the second answers it but immediately digresses into unrelated Paris salon material (the old Marquis de B—, Madame de Q—, Madame de V—). The structural joke is that the explanation, once given, is not nearly as interesting as the mystery. Sterne understands that unresolved questions have more narrative energy than resolved ones.
