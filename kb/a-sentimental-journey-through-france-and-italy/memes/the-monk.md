# The Monk

**The Monk** (the Franciscan of [[calais|Calais]]) is the first significant character [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters after landing in [[calais|France]], appearing at the very start of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). A poor monk of the order of St. Francis, he enters [[yorick|Yorick]]'s room at [[calais|Calais]] to beg for his convent — and [[yorick|Yorick]]'s harsh refusal, followed by immediate remorse, sets the novel's sentimental machinery in motion.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Monk is introduced as an old man — "might be about seventy" — with a striking face that Sterne describes in one of the novel's most painterly passages:

> It was one of those heads which Guido has often painted, — mild, pale — penetrating, free from all commonplace ideas of fat contented ignorance looking downwards upon the earth; — it look'd forwards; but look'd as if it look'd at something beyond this world. (002-031)

His presence is at once humble and dignified. With "a slender white staff" in his right hand and his left upon his breast, he makes his appeal "with so simple a grace, — and such an air of deprecation... I was bewitch'd not to have been struck with it." (002-053)

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Begging at Calais

The Monk enters [[yorick|Yorick]]'s room to beg "something for his convent." [[yorick|Yorick]], perversely, has "predetermined not to give him a single sous" — and delivers instead a lecture on charity, distinguishing between the deserving poor who work and those who "eat the bread of other people's." When [[yorick|Yorick]] tells him that had he been of "the *order of mercy*" rather than St. Francis, his purse would have opened "full cheerfully... for the ransom of the unfortunate," the Monk simply bows and withdraws. (004-002)

### Yorick's Remorse

The moment the Monk shuts the door, [[yorick|Yorick]]'s heart smites him. The remorse lingers, and the Monk reappears throughout the novel in [[yorick|Yorick]]'s memory — a silent rebuke to his own ungenerosity. The Monk's horn snuff-box becomes a recurring token, and [[yorick|Yorick]]'s later apostrophe — "my dear monk! so temper'd to bear and forbear!" — acknowledges a lesson in patience that [[yorick|Yorick]] himself failed to extend. (Ch. 3, [[calais|Calais]]; Ch. 31, [[paris|Paris]])

### The Lady of Calais

The Monk later appears in a tableau that deepens his significance: [[yorick|Yorick]] observes him in "close conference with a lady just arrived at the inn," exchanging a horn snuff-box — a scene of quiet, wordless generosity that silently reproaches [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own earlier conduct. This exchange becomes one of the novel's most discussed scenes, emblematic of Sterne's technique of conveying sentiment through gesture rather than speech. (023-032)

### The Snuff-Box

The Monk's little horn box becomes a touchstone object. [[yorick|Yorick]] carries it through his journey, and late in the novel, when he takes "a pinch of snuff out of my monk's little horn box," the gesture reconnects him — and the reader — to the novel's founding moral moment. (Ch. 31, [[paris|Paris]])

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**[[charity|Charity]] and its Limits**: The Monk episode is the novel's first and most explicit examination of charity — its obligations, its failures, and its relationship to sentiment. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s reasoned refusal ("we distinguish... betwixt those who wish only to eat the bread of their own labour — and those who eat the bread of other people's") is immediately exposed as a self-serving rationalization by the pang of remorse that follows. (Ch. 2, [[calais|Calais]])

**Silent Dignity**: The Monk speaks little but communicates profoundly. His "cordial wave with his head," his blush, his wordless pressing of hands to breast — these gestures constitute a language of sentiment more eloquent than [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sermonizing. Sterne uses the Monk to demonstrate that genuine feeling need not announce itself.

**[[sentiment|Sentiment]] as Aftermath**: The Monk's most important function is to show that sentiment often arrives too late — as remorse rather than preemptive kindness. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s heart smites him only "the moment he shut the door," making the Monk an enduring emblem of missed opportunities for goodness.
