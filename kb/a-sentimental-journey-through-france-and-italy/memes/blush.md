# Blush

The blush in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the body's confession — a surge of blood that speaks what the tongue cannot or will not. Sterne treats the blush not as mere embarrassment but as a moral index: it registers the presence of conscience, the sting of self-knowledge, and the subtle commerce of feeling between strangers. In a novel where so much passes through gesture and glance, the blush is the most eloquent of all involuntary signs.

## 定义与历史语境

Eighteenth-century culture was intensely interested in the blush. Addison called it "the colour of virtue"; Chesterfield advised his son to suppress it as a mark of ill-breeding; novelists from Richardson to Burney made it a central device of moral characterization. Physiologically, the blush was understood as the visible effect of the passions upon the blood — a direct line from the soul to the skin. Sterne, with his keen interest in the mind-body connection, makes the blush a recurring motif, tracking its shades from scarlet to rose, from shame to pleasure.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's first great blush belongs to [[the-monk|the monk of Calais]]. When [[yorick|Yorick]] refuses him charity, "[[the-monk|the poor monk]] blush'd as red as scarlet" ([[calais|Calais]], the Monk). The blush is not the monk's shame but [[yorick|Yorick]]'s — the monk's colour registers the insult [[yorick|Yorick]] has dealt him, and it triggers [[yorick|Yorick]]'s answering blush: "I blush'd in my turn; but from what movements, I leave to the few who feel, to analyze" ([[calais|Calais]], the Monk). This double blush inaugurates the novel's central moral transaction: feeling passes from body to body without a word spoken.

The blush reappears in [[paris|Paris]] when [[yorick|Yorick]] contemplates the portrait of the lady who gave it to him: "I look'd at the picture... and blush'd as I look'd at it" ([[paris|Paris]] chapters). Here the blush is private and erotic, a response to a memory rather than a present person. Later, the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] produces "an animated blush" ([[paris|Paris]], the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]]) that reveals him to be a man of feeling beneath his rank.

Sterne's most analytical passage on the blush comes near the novel's end: "There is a sort of a pleasing half guilty blush, where the blood is more in fault than the man" (the Case of Delicacy). The blush here is neither wholly innocent nor wholly guilty — it occupies the ambiguous moral territory that is Sterne's true subject.
