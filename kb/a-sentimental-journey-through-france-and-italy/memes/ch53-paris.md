## PARIS.

[053-001]If a man knows the heart, he will know it was impossible to go

[053-002]back instantly to my chamber;—it was touching a cold key

[053-003]with a flat third to it upon the close of a piece of music, which

[053-004]had call’d forth my affections:—therefore, when I let

[053-005]go the hand of the fille de chambre , I remained at the

[053-006]gate of the hotel for some time, looking at every one who

[053-007]pass’d by,—and forming conjectures upon them, till my

[053-008]attention got fix’d upon a single object which confounded

[053-009]all kind of reasoning upon him. 

[053-010]It was a tall figure of a philosophic, serious, adust look,

[053-011]which passed and repass’d sedately along the street, making

[053-012]a turn of about sixty paces on each side of the gate of the

[053-013]hotel;—the man was about fifty-two—had a small cane

[053-014]under his arm—was dress’d in a dark

[053-015]drab-colour’d coat, waistcoat, and breeches, which

[053-016]seem’d to have seen some years service:—they were

[053-017]still clean, and there was a little air of frugal propreté throughout him. By his pulling off

[053-018]his hat, and his attitude of accosting a good many in his way, I

[053-019]saw he was asking charity: so I got a sous or two out of my

[053-020]pocket ready to give him, as he took me in his turn.—He

[053-021]pass’d by me without asking anything—and yet did not

[053-022]go five steps further before he ask’d charity of a little

[053-023]woman.—I was much more likely to have given of the

[053-024]two.—He had scarce done with the woman, when he

[053-025]pull’d off his hat to another who was coming the same

[053-026]way.—An ancient gentleman came slowly—and, after

[053-027]him, a young smart one.—He let them both pass, and

[053-028]ask’d nothing. I stood observing him half an hour, in

[053-029]which time he had made a dozen turns backwards and forwards, and

[053-030]found that he invariably pursued the same plan. 

[053-031]There were two things very singular in this, which set my

[053-032]brain to work, and to no purpose:—the first was, why the

[053-033]man should only tell his story to the sex;—and,

[053-034]secondly,—what kind of story it was, and what species of

[053-035]eloquence it could be, which soften’d the hearts of the

[053-036]women, which he knew ’twas to no purpose to practise upon

[053-037]the men. 

[053-038]There were two other circumstances, which entangled this

[053-039]mystery;—the one was, he told every woman what he had to

[053-040]say in her ear, and in a way which had much more the air of a

[053-041]secret than a petition;—the other was, it was always

[053-042]successful.—He never stopp’d a woman, but she

[053-043]pull’d out her purse, and immediately gave him

[053-044]something. 

[053-045]I could form no system to explain the phenomenon. 

[053-046]I had got a riddle to amuse me for the rest of the evening; so

[053-047]I walk’d upstairs to my chamber.
