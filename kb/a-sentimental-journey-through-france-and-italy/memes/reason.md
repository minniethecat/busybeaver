# Reason

Reason in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* occupies a paradoxical position: it is constantly invoked, yet constantly shown to be inadequate. [[yorick|Yorick]] is a philosopher by training, but his philosophy repeatedly fails him in the presence of feeling. The novel's argument is not that reason is worthless, but that it is insufficient — a "bubble" that instinct, music, and sentiment can dissolve in an instant.

Sterne's sentimentalism is not anti-intellectual; it is anti-rationalist in the specific sense that it denies reason's claim to govern the heart.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century was the age of reason — and the age of reason's critics. The Enlightenment celebrated rational inquiry, empirical science, and the power of the human intellect to order the world.

But alongside this celebration ran a counter-current: Shaftesbury's moral sense, Hutcheson's benevolence, Hume's insistence that "reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions." Sterne belongs emphatically to this second tradition.

His novel is a sustained demonstration that the most important human transactions — love, pity, gratitude, forgiveness — are matters of feeling, not calculation. Reason can explain them afterward; it cannot generate them.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sterne states his position most directly in a passage about the impotence of rational deliberation: "man oftener does disquiet himself in vain by trusting the issue of his commotions to reason only." The "only" is crucial — reason is not rejected, but it cannot be the sole guide. This principle plays out in scene after scene.

When [[yorick|Yorick]]'s hand is kissed by the lady at [[calais|Calais]], he notes that "instinct more than reason directed me to the last resource in these dangers — to hold it loosely." Reason would have dictated resistance; instinct knew better.

The novel's most striking image for reason's fragility appears in the [[paris|Paris]] chapters, where [[yorick|Yorick]] recalls "an incident in my life, where the dissipated spirits, to which my reason had been a bubble, were so suddenly called home" by music. Reason is a bubble — a thin, iridescent membrane that a single true note can burst.

The image captures Sterne's entire epistemology: reason is real, but it is surface; the depths are feeling.

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s hiring of [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] makes the same point from a different angle: "I had never less reason to repent of the impulses which generally do determine me, than in regard to this fellow." Impulse, not reason, governs his most successful decisions.
