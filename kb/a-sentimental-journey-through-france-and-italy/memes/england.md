# England

**England** is the unspoken constant of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s homeland, the cultural baseline he carries with him across the Channel, and the silent comparator in every observation of French life. Though the novel unfolds entirely on continental soil, England is perpetually present in the traveler's consciousness.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

England functions in the novel as a set of ingrained habits and assumptions that [[france|France]] continually unsettles:

**The Implicit Comparison**: Every French custom [[yorick|Yorick]] observes is measured against English practice. At the [[paris|Paris]] theater, he notes that "in England we sit all at our ease" (Ch. 30, [[paris|Paris]]) — a passing observation that crystallizes a broader cultural contrast between English informality and French formality. The old French officer makes the same remark, noting "this, said [[the-old-french-officer|the old officer]], in England" (Ch. 30, [[paris|Paris]]), confirming that national character is a two-way mirror.

**The Home Left Behind**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey begins with his departure from England, and the novel's opening meditation — "They order, said I, this matter better in [[france|France]]" — immediately invokes the comparison. England is what he has left; [[france|France]] is what he has chosen. The journey is, in part, an experiment in whether leaving England makes one a better judge of England.

## 文化对比 (Cultural Contrast)

The England Sterne sketches through [[yorick|Yorick]]'s observations is a land of understatement, practicality, and emotional restraint — qualities that [[france|France]], with its theatrical bows and elaborate courtesy, simultaneously exposes and makes newly appreciable. The sentimental journey is, ultimately, a journey away from English reserve and toward the possibility of more open, spontaneous feeling — a possibility that [[france|France]], for all its contradictions, seems to offer.
