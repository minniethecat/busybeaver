# Embarrassment

Embarrassment is the emotional key in which much of *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is composed. [[yorick|Yorick]] is perpetually embarrassed — by a monk's silent appeal, by a lady's misinterpretation, by a fille de chambre's expectations, by the very architecture of a shared bedroom. This is not simply comic awkwardness; for Sterne, embarrassment is the revealing moment when social performance breaks down and something truer, if more uncomfortable, emerges.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century witnessed the invention of embarrassment as a distinct psychological category. Earlier periods had known "shame" (a moral emotion tied to sin) and "confusion" (a cognitive state), but "embarrassment" — from the French *embarras*, meaning obstacle or hindrance — came to describe the peculiarly modern experience of being tangled in social codes. The rise of politeness as a social ideal inevitably produced its shadow: the fear of failing at it. Sterne, writing at the intersection of sentimentalism and social satire, found in embarrassment a uniquely productive emotional register.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Embarrassment follows [[yorick|Yorick]] like a shadow. At [[calais|Calais]], the lady corrects his self-congratulation on their meeting: "she has embarrassed you by the adventure." At the glove shop, the shopkeeper's wife "mistaking my embarrassment" thinks he is haggling when he is actually trying to be more generous. At the concert, he must "beg pardon for the embarrassment I had given her" after blocking the Marquise's way. In the bookseller's shop, the entire comic misunderstanding with the fille de chambre turns on [[yorick|Yorick]]'s "embarrassment, which was the reason I had treated it so cavalierly."

The most sustained treatment comes when [[yorick|Yorick]] confesses his "little embarrassment" to the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], who asks: "And what is your embarrassment? let me hear it." The Count's gracious reception transforms embarrassment from a source of shame into a passport to intimacy. The shared bedroom scene in the [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnais]] pushes embarrassment to its physical limit — "I found myself as much embarrassed as it was possible the lady could be herself" — and the novel finds there its most delicate resolution.
