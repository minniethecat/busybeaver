# Bourbonnois

The **Bourbonnois** (*Bourbonnais*), a historic province in central [[france|France]] north of the Auvergne, appears in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) as the landscape through which [[yorick|Yorick]] travels en route from [[moulines|Moulines]] toward [[lyons|Lyons]] — a journey whose anticipated joys are overcast by the lingering memory of [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]].

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

The Bourbonnois is introduced twice, in two closely linked passages that form one of the novel's most concentrated meditations on the nature of [[feeling|Sensibility]]. [[yorick|Yorick]] first names it at the beginning of the [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] episode: he describes "the distress of plenty" — "to travel it through the Bourbonnois, the sweetest part of [[france|France]], — in the heyday of the vintage, when [[nature|Nature]] is pouring her abundance into every one's lap, and every eye is lifted up" (Ch. 63, [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]). The passage sets up a cruel contrast: the richest, most joyful season in the richest, most joyful province — and [[yorick|Yorick]]'s heart too burdened with the approaching encounter with [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] to receive any of it.

The second passage, under the chapter heading "THE BOURBONNNOIS" (Sterne's eccentric spelling), deepens this tension. "There was nothing from which I had painted out for my self so joyous a riot of the affections, as in this journey in the vintage, through this part of [[france|France]]; but pressing through this gate, of sorrow to it, my sufferings have totally unfitted me" (Ch. 65, The Bourbonnois). Every scene of festivity now contains [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] "in the background of the piece, sitting pensive under her poplar." [[yorick|Yorick]] confesses he "had got almost to [[lyons|Lyons]]" before he could cast a shade across her memory.

The Bourbonnois thus functions in the novel not as a described place but as an emotional landscape — a province transformed by feeling. Its vineyards and harvest festivals, which should have been the high point of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s French journey, become instead the setting for his most extended meditation on [[feeling|Sensibility]]: "Dear [[feeling|Sensibility]]! source inexhausted of all that's precious in our joys, or costly in our sorrows!" (Ch. 65, The Bourbonnois). The apostrophe to [[feeling|Sensibility]] that fills the Bourbonnois chapter is arguably the novel's philosophical centre — and it is occasioned by a place [[yorick|Yorick]] can barely bring himself to see.
