# Brittany

**Brittany** (*Bretagne*), an ancient province and former duchy in northwestern [[france|France]], appears in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) as the ancestral home of the [[marquis-de|Marquis d'E—]] and the legal backdrop against which one of the novel's most fully realised embedded narratives — THE SWORD — unfolds.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

Brittany enters the narrative in the [[rennes|Rennes]] episode, where Sterne pauses to tell the story of the [[marquis-de|Marquis d'E—]], a Breton nobleman whose house "in Brittany" has fallen into gradual decay (Ch. 66, The Sword). The Marquis has two sons who look to him for guidance, but his sword — the traditional instrument of noble advancement — "could not open the way" and "the mounting was too expensive." Commerce is the only remaining path.

Yet Sterne adds a crucial legal detail that turns the story from tragedy to redemption: "In any other province in [[france|France]], save Brittany, this was smiting the root for ever of the little tree his pride and affection wish'd to see re-blossom" (Ch. 66, The Sword). Brittany, alone among French provinces, possessed an ancient ducal law permitting nobles to deposit their swords, take up trade, and later reclaim their rank once their fortunes were restored. The Marquis avails himself of this provision, surrendering his sword to the court at [[rennes|Rennes]] — where the states of Brittany were then assembled — before embarking with his family for Martinico.

The narrative function of Brittany in the novel is thus juridical: it is the province whose distinctive legal tradition makes the Marquis's story possible, transforming what elsewhere would be irreversible social degradation into temporary, honourable exile. After "nineteen or twenty years of successful application to business," the Marquis returns home to reclaim both his sword and his nobility — a restoration that would have been legally impossible in any other French province.
