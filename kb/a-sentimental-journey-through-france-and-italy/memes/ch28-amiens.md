## AMIENS.

[028-001]Fortune had not smiled upon La

[028-002]Fleur; for he had been unsuccessful in his feats of

[028-003]chivalry,—and not one thing had offered to signalise his

[028-004]zeal for my service from the time that he had entered into it,

[028-005]which was almost four-and-twenty hours. The poor soul

[028-006]burn’d with impatience; and the Count de L—’s

[028-007]servant coming with the letter, being the first practicable

[028-008]occasion which offer’d, La Fleur had laid hold of it; and,

[028-009]in order to do honour to his master, had taken him into a back

[028-010]parlour in the auberge, and treated him with a cup or two of the

[028-011]best wine in Picardy; and the Count de L—’s servant,

[028-012]in return, and not to be behindhand in politeness with La Fleur,

[028-013]had taken him back with him to the Count’s hotel. La

[028-014]Fleur’s prevenancy (for there was a passport in his

[028-015]very looks) soon set every servant in the kitchen at ease with

[028-016]him; and as a Frenchman, whatever be his talents, has no sort of

[028-017]prudery in showing them, La Fleur, in less than five minutes, had

[028-018]pulled out his fife, and leading off the dance himself with the

[028-019]first note, set the fille de chambre , the maître

[028-020]d’hôtel , the cook, the scullion, and all the

[028-021]house-hold, dogs and cats, besides an old monkey, a dancing: I

[028-022]suppose there never was a merrier kitchen since the flood. 

[028-023]Madame de L—, in passing from her brother’s

[028-024]apartments to her own, hearing so much jollity below stairs, rung

[028-025]up her fille de chambre to ask about it; and, hearing it

[028-026]was the English gentleman’s servant, who had set the whole

[028-027]house merry with his pipe, she ordered him up. 

[028-028]As the poor fellow could not present himself empty, he had

[028-029]loaded himself in going up stairs with a thousand compliments to

[028-030]Madame de L—, on the part of his master,—added a long

[028-031]apocrypha of inquiries after Madame de L—’s

[028-032]health,—told her, that Monsieur his master was au

[028-033]désespoire for her re-establishment from the fatigues

[028-034]of her journey,—and, to close all, that Monsieur had

[028-035]received the letter which Madame had done him the honour—And

[028-036]he has done me the honour, said Madame de L—, interrupting

[028-037]La Fleur, to send a billet in return. 

[028-038]Madame de L— had said this with such a tone of reliance

[028-039]upon the fact, that La Fleur had not power to disappoint her

[028-040]expectations;—he trembled for my honour,—and possibly

[028-041]might not altogether be unconcerned for his own, as a man capable

[028-042]of being attached to a master who could be wanting en

[028-043]égards vis à vis d’une femme ! so that

[028-044]when Madame de L— asked La Fleur if he had brought a

[028-045]letter,— O qu’oui , said La Fleur: so laying

[028-046]down his hat upon the ground, and taking hold of the flap of his

[028-047]right side pocket with his left hand, he began to search for the

[028-048]letter with his right;—then

[028-049]contrariwise.— Diable ! then sought every

[028-050]pocket—pocket by pocket, round, not forgetting his

[028-051]fob:— Peste !—then La Fleur emptied them upon

[028-052]the floor,—pulled out a dirty cravat,—a

[028-053]handkerchief,—a comb,—a whip lash,—a

[028-054]nightcap,—then gave a peep into his hat,— Quelle

[028-055]étourderie ! He had left the letter upon the

[028-056]table in the auberge;—he would run for it, and be back with

[028-057]it in three minutes. 

[028-058]I had just finished my supper when La Fleur came in to give me

[028-059]an account of his adventure: he told the whole story simply as it

[028-060]was: and only added that if Monsieur had forgot ( par

[028-061]hazard ) to answer Madame’s letter, the arrangement gave

[028-062]him an opportunity to recover the faux pas ;—and if

[028-063]not, that things were only as they were. 

[028-064]Now I was not altogether sure of my étiquette ,

[028-065]whether I ought to have wrote or no;—but if I had,—a

[028-066]devil himself could not have been angry: ’twas but the

[028-067]officious zeal of a well meaning creature for my honour; and,

[028-068]however he might have mistook the road,—or embarrassed me

[028-069]in so doing,—his heart was in no fault,—I was under

[028-070]no necessity to write;—and, what weighed more than

[028-071]all,—he did not look as if he had done amiss. 

[028-072]—’Tis all very well, La Fleur, said

[028-073]I.—’Twas sufficient. La Fleur flew out of the

[028-074]room like lightning, and returned with pen, ink, and paper, in

[028-075]his hand; and, coming up to the table, laid them close before me,

[028-076]with such a delight in his countenance, that I could not help

[028-077]taking up the pen. 

[028-078]I began and began again; and, though I had nothing to say, and that

[028-079]nothing might have been expressed in half a dozen lines, I made

[028-080]half a dozen different beginnings, and could no way please

[028-081]myself. 

[028-082]In short, I was in no mood to write. 

[028-083]La Fleur stepp’d out and brought a little water in a

[028-084]glass to dilute my ink,—then fetch’d sand and

[028-085]seal-wax.—It was all one; I wrote, and blotted, and tore

[028-086]off, and burnt, and wrote again.— Le diable

[028-087]l’emporte ! said I, half to myself,—I cannot write

[028-088]this self-same letter, throwing the pen down despairingly as I

[028-089]said it. 

[028-090]As soon as I had cast down my pen, La Fleur advanced with the

[028-091]most respectful carriage up to the table, and making a thousand

[028-092]apologies for the liberty he was going to take, told me he had a

[028-093]letter in his pocket wrote by a drummer in his regiment to a

[028-094]corporal’s wife, which he durst say would suit the

[028-095]occasion. 

[028-096]I had a mind to let the poor fellow have his

[028-097]humour.—Then prithee, said I, let me see it. 

[028-098]La Fleur instantly pulled out a little dirty pocket book

[028-099]cramm’d full of small letters and billet-doux in a sad

[028-100]condition, and laying it upon the table, and then untying the

[028-101]string which held them all together, run them over, one by one,

[028-102]till he came to the letter in question,— La voila !

[028-103]said he, clapping his hands: so, unfolding it first, he laid it

[028-104]open before me, and retired three steps from the table whilst I

[028-105]read it.
