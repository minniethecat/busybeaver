# Honour

Honour in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* operates at the intersection of social reputation and personal integrity. Sterne explores the gap between the outward performance of honour — the codes of politeness, gallantry, and social standing — and the inward sentiment that constitutes true moral worth.

## 定义与历史语境

In eighteenth-century Europe, honour was a complex social currency. Aristocratic honour demanded the defense of reputation through dueling and public display; bourgeois honour increasingly emphasized commercial trustworthiness and domestic virtue. Sterne's novel participates in the sentimental redefinition of honour — relocating it from external reputation to internal feeling. The sentimental hero is honourable not because he fights duels, but because his heart is tender.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The word "honour" recurs throughout the novel in contexts ranging from gallant social formulas ("he had had the honour to be at the bombardment of it") to genuine moral concern. The most significant treatment comes in the [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]] episode, where [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s companion "trembled for my honour" — a moment that exposes how the concept simultaneously protects and constrains human relations.

[[yorick|Yorick]] himself navigates honour carefully: he wishes to give "no ill impression of myself to a man of honour" while simultaneously recognizing that excessive concern for reputation can obstruct genuine sentiment. The novel ultimately suggests that true honour resides in the heart's response, not in social performance.

## 关键段落

> "giving an ill impression of myself to a man of honour, and lying under the restraint of such a reflection all my life." (Calais chapters)

> "He had had the honour, he said, to be at the bombardment of it." (Paris chapters)

> "he trembled for my honour, — and possibly for his own." (Madame de L— episode)

> "the officious zeal of a well meaning creature for my honour." (Madame de L— episode)
