# The Barber

**The Barber** is a [[paris|Paris]]ian periwig-maker who appears briefly in the chapter titled "The Wig" during [[yorick|Yorick]]'s first days in [[paris|Paris]]. Though his role is confined to a single transaction, his grandiloquent turn of phrase — comparing the durability of a wig buckle to surviving immersion in the ocean — triggers one of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s most extended meditations on national character and the French sublime (031-001).

## Role Overview

The barber is summoned by [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] at [[yorick|Yorick]]'s request when [[yorick|Yorick]] arrives in [[paris|Paris]] and needs attention to his wig. Upon arrival, the barber "absolutely refused to have any thing to do with my wig: 'twas either above or below his art" (031-002). He insists instead that [[yorick|Yorick]] take a ready-made replacement of his own recommendation. This commercial assertiveness, combined with the barber's rhetorical extravagance, provides Sterne with material for a comic yet philosophically serious analysis of the differences between French and English modes of expression.

## Appearances

The encounter takes place in [[yorick|Yorick]]'s lodgings in [[paris|Paris]]. When the barber presents his recommended wig, [[yorick|Yorick]] expresses concern about the buckle: "But I fear, friend! said I, this buckle won't stand" (031-003). The barber's reply is immediate and magnificent: "You may emerge it into the ocean, and it will stand."

This single sentence sets [[yorick|Yorick]]'s mind in motion. He reflects on the scale of the barber's comparison: "What a great scale is every thing upon in this city thought I." An English periwig-maker, [[yorick|Yorick]] reasons, would have gone no further than to say the buckle could be "dipped into a pail of water." The difference between the ocean and a pail is — as [[yorick|Yorick]] puts it — "like Time to Eternity!" (031-004).

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s meditation deepens. He confesses his love of grand conceptions and his hatred of "cold conceptions" and "puny ideas." Yet he qualifies his admiration: in this instance of the French sublime, "the grandeur is more in the *word*, and less in the *thing*" (031-005). The ocean fills the mind with vast ideas, but [[paris|Paris]] is so far inland that nobody would actually ride a hundred miles to test the buckle's durability in seawater. The pail of water, standing in the next room, has the advantage of being immediately verifiable.

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s final judgment on the matter — "The French expression professes more than it performs" (031-006) — encapsulates a theory of national character derived from this trivial commercial exchange. He concludes that he can "see the precise and distinguishing marks of national characters more in these nonsensical minutiæ than in the most important matters of state" (031-007).

The barber takes so long that by the time [[yorick|Yorick]] is dressed, it is too late to deliver his letter to [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] that night, setting in motion the evening walk that leads to further adventures.

## Character and Themes

The barber exemplifies Sterne's method of extracting grand philosophical observations from the most mundane encounters. From a two-minute conversation about a wig buckle, [[yorick|Yorick]] derives a theory of the French sublime, a comparative analysis of English and French rhetoric, and a principle about how national character is best discerned in trivial details rather than in affairs of state.

The barber himself remains opaque — we learn nothing of his person, his history, or his inner life. He exists entirely as the occasion for [[yorick|Yorick]]'s reflection. This is characteristic of Sterne's minor figures: they are vivid but incomplete, sketched with a few strokes that convey a social type without pretending to full characterization.
