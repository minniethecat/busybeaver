# Heaven

Heaven in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is less a theological doctrine than a pervasive presence — invoked in oaths and prayers, appealed to as witness and judge, and held out as the ultimate horizon against which earthly conduct is measured.

Sterne's characters call upon heaven with an intimacy that blurs the line between the sacred and the everyday: it is "Just Heaven!" in moments of moral indignation, "Gracious Heaven!" at turns of fortune, and "heaven be their resource" for those whom worldly charity has abandoned.

## 定义与历史语境

In eighteenth-century Britain, heaven occupied a complex position in public discourse. Orthodox Christianity taught heaven as the reward for virtue; deists and freethinkers questioned its particulars while retaining a general providentialism; and sentimental writers increasingly treated heaven as a matter of feeling rather than doctrine. Sterne, an Anglican clergyman, drew on all three currents.

His sermons preached a conventional heaven; his fiction let heaven circulate through the speech of monks, peasants, soldiers, and sentimental travelers alike, becoming a shared vocabulary that transcended sectarian division.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Heaven appears in the novel across the full social spectrum.

The monk at [[calais|Calais]] invokes it as the dispenser of his poverty's meaning: "heaven be their resource who have no other but the charity of the world." [[yorick|Yorick]], in a patriotic vein, swears "there is no nation under heaven abounding with more variety of learning" than [[calais|France]] — enlisting heaven as rhetorical intensifier for a worldly compliment.

The old German beggar at [[montreuil|Montreuil]] attributes the survival of his last son to heaven's direct intervention: "heaven had accepted the conditions." And at the novel's most dramatic moment, upon learning he may be imprisoned in [[paris|Paris]], [[yorick|Yorick]] cries "Gracious Heaven! ... exiled!" — the exclamation marking the collapse of his sentimental composure into genuine fear.

Sterne's most theologically pointed use of heaven comes in the meditation on [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] and [[mundungus|Mundungus]]: "were the happiest mansion in heaven to be allotted to [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] and [[mundungus|Mundungus]], they would be so far from being happy,

that the souls of [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] and [[mundungus|Mundungus]] would do penance there to all eternity." Heaven here is not merely reward but a test of character — those without the sentimental faculty cannot enjoy it, even were it granted.

## 关键段落

> "Heaven be their resource who have no other but the charity of the world, the stock of which, I fear, is no way sufficient for the many great claims which are hourly made upon it." (003-004)

> "There is no nation under heaven — and God is my record (before whose tribunal I must one day come and give an account of this work) — that I do not speak it vauntingly — but there is no nation under heaven abounding with more variety of learning." (Paris chapters)

> "It had pleased heaven, he said, to bless him with three sons, the finest lads in Germany; but having in one week lost two of the eldest of them by the small-pox ... he made a vow, if heaven would not take him from him also, he would go in gratitude to St. Iago." (025-028)

> "Gracious Heaven! cried I, kneeling down upon the last step but one in my ascent — 'grant me only health, thou great Bestower of it, and give me but this fair goddess as my companion — and shower down thy mitres, if it seem good unto thy divine providence, upon those heads which are aching for them.'" (040-111)

> "Peace be to them! if it is to be found; but heaven itself, were it possible to get there with such tempers, would want objects to give it; every gentle spirit would come flying upon the wing of heaven to welcome such a man." (On Smelfungus and Mundungus)
