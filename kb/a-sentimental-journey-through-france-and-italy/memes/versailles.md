# Versailles

**Versailles** is the royal palace town some twelve miles west of [[paris|Paris]], where [[yorick|Yorick]] travels to obtain a passport from the [[duc-de-c|Duc de C—]]. Occupying chapters 42 through 50 of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), Versailles serves as the stage for the novel's most concentrated engagement with power, status, and the absurdities of court life.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, Versailles was the seat of the French court under Louis XV — a self-contained world of ceremony, intrigue, and rigid hierarchy, physically separated from [[paris|Paris]]. For an English traveler like [[yorick|Yorick]], who enters without letters of introduction and armed only with a set of Shakespeare, Versailles represents the ultimate test of his ability to navigate French society through sentiment rather than credentials.

## 在小说中的角色

Versailles functions as the novel's threshold between private sentiment and public power. [[yorick|Yorick]] arrives with a practical problem — he lacks a passport and has been warned by his [[paris|Paris]] landlord that he risks the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] — but the Versailles chapters transform this bureaucratic errand into a series of encounters that dramatize Sterne's themes of identity, status, and the performance of self.

Three events define the Versailles sequence:

**The Road to Versailles (Ch. 42–43)**. En route, [[yorick|Yorick]] rehearses the "mean plans of dirty address" his "servile heart" forms when approaching power, then recoils at his own cowardice. "Go to the [[duc-de-c|Duc de C—]] with the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] in thy looks," he rebukes himself — "my life for it, thou wilt be sent back to [[paris|Paris]] in half an hour with an escort" (043-003). This inner debate between self-abasement and self-respect is resolved when [[yorick|Yorick]] decides to approach the Duke with "gaiety and debonairness" rather than supplication.

**Shakespeare as Introduction (Ch. 46–47)**. At the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]]'s apartment, [[yorick|Yorick]] transforms a set of Shakespeare's works into an improbable letter of introduction. Laying his finger on the name "[[yorick|Yorick]]" in Hamlet's gravedigger scene, he presents himself with the single phrase *"Me voici!"* — "Here I am!" (047-003). The gesture is pure Sterne: identity established not by title or rank, but by literary association and wit.

**The [[chevalier-de-st-louis|Chevalier de St. Louis]] (Ch. 48–50)**. On a Versailles street, [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters an old officer wearing the Cross of St. Louis while selling pâtés from a basket — a living emblem of the reversal of fortune. The Chevalier's story of military service rewarded with poverty, and the king's eventual grant of a pension of 1500 livres, forms one of the novel's most complete sentimental arcs. The Versailles setting is essential: only in the shadow of the palace does the contrast between service and neglect register with full ironic force.
