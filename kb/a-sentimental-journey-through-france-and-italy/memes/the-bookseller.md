# The Bookseller

**The Bookseller** is a [[paris|Paris]]ian shopkeeper whose establishment at the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]] serves as the setting for one of the most delicately rendered encounters in Sterne's novel. Though a minor figure, he facilitates two consequential meetings: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s purchase of Shakespeare, which leads to the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], and the encounter with the fille de chambre, which becomes one of the novel's exemplary scenes of sentimental exchange (038-001).

## Role Overview

The bookseller appears in the chapter "[[the-fille-de-chambre|The Fille de Chambre]]" during [[yorick|Yorick]]'s stay in [[paris|Paris]]. Prompted by [[the-old-french-officer|the old French officer]]'s advice — which recalls Polonius's counsel to Laertes and thereby sets [[yorick|Yorick]] thinking of *Hamlet* and the complete works of Shakespeare — [[yorick|Yorick]] stops at the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]] to purchase the set (038-002). The bookseller's role, though functional, is drawn with Sterne's characteristic attention to national character: he is civil, commercially obliging, and serves as an informal herald of [[count-de-b|Count de B—]]'s enlightened tastes.

## Appearances

[[yorick|Yorick]] enters the bookseller's shop and asks for a complete set of Shakespeare. The bookseller replies that he has not a set in the world — whereupon [[yorick|Yorick]], with comic incredulity ("Comment!"), picks up a volume from a set lying on the counter between them. The bookseller explains that these volumes have been sent to him only to be bound, and are to be returned to [[versailles|Versailles]] the following morning — to the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] (038-003).

When [[yorick|Yorick]] asks whether the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] reads Shakespeare, the bookseller's reply — "C'est un esprit fort" — reveals not only the Count's intellectual reputation but the bookseller's own capacity for discerning praise. He adds, with what [[yorick|Yorick]] recognizes as genuine civility, that the Count "loves English books! and what is more to his honour, Monsieur, he loves the English too" (038-004). This courteous remark so pleases [[yorick|Yorick]] that he feels obliged to spend a louis d'or or two at the shop.

At that moment, a young woman of about twenty enters — the fille de chambre to [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] — seeking a copy of *Les Égarements du Cœur et de l'Esprit*. The bookseller gives her the book directly, the transaction is completed, and [[yorick|Yorick]] and the young woman leave the shop together, beginning the walk along the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]] that becomes one of the novel's most celebrated passages (038-005).

The bookseller is mentioned once more, later in the narrative, when the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] recognizes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s name from "the bookseller at the Quai de Conti" who had spoken of the Englishman who wanted Shakespeare (044-001).

## Character and Themes

The bookseller represents the commercial civility that Sterne associates with [[paris|Paris]]ian life. Unlike the grasping or indifferent tradesmen who populate other eighteenth-century narratives, he is competent, obliging, and provides the occasion for generosity rather than exploitation. His casual mention of [[count-de-b|Count de B—]]'s love of English books sets in motion the chain of events — the passport, the supper, the case of delicacy — that structure the latter portion of the novel.

The bookseller's shop also functions as a liminal space where social boundaries dissolve: here [[yorick|Yorick]] the English traveller, the fille de chambre of a devout woman of fashion, and (by reputation) the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] all intersect through their shared relationship to books. In this, Sterne suggests the democratizing power of literary culture.
