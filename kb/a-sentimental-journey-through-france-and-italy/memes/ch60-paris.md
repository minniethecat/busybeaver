## PARIS.

[060-001]The man who either disdains or

[060-002]fears to walk up a dark entry may be an excellent good man, and

[060-003]fit for a hundred things, but he will not do to make a good

[060-004]Sentimental Traveller.—I count little of the many things I

[060-005]see pass at broad noonday, in large and open

[060-006]streets.—Nature is shy, and hates to act before spectators;

[060-007]but in such an unobserved corner you sometimes see a single short

[060-008]scene of hers worth all the sentiments of a dozen French plays

[060-009]compounded together,—and yet they are absolutely

[060-010]fine;—and whenever I have a more brilliant affair upon my

[060-011]hands than common, as they suit a preacher just as well as a

[060-012]hero, I generally make my sermon out of ’em;—and for

[060-013]the text,—“Cappadocia, Pontus and Asia, Phrygia and

[060-014]Pamphylia,”—is as good as any one in the Bible. 

[060-015]There is a long dark passage issuing out from the Opera

[060-016]Comique into a narrow street; ’tis trod by a few who humbly

[060-017]wait for a fiacre , [649] or wish to get off

[060-018]quietly o’foot when the opera is done. At the end

[060-019]of it, towards the theatre, ’tis lighted by a small candle,

[060-020]the light of which is almost lost before you get half-way down,

[060-021]but near the door—’tis more for ornament than use:

[060-022]you see it as a fixed star of the least magnitude; it

[060-023]burns,—but does little good to the world, that we know

[060-024]of. 

[060-025]In returning along this passage, I discerned, as I approached

[060-026]within five or six paces of the door, two ladies standing

[060-027]arm-in-arm with their backs against the wall, waiting, as I

[060-028]imagined, for a fiacre ;—as they were next the door,

[060-029]I thought they had a prior right; so edged myself up within a

[060-030]yard or little more of them, and quietly took my stand.—I

[060-031]was in black, and scarce seen. 

[060-032]The lady next me was a tall lean figure of a woman, of about

[060-033]thirty-six; the other of the same size and make, of about forty:

[060-034]there was no mark of wife or widow in any one part of either of

[060-035]them;—they seem’d to be two upright vestal sisters,

[060-036]unsapped by caresses, unbroke in upon by tender

[060-037]salutations.—I could have wish’d to have made them

[060-038]happy:—their happiness was destin’d that night, to

[060-039]come from another quarter. 

[060-040]A low voice, with a good turn of expression, and sweet cadence

[060-041]at the end of it, begg’d for a twelve-sous piece betwixt

[060-042]them, for the love of heaven. I thought it singular that a

[060-043]beggar should fix the quota of an alms—and that the sum

[060-044]should be twelve times as much as what is usually given in the

[060-045]dark.—They both seemed astonished at it as much as

[060-046]myself.—Twelve sous! said one.—A twelve-sous piece!

[060-047]said the other,—and made no reply. 

[060-048]The poor man said, he knew not how to ask less of ladies of

[060-049]their rank; and bow’d down his head to the ground. 

[060-050]Poo! said they,—we have no money. 

[060-051]The beggar remained silent for a moment or two, and

[060-052]renew’d his supplication. 

[060-053]—Do not, my fair young ladies, said he, stop your good

[060-054]ears against me.—Upon my word, honest man! said the

[060-055]younger, we have no change.—Then God bless you, said the

[060-056]poor man, and multiply those joys which you can give to others

[060-057]without change!—I observed the elder sister put her hand

[060-058]into her pocket.—I’ll see, said she, if I have a

[060-059]sous. A sous! give twelve, said the supplicant; Nature has

[060-060]been bountiful to you, be bountiful to a poor man. 

[060-061]—I would friend, with all my heart, said the younger, if

[060-062]I had it. 

[060-063]My fair charitable! said he, addressing himself to the

[060-064]elder,—what is it but your goodness and humanity which

[060-065]makes your bright eyes so sweet, that they outshine the morning

[060-066]even in this dark passage? and what was it which made the Marquis

[060-067]de Santerre and his brother say so much of you both as they just

[060-068]passed by? 

[060-069]The two ladies seemed much affected; and impulsively, at the

[060-070]same time they both put their hands into their pocket, and each

[060-071]took out a twelve-sous piece. 

[060-072]The contest betwixt them and the poor supplicant was no

[060-073]more;—it was continued betwixt themselves, which of the two

[060-074]should give the twelve-sous piece in charity;—and, to end

[060-075]the dispute, they both gave it together, and the man went

[060-076]away.
