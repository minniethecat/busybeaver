# Montreuil

**Montreuil** is the French town on the road from [[calais|Calais]] to [[paris|Paris]] where [[yorick|Yorick]] hires [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] as his servant. Occupying chapters 18 through 21 of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), Montreuil is the novel's most consequential roadside stop — the place where [[yorick|Yorick]] acquires the companion who will accompany him through every subsequent adventure.

## 地理与文化背景

Montreuil (Montreuil-sur-Mer) was a natural stopping point on the 18th-century coach route between [[calais|Calais]] and [[paris|Paris]], roughly a day's journey from the coast. Its inns catered to English travelers making the crossing, and its landlords had learned to profit from their needs — including the universal English need for a French-speaking servant.

## 在小说中的角色

Montreuil serves as the stage for three connected events that shape the remainder of the journey:

**The Lost Portmanteau and the Discovery of Need (Ch. 18)**. [[yorick|Yorick]] arrives at Montreuil in a state of comic disarray — he has "once lost my portmanteau from behind my chaise, and twice got out in the rain, and one of the times up to the knees in dirt, to help the postilion to tie it on" (018-001). It is only when the landlord asks if he wants a servant that [[yorick|Yorick]] realizes "that was the very thing." The landlord's recommendation — "they are so generous, said the landlord. — I'll be shot if this is not a livre out of my pocket, quoth I to myself, this very night" (018-002) — captures the commercialized hospitality of the French coaching inn.

**The Hiring of [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] (Ch. 19)**. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] enters, and [[yorick|Yorick]] is won over at first sight: "the genuine look and air of the fellow determined the matter at once in his favour; so I hired him first, — and then began to enquire what he could do" (019-002). The comic inversion — hiring before interviewing — is characteristic of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s impulsive sentimental method. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], it transpires, "could do nothing in the world but beat a drum, and play a march or two upon the fife" (019-003). Yet [[yorick|Yorick]] is "hourly recompensed by the festivity of his temper" (020-002), and [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] becomes the journey's most constant and beloved presence.

**[[janatone|Janatone]] (Ch. 20)**. The landlord's daughter, [[janatone|Janatone]], appears briefly in the Montreuil chapters — a "fair, brown, girl of five-and-twenty" who serves at the inn. Sterne sketches her in a few deft strokes, creating one of the novel's many fleeting but memorable minor characters. She represents the unspoken attractions that every roadside stop potentially offers.

**The Departure (Ch. 21)**. When [[yorick|Yorick]] prepares to leave Montreuil, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] is surrounded by "half a dozen wenches" taking their leave — "[[la-fleur|La Fleur]] kissed all their hands round and round again, and thrice he wiped his eyes, and thrice he promised he would bring them all pardons from [[rome|Rome]]" (021-002). The scene encapsulates [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s character: affectionate, theatrical, and universally beloved.
