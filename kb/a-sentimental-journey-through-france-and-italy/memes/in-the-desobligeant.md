# In the Desobligeant

The "In the Desobligeant" episode from [[sentimentalism|Laurence Sterne]]'s [[a-sentimental-journey|A Sentimental Journey]] finds [[yorick]] seated alone in his hired [[the-desobligeant|desobligeant]] carriage in [[calais]], reflecting on the nature of travel, human character, and the advantages of solitary confinement for the [[sentimentalism|sentimental]] observer.

## Historical Context

The [[the-desobligeant|desobligeant]] was a French one-person carriage popular in the 18th century, its name suggesting that it "dis-obliged" the traveler from carrying companions. For [[yorick]], this enforced solitude becomes a philosophical advantage, allowing him to observe and reflect without distraction.

## Narrative

[[yorick]] begins with a mock-philosophical observation attributed to "peripatetic philosophers" — that nature has established certain boundaries and thresholds which, once crossed, change a man's character irrevocably. He applies this principle to his own situation: having crossed the English Channel and arrived in [[calais]], he feels himself a different man, freed from English prejudices.

Seated in the [[the-desobligeant|desobligeant]], [[yorick]] debates whether to hire a servant or continue alone. The cramped quarters of the carriage mirror the constraint of philosophical categories, and [[yorick]]'s meditation ranges across topics of [[liberty]], social obligation, and the nature of true [[travel]].

The chapter establishes the novel's central conceit — that the [[sentimentalism|sentimental]] traveler finds meaning not in monuments and guidebook sights but in encounters with fellow human beings and the reflections they provoke.

## Themes

The episode introduces key themes of the novel: the transformatory power of [[travel]], the superiority of [[sentimentalism|sentimental]] observation over conventional tourism, and the philosophical productivity of solitude. The [[the-desobligeant|desobligeant]] itself becomes a symbol of the narrator's chosen isolation and independence.
