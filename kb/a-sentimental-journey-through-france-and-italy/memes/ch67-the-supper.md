## THE SUPPER.

[067-001]A shoe coming loose from the fore

[067-002]foot of the thill-horse, at the beginning of the ascent of mount

[067-003]Taurira, the postilion dismounted, twisted the shoe off, and put

[067-004]it in his pocket; as the ascent was of five or six miles, and

[067-005]that horse our main dependence, I made a point of having the shoe

[067-006]fastened on again, as well as we could; but the postilion had

[067-007]thrown away the nails, and the hammer in the chaise box being of

[067-008]no great use without them, I submitted to go on. 

[067-009]He had not mounted half a mile higher, when, coming to a flinty

[067-010]piece of road, the poor devil lost a second shoe, and from off

[067-011]his other fore foot. I then got out of the chaise in good

[067-012]earnest; and seeing a house about a quarter of a mile to the left

[067-013]hand, with a great deal to do I prevailed upon the postilion to

[067-014]turn up to it. The look of the house, and of every thing

[067-015]about it, as we drew nearer, soon reconciled me to the

[067-016]disaster.—It was a little farm-house, surrounded with about

[067-017]twenty acres of vineyard, about as much corn;—and close to

[067-018]the house, on one side, was a potagerie of an acre and a

[067-019]half, full of everything which could make plenty in a French

[067-020]peasant’s house;—and, on the other side, was a little

[067-021]wood, which furnished wherewithal to dress it. It was about

[067-022]eight in the evening when I got to the house—so I left the

[067-023]postilion to manage his point as he could;—and, for mine, I

[067-024]walked directly into the house. 

[067-025]The family consisted of an old grey-headed man and his wife,

[067-026]with five or six sons and sons-in-law, and their several wives,

[067-027]and a joyous genealogy out of them. 

[067-028]They were all sitting down together to their lentil-soup; a

[067-029]large wheaten loaf was in the middle of the table; and a flagon

[067-030]of wine at each end of it promised joy through the stages of the

[067-031]repast:—’twas a feast of love. 

[067-032]The old man rose up to meet me, and with a respectful

[067-033]cordiality would have me sit down at the table; my heart was set

[067-034]down the moment I enter’d the room; so I sat down at once

[067-035]like a son of the family; and to invest myself in the character

[067-036]as speedily as I could, I instantly borrowed the old man’s

[067-037]knife, and taking up the loaf, cut myself a hearty luncheon; and,

[067-038]as I did it, I saw a testimony in every eye, not only of an

[067-039]honest welcome, but of a welcome mix’d with thanks that I

[067-040]had not seem’d to doubt it. 

[067-041]Was it this? or tell me, Nature, what else it was that made

[067-042]this morsel so sweet,—and to what magic I owe it, that the

[067-043]draught I took of their flagon was so delicious with it, that

[067-044]they remain upon my palate to this hour? 

[067-045]If the supper was to my taste,—the grace which followed

[067-046]it was much more so.
