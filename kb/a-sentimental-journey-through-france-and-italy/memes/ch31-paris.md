## PARIS.

[031-001]When the barber came, he absolutely

[031-002]refused to have any thing to do with my wig: ’twas either

[031-003]above or below his art: I had nothing to do but to take one ready

[031-004]made of his own recommendation. 

[031-005]—But I fear, friend! said I, this buckle won’t

[031-006]stand.—You may emerge it, replied he, into the ocean, and

[031-007]it will stand.— 

[031-008]What a great scale is every thing upon in this city thought

[031-009]I.—The utmost stretch of an English periwig-maker’s

[031-010]ideas could have gone no further than to have “dipped it

[031-011]into a pail of water.”—What difference! ’tis

[031-012]like Time to Eternity! 

[031-013]I confess I do hate all cold conceptions, as I do the puny

[031-014]ideas which engender them; and am generally so struck with the

[031-015]great works of nature, that for my own part, if I could help it,

[031-016]I never would make a comparison less than a mountain at

[031-017]least. All that can be said against the French sublime, in

[031-018]this instance of it, is this:—That the grandeur is more in the word , and less in the thing . No doubt, the ocean fills the mind with vast

[031-019]ideas; but Paris being so far inland, it was not likely I should

[031-020]run post a hundred miles out of it, to try the

[031-021]experiment;—the Parisian barber meant nothing.— 

[031-022]The pail of water standing beside the great deep, makes, certainly,

[031-023]but a sorry figure in speech;—but, ’twill be

[031-024]said,—it has one advantage—’tis in the next

[031-025]room, and the truth of the buckle may be tried in it, without

[031-026]more ado, in a single moment. 

[031-027]In honest truth, and upon a more candid revision of the

[031-028]matter, The French expression professes more than it

[031-029]performs . 

[031-030]I think I can see the precise and distinguishing marks of

[031-031]national characters more in these nonsensical minutiæ than in the most important matters of state;

[031-032]where great men of all nations talk and stalk so much alike, that

[031-033]I would not give ninepence to choose amongst them. 

[031-034]I was so long in getting from under my barber’s hands,

[031-035]that it was too late to think of going with my letter to Madame

[031-036]R— that night: but when a man is once dressed at all points

[031-037]for going out, his reflections turn to little account; so taking

[031-038]down the name of the Hôtel de Modene, where I lodged, I

[031-039]walked forth without any determination where to go;—I shall

[031-040]consider of that, said I, as I walk along.
