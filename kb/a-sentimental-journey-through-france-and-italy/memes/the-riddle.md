# The Riddle

**The Riddle** is an enigmatic episode in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occurring in chapter 53. Leaving the hand of the *fille de chambre*, [[yorick|Yorick]] lingers at the gate of his [[paris|Paris]] hotel and becomes transfixed by a mysterious beggar — a man who asks charity only from women, always whispers his plea in their ear, and is never refused. The episode is one of the novel's most teasing unsolved mysteries.

## 概要

[[yorick|Yorick]] cannot go straight back to his chamber — it would be "touching a cold key with a flat third to it upon the close of a piece of music, which had call'd forth my affections." He remains at the hotel gate, watching the passers-by, until "my attention got fix'd upon a single object which confounded all kind of reasoning upon him."

The object is a man of about fifty-two — "a tall figure of a philosophic, serious, adust look" (053-010) — who paces sedately along the street, making "a turn of about sixty paces on each side of the gate." He carries a small cane, wears a dark drab-coloured coat that has "seen some years service," and has "a little air of frugal *propreté* throughout him." He is asking charity.

[[yorick|Yorick]] gets a sou or two ready — but the man passes him by without asking. Yet he does not go five steps further before asking charity of "a little woman." [[yorick|Yorick]] observes him for half an hour, in which time the man makes "a dozen turns backwards and forwards," and finds that "he invariably pursued the same plan."

## The Mystery

Two things strike [[yorick|Yorick]] as singular and set his "brain to work, and to no purpose":

1. **Why only women?** The man "should only tell his story to the sex" — ignoring every man, including [[yorick|Yorick]], who was "much more likely to have given."

2. **What is the secret?** "What kind of story it was, and what species of eloquence it could be, which soften'd the hearts of the women, which he knew 'twas to no purpose to practise upon the men."

Two further circumstances entangle the mystery: the man tells every woman what he has to say "in her ear, and in a way which had much more the air of a secret than a petition"; and — most remarkably — "it was always successful. He never stopp'd a woman, but she pull'd out her purse, and immediately gave him something."

[[yorick|Yorick]] can "form no system to explain the phenomenon" (053-045). He walks upstairs to his chamber with "a riddle to amuse me for the rest of the evening" — a riddle that, characteristically for Sterne, is never solved.

## 主题分析

- **The Unsolved Mystery**: The Riddle is a Sterne set-piece in the tradition of the unresolved enigma. Unlike a conventional novelist, Sterne refuses to provide the answer. The beggar's secret remains secret — to [[yorick|Yorick]] and to the reader. The episode is a small rebellion against narrative closure: some things in life, Sterne implies, are puzzles without solutions.

- **The Gender of Charity**: The beggar's strategy — asking only women, always successfully — is a miniature study in the gender politics of eighteenth-century sentiment. Women, in Sterne's world, are more susceptible to whatever "species of eloquence" the beggar deploys. But what that eloquence is — flirtation? pathos? conspiracy? — Sterne leaves deliberately unspecified, inviting the reader to supply their own theory.

- **Observation as Narrative Mode**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s half-hour vigil at the hotel gate — watching, counting, forming hypotheses — is a model of the sentimental observer at work. He is less interested in the beggar's actual story than in the pattern of the begging: the "system" he cannot form. The episode illustrates the novel's commitment to observation over intervention, curiosity over charity.

- **The Philosophy of Small Mysteries**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s unsolved riddle is a counterweight to the grand philosophical claims made elsewhere in the novel. If the [[the-desobligeant|desobligeant]] chapter proposes a taxonomy of travellers, the Riddle episode reminds us that some phenomena resist all taxonomy. The novel's intelligence lies in its willingness to be baffled.
