# Courtesy

Courtesy in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the social lubricant that makes sentimental exchange possible. More than mere etiquette, Sterne's courtesy is a moral language — the visible sign of an inward benevolence. Every bow, every "humble courtesy," every apology for "the liberty he was going to take" is a small ritual that transforms the stranger into the acquaintance and the encounter into a sentimental event.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century was the great age of conduct literature — Chesterfield's *Letters*, Addison and Steele's *Spectator* papers, and countless manuals of polite behavior. Courtesy was understood not merely as social form but as the outward expression of moral refinement. The French "civilité" and the English "civility" both traced their lineage to the Renaissance ideal of the courtier, transformed by Enlightenment optimism into a universal code of humane interaction.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Courtesy permeates every encounter in the novel. The landlord who "took the liberty to" address [[yorick|Yorick]] in French; the young girl who "made me more a humble courtesy than a low one"; the countless apologies, compliments, and delicate withdrawals — these are not decorative flourishes but the very medium of sentimental exchange. Sterne presents courtesy as the language through which strangers negotiate proximity and distance, benevolence and self-protection.

The French setting sharpens the theme. [[yorick|Yorick]] moves through a culture where courtesy is elaborate and codified — yet he consistently finds that true courtesy transcends national form. The courtesy of the heart speaks its own language.

## 关键段落

> "The young girl made me more a humble courtesy than a low one — 'twas one of those quiet, thankful sinkings, where the spirit bows itself down." (038-044)

> "The landlord, supposing I was young in French, took the liberty to put in a word." (018-021)

> "She made me a second courtesy in setting off, and before we reached the end of the street, I saw her courtesy twice more." (038-062)

> "I saw something in her face which had much to do with the sense I had of her courtesy." (Paris chapters)
