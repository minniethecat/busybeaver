# Brussels

**Brussels** is the capital of the Austrian Netherlands (present-day Belgium), mentioned seven times across Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). It serves a triple narrative function: the home city of the young fille de chambre, the emotional crux of a route decision in [[amiens|Amiens]], and a practical travel node in the passport crisis — a city [[yorick|Yorick]] repeatedly contemplates but never actually visits.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, Brussels was the capital of the Austrian Netherlands, a Habsburg territory that served as a buffer between [[france|France]] and the Dutch Republic. The city had been bombarded during the War of the Austrian Succession (1740–1748) — a fact the French captain relays to the fille de chambre: "he had had the honour to be at the bombardment of it last war — that it was finely situated, pour cela, and full of noblesse when the Imperialists were driven out by the French" (024-PPP). This military history positions Brussels as a contested frontier city, sitting precisely at the crossroads where French, Flemish, and German Europe meet.

## 在小说中的角色

Brussels appears in the novel not as a visited destination but as a recurring node of possibility — a city that shapes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey precisely by remaining unreached.

**[[the-fille-de-chambre|The Fille de Chambre]]'s Origin**. When the French captain interrogates the young woman about her provenance — "Apparemment vous êtes Flammande?" — and guesses successively at [[lisle|Lisle]], Arras, Cambray, and Ghent, she finally answers "she was of Brussels." (024-PPP) The identification matters: Brussels marks the fille de chambre as Flemish, not French, adding a layer of cultural difference to an already saturated sentimental encounter. The captain's immediate recollection of bombarding her city introduces the uneasy proximity of war and gallantry that runs through the novel.

**The Route Not Taken**. In [[amiens|Amiens]], [[yorick|Yorick]] receives [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]]'s letter extending an invitation: "if my route should ever lay through Brussels, and I had not by then forgot the name of [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]], that [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]] would be glad to discharge her obligation." (044-PPP) [[yorick|Yorick]]'s initial response is expansive — "'twill scarce be ten posts out of my way; but, were it ten thousand! with what a moral delight will it crown my journey" — before abruptly reversing course. "I will not go to Brussels," he declares, and then kneels to swear "I would not travel to Brussels, unless Eliza went along with me, did the road lead me towards heaven!" (044-PPP) This extraordinary scene — a kneeling oath before the "Eternal Fountain of [[happiness|Happiness]]" — transforms a geographical waypoint into the novel's most intense declaration of sentimental fidelity.

**Passport Geography**. In the later passport chapters, the Count de —— agrees to take [[yorick|Yorick]] "in his suite" as far as [[calais|Calais]], "as he was to return by way of Brussels to [[paris|Paris]]." (066-PPP) Brussels here functions as a practical coordinate in the travel network of 18th-century Europe — a reminder that beneath the novel's sentimental flights lies a real geography of post-chaises, passports, and cross-border routes.
