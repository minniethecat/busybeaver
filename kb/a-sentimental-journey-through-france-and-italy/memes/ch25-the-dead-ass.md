## THE DEAD ASS.

[025-001]— And this, said he, putting

[025-002]the remains of a crust into his wallet—and this should have

[025-003]been thy portion, said he, hadst thou been alive to have shared

[025-004]it with me.—I thought, by the accent, it had been an

[025-005]apostrophe to his child; but ’twas to his ass, and to the

[025-006]very ass we had seen dead in the road, which had occasioned La

[025-007]Fleur’s misadventure. The man seemed to lament it

[025-008]much; and it instantly brought into my mind Sancho’s

[025-009]lamentation for his; but he did it with more true touches of

[025-010]nature. 

[025-011]The mourner was sitting upon a stone bench at the door, with

[025-012]the ass’s pannel and its bridle on one side, which he took

[025-013]up from time to time,—then laid them

[025-014]down,—look’d at them, and shook his head. He

[025-015]then took his crust of bread out of his wallet again, as if to

[025-016]eat it; held it some time in his hand,—then laid it upon

[025-017]the bit of his ass’s bridle,—looked wistfully at the

[025-018]little arrangement he had made—and then gave a sigh. 

[025-019]The simplicity of his grief drew numbers about him, and La

[025-020]Fleur amongst the rest, whilst the horses were getting ready; as

[025-021]I continued sitting in the post-chaise, I could see and hear over

[025-022]their heads. 

[025-023]—He said he had come last from Spain, where he had been

[025-024]from the furthest borders of Franconia; and had got so far on his

[025-025]return home, when his ass died. Every one seemed desirous

[025-026]to know what business could have taken so old and poor a man so

[025-027]far a journey from his own home. 

[025-028]It had pleased heaven, he said, to bless him with three sons,

[025-029]the finest lads in Germany; but having in one week lost two of

[025-030]the eldest of them by the small-pox, and the youngest falling ill

[025-031]of the same distemper, he was afraid of being bereft of them all;

[025-032]and made a vow, if heaven would not take him from him also, he

[025-033]would go in gratitude to St. Iago in Spain. 

[025-034]When the mourner got thus far on his story, he stopp’d

[025-035]to pay Nature her tribute,—and wept bitterly. 

[025-036]He said, heaven had accepted the conditions; and that he had

[025-037]set out from his cottage with this poor creature, who had been a

[025-038]patient partner of his journey;—that it had eaten the same

[025-039]bread with him all the way, and was unto him as a friend. 

[025-040]Every body who stood about, heard the poor fellow with

[025-041]concern.—La Fleur offered him money.—The mourner said

[025-042]he did not want it;—it was not the value of the

[025-043]ass—but the loss of him.—The ass, he said, he was

[025-044]assured, loved him;—and upon this told them a long story of

[025-045]a mischance upon their passage over the Pyrenean mountains,

[025-046]which had separated them from each other three days; during which

[025-047]time the ass had sought him as much as he had sought the ass, and

[025-048]that they had scarce either eaten or drank till they met. 

[025-049]Thou hast one comfort, friend, said I, at least, in the loss

[025-050]of thy poor beast; I’m sure thou hast been a merciful

[025-051]master to him.—Alas! said the mourner, I thought so when he

[025-052]was alive;—but now that he is dead, I think

[025-053]otherwise.—I fear the weight of myself and my afflictions

[025-054]together have been too much for him,—they have shortened

[025-055]the poor creature’s days, and I fear I have them to answer

[025-056]for.—Shame on the world! said I to myself.—Did we but

[025-057]love each other as this poor soul loved his

[025-058]ass—’twould be something.—
