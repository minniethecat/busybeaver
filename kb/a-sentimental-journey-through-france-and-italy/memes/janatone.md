# Janatone

**Janatone** is the landlord's daughter at [[montreuil|Montreuil]], mentioned in the scene where [[yorick|Yorick]] arrives at the inn and is eventually introduced to [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]. Though her appearance is fleeting, her name serves as the pivot for one of Sterne's most characteristic comic digressions — a miniature lesson in French usage that reveals both [[yorick|Yorick]]'s imperfect French and Sterne's pleasure in linguistic play (018-005).

## Appearance

Janatone enters the narrative indirectly. The landlord, praising the generosity of English travellers, tells [[yorick|Yorick]] that "qu'un milord Anglois présentoit un écu à la fille de chambre" the previous night (018-005). [[yorick|Yorick]], attempting a French expression, replies: "Tant pis pour Mademoiselle Janatone" (018-005) — too bad for Miss Janatone.

The landlord immediately corrects him. "Now Janatone, being the landlord's daughter," he explains, [[yorick|Yorick]] should have said "tant mieux" rather than "tant pis" — for "tant mieux, toujours, Monsieur, when there is any thing to be got — tant pis, when there is nothing" (018-006). The correction is gentle but pointed: Janatone's relation to the landlord makes the matter one of family profit, not loss.

## Context and Significance

Janatone herself says nothing and reappears nowhere else in the novel. Her name functions as a narrative device: the brief reference to the landlord's daughter triggers Sterne's celebrated digression on the two "great hinges in French conversation," tant pis and tant mieux, which he advises any traveller to master before reaching [[paris|Paris]] (018-006).

The scene is characteristic of Sterne's method — a chance remark in an inn parlour spirals into a philosophical observation, punctuated by the exemplary anecdote of Mr. H— at the ambassador's table, who was variously greeted with "Tant pis" and "Tant mieux" depending on whether he was identified as H— the poet or H— the historian.
