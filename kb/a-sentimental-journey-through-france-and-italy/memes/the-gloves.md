# The Gloves

**The Gloves** is one of the most celebrated comic-erotic episodes in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occurring in chapter 34. [[yorick|Yorick]] visits a Parisian glove shop and finds himself engaged in a wordless exchange of glances with the beautiful *grisette* behind the counter — a scene that Sterne transforms into a miniature treatise on the language beyond language, where "all the languages of Babel set loose together, could not express" what passes between them.

## 概要

The episode begins prosaically: [[yorick|Yorick]] enters a glove shop in [[paris|Paris]], and the beautiful *grisette* (shop-girl) rises to serve him. She unties a parcel of gloves and measures them one by one across his hand. They are all too large. She begs him to try a single pair, "which seemed to be the least." She holds it open — his hand "slipped into it at once." But it still will not do. She shakes her head; he shakes his.

Then Sterne pauses the narrative for one of his most extraordinary passages on the limits of language:

"There are certain combined looks of simple subtlety, — where whim, and sense, and seriousness, and nonsense, are so blended, that all the languages of Babel set loose together, could not express them; — they are communicated and caught so instantaneously, that you can scarce say which party is the infector" (034-011).

The gloves, it is agreed, will not do. So, "folding our hands within our arms, we both lolled upon the counter." The parcel lies between them on the narrow counter — a chaste barrier and an erotic centrepiece.

## The Silent Duel

What follows is a wordless exchange conducted entirely through glances. The grisette looks at the gloves, then sideways to the window, then at the gloves, then at [[yorick|Yorick]]. He follows her example: gloves, window, gloves, her — "and so on alternately."

[[yorick|Yorick]] quickly realises he is losing: "I found I lost considerably in every attack" (034-027). She has a quick black eye, and "shot through two such long and silken eyelashes with such penetration, that she look'd into my very heart and reins." He can actually *feel* her looking through him.

Defeated by the silent duel, [[yorick|Yorick]] capitulates in the only language of commerce left to him: he takes up two pairs of gloves and puts them in his pocket.

## The Commerce of Politeness

The grisette has not asked above a single livre above the price — and [[yorick|Yorick]] wishes she had asked a livre more. He puzzles over how "to bring the matter about" — how to give her more money without insulting her. She mistakes his embarrassment and delivers the killing blow of politeness: could she possibly ask "a sous too much of a stranger — and of a stranger whose politeness, more than his want of gloves, has done me the honour to lay himself at my mercy?" *M'en croyez capable?*

"Faith! not I," says [[yorick|Yorick]], counting the money into her hand. He exits with "a lower bow than one generally makes to a shopkeeper's wife," and her lad follows with the parcel.

## 主题分析

- **The Language Beyond Language**: Sterne's digression on "combined looks of simple subtlety" is the episode's philosophical core. The entire transaction between [[yorick|Yorick]] and the grisette occurs in a register that language cannot capture — the rhetoric of glances, postures, and silences. The episode is, in effect, a demonstration of its own thesis: Sterne describes what cannot be described by describing the attempt to describe it.

- **Commerce as Seduction**: The glove shop is a theatre of erotic commerce where the boundary between buying and desiring dissolves. Every gesture — measuring gloves across a hand, holding a glove open, slipping a hand inside — carries a double meaning. The episode belongs to a tradition of scenes in which shopping becomes a metaphor for courtship, but Sterne's innovation is to make the transaction almost entirely silent.

- **Politeness as Power**: The grisette's final speech — she could not possibly overcharge a stranger "whose politeness...has done me the honour to lay himself at my mercy" — is a masterpiece of social rhetoric. She wins the commercial exchange by framing [[yorick|Yorick]]'s purchase as an act of gallantry to which she has graciously submitted. The politeness is genuine and strategic at once.

- **Losing as Winning**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s admission that he "lost considerably in every attack" of the silent duel is characteristic of the novel's moral economy, in which sentimental defeat is a form of victory. He leaves the shop having spent more than necessary on gloves he did not need — and the reader understands this as the correct outcome.
