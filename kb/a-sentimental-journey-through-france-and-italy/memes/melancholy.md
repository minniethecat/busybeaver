# Melancholy

Melancholy in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is sorrow aestheticized — the raw material of grief transformed by reflection and distance into a pleasurable, almost luxurious emotional state. It is the mood most characteristic of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sensibility, the tonality in which the narrator is most fully himself. When Sterne writes "melancholy! to see such sprightliness the prey of sorrow," the exclamation itself performs what it describes: the transformation of painful observation into a refined sentimental pleasure.

## 定义与历史语境

Melancholy had a distinguished pedigree in eighteenth-century culture. From Burton's *Anatomy of Melancholy* (1621) to Gray's "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard" (1751), the English tradition had made melancholy the mark of sensitive, cultivated minds. It was associated with genius, with religious seriousness, and — crucially for Sterne — with the capacity for deep feeling. The graveyard poets had made melancholy fashionable; the sentimental novelists made it moral. To feel melancholy was to demonstrate that one possessed a soul capable of being moved by the sorrows of others and by the transience of all earthly things.

Sterne's innovation was to make melancholy mobile. Unlike the graveyard poet who sits in a fixed spot and meditates, [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters melancholy on the road — it is a traveling mood, triggered by chance encounters and passing scenes. This mobility gives Sterne's melancholy a lightness and unpredictability that distinguishes it from the heavier, more static melancholy of the graveyard tradition.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Melancholy appears at four key moments in the novel, each revealing a different facet of the emotion. The first and most powerful is the [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] episode: "melancholy! to see such sprightliness the prey of sorrow." Here melancholy is the observer's response to the contrast between past vitality and present suffering — the aesthetic emotion par excellence, which requires distance to function.

The second appearance occurs in a famous passage of self-reflection: "if I could not do better, I would fasten them upon some sweet myrtle, or seek some melancholy cypress to connect myself to." The melancholy cypress, traditional emblem of mourning, becomes an object of voluntary attachment — [[yorick|Yorick]] would *seek* it out, deliberately cultivating the melancholy mood. This is melancholy as sentimental practice, a chosen emotional posture.

The third, more satiric treatment occurs after the Opéra Comique: "Melancholy application! especially where the size was extremely little." Here [[yorick|Yorick]] catches himself applying sentimental standards to everyday people on the street — measuring passersby against the ideal figures he has just seen on stage. The "melancholy application" is the gap between sentimental ideal and ordinary reality.

The final appearance is the most self-aware: "'Tis going, I own, like the Knight of the [[sorrow|Woe]]ful Countenance in quest of melancholy adventures." [[yorick|Yorick]] compares himself to Don Quixote — the supreme example of a sensibility so refined it has become comic. The allusion suggests that Sterne is fully aware of melancholy's potential for self-parody, even as he continues to cultivate it.

## 关键段落

> "Melancholy! to see such sprightliness the prey of sorrow, — I pitied her from my soul." (010-036)
