# Sentimentalism

Sentimentalism — sometimes called the Cult of Sensibility — was the dominant literary and philosophical movement of mid-to-late eighteenth-century Europe.

It held that human beings are fundamentally benevolent, that moral knowledge is felt rather than reasoned, and that literature's highest task is to exercise and refine the sympathetic imagination.

Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768) is, alongside his own *Tristram Shandy* and the works of Richardson, Mackenzie, and Rousseau, one of the movement's defining texts.

## 定义与历史语境

Sentimentalism emerged in reaction to Hobbesian pessimism about human nature and Lockean empiricism about human knowledge.

Against Hobbes's view of man as naturally selfish, philosophers like Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, and Hume argued for an innate "moral sense" — a capacity to feel pleasure at virtue and pain at vice. Against Locke's blank-slate model of the mind, the sentimentalists insisted that feeling itself was a mode of knowledge.

By the 1760s, Sterne's era, the movement had produced many novels, poems, and treatises — and was beginning to attract parody for its excesses.

The "man of feeling" was by turns celebrated as a moral ideal and ridiculed as a creature of self-indulgent emotional display. Sterne's novel inhabits this ambiguity from its first page to its last.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sterne's title announces his allegiance to the movement — the journey is "sentimental" — but the novel consistently tests sentimental ideals against the friction of real encounter.

[[yorick|Yorick]] is the model man of feeling. His responses to beggars, monks, shopkeepers, and women are governed by spontaneous emotion rather than calculated self-interest. His heart "smites" him, his tears flow freely, and his sympathy reaches across barriers of nation, class, and language.

Yet Sterne never lets the sentimental posture go unexamined. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s feeling is sometimes self-regarding. His sympathy can be selective (the monk is snubbed, then mourned). The novel's most charged encounters raise uncomfortable questions about whether sentiment is ever truly disinterested. The novel presents sentimentalism not as a settled doctrine but as a living practice.

## 关键段落

> "The sweetest sensibility — the most refined sentiments — what are they, when the heart is shut?" (The Preface chapter)

> "When the heart flies out before the understanding, it saves the judgment a world of pains." (The Remise Door chapter)

> "Dear sensibility! source inexhausted of all that's precious in our joys, or costly in our sorrows!" (The Bourbonnois chapter)

> "I am positive I have a soul; nor can all the books with which materialists have pester'd the world ever convince me to the contrary." (The Bourbonnois chapter)
