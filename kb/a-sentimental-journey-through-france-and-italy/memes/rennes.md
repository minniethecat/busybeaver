# Rennes

**Rennes** is the capital of [[brittany|Brittany]] and the setting of one of Laurence Sterne's most celebrated digressive episodes in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). In the chapter titled "RENNES." (045-PPP), [[yorick|Yorick]] witnesses the [[marquis-de|Marquis d'E—]]'s solemn reclamation of his sword — a ceremony that condenses the novel's themes of nobility, poverty, commerce, and the sentimental recovery of honour into a single sustained scene.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, Rennes was the historic capital of the Duchy of [[brittany|Brittany]], a province with distinct legal traditions that set it apart from the rest of [[france|France]]. The Estates of [[brittany|Brittany]] assembled at Rennes, making it the political and judicial center of the region. Most significantly for Sterne's purposes, [[brittany|Brittany]] preserved an ancient law permitting its nobility to engage in commerce without derogation — that is, without losing their noble status. This legal peculiarity is the hinge on which the entire Rennes episode turns.

## 在小说中的角色

Rennes appears in the novel not as a city [[yorick|Yorick]] explores but as the theater of a single, unforgettable ceremony. Its narrative function is symbolic rather than geographical — it is the place where the abstract question of whether commerce degrades nobility receives a concrete, emotional answer.

**The Ancient Law**. Sterne frames the episode with careful legal exposition: "In any other province in [[france|France]], save [[brittany|Brittany]], this was smiting the root for ever of the little tree his pride and affection wish'd to see re-blossom. — But in [[brittany|Brittany]], there being a provision for this, he avail'd himself of it; and, taking an occasion when the states were assembled at Rennes, the Marquis, attended with his two boys, entered the court." (045-PPP) The specificity of "Rennes" — not "a town in [[brittany|Brittany]]" — grounds the ceremony in an actual legal geography.

**The Surrender**. The [[marquis-de|Marquis d'E—]], his house fallen into decay through ancestral indiscretions, pleads the ancient law and surrenders his sword to the president of the court: "Here, said he, take it; and be trusty guardians of it, till better times put me in condition to reclaim it." (045-PPP) The sword is deposited in the archives of his house, and the Marquis and his family embark for Martinico — a journey from aristocratic poverty to colonial commerce.

**The Reclamation**. Nineteen or twenty years later, the Marquis returns to Rennes. Sterne emphasizes the coincidence that places [[yorick|Yorick]] there: "It was an incident of good fortune which will never happen to any traveller but a [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al one, that I should be at Rennes at the very time of this solemn requisition: I call it solemn; — it was so to me." (045-PPP) This self-conscious framing — the acknowledgement that sentimental travelers are uniquely privileged to witness such moments — elevates Rennes from a setting to a gift of narrative providence.

**The Tear**. The ceremony reaches its emotional climax when the Marquis draws his reclaimed sword: "he look'd attentively along it, beginning at the hilt, as if to see whether it was the same, — when, observing a little rust which it had contracted near the point, he brought it near his eye, and bending his head down over it, — I think — I saw a tear fall upon the place." (045-PPP) The rust on the sword — the trace of time and neglect — prompts the Marquis's tear, and [[yorick|Yorick]]'s response is immediate: "O, how I envied him his feelings!" (045-PPP) The scene crystallizes the sentimental ideal: feeling is the truest wealth, and the recovery of honour through tears is more valuable than the honour itself.
