# Temper

Temper in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the internal climate of the self — the disposition that governs how one receives the shocks, reversals, and tender encounters of travel. More than mere mood, temper in Sterne's usage encompasses the entire economy of emotional self-regulation: the capacity to bear provocation "temper'd to bear and forbear," to recover from vexation, and to maintain the openness of heart that sentimental responsiveness requires.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century inherited from classical and Renaissance psychology a model of the self in which the "temperament" — sanguine, choleric, melancholic, phlegmatic — determined one's constitutional disposition. Hume and the Scottish Enlightenment philosophers developed this into a sophisticated account of the passions and their regulation. Sterne's contribution is to make temper visible in narrative: his characters' tempers are not described but dramatized, revealed through their responses to the small frictions and large reversals of travel.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel's first mention of temper comes early, when [[yorick|Yorick]] praises the "humanity of his temper" in the French — a national temper that rises above political prejudice. This sets the pattern: temper is what allows one person to see past another's national or personal failings. The word appears in contexts of friction and accommodation: "the weakness or goodness of our tempers which had involved us in this contest," says the stranger who exchanges snuffboxes with [[yorick|Yorick]]. Two men, both of good temper, transform a potential quarrel into an act of mutual courtesy.

The most important temper in the novel is [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], his servant, possesses "the festivity of his temper" — a natural cheerfulness that "supplied all defects." [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own temper is more volatile: "he had put me out of temper with him, — and then with myself, for being so." This self-awareness — the ability to observe and correct one's own temper — is the mark of the sentimental hero. True temper is not placidity but the capacity for self-recovery.

## 关键段落

> "high honour for the humanity of his temper, — I rose up an inch taller for the accommodation." (Calais chapters)

> "that sort of fire which was in them, which seemed more temper'd by courtesy than years." (The Monk episode)

> "the weakness or goodness of our tempers which had involved us in this contest." (The Snuffbox episode)

> "by the festivity of his temper; — it supplied all defects." (On La Fleur, Montreuil chapters)

> "he had put me out of temper with him, — and then with myself, for being so." (Nampont chapters)

> "how would thy meek and courteous spirit, my dear monk! so temper'd to bear and forbear!" (The Monk episode)

> "a part of the temperature; and certainly, if it is the same blood which comes from the heart." (Paris chapters)
