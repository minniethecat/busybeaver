# Madame de L—

**Madame de L—** (also referred to as [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] in later chapters) is a French lady of quality whom [[yorick|Yorick]] first encounters at [[amiens|Amiens]] and later attempts to visit in [[paris|Paris]] in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). The sister of Count de L—, she embodies the gracious, socially fluent French sensibility that [[yorick|Yorick]] alternately admires and is unsettled by.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

Madame de L— appears first as a fleeting glimpse — a bow of recognition from a passing post-chaise at [[amiens|Amiens]] — and gradually becomes one of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s [[paris|Paris]]ian connections. She is a woman of sufficient rank to maintain a household with servants (including her own *fille de chambre*) and a brother with a Count's hotel, yet Sterne presents her through intimate domestic moments rather than formal grandeur. (027-004)

Her role in the novel is as a touchstone of French politeness and sentimental correspondence. Through her, Sterne explores the rituals of letter-writing, the obligations of social calls, and the delicate tension between genuine feeling and the forms that contain it.

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Glimpse at Amiens

[[yorick|Yorick]] first sees Madame de L— at [[amiens|Amiens]], just as he awakens from a nap in his carriage, thinking of "the very town where my poor lady is to come." At that moment, the Count de L—'s post-chaise drives hastily by, and she makes him "a bow of recognition, — and of that particular kind of it, which told me she thought of something else." (Ch. 27, [[amiens|Amiens]])

### La Fleur's Letter Episode

The most extended episode involving Madame de L— occurs at the Count's hotel in [[amiens|Amiens]]. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], having charmed the entire household with his fife, is summoned upstairs by Madame de L—, who has heard the commotion:

> Madame de L—, in passing from her brother's apartments to her own, hearing so much jollity below stairs, rung up her fille de chambre to ask about it; and, hearing it was the English gentleman's servant, who had set the whole house merry with his pipe, she ordered him up. (028-023)

When she asks [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] whether he carries a letter from [[yorick|Yorick]], [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] — unwilling to disappoint — improvises an elaborate pantomime of searching every pocket before confessing he left the letter at the inn. Madame de L—'s response reveals her character: she had asked "with such a tone of reliance upon the fact, that [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] had not power to disappoint her expectations." (Ch. 28, [[amiens|Amiens]])

[[yorick|Yorick]], moved by [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s zeal, adapts a drummer's love-letter and sends it to Madame de L—, and "the next morning we pursued our journey to [[paris|Paris]]." (Ch. 28, [[amiens|Amiens]])

### The Unkept Call in Paris

In [[paris|Paris]], [[yorick|Yorick]] resolves to call upon [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] at her residence in the Rue St. Pierre, but his intentions are perpetually diverted by circumstance. On one occasion, he changes his plan three times in a single afternoon — first intending to visit her, then deciding to tour [[versailles|Versailles]], then changing course again to visit [[count-de-b]]:

> I had intended that day for Madame de R—, in the Rue St. Pierre, and had devoutly sent her word by her fille de chambre that I would assuredly wait upon her; — but I am governed by circumstances; — I cannot govern them. (044-016)

### The Sunday Vis-à-Vis

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s final reference to [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] comes on a Sunday in [[paris|Paris]], when [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] asks for the day off to visit his own mistress. [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects that "it was the very thing I intended to do myself vis-à-vis [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]]," having retained a remise on purpose for the visit — though whether the visit ever occurs remains untold. (Ch. 68, [[paris|Paris]])

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**French [[courtesy|Politeness]] and [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: Madame de L— embodies the French social virtues that Sterne's narrator both appreciates and gently satirizes. Her graciousness is genuine — she receives [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] kindly, sends her *fille de chambre* to enquire after [[yorick|Yorick]] — but it operates within a code of etiquette that the more impulsive [[yorick|Yorick]] finds constraining.

**The Unwritten Letter**: The comic episode of [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s pretended letter, and [[yorick|Yorick]]'s subsequent struggle to compose a reply, crystallizes one of the novel's recurring themes — the gap between spontaneous feeling and its formal expression. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s inability to write the letter ("I began and began again... and could no way please myself") contrasts with Madame de L—'s poised expectation of one. (Ch. 28, [[amiens|Amiens]])

**Circumstance and Intention**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s repeated failures to visit [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] in [[paris|Paris]] — always diverted by "circumstances" — reflect the novel's broader meditation on the gap between sentimental intention and actual conduct.
