# Paris

**Paris** is the central setting of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occupying chapters 30 through 62 — nearly half the novel. For [[yorick|Yorick]], Paris is simultaneously a theater of dazzling spectacle and a labyrinth of intimate encounters, where the grand scale of French society meets the private dramas of the sentimental heart.

## 地理与背景 (Setting and Context)

[[yorick|Yorick]] arrives in Paris after passing through [[calais|Calais]], [[montreuil|Montreuil]], and [[amiens|Amiens]]. The city — the largest and most cosmopolitan in 18th-century Europe — immediately overwhelms him. Looking through his hotel window in his "dusty black coat," he sees "all the world in yellow, blue, and green, running at the ring of pleasure" — a kaleidoscopic vision of urban life that reduces him to an "atom." (Ch. 30, Paris)

Paris in Sterne's novel is less a mapped geography than a succession of interiors: hotels, parlors, barber shops, bookshops, and the Opera. Its public spaces — streets, the Tuileries, the road to [[versailles|Versailles]] — serve as stages for [[yorick|Yorick]]'s encounters with a cross-section of French society.

## 关键地点与场景 (Key Locations and Scenes)

### The Hotel de Modene

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s lodgings in Paris, from which he ventures forth each day. It is here that [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] delivers his theatrical reports, and where [[yorick|Yorick]] composes (and fails to compose) his letters. The hotel room becomes a retreat from the city's sensory assault — a private space for reflection amid public performance.

### The Barbershop

The Parisian barber who refuses to dress [[yorick|Yorick]]'s wig provides one of the novel's sharpest cultural observations. When the barber claims his wig-buckle "may emerge it into the ocean, and it will stand," [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects on the difference between English and French expression:

> What a great scale is every thing upon in this city thought I. — The utmost stretch of an English periwig-maker's ideas could have gone no further than to have "dipped it into a pail of water." — What difference! 'tis like Time to Eternity! (Ch. 31, Paris)

This episode crystallizes Sterne's running commentary on national character — the French "sublime" that "professes more than it performs."

### The Bookshop (Shakespeare)

In perhaps the most philosophically dense Paris episode, [[yorick|Yorick]] visits a bookshop to purchase Shakespeare's works. The encounter with the bookseller becomes a meditation on cultural inheritance, literary reputation, and the commerce of ideas. (Ch. 36, Paris)

### The Opera

The [[the-opera|Paris Opera]] serves as a site where [[yorick|Yorick]] observes French society in its most theatrical mode. The performances on stage are, for [[yorick|Yorick]], less interesting than the performances in the boxes — the social choreography of glances, fans, and whispered judgments. (Ch. 43–50)

### Versailles and the Road Thither

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey to [[versailles|Versailles]] represents his brief foray into the world of court and power. The contrast between Parisian urbanity and [[versailles|Versailles]]' formal grandeur highlights the range of French social experience [[yorick|Yorick]] navigates. (Ch. 43–50)

## 文化意义 (Cultural Significance)

Paris functions in the novel as:

**A Mirror for English Identity**: Every Parisian encounter prompts [[yorick|Yorick]] to compare French and English manners. The city becomes a laboratory for Sterne's observations on national character — the French tendency toward theatrical grandeur, the English toward understated practicality.

**The Stage of [[feeling|Sensibility]]**: Paris's social density provides the ideal conditions for sentimental encounters. The city's crowded spaces — streets, shops, theaters — throw strangers together in ways that test and reveal [[yorick|Yorick]]'s capacity for spontaneous feeling.

**A City of Commerce and Exchange**: From wig-buckles to book prices to the "livres" [[yorick|Yorick]] constantly weighs, Paris foregrounds the economic dimensions of travel and social interaction. [[sentiment|Sentiment]] itself becomes, in the Paris chapters, something that can be exchanged, performed, and — sometimes — purchased.
