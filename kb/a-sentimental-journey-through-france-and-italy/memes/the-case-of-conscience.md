# The Case of Conscience

**The Case of Conscience** is an episode in the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), in which the master of the [[hotel-de-modene|Hôtel de Modène]] confronts [[yorick|Yorick]] for keeping a young woman in his bedchamber — an accusation that launches a comedy of moral reasoning, followed by [[yorick|Yorick]]'s encounter with a [[the-grisset|grisette]] selling lace.

## 概要

The episode opens with the master of the hotel bursting into [[yorick|Yorick]]'s room to inform him he must find other lodgings: a young woman had been "lock'd up" with him for two hours, against the rules of the house. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s reply is a masterpiece of sentimental deflection: "We'll all part friends then, — for the girl is no worse, — and I am no worse, — and you will be just as I found you." The innkeeper, unmollified, points to the rumpled bed as evidence, but [[yorick|Yorick]]'s "pride not suffering me to enter into any detail of the case," he simply resolves to discharge his bill at breakfast.

The hotel master then offers a fine [[paris|Paris]]ian distinction: he would not have minded twenty girls, "provided it had been but in a morning." [[yorick|Yorick]] seizes on this: "And does the difference of the time of the day at [[paris|Paris]] make a difference in the sin?" The man replies it makes a difference "in the scandal" — a distinction [[yorick|Yorick]] finds himself perversely enjoying: "I like a good distinction in my heart."

The host's commercial logic surfaces: a stranger at [[paris|Paris]] should have opportunities to buy lace and silk stockings, and " 'tis nothing if a woman comes with a band-box." [[yorick|Yorick]] protests his innocence — she had such a box but he "never look'd into it" (054-029) — and the host, smelling commerce, recommends a vendor who would "use you en conscience."

Within minutes, a [[the-grisset|grisette]] appears with her box of lace. [[yorick|Yorick]] resolves to buy nothing, but her patient manner — unfolding and refolding each piece "with the most patient sweetness," letting him have everything at his own price — undoes him. The thought that she is "tributary to this tyrant of an host" softens his heart, and he spends three of his four louis d'ors on a pair of ruffles, philosophising: "I have only paid as many a poor soul has paid before me, for an act he could not do, or think of."

The chapter closes with [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] conveying the host's apology and [[yorick|Yorick]] resolving, "after so narrow an escape, to run no more risks, but to leave [[paris|Paris]], if it was possible, with all the virtue I enter'd it."

## 主题分析

- **Casuistry and Self-Deception**: The title "The Case of Conscience" is ironic — what follows is less a moral examination than a series of rationalisations. [[yorick|Yorick]] buys the ruffles not because he wants them but because he pities the grisette; yet he knows this pity is the mechanism by which his resolutions are defeated. The episode dramatizes the gap between moral intention and sentimental action.

- **Commerce and [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: The hotel master's world is one of pure commerce — scandal is a business risk, the grisette's visit is a sales opportunity. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental morality exists in tension with this commercial frame; he is always being drawn into transactions he did not intend. The ruffles he buys are at once a charitable gesture and a surrender.

- **The Structure of Temptation**: The grisette's method — patience, sweetness, the appearance of leaving everything to the customer's own price — is a study in how virtue is undone not by force but by apparent gentleness. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s resolution "to run no more risks" is immediately undercut by the novel's structure, which knows that risks are precisely what the sentimental journey requires.
