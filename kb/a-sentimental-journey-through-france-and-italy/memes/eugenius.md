# Eugenius

**Eugenius** is the silent addressee of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s most private thoughts in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). He never appears on stage — he exists only in the second person, a "dear Eugenius" to whom [[yorick|Yorick]] confides his embarrassments, his whims, and his philosophical justifications. He is the ideal friend: the one who worries about [[yorick|Yorick]]'s finances before the journey, who would laugh and moralize at his follies, and who draws the curtain when the traveller returns home ill. (032-001)

## Role Overview

Eugenius functions as the novel's interior conscience — a listener who never speaks in direct dialogue but whose imagined reactions shape [[yorick|Yorick]]'s self-presentation. He is named in four key passages, each marking a moment where [[yorick|Yorick]] steps outside the immediate narrative to reflect on his own conduct. The name "Eugenius" (from the Greek *eu-genes*, "well-born") suggests nobility of character rather than rank; he is a friend of the heart, not of station.

The relationship between [[yorick|Yorick]] and Eugenius is established before the journey begins. As [[yorick|Yorick]] prepares to depart, Eugenius draws him aside to ask about his finances — a gesture of practical care that reveals the depth of their friendship. (040-001)

## Appearances

### The Pulse-Taking Episode

The first mention of Eugenius comes during one of the novel's most characteristic scenes: [[yorick|Yorick]], seated in a [[paris|Paris]]ian glove-shop, feeling the pulse of the pretty *grisette* who serves him. He imagines Eugenius passing by:

> "Would to heaven! my dear Eugenius, thou hadst passed by, and beheld me sitting in my black coat, and in my lack-a-day-sical manner, counting the throbs of it, one by one, with as much true devotion as if I had been watching the critical ebb or flow of her fever." (032-001)

[[yorick|Yorick]] anticipates Eugenius would laugh and moralize upon his "new profession" of pulse-taking — and concedes he would deserve it. But he also mounts a defence: "There are worse occupations in this world than feeling a woman's pulse." When Eugenius (in imagination) objects that the subject is a *grisette* in an open shop, [[yorick|Yorick]] retorts: "So much the better: for when my views are direct, Eugenius, I care not if all the world saw me feel it." (032-002)

### The Financial Advice

Before the journey, Eugenius — "knowing that I was as little subject to be overburden'd with money as thought" — takes [[yorick|Yorick]] aside and asks what provision he has made. When [[yorick|Yorick]] names the sum, Eugenius shakes his head:

> "Eugenius shook his head, and said it would not do; so pull'd out his purse in order to empty it into mine. — I've enough in conscience, Eugenius, said I. — Indeed, Yorick, you have not, replied Eugenius; I know France and Italy better than you." (040-002)

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s reply is characteristic: he will soon get himself clapped into the [[the-bastile|Bastille]] and live there "a couple of months entirely at the king of [[calais|France]]'s expense." Eugenius's dry response — "I beg pardon, said Eugenius drily: really I had forgot that resource" — is one of the novel's few moments of genuine wit at [[yorick|Yorick]]'s expense, and it comes from the friend who knows him best. (040-003)

### The Letter to Eugenius

In [[paris|Paris]], struggling to decipher a faded Gothic manuscript, [[yorick|Yorick]] describes his restless process: "I threw it down; and then wrote a letter to Eugenius; — then I took it up again, and embroiled my patience with it afresh; — and then to cure that, I wrote a letter to Eliza." (044-001) The letter to Eugenius is never quoted — we never learn its contents — but its presence in this domestic tableau confirms Eugenius as the absent companion to whom [[yorick|Yorick]] naturally turns in moments of intellectual frustration.

### The Final Benediction

The last mention of Eugenius appears near the novel's close, in one of its most elevated philosophical passages. Contemplating the "great Sensorium of the world" that vibrates with every human sorrow, [[yorick|Yorick]] writes:

> "Touch'd with thee, Eugenius draws my curtain when I languish — hears my tale of symptoms, and blames the weather for the disorder of his nerves." (066-001)

Eugenius here is transformed from friend into almost a spiritual presence — the hand that draws the curtain, the ear that receives the tale of illness, the sensibility that blames weather for its own nervous disorder. The language echoes the novel's deepest sentimental conviction: that true friendship is a form of sympathetic vibration, a sharing of the "generous joys and generous cares" that unite all feeling beings.

## Character and Themes

**[[friend|Friendship]] as Internal Dialogue**: Eugenius is not a character in any conventional sense. He never speaks an audible word in the novel's present action — every one of his remarks is recalled or imagined. Yet this absence is precisely his function: he represents the interiorized friendship that Sterne's sentimentalism prizes above all other social bonds. [[yorick|Yorick]] carries Eugenius with him across [[calais|France]], addressing him in thought, measuring his own conduct against Eugenius's imagined judgment.

**The Practical [[friend|Friend]] vs. the [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Travel]]ler**: The financial exchange before the journey establishes a comic contrast that runs through all the Eugenius passages. Eugenius is prudent, knowing, realistic about the cost of travel — everything [[yorick|Yorick]] is not. The friend who offers his purse is the anchor to a world of practical concerns that [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental philosophy perpetually floats above.

**[[feeling|Sensibility]] and Illness**: The final passage links friendship to the novel's running concern with nervous sensibility. Eugenius, like [[yorick|Yorick]], suffers from "the disorder of his nerves" — the fashionable eighteenth-century malady that marked a person of refined feeling. Their friendship is cemented not by shared adventures but by shared susceptibility.
