# Sentiment

Sentiment is the defining emotional register of *A Sentimental [[travel|Journey]]*. More than mere emotion, sentiment in Sterne's usage denotes refined, cultivated feeling — the capacity to be moved by the sufferings and joys of others, and to respond with propriety and grace. It is both the subject of the novel and its narrative method.

## 定义与历史语境

In eighteenth-century moral philosophy, "sentiment" occupied a middle ground between brute passion and cold reason. Following the Earl of Shaftesbury and Francis Hutcheson, the sentimental tradition held that moral judgment arose not from rational calculation but from an innate "moral sense" — a capacity for feeling the right response to virtue and vice. Sterne's novel translates this philosophical position into literary practice: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey is a sustained exercise in responding to the world through sentiment rather than through calculation or prejudice.

The French were thought to have perfected this quality — Sterne describes them as "so renowned for sentiment and fine feelings" — and part of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey is to measure himself against this standard.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sentiment governs every encounter in the novel. [[yorick|Yorick]] responds to the Monk not with theological argument but with sentimental judgment: he evaluates the man's "sentiment" rather than his doctrine. The commercial metaphor of "sentimental commerce" frames human exchange as a trade in feeling rather than in goods or ideas.

Crucially, Sterne distinguishes genuine sentiment from its performance. The novel is populated with characters who perform feeling without possessing it — and [[yorick|Yorick]] himself is often uncertain whether his own responses are authentic sentiment or mere self-regard. "There was nothing wrong in the sentiment," he reflects at one point, "and yet I instantly felt something wrong in the manner."
