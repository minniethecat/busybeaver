# The Address

**The Address** is an episode in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), set en route to [[versailles|Versailles]], in which [[yorick|Yorick]] rehearses and then dismantles the servile forms of address he might use to petition the [[duc-de-c|Duc de C—]] for a passport. The chapter is a dramatic internal dialogue — a sentimental traveller's struggle to reconcile prudence with self-respect.

## 概要

[[yorick|Yorick]] approaches [[versailles|Versailles]] with a mind full of trepidation. "I should not like to have my enemy take a view of my mind when I am going to ask protection of any man," he confesses, recognising that his errand to the [[duc-de-c|Duc de C—]] is "an act of compulsion" rather than choice. As his coach advances, he catches himself forming "mean plans of dirty address" (043-007) and convicts himself: "I deserved the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] for every one of them."

A theatrical internal debate ensues. One voice in [[yorick|Yorick]] tries to compose words, attitudes, and tones to "wreath himself into [[duc-de-c|Monsieur le Duc de C—]]'s good graces." A second voice retorts that this is as futile as "a coat carried up to him by an adventurous tailor, without taking his measure." The corrective advice follows: observe the Duke's face first, note his posture, mark his expressions — and "from all these together you'll compound an address at once upon the spot, which cannot disgust the Duke; the ingredients are his own, and most likely to go down."

Yet a third voice pushes further, challenging the very premise of supplication: "Coward again! as if man to man was not equal throughout the whole surface of the globe; and if in the field — why not face to face in the cabinet too? … whenever it is not so, man is false to himself and betrays his own succours ten times where nature does it once." The advice is stark: "Go to the [[duc-de-c|Duc de C—]] with the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] in thy looks; — my life for it, thou wilt be sent back to [[paris|Paris]] in half an hour with an escort."

[[yorick|Yorick]] resolves on a middle course — "with all the gaiety and debonairness in the world" — only to be checked again: "A heart at ease, [[yorick|Yorick]], flies into no extremes — 'tis ever on its centre." By the time the coachman wheels into the court and stops at the door, [[yorick|Yorick]] finds himself "so much the better for my own lecture, that I neither ascended the steps like a victim to justice … nor did I mount them with a skip and a couple of strides."

At the door, however, a secretary tells him the Duke is busy for two hours. Rather than pace the saloon alone "as bad as being in the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] itself," [[yorick|Yorick]] returns to his remise and drives away — the address never delivered.

## 主题分析

- **The Drama of Self-Address**: The chapter's title is doubly significant — it refers both to the address [[yorick|Yorick]] means to deliver to the Duke and to the address he delivers to himself. The episode is a miniature of sentimental self-regulation: the mind divided against itself, negotiating between fear and dignity, until it finds its centre.

- **Equality and the Passport Crisis**: The internal argument that "man to man was not equal throughout the whole surface of the globe" in the field, so "why not face to face in the cabinet too?" is one of the novel's most radical statements. It frames the passport crisis — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s vulnerability as an Englishman in [[france|France]] — as a question not merely of politics but of existential equality.

- **The Unperformed Gesture**: That [[yorick|Yorick]] never actually delivers his address is a characteristic Sternean move. The significant action is internal — the re-calibration of the self — not external. The chapter ends with an anti-climax (the Duke is busy), yet the real work has already been done in the coach.
