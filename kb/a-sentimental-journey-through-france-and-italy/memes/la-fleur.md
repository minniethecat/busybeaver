# La Fleur

**La Fleur** is [[yorick|Yorick]]'s French servant, hired at [[montreuil|Montreuil]] in chapter 18 of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). A "clever young fellow" of boundless energy and theatrical charm, La Fleur serves as both comic relief and a foil to [[yorick|Yorick]]'s English sensibility, embodying the spontaneity and social ease that [[yorick|Yorick]] admires in the French character.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

La Fleur is introduced when [[yorick|Yorick]], having lost his portmanteau and repeatedly scrambled in the mud to secure it, realizes at the [[montreuil|Montreuil]] inn that what he truly lacks is a servant. The landlord recommends La Fleur as a young man who "would be very proud of the honour to serve an Englishman" — Englishmen being, in the landlord's estimation, "so generous." (018-010)

From this casual hiring, La Fleur becomes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s constant companion through [[amiens|Amiens]], [[paris|Paris]], [[versailles|Versailles]], and [[moulines|Moulines]]. He is by turns a valet, messenger, musician, diplomat, and master of ceremonies — a one-man ensemble whose zeal for his master's service is matched only by his talent for improvisation.

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### Hiring at Montreuil

La Fleur enters the novel standing "without, in that breathless expectation which every son of nature of us have felt in our turns" — a description that immediately casts him as a natural, unguarded character in contrast to the calculating types [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters elsewhere. The landlord vouches only for La Fleur's fidelity, leaving his talents for "Monsieur" to judge. (Ch. 18, [[montreuil|Montreuil]])

### The Letter Adventure at Amiens

La Fleur's most extended episode occupies the whole of chapter 28. Having entered [[yorick|Yorick]]'s service scarcely twenty-four hours earlier, he burns with impatience to prove himself. When the Count de L—'s servant arrives with a letter, La Fleur seizes the occasion:

> Fortune had not smiled upon La Fleur; for he had been unsuccessful in his feats of chivalry,—and not one thing had offered to signalise his zeal for my service from the time that he had entered into it, which was almost four-and-twenty hours. The poor soul burn'd with impatience; and the Count de L—'s servant coming with the letter, being the first practicable occasion which offer'd, La Fleur had laid hold of it. (028-001)

La Fleur treats the Count's servant to "the best wine in Picardy," is invited back to the Count's hotel, and soon has the entire kitchen — "dogs and cats, besides an old monkey" — dancing to his fife. His charm reaches [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]] herself, who summons him upstairs. When she asks whether he has brought a reply from [[yorick|Yorick]], La Fleur — unwilling to disappoint — invents one on the spot, then theatrically searches every pocket before confessing he left the letter at the inn.

The episode climaxes with La Fleur providing [[yorick|Yorick]] a love-letter template written by a drummer in his regiment to a corporal's wife — a document he produces from "a little dirty pocket book cramm'd full of small letters and billet-doux." The entire sequence displays La Fleur's signature blend of loyalty, theatricality, and endearing incompetence.

### Service in Paris and Versailles

La Fleur accompanies [[yorick|Yorick]] through [[paris|Paris]] (chapters 30–62) and to [[versailles|Versailles]] (chapters 43–50), where his social fluency proves invaluable. His ability to navigate French society — chatting with servants, charming landlords, and smoothing over [[yorick|Yorick]]'s linguistic and cultural missteps — makes him more than a servant; he is [[yorick|Yorick]]'s cultural interpreter.

### The Final Journey to Moulines

La Fleur remains with [[yorick|Yorick]] through the journey to [[moulines|Moulines]] (chapters 63–65), where they encounter [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]. His presence through the novel's most emotionally charged scene underscores his role as a steady, loyal companion through all of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental adventures.

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

La Fleur embodies several key themes of *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*:

**French Spontaneity vs. English Reserve**: Where [[yorick|Yorick]] hesitates, reflects, and moralizes, La Fleur acts. His instinctive social grace — what Sterne calls his "prevenancy" — represents a natural virtue that [[yorick|Yorick]]'s more self-conscious sensibility can only admire from a distance.

**The Dignity of Service**: Though a servant, La Fleur is never servile. His loyalty is freely given, and his schemes — however hapless — arise from genuine affection for his master. Sterne grants him a full interior life, evident in his trembling concern for [[yorick|Yorick]]'s honour in the letter episode.

**Comedy and [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: La Fleur bridges the novel's comic and sentimental registers. His escapades provide the book's broadest humor, yet his devotion to [[yorick|Yorick]] is presented with genuine warmth, never as mere buffoonery.
