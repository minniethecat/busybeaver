# Smelfungus

**Smelfungus** is a satirical portrait of the novelist and travel-writer Tobias Smollett (1721–1771), whom Laurence Sterne mercilessly lampoons in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*. [[travel|Travel]]ling "from [[boulogne|Boulogne]] to [[paris|Paris]] — from [[paris|Paris]] to [[rome|Rome]] — and so on," Smelfungus embodies everything that [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental mode of travel is not: splenetic, fault-finding, and constitutionally incapable of joy (017-001).

## Role Overview

Smelfungus appears in the early pages of the novel as a cautionary example of how *not* to travel. Sterne introduces him after a lyrical passage in which [[yorick|Yorick]] declares that he would carve his name upon the trees that sheltered him and teach himself to mourn if their leaves withered — a rhapsody of sympathetic connection to the world that sets up, by the sharpest possible contrast, the figure of the learned but miserable Smelfungus (017-002).

## Appearances

Smelfungus's travels take him across Europe, but Sterne records only the pattern of his responses, not the itinerary. "He set out with the spleen and jaundice, and every object he pass'd by was discoloured or distorted" (017-003). His written account of his journey, Sterne notes, was "nothing but the account of his miserable feelings" — a direct jab at Smollett's *[[travel|Travel]]s Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1766), which was known for its dyspeptic tone.

[[yorick|Yorick]] recounts meeting Smelfungus in the grand portico of the Pantheon in [[rome|Rome]]. Smelfungus has just emerged, and his verdict is delivered in a single dismissive phrase: "'Tis nothing but a huge cockpit" (017-004). [[yorick|Yorick]]'s reply is pointed: "I wish you had said nothing worse of the Venus of Medicis" — for in passing through Florence, [[yorick|Yorick]] had heard that Smelfungus "had fallen foul upon the goddess, and used her worse than a common strumpet, without the least provocation in nature" (017-005).

A second encounter occurs at [[turin|Turin]], where Smelfungus is returning home with "a sad tale of sorrowful adventures" — he has been "flayed alive, and bedevil'd, and used worse than St. Bartholomew, at every stage he had come at" (017-006). When Smelfungus threatens to publish his complaints to the world, [[yorick|Yorick]] delivers the novel's most devastating advice: "You had better tell it to your physician" (017-007).

## Character and Themes

Smelfungus functions as the novel's anti-type — the traveller whose internal disposition determines his external experience. Where [[yorick|Yorick]] finds occasions for sentiment and connection at every turn, Smelfungus finds only cause for complaint. The satire operates on two levels: as personal mockery of Smollett (whom Sterne regarded as a rival and whose travel book he disdained), and as a philosophical argument that the quality of experience depends on the quality of the experiencing soul.

Sterne's final gesture toward Smelfungus is one of pity rather than condemnation: "I heartily pity them; they have brought up no faculties for this work; and, were the happiest mansion in heaven to be allotted to Smelfungus and [[mundungus|Mundungus]], they would be so far from being happy, that the souls of Smelfungus and [[mundungus|Mundungus]] would do penance there to all eternity!" (017-008). This is the ultimate sentimental judgment — not that Smelfungus is wicked, but that he is incapable of happiness, which is the greater tragedy.
