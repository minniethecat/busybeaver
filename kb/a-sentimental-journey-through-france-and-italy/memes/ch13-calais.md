## CALAIS.

[013-001]Having , on the first sight of the

[013-002]lady, settled the affair in my fancy “that she was of the

[013-003]better order of beings;”—and then laid it down as a

[013-004]second axiom, as indisputable as the first, that she was a widow,

[013-005]and wore a character of distress,—I went no further; I got

[013-006]ground enough for the situation which pleased me;—and had

[013-007]she remained close beside my elbow till midnight, I should have

[013-008]held true to my system, and considered her only under that

[013-009]general idea. 

[013-010]She had scarce got twenty paces distant from me, ere something

[013-011]within me called out for a more particular enquiry;—it

[013-012]brought on the idea of a further separation:—I might

[013-013]possibly never see her more:—The heart is for saving what

[013-014]it can; and I wanted the traces through which my wishes might

[013-015]find their way to her, in case I should never rejoin her myself;

[013-016]in a word, I wished to know her name,—her

[013-017]family’s—her condition; and as I knew the place to

[013-018]which she was going, I wanted to know from whence she came: but

[013-019]there was no coming at all this intelligence; a hundred little

[013-020]delicacies stood in the way. I form’d a score

[013-021]different plans.—There was no such thing as a man’s

[013-022]asking her directly;—the thing was impossible. 

[013-023]A little French débonnaire captain, who came

[013-024]dancing down the street, showed me it was the easiest thing in

[013-025]the world: for, popping in betwixt us, just as the lady was returning

[013-026]back to the door of the Remise, he introduced himself to my

[013-027]acquaintance, and before he had well got announced, begg’d

[013-028]I would do him the honour to present him to the lady.—I had

[013-029]not been presented myself;—so turning about to her, he did

[013-030]it just as well, by asking her if she had come from Paris?

[013-031]No: she was going that route, she said.— Vous

[013-032]n’êtes pas de Londres ?—She was not, she

[013-033]replied.—Then Madame must have come through

[013-034]Flanders.— Apparemment vous êtes Flammande ?

[013-035]said the French captain.—The lady answered, she

[013-036]was.— Peut être de Lisle ? added he.—She

[013-037]said, she was not of Lisle.—Nor Arras?—nor

[013-038]Cambray?—nor Ghent?—nor Brussels?—She answered,

[013-039]she was of Brussels. 

[013-040]He had had the honour, he said, to be at the bombardment of it

[013-041]last war;—that it was finely situated, pour

[013-042]cela ,—and full of noblesse when the Imperialists were

[013-043]driven out by the French (the lady made a slight

[013-044]courtesy)—so giving her an account of the affair, and of

[013-045]the share he had had in it,—he begg’d the honour to

[013-046]know her name,—so made his bow. 

[013-047]— Et Madame a son Mari ?—said he, looking

[013-048]back when he had made two steps,—and, without staying for

[013-049]an answer—danced down the street. 

[013-050]Had I served seven years apprenticeship to good breeding, I

[013-051]could not have done as much.
