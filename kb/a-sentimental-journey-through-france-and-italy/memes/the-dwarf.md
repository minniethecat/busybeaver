# The Dwarf

**The Dwarf** is an unnamed figure whom [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters at the [[paris|Paris]] Opéra Comique. Physically small and defenceless, he becomes the centerpiece of a brief but pointed episode in which Sterne contrasts cruelty with compassion, dramatizing the sentimental ideal of intervening on behalf of the powerless (036-001).

## Role Overview

The dwarf appears in the chapter titled "The Dwarf" during [[yorick|Yorick]]'s stay in [[paris|Paris]]. He belongs to a class of people whom Sterne describes with characteristic sympathy — those "who have neither size nor strength to get on in the world" (036-002). Sterne introduces him after a philosophical digression on the prevalence of dwarfish figures in [[paris|Paris]], attributing their stunted growth to the cramped living conditions of the city. The dwarf serves as a narrative vehicle for exploring the theme of natural versus social disadvantage, and for demonstrating how a man of sentiment responds to witnessed injustice.

## Appearances

The dwarf's single but memorable appearance occurs at the Opéra Comique in [[paris|Paris]]. He has somehow found his way into a crowded standing area between the orchestra and the first side-box — a space where, despite paying the same price as orchestra seats, patrons must stand (036-003). The night is hot, and the dwarf is surrounded by people "two feet and a half higher than himself" (036-004).

His predicament deepens when a tall, corpulent German, near seven feet high, positions himself directly between the dwarf and the stage, blocking all possibility of seeing the performance. The dwarf tries every angle — seeking an opening between the German's arm and body, first on one side, then the other — but the German stands "square in the most unaccommodating posture that can be imagined" (036-005).

When the dwarf politely reaches up to touch the German's sleeve and explain his distress, the German looks down upon him "as Goliah did upon David — and unfeelingly resumed his posture" (036-006). In desperation, the dwarf threatens to cut off the German's long queue with his knife, to which the German coolly replies that he is welcome to try — if he can reach it (036-007).

At this point [[the-old-french-officer|the old French officer]], seated beside [[yorick|Yorick]], notices [[yorick|Yorick]]'s emotional response and asks what is the matter. After hearing the story, the officer leans over, nods to a sentinel, and points at the scene. The sentinel makes his way through the crowd and thrusts the German back with his musket, taking the dwarf by the hand and placing him in front where he can see (036-008). [[yorick|Yorick]] claps his hands together and exclaims, "This is noble!"

## Character and Themes

The dwarf embodies several of Sterne's core sentimental themes. Physically powerless in a world built for larger bodies, he represents the vulnerable members of society whom Sterne's philosophy of sentiment commands us to protect. His polite initial approach to the German — a civil touch on the sleeve — contrasts sharply with the German's brutish indifference, establishing a moral hierarchy in which physical size is inversely proportional to human decency.

The episode also illustrates the sentimental principle of active intervention. [[yorick|Yorick]], though moved by the scene, does not act directly; instead, [[the-old-french-officer|the old French officer]] provides the model of effective compassion — a quiet word and a pointed finger, using institutional authority (the sentinel) to achieve justice without confrontation. This indirect, graceful resolution reflects Sterne's broader preference for the French mode of social correction over what he portrays as the more rigid English approach.
