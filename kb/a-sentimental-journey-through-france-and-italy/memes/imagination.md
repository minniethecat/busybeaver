# Imagination

Imagination is the engine of sentimental experience in Sterne's novel. More than a mere picture-making faculty, [[yorick|Yorick]]'s imagination is an active, transformative power — the agent that converts a bare perception into a scene of feeling. "My imagination went on," he writes, and the phrase captures the dynamic: imagination extends, embellishes, and deepens what the senses merely register.

## 定义与历史语境

Eighteenth-century philosophy was deeply ambivalent about the imagination. For Hobbes it was "decaying sense"; for Hume, the associative faculty that binds experience into coherent thought; for the Romantics-to-be, the creative power that makes art and sympathy possible. Sterne, writing at mid-century, occupies a middle ground: his imagination is both a susceptibility (prone to "illusions" and misreadings) and a moral faculty (the means by which we enter into the feelings of others). This ambiguity — imagination as both weakness and strength — runs through every passage in which the word appears.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The novel opens with imagination: "it stands presented to my imagination," [[yorick|Yorick]] says of the journey, acknowledging from the first line that travel is as much an act of mental construction as of physical movement. Throughout the book, Sterne tracks the imagination's operation with precise attention. A syllable uttered "crowded back into my imagination"; a thought "gave full scope to my imagination"; an error proves to be "the fault of his own imagination."

The imagination in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the faculty that bridges the gap between what is seen and what is felt. When [[yorick|Yorick]] looks at a woman, his imagination supplies a history, a character, a probable future — and it is this imagined supplement that makes the encounter sentimental. The danger, which Sterne acknowledges, is that imagination can mislead: "the illusions of an imagination which is eternally misleading me."

## 关键段落

> "it stands presented to my imagination, it gained more than it lost by the change." (The Preface chapter)

> "every syllable I had utter'd crowded back into my imagination." (Calais chapters)

> "nothing operated more upon their imaginations." (Paris chapters)

> "But my imagination went on,—I recalled her." (Paris chapters)

> "the illusions of an imagination which is eternally misleading me." (The Grace chapter)
