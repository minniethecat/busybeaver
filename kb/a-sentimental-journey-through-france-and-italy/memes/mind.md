# Mind

The mind in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is the heart's companion and occasional antagonist. Sterne gives the mind a distinct voice and presence — it is the faculty that "feeds" on variety, "frames" experience, "takes a certain turn," and is filled or expanded by what it encounters. If the heart feels, the mind reflects on the feeling; if the heart leaps, the mind names the leap. The two faculties together constitute the novel's model of full human response.

## 定义与历史语境

The eighteenth century was the great age of the philosophy of mind. Locke's *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1689) had made the mind's operations the central subject of philosophy; Hume and Hartley developed associationist models; the Scottish Common Sense school insisted on the mind's direct access to reality. Sterne was steeped in this literature — *Tristram Shandy* is in large part a novel about the mind's associative habits — and *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* extends the inquiry into the less comic, more affective territory of travel and encounter.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The mind appears in the novel in several characteristic modes. It is the faculty that seeks "variety of character to feed" itself — travel as intellectual appetite. It "takes a certain turn" — the mental disposition that colours all perception. It is framed or unframed by mood: "the frame of mind I was in attached me much more to it." And it is the seat of judgment, for better and worse — [[yorick|Yorick]] can be "satisfied to my heart's content with my empire" in a kind of mental self-congratulation, or he can "set my mind to the business I was upon" with good-humoured purpose.

Crucially, Sterne never opposes mind to heart in simple binary. The mind feeds the heart by supplying material for sympathy; the heart quickens the mind by lending it motive. The ideal state — glimpsed in the novel's finest passages — is a mind animated by feeling and a heart illuminated by reflection.
