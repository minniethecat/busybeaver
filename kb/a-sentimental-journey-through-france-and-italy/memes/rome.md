# Rome

**Rome** appears in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) not as an experienced destination but as a point of reference — a city reached by the satirical traveler [[mundungus|Smelfungus]] and glimpsed through the longing of the marginalized. It embodies the classical terminus of the Grand Tour, the cultural capital towards which all continental travel ostensibly tended in the 18th century.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

Rome enters the novel through two contrasting lenses:

**[[smelfungus|Smelfungus]]'s Rome**: Sterne's caricature of Tobias Smollett "travelled from [[boulogne|Boulogne]] to [[paris|Paris]], — from [[paris|Paris]] to Rome, — and so on" (Ch. 2, [[calais|Calais]]), finding nothing but complaint along the way. [[smelfungus|Smelfungus]] "wrote an account of 'em, but 'twas nothing but the account of his miserable feelings." His Rome, extending "from Rome to [[naples|Naples]], — from [[naples|Naples]] to [[venice|Venice]]" (Ch. 2, [[calais|Calais]]), is a litany of grievances — a warning that no destination, however storied, can satisfy a sour disposition.

**[[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]'s Rome**: In one of the novel's most poignant details, [[maria-of-moulines|Maria of Moulines]] tells [[yorick|Yorick]] that she had "stray'd as far as Rome" (Ch. 64, [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]) in her wanderings. For [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] — a grief-stricken young woman who has lost her lover and her reason — Rome is not a Grand Tour triumph but a measure of her displacement, the farthest point of her unmoored travels before she returned to sit by the roadside at [[moulines|Moulines]].

## 象征意义 (Symbolic Significance)

Rome in Sterne's novel functions ironically. The Eternal City — repository of classical learning, papal authority ("pardons from Rome," Ch. 35), and artistic pilgrimage — is reduced to a name checked off by a miserable tourist and a waypoint in a mad girl's wanderings. This is consistent with the novel's broader project: to privilege the heartfelt encounter over the monumental sight, the roadside sentiment over the celebrated destination.
