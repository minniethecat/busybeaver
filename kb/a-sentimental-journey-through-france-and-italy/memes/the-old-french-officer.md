# The Old French Officer

**The Old French Officer** is a retired French military man whom [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters in [[paris|Paris]] in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Seated beside [[yorick|Yorick]] at some public venue (implicitly a theatre or opera), the officer is reading attentively when a disturbance — a cry of "Haussez les mains!" — draws both men's attention and launches a discussion of honour, national character, and the difference between English and French sensibilities.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Old French Officer is introduced with warmth and immediate sympathy:

> I was let into but a kindly old French officer. I love the character, not only because I have honour for the man whose heart is the home of the quality, but because I have a filial reverence for the corps. (035-002)

He is reading a small pamphlet with absorption when the incident occurs. His responses to [[yorick|Yorick]]'s questions are measured, philosophical, and tinged with a gentle world-weariness that marks him as one of the novel's most quietly admirable figures.

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Cry of "Haussez les mains!"

[[yorick|Yorick]] and the Old Officer hear a cry — someone demanding that hands be raised. The incident turns out to involve a suspected pickpocket, and the officer explains the scene with calm detachment. His manner — "an air of such benignity" — transforms what might be a tense moment into an occasion for cross-cultural reflection. (035-002)

### The English Constitution

When [[yorick|Yorick]] reacts with indignation to an illiberal remark about the English constitution, the Old Officer gently corrects him:

> The old French officer smiled, and whispering in my ear, assured me it was an illiberal sarcasm at the English constitution, which had no more foundation than the cry itself. (Ch. 30, Paris)

The officer's smile and whisper — his refusal to escalate — model a civility that [[yorick|Yorick]], in his more impulsive moments, struggles to achieve.

### A Lesson in Honour

The officer's most significant contribution is his discussion of honour. Having spent his life in military service, he speaks of it not as an abstraction but as a lived code — one that, in the [[calais|France]] of the *ancien régime*, is increasingly precarious. His own circumstances (a "small patrimony" spent in service, a company and cross earned, then the regiment reformed and himself left "without friends, without a livre") give his reflections the weight of experience rather than theory. (Ch. 30, [[paris|Paris]])

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**French [[honour|Honour]] vs. English [[feeling|Sensibility]]**: The Old Officer provides a counterpoint to [[yorick|Yorick]]'s English sentiment. Where [[yorick|Yorick]] feels, the officer acts according to a code. The contrast is not adversarial — Sterne presents both modes with sympathy — but it illuminates the different moral vocabularies available to the two nations.

**The Dignity of Decline**: Like the Monk of [[calais|Calais]], the Old Officer is a figure of diminished circumstances borne with composure. His "sedate look, something approaching to gravity" speaks to a life of service rewarded with neglect — a quiet indictment of the system that has left him selling pâtés or reading pamphlets in his retirement.

**Cosmopolitan [[courtesy|Civility]]**: In the officer's willingness to smile at national slights rather than bristle at them, Sterne sketches an ideal of cosmopolitan exchange — the very quality his narrator aspires to but frequently falls short of.
