# Quai de Conti

The **Quai de Conti** is a quay along the Seine in [[paris|Paris]] that appears repeatedly in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Though mentioned only five times, it serves as a narrative crossroads where books, chance encounters, and aristocratic connections intersect — embodying the novel's sentimental logic of association.

## 在小说中的角色

The Quai de Conti functions as a backstage of [[paris|Paris]]ian life, threading together three distinct but interwoven threads of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s [[paris|Paris]] experience. Unlike grand public spaces like the Opera, the quay is a place of transit and informal encounter — making it the perfect setting for the novel's associative method.

**[[the-bookseller|The Bookseller]]'s Shop**. The Quai de Conti first appears when [[yorick|Yorick]], having reflected on [[the-old-french-officer|the old French officer]]'s literary advice, stops there "in my return home, to purchase the whole set" of Shakespeare's works. The bookseller tells him the set on the counter is reserved for the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], triggering [[yorick|Yorick]]'s surprised question: "And does the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], said I, read Shakespeare? C'est un esprit fort, replied the bookseller. — He loves English books! and what is more to his honour, Monsieur, he loves the English too." (036-PPP) This fleeting exchange plants the seed for one of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s most consequential [[paris|Paris]] decisions.

**The Walk with the Fille de Chambre**. After the bookshop, [[yorick|Yorick]] and the young fille de chambre — who has just purchased *Les Égarements du Cœur et de l'Esprit* — walk out together. "Notwithstanding it was dusky, yet as both our roads lay the same way, we made no scruple of walking along the Quai de Conti together." (037-PPP) The walk becomes a miniature sentimental drama: the girl thanks [[yorick|Yorick]] repeatedly, he reflects on her innocence, they navigate the streets toward the [[rue-de-nevers|Rue de Nevers]], and the encounter culminates in a parting so warm that [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects, "had it happened any where else, I'm not sure but I should have signed it with a kiss of charity, as warm and holy as an apostle." (037-PPP)

**[[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s Observation**. Unbeknownst to [[yorick|Yorick]], [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] has followed him through the streets "as far as the bookseller's shop; but seeing me come out with the young fille de chambre, and that we walk'd down the Quai de Conti together, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] deem'd it unnecessary to follow me a step further." (041-PPP) The loyal servant makes his own reflections, takes a shorter route, and arrives at the hotel in time to learn of the police affair against [[yorick|Yorick]]'s arrival — a narrative detail that transforms the quay from a simple setting into a switchboard of observation and misdirection.

**The [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] Connection**. Days later, on the road to [[versailles|Versailles]], [[yorick|Yorick]] recalls "the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], of whom [[the-bookseller|the bookseller at the Quai de Conti]] had spoke so handsomely the night before." (046-PPP) This memory triggers [[yorick|Yorick]]'s decision to seek out the Count — a decision that will dominate the [[versailles|Versailles]] chapters and ultimately secure his passport. The Quai de Conti thus serves as the origin point for one of the novel's major narrative arcs.

**[[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]]'s Errand**. The quay receives its final mention when [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]]'s fille de chambre comes to find [[yorick|Yorick]]: "It was [[the-fille-de-chambre|the fair fille de chambre]] I had walked along the Quai de Conti with; [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] had sent her upon some commission." (061-PPP) The circular return to the Quai de Conti walk underscores the novel's associative structure — every encounter leaves a thread that can be pulled later.
