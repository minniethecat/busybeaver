# The Fille de Chambre

**The Fille de Chambre** (the chambermaid) is a young Frenchwoman employed by [[madame-de-l|Madame de R—]] in [[paris|Paris]]. She appears in a chapter entirely devoted to her in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), where her encounter with [[yorick|Yorick]] — involving lace, a walk along the Seine, and a charged moment in his hotel room — becomes one of the novel's most delicately handled explorations of desire and restraint.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Fille de Chambre is introduced as a *femme de chambre* to "some devout woman of fashion" — later revealed to be [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]]. She is young, attractive, and possesses a natural grace that [[yorick|Yorick]] finds both charming and unsettling. Unlike the novel's more theatrical characters ([[la-fleur|La Fleur]], the Monk), she is understated — her power lies in what is half-said, half-done, and in the blush that seems to communicate more than words.

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Lace Purchase

The Fille de Chambre first appears when she approaches [[yorick|Yorick]] to sell lace — a pretext that allows Sterne to stage an encounter between the English traveller and a pretty young Frenchwoman on terms of commercial transaction that quickly shade into something more intimate. (038-019)

### The Walk on the Quai de Conti

[[yorick|Yorick]] walks with her along the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]] by the Seine, and the stroll becomes an occasion for conversation — part flirtation, part moral instruction, as [[yorick|Yorick]] finds himself offering her "a lesson of virtue" that his own impulses are already undermining. (Ch. 46, [[paris|Paris]])

### The Hotel Room

The most memorable episode occurs when the Fille de Chambre, sent by [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] to enquire after [[yorick|Yorick]], follows him into his hotel room:

> It was a fine still evening in the latter end of the month of May, — the crimson window curtains (which were of the same colour as those of the bed) were drawn close: — the sun was setting, and reflected through them so warm a tint into the fair fille de chambre's face, — I thought she blush'd. (051-018)

The scene that follows — [[yorick|Yorick]] searching for a card he knows he doesn't have, his trembling hand, her offer to hold the ink — is a masterpiece of erotic suspension. "The devil was in me," [[yorick|Yorick]] confesses, and rather than succumb, he leads her to the door and bids her goodnight. The restraint, as much as any passion, defines the episode.

### Madame de R—'s Messenger

Throughout the [[paris|Paris]] chapters, the Fille de Chambre functions as messenger between [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] and [[yorick|Yorick]] — carrying enquiries, delivering word of intended visits, and embodying the connection [[yorick|Yorick]] perpetually fails to honor with his actual presence.

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**[[desire|Desire]] and [[virtue|Virtue]]**: The Fille de Chambre episode is Sterne's most concentrated study of erotic temptation — and of the sentimentalist's strategy of fleeing rather than fighting. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s admission that "I generally fly myself" rather than resist the devil directly makes the Fille de Chambre a test case for the limits of sentimental self-control.

**The [[blush|Blush]] as Language**: Throughout the episode, blushes communicate what words cannot. "There is a sort of a pleasing half guilty blush, where the blood is more in fault than the man" — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s meditation on the blush elevates it to a sentimental semiotics, a language of the body more honest than speech.

**Class and Intimacy**: The Fille de Chambre's status as a servant creates an asymmetry that Sterne neither exploits nor ignores. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s "lesson of virtue" carries an uncomfortable pedagogical tone, and the episode's tension partly arises from the unstable power dynamics between an English gentleman and a French lady's maid.
