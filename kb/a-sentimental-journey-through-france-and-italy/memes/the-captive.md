# The Captive

**The Captive** is a brief but emotionally intense episode in the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), immediately preceding "[[the-starling|The Starling]]." Haunted by the caged bird's refrain "I can't get out," [[yorick|Yorick]] retreats to his room and, through an act of concentrated imagination, conjures the portrait of a prisoner in a dungeon — a fantasy of solitary confinement so powerful that it moves him to tears and propels him toward [[versailles|Versailles]] to secure his own liberty.

## 概要

"The bird in his cage pursued me into my room." [[yorick|Yorick]] sits down at his table, leans his head upon his hand, and "began to figure to myself the miseries of confinement." He considers beginning with "the millions of my fellow-creatures born to no inheritance but slavery," but finds the multitude too diffuse — "the multitude of sad groups in it did but distract me." Instead, he takes "a single captive" and shuts him in his dungeon (041-011).

What follows is one of the novel's most sustained passages of imaginative sympathy. Looking through "the twilight of his grated door," [[yorick|Yorick]] beholds a body "half-wasted away with long expectation and confinement," feeling "what kind of sickness of the heart it was which arises from hope deferr'd." The prisoner is pale and feverish: "in thirty years the western breeze had not once fann'd his blood; — he had seen no sun, no moon, in all that time — nor had the voice of friend or kinsman breathed through his lattice."

The portrait accumulates detail with mounting pathos. The captive sits on a little straw in the furthest corner, which is "alternately his chair and bed." A "little calendar of small sticks" at his head is notched with the dismal days and nights; in his hand he holds one stick, etching "another day of misery to add to the heap" with a rusty nail. When [[yorick|Yorick]]'s imagining darkens the little light, the captive "lifted up a hopeless eye towards the door, then cast it down, — shook his head, and went on with his work of affliction."

The climax comes with the sound of chains: "I heard his chains upon his legs, as he turned his body to lay his little stick upon the bundle. — He gave a deep sigh. — I saw the iron enter into his soul!" [[yorick|Yorick]] bursts into tears — "I could not sustain the picture of confinement which my fancy had drawn" — and springs into action. He calls for [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], orders a remise ready by nine in the morning, and declares: "I'll go directly to [[duc-de-c|Monsieur le Duc de Choiseul]]."

The episode is a turning point in the passport plot. What began as bureaucratic inconvenience becomes, through the power of imagination, an existential threat — and the spur to decisive action.

## 主题分析

- **The Work of [[imagination|Imagination]]**: The chapter is a demonstration of sentimental imagination at its most powerful and most self-reflexive. [[yorick|Yorick]] knows the captive is his own invention — "the picture of confinement which my fancy had drawn" — yet the tears it produces are real, and so is the action that follows. [[imagination|Imagination]] is not escape from reality but its intensification.

- **Confinement and [[liberty|Liberty]]**: The caged starling's cry — "I can't get out" — echoes through the chapter as a metaphor for the human condition. The prisoner's dungeon is also [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own vulnerability as an Englishman without a passport in [[france|France]]. The episode transforms the passport plot from a comic inconvenience into a meditation on the fragility of freedom.

- **From [[feeling|Feeling]] to Action**: Unlike other sentimental episodes where feeling is its own end, "The Captive" shows sentiment driving practical resolution. The tears lead directly to the decision to seek the [[duc-de-c|Duc de Choiseul]]. The episode is a model of how sympathy, properly cultivated, issues in effective action.
