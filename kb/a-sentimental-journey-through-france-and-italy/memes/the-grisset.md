# The Grisset

**The Grisset** is a young [[paris|Paris]]ian glove-seller whom [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters in a shop on his way to the opera in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). The brief, largely silent interaction between the traveller and the shop-girl is one of the novel's most celebrated miniature scenes — a study in unspoken gallantry, mutual attraction conducted entirely through glances, and the sentimental economy of small commercial transactions.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

The Grisset (from French *grisette*, a young working-class woman of coquettish reputation) appears in a single chapter — "The Gloves" — but the scene has become one of the most discussed episodes in Sterne's novel. She is the object of one of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s most characteristic moments: a flirtation conducted not through words but through a silent exchange of looks across a shop counter, mediated by a pair of gloves that will not fit. The encounter condenses the novel's themes of desire, politeness, and the sentimental transaction into a few pages of exquisite dramatic economy. (034-022)

## 出场与经历 (Appearances)

### The Glove-Shop Encounter

[[yorick|Yorick]] enters the shop ostensibly to purchase gloves, having just had an exchange with another young woman about the beating of hearts. The Grisset rises, goes behind the counter, and reaches down a parcel of gloves. She measures them against his hand one by one — none fit. She begs him to try a single pair that seems smallest; she holds it open, his hand slips in at once — but "it will not do." Both shake their heads. (034-022)

What follows is the famous pantomime at the counter:

> "The beautiful grisette looked sometimes at the gloves, then sideways to the window, then at the gloves, — and then at me. I was not disposed to break silence: — I followed her example: so, I looked at the gloves, then to the window, then at the gloves, and then at her, — and so on alternately. I found I lost considerably in every attack: — she had a quick black eye, and shot through two such long and silken eyelashes with such penetration, that she look'd into my very heart and reins. — It may seem strange, but I could actually feel she did." (034-035)

The entire exchange occurs without a word spoken. [[yorick|Yorick]], acknowledging defeat in this silent contest, takes up two pairs of gloves — any pairs — and puts them into his pocket, ending the scene.

### The Economic Delicacy

The Grisset "had not asked above a single livre above the price." [[yorick|Yorick]], wishing she had asked more, puzzles over how to bring the matter about without offending her. She mistakes his hesitation for suspicion and asks, with wounded dignity, whether he thinks her capable of overcharging a stranger — "and of a stranger whose politeness, more than his want of gloves, has done me the honour to lay himself at my mercy." [[yorick|Yorick]] pays, makes an unusually low bow for a shopkeeper, and departs. (Ch. 39, [[paris|Paris]])

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**The Language of Looks**: The Grisset scene is Sterne's purest demonstration of non-verbal communication. The entire emotional content of the encounter — attraction, playfulness, self-consciousness, surrender — is conveyed through the choreography of eyes, gloves, window, and counter. Language, when it finally arrives, is only about money and manners; the real transaction has already occurred.

**Commerce and [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: The encounter blurs the line between commercial and sentimental exchange. [[yorick|Yorick]] buys gloves he does not need as a pretext for a flirtation he cannot name. The Grisset charges a fair price but accepts his gallantry as a form of extra-economic tribute. The scene suggests that all human transactions — even the most openly commercial — are suffused with sentiment.
