# The Rose

**The Rose** is a two-part episode from the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768). In the first part, the [[the-old-french-officer|old French officer]] educates [[yorick|Yorick]] on national prejudice and mutual toleration at the [[paris|Paris]] [[the-opera|Opéra]]. In the second, [[yorick|Yorick]] recounts a coach ride with [[madame-de-rambouliet|Madame de Rambouliet]] that tests the boundaries of delicacy — and concludes with one of the novel's most famous metaphors: "go each one pluck your rose."

## 概要

The episode begins at the opera, where a cry of "Haussez les mains, Monsieur l'Abbé!" rings through the parterre. [[yorick|Yorick]], bewildered, asks the [[the-old-french-officer|old French officer]] what it means. The officer explains that a poor abbé has been caught hiding behind two grisettes in the upper boxes, and the crowd is demanding he hold up both hands to prove he is not picking their pockets.

[[yorick|Yorick]] is scandalized — "is it possible, that a people so smit with sentiment should at the same time be so unclean?" The French officer gently corrects him, delivering a miniature philosophy of travel and toleration: every nation has its "refinements and grossièrtés," and "there is a balance of good and bad everywhere; and nothing but the knowing it is so, can emancipate one half of the world from the prepossession which it holds against the other." The advantage of travel, he concludes with a bow, is that "mutual toleration taught us mutual love" (037-033).

[[yorick|Yorick]] is so struck by the officer's candour and good sense that he thinks he loves the man — before honestly admitting it was his own way of thinking, "the difference was, I could not have expressed it half so well."

The second part shifts abruptly to an intimate anecdote. [[madame-de-rambouliet|Madame de Rambouliet]], "the most correct" of all women, has taken [[yorick|Yorick]] in her coach two leagues out of town. On the return journey, she asks him to pull the cord and declares, "Rien que pour pisser." [[yorick|Yorick]] addresses the reader directly: "Grieve not, gentle traveller, to let [[madame-de-rambouliet|Madame de Rambouliet]] p—ss on." He then delivers the episode's emblematic line: "And, ye fair mystic nymphs! go each one pluck your rose, and scatter them in your path, — for [[madame-de-rambouliet|Madame de Rambouliet]] did no more." He hands her out of the coach with the reverence of "the priest of the chaste Castalia."

## 主题分析

- **Toleration Through [[travel|Travel]]**: The old French officer's speech is one of the novel's most explicit pronouncements on the moral purpose of travel. The recognition that every nation balances good and bad — and that knowing this "emancipates" us from prejudice — is central to Sterne's cosmopolitan vision.

- **Delicacy and [[nature|Nature]]**: The "rose" metaphor transforms an indelicate bodily function into an emblem of natural grace. By elevating [[madame-de-rambouliet|Madame de Rambouliet]]'s frankness to the level of classical purity (Castalia's fountain), Sterne challenges the reader's own delicacy — or prurience.

- **Honest Self-Reflection**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s admission that his love for the officer was really self-love — "my own way of thinking" — is characteristic of the novel's psychological honesty. [[sentiment|Sentiment]], Sterne suggests, can easily be mistaken for virtue when it is merely self-congratulation.
