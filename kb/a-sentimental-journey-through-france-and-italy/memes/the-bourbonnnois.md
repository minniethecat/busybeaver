# The Bourbonnois

**The Bourbonnois** (also spelled Bourbonnais) is a historic province in central France, corresponding roughly to the modern department of Allier. In Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), it is the region through which [[yorick|Yorick]] passes on his way from [[paris|Paris]] towards [[italy|Italy]] — a landscape of vintage abundance that forms a poignant contrast with the suffering he encounters there.

## 书中的 Bourbonnois

[[yorick|Yorick]] enters the Bourbonnois after a period of social exhaustion in [[paris|Paris]]. Having "forced upon my beggarly system" the demands of Parisian society — "the better the coterie, the more children of Art" — he grows sick, goes to bed, and orders horses to set out for [[italy|Italy]].

What follows is one of the novel's most lyrical passages of landscape description. The Bourbonnois is "the sweetest part of France," traveled "in the heyday of the vintage, when Nature is pouring her abundance into every one's lap, and every eye is lifted up, — a journey, through each step of which Music beats time to Labour, and all her children are rejoicing as they carry in their clusters."

The abundance is almost painful to [[yorick|Yorick]], whose "affections" fly out and kindle "at every group before me — and every one of them was pregnant with adventures." He laments that "it would fill up twenty volumes — and alas! I have but a few small pages left of this to crowd it into."

## Maria at Moulines

The Bourbonnois is the setting for the novel's most celebrated sentimental episode: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s encounter with Maria near [[moulines|Moulines]]. The juxtaposition is deliberate and powerful — a landscape of communal rejoicing giving way to an isolated figure of private grief.

Maria, the disordered maid whose story [[yorick|Yorick]] had read in a friend's account, is found sitting under a poplar tree near Moulines, her elbow in her lap and her head leaning on her hand. The encounter — in which [[yorick|Yorick]] wipes her tears, feels "undescribable emotions," and declares "I am positive I have a soul" — is the novel's emotional climax, and the Bourbonnois is the landscape that frames it.

The region thus functions in two registers simultaneously: as a real French province celebrated for its beauty and vintage, and as a symbolic landscape in which the novel's deepest emotional work is done. The sweetness of the place makes the suffering it contains all the more acute.

## 主题分析

- **Landscape as Emotional Contrast**: The Bourbonnois passage is a masterclass in the sentimental use of landscape. The "heyday of the vintage," with "Nature pouring her abundance into every one's lap," establishes a world of communal joy against which Maria's solitary sorrow is measured. The contrast is not merely descriptive but moral: abundance makes want visible, and collective rejoicing throws individual grief into sharper relief.

- **The Limits of Narrative**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s lament that the Bourbonnois "would fill up twenty volumes — and alas! I have but a few small pages left" is one of the novel's most explicit acknowledgements of its own formal constraints. The Journey is defined by what it cannot contain — the "adventures" that every group of reapers might yield, the volumes that will never be written. The Bourbonnois represents narrative plenitude, and the novel's necessary response is renunciation.
