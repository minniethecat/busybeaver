# The Letter

**The Letter** is a comic-sentimental episode in the [[amiens|Amiens]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), framed as the chapter "THE LETTER. AMIENS." It marks [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s first significant service to [[yorick|Yorick]] and is a masterclass in Sterne's humor of social imposture — a servant who cannot disappoint, a master who cannot write, and a borrowed billet-doux that bridges the gap.

## 概要

[[fortune|Fortune]] had not smiled upon [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] — for he had been unsuccessful in any feats of chivalry from the time he entered [[yorick|Yorick]]'s service, almost four-and-twenty hours prior. The poor soul "burn'd with impatience" (001-001); and the [[count-de-b|Count de L—]]'s servant arriving with a letter became the "first practicable occasion which offer'd."

[[la-fleur|La Fleur]] seized it. He took the Count's servant into a back parlour of the auberge in [[amiens]] and "treated him with a cup or two of the best wine in Picardy" (001-002). The servant, not to be behindhand in politeness, took [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] back to the Count's hotel. There [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s prevenancy — "for there was a passport in his very looks" — soon set every servant at ease. In less than five minutes, he "had pulled out his fife, and leading off the dance himself with the first note, set the *fille de chambre*, the *maître d'hôtel*, the cook, the scullion, and all the household, dogs and cats, besides an old monkey, a dancing" (001-003). "I suppose there never was a merrier kitchen since the flood."

[[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]], passing from her brother's apartments to her own, heard the jollity below stairs and rang up her *[[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]]* to ask about it. Hearing it was the English gentleman's servant who had "set the whole house merry with his pipe," she ordered him up. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], unable to present himself empty, loaded himself "with a thousand compliments to [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]], on the part of his master" — adding "a long apocrypha of inquiries" after her health, telling her that "Monsieur his master was *au désespoire* for her re-establishment from the fatigues of her journey" — and, to close all, that Monsieur had received the letter which Madame had done him the honour to send (001-004).

And, said [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]], interrupting — "he has done me the honour to send a billet in return." She said this "with such a tone of reliance upon the fact, that [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] had not power to disappoint her expectations." He trembled for [[yorick|Yorick]]'s honour — "and possibly might not altogether be unconcerned for his own, as a man capable of being attached to a master who could be wanting *en égards vis à vis d'une femme!*" (001-005). So when Madame asked if he had brought a letter — "*O qu'oui*," said [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]. He laid his hat on the ground, took hold of his right pocket with his left hand, and searched — then contrariwise — "*Diable!*" Then every pocket, not forgetting his fob — "*Peste!*" Then emptied them upon the floor: a dirty cravat, a handkerchief, a comb, a whip lash, a nightcap. He peeped into his hat: "*Quelle étourderie!*" He had left the letter upon the table in the auberge — he would run for it and be back in three minutes (001-005).

The episode now shifts to [[yorick|Yorick]], who had just finished his supper when [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] came in to give an account of the adventure. He "told the whole story simply as it was," adding only that if Monsieur had forgot (*par hazard*) to answer Madame's letter, "the arrangement gave him an opportunity to recover the *faux pas*" — and if not, things were only as they were (001-006).

[[yorick|Yorick]] was not altogether sure of his *étiquette* — whether he ought to have written or no. But if he had, "a devil himself could not have been angry": it was but "the officious zeal of a well-meaning creature for my honour." However [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] might have "mistook the road — or embarrassed me in so doing — his heart was in no fault." [[yorick|Yorick]] was under no necessity to write — "and what weighed more than all — he did not look as if he had done amiss" (001-006).

"'Tis all very well, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]," said [[yorick|Yorick]]. That was sufficient. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] "flew out of the room like lightning, and returned with pen, ink, and paper, in his hand; and coming up to the table, laid them close before me, with such a delight in his countenance, that I could not help taking up the pen" (001-007).

But writing proved impossible. "I began and began again; and though I had nothing to say, and that nothing might have been expressed in half a dozen lines, I made half a dozen different beginnings, and could no way please myself." [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] brought water to dilute the ink — then sand and seal-wax. "It was all one; I wrote, and blotted, and tore off, and burnt, and wrote again." At last, [[yorick|Yorick]] threw the pen down despairingly: "*Le diable l'emporte!* — I cannot write this self-same letter" (001-008).

Then [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] advanced "with the most respectful carriage up to the table, and making a thousand apologies for the liberty he was going to take," told me he had "a letter in his pocket wrote by a drummer in his regiment to a corporal's wife, which he durst say would suit the occasion" (001-009). [[yorick|Yorick]], having "a mind to let the poor fellow have his humour," asked to see it. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] instantly produced "a little dirty pocket-book cramm'd full of small letters and *billet-doux* in a sad condition," untied the string, ran them over one by one, and arrived at the letter in question. "*La voila!*" he cried, clapping his hands. He unfolded it, laid it open before [[yorick|Yorick]], and "retired three steps from the table whilst I read it."

The letter itself is a French love-letter — an artifact embedded whole within Sterne's text:

> Madame,  
> *Je suis pénétré de la douleur la plus vive, et réduit en même temps au désespoir par ce retour imprévù du Caporal qui rend notre entrevûe de ce soir la chose du monde la plus impossible.*  
> *Mais vive la joie! et toute la mienne sera de penser à vous.*  
> *L'amour n'est rien sans sentiment.*  
> *Et le sentiment est encore moins sans amour.*  
> *On dit qu'on ne doit jamais se désesperér.*  
> *On dit aussi que Monsieur le Caporal monte la garde Mercredi: alors ce cera mon tour.*  
> *Chacun à son tour.*  
> *En attendant — Vive l'amour! et vive la bagatelle!*  
> *Je suis,* Madame,  
> *Avec tous les sentimens les plus respectueux et les plus tendres, tout à vous,*  
> **Jaques Roque**.

"It was but changing the Corporal into the Count — and saying nothing about mounting guard on Wednesday — and the letter was neither right nor wrong" (001-009). To gratify the poor fellow, who "stood trembling for my honour, his own, and the honour of his letter," [[yorick|Yorick]] "took the cream gently off it, and whipping it up in my own way, I seal'd it up and sent him with it to [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]]." The next morning they pursued their journey to [[paris]].

## 主题分析

- **The Comedy of Servant Zeal**: [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s entire performance — the thousand compliments, the apocrypha of inquiries, the pantomime of searching every pocket — is not deceit but devotion. He cannot bear to disappoint [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]]'s expectation because it would reflect on his master's honour (and "possibly might not altogether be unconcerned for his own"). Sterne presents [[la-fleur|the servant]]'s imposture as a form of affection (001-004–005).

- **Epistolary Etiquette and Language**: The letter is the chapter's central object, but [[yorick|Yorick]] cannot produce one because he is "in no mood to write." Language here is both social currency and burden. The solution arrives in French — a letter not [[yorick|Yorick]]'s own, written by a drummer to a corporal's wife. "L'amour n'est rien sans sentiment" becomes the chapter's accidental motto: the letter's sentiment is borrowed, but the social act it enables is real (001-008–009).

- **[[sentiment|Sentiment]]al Imposture**: The chapter stages a layered imposture. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] pretends a letter exists; [[yorick|Yorick]] cannot write one; the drummer's billet-doux fills the gap — "neither right nor wrong." In Sterne's world, sincerity and fabrication are not opposites but partners. The letter succeeds not because it is true but because it satisfies the social demands of politeness and feeling (001-009).

- **Class and Cultural Translation**: The Count's letter (aristocratic, diplomatic) is answered by a drummer's love-letter (plebeian, amorous) — and the substitution works. Sterne's French-English servant is the agent of this cultural translation, moving fluidly between kitchens and ladies' apartments, Picardy wine and *billet-doux*. The chapter is a miniature of the [[travel|Journey]]'s larger interest in how feeling travels across boundaries of class and language.
