# The Fragment

**The Fragment** (titled "A FRAGMENT") is a self-contained meta-fictional episode in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), appearing as chapter 22. Sterne interrupts [[yorick|Yorick]]'s travel narrative to insert a brief, parable-like story about the ancient town of Abdera — a fragment that reflects on the power of art, specifically Euripides' tragedy *Andromeda*, to transform a society from violence to love.

## 概要

The Fragment opens by establishing Abdera as "the vilest and most profligate town in all Thrace" (022-004), despite the presence of the philosopher Democritus, who tried "all the powers of irony and laughter to reclaim it." The town is consumed by "poisons, conspiracies, and assassinations, — libels, pasquinades, and tumults." It is unsafe by day and worse by night.

Then, "when things were at the worst," a performance of Euripides' *Andromeda* is staged. The entire orchestra is delighted, but "of all the passages which delighted them, nothing operated more upon their imaginations than the tender strokes of nature" in Perseus's speech: "*O Cupid, prince of gods and men!*" (022-016)

The effect is instantaneous and total. "Every man almost spoke pure iambics the next day." In every street, in every house, in every mouth — "nothing but 'Cupid! Cupid! prince of gods and men!'" The fire catches, and "the whole city, like the heart of one man, open'd itself to Love."

The social consequences are immediate: "No pharmacopolist could sell one grain of hellebore, — not a single armourer had a heart to forge one instrument of death." Friendship and Virtue meet and kiss in the street. "The golden age returned, and hung over the town of Abdera" (022-027). Every Abderite man takes up his pipe, and every Abderitish woman leaves her purple web to "chastely sat her down and listened to the song."

The Fragment closes with its own moral: "'Twas only in the power...of the God whose empire extendeth from heaven to earth, and even to the depths of the sea, to have done this."

## 主题分析

- **Art versus Philosophy**: The Fragment stages a contest between philosophy and art as agents of moral transformation. Democritus — the "laughing philosopher" — tried "all the powers of irony and laughter" and failed. Euripides' poetry succeeds in a single performance. Sterne, himself a parson-novelist hovering between sermon and story, seems to vote for art — or at least for the kind of art that moves the affections rather than the intellect.

- **The Fragment as Meta-Fiction**: By inserting a self-contained story — and labelling it "A FRAGMENT" — Sterne draws attention to the constructedness of his own narrative. The Fragment is a story about the power of stories, and its placement within *A Sentimental Journey* implicitly claims that same power for the novel that contains it. The reader who is moved by Sterne is the Abderite who is moved by Euripides.

- **The Golden Age as Sentimental Utopia**: The image of the golden age returning to Abdera — no weapons forged, no poisons sold, every citizen united in song — is the sentimental utopia in miniature. It is the same dream that underwrites [[yorick|Yorick]]'s entire journey: that feeling, properly cultivated, can remake the social world.

- **The Unreason of Love**: The Fragment is also a warning. The Abderites do not become virtuous through rational persuasion; they are seized by an irrational enthusiasm — a "fire" that catches and spreads. The transformation is real, but its mechanism is closer to contagion than to enlightenment. Sterne's [[sentiment|sentimentalism]] always carries this ambiguity: feeling can civilise, but it cannot be reasoned into existence.
