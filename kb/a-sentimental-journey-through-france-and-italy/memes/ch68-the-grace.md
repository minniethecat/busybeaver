## THE GRACE.

[068-001]When supper was over, the old man

[068-002]gave a knock upon the table with the haft of his knife, to bid

[068-003]them prepare for the dance: the moment the signal was given, the

[068-004]women and girls ran altogether into a back apartment to tie up

[068-005]their hair,—and the young men to the door to wash their

[068-006]faces, and change their sabots; and in three minutes every soul

[068-007]was ready upon a little esplanade before the house to

[068-008]begin.—The old man and his wife came out last, and placing

[068-009]me betwixt them, sat down upon a sofa of turf by the door. 

[068-010]The old man had some fifty years ago been no mean performer

[068-011]upon the vielle ,—and at the age he was then of,

[068-012]touch’d it well enough for the purpose. His wife sung

[068-013]now and then a little to the tune,—then

[068-014]intermitted,—and join’d her old man again, as their

[068-015]children and grand-children danced before them. 

[068-016]It was not till the middle of the second dance, when, from

[068-017]some pauses in the movements, wherein they all seemed to look up,

[068-018]I fancied I could distinguish an elevation of spirit different

[068-019]from that which is the cause or the effect of simple

[068-020]jollity. In a word, I thought I beheld Religion mixing in the dance:—but, as I had never seen her so

[068-021]engaged, I should have look’d upon it now as one of the

[068-022]illusions of an imagination which is eternally misleading me, had

[068-023]not the old man, as soon as the dance ended, said, that this was

[068-024]their constant way; and that all his life long he had made it a

[068-025]rule, after supper was over, to call out his family to dance and

[068-026]rejoice; believing, he said, that a cheerful and contented mind

[068-027]was the best sort of thanks to heaven that an illiterate peasant

[068-028]could pay,— 

[068-029]Or a learned prelate either, said I.
