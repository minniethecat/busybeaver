# Calais

**Calais** is the French port city where [[yorick|Yorick]] arrives after crossing the English Channel, serving as the setting for the opening seventeen chapters of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). More than a mere location, Calais functions as a symbolic threshold — the space between [[england|England]] and [[france|France]], reserve and expression, calculation and sentiment.

## 地理与背景 (Setting and Context)

In the 1760s, Calais was the primary point of entry for English travelers crossing to the Continent. The short sea passage from [[dover|Dover]] made it the natural gateway to the Grand Tour. Sterne uses this familiar geography to stage his novel's opening movement: the crossing from one nation to another mirrors the crossing from one mode of feeling to another.

[[yorick|Yorick]] arrives with no particular agenda — he is "sentimental," not practical — and Calais provides the first occasions for the encounters that will define his journey.

## 关键场景 (Key Scenes at Calais)

### The Monk's Arrival

The novel's first moral encounter occurs in [[yorick|Yorick]]'s room at a Calais inn. A poor Franciscan monk enters to beg for his convent, and [[yorick|Yorick]] — irritated rather than moved — refuses him. The episode unfolds across chapters 2–4, culminating in the famous exchange of snuffboxes that transforms a failed act of charity into a ritual of mutual recognition.

> When a poor monk of the order of St. Francis came into the room to beg something for his convent. No man cares to have his virtues the sport of contingencies. (Ch. 2, Calais)

### The Desobligeant

Chapter 6 takes its title from the carriage [[yorick|Yorick]] contemplates in the coachyard at Calais — the *désobligeante*, a single-passenger chaise whose name means "the disobliging one." The carriage becomes a metaphor for travel itself: solitary, uncomfortable, and somehow appropriate to a journey of self-examination.

> I had long been bearing it in my fancy, to try what I could do in a désobligeante — and the very look of it was enough to satisfy my curiosity. (Ch. 6, In the Desobligeant)

### Madame de L—

Chapters 7–17, all set at Calais, introduce [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]], a fellow traveler with whom [[yorick|Yorick]] develops a relationship poised between gallantry and genuine affection. The Calais chapters thus establish the novel's second major mode — after charity comes flirtation, and after the monk comes the lady.

## 象征意义 (Symbolic Significance)

Calais is not described in topographic detail. Sterne shows no interest in its streets, fortifications, or harbor. Instead, the city functions as a psychological space:

**The Threshold**: Calais is the first French ground [[yorick|Yorick]] treads, and every encounter there is freighted with the consciousness of crossing. His English habits of reserve are tested against French modes of social warmth.

**The Microcosm**: The seventeen Calais chapters contain in miniature the novel's entire moral repertoire — charity, flirtation, cultural comparison, self-mockery, and the perpetual negotiation between feeling and performance.

**The Inn as Stage**: Nearly all the Calais scenes take place indoors — in [[yorick|Yorick]]'s room, the coachyard, the parlor. This cloistered setting makes the city a theater of interiors, where external geography matters less than internal response.
