# The Bidet

**The Bidet** is a comic road episode in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), occurring in chapter 24. Having settled his affairs at [[montreuil|Montreuil]], [[yorick|Yorick]] sets out in his post-chaise, with his servant [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] riding a small bidet alongside. The journey is interrupted when [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s bidet refuses to pass a [[the-dead-ass|dead ass]] lying in the road — triggering a slapstick struggle and a meditation on the French language of exclamation.

## 概要

The chapter opens with [[yorick|Yorick]] settling "all these little matters" and climbing into his post-chaise "with more ease than ever." [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], having got "one large jack-boot on the far side of a little bidet (024-004), and another on this," canters ahead "as happy and as perpendicular as a prince."

Sterne immediately undercuts the idyllic tableau: "But what is happiness! what is grandeur in this painted scene of life!" Before they have gone a league, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s bidet stops dead before a [[the-dead-ass|dead ass]] in the road. The bidet will not pass it. A "contention" arises between man and beast — and "the poor fellow was kick'd out of his jack-boots the very first kick."

[[la-fleur|La Fleur]] bears his fall "like a French Christian," saying nothing more than *Diable!* He remounts, beating the bidet "as he would have beat his drum." The bidet "flew from one side of the road to the other, then back again, — then this way, then that way, and in short, every way but by the dead ass." [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] insists — and the bidet throws him again.

## The Taxonomy of Exclamations

This comic scene becomes the occasion for one of Sterne's most characteristic digressions: a mock-scholarly taxonomy of French exclamations. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] uses only two — *Diable!* and *Peste!* — but Sterne insists there are three, "like the positive, comparative, and superlative, one or the other of which serves for every unexpected throw of the dice in life."

- ***Le Diable!*** — the "first and positive degree," used for "ordinary emotions of the mind, where small things only fall out contrary to your expectations; such as — the throwing once doublets — La Fleur's being kick'd off his horse, and so forth." Cuckoldom, Sterne adds, "for the same reason, is always — *Le Diable!*"

- ***Peste!*** — the second degree, reserved for "cases where the cast has something provoking in it, as in that of the bidet's running away after, and leaving La Fleur aground in jack-boots."

- **The third degree** — Sterne cannot bring himself to name it. His heart is "wrung with pity and fellow-feeling" when he reflects "what miseries must have been their lot, and how bitterly so refined a people must have smarted, to have forced them upon the use of it." He prays instead for "decent words to exclaim in" and resolves to "take every evil just as it befell me, without any exclamation at all."

## Resolution

[[yorick|Yorick]], having philosophically conceded that a "conceited beast" must "go his own way," watches as [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] gives the bidet "a good sound lash" — whereupon the bidet "took me at my word, and away he scampered back to [[montreuil|Montreuil]]." *Peste!* says [[la-fleur|La Fleur]].

With the bidet vanished and a frightened horse "not to be hunted down in jack-boots," there remains "no alternative but taking La Fleur either behind the chaise, or into it." [[yorick|Yorick]] prefers the latter, and in half an hour they arrive at the post-house at [[nampont|Nampont]].

## 主题分析

- **Comic Deflation**: The chapter is a masterclass in Sterne's technique of comic deflation — the grand opening ("as happy and as perpendicular as a prince") is immediately punctured by the dead ass and the slapstick that follows. The pattern of elevation followed by fall mirrors [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s own trajectory from saddle to ground.

- **The Philosophy of Small Things**: Sterne's taxonomy of exclamations transforms a trivial road incident into a mock-philosophical treatise. The gradation from *Diable!* to *Peste!* to the unspeakable third degree is a parody of scholastic classification, but it also contains genuine insight into how language maps onto the gradations of human frustration.

- **The Servant as Spectacle**: [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] is the chapter's comic centre, but he is also an object of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s amused observation. The chapter exemplifies the relationship between master and servant in the novel: [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] provides the action, [[yorick|Yorick]] provides the reflection.
