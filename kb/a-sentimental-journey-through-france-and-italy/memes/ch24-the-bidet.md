## THE BIDET.

[024-001]Having settled all these little

[024-002]matters, I got into my post-chaise with more ease than ever I got

[024-003]into a post-chaise in my life; and La Fleur having got one large

[024-004]jack-boot on the far side of a little bidet , [588] and another on this (for I count

[024-005]nothing of his legs)—he canter’d away before me as

[024-006]happy and as perpendicular as a prince.—But what is

[024-007]happiness! what is grandeur in this painted scene of life!

[024-008]A dead ass, before we had got a league, put a sudden stop to La

[024-009]Fleur’s career;—his bidet would not pass by

[024-010]it,—a contention arose betwixt them, and the poor fellow

[024-011]was kick’d out of his jack-boots the very first kick. 

[024-012]La Fleur bore his fall like a French Christian, saying neither

[024-013]more nor less upon it, than Diable ! So presently got

[024-014]up, and came to the charge again astride his bidet, beating him

[024-015]up to it as he would have beat his drum. 

[024-016]The bidet flew from one side of the road to the other, then

[024-017]back again,—then this way, then that way, and in short,

[024-018]every way but by the dead ass:—La Fleur insisted upon the

[024-019]thing—and the bidet threw him. 

[024-020]What’s the matter, La Fleur, said I, with this bidet of

[024-021]thine? Monsieur, said he, c’est un cheval le plus

[024-022]opiniâtre du monde .—Nay, if he is a conceited

[024-023]beast, he must go his own way, replied I. So La Fleur got

[024-024]off him, and giving him a good sound lash, the bidet took me at

[024-025]my word, and away he scampered back to

[024-026]Montreuil.— Peste ! said La Fleur. 

[024-027]It is not mal-à-propos to take notice here, that

[024-028]though La Fleur availed himself but of two different terms of

[024-029]exclamation in this encounter,—namely, Diable ! and Peste ! that there are, nevertheless, three in the French

[024-030]language: like the positive, comparative, and superlative, one or

[024-031]the other of which serves for every unexpected throw of the dice

[024-032]in life. 

[024-033]Le Diable ! which is the first, and positive degree, is

[024-034]generally used upon ordinary emotions of the mind, where small

[024-035]things only fall out contrary to your expectations; such

[024-036]as—the throwing once doublets—La Fleur’s being

[024-037]kick’d off his horse, and so forth.—Cuckoldom, for

[024-038]the same reason, is always— Le Diable ! 

[024-039]But, in cases where the cast has something provoking in it, as

[024-040]in that of the bidet’s running away after, and leaving La

[024-041]Fleur aground in jack-boots,—’tis the second

[024-042]degree. 

[024-043]’Tis then Peste ! 

[024-044]And for the third— 

[024-045]—But here my heart is wrung with pity and fellow

[024-046]feeling, when I reflect what miseries must have been their lot,

[024-047]and how bitterly so refined a people must have smarted, to have

[024-048]forced them upon the use of it.— 

[024-049]Grant me, O ye powers which touch the tongue with eloquence in

[024-050]distress!—what ever is my cast , grant me but decent

[024-051]words to exclaim in, and I will give my nature way. 

[024-052]—But as these were not to be had in France, I resolved

[024-053]to take every evil just as it befell me, without any exclamation

[024-054]at all. 

[024-055]La Fleur, who had made no such covenant with himself, followed

[024-056]the bidet with his eyes till it was got out of sight,—and

[024-057]then, you may imagine, if you please, with what word he closed

[024-058]the whole affair. 

[024-059]As there was no hunting down a frightened horse in jack-boots,

[024-060]there remained no alternative but taking La Fleur either behind

[024-061]the chaise, or into it.— 

[024-062]I preferred the latter, and in half an hour we got to the

[024-063]post-house at Nampont.
