## PARIS.

[038-001]What the old French officer had

[038-002]delivered upon travelling, bringing Polonius’s advice to

[038-003]his son upon the same subject into my head,—and that

[038-004]bringing in Hamlet, and Hamlet the rest of Shakespeare’s

[038-005]works, I stopp’d at the Quai de Conti in my return home, to

[038-006]purchase the whole set. 

[038-007]The bookseller said he had not a set in the world. Comment ! said I, taking one up out of a set which lay upon

[038-008]the counter betwixt us.—He said they were sent him only to

[038-009]be got bound, and were to be sent back to Versailles in the

[038-010]morning to the Count de B—. 

[038-011]—And does the Count de B—, said I, read

[038-012]Shakespeare? C’est un esprit fort , replied the

[038-013]bookseller.—He loves English books! and what is more to his

[038-014]honour, Monsieur, he loves the English too. You speak this

[038-015]so civilly, said I, that it is enough to oblige an Englishman to

[038-016]lay out a louis d’or or two at your shop.—The

[038-017]bookseller made a bow, and was going to say something, when a

[038-018]young decent girl about twenty, who by her air and dress seemed

[038-019]to be fille de chambre to some devout woman of fashion,

[038-020]come into the shop and asked for Les Égarements du Cœur

[038-021]et de l’Esprit : the bookseller gave her the book

[038-022]directly; she pulled out a little green satin purse run round

[038-023]with a riband of the same colour, and putting her finger and

[038-024]thumb into it, she took out the money and paid for it. As I

[038-025]had nothing more to stay me in the shop, we both walk’d out

[038-026]at the door together. 

[038-027]—And what have you to do, my dear, said I, with The

[038-028]Wanderings of the Heart , who scarce know yet you have one?

[038-029]nor, till love has first told you it, or some faithless shepherd

[038-030]has made it ache, canst thou ever be sure it is so.— Le

[038-031]Dieu m’en garde ! said the girl.—With reason, said

[038-032]I, for if it is a good one, ’tis pity it should be stolen;

[038-033]’tis a little treasure to thee, and gives a better air to

[038-034]your face, than if it was dress’d out with pearls. 

[038-035]The young girl listened with a submissive attention, holding her

[038-036]satin purse by its riband in her hand all the

[038-037]time.—’Tis a very small one, said I, taking hold of

[038-038]the bottom of it—she held it towards me—and there is

[038-039]very little in it, my dear, said I; but be but as good as thou

[038-040]art handsome, and heaven will fill it. I had a parcel of

[038-041]crowns in my hand to pay for Shakespeare; and, as she had let go

[038-042]the purse entirely, I put a single one in; and, tying up the

[038-043]riband in a bow-knot, returned it to her. 

[038-044]The young girl made me more a humble courtesy than a low

[038-045]one:—’twas one of those quiet, thankful sinkings,

[038-046]where the spirit bows itself down,—the body does no more

[038-047]than tell it. I never gave a girl a crown in my life which

[038-048]gave me half the pleasure. 

[038-049]My advice, my dear, would not have been worth a pin to you,

[038-050]said I, if I had not given this along with it: but now, when you

[038-051]see the crown, you’ll remember it;—so don’t, my

[038-052]dear, lay it out in ribands. 

[038-053]Upon my word, Sir, said the girl, earnestly, I am

[038-054]incapable;—in saying which, as is usual in little bargains

[038-055]of honour, she gave me her hand:— En

[038-056]vérité , Monsieur , je mettrai cet

[038-057]argent àpart , said she. 

[038-058]When a virtuous convention is made betwixt man and woman, it

[038-059]sanctifies their most private walks: so, notwithstanding it was

[038-060]dusky, yet as both our roads lay the same way, we made no scruple

[038-061]of walking along the Quai de Conti together. 

[038-062]She made me a second courtesy in setting off, and before we

[038-063]got twenty yards from the door, as if she had not done enough

[038-064]before, she made a sort of a little stop to tell me

[038-065]again—she thank’d me. 

[038-066]It was a small tribute, I told her, which I could not avoid

[038-067]paying to virtue, and would not be mistaken in the person I had

[038-068]been rendering it to for the world;—but I see innocence, my

[038-069]dear, in your face,—and foul befall the man who ever lays a

[038-070]snare in its way! 

[038-071]The girl seem’d affected some way or other with what I

[038-072]said;—she gave a low sigh:—I found I was not

[038-073]empowered to enquire at all after it,—so said nothing more

[038-074]till I got to the corner of the Rue de Nevers, where, we were to

[038-075]part. 

[038-076]—But is this the way, my dear, said I, to the

[038-077]Hotel de Modene? She told me it was;—or that I might

[038-078]go by the Rue de Gueneguault, which was the next turn.—Then

[038-079]I’ll go, my dear, by the Rue de Gueneguault, said I, for

[038-080]two reasons; first, I shall please myself, and next, I shall give

[038-081]you the protection of my company as far on your way as I

[038-082]can. The girl was sensible I was civil—and said, she

[038-083]wished the Hotel de Modene was in the Rue de St.

[038-084]Pierre.—You live there? said I.—She told me she was fille de chambre to Madame R—.—Good God! said

[038-085]I, ’tis the very lady for whom I have brought a letter from

[038-086]Amiens.—The girl told me that Madame R—, she

[038-087]believed, expected a stranger with a letter, and was impatient to

[038-088]see him:—so I desired the girl to present my compliments to

[038-089]Madame R—, and say, I would certainly wait upon her in the

[038-090]morning. 

[038-091]We stood still at the corner of the Rue de Nevers whilst this

[038-092]pass’d.—We then stopped a moment whilst she disposed

[038-093]of her Égarements du Cœur , &c. more commodiously

[038-094]than carrying them in her hand—they were two volumes: so I

[038-095]held the second for her whilst she put the first into her pocket;

[038-096]and then she held her pocket, and I put in the other after

[038-097]it. 

[038-098]’Tis sweet to feel by what fine spun threads our

[038-099]affections are drawn together. 

[038-100]We set off afresh, and as she took her third step, the girl

[038-101]put her hand within my arm.—I was just bidding

[038-102]her,—but she did it of herself, with that undeliberating

[038-103]simplicity, which show’d it was out of her head that she

[038-104]had never seen me before. For my own part, I felt the

[038-105]conviction of consanguinity so strongly, that I could not help

[038-106]turning half round to look in her face, and see if I could trace

[038-107]out any thing in it of a family likeness.—Tut! said I, are

[038-108]we not all relations? 

[038-109]When we arrived at the turning up of the Rue de Gueneguault, I

[038-110]stopp’d to bid her adieu for good and all: the girl would

[038-111]thank me again for my company and kindness.—She bid me

[038-112]adieu twice.—I repeated it as often; and so cordial was the

[038-113]parting between us, that had it happened any where else,

[038-114]I’m not sure but I should have signed it with a kiss of

[038-115]charity, as warm and holy as an apostle. 

[038-116]But in Paris, as none kiss each other but the men,—I did, what

[038-117]amounted to the same thing— 

[038-118]—I bid God bless her.
