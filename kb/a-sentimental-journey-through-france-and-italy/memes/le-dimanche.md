# Le Dimanche

**Le Dimanche** ("Sunday") is an episode in the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), in which [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] — [[yorick|Yorick]]'s French servant hired at [[montreuil|Montreuil]] — appears on a Sunday morning splendidly arrayed (056-001) in his new scarlet coat, blue satin waistcoat, and muslin ruffles, and asks for the day off to "faire le galant" with his [[paris|Paris]]ian sweetheart.

## 概要

It is Sunday morning in [[paris|Paris]], and when [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] enters with [[yorick|Yorick]]'s coffee and roll, he is so gallantly dressed that [[yorick|Yorick]] "scarce knew him." The transformation has been wrought with the four louis d'ors and new hat promised at [[montreuil|Montreuil]] "pour s'adoniser." [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] has purchased a bright scarlet coat and matching breeches, a handsome blue satin waistcoat "fancifully enough embroidered," a new bag and solitaire, gold garters, muslin ruffles costing four livres of his own money, and white silk stockings for five more. "To top all," Sterne notes dryly, "nature had given him a handsome figure, without costing him a sous."

The sight of [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] in full finery, with a bouquet in his breast and "that look of festivity in everything about him," instantly puts [[yorick|Yorick]] in mind of Sunday — and he immediately divines that the favour [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] had wished to ask the night before was to spend the day as "every body in [[paris|Paris]] spent it." [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] confirms this with "infinite humility, but with a look of trust," asking for the day "pour faire le galant vis-à-vis de sa maîtresse."

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s initial resistance — he had intended to visit [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] himself that day and wanted [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] behind his remise — gives way to a characteristic reflection on the condition of servants: "The sons and daughters of Service part with liberty, but not with nature, in their contracts; they are flesh and blood, and have their little vanities and wishes in the midst of the house of bondage, as well as their task-masters." The thought that "Behold, I am thy servant — disarms me at once of the powers of a master" settles the matter. "Thou shalt go, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]!" [[yorick|Yorick]] declares.

[[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s mistress turns out to be a "petite demoiselle" in the household of [[count-de-b|Monsieur le Count de B—]], whom he had connected with upon the landing of the staircase while [[yorick|Yorick]] was occupied with his passport. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] had contrived to make the Count's interest serve his own romantic purposes — a parallel to his master's own sentimental negotiations.

## 主题分析

- **Servants and [[liberty|Liberty]]**: The episode is Sterne's most explicit meditation on [[la-fleur|the servant]]-master relationship. [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] is not a mere functionary but a fellow creature with vanities, loves, and a social life. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s granting of the day off is not condescension but an acknowledgment of their shared humanity — a sentimental recognition that service does not extinguish nature.

- **The Spectacle of Dress**: [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s transformation through clothing is treated with affectionate irony. His scarlet coat from the Rue de Friperie, touched-up gold embroidery, and carefully negotiated accessories make him a comic double of the fashionable [[paris|Paris]]ian — yet the comedy is warm, never cruel. The episode gently satirises the universal human investment in appearances.

- **[[paris|Paris]]ian Sunday**: The episode captures the rhythm of [[paris|Paris]]ian life — Sunday as a day of collective festivity when "happy people" lay down their cares to "dance and sing and sport away the weights of grievance." [[yorick|Yorick]]'s sentimental cosmopolitanism finds in this weekly ritual a counterpoint to English sabbatarianism.
