# London

**London** is the English capital from which [[yorick|Yorick]] departs on his sentimental journey through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]. Though never directly depicted as a setting in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), London haunts the narrative as a cultural and psychological point of reference — the home [[yorick|Yorick]] has left behind in haste, the standard against which French manners are measured, and the place to which the entire journey will eventually return.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, London was the largest city in Europe, the seat of British commerce and empire, and the cultural center of the English Enlightenment. For a clergyman-writer like Sterne (and his fictional surrogate [[yorick|Yorick]]), London represented both the world of literary celebrity and the mundane realities of English parish life. The crossing from London to [[paris|Paris]] was, in the 18th century, not merely a geographical journey but a passage between two rival civilizations — a fact Sterne exploits throughout the novel for comic and satirical effect.

## 在小说中的角色

London functions in the novel as an absent presence — the unvisited pole around which the journey's cultural comparisons revolve. Its narrative role operates through five distinct modes:

**The Precipitate Departure (Ch. 50)**. [[yorick|Yorick]] reveals retrospectively that he "had left London with so much precipitation, that it never enter'd my mind that we were at war with [[france|France]]" (050-001). It is only upon reaching [[dover|Dover]] and looking through his glass "at the hills beyond [[boulogne|Boulogne]]" that the passport crisis dawns on him. London is thus the site of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s characteristic impulsiveness — the unconsidered starting point from which all subsequent complications flow.

**London vs. [[paris|Paris]]: The Commerce of the Sexes (Ch. 39)**. Sterne uses London as a comparative foil in his analysis of French and English domestic arrangements. "In London a shopkeeper and a shopkeeper's wife seem to be one bone and one flesh," he observes, describing the English couple as being "upon a par, and totally with each other as nearly as man and wife need to do" (039-005). This stands in stark contrast to [[paris|Paris]], where the husband "seldom comes" to the shop and sits instead "in some dark and dismal room behind... commerce-less, in his thrum nightcap, the same rough son of [[nature|Nature]] that [[nature|Nature]] left him" (039-006). The comparison is both ethnographic and satirical, using London as the baseline of English domestic normality against which French arrangements appear exotic.

**The Translator of Streets (Ch. 41)**. [[yorick|Yorick]] describes a peculiarly urban skill he has cultivated: "when I walk the streets of London, I go translating all the way" (041-005). This habit of reading faces, postures, and gestures into "plain words" — of having "brought off twenty different dialogues... where not three words have been said" (041-005) — is presented as a London-acquired art. London, in this rendering, is the school of sentimental interpretation, the place where [[yorick|Yorick]] learned to read human nature in its silent expressions.

**[[the-starling|The Starling]]'s London Odyssey (Ch. 55)**. The [[the-starling|starling]] whose caged cry — "I can't get out" — becomes [[yorick|Yorick]]'s personal emblem traces a comically elaborate path through London society. Passed from Lord A to Lord B to Lord C "half round the alphabet," and then descending "into the lower house" through the hands of "as many commoners," the starling's London career is a miniature satire of the city's social circulation: "all these wanted to *get in*, and my bird wanted to *get out*" (055-003). London is both the bird's prison and the theater of its celebrity.

**The Laughing [[travel|Journey]] (Ch. 69)**. In one of the novel's final chapters, [[yorick|Yorick]] declares to the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] that he has "come laughing all the way from London to [[paris|Paris]]" (069-007). The phrase encapsulates London's role as the journey's tonal origin — the place from which [[yorick|Yorick]] has carried his irrepressible mirth across the Channel, even into the presence of French ministers and the shadow of the [[the-bastile|Bastile]].
