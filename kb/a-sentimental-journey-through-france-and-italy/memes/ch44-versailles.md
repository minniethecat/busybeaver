## VERSAILLES.

[044-001]Before I had got half way down the

[044-002]street I changed my mind: as I am at Versailles, thought I, I

[044-003]might as well take a view of the town; so I pull’d the

[044-004]cord, and ordered the coachman to drive round some of the

[044-005]principal streets.—I suppose the town is not very large,

[044-006]said I.—The coachman begg’d pardon for setting me

[044-007]right, and told me it was very superb, and that numbers of the

[044-008]first dukes and marquises and counts had hotels.—The Count

[044-009]de B—, of whom the bookseller at the Quai de Conti had

[044-010]spoke so handsomely the night before, came instantly into my

[044-011]mind.—And why should I not go, thought I, to the Count de

[044-012]B—, who has so high an idea of English books and English

[044-013]men—and tell him my story? so I changed my mind a second

[044-014]time.—In truth it was the third; for I had intended that

[044-015]day for Madame de R—, in the Rue St. Pierre, and had

[044-016]devoutly sent her word by her fille de chambre that I

[044-017]would assuredly wait upon her;—but I am governed by

[044-018]circumstances;—I cannot govern them: so seeing a man

[044-019]standing with a basket on the other side of the street, as if he

[044-020]had something to sell, I bid La Fleur go up to him, and enquire

[044-021]for the Count’s hotel. 

[044-022]La Fleur returned a little pale; and told me it was a

[044-023]Chevalier de St. Louis selling pâtés.—It is

[044-024]impossible, La Fleur, said I.—La Fleur could no more

[044-025]account for the phenomenon than myself; but persisted in his

[044-026]story: he had seen the croix set in gold, with its red riband, he

[044-027]said, tied to his buttonhole—and had looked into the basket

[044-028]and seen the pâtés which the Chevalier was selling; so could

[044-029]not be mistaken in that. 

[044-030]Such a reverse in man’s life awakens a better principle

[044-031]than curiosity: I could not help looking for some time at him as

[044-032]I sat in the remise:—the more I look’d at him, his

[044-033]croix, and his basket, the stronger they wove themselves into my

[044-034]brain.—I got out of the remise, and went towards him. 

[044-035]He was begirt with a clean linen apron which fell below his

[044-036]knees, and with a sort of a bib that went half way up his breast;

[044-037]upon the top of this, but a little below the hem, hung his

[044-038]croix. His basket of little pâtés was covered

[044-039]over with a white damask napkin; another of the same kind was

[044-040]spread at the bottom; and there was a look of propreté and neatness throughout, that one might

[044-041]have bought his pâtés of him, as much from appetite

[044-042]as sentiment. 

[044-043]He made an offer of them to neither; but stood still with them

[044-044]at the corner of an hotel, for those to buy who chose it without

[044-045]solicitation. 

[044-046]He was about forty-eight;—of a sedate look, something

[044-047]approaching to gravity. I did not wonder.—I went up

[044-048]rather to the basket than him, and having lifted up the napkin,

[044-049]and taking one of his pâtés into my hand,—I

[044-050]begg’d he would explain the appearance which affected

[044-051]me. 

[044-052]He told me in a few words, that the best part of his life had

[044-053]passed in the service, in which, after spending a small

[044-054]patrimony, he had obtained a company and the croix with it; but

[044-055]that, at the conclusion of the last peace, his regiment being

[044-056]reformed, and the whole corps, with those of some other

[044-057]regiments, left without any provision, he found himself in a wide

[044-058]world without friends, without a livre,—and indeed, said

[044-059]he, without anything but this,—(pointing, as he said it, to

[044-060]his croix).—The poor Chevalier won my pity, and he finished

[044-061]the scene with winning my esteem too. 

[044-062]The king, he said, was the most generous of princes, but his

[044-063]generosity could neither relieve nor reward everyone, and it was

[044-064]only his misfortune to be amongst the number. He had a

[044-065]little wife, he said, whom he loved, who did the pâtisserie ; and added, he felt no

[044-066]dishonour in defending her and himself from want in this

[044-067]way—unless Providence had offer’d him a better. 

[044-068]It would be wicked to withhold a pleasure from the good, in

[044-069]passing over what happen’d to this poor Chevalier of St.

[044-070]Louis about nine months after. 

[044-071]It seems he usually took his stand near the iron gates which

[044-072]lead up to the palace, and as his croix had caught the eyes of

[044-073]numbers, numbers had made the same enquiry which I had

[044-074]done.—He had told them the same story, and always with so

[044-075]much modesty and good sense, that it had reach’d at last

[044-076]the king’s ears;—who, hearing the Chevalier had been

[044-077]a gallant officer, and respected by the whole regiment as a man

[044-078]of honour and integrity,—he broke up his little trade by a

[044-079]pension of fifteen hundred livres a year. 

[044-080]As I have told this to please the reader, I beg he will allow

[044-081]me to relate another, out of its order, to please

[044-082]myself:—the two stories reflect light upon each

[044-083]other,—and ’tis a pity they should be parted.
