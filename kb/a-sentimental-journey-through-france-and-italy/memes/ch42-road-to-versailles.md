## ROAD TO VERSAILLES.

[042-001]I got into my remise the hour I

[042-002]proposed: La Fleur got up behind, and I bid the coachman make the

[042-003]best of his way to Versailles. 

[042-004]As there was nothing in this road, or rather nothing which I

[042-005]look for in travelling, I cannot fill up the blank better than

[042-006]with a short history of this self-same bird, which became the

[042-007]subject of the last chapter. 

[042-008]Whilst the Honourable Mr. — was waiting for a wind at

[042-009]Dover, it had been caught upon the cliffs, before it could well

[042-010]fly, by an English lad who was his groom; who, not caring to

[042-011]destroy it, had taken it in his breast into the

[042-012]packet;—and, by course of feeding it, and taking it once

[042-013]under his protection, in a day or two grew fond of it, and got it

[042-014]safe along with him to Paris. 

[042-015]At Paris the lad had laid out a livre in a little cage for the

[042-016]starling, and as he had little to do better the five months his

[042-017]master staid there, he taught it, in his mother’s tongue,

[042-018]the four simple words—(and no more)—to which I

[042-019]own’d myself so much its debtor. 

[042-020]Upon his master’s going on for Italy, the lad had given

[042-021]it to the master of the hotel. But his little song for

[042-022]liberty being in an unknown language at Paris, the bird

[042-023]had little or no store set by him: so La Fleur bought both him

[042-024]and his cage for me for a bottle of Burgundy. 

[042-025]In my return from Italy I brought him with me to the country

[042-026]in whose language he had learned his notes; and telling the story

[042-027]of him to Lord A—, Lord A— begg’d the bird of

[042-028]me;—in a week Lord A— gave him to Lord B—; Lord

[042-029]B— made a present of him to Lord C—; and Lord

[042-030]C—’s gentleman sold him to Lord D—’s for

[042-031]a shilling; Lord D— gave him to Lord E—; and so

[042-032]on—half round the alphabet. From that rank he

[042-033]pass’d into the lower house, and pass’d the hands of

[042-034]as many commoners. But as all these wanted to get

[042-035]in , and my bird wanted to get out , he had almost as

[042-036]little store set by him in London as in Paris. 

[042-037]It is impossible but many of my readers must have heard of him; and if

[042-038]any by mere chance have ever seen him, I beg leave to inform

[042-039]them, that that bird was my bird, or some vile copy set up to

[042-040]represent him. 

[042-041]The starling as the crest of arms I have nothing farther to add upon him, but that from that

[042-042]time to this I have borne this poor starling as the crest to my

[042-043]arms.—Thus: 

[042-044]—And let the herald’s officers twist his neck

[042-045]about if they dare.
