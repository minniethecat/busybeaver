# Duc de C—

**The Duc de C—** (Duc de Choiseul) is the French foreign minister in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* — an off-stage figure of political authority around whom the passport narrative revolves. [[yorick|Yorick]] never meets him face to face, yet the Duke's power over [[yorick|Yorick]]'s freedom of movement drives one of the novel's most sustained plotlines. He is based on the historical **Étienne François, Duc de Choiseul** (1719–1785), who served as [[calais|France]]'s foreign minister and chief minister under Louis XV.

## Role Overview

The Duc de C— enters the narrative indirectly, through [[yorick|Yorick]]'s anxiety about his passport. Having travelled to [[paris|Paris]] without proper documentation, [[yorick|Yorick]] learns that a war between [[calais|France]] and [[england|England]] makes his position precarious. The solution, suggested by various French acquaintances, is to obtain a passport from the Duc de Choiseul — the one man in [[calais|France]] with the authority to grant safe passage to an Englishman in wartime.

What follows is one of the novel's most elaborate comic sequences: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s journey to [[versailles|Versailles]], his mounting dread of the [[the-bastile|Bastille]], his internal debates about how to address a great man, and ultimately his discovery that wit and good humour — not diplomatic protocol — are the keys to securing the Duke's favour. The Duc de C— himself never appears; he is a presence made entirely of reputation, anticipation, and the testimony of intermediaries.

## Appearances in the Narrative

### The Decision to Petition

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s decision to seek out the Duc de Choiseul comes after his encounter with [[the-starling|the caged starling]], whose cry of "I can't get out" triggers a sudden terror of imprisonment. Rising from his chair, he summons [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] and declares: "I'll go directly, said I, myself to Monsieur le Duc de Choiseul" (042-001). The declaration is characteristically impulsive — an emotional response to the starling's plight rather than a calculated diplomatic move.

### The Internal Debate

En route to [[versailles|Versailles]], [[yorick|Yorick]] engages in an extended dialogue with himself about the proper way to address a man of power. He acknowledges that "this going to Monsieur le Duc de C— was an act of compulsion; had it been an act of choice, I should have done it, I suppose, like other people" (043-001). He castigates himself for the "mean plans of dirty address" his servile heart forms, deserving "the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] for every one of them" (043-002).

The debate crystallizes around a central comic insight: rather than crafting an elaborate petition, [[yorick|Yorick]] should observe the Duke's face, posture, and tone, and "from all these together you'll compound an address at once upon the spot, which cannot disgust the Duke; — the ingredients are his own, and most likely to go down" (043-003). This plan — to let the Duke's own character dictate the mode of address — is both brilliantly adaptive and entirely impractical.

He then steels himself with a declaration of democratic equality: "as if man to man was not equal throughout the whole surface of the globe; and if in the field — why not face to face in the cabinet too?" But he also recognizes the danger: "Go to the Duc de C— with the [[the-bastile|Bastile]] in thy looks; — my life for it, thou wilt be sent back to [[paris|Paris]] in half an hour with an escort" (043-004).

### The Blocked Audience

Arriving at [[versailles|Versailles]], [[yorick|Yorick]] is met by an under-secretary who tells him "the Duc de C— was busy" (043-005). When [[yorick|Yorick]] explains he has brought something of importance, the secretary looks towards the stairs — but [[yorick|Yorick]], with characteristic honesty, corrects himself: "what I have to say is of no manner of importance to Monsieur le Duc de C— — but of great importance to myself" (043-006). The secretary's reply — "*C'est une autre affaire*" — is a masterpiece of bureaucratic understatement, and [[yorick|Yorick]] is told to wait at least two hours.

### Resolution Through the Count de B—

The passport is ultimately obtained not through direct petition but through the intercession of the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]], who presents [[yorick|Yorick]]'s case to the Duke off-stage. When the Count returns with the passport, he reports the Duke's verdict: "*Un homme qui rit ne sera jamais dangereux*" — "A man who laughs will never be dangerous" (049-001). The Duke's aphorism, delivered through the Count, transforms [[yorick|Yorick]]'s comic anxiety into a principle of state: laughter as political safe-conduct.

The Duke's reputation for wit and generosity is further confirmed at the dinner table, where [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects: "I do not think Monsieur le Duc de Choiseul is such an enemy to mirth as to send me back crying for my pains" (049-002). Near the end of the [[versailles|Versailles]] sequence, the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] excuses himself "being engaged to go that moment to dine with the Duc de C—" (050-001), a small detail that confirms the Duke's place at the centre of [[versailles|Versailles]] social and political life, even as he remains invisible to the reader.

## Character and Themes

**Power as Absence**: The Duc de C— exemplifies Sterne's technique of building a character entirely through others' speech. He never speaks a line of direct dialogue; his power is conveyed through the anxiety, deference, and relief of those who orbit him. This structural absence makes him more imposing, not less — the unseen sovereign whose word can liberate or confine.

**The Politics of Laughter**: The Duke's reported maxim — that a man who laughs is never dangerous — is one of the novel's most revealing political statements. It transforms [[yorick|Yorick]]'s comic inadequacy as a diplomat into a form of credential, suggesting that Sterne's sentimentalism has a political dimension: good humour is not merely a social grace but a form of international passport.

**Historical Resonance**: Sterne's choice of Choiseul was pointed. The historical Duc de Choiseul was [[calais|France]]'s foreign minister during the Seven Years' War (1756–1763), which pitted [[calais|France]] against Britain. By 1768, when *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* was published, Choiseul remained a dominant figure in French politics. An English clergyman travelling in [[calais|France]] shortly after the war would indeed have needed Choiseul's favour — a real political reality that Sterne transforms into sentimental comedy.
