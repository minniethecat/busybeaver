## CALAIS.

[007-001]I perceived that something

[007-002]darken’d the passage more than myself, as I stepp’d

[007-003]along it to my room; it was effectually Mons. Dessein, the master

[007-004]of the hôtel, who had just returned from vespers, and with

[007-005]his hat under his arm, was most complaisantly following me, to

[007-006]put me in mind of my wants. I had wrote myself pretty well

[007-007]out of conceit with the désobligeant , and Mons.

[007-008]Dessein speaking of it, with a shrug, as if it would no way suit

[007-009]me, it immediately struck my fancy that it belong’d to some Innocent Traveller , who, on his return home, had left it

[007-010]to Mons. Dessein’s honour to make the most of. Four

[007-011]months had elapsed since it had finished its career of Europe in

[007-012]the corner of Mons. Dessein’s coach-yard; and having

[007-013]sallied out from thence but a vampt-up business at the first,

[007-014]though it had been twice taken to pieces on Mount Sennis, it had

[007-015]not profited much by its adventures,—but by none so

[007-016]little as the standing so many months unpitied in the corner of

[007-017]Mons. Dessein’s coach-yard. Much indeed was not to be

[007-018]said for it,—but something might;—and when a few

[007-019]words will rescue misery out of her distress, I hate the man who

[007-020]can be a churl of them. 

[007-021]—Now was I the master of this hôtel, said I,

[007-022]laying the point of my fore-finger on Mons. Dessein’s

[007-023]breast, I would inevitably make a point of getting rid of this

[007-024]unfortunate désobligeant ;—it stands swinging

[007-025]reproaches at you every time you pass by it. 

[007-026]Mon Dieu ! said Mons. Dessein,—I have no

[007-027]interest—Except the interest, said I, which men of a

[007-028]certain turn of mind take, Mons. Dessein, in their own

[007-029]sensations,—I’m persuaded, to a man who feels for

[007-030]others as well as for himself, every rainy night, disguise it as

[007-031]you will, must cast a damp upon your spirits:—You suffer,

[007-032]Mons. Dessein, as much as the machine— 

[007-033]I have always observed, when there is as much sour as sweet in a compliment, that an Englishman is eternally at

[007-034]a loss within himself, whether to take it, or let it alone: a

[007-035]Frenchman never is: Mons. Dessein made me a bow. 

[007-036]C’est bien vrai , said he.—But in this case

[007-037]I should only exchange one disquietude for another, and with

[007-038]loss: figure to yourself, my dear Sir, that in giving you a

[007-039]chaise which would fall to pieces before you had got half-way to

[007-040]Paris,—figure to yourself how much I should suffer, in

[007-041]giving an ill impression of myself to a man of honour, and lying

[007-042]at the mercy, as I must do, d’un homme

[007-043]d’esprit . 

[007-044]The dose was made up exactly after my own prescription; so I

[007-045]could not help tasting it,—and, returning Mons. Dessein his

[007-046]bow, without more casuistry we walk’d together towards his

[007-047]Remise, to take a view of his magazine of chaises.
