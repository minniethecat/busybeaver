# Madame de R—

**Madame de R—** is the most elaborately deferred encounter in Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]*. [[yorick|Yorick]] arrives in [[paris|Paris]] charged with a letter of introduction to her from [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]], repeatedly resolves to visit her, meets her *fille de chambre* who confirms her impatience to see him — and yet, through a chain of interruptions and changes of plan, the visit never takes place. She exists entirely off-stage, defined by the accumulating weight of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s unkept promises (027-001).

## Role Overview

Madame de R— first enters the narrative when [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]], taking leave of [[yorick|Yorick]] at [[amiens|Amiens]], sends her brother's servant with a billet charging him to "present myself to Madame R— the first morning I had nothing to do at [[paris|Paris]]" (027-002). She thus arrives in the story as an obligation — a social duty [[yorick|Yorick]] accepts but perpetually postpones.

Her significance lies not in any scene she appears in, but in the narrative pattern she generates: anticipation, deferral, and the comedy of a man governed by circumstances rather than governing them. She lives in the Rue St. Pierre, and her *fille de chambre* — encountered by chance on the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]] — becomes the intermediary through whom [[yorick|Yorick]]'s intentions are relayed and his failures reported back.

## Journey and Deferred Encounter

[[yorick|Yorick]]'s first resolution to visit Madame de R— comes at the beginning of his [[paris|Paris]] stay. Pulling out the letter, he declares: "May I perish! if I do, said I, pulling out the letter which I had to present to Madame de R—. I'll wait upon this lady, the very first thing I do" (030-001). But he immediately calls for a barber, and by the time he emerges from the barber's hands it is "too late to think of going with my letter to Madame R— that night" (030-003).

The most significant encounter comes by chance. Walking along the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]], [[yorick|Yorick]] accompanies a young woman who, in conversation, reveals "she was *fille de chambre* to Madame R—" (038-001). The coincidence electrifies him: "'Good God! said I, 'tis the very lady for whom I have brought a letter from [[amiens|Amiens]]'" (038-002). The girl tells him that Madame R— "expected a stranger with a letter, and was impatient to see him" (038-003). [[yorick|Yorick]] sends his compliments and promises to "certainly wait upon her in the morning" (038-004).

Morning comes, and [[yorick|Yorick]] changes his plan. He had intended "that day for Madame de R—, in the Rue St. Pierre, and had devoutly sent her word by her *fille de chambre* that I would assuredly wait upon her" (046-001). But seeing a man with a basket, he diverts to visit the [[count-de-b|Count de B—]] instead, concluding philosophically: "I am governed by circumstances; I cannot govern them" (046-002).

Later, the *fille de chambre* reappears — sent by Madame de R— "upon some commission to a *marchande des modes* within a step or two of the [[hotel-de-modene|Hôtel de Modene]]" (051-001). Madame de R—, having been stood up, has bid her "enquire if I had left [[paris|Paris]]; and if so, whether I had not left a letter addressed to her" (051-002). The query transforms Madame de R— from passive recipient of [[yorick|Yorick]]'s neglect into an active character with her own curiosity and initiative.

Near the end of the [[paris|Paris]] sequence, [[yorick|Yorick]] still retains his carriage "on purpose for" visiting Madame de R— (057-001), yet the visit never materialises. She remains the novel's most conspicuous absent presence — a character built entirely from the space where a meeting should have been.

## Character and Themes

Madame de R— embodies Sterne's interest in the **deferred encounter** as a narrative device. The perpetually postponed visit becomes a running joke, but it also illustrates a deeper sentimental theme: the gap between intention and action. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s feelings are always sincere — his resolutions are "devout" — yet his will is helpless before serendipity.

Her off-stage status also serves as a structural mirror to the reader's own position. Just as Madame de R— waits in the Rue St. Pierre for a visitor who never arrives, the reader waits for a scene that Sterne never provides. The absence becomes the point.
