# Savoy

**Savoy** (Savoie) is the mountainous region lying between [[france|France]] and [[italy|the Italian states]], referenced in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* (1768) as both a geographical reality of 18th-century travel and a symbolic landscape of hardship. For [[yorick|Yorick]], Savoy represents the challenging final leg of the journey — the passage through which sentiment must prove its resilience.

## 在小说中的角色 (Role in the Novel)

Savoy enters the novel through two key moments:

**[[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]'s Wanderings**: [[maria-of-moulines|Maria of Moulines]], the grief-stricken young woman [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters near [[moulines|Moulines]], has wandered "without money, — and through the flinty roads of Savoy" (Ch. 64, [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]) in her unmoored travels. The detail is carefully chosen: Savoy's "flinty roads" — hard, unyielding, punishing — mirror [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]'s emotional landscape. Her bare feet and broken spirit have traversed the same terrain that wealthy Grand Tourists navigated in comfort.

**The [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]] Episode**: After leaving [[moulines|Moulines]], [[yorick|Yorick]] continues toward "[[turin|Turin]], through Savoy" (Ch. 67, The [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]]). The journey through the Alpine foothills brings him to the humble inn where he shares supper with a peasant family — a scene of rustic sincerity that represents the novel's ideal of unmediated human connection. The inn, with "so little of urbanity in it than a little inn in Savoy could have furnish'd" (Ch. 67, The [[bourbonnois|Bourbonnois]]), becomes a temple of genuine feeling precisely because it lacks the polish of [[paris|Paris]].

## 象征意义 (Symbolic Significance)

In Sterne's geographical imagination, Savoy occupies a transitional zone — neither fully French nor Italian, neither civilized nor wilderness. It is a landscape that strips away social pretense, leaving travelers (whether [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] or [[yorick|Yorick]]) to confront the essentials: hunger, fatigue, and the simple kindness of strangers. The "flinty roads of Savoy" are the crucible through which sentiment passes on its way toward [[italy|Italy]] — a passage the novel hints at but never completes.
