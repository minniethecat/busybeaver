# The Mystery

**The Mystery** is a brief episode in the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), in which [[yorick|Yorick]], having just released the hand of the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]], pauses at the gate of the [[hotel-de-modene|Hôtel de Modène]] and becomes absorbed in watching a mysterious beggar whose method of solicitation defies all his powers of analysis.

## 概要

After parting from the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]], [[yorick|Yorick]] finds it impossible to return directly to his chamber — "it was touching a cold key with a flat third to it upon the close of a piece of music, which had call'd forth my affections." He lingers at the gate of the hotel, observing passers-by and "forming conjectures upon them," until his attention fixes upon a single figure.

The man is about fifty-two, tall, with "a philosophic, serious, adust look" (053-010). He paces sedately along the street, making a circuit of about sixty paces on each side of the hotel gate, carrying a small cane and dressed in a drab-coloured suit that has "seen some years service" but remains clean, with "a little air of frugal propreté throughout him." From his repeated doffing of his hat and his manner of accosting passers-by, [[yorick|Yorick]] identifies him as a beggar and readies a sou or two.

The beggar passes [[yorick|Yorick]] by without asking anything — yet not five steps further, he solicits a little woman. [[yorick|Yorick]], "much more likely to have given of the two," is puzzled. Over the next half-hour, watching the man make a dozen turns, a clear pattern emerges: the beggar solicits exclusively from women. Two further circumstances deepen the mystery: he tells every woman what he has to say "in her ear, and in a way which had much more the air of a secret than a petition"; and he is always successful — "He never stopp'd a woman, but she pull'd out her purse, and immediately gave him something."

[[yorick|Yorick]] can "form no system to explain the phenomenon." The riddle will occupy his mind through the night and into the next chapter, "[[le-dimanche|Le Dimanche]]," where it is finally solved in "[[the-riddle-explained|The Riddle Explained]]": the beggar's secret is flattery — calibrated, concentrated, and delivered in just the right dose for each target.

## 主题分析

- **Observation and the Limits of [[reason|Reason]]**: The episode is a case study in the sentimental method — close observation of human behaviour that yields not explanation but mystery. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s inability to "form a system" is not a failure but a recognition that human nature exceeds rational schematisation. The pleasure of the riddle is itself the point.

- **The Gender of [[sentiment|Sentiment]]**: The beggar's exclusive targeting of women — and his invariable success — raises questions Sterne returns to throughout the novel. Are women more susceptible to flattery? Or is the beggar simply a specialist who has found his market? The episode refrains from judgment, offering instead the pure fascination of an unsolved puzzle.

- **Secrecy and Intimacy**: The beggar's method — whispering in the ear "in a way which had much more the air of a secret than a petition" — transforms a public solicitation into a private exchange. His art is to create, in an instant, a bubble of intimacy in the street. This is, in miniature, the sentimental traveller's own technique.
