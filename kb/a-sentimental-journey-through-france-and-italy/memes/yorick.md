# Yorick

**Yorick** is the narrator and protagonist of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[calais|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768).

An English clergyman traveling through [[calais|France]] on the eve of the Seven Years' War, Yorick embodies the 18th-century "man of feeling" — responsive to every encounter, skeptical of his own motives, and committed to the proposition that a journey measured in sentiments matters more than one measured in miles.

## 角色概述 (Role Overview)

Yorick is both narrator and subject, commentator and participant. His voice — digressive, self-mocking, tender, and abruptly learned — is the novel's primary achievement.

He is by turns a moralist, a comic, a cultural critic, and a man perpetually surprised by his own emotions. The name "Yorick" is borrowed from Shakespeare's *Hamlet* (the jester whose skull the gravedigger unearths), and Sterne's Yorick shares with his namesake a consciousness of mortality that shadows even his lightest moments.

## 出场与经历 (Journey and Key Moments)

### The Monk at Calais

Yorick's first major encounter — with a begging Franciscan monk at [[calais|Calais]] — establishes the novel's moral method. He initially refuses the monk, then is overcome with remorse, and the two exchange snuffboxes in an elaborate ritual of mutual gift-giving.

The episode reveals Yorick's defining trait: his sentiments are genuine but belated, always catching up to the moment they should have occupied.

> No man cares to have his virtues the sport of contingencies — or one man may be generous, as another is puissant; — sed non quoad hanc — or be it as it may, — for there is no regular reasoning upon the ebbs and flows of our humours (002-004).

### The Dead Ass at Nampont

Encountering a grieving peasant mourning his dead ass, Yorick is moved to tears — a response Sterne presents without irony. The episode tests the limits of sentimental identification: can a man of feeling truly share the grief of a stranger over an animal? (Ch. 25)

### Arrival in Paris

Yorick's first sight of [[paris|Paris]] — "all the world in yellow, blue, and green, running at the ring of pleasure" — prompts his most famous cry of self-doubt:

> Alas, poor Yorick! cried I, what art thou doing here? On the very first onset of all this glittering clatter thou art reduced to an atom (030-023).

This moment of existential diminishment before the city's scale is typical of Yorick's self-awareness: he knows his sensibility is also a form of vanity.

### Maria at Moulines

Yorick's pilgrimage to find [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] — the grief-maddened girl from *Tristram Shandy* — represents the journey's emotional apex. Sitting beside her under a poplar tree, weeping with her, Yorick achieves the novel's deepest state of sentimental communion. The moment is also its most troubling recognition of sentiment's limits: feeling cannot cure. (Ch. 63–65)

## 性格与主题 (Character and Themes)

**The [[sentimentalism|Man of Feeling]]**: Yorick is the quintessential sentimental hero — but Sterne complicates the type. Yorick's sensibility is genuine and performative, spontaneous and calculated. He weeps for strangers and simultaneously observes himself weeping. This doubleness — feeling and watching oneself feel — is the novel's great formal innovation.

**Englishman Abroad**: Yorick's Englishness is constantly in play. He measures French manners against English ones, sometimes to [[calais|France]]'s advantage, sometimes not. His cultural identity is both a lens and a limitation — a source of comic misprision and genuine insight.

**The Clergyman as Moralist**: Yorick's clerical identity is understated but pervasive. His reflections on charity, grace, and human nature are informed by a religious sensibility that Sterne (himself a clergyman) renders without dogma — secular in tone, sacred in concern.
