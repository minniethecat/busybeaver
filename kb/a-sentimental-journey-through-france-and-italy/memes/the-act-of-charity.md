# The Act of Charity

**The Act of Charity** is a brief episode in the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), immediately preceding "[[the-riddle-explained|The Riddle Explained]]." In the dark passageway behind the [[the-opera|Opera Comique]], [[yorick|Yorick]] observes a beggar successfully persuade two stern [[paris|Paris]]ian ladies to each give him a twelve-sous piece — a sum dramatically larger than the customary alms — through a calculated appeal to their vanity.

## 概要

Returning along a long dark passage issuing out from the [[the-opera|Opera]] Comique (060-015), [[yorick|Yorick]] notices two women — "two upright vestal sisters, unsapped by caresses" — standing arm-in-arm waiting for a fiacre. He positions himself nearby, "in black, and scarce seen," becoming an invisible spectator to the scene that unfolds.

A low voice with "a good turn of expression, and sweet cadence at the end of it" begs a twelve-sous piece from the ladies. [[yorick|Yorick]] notes the singularity of a beggar who fixes the quota of an alms at twelve times the usual rate. The ladies express astonishment — "Twelve sous! … A twelve-sous piece!" — and claim they have no money.

The beggar remains silent a moment, then renews his plea with a shrewd pivot: "Do not, my fair young ladies, stop your good ears against me." When the younger repeats that they have no change, he blesses them and wishes the multiplication of "those joys which you can give to others without change" — a phrase that moves the elder sister to reach into her pocket.

When she offers only a single sou, the beggar presses further: "[[nature|Nature]] has been bountiful to you, be bountiful to a poor man." His final stroke — asking "what is it but your goodness and humanity which makes your bright eyes so sweet, that they outshine the morning even in this dark passage?" and invoking the Marquis de Santerre's supposed praise — proves irresistible. Both women simultaneously put their hands into their pockets and each produces a twelve-sous piece. A brief contest ensues over which of the two should give, and they resolve it by both giving together.

In the immediately following chapter "The Riddle Explained," [[yorick|Yorick]] pursues the beggar and discovers his secret: flattery. "Delicious essence! how refreshing art thou to [[nature|Nature]]!" he exclaims, marvelling at how it "sweetly dost thou mix with the blood, and help it through the most difficult and tortuous passages to the heart."

## 主题分析

- **The Mechanics of [[charity|Charity]]**: The episode anatomises charity not as a pure moral reflex but as a transaction lubricated by vanity. The beggar's success depends entirely on his ability to make the women feel seen as beautiful and good — their charity is purchased with flattery, not solicited by need. [[charity|Charity]] in Sterne's hands becomes a phenomenon to be studied, not merely praised.

- **The Spectacle of [[nature|Human Nature]]**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s position as a hidden observer — dressed in black, barely visible — mirrors the novel's broader method. "[[nature|Nature]] is shy, and hates to act before spectators; but in such an unobserved corner you sometimes see a single short scene of hers worth all the sentiments of a dozen French plays compounded together." The dark passage becomes a theatre where the true drama of human motivation plays out.

- **Flattery as Social Currency**: The beggar's art lies in correcting, sweetening, concentring, and qualifying his flattery to the precise dosage each target requires. This makes him a figure of the sentimental traveller himself — both are students of the human heart, translating observation into effective action.
