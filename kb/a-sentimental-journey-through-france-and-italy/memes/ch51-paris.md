## PARIS.

[051-001]When I alighted at the hotel, the

[051-002]porter told me a young woman with a bandbox had been that moment

[051-003]enquiring for me.—I do not know, said the porter, whether

[051-004]she is gone away or not. I took the key of my chamber of

[051-005]him, and went upstairs; and when I had got within ten steps of

[051-006]the top of the landing before my door, I met her coming easily

[051-007]down. 

[051-008]It was the fair fille de chambre I had walked along the

[051-009]Quai de Conti with; Madame de R— had sent her upon some

[051-010]commission to a marchande des modes within a step or two

[051-011]of the Hôtel de Modene; and as I had fail’d in

[051-012]waiting upon her, had bid her enquire if I had left Paris; and if

[051-013]so, whether I had not left a letter addressed to her. 

[051-014]As the fair fille de chambre was so near my door, she

[051-015]returned back, and went into the room with me for a moment or two

[051-016]whilst I wrote a card. 

[051-017]It was a fine still evening in the latter end of the month of

[051-018]May,—the crimson window curtains (which were of the same

[051-019]colour as those of the bed) were drawn close:—the sun was

[051-020]setting, and reflected through them so warm a tint into the fair fille de chambre’s face,—I thought she

[051-021]blush’d;—the idea of it made me blush

[051-022]myself:—we were quite alone; and that superinduced a second

[051-023]blush before the first could get off. 

[051-024]There is a sort of a pleasing half guilty blush, where the

[051-025]blood is more in fault than the man:—’tis sent

[051-026]impetuous from the heart, and virtue flies after it,—not to

[051-027]call it back, but to make the sensation of it more delicious to

[051-028]the nerves:—’tis associated.— 

[051-029]But I’ll not describe it;—I felt something at first

[051-030]within me which was not in strict unison with the lesson of

[051-031]virtue I had given her the night before.—I sought five

[051-032]minutes for a card;—I knew I had not one.—I took up a

[051-033]pen.—I laid it down again;—my hand

[051-034]trembled:—the devil was in me. 

[051-035]I know as well as any one he is an adversary, whom, if we

[051-036]resist, he will fly from us;—but I seldom resist him at

[051-037]all; from a terror, though I may conquer, I may still get a hurt

[051-038]in the combat;—so I give up the triumph for security; and,

[051-039]instead of thinking to make him fly, I generally fly myself. 

[051-040]The fair fille de chambre came close up to the bureau

[051-041]where I was looking for a card—took up first the pen I cast

[051-042]down, then offer’d to hold me the ink; she offer’d it

[051-043]so sweetly, I was going to accept it;—but I durst

[051-044]not;—I have nothing, my dear, said I, to write

[051-045]upon.—Write it, said she, simply, upon anything.— 

[051-046]I was just going to cry out, Then I will write it, fair girl!

[051-047]upon thy lips.— 

[051-048]If I do, said I, I shall perish;—so I took her by the

[051-049]hand, and led her to the door, and begg’d she would not

[051-050]forget the lesson I had given her.—She said, indeed she

[051-051]would not;—and, as she uttered it with some earnestness,

[051-052]she turn’d about, and gave me both her hands, closed

[051-053]together, into mine;—it was impossible not to compress them

[051-054]in that situation;—I wish’d to let them go; and all

[051-055]the time I held them, I kept arguing within myself against

[051-056]it,—and still I held them on.—In two minutes I found

[051-057]I had all the battle to fight over again;—and I felt my

[051-058]legs and every limb about me tremble at the idea. 

[051-059]The foot of the bed was within a yard and a half of the place

[051-060]where we were standing.—I had still hold of her

[051-061]hands—and how it happened I can give no account; but I

[051-062]neither ask’d her—nor drew her—nor did I think

[051-063]of the bed;—but so it did happen, we both sat down. 

[051-064]I’ll just show you, said the fair fille de

[051-065]chambre , the little purse I have been making to-day to hold

[051-066]your crown. So she put her hand into her right pocket,

[051-067]which was next me, and felt for it some time—then into the

[051-068]left.—“She had lost it.”—I

[051-069]never bore expectation more quietly;—it was in her right

[051-070]pocket at last;—she pull’d it out; it was of green

[051-071]taffeta, lined with a little bit of white quilted satin, and just

[051-072]big enough to hold the crown: she put it into my hand;—it

[051-073]was pretty; and I held it ten minutes with the back of my hand

[051-074]resting upon her lap—looking sometimes at the purse,

[051-075]sometimes on one side of it. 

[051-076]A stitch or two had broke out in the gathers of my stock; the

[051-077]fair fille de chambre , without saying a word, took out her

[051-078]little housewife, threaded a small needle, and sew’d it

[051-079]up.—I foresaw it would hazard the glory of the day; and, as

[051-080]she pass’d her hand in silence across and across my neck in

[051-081]the manœuvre, I felt the laurels shake which fancy had

[051-082]wreath’d about my head. 

[051-083]A strap had given way in her walk, and the buckle of her shoe

[051-084]was just falling off.—See, said the fille de

[051-085]chambre , holding up her foot.—I could not, for my soul

[051-086]but fasten the buckle in return, and putting in the

[051-087]strap,—and lifting up the other foot with it, when I had

[051-088]done, to see both were right,—in doing it too suddenly, it

[051-089]unavoidably threw the fair fille de chambre off her

[051-090]centre,—and then—
