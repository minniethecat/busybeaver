# Virtue

Virtue in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is both an abstract moral ideal and a practical challenge. [[yorick|Yorick]] continually professes his devotion to virtue — yet Sterne repeatedly places him in situations where virtuous intention and natural feeling collide. The novel treats virtue not as a fixed possession but as a quality tested by circumstance, refined by sentiment, and ultimately inseparable from the heart's spontaneous movements.

## 定义与历史语境

In eighteenth-century moral philosophy, virtue was a central concern — debated by Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, Hume, and Smith. The dominant "moral sense" school held that virtue was rooted in natural sentiment rather than rational calculation: humans possess an innate benevolence that, properly cultivated, produces virtuous character. Sterne, a clergyman steeped in this tradition, brings the philosophical debate into fiction by making virtue the subject of lived experience rather than abstract argument.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Virtue appears throughout the novel as [[yorick|Yorick]]'s stated ideal — and his persistent test. From the earliest chapters, he frames his conduct in moral terms: he is "paying to virtue" when he offers compliments to a lady; he resolves to "leave [[paris|Paris]] with all the virtue I enter'd it" after a narrow escape from temptation. Yet Sterne is never content with simple moralizing. The narrator's internal struggles — his trembling hand, his blushes, his rationalizations — reveal virtue as an ongoing negotiation between principle and impulse.

The novel's most pointed treatment of virtue comes in the [[paris|Paris]] chapters, where [[yorick|Yorick]], after an evening of sentimental conversation, feels "something within me which was not in strict unison with the lesson of virtue I had given her the night before." The admission is characteristic: virtue is professed, felt, and threatened all at once.

## 关键段落

> "It was a small tribute, I told her, which I could not avoid paying to virtue, and would not be mistaken in the person I had been rendering it to for the world;—but I see innocence, my dear, in your face,—and foul befall the man who ever lays a snare in its way!" (038-066)

> "There is a sort of a pleasing half guilty blush, where the blood is more in fault than the man:—'tis sent impetuous from the heart, and virtue flies after it,—not to call it back, but to make the sensation of it more delicious to the nerves." (051-024)

> "I felt something at first within me which was not in strict unison with the lesson of virtue I had given her the night before." (051-030)

> "Great Governor of Nature! said I to myself:—wherever thy providence shall place me for the trials of my virtue;—whatever is my danger,—whatever is my situation,—let me feel the movements which rise out of it, and which belong to me as a man." (052-010)

> "I had resolved, after so narrow an escape, to run no more risks, but to leave Paris, if it was possible, with all the virtue I enter'd it." (055-011)
