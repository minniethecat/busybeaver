# The Temptation

**The Temptation** is an episode from the [[paris|Paris]] section of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]]* (1768), in which [[yorick|Yorick]] confronts a powerful erotic temptation in his room at the [[hotel-de-modene|Hôtel de Modène]]. The [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]], sent by [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] to enquire whether [[yorick|Yorick]] has left [[paris|Paris]], finds him alone at sunset — and the crimson curtains, mutual blushes, and physical proximity that follow test [[yorick|Yorick]]'s virtue to its limit. The episode extends into a companion section, **[[the-conquest|The Conquest]]**, in which [[yorick|Yorick]] achieves a "decisive" moral victory.

## 概要

Returning to the [[hotel-de-modene|Hôtel de Modène]], [[yorick|Yorick]] learns from the porter that a young woman with a bandbox has been enquiring for him. Climbing the stairs, he meets the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]] coming down — the same woman he had walked with along the [[quai-de-conti|Quai de Conti]]. She has been sent by [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]] to ask whether he has left a letter addressed to her.

Since she is already at his door, the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]] returns with [[yorick|Yorick]] into his room while he writes a card. The scene is set with careful visual sensuality: "It was a fine still evening in the latter end of the month of May, — the crimson window curtains (which were of the same colour as those of the bed) were drawn close: — the sun was setting, and reflected through them so warm a tint into [[the-fille-de-chambre|the fair fille de chambre]]'s face, — I thought she blush'd."

What follows is a cascade of blushes — hers, then his, then "a second blush before the first could get off." [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects on the physiology of the blush: "There is a sort of a pleasing half guilty blush, where the blood is more in fault than the man: — 'tis sent impetuous from the heart, and virtue flies after it, — not to call it back, but to make the sensation of it more delicious to the nerves."

Unable to find a card, [[yorick|Yorick]] fumbles with pen and ink — "my hand trembled: — the devil was in me." The [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]] comes close, offers to hold the ink, and when he protests he has nothing to write upon, says simply, "Write it upon anything." [[yorick|Yorick]] is on the verge of crying, "Then I will write it, fair girl! upon thy lips" — but checks himself: "If I do, I shall perish."

He leads her to the door — but she turns, gives him both her hands closed into his, and in a moment they are both seated on the bed. She shows him a little green taffeta purse she has made to hold his crown. She sews a stitch in his stock. He fastens her shoe buckle. The accumulation of small intimacies threatens to overwhelm his resolve.

In **[[the-conquest|The Conquest]]**, [[yorick|Yorick]] delivers an impassioned address to the "great Governor of [[nature|Nature]]" defending human passion as natural: "If [[nature|Nature]] has so wove her web of kindness, that some threads of love and desire are entangled with the piece, — must the whole web be rent in drawing them out?" He then raises the [[the-fille-de-chambre|fille de chambre]] by the hand, leads her out, locks the door, and — "the victory being quite decisive — and not till then" — presses his lips to her cheek and leads her safely to the gate.

## 主题分析

- **[[virtue|Virtue]] Tested**: The episode is the novel's most sustained examination of moral struggle. [[yorick|Yorick]] does not pretend to be immune to temptation — his hand trembles, his legs shake, and he admits "the devil was in me." His virtue is not innocence but resistance under fire.

- **The [[blush|Blush]] as Moral Barometer**: Sterne devotes extraordinary attention to the blush — its physiology, its half-guilty pleasure, its role as the body's honest emissary. The blush communicates what words cannot and, paradoxically, intensifies the very desire it signals.

- **Flight as Strategy**: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s philosophy of temptation is pragmatic rather than heroic: "I seldom resist him at all (051-036); from a terror, though I may conquer, I may still get a hurt in the combat; — so I give up the triumph for security; and, instead of thinking to make him fly, I generally fly myself." This is a worldly, unheroic virtue — and all the more believable for it.

- **[[nature|Nature]]'s Defence**: The address to the "Governor of [[nature|Nature]]" in [[the-conquest|The Conquest]] is a theological argument for embodied feeling. [[yorick|Yorick]] refuses to be a stoic who denies his nature; his morality consists in governing his passions, not extinguishing them.
