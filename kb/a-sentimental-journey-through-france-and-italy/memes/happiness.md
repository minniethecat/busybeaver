# Happiness

Happiness in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is never a settled condition. It is the object of a sustained philosophical inquiry that runs beneath the novel's surface — an inquiry that asks what happiness consists of, where it may be found, and why it so often eludes the traveler who seeks it. Sterne frames happiness not as a private achievement but as something constitutively shared: "It is there only that she has provided him with the most suitable objects to partake of his happiness, and bear a part of that burden."

## 定义与历史语境

Eighteenth-century moral philosophy was centrally occupied with happiness. From Locke's hedonism to Hutcheson's moral sense theory to the "pursuit of happiness" enshrined in the American Declaration, the century treated happiness as both a natural right and a philosophical problem. [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al literature added a distinctive thesis: that happiness is achieved not through self-interest but through sympathy — through the capacity to "partake" of another's feelings. Sterne's novel is a sustained experiment in this thesis, testing whether the sentimental traveler can genuinely share in the happiness of those he encounters, or whether his own sensibilities inevitably color and distort what he sees.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sterne treats happiness through a series of emblematic scenes, each of which complicates the initial, optimistic claim that happiness is naturally shared. The novel opens with the assertion that [[nature|Nature]] has provided "objects to partake of his happiness," but the encounters that follow test this claim severely. The dead ass episode interrupts [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s "happy and perpendicular" canter with the blunt question: "what is happiness! what is grandeur in this painted scene of life!" The exclamation undercuts the narrator's own narrative pleasure with a reminder of mortality and suffering.

The novel's most concentrated meditation on happiness occurs in the "Sword" episode, where [[yorick|Yorick]] kneels and invokes the "Eternal Fountain of Happiness" as witness to his refusal to accept a pension. Here happiness is redefined as moral integrity — the refusal to sell one's sentimental independence. The echo of this image returns in the novel's final pages: "Eternal Fountain of our feelings! — 'tis here I trace thee." Happiness, feeling, and moral sentiment are fused into a single devotional register.

Yet the novel also recognizes simpler, less philosophically freighted forms of happiness. The "happy people" dancing on the [[paris|Paris]] boulevards "once a week at least are sure to lay down all your cares together, and dance and sing and sport away the weights of grievance." And the final pastoral vision of the Supper episode blesses the "happy cottage" and "happy sharer of it" and "happy lambs" — a chain of domestic felicity that the novel's own narrative ironies cannot fully dispel.

## 关键段落

> "'Tis true, we are endued with an imperfect power of spreading our happiness sometimes beyond her limits, but 'tis so ordered, that, from the want of languages, connections, and dependencies, and from the difference in education, customs, and habits, we lie under so many impediments in communicating our sensations out of our own sphere, as can amount to a prohibition." (Opening meditation on the limits of shared happiness)

> "What is happiness! what is grandeur in this painted scene of life! A dead ass, before we had got a league, put a sudden stop to La Fleur's career." (024-008)

> "Eternal Fountain of Happiness! said I, kneeling down upon the ground, — be thou my witness — and every pure spirit which tastes it, be my witness also, That I would not travel to Brussels, and engage in this affair, for any consideration." (027-055)

> "Happy people! that once a week at least are sure to lay down all your cares together, and dance and sing and sport away the weights of grievance, which bow down the spirit of other nations to the earth." (056-075)

> "Happy is thy cottage, — and happy is the sharer of it, — and happy are the lambs which sport about you!" (The Supper episode, blessing the peasant's domestic felicity)
