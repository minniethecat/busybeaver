# Sorrow

Sorrow is the bass note of Sterne's sentimental symphony. If the journey is propelled by curiosity, desire, and the pursuit of pleasure, it is continually grounded by encounters with sorrow — the widow's declined beauty, [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]]'s "two first paroxysms," the dead ass that halts the happy canter. Sterne does not treat sorrow as something to be avoided or cured; he treats it as the primary material of sympathy, the emotion that most reliably calls forth the traveler's — and the reader's — capacity for fellow-feeling.

## 定义与历史语境

In eighteenth-century moral philosophy, sorrow occupied an ambiguous position. Stoic and rationalist traditions treated grief as a passion to be mastered; the sentimental tradition, by contrast, treated the capacity to feel sorrow — and to be moved by another's sorrow — as the very foundation of moral sensibility. Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral [[sentiment|Sentiment]]s* (1759) made "sympathy" with another's grief the paradigm case of moral feeling. Sterne's novel belongs to this tradition, but it goes further: it suggests that sorrow is not merely an occasion for sympathy but a form of beauty in its own right. The "characters of a widow'd look" that [[yorick|Yorick]] reads in the old woman's face are treated as an aesthetic object — a composition of distress that rewards contemplative attention.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Sorrow enters the novel early and returns with the regularity of a musical motif. The old woman in [[calais|Calais]], with her "two first paroxysms of sorrow" now past, presents a face in the process of "quietly beginning to reconcile itself to its loss." This is sorrow in its second phase — no longer acute but settled into the features as a permanent modification of character. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s response is characteristic: he wishes to know her story, to enter into her sorrow through imaginative sympathy.

The [[maria-of-moulines|Maria]] episode is the novel's emotional center and its most sustained treatment of sorrow. "[[melancholy|Melancholy]]! to see such sprightliness the prey of sorrow" — the exclamation captures the sentimental aesthetic at its purest: the contrast between what was (sprightliness) and what is (sorrow) creates the emotional charge. [[yorick|Yorick]]'s response — "I could have taken her into my arms, and cherished her, though it was in the open street" — crosses the line from sympathetic observation to physical tenderness, a boundary the novel continually tests.

The novel's philosophical reflection on sorrow culminates in a passage that treats the mind's capacity to "surrender itself to illusions, which cheat expectation and sorrow of their weary moments." Here sorrow is not the object of sympathy but the condition of life itself — the "weary moments" that must be "cheated" by illusion, art, and the sentimental imagination. The final image of sorrow comes near the novel's end: "pressing through this gate, of sorrow to it, my sufferings have totally unfitted me" — sorrow as a gate through which the traveler must pass, a threshold that transforms the one who crosses it.

## 关键段落

> "I fancied it wore the characters of a widow'd look, and in that state of its declension, which had passed the two first paroxysms of sorrow, and was quietly beginning to reconcile itself to its loss." (009-037)

> "Melancholy! to see such sprightliness the prey of sorrow, — I pitied her from my soul; and though it may seem ridiculous enough to a torpid heart, — I could have taken her into my arms, and cherished her, though it was in the open street." (010-036)

> "Sweet pliability of man's spirit, that can at once surrender itself to illusions, which cheat expectation and sorrow of their weary moments!" (048-011)

> "Pressing through this gate, of sorrow to it, my sufferings have totally unfitted me. In every scene of festivity, I saw Maria in the background of the piece, sitting pensive under her poplar." (066-004)
