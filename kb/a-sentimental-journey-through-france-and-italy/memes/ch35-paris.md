## PARIS.

[035-001]There was nobody in the box I was

[035-002]let into but a kindly old French officer. I love the

[035-003]character, not only because I honour the man whose manners are

[035-004]softened by a profession which makes bad men worse; but that I

[035-005]once knew one,—for he is no more,—and why should I

[035-006]not rescue one page from violation by writing his name in it, and

[035-007]telling the world it was Captain Tobias Shandy, the dearest of my

[035-008]flock and friends, whose philanthropy I never think of at this

[035-009]long distance from his death—but my eyes gush out with

[035-010]tears. For his sake I have a predilection for the whole

[035-011]corps of veterans; and so I strode over the two back rows of

[035-012]benches and placed myself beside him. 

[035-013]The old officer was reading attentively a small pamphlet, it

[035-014]might be the book of the opera, with a large pair of

[035-015]spectacles. As soon as I sat down, he took his spectacles

[035-016]off, and putting them into a shagreen case, return’d them

[035-017]and the book into his pocket together. I half rose up, and

[035-018]made him a bow. 

[035-019]Translate this into any civilized language in the

[035-020]world—the sense is this: 

[035-021]“Here’s a poor stranger come into the box—he

[035-022]seems as if he knew nobody; and is never likely, was he to be

[035-023]seven years in Paris, if every man he comes near keeps his

[035-024]spectacles upon his nose:—’tis shutting the door of

[035-025]conversation absolutely in his face—and using him worse

[035-026]than a German.” 

[035-027]The French officer might as well have said it all aloud: and if he

[035-028]had, I should in course have put the bow I made him into French

[035-029]too, and told him, “I was sensible of his attention, and

[035-030]return’d him a thousand thanks for it.” 

[035-031]There is not a secret so aiding to the progress of sociality,

[035-032]as to get master of this short hand , and to be quick in

[035-033]rendering the several turns of looks and limbs with all their

[035-034]inflections and delineations, into plain words. For my own

[035-035]part, by long habitude, I do it so mechanically, that, when I

[035-036]walk the streets of London, I go translating all the way; and

[035-037]have more than once stood behind in the circle, where not three

[035-038]words have been said, and have brought off twenty different

[035-039]dialogues with me, which I could have fairly wrote down and sworn

[035-040]to. 

[035-041]I was going one evening to Martini’s concert at Milan,

[035-042]and, was just entering the door of the hall, when the Marquisina

[035-043]di F— was coming out in a sort of a hurry:—she was

[035-044]almost upon me before I saw her; so I gave a spring to once side

[035-045]to let her pass.—She had done the same, and on the same

[035-046]side too; so we ran our heads together: she instantly got to the

[035-047]other side to get out: I was just as unfortunate as she had been,

[035-048]for I had sprung to that side, and opposed her passage

[035-049]again.—We both flew together to the other side, and then

[035-050]back,—and so on:—it was ridiculous: we both

[035-051]blush’d intolerably: so I did at last the thing I should

[035-052]have done at first;—I stood stock-still, and the Marquisina

[035-053]had no more difficulty. I had no power to go into the room,

[035-054]till I had made her so much reparation as to wait and follow her

[035-055]with my eye to the end of the passage. She look’d

[035-056]back twice, and walk’d along it rather sideways, as if she

[035-057]would make room for any one coming up stairs to pass

[035-058]her.—No, said I—that’s a vile translation: the

[035-059]Marquisina has a right to the best apology I can make her, and

[035-060]that opening is left for me to do it in;—so I ran and

[035-061]begg’d pardon for the embarrassment I had given her, saying

[035-062]it was my intention to have made her way. She answered, she

[035-063]was guided by the same intention towards me;—so we

[035-064]reciprocally thank’d each other. She was at the top

[035-065]of the stairs; and seeing no cicisbeo near her, I

[035-066]begg’d to hand her to her coach;—so we went down the

[035-067]stairs, stopping at every third step to talk of the concert

[035-068]and the adventure.—Upon my word, Madame, said I, when I had

[035-069]handed her in, I made six different efforts to let you go

[035-070]out.—And I made six efforts, replied she, to let you

[035-071]enter.—I wish to heaven you would make a seventh, said

[035-072]I.—With all my heart, said she, making room.—Life is

[035-073]too short to be long about the forms of it,—so I instantly

[035-074]stepp’d in, and she carried me home with her.—And

[035-075]what became of the concert, St. Cecilia, who I suppose was at it,

[035-076]knows more than I. 

[035-077]I will only add, that the connexion which arose out of the

[035-078]translation gave me more pleasure than any one I had the honour

[035-079]to make in Italy.
