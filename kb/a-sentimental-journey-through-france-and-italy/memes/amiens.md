# Amiens

**Amiens** is the French town where [[yorick|Yorick]], traveling from [[montreuil|Montreuil]] toward [[paris|Paris]], receives a letter of introduction to Madame R— from the Count de L—'s sister. Occupying chapters 27 through 29 of Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768), Amiens is the site of two narrative turning points: the commission that will eventually lead [[yorick|Yorick]] to [[madame-de-r|Madame de R—]], and the first test of [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s devotion as a servant.

## 地理与文化背景

In the 1760s, Amiens was a substantial cathedral town on the main coach route between [[calais|Calais]] and [[paris|Paris]], roughly midway through the journey. Its inns served the steady stream of English travelers crossing from [[dover|Dover]], and its position as a postal stop made it a natural point for the exchange of letters and commissions. For an 18th-century traveler like [[yorick|Yorick]], Amiens was a place of transit — not a destination, but a place where journeys acquired new direction.

## 在小说中的角色

Amiens serves as the hinge between the journey's opening movement and its [[paris|Paris]]ian center. Three events define its narrative role:

**[[the-letter|The Letter]] from [[madame-de-l|Madame de L—]] (Ch. 27–28)**. [[yorick|Yorick]] arrives at Amiens and falls asleep exhausted, only to be woken by the postilion's cry of "Amiens!" — "Bless me! said I, rubbing my eyes, — this is the very town where my poor lady is to come" (027-001). Moments later, the Count de L—'s post-chaise drives hastily past, and his sister — the "poor lady" [[yorick|Yorick]] refers to — makes him a "bow of recognition" that tells him "she had not yet done with me" (028-001). Before [[yorick|Yorick]] finishes his supper, her brother's servant arrives with a billet charging him to deliver a letter to Madame R— in [[paris|Paris]]. The commission is laden with emotional resonance: the Count's sister hints at an untold story, promises to discharge her obligation if [[yorick|Yorick]]'s route ever passes through [[brussels|Brussels]], and leaves [[yorick|Yorick]] in transports of sentimental anticipation.

**[[yorick|Yorick]]'s Inner Conflict (Ch. 28)**. The commission provokes one of the novel's most revealing interior monologues. [[yorick|Yorick]] imagines meeting the Count's sister in [[brussels|Brussels]] — "to see her weep! and, though I cannot dry up the fountain of her tears, what an exquisite sensation is there still left, in wiping them away from off the cheeks of the first and fairest of women" (028-005). But he instantly reproaches his heart: he has sworn eternal fidelity to Eliza, and the thought of divided affections torments him. Kneeling on the ground, he calls upon the "Eternal Fountain of [[happiness|Happiness]]" to witness that he "would not travel to [[brussels|Brussels]], unless Eliza went along with me, did the road lead me towards heaven" (028-007). The Amiens episode thus becomes a test of sentimental constancy, with [[yorick|Yorick]] caught between the pull of new sympathy and the bond of prior devotion.

**[[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s First Service (Ch. 29)**. [[fortune|Fortune]] has not smiled upon [[la-fleur|La Fleur]], who has been unsuccessful in his "feats of chivalry" during his first four-and-twenty hours of service. When the Count's servant arrives with the letter, [[la-fleur|La Fleur]] seizes the opportunity: he takes the man into a back parlour at the auberge and treats him with a cup or two of wine — his first independent act of zeal for his master. The scene is slight but structurally significant: it establishes [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s character as a servant whose devotion expresses itself through sociability rather than efficiency, and it marks the beginning of the master-servant bond that will sustain the entire journey.

**The [[paris|Paris]] Connection (Ch. 61)**. Amiens reappears retroactively in the [[paris|Paris]] chapters, when [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters the *fille de chambre* to Madame R— on a [[paris|Paris]] street. She tells him her mistress has been expecting "a stranger with a letter from Amiens" (061-005), and [[yorick|Yorick]] promises to wait upon Madame R— the next morning. The thread begun in Amiens thus weaves through the entire [[paris|Paris]] sequence, connecting the journey's beginning to its elaborations.
