# Life

Life in *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* is not merely the biological fact of existence but the "little span" within which all sentimental adventure unfolds. Sterne treats life as a painted scene — at once grand and fragile — where every encounter, every courtesy, and every touch of the heart acquires its urgency from the brevity that frames it.

## 定义与历史语境

Eighteenth-century thought inherited from both classical philosophy and Christian theology a deep preoccupation with life's brevity and purpose. The *vanitas* tradition, the *memento mori* of medieval Christianity, and the Enlightenment's new emphasis on terrestrial happiness all converge in Sterne's treatment of life. Unlike the moralists who stressed life's vanity, Sterne's sentimentalism insists that life's shortness is precisely what makes its small courtesies and affections precious.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

The word "life" appears across the entire novel as the implicit horizon against which [[yorick|Yorick]]'s encounters are measured. "What a large volume of adventures may be grasped within this little span of life by him who interests his heart in every thing" — this passage articulates the novel's central wager: that sentimental attention can expand the felt dimensions of a brief existence. [[yorick|Yorick]] speaks of being "almost every hour of it miserably in love with some one," transforming life into a continuous sequence of affective engagements.

The theme intensifies in moments of reversal — when [[yorick|Yorick]] encounters the Chevalier reduced from military honour to selling pâtés, "such a reverse in man's life awakens a better principle than curiosity." Life's vicissitudes become occasions for sentimental awakening. The old gentleman at the notary's office, crying "Almighty Director of every event in my life," embodies life as a labyrinth of "strange passages" through which only [[fortune|Providence]] can guide.

## 关键段落

> "What a large volume of adventures may be grasped within this little span of life by him who interests his heart in every thing." (Calais chapters)

> "Hail, ye small sweet courtesies of life, for smooth do ye make the road of it! like grace and beauty, which beget inclinations to love at first sight." (Paris chapters)

> "Life is too short to be long about the forms of it." (The Concert episode)

> "Such a reverse in man's life awakens a better principle than curiosity." (The Chevalier episode)

> "Almighty Director of every event in my life! said the old gentleman, looking up earnestly, and raising his hands towards heaven." (The Notary episode)

> "the best part of his life had passed in the service." (The Chevalier episode)
