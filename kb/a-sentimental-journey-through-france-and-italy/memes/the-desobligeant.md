# The Desobligeant

**The Desobligeant** ("In the Desobligeant," chapter 6) is one of the most important meta-fictional passages in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentimental]] [[travel|Journey]] Through [[france|France]] and [[italy|Italy]]* (1768). Sitting in his *désobligeante* — a light, single-person French carriage — at [[calais|Calais]], [[yorick|Yorick]] delivers a philosophical meditation on travel, human discontent, and the nature of narrative itself. The carriage's "see-saw" motion becomes a figure for the digressive structure of the novel that follows.

## 概要

The chapter opens with a reflection on the "boundaries and fences" that Nature has "set up by her own unquestionable authority to circumscribe the discontent of man" (006-004). Nature, Sterne argues, has "laid him under almost insuperable obligations to work out his ease, and to sustain his sufferings at home." It is only at home that she provides "the most suitable objects to partake of his happiness."

The traveller — the "expatriated adventurer" — operates at a permanent disadvantage. The "balance of sentimental commerce is always against" him (006-019): "he must buy what he has little occasion for, at their own price; — his conversation will seldom be taken in exchange for theirs without a large discount." This economic metaphor for social exchange is characteristically Sternian: conversation is a currency, and the traveller abroad is always trading at a loss.

Then, with a sudden turn, Sterne brings the meditation back to the carriage itself: "This brings me to my point; and naturally leads me (if the see-saw of this *désobligeant* will but let me get on) into the efficient as well as final causes of travelling."

## The Causes of Travel

[[yorick|Yorick]] catalogues the reasons people leave their native country:

- **Infirmity of body**
- **Imbecility of mind**
- **Inevitable necessity**

He sets out to enumerate the various sub-classes — "idle travellers," "inquisitive travellers," "lying travellers," "proud travellers," "vain travellers," "splenetic travellers" — before arriving at his own category: the "Sentimental Traveller." The catalogue is both a parody of Enlightenment taxonomy and a pre-emptive self-definition: [[yorick|Yorick]] is not merely a traveller but a *sentimental* one, whose journey is governed by the heart's impulses rather than any rational itinerary.

## The Carriage as Metaphor

The *désobligeante* — whose very name means "disobliging" — is itself a narrative device. The carriage's rocking motion ("the see-saw of this *désobligeant*") mirrors the digressive rhythm of Sterne's prose: forward movement perpetually interrupted by lateral excursions. The reader, like [[yorick|Yorick]] in his carriage, is carried forward not by the steady progress of plot but by the "see-saw" of digression and return.

The name is also a pun on Sterne's own relationship to his reader. A *désobligeante* is a carriage designed for one person — it "disobliges" by refusing to accommodate a companion. Sterne's narrative, similarly, proceeds on its own terms, "disobliging" the reader who expects a conventional travelogue. The novel will not go where it is told.

## 主题分析

- **Home and Away**: The chapter's opening argument — that Nature has designed us to find happiness at home — is paradoxical for a travel narrative. Sterne begins his journey by explaining why journeys are inherently self-defeating. This self-undermining gesture is characteristic: the novel constantly questions its own premise while proceeding anyway.

- **Sentimental Commerce**: The metaphor of conversation as currency — exchanged at a "discount" abroad, brokered by "more equitable" intermediaries — is a sharp analysis of the social economy of travel. The traveller is always the weaker party in the transaction, paying more and receiving less.

- **Narrative Self-Consciousness**: By calling attention to the "see-saw of this *désobligeant*," Sterne makes the carriage into a figure for the narrative itself. The chapter is a manifesto for the digressive method: the story will go wherever the carriage — or the mind — takes it.
