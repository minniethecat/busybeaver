# Nature

The concept of Nature in Laurence Sterne's *A [[sentiment|Sentiment]]al [[travel|Journey]]* operates on two levels: as an abstract moral authority governing human sentiment, and as the innate disposition that reveals true character. In the 18th-century sentimental tradition, Nature serves as the benchmark against which artificial social conventions are measured.

## 定义与历史语境

In eighteenth-century philosophy, Nature was not merely the physical world but a normative standard — the natural order against which human conduct could be judged. Thinkers from Shaftesbury to Rousseau held that natural sentiment, uncorrupted by artificial civilization, was the truest guide to virtue. Sterne draws heavily on this tradition, invoking Nature as an authority that validates [[yorick|Yorick]]'s spontaneous emotional responses throughout his journey.

The "peripatetic philosopher" passage in the Preface explicitly frames Nature as having "set up by her own unquestionable authority certain boundaries and fences to circumscribe the discontent of man" — positioning Nature as the governing principle of human contentment.

## 在 Sterne 小说中的表现

Nature appears throughout the novel as the force behind genuine human feeling. When [[yorick|Yorick]] reflects on the Monk's patient resignation, he observes that "Nature seemed to have done with her resentments in him" — suggesting Nature as an active agent that shapes emotional response.

In [[paris|Paris]], [[yorick|Yorick]] declares that Nature arms women "for immediate preservation," framing natural instinct as superior to calculated reason. The novel consistently privileges natural impulse over artificial restraint: [[yorick|Yorick]]'s tears, his spontaneous charity, and his quick sympathies are all portrayed as expressions of Nature rather than rational deliberation.

Sterne also uses Nature to characterize individuals. [[yorick|Yorick]] describes himself as "a son of Nature," and the novel evaluates characters by how closely their conduct aligns with natural goodness — [[la-fleur|La Fleur]]'s simple honesty and the Monk's patient resignation are praised precisely because they spring from Nature, not artifice.

## 关键段落

> "Nature has set up by her own unquestionable authority certain boundaries and fences to circumscribe the discontent of man; she has effected her purpose in the quietest and easiest manner by laying him under almost insuperable obligations to work out his ease, and to sustain his sufferings at home." (Preface chapter)

> "Nature seemed to have done with her resentments in him; — he showed none." (The Monk chapter)

> "Nature arms her with it, said I, for immediate preservation." (The Fille de Chambre episode, Paris chapters)

> "'tis an assay upon human nature — I get my labour for my pains, — 'tis enough; — the pleasure of the experiment has kept my senses and the best part of my blood awake, and laid the gross to sleep." (Paris chapters)
