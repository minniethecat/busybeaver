## PARIS.

[032-001]Hail , ye small sweet courtesies of

[032-002]life, for smooth do ye make the road of it! like grace and

[032-003]beauty, which beget inclinations to love at first sight:

[032-004]’tis ye who open this door and let the stranger in. 

[032-005]—Pray, Madame, said I, have the goodness to tell me

[032-006]which way I must turn to go to the Opéra

[032-007]Comique?—Most willingly, Monsieur, said she, laying aside

[032-008]her work.— 

[032-009]I had given a cast with my eye into half a dozen shops, as I

[032-010]came along, in search of a face not likely to be disordered by

[032-011]such an interruption: till at last, this, hitting my fancy, I had

[032-012]walked in. 

[032-013]She was working a pair of ruffles, as she sat in a low chair,

[032-014]on the far side of the shop, facing the door. 

[032-015]— Très volontiers , most willingly, said she,

[032-016]laying her work down upon a chair next her, and rising up from

[032-017]the low chair she was sitting in, with so cheerful a movement,

[032-018]and so cheerful a look, that had I been laying out fifty louis

[032-019]d’ors with her, I should have said—“This woman

[032-020]is grateful.” 

[032-021]You must turn, Monsieur, said she, going with me to the door

[032-022]of the shop, and pointing the way down the street I was to

[032-023]take,—you must turn first to your left hand,— mais

[032-024]prenez garde —there are two turns; and be so good as to

[032-025]take the second—then go down a little way and you’ll

[032-026]see a church: and, when you are past it, give yourself the

[032-027]trouble to turn directly to the right, and that will lead you to

[032-028]the foot of the Pont Neuf, which you must cross—and there

[032-029]any one will do himself the pleasure to show you.— 

[032-030]She repeated her instructions three times over to me, with the

[032-031]same goodnatur’d patience the third time as the

[032-032]first;—and if tones and manners have a meaning,

[032-033]which certainly they have, unless to hearts which shut them

[032-034]out,—she seemed really interested that I should not lose

[032-035]myself. 

[032-036]I will not suppose it was the woman’s beauty,

[032-037]notwithstanding she was the handsomest grisette, I think, I ever

[032-038]saw, which had much to do with the sense I had of her courtesy;

[032-039]only I remember, when I told her how much I was obliged to her,

[032-040]that I looked very full in her eyes,—and that I repeated my

[032-041]thanks as often as she had done her instructions. 

[032-042]I had not got ten paces from the door, before I found I had

[032-043]forgot every tittle of what she had said;—so looking back,

[032-044]and seeing her still standing in the door of the shop, as if to

[032-045]look whether I went right or not,—I returned back to ask

[032-046]her, whether the first turn was to my right or left,—for

[032-047]that I had absolutely forgot.—Is it possible! said she,

[032-048]half laughing. ’Tis very possible, replied I, when a

[032-049]man is thinking more of a woman than of her good advice. 

[032-050]As this was the real truth—she took it, as every woman

[032-051]takes a matter of right, with a slight curtsey. 

[032-052]— Attendez ! said she, laying her hand upon my arm

[032-053]to detain me, whilst she called a lad out of the back shop to get

[032-054]ready a parcel of gloves. I am just going to send him, said

[032-055]she, with a packet into that quarter, and if you will have the

[032-056]complaisance to step in, it will be ready in a moment, and he

[032-057]shall attend you to the place.—So I walk’d in with

[032-058]her to the far side of the shop: and taking up the ruffle in my

[032-059]hand which she laid upon the chair, as if I had a mind to sit, she sat

[032-060]down herself in her low chair, and I instantly sat myself down

[032-061]beside her. 

[032-062]—He will be ready, Monsieur, said she, in a

[032-063]moment.—And in that moment, replied I, most willingly would

[032-064]I say something very civil to you for all these courtesies.

[032-065]Any one may do a casual act of good nature, but a continuation of

[032-066]them shows it is a part of the temperature; and certainly, added

[032-067]I, if it is the same blood which comes from the heart which

[032-068]descends to the extremes (touching her wrist) I am sure you must

[032-069]have one of the best pulses of any woman in the world.—Feel

[032-070]it, said she, holding out her arm. So laying down my hat, I

[032-071]took hold of her fingers in one hand, and applied the two

[032-072]forefingers of my other to the artery.— 

[032-073]—Would to heaven! my dear Eugenius, thou hadst passed

[032-074]by, and beheld me sitting in my black coat, and in my

[032-075]lack-a-day-sical manner, counting the throbs of it, one by one,

[032-076]with as much true devotion as if I had been watching the critical

[032-077]ebb or flow of her fever.—How wouldst thou have

[032-078]laugh’d and moralized upon my new profession!—and

[032-079]thou shouldst have laugh’d and moralized on.—Trust

[032-080]me, my dear Eugenius, I should have said, “There are worse

[032-081]occupations in this world than feeling a woman’s

[032-082]pulse .”—But a grisette’s! thou wouldst have

[032-083]said,—and in an open shop! Yorick— 

[032-084]—So much the better: for when my views are direct,

[032-085]Eugenius, I care not if all the world saw me feel it.
