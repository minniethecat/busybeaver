# Vardaman Bundren

The youngest Bundren child, approximately seven or eight years old, who narrates eight chapters of As I Lay Dying. Vardaman's consciousness is the novel's most radically experimental narrative register: his monologues refract the trauma of [[addies-death|Addie's death]] through sensory association, animistic logic, and the famous formulation "My [[motherhood|mother]] is a fish" — a statement that is not metaphor but literal equivalence within his cognitive world.

## Role in the Novel

Vardaman serves as the novel's most unmediated lens on grief. Where his siblings process [[addies-death|Addie's death]] through [[language-and-meaning|language]], [[family-duty|duty]], or rage, Vardaman experiences it as a catastrophic reorganization of reality itself. His first major action — drilling holes into [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin so she can breathe, inadvertently boring into her face — literalizes the child's inability to distinguish between the living [[the-body|body]] and the dead one (P03-014, P03-016). He is the only character besides [[darl-bundren|Darl]] who is present at the novel's final scene, observing [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s introduction of the new Mrs. Bundren with the same wide-eyed registration that characterized his earliest chapters.

## Biography

Vardaman is the youngest of the five Bundren children, born to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] and [[anse-bundren|Anse]] on their failing farm in Yokna[[anse-bundren|pa]]tawpha County. He is old enough to help with chores — driving the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] team, fetching [[water-and-river|water]] — but young enough to still sleep beside his [[motherhood|mother]] during her final illness. His world before [[addies-death|Addie's death]] consists of fishing trips, the family [[the-cow|cow]], and the sensory texture of rural life; afterward, it becomes a landscape of incomprehensible loss that he processes by displacing grief onto objects and animals: [[vardamans-fish|the fish]] he caught and cleaned becomes his [[motherhood|mother]], the [[the-buzzards|buzzards]] following the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] become a threat he tries to ward off, the t[[water-and-river|rain]] in the shop window in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] represents a Christmas gift he will never receive (013-009).

## Narrative Voice

Vardaman's eight chapters are among Faulkner's most radical experiments with stream of consciousness. His monologues are characterized by:

- **Associative logic**: Events are linked by sensory similarity rather than causality. The fish's transformation from living creature to cut pieces mirrors his [[motherhood|mother]]'s transformation from person to corpse (019-001).

- **Animistic perception**: Objects and animals are endowed with consciousness — the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] "knows," the [[the-buzzards|buzzards]] "watch," and "my [[motherhood|mother]] is a fish" is not simile but identity (035-001).

- **Paratactic syntax**: Short declarative sentences joined by "and," mirroring a child's oral storytelling [[anse-bundren|pa]]tterns (015-001).

- **Visual primacy**: Vardaman's chapters are dominated by what he sees — [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s eyes, [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] in the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]], [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] — rather than what he thinks, producing a documentary immediacy that the more reflective narrators cannot achieve (024-001).

## Key Relationships

**[[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]**: Vardaman's relationship with his dead [[motherhood|mother]] is the emotional center of his narrative. He drills holes in her coffin to let her breathe, conflating her with [[vardamans-fish|the fish]] he caught and cleaned on the day she died (008-016 through 008-023). His grief is expressed not through mourning but through protest: he tries to open [[addies-coffin|the coffin]], attacks the men lowering it into the grave, and runs [[jewels-horse|the horse]] away from the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]], as if movement can undo what has happened.

**[[darl-bundren|Darl Bundren]]**: [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is the brother Vardaman most resembles in perceptual sensitivity, and [[darl-bundren|Darl]] speaks to him with a gentleness he shows no one else. When [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is seized for the asylum, Vardaman's response — "[[darl-bundren|Darl]] is my brother" — is one of the novel's simplest and most devastating lines (P03-057).

**[[jewel-bundren|Jewel Bundren]]**: Vardaman's relationship with [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] is defined by rivalry for the [[motherhood|mother]]'s attention. Vardaman perceives [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] as having a claim on [[addie-bundren|Addie]] that he lacks, and when [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] saves [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin from [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]], Vardaman's response is not gratitude but bewildered resentment (P03-048, P03-050).

## Critical Reception

Vardaman's chapters have attracted sustained critical attention as Faulkner's most ambitious rendering of childhood consciousness. The "my [[motherhood|mother]] is a fish" [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage is among the most analyzed sentences in American modernism, read variously as an expression of transubstantiation (Bleikasten), a demonstration of preoperational cognition (psychoanalytic readings), and a proto-Lacanian entry into the symbolic order. More recent ecocritical readings connect Vardaman's animistic perception to a b[[the-journey|road]]er Faulknerian critique of the Cartesian [[isolation|separation]] of human and animal consciousness.

> "Vardaman's monologues represent Faulkner's most radical attempt to dismantle the ego as a stable, self-identical substance and to render consciousness as a field of sensory intensities rather than a container of thoughts." — Donald M. Kartiganer, *The Fragile Thread* (1979)
