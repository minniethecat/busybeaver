# The River Crossing

The river crossing is the [[the-journey|journey]]'s moment of maximum peril — a trial by [[water-and-river|water]] that transforms the Bundrens' funeral procession from a difficult [[family-duty|duty]] into a near-tragedy, and that reveals each character's essential nature with the clarity of crisis.

## Summary

After days of [[water-and-river|rain]], [[the-river-crossing-location|the ford]] where the Bundrens planned to cross [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] has become a torrent. Against the advice of local farmers, [[anse-bundren|Anse]] insists on [[water-and-river|crossing]]. The [[the-mule-team|mules]] are swept away and drown; the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] overturns; [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] is nearly lost to the current; [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]] is broken. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], diving into the [[water-and-river|water]], saves [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] through sheer physical force. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] narrates the scene with hallucinatory precision. The family regroups on the far bank, diminished and damaged, but [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] — and the mission — survive.

## Context

The river crossing occurs because [[anse-bundren|Anse]] refuses to wait or to detour. The [[water-and-river|flood]]ed ford is a known danger; the local farmers, including [[vernon-tull|Tull]], advise against [[water-and-river|crossing]]. But [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s stubbornness — his conviction that his [[family-duty|promise]] to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] cannot be delayed — overrides all prudence. The [[water-and-river|crossing]] thus embodies the novel's central [[the-journey|quest]]ion: is the fulfillment of a sacred [[family-duty|promise]] worth any cost, or does the cost eventually disqualify the [[family-duty|promise]]?

## Significance

The [[water-and-river|crossing]] functions as a revelation machine. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s furious physical courage — he alone saves [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] — exposes the depth of his [[love|devotion]] to his [[motherhood|mother]]. [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s stoic endurance of his [[cashs-broken-leg|broken leg]] reveals his ethic of silent suffering. [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s detached, poetic narration of the disaster reveals his growing [[isolation|alienation]]. And [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssivity — he stands on the bank, worrying about his tools — reveals his essential self-absorption. The [[water-and-river|crossing]] does not change the characters; it strips away the pretense that they are anything other than what they are.

## Key Passages

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s description of the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] in the [[water-and-river|water]] reaches toward the hallucinatory: the [[the-mule-team|mules]] "rolling in the [[water-and-river|water]], their legs interleaped and cocked, their eyes rolling, and their mouths gaping" (023-001). [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s concern, even as his leg breaks, is for [[addies-coffin|the coffin]]'s structural integrity: "It wasn't on a balance" (023-001). The juxtaposition of physical catastrophe with [[work-and-craft|craft]]-specific concern is pure Faulkner — the tragic and the absurd, inse[[anse-bundren|pa]]rable.
