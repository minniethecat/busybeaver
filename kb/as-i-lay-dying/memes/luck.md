# Luck and Fate

The vocabulary of luck — luckless, bad luck, fortune, misfortune — runs through *As I Lay Dying* as a persistent refrain, most insistently in the voice of Anse Bundren. "I am a luckless man," Anse declares [005-020], and this self-characterization shapes his entire approach to the novel's events: he is not responsible for his circumstances but is instead the victim of cosmic forces that have targeted him. Faulkner uses the theme of luck to explore the boundary between fate and agency, between genuine misfortune and the self-serving narratives that characters construct to absolve themselves of responsibility.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Anse Bundren's Fatalism

Anse is the novel's primary spokesman for the luck-as-fate worldview. His repeated invocations of bad luck serve multiple functions: they explain his poverty, justify his inaction, and preemptively excuse his failures. "Every bad luck that comes and goes is bound to find it," he says of his own doorstep [009-002], as though misfortune were a predator that has learned where he lives. Anse's luck-talk is a form of narrative self-construction: by casting himself as fortune's victim, he positions himself outside the chain of cause and effect, exempt from the obligations that bind other men. His luckless identity is, paradoxically, a form of freedom — it allows him to demand help from neighbors, to avoid labor, and ultimately to acquire new teeth and a new wife while his children suffer the consequences of the journey.

### Through Cora Tull's Religious Framework

Cora Tull provides the novel's alternative framework for understanding fortune: divine providence. Where Anse sees bad luck, Cora sees God's will. Her religious interpretation of events — that suffering has moral meaning, that earthly misfortune will be compensated in the afterlife — stands in direct tension with Anse's secular fatalism. Yet Faulkner complicates both positions: the novel's events are too random to confirm Cora's providentialism and too patterned to confirm Anse's pure-chance model. The truth lies somewhere between, in a world where things happen for reasons that are real but not necessarily moral.

### The Journey as Trial by Fortune

The Bundrens' journey to Jefferson becomes a test case for the luck hypothesis. The washed-out bridge, the swollen river, the broken leg, the burning barn, the mounting hostility of strangers — are these bad luck, bad planning, or something else? Faulkner refuses to resolve the question, instead presenting the events as overdetermined: they result simultaneously from natural forces (rain, gravity, fire), human choices (Anse's insistence on the journey, Darl's arson), and the kind of narrative patterning that we call fate. The novel's genius is to hold these explanatory frameworks in tension without privileging any one of them.

### Luck and Class

The theme of luck also carries a class dimension. The Bundrens are poor farmers, and their "bad luck" is inseparable from their economic position. What Anse calls luck is, in part, the predictable consequence of poverty: lack of resources, lack of options, lack of margin for error. The novel hints at this reading without fully endorsing it — Anse's lucklessness is real, but it is also a story he tells to avoid confronting his own choices.

## Critical Interpretation

The luck motif connects *As I Lay Dying* to broader traditions in American literature, particularly the naturalist tradition that emphasizes the power of environment and circumstance over individual agency. Yet Faulkner departs from strict naturalism by preserving a space for human choice and moral judgment: Anse may be luckless, but he is also selfish, and the novel does not excuse one by invoking the other.
