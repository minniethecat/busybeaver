The Bundren wagon is the central object of *As I Lay Dying*, serving as the vehicle that carries Addie Bundren's coffin across Yoknapatawpha County to Jefferson. It is both a literal means of transport and a powerful symbol of the family's shared burden.

## Role in the Journey

The wagon first appears early in the novel, referenced as Tull's wagon standing beside the spring [001-004]. The Bundrens load Addie's coffin onto their own wagon and set out across the countryside. As Darl observes, "the road vanishes beneath the wagon as though it were a ribbon and the front axle has been spooled on it" [010-002], capturing the hypnotic, surreal quality of the journey.

Anse insists on using the family wagon rather than borrowing one: "She'll rest easier in her own wagon" [005-017]. This insistence on using their own wagon, despite its poor condition, reflects Anse's stubborn pride and the family's isolation.

## Symbolic Weight

The wagon functions as more than transportation — it embodies the burden of Addie's death that the family must carry. Cash builds the coffin and secures it to the wagon; Jewel rides ahead on his horse, both physically and emotionally distancing himself from the load. The wagon's slow progress through mud, flood, and fire mirrors the family's deteriorating cohesion [025-008].

The physical weight of the wagon and its cargo is constantly remarked upon: "They had already dragged the wagon out of the ditch" [020-001]. Each obstacle the wagon encounters — the fallen bridge, the flooded river, the burning barn — tests the family's resolve.
