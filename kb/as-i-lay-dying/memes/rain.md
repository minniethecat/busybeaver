# Rain

In William Faulkner's *As I Lay Dying*, rain is a pervasive atmospheric presence that shapes the novel's physical and symbolic landscape. From the opening chapters, the threat of rain hangs over the Bundren household, ultimately materializing as the storm that swells the Yoknapatawpha River and transforms the family's journey to Jefferson into a trial by water. Rain functions simultaneously as a natural phenomenon, a narrative device that heightens tension, and a symbol of the indifferent forces that shape human fate.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Anse Bundren's Fatalism

Anse Bundren, the family patriarch, repeatedly invokes the coming rain as evidence of his lifelong bad luck. His refrain "It's fixing to rain" becomes a mantra of passive resignation. Standing on the porch, looking out over the land, Anse sees the weather as yet another cosmic force aligned against him: "I am a luckless man" [005-020]. For Anse, the rain is not merely weather but metaphysical confirmation of his victimhood — a force that justifies his inaction and his reliance on others to carry the family's burdens. He invokes the rain as a preemptive excuse, using it to explain why the journey must happen quickly despite the obvious dangers [008-004].

### Through Darl Bundren's Poetic Perception

Darl, the novel's most perceptive narrator, experiences rain through a markedly different register. Where Anse sees fate, Darl sees form and transformation. His descriptions of the approaching storm carry an almost painterly quality: he observes "the rain shutting down betwixt us, a-coming up the road" with the detached clarity of an artist observing a composition [009-014]. In the river-crossing scene, Darl's narration captures the rain's effect on the mules — "In the rain the mules smoke a little" [012-012] — transforming a mundane detail into an image of ethereal strangeness. Darl's rain is a phenomenon of perception, not fate; it reveals the world's texture rather than its meaning.

### Through the Physical Journey

Beyond individual perception, rain exerts a concrete, material force on the Bundrens' journey. The rain that falls throughout the early chapters swells the river, destroys the bridge, and turns the roads to mud. It is rain that forces the family to attempt the disastrous ford at the washed-out bridge [008-004]. The rain transforms the landscape itself: the dust of the Bundren farm becomes clinging mud, the river rises to deadly heights, and the family's wagon — their sole means of transporting Addie's body — becomes mired and waterlogged. Rain in this sense is the novel's great antagonist, a natural force that the Bundrens cannot bargain with, pray away, or outwit.

### Through the Women's Experience

Cora Tull, the novel's most overtly religious character, interprets the rain within a providential framework. She sees divine purpose in the weather, reading it as God's commentary on human affairs. Yet Faulkner subtly undermines this reading: the rain falls on the just and unjust alike, indifferent to Cora's piety, Addie's suffering, or Dewey Dell's desperation. The rain's indifference to human categories of meaning and morality aligns it with the novel's broader naturalistic vision — a world in which human consciousness struggles to impose order on forces that remain fundamentally beyond comprehension.

## Critical Interpretation

The rain motif in *As I Lay Dying* participates in what critic Cleanth Brooks identified as Faulkner's use of nature not as Romantic backdrop but as active, often hostile presence. The rain is not symbolic in the conventional sense — it does not "stand for" a single idea — but operates instead as what Olga Vickery called a "structural counterpoint" to human intention. The Bundrens plan, speak, and desire; the rain falls. This disjunction between human purpose and natural process is central to Faulkner's modernist vision.

The biblical echoes are also unmistakable: the rain and subsequent flood evoke the Deluge, with the Bundrens' wagon as a kind of absurd ark carrying not the renewal of life but a decaying corpse. Yet Faulkner resists allegory; the rain remains stubbornly, materially rain — wet, cold, and indifferent.
