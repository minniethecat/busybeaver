# Vernon Tull

A neighboring farmer who lives near the Bundrens and serves as one of the novel's fifteen first-person narrators. Across his six narrative chapters, Tull provides a grounded, practical counterpoint to the Bundrens' more turbulent interior lives, offering observations on the limits of neighborly [[family-duty|obligation]], the nature of [[work-and-craft|work]] and property, and the quiet endurance of rural marriage.

## Role in the Novel

Vernon Tull functions as the novel's primary "outsider" narrator among the Bundrens' immediate neighbors. His chapters frame key events — [[addies-death|Addie's death]] night, [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]] [[water-and-river|crossing]], and the aftermath of [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] disaster — from a perspective unburdened by the family's internal grief and dysfunction. He is present at the Bundren farm on the night [[addie-bundren|Addie]] dies, accom[[anse-bundren|pa]]nies the family to the [[the-washed-out-bridge|washed-out bridge]], and helps them cross [[the-river-crossing-location|the flooded river]]. His presence grounds the novel's more hallucinatory [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssages in the physical realities of rural [[jefferson|Mississippi]] life: mud, [[water-and-river|rain]], [[the-mule-team|mules]], and the practical calculations of farm [[work-and-craft|work]].

Tull's narration is [[anse-bundren|pa]]rticularly important in [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]]-[[water-and-river|crossing]] sequence. Standing on the wrecked bridge with [[anse-bundren|Anse]], [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]], he renders the experience of vertigo and danger with a farmer's concrete imagery: "[[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]] shaking and swaying under us, going down into the moiling [[water-and-river|water]] like it went clean through to the other side of the earth" [031-017]. His perspective makes the surreal feel physically immediate.

## Biography

Vernon Tull is a small-scale farmer who lives within a few miles of [[bundren-farm|the Bundren place]]. He is married to [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], a devoutly religious woman whose moral pronouncements frequently contrast with her husband's pragmatism. Tull owns at least one mule, which [[anse-bundren|Anse]] repeatedly eyes as a potential replacement for the team lost in [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]], though Tull is careful to avoid surrendering it [034].

Tull's age is never specified, but his narrative voice — seasoned, wry, and steeped in rural aphorism — suggests a man of middle years who has spent his life [[work-and-craft|work]]ing the land. He recalls his own [[addies-death|mother's death]] with characteristic understatement: "I mind my mammy lived to be seventy and more. Worked every day, [[water-and-river|rain]] or shine; never a sick day [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]ce her last chap was born until one day she kind of looked around her and then she went and taken that lace-trimmed nightgown she had had forty-five years and never wore out of the chest and put it on and laid down on the bed and pulled the covers up and shut her eyes" [008-004].

## Narrative Voice

Tull is one of the novel's fifteen narrators, contributing six chapters [008, 016, 020, 027, 031, 036]. His voice is among the most colloquial in the novel, marked by rural [[jefferson|Mississippi]] dialect ("I be durn," "a fellow's got to guess ahead," "mought have been"), concrete agricultural metaphors, and a dry, understated humor. Unlike the Bundrens' more fractured, stream-of-consciousness narration, Tull's sentences tend toward the declarative and the cumulative — he builds observations through repetition and practical example rather than interior monologue.

His reflections on marriage and [[family-duty|duty]] are [[anse-bundren|pa]]rticularly distinctive. Describing his relationship with [[cora-tull|Cora]], he reaches for a homely metaphor drawn from farm life:

> "When I looked back at my mule it was like he was one of these here spyglasses and I could look at him standing there and see all the broad land and my house sweated outen it like it was the more the sweat, the broader the land; the more the sweat, the tighter the house because it would take a tight house for Cora, to hold Cora like a jar of milk in the spring: you've got to have a tight jar or you'll need a powerful spring, so if you have a big spring, why then you have the incentive to have tight, well-made jars, because it is your milk, sour or not, because you would rather have milk that will sour than to have milk that won't, because you are a man." [036-007]

This [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage typifies Tull's narrative method: a concrete image (the mule, the spyglass) unfolds into an extended analogy that circles around a core truth about his life — the need to build something solid enough to hold what matters.

## Key Relationships

**[[cora-tull|Cora Tull]]**: Tull's wife, whose fierce Christian piety often puts her at odds with his pragmatism. On the night of [[addies-death|Addie's death]], [[cora-tull|Cora]] insists it is their "Christian [[family-duty|duty]]" to go to the Bundrens immediately despite the approaching storm; Tull refuses, arguing "when folks wants a fellow, it's best to wait till they sends for him" [016-012]. Their marriage, while marked by these small tensions, is presented as fundamentally stable — a [[work-and-craft|work]]ing [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtnership built on long acquaintance and mutual accommodation.

**[[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]**: Tull regards [[anse-bundren|Anse]] with a mixture of neighborly [[family-duty|obligation]] and quiet exasperation. He is keenly aware of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s tendency to avoid [[work-and-craft|work]] and shift burdens onto others, [[anse-bundren|pa]]rticularly regarding the [[the-mule-team|mule team]]. When [[anse-bundren|Anse]] lingers on [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]], looking back as if hoping the whole situation might "blow up," Tull's narration conveys a farmer's im[[anse-bundren|pa]]tience with another man's reluctance to act [031-013].

**[[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman Bundren]]**: Tull's interaction with [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] on [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]] is one of his most tender moments. He reaches out to steady the boy and is struck by the reversal: "I be durn if it wasn't like he come back and got me; like he was saying They won't nothing hurt you" [036-006]. The child's presence transforms Tull's fear into something approaching wonder.
