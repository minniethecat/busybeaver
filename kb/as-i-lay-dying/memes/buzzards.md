The buzzards are among the most potent symbols in *As I Lay Dying*, appearing at key moments throughout the Bundrens' journey to Jefferson. As carrion birds drawn to the odor of Addie Bundren's decaying body, they serve as an inescapable reminder of death's physical reality.

## First Appearances

The buzzard motif is introduced early, even before the journey begins. Darl describes Tull and the other men "sitting there, like buzzards. Waiting, fanning themselves" [004-002], establishing the bird as a metaphor for those who gather around death. Vardaman, with a child's directness, calls Tull "old turkey-buzzard Tull coming to watch her die" [007-006].

These early associations link the buzzards not just to death itself but to the social performance surrounding death — the neighbors who watch, wait, and judge.

## Following the Wagon

Once the journey begins, literal buzzards appear. Darl's narrative eye captures them with precision: "Motionless, the tall buzzards hang in soaring circles, the clouds giving them an illusion of retrograde" [021-004]. The buzzards' patient, indifferent circling above the wagon creates an image that is both beautiful and horrifying.

The buzzards become increasingly bold as the journey progresses. Vardaman notices one close: "It was a buzzard. It looked around and saw me" [029-054]. The buzzard is no longer an abstract symbol but a living creature, looking back at the living.

Darl's description reaches its peak with the image of a buzzard so still "as if he were nailed to the sky" [030-008] — an image that suggests both crucifixion and the fixity of death's gaze.

## The Chase

By the time the family reaches the later stages of their journey, the buzzards have become an active nuisance. Vardaman recalls "when I see the first buzzard. Because of the wetting, I reckon" [043-013] — the rain having intensified the odor. The boy chases the buzzards away, and the image of "that boy chasing them buzzards all day in the hot sun" [043-057] captures the futility of fighting death's approach.

## Symbolic Meaning

The buzzards embody the novel's preoccupation with the physical reality of death. Unlike the abstractions of grief or memory, the buzzards respond to the body's material decay. They represent nature's indifference to human ritual and dignity — the body must return to the earth, regardless of promises made or journeys undertaken.
