# Bundren Farmhouse

The Bundren farmhouse is the physical dwelling on [[bundren-farm|the Bundren farm]], distinct from the broader property of fields, barn, and cotton house. It is the novel's primary interior space — the room where [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]] lies dying, where [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]] offers her judgments, and where the family's private rituals of grief and obligation play out before the journey to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] begins.

## The House as Setting

The farmhouse is defined less by its physical description than by what happens within it. [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s bedroom, with its window facing [[cash-bundren|Cash]] as he saws and bevels the coffin boards, becomes the novel's focal point in the early chapters. The house contains the sounds of [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s adze (001-001), the whispers of [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s fan, and the comings and goings of neighbors — [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], [[vernon-tull|Vernon Tull]], [[peabody|Doctor Peabody]] — who gather to witness [[addies-death|Addie's death]]. The spatial arrangement of the house — Addie in bed, Cash at the window, [[anse-bundren|Anse]] on the porch — maps the family's emotional positions onto physical space.

## Distinction from the Farm

While [[bundren-farm|the Bundren farm]] encompasses the full property — the cotton fields, the bluff, the bottom land, the barn, the cotton house — the farmhouse is specifically the domestic interior. It is the space of [[motherhood|motherhood]], illness, and the private life of the family, as opposed to the exterior spaces of [[labor]] and the natural world. The journey to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] is also a movement away from the farmhouse — the domestic, the known, the private — and into the public world of the road, where the family's grief becomes spectacle.

## Key Scenes

The farmhouse is the setting for the novel's opening drama: [[cash-bundren|Cash]] building [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] within sight and sound of [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s deathbed (001–012), [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s visits and her moral commentary on the Bundrens' perceived failings (006), [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] fanning her mother in the close air of the sickroom, and the night of [[addies-death|Addie's death]] when [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] drills holes into [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] — and into his mother's face. Each scene roots the novel's metaphysical concerns in the specific, claustrophobic reality of the farmhouse interior.
