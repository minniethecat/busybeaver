# Vardaman's Bananas

The bananas that [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] [[family-duty|promise]]s to buy for her younger brother [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] constitute one of the most quietly devastating recurring motifs in William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_. First mentioned as a [[family-duty|promise]] in the novel's early chapters, they reappear at critical junctures until the final scene, where [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] is seen eating bananas — the [[family-duty|promise]]d consolation delivered, but rendered hollow by everything that has happened in between.

## The Promise

The bananas are introduced in chapter 15, in [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s first narrated chapter, as [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s attempt to manage her brother's grief over their [[addies-death|mother's death]]:

> Dewey Dell said we will get some bananas. The train is behind the glass, red on the track. When it runs the track shines on and off. [015-003]

The sentence [[anse-bundren|pa]]irs the [[family-duty|promise]] of bananas with the image of the toy t[[water-and-river|rain]] [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] desires but cannot have — the real object of his longing immediately displaced onto the edible substitute. [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s [[family-duty|promise]] is a [[motherhood|maternal]] gesture from a sister who is herself a child, and it establishes the [[anse-bundren|pa]]ttern that will govern [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s experience of the [[the-journey|journey]]: consolation offered, deferred, and hollowed out by accumulating losses.

## The Repeated Promise

The bananas recur as [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s management strategy for [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] throughout the [[the-journey|journey]]. In chapter 33, [[vernon-tull|Tull]] observes the family dynamic with characteristic detachment:

> "...and all just to eat a sack of bananas. You ought to laid over a day and rested." [033-015]

The phra[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g reduces the entire [[the-journey|journey]] to a banana run — a misreading that is also, in its way, accurate. [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s grief has been channeled entirely into the [[family-duty|promise]] of bananas, and the [[family-duty|promise]] has become the mechanism by which he is kept quiet through the [[the-journey|journey]]'s disasters.

In the [[jefferson|Jefferson]] chapters (ch056), [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s interior monologue circles obsessively around the bananas as an alternative to thinking about [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s committal:

> Wouldn't you rather have bananas? Dewey Dell said. You wait till Christmas. It'll be there then. Then you can see it. So we are going to have some bananas. We are going to have a bag full, me and Dewey Dell. [056-015]

The bananas and [[darl-bundren|Darl]] are braided together in [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s consciousness — the [[family-duty|promise]]d sweetness and the brother who has been taken away. "[[darl-bundren|Darl]] he went to Jackson my brother [[darl-bundren|Darl]]" alternates with "we are going to have some bananas," as if the one might cancel the other.

## Fulfillment

In the novel's final chapter (ch059), [[cash-bundren|Cash]] describes the scene as [[anse-bundren|Anse]] introduces his new wife:

> Dewey Dell and Vardaman eating bananas, when we see them coming up the street. [059-020]

The image is precise and devastating. The bananas have been bought — [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] kept her [[family-duty|promise]] — but they are being eaten in the moment of their father's remarriage, [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s institutionalization, and the family's final dissolution. The bananas, purchased with the ten dollars [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] fought to protect from [[anse-bundren|Anse]], are the [[the-journey|journey]]'s hollow consolation: the thing [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] wanted, delivered at the moment when it no longer means anything.
