# Time

Time in *As I Lay Dying* does not move forward like a clock. Faulkner fractures chronological time into fifty-nine subjective monologues spread across multiple narrators, creating a temporal experience that is closer to memory than to history. The journey to Jefferson takes roughly nine days, but the novel's internal time stretches backward into childhood, forward into prophecy, and outward into the timeless space of consciousness.

## Overview

The novel's treatment of time is central to its modernist technique. Darl Bundren, the novel's most philosophical narrator, perceives time as a fluid medium: past, present, and future collapse into a single field of awareness. His ability to narrate events he does not witness — most famously Addie's death while he is miles away with Jewel — suggests a consciousness that transcends temporal limitation. Addie Bundren, in her single posthumous chapter, collapses the distinction between living and dead time, narrating from beyond the grave as though death were merely another temporal register.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Darl: Temporal Omniscience

Darl's narration defies chronological constraint. He describes Addie's death (012-001) while hauling lumber with Jewel, as though physical distance and temporal sequence were irrelevant to his perception. His language is saturated with temporal markers — "yet," "still," "already" — that work against the simple forward motion of the journey plot, creating instead a sense of layered time in which everything happens at once.

### Through Addie: Time Beyond Death

Addie's single chapter is the novel's most radical temporal experiment. She speaks from death, recounting her life — her father's nihilism, her marriage to Anse, her affair with Whitfield, her revenge — in a voice that exists outside clock time. Her father taught her "that the reason for living was to get ready to stay dead a long time" (040-002). She later understands that he "could not have known what he meant himself" (040-028), erasing the boundary between living and dead time and making death not an ending but a continuation of consciousness in another form.

### Through Vardaman: Childhood Temporality

Vardaman experiences time with a child's refusal of sequence. His mother's death and the fish he caught become simultaneous events in his mind: "My mother is a fish" (019-001). For Vardaman, temporal order is not yet learned; events exist in a present tense that absorbs everything — grief, confusion, the smell of the coffin, the motion of the wagon — without hierarchy or progression.
