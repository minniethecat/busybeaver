# Moseley

The druggist in [[mottson|Mottson]] who narrates chapter 49 of *As I Lay Dying*. Moseley's single chapter records his encounter with [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], who enters his drugstore seeking a remedy for her pregnancy — what she calls "the female trouble." Moseley's refusal to help her, grounded in professional ethics and religious [[sin-and-judgment|morality]], makes him one of the novel's most morally complex minor characters: he is both principled and, in his own way, cruel.

## Role in the Novel

Moseley represents institutional authority — the law, professional medicine, and religious convention — encountering the Bundrens' private desperation. Where [[skeet-macgowan|MacGowan]] exploits [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] by posing as a doctor, Moseley refuses even to engage. His chapter is a study in the failure of [[morality|moral]] systems to address human need: he lectures [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] about the law and God, but offers her nothing — no help, no alternative, not even [[compassion]].

His presence also extends the novel's geographic and social range. As a [[mottson|Mottson]] businessman — not a farmer, not a Bundren neighbor — Moseley provides an urban, middle-class perspective on the Bundrens' [[the-journey|journey]]. He sees [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] not as a person in crisis but as a social problem: a country girl who should have talked to "her ma" and whose request threatens his business.

## Biography

Moseley is the proprietor of a drugstore in [[mottson|Mottson]], the town the Bundrens pass through on their [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. He stocks "female dope" — abortifacient remedies — because "a man's got to stock it or go out of business in this country," but he regards the trade with contempt, noting that it is "a shame, the way they poison themselves with it" [049-015].

He is a man of conventional piety and professional caution. When [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] asks for the remedy, Moseley's response is to invoke the law ("it's against the law") and God ("it's for your own good"), using institutional authority to shield himself from the human consequences of his refusal.

## Narrative Voice

Moseley narrates chapter 49 in a voice that is colloquial, self-assured, and implicitly judgmental. His narration reveals a man accustomed to authority — the authority of the shopkeeper over the customer, the professional over the layperson, the man over the woman. His observation that [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] is "pretty in a kind of sullen, awkward way" [049-004] betrays a proprietary male gaze that undercuts his moral posturing.

His language is the language of rationalization. He tells himself he is acting in [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s interest: "it's for your own good" [049-065]. But the chapter's dramatic irony is unmistakable — the reader knows [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s situation, knows what awaits her in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] at the hands of [[skeet-macgowan|MacGowan]], and recognizes Moseley's refusal not as protection but as abandonment.

## Key Relationships

**[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell Bundren]]**: Moseley's sole interaction is with [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], who enters his store carrying a package ([[corabelle-tull|Cora]]'s failed cakes) and asks — inarticulately, obliquely — for help. Moseley recognizes she is "in trouble of some sort" [049-020] but interprets her silence as shame rather than as the communication difficulty of someone who has never been taught the language for what she needs.

His refusal is delivered as a sermon: he tells her the medicine is "a poison" and that "no girl in trouble would come to a strange town, I don't care who she is" — a statement that rewrites [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s desperate journey as a moral failing.

**[[skeet-macgowan|MacGowan]]** (counterpart): Moseley and [[skeet-macgowan|MacGowan]] represent opposite failures of the male professional world. [[skeet-macgowan|MacGowan]] gives [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] what she asks for — but as a predatory deception. Moseley refuses what she asks for — out of principle. Between the predator and the moralist, [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] finds no genuine help anywhere.

## Symbolic Significance

Moseley embodies the gap between institutional morality and human compassion. His professional ethics, his religious convictions, and his respect for the law are all genuine, yet they produce an outcome indistinguishable from cruelty: a seventeen-year-old girl, pregnant and terrified, is sent away with nothing but a lecture. Faulkner's critique is not that Moseley is a hypocrite — he is not — but that his moral system has no provision for the actual suffering in front of him.
