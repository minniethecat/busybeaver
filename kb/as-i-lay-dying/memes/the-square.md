# The Jefferson Square

The town square of Jefferson, Mississippi, is the spatial and symbolic terminus of the Bundrens' journey. After miles of dirt roads, swollen rivers, and rural farmsteads, the family arrives in the county seat, where the square — with its courthouse, stores, and gathered community — represents everything the Bundren farm is not: urban, commercial, legalistic, and judgmental. The square is where the journey's meaning is finally adjudicated — where Addie is buried, where Anse acquires new teeth and a new wife, and where Darl is taken away to the asylum at Jackson.

## Description

The Jefferson square is the center of the fictional Yoknapatawpha County's seat. It is dominated by the courthouse, around which are arranged stores, pharmacies, and the other institutions of rural Southern civic life. For the Bundrens, who have spent their lives on an isolated hill-country farm, the square is an alien environment — a space governed by laws and customs they understand imperfectly and navigate with difficulty. Faulkner's rendering of the square emphasizes its difference from the rural landscape: the hard surfaces, the gathered people, the sense of being watched and judged by a community that has no stake in the family's private obligations.

## Role in the Novel

The square serves as the stage for the novel's denouement. It is here that the family's journey, which began as a private act of devotion to Addie's dying wish, becomes a public spectacle. The square is where the tension between the Bundrens' internal logic — their sense of obligation, their family dynamics, their private grief — and the external logic of the community reaches its breaking point. The square represents the public sphere that the Bundrens cannot finally avoid, the social world that demands an accounting.

## Key Scenes

### Arrival in Jefferson

The family's arrival in the square marks the end of the physical journey. The wagon, battered and odorous, enters a space of paved streets and town buildings, and the contrast between the rural road and the urban square crystallizes the journey's transformation from a private pilgrimage into a public scandal. The townspeople's reactions — staring, commenting, judging — confirm what the family has sensed all along: that their journey, however sacred its intention, appears grotesque to those outside their circle.

### The Courthouse and the Pharmacy

The square hosts the novel's final commercial transactions. Anse visits the courthouse and the stores; Dewey Dell goes to the pharmacy for the abortion medication she will not receive; the family's various errands draw them into the square's commercial and legal spaces. These transactions — some successful, some failed — represent the family's encounter with the institutional structures that govern town life, structures as foreign to them as the river was familiar.

### Anse's Transformation

The square is where Anse Bundren completes his transformation from luckless farmer to remade man. He acquires his new false teeth from a dentist near the square, and he returns to the family with a new wife — the "duck-shaped woman" who has been waiting for him. The square thus becomes the site of Anse's grotesque self-renewal, a transaction as shocking in its way as any of the journey's disasters. The family who entered the square to bury Addie leaves it with a new Mrs. Bundren, the journey's final and most bitter irony.

## Critical Interpretation

The Jefferson square in Faulkner's work is a recurring setting that embodies the tension between rural and urban, private and public, traditional and modern. In *As I Lay Dying*, the square represents the world of law, commerce, and social convention that the Bundrens have spent the novel avoiding and that finally catches up with them. It is a space of judgment — legal, economic, and moral — and the family's various fates are sealed within its perimeter.
