# Suffering

Suffering in *As I Lay Dying* is not a single event but a pervasive condition — physical, emotional, and existential — that defines the Bundren family's experience from Addie's prolonged deathbed vigil through the catastrophic river crossing to the final humiliations of the journey's end.

## Overview

The novel charts suffering across multiple registers: the slow physical deterioration of Addie Bundren, the acute trauma of Cash's broken leg, Dewey Dell's silent anguish over her pregnancy, and Darl's progressive psychological collapse. Anse Bundren frames his family's ordeal in self-pitying terms — "ere a man in the living world suffered the trials and floutings I have suffered" (043-043) — yet his complaints are undercut by his own complicity in prolonging the family's misery. Faulkner presents suffering not as ennobling but as a brute fact of existence, testing the limits of human endurance without offering transcendence.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Cash Bundren: Physical Suffering

Cash Bundren's broken leg is the novel's most sustained depiction of physical suffering. After the river crossing, his leg is fractured and encased in a cement cast by his own family — a crude remedy that causes excruciating pain and threatens to cost him the leg entirely:

> I mix the cement in the can, stirring the slow water into the pale-green thick coils. I bring the can to the wagon where Cash can see. He lies on his back, his thin profile in silhouette, ascetic and profound against the sky. "Does that look about right?" I say. [046-016]

The clinical precision with which the cement-cast scene is narrated — Anse mixing the cement, Dewey Dell tearing paper to wipe the excess, the "pale green slow surges" of cement among the splints — creates a horror that is both visceral and understated. Cash endures with stoic silence: "It don't bother to speak of" (046-014). His suffering is compounded by the family's continued journey; every jolt of the wagon over rough roads is an assault on his broken body. Cash's physical pain is rendered with clinical precision, yet his refusal to complain transforms it into a study of endurance stripped of heroism.

> We replace the splints, the cords, drawing them tight, the cement in thick pale green slow surges among the cords, Cash watching us quietly with that profound questioning look. [046-031]

### Addie Bundren: The Prolonged Death

Addie Bundren's death is not sudden but a drawn-out process that the family watches from her bedroom. Her suffering before death is described indirectly through the observations of others — the sound of the adze as Cash builds her coffin within her hearing, the fan Dewey Dell uses, the visitors who come to pay respects. Cora Tull's religious framing — speaking of suffering as a token of God's love — is contradicted by the brute physical reality of Addie's decline. The moment of death is rendered through the child Vardaman's bewildered consciousness: "Addie Bundren is dead" (012-023). The novel refuses to sentimentalize her death, presenting it instead as a slow extinguishing that the family endures with a mixture of grief, impatience, and incomprehension.

### Anse Bundren: Self-Pity as Performance

Anse Bundren's relationship to suffering is fundamentally theatrical. He proclaims his trials with a rhetorical grandeur that belies their self-serving nature:

> "You all don't know," pa says. "The somebody you was young with and you growed old in her and she growed old in you, seeing the old coming on and it was the one somebody you could hear say it don't matter and know it was the truth outen the hard world and all a man's grief and trials." [043-043]

His refrain — "ere a man in the living world suffered the trials and floutings I have suffered" (043-043) — appropriates the family's collective suffering as his personal grievance. Yet Anse is the cause of much of the suffering he laments: his insistence on the journey to Jefferson, his refusal to seek proper medical care for Cash, his exploitation of his children's labor and loyalty. Faulkner uses Anse's self-pity to expose the gap between real suffering and its rhetorical appropriation.

### Darl Bundren: Psychological Unraveling

Darl's suffering is psychological rather than physical — a progressive dissolution of self that culminates in his committal to the state asylum at Jackson. His clairvoyant knowledge distances him from the embodied experience of the other characters, yet this very distance becomes a form of torment. His decision to burn Gillespie's barn — an attempt to end his mother's grotesque posthumous journey — reveals a mind that can no longer bear what it perceives. By the novel's end, Darl speaks of himself in the third person, his identity having fractured under the weight of consciousness itself: his suffering is the suffering of one who sees too much.
