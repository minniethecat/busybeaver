# Skeet MacGowan

The soda-fountain clerk at the Jefferson drugstore who exploits [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s desperation. MacGowan appears in a single chapter [058], yet his interaction with [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] crystallizes one of the novel's darkest themes: the vulnerability of women in a world of predatory men who exploit their isolation and need.

## Role in the Novel

MacGowan functions as a minor antagonist whose casual cruelty illustrates the moral indifference the Bundrens encounter beyond their own community. Unlike the religious [[sin-and-judgment|judgment]] of [[corabelle-tull|Cora]] or the paternalistic refusal of [[moseley|Moseley]], MacGowan's response to [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] is purely exploitative: he recognizes her desperation, deceives her into believing he is a doctor, and takes sexual advantage of her under the pretense of providing medical treatment.

His chapter — narrated in his own voice — reveals a young man who treats deception as a game, self-consciously adopting the role of "doctor" as a performance. The narrative's use of first-person for such a minor character underscores Faulkner's interest in giving voice to every participant in the novel's chain of exploitation.

## Biography

Skeet MacGowan works behind the prescription counter at the Jefferson drugstore, a position that grants him access to the pharmacy's medicines and a veneer of medical authority he is quick to exploit. He is young — [[jody|Jody]] calls him by his nickname "Skeet" — and his dialogue reveals a sardonic awareness of the ethical boundaries he is crossing: when [[jody|Jody]] asks what he is doing with [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], MacGowan replies, "I can't tell you. It wouldn't be ethical" [058-012].

His pretense of professionalism masks a crude opportunism. He tells [[jody|Jody]] he is "filling a prescription" [058-015] when he is, in fact, preparing to sexually exploit a desperate young woman.

## Narrative Voice

MacGowan narrates chapter 58, the only chapter in the novel told from the perspective of a Jefferson townsperson outside the Bundrens' rural world. His narration is notable for its colloquial, confessional tone — he addresses the reader as if sharing a story among acquaintances — and for its complete absence of moral self-awareness. He describes his deception of [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] with a casualness that is more damning than any explicit condemnation would be.

His language is the language of the marketplace: "ten dollars" [058-020], "my knowledge and skill" [058-022], "no little paltry sawbuck" [058-024] — economic terms that reduce [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s medical and emotional crisis to a [[commerce-and-exchange|transaction]].

## Key Relationships

**[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell Bundren]]**: MacGowan's sole function in the novel is to victimize [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]. She comes to the drugstore seeking a doctor who will terminate her pregnancy; MacGowan, recognizing her desperation, poses as a physician and prescribes a sham treatment, extracting both money and sexual compliance. The scene is among the novel's most troubling depictions of female vulnerability and male predation.

**[[jody|Jody]]**: MacGowan's coworker, who acts as lookout and accomplice. [[jody|Jody]] alerts MacGowan to [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s presence and later questions what MacGowan is doing, but [[jody|Jody]]'s complicity is established by his decision to "kind of watch out and whistle if the old man comes" [058-009].

## Symbolic Significance

MacGowan embodies the corruption that awaits the Bundrens in town. If the rural world of Frenchman's Bend is defined by [[family-duty|family obligation]] and [[neighborly-duty|neighborly duty]] — however imperfectly observed — [[jefferson|Jefferson]] represents a [[commerce-and-exchange|commercial]] world where human needs are reduced to transactions, and the vulnerable are preyed upon without consequence. MacGowan, with his counterfeit medical authority and his glib talk of "ethics," is the urban counterpart to the novel's many forms of rural failure.
