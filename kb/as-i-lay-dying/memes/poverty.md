# Poverty

Poverty in *As I Lay Dying* is not a theme to be discussed but a condition to be endured. The Bundren family's material circumstances — their "broke-down wagon" (005-017), their reliance on neighborly charity, Anse's obsessive accounting of costs — form the unglamorous substrate of every action in the novel. Faulkner refuses to romanticize rural poverty or treat it as ennobling; instead, he presents it as a grinding fact that shapes character, limits possibility, and exposes the gap between what people need and what they can afford.

> "You can't get two dollars a piece for them," Kate says. [002-006]

Kate Tull's blunt assessment of Cora's cakes — the price, the buyer, the transaction — introduces the economic calculus that governs the novel's world before the journey even begins.

## Overview

The novel opens in a world defined by material scarcity. The Bundrens farm cotton on land that may not be theirs, travel in a wagon held together by borrowed parts, and depend on neighbors like Vernon Tull for the labor and resources they cannot provide themselves. When Addie dies, Anse's first concern is not grief but the cost of the coffin and the journey. His refrain throughout the novel — the need for "teeth" and the cost of "three dollars" for the shirt (005-004) — reduces every human transaction to its economic dimension.

Kate Tull's observation establishes the novel's class framework explicitly: "those rich town ladies can change their minds. Poor folks can't" (002-004). Poverty in Yoknapatawpha County is not just economic but social — it determines who matters, whose grief is respected, and whose body is worth burying properly.

> "It means three dollars," I say. The shirt across pa's hump is faded lighter than the rest of it. There is no sweat stain on his shirt. I have never seen a sweat stain on his shirt. [005-004]

Dewey Dell's report of the shirt's cost collapses economic and physical description into a single observation — the three dollars, the faded hump, the absent sweat — as if money, labor, and identity were three names for the same thing.

## The Economics of Death

The journey to Jefferson is itself an economic transaction. Anse calculates the cost of every step — the lost days of farming, the price of the new team of mules (bought with Jewel's horse, itself bought with Jewel's labor), the tools Cash must replace. Death, in the Bundren economy, is expensive. The coffin Cash builds so carefully is an object of labor but also of cost — every board and nail represents resources the family cannot spare.

> I could have used the money real well. But it's not like I had anything to do with it. [002-015]

Anse's characteristic deflection — acknowledging the utility of money while disclaiming responsibility for its absence — reveals poverty as a condition he regards as something that happens to him, not something he acts upon.

## Anse and the Rhetoric of Poverty

Anse Bundren uses poverty as both identity and excuse. He frames every obligation as an imposition on his meager resources, every request for help as an appeal to his status as a "put-upon" man. His claim that "it ain't that I am afraid of work; I always have fed me and mine" (009-004) is undercut by the evidence of the novel — he has fed his family poorly, both literally and metaphorically. Anse's poverty is real, but his response to it — passivity, complaint, self-justification — is a choice.

> "Plain turnip greens is mighty spindling eating for a man my size," he says. [014-015]

Anse's complaint about the meal — delivered while Cash eats without comment, his sweat-soaked shirt recording the labor Anse refuses — captures the paradox of his position: he demands the provision due to a patriarch but accepts none of the work that provision requires.

## The Social Meaning of Scarcity

Cora Tull's cakes — baked for "the ladies" who can afford them, sold at two dollars apiece — mark the boundary between the Bundrens' world and the world of "rich town ladies." The cakes represent the economy of women, a domestic economy of baking and selling that operates alongside the farming economy of men. When Dewey Dell attempts her own economic transaction in Jefferson — trading her body for an abortion remedy — the novel reveals how poverty and gender intersect: a poor woman's body is currency, and the transaction can go badly wrong.

## Poverty and Dignity

The novel refuses the easy consolation that poverty confers dignity. Cash works with patient care, but his labor is exploited — first by Anse, later by the family that employs him after the journey. Jewel sacrifices his horse, his most valued possession, and receives nothing in return. The Bundrens arrive in Jefferson not ennobled by their suffering but diminished by it — wet, bruised, bandaged, and, in the final irony, greeted by Anse's new wife, purchased with money borrowed against the children's future labor.

> "He's a close man to trade with," he says, mumbling his words to himself. [043-004]

The final economic transaction — trading with Snopes for the team, haggling over the last of the family's resources — reduces even the sacred obligation to bury Addie to a matter of price. In the novel's world, nothing escapes the mathematics of scarcity.
