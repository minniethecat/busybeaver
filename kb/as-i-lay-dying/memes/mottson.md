# Mottson

Mottson is a small [[jefferson|town]] encountered along the Bundren family's [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] in William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_. It appears in the novel primarily as a site of failed transaction — a place where the family attempts to secure cement for [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s [[cashs-broken-leg|broken leg]] and sell [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s horse team — and as the location of Moseley's drugstore, where [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] makes her first, unsuccessful attempt to obtain an abortion.

## Description

Mottson is never described in physical detail, but its character emerges through the commercial and medical transactions that occur there. It has a drugstore (Moseley's), a marshal, and a hardware store where [[anse-bundren|Anse]] attempts to buy cement. The [[jefferson|town]] is sufficiently developed to have civic authority (the marshal intervenes when [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s condition is discovered), but remains a rural waypoint — not the destination, but a stop along the way.

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s dry assessment of the Snopes horse team captures the [[jefferson|town]]'s marginal character in a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle line: "You better try to sell them in Mottson" [044-032]. The implication is that Mottson is a place where one might unload defective goods — a market of last resort.

## Role in the Novel

Mottson marks a critical pivot in the [[the-journey|journey]]'s trajectory from grim comedy toward genuine crisis. Three overlapping disasters converge in the [[jefferson|town]]:

1. **[[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]]** has been broken in [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] and crudely set with cement. The family needs medicine but cannot afford a real doctor [044-010]

2. **[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]** attempts to obtain an abortion at Moseley's drugstore, where she encounters not the predatory Skeet Macgowan (that comes later, in [[jefferson|Jefferson]]) but Moseley himself, who refuses her [ch045]

3. **[[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s stinginess** and the community's reaction to it give Mottson its narrative texture: the marshal threatens [[anse-bundren|Anse]] for endangering [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s life, and [[anse-bundren|Anse]] responds with his characteristic litany of non-[[family-duty|obligation]]: "I wouldn't be beholden, God knows" [044-028]

Mottson thus functions as the [[the-journey|journey]]'s crisis point — the place where the cumulative damage of [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]], the decaying corpse, and the family's dysfunction becomes publicly visible.

## Key Scenes

### The Cement and the Marshal (ch044)

[[anse-bundren|Anse]] attempts to purchase cement at a Mottson hardware store for [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]]. The storekeeper and [[jefferson|town]] marshal, upon discovering [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s condition — a leg "turned black" and crudely set with cement — are horrified. The marshal threatens [[anse-bundren|Anse]]: "You'll cause him to lose his leg. You take him on to a doctor, and you get this thing buried soon as you can" [044-028]. [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s response — a rambling account of the [[the-washed-out-bridge|washed-out bridge]]s and his [[family-duty|promise]] to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] — registers as both evasion and a kind of rural fatalism that the [[jefferson|town]] finds incomprehensible.

### Dewey Dell at Moseley's Drugstore (ch045)

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] enters Moseley's drugstore seeking the same remedy she will later seek from Macgowan in [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. Unlike Macgowan, Moseley is a legitimate pharmacist who refuses to provide the re[[the-journey|quest]]ed "medicine," recognizing its intended purpose. The scene at Moseley's is one of the novel's most rest[[water-and-river|rain]]ed treatments of [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s desperation: she speaks little, offers her ten dollars, and is turned away without drama — but also without help. "I got the money," she says [044-010], the same phrase she will repeat in [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. Mottson marks the first failure of that transactional logic.
