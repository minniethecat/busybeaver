# Pride

In *As I Lay Dying*, pride is the central psychological d[[water-and-river|river]] of character action — [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]'s fierce self-containment, [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s stubbornness, and the family's refusal to accept help all trace to pride's complex operations in the human heart.

## Overview

Faulkner presents pride not as simple vanity but as an existential stance — a way of holding oneself a[[anse-bundren|pa]]rt from others that is simultaneously defensive and destructive. [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]'s pride is repeatedly characterized as a form of concealment: [[cora-tull|Cora]] describes her as "a lonely woman, lonely with her pride, trying to make folks believe she was different from what she was" (006-003). This formulation is crucial: pride creates isolation, and that isolation is itself a form of suffering that pride refuses to acknowledge. [[peabody|Doctor Peabody]], arriving too late to treat [[addie-bundren|Addie]], reflects on "that pride, that furious desire to hide that abject nakedness which we bring here with us" (011-028), linking pride to the universal human condition of vulnerability.

## Manifestations in the Text

Pride operates differently for different characters, but in every case it functions as both armor and wound.

### Through Addie Bundren: Pride as Self-Concealment

[[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s pride is the novel's most developed instance of the theme. She dies "alone, hiding her pride and her broken heart" (006-011), and her posthumous narration reveals a woman who built her identity around refu[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g to need others. Her affair with [[whitfield|Whitfield]], her [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtiality toward [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], and her emotional distance from [[darl-bundren|Darl]] all trace to a pride that cannot bear to ask for what it wants. She describes herself as one "lost in her vanity and her pride, that had closed her heart" (039-006) — the self-awareness of this confession makes it more tragic, not less. [[addie-bundren|Addie]] knows what pride costs her and cannot relinquish it.

### Through Anse Bundren: Pride as Stubbornness

[[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s pride manifests as an immovable refusal to adapt. His insistence on fulfilling his [[family-duty|promise]] to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] despite every obstacle — [[the-river-crossing-location|the flooded river]], the rotting corpse, the [[cashs-broken-leg|broken leg]] — can be read as either [[love|devotion]] or pride too stubborn to admit error. The ambiguity is deliberate: Faulkner refuses to distinguish between noble persistence and prideful obstinacy, suggesting they may be the same thing viewed from different angles.

### Through Jewel Bundren: Pride as Physical Action

[[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s pride is embodied rather than articulated — it expresses itself through his violent physicality, his furious determination to get his [[motherhood|mother]]'s [[the-body|body]] across [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]], and his silent refusal to accept help. Unlike [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s interiorized pride or [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s verbal stubbornness, [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s pride is pure action, making it the most formidable and the least self-aware form of the trait.
