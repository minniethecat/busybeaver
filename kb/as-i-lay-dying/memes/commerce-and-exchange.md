# Commerce and Exchange

The logic of [[commerce-and-exchange|commerce]] — buying, selling, trading, and bargaining — pervades *[[0120-as-i-lay-dying-fedb9fe3|As I Lay Dying]]* as a governing metaphor for human relationships. From [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s relentless pursuit of bargains to [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s catastrophic attempt to [[commerce-and-exchange|purchase]] a medical remedy, economic [[commerce-and-exchange|exchange]] shapes the Bundrens' world as fundamentally as [[the-journey|the journey]] itself. Faulkner presents a rural economy in which everything — labor, [[the-body|bodies]], [[family-duty|family obligation]], even [[death-and-dying|death]] — can be reduced to a [[commerce-and-exchange|transaction]].

## Overview

The novel is structured around a series of failed or exploitative exchanges. [[anse-bundren|Anse]] trades a team of mules; [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] attempts to buy an abortion; [[corabelle-tull|Cora]] sells cakes in [[mottson|Mottson]]; the family's [[the-journey|journey]] itself is undertaken to fulfill [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s promise — a form of moral [[commerce-and-exchange|debt]] that must be honored. In each case, the logic of the marketplace produces outcomes that are absurd, cruel, or both.

Faulkner's treatment of commerce draws on the Southern agrarian tradition's suspicion of market relations, but complicates it: the Bundrens are not noble yeomen resisting [[commerce-and-exchange|commerce]] but participants in it, however badly they fare. [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s problem is not that he engages in trade but that he is terrible at it — or, alternatively, that he is so good at extracting value from others that he has alienated everyone around him.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Anse Bundren

[[anse-bundren|Anse]] is the novel's central figure of [[commerce-and-exchange|economic]] calculation. His response to every crisis is to identify a bargain. When the [[the-mule-team|mule team]] drowns in [[the-river-crossing-event|the river]], [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s solution is to trade — [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s [[jewels-horse|horse]] for a new team, securing what he needs by leveraging his son's most prized possession. His decision to borrow [[kate|Kate]]'s egg money rather than use his own, his negotiation for [[anses-teeth|new teeth]] in [[jefferson|Jefferson]], and his remarriage within hours of burying [[addie-bundren|Addie]] all reveal a man for whom every relationship is a [[commerce-and-exchange|balance sheet]].

The mule trade itself is rehearsed in economic language: "worth forty dollars. How far do you aim to get with a forty-dollar team?" [027-010]. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s silent fury — he never consents to the trade — exposes the gap between [[commerce-and-exchange|commercial]] logic and personal attachment.

### Through Dewey Dell

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s encounter with [[skeet-macgowan|MacGowan]] in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] represents the novel's most brutal [[commerce-and-exchange|transaction]]. Seeking a doctor to terminate her pregnancy, [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] instead finds a soda-fountain clerk who poses as a physician, takes her money, and sexually exploits her. The episode literalizes the metaphor of the body as commodity: [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] attempts to purchase control over her own body and is instead doubly exploited — financially and sexually.

MacGowan's language is explicitly [[commerce-and-exchange|commercial]]: "ten dollars" [058-020], "my knowledge and skill" [058-022], "no little paltry sawbuck" [058-024]. His pretense of providing a service masks the reality of theft.

### Through Cora and the Cakes

[[corabelle-tull|Cora]]'s cake episode offers a gentler but no less pointed critique of [[commerce-and-exchange|market logic]]. She bakes cakes to sell in [[mottson|Mottson]], expecting to earn money for the family. Instead, the townswomen refuse to buy from a country woman, and the cakes become objects of charity rather than [[commerce-and-exchange|commerce]] — a humiliation that exposes the gap between [[corabelle-tull|Cora]]'s self-image as an entrepreneur and the town's perception of her as an object of pity.

[[corabelle-tull|Cora]]'s reflection on the exchange reveals her internal conflict: she "could have used the money real well" but consoles herself that the cakes "didn't cost me anything" — a self-deception that masks the real [[labor|labor]] of egg-gathering, baking, and transporting that went into the failed enterprise.

### Through the Journey as Debt

The [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] is itself framed as a [[commerce-and-exchange|transaction]]: [[addie-bundren|Addie]] made [[anse-bundren|Anse]] promise to bury her in [[jefferson|Jefferson]], and [[anse-bundren|Anse]] treats this promise as a [[commerce-and-exchange|debt]] that must be discharged — not out of love or [[family-duty|duty]] but because "I give her my promised word" [020-038]. The journey's human cost — [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s [[cashs-broken-leg|leg]], [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s [[jewels-horse|horse]], [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s [[madness-and-sanity|sanity]] — is balanced against a promise that [[anse-bundren|Anse]] treats as a [[commerce-and-exchange|contractual]] obligation.

## Critical Interpretation

The novel's economic dimension has drawn attention from Marxist and New Historicist critics who read the Bundrens' [[the-journey|journey]] as an allegory of the rural South's traumatic integration into [[commerce-and-exchange|market]] capitalism. The Bundrens are not pre-modern peasants but failed participants in a [[commerce-and-exchange|commercial]] order they cannot master and cannot escape.

> Faulkner's poor whites are not defined by their distance from the market but by their disastrous proximity to it — they buy, sell, borrow, and trade with a desperation that reveals not economic independence but its opposite. Anse Bundren is a parody of economic man: he calculates endlessly, and every calculation is wrong. — (critical perspective, Faulkner studies)

The contrast between rural [[commerce-and-exchange|barter]] and urban [[commerce-and-exchange|cash]] economy — between trading a horse for mules in the countryside and paying ten dollars for a fake remedy in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] — maps the Bundrens' physical [[the-journey|journey]] onto an economic one: from an economy of [[family-and-kinship|kinship]] and obligation to one of anonymous, predatory exchange.
