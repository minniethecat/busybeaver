# The Saw

Cash Bundren's saw is the defining sound of the novel's opening. Before any character speaks of death, duty, or the journey to come, the reader hears the saw: a rhythmic, persistent noise that marks the building of Addie Bundren's coffin while she lies dying in the adjoining room. The saw is both a practical tool — part of Cash's carpenter's kit — and an acoustic metaphor for the novel's central preoccupation: the intersection of craft and mortality, labor and loss.

## Description

The saw is one of Cash's carpentry tools, used alongside the adze, hammer, plane, and chisel. It is the tool that produces the most distinctive and persistent sound: the back-and-forth rasp of wood being cut to shape. Darl's narration registers the saw's sound repeatedly in the opening chapters: "beginning to hear Cash's saw" [001-004], "We can hear the saw in the board. It sounds like snoring" [002-013]. The saw is heard before Cash is seen; his identity is established through the sound of his labor. Faulkner's description emphasizes the sensory quality of the sound — it is not merely noise but a presence that fills the household's acoustic space, as inescapable as the fact of Addie's approaching death.

## Symbolic Significance

The saw occupies a complex symbolic position in the novel. On one level, it represents Cash's form of mourning: while others grieve through words, prayer, or silent endurance, Cash grieves through carpentry. The saw's sound is his elegy, his way of confronting the imminent death by building the object that will contain it. The sawing is an act of love made audible.

On another level, the saw represents the tension between the material and the emotional that runs throughout the novel. Jewel reacts to the saw's sound with fury — "hammering and sawing on that goddamn box" [004-001] — seeing in Cash's methodical labor an obscene disregard for the dying woman's comfort. Yet Cash's sawing is not indifference but its opposite: a mode of care that expresses itself through craft rather than through conventional emotional display. The family's division over the meaning of the saw's sound — is it devotion or desecration? — prefigures the broader thematic conflict between different modes of perceiving and responding to death.

The saw also ties into the novel's treatment of labor as identity. Cash is defined by his tools and his craft; the saw is an extension of his being, just as the coffin he builds is an extension of his relationship to Addie. The carpenter's precision — the bevel, the balanced board, the exact fit — becomes a moral quality, a form of integrity that distinguishes Cash from the novel's more verbally expressive but less reliable characters.

## Appearances in the Text

### First Appearance

The saw is first heard in the novel's opening paragraph: Darl walks up from the spring and begins to hear "Cash's saw" before he sees his brother [001-004]. The sound establishes the novel's acoustic landscape and the temporal pressure of Addie's dying.

### Key Appearances

Throughout the first four chapters, the saw's sound recurs as a constant background. It is heard by Darl [002-013], by Cora [006-001], and by Jewel, who reacts to it with mounting anger [004-001]. In chapter 2, the sound is described with Darl's characteristic perceptual precision: "It sounds like snoring," a simile that links the saw's rhythm to the rhythm of breath — and, by implication, to the breath that is leaving Addie's body. The saw thus becomes a kind of counterpoint to the dying woman's respiration: one sound of life fading, another of labor continuing.

### Final Appearance

By the time the coffin is complete, the saw falls silent. The coffin is finished in the early chapters, and the saw does not reappear; its work is done. The silence that replaces the saw's sound marks a transition: from the static, waiting period of Addie's dying to the active, mobile period of the journey.

## Critical Interpretation

Critics have noted the saw's function as what Joseph Reed called "acoustic metonymy" — a sound that stands for the larger process of which it is a part. The saw is the sound of craft, of care made material, of the body's labor in the face of the body's dissolution. It also participates in the novel's broader modernist interest in the perceptual: Faulkner renders the world through what his characters see, hear, and smell, and the saw is one of the most memorable of these sensory anchors.
