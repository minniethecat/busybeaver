# Neighborly Duty

The concept of neighborly duty in *As I Lay Dying* represents the rural social contract by which community members are expected to assist one another in times of hardship, and the ways this obligation is variously honored, strained, and betrayed over the course of the Bundrens' journey.

## Overview

In the rural Mississippi setting of the novel, neighborly duty functions as an unwritten but powerful social code. Characters such as Vernon Tull, Armstid, and Samson offer food, shelter, and labor to the Bundrens, yet Anse Bundren's exploitation of this goodwill — accepting help without reciprocation or gratitude — tests the limits of community obligation. The theme illuminates both the genuine compassion of the rural community and the tension between individual need and collective responsibility. Across the novel's fifty-nine chapters, neighborly duty emerges as one of the few social forces capable of transcending the Bundrens' isolation, yet its limits are starkly revealed by Anse's parasitic relationship to community goodwill.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Vernon Tull

Vernon Tull is the most consistent embodiment of neighborly duty. He helps the Bundrens repeatedly — assisting with the wagon after the river crossing, offering his mules, and providing practical support throughout the journey. When the wagon is damaged and the planks are scattered, Vernon offers without hesitation:

> "If we get in a tight, we could take some of them planks," Vernon says. "I'll help you put them back." [017-020]

Even when Anse's obtuseness strains his patience, Tull continues to help out of a deep sense of obligation. His help is practical rather than sentimental — he understands the actual labor required to keep the Bundrens moving and provides it without fanfare. Significantly, Tull does not help because he respects Anse; he helps despite knowing Anse's deficiencies, suggesting that neighborly duty in this world is impersonal — an obligation to the role of neighbor rather than to the person who occupies it.

### Through Armstid

Armstid provides the Bundrens with shelter for the night, hosting them at his farm despite the smell of Addie's decomposing body. His response to their plight is framed by a recognition of shared humanity that transcends personal judgment:

> Because be durn if there ain't something about a durn fellow like Anse that seems to make a man have to help him, even when he knows he'll be wanting to kick himself next minute. [043-067]

Armstid's help is grudging but genuine — he recognizes Anse's character yet feels compelled by a neighborly code rooted in the shared hardships of rural life. The particular poignancy of his observation lies in its self-awareness: he knows helping Anse is a mistake, yet he does it anyway, driven by a moral reflex that overrides rational self-interest.

### Through Samson

Samson offers the Bundrens hospitality earlier in the journey, putting them up at his farm. His reflections on the situation reveal the strain that Anse's conduct places on neighborly obligation. Samson recognizes the absurdity of the journey — transporting a decaying corpse through flood-damaged country — yet feels bound to offer what help he can. His chapter, like those of Tull and Armstid, establishes a pattern: the neighbors see the foolishness and the exploitation, yet continue to help, bound by a code that predates and exceeds the particular case.

### Through Anse Bundren

Anse Bundren's relationship to neighborly duty is fundamentally parasitic. He receives help from nearly every character the family encounters — Tull, Armstid, Samson, the Mottson townspeople — but offers nothing in return. His refrain that others should help him because of his misfortune reveals an expectation of community support untethered from reciprocity.

> "Hit was jest one thing and then another," he says. "That ere corn me and the boys was aimin' to git up with, and Dewey Dell a-takin' good keer of her, and folks comin' in, a-offerin' to help and sich." [011-018]

Anse's account of his troubles is always framed in terms of what others owe him — the corn crop lost, the help accepted as due. His refrain "I just aimed to help him" (051-010), repeated after the disastrous cement-cast incident, reveals a man who mistakes intention for action and expects credit for help he never actually provides. His final betrayal — using the journey to acquire a new wife and a set of false teeth — redefines neighborly help as resource extraction, exposing the community's goodwill as a resource to be mined rather than a relationship to be sustained.
