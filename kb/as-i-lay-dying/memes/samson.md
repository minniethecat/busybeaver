# Samson

A farmer and store-owner who lives roughly eight miles from the Bundren farm, on the route toward [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. Samson's [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle narrative chapter [029] is one of the novel's richest contributions from the community chorus — it combines a devastating psychological portrait of [[anse-bundren|Anse]] with a searing feminist critique voiced by Samson's wife [[rachel|Rachel]], and concludes with the novel's first unambiguous external confirmation that [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s corpse is drawing [[the-buzzards|buzzards]].

## Role in the Novel

Samson is one of several outsider-narrators (with [[vernon-tull|Tull]], [[armstid|Armstid]], Moseley, and Macgowan) whose perspectives frame the Bundren [[the-journey|journey]] as social spectacle. He represents the normative community response to the Bundren catastrophe: practical, charitable within limits, and increa[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gly horrified. His bridge — "Samson's bridge" [020-074] — is a geographical landmark referenced before his chapter, and its destruction by [[the-river-crossing-event|the flood]] sets the stage for the Bundrens' arrival at his farm.

Among the community witnesses, Samson is distinguished by the clarity of his psychological insight. His observation of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s peculiar inertia is one of the novel's most quoted [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssages.

## Narrative Voice

Samson's prose is plainspoken and rural, characterized by dry humor and understated irony. When Quick tells the Bundrens [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]] is out, Samson quips: "They'll have to fly, then. I don't reckon there's ere a bridge between here and Mouth of Ishatawa" [029-010].

His most famous [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage is a sustained analysis of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s psychology:

> "I notice how it takes a lazy man, a man that hates moving, to get set on moving once he does get started off, the same as he was set on staying still, like it ain't the moving he hates so much as the starting and the stopping. And like he would be kind of proud of whatever come up to make the moving or the setting still look hard." [029-016]

This diagnosis — that [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s stubbornness is not will but inertia, and that he takes a perverse pride in obstacles — captures the novel's central [[anse-bundren|pa]]radox: the most [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssive character is also the most immovable.

## Key Relationships

**[[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]**: Samson tries to reason with [[anse-bundren|Anse]], offering hospitality, grave-digging tools, and practical alternatives. [[anse-bundren|Anse]] refuses everything, citing his [[family-duty|promise]] to [[addie-bundren|Addie]]. Samson sees through the piety to the self-serving core: [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s "[[family-duty|promise]]" is an excuse for immobility that has [[anse-bundren|pa]]radoxically produced the most extreme mobility.

**[[rachel|Rachel Samson]]**: Samson's wife provides the chapter's emotional counterweight. Her grief-rage generalizes into a devastating feminist indictment: "I just wish that you and him and all the men in the world that torture us alive and flout us dead, dragging us up and down the country—" [029-048]. Samson's bemused response — "A man can't tell nothing about them" [029-051] — shows his limits as a narrator even as the text uses his chapter to voice radical critique.

**[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]**: When Samson suggests burying [[addie-bundren|Addie]] locally, [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s eyes "blaze" at him. "If her eyes had a been pistols, I wouldn't be talking now" [029-017]. Her hostility is motivated not by filial piety but by her secret pregnancy — she needs to reach [[jefferson|Jefferson]] for an abortion, making Samson's practical suggestion a mortal threat to her plan.

**[[jewel-bundren|Jewel Bundren]]**: [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] insists on [[anse-bundren|pa]]ying for horse feed, refu[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g to be "beholden" to anyone. Samson's internal response — "if I had my rathers, you wouldn't be here a-tall" [029-037] — reveals his true feelings beneath the hospitality.

## Key Scenes

- **The arrival at sundown [029-001–011]**: Samson sits on his porch with MacCallum and Quick when the [[the-bundren-wagon|Bundren wagon]] [[anse-bundren|pa]]sses. Quick recognizes them and warns about the [[the-washed-out-bridge|washed-out bridge]]. No[[the-body|body]] initially realizes the [[the-body|body]] is still in the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]].

- **The attempt to reason with [[anse-bundren|Anse]] [029-015–024]**: Samson offers practical alternatives — stay the night, bury [[addie-bundren|Addie]] locally, use his tools. [[anse-bundren|Anse]] refuses, and [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] enforces his [[family-duty|promise]]: "If you don't do it, it will be a curse on you" [029-019].

- **The night vigil [029-038–051]**: The Bundrens refuse to enter the house, squatting around the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] all night in the [[water-and-river|rain]]. [[rachel|Rachel]] makes up beds that go unused. Her furious outburst at the men who "torture us alive and flout us dead" is one of the novel's most powerful moments.

- **The buzzard [029-054]**: The next morning, Samson finds a buzzard in his barn — "hunkered up," "spraddle-legged," "like a old bald-headed man." It has been drawn by the corpse. The buzzard's struggle to fly — "with it thick and heavy and full of [[water-and-river|rain]]" — mirrors the Bundrens' own impossible, [[water-and-river|water]]logged [[the-journey|journey]].

- **De[[anse-bundren|pa]]rture [030-007]**: The Bundrens leave before dawn. [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] later asks, "Why didn't we go to [[new-hope|New Hope]], [[anse-bundren|pa]]? Mr. Samson said we was, but we done [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssed the [[the-journey|road]]" — confirming that Samson's advice was deliberately ignored.
