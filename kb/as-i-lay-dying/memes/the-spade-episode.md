# The Spade Episode

The Spade Episode is a darkly comic subplot occupying the final chapters of William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_ (ch053–ch059). Having transported [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]'s corpse across Yokna[[anse-bundren|pa]]tawpha County to [[jefferson|Jefferson]], [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]] discovers that he has forgotten to bring the most basic implement of burial: a s[[anse-bundren|pa]]de. The episode — which encom[[anse-bundren|pa]]sses the borrowing, use, mockery, and return of the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des — distills the [[the-journey|journey]]'s central absurdity into a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle, material detail.

## Narrative Arc

### The Discovery (ch053, Cash)

Arriving in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] with [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[the-body|body]] after nine days of [[the-journey|travel]], the family faces the immediate task of burial. It is at this moment that [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s chronic unpre[[anse-bundren|pa]]redness becomes literal:

> "We forgot our spade, too," pa says. [053-020]

The word "too" carries the weight of accumulated failures: [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]]s washed out, the [[the-mule-team|mules]] drowned, [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]] broken, [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s committal, and now — at the very threshold of completion — the absence of the tool needed to finish the [[the-journey|journey]]'s ostensible purpose. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s response is characteristically direct and exasperated: "Go on and get a s[[anse-bundren|pa]]de" [053-024].

### The Borrowing (ch053)

[[anse-bundren|Anse]] drives "along the street until he seen a house with some s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des throwed out in the yard" [053-029], [[anse-bundren|pa]]u[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g at Mrs. Bundren's house (a name-sharing coincidence that Faulkner leaves unglossed). The image of [[anse-bundren|Anse]] surveying [[jefferson|Jefferson]] yards for abandoned s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des is one of the novel's most quietly devastating comic moments — the [[anse-bundren|pa]]triarch, after his epic ordeal, reduced to scavenging for tools.

He returns "with two s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des" [053-039], the second presumably for [[cash-bundren|Cash]] or [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] — an uncharacteristic act of foresight that, in context, only underscores the absurdity of forgetting the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des in the first place.

### The Mockery (ch054, Cash/Darl)

The s[[anse-bundren|pa]]de-borrowing becomes immediate community gossip. The [[jefferson|Jefferson]] marshal, recounting the Bundrens' arrival to a bystander, frames the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]de as the [[the-journey|journey]]'s emblematic detail:

> "Of course he'd have to borrow a spade to bury his wife with. Unless he could borrow a hole in the ground." [054-009]

The joke is cruel and precise: [[anse-bundren|Anse]], who borrowed everything else ([[the-mule-team|mules]], time, the forbearance of his children), must now borrow the means of burial itself. The marshal's sardonic "Too bad you all didn't put him in it" [054-009] — referring to the grave — registers the community's verdict on [[anse-bundren|Anse]] before the burial has even occurred.

### The Return (ch059, Cash)

In the novel's final chapter, [[cash-bundren|Cash]] narrates the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des' return with characteristic understatement:

> "I reckon I better do it," pa says. "Long as it was me that borrowed them." [059-005]

[[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s insistence on personally returning the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des is framed as responsibility but reads as [[pride|self-regard]] — the same man who took [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s cake money, who committed [[darl-bundren|Darl]] to the asylum, who acquired new teeth and a new wife before his old wife was in the ground, now performs the small ritual of tool-return as if it might balance the ledger.

## Thematic Significance

The Spade Episode crystallizes the [[the-journey|journey]]'s governing irony: [[anse-bundren|Anse]] has transported his wife's [[the-body|body]] across [[water-and-river|flood]]ed [[water-and-river|river]]s and burning barns to [[pride|honor]] her "[[family-duty|promise]]," yet forgot the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]de. The episode's dark comedy — its reduction of epic [[the-journey|pilgrimage]] to the borrowing of a garden implement — is Faulkner's most concentrated critique of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s enterprise. The s[[anse-bundren|pa]]de, like the new teeth [[anse-bundren|Anse]] acquires in [[jefferson|Jefferson]], is a borrowed object that stands in for genuine grief.
