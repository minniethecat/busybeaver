# The Washed-Out Bridge

The bridge over the [[the-river-crossing-location|Yoknapatawpha River]], washed out by recent [[water-and-river|flood]]ing, that serves as the pivotal obstacle in the Bundren family's [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. Its destruction forces the family to ford [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] with [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin in the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] — a [[water-and-river|crossing]] that nearly kills the [[the-mule-team|mule team]], leaves [[cash-bundren|Cash]] with a re-[[cashs-broken-leg|broken leg]], and marks the [[the-journey|journey]]'s definitive turn from funeral procession into ordeal.

## Location and Setting

The bridge s[[anse-bundren|pa]]ns the [[the-river-crossing-location|Yoknapatawpha River]] on the route from the Bundren farm to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. By the time the family reaches it — approximately two days into their [[the-journey|journey]] — recent heavy [[water-and-river|rain]]s have swollen [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] and collapsed the bridge, leaving only "one s[[anse-bundren|pa]]n of it still above [[water-and-river|water]]" visible to [[the-journey|travel]]ers (026-001). The surrounding ter[[water-and-river|rain]] is described through [[vernon-tull|Tull]]'s retrospective narration: [[the-river-crossing-location|the ford]] is deep, the current strong, and the banks are steep, making it "chancey" to cross with a loaded [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] even in good weather (027-003). The bridge's destruction is a recent event; [[vernon-tull|Tull]] and his sons crossed it only days before the Bundrens arrive.

## Narrative Significance

The washed-out bridge is more than a physical obstacle — it is the novel's most concentrated symbol of the [[the-journey|journey]]'s futility and the Bundrens' stubborn determination. [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s refusal to turn back despite multiple warnings ("I be durn if I do," he says when [[vernon-tull|Tull]] offers an alternative route) crystallizes his combination of inertia and obstinacy that drives the entire novel forward (028-001). The bridge's collapse, an act of nature, becomes the pretext for a human disaster: [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] that follows destroys the [[the-mule-team|mule team]], re-breaks [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]] (which is subsequently set in concrete by [[anse-bundren|Anse]]), and deepens the chasm between [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and the rest of the family as [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s attempts to dissuade [[the-river-crossing-event|the crossing]] are ignored.

The [[water-and-river|crossing]] attempt itself — narrated by [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and [[vernon-tull|Tull]] — is one of the novel's most kinetically rendered sequences. The [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]], [[addies-coffin|the coffin]], the flailing [[the-mule-team|mules]], [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] on horseback, [[cash-bundren|Cash]] gripping his tools even as [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] takes him: the scene compresses the novel's central tensions into a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle, desperate action (031-001 through 031-015).

## Symbolic Resonance

Critics have read the washed-out bridge as a secularized baptism: the family must descend into the [[water-and-river|water]] with the [[motherhood|mother]]'s [[the-body|body]] and emerge transformed — but unlike baptism, what emerges is not purified but further broken. [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]], already broken from a church-building accident, is rebroken in [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] and set with cement, turning a religious injury into a grotesque [[anse-bundren|pa]]rody of healing. The bridge's absence creates a forced [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage through [[water-and-river|water]] laden with [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s own associations with [[water-and-river|water]] and clean[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g, from her hatred of her students to her affair with [[whitfield|Whitfield]], the minister.
