# Rachel

Rachel is the wife of [[samson|Samson]], [[bundren-farm|the farm]]er who hosts the Bundren family overnight on their [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. Though she appears only in [[samson|Samson]]'s [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle narrative chapter (ch029) of William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_, her brief but intense protest against the Bundrens' transportation of [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s decaying [[the-body|body]] provides the novel with one of its most direct and powerful critiques of [[anse-bundren|pa]]triarchal ritual.

## Role in the Novel

Rachel embodies the domestic moral authority that [[samson|Samson]]'s pragmatic, "let every man run his own bu[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]ess" [029-014] philosophy deliberately avoids. She is the character who names the [[the-journey|journey]] for what it has become: an outrage against the dead. Her voice interrupts the novel's prevailing masculine acceptance of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s "[[family-duty|promise]]" with a fury that the narrative cannot fully absorb or resolve.

## Domestic Labor and Hospitality

Rachel's first actions in the chapter are those of a competent farm wife: she pre[[anse-bundren|pa]]res supper and goes "to the kitchen to help Rachel" when [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] arrives, arranging beds for the family [029-028]. Her hospitality is genuine, but it is grounded in a domestic ethic that the Bundrens' treatment of [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[the-body|body]] violates at a fundamental level:

> When Rachel put supper on, her and the girl went and fixed some beds. But wouldn't any of them come in. [029-038]

The Bundrens' refusal to eat at the table, to sleep in beds — their insistence on squatting in the barn around the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] — is, from Rachel's perspective, not piety but perversity.

## The Outrage

Rachel's reaction, relayed through [[samson|Samson]], constitutes one of the novel's most concentrated moments of gendered critique:

> "It's a outrage," she says. "A outrage." [029-045]

>

> "What could he 'a' done?" I says. "He give her his promised word." [029-045]

>

> "Who's talking about him?" she says. "Who cares about him?" she says, crying. "I just wish that you and him and all the men in the world that torture us alive and flout us dead, dragging us up and down the country—" [029-045]

This [[language-and-meaning|speech]] ex[[anse-bundren|pa]]nds from the specific case ([[anse-bundren|Anse]] and [[addie-bundren|Addie]]) to a universal indictment: "all the men in the world that torture us alive and flout us dead." Rachel sees what [[samson|Samson]]'s masculine code of non-interference cannot — that [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s "[[family-duty|promise]]" and [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s "word" are instruments of a system that uses women's bodies both in life and in [[death-and-dying|death]]. [[samson|Samson]]'s response — "Now, now. You're upset" [029-052] — is a classic gesture of dismissal that the novel registers without endor[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g.

[[samson|Samson]]'s subsequent reflection — "A man can't tell nothing about them. I lived with the same one fifteen years and I be durn if I can" [029-053] — reveals the gulf between Rachel's moral clarity and her husband's willful incomprehension.

## Aftermath

After the Bundrens de[[anse-bundren|pa]]rt at dawn, [[samson|Samson]] avoids them, walking "down the [[the-journey|road]] toward [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]]" [029-052], and returns to find "Rachel jumped on me because I wasn't there to make them come in to breakfast" [029-053]. Her anger persists [[anse-bundren|pa]]st the event itself, a residue that [[samson|Samson]]'s narrative cannot contain or explain away.
