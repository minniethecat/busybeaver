# Family Duty

The binding moral force that compels the Bundren family to undertake their harrowing [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]: [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s promise to [[addie-bundren|Addie]], the children's obligation to their [[motherhood|mother]], and the tension between duty performed and duty resented.

## Overview

Family duty in *As I Lay Dying* is as much a source of suffering as it is of moral coherence. [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]] invokes his promise to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] — to bury her in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] with her own people — as the justification for the grotesque [[the-journey|journey]], yet the novel consistently asks whether this "duty" is [[sin-and-judgment|sincere]] or self-serving.

The experience of duty fractures along individual lines. [[cash-bundren|Cash]] builds [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] as a [[work-and-craft|craftsman]]'s duty — precise, silent, and complete. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] [[the-journey|questions]] the entire enterprise from within, his philosophical consciousness unable to accept the performance at face value. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] performs the duty with furious physicality, his body arguing against death where words cannot. [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] carries her own secret burden beneath the family's shared task, her hidden [[the-journey|journey]] to the drugstore running parallel to the funeral procession.

[[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], the Bundrens' neighbor, provides the novel's most explicit articulation of duty as a Christian virtue, declaring that she will face [[death-and-dying|death]] "in the consciousness of my duty and reward" (006-011). But her confidence in the moral clarity of duty is precisely what the novel calls into question.

## Manifestations in the Text

The theme of duty operates on multiple levels: as religious imperative, as social expectation, and as intimate family bond.

### Through Cora Tull: Duty as Christian Virtue

[[cora-tull|Cora]] frames duty in explicitly theological terms, seeing it as the measure of a righteous life. Her self-assessment — "I have tried to live right in the sight of God and man, for the honour and comfort of my Christian husband and the love and respect of my Christian children" (006-011) — presents duty as a performance for divine and human audiences alike.

She judges [[addie-bundren|Addie]] harshly for what she perceives as a failure of [[motherhood|maternal]] and spiritual duty, yet her own "duty" is indistinguishable from self-righteousness. When she insists "It's my duty" (016-005) to care for the Bundren children, the repetition of the phrase reveals how duty can become a form of social control.

### Through Addie Bundren: Duty as Revenge

[[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s posthumous narration reveals that her understanding of duty was shaped by her disillusionment with love and [[language-and-meaning|language]]. Her "duty to him, to not ask that" — meaning her duty to [[anse-bundren|Anse]] not to demand emotional intimacy (040-022) — recasts marital duty as a negative obligation, a withholding rather than a giving.

Her most striking formulation is the "duty to the alive, to the terrible blood" (040-024), suggesting that the only duty she ultimately recognized was to the living, embodied continuation of family, not to its abstract ideals. This grounding of duty in blood rather than sentiment makes her a far more complex figure than [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s [[sin-and-judgment|judgments]] allow.

### Through Anse Bundren: Duty as Self-Justification

[[anse-bundren|Anse]] repeatedly invokes his promise to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] as the reason for the [[the-journey|journey]], yet the novel plants doubts about his [[sin-and-judgment|sincerity]]. His duty to his dead wife conveniently aligns with his desire to acquire new false teeth in [[jefferson|Jefferson]].

The ambiguity — is [[anse-bundren|Anse]] genuinely [[pride|honoring]] a promise or exploiting it? — is never resolved, and this irresolution is precisely Faulkner's point: duty, when publicly performed, can never be distinguished from self-interest. The family follows [[anse-bundren|Anse]] not because they believe in his motives but because the structure of family duty leaves them no choice.
