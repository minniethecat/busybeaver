# Smell and Odor

Smell is the most visceral and inescapable sensory register in *As I Lay Dying*. While characters may avert their eyes, close their ears, or retreat into silence, the odor of Addie Bundren's decaying body permeates the novel's world, crossing boundaries that other senses respect. Faulkner uses smell to insist on the irreducible materiality of death: the corpse is not merely a symbol of mortality but a physical object that announces its presence through the nose. The motif extends beyond the corpse to encompass the broader olfactory landscape of rural Mississippi — cedar trees, sweat, mules, rain — creating what critic André Bleikasten called a "sensorium of decay" that grounds the novel's metaphysical concerns in bodily experience.

## Manifestations in the Text

### The Corpse's Odor

The most prominent olfactory motif is the smell of Addie's decaying body. As the journey progresses under the Mississippi summer heat, the corpse's odor intensifies, becoming a physical presence that announces the Bundrens' arrival before they are seen. The smell crosses the boundary between the wagon and the outside world, between the family and the community, between the private fact of Addie's death and the public scandal of their journey. The odor is the body's insistence on its own reality, a reality that language — including the family's justifications and the neighbors' judgments — cannot fully contain or deny. Faulkner renders the smell through sensory details: "the hot July wind in cedar trees smells" [003-002], the sulphurous odor of the approaching storm [010-005], the warm animal smell of the circus [013-004].

### Through Vardaman's Sensory World

Vardaman, the youngest Bundren, experiences the world primarily through sensory impressions, and smell is central to his narrative. His famous equation of his mother with the fish he caught — "My mother is a fish" — emerges from a consciousness that processes death through sensory analogy rather than abstract understanding. For Vardaman, the smell of the dying woman, the smell of the fish, and the smell of the coffin are not distinct categories but overlapping sensory experiences that constitute his traumatic knowledge of mortality.

### Smell as Social Transgression

The corpse's odor functions as a social marker, separating the Bundrens from the communities they pass through. Neighbors who might tolerate the family's eccentricities cannot ignore the smell: it forces a response. The odor transforms the journey from a private act of family obligation into a public nuisance, and the community's shifting reaction — from sympathy to disgust to hostility — tracks the intensification of the smell. Faulkner uses this olfactory register to explore the limits of social tolerance and the way the body's material processes disrupt social decorum.

### The Natural Olfactory World

Beyond the corpse, the novel is saturated with smells: cedar trees, rain, mules, sweat, hay, earth. These natural odors establish a baseline against which the corpse's smell registers as disruption. Darl's narrations in particular attend to the olfactory texture of the landscape, rendering the world as a field of sensory data that consciousness must organize. The natural smells are not merely background but participate in the novel's broader attention to the embodied quality of experience.

## Critical Interpretation

The smell motif in *As I Lay Dying* has been read as part of Faulkner's broader engagement with what Julia Kristeva called "the abject" — that which disturbs identity, system, and order by refusing to respect boundaries. The corpse's smell is abject precisely because it crosses the boundary between inside and outside, private and public, the living body and the dead one. Faulkner's rendering of smell throughout the novel insists on the body's recalcitrant materiality, its resistance to the symbolic orders that language, religion, and social convention attempt to impose upon it.
