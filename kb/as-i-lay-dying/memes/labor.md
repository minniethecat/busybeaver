# Labor

Labor in *As I Lay Dying* is never merely economic activity — it is a language of character. The way each member of the Bundren family works (or refuses to work) reveals their inner nature more reliably than their words ever could. Cash's meticulous joinery, Jewel's furious physical intensity, Anse's constitutional aversion to sweat, and Darl's detached observation all articulate themselves through their relationship to labor.

> A good carpenter, Cash is. He holds the two planks on the trestle, fitted along the edges in a quarter of the finished box. [001-005]

The novel's first pages establish Cash at work before any character speaks — labor precedes language as the novel's primary mode of self-expression.

## Overview

The novel's agrarian Mississippi setting makes physical work inescapable. The Bundrens are a farming family — cotton fields, barns, wagons, and hand tools form the material basis of their existence. Faulkner treats labor not as backdrop but as a form of characterization.

Cash's coffin-building, the novel's central act of craftsmanship, becomes a meditation on care, precision, and the limits of what work can express. He works with the patience of someone for whom the work itself is the point. Jewel's labor, by contrast, is all body — he works "so hard, so fast" that Darl describes him as moving with "the rigid yapping of a dog." Anse's claim that "if he ever sweats, he will die" (007-001) transforms his avoidance of labor from laziness into a kind of existential condition.

> Pa dassent sweat because he will catch his death from the sickness so everybody that comes to help us. [007-001]

This single belief — more a cosmology than an excuse — organizes the entire family economy, placing the full weight of physical survival onto the children.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Cash and the Coffin: Craftsmanship as Care

Cash's construction of Addie's coffin is the novel's most sustained depiction of labor. He works "by lantern" at night (032-053), beveling each board with the precision of a cabinetmaker. His single-minded focus — "working on, half turned into the feeble lamp-light" (017-003) — transforms carpentry into an act of devotion. Cash cannot articulate his feelings about his mother's death, but his work speaks for him. The coffin is his grief made visible, each joint and bevel a word he cannot say.

> "I'm going to bevel it," he says. [017-014]

Cash's announcement of the bevel is one of his longest speeches. He explains his craft not to persuade but simply to make visible what he sees — that the coffin, like the body it will hold, must be shaped to acknowledge its purpose. His sawdusted arms (014-006) and sweat-powdered skin (012-015) mark him as the family's primary laborer, the one whose body bears the physical record of work.

### Anse: The Man Who Cannot Sweat

Anse Bundren defines himself through his relationship to labor — specifically, his avoidance of it. He claims that "if he ever sweats, he will die" (007-001), a belief that insulates him from physical work and places the burden of survival on his children. His philosophy is explicit: "It ain't that I am afraid of work; I always have fed me and mine" (009-004) — but the novel shows otherwise.

When the family works, Anse supervises. When the journey demands effort, Anse complains. His labor-avoidance is not sloth but a carefully maintained identity, one that protects him from the bodily demands that define his family's lives.

> You could tell they ain't been nobody else's but Anse's that way without no mistake. His eyes look like pieces of burnt-out cinder fixed in his face, looking out over the land. [008-024]

Darl's observation of Anse's shirt — free of sweat stains and ill-fitting — reads labor avoidance as a physical mark, as legible on the body as Cash's sawdust-covered arms. The absence of sweat is Anse's signature, the negative space that defines him.

### Jewel: Labor as Fury

Jewel works with violence. He moves "straight as a plumb-line, his jaw set" (036-008), his physical effort indistinguishable from rage. His labor is never detached from emotion — every action carries the full weight of his feeling.

When the wagon tips in the river, it is Jewel who fights the current to save the coffin, working against the water with "the back of his red neck" visible above the flood. Jewel's labor is the body's argument against death — furious, desperate, and absolute. His purchase of a horse through night labor, clearing a field by lantern, demonstrates an alternative economy of effort: work done in secret, for love, outside the family's shared accounting.

### The Farming Economy

The Bundrens exist within a subsistence farming economy where labor is survival. Their identity as "farmers" (028-001) locates them in a specific social stratum — not landowners in any meaningful sense, but people who work the land and depend on its yield.

> Jewel and I come up from the field, following the path in single file. [001-001]

The novel opens with work — Darl and Jewel returning from the cotton field, already defined by labor before any character speaks. The cotton-house, the spring, the wagon, the barn — each setting is a site of production. The family's poverty is inseparable from their labor: they work not to accumulate but to persist. This is a world where Cash's sawing provides the novel's most constant sound — a rhythmic, ongoing assertion that work continues, even as death approaches.
