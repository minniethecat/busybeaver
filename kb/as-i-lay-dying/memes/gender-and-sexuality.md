# Gender and Sexuality

Gender and sexuality in *As I Lay Dying* are explored primarily through the experiences of its female characters — Dewey Dell Bundren's unwanted pregnancy and Addie Bundren's lifelong dissatisfaction with marriage and motherhood. Faulkner presents female sexuality as a site of both vulnerability and resistance within the patriarchal structures of the rural South.

## Overview

The novel examines the constraints placed on women in early twentieth-century rural Mississippi, where female bodies are subject to male control and female desire is systematically denied expression. Through Dewey Dell's secret pregnancy by Lafe and Addie's single monologue chapter — the only chapter narrated by her — Faulkner gives voice to female interiority within a social order that demands women's silence on matters of sexuality.

> He could do so much for me if he just would. He could do everything for me. It's like everything in the world for me is inside a tub full of guts, so that you wonder how there can be any room in it for anything else very important. He is a big tub of guts and I am a little tub of guts and if there is not any room for anything else important in a big tub of guts, how can it be room in a little tub of guts. But I know it is there because God gave women a sign when something has happened bad. [014-001]

Dewey Dell's metaphor of her body as "a tub full of guts" captures the visceral alienation of female embodiment in the novel — the sense that a woman's physical existence is always already claimed by reproductive biology.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Dewey Dell Bundren

Dewey Dell's pregnancy is the most sustained exploration of sexuality in the novel. Her sexual encounter with Lafe occurred while picking cotton — an act narrated through the language of agricultural inevitability:

> We picked on down the row, the woods getting closer and closer and the secret shade, picking on into the secret shade with my sack and Life's sack. Because I said will I or won't I when the sack was half-full because I said if the sack is full when we get to the woods it won't be me. I said if it don't mean for me to do it the sack will not be full and I will turn up the next row but if the sack is full, I cannot help it. It will be that I had to do it all the time and I cannot help it. [007-001]

The conflation of her cotton sack with "Life's sack" and the repeated refrain "I cannot help it" reveals both the passivity imposed on female sexuality and Dewey Dell's strategic deployment of that passivity as a form of self-exoneration. Her internal monologue reveals a body that has been claimed by another: "He is his guts and I am my guts. And I am Lafe's guts. That's it" (014-011).

Her journey to Jefferson is driven not by grief for her mother but by the desperate hope of obtaining an abortion, a quest that ends in humiliation at the Mottson drugstore. The clerk exploits her inability to name her condition, selling her worthless "medicine" — turpentine and alcohol (045-032). Through Dewey Dell, Faulkner dramatizes the vulnerability of a young woman whose body has become a site of social transgression she has no language or power to resolve.

### Through Addie Bundren

Addie Bundren's single monologue chapter offers the novel's most explicit reflection on female sexuality and marital discontent. She describes her discovery that motherhood was not a fulfillment but a violation:

> When he was born I knew that motherhood was invented by someone who had to have a word for it because the ones that had the children didn't care whether there was a word for it or not. I knew that fear was invented by someone that had never had the fear; pride, who never had the pride. [040-015]

Addie's experience of sexuality is bound up with her hatred of being defined by her reproductive function. She recounts how "my aloneness had been violated and then made whole again by the violation: time, Anse, love, what you will, outside the circle" (040-017). Her voice reveals a woman who experienced sexuality not as intimacy but as invasion, and who took her revenge through a secret — her affair with Whitfield and Jewel's true paternity — that would only be revealed after death.

> I would think: The shape of my body where I used to be a virgin is in the shape of a    and I couldn't think Anse, couldn't remember Anse. [040-020]

The deliberate blank space in Addie's consciousness — the word that eludes her — represents the unspeakability of female sexual experience within the language available to her. She can only define her body by what it no longer is, by the absence where a word should be.

### Through the Social Order

The novel's wider community reinforces patriarchal norms about women's place. Cora Tull articulates the prevailing ideology: "A woman's place is with her husband and children, alive or dead" (006-009). Vernon Tull describes Addie as "ever a private woman" (005-017), suggesting that female respectability requires concealment of interior life. Cash's observation that "It's a married woman somewhere. Ain't any young girl got that much daring and staying power" (032-029) deflects the reality of Dewey Dell's pregnancy onto a convenient fiction, demonstrating the community's refusal to acknowledge female sexuality outside marriage.
