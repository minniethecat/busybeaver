# Darl

## Darl

[027-001]He comes up the lane fast, yet we are three hundred yards beyond the mouth of it when he turns into the [[the-journey|road]], the mud flying beneath the flickering drive of the hooves. Then he slows a little, light and erect in the saddle, [[jewels-horse|the horse]] mincing through the mud.

[027-002]Tull is in his lot. He looks at us, lifts his hand. We go on, the wagon creaking, the mud whispering on the wheels. Vernon still stands there. He watches [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] as he passes, the horse moving with a light, high-kneed driving gait, three hundred yards back. We go on, with a motion so soporific, so dreamlike as to be uninferant of progress, as though time and not space were decreasing between us and it.

[027-003]It turns off at right angles, the wheel-marks of last Sunday healed away now: a smooth, red scoriation curving away into the pines; a white signboard with faded lettering: New Hope Church. 3 mi. It wheels up like a motionless hand lifted above the profound desolation of the ocean; beyond it the red road lies like a spoke of which [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]] is the rim. It wheels past, empty, unscarred, the white signboard turns away its fading and tranquil assertion. [[cash-bundren|Cash]] looks up the road quietly, his head turning as we pass it like an owl’s head, his face composed. Pa looks straight ahead, humped. Dewey Dell looks at the road too, then she looks back at me, her eyes watchful and repudiant, not like that question which was in those of Cash, for a smouldering while. The signboard passes; the unscarred road wheels on. Then Dewey Dell turns her head. The wagon creaks on.

[027-004]Cash spits over the wheel. “In a couple of days now it’ll be smelling,” he says.

[027-005]“You might tell Jewel that,” I say.

[027-006]He is motionless now, sitting the horse at the junction, upright, watching us, no less still than the signboard that lifts its fading capitulation opposite him.

[027-007]“It ain’t balanced right for no long ride,” Cash says.

[027-008]“Tell him that, too,” I say. The wagon creaks on.

[027-009]A mile farther along he passes us, the horse, arch-necked, reined back to a swift single-foot. He sits lightly, poised, upright, wooden-faced in the saddle, the broken hat raked at a swaggering angle. He passes us swiftly, without looking at us, the horse driving, its hooves hissing in the mud. A gout of mud, back-flung, plops on to [[addies-coffin|the box]]. Cash leans forward and takes a tool from his box and removes it carefully. When the road crosses Whiteleaf, the willows leaning near enough, he breaks off a branch and scours at the stain with the wet leaves.
