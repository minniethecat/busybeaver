# Kate

A neighbor woman in William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_ who appears in two early chapters set at the Bundren household and during the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] ride back from [[jefferson|town]]. Kate functions as a chorus-like figure whose repeated, stubborn pronouncements on the cake episode establish an early tonal register of rural pragmatism and moral rigidity.

## Role in the Novel

Kate appears exclusively in the novel's opening sequence, during the vigil for [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]. She is present in [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]]'s first narrative chapter (ch002), seated at [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s bedside, and again in [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s narration of the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] ride home (ch008). Her role is structurally modest — she speaks fewer than a dozen lines — but thematically significant: her voice anchors the novel's opening in the vernacular ethics of the rural community, contrasting the private, lyrical consciousness that will dominate later chapters. [002-002] [008-043]

## Biography

Little biographical detail is given for Kate beyond her association with the [[vernon-tull|Tull]] household. She appears to be a neighbor or relative of [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], present for neighborly [[family-duty|duty]] at the [[death-and-dying|dying]] woman's bedside. Her repeated invocations of what [[addie-bundren|Addie]] "ought to" have done with the cakes suggest a woman deeply invested in the social [[family-duty|obligation]]s and reciprocal economies of rural life. [002-008] [002-014]

## Key Dialogue and Thematic Function

Kate's dialogue constitutes one of the novel's earliest examples of communal voice — the choric [[sin-and-judgment|judgment]] of the countryside upon individual action. Her position on the cakes is absolute and unchanging across two chapters:

> "She ought to taken those cakes anyway," Kate says. [002-002]

> "She ought to taken them," Kate says. "But those rich town ladies can change their minds. Poor folks can't." [002-004]

This ref[[water-and-river|rain]] — repeated five times across chapters 2 and 8 — establishes the novel's concern with broken [[family-duty|promise]]s and the economics of rural [[family-duty|obligation]]. The [[jefferson|town]] lady's withdrawal from the cake order becomes, in Kate's rendering, a class injury: the rich can "change their minds" in ways the poor cannot afford. [002-006]

In the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] scene (ch008), Kate's dialogue shifts from cakes to the Bundren family's future after [[addies-death|Addie's death]]:

> "And I reckon she'll be behind him for thirty years more," Kate says. "Or if it ain't her, he'll get another one before cotton-picking." [008-043]

This cynical assessment of [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]'s prospects — that he will remarry quickly — proves prophetic, and her remark about [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] ("there's more gals than one around here that don't want to see [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] tied down" [008-048]) hints at the sexual undercurrents of the community. [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s rebuke ("Why, Kate!" [008-049]) marks the boundary between Kate's bluntness and [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s piety.
