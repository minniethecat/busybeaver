# Darl

## Darl

[025-001]He goes on toward the barn, entering the lot, wooden-backed.

[025-002]Dewey Dell carries the basket on one arm, in the other hand something wrapped square in a newspaper. Her face is calm and sullen, her eyes brooding and alert; within them I can see Peabody’s back like two round peas in two thimbles: perhaps in Peabody’s back two of those worms which [[work-and-craft|work]] surreptitious and steady through you and out the other side and you waking suddenly from sleep or from waking, with on your face an expression sudden, intent, and concerned. She sets the basket into the wagon and climbs in, her leg coming long from beneath her tightening dress: that lever which moves the world; one of that caliper which measures the length and breadth of life. She sits on the seat beside Vardaman and sets the parcel on her lap.

[025-003]Then he enters the barn. He has not looked back.

[025-004]“It ain’t right,” [[anse-bundren|pa]] says. “It’s little enough for him to do for her.”

[025-005]“Go on,” [[cash-bundren|Cash]] says. “Leave him stay if he wants. He’ll be all right here. Maybe he’ll go up to Tull’s and stay.”

[025-006]“He’ll catch us,” I say. “He’ll cut across and meet us at Tull’s lane.”

[025-007]“He would have rid that horse, too,” pa says, “if I hadn’t a stopped him. A durn spotted critter wilder than a cattymount. A deliberate flouting of her and of me.”

[025-008]The wagon moves; the mules’ ears begin to bob. Behind us, above the house, motionless in tall and soaring circles, they diminish and disappear.
