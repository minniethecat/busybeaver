# Consciousness

*As I Lay Dying* is built from the inside out. Each of its fifty-nine chapters is a monologue filtering the world through a single character's consciousness. Faulkner's radical premise is that reality — the death of a mother, the journey to bury her — exists only as it is perceived, and each perception reshapes it.

## Overview

The novel's stream-of-consciousness technique is not merely stylistic. It is an argument about the nature of experience: that what happens and what is perceived to happen are inseparable. The novel deploys fifteen different narrators across fifty-nine chapters, each filtering the same events — Addie's dying, the river crossing, the barn fire, the arrival in Jefferson — through a distinct perceptual apparatus. Darl Bundren's clairvoyant narration, Vardaman's sensory immediacy, Dewey Dell's bodily preoccupation, and Addie's posthumous voice each demonstrate a different mode of consciousness, from the philosophically abstract to the viscerally physical. Together they form a composite portrait of a family in which no two members experience the same event in the same way.

## The Hierarchy of Consciousness

Faulkner imposes an implicit hierarchy on his narrators' modes of consciousness. Darl occupies the apex — his perception transcends physical limitation, giving him access to events he cannot witness and interior states he should not know. His narration is dense with subordination and abstraction, a consciousness that processes raw experience into metaphysical inquiry:

> It is as though the dark were resolving him out of his integrity, into an unrelated scattering of components — snuffings and stampings; smells of cooling flesh and ammoniac hair; an illusion of a coordinate whole of splotched hide and strong bones within which, with which, they figure as a rooted though agile skeleton to which their every breath and smell is sympathetic. [011-016]

At the other extreme stands Vardaman, whose consciousness operates through sensory conflation rather than conceptual analysis. His famous declaration — "My mother is a fish" (019-001) — represents a mode of perception in which death can only be grasped by collapsing it into a concrete image. Between these poles, Dewey Dell's bodily consciousness, Cash's object-oriented perception, and Anse's self-justifying monologue each occupy distinct positions on Faulkner's spectrum of interior life.

## The Limits of Consciousness

The novel also dramatizes what consciousness cannot do. Darl sees everything and understands nothing that can save him — his knowledge of Jewel's parentage, of Dewey Dell's pregnancy, of the journey's absurdity, leads not to mastery but to the asylum. Addie's single chapter (040) delivers the novel's most profound meditation on language and the body, but it arrives after her death — consciousness speaking from a position of terminal irrelevance. As Addie herself recognizes:

> I would think how words go straight up in a thin line, quick and harmless, and how terribly doing goes along the earth, clinging to it, so that after a while the two lines are too far apart for the same person to straddle from one to the other. [040-003]

This gap between thought and action, between consciousness and consequence, is the novel's deepest subject. Faulkner grants his characters extraordinary interior richness — and then shows that richness to be powerless against the brute facts of mud, flood, fire, and decay.

## Modernist Context

Faulkner's treatment of consciousness places *As I Lay Dying* within the modernist revolution associated with Joyce, Woolf, and Proust. Like *Ulysses*, the novel attempts to render the texture of interior life with unprecedented fidelity. Like *Mrs. Dalloway*, it moves between consciousnesses to build a composite reality no single perspective could contain. But Faulkner's innovation is to democratize interiority — his poor white farmers, children, and dying women think as richly as Joyce's intellectuals. Consciousness in Yoknapatawpha County is as deep as consciousness anywhere.
