# Addie Bundren

The [[motherhood|mother]] of the Bundren family, whose [[death-and-dying|death]] from an unspecified illness sets the novel's [[the-journey|journey]] in motion and whose single monologue (chapter 40) — narrated from beyond the grave — contains the novel's most radical philosophical statement about [[language-and-meaning|language]], the [[the-body|body]], and the impossibility of love as a shared experience. Addie is both the novel's absent center and its most powerful narrative presence.

## Role in the Novel

Addie Bundren occupies a unique structural position: she is the character around whom all action revolves but whose consciousness remains inaccessible until chapter 40, long after her [[death-and-dying|death]]. Her dying is narrated by [[darl-bundren|Darl]], [[cash-bundren|Cash]], [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], and [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]; her corpse is the [[the-journey|journey]]'s cargo; her burial wish is the [[the-journey|journey]]'s stated purpose. When she finally speaks, her monologue rewrites everything the reader thought they understood about her marriage, her children, and her relationship to [[language-and-meaning|language]] itself.

## Biography

Addie was a schoolteacher before marrying [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]. Her monologue reveals that she entered the marriage with contempt — for [[anse-bundren|Anse]], for the rural life he represented, for the cycle of pregnancy and [[work-and-craft|labor]] that awaited her. She bore [[cash-bundren|Cash]] as a way of "making a thing" that was hers alone; [[darl-bundren|Darl]] was given to [[anse-bundren|Anse]] to "negative" [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]; [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] was the product of her affair with the [[whitfield|Reverend Whitfield]], a child of passion rather than [[family-duty|obligation]]; [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] were born to "negative" [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] in turn, a system of compensation that hollowed out each successive birth. She died of an unspecified illness approximately ten days before the novel's opening, having extracted from [[anse-bundren|Anse]] a [[family-duty|promise]] to bury her in [[jefferson|Jefferson]], forty miles from [[bundren-farm|the farm]].

> I could just remember how my father used to say that the reason for living was to get ready to stay dead a long time. And when I would have to look at them day after day, each with his and her secret and selfish thought, and blood strange to each other blood and strange to mine, and think that this seemed to be the only way I could get ready to stay dead, I would hate my father for having ever planted me. [040-002]

> When the switch fell I could feel it upon my flesh; when it welted and ridged it was my blood that ran, and I would think with each blow of the switch: Now you are aware of me! Now I am something in your secret and selfish life, who have marked your blood with my own forever and ever. [040-002]

## Narrative Voice

Addie's single chapter is the novel's philosophical core. Her prose is denser and more aphoristic than any other narrator's — she speaks in declarations rather than descriptions, and her subject is the failure of [[language-and-meaning|language]] to capture experience. Her hatred of the word "love" — "just a shape to fill a lack" — and her insistence that "sin" is only a word that others impose on the [[the-body|body]] articulate a radical rejection of the linguistic frameworks that the other characters (particularly [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]] and [[whitfield|Whitfield]]) depend upon. Her voice, emerging from [[death-and-dying|death]], claims an authority that no living narrator can match.

> That was when I learned that words are no good; that words don't ever fit even what they are trying to say at. When he was born I knew that motherhood was invented by someone who had to have a word for it because the ones that had the children didn't care whether there was a word for it or not. I knew that fear was invented by someone that had never had the fear; pride, who never had the pride. [040-015]

> I knew that it had been, not that they had dirty noses, but that we had had to use one another by words like spiders dangling by their mouths from a beam, swinging and twisting and never touching, and that only through the blows of the switch could my blood and their blood flow as one stream. I knew that it had been, not that my aloneness had to be violated over and over each day, but that it had never been violated until Cash came. Not even by Anse in the nights. [040-015]

## Key Relationships

**[[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]**: Addie married [[anse-bundren|Anse]] without love, and her contempt for him — his passivity, his repetitive speech, his refusal to sweat — is one of her monologue's most bitter threads. Yet [[anse-bundren|Anse]] keeps his [[family-duty|promise]] to bury her in [[jefferson|Jefferson]], and the irony of this fidelity, from a man she despised, is characteristic of the novel's moral complexity.

> He had a word, too. Love, he called it. But I had been used to words for a long time. I knew that that word was like the others: just a shape to fill a lack; that when the right time came, you wouldn't need a word for that any more than for pride or fear. Cash did not need to say it to me nor I to him, and I would say, Let Anse use it, if he wants to. So that it was Anse or love; love or Anse: it didn't matter. [040-016]

**The [[whitfield|Reverend Whitfield]]**: Addie's affair with [[whitfield|Whitfield]] produced [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], the child she loved most. Her description of the affair is stripped of romance — it is a bodily event, not a spiritual one — and her contempt for [[whitfield|Whitfield]]'s religious hypocrisy anticipates his own monologue's confession.

**Her Children**: Addie's relationship to each child is defined by her system of compensation: [[cash-bundren|Cash]] was hers, [[darl-bundren|Darl]] was [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s, [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] was passion's, [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] were [[family-duty|obligation]]s. This calculus of love and debt shapes each child's identity and is one of the novel's most unsettling psychological insights.

## Death and Aftermath

[[addies-death|Addie's death]] scene — narrated by [[darl-bundren|Darl]] despite his absence — is one of the novel's most extraordinary passages. The fan moves above her [[the-body|body]] until the last moment, "whispering the useless air." The coffin that [[cash-bundren|Cash]] builds for her becomes the [[the-journey|journey]]'s physical and symbolic cargo. Her decomposing [[the-body|body]], refusing to stay buried in [[language-and-meaning|language]] or in earth, is the novel's most powerful assertion of the [[the-body|body]]'s primacy over the word.
