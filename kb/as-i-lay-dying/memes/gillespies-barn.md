# Gillespie's Barn

A barn belonging to the [[gillespie|Gillespie]] family, located near the end of the Bundrens' [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]], where [[darl-bundren|Darl Bundren]] sets a fire in an attempt to cremate his [[motherhood|mother]]'s decompo[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g [[the-body|body]] and end the grotesque funeral procession. The barn becomes the stage for the novel's climactic confrontation — [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s heroic rescue of [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] from the flames and [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s subsequent arrest and committal to the Jackson asylum.

## Location and Context

[[gillespie|Gillespie]]'s farm lies within a day's [[the-journey|travel]] of [[jefferson|Jefferson]], the Bundrens' destination. The family stops there to rest the [[the-mule-team|mules]] — by this point in the [[the-journey|journey]], [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[the-body|body]] has been decaying for over a week, and the stench has become a public spectacle. The [[gillespie|Gillespie]]s, like the other farm families encountered along the route, are unwilling hosts to the Bundrens' increa[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gly monstrous procession. The barn is described through [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s and [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s eyes: a large structure filled with hay and farm equipment, "high and warm and full of the smell of [[the-cow|cow]]s and feed" — a s[[anse-bundren|pa]]ce of rural shelter that [[darl-bundren|Darl]] transforms into a funeral pyre.

## The Fire

[[darl-bundren|Darl]] sets [[darl-burns-the-barn|the fire]] at night, after [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] has been stored in the barn. His motivation — never explicitly stated — is understood by the reader through his accumulated narrative authority: [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is the only Bundren who fully comprehends the absurdity of the [[the-journey|journey]], the indignity being visited upon [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s corpse, and the family's collective refusal to acknowledge what they are doing. The fire is his most radical attempt to impose [[language-and-meaning|meaning]] — to give [[addie-bundren|Addie]] a proper cremation rather than letting her rot incrementally across the [[jefferson|Mississippi]] countryside.

[[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], who has been sleeping a[[anse-bundren|pa]]rt from the family, sees the flames and races into the burning barn to rescue [[addies-coffin|the coffin]], carrying it out "on his back" in a scene of spectacular physical heroism that contrasts with [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s cerebral desperation. The animals trapped in the barn perish; [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] survives. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is identified as the arsonist and, to avoid criminal prosecution, the family agrees to have him committed to the state asylum in Jackson.

## Thematic Significance

The barn fire is the novel's most concentrated convergence of its major themes: [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s clairvoyant insight versus the family's willful blindness, [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s physical action versus [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s philosophical [[anse-bundren|pa]]ralysis, the tension between fire (purification, destruction, [[darl-bundren|Darl]]) and [[water-and-river|water]] (persistence, endurance, [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]]), and the [[the-journey|quest]]ion of what constitutes proper care for the dead. The barn, associated with shelter, livestock, and rural subsistence, becomes a site of sacrifice — the animals die so that [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin may be preserved, a trade that encapsulates the Bundrens' perverse economy of value.
