# Death and Mortality

Death is the engine of *As I Lay Dying*: [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]] dies in the novel's opening pages, and her corpse — decaying, smelling, drawing buzzards — drives the journey that structures all fifty-nine chapters. Faulkner examines death not as a single event but as a prolonged physical and social process, refracted through fifteen narrators' consciousnesses.

## Death as Process

The novel's title plays on the double meaning of "dying": both the moment of death and the prolonged process of dying. Addie dies early, but her dying reverberates through every subsequent chapter — in [[cash-bundren|Cash's]] careful coffin-making, [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman's]] traumatized confusion of his mother with a fish, [[darl-bundren|Darl's]] increasingly unhinged attempts to end the journey by burning the barn, and [[anse-bundren|Anse's]] pragmatic acquisition of a new wife before the old one is even buried. Death, Faulkner suggests, is not a boundary but a condition that spreads backward and forward through the lives it touches.
