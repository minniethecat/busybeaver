# Salvation

Salvation in *As I Lay Dying* is a contested concept. For Cora Tull, salvation is a matter of prayer, faith, and divine grace — a transaction between the sinner and God. For Whitfield, it is a performance: he crosses the flooded river to confess his adultery with Addie, only to find she is already dead, rendering his confession cost-free. For the Bundrens, salvation has no theological content at all — it means surviving the journey to Jefferson and burying Addie as promised.

## Overview

Faulkner places religious language and earthly reality in constant tension. Cora speaks of salvation with the confidence of the saved; her prayers for Addie are framed as spiritual acts superior to the Bundrens' physical struggle. But the novel repeatedly undercuts religious claims: Whitfield's confession is a masterpiece of self-serving piety, and Anse's invocation of "God's will" is indistinguishable from his laziness. The novel's deepest salvation may be entirely secular — Jewel saving Addie's coffin from the river, Cash enduring his broken leg, Darl's final, terrible honesty.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Cora: Salvation as Certainty

Cora Tull's faith is absolute and her salvation assured. She prays for Addie's soul, judges the Bundrens' decisions, and interprets events through the lens of divine providence. "The Lord giveth" she repeats, certain of meanings the novel refuses to confirm. Cora's salvation is real to her but invisible to the reader — a private conviction that cannot be falsified, only juxtaposed against the secular suffering that surrounds her.

Cora's most revealing statement on salvation concerns the cakes she was commissioned to bake — a failed commercial transaction that she recasts as spiritual merit:

> Riches is nothing in the face of the Lord, for He can see into the heart. [002-019]

The irony is that Cora cannot see into hearts — she misreads Addie entirely, seeing a woman of faith where there was only a woman of pride and despair. Her certainty about salvation is inseparable from her blindness to the actual human beings around her.

### Through Whitfield: Salvation as Farce

Reverend Whitfield's chapter (041) is the novel's most savage treatment of religious hypocrisy. He resolves to confess his adultery with Addie, crosses the flooded river at great personal risk, and arrives to find her dead — at which point his confession becomes entirely safe. He thanks God for this outcome:

> He is merciful; He will accept the will for the deed. [041-006]

Salvation for Whitfield is not transformation but escape from consequences. The flooded river — the danger he risked to do the right thing — becomes the alibi for his virtue rather than the test of it. Faulkner's satire is devastating: Whitfield's God is a deity who rewards cowardice with the appearance of courage, who accepts intentions as substitutes for actions, and who allows a man to feel righteous about a confession he never had to make.

### Through Addie: Salvation Rejected

Addie Bundren, the novel's theological center, rejects salvation entirely. Her monologue contains no prayer, no appeal to divine mercy, no hope of an afterlife. Her relationship to sin is not guilt but possession — her affair with Whitfield gave her Jewel, the only child she truly loved, and she refuses to regret what religious morality would condemn:

> Sin is just a matter of words, to them that are not in love. [040-007]

For Addie, salvation is not a spiritual state but a physical fact — being seen, being known, having one's blood "marked" by another's. Her whipping of the schoolchildren, which she describes with chilling precision, is her attempt to force awareness of her existence into others, a secular sacrament of recognition. The novel's deepest theological insight may be Addie's: that salvation, if it exists at all, is not forgiveness but presence, not absolution but being acknowledged.

### Through the Bundrens: Salvation as Survival

The Bundren family pursues a promise, not a prayer. Their "salvation" is the completion of their journey — burying Addie in Jefferson as she asked. This secular version of deliverance requires no faith, only endurance. Jewel's physical courage, Cash's stoic suffering, and even Anse's stubbornness constitute a form of salvation measured in miles traveled and obstacles overcome, not in grace received.
