# The Bundren Wagon

The Bundren family's farm wagon, loaded with [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin and the accumulated detritus of the [[the-journey|journey]] — tools, bedding, food, and the children themselves — serves as both the literal vehicle of the novel's plot and its most persistent symbol of the weight of [[family-duty|family obligation]]. From the first loading at the Bundren farm to its final arrival in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] with the new Mrs. Bundren aboard, the wagon is where the novel's physical and moral action takes place.

## Description

The wagon is a standard farm wagon of the rural [[jefferson|Mississippi]] type — wooden, horse-drawn, built for hauling crops rather than coffins. It is pulled initially by the family's [[the-mule-team|mule team]] and, after [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] drowns the [[the-mule-team|mules]], by a replacement team purchased with the money from [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s sold horse. The wagon's bed carries [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin — itself built by [[cash-bundren|Cash]] on the family porch — along with [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s [[cashs-tools|carpenter's tools]], [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s [[dewey-dells-fan|fan]], baskets of food contributed by neighbors, and whatever other belongings the family deems necessary for a [[the-journey|journey]] that was supposed to take three days and instead takes nine.

## Role in the Journey

The wagon is the [[the-journey|journey]]'s central object — every major event is organized around it. The loading of [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] onto the wagon bed is the novel's first collective action after [[addies-death|Addie's death]]. The [[the-river-crossing-event|river crossing]], in which the wagon overturns and nearly loses [[addies-coffin|the coffin]], is the [[the-journey|journey]]'s most harrowing physical sequence. The wagon's arrival at the [[gillespie|Gillespie]] farm leads to [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s act of arson. And at the novel's end, the wagon carries not only the surviving Bundrens but also the new Mrs. Bundren, clo[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g the circle of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s practical needs in a way that makes the entire [[the-journey|journey]] feel like a delivery — of a coffin out, of a new wife in.

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s early description establishes the wagon as a visual presence against the landscape: "The wagon stands in the [[the-journey|road]] before the house, the team in the traces, [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s horse beside the wagon, and [[cash-bundren|Cash]] is nailing the last board on [[addies-coffin|the box]]" (001-001). The wagon is, from the beginning, what the family gathers around — the gravitational center that organizes their collective movement.

## Symbolic Meaning

The wagon functions as:

- **The burden of family**: What cannot be left behind, even when it [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]ks in [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]]

- **The [[the-journey|journey]] as spectacle**: As the wagon [[anse-bundren|pa]]sses through the county, it becomes a public object of horror and fascination — the decompo[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g [[the-body|body]] in [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] transforms the humble farm wagon into a grotesque [[anse-bundren|pa]]rade float

- **[[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s world**: The wagon is [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s property and [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s domain — he sits in its seat, drives its team, and uses it to conduct what is, in his understanding, primarily a practical errand
