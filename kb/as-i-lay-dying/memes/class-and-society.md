# Class and Society

The Bundrens are poor. This fact — material, inescapable, and socially defining — underlies every decision in *As I Lay Dying*. Faulkner's novel is not a sociological study, but its immersion in the economic realities of rural Mississippi makes class an unavoidable dimension of the narrative. The family's [[poverty]] is not merely backdrop; it shapes how they grieve, how they travel, and how they are judged by the communities they pass through.

> "Those rich town ladies can change their minds. Poor folks can't." [002-004]

Kate Tull delivers the novel's most direct class thesis in a single sentence. The distinction between "rich town ladies" and "poor folks" is not about money alone — it is about agency, about who gets to revise their decisions and who must live with the consequences.

## The Economics of the Bundren Family

[[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]] is a subsistence farmer, not a landowner in any meaningful sense. The farm on the hillside produces cotton and corn, but the family lives at the edge of survival. [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s refusal to sweat — his conviction that perspiration would kill him — is, from one angle, a comic character trait; from another, it is an economic catastrophe for a family dependent on manual [[labor]]. The children work the fields; [[cash-bundren|Cash]] is a carpenter; [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] works nights to buy his horse. Every character's relationship to work defines their material existence.

## Rural Poverty and Social Judgment

The novel's class dynamics are most visible in the encounters between the Bundrens and the townspeople they meet on the [[the-journey|journey]]. In Mottson, the druggist refuses to sell [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] what she needs; the marshal threatens [[anse-bundren|Anse]] with arrest for transporting a decomposing corpse. In Jefferson, the family becomes an object of disgust and fascination — their poverty, their smell, their refusal to observe the forms of respectable mourning mark them as outsiders. [[moseley]] the pharmacist, [[macgowan]] the soda jerk, and the Jefferson marshal all judge the Bundrens from positions of relative social security, their contempt revealing the class lines that the novel otherwise leaves implicit.

## The Neighbor Class

The Bundrens' rural neighbors — [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], [[vernon-tull|Vernon Tull]], [[samson]], [[armstid]] — share their economic condition but distinguish themselves through piety, propriety, or simple competence. [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s moral judgments of the Bundrens are inseparable from her self-image as a Christian woman who manages her household properly — a form of class distinction within poverty. [[vernon-tull|Vernon]] helps the Bundrens without sharing their chaos; [[samson]] offers shelter but cannot comprehend the family's stubbornness. These neighbors function as a mirror: they reflect what the Bundrens might be if their family were bound by different codes.

## Cash's Tools and the Value of Work

[[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s relationship to his carpentry tools is the novel's most precise economic statement. When the wagon tips in the river, Cash's first thought is for his tools — "My tools," he says, and the family spends hours retrieving them from the water.

> "It ain't respectful," pa says. "It's a deliberate flouting of her and of me." [024-020]

Anse's appeal to "respect" when the family questions his authority reveals class anxiety operating through the language of propriety — a poor man can still demand the deference due a patriarch, even when he has done nothing to earn it. The tools are Cash's livelihood, his identity, and his only form of social capital. In a world where the Bundrens have nothing, Cash's tools represent the one form of value the family can reliably produce: skilled [[labor]].

## The New Mrs. Bundren

The novel's final image — [[anse-bundren|Anse]] introducing his children to a new wife, the gramophone-playing woman he has acquired along with his false teeth — is a class statement in miniature. The new Mrs. Bundren, with her gramophone, represents a world of purchased pleasure that the old [[addie-bundren|Addie]] — the schoolteacher who married down, the woman who hated the word "love" — could never inhabit. [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s remarriage is an act of class aspiration as much as of personal renewal: he has, at last, gotten something for himself.
