# Music and Song

Music in *As I Lay Dying* exists at the margins of the novel's verbal world — in hymns, in the rhythmic sounds of labor, in the cadences of speech that approach song. It is not a dominant motif but a persistent counterpoint to the novel's more explicit themes, representing a mode of meaning-making that operates through rhythm, repetition, and embodied sound rather than through the denotative logic of language. In a novel populated by characters who struggle to say what they mean, music offers an alternative: expression that does not depend on verbal precision.

## Manifestations in the Text

### The Rhythm of Labor

The most pervasive "music" in the novel is the rhythmic sound of Cash's carpentry — the saw, the hammer, the adze — which establishes a sonic ground-bass beneath the opening chapters. "We can hear the saw in the board. It sounds like snoring" [002-013]. This sound is not musical in the conventional sense, but it has music's qualities: rhythm, repetition, the ability to fill a space with meaning that exceeds any particular semantic content. Cash's sawing is his elegy, his hymn, his love song — a music of labor that expresses what he cannot say.

### Cora's Hymns

Cora Tull, the novel's most explicitly religious character, brings hymn-singing into the narrative as an expression of faith and a form of comfort. The hymns she references — unnamed but recognizable as the repertoire of rural Southern Protestantism — provide a framework for interpreting suffering that the novel simultaneously invokes and complicates. Cora's music is sincere but insufficient; it explains death as God's will but cannot account for the sheer material mess of Addie's decaying body or the family's grotesque journey.

### Sound and Meaning

The novel's broader attention to sound — the sound of the saw, the sound of the rain, the sound of the river, the sound of voices — participates in a musical sensibility that exceeds the specific motif of song. Faulkner's prose itself has a musical quality: its rhythms, its repetitions, its variations on thematic material. The novel can be read as a kind of verbal composition, a polyphonic arrangement of voices that operates according to musical rather than strictly narrative logic.

### Singing as Community

Brief references to communal singing — in church, at gatherings — suggest music's function as a social bond. Yet this communal music is largely absent from the Bundrens' experience; their journey isolates them from the communities where singing would occur. The novel's relative silence about music is thus itself meaningful: the Bundrens exist outside the sonic community that music creates, alone with the sounds of their own labor and suffering.

## Critical Interpretation

The musical dimension of Faulkner's prose has been extensively analyzed, with critics noting the influence of modernist musical aesthetics — particularly the contrapuntal techniques of composers like Bach — on Faulkner's narrative method. In *As I Lay Dying*, the fifteen different narrators can be heard as voices in a complex polyphonic composition, each with its own tonal register and rhythmic signature. The saw's steady rhythm, Cora's hymnodic cadences, Darl's lyrical flights, and Vardaman's percussive repetitions constitute a kind of vernacular symphony, a music of Mississippi that Faulkner transcribes into prose.
