# Body and Physicality

No modernist novel is more relentlessly embodied than *As I Lay Dying*. From [[addie-bundren|Addie's]] decaying corpse to [[cash-bundren|Cash's]] broken leg set in cement, from [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell's]] pregnancy to [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman's]] drilled fish, Faulkner insists on the body as the irreducible ground of experience — the site where consciousness, pain, labor, and language collide.

## The Body Against Language

Addie Bundren's monologue provides the novel's philosophical core on this theme: language is inadequate to bodily experience — the word "motherhood" can never capture the physical fact of childbirth; "love" is "just a shape to fill a lack." Throughout the novel, characters struggle to translate embodied reality into words, and Faulkner's stream-of-consciousness technique dramatizes this struggle by foregrounding sensory perception over abstract thought.
