# The Bundren Barn

The barn on the Bundren farm is one of the novel's most emotionally charged domestic spaces. Unlike the house — which is dominated by the fact of Addie's dying and the building of her coffin — the barn is a space of animal life, physical labor, and the private intensities that the house cannot contain. It is where Jewel tends his horse in secrecy, where Cash works when he is not at the coffin, and where the family's relationship to the land and its creatures is enacted in daily rhythms that persist even as the central crisis unfolds.

## Description

The barn is a wooden structure typical of rural Mississippi farmsteads. Faulkner describes it through sensory details: it is "dark in the barn, warm, smelling, silent" [013-022], a space of enclosure and interiority that contrasts with the open porch and the sweeping vista of the land. The barn contains stalls for the animals — the mules, the cow, and Jewel's horse — and houses the tools and feed of farm work. Its darkness and warmth make it a space of refuge as well as labor, a place where characters can be alone with their thoughts and their animals.

## Role in the Novel

The barn serves as the spatial counterpart to Addie's bedroom: both are enclosed, intimate spaces, but where the bedroom is the site of death and waiting, the barn is the site of life and action. Jewel's relationship with his horse — one of the novel's most intense emotional bonds — is centered in the barn, conducted in secrecy and at night, "so I could get up and go back to the barn" [003-003]. The barn is where Jewel expresses the love he cannot articulate in words, through the physical labor of caring for his horse.

The barn also functions as a space of separation from the family's collective crisis. When characters retreat to the barn, they enter a different temporal and emotional register — one governed by the rhythms of animals and labor rather than the pressure of Addie's dying and the impending journey.

## Key Scenes

### Jewel and the Horse

The barn is the stage for Jewel's secret nighttime labor with his horse, a ritual of care that Darl observes and narrates with characteristic perceptual intensity. "Inside the barn Jewel slides running to the ground" [003-011] — Darl's description captures the fluid, almost choreographic quality of Jewel's movements in this private space. The barn is where Jewel is most fully himself, freed from the family dynamics that constrain him in the house.

### The Morning of Departure

On the morning the journey begins, the barn is the site of departure preparations. Darl goes "down to the barn" [003-005] to harness the team; the animals are readied for the journey; the rhythms of farm life are suspended in favor of the journey's demands. The transition from barn to road marks the shift from the stationary, domestic world to the mobile, exposed world of the journey.

### Vardaman and the Cow

For Vardaman, the barn is associated with the cow — "The cow is standing in the barn door, chewing" [013-015] — an image of animal placidity that contrasts with the human turmoil surrounding Addie's death. Vardaman's relationship to the barn, like his relationship to the natural world more broadly, is sensory and immediate, unmediated by the abstractions that preoccupy the adult characters.

## Critical Interpretation

The barn in Faulkner's work often functions as what John T. Matthews called a "counter-domestic space" — a site of authentic being that stands in opposition to the house's social constraints. In *As I Lay Dying*, the barn is where the characters' private selves are most visible: Jewel's devotion, Vardaman's wonder, Darl's observational detachment. The barn's darkness and warmth make it a kind of unconscious space, a repository for the emotions and impulses that the house's domestic order cannot accommodate.
