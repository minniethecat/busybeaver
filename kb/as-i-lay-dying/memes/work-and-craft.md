# Work and Craft

[[cash-bundren|Cash Bundren]]'s coffin-building — meticulous, [[anse-bundren|pa]]tient, almost ritualistic — is the novel's emblem of work as a form of love. Against the chaos of grief, talk, and self-justification, [[cash-bundren|Cash]] offers the discipline of craft: a thing made well, for a purpose, without the need for [[language-and-meaning|words]].

## Overview

In a novel saturated with [[language-and-meaning|language]] — narrators speaking, explaining, justifying, interpreting — [[cash-bundren|Cash Bundren]] stands out for his silence and his making. His coffin for [[addie-bundren|Addie]] is built in full view of her [[death-and-dying|dying]], each stroke of the adze a wordless act of [[love|devotion]]. His [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle narrated chapter is a numbered list of reasons for building [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] "on the bevel" (018-010), a craftsman's technical justification that replaces emotional expression with professional precision. Work, for [[cash-bundren|Cash]], is not a distraction from grief but its proper form.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Cash: Craft as Devotion

[[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s relationship to work defines his character. He measures his coffin against an ideal of craft: whether the joints are tight, whether the bevel is correct, whether the balance is true. When [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] is nearly lost in [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]], his first thought is not for his [[motherhood|mother]]'s [[the-body|body]] but for his work: "It wasn't on a balance. I told them that if they wanted it to tote and ride on a balance, they would have to" (023-001). This may seem callous, but the novel suggests that [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s attention to craft *is* his grief — that making [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] well is the only way he knows to [[pride|honor]] his [[motherhood|mother]].

### Through Jewel: Work as Fury

[[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s work is not craft but force — the violent physical effort of manhandling the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] across [[the-river-crossing-location|the flooded river]], of saving [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] from the [[water-and-river|water]], of dragging the [[the-journey|journey]] forward through sheer will. Where [[cash-bundren|Cash]] works with [[anse-bundren|pa]]tient precision, [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] works with explosive energy. Both are forms of [[love|devotion]], but [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s is inarticulate, almost animal, expres[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g love through the [[the-body|body]] rather than through skill.

### Through Anse: The Evasion of Work

[[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]'s relationship to work is defined by avoidance. His famous self-description — "If He'd a aimed for me to be always a-moving and going, He'd a put me on wheels" — is the novel's comic counterpoint to [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s labor. [[anse-bundren|Anse]] talks about work, complains about work, justifies not working, but rarely does it. His final acquisition of new false teeth and a new wife in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] suggests that his [[the-journey|journey]], unlike his sons' labor, was always self-serving. In the novel's moral economy, the relationship to work is the relationship to truth.
