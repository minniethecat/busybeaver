# The Flood

The flood is the swelling of the river beyond its banks during the Bundrens' [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] in Faulkner's *As I Lay Dying*. What begins as a practical obstacle — the river is "up and still rising" [020-001] — becomes a symbolic trial that tests the family's resolve, claims the lives of [[the-mule-team|the mule team]], nearly drowns [[cash-bundren|Cash]], and provides the novel with its most concentrated sequence of action and peril. The flood transforms [[the-river-crossing-event|the river crossing]] from a ford into a life-threatening passage, and Faulkner uses it to crystallize the novel's themes of nature's indifference, divine judgment, and the cost of [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]'s promise to [[addie-bundren|Addie]].

## Summary

The flood is first reported by Quick, who tells the Bundrens that the river is rising and the bridge at [[the-washed-out-bridge|the washed-out bridge]] will soon be impassable [020-001]. By the time the family reaches the river, the water has transformed the familiar ford into a swollen torrent — "you couldn't tell where was the river and where the land" [031-003]. The family attempts the crossing with a borrowed team of mules; the wagon is swept sideways, [[cash-bundren|Cash]] is thrown into the water with his broken leg, [[the-mule-team|the mules]] drown, and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] — riding his horse in the torrent — becomes the family's improbable savior. The wagon is eventually recovered, the coffin secured, and [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s leg is further damaged, but the family survives to continue the journey.

## Narrative Structure

Faulkner distributes the flood across multiple narrators, creating a fragmented, multi-perspective account:

- **Tull** (ch020, ch028): Reports the rising river, watches the Bundrens prepare, and later recounts what he witnessed from the bank.

- **Darl** (ch031, ch034): Provides the most vivid sensory description of the crossing itself, including the wagon's loss of control and Jewel's heroism.

- **Vardaman** (ch035): Offers a child's-eye view of the chaos, focused on his mother's fish-body in the water.

- **Cora / Addie / Whitfield** (ch037–ch041): Provide retrospective religious interpretations — the flood as divine trial, as baptism, as judgment.

This narrative dispersal is characteristic of Faulkner's technique in the novel: no single account is authoritative, and the flood's meaning shifts depending on who remembers it.

## Significance

The flood is the novel's central test. It is simultaneously a natural event (heavy rains swelling the river), a practical crisis (the loss of the mules and near-loss of the wagon), and a symbolic ordeal. For [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], the flood is evidence of divine will — God testing or punishing. For [[whitfield|Whitfield]], it is the instrument of his near-death and false repentance: "He that bore me safely above the flood, that fended from me the dangers of the waters" [041-003]. For [[darl-bundren|Darl]], it is a moment of pure perception — the chaotic intersection of water, wood, flesh, and will — and his narration captures it with hallucinatory precision.

The flood also reveals the characters' essential natures: [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] acts without hesitation, [[darl-bundren|Darl]] observes and describes, [[anse-bundren|Anse]] stands on the bank and watches others risk themselves, [[cash-bundren|Cash]] endures suffering without complaint.

## The Mule Team

The drowning of [[the-mule-team|the mule team]] is the flood's most concrete cost. The mules, which the Bundrens could not afford to lose and cannot afford to replace, are swept away "rolling on the current in a succession of troughs and hillocks" [034-052]. Their death is both a practical catastrophe (forcing [[anse-bundren|Anse]] into a transaction with [[snopes|Snopes]] for a new team) and a symbolic sacrifice — the animals die so that [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s corpse may continue its journey.
