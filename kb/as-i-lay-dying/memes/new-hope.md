# New Hope

New Hope is a rural church and community in the Yokna[[anse-bundren|pa]]tawpha County of William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_, located approximately three miles from the Bundren farm. It functions throughout the novel as the [[the-journey|road]] not taken — a nearer, more practical burial site for [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]] that is repeatedly proposed and just as repeatedly rejected in favor of the distant goal of [[jefferson|Jefferson]].

## Description

New Hope is identified primarily by its church and its signboards along the [[the-journey|road]]. The signboard appears twice in the narrative: once as a [[anse-bundren|pa]]s[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g marker in [[vernon-tull|Tull]]'s chapter ("New Hope Church. 3 mi." [027-003]), and again — more memorably — in [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s chapter, where it becomes an object of obsessive, grief-haunted attention:

> The signboard comes in sight. It is looking out at the road now, because it can wait. New Hope. 3 mi. it will say. New Hope. 3 mi. [030-001]

The signboard's temporal shift — from future ("it will say") to [[anse-bundren|pa]]st ("Was 3 mi. Was 3 mi." [030-003]) — marks the [[the-bundren-wagon|Bundren wagon]]'s [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage beyond the alternative. New Hope dwindles from possibility to memory in the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]ce of a sentence.

The church itself appears in [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s brief reference during the initial [[the-journey|journey]] planning: "His folks buries at New Hope, too, not three miles away" [008-004] — situating New Hope as the Bundren family's own burial ground, which makes [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s insistence on [[jefferson|Jefferson]] all the more perverse.

## Role in the Novel

New Hope serves as the novel's s[[anse-bundren|pa]]tial [[the-body|embodiment]] of the reasonable alternative. It is proposed no fewer than four times across the [[the-journey|journey]]:

1. **[[cash-bundren|Cash]]** notes the proximity and family connection early on: the Bundrens bury at New Hope [008-004]

2. **[[armstid|Armstid]]** urges [[anse-bundren|Anse]] to change course: "He better go on and bury her at New Hope" [020-003]

3. **[[samson|Samson]]** advises the Bundrens to return to New Hope rather than continue toward the [[the-washed-out-bridge|washed-out bridge]] [029-012]

4. **[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]** and **[[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]** register New Hope's [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage as a moment of irrevocable loss — [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s [[the-journey|quest]]ion "Why didn't we go to New Hope, [[anse-bundren|pa]]?" [030-004] goes unanswered

Each proposal is refused by [[anse-bundren|Anse]] with the same implacable formulation: "I give her my [[family-duty|promise]]. Her mind was set on it." The repeated invocation of New Hope thus serves as a narrative counterweight — the voice of community, practicality, and common sense — against which [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s stubbornness is measured and found incomprehensible.

## Key Scenes

### The Signboard (Dewey Dell, ch030)

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s interior monologue as the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] [[anse-bundren|pa]]sses the New Hope signboard is one of the novel's most compressed expressions of grief. The signboard "looking out at the [[the-journey|road]] now, because it can wait" [030-001] registers as reproach — New Hope can wait, but [[addie-bundren|Addie]] cannot. The transformation of the sign's message from future to [[anse-bundren|pa]]st tense ("New Hope three miles" → "Was 3 mi.") enacts the [[the-journey|journey]]'s irreversible forward movement away from the practical, the local, the familial.

### Armstid's Appeal (ch020)

At [[armstid|Armstid]]'s farm, the assembled neighbors discuss [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]] and its dangers. [[armstid|Armstid]]'s suggestion — "He better go on and bury her at New Hope. That bridge is old. I wouldn't monkey with it" [020-003] — is the voice of rural experience. Quick's response — "His mind is set on taking her to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]" [020-004] — highlights the gap between community wisdom and [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s monomania.
