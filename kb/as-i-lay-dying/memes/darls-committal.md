# Darl's Committal to Jackson

The arrest and committal of [[darl-bundren|Darl Bundren]] to the state asylum in Jackson, [[jefferson|Mississippi]], following his attempt to burn [[gillespie|Gillespie]]'s barn with [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin inside. This event — narrated in [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s final monologues and witnessed by [[cash-bundren|Cash]] and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] — is the novel's emotional and structural climax, in which the family collectively sacrifices its most perceptive member to preserve the [[the-journey|journey]]'s momentum.

## Build-up: The Barn Fire

By the time the Bundrens reach [[gillespie|Gillespie]]'s farm, [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[the-body|body]] has been decompo[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g for over a week. The stench precedes the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] through every [[jefferson|town]]; the family is refused shelter and treated as a spectacle. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] — who alone among the Bundrens fully comprehends the grotesquerie of the [[the-journey|journey]] — sets fire to the barn where [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] is stored for the night, attempting to give his [[motherhood|mother]] a proper cremation rather than letting her rot incrementally across the [[jefferson|Mississippi]] countryside.

[[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], sleeping a[[anse-bundren|pa]]rt from the family, is alerted to the flames and charges into the burning barn to rescue [[addies-coffin|the coffin]], carrying it out on his back in a moment of spectacular physical heroism. The contrast between [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s philosophical desperation and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s unthinking physical action is one of the novel's sharpest character oppositions.

## The Arrest

[[darl-bundren|Darl]] is identified as the arsonist by [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]], who witnessed him set [[darl-burns-the-barn|the fire]], and by [[gillespie|Gillespie]], who finds evidence at the scene. Rather than face criminal prosecution — which would delay the [[the-journey|journey]] indefinitely — the family agrees to have [[darl-bundren|Darl]] committed to the state asylum. [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s role in this decision is characteristically [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssive: he does not initiate the committal but does nothing to prevent it.

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] — who has feared [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s knowing gaze [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]ce his confrontation with her about her pregnancy in chapter 10 — attacks him physically during the arrest, scratching and clawing at him in an eruption of pent-up terror. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], who has just saved [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] from [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s fire, [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtici[[anse-bundren|pa]]tes in subduing his brother.

## Darl's Final Monologues

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s two final chapters (ch051 and ch053) are among the most remarkable in the novel. In chapter 51, he describes his own actions in the third person — "[[darl-bundren|Darl]] has gone to Jackson. They put him on the t[[water-and-river|rain]], laughing" — as if his consciousness has already detached from his [[the-body|body]], observing himself from outside. In chapter 53, the fragmentation is complete: [[darl-bundren|Darl]] narrates his [[the-journey|journey]] to Jackson on the t[[water-and-river|rain]], laughing without reason, his voice dissolving into the third person permanently: "[[darl-bundren|Darl]] is our brother, our brother [[darl-bundren|Darl]]. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is going to Jackson. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is my brother."

[[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s final [[sin-and-judgment|judgment]] — "This world is not his world; this life his life" — registers both sym[[anse-bundren|pa]]thy for [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and complicity in his removal. The family that has endured [[water-and-river|flood]], fire, and public humiliation cannot endure [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s seeing.

## Thematic Analysis

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s committal is the novel's most concentrated statement about the relationship between perception and survival. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] sees too much — his clairvoyant narrative power, which opens the novel as a gift (he narrates [[addies-death|Addie's death]] despite not being present), becomes by the end a liability that must be removed. The family's choice is between [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s truth and the [[the-journey|journey]]'s continuation; they choose the [[the-journey|journey]].

The committal also completes the novel's arc of substitution: [[addie-bundren|Addie]] in [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] is replaced by the new Mrs. Bundren, the [[the-mule-team|drowned mules]] are replaced by a new team, and [[darl-bundren|Darl]] — the son who most resembles his [[motherhood|mother]] in his refusal of easy [[language-and-meaning|language]] — is replaced by silence.
