# Lafe

A farmhand who appears in exactly zero scenes yet drives one of the novel's most consequential plots. Lafe impregnated [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell Bundren]] during the cotton harvest, gave her ten dollars, and told her she could "get something at the drugstore" — an instruction that sends her on a hidden [[anse-bundren|pa]]rallel [[the-journey|journey]] alongside the family's funeral procession. He never speaks in his own voice, is never described by any narrator other than [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], and by the novel's events has vanished entirely. His absence is his power.

## Role in the Novel

Lafe is the catalyst of [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s secret mission. While the Bundren family [[the-journey|travel]]s to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] to bury [[addie-bundren|Addie]], [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] carries a second, hidden purpose: to terminate Lafe's pregnancy. The novel never names this pregnancy directly — [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s condition is communicated entirely through indirection, the euphemism of "female trouble," and the mantra "Lafe said I could get something at the drugstore."

His structural function is to em[[the-body|body]] the gendered asymmetry of rural sexual consequence. He impregnates her and disappears. She carries the pregnancy alone, keeps the secret alone, attempts to terminate alone, and is exploited by Macgowan alone. Lafe's physical absence is the structural fact of her narrative.

## The Cotton Row [007]

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s memory of Lafe is concentrated in a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle scene, narrated in her first chapter. The two of them are picking cotton, moving down a row toward the woods:

> "We picked on down the row, the woods getting closer and closer and the secret shade, picking on into the secret shade with my sack and Life's sack." [007-002]

The scene is structured as an e[[work-and-craft|labor]]ate psychological drama of choice and compulsion. [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] sets herself a test: if the sack is full when they reach the woods, "it will be that I had to do it all the time and I cannot help it." Lafe, understanding the game, responds in kind:

> "I said 'What are you doing?' and he said 'I am picking into your sack.' And so it was full when we came to the end of the row and I could not help it." [007-002]

The cotton sack is a sustained sexual metaphor — "picking into your sack" carries an explicit double [[language-and-meaning|meaning]]. The scene reveals Lafe not as a rapist but as a knowing [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtici[[anse-bundren|pa]]nt in [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s ritual of self-absolution: he helps her construct the fiction that the act was inevitable.

## "I Am Lafe's Guts" [014]

In her second chapter, [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] reduces her condition to its most brutal biological terms:

> "He is his guts and I am my guts. And I am Lafe's guts. That's it." [014-011]

This is the novel's most radical statement of female bodily [[isolation|alienation]]. [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] defines herself not through autonomy but as an extension of Lafe's [[the-body|body]] — she is his guts, his reproductive matter, carrying his child. [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s knowledge of the affair "came in between me and Lafe" [014-003], se[[anse-bundren|pa]]rating them rather than joining them. The consequence is mutual isolation: "Lafe is alone too" [014-003].

The incantatory repetition of his name — "Lafe. Lafe. 'Lafe' Lafe. Lafe" [014-025] — functions as a verbal talisman, a word that both summons and exorcises the absent father.

## The Drugstore Mantra [045, 055]

Lafe's most consequential act is a simple instruction: "Lafe said I could get it at the drugstore" [045-032]. This phrase becomes [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s mantra, her article of [[religion-and-faith|faith]], her explanation to every authority figure who stands between her and her goal.

In [[mottson|Mottson]], the druggist Moseley refuses her and offers a devastating reading of Lafe's character: "if he ain't halfway to Texas by now, which I don't doubt" [045-033]. He tells her to "go on back to Lafe and you and him take that ten dollars and get married with it" [045-037] — advice as useless as it is conventional.

In [[jefferson|Jefferson]], [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] tries again. She tells Macgowan, the pharmacy assistant who poses as a doctor: "He told me I could get something. He told me I could get it at the drugstore" [055-054]. Macgowan exploits her desperation, giving her talcum-powder capsules and demanding sex in the cellar as "the rest of the treatment." The "Lafe said" mantra has propelled her from one man's exploitation directly into another's.

## The Absent Presence

Lafe is the novel's most structurally significant absent presence. He appears in zero scenes, speaks zero lines, and is present only in [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s memory and in her repeated citation of his [[language-and-meaning|words]]. Faulkner's decision to deny Lafe a voice or a scene is a formal choice: by making his absence absolute, the novel denies the reader any possibility of understanding his motives, feelings, or fate. He is what he did, and he is gone.

This absence creates a structural [[anse-bundren|pa]]rallel with [[whitfield|Whitfield]], the minister who fathered [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] with [[addie-bundren|Addie]]. Both men sleep with Bundren women and then absent themselves from the consequences. But where [[whitfield|Whitfield]] gets an entire chapter to rationalize his [[the-cow|cow]]ardice in biblical rhetoric, Lafe is denied even that consolation. He is a blank, a name, a force that acts and vanishes — leaving a seventeen-year-old girl to navigate pregnancy, shame, and sexual predation alone.
