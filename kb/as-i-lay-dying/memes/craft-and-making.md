# Craft and Making

**Craft and making** in *As I Lay Dying* center on the figure of Cash Bundren, whose meticulous construction of his mother's coffin is the novel's primary image of skilled labor. Through the sound and rhythm of Cash's saw and adze, Faulkner transforms physical work into a meditation on love, grief, and the human relationship to material things.

## The Coffin as Act of Devotion

Cash builds Addie's coffin beneath her window as she lies dying — a fact that outrages some characters but represents, for Cash, the only response to death he knows:

> "A good carpenter. Addie Bundren could not want a better one, a better box to lie in."

The construction is described with technical precision. Cash works "on the bevel" — cutting the planks at an angle so the joints fit more securely — and his reasoning is laid out in a numbered list, one of the novel's rare moments of rational, ordered thought amid the chaos of consciousness:

> "I made it on the bevel. / 1. There is more surface for the nails to grip. / ... / 12. So I made it on the bevel."

## The Sound of Work

Cash's labor is experienced by the other characters primarily as *sound* — the steady rhythm of sawing that threads through the early chapters like a metronome:

> "The sound of the saw is steady, competent, unhurried, stirring the dying light so that at each stroke her face seems to wake a little."

The sawing becomes unbearable to Jewel, who hears in it a kind of selfish display: "See what a good one I am making for you." But the novel does not resolve this ambiguity — Cash's craftsmanship may be devotion, vanity, or simply the only language he has.

## Work as Moral Order

Throughout the novel, characters are defined by their relationship to work. Cash works continuously. Anse avoids work at every turn, justifying his idleness with folk wisdom: "If he'd ever sweat, he'd die." Jewel works secretly, at night, to earn his horse. The Tulls and Armstids work the land with the competence of settled farmers.

Cash's coffin-making thus becomes the novel's moral center of gravity — an act of making that stands against the entropy and dissolution that threaten the Bundrens at every turn.
