# Eula

A young woman who appears in two early chapters of William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_, present at [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]'s [[death-and-dying|death]]bed vigil and on the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] ride back from [[jefferson|town]]. Eula's most distinctive moment is a silent, revealing glance at [[darl-bundren|Darl Bundren]] — one of the novel's earliest signals of the erotic tensions that circulate beneath its surface of rural propriety.

## Role in the Novel

Eula appears in [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]]'s first narration (ch002) and [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s subsequent chapter (ch008). She is a watcher — she speaks only one line in the entire novel — but her silent observation of [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is freighted with significance. Faulkner uses her as a lens through which [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s unsettling presence is registered by the community. [002-013] [008-044]

## Biography

Eula is identified by her "necklace" and "red hat," purchased for twenty-five cents — markers of a young woman attentive to appearance within the const[[water-and-river|rain]]ts of rural poverty. She is almost certainly unmarried, given her presence among the [[vernon-tull|Tull]] women rather than with a husband. [[cora-tull|Cora]] notes that "her necklace looks real nice with her red hat. You wouldn't think it only cost twenty-five cents" [002-013], a detail that simultaneously describes Eula's self-presentation and the economic gaze through which the community assesses it.

## The Gaze at Darl

Eula's most narratively charged moment occurs when [[darl-bundren|Darl]] [[anse-bundren|pa]]sses through the room where the women are keeping vigil. The [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage is brief but carefully rendered:

> Someone comes through the hall. It is Darl. He does not look in as he passes the door. Eula watches him as he goes on and passes from sight again toward the back. Her hand rises and touches her beads lightly, and then her hair. When she finds me watching her, her eyes go blank. [002-016]

The sequence — [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s refusal to look in, Eula's involuntary tracking of his [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage, the self-conscious touching of beads and hair, the sudden blankness when caught — compresses an entire drama of desire, self-awareness, and community surveillance into three sentences. Eula's "blank" eyes when she realizes [[cora-tull|Cora]] is watching her mark the point where private desire meets public visibility.

On the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] ride home, Eula speaks her [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle line: "I reckon [[cash-bundren|Cash]] and [[darl-bundren|Darl]] can get married now" [008-044], following [[kate|Kate]]'s speculation about [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s remarriage. The remark conflates [[addies-death|Addie's death]] with the liberation of her sons into marriageability — a blunt, practical reading of [[death-and-dying|death]]'s social function.
