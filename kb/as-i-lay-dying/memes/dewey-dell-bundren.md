# Dewey Dell Bundren

The fourth Bundren child and only daughter, a seventeen-year-old narrator whose four interior monologues reveal an unwanted pregnancy by a farmhand named [[lafe|Lafe]], and her desperate, ultimately futile attempts to obtain an abortion in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] under the cover of the family's burial [[the-journey|journey]].

## Role in the Novel

Dewey Dell occupies a structurally unique position in the novel: she is both a [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtici[[anse-bundren|pa]]nt in the family's collective [[the-journey|journey]] and the keeper of a secret [[the-journey|journey]] of her own. Her pregnancy — unknown to any family member except [[darl-bundren|Darl]], who intuits it without being told — creates a second, hidden motivation for reaching [[jefferson|Jefferson]] that mirrors and undermines the family's stated purpose of burying [[addie-bundren|Addie]] (P03-031). She is the only female Bundren child and the only daughter to survive [[addie-bundren|Addie]]; her narrative chapters are among the most physically visceral in the novel, preoccupied with the [[the-body|body]] — her [[motherhood|mother]]'s [[death-and-dying|dying]] [[the-body|body]], her own pregnant [[the-body|body]], the animal bodies of the [[the-cow|cow]] and [[vardamans-fish|the fish]] (014-003).

## Biography

Dewey Dell is seventeen years old at the time of [[addies-death|Addie's death]]. Her life before the novel's events is s[[anse-bundren|pa]]rsely drawn: she has been [[work-and-craft|work]]ing on the Bundren farm, where she met [[lafe|Lafe]], a farmhand who impregnated her during cotton-picking season. The pregnancy is a source of terror and isolation — she cannot tell her father [[anse-bundren|Anse]], her brothers, or the dead [[addie-bundren|Addie]], and her attempts to terminate the pregnancy structure her entire experience of the [[the-journey|journey]] (030-001). Her relationship with [[addie-bundren|Addie]] is complex: in the days before [[addies-death|Addie's death]], Dewey Dell [[dewey-dells-fan|fan]]s her [[motherhood|mother]] continuously, a [[family-duty|duty]] that [[darl-bundren|Darl]] perceives as motivated by a wish for [[death-and-dying|death]] to come faster so she can reach the druggist in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] (010-004).

## Narrative Voice

Dewey Dell's four monologues (chapters 7, 14, 30, and 34) are characterized by physical immediacy and a striking conflation of [[the-body|body]] and landscape. Her [[language-and-meaning|language]] resists abstraction; she experiences pregnancy not as a social condition but as an organic invasion: "I am a little tub of guts and there is room for something else very small laying quiet and wait" (030-001). Her stream of consciousness is marked by repetition of the word "[[lafe|Lafe]]" as a mantra, s[[anse-bundren|pa]]tial metaphors of inside/outside, and a recursive return to the [[the-cow|cow]] as an emblem of bodily distress. Her perception of [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is uniquely acute — she knows he "knows" without being told, and this knowledge becomes unbearable, contributing to her eventual attack on him at [[gillespie|Gillespie]]'s barn (P03-059).

## Key Relationships

**[[darl-bundren|Darl Bundren]]**: [[darl-bundren|Darl]] intuits Dewey Dell's pregnancy without being told, and his knowing becomes a threat. In chapter 10, he confronts her: "You want her to die so you can get to [[jefferson|town]]: is that it?" (010-004). Dewey Dell's fear of exposure culminates in her violent attack on [[darl-bundren|Darl]] when he is seized for the asylum: she throws herself on him, scratching and clawing, desperate to silence the brother who sees too much (P03-059).

**[[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]**: Dewey Dell's relationship with her [[death-and-dying|dying]] [[motherhood|mother]] is defined by physical proximity and emotional distance. She [[dewey-dells-fan|fan]]s [[addie-bundren|Addie]] for ten days, an act of [[family-duty|duty]] that [[darl-bundren|Darl]] reads as a wish for [[death-and-dying|death]] (004-002). At the moment of [[addies-death|Addie's death]], Dewey Dell flings herself across the [[the-body|body]] with "the furious strength of the young" (012-008, 012-016, 012-017).

**[[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman Bundren]]**: As the two youngest Bundrens, Dewey Dell and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] share a s[[anse-bundren|pa]]tial and narrative proximity — but [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s grief over their [[addies-death|mother's death]] is explicitly rendered, while Dewey Dell's interior state remains focused on the pregnancy, creating a contrast between childhood grief and adolescent bodily crisis.

## Critical Reception

Dewey Dell has been read by feminist critics as Faulkner's most sustained exploration of female [[the-body|embodiment]] and reproductive autonomy — or its absence. Her chapters, [[anse-bundren|pa]]rticularly chapter 30 with its circular, incantatory prose, are frequently cited as exemplary of Faulkner's ability to render consciousness shaped by physical experience rather than abstract thought. The pharmacy scene in [[jefferson|Jefferson]], in which she is sexually exploited by Skeet MacGowan po[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g as a doctor, has been analyzed as a critique of rural women's vulnerability in a medical system that denied them reproductive agency.

> "Dewey Dell is the novel's most corporeal consciousness — her body is not something she has but something she is, and Faulkner's prose registers this with extraordinary precision." — André Bleikasten, *The Ink of Melancholy* (1990)
