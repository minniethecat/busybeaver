# Littlejohn's

Littlejohn's is a farm and homestead that serves as a gathering place for the rural community along the Bundrens' [[the-journey|journey]] route to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] in Faulkner's *As I Lay Dying*. It functions primarily as the setting for the chapter narrated by [[armstid|Armstid]] (ch020), where local farmers including [[armstid|Armstid]] and Littlejohn himself discuss [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]'s improbable quest to bury [[addie-bundren|Addie]] in Jefferson. Littlejohn's store represents the collective voice of the Yoknapatawpha farming community — a chorus of practical, earthy commentary on the Bundrens' increasingly grotesque procession.

## Description

Littlejohn's is a working farmstead with a store where local men gather. The chapter set there establishes it as one of the informal social centers of the rural community — a place where news circulates, judgments are passed, and the rhythms of farm life are observed between laconic conversations [020-036]. [[armstid|Armstid]]'s narration mentions the "team" at Littlejohn's [043-003], indicating that the farm keeps draft animals and serves as a resource for neighboring farmers.

## Role in the Novel

Littlejohn's provides the novel's first extended external perspective on the Bundren family. Through [[armstid|Armstid]]'s narration and Littlejohn's deadpan commentary, the reader sees the Bundrens as their neighbors see them: stubborn, peculiar, and engaged in a funeral journey that has become a public spectacle. Littlejohn's repeated refrain — "It's a fact" [020-036], [020-044] — becomes a kind of communal verdict on the absurdity unfolding around them. His observation that "a fellow with a broke bone always feels it" [020-050] is both practical farm wisdom and a quiet indictment of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s indifference to [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s suffering.

The store also serves as an information node. It is at Littlejohn's that [[armstid|Armstid]] tells the Bundrens about [[the-washed-out-bridge|the washed-out bridge]] [020-003], and the men discuss the weather, the river, and the feasibility of the journey — all the practical realities that the Bundrens seem to ignore. Littlejohn's is the voice of the grounded world against which the Bundrens' obsessive quest is measured.

## The Littlejohn Character

Littlejohn himself appears only through [[armstid|Armstid]]'s reported speech, but his voice is distinctive: laconic, self-assured, and gently sardonic. His "It's a fact" is less agreement than a verbal tic that closes off further discussion — a way of saying the truth is plain and needs no elaboration. He has told [[armstid|Armstid]] about the levee [043-003], indicating he is a source of local knowledge about terrain and conditions, part of the informal network of information that the Bundrens rely on (and ignore) during their journey.

## Key Scene: The Gathering

Chapter 20, narrated by [[armstid|Armstid]], takes place entirely at Littlejohn's. The men discuss [[the-washed-out-bridge|the bridge]], the weather, [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s broken leg, and the Bundrens' journey. The chapter's structure — a series of elliptical, half-overheard exchanges — captures the rhythm of rural conversation. The farmers understand more than they say, and their restrained commentary functions as the novel's moral baseline: these are people who know what a broken leg costs, what weather means for travel, and what a man owes his family.
