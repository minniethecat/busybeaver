# Armstid

A Yokna[[anse-bundren|pa]]tawpha farmer whose [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle narrative chapter [043] is one of the novel's most psychologically acute accounts of [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]'s strange power — the way a shiftless, helpless man "conjures" others into doing for him. Armstid provides the Bundrens with shelter, food, and ultimately his team of [[the-mule-team|mules]] after the catastrophic [[the-river-crossing-event|river crossing]], all while maintaining a clear-eyed, self-aware recognition that he is being exploited.

## Role in the Novel

Armstid belongs to the novel's chorus of community witnesses — farmers and [[jefferson|town]]speople who observe the Bundren catastrophe from the outside and react with varying combinations of horror, charity, and pragmatic help. He is present at the funeral gathering before the [[the-journey|journey]] begins [020], hosts the Bundrens after [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] [042–043], and is later referenced as one of the sensible voices whose advice [[anse-bundren|Anse]] ignored [052–054].

Among the community narrators, Armstid represents the most grounded response to [[anse-bundren|Anse]]. Where [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]] offers sanctimonious piety and [[samson|Samson]] offers exasperated bafflement, Armstid articulates a wry, self-aware form of reluctant charity: he knows he is being used, he resents it, and he helps anyway.

## Biography

Armstid is a subsistence farmer living somewhere between Frenchman's Bend and the route to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. He is married to Lula Armstid, who is outraged by the decaying corpse in their barn shed [043-045–049]. He appears in multiple Faulkner [[work-and-craft|work]]s as a recurring Yokna[[anse-bundren|pa]]tawpha character — a poor white farmer whose encounters with the Bundrens, the Snopeses, and other county figures place him at the center of Faulkner's rural social world.

He owns a team of [[the-mule-team|mules]], a fact he mentions with grim foresight when he sees [[anse-bundren|Anse]] scheming: "I looked at my [[the-mule-team|mules]] and same as told them goodbye for a spell" [043-033]. His prediction proves accurate — [[anse-bundren|Anse]] trades [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s horse to Snopes for a team, and Armstid's [[the-mule-team|mules]] are pressed into service to pull the [[the-bundren-wagon|Bundren wagon]] onward.

## Narrative Voice

Armstid's chapter [043] is a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle sustained monologue, ranging from the Bundrens' arrival through [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s midnight ride to return [[jewels-horse|the horse]]. His voice is plainspoken, rural, and characterized by a dry, self-deprecating humor:

> "I be durn if there ain't something about a durn fellow like Anse that seems to make a man have to help him, even when he knows he'll be wanting to kick himself next minute." [043-067]

The chapter ends on this note of exasperated wonder: "I be durn if [[anse-bundren|Anse]] don't conjure a man, some way. I be durn if he ain't a sight" [043-072]. Armstid's insight — that [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s helplessness is itself a form of power — is one of the novel's most penetrating observations about the dynamics of rural mutual [[family-duty|obligation]].

[[darl-bundren|Darl]], in the preceding chapter, provides an indelible physical description: Armstid has a "wooden look on his face... that bold, surly, high-coloured rigid look like his face and eyes were two colours of wood, the wrong one [[anse-bundren|pa]]le and the wrong one dark" [042-010].

## Key Relationships

**[[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]**: Armstid regards [[anse-bundren|Anse]] with a mixture of contempt, exasperation, and grudging awe. He sees [[anse-bundren|Anse]] as shiftless and manipulative but concedes [[anse-bundren|Anse]] has an almost supernatural ability to extract help: "He's getting her into the ground the best he can" [043-019] is Armstid's defensive explanation to his outraged wife, even as he knows the defense is inadequate.

**Lula Armstid**: The domestic tension between Armstid and his wife structures the chapter. Lula is horrified by the corpse in the barn: "It's a outrage. He should be lawed for treating her so" [043-046]. Armstid repeatedly defends [[anse-bundren|Anse]] to her, creating a marital friction that dramatizes the community's divided response.

**[[cash-bundren|Cash Bundren]]**: Armstid shows practical concern for [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s [[cashs-broken-leg|broken leg]], helping arrange Uncle Billy's horse-physic treatment. [[peabody|Peabody]] later blames Armstid for letting [[cash-bundren|Cash]] [[the-journey|travel]]: "What in hell did Armstid mean by even letting them put you on that [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] again?" [054-003]. As [[cash-bundren|Cash]] replies, "We never had time to wait" [054-004].

**[[jewel-bundren|Jewel Bundren]]**: Armstid witnesses [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s fury when his horse is traded and admires [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s strength in the aftermath. He does not blame [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] for de[[anse-bundren|pa]]rting into the night: "I be durn if I could blame him. Not for wanting to not give up his horse, but for getting shut of such a durn fool as [[anse-bundren|Anse]]" [043-058].

## Key Scenes

- **The funeral gathering [020]**: Armstid advises [[anse-bundren|Anse]] to bury [[addie-bundren|Addie]] at [[new-hope|New Hope]] rather than attempt [[the-river-crossing-location|the flooded river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]]. "That bridge is old. I wouldn't monkey with it" [020-003]. His practical warnings are ignored.

- **Arrival at [[bundren-farm|the farm]] [042]**: After [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]], the Bundrens arrive at Armstid's gate. Armstid repeatedly offers the hospitality of his house — "You're welcome to the house" [042-008] — which [[anse-bundren|Anse]] refuses, keeping the [[the-body|body]] in the barn shed.

- **The overnight stay [043]**: Armstid's chapter chronicles the long night: Uncle Billy sets [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]], the [[the-buzzards|buzzards]] arrive, Lula erupts in outrage, [[anse-bundren|Anse]] trades [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s horse, and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] rides off into the darkness. Armstid's wry narration holds these elements in uneasy balance.

- **The [[the-mule-team|mules]] [043-043]**: When [[anse-bundren|Anse]] returns with the news that he has "bought a team," Armstid recognizes the inevitable — his own team has been drafted into Bundren service. His foreknowledge of this outcome ("I looked at my [[the-mule-team|mules]] and same as told them goodbye") is the chapter's darkly comic center.
