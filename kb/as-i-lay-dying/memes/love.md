# Love

In *As I Lay Dying*, love is the emotion that characters most urgently need and least reliably possess. Faulkner examines love not as a stable sentiment but as a contested site — claimed by [[religion-and-faith|religion]], demanded by family, distorted by pride, and sometimes indistinguishable from violence or absence.

## Overview

The novel asks what it means to love, and whether love can exist without expression or recognition. [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s devastating confession — "I cannot love my [[motherhood|mother]] because I have no [[motherhood|mother]]" (021-003) — suggests that love requires not just a subject but an object: one cannot love what one has never truly known. [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], by contrast, inhabits a world saturated with love — God's love, husbandly love, [[motherhood|maternal]] love — yet her love is so tightly bound to her theological frame[[work-and-craft|work]] that it becomes a form of [[sin-and-judgment|judgment]]. [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s posthumous narration reveals a woman who loved [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] with a fierce [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtiality that excluded her other children, a love described not as tenderness but as compulsion: "she had to love him so that she had to act the way she did" (032-018).

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Cora Tull: Love as Religious Performance

[[cora-tull|Cora]]'s conception of love is inse[[anse-bundren|pa]]rable from her [[religion-and-faith|Christianity]]. She speaks of "the bounteous love of the Lord" (006-012) as a demonstrated fact, and her own love for her family is framed in similar terms of [[family-duty|duty]] and reward: she expects "the love and respect of my Christian children" (006-011) as an entitlement earned through righteous living. This transactional model of love — love given in expectation of love returned — contrasts sharply with the Bundrens' inarticulate, often destructive attachments. [[cora-tull|Cora]] loves correctly, by the book; the Bundrens love badly, but perhaps more authentically.

### Through Addie: Love as Partiality and Punishment

[[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s love is exclusive and fierce. Her preference for [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] over her other children is not a secret — it structures the entire family dynamic. Her posthumous narration reveals that this [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtiality was itself a form of punishment: [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] "never loved her and was its own punishment" (039-002); she loved him precisely because he refused to love her back, recognizing in his resistance a quality of self-sufficiency she valued above the dutiful affection of [[cash-bundren|Cash]] or the probing intelligence of [[darl-bundren|Darl]]. This makes [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s love not a gift but a wound, inflicted on both the loved and the unloved.

### Through Darl: The Inability to Love

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s emotional estrangement — his sense that his [[motherhood|mother]] was never really present to him — raises the [[the-journey|quest]]ion of whether love is even possible when the self feels unreal. His narrative gifts (insight into others' thoughts, poetic perception) coexist with an emotional void at his center. The novel suggests that [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s inability to love is not a moral failure but an ontological one: you cannot give what you do not have. His final breakdown in the asylum represents the logical endpoint of a self that was never anchored by being loved.
