# Silence and Voice

The interplay of silence and voice in *As I Lay Dying* is both a structural principle — governing who narrates and who does not — and a thematic concern about the limits of language and the things that cannot be spoken aloud in the world of the novel.

## Overview

Faulkner distributes voice unevenly across his characters: Darl Bundren narrates nineteen chapters, while his mother Addie — whose death drives the entire plot — is given only a single monologue, spoken from beyond the grave. Jewel Bundren, the most physically active character, has only one brief chapter.

This asymmetrical distribution of narrative voice foregrounds questions about who is permitted to speak, what can be said, and what remains in the silence between words. Silence in the novel is not merely the absence of speech but a charged presence — the space where grief, shame, and the incommunicable reside.

Addie Bundren's philosophy of language, articulated in her sole chapter, provides the novel's theoretical framework for understanding this tension:

> I would think how words go straight up in a thin line, quick and harmless, and how terribly doing goes along the earth, clinging to it, so that after a while the two lines are too far apart for the same person to straddle from one to the other; and that sin and love and fear are just sounds that people who never sinned nor loved nor feared have for what they never had and cannot have until they forget the words. [040-021]

Words rise weightlessly upward while deeds cling terribly to the earth — a distinction that organizes the entire polyphonic structure. The fifteen narrators do not so much communicate as broadcast their separate consciousnesses, each voice a thin line rising into air that never intersects with another. Faulkner's refusal of an omniscient narrator embodies Addie's thesis: the truest things resist articulation.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Addie Bundren: The Silent Center

Addie Bundren's sole monologue chapter is the structural and philosophical center of the novel's treatment of silence and voice. Throughout her life, she was described by others as "a private woman" (005-017), and her interior life remained inaccessible to her family. Her chapter breaks the silence of death itself, and within it she articulates the most sustained philosophy of language in the novel:

> That was when I learned that words are no good; that words don't ever fit even what they are trying to say at. When he was born I knew that motherhood was invented by someone who had to have a word for it because the ones that had the children didn't care whether there was a word for it or not. I knew that fear was invented by someone that had never had the fear; pride, who never had the pride. [040-015]

Addie's radical linguistic skepticism — words as empty shapes "like spiders dangling by their mouths from a beam, swinging and twisting and never touching" (040-015) — transforms silence from absence into a form of truth. Her single utterance reveals that her silence in life was not emptiness but a willed privacy, a refusal to be known by those who claimed to love her.

The distinction she draws between words and deeds — between the weightless rising of language and the terrible earth-clinging of action — provides a framework for understanding every other character's relationship to voice in the novel. Those who speak most, like Darl, are least effective in the world of deeds. Those who act most, like Jewel, barely speak at all.

### Darl and Jewel: Speech and Action

Darl Bundren's volubility — nineteen chapters of acute, philosophical observation — contrasts sharply with Jewel Bundren's near-muteness. Darl's voice is characterized by its extraordinary reach: he narrates events he cannot physically witness, including Addie's death. Yet his verbal fluency is inseparable from his eventual undoing.

Jewel speaks only one brief chapter and is characterized primarily through physical action. The novel suggests an inverse relationship between physical competence and verbal fluency, with Darl's excessive consciousness ultimately leading to his institutionalization while Jewel's wordless action ensures his survival. When the brothers speak to each other during the river crossing, their voices register the quiet tension between them:

> When we speak our voices are quiet, detached. "I reckon we are. I reckon we are still on it." He looks about quietly, at the position of the trees, leaning this way and that. [034-003]

### Dewey Dell: The Unspeakable

Dewey Dell Bundren carries the novel's most consequential secret — her pregnancy — in a silence enforced by social taboo. She cannot name her condition aloud: "I feel like a wet seed wild in the hot blind earth" (014-011). Her internal monologue circles around what she cannot say directly, using metaphor and indirection to approach the unspeakable.

When she finally attempts to voice her need — to obtain an abortion — she is met with deception at the drugstore, her inability to speak the truth exploited by the clerk who gives her worthless "medicine" (045-032). Dewey Dell's silence is not chosen but imposed, and her story demonstrates the violence done to those who cannot speak.

### Vardaman: Beyond Language

Vardaman Bundren's chapters explore silence and voice at the limits of linguistic competence. As a child confronting his mother's death, he lacks the conceptual framework to articulate his experience: "My mother is a fish" (013-029). His voice is characterized by sensory immediacy rather than reflection: "It is dark. I can hear wood, silence: I know them" (013-029).

Vardaman's struggle to speak represents the pre-linguistic encounter with trauma, where silence and fragmented expression are the only available responses. In this, his voice is the inverse of Addie's — where she has rejected language as inadequate, Vardaman has not yet acquired it, and both positions expose the gap between experience and its articulation.
