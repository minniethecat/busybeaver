# Anse's Teeth

[[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]'s teeth — or, more precisely, their absence and eventual replacement — constitute one of William Faulkner's most economically deployed motifs in _As I Lay Dying_. Mentioned recurrently across the novel, the teeth function simultaneously as comic detail, character index, and structural revelation: the [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]], ostensibly undertaken to [[pride|honor]] [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[death-and-dying|dying]] wish, is retrospectively revealed as [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s opportunity to acquire new teeth.

## Absence and Vanity

[[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s toothlessness is introduced early and rendered with characteristic grotesquerie. [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s description in the novel's opening chapters establishes the physical fact and its social consequence:

> Since he lost his teeth his mouth collapses in slow repetition when he dips. [005-008]

The collapsed mouth is both physical description and moral portrait — a face that has caved in on itself, a man whose [[language-and-meaning|words]] have no structural support. When [[anse-bundren|Anse]] speaks of [[family-duty|duty]] and [[family-duty|promise]]s, the mis[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g teeth hover behind his pronouncements as a reminder of what he will not say: that the [[the-journey|journey]] serves his own purposes.

The toothlessness is linked explicitly to his [[pride|vanity]]: [[anse-bundren|Anse]] "has not been in [[jefferson|town]] in twelve years" [011-005] because of embarrassment over his appearance. [[jefferson|Jefferson]] for [[anse-bundren|Anse]] is not primarily the place where [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s family plot lies — it is the place where "them teeth" can be obtained.

## The Promise Concealed

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s narration in chapter 12 reveals [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s unspoken motive through juxtaposition. As [[addie-bundren|Addie]] lies [[death-and-dying|dying]], [[cash-bundren|Cash]] sawing her coffin boards outside the window, [[anse-bundren|Anse]] stands beside the bed with a calculation entirely his own:

> "Now I can get them teeth." [012-022]

The sentence lands with prosecutorial precision. [[addie-bundren|Addie]] is not yet dead, but [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s thoughts have already moved [[anse-bundren|pa]]st her — to the teeth her [[death-and-dying|death]] will enable him to buy. Faulkner's rest[[water-and-river|rain]]t (the revelation is a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle line, unglossed, embedded in [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s narrative flow) makes the indictment all the more devastating.

## Acquisition

The teeth are acquired offstage, between the burial and the novel's final scene. When the family reunites at the end of chapter 59, [[anse-bundren|Anse]] appears transformed:

> He looks different. His face is different. His face looks, now he's got them teeth. [059-007]

The repetition of "different" resists celebration. [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s new face is the face of a man who has gotten what he wanted from the [[the-journey|journey]]. The teeth themselves are borrowed objects — acquired on credit, like the [[the-mule-team|mules]], the s[[anse-bundren|pa]]des, and everything else [[anse-bundren|Anse]] has used to accomplish his ends.

The final tableau, in which [[anse-bundren|Anse]] introduces his new wife (the "duck-shaped woman" who loaned him the graphophone) while wearing his new teeth, completes the substitution: [[addie-bundren|Addie]] has been replaced by a set of false teeth and a new Mrs. Bundren, both acquired in the same [[jefferson|town]], on the same trip, within hours of the burial.
