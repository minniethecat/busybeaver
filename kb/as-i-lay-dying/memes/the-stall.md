# The Stall

The stall in the Bundren barn is the site of Jewel Bundren's secret labor — the nighttime tending of the horse he acquired through months of furtive work, sleeping while his family assumed him idle. Within the enclosed darkness of the stall, Jewel expresses a devotion that he cannot articulate in words, caring for the animal with an intensity that the novel explicitly links to his relationship with his mother Addie. The stall is a space of hidden love, a sanctuary where feeling can be enacted through the body rather than spoken through the mouth.

## Description

The stall is a simple enclosure within the larger barn structure — a space of darkness, warmth, and animal smell that Faulkner renders through Darl's perceptive narration. It is "dark in the barn, warm, smelling, silent" [013-022], a sensory environment that contrasts sharply with the verbal and social world of the farmhouse. The stall contains the horse and the evidence of Jewel's labor — the feed, the tack, the traces of his presence that Darl observes with characteristic detachment.

## Role in the Novel

The stall functions as a space of emotional authenticity in a novel where language is persistently unreliable. Jewel cannot say what he feels for his mother; he can only act it, and the stall is where this acting takes place. His acquisition and care of the horse — clearing land by moonlight, working in secret, spending his nights in the stall — constitute a labor of love that parallels Cash's construction of the coffin. Both sons express their devotion through physical work rather than verbal declaration, and both do so in spaces — the stall, the sawhorse — that are separate from the domestic center of the farmhouse.

The stall is also a space of secrecy. Jewel's moonlight labor is conducted out of sight of his family, and Darl's knowledge of it — obtained through his uncanny perceptiveness — becomes one of the sources of tension between the two brothers. Darl sees what Jewel would keep hidden, and this violation of privacy, however unintentional, contributes to the antagonism that culminates in Darl's committal.

## Key Scenes

### The Night Work

Jewel's nocturnal labor in the stall is described by Darl in some of the novel's most striking passages. "I would lie in bed... until I could hear them all asleep, so I could get up and go back to the barn" [003-003] — Darl describes his own sleeplessness as a condition of observing Jewel's secret work. The stall at night, lit by lantern, is a theater of devotion: Jewel moving in the darkness, caring for the horse, expressing through his body what his voice cannot say.

### Jewel and the Horse

The stall is the primary site of the relationship between Jewel and his horse — a bond that Darl characterizes as almost violent in its intensity. "Inside the barn Jewel slides running to the ground" [003-011] — the verb "slides" captures the fluid, almost wordless quality of Jewel's movement in this private space. In the stall, Jewel is not the sullen, hostile presence he is in the house; he is absorbed, purposeful, fully himself.

## Critical Interpretation

The stall, like the barn that contains it, participates in what critic John T. Matthews identified as Faulkner's "spatialization of emotion" — the use of physical spaces to represent psychological states that characters cannot directly express. Jewel's devotion to his horse, and through the horse to his mother, is contained within the stall as Cash's devotion is contained within the coffin he builds. Both spaces are enclosures of love, and both are threatened by the journey that will force the family out of the domestic world and into the exposed space of the road.
