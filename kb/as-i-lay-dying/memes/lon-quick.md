# Lon Quick

Lon Quick is an offstage figure in William Faulkner's _As I Lay Dying_ — a man who never appears directly in the narrative but whose reputation for horsemanship and uncanny perception circulates through the community's talk. He is mentioned in three se[[anse-bundren|pa]]rate chapters by three different narrators, each reference building a portrait of a man who has become, in local memory, something closer to legend than neighbor.

## Role in the Novel

Lon Quick exists entirely in hearsay — a figure constructed through the community's collective recollection. He is invoked by [[samson|Samson]] (ch029), [[darl-bundren|Darl]] (ch032, ch036), and [[vernon-tull|Vernon Tull]] (ch037), each reference adding a distinct dimension to his legend. His narrative function is to em[[the-body|body]] a vanishing rural competence: the ability to read weather, handle horses, and command respect through [[work-and-craft|skill]] rather than position.

## The Texas Ponies

Lon Quick's defining achievement — referenced in [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s extended flashback in chapter 32 — is his mastery of one of the wild Texas ponies that Flem Snopes brought to Frenchman's Bend and auctioned for two dollars a head:

> It was a descendant of those Texas ponies Flem Snopes brought here twenty-five years ago and auctioned off for two dollars a head and nobody but old Lon Quick ever caught his and still owned some of the blood because he could never give it away. [032-046]

The detail that Lon Quick "could never give it away" — that [[jewels-horse|the horse]] blood persisted in his possession not by effort but by the community's refusal — suggests something about the marginal value of the wild pony lineage. Yet Lon Quick's [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gular success in catching and keeping one marks him as exceptional in a community where exceptionalism is measured in practical, physical terms.

## "Spotted Shoats" and the River Crossing

In [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s narration of [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] (ch036), one of Lon Quick's "spotted shoats" drifts by the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]]:

> There was a shoat come by, blowed up like a balloon: one of them spotted shoats of Lon Quick's. It bumped against the rope like it was a iron bar and bumped off and went on. [036-015]

The dead pig, bloated and adrift, functions as an omen within the catastrophic [[water-and-river|river]] scene — a piece of Lon Quick's property swept into [[the-river-crossing-event|the flood]], transformed from livestock to portent. The detail of "spotted shoats" is characteristic of Faulkner's world-building: even a minor character's livestock carries a signature breed.

## "Big Lon" — The Weather Eye

In chapter 37, [[vernon-tull|Vernon Tull]] invokes Lon Quick's most uncanny gift — his ability to read the sky:

> Lon Quick could look even at a cloudy sky and tell the time to ten minutes. Big Lon I mean, not little Lon. [037-039]

The distinction — "Big Lon I mean, not little Lon" — suggests a father-son [[anse-bundren|pa]]ir, with the elder Lon Quick posses[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g the legendary perception. This ability to read time from a clouded sky belongs to an older, pre-modern competence: a [[the-body|body]] of knowledge being lost as the novel's world tilts toward [[jefferson|Jefferson]] and modernity.

## The Name "Quick"

The surname "Quick" carries Faulkner's characteristic irony. In a novel structured around the slow, rotting [[the-journey|journey]] of a corpse, "Quick" — [[language-and-meaning|meaning]] alive, lively, the opposite of dead — names a family whose [[anse-bundren|pa]]triarch is invoked only as legend, whose pigs float dead in [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]], and whose bloodline (the ponies) no[[the-body|body]] wants. The name functions as a quiet counterpoint to the Bundrens' mortuary [[the-journey|pilgrimage]].
