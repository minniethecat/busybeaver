# Family and Kinship

The Bundren family is the novel's subject. More than any individual character, it is the family as a system — with its debts, its unspoken contracts, its economies of love and resentment — that Faulkner anatomizes across fifty-nine chapters. Kinship in *As I Lay Dying* is never simply a fact of biology; it is a structure of obligation that each character negotiates differently, and the journey to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] tests every bond the family possesses.

## The Bundren Family Structure

The family consists of [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]] (the father), [[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]] (the mother, dying as the novel opens), and their five children: [[cash-bundren|Cash]], [[darl-bundren|Darl]], [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]. But this surface structure conceals deeper rifts. [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s monologue reveals that she apportioned her love — and her children — according to a private calculus:

> I gave Anse the children. I did not ask for them. I did not ask for them. I did not ask for them. I would look at him, his hump, his stick, and I would think, He is my husband, and he is dead, and I am not dead. And then he died, and I did not. And then he died. He did not know he was dead. [040-006]

[[cash-bundren|Cash]] was hers alone, a child conceived in the early, bitter years of marriage, made as deliberately as Cash himself makes coffins. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] was given to [[anse-bundren|Anse]] — a child she could not love because he belonged to the man she despised. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] was the product of her affair with [[whitfield|Reverend Whitfield]], a child of passion and transgression, the only one she loved without reservation. [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] were conceived to "negative" [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] — compensations for a love that could not be repeated, each successive birth an attempt to fill a void that only deepened with each child.

## Obligation as Kinship

For [[anse-bundren|Anse]], kinship is obligation and nothing more. He keeps his promise to bury [[addie-bundren|Addie]] in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] not out of love but because a promise is a debt, and a man who cannot sweat can at least be a man who keeps his word. This transactional understanding of family — kinship as a series of debts incurred and repaid — is challenged by every other character. [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s devotion is expressed through labor, [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s through fury, [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s through the conflicted loyalty of a daughter who is also about to become a mother.

The journey exposes the limits of obligation as the basis for kinship. When [[darl-bundren|Darl]] burns the Gillespie barn — an act of desperate mercy meant to spare his mother's corpse further indignity — the family's response is not gratitude but betrayal. [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] saves the coffin and [[anse-bundren|Anse]] consigns Darl to the asylum, a decision in which the entire family is complicit. The kinship that obligation sustains is also the kinship that sacrifices its own.

## Sibling Relationships

The novel's sibling dynamics are among its richest threads. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] are defined by their rivalry — [[darl-bundren|Darl]] knows [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s true parentage, and this knowledge shapes their every interaction. Darl's taunting of Jewel, his probing questions about identity and belonging, are the intellect's assault on a bond of blood that Darl himself cannot share.

[[cash-bundren|Cash]] is the steady eldest brother, his carpentry a form of care that the others depend on without acknowledging. His relationship with Darl is particularly fraught — Cash is the one who restrains Darl at the end, the one who tells the asylum attendants "it'll be better for him" (053-011), a judgment that is both an act of love and an act of betrayal.

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] and [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]], the youngest, are bound by their incomplete understanding of death — she fans [[addie-bundren|Addie]] while he drills holes in the coffin, each acting out a grief the other cannot articulate. Dewey Dell's attack on Darl at the novel's end — she is the first to restrain him — is the novel's most painful sibling moment, a daughter protecting a secret that a brother already knows.

## The Dissolution of the Family

The novel ends with the family reconstituted in a new form: [[addie-bundren|Addie]] buried, [[darl-bundren|Darl]] consigned to the asylum at Jackson, and [[anse-bundren|Anse]] introducing a new Mrs. Bundren to his children. The journey that was supposed to honor a family promise has dissolved the family it was meant to serve. Faulkner's vision of kinship is unsentimental: family is not a refuge but a crucible, and the bonds of blood are as likely to destroy as to sustain.

> "Meet Mrs Bundren," he says. [059-005]

Anse's final line is the novel's last word on kinship — the family is not a sacred unit but a replaceable arrangement, the bond of marriage not a covenant but a convenience. The children, stunned into silence by the appearance of a new mother before the old one is cold in the ground, embody Faulkner's bleakest insight: that kinship, for all its claims on loyalty and love, may finally be nothing more than proximity.
