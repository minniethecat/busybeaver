# Yoknapatawpha County

Yoknapatawpha County is William Faulkner's fictional Mississippi county, the setting for *As I Lay Dying* and the majority of his major works. Modeled on Lafayette County, Mississippi, where Faulkner lived most of his life, Yoknapatawpha functions as a self-contained literary cosmos — a place where the same families, towns, and histories recur across novels, creating a dense web of cross-reference and accumulated meaning.

## Overview

Faulkner created Yoknapatawpha County as the imaginative geography for his fiction, populating it with the Compsons, the Sartorises, the Snopeses, and the Bundrens — families whose stories interlock across decades of literary production. The county's seat is Jefferson, where the Bundren family arrives at the end of their journey. *As I Lay Dying* mentions the county explicitly when a character in Jefferson observes that the Bundrens "came from some place out in Yoknapatawpha county, trying to get to Jefferson" (045-041), establishing the geographic frame of the narrative.

## The Bundrens' Yoknapatawpha

The Bundren farm occupies a specific location within Yoknapatawpha's rural landscape — far enough from Jefferson that the journey to bury Addie requires fording a flooded river, buying new mules, and enduring days of hardship. The farm itself, with its cotton fields, cotton-house, and the bluff overlooking it, is a microcosm of the county's agrarian economy. The Bundrens are not the county's aristocratic planters (the Sartorises) nor its rising commercial class (the Snopeses) — they are subsistence farmers, the kind of family that Faulkner's fiction often overlooks in favor of more dramatic falls from grace.

## Jefferson

The town of Jefferson serves as the destination of the Bundrens' journey — and as the symbolic center of Yoknapatawpha's social and economic order. It is where the county's legal and medical institutions reside, where "rich town ladies" (002-004) buy cakes from country women like Cora Tull, and where Anse Bundren will acquire both new teeth and a new wife. For the Bundrens, Jefferson represents the world beyond the farm — a world of transactions, institutions, and the public gaze that they enter as outsiders.

## Meta-Literary Significance

Yoknapatawpha County is one of the most famous fictional places in American literature, comparable to Hardy's Wessex or García Márquez's Macondo. Faulkner mapped it — literally, drawing a map of the county for *Absalom, Absalom!* — and populated it with enough consistency across novels that readers and scholars have constructed elaborate genealogies and geographies of its inhabitants. *As I Lay Dying*, published in 1930, sits early in the Yoknapatawpha cycle, before the full richness of the county's history had been developed in later novels. But the novel's treatment of place — the specificity of the landscape, the social hierarchy of farm and town, the journey from rural isolation to county seat — is foundational to the fictional world Faulkner would spend his career building.
