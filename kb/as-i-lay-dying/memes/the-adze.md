# The Adze

The adze is one of Cash Bundren's essential carpentry tools, used alongside the saw, hammer, plane, and chisel in the construction of Addie's coffin. While the saw receives more narrative attention — its sound dominates the novel's opening chapters — the adze represents a different dimension of Cash's craft: the specialized skill of shaping and smoothing, the quiet precision that transforms rough lumber into a finished object worthy of its solemn purpose.

## Description

An adze is a woodworking tool with a curved blade set at a right angle to the handle, used for shaping and smoothing wood surfaces. It is an ancient tool, predating the industrial era, and its presence in Cash's toolkit connects his labor to a long tradition of hand-craft. Faulkner mentions the adze in passing — "he takes up the adze or the saw" [002-010] — but this very casualness is significant: the tool is so integral to Cash's identity that it needs no introduction or explanation. The adze is simply what Cash reaches for when it is time to shape wood.

## Symbolic Significance

The adze represents the specialized, almost esoteric dimension of Cash's craft. Anyone can recognize a saw or a hammer; the adze requires a more specific knowledge, a familiarity with the traditions of woodworking that Cash embodies. This specialization is thematically significant: Cash's relationship to his tools is a form of knowledge that cannot be reduced to language, a mode of being-in-the-world that is expressed through action rather than speech.

In the novel's moral economy, this practical knowledge carries ethical weight. Cash's expertise with the adze — his ability to shape wood to his purpose, to achieve the exact bevel he intends — contrasts with the verbal expertise of characters like Anse, whose words are as imprecise as Cash's carpentry is exact. The adze is an instrument of truth in a world of linguistic approximation and deception.

## Appearances in the Text

### In Cora's Chapter

The adze first appears in Cora's narration, where it is paired with the saw as one of the tools Cash alternates between: "he takes up the adze or the saw" [002-010]. Cora's observation establishes the adze as part of Cash's working rhythm — the tool he reaches for when the task requires a different kind of attention.

### In Darl's Chapter

Darl's narration of the coffin's construction includes the adze in a description of Cash's labor that emphasizes the sensory quality of the work. The adze shapes the wood that the saw has cut, smoothing what was rough, refining what was crude. This progression — from rough cutting to fine shaping — mirrors the movement from the rawness of grief to something like acceptance, a parallel that Darl's perceptive narration registers without explicitly stating.

## Critical Interpretation

Critics have noted Cash's tools as representations of what Joseph Blotner called "the dignity of skilled labor" in Faulkner's work. The adze, as the most specialized of Cash's tools, embodies the expertise that distinguishes authentic craft from mere effort. In a novel full of characters who talk about what they are doing or feeling, Cash simply works, and his tools — the adze among them — are the instruments of this wordless eloquence.
