The mule team belonging to Anse Bundren plays a crucial role in *As I Lay Dying*, pulling the wagon that carries Addie's coffin. The mules meet a violent end during the river crossing, their death marking one of the novel's most dramatic and costly episodes.

## Role in the Journey

The mules are first mentioned in connection with Anse's determination to reach Jefferson quickly: "She would get her there quick as mules could walk it" [005-020]. They appear throughout the early journey as a steady, suffering presence — Darl describes "the two sets of bobbing mule ears" [010-002] and notes how "the mules smoke a little, splashed yellow" in the rain [012-012].

The mules' physical state reflects the toll of the journey: "He looked like he was sorry for them bone-gaunted mules" [029-018]. Their gaunt condition foreshadows their eventual fate.

## Death at the River

The river crossing scene is where the mules meet their end. As the wagon enters the flooded river, the mules struggle against the current. The crossing becomes catastrophic when the wagon overturns, and the mules drown — a loss that is both practical (the family loses its means of transport) and symbolic (nature punishes human stubbornness).

After the crossing, Anse must acquire a new team, which he does by trading Jewel's horse — deepening the cost of the journey [031-025]. The exchange of the mules' lives and Jewel's beloved horse underscores the mounting sacrifice required by Addie's burial promise.

## Symbolic Meaning

The mules represent the working-class identity of the Bundrens — they are farm animals, not show horses. Their death strips the family of its economic foundation, just as Addie's death has stripped them of their emotional center. The mules become one more casualty of Anse's inflexible insistence on honoring a promise at any cost.
