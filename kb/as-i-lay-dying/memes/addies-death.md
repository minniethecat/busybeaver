# Addie Bundren's Death

[[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]'s [[death-and-dying|death]] is the event that defines the novel — not through dramatic description (it occurs offstage, between narrators) but through its radiating consequences. Every subsequent action is shaped by the fact of her [[death-and-dying|dying]] and the [[family-duty|promise]] extracted from it.

## Summary

[[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]] dies in her bed at the Bundren farm, witnessed by her daughter [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] and attended (too late) by [[peabody|Doctor Peabody]]. Her [[death-and-dying|death]] has been antici[[anse-bundren|pa]]ted for weeks — [[cash-bundren|Cash]] builds her coffin within her sight, [[cora-tull|Cora]] visits to offer Christian consolation, and the family organizes its [[work-and-craft|labor]] around her approaching end. Yet the death itself is marked by absence: [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] are away hauling lumber, and [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s clairvoyant narration of the moment — "She is dead. She died" — occurs while he is still miles distant.

## Context

Addie's death is the culmination of a long decline but also, in her own account, the endpoint of a life defined by disillusionment. Her marriage to [[anse-bundren|Anse]], her affair with [[whitfield|Whitfield]], her [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtiality toward [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]], her estrangement from [[darl-bundren|Darl]] — all lead to this bed, this window, this coffin being built by her eldest son. The [[death-and-dying|death]]bed [[family-duty|promise]] extracted from [[anse-bundren|Anse]] — to bury her in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] — binds the family to a course of action whose costs far exceed its benefits.

## Significance

The [[death-and-dying|death]]'s significance lies in its polyphonic treatment. Faulkner gives Addie's death to us through [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s moralizing (006), [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s remote vision, [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s categorical confusion ("My [[motherhood|mother]] is a fish" (019-001)), [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s bodily knowledge, and finally [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s own voice (040). No [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle account is authoritative. The event is refracted through so many consciousnesses that it becomes less a fact than a field of interpretations — which is, the novel suggests, what any [[death-and-dying|death]] ultimately is.

## Key Passages

From [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s remote narration: "She is dead. She died before we knew it. She died" — the repetition enacting comprehension through [[language-and-meaning|language]]. From [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]: "My [[motherhood|mother]] is a fish" (019-001) — the novel's most famous line, capturing a child's attempt to process [[death-and-dying|death]] through the only categories available to him. From [[peabody|Peabody]]: "I can remember how when I was young I believed [[death-and-dying|death]] to be a phenomenon of the [[the-body|body]]; now I know it to be merely a function of the mind" (011-013) — the novel's philosophical key.
