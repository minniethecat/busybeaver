# The Cement

The cement is a bag of raw cement powder purchased by [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]] at a store along the route to [[jefferson|Jefferson]], which he applies to [[cash-bundren|Cash Bundren]]'s broken leg as a makeshift cast in Faulkner's *As I Lay Dying*. What is intended as a practical remedy becomes one of the novel's most grotesque images of bodily suffering and paternal neglect. The cement hardens around [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s leg, cracking into "jagged shards" [052-003], and by the journey's end, a doctor in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] will observe with grim understatement that a man who treats his son with "raw cement" cannot be reasoned with [054-001].

> They had stopped to buy some cement. The other son was in Grummet's, he told me, getting some tools mended. [045-043]

The town marshal's matter-of-fact report of the cement purchase captures the novel's method: horrific events rendered in flat, observational language, their moral weight left for the reader to register.

## Description

The cement is bought in a bag — ordinary building material, the kind used to set posts or patch foundations. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] describes it being mixed "in the can, stirring the slow water into the pale grain" [046-016], the domestic language of cooking applied to an act of grotesque medical improvisation. Applied to [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s leg with splints and strips of cloth, it hardens into a rigid shell that causes the flesh beneath to swell and darken. By the time the family reaches [[jefferson|Jefferson]], the leg below the cement "looked like they had been boiled" [048-013], and the cast itself has cracked into shards that shift against the bone with each jolt of [[the-bundren-wagon|the wagon]] [052-003].

> We loosen the splints and pour the cement over his leg, slow. [046-023]

Darl's narration of the application — "slow" set off by a period, an afterthought made into its own sentence — makes the reader feel every second of the procedure. The word hangs at the end of the sentence like the cement settling into place: deliberate, irreversible, wrong.

## Symbolic Significance

The cement is the novel's starkest symbol of [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]'s failure as a father. [[cash-bundren|Cash]], the most selfless and competent of the Bundren children — the one who built [[addies-coffin|Addie's coffin]] with meticulous care — is repaid for his loyalty with a treatment that is worse than no treatment at all. The cement cast is the inverse of Cash's own craftsmanship: where Cash shaped wood into a coffin with precision and love, [[anse-bundren|Anse]] slathers cement onto his son's broken body with an indifference that borders on cruelty.

> We replace the splints, the cords, drawing them tight. [046-031]

The mechanical language — "replace," "drawing them tight" — describes the final stage of the application as if it were a routine repair. The tightening of the cords over hardening cement is the novel's most compressed image of suffering inflicted in the name of care.

The cement also participates in the novel's broader critique of misguided care. [[anse-bundren|Anse]] insists he is fulfilling his promise to [[addie-bundren|Addie]], but the cement reveals the truth: he will sacrifice his children's bodies to keep up the appearance of duty. The doctor's verdict — that "a man that would treat his own son with raw cement ain't got more spare legs than he has sense" [054-001] — is the novel's most direct moral condemnation of [[anse-bundren|Anse]], and it comes not from a family member but from an outside observer, a man of practical medicine.

## Appearances in the Text

### First Appearance: The Purchase

The cement is purchased at a store, narrated from the perspective of an anonymous town marshal who observes the Bundrens' arrival. The marshal describes "the one with the cement" [045-048] and notes the family's strange, hurried manner. The purchase is made with what little money the family has — another subtraction from their meager resources.

### The Application

In chapter 46, [[darl-bundren|Darl]] narrates the application of the cement in precise, almost clinical detail: mixing the powder in a can, loosening the splints, pouring the mixture over [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s leg "slow" [046-023]. [[cash-bundren|Cash]] endures without complaint. The scene is both intimate and horrific — a family gathered around their wounded member, administering a treatment that will cause more harm than the original injury.

### Final Appearances

By chapter 52, the cement has cracked into jagged shards, and [[darl-bundren|Darl]] observes the cement cracking further with each jolt of the wagon [052-003]. In chapter 54, the doctor's verdict on the cement cast serves as the novel's final, damning assessment of [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s character.

## Critical Interpretation

The cement cast is widely discussed in Faulkner criticism as an exemplar of the novel's grotesque mode — the transformation of the ordinary (building material) into the horrific (a torture device) through the logic of poverty and neglect. Critics have also noted the cement's thematic connection to Cash's own craft: both coffin and cast are attempts to contain the body, but where the coffin is carefully measured and planed, the cast is slathered on without skill or care. The contrast between Cash's workmanship and Anse's improvisation is the novel's most compressed statement about what the Bundrens give each other versus what they take.
