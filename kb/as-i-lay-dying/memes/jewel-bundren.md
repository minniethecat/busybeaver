# Jewel Bundren

Jewel Bundren is [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s third son, the child of her affair with the preacher [[whitfield|Whitfield]], and the character whose love for his [[motherhood|mother]] is most intense and least articulate. Where [[darl-bundren|Darl]] perceives and [[cash-bundren|Cash]] makes, Jewel acts — with a fury that borders on violence.

## Role in the Novel

Jewel is the novel's figure of pure physical will. His [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle narrated chapter (004) is a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle sentence — a terse, rhythmic interior monologue that captures his kinetic, wordless consciousness. Throughout the [[the-journey|journey]], Jewel's role is to save what the family nearly loses: he rescues [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] from [[the-river-crossing-location|the flooded river]], he [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle-handedly manhandles the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] across obstacles, and he retrieves his [[motherhood|mother]]'s [[the-body|body]] from [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s barn fire. His actions speak because he cannot — or will not.

His illegitimacy, known to [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and perhaps to others, creates the novel's central tension between brothers. [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s goading — "Jewel's [[motherhood|mother]] is a horse" (024-004) — is simultaneously cruel and truthful, expo[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g the secret that defines Jewel's identity.

## Biography

Jewel is the product of [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s affair with [[whitfield|Whitfield]], the local preacher. Unlike her other children, Jewel was conceived in [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssion rather than [[family-duty|duty]], and [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtiality toward him is the defining fact of the family's emotional economy. He was raised as [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s son, but his difference — his olive complexion, his fierce independence, his violent temper — marks him as other. He [[work-and-craft|work]]s nights clearing a field to earn the money to buy a horse, his one possession and the symbol of his self-sufficiency.

Jewel's [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle chapter (004) is the novel's shortest and most formally radical — a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle sentence running several [[anse-bundren|pa]]ges that mimics the rhythm of physical [[work-and-craft|labor]] and the stream of a consciousness organized around action rather than reflection. ## Key Relationships

**With [[addie-bundren|Addie]]**: Jewel is the son [[addie-bundren|Addie]] loved above all others — a love that was itself a form of punishment, [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]ce he "never loved her and was its own punishment" (039-002). His [[love|devotion]] to her is expressed entirely through physical action: the furious effort of saving her coffin, the violent protection of her [[the-body|body]]. He never says he loved her. He does not need to.

**With [[darl-bundren|Darl]]**: Jewel's relationship with [[darl-bundren|Darl]] is defined by mutual incomprehension and submerged violence.
