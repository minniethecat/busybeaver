# Blood as Motif

Blood functions as a central recurring motif in *As I Lay Dying*, carrying layered symbolic meanings that encompass kinship and familial obligation, physical violence and bodily vulnerability, and the fundamental life-force that Addie Bundren calls "the terrible blood."

## Overview

Blood appears throughout the novel in contexts ranging from the literal (Vardaman's bloody fish, Cash's hemorrhaging leg) to the metaphysical (Addie's meditation on "the wild blood boiling along the earth"). Faulkner uses the motif to connect the novel's physical and philosophical registers, reminding readers that the Bundrens' journey is not merely a symbolic pilgrimage but a visceral ordeal of flesh and blood.

> When the switch fell I could feel it upon my flesh; when it welted and ridged it was my blood that ran, and I would think with each blow of the switch: Now you are aware of me! Now I am something in your secret and selfish life, who have marked your blood with my own forever and ever. [040-002]

The word and its variants appear across multiple narrators and contexts, creating a unifying thread through the novel's fragmented consciousnesses.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Blood as Kinship

The most frequent use of "blood" in the novel refers to family ties. Addie contemplates whether her children are truly hers, reflecting on how physical punishment became a means of forging connection: "only through the blows of the switch could my blood and their blood flow as one stream" (040-015). The phrase "flesh and blood" recurs throughout, as when Anse speaks of the coffin made by Cash: "it was her own blood sawed out the boards and drove the nails" (005-022). Yet blood kinship in the novel is fraught — Jewel is not Anse's biological son, and Addie's blood connection to her children is questioned through her own philosophical skepticism about the meaning of family bonds.

> And when I would have to look at them day after day, each with his and her secret and selfish thought, and blood strange to each other blood and strange to mine, and think that this seemed to be the only way I could get ready to stay dead, I would hate my father for having ever planted me. [040-002]

Addie's conception of blood as the substance of kinship is fundamentally ambivalent: it binds her to her children yet also defines her entrapment within the cycles of motherhood. Her description of lying beside Anse, "hearing the land that was now of my blood and flesh" (040-020), suggests that even the earth itself becomes kin through the blood-price of childbearing.

### Blood as Violence and Mortality

Blood marks the novel's violent and mortal moments. After the river crossing, Cash's broken leg causes him to hemorrhage: "He is bleeding to death is Cash" (046-004). Vardaman's traumatic encounter with the fish he catches and cleans produces an image of primal violence:

> I saw Vardaman rise and go to the window and strike the knife into the fish, the blood gushing, hissing like steam but I could not see. [030-004]

Earlier, Vardaman appears "bloody as a hog to his knees" after some unseen event, and describes something "full of blood and guts as a hog" (009-018). Through the child's eyes, blood becomes a sign of the incomprehensible violence of death — raw, visceral, and beyond language to contain.

> Vardaman comes around the house, bloody as a hog to his knees, and that ere fish chopped up with the axe like as not, or maybe throwed away for him to lie about the dogs et it. [009-016]

### Blood as Life-Force

In Addie Bundren's single monologue chapter, blood becomes a metaphysical principle. She speaks of "the terrible blood, the red bitter flood boiling through the land" (040-024) — the primal life-force that drives reproduction, kinship, and ultimately her own entrapment within the cycles of female biology.

> I believed that the reason was the duty to the alive, to the terrible blood, the red bitter flood boiling through the land. I would think of sin as I would think of the clothes we both wore in the world's face, of the circumspection necessary because he was he and I was I; the sin the more utter and terrible since he was the instrument ordained by God who created the sin, to sanctify that sin He had created. [040-024]

Her description of "the wild blood boiling along the earth" captures the elemental vitality she associates with blood, a force both creative and destructive (040-027). This is contrasted with the moment of its cessation:

> With Jewel — I lay by the lamp, holding up my own head, watching him cap and suture it before he breathed — the wild blood boiled away and the sound of it ceased. [040-028]

This passage marks the threshold between life and death. Addie's blood — which boiled wildly through her life, through childbirth, through her hidden passions — finally quiets. The "milk, warm and calm" that replaces it suggests a post-mortal peace, the cessation of the terrible vitality that defined her existence.
