# The Body

As I Lay Dying is a novel saturated with the body — the [[death-and-dying|dying]] body of [[addie-bundren|Addie]], the decompo[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]g body in [[addies-coffin|the coffin]], the pregnant body of [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], the broken body of [[cash-bundren|Cash]], the animal bodies of the [[the-mule-team|mules]], [[vardamans-fish|the fish]], the [[the-cow|cow]]. Faulkner presents the body not as the vessel of a soul but as the primary medium through which his characters experience reality, a site of sensation that [[language-and-meaning|language]] can describe but never fully capture.

## The Dying Body

[[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]'s ten-day [[death-and-dying|dying]] is the novel's narrative anchor, and Faulkner renders it through the eyes of multiple witnesses. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] describes her "wasted body" with the precision of a clinician (001-001). [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], [[dewey-dells-fan|fan]]ning her [[motherhood|mother]], experiences the [[death-and-dying|dying]] body as a physical demand that shapes her own consciousness (004-002). The body in decline becomes the novel's structuring absence — once [[addie-bundren|Addie]] dies, her body (now a corpse) remains the gravitational center around which all action revolves.

> "Her face is wasted away so that the bones draw just under the skin in white lines. Her eyes are like two candles when you watch them gutter down into the sockets of iron candlesticks" (012-006).

## The Decaying Body

After [[addies-death|Addie's death]], her body becomes the novel's most potent symbol and its most grotesque physical presence. The [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] takes nine days in summer heat; by the time the family reaches [[mottson|Mottson]], the stench is detectable at a distance and the family is refused entry into [[jefferson|town]]. [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s attempt to burn the barn is motivated by his unbearable awareness of this decay — a body that was once his [[motherhood|mother]] now becoming "something not her" — while [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s refusal to acknowledge the stench becomes a form of willed blindness that the novel treats as both absurd and, in its way, heroic.

The [[the-buzzards|buzzards]] that circle the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] throughout the [[the-journey|journey]] literalize the body's claim on the living: the [[the-buzzards|vultures]] recognize what the Bundrens, in their collective refusal, cannot admit — that [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s body belongs to the natural order of decomposition, not to the human order of memorialization.

## The Pregnant Body

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s pregnancy is the novel's other great bodily reality. Her chapter 30 monologue is one of Faulkner's most sustained renderings of embodied consciousness: "I am a little tub of guts and there is room for something else very small laying quiet and wait." The pregnancy is experienced as an interior otherness — a body within a body — that [[anse-bundren|pa]]rallels the novel's larger concern with what the body knows that [[language-and-meaning|language]] cannot say.

## The Broken Body

[[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]] — broken when he fell from a church roof, re-broken in [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]], and finally set in concrete by [[anse-bundren|Anse]] — is the novel's most literal instance of the body as a site of cumulative violence. [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s stoicism ("It never bothered me none") contrasts with the graphic physical detail of the injury, and the [[cashs-broken-leg|cement cast]] becomes a darkly comic emblem of the Bundrens' approach to bodily care: functional, approximate, and disastrous.

## Faulkner's Embodied Aesthetic

Critics have long recognized As I Lay Dying as Faulkner's most corporeal novel — a [[work-and-craft|work]] in which the body is not a metaphor for something else (the soul, the self, the unconscious) but the irreducible ground of experience. The novel's formal innovations — its multiple narrators, its stream-of-consciousness technique, its resistance to omniscient summary — are inse[[anse-bundren|pa]]rable from this commitment to embodied perspective. Each narrator speaks from a specific body, and what that body is doing, feeling, or becoming shapes what that narrator can know.
