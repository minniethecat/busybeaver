# Shame

Shame in *As I Lay Dying* operates on multiple registers: as sexual shame enforced upon female bodies, as social shame inflicted by community judgment, and as the private shame of characters who fail to meet their own standards. Faulkner traces how shame moves through the Bundren family — sometimes visible, sometimes buried — shaping action and silencing speech.

## Overview

The novel's treatment of shame is inseparable from its treatment of gender. Dewey Dell Bundren carries the novel's most explicit burden of shame — an unwanted pregnancy she cannot name, a body she experiences as public property. Cora Tull weaponizes shame as moral authority, judging the Bundrens from the position of religious certainty. Anse Bundren, by contrast, seems immune to shame entirely — his shamelessness becoming itself a kind of moral failure. The novel suggests that shame, like language, is a social construct that adheres unequally: it sticks to women, to the poor, to those without power to deflect it.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Dewey Dell: Bodily Shame

Dewey Dell's pregnancy drives her narrative but never appears in her language directly. She thinks of her body in terms of its "female trouble" (014-005) and seeks a remedy not through confession but through transaction — first with Peabody, then with MacGowan in Jefferson. Her shame is so deeply internalized that it shapes her perception of the world. She experiences every male gaze as a threat and her own body as a trap.

### Through Cora Tull: Moral Judgment

Cora Tull embodies the shaming gaze of the community. Her religious certainty — her conviction that Addie Bundren was "a faithful wife" (002-012) and that the Bundrens' journey is a desecration — turns empathy into judgment. Cora's shame is externalized: she projects moral failure onto others to confirm her own righteousness, making shame a weapon rather than a feeling.

### Through Addie: Refusing Shame

Addie Bundren refuses the shame her world assigns her. Her affair with Whitfield, her hatred of her husband, her preference for Jewel over Darl — none of these provoke the guilt or concealment that shame would dictate. She "hid nothing" and "tried to deceive no one" (040-025). In a novel saturated with shame and silence, Addie's shamelessness reads as a form of radical honesty — and a source of her power.
