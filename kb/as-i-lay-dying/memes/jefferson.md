# Jefferson

Jefferson, Mississippi is the destination of the Bundrens' [[the-journey|journey]] — the town where [[addie-bundren|Addie]] wished to be buried "with her own people" and where the novel's multiple plot threads converge in a climax of grotesque comedy and unexpected renewal.

## Description

Jefferson appears in the novel primarily through the reactions of its inhabitants to the Bundrens' arrival. The town's streets, drugstores, and hardware stores provide the backdrop for the novel's final scenes: [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s failed abortion attempt, [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s acquisition of false teeth, and the family's abrupt reconstitution around a new matriarch. Jefferson represents civilization — order, commerce, social norms — against which the Bundrens' rural chaos is measured and found wanting.

## Role in the Novel

Jefferson functions as both literal destination and symbolic endpoint. The [[the-journey|journey]] to Jefferson is motivated by [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[death-and-dying|dying]] wish, but each character arrives with a private agenda: [[anse-bundren|Anse]] wants teeth, [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] wants an abortion, [[cash-bundren|Cash]] wants to show his leg to a real doctor. The town is the place where these hidden purposes are revealed and the family's public mission collapses into individual self-interest. The novel's final irony — that [[anse-bundren|Anse]] finds a new wife in Jefferson within hours of burying the old one — drives home the town's function as a site of transformation, whether welcome or not.

## Key Scenes

The Jefferson sequence includes [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s encounter with the pharmacist's assistant Moseley (047), her subsequent deception by Macgowan the soda jerk (055), and the family's final gathering where [[anse-bundren|Anse]] introduces his new wife with the [[language-and-meaning|words]] "Meet Mrs. Bundren" (059-001). Each scene exposes the gap between the [[the-journey|journey]]'s stated purpose ([[pride|honor]]ing [[addie-bundren|Addie]]) and its actual outcomes (teeth, deception, remarriage).

## The Town as Witness

Jefferson's residents observe the Bundrens with a mixture of horror, pity, and sardonic amusement. The druggist Moseley, a respectable church-member of fifty-six years, recoils from [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s request for an abortion, threatening to report her to her family (Moseley's chapter). The town's reaction to the Bundrens' arrival — the stench of the decomposing corpse, the bizarre procession — functions as a narrative mirror, reflecting back the grotesquerie that the family itself has grown accustomed to during their week-long ordeal.

## Symbolic Function

As the county seat of [[yoknapatawpha|Yoknapatawpha]], Jefferson represents the civilizing force that the Bundrens both seek and resist. The journey's stated purpose is to honor a promise — Addie's request to be buried among "her own people" in the Jefferson cemetery rather than among the Bundrens in the country — yet every step toward Jefferson exposes the family's internal fissures. The town's name itself, carrying the weight of American democratic ideals, ironically frames [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s profoundly anti-heroic self-interest: having buried one wife, he acquires teeth, a new wife, and a gramophone, declaring himself "a luckless man" even as he remakes his life in a single afternoon.
