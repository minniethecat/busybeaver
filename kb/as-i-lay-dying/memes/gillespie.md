# Gillespie

A farmer whose barn becomes the site of the novel's climactic crisis. When [[darl-bundren|Darl]] sets fire to the barn containing [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin, Gillespie — stripped naked in the night, fighting to save his livestock — becomes the innocent [[anse-bundren|pa]]rty whose property is sacrificed to the Bundren family's internal [[anse-bundren|pa]]thology. His threatened lawsuit forces the Bundrens into their final moral choice: face the law's [[sin-and-judgment|judgment]] or commit [[darl-bundren|Darl]] to the asylum.

## Role in the Novel

Gillespie is the last and most damaged in the novel's sequence of involuntary hosts. [[samson|Samson]] is inconvenienced and argued with; [[armstid|Armstid]] is shamed by [[the-buzzards|buzzards]] and stench; Gillespie loses his barn entirely. The escalating harm to hosts mirrors the escalating grotesquerie of the [[the-journey|journey]]. Each farmer who shelters the Bundrens is re[[anse-bundren|pa]]id with damage, and Gillespie's case — the destruction of his livelihood — represents the furthest point of that trajectory.

His practical, incredulous response to the [[cashs-broken-leg|cement cast]] on [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]] — "Didn't none of you have more sense than that? I'd 'a' thought he would, anyway" [051-017] — is the voice of rural competence confronting the Bundrens' staggering ineptitude.

## Biography

Gillespie is a farmer living within a day's [[the-journey|travel]] of [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. He has a son, Mack, who helps with [[bundren-farm|the farm]] [[work-and-craft|work]]. Nothing else is known of his background; like [[samson|Samson]] and [[armstid|Armstid]], he exists in the novel only as a point of contact between the Bundren [[the-journey|journey]] and the settled rural community.

The Bundrens arrive at his farm late in the [[the-journey|journey]] and store [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin in his barn. [[darl-bundren|Darl]], in his desperate attempt to end the grotesque [[the-journey|pilgrimage]], sets the barn on fire — a destructive act of mercy that destroys Gillespie's property.

## Key Scenes

### The Fire [050]

[[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s narration of the barn fire is one of the novel's most intense [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssages. Gillespie and Mack emerge from the house in their nightshirts. Gillespie strips off his nightshirt to wrap around a mule's head — the technique to blind and calm a [[anse-bundren|pa]]nicked animal — and [[work-and-craft|work]]s naked through the rest of the scene, beating the maddened horse from the barn:

> "Gillespie passes me, stark naked, his nightshirt wrapped about the mule's head, and beats the maddened horse on out of the door." [050-005]

When [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] tries to run back into the burning barn, Gillespie attempts to rest[[water-and-river|rain]] him physically:

> "He is struggling with Gillespie; the one lean in underclothes, the other stark naked. They are like two figures in a Greek frieze, isolated out of all reality by the red glare. Before I can reach them he has struck Gillespie to the ground and turned and run back into the barn." [050-014]

The "Greek frieze" simile — [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s typical elevation of the immediate into the mythic — frames Gillespie as an unwilling [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtici[[anse-bundren|pa]]nt in a tragedy he never asked to join.

### The Cement Cast [051]

After [[darl-burns-the-barn|the fire]], Gillespie's attention turns to [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]], which has been "re[[anse-bundren|pa]]ired" by [[anse-bundren|Anse]] with builder's cement:

> "'It'll take the hide, too,' Mr. Gillespie said. 'Why in the tarnation you put it on there? Didn't none of you think to grease his leg first?'" [051-014]

His dialogue is all practical incredulity — the voice of a man who knows how to treat animals and cannot fathom the incompetence before him.

### The Threat of Law [052–053]

Gillespie, along with [[armstid|Armstid]], had advised [[anse-bundren|Anse]] to send word ahead to [[jefferson|Jefferson]] to have the grave dug in advance:

> "We ought to done like Armstid and Gillespie said and sent word to town and had it dug and ready." [052-016]

[[anse-bundren|Anse]] ignored the advice. Now, with the barn destroyed, Gillespie knows [[darl-bundren|Darl]] set [[darl-burns-the-barn|the fire]]. [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], desperate to reach [[jefferson|Jefferson]] for her abortion, has told him — a betrayal that [[cash-bundren|Cash]] only understands in retrospect:

> "And then I believed I knowed how Gillespie knowed about how his barn taken fire." [053-040]

The family's calculation is stark: "It was either send him to Jackson, or have Gillespie sue us" [053-001]. The threat of a lawsuit from an innocent farmer forces the Bundrens to sacrifice [[darl-bundren|Darl]] to the asylum. Gillespie, who never sought this role, becomes the catalyst for the family's final moral collapse.

## Narrative Function

Gillespie serves as the novel's most damaged innocent bystander — proof that the Bundren [[the-journey|quest]], which began as a [[family-duty|promise]] to a [[death-and-dying|dying]] woman, has become a rolling catastrophe that harms everyone it touches. His threatened lawsuit is the rational world's intrusion into the Bundrens' closed system of mutual [[family-duty|obligation]] and dysfunction, and the family's response — committing [[darl-bundren|Darl]] — reveals how far they will go to avoid facing that [[sin-and-judgment|judgment]].

His few lines of dialogue are all practical, incredulous, and sensible. He represents the competence and rural wisdom against which the Bundrens' behavior is measured and found wanting.
