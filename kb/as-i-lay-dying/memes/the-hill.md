# The Hill

The hill (also referred to as "the bluff" and "the ridge" by various characters) stands above the [[bundren-farm|Bundren farm]] in [[jefferson|Yoknapatawpha County]] and serves as a recurring geographic landmark in Faulkner's *As I Lay Dying*. Its elevation offers literal perspective — characters who ascend or descend stand in relation to something beyond the immediate — and Faulkner repeatedly uses the hill's crest and slope to frame transitions between the domestic sphere of the farm and the world beyond.

## Description

The hill rises behind the Bundren farmhouse, its crest visible from the cotton-house where [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] walk at the novel's opening [003-009]. The path from the farm to the hill follows a rutted track past the bluff's foot [001-003], and the hill itself is described as "dissolving into a streaming mass of dark green" by [[darl-bundren|Darl]] in his characteristic mode of perceptual dissolution [012-012]. The farmhouse tilts "a little down the hill" [005-027], giving the domestic space a precarious orientation — as though the house itself is leaning toward the downward slope of the journey to come.

## Role in the Novel

The hill functions as a boundary marker between the Bundrens' domestic world and the outside. In the early chapters, ascending or descending the hill marks the transition between farm labor and the family's interior life. For [[darl-bundren|Darl]], the hill is a point from which he observes and contemplates — his clairvoyant gaze repeatedly fixes on figures coming up or going down the hill [008-012], [013-022]. For [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]], the hill is a private space of fantasy and escape — she imagines herself "on a high hill" rolling rocks down at faces below [004-002], a solitary vision that contrasts with the crowded wagon of the journey.

After [[addies-death|Addie's death]], the hill becomes the threshold the family must cross to begin their [[the-journey|journey]] to [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. The loaded wagon descends the hill "rattling and clanking" [052-003], a precarious passage that marks the physical and symbolic commencement of the funeral procession. The hill is the last piece of Bundren land they traverse before entering the public road, and Faulkner emphasizes the crossing with Darl's reflection on "the old terrors, the old hopes" carried on the wind from the valleys [052-004].

## Key Scenes

### Darl's Opening Observations

The novel's first chapter establishes the hill as a point of vantage. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] walk from the cotton-house toward the foot of the bluff [001-003], and later [[darl-bundren|Darl]] watches the wagon descend "the hill in a series of spine-jolting jumps" as [[anse-bundren|Anse]] and the family begin their journey [003-009]. The hill is present from the very first sentences, anchoring the reader in the Bundrens' physical world.

### Dewey Dell's Fantasy

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]] imagines herself alone on a high hill, rolling rocks down at faces below [004-002]. This solitary vision — she and the hill, no one else — is her most private moment in the novel, expressing her desire to be removed from the family's collective burden and her own unwanted pregnancy.

### The Departure

As the family leaves the farm with [[addies-coffin|Addie's coffin]] loaded on the wagon, they descend the hill, and [[darl-bundren|Darl]] describes the wind carrying "the old terrors, the old hopes" from the valleys below [052-004]. At the foot of the hill, the road flattens [052-005], and the family enters the public world — the journey has formally begun.
