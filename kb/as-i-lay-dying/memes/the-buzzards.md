# The Buzzards

The buzzards that circle the Bundrens' [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] are the [[the-journey|journey]]'s most persistent witnesses — drawn by the scent of decomposition, they transform a private funeral procession into a public spectacle that no amount of talk can disguise.

## Description

The buzzards first appear as the [[the-journey|journey]] progresses and [[addies-coffin|the coffin]]'s contents begin to decay. They circle overhead, perch on the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]], and follow the family with the [[anse-bundren|pa]]tience of creatures who know that all [[the-journey|journey]]s end. [[darl-bundren|Darl]] describes them with characteristic precision: "the buzzards were not waiting. They were not going to wait. They were circling" (042-001). The birds are neither malevolent nor symbolic — they are simply doing what buzzards do, which is to follow the scent of [[death-and-dying|death]]. Their utter indifference to human [[language-and-meaning|meaning]] makes them the novel's most honest commentators.

## Symbolic Role

The buzzards represent what the novel's characters [[work-and-craft|work]] hardest to ignore: the physical reality of [[death-and-dying|death]]. While [[cora-tull|Cora]] speaks of Christian [[death-and-dying|death]] and [[anse-bundren|Anse]] speaks of sacred [[family-duty|promise]]s, the buzzards speak of rotting meat. Their presence exposes the gap between the [[the-journey|journey]]'s stated purpose ([[pride|honor]]ing [[addie-bundren|Addie]]) and its physical reality (transporting a corpse). The [[jefferson|town]]speople who recoil from the Bundrens' [[anse-bundren|pa]]ssage are responding to what the buzzards already know: this is not a dignified funeral but a grotesque [[anse-bundren|pa]]rade of decay.

## Appearances in the Text

The buzzards accumulate as the [[the-journey|journey]] continues, their numbers growing in proportion to [[addies-coffin|the coffin]]'s deterioration. By the time the Bundrens reach [[mottson|Mottson]], the buzzards are "circling and circling" (042-001), a dark halo above the [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]] that announces the family's approach before they are visible. The [[jefferson|town]]speople's horror — "Great God," one says, "what have you got in that [[the-bundren-wagon|wagon]]?" — is the human equivalent of the buzzards' interest, and no less instinctive.
