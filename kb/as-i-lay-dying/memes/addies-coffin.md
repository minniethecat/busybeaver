# Addie's Coffin

[[cash-bundren|Cash Bundren]]'s coffin for his [[motherhood|mother]] [[addie-bundren|Addie]] is the novel's most physically prominent object — built within sight of the [[death-and-dying|dying]] woman, carried through [[water-and-river|flood]] and fire, and ultimately buried in [[jefferson|Jefferson]]. It is simultaneously a [[work-and-craft|craft]]sman's [[work-and-craft|labor]] of love and the [[the-journey|journey]]'s increa[[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gly grotesque burden.

## Description

[[cash-bundren|Cash]] builds the coffin on the bevel — his signature technique, explained in a numbered list of thirteen reasons (018-010). The coffin is constructed from fresh lumber, sawed and planed at [[bundren-farm|the farm]], each board measured and fitted with a carpenter's precision. As the [[the-journey|journey]] progresses, the coffin acquires new physical properties: [[water-and-river|water]]logged from [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]], scorched from the barn fire, buzzing with the sounds of decomposition. Its physical deterioration mirrors the moral deterioration of the [[the-journey|journey]] it contains.

## Symbolic Significance

The coffin functions on multiple symbolic levels. As [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s handi[[work-and-craft|work]], it represents love expressed through [[work-and-craft|craft]] — each joint and bevel an unspoken word of [[love|devotion]]. As a container for [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[the-body|body]], it represents the human attempt to bound and control [[death-and-dying|death]] — an attempt the novel's events consistently undermine. As the [[the-journey|journey]]'s burden, it represents the weight of the [[anse-bundren|pa]]st, the cost of [[family-duty|promise]]s kept, and the absurdity of human rituals when stripped of their social context. The coffin's trajectory — from lovingly [[work-and-craft|craft]]ed object to [[water-and-river|water]]logged, scorched, decaying box — traces the arc of the novel's relationship to its own central premise: what begins as sacred [[family-duty|duty]] becomes grotesque farce.

## Appearances in the Text

### First Appearance

The coffin first appears in [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s opening narration: [[cash-bundren|Cash]] sawing and planing boards within sight of [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s window (001-001), the sound of his adze marking the rhythm of the novel's opening movement. [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s observations provide the moral frame: "she could watch [[cash-bundren|Cash]] building the coffin, having to watch him so he would not skimp on it" (006-011).

### Key Scenes

The coffin is central to [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] (023), where it is nearly swept away and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] saves it from the current. It is the object of [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s barn arson (050), where it survives [[darl-burns-the-barn|the fire]] through [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s intervention. And it is finally buried in [[jefferson|Jefferson]], its [[the-journey|journey]] complete, its physical deterioration having accumulated the entire history of the family's ordeal.

### Final Appearance

The coffin's burial in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] is not narrated — the novel's final chapter ([[cash-bundren|Cash]], 063) concerns itself with the family's immediate future. The coffin's disappearance from the narrative is itself significant: once buried, it ceases to be the family's burden, replaced immediately by [[anse-bundren|Anse]]'s new teeth and new wife.
