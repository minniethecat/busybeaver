The Yoknapatawpha River is one of the most significant geographical features in *As I Lay Dying*. When the Bundrens reach it on their journey to Jefferson, they find the bridge destroyed and the river swollen by rain, forcing a dangerous ford that nearly costs them everything.

## The Flooded Crossing

The river is introduced through reports of its dangerous condition. Peabody notes that Anse should have crossed before "the river got too high to get across it" [006-011]. Quick observes that "the river was up and still rising" [020-001], and warns the Bundrens. Despite these warnings, Anse insists on fording the river rather than taking the longer route.

The river becomes a character in its own right — Darl's description reaches toward the elemental: "Like our rivers, our land: opaque, slow, violent" [011-020]. This meditation connects the physical river to the deeper currents of the novel's preoccupation with nature's indifference to human designs.

## The Catastrophe

The actual crossing is the novel's most violent sequence. Cash, who had been building the coffin with careful precision, is nearly swept away. The wagon overturns, the mules drown, and Addie's coffin is barely recovered from the current [031-001]. The description emphasizes the indistinguishability of river and land: "You couldn't tell where was the river and where the land. It was just all yellow and swirling" [031-003].

After the disaster, the family must regroup. "The river itself is not a hundred yards across" [034-044] — Darl's clinical observation contrasts with the devastation it has caused. The wagon is eventually "hauled clear" [037-007] and the journey resumes, but the river has extracted its price.

## Symbolic Significance

The river functions as both a literal obstacle (setting) and a symbolic boundary (symbolic). It represents the point of no return — once crossed, the family can never go back to what they were. The river's violence exposes the fragility of the family's cohesion and the true cost of Anse's promise.
