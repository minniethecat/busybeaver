# Land

Land in *As I Lay Dying* is simultaneously an economic reality, a marker of identity, and a visual presence that shapes the consciousness of Faulkner's characters. The Bundrens are a land-owning family, however poor; their farm in the hill country of Yoknapatawpha County is the ground of their existence, the soil that Anse has "sweated outen" [033-008]. Yet land is also what they leave behind — and what characters repeatedly gaze across in moments of stillness, as though the landscape itself might yield answers that words cannot.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Through Anse Bundren: Land as Identity and Burden

For Anse Bundren, land is the defining fact of his existence. He is a farmer, bound to the soil by labor and inheritance. His identification with the land is so complete that it becomes a kind of imprisonment: he looks "out over the land" in nearly every chapter in which he appears [005-012], [008-005], [008-018], [008-024], as though the horizon itself were a wall. The land for Anse is something to be worked and endured, not enjoyed or transformed. It is the source of his meager livelihood and the stage for his complaints; it is also, paradoxically, what he fears to leave, even as he insists on the journey to Jefferson. His famous declaration — "No man so much as looks at the ground he stands on" [008-005] — reveals his understanding of land as the foundation that goes unappreciated until it is jeopardized.

### Through Darl Bundren: Land as Perception

Darl experiences land not as property but as pure visual phenomenon. His gaze across the landscape — "the land runs out of Darl's eyes" [030-004] — suggests a consciousness that dissolves boundaries between self and environment. Darl sees the land as shape, color, and distance; his descriptions lack Anse's proprietary concern and instead register the land's formal qualities. This perceptual relationship to land aligns with Darl's broader role as the novel's aesthetic consciousness, but it also marks his alienation: to see land as form rather than home is to stand outside the social world that land anchors.

### Through the Journey: Land Left Behind

The journey to Jefferson is a journey away from the Bundren land. Each mile the wagon travels carries the family farther from the ground that defines them. The road that crosses the landscape becomes a line of separation between the known world of the farm and the unknown territory of the town. The river crossing, in which the boundaries between land and water dissolve — "you could not tell where was the river and where the land" [031-003] — represents a moment of maximum dislocation, when the very category of "land" becomes uncertain.

### Through Rural Community

Land also defines the social world of the novel. The farms of Samson, Armstid, and Gillespie are not merely settings but extensions of their owners' identities, each homestead a self-contained moral and economic universe. The novel's community is a community of landowners, and the Bundrens' passage across their neighbors' land — stopping at each farm for shelter, assistance, and judgment — maps the social geography of rural Yoknapatawpha onto the physical landscape.

## Critical Interpretation

In Faulkner's work, land consistently functions as what critic Myra Jehlen called "the ground of identity" — the physical basis from which character emerges. The Bundrens' relationship to their land reflects the broader Southern agrarian experience: ownership without wealth, rootedness without mobility, labor without accumulation. The land is both home and trap, identity and limitation.

The novel's treatment of land also engages with the Jeffersonian ideal of the yeoman farmer, a myth that Faulkner simultaneously invokes and undermines. Anse Bundren is a landowner in the Jeffersonian sense, but he is no virtuous agrarian; his relationship to the land is one of resentment and obligation rather than stewardship or pride.
