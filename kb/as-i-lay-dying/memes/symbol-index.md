## Symbol and Substance

In Faulkner's Mississippi, symbols are never merely literary devices: [[addies-coffin|Addie's coffin]] is both a symbol of the family's burden and a physical object Cash constructs with obsessive precision; [[the-buzzards|the buzzards]] that circle the wagon are at once real carrion birds and emblems of death's imminence; [[cashs-broken-leg|Cash's leg]] in its concrete cast literalizes Anse's selfishness and the novel's meditation on misguided care. The symbol is always first a thing — wood, flesh, iron, river water — before it accrues meaning through the consciousnesses that perceive it.
