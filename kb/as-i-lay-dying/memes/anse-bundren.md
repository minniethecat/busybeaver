# Anse Bundren

Anse Bundren is the patriarch of the Bundren family, the husband of [[addie-bundren|Addie]], and the man whose [[family-duty|promise]] to bury her in [[jefferson|Jefferson]] sets the entire [[the-journey|journey]] in motion. He is simultaneously the [[the-journey|quest]]'s guardian and its most self-interested participant.

## Role in the Novel

Anse is the novel's most morally ambiguous figure. He invokes his [[family-duty|promise]] to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] as sacred [[family-duty|duty]], yet the fulfillment of that [[family-duty|duty]] coincides with the satisfaction of his own desires: new false teeth and, in the novel's final pages, a new wife — the "duck-shaped woman" he introduces to his children with the [[language-and-meaning|words]] "Meet Mrs. Bundren" (059-001). Is Anse a man honoring a sacred [[family-duty|promise]] or exploiting one? Faulkner refuses to answer, and this irresolution is the point: moral purity and self-interest are inseparable in Anse, as perhaps they are in everyone.

His signature pronouncement — "If He'd a aimed for me to be always a-moving and going, He'd a put me on wheels" — is simultaneously a confession of laziness and a statement of principle. Anse does not move unless forced; when forced, he moves with a stubbornness that is indistinguishable from [[love|devotion]].

> It made him look a foot taller, kind of holding his head up, hangdog and proud too, and then we see her behind him, carrying the other grip — a kind of duck-shaped woman all dressed up, with them kind of hard-looking pop eyes like she was daring ere a man to say nothing. … "It's Cash and Jewel and Vardaman and Dewey Dell," pa says, kind of hangdog and proud too, with his teeth and all, even if he wouldn't look at us. "Meet Mrs. Bundren," he says. [059-023–024]

## Narrative Voice

Anse's four chapters are characterized by self-pity, folksy fatalism, and a relentless focus on his own misfortunes. His [[language-and-meaning|language]] is peppered with proverbs, religious clichés, and complaints about the weather, the [[the-journey|road]], his sons, and the universe's general unfairness. He speaks of "God's will" (012-022) as a force that justifies his inaction and displaces his responsibility. Yet beneath the verbal fog, Anse's narration reveals a certain cunning — an instinct for survival that has kept him going through poverty, loss, and the disaster of the [[the-journey|journey]].

> Durn that road. And it fixing to rain, too. I can stand here and same as see it with second-sight, a-shutting down behind them like a wall, shutting down betwixt them and my given promise. [009-001]

> I says I got some regard for what folks says about my flesh and blood even if you haven't, even if I have raised such a durn passel of boys, and when you fixes it so folks can say such about you, it's a reflection on your ma, I says, not me: I am a man and I can stand it; it's on your womenfolks, your ma and sister that you should care for. [026-001]

## Key Relationships

**With [[addie-bundren|Addie]]**: Anse's marriage to [[addie-bundren|Addie]] is the novel's great void at the center. [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s posthumous narration reveals her contempt for him: "And then he died. He did not know he was dead" (040-022). Yet Anse keeps his [[family-duty|promise]] to her — at enormous cost to his family and with considerable benefit to himself. Whether this constitutes love, [[family-duty|duty]], or exploitation is the question the novel refuses to resolve.

**With His Children**: Anse's relationship to his children is defined by instrumental use. He takes [[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s abortion money for his teeth; he commits [[darl-bundren|Darl]] to the asylum to avoid paying for the burned barn; he relies on [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s physical strength and [[cash-bundren|Cash]]'s [[work-and-craft|craftsmanship]] without acknowledging either as gifts. And yet the children follow him, obey him, and in the end accept his new wife. The novel offers no explanation for this compliance beyond the brute fact of family structure.
