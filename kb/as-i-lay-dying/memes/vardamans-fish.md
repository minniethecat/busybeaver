# Vardaman's Fish

[[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman Bundren]]'s fish — caught, cleaned, and dismembered on the day his [[motherhood|mother]] dies — is the novel's most famous animal image and the key to its youngest narrator's understanding of [[death-and-dying|death]].

## Description

[[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] catches "the biggest fish I ever saw" (009-018) and brings it home, where he cleans it, cutting it into pieces. The fish's physical transformation — from living whole to dead [[anse-bundren|pa]]rts — becomes, in [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s mind, the model for understanding what has happened to his [[motherhood|mother]]. His famous conclusion, "My [[motherhood|mother]] is a fish" (019-001), is not madness but a child's attempt to bridge the gap between experience and abstraction: if a fish can be alive and then be cut into not-fish, perhaps a [[motherhood|mother]] can be alive and then be dead. The equation is logically flawed but emotionally precise.

## Symbolic Role

The fish serves as [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s private theology of [[death-and-dying|death]]. Unable to grasp the abstraction "[[death-and-dying|death]]," he substitutes the concrete: the fish's transformation from living creature to cut pieces [[anse-bundren|pa]]rallels his [[motherhood|mother]]'s transformation from living woman to dead [[the-body|body]]. The blood on his hands — "not-fish now, not-blood on my hands and overalls" (013-001) — marks the boundary between the thing and its transformation, the living and the dead. [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s later drilling of holes in [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin (so she can breathe) extends this logic: if the fish died because it was cut, the [[motherhood|mother]] died because she was sealed in a box. Both explanations are wrong, but they are attempts to make sense of a world that offers no satisfying explanations.

## Appearances in the Text

### First Appearance

[[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]] announces his catch: "It was full of blood and guts as a hog" (009-018). He cleans it at the same time [[cash-bundren|Cash]] is building [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] and [[addie-bundren|Addie]] is [[death-and-dying|dying]] — the three events (catching, building, [[death-and-dying|dying]]) linked by [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s narrative consciousness.

### Key Scene

The articulation "My [[motherhood|mother]] is a fish" (019-001) stands as a [[sin-and-judgment|sin]]gle-sentence chapter — the novel's shortest and most famous. Its isolation on the [[anse-bundren|pa]]ge, surrounded by white s[[anse-bundren|pa]]ce, gives it the weight of a philosophical proposition. [[vardaman-bundren|Vardaman]]'s subsequent reflections on the fish — cutting it, watching it become not-fish — constitute his chapter-long meditation on the relationship between being and non-being, expressed in the only [[language-and-meaning|language]] available to him: the [[language-and-meaning|language]] of things.
