# Sin and Judgment

The tension between religious concepts of sin — articulated most forcefully by [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]] and the [[whitfield|Reverend Whitfield]] — and the novel's portrayal of human action as driven by bodily need, [[family-duty|family obligation]], and private desperation rather than moral choice. Faulkner presents a world in which the [[language-and-meaning|language]] of sin is omnipresent but inadequate to the reality of human suffering.

## Religious Framework

The novel's religious discourse is dominated by [[cora-tull|Cora Tull]], whose Calvinist piety interprets every event as divine will and every misfortune as judgment upon the sinful. Her moralizing voice is present from the opening chapters: she condemns [[addie-bundren|Addie]] for her pride, judges the Bundrens for their refusal to bury [[addie-bundren|Addie]] near home, and sees [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] disaster as "the hand of the Lord" in retribution for "sinful" behavior (009-015). [[cora-tull|Cora]]'s frame[[work-and-craft|work]] is totalizing — nothing happens outside God's plan — and her function in the novel is to provide the voice that the narrative itself systematically undermines.

The [[whitfield|Reverend Whitfield]], by contrast, represents institutionalized religious hypocrisy. His affair with [[addie-bundren|Addie]] — revealed in his own monologue (chapter 41) — is a confession of lust dressed in the [[language-and-meaning|language]] of spiritual crisis. His decision to cross [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] and confess to [[anse-bundren|Anse]] is motivated by terror of exposure, not genuine repentance; when he arrives to find [[addie-bundren|Addie]] already dead, his relief is [[anse-bundren|pa]]l[[anse-bundren|pa]]ble: "He has been watching me all the time ... Praise be to God."

## Addie's Counter-Theology

[[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]'s single chapter (ch040) provides the novel's most radical rejection of sin as a [[language-and-meaning|meaning]]ful category. Her famous declaration — "I would be I; I would let him be the shape of my lying where I was not" — and her hatred of the word "love" as "just a shape to fill a lack" dismantle the linguistic and moral frame[[work-and-craft|work]]s that [[cora-tull|Cora]] and [[whitfield|Whitfield]] depend upon. For [[addie-bundren|Addie]], "sin" is a word that others impose on experience; her own experience of her [[the-body|body]], her affair, and her children resists this vocabulary entirely:

> "I would think of sin as I would think of the clothes we both wore in the world's face, of the circumspection necessary because he was he and I was I" (040-015).

## Dewey Dell and Bodily "Sin"

[[dewey-dell-bundren|Dewey Dell]]'s pregnancy is the novel's most concrete instance of "sin" as socially defined — but nothing in her own consciousness treats it as such. Her four monologues are entirely free of moral self-judgment; her pregnancy is a physical fact to be dealt with, not a transgression to be confessed. The irony of her situation — that the one character who might be expected to feel shame feels none, while the characters who speak most of sin ([[cora-tull|Cora]], [[whitfield|Whitfield]]) are the most morally com[[family-duty|promise]]d — is characteristic of Faulkner's treatment of the theme.

## Critical Context

Faulkner's treatment of sin in As I Lay Dying has been read alongside his b[[the-journey|road]]er Southern Calvinist inheritance. Critics note that the novel retains the structural intensity of a religious worldview — the [[the-journey|journey]] as [[the-journey|pilgrimage]], the [[the-body|body]] as vessel, [[language-and-meaning|language]] as failed mediation — while evacuating it of doctrinal content. The result is a world of moral weight without moral certainty, in which "sin" names a relationship to judgment rather than to wrongdoing.
