# Wind

Wind in *As I Lay Dying* is the element of mediation — it carries smells, sounds, and the pressure of the coming storm across the landscape that the Bundrens must traverse. Unlike rain, which falls and floods, or the river, which must be crossed, wind is invisible; its effects are registered in what it moves, carries, and announces. Faulkner uses wind to create the novel's distinctive atmospheric texture, a world in which the air itself is thick with sensory information that characters must process and interpret.

## Manifestations in the Text

### Wind as Harbinger

The wind in the novel's opening chapters is associated with the coming rain. It carries the pressure change that signals the storm, the feeling of imminent weather that hangs over the Bundren farm. Characters read the wind the way they read each other's faces — as a source of information about what is to come. Darl, with his characteristic perceptual acuity, is particularly attuned to the wind's messages: his narrations register the quality of the air, the direction of the breeze, the way the wind moves through the cedar trees.

### Wind as Carrier of Smell

Perhaps the wind's most significant narrative function is as the carrier of the corpse's odor. The smell of Addie's decaying body does not stay contained within the wagon; the wind spreads it, announcing the Bundrens' approach to communities downwind before the wagon is visible. The wind thus functions as a force of exposure, making private realities public, transforming the family's sacred obligation into a communal nuisance. Characters who would prefer to ignore the Bundrens cannot ignore what the wind brings them.

### Wind and the Landscape

Faulkner's landscape descriptions are saturated with wind imagery — the wind in the cedar trees, the wind across the cotton fields, the hot July wind that "smells" of the season [003-002]. The wind is one of the ways the novel renders the non-human world as active and present, not merely a backdrop for human drama but a co-participant in the unfolding events. The wind does not cause the novel's catastrophes — the rain, the river, and human choices do that — but it shapes the sensory field in which those catastrophes are experienced and narrated.

### Wind and Consciousness

For Darl in particular, the wind seems to participate in consciousness itself. His narrations blur the boundary between internal and external, and the wind — moving, invisible, everywhere and nowhere — serves as an objective correlative for his own diffuse, boundaryless awareness. When Darl describes the world, he describes the wind in it; when he describes his own mind, the language he uses echoes his descriptions of wind.

## Critical Interpretation

The wind motif connects Faulkner's novel to a tradition of nature writing in which atmospheric forces are invested with symbolic and even metaphysical significance. The wind in *As I Lay Dying* is not anthropomorphized — it does not have intentions or meanings — but it is rendered as a force that shapes human experience in ways that exceed human control or comprehension. This treatment of nature aligns with the novel's broader naturalistic vision while preserving the sensory richness that distinguishes Faulkner's prose.
