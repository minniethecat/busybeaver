# The Road

The road from the Bundren farm to Jefferson is the spine of *As I Lay Dying*'s narrative geography. It is not a single place but a corridor of movement, connecting the isolated hill country homestead to the county seat where Addie Bundren wished to be buried. Along this road, the family encounters neighbors, strangers, obstacles, and transformations. The road is both literal — a dirt track through rural Mississippi — and figurative, embodying the journey's function as a test of character, obligation, and endurance.

## Description

The road is a dirt road, subject to the weather that dominates the novel's early chapters. When dry, it is dusty; when the rains come, it turns to treacherous mud. Faulkner describes it through the sensory impressions of his narrators: the dust that clings to wagon wheels, the ruts that catch the wheels, the way it "vanishes beneath the wagon as though it were a ribbon" [010-002]. The road passes landmarks that anchor the journey in a specific geography: the spring where Tull's wagon stands [020-001], the bluff overlooking the Bundren land, the washed-out bridge that forces the river crossing, and the various farms and homesteads where the family stops.

## Role in the Novel

The road serves multiple narrative functions. It is the literal path of Addie's funeral procession, the route the family must travel to honor her dying wish. It is also the stage on which the Bundrens' character is tested and revealed: each encounter along the road — with Armstid, with Moseley, with the marshal in Jefferson — exposes the family's deteriorating condition and the community's shifting response to their quest.

The road's condition directly shapes the novel's central crisis. The rains that Anse repeatedly predicts render the roads nearly impassable. "With the roads like they are now, it won't take long," Cora observes [005-019], but the opposite proves true: the muddy roads slow the journey, forcing the family to spend days on what should be a short trip, and the swollen river makes the direct route impossible. The road thus becomes an antagonist, a force that resists the family's purpose and reveals the gap between intention and reality.

## Key Scenes

### The Departure

The journey begins as the wagon pulls onto the road from the Bundren farm, with Addie's coffin loaded and the family assembled. Darl's narration captures the moment with characteristic perceptual intensity: the road vanishing beneath the wagon, the two sets of bobbing mule ears, the sense of forward motion into an uncertain landscape [010-002].

### The Spring and the Washed-Out Bridge

At the spring where Tull's wagon normally stands, the family confronts the first major obstacle: the bridge is gone, washed out by the flood [020-001]. This discovery forces the decision to ford the river, a choice that will result in catastrophe — the drowning of the mule team, the loss of the wagon's contents, and Cash's broken leg.

### The Mud and the Delays

As the journey continues, the road conditions deteriorate. "When the road crosses Whiteleaf, the willows stoop under the weight of the rain" [027-009]. The mud slows progress to a crawl. The family spends nights at strangers' farms — Samson's, Armstid's, Gillespie's — each stop marking the road's refusal to yield a swift passage.

### The Final Approach to Jefferson

The road's character changes as the family nears Jefferson. The rural dirt gives way to town streets, and the community's response shifts from neighborly aid to urban indifference and hostility. The road that began as the Bundrens' own familiar territory ends in the alien space of the county seat, where Anse will borrow shovels, buy new teeth, and ultimately acquire a new wife.
