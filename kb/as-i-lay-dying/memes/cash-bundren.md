# Cash Bundren

The eldest Bundren child, a carpenter who builds [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s coffin on the family porch while she lies [[death-and-dying|dying]] inside the house. Cash is a narrator of few [[language-and-meaning|words]] whose [[work-and-craft|craftsmanship]] and stoic physical endurance define his identity. His [[cashs-broken-leg|broken leg]] — rebroken during [[the-river-crossing-location|the river]] [[water-and-river|crossing]] and set in concrete by [[anse-bundren|Anse]] — is one of the novel's most powerful images of care gone grotesquely wrong.

## Role in the Novel

Cash is the practical, physical center of the Bundren family — the one who builds, fixes, and carries. While [[darl-bundren|Darl]] perceives and [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]] acts, Cash constructs: he builds [[addies-coffin|the coffin]] plank by plank, measuring each cut against [[addie-bundren|Addie]]'s [[the-body|body]] as she dies in the next room. His relationship to the [[the-journey|journey]] is defined by [[work-and-craft|work]] rather than emotion; he [[anse-bundren|pa]]rtici[[anse-bundren|pa]]tes not out of filial [[love|devotion]] but because he is a Bundren, and Bundrens do what must be done.

## Biography

Cash is the eldest son of [[addie-bundren|Addie]] and [[anse-bundren|Anse Bundren]]. Before the novel's events, he fell from a church roof while helping to build it, breaking his leg — an injury that is still healing when [[addie-bundren|Addie]] dies. He is a carpenter by trade and temperament; his narrative chapters are characterized by numbered, procedural thinking ("1. The bevelled boards are for the sides. 2. The square boards are for the ends"), reflecting a mind that processes experience through the logic of construction.

## Narrative Voice

Cash's five chapters — including the famous list-form chapter 18 — are the novel's most stripped-down prose. His [[language-and-meaning|language]] is direct, unadorned, and procedural; he narrates the way he builds, one piece at a time. His final [[sin-and-judgment|judgment]] on [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s committal — "This world is not his world; this life his life" — is one of the novel's most com[[anse-bundren|pa]]ssionate statements, and its simplicity is characteristic of Cash's narrative economy.

## Key Relationships

**[[addie-bundren|Addie Bundren]]**: Cash's relationship with his [[motherhood|mother]] is expressed through his [[work-and-craft|carpentry]] — he builds her coffin with the same care he would give any piece of [[work-and-craft|work]], and his refusal to dramatize her [[death-and-dying|death]] is itself a form of [[love|devotion]] distinct from [[jewel-bundren|Jewel]]'s rage or [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s philosophical des[[anse-bundren|pa]]ir.

**[[darl-bundren|Darl Bundren]]**: Cash and [[darl-bundren|Darl]] form a [[anse-bundren|pa]]ir of complementary perceptions — [[darl-bundren|Darl]] sees and understands, Cash builds and endures. Cash's final chapter acknowledges [[darl-bundren|Darl]]'s committal with a sym[[anse-bundren|pa]]thy that the rest of the family cannot express.
