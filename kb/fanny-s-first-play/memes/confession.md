## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, confession is the act by which characters reveal their true selves and their hidden transgressions. It is the dramatic mechanism through which the play's moral argument is advanced — the moment when the illusions of respectability are voluntarily surrendered in favour of honest self-knowledge. Shaw treats confession not as a religious sacrament but as a secular moral necessity: the precondition for authentic relationships, genuine self-respect, and freedom from the tyranny of social convention.

## In the Play

### Bobby's Confession

The first major confession occurs in Act II, when Bobby Gilbey reveals to Margaret Knox the truth he has been hiding. "I wasn't at Brighton, Meg. I'd better tell you: you're bound to find out sooner or later," he begins. "He begins his confession humbly, avoiding her gaze. Meg: it's rather awful: you'll think me no end of a beast. I've been in prison" [003-024]. Bobby's confession is motivated by pragmatic necessity — "you're bound to find out" — but the stage direction's emphasis on humility and averted gaze suggests genuine shame.

Bobby expects his confession to end his engagement: "Of course I couldn't hold you to our engagement after that. I was writing to you to break it off" [003-028]. He assumes that his social disgrace disqualifies him from respectable marriage. But the confession produces an unexpected result: Margaret confesses in turn that she too has been in prison — "That's what I was in for" [003-031]. The mutual confession transforms their relationship, replacing a conventional engagement based on social compatibility with a bond founded on shared experience and honest self-knowledge.

### Margaret's Confession to Her Parents

Margaret's confession to her parents in Act II is the moral centre of the inner play. She does not attempt to conceal or minimise what she has done: "Drunk and assaulting the police! Forty shillings or a month!" [003-062]. When her father cannot process the reality — "My daughter in Holloway Gaol!" — she responds with devastating clarity: "All the women in Holloway are somebody's daughters. Really, father, you must make up your mind to it" [003-070].

Her confession is not an apology but a declaration of self-knowledge. She refuses to say she is sorry: "I wasn't sorry. The one thing that gave me any satisfaction was getting in that smack on his mouth; and I said so" [003-090]. This refusal to perform contrition outrages her parents' expectations but establishes Margaret as the play's moral heroine — a character who has confronted the reality of her own nature and refuses to retreat into comfortable falsehood.

Margaret's confession is also a theological challenge. She frames her experience in religious language — "I've made a sort of descent into hell" [003-108] — and declares her purpose as redemptive: "it seems to me now that nothing is worth doing but redeeming people from it" [003-112]. Her mother's response — "They are redeemed already if they choose to believe it" — is met with Margaret's devastating rejoinder: "What's the use of that if they don't choose to believe it?" [003-113].

### Dora's Unapologetic Honesty

Dora Delaney represents an alternative model of confession: she never conceals who she is. When she arrives at the Gilbey household to tell them about Bobby's imprisonment, she speaks with unflinching directness: "Bobby's been and got hiself into trouble" [002-080]. She makes no excuses for herself: "I'm afraid it was all my fault" [002-050], yet she is not ashamed. Her honesty disarms the Gilbeys' moral condemnation — she is, as she says, "too much the lady to lose my temper" [002-094].

### The Final Confession: Juggins

The play's final confession comes from Juggins, who reveals his true identity as the brother of a duke. His story is itself a confession of past sin — he "once insulted a servant" who "rebuked me for trifling with a girl of his own class" [005-080]. The servant's words — "God would remember" — "stuck like a poisoned arrow" [005-084], leading Juggins to apprentice himself as a footman. His confession completes the play's argument: every character, regardless of class, has a truth to tell and a past to reckon with.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface does not use the word "confession" but articulates its moral function. He argues for the superiority of honest disgrace over hypocritical respectability: "the young had better have their souls awakened by disgrace, capture by the police, and a month's hard labor, than drift along from their cradles to their graves doing what other people do for no other reason than that other people do it" [P01-001]. Confession, in this framework, is not a punishment but a liberation — the act that "awakens" the soul.

The play's title itself is a confession of sorts. *Fanny's First Play* announces its amateur status, its author's inexperience, its self-conscious modesty. Shaw's strategy of concealed authorship transforms the entire work into a confession that cannot be made until the Epilogue, when Trotter reveals Fanny's identity and the truth is finally spoken.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period saw intense public debate about confession, sin, and redemption. The Salvation Army — which Margaret attends before her night of transgression — had made public confession a central feature of its evangelical practice. The suffragette movement, in which Fanny participates, transformed confession into a political act: hunger strikers publicly testified to the brutality of forced feeding, turning personal suffering into political witness.

Shaw's treatment of confession draws on both religious and secular traditions. The language of "descent into hell" and "redemption" is biblical, but the content is thoroughly modern. For Shaw, confession is not about sin and salvation but about truth and freedom. The characters who confess — Bobby, Margaret, Juggins — are not punished but liberated. The characters who refuse to confess — Mr. Gilbey, who wants to "keep it dark" [003-118]; Mr. Knox, who begs Margaret "not to let it out" [003-128] — are the play's moral failures.
