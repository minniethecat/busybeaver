## Definition

Pleasure in *Fanny's First Play* reveals the class and moral divisions among its characters. The Gilbey-Knox world has domesticated pleasure into scheduled, respectable forms — dinner parties, "at home" days — while the younger characters discover a wilder, more dangerous pleasure that the respectable world can only classify as sin. Shaw's implicit argument is that pleasure itself is morally neutral; what matters is whether it is genuine or merely conventional.

## In the Play

Mrs. Gilbey's account of her social calendar is a masterpiece of unconscious self-revelation: "I'm at home on first Thursdays. And we have the Knoxes to dinner every Friday... So that's two evenings pleasure every week" (002-334). The pleasure here is entirely social and entirely scheduled — it is the pleasure of being seen to be respectable, not the pleasure of genuine enjoyment.

Margaret's night out offers a radical alternative. "I never enjoyed anything so much," she says of the dancing (003-477). Her pleasure is spontaneous, physical, and shared with strangers — everything that respectable pleasure is not. The parents cannot process this: for them, pleasure that does not follow the rules is not pleasure at all but wickedness.

Duvallet's speech extends the critique internationally. His praise of England as a place of freedom includes the observation that the French are "stifled with prudery, hypocrisy and the tyranny of the family and the home" (004-919) — stifled, that is, in their capacity for genuine pleasure.
