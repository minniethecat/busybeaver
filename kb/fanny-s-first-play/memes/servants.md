## Definition

Servants in *Fanny's First Play* are both a social reality — the domestic labor that sustains the respectable household — and a dramatic device through which Shaw exposes the fictions of [[class]] hierarchy. The play features two footmen: the unnamed footman of the Induction who announces the guests at Florence Towers, and [[juggins|Juggins]], the Gilbeys' footman who turns out to be the brother of a duke. Together they embody Shaw's argument that the servant class sees more, knows more, and often possesses more genuine distinction than the masters they serve.

The footman in livery is the most visible sign of middle-class aspiration — a human marker of status whose function is to be seen performing deference. Shaw's treatment of this figure is comic but pointed: the servant is always watching, always understanding, and always, in some sense, acting.

## In the Play

### Juggins: The Knowing Servant

[[juggins|Juggins]] is the most intelligent character in the inner play and arguably in the entire work. He is introduced as "a rather low-spirited man of thirty-five or more, of good appearance and address, and iron self-command" (002-030). His "iron self-command" is the key to his character: he performs servitude with perfect competence while maintaining a private self that his employers cannot access.

His exchanges with [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] reveal the servant as moral tutor. When Bobby asks how to get out of an engagement, Juggins delivers a crisp ethical analysis: "You can't get out of an engagement without behaving like a cad if the lady wishes to hold you to it" (004-025). His advice is more honest and more principled than anything Bobby's parents offer.

The revelation of Juggins's true identity — brother of a duke, former Guards officer — transforms the play's understanding of service. He has been, all along, a gentleman playing a servant, and his performance has been so convincing that no one suspected. The implication is radical: the difference between gentleman and servant is a matter of costume and convention, not nature.

### The Footman as Social Observer

The Induction's footman performs a similar function in the frame story. He is the first character we see, and his interactions with [[savoyard|Savoyard]] establish the social protocols of Florence Towers. He is, like Juggins, a figure of competence and discretion — the machinery that makes the Count's aesthetic life possible.

Shaw's stage directions and dialogue make clear that servants see everything. When [[dora-delaney|Dora]] buttonholes Juggins — "You won't let on downstairs, old man, will you?" (002-046) — she acknowledges that the servant class has its own information network, its own judgments, its own solidarity.

### The Performance of Deference

The master-servant relationship is exposed as a theatrical performance. Juggins's explanation — "Your father pays me to call you sir; and as I take the money, I keep my part of the bargain" (004-015) — reduces deference to a commercial transaction. He would not call Bobby "sir" if he were not paid to do it. The social hierarchy that deference appears to express is revealed as an economic arrangement: the servant sells the performance of inferiority, and the master buys the performance of superiority.

This insight, applied to the play's larger themes, suggests that all social relations are performances for which people are "paid" in various currencies — money, status, approval. The servant, by being explicitly paid, is more honest about the transaction than the master, who pretends the deference is natural.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's own political commitments — Fabian socialism, with its emphasis on the dignity of labor and the irrationality of class hierarchy — inform the treatment of servants throughout. Juggins is not a figure of pity but of respect: he has earned his [[character]] through work, while his employers have merely inherited or purchased theirs.

The play's resolution — Juggins revealed as a duke's brother, marrying Margaret and presumably leaving service — might seem to undercut this argument by restoring class hierarchy. But Shaw's point is subtler: Juggins's worth has been demonstrated by his performance as a servant, not by his birth. The revelation confirms what the audience should already know — that this man is worthy of respect — rather than creating worth where none existed.

## Key Passages

- **Iron self-command**: "He is a rather low-spirited man of thirty-five or more, of good appearance and address, and iron self-command" — Shaw's introduction of Juggins (002-030).
- **Paid to say sir**: "Your father pays me to call you sir; and as I take the money, I keep my part of the bargain" — Juggins on the economics of deference (004-015).
- **What I am paid for**: "It is what I am paid for" — Juggins on keeping a straight face (002-146).
- **A man with a character**: "As a sober, honest, and industrious domestic servant, who has, I trust, given satisfaction to his employer ... I feel I am a man with a character" — Juggins's self-vindication (004-435).
- **The knowing servant**: "You won't let on downstairs, old man, will you?" — Dora acknowledging the servants' information network (002-046).

## Historical Context

Domestic service was the largest employment category for women in Edwardian England, and the footman was a standard feature of middle-class and upper-class households. The relationship between masters and servants was governed by elaborate codes of deference and distance — the servant was expected to be invisible except when performing a function, and "character" (a reference from a previous employer) was the essential credential.

Shaw's treatment of Juggins participates in a long theatrical tradition of the clever servant who outwits his masters — from Roman comedy through Moliere to Wodehouse's Jeeves. But Shaw gives the tradition a distinctive political edge: Juggins's superiority is not merely intellectual but moral, and his final elevation is not a reward for cleverness but a recognition of worth that was present from the beginning.
