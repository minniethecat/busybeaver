## Definition

Silence in *Fanny's First Play* is a dramatic resource Shaw deploys with precision. The play's stage directions repeatedly call for "dead silence" — moments when speech fails and the unsaid fills the stage. These silences are not absences but presences: the weight of unspoken truths, the power of authority, the shock of recognition.

## In the Play

The most charged silences occur in the Knox household scenes. When [[knox|Knox]] "goes out to the landing and shouts: Margaret! Sudden dead silence" (004-885), the silence that follows his command is dense with meaning: the daughter's defiance, the father's impotence, the household's complicity.

The Epilogue's verdict scene is built around a different kind of silence — the critics' inability to speak coherently about what they have seen. Their critical frameworks having collapsed, what remains is not insight but noise, and behind the noise, an embarrassed silence about their own limitations.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s refusal to apologize to the policeman is itself a form of meaningful silence — the silence of someone who will not speak the words of contrition that convention demands because she does not feel contrition. "I wasn't sorry," she says, breaking the silence that respectability would have imposed (003-467).

## Key Passages

- **Knox's commanding silence**: "Margaret! Sudden dead silence" (004-885).
- **Margaret's defiant silence**: "I wouldn't say I was sorry, or apologize to the policeman" (003-467).
- **The critics' stunned silence**: The Epilogue's verdict scene (005).
