## Setting

Florence — the Italian Renaissance city — is never directly named in the play except as the unspoken referent behind "Florence Towers," the [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda's]] English country house. The Count has named his English seat after the Italian city, signaling that his entire identity is an act of geographical and temporal displacement. He does not live in Florence; he lives in the idea of Florence.

The city functions in the play as a synecdoche for the Count's larger aesthetic project: the rejection of modern England in favor of an imagined Italian paradise. His "palace on the grand canal" is in Venice, but the two Italian cities merge in his rhetoric into a single enchanted elsewhere — "the enchanted region of which great artists alone have had the secret" [001-022].

## In the Play

Florence is present only by implication and association. The Count describes his Italian life in detail to [[cecil-savoyard|Savoyard]]: he speaks Italian, listens to Cimarosa and Pergolesi, and lives in a Venetian palace. His daughter Fanny "has never seen an ugly sight or heard an ugly sound" and "her perambulator was a gondola" [001-020]. The name of his English house — Florence Towers — completes the triangulation: Venice, Florence, and the English country house form a geographic triad that maps the Count's psyche.

The crucial line is the Count's boast: "if you are a man with a sense of beauty you can make an earthly paradise for yourself in Venice on 1500 pounds a year" [001-019]. The phrase "earthly paradise" captures the Count's utopian aestheticism — his belief that beauty, properly curated, can create a refuge from the "soot and fog and mud and east wind" of modern England [001-022].

## Dramatic Function

Florence-as-ideal is the play's target. The inner play — with its frank depiction of prison, drunkenness, class tension, and police brutality — is the Count's aesthetic nightmare made flesh. His daughter, raised in the "earthly paradise" of Venice and Florence, has written a play that is aggressively, deliberately modern. The tension between the Count's Florentine ideal and Fanny's dramatic realism is the central conflict of the frame narrative.

The revelation that Fanny "did a month with Lady Constance Lytton" [005-040] shatters the Florentine fantasy definitively. The Count's response — "She will never return to Venice. I feel now as I felt when the Campanile fell" [005-041] — equates the collapse of his daughter's innocence with the literal collapse of Venice's great bell tower in 1902, a historical event that symbolized for many the end of an era.

## Real-World Context

For Edwardian aesthetes and expatriates, Florence and Venice were twin poles of the Grand Tour imagination. The Anglo-Italian community that the Count represents was a real social phenomenon — wealthy Britons who rejected industrial England for the art, architecture, and (they believed) more cultivated society of Italy. Shaw's portrait of the Count is a gentle but pointed satire of this type: the man who cannot bear English ugliness and retreats into an Italy of the imagination, only to find that his English daughter has been shaped by forces he cannot control.

## Key Passages

- **The earthly paradise**: "if you are a man with a sense of beauty you can make an earthly paradise for yourself in Venice on 1500 pounds a year" [001-019].
- **The enchanted region**: "in the sunshine, in the enchanted region of which great artists alone have had the secret, in the sacred footsteps of Byron, of Shelley, of the Brownings, of Turner and Ruskin" [001-022].
- **Fanny's gondola**: "She has lived in a palace; and her perambulator was a gondola" [001-020].
- **The Campanile falls**: "She will never return to Venice. I feel now as I felt when the Campanile fell" [005-041].
