## Definition

In [[fanny-odowda|Fanny's First Play]], "respectability" denotes the outward performance of moral and social propriety demanded by Edwardian middle-class society. It is a code of conduct measured not by inner virtue but by public appearances — the right address, the right manners, the right church attendance. Shaw treats respectability not as a moral achievement but as a social facade, a "pretending" that substitutes for genuine ethical reflection.

The Preface frames the play's entire moral argument around this concept: Shaw contrasts "mere morality, or the substitution of custom for [[morality|conscience]]" — once "accounted a shameful and cynical thing" — with a world in which "the young had better have their souls awakened by [[shame|disgrace]], capture by the police, and a month's hard labor, than drift along from their cradles to their graves doing what other people do for no other reason than that other people do it" (P01-001).

## In the Play

Respectability functions as the central satirical target of the inner play, operating across three distinct registers:

### The Domestic Temple

The [[gilbey|Gilbey]] household on the [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]] is respectability's domestic shrine. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] polices the boundaries of respectable behavior with anxious vigilance; [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] stakes his identity on being "an honest respectable husband"; and [[juggins|Juggins]] the footman performs a parody of respectable servitude that exposes the code's absurdity (002-032)(002-033).

### The Rebellion of the Young

The young characters each rebel against respectability in different ways, forming a taxonomy of dissent:

| Character | Form of Rebellion | Consequence |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] | Im[[crime-and-punishment|prison]]ment (public [[shame|disgrace]]) | [[family|Family]] crisis threatening social standing (004-007) |
| [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] | Sexual transgression ("went wrong") | Discovery of freedom: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" (004-253) |
| [[dora-delaney|Dora]] | Professional criminality ("Hardened at Eighteen") | Serves as the respectable world's cautionary tale (004-100) |

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s journey is the most radical — she discovers that the loss of respectability is not a fall but a [[freedom|liberation]]. Her mother's eventual recognition — "I never knew we could live without being respectable" — marks the play's moral climax (004-253).

### The Institutional Guarantors

Bobby's rhetorical challenge — "Where's the Government? Where's the [[religion|Church]]? Where's respectability and right reason?" — identifies the institutions that uphold the social order (002-117). The [[church-of-england|Church]], the state, and the unspoken code of "right reason" form a triad of enforcement, each lending the others legitimacy.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface argues that conventional respectability is a mechanism for avoiding true moral engagement. His paradoxical advice to "young people in our suburbs" — "Do something that will get you into trouble" — is a call to break the stranglehold of respectability on the soul (P01-001).

The Induction's theatre critics — [[trotter|Trotter]], [[vaughan|Vaughan]], and [[gunn|Gunn]] — embody respectable critical opinion. Shaw lampoons their inability to recognize genuine [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic merit when it violates their expectations, suggesting that respectability functions as an aesthetic as well as moral filter, blinding its adherents to [[truth]]:

> I hate to see dead people walking about: it is unnatural. And our respectable middle class people are all as dead as mutton. Out of the mouth of Mrs. Knox I have delivered on them the judgment of her God. (P01-001)

## Key Passages

- **The definition of respectability as [[hypocrisy|pretense]]**: "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" — [[knox|Knox]], recognizing the emptiness of the social code (003-098)
- **The discovery of freedom**: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" — [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s revelation, the play's moral center (004-253)
- **The injured husband**: "Who wants to give up being respectable? All this for a pint of whisky" — Knox's wounded dignity exposing the fragility of male respectability (004-287)
- **The institutional challenge**: "Where's the Government? Where's the [[religion|Church]]? Where's respectability and right reason?" — Bobby's rhetorical demand (002-117)
- **The employer's defense**: Knox acknowledges turning girls "out of the shop for being light" to protect his reputation — respectability as economic weapon (003-022)

## Historical Context

Respectability was a defining ideology of the Victorian and Edwardian [[class|middle class]], demanding regular church attendance, sexual propriety, temperance, and the maintenance of appearances. The "[[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]]" address that [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] anxiously verifies (002-032) was emblematic of the lower-middle-class aspiration to gentility — geographically and socially on the circumference of respectable [[london|London]].

Shaw wrote *Fanny's First Play* in 1911, on the eve of social upheavals that would dismantle much of this ideology. The suffragette movement was challenging gender norms, labour unrest was exposing [[class|class]] tensions, and the theatre itself was being transformed by the new [[art-and-theatre|drama]] of Ibsen and Shaw. The play's mockery of respectability was thus both timely and prophetic — within a decade, the First World War would do more to shatter Edwardian respectability than any playwright's satire could.

## The Structure of the Argument

Respectability in Shaw's critique operates through a specific mechanism: it substitutes external judgment (what others think) for internal judgment (what one knows to be right). The Preface states this directly — "the substitution of custom for [[morality|conscience]]" — and the inner play [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tizes it through characters who have internalized the surveillance of their neighbors to the point where they no longer have access to their own moral intuitions.

The resolution Shaw offers is not the abolition of morality but its re-foundation on [[truth|honesty]] rather than appearance. When [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] finally says "I never knew we could live without being respectable," she has not become immoral — she has discovered that [[freedom]] from social surveillance makes genuine moral life possible for the first time.
