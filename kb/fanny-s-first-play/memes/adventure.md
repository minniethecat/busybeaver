## Definition

Adventure in *Fanny's First Play* is what happens when young people leave the orbit of parental authority. [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]]'s fortnight's absence, [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s night of dancing and prison, [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s life of perpetual hustle — each is an adventure that the respectable world can only frame as moral failure. Shaw suggests that adventure — the willingness to risk social standing for genuine experience — is the path to moral maturity.

## In the Play

Bobby's adventure is the least dramatic but perhaps the most significant. He has been away for two weeks — where, the play never specifies — and returns transformed. His parents want confession and penitence; Bobby offers only silence and self-possession. His adventure has made him an adult.

Margaret's adventure is the play's central narrative. Her night out — the theatre, the dancing, the champagne, the fight, the prison — is an adventure that strips away every layer of her respectable identity. What emerges is not a "fallen woman" but a self: "Now that it's all over I'm rather proud of it" (003-477).

Dora has made adventure into a way of life. Her story of fooling the policeman — the genteel performance followed by the helmet-chuck — is adventure as art form. She is never more alive than when she is risking capture.
