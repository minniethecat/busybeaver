## Definition

Theatre criticism in *Fanny's First Play* is both the subject of satire and the mechanism through which satire operates. Shaw, who spent years as a drama critic before becoming a playwright, turns the critical apparatus inside out: the critics in the Induction become characters in a play whose purpose is to expose the bankruptcy of the critical tradition they represent.

## In the Play

The Induction assembles a gallery of critical types: Trotter (the moralistic amateur), Vaughan (the intellectual poseur), Gunn (the silent establishment man), and Flawner Bannal (the press representative). Their collective response to Fanny's play—bewilderment, indignation, condescension—is Shaw's indictment of a critical culture that cannot recognize truth when it sees it, because truth would require the critics to see themselves.

The play-within-the-play structure makes the audience complicit in this critique. We watch the critics watch the play; we see what they cannot see. Shaw thus educates his audience in how to be better readers of drama than the professional critics.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface develops the critique of criticism at length. He argues that English dramatic criticism has never produced a single first-rate critic because it has never been allowed to be honest about English life. Critics are trained to ask "is this play improving?" rather than "is this play true?"—and the distinction, for Shaw, is the difference between art and propaganda [P01-002].

The Preface's discussion of the critics' response to *Fanny's First Play* blurs the line between fiction and reality. Shaw reports that real-life Trotters did exactly what the fictional Trotter does: they denounced the play's immorality while remaining blissfully unaware that they were the targets of its satire. Art imitates life imitating art. As Shaw notes:

> [P01-002] The critics whom I have lampooned in the induction to this play under the names of Trotter, Vaughan, and Gunn will forgive me, I hope, when I tell them that the real authors of the play were themselves.

## Key Passages

The Induction scenes constitute Shaw's most concentrated dramatic treatment of criticism. The critics' dialogue is a masterclass in dramatic irony: everything they say about the play is wrong, and we know it. The concealment of the authorship was, as Shaw explains:

> [P01-003] The concealment of the authorship... was a necessary part of the play. If a secret de Polichinelle can be said to involve concealment, the authorship was kept a secret during the entire run of the piece.

## Historical Context

Shaw wrote drama criticism for *The Saturday Review* from 1895 to 1898, during which he established himself as the most formidable critical voice in London. His critiques of the theatrical establishment—the actor-manager system, the censorship, the star culture—inform every line of the Induction. *Fanny's First Play* can be read as Shaw settling accounts with the institution he had spent years trying to reform from within.
