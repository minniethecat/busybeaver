## Role

Flawner Bannal is one of the four critics assembled by [[savoyard|Cecil Savoyard]] to judge [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]]'s play. At twenty years old, he is the youngest of the four — younger even than Gilbert Gunn (thirty) — and represents a distinct type: the commercial journalist whose critical judgment is not aesthetic but economic. Where Trotter judges by impression, Vaughan by musical-[[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic sensibility, and Gunn by intellectual fashion, Bannal judges by the only criterion that matters to him: the author's name. His presence completes Shaw's taxonomy of English criticism, and his question — "Who's it by?" — delivers the play's most pointed joke about the commerce of reputation.

## In the Play

Bannal is introduced as a last-minute addition to the critic panel, and at a premium: Savoyard had to pay him ten guineas, while the others presumably came for less. Savoyard's justification is revealing — he tells the Count that Bannal "really represents the British playgoer" and that when Bannal likes a thing, "there are a hundred thousand people in [[london|London]] that'll like it" [P01-025]. Bannal's value, in other words, is not critical discernment but commercial reach.

Shaw's stage directions paint an unflattering portrait. Bannal is described as "one of those unemployables of the business class who manage to pick up a living by a sort of courage which gives him cheerfulness, conviviality, and bounce, and is helped out positively by a slight turn for writing, and negatively by a comfortable ignorance and lack of intuition which hides from him all the dangers and [[shame|disgrace]]s that keep men of finer perception in check" [001-005]. This is Shaw at his most savagely satirical — but the description also contains a backhanded recognition of Bannal's survival instinct.

Bannal's [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic function culminates in the Epilogue, when the Count asks each critic for his verdict. Bannal's response is the play's funniest and most devastating exchange:

When the Count asks whether it is a good play, Bannal replies with perfect commercial logic: "If it's by a good author, it's a good play, naturally. That stands to reason. Who is the author? Tell me that; and I'll place the play for you to a hair's breadth" [005-098]. He cannot judge a work on its own terms; he can only place it within a brand hierarchy. Shaw uses Bannal to expose the fundamental absurdity of criticism-by-reputation, a system in which the name on the title page matters more than the words on the stage.

Earlier, Bannal has asked a series of questions that reveal his categorical thinking: "Is it a comedy or a tragedy? Is it a farce or a melo[[art-and-theatre|drama]]? Is it repertory theatre tosh, or really straight paying stuff?" [005-098]. His inability to process a play that refuses genre classification mirrors the broader critical confusion Shaw [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tizes throughout the frame.

## Character Analysis

Bannal is Shaw's portrait of the critic as commercial animal. Unlike Trotter, who has enough self-awareness to recognize his own impressionability, or Vaughan, who has genuine musical knowledge, or Gunn, who has intellectual pretensions, Bannal has no principles at all — only market instincts. And yet, Shaw suggests, this very absence of principle makes Bannal the most honest of the four critics: he does not pretend to be anything other than what he is.

Bannal's youth (twenty) is signifi[[hypocrisy|cant]]. Shaw uses him to satirize the generational dimension of critical authority: here is a barely-adult journalist whose opinion, according to Savoyard, carries more weight than any of his elders. The absurdity lies not in Bannal personally but in a system that assigns cultural authority to those who can most effectively channel public prejudice.

The name "Flawner" is Shaw's invention — a comic, slightly ridiculous name that suits a character who is himself a kind of comic invention. "Bannal" echoes "banal," the perfect surname for a critic whose entire critical vocabulary consists of brand names and genre labels.

## Key Passages

- **The stage direction portrait**: Shaw's extended description of Bannal as an "unemployable of the business class" sustained by "cheerfulness, conviviality, and bounce" [001-005].
- **Savoyard's endorsement**: "Bannal really represents the British playgoer. When he likes a thing, you may take your oath there are a hundred thousand people in [[london|London]] that'll like it" [P01-025].
- **The commercial test**: "Is it repertory theatre tosh, or really straight paying stuff?" [005-098].
- **The brand-logic of criticism**: "If it's by a good author, it's a good play, naturally. That stands to reason. Who is the author? Tell me that; and I'll place the play for you to a hair's breadth" [005-098].
- **The genre question**: "Is it a comedy or a tragedy? Is it a farce or a melo[[art-and-theatre|drama]]?" [005-098].

## Critical Reception

Bannal has been recognized as one of Shaw's most effective satirical creations — a character who is simultaneously ridiculous and truthful. His "Who's it by?" has become a touchstone for discussions of commercial criticism and the cult of the author. The critic Eric Bentley noted that Bannal's question is "the reductio ad absurdum of all criticism that substitutes sociology for judgment" — though Shaw would likely reply that Bannal's commercial sociology is just an extreme version of what all critics do.
