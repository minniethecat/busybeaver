## Definition

Appearance in *Fanny's First Play* is what the respectable world lives by — and what Shaw's comedy systematically demolishes. The play is structured around the gap between how things appear and how they are: the Induction's critics appear authoritative but are incompetent; the parents appear moral but are hypocritical; the children appear delinquent but are the only honest characters in the play.

## In the Play

The critics' appearance of authority is the play's first target. [[trotter|Trotter]] arrives in diplomatic dress (001-127), projecting establishment authority. But his critical judgment proves as decorative as his costume. Appearance, Shaw suggests, is no guide to substance.

Margaret's appearance is radically transformed by her adventure. She tells her mother: "I'm not a bit the sort of girl you thought me. I'm not a bit the sort of girl I thought myself" (003-467). Her external appearance — the good girl, the Sunday school prize-winner — concealed a capacity for wildness, anger, and joy that experience has now revealed.

Juggins is the play's deepest meditation on appearance. He has "good appearance and address, and iron self-command" (001-258), but his true character is hidden beneath his livery. Dora's insistence that he must be more than a footman — that his "manners" don't match his station — is an intuition that appearance does not exhaust reality.

## Key Passages

- **Juggins's appearance**: "A rather low-spirited man... of good appearance and address, and iron self-command" (001-258).
- **Margaret's self-revelation**: "I'm not a bit the sort of girl you thought me" (003-467).
- **The critics' costumes**: Trotter "wears a diplomatic dress, with sword and three-cornered hat" (001-127).
