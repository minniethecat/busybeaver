## Definition

Respect in *Fanny's First Play* operates on two distinct planes that the play carefully distinguishes. There is the respect one commands from others — the social deference owed to a "respectable" household, a "respectable" address, a "respectable" marriage — and there is self-respect, the internal sense of worth that does not depend on external validation. Shaw's argument is that these two forms of respect are not merely different but often opposed: the pursuit of social respect destroys self-respect, and the achievement of self-respect often requires the loss of social standing.

The Preface articulates this distinction precisely: Shaw seeks a "getting into trouble that will combine loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable consideration for other peoples' feelings and interests on every point except their dread of losing their own respectability" (P01-001). The entire play can be read as a dramatization of this formula.

## In the Play

### The Respectable World

The inner play's older generation measures everything by the standard of social respect. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] declares his household "respectable" as if it were a fixed property of the building (002-095). [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] anxiously checks whether the [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]] is "a respectable address" (002-032). [[knox|Mr. Knox]] has "worked hard to get a position and be respectable" and has "turned many a girl out of the shop for being half an hour late at night" — respect as discipline, respect as exclusion (003-025).

The anxiety is constant. Knox "can't" be around other people because he fears "everybody knows it and is sniggering about it" (003-023). The judgment of others is so internalized that it has become a form of self-surveillance, making genuine self-respect impossible.

### The Discovery

The play's turning point comes with the discovery that respect is optional. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]]'s astonished confession — "I never knew we could live without being respectable" (004-256) — is the older generation's moment of liberation. The sky has not fallen; the business has not collapsed; the invitations have not stopped. Respectability was a choice all along, not a necessity.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]] has already made this discovery, articulated most forcefully in her attack on respectability as pretense: "What's the good of pretending? That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending. Thank heaven I've had it knocked out of me once for all!" (003-098). Her self-respect is intact — stronger than ever — precisely because she has stopped caring about the respect of others.

### Juggins: The Man With a Character

[[juggins|Juggins]] embodies the distinction between the two forms of respect. As a footman, he is paid to perform deference — "Your father pays me to call you sir; and as I take the money, I keep my part of the bargain" (004-015) — but his self-respect is entirely independent of his social position. When he reveals his identity, his claim is not to social standing but to [[character]]: "I feel I am a man with a character" (004-435). He has earned his self-respect through work, and it is this, not his ducal connections, that qualifies him for [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s hand.

### Mrs. Knox's Warning

[[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] offers the play's most precise analysis of the relationship between the two kinds of respect. She tells her husband: "if he gives up being respectable he'll go right down to the bottom of the hill. He has no powers inside himself to keep him steady; so let him cling to the powers outside him" (004-287). This is a diagnosis of a man who has social respect but no self-respect — and therefore depends entirely on the opinion of others for his moral stability.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface is built around the distinction between "loss of respectability" and "integrity of self-respect." The two are not only distinct but potentially opposed: the maintenance of respectability often requires the sacrifice of self-respect (by conforming to customs one knows to be hollow), while the achievement of self-respect often requires the sacrifice of respectability (by acting on convictions that society condemns).

The play's advice — "Do something that will get you into trouble" — is advice to prioritize self-respect over social respect. It is a counsel of moral courage, and Shaw acknowledges its difficulty: "how difficult it is to find out a way of getting into trouble that will combine loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect."

## Key Passages

- **The formula**: "getting into trouble that will combine loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable consideration for other peoples' feelings and interests" — Shaw's moral calculus (P01-001).
- **The household**: "I want none of your gaiety here. This is a respectable household" — Gilbey asserting the sanctity of his domestic space (002-095).
- **Pretending**: "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" — Margaret's diagnosis (003-098).
- **Living without it**: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" — Gilbey's astonished discovery (004-256).
- **A man with a character**: "I feel I am a man with a character" — Juggins claiming self-respect through work (004-435).

## Historical Context

The distinction between social respect and self-respect was central to the moral philosophy of the Victorian and Edwardian periods. The ideal of the "self-made man" — Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* (1859) was one of the century's most influential books — linked economic independence to moral worth, while the "Nonconformist conscience" emphasized the individual's direct responsibility to God, bypassing social mediation.

Shaw's contribution was to insist that these two forms of respect could conflict, and that the social form was often the enemy of the personal. This was a radical claim in a society that treated "respectability" as an unqualified good.
