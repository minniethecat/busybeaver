## Definition

Freedom in *Fanny's First Play* is a paradoxical condition: it is achieved not through the exercise of rights but through the experience of [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]]. Bobby, [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], and [[dora-delaney|Dora]] all go to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], and all emerge freer than they were before — freer from the expectations of their parents, freer from the performance of respectability, freer to speak honestly about who they are and what they want. Shaw's argument is that authentic freedom requires the loss of something — "loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable freedom" (P01-001) — and that the institutions that claim to protect freedom (the family, the church, the law) are in fact its enemies.

## In the Play

[[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]] is the play's philosopher of freedom. Her testimony is the clearest statement of the play's thesis: "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free. And when I got free I was what most people call bad" (003-088). The categories "good" and "bad" are revealed as inversions of "unfree" and "free": what society calls goodness is submission; what it calls badness is liberation.

[[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s discovery — "I never knew we could live without being respectable" (004-253) — is the parental generation's version of the same insight. Freedom, for her, is the terrifying realization that respectability is optional, that the entire structure of social obligation that has governed her life is a choice rather than a necessity.

Bobby's freedom takes the form of refusal. He won't marry [[dora-delaney|Dora]] just because convention demands it: "I won't marry her: that's flat" (004-025). His [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] has given him the courage to say no — a freedom his father, who has spent his life being "an honest respectable husband" (004-290), has never possessed.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of freedom is characteristically unsentimental. He does not celebrate liberation as an unambiguous good; he acknowledges its costs. The "getting into trouble that will combine loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable freedom" is a formula for growth that includes loss as a component (P01-001). Freedom is not free.

This is the Shavian version of the liberal dilemma: authentic freedom requires the capacity to choose against convention, but convention exists because most people, most of the time, need guidance. The play's resolution — [[juggins|Juggins]] arranging marriages — suggests that freedom and order can coexist, but only if the order is chosen rather than imposed.

- **The cost of freedom**: "Loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable freedom" — Shaw's formula (P01-001).
- **Living without respectability**: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" — [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] (004-253).
- **The right to say no**: "I won't marry her: that's flat" — Bobby's assertion of freedom (004-025).

## Historical Context

Freedom in the Edwardian context was being redefined by multiple movements. The suffragettes demanded political freedom for women; the labour movement demanded economic freedom for workers; the "[[gender-roles|New Woman]]" demanded social and sexual freedom. Each of these movements understood freedom as the removal of external constraints.

Shaw's contribution was to insist that the most important constraints were internal — the "custom" that substitutes for "[[morality|conscience]]." This placed him in a tradition of radical individualism that ran from Mill through Nietzsche to the existentialists. Freedom was not a political condition but a psychological achievement: the capacity to think and act for oneself.
