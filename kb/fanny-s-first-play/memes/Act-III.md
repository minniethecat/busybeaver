### Act III

[004-001] Again in the **Gilbeys’** dining room. Afternoon. The table is not laid: it is draped in its ordinary cloth, with pen and ink, an exercise book, and schoolbooks on it. **[[bobby-gilbey|Bobby Gilbey]]** is in the armchair, crouching over the fire, reading an illustrated paper. He is a pretty youth, of very suburban gentility, strong and manly enough by nature, but untrained and unsatisfactory, his parents having imagined that domestic restriction is what they call “bringing up.” He has learnt nothing from it except a habit of evading it by deceit.

[004-002] He gets up to ring the bell; then resumes his crouch. **[[juggins|Juggins]]** answers the bell.

[004-003] | Bobby | Juggins. |

[004-004] | Juggins | Sir? |

[004-005] | Bobby | *Morosely sarcastic.* Sir be blowed! |

[004-006] | Juggins | *Cheerfully.* Not at all, sir. |

[004-007] | Bobby | I’m a gaol-bird: you’re a respectable man. |

[004-008] | Juggins | That doesn’t matter, sir. Your father pays me to call you sir; and as I take the money, I keep my part of the bargain. |

[004-009] | Bobby | Would you call me sir if you weren’t paid to do it? |

[004-010] | Juggins | No, sir. |

[004-011] | Bobby | I’ve been talking to [[dora-delaney|Dora]] about you. |

[004-012] | Juggins | Indeed, sir? |

[004-013] | Bobby | Yes. Dora says your name can’t be Juggins, and that you have the manners of a gentleman. I always thought you hadn’t any manners. Anyhow, your manners are different from the manners of a gentleman in my set. |

[004-014] | Juggins | They would be, sir. |

[004-015] | Bobby | You don’t feel disposed to be communicative on the subject of Dora’s notion, I suppose. |

[004-016] | Juggins | No, sir. |

[004-017] | Bobby | *Throwing his paper on the floor and lifting his knees over the arm of the chair so as to turn towards the footman.* It was part of your bargain that you were to valet me a bit, wasn’t it? |

[004-018] | Juggins | Yes, sir. |

[004-019] | Bobby | Well, can you tell me the proper way to get out of an engagement to a girl without getting into a row for breach of promise or behaving like a regular cad? |

[004-020] | Juggins | No, sir. You can’t get out of an engagement without behaving like a cad if the lady wishes to hold you to it. |

[004-021] | Bobby | But it wouldn’t be for her happiness to marry me when I don’t really care for her. |

[004-022] | Juggins | Women don’t always marry for happiness, sir. They often marry because they wish to be married women and not old maids. |

[004-023] | Bobby | Then what am I to do? |

[004-024] | Juggins | Marry her, sir, or behave like a cad. |

[004-025] | Bobby | *Jumping up.* Well, I won’t marry her: that’s flat. What would you do if you were in my place? |

[004-026] | Juggins | I should tell the young lady that I found I couldn’t fulfil my engagement. |

[004-027] | Bobby | But you’d have to make some excuse, you know. I want to give it a gentlemanly turn: to say I’m not worthy of her, or something like that. |

[004-028] | Juggins | That is not a gentlemanly turn, sir. Quite the contrary. |

[004-029] | Bobby | I don’t see that at all. Do you mean that it’s not exactly true? |

[004-030] | Juggins | Not at all, sir. |

[004-031] | Bobby | I can say that no other girl can ever be to me what she’s been. That would be quite true, because our circumstances have been rather exceptional; and she’ll imagine I mean I’m fonder of her than I can ever be of anyone else. You see, Juggins, a gentleman has to think of a girl’s feelings. |

[004-032] | Juggins | If you wish to spare her feelings, sir, you can marry her. If you hurt her feelings by refusing, you had better not try to get credit for considerateness at the same time by pretending to spare them. She won’t like it. And it will start an argument, of which you will get the worse. |

[004-033] | Bobby | But, you know, I’m not really worthy of her. |

[004-034] | Juggins | Probably she never supposed you were, sir. |

[004-035] | Bobby | Oh, I say, Juggins, you are a pessimist. |

[004-036] | Juggins | *Preparing to go.* Anything else, sir? |

[004-037] | Bobby | *Querulously.* You haven’t been much use. *He wanders disconsolately across the room.* You generally put me up to the correct way of doing things. |

[004-038] | Juggins | I assure you, sir, there’s no correct way of jilting. It’s not correct in itself. |

[004-039] | Bobby | *Hopefully.* I’ll tell you what. I’ll say I can’t hold her to an engagement with a man who’s been in quod. That’ll do it. *He seats himself on the table, relieved and confident.* |

[004-040] | Juggins | Very dangerous, sir. No woman will deny herself the romantic luxury of self-sacrifice and forgiveness when they take the form of doing something agreeable. She’s almost sure to say that your misfortune will draw her closer to you. |

[004-041] | Bobby | What a nuisance! I don’t know what to do. You know, Juggins, your cool simple-minded way of doing it wouldn’t go down in [[denmark-hill|Denmark Hill]]. |

[004-042] | Juggins | I daresay not, sir. No doubt you’d prefer to make it look like an act of self-sacrifice for her sake on your part, or provoke her to break the engagement herself. Both plans have been tried repeatedly, but never with success, as far as my knowledge goes. |

[004-043] | Bobby | You have a devilish cool way of laying down the law. You know, in my class you have to wrap up things a bit. Denmark Hill isn’t [[camberwell|Camberwell]], you know. |

[004-044] | Juggins | I have noticed, sir, that Denmark Hill thinks that the higher you go in the social scale, the less sincerity is allowed; and that only tramps and riffraff are quite sincere. That’s a mistake. Tramps are often shameless; but they’re never sincere. Swells﻿—if I may use that convenient name for the upper classes﻿—play much more with their cards on the table. If you tell the young lady that you want to jilt her, and she calls you a pig, the tone of the transaction may leave much to be desired; but it’ll be less Camberwellian than if you say you’re not worthy. |

[004-045] | Bobby | Oh, I can’t make you understand, Juggins. The girl isn’t a scullery-maid. I want to do it delicately. |

[004-046] | Juggins | A mistake, sir, believe me, if you are not a born artist in that line.﻿—Beg pardon, sir, I think I heard the bell. *He goes out.* |

[004-047] |  | ***Bobby**, much perplexed, shoves his hands into his pockets, and comes off the table, staring disconsolately straight before him; then goes reluctantly to his books, and sits down to write. **Juggins** returns.* |

[004-048] | Juggins | *Announcing.* Miss Knox. |

[004-049] |  | ***[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]** comes in. **Juggins** withdraws.* |

[004-050] | Margaret | Still grinding away for that Society of Arts examination, Bobby? You’ll never pass. |

[004-051] | Bobby | *Rising.* No: I was just writing to you. |

[004-052] | Margaret | What about? |

[004-053] | Bobby | Oh, nothing. At least﻿—How are you? |

[004-054] | Margaret | *Passing round the other end of the table and putting down on it a copy of *Lloyd’s Weekly* and her purse-bag.* Quite well, thank you. How did you enjoy Brighton? |

[004-055] | Bobby | Brighton! I wasn’t at﻿—Oh yes, of course. Oh, pretty well. Is your aunt all right? |

[004-056] | Margaret | My aunt! I suppose so. I haven’t seen her for a month. |

[004-057] | Bobby | I thought you were down staying with her. |

[004-058] | Margaret | Oh! was that what they told you? |

[004-059] | Bobby | Yes. Why? Weren’t you really? |

[004-060] | Margaret | No. I’ve something to tell you. Sit down and lets be comfortable. |

[004-061] |  | *She sits on the edge of the table. He sits beside her, and puts his arm wearily round her waist.* |

[004-062] | Margaret | You needn’t do that if you don’t like, Bobby. Suppose we get off duty for the day, just to see what it’s like. |

[004-063] | Bobby | Off duty? What do you mean? |

[004-064] | Margaret | You know very well what I mean. Bobby: did you ever care one little scrap for me in that sort of way? Don’t funk answering: *I* don’t care a bit for you﻿—that way. |

[004-065] | Bobby | *Removing his arm rather huffily.* I beg your pardon, I’m sure. I thought you did. |

[004-066] | Margaret | Well, did you? Come! Don’t be mean. I’ve owned up. You can put it all on me if you like; but I don’t believe you care any more than I do. |

[004-067] | Bobby | You mean we’ve been shoved into it rather by the pars and mars. |

[004-068] | Margaret | Yes. |

[004-069] | Bobby | Well, it’s not that I don’t care for you: in fact, no girl can ever be to me exactly what you are; but we’ve been brought up so much together that it feels more like brother and sister than﻿—well, than the other thing, doesn’t it? |

[004-070] | Margaret | Just so. How did you find out the difference? |

[004-071] | Bobby | *Blushing.* Oh, I say! |

[004-072] | Margaret | I found out from a Frenchman. |

[004-073] | Bobby | Oh, I say! *He comes off the table in his consternation.* |

[004-074] | Margaret | Did you learn it from a Frenchwoman? You know you must have learnt it from somebody. |

[004-075] | Bobby | Not a Frenchwoman. She’s quite a nice woman. But she’s been rather unfortunate. The daughter of a clergyman. |

[004-076] | Margaret | *Startled.* Oh, Bobby! That sort of woman! |

[004-077] | Bobby | What sort of woman? |

[004-078] | Margaret | You don’t believe she’s really a clergyman’s daughter, do you, you silly boy? It’s a stock joke. |

[004-079] | Bobby | Do you mean to say you don’t believe me? |

[004-080] | Margaret | No: I mean to say I don’t believe her. |

[004-081] | Bobby | *Curious and interested, resuming his seat on the table beside her.* What do you know about her? What do you know about all this sort of thing? |

[004-082] | Margaret | What sort of thing, Bobby? |

[004-083] | Bobby | Well, about life. |

[004-084] | Margaret | I’ve lived a lot since I saw you last. I wasn’t at my aunt’s. All that time that you were in Brighton, I mean. |

[004-085] | Bobby | I wasn’t at Brighton, Meg. I’d better tell you: you’re bound to find out sooner or later. *He begins his confession humbly, avoiding her gaze.* Meg: it’s rather awful: you’ll think me no end of a beast. I’ve been in [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]. |

[004-086] | Margaret | You! |

[004-087] | Bobby | Yes, me. For being drunk and assaulting the police. |

[004-088] | Margaret | Do you mean to say that you﻿—oh! this is a letdown for me. *She comes off the table and drops, disconsolate, into a chair at the end of it furthest from the hearth.* |

[004-089] | Bobby | Of course I couldn’t hold you to our engagement after that. I was writing to you to break it off. *He also descends from the table and makes slowly for the hearth.* You must think me an utter rotter. |

[004-090] | Margaret | Oh, has everybody been in prison for being drunk and assaulting the police? How long were you in? |

[004-091] | Bobby | A fortnight. |

[004-092] | Margaret | That’s what I was in for. |

[004-093] | Bobby | What are you talking about? In where? |

[004-094] | Margaret | In quod. |

[004-095] | Bobby | But I’m serious: I’m not rotting. Really and truly﻿— |

[004-096] | Margaret | What did you do to the copper? |

[004-097] | Bobby | Nothing, absolutely nothing. He exaggerated grossly. I only laughed at him. |

[004-098] | Margaret | *Jumping up, triumphant.* I’ve beaten you hollow. I knocked out two of his teeth. I’ve got one of them. He sold it to me for ten shillings. |

[004-099] | Bobby | Now please do stop fooling, Meg. I tell you I’m not rotting. *He sits down in the armchair, rather sulkily.* |

[004-100] | Margaret | *Taking up the copy of *Lloyd’s Weekly* and going to him.* And I tell you I’m not either. Look! Here’s a report of it. The daily papers are no good; but the Sunday papers are splendid. *She sits on the arm of the chair.* See! *Reading:* “Hardened at Eighteen. A quietly dressed, respectable-looking girl who refuses her name”﻿—that’s me. |

[004-101] | Bobby | *Pausing a moment in his perusal.* Do you mean to say that you went on the loose out of pure devilment? |

[004-102] | Margaret | I did no harm. I went to see a lovely dance. I picked up a nice man and went to have a dance myself. I can’t imagine anything more innocent and more happy. All the bad part was done by other people: they did it out of pure devilment if you like. Anyhow, here we are, two gaolbirds, Bobby, disgraced forever. Isn’t it a relief? |

[004-103] | Bobby | *Rising stiffly.* But you know, it’s not the same for a girl. A man may do things a woman mayn’t. *He stands on the hearthrug with his back to the fire.* |

[004-104] | Margaret | Are you scandalized, Bobby? |

[004-105] | Bobby | Well, you can’t expect me to approve of it, can you, Meg? I never thought you were that sort of girl. |

[004-106] | Margaret | *Rising indignantly.* I’m not. You mustn’t pretend to think that *I’m* a clergyman’s daughter, Bobby. |

[004-107] | Bobby | I wish you wouldn’t chaff about that. Don’t forget the row you got into for letting out that you admired Juggins *she turns her back on him quickly*﻿—a footman! And what about the Frenchman? |

[004-108] | Margaret | *Facing him again.* I know nothing about the Frenchman except that he’s a very nice fellow and can swing his leg round like the hand of a clock and knock a policeman down with it. He was in Wormwood Scrubbs with you. I was in Holloway. |

[004-109] | Bobby | It’s all very well to make light of it, Meg; but this is a bit thick, you know. |

[004-110] | Margaret | Do you feel you couldn’t marry a woman who’s been in prison? |

[004-111] | Bobby | *Hastily.* No. I never said that. It might even give a woman a greater claim on a man. Any girl, if she were thoughtless and a bit on, perhaps, might get into a scrape. Anyone who really understood her character could see there was no harm in it. But you’re not the larky sort. At least you usen’t to be. |

[004-112] | Margaret | I’m not; and I never will be. *She walks straight up to him.* I didn’t do it for a lark, Bob: I did it out of the very depths of my nature. I did it because I’m that sort of person. I did it in one of my religious fits. I’m hardened at eighteen, as they say. So what about the match, now? |

[004-113] | Bobby | Well, I don’t think you can fairly hold me to it, Meg. Of course it would be ridiculous for me to set up to be shocked, or anything of that sort. I can’t afford to throw stones at anybody; and I don’t pretend to. I can understand a lark; I can forgive a slip; as long as it is understood that it is only a lark or a slip. But to go on the loose on principle; to talk about religion in connection with it; to﻿—to﻿—well, Meg, I do find that a bit thick, I must say. I hope you’re not in earnest when you talk that way. |

[004-114] | Margaret | Bobby: you’re no good. No good to me, anyhow. |

[004-115] | Bobby | *Huffed.* I’m sorry, Miss Knox. |

[004-116] | Margaret | Goodbye, Mr. [[gilbey|Gilbey]]. *She turns on her heel and goes to the other end of the table.* I suppose you won’t introduce me to the clergyman’s daughter. |

[004-117] | Bobby | I don’t think she’d like it. There are limits, after all. *He sits down at the table, as if to to resume work at his books: a hint to her to go.* |

[004-118] | Margaret | *On her way to the door.* Ring the bell, Bobby; and tell Juggins to show me out. |

[004-119] | Bobby | *Reddening.* I’m not a cad, Meg. |

[004-120] | Margaret | *Coming to the table.* Then do something nice to prevent us feeling mean about this afterwards. You’d better kiss me. You needn’t ever do it again. |

[004-121] | Bobby | If I’m no good, I don’t see what fun it would be for you. |

[004-122] | Margaret | Oh, it’d be no fun. If I wanted what you call fun, I should ask the Frenchman to kiss me﻿—or Juggins. |

[004-123] | Bobby | *Rising and retreating to the hearth.* Oh, don’t be disgusting, Meg. Don’t be low. |

[004-124] | Margaret | *Determinedly, preparing to use force.* Now, I’ll make you kiss me, just to punish you. *She seizes his wrist; pulls him off his balance; and gets her arm round his neck.* |

[004-125] | Bobby | No. Stop. Leave go, will you. |

[004-126] |  | ***Juggins** appears at the door.* |

[004-127] | Juggins | Miss Delaney, Sir. ***Dora** comes in. **Juggins** goes out. **Margaret** hastily releases **Bobby**, and goes to the other side of the room.* |

[004-128] | Dora | *Through the door, to the departing **Juggins**.* Well, you are a Juggins to show me up when there’s company. *To **Margaret** and **Bobby**.* It’s all right, dear: all right, old man: I’ll wait in Juggins’s pantry till you’re disengaged. |

[004-129] | Margaret | Don’t you know me? |

[004-130] | Dora | *Coming to the middle of the room and looking at her very attentively.* Why, it’s never No. 406! |

[004-131] | Margaret | Yes it is. |

[004-132] | Dora | Well, I should never have known you out of the uniform. How did you get out? You were doing a month, weren’t you? |

[004-133] | Margaret | My bloke paid the fine the day he got out himself. |

[004-134] | Dora | A real gentleman! *Pointing to **Bobby**, who is staring open-mouthed.* Look at him. He can’t take it in. |

[004-135] | Bobby | I suppose you made her acquaintance in prison, Meg. But when it comes to talking about blokes and all that﻿—well! |

[004-136] | Margaret | Oh, I’ve learnt the language; and I like it. It’s another barrier broken down. |

[004-137] | Bobby | It’s not so much the language, Meg. But I think﻿—*He looks at **Dora** and stops.* |

[004-138] | Margaret | *Suddenly dangerous.* What do you think, Bobby? |

[004-139] | Dora | He thinks you oughtn’t to be so free with me, dearie. It does him credit: he always was a gentleman, you know. |

[004-140] | Margaret | Does him credit! To insult you like that! Bobby: say that that wasn’t what you meant. |

[004-141] | Bobby | I didn’t say it was. |

[004-142] | Margaret | Well, deny that it was. |

[004-143] | Bobby | No. I wouldn’t have said it in front of Dora; but I do think it’s not quite the same thing my knowing her and you knowing her. |

[004-144] | Dora | Of course it isn’t, old man. *To **Margaret**.* I’ll just trot off and come back in half an hour. You two can make it up together. I’m really not fit company for you, dearie: I couldn’t live up to you. *She turns to go.* |

[004-145] | Margaret | Stop. Do you believe he could live up to me? |

[004-146] | Dora | Well, I’ll never say anything to stand between a girl and a respectable marriage, or to stop a decent lad from settling himself. I have a [[morality|conscience]]; though I mayn’t be as particular as some. |

[004-147] | Margaret | You seem to me to be a very decent sort; and Bobby’s behaving like a skunk. |

[004-148] | Bobby | *Much ruffled.* Nice language that! |

[004-149] | Dora | Well, dearie, men have to do some awfully mean things to keep up their respectability. But you can’t blame them for that, can you? I’ve met Bobby walking with his mother; and of course he cut me dead. I won’t pretend I liked it; but what could he do, poor dear? |

[004-150] | Margaret | And now he wants me to cut you dead to keep him in countenance. Well, I shan’t: not if my whole family were there. But I’ll cut him dead if he doesn’t treat you properly. *To **Bobby**, with a threatening move in his direction.* I’ll educate you, you young beast. |

[004-151] | Bobby | *Furious, meeting her halfway.* Who are you calling a young beast? |

[004-152] | Margaret | You. |

[004-153] | Dora | *Peacemaking.* Now, dearies! |

[004-154] | Bobby | If you don’t take care, you’ll get your fat head jolly well clouted. |

[004-155] | Margaret | If you don’t take care, the policeman’s tooth will only be the beginning of a collection. |

[004-156] | Dora | Now, loveys, be good. |

[004-157] |  | ***Bobby**, lost to all sense of adult dignity, puts out his tongue at **Margaret**. **Margaret**, equally furious, catches his protended countenance a box on the cheek. He hurls himself her. They wrestle.* |

[004-158] | Bobby | Cat! I’ll teach you. |

[004-159] | Margaret | Pig! Beast! *She forces him backwards on the table.* Now where are you? |

[004-160] | Dora | *Calling.* Juggins, Juggins. They’ll murder one another. |

[004-161] | Juggins | *Throwing open the door, and announcing.* Monsieur Duvallet. |

[004-162] |  | ***Duvallet** enters. Sudden cessation of hostilities, and dead silence. The combatants separate by the whole width of the room. **Juggins** withdraws.* |

[004-163] | Duvallet | I fear I derange you. |

[004-164] | Margaret | Not at all. Bobby: you really are a beast: Monsieur Duvallet will think I’m always fighting. |

[004-165] | Duvallet | Practising jujitsu or the new Iceland wrestling. Admirable, Miss Knox. The athletic young Englishwoman is an example to all Europe. *Indicating **Bobby**.* Your instructor, no doubt. Monsieur﻿—*He bows.* |

[004-166] | Bobby | *Bowing awkwardly.* How d’y’ do? |

[004-167] | Margaret | *To **Bobby**.* I’m so sorry, Bobby: I asked Monsieur Duvallet to call for me here; and I forgot to tell you. *Introducing.* Monsieur Duvallet: Miss Four hundred and seven. Mr. Bobby Gilbey. ***Duvallet** bows.* I really don’t know how to explain our relationships. Bobby and I are like brother and sister. |

[004-168] | Duvallet | Perfectly. I noticed it. |

[004-169] | Margaret | Bobby and Miss﻿—Miss﻿— |

[004-170] | Dora | Delaney, dear. *To **Duvallet**, bewitchingly.* Darling Dora, to real friends. |

[004-171] | Margaret | Bobby and Dora are﻿—are﻿—well, not brother and sister. |

[004-172] | Duvallet | *With redoubled comprehension.* Perfectly. |

[004-173] | Margaret | Bobby has spent the last fortnight in prison. You don’t mind, do you? |

[004-174] | Duvallet | No, naturally. *I* have spent the last fortnight in prison. |

[004-175] |  | *The conversation drops. **Margaret** renews it with an effort.* |

[004-176] | Margaret | Dora has spent the last fortnight in prison. |

[004-177] | Duvallet | Quite so. I felicitate Mademoiselle on her enlargement. |

[004-178] | Dora | *Trop merci*, as they say in Boulogne. No call to be stiff with one another, have we? |

[004-179] |  | ***Juggins** comes in.* |

[004-180] | Juggins | Beg pardon, sir. Mr. and Mrs. Gilbey are coming up the street. |

[004-181] | Dora | Let me absquatulate. *Making for the door.* |

[004-182] | Juggins | If you wish to leave without being seen, you had better step into my pantry and leave afterwards. |

[004-183] | Dora | Right oh! *She bursts into song.* Hide me in the meat safe till the cop goes by. Hum the dear old music as his step draws nigh. *She goes out on tiptoe.* |

[004-184] | Margaret | I won’t stay here if she has to hide. I’ll keep her company in the pantry. *She follows **Dora**.* |

[004-186] | Juggins | Certainly, sir. |

[004-187] | Bobby | Right. Say nothing to my mother. You don’t mind, Mr. Doovalley, do you? |

[004-188] | Duvallet | I shall be charmed. |

[004-189] | Bobby | Right you are. Come along. *At the door.* Oh, by the way, Juggins, fetch down that concertina from my room, will you? |

[004-190] | Juggins | Yes, sir. ***Bobby** goes out. **Duvallet** follows him to the door.* You understand, sir, that Miss Knox is a lady absolutely comme il faut? |

[004-191] | Duvallet | Perfectly. But the other? |

[004-192] | Juggins | The other, sir, may be both charitably and accurately described in your native idiom as a daughter of joy. |

[004-193] | Duvallet | It is what I thought. These English domestic interiors are very interesting. *He goes out, followed by **Juggins**.* |

[004-194] |  | *Presently **Mr. and Mrs. Gilbey** come in. They take their accustomed places: he on the hearthrug, she at the colder end of the table.* |

[004-195] | Mrs. Gilbey | Did you smell scent in the hall, Rob? |

[004-196] | Gilbey | No, I didn’t. And I don’t want to smell it. Don’t you go looking for trouble, Maria. |

[004-197] | Mrs. Gilbey | *Snuffing up the perfumed atmosphere.* She’s been here. ***Gilbey** rings the bell.* What are you ringing for? Are you going to ask? |

[004-198] | Gilbey | No, I’m not going to ask. Juggins said this morning he wanted to speak to me. If he likes to tell me, let him; but I’m not going to ask; and don’t you either. ***Juggins** appears at the door.* You said you wanted to say something to me. |

[004-199] | Juggins | When it would be convenient to you, sir. |

[004-200] | Gilbey | Well, what is it? |

[004-201] | Mrs. Gilbey | Oh, Juggins, we’re expecting Mr. and Mrs. Knox to tea. |

[004-202] | Gilbey | He knows that. *He sits down. Then, to **Juggins**.* What is it? |

[004-203] | Juggins | *Advancing to the middle of the table.* Would it inconvenience you, sir, if I was to give you a month’s notice? |

[004-204] | Gilbey | *Taken aback.* What! Why? Ain’t you satisfied? |

[004-205] | Juggins | Perfectly, sir. It is not that I want to better myself, I assure you. |

[004-206] | Gilbey | Well, what do you want to leave for, then? Do you want to worse yourself? |
