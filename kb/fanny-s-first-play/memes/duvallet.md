## Role

Lieutenant Duvallet is a supporting character in Act III of the inner play. A French naval officer encountered by Margaret Knox during her night out, he serves as Shaw's vehicle for contrasting French gallantry with English middle-[[class]] propriety. His involvement in the boat-race night brawl—and his chivalrous gesture of paying Margaret's fine—provides both comic relief and social commentary.

## In the Play

Duvallet first appears at the Knox household in Act III, formally announced by [[juggins]] [003-034]. He arrives to pay his respects after the shared ordeal of the night before, demonstrating a courtesy that confounds the Knox [[family]]'s expectations. Shaw introduces him as a man of action who speaks English "better than Knox, having learnt it on both sides of the Atlantic" [003-037].

His entrance momentarily halts the domestic hostilities between Margaret and Bobby:

> [003-034] | Margaret | Oh, Monsieur Duvallet, I'm so sorry—so ashamed. Mother: this is Monsieur Duvallet, who has been extremely kind to me. Monsieur Duvallet: my mother. *Duvallet bows.*

When Mr. Knox indignantly demands an explanation, Duvallet recounts the escapade with dashing self-deprecation:

> [003-059] I came in at your invitation—at your amiable insistence, in fact, not at my own. But you need have no anxiety on my account. I was concerned in the regrettable incident which led to your daughter's incarceration. I got a fortnight without the option of a fine on the ridiculous ground that I ought to have struck the policeman with my fist. I should have done so with pleasure had I known; but, as it was, I struck him on the ear with my boot—a magnificent *moulinet*, I must say—and was informed that I had been guilty of an act of cowardice, but that for the sake of the *entente cordiale* I should be dealt with leniently.

## Character Analysis

Duvallet embodies a Continental code of honour that Shaw juxtaposes against English self-righteousness. Where Knox blusters about propriety, Duvallet simply acts—paying Margaret's fine without expecting gratitude, then departing with a graceful triple bow [003-059].

Margaret's account of their meeting reveals Duvallet as a catalyst for her liberation. At the theatre, "he seemed quite happy; and he was nice and sailorlike" [003-075]. He bought her champagne, danced with her, and when the police turned violent, "swung his leg like a windmill and knocked the policeman down" [003-077]—a *moulinet* that the English courts deem cowardly while a punch would have been acceptable.

## Key Passages

Duvallet's brief but vivid appearance is concentrated in Act III, lines [003-034] through [003-059]. His parting exchange with Bobby Gilbey—"Practising jujitsu or the new Iceland wrestling. Admirable, Miss Knox. The athletic young Englishwoman is an example to all Europe" [003-082]—captures his urbane irony and Shaw's delight in puncturing national stereotypes.
