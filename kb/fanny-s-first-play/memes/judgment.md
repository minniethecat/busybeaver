## Definition

Judgment in *Fanny's First Play* operates at multiple levels: the frame story's critics sit in judgment on the inner play; the inner play's parents sit in judgment on their children; and Shaw, through the Preface, sits in judgment on the entire social order that produces such parents and such critics. The play's argument is that most judgment is premature, prejudiced, or based on the wrong criteria — and that the only judgment worth attending to is the judgment that proceeds from genuine understanding.

The Preface delivers what it calls "the judgment of her God" through the mouth of Mrs. Knox (P01-001). This is judgment as revelation — the exposure of what is — rather than judgment as condemnation. The play's movement is from the false judgment of appearances to the true judgment of [[character]].

## In the Play

### The Critics' Judgment

The Induction and Epilogue are structured as a trial of the inner play. The critics — [[trotter|Trotter]], [[vaughan|Vaughan]], [[gunn|Gunn]], and [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]] — render their verdicts before, during, and after the performance. Their judgments are uniformly distorting: they judge the play by its author rather than its content, by its conformity to generic expectations rather than its dramatic effectiveness, by their own prejudices rather than honest engagement.

[[trotter|Trotter]]'s acknowledgment — "God forbid that I should set up to be a judge, or do more than record an impression; but my impressions can be influenced; and in this case you're influencing them shamelessly all the time" (001-105) — is a confession that judgment is always situated, always partial, always subject to the very influences it claims to transcend.

The [[count-odowda|Count]]'s quiet request — "The author desires that the play should be judged on its merits" (005-026) — is the play's simple but radical counter-proposal: judge the work, not the name. The critics cannot do this, and their failure is Shaw's indictment of a critical culture that has lost the capacity for genuine judgment.

### Parental Judgment

In the inner play, judgment takes the form of parental condemnation. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] judges [[dora-delaney|Dora]] before she speaks: "It's the like of you that's the ruin of the like of him" (002-095). [[knox|Mr. Knox]] judges his daughter without knowing her story. The parents are so habituated to judgment that they cannot hear, cannot understand, cannot see the people in front of them.

The play's reversal is that the judged become the judges. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] judges her parents' world as "pretending, pretending, pretending" (003-098). [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] judges her husband's lack of inner resources. And the play itself judges the entire system of respectability as a form of spiritual death.

### The Final Judgment

The Epilogue's close brings judgment full circle. [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s revelation that she wrote the play and that she "did a month with Lady Constance Lytton" (005-085) forces the critics to re-judge everything they have said. Her final line — "Yes: it'll teach you what it feels like to be forcibly fed" (005-086) — is a judgment on the critics themselves, who have been force-fed a play they could not properly digest.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's own practice of judgment is complex. He judges the critics (by lampooning them), judges the middle class (by exposing their hypocrisy), and judges the young (by showing the consequences of their rebellions). But his judgment is always accompanied by understanding — he knows why the critics judge as they do, why the parents cling to respectability, why the young rebel.

The difference between Shaw's judgment and his characters' is that Shaw judges from knowledge while they judge from ignorance. The play is an education in judgment — teaching the audience how to judge, and how not to.

## Key Passages

- **The reluctant judge**: "God forbid that I should set up to be a judge, or do more than record an impression; but my impressions can be influenced" — Trotter's honest confession of critical fallibility (001-105).
- **What's the good of pretending**: "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" — Margaret's judgment on her parents' world (003-098).
- **Judged on its merits**: "The author desires that the play should be judged on its merits" — The Count's challenge to the critics (005-026).
- **Judgment by authorship**: "If it's by a good author, it's a good play, naturally. That stands to reason" — Bannal's reductive critical method (005-025).
- **The judgment of her God**: "Out of the mouth of Mrs. Knox I have delivered on them the judgment of her God" — Shaw's claim for his play's moral authority (P01-001).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian theatre was dominated by a critical establishment that Shaw regarded as complacent, ignorant, and hostile to innovation. The "critic as judge" model — authoritative, definitive, beyond appeal — was challenged by Shaw's own critical practice (he was the most important drama critic of his generation) and by his plays, which demanded a more engaged and less prejudiced form of attention.

The question of who has the right to judge was also a live political issue: the suffragettes were judged as hysterics and criminals by a legal system that denied them the vote; workers were judged by employers who controlled their livelihoods; children were judged by parents who claimed absolute authority. Shaw's play contributes to this broader questioning of judgment by showing how easily judgment becomes an instrument of power rather than a search for [[truth]].
