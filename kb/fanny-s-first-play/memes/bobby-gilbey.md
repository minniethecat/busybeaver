## Role

Bobby Gilbey is the closest thing the inner play has to a male protagonist — a young man of the lower-[[class|middle class]] whose drunken assault on a policeman lands him in [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] and triggers the family crisis that drives the plot. He is neither hero nor villain but a Shawian truth-teller: his [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] strips away the [[hypocrisy|pretense]] of respectability that has governed his family's life, and his refusal to marry [[dora-delaney|Dora]] out of mere obligation marks him as more honest than his parents.

## In the Play

Bobby appears primarily in Acts II and IV. In Act II, he has just been released from [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] and returns to the [[gilbey|Gilbey]] household on the [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]], where his mother greets his [[shame|disgrace]] with moral panic. In Act IV, the full cast assembles at the Knox household for a reckoning: who will marry whom, and what does respectability demand?

Bobby's [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic arc is from passive shame to active [[truth|honesty]]. His initial response to his [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] is defensive: he insists he was "perfectly sober" and that the policeman started it. But by the final act, he has embraced his status as a social outcast. "I'm a gaol-bird: you're a respectable man," he tells his father, inverting the moral hierarchy (004-007). His refusal to marry [[dora-delaney|Dora]] — "I won't marry her: that's flat" (004-025) — is not cowardice but integrity: he will not enter a marriage he does not mean.

## Character Analysis

Bobby is Shaw's study in the moral education of the mediocre. He is not particularly intelligent, not particularly brave, not particularly anything — except honest. His [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]], which should be his shame, becomes his [[freedom|liberation]]. The experience teaches him something his respectable parents never learned: that the social performance of virtue is not the same as virtue itself.

His relationship with [[dora-delaney|Dora]] is the play's most interesting romantic subplot. [[dora-delaney|Dora]] has "educated Bobby a bit: he's not the mollycoddle he was when you had him in hand" (002-092). Their shared [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] experience has created a bond that defies the conventions of courtship. Bobby's reluctance to marry her is not a rejection but a refusal to reduce their relationship to a social formula.

## Key Passages

- **The gaol-bird's confession**: "I'm a gaol-bird: you're a respectable man" — Bobby's inversion of the moral order (004-007).
- **The refusal**: "I won't marry her: that's flat" — Bobby's assertion of autonomy (004-025).
- **[[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s education**: "I've educated Bobby a bit: he's not the mollycoddle he was when you had him in hand" — the young woman's assessment (002-092).
- **The institutional challenge**: "Where's the Government? Where's the [[religion|Church]]? Where's respectability and right reason?" — Bobby's rhetorical indictment (002-117).
- **The desperate threat**: "If I'm not let marry her, I'll do something downright [[shame|disgrace]]ful. I'll enlist as a soldier" — comic desperation revealing genuine feeling (004-415).

## Critical Reception

Bobby has received less critical attention than [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] or [[juggins|Juggins]], partly because his function is reactive rather than active. Critics have noted his kinship with other Shawian young men — the awkward suitors of *You Never Can Tell*, the reluctant heroes of *Arms and the Man* — who discover moral courage through comic humiliation.
