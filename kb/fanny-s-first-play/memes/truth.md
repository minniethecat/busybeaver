## Definition

Truth in *Fanny's First Play* is a disruptive force. Characters who tell the truth — Bobby admitting he is "a gaol-bird" (004-007), [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] refusing to be ashamed of her [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]], Knox confessing that "all our respectability is pretending" (003-098) — shatter the social performance that sustains Edwardian middle-class culture. Shaw treats truth-telling not as a virtue that can be added to respectability but as its antithesis: you cannot be both truthful and respectable.

## In the Play

The inner play is structured as a sequence of truth-tellings. Each act exposes something the characters have been pretending not to know. Bobby's [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]], [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s "fall," [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s criminal record, Duvallet's existing marriage, [[juggins|Juggins]]'s parentage — each revelation strips away a layer of the respectable facade. The play's comic energy comes from the characters' desperate attempts to maintain [[hypocrisy|pretense]] in the face of accumulating truth.

Bobby's self-description — "I'm a gaol-bird: you're a respectable man" (004-007) — is an act of truth-telling that inverts the moral hierarchy. The "gaol-bird" who admits his status is more honest than the "respectable man" who conceals his. The truth, in Shaw's [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic economy, always favors the transgressor over the conformist.

The Induction frames truth-telling as the theatre's function. Fanny's play tells truths that the critics cannot hear because they are listening for convention. The concealed authorship is itself a truth experiment: can a play be judged truly when the author's name is removed from the equation (P01-003)?

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw believed that the theatre's highest function was truth-telling — not entertainment, not beauty, not moral instruction, but the uncomfortable exposure of what society prefers not to know. His critical writings consistently attacked the "well-made play" for substituting conventional resolution for truthful observation.

*Fanny's First Play* enacts this belief through its structure. The inner play tells uncomfortable truths about middle-class family life; the Induction's critics cannot hear them; the Epilogue confirms that the critics have learned nothing. The truth has been told, and it has been rejected — which is, in Shaw's view, exactly what happens when the theatre does its job properly.

## Key Passages

- **The gaol-bird's honesty**: "I'm a gaol-bird: you're a respectable man" — Bobby's truth-telling inversion (004-007).
- **The confession of [[hypocrisy|pretense]]**: "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" — Knox's truth (003-098).
- **The authorship truth**: The concealment of authorship was "a necessary part of the play" — Shaw's truth experiment (P01-003).
- **No real religion**: Knox admits the truth about his spiritual condition (004-308).
- **Good but not free**: [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s truth about her moral education (003-088).

## Historical Context

Truth-telling in Edwardian culture was constrained by multiple forces: the libel laws, the Lord Chamberlain's theatre censorship, the social penalties for "frankness" about sex and religion. The "New Drama" of Ibsen and Shaw was controversial precisely because it told truths that the culture preferred to suppress.

The Edwardian period also saw the rise of investigative journalism (the "muckrakers"), the exposure of social conditions through the new social sciences, and the campaign against "the conspiracy of silence" around venereal disease and prostitution. Shaw's theatre was part of a broader movement to replace euphemism with honesty.
