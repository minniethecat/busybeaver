## Role

Joseph Knox is the inner play's tragicomic center — a man whose moral authority is built on the enforcement of rules he cannot himself live by. As a shopkeeper, he has policed the sexual morality of his female employees: "I've turned many a girl out of the shop for being light" (003-022). As a father, he has produced a daughter who went to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]. The gap between these two facts is the space in which Shaw's critique of respectability operates. Knox's confession — "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" (003-098) — is the play's diagnostic climax.

## In the Play

Knox dominates Act III, where he confronts his daughter [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] after her [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]]. His initial response is bluster and denial: he cannot reconcile the prize-winning schoolgirl with the "fallen woman" who stands before him. But [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s [[truth|honesty]] forces his own: by the act's end, he has confessed the emptiness of the [[morality|moral code]] he has spent his life enforcing.

In Act IV, Knox becomes a figure of comic desperation as he tries to arrange marriages that will restore social order. His prejudice against the Frenchman Duvallet — "You sit there after carrying on with my daughter, and tell me coolly you're married" (004-395) — reveals the xenophobia that often accompanies respectable morality. His recognition of [[juggins|Juggins]] as the "brother of a duke" (004-429) reveals the class deference that is respectability's secret foundation.

## Character Analysis

Knox is Shaw's study in the structural nature of hypocrisy. He is not a conscious deceiver — he genuinely believes he is a moral man. His tragedy (and comedy) is that the morality he believes in is a system of [[hypocrisy|pretense]], and he has never been forced to confront this fact until his daughter's [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] strips away the illusion.

His confession — "We find out then that with all our respectability and [[religion|piety]], we've no real religion and no way of knowing what is right" (004-308) — is the most devastating line in the play. It is not just Knox's confession but the confession of an entire social order.

## Key Passages

- **The confession of [[hypocrisy|pretense]]**: "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" (003-098).
- **The shopkeeper's hypocrisy**: "I've turned many a girl out of the shop for being light" (003-022).
- **The spiritual bankruptcy**: "We find out then that with all our respectability and [[religion|piety]], we've no real religion" (004-308).
- **The unanswered question**: "Was your mother married to your father?" — Knox asks [[juggins|Juggins]] the question that undermines his own moral framework (004-324).
- **The xenophobic outburst**: "You sit there after carrying on with my daughter, and tell me coolly you're married" (004-395).
- **The class recognition**: "Yes: the brother of a duke: that's what he is" (004-429).

## Critical Reception

Knox has been analyzed as a Shavian variant of the "guilty father" archetype — a figure who appears throughout Shaw's work (Mr. Warren in *Mrs Warren's Profession*, Andrew Undershaft in *Major Barbara*). Critics have noted that Knox's confession, unlike those of Shaw's more powerful fathers, leads not to transformation but to chaos — he can diagnose his condition but cannot cure it.
