## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, love operates on two levels: as a genuine emotional force that draws characters together, and as a social institution — marriage — that the respectable middle class treats as a transaction. Shaw satirizes the latter while affirming the former, most strikingly through the unexpected [[juggins|Juggins]]/Margaret pairing and through [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s fiercely independent version of devotion, which defies the conventions of romantic "womanliness."

The Count declares himself "a lover of the arts" rather than a professional critic, embodying love as an amateur passion — a position Shaw treats with amused respect (001-090). The inner play then tests this ideal against the gritty realities of the Gilbey/Knox world, where love is subordinate to [[respectability]].

## In the Play

### Bobby, Margaret, and the Marriage Market

[[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] and [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] are "as good as engaged" through their families' social arrangements — Tuesday dinners at the Knoxes', Friday dinners at the Gilbeys' — a world in which courtship is indistinguishable from business partnership (002-100). Their engagement is a product of [[class]] convenience rather than passion.

After both have been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], the engagement becomes both impossible (by respectable standards) and newly possible (by the standards of honesty). Bobby's instinct is to escape — he asks [[juggins|Juggins]] how to get out of an engagement "without behaving like a regular cad" — while Margaret's is to face the truth (004-025).

### Dora's Unconventional Love

[[dora-delaney|Dora]] represents love without institutional sanction. She calls Bobby "Darling" openly, visits his parents to secure his release from prison, and refuses to abandon him despite [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]]'s demand that she do so. Her love is practical — she brings money for his bail — and fiercely loyal, yet she acknowledges that Bobby "cut me dead" when walking with his mother, accepting this with a realism that the respectable characters lack (004-150).

Dora's independence from romantic convention is what makes her "unwomanly" by Edwardian standards: she neither demands marriage nor performs the helplessness expected of women in love.

### Juggins and Margaret: Love Across Class

The play's most surprising romantic resolution is [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s declaration: "I have loved Juggins since the first day I beheld him. I felt instinctively he had been in the Guards" (004-448). This pairing crosses [[class]] boundaries — a shopkeeper's daughter and a duke's brother — and resolves the play's romantic tensions not through convention but through genuine attraction, validated by Juggins's revealed [[character]].

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw treats romantic love less as a theme in itself than as a lens through which to examine social hypocrisy. The Preface does not mention love directly, but its critique of "mere morality" applies to the marriage market: the young are pressured into loveless partnerships by the same "custom" that substitutes for [[morality|conscience]] (P01-001).

The Induction's critics debate whether the play contains "the note of passion," with [[vaughan|Vaughan]] insisting it does: "beneath all the assumed levity of that poor waif and stray, she really loves Bobby and will be a good wife to him" (005-040). This critical debate mirrors the play's own ambivalence about whether love can survive the institutions that claim to protect it.

## Key Passages

- **The Count as lover**: "I have no right to my costume except the right of a lover of the arts to dress myself handsomely" — [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] distinguishing amateur love from professional criticism (001-090).
- **Dora's open affection**: "Bobby says to me, 'Darling—'" — [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s unembarrassed use of the language of love in the Gilbeys' respectable parlor (002-070).
- **Marriage as transaction**: "Women don't always marry for happiness, sir. They often marry because they wish to be married women and not old maids" — [[juggins|Juggins]]'s clinical analysis (004-026).
- **Love across class**: "I have loved Juggins since the first day I beheld him" — [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s declaration (004-448).
- **The note of passion**: "beneath all the assumed levity of that poor waif and stray, she really loves Bobby and will be a good wife to him" — [[vaughan|Vaughan]] detecting genuine emotion in the inner play (005-040).

## Historical Context

Edwardian courtship was governed by strict protocols: chaperonage, formal introductions, and the requirement that a proposal be preceded by the man's demonstration of financial capacity. The Gilbeys' dinner-circuit courtship — "We dine with them on Tuesdays. So that's two evenings pleasure every week" — is a comic but accurate representation of middle-class matchmaking (002-100).

Shaw's presentation of Dora — a working-class woman who loves without demanding marriage, who has been to prison, who speaks of her "Darling" openly — was provocative in 1911. The "New Woman" movement was challenging the idea that women's lives must be defined by marriage, and Dora represents a working-class version of that independence.
