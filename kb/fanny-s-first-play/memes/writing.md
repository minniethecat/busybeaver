## Definition

Writing in *Fanny's First Play* is an act of self-assertion — particularly for [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]], whose play within the play is her bid for adult authority. The play's layers of authorship — Fanny's play, Shaw's play, the critics' reviews — create a hall of mirrors in which writing is simultaneously creative expression, social performance, and philosophical argument.

## In the Play

Fanny's writing is the play's generative act. A young woman just returned from Cambridge, she has written a play that her father has commissioned a private performance of. The writing is her own — not her father's aesthetic project, not the critics' expectations, but something genuinely hers. And it shocks everyone.

The critics are writers too, but of a different kind. Their writing is parasitic on others' creation — they judge, they classify, they dismiss. Shaw's satire of criticism is, among other things, a defense of creative writing against critical writing.

Shaw's own presence as writer is complicated by the play's anonymous premiere. By concealing his authorship, Shaw separated the writing from the writer — forcing critics to judge the work rather than the name. The experiment demonstrated, as Shaw intended, that they could not.
