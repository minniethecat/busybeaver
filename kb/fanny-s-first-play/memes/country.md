## Definition

"Country" in *Fanny's First Play* is an unstable category. The Count declares he is "not really an Englishman: my family is Irish: I've lived all my life in Italy" (001-058). National identity is presented not as a fixed essence but as a series of choices and performances. Duvallet's extravagant praise of English freedom — and equally extravagant critique of French tyranny — exposes patriotic sentiment as a form of theatre, as staged as the play within the play.

## In the Play

The Count's relationship to England is one of exile and aesthetic rejection. "I find England ugly and Philistine. Well, I don't live in it" (001-072). His solution is withdrawal — to Venice, to Italy, to the eighteenth century. National belonging is, for him, a matter of aesthetic choice rather than birth.

Duvallet's long speech in Act IV is the play's most sustained meditation on national character. He praises England for its freedom — "everywhere in these islands one can enjoy the exhilarating, the soul-liberating spectacle of men quarrelling with their brothers, defying their fathers, refusing to speak to their mothers" — while condemning France for its militarism and family tyranny (004-919). The irony is that Duvallet's "England" is as much a fantasy as the Count's "Venice."

The play's English characters are notably unpatriotic. No one delivers a speech about English greatness; the word "England" appears mostly in the mouths of foreigners — the Count and Duvallet — who use it as a screen for their own projections.

## Key Passages

- **The Count's displaced identity**: "I'm not really an Englishman: my family is Irish: I've lived all my life in Italy" (001-058).
- **The Count's rejection of England**: "I find England ugly and Philistine. Well, I don't live in it" (001-072).
- **Duvallet on English freedom**: "Everywhere in these islands one can enjoy the... spectacle of men quarrelling with their brothers" (004-919).
- **The Count's final departure**: "Tomorrow I start for Venice, never to return" (005-988).
