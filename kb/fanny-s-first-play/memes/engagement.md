## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, "engagement" is more than a promise to marry. It is a social institution — a public declaration that binds two families, regulates sexual behavior, and signals respectability. To be "as good as engaged" is to be placed in a social category from which there is, as [[juggins|Juggins]] insists, no honorable escape. The engagement is the play's emblem of how social convention entraps individuals: once entered, it can only be broken by behaving "like a cad" [003-030].

## In the Play

The engagement between [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby Gilbey]] and [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]] is established in Act I as a family arrangement embedded in dinner-party routine. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] explains: "Margaret Knox and Bobby are as good as engaged. Mr. Knox is my husband's partner. Mrs. Knox is very religious; but she's quite cheerful. We dine with them on Tuesdays. So that's two evenings pleasure every week" [002-011]. The engagement is presented not as a romantic commitment but as a social convenience — part of the rhythm of respectable suburban life.

The engagement plot reaches its crisis in Act III, where Bobby asks Juggins "the proper way to get out of an engagement to a girl without getting into a row for breach of promise or behaving like a regular cad" [003-029]. Juggins's reply is devastating: "You can't get out of an engagement without behaving like a cad if the lady wishes to hold you to it" [003-030]. When Bobby protests that "it wouldn't be for her happiness to marry me when I don't really care for her," Juggins responds with sociological precision: "Women don't always marry for happiness, sir. They often marry because they wish to be married women and not old maids" [003-031].

The engagement is finally dissolved not by Bobby's stratagems but by the revelation that both he and Margaret have been in prison. Bobby's attempt to use his imprisonment as grounds for breaking the engagement — "I can't hold her to an engagement with a man who's been in quod" [003-040] — fails when Margaret reveals she too was imprisoned. The engagement is broken not by masculine honor but by the deeper truth that neither partner is the person their parents thought they were.

The resolution replaces the hollow engagement with authentic pairings. [[juggins|Juggins]] proposes to Margaret; [[dora-delaney|Dora]] is "ready to marry Bobby, if that will be any satisfaction" [004-088]. These new arrangements bypass the formal engagement altogether — they are the products of experience, not social convention.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of engagement is characteristically unsentimental. The play exposes engagement as a social performance rather than a personal commitment. Bobby's attempts to extricate himself — the "gentlemanly turn," the "act of self-sacrifice" — are all performances of honor that conceal dishonorable intentions. Juggins, the clear-eyed footman, sees through every one: "If you wish to spare her feelings, sir, you can marry her. If you hurt her feelings by refusing, you had better not try to get credit for considerateness at the same time by pretending to spare them" [003-036].

The play's deeper argument is that engagement — like respectability itself — is a trap that can only be escaped by transgressing its rules. Prison, ironically, provides the liberation that engagement cannot.

## Historical Context

In Edwardian England, a broken engagement could be grounds for a lawsuit. "Breach of promise" actions — in which a jilted woman could sue for damages — were a real legal threat, particularly for middle-class men. Bobby's anxiety about "getting into a row for breach of promise" reflects a genuine social reality. Shaw's treatment of the engagement plot derives much of its comic tension from this legal backdrop: the engagement is not merely a personal promise but a contract enforceable at law.
