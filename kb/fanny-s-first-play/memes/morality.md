## Definition

Shaw opens *Fanny's First Play* with a declaration of intent: the play's "lesson" concerns "mere morality, or the substitution of custom for conscience" (P01-001). This distinction — between morality-as-convention and morality-as-conscience — is the philosophical spine of the entire work. "Mere morality" is the code of respectability that the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s and Knoxes live by: church attendance, sexual propriety, the right address. Genuine morality, Shaw suggests, may require the transgression of that code — going to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], losing one's reputation, telling the truth.

## In the Play

The inner play is structured as a moral education. The young characters — Bobby, [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], [[dora-delaney|Dora]] — have all transgressed the moral code of their parents. Each has been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]. And each has emerged not corrupted but clarified: [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] has been a more effective moral teacher than church, school, or family.

The older generation's morality is revealed as self-serving. Knox enforces sexual morality on his shop assistants ("I've turned many a girl out of the shop for being light") while failing to provide his own daughter with any moral formation that survives contact with freedom (003-022). [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s moral vigilance produces a son who gets drunk and assaults a policeman.

The play's moral climax is [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s realization that "when I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free. And when I got free I was what most people call bad" (003-088). The categories "good" and "bad" have been inverted: what society calls goodness is servitude; what it calls badness is freedom.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's moral philosophy — developed across his plays, prefaces, and essays — is a form of creative evolution in which ethical progress requires the rejection of conventional morality. The "lesson" of *Fanny's First Play* is that respectability is a moral dead end: it produces conformity without conscience, propriety without principle.

The Preface frames this explicitly. The play is "a potboiler" that "needs no preface" — and then proceeds to deliver a preface that states the moral thesis with Shaw's characteristic directness. The concealment of authorship was itself a moral experiment: can a play be judged on its merits, or only by the reputation of its author (P01-003)?

## Key Passages

- **The thesis statement**: "Mere morality, or the substitution of custom for conscience was once accounted a sufficient substitute" — Shaw's Preface (P01-001).
- **Good but not free**: "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free" — [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s moral awakening (003-088).
- **No real religion**: "We find out then that with all our respectability and [[religion|piety]], we've no real religion" — Knox's confession (004-308).
- **The cost of growth**: The "getting into trouble that will combine loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable freedom" — Shaw's formula for moral development (P01-001).

## Historical Context

Edwardian moral discourse was dominated by what contemporaries called the "Nonconformist conscience" — the evangelical Protestant ethos that equated morality with sexual purity, temperance, and Sabbath observance. This was the morality of the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s and Knoxes, and it was already under siege from multiple directions: the "[[gender-roles|New Woman]]" movement, secular rationalism, the social sciences, and the theatre of Ibsen and Shaw.

Shaw's contribution was to argue that conventional morality was not merely outdated but actively harmful — it prevented people from developing genuine ethical judgment. This placed him in the tradition of Nietzsche (whom he admired) and anticipated the existentialist ethics of the mid-twentieth century.
