## Setting

Paris does not appear as a physical setting in *Fanny's First Play*, but it functions as the French cultural pole against which English provincialism is measured. Paris represents sophistication, freedom from English moral constraints, and a certain Continental worldliness that the play's English characters both admire and resist.

## In the Play

### Induction: Trotter as Parisian

Savoyard's character sketch of [[trotter|Trotter]] includes the telling detail that Trotter is "thoroughly English: never happy except when he's in Paris, and speaks French so unnecessarily well that everybody there spots him as an Englishman the moment he opens his mouth" [001-026]. Trotter in London is a fish out of water; Trotter in Paris is an Englishman playing at being French.

The Parisian connection is deepened when [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] teases Trotter about his Cambridge reputation: "In Cambridge we regard you as a bel esprit, a wit, an Irresponsible, a Parisian Immoralist, tres chic" [001-110]. Trotter is mortified by this characterization — "Well, upon my word!" — but Fanny's mockery has a serious edge. The "Parisian Immoralist" is the sophisticated, amoral figure who stands outside English pieties, and Trotter's horror at the label reveals the gap between his self-image and how the young generation sees him.

### Act III: Duvallet's France

The character of [[duvallet|Lieutenant Duvallet]] embodies the French contrast without ever naming Paris directly. He is a French naval officer who speaks English "better than Knox, having learnt it on both sides of the Atlantic" [002-088]. His presence in the play — and in Margaret's story — represents an alternative set of values: French gallantry, frank enjoyment, and a refreshing lack of English moral panic.

His climactic speech contrasts French social repression with English freedom: "If you were a Frenchman, stifled with prudery, hypocrisy and the tyranny of the family and the home, you would understand how an enlightened Frenchman admires and envies your freedom" [004-090]. The irony is that the English characters have just spent the entire play demonstrating that they are anything but free.

## Dramatic Function

Paris functions as a mirror for English self-examination. Trotter, who is "never happy except when he's in Paris," is also the critic most trapped in English intellectual conventions. Duvallet, the Frenchman who admires English freedom, exposes by his admiration just how unfree the English characters actually are. The play's triangulation of English, French, and Italian (Venice/Florence) cultures creates a geography of values: Italy is aesthetic retreat, France is sophisticated freedom, and England is — what? Respectability, hypocrisy, and the desperate advice to "do something that will get you into trouble" [P01-001].

## Key Passages

- **The Parisian Immoralist**: "In Cambridge we regard you as a bel esprit, a wit, an Irresponsible, a Parisian Immoralist, tres chic" [001-110].
- **Duvallet on English freedom**: "so natural to you as an Englishman that you are hardly conscious of possessing them" [004-092].
- **La belle Angleterre**: "In France it would be impossible. But here — ah! *la belle Angleterre*!" [004-095].

## Real-World Context

In the Edwardian imagination, Paris occupied a specific cultural position: the capital of art, sophistication, and (to English sensibilities) a certain moral laxity. The "Parisian Immoralist" was a recognizable stereotype — the figure who had escaped English provincialism into Continental sophistication. Shaw, who had lived in both cultures, uses the English-French contrast throughout his work to defamiliarize English pieties.
