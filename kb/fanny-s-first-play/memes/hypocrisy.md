## Definition

Hypocrisy in *Fanny's First Play* is not a minor character flaw but the organizing principle of respectable society. Characters do not merely pretend to be better than they are; the entire social system is built on the pretense that respectable appearances correspond to moral realities. Shaw's play is a machine for exposing this pretense — the Induction's framing, the inner play's revelations, and the Epilogue's critical confusion all work to dismantle the hypocrisy that sustains Edwardian middle-class culture.

## In the Play

Knox is the play's central hypocrite. He runs a respectable shop, serves on the local board, and enforces sexual morality on his female employees — turning girls "out of the shop for being light" (003-022). Yet his own daughter has been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], and when the family's reputation is threatened, his moral principles collapse into panic. His confession — "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" (003-098) — is the play's diagnosis of hypocrisy as a social condition, not an individual failing.

[[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]], who insists "This is a respectable household" (002-096), discovers that her respectability has been sustained by ignorance of what her son actually does. The critics in the Induction, who pronounce on the inner play's morality, cannot recognize themselves in its satire. Even [[juggins|Juggins]], the play's moral authority, maintains a strategic silence about his origins that makes him complicit in the system he helps dismantle.

The play's resolution is not the abolition of pretense but its exposure. When Knox admits "we've no real religion," when [[gilbey|Gilbey]] discovers he "never knew we could live without being respectable," hypocrisy loses its power — not because characters stop pretending, but because the pretense has been named and acknowledged (004-308)(004-253).

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's critique of hypocrisy draws on a tradition of moral satire running from Molière through Ibsen. But where Molière's Tartuffe is an individual hypocrite — a conscious deceiver — Shaw's hypocrites are sincere in their self-deception. Knox genuinely believes he is a moral man; his hypocrisy is structural, not personal.

The Preface sets up this structural analysis. The play's "lesson" is that society has substituted "custom for [[morality|conscience]]" — and custom is, by definition, a collective performance. Hypocrisy, in Shaw's treatment, is not the gap between what we say and what we do; it is the gap between what we pretend to believe and what we actually know.

## Key Passages

- **The confession of pretense**: "That's all our respectability is, pretending, pretending, pretending" — Knox (003-098).
- **The shopkeeper's enforcement**: Knox admits turning girls "out of the shop for being light" while his own daughter is im[[crime-and-punishment|prison]]ed (003-022).
- **No real religion**: "We find out then that with all our respectability and [[religion|piety]], we've no real religion and no way of knowing what is right" (004-308).
- **The gaol-bird's [[truth|honesty]]**: "I'm a gaol-bird: you're a respectable man" — Bobby's inversion of the moral hierarchy (004-007).

## Historical Context

The exposure of hypocrisy was a central project of Edwardian progressive culture. The social purity movement, the campaign against the white slave trade, the agitation for divorce law reform — all sought to close the gap between public morality and private conduct. The theatre was a key site of this exposure: Ibsen's *A Doll's House* and *Ghosts* had made hypocrisy a [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic subject, and Shaw extended the project with specifically English materials.

The Edwardian [[class|middle class]] was particularly vulnerable to charges of hypocrisy because its social position depended so heavily on moral credentials. Unlike the aristocracy (which could claim birth) or the working class (which could claim labor), the [[class|middle class]] claimed virtue — and was therefore exposed when its virtue proved performative.
