## Definition

Duty in *Fanny's First Play* is a concept pulled in two directions. There is duty to convention — the obligations imposed by family, class, and [[respectability]] — and there is duty to oneself, to one's own ethical judgment. The play's young characters discover that these two forms of duty are often incompatible, and that choosing conscience over convention is both the harder and the more honourable path.

## In the Play

The characters invoke duty most frequently when they are trying to avoid honest confrontation. [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s breezy narrative of the police court includes a telling phrase: "Bobby behaved like a perfect gentleman. Besides, it was all my fault. I'll own it" (002-081). Duty here is the duty to take blame — but Dora takes it so lightly, so cheerfully, that the concept is robbed of its moral weight.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]] and [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] negotiate duty more seriously. Bobby suggests they "get off duty" — escape their obligations to their families and society (004-062). Margaret, at first puzzled by the phrase ("Off duty? What do you mean?"), comes to understand that duty-to-family can be a form of evasion — a way of avoiding the harder duty to truth.

The older generation's conception of duty is narrower. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] feels the duty to maintain appearances, to keep the shop running, to discipline his son. [[knox|Joseph Knox]] feels the duty to enforce morality — in his shop, if not in his home. Both fail to recognize that duty can be corrupted into a tool of oppression.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface offers an alternative vision of duty: the duty to "get into trouble" — that is, to risk respectability in the service of integrity (P01-001). This is duty redefined as moral courage rather than social conformity. The "desperate advice" to the young is not to abandon duty but to discover what their true duties are — and to recognize that many of their supposed obligations are simply the demands of a morally bankrupt social order.

## Key Passages

- **Dora's confession**: "I'll own it: I took too much champagne" — duty as cheerful blame-taking (002-081).
- **Bobby's proposal**: "Suppose we get off duty" — the desire to escape social obligation (004-062).
- **Margaret's question**: "Off duty? What do you mean?" — the concept of duty interrogated (004-063).
