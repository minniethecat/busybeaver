## Role

Harley Granville Barker (1877–1946) is a historically real figure referenced in the Epilogue of *Fanny's First Play*. He never appears on stage, but his name functions as a critical touchstone: his style is recognized (or misrecognized) by the critics, and his reputation as a serious dramatist is used to measure the quality of Fanny's anonymous play.

## In the Play

Granville Barker is mentioned four times in the Epilogue as the critics debate the authorship of the inner play. Gunn attributes it to him: "Granville Barker, of course. Why, old Gilbey is straight out of *The Madras House*" [005-028]. Bannal responds with a dismissive "Poor old Barker!" [005-029]. Later, when Fanny reveals herself as the author and Gunn apologizes, she is "Radiant. Granville Barker! Oh, you couldn't really have thought it so fine as that" [005-032].

The invocation of Granville Barker serves several dramatic purposes. First, it places Fanny's play in the context of the "new drama" — the realist, socially conscious theatre that Barker championed at the Court Theatre. Second, it exposes the critics' reliance on authorial identity rather than textual evidence: they recognize stylistic fingerprints but cannot agree on whose they are. Third, Fanny's delighted response to the Barker attribution reveals her own theatrical ambitions and allegiances.

## Character Analysis

Shaw's inclusion of Granville Barker is both a professional courtesy and a critical argument. Barker was Shaw's collaborator at the Court Theatre (1904–1907), where together with J. E. Vedrenne they revolutionized English theatre by staging new plays of ideas — including Shaw's own *John Bull's Other Island*, *Man and Superman*, and *Major Barbara*. Barker directed the premieres of several Shaw plays and was instrumental in establishing Shaw's reputation as a major dramatist.

The reference to *The Madras House* (1910) — Barker's own play about the sexual and commercial exploitation of women in Edwardian society — is pointed. It suggests thematic kinship between Barker's social drama and Fanny's play-within-the-play, both of which examine the gap between respectability and reality.

## Historical Context

At the time of *Fanny's First Play* (1911), Granville Barker was at the height of his reputation as a director and theatre reformer. His productions at the Court Theatre had established new standards of ensemble acting and directorial vision. His own plays — *The Voysey Inheritance* (1905), *Waste* (1907), *The Madras House* (1910) — were admired for their intellectual seriousness and technical innovation. His advocacy of a national theatre and repertory system aligned him with Shaw's own reformist impulses. The reference places *Fanny's First Play* squarely within the "new drama" movement that Barker and Shaw had pioneered.
