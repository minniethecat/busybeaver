## Definition

Knowledge in *Fanny's First Play* is not book-learning but experiential understanding — the kind of knowledge that comes from [[prison]], from disgrace, from honest confrontation with the self. The play distinguishes between the knowledge of the schoolroom and the knowledge of the police cell, and it argues that the latter is more valuable. [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s Cambridge education has given her intellectual tools; [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s night in prison has given her moral knowledge. The play does not reject education, but it insists that knowledge without experience is incomplete.

## In the Induction

Fanny's conversation with [[trotter|Trotter]] is a contest between two kinds of knowledge. Fanny has the knowledge of the [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] — sociological categories, analytical frameworks, the vocabulary of progressive thought. Trotter has the knowledge of experience — years of watching plays, judging performances, navigating the social world of the theatre. His condescension toward her — "This is Cambridge education!" (001-147) — is partly a defence of experiential knowledge against academic theory.

Yet the play does not simply side with Trotter. Fanny's categories may be reductive, but they give her a power that Trotter lacks: the ability to see through convention, to analyze what others take for granted. The Induction is a dramatization of the competition between academic and experiential knowledge — and it ends in a draw.

## In the Inner Play

The inner play's young characters acquire knowledge through transgression. [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] knows more about himself after a night in a police cell than he learned in eighteen years of respectable upbringing. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s moral awakening comes not from her mother's sermons but from the experience of freedom and its consequences. [[dora-delaney|Dora]] has the knowledge of the streets — practical, unsentimental, and more reliable than the conventional wisdom of the Gilbeys and Knoxes.

The older generation, by contrast, is defined by its refusal of knowledge. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] does not want to know what his son has done. [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] does not want to know what her daughter has become. Their ignorance is willed — a strategy for preserving their [[respectability]] at the cost of their understanding.
