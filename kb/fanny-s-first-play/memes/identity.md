## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, identity is the central epistemological question: who are people really, and how can they — or we — know? The play explores identity across three registers: the concealed authorship of the play itself (who wrote it?), the disguised identities of characters within the inner play (who is Juggins really?), and the self-discovery of the young characters (who am I really?). Shaw argues that identity is not a fixed property but something discovered through experience, tested through crisis, and revealed through the stripping away of social convention.

## In the Play

### Concealed Authorship: The Identity of the Artist

The play's framing device is a deliberate experiment in concealed identity. Shaw writes in the Preface that "the concealment of the authorship ... was a necessary part of the play" [P01-003]. The critics spend the Epilogue trying to identify the author: Gunn attributes the play to Granville Barker [005-029], Vaughan to Pinero [005-036], Bannal to Shaw [005-037]. Each attribution reveals more about the critic than the author — Gunn sees "old-fashioned Ibsenite drivel" [005-008], Vaughan detects "the note of passion" that he believes Shaw incapable of [005-039], Bannal can only judge a play if he knows "who the author is" [005-014].

When Trotter reveals that Fanny is the author, the critics instantly reverse their judgments [005-061]. This reversal is the play's most damning comment on identity: the same words, spoken by the same actors, judged by the same critics, become a different play once the author's identity is known. Identity, Shaw suggests, is not merely a fact about a person but a lens that transforms how everything they do is perceived.

Shaw extends this argument to his own career: "If it were possible, I should put forward all my plays anonymously, or hire some less disturbing person, as Bacon is said to have hired Shakespeare, to father my plays for me" [P01-003]. His "strained legendary reputation" has made it impossible for critics to see his plays clearly; his identity has become an obstacle to his art.

### Juggins: The Duke in Disguise

The inner play's most theatrical exploration of identity is the revelation that Juggins, the Gilbeys' footman, is the brother of a duke [005-064]. This is a conventional comic device — the servant who turns out to be noble — but Shaw uses it to make a serious point about identity and worth. Juggins explains that he became a footman as a moral discipline after insulting a servant: "I once insulted a servant — rashly; for he was a sincere Christian. He rebuked me for trifling with a girl of his own class. I told him to remember what he was, and to whom he was speaking. He said God would remember. I discharged him on the spot" [005-080].

The servant's rebuke forced Juggins to confront the gap between his social identity (aristocrat) and his moral identity (someone who had acted cruelly). His response — apprenticing himself as a footman — is an attempt to close that gap, to make his social identity match his moral self-assessment. "If ever I meet that man again I shall be able to look him in the face" [005-084]. Juggins has discovered that identity is not inherited but earned.

Juggins also offers a sharp observation about the relationship between identity and occupation: "As an idle younger son, unable to support myself ... I could not have aspired to Miss Knox's hand. But as a sober, honest, and industrious domestic servant, who has, I trust, given satisfaction to his employer ... I feel I am a man with a character" [005-074]. Social status, in this reversal, is an obstacle to authentic identity; menial work is a path to self-respect.

### Margaret: The Discovery of Self

Margaret Knox's journey is a narrative of self-discovery. Before her night of transgression, she was a clergyman's daughter who took "the good conduct prize for three years running at school" [003-090]. After her descent into experience, she discovers a self she did not know existed: "I'm not a bit the sort of girl you thought me. I'm not a bit the sort of girl I thought myself" [003-090].

This discovery is both terrifying and liberating. Margaret has learned that her respectable identity was a construction — a set of behaviours prescribed by her social position — and that beneath it lies a self of unexpected strength and passion. "I know now that I am stronger than you and Papa" [003-116]. She has also learned that identity is expressed through language: "I heard words that I didn't even know that I knew, coming out of my mouth just as if somebody else had spoken them" [003-090]. The discovery of self is simultaneously the discovery of a voice.

Margaret's self-discovery challenges her parents' understanding of identity as inheritance. "I don't know what sort of person you really are, or what sort of person father really is" [003-090]. If identity must be discovered through experience, then those who have avoided experience — the respectable, the sheltered, the conventional — cannot know who they are.

### Savoyard's Name

Even minor characters participate in the play's identity games. Cecil Savoyard's wife is known as "Mrs. William Tinkler" rather than "Mrs. Cecil Savoyard" [001-003] — a detail that hints at the theatrical world's casual relationship with names and the identities they signify. The Count's title — "a Count of the Holy Roman Empire" — is described as "at present, nowhere, except as a memory and an ideal" [001-022], an identity that exists only as an assertion.

### Fanny's Dual Identity

Fanny O'Dowda herself embodies the play's identity puzzle. To her father, she is the innocent daughter raised in a Venetian palace; to Cambridge, she is a Fabian intellectual; to the critics, she is an unknown playwright; and to herself, she is a suffragette who "did a month with Lady Constance Lytton" [005-081]. Trotter is the first to perceive the connection between her life and her art: "That bit about the police was real. You're a Suffragette, Miss O'Dowda" [005-079]. Fanny's identity as a political actor informs her identity as a playwright — but her father knew neither.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of identity draws on his own complicated relationship with his public persona. The "strained legendary reputation" he laments in the Preface had, by 1911, made "Shaw" into a brand — the paradoxical wit, the Fabian provocateur, the author of "unpleasant plays." By concealing his authorship, Shaw attempted to escape this identity and allow the play to be encountered on its own terms.

The experiment was only partly successful. The critics guessed Shaw anyway, or attributed the play to his imitators. But the gesture — the attempt to separate the work from the reputation — reveals Shaw's understanding of identity as something constructed, contingent, and potentially falsifying. If "Shaw" means something predetermined to an audience, then the playwright who wants to say something new must become someone else.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period was intensely preoccupied with questions of identity. The suffragette movement was redefining female identity; the rise of the Labour Party was challenging class identity; the work of Freud (whose theories were beginning to circulate in England) was unsettling the very concept of a unified self. Shaw's play participates in this broader cultural interrogation.

The specific device of the disguised nobleman — Juggins as footman — draws on a long theatrical tradition (from Plautus through Shakespeare to Wilde) but Shaw gives it a distinctive Shavian twist. Juggins's disguise is not a plot device but a moral experiment: he has changed his social identity in order to change his moral character. This is identity as project, not fate — a thoroughly modern conception.

The play's meta-theatrical dimension — the concealed authorship, the critics' guessing game — also reflects the Edwardian theatre's growing self-consciousness about authorship and reputation. The rise of the "problem play" and the "New Drama" had made the identity of the playwright a matter of public interest and critical debate. Shaw's experiment was, in part, a satire of this culture of authorial celebrity.
