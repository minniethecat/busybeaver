## Definition

Prison occupies an extraordinary symbolic position in *Fanny's First Play*. It is simultaneously the ultimate mark of disgrace in respectable society and — in Shaw's inversion — the most effective form of moral education available to the young. The play's three young protagonists — [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]], [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], and [[dora-delaney|Dora]] — have each spent time in [[prison-setting|prison]], and each has emerged not degraded but clarified. Prison, Shaw suggests, teaches what churches and schools cannot.

## In the Play

The word "prison" haunts the Gilbey household from the first act. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] cannot bear to hear Bobby's absence called "fourteen days" — the standard sentence for drunk and disorderly conduct (002-021). [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] is in a state of nervous collapse: "It's got on my nerves so, that I'm hardly responsible for what I say" (002-022). The comedy of their anxiety underscores the genuine terror that prison represents for the respectable classes.

Yet when Bobby finally returns, he is not the broken prodigal his parents expect. He has met [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] in prison, and their shared experience has given him a perspective his parents' world cannot offer. Dora explains the paradox: she took too much champagne, she wanted to show off — and yet the result was not disgrace but liberation.

For Margaret, prison is a rite of passage. The experience strips away the false [[morality]] of her upbringing and leaves her with something more real. "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free," she says. "And when I got free I was what most people call bad" (003-088).

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface frames prison as an explicit alternative to respectable drift: "the young had better have their souls awakened by disgrace, capture by the police, and a month's hard labor, than drift along from their cradles to their graves doing what other people do" (P01-001). The police court becomes a site of moral awakening — a harder school than any [[cambridge-university|Cambridge]] college.

This is not to say Shaw endorses crime. His point is that the legal consequences of transgression — arrest, trial, imprisonment — are more honest and ultimately more educational than the social consequences of respectability, which produce conformity without conscience.

## Key Passages

- **Shaw's thesis**: "The young had better have their souls awakened by disgrace, capture by the police, and a month's hard labor" (P01-001).
- **Police court**: Dora plunges "into the middle of the room" and announces the police court (002-044).
- **Margaret's transformation**: The prison experience that made her "free" (003-088).
