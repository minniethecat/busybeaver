## Definition

Success in *Fanny's First Play* is a question rather than an answer. The frame story presents a play that is a "success" when read to the [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] (005-065) but is received with contempt by London critics. The inner play presents characters who have succeeded by conventional standards — the Gilbeys and Knoxes are prosperous, respectable, settled — yet are revealed as spiritual failures. And [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s supreme achievement — writing a play so good that the critics cannot identify its author — is, in worldly terms, a failure of recognition.

Shaw's treatment of success follows from his broader critique of [[respectability]]: worldly success is measured by the wrong criteria, and genuine success — the achievement of self-knowledge, [[freedom]], and [[character]] — often looks like failure from the outside.

## In the Play

### Critical and Commercial Success

The Epilogue's critics cannot agree on whether the play they have just seen is successful. [[trotter|Trotter]] dismisses it as impertinence, [[vaughan|Vaughan]] detects "the note of passion" but cannot endorse it, [[gunn|Gunn]] finds it derivative, and [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]] refuses to judge it without knowing the author's name. The one agreement — that the play is not by Shaw, because Shaw is incapable of writing successful drama — is undercut by the audience's knowledge that the play *is* by Shaw.

[[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s tearful entrance — "I'm so sorry, gentlemen. And it was such a success when I read it to the Cambridge Fabian Society!" (005-065) — juxtaposes two audiences, two measures of success. The Fabians (Shaw's own people, socialist intellectuals) recognized the play's quality; the professional critics did not. The implication is that success is audience-dependent — a play succeeds or fails relative to the capacities of those who judge it.

### The Count's Failed Project

[[count-odowda|The Count]]'s entire life is a project of success — the successful creation of a beautiful environment, a beautiful daughter, a beautiful existence insulated from vulgarity. The play he commissions is intended as the crowning achievement of this project. Instead, it reveals that his daughter has been shaped not by his aesthetic ideals but by experiences he knows nothing about — [[prison|prison]] visits with Lady Constance Lytton, engagement with the very world he fled. The Count's success as a father is revealed as failure: he has raised a daughter he does not know.

### The Inner Play's Definition of Success

The inner play's characters achieve success not through the metrics of their world but in spite of them. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s successful self-discovery comes through [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]. [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]]'s successful assertion of independence comes through disgrace. [[juggins|Juggins]]'s successful claim to [[character]] comes through service. In each case, success is redefined: not the achievement of social approval but the achievement of authentic selfhood.

[[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s closing verdict — "I call it quite a happy ending: don't you, lieutenant?" (004-452) — is a judgment of success. The play has produced marriages, reconciliations, revelations. By the standards of comedy, this is success. By the standards of respectable society, it is chaos. Shaw leaves the audience to decide which standards matter.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's own career was a study in the ambiguity of success. By 1911, he was famous as a critic and a controversialist, but his plays were more respected than performed, more debated than loved. *Fanny's First Play* was, in fact, one of his greatest commercial successes — it ran for 622 performances — but the Epilogue's satire of critics who cannot recognize quality is also a preemptive defense against the critics who would not recognize his.

The Preface's concealment of authorship — "If it were possible, I should put forward all my plays anonymously" (P01-003) — is a rejection of success-by-reputation. Shaw wants his plays to succeed on their merits, not on his name. This is the only success worth having, and the play's satire of critics who cannot judge without knowing the author is Shaw's demonstration that such success is nearly impossible to achieve.

## Key Passages

- **Success with the Fabians**: "I'm so sorry, gentlemen. And it was such a success when I read it to the Cambridge Fabian Society!" — Fanny's tearful contrast of audiences (005-065).
- **The Count's model of success**: "I am not content with beautiful dreams: I want beautiful realities" — the Count's criteria for a successful life (001-020).
- **A happy ending**: "I call it quite a happy ending: don't you, lieutenant?" — Dora's judgment of the play's success (004-452).
- **The mechanism of success**: "the makeup by which Mr. Claude King so successfully simulated his personal appearance" — Shaw acknowledging Trotter's successful impersonation (P01-002).
- **Anonymous success**: "If it were possible, I should put forward all my plays anonymously" — Shaw rejecting success-by-reputation (P01-003).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period was obsessed with success. The cult of the "self-made man," the proliferation of success manuals (Samuel Smiles's works continued to sell), and the visibility of new wealth from industry and empire created a culture in which success was the measure of worth. The theatre itself was increasingly commercialized, with the long run and the box-office return becoming the primary measures of a play's value.

Shaw's critique of success-as-reputation participates in a broader Edwardian anxiety about the relationship between commercial and artistic value. The "New Drama" — Ibsen, Shaw, Granville Barker — was often commercially unsuccessful by the standards of the West End, and its advocates argued that this was evidence of its quality, not its failure.
