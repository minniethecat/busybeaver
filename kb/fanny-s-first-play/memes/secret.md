## Definition

Secrecy is woven into the fabric of *Fanny's First Play* at every level. The play itself was performed anonymously — its authorship "a *secret de Polichinelle*" (P01-003), an open secret that everyone pretended not to know. Within the Induction, the critics are kept in the dark about the play they are to judge. Within the inner play, each young character has a secret — an arrest, a night in prison, a hidden relationship — that must be either confessed or discovered. Secrets, in Shaw's handling, are not merely plot devices but moral tests: what you conceal reveals what you fear.

## The Authorial Secret

Shaw's concealment of his authorship was, by his own account, "a necessary part of the play" (P01-003). Critics who approached a Shaw play "in a condition which is really one of derangement" — expecting paradoxes, bracing for intellectual combat — were unable to judge the work on its merits. Anonymity was a remedy: it forced the critics to engage with the play rather than the playwright. The experiment succeeded: the critics praised the unknown author — and then, when the secret was revealed, found themselves in the awkward position of having admired a Shaw play.

## In the Inner Play

The inner play is built on secrets. [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] has been missing for a fortnight, and his parents' frantic speculation — Is he in [[prison]]? Is he dead? Has he run away? — is the engine of the first act's comedy. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] has kept her arrest from her parents, and her confession is the play's moral turning point. [[dora-delaney|Dora]], by contrast, has no secrets: she announces her police-court appearance "plunging at once into privileged intimacy and into the middle of the room" (002-044). Her transparency is her freedom.

The play's resolution depends on the revelation of secrets. When the truth comes out — Bobby's arrest, Margaret's night in prison, the connection between them — the moral structure of both households collapses and must be rebuilt on more honest foundations.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw understood that secrecy is not simply a moral failing but a social mechanism. Respectable society depends on secrets: the things everyone knows but no one admits. The "open secret" of Shaw's authorship mirrors the open secrets of respectable family life — the hypocrisies, the hidden transgressions, the truths that would destroy the facade if spoken aloud. The play's argument is that these secrets must be broken open — not for the sake of scandal but for the sake of [[truth]].

## Key Passages

- **Shaw's admission**: "The concealment of the authorship, if a *secret de Polichinelle* can be said to involve concealment" (P01-003).
- **Dora's entrance**: "Plunging at once into privileged intimacy and into the middle of the room" — no secrets (002-044).
- **Margaret's confession**: The secret that transforms both households.
