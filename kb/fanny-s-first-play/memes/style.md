## Definition

Style in *Fanny's First Play* is never merely decorative. Each character's style — of speech, of dress, of thought — is a moral and social statement. The critics' varying critical styles reveal their limitations; the Count's aesthetic style is his philosophy made visible; Shaw's own stylistic performance — the play's mix of satire, farce, and moral seriousness — is an argument about what dramatic style should be.

## In the Play

The four critics represent four critical styles. Trotter's diplomatic circumlocution, Vaughan's intellectual abstraction, Gunn's journalistic immediacy, and Bannal's cheerful vulgarity are styles of thought before they are styles of writing. Each style enables certain insights and disables others.

The Count's style is a deliberate anachronism — eighteenth-century dress, eighteenth-century music, eighteenth-century manners — that functions as a critique of modernity. His style is beautiful but sterile; it can reject the present but cannot engage with it.

Shaw's own style in the Preface is characteristically self-aware: he discusses his stylistic choices — why he lampooned the critics, why he concealed his authorship — as part of the play's artistic project. Style, for Shaw, is never innocent: it always has designs on the reader.
