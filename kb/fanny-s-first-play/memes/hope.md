## Definition

Hope in *Fanny's First Play* cuts in two directions. For the young characters — [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]], [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] — hope is the engine of change, the belief that life can be better than their parents have made it. For the older generation, hope can be a retreat from reality: [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]]'s hope for a world of beauty, [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]]'s hope for Margaret's spiritual enlightenment, [[knox|Knox]]'s hope to avoid scandal. Shaw treats hope sympathetically but skeptically — it is only as good as the action it inspires.

## In the Play

[[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s hope is the play's emotional engine. She tells her father: "Oh, I hope it will go off well" (001-219). This simple hope — that her art will succeed, that the critics will understand — drives the Induction and is tested by the Epilogue's verdict.

[[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]]'s hope is more problematic. She tells Margaret: "I've waited for you to find out that happiness is within ourselves and doesn't come from outward pleasures. I've prayed oftener than you think that you might be enlightened. But if all my hopes and all my prayers are to come to this... then I don't know what I shall do: it'll kill me" (003-492). Hope here becomes emotional blackmail — a demand that reality conform to the hoper's wishes.

[[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] represents hope as aesthetic escapism. His hope for a world of beauty leads him to flee [[venice|Venice]] and ultimately to flee [[england|England]] when reality proves too vulgar. "Tomorrow I start for Venice, never to return," he declares (005-988). His hope is genuine but sterile — it produces no action beyond withdrawal.

## Key Passages

- **Fanny's hope**: "Oh, I hope it will go off well" (001-219).
- **Mrs. Knox's hopes**: "If all my hopes and all my prayers are to come to this..." (003-492).
- **The Count's retreat**: "Tomorrow I start for Venice, never to return" (005-988).
