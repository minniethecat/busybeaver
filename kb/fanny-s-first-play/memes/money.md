## Definition

Money in *Fanny's First Play* is never just money. It is a marker of [[respectability]], a measure of [[character]], and the foundation upon which the entire Gilbey/Knox social world is built. When characters speak of money — the cost of bail, the price of a concertina, the wages of a footman — they are simultaneously speaking of freedom, love, and power. Shaw's treatment of money exposes the economic infrastructure beneath the moral pretensions of the middle class.

Mr. Gilbey's first response to [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s arrival is financial: "If she wants money, she shan't have it. Not a farthing" (002-042). The instinct is revealing: money is the medium through which he understands all relationships, including his son's troubles.

## In the Play

### The Price of Freedom

Money buys freedom in the most literal sense: [[dora-delaney|Dora]] comes to the Gilbeys to "get the money to get Bobby out" of prison (002-105). The bail payment is a transaction that exposes the family's priorities — Gilbey's solicitor "will do what is right," but Dora's willingness to "fork out" represents a competing claim of care. [[knox|Mr. Knox]]'s payment of [[duvallet|Duvallet]]'s fine — "He takes out some money" — is another moment where cash mediates between transgression and respectability (003-065).

### The Economics of Class

[[juggins|Juggins]]'s entire position is defined by money. He is paid to be a footman, paid to say "sir," and his eventual revelation as the brother of a duke is also a revelation about money — he was "an idle younger son, unable to support myself" who could not "aspire to Miss Knox's hand" until he became "a sober, honest, and industrious domestic servant" earning his own living (004-435). The play's romantic resolution depends on this financial transformation, suggesting that in Shaw's world, love is only as valid as the economic independence that supports it.

### The Squiffer: Desire and Constraint

[[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s longing for a concertina — "ivory inlaid, with gold keys and Russia leather bellows" — is a small but telling detail (002-075). [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] "couldn't afford it, poor lad, though I knew he just longed to give it to me." The squiffer crystallizes the relationship between money, love, and frustration: Bobby's desire to give is real, but his economic dependence on his father makes him incapable of acting on it.

### Respectability as Economic Investment

[[knox|Mr. Knox]] has "worked hard to get a position and be respectable" (003-025). The language is commercial: respectability is an asset acquired through labour, maintained through vigilance, and threatened by any association with scandal. This economic framing of morality is precisely what Shaw satirizes — the reduction of [[character]] to credit rating.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw, a Fabian socialist, was acutely aware of the economic determinants of social life. The Preface does not address money directly, but the play's economic realism — the careful attention to who has money, who needs it, and what it can and cannot buy — is characteristic of Shaw's insistence that morality cannot be separated from material conditions.

The frame story provides an aristocratic counterpoint. The [[count-odowda|Count]] has enough money to live in a Venetian palazzo and dress in an eighteenth-century costume; his aestheticism is made possible by wealth. [[savoyard|Savoyard]]'s comment — "It's all right when you can afford it" — is Shaw's acknowledgment that the escape from vulgarity that the Count represents is itself a luxury good (001-030).

## Key Passages

- **Money as first response**: "If she wants money, she shan't have it. Not a farthing" — Mr. Gilbey's instinctive reduction of all problems to financial ones (002-042).
- **The squiffer**: "Bobby knew I hankered after it; but he couldn't afford it, poor lad" — Dora on the economics of courtship (002-075).
- **Dora offers to pay**: "I must have the money to get Bobby out; and if you won't fork out, I'll hunt up Holy Joe" — Dora challenging Gilbey's financial authority (002-105).
- **Paying the fine**: Knox "takes out some money" to pay Duvallet's fine — respectability settling its accounts (003-065).
- **Juggins on independence**: "As a sober, honest, and industrious domestic servant, who has, I trust, given satisfaction to his employer ... I feel I am a man with a character" — Juggins linking economic independence to moral standing (004-435).

## Historical Context

In Edwardian England, a middle-class family's income determined not only its standard of living but its social identity. The Gilbeys and Knoxes, as shopkeepers, occupied the precarious lower-middle-class position — respectable but never secure, one scandal away from social ruin. The "five shillings" that [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] is aghast at Margaret spending on a theatre ticket (003-085) represents a significant sum for a family of this class.

The period also saw the rise of consumer culture and the department store, making visible the relationship between money and identity that Shaw dramatizes. The suffragette movement's focus on women's economic independence — the right to own property, to earn wages, to control money — forms the political backdrop to the play's attention to who controls the purse strings.
