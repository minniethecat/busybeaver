## Definition

Home in *Fanny's First Play* is a site of contradiction. The Gilbey and Knox households are presented as models of English domestic respectability — orderly, comfortable, morally upright — yet they are also prisons from which the young must escape to achieve genuine adulthood. Shaw exposes the home not as a sanctuary from the world's corruption but as the primary site where that corruption — in the form of hypocrisy, emotional manipulation, and unearned moral authority — is reproduced.

## In the Play

Mrs. Gilbey describes their domestic routine with pride: "I'm at home on first Thursdays. And we have the Knoxes to dinner every Friday... We dine with them on Tuesdays. So that's two evenings pleasure every week" (002-334). The mechanical regularity of these "pleasures" reveals the emptiness at the heart of respectable domesticity.

When Margaret returns from prison, her mother's first thought is for the home's reputation: "When a girl runs away from home like that, people know what to think of her and her parents" (003-408). Knox adds: "She had a happy, respectable home — everything —" (003-409). The home's function, in their view, is to produce respectability, not happiness.

Margaret's prison experience reframes home entirely. She tells her mother: "I slept in that filthy cell with all the other drunks sounder than I should have slept at home" (003-477). The irony is devastating: a prison cell offers more peace than a "respectable" home.

Duvallet's speech to Mrs. Knox completes the critique. As a Frenchman, he admires the English for having "made an end of the despotism of the parent" and for a society where "home life can hardly be said to exist in England" (004-919). This backhanded compliment — home life's absence is a form of liberation — is the play's most radical statement about domesticity.
