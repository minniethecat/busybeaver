## Identity

George Bernard Shaw (1856–1950) is the author of *Fanny's First Play*, a play that self-consciously deploys its author's public reputation as a controversialist, Fabian, and critic. Shaw appears in the play not as a character but as an implicit presence — the unnamed author whose identity the Induction critics are desperate to discover, and whose known style and politics shape their responses to the work.

## Authorial Presence in the Play

### The Preface

Shaw opens the play with a characteristic Preface that simultaneously dismisses the work as "a potboiler" and delivers a sharp social critique. He attacks "mere morality, or the substitution of custom for conscience" and diagnoses the condition of the English middle class: "our respectable middle class people are all as dead as mutton" [P01-001]. This framing establishes the authorial voice as socially engaged and provocatively direct.

### The Anonymous Author

The central dramatic device of the Induction — that the critics do not know who wrote the play they are watching — functions as a commentary on Shaw's own relationship with the critical establishment. The critics' desperation to identify the author ("Who's it by?" [001-989]) mirrors the real-world phenomenon of Shaw's plays being judged by his reputation rather than their content. Shaw uses the anonymity to force both the fictional critics and the real audience to confront the question of whether art can be evaluated independently of its creator's identity.

### Self-Satire

Shaw satirizes his own public persona through the character of Trotter, whom he acknowledges in the Preface: "The critics whom I have lampooned in the induction to this play under the names of Trotter, Vaughan, and Gunn will forgive me: in fact Mr. Trotter forgave me beforehand, and assisted the makeup by which Mr. Claude King so successfully simulated his personal appearance" [P01-001]. The critics not lampooned "were somewhat hurt, as I should have been myself under the same circumstances" — Shaw extends the satire to include himself in the critical fraternity.

## The Fabian Connection

Shaw was a prominent member of the [[fabian-society]], and his political commitments inform the play's social themes. The Preface's call for the young to "do something that will get you into trouble" reflects Fabian gradualism combined with a more radical impulse toward individual moral awakening. The play's Cambridge Fabian Society setting directly references Shaw's own experience as a Fabian lecturer.

## Shaw and Theatrical Innovation

By 1911, Shaw was established as a leading figure in British theatre, known for plays that challenged theatrical conventions. The Induction's debate about what constitutes a "real play" echoes contemporary critical responses to Shaw's own work, which was frequently accused of being "discussions" rather than proper drama. Trotter's insistence that certain works are "not plays" [001-174] directly mirrors criticism Shaw received throughout his career.
