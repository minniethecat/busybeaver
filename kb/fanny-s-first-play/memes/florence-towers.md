## Setting

Florence Towers is the [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]]'s country house — an "old-fashioned" estate where the Induction and Epilogue of *Fanny's First Play* take place. The house serves as both a physical location and a symbolic space: it represents the Count's aesthetic retreat from the modern world, his attempt to create an eighteenth-century enclave in the midst of twentieth-century England.

The stage directions describe "the end of a saloon" that "has been curtained off to form a stage for a private theatrical performance" (001-001). This layering of theatricality — a stage within a stage within a country house — is central to the play's meta-theatrical structure. Florence Towers is simultaneously a real house, a theatrical venue, and a metaphor for the Count's carefully constructed self-image.

## In the Frame Narrative

The Induction takes place entirely at Florence Towers. The [[footman|Footman]] in "grandiose Spanish livery" (001-001) announces the arriving critics: [[cecil-savoyard|Savoyard]], [[trotter|Trotter]], [[vaughan|Vaughan]], [[gunn|Gunn]], and [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]]. Each critic's reaction to the house reveals character. Trotter is charmed by the dinner awaiting him. The Count hovers anxiously, managing the social dynamics of his daughter's theatrical experiment.

The Epilogue returns to Florence Towers after the inner play's performance. The critics deliver their verdict, and the revelation of [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s authorship reshapes everyone's understanding of what they have just seen. The house, which began as a neutral venue, becomes a site of revelation and reckoning.

## Significance

Florence Towers embodies the contradiction at the heart of the Count's character. He has created a beautiful refuge from the modern world — but the refuge is itself a theatrical performance, dependent on the very modernity it claims to transcend. The house requires servants, money, and an audience; it cannot exist in isolation. Fanny's play, performed within these walls, exposes the impossibility of her father's aesthetic withdrawal.
