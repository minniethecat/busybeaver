## Role

Dora Delaney is the inner play's moral wild card — a young woman who has been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] and emerged not shamed but clarified. She is what the respectable world calls "hardened" and what Shaw calls free. Her function is to demonstrate, by contrast with the panicking parents, that the loss of respectability can be a [[freedom|liberation]] rather than a catastrophe. She is ready to marry Bobby, ready not to, ready for whatever comes — because [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] has taught her that survival is more important than propriety.

## In the Play

Dora appears primarily in Acts II and IV. In Act II, she visits the [[gilbey|Gilbey]] household after her release from [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], and her interaction with [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] is a masterclass in class and moral tension. Dora refuses to perform shame: she is who she is, she has done what she has done, and she will not apologize for existing.

The newspaper report of her arrest — "Hardened at Eighteen. A quietly dressed, respectable-looking girl who refuses her name" (004-100) — is the play's most concise statement of the gap between appearance and reality. She looks respectable, but she "refuses her name" — refuses the identity that respectable society would assign her.

In the marriage negotiations of Act IV, Dora is the calmest person in the room. "I'm ready to marry Bobby, if that will be any satisfaction" (004-408) — her offer is both generous and ironic, a gift that exposes the absurdity of the social machinery grinding around her.

## Character Analysis

Dora is Shaw's portrait of working-class resilience. Unlike [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], who philosophizes her experience, Dora simply lives it. She has "educated Bobby a bit" (002-092) not through argument but through example. Her insistence that she would never "stand between a girl and a respectable marriage" (004-146) is not hypocrisy but generosity — she values what others need even when she doesn't need it herself.

Her moral clarity is the product of experience rather than reflection. She knows that respectability is a performance because she has been cast as the villain in its [[art-and-theatre|drama]] and has refused the role. She is the character who most fully embodies Shaw's conviction that "getting into trouble" may be the necessary price of moral growth (P01-001).

## Key Passages

- **The newspaper headline**: "Hardened at Eighteen. A quietly dressed, respectable-looking girl who refuses her name" (004-100).
- **Dora's education of Bobby**: "I've educated Bobby a bit: he's not the mollycoddle he was when you had him in hand" (002-092).
- **The generous offer**: "I'm ready to marry Bobby, if that will be any satisfaction" (004-408).
- **The [[morality|conscience]] of the fallen**: "I have a [[morality|conscience]]; though I mayn't be as particular as some" — Dora's quiet moral claim (004-146).
- **The respectable marriage**: "I'll never say anything to stand between a girl and a respectable marriage, or to stop a decent lad from settling himself" (004-146).

## Critical Reception

Dora has been read as Shaw's intervention in the "fallen woman" narrative that dominated Victorian and Edwardian fiction and [[art-and-theatre|drama]]. Where the conventional fallen woman died of shame or retreated to a convent, Dora survives, thrives, and becomes the moral center of her community. Feminist critics have noted her kinship with Mrs Warren — another Shaw "fallen woman" who refuses the script of shame.
