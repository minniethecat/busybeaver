## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, "experience" denotes the knowledge gained through direct, often painful, encounter with reality — as opposed to the second-hand knowledge of convention, education, and parental authority. Shaw argues that experience is the only reliable path to genuine moral understanding. The older generation, defined by their avoidance of experience, cannot teach the young anything worth knowing; the young, through transgression and its consequences, acquire a moral authority their parents lack. The play's central dramatic argument is that a night in prison teaches more about life, morality, and the self than a lifetime of respectable conformity.

## In the Play

### Margaret's Transformation

Margaret Knox is the play's primary vehicle for the argument from experience. Her night of transgression — leaving the Salvation Army meeting, going to a music hall, dancing, drinking champagne, fighting the police, and spending a fortnight in Holloway Gaol — transforms her from a dutiful clergyman's daughter into a woman who knows herself. She describes the experience in language that fuses the religious and the visceral: "I've made a sort of descent into hell ... Now hell is as real to me as a turnip" [003-108].

The core of Margaret's argument is that experience confers authority that abstract belief cannot match. When her mother speaks of redemption in conventional religious terms, Margaret retorts: "You don't believe it yourself, or you wouldn't pay policemen to twist their arms" [003-088]. Her mother's faith is a "tone that shows they don't mean anything real" [003-112]. Margaret's knowledge, by contrast, has been "knocked into" her — it is embodied, undeniable, real.

The most striking formulation comes when Margaret claims: "I'm a heroine of reality, if you can call me a heroine at all. And reality is pretty brutal, pretty filthy, when you come to grips with it. Yet it's glorious all the same. It's so real and satisfactory" [003-106]. The word "satisfactory" is key — experience, however brutal, produces a satisfaction that respectability cannot. She has been "set free" [003-116].

### The Older Generation's Inexperience

The play systematically exposes the older generation's lack of meaningful experience. Count O'Dowda has retreated to Venice to avoid modern life; he is "rather a stranger in your world" [001-020], and when confronted with his daughter's play, he is "dazed and agitated" [005-003]. Mr. Gilbey cannot process his son's imprisonment: "The disgrace of it will kill me" [002-086]. Mr. Knox's first instinct is concealment: "If only we can keep it dark, I don't care for anything else" [003-118].

The parents' authority is revealed as hollow precisely because it is not grounded in experience. They have never been tested. They have never confronted the police, or been hungry, or been thrown into a cell. Their morality is a set of inherited rules, not a lived truth. When Margaret says "I know now that I am stronger than you and Papa" [003-116], she is not being arrogant — she is stating a fact that the play endorses.

### The Preface's Argument

Shaw's Preface makes the argument explicit. He contrasts those who "drift along from their cradles to their graves doing what other people do for no other reason than that other people do it, and knowing nothing of good and evil, of courage and cowardice" with those who have been "awakened" by direct experience of disgrace [P01-001]. The shocking advice — "Do something that will get you into trouble" — is Shaw's rhetorical condensation of the play's thesis: experience, however costly, is worth more than a lifetime of untested virtue.

Shaw extends the argument to the theatre itself. The critics' judgment is warped by their lack of genuine experience — they know plays but not life. Trotter's surprise at Fanny's knowledge of the world is itself evidence of how little he understands: "I'm delighted to find there's something you don't know; and I shan't spoil you by dispelling an ignorance which, in my opinion, is highly becoming to your age and sex" [001-148]. His patronising dismissal of her knowledge is the voice of inexperience pretending to authority.

### Experience as Equaliser

The play's most radical move is to treat experience as a social equaliser. Bobby, Margaret, Dora, and Duvallet are all prison veterans — "Here we are, two gaolbirds, Bobby, disgraced forever. Isn't it a relief?" [003-042] — and this shared experience creates a bond that transcends class, nationality, and gender. Dora, the "common drunk and disorderly," is Margaret's equal in the fellowship of the punished. The play suggests that shared experience of the world's brutality is a more solid foundation for human community than shared adherence to social convention.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of experience draws on his own intellectual biography. As a self-educated Irishman in London, a Fabian socialist, and a music and drama critic, Shaw consistently championed experiential knowledge over institutional authority. The Preface's argument — that disgrace is preferable to conformity — echoes his lifelong attack on the "middle-class morality" that he saw as England's fundamental problem.

The play does not, however, romanticise suffering. Margaret's experience is genuinely brutal — "I've been shoved and bullied. I've had my arms twisted. I've been made scream with pain" [003-090]. Shaw is not arguing that suffering is good but that the knowledge gained through suffering is valuable. The alternative — the Count's aesthetic retreat, the Gilbeys' anxious respectability — is a kind of living death: "I hate to see dead people walking about: it is unnatural" [P01-001].

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period was marked by a crisis of authority across multiple domains — political (the suffragette movement, the Parliament Act of 1911), religious (the decline of orthodox belief), and social (the erosion of class deference). Shaw's argument for experience over authority was part of a broader intellectual movement that included the pragmatism of William James, the educational theories of John Dewey, and the literary realism of H.G. Wells and Arnold Bennett.

The specific context of women's experience was particularly charged. The suffragette movement, in which Fanny participates, politicised female experience — imprisonment, forced feeding, public protest — as a source of moral authority. Margaret's claim that her prison experience makes her "stronger" than her parents echoes the suffragette argument that direct action confers a legitimacy that passive virtue cannot.
