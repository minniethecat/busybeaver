## Setting

[[london|London]] in [[fanny-odowda|Fanny's First Play]] is not a single location but a map of Edwardian social geography. Each neighborhood, street, and venue encodes a specific class position, moral attitude, and relationship to the [[respectability|respectable]] order. Shaw uses London's geography with the precision of a social cartographer — the city's physical distances measure moral distances, and its named locations function as shorthand for entire ways of life.

## The Geography of Class

### The West End: The Little Theatre, Adelphi

The [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] in the Adelphi is the play's frame location — the site of the first performance on April 19, 1911. The Adelphi district, between the Strand and the Thames, was associated with experimental and alternative theatre, distinct from the commercial West End houses. This geographic choice signals Shaw's artistic allegiances: the [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] represents the insurgent [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic culture that challenged the "smart theatres" where harlequinades had been "dropped."

### The Suburbs: West Circular Road and Denmark Hill

The [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]] — the [[gilbey|Gilbey]] family's address — is the geographic emblem of lower-middle-class aspiration. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s anxious verification of the address (002-032) reflects the suburban anxiety about precise social positioning. [[camberwell|Denmark Hill]], nearby, anchors the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]-Knox world in the indeterminate zone between London proper and the countryside — close enough to the city to feel its gravitational pull, far enough to maintain the fiction of gentility.

### The West End at Night: Piccadilly Circus and Leicester Square

[[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]]'s transformative night begins at Piccadilly Circus, where she alights from the bus after a religious meeting:

> I got out of the bus at Piccadilly Circus, because there was a lot of light and excitement there. I walked to Leicester Square; and went into a great theatre. (003-056)

Piccadilly Circus — the illuminated heart of Edwardian entertainment — represents everything the [[religion|religious]] meeting was designed to protect her from. The trajectory from church to Piccadilly to Leicester Square theatre maps [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s journey from respectable daughter to independent woman in London's own geography.

### Variety Theatres and the Demimonde

[[duvallet|Lieutenant Duvallet]] meets "a charming young lady of perfect respectability" at a variety theatre — a venue type that occupied an ambiguous position in Edwardian moral geography, neither fully respectable nor openly disreputable. The irony of "perfect respectability" found in such a place is characteristically Shavian.

## Dramatic Function

London functions [[art-and-theatre|drama]]turgically as the space of encounter and transformation. Characters move between its districts, and each movement across the city's geography is also a movement across moral and social boundaries:

- [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s journey from the prayer meeting to Piccadilly Circus is a geographic enactment of her moral awakening
- The [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]]'s circumference position mirrors the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s' social position — on the edge of respectability, neither in nor out
- The [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] in the Adelphi anchors the frame story in a specific, real venue, grounding Shaw's metatheatrical experiment in actual London geography
- [[savoyard|Savoyard]]'s claim that "[[flawner-bannal|Bannal]] really represents the British playgoer" and that "there are a hundred thousand people in London that'll like it" maps cultural influence onto the city's demographic mass

## Key Passages

- **The first performance**: "Fanny's First Play was performed for the first time at the [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] in the Adelphi, London, on the afternoon of Wednesday, April 19th 1911" — the factual anchor of the fictional frame
- **[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s London night**: "I got out of the bus at Piccadilly Circus, because there was a lot of light and excitement there" — the city as catalyst of [[freedom|liberation]] (003-056)
- **London's critical mass**: "When he likes a thing, you may take your oath there are a hundred thousand people in London that'll like it" — Savoyard on [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]]'s representative power
- **The variety theatre encounter**: Duvallet's meeting with "a charming young lady of perfect respectability" — the city's moral geography in miniature
