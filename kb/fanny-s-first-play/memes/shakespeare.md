## Definition

Shakespeare haunts *Fanny's First Play* as the standard against which all English drama is measured — and as the obstacle that any modern playwright must overcome. Shaw's relationship to Shakespeare was famously ambivalent: he admired Shakespeare's craft while rejecting the idolatry that surrounded it. In the play, Shakespeare is invoked by critics as a club with which to beat modern drama — and by Shaw as evidence that critical categories are inadequate to living art.

## In the Play

The Induction's critics invoke Shakespeare as the gold standard of English drama, using his name to disparage the modern work they are about to see. Shaw's irony is that the critics understand Shakespeare no better than they understand Fanny's play — they invoke his name as a ritual, not as an engagement with his art.

Gunn's dismissal of "Shakespeare — heart and brain" psychology as "schoolboyish" (005-1017) is the play's most explicit Shakespeare reference. Gunn means to dismiss both Shakespeare and the modern play; Shaw means to show Gunn dismissing the greatest English playwright with the same glibness with which he dismisses everything else.

## Key Passages

- **Gunn on Shakespeare**: "Shakespeare would have called it liver and wits" (005-1017).
- **The critics' Shakespeare**: The Induction's invocations of tradition.
