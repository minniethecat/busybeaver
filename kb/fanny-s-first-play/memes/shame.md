## Definition

Shame in *Fanny's First Play* operates as the hidden engine of middle-class morality. Where respectability is the public performance, shame is the private terror — the dread of exposure that keeps the performance in place. Shaw distinguishes between two kinds of shame: the false shame of respectable people who fear being found out, and the genuine shame of those who have done wrong and know it. The Preface makes this distinction explicit, setting "loss of respectability" against "integrity of self-respect" [P01-001]. The play's [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic action tests whether shame can be overcome — and what happens to people when it is.

## In the Play

Four characters experience shame — or resist it — in revealing ways. [[gilbey|Gilbey]], on learning his son has been im[[crime-and-punishment|prison]]ed, declares "The disgrace of it will kill me" [002-045]. His shame is entirely social: he cares not what Bobby did but what the neighbors will think. Knox echoes this terror when [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] threatens to go public: "Is she going to ruin us? To let everybody know of her disgrace and shame? To tear me down from the position I've made for myself and you by forty years hard struggling?" [003-055]. For both fathers, shame is the fear of losing what they have built — a fear that proves stronger than any moral principle.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]], by contrast, refuses to feel shame. When Bobby asks if she is "scandalized," she replies that she "did no harm" — her [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] was for a harmless impulse, and the bad part "was done by other people" [003-072]. She treats her disgrace not as a burden but as a relief: "Here we are, two gaolbirds, Bobby, disgraced forever. Isn't it a relief?" [003-072]. This inversion — disgrace as [[freedom|liberation]] — is Shaw's central moral paradox.

[[juggins|Juggins]] provides the philosophical commentary. He observes that among the respectable classes, shame and sincerity are inversely related: "Tramps are often shameless; but they're never sincere" [003-062]. His point is that shame — the fear of exposure — produces not [[truth|honesty]] but its opposite: the elaborate [[hypocrisy|pretense]] that Knox's confession finally names.

[[count-odowda|The Count]], in the Epilogue, experiences a more aristocratic version of shame. Confronted with a play that violates his aesthetic and moral sensibilities, he declares he will "die of shame" and retreat to [[venice|Venice]] [005-098]. His shame is not about social standing but about being surpassed by a world he cannot comprehend — a generational shame, not a class one.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface establishes the philosophical framework. He contrasts "loss of respectability" with "integrity of self-respect" [P01-001], arguing that shame — the fear of losing social standing — is a worse moral guide than [[morality|conscience]]. The famous advice — "Do something that will get you into trouble" — is a prescription for overcoming shame: the young must risk disgrace to discover what they actually believe.

Shaw's treatment of shame owes something to Nietzsche's critique of slave morality — the idea that shame is an internalized form of social control — but Shaw gives it a distinctively English, middle-class inflection. For Shaw, shame is not a universal human emotion but a specific product of the suburban respectability system, sustained by the dread of what the neighbors will think.

## Key Passages

- **The Preface's distinction**: Shaw contrasts "loss of respectability" with "integrity of self-respect," arguing the young "had better have their souls awakened by disgrace" than drift through life knowing nothing of good and evil [P01-001].
- **[[gilbey|Gilbey]]'s terror**: "The disgrace of it will kill me. And it will leave a mark on him to the end of his life" [002-045] — shame as the annihilation of social identity.
- **[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s [[freedom|liberation]]**: "Here we are, two gaolbirds, Bobby, disgraced forever. Isn't it a relief?" [003-072] — disgrace as the end of [[hypocrisy|pretense]].
- **Knox's plea**: "Is she going to ruin us? To let everybody know of her disgrace and shame?" [003-055] — the shopkeeper's horror of exposure.
- **[[juggins|Juggins]] on shamelessness**: "Tramps are often shameless; but they're never sincere" [003-062] — the paradox of shame and [[truth|authenticity]].
- **[[count-odowda|The Count]]'s retreat**: "I should die of shame: I could not face it. I must go back" [005-098] — generational shame as the inability to adapt.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period saw a crisis in the social function of shame. The rise of mass-circulation newspapers meant that disgrace — once a local, manageable affair — could become public spectacle overnight. The suffragette movement deliberately weaponized shame, with women of good family courting [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] and public scandal to expose the hypocrisy of the system. The 1908 Old Bailey trial of the suffragettes, and the subsequent force-feeding controversy, turned respectable women into "gaolbirds" — exactly the transformation Shaw [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tizes through [[margaret-knox|Margaret]].

At the same time, the decline of religious belief — what Shaw called the substitution of "custom for [[morality|conscience]]" — left shame without its traditional theological framework. Without sin and damnation, shame became purely social: not a matter of the soul but of the neighbors. Shaw's play registers this secularization of shame and offers disgrace itself as a cure: if there is no God to be ashamed before, at least one can be honest before one's fellow [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]ers.
