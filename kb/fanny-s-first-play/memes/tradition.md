## Definition

Tradition in *Fanny's First Play* is the accumulated weight of the past — the customs, values, and institutions that the older generation treats as sacred and the younger generation discovers are hollow. The play stages a conflict between tradition and [[modern|modernity]] that is also a conflict between parents and children, convention and conscience, performance and reality. Tradition, in Shaw's analysis, is not wisdom inherited but power disguised: it is the mechanism by which the dead hand of the past controls the living.

## The Count's Tradition

The [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] is the play's embodiment of tradition-as-aesthetic. His eighteenth-century fantasy — the Venetian decor, the Spanish livery, the horror at modern dress — is a tradition that never existed. He has fabricated a past to escape the present. His daughter [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] sees through him: his traditions are costumes, his aestheticism is evasion. The Count's tradition is a refusal of history, not a respect for it.

## The Gilbeys' and Knoxes' Traditions

The Gilbeys and Knoxes practice a more familiar form of tradition: the inherited morality of the English middle class. Church attendance, sexual propriety, filial obedience — these are traditions that need no justification because they have always been observed. The play's dramatic argument is that such traditions are morally bankrupt. They produce conformity without conscience, propriety without principle.

When [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] breaks with tradition — going to [[prison]], refusing to be ashamed, telling the truth — she discovers that tradition's power is illusory. The sky does not fall. The neighbours may talk, but their talk cannot touch her. Tradition, she learns, is a paper tiger.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a radical critic of tradition who understood its appeal. The Preface's tone is not scornful but urgent: tradition has failed, and the young need something new. The play is an argument for replacing inherited morality with lived ethics, traditional religion with genuine spiritual inquiry, conventional art with honest expression. "Do something that will get you into trouble" is advice to break with tradition — not for the thrill of rebellion but for the sake of moral growth.
