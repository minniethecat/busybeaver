## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, the family is the primary social unit through which Shaw examines Edwardian morality. Two families — the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s and the Knoxes — provide the domestic architecture of the inner play. Both are respectable, churchgoing, middle-class households. Both have children who have been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]. And both discover, in the course of the play, that the family has functioned not as a moral shelter but as a theatre of [[hypocrisy|pretense]] where "respectability" substitutes for genuine relationship.

## In the Play

The [[gilbey|Gilbey]] household on the [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]] is the inner play's primary setting. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] presides over its moral hygiene with anxious vigilance: "This is a respectable household" (002-096). Mr. [[gilbey|Gilbey]] defines himself by his role as provider and moral exemplar — "an honest respectable husband" (004-290). Their son Bobby's [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] threatens the family's social standing more than it troubles their [[morality|conscience]].

The Knox household mirrors the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s. Knox is a shopkeeper who has enforced respectability by dismissing employees — "I've turned many a girl out of the shop for being light" (003-022). His daughter [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] exposes the gap between the morality he enforces on others and the moral formation he has provided his own child.

[[juggins|Juggins the footman]], whose mysterious parentage is hinted at throughout, embodies the family secret — the possibility that respectable domesticity is built on foundations it dare not acknowledge. "Was your mother married to your father?" Knox asks him directly, and the question goes unanswered (004-324).

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw treats the family with characteristic ambivalence. He recognizes it as a site of genuine affection — the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s and Knoxes do love their children — but he refuses to sentimentalize it. The family's function in Shaw's [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic economy is to provide the framework that the younger generation must escape.

The play's resolution, in which [[juggins|Juggins]] orchestrates marriages and social realignments, suggests that the family can be reformed rather than abolished. But the reform requires the destruction of [[hypocrisy|pretense]] — the acknowledgment that "we've no real religion" and that "all our respectability is pretending" (004-308)(003-098).

## Key Passages

- **The respectable household**: "This is a respectable household. You've gone and got my poor innocent son into trouble" — [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] (002-096).
- **The honest husband**: "What right have you to treat a man like that? an honest respectable husband? as if he were dirt under your feet?" — Knox's wounded dignity (004-290).
- **The shopkeeper's discipline**: "I've turned many a girl out of the shop for being light" — Knox enforcing the morality he failed to teach his daughter (003-022).
- **The family secret**: "Was your mother married to your father?" — Knox to [[juggins|Juggins]], unanswered (004-324).
- **Living without respectability**: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" — [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s discovery (004-253).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian middle-class family was organized around the doctrine of "separate spheres": the husband as breadwinner and public actor, the wife as domestic manager and moral guardian. Children were expected to defer to parental authority well into adulthood. The family was seen as the basic unit of social order — its health was the nation's health.

Shaw's critique reflects the Fabian socialist view that the family, like other institutions, needed to be examined rationally rather than revered sentimentally. The rise of the "[[gender-roles|New Woman]]," the suffrage movement, and the beginnings of birth control advocacy all challenged traditional family ideology in the years before the First World War.
