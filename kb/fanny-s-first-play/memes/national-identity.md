## Overview

National identity is a pervasive satirical motif in *Fanny's First Play* (1911), functioning both as characterisation and as a vehicle for Shaw's critique of English provincialism. The play triangulates four national perspectives — English, Irish, Italian, and French — with each serving as a distorting mirror for the others. [[count-odowda|The Count]]'s Irish-Italian cosmopolitanism, Trotter's self-conscious Englishness, and Duvallet's Francophone encomium to English [[freedom|liberty]] collectively produce a kaleidoscopic portrait of Edwardian national self-understanding that is at once affectionate and devastating.

## The Count: Irish Exile and Italian Aestheticism

[[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] is Shaw's most elaborate vehicle for an outsider's critique of English modernity. His self-description establishes the layered national identity that defines his perspective:

> [001-015] Well, you see, Mr. Savoyard, I'm rather a stranger in your world. I am not, I hope, a modern man in any sense of the word. I'm not really an Englishman: my family is Irish: I've lived all my life in Italy — in Venice mostly — my very title is a foreign one: I am a Count of the Holy Roman Empire.

This triple displacement — Irish by blood, Italian by residence, cosmopolitan by self-conception — enables the Count's uncompromising verdict on English civilisation:

> [001-021] I found England befouled with industrialism: well, I did what Byron did: I simply refused to live in it.

[[count-odowda|The Count]]'s aesthetic nationalism — his preference for Cimarosa and Pergolesi over Beethoven and Wagner, Venetian palazzi over English country houses, eighteenth-century costume over modern dress — is Shaw's satire of the gentility that treats national identity as a consumer choice. [[count-odowda|The Count]] has purchased his Italian identity, and his disdain for [[england|England]] is the luxury of a man who can afford to live elsewhere.

Yet Shaw does not let Irishness function as simple moral superiority. [[count-odowda|The Count]]'s title — "a Count of the Holy Roman Empire" — is, as Savoyard dryly observes, from nowhere: "Where's that?" "At present, nowhere, except as a memory and an ideal" [001-016]. Irish aristocratic pretension, Shaw implies, is as constructed as any other national mythology.

## Trotter: Englishness as Anxiety

Vaughan Trotter represents the English critical establishment's uneasy relationship with its own national character. Savoyard's introduction captures the paradox:

> [001-065] As you don't like English people, I don't know that you'll get on with Trotter, because he's thoroughly English: never happy except when he's in Paris, and speaks French so unnecessarily well that everybody there spots him as an Englishman the moment he opens his mouth.

Trotter's Frenchness — his Academic Committee uniform modelled on the French Academy, his Parisian mannerisms, his cultural references — is a performance that only reinforces his Englishness. The more he tries to escape national type, the more indelibly he is marked by it. His outburst against Fanny's French — "Stop talking French to me: it's not a proper language for a young girl" [001-140] — reveals the provincial anxiety beneath the cosmopolitan performance.

## Duvallet: France as England's Mirror

[[duvallet|Lieutenant Duvallet]]'s extended monologue in Act III is the play's most sustained treatment of national identity. Ostensibly a Frenchman's praise of English [[freedom|liberty]], it functions as Shaw's ironic anatomy of English self-regard:

> [003-253] Your daughter, madam, is superb. Your country is a model to the rest of Europe. If you were a Frenchman, stifled with prudery, hypocrisy and the tyranny of the family and the home, you would understand how an enlightened Frenchman admires and envies your freedom.

The speech systematically inverts Anglo-French stereotypes: France is portrayed as the land of patriarchal tyranny ("we are not men: we are only sons — grown-up children"), while [[england|England]] becomes the beacon of individualism ("here one is a human being — an end in himself"). Duvallet's catalogue of English virtues — freedom, broadmindedness, the absence of home life — is so hyperbolic that it reads as Shaw's satire of English self-congratulation delivered through a foreign mouthpiece.

The military comparison that follows — Wellington's pragmatism versus French *gloire*, Nelson's strategic caution versus Napoleonic bravado — extends the national satire to the realm of martial mythology. Duvallet's conclusion — "a Frenchman is not really stronger than a German, than an Italian, even than an Englishman" [003-253] — inverts the chauvinism it appears to endorse.

## "A bit of English fun": Violence as National Character

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s account of the boat-race night riot concludes with a chillingly casual national epithet: "everybody said it was a bit of English fun" [002-186]. The police brutality she describes — women "attacked" and "treated just as roughly" as male students — is naturalised as national character: English fun, English rowdiness, English high spirits. Shaw's irony is surgical: the phrase "English fun" is made to contain assault, institutional violence, and the casual acceptance of both.

## The Critics' Post-Mortem: National Stereotype as Literary Signature

The Epilogue's critical roundtable reduces national identity to literary brand. Gunn identifies the "Frenchman who cracks up the English and runs down the French" as "the hackneyed old Shaw touch" [005-015]; Vaughan diagnoses the play as "an attempt to expose the supposed hypocrisy of the Puritan [[class|middle class]] in [[england|England]]" [005-023]; [[bannal|Bannal]], the man in the street, cuts through the analysis: "I believe it's Shaw" [005-024]. National identity, in the play's final movement, is revealed as authorial signature — a set of tropes so recognisable that they function as attribution.
