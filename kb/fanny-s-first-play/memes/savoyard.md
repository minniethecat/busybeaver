## Role

Cecil Savoyard is a character in the Induction of *Fanny's First Play*. He is an actor-manager associated with the Savoy Theatre (hence his name), who agrees to produce Fanny O'Dowda's anonymous play. Shaw uses Savoyard to represent the practical, professional side of theatre—the artist who works in the commercial theatre but retains artistic integrity.

## In the Play

Savoyard first enters in the Induction, announced by the Footman as "Mr. Cecil Savoyard" — a middle-aged man in evening dress and a fur-lined overcoat [P02-008]. His first concern is practical rather than artistic: "When does the play begin? Half-past eight?" [P02-009]. When told it's not until nine, he immediately arranges to telephone his wife — not "Mrs. Cecil Savoyard" but "Mrs. William Tinkler" — a wry detail that hints at the theatrical world's casual relationship with conventional names and identities [P02-012].

His exchange with Count O'Dowda establishes the dynamic between patron and producer. The Count fusses over unlocking bookcases for the literary guests; Savoyard's response — "Oh, you mean the dramatic critics. M'yes. I suppose there's a smoking room?" [P02-014] — is a masterpiece of compressed characterisation. The actor-manager is indifferent to critical opinion and intellectual pretension; he wants practical comfort and a working theatre.

## Character Analysis

The name "Savoyard" carries multiple resonances. It refers to the Savoy Theatre (home of the Gilbert and Sullivan operas, which Shaw admired and criticised in equal measure) and, more broadly, to the tradition of English comic opera. By naming his actor-manager "Savoyard," Shaw signals that Fanny's play belongs to a theatrical tradition that values wit and entertainment alongside ideas.

Shaw's Savoyard is notably free of the moral panic that grips Trotter and the intellectual posturing that marks Vaughan. He represents the theatre professional who understands that a play's first duty is to hold an audience—a view Shaw himself vigorously defended throughout his career as a critic and playwright.

## Key Passages

Savoyard's dialogue is found in the Induction. His exchanges with Count O'Dowda establish the theatrical framework for the play-within-the-play.
