## Overview

The [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] in the Adelphi, [[london|London]], is the real-world venue where [[fanny-odowda|Fanny's First Play]] received its first performance on "the afternoon of Wednesday, April 19th 1911." Its presence in the play's frame narrative is both factual and symbolic — it represents the alternative theatrical culture that Shaw championed throughout his career, a culture distinct from both the commercial West End houses and the conservative [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic establishment the play's critics represent.

## The Venue

The Little Theatre was located in John Street, Adelphi, just south of the Strand. Opened in 1910 under the management of Gertrude Kingston, it was one of several small [[london|London]] venues — along with the Court Theatre, the Royalty Theatre, and the Kingsway — that constituted the "alternative" theatre circuit. These venues were characterized by:

- **Smaller auditoriums** (typically 300–500 seats vs. the 1,000+ of West End houses)
- **Repertory programming** rather than long commercial runs
- **Commitment to new and experimental work**, including Ibsen, Shaw, and the "New Drama"
- **Lower ticket prices** that made theatre accessible to a broader audience

The theatre closed in 1941 and was demolished, but its brief life coincided with a crucial period in the development of modern British [[art-and-theatre|drama]].

## In the Play

The Little Theatre appears explicitly only in the play's performance note:

> *Fanny's First Play* was performed for the first time at the Little Theatre in the Adelphi, London, on the afternoon of Wednesday, April 19th 1911.
> [title-page]

But its significance permeates the Induction. The critics — [[trotter|Trotter]], [[vaughan|Vaughan]], [[gunn|Gunn]], and [[bannal|Flawner Bannal]] — represent the established critical culture that the Little Theatre movement was challenging. Fanny's decision to stage a private performance at her father's country house, with critics brought in specially, mirrors the actual dynamics of the period: new [[art-and-theatre|drama]] often had to create its own institutional spaces because the existing theatrical infrastructure was hostile to innovation.

## The Repertory Movement

The Little Theatre was part of the broader repertory theatre movement that Shaw championed in his criticism and practice. Unlike the commercial system — in which a single play ran as long as it drew audiences — the repertory system maintained a rotating repertoire of productions, allowing for:

- Artistic risk-taking that commercial runs discouraged
- Development of ensemble acting companies
- Programming of challenging new work alongside classics
- Creation of an educated audience rather than a merely entertained one

Shaw's own plays, including *Fanny's First Play*, were beneficiaries of this movement. Without the repertory theatres' willingness to stage unconventional work, the New Drama would have had no platform.

## Dramatic Function

The Little Theatre serves multiple functions in the play's symbolic architecture:

1. **Historical [[truth|authenticity]]**: The specific date and venue anchor the frame narrative in real theatrical history, giving the play's metatheatrical experiment concrete grounding
2. **Cultural positioning**: By naming an alternative theatre rather than a West End house, Shaw signals his artistic allegiances before the play proper begins
3. **Institutional contrast**: The Little Theatre's experimental ethos contrasts sharply with the conventional critical sensibilities of Trotter, Vaughan, and Gunn
4. **The repertory ideal**: Fanny's description of her play's "style and construction" as "considered the very highest art nowadays" for "repertory theatres of the most superior kind" explicitly invokes the repertory movement's aesthetic standards (001-017)
