## Definition

Convention — the unwritten rules that govern social behaviour — is Shaw's primary target in *Fanny's First Play*. The Preface opens with a distinction between "mere morality" and "the substitution of custom for conscience" (P01-001): convention is morality without thought, obedience without conviction. The play's dramatic argument is that conventions must be broken — not for the sake of rebellion but for the sake of genuine moral life.

## In the Play

The conventions of Edwardian family life are the play's primary satirical target. Children must obey their parents. Young women must be chaste. Young men must be sober. Church must be attended. The shop must be kept. These conventions are enforced not by law but by the fear of [[respectability]] — the terror of what the neighbours will say.

The play's young characters have violated these conventions — and discovered that the consequences are less terrible than the conventions themselves. [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] has been drunk and disorderly; [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] has been in [[prison]]; [[dora-delaney|Dora]] has violated every convention of feminine modesty. And all three are happier, freer, and more morally alive than their parents.

The critics, too, are governed by convention. [[trotter|Trotter]]'s critical judgments are determined by what a gentleman is supposed to admire. [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]]'s "common sense" is simply the conventional wisdom of the man in the street, elevated to the status of critical principle. [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s experiment — an anonymous play judged without reference to its author's reputation — is designed to bypass critical convention and force genuine engagement.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a critic of convention in all its forms — social, artistic, intellectual. His plays are arguments against doing things "because other people do them." *Fanny's First Play* makes this argument dramatically: it shows young people breaking conventions and discovering that the sky does not fall. The "desperate advice" of the Preface — "Do something that will get you into trouble" — is advice to violate convention in the service of integrity.

## Key Passages

- **"Custom for conscience"**: "Mere morality, or the substitution of custom for conscience" — the definition of convention as moral failure (P01-001).
- **"Doing what other people do"**: The Preface's critique of conventional life — "drifting along from their cradles to their graves doing what other people do for no other reason than that other people do it" (P01-001).
