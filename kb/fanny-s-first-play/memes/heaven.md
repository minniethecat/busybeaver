## Definition

Heaven appears in *Fanny's First Play* as both a theological concept and a satirical device. Characters invoke heaven to express shock, to swear sincerity, or to gesture toward a standard of judgment higher than social opinion. The word is used more often in the Induction than in the inner play — the critics and the Count reach for it as a rhetorical crutch — which itself is a commentary on the gap between religious language and genuine religious conviction.

## In the Induction

[[cecil-savoyard|Savoyard]] is the first to invoke heaven: "Lord! no: heaven forbid!" he exclaims when warned about criticizing the critics (001-074). The phrase is conventional, but in context it registers something real: the social danger of offending the men who make or break theatrical reputations. Heaven here is a stand-in for the feared consequences — not divine judgment but critical judgment.

[[trotter|Trotter]]'s invocation of heaven is more theatrical: "Stop talking French to me: it's not a proper language for an Englishman to argue in" is an outburst that channels the heavens as a witness to his linguistic xenophobia (001-157). Heaven, for Trotter, is English-speaking.

## In the Inner Play

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s references to heaven are more serious. In her confession to her mother, she describes the "meeting" at the [[prison-setting|prison]]: "The meeting got on my nerves, somehow. It was the singing that set me wrong" (003-078). The prison chapel service — a religious ritual in a secular institution — provokes not piety but nervous breakdown. Heaven, in this context, is what the respectable world claims to believe in but cannot genuinely access.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a religious sceptic who took religion seriously. His concept of the Life Force — the evolutionary drive toward higher consciousness — was his alternative to traditional heaven. The play's religious language is consistently ironized, but the longing for something beyond respectability — for "salvation" rather than "morality" — is real. When [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] asks, "What's the use of that if they don't choose to believe it? You don't expect them to take your word, do you?" (003-098), she touches on the epistemological problem that religion exists to solve: how do we know what is true? Heaven, in this reading, is the name for a certainty that respectable society cannot provide.

## Key Passages

- **Savoyard's oath**: "Lord! no: heaven forbid!" — the critic's fear of the critics (001-074).
- **Trotter's heaven**: "Stop talking French to me: it's not a proper language" — heaven as English (001-157).
- **The prison service**: "The meeting got on my nerves" — heaven invoked in the prison chapel (003-078).
