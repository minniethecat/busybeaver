## Role

Mr. Gunn is a minor character in the Induction (the frame narrative). He appears among the party of critics and cultural figures invited to judge the anonymous play that Fanny O'Dowda has written. He represents the intellectual establishment—the urbane, self-satisfied critic whose judgments are governed more by convention than by genuine discernment.

## In the Play

Gunn appears alongside Trotter, Vaughan, and Flawner Bannal in the Induction scenes. Their collective response to the play-within-the-play forms Shaw's satirical commentary on dramatic criticism. Gunn's reactions, though less pronounced than Trotter's moral indignation, reflect the complacency of the Edwardian critical establishment.

## Character Analysis

Shaw sketches Gunn as a type: the cultivated gentleman whose opinions are received rather than earned. Like the other Induction critics, he serves as a mirror in which the audience can observe how criticism operates as a form of social performance. When the critics fail to recognize that the play savages their own class, Gunn's quiet complicity in that blindness is telling.

## Key Passages

Gunn's dialogue appears in the Induction scene. Shaw uses the Induction critics collectively to dramatise his long-standing complaint: that English dramatic criticism judges plays by propriety rather than truth.

> [P01-027] Gunn is not given extended speeches; his significance lies in what he does not say—the silent assent that enables the critical consensus Shaw deplores.
