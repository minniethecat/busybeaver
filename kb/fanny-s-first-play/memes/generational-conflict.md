## Definition

*Fanny's First Play* is structured as a generational war fought on two fronts. In the Induction, [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]] presents a play that her father, [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]], finds morally objectionable — a conflict between a [[education|Cambridge]]-educated daughter and her aesthete father. In the inner play, Bobby, [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], and [[dora-delaney|Dora]] — the younger generation — have all transgressed the [[morality|moral code]] of their parents, and the play's [[art-and-theatre|drama]] consists of the parents' discovery that their children are not the people they thought they had raised.

## In the Play

The Induction's generational conflict is comic but real. [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] sent his daughter to [[education|Cambridge]] and now regrets it: she has returned with [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] jargon, intellectual independence, and a play that offends his aesthetic sensibilities. "[[education|Cambridge]] is not what it was in my father's time," he laments (001-110). Fanny, for her part, treats her father with affectionate condescension — "In [[education|Cambridge]] we regard you as a bel esprit, a wit, an Irresponsible, a Parisian Immoralist" (001-150).

The inner play's generational conflict is more consequential. Bobby, [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], and [[dora-delaney|Dora]] have all been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] — an experience that their parents cannot assimilate into their moral framework. The parents' response oscillates between denial and panic. They want the children married off, the scandal contained, and respectability restored. But the children have discovered something their parents never learned: that respectability is a [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] from which actual [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] can liberate you.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s testimony is the play's generational manifesto: "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free. And when I got free I was what most people call bad" (003-088). The parents' morality has produced children who can only become moral by rejecting everything their parents taught them.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was fifty-five when *Fanny's First Play* was produced — old enough to be the parent generation, yet his sympathies are entirely with the young. This is characteristic: Shaw's creative evolution philosophy required each generation to surpass its predecessor, and his plays consistently side with the children against the parents (*Mrs Warren's Profession*, *Major Barbara*).

The innovation of *Fanny's First Play* is the framing device: by placing the inner play's generational conflict inside another generational conflict (Fanny vs. the Count), Shaw suggests that the pattern is recursive. Every generation rebels; every generation becomes the establishment the next generation must rebel against.

- **The kittens**: "I say again in the teeth of the whole [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]], kittens. Impertinent little kittens" — the Count on the young (001-115).
- **Good but not free**: "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free" — [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] (003-088).
- **Living without respectability**: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" — the parental generation's discovery (004-253).
- **[[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s education of Bobby**: "I've educated Bobby a bit: he's not the mollycoddle he was when you had him in hand" — the young educating each other (002-092).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period saw the emergence of a distinctive youth culture for the first time in British history. The expansion of secondary education, the growth of white-collar employment, and the influence of popular entertainment (music halls, variety theatres, the early cinema) created a generational experience that differed sharply from that of the Victorian parents.

The suffragette movement was largely a young women's movement, and its militancy — window-smashing, arson, hunger strikes — was a form of generational rebellion as well as political protest. [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]], with her [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] experience and her refusal to be shamed, is a fictional cousin of the suffragettes.
