## Role

Lady Constance Lytton (1869–1923) is a historically real figure referenced in the Epilogue of *Fanny's First Play*. She is never seen on stage, but her name carries enormous dramatic weight: Fanny O'Dowda's revelation that she "did a month with Lady Constance Lytton" is the climactic moment of the frame narrative. It transforms Fanny from a charming but sheltered young lady into a participant in the most radical political movement of her time — the militant suffrage campaign.

## In the Play

Lady Constance is referenced in a single, electrifying line in the Epilogue. When Trotter accuses Fanny of being a suffragette who "was on that Deputation," Fanny admits: "It is. I did a month with Lady Constance Lytton; and I'm prouder of it than I ever was of anything or ever shall be again" [005-040]. This confession shatters her father's illusions. The Count, whose entire identity rests on aesthetic withdrawal from modern England, responds: "She will never return to Venice. I feel now as I felt when the Campanile fell" [005-041].

The reference to Lady Constance does more than identify Fanny's political affiliation — it explains the inner play. Fanny's play-within-the-play, with its frank treatment of prison, class, and the hollowness of respectability, is not the work of an innocent but of a radical.

## Character Analysis

Shaw's choice of Lady Constance Lytton as Fanny's reference point is precise and significant. Lady Constance was the daughter of the Viceroy of India, a member of the aristocracy who renounced privilege to join the Women's Social and Political Union. She was imprisoned multiple times, force-fed during hunger strikes, and famously disguised herself as a working-class woman ("Jane Warton") to expose the differential treatment of prisoners by class.

By aligning Fanny with Lady Constance, Shaw signals that Fanny's rebellion is not adolescent contrarianism but principled political action. The historical Constance Lytton provides the moral authority that Fanny's youth and gender might otherwise deny her. The critics' patronizing response — Trotter's "My dear young lady" — is exposed as inadequate in light of what Fanny has actually done.

## Key Passages

- **The revelation**: "I did a month with Lady Constance Lytton; and I'm prouder of it than I ever was of anything or ever shall be again" [005-040].
- **Trotter's deduction**: "You can't deceive me. That bit about the police was real. You're a Suffragette, Miss O'Dowda. You were on that Deputation" [005-039].
- **The Count's collapse**: "She will never return to Venice. I feel now as I felt when the Campanile fell" [005-041].
- **Fanny's defiant purpose**: "Yes: it'll teach you what it feels like to be forcibly fed" [005-042] — linking her theatrical project directly to her prison experience.

## Historical Context

Lady Constance Lytton was a leading figure in the Women's Social and Political Union (WSPU), the militant suffrage organization founded by Emmeline Pankhurst. She was imprisoned in 1909 and 1910, and her accounts of force-feeding — published in her 1914 memoir *Prisons and Prisoners* — were instrumental in turning public opinion against the practice. Her actions embodied Shaw's Preface dictum: "Do something that will get you into trouble" [P01-001].
