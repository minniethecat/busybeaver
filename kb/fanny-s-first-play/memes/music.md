## Definition

Music in *Fanny's First Play* operates as a marker of class, taste, and spiritual aspiration. The Count's devotion to Cimarosa, Pergolesi, Gluck, and Mozart (and his rejection of Beethoven and Wagner) defines his aesthetic — and class — position. Margaret's love of hymn-singing expresses a different, more democratic relationship to music: music as communal joy rather than refined connoisseurship. Between these poles, Shaw maps music as a terrain of social conflict and individual longing.

## In the Play

[[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]]'s musical tastes are his aesthetic manifesto. He tells Savoyard: "I find Beethoven's music coarse and restless, and Wagner's senseless and detestable. I do not listen to them. I listen to Cimarosa, to Pergolesi, to Gluck and Mozart" (001-072). This is class judgment disguised as aesthetic judgment: the eighteenth century against the nineteenth, aristocracy against the bourgeoisie, order against chaos.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s relationship to music is the opposite. It is hymn-singing at a religious meeting that triggers her night of liberation: "It was the singing, I suppose: you know I love singing a good swinging hymn; and I felt it was ridiculous to go home in the bus after we had been singing so wonderfully about climbing up the golden stairs to heaven. I wanted more music — more happiness — more life" (003-471). Music here is not an object of refined contemplation but a force that drives action.

[[dora-delaney|Dora]] brings music into the play's closing scenes with her concertina (the "squiffer"): "A concertina adds its music to the revelry" (004-882). Dora's music is popular, democratic, and joyful — the antithesis of the Count's rarefied classicism. She "bursts into song: Hide me in the meat safe till the cop goes by. Hum the dear old music as his step draws nigh" (004-700).
