## Definition

Courage, in the moral universe of *Fanny's First Play*, is not physical bravery but the willingness to defy social convention. Shaw's Preface links courage and [[morality]] explicitly: the young need "to know something of good and evil, of courage and cowardice" rather than drifting through life "doing what other people do for no other reason than that other people do it" (P01-001). Courage is the virtue that makes genuine moral development possible — without it, one remains trapped in [[respectability]].

## In the Play

The inner play is structured around acts of moral courage — small in scale but profound in consequence. [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]] displays the most conspicuous courage: she confesses her night in prison to her horrified parents, refusing to lie or minimize what happened. "I don't care for you and Papa any the less," she tells her mother, "but I'm not afraid of you" (003-092). This is courage as honesty — the willingness to tell the truth when lying would be easier.

[[bobby-gilbey|Bobby Gilbey]]'s courage is more muted but no less real. Facing his father's bluster and his mother's tears, he refuses to be ashamed of his arrest. [[dora-delaney|Dora Delaney]] possesses the courage of indifference: she simply does not care what the respectable world thinks. "You needn't fuss: there's no disgrace," she tells Gilbey (002-081).

The older generation notably lacks this virtue. [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] tells her husband, "You wouldn't have the courage" (004-272) to face the truth about their daughter. Knox's morality is a morality of fear — fear of scandal, fear of the neighbours, fear of his own conscience.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's conception of courage is inseparable from his critique of conventional morality. The Preface frames the entire play as an exercise in moral courage: it offers "to young people in our suburbs the desperate advice: Do something that will get you into trouble" (P01-001). Getting into trouble — the right kind of trouble — is Shaw's formula for ethical growth. It requires the courage to risk respectability in pursuit of integrity.

## Key Passages

- **Courage and cowardice**: The Preface contrasts "courage and cowardice" with the drift of conventional life (P01-001).
- **Margaret's defiance**: "I don't care for you and Papa any the less; but I'm not afraid of you" — courage as honesty (003-092).
- **Mrs. Knox's diagnosis**: "You wouldn't have the courage" — the older generation's failure of nerve (004-272).
