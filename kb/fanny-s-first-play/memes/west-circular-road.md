## Setting

West Circular Road is the address of the Knox family household in *Fanny's First Play*. Like Denmark Hill for the Gilbeys, this suburban [[london]] location is a marker of social position. [[juggins]], the Knoxes' footman, offers the play's most precise social commentary on the address: "A great many most respectable people live in the West Circular Road, madam; but the address is not a guarantee of respectability" [001-067].

## In the Play

The Knox home on West Circular Road is the setting for key scenes in the inner play, particularly the confrontations between Margaret Knox and her parents, and the arrival of Duvallet bearing news of the boat-race night's adventures. The domestic space becomes a theatre of social anxiety, where the Knoxes struggle to maintain appearances even as Margaret's experience threatens to shatter their carefully constructed world.

The Gilbeys' anxious inquiry about the address—"The West Circular Road. Is that a respectable address, Juggins?" [001-067]—reveals the obsessive geography of class in Edwardian London. Every street carries a precise social valuation, and the Gilbeys need to know where they stand.

## Dramatic Function

Shaw uses these suburban London addresses with precision. Denmark Hill (Gilbey) and West Circular Road (Knox) are literally and figuratively on the same social circuit—the circularity of the road name is itself suggestive of the closed loop of respectability in which these families are trapped. The geography of the play maps the geography of class.

## Key Passages

Juggins's observation that the address "is not a guarantee of respectability" [001-067] is one of the play's most economical pieces of social criticism. The Knoxes, who have taken their address as a certificate of class standing, are about to discover that Margaret has shattered that assumption.
