## Role

Mr. Trotter is a character in the Induction of *Fanny's First Play*. He is the gentleman-amateur critic who embodies everything Shaw despised about English dramatic criticism: the insistence that plays should be "improving," the terror of anything that challenges respectability, and the comical inability to recognize oneself as the target of satire.

## In the Play

Trotter's most memorable moment comes when he denounces the inner play for its immorality—completely failing to see that the satire is aimed at people exactly like him. Shaw's stage direction notes that Trotter "forgave me beforehand"—a wry acknowledgment that real-life Trotters would respond to the play exactly as the fictional Trotter does.

## Character Analysis

Trotter is the most fully drawn of the Induction critics because he represents the type Shaw had spent decades battling as a drama critic. The critic who judges by propriety—who asks "is this play suitable for respectable people?" rather than "is this play true?"—is the enemy of serious drama. Trotter's indignation is both funny and, to Shaw, genuinely harmful.

Shaw's Preface makes the point explicit: the Trotter type "has never seen a first-rate play or read a first-rate book in his life, and knows nothing of art or literature, yet sets up as a judge of both." The Induction dramatises this critique by letting Trotter condemn himself with his own words.

## Key Passages

Trotter's dialogue appears in the Induction scenes. His exchange with Fanny and Count O'Dowda about the play's morality is one of Shaw's most economical pieces of satirical characterisation.

> [P01-027] Trotter's bewilderment at being asked to judge a play that refuses to flatter his prejudices is the comic engine of the Induction.
