## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, "self-sacrifice" is the language that respectable people use to dignify their self-interested actions. It is the rhetoric of noble suffering that disguises the pursuit of what one already wants. Juggins, the play's most penetrating moral analyst, dismisses it with surgical precision: "No woman will deny herself the romantic luxury of self-sacrifice and forgiveness when they take the form of doing something agreeable" [003-041]. Self-sacrifice, in Shaw's account, is rarely a sacrifice of self at all — it is self-expression dressed in moral costume.

## In the Play

The concept of self-sacrifice surfaces in Act III during Bobby's attempt to escape his engagement to [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]. Bobby proposes to use his prison sentence as grounds for honorable withdrawal: "I'll say I can't hold her to an engagement with a man who's been in quod" [003-040]. Juggins immediately identifies the flaw: "Very dangerous, sir. No woman will deny herself the romantic luxury of self-sacrifice and forgiveness when they take the form of doing something agreeable. She's almost sure to say that your misfortune will draw her closer to you" [003-041].

Juggins then generalizes the point. Bobby, he observes, would prefer "to make it look like an act of self-sacrifice for her sake on your part, or provoke her to break the engagement herself. Both plans have been tried repeatedly, but never with success, as far as my knowledge goes" [003-042]. The "act of self-sacrifice" is revealed as a strategic performance — a way of getting what one wants (freedom from the engagement) while claiming the moral credit for it.

The irony is compounded by the play's treatment of actual sacrifice. The characters who genuinely give something up — [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]], who goes to prison for the suffrage cause; [[dora-delaney|Dora]], who is "ready to marry Bobby, if that will be any satisfaction" [004-088] — do not invoke the language of self-sacrifice. They simply act. It is the characters who are most self-interested — Bobby, the critics, the parents — who reach for the vocabulary of noble suffering.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's critique of self-sacrifice is part of his broader attack on the romantic idealization of suffering. Throughout his work, he insists that genuine morality consists in doing what is right because it is right, not in the performance of noble suffering. The "romantic luxury of self-sacrifice" is a form of moral vanity — the pleasure of seeing oneself as a tragic hero.

The Preface frames this critique in its opening lines: "Mere morality, or the substitution of custom for conscience was once accounted a shameful and cynical thing" [P01-001]. Self-sacrifice, as the respectable characters deploy it, is a form of "mere morality" — the substitution of social convention for genuine ethical reflection.

## Key Passages

- **The romantic luxury**: "No woman will deny herself the romantic luxury of self-sacrifice and forgiveness when they take the form of doing something agreeable" [003-041].
- **The strategic act**: "No doubt you'd prefer to make it look like an act of self-sacrifice for her sake on your part, or provoke her to break the engagement herself" [003-042].
- **The failure of both plans**: "Both plans have been tried repeatedly, but never with success, as far as my knowledge goes" [003-042].
- **Juggins on sincerity**: "Denmark Hill thinks that the higher you go in the social scale, the less sincerity is allowed; and that only tramps and riffraff are quite sincere. That's a mistake" [003-044].

## Historical Context

The ideal of feminine self-sacrifice was central to Victorian and Edwardian gender ideology. Women were expected to sacrifice their desires, ambitions, and independence for the sake of family, propriety, and male comfort. The "angel in the house" — Coventry Patmore's phrase for the ideal Victorian wife — was defined by her capacity for self-abnegation. Shaw, a lifelong feminist, saw this ideal as a form of ideological imprisonment. Juggins's dismissal of the "romantic luxury of self-sacrifice" is a direct assault on one of the period's most cherished moral pieties.
