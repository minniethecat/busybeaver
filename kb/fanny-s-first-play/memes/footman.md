## Role

The Footman appears only in the Induction, where he announces the arrival of the four critics — [[savoyard|Cecil Savoyard]], then Trotter, Vaughan, Gunn, and [[flawner-bannal|Flawner Bannal]] — at [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]]'s country house, Florence Towers. He has six speaking lines and no name beyond his function. Yet his presence is essential to the Induction's [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic architecture: he is the first servant in a play obsessed with servants, and his "grandiose Spanish livery" establishes the Count's aesthetic program before the Count himself appears.

## In the Play

The Footman is the first character to speak after the stage directions. He enters before the curtain — like a theatrical footman, he is literally a curtain-raiser — and announces Mr. [[savoyard|Cecil Savoyard]] [001-001]. His interaction with Savoyard is brief but efficient: he confirms the play's start time (nine o'clock), offers to telephone Savoyard's wife, and corrects himself when Savoyard mentions "my wife" rather than "Mrs. [[savoyard|Cecil Savoyard]]" [001-002]. This small correction hints at a competence that exceeds his ornamental function.

When the Count enters, the Footman withdraws — his work done. He reappears only once more, to announce the four critics en masse: "[[trotter|Mr. Trotter]], [[vaughan|Mr. Vaughan]], [[gunn|Mr. Gunn]], Mr. [[flawner-bannal|Flawner Bannal]]" [001-005]. After this, he disappears from the play entirely. The Induction's servant function is effectively performed; the inner play will develop the servant theme through [[juggins|Juggins]], who is everything the Footman is not — articulate, philosophically aware, and central to the [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic action.

The Footman's "grandiose Spanish livery" is a detail worth pausing over. [[count-odowda|The Count]], who prides himself on eighteenth-century taste and Venetian elegance, dresses his English footman in Spanish costume — a triple displacement (English servant, Italian setting, Spanish uniform) that epitomizes the Count's aesthetic eclecticism. The livery is a costume, and the Footman's function is entirely theatrical: he is an actor playing a servant, just as the critics are audience members playing judges.

## Character Analysis

The Footman has no character arc — he is a function, not a person. But Shaw's functional characters often carry thematic weight precisely because of their blankness. The Footman is the first instance of a pattern that runs through the entire play: the servant who knows more than his employer suspects. He corrects Savoyard's domestic arrangements; he manages the Count's theatrical machinery; he performs his role with a competence that the aristocrats and critics conspicuously lack.

Shaw's stage directions describe the Footman entering "before the curtain, on its O.P. side" — opposite prompt, the stage-right entrance traditionally associated with servants and minor characters. This theatrical self-consciousness (a footman entering from the footman's side) is typical of Shaw's frame-device: the Induction is not just a play-within-a-play but a play about the conventions of playing.

## Key Passages

- **The entrance**: "A footman in grandiose Spanish livery enters before the curtain, on its O.P. side" — establishing the theatrical frame [001-001].
- **The correction**: When Savoyard says "my wife," the Footman asks "Mrs. [[savoyard|Cecil Savoyard]], sir?" — and is corrected to "Mrs. Tinkler," the Savoyards' domestic name for their cook [001-002].
- **The announcement**: "[[trotter|Mr. Trotter]], [[vaughan|Mr. Vaughan]], [[gunn|Mr. Gunn]], Mr. [[flawner-bannal|Flawner Bannal]]" — the ceremonial naming of the four critics [001-005].
- **The withdrawal**: "He withdraws" — the Footman's exit, his function complete, the play proper about to begin [001-005].
