## Definition

The stage is the central metaphor of *Fanny's First Play* — not merely the physical platform on which the inner play is performed but a figure for all the performances that constitute social life. The Induction takes place on a stage within a stage: "the end of a saloon … curtained off to form a stage" (001-001). The critics sit in the audience, judging the play. And the play they judge is itself about the performance of [[respectability]] in everyday life. At every level, Shaw asks: what is real, and what is performance?

## The Physical Stage

The stage at Florence Towers is an amateur construction — a curtain hung in a saloon. Its modesty is deliberate: it is not the professional stage of the [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] but a domestic space transformed. The [[count-odowda|Count]]'s horror at the idea of a modern-dress production — "No costumes! Do you mean to say it is a modern play?" (001-041) — reflects his understanding of the stage as a place of escape from modernity, not confrontation with it.

## The Stage as Social Metaphor

Shaw extends the stage metaphor to social life itself. The Gilbeys and Knoxes are performers in the drama of respectability: they play their parts — the concerned father, the anxious mother, the dutiful son — with the precision of experienced actors. When [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] breaks character and tells the truth, she shatters the performance. The play's argument is that most social life is theatre, and that moral growth requires stepping off the stage.

## The Critics' Stage

The Induction gives us a second stage: the critics' arena. [[trotter|Trotter]], [[vaughan|Vaughan]], [[gunn|Gunn]], and [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]] are themselves performers, each playing his assigned role in the drama of critical judgment. Their verdict on the play is partly a verdict on themselves — a revelation of their own prejudices, tastes, and limitations. The stage, in this sense, is a mirror: what you see in it reveals who you are.

## Key Passages

- **The curtained saloon**: "The end of a saloon … has been curtained off to form a stage for a private theatrical performance" (001-001).
- **The Count's shock**: "No costumes! Do you mean to say it is a modern play?" — the stage as escape vs. confrontation (001-041).
- **"A stage cliché"**: Trotter's dismissal of Cambridge as "a stage cliché" — the stage as source of exhausted conventions (001-121).
