## Definition

Forgiveness in *Fanny's First Play* is not the simple virtue it appears to be. The play's characters forgive each other constantly — or claim to — but these acts of forgiveness are often exercises of power disguised as acts of grace. The parent who "forgives" a child's transgression may be using forgiveness to reassert control. The critic who "forgives" the playwright may be displaying condescension rather than generosity. Shaw's treatment of forgiveness is characteristically double-edged: it can be an act of liberation or an act of domination.

## In the Inner Play

The Gilbey and Knox households are soaked in forgiveness — or what passes for it. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]]'s rage at [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]]'s disappearance quickly gives way to a more complicated negotiation: he will forgive the boy if the boy shows proper contrition. But Bobby's refusal to be ashamed — his insistence that his arrest was not a moral catastrophe — frustrates this transaction. Forgiveness requires a guilty party, and Bobby will not play that role.

[[knox|Joseph Knox]]'s forgiveness is even more problematic. He forgives [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] for her night in prison — but his forgiveness is conditional on her resuming the role of dutiful daughter. Margaret's rejection of this condition is the play's moral climax: she does not want forgiveness on those terms. She wants [[freedom]].

## The Preface

Shaw's Preface extends the theme. He asks the critics he has "lampooned" to forgive him — and then notes that "Mr. Trotter forgave me beforehand" (P01-002). The pre-emptive forgiveness of the satirized critic becomes a joke at the critic's expense: Trotter's forgiveness is a claim to superiority that the play itself undermines.

## Key Passages

- **Shaw's request**: "The critics whom I have lampooned … will forgive me: in fact Mr. Trotter forgave me beforehand" (P01-002).
- **Gilbey's negotiation**: The father's conditional forgiveness (002-103).
