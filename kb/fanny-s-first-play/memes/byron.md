## Definition

Byron appears in *Fanny's First Play* as a cultural touchstone — the embodiment of romantic rebellion that Shaw both invokes and ironizes. The young characters' defiance of their parents is not Byronic heroism but something more domestic, more comic, and ultimately more genuinely liberating.

## In the Play

Byron is mentioned in the Induction's critical debate, where his name functions as shorthand for a certain kind of Romantic artistic rebellion. The play's own rebels — [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]], [[dora-delaney|Dora]] — are pointedly un-Byronic: they are shopkeepers' children, not aristocratic outcasts; their rebellion takes the form of dancing and prison rather than Alpine exile.

Shaw's use of Byron reflects his broader argument about Romanticism: that its grand gestures conceal an evasion of real social problems. The play's young characters achieve more genuine freedom than any Byronic hero, precisely because their rebellion is practical, comic, and grounded in the realities of Edwardian domestic life.

## Key Passages

- **Byron referenced**: In the Induction's discussion of literary tradition (001).
- **The un-Byronic rebels**: Margaret, Bobby, and Dora as anti-romantic heroes.
