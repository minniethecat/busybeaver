## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, class is the invisible architecture that shapes every character's choices. The [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s and Knoxes are lower-middle-class — shopkeepers and clerks who inhabit the "[[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]]," a geographical metaphor for their social position: on the circumference of gentility, neither in the center nor entirely outside. Class anxiety drives their obsession with respectability, their horror at [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]], and their complicated relationship with the footman [[juggins|Juggins]], who may be the brother of a duke.

## In the Play

The [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]] address is the play's class signifier. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] anxiously confirms that "a great many most respectable people live in the [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]]" when [[juggins|Juggins]] mentions it (002-033). The address locates the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s precisely: respectable but not grand, comfortable but not wealthy, genteel but not aristocratic.

[[juggins|Juggins]] is the class puzzle at the play's heart. A footman — the lowest member of the household hierarchy — he speaks with greater moral authority than his employers. The revelation that he may be the "brother of a duke" (004-429) inverts the class pyramid: the servant is an aristocrat in disguise, the shopkeepers are pretenders to gentility.

The Frenchman Duvallet provides an outsider's perspective on English class. His observation that "nowhere else in the world but in [[england|England]]" could he have met a "charming young lady of perfect respectability" at a variety theatre captures the English talent for maintaining class distinctions even in spaces of popular entertainment (004-402).

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a socialist who believed class was a moral as well as economic category. His Fabian politics informed his treatment of class as a system of [[hypocrisy|pretense]] — the middle class pretending to be more genteel than it is, the aristocracy pretending to deserve its privileges, and everyone colluding in the fiction that social position reflects moral worth.

The play's treatment of [[juggins|Juggins]] is Shaw's class argument in [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic form. The footman is the most intelligent, the most articulate, and the most morally serious character in the play. His superiority to his employers is an argument for merit over birth — and his possible aristocratic origins are a Shawian joke: perhaps merit and birth coincide after all, but only when the aristocrat is disguised as a servant.

## Key Passages

- **The [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]]**: "Is that a respectable address, [[juggins|Juggins]]?" — [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s class anxiety (002-032).
- **Respectable people**: "A great many most respectable people live in the [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]]" — [[juggins|Juggins]]'s ironic reassurance (002-033).
- **The duke's brother**: "Yes: the brother of a duke: that's what he is" — Knox's recognition of [[juggins|Juggins]] (004-429).
- **The variety theatre respectability**: Duvallet marvels at meeting "a charming young lady of perfect respectability" at a variety theatre — an English class paradox (004-402).
- **Social outcast**: [[gilbey|Gilbey]]'s horror: "My boy is to marry this woman and be a social outcast!" — class endogamy enforced (004-420).

## Historical Context

Edwardian Britain was a society in class transition. The Victorian tripartite structure — aristocracy, middle class, working class — was being complicated by the rise of the lower-middle class (clerks, shopkeepers, the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s and Knoxes), the expansion of the professions, and the political challenge of organized labor. The "[[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]]" class — respectable, anxious, precarious — was a distinct Edwardian phenomenon.

The Liberal government's social reforms (old-age pensions, national insurance) were beginning to address class inequality, but the cultural hierarchy remained intact. Shaw's satire targets precisely this cultural dimension — not economic exploitation (he leaves that for other plays) but the psychological damage of class anxiety.
