## Role
Mrs. Gilbey is the inner play's embodiment of respectable domestic anxiety. She polices the boundaries of her household with the vigilance of a woman who knows that respectability is precarious — one wrong address, one scandal, and the family falls. Her discovery in Act IV that respectability is not a necessity but a choice is the play's most poignant moment of parental awakening.

## In the Play
Mrs. Gilbey dominates the domestic scenes of Act II, where she receives the returned Bobby and the visiting [[dora-delaney|Dora]] with a mixture of maternal concern and class panic. Her anxiety about the [[west-circular-road|West Circular Road]] address — "Is that a respectable address, [[juggins|Juggins]]?" (002-032) — is both comic and revealing. In Act IV, she participates in the marriage negotiations with dwindling authority, culminating in her confession: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" (004-253).

## Character Analysis
Mrs. Gilbey is not a villain — she is a victim of the moral system she enforces. Her life has been organized around the maintenance of appearances, and the discovery that appearances are optional is both liberating and terrifying. Shaw treats her with more sympathy than her husband, perhaps because her anxiety is rooted in genuine love for her son.
