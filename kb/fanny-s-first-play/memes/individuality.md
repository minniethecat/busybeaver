## Definition

Individuality is the positive value for which Shaw's young characters are fighting. Against the claims of [[society]], [[tradition]], [[family]], and [[respectability]], the play asserts the primacy of the individual conscience. To be an individual is to have one's own moral judgment — not borrowed from parents, not imposed by neighbours, not dictated by convention. The play's young characters are individuals in this sense, however imperfectly; their parents are not.

## In the Play

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]] is the play's most articulate spokesperson for individuality. Her journey from "good girl" to "free woman" is a journey from conformity to selfhood. "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free," she tells her mother. "And when I got free I was what most people call bad" (003-088). The categories "good" and "bad" are revealed as social constructs: what matters is not goodness but selfhood.

[[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]]'s individuality is less articulate but no less real. He refuses to perform penitence for his parents. He will not be shamed. His quiet defiance is a form of self-assertion that his father's bluster cannot overwhelm.

[[dora-delaney|Dora]] is individuality without ideology. She is simply herself — cheerful, frank, unsentimental — and her selfhood is a rebuke to the elaborate performances of the respectable world. She does not argue for individuality; she embodies it.

## In the Induction

[[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s individuality is intellectual. She has been educated at [[cambridge-university|Cambridge]], exposed to progressive ideas, and given the confidence to manage her own theatrical production. Her anonymous play is an assertion of individuality: a demand to be judged on her merits, not her name. Yet her individuality is constrained — by her father, by her critics, by the conventions of her class. Her struggle to be herself is the Induction's unspoken drama.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's commitment to individuality was absolute. His plays are populated by characters who refuse to be types — who insist on their own particularity against the flattening forces of convention. *Fanny's First Play* extends this commitment to the next generation: it shows young people discovering that they are individuals, not merely the products of their upbringing.

## Key Passages

- **Margaret's self-discovery**: "When I was free I was what most people call bad" — individuality beyond good and evil (003-088).
- **Fanny's experiment**: The anonymous play as an assertion of individual merit (P01-003).
