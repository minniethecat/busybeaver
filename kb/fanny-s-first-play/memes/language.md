## Definition

Language in *Fanny's First Play* is never neutral. Every character's speech marks their class position, their education, their moral framework. Shaw, the great phonetician and language reformer, uses dialect, idiolect, and register with precision to expose the social determinations of thought. What you can say — and how you can say it — determines what you can think.

## In the Play

The Count's objection to "the Cockney twang" is a class judgment disguised as an aesthetic one: "My ears are offended by the Cockney twang: I keep out of hearing of it and speak and listen to Italian" (001-072). Language here is a class filter: the Count retreats from English into Italian as he retreats from modernity into the eighteenth century.

Margaret's prison experience transforms her language. She tells her mother: "I heard words that I didn't even know that I knew, coming out of my mouth just as if somebody else had spoken them" (003-467). The prison releases a language Margaret didn't know she possessed — the language of anger, defiance, and authentic self-expression that respectability had suppressed.

Mrs. Knox's objection to Margaret's speech is itself a linguistic act of class positioning: "I can't bear to hear you talking wickedly. I can bear to hear the children of this world talking vainly and foolishly in the language of this world. But when I hear you justifying your wickedness in the words of grace, it's too horrible: it sounds like the devil making fun of religion" (003-492). Mrs. Knox is troubled not by what Margaret says but by the register in which she says it — the "words of grace" used to justify "wickedness."

Duvallet's French-accented English is a running joke that also carries thematic weight. His linguistic foreignness enables him to say things no English character could say — his critique of English "freedom" and French "tyranny" of the family (004-919) is credible precisely because he is an outsider.
