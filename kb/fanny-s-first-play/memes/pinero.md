## Role

Sir Arthur Wing Pinero (1855–1934) is a historically real figure referenced five times in the Epilogue of *Fanny's First Play*. He never appears on stage, but his name is central to the play's satire of dramatic criticism. The critics debate whether Fanny's play could be by Pinero — invoking him as a marker of commercial success, theatrical craft, and a particular kind of well-made play that Shaw's own work was reacting against.

## In the Play

Pinero is first mentioned when Bannal protests the anonymity of the play: "Suppose you had to write about a play by Pinero and one by Jones! Would you say exactly the same thing about them?" [005-020]. His point is that critical evaluation depends on authorial identity, not the work itself.

Later, Vaughan — seeking to console Fanny after the critics have savaged her play — lies: "I was just saying that it must have been written by Pinero" [005-030]. Fanny is "enormously flattered" by this, though Trotter undercuts the compliment: "I thought Pinero was much too popular for the Cambridge Fabian Society" [005-030].

Bannal also uses Pinero in his attempt to place the play: "Why are you so down on Pinero? And what about that touch that Gunn spotted? the Frenchman's long speech. I believe it's Shaw" [005-027].

## Character Analysis

Shaw's use of Pinero is layered. On one level, Pinero represents the commercially successful "well-made play" tradition — the formulaic drama that Shaw and the new dramatists sought to displace. On another level, Shaw's treatment of Pinero in the text is more respectful than one might expect from a satirist. Pinero is not lampooned as the critics are; rather, his name is used by the critics as a lazy shorthand, which exposes their limitations, not his.

Pinero's presence in the Epilogue serves Shaw's meta-theatrical argument: that criticism is often less about aesthetic judgment than about social positioning. Knowing the author's name tells the critic what to think; without it, they are lost.

## Key Passages

- **Bannal on authorial identity**: "Suppose you had to write about a play by Pinero and one by Jones! Would you say exactly the same thing about them?" [005-020].
- **Vaughan's consolation**: "I was just saying that it must have been written by Pinero. Didn't I, Gunn?" [005-030].
- **Trotter's qualification**: "I thought Pinero was much too popular for the Cambridge Fabian Society" [005-030].
- **Bannal's critical calculus**: "Why are you so down on Pinero?" [005-027].
- **Bannal on genre classification**: "Then how could you write about them until you knew which was Pinero and which was Jones?" [005-021].

## Historical Context

At the time of *Fanny's First Play* (1911), Pinero was one of the most successful playwrights in England. His best-known works — *The Second Mrs Tanqueray* (1893), *Trelawny of the "Wells"* (1898), *The Gay Lord Quex* (1899) — combined technical skill with a willingness to address social issues, albeit within the conventions of the well-made play. Shaw respected Pinero's craftsmanship while fundamentally disagreeing with his dramaturgy. The two represented competing visions of what theatre could be: Pinero's polished problem plays versus Shaw's dialectical drama of ideas.
