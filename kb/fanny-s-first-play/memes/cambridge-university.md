## Setting

[[cambridge-university|Cambridge University]] never appears on stage in [[fanny-odowda|Fanny's First Play]], yet it is the play's most consequential location. It is the site of [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]]'s transformation — the place where the Count's carefully cultivated Venetian daughter encountered the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]], modern intellectual life, and a world her father had spent two decades excluding. Without [[education|Cambridge]], there is no play.

## The Count's Miscalculation

[[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] sent Fanny to [[education|Cambridge]] believing it would preserve his eighteenth-century aesthetic paradise. His reasoning reveals everything about his misunderstanding of both the institution and his daughter:

> Cambridge was my own University; and though of course there were no women there in my time, I felt confident that if the atmosphere of the eighteenth century still existed anywhere in England, it would be at Cambridge. (001-018)

The irony is layered. [[education|Cambridge]] had no women in the Count's time because it *excluded* them — the "eighteenth-century atmosphere" he romanticizes was one of male privilege and institutional conservatism. By 1911, [[education|Cambridge]] was a crucible of intellectual and social change, precisely because women's presence was transforming its ancient structures.

## Fanny's Cambridge

Fanny's two years at [[education|Cambridge]] gave her the analytical tools she uses to devastating effect on the critics. Her classification system — dividing people into taxonomic categories like the "I must says" — is explicitly attributed to the [[fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]]:

> That's one of our classifications in the Cambridge Fabian Society. We classify our aunts into different sorts. And one of the sorts is the "I must says." (001-020)

This is simultaneously a parody of Fabian taxonomy and a demonstration of its intellectual power. Fanny can see through [[trotter|Mr. Trotter]] because she has a systematic framework for understanding him — a framework [[education|Cambridge]] gave her:

> we've discussed you thoroughly at Cambridge; and you've never discussed yourself, have you? (001-020)

## Trotter's Counter-Attack

Trotter, threatened by Fanny's analytical clarity, responds with the misogyny of the period dressed as wit:

> If you're going to be a charming healthy young English girl, you may coax me. If you're going to be an unsexed Cambridge Fabian virago, I'll treat you as my intellectual equal, as I would treat a man. (001-022)

The phrase "unsexed [[education|Cambridge]] Fabian virago" condenses Edwardian anxieties about women's higher education into four words. Trotter's threat — to treat her "as my intellectual equal" — is meant as a deterrent, but Fanny has already demonstrated that she is more than his equal. His condescension is his defense.

## Cambridge vs. Oxford

The play's single reference to [[education|Oxford]] reinforces [[education|Cambridge]]'s role as the site of intellectual transformation. Trotter dismisses [[education|Cambridge]] education with "Pooh! [[education|Cambridge]]! If you had been educated at Oxford, you would know that the definition of a play has been settled exactly and scientifically for two thousand two hundred and sixty years" — invoking Aristotle as though the *Poetics* settled the question (001-024). The joke is on Trotter: his Oxonian certainty about [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic form is precisely what blinds him to Fanny's innovative play, while her [[education|Cambridge]] education has equipped her to create something genuinely new.

## Historical Context

By 1911, [[education|Cambridge]] was deep in the transformation that would eventually produce the modern [[education|university]]. Girton College (founded 1869) and Newnham College (founded 1871) had established women's presence, though full degree rights would not come until 1948. The [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] had an active [[education|Cambridge]] branch that attracted the intellectually adventurous young — exactly the kind of student Fanny represents.

Shaw himself had no [[education|university]] education, which may account for the play's ambivalent treatment of [[education|Cambridge]]: it is simultaneously the source of Fanny's [[freedom|liberation]] and the target of Trotter's (and Shaw's) satire of academic pretension.
