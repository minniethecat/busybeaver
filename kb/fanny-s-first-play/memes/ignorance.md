## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, "ignorance" is neither innocence nor simple lack of information. It is the active, willed refusal to know — the strategy by which the older generation preserves its respectability by not seeing what its children are doing, not asking what its children have done, and not acknowledging what its children have become. Shaw treats this ignorance not as a forgivable limitation but as a profound moral failure. The parents who do not know their children are not unlucky; they are complicit in their own blindness.

The word "ignorant" appears four times in the play, most strikingly when Trotter patronisingly tells Fanny: "I'm delighted to find there's something you don't know; and I shan't spoil you by dispelling an ignorance which, in my opinion, is highly becoming to your age and sex" [001-148]. This exchange crystallises the play's critique: ignorance is a tool of power, imposed by the old on the young and by men on women, dressed up as protection but functioning as control.

## In the Play

### The Count's Willful Ignorance

Count O'Dowda is the play's most elaborate study in self-imposed ignorance. He has constructed a life designed to exclude knowledge of the modern world: "I find England ugly and Philistine. Well, I don't live in it. I find modern houses ugly. I don't live in them" [001-023]. He has raised his daughter in the belief that she has "never seen an ugly sight or heard an ugly sound" [001-030], and he assumes her play will be "a Louis Quatorze ballet painted by Watteau" [001-030].

The Count's ignorance is aesthetic and moral. He "does not know of the existence" of English pantomime [001-036]; he is "rather a stranger in your world" [001-020]; he needs Savoyard to brief him on the critics because he is "rather out of it in England" [001-046]. When confronted with the reality of his daughter's play, he is shattered: "I could never have believed it" [005-003]. His ignorance has been comprehensive, deliberate, and — the play argues — culpable.

The Count's final line — "She will never return to Venice. I feel now as I felt when the Campanile fell" [005-083] — captures the catastrophic nature of his awakening. The Campanile of St. Mark's, which collapsed in 1902, was a symbol of Venetian stability and beauty. Its sudden fall is Shaw's metaphor for the collapse of the Count's carefully constructed ignorance.

### The Middle-Class Parents' Ignorance

Mr. and Mrs. Gilbey and Mr. and Mrs. Knox embody the ordinary ignorance of respectable parents. Mr. Gilbey does not know where his son has been for a fortnight: "he's been lost these fourteen days — and not a word have we heard of him since" [002-053]. Mrs. Gilbey has been told Bobby is "in Brighton for the sea air" [002-111] — a convenient fiction that she accepts without question. When Dora arrives to tell them the truth, Mr. Gilbey's response is not gratitude for information but anger at the messenger: "Where's my son? What's happened to my son? Will you tell me that, and stop cackling about your squiffer?" [002-075].

The Knoxes are equally ignorant. Mrs. Knox does not know that Margaret was not at her aunt's; she accepted the story her daughter told her. When the truth emerges — "Holloway Gaol!" — her response is disbelief: "Margaret! Who accused you of such a thing?" [003-063]. The parents' ignorance is sustained by a network of polite fictions, convenient lies, and willed blindness.

Mrs. Knox's lament — "It only ends in our not knowing what to believe" [002-111] — is the epistemological confession of the respectable class. They have built a world in which they cannot distinguish truth from falsehood because they have systematically avoided knowing uncomfortable truths.

### The Children's Knowledge

The play contrasts the parents' ignorance with the children's hard-won knowledge. Margaret has "lived a lot" since she last saw Bobby [003-023]; Bobby has been to prison and learned "about life" [003-022]; Dora has the practical knowledge of the streets. The young characters know things their parents cannot imagine — and, crucially, they know things about themselves.

Margaret's declaration — "I don't know what sort of person you really are, or what sort of person father really is" [003-090] — inverts the expected relationship. The child, through experience, has acquired more self-knowledge than her parents possess. The parents' ignorance is not merely about their children but about themselves.

### Trotter and the Ignorance of the Young

Trotter's comment to Fanny — "I'm delighted to find there's something you don't know; and I shan't spoil you by dispelling an ignorance which, in my opinion, is highly becoming to your age and sex" [001-148] — is the play's most explicit statement of the ideology of ignorance. Trotter believes that ignorance is appropriate and even attractive in young women. Knowledge, in this view, is a masculine prerogative; female ignorance is a form of charm.

Fanny's response — revealing that she knows far more about Trotter than he knows about himself — demolishes this ideology. "I'm much more conscious of what you really are than you are yourself, because we've discussed you thoroughly at Cambridge; and you've never discussed yourself, have you?" [001-155]. The young woman whom Trotter patronised as ignorant possesses analytical tools he lacks. The ignorance was his all along.

### Fanny's Subversion

Fanny plays with the expectation of female ignorance throughout the Induction. She pretends not to know "who the Stagirite was" [001-150] — a reference to Aristotle that Trotter assumes she should know. Her performance of ignorance is strategic: it allows her to manage Trotter's expectations while concealing the full extent of her knowledge. When she finally reveals herself — as author, as suffragette, as someone who has "seen a good deal of life since I came to England" [001-153] — the accumulated effect is devastating to Trotter's assumptions.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface frames the entire play as an intervention against ignorance. The "respectable middle class people" are "all as dead as mutton" [P01-001] precisely because they do not know — do not know good and evil, do not know courage and cowardice, do not know themselves. The play's lesson is that ignorance is not a refuge but a tomb.

Shaw offers "young people in our suburbs the desperate advice: Do something that will get you into trouble" [P01-001] — which is to say, do something that will force you to know. Knowledge, for Shaw, comes through experience, and experience often comes through transgression. The alternative — the ignorance preserved by respectability — is a kind of living death.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period was marked by a growing generational divide in knowledge and experience. The children of the Victorian middle class were better educated, more mobile, and more exposed to new ideas than their parents had been. Cambridge, where Fanny studies, had become a centre of progressive thought — the Fabian Society, the suffrage movement, the "New Drama." The parents of *Fanny's First Play* represent a generation that had not kept pace with these changes and actively resisted knowing about them.

The specific site of the Count's ignorance — Venice — is significant. The British upper classes had long used Italy as a refuge from English modernity. The Grand Tour, the expatriate communities of Florence and Venice, the cult of Renaissance art — these were strategies for avoiding engagement with industrial England. The Count's Venetian palace is not merely a home but a fortress against knowledge.

The play's gender dimension — Trotter's belief that ignorance is "becoming" to young women — reflects the Edwardian ideology of female innocence. The "angel in the house" was expected to be ignorant of worldly matters; knowledge was a threat to feminine purity. Shaw's play systematically destroys this ideology, showing that the "innocent" young woman (Fanny) knows more than the worldly critic (Trotter), and that the "disgraced" young woman (Margaret) has achieved a moral clarity her ignorant parents lack.
