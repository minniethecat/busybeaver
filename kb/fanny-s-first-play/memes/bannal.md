## Definition

Bannal is the critical philosophy embodied by [[flawner-bannal|Flawner Bannal]], the twenty-year-old critic in Shaw's Induction to *Fanny's First Play*. It represents the reduction of artistic judgment to a single question — "Who's it by?" — and the conviction that a play's quality is wholly determined by its author's established reputation. Bannal's approach is the antithesis of genuine critical engagement: he refuses to evaluate the work itself, demanding instead the external authority of a known name before he can "place the play for you to a hair's breadth" [001-999].

## Core Tenets

Bannal's critical method rests on three principles, each revealed through his dialogue in the Induction:

1. **[[authorship|Authorship]] Determinism** — The belief that a play's merit is entirely a function of its author's identity: "If it's by a good author, it's a good play, naturally. That stands to reason" [001-999]. This eliminates the need for actual analysis of the work.

2. **Genre [[class|Class]]ification as Prerequisite** — Before offering any opinion, Bannal demands to know the play's genre category: "Is it a comedy or a tragedy? Is it a farce or a melo[[art-and-theatre|drama]]? Is it repertory theatre tosh, or really straight paying stuff?" [001-995]. Without classification, judgment is impossible.

3. **Market Validation** — Bannal's authority derives not from expertise but from his claimed identity as the representative consumer: "Bannal really represents the British playgoer. When he likes a thing, you may take your oath there are a hundred thousand people in [[london|London]] that'll like it" [001-117].

> Savoyard: Flawner Bannal's your man. Bannal really represents the British playgoer. When he likes a thing, you may take your oath there are a hundred thousand people in London that'll like it if they can only be got to know about it. Besides, Bannal's knowledge of the theatre is an inside knowledge. We know him; and he knows us. He knows the ropes: he knows his way about: he knows what he's talking about.
> [001-117]

## The Induction Scene

Bannal appears as one of four critics invited to a private performance of *Fanny's First Play*. He is the youngest, aged twenty, and is described as "obviously one of those unemployables of the business class who manage to pick up a living by a sort of courage which gives him cheerfulness, conviviality, and bounce" [001-127]. Unlike the other critics — Trotter the aesthete, Vaughan the moralist, and Gunn the young intellectual — Bannal represents the commercial, anti-intellectual perspective.

When [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] requests his verdict on the play, following the court-martial custom of the youngest speaking first, Bannal's immediate response is: "Well, who's it by?" [001-989]. This single question encapsulates his entire critical philosophy.

> Bannal: Well, who's it by?
>
> The Count: I do not know.
>
> Bannal: You don't expect me to know what to say about a play when I don't know who the author is, do you?
> [001-989–991]

## Satirical Function

Shaw uses Bannal to satirize several targets simultaneously:

- **The Commercial Critic** — Bannal embodies the newspaper critic who judges by reputation rather than content, reflecting Shaw's long-standing critique of the critical establishment.

- **The British Playgoer** — Savoyard explicitly positions Bannal as the representative audience member, suggesting that the average consumer of art makes judgments on the same superficial basis.

- **The Cult of [[authorship|Authorship]]** — Bannal's philosophy exposes the absurdity of judging works by their author's name rather than their intrinsic qualities, a practice Shaw frequently attacked in his critical writings.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Bannal stands in opposition to genuine critical engagement, represented in the play by Trotter's more considered (though equally flawed) aesthetic judgments. It relates to [[philistinism]] as its practical manifestation in the theatre world, and to [[theatre-criticism]] as the degenerate form of that practice. Bannal's insistence that a play cannot be judged without knowing its author parallels the [[censorship-and-art]] debate about who has the authority to evaluate artistic works.
