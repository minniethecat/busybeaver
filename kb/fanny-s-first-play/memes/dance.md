## Definition

Dance in *Fanny's First Play* is the physical expression of freedom. When [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] escapes her respectable world, she does so through dance — first watching a dancer on stage, then dancing herself at a public hall. Dance represents everything the Gilbey-Knox world suppresses: bodily joy, spontaneity, connection with strangers, pleasure without propriety's permission.

## In the Play

Margaret's account of her night of liberation is structured around dance. At the theatre, she sees "one of them was the most beautiful dancer I ever saw" and longs "to join the people on the stage and dance with them" (003-477). Dance here is an image of a fuller, more vital life — a life beyond the "two evenings pleasure every week" of her parents' world.

The dance hall becomes the site of Margaret's transformation. "We went to a place where there was a band in a gallery and the floor cleared for dancing. Very few people danced: the women only wanted to show off their dresses; but we danced and danced until a lot of them joined in" (003-477). Margaret and Duvallet's dancing is contagious — it draws others in, breaking down the self-consciousness that keeps the other women frozen in display.

When Margaret describes her actions to her parents, she frames dance as innocence itself: "I went to see a lovely dance. I picked up a nice man and went to have a dance myself. I can't imagine anything more innocent and more happy" (004-619). Shaw's point is that Margaret is right — her dancing was innocent — and that it is her parents' framework that makes it seem otherwise.

Duvallet, from his French perspective, sees the dance as evidence of English freedom: "Nowhere else in the world but in England could I have met at a charming young lady of perfect respectability, and enjoyed a dance with her at a public dancing saloon" (004-919).
