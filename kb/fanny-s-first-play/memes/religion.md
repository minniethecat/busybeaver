## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, religion functions primarily as a social credential — a marker of respectability rather than a source of genuine moral guidance. Characters invoke the Church, prayer, and salvation not as matters of faith but as props in the performance of middle-class virtue. Shaw's treatment is characteristically irreverent: religious observance is exposed as empty ritual, and those who claim piety are revealed to lack "real religion."

## In the Play

The [[gilbey|Gilbey]] household, that temple of respectability, is also a site of ostentatious religiosity. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] is scandalized by any breach of the Sabbath. Bobby's [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] is a religious as well as social crisis — it calls into question whether the family's churchgoing has produced any real moral formation.

The most devastating critique comes in Act IV when Knox, the respectable shopkeeper, confesses: "We find out then that with all our respectability and piety, we've no real religion and no way of knowing what is right" (004-308). This is the play's theological climax — the admission that Edwardian Christianity has failed to equip its adherents for genuine moral discernment.

Bobby's rhetorical challenge earlier in the play — "Where's the Government? Where's the Church? Where's respectability and right reason?" (002-117) — frames religion as one of three failed institutions alongside government and social convention.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a committed atheist and secularist whose critiques of organized religion run through his entire oeuvre. The Preface to *Fanny's First Play* sets up the distinction between "mere morality" — the "substitution of custom for [[morality|conscience]]" — and genuine ethical reflection (P01-001). This distinction maps directly onto the play's treatment of religion: the characters have substituted church attendance for [[morality|conscience]].

The theatre critics in the Induction, who represent cultural gatekeeping, also embody a kind of religious orthodoxy — they enforce received opinion with the dogmatism of inquisitors. Shaw's lampoon of them extends his critique of institutional authority from the church to the theatre.

## Key Passages

- **The admission of spiritual bankruptcy**: "We find out then that with all our respectability and piety, we've no real religion" — Knox (004-308).
- **The institutional challenge**: "Where's the Government? Where's the Church? Where's respectability and right reason?" — Bobby (002-117).
- **Custom versus [[morality|conscience]]**: Shaw's Preface frames the entire play as a lesson in the inadequacy of "mere morality, or the substitution of custom for [[morality|conscience]]" (P01-001).
- **The Sabbath**: [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s horror at gaiety in a "respectable household" reflects the fusion of religious observance with social propriety (002-096).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian era saw the continuing decline of organized religion's authority in British public life. Church attendance was falling, particularly among the working class. The Nonconformist [[morality|conscience]], which had been a powerful political force in Victorian Britain, was losing its grip. Biblical criticism, Darwinism, and the rise of secular philosophies (including Shaw's own Fabian socialism) all challenged traditional Christianity.

The [[church-of-england|Church of England]] remained the established church, but its social role was increasingly ceremonial. For the [[class|middle class]], church attendance functioned as a marker of respectability rather than an expression of conviction — exactly the syndrome Shaw satirizes.
