## Overview

The term "Philistine" — popularised in English cultural criticism by Matthew Arnold in *Culture and Anarchy* (1869) — designates the person indifferent or hostile to art, intellect, and beauty, content with material comfort and moral convention. In *Fanny's First Play*, Shaw deploys the concept as a diagnostic instrument, using it to anatomise the cultural complacency of Edwardian [[england|England]]. But Shaw's treatment of Philistinism is characteristically dialectical: the play exposes not only the Philistinism of the suburban [[class|middle class]] but also the sterile aestheticism that defines itself purely in opposition to it.

## The Count's Diagnosis: "England ugly and Philistine"

[[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] delivers the play's most explicit indictment of English Philistinism in the Induction:

> [001-023] I find England ugly and Philistine. Well, I don't live in it. I find modern houses ugly. I don't live in them: I have a palace on the grand canal. I find modern clothes prosaic. I don't wear them, except, of course, in the street. My ears are offended by the Cockney twang: I keep out of hearing of it and speak and listen to Italian. I find Beethoven's music coarse and restless, and Wagner's senseless and detestable. I do not listen to them. I listen to Cimarosa, to Pergolesi, to Gluck and Mozart. Nothing simpler, sir.

The diagnosis is comprehensive: architecture, dress, language, music — every sensory domain is colonised by ugliness. [[england|England]] is "befouled with industrialism" [001-021]; its millionaires are "wretched vulgar industrial" types spending fortunes on "the amusements of billiard markers" [001-024]. [[count-odowda|The Count]]'s solution — withdrawal to [[venice|Venice]], to the eighteenth century, to the aesthetic paradise of the Grand Canal — is the logic of the connoisseur: if the world is Philistine, one simply declines to inhabit it.

## The Limits of Aesthetic Withdrawal

Shaw's satire, however, cuts both ways. [[count-odowda|The Count]]'s aestheticism is itself a form of Philistinism — a refusal to engage with the world as it is, a retreat into an idealised past that is as culturally sterile as the modernity it flees. Savoyard's deflating response — "Some of us must live in [[england|England]], you know, just to keep the place going" [001-030] — punctures the Count's posture with practical English common sense.

[[count-odowda|The Count]]'s inability to recognise his daughter's play as art — his assumption that it will be "a Louis Quatorze ballet painted by Watteau" [001-042] with a "dainty Harlequin" and "picturesque Pantaloon" — reveals the narrowness of his aesthetic. His Philistinism is the Philistinism of taste: the conviction that art ended in 1800, that Beethoven is "coarse," that the modern is definitionally ugly. When Fanny's play turns out to be a work of social realism depicting arrest, police brutality, and moral [[freedom|liberation]], the Count's horror is not moral but aesthetic: "tomorrow I start for [[venice|Venice]], never to return" [005-043].

## The Knox-Gilbey World: Respectable Philistinism

The inner play anatomises a different species of Philistinism: the moral and cultural complacency of the suburban [[class|middle class]]. The [[gilbey|Gilbey]] household — [[denmark-hill|Denmark Hill]], the shop on the ground floor, the "elderly lady" reading the newspaper at breakfast — is Shaw's portrait of English respectability as cultural vacuum. Mr. [[gilbey|Gilbey]]'s horror at [[dora-delaney|Dora Delaney]] — "Creatures like you that take advantage of a child's innocence ought to be whipped through the streets" [002-050] — is Philistinism expressed as moral panic: the inability to process any form of life that does not conform to suburban convention.

The Knox household presents a variant: the pious, plain-living, plain-thinking respectability that substitutes religious observance for cultural engagement. [[knox|Mr. Knox]]'s reaction to Duvallet — "A Frenchman! It only needed this" [003-014] — condenses national, cultural, and moral suspicion into four words.

## Dora and the Concertina: Philistinism Inverted

Shaw's most daring inversion of Philistine aesthetics comes through [[dora-delaney|Dora Delaney]]'s concertina:

> [002-051] Oh, of course: excuse my vulgarity: a concertina. There's one in a shop in Green Street, ivory inlaid, with gold keys and Russia leather bellows; and Bobby knew I hankered after it; but he couldn't afford it, poor lad.

[[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s self-conscious "excuse my vulgarity" is a satirical mirror: the concertina is vulgar only by the standards of the Count's aesthetic hierarchy (Cimarosa good, Beethoven coarse, concertina unthinkable). [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s frank delight in the material world — gold keys, Russia leather — is a kind of anti-Philistinism expressed through Philistine objects. She loves beauty; she simply loves it in forms the connoisseur cannot recognise.

## The Resolution: Art as Engagement

Fanny's play itself — the inner [[art-and-theatre|drama]] of [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]]'s [[freedom|liberation]] — is Shaw's answer to both the Count's aesthetic withdrawal and the Knox-[[gilbey|Gilbey]] moral Philistinism. The play confronts the audience with real experience: arrest, police violence, sexual [[truth|frankness]], moral growth. It refuses to prettify. It is, in the Count's horrified terms, a "modern play" — which is to say, a play that engages with the world rather than retreating from it.

The critics' post-mortem in the Epilogue — [[bannal|Bannal]]'s "I believe it's Shaw" [005-024], Vaughan's diagnosis of "the supposed hypocrisy of the Puritan [[class|middle class]] in [[england|England]]" [005-023] — completes the circle. The Philistines recognise themselves in the mirror and, in recognising, confirm the diagnosis.
