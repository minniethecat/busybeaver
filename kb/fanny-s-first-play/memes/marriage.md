## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, marriage is treated as a social institution under pressure — a contract that the younger generation approaches with ambivalence, the [[class|middle class]] treats as the cornerstone of respectability, and Shaw's satire exposes as riddled with hypocrisy. It is simultaneously a moral imperative ("marry her, sir, or behave like a cad") and a source of farcical complication (Duvallet's existing marriage, [[juggins|Juggins]]'s mysterious parentage).

## In the Play

Marriage dominates the fourth act's resolution. [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby Gilbey]] must decide whether to marry [[dora-delaney|Dora Delaney]] after their shared [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]]; his reluctance ("I won't marry her: that's flat") collides with [[juggins|Juggins]]'s moral ultimatum (004-024)(004-025). The farce escalates when the French stranger Duvallet, revealed as [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]]'s companion, turns out to be already married with two daughters (004-394)(004-395).

The parental generation treats marriage as non-negotiable social repair. Mrs. Knox insists "He ought to marry her whether or no" (004-412). Mr. [[gilbey|Gilbey]] is horrified that his son might "marry this woman and be a social outcast" (004-420). [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], who has been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], tests the limits of marriageability by asking Bobby whether he "couldn't marry a woman who's been in [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]" (004-110).

[[juggins|Juggins]], the footman who orchestrates much of the resolution, delivers the play's clearest marriage doctrine: "It is gentlemanly to marry the lady you make love to" (004-419). Yet his own origins — "Was your mother married to your father?" — remain pointedly unanswered (004-324).

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw uses marriage as a pressure-test for his characters' moral seriousness. The younger generation's casual attitude toward the institution — [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] going to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s "hardened" past, Bobby's reluctance — exposes the gap between respectable form and actual conduct. The older generation's insistence on marriage as remedy reveals their inability to imagine any other social framework for sexual morality.

The play's marriage negotiations function as a parody of the conventional comedy ending. Where Shakespeare or Austen would end with weddings, Shaw ends with chaotic marriage proposals, existing marriages that complicate new ones, and a footman who may be a duke's son calmly arranging everyone's fate.

## Key Passages

- **The moral ultimatum**: "Marry her, sir, or behave like a cad" — [[juggins|Juggins]] to Bobby (004-024).
- **The reluctant groom**: "I won't marry her: that's flat" — Bobby's initial refusal (004-025).
- **The already-married suitor**: "But I am married already. I have two daughters" — Duvallet's complication (004-394).
- **The marriage imperative**: "He ought to marry her whether or no" — Mrs. Knox's simple moral arithmetic (004-412).
- **The gentle man's code**: "It is gentlemanly to marry the lady you make love to" — [[juggins|Juggins]]'s formulation (004-419).
- **Respectable marriage**: [[dora-delaney|Dora]] declares she would never "stand between a girl and a respectable marriage" (004-146).

## Historical Context

Marriage in Edwardian [[england|England]] was undergoing signifi[[hypocrisy|cant]] transformation. The Married Women's Property Acts (1870–1908) had granted wives legal and economic rights. The divorce rate, though still low by modern standards, was rising. Debates about "free love," eugenics, and women's suffrage all challenged traditional marriage ideology.

Shaw himself was a critic of conventional marriage — he and Charlotte Payne-Townshend had a famously unconventional (possibly celibate) marriage. His plays consistently treat marriage as a social contrivance rather than a romantic destiny. *Fanny's First Play* continues this critique through farce rather than the earnest debate of *Man and Superman* or *Getting Married*.
