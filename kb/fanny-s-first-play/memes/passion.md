## Definition

Passion in *Fanny's First Play* is the force that respectability cannot contain. It appears as [[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s irrepressible gaiety, as [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s "religious fits" of rebellion, as the vitality of the inner play that the frame story's critics both detect and distrust. Shaw treats passion not as the enemy of [[morality]] but as its necessary foundation — the emotional energy without which moral life becomes "dead people walking about" (P01-001).

The play's critics debate whether the anonymous author is "physiologically incapable of the note of passion" (005-040). This critical debate is Shaw's self-mocking acknowledgment that his own reputation — "a giant brain, if you ask me; but no heart" (005-041) — is one he wishes to refute. The inner play is his evidence that passion and intellect are not opposites.

## In the Play

### Dora's Vitality

[[dora-delaney|Dora]] is passion embodied. She enters the Gilbey household "of hilarious disposition" and immediately disrupts its funereal respectability (002-043). Her laughter, her frankness, her physical energy — "exploding into ecstasies of mirth" (002-145) — are the life-force that the Gilbeys have systematically excluded from their lives. She is what [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] was seeking when he fled the dinner circuit of Denmark Hill for the vitality of the music hall.

Dora's passion is not romantic in the conventional sense. It is a general vitality — an appetite for life that expresses itself in her love for Bobby, her fondness for "old dears" who disapprove of her, and her refusal to be shamed or diminished by anyone.

### Margaret's Transformation

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s rebellion is described as passionate in a different register. She "did it out of the very depths of my nature ... in one of my religious fits" (004-110). Her transgression is not a lark but an eruption of authentic selfhood — the passion of a soul that can no longer tolerate the prison of respectability. The paradox is that this passionate eruption leads to actual imprisonment, yet makes her freer than she has ever been.

### Passion vs. Propriety

The conflict between passion and propriety structures the inner play. The older generation has suppressed passion in favor of the performance of respectability; the younger generation, having experienced passion — in prison, in the music hall, in the streets — can no longer pretend. [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]]'s poignant confession — "I wanted a man who had that happiness within himself. You made me think you had it; but it was nothing but being in love with me" (004-285) — reveals that even the older generation once knew passion, and that its loss is what has deadened them.

### The Critical Debate

In the Epilogue, [[vaughan|Vaughan]] detects "the note of passion" in the inner play: "beneath all the assumed levity of that poor waif and stray, she really loves Bobby and will be a good wife to him" (005-040). His colleagues dismiss this as evidence that the play cannot be by Shaw, "physiologically incapable of the note of passion." Vaughan's counter-insistence — "Only be true to yourself, Miss O'Dowda. Keep serious. Give up making silly jokes. Sustain the note of passion" (005-080) — is both comic and earnest: the critic who can recognize passion is the one critic who may be worth listening to.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's reputation as a cold intellectual — "Intellect without emotion" — was already established by 1911, and the Epilogue's critical debate is his witty acknowledgment of this reputation. But the inner play makes clear that Shaw's critique of passionlessness is not a defense of irrationality. The passion he values is not the enemy of [[reason]] but its ally: the emotional energy that makes reason worth exercising.

The Preface's famous passage — "I hate to see dead people walking about: it is unnatural. And our respectable middle class people are all as dead as mutton" (P01-001) — is a cry for passion, for the vitality that respectability extinguishes. The play's "desperate advice" to get into trouble is advice to feel something, to risk something, to be alive.

## Key Passages

- **Dora's mirth**: "Exploding into ecstasies of mirth. Oh my! isn't she an old love?" — Dora's irrepressible vitality (002-145).
- **Margaret's depths**: "I did it out of the very depths of my nature. I did it because I'm that sort of person. I did it in one of my religious fits" — Margaret describing passionate rebellion (004-110).
- **The note of passion**: "Poor as this play is, there's the note of passion in it ... beneath all the assumed levity of that poor waif and stray, she really loves Bobby and will be a good wife to him" — Vaughan's critical detection of genuine emotion (005-040).
- **Intellect without emotion**: "A giant brain, if you ask me; but no heart" — Bannal on Shaw's reputation (005-041).
- **Sustain the note of passion**: "Only be true to yourself ... Keep serious. Give up making silly jokes. Sustain the note of passion. And you'll do great things" — Vaughan's advice to Fanny (005-080).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period inherited from the Victorians a deep suspicion of passion — particularly female passion — as a threat to social order. The "New Woman" movement was challenging this by asserting women's right to emotional and sexual autonomy, while the suffragettes' militant tactics were denounced as "hysterical" — passion as political pathology.

Shaw's treatment of passion as a positive force — as the energy that makes authentic moral life possible — aligned him with the progressive currents of his time, from the Vitalist philosophy of Bergson to the emerging recognition of the unconscious in psychoanalysis. His insistence that passion and [[reason]] are complementary rather than opposed was a distinctive contribution to the period's debates about emotion.
