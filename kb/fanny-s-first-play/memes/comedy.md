## Definition

Comedy in *Fanny's First Play* is Shaw's vehicle for truth-telling. The play is built on comic structures — the Induction's satire of critics, the inner play's farcical collisions between parents and children, the Epilogue's ironic reversal — but its purpose is serious. Shaw's comedy does not sugarcoat unpleasant truths; it exposes them. Laughter, in this play, is the sound of recognition.

## In the Play

The Induction is pure satiric comedy: four critics, each representing a different theory of art, each rendered ridiculous by their own pronouncements. [[trotter|Trotter]]'s diplomatic pomposity, [[vaughan|Vaughan]]'s intellectual pretension, [[gunn|Gunn]]'s journalistic coarseness, and [[bannal|Bannal]]'s cheerful ignorance are comic types that also serve as critical analysis. Shaw uses comedy to do what the critics cannot: judge the play accurately.

The inner play uses farcical structures — the coincidence of Bobby and Margaret both being in trouble, the parents' mutual incomprehension, the servants' knowing commentary — to explore genuinely serious themes. The comedy is not an escape from the play's moral concerns but their vehicle.

The Epilogue's comedy is the darkest: the critics, having failed to understand the play, retroactively adjust their opinions when they learn (or think they learn) its authorship. The comedy here is at the critics' expense, but it makes a serious point about the relationship between artistic reputation and critical judgment.
