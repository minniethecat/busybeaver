## Definition

Aristotle appears in *Fanny's First Play* — under the scholarly epithet "the Stagirite" — as the fountainhead of dramatic theory. The critics invoke Aristotelian categories (comedy, tragedy, unity) to judge Fanny's play, and their failure to reach consensus demonstrates that classical categories are inadequate to modern art. Shaw's implicit argument is that Aristotle's *Poetics* describes Greek tragedy, not universal dramatic law.

## In the Play

The Induction's critical debate is, at one level, a comedy of Aristotelian misapplication. Each critic applies a different Aristotelian criterion — is it comedy or tragedy? Does it instruct? Does it move? — and each reaches a different conclusion. The play's refusal to fit any single Aristotelian category is not a failure but a demonstration that the categories themselves are the problem.

Shaw, who wrote extensively about dramatic theory (most notably in *The Quintessence of Ibsenism*), uses the Aristotle references to position his own play within — and against — the western dramatic tradition. Fanny's play, like Shaw's, is post-Aristotelian: it obeys dramatic logic but not classical prescription.
