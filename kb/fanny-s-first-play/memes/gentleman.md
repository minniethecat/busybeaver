## Definition

The "gentleman" is a recurring standard of conduct in *Fanny's First Play*, invoked by characters across the social spectrum as a measure of proper behaviour. The term is used both earnestly — as when Dora praises Bobby for behaving "like a perfect gentleman" despite having been arrested (002-081) — and satirically, as when the critics and the Count debate what constitutes gentlemanly taste. The concept bridges [[class]] and [[morality]]: to be a gentleman is to have both social standing and ethical decency, though the play questions whether the two can truly coincide.

## In the Induction

The Induction frame is saturated with gentlemanly discourse. The [[footman|Footman]] in "grandiose Spanish livery" embodies the theatrical performance of gentlemanly service (001-001). [[cecil-savoyard|Savoyard]], the theatre professional, navigates between gentlemanly deference to the Count and the commercial realities of the theatre business. The Count's refusal to pay the full fifty guineas to one of the critics because he gave a negative opinion is framed as potentially ungentlemanly (001-077).

The critics themselves — [[trotter|Trotter]], [[vaughan|Vaughan]], [[gunn|Gunn]], and [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]] — represent different versions of English manhood, from the fastidious aesthete to the bluff man-in-the-street. Their collective judgment of the play becomes a test of what a gentleman should admire.

## In the Inner Play

In the Gilbey and Knox households, "gentleman" is a term of social aspiration. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]], a linen draper, is anxious about his son [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]]'s conduct: a fortnight's absence from the shop could mean disgrace — or it could mean the boy has been behaving like a gentleman on a spree. The ambiguity is telling. Dora's declaration that Bobby "behaved like a perfect gentleman" (002-081) is meant to reassure, but it also reveals that gentlemanly conduct and criminal mischief are not incompatible in this world.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]] invokes the term when introducing a gentleman who has helped her to her father: "Father: do please be commonly civil to a gentleman who has been of the greatest service to me" (003-036). The appeal to gentlemanly civility is a plea for basic decency across class and gender lines — one that her father struggles to honour.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of the gentleman ideal is characteristically double-edged. He respects the genuine courtesy and self-possession that the term implies, but he mocks its use as a class marker that substitutes for moral judgment. The Count, who has constructed an entire aesthetic life around being a gentleman of taste, is the play's most thorough satire of the type: a man who has retreated from the modern world into a fantasy of eighteenth-century grace.

## Key Passages

- **The Count's complaint**: "I am almost sorry you didn't give the gentleman his full terms. I should not have grudged the fifty guineas for a sound opinion" — on the ethics of paying critics (001-077).
- **Dora's testimony**: "Bobby behaved like a perfect gentleman. Besides, it was all my fault" — the gentleman defence in court (002-081).
- **Margaret's appeal**: "Father: do please be commonly civil to a gentleman who has been of the greatest service to me" — the demand for gentlemanly civility (003-036).
