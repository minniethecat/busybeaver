## Overview

The [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] appears in [[fanny-odowda|Fanny's First Play]] primarily through the "[[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]]" — the student organization that shaped [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]]'s intellectual development during her two years at [[cambridge-university|Cambridge]]. Founded in 1884, the real Fabian Society was the leading gradualist socialist organization in Britain, counting [[shaw|George Bernard Shaw]], Sidney and Beatrice Webb, H.G. Wells, and Annie Besant among its members. Its presence in the play is both a self-deprecating in-joke (Shaw was a leading Fabian) and a serious acknowledgment of the society's role in reshaping Edwardian intellectual life.

## In the Play

The [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] is mentioned in three key moments of the Induction, each revealing a different aspect of its function:

### The Classification of Aunts

When [[trotter|Mr. Trotter]] prefaces a remark with "I must say," Fanny corrects him with clinical precision:

> That's one of our classifications in the Cambridge Fabian Society. We classify our aunts into different sorts. And one of the sorts is the "I must says." (001-020)

This moment establishes the Fabian Society — and by extension progressive intellectual culture — as a source of systematic analytical power. Fanny has been trained to observe, classify, and diagnose social behavior. Trotter, for all his professional critical authority, is an un-self-aware specimen in her taxonomic collection.

### The "Unsexed Virago"

Trotter's response to Fanny's intellectual confidence reveals the gender anxiety that Fabian women provoked:

> If you're going to be a charming healthy young English girl, you may coax me. If you're going to be an unsexed Cambridge Fabian virago, I'll treat you as my intellectual equal, as I would treat a man. (001-022)

Trotter's threat is revealing: treating a woman "as my intellectual equal" is presented as a punishment, not a recognition. The Fabian Society's commitment to women's participation — it admitted women from its founding and had prominent female members — threatened the gendered foundations of Edwardian intellectual authority.

### The Father's Warning

Trotter later tells Fanny what he will say to her father, making explicit the perceived danger:

> I shall say that you're a very foolish young lady; that you've got into a very questionable set; and that the sooner he takes you away from Cambridge and its Fabian Society, the better. (001-025)

The Fabian Society is "a very questionable set" — not morally questionable in the conventional sense, but intellectually dangerous to the existing order. This is precisely Shaw's point: the Fabians were threatening because they were right, not because they were wrong.

## Shaw's Connection

Shaw joined the Fabian Society in 1884, the year of its founding, and remained an active member throughout his life. His relationship to the organization was characteristically ambivalent — he was both its most famous public face and its most irreverent internal critic. The play's treatment of the Fabian Society is thus an exercise in affectionate self-mockery:

| Shaw's Relationship | Expression in the Play |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| Fabian pamphleteer (1884–) | Fanny's "classifications" parody Fabian taxonomy |
| Advocate of women's equality | Trotter's "unsexed virago" anxiety |
| Champion of gradualist reform | The society's influence on Fanny's transformation |

Shaw's 1891 *Fabian Essays in Socialism* had helped define the society's intellectual program. Twenty years later, he could afford to laugh at the culture his own activism had helped create.

## The Fabian Method

The Fabian Society's core commitment was to gradual, intellectual persuasion rather than revolutionary action — "Fabian" tactics, named after the Roman general Fabius Cunctator, who defeated Hannibal through patience and attrition rather than direct confrontation. This method is reflected in the play's structure: Fanny's play-within-a-play is itself a Fabian intervention — it changes minds through art rather than polemic, persuasion rather than confrontation.
