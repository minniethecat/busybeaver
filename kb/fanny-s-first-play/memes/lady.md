## Definition

"Lady" is one of the most frequently used words in *Fanny's First Play* — a term of address, a social category, and an ideological construct. To be a lady is to embody [[respectability]] in its feminine form: chastity, modesty, gentility. But the play systematically undermines this ideal, showing that the constraints of ladyhood are suffocating and that the women who escape them — [[dora-delaney|Dora]], [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], and [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] herself — are more alive than those who conform.

## In the Induction

[[trotter|Trotter]] repeatedly addresses [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] as "my dear young lady" — a phrase that begins as courtesy and becomes, over the course of their conversation, a form of containment (001-107). She is charming, she is intelligent, but she is, after all, a *young lady* — and the phrase carries an implicit ceiling. Trotter's inability to take Fanny seriously as an intellectual equal is partly a function of her being a lady.

Fanny's [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] education has given her tools to analyze this dynamic, but even she cannot entirely escape it. Her father's world — the world of Florence Towers — is built on the assumption that ladies are decorative, not intellectual.

## In the Inner Play

The inner play offers three versions of womanhood. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] and [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] are ladies in the conventional sense: respectable, churchgoing, morally vigilant. But their ladyhood has made them anxious and ineffectual. They police their children's behaviour while being unable to understand it.

[[dora-delaney|Dora]] is the anti-lady. She drinks champagne, goes to prison, speaks her mind, and laughs at the conventions that constrain other women. She is not respectable, but she is free. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] occupies a middle position: raised as a lady, she discovers that ladyhood is a cage. Her journey from "good" girl to "bad" woman is a journey from the performance of ladyhood to the reality of selfhood.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of the lady ideal is consistent with his broader feminist convictions. The lady is a social construct designed to constrain women's freedom while flattering their vanity. The play's argument — that the women who break free of ladyhood are morally superior to those who remain within it — is one of its most radical propositions.

## Key Passages

- **Trotter's address**: "My dear young lady" — the phrase that contains Fanny (001-107).
- **Cambridge education**: "This is Cambridge education! Well, my dear young lady, I'm delighted" — Trotter's patronizing response to Fanny's intelligence (001-147).
- **Fanny's reply**: "I can't possibly tell you what I shall say to your father about the play" — the lady's strategic reticence (001-149).
