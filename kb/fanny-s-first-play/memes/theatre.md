## Definition

Theatre in *Fanny's First Play* functions on two levels: as the literal medium of the play-within-a-play performed at [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]]'s country house, and as the subject of sustained critical argument in the Induction frame. Shaw uses the theatrical setting to interrogate fundamental questions about what constitutes a play, who decides its merit, and how art is consumed.

## Theatre as Frame

The physical theatre is established in the opening stage direction: "The end of a saloon in an old-fashioned country house (Florence Towers, the property of [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]]) has been curtained off to form a stage for a private theatrical performance" [001-043]. This domestic conversion of space into theatre mirrors the play's thematic concern with the boundary between art and life.

The performance itself is anomalous — a private showing with real actors and real critics, arranged as a birthday present. Savoyard notes: "It was easy enough to engage a company for a private performance: it's done often enough. But the notion of having critics was new" [001-091]. This peculiar arrangement foregrounds the artificiality of the critical enterprise.

> The Footman: Mr. Cecil Savoyard. Cecil Savoyard comes in: a middle-aged man in evening dress and a fur-lined overcoat. He is surprised to find nobody to receive him.
>
> Savoyard: Half a mo. When does the play begin? Half-past eight?
> [001-045–046]

## The Definition of a Play

A central preoccupation of the Induction is the question of what qualifies as a "play." Trotter insists on a narrow definition, repeatedly asserting that certain contemporary works are "not plays":

> Trotter: I say they are not plays. Dialogues, if you will. Exhibitions of character, perhaps: especially the character of the author. Fictions, possibly, though a little decent reticence as to introducing actual persons, and thus violating the sanctity of private life, might not be amiss. But plays, no. I say no. Not plays.
> [001-174]

This taxonomic dispute reflects genuine early-twentieth-century debates about [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic form. Shaw himself was accused of writing "discussions" rather than plays, and Trotter's position — simultaneously conservative and self-parodic — captures the resistance to formal innovation in the theatre of the period.

## Art and Commerce

The Induction reveals the theatre as a site where artistic and commercial values collide. [[bannal|Bannal]]'s demand to know whether *Fanny's First Play* is "repertory theatre tosh, or really straight paying stuff" [001-995] exposes the economic reality underlying critical judgment. The theatre is simultaneously an art form and a commercial enterprise, and the critics' role is ambiguously positioned between aesthetic gatekeeping and consumer guidance.

[[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] represents an older, idealized conception of theatre as pure art. He is horrified to learn the play is modern and performed without costumes: "Do you mean to say it is a modern play?" [001-084]. For the Count, theatre belongs to "the graceful and charming fantasies of the Italian and French stages of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries" [001-088] — anything contemporary is a violation of his aesthetic sensibilities.

## Meta-Theatricality

*Fanny's First Play* is self-consciously meta-theatrical. The audience watches critics watch a play, and the critics' responses become the real [[art-and-theatre|drama]]. Shaw collapses the distance between performance and reception, suggesting that criticism is itself a form of performance — each critic plays a role (the aesthete, the moralist, the intellectual, the man-in-the-street) as scripted as any character in the inner play.
