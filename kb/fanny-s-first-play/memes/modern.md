## Definition

"Modern" is a charged word in *Fanny's First Play* — sometimes a term of contempt, sometimes of aspiration. The [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]] uses it to condemn everything he detests: modern dress, modern morals, modern critics. The young characters use it, implicitly, to describe the freedom they are trying to achieve. The play stages the conflict between tradition and modernity not as a debate but as a generational drama: the old world is dying, and the new world is being born in police cells and suburban parlours.

## The Count's Anti-Modernism

The Count is the play's most articulate critic of modernity. He has constructed his life as a refuge from the nineteenth and twentieth centuries: an eighteenth-century fantasy of Venetian elegance maintained on an Irish country estate. "To me the century I shut out will always be the nineteenth century," he tells Savoyard — the century of industry, democracy, and ugliness (001-021). His horror at the idea of a modern-dress production of his daughter's play — "No costumes! Do you mean to say it is a modern play?" (001-041) — is the play's most explicit statement of the anti-modern position.

Yet the Count's anti-modernism is itself a product of modernity. His aesthetic retreat requires the income and infrastructure of the modern world he despises. [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] sees through him: his pose is not a genuine alternative to modern life but a theatrical escape from it.

## Fanny's Modernism

Fanny is, by contrast, thoroughly modern. Her [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] education has given her the tools to analyze and classify the world — including her father. She finds his "dread of ridicule" to be "so frightfully out of date" (001-164). Her anonymous play is itself a modern gesture: a refusal to trade on reputation, a demand to be judged on merit alone.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a committed modernist who understood modernity's contradictions. The Preface links the play's modernity to its moral purpose: the young need a new morality because the old one has failed. "We do not seem to know that there is any other test of conduct except morality" (P01-001) — meaning conventional morality, which is revealed as insufficient. Modernity, for Shaw, is not a style but a necessity: the old moral codes cannot survive contact with modern freedom.

## Key Passages

- **The Count's century**: "To me the century I shut out will always be the nineteenth century" — the anti-modern creed (001-021).
- **No costumes**: "No costumes! Do you mean to say it is a modern play?" — the Count's horror at modernity on stage (001-041).
- **Fanny's diagnosis**: "This dread of ridicule is so frightfully out of date" — the modern critique of convention (001-164).
