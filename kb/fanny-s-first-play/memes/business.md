## Definition

Business in *Fanny's First Play* is the economic reality that underlies the moral posturing of the Gilbeys and Knoxes. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] is a linen draper; [[knox|Joseph Knox]] runs a shop. Their respectability is not merely a moral stance but a business asset — the goodwill of customers depends on the proprietor's reputation. Business thus becomes both the justification for conventional [[morality]] and its prison: the fear of losing trade enforces conformity more effectively than any sermon.

## In the Play

The Gilbey household is saturated with commercial anxiety. Gilbey's first instinct when his son goes missing is not paternal concern but business worry: how will this affect the shop? He explains to Dora that Bobby's conduct is "my business" — meaning not only his concern but his commercial interest (002-082). The conflation of family and commerce is central to the play's satire of middle-class life.

Knox's moral authority in his own household is similarly compromised by his commercial practices. He boasts of having "turned many a girl out of the shop for being light" — enforcing sexual morality on his employees while failing to provide his own daughter with moral guidance that survives contact with freedom. Business provides the power to impose morality on others without the obligation to live by it oneself.

Dora, by contrast, is free of commercial anxiety. She works for a living but does not define herself by her trade. Her ease with herself — her indifference to respectability — is partly a function of her economic independence from the world of shops and customers.

## The Theatre Business

The Induction adds another dimension: the theatre as business. [[cecil-savoyard|Savoyard]] is a theatre professional who must negotiate between artistic standards and commercial realities. The Count's distaste for the business side of theatre — his insistence on paying the critics, his horror at modern dress — marks him as an amateur, insulated by wealth from commercial necessity.

## Key Passages

- **Gilbey's boundary**: "Never you mind how he's been brought up: that's my business" — the father's conflation of family and commerce (002-082).
- **Knox's shop morality**: "I've turned many a girl out of the shop for being light" — commercial power as moral enforcement (003-022).
- **Savoyard's negotiation**: The business of paying critics and the fifty-guinea dispute (001-077).
