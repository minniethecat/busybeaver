## Definition

*Fanny's First Play* is, among other things, a play about what plays are for.

The Induction stages a critical debate about dramatic art; the inner play provides an example of the art being debated; the Epilogue shows the critics failing to learn anything from the example.

Shaw's implicit argument is that theatre is neither entertainment (Gunn's position) nor moral edification (Trotter's) nor intellectual display (Vaughan's), but the exposure of uncomfortable truths through comedy.

## In the Play

The Induction's four critics represent four theories of dramatic art. Trotter, the gentleman-amateur, judges by propriety: is the play suitable for "the British family"? Vaughan, the intellectual, judges by profundity: does the play contribute to the tradition of "the drama of ideas"? Gunn, the journalist, judges by sensation: is there "a good strong curtain"? [[flawner-bannal|Flawner Bannal]], the man-about-town, judges by personal comfort: was he "bored"?

The inner play — the [[gilbey|Gilbey]]-Knox comedy — defies all four frameworks. It is vulgar enough to offend Trotter, light enough to disappoint Vaughan, serious enough to bore Gunn, and moral enough to discomfit [[bannal|Bannal]]. The critics' failure to agree on its merits is Shaw's demonstration that their critical categories are inadequate to the art they purport to judge.

[[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]], the playwright within the play, represents the artist as innocent — she has written a play that she does not fully understand, and its effect on the critics is beyond her control. Her father, [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]], represents the artist as aesthete — he values beauty and refinement and is horrified by his daughter's vulgar subject matter. Neither fully grasps what art is for.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's own theory of dramatic art, developed across decades of criticism and practice, held that the theatre was a "factory of thought, a prompter of [[morality|conscience]], an elucidator of social conduct." *Fanny's First Play* is a comic demonstration of this theory: the inner play prompts [[morality|conscience]] in its characters (Knox's confession, [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]]'s discovery) and in its audience, while the critical framework shows what happens when the prompting is resisted.

The concealed authorship was itself an artistic experiment. By removing the author's name, Shaw forced critics to judge the play rather than the playwright — and demonstrated, in the process, that they were incapable of doing so (P01-003).

## Key Passages

- **The artistic experiment**: "The concealment of the authorship... was a necessary part of the play" (P01-003).
- **The [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]]**: The play premiered "at the [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] in the Adelphi, [[london|London]]" — a venue for experimental art (P01-004).
- **The critics lampooned**: Shaw acknowledges the critics "whom I have lampooned in the induction" (P01-002).
- **The [[education|Cambridge]] Fabian aesthetic**: Fanny's play is a product of her [[education|Cambridge]] education — art as intellectual exercise (001-047).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian theatre was divided between the commercial West End (entertainment, melodrama, the well-made play) and the emerging "New Drama" (Ibsen, Shaw, Granville Barker) that demanded intellectual engagement. The Court [[theatre|Theatre]]'s seasons of Shaw and Barker (1904–1907) had established the possibility of a serious English drama, but the battle between art and commerce was ongoing.

The "[[little-theatre|Little Theatre]]" movement — small, experimental venues like the [[little-theatre|Little Theatre]] in the Adelphi — provided space for work that the commercial theatre would not touch. Shaw's choice of venue for *Fanny's First Play* was itself an artistic statement.
