## Setting
The [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] in *Fanny's First Play* is never shown on stage — it exists as a reported experience, a shared memory that the three young characters carry into the respectable world of the [[gilbey|Gilbey]] and Knox households. Its invisibility is thematically signifi[[hypocrisy|cant]]: the moral transformation it produces is more real than the visible institutions (church, family, shop) that claim to provide moral formation.

## In the Play
Bobby, [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], and [[dora-delaney|Dora]] have all been im[[crime-and-punishment|prison]]ed before the inner play begins. Their shared experience of incarceration creates a bond that the respectable characters cannot comprehend. The [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] is referenced throughout Acts II–IV as the characters negotiate its social consequences.

## Dramatic Function
The [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] functions as an inverted moral academy. Where church and school produce conformity without [[morality|conscience]], [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] produces [[truth|honesty]] without shame. Shaw's argument is that the institutions society calls punitive may be more genuinely educative than those it calls moral.

## Real-World Context
Edwardian [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]s for young offenders included Holloway (for women) and Wormwood Scrubs (for men). The Borstal system for young offenders had been established in 1902, reflecting reformist impulses that Shaw's critique both drew on and extended.
