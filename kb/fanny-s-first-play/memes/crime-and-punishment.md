## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, crime and punishment are inverted from their conventional meanings. The young characters — [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby Gilbey]], [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]], and [[dora-delaney|Dora Delaney]] — all experience imprisonment, yet their "crimes" (a drunken assault on a policeman, shoplifting, and unspecified "light" behavior) are trivial or even admirable. The real crime, Shaw suggests, is the hypocrisy of a society that imprisons young people for offenses it secretly condones, while congratulating itself on its respectability.

## In the Play

Imprisonment functions as the play's central plot device. Bobby and [[dora-delaney|Dora]] meet in prison after Bobby punches a policeman during a night out; [[dora-delaney|Dora]] is there for reasons reported under the headline "Hardened at Eighteen. A quietly dressed, respectable-looking girl who refuses her name" (004-100). [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]], the respectable shopkeeper's daughter, has also been imprisoned — she "went wrong" and found the experience liberating rather than shaming.

The scandal of imprisonment drives the second half of the play. The [[gilbey|Gilbey]]s face the terror of social exposure. Knox, who has "turned many a girl out of the shop for being light," must confront the fact that his own daughter is now one of the fallen (003-022). The resolution — [[juggins|Juggins]] arranging marriages, revealing his own mysterious origins, and orchestrating a social reckoning — turns punishment into [[freedom|liberation]].

Bobby's sardonic self-description — "I'm a gaol-bird: you're a respectable man" (004-007) — crystallizes the play's inversion. The prison becomes a site of [[truth|authenticity]]; respectable society, a prison of [[hypocrisy|pretense]].

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a lifelong critic of the penal system, which he viewed as a mechanism for punishing the poor while excusing the rich. His Fabian socialism informed his critique: the criminal justice system was not a neutral arbiter of morality but an instrument of class discipline.

The Preface frames the play as an intervention in the debate about "mere morality" versus genuine [[morality|conscience]]. The characters who have been to prison emerge with more moral clarity than those who have merely attended church and kept up appearances. Shaw's lesson is that "getting into trouble" — "loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable freedom" — may be the necessary price of moral growth (P01-001).

## Key Passages

- **The gaol-bird and the respectable man**: "I'm a gaol-bird: you're a respectable man" — Bobby (004-007).
- **The hardened girl**: "Hardened at Eighteen. A quietly dressed, respectable-looking girl who refuses her name" — the newspaper report of [[dora-delaney|Dora]] (004-100).
- **The shopkeeper's hypocrisy**: Knox admits turning girls "out of the shop for being light" to protect his reputation (003-022).
- **[[respectability|Respectability]] and punishment**: [[gilbey|Gilbey]] laments that "it's not what I call respectable to have your children in and out of gaol" (004-255).
- **The discovery of freedom**: "I never knew we could live without being respectable" (004-253).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian penal system distinguished between "deserving" and "undeserving" poor, with middle-class offenders often receiving more lenient treatment. The Borstal system for young offenders was established in 1902, reflecting reformist impulses. Women's prisons were sites of particular moral anxiety — the "fallen woman" who had been imprisoned was considered permanently [[shame|disgrace]]d.

Shaw's critique reflects broader progressive concerns about the criminalization of poverty and the class bias of the justice system. The idea that a young man could go to prison for punching a policeman while drunk — and that this would be a social catastrophe rather than a youthful indiscretion — was exactly the kind of absurdity Shaw delighted in exposing.
