## Role

Margaret Knox is the intellectual and moral center of the inner play. More than any other character, she articulates the play's philosophical argument: that the morality of respectability is a form of [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]], and that genuine moral development requires the experience of freedom — even if that freedom is called "badness" by respectable society. Her testimony — "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free" (003-088) — is the play's thesis statement in [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic form.

## In the Play

Margaret appears primarily in Acts III and IV. In Act III, she returns to her father's shop after her [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] and delivers the play's most important speech: a confession that is also a manifesto. She describes her journey from prize-winning schoolgirl ("the good conduct prize for three years running at school") to im[[crime-and-punishment|prison]]ed "fallen woman" to liberated truth-teller (003-074).

In Act IV, Margaret participates in the chaotic marriage negotiations with a detachment that contrasts sharply with the parents' panic. She asks Bobby directly: "Do you feel you couldn't marry a woman who's been in [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]?" (004-110). The question is a test — of Bobby, of the social order, of the audience.

## Character Analysis

Margaret is Shaw's portrait of the "[[gender-roles|New Woman]]" — educated, independent, unashamed of her body and her experiences. Her [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]], which should destroy her marriageability, has instead given her clarity. She has learned what her father's shop, her school prizes, and her church attendance never taught her: that respectability is a performance and freedom is a condition.

Her relationship with Duvallet, the married Frenchman, is the play's most morally ambiguous subplot. Margaret refuses to be shamed by it — she has been to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]], after all, and [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] has taught her that shame is a weapon the respectable use against the free.

## Key Passages

- **The thesis statement**: "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free. And when I got free I was what most people call bad" (003-088).
- **The good conduct prize**: "I got the good conduct prize for three years running at school" — the irony of the prize-winning girl in [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] (003-074).
- **The marriage test**: "Do you feel you couldn't marry a woman who's been in [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]?" — Margaret's question to Bobby (004-110).
- **The [[education|Cambridge]] connection**: Margaret's education is paralleled by Fanny's — two young women transformed by intellectual freedom.

## Critical Reception

Margaret has been recognized as one of Shaw's most important "[[gender-roles|New Woman]]" characters, a fictional cousin of Vivie Warren and Major Barbara. Feminist critics have noted that her [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] experience functions as a form of sexual [[freedom|liberation]] — she emerges from [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] unashamed of desires that respectable society condemns.
