## Setting

Brighton is the quintessential English seaside resort — a place of piers, promenades, and holiday pleasures. By the Edwardian period, it was a well-established destination for Londoners seeking "sea air" and respectable recreation. But its reputation also had a racier side: the "gay place" of Mrs. Knox's description, where a young man might encounter "temptations" [002-013].

In *Fanny's First Play*, Brighton never appears on stage. It exists entirely as a story told by parents about their children's whereabouts — and like most stories the parents tell in this play, it is not true.

## In the Play

### Act II: The Cover Story

[[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] reports the Gilbeys' version of events to her husband: "Mrs. Gilbey told me Bobby was in Brighton for the sea air. There's something queer about that. Gilbey would never let the boy loose by himself among the temptations of a gay place like Brighton without his tutor; and I saw the tutor in Kensington High Street the very day she told me" [002-013]. The Brighton story is already unraveling in its first telling — the tutor was in London, not Brighton, so Bobby wasn't there either.

The phrase "Brighton for the sea air" encapsulates the play's critique of respectable language. "Sea air" is a euphemism for nothing at all — a blank placeholder that sounds wholesome and reveals nothing. The Gilbeys deploy it to cover the unspeakable truth: their son is in [[holloway|prison]].

### Act III: The Lies Unravel

When [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] visits [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]], she opens with ironic awareness: "Quite well, thank you. How did you enjoy Brighton?" [003-062]. Bobby nearly slips — "Brighton! I wasn't at — Oh yes, of course. Oh, pretty well" — before recovering the lie. The exchange dramatizes the complicity of deception: both families have been telling the same kind of lies (Bobby in "Brighton," Margaret in "Cornwall with her aunt"), and both young people know it.

Margaret presses: "I've lived a lot since I saw you last. I wasn't at my aunt's. All that time that you were in Brighton, I mean" [003-092]. This prompts Bobby's confession: "I wasn't at Brighton, Meg. I'd better tell you: you're bound to find out sooner or later. Meg: it's rather awful: you'll think me no end of a beast. I've been in prison" [003-093].

## Dramatic Function

Brighton serves as the perfect symbol of the play's central theme: the gap between respectable appearance and actual reality. "Brighton" is what you say when you can't say "prison." It is the geographical equivalent of the social performances the play exposes. The town's dual reputation — wholesome sea air on one hand, "gay" temptations on the other — makes it the ideal vehicle for a lie that must be plausible enough to work and vague enough to cover anything.

## Real-World Context

Brighton in the Edwardian period was at the height of its popularity as a resort. The London, Brighton and South Coast Railway made it accessible for day trips and holidays. The town's piers, aquarium, and Pavilion attracted respectable families; its less visible entertainments attracted those seeking "temptations." Shaw's use of Brighton draws on this double life — the town, like the families in the play, had a public face and a private reality.
