## Definition

"Society" in *Fanny's First Play* is not an abstract concept but a concrete force — the collective judgment of neighbours, churchgoers, shop customers, and critics that enforces conformity and punishes deviation. It is the entity that [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] and the [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] study and classify; the entity that [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] and [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] fear; and the entity that Shaw, through the play and its Preface, challenges to reform itself.

## In the Induction

The Induction is an anatomy of society's relationship to art. [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]] has invited four critics representing different social types to judge her anonymous play. The [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] serves as Fanny's model of society — a progressive intellectual circle that has taught her to classify people "just as you'd classify the characters in a novel" (001-112). [[trotter|Trotter]]'s reaction — "I withdraw 'I must say.' I substitute 'Blame my cats!'" — registers the absurdity of scientific sociology applied to actual human beings (001-115).

## In the Inner Play

In the inner play, society is the invisible judge. The Gilbeys and Knoxes live in terror of "what people will say." [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]]'s linen-draper business depends on the goodwill of customers who demand respectability (002-053). [[knox|Joseph Knox]] enforces sexual morality on his shop assistants because "society" demands it — even as his own household is falling apart.

The young characters have moved beyond this fear. [[dora-delaney|Dora]] has been to prison and back; she knows society's worst judgment and has survived it. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] has discovered that "society" is not the moral authority it claims to be. [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] is learning that his father's terror of social opinion is a paper tiger.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's relationship to society was that of a critic who loved it enough to want it reformed. The Preface's call for the young to "get into trouble" is a call to break the social contract of respectability — not out of nihilism but out of a conviction that society's moral code is false and must be replaced by something more genuine. The concealment of the play's authorship was itself a social experiment: can society judge a work on its merits, or only by the reputation of its author?

## Key Passages

- **Fanny's classification**: "That's one of our classifications in the Cambridge Fabian Society" — society as sociological specimen (001-112).
- **Trotter's recoil**: "I withdraw 'I must say.' I substitute 'Blame my cats!'" — the critic's resistance to classification (001-115).
- **Society's outdatedness**: "This dread of ridicule is so frightfully out of date" — Fanny on society's fear of judgment (001-164).
