## Role

Cecil Savoyard is a character in the Induction and Epilogue of *Fanny's First Play*. He is the impresario — an actor-manager associated with the Savoy Theatre — whom Fanny O'Dowda engages to produce her anonymous play at Florence Towers. His role is that of the theatrical professional: pragmatic, commercially astute, and refreshingly free of the pretensions that afflict both the Count and the critics. Shaw uses Savoyard as a foil to Count O'Dowda's aesthetic idealism and as an insider's guide to the world of dramatic criticism, translating its personalities and conventions for the bewildered Count.

## In the Play

Savoyard first enters in the Induction, announced by the Footman as "Mr. Cecil Savoyard" — "a middle-aged man in evening dress and a fur-lined overcoat" [001-001]. His first concern is practical rather than artistic: "When does the play begin? Half-past eight?" [001-002]. Learning it is not until nine, he immediately arranges to telephone his wife — not "Mrs. Cecil Savoyard" but "Mrs. William Tinkler" — a wry detail that hints at the theatrical world's casual relationship with conventional names and identities [001-003].

His extended exchange with Count O'Dowda establishes the dynamic between patron and producer. The Count fusses over unlocking bookcases for the literary guests; Savoyard's response — "Oh, you mean the dramatic critics. M'yes. I suppose there's a smoking room?" [001-005] — is a masterpiece of compressed characterisation. The actor-manager is indifferent to critical opinion and intellectual pretension; he wants practical comfort and a working theatre. When the Count delivers his rhapsody about living in Venice and shutting out the nineteenth century, Savoyard corrects him: "The twentieth" [001-018]. His characteristic response to the Count's aesthetic manifesto is grounded: "It's all right when you can afford it" [001-023], and "Some of us must live in England, you know, just to keep the place going" [001-029].

Savoyard reveals himself as a shrewd judge of the critical profession. He has assembled four representative critics at a cost of "ten guineas, and expenses" [001-041], explaining to the Count: "Out of a hundred notices you won't find more than four at the outside that say anything different" [001-040]. His thumbnail sketches of the critics are a theatrical insider's briefing: Trotter is "a man of the world and a man of sense" [001-049], Vaughan "has no sense of humor; and if you joke with him he'll think you're insulting him on purpose" [001-051], and Gunn "is one of the intellectuals" [001-054]. Of Bannal he says, "He's the man in the street; and that's what you want" [001-057].

Savoyard is scandalised to learn the hero is a Frenchman but is reassured to hear there are no costumes [001-032]. He has not read the play: "I handed it to Billy Burjoyce — the producer, you know — and left it to him to select the company and so on" [001-035]. The authorship is "a dead secret" that Savoyard carefully protects [001-042].

In the Epilogue he re-enters after the critics' furious debate about authorship, asking the Count to congratulate the company who are "rather upset at having had no curtain call" [005-085]. He accompanies the Count and critics onto the stage for the final reconciliation [005-087].

## Character Analysis

The name "Savoyard" carries multiple resonances. It refers to the Savoy Theatre — home of the Gilbert and Sullivan operas — and, more broadly, to the tradition of English comic opera. By naming his actor-manager "Savoyard," Shaw signals that Fanny's play belongs to a theatrical tradition that values wit and entertainment alongside ideas. The name also evokes the Savoy Hotel and the Savoy's association with elegant, professional hospitality — appropriate for a man who "produces" artistic experiences for paying customers.

Savoyard is notably free of the moral panic that grips Trotter and the intellectual posturing that marks Vaughan and Gunn. He treats the private performance as a job — well-paid, efficiently organised, professionally executed. His pragmatism is not cynicism; it is the working philosophy of a man who has spent his life in the commercial theatre and understands that a play's first duty is to hold an audience. When the Count worries about having offended Bannal by paying only ten guineas, Savoyard dismisses the concern: "Well, let him. It was a bit of side, his asking fifty. After all, what is he? Only a pressman" [001-059].

Shaw's portrait of Savoyard is affectionate. The actor-manager is competent, unpretentious, and fundamentally honest — qualities Shaw valued in the theatre and found all too rare. Savoyard represents the professional artist who serves the work rather than his own reputation, a figure Shaw himself aspired to be.
