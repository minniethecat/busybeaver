## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, "nature" is the standard against which social convention is measured and found wanting. To be "natural" is to act from genuine impulse rather than social performance; to be "unnatural" is to have one's humanity eroded by the demands of respectability. The play proposes that what society calls "goodness" is often the suppression of nature, and that the recovery of nature — even through the extreme measure of imprisonment — is a moral imperative.

Shaw's Preface establishes the terms: "I hate to see dead people walking about: it is unnatural. And our respectable middle class people are all as dead as mutton" [P01-001]. The judgment is stark: respectability is not merely boring or constraining — it is a form of living death, a violation of nature itself.

## In the Play

### Nature as Authentic Self

The play's most concentrated statement on nature comes from [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]: "I didn't do it for a lark, Bob: I did it out of the very depths of my nature. I did it because I'm that sort of person. I did it in one of my religious fits. I'm hardened at eighteen, as they say" [003-120]. Margaret insists that her night of freedom was not frivolous transgression but authentic self-expression. The distinction is crucial: a "lark" is social deviance performed for effect; an act from "the very depths of my nature" is morally serious even if it violates every social norm.

[[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]] cannot accept this distinction. For him, there are only two categories: "a lark" (forgivable) and "to go on the loose on principle" (unforgivable) [003-121]. Margaret's claim that her actions were natural — expressions of her deepest self — falls into neither category. The gap between Margaret's self-understanding and Bobby's framework is unbridgeable, and it is why she ultimately rejects him: "Bobby: you're no good" [003-121].

### Nature as Stage Direction

Shaw's stage directions make the nature-versus-convention argument visually. The description of Bobby himself is diagnostic: "He is a pretty youth, of very suburban gentility, strong and manly enough by nature, but untrained and unsatisfactory, his parents having imagined that domestic restriction is what they call 'bringing up.' He has learnt nothing from it except a habit of evading it by deceit" [003-015]. Bobby's nature — "strong and manly enough" — has been ruined by his upbringing. The parents' attempt to produce a respectable son has produced a liar.

### Nature as Cultural Contrast

[[duvallet|Lieutenant Duvallet's]] climactic speech reframes the nature-versus-convention debate in national terms. He praises English freedom as "so natural to you as an Englishman that you are hardly conscious of possessing them" [004-092]. The irony, of course, is that the English characters have just spent the entire play demonstrating their enslavement to convention. Duvallet's praise of English natural freedom is a mirror held up to their actual unfreedom.

### Minor Uses

The play deploys "nature" in several smaller, colloquial senses that reinforce the larger theme. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] dismisses [[dora-delaney|Dora]] as "extravagant by nature" [002-122] — using nature to explain away behavior that challenges her worldview. Knox complains of people in the street "going up and down as if nothing had happened. It seems unnatural, as if they all knew and didn't care" [002-070] — the disruption of normal life feels unnatural because it exposes the fragility of the social order. Duvallet excuses Knox's anger as "very natural" [002-088] — acknowledging the authenticity of parental feeling beneath the social performance.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's treatment of nature aligns with his broader philosophical commitments. He was a vitalist — he believed in a life force that drives human beings toward growth, creativity, and moral evolution. Social convention, in this view, is the enemy of life: it deadens, constrains, and produces the "dead people walking about" that the Preface denounces. The characters who recover their nature — Margaret, Dora, even Fanny — do so through experiences that conventional society condemns: prison, disgrace, political activism.

The play's paradox is that nature can only be recovered through the unnatural act of getting into trouble. The "desperate advice" of the Preface — "Do something that will get you into trouble" — is advice to violate convention in order to recover authenticity.

- **The depths of nature**: "I didn't do it for a lark, Bob: I did it out of the very depths of my nature. I did it because I'm that sort of person" [003-120].

- **Duvallet on English nature**: "so natural to you as an Englishman that you are hardly conscious of possessing them" [004-092].
- **Duvallet excuses nature**: "But it's very natural. I understand Mr. Knox's feelings perfectly" [002-088].
- **Mrs. Gilbey's dismissal**: "I suppose you're extravagant by nature" [002-122].

## Historical Context

The nature-versus-convention debate was central to late Victorian and Edwardian intellectual life. The influence of Darwin, Nietzsche, and Bergson had made "nature" a contested concept: was human nature fixed or evolving? Was civilization the fulfillment or the suppression of natural impulses? Shaw's vitalism — his belief in a creative evolutionary force — placed him firmly on the side of nature as dynamic, progressive, and opposed to the dead weight of convention. The play's treatment of nature is a popular dramatization of this philosophical position.
