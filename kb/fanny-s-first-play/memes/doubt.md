## Definition

Doubt in *Fanny's First Play* is not weakness but intellectual honesty. When characters express doubt — about religion, about parental authority, about conventional morality — Shaw presents this uncertainty as a form of courage. Certainty, by contrast, belongs to the play's moral failures: the critics' unearned confidence, the parents' unexamined convictions.

## In the Play

[[knox|Joseph Knox]] is the play's most important doubter. A deacon of his church, he confesses to [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]: "I have no real religion" (004-498). This admission — devastating in its context — is also the most honest statement any parent makes in the play. Knox's doubt is not a loss of faith but an acknowledgment that his faith was never real to begin with.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s experience in [[prison]] teaches her to doubt everything her parents have taught her. She tells her mother: "I'm not a bit the sort of girl you thought me. I'm not a bit the sort of girl I thought myself. And I don't know what sort of person you really are, or what sort of person father really is" (003-467). This radical doubt is the beginning of her moral independence — and her [[adulthood]].

The missionary's report that Margaret "seemed hardened and that no doubt I would tell who I was after a day in prison" (003-467) is ironic: the official's "no doubt" is the certainty of the unexamined, set against Margaret's genuine uncertainty about herself and her world.

## Key Passages

- **Knox's confession**: "I have no real religion" (004-498).
- **Margaret's self-doubt**: "I'm not a bit the sort of girl I thought myself" (003-467).
- **The missionary's certainty**: "No doubt I would tell who I was after a day in prison" (003-467).
