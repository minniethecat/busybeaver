## Definition

In *Fanny's First Play*, "guilt" is the moral category that the respectable older generation uses to judge and control the young. It is the framework of sin, blame, and punishment inherited from Victorian Christianity and middle-class convention. Shaw subjects this framework to sustained critique: the characters who are most obsessed with guilt — Mr. Gilbey, Mr. Knox, Mrs. Knox — are the least morally perceptive, while the characters who refuse to feel guilty — Margaret, Dora — are the most morally awake. The play argues for replacing the language of guilt with the language of self-knowledge and honest experience.

## In the Play

### The Parents' Moral Framework

The older generation operates entirely within the guilt paradigm. Mr. Gilbey's first response to Bobby's imprisonment is fear of disgrace: "The disgrace of it will kill me. And it will leave a mark on him to the end of his life" [002-086]. His concern is not for his son's wellbeing but for the family's social standing, framed in the language of permanent moral stain.

Mr. Knox similarly treats Margaret's transgression as a catastrophe of shame: "To tear me down from the position I've made for myself and you by forty years hard struggling?" [003-126]. He begs Margaret "not to let it out" [003-128], prioritising concealment over honesty. The guilt framework is, for both fathers, primarily a tool of social control rather than genuine moral evaluation.

Mrs. Knox represents the religious dimension of guilt. She interprets Margaret's experience through the lens of sin and redemption: "They are redeemed already if they choose to believe it" [003-113]. When Margaret refuses the language of contrition, Mrs. Knox is horrified: "when I hear you justifying your wickedness in the words of grace, it's too horrible: it sounds like the devil making fun of religion" [003-115].

The Gilbeys and Knoxes are terrified by the idea that their children might not feel guilty. Margaret's refusal of contrition — "I wasn't sorry" — is more disturbing to them than the transgression itself. Guilt, in their world, is the emotion that restores social order; its absence threatens chaos.

### Margaret's Refusal of Guilt

Margaret Knox is the play's most radical challenger of the guilt framework. She explicitly refuses to feel guilty about what she has done: "I don't feel that I did anything wrong, really" [003-100]. This is not amoralism but a redefinition of morality. She distinguishes between her own actions ("I did no harm") and the actions of others ("All the bad part was done by other people" [003-042]). Her moral calculus is based on intention and harm, not on social convention.

Margaret's theological challenge is particularly pointed. She appropriates religious language — "descent into hell," "redeeming people" — but empties it of its guilt content. For Margaret, hell is a real place (Holloway Gaol) and redemption is a social project (freeing people from the conditions that create suffering), not a transaction of sin and forgiveness. Her mother's religion, by contrast, is "pretending, pretending, pretending" [003-088].

### Dora's Alternative Morality

Dora Delaney operates entirely outside the guilt framework. She is "Hardened at Eighteen" — the newspaper headline that describes her — but her hardness is not moral callousness. It is practical resilience. She takes responsibility for her actions without accepting the moral condemnation that accompanies them: "I'm afraid it was all my fault" [002-050], she says, but she makes no apology for who she is.

Dora's morality is based on loyalty, honesty, and care — she pays Bobby's fine, she speaks truthfully to his parents, she offers to marry him. These are not the actions of a guilty person seeking redemption but of a responsible person acting on her commitments. Her presence in the play exposes the guilt framework as a class weapon: the respectable middle class uses guilt to exclude people like Dora from moral consideration, while Dora herself demonstrates a more functional morality.

### The "Sinned Against" Motif

The play invokes but subverts the language of sin. When Mrs. Gilbey describes the Monsignor's brother as "more sinned against than sinning" [002-048], the phrase is used in its conventional sense: a good man who has suffered unjustly. But the play as a whole inverts this moral calculus. The young characters are "sinned against" by a society that punishes their pursuit of life and freedom, yet they refuse to claim victim status. Bobby and Margaret's mutual recognition — "Here we are, two gaolbirds, Bobby, disgraced forever. Isn't it a relief?" [003-042] — transforms guilt into solidarity.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface launches a direct attack on the language of guilt. He contrasts "right and wrong, of honor and dishonor, of sin and grace, of salvation and damnation" — the vocabulary of genuine moral judgment — with the modern substitution of "morality and immorality" [P01-001]. The older vocabulary, Shaw suggests, at least took moral questions seriously; the modern reduction of ethics to "morality" — a code of social conformity — has emptied guilt of its meaning.

Shaw's alternative is not moral relativism but a more demanding ethics. The young must "know good and evil," must develop "courage and cowardice" as real categories of character. This requires experience, not the avoidance of guilt. The play's title character, Fanny, has been to prison as a suffragette — and she is "prouder of it than I ever was of anything or ever shall be again" [005-081]. For Shaw, guilt properly understood is not about social disgrace but about failures of courage, honesty, and self-knowledge.

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period saw a significant shift in moral discourse. The Victorian language of sin and guilt was being challenged on multiple fronts: by Fabian socialism (which reframed moral problems as social problems), by the suffragette movement (which redefined female transgression as political action), and by secularisation (which eroded the theological framework of sin and redemption). Shaw, as a leading Fabian, was at the centre of these debates.

The Salvation Army — the organisation whose meeting Margaret attends before her transgression — represented a distinctive Edwardian response to guilt: evangelical activism that combined the language of sin with practical social work. Margaret's experience both draws on and subverts this tradition. She goes from a Salvation Army meeting to a police cell; the "descent into hell" she describes is framed in Salvationist language but leads to a conclusion — "nothing is worth doing but redeeming people from it" — that is more social-democratic than evangelical.

The play's treatment of guilt also reflects the 1911 context of prison reform. The imprisonment of suffragettes had made prison conditions a matter of public debate. Margaret's detailed account of police brutality — "They kicked me with their knees; they twisted my arms; they taunted and insulted me" [003-089] — is not merely dramatic colour but a political intervention. In this context, the question of who is guilty — the young woman who seeks a night of freedom or the policemen who brutalise her — becomes urgently political.
