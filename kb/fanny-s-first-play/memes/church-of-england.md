## Overview

The Church of [[england|England]], as the established church, hovers over *Fanny's First Play* as an implied moral authority—the institution that defines respectability for the Gilbeys and Knoxes, and whose teachings they invoke while systematically violating their spirit. Shaw, a lifelong critic of established religion, embeds the Church in the play's fabric of [[hypocrisy]].

## In the Play

The Church's presence in the play is more atmospheric than explicit. The Gilbeys' terror of social disgrace, the Knoxes' rigid morality, and the Induction critics' conviction that plays should be "improving" all derive from a cultural framework shaped by the established Church.

The Gilbeys' religious observance is suggested by their connection to Monsignor Grenfell [001-013], a Catholic clergyman whose letter from New York triggers the play's opening crisis. That the respectable Protestant Gilbeys would be deferential to a Catholic monsignor is one of Shaw's quiet jokes about the ecumenical reach of English [[class]] anxiety.

When Mrs. Knox declares that "in a Christian country it is the duty of every father to turn his daughter out of doors" if she disobeys, she invokes a specifically Anglican fusion of propriety and Christianity that Shaw found both absurd and destructive. The Church of England, in Shaw's rendering, is not a spiritual institution but a department of social policing.

## Shaw's Connection

Shaw's critique of the Church of England runs throughout his work. As a Fabian socialist and a member of the intellectual avant-garde, he saw the established Church as an instrument of social control rather than spiritual liberation. The Preface to *Fanny's First Play* discusses the cultural conservatism that the Church underwrites, including the censorship regime that the play's anonymous production sidesteps [P01-002].

## Key References

The play's most pointed religious satire is indirect: the contrast between the characters' professed Christianity and their actual behaviour. The critics who demand that plays be "improving" [P01-002] are the play's truest portrait of the Church of England's cultural influence: an institution that values propriety over truth.
