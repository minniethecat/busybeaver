## Definition

Education in *Fanny's First Play* operates on two levels: the formal education represented by [[cambridge-university|Cambridge University]] and the informal moral education that the young characters acquire through experience — particularly the experience of going to [[crime-and-punishment|prison]]. Shaw contrasts the pretensions of academic culture (the [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]], classical education, critical jargon) with the genuine learning that comes from transgression and suffering.

## In the Play

The Induction is saturated with Cambridge references. [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]] has just returned from "completing her education at Cambridge" — her father's old university (001-047). She speaks in the idiom of the [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]], deploying terms like "kittens" as intellectual classification (001-112)(001-115). Trotter, the Oxford-educated critic, sneers at "this Cambridge education" and insists that "if you had been educated at Oxford, you would know that the definition of a play" is something quite different (001-143)(001-147).

The Inductions Cambridge banter is Shaw's satire of intellectual fashion — the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]] jargon, the self-congratulation of the "bel esprit," the substitution of classification for thought. Yet Fanny's Cambridge education has also given her the courage to write a play that challenges respectable opinion.

In the inner play, education takes a different form. [[dora-delaney|Dora]] declares she has "educated Bobby a bit: he's not the mollycoddle he was" (002-092). [[margaret-knox|Margaret Knox]], the school prize-winner, discovers that "when I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free" (003-088). The real education happens not in classrooms but in [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] cells and on the street.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was largely self-educated — he left school at fifteen and acquired his formidable learning through reading, debate, and experience. His skepticism toward formal education runs through his work. In *Fanny's First Play*, Cambridge is both celebrated (as a site of intellectual ferment, embodied in the [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]]) and mocked (as a producer of jargon and pretension).

The deeper point is that respectability-based education — "the good conduct prize for three years running at school" (003-074) — produces conformity rather than character. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s [[crime-and-punishment|prison]] experience teaches her more about morality than her prize-winning school career ever did.

## Key Passages

- **Cambridge and its claims**: [[count-odowda|The Count]] explains that [[margaret-knox|Margaret]] "left me to complete her education at Cambridge. Cambridge was my own University" (001-047).
- **The Oxford put-down**: Trotter dismisses "Cambridge education" and insists on Oxford's superiority (001-143)(001-147).
- **The Fabian jargon**: "That's one of our classifications in the [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]]" — intellectual labeling as substitute for thought (001-112).
- **The kittens**: "I say again in the teeth of the whole [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]], kittens. Impertinent little kittens" — the Count's exasperated classification of the young (001-115).
- **[[dora-delaney|Dora]]'s education of Bobby**: "I've educated Bobby a bit: he's not the mollycoddle he was when you had him in hand" (002-092).
- **School versus freedom**: "When I was at home and at school I was what you call good; but I wasn't free" (003-088).
- **The immigrant's faith in English education**: Duvallet intends "to have my daughters educated in [[england|England]]. Nowhere else in the world but in [[england|England]] could I have met..." — a comic endorsement of English educational exceptionalism (004-402).

## Historical Context

[[cambridge-university|Cambridge University]] in the Edwardian period was still largely a finishing school for the upper classes, though reforms were slowly opening it to merit. Women could attend lectures but were not awarded degrees until 1948. The [[fabian-society|Fabian Society]], founded in 1884, was the intellectual engine of gradualist socialism, attracting many Cambridge intellectuals.

Shaw was a leading Fabian — he joined in 1884 and wrote *Fabian Essays in Socialism* (1889). His satire of the [[cambridge-fabian-society|Cambridge Fabian Society]] is thus self-satire: he knew the culture from the inside and understood both its virtues (intellectual seriousness) and its vices (jargon, self-importance, mandarin detachment).
