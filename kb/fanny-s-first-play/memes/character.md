## Definition

"Character" in *Fanny's First Play* is a word that the characters use constantly but rarely examine. It means, in the language of the Gilbey/Knox world, a reputation for respectability — the certification that one is fit for employment, for marriage, for social acceptance. But Shaw systematically exposes the gap between "character" in this social sense and character in the moral sense — between having a reputation and being a person of genuine integrity.

The word appears in two crucial contexts: employment (a servant must have "a character," meaning a reference from a previous employer) and marriage (a woman must not have her "character gone," meaning her sexual reputation). In both cases, the social meaning of "character" is shown to be a mechanism of control rather than a measure of worth.

## In the Play

### Character as Employment Reference

[[juggins|Juggins]]'s great speech in Act III turns on the word "character." Revealing his identity as the brother of a duke, he explains that "as an idle younger son, unable to support myself" he "could not have aspired to Miss Knox's hand." But "as a sober, honest, and industrious domestic servant, who has, I trust, given satisfaction to his employer ... I feel I am a man with a character" (004-435). The word does double work here: in its social sense, it means Juggins has a good employment record; in its moral sense, it means he has earned his self-respect through honest work.

The paradox is deliberate: Juggins's "character" — his reference, his reputation — is more real and more valuable than the inherited status of a duke's brother. Shaw is arguing that character must be achieved, not inherited.

### Character and Reputation

[[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] worries about Juggins's "character" in the narrow employment sense: "Do you want to lose him his character, Maria?" (004-215). But his own "character" — his reputation as a respectable tradesman — is revealed as a fragile construction that cannot survive his son's imprisonment. The play exposes the difference between having a social reputation and possessing genuine moral quality.

[[knox|Mr. Knox]] makes the same discovery. He has "worked hard to get a position and be respectable" — "character" as social standing — but his wife's quiet judgment is that "he has no powers inside himself to keep him steady" (004-287). His character is entirely external; remove the social scaffolding, and there is nothing left.

### Margaret's Character

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s imprisonment is, in the language of the play's world, the loss of her "character." As [[knox|Mr. Knox]] says, "who would have her with her character gone?" (004-426). But the play argues the reverse: Margaret's experience has given her a character — a self, a moral identity — that she never possessed when she was merely "good." Her declaration — "I did it out of the very depths of my nature. I did it because I'm that sort of person" (004-110) — is an assertion of character as something innate and authentic, not something conferred by social approval.

### The Critics on Dramatic Character

The Epilogue extends the theme to aesthetics. [[vaughan|Vaughan]] argues that the play cannot be by Shaw because "the characters in this play were quite distinguishable from one another. That proves it's not by Shaw, because all Shaw's characters are himself: mere puppets stuck up to spout Shaw" (005-046). The joke is multi-layered: the critics are themselves Shaw's characters, yet they are "quite distinguishable" from one another; their debate about whether the play has "character" in the dramatic sense parallels the inner play's debate about character in the moral sense.

## Shaw's Commentary

The Preface does not use the word "character" directly, but its argument is built on the distinction between reputation and reality. "Mere morality, or the substitution of custom for conscience" (P01-001) is precisely the substitution of social character for moral character. The play's "desperate advice" — get into trouble — is advice to risk one's social character in order to discover one's moral character.

Shaw's Fabianism informs this argument: character, like everything else in the social world, is a product of material conditions. Juggins earns his character through work; the Gilbeys and Knoxes purchase theirs through the performance of respectability. One is real, the other is not.

## Key Passages

- **A man with a character**: "I feel I am a man with a character" — Juggins transforming a social credential into a moral claim (004-435).
- **Character gone**: "Yes: if he'd have her with her character gone. But who would?" — Knox on his daughter's loss of social standing (004-426).
- **That sort of person**: "I did it out of the very depths of my nature. I did it because I'm that sort of person" — Margaret asserting authentic character (004-110).
- **Lose him his character**: "Do you want to lose him his character, Maria?" — Gilbey on Juggins's employment reference (004-215).
- **Distinguishable characters**: "the characters in this play were quite distinguishable from one another. That proves it's not by Shaw" — Vaughan's aesthetic judgment (005-046).

## Historical Context

The concept of "character" was central to Victorian and Edwardian moral discourse. Samuel Smiles's *Character* (1871) was one of the era's most influential books, arguing that character — understood as perseverance, honesty, and self-discipline — was the foundation of individual and national success. Employers required "characters" (references) from servants; marriage required "character" (sexual reputation) from brides.

Shaw's critique of this discourse was that it confused reputation with reality. The person with the best "character" in the social sense might be the person most skilled at concealment; the person with the best character in the moral sense might be the person society condemned. This was a radical argument in a culture that treated character as a measurable, certifiable quality.
