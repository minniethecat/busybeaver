## Definition

God is invoked repeatedly in *Fanny's First Play* — in oaths, in exclamations, in moments of genuine spiritual searching. The word appears in the Preface ("Out of the mouth of Mrs. Knox I have delivered on them the judgment of her God," P01-001), in the Induction ("God forbid that I should set up to be a judge," 001-101), and throughout the inner play. But the God of conventional religion is conspicuously absent — a silence that is itself a theological statement.

## In the Preface

Shaw's Preface uses "God" in the context of judgment. [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]], he says, delivers "the judgment of her God" on the respectable middle class (P01-001). The possessive pronoun is crucial: it is *her* God, not God in general. Each character in the play has their own conception of the divine — or none — and each conception reveals more about the character than about God.

## In the Induction

[[trotter|Trotter]] invokes God in protest against [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]]'s influence: "God forbid that I should set up to be a judge, or do more than record an impression; but my impressions can be influenced; and in this case you're influencing them shamelessly all the time" (001-101). The invocation of God is a rhetorical flourish — Trotter is not making a theological argument but a social one. He wants Fanny to stop charming him into liking the play before he sees it.

## In the Inner Play

The inner play's relationship to God is more complex. [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] is the most explicitly religious character: she goes to church, she prays, she invokes divine judgment on her wayward daughter. But her religion proves hollow when confronted with Margaret's honesty. "I went to the prison chapel," Margaret tells her, and the religious ritual that should have been a comfort becomes instead a trigger for breakdown (003-078).

[[knox|Joseph Knox]]'s final confession — "with all our respectability and piety, we've no real religion" (004-308) — is the play's most damning theological statement. The respectable classes have God without faith, religion without conviction, piety without God.

## Shaw's Theology

Shaw was a religious thinker who rejected orthodox Christianity. His concept of the Life Force — a creative evolutionary drive toward higher consciousness — was his answer to the death of conventional religion. *Fanny's First Play* does not articulate this theology explicitly, but it prepares the ground for it by demolishing the alternative. The God of the Gilbeys and Knoxes is a social function, not a living presence. Until that false god is dead, the real one cannot be born.

## Key Passages

- **Shaw's judgment**: "Out of the mouth of Mrs. Knox I have delivered on them the judgment of her God" (P01-001).
- **Trotter's prayer**: "God forbid that I should set up to be a judge" — the critic's rhetorical piety (001-101).
- **Knox's confession**: "We've no real religion" — the death of the respectable God (004-308).
- **The Count's century**: "To me the century I shut out will always be the nineteenth century" — the God of the past century (001-021).
