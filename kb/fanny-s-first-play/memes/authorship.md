## Definition

Authorship in *Fanny's First Play* operates on multiple levels.

Within the fiction, [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]] has written a play that she presents anonymously to a panel of critics. Outside the fiction, Shaw wrote the play and concealed his authorship — "a secret de Polichinelle" that was, as Shaw insists, "a necessary part of the play" (P01-003).

The question of authorship — who wrote this, and does it matter? — is both a plot device and a philosophical experiment in the relationship between artistic identity and critical judgment.

## In the Play

The Induction's framing establishes authorship as the central puzzle. [[fanny-odowda|Fanny O'Dowda]], a young woman just returned from [[education|Cambridge]], has written a play. Her father, [[count-odowda|Count O'Dowda]], is the aristocratic aesthete who commissioned a private performance. The critics — Trotter, Vaughan, Gunn, and [[flawner-bannal|Flawner Bannal]] — have been invited to judge the work without knowing its author.

This anonymity is the play's [[art-and-theatre|drama]]tic engine. The critics, deprived of the author's name, cannot agree on what they have seen. Is it a comedy? A morality play? A socialist tract? An impertinence? Without the author to anchor their judgment, their critical frameworks collapse into contradiction. Shaw's point is that criticism depends on reputation — that the same play, attributed to different authors, would receive different judgments.

The Epilogue confirms the experiment's result. The critics, having learned the author's identity (or think they have — Shaw teases multiple possibilities), retroactively adjust their judgments. Authorship, the play demonstrates, is the critic's crutch.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's own experience made this experiment personal. As a critic, he had seen how reputation determined reception — how the same ideas, expressed by a famous name, were received differently than when expressed by an unknown. The concealment of *Fanny's First Play*'s authorship was a test of this proposition, and the result — the critics' contradictory judgments, their retroactive adjustments — confirmed his skepticism.

The Preface details the experiment with Shaw's characteristic mix of self-congratulation and irony. [[trotter|Mr. Trotter]] "forgave me beforehand, and assisted the play in every way" — the critic lampooned in the Induction became the play's ally, proving that critics cannot recognize themselves in satire (P01-002).

## Key Passages

- **The necessary concealment**: "The concealment of the authorship... was a necessary part of the play" (P01-003).
- **The open secret**: "If a secret de Polichinelle can be said to involve concealment" — Shaw's ironic admission that the anonymity was never absolute (P01-003).
- **The critics lampooned**: "The critics whom I have lampooned in the induction to this play under the names of Trotter, Vaughan, and Gunn will forgive me" (P01-002).
- **Trotter's forgiveness**: "[[trotter|Mr. Trotter]] forgave me beforehand, and assisted the play in every way" (P01-002).

## Historical Context

Anonymous and pseudonymous publication had a long history in English letters — from the eighteenth-century practice of anonymous pamphleteering to the Victorian convention of anonymous reviewing. By the Edwardian period, the convention was fading: authors increasingly claimed their work, and "celebrity authorship" was a recognized phenomenon.

Shaw was himself a celebrity author by 1911 — his name was a brand. The experiment of anonymous production was thus a genuine departure: could the most famous playwright in [[england|England]] write a play that would be judged without reference to his fame? The answer, Shaw suggests, was no — and the failure was the critics', not the play's.
