## Definition

Adulthood in *Fanny's First Play* is not a matter of age but of moral independence.

The play's young characters — [[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]], [[margaret-knox|Margaret]], [[dora-delaney|Dora]], and [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] — come of age by breaking with their parents' moral authority rather than by reaching a certain birthday.

Adulthood, in Shaw's conception, is the achievement of one's own ethical judgment — the refusal to be governed by convention, [[family]], or fear.

## The Failed Adults

The play's older generation has reached biological adulthood without achieving moral maturity. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] is a middle-aged man who governs his household by bluster and sentiment. [[knox|Joseph Knox]] is a father who cannot face his own hypocrisy. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] and [[mrs-knox|Mrs. Knox]] are mothers whose moral authority is a function of anxiety, not wisdom. None of them has achieved genuine adulthood in Shaw's sense: they obey convention, not conscience.

## The Young Adults

The play's young characters achieve adulthood through crisis. [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s night in [[prison]] forces her to confront the gap between her parents' morality and her own experience. Her refusal to be ashamed — her insistence that she has done nothing wrong — is an assertion of adult moral independence. She is no longer her parents' child.

[[bobby-gilbey|Bobby]]'s adulthood is quieter but no less real. He returns from his fortnight's absence not as a penitent son but as a young man who has had an experience his parents cannot understand. His refusal to perform the role of the guilty child is a declaration of independence.

[[dora-delaney|Dora]] has been an adult from the beginning — possibly since childhood. She is one of Shaw's "unwomanly" women: self-sufficient, unsentimental, immune to shame. Her adulthood is not an achievement but a condition — the natural state of someone who has never learned to be a child.

## Key Passages

- **Margaret's independence**: "I'm not afraid of you" — the declaration of adult autonomy (003-092).
- **Bobby's return**: The son who will not play the penitent (002-081 onwards).
- **The parents' failure**: Knox's confession that he has no real religion — the admission of failed adulthood (004-308).
