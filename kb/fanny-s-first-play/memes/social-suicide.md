## Definition

"Social suicide" — the loss of [[respectability]], the destruction of one's reputation — is the spectre that haunts the Gilbey and Knox households. For the older generation, it is the worst fate imaginable: to be talked about, to be shamed, to lose the good opinion of one's neighbours. Shaw's argument is that this fear is itself a form of death — and that the young people who have experienced "disgrace" are actually more alive than their parents who live in terror of it.

## In the Play

The play's comedy depends on the gap between the older generation's terror of scandal and the younger generation's indifference to it. [[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]] cannot bear the thought that his son might be in [[prison]] — not because prison is terrible but because it is shameful. [[mrs-gilbey|Mrs. Gilbey]] cannot say "fourteen days" — the standard sentence for drunk and disorderly conduct — because the phrase itself is contaminating.

[[margaret-knox|Margaret]] has been through the worst that respectability can threaten: she has been arrested, imprisoned, and exposed. And she has emerged not destroyed but transformed. "I'm not afraid of you," she tells her mother (003-092). The social death that her parents fear so desperately has already happened — and she is still standing. This is Shaw's most radical proposition: that the fear of social death is worse than social death itself.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw's Preface offers "the desperate advice: Do something that will get you into trouble" (P01-001). This is advice to commit social suicide — to risk the loss of respectability in pursuit of integrity. The play's young characters have followed this advice, whether they knew it or not. Bobby got drunk and assaulted a policeman. Margaret went to prison. Dora has been "hardened" by experience. And all three are more morally alive than their parents.

## Key Passages

- **Shaw's advice**: "Do something that will get you into trouble" — the invitation to social suicide (P01-001).
- **Margaret's defiance**: "I'm not afraid of you" — surviving the social death (003-092).
- **Gilbey's terror**: "It's got on my nerves so, that I'm hardly responsible for what I say" — the father's fear of disgrace (002-022).
