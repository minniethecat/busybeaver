## Role

Mr. Robin Gilbey is a supporting character in the inner play, appearing in the Induction and Acts I–III. He is the patriarch of the Gilbey household in [[denmark-hill]], a shopkeeper whose entire identity rests on respectability. Shaw uses him to expose the hollowness of lower-middle-[[class]] morality: Gilbey's outrage at his son Bobby's marriage to a woman with a [[prison-setting]] record is driven not by genuine moral concern but by terror of social disgrace.

## In the Play

Gilbey first appears at the breakfast table in the Induction, bursting into the room in agitation over a letter:

> [001-005] | Gilbey | *Grinding his teeth.* This is a nice thing. This is a b— —

His wife Maria, a placid woman who has long managed her husband's temper, cuts him short [001-006]. The letter—from Monsignor Grenfell in New York—is withdrawing support, and Gilbey immediately blames his wife: "Yes, put me in the wrong as usual. Take your boy's part against me" [001-007].

When Bobby finally returns home with the news that he has spent two weeks in prison, Gilbey's reaction is characteristic: he is less concerned with his son's moral condition than with the social consequences. His refrain—"My boy is to marry this woman and be a social outcast!"—captures the shallow calculus of respectability that Shaw satirises throughout the play.

## Character Analysis

Gilbey represents the petty tyranny of respectability. He cannot think beyond the social ledger: what will the neighbours say? Will the business suffer? His moral universe is bounded entirely by Denmark Hill and the opinions of his class. Shaw contrasts Gilbey's frantic self-interest with his wife Maria's steady, practical wisdom, and with Margaret Knox's daring embrace of her own disgrace.

The Gilbeys' class anxiety is particularly evident in their attitude toward the Knox family. Gilbey is both deferential to and resentful of the Knoxes' slightly higher social standing, making the eventual rapprochement between the families all the more comedically satisfying.

## Key Passages

Gilbey's dialogue is concentrated in the Induction [001-005] through [001-030] and Act I [001-031] through [001-065]. His most revealing speeches concern not Bobby's welfare but the damage to the family's reputation.
