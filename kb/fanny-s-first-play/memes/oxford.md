## Setting

Oxford does not appear as a physical setting in *Fanny's First Play*, but it functions as a potent cultural symbol invoked twice — first in the Induction as an emblem of classical authority, and second in Act II as a metonym for elite student privilege. These two invocations frame Oxford at opposite ends of the play's thematic spectrum: as the seat of ancient wisdom and as the source of drunken disorder.

## In the Play

### Induction: Trotter's Pedantry

When [[fanny-odowda|Fanny]] claims a classical education, [[trotter|Trotter]] dismisses her with "Pooh! Cambridge! If you had been educated at Oxford, you would know that the definition of a play has been settled exactly and scientifically for two thousand two hundred and sixty years. When I say that these entertainments are not plays, I don't mean in my sense of the word, but in the sense given to it for all time by the immortal Stagirite" [001-105]. When Fanny admits she has never heard of "the Stagirite" (Aristotle), Trotter is delighted: "And this is Cambridge education! Well, my dear young lady, I'm delighted to find there's something you don't know; and I shan't spoil you by dispelling an ignorance which, in my opinion, is highly becoming to your age and sex" [001-107].

Oxford here functions as a conservative intellectual club: the repository of Aristotelian authority, deployed by an aging male critic to patronize a young woman with Cambridge connections. The joke is at Trotter's expense — his appeal to a two-thousand-year-old definition is absurdly pedantic — but the Oxford-Cambridge rivalry is a real cultural fault line that Shaw exploits for comic effect.

### Act II: The Boat Race Riot

Margaret Knox's long confession to her mother includes the following: "at last it got spoilt by the Oxford and Cambridge students up for the boat race. They got drunk; and they began to smash things; and the police came in. Then it was quite horrible. The students fought with the police; and the police suddenly got quite brutal, and began to throw everybody downstairs" [002-095].

Here Oxford and Cambridge appear together, not in rivalry but in complicity, as emblems of privileged male hooliganism. The annual Boat Race between Oxford and Cambridge was already notorious in Edwardian London as an occasion for public drunkenness among the university set. Margaret's night of freedom — her dance with Duvallet, her champagne, her brief taste of joy — is destroyed by the rowdiness of elite young men whose disorder provokes a police response that sweeps up innocent bystanders.

## Dramatic Function

The two Oxford references bookend the play's critique of institutional authority. Trotter's Oxford represents the dead hand of the past — "settled exactly and scientifically for two thousand two hundred and sixty years" — used to police what can and cannot be called a play. The Boat Race Oxford represents the anarchy of privilege — young men whose social position licenses them to "smash things" while Margaret goes to prison for the crime of being in the wrong place at the wrong time. Together, they indict a culture that venerates ancient authority while tolerating modern brutality.

## Key Passages

- **Trotter's appeal to Oxford authority**: "If you had been educated at Oxford, you would know that the definition of a play has been settled exactly and scientifically for two thousand two hundred and sixty years" [001-105].
- **The Stagirite**: "And this is Cambridge education! Well, my dear young lady, I'm delighted to find there's something you don't know" [001-107].
- **The Boat Race violence**: "at last it got spoilt by the Oxford and Cambridge students up for the boat race. They got drunk; and they began to smash things; and the police came in" [002-095].

## Real-World Context

The Oxford-Cambridge Boat Race, first held in 1829, was by the Edwardian period a major fixture of the London social calendar. Contemporary newspapers regularly reported on the drunkenness and disorder associated with race night. Shaw's inclusion of this detail anchors Margaret's story in recognizable social reality while also making a pointed comment about class and justice: the Oxford and Cambridge students who "got drunk and began to smash things" presumably went back to their colleges, while Margaret Knox went to Holloway.
