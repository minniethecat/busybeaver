## Location

Camberwell is a district in south London, situated south of the Thames and adjacent to more affluent areas such as Denmark Hill. In the early twentieth century, Camberwell was predominantly working-class and lower-middle-class, in contrast to the aspirational respectability of Denmark Hill, where the Gilbey and Knox families reside.

## In the Play

Camberwell appears in the play not as a setting but as a social marker. Bobby Gilbey, asserting his class consciousness, declares: "Denmark Hill isn't Camberwell, you know" [002-560]. This distinction is central to the play's exploration of class identity — the Gilbeys and Knoxes have achieved a measure of suburban respectability but remain anxious about their proximity to less genteel neighborhoods.

Juggins, the philosophical footman, deploys "Camberwellian" as an adjective for social pretension and insincerity. He observes that "if you tell the young lady that you want to jilt her, and she calls you a pig, the tone of the transaction may leave much to be desired; but it'll be less Camberwellian than if you say you're not worthy" [002-561]. In Juggins's usage, "Camberwellian" signifies the middle-class habit of wrapping unpleasant truths in polite circumlocution — a form of dishonesty disguised as good manners.

## Social Significance

The Camberwell/Denmark Hill contrast maps onto the play's broader critique of respectability. Denmark Hill represents achieved respectability — a neighborhood for families who have risen above their origins. Camberwell represents those origins themselves, the class reality that respectability seeks to escape. Bobby's defensive assertion that Denmark Hill "isn't Camberwell" reveals the fragility of this social positioning — the constant fear of slipping back into a less respectable class identity.
