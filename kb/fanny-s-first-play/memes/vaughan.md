## Role

Mr. Vaughan is a minor character in the Induction of *Fanny's First Play*. He is one of the critics assembled to judge Fanny's anonymous play, representing the more intellectual wing of the critical establishment—less overtly moralistic than Trotter but no less blind to the play's satirical intent.

## In the Play

Vaughan appears alongside Trotter, Gunn, and [[flawner-bannal]] in the Induction. His responses to the play are more measured than Trotter's—he tries to engage with it intellectually—but he ultimately shares the same inability to see that the satire is directed at people like himself. His intellectual apparatus becomes, in Shaw's hands, simply a more sophisticated form of evasion.

## Character Analysis

Vaughan represents the critic who prides himself on his learning and discernment. Where Trotter reacts with moral outrage, Vaughan reacts with intellectual condescension. Both responses are forms of the same fundamental failure: the refusal to allow a play to speak [[truth]] that disturbs the critic's own self-image.

Shaw's treatment of Vaughan is gentler than his treatment of Trotter—Vaughan at least tries to engage with ideas—but the ultimate verdict is the same. The Induction critics collectively demonstrate that the English critical tradition, whether moralistic or intellectual, cannot accommodate art that tells uncomfortable truths.

## Key Passages

Vaughan's dialogue, like that of the other Induction critics, is found in the frame narrative. His exchanges with the group illuminate the varieties of critical blindness that Shaw spent his career documenting.
