## Definition

Noise in *Fanny's First Play* is the audible sign of vitality — and, for the respectable, of threat. The Count flees from the "Cockney twang" (001-072); the respectable households try to maintain quiet order; but the play's movement is toward more noise — Dora's concertina, the dancing, the shouting, the laughter. Shaw sides with noise against silence, with vitality against order.

## In the Play

The Count's objection to "the Cockney twang" is the play's most explicit statement of the noise/refinement binary. For the Count, noise is class: the sound of the people he has spent his life avoiding. His retreat to Venice is a retreat from English noise into Italian silence — or rather, into an Italian soundscape he has curated to his taste.

Dora's concertina — the "squiffer" — is noise as liberation. When "a concertina adds its music to the revelry" (004-882), the respectable Gilbey household is invaded by popular, democratic sound. The squiffer is the antithesis of the Count's Cimarosa — it is music anyone can make, noise anyone can dance to.

Margaret's account of her night out is full of sound: singing hymns, the theatre's music, the noise of the dance hall, the crash of the students' rampage, the shouted insults of the police. This sonic journey from sacred music to street noise is her moral education.

## Key Passages

- **The Count on Cockney noise**: "My ears are offended by the Cockney twang" (001-072).
- **The concertina**: "A concertina adds its music to the revelry" (004-882).
- **Margaret's sonic journey**: From hymn-singing to the noise of police violence (003-471–477).
