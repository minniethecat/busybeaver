## Definition

Reason in *Fanny's First Play* is an ideal everywhere invoked and nowhere realized. Characters appeal to "right reason" as a standard of judgment, cite "sound sense" as a guide to conduct, and claim that their positions "stand to reason" — but the play's comedy arises from the gap between these appeals and the irrationality of actual behavior. Shaw's argument is not that reason is worthless but that genuine reason is harder to achieve than its invocation suggests, and that most of what passes for reason is [[respectability]] dressed in rational language.

The Preface frames the play's moral argument in the vocabulary of the Enlightenment: "reasonable consideration for other peoples' feelings and interests" is the standard Shaw proposes to replace "mere morality" (P01-001). The play then tests this standard against the messiness of actual human conduct and finds it aspirational — worth striving for, rarely attained.

## In the Play

### Right Reason as Rhetorical Weapon

[[gilbey|Mr. Gilbey]]'s desperate rhetorical question — "Where's the police? Where's the Government? Where's the Church? Where's respectability and right reason?" (002-117) — is the play's most concentrated invocation of reason as a social force. He appeals to "right reason" as if it were an institution alongside the police and the Church, a guarantor of order. But his appeal is itself irrational — a cry of panic dressed in the language of rationality.

The phrase "right reason" is telling. It is not reason in the neutral, Enlightenment sense — the faculty of logical thought available to all — but reason with a moral qualifier, reason that has been aligned with the right (respectable) conclusions before it even begins. This is reason as ideology, reason as the justification of existing arrangements rather than their examination.

### Reason as Common Sense

[[juggins|Juggins]] appeals to reason in a different register. When he explains his decision to leave the Gilbeys' service, his brother's objection is met with: "There is sound sense in that, sir" (004-220). Reason here is practical judgment — the ability to assess a situation and act appropriately. Juggins is the play's most reasonable character because his reason is grounded in experience rather than convention.

But even Juggins's reason has limits. His advice to Bobby — "Marry her, sir, or behave like a cad" (004-028) — is logically sound but emotionally inadequate. It is reason without feeling, and the play suggests that this, too, is a form of irrationality — the irrationality of treating human beings as logical propositions.

### The Critics and Reason

The Epilogue's critics exemplify the failure of reason in the domain where it should be most at home. [[flawner-bannal|Bannal]]'s method — "If it's by a good author, it's a good play, naturally. That stands to reason" (005-025) — is a parody of reasoning. The word "naturally" does the work of an argument that does not exist; "that stands to reason" is an appeal to a rational process that has not occurred.

[[gunn|Gunn]]'s dismissal — "Rubbish!" — and [[vaughan|Vaughan]]'s — "Rot!" — are not arguments but reflexes. The critics, who should be the play's representatives of reason, are its most conspicuous failures of rationality. Their judgments are dictated by fashion, prejudice, and professional rivalry, not by the exercise of critical intelligence.

### Reason and Passion

The play's treatment of the relationship between [[reason]] and [[passion]] is one of its most characteristic Shavian moves. The critics debate whether Shaw (or the play's anonymous author) is "Intellect without emotion ... a giant brain, if you ask me; but no heart" (005-041). But the inner play demonstrates that reason and passion are not opposed: [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s rebellion is both rational (a clear-eyed rejection of hypocrisy) and passionate ("out of the very depths of my nature").

Shaw's position, dramatized rather than stated, is that reason without passion is sterile and passion without reason is destructive. The integration of the two — reason energized by passion, passion disciplined by reason — is the condition of genuine moral life.

## Shaw's Commentary

Shaw was a child of the Enlightenment, committed to the power of rational argument to improve human life. His plays are arguments — structured, logical, proceeding by thesis and antithesis toward synthesis. But he was also a satirist, acutely aware of how rarely reason governs human affairs.

The Preface's call for "reasonable consideration" is genuine, but the play demonstrates how difficult such consideration is to achieve. The characters who invoke reason most loudly are the least reasonable; the characters who are most reasonable — Juggins, Mrs. Knox, Margaret — invoke it least. Reason, Shaw suggests, is not a possession but a discipline, and most people lack either the capacity or the courage to practice it.

## Key Passages

- **Right reason**: "Where's the police? Where's the Government? Where's the Church? Where's respectability and right reason?" — Gilbey's invocation of reason as social guarantor (002-117).
- **That stands to reason**: "If it's by a good author, it's a good play, naturally. That stands to reason" — Bannal's parody of rational judgment (005-025).
- **Sound sense**: "There is sound sense in that, sir" — Juggins acknowledging the practical rationality of his brother's position (004-220).
- **Intellect without emotion**: "A giant brain, if you ask me; but no heart" — Bannal on the Shaw reputation that the play contests (005-041).
- **Reasonable consideration**: "loss of respectability with integrity of self-respect and reasonable consideration for other peoples' feelings and interests" — Shaw's formula for ethical conduct (P01-001).

## Historical Context

The Edwardian period inherited from the Victorians a powerful faith in reason as the engine of progress — scientific, social, and moral. The triumphs of nineteenth-century science, the reform of institutions along rational lines, and the secularization of intellectual life all contributed to a culture in which "reason" was the highest term of praise.

But this faith was also under attack. The emerging disciplines of psychology and anthropology were revealing the irrational foundations of human behavior. The aesthetic movement championed beauty against utility. And political movements — suffragism, socialism, Irish nationalism — challenged the "reasonable" arrangements of the existing order.

Shaw's treatment of reason reflects this ambivalence. He is a rationalist who knows that reason is rare and that most claims to reason are fraudulent. His plays argue for reason while dramatizing its absence — a project that is both Enlightenment and modernist, both Victorian and twentieth-century.
