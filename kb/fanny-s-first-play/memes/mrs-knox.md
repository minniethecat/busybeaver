## Role
Mrs. Knox is the maternal counterpart to her husband's paternal authority — a woman whose moral framework is simpler but no less rigid than his. Her insistence on marriage as the solution to every problem ("He ought to marry her whether or no," 004-412) represents the reduction of morality to social formula.

## In the Play
Mrs. Knox appears in Acts III and IV as a voice of maternal concern that shades into moral absolutism. She is horrified by [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s [[crime-and-punishment|imprisonment]] but more horrified by the threat to [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]'s marriageability. Her exchange with Duvallet — "If it's not proper for her to say, it's not proper for a man to say, either" (004-401) — displays a moral consistency that her husband lacks.

## Character Analysis
Mrs. Knox is Shaw's study in the limitations of maternal morality. She loves her daughter genuinely — but the only tools her moral universe provides for expressing that love are marriage, propriety, and the restoration of appearances.

## Key Passages
- **The marriage imperative**: "He ought to marry her whether or no" (004-412).
- **The propriety defense**: "If it's not proper for her to say, it's not proper for a man to say, either" (004-401).
- **Marry [[margaret-knox|Margaret]]!**: Her shock at the suggestion [[juggins|Juggins]] might marry her daughter (004-433).
