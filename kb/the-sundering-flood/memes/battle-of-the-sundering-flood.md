# Battle of the Sundering Flood

A series of armed engagements fought along and across the [[sundering-flood]] river, culminating in the great battle of May 1st that broke the Barons' League and established Sir Godrick's dominance over the region (021-002) (045-010).

## Context

The Sundering Flood was a swift, uncrossable river that divided the eastern and western lands (001-004). The eastern side held [[wethermel]], the home of Osberne, while the western side held Hartshaw Knolls, home of [[elfhild]] (002-001) (011-001). The region was under threat from the Barons' League, a coalition of robber-barons who sought to dominate the free dales and towns along the river (021-002). These barons were, as Sir Godrick observed, "but the catspaw of the King and the City and the tyrannous [[porte-of-eastcheaping|Porte]]" — proxies for a larger power that dared not act openly (045-005).

The war arose shortly after Marymass, when Sir Godrick learned that "war, and big war" had arisen against Longshaw (045-005).

## Phases of the War

### Northern Campaign (Osberne's Command)

Sir Godrick summoned Osberne and "talks long with him," then dispatched him on a critical errand: to seek out the northernmost hosting of the Barons' League and break it before it could join the main Barons' host on Sir Godrick's flank (045-006). Osberne led thirteen hundred men against a force of six thousand and utterly routed them — "they scatter them to the winds so that they can never come together again, and all their munitions of war and matters for feeding and wending are destroyed" (045-007).

### The Great Battle (May 1st)

Having shattered the northern host, Osberne turned not back to Longshaw but rode directly to where "he wots the great battle shall be." On the very eve of battle, he rode into Sir Godrick's camp bearing "the taken banners and such spoil as was not over-heavy to ride with" (045-007). The arrival of Osberne and his trophies so heartened Sir Godrick's host that "no man there was of Sir Godrick's but he knew full surely that the victory would be theirs on the morrow" (045-007).

The great battle was "fully foughten on the first of May" (045-010). The Barons' forces, seeing the captured banners of their northern allies paraded before them, knew they would receive no reinforcement. Mocked by the Longshaw men who "bade them come across to serve under such and such a banner," their morale broke (045-008). By noontide the Barons' host was utterly routed — "at noontide there was no host left that at matins had been as great and goodly an host as ever was seen in those parts" (045-008).

Sir Godrick embraced Osberne before all the host and declared, "Here is one shall lead you when I am slain" (045-007).

### Reduction of the Strongholds

After the great battle, Sir Godrick did not rest. The Barons' League sent a herald suing for peace within half a month, but Sir Godrick demanded unconditional surrender of all their strengths (045-010). When they refused, he "rose up and led a host against the castle of the greatest of these Barons, and took it in ten days, after much loss of his men" (045-010). One by one the strongholds fell, and "before the winter came he had all he wanted, and most of the Barons captive at Longshaw" (045-010).

## Osberne's Prowess

Osberne earned the name the Red Lad for his red hair and his ferocity in battle (039-021). He wielded a [[dwarfs|dwarf-wrought]] bow of exceptional power, crafted by the Dwarfs who inhabited the eastern mountains (040-009). His tactical acumen, developed from years of defending Wethermel against wolves and outlaws (003-001) (005-008), proved decisive: his destruction of the northern hosting with thirteen hundred men against six thousand was the strategic stroke that made the great victory possible (045-007).

## Outcome and Aftermath

The war ended with the dissolution of the Barons' League. The remnants sued for peace after Yule, and Sir Godrick established a lasting peace on his own terms (045-011) (045-012). The King and the Porte of the Great City saw "their purpose broken, and they must sit still and do nothing" (045-009).

Osberne's reputation as the Red Lad spread throughout the Dales. His name alone was enough to scatter hostile forces years after the war had ended (067-005). The peace allowed for the eventual reunion of Osberne and Elfhild, who had been separated by the Sundering Flood throughout their youth: at Wethermel, five years after his departure, Osberne at last presented Elfhild to his folk as the woman he had won (065-001) (010-013).
