# Sundering Flood

The Sundering Flood is the great river at the heart of William Morris's novel — a mighty waterway running north to south from its wells in the high mountains to the sea at the City of the Sundering Flood, its impassable waters cleaving the East and West Dales and shaping every aspect of life along its course.

## Geography

The Sundering Flood rises in the northern mountains, where it comes forth in three great forces and many smaller ones, filling the mountain ghyll with a torrent so perilous that none has ever dared cast a bridge across it (001-005). From there it flows southward: first through a great waste of rocks and sand some sixty miles across where the flood runs wide, shallow, and unspeakably swift, and neither tree nor bush nor grass grows (001-006). Emerging from this waste, the river gathers into straiter compass, becoming deep and exceeding strong, flowing between sheer black rock banks twenty feet and upward on either side (001-007). As it rises toward the northern mountains the land betters, with birch and quicken trees in the nooks, and the best grass growing nigh unto the river's lips (001-008). The river runs nigh due north to south (001-008).

Below the Dale, the flood of tide runs far up the river — hard on a hundred miles from the sea — so that seagoing dromonds and round-ships may fare up it, their yards all but touching the windows of husbandmen's steads (001-002). When the tide fails, the river remains a noble and wide-spreading water, navigable by barges and lesser keels either under sail or tracked up by horses and bullocks (001-002). At the river's mouth lies the City of the Sundering Flood, a great and rich city (001-001, 001-004).

The land is ever better and the getting more on the east side of the Sundering Flood than on the west (001-008). Yet folk dwell on both sides, not utterly cut off from the world outside the Dale — by climbing the bents one may reach cheaping-towns some forty miles from the west bank and fifty from the east (001-009). Many lesser rivers fall into the Flood along its course (001-003).

## Significance as Barrier

For all the river's commercial life lower down, up in the Dale the Sundering Flood is true to its name: nought may cross its waters undrowned save the fowl flying (001-010). The waters run deep and swift between high black-rock banks, a barrier so absolute that the flooding of tide never lays it with ice even in winter — for the frost in that land is not very hard, and the waters never freeze over (006-002).

All dealings between the folk of the East Dale and the West are reduced to shouting and crying across the swirling and gurgling eddies of the black water, which themselves seem to talk together in some dread and unknown tongue (001-010). Never may any man on the east touch the hand of any on the west, save by some strange wandering far from the Dale (001-011). No race of folk who have ever dwelt in the Dale from the beginning of the world have ever won across the Sundering Flood (014-003).

Yet paradoxically, it is at this very barrier that Osberne and Elfhild first meet, talk, and fall in love. The Bight of the Cloven Knoll — where the western bank thrusts forth into a sharp ness, narrowing the river to some thirty feet — becomes their trysting-place (009-003). Here, on opposite banks, they speak, sing, and exchange gifts across the water, the very impossibility of crossing making their bond the more precious (010-010, 012-001).

Osberne's child's heart experiences the river as both connection and torment: at times the Sundering Flood seems to him "like the coils of a deadly serpent which was strangling the life out of him" (012-001). At other moments, lost in the sweetness of their talk, "they both forgat the Sundering Flood, and how they should never meet" (018-008).

## Crossings

No bridge spans the Sundering Flood. None has ever been so bold as to attempt one, for the river is perilous and awful to behold, filling the mountain ghyll so that there is no foothold for man or goat (001-005). No ford exists. The river cannot be swum — when Osberne, feeling his might eked by [[steelhead]]'s gifts, declares he could swim the Flood, the hillman warns him against it, for none have ever won across (014-003). Even in winter the Flood never freezes over, for the frost is not hard enough to lay it with ice (006-002).

The only means of "crossing" is to go around — to encompass the Sundering Flood by journeying far from the Dale, whether by climbing the mountain-necks east or west to reach the outside trading towns (001-009), or by following the river far south beyond the Wood Masterless. This is the great quest that Osberne undertakes: to "compass earth and sea till I have gone about the Sundering Flood" and find Elfhild on the other side (022-006). His journey southward through the Wood Masterless, to Longshaw, and eventually to the City of the Sundering Flood is the narrative realization of this geographic necessity (039-007, 046-007).

Down below, where the river widens and becomes navigable, it ceases to be a barrier — it becomes a highway. [[sir-godrick]] tells Osberne that in the Masterless Wood, the Flood "is a mighty great river, whereon are barges and cutters and seagoing dromonds even, so that it sunders nought, but joins rather" (039-007). The folk of the lower river love their waterway and jest that it is no sunderer but a uniter, calling it the wet Highway of the Flood (001-004).

## Role in the Narrative

The Sundering Flood is the novel's central geographic fact and its driving narrative engine. Every major plot development is shaped by the river's presence:

- **Discovery and First Love**. Osberne, following Steelhead's counsel to seek the side of the Sundering Flood (007-014), discovers the Bight of the Cloven Knoll and meets Elfhild on the far bank — the defining relationship of the novel begins across these waters (009-003, 009-005).

- **The Tryst**. For years, Osberne and Elfhild meet at the Bight of the Cloven Knoll, exchanging gifts shot across the water by bow (011-010, 012-002, 020-001). These trysts form the emotional core of the novel's early chapters.

- **The Battle of the Sundering Flood**. When the strong-thieves ([[black-skinners]]) raid the West Dale, the Eastdalers under Osberne line the eastern bank and fight a shot-battle across the Flood (032-004, 032-006). The Flood's width makes spear-shot impossible, and the battle becomes a matter of archery and sling-spears (032-006). Some of the foemen, so fierce and eager, rush heedlessly into the boil of waters and are lost in the bottomless depths (032-015). The Eastdalers deem "nought evil save the sundering of the Flood" (032-016). Seventy-seven thieves are slain in the shot-stour (034-011).

- **Separation and the Quest**. Elfhild is carried off by the Black Skinners, and Osberne's quest to find her is fundamentally a quest to encompass the Sundering Flood — to go around it and reach the western side (022-006, 035-012). His journey south with [[sir-medard]] and later with Sir Godrick is the long way around.

- **Reunion**. Osberne and Elfhild are ultimately reunited not by bridging the Flood but by both having wandered wide in the world, meeting far from the Dale (052-001). When they return to Wethermel together in the final chapter, they walk along the very lip of the Sundering Flood on "the crown of all midsummer days" (066-001).

## Symbolism

The Sundering Flood operates on multiple symbolic levels. At its simplest, it is a barrier — the physical embodiment of separation between lovers, between peoples, between the known and the unknown. Yet Morris complicates this reading from the outset: the folk of the lower river insist the name is a misnomer, that the Flood is "no sunderer but a uniter; that it joined land to land and shore to shore; that it had peopled the wilderness and made the waste places blossom" (001-004).

The river thus embodies a central paradox: it is both what keeps the lovers apart and what brings them together. Without the Flood, Osberne would have no reason to call across the water to a strange maiden; their love is literally born of the barrier that separates them. The Flood gives them their trysting-place, their shared ritual of [[gift-giving]] across the water, and eventually the great quest that transforms Osberne from a shepherd boy into the [[red-lad]], the Knight of Longshaw.

The black water, with its "swirling and gurgling eddies" that seem to talk "in some dread and unknown tongue" (001-010), also carries an air of the numinous and the supernatural. The river speaks in a language beyond human comprehension. It is akin to the Dwarfs and [[dwarfs|Land-wights]], the other supernatural forces that haunt the edges of the Dale — a living presence that shapes human fate.

When Osberne is separated from Elfhild, the Flood becomes a symbol of grief and helpless rage, seeming "like the coils of a deadly serpent" (012-001). When he returns victorious with her at his side, the Flood is transformed into a place of beauty and completion, its banks flower-strewn on the finest of midsummer days (066-001). The river's meaning shifts with the emotional state of those who stand upon its banks.

The Flood also represents the boundary between childhood and adulthood. Osberne's childhood is bounded by the river; his transition to manhood begins when he leaves the Dale, goes around the Flood, and enters the wider world of war, politics, and chivalry. His return to Wethermel with Elfhild marks a return to the river on new terms — no longer a barrier but a familiar companion.

## Settlements Along the Flood

The Sundering Flood defines the geography of settlement in the Dale. On the east side lies Wethermel, the lonely stead where Osberne is raised, the last house but one toward the mountains at the head of the Dale (002-001). Further down the east bank stands Eastcheaping, the chief trading town of the East Dale, where Osberne sells his wool and buys his goods (020-001).

On the west side, across the water, are the stead Hartshaw where Elfhild dwells with her aunts, and the homes of the Westdalers who share the Dale's life of sheep-herding and small husbandry. The churches of [[allhallows]] stand on both sides of the water at the Cloven Mote — identical in all ways, mirroring each other across the Flood (006-002).

Far south, beyond the Wood Masterless, lies Longshaw, the seat of Sir Godrick, "no great way from the Sundering Flood" but separated from it by forest (039-007). At the river's mouth stands the City of the Sundering Flood, the great and rich city that is the ultimate destination of all who travel the river's full length (001-004, 046-007).
