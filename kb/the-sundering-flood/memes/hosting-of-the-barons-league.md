# The Hosting of the Barons' League

The great military muster of the [[barons-league|Barons' League]], summoned in the year after the initial campaign against [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick of Longshaw]], to gather all the forces of the allied Barons for a decisive confrontation. The hosting was the most significant military assembly of the [[war-of-the-dales|War of the Dales]], drawing in the [[blue-knight|Blue Knight of Brookside]] and threatening to overwhelm Longshaw.

## The Summons

In the winter before the campaign, a knight and two squires rode to [[brookside|Brookside]] bearing a special message to the Blue Knight: "the hosting of the Barons' League would be in such place, east of the water and far to the south, a month before Marymass of next year" (059-002). The [[blue-knight|Blue Knight]] was called upon to "be leal and true to the League, and gather to him what force he might, as well armed and formed in all ways as could be done" (059-002). He accepted the summons, committing to "do his devoir to the uttermost of his power" (059-002).

## Strategic Importance

The hosting represented the Barons' attempt to gather overwhelming force against Longshaw. The League sought to draw in not only their own members but also the [[city-king|King of the City of the Sundering Flood]] (058-004). The Blue Knight, though bound by his oath to answer the summons, privately confessed his misgivings to [[elfhild|the Maiden]]: "I deem that they be the luckiest who may keep them out of this strife, as I may not" (059-003).

## The Gathering

Throughout the winter, the Blue Knight "had his hands full of business in seeing to the gathering of men and stuff for the hosting of the Barons' League" (060-009). When March came, he let it be known that he would depart on the very first day of the month, before sunrise (060-010). The gathering drew forces from across the lands east of the [[sundering-flood|Sundering Flood]] and far to the south, creating the largest army the Barons had ever assembled.

## Outcome

Despite the formidable hosting, the campaign ended in disaster for the League. False tidings reached Brookside that April — "the Barons' League had driven him of Longshaw out of the field by the mere terror of their host and the wind of its banners" (060-013) — but the reality proved otherwise. [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick]] had not been driven from the field but had chosen his ground. The [[battle-of-longshaw|battle on the first of May]] saw the Barons' host utterly overthrown, their banners taken, and the League broken forever.

## The Blue Knight's Role

The Blue Knight's participation in the hosting proved fateful. Leaving only three score men-at-arms at Brookside under an ancient knight, he departed for the muster (060-012). His absence left [[brookside|Brookside]] vulnerable, and the Maiden and [[the-carline|the Carline]] were left to await tidings of the war alone. The Blue Knight survived the campaign and returned to Brookside, but the League he had served was shattered beyond recovery.

## Significance

The Hosting of the Barons' League marks the climax of the feudal military system in Morris's narrative. The League's ability to summon lords from across the land demonstrates the reach of feudal obligation, but its catastrophic defeat at Longshaw represents the triumph of communal defense over baronial aggression — a recurring theme in Morris's medieval romances.
