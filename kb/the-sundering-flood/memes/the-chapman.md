# The Chapman

The **Chapman** is a travelling merchant who plays a crucial role in Elfhild's story. He purchases the captive maiden from the Red Skinners and later sells her to Sir Mark, the Blue Knight — a transaction that, though motivated by greed, brings Elfhild into the protection of a chivalrous knight.

## Role in the Tale

### The Purchase

After raiders sweep through West Dale, Elfhild is taken captive and eventually falls into the hands of the Red Skinners (the Black Skinners' western counterparts). The chapman buys her from them, presumably intending to sell her at profit in the settled lands [055-002].

### The Sale to the Blue Knight

The chapman encounters the Blue Knight (Sir Mark) on the road and offers to sell the Maiden. The Blue Knight, recognising her quality and moved by her plight, purchases her freedom:

> "The Blue Knight buys the Maiden of the Chapman" [055-001].

The chapman's greed is evident — he was "as eager as had been his bargain" to profit from the sale [055-003]. Yet his mercenary transaction proves providential, placing Elfhild in the hands of an honourable protector rather than some baser master.

### A Brief Reappearance

The chapman appears again in the tale when Osberne, in his wanderings, encounters a chapman's servant — a reminder of the web of trade and travel that connects the disparate parts of the medieval world [051-002].

## Significance

The chapman is a minor figure, yet his role is pivotal. He is the link between the wild raiders who captured Elfhild and the civilised world of knighthood and castle. Without his greed-driven transaction, Elfhild would not have been delivered to Brookside, would not have been reunited with the Carline, and might never have found her way back to Osberne.

In Morris's medieval world, chapmen represent the connective tissue of society — they travel between communities, carry news, and through their commerce link the scattered steads and dales to the wider world. The chapman who sells Elfhild embodies both the dangers of this commerce (traffic in human lives) and its unintended redemptive possibilities.
