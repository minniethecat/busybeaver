# Goodwife of Wethermel

The **Goodwife of Wethermel** is Osberne's grandam, the wife of the goodman of Wethermel. She is about forty-five winters old at the beginning of the tale, a kindly and maternal figure who, along with the old carline Bridget, dotes upon the young Osberne [002-003].

## Nature

Unlike her husband the goodman, who is gruff and quick to threaten beatings, the goodwife is tender-hearted and protective. When Osberne returns from his nightlong adventure with the Dwarf, she "caught him up and hugged him and kissed him well favouredly" [002-007]. Her tears and lamentations over Osberne's perilous strayings move the boy, though not enough to keep him wholly within bounds [002-005].

## Role at Wethermel

The goodwife manages the household alongside the carline Bridget. When strangers come to the door — as when Steelhead arrives in the great storm — it is the goodwife who, at Osberne's bidding, takes the guest into the inner chamber to wash his feet and find him dry raiment [012-001]. She is the softening influence in the household, balancing the goodman's harshness with warmth and kindness.

## Kinship

She lost both her son (Osberne's father) and his wife within a short span, leaving her and the goodman to raise Osberne from infancy [002-003]. She treasures the boy "exceedingly" and is loth to trust him far out of sight because of his bold heart and wilful spirit [002-003].

## In Osberne's Verses

Osberne includes his grandam among his "three mothers" in one of his improvised staves:

> Three mothers had I,
> And one is gone by,
> But two are left here,
> Leal, buxom, and dear. [003-008]

The two living mothers are the goodwife and the carline Bridget, with Osberne's birth-mother being the one "gone by."
