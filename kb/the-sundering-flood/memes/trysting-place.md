# The Trysting-Place

The **Trysting-Place** is the spot along the Sundering Flood where Osberne of Wethermel and Elfhild meet across the great river, speaking to one another from opposite banks. It is the central meeting-point of their love story, serving as both a physical location and a powerful symbol of their bond across separation.

## Location

The trysting-place lies on the Sundering Flood, somewhere between Wethermel on the east bank and Elfhild's dwelling on the west. It is described as being within Osberne's swift running distance from Wethermel, and the river at this point is wide but not so wide that voices cannot carry across [030-002]. A timber bridge is later built nearby, at the end of the tale [066-001].

## Role in the Tale

### Discovery and Early Meetings

Osberne and Elfhild discover one another across the Flood and establish the trysting-place as their regular meeting ground. "The one who came to the trysting-place and missed the other was sore at heart" [012-001]. Through autumn and winter they meet there, exchanging words, gifts, and the growing bond of young love.

### Gifts Across the Flood

At the trysting-place, Osberne shoots gifts across the Flood to Elfhild using his bow — an early symbol of their ingenuity in overcoming the barrier between them [011-001]. Elfhild wears the gifts about her shoulders when she comes to the trysting-place [022-003].

### Loss and Longing

When Elfhild's kinswoman dies and raiders trouble the West Dale, she fails at the trysting-place, and "Osberne had to go sorrowfully back" [019-005]. Later, when Elfhild disappears entirely, Osberne's visits to the trysting-place become a ritual of longing and hope. Before his departure from Wethermel, his thoughts of making his way to the trysting-place "once more were speedily swept away" by greater purposes [023-010].

### Return and Reunion

At the tale's climax, Osberne declares: "I will hie me straight to that old trysting-place, and look across the Sundering Flood" [050-004]. The trysting-place becomes the goal of his return journey, and their final reunion echoes their first meetings there.

## Significance

The trysting-place is the geographical heart of the novel's central romance. It embodies the theme of love across division — the Sundering Flood separates the lovers physically, but the trysting-place joins them spiritually. The name itself, evoking the medieval word "tryst" (a lovers' meeting), sets the tale firmly within the romance tradition.
