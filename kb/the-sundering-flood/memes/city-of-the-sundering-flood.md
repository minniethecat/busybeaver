# City of the Sundering Flood

The great city situated at the mouth of the Sundering Flood river, serving as the principal urban center of the region. Built and thriven because of its great and most excellent haven, it was a city of walls, gates, bridges, palaces, and gildhalls — and the stage for a civic revolution that abolished the monarchy and established rule by Porte, Great Council, and Burgreve.

## Cityscape

The City stood at the mouth of the Sundering Flood, where the great river met the sea (001-001). Its most striking feature was the haven — a great and most excellent natural harbor that was the source of the city's wealth and power. Looking upon the haven was "like looking at a huge wood of barked and smoothened fir-trees when one saw the masts of the ships that lay in the said haven" (001-001).

The tidal flood ran up the river a long way, so that the biggest dromonds and round-ships could sail up it, and often they lay amid pleasant upcountry places, with their yards all but touching the windows of husbandmen's steads (001-002). Even beyond the tide's reach — hard on a hundred miles from the sea — the river remained a noble and wide-spreading water where barges and lesser keels spread their sails when the southwest blew (001-002).

The City proper was a walled settlement with multiple gates. The North Gate was of particular importance — it controlled the quarter where the Lesser Crafts dwelt and became the foothold of the Small Crafts' uprising (046-001). The East Gate served as a sally port for the King's forces (046-004). Two bridges, made of great barges linked together, crossed the Sundering Flood — one near to the haven, the other a good way higher up the river (048-002). These floating bridges were critical strategic assets; neither the King nor his enemies dared destroy them.

The King's palace and the great gildhall stood within the city, both defensible buildings with full command of all the haven and the ships therein, controlling all the quays, landing-places, and warehouses — so that both the sea and the river were under their wielding (048-002). After the peace, Sir Godrick set to work strengthening the city's defenses, looking to "all points of defence, both the castles of the town and especially the ships in the haven" (049-001), until "the City of the Sundering Flood [was] exceeding strong, so that it might scarce be stronger" (049-002).

Beyond the walls lay the fields without the City and the countryside that stretched eastward some twenty miles to the sea — the landing ground where the Outland kings would later bring their host (046-002, 049-006). The city-folk loved their river much and were proud of it, jesting that it was no sunderer but a uniter (001-004). The southernmost skirts of the Wood Masterless came down within a score of miles of the great city, and the city-folk "love the forest little, save they might master it and make it their own" (039-007).

## Governance

The City maintained an elaborate system of governance with multiple overlapping institutions. At its apex sat the King, a hereditary monarch whose power was constrained by the Porte and the Great Council. The King was described by Sir Alwyn as "feathered-headed, stubborn in wrong, and hard-hearted" — a capable fighter but a poor ruler who was "of little account before such a man as was Sir Godrick" (057-002).

The Porte was the fortified gate and its governing body, representing the old mercantile aristocracy — the masters of the Great Crafts. Together with the King, the Porte controlled the haven, the ships, and the river (048-002). They were the established power, described by Sir Godrick as "hard masters and tyrants" (039-007).

The Great Council represented both the merchant elite and originally only the Great Crafts. It shared legislative and deliberative power with the Porte and King. The heart of the peace settlement was that "so many of the masters of the Small Crafts should sit on the Great Council of the City, and that enough of them to make them of due weight in the Council" (048-006).

The Burgreve was the chief magistrate of the City, wielding executive power to hold a guard and enforce order. After the peace, the Burgreve was to be "chosen by all the Gilds of Craft, voting one with another, and not by the Great Council" — which gave the naming of him into the hands of the Lesser Crafts, who were more numerous than the great ones, though far less rich and mighty (048-006). Sir Godrick himself was chosen as Burgreve by acclamation, "both the big crafts and the little" (048-007).

The Crafts themselves were the gilds of the city's working people. The Great Crafts comprised the wealthy merchant gilds; the Lesser (or Small) Crafts represented the weavers, husbandmen, simple mariners, and other working folk. The Lesser Crafts were the oppressed underclass who rose against the Porte and King. As Sir Godrick explained, they were "good fellows" who might "rise against their lords" (039-013). When the men of the Small Crafts and the lesser commons rose, they seized the North Gate and held it against their foemen, together with that quarter of the city which lay round about it (046-001).

After the Outland king was deposed, a great [[cloven-mote|Mote]] was called and the office of King was abolished altogether, since "the City had a Porte and a Great Council, and a Burgreve under these, the office of King was little needed there" (049-005). The King of the City fled to the Outland king and became his ally, and was slain by the [[red-lad]] (Osberne) in the beginning of the rout of the Outland host (049-004, 049-007).

## Trade

The City's wealth was built on maritime trade. Its excellent haven drew ships from across the seas, and the Sundering Flood served as a commercial artery carrying wares deep into the interior (001-001, 001-002). The city controlled the quays, landing-places, and warehouses that lined the haven (048-002).

Merchants operated from the city, including the traveling chapmen who ventured as far as the Wood Masterless and beyond, trading in goods and — darkly — in captive women (043-012, 054-008). The hostelry of the Wool-pack in Westcheaping served as a banking house where ransoms and payments could be settled (055-022).

When the city-folk and the Lesser Crafts were in conflict, the city's merchant elite shut the rebels out from chaffer with the merchants of the City (048-003). Sir Godrick countered this by sending out riders to secure the countryside, ensuring the rebels "were not like to fall short of victuals" (048-003).

During the siege, the King's forces "set swift keels on the Sundering Flood stuffed of men-at-arms" who would land twenty or thirty miles upstream to plunder and ravage the country-folk, or to intercept trains of victuals heading toward the eastern city (048-003).

## Role in the Story

The City of the Sundering Flood is the political and military focal point of the novel's second half. It first enters the story as a distant but oppressive power, the home of the King and the tyrannous Porte against whom Sir Godrick of Longshaw resisted. The [[barons-league]] was described as "but the catspaw of the King and the City and the tyrannous Porte" (045-005).

The turning point came when the men of the Small Crafts and the lesser commons rose against the Porte and the King, seizing the North Gate (046-001). Sir Godrick dispatched Osberne with fifteen hundreds of his best men to ride to the City and reinforce the rebels (046-002). Osberne's spies found the rebels still holding the North Gate, their banners flying from the walls (046-004), and he launched a night assault that crushed the forces of the Porte encamped outside the gate. "Thuswise first came Osberne to the City of the Sundering Flood" (046-007).

After a prolonged standoff through the autumn, the King's folk came to the upper bridge with a white shield in late September, seeking terms (048-005). A peace was signed that reformed the city's government: the Lesser Crafts gained seats on the Great Council, and the Burgreve would be chosen by all the Gilds of Craft equally (048-006). The Outland king's threat — which the King and Porte had kept secret — drove them toward peace, as they knew they could not face an outside invader while divided (048-006).

After peace, "great feast and glee" filled the City. Gates were thrown open, bridges made free, country-folk flocked in, and markets thronged (049-001). Sir Godrick, now Burgreve, set to work fortifying the city until it was exceeding strong; "old foes became new friends, and all men were well content" (049-002).

When the Outland king finally came the next year, the combined defenders let him land twenty miles east of the city, then routed his host. The old City King was slain in the rout by Osberne; the Outland king fled to his ships and sailed away "never to trouble the City of the Sundering Flood" again (049-006, 049-007). Thereafter, the monarchy was abolished and the City throve "as well as ever it had done" (049-005).
