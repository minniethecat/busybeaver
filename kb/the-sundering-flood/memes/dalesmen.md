# Dalesmen

The Dalesmen are the free folk who dwell in the East and West Dales along the banks of the Sundering Flood. A scattered but cohesive people living in remote homesteads — steads — bound together by shared custom, the Cloven Mote, and a fierce commitment to liberty, they represent the ideal of free rural community in William Morris's medieval fantasy.

## Social Structure

The Dalesmen lived in a decentralized society of independent homesteads, with no lord, earl, or king over them. As Osberne declared to the Knight of Eastcheaping: "we dalesmen deem that we be free men, owing no service to any lord, or earl, or king" (021-005). This was not rhetoric but the lived reality of the Dales — neither the East nor the West Dale owed feudal service to any outside power.

Each stead (homestead) was largely self-governing. Households were scattered across the dale with long distances between neighbors (002-001). The household was the fundamental social unit — the goodman (master of the stead), the goodwife, their children, and hired hands like the shepherd. When the goodman of Wethermel rode to Eastcheaping, he went as a free man conducting his own chaffer (009-001).

Leadership was earned, not inherited. When the Dalesmen needed a captain for war, they gathered at a Mote of the neighbours and chose one from among their own number by acclamation (023-001, 023-007). The Lawman of the Dale presided over such gatherings but did not command — he facilitated the collective will of the people (023-005). As Osberne himself observed: "ye are most wont to go each after his own will, and it is well-nigh enough to put a man off from doing a thing if another man say to him, Do it" (023-008).

## Customs and Gatherings

The Dalesmen maintained their shared identity through seasonal gatherings. In mid-April, the goodmen gathered at a stead called Bullmeads, whence they rode together to Eastcheaping to sell the autumn clip of wool and conduct trade (009-001).

The Cloven Mote was the greatest institution of the Dalesmen — a twice-yearly gathering at midwinter and midsummer where the folk of both the East and West Dales came together for worship, trade, and social bonding (006-002, 022-002, 036-001). The midsummer feast of the Cloven Mote was "the greatest of all the feasts of the Dalesmen" (036-001). At the [[cloven-motes|midwinter Cloven Mote]], young people were paired or "mated" for the festivities — a custom of companionship during the gathering (019-001, 019-003).

At the height of the midsummer Cloven Mote, the folk of the two sides of the Dale ranged themselves each in a line on their own shore of the river and sang staves across the Sundering Flood — the Westdalers beginning, the Eastdalers taking up the response (036-001). This ritual of song across the uncrossable water embodied the paradox of the Dalesmen: a single people divided by the Flood, yet unified by custom and kinship.

At the smaller spring mote at Bullmeads, the neighbours gathered to coordinate the wool-trading journey to Eastcheaping (009-001).

When Osberne prepared to leave Wethermel, he gathered all the folk before the porch of the hall and asked each one to say if he had wronged them — a custom of communal reconciliation and atonement before a long departure (037-002).

## Way of Life

The Dalesmen lived in remote homesteads — steads — scattered across the dales, with few neighbors and long distances between households (002-001). They were primarily sheep-farmers and shepherds, with good pasture for kine and horses and sheep all to the water's lip (002-001). Surplus wool was woven into cloth for home use; the autumn clip of wool was sold at Eastcheaping (001-009, 009-001). They also hunted — heathfowl, whimbrel, and other game — and kept cattle and horses (008-005).

They bought wrought wares such as cutlery and pots from the cheaping-towns, and above all boards and timber, whereof the Dales themselves had nought (001-009). Life followed the rhythms of the agricultural year: spring wool-marketing at Eastcheaping, summer shepherding on the bents, autumn harvest, winter with sheep folded in cotes where no shepherding was possible (019-005).

## Territory

The Dalesmen inhabited the East Dale and the West Dale, the valleys on either side of the Sundering Flood (001-009). The two Dales were physically separated by the uncrossable river — "never might any man on the east touch the hand of any on the west" (001-011). All direct dealings between the two sides were "but shouting and crying across the swirling and gurgling eddies of the black water" (001-010).

The East and West Dales were not utterly cut off from the outside world. By climbing the bents and wending over the necks east and west, some forty miles from the west bank and fifty from the east, one might come down into a valley fairly well peopled with two or three cheaping-towns (001-009).

Within the East Dale, Wethermel was the most remote stead — the last house but one toward the mountains at the head of the Dale (002-001). Steads were scattered down the Dale: Woodneb lay some distance up the river from the Cloven Mote meeting-place (022-002), while Bullmeads served as a gathering point for the spring mote (009-001). In the West Dale, steads like Coldburne (home of Wulfstan) lay in the lower Dale, and Hartshaw Knolls ([[elfhild]]'s home) was a small holding of two women near the river (033-001, 033-002). The space between steads was vast — five miles or more between good homesteads (033-002).

## Relationship to Wethermel

Wethermel held a special place among the Dalesmen as the home of Osberne Wulfgrimsson, who became the most renowned captain of the East Dale. It was the first stead on the road into the Dale, so that riders from Eastcheaping always came to Wethermel before any other household (021-003).

When the men-at-arms of Eastcheaping came seeking allies against the [[baron-of-deepdale]], they arrived first at Wethermel (021-003). It was at Wethermel that Osberne mustered his messengers — Otter, Simon, Longdeer, and Alison — to ride down the Dale and summon the Dalesmen to the riverbank with their bows when the Black Skinners invaded (032-004).

When Osberne departed the Dale to seek Elfhild, he gathered the Wethermel folk before the hall porch and took his leave, asking forgiveness of any he might have wronged (037-001, 037-002). The stead throve under the stewardship of [[stephen-eater]], who had come to Wethermel as a hired shepherd and risen to be the most trusted man of the household (008-001, 019-002). Stephen and the Wethermel folk remained ready for Osberne's return; when he finally came back with Elfhild, there sat Stephen "wise of aspect and thoughtful, as if he were awaiting something that should happen" and all the carles and queans who had been familiar to Osberne before he left for warfare (053-001).

[[sir-godrick|The Knight of Longshaw]] himself visited Wethermel and "abode at Wethermel in much content, and much it pleased him to look upon the beauty of Elfhild and the fairness of the life that men lived in the Dale" (068-001). The men of Wethermel were known as the "Lightfoot men" — swift and deadly in battle (067-005).

## In War

When the Baron of Deepdale challenged Eastcheaping, the men-at-arms of the town rode into the Dale seeking help — not as a right, but "as bold and free neighbours who for love's sake may be fain of helping friends in need" (021-006). At a Mote of the neighbours, the Dalesmen pledged ten score and six good men to ride to war, and chose Osberne Wulfgrimsson as their captain, despite his youth (023-001).

In the Battle of Eastcheaping against the Baron of Deepdale, the Dalesmen fought as footmen — half with bows, half with spears and swords (024-002). At the causeway, they blocked the Baron's retreat, standing firm with long spears until the first four riders fell (024-006). Osberne led the charge with [[boardcleaver]], crying "For the Dale!" (024-006). The Dalesmen became "a terror to them" of Deepdale (025-001).

When the Black Skinners (strong-thieves) invaded the Dale, Osberne roused the Eastdalers to action, bidding them gather along the riverbank with bows and sling-spears, drawn out in line with space between each shooter (032-004). In the shot-stour at the Bight of the Cloven Knoll, the Eastdalers shot across the Flood, while the Westdalers attacked the Skinners from the rear with spear, axe, and sword (032-017). This was the first time in memory that the Eastdalers and Westdalers had fought side by side against a common enemy, and "the Eastdalers knew the voice of their kinsmen" across the water (032-017). Together they killed seventy-seven Skinners, and Thieves' Howe was raised over the slain (034-011).

The Dalesmen also fought alongside Sir Godrick and the Knight of Longshaw against the [[barons-league]] and the King's tyranny, extending their concern for liberty beyond their own borders.

## East and West

The Sundering Flood divided the Dalesmen into two peoples — the Eastdalers and the Westdalers — who could shout across the water but never touch hands (001-010, 001-011). Each side maintained its own steads, its own chiefs, and its own affairs. Wulfstan of Coldburne spoke for the Westdalers when the two sides conferred after battle (033-001). Yet when the strong-thieves threatened both communities, the Eastdalers and Westdalers acted as one people, their voices recognized across the water as the voices of "their kinsmen" (032-017).
