# Woodneb

A stead in the East Dale, situated "some little way up the river from the place where the East and West Dales held the [[cloven-mote|Cloven Mote]], and by consequence not over far from the trysting-place" of [[osberne|Osberne]] and [[elfhild|Elfhild]] (028-003). Woodneb appears in two distinct contexts in the narrative — as a gathering place during the [[deepdale-conflict|Deepdale campaign]] and later as a fortified outpost of [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick of Longshaw]].

## The Errand of Peace

During the campaign against the [[baron-of-deepdale|Baron of Deepdale]], [[osberne|Osberne]] rode with [[sir-medard|Sir Medard]] through the mid and lower Dales to rally support. They "made stay for the night at a stead hight Woodneb" (028-003). That evening, they decided to gather a mote there the next morning, sending folk to bear the war-arrow to the steads above and below. Osberne "spake his mind without fear or favour to the boldest and wisest that were there" (028-003), demonstrating his growing leadership.

It was at Woodneb that Osberne realized the next day was his appointed tryst with [[elfhild|Elfhild]] at the [[bight-of-the-cloven-knoll|Bight of the Cloven Knoll]], causing him a sleepless night and an early departure at dawn to bid her farewell before battle.

## Sir Godrick's Outpost

Later in the narrative, after Osberne had joined Sir Godrick, Woodneb appears as a fortified outpost at the edge of the [[wood-masterless|Wood Masterless]]. When Osberne first saw it, he mistook it for [[longshaw|Longshaw]] itself, but Sir Godrick laughed: "What, thou deemest this no very lordly castle for him who hath to withstand barons and portes and kings? Nay lad, look again" (044-002). The outpost was commanded by **Edward the Brown**, "a short thick man, but very sturdy-looking" — "a valiant man he was, and a trusty" (044-004).

At this Woodneb, Sir Godrick's company rested seven days, with "good disport of hunting and hawking" and "much minstrelsy and tale-telling in the hall a-nights" (044-005). Osberne was called upon to tell the stories of the war of Eastcheaping and the matters of the Dale, and proved himself "a good tale-teller" (044-005).

## Geography

Woodneb's location was significant — situated at the boundary between settled Dale country and the Wood Masterless. From Woodneb, it was a twelve-day journey through the wild wood to Longshaw (044-003). The hold was "all too strait to keep within its walls the valiancy of Sir Godrick and his host" (044-003), marking it as a small but strategic outpost rather than a great castle.
