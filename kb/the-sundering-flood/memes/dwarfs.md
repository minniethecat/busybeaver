Dwarfs, also called Land-wights, are supernatural craftsmen who
dwell in a cave above the [[Sundering Flood]]. Feared and respected
by the [[Dalesmen]], they are the creators of [[Boardcleaver]] and
the gemmed necklace given to [[Elfhild]]. Their presence in the
story connects the medieval world of the Dales to an older, mythic
layer of folklore.

## Nature

Dwarfs are ambiguous figures — part craftsmen, part spirits of the
land. They are described both as "the Dwarfs" and as "Land-wights"
(002-002), suggesting they are simultaneously a distinct race and
a manifestation of place-spirit. They dwell underground, in a cave,
and "cut off their own heads" (004-003) according to rustic legend,
though this is likely a folk misunderstanding of their subterranean
nature.

## In the Story

### The Dwarf-Cave

The Dwarfs' dwelling "above the Sundering Flood" is a cave located
near the [[Cloven Knoll]]. The [[Dalesmen]] "feared the begrudging
of the Dwarfs" (034-011) and avoided the cave. Yet it serves as
a refuge for [[Osberne]] and his companions — the very fear that
keeps others away makes it a safe hiding place.

### Magical Craft

The Dwarfs are master craftsmen who work in metal and gems.
[[Boardcleaver]], Osberne's great sword, "was fashioned by the
father of all the Dwarfs" ([[boardcleaver]] PN). The gemmed necklace
given to Elfhild is explicitly "the gift of the Dwarfs" (059-001).
These artifacts carry the Dwarfs' power into the human world.

### Folk Belief

The Dalesmen hold complex attitudes toward the Dwarfs — fear
mingled with reverence. "Whether they be Land-wights or Dwarfs,
or ride-a-nights of the outlands, God wot" (003-003), says one
character, capturing the uncertainty. [[Elfhild]], however, is
unafraid: "Dear bairn or boy, I fear them not" (009-015).

### The Necklace

The "gemmed necklace which the Dwarfs had given me" (030-016) is
Elfhild's most treasured possession. Its magical properties are
subtle — it gleams and catches light (011-002) — but its true
power lies in its origin, marking Elfhild as someone touched by
the mythic world.

## Significance

The Dwarfs represent the mythic substrate beneath Morris's medieval
surface. They belong to the same imaginative world as Norse and
Germanic folklore, connecting The Sundering Flood to the tradition
of saga and fairy-tale. Their ambiguous nature — neither wholly
benevolent nor hostile — preserves the sense of a world not fully
disenchanted, where the old powers still dwell in caves and
bestow gifts on the worthy.
