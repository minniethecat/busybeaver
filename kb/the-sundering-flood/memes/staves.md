# The Staves

The **Staves** (quarterstaves) are the long wooden pole-weapons that Osberne teaches the Dalesmen to wield, transforming them from scattered shepherd-folk into an organised and feared fighting force. The staves become the signature weapon of the Dalesmen and a symbol of their identity as free folk who fight with skill rather than heavy armour.

## Osberne's Teaching

When Osberne is chosen as captain of the Dalesmen, he introduces them to the art of stave-fighting:

> "Osberne taught his folk the use of the long staves, and they had soon many of them who were well skilled therein, and the fame thereof went abroad through all the country of the Eastdalers" [036-001].

The stave is an unconventional weapon of war — it is essentially a long, sturdy pole, wielded with both hands. In the hands of a skilled fighter, however, it becomes a formidable weapon capable of striking down armoured knights and scattering cavalry.

## Tactical Advantage

The staves give the Dalesmen several advantages:

1. **Simplicity** — Unlike swords or lances, staves can be made from local wood and require no smith's craft
2. **Reach** — The long stave outreaches a sword, keeping armoured opponents at distance
3. **Mobility** — The Dalesmen, unencumbered by heavy armour, can move swiftly with their staves
4. **Surprise** — Feudal knights, trained to face swords and lances, are unprepared for the stave's unconventional reach and striking patterns

## In Battle

The effectiveness of the staves is demonstrated in multiple engagements. Against the Baron of Deepdale's forces, the Dalesmen's staves prove decisive. Later, at Longshaw and in the campaign against the Barons' League, the stave-wielding Dalesmen under the Red Lad's command scatter the feudal host [045-001].

## The Staves as Song

Osberne's gift for extemporised verse — his "staves" in the poetic sense — creates a double meaning. The young Osberne already composes staves (verses) at the Wethermel hearth [003-006, 003-008], and later he teaches his folk staves (weapons) for war. This wordplay links Osberne's two gifts: poetry and war-craft, the singer and the captain united in one person.
