Hardcastle is a landless man who attempts to seize [[Wethermel]]
by force. His arrival at the steading and subsequent death at
[[Osberne]]'s hands is a pivotal early event in the novel — the
moment when the boy proves himself capable of violence in
defense of his home.

## Identity

A man of uncertain status — "I care not whether he be earl or
churl, for it is all one" (015-002) — Hardcastle is a would-be
lord with no legitimate claim. He arrives at Wethermel with an
armed following, expecting deference, but encounters resistance.

## Biography

### Arrival at Wethermel

Hardcastle arrives at Wethermel after dark, announced by [[Surly
John]] with the ominous question: "May Hardcastle be here tonight,
ye folk?" (015-001). He immediately asserts dominance, demanding
food and lodging as though he were the master of the house.

### Confrontation with Osberne

When [[Osberne]] presents himself, Hardcastle tests the boy with
bluster and threats. "Hardcastle scowled on the lad" (015-002),
but Osberne meets him with steady defiance. At table, Hardcastle
drinks heavily: "Hardcastle, whose head was now pretty much
addled with drink" (015-007), rendering him vulnerable.

### The Claim on Wethermel

The next morning, Hardcastle reveals his purpose: to take
Wethermel as his own, reducing its folk to "thralls" (016-005).
He tries to cow Osberne with talk of rights and titles, but the
boy sees through him: "Great fool, what matters that to thee?"
(016-005).

### Death

Osberne challenges Hardcastle. In the ensuing fight — a knife
struggle in the hall — Osberne kills him. The slaying of
Hardcastle (detailed in the event page [[slaying-of-hardcastle]])
is Osberne's first blood-letting and his transition from boy to
man. The body is buried, and Wethermel is secured.

## Relationships

- [[Osberne]] — the boy who killed him
- [[Surly John]] — brought Hardcastle to Wethermel
- [[Stephen the Eater]] — Osberne's loyal man who stood against him
