# Porte of Eastcheaping

The Porte was the governing authority of the town of Eastcheaping, a combined civic and military body that managed the town's defense, diplomatic relations, and internal administration. It functioned as an oligarchic city council wielding executive and military powers (021-002).

## Nature and Structure

The Porte served as Eastcheaping's seat of government, a collective body rather than a single ruler (028-001). It combined the functions of a city council with those of a military command (023-010). The Porte received diplomatic challenges, issued orders to the town's defenders, and directed military campaigns (021-002). Its authority extended over the town's armed forces and civic affairs alike.

The Porte was closely allied with, though distinct from, the King of the Great City. [[sir-godrick]] described the Porte of the City as "tyrannous," noting how the barons who lay to the east and north of [[longshaw]] were "but the catspaw of the King and the City and the tyrannous Porte" (045-005). This suggests the Eastcheaping Porte was a local manifestation of a wider civic governance model found throughout the region.

The Porte operated alongside a military captain (Sir Medard in Eastcheaping's case), who "by bidding of the Porte, furnished and arrayed the [[dalesmen]] with weapons" (023-010). After victories, captives and matters of diplomacy were brought before Sir Medard and the Porte jointly (027-001).

## Role in the Deepdale War

When threatened by the Baron of Deepdale over a matter of truage, the Porte received his formal challenge and determined to fight: "seeing that it was nought feeble, it had a settled mind to fight it out" (021-002). When the Baron later demanded the town surrender and accept his burgreve, the Porte "naysaid him in few words" (025-004).

During the fighting, the town's defenders fought under the banners of Sir Medard and the Porte, crying its name as a battle cry (024-008) (024-010). Eastcheaping's forces marched under a combined standard — military and civic authority united.

After the capture of the Baron of Deepdale, Sir Medard told the captive lord that counsel would be held "thou and I and the Porte" (027-001). The peace deed was signed to the Porte's satisfaction: the Baron "gave up to the Porte of Eastcheaping all that for which they had withstood him" and paid ransom for his own body (028-001).

## Porte of the Great City

Beyond Eastcheaping, the Porte of the Great City was a more powerful and less benevolent institution. It was described by Sir Godrick as a force of tyranny aligned with the King against the Small Crafts and the folk (039-012). The Porte of the City used barons as proxies ("catspaw") to harry Sir Godrick's lands, while neither the King nor the Porte would openly show their hand (045-005).

During the civil strife in the City, the men of the Small Crafts and lesser commons "were risen against the Porte and the King" and seized the North Gate (046-001). The Porte's archers "did not dare to show much face to the [[red-lad]]" (047-003). Eventually, the Porte and the Crafts jointly chose a new Burgreve, and the office of King was abolished — all holdings escheated to the Porte (048-007) (049-005).

## Significance

The Porte represented civic self-governance in a region dominated by feudal lords (025-004). Unlike the robber-barons who ruled by force alone, Eastcheaping maintained a structured governance system through its Porte (028-001). Yet the Porte of the Great City revealed how such civic oligarchies could themselves become instruments of tyranny, setting the stage for the popular rising that ended the dual rule of King and Porte.

The combined authority of the Porte and Sir Medard proved effective in defending Eastcheaping against the Deepdale threat and maintaining order (028-001).
