# Deepdale Conflict

A military campaign waged by the [[baron-of-deepdale|Baron of Deepdale]] against the town of Eastcheaping, involving Osberne of [[wethermel]] as a key defender of the town, culminating in the daring capture of the Baron and the breaking of Deepdale's power (021-002).

## Context

The Baron of Deepdale was a powerful robber-baron whose territory lay near Eastcheaping. In early May, riders from Eastcheaping came into the Dale "all-armed" with news: "war and strife were at hand for the good town, for the Baron of Deepdale had sent the Porte his challenge for some matter of truage, wherein the town deemed it had a clear right, and seeing that it was nought feeble, it had a settled mind to fight it out" (021-002).

The conflict was part of the larger pattern of baronial aggression against free communities. The Baron aimed to subjugate Eastcheaping and extend his dominion. By May, he "had waxed so mighty that he gathered a great host together" (025-004).

## Narrative

### First Engagements

The chronicler notes: "I have nought to do to write a chronicle of the good town of Eastcheaping, or a history of this war of them of the town with the Baron of Deepdale, or else a long tale I might make of it" (024-001). Nevertheless, the key engagements are preserved.

The first major skirmish took place outside the town walls. Sir Medard, who knew the terrain well, rode out with a small company "to give the Baron a brush by the way," while a larger body of footmen lay in ambush along a causeway over a marshy place (024-002). When the Deepdale forces attacked, the cry went up: "Slay, Slay! Deepdale, Deepdale!" (024-004). Stephen the Eater shouted back: "Go back, ye swine, to Deepdale" (024-005). Osberne entered among the Deepdale folk and "s[[cloven-mote|mote]] right and left" with his blade [[boardcleaver]], distinguishing himself in the fighting. "With each stroke hewed a man, and they fell back before him" (024-006). The first riding of the Baron of Deepdale was repulsed (024-011).

After this, the defenders "began to be well known to them of Deepdale and were a terror to them" (025-001). Osberne was hurt twice but not sorely, and "ever he waxed in manhood, and was well accounted of by all men" (025-001).

### The Siege

When May came, the Baron gathered an even greater host and came against Eastcheaping, forcing the defenders to "draw within their walls." The Baron sent a herald demanding surrender on harsh terms: the town must pay truage according to his will, "batter down their walls, and take his men into their castle and have his burgreve over them, and moreover that they give over ten of their best to his mercy" (025-004). The Porte "naysaid him in few words" (025-004).

### The Capture of the Baron

Stephen the Eater, a resourceful Dalesman, devised a plan. Disguised as a minstrel with a fiddle, he had infiltrated the enemy camp five nights earlier. A Deepdale man-at-arms showed him "the great bastide wherein, said he, the Baron of Deepdale was lodged, and that it was little guarded" — the man deemed it "something rash of the Baron to suffer so few men of his anigh him" (025-008).

Osberne immediately understood: "thou wilt mean it in a day or two that we should go, we two, by night and cloud to the Great Bastide and carry off the Baron of Deepdale, that we may give him guesting in the good town" (025-011). They executed the plan, and "thus they brought the lordship of Deepdale to the door" — the Baron, drugged or dazed, walked compliantly with them as Stephen played "a slow sweet tune" on his fiddle and sang with his "big but melodious voice" (026-007).

The Baron was brought to Sir Medard in the great tower (027-001). Sir Degore and the other Deepdalers "were abashed when they knew that their lord was a captive in the hands of the foemen" (027-013). Sir Medard spoke to the Baron with grim courtesy: "Doubtless tomorrow, after we have had some talk together, thou and I and the Porte, thou mayst go back home to Deepdale, or abide here to see how we can feast" (027-001).

## Outcome and Aftermath

The next day, the Baron of Deepdale "signed the deed of peace which gave up to the Porte of Eastcheaping all that for which they had withstood him; and withal some deal of ransom he had to pay for his own body" — a baron's ransom fitting "a free baron, a lord of wide lands, who had under him towns, tolls, and markets" (028-001). The "stout men-at-arms of Deepdale began to flow away from before Eastcheaping" (027-017).

The defeat broke Deepdale's power and secured Eastcheaping's independence. The conflict established Osberne's reputation as a formidable warrior beyond Wethermel. Sir Medard told the Baron that "much of the valiancy shown by Eastcheaping in the war came of this lad of Wethermel" (027-017). The Baron himself acknowledged Osberne's prowess, saying "Well, lad, if ever thou art hard bestead, come thou to Deepdale, and we shall find somewhat for thee to do" (027-017).

Osberne took as his only spoil from the war "a very fair armour of the spoils of Deepdale" — a gift Sir Medard insisted was "no gift, but what he had earned himself by hard toil enough" (028-003). Later, when [[sir-godrick]] passed through Deepdale lands, the Baron granted a let-pass but "would not suffer Sir Godrick to take any men from his country" (040-002) — a sign of his continued caution toward [[longshaw]].
