# The City King

The unnamed monarch of the [[city-of-the-sundering-flood|City of the Sundering Flood]], whose power was steadily eclipsed by the [[porte-of-eastcheaping|Porte]] and the rising [[eastcheaping-crafts|Small Crafts]], ultimately leading to his betrayal of the city and death at the hands of [[osberne|Osberne the Red Lad]] (049-001).

## Waning Power

By the time of Sir Godrick's campaign, the King's authority had already been deeply eroded. The Porte had grown mightier than the King, and the rising demand for a [[eastcheaping-crafts|Burgreve]] to govern the City was "much like putting the King out of his place" (048-007). The King and his councillors were among the few who still worried about the Outland threat, while all other men trusted in [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick]] (049-001). When Sir Godrick's terms for the Small Crafts were brought forward, the King was driven toward peace by fear of the Outland king's heralds, knowing the divided city could make no head against a foreign host (048-007).

## Betrayal and Alliance with the Outland King

In the spring of the following year, when the Outland conqueror's host hung about the city, the King made his fatal choice: "ere he brake up his host the King of the City fled to him and became his friend" (049-006). Together, the two kings plotted to fall upon the lands of the City in the next year (049-006). In response, the City held a great Mote and formally abolished the office of King, escheating the runaway monarch and declaring that all who had held of him should now hold of the Porte (049-007).

## Death in Battle

In the final confrontation, Sir Godrick chose the time and place for battle, and the two kings could not draw to a head again. "The old City King, fighting desperately, was slain by the Red Lad in the beginning of the rout" (049-012). His ally the Outland King escaped to his ships and sailed away forever.

## Character Significance

The City King represents the decline of hereditary monarchy in the face of the rising power of guilds and free crafts. His arc — from grudging accommodation with the [[eastcheaping-crafts|Crafts]], to betrayal of his own city, to death on the battlefield — illustrates Morris's recurring theme of feudal authority giving way to communal self-governance. His slaying by [[osberne|Osberne]] is one of the Red Lad's most significant kills, alongside the [[slaying-of-hardcastle|slaying of Hardcastle]].
