# The Small Crafts

The **Small Crafts** (also called the Lesser Crafts) are the craft-guilds of the common working folk in the City of the Sundering Flood. They stand in political opposition to the Porte (the merchant oligarchy) and the masters of the Great Crafts, forming a popular faction that aligns with Sir Godrick of Longshaw and the Red Lad (Osberne).

## Political Position

The Small Crafts occupy a distinct position in the City's political landscape. While the Porte and the Great Crafts represent the established wealth and power, the Small Crafts speak for the "lesser commons" — the artisans, journeymen, and working folk who make up the bulk of the City's population [046-001].

Sir Alwyn of Warding Knowe describes the political tension:

> "There is talk also how that the Small Crafts have in their hearts to rise against both Porte and King, and certes if they may have Sir Godrick on their side, which is not wholly unlike, they will perchance come to their above" [057-002].

## Alliance with Sir Godrick

When Osberne (as the Red Lad) enters the City, the Small Crafts rally to him:

> "The men of the Small Crafts and the lesser commons were raised from their dejection, and were of good heart, for they saw that the Red Lad was a true helper of their cause" [046-001].

Their battle-cry becomes: "The Red Lad! Longshaw for the Small Crafts!" [046-007].

## Role in the City's Governance

After the battle in the square and Sir Godrick's election as Burgreve, the Small Crafts gain representation. Sir Godrick arranges that "so many of the masters of the Small Crafts should sit on the Great Council" [048-006], giving the common folk a voice in the City's governance for the first time.

## Significance

The Small Crafts represent the democratic element in William Morris's medieval fantasy. Morris, a lifelong socialist, portrays the craft-guilds as the voice of the common people against oligarchic rule — the King of the City, the Porte, and the feudal Barons. Their rise through alliance with the heroic Sir Godrick and the Red Lad embodies Morris's vision of popular power achieving political representation through right action and worthy leadership.
