# Allhallows

A sacred figure or divine power invoked by the Dalesmen as the patron of their churches and the recipient of their prayers, oaths, and festive toasts. Two churches dedicated to Allhallows stood on opposite sides of the Sundering Flood at the Cloven Mote (006-002).

## The Two Churches

Allhallows was the invocation under which the two churches at the Cloven Mote were built — one on the east side of the water and one on the west side, "like to it in all ways and under the same invocation" (006-002). The eastern church served the eastern Dalesmen; the western church, Allhallows the West, served the western folk (030-015).

These paired churches were the religious and social center of the Dalesmen's community life. They hosted the Christmass at Yule (006-002) and the high mass at Midsummer (036-001). When [[elfhild]]'s kinswoman died, she was "brought to earth by the church of Allhallows the West, hard by the place of the Cloven Mote" (030-015).

## Religious Role and Invocations

The Dalesmen swore oaths by Allhallows as their most solemn binding. When [[osberne]] pledged fealty to Sir Godrick of [[longshaw]], "he knelt before the Knight and put his hands between his hands, and swore by Allhallows to be true to him" (039-019). Sir Godrick himself invoked "by Allhallows" in his speech (056-008).

The invocation "God and Allhallows" was a common formula used in blessings and wards. The phrase "which may God and Allhallows forfend" was used to ward off feared events like the Winter of Fear (006-003). Sir Godrick praised Allhallows that [[city-of-the-sundering-flood|the city]]-folk had failed to master [[wood-masterless|the Wood Masterless]] (039-007).

The hermit who healed a wounded man invoked "God's love and Allhallows'" as his motivation (051-017), and gave his blessing with "God and Allhallows keep thee" (051-019). This pairing of God and Allhallows suggests that Allhallows was understood as an intercessor or patron saint, distinct from but subordinate to the divine.

Even Elfhild in her youthful talk invoked Allhallows, saying "Allhallows grant it that my face may change" (011-016), showing how deeply the invocation permeated everyday speech.

## Feasts and Rituals

At the great feasts, cups were drunk in a specific order: first to Christ, second to Allhallows, and third to the home-comers from the war (029-005). This tripartite toast reflected the Dalesmen's religious hierarchy — Christ, then the patron saint, then the living community.

The Midsummer Feast at the Cloven Mote, the greatest of all the feasts of the Dalesmen, began with high mass "in the goodliest fashion in each of the two churches of Allhallows for the good rest of them who had fallen manfully in battle with the thieves" (036-001).

The Midsummer rites also included pre-Christian folk customs: "the Trundling of the Fiery Wheel, and the Kindling of the Bale, and the Leaping through the Fire" — suggesting a syncretic blend of Christian worship under Allhallows with older seasonal traditions.

The Yule custom likewise combined Christian mass with the gathering of the folk at the Cloven Mote (006-002).

## Cultural Significance

The frequent invocation of Allhallows in oaths, blessings, prayers, and everyday speech demonstrates the deep religious foundation of Dalesmen society. The double church arrangement — identical structures on both banks of an uncrossable river — embodied the central tension of the novel: a people united in faith and custom yet physically divided by the Sundering Flood.

The shared invocation of Allhallows was one of the few bonds that connected the eastern and western communities across the impassable waters.
