# Battle of Longshaw

The Battle of Longshaw was the decisive engagement of the [[barons-league|Barons' War]], fought near the House of [[longshaw|Longshaw]] on the first of May. Sir Godrick of Longshaw and his forces, including [[osberne|Osberne the Red Lad]], faced a coalition of robber-barons who outnumbered them three to one — yet through superior strategy and fierce fighting, the Longshaw host achieved a complete and annihilating victory (045-008).

## Prelude

The barons of the lands about Longshaw "would not endure Sir Godrick's ways" and his independence from their feudal claims. They formed a league, "swearing nothing less than the undoing of Sir Godrick and the House of Longshaw for ever" (045-005). By spring, the Barons' League had gathered a great host and was marching toward Longshaw.

Sir Godrick, though outnumbered, refused to wait passively for siege. He declared "he will abide their coming to Longshaw" but also dispatched Osberne on a flanking errand — to seek the hosting of the Barons' League who dwelt furthest north, "to fall upon them fast and fierce, so they could not be on his flank when he marched against the main host" (045-006). Osberne "wendeth, not back again to Longshaw, but thither whereas he wots the battle shall be" (045-007).

## The Battle

The battle was joined on the first of May. Despite the Barons' League outnumbering Longshaw's forces "three to Sir Godrick's one" (045-008), Sir Godrick's superior generalship carried the day. The battle-cry of the Longshaw host, led by Osberne, rang across the field — "The Red Lad! the Red Lad! Longshaw for the Small Crafts!" (046-007). The men of Longshaw "rushed forward, smiting and hewing" with Osberne at their head (047-003).

Osberne himself became the focal point of the charge, his war-cry thundering: "The Red Lad, the Red Lad for Longshaw and the Crafts! On, on at the beginning!" (047-005). The Barons' host, broken by the ferocity of the assault and Osberne's flanking maneuver, collapsed. What should have been an overwhelming numerical advantage dissolved into rout.

## Aftermath

The victory was complete and decisive. Within a half-month, the Barons' League "sent a herald to Longshaw praying for peace" (045-010). Sir Godrick refused easy terms, demanding that the barons deliver up all their strengths into his hands. He led a counter-campaign, taking the greatest baron's castle in ten days, and "before winter he had most of the Barons captive at Longshaw" (045-010).

By Yule, "noble Yule they kept at Longshaw that year, with all those great men and war-farers" (045-011) — the feast of victors. What remained of the League sent a herald through the snow, and Sir Godrick granted terms: he would keep what he had won and ransom his captives duly, or let them dwell at Longshaw all their lives (045-011).

The battle secured Longshaw as the dominant power in the southern Dales. All men later reflected that disaster would have befallen the free folk "if the Barons' League had not been so utterly broken up" (048-003). The victory freed Osberne to turn his attention northward toward [[wethermel]] and his search for [[elfhild]].
