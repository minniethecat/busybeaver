# Chapmen

The chapmen were the travelling merchants and traders of the Sundering Flood world, a distinct social class who moved goods between towns, markets, and rural steadings, serving as the economic circulatory system of the land.

## Nature

Chapmen are a social and economic class — free commoners who travel and trade rather than farm or fight. They are explicitly distinguished from men-at-arms: when Sir Godrick encountered a group, he noted "they were not men-at-arms, but chapmen" and treated with them accordingly (044-001). They dwell alongside "peaceful craftsmen" in good towns (044-006), forming part of the urban commercial population.

## Description

The visible presence of chapmen in the markets was distinctive. At Eastcheaping, the market was "wondrous" with the activity of chapmen "in their fine coats of strange fashion" conducting their chaffer (trade) (020-001). Traders arriving from distant lands brought not only goods but also exotic styles of dress that marked them as outsiders to the Dales.

Chapmen operated at multiple scales. Some were modest itinerant traders, appearing "more than two or three" in early spring, "almost in the face of May," bringing goods to remote steadings (061-002). Others were substantial merchants who attended the great seasonal markets — the Midsummer and Michaelmas markets held in the meadows (058-009). At the Michaelmas market, "great recourse was there" of folk buying wares "of likely chapmen" (059-001).

The chapmen were described as "king-ruled" (039-007), suggesting they operated under some form of royal authority or charter, distinct from the more independent Dalesmen. They could be encountered at houses of religion along the roads, where they lodged alongside other travellers (045-004). At inns and way-stations, "pilgrims and chapmen, and knights and men-at-arms" all mingled together (058-009).

## Role in the Story

Chapmen appear at several key junctures. Sir Godrick of Longshaw was entrusted with "the errand of warding chapmen and others who were bringing" goods and supplies, treating their safe passage as a responsibility of his lordship (044-009). When he encountered a group of chapmen on the road, "he entreated them courteously" and after hearing him out, "the chapmen laid their heads together" in consultation before responding (044-001, 044-002).

Late in the tale, a group of three chapmen plays a significant role in the narrative involving a female character. The youngest of the three chapmen urged action, and when the chapmen "looked keenly on her" they assessed the situation (062-006, 062-007). Ultimately "the chapmen have awakened and have striven" on behalf of those in need (063-004).

## Cultural Significance

The chapmen represent the commercial dimension of the Sundering Flood world — a reminder that beyond the heroic deeds of knights and the pastoral life of the Dales, there existed a network of trade and exchange. Their presence at markets, festivals, and along the roads connected the scattered communities of the novel, making them agents of both economic and cultural exchange. The distinction Morris draws between chapmen and men-at-arms (044-001) reflects the medieval social order in which merchants occupied a category separate from those who fought and those who prayed.
