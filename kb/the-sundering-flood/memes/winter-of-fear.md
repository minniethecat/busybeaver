# Winter of Fear

A legendary winter of such severity that the Sundering Flood would be overlaid with ice, temporarily removing the natural barrier that separated the eastern and western dales — an event both dreaded and secretly desired by those separated by the river.

## Nature

The Winter of Fear was spoken of as a potential catastrophe that would freeze the Sundering Flood solid. In normal winters, the snow and the frost was not very hard in that land (006-002).

Many would have been glad if it were, for then might the Sundering Flood have been laid with ice — which never betid (006-002). The concept carried dual meanings: for those separated by the river, a frozen Flood would allow passage across; but for the community at large, such a winter would mean devastation to crops, livestock, and the old and vulnerable.

The days wore away to winter, and ever thereafter might Osberne do what he would and go where he would, for as little a lad as he was (006-001). But the winter wore away at [[wethermel]] with nought to tell of (006-010).

## Cultural Significance

The Winter of Fear was invoked in prayers and oaths. When Osberne saw Hartshaw Knolls and wished to be there, his grandsire replied it would be "long and long" before anyone could encompass the Sundering Flood unless the Winter of Fear came upon them, and all the land were overlaid with ice, and the waters of the Flood be stayed (006-003).

The grandsire added "which may God and Allhallows forfend," treating the Winter of Fear as a genuine calamity to be prayed against (006-003). The Winter of Fear thus embodied the tension between the desire for union and the fear of destruction.

## Narrative Role

The Winter of Fear never actually occurred in the story — the Flood was never laid with ice in Osberne's lifetime (006-002). This absence reinforced the permanence of the separation between the eastern and western dales.

Osberne's eventual reunion with [[elfhild]] thus depended on other means — his military campaigns and the defeat of hostile forces. The concept served as a narrative device articulating both hope and its impossibility through natural means.
