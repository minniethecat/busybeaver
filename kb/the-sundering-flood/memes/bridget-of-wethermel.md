# Bridget of Wethermel

**Bridget** is an old kinswoman of the house at Wethermel, a carline of seventy winters who had fostered the late goodman (Osberne's father) and now serves as foster-mother to the young Osberne [002-003]. She is one of the four permanent dwellers at Wethermel when the tale begins.

## Nature and Role

Bridget is a warm, affectionate old woman who dotes upon Osberne. She follows the same road as the goodwife in hugging and kissing the boy when he returns safely from peril [002-007]. Osberne counts her among his "three mothers": his birth-mother, the goodwife, and Bridget — his foster-mother [003-008].

## Folk-Magic and Protective Charms

Bridget possesses knowledge of charms and protective magic. She gave Osberne a parchment scored with the Holy Rood and the names of the three Holy Kings, sealed in a silk bag to wear around his neck as "strong neckguard against all evil things" [002-008]. This charm, which she had from the priest and which bore "clerks' Latin" writing, was meant to protect Osberne from the Dwarf-kin and Land-wights.

When Osberne returns from his nightlong adventure with the Dwarf, Bridget attributes his safe return to her charm: "See you, kinsmen, if it be not my doing that the blessed bairn has come back to us" [002-007].

## Later Years

Bridget remains at Wethermel through the years of Osberne's adventures. When strangers come to Wethermel in later chapters, she is still there — "not much aged in the five years" that have passed, and as kind as ever [053-001].

## Significance

Bridget represents the old, half-pagan folk-wisdom of the Dale, blending Christian symbols (the Holy Rood, the three Kings) with older protective magic. Her charms and the Dwarf's gifts together form the twin supernatural protections that watch over Osberne's childhood.
