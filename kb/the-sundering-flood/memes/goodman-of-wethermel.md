# Goodman of Wethermel

The **Goodman of Wethermel** is Osberne's grandsire and the master of the Wethermel stead at the beginning of the tale. He is a man of about fifty winters, hale and stout, who works the land alongside his wife (the goodwife), an old kinswoman named Bridget, and the young Osberne.

## Nature

A grudging, grumbling man of the old Dale breed — proud, warrior-like, and stubborn. He is quick to anger, easily provoked by talk of Wethermel's ill-luck, and not above striking his hired man John across the face. Yet beneath his rough exterior he loves and treasures the young Osberne, though he shows it more through discipline than affection [003-005]. He is also notably superstitious, regarding the Dwarf-kin and Land-wights with wary respect and disliking the old carline Bridget's charms and parchments [002-006].

## At Wethermel

The stead of Wethermel had "no great name for luck," and folk who had the choice would liever dwell otherwhere, so that it was hard for the goodman to get men to work there for hire [002-002]. Many deemed the knoll had been of old time a dwelling of the Dwarfs, who grudged that the children of Adam had supplanted them [002-002]. Despite this, the goodman persevered, working the corn-land on the knoll's southwest face and pasturing kine, horses, and sheep on the rich grasslands to the water's lip [002-001].

He had lost his own son (Osberne's father) at a young age, and his son's wife followed within a month or two, leaving him and the goodwife to raise the child Osberne [002-003].

## Temper and Discipline

The goodman is a man of quick wrath and hard words. When the hired man John returns from the bents having lost seventeen wethers to wolves, the goodman "berated him for his cowardice, and seemed to begrudge him his victuals somewhat that night" [003-001]. When John speaks of skin-changers and Wethermel's ill-luck, the goodman "waxed wood-wrath" and struck him across the face [003-005].

He threatens Osberne with beatings for straying into peril, yet on more than one occasion the women's prayers and Osberne's clever staves save the boy from the promised drubbing [002-008].

## Significance

The goodman represents the old generation of the Dale — stubborn, hard-handed, suspicious of strangers, yet fiercely protective of his bloodline. His grudging acceptance of Steelhead as a guest at Wethermel, despite his mutterings about "runagates," shows that Osberne's growing mastery in the household has begun to eclipse his grandsire's authority [012-001]. He stands as the unglamorous but necessary foundation from which Osberne rises.
