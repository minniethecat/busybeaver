Surly John is among the folk of [[Wethermel]], "who is henceforth
called Surly John" (004-004) for his churlish temper. While not
a major character, his act of bringing [[Hardcastle]] to the
steading triggers one of the novel's key early events.

## Identity

- **Name Origin**: Earned his epithet for his dour and quarrelsome
  nature
- **Role**: Retainer and worker at Wethermel
- **Position**: Marginal — "all men save Surly John were well with
  Osberne" (006-009)

## Biography

### The Hiring

Surly John is taken into service alongside [[Stephen the Eater]],
but where Stephen proves loyal and good-humored, John is sullen
and difficult. Osberne's grandmother, the goodwife, has a "soft,
kind word for all, save Surly John" (006-006).

### The Taunting

The other men of Wethermel amuse themselves by mocking Surly John
with a satirical verse, which he must "abide the telling of"
(006-007). His reaction — cursing and bitterness — only deepens
the household's division against him.

### Bringing Hardcastle

Surly John's most consequential act is leading [[Hardcastle]]
to Wethermel: "May Hardcastle be here tonight, ye folk?"
(015-001). Whether he acted from malice or fear is unclear, but
his introduction of the would-be usurper nearly costs Osberne
his home. When Osberne confronts Hardcastle, Surly John "laughed,
and made a mow" (015-002), revealing his petty nature.

## Relationships

- [[Osberne]] — his lord, who tolerates him
- [[Stephen the Eater]] — fellow retainer, a contrast in loyalty
- [[Hardcastle]] — the dangerous guest he brought to Wethermel
