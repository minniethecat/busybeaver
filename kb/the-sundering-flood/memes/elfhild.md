# Elfhild

Elfhild is the co-protagonist of William Morris's *The Sundering Flood*, the maiden who dwells on the western side of the great river. Her bond with Osberne of [[wethermel]] — sustained through conversation and gift-exchange across the impassable Sundering Flood — forms the emotional heart of the novel. From their first meeting as children to their reunion after years of war and abduction, Elfhild's story is one of constancy, resourcefulness, and love that endures the harshest separation.

## Identity

Elfhild is introduced as "a little maiden of about his own age, with ruddy golden hair streaming down from her head" (009-005). Her full name is Elfhild of Hartshaw Knolls (011-013). She is sometimes referred to simply as "the maiden across the Flood" or "the Maiden" in later chapters. She is a shepherdess by trade, herding sheep on the western side of the Sundering Flood (009-015).

## Role in the Story

Elfhild serves as the co-protagonist and love interest of the novel. She is not a passive damsel — she is resourceful, brave, and constant. Her role is fourfold: as Osberne's childhood companion and the emotional anchor that drives his quest; as a recipient of mysterious cave-folk gifts that connect the story to the ancient and magical world; as a victim of abduction whose disappearance propels the plot into its war narrative; and as the living reward and symbol of peace restored when the lovers are reunited at Wethermel.

## Biography

### Early Life / Origin

Elfhild was born at Hartshaw Knolls, a stead on the western side of the Sundering Flood (011-013). She lived there with her kinswomen — two aunts, described as "one stiff, as if she were cut out of timber, and the other exceeding fat" (009-014). She was put to work as a shepherdess from a very young age, and her kinswomen were often unkind to her; if sheep went missing, "then would I be wyted or even whipped for what was no fault of mine" (009-015).

She discovered the cave in the cliff-face by the river when she "was very little, and the women had been less than kind" to her, and it became her refuge (009-014). The cave was rumoured to be a dwelling of the [[dwarfs]], which kept her kinswomen from following her there (009-014). From this cave, she received three mysterious gifts from unseen cave-folk: a shepherd's pipe whose music compelled sheep to come to her (009-015), a magnificent necklace of gold and gems — emerald, sapphire, ruby — that "scarce a queen had got aught so fair in her treasure" (010-001), and healing that cured her whenever she fell sick and slept in the cave (010-002).

### Separation from Osberne

Elfhild and Osberne met as children across the Sundering Flood at the [[cloven-knoll|Bight of the Cloven Knoll]], and for years they sustained their bond through conversation across the water and gifts shot from Osberne's bow (012-002). But as spring returned, Osberne "kept [[tryst]] thrice and Elfhild came not, and the fourth time she came and had tidings, to wit that one of her kinswomen had died of sickness" (019-007), leaving her with [[the-carline]] Anna as her sole protector.

Elfhild was nearly abducted once before the final attack. A tall man clad in a long black gown attempted to steal her, but the carline Anna used her wits to "egg him on and draw him back" until she could spirit Elfhild into the nearby shaw (030-018). However, when the [[black-skinners|Red Skinners]] invaded the West Dale, the aliens "poured on to the ness, howling like dogs, and on to Elfhild's very standing-place" (032-015). Elfhild was abducted during this attack, and her house and garth at Hartshaw Knolls lay waste thereafter (035-001). Osberne knew she was gone, "and either slain or carried away to where he should never hear of her or see her again" (034-003).

### Life Across the Flood

During the years of separation, Elfhild lived with the carline — an elderly woman of mysterious power who was more than she seemed. The carline "had more of a might than were to be looked for of so old and frail-seeming a woman" (030-015). She served as Elfhild's protector, guide, and companion through the danger that followed the abduction.

After being bought by a merchant who desired her and taken eastward across the mountains (055-002), Elfhild lived at Brookside in a small cot, waiting through the long years. Though "oftenest the Maiden was patient, and abode the sundering with no ill cheer," sometimes "her trouble was over heavy for her, and she would wander forth into the wood or the field, and go weeping and lamenting there" (054-003). The carline comforted her with "some little inkling of the hope which she, the old woman, had conceived in her heart, that the meeting of those two should come about whiles they were yet young and lovely" (054-005).

Osberne searched unceasingly for her. He "spared not to question every wight whom he deemed anywise likely to have heard aught of Elfhild; and heavy and grievous became the words of his questioning, and ever his heart sickened before the answer came" (045-003). He travelled twenty days upriver seeking news of her, but "got no good, and were no nearer to hearing a true word of Elfhild than ever" (045-004). He told his lord Sir Godrick "the whole tale of his dealings with Elfhild, and how she had vanished away before hand might touch hand or face face" (043-012).

### Reunion

The reunion came at last at the house of Brookside, where the carline led Osberne. When Osberne entered the cot, he "scarce knew where he was, but looked down on the floor, as though the Sundering Flood of the Dales rolled betwixt him and the maiden; for indeed when his eyes first fell upon her he knew that it was Elfhild" (052-015). That very night, three felons — the same White Knight and his men who had attacked Osberne earlier — entered the house and threatened Elfhild, but Osberne stood between them, revealed himself, and slew them with [[boardcleaver]] (052-017).

After crossing through [[wood-masterless|the Wood Masterless]] with the help of the Sub-prior and the Knight of [[longshaw]] (064-012), Elfhild came to Wethermel. In a triumphant scene at the hall, "Elfhild arose and cast off her grey clothes, and was clad thereunder in the finest of fine gear of gold and of green, and surely, said everybody, that never was such beauty seen in hall" (065-001). She and Osberne were united before the assembled folk of the Dale.

In the final chapter, on "the crown of all midsummer days," she and Osberne walked together along the very lip of the Flood to the Bight of the Cloven Knoll (066-001). She looked toward the cave where they first met and showed Osberne that she still had the shepherd's pipe, though its magic power had departed (066-001). She told him "a tale which was sweet and pleasant, and little like to those terrible things that had happened to those two since they were sundered by the Flood" (066-004).

### Gifts and the Cave-Folk

The three gifts Elfhild received from the cave-folk are the most overt supernatural element in the novel. The pipe came first, when she wept in the cave after being punished for lost sheep; it gave her the power to call and control her flock (009-015). The necklace of gold and gems came second, when she lamented that she had nothing to deck herself with like other maidens (010-001). The healing sleep came third, curing her of sickness whenever she lay down in the cave and fell into a drowsy slumber (010-002). These gifts, which she dared not show to her kinswomen "who would have taken them away from me and cried horror at them" (010-001), connected her to an older, magical world that underlies the medieval surface of the story.

## Character Significance

Elfhild embodies constancy and the power of love sustained across impossible distance. She is not merely a love-interest but a co-protagonist whose experiences mirror and complement Osberne's martial journey: where he fights [[events-index|battles]] and slays enemies, she endures captivity, survives by her wits, and preserves hope through years of uncertainty. Her connection to the cave-folk marks her as touched by the numinous, and her shepherd's pipe — which loses its magic once she is reunited with Osberne — symbolizes that the supernatural aid was always in service of bringing the lovers together. The Knight of Longshaw, in the novel's closing line, is said to be much pleased "to look upon the beauty of Elfhild and the fairness of the life that men lived in the Dale" (068-001), making her the living emblem of the peace and beauty that the novel's long struggle finally secures.
