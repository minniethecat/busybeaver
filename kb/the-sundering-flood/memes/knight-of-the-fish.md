# Knight of the Fish

Sir Raynold Fisher, the lord of the Castle of the Fish, a fair white castle on a hill whose banner bore "two silver Fish addorsed on a blue ground" (041-001). He maintained an ancient custom requiring all armed parties to joust with him before passing his lands, and was encountered and overthrown by [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick of Longshaw]] and [[osberne|Osberne the Red Lad]] during their journey to the [[city-of-the-sundering-flood|City of the Sundering Flood]].

## The Custom of the Castle of the Fish

The Castle of the Fish had maintained for a hundred years the custom that any armed party exceeding ten riders must halt and joust with the lord of the castle: "two of theirs against two of his, and must run with sharp spears of war till one side is overthrown or sorely hurt" (041-002). The custom was celebrated as an "honorable" tradition, though [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick]] called it "an evil custom" that sorted ill with his urgent errand (041-003).

## The Joust

When Sir Godrick's party approached the Castle of the Fish, a herald formally challenged them. Sir Godrick, though wroth at the delay, accepted the challenge under the name "the Knight of the Weary-Strife" (041-004). The joust paired Sir Godrick against the Knight of the Fish and Osberne against the Knight's champion, a "very tall, stark man-at-arms" (041-006).

The contest was brief and decisive. Sir Godrick and the Fish broke spears in the first course, with the Castle-knight losing his stirrups. Osberne struck his opponent with surgical precision: "he set his spear-point in the default of the long man's defence just where arm joins shoulder, and the spear went through and through him" (041-006). In the second course, Sir Godrick drove the Knight of the Fish "clean over the arson of his saddle" (041-006).

## Aftermath

Despite his defeat, the Knight of the Fish proved good-natured and "debonnaire in his folly" (041-008). He invited the victors into his castle to "drink a cup with us for the washing away of all grudge against the honorable custom of the Fish" (041-008). Sir Godrick declined but departed with good will. The Knight further promised that if his wounded champion recovered, he would send him to serve Sir Godrick — a testament to the chivalric spirit that governed even foolish customs in Morris's medieval world.

## Significance

The episode of the Knight of the Fish provides a moment of chivalric comedy in the otherwise grim [[war-of-the-dales|War of the Dales]] narrative. The custom of the Fish — formalized violence without malice — contrasts with the brutal warfare of the [[barons-league|Barons' League]] and the [[black-skinners|Black Skinners]]. Sir Raynold's good humor in defeat and his offer of his champion's service demonstrate the ideal of knightly courtesy that William Morris valued.
