# Knight of Longshaw

The Knight of Longshaw is the formal title by which [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick]] is known as the lord and war-leader of [[longshaw|Longshaw]]. This epithet appears throughout the later chapters of the narrative, marking his stature as a chivalric figure of renown whose deeds and uprising become the talk of the countryside.

## The Title and Its Bearer

Sir Godrick of Longshaw first appears at [[eastcheaping|Eastcheaping]], where he seeks good men for his fellowship (039-002). He is introduced as "Sir Godrick of Longshaw, who is here with us in Eastcheaping" (039-004), and from that point forward the title "Knight of Longshaw" becomes synonymous with his name and cause. The narrative explicitly links the two, noting that "their talk was concerning the Knight of Longshaw, Sir Godrick, and his uprising" (055-007).

## The Uprising Against the Barons

As the Knight of Longshaw, Sir Godrick leads the resistance against the [[barons-league|Barons' League]], gathering men from across the land to his banner. His cause draws supporters including [[osberne|Osberne]] the Red Lad, who fights at his side through the war. Even distant folk speak of "that mighty and good lord, the Knight of Longshaw" and his victories against the oppressors (063-007). The [[sir-medard|Porte of Eastcheaping]] speaks confidently: "I look for it that the Knight of Longshaw will yet give this league a tumble" (059-002).

## The Reunion at Wethermel

In the novel's climactic reunion, the Knight of Longshaw appears at [[wethermel|Wethermel]] itself, where Osberne runs to him and casts his arms about him in joy (067-001). He witnesses the reunion of Osberne and [[elfhild|Elfhild]], standing with them hand in hand as the long-sundered lovers are brought together at last (067-002). He abides at Wethermel "in much content" before receiving Osberne's farewell, the young hero weeping as he bids his lord depart (068-001) (068-004).

## Character Assessment

The title "Knight of Longshaw" elevates Sir Godrick from a mere named character to an almost archetypal figure — the good knight fighting against tyranny. Morris uses this formal epithet consistently in passages that emphasize Godrick's public role and reputation, reserving "Sir Godrick" for more intimate narrative moments. The dual naming reflects the medieval romance tradition in which a knight is known by both his personal name and his territorial title, each carrying distinct narrative weight.
