# Wood Masterless

A vast and wild forest on the eastern side of the Sundering Flood, stretching between the settled lands of Wethermel and Longshaw. Also called the Masterless Wood, it was a lawless borderland — neither claimed nor tamed by any lord (039-007).

## Geography and Extent

The Wood Masterless lay on the eastern side of the Sundering Flood, forming a natural barrier between the northern and southern dales. It was "a great waste and forest lying betwixt Longshaw and the Flood" (039-007).

The river ran through this forest as "a mighty great river, whereon are barges and cutters and seagoing dromonds even, so that it sunders nought, but joins rather" (039-007). The southernmost skirts of the Wood came down within a score of miles of the great City of the Sundering Flood (039-007).

The beginning of the Wood Masterless was at a place called Woodneb, where a captain of Sir Godrick's named Edward the Brown held a stronghold (043-006). From Woodneb onward, the land was rough and sparsely inhabited — travelers saw "but three little houses of hunters or fowlers" over two days' ride (044-001). The road through the Wood Masterless led onward to Longshaw, a twelve days' journey from Woodneb (043-006).

## Strategic Importance

The Wood Masterless was both a barrier and a corridor. It was the nearest way to Longshaw for those traveling from the north, though its wildness made passage dangerous (040-013). The wood's borders ran nigh unto the Flood, making it a strategic route for forces moving along the river (043-011).

Hostile barons used the Wood Masterless as a staging ground to harry Sir Godrick's lands. The barons who held of the King and [[porte-of-eastcheaping|the Porte]] on the east side of the Wood harried Sir Godrick "from time to time" (039-007). Sir Godrick noted that the King and the Porte would not meddle with him themselves, but "set two or three of the barons who hold of them on the east side of the Wood Masterless to harry me" (039-007).

After the [[battle-of-the-sundering-flood|great battle]], Sir Godrick sent forces to clear the wood's borders so he might "carry on his sieges of the strongholds without let or hindrance" (045-010).

## Inhabitants and Dangers

The Masterless Wood was a refuge for outlaws: "rufflers and runagates and strong-thieves" (039-016). The [[black-skinners|Red Skinners]], a tribe of ill-doers, were known to haunt the borders of the Wood Masterless nigh unto the Flood (043-011). Sir Godrick told [[osberne]] that it was mostly the other side of the Sundering Flood which the Red Skinners haunted, "though whiles we meet them about the borders of the Wood Masterless nigh unto the Flood" (043-011).

Travelers from the City of the Sundering Flood rode through the Wood Masterless on their way to the eastern settlements (044-001). Osberne asked much concerning [[elfhild]] of chapmen passing through the wood, and "they could tell him many tales of the Red Skinners and their misdeeds" (044-009).

## Osberne and the Wood

Osberne's relationship with the Wood Masterless evolved through the narrative. After the great battle and peace, he spent more time at Longshaw and the borders of the Wood than in the city, "asking of numberless folk" about Elfhild (049-003). His search through the Wood yielded only the same answer — no word of her — and he grew "sick at heart of forever asking questions to which came evermore but one answer" (045-012).

Late in the tale, Elfhild and [[the-carline]] crossed the Flood and obtained "guide and guards through the Wood Masterless" from the good brothers, who furnished them with "way-beasts and guides" to find a dwelling there (064-012) (064-013) (064-014). The Wood, once a barrier and a haunt of enemies, became the path to their new life.
