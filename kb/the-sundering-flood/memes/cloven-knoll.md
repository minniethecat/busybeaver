# Cloven Knoll

A landmark on the Sundering Flood river, formed where a knoll had been cast down across the stream, creating a sheltered bight on the eastern side that became the [[tryst]]ing place of Osberne and Elfhild, the site of a decisive battle against the Black Skinners, and the geographical and emotional heart of the story.

## Geography

The Cloven Knoll was formed where a section of the riverbank — a knoll — had collapsed or been cast down across the stream, partially blocking the river's course (009-003). The stream had washed away much of the fallen earth, but what remained created a distinctive bight — a sheltered curve in the riverbank on the eastern side. The Dalesmen on either side of the Flood called it the Bight of the Cloven Knoll (009-003).

At this point, the Sundering Flood narrowed somewhat, and the water swirled in a whirlpool that turned away from the cliff face (009-003). In the face of the wall, in the bight where the whirlpool turned, was a cave the height of a tall man — a hollow under a ledge thrust out from the sheer rock, hanging over the terrible water (009-003). It was within this cave that Osberne first glimpsed Elfhild on the western bank, and she saw him framed in the rocky opening.

The Cloven Knoll lay about two miles up the river from where Osberne first reached the water's edge on his early explorations (009-003). It was within his accustomed range from Wethermel and accessible by following the riverbank upstream. The [[dwarfs]]' cave lay nearby, a place that some Dalesmen feared even in the turmoil of battle, "deeming it might be unlucky to them" (032-013).

On the western side, opposite the bight, was a ness or crown of land where Elfhild was wont to stand during their trysts (033-001). This western crown later became the place where the Westdalers' chiefs stood among the corpses of the slain aliens after the shot-stour (033-001).

Nearby, the terrain tightened into narrows above the Bight — a pinch point along the river where the stream grew narrower and the banks drew closer, making it possible for archers on both sides to shoot across the water (032-011, 032-012).

## The Trysting Place

The Bight of the Cloven Knoll was the designated meeting place for Osberne and Elfhild, the two lovers separated by the Sundering Flood. It was here, on opposite banks of the uncrossable river, that they met and spoke across the swirling water throughout their youth.

Osberne first saw Elfhild from the cave beneath the ledge at the Cloven Knoll. She appeared on the western bank, and they spoke — the first of countless trysts that would come to define both their lives (009-003). The trysts became regular: Osberne went armed with [[boardcleaver]], aware of the dangers of the region (014-005). Elfhild came to the trysting-place clad in the fair weed he had given her, sometimes with a garland of white May blossom about her shoulders (022-003).

In winter, when the snow was on the earth and the grass grew not, the trysts were fewer and farther between — the sheep were shut up in folds and cotes, and Elfhild struggled to find pretense to leave her house at Hartshaw Knolls (019-005). Not seldom she failed at the trysting-place, and Osberne had to go sorrowfully away (019-005). Yet he tarried at the Cloven Knoll many times, waiting for her to appear on the western bank (030-002).

After the battle with the Black Skinners, and after learning that Hartshaw Knolls lay waste with no newcomer there, Osberne went every third day all alone to the Bight of the Cloven Knoll and sat there long through the day — but never had any tidings of Elfhild (035-001).

Much later, during his campaigns with [[sir-godrick]] far from the Dale, Osberne stood at the mouth of the Sundering Flood and beheld the great water for the first time since leaving home. His sorrow stirred within him when he thought "how far they two had come from the Bight of the Cloven Knoll, he and the Sundering Flood" (044-009). The Cloven Knoll remained the fixed emotional center of his world, the measure of all distance and all loss.

## The Song Across the Water

At the height of the [[cloven-motes|midsummer Cloven Mote]]te]], the folk of the East and West Dales ranged themselves each in a line on their own shore of the Sundering Flood and sang staves from side to side across the water, the Westdalers beginning, and the Eastdalers taking up the response (036-001). Though the narration does not specify that this ritual occurred at the Cloven Knoll, the narrowing of the river there — which made hearing and shooting across possible — would have made it one of the few places where such a cross-river chorus could be clearly heard.

## The Shot-Stour

The Cloven Knoll was the site of one of the most significant [[events-index|battles]] in the Dales' history — the shot-stour against the Black Skinners (strong-thieves). As the aliens thronged down the Dale on the western bank, burning and wasting as they went, Osberne mustered the Eastdalers along the riverbank with bows and sling-spears (032-004).

Both sides were inevitably "drifting toward the Bight of the Cloven Knoll" — the narrows above the bight being the place where the stream grew narrower and archery across the water became possible (032-011, 032-012). As the Skinners saw the stream narrowing, they set up a whoop and drew closer to the Eastdalers; they loosed shafts and hurt divers of the Dalesmen, but Osberne held his shooters' fire (032-012).

The tension reached its peak when the gold-decked chieftain of the Skinners appeared on the far bank, poising a great spear. In that moment, Osberne cast his own weapon with a fierce shout, and all around him and behind him "he heard the loose of the Dalesmen's bows" (032-015). The chieftain and his spear fell clashing into the green water, and "many of the strong-thieves fell before the shaft-storm of the Dalesmen" (032-015). The Skinners shot back, and some Dalesmen were slain and divers hurt, "but that abated their hearts no jot" (032-015).

The battle turned when the Westdalers, who had been shadowing the Skinners from the landward side, fell upon them with spear, axe, and sword (032-017). "The Eastdalers knew the voice of their kinsmen" across the water and shouted in answer as they plied the bow (032-017). The Skinners broke and fled, got their horses, and rode away — the Dalesmen "saw them never again" (032-018).

After the battle, Osberne and his Wethermelers stood on the east side of the Cloven Knoll, not very triumphantly because of their slain (033-001). The chiefs of the Westdalers, including Wulfstan of Coldburne, came and "stood among the corpses of the aliens on the crown of the ness where Elfhild was wont to stand" — the very trysting place now a field of dead (033-001).

The Eastdalers who fell in the shot-stour were laid in earth "but a little way from the Bight of the Cloven Knoll" at a place thereafter called Shooters' Knowe (033-003). The slain Skinners were dragged away by the Westdalers — "for that they feared the begrudging of the Dwarfs" — and buried under a great howe called Thieves' Howe (034-011). The tally of Skinners slain by the Eastdalers' arrows was three score and ten and seven (034-011).

## Return and Reunion

After all his wanderings and wars — the campaigns with Sir Godrick, the capture of the [[baron-of-deepdale]], the revolution in the [[city-of-the-sundering-flood]], the defeat of the Outland king, and the long search for Elfhild — Osberne finally returned to the Dale with Elfhild beside him. They walked together "along the very lip of the Flood to the Bight of the Cloven Knoll" on "the crown of all midsummer days" (066-001).

At the Cloven Knoll, they looked across the water for a while and said nothing (066-001). Elfhild looked curiously toward the cave wherein Osberne had first espied her, and asked how it would be if there were another one there — and he laughed and said there was not another one (066-001). She reminded him of the game she played with the shepherd's pipe, how the sheep came bundling toward her — a memory of their earliest days of trysting across the Flood.

This final scene at the Cloven Knoll closes the circle of the story. The place that had been the scene of their first meeting, their youthful trysts, their separation after the Skinners' raid, and Osberne's long sorrowful vigils — now became the place of their reunion, no longer separated by the water but walking together on the same eastern bank.
