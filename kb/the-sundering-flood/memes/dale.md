# The Dale

**The Dale** is the valley region along the Sundering Flood where the main pastoral communities of the tale dwell. It is the homeland of Osberne, the Dalesmen, and the rustic free folk who stand apart from the feudal society of the City and the Barons.

## Geography

The Dale runs along both sides of the Sundering Flood, stretching from the mountains at the head of the valley down toward the more settled lands. It is divided into East Dale and West Dale by the great river itself.

The upper reaches of the Dale are rough and wild, with Wethermel being "the last house but one" toward the mountains [002-001]. Downstream, the Dale opens into broader plains and eventually reaches Eastcheaping and the settled lands.

## The Folk of the Dale

The folk of the Dale — the Dalesmen — are described as "both proud and warrior-like, as if they were earls' kindred" [058-006]. They are free folk who owe no allegiance to any lord. Their governance is communal, centred on the Cloven Motes — regular gatherings where disputes are settled and common decisions made [002-003].

Their way of life is pastoral: sheep-herding on the eastern bents, cattle and horses in the valley pastures, and corn grown on the sunny slopes of knolls like Wethermel's [002-001]. It is a hard life, beset by wolves, raiders, and the ever-present dangers of the wild margins.

## Perils

The Dale is not a peaceful pastoral idyll. Perils include:
- **Wolves** — "huge and fierce beyond measure" that harry the flocks [003-001]
- **Raiders** — outlaws from over the mountain-necks who steal sheep and cattle [002-003]
- **Dwarf-kin** — the Land-wights whose ancient dwellings underlie the knolls [002-002]
- **The Flood itself** — "the deep and hurrying stream" that could sweep away a child [002-003]

## Role in the Tale

The Dale is the cradle from which Osberne rises. His childhood among the sheep, his encounters with the Dwarf-kin in the wooded bents, his training of the Dalesmen in the staves, and his eventual departure from Wethermel — all are grounded in the Dale's rhythms and its people. The Dale represents the pastoral world that Morris loved and celebrated, standing in deliberate contrast to the urban politics of the City and the feudal ambitions of the Barons.
