# Midsummer Feast

The Midsummer Feast was the great summer festival of the Sundering Flood world, held at the summer solstice when "night and day hold each other" in balance (053-004), combining the sacred gathering of the Cloven Mote with seasonal markets and folk celebrations.

## Nature

Midsummer is a seasonal festival and religious observance — the summer counterpart to the midwinter Cloven Mote, and one of the two great turning points of the year in the calendar of the Dales. It served simultaneously as a sacred gathering, a commercial fair, and a time of folk custom and celebration.

## Description

The anticipation of Midsummer shaped the rhythms of Dales life. Osberne noted that "it lacked but three nights of Midsummer" when he made an important decision, marking time by the approach of the festival (035-002). He planned his departure from Wethermel with the Feast as his deadline, resolving that "when the Midsummer Feast is over I shall say farewell" to his home (035-012). The Feast thus served as a temporal anchor — a fixed point in the year around which personal decisions and communal events were organized.

The central event was the Midsummer Feast of the Cloven Mote, which drew together the folk of the East and West Dales in a great assembly (036-001). As with the midwinter gathering, this was a time for worship, for the settling of community business, and for the reaffirmation of the bonds that held the scattered Dales together across the barrier of the Sundering Flood.

The Midsummer markets were held in the meadows, concurrent with the Feast (058-009). These were among the great seasonal fairs of the year — alongside the Michaelmas market in autumn — drawing chapmen and traders from distant lands to sell their wares to the gathered Dalesmen.

## Folk Customs

Midsummer was the occasion for distinctive folk practices. "Above all on Midsummer night, the folk would pluck up" certain plants or tokens, a custom associated with the magical properties of the solstice (001-011). The night when daylight lingered longest held a numinous quality, a time when the boundary between the ordinary and the supernatural seemed thin.

## Role in the Story

Midsummer marks significant transitions in the narrative. For Osberne, it was the season of departures and new beginnings — he used the Feast as the milestone after which he would leave his childhood home (035-012). The novel's chronology is anchored to Midsummer: a key event is dated "on the morrow of Midsummer, five years to the day" since another pivotal moment (053-001). This careful marking of Midsummer anniversaries underscores the festival's importance as a temporal reference point in the world of the tale.

## Cultural Significance

Midsummer in The Sundering Flood represents the cyclical rhythm of Dales life — the balance point of the year, the season of fullness before the turning toward winter. As both a sacred feast and a commercial fair, it embodied the integration of religious, social, and economic life that characterized the medieval world Morris sought to evoke. The Midsummer markets (058-009) show how even the most isolated Dales communities were connected to wider networks of trade through these seasonal gatherings.
