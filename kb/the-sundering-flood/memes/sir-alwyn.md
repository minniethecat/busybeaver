# Sir Alwyn

The lord of [[warding-knowe|Warding Knowe]], "a fair white house garnished with towers on a knoll" (056-006), and a dear friend of Sir Mark, the [[blue-knight|Blue Knight]]. A knight "well on in years, more than grizzled, but a stout and stark knight" (057-001), Sir Alwyn was a member of the [[barons-league|Barons' League]] who possessed remarkable foresight and provided crucial hospitality to the [[the-maiden|Maiden]] on her journey to [[brookside|Brookside]].

## Hospitality at Warding Knowe

When the [[blue-knight|Blue Knight]] brought the [[the-maiden|Maiden]] and [[the-carline|Carline]] to Warding Knowe, Sir Alwyn stood at the gate "bidding them a free and fair welcome" (057-001). He embraced Sir Mark warmly and treated the Maiden with marked honour — taking her by the hand, leading her in personally, and later consulting the Blue Knight to ensure he had not erred in the degree of honor shown (057-001).

## Political Wisdom

At supper, Sir Alwyn demonstrated keen political insight. He assessed the [[city-king|King of the City]] as "of little account" — "feathered-headed, stubborn in wrong, and hard-hearted" — and warned that unless the King allied with "the best men of the City, as the Porte and the masters of the Great Crafts, he was undone" (057-003). He also spoke of the rising [[eastcheaping-crafts|Small Crafts]] who might rise against both [[porte-of-eastcheaping|Porte]] and King, suggesting that with [[sir-godrick|Sir Godrick]] on their side, "they will perchance come to their above" (057-004).

Most presciently, Sir Alwyn envisioned a companion for Sir Godrick — "one who should be even better than his double, filling up what little lacks there may be in him; one who should cheer the heart of his host as much even as the captain, and yet should be liker to the men themselves" (057-006). This description perfectly anticipated [[osberne|Osberne the Red Lad]], though Sir Alwyn did not yet know his name. The [[the-carline|Carline]], overhearing this, wondered "if this old knight is somewhat wise of foresight" (057-009).

## Prophecy of Death

Sir Alwyn's most remarkable quality was his foresight of his own death. When the Carline privately asked him "if thou hast any inkling of what thine end shall be," he answered with chilling clarity: "I shall fall in battle, and in that said battle our backs shall be turned toward the foe and our faces toward the world beyond; and this shall be ere the earth is eighteen months older" (057-010). This prophecy, given without bitterness or fear, marked Sir Alwyn as one of the few characters in the narrative who could see beyond the veil of ordinary perception.

## Warding Knowe's Defense

Sir Alwyn's stronghold served as "a bar against the strong-thieves of the forest and thereabout" (057-011). After the Maiden's party departed, "the franklin's men turned back home" — their duty as escorts ended at Warding Knowe, beyond which Sir Alwyn's protection made the roads safer. The stronghold's strategic position in the grassy valley made it an important bastion of order in a land threatened by both forest outlaws and the larger political turmoil of the [[war-of-the-dales|War of the Dales]].

## Character Significance

Sir Alwyn represents the ideal of aged knighthood in Morris's medieval world — wise rather than merely strong, hospitable rather than merely martial, facing his prophesied death with stoic dignity. His recognition of Osberne's potential before ever meeting him foreshadows the Red Lad's emergence as Sir Godrick's indispensable lieutenant. As a member of the Barons' League who can nonetheless recognize Sir Godrick's superiority, he embodies the complexity of the feudal landscape — where personal honor and political allegiance did not always align.
