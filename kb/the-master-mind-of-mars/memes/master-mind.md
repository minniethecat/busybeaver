## Master Mind of Mars

The **Master Mind of Mars** is the epithet for [[Ras Thavas]], the preeminent surgeon of Barsoom, whose unparalleled skill in brain transplantation and bodily restoration forms the central premise of the novel. The title is not merely descriptive of his surgical genius but reflects a fundamental claim about the nature of intelligence and power on Barsoom: that "in my world nothing counts but brain" [002-002]. Ras Thavas's cold, calculating intellect and his deliberate suppression of sentiment make him simultaneously the most capable and the most dangerous figure in the narrative.

### The Title and Its Meaning

The appellation "Master Mind of Mars" operates on multiple levels. Most literally, it acknowledges Ras Thavas as a surgeon of unprecedented skill, one whose technique "baffled description" [001-056]. But the title also encodes Ras Thavas's own philosophical position: his belief that intellect alone should govern action, unclouded by sentiment or moral consideration. When asked why he does not bow to the Jeddak of Toonol, Ras Thavas replies simply, "In my world nothing counts but brain and in that respect, and without egotism, I may say that I acknowledge no superior" [002-002]. This is not arrogance in the ordinary sense but a categorical rejection of the principle -- hereditary rank -- upon which Barsoomian society is organized. In claiming this title, Ras Thavas asserts the supremacy of merit over birth, of reason over sentiment.

Yet the title also carries an ironic dimension. For all his intellectual mastery, Ras Thavas is ultimately dependent on the very political structures he disdains: he must flee to Xaxa for protection when Vobis Kan sends warriors against him [017-001], and he must petition Dar Tarus to restore his island [017-002]. The "master mind" cannot escape the world of Jeddaks, sentiment, and force that he claims to transcend.

### The Character of the Master Surgeon

Ras Thavas embodies a paradox central to the novel's exploration of scientific ethics. The narrator describes him as "never intentionally cruel" and "not, I am sure, intentionally wicked," yet he was "guilty of the most diabolical cruelties and the basest of crimes" [001-018]. In the next moment, he might perform a deed that "would have raised him to the highest pinnacle of man's esteem" [001-018]. This duality is explained by his defining characteristic: "He had a purely scientific mind entirely devoid of the cloying influences of sentiment, of which he possessed none" [001-018].

> He was never intentionally cruel; he was not, I am sure, intentionally wicked. He was guilty of the most diabolical cruelties and the basest of crimes; yet in the next moment he might perform a deed that if duplicated upon Earth would have raised him to the highest pinnacle of man's esteem. Though I know that I am safe in saying that he was never prompted to a cruel or criminal act by base motives, neither was he ever urged to a humanitarian one by high motives. He had a purely scientific mind entirely devoid of the cloying influences of sentiment, of which he possessed none. [001-018]

This absence of sentiment is the key to understanding everything Ras Thavas does. He is not motivated by cruelty, greed, or malice -- but neither is he motivated by compassion, justice, or love. His motives are purely scientific, which means they can produce results that appear benevolent or malevolent depending entirely on circumstance. The surgery that restores a warrior's crushed arm and the operation that transfers Xaxa's aged brain into Valla Dia's young body spring from the same source: a mind that evaluates procedures by their technical interest, not their moral weight [002-019].

His treatment of the narrator illustrates this perfectly. Vad Varo is neither guest nor prisoner but a scientific specimen -- "I offered a problem" [001-020]. Ras Thavas trains him not from generosity nor for practical need (his practice had not grown, as Vad Varo later discovers from the records [008-002]) but because the training itself serves some scientific purpose that Ras Thavas does not disclose.

### Surgical Genius and Its Methods

Ras Thavas's signature procedure is the transplantation of brains between bodies. The operation the narrator witnesses in the first chapter transfers the aged brain of Jeddara Xaxa into the young, beautiful body of Valla Dia. The technique involves removing the scalp "following the hair line entirely around her head," sawing through the skull with "a tiny circular saw," and deftly connecting "the severed nerves and ganglia" [001-056]. The procedure takes approximately four hours, after which both patients' heads are bound with "his peculiar adhesive tape, which was not only antiseptic and healing but anesthetic, locally, as well" [001-056].

The revivification process -- reheating withdrawn blood and adding "a few drops of some clear chemical solution" before returning it to the veins -- is taught to Vad Varo as one of his first duties [001-020]. This "embalming fluid" arrests decay "without altering in the minutest detail the nerve or tissue structure of the body" [001-020], enabling Ras Thavas to maintain a vast storage of preserved bodies in states of suspended animation, some for as long as fifty years [003-003].

Ras Thavas's surgical repertoire extends far beyond brain transplantation. Under his tutelage, Vad Varo progresses from transferring limbs and internal organs to performing complete operations on paying clients -- replacing failing kidneys, grafting new thyroid glands into stunted children, and finally, in a culminating achievement, transplanting an entire brain unassisted [008-001]. When Vad Varo completes this operation, Ras Thavas lays a hand on his shoulder and says, "I could not have done better myself" -- an "unusual demonstration of emotion" from a man who prides himself on possessing none [008-002].

> When I had done Ras Thavas laid a hand upon my shoulder. "I could not have done better myself," he said. He seemed much elated and I could not but wonder at this unusual demonstration of emotion upon his part, he who so prided himself upon his lack of emotionalism. [008-001]

## The Brain-Swap Experiments

One of the novel's most thematically rich episodes involves Ras Thavas's experimental transplantation of partial brains between a human and a Barsoomian white ape. The human subject, having received a portion of ape brain, drops to all fours, growls, and climbs to the top of the highest shelf [005-001]. Meanwhile, the ape, having received human brain tissue, stands erect and speaks in halting but intelligible words: "I was born and raised in the island of Ompt" [005-002].

> There is nothing remarkable about these subjects. They bear out what I had already determined years ago in the transplanting of entire brains; that the act of transplanting stimulates growth and activity of brain cells. You will note that in each subject the transplanted portions of the brains are more active -- they, in a considerable measure, control. That is why we have the human subject displaying distinctly simian characteristics, while the ape behaves in a more human manner. [005-005]

Ras Thavas dismisses these subjects as unremarkable: "They bear out what I had already determined years ago in the transplanting of entire brains; that the act of transplanting stimulates growth and activity of brain cells" [005-005]. He notes that the transplanted portions of brain are "more active -- they, in a considerable measure, control" [005-005]. The experiment confirms the plasticity of identity that lies at the heart of the novel's philosophical concerns: if a fraction of brain tissue can make a man behave like an ape and an ape speak like a man, then the boundaries between self and other, human and animal, are far more permeable than Barsoomian society -- or any society -- would like to believe.

## Knowledge and Secrecy

The master mind operates on a strict need-to-know basis. The narrator, who served Ras Thavas "for months," notes that "Questions which I asked he always answered, if he reasoned that the effects would not be harmful to his own interests, but he volunteered nothing that he did not particularly wish me to know" [007-106]. Vad Varo only learned of Ras Thavas's small fleet of fliers "the day before" a critical mission, despite months of service. This economy of information is a direct expression of Ras Thavas's character: knowledge is power, and power is never to be shared unnecessarily.

> Questions which I asked he always answered, if he reasoned that the effects would not be harmful to his own interests, but he volunteered nothing that he did not particularly wish me to know; and the fact that there were no windows in the outside walls of the building facing towards Toonol, that I had never before the previous day been upon the roof and that I never had seen a ship sail over the inner court towards the east all tended to explain my ignorance of the fleet and its customary operations. [007-106]

The Tower of Thavas reflects this same principle in architecture. Built some twenty-three thousand years before the events of the novel by an ancestor of Ras Thavas who was driven from Toonol by the reigning Jeddak, the tower has "no windows in the outside walls of the building facing towards Toonol" [007-106]. Its construction embodies the defensive secrecy that characterizes its current master. Ras Thavas's brain is described as "sealed like one of his own blood jars," and the narrator notes that he was never "for a moment found with his barriers down" [008-003] -- a Martian would ordinarily be able to read another's thoughts, but Ras Thavas's mental defenses are impenetrable.

> He kept his brain sealed like one of his own blood jars, nor was he ever for a moment found with his barriers down. [008-003]

## The Limits of Mastery

For all his intellectual supremacy, the master mind is ultimately subject to forces beyond his control. Vobis Kan, acting on the suspicion that Ras Thavas freed Gor Hajus to assassinate him, sends warriors who take the island and force Ras Thavas to flee [017-001]. The surgeon who defied the principle of hereditary rule by refusing to bow to any Jeddak must now petition Dar Tarus, the new Jeddak of Phundahl, for military assistance to recover his laboratory [017-002]. The master mind, whose whole philosophy is built on the supremacy of brain over sentiment, must appeal to the very political order he disdains.

This irony is deepened by the fact that Ras Thavas's own actions created the conditions for his predicament. His sale of Valla Dia's body to Xaxa set in motion Vad Varo's rebellion. His training of Vad Varo gave his assistant the skills needed to revive and mobilize allies against him. His preservation of Gor Hajus's body provided the assassin whose reputation and connections would prove essential to the revolution. The master mind, in his scientific detachment, failed to anticipate that his creations might acquire purposes of their own.

## Moral Implications of the Master Mind

The figure of Ras Thavas raises profound ethical questions about the relationship between intelligence and morality, questions that Burroughs leaves deliberately unresolved. The master mind's core philosophical tenet -- that self-interest is the sole motivator of intelligent action and that sentiment is "a bar to all progress" [002-002] -- is both the source of his achievements and the root of his moral failures.

> Sentiment is indeed a bar to all progress. We of Toonol are probably less subject to its vagaries than most other nations upon Barsoom, but yet most of my fellow countrymen are victims of it in varying degrees. It has its rewards and compensations, however. Without it we could preserve no stable form of government and the Phundahlians, or some other people, would overrun and conquer us; but enough of our lower classes have sentiment to a sufficient degree to give them loyalty to the Jeddak of Toonol and the upper classes are brainy enough to know that it is to their own best interests to keep him upon his throne. [002-002]

This passage reveals a contradiction in Ras Thavas's own reasoning: he dismisses sentiment as a bar to progress, yet concedes that it is exactly what preserves stable government -- the very civilizational foundation that makes his own work possible. His dismissal of sentiment is thus not purely scientific but selective; he benefits from the sentiment of others while denying it any role in his own decision-making.

The moral calculus of Ras Thavas's practice is particularly troubling in the treatment of living subjects. When Vad Varo learns that Valla Dia was alive when Ras Thavas acquired her, he demands to know whether Ras Thavas killed her. The surgeon's reply is chillingly clinical: "Killed her! No; I preserved her. That was some ten years ago. Why should I permit her to grow old and wrinkled? She would no longer have the same value then, would she?" [002-002]. In Ras Thavas's framework, a human being is reduced to market value -- a commodity whose preservation is an economic calculation, not a moral one. The body is an asset to be maintained at peak condition, and the sentient being within it is, as he says elsewhere, "merely a subject" [002-002].

The novel does not offer easy judgment. Ras Thavas's surgical genius genuinely heals the sick, restores the maimed, and extends life. The same hands that preserve a beautiful girl in suspended animation for a decade also transplant kidneys into a rich old man and give new thyroid glands to a stunted child. Burroughs presents a figure who is simultaneously healer and monster, savior and enslaver -- and refuses to resolve the tension between these roles.

## Legacy and Narrative Significance

The title "Master Mind of Mars" serves a structural function within the novel that extends beyond its application to Ras Thavas. The phrase recurs at the novel's conclusion, when Vad Varo recounts his adventures to [[John Carter]] on [[Jasoom]]. Carter, the Warlord of Mars, provides the frame narrative through which Burroughs presents the entire story -- a manuscript conveyed across space -- and this framing device positions Ras Thavas's achievements within the broader mythology of Barsoom.

The master mind's legacy within the narrative is ambiguous. By the novel's end, Ras Thavas has been displaced from his island and must seek the protection of the political order he once scorned. Yet his surgical techniques have been mastered by Vad Varo, who uses them to restore Valla Dia, install Dar Tarus as Jeddak, and set right the wrongs that Ras Thavas's commerce in bodies had created. The master's knowledge survives even as the master himself is diminished -- a pattern that suggests Burroughs's ultimate judgment may be that scientific knowledge transcends the moral character of its discoverer.

Ras Thavas's philosophical position -- that "all men of intelligence" recognize self-interest as the sole genuine motivator, and that words like "love, loyalty, friendship" are merely "the drooling drivel of sentiment" [002-002] -- is contested by the entire narrative arc of the novel. The revolution that topples Xaxa is driven by loyalty, friendship, and love. Vad Varo risks everything not from calculated self-interest but from devotion to Valla Dia. The master mind's philosophy is refuted not by argument but by plot: the very sentiment he dismisses proves more powerful than the intellect he reveres.

> "Sentimentalists have words: love, loyalty, friendship, enmity, jealousy, hate, a thousand others; a waste of words -- one word defines them all: self-interest. All men of intelligence realize this. They analyze an individual and by his predilections and his needs they classify him as friend or foe, leaving to the weak-minded idiots who like to be deceived the drooling drivel of sentiment." [002-002]

In the broader [[barsoom]] canon, the title "Master Mind" represents the most extreme articulation of a recurring Burroughs theme: the tension between scientific rationality and human sentiment. Ras Thavas stands as perhaps the most fully realized scientist-character in the Martian series -- more developed than the inventors and savants who appear in other novels -- precisely because Burroughs allows his philosophy to be stated, tested, and ultimately found wanting by the human relationships that drive the story.
