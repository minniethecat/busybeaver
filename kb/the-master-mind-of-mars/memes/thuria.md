## Overview

**Thuria** is the nearer and faster-moving of the two moons of Barsoom (Mars). Known to Earth astronomers as Phobos, Thuria orbits Barsoom in a rapid cycle of approximately four hours, making it a prominent and frequent presence in the Barsoomian sky. Its brief orbital period makes it a practical timekeeping reference for the inhabitants of Barsoom, and it figures significantly in the nocturnal activities of the planet's heroes.

## Orbital Characteristics

Thuria is distinguished by its swift passage across the Barsoomian heavens. Vad Varo, the Earthman narrator of *The Master Mind of Mars*, repeatedly notes Thuria's rapid orbit as a natural clock governing his covert movements. The moon completes a full circuit of Barsoom in less than four hours, meaning that a period of darkness between Thuria's setting and the sudden Barsoomian dawn is a narrow window—often the only window—for clandestine action [P07].

Thuria's partner moon, Cluros, follows a much slower and more deliberate path. Where Thuria "fled through the heavens" and "shot suddenly above the horizon," Cluros is described as taking "his slow deliberate way" [P10]. The contrast between the two moons is a recurring motif in the narrative: Thuria is energetic and feminine (often personified as "she"), while Cluros is patient and masculine.

## Role in the Narrative

Thuria appears at several critical junctures in Vad Varo's account. During his first escape from Ras Thavas's tower, he waits for Thuria to set before lowering himself from his third-floor window on a rope braided from his sleeping silks [P07]. The narrator explicitly times his actions by the moon: he knows that "in less than four hours Thuria would return" with the dawn, giving him a limited window to reach Valla Dia and persuade her of his plan [P07].

Later, during the nighttime approach to the Tower of Thavas with Dar Tarus, Gor Hajus, Xaxa, and Sag Or, the party again uses Thuria's cycle to their advantage. They stop their flier a mile or two south of the tower and "waited impatiently for Thuria to leave the heavens to darkness and the world to us" [P15]. Cluros had already set, and when Thuria "dropped plummetlike beneath the horizon," the total darkness enabled their silent approach [P15].

On the return to the tower after a jungle excursion, clouds obscure Thuria's light: "clouds having followed Thuria and blotted out the stars after the moon had set" [P15]. This accidental darkness proves useful, concealing the flier from any observer within fifty feet [P15].

## Barsoomian Cultural Context

Thuria, like many celestial bodies in the Barsoomian worldview, carries a sense of personality and agency. Vad Varo describes her as a "mistress" who "fled through the heavens" [P10], and her rays are described in almost sentient terms—"Thuria's brilliant rays" casting shadows and illuminating the landscape [P10]. This personification aligns with the broader Barsoomian tendency to imbue natural phenomena with volition and character.

## Significance

Thuria serves both a practical narrative function (as a timekeeping device that drives suspense) and a world-building function (as a detail that differentiates Barsoom from Earth). The moon's rapid orbit creates a distinctive rhythm of light and darkness that shapes the tactics of the story's protagonists. Along with Cluros, Jasoom (Earth), and the Red Planet itself, Thuria is part of the celestial framework that defines the Barsoomian sense of place in the cosmos.
