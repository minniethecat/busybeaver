## Overview

**Barsoomian** refers to an inhabitant of Barsoom (Mars) and, more broadly, to the culture, language, and customs of the Red Planet as depicted in Edgar Rice Burroughs's Martian chronicles. In *The Master Mind of Mars*, the term serves as both a demonym and an adjective describing everything from the Barsoomian language to the Barsoomian dawn. The protagonist Vad Varo, an Earthman, must navigate Barsoomian society as an outsider, offering the reader a comparative lens on Barsoomian customs.

## Physical Characteristics

Barsoomians are humanoid but visibly distinct from Earthmen. Vad Varo notes that Ras Thavas immediately recognized him as alien: "I was either, quite evidently, not a Barsoomian at all, or I was of a species of which he had no knowledge" [P04]. The physical differences between Earthmen and Barsoomians are sufficient that Vad Varo's appearance draws attention wherever he goes. Valla Dia, meeting him for the first time, remarks on the difference in his appearance: "That is why I differ in appearance from any you have known before" [P03].

Barsoomians have red skin—the "Red Planet" and "red men of Barsoom" are recurring phrases. Vad Varo's Earth-toned skin marks him as unmistakably foreign, making disguise difficult and drawing curiosity from the Barsoomians he encounters.

## Language

The Barsoomian language is Vad Varo's first major challenge upon arriving on the Red Planet. He receives "an intensive course of instruction in the Barsoomian language" from Ras Thavas, who works with him "long after dark" on the first day [P03]. Within three weeks, Vad Varo reports: "I had mastered enough of the Barsoomian tongue to enable me to converse with my host in a reasonably satisfactory manner, and I was also progressing slowly in the mastery of the written language of his nation" [P04].

The narrative notes an important linguistic distinction: the written language of Ras Thavas's nation differs from "the written language of all Barsoomians" [P04]. This implies that while there is a common written script shared across the planet, individual nations or city-states may have their own writing systems as well—a detail that hints at Barsoom's complex political geography without elaborating on it.

## Culture and Society

Barsoomian society is marked by technological sophistication alongside martial traditions. Vad Varo encounters "the wonderful, silent motor of Barsoom" [P15], advanced surgical techniques, and flying machines (fliers), yet the society also maintains warriors, guards, and a complex hierarchy of nobles and rulers.

Customs that appear peculiar to the Earthman narrator include the use of sleeping silks and furs instead of beds [P07], the "Barsoomian bath" appointed in Ras Thavas's guest quarters [P01], and the "sudden Barsoomian dawn" that follows Thuria's setting with startling speed [P07]. The Barsoomian naming convention also strikes Vad Varo as distinctive—Ras Thavas gives him the Barsoomian name "Vad Varo" because his Earth name was "meaningless and impractical" [P04].

Barsoomian cuisine is served on a Barsoomian table, and the architecture includes features such as inclined runways between floors rather than staircases [P01]. These small but consistent details build a picture of a civilization that is both advanced and alien.

## Nations and Peoples

Barsoom is not a unified political entity but a planet of competing city-states and nations. The narrative references several Barsoomian peoples: the Toonolians of Toonol, the Phundahlians of Phundahl, the Amhorians of Amhor, and the Heliumites of Helium, among others. Inter-nation conflict is common, and Vad Varo finds himself navigating the political tensions between these groups. As Valla Dia explains, her people "were not friendly with those of Helium" [P03], illustrating the fragmented nature of Barsoomian politics.

## Significance

The concept of "Barsoomian" as a cultural identity is central to the world-building of *The Master Mind of Mars*. By positioning his narrator as an outsider learning Barsoomian ways, Burroughs creates a natural vehicle for exposition—every Barsoomian custom that Vad Varo encounters is new to the reader as well. The Barsoomian world that emerges is one of advanced science, martial honor, complex politics, and a distinctive daily rhythm governed by the planet's two moons.
