## Overview

Jeddak is the highest political title on Barsoom, equivalent to an emperor or king who rules a sovereign nation with absolute authority. The position is hereditary, passed from father to son through the generations, and carries with it the unquestioning loyalty of the populace — a loyalty rooted in sentiment and tradition rather than rational consideration of the ruler's merits [002-001]. The office of Jeddak is central to the political structure of every Barsoomian nation-state, from Toonol to Phundahl to Duhor to Helium. The female equivalent of the title, Jeddara, is employed when a woman occupies the throne, as in the case of Xaxa of Phundahl [002-001].

> "We are a practical people, little influenced by sentiment; yet there is enough to keep the masses loyal to their Jeddak even after he has ceased to deserve their loyalty, while the fear of the wrath of the masses keeps the nobles loyal." [009-011]

The institution of Jeddak serves as a central thematic device in the novel, illustrating the conflict between hereditary rule grounded in sentiment and the rationalist, scientific worldview that defines Ras Thavas's character. Through the fates of multiple Jeddaks and Jeddaras — Xaxa's overthrow, Vobis Kan's tyranny, Kor San's tragedy, and Dar Tarus's elevation — Burroughs explores how political power is acquired, maintained, and transferred on Barsoom.

## Description

### Hereditary Structure and Absolute Authority

A Jeddak's power is near-absolute within their domain. The Jeddak commands the military forces, collects taxes, administers justice, and is the ultimate authority in all matters of state. Unlike the subordinate title of Jed (a lesser ruler or prince), a Jeddak rules an entire sovereign nation [002-001]. The distinction between Jeddak and Jed is structurally important: a Jed governs a city or region within a larger nation, while a Jeddak rules the entire nation. For instance, Mu Tel is a prince and potential heir to the throne of Toonol, but Vobis Kan is the reigning Jeddak [010-003].

> "Sentiment is indeed a bar to all progress. We of Toonol are probably less subject to its vagaries than most other nations upon Barsoom, but yet most of my fellow countrymen are victims of it in varying degrees. It has its rewards and compensations, however. Without it we could preserve no stable form of government and the Phundahlians, or some other people, would overrun and conquer us; but enough of our lower classes have sentiment to a sufficient degree to give them loyalty to the Jeddak of Toonol and the upper classes are brainy enough to know that it is to their own best interests to keep him upon his throne." [002-015]

The hereditary nature of the office is deeply ingrained in Barsoomian culture. Ras Thavas, himself a Toonolian, observes that the masses remain loyal to their Jeddak even after the ruler has ceased to deserve their loyalty, while fear of the masses keeps the nobles loyal [010-001]. This system perpetuates dynasties that can span millennia — as evidenced by the Jeddaks of Phundahl whose lineage traces back "into antiquity" [002-001]. The Jeddak of Duhor's line, represented by Kor San, demonstrates another facet of hereditary rule: the anxiety over succession when a Jeddak has no male heir, leading Kor San to determine that his daughter Valla Dia should wed one of his own Jeds so that a grandson with "the blood of Kor San in his veins" might inherit the throne [004-003].

### The Jeddara: Female Sovereignty

The title Jeddara designates a female sovereign. Xaxa, Jeddara of Phundahl, inherited the throne from her father and rules with the same absolute authority as any male Jeddak [002-001]. Ras Thavas's critique of Xaxa is scathing and gendered in its particulars: he describes her as "an ignorant, arrogant, selfish, stupid, cruel virago" who taxes her subjects until "they can scarce stagger beneath their burden," misrules them, exploits them, and betrays them — yet they fall down and worship at her feet [002-001]. The Phundahlians' loyalty to Xaxa, despite her manifest unfitness, is Ras Thavas's prime exhibit in his argument against sentiment-based governance.

> "The Phundahlians, upon the other hand, are egregious sentimentalists, filled with crass stupidities and superstitions, slaves to every variety of brain withering conceit. Why the very fact that they keep the old termagant, Xaxa, on the throne brands them with their stupid idiocy. She is an ignorant, arrogant, selfish, stupid, cruel virago, yet the Phundahlians would fight and die for her because her father was Jeddak of Phundahl. She taxes them until they can scarce stagger beneath their burden, she misrules them, exploits them, betrays them, and they fall down and worship at her feet. Why? Because her father was Jeddak of Phundahl and his father before him and so on back into antiquity; because they are ruled by sentiment rather than reason; because their wicked rulers play upon this sentiment." [002-016]

The irony is that Xaxa's own vanity — her desire to inhabit a younger, more beautiful body — ultimately leads to her downfall, as the body-swapping technology that was meant to serve her becomes the instrument of her replacement by Dar Tarus [016-002].

### Loyalty and Its Expressions

The loyalty commanded by a Jeddak extends to military oaths and ritual expressions. The phrase "By the sword of the Jeddak!" serves as an oath of the highest solemnity, as when Sag Or exclaims it upon recognizing Gor Hajus [013-001].

> "By the sword of the Jeddak!" he cried. "Fortune heaps her favors upon me. Who would have thought an hour since that it would be I who would collect the reward for the capture of Gor Hajus?" [008-043]

The Jeddak's Guard represents the elite military unit directly under the ruler's personal command [010-003]. The declaration "I, Dar Tarus, Jeddak of Phundahl, have spoken" carries the absolute finality of a sovereign decree, embodying the performative nature of Jeddak authority — the ruler's word is law simply because it has been spoken [014-003].

### The Jeddak's Enmity: Power and Fear

The relationship between a Jeddak and those who might threaten his power is dramatized through Vobis Kan's pursuit of Gor Hajus. The assassin Gor Hajus earned the enmity of Vobis Kan, Jeddak of Toonol, through his refusal to assassinate a man who had once befriended him. This act of loyalty to a friend was interpreted by Vobis Kan as implicit disloyalty to the throne, and the Jeddak conceived the suspicion that Gor Hajus had marked him for assassination [006-002]. The result was inevitable under such a system: Gor Hajus was arrested and condemned to death. Even after Gor Hajus's apparent death and six-year absence, Vobis Kan's fear of him remained so potent that upon learning of the assassin's escape, the Jeddak dispatched his best ships in pursuit [007-003].

> "name me some other, preferably Vobis Kan, Jeddak of Toonol. Find me a blade and I will slay a hundred to regain life." [007-007]

The reward for Gor Hajus's capture was substantial enough that a patrol officer exclaimed at his fortune upon recognizing the fugitive [009-011].

This episode reveals the Jeddak's fundamental insecurity: even absolute power is fragile when it rests on sentiment rather than genuine consent, and the hereditary ruler must constantly guard against threats both real and imagined.

## Notable Jeddaks and Jeddaras

### Xaxa, Jeddara of Phundahl

Xaxa inherited the throne from her father, whose line stretched back into antiquity. Her reign exemplified the worst excesses of hereditary rule: taxation that impoverished her subjects, misrule, exploitation, and betrayal [002-001]. Her obsession with physical beauty led her to commission Ras Thavas to transplant her brain into the body of Valla Dia, setting in motion the chain of events that would culminate in her overthrow [016-002]. She was ultimately deposed through the machinations of Vad Varo and his allies, who used the superstition of the Phundahlians — the very sentiment that kept her on the throne — to engineer her replacement by Dar Tarus [016-003].

### Vobis Kan, Jeddak of Toonol

The reigning Jeddak of Toonol, Vobis Kan is characterized as "a conscienceless tyrant" by his own nephew, Mu Tel [010-001].

> "The nobles have long been tired of Vobis Kan," he said, "and the people are tiring of him — he is a conscienceless tyrant — but he is our hereditary ruler, and so they hesitate to change." [009-011]

His persecution of Gor Hajus demonstrates the paranoia inherent in absolute power. His long-standing jealousy of Mu Tel's growing popularity reflects the dynastic tensions that hereditary systems generate, as the heir apparent becomes a threat to the sitting ruler long before any succession occurs [010-003].

### Kor San, Jeddak of Duhor

Kor San, father of Valla Dia, represents a more sympathetic portrayal of hereditary rule. Having no son, he determined to wed his daughter to one of his own Jeds so that a grandson carrying "the blood of Kor San in his veins" might inherit [004-003]. His city fell to the treacherous attack of Jal Had, Prince of Amhor, who had been refused Valla Dia's hand [004-003]. Kor San's plight — a father and ruler undone by the same hereditary system he sought to preserve — embodies the tragedy at the heart of Barsoomian politics.

### Dar Tarus, Jeddak of Phundahl

Dar Tarus's elevation from warrior of the Jeddak's Guard to Jeddak of Phundahl represents the novel's most dramatic political transformation. Proclaimed Jeddak through the voice of "Tur" (actually Vad Varo speaking through the great idol), Dar Tarus assumed the throne wearing the trappings of Hora San, a long-dead Jeddak and high priest [016-004].

> "Then it is my will," I boomed, "that Xaxa and Sag Or shall give back to those from whom they stole them, the beautiful bodies they now wear; that he from whom Sag Or took this body shall become Jeddak of Phundahl and High Priest of Tur; and that she from whom Xaxa stole her body shall be returned in pomp to her native country. I have spoken. Let any who would revolt against my word speak now or forever hold his peace." [014-012]

His reign combined the roles of Jeddak and High Priest of Tur, a unification of secular and religious authority unprecedented in a century [016-005].

> "Tell them that Xaxa is dead and that I, Dar Tarus, Jeddak of Phundahl, will receive the commander of Helium's fleet in peace upon the deck of this ship, or that I will receive him in war with all my guns. I, Dar Tarus, have spoken!" [014-042]

The nobles were content with this arrangement because it relieved them from the wrath of their god, and because Dar Tarus was of noble family — an accommodation that demonstrates how even revolutionary change on Barsoom must acknowledge the claims of hereditary status.

## Significance

The institution of Jeddak illustrates the tension between hereditary rule and practical governance that runs throughout the novel. Ras Thavas's critique of sentiment-based loyalty to hereditary Jeddaks represents the rationalist, scientific worldview that defines his character [002-001]. Yet even Ras Thavas, the arch-rationalist, must ultimately seek the aid of a Jeddak — first Xaxa, then Dar Tarus — to recover his island and continue his scientific work [017-001], suggesting that even the most rational Barsoomian cannot escape the political system in which they are embedded.

The overthrow of Xaxa and the installation of Dar Tarus as Jeddak of Phundahl demonstrates that even the most entrenched hereditary system can be disrupted through extraordinary events — in this case, the appearance of Tur speaking through the great idol [016-002]. The revolution was achieved without open warfare, through the manipulation of the very sentiment that sustains hereditary rule. The conclusion of the novel sees multiple Jeddak-related resolutions: Dar Tarus established as Jeddak through divine mandate, Valla Dia returned to her father Kor San in Duhor, and Vad Varo himself honored by the Jeddak of Duhor and wed to his daughter [017-002].

The Jeddak system, for all its flaws, provides the political framework within which all the novel's action occurs. From Ras Thavas's island laboratory to the streets of Toonol to the Temple of Tur in Phundahl, the shadow of the Jeddak falls over every character and every decision, a constant reminder that on Barsoom, power is inherited, sentiment is currency, and the throne is never truly secure.
