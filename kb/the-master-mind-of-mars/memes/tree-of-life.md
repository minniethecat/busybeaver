## Overview

The Tree of Life is the central element of the Barsoomian creation myth in Edgar Rice Burroughs' *The Master Mind of Mars*. According to this belief, all Barsoomians trace their origin to the Tree of Life, from which mankind originally came. The myth is nearly universally accepted across Barsoomian society, though interpretations of its meaning differ significantly between the religious and the rationalist segments of the population.

## In the Narrative

The Tree of Life belief is revealed through the character of Mu Tel, a Toonolian noble who explains the philosophical outlook of his people to the narrator. According to Mu Tel, "all educated Barsoomians" believe "that man came originally from the Tree of Life" [009-066]. This belief transcends national boundaries and social classes, serving as a shared cultural foundation across the diverse peoples of Barsoom.

However, interpretations of the Tree of Life diverge sharply along philosophical lines. The majority of Barsoomians — those who are religious — believe "that an omnipotent being created the Tree of Life" [009-066], giving the myth a theistic character. In contrast, the educated classes of Toonol reject this divine interpretation. While they accept the Tree of Life as humanity's origin, "they do not believe that an omnipotent being created the Tree of Life" [009-066]. For Toonolians, the Tree represents a naturalistic origin, not a divine creation.

## Significance

The Tree of Life functions as a cultural touchstone that reveals the philosophical divisions within Barsoomian society. The universal acceptance of the Tree as humanity's origin provides a common ground that unites Barsoomians across nations, while the disagreement over divine versus naturalistic causation exposes a fundamental rift between religious traditionalists and Toonolian rationalists.

This division is consistent with Toonolian philosophy more broadly. As Mu Tel explains, Toonolians "have no god and no religion" and "believe that no good deed was ever performed except for a selfish motive" [009-066]. Their rejection of a creator deity behind the Tree of Life is not an isolated skepticism but part of a comprehensive materialist worldview. The Tree of Life myth thus serves as both a unifying origin story for Barsoomian culture and a fault line that separates its religious and secular communities.
