## Overview

**Amhorian** refers to an inhabitant or native of Amhor, one of the city-states of Barsoom (Mars). The Amhorians appear in *The Master Mind of Mars* primarily through the actions of their ruler, Jal Had, whose military campaign against the city of Duhor forms a key episode in the backstory of Valla Dia, the princess of Duhor.

## Amhor and Its People

Amhor is depicted as a militaristic nation-state on Barsoom, capable of equipping "a great fleet" of war vessels for conquest [P06]. The Amhorian war-making capacity includes not only aerial warships but also ground forces sufficient to occupy a captured city and transport prisoners en masse. The political structure of Amhor appears to be monarchical—Jal Had is described as an Amhorian nobleman who commands the Amhorian military.

The Amhorian people are part of the broader Barsoomian civilization and share the general physical and cultural characteristics of the Red Planet's inhabitants, but they maintain their own distinct political identity and national interests, often in conflict with neighboring city-states.

## The Amhorian Invasion of Duhor

The most detailed account of Amhorian actions in the narrative comes from Valla Dia's account of the conquest of her home city, Duhor. According to her testimony, Jal Had, the Amhorian leader, sought to marry Valla Dia. When his suit was rejected, "this so incensed the Amhorian that he equipped a great fleet and set forth to conquer Duhor and take by force that which he could not win by honorable methods" [P06].

The timing of the invasion was calculated for maximum advantage: "Duhor was, at that time, at war with Helium and all her forces were far afield in the south, with the exception of a small army that had been left behind to guard the city. Jal Had, therefore, could not have selected a more propitious time to attack" [P06]. This opportunistic strategy reveals the Amhorian leadership as politically astute and willing to exploit the vulnerabilities of neighboring states.

## Occupation and Atrocities

The Amhorian occupation of Duhor was marked by harsh measures. When Jal Had could not locate Valla Dia within the palace—she had been hidden by loyal servants—"no amount of threat or torture could force a statement of her whereabouts from the loyal lips of her people" [P06]. In response, Jal Had "ordered that every woman within the palace be seized and taken to Amhor; there to be held as hostages until the princess of Duhor should be delivered to him in marriage" [P06].

The mass seizure of the women of Duhor's palace, including Valla Dia herself, demonstrates the Amhorian willingness to use collective punishment as a tool of political coercion. The captives were transported aboard "an Amhorian war ship which was sent back to Amhor ahead of the balance of the fleet, which remained to complete the sacking of Duhor" [P06]. This detail—that the fleet remained to sack the city even as prisoners were transported away—indicates the comprehensive nature of the Amhorian military operation.

## Political Significance

The Amhorians represent one of several antagonistic nation-states in the Barsoomian political landscape. Their aggression against Duhor is part of a larger pattern of inter-city conflict that characterizes Barsoomian politics. The Amhorian campaign also serves as a narrative catalyst: Valla Dia's capture by the Amhorians, her subsequent sale or transfer to Ras Thavas, and her eventual role as the subject of brain-transplantation experiments all trace back to Jal Had's invasion of Duhor.

Unlike the Toonolians or Phundahlians—whose cities Vad Varo visits or passes near—the Amhorians are encountered primarily through the lens of Valla Dia's testimony. They remain a somewhat distant but menacing presence, their actions shaping the fates of the protagonists without appearing directly in the foreground of the narrative.
