# Barsoomian God

**Barsoomian religious belief** varies by nation, ranging from the worship of Tur, the Father of Barsoom, in Phundahl to the atheistic rationalism of Toonol. Religious practice on Barsoom reflects the deep cultural divisions between its city-states.

## Tur — The Great God of Phundahl

The dominant deity worshiped in Phundahl is **Tur**, also called the "Great Tur" and "Father of Barsoom." His temple in Phundahl houses a towering idol before which the Jeddara Xaxa performs public ceremonies. Paxton, observing a ritual, describes the temple: "the interior of the temple was illuminated by a soft white light... More priests came and many young girls, priestesses. They performed before the idols, chanting meaningless gibberish."[012-021]

The Great Tur was believed to be a speaking god, though by the time of the novel's events, he had been silent for a hundred years. Xaxa's public prayer reveals the nature of Phundahlian worship: "O, Tur, Father of Barsoom, behold the offering we place before you, All-seeing, All-knowing, All-powerful One, and frown no more upon us in silence. For a hundred years you have not deigned to speak aloud to your faithful slaves; never since Hora San, the high priest, was taken away by you on that long-gone night of mystery have you unsealed your lips to your people."[012-033]

In reality, the "god" is a mechanical contrivance — an idol with movable eyes and a speaking-tube that allows a hidden operator to project a voice. Vad Varo and Gor Hajus exploit this mechanism to impersonate Tur and rescue Dar Tarus from sacrifice.

## The Temple of Tur

The Temple of Tur in Phundahl serves as the center of religious and political ceremony. It houses multiple idols, with the Great Tur occupying the central position of prominence. The temple ceremonies involve priests, priestesses, and elaborate rituals that reinforce the Jeddara's authority. Noble worshippers "forming in two lines along either side of the temple before the lesser idols, leaving a wide aisle from the great entrance to the foot of the Great Tur."[012-021]

## Toonolian Atheism

In contrast to Phundahl's theocratic practices, the educated classes of Toonol reject religion entirely. Mu Tel explains: "They believe that no good deed was ever performed except for a selfish motive; they have no god and no religion; they believe, as do all educated Barsoomians, that man came originally from the Tree of Life, but unlike most of their fellows they do not believe that an omnipotent being created the Tree of Life."[009-066]

Yet even the atheistic Toonolians are not free of irrational devotion. As Paxton observes: "They denied deity, and in the same breath worshiped the fetish of science that they had permitted to obsess them quite as harmfully as do religious fanatics accept the unreasoning rule of their imaginary gods; and so, with all their vaunted knowledge, they were unintelligent because unbalanced."[009-068]

## The Tree of Life

The Barsoomian creation myth centers on the **Tree of Life**, from which all Barsoomians believe mankind originated. This belief is shared across nations, though interpretations differ — the religious accept a divine creator behind the Tree, while the rationalist Toonolians attribute it to natural processes.

## Sacrificial Practices

Religious worship in Phundahl includes sacrificial offerings. The ceremony Paxton witnesses involves a human sacrifice — Dar Tarus chained to the altar — with the Jeddara herself poised to plunge a dagger into the victim's heart as "the offering we place before you."[012-033] The sacrificial rite serves a dual purpose: placating the god and demonstrating the Jeddara's absolute power over life and death.
