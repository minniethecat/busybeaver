## Pimalia

The **pimalia** is a flowering shrub native to Barsoom, prized for its ornamental value and cultivated in the prestigious gardens of Phundahl. It appears in the novel during Vad Varo's approach to the palace of the Jeddara Xaxa, where the narrator describes walking along a grand, palace-lined avenue and observing the cultivated flora of the city's wealthiest district.

Vad Varo recounts that along this boulevard "the sun shone down upon vivid scarlet lawns, gorgeous flowered **pimalia** and a score of other rarely beautiful Barsoomian shrubs and trees" [010-093]. The description situates the pimalia among exotic and highly valued botanical specimens — "rarely beautiful Barsoomian shrubs and trees" — indicating that it is not a common wild plant but rather a cultivated species reserved for the ornamentation of aristocratic and royal grounds. The emphasis on its "gorgeous flowered" quality confirms that the pimalia is grown specifically for its blossoms.

The pimalia is mentioned in the same passage as the *sorapus* tree, another prestigious Barsoomian botanical species that shades the same avenue [010-093]. The broader landscape described includes "vivid scarlet lawns," a characteristic feature of Barsoomian horticulture — the red-hued ground cover common in Martian gardens. This setting, along the "broad and beautiful avenue" leading directly to the palace gates of Phundahl, places the pimalia within the curated landscape of Barsoom's ruling elite.

In the larger context of Barsoomian botany, the pimalia represents one of several ornamental plants that appear in Burroughs's Martian tales. Like the *sorapus* tree, it serves as a marker of wealth and cultivation, distinguishing the palace districts from the harsher, untamed landscapes of the dying planet. Its single mention in the corpus, placed at a key moment of visual description as the protagonist first beholds the seat of Phundahlian power, suggests the pimalia contributes to the novel's deliberate contrast between the civilized opulence of Barsoom's cities and the desolation of its wilderness.
