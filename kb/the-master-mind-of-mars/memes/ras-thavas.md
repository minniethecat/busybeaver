## Overview

Ras Thavas is a millennia-old Red Martian surgeon based on an island near [[Toonol]], renowned across [[Barsoom]] as the greatest living master of brain transplantation. The novel's title derives from his unparalleled surgical genius. He operates a vast medical facility known colloquially as the [[House of the Dead]], where he preserves bodies in [[Suspended Animation]] and performs brain-transfer operations for enormous fees. His character embodies the tension between pure scientific rationality and moral emptiness — a man whose thousand-year pursuit of knowledge has stripped him of every human sentiment.

## Biography

Ras Thavas has lived for over a thousand years, devoting his entire existence to surgical research and experimentation [109-P111]. By the time [[Vad Varo]] (Ulysses Paxton) arrives on Barsoom, Thavas is physically decrepit — wrinkled, withered, nearly blind without his enormous multi-lensed spectacles, almost deaf, and suffering from heart failure — yet his mind remains the most formidable on the planet [047-P001].

> Ras Thavas himself was as remarkable as the things he accomplished. He was never intentionally cruel; he was not, I am sure, intentionally wicked. He was guilty of the most diabolical cruelties and the basest of crimes; yet in the next moment he might perform a deed that if duplicated upon Earth would have raised him to the highest pinnacle of man's esteem. [111-P112]

Thavas's life work revolves around the perfection of [[Brain Transplantation]], a procedure he has refined to the point of routine over centuries of practice [109-P111]. He maintains scores of preserved bodies in his facility, treating them as inventory to be bought and sold — each identified only by a catalog number. His most notorious transaction occurs when he sells the perfect body of the young princess [[Valla Dia]] to [[Xaxa]], the aging Jeddara of [[Phundahl]], transplanting Xaxa's brain into Valla Dia's beautiful form [081-P005].

> "It" was only another *case* to him. [267-P002]

Despite his amorality, Thavas is not wantonly cruel. He performs both beneficial and harmful operations with equal detachment. He gave a crushed warrior a new arm and a demented child a new brain, yet he also facilitated Xaxa's theft of Valla Dia's body for profit [261-P005]. His sole motivation is scientific inquiry; sentiment, ethics, and compassion are, to him, mere "waste of words" [125-P002].

Thavas takes on Ulysses Paxton — whom he renames Vad Varo — as his assistant after Paxton saves his life, training him in the techniques of blood replacement and brain transfer [113-P002]. He acknowledges Paxton's intelligence and makes him his personal bodyguard, believing self-interest alone will ensure loyalty [121-P002].

### The Brain Transfer of Ras Thavas

Near the novel's midpoint, Thavas's failing body compels him to enact his long-planned regeneration. He reveals to Vad Varo that he has lived over a thousand years and that his body is "about worn out" [431-P001]. He has selected a perfect young body from his vast collection and demands that Vad Varo perform the transfer of his brain into it — the operation for which he has been secretly training his assistant all along [501-P001].

When Vad Varo, following his mentor's own philosophy of self-interest, demands a higher price before completing the procedure, he bargains for the restoration of [[Valla Dia]]'s rightful body in exchange for Thavas's survival [505-P001]. The operation succeeds, and Thavas emerges transformed:

> Ras Thavas awakened from the anesthetic a new and gorgeous creature — a youth of such wondrous beauty that he seemed of heavenly rather than worldly origin; but in that beautiful head was the hard, cold, thousand-year-old brain of the master surgeon. As he opened his eyes he looked upon me coldly. [541-P001]

The transformation changes nothing of his essential character. Though his physical form is now the most beautiful on Barsoom, the ancient, calculating mind within remains wholly unchanged [541-P001].

### Final Appearance and Fate

After Vad Varo and his allies succeed in restoring Valla Dia's body and overthrowing Xaxa, Ras Thavas appears in [[Phundahl]] before the new Jeddak [[Dar Tarus]] [1709-P001]. His island has been seized by warriors of [[Toonol]] under the orders of [[Vobis Kan]], who accused Thavas of freeing [[Gor Hajus]] to assassinate him [1719-P001]. Thavas comes begging for military aid to reclaim his laboratory.

Vad Varo intervenes, demanding that Thavas devote his skill "to the amelioration of human suffering" and "no longer prostitute it to the foul purposes of greed and sin" as a condition of restoration [1723-P001]. Ras Thavas agrees, and [[Dar Tarus]] commissions [[Gor Hajus]] to lead an army against Toonol to reclaim the island [1723-P001]. This agreement marks the closest Thavas ever comes to moral reformation — a pragmatic concession, perhaps, rather than a genuine change of heart, but one that holds the promise of redirecting his genius toward beneficent ends.

## Surgical Genius

### Mastery of Brain Transplantation

Ras Thavas stands alone in the history of Barsoom as the inventor and sole master of brain transplantation. His method involves draining the subject's blood, replacing it with a preservative-anesthetic solution, surgically removing the skull cap along the hairline, and transferring the entire brain — reconnecting severed nerves and ganglia with a precision that baffles description [087-P001]. The blood is then rewarmed, treated with a few drops of a revitalizing solution, and returned to the veins, restoring the subject to normal function [113-P001].

> I learned also the secret of the few drops of solution which, added to the rewarmed blood before it is returned to the veins of the subject, revitalizes the latter and restores to normal and healthy activity each and every organ of the body. [113-P002]

Thavas's technique extends far beyond simple transfers. He has experimented with partial brain grafts, cross-species transplants between humans and apes, and even the transplantation of a woman's brain into a man's body and vice versa [197-P001]. He regards his experimental subjects — including those who were once living beings with names and histories — by catalog numbers alone, speaking of them with clinical detachment.

### The Embalming Fluid and Suspended Animation

Central to Thavas's enterprise is his invention of the "embalming fluid," a preservative that arrests decay without altering nerve or tissue structure in the minutest detail [113-P002]. This fluid allows him to maintain a vast inventory of bodies in suspended animation for indefinite periods — some for over fifty years [245-P001]. The technique of [[Suspended Animation]] is the foundation upon which his entire brain-transplantation enterprise rests, as it preserves donor bodies in pristine condition until a paying client requires them [077-P001].

### The Scale of His Institution

Thavas's establishment is a sprawling complex of buildings housing row upon row of bodies on *ersite* slabs, each with a case history suspended at its head [074-P001]. A staff of slaves and attendants — "hewers of wood and carriers of water" — maintains the physical labor while Thavas's brain alone directs every scientific operation [109-P111]. The institution includes vaults beneath the buildings where experimental subjects have lain forgotten for years, and a tower that rises above the complex, once part of an ancient city predating the founding of Toonol [751-P001]. [[Gor Hajus]], the famous Assassin of Toonol, calls him "the old butcher" — a name that speaks to his fearsome reputation among the common people [777-P001].

## Philosophy and Worldview

### Self-Interest as the Sole Human Motive

Ras Thavas has constructed an entire philosophical system around the proposition that self-interest is the only genuine human motivation. When Vad Varo questions his confidence in an assistant's loyalty, Thavas delivers his most famous monologue:

> "I am only sure that I know perfectly where your interests lie — sentimentalists have words: love, loyalty, friendship, enmity, jealousy, hate, a thousand others; a waste of words — one word defines them all: self-interest. All men of intelligence realize this. They analyze an individual and by his predilections and his needs they classify him as friend or foe, leaving to the weak-minded idiots who like to be deceived the drooling drivel of sentiment." [125-P002]

This worldview governs every relationship in Thavas's life. He selects Vad Varo as his assistant not out of affection but because Paxton, as a stranger on Barsoom, has no other social ties to compete with his loyalty [117-P001]. He trusts no one — he keeps his slaves unarmed because he fears assassination — yet he arms Vad Varo because he has calculated that Paxton's self-interest aligns perfectly with Thavas's survival [119-P001].

### The Rejection of Sentiment

Thavas's contempt for sentiment extends to every facet of human experience. He dismisses the rage of a subject whose body he sold to a paying client as the irrational reaction of a "sentimentalist of the most pronounced type" [129-P001]. He considers the subject's desire to reclaim his original body absurd, arguing that Thavas has bestowed life upon a dead man and is therefore owed gratitude rather than resentment [133-P001].

> "Sentiment is indeed a bar to all progress." [137-P001]

Yet Thavas is not entirely consistent. When Vad Varo catches him in a moment of intellectual pride, Thavas qualifies that pride is "merely a fact that I state" — a distinction that reveals the blind spot in his self-conception [157-P001]. His belief in his own mental superiority borders on the very sentiment he decries:

> "I am Ras Thavas," said the old man. "Why should I incline the head to any other? In my world nothing counts but brain and in that respect, and without egotism, I may say that I acknowledge no superior." [153-P001]

### Science Without Morality

Thavas's scientific mind is "entirely devoid of the cloying influences of sentiment, of which he possessed none" [111-P112]. He is as willing to perform a life-saving operation as a life-destroying one, provided the case offers scientific interest or financial reward. He would preserve a subject in suspended animation indefinitely "in the interests of science" without a moment's consideration for the subject's wishes:

> "Nonsense!" he cried. "You are better off here, where you can be preserved in the interests of science." [381-P001]

This moral vacancy is the engine of the novel's central conflict. It is Thavas's willingness to sell Valla Dia's body to Xaxa that sets the entire plot in motion, and it is the knowledge he imparts to Vad Varo that ultimately enables the wrong to be righted.

## Relationships

### Vad Varo

Ras Thavas forms his most significant relationship with [[Vad Varo]], the Earthman who saves his life and becomes his apprentice. Their bond is built on calculated mutual dependency: Thavas needs a skilled assistant for his own brain transfer, and Paxton has nowhere else to go [117-P001]. Over time, however, Paxton learns to use Thavas's own philosophy against him, bargaining with the old surgeon's life at the critical moment of the brain transfer to extract promises of justice [505-P001]. Thavas respects this — it is the kind of rational self-interest he understands and expects, even as he resents being outmaneuvered.

### Valla Dia

To Ras Thavas, [[Valla Dia]] is merely "a subject" — catalog number 4296-E-2631-H [161-P001]. Her beauty, her royal lineage, her suffering after awakening in Xaxa's hideous body — none of these register with him as having moral weight. He sells her body to Xaxa for money and later, when Vad Varo demands her restoration, casually offers to give her "any brain" transferred into another body instead [551-P001]. His inability to grasp why Vad Varo would insist on her *own* body reveals the depth of his emotional poverty.

### Xaxa

Thavas's relationship with [[Xaxa]], the Jeddara of [[Phundahl]], is purely transactional. He treats her with the same brusque indifference he shows all clients, refusing to accord her any deference on account of her royal status [153-P001]. When her attendants protest his casual treatment of their sovereign, he offers to return her money with disgust — a gesture that reveals his supreme confidence in the irreplaceable value of his services [079-P001].

### Clients and Subjects

Thavas's broader relationships are defined entirely by commerce. Wealthy clients pay enormous fees for brain transfers, often to acquire more beautiful or youthful bodies. His "material" — the bodies he uses — is acquired through agents who purchase corpses from bereaved families [129-P001]. He sees no ethical distinction between paying for a corpse and buying any other commodity. His subjects, once revived, are either returned to their purchasers or kept as slaves and experimental material. [[Gor Hajus]] calls him "the old butcher," and [[Valla Dia]], upon first learning of him, calls him "this horrid Ras Thavas" whose establishment is a "torture chamber" [259-P001].

### Subordinates

Thavas is served by numerous slaves and attendants, including his personal body-servant [[Yamdor]], a towering figure rumored to be the result of an experiment combining the brain of a woman with the body of a man [561-P001]. Yamdor's loyalty is purchased through lavish gifts and favors, consistent with Thavas's belief that all allegiance must be bought. The great institution is, as Vad Varo observes, "presided over by the sovereign mind of Ras Thavas" — a one-man empire of science in which every other person is a replaceable component [563-P001].

## Significance

Ras Thavas represents the ultimate product of a civilization that has divorced intellect from morality. His thousand-year lifespan and technological mastery have not produced wisdom, but rather a cold instrumentalism that treats all life as raw material. He is both the enabler of the novel's central crime (the theft of [[Valla Dia]]'s body) and the source of the knowledge that ultimately permits its rectification.

As a character, he illustrates Edgar Rice Burroughs's implicit critique of pure scientism untempered by human feeling. The novel's title — *The Master Mind of Mars* — is rich with irony: the greatest mind on the planet is also its most morally stunted. Thavas can transplant a brain but cannot understand a heart. His story raises enduring questions about the relationship between intelligence and wisdom, between scientific capability and ethical responsibility.

The transformation of his body in Chapter V provides the novel's most striking visual metaphor: outward beauty concealing an ancient and unaltered soul. However beautiful his new form, the mind within remains the same — a thousand-year-old intellect that has learned everything except how to feel. His ultimate agreement to devote his skills to "the amelioration of human suffering" represents the novel's cautious optimism: even the most hardened rationalist may be nudged, if not transformed, toward the good.
