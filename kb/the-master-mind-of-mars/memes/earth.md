## Earth

Earth — known on Barsoom as **Jasoom** — is the home planet of the novel's protagonist and narrator, Ulysses Paxton ([[Vad Varo]]). It serves as the constant point of contrast against which the wonders and terrors of [[Barsoom]] are measured. The narrator's Earthly origin shapes his perspective, his physical capabilities, and his interactions with every character he meets on the Red Planet.

## The Journey from Earth

The narrator's passage across interplanetary space is the foundational event of the novel. As a young American army captain in the trenches of World War I, Paxton was caught in a shell burst that severed both his legs (001-001). Lying in a shell hole in France, he fixed his eyes on the red planet above him and willed himself across the void:

> I stretched out my arms towards Mars, I did not seem to question or to doubt for an instant as I prayed to the god of my vocation to reach forth and succor me. I knew that he would do it, my faith was complete, and yet so great was the mental effort that I made to throw off the hideous bonds of my mutilated flesh that I felt a momentary qualm of nausea and then a sharp click as of the snapping of a steel wire, and suddenly I stood naked upon two good legs looking down upon the bloody, distorted thing that had been I. (P01-006)

The next instant, he was drawn through space:

> Suddenly I felt myself drawn with the speed of thought through the trackless wastes of interplanetary space. There was an instant of extreme cold and utter darkness, then — (P01-007)

He awakened upon Barsoom naked and disoriented, lying on a crimson sward within the walled enclosure of Ras Thavas's island laboratory (001-005). His first encounter with a Martian — "as strange a looking individual as my eyes ever had rested upon" — marks the beginning of his immersion in an alien world (001-016).

## The Narrator's Origin

The narrator is an American army captain from Earth who, after being mortally wounded in the trenches of World War I, is transported to Mars by the same mysterious force that carried John Carter across space (001-001). He arrives on Barsoom naked and disoriented, awakening upon a crimson sward within the walled enclosure of [[Ras Thavas]]'s island laboratory (001-005). His first encounter with a Martian — "as strange a looking individual as my eyes ever had rested upon" — marks the beginning of his immersion in an alien world (001-016).

Paxton's fascination with Mars predated his transport. While at an officers' training camp in 1917, he had read the novel *A Princess of Mars* and became obsessed with John Carter's adventures:

> The story made a profound impression upon me and while my better judgment assured me that it was but a highly imaginative piece of fiction, a suggestion of the verity of it pervaded my inner consciousness to such an extent that I found myself dreaming of Mars and John Carter, of Dejah Thoris, of Tars Tarkas and of Woola as if they had been entities of my own experience rather than the figments of your imagination. (P01-002)

This obsession became a nightly ritual aboard the transport ship to France:

> ...at night, on the deck of the transport, I would lie on my back gazing up into the red eye of the god of battle — my god — and wishing that, like John Carter, I might be drawn across the great void to the haven of my desire. (P01-003)

## Earthly Physiology on Barsoom

The narrator's Earthly muscles grant him extraordinary advantages in Mars's lesser gravity. Upon his first attempt to stand, he recounts: "I must have sailed ten feet into the air and back about twenty feet from where I had been sitting" (001-022). This superior agility proves decisive in his first confrontation — defending Ras Thavas from a homicidal attacker — and remains a critical asset throughout his adventures. However, he also discovers that Earthly muscles have drawbacks: "the greater agility of my Earthly muscles had its disadvantages as well as its advantages, for, indeed, I had to learn to walk at the very instant that I had to learn to fight" (001-030).

## Knowledge of Barsoom

Before his transport, the narrator had read the manuscripts of John Carter, which he knew as "marvelous and as yet unappreciated contributions to the scientific literature of a world" (001-022). These writings gave him foreknowledge of Martian physiology — including the fact that "all the Martian internal organs are not disposed identically with those of Earthmen" — though he admits to forgetting this at critical moments (001-034).

## Earthly Perspective

Throughout the novel, the narrator's Earthly origins provide a running commentary on the strangeness of Barsoomian society. His outsider's view allows Burroughs to explain Martian customs through the lens of an Earthman's understanding, filtering the alien through the familiar. When he first witnesses the horrors of Ras Thavas's laboratory, his Earthly mind struggles to comprehend:

> Naturally I had been thinking much upon the strange things I had witnessed since my advent upon Mars, but what puzzled me most lay in the seemingly inexplicable act of the old woman in paying my host what was evidently a considerable sum to murder her and transfer to the inside of her skull the brain of a corpse. Was it the outcome of some horrible religious fanaticism, or was there an explanation that my Earthly mind could not grasp? (001-062)

His scientific knowledge is humbled by Barsoomian advancement. Ras Thavas, after milking Paxton for all the scientific knowledge of Earth, concludes dismissively:

> "...the learned sciences on Earth have not advanced even to the swaddling-clothes stage as compared with the remarkable progress of corresponding activities on Mars." (002-020)

Yet the narrator also finds deep commonality. Recognizing in the crowds before Xaxa's palace the universal human trait of curiosity, he observes:

> ...after all human nature is much the same everywhere, whether skins be black or white, red or yellow or brown, upon Earth or upon Mars. (010-095)

His Earthly experience even proves useful in mundane ways. When coaching Gor Hajus in the art of public showmanship for their infiltration of Phundahl, he draws directly on his American past:

> I had to smile as I listened, here upon Mars, to the familiar lines that I had taught him out of my Earthly experience of county fairs and amusement parks... (010-099)

## Barsoomian Knowledge of Earth

Earth is not unknown to Barsoomians. Valla Dia, upon learning that Vad Varo is from Jasoom, is astonished: "How did you cross the great void?" (003-120). She associates his origin with John Carter, the only other Jasoomian she has heard of, and marvels: "Jasoom must be peopled with wonderful men" (003-124).

[[Mu Tel]], Prince of the House of Kan, displays a sophisticated observatory capable of viewing Earth's surface through "marvelous development of Barsoomian astronomical instruments, wireless photography and wireless telephony" (009-046). Mu Tel questions the narrator extensively "concerning the customs, the politics, the civilization and the geography of Earth, with much of which, I was surprised to note, he seemed quite familiar" (009-046). Some Barsoomian savants have even learned Earthly languages including Urdu, English, and Russian (009-046).

## Cultural Contrast

Throughout the novel, the narrator's Earthly perspective provides a running commentary on Barsoomian society. He compares the palace of Mu Tel to "private projection rooms on Earth" (009-048), notes that Barsoomian life belts resemble those "used aboard transoceanic liners upon Earth" (008-082), and recognizes in Phundahl's crowd the universal human trait of curiosity — "after all human nature is much the same everywhere, whether skins be black or white, red or yellow or brown, upon Earth or upon Mars" (010-095).
