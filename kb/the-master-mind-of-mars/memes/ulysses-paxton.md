## Overview

**Ulysses Paxton** is an American Army captain who, after being mortally wounded in the trenches of World War I France, is transported across space to Barsoom (Mars). There he takes the Barsoomian name **Vad Varo** and becomes the assistant to the legendary surgeon [[Ras Thavas]]. As narrator, his perspective as an Earthman among Martians provides the reader's lens into Barsoomian society, and his growing mastery of Barsoomian medical science drives the novel's central conflicts.

## Earth Background

Paxton describes himself as "late Captain, ——th Inf., U.S. Army" [001-001]. He first encountered Barsoom through the novels of Edgar Rice Burroughs while at an officers' training camp in the Fall of 1917. Though his "better judgment assured [him] that it was but a highly imaginative piece of fiction," he found himself dreaming of Mars — an obsession that persisted through his training and across the Atlantic to the trenches of France [001-001].

During an advance of two kilometers, Captain Paxton was holding an advanced position with a small detachment when a shell burst among them. He regained consciousness after dark to discover he had been "dead from the waist down" — his legs blown away between hip and knee. Rather than face life maimed, he "prayed to the god of [his] vocation" — Mars — to take him. In a moment of transcendent will, he felt "a sharp click as of the snapping of a steel wire, and suddenly I stood naked upon two good legs looking down upon the bloody, distorted thing that had been I" [001-001].

## Arrival on Barsoom

Transported through "the trackless wastes of interplanetary space" with "the speed of thought," Paxton experienced "an instant of extreme cold and utter darkness" before awakening on the crimson sward of Barsoom [001-003]. He found himself in a high-walled enclosure facing an old man (Ras Thavas) who tested his alien nature by commanding him in Barsoomian — a language Paxton did not yet understand [001-004].

The old man drew a sword, and Paxton's first experience with Martian gravity sent him sailing ten feet into the air. A naked, club-wielding warrior then attacked them both, and Paxton — learning to command his Earthly muscles under Martian conditions — fought and defeated the assailant with the old man's discarded sword [001-005].

## Life in the House of the Dead

Ras Thavas, recognizing Paxton's alien origin and quick mind, took him as an assistant. Within three weeks Paxton had "mastered enough of the Barsoomian tongue to enable me to converse with my host in a reasonably satisfactory manner" [004-001]. The surgeon gave him the Barsoomian name Vad Varo, dismissing his Earth name as "meaningless and impractical" [004-001].

Over the following months, Paxton learned the art of brain transplantation — Ras Thavas's signature medical innovation. He became competent enough that Ras Thavas trusted him to perform operations independently, and eventually the old surgeon entrusted him with the procedure that would transfer Ras Thavas's own brain into a younger, healthier body [002-044].

## Narrative Role

As first-person narrator, Paxton serves as both participant and observer. His Earthly perspective allows Burroughs to present Barsoomian customs, technologies, and social structures through the lens of a relatable outsider. His "Earthly muscles" give him advantages in Barsoom's lower gravity, but his unfamiliarity with Martian telepathy makes him uniquely suited to keep secrets — as Ras Thavas discovered, "he kept his brain sealed like one of his own blood jars" [005-034].
