## Overview

Tur is the supreme deity of Barsoomian religion, worshiped throughout the Red Planet but most elaborately venerated in Phundahl. The Temple of Tur in that city represents the pinnacle of Barsoomian religious architecture and ritual practice, featuring a complex system of ceremonial observances, a wealthy and influential priesthood, and massive public participation — all centered on a deity about whose nature even the most devout worshiper can articulate little [012-015].

## Description

The Temple of Tur in Phundahl is a building of extraordinary beauty and wealth, with constant streams of worshipers flowing in and out at all hours [012-012]. The interior displays a lavishness of ornamentation that astonishes the Earth-born observer: gold and jewels adorn every surface, and the organized system of worship resembles nothing so much as a commercial enterprise conducted on a grand scale [012-016].

The ritual structure is elaborate and varied. Upon entering, worshipers pay a gold coin to a white-robed priest, who makes passes above their heads, anoints their noses with water from a bowl, and murmurs unintelligible phrases [012-014]. Inside, different stations feature distinct rituals: at one, worshipers swing from overhead bars while chanting; at another, they crawl on hands and knees seven times around a pedestal; at yet another, they stand on their heads repeating "Tur is Tur; Tur is Tur; Tur is Tur" [012-020]. Each station requires a monetary offering.

What is most striking about Tur worship is the absence of theological content. When Vad Varo asks Dar Tarus what the chanted words mean, Dar Tarus confesses he does not know — and is shocked that anyone would ask [012-018]. When questioned about the apparently idolatrous statues in the temple, Dar Tarus insists they are "not idols" and that "there is but one god and he is Tur" — yet cannot explain what Tur is, what Tur wants, or how the rituals connect to any larger doctrine [012-022]. The refrain "Tur is Tur" encapsulates a faith that demands observance without understanding.

The priesthood wields considerable power and accumulates significant wealth through the temple offerings. The priests themselves are completely shrouded in white cloth masks and robes, lending mystery and anonymity to their function [012-013]. Their role appears to be purely ceremonial — facilitating the rituals and collecting offerings — rather than pastoral or doctrinal.

## Significance

Tur worship in the novel serves as Burroughs's pointed satire of organized religion. The elaborate rituals, the accumulation of wealth, the shrouded priesthood, and — most tellingly — the complete absence of theological substance all combine to portray a religious system hollowed out into pure performance. The faithful participate without comprehension, the priests profit without teaching, and the god remains an empty signifier: "Tur is Tur." This critique is delivered through the perspective of Ulysses Paxton, an outsider whose bafflement at the proceedings mirrors the reader's own.
