## Overview

The **banth** is the Barsoomian lion, a fierce predator that roams the wilds of Mars. While greatly feared for its savagery, the banth is also domesticated and used by Barsoomian royalty for ceremonial purposes — most prominently as the creature that draws the Jeddara's chariot during state processions to the temple of Tur [P14]. The banth occupies a unique position in Barsoomian culture as both a symbol of untamed ferocity and a marker of royal prestige.

## Description and Behavior

Banth are formidable predators of Barsoom, though the great white apes of the planet are said to be even more savage — creatures so fierce "that even that fierce Barsoomian lion, the banth, hesitates to cross their path" [P08]. This comparison establishes the banth as a benchmark of ferocity against which other dangerous Barsoomian creatures are measured.

The narrative does not provide a detailed physical description of the banth beyond its reputation for ferocity, but its role as a chariot-drawing beast implies considerable size and strength. The creature's temperament is sufficiently dangerous that even in captivity, attendants must use caution when handling them.

## Ceremonial Role

The most prominent appearance of banths in *The Master Mind of Mars* occurs during the temple ceremonies in Phundahl. Xaxa, the Jeddara of Phundahl, enters the temple of Tur in a "splendid chariot drawn by two banths" held in leash by slaves on either side [P14]. This grand entrance is part of the state religious ceremony that reinforces the Jeddara's authority and the theatrical pageantry of Phundahlian political life.

The same two banths appear during a subsequent temple ceremony, drawing the chariot carrying Valla Dia — whom the populace believes to be Xaxa, due to the brain-transplantation that had switched the two women's bodies [P14]. The banths, as creatures incapable of perceiving the deception, serve as unwitting participants in the plot to overthrow the Jeddara.

After the ceremony, the banths are led away by their attendants "to one side of the temple behind the lesser idols" [P14], treated as valuable ceremonial assets rather than mere beasts. The twenty slaves who handle the temple apparatus, including the banths' handlers, are described as "naked but for their scant leather harness" [P14], underscoring the hierarchical nature of Phundahlian society.

## The Temple Carpet Ritual

The banths' entrance is preceded by an elaborate ritual involving a great roll of carpet, unrolled by twenty slaves across the temple floor [P14]. This carpet serves as a ceremonial pathway for the banth-drawn chariot, emphasizing the formality and precision of the state religious proceedings. The combination of banths, chariot, carpet, and priestly attendants creates a spectacle designed to awe the Phundahlian populace and reinforce the Jeddara's divine mandate.

## In Barsoomian Language and Insult

The term "banth" also appears in Barsoomian vernacular as a colorful insult. When one character refers to Xaxa as "the old she-banth" [P13], the epithet conveys contempt for the Jeddara's ferocity, cruelty, and predatory nature. The insult draws on the banth's reputation as a savage beast, transferring those qualities to the target of the insult. This linguistic usage demonstrates how deeply the banth is embedded in Barsoomian culture — not merely as a physical creature but as a metaphor in everyday speech.

## Comparison with Other Barsoomian Fauna

The banth exists within a distinctive Barsoomian ecosystem that includes several notable creatures. The great white apes surpass the banth in savagery [P08]. The calot serves as the Barsoomian dog — domesticated and loyal, in contrast to the banth's wild ferocity. The thoat functions as a riding and draft animal, while the banth serves specifically as a ceremonial beast. Together, these creatures form a fauna that is both alien and familiar — recognizable analogues of Earth animals, transformed by the unique conditions of Barsoom.

## Significance

The banth serves multiple symbolic functions in the novel. As a creature of the wild, it represents the untamed and dangerous nature of Barsoom itself. As a domesticated beast drawing the royal chariot, it symbolizes the power of the Jeddara to command even nature's most fearsome creations. The use of banths in the temple ceremonies also reinforces the theatrical pageantry that characterizes Phundahlian religious and political life — a pageantry that Vad Varo exploits in his plot to overthrow Xaxa and restore Valla Dia to her rightful body.
