## Overview

**Woola** is a calot — a Barsoomian creature resembling a large, ten-legged dog — who serves as the loyal companion and guardian of John Carter, the War Lord of Barsoom. Woola appears in the broader Barsoom saga rather than in the main narrative of *The Master Mind of Mars*, where he is referenced only in the framing letter to Edgar Rice Burroughs as a familiar figure from the Martian chronicles.

## Origin in A Princess of Mars

Woola first appears in *A Princess of Mars* (1912), the inaugural novel of the Barsoom series. John Carter encounters Woola among the green Martians (Tharks), where the calot is kept as a guard beast and poorly treated. Carter's kindness to Woola — saving the creature from abuse — forges an immediate and lifelong bond. Woola becomes Carter's constant companion, following him across the dead sea bottoms of Barsoom and defending him against all threats, human and beast alike.

The calot's defining trait is his absolute, unconditional loyalty. Woola's ten legs give him tremendous speed, and his powerful jaw makes him a formidable combatant. Unlike the calots used as mere guard animals — such as those patrolling Mu Tel's garden in *The Master Mind of Mars* — Woola is a companion who acts out of devotion rather than trained instinct.

## Woola in The Master Mind of Mars

Woola does not appear as an active character in *The Master Mind of Mars*. His presence in the novel is limited to a single reference in the frame narrative. Ulysses Paxton, the narrator, describes how he first encountered the world of Barsoom through Burroughs's earlier novel while at an officers' training camp in 1917:

> "I found myself dreaming of Mars and John Carter, of Dejah Thoris, of Tars Tarkas and of Woola as if they had been entities of my own experience rather than the figments of your imagination." [P01]

This sentence places Woola in a quartet of names that define the Barsoom mythos for Paxton: John Carter, Dejah Thoris, Tars Tarkas, and Woola. The inclusion of Woola alongside these central human characters testifies to the calot's importance in the Barsoom canon — he is not merely Carter's pet but a character in his own right, as memorable as the human heroes.

## The Calot as Character

Woola occupies a distinctive position in the Barsoom series as an animal character with genuine emotional depth. Unlike the banth (a ceremonial prop) or the anonymous calots of the guard gardens (functional threats), Woola has a name, a personality, and an emotional arc. His devotion to Carter is reciprocated — Carter treats Woola not as a beast of burden but as a trusted ally.

This relationship between man and animal is a recurring motif in Burroughs's work, often contrasting favorably with the treachery and political intrigue of Barsoomian society. Woola's loyalty is absolute and uncalculating, a quality that the human characters of the series — with their shifting alliances and hidden agendas — can rarely match.

## The Metafictional Frame

Woola's appearance in Paxton's letter serves a metafictional purpose. The narrator cites Woola alongside Carter, Dejah Thoris, and Tars Tarkas as proof that Burroughs's earlier novel had achieved such vividness that its characters seemed real. When Paxton later finds himself transported to Barsoom, the experience confirms his suspicion that Burroughs's "fiction" was in fact reportage.

Woola thus functions as part of the novel's reality-establishing mechanism. The creature's mention in the frame narrative signals to readers familiar with the Barsoom series that this new tale takes place in the same universe. For readers unfamiliar with the earlier novels, the name-check invites curiosity about Woola and the world he inhabits.

## Comparison with Other Calots

The calots that appear directly in *The Master Mind of Mars* — the guard beasts of Mu Tel's garden [P11] and the concept of a calot's body as the ultimate humiliation [P06] — present a narrower view of the species. These calots are dangerous predators, useful for security but devoid of the emotional richness that Woola embodies. The contrast between Woola (known by name, loved by his master, remembered by readers) and the anonymous garden calots (feared, utilitarian, interchangeable) highlights the difference between a character and a creature — between an individual and a type.

## Significance

Though Woola does not appear directly in the action of *The Master Mind of Mars*, his mention in the frame narrative serves to anchor the novel within the broader Barsoom literary universe. The reference works on multiple levels: for characters within the story, Woola is a living creature of Barsoom; for the metafictional frame, he is a literary figure whose reality the narrator has come to accept as genuine; and for the reader, he is a familiar touchstone that connects this new adventure to the beloved earlier chronicles. Woola thus embodies the permeable boundary between fiction and reality that defines the novel's narrative structure.
