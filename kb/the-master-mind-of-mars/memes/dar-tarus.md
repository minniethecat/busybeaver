## Overview

Dar Tarus is a warrior of [[Phundahl]] who served in the Jeddara's Guard before falling victim to one of the most personal crimes in the narrative: the treacherous noble Sag Or coveted Dar Tarus's magnificent body and, through the surgical services of [[Ras Thavas]], had his own aged brain transplanted into the young warrior's form [011-003]. For six years Dar Tarus lay preserved in the House of the Dead as Case 378-J-493811-P, until [[Vad Varo]] revived him. His journey from preserved corpse to Jeddak of Phundahl is one of the most complete character arcs in the novel, culminating in his reunion with his lost love Kara Vasa and his coronation as ruler.

## Biography

### Theft of Body

Dar Tarus was a young warrior of exceptional physical beauty serving in the Guard of [[Xaxa]], the Jeddara of Phundahl. Sag Or, a powerful noble, coveted Dar Tarus's body and conspired with Xaxa — who herself desired the body of the princess [[Valla Dia]] — to arrange the transplants through Ras Thavas [011-003]. Dar Tarus's brain was removed to an aged, decrepit form, while Sag Or took possession of the warrior's magnificent physique.

### Resurrection

Vad Varo, discovering the preserved body labeled 378-J-493811-P, repaired the lung damaged in the duel that had felled Dar Tarus and revived him alongside [[Gor Hajus]] [007-001]. On awakening, Dar Tarus's first reaction was venomous hatred toward the man who had run him through — until Vad Varo explained the larger plan.

> "Strike off my bonds!" Dar Tarus cried. "I will follow you to the ends of Barsoom and the way leads thus far to the fulfillment of your design." [007-006]

Gor Hajus himself vouched for Dar Tarus's warrior credentials:

> You could not have chosen one better fitted for your mission than Dar Tarus, one time soldier of the Jeddara's Guard, whom she had slain that in my former body one of her rotten nobles might woo the girl I loved. [007-008]

Dar Tarus's knowledge of Phundahl proved essential. As a native of the city and former member of the Jeddara's Guard, he led the party through devious back routes to safe lodging, avoiding the main thoroughfares where they might be recognized [010-003]. When their ruse was discovered and Vad Varo and Gor Hajus were captured, Dar Tarus coordinated from the outside, and it was his arrest — dragged before the Great Tur as a sacrifice — that triggered the dramatic temple deception [012-008].

During the aerial pursuit that followed their escape, Dar Tarus demonstrated his skill at the controls:

> Dar Tarus, at the controls, turned the nose of our flier upward directly towards the patrol boat and at the same time shouted to us to concentrate our fire upon her propellers. [008-004]

### Restoration and Coronation

After the party returned to Ras Thavas's laboratory, Vad Varo restored Dar Tarus to his original body. The restoration was a moment of profound personal significance:

> Dar Tarus stood looking at Sag Or—looking at his own body like some disembodied soul—as weird a situation as I have ever encountered. [013-008]

In the climactic scene at the temple, Vad Varo — speaking through the idol of Tur — proclaimed:

> "Prepare to greet the new Jeddak, Dar Tarus of Phundahl!" [014-008]

The doors swung open to reveal Dar Tarus "resplendent in the trappings of Hora San, Jeddak of Phundahl" — a moment of supreme vindication. His first acts as Jeddak showed the character of the man: he pardoned Sag Or, permitting him to "go your way and live in peace" [014-009], and when [[John Carter]] of Helium arrived, Dar Tarus received him with the measured dignity befitting a true ruler.

## Relationships

Dar Tarus's devotion to his betrothed, **Kara Vasa**, survived six years of separation. His reunion with her — brought to Phundahl by John Carter himself — is one of the novel's most satisfying resolutions [014-014]. His warrior's bond with Gor Hajus bridges the historic rivalry between Phundahl and [[Toonol]]. With Vad Varo he shares the unique intimacy of men who have been resurrected together and fought side by side through impossible odds.

## Significance

Dar Tarus embodies the theme of bodily identity that runs throughout the novel. More than any other character, he illustrates the horror of having one's physical form stolen and the profound yearning to be restored to oneself. His elevation from preserved corpse to Jeddak represents the narrative's deepest commitment to poetic justice.

> Little wonder, then, that Sag Or had coveted the body of this young warrior; for his own, which Dar Tarus now possessed, was marked by dissipation and age; nor that Dar Tarus yearned to come again into his own. [013-009]

Unlike the morally complex Ras Thavas or the honor-bound Gor Hajus, Dar Tarus is a straightforward hero whose physical beauty, martial courage, and essential decency make him the natural choice to lead Phundahl into a new era.
