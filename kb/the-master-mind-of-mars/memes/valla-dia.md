## Overview

Valla Dia is the daughter of Kor San, Jeddak of Duhor, and the central female figure of the novel. Before the story begins, she was betrayed into Ras Thavas's hands and her perfect body was sold to Xaxa, the aging Jeddara of Phundahl. Her brain was transplanted into Xaxa's old, disfigured body, where it remained in suspended animation for ten years until Ulysses Paxton revived her. Her story arc embodies the novel's exploration of identity, beauty, and the relationship between body and soul.

## Biography

Valla Dia was the only daughter of Kor San, Jeddak of Duhor, who intended to wed her to one of his own Jeds so that a grandson carrying the blood of Kor San might rule [005-005]. Her beauty was legendary throughout Barsoom, attracting the unwanted attention of Jal Had, Prince of Amhor. When Kor San rejected Jal Had's marriage proposal, the Amhorian prince attacked Duhor while its army was away at war. Rather than be captured, Valla Dia fled — eventually falling into the hands of Ras Thavas's agents [005-006].

At the House of the Dead, her body was catalogued as specimen 4296-E-2631-H and preserved for a decade [005-005]. When the aged, disfigured Xaxa came seeking a new body, she selected Valla Dia's form, paying Ras Thavas an enormous sum for the transplant [001-005]. Valla Dia's brain was transferred into Xaxa's withered husk and left in suspended animation.

Ulysses Paxton, moved by the tragedy of the beautiful girl whose body had been stolen, revived Valla Dia after ten years of deathlike sleep [005-005]. Awakening in Xaxa's hideous form, she initially despaired but ultimately accepted her circumstances with remarkable grace and resilience. In the weeks following her resurrection, she and Paxton developed a deep bond, becoming each other's sole confidants [005-006].

When Paxton formulated a plan to restore her body, Valla Dia pleaded with him not to risk his life, valuing his friendship above her physical restoration [005-006]. She expressed preference for remaining in Xaxa's "tough and healthy envelope" rather than risking another stolen body that might later be reclaimed by a paying client [006-001].

## Relationships

Valla Dia's primary relationship is with Ulysses Paxton (Vad Varo), who revives her and becomes her devoted friend and protector. Their bond deepens into love, though Paxton struggles to express it while she wears Xaxa's disfigured face [005-006]. She is the victim of both Xaxa, who stole her body, and Ras Thavas, who facilitated the crime. Her father Kor San remains unaware of her fate.

## Significance

Valla Dia embodies the novel's central philosophical question: whether identity resides in the mind or the body. Trapped in the ugliest of forms, she retains her nobility, grace, and capacity for love — demonstrating that true beauty is internal. Her willingness to remain disfigured rather than have Paxton risk his life for her restoration reveals a selflessness that contrasts sharply with the novel's villains, who value appearance above all else.
