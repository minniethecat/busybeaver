## Overview

The Red Planet — known to its inhabitants as [[Barsoom]] — is the world of Mars, a dying planet of ochre deserts, ancient civilizations, and diminished gravity. The novel uses this designation primarily from the Earth-born perspective of Ulysses Paxton (Vad Varo), for whom Mars represents an object of lifelong fascination before it becomes his physical reality [P01-001].

## Description

From Earth, the Red Planet appears as a "red eye" in the night sky, an object of mystery that has "presented to the Earthman for ages" a "seemingly unfathomable riddle" [P01-001]. For the protagonist, the Red Planet took on the character of destiny — "the red eye of the god of battle — my god" — a celestial body he wished would draw him across the void as it had drawn John Carter before him [P01-002].

Upon arrival, the effects of the Red Planet's environment are immediately tangible. The first physical sensation is the lesser gravity, which causes the protagonist to "sail ten feet into the air" when attempting to stand, the distinctive color of the sward, and the copper-red skin of the native inhabitants — all features described in John Carter's manuscripts that confirm the protagonist has indeed reached Mars [P01-003]. The landscape, when finally seen beyond the walls of Ras Thavas's laboratory, inspires "an urge for freedom, for space, for room to move about" — the vast open country of the Red Planet offering liberation after nearly a year of confinement [006-001].

The planet's denizens include the "great white ape of Barsoom," described as "one of the most savage and fearsome" creatures of the Red Planet, whose ferocity necessitates the precaution of secure binding before surgical resurgence [003-001]. This characterization underscores the planet's dangerous and untamed nature.

## Significance

The Red Planet designation serves as the novel's linkage between Earth and Mars, bridging the terrestrial reader's perspective with the Barsoomian world. The repeated use of "Red Planet" in the narrative, particularly in the early chapters and during moments of landscape revelation, reinforces the protagonist's dual identity — an Earthman navigating an alien world. The planet itself functions as both backdrop and character, its physical properties (lesser gravity, red landscapes) directly enabling the story's events while its cultural structures (the Jeddak system, the assassin culture) provide the conflict.
