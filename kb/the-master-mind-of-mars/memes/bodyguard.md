## Overview

On Barsoom, the bodyguard occupies a position defined by pragmatic self-interest rather than sentimental loyalty. Unlike Earthly conceptions of the protector motivated by duty or devotion, the Barsoomian bodyguard relationship is built on a calculus of mutual benefit — the bodyguard's own survival and prosperity are inextricably tied to the safety of the person they protect [002-001]. This role is exemplified in the arrangement between Ras Thavas, the master surgeon of Toonol, and his Earth-born assistant Vad Varo.

## Description

Ras Thavas appointed Vad Varo as his personal bodyguard after concluding that the Earthman's unique circumstances made him the ideal protector. Unlike the red-Martian assistants who surrounded the surgeon, Vad Varo had no friends, no country, and no alternative prospects on Barsoom — his survival depended entirely on Ras Thavas [002-001]. This vulnerability, paradoxically, became his qualification. As Ras Thavas explained, Vad Varo "cannot harm me without harming yourself and no man will intentionally do that" [004-002].

The bodyguard role came with significant privileges. Ras Thavas alone among the laboratory staff went armed — an unusual practice on Barsoom where most citizens habitually carry weapons — because he could not trust his assistants not to turn their arms against him. By arming Vad Varo as bodyguard, Ras Thavas broke this isolation, provisioning him with a long-sword, short-sword, pistol, and dagger [002-002]. The act was not one of trust in any sentimental sense but a calculated decision: Vad Varo's self-interest aligned perfectly with protecting his patron.

The rationale behind the bodyguard selection reflects the broader Barsoomian philosophy articulated by Ras Thavas: "love, loyalty, friendship, enmity, jealousy, hate — one word defines them all: self-interest" [002-003]. A bodyguard chosen on this basis was, in Ras Thavas's analytical framework, more reliable than one motivated by emotions that might shift with circumstance.

## Significance

The bodyguard concept in the novel illustrates the transactional nature of Barsoomian social relations, where even the most intimate protective role is reduced to a cold calculus of interests. The arrangement between Ras Thavas and Vad Varo subverts the traditional protector archetype: instead of sworn loyalty, there is mutual dependence; instead of honor, there is rational self-preservation. This mirrors the broader theme of the novel in which identity, loyalty, and personhood are subject to the dispassionate logic of the surgical laboratory.
