## Overview

A panthan is a Barsoomian mercenary, a soldier without a country who fights for pay rather than patriotic duty. These "men without a country" form an informal class of professional warriors who wander the Red Planet, offering their services to any Jeddak or Jeddara willing to meet their price. Despite lacking formal organization, panthans are governed by an unwritten but severe code of ethics that demands absolute loyalty to their current employer [009-001].

## Description

The panthan lifestyle is one of rootless adventure punctuated by intense loyalty to whoever holds their contract. Most panthans are men who have "flown from the wrath of their own Jeddaks or the justice of their own courts," making them effectively political exiles [009-001]. A smaller contingent consists of "adventurous souls who have adopted their calling because of the thrills and excitement it offers" — those who choose the life rather than being forced into it [009-001].

Economically, panthans occupy a paradoxical position. While well paid when employed, they are "great gamblers and notorious spenders," perpetually without funds between engagements. This financial precarity forces them to resort to "strange expedients for the gaining of their livelihood" — a circumstance that Gor Hajus and Dar Tarus exploit when presenting themselves as panthans with a trained white ape, a plausible cover story for their covert operations in Phundahl [009-001].

In public performance, panthans leverage their outsider status to advantage. Gor Hajus, addressing the crowd in Phundahl, introduces himself and his companions as "two poor panthans" who have captured and trained a great white ape "for your entertainment and edification" — a narrative that would seem unremarkable precisely because panthans were known to engage in unusual money-making schemes [010-001]. The claim that their ape "has entertained the crowned heads of Barsoom and mystified the minds of her most learned savants" plays on the panthan's reputation for far-flung adventures and audacious claims [010-001].

The plain harness worn by panthans — "free of all insignia either of rank or service" — visually marks their lack of fixed allegiance. Unlike warriors in national service who display the colors and ranks of their Jeddak, the panthan's unadorned gear signals availability to any bidder [009-001].

## Significance

The panthan represents a unique Barsoomian social institution: a warrior class that exists outside the rigid hierarchy of Jeddak-ruled states yet serves as an essential instrument of those same powers. The panthan's code of ethics — "while in the employ of a master are, almost without exception, loyal to him" — establishes that even Barsoom's outcasts operate within predictable moral constraints. For the novel's protagonists, assuming panthan identity provides the perfect disguise: it explains their lack of fixed residence, their possession of a trained ape, and their presence in foreign cities, all without arousing the suspicion that would attend men of known national allegiance.
