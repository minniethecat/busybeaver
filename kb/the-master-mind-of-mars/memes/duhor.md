## Overview

Duhor is a Barsoomian city-state located on the far side of the snow-clad Artolian Hills, a full "seven thousand, eight hundred *haads* from Toonol" [003-003]. It is the home of Princess [[Valla Dia]], whose restoration to her rightful throne provides the narrative's emotional and political endpoint. Though the action never actually reaches Duhor within the novel, it functions as the promised destination — the safe harbor toward which the protagonists journey throughout their adventures.

## Description

Duhor is first mentioned by Valla Dia herself, who describes it to [[Vad Varo]] when he impulsively offers to escort her home. She calls him "mad" for the suggestion, emphasizing the impossible distance and dangers: "between lie the Toonolian Marshes, wild hordes, savage beasts and warlike cities. You would but die uselessly within the first dozen *haads*" [003-003]. This description establishes Duhor as a place of safety and civilization separated from [[Toonol]] by a vast and hostile wilderness.

## Events

Though Duhor is never directly depicted, its gravitational pull shapes the entire narrative. Valla Dia's desire to return home motivates Vad Varo's initial escape plan. After the liberation of [[Phundahl]], a small fleet is prepared to transport Valla Dia to Duhor [014-011]. The journey is interrupted by the appearance of [[John Carter]]'s fleet from Helium, and it is John Carter himself who ultimately escorts Valla Dia and Vad Varo to Duhor after attending the nuptials of [[Dar Tarus]] and Kara Vasa [014-015].

In Duhor, Vad Varo discovers a great surprise: he learns that he is in fact a prince of the house of Duhor — a revelation that transforms his status from displaced Earthman to Barsoomian royalty and makes his union with Valla Dia politically appropriate.

## Significance

Duhor operates as a narrative MacGuffin — a desired destination that drives the action without ever being directly represented. Its structural role is to provide Valla Dia with a legitimate political identity beyond that of victim, and to create the geographic scope that makes the protagonists' journey feel genuinely epic. The revelation of Vad Varo's Duhorian heritage in the closing pages provides the final twist that resolves the romance plot on terms acceptable to Barsoomian aristocratic convention.
