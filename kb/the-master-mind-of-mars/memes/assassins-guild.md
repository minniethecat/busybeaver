## Overview

On Barsoom, assassination is not a crime but a recognized profession with its own code of honor and social standing. The Assassins' Guild operates openly within Barsoomian society, particularly in Toonol, where killers-for-hire are respected practitioners of a grim but honorable calling [001-030]. Far from being outcasts, accomplished assassins like Gor Hajus achieve widespread fame for their skill with the blade and their adherence to a personal code of conduct.

## Description

The cultural acceptance of assassination on Barsoom represents one of the most striking contrasts with Earth society. As Ras Thavas explains to Ulysses Paxton, assassination is "the order of society and natural death one of the rarest of phenomena" [001-029]. The practice is institutionalized to such a degree that bodies of assassination victims form the primary source of material for Ras Thavas's surgical experiments — agents of the master surgeon routinely pay relatives for the corpses of those slain [002-010].

The most celebrated practitioner of the profession in the novel is Gor Hajus, an assassin of Toonol whose reputation extends far beyond his homeland. His fame rests not merely on his lethality with a sword but on a rigorous ethical framework: he never struck down a woman or a good man, and he never struck from behind [007-035]. His killings were always the results of fair fights in which the victim had every opportunity to defend himself and slay his attacker [007-035]. This code elevated him above common killers and earned him genuine respect.

Gor Hajus's downfall came precisely because of his principles. When Vobis Kan, Jeddak of Toonol, ordered him to assassinate a man who had once shown Gor Hajus a kindness, the assassin refused — an act of loyalty that marked him for death [007-036]. Vobis Kan, suspecting that the assassin now had him marked for retribution, had Gor Hajus arrested and condemned to execution, after which his body was purchased by an agent of Ras Thavas [007-036].

The Guild's relationship with political power is complex. While assassins serve the powerful, their personal loyalties can override professional obligations, making them unpredictable instruments. Gor Hajus's offer to Vad Varo upon being revived — "Find me a blade and I will slay a hundred to regain life" — underscores both the professionalism and the transactional nature of the assassin's trade [007-038].

## Significance

The Assassins' Guild serves multiple narrative functions. It establishes the alien moral landscape of Barsoom, where killing is a regulated profession rather than a crime. It provides the mechanism by which Ras Thavas obtains experimental subjects, making the entire brain-transplantation plot possible. And through Gor Hajus, it introduces a character whose code of honor — however violent — proves essential to the protagonists' escape and eventual success.
