## Overview

The **calot** is a Barsoomian animal resembling a large, fierce dog. It is employed as a guard beast, particularly in the gardens and grounds of noble estates, where its speed and ferocity make it an effective deterrent against intruders. The calot is dangerous enough that even an armed warrior considers it a serious threat.

## Physical Characteristics and Behavior

The narrative of *The Master Mind of Mars* provides limited physical description of the calot beyond its dog-like nature, but its behavior is vividly depicted. Calots are fast enough to nearly overtake a running man across open ground. They are deployed as nocturnal sentinels, patrolling the gardens of Mu Tel's palace and responding aggressively to intruders.

The calot's hunting behavior appears instinctive and predatory. When it detects Vad Varo in the garden, it does not bark or raise an alarm — it pursues silently and with lethal intent. This suggests the calot is less a watchdog in the Earth sense (alerting handlers) and more an autonomous predator trained to eliminate threats.

## The Garden Encounter

The calot's most significant appearance in the narrative occurs during Vad Varo's clandestine visit to Mu Tel's palace in Toonol. Having navigated the city and its defenses, Vad Varo enters the palace garden only to find himself pursued by one of "the calots of the garden" [P11]. He describes himself as experiencing "all of the reactions of the cornered rat" as the beast closed in — a rare admission of fear from the battle-hardened Earthman [P11].

The pursuit is resolved at the last possible instant. As Vad Varo presses himself against a wall in the garden, "a hand reached out of the darkness behind me and seized my arm" [P11]. Mu Tel's servant pulls him through a hidden door, and the calot's silhouette is blotted out as the door closes. The servant remarks: "Prince, I was scarce a tal too soon. In fact, as I opened the door to step out into the garden in search of him, as you directed, there he was upon the opposite side with one of the calots of the garden almost upon him" [P11].

The servant's use of "a tal" — a Barsoomian unit of measurement approximating a foot — to describe the margin of rescue emphasizes just how close the calot came to its prey. The entire episode occurs in seconds, underscoring both the creature's speed and the constant danger that permeates Barsoomian life.

## Calot as Symbol of Degradation

Beyond its physical threat, the calot appears in the narrative as a symbol of the ultimate bodily degradation. Vad Varo reflects that his master Ras Thavas is so completely in his power that he could "play any trick upon him that I chose, even to giving him the body of a calot" [P06]. In a world where brain transplantation is a scientific reality, the idea of transferring a human consciousness into the body of a calot represents the most profound form of humiliation and punishment imaginable.

This reflection reveals Vad Varo's dark humor as well as the ethical boundaries that Ras Thavas's science had already crossed. The calot's body — that of a subservient, non-sentient beast — is the antithesis of human dignity in the Barsoomian worldview.

## Guard Animals in Barsoomian Society

The calot's role as a guard animal reveals the layered security arrangements of Barsoomian noble estates. Mu Tel's palace employs multiple calots patrolling the gardens, functioning as a silent perimeter defense. This system operates alongside human guards and mechanical defenses — the calots are one component of a comprehensive security apparatus.

The use of animals rather than technology for perimeter defense reflects the Barsoomian integration of wildlife into civilized life. Like the banth used in ceremonial chariots and the thoat used for riding, the calot represents a domesticated (or semi-domesticated) predator pressed into human service.

## Comparison with Other Barsoomian Fauna

The calot occupies a specific ecological and functional niche in Barsoomian society. The banth is larger, more dangerous, and reserved for ceremonial use. The calot is smaller, faster, and used for practical security. The white ape exceeds both in savagery but is not domesticated. The thoat is a herbivorous mount. These distinctions suggest a Barsoomian approach to animal management that is more sophisticated than simple domestication — each creature is assigned a role commensurate with its natural characteristics.

## Significance

The calot illustrates the integration of dangerous wildlife into Barsoomian domestic life. Like the banth used in ceremonial contexts, the calot represents a tamed but still lethal aspect of Barsoomian fauna that the ruling classes deploy for practical purposes. The fact that even a trained soldier like Vad Varo fears the calot establishes the creature as a credible physical threat in a world already filled with dangers. The calot's cameo in the narrative — brief but intense — exemplifies Burroughs's technique of populating his world with details that feel both alien and functional.
