## Overview

Vobis Kan is the Jeddak — the absolute monarch — of [[Toonol]], the city-state that serves as the primary setting for the novel's opening chapters. A ruler who governs through fear and coercion, Vobis Kan is directly responsible for the execution of [[Gor Hajus]] when the assassin refused to murder a man who had once befriended him [004-004]. Though he appears only briefly in the narrative, his shadow looms over the entire first half of the story, representing the tyranny that [[Vad Varo]]'s quest implicitly challenges.

## Biography

### The Execution of Gor Hajus

When Vobis Kan ordered Gor Hajus to kill a man who had shown the assassin a past kindness, Gor Hajus refused — an act of principle that the Jeddak interpreted as defiance and, more dangerously, as a threat [004-004]. Vobis Kan, "conceiving the suspicion that Gor Hajus had him marked for slaying," ordered the assassin's immediate arrest. The sentence was death by the sword, and it was carried out without appeal [004-004].

### Pursuit of the Fugitives

When Gor Hajus was resurrected by Vad Varo and escaped from Ras Thavas's island, Vobis Kan responded with the full resources of his military. His patrol boats scoured the skies above Toonol, and his warriors boarded and searched ships — including Bal Zak's flier, which had been commandeered by the fugitives [008-006]. The company narrowly evaded capture on multiple occasions, their peril heightened at every turn by the Jeddak's relentless pursuit.

Despite his power, Vobis Kan's authority proved limited. His warriors searched without discovering the fugitives aboard the *Vosar*, and his intelligence network failed to locate them within Toonol — where Mu Tel's loyalty and the city's underground networks of friendship shielded the company more effectively than walls or weapons [010-004].

## Relationships

Vobis Kan's most significant relationship is with his former subject Gor Hajus, whom he first condemned to death and then hunted across the Toonolian skies. He maintains the formal allegiance of his nobles and warriors through fear rather than loyalty. His interactions with Ras Thavas appear to be those of a ruler who tolerates a useful but disquieting eccentric within his domain. Notably, Vobis Kan's authority does not extend universally — Mu Tel, a prince of a rival house, defies him with impunity, suggesting the Jeddak's power is more fragile than it appears.

## Significance

Vobis Kan represents the institutional face of tyranny in the novel. Unlike [[Xaxa]], whose evil is personal and intimate (the theft of a specific woman's body), Vobis Kan's corruption is structural — he is the system that punishes integrity and rewards obedience. His pursuit of Gor Hajus drives much of the novel's second-act tension, providing the external pressure that forges Vad Varo's company into a cohesive fighting unit. That the story resolves without Vobis Kan's explicit defeat — Gor Hajus never gets his "chance for one thrust" — is perhaps the novel's most realistic touch: the tyrant remains in power even as the small band of heroes achieves its personal victory.
