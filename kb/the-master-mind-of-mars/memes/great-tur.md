## Overview

The Great Tur is a forty-foot-tall idol representing Tur, the principal deity of [[Phundahl]], housed in the city's main temple. In the novel's penultimate act, [[Vad Varo]] and [[Gor Hajus]] conceal themselves inside the hollow statue, using its periscope eyes to spy upon the priests and orchestrate a dramatic "miracle" that topples the regime of Jeddara [[Xaxa]] [012-002]. The Great Tur thus serves as both a physical location and a narrative mechanism — the literal mouthpiece through which the heroes speak divine judgment.

## Description

The idol of Tur dominates the temple chamber, facing the main entrance across a broad nave lined with lesser idols. Its interior is hollow, accessible through a hidden passage that leads upward approximately forty feet to a circular platform running around the inside of the neck, just below the mouth, ears, and nostrils through which outside light enters [012-003]. A ladder with flat rungs provides access to this platform.

The eyes of Tur are constructed as functional periscopes. By turning them, an occupant can observe any portion of the temple below, and the view through the eyes is magnified — nothing within the chamber escapes Tur's gaze [012-005]. The acoustics of the hollow head are such that a voice spoken within projects outward with resonant authority, audible throughout the temple.

The temple itself is illuminated by a soft white light and features tall windows along two sides. At the far end, opposite the Great Tur, enormous doors provide the main entrance. The chamber contains incense burners, altars, and the idols of lesser Barsoomian deities, their trappings "rich beyond the conception of man" [012-004].

## Events

### The Oracle of Tur

Vad Varo and Gor Hajus entered the Great Tur through a secret passage revealed by their knowledge of the temple's architecture. From within, they overheard the priests discussing court intrigues — including the details of Xaxa's planned sacrifice of the captured [[Dar Tarus]] [012-006].

### The Miracle

When the temple filled with worshippers and Xaxa arrived to conduct the sacrifice, Vad Varo spoke through the mouth of Tur — the first time in a century that the god had "spoken" to his followers [012-008]. Proclaiming himself the true voice of Tur, Vad Varo declared Xaxa a usurper, condemned Sag Or, and commanded the Phundahlians to accept Dar Tarus as their rightful Jeddak. The dramatic intervention, amplified by the statue's resonant acoustics, convinced the superstitious Phundahlians and precipitated Xaxa's downfall [012-010].

## Inhabitants

The Great Tur is not inhabited in the conventional sense, but it is tended by a rotating cadre of priests and young priestesses who perform rites before the idols, "chanting meaningless gibberish" [012-005]. The high priest Hora San, who vanished mysteriously a century before the novel's events, was the last to whom Tur had "spoken" — a fact that Vad Varo exploits in crafting his divine intervention.
