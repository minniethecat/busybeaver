## Overview

Amhor is a Barsoomian city-state located approximately five thousand *haads* from [[Duhor]], its hereditary enemy [004-003]. Ruled by Jal Had, Prince of Amhor, the city plays a pivotal role in the backstory of Princess [[Valla Dia]], whose abduction from Duhor sets the novel's central conflict in motion. Though Amhor never appears directly in the narrative, its military aggression is the catalyst for the chain of events that leads Valla Dia into the hands of [[Ras Thavas]].

## Description

Amhor is situated on the opposite side of Barsoom from [[Toonol]], with Duhor lying between them along a route that passes through Phundahl territory. The distance from Duhor to Amhor is five thousand *haads*, a journey of considerable length even by Barsoomian standards, accomplished by war ships of the Amhorian fleet [004-003]. Amhor maintains a military navy capable of projecting power across inter-city distances, reflecting the competitive and warlike nature of Barsoomian city-state politics.

The city is governed by Jal Had, who holds the title of Prince of Amhor — a rank indicating rulership of a significant city-state within the broader Barsoomian political landscape [004-066].

## Rivalry with Duhor

The enmity between Amhor and Duhor is ancient and deeply rooted. As Valla Dia explains: "Hereditary enemies were Duhor and Amhor" [004-066]. This hereditary status suggests a feud spanning generations, the specific origins of which are not detailed in the narrative but which has become an established fact of Barsoomian geopolitics.

The catalyst for the novel's events came when Jal Had "came disguised into the city of Duhor, having heard, they say, of the great beauty attributed to the only daughter of Kor San, Jeddak of Duhor" [004-066]. Upon seeing Valla Dia, the Amhorian prince resolved to possess her and sent ambassadors to sue for her hand in marriage. Kor San, the Jeddak of Duhor, rejected the offer, having determined to wed his only daughter to one of his own Jeds so that a grandson carrying the blood of Kor San might rule Duhor [004-066].

## The Attack on Duhor

Jal Had's response to the rejection was swift and ruthless. He "equipped a great fleet and set forth to conquer Duhor and take by force that which he could not win by honorable methods" [004-068]. The timing of his attack was strategically calculated: Duhor's forces were "far afield in the south" at war with [[Helium]], leaving the city defended by only a small garrison [004-068]. Duhor fell to the Amhorian assault.

Jal Had personally led a picked force that sacked the palace of Kor San and "searched for the princess" Valla Dia [004-068]. However, the princess had anticipated his intentions and used a cosmetologist to disguise her radiant beauty, rendering herself unrecognizable. Unable to locate her through threats or torture of her loyal servants, Jal Had ordered that every woman within the palace be "seized and placed upon an Amhorian war ship" to be held as hostages in Amhor until the princess was delivered to him in marriage [004-068].

The hostage ship never reached Amhor. Having covered some four thousand of the five thousand *haads* between Duhor and Amhor, it was intercepted by a fleet from [[Phundahl]], which attacked the convoy, captured the vessel, and took its captives to Phundahl for auction — where Valla Dia fell to the bid of an agent of Ras Thavas [004-003].

## Military Power

Amhor's ability to equip "a great fleet" and project force across five thousand *haads* indicates a city-state of substantial military and naval capability [004-068]. The Amhorian war ships are capable of carrying both troops and prisoners on extended voyages. This naval strength, combined with the opportunistic timing of the attack on Duhor, suggests a political culture that values martial prowess and strategic cunning.
