## Overview

The haad (plural: haads) is a Barsoomian unit of linear distance used throughout Mars for measuring geographic separation and travel. It appears in *The Master Mind of Mars* as the standard unit for expressing the distances between Barsoomian city-states and for describing the scale of overland journeys [004-003] [004-004].

## Description

The haad provides a consistent metric for understanding Barsoomian geography. Valla Dia, describing her abduction, states that the Amhorian war ship "had covered some four thousand of the five thousand *haads* that separate Duhor from Amhor" when it was intercepted by a Phundahlian fleet [004-003]. This establishes the Duhor–Amhor distance at approximately five thousand haads, and positions Phundahl roughly four thousand haads from Duhor along the same route.

The scale of Barsoomian distances becomes clearer when Valla Dia describes the journey Vad Varo proposes. She tells him that "Duhor lies a full seven thousand, eight hundred *haads* from Toonol, upon the opposite side of the snow-clad Artolian Hills" [004-004]. She warns that the route crosses "the Toonolian Marshes, wild hordes, savage beasts and warlike cities" and that he "would but die uselessly within the first dozen *haads*" were he to attempt the journey alone [004-004].

Comparative distances from the text suggest the haad is broadly analogous to the Earthly mile or kilometer in practical use. A journey of "a dozen haads" represents a modest but non-trivial distance — enough to encounter multiple dangers in the untamed Barsoomian wilderness. Five to eight thousand haads separate major city-states, indicating a planetary scale comparable to Earth.

## Significance

The haad is one of several Barsoomian units of measurement — alongside the *zode* (a unit of time) and the *xat* (a shorter time unit) — that collectively establish the Martian world as having its own coherent system of weights and measures, independent of Earth. The use of haads in geographic description grounds the novel's fantastic elements in a sense of real physical space, reinforcing the illusion that Barsoom is a fully realized world with its own cartography and travel logistics.
