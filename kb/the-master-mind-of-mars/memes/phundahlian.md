## Phundahlian

**Phundahlian** refers to the people, culture, and artistic traditions of the Barsoomian city-state of Phundahl, ruled by Jeddara Xaxa. In contrast to the pragmatic Toonolians, Phundahlians are characterized by their deep religious sentiment, superstitious nature, and fierce loyalty to their hereditary ruler.

### Character and Governance

Ras Thavas described the Phundahlians as "egregious sentimentalists, filled with crass stupidities and superstitions, slaves to every variety of brain withering conceit" (002-044). The Toonolian surgeon found it remarkable that they kept "the old termagant, Xaxa, on the throne," branding them with "stupid idiocy" (002-044). He characterized Xaxa as "an ignorant, arrogant, selfish, stupid, cruel virago" whom the Phundahlians would nevertheless "fight and die for her because her father was Jeddak of Phundahl" (002-044).

The Phundahlians' loyalty to their hereditary ruler, however irrational by Toonolian standards, represents the counterpoint to Toonol's cynical pragmatism. Ras Thavas acknowledged that without sentiment, "no stable form of government" could be preserved and "the Phundahlians, or some other people, would overrun and conquer" Toonol (002-046).

### Religion and Art

Phundahlian religious life centers on the worship of [[Tur]], the Great God, and their sacred text *Turgan*, "the Phundahlian bible" (011-021). Phundahlian art reflects this religious devotion, with paintings described as "heavy and oppressing and without indication of high imaginative genius" (011-021). Temple and palace decorations feature "a series of monotonous repetitions," with the most common motif depicting "Turgan creating a round, flat Mars and hurling it into Space" (011-021).

Xaxa served as both Jeddara and High Priestess of Tur, wielding absolute political and religious authority. The narrator exploited the Phundahlians' superstitious terror of their god during the rescue of Valla Dia, when a trick with a skull in the Temple of Tur sent the entire congregation — including the nobles — fleeing in panic (012-055).

### Royal Portraiture

Phundahlian court paintings documenting the royal line had been recently altered: "the more recent ones in which Xaxa appeared had had the principal figure repainted so that there confronted me from time to time portraits, none too well done, of the beautiful face and figure of Valla Dia in the royal trappings of a Jeddara" (011-021). This substitution reflected Xaxa's vanity after her brain was transplanted into Valla Dia's young body.
