## Overview

**Jasoom** is the Barsoomian name for the planet Earth. In the terminology of Edgar Rice Burroughs's Mars (Barsoom), Jasoom is the distant green world from which John Carter and later Vad Varo originate. To the inhabitants of Barsoom, Jasoom is a mysterious and somewhat legendary place—known to exist and known to have produced remarkable men, but remote and poorly understood.

## Etymology and Usage

The term "Jasoom" is used exclusively by Barsoomians. Earth-born characters in *The Master Mind of Mars* refer to their home planet as Earth, but when speaking with Barsoomians they adopt the local nomenclature. Vad Varo introduces himself to Valla Dia by explaining: "I am not of Barsoom, but from Earth, the planet you sometimes call Jasoom" [P03]. This establishes Jasoom as the common Barsoomian term rather than a technical or esoteric one—it is "sometimes called" Jasoom, suggesting the name is in vernacular use.

A person from Jasoom is termed a **Jasoomian**. Valla Dia remarks: "There is another Jasoomian here whose fame has reached to the remotest corners of Barsoom," referring to John Carter, the War Lord of Helium [P03].

## Barsoomian Perceptions of Jasoom

The arrival of Earthmen on Barsoom is a source of wonder to its inhabitants. When Valla Dia learns that Vad Varo claims to be from Jasoom, she expresses amazement at how two Earthmen could have crossed the "great void" of interplanetary space: "How did you cross the great void?" [P03]. The fact that John Carter had already established himself as a legendary figure on Barsoom created a favorable impression of Jasoomians in general. Valla Dia offers a telling compliment: "Jasoom must be peopled with wonderful men" [P03].

## The Jasoomian Globe Device

In the palace of Mu Tel, Prince of the House of Kan, Vad Varo encounters a remarkable technological artifact: a globe that displays the surface of Jasoom. Mu Tel explains its operation: "The side of this globe now presented to you represents the face of the Earth turned towards us. You will note that the globe is slowly revolving. Place this pointer where you will upon the globe and that portion of Jasoom will be revealed for you" [P10].

This device suggests that Barsoomian science has developed instruments capable of remote observation of Earth, though the mechanism is not explained in detail. Vad Varo uses the device to locate the exact position of his home on the Earth's surface, a moment of nostalgic connection to his distant world.

## Interplanetary Travel

The means by which Earthmen travel from Jasoom to Barsoom is never fully explicated in *The Master Mind of Mars*. John Carter's method of transit—a form of astral projection described in earlier chronicles—is alluded to but not detailed. Vad Varo's own journey is described as the result of a battlefield injury followed by a mysterious transference of consciousness, echoing Carter's experience. The "great void" between Jasoom and Barsoom remains a boundary crossed by only a handful of individuals, reinforcing the rarity and significance of Jasoomians on the Red Planet.

## Relationship to Other Celestial Bodies

Jasoom occupies a unique place in the Barsoomian cosmology alongside Thuria (the nearer moon), Cluros (the farther moon), and Barsoom itself. Unlike the moons, which are ever-present in the Barsoomian sky, Jasoom is a remote point of light—visible but unreachable by conventional means. The cultural fascination with Jasoom on Barsoom mirrors the terrestrial fascination with Mars in Burroughs's own era, creating a symmetrical relationship between the two worlds.
