## Overview

The eighth Barsoomian ray, also referred to as the ray of propulsion, is a form of energy harnessed by Barsoomian science that exerts a force opposite to the pull of gravity. It is the fundamental principle behind the equilibrimotor, the standard personal flight device used on Barsoom for individual aerial movement.

The eighth ray is one of several distinct "Barsoomian rays" known to Martian science, each representing a different physical force. Unlike the fliers that use buoyancy tanks for lift, the equilibrimotor achieves flight through direct manipulation of gravitational force via the eighth ray, calibrated to precisely balance the user's weight without negating it entirely.

## In the Narrative

The eighth Barsoomian ray is introduced in Chapter VIII (Hands Up!) when Vad Varo and his companions equip themselves with equilibrimotors at a public depot in Toonol's landing tower. The equilibrimotor belt is described as being "filled with the eighth Barsoomian ray, or ray of propulsion, to a sufficient degree to just about equalize the pull of gravity and thus to maintain a person in equilibrium between that force and the opposite force exerted by the eighth ray" [008-003].

The ray is contained within a sealed belt worn around the waist. The containment is apparently critical — when Vad Varo struggles to control his descent towards the gardens of Mu Tel, he considers "ripping open my belt and letting the eighth ray escape" but hesitates, fearing that the sudden release of the counter-gravitational force "might precipitate me to the ground with too great violence" [009-003]. The ray's effect is immediately nullified when the belt is breached. Vad Varo later exploits this property when he slashes the buoyancy belt of a dead patrol warrior with his dagger, causing the body to drag him downward. "Instantly, as the rays escaped, his body started to drag me downward," he recounts, describing a rapid but controlled descent to safety [009-005].

## Significance

The eighth Barsoomian ray embodies the technological sophistication of Barsoomian civilization as depicted in Edgar Rice Burroughs's Mars series. Rather than presenting flight as magical or unexplained, the narrative grounds it in a specific physical principle — a named, numbered ray with defined properties — lending the world a sense of scientific coherence.

The eighth ray's operational mechanics reflect a consistent fictional physics: it does not negate gravity but exerts an equal opposing force, creating equilibrium rather than true anti-gravity [008-003]. This precision of description gives the equilibrimotor a functional plausibility that distinguishes it from more fantastical flight devices in early science fiction. The ray's behavior when its container is breached — dissipating immediately and causing loss of buoyancy — further reinforces the sense of a consistent, rule-governed fictional technology [009-003][009-005].

The eighth ray is notably the only numbered Barsoomian ray referenced in *The Master Mind of Mars*, where it serves as the power source for the novel's most visually dramatic action sequence: the aerial escape through the night skies of Toonol.
