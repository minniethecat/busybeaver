## Overview

The title of High Priestess in Phundahl represents the fusion of religious and political authority — the Jeddara of Phundahl simultaneously serves as the High Priestess of Tur, the city's principal deity. This dual role is held by Xaxa during the events of the narrative, making her the supreme authority over Phundahlian society through both secular and sacred offices [011-005].

## Description

The High Priestess presides over the Temple of Tur, a massive structure in the center of Phundahl that houses the Great Tur idol. The temple serves as the ceremonial and political center of the city, where public ceremonies, sacrifices, and pronouncements take place before gathered crowds of Phundahlians. Xaxa's role as both Jeddara and High Priestess means that religious authority and state power are indistinguishable in Phundahl.

The temple rituals involve dramatic ceremonies before the idol of Tur, who is regarded as the Father of Barsoom. During sacrifices, the High Priestess addresses Tur directly in front of the assembled populace: "O, Tur, Father of Barsoom, behold the offering we place before you, All-seeing, All-knowing, All-powerful One" [012-018]. When the god appears to speak through the idol — actually a hoax engineered by the protagonists — it is the High Priestess who stands at the altar holding the sacrificial blade.

The position carries significant political weight. Xaxa uses her religious authority to maintain control over Phundahl, and the threat of divine wrath — invoked by the protagonists through their manipulation of the Tur idol — is the only force capable of compelling her obedience. The Great Tur's pronouncement through the idol demands that "Xaxa and Sag Or shall give back to those from whom they stole them, the beautiful bodies they now wear" [012-022].

## Significance

The High Priestess title exemplifies the Barsoomian pattern of merging religious and temporal power. Xaxa's identity as both Jeddara and High Priestess means that challenges to religious authority are simultaneously challenges to political rule — and vice versa. This fusion is precisely what the protagonists exploit: by impersonating Tur's voice and commanding the High Priestess to obey, they bypass the political hierarchy entirely, addressing the power structure at its theological root [012-021].
