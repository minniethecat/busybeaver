## Overview

An equilibrimotor is a Barsoomian personal flight device that enables individual air travel by neutralizing gravitational pull. It is the standard means of personal aerial movement on Barsoom, used by civilians for transport, by police for urban patrol, and by military personnel for tactical movement [008-003]. Public depots where equilibrimotors are kept and rented exist in Barsoomian cities, making the technology widely accessible [008-001]. The equilibrimotor represents one of the novel's most vivid depictions of Barsoomian technological achievement — a society where personal flight has been democratized to the point of becoming a public utility.

## Technical Description

### Components

The equilibrimotor consists of several integrated components, described in detail when Vad Varo and his companions equip themselves at the Toonolian depot:

1. **The Belt**: A broad belt, "not unlike the life belt used aboard transoceanic liners upon Earth," serves as the core of the device [008-003]. This belt is filled with the eighth Barsoomian ray — also called the ray of propulsion — calibrated "to a sufficient degree to just about equalize the pull of gravity and thus to maintain a person in equilibrium between that force and the opposite force exerted by the eighth ray" [008-003]. The belt does not produce true anti-gravity but rather a state of neutral buoyancy, cancelling the wearer's weight without providing lift.

2. **The Radium Motor**: Permanently attached to the back of the belt is a small radium motor that provides the actual propulsive force [008-003]. Controls for the motor are mounted on the front of the belt, within easy reach of the wearer's hands.

3. **The Wings**: Rigidly attached to and projecting from each side of the upper rim of the belt is "a strong, light wing with small hand levers for quickly altering its position" [008-003]. These wings provide both lift and directional control; their position relative to the airstream determines whether the wearer ascends, descends, or maintains level flight.

### Operation

Operating an equilibrimotor requires skill and practice. The basic technique involves tilting the wings downward while walking to avoid lifting off the ground with every step [008-003]. To ascend, the wearer adjusts the wing angle to generate lift. Directional control is achieved by asymmetric wing adjustment, allowing turns and banks.

The learning curve for novice users is steep and dangerous. Vad Varo's first attempt at flight is a near-disaster: "I rose beautifully for about twenty feet, floating out over the city which lay hundreds of feet below, and then, quite suddenly, I turned upside down" [008-005]. He describes the experience as "a most startling sensation," noting with characteristic understatement that "below me lay the streets of a great city and no softer, I was sure, than the streets of Los Angeles or Paris" [008-005]. His uncontrolled flight produces "all sorts of strange loops and spirals and spins" until Dar Tarus, a more experienced flier, talks him through the recovery — first instructing him to "lie quietly" and then directing the manipulation of each wing until equilibrium is restored [008-005].

The device's sensitivity to control input is such that small errors produce large deviations. Vad Varo's difficulty in descending — "depress my wings as I would I moved only slightly downward" — nearly causes him to miss his intended landing zone entirely, drifting towards an elevated wing of Mu Tel's palace [009-003].

### Police-Grade Models

Law enforcement equilibrimotors are superior to civilian models. When a police patrol intercepts the fugitives, Vad Varo notes that the officer was "equipped with a much swifter and handier equilibrimotor than ours" [008-006]. The disparity is decisive: "he could have given us ten minutes start and overhauled each of us within another ten minutes, even though we had elected to fly in different directions" [008-006]. This technological asymmetry between state power and civilian capability is a recurring feature of Barsoomian society — the police have better tools than the policed.

The police model's advantages are not merely in speed but in handling. The civilian model proves difficult to control even in straight flight; the police model permits the kind of aggressive maneuvering necessary for urban patrol and pursuit. The warriors of Vobis Kan's army, "detailed to duty such as our Earthly police officers perform," patrol the city "both day and night" on these devices [008-006], maintaining a constant aerial surveillance of Toonol's streets and landing stages.

## The Equilibrimotor in the Escape Sequence

### Acquisition

The equilibrimotor escape is enabled by Bal Zak's exploitation of the jetan-loving watchman. While the watchman is absorbed in his game in Bal Zak's cabin, Gor Hajus and Dar Tarus "quickly selected four equilibrimotors and adjusted them upon us" [008-003]. The depot's doors are open and unguarded because "theft is practically unknown upon Barsoom, except for purposes entirely disassociated from a desire to obtain pecuniary profit through the thing stolen" [008-003]. This detail reveals a fundamental assumption of Barsoomian civic design: security is structured around property crime (which doesn't exist) rather than unauthorized use (which, in the fugitives' case, most certainly does).

### Flight

The flight sequence from the landing tower to the grounds of Mu Tel's palace is one of the novel's most kinetically vivid passages. The four fugitives launch from the stage in sequence — Gor Hajus first, then Hovan Du, then Vad Varo, then Dar Tarus — each rising into the night sky over Toonol. Vad Varo's comical ineptitude (the inverted flight, the loops and spirals) provides a moment of levity amid the tension of the escape, while also serving as a reminder that the narrator, for all his acquired surgical skill, is still fundamentally a stranger to Barsoomian technology.

### The Police Encounter

The flight is interrupted by a police patrol, and the ensuing confrontation demonstrates both the equilibrimotor's role in law enforcement and its vulnerability in combat. Gor Hajus's attack on the patrol officer is enabled by the equilibrimotor's wing structure — "the blow was broken by the wing of the warrior's equilibrimotor, which it demolished, yet it inflicted a severe wound in the fellow's shoulder" [009-001]. The damaged wing causes the officer to "wheel around erratically," preventing both escape and effective defense, and a second blow from Gor Hajus "split the man's head open to the bridge of his nose" [009-001].

The officer's corpse, still buoyed by its equilibrimotor, becomes a macabre beacon: "a guide even in death to point the way for his fellows to search us out" [009-003]. The floating body draws other patrols to the area, and the "screaming whistles of the air patrols" create an audible map of the closing net. This detail highlights a dark irony of the equilibrimotor's design: the very buoyancy that enables flight also prevents the dead from falling away into obscurity.

## Social and Cultural Dimensions

### Accessibility and Infrastructure

The existence of public equilibrimotor depots suggests a society where personal flight is treated as a municipal service rather than a luxury good. The depots are "public," the devices are "rented" rather than sold, and they are left unguarded because theft for profit is culturally unimaginable [008-001]. This infrastructure implies a level of social trust and technological standardization that speaks to the sophistication of Barsoomian civilization.

### The Eighth Ray

The physics underlying the equilibrimotor — the eighth Barsoomian ray — represents one of the novel's most interesting scientific conceits. Unlike the fliers that use buoyancy tanks for lift, the equilibrimotor achieves flight through direct manipulation of gravitational force. The eighth ray is described not as anti-gravity but as a counter-force: it "just about equalize[s] the pull of gravity and thus maintain[s] a person in equilibrium" [008-003]. The user remains subject to gravity; the belt simply provides an opposing force of equal magnitude. This precision of description — the belt does not negate gravity but balances it — gives the fictional technology a plausibility that pure anti-gravity would lack.

The eighth ray is one of several "Barsoomian rays" referenced throughout Burroughs's Martian corpus, each representing a distinct physical force that Martian science has learned to harness. The equilibrimotor is thus not merely a plot device but a window into the broader scientific framework of the Barsoomian world.

## Significance

The equilibrimotor sequence serves multiple narrative functions. It provides the action set-piece for the escape from Toonol, demonstrating the physical danger and technical challenge of flight in a way that raises the stakes for the fugitives. It introduces the police patrol system that will pursue them throughout the city, establishing the opposition they face. And it offers a moment of character comedy — Vad Varo's inverted, spinning flight — that humanizes the narrator and relieves narrative tension.

More broadly, the equilibrimotor embodies the technological sophistication that distinguishes Barsoom from early twentieth-century Earth. A society where personal flight is mundane enough to be a public utility, yet operationally challenging enough to threaten inexperienced users, is a society that has solved the engineering problem of anti-gravity without solving the human problem of training. The technology is democratized but not domesticated — anyone can fly, but not everyone can fly well.
