# Barsoomian Honor

**Barsoomian honor** encompasses the ethical codes, warrior traditions, and principles of conduct that govern interpersonal relationships across the planet Barsoom (Mars). The nature and strength of honor codes vary significantly between nations, from the rigid code of the *panthan* mercenaries to the utilitarian ethics of Toonol.

## The Word of a Barsoomian Noble

Among the Barsoomian nobility, a spoken word carries binding force. Dar Tarus offers Paxton "the word of honor of a Barsoomian noble"[ch04p] as the highest assurance of truthfulness. This concept of noble honor distinguishes the aristocracy from common warriors and serves as the foundation of trust in political dealings between rival houses.

## The Panthan Code

The **panthan**, or soldier of fortune, adheres to one of Barsoom's most rigorous ethical codes. Paxton describes them as "roving mercenaries ready to sell their swords to the highest bidder. Although they have no organization they are ruled by a severe code of ethics and while in the employ of a master are, almost without exception, loyal to him."[009-044]

This code provides the panthan class — men with no country, often fugitives from justice — with a substitute for national identity. Their professional honor, once engaged, is unbreakable. Gor Hajus, the assassin of Toonol, exemplifies this paradox: a hired killer whose reputation for integrity is as famous as his reputation for ruthlessness.

## Toonolian Paradox: Word-Keeping as Transaction

The Toonolians present Barsoom's most extreme ethical system. Mu Tel explains: "They hold that the only sin is failure—success, however achieved, is meritorious; and yet, paradoxical as it may seem, they never break their given word."[009-066] The Toonolians resolve this contradiction through temporal limitation — they "seldom, if ever, binding themselves to loyalty to another, and then only for a definitely prescribed period."[009-066]

Thus in Toonol, honor is a time-limited contract rather than a permanent bond. The word, once given, is absolute — but the wise never give it for long.

## Honor and Sentiment

Barsoomian honor stands in tension with sentiment. Ras Thavas considers sentiment "a bar to all progress"[002-044] and prides himself on freedom from emotional bonds. Yet even he operates within an honor framework — he trains Paxton as his surgical assistant not from altruism but from calculated self-interest, expecting "self-interested loyalty"[004-032] as the reliable basis of their partnership.

Gor Hajus embodies the internalization of honor beneath a cynical exterior. Paxton reflects: "His pride in his reputation for integrity and loyalty proved him a man of heart as truly as did his jealousy of his reputation for heartlessness prove him a man of sentiment; and in all this he was but typical of the people of Toonol."[009-068]

## Fair Fight

The concept of fair combat is integral to Barsoomian honor. Mu Tel's plan to seize the Toonolian throne requires that he "slain him in fair fight"[009-038] — assassination through a proxy like Gor Hajus would lack legitimacy. The form of combat matters: a Jeddak who falls in honorable combat loses his throne justly; one murdered in secret provokes dynastic crisis.

## Honor Among Enemies

When Paxton's party conquers an enemy patrol ship, the defeated captain negotiates surrender. After a brief council, the defeated warriors accept terms — the recognition that an honorable surrender to a worthy opponent preserves status in a way that death in a lost cause does not. This pragmatic dimension of Barsoomian honor enables the ceaseless warfare between city-states to continue without genocide.
