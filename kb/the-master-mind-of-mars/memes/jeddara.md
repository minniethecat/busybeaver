## Overview

Jeddara is the Barsoomian title for a female sovereign — the equivalent of empress or queen — who rules a city-state with absolute authority. The novel's central antagonist, **Xaxa**, holds the title of Jeddara of [[Phundahl]], and her misrule provides the political dimension of the narrative's conflict. The title's male counterpart is **Jeddak**, held by rulers such as Vobis Kan of [[Toonol]] and, by the novel's end, [[Dar Tarus]] of Phundahl.

## Description

### Powers and Authority

The Jeddara wields absolute power within her domain. Xaxa commands the Jeddara's Guard, controls the palace, and can order arrests and executions at will. When Vad Varo, [[Gor Hajus]], and Hovan Du infiltrate her court, she has them disarmed and confined with a gesture [011-005]. Her authority extends to the temple, where she presides over the sacrifices to Tur and interprets the god's will — or rather, exploits that interpretation for political ends.

|-------|--------|---------|
| Jeddak | Male ruler | Vobis Kan (Toonol), Dar Tarus (Phundahl) |
| Jeddara | Female ruler | Xaxa (Phundahl), Valla Dia (Duhor, by inheritance) |

### Xaxa's Rule

Xaxa embodies the corruption of absolute power. Aging and desperate to preserve her beauty and authority, she commissions [[Ras Thavas]] to transplant her brain into the young body of Princess [[Valla Dia]] [003-002]. This act — the theft of another's physical form for personal vanity — is the novel's originating crime. As Jeddara, Xaxa leverages the machinery of state and religion to conceal her secret: she employs spies, commands warriors, and manipulates the temple of Tur to maintain her grip on Phundahl.

Her reign ends not through military conquest but through the manipulation of the very religious superstition she had exploited. When Vad Varo, speaking through the idol of Tur, denounces her before the assembled court, her power evaporates instantly. She dies of apoplexy on the temple floor — "a wicked old woman dead of apoplexy" — a fitting end for a ruler whose authority rested on illusion [014-009].

### The Title in Succession

The novel explores the question of legitimate succession through multiple lenses. Valla Dia is the rightful Jeddara of [[Duhor]] by inheritance, yet spends most of the narrative dispossessed of her very body. Dar Tarus becomes Jeddak not by birth but by acclamation — proclaimed by "Tur" himself. This suggests that on Barsoom, political legitimacy can flow from multiple sources: heredity, divine mandate, popular support, or sheer martial prowess.

## Significance

The Jeddara/Jeddak system represents the Barsoomian variation on absolute monarchy. By making the primary antagonist a Jeddara and the primary victim a displaced princess, Burroughs sets up a political framework in which the restoration of rightful rule is inseparable from the restoration of bodily integrity. Xaxa's corruption as Jeddara is specifically gendered — she abuses power to preserve youth and beauty — but the critique extends to the system itself: absolute power, unchecked by any constitutional mechanism, enables the novel's central crimes.
