## Overview

**Cluros** is the farther and slower-moving of the two moons of Barsoom (Mars). Known to Earth astronomers as Deimos, Cluros follows a deliberate, unhurried path across the Barsoomian sky. In contrast to its sister moon Thuria—which races through the heavens in less than four hours—Cluros is characterized by patience and steadiness, often personified in masculine terms.

## Orbital Characteristics

Cluros orbits Barsoom at a greater distance than Thuria and consequently moves with visible slowness. Vad Varo, the narrator of *The Master Mind of Mars*, describes watching Cluros from his window in Ras Thavas's tower: "Across the open casement I had watched Cluros, the farther moon, take his slow deliberate way" [P10]. The adjective "deliberate" is significant—where Thuria's motion is urgent and fleeting, Cluros seems purposeful and measured.

Because of its higher orbit, Cluros sets well before Thuria on most nights. During the stealthy approach to the Tower of Thavas, the narrative notes: "Cluros had long since set and Thuria was streaming towards the horizon as we stopped our motor and drifted without lights a mile or two south of the tower" [P15]. The sequential setting of the two moons—first Cluros, then Thuria—creates a gradient of darkness that the protagonists exploit for concealment.

## Personification and Gender

Edgar Rice Burroughs consistently personifies the two moons along gendered lines. Thuria is "she" and "her"—a "mistress" who "fled through the heavens" and whose "brilliant rays" illuminate the night [P10]. Cluros is "he" and "his"—a patient masculine presence whose "slow deliberate way" contrasts with Thuria's feminine haste. This gendering aligns with the broader Barsoomian cultural tendency to personify celestial bodies.

The personification is not merely decorative. The moons' contrasting characters serve as a natural metaphor for the duality that runs through the narrative: speed versus patience, urgency versus deliberation, feminine versus masculine. When Vad Varo must act quickly, he watches Thuria; when the party can afford to wait, Cluros's slower pace sets the rhythm.

## Narrative Function

Cluros appears less frequently than Thuria in the text, which is consistent with its role as a background presence rather than an urgent timekeeper. Its primary narrative function is to establish the depth of night—once Cluros has set, only Thuria remains to delay total darkness. The sequence "Cluros had long since set and Thuria was streaming towards the horizon" [P15] signals that the characters are in the deepest part of the Barsoomian night, the optimal moment for covert action.

## Comparison with Thuria

| Attribute | Thuria | Cluros |
|-----------|--------|--------|
| Earth name | Phobos | Deimos |
| Orbit | Inner, fast (~4 hours) | Outer, slow |
| Personification | Feminine | Masculine |
| Character | Swift, brilliant, urgent | Deliberate, patient, steady |
| Narrative role | Timekeeper, tension driver | Depth-of-night marker |

Together, Thuria and Cluros form a complementary pair that defines the rhythm of the Barsoomian night. Their sequential setting creates the window of total darkness that the story's heroes repeatedly exploit, and their contrasting characters enrich the celestial backdrop against which the action unfolds.
