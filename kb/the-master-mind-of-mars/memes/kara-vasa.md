## Overview

Kara Vasa is the betrothed of [[Dar Tarus]], the Phundahlian warrior whose body was stolen by Sag Or. Her disappearance from [[Phundahl]] following Dar Tarus's arrest and execution sets in motion one of the narrative's parallel quests. While [[Vad Varo]] seeks to restore [[Valla Dia]] to her body, Dar Tarus simultaneously searches for his lost love — a quest that reaches its resolution only in the novel's final pages, when Kara Vasa is reunited with Dar Tarus in his restored body [014-002].

## Biography

Kara Vasa was a young noblewoman of Phundahl, the daughter of a wealthy father who opposed the regime of Jeddara [[Xaxa]]. She was betrothed to Dar Tarus, a warrior of the city, and their love was well known among the Phundahlian nobility [013-003].

### Disappearance

When Dar Tarus was arrested and condemned to death for his loyalty to the deposed Valla Dia, Kara Vasa's world collapsed. The day after Dar Tarus's execution, she vanished from Phundahl. Xaxa's court suspected that Kara Vasa's father had spirited her away to safety, but the father was subsequently assassinated and his property confiscated — and Kara Vasa remained missing [013-007]. Sag Or, who had inherited Dar Tarus's body, claimed ignorance of her whereabouts.

### Reunion

Dar Tarus never abandoned hope of finding Kara Vasa. Throughout the company's adventures, he periodically asked after her, his longing for reunion equal to Vad Varo's determination to save Valla Dia. "Would that I might be reunited with my sweet Kara Vasa," he sighed [013-002]. His persistence was rewarded in the novel's denouement: after the restoration of all bodies and the return to Toonol, John Carter himself interceded, and Kara Vasa was found and brought to Dar Tarus. The two were married shortly thereafter, with John Carter and Valla Dia in attendance [014-002].

## Relationships

Kara Vasa's defining relationship is with Dar Tarus, her betrothed and eventual husband. Their love story — interrupted by political assassination, brain transplantation, and the theft of Dar Tarus's body — parallels the central romance of Vad Varo and Valla Dia, reinforcing the novel's theme that love can survive even the literal loss of physical identity. She has no direct interaction with Xaxa or Sag Or, though their actions determine the course of her life.

## Significance

Kara Vasa functions as a structural mirror to Valla Dia: both are noblewomen separated from the men who love them by the machinations of Xaxa's court. While Valla Dia's separation is physical (her brain trapped in a stranger's body), Kara Vasa's is geographical (lost and hidden). The dual resolution — Valla Dia restored to her body, Kara Vasa restored to Dar Tarus — gives the novel's ending a double wedding symmetry that reinforces its romantic comedy underpinnings beneath the science-fictional surface.
