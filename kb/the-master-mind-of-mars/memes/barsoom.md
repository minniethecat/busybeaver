## Overview

Barsoom is the name by which the inhabitants of Mars know their world. A planet of great antiquity, its civilizations have risen and fallen over countless millennia, leaving behind technologies — such as the brain transplantation mastered by [[Ras Thavas]] — that seem miraculous to Earthly observers. The Barsoom of *The Master Mind of Mars* is a world of city-states ([[Toonol]], [[Phundahl]], [[Duhor]], [[Helium]]), warrior codes, and a complex religious landscape ranging from the atheism of Toonol to the elaborate Tur-worship of Phundahl. The novel's narrator, [[Vad Varo]], arrives on Barsoom from [[Earth]] after a near-fatal wound in the trenches of World War I, transported across interplanetary space through a force he can neither explain nor understand.

Ulysses Paxton, as narrator, describes his first confirmation that he has reached the world of his obsession:

> There could be no doubt of it, I stood upon the soil of the Red Planet, I had come to the world of my dreams — to Barsoom. [010-008]

This is a dying planet, ancient beyond Earthly comprehension, where oceans have long since vanished and the thin air is sustained only by technological artifice. Yet it remains a world of fierce vitality — of flashing swords, soaring fliers, and codes of honor that would seem impossibly romantic to a jaded Earthman.

## Description

Barsoom is characterized by its dying ecology and ancient sophistication. The oceans have receded, leaving vast dead sea bottoms crossed by the great canals. The atmosphere is thin but maintained by an atmosphere plant, the details of which are more fully explored in other volumes of Burroughs's Barsoom series. The planet's lower gravity permits Earthmen like [[Vad Varo]] to possess seemingly superhuman strength.

The landscape Vad Varo first encounters from the tower of Ras Thavas reveals the peculiar geography of this portion of Barsoom. Beyond the rocky island on which the laboratory sits stretches the [[Great Toonolian Marshes]], an immense wetland whose full scale is striking:

> This landscape extended as far as the eye could reach, broken by occasional islands similar to that upon which we were and at a short distance by the skyline of a large city, whose towers and domes and minarets glistened and sparkled in the sun as though plated with shining metals and picked out with precious gems. [014-012]

This city is [[Toonol]], one of the principal Barsoomian city-states of the narrative. The marshes that surround it "extend nearly eighteen hundred Earth miles east and west and in some places have a width of three hundred miles" [014-012] and are "commanded by Phundahl at their western end and Toonol at the east; inhospitable kingdoms that invite no intercourse with the outside world and maintain their independence alone by their inaccessibility and savage aloofness" [014-012].

### Peoples of Barsoom

| Race | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Red Martians | Dominant humanoid race; inhabit the city-states |
| Green Martians | Gigantic, four-armed nomadic warriors |
| White Apes | Fierce, intelligent beasts (see [[Hovan Du]]) |
| Kaldanes | Mentioned as extinct; brain-like creatures |
| Hormads | Synthetic men created in laboratories |

The Red Martians, to which all major characters belong, are a copper-skinned people who have inherited the cities and technologies of more ancient races. Their culture combines high sophistication — brain transplantation, fliers, equilibrimotors — with a warrior ethos that values personal combat and honor above all else.

The Red Martians are not, however, the first people of Barsoom. The tower from which Vad Varo first views the Toonolian Marshes was, Ras Thavas explains, built some twenty-three thousand years ago:

> This tower was built some twenty-three thousand years ago by an ancestor of mine who was driven from Toonol by the reigning Jeddak of the time. Here, and upon other islands, he gathered a considerable following, dominated the surrounding marshes and defended himself successfully for hundreds of years. [014-013]

This demonstrates the immense temporal scale of Barsoomian civilization. Vad Varo notes "the color of the sward and the skin-hue of the red Martians" [010-008] as proof of his arrival, but Barsoom's true human diversity — the fair-skinned white race, the black race, and the yellow race of the older series — lies beyond the immediate scope of this novel.

The [[white-ape]] merits particular attention. When a specimen is revived in Ras Thavas' laboratory, Vad Varo records its physical description in detail:

> It was a colossal creature about ten or fifteen feet tall, standing erect, and had an intermediary set of arms or legs midway between its upper and lower limbs. The eyes were close together and non-protruding; the ears were high set, while its snout and teeth were strikingly like those of our African gorilla. [005-029]

Ras Thavas calls it "one of the most savage and fearsome denizens of the Red Planet" [005-029]. These creatures prove lethally useful — Ras Thavas keeps white apes in the jungle surrounding his island to discourage intruders and prevent desertion. Even the Barsoomian lion, the [[banth]], "hesitates to cross their path" [014-012].

### Religion

Barsoomian religion varies by city-state. The Phundahlians worship **Tur**, the Father of Barsoom, in an elaborate temple ritual that involves "swinging the bars" before rows of idols and repeating the phrase "Tur is Tur" [010-008]. The Toonolians, by contrast, are avowed atheists who regard Phundahl's devotions with contempt. This religious tension plays a crucial role in the plot, as Vad Varo exploits Phundahlian piety to orchestrate the "miracle" that topples Sag Or.

The [[temple-of-tur]] in Phundahl provides one of the novel's most vivid set pieces. Inside, the worshippers perform a bewildering series of rituals before a gallery of grotesque images:

> Never have I seen such a gorgeous display of wealth and lavish ornamentation as confronted my eyes in this, the first of the temples of Tur that it was my fortune to behold. [016-005]

At one image, worshippers "lay prone upon the floor against which they bumped their heads seven times and then arose and dropped a piece of money into a receptacle" [016-005]. At another, they "swung to the bars" and "mumbled the meaningless phrase" [016-005]. A third station required "standing upon his head" while "repeating, over and over, 'Tur is Tur; Tur is Tur; Tur is Tur'" [016-006]. Vad Varo, an outsider baffled by the proceedings, questions what the words mean. [[Dar Tarus]] explains with devastating deadpan: the previous image's worshippers "said, Tur is Tur; while at this they absolutely reversed it and said, Tur is Tur. Do you not see? They turned it right around backwards, which makes a very great difference" [016-007]. The absurdity of this explanation — the phrases are phonetically identical — is lost on the devout Dar Tarus, who sighs that Vad Varo's failure to appreciate the distinction is because he "lack[s] faith" [016-007].

## The Dying World

Barsoom is above all an aged planet. The oceans have departed; the atmosphere must be artificially maintained. Vad Varo arrives on "the soil of the Red Planet" [010-008] and finds a world where even the corridors beneath Ras Thavas' laboratory are "covered with an impalpable dust, long undisturbed" and where "the tiny radium bulbs that faintly illuminated the sub-barsoomian depths were likewise coated" [005-027]. Several doorways in these ancient vaults "had been tightly sealed with masonry" [005-027]. The very architecture bespeaks vast age — the tower is twenty-three millennia old, and to the Barsoomians this is merely the work of a recognizable ancestor with a documented political grievance.

This sense of exhaustion permeates Barsoomian society itself. When Vad Varo first encounters Valla Dia in the withered body of Xaxa, she embodies the literal theft of youth — beauty stolen and hoarded by the old and powerful. The brain-transplantation technology of Ras Thavas, which might revitalize a dying civilization, is instead used to enable narcissistic immortalism:

> I am a very old man, even as age goes upon Barsoom. I have lived more than a thousand years. I have passed the allotted natural span of life, but I am not through with my life's work — I have but barely started it. I must not die. Barsoom must not be robbed of this wondrous brain and skill of mine. [005-035]

His solution is to transfer his aged brain into a succession of young bodies, preserving himself indefinitely while the young die to supply his envelopes. This is Barsoom's dying-world logic: the old consume the young.

Yet Barsoom is not without its counter-currents of renewal. Vad Varo, the Earthman, brings to this ancient world a peculiarly Earthly vigor. His mission — to restore Valla Dia to her proper body and return her to Duhor — enacts a kind of correction of Barsoom's unnatural consumption of its own youth. The [[red-planet]] may be dying, but in Burroughs's conception, it is still a world where justice and restoration remain possible through courage and audacity.

## Barsoomian Technology

Barsoomian science, though described in terms that to modern readers appear fantastic, represents the accumulated knowledge of a civilization far older than Earth's. The novel showcases four principal technologies:

**Brain Transplantation.** The central marvel of *The Master Mind of Mars*, mastered by Ras Thavas and transferred to Vad Varo. The process involves draining and replacing blood, transferring the brain from one cranium to another, and reviving the subject through methods that Ras Thavas guards jealously. As technology, it literalizes the novel's philosophical concerns with identity and embodiment.

**The Equilibrimotor.** A personal flying device consisting of a broad belt "filled with the eighth Barsoomian ray, or ray of propulsion, to a sufficient degree to just about equalize the pull of gravity and thus to maintain a person in equilibrium between that force and the opposite force exerted by the eighth ray" [012-009]. A small [[radium]] motor and wing controls allow the wearer to navigate the skies. Vad Varo's first attempt to fly one nearly ends in disaster: "I rose beautifully for about twenty feet, floating out over the city which lay hundreds of feet below, and then, quite suddenly, I turned upside down" [012-010].

**Controlled Light.** The street lighting of Toonol demonstrates a sophisticated command of light-wave physics:

> The luminosity below me seemed confined directly to the area to be lighted; there was no diffusion of light upward or beyond the limits the lamps were designed to light. This was effected, I was told, by lamps designed upon principles resulting from ages of investigation of the properties of light waves and the laws governing them which permit Barsoomian scientists to confine and control light as we confine and control matter. The light waves leave the lamp, pass along a prescribed circuit and return to the lamp. [010-013]

**Martian Telepathy.** Barsoomians possess a natural telepathic ability. Vad Varo learns that "unless a Martian is constantly upon the alert any other Martian can read clearly his every thought" [005-034]. Ras Thavas finds himself uniquely frustrated because Vad Varo's Earthly origin renders his mind opaque to this faculty — he "kept his brain sealed like one of his own blood jars, nor was he ever for a moment found with his barriers down" [005-034]. The assassin [[Gor Hajus]] was legendary precisely because he could strike his victims openly, in fair combat, without relying upon the ambush and stealth that telepathy would otherwise necessitate.

## Martian Society

Barsoomian society is feudal and military in structure, organized around a hierarchy of hereditary rulers. The [[jeddak]] (emperor) and [[jeddara]] (empress) sit at the apex; below them are jed (princes) and [[odwar]] (generals); beneath these ranks are the warrior caste and the common citizenry. City-states like Toonol and Phundahl function as independent polities, each with its own ruler, army, and customs.

The social fabric is woven through with the institution of the [[panthan]] — the Barsoomian soldier of fortune. These mercenaries, described as "virtually men without a country," are "ruled by a severe code of ethics and while in the employ of a master are, almost without exception, loyal to him" [010-017]. Gor Hajus, the notorious Toonolian assassin, exemplifies the peculiar honor system of the panthan:

> His killings were always the results of fair fights in which the victim had every opportunity to defend himself and slay his attacker; and he was famous for his loyalty to his friends. [006-012]

Gor Hajus "never struck down a woman or a good man" and "never struck from behind" [006-012] — a code of honor that makes assassination a recognized profession rather than a crime.

The institution of [[barsoomian-slavery]] underpins the economy. Slaves attend the wealthy, guard them at night, and form the bulk of the labor force. Vad Varo, who is technically Ras Thavas' slave, finds that "by kindness and understanding" [006-009] he can win the loyalty of those who serve him — a loyalty that proves essential to his plans. The slaves "had no reason to love Ras Thavas and every reason to hate him" [006-009], a reminder that Barsoom's social order, however ancient, rests on foundations of coercion.

Assassination is not only common but institutionalized. The [[barsoomian-duel]] provides a formalized channel for personal enmity, but the broader culture regards assassination — at least the open, honorable variety practiced by Gor Hajus — as a legitimate profession. Mu Tel, Prince of the House of Kan, sleeps in an apartment raised "forty or fifty feet" [010-017] above the ground and protected by wire netting "that fends off assassins of the air from these high-flung sleeping apartments" [013-003]. The constant threat of the assassin's blade has shaped Barsoomian architecture, urban planning, and daily life.

Barsoomian customs also reflect deep cultural assumptions about identity and the body. The transaction by which Xaxa purchases Valla Dia's youthful body — "She brought the highest price that I have ever been paid," Ras Thavas remarks with professional satisfaction [003-018] — treats physical form as a commodity. That no character in the novel expresses moral horror at the *principle* of such transactions (only at their consequences when applied to oneself) reveals how thoroughly the commodification of the body has been normalized in Barsoomian society.

## Significance

Barsoom in this novel serves as both exotic setting and thematic mirror. Its dying-world atmosphere — ancient, weary, morally exhausted — contrasts with the youthful vigor of Vad Varo and the restorative justice he brings. The planet's signature technology, brain transplantation, literalizes the novel's central preoccupation with identity: if a brain can be moved from body to body, what does it mean to be oneself? Barsoom is not merely a backdrop but the enabling condition for the narrative's deepest philosophical questions.
