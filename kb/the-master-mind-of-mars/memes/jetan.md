## Overview

Jetan is a Barsoomian board game of strategy whose cultural position mirrors that of chess on Earth. It is played by warriors, officers, and common citizens alike, and its devoted players are known to neglect their duties for the pleasure of a match [008-001]. The game serves a dual function in the narrative: it enriches the texture of Barsoomian daily life as a shared cultural touchstone, and it provides the mechanism by which Bal Zak enables the escape of Vad Varo, Gor Hajus, Dar Tarus, and Hovan Du from the Toonolian landing tower.

## Description

### The Game and Its Players

The nature of jetan is revealed not through direct description of its rules or pieces, but through the social role it plays. Bal Zak, captain of the patrol ship *Vosar*, identifies the watchman at the equilibrimotor depot as "a devotee of *jetan*" who "would rather play *jetan* than attend to his duties as watchman" [008-001]. This characterization is simultaneously an observation about the watchman's character and a statement about the game's power: jetan is absorbing enough to make a man neglect his professional obligations, and its devotees are known quantities whose passion can be predicted and exploited.

Bal Zak himself is a jetan player, noting that he "often remain[s] aboard the Vosar at night and occasionally he and I indulge in a game" [008-001]. This detail establishes that the game crosses rank boundaries — a ship captain and a depot watchman sitting down as equals over the board. The social leveling implicit in jetan play mirrors the egalitarian dimension of chess on Earth, where rank and station are suspended during the match.

### The Jetan Diversion

The jetan game serves as the linchpin of Bal Zak's escape plan. His proposal is elegantly simple: "I will ask him up tonight and while he is thus engaged you may go to the depot, help yourselves to equilibrimotors" [008-001]. Gor Hajus responds with immediate approval: "Good!" [008-003]. The plan requires no violence, no deception beyond a simple invitation, and no breach of honor — the watchman voluntarily abandons his post, drawn by the irresistible lure of a match.

The episode illustrates a recurring theme in the novel: the gap between institutional systems and human nature. The Toonolian landing tower has watchmen, patrols, and procedures designed to prevent exactly this kind of escape — yet all of these formal defenses are undone by one man's preference for a board game over his duties. Security is only as strong as the weakest human link in its chain.

## Cultural Significance

### A Window into Barsoomian Society

Jetan functions primarily as a world-building detail, but its presence carries significant cultural implications. The fact that a board game has a name, recognized devotees, and established patterns of play across social classes suggests a civilization with sufficient leisure, literacy, and cultural cohesion to sustain such a pastime. The game represents a universal touchstone — something as familiar to a Toonolian watchman as to a ship captain — that helps establish Barsoom as a coherent civilization rather than a collection of warring city-states.

The detail that Bal Zak plays jetan "occasionally" with the watchman further suggests that these games are a regular, almost ritualized feature of life in the landing tower. The watchman's predictable availability for a match implies a standing arrangement, a recurrent social occasion that has become part of the rhythm of Toonolian life. This small detail contributes to the novel's larger project of making Barsoom feel like a lived-in world rather than a mere backdrop for adventure.

### The Psychology of Distraction

The jetan episode also functions as a character study in miniature. The watchman is not corrupt, not negligent in any malicious sense — he is simply a man whose passion outweighs his sense of duty. His willingness to abandon his post for a game is presented not as a moral failing but as a recognizable human weakness, the kind of vulnerability that exists in any society and that can be exploited by those sharp enough to recognize it. Bal Zak's exploitation of this weakness is presented as clever rather than cruel — the watchman gets his game, the fugitives get their equilibrimotors, and no one is harmed.

This tolerance for human frailty stands in contrast to the novel's treatment of more systematic evils. Xaxa's tyranny, Vobis Kan's paranoia, and Ras Thavas's moral indifference are presented as profound problems requiring revolutionary solutions; the watchman's jetan habit is merely a foible, a small crack in the social edifice through which the fugitives can slip.

## Significance

Jetan functions in the novel primarily as a plot device, enabling a critical transition in the escape sequence without requiring violence or confrontation. Yet its presence enriches the novel's world-building by establishing Barsoom as a civilization with shared cultural institutions, leisure activities, and recognizable human weaknesses. The game represents the everyday life of Barsoom — the small pleasures and distractions that persist alongside the grand dramas of tyranny, revolution, and scientific marvel.

The jetan diversion also exemplifies a narrative strategy that Burroughs employs throughout the novel: the use of minor, seemingly incidental details to solve major plot problems. The escape from the Toonolian landing tower, which could have required an elaborate action sequence, is instead accomplished through a game invitation. This economy of means — achieving significant effects through small, character-driven actions — is characteristic of the novel's approach to adventure storytelling.
