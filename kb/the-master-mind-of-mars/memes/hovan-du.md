## Overview

Hovan Du is one of the most extraordinary beings in the annals of [[Barsoom]] — a massive white ape of the species native to the dead sea bottoms, whose simian body was given human intelligence through [[Ras Thavas]]'s brain-transplantation technology. Fierce in combat and unwaveringly loyal, Hovan Du becomes an indispensable member of [[Vad Varo]]'s company during their quest to restore [[Valla Dia]] to her rightful body.

> I think that the sight of the great, savage beast must have had a demoralizing effect upon them, for they hesitated, each seeming to be willing to accord his fellow the honor of first engaging us [008-006]

He represents the novel's recurring theme of the disjunction between physical form and inner identity — a creature whose terrifying exterior conceals human reason, strategic acumen, and fierce devotion to his companions.

## Biography

Hovan Du was originally an ordinary great white ape of Barsoom, a creature of immense strength and ferocity native to the dead sea bottoms. Like [[Gor Hajus]], his body came into Ras Thavas's possession after death, and the surgeon transplanted a human brain into the ape's massive frame [008-004].

### Joining the Company

When Vad Varo assembled his band of resurrected allies — [[Dar Tarus]] and Gor Hajus — Hovan Du joined them as a fellow "experiment" of Ras Thavas [007-005]. Though his formidable appearance initially inspired fear, his human intelligence made him a valuable strategist.

> the ape, whom we now called by the name belonging to the human half of his brain, Hovan Du, preferred the first alternative of the second plan; and Gor Hajus the second alternative [007-003]

During the escape from the island of Thavas, Hovan Du contributed to the tactical debates, arguing that seizing a ship after departure would grant more escape time than taking one from the hangar [007-002].

### The Ape Who Spoke

Hovan Du's most dramatic moment came in the palace of [[Xaxa]], Jeddara of [[Phundahl]]. Disguised as a trained beast, he performed in the throne room while Gor Hajus acted as his handler [011-003]. When the ruse was discovered and Vad Varo and Gor Hajus faced arrest, Hovan Du revealed his true nature.

> Heretofore the Phundahlians must have looked upon him as a remarkable specimen of great ape, marvelously trained, but now, of a sudden, he paralyzed them with awe, for his roars and growls took the form of words and he spoke with the tongue of a human [011-008]

Leaping through the palace windows with the traitorous noble Sag Or clutched in his grasp, Hovan Du escaped into the trees, securing a valuable hostage.

> "I go," he cried, "but let harm befall my friends and I shall return and tear the heart from Xaxa. Tell her that, from the Great Ape of Ptarth." [011-009]

He later reappeared atop the royal hangar at the critical moment when Vad Varo confronted Xaxa, his arrival turning a tense standoff into victory.

> In the dim light I saw something that seemed a human head, and yet unhuman, rise slowly above the edge of the roof, and then, silently, a great form followed, and then I recognized it—Hovan Du, the great white ape. [012-003]

## Relationships

Hovan Du forms a close bond with [[Gor Hajus]], the two often fighting side by side. His relationship with Dar Tarus is that of brothers-in-arms. He regards Vad Varo with fierce loyalty, following the Earthman's commands without hesitation.

> It was really only when feeding or fighting that the simian half of Hovan Du's brain appeared to exercise any considerable influence upon him; yet there seemed little doubt that it always colored all his thoughts and actions to some extent, accounting for his habitual taciturnity and the quickness with which he was aroused to anger [010-004]

His simian half emerges only "when feeding or fighting" [010-008], making him both a terrifying combatant and, in quieter moments, a being of surprising gentleness — it is Hovan Du who finds fruits and nuts for the company when they hide in the [[Great Toonolian Marshes]].

> Here Hovan Du found fruits and nuts for us which the simian section of his brain pronounced safe for human consumption, and instinct led him to a nearby spring from which there bubbled delicious water. [013-003]

## Significance

Hovan Du embodies the central theme of *The Master Mind of Mars*: that identity resides in the brain, not the body. Though trapped in the form of a beast, he demonstrates intelligence, loyalty, and even eloquence. His dual nature — the "simian half" of his brain that governs instinct, and the human half that governs reason — mirrors the larger tension in the novel between savagery and civilization. In the narrative structure, Hovan Du serves as both comic relief (in the Phundahl "performing ape" sequence) and as the deus ex machina whose unexpected appearance repeatedly saves the protagonists at moments of crisis.
