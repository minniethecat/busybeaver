## Medical College of Ras Thavas

The **Medical College of Ras Thavas** is the vast surgical facility maintained by [[Ras Thavas]], the Master Mind of Mars. Located on an island in the Toonolian Marshes, the institution serves as both a hospital for wealthy clients and a repository for preserved bodies, where the surgeon conducts his revolutionary brain transplantation and organ replacement procedures.

> The old man led me into a small chamber from which opened numerous doors, through one of which they were just bearing my late antagonist. We followed into a large, brilliantly lighted chamber wherein there burst upon my astounded vision the most gruesome scene that I ever had beheld. Rows upon rows of tables arranged in parallel lines filled the room and with few exceptions each table bore a similar grisly burden, a partially dismembered or otherwise mutilated human corpse. Above each table was a shelf bearing containers of various sizes and shapes, while from the bottom of the shelf depended numerous surgical instruments, suggesting that my entrance upon Barsoom was to be through a gigantic medical college. [001-013]

The narrator's first impression of the facility established its defining character: industrial in scale, clinical in organization, and horrifying in its contents. Over months of service, Vad Varo came to understand it as "the great institution presided over by the sovereign mind of Ras Thavas" [005-013] -- a self-contained world where every activity was directed by a single, calculating intelligence.

### The Nature of the Institution

The medical college was simultaneously hospital, research institute, body bank, and prison. Vad Varo recognized his own ambiguous status from the beginning:

> In these three weeks I had learned much of the strange place in which I was half guest and half prisoner and of my remarkable host-jailer, Ras Thavas, the old surgeon of Toonol, whom I had accompanied almost constantly day after day until gradually there had unfolded before my astounded faculties an understanding of the purposes of the institution over which he ruled and in which he labored practically alone; for the slaves and attendants that served him were but hewers of wood and carriers of water. It was his brain alone and his skill that directed the sometimes beneficent, the sometimes malevolent, but always marvelous activities of his life's work. [002-002]

Ras Thavas himself characterized the institution's moral logic with brutal clarity. When a revived subject protested his captivity, the surgeon dismissed the complaint as irrelevant:

> Ras Thavas made a gesture of impatience. "Nonsense!" he cried. "You are better off here, where you can be preserved in the interests of science." [003-099]

This perspective -- that the institution existed for scientific inquiry, and that all who entered it were properly subordinated to that purpose -- pervaded every aspect of the college's operation. Yet Vad Varo succeeded in establishing his own standing within it, asserting the limits of the surgeon's authority:

> I laid a hand upon the hilt of my long-sword and looked him steadily in the eye. "Ras Thavas," I said, "you are master in your own house; but while I am your guest treat me with courtesy." [003-010]

The administrative machinery was substantial. The office adjoining Ras Thavas's private room housed "a score of clerks arranging and classifying reports from various departments of the great laboratory" [003-068], and even the master surgeon's subordinates were defined by their subordinate relationship to the institution's central organizing principle:

> He was, however, merely a minor cog in the machinery of the great institution presided over by the sovereign mind of Ras Thavas, and as such I had given him little consideration; nor did I now as I bent my steps towards the office. [005-013]

### Layout and Architecture

The facility is a sprawling complex of interconnected buildings. The narrator describes the central tower and its surrounding structures as "the building or series of buildings that composed the whole" [001-024], suggesting that what appears to be a single structure from outside is in fact an aggregation of wings and additions built up over millennia. The central tower, constructed "some twenty-three thousand years ago" by an ancestor of Ras Thavas who was driven from Toonol by the reigning Jeddak, forms the architectural nucleus.

> "This tower," said Ras Thavas, "was built some twenty-three thousand years ago by an ancestor of mine who was driven from Toonol by the reigning Jeddak of the time. Here, and upon other islands, he gathered a considerable following, dominated the surrounding marshes and defended himself successfully for hundreds of years. While my family has been permitted to return to Toonol since, this has been their home; to which, one by one, have been added the various buildings which you see about the tower, each floor of which connects with the adjacent building from the roof to the lowest pits beneath the ground." [006-060]

The tower served as the vertical spine of the entire complex:

> He led me immediately to a lofty tower that rose at the corner of the largest building of the group that comprised his vast establishment. Within was a circular runway which led not only upward, but down as well. This we ascended, passing openings at each floor, until we came at last out upon its lofty summit. [006-048]

The complex is organized vertically across multiple floors connected by inclined runways:

**First Floor -- Surgical Wards and Laboratories**: The narrator entered "a large, brilliantly lighted chamber wherein there burst upon my astounded vision the most gruesome scene that I ever had beheld" [001-013]. "Rows upon rows of tables arranged in parallel lines filled the room and with few exceptions each table bore a similar grisly burden, a partially dismembered or otherwise mutilated human corpse" [001-013]. Each table was equipped with surgical instruments depending from shelves above and a detailed case record hanging from a hook at the head of each table [001-015]. The scale was industrial: "we went from room to room, in each of which were the same gruesome exhibits" [001-015].

Ras Thavas's office, "a small room which adjoined a very large one in which were a score of clerks arranging and classifying reports from various departments of the great laboratory" [003-068], served as the administrative nerve center of the institution.

**Second Floor -- Preserved Bodies**: Up an inclined runway, the second floor held rooms with tables bearing "whole rather than mutilated bodies, all of which were patched in various places with adhesive tape" [001-016] -- subjects who had undergone completed operations and were awaiting revival. One specialized chamber, to which Ras Thavas led Xaxa for the selection of a new body, contained "all the bodies therein were of young women, many of them of great beauty" [001-019].

**Operating Theater**: A smaller, specialized chamber with "several empty tables similar to those upon which the corpses lay in adjoining rooms" [001-020] served as the primary surgical suite, staffed by "two female slaves or attendants" [001-020]. The tables were made of polished *ersite* slabs [001-022].

**Subterranean Vaults**: Beneath the main buildings lay a network of vaults accessed by runways. Vad Varo and Ras Thavas descended through "corridors and passageways" that "indicated long disuse" with "floors covered with an impalpable dust, long undisturbed" [003-073]. Some doorways had been "tightly sealed with masonry" [003-073], hiding secrets even from the institution's junior staff. These vaults housed the long-term storage of bodies, arranged on shelves in rooms where subjects might lie for decades -- Valla Dia's body had been preserved there for ten years [003-002].

**Defensive Architecture**: The Tower of Thavas had windows that were deliberately excluded from the Toonol-facing side. Vad Varo noted that "the fact that there were no windows in the outside walls of the building facing towards Toonol, that I had never before the previous day been upon the roof and that I never had seen a ship sail over the inner court towards the east all tended to explain my ignorance of the fleet and its customary operations" [007-048]. Ras Thavas maintained a small fleet of fliers on the tower's roof, invisible to those within the building, contributing to the narrator's ignorance of their existence for months [006-044]. Access to the tower was via the circular runway that connected all floors, from the roof down to the deepest pits [006-048].

**Private Laboratory**: Ras Thavas maintained a private surgical suite adjoining his sleeping quarters, "a perfectly lighted and appointed laboratory containing two operating tables" [010-014], built specifically for the brain transfer he intended Vad Varo to perform upon him -- transplanting his ancient brain into a young, beautiful body he had selected.

### Scale of Operations

The institution operated on a scale that was simultaneously industrial and deeply personal. Ras Thavas "labored practically alone" -- "the slaves and attendants that served him were but hewers of wood and carriers of water. It was his brain alone and his skill that directed the sometimes beneficent, the sometimes malevolent, but always marvelous activities of his life's work" [002-002]. The administrative machinery was substantial: the office adjoining Ras Thavas's private room housed "a score of clerks arranging and classifying reports from various departments of the great laboratory" [003-068].

The clientele included Barsoom's wealthiest and most powerful figures. Jeddara [[Xaxa]] of Phundahl paid "a considerable sum" in Martian coins for her brain transplantation [001-018]. Her entourage included "a score of women and armed warriors" [001-017], suggesting the facility catered to a ruling-class clientele that traveled with substantial retinues. Yet the quarters that Ras Thavas "furnished his waiting clients were overflowing with wealthy patrons waiting to pour money into his coffers" [002-003], indicating demand exceeded capacity.

The economic model was based on the sale of bodies. Ras Thavas obtained "practically all" of his material through agents who paid relatives "a satisfactory sum for the corpse" [002-012]. The body of a handsome young warrior sold for a sum sufficient to satisfy his family; Ras Thavas then preserved the body until a wealthy client -- "a not overly prepossessing man of considerable years" -- required it, paying "an enormous fee" for the transplantation [002-012].

### The Surgical Method

Ras Thavas employed a meticulous, multi-stage procedure that combined advanced technology with chemical preservation:

1. **Antiseptic Preparation**: Both patient and donor body were sprayed with a full-body antiseptic solution. The surgeon stripped to the waist for the operation [001-022].

2. **Blood-Liquid Exchange**: Two incisions were made in the patient's body. Tubes connected to "an empty glass receptacle" and "a similar receptacle filled with a colorless, transparent liquid resembling clear water" were attached [001-014]. A small motor pumped the blood out while forcing the substitute liquid in, replacing the blood with a preservative-anesthetic that sustained tissue viability during surgery. The blood was stored in "hermetically sealed vessels" on shelves above the operating table [003-013].

3. **Tissue Removal and Transfer**: For brain transplantation, the scalp was removed "following the hair line entirely around her head" [001-022]. A "tiny circular saw attached to the end of a flexible, revolving shaft" cut through the skull following the line exposed by scalp removal [001-022].

4. **Neural Reconnection**: The surgeon transferred the brain and "deftly connected the severed nerves and ganglia" [001-022].

5. **Closure**: The skull and scalp were replaced and secured with "his peculiar adhesive tape, which was not only antiseptic and healing but anesthetic, locally, as well" [001-022].

6. **Revivification**: The stored blood was reheated, "a few drops of some clear chemical solution" were added to "revitalize the latter and restore to normal and healthy activity each and every organ of the body" [002-004], and the blood was returned to the veins via hypodermic injection [001-023].

A single brain transplantation operation took approximately **four hours** [001-022]. During the entire procedure, Ras Thavas "had not spoken a word" [001-024], operating with a silent concentration that reflected his total absorption in the technical challenge.

### Vad Varo's Integration

The narrator's journey from patient to practitioner illustrates the institution's absorptive power. Initially a specimen -- "I offered a problem" [002-004] -- Vad Varo was gradually inducted into the surgical arts. He was first "entrusted with the formula of the 'embalming fluid'" [002-004], then progressed through a systematic curriculum: transferring limbs, then internal organs, then performing complete operations on paying clients [008-001]. He replaced failing kidneys, grafted thyroid glands into a stunted child, transferred two hearts, and finally -- unassisted, with Ras Thavas "standing silently beside me" -- transplanted an entire human brain [008-001].

This apprenticeship had a purpose Vad Varo did not initially understand. Ras Thavas revealed it only when his ancient body began to fail: he intended Vad Varo to transfer his brain into a young, beautiful body he had selected and prepared [010-010]. The training had been, from the beginning, a long-term investment in Ras Thavas's own immortality. The medical college was not merely Ras Thavas's workplace -- it was the instrument by which he intended to live forever.

Vad Varo's counter-offer -- demanding Valla Dia's body in exchange for completing the operation -- transformed the institution's power dynamics in a single moment. The assistant who had been trained to serve the master's immortality used that very training to extract the master's compliance [010-015]. The medical college, designed to concentrate all power in Ras Thavas's hands, had inadvertently created the conditions for his dependency on another human being.
