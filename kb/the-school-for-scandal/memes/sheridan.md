# Richard Brinsley Sheridan

Richard Brinsley Sheridan (1751–1816) was the author of The School for Scandal and one of the most remarkable figures of late eighteenth-century British culture.

## Life and Career

Sheridan was born in Dublin, the son of an actor-manager father and a novelist mother. He came to London as a young man, achieved spectacular success with his first play, The Rivals (1775), and followed it with The School for Scandal (1777) and The Critic (1779) — three masterpieces written before the age of thirty. He then largely abandoned playwriting for politics, serving as a Whig MP and becoming one of the greatest parliamentary orators of his age. His later years were marked by financial difficulty and personal tragedy.

## The Playwright

Sheridan wrote only a handful of plays, but they include two of the greatest comedies in the English language. His gift was for the comedy of wit — brilliant dialogue, ingeniously constructed scenes, and a moral vision that is both sharper and kinder than his Restoration predecessors'. He combined Congreve's verbal brilliance with a warmth of feeling that is distinctively his own.

## The Man of the Theatre

As manager of Drury Lane, Sheridan was a working man of the theatre — not a literary gentleman dabbling in drama. This practical experience is visible in every scene of The School for Scandal: the timing of the entrances, the precision of the comic climaxes, the instinct for what will play before an audience. Sheridan wrote for actors he knew, for a stage he controlled, and for an audience whose taste he understood.
