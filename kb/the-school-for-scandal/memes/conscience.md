# Conscience

Joseph Surface, exposed before [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] in the screen scene, is left with nothing to say. His single spoken word — "conscience" — is perhaps the most famous utterance in The School for Scandal. It is not an argument but an invocation, and its emptiness reveals everything about the man who speaks it.

## In The School for Scandal

The word occurs at the climax of the screen scene, when [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]], exposed before [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]], is left with nothing to say. "Conscience" is his only defence — a word that has no meaning in his mouth because he has spent the entire play proving he has none. The moment is both comic and devastating: the man whose public identity is built on moral refinement can produce only a one-word appeal to a faculty he has systematically ignored.

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]], by contrast, discovers her conscience in the same scene. She speaks of the "consciousness of having deserved it" (004-279) — the language of conscience as self-knowledge rather than self-justification. Her conscience is not a word but an action — she leaves Joseph's rooms, returns to her husband, and renounces the scandalous society. Conscience, for Lady Teazle, is the faculty that enables reform.

[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]'s relationship to conscience is characteristically untheoretical. He never speaks of it, but his actions — selling the family portraits to pay debts, refusing to sell [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s picture — demonstrate a moral instinct that his brother's eloquence cannot counterfeit. Sheridan thus distributes conscience along the same axis as good nature: the character who talks about it least possesses it most.
