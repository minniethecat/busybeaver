# Youth

Youth in The School for Scandal is not merely an age but a moral condition — a period of susceptibility, testing, and potential either for reform or for ruin.

## In The School for Scandal

The play's young characters navigate a social world that simultaneously indulges and judges them. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] "is indeed what a youth should be — everybody in the world speaks well of him" (002-427): the irony, of course, is that his perfect public reputation conceals private vice. Sheridan thus sets up youth as the theatre of [[appearance-vs-reality|appearance]] — the young man who appears virtuous is rotten; the one who appears dissolute, [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]], possesses the "green juices of youth" (002-454) that promise eventual maturity.

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] embodies youth's dangers most vividly. Fresh from the country and married to an older man, she is drawn into the scandalous circle precisely because her youth makes her impressionable. Sir Peter recalls how "you have so good naturally Described" (002-085) her transformation, and she herself recalls being "chuck[ed] under the chin" (003-216) as a young bride. The play traces her trajectory from naive country girl to aspiring woman of fashion and, ultimately, to reformed wife — youth as a period of error from which one may yet recover.

[[maria|Maria]] stands apart: young but steadfast, she resists the school's temptations entirely. Her youth is characterised by moral clarity rather than experimentation, making her the play's normative centre. Sheridan's treatment of youth thus maps onto his larger moral scheme — youth reveals character rather than determining it.
