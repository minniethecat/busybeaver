# Gossip

Gossip is both the subject and the engine of The School for Scandal — a social practice so pervasive that it structures the play's action, characterisation, and moral universe.

## The Machinery of Gossip

Sheridan depicts gossip as a self-sustaining system. In the opening scene, Lady Sneerwell and Snake discuss the mechanics of rumour production: paragraphs are planted in newspapers, stories are fed to servants who tell hairdressers who tell milliners, and within a week a reputation is destroyed (001-001). The system is efficient, anonymous, and almost impossible to trace. Mrs. Candour's role is crucial — she spreads gossip under the guise of defending reputations, a tactic that makes her both more effective and more culpable than overt slanderers.

## Gossip as Social Performance

The scandalous colloquies — scenes in which Crabtree, Backbite, and Mrs. Candour compete to circulate the most damaging intelligence — are structured as performances. Each character vies for the floor, each claims exclusive sources, and each embellishes the story with theatrical flair (002-108). Gossip in Sheridan's world is not merely information exchange but a form of competitive entertainment, a game with rules, stakes, and winners.

## Moral Economy

Sheridan's treatment of gossip is morally precise. The play distinguishes between the active manufacturers (Lady Sneerwell, Snake), the competitive performers (Crabtree, Backbite), and the passive enablers (Mrs. Candour). This taxonomy allows the audience to calibrate blame. The play's resolution — Snake's purchased confession (005-240) — exposes the system's vulnerability: gossip depends on secrecy, and a single honest testimony can unravel years of malicious construction.
