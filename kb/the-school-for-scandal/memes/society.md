# Society

"Society" in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* means something specific: fashionable London, the network of drawing rooms, scandal parties, and public assemblies in which reputation is both currency and target. Sheridan's anatomy of this society is unsparing — he shows it as a system for the production and [[ruin|destruction]] of [[reputation|character]], a mechanism that rewards [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] and punishes honesty. The play's satire is directed not at individuals but at the social conditions that make individuals behave as they do.

## Society as System

### The Drawing Room as Arena

[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s dressing room and drawing room are the play's social headquarters. Here the scandal economy operates — strategy is set, reports are filed, campaigns are launched. The scene in which [[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]], [[sir-benjamin-backbite|Sir Benjamin Backbite]], and [[crabtree|Crabtree]] exchange malicious reports (Act II, Scene 2) is a social ritual as formalised as any court ceremony:

> "I have no satisfaction in this society." (002-313)

The protestation — [[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]] claims to be wearied by the very [[scandal-theme|gossip]] she propagates — is the society's [[reputation|character]]istic hypocrisy. No one in this world admits to enjoying what everyone does. The denial of participation is the condition of participation; scandal requires performers who insist they are not performing.

### Society as Surveillance

Sheridan's London is a panopticon of reputation. Everyone watches everyone; everyone reports on everyone. The chain of [[scandal-theme|gossip]] transmission — "maid to hairdresser to milliner's apprentice to Mrs. Clackit" (001-014–017) — maps a surveillance network that extends from the drawing room to the servant's quarters and back. Society, in this depiction, is a system for making private conduct public, for converting action into [[tale|narrative]], for transforming people into stories about people.

> "Certainly society suffers by it intolerably." (003-363)

The remark, made about [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance, captures the society's self-conception: it is not the individuals who mismanage their affairs who suffer, but "society" itself. The abstraction — "society suffers" — is the move by which individual malice cloaks itself in collective concern.

## Town and Country

The town/country opposition structures the play's social geography. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s "Country Education" (002-038) is what fashionable society has taught her to despise; her transformation from country girl to town lady is the [[tale|story]] society tells about its own civilising power. But Sheridan complicates the [[tale|narrative]]: the country that [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] left was limiting, but the town that remade her is corrupting.

The play's resolution does not send [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] back to the country. She remains in London, reconciled to [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]; the solution is not withdrawal from society but reformation within it. Sheridan's social critique is reformist, not revolutionary: society can be improved, and improvement begins with the exposure of its mechanisms.

## Society and Character

The play's central social argument is that [[reputation|character]] is shaped by the society that judges it. [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] is a hypocrite because society rewards hypocrisy; [[charles-surface|Charles]] is profligate because society permits profligacy; [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] runs a scandal factory because society consumes its products. The "School" of the title is society itself — an institution that teaches its members to perform, to dissemble, and to destroy.

Sheridan's treatment is more sophisticated than simple satire because he acknowledges society's pleasures. The scandal scenes are funny; the wit is genuine; the energy of the social [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]]s is real. The play implicates its audience in the enjoyment of what it critiques — we laugh at the scandal-mongers, but our laughter is itself a form of social pleasure. The School has enrolled us too.
