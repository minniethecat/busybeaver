## Definition

In the world of *The School for Scandal*, the adjective "scandalous" is both accusation and commodity. To call something scandalous is to participate in the very circulation of damaging information that the word condemns. Sheridan's characters deploy the term as a weapon, a currency, and a badge of knowing sophistication — often all at once.

## In The School for Scandal

### The Term as Social Weapon

The word "scandalous" appears throughout the play in contexts that reveal its double function. When Maria protests that "the World says scandalous things of you" (001-191), she uses the word to characterize the accusations made against Charles Surface. But the utterance itself joins the chain of scandalous speech, demonstrating that one cannot even deny a scandal without repeating it.

Mrs. Candour's claim that "such reports are highly scandalous" (001-209) performs the same paradox: her condemnation of scandalous gossip is indistinguishable from the gossip itself. Her method is to repeat the scandalous report in full while affecting to deplore it — a technique that Sheridan presents as the quintessential manoeuvre of polite society.

### Scandalous Society

The play's most explicit social critique comes through Joseph's reference to "a scandalous Society" (002-387) — a phrase that names the very institution the play satirizes. Joseph, the character who most thoroughly embodies the hypocrisy of reputation management, is the one who identifies the problem. The irony is that he uses the phrase to deflect responsibility from himself onto the society he inhabits.

Later, Joseph remarks "I am glad my scandalous Friends keep that up" (004-240), acknowledging that he depends on the scandal-mongering of his associates to maintain his own position. The line reveals that scandalousness is a collaborative enterprise: no single character creates scandal alone; it is the collective activity of "scandalous Friends."

### The Grammar of Scandal

Sheridan is attentive to the linguistic mechanics of scandalous speech. The word "scandalous" in the play functions almost always as an adjective that precedes rather than follows its noun — "scandalous reports," "scandalous Friends," "scandalous Society" — creating a pattern in which the label arrives before the content. This syntactic habit mirrors the social logic of the scandal school: reputation is damaged by the accusation before any evidence can be examined. The adjective does the work of destruction before the noun has even been specified.

### Scandalous after All

Sir Peter's prediction that he "will grow scandalous" (005-735) at the play's conclusion is a comic admission of defeat. The man who began the play lamenting the scandal-mongering of others ends by threatening to join them. Sheridan's point is that scandalousness is contagious — in a society that rewards the circulation of damaging information, even the virtuous are drawn into its orbit. The play's epilogue, spoken by Lady Teazle, extends this warning to the audience: the pleasure we have taken in watching scandal performed on stage implicates us in its economy.

## Key Scenes

In Act II, the exchange between Lady Sneerwell's circle about "scandalous" reports functions as a masterclass in performative indignation. Each character denounces scandalous speech while contributing to it, creating a cascade of contradictions that Sheridan orchestrates for maximum comic effect.

In Act IV, the screen scene — the play's theatrical centrepiece — literalizes the structure of scandalous disclosure. Characters are hidden, overheard, and exposed in a sequence that reproduces, in theatrical form, the very mechanisms of scandalous revelation that the play condemns.

## Historical Context

The word "scandalous" in the eighteenth century carried legal as well as social weight. Ecclesiastical courts could punish "scandalous" behaviour, and "scandalum magnatum" — the spreading of false reports about peers of the realm — remained technically a crime. Sheridan's use of the term draws on this legal residue even as he treats scandal primarily as a social phenomenon, exploring the boundary between actionable defamation and the informal damage of drawing-room gossip.
