# Temper

Temper in *The School for Scandal* is more than a personality trait: it is a social performance, a marital weapon, and an index of moral worth. The word recurs across the play's domestic scenes as Sir Peter and Lady Teazle negotiate — or fail to negotiate — the terms of their coexistence. Sheridan's treatment of temper is unusually psychological for eighteenth-century comedy: he sees it not as a fixed quality of character but as something that can be deployed, withheld, and manipulated.

## Temper as Marital Combat

The Teazles' marriage is a running battle over temper. Sir Peter opens the conflict: "my life may be made unhappy by your Temper — I'll not be ruined by your Extravagance!" (002-013). He frames temper as Lady Teazle's property and his affliction. But Sheridan immediately complicates this: Sir Peter's own temper is as volatile as his wife's. He is, by his own admission, "the sweetest temper'd man alive" who happens to "hate a teasing temper" (001-415) — a self-description whose contradictions the play mercilessly exposes.

## The Performance of Temper

The play's most sophisticated treatment of temper comes when Lady Teazle decides to weaponise composure. Sir Peter, enraged by her extravagance, finds her infuriatingly calm: "She pretends to keep her temper, can't — No she may break my Heart — but she shan't keep her Temper" (003-280). The passage reveals temper as performance: Lady Teazle is not calm by nature but has chosen calm as a tactic, and Sir Peter's fury is partly fury at being denied the satisfaction of a quarrel. Temper, in this reading, is a resource to be managed, not a fate to be endured.

## Temper Across the Social Spectrum

The play distributes temper unevenly across its characters, using it as a marker of social position as much as personality. Sir Peter, a man of rank and property, is permitted displays of ill temper that would be ruinous in a younger or poorer man. His explosions are comic rather than threatening, cushioned by his status as a knight and husband. Charles Surface, by contrast, cannot afford ill temper: as a younger son already beset by creditors, his charm is his only remaining asset, and he maintains it scrupulously. Even when baited by Joseph during Sir Oliver's testing, Charles remains affable — his good temper is not innate virtue but survival strategy. Sheridan's insight is that the licence to lose one's temper is a privilege unevenly distributed: the powerful may rage; the vulnerable must smile.

## Temper and Morality

Sheridan links temper to moral character through the contrast between the Surface brothers. Charles, despite his dissipation, is consistently good-humoured; he meets Sir Oliver's test with wit and warmth. Joseph, the sentimentalist, reveals a vicious temper when exposed — snarls "Sdeath, you Blockhead" (004-333) at his servant. The unmasking scene shows that moral hypocrisy and foul temper are linked: the man who performed benevolence in public is, in private, the man who cannot control his rage.

## Temper and the Comic Resolution

Charles Surface, in a moment of surprising insight, tells Lady Teazle: "you must watch your Temper very narrowly" (003-231). The advice is practical rather than moralistic — temper is something to be managed, not transcended — and it anticipates the play's resolution. The Teazles' reconciliation depends not on either party becoming virtuous but on both parties learning, however imperfectly, to govern their tempers. Sheridan's comedy thus offers a modest Enlightenment optimism: temper, unlike fortune or birth, is within our power to improve.
