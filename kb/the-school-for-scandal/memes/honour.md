# Honour

"Honour" appears in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* as a word perpetually invoked and perpetually strained. Characters appeal to it, question it, and in the play's most famous exchange, propose to leave it out of their moral calculations entirely.

## The Contested Meaning of Honour

The play's [[reputation|character]]s use "honour" in incompatible ways. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] stakes "My life on [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s Honour" (002-443) — a wager he will come to regret. [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]], reproached by [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]], responds with the devastating demurral: "I have too much honor to desire it" (004-323). Her reply — "Don't — you think we may as well leave Honor out of the argument?" (004-324) — is the play's most cynical moment, and perhaps its most honest.

## Honour and Gender

Honour in the play is [[marriage-and-gender|gender]]ed. Male honour is tied to reputation, [[debt|credit]], and public [[reputation|standing]]; female honour is sexual reputation, the "[[reputation|character]]" that scandal can "murder." [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s entire campaign is driven by injury to her "injured [[reputation|Reputation]]" (001-043) — a phrase that collapses the distinction between honour as virtue and honour as social [[reputation|standing]].

## The Honourable Libertine

[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] represents the play's attempt to reconcile honour with libertinism. His refusal to sell his uncle's portrait — despite his debts — is the play's definitive act of honour, performed by the [[reputation|character]] everyone has written off as a wastrel. Sheridan's moral economy values the honour that costs something over the honour that costs nothing but words.
