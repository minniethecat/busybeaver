# Auction

The auction scene in Act IV, Scene I is one of the most celebrated set pieces in English comedy — a scene in which Charles Surface sells his family portraits to a disguised Sir Oliver.

## Comic Structure

The auction is structured as a series of comic portraits — each ancestor described, satirised, and sold with escalating irreverence. Careless serves as auctioneer, Charles as commentator, and Sir Oliver (as 'Mr. Premium') as appalled witness. The comedy derives from the collision of values: Charles treats his ancestors as commodities while simultaneously revealing his genuine affection for the one relative who matters.

## The Moral Turning Point

When the auction reaches Sir Oliver's portrait, Charles refuses to sell it — 'No, hang it, I'll not part with poor Noll' (004-080). This refusal, entirely spontaneous and financially irrational, is the play's moral hinge. It demonstrates that Charles's values, however disordered, are genuine: he will sell anything except what he loves.

## The Auction as Social Critique

The scene also functions as social critique. The portraits represent lineage, tradition, and the eighteenth-century cult of ancestry. Charles's irreverent disposal of them is a form of leveling — a comic assertion that character matters more than pedigree.
