# Lappet

Lappet is Lady Sneerwell's maid, present at her dressing table in the play's opening scene. She is a silent character — she has no lines and takes no part in the action — but her presence is significant. The opening stage direction places Lady Sneerwell 'at her dressing table with LAPPET; MISS VERJUICE drinking chocolate' (001-001). The scene of a lady at her toilette, attended by servants, is a familiar visual trope of eighteenth-century comedy, immediately establishing Lady Sneerwell as a woman of fashion and artifice.

## Dramatic Significance

Lappet's wordless presence establishes the atmosphere of the opening scene: a private, feminine space where the machinery of public scandal is manufactured. The dressing table — site of cosmetic transformation — mirrors the play's theme of appearance and reality. Lady Sneerwell, who transforms reputations as easily as she transforms her face, begins her work with Lappet as silent accomplice.
