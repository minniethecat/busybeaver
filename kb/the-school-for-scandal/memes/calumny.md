## Definition

Calumny is the making of false and defamatory statements intended to damage another's reputation. Unlike casual gossip, calumny implies deliberate malice — the knowing circulation of untruths for the purpose of harm. In the moral vocabulary of *The School for Scandal*, calumny is the darkest form of scandalous speech, distinguished from mere reportage by the element of conscious falsehood.

## In The School for Scandal

### The "Receipt for Avoiding Calumny"

The play's most direct engagement with the concept comes in Joseph Surface's "Receipt for avoiding calumny" (004-317), an ironic formulation that reveals the character's moral bankruptcy. The "receipt" — a recipe or prescription — is never spelled out, but its implication is clear: to avoid calumny, one must never do anything that could be misrepresented. Joseph's advice is to live a life of such calculated propriety that no scandal can attach, a counsel of cowardice disguised as prudence.

### Slander as Revenge

Lady Sneerwell's motivation is explicitly grounded in calumny. She confesses that she was "Wounded myself, in the early part of my Life by the envenomed Tongue of Slander" and has since devoted herself to "reducing others to the Level of my own injured Reputation." Her campaign of calumny is a project of leveling: having been the victim of slander, she becomes its most accomplished practitioner, ensuring that no reputation rises higher than her own damaged one.

### The Gendered Dimension

Maria draws a sharp distinction between male and female slanderers, arguing that "in a man I am sure it is always contemtable" while women have "Pride, envy, Rivalship, and a Thousand motives to depreciate each other." Her analysis — however problematic to modern readers — registers the play's awareness that calumny operates differently across gender lines. The "male-slanderer," she insists, "must have the cowardice of a woman before He can traduce one," yoking calumny to cowardice through a gendered logic that the play's action both illustrates and complicates.

### Sir Peter's Legal Imaginary

Sir Peter's frustrated proposal — "I would have Law Merchant for that too — and in all cases of slander currency, whenever the Drawer of the Lie was not to be found, the injured Party should have a right to come on any of the indorsers" — imagines a legal remedy for calumny modelled on commercial law. The metaphor of "slander currency" — lies circulating like negotiable instruments, with "drawers" and "indorsers" — captures the play's vision of scandal as an economy. In this economy, calumny is the counterfeit coin: it passes as truth but is known by its originators to be worthless.

## Calumny vs. Gossip: A Moral Distinction

The play's moral taxonomy distinguishes carefully between calumny and [[gossip|gossip]]. Gossip, as practiced by [[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]] and her circle, may be destructive but is not necessarily *false*. The scandalous company trades in truth distorted and truth selectively told — but truth nonetheless. Calumny crosses a line: it fabricates where gossip merely circulates.

This distinction maps onto eighteenth-century moral philosophy, which had long debated the ethics of detraction. Aquinas distinguished between *detractio* (revealing true faults) and *calumnia* (inventing false ones), treating the latter as the graver sin. Sheridan's play stages this distinction theatrically: Mrs. Candour's gossip is treated as absurd and hypocritical, but Lady Sneerwell's calumny — her systematic campaign of falsehood — is treated as genuinely dangerous. The difference is the difference between [[reputation|reputation]] damage and reputation annihilation.

The resolution of the play reinforces this hierarchy. The gossipers are not punished but merely exposed to ridicule; their social function, however unpleasant, is not eliminated. But calumny — embodied in the combined schemes of Lady Sneerwell and Joseph Surface — is driven from the stage. The play can tolerate gossip; it cannot tolerate the knowing destruction of the innocent through falsehood.

## Key Scenes

The opening scene of Act I establishes calumny as the play's moral centre. Lady Sneerwell's confession of her motives, Maria's critique of the male slanderer, and the entrance of the scandalous company all converge to delineate a world in which calumny is not an aberration but a social practice — taught, learned, and perfected.

## Historical Context

Eighteenth-century English law recognized slander as actionable, but the remedies were limited and expensive. The ecclesiastical courts handled defamation cases involving spiritual matters, while common law courts dealt with temporal slander. Sheridan, who was both a playwright and a politician, was familiar with the legal machinery of reputation management. "The School for Scandal" dramatizes what the law could not reach: the ambient, unverifiable, and socially devastating circulation of calumny through polite society.
