# Nabob

Sir Oliver Surface's return from the East Indies after sixteen years makes him a 'nabob' — a term loaded with significance in eighteenth-century Britain.

## The Nabob Figure

'Nabob' was the anglicised form of 'nawab,' a Mughal provincial governor. In eighteenth-century British usage, it designated a man who had made a fortune in India through the East India Company and returned to Britain wealthy — and, often, socially suspect. The nabob was a figure of fascination, envy, and anxiety: his wealth was undeniable, but its origins were opaque, and his manners were reputedly coarse.

## Sir Oliver as Nabob

Sheridan's treatment of Sir Oliver plays against the nabob stereotype. Sir Oliver is wealthy from his East India service, but he is the moral centre of the play — the figure who arrives from outside the closed world of London scandal and sees clearly what those within it cannot. His colonial experience, far from corrupting him, has given him the perspective to distinguish true character from false performance.

## Colonial Context

The play's invocation of the East Indies situates it within the broader context of eighteenth-century empire. The wealth that funds the Surface family's estates, the luxuries that Lady Teazle consumes, and the fortunes that young men like Sir Oliver sought abroad are all products of Britain's expanding imperial economy. Sheridan does not interrogate this context directly, but its presence is woven into the fabric of the play.
