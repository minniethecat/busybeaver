# Scandal Sheet

The scandal sheet — the newspaper or periodical devoted to the circulation of damaging personal information — was a feature of eighteenth-century London life, and The School for Scandal anatomises its operations.

## Print Scandal

The eighteenth century saw the rise of periodical print culture — newspapers, magazines, pamphlets — that created new possibilities for the dissemination of scandal. A 'paragraph' in a newspaper could destroy a reputation before breakfast, and its anonymous author was virtually untraceable. Lady Sneerwell's operation, as described in the play's opening scene, is a miniature version of this system: she manufactures the paragraphs, Snake places them, and the print infrastructure distributes them.

## Sheridan and Print

Sheridan knew the world of print scandal from the inside. He was a newspaper proprietor (The Englishman), and his political career exposed him to the full machinery of eighteenth-century press warfare — the libels, the planted stories, the anonymous attacks. The play's satire of scandal-mongering is informed by this experience: it is not merely a literary exercise but a practitioner's critique of the medium.

## The Print Circuit

The play traces the scandal paragraph's journey from the dressing room to the printer's shop to the coffee-house to the drawing room. At each stage, the paragraph acquires authority and sheds accountability. Sheridan's analysis of this circuit is proto-sociological — he understands that scandal is a system, not an event, and that moral responsibility is distributed across its many participants.
