# Marriage and Gender

"When an old Bachelor takes a young Wife, what can he expect?" (001-387). [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s opening question frames the play's examination of marriage as an institution structured by age, gender, and power. Sheridan's comedy anatomises the "matrimonial duel" (002-098) while holding open the possibility of genuine companionship between husbands and wives.

## Overview

The Teazles' marriage is the play's central case study. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s exasperation — "[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] — [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] you — may" (002-002) — and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s defiance — "Authority! no, to be sure" (002-009) — establish the terms of their conflict. He claims authority; she resists it; neither is satisfied.

Sheridan's treatment of marriage is comic rather than polemical, but its implications are radical. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s trajectory — from country innocence through fashionable affectation to recovered [[honour|integrity]] — argues that a wife's virtue is not a fixed quantity but something that can be lost and regained. The play's resolution, in which [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] reconcile as equals, proposes a model of marriage based on mutual recognition rather than male authority.

## Key Scenes

### Act II, Scene 1 — The Matrimonial Duel

The Teazles' domestic quarrel is one of English comedy's great set pieces. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s inventory of fashionable expenses provokes [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s catalogue of his wife's faults; the combat is equal because Sheridan gives both parties good lines. "I'm sure I'm not more extravagant than a Woman of Fashion ought to be" (002-015) — [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s defence is comic and, on its own terms, defensible.

### The Screen Scene (Act IV)

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s near-seduction by [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] and her emergence from [[screen|behind the screen]] transform the marriage plot. "Don't — you think we may as well leave Honor out of the argument?" (004-324) — her most cynical line is spoken before her moral recovery. The screen's fall is also the fall of her fashionable pretensions, enabling the genuine reconciliation of Act V.

## Characters Associated

- **[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]]**: the aging husband; his journey from authority to companionship models the play's marital ideal
- **[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]**: the young wife; her trajectory from defiance through danger to reconciliation is the play's emotional core
- **[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]**: the widow; her manipulation of sexual reputation reveals the gendered stakes of scandal
- **[[maria|Maria]]**: the ingenue; her steadfast preference for [[charles-surface|Charles]] models a marriage based on genuine [[love|affection]]

## Critical Context

Sheridan's treatment of marriage participates in the eighteenth-century debate about companionate marriage. The Teazles' reconciliation enacts the ideal — a marriage based on mutual [[love|affection]] rather than patriarchal authority — while the play's satire of [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s circle exposes the corruption of marriage by the scandal economy.
