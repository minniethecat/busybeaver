# Wit and Comedy

Wit in The School for Scandal is both a social asset and a weapon. Sheridan's comedy of manners uses verbal brilliance as a mechanism of character revelation: what characters say and how they say it exposes their moral natures more reliably than their stated principles. The play distinguishes between true wit — aligned with good nature — and its corrupt forms.

## Wit as Social Performance

The play's social world is organised around the display of wit. [[sir-benjamin-backbite|Sir Benjamin Backbite]] exemplifies this: his "little Productions are mostly Satires and Lampoons" (001-268), and he is introduced as having "a pretty wit — and is a pretty Poet" (001-158). The repetition of "pretty" is significant — wit here is decorative, a social accomplishment rather than a moral faculty. [[maria|Maria]] delivers the play's definitive critique of this mode: "wit loses its respect with me, when I see it in company with malice" (001-159).

The distinction between wit and humour runs through the play. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] observes that "true wit is more nearly allied to good nature than your Ladyship would allow" (002-273), connecting genuine verbal skill to moral character. [[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]] claims the "province of wit or Humour" (002-316) as her territory, though her performances show how wit can mask cruelty.

## Satire, Epigram, and the Weapons of Wit

The play's satirical lances take multiple forms. [[crabtree|Crabtree]] and [[sir-benjamin-backbite|Sir Benjamin]] compete in epigram and lampoon. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s circle uses satirical skill as a screening mechanism — the wit test determines who belongs. But Sheridan also shows the costs: [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] observes that "to smile at the jest which plants a Thorn on another's Breast is to become a principal in the mischief" (001-161).

## True Wit and Good Nature

Sheridan inherits from Addison the distinction between true and false wit, but transforms it into a dramatic principle. True wit in the play is characterised by its alliance with good nature: Charles's jesting about the family portraits proceeds from affection rather than malice, and his wit is convivial rather than destructive. Sir Peter's formulation — "true wit is more nearly allied to good nature" — is the play's theoretical statement; Charles's conduct is its dramatic proof. By contrast, the wit of Lady Sneerwell's circle is exercised at others' expense and ultimately rebounds upon its practitioners. The play's comic justice ensures that those who weaponize wit are themselves exposed to ridicule.

## Key Scenes

### The Scandal College (Act I, Scene 1)

The opening scene establishes wit's role in the scandal economy. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] and company wield epigram and bon mot as instruments of character assassination. [[sir-benjamin-backbite|Sir Benjamin]] has written "the best Epigram" (001-258), which the company treats as a credential. The scene shows wit operating as both passport and weapon.

### The Auction Scene (Act IV)

[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]'s irreverent wit during the portrait auction contrasts with [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s sententious hypocrisy. Charles's jesting — even about his own family — carries a fundamental honesty that Joseph's polished sentiments lack. The scene dramatises the play's central insight: wit aligned with candour is morally superior to sentiment aligned with hypocrisy.

## Critical Context

Sheridan's treatment of wit participates in the eighteenth-century debate about true and false wit — a tradition running from Addison and Steele through Pope. The play's achievement is to dramatise this distinction: it does not merely assert that wit should serve virtue but shows, through the fates of its characters, why malicious wit destroys its practitioners. This aligns Sheridan with the comedy-of-manners tradition while pushing it toward moral seriousness.
