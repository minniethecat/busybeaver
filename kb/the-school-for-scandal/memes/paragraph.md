# Paragraph

The 'paragraph' — a short item in a newspaper or periodical — was the primary vehicle of public scandal in eighteenth-century London, and The School for Scandal opens with an anatomy of its operation.

## The Paragraph Machine

The play's first lines establish the mechanism: Lady Sneerwell asks Snake, 'The Paragraphs you say were all inserted?' He confirms: 'They were Madam — and as I copied them myself in a feigned Hand there can be no suspicion whence they came' (001-001). The paragraph is anonymous, deniable, and devastating — a weapon that wounds without leaving a trace of its wielder.

## Print Culture and Scandal

The eighteenth century saw an explosion of print culture — newspapers, periodicals, pamphlets — that created new possibilities for public scandal. A reputation could be destroyed in print before the victim even knew the attack had been launched. Sheridan, himself a newspaper proprietor (he owned The Englishman), understood the power of print intimately. The play's satire of paragraph-mongering is thus also a satire of the medium in which Sheridan made part of his living.

## The Paragraph's Reach

The play traces the paragraph's trajectory: from Lady Sneerwell's dressing room to the printer's shop to the coffee-house to the drawing room. At each stage, the paragraph gathers authority — 'it must be true, I read it in the papers' — and sheds accountability. Sheridan's point is that the paragraph, like gossip itself, is a system: it requires manufacturers, distributors, and consumers, and moral responsibility attaches to all three.
