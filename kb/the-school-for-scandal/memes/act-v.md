# ACT V

SCENE I.--The Library

[005-001]Enter SURFACE and SERVANT

[005-002]SURFACE. [[sir-oliver-surface|Mr. Stanley]]! and why should you think I would see him?--you

[005-003]must know he came to ask something!

[005-004]SERVANT. Sir--I shouldn't have let him in but that Mr. [[rowley|Rowley]] came to

[005-005]the Door with him.

[005-006]SURFACE. Pshaw!--Blockhead to suppose that I should now be in a [[temper|Temper]]

[005-007]to receive visits from poor Relations!--well why don't you show the

[005-008]Fellow up?

[005-009]SERVANT. I will--Sir--Why, Sir--it was not my Fault that [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]

[005-010]discover'd my Lady----

[005-011]SURFACE. Go, fool!--

[005-012][Exit SERVANT.]

[005-013]Sure [[fortune|Fortune]] never play'd a man of my policy such a Trick before--my

[005-014][[reputation|character]] with Sir Peter!--my Hopes with [[maria|Maria]]!--destroy'd in a

[005-015]moment!--I'm in a rare Humour to listen to other People's Distresses!--I

[005-016]shan't be able to bestow even a benevolent sentiment on Stanley--So!

[005-017]here--He comes and Rowley with him--I MUST try to recover myself, and

[005-018]put a little [[charity|Charity]] into my Face however.----

[005-019][Exit.]

[005-020]Enter SIR OLIVER and ROWLEY

[005-021]SIR OLIVER. What! does He avoid us? that was He--was it not?

[005-022]ROWLEY. It was Sir--but I doubt you are come a little too abruptly--his

[005-023]Nerves are so weak that the sight of a poor Relation may be too much for

[005-024]him--I should have gone first to break you to him.

[005-025]SIR OLIVER. A Plague of his Nerves--yet this is He whom Sir Peter

[005-026]extolls as a Man of the most Benevolent way of thinking!--

[005-027]ROWLEY. As to his way of thinking--I can't pretend to decide[,] for, to

[005-028]do him justice He appears to have as much speculative [[benevolence|Benevolence]] as any

[005-029]private Gentleman in the Kingdom--though he is seldom so sensual as to

[005-030]indulge himself in the exercise of it----

[005-031]SIR OLIVER. Yet [he] has a string of charitable Sentiments I suppose at

[005-032]his Fingers' ends!--

[005-033]ROWLEY. Or, rather at his Tongue's end Sir Oliver; for I believe there

[005-034]is no sentiment he has more faith in than that 'Charity begins at Home.'

[005-035]SIR OLIVER. And his I presume is of that domestic sort which never stirs

[005-036]abroad at all.

[005-037]ROWLEY. I doubt you'll find it so--but He's coming--I mustn't seem to

[005-038]interrupt you--and you know immediately--as you leave him--I come in to

[005-039]announce--your arrival in your real Character.

[005-040]SIR OLIVER. True--and afterwards you'll meet me at Sir Peter's----

[005-041]ROWLEY. Without losing a moment.

[005-042][Exit.]

[005-043]SIR OLIVER. So--I see he has premeditated a Denial by the Complaisance

[005-044]of his Features.

[005-045]Enter SURFACE

[005-046]SURFACE. Sir--I beg you ten thousand Pardons for keeping--you a moment

[005-047]waiting--Mr. Stanley--I presume----

[005-048]SIR OLIVER. At your Service.

[005-049]SURFACE. Sir--I beg you will do me the honour to sit down--I entreat you

[005-050]Sir.

[005-051]SIR OLIVER. Dear Sir there's no occasion--too civil by half!

[005-052]SURFACE. I have not the [[pleasure|Pleasure]] of knowing you, Mr. Stanley--but I am

[005-053]extremely happy to see you look so well--you were nearly related to my

[005-054]mother--I think Mr. Stanley----

[005-055]SIR OLIVER. I was Sir--so nearly that my present Poverty I fear may do

[005-056]discredit to her Wealthy Children--else I should not have presumed to

[005-057]trouble you.--

[005-058]SURFACE. Dear Sir--there needs no apology--He that is in Distress tho' a

[005-059]stranger has a right to claim kindred with the wealthy--I am sure I wish

[005-060]I was of that [[wealth-and-class|class]], and had it in my power to offer you even a small

[005-061]relief.

[005-062]SIR OLIVER. If your Unkle, Sir Oliver were here--I should have a

[005-063]Friend----

[005-064]SURFACE. I wish He was Sir, with all my Heart--you should not want an

[005-065]advocate with him--believe me Sir.

[005-066]SIR OLIVER. I should not need one--my Distresses would recommend

[005-067]me.--but I imagined--his Bounty had enabled you to become the agent of

[005-068]his Charity.

[005-069]SURFACE. My dear Sir--you are strangely misinformed--Sir Oliver is a

[005-070]worthy Man, a worthy man--a very worthy sort of Man--but avarice

[005-071]Mr. Stanley is the vice of age--I will tell you my good Sir in

[005-072]confidence:--what he has done for me has been a mere--nothing[;] tho'

[005-073]People I know have thought otherwise and for my Part I never chose to

[005-074]contradict the Report.

[005-075]SIR OLIVER. What!--has he never

[005-076]transmitted--you--Bullion--Rupees--Pagodas!

[005-077]SURFACE. O Dear Sir--Nothing of the kind--no--no--a few Presents now and

[005-078]then--china, shawls, congo Tea, Avadavats--and indian Crackers--little

[005-079]more, believe me.

[005-080]SIR OLIVER. Here's [[gratitude|Gratitude]] for twelve thousand pounds!--Avadavats and

[005-081]indian Crackers.

[005-082]SURFACE. Then my dear--Sir--you have heard, I doubt not, of the

[005-083]extravagance of my Brother--Sir--there are very few would [[debt|credit]] what I

[005-084]have done for that unfortunate young man.

[005-085]SIR OLIVER. Not I for one!

[005-086]SURFACE. The sums I have lent him! indeed--I have been exceedingly to

[005-087]blame--it was an amiable weakness! however I don't pretend to defend

[005-088]it--and now I feel it doubly culpable--since it has deprived me of the

[005-089]power of serving YOU Mr. Stanley as my Heart directs----

[005-090]SIR OLIVER. Dissembler! Then Sir--you cannot assist me?

[005-091]SURFACE. At Present it grieves me to say I cannot--but whenever I have

[005-092]the ability, you may depend upon hearing from me.

[005-093]SIR OLIVER. I am extremely sorry----

[005-094]SURFACE. Not more than I am believe me--to pity without the [[power|Power]] to

[005-095]relieve is still more painful than to ask and be denied----

[005-096]SIR OLIVER. Kind Sir--your most obedient humble servant.

[005-097]SURFACE. You leave me deeply affected Mr. Stanley--William--be ready to

[005-098]open the door----

[005-099]SIR OLIVER. O, Dear Sir, no ceremony----

[005-100]SURFACE. Your very obedient----

[005-101]SIR OLIVER. Your most obsequious----

[005-102]SURFACE. You may depend on hearing from me whenever I can be of

[005-103]service----

[005-104]SIR OLIVER. Sweet Sir--you are too good----

[005-105]SURFACE. In the mean time I wish you Health and Spirits----

[005-106]SIR OLIVER. Your ever grateful and perpetual humble Servant----

[005-107]SURFACE. Sir--yours as sincerely----

[005-108]SIR OLIVER. [[charles-surface|Charles]]!--you are my [[heir|Heir]].

[005-109][Exit.]

[005-110]SURFACE, solus Soh!--This is one bad effect of a good Character--it

[005-111]invites applications from the unfortunate and there needs no small

[005-112]degree of address to gain the reputation of [[benevolence|Benevolence]] without

[005-113]incurring the expence.--The silver ore of pure Charity is an expensive

[005-114]article in the catalogue of a man's good Qualities--whereas the

[005-115]sentimental French Plate I use instead of it makes just as good a

[005-116]shew--and pays no tax.

[005-117]Enter ROWLEY

[005-118]ROWLEY. Mr. Surface--your Servant: I was apprehensive of interrupting

[005-119]you, tho' my Business demands immediate attention--as this Note will

[005-120]inform you----

[005-121]SURFACE. Always Happy to see Mr. Rowley--how--Oliver--Surface!--My Unkle

[005-122]arrived!

[005-123]ROWLEY. He is indeed--we have just parted--quite well--after a speedy

[005-124]voyage--and impatient to embrace his worthy Nephew.

[005-125]SURFACE. I am astonished!--William[!] stop Mr. Stanley, if He's not

[005-126]gone----

[005-127]ROWLEY. O--He's out of reach--I believe.

[005-128]SURFACE. Why didn't you let me know this when you came in together.--

[005-129]ROWLEY. I thought you had particular--Business--but must be gone to

[005-130]inform your Brother, and appoint him here to meet his Uncle. He will be

[005-131]with you in a quarter of an hour----

[005-132]SURFACE. So he says. Well--I am strangely overjoy'd at his coming--never

[005-133]to be sure was anything so damn'd unlucky!

[005-134]ROWLEY. You will be delighted to see how well He looks.

[005-135]SURFACE. O--I'm rejoiced to hear it--just at this time----

[005-136]ROWLEY. I'll tell him how impatiently you expect him----

[005-137]SURFACE. Do--do--pray--give my best duty and [[love|affection]]--indeed, I cannot

[005-138]express the sensations I feel at the thought of seeing him!--certainly

[005-139]his coming just at this Time is the cruellest piece of ill [[fortune|Fortune]]----

[005-140][Exeunt.]

[005-667]EPILOGUE

[005-668]BY MR. COLMAN

[005-669]SPOKEN BY LADY TEAZLE

[005-670]I, who was late so volatile and gay,

[005-671]Like a trade-wind must now blow all one way,

[005-672]Bend all my cares, my studies, and my vows,

[005-673]To one dull rusty weathercock--my spouse!

[005-674]So wills our virtuous bard--the motley Bayes

[005-675]Of crying epilogues and laughing plays!

[005-676]Old bachelors, who marry smart young wives,

[005-677]Learn from our play to regulate your lives:

[005-678]Each bring his dear to town, all faults upon her--

[005-679]London will prove the very source of honour.

[005-680]Plunged fairly in, like a cold bath it serves,

[005-681]When principles relax, to brace the nerves:

[005-682]Such is my case; and yet I must deplore

[005-683]That the gay dream of [[extravagance|dissipation]]'s o'er.

[005-684]And say, ye fair! was ever lively wife,

[005-685]Born with a genius for the highest life,

[005-686]Like me untimely blasted in her bloom,

[005-687]Like me condemn'd to such a dismal doom?

[005-688]Save money--when I just knew how to waste it!

[005-689]Leave London--just as I began to taste it!

[005-690]Must I then watch the early crowing cock,

[005-691]The melancholy ticking of a clock;

[005-692]In a lone rustic hall for ever pounded,

[005-693]With dogs, cats, rats, and squalling brats surrounded?

[005-694]With humble curate can I now retire,

[005-695](While good Sir Peter boozes with the squire,)

[005-696]And at backgammon mortify my soul,

[005-697]That pants for loo, or flutters at a vole?

[005-698]Seven's the main! Dear sound that must expire,

[005-699]Lost at hot cockles round a Christmas fire;

[005-700]The transient hour of fashion too soon spent,

[005-701]Farewell the tranquil mind, farewell content!

[005-702]Farewell the plumed head, the cushion'd tete,

[005-703]That takes the cushion from its proper seat!

[005-704]That spirit-stirring drum!--card drums I mean,

[005-705]Spadille--odd trick--pam--basto--king and queen!

[005-706]And you, ye knockers, that, with brazen throat,

[005-707]The welcome visitors' approach denote;

[005-708]Farewell all quality of high renown,

[005-709]Pride, pomp, and circumstance of glorious town!

[005-710]Farewell! your revels I partake no more,

[005-711]And Lady Teazle's occupation's o'er!

[005-712]All this I told our bard; he smiled, and said 'twas clear,

[005-713]I ought to play deep tragedy next year.

[005-714]Meanwhile he drew wise morals from his play,

[005-715]And in these solemn periods stalk'd away:--

[005-716]"Bless'd were the fair like you; her faults who stopp'd,

[005-717]And closed her follies when the curtain dropp'd!

[005-718]No more in vice or error to engage,

[005-719]Or play the fool at large on life's great stage."
