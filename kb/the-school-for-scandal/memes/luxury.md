# Luxury

Luxury was one of the most debated concepts in eighteenth-century British society, and The School for Scandal engages with this debate through its treatment of consumption, fashion, and display.

## The Luxury Debate

The eighteenth century saw an unprecedented expansion of consumer goods, fuelled by colonial trade and industrial production. Thinkers from Mandeville to Hume to Smith argued about whether luxury was a vice that corrupted morals or a virtue that stimulated commerce. Sheridan's play takes no abstract position but dramatises luxury's moral ambiguity through its characters.

## Lady Teazle's Transformation

Lady Teazle embodies the lure and the danger of luxury. She arrives from the country 'so very simple' (002-018) and is rapidly transformed by London's fashionable life into a woman who 'can neither bear a Country Journal nor a Parsons' wife.' Her extravagant spending on dress, furniture, and entertainment is Sir Peter's constant complaint — and it is directly linked to her moral peril.

## Charles's Extravagance

Charles Surface's relationship to luxury is more complex. He is extravagantly wasteful — he sells his possessions, borrows from usurers, and lives far beyond his means. But his extravagance is generous rather than selfish. Sheridan thus distinguishes between the luxury of consumption (Lady Teazle, selfish) and the luxury of generosity (Charles, selfless).
