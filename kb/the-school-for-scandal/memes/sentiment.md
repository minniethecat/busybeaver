# Sentiment

The word "sentiment" appears throughout *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* not as casual feeling but as a term of art. The eighteenth-century concept of sensibility — the belief that refined emotion was the mark of virtue — provides both the play's idiom and one of its sharpest satirical targets. Sheridan's treatment of sentiment is diagnostic: he shows how a culture that prizes the display of feeling inevitably rewards those who counterfeit it most convincingly.

## Sentiment as Performance

### The "Man of Sentiment" as Social Type

[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is introduced through the praise of others as "a MAN OF SENTIMENT" (001-090). The capitalisation in the original text is signifi[[hypocrisy|cant]]: "Man of Sentiment" is a recognised social type, a credential as definite as a title or [[fortune]]. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] acidly notes that "with the assistance of his sentiments and hypocrisy" (001-091) [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] has won [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s [[credit|trust]]. Sentiment is not the opposite of [[hypocrisy]] but its instrument — the vocabulary through which the hypocrite performs the virtue he lacks.

The play traces sentiment's career across multiple [[reputation|character]]s and situations:

| Character | Relationship to Sentiment | Function |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------|
| [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] | Performs sentiment | Uses sentimental language to seduce, deceive, and advance |
| [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] | Embodies sentiment | Acts on genuine feeling without performing it |
| [[maria|Maria]] | Distinguishes sentiment | Recognises the difference between [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] and [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s substance |
| [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] | Is deceived by sentiment | Trusts the display of feeling over its absence |
| [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] | Learns about sentiment | Moves from seduction by sentimental [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] to genuine feeling |

## The Critique of Sentimental Culture

### Sheridan's Ambivalence

Sheridan's satire of sentiment is sharper than his contemporaries' because it is more specific. He does not mock feeling — the play's positive [[reputation|character]]s, especially [[maria|Maria]] and the reformed [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]], feel genuinely. What he mocks is the systematisation of feeling: the emergence of a recognised vocabulary, a set of postures, a [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] tradition that makes sentiment reproducible.

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] articulates the problem when he explodes:

> "Yes his sentiments! ha! ha! a hypocritical Villain!" (005-346)

The outburst is comic, but the sequence — "sentiments" followed by "hypocritical Villain" — compresses the play's entire argument. Sentiment has become the language in which villainy operates. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s bitter laugh ("ha! ha!") marks the moment when he recognises how thoroughly he has been deceived by the [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] of refined feeling.

### The French Plate Episode

One of the play's most economical satirical gestures is [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s reference to:

> "the sentimental French Plate I use instead of a service of plate." (005-115)

The "sentimental" plate — decorative rather than functional, French rather than English, associated with [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance rather than [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s prudence — is a material emblem of the play's critique. Sentiment, like French plate, is ornamental; it signals refinement but does no real work. [[charles-surface|Charles]], who has sold his actual plate, keeps the sentimental substitute — and the audience understands that his honesty about the transaction is worth more than [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s preserved respectability.

## Sentiment and Gender

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s trajectory — from country innocence to fashionable affectation and back — traces the corrupting influence of a society that rewards performed sensibility over genuine feeling. She is seduced by [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s sentimental [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] because she has been taught to value it:

> "Don't — you think we may as well leave Honor out of the argument?" (004-324)

The line, half flirtation and half critique, exposes the gap sentiment opens between language and conduct. If honour can be "left out of the argument," then the argument — the sentimental discourse — is revealed as a [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] that has no necessary connection to behaviour.

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s recovery — her confession to [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] and their reconciliation — suggests that genuine sentiment survives the School that perverts it. But Sheridan is careful: her recovery requires withdrawal from the social scenes ([[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s gatherings, the scandal circle) that reward sentiment's [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]]. Authentic feeling, the play implies, may be possible only in private.

## Sentiment and Sheridan's Dramatic Technique

### The Prologue's Warning

The play's Prologue, spoken by "a man of sentiment" in ironic self-description, warns the audience against "sentimental Beaux" — establishing before the first scene that sentiment is a category under investigation. The Prologue's speaker is himself a comic figure, a writer who has failed at sentimental comedy and is trying his hand at satire. His confession — "from my soul I guess I am a [[wit|Wit]]" — is the self-deprecation of a genre that knows its own conventions well enough to subvert them.

### Sentiment as Plot Mechanism

Sheridan uses the gap between performed and genuine sentiment to drive his plot. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s [[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]]s (as Premium and Stanley) are testing mechanisms: they create situations in which the Surface brothers' sentimental [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]]s either hold or collapse. [[charles-surface|Charles]], who performs no sentiment, passes every test; [[joseph-surface|Joseph]], who performs nothing but, fails every one. The plot is the argument.

## Critical Context

Sheridan's treatment of sentiment positions *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* at a turning point in eighteenth-century theatre. The sentimental comedy that dominated the mid-century — plays in which virtue was always rewarded and feeling always genuine — had begun to seem naive. Sheridan's innovation was not to reject sentiment but to test it: to make genuine feeling one possibility among several, and to give the audience tools for distinguishing counterfeit from currency.

The play thus belongs to what critics have called the "anti-sentimental" tradition, alongside Goldsmith's *She Stoops to Conquer*. But Sheridan's anti-sentimentalism is more nuanced than Goldsmith's: he does not dismiss sentiment but investigates it, asking under what conditions feeling can be [[credit|trust]]ed.
