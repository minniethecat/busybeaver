# Opera

The Italian opera was a central institution of fashionable London life in the late eighteenth century, and it features in The School for Scandal as a marker of social status and a scene of display.

## The Opera House

The King's Theatre in the Haymarket was London's principal opera house. It was less a place to listen to music than a place to see and be seen — the boxes were stages for social performance as much as the stage itself was for musical performance. Sheridan's audience would have understood an 'opera box' as a badge of membership in the beau monde.

## Opera in the Play

Lady Teazle's desire for an opera box is one of Sir Peter's grievances — evidence of her determination to participate in the rituals of fashionable display. The opera box represents everything Sir Peter cannot control: his wife's expenditure, her social ambition, her exposure to the gazes and gossip of the town. It is also a site of sexual intrigue — the opera, with its darkened auditorium and private boxes, was notorious as a venue for assignations.

## Display and Morality

Sheridan's treatment of the opera is characteristic of his general approach to fashionable institutions: he does not condemn them outright but uses them as tests of character. The problem is not the opera itself but the uses to which it is put — whether it serves genuine enjoyment or mere display, whether it brings people together or provides cover for deceit.
