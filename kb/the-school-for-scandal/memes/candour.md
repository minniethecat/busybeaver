Candour — the virtue of frank, open, and sincere speech — is one of the most heavily ironised terms in *The School for Scandal*. The play's central joke is that the character named Mrs. Candour is anything but candid: she is the most industrious scandal-monger in the company, spreading rumour under the pretence of defending her friends. Sheridan uses the idea of candour to explore the gap between the language of virtue and the practice of vice, a gap that defines the moral world of the play.

## In The School for Scandal

### Mrs. Candour: The Ironic Namesake

Mrs. Candour is introduced as "a little talkative" but well-meaning (001-179), yet every one of her speeches exposes the hypocrisy of pretended benevolence. Her trademark is to defend a reputation while simultaneously destroying it — a technique Joseph Surface admiringly calls "malicious candour" (001-186). When she protests "I cannot bear to hear People attacked behind their backs," she immediately proceeds to attack everyone behind their backs, making her the embodiment of the play's insight that scandal wears the mask of virtue.

The phrase "in such Danger as when Candour undertakes their Defence" (001-186) encapsulates the play's moral logic. Candour, properly understood, should protect reputation; Mrs. Candour's version destroys it. The irony is structural: the character whose name promises truthfulness is the one who most effectively circulates falsehood.

### Candour and the Sentimental Tradition

The concept of candour had special resonance in eighteenth-century sentimental culture, where it was associated with transparency of heart and the natural goodness of uncorrupted feeling. Sheridan's play both invokes and subverts this tradition. Characters like [[maria|Maria]] and [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] embody something closer to genuine candour — Maria's direct speech, Charles's unguarded frankness — but the play is too knowing to treat candour as an uncomplicated virtue. In the world of the scandalous company, candour is either naive or performative, and the play distinguishes sharply between the two.

## Historical Context

In eighteenth-century moral discourse, candour was a key term in debates about sincerity and politeness. Addison and Steele's *Spectator* regularly praised candour as the mark of a gentleman, while moralists like Shaftesbury linked it to innate moral sense. Sheridan's treatment is characteristic of his comic method: he takes a fashionable moral term and exposes the self-serving uses to which it is put. Mrs. Candour is not just a hypocrite — she is a satire on the entire sentimental culture that made candour a badge of moral superiority while changing nothing about how people actually spoke of one another.
