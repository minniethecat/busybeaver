# Fashion

Fashion in Sheridan's *The School for Scandal* functions as a social currency and an instrument of moral critique. It is less about clothing than about belonging: to be "in the Fashion" is to participate in the rituals of London's beau monde — conspicuous consumption, gossip, and the performance of sophistication. To be "out of the Fashion," as Sir Peter Teazle would have his wife, is social death.

## Fashion as Social Conformity

Lady Teazle, the country girl transformed into a woman of town, articulates the imperative most plainly: "would you have me be out of the Fashion?" (002-071). The question is rhetorical — for her, fashion is not an option but an obligation, the price of admission to the circle [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] presides over. Sir Peter's retort — "The Fashion indeed! — what had you to do with the Fashion before you married me?" (002-071) — reveals the generational and gendered fault line: fashion is what the young and ambitious pursue and what their elders decry as ruinous novelty.

The play's critique extends beyond individual vanity. Fashion is shown to be a system of coercion. Lady Teazle was not born to it; she "plays her Part in all the extravagant Fopperies of the Fashion and the Town, with as ready a Grace as if she had never seen a Bush nor a grass Plot out of Grosvenor-Square" (001-396). This performance of urban sophistication is not natural but learned — and, Sheridan implies, corrosive to the simpler virtues of the countryside.

The pressure toward conformity is reinforced at the level of language. When Lady Teazle asks "would you have me be out of the Fashion?" the phrase connotes not only being unfashionable but being excluded — cast out — from the social world that gives her identity meaning. Fashion, in this sense, is a membership test, and failure to pass it is ostracism.

## Fashion and Consumption

Sir Peter frames his wife's extravagance as a personal failing, but the play makes clear that fashion is inseparable from expenditure. Lady Teazle defends her spending by the standard of her peers: "I'm sure I'm not more extravagant than a woman of Fashion ought to be" (002-016). The circular logic is telling — extravagance is defined by fashion, and fashion demands extravagance. Sir Peter, who "made you woman of Fashion of Fortune of Rank" (002-058), discovers that he has created a monster he cannot control: the social identity he bestowed now dictates behaviour that exceeds his means and his patience.

The economic dimension of fashion locates the play squarely within eighteenth-century debates about luxury. Sir Peter's anxiety about his wife's spending is not merely personal pique but reflects a broader cultural unease about the relationship between commerce, consumption, and female virtue. Lady Teazle's extravagance is, from one angle, simply participation in a market economy that measures status by display; from another, it is a betrayal of the domestic thrift that the country wife was supposed to embody.

## Fashion and Morality

[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]], the play's sentimental hypocrite, deploys fashion as a seduction strategy. When Lady Teazle observes that "one must not be out of the Fashion" (002-071) in the context of accommodating a lover, the line exposes fashion's complicity with vice. What begins as a code of dress and manners bleeds into a code of conduct that can excuse almost anything — provided it is done with [[taste]].

Sheridan's treatment of fashion thus participates in a long literary tradition that associates sartorial display with moral laxity, but with a specifically comic inflection. Unlike the tragic overtones that fashion-as-corruption carries in earlier drama, Sheridan treats it as a folly that is reversible. Lady Teazle can, and does, learn to see fashion's limits; the play's comic form guarantees that her education does not require her destruction.

## Dramatic Function

Fashion serves Sheridan as a synecdoche for the larger social machinery of the [[scandal-theme|scandal]] school. It is one of several currencies — alongside [[reputation]], [[fortune]], and [[wit]] — that characters trade, hoard, and weaponise. Lady Teazle's eventual recognition that fashion's demands nearly cost her her marriage and her [[honour]] is part of the play's comic resolution: the country values she abandoned prove, in the end, more durable than the town fashions she adopted.

> The transient hour of fashion too soon spent (005-700) — the play's epilogue registers fashion's transience, a verdict the comedy itself enacts.

## Fashion and Gender

The play's treatment of fashion is sharply gendered. Fashion is what wives pursue and husbands pay for, and the resulting conflict drives much of the Teazle subplot. Sir Peter's complaint — "a husband is to have no influence, no authority" (002-007) — is linked directly to his inability to control his wife's fashionable expenditure. Yet Sheridan does not make Lady Teazle a simple spendthrift: her engagement with fashion is a form of agency, one of the few routes to social power available to a married woman in her position. The play's resolution does not require her to renounce fashion entirely, only to subordinate it to marital loyalty — a compromise that acknowledges fashion's legitimate place in the social world while insisting on its limits.
