# Fortune

"Fortune" in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* operates on two registers simultaneously: the financial (inherited wealth) and the providential (luck, fate). Sheridan's plot exploits the tension between them — [[reputation|character]]s who possess the first kind of [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] are tested by the second, and the play's moral economy rewards those who prove deserving of both. The concept is central to the play's [[wealth-and-class|analysis of wealth and class]] and to its resolution.

## The Double Meaning

### Wealth and Luck

The double register of "[[wealth-and-class|fortune]]" is not Sheridan's invention — it is embedded in eighteenth-century English usage — but he exploits it with unusual precision. When [[reputation|character]]s speak of "[[wealth-and-class|fortune]]," the context determines which meaning is dominant:

| Occurrence | Dominant Register | Significance |
|------------|-------------------|-------------|
| [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s "[[wealth-and-class|fortune]]" from India | Wealth | Colonial commerce as source of English gentry wealth |
| [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s "misfortunes" | Luck | Framing financial failure as accident, not [[reputation|character]] flaw |
| [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s "no great jointure" | Wealth | Female economic dependency |
| [[maria|Maria]]'s inheritance | Wealth | Marriage as transfer of property |
| "Fortune" rewards [[charles-surface|Charles]] | Both | The pun resolves the plot |

The play's resolution — [[charles-surface|Charles]] inherits, [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] is disinherited — depends on the audience accepting that [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] (luck) should determine [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] (wealth), and that the mechanism of determination is moral testing.

## Fortune as Inheritance

### The East Indian Connection

[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]'s return from the East Indies introduces colonial [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] into the plot. He has been abroad for "sixteen years" (003-007), accumulating wealth that will now be distributed according to desert. The colonial dimension is never examined — Sheridan's interests are domestic — but it is present: the [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] that will determine the Surface brothers' fates originates in empire.

The question the play poses is whether [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] (wealth) should follow [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] (birth) or virtue. [[charles-surface|Charles]], the younger brother, would inherit last under [[prudence|primogeniture]]; [[joseph-surface|Joseph]], the elder, is the legal heir. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s testing — the Premium [[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]], the Stanley appeal — is designed to discover whether the legal distribution of [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] should be overturned by moral desert.

### The Testing of Heirs

[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s investigation of his nephews is structured as a series of moral tests that invert conventional expectations:

| Test | [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s Response | [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s Response | Verdict |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Premium (moneylender) | Refuses to lend to "Stanley" | Sells [[portrait|family portrait]]s | [[charles-surface|Charles]] generous, [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] stingy |
| Stanley (poor relation) | Claims to have given already | Gives what he can | [[charles-surface|Charles]] charitable, [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] lying |
| The portrait | Owns uncle's portrait | Refuses to sell it | [[charles-surface|Charles]] loyal, [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] merely propertied |

The tests make the same point three ways: [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] should follow [[reputation|character]], not birth. [[charles-surface|Charles]], who has squandered his inheritance, proves worthy of more; [[joseph-surface|Joseph]], who has preserved his, proves worthy of none.

## Misfortune as Moral Test

The play repeatedly frames financial disaster as "misfortune" rather than fault. [[charles-surface|Charles]] is described as the victim of "a series of undeserved misfortunes" (003-007), a formulation that separates his [[reputation|character]] from his circumstances. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]], too, is "sorry for your other Mis[[wealth-and-class|fortune]]" (005-289) — the language of sympathy displacing the language of blame.

> "Here's Success to the unfortunate." (003-449)

[[charles-surface|Charles]]'s toast — "Success to the unfortunate" — captures the play's moral logic in a phrase. It is a paradox: success to those who have not succeeded, [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] to those who lack it. The toast is the play's blessing on its own comic resolution, in which the "unfortunate" ([[charles-surface|Charles]]) receives everything and the fortunate ([[joseph-surface|Joseph]]) loses it.

### Sheridan's Consistent Usage

Sheridan's consistent deployment of "misfortune" for [[ruin|financial ruin]] — rather than "folly," "vice," or "extravagance" — is a moral choice. It suggests a worldview in which luck, not virtue, determines who falls and who rises. This is not the moralism of sentimental comedy (in which virtue is always rewarded) but something closer to the providential logic of [[love|romance]]: [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] is distributed by a mechanism that human agents cannot control but that audiences can recognise as just.

## Fortune and Gender

[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s position as a "young Widow with no great jointure" (001-063) illustrates how [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] shapes female agency in the play's world. [[wit|Wit]]hout independent [[wealth-and-class|fortune]], women must manoeuvre through [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]], inheritance, or — in [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s case — the manipulation of [[reputation]] itself. Fortune, in Sheridan's satire, is not merely money but the enabling condition of autonomy.

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] to [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] was a [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]]: "made me the happiest of men — and the most miserable" (001-388). The country girl with no [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] married the older man with one. The play is careful not to condemn this — it is simply how [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] works in its world — but it registers the asymmetry. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s extravagance is, among other things, an assertion of agency in a [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] that was never between equals.

[[maria|Maria]]'s inheritance — controlled by [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] as guardian — is the [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] around which much of the plot turns. [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] pursues her for it; [[charles-surface|Charles]] loves her despite it; [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] blocks their [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] to control it. [[maria|Maria]], the least active of the play's young [[reputation|character]]s, is the most fought-over because her [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] is the largest.

## Fortune and the Comic Resolution

The play's resolution is a redistribution of [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] according to desert. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] awards his inheritance to [[charles-surface|Charles]]; [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] is reconciled to [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]; [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] is exposed; [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] retreats. The comic pattern — [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] restored to its proper holders — is conservative in form but radical in implication: the proper holders are not those who hold by law but those who hold by virtue.

Sheridan's ambiguity about this resolution is legible in the play's final lines. "For even [[scandal|Scandal]] dies if you approve" (005-666) — the epilogue's conditional ("if") reminds the audience that comic resolutions are fragile. Fortune can be redistributed tonight; it will need to be redistributed again tomorrow.
