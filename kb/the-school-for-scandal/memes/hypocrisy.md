# Hypocrisy

Sheridan's title announces his theme: *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* is an institution that teaches hypocrisy as its core curriculum. The play anatomises the distance between public profession and private conduct, finding it not merely in its obvious villain but diffused throughout society.

## Joseph Surface: The Embodiment of Cant

[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is the play's most concentrated study in hypocrisy. His name itself signals the theme — "Surface" denoting the gap between appearance and reality. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] observes that through "the assistance of his sentiments and hypocrisy he has brought [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] entirely in his interests" (001-092). The word "assistance" is crucial: hypocrisy is [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s tool, deliberately wielded.

## The Social Functions of Hypocrisy

Hypocrisy in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* is not merely individual vice but social lubricant. The scandal-mongers of [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s circle all maintain facades of reluctant participation while actively feeding the [[scandal-theme|rumour]] mill. The play suggests that a society organised around reputation necessarily produces hypocrisy, because the cost of perfect candour is social death.

## The Libertine Alternative

[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] offers the play's counterpoint to hypocrisy: a libertinism that is at least honest about its vices. Sheridan's moral economy values authenticity over reputation — a position as radical in his time as in ours. As the play's design shows, the aim is "at least to expose Hypocrisy" (005-368) rather than to preach virtue.
