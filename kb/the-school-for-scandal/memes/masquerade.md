# Masquerade

Sir Oliver Surface's masquerade — his adoption of two disguises to test his nephews — is the central dramatic device of The School for Scandal.

## The Two Disguises

Sir Oliver adopts two personae: 'Mr. Premium,' a moneylender, to test Charles; and 'Mr. Stanley,' a poor relation, to test Joseph. The disguises are chosen to probe different moral qualities. Premium tests Charles's integrity in commerce — will he defraud a moneylender? Stanley tests Joseph's charity — will he help a relation in distress? The answers define the brothers' characters.

## The Masquerade Tradition

Disguise and mistaken identity are ancient comic devices, and Sheridan draws on a rich theatrical tradition. The novelty of his treatment lies in the moral purpose of the disguises: Sir Oliver is not trying to deceive but to discover. His masquerade is an instrument of truth, not of trickery, and this aligns him with the audience, who also want to see through appearances to the reality beneath.

## The Revelatory Moment

When Sir Oliver throws off his disguises in Act V (005-200), the revelation is both dramatic and moral. The mask removed reveals not just the uncle but the truth: Charles has been vindicated, and Joseph has been condemned. The masquerade, having served its purpose, dissolves.
