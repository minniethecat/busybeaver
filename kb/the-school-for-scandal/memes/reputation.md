# Reputation

In *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]*, reputation functions as both the central stake and the primary weapon of social combat. Characters are not merely [[scandal-theme|gossip]]ed about — they are financially and socially "Bankrupt in [[fortune|Fortune]] and Reputation" (001-079), a pairing that reveals how thoroughly Sheridan's London equated the two forms of capital.

## Reputation as Currency

[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s opening justification for her schemes rests on injury to her "own injured Reputation" (001-043). She frames her [[scandal-theme|scandal-mongering]] not as malice but as retaliation — a cycle of reputation-attack and counter-attack that no one can escape. The metaphor of [[ruin|bankruptcy]] applied to reputation recurs throughout the play, suggesting that in [[society|fashionable society]], one's [[honour|good name]] is a ledger subject to the same debits and [[debt|credit]]s as a merchant's account.

## The Fragility of Character

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] embodies the dilemma of the honest man in a dishonest world. He is "remarkably tenacious of reputation" (002-098) yet surrounded by those who "murder characters to kill time" (002-392). The verb "murder" is not accidental — Sheridan repeatedly casts the [[ruin|destruction]] of reputation as a form of social death, as fatal as [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s declaration that "a [[tale|Tale]] of [[scandal|Scandal]] is as fatal to the Reputation of a Woman as a Pistol is to the Life of a Man" (001-293).

## Virtue and Appearance

The Surface brothers represent opposing attitudes toward reputation. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] cultivates the appearance of "[[prudence|Prudence]] — good sense and [[benevolence|Benevolence]]" (001-088) without the substance, while [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] dissipates his reputation through extravagance yet retains genuine virtue. The play's resolution — [[charles-surface|Charles]] inheriting [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s approval despite his "wild" reputation — argues that authentic character ultimately survives the machinery of scandal.
