# Dissipation

Dissipation — the wasteful expenditure of time, money, and moral energy — is the charge most frequently levelled against [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]. Sheridan uses it to test the distinction between a man's habits and his character.

## In The School for Scandal

Charles's dissipation is established before he appears on stage. He is described as a young man "in the midst of Folly and dissipation" (003-033), and the report shapes every character's judgment of him. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] condemns him; [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] exploits his reputation; [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]] arrives from the East Indies determined to test him. Dissipation, as a concept, functions as a screen — it obscures Charles's virtues as effectively as [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s hypocrisy conceals his vices.

The play's central structural irony is that Charles's dissipation proves to be superficial. He drinks, gambles, and spends recklessly, but when tested — by his uncle in disguise, by financial pressure, by the demand to sell the family portraits — he reveals a moral instinct that his respectable brother entirely lacks. The dissipation is real but not essential; it is the crust on a sound character, not the character itself.

Sheridan's treatment of dissipation participates in a larger eighteenth-century debate about luxury and virtue. The "gay dream of dissipation's o'er" (005-683) at the play's conclusion signals not Charles's reformation — he is never wholly reformed — but his integration into a social order that can absorb his excess without being destroyed by it.

## Historical Context

The discourse of dissipation — wasteful expenditure, moral laxity, the squandering of time and substance — was central to eighteenth-century debates about luxury, commerce, and virtue. Mandeville's provocative *Fable of the Bees* (1714) had argued that private vices produced public benefits, including the economic stimulus provided by extravagant consumption. More conventional moralists, from Addison and Steele's *Spectator* to Johnson's *Rambler*, warned that dissipation eroded both individual character and national virtue.

The figure of the dissipated young man — the rake, the spendthrift, the man of pleasure — was a staple of eighteenth-century comedy, and audiences knew the type well. What made Sheridan's treatment distinctive was his refusal to equate dissipation with villainy. Charles Surface is genuinely dissipated: he drinks, gambles, and squanders money he does not have. Yet his dissipation is presented as the excess of a generous nature rather than the expression of a corrupt one. Sheridan's argument, radical for its time, is that dissipation is not a reliable index of character — that a man may be improvident without being wicked, and that the sober hypocrite is a greater social danger than the drunken spendthrift. This argument reverses the moral calculus of a century's worth of conduct literature and sermonising, and it accounts for much of the play's enduring subversive energy.
