# Restoration Comedy

Restoration comedy — the brilliant, cynical, sexually frank comedies produced in the decades following the restoration of Charles II in 1660 — is the direct ancestor of The School for Scandal.

## The Tradition

Playwrights like George Etherege, William Wycherley, and William Congreve created a theatre of wit: dazzling dialogue, intricate plots of sexual pursuit, and a moral universe in which intelligence was the supreme virtue. Their heroes were rakes, their heroines were witty, and their fools were those who lacked the sophistication to navigate the comedy's verbal and social demands.

## Sheridan's Inheritance

Sheridan inherited the Restoration tradition's verbal brilliance and structural ingenuity but softened its cynicism. Where Restoration comedy often celebrates libertinism, Sheridan insists on genuine virtue. Charles Surface is a rake, but he is a rake with a heart — a figure impossible in Wycherley's world. Sheridan's innovation is to use Restoration wit in the service of a warmer, more generous moral vision.

## The Antidote to Sentiment

Sheridan saw Restoration comedy as an antidote to the sentimentality that dominated mid-eighteenth-century theatre. His prologue (written by Garrick) explicitly invokes the Restoration tradition as a model for 'laughing comedy' against the weepy excesses of sentimental drama. The School for Scandal is thus both a revival and a revision of Restoration comedy — a play that restores wit to the stage while refining the moral sensibility it serves.
