# Country

The 'country' in The School for Scandal functions as a moral reference point — the place of origin from which characters have come and against which their London behaviour is measured.

## Lady Teazle's Country Origins

Lady Teazle's transformation from a country girl into a fashionable London wife is the play's most explicit treatment of the town-country opposition. Sir Peter recalls that she was 'the very Picture of rustic Innocence' when he married her (002-018) — a recollection that measures the distance she has travelled. The country, in this formulation, represents authenticity, simplicity, and moral clarity; the town represents artifice, complexity, and moral danger.

## The Country Estate

The Surface family's country estate, though never seen on stage, is a constant presence in the play's moral economy. It represents the traditional values — lineage, land, stability — that both brothers, in their different ways, have betrayed. Charles has squandered its income; Joseph has abandoned its moral responsibilities for the pursuit of town advantage.

## Sheridan's Ambivalence

Sheridan's treatment of the town-country opposition is more nuanced than it first appears. The country is not simply good (Lady Teazle was 'simple' there in both senses — innocent and ignorant), and the town is not simply evil (it is in town that the moral tests are conducted and truth is revealed). The play's resolution does not send anyone back to the country but restores moral order within the town itself.
