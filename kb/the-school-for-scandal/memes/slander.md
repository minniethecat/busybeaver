# Slander

Sheridan distinguishes [[scandal|slander]] from the general [[scandal-theme|gossip]] of the scandal-mongers: [[scandal|slander]] is [[scandal-theme|gossip]] with intent to destroy. The play anatomises its mechanisms — who originates it, how it circulates, and whom it damages — with a precision that reflects the eighteenth century's growing legal and cultural concern with [[scandal-theme|defamation]].

## The Slander Economy

The play presents [[scandal|slander]] as a form of currency. "Cases of [[scandal|slander]] currency" (002-294) are traded among the fashionable, depreciating or appreciating according to the status of their target. The "male-[[scandal|slander]]er" (001-170) and his female counterpart operate a distributed network — [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] at its centre, [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] as its secret beneficiary, [[sir-benjamin-backbite|Sir Benjamin Backbite]] as its comic vehicle. Sheridan's metaphor of "currency" is exact: [[scandal|slander]] circulates, creates value (for its traders), and destroys it (for its objects).

## The Gendered Target

Female reputation bears the heaviest cost. "A [[scandal|slander]]ous [[tale|story]] is believed against" a woman (004-278) with a readiness that reflects the period's double standard. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s entire campaign originates in injury to her own "injured [[reputation|Reputation]]" (001-043) — she has been [[scandal|slander]]ed, and her response is to become a [[scandal|slander]]er. The play thus traces a cycle: [[scandal|slander]] creates [[scandal|slander]]ers, and the "School" perpetuates itself.

## Slander versus Truth

The play's resolution is underwritten by [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s preference for direct investigation over second-hand report. Where the scandal-mongers trade in [[scandal|slander]], [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] tests [[reputation|character]] through [[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]] and observation. The comic ending — [[scandal|slander]] exposed, virtue recognised — argues that [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]] can survive [[scandal|slander]], but only when someone is willing to look beneath the [[appearance|surface]].
