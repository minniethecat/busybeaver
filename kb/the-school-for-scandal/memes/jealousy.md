# Jealousy

Jealousy in The School for Scandal is the acid that corrodes the marriage bond. It is not a private emotion but a public performance — one that feeds the scandal economy and isolates the jealous partner from the very person whose fidelity he most needs to trust.

## In The School for Scandal

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]]'s jealousy of [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] is the central emotional dynamic of the Teazle marriage plot. His suspicion is "increased by a jealousy of him with Lady Teazle" (002-386), referring to [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] — the very man Sir Peter trusts as his moral confidant. Sheridan's irony is devastating: the husband's jealousy attaches to the wrong man, while the right man — Joseph — is the one he invites into his home as an ally.

Lady Teazle perceives the self-fulfilling nature of jealousy with a clarity her husband lacks. She observes that "the best way of curing his jealousy is to give him reason for't" (004-290) — a line that is both a threat and a diagnosis. Jealousy, she understands, creates the conditions for its own justification. A wife who is constantly accused of infidelity may eventually ask herself why she should remain faithful to a husband who already believes her faithless.

Sheridan resolves the jealousy plot not through proof of innocence but through the exposure of a greater guilt. When [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s treachery is revealed in the screen scene, Sir Peter's jealousy evaporates — not because he has been reassured of his wife's fidelity, but because the real threat has been named and neutralised. The cure for jealousy, Sheridan implies, is not surveillance but truth.
