# Sentiment and Sensibility

"Sentiment" in The School for Scandal is a keyword of the eighteenth-century culture of feeling — and Sheridan treats it as profoundly ambiguous. The play asks whether fine feelings are evidence of virtue or merely its most effective disguise. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is introduced as "a MAN OF SENTIMENT" (001-090), and the capital letters signal the term's cultural prestige. By the play's end, the phrase has become an accusation.

## The Man of Sentiment

The figure of the "Man of Sentiment" — cultivated, emotionally articulate, publicly benevolent — was an eighteenth-century ideal, popularised by the novels of Richardson and Mackenzie. Sheridan's critique operates through character contrast. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] "has a string of charitable Sentiments" (005-031) at his fingertips, but his actions consistently contradict his professions. He "acts up to the Sentiments he professes," [[rowley|Rowley]] notes — but only in public (005-323).

The contrast with [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] is structural. Charles lacks the vocabulary of sentiment — he is blunt, irreverent, and openly "extravagant" — yet his actions reveal a genuine generosity that Joseph's fine language never produces. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s testing of the brothers is, in effect, a test of whether sentiment correlates with virtue. The answer is decisive: it does not.

## Sensibility and Its Discontents

The play engages with the cult of sensibility — the belief that refined feeling is itself a moral faculty. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s circle uses the language of "sensibility and discernment" (001-109) as social credential. But Sheridan systematically exposes its vulnerability to manipulation. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s "sentiments" are revealed as "a hypocritical Villain!" (005-346) — the two terms, sentiment and hypocrisy, collide in the play's climactic judgment.

## Sheridan and the Anti-Sentimental Tradition

Sheridan's play occupies a specific position in theatrical history: it is both an anti-sentimental comedy and a work that preserves sentiment's moral claims. Unlike Goldsmith's more thoroughgoing rejection of sentimental comedy, Sheridan allows that genuine feeling exists — it is simply not reliably signalled by its performance. The play's resolution, in which Lady Teazle invites Sir Peter to "speak your sentiments" (005-561), reclaims the language of feeling for sincerity. Sheridan's achievement is to have exposed false sentiment without becoming cynical about feeling itself — a balance that distinguishes The School for Scandal from both the sentimental comedies it satirises and the harder-edged satires that preceded it.

## Key Scenes

### The Sentimental Soliloquy (Act IV)

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] catches [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] in a moment of apparent "Sentiment in soliloquy" (004-232). The scene is metatheatrical — it shows sentiment as performance, a role one adopts when believing oneself unobserved. Joseph's slip from sentiment to seduction, within moments, exposes the machinery: fine feelings are the prelude to predation.

### The Reconciliation (Act V)

The final scene offers a counter-model. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s "speak your sentiments" (005-561) is not a test but a genuine invitation — sentiment restored to its proper function as honest self-expression. The play's resolution suggests that true feeling exists, but only when it has survived the school of scandal's exposure.

## Critical Context

Sheridan's treatment of sentiment participates in the late eighteenth-century critique of sensibility — a line that runs from Sterne's ironic sentimentalism through Austen's more systematic scepticism. The School for Scandal (1777) arrives at a pivotal moment: the cult of sensibility is still culturally dominant but increasingly suspect. Sheridan's theatrical genius is to make the exposure of false sentiment into comedy, turning a philosophical problem into dramatic action.
