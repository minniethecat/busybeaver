# Charles Surface

"'Fore Heaven, 'tis true!" (003-358) — Charles Surface's first line is a confession of his own extravagance, delivered with the candour that will ultimately save him. Sheridan constructs Charles as the play's most radical argument: that an honest libertine is morally superior to a hypocritical saint.

## Role in the Play

Charles is the play's unlikely hero — the younger Surface brother whose "Dissipation and extravagance exceed anything" (001-118) yet whose heart remains uncorrupted by the society that celebrates his brother's counterfeit virtue. His dramatic function is to invert the play's apparent moral premises: every charge against Charles (extravagance, [[prudence|imprudence]], bad reputation) turns out, under [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s testing, to be evidence of his fundamental decency.

The structural brilliance of Charles's role is that he never knows he is being tested. Unlike Joseph, who performs virtue for an audience, Charles acts according to his nature regardless of who is watching. When Sir Oliver, disguised as the moneylender Premium, offers to buy the family portraits, Charles's refusal to sell his uncle's picture is not a calculated performance — it is an involuntary revelation of loyalty. Sheridan's argument is that virtue, properly understood, is not what we display but what we cannot help revealing.

## The Reformed Libertine Archetype

Charles belongs to a recognizable eighteenth-century type: the reformed libertine, whose vices are acknowledged but whose fundamental goodness is vindicated. The type descends from Restoration comedy — Etherege's Dorimant, Congreve's Mirabell — but Sheridan gives it a distinctive moral inflection. Where Restoration libertines are rewarded for wit and punished for nothing, Charles must earn his reward through moral testing. His extravagance is real, his debts are real, and the play does not pretend otherwise. What it argues is that generosity of spirit matters more than prudence of conduct.

This is a radical claim in the context of eighteenth-century moral discourse, which consistently privileged prudence, economy, and self-discipline. Charles embodies the counter-argument: that the man who gives freely of what he has — even if he gives too freely — is morally superior to the man who hoards. His toast — "Here's Success to the unfortunate" (003-449) — is the play's moral credo in five words.

## Key Scenes

Charles is introduced through the testimony of others before he appears on stage. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s report that "The Town talks of nothing else" (001-196) but his extravagance establishes his public reputation before his private [[reputation|character]] can contradict it. This delayed entrance — Charles does not appear until Act III — is a structural choice: we must first hear the worst of him before we can discover the best.

### The Drinking Scene (Act III, Scene 3)

The drinking scene with [[careles|Careless]] and his companions — "Here's to the widow of fifty" (003-402) — displays Charles's charm and his recklessness in equal measure. The scene is comic in surface and serious in implication: Charles is drinking away his inheritance, and he knows it. His gaiety is genuine but it is also performative — the bravado of a man who has chosen pleasure over prudence and is determined to enjoy the choice even as it ruins him.

### The Portrait Auction (Act IV, Scene 1)

The auction scene transforms Charles from comic wastrel to moral hero. Selling his family's portraits to pay his debts, he refuses to part with his uncle's [[portrait|picture]] despite his desperate need for [[fortune|money]]. "There is one portrait which you have always passed" (004-085) — the line, spoken by the disguised Sir Oliver, captures the entire moral argument. Charles may dissipate his own [[fortune|inheritance]], but he will not betray the people he loves.

The scene's dramatic irony is exquisitely calibrated. The audience knows Premium is Sir Oliver; Charles does not. Every gesture of loyalty Charles makes is therefore witnessed and authenticated by the very person whose portrait he refuses to sell. Sheridan has arranged a perfect test: Charles cannot fake loyalty because he does not know he is being tested for it. The refusal to sell is involuntary virtue — the best kind, in Sheridan's moral universe.

## Relationships

- **[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]**: brother and foil; Charles's honesty (about his vices) contrasts with [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s hypocrisy (about his virtues). The brothers are structured as a moral chiasmus: Charles appears bad and is good; Joseph appears good and is bad
- **[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]**: uncle; [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s [[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]]d testing of Charles reveals the nephew's true [[reputation|character]]. The Premium disguise is Sir Oliver's most successful experiment — Charles passes without knowing he is being examined
- **[[maria|Maria]]**: love interest; Charles's genuine [[sentiment|feeling]] for [[maria|Maria]] distinguishes him from [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s mercenary [[love|courtship]]. Maria's steadfast belief in Charles — against all available evidence — is vindicated by the play's resolution
- **[[careles|Careless]]**: friend and drinking companion; [[careles|Careless]]'s name signals the quality Charles shares — but the play ultimately distinguishes carelessness from heartlessness
- **[[moses|Moses]]**: the moneylender; Moses's presence in Charles's circle is a reminder that Charles's extravagance has real consequences in the world of debt and credit

## Critical Context

Charles has divided critics. Some read him as Sheridan's genuine hero — the embodiment of natural goodness in a world of artificial virtue. Others detect irony: Charles's "good heart" is convenient for a man who has spent his inheritance and needs a wealthy uncle to rescue him. The play supports both readings simultaneously, and its refusal to resolve the tension is part of its sophistication.

The portrait scene in particular has attracted competing interpretations. Is Charles's refusal to sell his uncle's portrait an act of genuine loyalty, or is it the canny calculation of a man who knows Premium's money will flow more freely if he appears sentimental? The scene's power derives from its undecidability — we cannot know Charles's motives with certainty, and neither can Sir Oliver. The test proves only that Charles *behaves* loyally, which is, in Sheridan's theatrical epistemology, the only proof available.
