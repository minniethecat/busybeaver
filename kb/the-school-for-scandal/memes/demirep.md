# Demirep

The term 'demirep' — a contracted form of 'demi-reputation' — was a common eighteenth-century label for a woman whose sexual reputation was compromised but not entirely destroyed.

## The Social Category

A 'demirep' occupied an ambiguous social position. She was not a prostitute, but she was not respectable; she might be received in some circles but excluded from others; her reputation was a matter of constant negotiation. The term appears in the play's opening scene when Lady Sneerwell, discussing the circulation of scandal about Lady Brittle, predicts that 'in a week the Men will toast her as a Demirep' (001-012).

## Lady Teazle's Peril

Lady Teazle's trajectory through the play traces the path toward demirep status. Her association with Lady Sneerwell's circle, her extravagance, and her flirtation with Joseph Surface all threaten to tip her from 'respectable married woman' to 'demirep.' The screen scene is the crisis of this trajectory — the moment when exposure would confirm the whispered rumours. Her reconciliation with Sir Peter (005-090) saves her from this fate.

## The Double Standard

Sheridan's treatment of the demirep category reveals the period's sexual double standard. Men might be rakes — a status that carried a certain glamour — but women who transgressed were irreparably damaged. The play's moral economy, while gentler than Restoration comedy's, still operates within this framework: Lady Teazle must be rescued from the brink, and her rescue is a condition of the comic resolution.
