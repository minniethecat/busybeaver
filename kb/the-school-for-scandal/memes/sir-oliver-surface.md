# Sir Oliver Surface

Sir Oliver Surface arrives from the East Indies in Act III and immediately becomes the play's moral investigator. His method — [[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]] — is Sheridan's device for penetrating the [[appearance-vs-reality|surfaces]] that the play's title announces. As "Mr. Premium" the [[fortune|money]]lender and "Mr. Stanley" the poor relation, Sir Oliver tests both nephews and finds the [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]] that reputation has concealed.

## Role in the Play

Sir Oliver's dramatic function is to be the [[reputation|character]] who refuses to [[credit|trust]] appearances. "What, no, no — I'll try 'em both" (003-138) — his determination to test rather than [[credit|trust]] distinguishes him from every other [[reputation|character]] in the play. Where [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] stakes his life on [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s honour, Sir Oliver reserves judgment until he has seen both nephews act.

His return from sixteen years in the East Indies brings colonial [[fortune|wealth]] into the play's moral economy. The [[wealth-and-class|fortune]] he has accumulated abroad will go to the nephew who proves worthy — and the play's testing structure ensures that worthiness, not reputation, determines the [[fortune|inheritance]].

## Key Scenes

Sir Oliver's scenes as Mr. Premium (Act III) expose [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance and his [[benevolence|generosity]] in equal measure. The auction scene — "rare joke to sell one's family by auction" (003-607) — reveals [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s irreverence and his loyalty simultaneously. As Mr. Stanley, Sir Oliver tests [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] and finds only coldness and evasion.
