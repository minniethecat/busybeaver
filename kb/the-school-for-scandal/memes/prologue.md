# Prologue

The prologue to The School for Scandal, written by David Garrick, is an important framing text that positions the play within contemporary theatrical debates.

## Garrick's Prologue

David Garrick (1717–1779) was the greatest actor of the eighteenth century and the manager of Drury Lane Theatre, where The School for Scandal premiered on 8 May 1777. His prologue, spoken before the first performance, addresses the audience directly and stakes a claim for Sheridan's play within the ongoing battle between 'laughing comedy' and 'sentimental comedy.'

## The Prologue's Argument

Garrick's prologue explicitly attacks the school of sentimental comedy, personified as a 'Muse' who 'drops her mask, and to the wondering age / Reveals the Comic Muse — but not the Tragic rage.' The prologue promises a return to the 'laughing' tradition of Restoration comedy — a comedy that exposes folly through wit rather than reforming it through tears.

## Historical Significance

The prologue matters historically because Garrick, as the most authoritative theatrical voice of his generation, was lending his prestige to Sheridan's assault on sentimentality. The prologue thus functions as a manifesto: it tells the audience what kind of comedy they are about to see and what kind of pleasure they should expect.
