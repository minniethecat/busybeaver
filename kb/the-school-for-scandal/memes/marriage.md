## Definition

Marriage in eighteenth-century English comedy functions simultaneously as plot resolution, social institution, and satirical target. The conventional comic ending — the marriage of young lovers — is both affirmed and destabilized in *The School for Scandal*, where existing marriages are shown to be sites of conflict, deception, and (sometimes) hard-won understanding.

## In The School for Scandal

### The Teazle Marriage

The central married couple, Sir Peter and Lady Teazle, embody the generational and social tensions that eighteenth-century marriage entailed. Sir Peter is an older man who married a young country wife and brought her to London, where she has been inducted into the fashionable world's expensive tastes and scandalous habits. Their quarrels — over money, society, and mutual suspicion — form the play's most substantial exploration of marital dynamics.

Sir Peter's exasperated curse — "Now may all the Plagues of marriage be doubled on me, if ever" (003-252) — voices the frustration of a husband who feels his authority eroded. Yet the couple's reconciliation in Act V, achieved through the shared crisis of Lady Teazle's near-disgrace behind Joseph's screen, suggests that marriage can be reformed through honest confrontation rather than sentimental pretence.

### Marriage as Social Transaction

Mrs. Candour's introduction of a character as "a Relation of mine by marriage" (002-222) reveals the institutional dimension of matrimony: marriage creates webs of obligation and connection that extend far beyond the couple. The marriage market is an acknowledged reality of the play's world — Maria is pursued as a match by both Surface brothers, her fortune making her an object of strategic courtship.

### The Marriage Plot

Charles and Maria's courtship provides the conventional comic spine of the play. Maria's steadfastness — she refuses to believe the scandalous reports about Charles — marks her as the virtuous heroine of sentimental comedy. Yet Sheridan complicates the formula: Charles's worthiness is demonstrated not through romantic declarations but through his refusal to sell his uncle's portrait, an act that has nothing to do with Maria. The marriage plot is resolved, but the resolution is secondary to the moral testing of character.

## Marriage and Gender Politics

Sheridan's treatment of marriage is inseparable from his analysis of gender. The play's three principal women — Lady Teazle, Lady Sneerwell, and Maria — occupy three distinct positions in the marital economy, and their trajectories illuminate the constraints and opportunities marriage offered eighteenth-century women.

Lady Teazle's position is the most complex. Married to Sir Peter for his fortune — "made me the happiest of men — and the most miserable" (001-388) — she has traded economic security for autonomy and finds the bargain unsatisfactory. Her extravagance, her pursuit of fashionable life, and her near-seduction by Joseph Surface are all, in part, responses to a marriage that was never between equals. Her reconciliation with Sir Peter at the play's end is not a return to the status quo but a renegotiation: the shared crisis has taught Sir Peter that authority without understanding is tyranny, and Lady Teazle that freedom without loyalty is danger.

Lady Sneerwell represents the woman who has opted out of the marriage market and into the scandal market. As "a young Widow with no great jointure" (001-063), her economic position precludes remarriage on favourable terms; her response is to weaponise reputation — the currency of the marriage market — against those who still trade in it. Her scandal operation is, viewed from one angle, a form of economic retaliation.

Maria, by contrast, occupies the conventional position of the marriageable heiress — pursued for her fortune, defended by her guardian, ultimately united with the deserving hero. But Sheridan gives her more agency than the type conventionally allows. Her refusal to credit scandal about Charles, her resistance to Lady Sneerwell's schemes, and her clear-eyed recognition of Joseph's hypocrisy all demonstrate a judgment that the play rewards. Maria is not merely a prize; she is a chooser, and her choice is validated by the play's resolution.

## The Companionate Marriage Ideal

The Teazles' trajectory maps onto a broader eighteenth-century cultural shift: the movement from marriage-as-alliance to marriage-as-companionship. Historians of the family, from Lawrence Stone to Amanda Vickery, have documented the gradual emergence of the "companionate marriage" ideal — a union based on affection and mutual respect rather than purely economic calculation.

Sheridan's treatment is sceptical but not dismissive. The Teazles begin the play in a state of mutual incomprehension: Sir Peter cannot understand his wife's appetite for fashionable life, and Lady Teazle cannot understand her husband's insistence on domestic economy. Their reconciliation in Act V does not eliminate these differences but reframes them. Lady Teazle's transformation — from "the most admired and powerfull" lady of fashion to a wife who can say "my Neeves are shaken to that degree" (004-670) — is not a rejection of London society but a recalibration of its claims against the claims of marriage.

Sheridan's answer to the companionate ideal is characteristically double-edged: companionship is possible, but only after the illusions that made it impossible have been stripped away. The screen scene literalizes this process of stripping away — Lady Teazle must literally come out from behind the screen, acknowledging where she has been and what she has heard, before reconciliation with Sir Peter can begin.

## The Unmarried Woman: Maria's Position

[[maria|Maria]] occupies a distinct position in the play's marital economy. As an unmarried heiress, she is the object of strategic pursuit by both [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] (who wants her fortune) and [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] (whose motives are less calculating but still initially obscured). Her predicament illustrates the vulnerability of the unmarried woman in a society where marriage is the only respectable destiny. Her steadfast refusal to believe scandal about Charles is both a romantic loyalty and a strategic judgment — she is, in effect, betting on the right horse.

Maria's eventual union with Charles satisfies the comic formula while subverting its assumptions. The marriage is not a reward for compliant femininity but the outcome of Maria's own discrimination. She has seen through Joseph's performance and recognised something genuine in Charles — even when the evidence against him was abundant. Her judgment, not her fortune, is the play's true marriage portion.

## Key Scenes

The screen scene (Act IV) is the play's most famous set-piece and its most acute examination of marriage. Lady Teazle's concealment behind the screen — overhearing Joseph's attempt to seduce her while simultaneously courting Maria — literalizes the hidden dimensions of marital life. The scene exposes both Joseph's hypocrisy and Lady Teazle's naivety, while providing the crisis that enables her reconciliation with Sir Peter.

## Historical Context

The eighteenth century saw significant evolution in English marriage practices. The Marriage Act of 1753 (Hardwicke's Act) regulated clandestine marriages, while debates about companionate marriage versus arranged unions were active in periodical literature. Sheridan's plays engage with these debates indirectly: Sir Peter and Lady Teazle's marriage is a mismatch by any standard, yet the play allows for its repair through mutual recognition rather than dissolution.

The play's treatment of the marriage market reflects the specific economic realities of eighteenth-century gentry life. A woman's fortune was her primary asset in the marriage market, and the transfer of property through marriage was a central mechanism of class reproduction. Sheridan does not challenge this system — marriages in his play remain transactions — but he insists that the transaction should be validated by genuine affection. Charles and Maria's marriage satisfies both criteria: it consolidates property and it unites lovers.
