# Trip

Trip is [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]'s footman, and like all of Sheridan's servants, he is a comic mirror of his social superiors. His name suggests both a journey (he carries messages) and a stumble (his pretensions are comic). In a play about the [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] of social identity, Trip is the performer at the lowest rung — the servant who acts the gentleman.

## Role in the Play

Trip's dramatic function is comic — he provides the servant's-eye view of [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance. His scenes with [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] ([[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]]d as [[sir-oliver-surface|Mr. Premium]]) demonstrate that even the household staff are participants in the economy of appearances. Trip's self-importance is a miniature of the play's larger theme: everyone, at every social level, is performing.

## Key Scenes

Act III, Scene 2: Trip receives [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] at [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s lodgings. His comic pretensions — the footman who affects the airs of his master — provide relief before the intensity of the auction scene.

## Relationships

- **[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]**: master; Trip's comic imitation of [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance mirrors the play's theme of [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] at all social levels
