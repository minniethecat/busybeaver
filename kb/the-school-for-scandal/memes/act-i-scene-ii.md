# Act I, Scene II — Sir Peter and Rowley

**Setting**: Sir Peter Teazle's House  
**Characters present**: sir-peter-teazle, rowley  
**Dramatic function**: exposition

## Summary

Sir Peter Teazle, an aging husband married to a young country wife, confides in the family steward Rowley. He laments Lady Teazle's transformation from a modest country girl into a fashionable London lady who spends extravagantly and defies his authority. Rowley shifts the conversation to the Surface brothers, defending Charles's essential goodness despite his extravagance while expressing suspicion of Joseph's calculated virtue. Sir Peter reveals that he is Maria's guardian and favours Joseph's suit over Charles's. The scene establishes the central domestic conflict (the Teazles' marriage) and the moral test that will drive the plot (distinguishing true virtue from hypocrisy).

## Key Moments

- **Sir Peter laments his marriage — an old bachelor who married a young wife** (001-207)
- **Rowley defends Charles Surface, the 'dissipated' brother** (001-238)
- **Sir Peter reveals his preference for Joseph Surface as Maria's suitor** (001-260)
