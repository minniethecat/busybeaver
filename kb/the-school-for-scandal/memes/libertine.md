The libertine — the reckless, pleasure-seeking free-liver whose wit and charm excuse his vices — was one of the most durable character types in English comedy from the Restoration through the eighteenth century. In *The School for Scandal*, Charles Surface is repeatedly named a libertine, yet Sheridan's treatment transforms the type: Charles is a libertine who can be reclaimed, and his reclamation is the play's central moral action.

## In The School for Scandal

### The Libertine as Accusation

From the play's opening scene, Charles is defined by the label. Lady Sneerwell describes him as "that Libertine, that extravagant, that bankrupt in Fortune and Reputation" (001-078), yoking libertinism to financial and social ruin in a single breath. The triple epithet — libertine, extravagant, bankrupt — establishes the downward trajectory that the libertine type conventionally follows: moral license leads to fiscal ruin.

Yet the play immediately complicates this judgment. Maria, who loves Charles, acknowledges the label while rejecting its finality: she describes him as a "Libertine who — if He is to be reclaim'd, can be so only by a Person of your —" (001-130). The dash is significant — it leaves unspecified what quality might effect reclamation, but the assertion that reclamation is possible marks Sheridan's departure from the Restoration model, where libertines were celebrated rather than reformed.

### Reformation Over Rejection

The play's resolution does not punish Charles for his libertinism but absorbs it into a larger moral economy. When Sir Oliver reveals himself and forgives Charles, the libertine is not condemned but reclaimed — his generosity and good nature are judged to outweigh his extravagance and indiscretion. The final character who invokes the label is Lady Sneerwell, speaking of "that ungrateful Libertine" (005-414) — now the word is a mark of her own bitterness rather than an accurate description, for Charles has been publicly re-established as the worthy heir.

This reformation is contrasted with Joseph Surface's fate. Joseph, who never bears the libertine label because his vices are concealed rather than displayed, is the one ultimately exiled from the comic resolution. The play's argument is clear: open vice can be reformed; secret vice cannot. In this moral arithmetic, the libertine — by wearing his faults openly — is more salvageable than the hypocrite.

## Historical Context

The libertine figure descends from the rakes of Restoration comedy — characters like Dorimant in Etherege's *The Man of Mode* or Horner in Wycherley's *The Country Wife* — whose sexual adventures and witty contempt for morality were presented as admirable rather than censurable. By Sheridan's time, the cultural valence of the libertine had shifted. The rise of sentimental comedy in the mid-eighteenth century, with its emphasis on moral reformation and innate goodness, created a demand for libertines who could be redeemed. Charles Surface is a product of this evolution: he retains the charm and wit of the Restoration rake but is placed within a sentimental plot that requires his moral rehabilitation. Sheridan, ever the master of comic synthesis, gives the audience both the pleasures of libertine wit and the satisfactions of libertine reform.
