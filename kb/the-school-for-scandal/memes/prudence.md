# Prudence

Few words in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* carry as much moral ambiguity as "prudence." [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is praised for his prudence; [[charles-surface|Charles]] is condemned for lacking it. Yet the play's resolution systematically inverts this valuation, rewarding the imprudent brother and exposing the prudent one. Prudence, Sheridan suggests, is a virtue only when it serves [[benevolence|generosity]] — and a vice when it serves only itself.

## The Prudent Hypocrite

[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s defining [[reputation|character]]istic — listed alongside "good sense and [[benevolence|Benevolence]]" (001-088) as his public credentials — is prudence. The pairing is revealing: prudence, in [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]], is indistinguishable from calculation. His every action is prudent — that is, it advances his interests while preserving his reputation. Sheridan's satire targets not prudence itself but its weaponisation as a moral credential.

## Imprudence as Virtue

The play's most striking moral move is its rehabilitation of imprudence. [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s "imprudence and bad [[reputation|character]]" (004-218) are the charges against him, yet [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s investigation reveals that [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s imprudence is inseparable from his [[benevolence|generosity]]. He "run[s] into a thousand little imprudences" (004-300) because he refuses to calculate — and it is precisely this refusal that distinguishes him from [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]. Sheridan's paradox: in a society of calculators, the imprudent man may be the only honest one.

## The Rescue of Misfortune

[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s stated aim is "to snatch [[fortune|misfortune]] and imprudence from the rapacious Relief" (003-137) that would exploit them. The formulation treats imprudence and [[fortune|misfortune]] as a pair — both are conditions to be rescued from, not crimes to be punished. The play's resolution thus offers a qualified defence of imprudence: it is dangerous, certainly, but less dangerous than the prudence that conceals a hollow heart.
