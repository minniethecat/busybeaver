# Husband

In *The School for Scandal*, the husband is a figure of structural paradox: legally sovereign within the household, yet perpetually outmanoeuvred within the social world that surrounds it. Sir Peter Teazle, the play's primary specimen of the type, embodies this contradiction — a man of means and moral seriousness who cannot govern his own drawing room. Sheridan uses the husband figure to explore the gap between formal authority and actual influence in a society where reputation matters more than law.

## The Husband's Complaint

Sir Peter's predicament is established early and sustained throughout. His opening complaint — "a husband is to have no influence, no authority" (002-007) — is both a statement of legal fact and a confession of practical impotence. He married Lady Teazle, a country girl with no fortune, expecting gratitude and obedience; he received instead a woman who has mastered the arts of fashion and extravagance with alarming speed. The irony is structural: by making her "woman of Fashion of Fortune of Rank" (002-058), Sir Peter has created a wife whose social position now exceeds his capacity to control it.

The husband's complaint extends beyond mere spending to encompass the fundamental imbalance of the marriage economy. Sir Peter laments that "when an old Bachelor marries a young Wife — he deserves it — 'tis a Judgment upon him" (002-054), acknowledging that his predicament is, in some sense, self-inflicted. The play thus presents the husband not as a victim of external forces but as an agent who has unwittingly authored his own comic suffering.

## Marriage and Surveillance

The play examines the husband's role as surveillant. Sir Peter, suspecting an affair between Lady Teazle and Charles Surface, is trapped in what Sheridan presents as the husband's eternal dilemma: to investigate is to admit suspicion, and to admit suspicion is to acknowledge vulnerability. The discovery scene in Act IV literalises this — the screen behind which Sir Peter hides is both a physical prop and a metaphor for the husband's position: present but unseen, invested in the truth but terrified of what it might reveal.

Sheridan extends the surveillance motif beyond Sir Peter to encompass the broader social dynamics of the play. The husband who monitors his wife is mirrored by the gossip who monitors her neighbours; both are engaged in the production of knowledge about others that is simultaneously prurient and anxious. The play's resolution, in which Sir Peter reconciles with Lady Teazle only after the screen has fallen and the truth has been made public, suggests that marriage cannot function on the basis of surveillance — only on the basis of transparency.

## The Husband and His Double

Charles Surface remarks that Sir Peter has "a settled married look — one may read Husband in his Face through all the Disguises of Taste" (002-407). This is a comic but cutting observation: the husband, in this world, is not a state of relationship but a state of being — visible, legible, and faintly ridiculous. Joseph Surface, the bachelor, performs sensibility; Sir Peter, the husband, performs authority. Neither performance is entirely successful, but the husband's failure is the more publicly visible.

The "settled married look" also participates in the play's broader concern with surfaces and legibility. Just as Joseph's surface of sentiment conceals his true character, Sir Peter's surface of husbandly authority conceals his actual powerlessness. Sheridan's comedy delights in making both surfaces transparent, exposing the gap between social performance and private reality.

## Comic Resolution

The play's resolution restores Sir Peter's marriage but not his dominion. Lady Teazle, having learned the cost of scandal, returns to her husband voluntarily — and it is the voluntariness that matters: "you will practice the Virtue you profess — you will forgive" (004-598). Sheridan's comedy does not endorse the husband's authority as a right; it endorses it only when it is earned through mutual regard. The play thus offers, beneath its surface cynicism, a surprisingly companionate vision of marriage.

> The husband in Sheridan's comedy is neither hero nor villain but a figure caught between two models of marriage — the patriarchal and the companionate — and rendered comic by his inability to reconcile them.
