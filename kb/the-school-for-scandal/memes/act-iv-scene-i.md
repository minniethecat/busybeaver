# Act IV, Scene I — The Portrait Auction

**Setting**: A Picture Room in Charles Surface's House — the family portrait gallery  
**Characters present**: [[charles-surface]], [[sir-oliver-surface]], [[moses]], [[careles]]  
**Dramatic function**: revelation

## Summary

The auction of the Surface family portraits is one of the most celebrated scenes in English comedy. Charles, needing money to relieve 'Mr. Stanley's' distress (and to pay his own debts), has resolved to sell the family portraits. Sir Oliver, still disguised as 'Mr. Premium' the moneylender, watches as Charles and Careless auction off each ancestor in turn — describing them with irreverent wit that is also, paradoxically, deeply revealing of character. When they reach Sir Oliver's own portrait, Charles refuses to sell it 'for fifty pounds' because his uncle has been his 'best friend.' This refusal — spontaneous, genuine, and entirely at odds with financial self-interest — completes Sir Oliver's conversion. He reveals his identity to the audience (though not yet to Charles) and forgives his nephew. The scene is the moral hinge of the play: Charles's refusal to sell his uncle's portrait proves that beneath the dissipation lies a heart of true feeling.
