# Marriage Market

The marriage market was the central institution of eighteenth-century upper-class social life, and The School for Scandal is saturated with its assumptions, anxieties, and negotiations.

## The Market Metaphor

'Marriage market' was not just a metaphor in the eighteenth century — it described a literal system in which eligible young people were presented, evaluated, and exchanged according to calculations of fortune, rank, and connection. The London Season was the market's trading floor; the assembly rooms, opera boxes, and drawing rooms were its trading posts; and the young women of the propertied classes were its commodities.

## The Play's Marriages

The play presents a spectrum of marriages: the Teazles' (an old man and a young wife, a mismatch of age and temperament), the Surface parents' (dead, their legacy the contrast between their sons), and the anticipated marriage of Charles and Maria (a love match that also happens to satisfy the demands of fortune). Each marriage represents a different bargain in the market, and the play's moral argument is that the bargain should be based on knowledge of character rather than calculation of advantage.

## Love and Interest

Sheridan's treatment of the marriage market is characteristically balanced. He does not condemn the market — Maria will bring a fortune to her marriage, and that fortune matters — but he insists that fortune alone is an insufficient basis for marriage. Joseph's mercenary pursuit of Maria is condemned not because he wants her money but because he wants only her money. The play's ideal is a marriage that satisfies both love and interest, heart and head.
