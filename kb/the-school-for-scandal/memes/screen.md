# Screen

The screen in Act IV of *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* is the most famous piece of furniture in English comedy. It is simultaneously a physical object, a theatrical device, and a metaphor — the thing behind which [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]]s are concealed and from behind which they emerge, catastrophically, into the light.

## The Screen as Dramatic Engine

Sheridan's stage direction is minimal: "[SERVANT draws the screen and exit]" (004-227), then "[Goes behind the screen]" (004-342). From these simple mechanics, the play's most complex scene unfolds. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] hides behind the screen to overhear [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s seduction attempt; [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] hides in the closet; [[charles-surface|Charles]] arrives and, in the play's most thrilling moment, cries "Behind the screen! Odds Life, let's unveil her!" (004-607). The screen is the pivot on which the entire plot turns — concealment, revelation, and the violent collision of private [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]] with public appearance.

## The Screen as Social Metaphor

The screen does more than serve the plot — it embodies the play's central theme. Every [[reputation|character]] in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* maintains a screen between their private conduct and their public presentation. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s entire identity is a screen; [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s [[scandal-theme|scandal-mongering]] is a screen for her own injured reputation. When the screen is "thrown down" (005-351), what is revealed is not merely [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] but the mechanism of concealment itself.

## The Aftermath

The screen scene's reverberations continue into Act V. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s recollection — "What of the closet and the screen" (005-339) — and his rueful laughter — "when the screen was thrown down — ha! ha!" (005-351) — suggest that the screen's fall is transformative. The [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]], once uncovered, cannot be re-screened. Sheridan's genius is to make a piece of furniture carry the entire weight of his moral argument: that the structures of concealment are, in the end, remarkably fragile.
