# Extravagance

"His Dissipation and extravagance exceed anything I have heard" (001-118) — this early assessment of [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] establishes extravagance as the play's most visible vice. Yet Sheridan's treatment of extravagance is surprisingly indulgent, contrasting it favourably with the hidden vice of hypocrisy that defines [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]].

## The Town's Verdict

Extravagance is the charge that defines [[charles-surface|Charles]] in the play's opening scenes. "The Town talks of nothing else" (001-196), [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] reports, positioning extravagance as a public spectacle rather than a private failing. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s indignant response to the same accusation — "My extravagance! I'm sure I'm not more extravagant than a Woman of Fashion ought to be" (002-015) — reveals that extravagance is in the eye of the beholder, a charge as much about social positioning as about actual spending.

## Extravagance versus Hypocrisy

The play's moral arithmetic is clearest in its treatment of the Surface brothers. [[charles-surface|Charles]] "has done all that his extravagance has threatened" (003-015) — he is exactly what he appears to be. [[joseph-surface|Joseph]], by contrast, conceals his vices beneath a "sentence of morality" (002-437). [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s proposal to "compound for his extravagance" (002-396) — to settle [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s debts — reflects the play's judgment: extravagance is forgivable because it is honest. [[hypocrisy|Hypocrisy]] is not.

## The Economics of Generosity

[[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance is inseparable from his [[benevolence|generosity]]. He gives [[fortune|money]] to "poor [[family|relations]]" (003-027) and refuses to sell his uncle's portrait despite his debts. Sheridan suggests that extravagance and benevolence are two sides of the same coin — an openness of hand that spends without counting, whether on oneself or on others. The play's resolution rewards this quality, finding in extravagance a form of virtue that prudence can never match.
