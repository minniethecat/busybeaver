# Lover

A lover in Sheridan's *The School for Scandal* is not merely a person who feels love but a social performer whose sincerity, motives, and standing are perpetually under scrutiny. In the drawing rooms of Lady Sneerwell's London, the role of lover intersects with calculations of [[fortune]], [[reputation]], and [[sentiment]], making it one of the play's most contested identities.

## Lovers and Fortune-Hunters

The play immediately establishes that romantic pursuit is entangled with money. Lady Sneerwell's opening exchange with Snake reveals that Joseph Surface's "real attachment is to Maria or her Fortune" (001-071), collapsing the distinction between genuine affection and mercenary design. Maria, as Sir Peter's ward, is a figure of wealth as well as desirability, and the question the play repeatedly asks is whether a lover can be separated from a fortune-hunter when the object of affection is an heiress. Charles Surface, by contrast, is characterised as "that Libertine, that extravagant" (001-078), and yet his disregard for money may paradoxically make him the more sincere lover — a possibility the play entertains before vindicating him.

The lover-fortune-hunter confusion is central to the play's treatment of courtship as a form of social theatre. In a world where marriage is an economic transaction as much as an emotional bond, the lover's declarations are always open to mercenary interpretation. Sheridan's achievement is to make this ambiguity comic rather than tragic, allowing the play to anatomise the marriage market without becoming cynical about love itself.

## The Disagreeable Lover

Sir Benjamin Backbite, the minor poet and would-be suitor, embodies the comic type of the lover as nuisance. Maria signals her distaste before he even enters: "O here is that disagreeable lover of mine, Sir Benjamin Backbite" (001-141). The phrase "lover of mine" is ironic — Sir Benjamin has assigned himself the role without Maria's consent, and his verses and attentions are forms of social presumption rather than courtship. His uncle, Crabtree, confirms the one-sidedness of the pursuit: the lover pursues, and Maria, as Lady Sneerwell notes, "you see your lover pursues you — Positively" (001-250).

Sir Benjamin represents the lover as a figure of comedic excess — his love poetry, his foppish manner, his inability to read Maria's clear signals of disinterest. Yet his presence serves a structural function: by embodying the lover in its most absurd form, he makes Charles Surface's eventual sincerity legible by contrast.

## The Sentimental Lover

Joseph Surface represents the lover who weaponises sentiment. His "sentiments" (001-091) are performances calculated to impress, and his courtship of Maria is conducted in the language of moral refinement even as he simultaneously attempts to seduce [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]. The play's exposure of Joseph is a demolition of the sentimental-lover pose: the man who speaks most beautifully of virtue is, in practice, its most accomplished violator. Sheridan thus suggests that the lover who talks most about love may be the least trustworthy — a caution that reverses the sentimental comedy tradition he is satirising.

The sentimental lover is, for Sheridan, the most dangerous type because he is the hardest to detect. Sir Benjamin's absurdity is self-evident; Joseph's hypocrisy is concealed beneath a surface of moral eloquence. The play's plot is, in large measure, the machinery by which this concealment is undone.

## The Sincere Lover

Charles Surface, the apparent libertine, emerges as the play's sincere lover. His refusal to sell Sir Oliver's picture — "the old gentleman has been very good to me — and I'll keep his picture" (004-057) — reveals a sentimental attachment to family that outweighs his financial need, and by extension suggests that his feeling for Maria is grounded in genuine affection rather than calculation. The test of the picture thus functions as a test of the lover: Charles passes where Joseph, who would have sold it, would have failed. Sheridan's dramatic economy makes the same gesture prove both filial devotion and romantic sincerity.

## Dramatic Function

The competing lovers — Charles the libertine, Joseph the hypocrite, Sir Benjamin the fop — structure the play's marriage plot, each representing a distinct failure mode of romantic pursuit. The resolution, in which Charles is revealed as fundamentally honourable beneath his extravagance, restores the lover to a position of genuine feeling, disentangled at last from [[fortune]], fashion, and false sentiment.

> The lover in Sheridan's world is a figure of performance before he is a figure of feeling — a truth the comedy exposes so that its final union may carry conviction.
