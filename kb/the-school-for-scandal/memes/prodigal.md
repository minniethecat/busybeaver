# Prodigal

Charles Surface is Sheridan's version of the prodigal son — the biblical archetype of the wastrel who squanders his inheritance but is ultimately forgiven.

## The Biblical Pattern

The parable of the Prodigal Son (Luke 15) provided eighteenth-century culture with one of its most durable narrative templates. Sheridan's innovation is to make the prodigal unrepentant — Charles never apologises for his extravagance — and yet to reveal, through the machinery of comic testing, that his heart is sound.

## Charles vs. Joseph

The contrast between the brothers is structured around the prodigal pattern. Joseph is the elder brother who never left home, who performed virtue and obeyed the rules — but whose heart is cold. Charles is the wastrel, the embarrassment, the one who 'cannot be serious' (003-160) — but whose impulsive generosity reveals a moral core that Joseph entirely lacks. Sheridan inverts the biblical pattern: the prodigal is the hero.

## Genuine Virtue

Charles's refusal to repent is crucial to the play's comic effect. He does not reform — he is simply revealed as already good. The play's argument is that genuine virtue does not require the performance of penitence; it is visible in action, not in sentiment.
