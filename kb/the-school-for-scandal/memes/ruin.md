# Ruin

In Sheridan's London, ruin is both a financial condition and a social verdict. The play treats the two as inseparable — a man "absolutely ruin'd" (001-240) is destroyed in [[debt|credit]] and [[reputation|character]] simultaneously, the victim of a coordinated assault by scandal-mongers and false friends.

## The Mechanics of Ruin

[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]'s predicament illustrates the play's anatomy of ruin. "A series of undeserved [[fortune|misfortune]]s" (003-007) initiates his decline, but it is the scandal-mongers who convert [[fortune|misfortune]] into catastrophe. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] marvels that [[charles-surface|Charles]] "has been ruined" before ever approaching a [[fortune|money]]lender (003-062), suggesting that reputation precedes and amplifies financial damage. Sheridan's point is that ruin is never purely economic — it requires an audience, a "School" that teaches society how to destroy its members.

## Prevention and Rescue

[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]] functions as the play's anti-ruin agent. His [[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]]d intervention is described as an attempt to "snatch [[fortune|misfortune]] and [[prudence|imprudence]] from the rapacious grip of ruin" (003-137). The word "rapacious" personifies ruin as an active predator, not a passive state. The play's comic resolution — [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s forgiveness and [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s [[fortune|inheritance]] — argues that ruin can be averted by familial loyalty, but only when the family member is willing to investigate beneath appearances.

## The Broader Warning

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s lament that "half his Acquaintance" will be found "ruin'd" (001-245) extends the warning beyond the Surface brothers. Ruin in this play is not exceptional but endemic — the natural outcome of a society that values appearance over substance, [[debt|credit]] over [[reputation|character]], and scandal over [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]].
