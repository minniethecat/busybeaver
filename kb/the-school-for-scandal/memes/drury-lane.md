# Drury Lane Theatre

Drury Lane Theatre was the venue for the first performance of The School for Scandal on 8 May 1777.

## The Theatre

Drury Lane, along with Covent Garden, was one of the two patent theatres in eighteenth-century London — the only venues legally permitted to perform spoken drama. It had been managed by David Garrick since 1747, and under his leadership it had become the most prestigious theatre in Britain. Sheridan succeeded Garrick as manager in 1776, acquiring a share in the patent and taking control of the theatre's operations.

## The Premiere

The first performance of The School for Scandal was one of the theatrical events of the decade. Garrick's prologue, spoken by the great actor himself, framed the play as a restoration of true comedy. The audience response was ecstatic, and the play entered the repertory immediately, where it has remained ever since — one of the few eighteenth-century comedies still regularly performed.

## Sheridan at Drury Lane

Sheridan's dual role as playwright and theatre manager was unusual. He wrote The School for Scandal for his own theatre, with his own company, and he could tailor the play to his actors' strengths. The result is a play that is supremely actable — its scenes are constructed with a practical knowledge of staging, timing, and audience response that only a working theatre manager could possess.
