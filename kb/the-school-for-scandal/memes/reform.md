# Reform

Reform — the capacity for moral self-correction — is the question on which The School for Scandal's comic resolution hinges. Can a young man of loose habits truly change? Is reform genuine or merely expedient?

## In The School for Scandal

The possibility of reform is introduced early as a defence of [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]. [[maria|Maria]] insists that despite reports of his dissolution, "he may reform" (001-329) — a hope that the entire plot will test. The play's answer is carefully structured: Charles does not reform in the sense of becoming prudent or respectable, but he is revealed to possess a sound moral core beneath his extravagance. Reform, for Sheridan, is less about changing one's nature than about stripping away the misleading appearances that conceal it.

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] undergoes the play's most dramatic reformation. Caught in [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s rooms, her exposure is also her redemption: she renounces the scandalous society that had seduced her and returns to her husband. "He's likely to reform I'll warrant" (005-569) extends the promise of change beyond Charles to the entire moral economy of the play.

The comic climax turns on [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]'s acceptance of this possibility. "[[rowley|Rowley]] always said you would reform" (005-656), he tells Charles, but Charles's response — "as to reforming Sir Peter I'll make no promises" (005-657) — is characteristically honest. He will not perform virtue for approval. Sheridan's point is that the truly reformable character is the one who makes no pretence of having already reformed.

[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]], by contrast, never reforms because he never acknowledges fault. His entire identity depends on the appearance of virtue; to reform would be to admit the appearance was false. The play thus draws a line between Charles's honest imperfection — which can improve — and Joseph's dishonest perfection — which cannot.
