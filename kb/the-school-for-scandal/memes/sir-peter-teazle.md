# Sir Peter Teazle

"When an old Bachelor takes a young Wife, what can he expect?" (001-387) — Sir Peter's opening line is a confession, a complaint, and the premise of his entire dramatic arc. As the play's moral centre, Sir Peter Teazle must learn to distinguish appearance from reality in both his [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] and his judgment of the Surface brothers.

## Role in the Play

Sir Peter serves as the play's primary point of moral identification. He is neither a wit nor a fool — he is an honest man in a dishonest world, "remarkably tenacious of reputation" (002-098) yet surrounded by those who traffic in its [[ruin|destruction]]. His function is to be deceived and then undeceived: deceived by [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] of virtue, deceived by the [[scandal-theme|gossip]] about [[charles-surface|Charles]], deceived about his own wife's fidelity — and then, in [[screen|the screen scene]], catastrophically enlightened.

His guardianship of [[maria|Maria]] places him at the intersection of the play's [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] plots. He initially supports [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s suit for [[maria|Maria]]'s hand, mistaking [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s prudence for virtue. His eventual recognition of [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s true [[reputation|character]] — despite the young man's extravagance — is the play's definitive moral judgment.

## The December-May Marriage

Sir Peter's marriage to Lady Teazle is the play's most sustained exploration of marital dynamics. He is an "old Bachelor" (001-387) who married a young country woman and brought her to London, where she has been transformed into a fashionable lady whose expensive tastes he cannot afford and whose social manoeuvres he cannot control. The age gap is not incidental — it is the structural condition of their marriage, shaping every argument about money, society, and authority.

Sheridan's treatment of the Teazle marriage is remarkable for its refusal of easy resolution. Sir Peter is not simply the comic cuckold of Restoration convention, nor is Lady Teazle simply the errant wife. Both are sympathetic, both are culpable, and their reconciliation requires not the restoration of Sir Peter's authority but its transformation into something more like partnership. "There is no trusting to appearances" (005-175) — Sir Peter's hard-won wisdom applies to his own marriage as much as to his judgment of others.

## Key Scenes

### The Domestic Quarrels (Act II, Scene 1)

The early domestic scenes with [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] establish the "matrimonial duel" (002-098) that defines their [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]]. Sir Peter's exasperated [[love|affection]] — "Come, come, Sir Peter, you love her, notwith[[reputation|standing]] your" quarrels (001-412) — reveals the genuine [[sentiment|feeling]] beneath the bickering. These scenes are the play's most naturalistic writing: the rhythms of marital argument, the way affection and irritation coexist, the impossibility of winning a quarrel with someone you love.

### The Screen Scene (Act IV)

The screen scene is Sir Peter's crucible. Hidden in [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s closet, he overhears the revelation of [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s treachery and his wife's [[honour|integrity]]. His cry — "O Sir Peter you mustn't [[debt|credit]] the" appearance (004-384) — is the play's thesis statement in miniature.

The scene's structure places Sir Peter in the position of eavesdropper — the same position occupied by the scandal-mongers he despises. But where they listen for material to destroy reputations, Sir Peter listens and discovers the truth. The scene thus distinguishes between the malicious eavesdropping of scandal and the accidental overhearing that leads to enlightenment.

### The Reconciliation (Act V)

Sir Peter's reconciliation with Lady Teazle — "when an old Bachelor takes a young Wife" — revisits his opening line but transforms its meaning. What began as a complaint becomes an acknowledgment: he did take a young wife, and the difficulties that followed were inherent in the choice. The reconciliation does not erase the play's conflicts but absorbs them into a marriage that has been tested and survived.

## The Moral Centre

Sir Peter's most important dramatic function is to model the process of learning from experience. He begins the play wrong about nearly everything: wrong about Joseph, wrong about Charles, wrong about his own authority as a husband. But unlike the play's villains, who cannot learn because they cannot admit error, Sir Peter revises his judgments in response to evidence. His declaration — "There is no trusting to appearances" (005-175) — carries weight because we have watched him learn it.

This capacity for revision is what makes Sir Peter the play's moral centre. In a world of fixed performances — Joseph's virtue, Lady Sneerwell's scandal, Mrs. Candour's candour — Sir Peter is the one character who can say "I was wrong" and mean it. His moral authority derives not from infallibility but from corrigibility.

## Relationships

- **[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]**: wife; a [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] of "December and May" that moves from comic warfare to genuine reconciliation. Their relationship is the play's most psychologically nuanced portrait of marriage
- **[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]**: ward and eventual moral vindication; Sir Peter's journey from suspicion to approval of [[charles-surface|Charles]] drives the B-plot. He comes to see that Charles's vices are on the surface while Joseph's virtues are superficial
- **[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]**: false friend; Sir Peter stakes "My life on [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s [[honour|Honour]]" (002-443) — a wager the play makes him regret. His disillusionment with Joseph is the play's most painful arc
- **[[rowley|Rowley]]**: steward and confidant; the one [[reputation|character]] Sir Peter [[credit|trust]]s without reservation. Rowley's steady good sense is the foil to Sir Peter's susceptibility to performance
- **[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]**: old friend; [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s return enables the testing of the nephews that Sir Peter cannot perform himself. Their friendship anchors the play's moral community
