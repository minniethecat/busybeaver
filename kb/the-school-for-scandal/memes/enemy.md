# Enemy

Enemy in *The School for Scandal* is a term that rarely means what it says. Sheridan's characters use the word to negotiate relationships that are more complicated than enmity — and more dangerous. The play repeatedly insists that the real enemy is not the declared antagonist but the friend who conceals his hostility behind a mask of benevolence.

## Sir Peter's Hidden Enemy

The play's most sustained treatment of concealed enmity centres on Sir Peter's blindness to Joseph Surface. Sir Peter believes Joseph to be his friend and Charles his adversary: "'Tis his Vices and Follies have made me his Enemy" (002-449). Sir Peter's formulation is revealing — he frames his hostility to Charles as something Charles has caused, not something he has chosen — and it prepares the irony of his eventual discovery. Joseph, the man Sir Peter trusts as an ally, is in fact "not your Enemy in the Affair" with Lady Teazle (004-452) — a denial so carefully phrased that it amounts to a confession. The line's ambiguity is Sheridan's point: the true enemy never declares himself.

## Lady Sneerwell's War

Lady Sneerwell's enmity is the frankest in the play, but even it operates through indirection. She wages war not in the open but through the gossip network, using scandal as her weapon and the school's members as her soldiers. Her enmity is real — she means to destroy Maria's reputation and secure Charles Surface — but its medium is social performance rather than open confrontation. The drawing room, in Sheridan's vision, is a battlefield where the weapons are words and the casualties are reputations.

## Friendship as Concealed Enmity

Sheridan complicates the friend-enemy binary through the structure of the scandal school itself. The members of Lady Sneerwell's circle are nominally friends — they visit each other's drawing rooms, exchange confidences, share the latest gossip — but their friendship is predatory. Mrs. Candour's affected concern for reputations masks an appetite for their destruction; Sir Benjamin Backbite's wit is a weapon; Crabtree's inventions are a form of social assassination. The play's anatomy of the circle suggests that the most dangerous enemies are those who have been admitted as friends. As Snake's betrayal of Lady Sneerwell in the final scene demonstrates, the school for scandal produces enemies even among its own membership — the logic of gossip consumption eventually consumes its producers.

## Enmity and the Comic Denouement

The play's resolution converts enemies into, if not friends, at least manageable antagonists. Joseph Surface, exposed, becomes nobody's enemy because he is nobody's confidant — his power depended on the trust he has now forfeited. Sir Peter makes peace with Charles, and Sir Oliver's final judgment — "London will prove the very source of honour" — suggests that the city's social machinery, properly understood, can neutralise enmity as efficiently as it spreads scandal. Sheridan's comedy does not abolish enemies; it renders them harmless by making them visible. The play's final gesture is not reconciliation but demystification: once the school for scandal is exposed, its members lose their power to wound because they have lost their power to deceive.

## Rhetoric of Enmity

The language of enmity in the play is performative as much as descriptive. To call someone an enemy is to perform a social act — it aligns allies, warns third parties, and shapes the expectations that govern future interactions. Sir Peter's declaration that Charles's vices have "made me his Enemy" (002-449) is less a report on his emotional state than a public stance intended to clarify his position within the social network. The rhetoric of enmity, in Sheridan's hands, is a social tool — one that characters deploy strategically, often at cross-purposes with the reality of their relationships.
