# ACT III

SCENE I.--At SIR PETER'S

[003-001]SIR PETER, SIR OLIVER, and ROWLEY

[003-002]SIR PETER. Well, then, we will see the Fellows first and have our wine

[003-003]afterwards.--but how is this, [[rowley|Master Rowley]]--I don't see the Jet of your

[003-004]scheme.

[003-005]ROWLEY. Why Sir--this [[sir-oliver-surface|Mr. Stanley]] whom I was speaking of, is nearly

[003-006]related to them by their mother. He was once a merchant in Dublin--but

[003-007]has been ruined by a series of undeserved misfortunes--and now lately

[003-008]coming over to solicit the assistance of his friends here--has been

[003-009]flyng [flung] into prison by some of his Creditors--where he is now with

[003-010]two helpless Boys.--

[003-011]SIR OLIVER. Aye and a worthy Fellow too I remember him. But what is this

[003-012]to lead to--?

[003-013]ROWLEY. You shall hear--He has applied by letter both to Mr. Surface and

[003-014][[charles-surface|Charles]]--from the former he has received nothing but evasive promises

[003-015]of future service, while Charles has done all that his extravagance has

[003-016]left him power to do--and He is at this time endeavouring to raise a sum

[003-017]of [[wealth-and-class|money]]--part of which, in the midst of his own distresses, I know He

[003-018]intends for the service of poor Stanley.

[003-019]SIR OLIVER. Ah! he is my Brother's Son.

[003-020]SIR PETER. Well, but how is Sir Oliver personally to----

[003-021]ROWLEY. Why Sir I will inform Charles and his Brother that Stanley has

[003-022]obtain'd permission to apply in person to his Friends--and as they

[003-023]have neither of them ever seen him[,] let Sir Oliver assume his

[003-024][[reputation|character]]--and he will have a fair opportunity of judging at least of

[003-025]the [[benevolence|Benevolence]] of their Dispositions.

[003-026]SIR PETER. Pshaw! this will prove nothing--I make no doubt Charles is

[003-027]Coxcomb and thoughtless enough to give [[fortune|money]] to poor [[family|relations]] if he had

[003-028]it--

[003-029]SIR OLIVER. Then He shall never want it--. I have brought a few Rupees

[003-030]home with me [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]--and I only want to be sure of bestowing them

[003-031]rightly.--

[003-032]ROWLEY. Then Sir believe me you will find in the youngest Brother one

[003-033]who in the midst of Folly and [[extravagance|dissipation]]--has still, as our immortal

[003-034]Bard expresses it,--

[003-035]"a Tear for Pity and a Hand open as the day for melting [[charity|Charity]]."

[003-036]SIR PETER. Pish! What signifies his having an open Hand or Purse

[003-037]either when He has nothing left to give!--but if you talk of humane

[003-038]Sentiments--[[joseph-surface|Joseph]] is the man--Well, well, make the trial, if you

[003-039]please. But where is the fellow whom you brought for Sir Oliver to

[003-040]examine, relative to Charles's affairs?

[003-041]ROWLEY. Below waiting his commands, and no one can give him better

[003-042]intelligence--This, Sir Oliver, is a friendly Jew, who to do him

[003-043]justice, has done everything in his power to bring your nephew to a

[003-044]proper sense of his extravagance.

[003-045]SIR PETER. Pray let us have him in.

[003-046]ROWLEY. Desire Mr. [[moses|Moses]] to walk upstairs.

[003-047][Calls to SERVANT.]

[003-048]SIR PETER. But Pray why should you suppose he will speak the [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]]?

[003-049]ROWLEY. Oh, I have convinced him that he has no chance of recovering

[003-050]certain Sums advanced to Charles but through the bounty of Sir Oliver,

[003-051]who He knows is arrived; so that you may depend on his Fidelity to his

[003-052]interest. I have also another evidence in my [[power|Power]], one [[snake|Snake]], whom I

[003-053]shall shortly produce to remove some of YOUR Prejudices[,] Sir Peter[,]

[003-054]relative to Charles and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]].

[003-055]SIR PETER. I have heard too much on that subject.

[003-056]ROWLEY. Here comes the honest Israelite.

[003-057]Enter MOSES

[003-058]--This is Sir Oliver.

[003-059]SIR OLIVER. Sir--I understand you have lately had great dealings with my

[003-060]Nephew Charles.

[003-061]MOSES. Yes Sir Oliver--I have done all I could for him, but He was

[003-062]ruined before He came to me for Assistance.

[003-063]SIR OLIVER. That was unlucky truly--for you have had no opportunity of

[003-064]showing your Talents.

[003-065]MOSES. None at all--I hadn't the [[pleasure|Pleasure]] of knowing his Distresses till

[003-066]he was some thousands worse than nothing, till it was impossible to add

[003-067]to them.

[003-068]SIR OLIVER. Unfortunate indeed! but I suppose you have done all in your

[003-069]Power for him honest Moses?

[003-070]MOSES. Yes he knows that--This very evening I was to have brought him a

[003-071]gentleman from the city who does not know him and will I believe advance

[003-072]some money.

[003-073]SIR PETER. What[!] one Charles has never had money from before?

[003-074]MOSES. Yes[--]Mr. Premium, of Crutched Friars.

[003-075]SIR PETER. Egad, Sir Oliver a Thought strikes me!--Charles you say

[003-076]does'nt know Mr. Premium?

[003-077]MOSES. Not at all.

[003-078]SIR PETER. Now then Sir Oliver you may have a better opportunity of

[003-079]satisfying yourself than by an old romancing tale of a poor Relation--go

[003-080]with my friend Moses and represent Mr. Premium and then I'll answer

[003-081]for't you'll see your Nephew in all his glory.

[003-082]SIR OLIVER. Egad I like this Idea better than the other, and I may visit

[003-083]Joseph afterwards as old Stanley.

[003-084]SIR PETER. True so you may.

[003-085]ROWLEY. Well this is taking Charles rather at a disadvantage, to be

[003-086]sure--however Moses--you understand Sir Peter and will be faithful----

[003-087]MOSES. You may depend upon me--and this is near the Time I was to have

[003-088]gone.

[003-089]SIR OLIVER. I'll accompany you as soon as you please, Moses----but

[003-090]hold--I have forgot one thing--how the plague shall I be able to pass

[003-091]for a Jew?

[003-092]MOSES. There's no need--the Principal is Christian.

[003-093]SIR OLIVER. Is He--I'm very sorry to hear it--but then again--an't I

[003-094]rather too smartly dressed to look like a money-Lender?

[003-095]SIR PETER. Not at all; 'twould not be out of character, if you went in

[003-096]your own carriage--would it, Moses!

[003-097]MOSES. Not in the least.

[003-098]SIR OLIVER. Well--but--how must I talk[?] there's certainly some [[hypocrisy|cant]] of

[003-099]usury and mode of treating that I ought to know.

[003-100]SIR PETER. Oh, there's not much to learn--the great point as I take it

[003-101]is to be exorbitant enough in your Demands hey Moses?

[003-102]MOSES. Yes that's very great Point.

[003-103]SIR OLIVER. I'll answer for't I'll not be wanting in that--I'll ask him

[003-104]eight or ten per cent. on the loan--at least.

[003-105]MOSES. You'll be found out directly--if you ask him no more than that,

[003-106]you'll be discovered immediately.

[003-107]SIR OLIVER. Hey!--what the Plague!--how much then?

[003-108]MOSES. That depends upon the Circumstances--if he appears not very

[003-109]anxious for the supply, you should require only forty or fifty per

[003-110]cent.--but if you find him in great Distress, and want the monies very

[003-111]bad--you may ask double.

[003-112]SIR PETER. A good--[h]onest Trade you're learning, Sir Oliver--

[003-113]SIR OLIVER. Truly, I think so--and not unprofitable--

[003-114]MOSES. Then you know--you haven't the monies yourself, but are forced to

[003-115]borrow them for him of a Friend.

[003-116]SIR OLIVER. O I borrow it of a Friend do I?

[003-117]MOSES. And your friend is an unconscion'd Dog--but you can't help it.

[003-118]SIR OLIVER. My Friend's an unconscionable Dog, is he?

[003-119]MOSES. Yes--and He himself hasn't the monies by him--but is forced to

[003-120]sell stock--at a great loss--

[003-121]SIR OLIVER. He is forced to sell stock is he--at a great loss, is

[003-122]he--well that's very kind of him--

[003-123]SIR PETER. Efaith, Sir Oliver--Mr. Premium I mean--you'll soon be master

[003-124]of the Trade--but, Moses would have him inquire if the borrower is a

[003-125]minor--

[003-126]MOSES. O yes--

[003-127]SIR PETER. And in that case his Conscience will direct him--

[003-128]MOSES. To have the Bond in another Name to be sure.

[003-129]SIR OLIVER. Well--well I shall be perfect--

[003-130]SIR PETER. But hearkee wouldn't you have him also run out a little

[003-131]against the annuity Bill--that would be in character I should think--

[003-132]MOSES. Very much--

[003-133]ROWLEY. And lament that a young man now must be at years of [[prudence|discretion]]

[003-134]before He is suffered to ruin himself!

[003-135]MOSES. Aye, great Pity!

[003-136]SIR PETER. And abuse the Public for allowing merit to an act whose only

[003-137]object is to snatch [[fortune|misfortune]] and imprudence from the rapacious Relief

[003-138]of usury! and give the minor a chance of inheriting his estate without

[003-139]being undone by coming into Possession.

[003-140]SIR OLIVER. So--so--Moses shall give me further instructions as we go

[003-141]together.

[003-142]SIR PETER. You will not have much time[,] for your Nephew lives hard

[003-143]bye--

[003-144]SIR OLIVER. Oh Never--fear[:] my Tutor appears so able that tho' Charles

[003-145]lived in the next street it must be my own Fault if I am not a compleat

[003-146]Rogue before I turn the Corner--

[003-147][Exeunt SIR OLIVER and MOSES.]

[003-148]SIR PETER. So--now I think Sir Oliver will be convinced--you shan't

[003-149]follow them Rowley. You are partial and would have prepared Charles for

[003-150]'tother plot.

[003-151]ROWLEY. No upon my word Sir Peter--

[003-152]SIR PETER. Well, go bring me this Snake, and I'll hear what he has to

[003-154][Exit ROWLEY.]

[003-155]I should be glad to be convinced my suspicions of Lady Teazle and

[003-156]Charles were unjust--I have never yet opened my mind on this subject to

[003-161]MARIA. No Sir--He was engaged.

[003-164]MARIA. Indeed Sir Peter--your frequent importunity on this subject

[003-165]distresses me extremely--you compell me to Declare that I know no man

[003-166]who has ever paid me a particular Attention whom I would not prefer to

[003-167]Mr. Surface--

[003-171]MARIA. This is unkind Sir--You know I have obey'd you in neither seeing

[003-176]SIR PETER. Well well pity him as much as you please, but give your Heart

[003-178]MARIA. Never to his Brother!

[003-182]MARIA. I can only say, you shall not have just Reason--'tis true, by

[003-186][Exit.]

SCENE III.--CHARLES, CARELESS, etc., etc.

[003-357]At Table with Wine
