# Coquette

The coquette — the woman who flirts without committing, who uses her charms as weapons and her reputation as currency — was a familiar figure in eighteenth-century comedy, and Lady Teazle begins the play as her embodiment.

## The Coquette Tradition

The coquette was a stock figure of Restoration and eighteenth-century comedy — a woman who delighted in the power of her attractiveness and used it to manipulate men. She was both celebrated (for her wit and independence) and condemned (for her moral danger). The stage coquette was typically punished — usually by marriage — but the punishment was often so perfunctory that the audience was left admiring her spirit.

## Lady Teazle as Coquette

Lady Teazle's trajectory takes her from country innocence, through fashionable coquetry, to near-disgrace, and finally to genuine reconciliation with her husband. Her coquette phase — the period of extravagant dress, flirtation with Joseph Surface, and delight in the power of her attractiveness — is treated by Sheridan as a stage in her moral education rather than a fixed character. She learns, through the screen scene's near-catastrophe, that the coquette's game has real consequences.

## The Reformed Coquette

Sheridan's treatment of the coquette is gentler than his Restoration predecessors'. Lady Teazle is not punished but rescued — her reconciliation with Sir Peter is generous rather than punitive. The play's argument is that coquetry, like extravagance, is a folly of inexperience rather than a mark of corruption, and that its cure is knowledge rather than punishment.
