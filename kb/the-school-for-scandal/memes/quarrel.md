# Quarrel

Quarrelling in *The School for Scandal* is the domestic equivalent of scandal: a performance that, in its excess, reveals truths its participants would rather conceal. The Teazles' marriage is a running quarrel, and Sheridan treats it not as tragic dysfunction but as a comic idiom — a form of communication that, for all its noise, keeps them engaged with each other.

## The Anatomy of a Quarrel

Sheridan provides a textbook anatomy of marital quarrelling in the Teazles' Act III exchanges. Sir Peter complains "my life may be made unhappy by your Temper" and Lady Teazle replies "Lud! Sir Peter I hope you haven't been quarrelling with Maria" (003-194), a deflection that escalates rather than defuses the conflict. The quarrel's rhythm is familiar to any observer of domestic warfare: accusation, counter-accusation, and a retreat into self-pity. Yet Sheridan's point is that this pattern is not meaningless — it is the couple's way of negotiating power.

## Who Begins the Quarrel?

The question of who starts quarrels becomes itself a subject of contention. Lady Teazle insists "if you recollect my Love you always began first" (003-232), and Sir Peter immediately responds "There now you want to quarrel again" (003-243). The recursion is comic but also diagnostic: the couple is trapped in a loop where trying not to quarrel becomes another quarrel. Sheridan's insight is that quarrelling, like scandal, is self-perpetuating — the attempt to escape it only deepens one's entanglement.

## The Settlement Quarrel

A more serious quarrel concerns Sir Peter's failure to make a financial settlement on his wife. Sir Peter acknowledges "She has lately reproach'd me more than once with having made no settlement on her — and, in our last Quarrel, she almost hinted" (004-427-428). This quarrel is different from the play-fighting of Act III: money, as always, raises the stakes. The settlement quarrel reveals that beneath the Teazles' comic bickering is a real asymmetry of power — Sir Peter holds the purse strings, and his wife knows it.

## Quarrel as Intimacy

Sir Peter, for all his complaints, confesses that "there is certainly a great satisfaction in quarrelling" with a woman one loves (002-118). The admission is striking: quarrelling is not merely endured but enjoyed. Sheridan, following the Restoration comic tradition, treats verbal combat between the sexes as a form of courtship by other means. The play's happy ending is not a marriage without quarrels but a marriage in which quarrelling has been brought within the governance of mutual affection.
