# Virtue

Virtue is the currency of reputation in The School for Scandal — the more a character professes it, the less they possess it. Sheridan's comedy systematically dismantles the equation of virtue with public esteem.

## In The School for Scandal

[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is the play's virtuoso of counterfeit virtue. He presents himself as a man of impeccable moral sentiment while conducting an affair with [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] behind her husband's back. His brother [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]], by contrast, makes no claim to virtue — yet when tested, he proves to have "virtue enough to be faithful even to" (001-138) those who depend on him.

The play's deepest irony is that the truly virtuous character, [[maria|Maria]], is the one most constrained by the social machinery of virtue. Her moral clarity is not rewarded with freedom but with the pressure to marry according to her guardian's wishes. Virtue, in Sheridan's world, is not a path to happiness but a condition that must survive the assaults of those who resent it.

[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s near-seduction turns on the question of whether she will "part with my virtue to preserve my" (004-313) reputation — a choice between inner integrity and outer appearance that crystallises the play's central moral distinction. She chooses virtue over reputation, and the choice redeems her.
