# Act III, Scene III — The Drinking Party

**Setting**: Charles Surface's House — Charles, Careless, and friends at table with wine  
**Characters present**: [[charles-surface]], [[careles]], [[first-gentleman]], [[second-gentleman]], [[sir-oliver-surface]]  
**Dramatic function**: comic relief

## Summary

Charles Surface hosts a rowdy drinking party with Careless and friends. The gentlemen drink toasts to various ladies and sing comic songs. Sir Oliver, still disguised as 'Mr. Premium,' watches from the side, appalled and amused by turns. The scene is the play's most sustained comic set piece — Charles and his friends drink, joke, and ridicule the very idea of respectability. When Careless leads a song mocking 'sentiment,' Charles's enthusiasm reveals his genuine, if misguided, sincerity. He toasts his uncle Sir Oliver — not knowing the man is in the room — with genuine affection, and this sincerity beneath the dissipation begins to win Sir Oliver over. The scene demonstrates that Charles, unlike Joseph, has no hypocrisy: what you see, however alarming, is what you get.
