# Sentimental Comedy

Sentimental comedy was the dominant theatrical mode in mid-eighteenth-century England, and The School for Scandal is in part a sustained attack upon it.

## The Sentimental Formula

Sentimental comedy replaced the wit and sexual frankness of Restoration comedy with tearful scenes of moral elevation. Its heroes were not rakes but men of 'sensibility' — finely tuned emotional instruments who wept at virtue and shuddered at vice. Its plots rewarded moral purity and punished wickedness with the inexorable logic of poetic justice. Playwrights like Richard Steele (The Conscious Lovers, 1722) and Hugh Kelly made sentimentality the default mode of English comedy for two generations.

## Sheridan's Critique

Sheridan saw sentimental comedy as a form of hypocrisy — a theatrical mode that confused feeling with doing. Joseph Surface is the perfect embodiment of this critique. He has 'sentiments' ready for every occasion: he can moralise about charity, fidelity, and brotherly love with fluency and apparent conviction. But when tested by action — when asked to give money, or defend his brother, or resist an adulterous opportunity — he fails every time. His sentiment is a performance, a substitute for virtue rather than its expression.

## The Play's Alternative

The School for Scandal offers Charles Surface as the antidote to Joseph. Charles has no fine sentiments — he drinks, jokes, and squanders money with cheerful recklessness. But when tested, he acts: he sells his possessions to relieve a stranger's distress, and he refuses to sell his uncle's portrait out of genuine gratitude. Sheridan's argument is that action trumps feeling, and that comedy's proper business is to expose the gap between the two.
