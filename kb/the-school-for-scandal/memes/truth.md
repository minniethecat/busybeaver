# Truth

In Sheridan's *The School for Scandal*, truth is the currency that everyone professes to value and no one wishes to spend. The play is structured around a series of exposures — of lies, hypocrisies, and disguises — that collectively ask whether truth can survive in a society organised around its suppression. The answer, characteristically for comedy, is yes — but only after everyone has been thoroughly embarrassed.

## Truth and Scandal

Lady Sneerwell opens the play with a paradoxical observation: scandal-mongers "happen to have a little truth on their side to support them" (001-093). The insight is sharp: the most effective lies are those wrapped around a kernel of truth. The scandal economy operates not by pure fabrication but by distortion — taking a real event or relationship and spinning it into something damaging. Truth, in this machinery, is not an antidote to scandal but its raw material.

## Truth and Disguise

The play's elaborate structure of disguise — Sir Oliver as Mr. Premium and Mr. Stanley, Lady Teazle behind the screen, Snake as double agent — functions as a sustained experiment in truth's relationship to identity. When Sir Oliver disguises himself, he learns truths about his nephews that no undisguised interrogation could have yielded: "They who live in the world are the only people to know it" (003-002). The paradox is characteristically Sheridanian: truth requires deception to be discovered. Disguise strips away the protective layer of social performance and reveals what lies beneath, but the revelation is itself a performance — a truth-telling enabled by lying. The screen scene literalises this paradox: Lady Teazle hides behind a physical screen to overhear a truth about Joseph's character that she could never have learned face to face.

## Truth and Performance

The play's characters invoke truth constantly, but almost always as a rhetorical posture. Moses the moneylender protests that "I always speak the truth, and scorn to tell a lie" (003-500) — and Charles wryly notes that "People that speak truth generally do" (003-502), a line that exposes the self-serving nature of truth-claims. The sentimental school, represented by Joseph Surface, has turned truth into a performance: one *appears* truthful rather than *being* truthful.

## Truth and Exposure

The discovery scene in Act IV is the play's truth-machine. Lady Teazle, hidden (and then revealed) behind the screen; Joseph Surface, exposed as a seducer and hypocrite; Sir Peter, forced to confront what he has refused to see — the scene functions as a forced reckoning. Sir Peter's line — "Now, I believe, the truth is coming indeed" (004-437) — is both comic and cathartic. In Sheridan's moral economy, truth is what emerges when artifice collapses under its own weight.

## Charles Surface and Truth

Charles Surface, the "dissipated" younger brother, is paradoxically the play's most truthful character. He does not pretend to virtue; his vices are transparent and his generosity genuine. Sir Oliver's test of the two brothers — the moneylender disguise — is fundamentally a test of who will speak truth when it costs something. Charles sells the family portraits but refuses to part with Sir Oliver's own picture, a gesture that speaks truer than any of Joseph's moralising sentences. Sheridan's verdict is clear: truth resides less in what one says than in what one does.

## The Epilogue's Truth

The play's Epilogue, spoken by Lady Teazle, delivers the play's final and most unsettling word on truth. Addressing the audience directly, she warns that the scandals depicted on stage are "but faint copies" of what occurs in the drawing-rooms of those who watch. The theatrical frame collapses: the truth the play has been pursuing is not confined to the stage but extends into the audience's own lives. It is a characteristically eighteenth-century move — the comedy that laughs at itself — but it also refuses the comfort of a clean distinction between representation and reality. Truth, Sheridan suggests, is not something a play can deliver; it is something an audience must take home.

## Dramatic Function

The play's resolution depends on truth's eventual triumph, but Sheridan is careful not to make the triumph too neat. Snake, the paid informant, is bribed to tell the truth at the end — a transaction that undercuts any simple moral. Truth is restored, but at a price, and by the very mechanisms of manipulation that produced the scandal in the first place.
