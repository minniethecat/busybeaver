## Character Overview

The First Gentleman is one of the unnamed companions who join Charles Surface, Careless, and Sir Harry Bumper for the drinking party in Act III, Scene III (003-357–475). He appears alongside the Second Gentleman, and the two function as a comic double-act — egging on the revelry, supporting Careless's toasts and songs, and enthusiastically participating in the prank played upon Sir Oliver Surface (disguised as the moneylender "Mr. Premium"). He has seven speaking lines in total, making him the more vocal of the two unnamed gentlemen.

## Role in the Play

### The Drinking Companion

The First Gentleman's primary function is to sustain the atmosphere of convivial excess that defines Charles Surface's household. When Careless laments "the great Degeneracy of the age" (003-358) — that men "give into all the substantial Luxuries of the Table — and abstain from nothing but wine and wit" (003-362) — the First Gentleman raises a practical objection: "But what are they to do who love Play better than wine" (003-368). His question prompts Charles's spirited defence of drinking as essential to both gaming and love, setting the philosophical tone for the scene.

### The Toasting Game

When Charles calls for bumpers and proposes the toast "Maria — Maria —" (003-390), the First Gentleman interjects at 003-391. Though his exact line "Maria who?" is not separately indexed in the sentence corpus, the speech prefix confirms his interjection, which Charles immediately dismisses: "Oh, damn the Surname 'tis too formal to be register'd in Love's calendar" (003-392). The First Gentleman's deflating question serves a precise comic function: it undercuts Charles's romantic grandiosity with mundane curiosity, reminding the audience that Charles's famous toast is, to his drinking companions, merely another name in a long night of bumpers.

Shortly after, when Careless must supply a song, the First Gentleman reassures him: "Nay Never study, Careless — we'll stand to the Toast — tho' your mistress should want an eye — and you know you have a song will excuse you" (003-395–396). After Careless delivers his catalogue of toasts, the First Gentleman offers warm approval: "E' faith there's infinite charity in that song" (003-426). His appreciation of the song's democratic spirit of toasting all women regardless of age or appearance aligns him with the scene's ethos of genial excess.

### The Mr. Premium Prank

The First Gentleman is an eager participant in the scene's central comic situation: the arrival of Sir Oliver Surface disguised as the moneylender Mr. Premium, accompanied by the broker Moses. When Charles announces that "'tis a Jew and a Broker who are come by appointment" (003-431), the First Gentleman calls "Aye and the Broker too by all means" (003-434), urging that both be brought in. He specifically insists "Yes — yes Mr. Premium certainly" (003-439), unaware that the moneylender he is so keen to mock is in fact Charles's wealthy uncle. Later, when Careless demands that Mr. Premium drink a pint bumper, the First Gentleman echoes "A pint bumper, at least" (003-461), escalating the prank's intensity.

## Dramatic Function

The First Gentleman, together with the Second Gentleman, represents the anonymous social circle that surrounds a figure like Charles Surface. Never named, never individuated beyond their speech prefixes, they are the "gentlemen" of the stage direction — the collective voice of fashionable male society. Their function is choric: they approve, encourage, and amplify, creating the social pressure that drives the scene's comedy. Their eagerness to mock "Mr. Premium" sharpens the dramatic irony, since the audience knows the moneylender's true identity.

## Key References

The First Gentleman speaks at seven points in Act III, Scene III:
- Questions how those who love play can abstain from wine (003-368)
- Interjects during Charles's toast to Maria (003-391)
- Reassures Careless before the song: "we'll stand to the Toast" (003-395)
- Praises Careless's song: "infinite charity in that song" (003-426)
- Calls for the Broker to be brought in (003-434)
- Insists Mr. Premium should certainly join them (003-439)
- Demands "A pint bumper, at least" for Mr. Premium (003-461)

He exits with the other gentlemen as Careless leads them to the dice room: "[Exeunt SIR HARRY BUMPER and GENTLEMEN; CARELESS following]" (003-475).
