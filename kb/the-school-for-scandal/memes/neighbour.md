# Neighbour

The neighbour in The School for Scandal is not a figure of community but of surveillance. To live near someone in Sheridan's London is to be perpetually observed, judged, and reported upon.

## In The School for Scandal

The play opens with [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] asking Snake about "my neighbour, Sir Peter" (001-047) — a locution that collapses physical proximity into social intimacy. The neighbour is the person whose affairs one knows in detail but to whom one owes no loyalty. This is the paradox of the scandal economy: intimacy without obligation, knowledge without responsibility.

The neighbourhood itself becomes an extension of the scandal school. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] laments those in the "neighbourhood — who have contributed not" (002-388) to the general goodwill, measuring community by what it withholds rather than what it shares. The neighbour who does not participate in scandal is an anomaly; the neighbour who spreads it is the norm.

Sheridan's treatment of neighbourly relations is part of his larger critique of urban social life. In the country, neighbours are known and accountable; in London, they are proximate but anonymous — close enough to observe, distant enough to condemn. The scandal school flourishes in this gap between physical closeness and moral distance, turning the neighbour from a potential ally into a probable enemy.
