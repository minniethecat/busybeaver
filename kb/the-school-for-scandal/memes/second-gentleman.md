## Character Overview

The Second Gentleman is one of the unnamed companions who join Charles Surface, Careless, Sir Harry Bumper, and the First Gentleman for the drinking party in Act III, Scene III (003-357–475). With four speaking lines, he is the less vocal of the two unnamed gentlemen, but his contributions punctuate the scene's comedy at key moments. Like his companion, he serves to amplify the atmosphere of genial excess and to sharpen the dramatic irony of the Mr. Premium prank.

## Role in the Play

### The Voice of Cynical Realism

The Second Gentleman's first line establishes him as a voice of worldly scepticism within the drinking party. When Charles claims that a bottle of champagne ensures he never loses at gaming — "at least I never feel my losses which is exactly the same thing" (003-375–376) — the Second Gentleman responds with deflating pragmatism: "Aye that may be — but it is as impossible to follow wine and play as to unite Love and Politics" (003-377–378). His aphoristic turn of phrase introduces a note of cynical wisdom into the revelry, hinting that the gentleman's world is one of accepted contradictions: the ideal of combining pleasures is as elusive as reconciling passion with power.

### The Enthusiastic Audience

When Careless finishes his catalogue of toasts — a song that proposes drinking to every woman from "the maiden of bashful fifteen" to "the widow of fifty" (003-401–402) — the Second Gentleman supplies the applause: "Bravo Careless — Ther's Toast and Sentiment too" (003-425). His praise explicitly names the two qualities Careless has delivered: a toast (the social ritual) and a sentiment (the moral justification). This brief line functions as a miniature review, confirming for the audience that Careless's performance has succeeded on both counts.

### Escalating the Prank

As the scene shifts towards the entrance of Sir Oliver (disguised as Mr. Premium) and Moses, the Second Gentleman joins the chorus calling for the visitors to be brought up: "Yes yes the Jew and the Broker" (003-435). His most memorable contribution comes during the drinking contest forced upon the reluctant Sir Oliver. When Moses protests that "Mr. Premium's a gentleman" (003-463), the Second Gentleman escalates the mockery: "Give Moses a quart glass — this is mutiny, and a high contempt for the chair" (003-464–465). The line parodies naval discipline ("mutiny," "contempt for the chair") to comic effect, treating the drinking party as though it were a court-martial and Moses's polite objection as a capital offence against the sovereign authority of the toast-master.

## Dramatic Function

The Second Gentleman's dramatic role is to provide the second voice in the comic duet of unnamed gentlemen. Where the First Gentleman asks questions and makes demands, the Second Gentleman offers aphorisms and escalations. Together they create the sense of a cohesive social group — a "set" that moves, drinks, and mocks as one. Their anonymity is itself the point: in Charles Surface's world, individual identity matters less than participation in the collective ritual of drinking and wit.

## Key References

The Second Gentleman speaks at four points in Act III, Scene III:
- Observes the impossibility of following both wine and play, comparing it to uniting "Love and Politics" (003-377)
- Praises Careless's song: "Bravo Careless — Ther's Toast and Sentiment too" (003-425)
- Joins the call for the Jew and Broker to be brought in: "Yes yes the Jew and the Broker" (003-435)
- Demands a quart glass for Moses, framing his protest as "mutiny, and a high contempt for the chair" (003-464)

He exits with the other gentlemen when Careless leads them to the dice room: "[Exeunt SIR HARRY BUMPER and GENTLEMEN; CARELESS following]" (003-475).
