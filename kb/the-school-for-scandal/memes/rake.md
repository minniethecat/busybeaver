# Rake

The rake — the witty, sexually predatory, financially reckless gentleman — was a stock figure of Restoration and eighteenth-century comedy. Charles Surface is Sheridan's contribution to this tradition.

## The Restoration Rake

In Restoration comedy, the rake was typically the hero — his wit, energy, and sexual conquests were celebrated as expressions of aristocratic vitality. Figures like Dorimant in Etherege's The Man of Mode (1676) set the pattern: charming, amoral, and ultimately irresistible. But by Sheridan's time, the rake had become a problem. Sentimental comedy had replaced him with the man of feeling.

## Charles Surface as Reformed Rake

Sheridan solves the problem by giving Charles all the rake's surface characteristics — wit, extravagance, drinking, sexual reputation — while revealing, through the machinery of comic testing, that his heart is fundamentally good. Charles's rakehell manners are a style, not a substance. When Careless sings 'Here's to the maiden of bashful fifteen' (003-200), the song's libertine content is undercut by the genuine affection the men display.

## Moral Revision

Charles does not need to be reformed because he was never truly corrupt. The play's argument — a subtle but significant revision of the rake tradition — is that a man may be dissolute in manner and yet sound in character. The test of character is not sobriety but sincerity, not prudence but generosity.
