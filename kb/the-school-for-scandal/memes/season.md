# Season

The London Season — the period from late autumn to early summer when Parliament sat and fashionable society assembled — is the temporal frame of The School for Scandal.

## The Rhythms of Fashion

The Season governed the lives of the fashionable classes. Families came to London for the sitting of Parliament, the round of balls and assemblies, and the marriage market. In summer, they dispersed to country estates, spa towns, or continental travel. This rhythm — concentration in winter, dispersal in summer — created the conditions for scandal: fashionable society was a closed world for half the year, and in that hothouse atmosphere, reputations bloomed and withered with extraordinary speed.

## The Season in the Play

The play is set during the Season — the characters are in town, the social machinery is operating at full capacity, and the gossip circulates with seasonal intensity. Lady Teazle's absorption in 'the Fashionable World' is specifically her absorption in the Season's rituals: the morning visits, the evening assemblies, the opera, the park. Her country origins make her particularly susceptible to the Season's seductions.

## Temporal Pressure

Sheridan uses the Season to create temporal pressure. The events of the play unfold over a compressed period — a few days of intense social activity — and this compression gives the comedy its urgency. Reputations are made and broken in a week, and the Season's rhythm means that by summer, the damage will be done. The play's moral reckoning must happen now, before the fashionable world disperses and the scandal hardens into fact.
