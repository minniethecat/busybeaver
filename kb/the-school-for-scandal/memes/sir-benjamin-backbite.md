# Sir Benjamin Backbite

Sheridan's naming is at its most transparent with Sir Benjamin Backbite: he "bites" from behind, wounding reputations with verses and [[scandal-theme|gossip]] while maintaining the appearance of a gentleman. He is the play's purest comic creation — absurd, self-regarding, and entirely unaware of his own ridiculousness.

## Role in the Play

Sir Benjamin's dramatic function is comic relief with satirical edge. His inept pursuit of [[maria|Maria]] — "O here is that disagreeable lover of mine, Sir Benjamin Backbite" (001-141) — provides running comedy throughout the play's first acts. His "love-Elegies" (001-270) parody the sentimental poetry of the period, and his participation in the scandal-mongers' circle implicates literary culture in the play's critique of [[society|fashionable society]].

## Key Scenes

Sir Benjamin's [[love|courtship]] of [[maria|Maria]] (Act I) establishes his comic credentials. His [[scandal-theme|gossip]] with [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] and [[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]] demonstrates that even the play's fools are dangerous — his verses and his [[scandal-theme|scandal-mongering]] are two forms of the same impulse to wound.

## Relationships

- **[[maria|Maria]]**: object of comic [[love|courtship]]; [[maria|Maria]]'s evident contempt for Sir Benjamin's advances provides the audience with a reliable moral signal
- **[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]**: fellow scandal-monger; Sir Benjamin is a foot soldier in [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s army of [[scandal-theme|gossip]]
