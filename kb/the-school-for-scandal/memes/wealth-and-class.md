# Wealth and Class

Money flows through *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* in multiple currencies — [[fortune|inheritance]], [[debt|credit]], colonial fortune, dowry, debt. Each [[reputation|character]]'s [[family|relations]]hip to [[fortune|money]] defines their moral [[reputation|character]]: [[charles-surface|Charles]] dissipates his fortune with [[benevolence|generosity]], [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] hoards his with calculation, [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] distributes his with judgment.

## Overview

The play's financial architecture is centred on the Surface [[fortune|inheritance]]. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s colonial fortune — accumulated over sixteen years in the East Indies — will go to whichever nephew proves worthy. The testing that occupies Acts III and IV is thus both a moral investigation and a financial audit: which Surface brother deserves the fortune?

[[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance is inseparable from his [[benevolence|generosity]]. "Thoughtless enough to give [[fortune|money]] to poor [[family|relations]]" (003-027) — the charge against him is also his vindication. [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s prudence, by contrast, conceals a fundamental selfishness: when [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] tests him as "[[sir-oliver-surface|Mr. Stanley]]," [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s refusal to help a poor relation exposes the meanness beneath his respectable [[appearance|surface]].

## Key Scenes

### The Auction (Act IV, Scene 1)

[[charles-surface|Charles]]'s sale of the [[portrait|family portrait]]s is the play's most concentrated exploration of [[fortune|wealth]] and value. He sells everything — "unless you have a mind to the family" portraits (003-578) — except his uncle's. The refusal is the proof: some things are worth more than [[fortune|money]].

### The Moneylender Scenes (Act III)

[[moses|Moses]] and "[[sir-oliver-surface|Mr. Premium]]" ([[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]) represent the financial infrastructure that services the gentry's need for [[debt|credit]]. [[moses|Moses]]'s professional frankness — "I know [[fortune|money]] isn't to be bought without paying for it" (003-503) — is a comic antidote to the play's more elaborate financial [[appearance-vs-reality|deception]]s.

## Characters Associated

- **[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]**: the extravagant heir whose [[benevolence|generosity]] redeems his [[extravagance|prodigality]]
- **[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]**: the prudent heir whose selfishness condemns his respectability
- **[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]**: the colonial capitalist whose fortune funds the play's moral testing
- **[[moses|Moses]]**: the professional [[fortune|money]]lender whose candour about [[debt|credit]] contrasts with the play's social hypocrisies
