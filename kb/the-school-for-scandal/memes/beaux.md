# Beaux

The beau — the fashionable man who devotes himself to dress, manners, and social display — was a stock figure of eighteenth-century satire, and Sir Benjamin Backbite is Sheridan's contribution to the type.

## The Beau Type

The beau was the male counterpart to the coquette — a figure whose identity was entirely constructed from the materials of fashion. He dressed elaborately, spoke in the latest cant, composed bad verses, and devoted his life to the cultivation of surface. Restoration comedy had its fops (Sir Fopling Flutter in Etherege's The Man of Mode); Sheridan has his beaux.

## Sir Benjamin Backbite

Sir Benjamin is the play's beau par excellence: a would-be poet whose verses are weapons of social destruction, a gossip who dresses his malice in the language of wit, a suitor to Maria who is too absorbed in his own performance to notice that no one is listening. His name — Backbite — is the play's judgment on his type: the beau is not merely ridiculous but dangerous, his wit a form of wounding.

## The Beau as Social Critic

Sheridan's treatment of Sir Benjamin is more than caricature. The beau is also, in his way, a truth-teller — his verses, however malicious, often hit their mark. The play recognises that the beau is a product of the same social system that produces the hypocrite and the scandal-monger, and that all three types are symptoms of a culture that values surface over substance.
