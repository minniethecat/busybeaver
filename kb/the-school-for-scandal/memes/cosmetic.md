# Cosmetic

The opening stage direction of The School for Scandal places Lady Sneerwell 'at her dressing table' — an image that immediately establishes the play's central theme of appearance and reality.

## The Dressing Table

The dressing table was a loaded symbol in eighteenth-century culture. It was the site where women applied cosmetics — paints, powders, patches — to transform their appearance. Moralists condemned it as a site of deception; satirists mocked the 'painted lady' whose face was a work of artifice. Sheridan opens his play at the dressing table to signal that what follows will be an anatomy of deception in all its forms.

## Lady Sneerwell's Artifice

Lady Sneerwell's name itself is a cosmetic joke: she sneers well, she performs the elegant disdain of the fashionable lady, and her entire identity is a construction. Her dressing table is the site where she manufactures not only her own appearance but the appearance of others — she creates scandals as she creates her face, through the calculated application of false colour.

## The Natural vs. The Painted

The play consistently values the natural over the artificial. Charles is natural — impulsive, unstudied, incapable of performance. Joseph is artificial — calculated, studied, performing virtue at every turn. Lady Teazle is caught between the two poles, tempted by artifice but ultimately capable of returning to her natural self. The dressing table that opens the play is thus a visual thesis statement: this is a world of painted surfaces, and the play's business is to strip the paint away.
