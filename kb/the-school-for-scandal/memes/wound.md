# Wound

Wounding in The School for Scandal operates on two registers simultaneously: the literal wound of the duel and the metaphorical wounds inflicted by scandal. Sheridan uses the former to expose the absurdity of the latter.

## In The School for Scandal

The duel between [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] and [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] — or rather, the duel Sir Peter believes he has fought — produces a wound that "has double danger in it" (004-412). The phrase is carefully chosen: the wound endangers Sir Peter physically, but it also endangers the web of deception Joseph has constructed. A wound demands explanation, and explanation threatens exposure.

The play's most brilliant comic turn involves the wound's amplification through the scandal machinery. "Sir Peter's wound!" (005-189) becomes a contagion of misinformation: [[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]] hears of it and immediately embellishes, until the rumour claims Sir Peter is "dangerously wounded" (005-212). By the time the story reaches its apogee, the wound has migrated from a small-sword injury to a postman "wounded ... who was just coming to the house with a double letter from Northamptonshire" (005-234). Sheridan's point is clear: scandal inflates a wound the way it inflates every other piece of damaging information — exponentially, and with no regard for truth.

[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] emerges from the affair unwounded but morally vindicated. The wound that Sir Peter does not actually have becomes a test of character: Joseph's concern is performative, Charles's is genuine. The wound, in Sheridan's hands, is a diagnostic instrument — it reveals who cares and who merely pretends to.
