# Joseph Surface

Joseph Surface is Sheridan's masterpiece of [[reputation|character]] construction — the hypocrite who is also the play's most accomplished performer. He "passes for" (001-086–087) a man of sentiment, benevolence, and honour with everyone he meets, and the play's dramatic energy derives from the question of whether anyone will see through the [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] before it succeeds.

## Role in the Play

Joseph is the play's structural antagonist — not because he drives the action (that is [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s function) but because he embodies the vice that the play exists to anatomise. His name announces Sheridan's thesis: "Surface" denotes the gap between appearance and reality that the play systematically explores, and Joseph is the [[reputation|character]] in whom that gap is widest.

His method is the weaponisation of moral language. "[[wit|Wit]]h the assistance of his sentiments and hypocrisy he has brought [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] entirely in his interests" (001-092) — [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s assessment captures the precision of Joseph's technique. He does not merely deceive; he deploys the vocabulary of virtue as camouflage.

## The Psychology of the Hypocrite

Sheridan gives Joseph a psychology that is more sophisticated than mere villainy. Joseph does not simply lie — he inhabits a persona that is more appealing to himself than his true self would be. The "youthful Miracle of Prudence, good Sense, and Benevolence" (001-061) that the world sees is a creation Joseph has come to believe in, even as he acts in ways that contradict it. His hypocrisy is not cold calculation alone; it is self-deception married to calculation, each reinforcing the other.

This psychological complexity distinguishes Joseph from the simpler villains of sentimental comedy. He is not a stage Machiavel gloating over his schemes; he is a man who has constructed an identity out of borrowed moral language and now cannot escape it. When the screen falls and his duplicity is exposed, the collapse is not merely social but existential — the self he has performed ceases to exist, and what remains is only the "hypocritical Villain" (005-346) that Sir Peter names.

## Key Scenes

Joseph is introduced through others' testimony — [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s cool analysis (Act I, Scene 1) establishes his [[reputation|character]] before he speaks a line. This deferred entrance is strategic: we first learn what Joseph is, then watch him perform what he is not.

### The Library Scene (Act II, Scene 2)

Joseph's first appearance establishes his method. Seated among his books, he receives [[maria|Maria]] with professions of moral seriousness that are transparently strategic. He speaks of "sentiment" and "delicacy" while his eyes remain fixed on her fortune. The scene anatomises the sentimental vocabulary — showing how a language designed to express genuine feeling can be deployed to disguise its absence.

### The Seduction Scene (Act IV)

His attempted seduction of [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] (Act IV) is the play's most dangerous scene. "My life on Joseph's [[honour|Honour]]" (002-443), [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] has said — and Joseph's simultaneous pursuit of [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] and [[maria|Maria]] exposes the full scope of his duplicity. The scene's dramatic irony is brutal: Sir Peter is in the room, hidden, while Joseph attempts to seduce his wife using the language of sentimental gallantry.

### The Screen Scene — Undoing

The screen scene is Joseph's undoing. "Re-enter JOSEPH SURFACE" (004-614) — the stage direction, after the screen has fallen and the [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]] is exposed, marks the end of his [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]]. What is remarkable about Joseph's exposure is its comprehensiveness: every relationship he has cultivated, every persona he has constructed, collapses simultaneously. Sir Peter, Lady Teazle, Sir Oliver, Maria — each discovers a different dimension of his fraud, and the accumulation is annihilating.

## Joseph and the Sentimental Tradition

Joseph's character is Sheridan's most sustained attack on the cult of sensibility that dominated mid-eighteenth-century literature. The "Man of Sentiment" — the figure who performs refined feeling as a sign of moral superiority — was a cultural type that writers from Sterne to Mackenzie had celebrated. Sheridan's insight is that the performance of feeling is structurally indistinguishable from the experience of feeling, and that a culture that rewards the performance will inevitably produce performers who feel nothing.

Joseph is not merely a hypocrite in the moral sense; he is a critique of a literary and cultural fashion. His library, his "moral" books, his professions of benevolence — these are the props of a character type that Sheridan's audience would have recognized from a hundred sentimental novels. The play's satirical force derives from its demonstration that the conventions of sensibility are, in practice, a toolkit for predators.

## Relationships

- **[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]**: brother and rival; Joseph's prudence is the foil to [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s extravagance, but the play's moral arithmetic favours the wastrel over the hypocrite. The brothers are a moral experiment: what happens when identical circumstances produce opposite characters?
- **[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]]**: dupe and benefactor; Joseph's manipulation of [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] is his most sustained and successful [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]]. Sir Peter's trust is the wager Joseph loses
- **[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]**: target of seduction; Joseph's attempt to seduce [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] while courting [[maria|Maria]] is the duplicity that destroys him. Lady Teazle's resistance — and her eventual exposure of him — is the play's turning point
- **[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]**: co-conspirator; their alliance is purely instrumental, and each would betray the other if it served their interests. Lady Sneerwell's clear-eyed assessment of Joseph — she is never deceived by him — is the play's backhanded tribute to her intelligence
- **[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]**: the uncle Joseph fails to deceive. Sir Oliver's tests expose what Joseph's performances conceal

## Notable Lines

Joseph's character is defined by what others say of him. "With Sir Peter, and indeed with all his acquaintance, He passes for a youthful Miracle of Prudence — good sense and Benevolence" (001-086–088) — the verb "passes for" is Sheridan's most devastating indictment.

> "Good Mr. Hypocrite by your leave I will speak for myself." (004-648)

The line is Lady Teazle's, but the epithet is Joseph's epitaph. To be named "Mr. Hypocrite" in a play about hypocrisy is to be identified with the vice the play exists to condemn.
