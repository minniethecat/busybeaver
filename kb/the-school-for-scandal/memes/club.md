# Club

The gentlemen's club, though never directly shown on stage, is a significant off-stage space in The School for Scandal — a male counterpart to Lady Sneerwell's scandalous drawing room.

## The Club as Social Institution

Eighteenth-century London was thick with clubs — political, literary, convivial, scandalous. They were spaces of male sociability where politics was debated, business was transacted, and reputations were made and broken. Sheridan, himself a member of the famous Literary Club (alongside Johnson, Burke, and Goldsmith), knew club culture from the inside.

## Club Talk in the Play

The club is referenced throughout the play as the place where male gossip circulates. Sir Peter mentions 'the Club' as a source of his information; Sir Benjamin Backbite's verses are recited there; the drinking scene at Charles's house (003-160) is a kind of private club meeting. Sheridan suggests that male sociability is as gossip-driven as female — the club is Lady Sneerwell's drawing room with better wine.

## Gendered Spaces

The play maps its moral geography onto gendered spaces. Lady Sneerwell's dressing room is the site of scandal's manufacture; the drawing room is the site of its circulation; the library (Joseph's, then Sir Peter's) is the site of its exposure; and the club is the site of its consumption. Each space carries a different moral valence, and the characters' movement between them traces their moral trajectories.
