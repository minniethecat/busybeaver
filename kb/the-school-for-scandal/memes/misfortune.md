# Misfortune

Misfortune in The School for Scandal is a diagnostic instrument — it reveals who has charity and who has only the appearance of it. The play repeatedly tests its characters by how they respond to the undeserved suffering of others.

## In The School for Scandal

The scandal school's cruelty is most visible in its treatment of misfortune. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] remarks that "her bulk is her misfortune" (002-209) — a cattish observation that turns physical appearance into a moral failing. The school operates on the principle that misfortune is always someone's fault, and therefore always fair game for ridicule.

[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] understands misfortune differently. He is described as "ruined by a series of undeserved misfortunes" (003-007), and his response to his own financial distress is to help those even worse off — sending money to a poor relation when he has almost nothing himself. His instinct is to "snatch misfortune and imprudence from the rapacious" (003-137) grasp of poverty, a generosity that [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]] finds more persuasive than any profession of virtue.

The play's treatment of misfortune maps onto its larger moral scheme. The scandalous characters exploit misfortune for entertainment; the sentimental characters profess sympathy for it; [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] alone acts to relieve it. Sheridan's point is that the only valid response to another's suffering is practical help — everything else is self-regarding performance.
