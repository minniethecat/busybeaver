# Surgeon

The surgeon appears briefly in the aftermath of the threatened duel between Sir Peter and Charles Surface, serving both a practical and a symbolic function.

## The Wound

Sir Peter's wound — sustained in the confusion of the screen scene, not from an actual duel — is a comic injury that nevertheless carries symbolic weight. The wound is minor but genuine; it marks Sir Peter as a man who has been hurt, literally and figuratively, by the events he has witnessed. The surgeon who tends him is the agent of healing, restoring not just the body but, by implication, the social fabric that has been torn.

## Healing and Resolution

The surgeon's appearance signals the play's turn toward resolution. After the violent exposures of the screen scene, the healing arts are required — for Sir Peter's wound, for the Teazles' marriage, for the Surface family's reputation. The surgeon is a minor figure, but his presence is part of the play's movement from rupture to repair, from scandal to truth.
