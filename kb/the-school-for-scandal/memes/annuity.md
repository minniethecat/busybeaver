The annuity — a financial contract providing a fixed annual income for life — might seem an unlikely plot device for a comedy of manners, yet in *The School for Scandal* it serves as a precise instrument of character revelation. The attempt by Charles Surface's servant Trip to raise money by selling an annuity exposes the financial desperation that pervades the Surface household, while Sir Oliver's response — part amusement, part dismay — registers the economic recklessness that defines Charles's world.

## In The School for Scandal

The annuity scheme surfaces in Act III when Trip, Charles's footman, approaches Moses the moneylender with a proposal: "could get it me by way of annuity" (003-330). The absurdity of a footman attempting to raise capital on an annuity — a financial instrument typically associated with the landed gentry and their estates — produces Sir Oliver's incredulous laugh: "a Footman raise money by annuity" (003-331). The joke cuts two ways: it mocks Trip's pretensions while simultaneously revealing that in Charles's household, even the servants have learned the master's habits of living on credit.

The annuity thus functions as an index of social disorder. In a properly ordered household, financial instruments serve the preservation of estate and family; in Charles's establishment, they have become a desperate expedient, a sign that consumption has outstripped income at every level. When Moses reminds Trip "don't forget the annuity" (003-353), the line has the quality of a refrain: the annuity is the phantom solution to a problem that spending itself creates.

The episode is later reported to Sir Oliver by Moses: "they are now planning an annuity Business" (004-207). By this point, the annuity has ceased to be a specific financial instrument and has become a synecdoche for Charles's entire mode of life — improvident, charming, and unsustainable.

## Historical Context

Annuities were a significant feature of eighteenth-century English finance, used both as retirement instruments and as means of raising capital through the sale of future income streams. They were particularly associated with the financial revolution that followed the establishment of the Bank of England and the growth of the national debt. Sheridan, who knew something of debt himself, understood the annuity as a middle-class financial tool that, in the wrong hands, became a mechanism for converting future security into present pleasure. The comedy of Trip's annuity scheme is the comedy of financial literacy meeting financial illiteracy — a servant who has learned the vocabulary of capital without understanding its consequences.
