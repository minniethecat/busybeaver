# Wager

Wagering was a pervasive social practice in eighteenth-century London, and it surfaces repeatedly in The School for Scandal as a marker of character and class.

## The Gambling Culture

The eighteenth century was an age of extraordinary gambling — on cards, on horses, on elections, on the longevity of public figures, on anything that could be made the subject of a bet. The gaming table was a leveler: fortunes were won and lost in an evening, and a man's character was revealed by how he bore his losses. Charles Surface's circle participates in this culture as a matter of course.

## The Wager as Test

Sheridan uses the wager as a minor moral test. When 'Mr. Premium' (Sir Oliver in disguise) offers Charles money in exchange for the family portraits, the transaction is framed as a kind of wager — Charles bets that the money will relieve 'Stanley's' distress, and Sir Oliver bets that Charles will default. The wager, like the disguise, is a mechanism for revealing character: how a man bets, and on what terms, reveals his nature.

## Risk and Morality

The play's treatment of wagering is morally ambivalent. Charles's financial recklessness is a fault, but it is also, paradoxically, the source of his moral strength — his willingness to risk everything is what enables him to give everything. Joseph, who never risks anything, also never gives anything. Sheridan thus complicates the conventional moralising about gambling, suggesting that the willingness to risk may be a form of generosity.
