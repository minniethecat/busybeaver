## Definition

In eighteenth-century England, an heir was the person legally entitled to inherit property, titles, or wealth upon the death of a relative — typically through primogeniture, the system by which the eldest son inherited the bulk of an estate. The status of "heir" was not merely legal but social, carrying expectations of conduct, obligation, and family duty.

## In The School for Scandal

### Inheritance as Plot Engine

The question of who will inherit Sir Oliver Surface's fortune drives the entire action of the play. Sir Oliver's return from the East Indies after sixteen years — unannounced, in disguise — is motivated by his desire to test his two nephews before deciding how to dispose of his wealth. His exclamation "Charles! — you are my Heir" (004-057) at the play's climax resolves the central dramatic question.

At the play's outset, the audience learns that the Surface brothers "the eldest possess'd" of their father's estate (001-056), a reference to Joseph inheriting the bulk of the family property. Charles, as the younger brother, has been left dependent on the prospect of Sir Oliver's favour — a classic comic setup that Sheridan uses to examine the moral claims each brother has on inheritance.

### The Heir-Loom

In the famous auction scene (Act IV), Charles offers to sell the family portraits but refuses to part with Sir Oliver's — protesting that "it has been in the family" like "an heir-loom." The term is ironically apt: the portrait is literally all Charles possesses of his uncle's favour, and his refusal to sell it is the act that convinces Sir Oliver of his worthiness as heir. The word "heir-loom" — a pun that Sheridan places deliberately — collapses the distance between object and inheritor: the portrait is what the heir keeps, and keeping it is what makes him the heir.

### Moral vs Legal Inheritance

Sheridan constructs a contrast between legal inheritance (Joseph's position as elder son) and moral inheritance (Charles's demonstration of genuine affection and generosity). Joseph's careful cultivation of a reputation for virtue is revealed as a strategy for securing Sir Oliver's fortune, while Charles's apparent recklessness masks a fundamentally honest nature. The play thus questions whether inheritance should follow bloodline or character.

### Heirship and the Comic Structure

The heir plot participates in a venerable tradition of eighteenth-century comedy. From Congreve to Goldsmith, the question of who will inherit was a reliable engine of dramatic action, capable of generating misunderstandings, disguises, and reversals. Sheridan's innovation is to bind the inheritance question to a test of character: Sir Oliver's disguises (as Mr. Premium the moneylender and Stanley the poor relation) subject each nephew to a trial that inheritance law alone could never provide. The legal heir is determined by birth; the moral heir must be discovered through performance.

## Key Passages

The inheritance motif surfaces in the play's opening scene. Lady Sneerwell's interest in Charles, we learn, is partly financial: she has designs on securing his favour should he come into wealth. The gossip network that the play anatomizes is, at its base, an arena for competing claims on patronage and inheritance.

Sir Oliver's testing of the brothers culminates in the auction scene, where the portrait — "an heir-loom" that Charles refuses to sell — becomes the instrument of moral discrimination. The scene is a tour de force of dramatic economy: a single gesture (keeping the portrait) simultaneously proves Charles's filial devotion, exposes Joseph's mercenary character, and resolves the inheritance plot.

## Historical Context

Primogeniture was the dominant inheritance practice in eighteenth-century England, concentrating family wealth in the eldest son. Younger sons faced the prospect of making their own way — through the professions, the military, colonial ventures, or advantageous marriage. Sir Oliver's journey to the East Indies reflects this economic reality: colonial wealth offered an alternative path to fortune for those without inheritance.

Sheridan himself knew this world intimately. His own financial management was notoriously precarious, and Drury Lane Theatre, which he owned, was a high-stakes commercial enterprise. The play's treatment of inheritance anxiety reflects the lived experience of Georgian London's propertied classes.
