# Love

Love in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* is what survives the satire. Sheridan subjects every other value — [[reputation]], [[sentiment]], [[honour]], [[fortune]] — to comic dissection; love is the one thing the play exempts. This is not sentimentality but comic structure: the play that exposes the counterfeiting of every other social currency must leave one currency intact, or comedy becomes tragedy.

## Love Tested

### The Marriage Quarrel

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] is the play's sustained investigation of love under pressure. Their quarrels — "[[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] — [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] I'll not bear it!" (002-001) — are comic, but Sheridan gives them emotional weight. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s genuine affection coexists with genuine frustration; [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s genuine wit coexists with genuine fondness:

> "Come, come, Sir Peter, you love her, notwithstanding your." (001-412)

The interruption — [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] cuts off the sentence before it can name what he loves her "notwith[[reputation|standing]]" — preserves the [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]]'s privacy. Love, in this treatment, is what survives the scrutiny that comedy directs at everything else. The play exposes [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s foolishness, [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s extravagance, their mutual misunder[[reputation|standing]] — and then shows them reconciled, because love is what the exposure cannot destroy.

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s declaration — "I love her or I should never bear all this" (001-401) — is the play's definitive statement on marital love. It is not romantic transport but endurance: love is what enables him to bear what he could not otherwise bear. The formulation is [[reputation|character]]istically unsentimental, and [[reputation|character]]istically true.

### Charles and Maria

[[charles-surface|Charles]] and [[maria|Maria]]'s courtship is the play's normative love plot: the deserving young couple whose union is blocked by villainy and enabled by investigation. [[charles-surface|Charles]] loves [[maria|Maria]] for herself — "His own Heart" (005-570), [[maria|Maria]] insists, knows the cause of his devotion — not for her [[fortune]]. [[maria|Maria]] loves [[charles-surface|Charles]] despite his reputation. Their love is the simplest element in the play's moral economy, and its simplicity is functional: the play needs a fixed point from which the anatomy of everything else can proceed.

[[maria|Maria]]'s steadfastness is the quiet force that opposes [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s manipulation. She will not be married to [[joseph-surface|Joseph]], and her refusal — culminating in [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s urging to "speak your sentiments — you know — we are going to be happy" (005-561) — reclaims the language of [[sentiment]] from the hypocrites and restores it to the lovers.

### Joseph's Counterfeit Love

[[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s professions of love for [[maria|Maria]] are transparently strategic — he pursues her [[wealth-and-class|fortune]]. His simultaneous attempt to seduce [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] while courting [[maria|Maria]] exposes love's counterfeiting in its boldest form. [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s love is entirely instrumental; he wants what love can get him ([[fortune|money]], status, conquest) without [[sentiment|feeling]] what love requires. His sentimental [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] — the language of refined [[sentiment|feeling]] deployed against [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] — is love's vocabulary emptied of love's substance.

## Love in the Moral Economy

Sheridan structures the play's moral economy around love as the ultimate test of character. The Surface brothers are distinguished not by their financial conduct — both are, in different ways, failures by the standards of prudence — but by their capacity for genuine love. Charles loves Maria; Joseph loves no one. This is the play's definitive moral distinction, and it is characteristic of Sheridan's comic vision that the capacity to love matters more than the capacity to manage money.

The test is not merely asserted but dramatised. Charles's refusal to sell his uncle's portrait — the act that vindicates him — is an act of love, not of prudence. It violates every principle of financial rationality; it is precisely the kind of gesture that the "prudent" Joseph would never make. And yet it is the gesture that saves Charles, because it reveals a loyalty that the calculus of self-interest cannot account for. Sheridan's argument is that love — genuine, uncalculated attachment — is the one human capacity that the machinery of hypocrisy and self-interest cannot simulate.

## Love and the Sentimental Tradition

Sheridan's treatment of love engages critically with the eighteenth-century cult of sensibility. The sentimental novel — Richardson's *Pamela*, Sterne's *A Sentimental Journey*, Mackenzie's *The Man of Feeling* — had elevated refined feeling to the status of moral proof. The person of sensibility demonstrated virtue through the intensity and delicacy of their emotional responses.

Sheridan's scepticism about this tradition is embodied in Joseph Surface, who deploys the vocabulary of sensibility with precision and feels nothing. But the play does not reject sensibility outright; it relocates it. Charles and Maria's love, Sir Peter's devotion — these are genuine feelings that the play validates. Sheridan's position is not anti-sentimental but selectively sentimental: love is the one feeling that cannot be counterfeited, because the counterfeit is always eventually exposed by the behaviour love requires.

## Love and the Comic Resolution

The play's resolution is a triumph of love over its obstacles: [[charles-surface|Charles]] and [[maria|Maria]] are united, [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] are reconciled. Sheridan's comic optimism — the belief that love can survive the School that threatens it — is the play's deepest argument. The scandal-mongers disperse, the hypocrite is exposed, and what remains is love that has been tested and found genuine.

> "May your love for each other never know abatement." (005-646)

The final blessing speaks to the audience as much as to the [[reputation|character]]s. After a play that has anatomised the counterfeiting of every social value, the blessing on love is Sheridan's affirmation that not everything can be counterfeited. Love, in the play's final accounting, is what comedy preserves when satire has done its work.
