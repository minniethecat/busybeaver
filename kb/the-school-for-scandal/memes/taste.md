# Taste

Taste in *The School for Scandal* is at once a genuine faculty of discernment and a weapon of social exclusion. To possess taste is to belong to the inner circle of London's fashionable world; to lack it is to be marked as provincial, outmoded, or — worst of all — laughable. Sheridan explores taste as a concept that the fashionable invoke to justify their choices and as a screen behind which conspicuous consumption proceeds unchecked.

## Taste as Social Weapon

The most concentrated debate about taste occurs in the quarrel between Sir Peter and Lady Teazle in Act 2. When Sir Peter complains of his wife's extravagance, she retorts "I'm sure I'm not more extravagant than a woman of Fashion ought to be" (002-016). Sir Peter's reply — "Aye there again — Taste! Zounds Madam you had no Taste when you married me!" (002-076) — reveals the term's double edge: taste is what the upwardly mobile claim in order to justify expenditure, and what their critics deploy to remind them of humbler origins. Sir Peter's follow-up, "I should never pretend to Taste again I allow" (002-079), is irony of the bitterest kind: he has purchased his wife's social ascent and now finds himself excluded from the very standards of judgement his money enabled.

## The Country-to-Town Arc

Lady Teazle's transformation from country squire's daughter to woman of "Taste — Spirit, and Politeness" (003-359) is the play's central case study in the acquisition of taste. Sir Peter recalls her former life — "sitting at your tambour in a pretty figured linen gown — with a Bunch of Keys at your side" (002-032) — and contrasts it with her present state: "no one more extravagantly in the Fashion" (002-046). The play's implication is that taste, like fashion, is learned performance rather than innate refinement. Lady Teazle has been an apt pupil, but the lesson may cost her more than tuition: it nearly costs her marriage and her honour.

## Taste and Commerce

Charles Surface's auction scene (Act 4) introduces a different model of taste — one grounded in connoisseurship rather than fashion. His family portraits, including the lineage he is willing to sell, provoke from Sir Oliver (disguised as Premium) the observation: "taste for old paintings, egad" (003-580). Charles's taste is discriminating where Lady Teazle's is merely acquisitive, and this distinction counts in his favour when the play's moral reckoning arrives.

## Taste as Moral Metaphor

Sheridan ultimately treats taste as a metaphor for moral judgement. Those who lack taste in Sheridan's scheme are not merely unfashionable — they are, like [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]], aesthetically and morally fraudulent. The play's epilogue registers the transience of "the transient hour of fashion" (005-700); taste, by implication, is equally fleeting, but the judgement it metaphorises — the ability to distinguish the genuine from the counterfeit — endures beyond the final curtain.
