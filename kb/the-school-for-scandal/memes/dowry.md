# Dowry

The dowry — the portion a bride brought to a marriage — was a central institution of eighteenth-century marriage, and Maria's fortune is the economic engine of the play's romantic plot.

## The Marriage Economy

Among the propertied classes, marriage was a financial transaction as well as a personal one. A bride's portion could range from a few hundred pounds to tens of thousands, and the size of the portion determined the match she could make. Maria's fortune — the exact amount is never specified, but it is clearly substantial — makes her the object of both brothers' pursuit.

## Maria's Agency

Sheridan gives Maria a degree of agency unusual in stage heiresses. She knows she is being pursued for her money and resists the mercenary suitor (Joseph) in favour of the man who loves her for herself (Charles). Her choice is the play's endorsement of love over interest, of personal knowledge over financial calculation.

## The Portion as Test

Maria's fortune functions as a moral test. Joseph's pursuit of it reveals his mercenary nature; Charles's indifference to it (he loves Maria regardless of her fortune) reveals his sincerity. The dowry is thus another of Sheridan's instruments for distinguishing appearance from reality — what characters want reveals who they are.
