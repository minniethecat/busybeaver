# Comedy of Manners

Comedy of manners is the Restoration and eighteenth-century theatrical tradition to which The School for Scandal belongs. Its subject is the behaviour of fashionable society — its codes, its hypocrisies, its wit, and its follies.

## Genre Conventions

The comedy of manners typically features: a witty, urban setting; characters defined by their social affectations (the fop, the coquette, the would-be wit); a plot driven by intrigue, disguise, and sexual pursuit; and a moral resolution that rewards sincerity over artifice. Its ancestors include Molière's comedies of character and the Restoration comedies of Etherege, Wycherley, and Congreve.

## Sheridan's Innovation

Sheridan inherits the genre's machinery but refines its morality. Where Restoration comedy often celebrates libertinism and treats marriage as a trap, Sheridan insists on genuine virtue. Charles Surface is a rake, but he is a rake with a heart — his goodness is impulsive rather than calculated, and this distinction is crucial to the play's moral scheme. Sheridan's innovation is to use the comedy of manners' own devices (disguise, intrigue, witty dialogue) to expose the emptiness of mere manners and affirm the value of sincere feeling.

## Against Sentimental Comedy

Sheridan wrote The School for Scandal partly as a riposte to sentimental comedy — the rival eighteenth-century genre that replaced wit with pathos and moralising. Joseph Surface, who can produce a 'sentiment' for every occasion but cannot perform a single generous act, is Sheridan's satirical portrait of the sentimental hero. The play's argument is that true virtue consists in action, not in fine feelings, and that comedy's proper business is to laugh the hypocrite off the stage rather than to weep over his redemption.
