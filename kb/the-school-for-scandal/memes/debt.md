# Debt

Debt in *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* is the material form of moral obligation. [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]'s financial debts are visible — he owes [[fortune|money]] to everyone — while [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s moral debts are invisible — he owes honesty to everyone he has deceived. Sheridan uses the language of debt to make a moral argument: what you owe, and to whom, and whether you acknowledge it, is the measure of your [[reputation|character]].

## Debt as Moral Accounting

### Charles's Visible Debts

[[charles-surface|Charles]] is introduced as "an Extravagant young Fellow" (001-058) whose debts are the visible evidence of his vice. The auction scene in Act IV literalises his indebtedness: he is selling the [[portrait|family portrait]]s to pay what he owes. [[moses|Moses]] the [[fortune|money]]lender reports that [[charles-surface|Charles]] has already sold "all the family plate" — the material [[fortune|inheritance]] has been converted to cash and consumed.

Yet [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s [[family|relations]]hip to debt is morally legible. He acknowledges what he owes; he sells his own possessions rather than defraud others; he refuses to sell the one possession — his uncle's portrait — that represents a debt of [[love|affection]] rather than finance. [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s financial debts are real, but they are not the sum of his [[reputation|character]]:

> "I'll pay his debts and his Benevolences too." (004-194)

[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s decision — to pay [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s debts — transforms debt from accusation into absolution. The debts are paid, and what remains is the [[reputation|character]] that the debts, in the process of being incurred and acknowledged, have revealed.

### Joseph's Invisible Debts

[[joseph-surface|Joseph]] has no financial debts — he is prudent, solvent, respectable. His debts are moral: he owes [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]] to [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]], fidelity to [[maria|Maria]], honesty to [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]], gratitude to his uncle. These debts are invisible — they do not appear on any balance sheet — but they are the debts the play's moral economy cares about.

[[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s refusal to "lend" to his uncle ([[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]]d as Stanley) is the scene in which his moral indebtedness becomes visible. He claims to have already given; he has given nothing. The financial debt he refuses is the visible form of the moral debts he has been accumulating throughout the play. When [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] reveals himself, [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s moral [[ruin|bankruptcy]] is exposed: he is indebted to everyone and has acknowledged nothing.

## Debt and the Language of Obligation

Sheridan's characters use the vocabulary of debt to describe relationships that are not financial. Sir Peter speaks of being "in my debt for the attempt" (005-654); Lady Teazle declares she "owe[s] him none" in reference to Joseph (004-663); Charles toasts "Gratitude for twelve thousand pounds" (005-080). The financial metaphor colonises the language of feeling: gratitude is a debt of the heart, injury is a debt to be repaid, and forgiveness is the discharge of an emotional obligation. Sheridan's point is not merely stylistic — the ubiquity of financial language in emotional contexts reflects an eighteenth-century society in which market relations had penetrated every sphere of life. When his characters speak of feeling in the language of finance, they are registering a historical truth: the credit economy had made debt the master metaphor for obligation of every kind.

## Debt and the Comic Resolution

The play's resolution is a discharge of debts. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] pays [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s financial debts and forgives his moral ones; [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] forgives [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]]'s emotional debts; [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s debts are called in and he is excluded. The comic pattern — debts incurred, tested, discharged — mirrors the play's larger structure of appearance and revelation.

> "I am in my debt for the attempt — but deserve my gratitude." (005-654)

The speaker acknowledges a debt ("in my debt") and simultaneously converts it into an obligation of gratitude ("deserve my gratitude"). The language of finance becomes the language of [[family|relations]]hip; debt, in the play's final accounting, is not an ending but a beginning — the ground on which new obligations can be built.

## Debt, Honour, and the Aristocratic Ethos

The play's treatment of debt engages with a live eighteenth-century debate about the relationship between debt and honour. The aristocratic ethos regarded debt as a mark of status — a gentleman borrowed freely and paid when convenient — while the emerging bourgeois morality treated debt as a failure of character. Charles Surface embodies the aristocratic position: his debts are extensive but he is not ashamed of them, and his refusal to sell his uncle's portrait demonstrates that even an indebted gentleman observes certain limits. Joseph's bourgeois prudence, by contrast, is revealed as a mask for deeper moral failure. Sheridan's comedy does not resolve this debate so much as exploit its contradictions: Charles's aristocratic debts are more forgivable than Joseph's bourgeois solvency, but only because Charles, unlike Joseph, acknowledges what he owes.

Sheridan's own biography — he was chronically in debt, pursued by creditors, and yet maintained a public reputation as a gentleman and a wit — suggests that the play's treatment of debt draws on personal experience as much as dramatic convention. The playwright who wrote Charles Surface's defence of the indebted libertine was himself a debtor who understood that credit was as much a matter of performance as of accounting.

## Debt and the Eighteenth-Century Context

Sheridan wrote in a period when debt was a central social fact. The eighteenth-century credit economy — the system of borrowing, lending, and reputation that made commerce possible — made debt a matter of [[reputation|character]] as much as finance. To be in debt was to have one's moral [[reputation|standing]] questioned; to be solvent was to be respectable. Sheridan's play exploits this connection, using debt as a moral diagnostic that reveals what [[reputation|character]]s truly owe each other.

The colonial dimension is present but unexplored: [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s [[wealth-and-class|fortune]], which pays [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s debts, comes from the East Indies. The debts of empire — the [[fortune|wealth]] extracted from British colonies that funds the play's comic resolution — are the invisible foundation on which the visible economy of debt and [[reputation|character]] rests.
