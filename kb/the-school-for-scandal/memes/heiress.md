# Heiress

Maria's status as an heiress is the economic engine of the play's romantic plot: both Surface brothers pursue her, but for different reasons.

## The Marriage Market

In eighteenth-century England, marriage among the propertied classes was a commercial transaction as much as a romantic one. An heiress was the most valuable commodity on the marriage market — her fortune could rescue a bankrupt estate, fund a political career, or sustain a life of fashion. Sheridan's audience understood immediately what was at stake when Lady Sneerwell observes that Joseph 'certainly wants the money' (001-180).

## Maria's Agency

Sheridan gives Maria more agency than the typical stage heiress. She consistently rejects Joseph — despite Lady Sneerwell's pressure and Sir Peter's preference — because she sees through his performance. Her love for Charles, whom she has known since childhood, is based on knowledge of his character rather than calculation of his prospects.

## The Fortune Plot

The fortune plot intersects with the play's broader moral concerns. Joseph wants Maria's money; Charles wants Maria herself. This distinction — between mercenary and genuine attachment — mirrors the distinction between hypocrisy and sincerity that structures the entire play.
