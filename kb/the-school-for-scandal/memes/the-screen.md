# The Screen

The screen scene (Act IV, Scene III) is the most celebrated single scene in The School for Scandal and one of the great comic climaxes in English drama.

## Construction

The scene is a masterpiece of dramatic engineering. Joseph Surface, attempting to seduce Lady Teazle in his library, hears Sir Peter announced and hides her behind a screen. Sir Peter, believing Joseph to be a man of honour, confides his marital anguish and his suspicions about Lady Teazle and Charles. When Charles unexpectedly arrives, Sir Peter — curious to hear the brothers' conversation — hides in a closet. Lady Teazle is behind the screen, Sir Peter is in the closet, and neither knows the other is present. The audience, uniquely privileged, watches the layers of dramatic irony accumulate.

## The Revelation

Charles, accused by Joseph of having designs on Lady Teazle, protests his innocence with characteristic vehemence. When the screen is thrown down (the precise mechanism is debated — Sheridan's stage direction is ambiguous), Lady Teazle is revealed. Sir Peter emerges from the closet. The exposure is total: Joseph's seduction of his friend's wife, Lady Teazle's folly, and the precise architecture of the conspiracy are all laid bare in a single motion.

## Theatrical Significance

The screen is a physical emblem of the play's central theme: the concealment and revelation of true character. Every character in the scene is hidden — literally (behind screen, in closet) or figuratively (behind masks of virtue, sentiment, or innocence). The fall of the screen is the fall of pretence itself, and the play's remaining action is devoted to the restoration of moral clarity that this moment makes possible.
