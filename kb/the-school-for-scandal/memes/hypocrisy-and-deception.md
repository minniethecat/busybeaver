# Hypocrisy and Deception

While [[appearance-vs-reality|appearance and reality]] addresses the social dimension of masking, hypocrisy in The School for Scandal has a specifically moral charge. Sheridan targets the character who uses the language of virtue to disguise predatory intentions. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is not merely a dissembler — he is a moral hypocrite, and the play traces his exposure with the precision of a theatrical prosecution.

## The Anatomy of a Hypocrite

[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is introduced through others' descriptions before he appears. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] reveals his method: "He has been obliged to mask his Pretensions — and profit by my Assistance" (001-108). The vocabulary — "mask," "Pretensions," "profit" — establishes hypocrisy as calculated performance for material gain.

The play distinguishes Joseph's hypocrisy from other forms of deception. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] declares "I am no Hypocrite to deny the satisfaction I reap" (001-039) — she is honest about her dishonesty, which marks her as morally superior to Joseph in the play's calculus. The truly dangerous figure is not the open scandal-monger but the man who "passes for a youthful Miracle of Prudence, good Sense, and Benevolence" (001-061) while being none of these things.

## Key Scenes

### The Screen Scene (Act IV)

The screen scene is the play's great exposure of hypocrisy. [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] confronts [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] with the accusation that gives the scene its moral force: "Good Mr. Hypocrite by your leave I will speak for myself" (004-648). The epithet lands with precision because the preceding action has demonstrated its truth — Joseph has simultaneously pursued a married woman and professed devotion to virtue.

Joseph's attempt to seduce [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] — "seduced by his insidious arguments — and pretended Passion" (004-653) — reveals the mechanism of his hypocrisy. He uses the language of sentiment and feeling to disguise desire, weaponising the moral vocabulary he publicly champions.

### The Discovery (Act V)

The final act completes the exposure. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]] discovers that Joseph "pretended honourable addresses to his ward" (004-665) while scheming for her fortune. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s verdict — "a hypocritical Villain!" (005-346) — carries the weight of cumulative revelation. Hypocrisy in this play is not a static trait but a practice that, when exposed, destroys the practitioner.

## Joseph Surface and the Performance of Sentiment

Joseph's hypocrisy operates through the vocabulary of [[sentiment-and-sensibility|sentiment]], the eighteenth-century cult of refined feeling that Sheridan both satirises and exploits. Joseph's opening exchange with [[maria|Maria]] in Act II establishes his method: he speaks the language of sensibility — of delicate feeling and moral refinement — while his eyes remain fixed on Maria's fortune. The irony is structural: the very vocabulary that should signal sincerity is, in Joseph's mouth, the most reliable indicator of its absence.

Sheridan's critique here is double-edged. The cult of sentiment, championed by writers like Sterne and Mackenzie, encouraged the public performance of private feeling — a cultural practice that, Sheridan suggests, was always vulnerable to exploitation by those who could simulate feeling without experiencing it. Joseph is not merely a hypocrite; he is the logical endpoint of a culture that prizes the *display* of virtue over its substance.

## Sir Oliver's Tests: Hypocrisy Unmasked

[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]'s two-stage testing of his nephews provides the play's most systematic examination of hypocrisy. Disguised first as the moneylender Mr. Premium and then as the poor relation Stanley, Sir Oliver subjects both brothers to identical moral tests. Joseph, who passes for a "youthful Miracle of Prudence" (001-061), fails both — refusing charity to Stanley while professing benevolence, and revealing himself as the true author of Charles's financial distress.

The genius of Sheridan's construction is that Joseph's hypocrisy is exposed not through argument but through action. Sir Oliver does not debate Joseph; he simply presents him with opportunities that Joseph's nature cannot refuse. The tests reveal that hypocrisy is unsustainable under sustained scrutiny — the mask must eventually slip because maintaining it requires a vigilance that human nature cannot sustain.

## The Language of Hypocrisy

Sheridan pays careful attention to the linguistic mechanisms by which hypocrisy operates. Joseph Surface's characteristic mode is the conditional — "if I could think," "were it possible" — a grammar that allows him to insinuate without asserting, to wound without taking responsibility for the wound. His most devastating attack on Charles, delivered to Sir Peter, is structured entirely in hypotheticals: "if Charles were undutiful" (002-316), "were I to suspect" (002-320). The conditional mood is, for Joseph, a tool of plausible deniability — he can always retreat to the claim that he was merely speculating, never accusing.

This linguistic strategy has a specific social function. In a world where direct accusation carries consequences — where a gentleman may be called to account for his words — the conditional provides cover. Joseph's hypocrisy is not merely moral but tactical: he has mastered the forms of speech that allow malice to present itself as concern, slander as speculation, and self-interest as disinterested judgment. Sheridan's ear for this register of polite malevolence is one of the play's sharpest satirical achievements.

## Hypocrisy and Social Class

Joseph's hypocrisy is also a class performance. He cultivates the persona of the "Man of Sentiment" (001-090) — a specifically genteel identity that associates moral refinement with social status. His library, his "moral" books, his professions of benevolence are the props of a performance designed to secure his position within the gentry. When Sir Oliver, disguised as the poor relation Stanley, appeals to Joseph for charity, Joseph's refusal is not merely stingy — it is a betrayal of the class identity he has constructed. The man who performs benevolence for status cannot practice it when status offers no reward.

This class dimension distinguishes Joseph's hypocrisy from other forms of deception in the play. Lady Sneerwell's scandal-mongering is the strategy of a woman without independent fortune; Snake's betrayals are the work of a paid agent without class standing. Joseph's hypocrisy, by contrast, is the strategy of a man who has everything to gain from appearing to embody the values of his class — and who discovers, in the play's final act, that the appearance cannot survive the test of practice.

## Critical Context

Sheridan's treatment of hypocrisy engages with a central eighteenth-century moral concern. The century that produced Mandeville's *Fable of the Bees* and Fielding's *Jonathan Wild* was preoccupied with the gap between public profession and private conduct. Sheridan's innovation is theatrical: he does not merely satirise hypocrisy but dramatises its structural dependence on the very moral language it corrupts. Joseph Surface cannot operate without the vocabulary of "sentiment" and "benevolence" — his hypocrisy is, in a sense, a tribute that vice pays to virtue.

The play's resolution is not merely punitive but restorative. Joseph is exiled not from society but from the audience's sympathy, while [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]], whose vices are open and whose virtues are hidden, is revealed as the true man of feeling. Sheridan's moral arithmetic is clear: honest vice is preferable to hypocritical virtue, and a good heart trumps a good reputation.
