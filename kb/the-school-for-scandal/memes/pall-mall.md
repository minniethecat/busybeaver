# Pall Mall

Pall Mall was one of the most fashionable streets in eighteenth-century London, and its invocation in The School for Scandal locates the play's world with geographical precision.

## The Street

Pall Mall ran from the Haymarket to St James's Palace, and its coffee-houses, clubs, and shops were the centres of fashionable male sociability. It was the address of choice for gentlemen of means — a street whose very name carried social significance. Sheridan's audience would have known Pall Mall as the epicentre of the world the play both celebrates and satirises.

## In the Play

The street is mentioned as a conduit for gossip — a place where scandal paragraphs are passed from hand to hand through the network of servants, hairdressers, and milliners that Lady Sneerwell's opening speech describes. Pall Mall represents the public space through which private scandal circulates, the physical infrastructure of the reputation economy.

## Social Geography

Sheridan's London is precisely mapped, and every location carries moral weight. Pall Mall — fashionable, public, commercial — is the opposite of the private spaces (dressing rooms, libraries) where scandal is manufactured and exposed. It is the conduit, not the source, but without conduits the scandal system could not function.
