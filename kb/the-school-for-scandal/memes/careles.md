# Careless

Careless is [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]'s foil and friend — his name, like so many in Sheridan's play, is his [[reputation|character]]. He is careless of reputation, careless of [[fortune|money]], and careless of consequence, sharing [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s vices without [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s hidden virtue. His function is to show what [[charles-surface|Charles]] might become without the [[benevolence|generosity]] that redeems him.

## Role in the Play

Careless serves as a companion figure who sets off [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s distinctive qualities. In the drinking scene (Act III, Scene 3), Careless matches [[charles-surface|Charles]] toast for toast — "And therefore loves good wine" (003-463) — but it is [[charles-surface|Charles]] who commands the scene's moral attention. Careless is the chorus to [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s solo, present to amplify and echo but not to lead.

In the auction scene, Careless assists [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s irreverent sale of the [[portrait|family portrait]]s, playing auctioneer — "what's an auctioneer without his hammer?" (004-018). His presence transforms what could be a solitary moral test into a social [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]], heightening the theatricality of [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s refusal to sell his uncle's portrait.

## Key Scenes

Act III, Scene 3: The drinking party at [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s lodgings. Act IV, Scene 1: The auction of the [[portrait|family portrait]]s.
