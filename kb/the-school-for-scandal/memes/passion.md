# Passion

Passion in *The School for Scandal* is a double-edged force: it is the energy that animates the pleasure of scandal and the weakness that exposes characters to manipulation. Sheridan's treatment of passion is neither sentimental nor moralising — he understands it as a fact of social life, one that his characters deploy, conceal, and occasionally overcome.

## Passion as Motive

The play opens with Lady Sneerwell confessing that wounded love has driven her into scandal-mongering. Her confidant Snake observes that "it is utterly unaccountable to me why you a young Widow with no great jointure — should not close with the passion of a man of such character and expectations as Mr. Surface" (001-063). Lady Sneerwell's reply — that "Love has no share whatever in the intercourse between Mr. Surface and me" (001-069) — reveals the emotional economy of the play: passion is a currency that the schemers pretend not to possess but trade upon nonetheless. Her "wounded" passion for Charles Surface has been transmuted into the colder satisfactions of scandal-mongering — a substitution that Sheridan presents as the school's founding psychological transaction.

## Performed Passion and Its Exposure

Joseph Surface is the master of performed passion. He presents himself as a man overwhelmed by feeling — "I am really too much affected by the subject we have been speaking of to bestow a thought on my own concerns" (004-453) — even as he schemes to seduce Lady Teazle behind her husband's back. The screen scene exposes the gap between Joseph's performed sensibility and his real intentions. When Lady Teazle confronts him after her discovery, she speaks of his "pretended Passion" (004-653): the counterfeit emotion that nearly destroyed her marriage. Passion, in Joseph's hands, is a rhetorical weapon — one he deploys against both the woman he courts and the friend he betrays.

## Passion and Temper

Sheridan links passion to the Teazles' domestic warfare. Sir Peter's confession — "you are going to be in a Passion I see" (003-277) — conflates passion with the stubborn ill-humour that defines his quarrels with Lady Teazle. The word slides between its eighteenth-century meanings: strong emotion generally, and anger specifically. When Sir Peter asks "Can Passion — furnish a Remedy?" (005-407), the question is rhetorical — passion has never been the solution to any of the play's problems, only their origin. Lady Teazle echoes this diagnosis when she describes the "Constant round of Caprice, and Passion" (005-427) that characterised their early quarrels.

## The Gendering of Passion

Sheridan distributes passion unevenly across his characters according to gender. Lady Sneerwell's passion is concealed beneath strategic calculation; Lady Teazle's is displayed but eventually disciplined; the men's passion — Charles's impulsiveness, Sir Peter's testiness, Joseph's simulated ardour — is treated as a matter of character rather than decorum. The asymmetry registers a social fact of eighteenth-century life: women's passions were subject to stricter surveillance and harsher judgment than men's. When Lady Teazle asks "Am I to be blamed ... because he has a bad Temper?" (003-242), she is protesting not only Sir Peter's unfairness but the double standard that makes female passion more culpable than male.

## Passion and the Comic Resolution

The play's resolution depends on passion giving way to cooler judgment. Sir Oliver forgives Charles not because passion has been extinguished — Charles remains impulsive and extravagant — but because passion, once understood, can be governed. Sheridan's comedy does not demand the elimination of strong feeling; it asks only that feeling be aligned with truth. Lady Teazle's "heart is not conscious" (004-656) that she deserves Joseph's flattery — the awakening of conscience is the discipline of passion. The marriage that ends the play is not a passionless union but one in which passion has been tempered by honesty. As Sir Peter reflects, "the Faults of a Husband ... may be corrected by experience" (005-428), a sentiment that applies as much to the correction of passionate excess as to any other marital failing.
