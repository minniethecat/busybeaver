# Town

London 'Town' is not merely the setting of The School for Scandal but a character in its own right — a social arena whose rhythms, codes, and pressures drive the play's action.

## The Geography of Scandal

Sheridan's London is precisely mapped. Lady Sneerwell's dressing room in 'Lady Sneerwell's House' is the scandal factory; Sir Peter's house is the site of domestic conflict; Charles's house is the site of comic excess; Joseph's library is the site of hypocrisy's exposure. Each location carries moral weight. The movement between these spaces — from the private dressing room where scandal is manufactured to the public drawing rooms where it circulates — traces the play's moral geography.

## The Season

The play takes place during the London Season — the period, roughly from November to June, when Parliament sat, the fashionable world assembled, and the marriage market operated. The Season's temporal pressure — the sense that reputations must be made or broken before the summer dispersal to country estates — gives the play's gossip its urgency. Reputations are currency, and the Season is the trading floor.

## Town and Morality

Sheridan's London is morally ambivalent. It is the place where Lady Teazle is corrupted, but also the place where she is redeemed. It is where scandal thrives, but also where it is exposed. The play does not endorse a simple retreat to the country — Rowley, the moral centre, is a London man — but insists that town life requires moral vigilance.
