# Lady Teazle

Lady Teazle is Sheridan's most psychologically credible creation — a country girl who has been "educated" by [[society|London society]] into a fashionable wife, and who must unlearn that education in the play's climactic scene. Her trajectory is the only complete [[character]] arc in the play: from innocence through affectation to recovered [[honour|integrity]]. Where the other [[reputation|character]]s are fixed in their natures — [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] in hypocrisy, [[charles-surface|Charles]] in honesty, [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] in malice — Lady Teazle changes.

## Role in the Play

Lady Teazle's dramatic function is to be the [[reputation|character]] who is tempted and who resists. "I'm sure I'm not more extravagant than a Woman of Fashion ought to be" (002-015), she protests — and the line crystallises her predicament. She has learned the language of fashionable self-justification so thoroughly that she no longer hears it as self-justification. Her "Country Education" (002-038) is the origin she has been taught to be ashamed of and the resource she must recover.

Her near-seduction by [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] is the play's most dangerous moment. [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s flattery — "with the assistance of his sentiments" (001-091) — targets precisely the gap between Lady Teazle's genuine virtue and her fashionable [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]]. She is vulnerable not because she is corrupt but because she is confused: the [[sentiment|sentimental]] culture she has absorbed tells her that refined [[sentiment|feeling]] is virtue, and [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] performs refined [[sentiment|feeling]] superbly.

## The Country Wife to Town Lady

### The Transformation

> "Recollect Lady Teazle when I first saw you—sitting at your tambour frame in a pretty figured linen gown—with a bunch of keys at your side." (002-031)

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s nostalgic recollection provides the baseline from which Lady Teazle has departed. The tambour frame, the linen gown, the keys — these are emblems of domestic industry, rural simplicity, the economy of the country house. Lady Teazle's transformation from this figure to the "Lady of Fashion" (002-012) who spends "six months since... made me the happiest of men" (001-388) is the [[tale|story]] London tells itself about its power to remake provincial identity.

Sheridan does not sentimentalise the country. Lady Teazle's defence — "you make no allowance for the ills one suffers from a Country Education" (002-038) — is genuinely comic, but it is also a real complaint. The country, in her account, was not Eden but limitation; London offered not corruption but possibility. Her journey is not from good to evil but from one set of constraints to another.

### The Fashionable Self

Lady Teazle's fashionable identity is constructed through consumption:

| Marker | Country Self | Town Self |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Dress | Figured linen gown | "Wired gauze with short sleeves" (002-048) |
| Activity | Tambour work | Visiting, scandal parties |
| Speech | Country directness | Fashionable wit |
| Authority | Keys at side (household) | "Liberty" from husband |
| Money | Household economy | "[[extravagance|Extravagance]]" |

The table traces a transaction: Lady Teazle has exchanged domestic power (the keys) for social display (the gauze). [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s complaint that "she has no taste for those simple pleasures any longer" (002-035) accurately describes the change while missing its cause — Lady Teazle has not so much lost her taste as acquired a new one, and the new taste is what her society rewards.

## Key Scenes

### The Marriage Quarrels (Act II, Scene 1)

The early quarrels with [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] establish the comedy of their [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] — "Lady Teazle — Lady Teazle I'll not bear it!" (002-001) — but also reveal the genuine [[love|affection]] beneath the bickering. Sheridan gives both parties their due: [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] is genuinely fond but genuinely patronising; Lady Teazle is genuinely witty but genuinely difficult. The [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] that looks comic from outside is, from inside, a negotiation between two people who love each other and have not yet learned how to say so.

### The Screen Scene (Act IV)

The screen scene is Lady Teazle's moment of [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]] and the play's moral fulcrum. Hidden [[screen|behind the screen]], she overhears [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s simultaneous attempt to seduce her and to maintain his moral reputation. What she learns — that the "[[sentiment|Man of Sentiment]]" (001-090) is a seducer using the language of virtue — is the education she needed.

> "Behind the screen! Odds Life, let's unveil her!" (004-607)

[[charles-surface|Charles's]] cry — he does not know who is [[screen|behind the screen]] — becomes the play's definitive unmasking. When Lady Teazle steps forward, she steps into honesty. Her confession to [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] is the play's emotional resolution: "Owned and acknowledged" (005-175) — the language of property law applied to moral [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]].

### The Reconciliation (Act V)

> "Child — speak your sentiments — you know — we are going to be happy." (005-561)

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s speech to [[maria|Maria]] in the final scene — "speak your sentiments" — echoes the language of the play's opening. But where "sentiment" in Act I meant performed emotion, here it means genuine [[sentiment|feeling]]. Lady Teazle's reconciliation with [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] completes the play's argument that authentic sentiment survives the School that perverts it.

## Relationships

- **[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]]**: husband; their "matrimonial duel" moves from comic warfare to genuine reconciliation. [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] is both Lady Teazle's antagonist and her partner in the play's happiest resolution
- **[[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]**: would-be seducer; [[joseph-surface|Joseph]] targets Lady Teazle's confusion between fashion and virtue. Her resistance to him — "Don't — you think we may as well leave Honor out of the argument?" (004-324) — is the ironic prelude to her recovery of honour
- **[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]**: social rival and foil; where Lady Teazle emerges from the School, [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] runs it
- **[[maria|Maria]]**: friend and contrast; [[maria|Maria]]'s steadfast virtue highlights Lady Teazle's susceptibility to fashion, but also points toward the [[honour|integrity]] Lady Teazle recovers
- **[[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]**: ally; [[charles-surface|Charles]]'s unmasking of [[screen|the screen scene]] liberates Lady Teazle from [[joseph-surface|Joseph]]'s manipulation
- **[[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]**: witness; [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] observes Lady Teazle's transformation from fashionable wife to honest woman

## Critical Context

Lady Teazle has attracted more critical attention than any other [[reputation|character]] in the play, and for good reason: she is the only [[reputation|character]] who develops. Restoration comedy typically presents fixed types — the fop, the coquette, the gull — whose humours are exposed but never transformed. Lady Teazle's capacity for change represents Sheridan's innovation on the form.

The feminist reading is necessarily double. Lady Teazle's "reformation" restores her to a patriarchal [[marriage-and-gender|marriage]] — the keys she picks up are the keys she laid down. But Sheridan gives her sufficient agency in the restoration that it reads less as submission than as choice. She has tried fashionable London and found it wanting; her return to [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] is not defeat but discernment.
