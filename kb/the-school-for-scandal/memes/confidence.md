# Confidence

Confidence — the trust one places in another's discretion or loyalty — is the essential raw material of the scandal economy. Without confidences first given, there can be no confidences betrayed.

## In The School for Scandal

The play's opening scene establishes confidence as the mechanism by which scandal operates. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] instructs Snake that "the confidence is not" (001-106) to be taken lightly: the secrets people entrust to her become the ammunition she fires back at them. Confidence, in this world, is a trap — the more intimately one confides, the more vulnerable one becomes.

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] learns this lesson painfully. His misplaced confidence in [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] — whom he trusts as a moral advisor and confidant — leads directly to the discovery of Joseph's treachery. When Sir Peter "withdraws his confidence from her" (004-287), referring to [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]], the marital bond itself is called into question. Confidence between husband and wife, once broken, threatens the entire domestic order.

Sheridan's structural irony is that the truly trustworthy character — [[rowley|Rowley]] — is a servant, while the gentlemen who command social confidence are the least deserving of it. "What he has done for me has" (005-072) earned [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]] the confidence of those who know him best, even as society at large withholds it from him. The play thus asks: in a world where confidence is routinely abused, how can genuine trust be distinguished from its counterfeit?

## Historical Context

The concept of confidence carried particular weight in eighteenth-century social relations. In a society governed by codes of politeness and reputation, the exchange of confidences was a recognised form of social currency — to confide in someone was to grant them a mark of intimacy and trust. The breach of confidence was correspondingly grave: it was not merely a personal betrayal but a violation of the social order that made polite society possible.

Sheridan's treatment of confidence draws on a long tradition in Restoration and eighteenth-century comedy, where the misuse of confidential information drives plot and exposes character. From Congreve's *The Way of the World* to Sheridan's own *The Rivals*, the confidante who cannot be trusted is a stock figure of comic drama. What distinguishes *The School for Scandal* is the systematic way it transforms confidence into a commodity: secrets are traded, sold, and weaponised, and the person who confides is the person who loses.
