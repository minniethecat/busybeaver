# Happiness

Happiness is the stated goal of nearly every character in The School for Scandal, yet almost no one in the play is happy. Sheridan's comedy anatomises the gap between the pursuit of happiness and its attainment.

## In The School for Scandal

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] entered marriage expecting happiness and found quarrel instead. His "tender concern I have for your happiness" (002-345) — addressed to [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] — is both genuine and self-serving: he wants her to be happy so that he can be happy too. The circular logic of marital happiness, in which each partner's contentment depends on the other's, is precisely what makes the Teazles' marriage both dysfunctional and, ultimately, salvageable.

The play's most devastating observation about happiness comes from [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]], who treats it as a rhetorical tool rather than an emotional state. He speaks of "a happiness to have a Friend" (004-378) while betraying that friend's trust — happiness, in his mouth, is a performance designed to disarm. The contrast with [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]]'s unreflective conviviality is telling: Charles pursues pleasure without calling it happiness, and in doing so comes closer to the genuine article than his brother ever does.

Sheridan's resolution distributes happiness in proportion to honesty. The characters who have been truthful — [[maria|Maria]], [[charles-surface|Charles]], the reconciled [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] and [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] — end the play with a prospect of happiness. Those who have traded in deception are left with nothing. The play's moral arithmetic is simple: happiness cannot be counterfeited, only earned.

## Historical Context

The pursuit of happiness was a defining preoccupation of eighteenth-century thought. Locke had argued that happiness was the natural object of human desire; Hume made it the foundation of his moral philosophy; and the American Declaration of 1776 — written the year before Sheridan's play — declared its pursuit an inalienable right. For Enlightenment thinkers, happiness was not merely a private feeling but a public good, the measurable outcome of a well-ordered society.

Sheridan's play participates in this discourse with characteristic comic scepticism. His characters pursue happiness with the same energy they pursue scandal, yet the pursuit itself is what makes them miserable. Sir Peter's desire for domestic happiness produces only domestic discord; Lady Teazle's pursuit of fashionable pleasure leads her to the edge of ruin. Sheridan's implicit argument — that happiness is a by-product of virtue rather than an object of pursuit — aligns him with the classical tradition against the more optimistic strains of Enlightenment thought. The play's resolution suggests that happiness comes not to those who chase it but to those who, like Charles Surface, are too busy living to calculate its pursuit.
