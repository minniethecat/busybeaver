# Curricle

The curricle — a light, two-wheeled carriage drawn by two horses — was the sports car of the 1770s, and its mention in The School for Scandal is a precise social marker.

## The Fashionable Vehicle

The curricle was the most stylish vehicle a young man of fashion could drive. It was fast, dangerous, and expensive — the perfect emblem of the rakish extravagance that Charles Surface embodies. To drive a curricle in Hyde Park during the fashionable hour was to announce one's membership in the beau monde.

## Charles's Curricle

Charles's curricle is another item in the catalogue of his extravagance — like the horses he sells and the portraits he auctions, it represents money spent on display rather than substance. But Sheridan's treatment of Charles's vehicles and horses is characteristically double-edged: they are evidence of dissipation, but they are also evidence of vitality, of a life lived with energy rather than calculation.

## Social Semiotics

The curricle, like the sedan chair, the opera box, and the card table, is part of the play's rich vocabulary of social semiotics. Every object in Sheridan's world carries meaning: what you drive, where you sit, who visits you, what you wear — all are legible signs of status, character, and moral condition.
