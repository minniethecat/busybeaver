# Malice

Malice is the conscious will to injure another's reputation or happiness — more active than mere gossip and more knowing than [[wit|wit]], it is the engine that powers the School for Scandal's social destruction.

## In The School for Scandal

[[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]]'s scandal factory runs not on idle chatter but on deliberate malice. She describes the pleasure of spreading ruin when she reflects on "the malice of a good thing" — the sharper the wound a remark inflicts, the more satisfying its effect (001-164). This is not careless talk but weaponised speech.

[[maria|Maria]] explicitly names the danger when she protests that "their malice is intolerable" (001-344), recognising that the school does not merely report scandal but actively manufactures it out of hostile intent. The distinction Sheridan draws is crucial: [[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]] pretends benevolence while spreading damage, whereas Lady Sneerwell openly admits her motives, claiming she "bear[s] no malice against the People I abuse" (002-108) — a transparent lie that exposes the true nature of the scandalous circle.

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] observes that [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s seeming virtue conceals "malice at heart" (002-319), linking malice to [[hypocrisy|hypocrisy]] — the play's central pairing of public goodness and private cruelty. Even [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]], the flawed hero, ultimately deflects malice with generosity: his friends "retort their malice only by showing them" (005-394) that good nature disarms ill intent.
