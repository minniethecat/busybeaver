# Appearance

The surname "Surface" announces Sheridan's theme before a line is spoken. *The School for [[scandal|Scandal]]* is a sustained meditation on the [[family|relations]]hip between appearance and reality — a [[family|relations]]hip that the play's society has inverted, rewarding those who manage appearances most skilfully and punishing those who neglect them.

## The Theatrical Metaphor

The play is saturated with the language of [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] and display. [[charles-surface|Charles]] is condemned for "what an appearance he makes" (003-336) — the phrasing suggests that [[reputation|character]] is something one *makes*, a crafted presentation rather than an expression of inner [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]]. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]]'s entire identity is a [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]]: "with [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]], and indeed with all his acquaintance, He passes for" a man of sentiment and benevolence (001-086–087). The verb "passes for" is devastating — it concedes the [[appearance-vs-reality|performance]] while denying the reality.

## The Crisis of Trust

[[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]]'s rueful conclusion — "there is no [[credit|trust]]ing to appearances" (005-175) — is the moral the play has been teaching its audience for five acts. Every [[reputation|character]] who [[credit|trust]]s appearances is deceived: [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter]] by [[joseph-surface|Joseph]], [[lady-teazle|Lady Teazle]] by [[society|fashionable society]], [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]] by his nephews' reputations. The play's testing structure ([[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s [[appearance-vs-reality|disguise]]s, [[screen|the screen scene]]) systematically dismantles the equation of appearance with [[appearance-vs-reality|truth]].

## The Comic Resolution

Yet Sheridan is not a tragedian. His comedy allows for the restoration of [[credit|trust]] — not because appearances become reliable, but because the play's honest [[reputation|character]]s learn to see through them. [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver]]'s admission that "appearances are" (004-629) against [[charles-surface|Charles]] — that the surface evidence condemns the wrong brother — is the play's turning point. The resolution argues that a society addicted to [[appearance-vs-reality|surfaces]] can be saved, but only by those willing to look beneath them.
