# Gallantry

Gallantry — the code of polite male attention to women — is both practiced and subverted throughout The School for Scandal.

## The Code of Gallantry

Eighteenth-century gallantry prescribed a complex set of behaviours: compliments, attentions, small gifts, verses, and an elaborate rhetoric of admiration. In theory, gallantry was a form of respect; in practice, it was often a form of seduction. Sheridan's play is acutely aware of this ambiguity. Joseph Surface, the master of gallant discourse, uses the language of admiration to mask his intentions; Charles, who is incapable of gallant pretence, is the more sincere lover.

## Joseph's Gallant Performance

Joseph's gallantry is a weapon. He deploys it against Lady Teazle in the screen scene with calculated precision — the right compliment, the right sigh, the right appeal to her 'sensibility.' His gallantry is the form his hypocrisy takes when confronting a married woman: it allows him to present seduction as admiration, adultery as gallantry.

## Charles's Sincerity

Charles has no gallantry — he tells Maria he loves her because he has loved her since childhood, and he makes no fine speeches about it. His inadequacy as a gallant is, paradoxically, the proof of his sincerity. Sheridan's argument is that gallantry, like sentiment, is a performance that can be detached from genuine feeling — and that the absence of performance may be the surest sign of truth.
