# Nature

In eighteenth-century moral discourse, "nature" denotes a person's innate disposition — the fundamental bent toward goodness or malice that underlies all outward behaviour. Sheridan uses the good-nature/ill-nature axis as his primary moral yardstick.

## In The School for Scandal

The play repeatedly returns to the question of who possesses good nature and who does not. [[charles-surface|Charles Surface]], for all his extravagance and dissolution, is repeatedly described as good-natured: "the world says ill-natured things of him but 'tis" (003-310) — the suspended judgement implying the opposite is true. His brother [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]], by contrast, "appear[s] more ill-natured than they are" (002-318): his polished manners conceal a fundamentally selfish nature.

[[mrs-candour|Mrs. Candour]] presents the most complex case. [[joseph-surface|Joseph Surface]] insists her scandal-mongering is not true ill nature — "how can you be so ill-natured!" (002-190) — yet [[sir-peter-teazle|Sir Peter Teazle]] calls her "damned good-natured" (002-233) with heavy irony, exposing her professed benevolence as a mask. The play's insight is that good nature is not the same as polite manners: Charles, who offends propriety, is truly good-natured; Joseph, who observes every form, is not.

Sheridan traces the social mechanics of this distinction. [[lady-sneerwell|Lady Sneerwell]] exploits "ill-nature" (001-212) as raw material for scandal, while the truly good-natured — like [[maria|Maria]] — refuse to participate in the system. "Human nature besides" (004-011) becomes [[sir-oliver-surface|Sir Oliver Surface]]'s diagnostic tool: observing how men behave when they think themselves unobserved reveals their true nature.
