In eighteenth-century English law and society, a ward was a minor placed under the legal guardianship of an adult, who controlled the ward's person, education, and — crucially — marriage choices. In *The School for Scandal*, Maria's status as Sir Peter Teazle's ward is not merely a plot convenience but a structural feature of the play's examination of authority, gender, and consent. Through Maria's wardship, Sheridan dramatises the tension between patriarchal authority and the emerging ideal of companionate marriage based on free choice.

## In The School for Scandal

### Maria's Position

Maria is introduced in the play's opening scene as the object of conflicting male desires: Charles Surface loves her, Joseph Surface courts her, and Sir Peter — "attached to Maria Sir Peter's ward" (001-060) — controls her legal person. The wardship arrangement gives Sir Peter what he calls "the Power of a Father" over Maria (001-421), a power he exercises by attempting to direct her marital choice toward Joseph. Maria's resistance to this direction is one of the play's moral through-lines: her insistence on choosing Charles, despite his reputation and her guardian's opposition, aligns wardship with the broader theme of genuine feeling versus calculated interest.

Sir Peter's complaint that "Maria — my Ward — whom I ought to have the Power of a Father over, is determined to dispose of herself" (001-421) registers the central anxiety of wardship in the period: the gap between the legal authority of the guardian and the affective autonomy of the ward. The play resolves this tension not by overthrowing guardianship but by reconciling it with the ward's own choice — a solution that preserves the institution while validating the individual.

### Wardship and Marriage

The wardship plot intersects with the play's marriage plot in ways that expose the economic logic underlying both. Sir Peter's preference for Joseph — the "man of Sentiment" — over Charles — the "Libertine" — is presented as a guardian's prudent judgment, but the play consistently undermines this judgment by revealing Joseph's prudence as hypocrisy and Charles's extravagance as redeemable. Maria's choice of Charles thus becomes a vindication of the ward's moral discernment over the guardian's worldly wisdom.

The play also contrasts Maria's position with Lady Teazle's: the ward and the wife are both legally subordinate to Sir Peter, but Maria's resistance is principled where Lady Teazle's was (until the screen scene) merely fashionable. Together, they represent two possible responses to patriarchal authority: steadfast moral independence and gradual moral awakening.

## Historical Context

Wardship in eighteenth-century England was governed by a complex amalgam of common law, equity, and statute. The Court of Wards had been abolished in 1660, but guardianship continued through the Chancery court's parens patriae jurisdiction and through private arrangements. For female wards, the guardian's control over marriage was particularly significant, since marriage determined a woman's legal and economic status for life. Sheridan's treatment of Maria's wardship reflects a society in which the ideal of free marital choice was increasingly asserted but the legal machinery of patriarchal control remained intact. Maria gets her choice, but only because the play's comic resolution makes her choice align with moral truth — a resolution that, in real eighteenth-century life, was far from guaranteed.
