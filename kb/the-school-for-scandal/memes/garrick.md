# David Garrick

David Garrick (1717–1779) was the dominant figure of the eighteenth-century English stage and an important presence in the story of The School for Scandal.

## The Actor-Manager

Garrick revolutionised English acting, replacing the declamatory style of the early eighteenth century with a more naturalistic approach that emphasised psychological truth. As manager of Drury Lane, he raised the theatre's standards and prestige, attracting the best playwrights and cultivating the most discerning audience in London. His retirement in 1776 and Sheridan's succession created a direct line of theatrical inheritance.

## Garrick and Sheridan

Garrick was Sheridan's mentor and champion. He wrote the prologue to The School for Scandal, lent the play his immense personal prestige, and ensured its reception as a major theatrical event. The relationship was both personal and professional: Garrick recognised Sheridan's genius and promoted it; Sheridan absorbed Garrick's practical wisdom and applied it.

## The Prologue

Garrick's prologue is a manifesto for laughing comedy against the encroachments of sentimentality. It was Garrick, more than anyone, who had kept the tradition of wit-based comedy alive through the mid-century decades when sentimental comedy dominated. His prologue to Sheridan's play is thus both a welcome to a new talent and a declaration of theatrical principles — a passing of the torch from one generation to the next.
