## Overview

Mary Dusak is one of the hired girls in Black Hawk and a member of the notorious "three Marys" — Mary Dusak, Mary Svoboda, and a third Mary — whose scandalous histories were a favorite subject of gossip among the old men at the drugstore. Bold, resourceful, and unscrupulous, she is among the immigrant working girls who dance at the Boys' Home hotel on the night of Mrs. Gardener's absence and who represent a new kind of female independence on the prairie frontier.

## Key Details

Mary Dusak is described in vividly physical terms. She was "broad and brown of countenance, slightly marked by smallpox, but handsome for all that." (026-030) She had "beautiful chestnut hair, coils of it," a "low and smooth" forehead, and "commanding dark eyes" that "regarded the world indifferently and fearlessly." (026-030) The narrator adds, with a frankness characteristic of the novel's treatment of the hired girls: "She looked bold and resourceful and unscrupulous, and she was all of these." (026-030)

Her past is the stuff of local legend. The old men at the drugstore cigar-stand tell how Mary Dusak "had been housekeeper for a bachelor rancher from Boston, and after several years in his service she was forced to retire from the world for a short time." (028-013) The euphemism barely conceals the sexual scandal — an unwanted pregnancy — and her return to town to "take the place of her friend, Mary Svoboda, who was similarly embarrassed" reinforces the pattern. (028-013) Yet despite, or perhaps because of, their histories, the three Marys "were such good cooks and such admirable housekeepers that they never had to look for a place." (028-013) Their domestic skills give them a bargaining power that their reputations cannot entirely undermine.

## In the Text

Mary Dusak first appears as one of the four hired girls discovered waltzing in the dining-room of the Boys' Home. When Anson Kirkpatrick peeps over the transom, he sees "Tiny and Lena, Ántonia and Mary Dusak, waltzing in the middle of the floor." (026-019) The four girls flee giggling into the kitchen, but their discovery sets in motion the evening's events, including Blind d'Arnault's impromptu concert.

During the dancing that follows, Mary Dusak's appearance draws narrative attention. The description of her "commanding dark eyes" and her fearless gaze places her among the novel's most vividly drawn minor characters, and the frank acknowledgment of her unscrupulousness aligns her with the novel's unsentimental view of the hired girls' strategies for survival. (026-030)

She is later cited as an example of the hired girls' professional competence: despite being "considered as dangerous as high explosives to have about the kitchen," the three Marys' cooking and housekeeping skills ensure their continued employment. (028-013) The paradox — dangerous yet indispensable — captures the ambiguous position of the immigrant hired girls in Black Hawk society.

## Significance

Mary Dusak embodies the independence and moral complexity of the hired girls. Unlike Ántonia, whose goodness is never in question, or Lena, whose ambition is channeled into legitimate business success, Mary Dusak operates in a moral gray zone. She is "unscrupulous" — the narrator does not soften the word — and her past includes an episode that respectable society could only discuss in euphemisms. Yet she is also handsome, commanding, and fearless, a woman who has made her way in the world by her own resources.

The "three Marys" as a group represent an alternative path for immigrant women: one that does not lead to marriage and a farm, like Ántonia's, or to entrepreneurial respectability, like Lena's, but to a kind of pragmatic survival at the margins of respectability. Their indispensability as cooks and housekeepers is their economic leverage, and their notoriety is, in its way, a form of power — they are known, they are discussed, they cannot be ignored.
