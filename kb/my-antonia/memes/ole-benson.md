## Overview

Ole Benson is a Norwegian settler living at the edge of the prairie settlement near Black Hawk. He is married to "Crazy Mary" Benson, and his infatuation with the young [[Lena Lingard]] becomes a source of scandal and gossip throughout the Norwegian community. Jim Burden later recognizes in Ole's behavior a pattern of helpless fascination that he himself comes to understand.

## Key Details

Ole Benson lived in a "leaky dugout somewhere at the edge of the settlement." (023-041) He is described bluntly as "fat and lazy and discouraged, and bad luck had become a habit with him." (023-041) After enduring a series of misfortunes, his wife's mental instability — she tried to set a neighbor's barn on fire and was sent to the asylum at Lincoln — became yet another burden in a life already defined by hardship. (023-041)

The scandal of Ole's infatuation with Lena Lingard circulates throughout the settlement. As Ántonia reports it, Lena "was accused of making Ole Benson lose the little sense he had — and that at an age when she should still have been in pinafores." (023-040) Despite Lena's youth, the gossip fixes on her as the cause of Ole's behavior, illustrating the harsh scrutiny directed at young immigrant women even when they are the objects of adult men's unwanted attention.

During the summer, Ole would become "discouraged in the field, tie up his team, and wander off to wherever Lena Lingard was herding." (023-042) He would sit on the drawside and watch her cattle, abandoning his own work entirely. "All the settlement was talking about it," and the Norwegian preacher's wife intervened, trying to persuade Lena to come to church as a way of restoring respectability. (023-042) The episode culminates in a scene of public scandal when, after church one Sunday, Ole lifted Lena onto her horse — an act considered "shocking; a married man was not expected to do such things." (023-043)

## In the Text

Ole Benson is referenced at several key points in the narrative. In the episode recounting the dancing pavilion summer, Sylvester Lovett's furtive watching of Lena at the Vannis' tent reminds Jim Burden of Ole Benson, "who used to sit on the drawside and watch Lena herd her cattle." (028-014) The parallel between the banker's son and the defeated Norwegian farmer suggests that Lena's power to fascinate crosses all social boundaries.

Years later, when Jim Burden visits [[Lena Lingard]] in her Lincoln dressmaking shop, he sits through a Sunday morning watching her, and reflects: "Ole Benson's behaviour was now no mystery to me." (038-008) This admission transforms Ole Benson from a pathetic figure of gossip into a point of empathetic understanding. Jim recognizes in Ole's helpless watching the same fascination he himself feels — the power of Lena's beauty and vitality to arrest attention and suspend judgment.

## Significance

Ole Benson is a portrait of frontier defeat. His leaky dugout, his laziness, his "habit" of bad luck, and his tragic marriage to a mentally ill wife paint a picture of the immigrant experience at its most bleak. He is the counterpoint to the novel's successful immigrants — to Ántonia, to Lena, to Tiny Soderball — a reminder that the prairie could break people as easily as it could make them.

His fascination with Lena Lingard is both pathetic and prophetic. It is pathetic because he is a married man fixated on a girl young enough to wear pinafores, and the settlement rightly sees it as shameful. But it is prophetic because Lena will indeed grow up to become a woman of extraordinary magnetism, a self-made success who captivates everyone from lonely farmers to banker's sons to Jim Burden himself. Ole Benson, in his crude and inappropriate way, is the first to recognize what Lena will become — and the last to pay the social price for it.
