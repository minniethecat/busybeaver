## Overview

Wick Cutter is the villainous money-lender of Black Hawk, Nebraska, described by [[Jim Burden]] as "a man of evil name throughout the country." (008-001) As the holder of mortgages on struggling immigrant farms — including those of [[Russian Peter]] and [[Mr. Shimerda]] — Cutter embodies the predatory economic forces that threatened homesteading families. His personal life is equally sordid: he lives in a state of perpetual warfare with his wife and has a reputation for pursuing young immigrant women in his employ.

## Key Details

Cutter's full name is Wycliffe Cutter, and he was proud of his "pious" first name, though Jim notes drily that "the rest of us found it anything but." (030-010) He belonged to the "fast set" of Black Hawk, a group of hard-drinking young men, and had moved to the town from another part of the state to escape restraint. (030-003)

He married a "terrifying-looking person" named [[Mrs. Cutter]], a woman with a "hysterical temperament" who painted china obsessively. (030-005) (030-006) The Cutters quarrelled constantly — "from the moment Cutter came into the house until they separated at night" — and their conflicts were public knowledge, including episodes where Mrs. Cutter threw hot grease and started a fire in a closet, which Cutter extinguished inelegantly by dumping the contents of his washbasin on it. (030-007) (030-008)

## In the Text

Cutter first appears in the narrative as the creditor who holds a mortgage on [[Russian Peter]]'s milk cow. When Peter falls into debt, Cutter threatens to take everything, forcing Peter to sell his belongings and eventually leave the country. (008-001)

His reputation as a sexual predator becomes central to the plot when [[Ántonia Shimerda]] goes to work in the Cutter household. Despite warnings from [[Grandmother Burden]] and others, Ántonia accepts the position because the Cutters pay four dollars a week — more than other employers. (029-009) (029-010) When [[Mrs. Harling]] warns Ántonia directly, saying "if you go to work for the Cutters, you're likely to have a fling of some kind that you'll be sorry for," Ántonia replies defiantly that she can take care of herself and that "I'm a lot stronger than Cutter is." (029-013) (029-010)

Jim provides a full portrait of the Cutter household in Book II, Chapter 9, describing their money-lending operation and domestic chaos. (030-001 to 030-010) The marriage is sustained by mutual hatred: Mrs. Cutter stays because the house and property are in her name, while Cutter stays because he cannot afford to leave. (030-004)

The climactic episode comes in Book II, Chapter 12, when the Cutters go to Omaha and Cutter — hoping to find Ántonia alone in the house — secretly returns on the night express. [[Jim Burden]], having guessed Cutter's intentions, stays in Ántonia's place and endures a humiliating confrontation. (034-001) (034-014) The "scandal about the Swedish girl" — an earlier victim of Cutter's predation — had soured the Cutters' marriage permanently, and Cutter "could never get his wife to sign a paper" giving him control of the property afterward. (034-001)

Cutter and his wife appear briefly in Book III, still living in Black Hawk and continuing their "strange warfare." (044-022) (044-024)

## Significance

Wick Cutter serves as the novel's clearest antagonist — not a villain of melodramatic evil but a realistic predator enabled by economic power and social indifference. His money-lending exploits the vulnerability of immigrant farmers, mirroring the broader economic forces that made frontier life precarious for newcomers. His sexual exploitation of hired girls exposes the particular dangers facing young immigrant women who had to work in domestic service.

The Cutters' marriage is one of Cather's sharpest satirical portraits: a union sustained purely by financial entanglement and mutual spite, devoid of affection or shared purpose. It stands in stark contrast to the novel's depictions of genuine community — the Burden household, the Harling family, and the bonds among the hired girls themselves.
