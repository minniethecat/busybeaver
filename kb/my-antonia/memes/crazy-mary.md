## Overview

Crazy Mary is the wife of Ole Benson, a Norwegian settler living in a leaky dugout at the edge of the prairie. After trying to burn down a neighbor's barn, she is sent to the asylum at Lincoln, but escapes and walks nearly two hundred miles home. Her violent jealousy of the young [[Lena Lingard]] — whom she chases across the prairie with a corn-knife — makes her one of the novel's most vivid and disturbing minor figures.

## Key Details

Mary Benson's story is one of the most harrowing episodes in the novel's depiction of frontier life. After her husband Ole had endured "every other kind of misfortune," Mary "tried to set a neighbour's barn on fire, and was sent to the asylum at Lincoln." (023-041) She was kept there for a few months, then escaped and walked all the way home — "nearly two hundred miles, travelling by night and hiding in barns and haystacks by day." (023-041) When she arrived back at the Norwegian settlement, "her poor feet were as hard as hoofs." (023-041)

Though she promised to behave, "everyone realized she was as crazy as ever, and she still ran about barefooted through the snow, telling her domestic troubles to her neighbours." (023-041) Her madness is not contained by institutions or by distance; it persists as a visible, public condition that the community accommodates without being able to cure.

Her jealousy of Lena Lingard becomes the central drama of her appearances. On the Sunday when Lena first comes to church dressed as a young woman, and Ole scandalously lifts her onto her horse, "Crazy Mary darted out from the group of women at the church door, and ran down the road after Lena, shouting horrible threats." (023-043)

## In the Text

The most dramatic encounter between Crazy Mary and Lena occurs at the Shimerdas' farm. Lena arrives "bounding through the red grass as fast as her white legs could carry her," fleeing Mary, and hides in Ántonia's feather-bed. (023-046) Mary comes "right up to the door" and graphically demonstrates what she plans to do to Lena with her corn-knife, "showing us very graphically just what she meant to do to Lena." (023-046) Mrs. Shimerda watches from the window and "enjoyed the situation keenly," until Ántonia sends Mary away with "an apronful of bottle-tomatoes." (023-046)

The image of Crazy Mary chasing Lena becomes part of the novel's shared memory. Years later, when Jim Burden visits Lena in Lincoln, he thinks "of how she used to run barefoot over the prairie until after the snow began to fly, and how Crazy Mary chased her round and round the cornfields." (036-015) The phrase recurs again when Ántonia's children ask for stories: "how the snow began to fly, and how Crazy Mary chased her round and round the cornfields." (036-015)

## Significance

Crazy Mary is more than a grotesque figure of frontier madness. Her story exposes the brutal underside of immigrant life on the prairie — the isolation, the inadequate medical care, the way mental illness was managed by families and communities rather than by institutions. Her two-hundred-mile walk home from the Lincoln asylum is both a testament to human endurance and a damning indictment of a system that could not keep a dangerous person contained.

Her pursuit of Lena Lingard also reveals the gender dynamics of scandal in the Norwegian settlement. While Ole Benson is regarded as a pathetic figure who has lost his senses to a pretty girl, Mary is a figure of violent threat — and yet it is Mary who is arguably the wronged party, a wife whose husband's public infatuation with a young girl has destroyed what little stability remained in her life. The novel does not sentimentalize Mary, but it does grant her a kind of terrible dignity: she walked two hundred miles on foot to get home, and she will not be ignored.
