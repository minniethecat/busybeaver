## Overview

Mrs. Thomas is a dressmaker in Black Hawk, Nebraska, and the employer of [[Lena Lingard]]. She runs a shop where she makes clothes for the town ladies and trains young hired girls in the craft of dressmaking. Through her mentorship, Lena develops the skills that eventually allow her to establish her own successful dressmaking business in Lincoln.

## Key Details

Mrs. Thomas is referred to as "retail trade" in Black Hawk, a designation that distinguishes her from the larger merchants yet still grants her access to the traveling salesmen's sample trunks at the Boys' Home hotel. (024-002) She makes "lovely things for all the town ladies" and is known to have created a purple velvet dress for [[Mrs. Gardener]], the proprietor of the Boys' Home hotel. (023-025)

She has cousins who live in Lincoln, Nebraska, a connection that later facilitates Lena's move to that city when she decides to expand her dressmaking career. (038-001) She is perceptive about her employees' potential — she tells Lena that she thinks Lena can move to another town and "go into business for myself." (033-034)

## In the Text

Mrs. Thomas first enters the narrative when [[Lena Lingard]] announces her intention to leave farm work and become a dressmaker. Lena tells Frances Harling, "For Mrs. Thomas, the dressmaker. She is going to teach me to sew. She says I have quite a knack." (023-015) Frances confirms that Mrs. Thomas believes Lena can learn the trade. (023-024)

Once employed, Lena lives at Mrs. Thomas's establishment, proudly telling [[Antonia Shimerda]], "I've got a room of my own at Mrs. Thomas's, with a carpet." (023-031) Lena runs errands for her employer, matching sewing silk and buying "findings" for Mrs. Thomas downtown. (024-001) It is at Mrs. Thomas's shop that Lena learns the social conventions and formal expressions of small-town propriety — phrases that sound both funny and engaging when delivered in Lena's soft, candid voice. (038-007)

Lena began working for Mrs. Thomas at the age of nineteen, having never before slept a night alone. (038-050) The training and independence she gained through this employment transformed her trajectory from farm laborer to urban businesswoman.

## Significance

Mrs. Thomas represents an important pathway of upward mobility for immigrant hired girls in Black Hawk. While she herself is not an immigrant, her willingness to train Lena Lingard — a Norwegian immigrant from a struggling farm family — demonstrates how skilled trades could serve as a bridge from the rural immigrant underclass to the urban middle class.

Her shop functions as more than a workplace; it is a site of cultural education. There Lena absorbs the language, manners, and aesthetic sensibilities of the town's respectable class, equipping her to navigate a world far beyond the sod house where she grew up. Mrs. Thomas's assessment that Lena could "go into business for myself" proves prescient — by the time Jim Burden visits Lena in Lincoln, she is already making clothes for "the young married set." (038-001)
