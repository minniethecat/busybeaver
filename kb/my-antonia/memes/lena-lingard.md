## Overview

Lena Lingard is one of the immigrant "hired girls" in Black Hawk — a Norwegian farm girl who comes to town to work as a seamstress and becomes one of the novel's most memorable figures of feminine independence. Unlike Ántonia, who eventually marries and raises a large family, Lena chooses a different path: economic self-sufficiency and a deliberate rejection of marriage.

Jim Burden's reintroduction to her in Black Hawk captures her transformation from barefoot farm girl to poised young woman:

> "I had never seen her before with a hat on her head — or with shoes and stockings on her feet, for that matter. And here she was, brushed and smoothed and dressed like a town girl, coming to pay a call." (023-008)

## Character and Ambition

Lena is defined by her serene self-possession and her clear-eyed refusal of the conventional life path expected of immigrant girls. She is "not at all disconcerted" (023-013) when examined by the formidable Mrs. Harling, and she answers questions about marriage with a calm that borders on radical:

> "I don't want to marry Nick, or any other man. I've seen a good deal of married life, and I don't care for it. I want to be so I can help my mother and the children at home, and not have to ask lief of anybody." (023-023)

Her candor is matched by her ambition. Working as a dressmaker's apprentice, she marvels at the fine fabrics and dreams of a larger world. "Mrs. Thomas makes lovely things for all the town ladies," she notes with admiration, and sighs at the thought of velvet from Omaha: "My, but it's lovely!" (023-025).

## The Hired Girl Community

Lena belongs to the community of immigrant hired girls who work in Black Hawk's households — Norwegian, Swedish, Danish, and Bohemian young women whose vitality and independence unsettle the town's more conventional residents. Frances Harling immediately recognizes her: "You are Lena Lingard, aren't you? I've been to see your mother, but you were off herding cattle that day" (023-012).

Mrs. Harling's advice to Lena reveals the tension between town propriety and the hired girls' freedom: "I expect you'll learn to sew all right, Lena, if you'll only keep your head and not go gadding about to dances all the time" (023-026).

## Later Life and Success

Lena eventually moves to Lincoln, where she establishes herself as a successful dressmaker. She becomes one of the few hired girls to achieve genuine economic independence, owning her own business and living on her own terms. Her friendship with Jim Burden continues in Lincoln, where she represents a different model of immigrant womanhood — not the fertile pioneer mother like Ántonia, but the self-made businesswoman.

Her success is quietly remarkable: a Norwegian immigrant's daughter who arrived barefoot and illiterate in English, she builds a career through talent and determination, refusing the marriage economy that trapped so many women of her generation.

## Significance

Lena Lingard serves as a counterpoint to Ántonia Shimerda in Cather's exploration of immigrant women's possibilities. Where Ántonia's fulfillment comes through marriage, motherhood, and connection to the land, Lena's comes through craft, economic independence, and freedom from domestic obligation. Neither path is presented as superior; together they show the range of outcomes available to the ambitious daughters of immigrants.

She also challenges the reader's assumptions about the hired girls — demonstrating that their vitality and unconventionality were not liabilities but assets in the making of new American lives.
