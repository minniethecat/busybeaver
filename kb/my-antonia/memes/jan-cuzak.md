## Overview

Jan Cuzak is one of the younger sons of [[Antonia Shimerda|Ántonia Cuzak]] and [[Anton Cuzak]], growing up on the Cuzak farm in Nebraska. Described as "the little boy whom they called Jan," he is a quiet presence among Ántonia's large brood of children — less bold than his brother [[Leo Cuzak|Leo]] but observant and curious in his own way. (043-038)

## Key Details

Jan is among the younger Cuzak children, grouped with Nina and [[Lucie Cuzak|Lucie]] rather than with the older boys like [[Rudolph Cuzak|Rudolph]] and [[Ambrosch Shimerda|Ambrosch]]. He is "the little boy whom they called Jan," a phrasing that suggests both his youth and his place in the family's affectionate regard. (043-038)

Jan has a sentimental attachment to a pet dog. When the children show Jim Burden the orchard, Ántonia explains: "Jan wants to bury his dog there." (043-059) The detail is small but revealing — a boy's grief honored with a grave in the family orchard, the kind of intimate domestic moment that defines the Cuzak household's warmth.

## In the Text

Jan first appears when Jim Burden arrives at the Cuzak farm. Ántonia calls for her children, and Jan comes in with the others, sitting down on the step with "interested faces" to observe the visitor. (043-038) (043-089) He is grouped with Nina and Lucie — the younger tier of the Cuzak children — as they shyly point out features of the farm to the guest. (043-046)

When the children take Jim on a tour of the orchard, Jan and his younger siblings "crept through" the hedge by a hole known only to them, a detail that captures the secret geography of childhood. (043-054) It is during this tour that Jan's wish to bury his dog in the orchard is revealed. (043-059)

In the parlour, when family photographs are brought out, Jan and Nina join the others in trying to see around the tall visitor — a moment of childlike curiosity. (043-089) Later, his father [[Anton Cuzak]] whispers something to "the little boy they called Jan," who then presents Jim with a gift of photographs — a small, wordless gesture of hospitality from the youngest generation. (044-013)

## Significance

Jan Cuzak is a minor but meaningful presence in Book V. Where Leo represents the irrepressible, mischievous energy of the Cuzak children, Jan embodies their quieter virtues: watchfulness, tenderness, and a capacity for attachment. His wish to bury his dog speaks to the emotional life of a child growing up on the Nebraska prairie, where even small losses are honored. His shy participation in the rituals of hospitality — the orchard tour, the photographs — suggests a boy learning the ways of welcome from his parents.

Like all of Ántonia's children, Jan is evidence of her vitality carried into the next generation, a living testament to the fertile abundance of the Cuzak farm.
