# Boys' Home Hotel

The Boys' Home Hotel is the premier hotel in Black Hawk, Nebraska, in *My Ántonia*. Managed by the formidable Mrs. Gardener, it serves as a hub for the town's commercial traffic, a site of Saturday-night entertainment, and a place where young immigrant women—particularly Tiny Soderball and Lena Lingard—encounter a world beyond the prairie.

## The Best Hotel on the Burlington

The hotel's status is established early in Book II: "The Boys' Home was the best hotel on our branch of the Burlington, and all the commercial travellers in that territory tried to get into Black Hawk for Sunday" (024-002). The traveling salesmen who stay at the hotel represent a mobile, urban, and commercially dynamic America that contrasts sharply with the settled routines of Black Hawk's families. They "used to assemble in the parlour after supper on Saturday nights," where "Marshall Field's man, Anson Kirkpatrick, played the piano and sang all the latest sentimental songs" (024-002).

Behind the hotel, an old store building serves as a showroom where "the salesmen opened their big trunks and spread out their samples on the counters" (024-002). The Black Hawk merchants visit to order goods, and the hotel thus functions as a conduit between the small prairie town and the commercial centers of Chicago and beyond.

## Mrs. Gardener's Domain

The hotel is inseparable from its proprietor, Mrs. Gardener, who is "admittedly the best-dressed woman in Black Hawk, drove the best horse, and had a smart trap and a little white-and-gold sleigh" (026-005). Her manner is cold and imposing: "Guests felt that they were receiving, not conferring, a favour when they stayed at her house" (026-005). Within the hotel's social hierarchy, "the patrons of the hotel were divided into two classes: those who had seen Mrs. Gardener's diamonds, and those who had not" (026-005).

Mrs. Gardener's authority extends to her staff. When she needs additional help, she takes Ántonia Shimerda from the Cutters to "work at the hotel" (029-008), recognizing competence across ethnic lines. The hotel thus becomes one of the few institutions in Black Hawk where an immigrant woman can advance through merit.

## A Window to a Larger World

For the young hired girls, the hotel offers exposure to a broader American culture. Lena Lingard "often met" Jim Burden downtown "when she was with Tiny Soderball at the hotel on Saturday nights" (024-001). After washing dishes, Tiny and Lena sit "on the other side of the double doors between the parlour and the dining-room, listening to the music and giggling at the jokes and stories" (024-002). The traveling men, generous with their surplus, give Tiny "handkerchiefs and gloves and ribbons and striped stockings, and so many bottles of perfume and cakes of scented soap that she bestowed some of them on Lena" (024-002).

The hotel also hosts special events that punctuate the town's routine. When Blind d'Arnault, the Negro pianist, visits Black Hawk, "he and his manager spent Saturday and Sunday at our comfortable hotel" (026-002), and Mrs. Harling tells Ántonia "she had better go to see Tiny that Saturday evening, as there would certainly be music at the Boys' Home" (026-002).

## Significance

The Boys' Home Hotel represents a threshold between the insular world of Black Hawk families and the commercial, mobile America beyond. It is a place of transaction—of goods, of culture, of social capital—where the hierarchical boundaries of the town are both reinforced and, occasionally, permeable. For Tiny Soderball, the hotel is a stepping stone to a career that will eventually take her to Alaska and independent wealth. For the novel, it serves as a microcosm of the economic forces that are transforming the prairie.
