## Overview

Marek Shimerda is the youngest son of the Shimerda family. Described from the first encounter, he is a "crazy boy" (010-003) — mentally disabled, largely nonverbal, and given to strange behaviors. His presence in the dugout adds to the family's burden and the pathos of their immigrant struggle:

> "The crazy boy lay under the only window, asleep, I thought, but when we entered he sat up and stared at us. He was a big, broad-shouldered fellow with a short neck and a flat, expressionless face. He never said anything, but I knew he could understand everything that was said to him because Jake told me about him." (010-003)

## The "Crazy Boy"

Marek is under the control of his mother, who disciplines him sternly when he acts out:

> "His mother scowled and said sternly, 'Marek!' then spoke rapidly to Krajiek in Bohemian." (003-014)

Despite his silence, Marek is not unfeeling. During Grandmother Burden's visit with provisions, he approaches timidly:

> "Marek slid cautiously toward us and began to stroke the cat, his dull eyes fixed on grandmother's face." (010-026)

He has an odd, almost animal-like quality — he crawls on the floor (010-020), and his movements are described as cautious, furtive, as if expecting rebuke.

## Work and Wages

Marek is physically strong, and Ambrosch puts him to work:

> "Marek was strong, and Ambrosch worked him hard." (018-020)

When a neighbor named Bushy hires Marek for a week's work, his wages are diverted — Ambrosch gives them to the priest at Black Hawk, "for prayers for their father's soul" (018-024). This episode highlights the family's religious devotion and the pragmatic exploitation of Marek's labor.

## Decline and Institutionalization

Marek's condition deteriorates over the years. In the later chapters, when Jim Burden visits the Shimerdas again, he learns of Marek's fate:

> "Poor Marek had got violent and been sent to the asylum at Lincoln." (041-027)

The phrase "poor Marek" recurs throughout the novel — a refrain of sorrow for a life limited by disability in a world with few accommodations.

## Significance

Marek Shimerda is one of the novel's most poignant minor figures. He represents the hidden suffering within immigrant families — those members who could not contribute, who required care rather than gave it, and who bore the additional burden of isolation in a foreign land.

His story also illuminates the Shimerda family's resourcefulness and limits. Mrs. Shimerda controls him with sharp commands; Ambrosch exploits his strength; and ultimately, when the family can no longer manage him, the state takes over. The arc from prairie dugout to state asylum encapsulates the harsh realities of disability on the frontier.

Marek's presence deepens the tragedy of Mr. Shimerda's suicide — with the father gone, the family's most vulnerable member lost his strongest protector. The line of Mrs. Shimerda scowling "Marek!" is one of the novel's sharpest portraits of the strain that poverty and displacement place on parental love.
