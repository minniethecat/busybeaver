## Overview

Mary Svoboda is one of the hired girls in Black Hawk and a member of the "three Marys" — Mary Svoboda, [[Mary Dusak]], and a third Mary — whose domestic skills and scandalous histories were widely known. A skilled butter-maker and cook, she is cited years later by [[Antonia Shimerda|Ántonia]] as the best in the region, a testament to the lasting reputation of the hired girls' professional competence. (028-013) (043-088)

## Key Details

Mary Svoboda worked for the Cutters, the most notorious employers in Black Hawk, and her departure from their household marked a significant transition. She "went away from the Cutters" for reasons that were a matter of public knowledge, and her friend Mary Dusak was called in to replace her. (028-013) (029-008) The old men at the drugstore observed that Mary Svoboda was "similarly embarrassed" to Mary Dusak — the euphemism barely concealing an unwanted pregnancy, the occupational hazard of the hired girls' vulnerable position. (028-013)

Despite the scandal, Mary Svoboda's professional reputation was impeccable. Years after her departure from Black Hawk, Ántonia remarks: "Mary Svoboda's the best butter-maker in all around here." (043-088) The judgment carries weight — Ántonia is herself a skilled farm wife, and her praise of Mary Svoboda's butter-making is a professional compliment from one expert to another.

## In the Text

Mary Svoboda appears only indirectly in the narrative, through the recollections of others. The old men at the drugstore cigar-stand recount how "Mary Dusak had been housekeeper for a bachelor rancher from Boston, and after several years in his service she was forced to retire from the world for a short time" — and then "returned to town to take the place of her friend, Mary Svoboda, who was similarly embarrassed." (028-013) The parallel phrasing — "similarly embarrassed" — links the two Marys in a shared predicament that the town's gossip could only discuss in code.

The narrative notes that Mary Svoboda's departure had emotional consequences for those left behind. After she left the Cutters, one of the other Marys "missed her and tried to get closer to her for a long while." (029-008) This small detail — a friendship strained by distance and circumstance — humanizes the hired girls beyond their scandalous reputations.

Mary Svoboda's post-Black-Hawk life is only glimpsed through Ántonia's remark about her butter-making, which places her somewhere in the Nebraska countryside, still practicing the domestic skills that made the hired girls indispensable. (043-088)

## Significance

Mary Svoboda represents a particular path among the hired girls: the skilled domestic worker whose reputation for professional excellence outlasts the scandals of her youth. Like the other Marys, she was "considered as dangerous as high explosives to have about the kitchen," yet her butter-making is remembered as the best in the region — a paradox that captures the ambiguous position of immigrant working women in Black Hawk society. (028-013)

Her story also illustrates the network of mutual support among the hired girls. Mary Dusak replaced her at the Cutters; the two women shared not only a first name but a predicament, and their friendship — strained by distance but remembered — is one of the quiet bonds that sustain the immigrant community. In Ántonia's praise of Mary Svoboda's butter, decades later, we hear the enduring solidarity of the hired girls, whose skills and resilience outlasted the gossip that once defined them.
