# Norwegian Settlement

The Norwegian Settlement is an immigrant enclave located west of Squaw Creek on the Nebraska prairie in *My Ántonia*. It is home to several of the novel's secondary characters—Lena Lingard, Norwegian Anna, Ole Benson, and Crazy Mary—and represents one of several ethnic communities that populate Cather's portrait of frontier settlement.

## Location and Character

The settlement is defined by its position relative to the Burden farm. Jim Burden learns early that "ours was the only wooden house west of Black Hawk—until you came to the Norwegian settlement, where there were several" (002-013). The contrast between the Burdens' frame house and the "sod houses and dugouts" of their neighbors establishes the settlement as a place of rougher, more provisional habitation.

The settlement sits "west of Squaw Creek" (015-022), with the creek forming a natural boundary between the Norwegian community and the Shimerdas' Bohemian farmstead to the east. This geography of ethnic clustering is characteristic of the novel's settlement pattern: each group occupies its own stretch of prairie, connected by rough roads and the shared experience of immigration.

## Life in the Settlement

Life in the Norwegian settlement is marked by poverty, hardship, and resilience. Ole Benson lives "in a leaky dugout somewhere at the edge of the settlement," described as "fat and lazy and discouraged" with "bad luck" that "had become a habit with him" (023-041). His wife, "Crazy Mary," is sent to the asylum at Lincoln but walks "all the way home, nearly two hundred miles, travelling by night and hiding in barns and haystacks by day" (023-041). When she arrives back at the Norwegian settlement, "her poor feet were as hard as hoofs" (023-041).

The settlement has its own social institutions and codes. The Norwegian preacher's wife intervenes when Lena Lingard is seen as a temptation to married men, going through "her old trunks and found some things she had worn before her marriage" (023-042) so that Lena might appear respectably at church. The church itself is a center of community life, "the Norwegian church, west of Squaw Creek" (015-022), near which the Norwegian graveyard is located.

## A Community Under Pressure

The settlement is not static. By the time Jim Burden returns years later, "the first road that went from Black Hawk out to the north country" passes "on to the Shimerdas' and to the Norwegian settlement" (045-011), but the road itself is vanishing: "Everywhere else it had been ploughed under when the highways were surveyed; this half-mile or so within the pasture fence was all that was left of that old road which used to run like a wild thing across the open prairie" (045-011). The settlement, like the road, is being absorbed into the larger pattern of American agricultural development.

## Significance

The Norwegian settlement provides Cather with a canvas for depicting the collective experience of Scandinavian immigrants. It is both a community of mutual support—the preacher's wife helping Lena, the church providing a social framework—and a place of isolation and struggle. The settlement's dugouts, leaky roofs, and barefoot children embody the material hardship of the first generation, while the church, the preacher, and the travelers who pass through suggest the beginnings of institutional permanence.
