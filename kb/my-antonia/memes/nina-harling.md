## Overview

Nina Harling is the youngest child of [[Mr. Harling]] and [[Mrs. Harling]] of Black Hawk, Nebraska. At six years old when [[Antonia Shimerda]] comes to work for the family, Nina forms a deep attachment to the hired girl, who in turn loves Nina best among all the Harling children. Their bond is so strong that years later, Ántonia names one of her own daughters after Nina.

## Key Details

Nina is the youngest of the Harling children, only six years old during the years Ántonia works for the family. (022-006) She is a sensitive child who cries easily when upset, prompting Ántonia to comfort her. (025-017) Her mother, Mrs. Harling, sometimes sternly tells her to stop crying, threatening to send her upstairs if she cannot compose herself. (025-018)

Nina loves stories and often coaxes Ántonia to tell her tales about life in Bohemia and the old country while they wait for taffy to cool or bread to bake. (025-006) She is fond of playing hide-and-seek, and Ántonia would join her in the game. (027-001)

## In the Text

Nina's attachment to Ántonia is evident throughout the Harlings' time in the narrative. Jim Burden observes that while Ántonia "loved Nina best" of all the Harling children, the affection was mutual — Nina was deeply fond of Ántonia in return. (022-006) Mrs. Harling acknowledges that "little Nina is so fond of her," a fact that makes Ántonia's eventual departure from the Harling household all the more painful. (036-019)

The household is at its liveliest when Mr. Harling is away: "Mrs. Harling and Nina and Ántonia made as much noise as a houseful of children," filling the home with laughter and chatter. (022-010) On quiet afternoons, while the family made candy, "Nina used to coax Ántonia to tell her stories" about Bohemia — tales of wolves and bears and the old life that Ántonia's own children would later beg to hear. (025-006)

One spring, Jim Burden invested romantic effort in "hanging a May-basket for Nina Harling," a gesture that reveals Nina's place at the heart of the neighborhood's affections. (032-012) Jim bought flowers and carefully prepared the basket, a small ceremony of childhood friendship.

The most poignant testament to Nina's significance comes years later. When Jim visits Ántonia on her farm, Ántonia introduces one of her daughters: "She's Nina, after Nina Harling." (043-032) The name lives on as a tribute to the little girl who first showed Ántonia love and acceptance in an American household. Ántonia later remarks that one of her boys reminds her of Nina Harling in his hardworking, determined nature. (043-059)

## Significance

Nina Harling represents the bonds of affection that cross ethnic and class lines in the novel. The love between this American-born child and the Bohemian hired girl is pure and uncomplicated by the prejudices that divide the adults of Black Hawk. Nina's unquestioning acceptance of Ántonia models the possibility of a more generous, less stratified community.

The naming of Ántonia's daughter after Nina closes a circle of memory and love. It suggests that the warmth Ántonia received in the Harling household became part of her own understanding of family — something she carried forward into her life as a mother and passed on to the next generation. A six-year-old girl's affection echoes across decades, preserved in a name spoken on a Nebraska farm.
