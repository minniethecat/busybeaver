## Overview

Jake Marpole is a "mountain boy" from Virginia who works as a hired hand on the Burden farm in Nebraska. He is most prominent in the novel's opening chapters, where he accompanies the young narrator Jim Burden on the cross-country train journey to Black Hawk:

> "I was ten years old then; I had lost both my father and mother within a year, and my Virginia relatives were sending me out to my grandparents, who lived in Nebraska. I travelled in the care of a mountain boy, Jake Marpole, one of the 'hands' on my father's old farm under the Blue Ridge, who was now going West to work for my grandfather." (001-001)

Jake is a simple, good-natured fellow — less sharp than his fellow farmhand Otto Fuchs, but loyal and well-meaning.

## Character

Jake is portrayed as cheerful but "duller than Otto." On the train journey, he buys Jim every treat the newsboy offers and settles in to read about Jesse James:

> "Jake bought everything the newsboy offered him: candy, oranges, brass collar buttons, a watch-charm, and for me a 'Life of Jesse James,' which I remember as one of the most satisfactory books I have ever read." (001-002)

In the evenings on the farm, Jake and Otto would sit on the long bench outside the bunk-house, "Jake's shaggy hair slicked flat in place with water" (009-011), talking about the West. Jake could never remember the details of the stories Otto told: "he could never remember the particulars of anything that had happened to him" (009-012).

## On the Farm

Jake works alongside Otto Fuchs under Grandfather Burden. He helps with the Christmas preparations:

> "During the week before Christmas, Jake was the most important person of our household, for he was to go to town and do all our Christmas shopping." (011-001)

Jake takes this responsibility seriously, certain he can make it to Black Hawk and back despite the winter snow.

## Visit to the Shimerdas

When Grandmother Burden prepares a hamper of food for the struggling Shimerda family, Jake accompanies her to their dugout. Grandmother instructs him firmly on how to behave:

> "'Now, Jake,' grandmother was saying, 'if you can manage that team better not to leave them standing in the cold, and when you go into the house you are not to make remarks about how it smells or how it looks, and you are not to repeat anything you hear.'" (010-008)

Jake obeys dutifully and helps carry the provisions into the Shimerda dugout.

## Significance

Jake represents the class of hired farm laborers common on the Nebraska frontier — uneducated but reliable men who worked for room, board, and modest wages. He provides a contrast to the more colorful Otto Fuchs: where Otto embellishes his past with tall tales, Jake is content with the simpler truth.

His role also frames the novel's opening: as the adult Jim Burden looks back, the memory of Jake — good-natured, uncomplicated, rooted in the Virginia home Jim has lost — is part of the nostalgic fabric of the prairie years.
