# Americanization

Americanization in *My Ántonia* is not a single event but a gradual, uneven process that transforms immigrant identity across a generation. Cather traces this transformation through language, labor, and social ambition, showing how Bohemian, Norwegian, and Russian settlers become American—and what is lost in that becoming.

## The Language Threshold

The first barrier—and the first bridge—is language. When the Shimerdas arrive, only Ántonia can speak any English at all: "'They can't any of them speak English, except one little girl'" (001-004). Without English, immigrant families are vulnerable to exploitation and isolation. The Shimerdas "could not speak enough English to ask for advice, or even to make their most pressing wants known" (003-003), leaving them dependent on Peter Krajiek, their dishonest countryman.

Learning English becomes a family project. Grandmother Burden insists that "the elder of the family should learn English" (004-005), and Ántonia applies herself with characteristic determination: "whenever we went to visit her, she ran up to me and began to talk, picking up phrases whenever she heard English spoken" (010-027). The two-alphabet primer—"one English and the other Bohemian" (003-023)—that Jim's grandfather gives to Ántonia symbolizes the dual literacy required of the immigrant: competence in the new language without total abandonment of the old.

## The Hired Girls

The "hired girls" represent Americanization through labor. Young immigrant women—Ántonia, Lena Lingard, Tiny Soderball—leave their families' farms to work in Black Hawk households, earning wages, learning American domestic customs, and encountering American social hierarchies. The term itself is freighted with class judgment: the Black Hawk matrons use it to distinguish these working immigrant daughters from their own "American" children (028-008).

This labor pathway is also a path to independence. The hired girls earn their own money, make their own decisions, and—in Lena's and Tiny's cases—build successful careers. The condescension they face ("condemnation for a waltz with 'the hired girls'" [027-009]) is balanced by their growing economic agency. Americanization, for these women, is inseparable from feminization of labor—the transformation from peasant daughters to wage-earning workers.

## Becoming American

The novel traces Americanization across the full arc of a life. Ántonia begins as a Bohemian child who can barely communicate, passes through the hired-girl years of linguistic and social adaptation, and ends as a Nebraska farm wife whose children "spoke to each other in their rich old language" (043-089)—Bohemian persisting as the intimate tongue even in an American-born generation. Americanization, Cather suggests, is additive rather than substitutive: the old identity is not erased but layered beneath the new.

There is a cost, however. Jim Burden's observation that the American-born children of immigrants "don't have" the "spiced plums" of the old country (043-047) functions as a small parable. Americanization brings prosperity and belonging, but it also attenuates the sensory, culinary, and linguistic richness of the immigrant past. What is gained in citizenship is shadowed by what is lost in flavor.

## An Ambiguous Triumph

Cather resists both triumphalist assimilation narratives and elegiac tales of cultural loss. The immigrants of *My Ántonia* become American on their own terms, at their own pace, and with varying degrees of success. Ambrosch Shimerda masters English only when it serves his economic interests (017-018); Lena Lingard Americanizes so thoroughly that she becomes a successful dressmaker in San Francisco. The novel refuses to rank these outcomes, treating Americanization as a complex negotiation rather than a pass/fail test.
