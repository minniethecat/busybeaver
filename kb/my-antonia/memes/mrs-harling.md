## Overview

Mrs. Harling is the formidable yet warm-hearted matriarch of the Harling family, the Burdens' nearest neighbors in Black Hawk. She becomes Ántonia Shimerda's employer and one of the most important adult influences on the teenage immigrant girl. Jim Burden's grandmother "thanked God she lived next the Harlings" (021-001), and the sentiment reflects the esteem in which the family was held.

Jim's portrait of her emphasizes her vitality from the first sentence:

> "Mrs. Harling was short and square and sturdy-looking, like her house. Every inch of her was charged with an energy that made itself felt the moment she entered a room. Her face was rosy and solid, with bright, twinkling eyes and a stubborn little chin." (021-002)

## Character and Household

The Harling household was a lively, cultured place. Three of the Harling children were near Jim's age — Charley, Julia, and Sally — and the grown-up daughter Frances was "a very important person in our world" (021-004), managing her father's Black Hawk office. Mrs. Harling herself was the household's emotional and practical center.

When their Danish cook left in August, grandmother Burden immediately recommended Ántonia (021-007). Mrs. Harling and her family "had liked Ántonia from their first glimpse of her; felt they knew exactly what kind of girl she was" (021-010).

## Relationship with Ántonia

Mrs. Harling's relationship with Ántonia was both maternal and disciplinary. She saw the girl's potential and was determined to shape it:

> "Oh, I'm not worrying, Mrs. Burden! I can bring something out of that girl. She's barely seventeen, not too old to learn new ways. She's good-looking, too!" (021-013)

Her approach to the Shimerdas was practical and amused rather than judgmental. She "chuckled whenever she spoke of" Mrs. Shimerda, saying: "I expect I am more at home with that sort of woman than you are, Mrs. Burden. They are a good deal like my own people in spirit" (021-010).

She also stood firm against Ambrosch's attempt to control Ántonia's wages, engaging in "a long argument with Ambrosch about Ántonia's allowance for clothes and pocket-money" (021-011). This intervention was significant — it established that Ántonia, not her brother, had rights to at least some of what she earned.

## Domestic Life and Family

The Harling home was described as "like a little farm, with a big barn and a garden, and an orchard and grazing lots" (021-001) — a transplanted piece of country life in town. Frances played piano and talked to her mother through open windows in the evening (021-008). Mr. Harling, though often absent on business, indulged his son Charley's expensive tastes (021-004).

Mrs. Harling's laugh was "quick" and "decided" (021-013) — a woman who knew her own mind and did not apologize for it.

## Significance

Mrs. Harling represents the settled, prosperous American matriarch in contrast to the struggling immigrant mothers like Mrs. Shimerda. Her competence and stability offered Ántonia a model of American domestic life different from both the Burden farm and the Shimerda dugout.

She also plays a crucial narrative role: the Harlings' home is the setting for key scenes in Book II — including Lena Lingard's introduction to town society and Ántonia's gradual transformation from farm hand to young woman. When Ántonia eventually leaves the Harlings over the dancing dispute, it marks a turning point in her adolescence and foreshadows her later troubles.
