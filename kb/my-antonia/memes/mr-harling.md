## Overview

Mr. Harling is the father of the Harling family and a successful businessman in Black Hawk, Nebraska. He is the husband of [[Mrs. Harling]] and the father of [[Frances Harling]], [[Charley Harling]], [[Sally Harling]], and [[Nina Harling]]. A man of quiet authority, he maintains a disciplined household while indulging his children's ambitions, most notably Charley's preparation for the Naval Academy at Annapolis.

## Key Details

Mr. Harling is a businessman who keeps a desk in his bedroom, suggesting that his commercial affairs occupy him even in the private quarters of the home. (022-008) He is a figure of palpable gravity in the household: Jim Burden notes that his shadow loomed over the domestic space, and when he was at home, a certain restraint fell over the house. (025-004)

Yet Mr. Harling is not a harsh father. He was "very indulgent" with Charley, buying his son a fur coat and cap for the winter and supporting his ambition to enter Annapolis. (021-004) Jim observes Mr. Harling walking home with his daughter on winter evenings, a picture of quiet paternal warmth. (021-005)

He has his working slippers made from his old coats, a practical, frugal detail that reveals the frontier ethic of making use of what one has. (022-005) He is also known to go down to the cellar for beer on Saturday nights, a small domestic ritual. (029-004)

## In the Text

Mr. Harling first appears through his expectations for his household. He "not only demanded a quiet house, he demanded a quiet house," a repetition that emphasizes his insistence on order and calm. (022-007) Mrs. Harling and the girls — including [[Antonia Shimerda]], who works for the family — make "as much noise as a houseful of children," but only when Mr. Harling is not present. (022-010)

His protective nature is evident when he is awakened from his "first sleep" by a commotion at the back gate, rising to investigate a crisis that threatens the household's peace. (029-003) On another occasion, he had gone to the cellar for beer when an incident occurred, his absence creating a moment of vulnerability. (029-004)

Jim Burden describes Mr. Harling as seeming "automatic" — a man of routine and predictable habits, solid and reliable rather than spontaneous or expressive. (022-009) This quality makes him a steady anchor for the lively Harling household, a counterweight to the exuberance of his wife and daughters.

## Significance

Mr. Harling represents the successful American businessman on the frontier — a man who has built prosperity through discipline and hard work, and who extends that discipline to his family life. His support for Charley's Annapolis education shows an aspiration for his children to rise beyond Black Hawk, while his quiet authority provides the stability that allows the Harling household to function as a haven for the hired girls.

In contrast to immigrant fathers like [[Mr. Shimerda]], Mr. Harling embodies the established, rooted American patriarch. His household is orderly, his children are well-fed and well-dressed, and his place in the community is secure. This contrast deepens the novel's exploration of the different fates that await immigrant and native-born families on the prairie.
