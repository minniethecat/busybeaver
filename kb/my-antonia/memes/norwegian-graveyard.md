# Norwegian Graveyard

The Norwegian Graveyard is a small burial ground near the Norwegian church, west of Squaw Creek, in *My Ántonia*. It becomes the focus of a community debate about religious boundaries when Mr. Shimerda's suicide demands a burial place.

## Burials of the Outcast

The graveyard first appears in connection with Pavel, the Russian laborer who dies after confessing his terrible secret to Mr. Shimerda. After his death, Pavel "was buried in the Norwegian graveyard" (008-031), and his partner Peter "sold off everything, and left the country" (008-031).

Pavel's burial establishes the graveyard as a place that receives those who exist at the margins of the community—immigrants without family, without wealth, and without a church of their own.

## The Shimerda Burial Controversy

The graveyard's most significant role comes after Mr. Shimerda's suicide. The nearest Catholic cemetery is at Black Hawk, "and it might be weeks before a wagon could get so far" (015-022). Moreover, "a man who had killed himself could not be buried in a Catholic graveyard" (015-022).

The Norwegian settlers initially seem to offer a solution: "There was a burying-ground over by the Norwegian church, west of Squaw Creek; perhaps the Norwegians would take Mr. Shimerda in" (015-022). But the hope is short-lived. After a meeting, "the officers of the Norwegian church... decided that the Norwegian graveyard could not extend its hospitality to Mr. Shimerda" (015-024).

The rejection is both theological and communal—the Norwegian Lutherans are no more willing than the Catholics to receive a suicide. The postmaster's report of this decision spreads through the snow-blocked community, as neighbors gather at the Burdens' house "eager for any details about the suicide" and "greatly concerned as to where Mr. Shimerda would be buried" (015-022).

## A Corner of the Shimerda Farm

The rejection by the Norwegian graveyard leads directly to Mr. Shimerda's burial at the corner of his own farm—a plot that, years later, Jim Burden finds marked by a weathered cross at a crossroads.

Ironically, Mr. Shimerda himself had anticipated the controversy: "I don't want the Norwegians holding inquisitions over me... I'll have to have an American graveyard that will be more liberal-minded" (015-025). His exclusion from institutional graveyards becomes, paradoxically, the source of one of the novel's most resonant images: the suicide's grave at the intersection of prairie roads.

## Significance

The Norwegian graveyard embodies the limits of immigrant community solidarity. The Norwegians share displacement and hardship with the Bohemians, yet their church draws a firm line at burying a Catholic suicide. The graveyard becomes a site where religious difference and the question of belonging are negotiated.
