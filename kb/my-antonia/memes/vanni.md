## Overview

Mr. and Mrs. Vanni are the operators of the dancing pavilion that arrives in Black Hawk one summer, bringing music, dance, and social transformation to the quiet prairie town. Mrs. Vanni teaches the children's afternoon dancing classes while Mr. Vanni, a harpist, instructs the older pupils and provides the music that draws the town's young people together.

## Key Details

The dancing pavilion was erected near the Danish laundry, on a vacant lot surrounded by tall cottonwood trees, resembling a merry-go-round tent with open sides and gay flags. (027-004) Mrs. Vanni received the children at the entrance, always dressed in lavender with black lace, her important watch-chain displayed on her bosom. She wore her hair built up "in a black tower, with red coral combs" and showed "two rows of strong, crooked yellow teeth" when she smiled. (027-004)

Mr. Vanni played the harp, producing music described as "the deep purring of Mr. Vanni's harp" that came "in silvery ripples through the blackness of the dusty-smelling night." (027-007) Together, the Vannis maintained "exemplary order" at their establishment, closing every evening precisely at the hour suggested by the city council. When the harp struck up "Home, Sweet Home," all Black Hawk knew it was ten o'clock. (027-006)

## In the Text

The arrival of the Vannis' tent marked a turning point in Black Hawk's social life. Before the pavilion, summer evenings had been oppressively quiet — married people sitting silently on porches, young people tramping the board sidewalks with nowhere to go. The tent created a space "where the girls could wear their new dresses, and where one could laugh aloud without being reproved by the ensuing silence." (027-007)

The Progressive Euchre Club arranged with the Vannis for exclusive use of the floor on Tuesday and Friday nights, while other nights were open to railroad men, mechanics, delivery boys, and farm-hands. (027-008) The tent served as neutral ground where "town boys and country girls" could meet — a rare space of social mixing in a stratified community. (028-014)

It was at the Vannis' tent that [[Antonia Shimerda]] was "discovered." The Vannis often said that Ántonia was the best dancer of them all. (029-001) The enthusiasm for dance that they kindled did not die out when the tent left town; the Euchre Club became the Owl Club and continued holding weekly dances at the Masonic Hall. (031-013) Weeks later, at the Firemen's Hall dances, Jim Burden met "the same people I used to see at the Vannis' tent." (031-021)

## Significance

The Vannis are catalysts of social transformation in Black Hawk. Their dancing pavilion disrupts the town's rigid social order by creating a shared space where class and ethnic boundaries temporarily dissolve. The hired girls — immigrant daughters like [[Lena Lingard]], [[Tiny Soderball]], and [[Antonia Shimerda]] — find at the tent a rare public arena where they can be seen, admired, and courted on their own merits rather than defined by their domestic servitude.

The Vannis themselves are outsiders who bring cosmopolitan culture to the prairie — the harp, the formal dance instruction, the European sense of festivity. Yet they operate within the town's rules, closing at ten o'clock as the council requires, maintaining what Jim Burden calls "exemplary order." Their tent represents a middle ground between wildness and respectability, and it is in this middle ground that Ántonia and the other hired girls begin to claim a public identity of their own.
