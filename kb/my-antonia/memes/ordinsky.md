## Overview

Ordinsky is a Polish immigrant and violinist who lives in the same building as [[Lena Lingard]] in Lincoln, Nebraska. Passionate, proud, and temperamental, he appoints himself Lena's protector against unwanted male attention and harbors strong opinions about the musical taste — or lack thereof — of the citizens of Lincoln.

## Key Details

Ordinsky is a musician who gives music lessons in Lincoln. (038-015) He owns a set of dress clothes and occasionally performs at concerts. (038-019) Jim Burden describes him as having "excitable, slanting brown eyes" and a head "the shape of a chocolate drop," covered with "dry, straw-coloured hair that fuzzed up about his pointed crown." (038-020)

He is deeply suspicious of any man who shows attention to Lena. His protective instinct borders on the theatrical: he once presented himself at Lena's door to offer to put a stop to the old Colonel Raleigh's attentions toward her, saying that if the landlord was "annoying her," he would "promptly put a stop to it." (038-015) Lena describes him as "so sort of wild all the time." (038-016)

## In the Text

Ordinsky's most vivid appearance occurs on an evening when he arrives at Lena's door in distress — his waistcoat has split down the back just before a concert. (038-019) [[Lena Lingard]] takes the garment into her workroom and replaces the ruined lining-silk in ten minutes, leaving Jim Burden to confront the Pole alone. Ordinsky makes a formal declaration: "Miss Lingard is a young woman for whom I have the utmost, the utmost respect." (038-020) When Jim assures him that he has known Lena a long time and appreciates her kindness, Ordinsky warns him against "trifling with" her. (038-025) Jim retorts that in their world, "a girl who makes her own living can ask a college boy to supper without being talked about." (038-027)

Ordinsky also channels his intensity into cultural criticism. He writes a furious article attacking the musical taste of Lincoln and asks Jim to deliver it to the editor of the morning paper, threatening that the editor would be answerable to Ordinsky "in person" if he refused to print it. (038-031) The article appears full of typographical errors that Ordinsky believes were intentional, yet he derives satisfaction from the belief that the citizens of Lincoln have "meekly accepted the epithet 'coarse barbarians.'" (038-031)

After Jim stands by him during this episode, Ordinsky becomes friendly toward him and behaves "as if there were some deep understanding between us." (038-031) He carries himself with increased assurance, striding up front porches and ringing doorbells with fresh disdain.

## Significance

Ordinsky embodies the figure of the exiled European artist on the American frontier — passionate, cultured by his own lights, and fundamentally ill-fitted to the practical, commercial society of a Western city like Lincoln. His exaggerated sense of honor and his conviction that Lincoln lacks "chivalry" and "amour-propre" speak to the cultural dislocation felt by many European immigrants who found themselves in communities that did not share their artistic traditions.

His fierce protectiveness toward Lena, while at times comic, also reveals the vulnerability of young immigrant women living alone in the city. In his awkward, theatrical way, Ordinsky recognizes that Lena needs allies — and he is determined to be one.
