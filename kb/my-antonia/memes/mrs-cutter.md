## Overview

Mrs. Cutter is the wife of Wick Cutter, the unscrupulous money-lender of Black Hawk. She is described as a frightening figure who matched her husband in temperament and cunning. Their marriage was a battleground of mutual suspicion, jealousy, and power struggles, particularly over the question of inheritance. Her death in Book V brings the Cutter saga to a close.

## Key Details

- Described as "a terrifying-looking person" with a monumental physical presence (030-005)
- Painted china assiduously, covering it with "all sorts of improbable flowers and impossible scenes" (030-006)
- Jealous of her husband's relationships with younger women (031-001)
- Engaged in a long-running battle with her husband over inheritance and household expenses (030-008, 030-009)
- The narrator notes he has "found Mrs. Cutters all over the world; sometimes founding new religions, sometimes being forcibly fed" (030-010)

## In the Text

Mrs. Cutter is introduced as a formidable physical and psychological presence. Jim Burden describes her as someone of whom Wick Cutter "met his match" when he married her (030-005). Her china painting produced works of dubious artistic merit but unmistakable ambition — "improbable flowers and impossible scenes" that she displayed to the town (030-006).

The Cutters' marriage was defined by conflict. Mrs. Cutter "had several times cut paragraphs out of the Black Hawk newspaper with a razor and pasted them to the door of his office" (030-007), publicly embarrassing her husband. On the question of inheritance, "Mrs. Cutter told her husband it was plain to any judge that if he predeceased her, the property must descend to her" (030-008). When disagreements about household expenses arose, she would put on her brocade and visit the bank president and the Protestant churches, "securing each end of the row against him" (030-009).

A dramatic episode occurs when Mrs. Cutter returns home unexpectedly one night to find the house locked. Jim Burden, who had been staying there as a temporary occupant, witnesses her fury as she pounds on the door (034-016). She later tells her story to Grandmother Burden, accompanied by threats against her husband: "The law will protect me, Mrs. Burden. He will pay!" (034-021).

In the novel's final book, Mrs. Cutter's death is reported with a grim touch. Wick Cutter, "with his lips closely pursed and his eyes fixed on the ceiling," pronounces: "Mrs. Cutter is quite dead, gentlemen, and I am sure you will all be pleased to hear it" (044-027).

## Significance

Mrs. Cutter, along with her husband, represents the dark underside of small-town life that the novel refuses to sentimentalize. The Cutters' marriage is a study in mutual predation, in which money and power have replaced any trace of affection. Jim's observation that he has "found Mrs. Cutters all over the world" (030-010) universalizes her type — the formidable, manipulative woman who channels thwarted ambition into domestic tyranny. Her death, and Wick Cutter's cold response to it, closes one of the novel's most cynical storylines with bitter finality.
