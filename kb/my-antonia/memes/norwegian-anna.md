## Overview

Norwegian Anna is one of the hired girls in Black Hawk, Nebraska, employed by the Marshall family. Dignified and reserved, she is part of the close-knit circle of immigrant working girls — along with [[Lena Lingard]], [[Tiny Soderball]], and [[Antonia Shimerda]] — who reshape the town's social landscape during the summer of the dancing pavilion.

## Key Details

Anna is consistently described as "always dignified," a quality that sets her apart from the other hired girls. (031-006) She works for the Marshall family, and the young men of Black Hawk refer to her as "the Marshalls' Anna," just as they call Ántonia "the Harlings' Tony" and Tiny "the Gardeners' Tiny." (029-001)

She drops in to help Ántonia with her housework so that Tony can get away early for the dancing pavilion, demonstrating the solidarity among the hired girls. (029-003) She has a young man of her own and attends the weekly dances at the Firemen's Hall. (031-027)

## In the Text

Norwegian Anna appears as a steadying presence among the more high-spirited hired girls. When Lena and Tiny are laughing at a joke at the bakery, "a glance from Norwegian Anna checked them" — the high-school principal had just entered the shop, and Anna knew the importance of maintaining appearances. (031-012)

She is part of the fashionable afternoon promenades downtown, walking along the board sidewalk with Tiny, Lena, and Ántonia, "two and two," while the high-school boys linger on the playground to watch them pass. (031-002) The young men of the town refer to the hired girls by their employers' names, a practice that both identifies their subordinate status and, paradoxically, acknowledges their individual reputations.

On one significant evening, Ántonia comes to the Firemen's Hall dance with "Norwegian Anna and her young man," and it is that night that Jim Burden walks Ántonia home and asks her to kiss him. (031-027) Anna's presence as a chaperone figure reflects her role as the most proper and restrained of the hired girls.

## Significance

Norwegian Anna represents a quieter strand of the immigrant experience — less dramatic than Ántonia's story, less entrepreneurial than Lena's, but no less dignified. Her consistent characterization as "always dignified" suggests a young woman navigating the precarious position of the hired girl with careful composure and self-possession.

Her presence also illustrates the collective nature of the hired girls' experience. They are not isolated individuals but a network: they help each other with housework, walk downtown together, attend dances as a group, and serve as chaperones and character witnesses for one another. Anna's reproving glance at the bakery and her steady companionship at the dances are small but significant acts of mutual protection in a town that is often quick to judge them.
