## Overview

Peter Krajiek is the first Bohemian the Shimerdas encounter upon arriving in Nebraska, and he becomes their exploiter rather than their ally. He sold the Shimerdas their homestead at an inflated price, along with a sod dugout that was barely fit for human habitation. As the only fellow countryman who could speak their language, he held a monopoly on their trust — and abused it.

Jim Burden's grandmother summarizes the situation bluntly:

> "The Shimerdas were the first Bohemian family to come to this part of the county. Krajiek was their only interpreter, and he could tell them anything he chose." (003-003)

## Exploitation of the Shimerdas

Krajiek's exploitation was comprehensive. He sold the Shimerdas land at more than its value, along with substandard livestock and equipment. Grandmother Burden saw through him immediately:

> "They had paid him more than it was worth, and all the while he was cheating them out of their few possessions. No wonder Krajiek got the better of these people, who were strange to the country and spoke no English." (003-021)

He encouraged the Shimerdas to stay isolated — "Krajiek encouraged them in the belief that the other Bohemian families in the county would cheat them" (004-008) — ensuring he remained their sole source of information and their only market for purchases.

The dugout he sold them as a home was more cave than house, a single cramped room with an earth floor. Grandmother Burden declared: "It's no better than a badger hole" (003-004).

## A Cowardly Figure

Krajiek was not merely dishonest; he was also cowardly. When Mr. Shimerda died, Krajiek's behavior drew suspicion from the entire community. Jake Marpole voiced his contempt plainly:

> "I hate to think of Krajiek getting a leg of that old rooster." (010-009)

The Burdens shared the unease. Grandfather Burden said to Grandmother:

> "Josiah, you don't suppose Krajiek would let them poor creatures starve, do you?" (010-004)

His axe was found under the manger where Mr. Shimerda's body lay, adding to the suspicion — though the death was clearly suicide by gunshot, the detail of the hidden axe underscored Krajiek's guilty conscience. When Jake Marpole found it, he said: "I found Krajiek's axe under the manger, and I picks it up and carries it over to the house" (014-012).

## Significance

Peter Krajiek represents the dark side of the immigrant experience — the exploitation of the vulnerable by those who should have been their natural allies. He is a fellow countryman who preys on his own people's ignorance of the new country, a figure who stands in direct opposition to the novel's many examples of immigrant solidarity and mutual aid.

His role also establishes the Shimerdas' vulnerability from the very first pages. Before they even appear on the page, we understand they have been cheated, isolated, and set up to fail. The harshness of their first winter is not just a natural hardship — it is compounded by Krajiek's deliberate exploitation. This makes their eventual survival and Ántonia's flourishing all the more remarkable.
