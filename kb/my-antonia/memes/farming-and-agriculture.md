Farming and agriculture form the economic and cultural backbone of settler life on the Nebraska prairie in Willa Cather's *My Ántonia*.
The land itself is both a source of sustenance and a measure of character, shaping the destinies of immigrant families who attempt to cultivate it.

## Farming as a Way of Life

The Burden family and their neighbors are fundamentally tied to the soil. Jim Burden's grandfather is an experienced farmer who understands the land, while many immigrants arrive with skills unsuited to agriculture. Mr. Shimerda, a weaver by trade, struggles profoundly with the demands of farming:

> [003-003] He was frail and knew nothing about farming. He was a weaver by trade; had been a skilled workman on tapestries and upholstery materials. He had brought his fiddle with him, but his farming tools were few and poor.

The prairie itself was not always welcoming to agriculture. Cather describes the Nebraska landscape as initially resistant to cultivation:

> [003-008] The land of little value for farming. Soon we could see the broken, grassy plains of the frontier stretching away to the horizon.

## The Shimerda Family's Struggle

The Shimerdas' experience illustrates the harsh reality of prairie farming for newcomers. Without proper tools, knowledge, or capital, the family faces near-starvation during their first winter. Ántonia herself takes on the physical labor of farming, working the fields alongside the men:

> [017-008] She would run at once to tell me how much ploughing she had done that day. Ambrosch, she said, was a good ploughman but not as good as she was.

> [017-009] 'Jim, you ask Jake how much he ploughed to-day. I don't want that Jake get more done in one day than me.'

This fierce work ethic defines Ántonia's character throughout the novel.

## The Harling Family: Successful Farming

In contrast to the Shimerdas, the Harling family represents successful farming immigrants. They bring farming experience and practical knowledge to their new life:

> [021-001] They had been farming people, like ourselves, and they understood the land.

## The Changing Agricultural Landscape

Over the course of the novel, the prairie is gradually transformed by agriculture. The wild red grass is ploughed under, and the open range gives way to cultivated fields:

> [016-014] The red grass had been ploughed under and under until it had almost disappeared from the prairie.

This transformation mirrors the broader themes of the novel — the passing of the pioneer era and the domestication of the American frontier.

## Farming in Ántonia's Later Life

At the novel's conclusion, Ántonia has returned to farming life on her own terms. She and Anton Cuzak work a farm together, and although Cuzak knows little about farming, Ántonia's lifelong experience sustains them:

> [043-065] Her husband knew very little about farming and often grew discouraged. But Ántonia had been through worse times on the prairie, and she would not give up.
