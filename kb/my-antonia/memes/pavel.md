## Overview

Pavel is a Russian immigrant who, with his friend Peter, lived near the Shimerdas on the Nebraska prairie. His name is anglicized from an unpronounceable Russian original: "They had no other names than Pavel and Peter, and went about making the mistake of thinking everyone could pronounce them." (005-003)

Pavel was often ill, and the Shimerdas kept a cow partly for his sake:

> "The milk was good for Pavel, who was often sick, and he could buttermilk with the rest of us." (005-007)

But behind his quiet, sickly presence on the prairie lies one of the novel's darkest episodes — a story of guilt and death by wolves that haunts Pavel to his final hour.

## The Deathbed Confession

In Chapter 8, Pavel falls gravely ill after straining himself lifting timber for barn-building:

> "Pavel strained himself lifting timbers for a new barn, and fell over among the shavings with such a gush of blood from the lungs that the men thought him dead." (008-002)

Mr. Shimerda and Krajiek drove to fetch a priest, but it was too late. Pavel was dying. As he lay gasping in the dugout, he began to speak — in Russian, to Mr. Shimerda alone:

> "Presently Pavel began to talk to Mr. Shimerda, scarcely above a whisper. He was telling a long story, and as he told it, Ántonia's father grew more and more troubled." (008-010)

The story Pavel told was the defining horror of his life. Years ago in Russia, he and Peter were groomsmen at a friend's wedding. Driving the bride and groom in a sleigh through the forest at night, they were attacked by a pack of wolves:

> "When Pavel and Peter were young men, living in Russia, they were asked to be groomsmen for a friend who was about to marry the belle of the village. The party was driving home from the church in six sledges, and Pavel and Peter were in the last sledge with the bridegroom and bride." (008-015)

The wolves swarmed the sledge-train. As the horses behind them were pulled down one by one, the terror mounted. Finally, with the wolves closing on their own sledge, Pavel made a terrible choice: he threw the bride and groom from the sledge to lighten the load, sacrificing them to save himself and Peter. They escaped, but both men were exiled from their village — "They were alone, and they were hunted." The guilt consumed Pavel's life.

## Death

Pavel died that night in the Shimerda dugout. Mr. Shimerda, deeply troubled by what he had heard, "never recovered his spirits" after Pavel's story. Five weeks later, Mr. Shimerda took his own life — and the novel links Pavel's dark confession to that tragedy, as if the weight of such primal guilt was too much for Mr. Shimerda's sensitive soul to bear.

> "Pavel made a rasping sound when he breathed, and he kept praying in a loud voice: 'God, have mercy on a sinful man!'" (008-006)

## Significance

Pavel's wolf story is one of the novel's most important embedded narratives. It introduces a note of Old World darkness into the prairie landscape — a reminder that the immigrants brought not only dreams but also memories of horror. The story's theme — the desperate will to survive at any moral cost — echoes throughout the novel, from the Shimerdas' own struggle against starvation to the broader immigrant experience of sacrificing the past for the future.

Pavel's death also functions as a grim prelude to Mr. Shimerda's suicide. Both men are crushed by memories they cannot escape, and both find the Nebraska prairie — for all its promise — unable to heal Old World wounds.
