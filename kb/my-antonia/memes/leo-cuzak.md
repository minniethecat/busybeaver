## Overview

Leo Cuzak is the son of [[Antonia Shimerda|Ántonia Cuzak]] and [[Anton Cuzak]], growing up on the Cuzak farm in Nebraska. Presented in Book V as a lively, impertinent boy, Leo embodies the irrepressible spirit of the second generation — bold, curious, and utterly unafraid of strangers. His mother calls him "the worst of all," yet his sauciness and talent at the piano make him one of the most memorable of Ántonia's children.

## Key Details

Leo is old enough that his mother expects better behavior from him. When introducing him to Jim Burden, Ántonia remarks, "This is Leo, and he's old enough to be better than he is." (043-023) He is a source of both exasperation and amusement to his mother, who singles him out as the family mischief-maker. (043-034)

Despite — or because of — his impudence, Leo is a gifted performer. When the family gathers in the parlour, he plays the piano for the visitor alongside his sister [[Yulka Shimerda|Yulka]]. (043-085) He speaks Bohemian with his mother, maintaining the family's connection to its immigrant roots. (043-086)

## In the Text

Leo makes a vivid first impression when Jim Burden visits the Cuzak farm. As Ántonia rushes about calling for her children, she shouts: "And call Leo. Where is that Leo!" (043-020) When he finally appears, she presents him with a mixture of affection and exasperation: "That Leo; he's the worst of all." (043-034)

Throughout the visit, Leo's behavior confirms his reputation. He "tossed off some sort of remark" in a low voice — the kind of sotto voce comment that earns him the rebuke from his uncle [[Ambrosch Shimerda]]: "Always too fresh, Leo." (043-048) (043-050) When confronted, Leo ducks behind his mother, using her as a shield against adult disapproval. (043-051)

Leo shows a more vulnerable side when he begs to sleep in the hayloft with the visitor, suggesting a boyish hero-worship beneath his bravado. (043-070) He accompanies Jim on a walk to see the cows, keeping "at some distance" in a display of mingled curiosity and shyness. (043-073)

The boy is also observant and clever. According to family lore, "Leo says he recognized you when you come, by your photograph in the Chicago paper" — a detail that flatters the adult Jim while revealing Leo's sharp eye and pride in the family's famous friend. (043-082)

In the parlour, Leo and Yulka play the piano for Jim. Ántonia speaks to Leo in Bohemian, and he responds — though "he frowned and wrinkled up his face" at whatever instruction she gives, a moment that captures his resistance to authority even in performance. (043-085) (043-086)

When family photographs are brought out, "only Leo was unmoved" by the legendary tales attached to them. (043-091) He then pokes out "a supple red tongue" at his mother from behind the photographs — the gesture of a born entertainer who knows exactly how far he can push. (043-095) Ántonia scolds him: "He's always so saucy and impertinent to him, Leo, like you are with people sometimes." (043-096)

## Significance

Leo Cuzak is the living proof of Ántonia's vitality carried into the next generation. His sauciness, his musical talent, his Bohemian speech, and his irrepressible energy all reflect his mother's own character. Where Ambrosch sees impertinence, Ántonia sees a spirit she half-admires even as she scolds it — Leo is "the worst of all" in the same way that young Ántonia was once the most spirited of the hired girls.

Leo's comfort with both Bohemian and English, his ease with visitors, and his sense of belonging on the Nebraska farm all speak to the successful integration of the immigrant family into American life. He is not a divided soul like his grandfather [[Mr. Shimerda]], but a fully realized product of the new world — rooted, confident, and unapologetically himself.
