## Overview

Martha Cuzak is the eldest daughter of [[Antonia Shimerda|Ántonia Cuzak]] and [[Anton Cuzak]], and the child Jim Burden first glimpsed as a baby during his earlier encounter with Ántonia in Black Hawk. By the time of Jim's visit in Book V, Martha has grown into a capable young woman who helps manage the household and whose absence is deeply felt by her mother. (043-065)

## Key Details

Martha is the child Jim saw as a baby — "the one you saw when she was a baby," Ántonia reminds him — making her the eldest daughter and a bridge between Ántonia's past as a hired girl and her present as the matriarch of a thriving farm family. (043-065)

When Jim visits, Martha is away from home. Ántonia repeatedly expresses her wish that Martha could be present: "I wish my Martha could come down to see you." (044-003) The depth of Ántonia's feeling is revealed when she adds, "She went round crying, when Martha was so happy, and the rest of us were all so glad" — a memory that connects Martha's happiness to a family celebration, perhaps her wedding. (044-004)

The narrative also reveals a surprising detail about Martha's parentage. Ántonia confides: "I didn't even know Martha wasn't my full sister until a..." (044-006) This unfinished sentence, left suspended in the text, hints at the complex family history of the Shimerdas — the kind of quiet revelation that complicates the surface warmth of the Cuzak household.

## In the Text

Martha is introduced early in Jim's visit when Ántonia speaks of how the children take care of each other: "Martha, the one you saw when she was a baby, is..." (043-065) The sentence trails off, leaving Martha's present circumstances implicit rather than stated. She is clearly a source of maternal pride, and her absence during Jim's visit is one of Ántonia's few regrets about the timing.

Ántonia's wish that Martha could be present recurs throughout the visit. (044-003) The memory of Martha's happiness — and the tears it provoked in Ántonia — suggests a daughter whose life has taken a significant and joyful turn. (044-004) The revelation about Martha not being a "full sister" adds a layer of complexity to the family portrait, a reminder that even the warmest households contain unspoken histories. (044-006)

## Significance

Martha Cuzak represents the successful passage of Ántonia's legacy to the next generation. As the eldest daughter, she is the inheritor of her mother's domestic competence and the living proof that Ántonia's sacrifices — the years of hired-girl labor, the hard work of building the farm — have borne fruit. Her absence during Jim's visit is itself meaningful: she is out in the world, independent, no longer defined solely by her place in the family circle.

The hint about Martha's parentage — "I didn't even know Martha wasn't my full sister" — is one of the novel's many quiet complications. It suggests that the Shimerda family's history contains losses or arrangements that the children themselves only gradually come to understand, and it deepens the portrait of Ántonia as a woman whose warmth coexists with a capacity for keeping secrets.
