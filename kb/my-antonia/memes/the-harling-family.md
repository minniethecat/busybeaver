## Overview

The Harling family are the Burdens' nearest neighbors in Black Hawk and play a crucial role in Ántonia Shimerda's life. When Ántonia comes to town to work as a hired girl after her father's death, the Harlings take her in and provide the stable, respectable household that shapes her teenage years.

Mrs. Harling, in particular, becomes a powerful maternal figure for Ántonia. Jim Burden notes that "Mrs. Harling, our nearest neighbour, kept an eye on us" (020-005), and his grandmother "thanked God she lived next the Harlings" (021-001).

## Members

Mrs. Harling is the household's central force — "short and square and sturdy" (021-002), decisive and warm. She has a characteristic "quick, decided laugh" (021-013) and runs her household with firm but affectionate authority.

Mr. Harling, a more indulgent presence, is preparing their eldest son for Annapolis and is described as "very indulgent" with his children (021-004). Frances Harling, their daughter, is an independent-minded young woman who works in her father's office.

Three of the Harling children are close in age to Jim: Charley, Julia ("the musical one"), and Sally. Charley becomes a particular object of Ántonia's admiration: "Because he was always first in his classes at school, and could mend the water-pipe or the doorbell and take the clock to pieces, she seemed to think him a sort of prince" (022-005).

## Ántonia and the Harlings

Ántonia arrives at the Harlings as a hired girl and quickly becomes part of the household's fabric. The family provides her with stability, regular meals, and the structure of a well-run American home. Jim describes their evenings: "We had jolly evenings at the Harlings' when the father was away" (022-007).

But the relationship is not without tension. Ántonia's growing love of dancing and the pavilion lifestyle puts her at odds with Mrs. Harling's standards. Eventually, this conflict leads to Ántonia leaving the Harling household for the Cutters — a decision that marks a turning point in her young life.

## Significance

The Harlings represent the best of what the established American family can offer immigrants: stability, opportunity, and genuine affection. Mrs. Harling's firm but loving guidance gives Ántonia a model of womanhood different from both her own mother and the more permissive culture of the dancing pavilion.
