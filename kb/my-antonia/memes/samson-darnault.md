## Overview

Samson d'Arnault, known as "Blind Samson," is a blind African-American pianist who performs at the hotel in Black Hawk. Born into slavery's aftermath on the d'Arnault plantation in the Far South, he was blinded by illness as an infant and developed an extraordinary musical ability. His piano playing is described as transcendent, drawing a diverse audience in the small prairie town.

## Key Details

- Born in the Far South on the d'Arnault plantation, "where the spirit if not the fact of slavery persisted" (026-012)
- Lost his sight at three weeks old from an illness (026-012)
- Also suffered from a nervous bodily motion that made his mother ashamed of his appearance (026-012)
- Named Samson by his mother "because he was blind," though on the plantation he was known as "yellow Martha's simple child" (026-012)
- As a child, repeatedly ran away from his cabin to listen to Miss Nellie d'Arnault practice the piano at the Big House (026-012)
- After being injured by the plantation mastiff, Miss Nellie took him in and taught him piano (026-014, 026-016)

## In the Text

Samson's origin story is given in rich detail. His mother, a laundress for the d'Arnaults, was ashamed of her "hideous little pickaninny" and hid him away. Yet the boy "began to talk early, remembered everything he heard" (026-012) and was irresistibly drawn to the sound of the piano at the Big House. Miss Nellie d'Arnault, the young white woman whose practice sessions he listened to from the garden, noticed him and was moved by his rapturous expression.

The turning point comes when young Samson approaches the plantation mastiff and is severely mauled. During his recovery, Miss Nellie took him under her care: "she went to the cabin to see him. When she came back she asked her mother's permission to take him into the Big House and teach him to play the piano" (026-016). His sensitivity to music and his memory proved extraordinary.

In Black Hawk, Samson performs at the town hotel, where his playing brings together the town's disparate social groups. His music transcends the racial and social boundaries that otherwise define life in the prairie community.

## Significance

Samson d'Arnault is one of the novel's most remarkable minor characters. He embodies the theme of art transcending circumstance: born blind, physically afflicted, into the harsh world of the post-slavery South, he nevertheless possesses a gift that elevates him and those who hear him. His presence in Black Hawk — a town otherwise uniformly white and largely Protestant — introduces a rare note of racial and cultural diversity to the narrative. Through Samson, Cather also quietly acknowledges the African-American presence on the frontier, a presence often erased from accounts of the period.
