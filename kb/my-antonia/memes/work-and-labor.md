Work and labor are central to the immigrant experience in Willa Cather's *My Ántonia*. The novel portrays a world in which physical work defines social status, shapes character, and serves as the primary means by which immigrant families establish themselves on the American frontier.

## The Hired Girls

One of the most significant labor dynamics in the novel is the phenomenon of the "hired girls" — young immigrant women who work as domestic servants in the households of established Black Hawk families. These women, primarily Scandinavian and Bohemian, form a distinct social class:

> [027-009] It was a social condemnation for a waltz with 'the hired girls.'

> [028-008] They were all Bohemians, all 'hired girls.'

Despite their marginalization, the hired girls — Ántonia, Lena Lingard, and Tiny Soderball among them — demonstrate remarkable work ethic. They send their wages home to help their families and save for their own futures:

> [029-001] She had lived in their house and worked for them, but she was not one of the 'hired girls' to the Harlings — she was a friend.

## Immigrant Labor and Economic Survival

For families like the Shimerdas, work is a matter of survival. Mr. Shimerda, a weaver by trade, struggles to adapt to the physical demands of farming. Ambrosch Shimerda, in contrast, proves a capable worker:

> [018-021] Ambrosch took Marek with him at full wages. Mrs. Shimerda then drove the wagon back to the dugout.

> [009-014] He worked in a furniture factory for modest wages, and when he met his family at the station, they were all dressed in clothes he had made for them.

## Women's Work

The novel pays particular attention to women's labor. Ántonia works in the fields alongside men, breaking gender conventions. Lena Lingard becomes a successful dressmaker. Tiny Soderball ventures west and prospers. Each woman's work defines her independence and her relationship to the broader society.

> [028-002] She had been early awakened and made observant by coming at a tender age from an old country where women worked alongside men in the fields.

## Labor as Character

Throughout the novel, a person's relationship to work reveals their character. Those who work hard and honestly — like Ántonia and the Harlings — earn respect and ultimately prosper. Those who avoid work — like Larry Donovan — are portrayed as morally suspect.
