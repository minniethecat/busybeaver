## Overview

Larry Donovan is a passenger conductor on the railroad who becomes Ántonia Shimerda's suitor and fiancé. He presents himself as a refined railway aristocrat, but his vanity and self-importance mask a shallow character. After promising to marry Ántonia, he deserts her, leaving her pregnant and alone — one of the cruelest betrayals in the novel.

## Key Details

- Worked as a passenger conductor on the railroad, a position he treated with exaggerated dignity (031-026)
- Was known in Black Hawk as "a kind of professional ladies' man" (031-026)
- Always maintained an "air of official aloofness," refusing to perform menial tasks like opening train windows (040-004)
- Changed out of his blue conductor's trousers immediately after each run, considering it "of the utmost importance" never to be seen in uniform off the train (040-004)
- Deserted Ántonia after promising marriage, leaving her with a baby (039-003)

## In the Text

Larry Donovan first appears in Book II as a frequent presence at the dances in Black Hawk. Ántonia "often went to the dances with Larry Donovan" (031-026), and the narrator notes how "admiringly all the boys looked at her" when she wore her new velveteen dress (031-026). Even later, when Jim returns from college, Lena Lingard asks casually: "Is she still going with Larry Donovan?" (036-020), indicating how public the relationship had become.

Jim Burden's description of Donovan in Book III is devastating. He characterizes him as "one of those train-crew aristocrats who are always afraid that someone may ask them to put up a car-window" (040-004). Donovan cultivated an elaborate personal mystique, sharing his "unappreciated worth" as a "tender secret" with his sweethearts, "always able to make some foolish heart ache over it" (040-004).

The betrayal is conveyed with painful economy. Jim learns that Ántonia "went away to marry Larry Donovan at some place where he was working; that he had deserted her, and that there was now a baby" (039-003). The brevity of this revelation mirrors the suddenness with which Ántonia's hopes were destroyed.

## Significance

Larry Donovan functions as a foil to the novel's more grounded male characters. His performative dignity and self-regard contrast sharply with the genuine, unassuming worth of figures like Otto Fuchs and Jake Marpole. His betrayal of Ántonia is the darkest personal injury she suffers in the novel, and her return to the prairie to work the land after this abandonment marks a crucial turn in her story — she emerges from the experience stronger and more rooted, having learned a harsh lesson about the gap between appearance and character.
