## Overview

Yulka Shimerda (sometimes spelled Julka) is the youngest daughter of the Shimerda family, a fair-haired, gentle child whose innocence stands in stark contrast to the harsh realities of pioneer life. She is introduced in Chapter 3 of Book I, shortly after the Burdens first meet the Shimerdas.

Jim Burden's first description captures her delicacy:

> "The little sister, whom they called Yulka (Julka), was fair, and seemed mild and obedient." (003-014)

## A Gentle Spirit

Where Ántonia was bold, physical, and eager to learn English, Yulka was quieter. On Jim's early visits to the Shimerda dugout, Ántonia led the way while Yulka followed:

> "We went down the steep drawside together, Yulka trotting after us." (003-016)

Yulka was quick to warm to kindness. When Jim gave her picture books, her delight was immediate and whole-hearted:

> "I sat down on the floor with Yulka and let her show me her kitten." (010-026)

Even in the depths of the family's poverty, Yulka retained a childlike capacity for joy. The scene where she shows Jim her kitten, while her father lies dead in the next room, is one of the novel's most poignant moments — childhood innocence persisting alongside tragedy.

## After Mr. Shimerda's Death

Yulka was present in the dugout when her father's body lay awaiting burial. Jim describes her huddled with her mother:

> "Yulka was on the floor at his feet, her face buried in her hands." (010-013)

Later, when the family moved to a better house, Yulka began to thrive. She learned to read along with Ántonia:

> "I used to get picture books for Yulka and Ántonia; even Yulka was able to read a little." (011-003)

## With Ántonia

Yulka was devoted to her older sister. During the sleigh-ride with Jim:

> "Ántonia and Yulka came running out, wearing little rabbit-skin hats." (009-004)

On the bitter cold ride, Ántonia wrapped her own comforter around Yulka:

> "She took off her comforter and wound it around Yulka's throat." (009-007)

This protective gesture defines their relationship: Ántonia as the strong older sister shielding the younger one from the worst of the prairie winter. Yulka clung to Ántonia when they parted at the train station (016-007), a moment that foreshadows Ántonia's lifelong role as caretaker.

## Significance

Yulka serves as a foil to Ántonia. Where Ántonia is strong, assertive, and takes after her father's sensitive spirit, Yulka is mild, obedient, and physically fragile. The contrast underscores the novel's interest in survival and resilience — Yulka survives because she is sheltered, while Ántonia survives because she is indomitable. Yulka also represents the child's perspective within the immigrant narrative: she is young enough to adapt fully to America, learning English and American ways without the anguish her parents experienced.
