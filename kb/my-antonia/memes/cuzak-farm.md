# Cuzak Farm

The Cuzak Farm is the Nebraska homestead of Ántonia Shimerda and Anton Cuzak in *My Ántonia*. Located in the countryside outside Black Hawk, it is the site of Jim Burden's reunion with Ántonia in Book V after twenty years of separation. The farm—with its orchards, fruit cave, livestock, and eleven children—embodies the fruition of Ántonia's frontier life: from the destitution of the Shimerdas' dugout to the hard-won abundance of a thriving family farm.

## Arrival at the Farm

Jim Burden reaches the Cuzak farm at midday, having taken "a good livery team to find the Cuzak farm" (043-005). The approach to the farm is marked by the children who greet him—"Are you Mrs. Cuzak's boys?" (043-006)—before he sees Ántonia herself, now a mother surrounded by a large, boisterous family. The farm's distance from town, requiring effort to reach, reminds the reader of the isolation and self-sufficiency that define prairie farm life.

The farmstead is substantial enough to include a windmill, barns, and outbuildings. Jim notes the signs of careful husbandry and the accumulated work of years, a stark contrast to the makeshift dugout where Ántonia's family first sheltered on the Nebraska prairie.

## The Orchards

The Cuzak farm is distinguished by its orchards, which embody the family's rootedness. Near the house stand "an ash grove and two orchards: a cherry orchard, with gooseberry bushes between the rows, and an apple orchard" (043-054). Jim and Ántonia walk through "the apple orchard, grown up in tall bluegrass" (043-055) until they come upon a grape arbour "with seats built along the sides and a warped plank table" (043-056). The arbour—a place of rest and gathering—suggests the farm is as much a home as an enterprise.

The narrator records a moment of profound stillness: "There was the deepest peace in that orchard. It was surrounded by a triple enclosure; the wire fence, then the hedge of thorny locusts, then the mulberry hedge which kept out the hot winds of summer and held fast to the protecting snows of winter" (043-060). The triple enclosure creates a sanctuary within the open prairie, a cultivated space that shelters the family from the elements.

## The Fruit Cave

The children are eager to show Jim "our new fruit cave" (043-041). Descending into the excavated cellar, Jim sees shelves lined with preserves: an abundance of cherries, strawberries, gooseberries, pickled mushrooms, dried apples, dried apricots, spiced plums, and spiced crabapples (043-042). The preserved fruit—the labor of Ántonia's hands—is a concrete measure of the farm's prosperity and the family's security against the lean Nebraska winters.

This fruit cave stands in pointed contrast to the Shimerdas' first cellar, a "hole" (003-004) so deficient that Grandmother Burden had declared it unfit. The arc from that hole to the Cuzaks' fruit cave traces Ántonia's material and spiritual journey across the novel.

## A Farmer by Necessity

Anton Cuzak did not choose farming. A Bohemian immigrant who had worked as a furrier in New York and participated in strikes in the East, he "was blacklisted" after the strikers won, and "as he had a large family, he went to farming in Nebraska" (044-035). That he took up the land under compulsion rather than by calling makes his evident contentment all the more remarkable. Walking with Cuzak after supper through the orchard, Jim reflects that "it did rather seem to me that Cuzak had been made the instrument of Ántonia's special mission" (044-040)—the land has used him as much as he has used the land.

Jim found Cuzak "a most companionable fellow" (044-037), and their evening conversation reveals a man who has made his peace with a life he never planned. The farm, for Cuzak as for Ántonia, has become not a second choice but a home.

## Significance

The Cuzak Farm is the novel's final and definitive place, the destination toward which Ántonia's entire narrative has been moving. From the Shimerdas' dugout, through the Harling household and the Cutters', Ántonia finally arrives at a farm that is truly her own. The orchards—planted, tended, and harvested—represent a permanence that immigrant families rarely achieved in a single generation.

In the novel's closing pages, Jim Burden reflects that Ántonia "had only to stand in the orchard, to put her hand on a little crab tree and look up at the apples, to make you feel the goodness of planting and tending and harvesting at last" (043-102). The farm is not merely a setting but the novel's final argument: that a life of labor on the land, built in partnership and surrounded by children, is a life of value and meaning.
