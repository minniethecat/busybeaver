## Overview

The Cutter household is the dark counterpart to the Harlings in *My Ántonia*. Where the Harlings offer stability and affection, the Cutters represent exploitation and menace. When Ántonia leaves the Harlings to work for the Cutters, she enters a household marked by constant conflict and moral danger.

Wick Cutter is introduced early in the novel as a predatory figure: "His creditor was Wick Cutter, the merciless Black Hawk money-lender" (008-001). This reputation precedes him through the entire story, coloring everything about the household he keeps.

## Wick Cutter

Wick Cutter is a money-lender by trade and a man of dubious character. "Cutter's first name was Wycliffe, and he was from some place in Iowa" (030-002). He is described as belonging to "the 'fast set' of Black Hawk business men" — men who lived dissolute lives under the cover of respectability (030-003).

His relationship with his wife is one of perpetual warfare: "They quarrelled from the moment Cutter came into the house until they went to bed at night" (030-007). Their conflicts are theatrical and relentless, defining the atmosphere of the household.

## Mrs. Cutter

Mrs. Cutter is "a terrifying-looking person" (030-005) who paints china obsessively — an incongruous domestic hobby in a house defined by strife. Her quarrels with her husband escalate to elaborate performances: "Mrs. Cutter put on her brocade and went among their friends soliciting orders for painted china, so that she could have money of her own" (030-009).

Despite the dysfunction, Mrs. Cutter is not without her own agency. She has learned to fight her husband on his own terms, turning domestic disputes into public performances that maintain her standing in Black Hawk society.

## Ántonia and the Cutters

Ántonia goes to work for the Cutters for the simplest of reasons: money. "They pay four dollars there, and there's no children" (029-010), she explains to Mrs. Harling. The higher wage and lighter duties come at a steep moral cost.

The Harlings warn her: "If you go to work for the Cutters, you're likely to have a flighty come-over before long" (029-013). Ántonia's time with the Cutters proves them right — the household's moral atmosphere, combined with Wick Cutter's predatory nature, puts her in genuine danger.

## Significance

The Cutter household serves as the novel's cautionary tale about what can happen to young immigrant women without family protection. It is the underside of Black Hawk's respectability — a place where money buys impunity and propriety masks predation.

Ántonia's passage through the Cutter household is a test she survives through her own strength and the support of friends like Jim Burden. Her eventual escape from the Cutters marks her transition from vulnerable hired girl to independent woman.
