# Class and Society

The social world of *My Ántonia* is stratified by origin, occupation, and respectability—forces that operate subtly but persistently beneath the surface of small-town life. Black Hawk, Nebraska, may lack the dramatic class divisions of an eastern city, but Cather reveals a community organized around unspoken hierarchies that determine who dances with whom, who sits where in church, and whose daughters are "hired girls" and whose are not.

## The Established Order

At the top of Black Hawk's social pyramid stand families like the Burdens and the Harlings—American-born, English-speaking, economically secure. Grandfather Burden is a figure of moral authority whose opinion carries weight in the community; the Harlings' household is a center of social activity, with Mrs. Harling presiding over a domestic realm that includes not only her own children but the immigrant girls who work for her.

The visible markers of this establishment are architectural and institutional. Black Hawk boasts "four white churches" and a courthouse (020-004), the physical infrastructure of settled, Protestant, respectable America. To belong to this order is to have a pew, a reputation, and a future that the community recognizes as its own.

## The Hired Girls

The most vivid class boundary in the novel runs between the "hired girls"—young immigrant women who work as domestic servants—and the American-born daughters of established families. The term itself performs social work: it distinguishes these young women from their employers' daughters, marking them as labor rather than family, immigrant rather than native, working-class rather than middle-class.

The hired girls' ambiguous status generates tension throughout Book II. They are admired for their vitality and beauty, yet "condemnation for a waltz with 'the hired girls'" (027-009) is a real social penalty for the young men of the town. "They were all Bohemians, all 'hired girls'" (028-008), Jim notes, collapsing nationality and class into a single category. Cather exposes the hypocrisy of a society that relies on these women's labor while denying them full social membership.

## Mobility and Its Limits

Class boundaries in Black Hawk are permeable but only partially so. Lena Lingard rises from hired girl to successful dressmaker in Lincoln and eventually San Francisco—a trajectory of economic mobility that the novel presents without sentimentality. Tiny Soderball makes a fortune in the Klondike gold rush. Ántonia herself, after the disgrace of her pregnancy, returns to farm life and finds contentment in marriage and motherhood.

Yet mobility is never complete. Lena's success takes her away from Black Hawk entirely—she does not so much transcend the class structure as exit it. Ántonia remains within the immigrant farming world, respected within her community but never crossing into the Harlings' or Burdens' social sphere. The novel suggests that American class is a persistent sorting mechanism, not easily overcome by individual effort.

## The View from the Farm

The class distance between town and country is another axis of stratification. The Burden farm, for all its prosperity, belongs to a different world than Black Hawk society. When the Burdens move to town, grandmother Burden immediately becomes "busy with church suppers and missionary societies" (020-005)—the social rituals of town respectability that have no equivalent on the isolated farm.

The immigrant farming families occupy the outermost ring of this social universe. The Shimerdas, living in a dugout, unable to speak English, dependent on their neighbors' charity—they are at the bottom of every hierarchy the novel depicts. Their ascent, measured across Ántonia's lifetime, is the novel's most sustained meditation on what class mobility can and cannot accomplish.
