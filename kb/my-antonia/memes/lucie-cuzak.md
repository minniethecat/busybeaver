## Overview

Lucie Cuzak is one of the younger daughters of [[Antonia Shimerda|Ántonia Cuzak]] and [[Anton Cuzak]], growing up on the Cuzak farm in Nebraska. Described as "a little girl named Lucie" and "Little Lucie," she is a quiet, watchful presence — shy with the visitor yet eager to share the secrets of the family home. (043-046) (043-085)

## Key Details

Lucie is among the youngest tier of the Cuzak children, grouped with [[Jan Cuzak|Jan]] and Nina as those who "crept through" the hedge rather than jumping over it like the older boys. (043-054) Her shyness is balanced by a desire to connect with the visitor: she "kept shyly pointing out" features of the farm, and later whispers to Jim about the family photographs. (043-046) (043-085)

## In the Text

Lucie first appears when Jim Burden arrives at the Cuzak farm. Along with Jan and Nina, she is one of the younger children who hang back at first, observing the stranger with shy curiosity. (043-046) When the children lead Jim on a tour of the orchard, Lucie and the other younger children "crept through" the hedge by a secret hole — a detail that captures both her youth and her insider's knowledge of the farm's geography. (043-054)

Her most memorable moment comes in the parlour, when the family photographs are brought out. "Little Lucie whispered to me that they were..." (043-085) The whisper is characteristically intimate — Lucie shares knowledge with the visitor in confidence, bridging the gap between stranger and family with a child's instinct for hospitality. Like her brother Jan, she participates in the rituals of welcome not through words but through small, thoughtful gestures.

## Significance

Lucie Cuzak, like her siblings, is evidence of Ántonia's vitality carried into the next generation. Her shy eagerness to share the family's stories with Jim Burden reflects the household's culture of welcome — even the smallest children are enlisted in the work of making a guest feel at home. Where her brother [[Leo Cuzak|Leo]] commands attention with sauciness, Lucie works more quietly, whispering her confidences and pointing out what she thinks the visitor should see.

In the tapestry of the Cuzak children, Lucie represents the quiet, watchful temperament — a child who observes more than she speaks, and whose whispered confidences carry the weight of genuine curiosity about the outside world that Jim represents.
