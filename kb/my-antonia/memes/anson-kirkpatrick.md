## Overview

Anson Kirkpatrick is a commercial traveller for Marshall Field's department store in Chicago, one of the "travelling men" who gather at the Boys' Home hotel in Black Hawk on Saturday nights. A "dapper little Irishman" with a gift for music and socializing, he is a representative of the wider world of commerce and culture that touches even the small prairie town. (024-002)

## Key Details

Anson Kirkpatrick is introduced as "Marshall Field's man," identifying him by the powerful Chicago department store he represents. (024-002) On Saturday nights at the Boys' Home, he "played the piano and sang all the latest sentimental songs," providing the musical entertainment for the assembled commercial travellers. (024-002) He is described as "a dapper little Irishman, very vain, homely as a monkey, with friends everywhere, and a sweetheart in every port, like a sailor." (026-006) This portrait — at once affectionate and unsparing — captures the travelling salesman as a type: rootless, charming, unattractive yet confident, a carrier of news and fashion from the metropolis to the provinces.

On the night of Mrs. Gardener's absence, Kirkpatrick is "at the piano, playing airs from a musical comedy then running in Chicago." (026-006) The detail places the scene precisely in its historical moment and emphasizes the cultural conduit that commercial travellers provided between Chicago and the small towns of the plains. Around him gather a furniture salesman from Kansas City, a drug man, and Willy O'Reilly, who sells jewelry and musical instruments — a cross-section of the commercial networks that sustained the prairie economy.

## In the Text

Kirkpatrick plays a pivotal role in the events of the night at the Boys' Home. It is he who discovers the hired girls dancing: "Anson Kirkpatrick mounted a chair and peeped over the transom. Springing down, he wrenched open the doors and ran out into the dining-room." (026-019) What he finds — Tiny, Lena, Ántonia, and Mary Dusak waltzing in the middle of the floor — sets off the evening's climax. The girls "separated and fled toward the kitchen, giggling," and the impromptu dance that follows, with Blind d'Arnault at the piano, becomes one of the novel's most memorable set pieces. (026-019)

Kirkpatrick's presence at the Boys' Home also illuminates the social world of the travelling salesmen. Their talk is "all about good and bad hotels, actors and actresses and musical prodigies," and they share news of the theatrical world — Mrs. Gardener has gone to Omaha to hear Booth and Barrett, and Mary Anderson is having "a great success in 'A Winter's Tale,' in London." (026-006) In the isolation of the prairie, these commercial travellers are bearers not only of goods but of culture, linking Black Hawk to the theaters of Chicago, Omaha, and London.

## Significance

Anson Kirkpatrick belongs to the novel's gallery of travelling men — a type that includes the itinerant piano salesman who first brought music to the prairie and the various commercial agents who pass through Black Hawk. These figures represent the reach of the market economy into the rural West, but they also represent culture, sociability, and the pleasures of urban life. Their Saturday night gatherings at the Boys' Home are, for the hired girls like Tiny and Lena, a glimpse of a world beyond domestic service.

Kirkpatrick's description as "a sweetheart in every port, like a sailor" aligns him with the novel's theme of restless mobility. He is a man without fixed attachments, defined by the places he passes through rather than any place he belongs to. In this, he is both a contrast to the rooted immigrants who are the novel's center — Ántonia on her farm, the Shimerdas in their dugout — and a reminder that modern American life is increasingly defined by movement and impermanence.
