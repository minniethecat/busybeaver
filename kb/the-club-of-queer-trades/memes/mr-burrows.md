# Mr Burrows

**Mr Burrows** is a character in G.K. Chesterton's *The Club of Queer Trades* (1905), appearing in the climactic chapter of "The Eccentric Seclusion of the Old Lady" (ch020). A man of formidable physical stature, he is discovered in the midst of a violent confrontation in the old lady's house — an encounter that proves to be not a crime but a queer trade in action. He is a member of [[the-professional-detainers|the Professional Detainers]], an agency whose business is the physical restraint of people about to act on rash impulses.

## The Confrontation

Burrows first appears when the narrator bursts into the old lady's cellar, expecting to rescue her from imprisonment. Instead, he finds a scene of chaos:

> The gigantic Burrows was toiling in the (020-074)

The struggle is desperate and physical: Burrows is described as being "particularly strong, but so was Mr Burrows" (020-076) — the repetition emphasising that strength meets strength in this underground battle.

Burrows and his opponent fight amid overturned furniture and a "waste-paper basket. Burrows and his companion were almost" (020-120) indistinguishable in the tangle of limbs. The narrator sees "Mr Burrows, which was turned towards me," (020-126) his face a mask of exertion. "Burrows' huge fists and those of the" (020-129) other man swing through the air, and "Burrows swung to and" (020-133) fro as the struggle shifts. Finally, he "sprang on top of Burrows, with one antimacassar in his" (020-138) hand — the domestic detail of the antimacassar a characteristically Chestertonian touch that undercuts the apparent violence with the absurd.

Burrows is ultimately subdued: his opponent "pinioned Mr Burrows" (020-148), bringing the struggle to its conclusion — at which point the truth emerges: this was not an attack but a professional detention.

## The Professional Detainers

The revelation that Burrows is a professional detainer reframes the entire scene. His violence was not criminal but contractual — a service performed for a client who needed to be physically prevented from making a disastrous decision. As the old lady explains, the Professional Detainers exist to "prevent people from doing things" they would regret, using whatever degree of physical force is necessary.

Burrows, in this light, is not a thug but a specialist — a man whose gigantism and strength are professional qualifications, not criminal attributes. The struggle the narrator witnesses is simply Burrows doing his job. Chesterton's delight in this inversion is palpable: the most frightening figure in the house turns out to be the most principled.

## Physical Description and Character

Burrows's most striking quality is his size. He is "gigantic" (020-074), possessed of "huge fists" (020-129) and formidable strength. The narrator's initial impression — a brute engaged in violence — is the obvious one, and Chesterton wants the reader to share it. Only after the revelation does the narrator (and reader) understand that Burrows's physicality is the tool of his trade, as legitimate as a surgeon's scalpel or a lawyer's brief.

The name "Burrows" itself — suggesting something that digs down, that goes underground — is apt for a man whose work takes place in a cellar, in the hidden spaces beneath the respectable surface of the old lady's house.
