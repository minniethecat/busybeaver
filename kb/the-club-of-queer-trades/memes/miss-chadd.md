# Miss Chadd

Miss Adelaide Chadd is the eldest of three sisters who share a household with their brother, [[professor-chadd|Professor Chadd]], in Shepherd's Bush. She appears in "The Noticeable Conduct of Professor Chadd" as a figure of brisk competence, holding the family together when her brother falls into a bewildering mental state — a crisis that proves to be, in Chesterton's characteristic fashion, something quite other than it appears.

## The Chadd Household

The Chadd household consists of Professor Chadd and his three sisters. The Professor is introduced as an ethnologist "known to the ethnological world (which is a very interesting world, but a long way off this one) as the second greatest" authority in his field (015-031).

The sisters are described primarily through their physical presence and practical management of the home. The eldest — Adelaide — is distinguished by a "tall and angular figure" (016-078) and a manner that combines blunt directness with self-possession.

Chesterton notes, with characteristic irony, that it is the Professor's sister who possesses "that masculinity which always in such families" interrupts the men's abstract debates (015-141). This gendered inversion — the academic brother lost in theory while his sister handles practical reality — is a recurring Chestertonian motif.

## Role in the Crisis

When Professor Chadd apparently loses his mind — discovered by Basil Grant dancing wildly in the garden — it is Miss Chadd who sends the urgent telegram: "Please come at once. James' mental state dangerous. Chadd." (016-042).

Basil Grant, receiving this message, is initially skeptical, noting that if "Miss Chadd has written down under the eye of a strange woman in a post-office that her brother is off his head you may be perfectly certain that she did it because it was a fact" (016-051) — a testament to her reliability and seriousness.

Upon Basil's arrival, the three Misses Chadd stand as a human barricade: "The tall and angular figure of the eldest Miss Chadd blocked the doorway as the door opened. Two other Miss Chadds seemed in the same way to be blocking the narrow passage and the little parlour" (016-078). Miss Chadd provides calm, practical updates: "Doctor Colman is with him," she reports levelly, explaining that "they are in the garden. Doctor Colman thought the air would do him good" (016-128).

## The Resolution

As the crisis reaches its absurd climax — with Basil Grant and Professor Chadd engaged in a strange kicking contest in the garden — it is Adelaide Chadd who breaks through.

"They did not see the eldest Miss Chadd come out feverishly into the garden with gestures of entreaty" (017-063), accompanied by [[bingham|Mr. Bingham]] of the British Museum. Her announcement — "Mr Bingham of the British Museum" (017-067) — freezes the combatants and precipitates the story's resolution, in which Basil Grant reveals that the Professor's apparent madness was caused by the news of his impending appointment as Keeper of Asiatic Manuscripts.

Basil Grant, ever courteous, acknowledges her authority even as he takes charge: "Miss Chadd will excuse me," he says smoothly, "if I know my way about the house" (017-088), before leading Mr. Bingham away for the private negotiation that secures her brother's future.

## Character

Miss Chadd is portrayed as competent, direct, and unflappable — a counterweight to her brother's academic abstraction. Her "brief" (016-167) and "calm" (016-128) utterances in moments of crisis contrast with the wild physical comedy unfolding around her. She is, in Chesterton's scheme, one of those practical women who quietly hold the world together while men pursue their elaborate and often ridiculous obsessions.
