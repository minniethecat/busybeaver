# Burrows

Burrows is a physically imposing character who appears in G.K. Chesterton's [[the-club-of-queer-trades-org|The Club of Queer Trades]] (1905) during the story "[[the-old-lady|The Eccentric Seclusion of the Old Lady]]." A "gigantic" young man of great strength and expansive humour, Burrows serves, together with his companion Greenwood, as guardian of the mysterious house where the old lady is held -- though the nature of that guardianship is not what [[rupert-grant|Rupert Grant]] initially assumes.

## Role

Burrows is introduced as a "big young man, whose name appeared to be Burrows" (020-049), engaged in an amicable philosophical discussion about the value of popular science when Rupert Grant suddenly attacks him from behind. Despite being taken by surprise, Burrows proves to be a formidable physical opponent: "Rupert and Basil were both particularly strong, but so was Mr Burrows; how strong, we knew a second afterwards" (020-076). He flings Rupert "head over heels, a catherine wheel of legs" with a convulsive heave, then butts Basil in the chest and leaps at the narrator with "a Berserker roar" (020-083).

Burrows's role in the story is that of a protective guardian, not a villain. The old lady's distress at hearing he and Greenwood have been "knocked down and tied up" (021-067) reveals that they were not her captors but her caretakers. His genial temperament is evident in his response to being released: unlike the bewildered Greenwood, "the gigantic Burrows, on the other hand, was heaving with herculean laughter" (021-122), and he jovially invites the Grants to search the house for imagined corpses [021-132-134].

## Appearances

Burrows appears in one story:

- **"The Eccentric Seclusion of the Old Lady"** (Sections 20-21): He is first seen in philosophical conversation at the house, then is attacked and overpowered by Basil and Rupert Grant in a chaotic fight scene. After the misunderstanding is resolved, he parts on good-natured terms, his laughter contrasting sharply with the general confusion of the story's conclusion.

Key passages include his initial identification (020-049), the physical struggle [020-074-084], and the aftermath in which his Rabelaisian good humour is displayed [021-122, 021-132-134].

## Relationships

- **Greenwood** -- Burrows's companion and fellow guardian of the house. Where Greenwood is small, wiry, and resentful of the Grants' intrusion, Burrows is vast, strong, and takes the whole affair with uproarious amusement (021-122).
- **[[the-old-lady|The Old Lady]]** -- Burrows is revealed to be the old lady's protector rather than her jailer. Her anxious inquiry "Where is Mr Burrows?" (021-055) and her refusal to leave the house until he and Greenwood are freed demonstrate their true relationship.
- **[[basil-grant|Basil Grant]]** and **[[rupert-grant|Rupert Grant]]** -- Burrows is attacked by the Grant brothers, who mistakenly believe he is imprisoning a captive woman. After his bonds are released by Basil (021-117), he bears no grudge and treats the whole incident as a farce.
