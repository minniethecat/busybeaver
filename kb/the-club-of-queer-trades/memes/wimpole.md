## Character Overview

Wimpole is introduced in "The Painful Fall of a Great Reputation" (Chapter 2) as a man Basil Grant identifies from a passing tram as "the wickedest man in England" (005-099). His appearance is striking: "a slim, smooth person, passing very quickly among the quickly passing crowd," wearing a black top-hat with "those strange curves whereby the decadent artist of the eighties tried to turn the top-hat into something as rhythmic as an Etruscan vase" (005-099). His "largely grey" hair was "curled with the instinct of one who appreciated the gradual beauty of grey and silver" (005-099).

## "The Wickedest Man in England"

Basil Grant's identification of Wimpole is characteristically intuitive and apparently groundless. When Charles Swinburne protests — "Never saw him before! Then what in heaven's name do you mean by saying that he is the wickedest man in England?" — Basil's answer is both absurd and profound: "The moment I saw that man, I saw all these people stricken with a sudden and splendid innocence. I saw that while all ordinary poor men in the streets were being themselves, he was not being himself" (005-099).

This is the inverted detective's method in its purest form: Basil reads moral character not from evidence but from a kind of spiritual perception — the contrast Wimpole creates against the "innocence" of ordinary people.

## The Unmasking

Wimpole's wickedness, as the story reveals, consists in his systematic manipulation of social relationships. He is a "besetting sin" specialist — his "desire to intrigue to the disadvantage of others" (005-099) — who insinuates himself into circles, wins admiration through dazzling conversation, and then quietly destroys reputations through rumor and innuendo.

Lord Beaumont, Wimpole's primary victim in the story, introduces him with a question that captures his mystique: "Wimpole? Who was Shakespeare?" (006-072) — the implication being that Wimpole is a conversational genius whose fame requires no qualification. Muriel Beaumont, Lord Beaumont's daughter, attempts to draw Wimpole out in conversation, but his responses, while superficially charming, reveal nothing of substance (006-154).

## Character Significance

Wimpole represents the dark underside of Chesterton's celebration of eccentricity. If the Club of Queer Trades transforms eccentricity into creative labor, Wimpole is the warning that charm without substance, wit without moral grounding, is a form of predation. He is what Basil Grant left the bench to oppose: not the common criminal who steals a spoon or beats a wife — those, Basil insists, are "heroes," "saints," "trying to be good" (005-099) — but the polished, articulate, and utterly insincere operator for whom other people are merely material.

Basil's solution is not to prosecute Wimpole (the law would be useless) but to expose him socially. Chesterton implies that this is the only meaningful consequence for a man whose currency is social credit.
