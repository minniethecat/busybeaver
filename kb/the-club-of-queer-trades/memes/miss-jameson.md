# Miss Jameson

Miss Jameson is an actress employed by the [[adventure-and-romance-agency|Adventure and Romance Agency]], the queer trade operated by [[northover|P.G. Northover]]. She appears in "The Tremendous Adventures of Major Brown," the first story in the collection, and plays a crucial role in the resolution of [[major-brown|Major Brown]]'s strange adventure.

## Role / Identity

Miss Jameson is one of the many actors and actresses whom Northover employs to stage elaborate romantic adventures for paying clients. Her task in Major Brown's case is to play the part of a damsel in distress, the green-clad lady with red hair who lures the Major into a series of increasingly alarming scenarios (004-011).

She is described as "the lady with the red hair and the green garments," a striking image that captures the theatrical artificiality of the Romance Agency's productions (005-002).

## Appearances

Miss Jameson appears in Section 4 and Section 5 of "The Tremendous Adventures of Major Brown" (Chapter 1 of the collection). Her salary is recorded in the Agency's accounts — "To salary of Miss Jameson £1 0 0" — indicating her professional status within Northover's organization (004-011).

## Relationships

Miss Jameson marries [[major-brown|Major Brown]] at the conclusion of the story. Their marriage is described as happy: "The Major and she are living as happily as birds, in an absurd villa, and the former has taken to smoking" (005-011). She belongs to "her languid and intellectualized set," a detail that suggests a Bohemian artistic circle distinct from the Major's military background (005-005).

Chesterton notes that she "always replied very quietly that she had met scores of men who acted splendidly in the charades provided for them by Northover, but that she had only met one man who went down into a coal-cellar when he really thought it contained a murderer" (005-006). This remark encapsulates the story's central theme: the Major's genuine courage, mistaken for theatrical participation, proves more authentic than any performance.

## Significance

Miss Jameson functions as the romantic reward at the end of the adventure, but Chesterton gives her a distinctive wit and self-possession that elevate her beyond the conventional love-interest. Her quiet observation about the Major's courage serves as the story's moral: the authentic trumps the theatrical.
