# James

James is the given name of [[professor-chadd|Professor Chadd]], the ethnologist and scholar who appears in G.K. Chesterton's [[the-club-of-queer-trades-org|The Club of Queer Trades]] (1905). While "Professor Chadd" is the public, professional identity known to the academic world, "James" is the private, domestic man -- the name used exclusively by his sister, who has cared for him through poverty, ill health, and his obsessive scholarly pursuits. This distinction between public and private names mirrors one of the story's central concerns: the gap between outward appearance and inner reality.

## Role

James Chadd is both a distinguished scholar and a man of fragile constitution. His sister describes his circumstances with a mixture of pride and pathos: "James' health has never been good, and while we are as poor as we are he had to do journalism and coaching, in addition to his own dreadful grinding notions and discoveries, which he loves more than anything else in the world" [015-158-160]. He is on the verge of being appointed keeper of Asiatic manuscripts at the [[london-in-the-queer-trades|British Museum]], an honour that would relieve the financial strain on his household.

The name "James" is used at moments of domestic intimacy or crisis. His sister calls him "James" when announcing a visitor (015-144), when recounting his medical history (015-158), and -- most dramatically -- when she fears for his sanity. Her telegram reads: "Please come at once. James' mental state dangerous. Chadd" (016-042), and her description of finding him standing on one leg is punctuated by the desperate cry: "'James, what is the matter?' I cried, for I was thoroughly frightened" (016-111).

Basil Grant, however, sees beyond the apparent madness. In a passionate rebuke to those who have failed to recognise Chadd's true nature, he exclaims: "Have you seen the man? Have you looked at James Chadd going dismally to and fro from his dingy house to your miserable library, with his futile books and his confounded umbrella, and never seen that he has the eyes of a fanatic?" [018-028-031]. The shift from "Professor Chadd" in formal discourse to "James Chadd" in this moment of insight signals Basil's understanding that the man and the scholar are one -- that the domestic eccentricity and the philosophical conviction spring from the same source.

## Appearances

James (as Professor Chadd) appears in one story:

- **"The Noticeable Conduct of Professor Chadd"** (Sections 15-18): The name "James" is used seven times across these chapters -- always by his sister, and always in contexts that reveal his private self: his poor health (015-158), his alarming behaviour at home [016-101, 016-111-112], and his sister's fear for his mental state (016-042). The only person outside the household to use the name "James" is Basil Grant, in his climactic defence of Chadd's sanity and genius (018-028).

## Relationships

- **[[professor-chadd|Professor Chadd]]** (same person) -- "James" is the private, domestic face of the public figure known as Professor Chadd. The page for Professor Chadd covers his academic career, his philosophical theories, and his role in the [[the-club-of-queer-trades-org|Club of Queer Trades]].
- **Miss Chadd** -- James's sister, who acts as his caregiver and protector. She manages the household, worries over his health, and sends the urgent telegram to Basil Grant when James's behaviour becomes inexplicable (016-042). Her use of "James" -- never "Professor Chadd" -- reflects their intimate, familial bond.
- **[[basil-grant|Basil Grant]]** -- The ex-judge who recognizes in James Chadd the "eyes of a fanatic" (018-031) and whose philosophical argument with him inadvertently triggers the crisis. Basil's use of "James" at the story's climax signals that he sees past the professor's eccentricity to the man beneath.
