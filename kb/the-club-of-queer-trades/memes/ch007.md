[007-001] Mr Secretary Drummond had a pale face and red hair; but at this his face

[007-002] became suddenly as red as his moustache.

[007-003] “I am not a fair judge of him,” he said.

[007-004] “Why not?” asked [[basil-grant|Grant]].

[007-005] “Because I hate him like hell,” said the other, after a long pause and

[007-006] violently.

[007-007] Neither [[rupert-grant|Grant]] nor I needed to ask the [[madness-and-sanity|reason]]; his glances towards Miss

[007-008] Beaumont and the stranger were sufficiently illuminating. Grant said

[007-009] quietly:

[007-010] “But before--before you came to hate him, what did you really think of

[007-011] him?”

[007-012] “I am in a terrible difficulty,” said the young man, and his voice told

[007-013] us, like a clear bell, that he was an honest man. “If I spoke about him

[007-014] as I feel about him now, I could not trust myself. And I should like to

[007-015] be able to say that when I first saw him I thought he was charming. But

[007-016] again, the fact is I didn't. I hate him, that is my private affair. But

[007-017] I also disapprove of him--really I do believe I disapprove of him quite

[007-018] apart from my private feelings. When first he came, I admit he was much

[007-019] quieter, but I did not like, so to speak, the [[moral-philosophy|moral]] swell of him. Then

[007-020] that jolly old [[sir-walter-cholmondeliegh|Sir Walter Cholmondeliegh]] got introduced to us, and this

[007-021] fellow, with his cheap-jack wit, began to score off the old man in the

[007-022] way he does now. Then I felt that he must be a bad lot; it must be

[007-023] bad to fight the old and the kindly. And he fights the poor old chap

[007-024] savagely, unceasingly, as if he hated old age and kindliness. Take, if

[007-025] you want it, the evidence of a prejudiced witness. I admit that I hate

[007-026] the man because a certain person admires him. But I believe that apart

[007-027] from that I should hate the man because old Sir Walter hates him.”

[007-028] This speech affected me with a genuine sense of esteem and pity for the

[007-029] young man; that is, of pity for him because of his obviously hopeless

[007-030] worship of [[miss-beaumont|Miss Beaumont]], and of esteem for him because of the direct

[007-031] realistic account of the history of [[wimpole|Wimpole]] which he had given. Still, I

[007-032] was sorry that he seemed so steadily set against the man, and could

[007-033] not help referring it to an instinct of his personal relations, however

[007-034] nobly disguised from himself.

[007-035] In the middle of these meditations, Grant whispered in my ear what was

[007-036] perhaps the most startling of all interruptions.

[007-037] “In the name of God, let's get away.”

[007-038] I have never known exactly in how odd a way this odd old man affected

[007-039] me. I only know that for some reason or other he so affected me that I

[007-040] was, within a few minutes, in the street outside.

[007-041] “This,” he said, “is a beastly but amusing affair.”

[007-042] “What is?” I asked, baldly enough.

[007-043] “This affair. Listen to me, my old friend. Lord and [[lady-beaumont|Lady Beaumont]] have

[007-044] just invited you and me to a grand dinner-party this very night, at

[007-045] which Mr Wimpole will be in all his glory. Well, there is nothing very

[007-046] extraordinary about that. [[the-reverend-vicar|The]] extraordinary thing is that we are not

[007-047] going.”

[007-048] “Well, really,” I said, “it is already six o'clock and I doubt if we

[007-049] could get home and dress. I see nothing extraordinary in the fact that

[007-050] we are not going.”

[007-051] “Don't you?” said Grant. “I'll bet you'll see something extraordinary in

[007-052] what we're doing instead.”

[007-053] I looked at him blankly.

[007-054] “Doing instead?” I asked. “What are we doing instead?”

[007-055] “Why,” said he, “we are waiting for one or two hours outside this house

[007-056] on a winter evening. You must forgive me; it is all my vanity. It is

[007-057] only to show you that I am right. Can you, with the assistance of this

[007-058] cigar, wait until both Sir Walter Cholmondeliegh and the mystic Wimpole

[007-059] have left this house?”

[007-060] “Certainly,” I said. “But I do not know which is likely to leave first.

[007-061] Have you any notion?”

[007-062] “No,” he said. “Sir Walter may leave first in a glow of rage. Or again,

[007-063] Mr Wimpole may leave first, feeling that his last epigram is a thing to

[007-064] be flung behind him like a firework. And Sir Walter may remain some

[007-065] time to analyse Mr Wimpole's character. But they will both have to leave

[007-066] within reasonable time, for they will both have to get dressed and come

[007-067] back to dinner here tonight.”

[007-068] As he spoke the shrill double whistle from the porch of the great house

[007-069] drew a dark cab to the dark portal. And then a thing happened that we

[007-070] really had not expected. Mr Wimpole and Sir Walter Cholmondeliegh came

[007-071] out at the same moment.

[007-072] They paused for a second or two opposite each other in a natural doubt;

[007-073] then a certain geniality, fundamental perhaps in both of them, made Sir

[007-074] Walter smile and say: “The night is foggy. Pray take my cab.”

[007-075] Before I could count twenty the cab had gone rattling up the street with

[007-076] both of them. And before I could count twenty-three Grant had hissed in

[007-077] my ear:

[007-078] “Run after the cab; run as if you were running from a mad dog--run.”

[007-079] We pelted on steadily, keeping the cab in sight, through dark mazy

[007-080] streets. God only, I thought, knows why we are running at all, but we

[007-081] are running hard. Fortunately we did not run far. The cab pulled up at

[007-082] the fork of two streets and Sir Walter paid the cabman, who drove away

[007-083] rejoicing, having just come in contact with the more generous among the

[007-084] rich. Then the two men talked together as men do talk together after

[007-085] giving and receiving great insults, the talk which leads either to

[007-086] forgiveness or a duel--at least so it seemed as we watched it from ten

[007-087] yards off. Then the two men shook hands heartily, and one went down one

[007-088] fork of the road and one down another.

[007-089] Basil, with one of his rare gestures, flung his arms forward.

[007-090] “Run after that scoundrel,” he cried; “let us catch him now.”

[007-091] We dashed across the open space and reached the juncture of two paths.

[007-092] “Stop!” I shouted wildly to Grant. “That's the wrong turning.”

[007-093] He ran on.

[007-094] “Idiot!” I howled. “Sir Walter's gone down there. Wimpole has slipped

[007-095] us. He's half a mile down the other road. You're wrong... Are you deaf?

[007-096] You're wrong!”

[007-097] “I don't think I am,” he panted, and ran on.

[007-098] “But I saw him!” I cried. “Look in front of you. Is that Wimpole? It's

[007-099] the old man... What are you doing? What are we to do?”

[007-100] “Keep running,” said Grant.

[007-101] Running soon brought us up to the broad back of the pompous old baronet,

[007-102] whose white whiskers shone silver in the fitful lamplight. My brain was

[007-103] utterly bewildered. I grasped nothing.

[007-104] “Charlie,” said Basil hoarsely, “can you believe in my common sense for

[007-105] four minutes?”

[007-106] “Of course,” I said, panting.

[007-107] “Then help me to catch that man in front and hold him down. Do it at

[007-108] once when I say 'Now'. Now!”

[007-109] We sprang on Sir Walter Cholmondeliegh, and rolled that portly old

[007-110] gentleman on his back. He fought with a commendable valour, but we got

[007-111] him tight. I had not the remotest notion why. He had a splendid and

[007-112] full-blooded vigour; when he could not box he kicked, and we bound him;

[007-113] when he could not kick he shouted, and we gagged him. Then, by Basil's

[007-114] arrangement, we dragged him into a small court by the street side and

[007-115] waited. As I say, I had no notion why.

[007-116] “I am sorry to incommode you,” said Basil calmly out of the darkness;

[007-117] “but I have made an appointment here.”

[007-118] “An appointment!” I said blankly.

[007-119] “Yes,” he said, glancing calmly at the apoplectic old aristocrat gagged

[007-120] on the ground, whose eyes were starting impotently from his head. “I

[007-121] have made an appointment here with a thoroughly nice young fellow.

[007-122] An old friend. Jasper Drummond his name is--you may have met him

[007-123] this afternoon at the Beaumonts. He can scarcely come though till the

[007-124] Beaumonts' dinner is over.”

[007-125] For I do not know how many hours we stood there calmly in the darkness.

[007-126] By the time those hours were over I had thoroughly made up my mind that

[007-127] the same thing had happened which had happened long ago on the bench of

[007-128] a British Court of Justice. Basil Grant had gone mad. I could imagine

[007-129] no other explanation of the facts, with the portly, purple-faced old

[007-130] country gentleman flung there strangled on the floor like a bundle of

[007-131] wood.

[007-132] After about four hours a lean figure in evening dress rushed into the

[007-133] court. A glimpse of gaslight showed the red moustache and white face of

[007-134] Jasper Drummond.

[007-135] “Mr Grant,” he said blankly, “the thing is incredible. You were right;

[007-136] but what did you mean? All through this dinner-party, where dukes and

[007-137] duchesses and editors of Quarterlies had come especially to hear him,

[007-138] that extraordinary Wimpole kept perfectly silent. He didn't say a funny

[007-139] thing. He didn't say anything at all. What does it mean?”

[007-140] Grant pointed to the portly old gentleman on the ground.

[007-141] “That is what it means,” he said.

[007-142] Drummond, on observing a fat gentleman lying so calmly about the place,

[007-143] jumped back, as from a mouse.

[007-144] “What?” he said weakly, “... what?”

[007-145] Basil bent suddenly down and tore a paper out of Sir Walter's

[007-146] breastpocket, a paper which the baronet, even in his hampered state,

[007-147] seemed to make some effort to retain.

[007-148] It was a large loose piece of white wrapping paper, which Mr Jasper

[007-149] Drummond read with a vacant eye and undisguised astonishment. As far as

[007-150] he could make out, it consisted of a series of questions and answers, or

[007-151] at least of remarks and replies, arranged in the manner of a catechism.

[007-152] The greater part of the document had been torn and obliterated in the

[007-153] struggle, but the termination remained. It ran as follows:

[007-154] C. Says... Keep countenance.

[007-155] W. Keep... British Museum.

[007-156] C. Know whom talk... absurdities.

[007-157] W. Never talk absurdities without...

[007-158] “What is it?” cried Drummond, flinging the paper down in a sort of final

[007-159] fury.

[007-160] “What is it?” replied Grant, his voice rising into a kind of splendid

[007-161] chant. “What is it? It is a great new [[profession-and-trade|profession]]. A great [[the-queer-trade-as-concept|new trade]]. A

[007-162] trifle immoral, I admit, but still great, like piracy.”

[007-163] “A new profession!” said the young man with the red moustache vaguely;

[007-164] “a new trade!”

[007-165] “A new trade,” repeated Grant, with a strange exultation, “a new

[007-166] profession! What a pity it is immoral.”

[007-167] “But what the deuce is it?” cried Drummond and I in a breath of

[007-168] blasphemy.

[007-169] “It is,” said Grant calmly, “the great new trade of the Organizer of

[007-170] Repartee. This fat old gentleman lying on the ground strikes you, as I

[007-171] have no doubt, as very stupid and very rich. Let me clear his character.

[007-172] He is, like ourselves, very clever and very poor. He is also not really

[007-173] at all fat; all that is stuffing. He is not particularly old, and

[007-174] his name is not Cholmondeliegh. He is a swindler, and a swindler of

[007-175] a perfectly delightful and novel kind. He hires himself out at

[007-176] dinner-parties to lead up to other people's repartees. According to a

[007-177] preconcerted scheme (which you may find on that piece of paper), he says

[007-178] the stupid things he has arranged for himself, and his client says the

[007-179] clever things arranged for him. In short, he allows himself to be scored

[007-180] off for a guinea a night.”

[007-181] “And this fellow Wimpole--” began Drummond with indignation.

[007-182] “This fellow Wimpole,” said Basil Grant, smiling, “will not be an

[007-183] intellectual rival in the future. He had some fine things, elegance and

[007-184] silvered hair, and so on. But the intellect is with our friend on the

[007-185] floor.”

[007-186] “That fellow,” cried Drummond furiously, “that fellow ought to be in

[007-187] gaol.”

[007-188] “Not at all,” said Basil indulgently; “he ought to be in the Club of

[007-189] [[queer-trades|Queer Trades]].”
