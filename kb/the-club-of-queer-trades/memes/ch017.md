[017-001] Suddenly he stepped up to [[professor-chadd|Professor Chadd]]'s elbow, and said, in a

[017-002] loud familiar voice, “Well, my boy, do you still think the Zulus our

[017-003] inferiors?”

[017-004] [[the-reverend-vicar|The]] doctor knitted his brows and looked anxious, seeming to be about to

[017-005] speak. The professor turned his bald and placid head towards [[basil-grant|Grant]] in a

[017-006] friendly manner, but made no answer, idly flinging his left leg about.

[017-007] “Have you converted Dr Colman to your views?” Basil continued, still in

[017-008] the same loud and lucid tone.

[017-009] Chadd only shuffled his feet and kicked a little with the other leg,

[017-010] his expression still benevolent and inquiring. The doctor cut in rather

[017-011] sharply. “Shall we go inside, professor?” he said. “Now you have shown

[017-012] me the garden. A beautiful garden. A most beautiful garden. Let us go

[017-013] in,” and he tried to draw the kicking ethnologist by the elbow, at the

[017-014] same time whispering to [[rupert-grant|Grant]]: “I must ask you not to trouble him with

[017-015] questions. Most risky. He must be soothed.”

[017-016] Basil answered in the same tone, with great coolness:

[017-017] “Of course your directions must be followed out, doctor. I will

[017-018] endeavour to do so, but I hope it will not be inconsistent with them if

[017-019] you will leave me alone with my poor friend in this garden for an hour.

[017-020] I want to watch him. I assure you, Dr Colman, that I shall say very

[017-021] little to him, and that little shall be as soothing as--as syrup.”

[017-022] The doctor wiped his eyeglass thoughtfully.

[017-023] “It is rather dangerous for him,” he said, “to be long in the strong sun

[017-024] without his hat. With his bald head, too.”

[017-025] “That is soon settled,” said Basil composedly, and took off his own big

[017-026] hat and clapped it on the egglike skull of the professor. The latter did

[017-027] not turn round but danced away with his eyes on the horizon.

[017-028] The doctor put on his glasses again, looked severely at the two for

[017-029] some seconds, with his head on one side like a bird's, and then saying,

[017-030] shortly, “All right,” strutted away into the house, where the three

[017-031] Misses Chadd were all looking out from the parlour window on to the

[017-032] garden. They looked out on it with hungry eyes for a full hour without

[017-033] moving, and they saw a sight which was more extraordinary than [[madness-and-sanity|madness]]

[017-034] itself.

[017-035] Basil Grant addressed a few questions to the madman, without succeeding

[017-036] in making him do anything but continue to caper, and when he had done

[017-037] this slowly took a red note-book out of one pocket and a large pencil

[017-038] out of another.

[017-039] He began hurriedly to scribble notes. When the lunatic skipped away from

[017-040] him he would walk a few yards in pursuit, stop, and make notes again.

[017-041] Thus they followed each other round and round the foolish circle of

[017-042] turf, the one writing in pencil with the face of a man working out a

[017-043] problem, the other leaping and playing like a child.

[017-044] After about three-quarters of an hour of this imbecile scene, Grant put

[017-045] the pencil in his pocket, but kept the note-book open in his hand, and

[017-046] walking round the mad professor, planted himself directly in front of

[017-047] him.

[017-048] Then occurred something that even those already used to that wild

[017-049] morning had not anticipated or dreamed. The professor, on finding Basil

[017-050] in front of him, stared with a blank benignity for a few seconds, and

[017-051] then drew up his left leg and hung it bent in the attitude that his

[017-052] sister had described as being the first of all his antics. And the

[017-053] moment he had done it Basil Grant lifted his own leg and held it out

[017-054] rigid before him, confronting Chadd with the flat sole of his boot. The

[017-055] professor dropped his bent leg, and swinging his weight on to it kicked

[017-056] out the other behind, like a man swimming. Basil crossed his feet like

[017-057] a saltire cross, and then flung them apart again, giving a leap into

[017-058] the air. Then before any of the spectators could say a word or even

[017-059] entertain a thought about the matter, both of them were dancing a sort

[017-060] of jig or hornpipe opposite each other; and the sun shone down on two

[017-061] madmen instead of one.

[017-062] They were so stricken with the deafness and blindness of monomania that

[017-063] they did not see the eldest Miss Chadd come out feverishly into the

[017-064] garden with gestures of entreaty, a gentleman following her. Professor

[017-065] Chadd was in the wildest posture of a pas-de-quatre, Basil Grant seemed

[017-066] about to turn a cart-wheel, when they were frozen in their follies by

[017-067] the steely voice of Adelaide Chadd saying, “Mr Bingham of the British

[017-068] Museum.”

[017-069] Mr Bingham was a slim, well-clad gentleman with a pointed and slightly

[017-070] effeminate grey beard, unimpeachable gloves, and formal but agreeable

[017-071] manners. He was the type of the over-civilized, as Professor Chadd was

[017-072] of the uncivilized pedant. His formality and agreeableness did him some

[017-073] credit under the circumstances. He had a vast experience of books and a

[017-074] considerable experience of the more dilettante fashionable salons. But

[017-075] neither branch of knowledge had accustomed him to the spectacle of two

[017-076] grey-haired middle-[[society-and-class|class]] gentlemen in modern costume throwing themselves

[017-077] about like acrobats as a substitute for an after-dinner nap.

[017-078] The professor continued his antics with perfect placidity, but Grant

[017-079] stopped abruptly. The doctor had reappeared on the scene, and his shiny

[017-080] black eyes, under his shiny black hat, moved restlessly from one of them

[017-081] to the other.

[017-082] “Dr Colman,” said Basil, turning to him, “will you entertain Professor

[017-083] Chadd again for a little while? I am sure that he needs you. Mr Bingham,

[017-084] might I have the pleasure of a few moments' private conversation? My

[017-085] name is Grant.”

[017-086] Mr Bingham, of the British Museum, bowed in a manner that was respectful

[017-087] but a trifle bewildered.

[017-088] “Miss Chadd will excuse me,” continued Basil easily, “if I know my way

[017-089] about the house.” And he led the dazed librarian rapidly through the

[017-090] back door into the parlour.

[017-091] “Mr Bingham,” said Basil, setting a chair for him, “I imagine that Miss

[017-092] Chadd has told you of this distressing occurrence.”

[017-093] “She has, Mr Grant,” said Bingham, looking at the table with a sort

[017-094] of compassionate nervousness. “I am more pained than I can say by this

[017-095] dreadful calamity. It seems quite heart-rending that the thing should

[017-096] have happened just as we have decided to give your eminent friend

[017-097] a position which falls far short of his merits. As it is, of

[017-098] course--really, I don't know what to say. Professor Chadd may, of

[017-099] course, retain--I sincerely trust he will--his extraordinarily valuable

[017-100] intellect. But I am afraid--I am really afraid--that it would not do to

[017-101] have the curator of the Asiatic manuscripts--er--dancing about.”

[017-102] “I have a suggestion to make,” said Basil, and sat down abruptly in his

[017-103] chair, drawing it up to the table.

[017-104] “I am delighted, of course,” said the gentleman from the British Museum,

[017-105] coughing and drawing up his chair also.

[017-106] The clock on the mantelpiece ticked for just the moments required for

[017-107] Basil to clear his throat and collect his words, and then he said:

[017-108] “My proposal is this. I do not know that in the strict use of words you

[017-109] could altogether call it a compromise, still it has something of that

[017-110] character. My proposal is that the Government (acting, as I presume,

[017-111] through your Museum) should pay Professor Chadd £800 a year until he

[017-112] stops dancing.”

[017-113] “Eight hundred a year!” said Mr Bingham, and for the first time lifted

[017-114] his mild blue eyes to those of his interlocutor--and he raised them

[017-115] with a mild blue stare. “I think I have not quite understood you. Did I

[017-116] understand you to say that Professor Chadd ought to be employed, in his

[017-117] present state, in the Asiatic manuscript department at eight hundred a

[017-118] year?”

[017-119] Grant shook his head resolutely.

[017-120] “No,” he said firmly. “No. Chadd is a friend of mine, and I would say

[017-121] anything for him I could. But I do not say, I cannot say, that he ought

[017-122] to take on the Asiatic manuscripts. I do not go so far as that. I merely

[017-123] say that until he stops dancing you ought to pay him £800 Surely you

[017-124] have some general fund for the endowment of research.”

[017-125] Mr Bingham looked bewildered.

[017-126] “I really don't know,” he said, blinking his eyes, “what you are talking

[017-127] about. Do you ask us to give this obvious lunatic nearly a thousand a

[017-128] year for life?”

[017-129] “Not at all,” cried Basil, keenly and triumphantly. “I never said for

[017-130] life. Not at all.”

[017-131] “What for, then?” asked the meek Bingham, suppressing an instinct meekly

[017-132] to tear his hair. “How long is this endowment to run? Not till his

[017-133] death? Till the Judgement day?”

[017-134] “No,” said Basil, beaming, “but just what I said. Till he has stopped

[017-135] dancing.” And he lay back with satisfaction and his hands in his

[017-136] pockets.

[017-137] Bingham had by this time fastened his eyes keenly on Basil Grant and

[017-138] kept them there.

[017-139] “Come, Mr Grant,” he said. “Do I seriously understand you to suggest

[017-140] that the Government pay Professor Chadd an extraordinarily high salary

[017-141] simply on the ground that he has (pardon the phrase) gone mad? That he

[017-142] should be paid more than four good clerks solely on the ground that he

[017-143] is flinging his boots about in the back yard?”

[017-144] “Precisely,” said Grant composedly.

[017-145] “That this absurd payment is not only to run on with the absurd dancing,

[017-146] but actually to stop with the absurd dancing?”

[017-147] “One must stop somewhere,” said Grant. “Of course.”

[017-148] Bingham rose and took up his perfect stick and gloves.

[017-149] “There is really nothing more to be said, Mr Grant,” he said coldly.

[017-150] “What you are trying to explain to me may be a joke--a slightly

[017-151] unfeeling joke. It may be your sincere view, in which case I ask your

[017-152] pardon for the former suggestion. But, in any case, it appears quite

[017-153] irrelevant to my duties. The mental morbidity, the mental downfall, of

[017-154] Professor Chadd, is a thing so painful to me that I cannot easily endure

[017-155] to speak of it. But it is clear there is a limit to everything. And if

[017-156] the Archangel Gabriel went mad it would sever his connection, I am sorry

[017-157] to say, with the British Museum Library.”

[017-158] He was stepping towards the door, but Grant's hand, flung out in

[017-159] dramatic warning, arrested him.

[017-160] “Stop!” said Basil sternly. “Stop while there is yet time. Do you want

[017-161] to take part in a great [[profession-and-trade|work]], Mr Bingham? Do you want to help in the

[017-162] glory of Europe--in the glory of science? Do you want to carry your head

[017-163] in the air when it is bald or white because of the part that you bore in

[017-164] a great discovery? Do you want--”

[017-165] Bingham cut in sharply:

[017-166] “And if I do want this, Mr Grant--”

[017-167] “Then,” said Basil lightly, “your task is easy. Get Chadd £800 a year

[017-168] till he stops dancing.”

[017-169] With a fierce flap of his swinging gloves Bingham turned impatiently

[017-170] to the door, but in passing out of it found it blocked. Dr Colman was

[017-171] coming in.

[017-172] “Forgive me, gentlemen,” he said, in a nervous, confidential voice, “the

[017-173] fact is, Mr Grant, I--er--have made a most disturbing discovery about Mr

[017-174] Chadd.”

[017-175] Bingham looked at him with grave eyes.

[017-176] “I was afraid so,” he said. “Drink, I imagine.”

[017-177] “Drink!” echoed Colman, as if that were a much milder affair. “Oh, no,

[017-178] it's not drink.”

[017-179] Mr Bingham became somewhat agitated, and his voice grew hurried and

[017-180] vague. “Homicidal mania--” he began.

[017-181] “No, no,” said the medical man impatiently.

[017-182] “Thinks he's made of glass,” said Bingham feverishly, “or says he's

[017-183] God--or--”

[017-184] “No,” said Dr Colman sharply; “the fact is, Mr Grant, my discovery is of

[017-185] a different character. The awful thing about him is--”

[017-186] “Oh, go on, sir,” cried Bingham, in agony.

[017-187] “The awful thing about him is,” repeated Colman, with deliberation,

[017-188] “that he isn't mad.”

[017-189] “Not mad!”

[017-190] “There are quite well-known physical tests of lunacy,” said the doctor

[017-191] shortly; “he hasn't got any of them.”
