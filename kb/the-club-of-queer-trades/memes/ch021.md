[021-001] “Precisely,” said the large man on the floor in an easy tone. “I hold

[021-002] that nothing more than a rough sketch of the universe as seen by science

[021-003] can be...”

[021-004] And here the voices died away as we descended into the basement. I

[021-005] noticed that Mr Greenwood did not join in the amicable controversy.

[021-006] Strange as it may appear, I think he looked back upon our proceedings

[021-007] with a slight degree of resentment. Mr [[burrows|Burrows]], however, was all

[021-008] [[philosophy-and-detection|philosophy]] and chattiness. We left them, as I say, together, and sank

[021-009] deeper and deeper into the under-world of that mysterious house, which,

[021-010] perhaps, appeared to us somewhat more Tartarean than it really was,

[021-011] owing to our knowledge of its semi-[[crime-in-fiction|criminal]] [[mystery-in-fiction|mystery]] and of the human

[021-012] [[secret-in-fiction|secret]] locked below.

[021-013] [[the-reverend-vicar|The]] basement floor had several doors, as is usual in such a house; doors

[021-014] that would naturally lead to the kitchen, the scullery, the pantry,

[021-015] the servants' hall, and so on. [[rupert-grant|Rupert]] flung open all the doors with

[021-016] indescribable rapidity. Four out of the five opened on entirely empty

[021-017] apartments. The fifth was locked. Rupert broke the door in like a

[021-018] bandbox, and we fell into the sudden blackness of the sealed, unlighted

[021-019] room.

[021-020] Rupert stood on the threshold, and called out like a man calling into an

[021-021] abyss:

[021-022] “Whoever you are, come out. You are free. The people who held you

[021-023] captive are captives themselves. We heard you crying and we came to

[021-024] deliver you. We have bound your enemies upstairs hand and foot. You are

[021-025] free.”

[021-026] For some seconds after he had spoken into the darkness there was a dead

[021-027] silence in it. Then there came a kind of muttering and moaning. We might

[021-028] easily have taken it for the wind or rats if we had not happened to have

[021-029] heard it before. It was unmistakably the voice of the imprisoned woman,

[021-030] drearily demanding liberty, just as we had heard her demand it.

[021-031] “Has anybody got a match?” said Rupert grimly. “I fancy we have come

[021-032] pretty near the end of this business.”

[021-033] I struck a match and held it up. It revealed a large, bare,

[021-034] yellow-papered apartment with a dark-clad figure at the other end of

[021-035] it near the window. An instant after it burned my fingers and dropped,

[021-036] leaving darkness. It had, however, revealed something more practical--an

[021-037] iron gas bracket just above my head. I struck another match and lit the

[021-038] gas. And we found ourselves suddenly and seriously in the presence of

[021-039] the captive.

[021-040] At a sort of workbox in the window of this subterranean breakfast-room

[021-041] sat an elderly lady with a singularly high colour and almost startling

[021-042] silver hair. She had, as if designedly to relieve these effects, a pair

[021-043] of Mephistophelian black eyebrows and a very neat black dress. The glare

[021-044] of the gas lit up her piquant hair and face perfectly against the brown

[021-045] background of the shutters. The background was blue and not brown in one

[021-046] place; at the place where Rupert's knife had torn a great opening in the

[021-047] wood about an hour before.

[021-048] “Madam,” said he, advancing with a gesture of the hat, “permit me

[021-049] to have the pleasure of announcing to you that you are free. Your

[021-050] complaints happened to strike our ears as we passed down the street, and

[021-051] we have therefore ventured to come to your rescue.”

[021-052] The old lady with the red face and the black eyebrows looked at us for

[021-053] a moment with something of the apoplectic stare of a parrot. Then she

[021-054] said, with a sudden gust or breathing of relief:

[021-055] “Rescue? Where is Mr Greenwood? Where is Mr Burrows? Did you say you had

[021-056] rescued me?”

[021-057] “Yes, madam,” said Rupert, with a beaming condescension. “We have very

[021-058] satisfactorily dealt with Mr Greenwood and Mr Burrows. We have settled

[021-059] affairs with them very satisfactorily.”

[021-060] The old lady rose from her chair and came very quickly towards us.

[021-061] “What did you say to them? How did you persuade them?” she cried.

[021-062] “We persuaded them, my dear madam,” said Rupert, laughing, “by knocking

[021-063] them down and tying them up. But what is the matter?”

[021-064] To the surprise of every one the old lady walked slowly back to her seat

[021-065] by the window.

[021-066] “Do I understand,” she said, with the air of a person about to begin

[021-067] knitting, “that you have knocked down Mr Burrows and tied him up?”

[021-068] “We have,” said Rupert proudly; “we have resisted their oppression and

[021-069] conquered it.”

[021-070] “Oh, thanks,” answered the old lady, and sat down by the window.

[021-071] A considerable pause followed.

[021-072] “The road is quite clear for you, madam,” said Rupert pleasantly.

[021-073] The old lady rose, cocking her black eyebrows and her silver crest at us

[021-074] for an instant.

[021-075] “But what about Greenwood and Burrows?” she said. “What did I understand

[021-076] you to say had become of them?”

[021-077] “They are lying on the floor upstairs,” said Rupert, chuckling. “Tied

[021-078] hand and foot.”

[021-079] “Well, that settles it,” said the old lady, coming with a kind of bang

[021-080] into her seat again, “I must stop where I am.”

[021-081] Rupert looked bewildered.

[021-082] “Stop where you are?” he said. “Why should you stop any longer where you

[021-083] are? What power can force you now to stop in this miserable cell?”

[021-084] “The question rather is,” said the old lady, with composure, “what power

[021-085] can force me to go anywhere else?”

[021-086] We both stared wildly at her and she stared tranquilly at us both.

[021-087] At last I said, “Do you really mean to say that we are to leave you

[021-088] here?”

[021-089] “I suppose you don't intend to tie me up,” she said, “and carry me off?

[021-090] I certainly shall not go otherwise.”

[021-091] “But, my dear madam,” cried out Rupert, in a radiant exasperation, “we

[021-092] heard you with our own ears crying because you could not get out.”

[021-093] “Eavesdroppers often hear rather misleading things,” replied the captive

[021-094] grimly. “I suppose I did break down a bit and lose my temper and talk to

[021-095] myself. But I have some sense of honour for all that.”

[021-096] “Some sense of honour?” repeated Rupert, and the last light of

[021-097] intelligence died out of his face, leaving it the face of an idiot with

[021-098] rolling eyes.

[021-099] He moved vaguely towards the door and I followed. But I turned yet once

[021-100] more in the toils of my conscience and curiosity. “Can we do nothing for

[021-101] you, madam?” I said forlornly.

[021-102] “Why,” said the lady, “if you are particularly anxious to do me a little

[021-103] favour you might untie the gentlemen upstairs.”

[021-104] Rupert plunged heavily up the kitchen staircase, shaking it with his

[021-105] vague violence. With mouth open to speak he stumbled to the door of the

[021-106] sitting-room and scene of battle.

[021-107] “Theoretically speaking, that is no doubt true,” Mr Burrows was saying,

[021-108] lying on his back and arguing easily with [[basil-grant|Basil]]; “but we must consider

[021-109] the matter as it appears to our sense. The origin of [[moral-philosophy|morality]]...”

[021-110] “Basil,” cried Rupert, gasping, “she won't come out.”

[021-111] “Who won't come out?” asked Basil, a little cross at being interrupted

[021-112] in an argument.

[021-113] “The lady downstairs,” replied Rupert. “The lady who was locked up. She

[021-114] won't come out. And she says that all she wants is for us to let these

[021-115] fellows loose.”

[021-116] “And a jolly sensible suggestion,” cried Basil, and with a bound he was

[021-117] on top of the prostrate Burrows once more and was unknotting his bonds

[021-118] with hands and teeth.

[021-119] “A brilliant idea. [[charles-swinburne|Swinburne]], just undo Mr Greenwood.”

[021-120] In a dazed and automatic way I released the little gentleman in the

[021-121] purple jacket, who did not seem to regard any of the proceedings as

[021-122] particularly sensible or brilliant. The gigantic Burrows, on the other

[021-123] hand, was heaving with herculean laughter.

[021-124] “Well,” said Basil, in his cheeriest way, “I think we must be getting

[021-125] away. We've so much enjoyed our evening. Far too much regard for you to

[021-126] stand on ceremony. If I may so express myself, we've made ourselves at

[021-127] home. Good night. Thanks so much. Come along, Rupert.”

[021-128] “Basil,” said Rupert desperately, “for God's sake come and see what you

[021-129] can make of the woman downstairs. I can't get the discomfort out of my

[021-130] mind. I admit that things look as if we had made a mistake. But these

[021-131] gentlemen won't mind perhaps...”

[021-132] “No, no,” cried Burrows, with a sort of Rabelaisian uproariousness. “No,

[021-133] no, look in the pantry, gentlemen. Examine the coal-hole. Make a tour of

[021-134] the chimneys. There are corpses all over the house, I assure you.”

[021-135] This [[adventure-in-fiction|adventure]] of ours was destined to differ in one respect from others

[021-136] which I have narrated. I had been through many wild days with Basil

[021-137] Grant, days for the first half of which the sun and the moon seemed to

[021-138] have gone mad. But it had almost invariably happened that towards the

[021-139] end of the day and its adventure things had cleared themselves like the

[021-140] sky after rain, and a luminous and quiet meaning had gradually dawned

[021-141] upon me. But this day's [[profession-and-trade|work]] was destined to end in confusion worse

[021-142] confounded. Before we left that house, ten minutes afterwards, one

[021-143] half-witted touch was added which rolled all our minds in cloud. If

[021-144] Rupert's head had suddenly fallen off on the floor, if wings had begun

[021-145] to sprout out of Greenwood's shoulders, we could scarcely have been more

[021-146] suddenly stricken. And yet of this we had no explanation. We had to go

[021-147] to bed that night with the prodigy and get up next morning with it and

[021-148] let it stand in our memories for weeks and months. As will be seen, it

[021-149] was not until months afterwards that by another accident and in another

[021-150] way it was explained. For the present I only state what happened.

[021-151] When all five of us went down the kitchen stairs again, Rupert leading,

[021-152] the two hosts bringing up the rear, we found the door of the prison

[021-153] again closed. Throwing it open we found the place again as black as

[021-154] pitch. The old lady, if she was still there, had turned out the gas: she

[021-155] seemed to have a weird preference for sitting in the dark.

[021-156] Without another word Rupert lit the gas again. The little old lady

[021-157] turned her bird-like head as we all stumbled forward in the strong

[021-158] gaslight. Then, with a quickness that almost made me jump, she sprang up

[021-159] and swept a sort of old-fashioned curtsey or reverence. I looked

[021-160] quickly at Greenwood and Burrows, to whom it was natural to suppose this

[021-161] subservience had been offered. I felt irritated at what was implied in

[021-162] this subservience, and desired to see the faces of the tyrants as they

[021-163] received it. To my surprise they did not seem to have seen it at all:

[021-164] Burrows was paring his nails with a small penknife. Greenwood was at the

[021-165] back of the group and had hardly entered the room. And then an amazing

[021-166] fact became apparent. It was Basil Grant who stood foremost of the

[021-167] group, the golden gaslight lighting up his strong face and figure. His

[021-168] face wore an expression indescribably conscious, with the suspicion of

[021-169] a very grave smile. His head was slightly bent with a restrained bow. It

[021-170] was he who had acknowledged the lady's obeisance. And it was he, beyond

[021-171] any shadow of reasonable doubt, to whom it had really been directed.

[021-172] “So I hear,” he said, in a kindly yet somehow formal voice, “I hear,

[021-173] madam, that my friends have been trying to rescue you. But without

[021-174] success.”

[021-175] “No one, naturally, knows my faults better than you,” answered the lady

[021-176] with a high colour. “But you have not found me guilty of treachery.”

[021-177] “I willingly attest it, madam,” replied Basil, in the same level tones,

[021-178] “and the fact is that I am so much gratified with your exhibition of

[021-179] loyalty that I permit myself the pleasure of exercising some very large

[021-180] discretionary powers. You would not leave this room at the request of

[021-181] these gentlemen. But you know that you can safely leave it at mine.”
