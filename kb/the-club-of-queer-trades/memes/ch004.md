[004-001] “I think you will find that all right, [[major-brown|Major]],” he said briefly.

[004-002] [[the-reverend-vicar|The]] Major looked at it; whether he found it all right or not will appear

[004-003] later, but he found it like this:

[004-004] Major Brown to [[northover|P. G. Northover]]. £ s. d.

[004-005] January 1, to account rendered 5 6 0

[004-006] May 9, to potting and embedding of 200 pansies 2 0 0

[004-007] To cost of trolley with flowers 0 15 0

[004-008] To hiring of man with trolley 0 5 0

[004-009] To hire of house and garden for one day 1 0 0

[004-010] To furnishing of room in peacock curtains, copper ornaments, etc. 3 0 0

[004-011] To salary of [[miss-jameson|Miss Jameson]] 1 0 0

[004-012] To salary of Mr Plover 1 0 0

[004-013] ----------

[004-014] Total L14 6 0

[004-015] A Remittance will oblige.

[004-016] “What,” said Brown, after a dead pause, and with eyes that seemed slowly

[004-017] rising out of his head, “What in heaven's name is this?”

[004-018] “What is it?” repeated Northover, cocking his eyebrow with amusement.

[004-019] “It's your account, of course.”

[004-020] “My account!” The Major's ideas appeared to be in a vague stampede. “My

[004-021] account! And what have I got to do with it?”

[004-022] “Well,” said Northover, laughing outright, “naturally I prefer you to

[004-023] pay it.”

[004-024] The Major's hand was still resting on the back of the chair as the words

[004-025] came. He scarcely stirred otherwise, but he lifted the chair bodily into

[004-026] the air with one hand and hurled it at Northover's head.

[004-027] The legs crashed against the desk, so that Northover only got a blow on

[004-028] the elbow as he sprang up with clenched fists, only to be seized by the

[004-029] united rush of the rest of us. The chair had fallen clattering on the

[004-030] empty floor.

[004-031] “Let me go, you scamps,” he shouted. “Let me--”

[004-032] “Stand still,” cried [[rupert-grant|Rupert]] authoritatively. “Major Brown's action is

[004-033] excusable. The abominable [[crime-in-fiction|crime]] you have attempted--”

[004-034] “A customer has a perfect right,” said Northover hotly, “to question an

[004-035] alleged overcharge, but, confound it all, not to throw furniture.”

[004-036] “What, in God's name, do you mean by your customers and overcharges?”

[004-037] shrieked Major Brown, whose keen feminine nature, steady in pain

[004-038] or danger, became almost hysterical in the presence of a long and

[004-039] exasperating [[mystery-in-fiction|mystery]]. “Who are you? I've never seen you or your insolent

[004-040] tomfool bills. I know one of your cursed brutes tried to choke me--”

[004-041] “Mad,” said Northover, gazing blankly round; “all of them mad. I didn't

[004-042] know they travelled in quartettes.”

[004-043] “Enough of this prevarication,” said Rupert; “your crimes are

[004-044] discovered. A policeman is stationed at the corner of the court. Though

[004-045] only a [[detective-in-fiction|private detective]] myself, I will take the responsibility of

[004-046] telling you that anything you say--”

[004-047] “Mad,” repeated Northover, with a weary air.

[004-048] And at this moment, for the first time, there struck in among them the

[004-049] strange, sleepy voice of [[basil-grant|Basil Grant]].

[004-050] “Major Brown,” he said, “may I ask you a question?”

[004-051] The Major turned his head with an increased bewilderment.

[004-052] “You?” he cried; “certainly, Mr Grant.”

[004-053] “Can you tell me,” said the mystic, with sunken head and lowering brow,

[004-054] as he traced a pattern in the dust with his sword-stick, “can you tell

[004-055] me what was the name of the man who lived in your house before you?”

[004-056] The unhappy Major was only faintly more disturbed by this last and

[004-057] futile irrelevancy, and he answered vaguely:

[004-058] “Yes, I think so; a man named Gurney something--a name with a

[004-059] hyphen--Gurney-Brown; that was it.”

[004-060] “And when did the house change hands?” said Basil, looking up sharply.

[004-061] His strange eyes were burning brilliantly.

[004-062] “I came in last month,” said the Major.

[004-063] And at the mere word the criminal Northover suddenly fell into his great

[004-064] office chair and shouted with a volleying laughter.

[004-065] “Oh! it's too perfect--it's too exquisite,” he gasped, beating the arms

[004-066] with his fists. He was laughing deafeningly; Basil Grant was laughing

[004-067] voicelessly; and the rest of us only felt that our heads were like

[004-068] weathercocks in a whirlwind.

[004-069] “Confound it, Basil,” said Rupert, stamping. “If you don't want me to go

[004-070] mad and blow your metaphysical brains out, tell me what all this means.”

[004-071] Northover rose.

[004-072] “Permit me, sir, to explain,” he said. “And, first of all, permit me to

[004-073] apologize to you, Major Brown, for a most abominable and unpardonable

[004-074] blunder, which has caused you menace and inconvenience, in which, if you

[004-075] will allow me to say so, you have behaved with astonishing courage and

[004-076] dignity. Of course you need not trouble about the bill. We will stand

[004-077] the loss.” And, tearing the paper across, he flung the halves into the

[004-078] waste-paper basket and bowed.

[004-079] Poor Brown's face was still a picture of distraction. “But I don't even

[004-080] begin to understand,” he cried. “What bill? what blunder? what loss?”

[004-081] Mr P. G. Northover advanced in the centre of the room, thoughtfully, and

[004-082] with a great deal of unconscious dignity. On closer consideration,

[004-083] there were apparent about him other things beside a screwed moustache,

[004-084] especially a lean, sallow face, hawk-like, and not without a careworn

[004-085] intelligence. Then he looked up abruptly.

[004-086] “Do you know where you are, Major?” he said.

[004-087] “God knows I don't,” said the warrior, with fervour.

[004-088] “You are standing,” replied Northover, “in the office of the Adventure

[004-089] and Romance Agency, Limited.”

[004-090] “And what's that?” blankly inquired Brown.

[004-091] The man of business leaned over the back of the chair, and fixed his

[004-092] dark eyes on the other's face.

[004-093] “Major,” said he, “did you ever, as you walked along the empty street

[004-094] upon some idle afternoon, feel the utter hunger for something to

[004-095] happen--something, in the splendid words of Walt Whitman: 'Something

[004-096] pernicious and dread; something far removed from a puny and pious life;

[004-097] something unproved; something in a trance; something loosed from its

[004-098] anchorage, and driving free.' Did you ever feel that?”

[004-099] “Certainly not,” said the Major shortly.

[004-100] “Then I must explain with more elaboration,” said Mr Northover, with a

[004-101] sigh. “The [[adventure-and-romance-agency|Adventure and Romance Agency]] has been started to meet a great

[004-102] modern desire. On every side, in conversation and in literature, we hear

[004-103] of the desire for a larger theatre of events for something to waylay us

[004-104] and lead us splendidly astray. Now the man who feels this desire for

[004-105] a varied life pays a yearly or a quarterly sum to the Adventure and

[004-106] Romance Agency; in return, the Adventure and Romance Agency undertakes

[004-107] to surround him with startling and weird events. As a man is leaving his

[004-108] front door, an excited sweep approaches him and assures him of a plot

[004-109] against his life; he gets into a cab, and is driven to an opium den; he

[004-110] receives a mysterious telegram or a dramatic visit, and is immediately

[004-111] in a vortex of incidents. A very picturesque and moving [[fiction-as-truth|story]] is first

[004-112] written by one of the staff of distinguished novelists who are at

[004-113] present hard at [[profession-and-trade|work]] in the adjoining room. Yours, Major Brown (designed

[004-114] by our Mr Grigsby), I consider peculiarly forcible and pointed; it is

[004-115] almost a pity you did not see the end of it. I need scarcely explain

[004-116] further the monstrous mistake. Your predecessor in your present house,

[004-117] Mr Gurney-Brown, was a subscriber to our agency, and our foolish clerks,

[004-118] ignoring alike the dignity of the hyphen and the glory of military rank,

[004-119] positively imagined that Major Brown and Mr Gurney-Brown were the same

[004-120] person. Thus you were suddenly hurled into the middle of another man's

[004-121] story.”

[004-122] “How on earth does the thing work?” asked Rupert Grant, with bright and

[004-123] fascinated eyes.

[004-124] “We believe that we are doing a noble work,” said Northover warmly. “It

[004-125] has continually struck us that there is no element in modern life that

[004-126] is more lamentable than the fact that the modern man has to seek all

[004-127] artistic existence in a sedentary state. If he wishes to float into

[004-128] fairyland, he reads a book; if he wishes to dash into the thick of

[004-129] battle, he reads a book; if he wishes to soar into heaven, he reads a

[004-130] book; if he wishes to slide down the banisters, he reads a book. We

[004-131] give him these visions, but we give him exercise at the same time, the

[004-132] necessity of leaping from wall to wall, of fighting strange gentlemen,

[004-133] of running down long streets from pursuers--all healthy and pleasant

[004-134] exercises. We give him a glimpse of that great morning world of Robin

[004-135] Hood or the Knights Errant, when one great game was played under the

[004-136] splendid sky. We give him back his childhood, that godlike time when we

[004-137] can act stories, be our own heroes, and at the same instant dance and

[004-138] dream.”

[004-139] Basil gazed at him curiously. The most singular psychological discovery

[004-140] had been reserved to the end, for as the little business man ceased

[004-141] speaking he had the blazing eyes of a fanatic.

[004-142] Major Brown received the explanation with complete simplicity and good

[004-143] humour.

[004-144] “Of course; awfully dense, sir,” he said. “No doubt at all, the scheme

[004-145] excellent. But I don't think--” He paused a moment, and looked dreamily

[004-146] out of the window. “I don't think you will find me in it. Somehow, when

[004-147] one's seen--seen the thing itself, you know--blood and men screaming,

[004-148] one feels about having a little house and a little hobby; in the Bible,

[004-149] you know, 'There remaineth a rest'.”

[004-150] Northover bowed. Then after a pause he said:

[004-151] “Gentlemen, may I offer you my card. If any of the rest of you desire,

[004-152] at any time, to communicate with me, despite Major Brown's view of the

[004-153] matter--”

[004-154] “I should be obliged for your card, sir,” said the Major, in his abrupt

[004-155] but courteous voice. “Pay for chair.”

[004-156] The agent of Romance and Adventure handed his card, laughing.

[004-157] It ran, “P. G. Northover, B.A., [[queer-trades|C.Q.T.]], Adventure and Romance Agency, 14

[004-158] [[tanners-court-fleet-street|Tanner's Court, Fleet Street]].”

[004-159] “What on earth is 'C.Q.T.'?” asked Rupert Grant, looking over the Major's

[004-160] shoulder.

[004-161] “Don't you know?” returned Northover. “Haven't you ever heard of the

[004-162] Club of Queer Trades?”

[004-163] “There seems to be a confounded lot of funny things we haven't heard

[004-164] of,” said the little Major reflectively. “What's this one?”

[004-165] “[[the-club-of-queer-trades-org|The Club of Queer Trades]] is a [[club-as-institution|society]] consisting exclusively of people

[004-166] who have invented some new and curious way of making money. I was one of

[004-167] the earliest members.”

[004-168] “You deserve to be,” said Basil, taking up his great white hat, with a

[004-169] smile, and speaking for the last time that evening.

[004-170] When they had passed out the Adventure and Romance agent wore a queer

[004-171] smile, as he trod down the fire and locked up his desk. “A fine chap,

[004-172] that Major; when one hasn't a touch of the poet one stands some chance

[004-173] of being a poem. But to think of such a clockwork little creature of all

[004-174] people getting into the nets of one of Grigsby's tales,” and he laughed

[004-175] out aloud in the silence.

[004-176] Just as the laugh echoed away, there came a sharp knock at the door. An

[004-177] owlish head, with dark moustaches, was thrust in, with deprecating and

[004-178] somewhat absurd inquiry.

[004-179] “What! back again, Major?” cried Northover in surprise. “What can I do

[004-180] for you?”

[004-181] The Major shuffled feverishly into the room.

[004-182] “It's horribly absurd,” he said. “Something must have got started in

[004-183] me that I never knew before. But upon my soul I feel the most desperate

[004-184] desire to know the end of it all.”

[004-185] “The end of it all?”

[004-186] “Yes,” said the Major. “'Jackals', and the title-deeds, and 'Death to

[004-187] Major Brown'.”

[004-188] The agent's face grew grave, but his eyes were amused.

[004-189] “I am terribly sorry, Major,” said he, “but what you ask is impossible.

[004-190] I don't know any one I would sooner oblige than you; but the rules

[004-191] of the agency are strict. The Adventures are confidential; you are an

[004-192] outsider; I am not allowed to let you know an inch more than I can help.

[004-193] I do hope you understand--”

[004-194] “There is no one,” said Brown, “who understands discipline better than I

[004-195] do. Thank you very much. Good night.”
