[012-001] “This is the office of which I spoke,” said Keith, in a cutting voice.

[012-002] “Will you wait here a moment, or does your astonishing tenderness about

[012-003] my welfare lead you to wish to overhear everything I have to say to my

[012-004] business adviser?”

[012-005] [[rupert-grant|Rupert]]'s face was white and shaking with excitement; nothing on earth

[012-006] would have induced him now to have abandoned his prey.

[012-007] “If you will excuse me,” he said, clenching his hands behind his back,

[012-008] “I think I should feel myself justified in--”

[012-009] “Oh! Come along in,” exploded the lieutenant. He made the same gesture

[012-010] of savage surrender. And he slammed into the office, the rest of us at

[012-011] his heels.

[012-012] [[mr-montmorency|P. Montmorency]], House-Agent, was a solitary old gentleman sitting behind

[012-013] a bare brown counter. He had an egglike head, froglike jaws, and a grey

[012-014] hairy fringe of aureole round the lower part of his face; the whole

[012-015] combined with a reddish, aquiline nose. He wore a shabby black

[012-016] frock-coat, a sort of semi-clerical tie worn at a very unclerical

[012-017] angle, and looked, generally speaking, about as unlike a house-agent as

[012-018] anything could look, short of something like a sandwich man or a Scotch

[012-019] Highlander.

[012-020] We stood inside the room for fully forty seconds, and the odd old

[012-021] gentleman did not look at us. Neither, to tell the [[truth-and-fiction|truth]], odd as he

[012-022] was, did we look at him. Our eyes were fixed, where his were fixed, upon

[012-023] something that was crawling about on the counter in front of him. It was

[012-024] a ferret.

[012-025] [[the-reverend-vicar|The]] silence was broken by Rupert [[basil-grant|Grant]]. He spoke in that sweet and

[012-026] steely voice which he reserved for great occasions and practised for

[012-027] hours together in his bedroom. He said:

[012-028] “Mr Montmorency, I think?”

[012-029] The old gentleman started, lifted his eyes with a bland bewilderment,

[012-030] picked up the ferret by the neck, stuffed it alive into his trousers

[012-031] pocket, smiled apologetically, and said:

[012-032] “Sir.”

[012-033] “You are a house-agent, are you not?” asked Rupert.

[012-034] To the delight of that [[crime-in-fiction|criminal]] investigator, Mr Montmorency's eyes

[012-035] wandered unquietly towards Lieutenant Keith, the only man present that

[012-036] he knew.

[012-037] “A house-agent,” cried Rupert again, bringing out the word as if it were

[012-038] “burglar”.

[012-039] “Yes... oh, yes,” said the man, with a quavering and almost coquettish

[012-040] smile. “I am a house-agent... oh, yes.”

[012-041] “Well, I think,” said Rupert, with a sardonic sleekness, “that

[012-042] Lieutenant Keith wants to speak to you. We have come in by his request.”

[012-043] Lieutenant Keith was lowering gloomily, and now he spoke.

[012-044] “I have come, Mr Montmorency, about that house of mine.”

[012-045] “Yes, sir,” said Montmorency, spreading his fingers on the flat counter.

[012-046] “It's all ready, sir. I've attended to all your suggestions er--about

[012-047] the br--”

[012-048] “Right,” cried Keith, cutting the word short with the startling neatness

[012-049] of a gunshot. “We needn't bother about all that. If you've done what I

[012-050] told you, all right.”

[012-051] And he turned sharply towards the door.

[012-052] Mr Montmorency, House-Agent, presented a picture of pathos. After

[012-053] stammering a moment he said: “Excuse me... Mr Keith... there was another

[012-054] matter... about which I wasn't quite sure. I tried to get all the

[012-055] heating apparatus possible under the circumstances ... but in winter...

[012-056] at that elevation...”

[012-057] “Can't expect much, eh?” said the lieutenant, cutting in with the same

[012-058] sudden skill. “No, of course not. That's all right, Montmorency. There

[012-059] can't be any more difficulties,” and he put his hand on the handle of

[012-060] the door.

[012-061] “I think,” said Rupert Grant, with a satanic suavity, “that Mr

[012-062] Montmorency has something further to say to you, lieutenant.”

[012-063] “Only,” said the house-agent, in desperation, “what about the birds?”

[012-064] “I beg your pardon,” said Rupert, in a general blank.

[012-065] “What about the birds?” said the house-agent doggedly.

[012-066] Basil, who had remained throughout the proceedings in a state of

[012-067] Napoleonic calm, which might be more accurately described as a state of

[012-068] Napoleonic stupidity, suddenly lifted his leonine head.

[012-069] “Before you go, Lieutenant Keith,” he said. “Come now. Really, what

[012-070] about the birds?”

[012-071] “I'll take care of them,” said Lieutenant Keith, still with his long

[012-072] back turned to us; “they shan't suffer.”

[012-073] “Thank you, sir, thank you,” cried the incomprehensible house-agent,

[012-074] with an air of ecstasy. “You'll excuse my concern, sir. You know I'm

[012-075] wild on wild animals. I'm as wild as any of them on that. Thank you,

[012-076] sir. But there's another thing...”

[012-077] The lieutenant, with his back turned to us, exploded with an

[012-078] indescribable laugh and swung round to face us. It was a laugh, the

[012-079] purport of which was direct and essential, and yet which one cannot

[012-080] exactly express. As near as it said anything, verbally speaking, it

[012-081] said: “Well, if you must spoil it, you must. But you don't know what

[012-082] you're spoiling.”

[012-083] “There is another thing,” continued Mr Montmorency weakly. “Of course,

[012-084] if you don't want to be visited you'll paint the house green, but--”

[012-085] “Green!” shouted Keith. “Green! Let it be green or nothing. I won't have

[012-086] a house of another colour. Green!” and before we could realize anything

[012-087] the door had banged between us and the street.

[012-088] Rupert Grant seemed to take a little time to collect himself; but he

[012-089] spoke before the echoes of the door died away.

[012-090] “Your client, Lieutenant Keith, appears somewhat excited,” he said.

[012-091] “What is the matter with him? Is he unwell?”

[012-092] “Oh, I should think not,” said Mr Montmorency, in some confusion. “The

[012-093] negotiations have been somewhat difficult--the house is rather--”

[012-094] “Green,” said Rupert calmly. “That appears to be a very important point.

[012-095] It must be rather green. May I ask you, Mr Montmorency, before I rejoin

[012-096] my companion outside, whether, in your business, it is usual to ask for

[012-097] houses by their colour? Do clients write to a house-agent asking for a

[012-098] pink house or a blue house? Or, to take another instance, for a green

[012-099] house?”

[012-100] “Only,” said Montmorency, trembling, “only to be inconspicuous.”

[012-101] Rupert had his ruthless smile. “Can you tell me any place on earth in

[012-102] which a green house would be inconspicuous?”

[012-103] The house-agent was fidgeting nervously in his pocket. Slowly drawing

[012-104] out a couple of lizards and leaving them to run on the counter, he said:

[012-105] “No; I can't.”

[012-106] “You can't suggest an explanation?”

[012-107] “No,” said Mr Montmorency, rising slowly and yet in such a way as to

[012-108] suggest a sudden situation, “I can't. And may I, as a busy man, be

[012-109] excused if I ask you, gentlemen, if you have any demand to make of me in

[012-110] connection with my business. What kind of house would you desire me to

[012-111] get for you, sir?”

[012-112] He opened his blank blue eyes on Rupert, who seemed for the second

[012-113] staggered. Then he recovered himself with perfect common sense and

[012-114] answered:

[012-115] “I am sorry, Mr Montmorency. The fascination of your remarks has unduly

[012-116] delayed us from joining our friend outside. Pray excuse my apparent

[012-117] impertinence.”

[012-118] “Not at all, sir,” said the house-agent, taking a South American spider

[012-119] idly from his waistcoat pocket and letting it climb up the slope of his

[012-120] desk. “Not at all, sir. I hope you will favour me again.”

[012-121] Rupert Grant dashed out of the office in a gust of anger, anxious

[012-122] to face Lieutenant Keith. He was gone. The dull, starlit street was

[012-123] deserted.

[012-124] “What do you say now?” cried Rupert to his brother. His brother said

[012-125] nothing now.

[012-126] We all three strode down the street in silence, Rupert feverish, myself

[012-127] dazed, Basil, to all appearance, merely dull. We walked through grey

[012-128] street after grey street, turning corners, traversing squares, scarcely

[012-129] meeting anyone, except occasional drunken knots of two or three.

[012-130] In one small street, however, the knots of two or three began abruptly

[012-131] to thicken into knots of five or six and then into great groups and then

[012-132] into a crowd. The crowd was stirring very slightly. But anyone with a

[012-133] knowledge of the eternal populace knows that if the outside rim of a

[012-134] crowd stirs ever so slightly it means that there is [[madness-and-sanity|madness]] in the

[012-135] heart and core of the mob. It soon became evident that something really

[012-136] important had happened in the centre of this excitement. We wormed our

[012-137] way to the front, with the cunning which is known only to cockneys, and

[012-138] once there we soon learned the nature of the difficulty. There had been

[012-139] a brawl concerned with some six men, and one of them lay almost dead

[012-140] on the stones of the street. Of the other four, all interesting matters

[012-141] were, as far as we were concerned, swallowed up in one stupendous fact.

[012-142] One of the four survivors of the brutal and perhaps fatal scuffle was

[012-143] the immaculate Lieutenant Keith, his clothes torn to ribbons, his eyes

[012-144] blazing, blood on his knuckles. One other thing, however, pointed at him

[012-145] in a worse manner. A short sword, or very long knife, had been drawn out

[012-146] of his elegant walking-stick, and lay in front of him upon the stones.

[012-147] It did not, however, appear to be bloody.

[012-148] The police had already pushed into the centre with their ponderous

[012-149] omnipotence, and even as they did so, Rupert Grant sprang forward with

[012-150] his incontrollable and intolerable [[secret-in-fiction|secret]].

[012-151] “That is the man, constable,” he shouted, pointing at the battered

[012-152] lieutenant. “He is a suspicious character. He did the murder.”

[012-153] “There's been no murder done, sir,” said the policeman, with his

[012-154] automatic civility. “The poor man's only hurt. I shall only be able to

[012-155] take the names and addresses of the men in the scuffle and have a good

[012-156] eye kept on them.”

[012-157] “Have a good eye kept on that one,” said Rupert, pale to the lips, and

[012-158] pointing to the ragged Keith.

[012-159] “All right, sir,” said the policeman unemotionally, and went the round

[012-160] of the people present, collecting the addresses. When he had completed

[012-161] his task the dusk had fallen and most of the people not immediately

[012-162] connected with the examination had gone away. He still found, however,

[012-163] one eager-faced stranger lingering on the outskirts of the affair. It

[012-164] was Rupert Grant.

[012-165] “Constable,” he said, “I have a very particular reason for asking you

[012-166] a question. Would you mind telling me whether that military fellow who

[012-167] dropped his sword-stick in the row gave you an address or not?”

[012-168] “Yes, sir,” said the policeman, after a reflective pause; “yes, he gave

[012-169] me his address.”

[012-170] “My name is Rupert Grant,” said that individual, with some pomp. “I

[012-171] have assisted the police on more than one occasion. I wonder whether you

[012-172] would tell me, as a special favour, what address?”

[012-173] The constable looked at him.

[012-174] “Yes,” he said slowly, “if you like. His address is: The Elms, Buxton

[012-175] Common, near Purley, Surrey.”

[012-176] “Thank you,” said Rupert, and ran home through the gathering night as

[012-177] fast as his legs could carry him, repeating the address to himself.

[012-178] Rupert Grant generally came down late in a rather lordly way to

[012-179] breakfast; he contrived, I don't know how, to achieve always the

[012-180] attitude of the indulged younger brother. Next morning, however, when

[012-181] Basil and I came down we found him ready and restless.

[012-182] “Well,” he said sharply to his brother almost before we sat down to the

[012-183] meal. “What do you think of your Drummond Keith now?”

[012-184] “What do I think of him?” inquired Basil slowly. “I don't think anything

[012-185] of him.”

[012-186] “I'm glad to hear it,” said Rupert, buttering his toast with an energy

[012-187] that was somewhat exultant. “I thought you'd come round to my view, but

[012-188] I own I was startled at your not seeing it from the beginning. The man

[012-189] is a translucent liar and knave.”

[012-190] “I think,” said Basil, in the same heavy monotone as before, “that I did

[012-191] not make myself clear. When I said that I thought nothing of him I meant

[012-192] grammatically what I said. I meant that I did not think about him; that

[012-193] he did not occupy my mind. You, however, seem to me to think a lot of

[012-194] him, since you think him a knave. I should say he was glaringly good

[012-195] myself.”
