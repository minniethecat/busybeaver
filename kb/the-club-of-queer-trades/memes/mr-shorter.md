# Mr Shorter

The Reverend **Ellis Shorter** is a character in G.K. Chesterton's *The Club of Queer Trades* (1905), appearing in the story "The Awful Reason of the Vicar's Visit" (ch008-ch009). A nervous, short-sighted clergyman of the Church of England, he seeks [[basil-grant|Basil Grant]]'s assistance with a mysterious and socially awkward situation -- only to discover that he has unwittingly become entangled in yet another queer trade. His predicament forms the central mystery of the collection's second adventure.

## The Vicar's Predicament

Shorter's story begins with a calling card: the narrator sees "a visiting card inscribed: 'Rev. Ellis Shorter'" (008-024), and the clergyman is soon admitted to Basil Grant's chambers. He is introduced as "the Reverend Mr Shorter" (008-053), immediately establishing his clerical identity and the social awkwardness that accompanies it.

The vicar is visibly agitated: "'Yes,' said the Reverend Mr Shorter, feverishly flapping his plaid shawl" (008-053) -- the detail of the plaid shawl suggesting both his modesty and his nervous energy. When asked directly "'Shorter?' I asked" (008-057), he struggles to explain himself. He insists on telling his story from the beginning: "'in at the beginning,' said Mr Shorter, 'and I will tell my'" (008-098) -- a narrative instinct that, in Chesterton's hands, becomes both comic and endearing.

## The Mistaken House

The crux of Shorter's predicament is a house he has entered by mistake -- or so he believes. His confusion deepens when the ladies of the house, far from ejecting him, seem to expect his presence and treat him as an honoured guest. The situation becomes increasingly surreal: he is being fed, entertained, and treated with a deference he cannot explain.

> Mr Shorter became indescribably flurried (009-031)

His short-sightedness compounds his confusion: "he peered short-sightedly" (009-007) at his surroundings, unable to recognise the faces or the room that have somehow claimed him. The vicar's agitation reaches its peak when a working-class man bursts in and confronts him directly: "'Look 'ere, Reverend Shorter, we ain't goin' to do" (009-090) -- a challenge that seems to come from an entirely different social world than the drawing-room in which Shorter finds himself.

> "Mr Shorter, it's like this. We've got to" (009-094)

The working man's exasperated address -- "'when I first saw it I said -- old Shorter. Those were my exact words -- old Shorter'" (009-104), (009-105) -- reveals a familiarity the vicar cannot account for, deepening the mystery.

## Basil Grant's Resolution

As always, [[basil-grant|Basil Grant]] unravels the mystery by recognising what others miss: Mr Shorter has not stumbled into a random house but has been claimed -- professionally -- by a queer trade. The ladies who detained him, the working man who addressed him so familiarly -- all are operatives of an agency whose business is the forcible prevention of rash actions. Mr Shorter, in some moment of crisis, had needed to be stopped from doing something disastrous, and the machinery of the queer trade had swung into motion around him without his knowledge.

## Character

Ellis Shorter is one of Chesterton's most sympathetic minor clerics -- a man whose genuine bewilderment is the proper response to an irrational situation. His nervousness is not weakness but sensibility: he knows, at some level, that the world has become stranger than his theological training prepared him for. His short-sightedness is both a physical fact and a metaphor -- he cannot see the queer trade operating around him because his frame of reference, like that of the constable and the physician, has no category for it.

The name "Shorter" is itself a gentle joke: the vicar's story is anything but short. His insistence on telling it "in at the beginning" and his convoluted narrative are part of Chesterton's broader satire of the way respectable people talk around the inexplicable rather than confronting it directly.
