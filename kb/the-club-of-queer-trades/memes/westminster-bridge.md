# Westminster Bridge

Westminster Bridge is a road-and-footbridge over the River Thames in central London, connecting [[westminster|Westminster]] on the west bank with Lambeth on the east. It appears in "The Eccentric Seclusion of the Old Lady" ([[ch016]]) as the first landmark on a cab journey undertaken by [[basil-grant|Basil Grant]] and the narrator.

## In the Stories

In [[ch016]], the cab route begins at Westminster Bridge:

> Basil scarcely said a word as we drove across Westminster Bridge, through [[trafalgar-square|Trafalgar Square]], along Piccadilly, and up the [[uxbridge-road|Uxbridge Road]].

Westminster Bridge is the starting point of this westward journey. Its selection as the first named landmark is significant — the bridge marks the boundary between the political and administrative heart of London (Westminster) and the wider city beyond. Basil's silence as they cross it creates a mood of anticipation; the narrator is being transported, both literally and figuratively, toward a revelation.

## Literary Context

Westminster Bridge holds a distinguished place in English literature. Wordsworth's famous sonnet "Composed upon Westminster Bridge, September 3, 1802" celebrates its view at dawn. Chesterton's mention, though briefer and less lyrical, participates in this tradition of London topography as literary material. Where Wordsworth saw the city "all bright and glittering in the smokeless air," Chesterton's Westminster Bridge is the gateway to a mysterious cab ride — fitting for a detective-story parody.
