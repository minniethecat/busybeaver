[013-001] “I sometimes think you talk [[paradox-in-fiction|paradox]] for its own sake,” said [[rupert-grant|Rupert]],

[013-002] breaking an egg with unnecessary sharpness. “What the deuce is the

[013-003] sense of it? Here's a man whose original position was, by our common

[013-004] agreement, dubious. He's a wanderer, a teller of tall tales, a man who

[013-005] doesn't conceal his acquaintance with all the blackest and bloodiest

[013-006] scenes on earth. We take the trouble to follow him to one of his

[013-007] appointments, and if ever two human beings were plotting together and

[013-008] lying to every one else, he and that impossible house-agent were doing

[013-009] it. We followed him home, and the very same night he is in the thick

[013-010] of a fatal, or nearly fatal, brawl, in which he is the only man armed.

[013-011] Really, if this is being glaringly good, I must confess that the glare

[013-012] does not dazzle me.”

[013-013] [[basil-grant|Basil]] was quite unmoved. “I admit his [[moral-philosophy|moral]] goodness is of a certain

[013-014] kind, a quaint, perhaps a casual kind. He is very fond of change and

[013-015] experiment. But all the points you so ingeniously make against him are

[013-016] mere coincidence or special pleading. It's true he didn't want to talk

[013-017] about his house business in front of us. No man would. It's true that he

[013-018] carries a sword-stick. Any man might. It's true he drew it in the shock

[013-019] of a street fight. Any man would. But there's nothing really dubious in

[013-020] all this. There's nothing to confirm--”

[013-021] As he spoke a knock came at the door.

[013-022] “If you please, sir,” said the landlady, with an alarmed air, “there's a

[013-023] policeman wants to see you.”

[013-024] “Show him in,” said Basil, amid the blank silence.

[013-025] [[the-reverend-vicar|The]] heavy, handsome constable who appeared at the door spoke almost as

[013-026] soon as he appeared there.

[013-027] “I think one of you gentlemen,” he said, curtly but respectfully, “was

[013-028] present at the affair in Copper Street last night, and drew my attention

[013-029] very strongly to a particular man.”

[013-030] Rupert half rose from his chair, with eyes like diamonds, but the

[013-031] constable went on calmly, referring to a paper.

[013-032] “A young man with grey hair. Had light grey clothes, very good, but torn

[013-033] in the struggle. Gave his name as Drummond Keith.”

[013-034] “This is amusing,” said Basil, laughing. “I was in the very act of

[013-035] clearing that poor officer's character of rather fanciful aspersions.

[013-036] What about him?”

[013-037] “Well, sir,” said the constable, “I took all the men's addresses and had

[013-038] them all watched. It wasn't serious enough to do more than that. All the

[013-039] other addresses are all right. But this man Keith gave a false address.

[013-040] The place doesn't exist.”

[013-041] The breakfast table was nearly flung over as Rupert sprang up, slapping

[013-042] both his thighs.

[013-043] “Well, by all that's good,” he cried. “This is a sign from heaven.”

[013-044] “It's certainly very extraordinary,” said Basil quietly, with knitted

[013-045] brows. “It's odd the fellow should have given a false address,

[013-046] considering he was perfectly innocent in the--”

[013-047] “Oh, you jolly old early Christian duffer,” cried Rupert, in a sort of

[013-048] rapture, “I don't wonder you couldn't be a judge. You think every one

[013-049] as good as yourself. Isn't the thing plain enough now? A doubtful

[013-050] acquaintance; rowdy stories, a most suspicious conversation, mean

[013-051] streets, a concealed knife, a man nearly killed, and, finally, a false

[013-052] address. That's what we call glaring goodness.”

[013-053] “It's certainly very extraordinary,” repeated Basil. And he strolled

[013-054] moodily about the room. Then he said: “You are quite sure, constable,

[013-055] that there's no mistake? You got the address right, and the police have

[013-056] really gone to it and found it was a fraud?”

[013-057] “It was very simple, sir,” said the policeman, chuckling. “The place

[013-058] he named was a well-known common quite near London, and our people were

[013-059] down there this morning before any of you were awake. And there's no

[013-060] such house. In fact, there are hardly any houses at all. Though it is

[013-061] so near London, it's a blank moor with hardly five trees on it, to

[013-062] say nothing of Christians. Oh, no, sir, the address was a fraud right

[013-063] enough. He was a clever rascal, and chose one of those scraps of lost

[013-064] England that people know nothing about. Nobody could say off-hand that

[013-065] there was not a particular house dropped somewhere about the heath. But

[013-066] as a fact, there isn't.”

[013-067] Basil's face during this sensible speech had been growing darker and

[013-068] darker with a sort of desperate sagacity. He was cornered almost for

[013-069] the first time since I had known him; and to tell the [[truth-and-fiction|truth]] I rather

[013-070] wondered at the almost childish obstinacy which kept him so close to his

[013-071] original prejudice in favour of the wildly questionable lieutenant. At

[013-072] length he said:

[013-073] “You really searched the common? And the address was really not known in

[013-074] the district--by the way, what was the address?”

[013-075] The constable selected one of his slips of paper and consulted it, but

[013-076] before he could speak Rupert Grant, who was leaning in the window in a

[013-077] perfect posture of the quiet and triumphant [[detective-in-fiction|detective]], struck in with

[013-078] the sharp and suave voice he loved so much to use.

[013-079] “Why, I can tell you that, Basil,” he said graciously as he idly plucked

[013-080] leaves from a plant in the window. “I took the precaution to get this

[013-081] man's address from the constable last night.”

[013-082] “And what was it?” asked his brother gruffly.

[013-083] “The constable will correct me if I am wrong,” said Rupert, looking

[013-084] sweetly at the ceiling. “It was: The Elms, Buxton Common, near Purley,

[013-085] Surrey.”

[013-086] “Right, sir,” said the policeman, laughing and folding up his papers.

[013-087] There was a silence, and the blue eyes of Basil looked blindly for a few

[013-088] seconds into the void. Then his head fell back in his chair so suddenly

[013-089] that I started up, thinking him ill. But before I could move further his

[013-090] lips had flown apart (I can use no other phrase) and a peal of gigantic

[013-091] laughter struck and shook the ceiling--laughter that shook the laughter,

[013-092] laughter redoubled, laughter incurable, laughter that could not stop.

[013-093] Two whole minutes afterwards it was still unended; Basil was ill with

[013-094] laughter; but still he laughed. The rest of us were by this time ill

[013-095] almost with terror.

[013-096] “Excuse me,” said the insane creature, getting at last to his feet.

[013-097] “I am awfully sorry. It is horribly rude. And stupid, too. And also

[013-098] unpractical, because we have not much time to lose if we're to get down

[013-099] to that place. The train service is confoundedly bad, as I happen to

[013-100] know. It's quite out of proportion to the comparatively small distance.”

[013-101] “Get down to that place?” I repeated blankly. “Get down to what place?”

[013-102] “I have forgotten its name,” said Basil vaguely, putting his hands in

[013-103] his pockets as he rose. “Something Common near Purley. Has any one got a

[013-104] timetable?”

[013-105] “You don't seriously mean,” cried Rupert, who had been staring in a sort

[013-106] of confusion of emotions. “You don't mean that you want to go to Buxton

[013-107] Common, do you? You can't mean that!”

[013-108] “Why shouldn't I go to Buxton Common?” asked Basil, smiling.

[013-109] “Why should you?” said his brother, catching hold again restlessly of

[013-110] the plant in the window and staring at the speaker.

[013-111] “To find our friend, the lieutenant, of course,” said Basil Grant. “I

[013-112] thought you wanted to find him?”

[013-113] Rupert broke a branch brutally from the plant and flung it impatiently

[013-114] on the floor. “And in order to find him,” he said, “you suggest the

[013-115] admirable expedient of going to the only place on the habitable earth

[013-116] where we know he can't be.”

[013-117] The constable and I could not avoid breaking into a kind of assenting

[013-118] laugh, and Rupert, who had family eloquence, was encouraged to go on

[013-119] with a reiterated gesture:

[013-120] “He may be in Buckingham Palace; he may be sitting astride the cross of

[013-121] St Paul's; he may be in jail (which I think most likely); he may be

[013-122] in the Great Wheel; he may be in my pantry; he may be in your store

[013-123] cupboard; but out of all the innumerable points of space, there is only

[013-124] one where he has just been systematically looked for and where we know

[013-125] that he is not to be found--and that, if I understand you rightly, is

[013-126] where you want us to go.”

[013-127] “Exactly,” said Basil calmly, getting into his great-coat; “I thought

[013-128] you might care to accompany me. If not, of course, make yourselves jolly

[013-129] here till I come back.”

[013-130] It is our nature always to follow vanishing things and value them if

[013-131] they really show a resolution to depart. We all followed Basil, and I

[013-132] cannot say why, except that he was a vanishing thing, that he vanished

[013-133] decisively with his great-coat and his stick. Rupert ran after him with

[013-134] a considerable flurry of rationality.

[013-135] “My dear chap,” he cried, “do you really mean that you see any good in

[013-136] going down to this ridiculous scrub, where there is nothing but beaten

[013-137] tracks and a few twisted trees, simply because it was the first place

[013-138] that came into a rowdy lieutenant's head when he wanted to give a lying

[013-139] reference in a scrape?”

[013-140] “Yes,” said Basil, taking out his watch, “and, what's worse, we've lost

[013-141] the train.”

[013-142] He paused a moment and then added: “As a matter of fact, I think we may

[013-143] just as well go down later in the day. I have some writing to do, and

[013-144] I think you told me, Rupert, that you thought of going to the Dulwich

[013-145] Gallery. I was rather too impetuous. Very likely he wouldn't be in. But

[013-146] if we get down by the 5.15, which gets to Purley about 6, I expect we

[013-147] shall just catch him.”

[013-148] “Catch him!” cried his brother, in a kind of final anger. “I wish we

[013-149] could. Where the deuce shall we catch him now?”

[013-150] “I keep forgetting the name of the common,” said Basil, as he buttoned

[013-151] up his coat. “The Elms--what is it? Buxton Common, near Purley. That's

[013-152] where we shall find him.”

[013-153] “But there is no such place,” groaned Rupert; but he followed his

[013-154] brother downstairs.

[013-155] We all followed him. We snatched our hats from the hat-stand and our

[013-156] sticks from the umbrella-stand; and why we followed him we did not and

[013-157] do not know. But we always followed him, whatever was the meaning of the

[013-158] fact, whatever was the nature of his mastery. And the strange thing was

[013-159] that we followed him the more completely the more nonsensical appeared

[013-160] the thing which he said. At bottom, I believe, if he had risen from

[013-161] our breakfast table and said: “I am going to find the Holy Pig with Ten

[013-162] Tails,” we should have followed him to the end of the world.

[013-163] I don't know whether this mystical feeling of mine about Basil on this

[013-164] occasion has got any of the dark and cloudy colour, so to speak, of the

[013-165] strange journey that we made the same evening. It was already very dense

[013-166] twilight when we struck southward from Purley. Suburbs and things on the

[013-167] London border may be, in most cases, commonplace and comfortable. But if

[013-168] ever by any chance they really are empty solitudes they are to the

[013-169] human spirit more desolate and dehumanized than any Yorkshire moors or

[013-170] Highland hills, because the suddenness with which the traveller drops

[013-171] into that silence has something about it as of evil elf-land. It

[013-172] seems to be one of the ragged suburbs of the cosmos half-forgotten by

[013-173] God--such a place was Buxton Common, near Purley.

[013-174] There was certainly a sort of grey futility in the landscape itself.

[013-175] But it was enormously increased by the sense of grey futility in our

[013-176] expedition. The tracts of grey turf looked useless, the occasional

[013-177] wind-stricken trees looked useless, but we, the human beings, more

[013-178] useless than the hopeless turf or the idle trees. We were maniacs akin

[013-179] to the foolish landscape, for we were come to chase the wild goose which

[013-180] has led men and left men in bogs from the beginning. We were three dazed

[013-181] men under the captaincy of a madman going to look for a man whom we knew

[013-182] was not there in a house that had no existence. A livid sunset seemed to

[013-183] look at us with a sort of sickly smile before it died.
