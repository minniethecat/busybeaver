[002-001] We shook our heads gently. Bit by bit, and mainly by the seemingly

[002-002] sleepy assistance of [[basil-grant|Basil Grant]], we pieced together the [[major-brown|Major]]'s

[002-003] fragmentary, but excited narration. It would be infamous to submit the

[002-004] reader to what we endured; therefore I will tell the [[fiction-as-truth|story]] of Major

[002-005] Brown in my own words. But the reader must imagine the scene. [[the-reverend-vicar|The]] eyes

[002-006] of Basil closed as in a trance, after his habit, and the eyes of [[rupert-grant|Rupert]]

[002-007] and myself getting rounder and rounder as we listened to one of the

[002-008] most astounding stories in the world, from the lips of the little man in

[002-009] black, sitting bolt upright in his chair and talking like a telegram.

[002-010] Major Brown was, I have said, a successful soldier, but by no means an

[002-011] enthusiastic one. So far from regretting his retirement on half-pay,

[002-012] it was with delight that he took a small neat villa, very like a doll's

[002-013] house, and devoted the rest of his life to pansies and weak tea. The

[002-014] thought that battles were over when he had once hung up his sword in

[002-015] the little front hall (along with two patent stew-pots and a bad

[002-016] water-colour), and betaken himself instead to wielding the rake in his

[002-017] little sunlit garden, was to him like having come into a harbour in

[002-018] heaven. He was Dutch-like and precise in his taste in gardening, and

[002-019] had, perhaps, some tendency to drill his flowers like soldiers. He was

[002-020] one of those men who are capable of putting four umbrellas in the stand

[002-021] rather than three, so that two may lean one way and two another; he saw

[002-022] life like a pattern in a freehand drawing-book. And assuredly he would

[002-023] not have believed, or even understood, any one who had told him that

[002-024] within a few yards of his brick paradise he was destined to be caught

[002-025] in a whirlpool of incredible [[adventure-in-fiction|adventure]], such as he had never seen or

[002-026] dreamed of in the horrible jungle, or the heat of battle.

[002-027] One certain bright and windy afternoon, the Major, attired in his usual

[002-028] faultless manner, had set out for his usual constitutional. In crossing

[002-029] from one great residential thoroughfare to another, he happened to pass

[002-030] along one of those aimless-looking lanes which lie along the back-garden

[002-031] walls of a row of mansions, and which in their empty and discoloured

[002-032] appearance give one an odd sensation as of being behind the scenes of a

[002-033] theatre. But mean and sulky as the scene might be in the eyes of most of

[002-034] us, it was not altogether so in the Major's, for along the coarse

[002-035] gravel footway was coming a thing which was to him what the passing of

[002-036] a religious procession is to a devout person. A large, heavy man, with

[002-037] fish-blue eyes and a ring of irradiating red beard, was pushing before

[002-038] him a barrow, which was ablaze with incomparable flowers. There were

[002-039] splendid specimens of almost every order, but the Major's own favourite

[002-040] pansies predominated. The Major stopped and fell into conversation, and

[002-041] then into bargaining. He treated the man after the manner of collectors

[002-042] and other mad men, that is to say, he carefully and with a sort of

[002-043] anguish selected the best roots from the less excellent, praised some,

[002-044] disparaged others, made a subtle scale ranging from a thrilling worth

[002-045] and rarity to a degraded insignificance, and then bought them all. The

[002-046] man was just pushing off his barrow when he stopped and came close to

[002-047] the Major.

[002-048] “I'll tell you what, sir,” he said. “If you're interested in them

[002-049] things, you just get on to that wall.”

[002-050] “On the wall!” cried the scandalised Major, whose conventional soul

[002-051] quailed within him at the thought of such fantastic trespass.

[002-052] “Finest show of yellow pansies in England in that there garden, sir,”

[002-053] hissed the tempter. “I'll help you up, sir.”

[002-054] How it happened no one will ever know but that positive enthusiasm of

[002-055] the Major's life triumphed over all its negative traditions, and with

[002-056] an easy leap and swing that showed that he was in no need of physical

[002-057] assistance, he stood on the wall at the end of the strange garden. The

[002-058] second after, the flapping of the frock-coat at his knees made him feel

[002-059] inexpressibly a fool. But the next instant all such trifling sentiments

[002-060] were swallowed up by the most appalling shock of surprise the old

[002-061] soldier had ever felt in all his bold and wandering existence. His eyes

[002-062] fell upon the garden, and there across a large bed in the centre of the

[002-063] lawn was a vast pattern of pansies; they were splendid flowers, but for

[002-064] once it was not their horticultural aspects that Major Brown beheld, for

[002-065] the pansies were arranged in gigantic capital letters so as to form the

[002-066] sentence:

[002-067] DEATH TO MAJOR BROWN

[002-068] A kindly looking old man, with white whiskers, was watering them. Brown

[002-069] looked sharply back at the road behind him; the man with the barrow had

[002-070] suddenly vanished. Then he looked again at the lawn with its incredible

[002-071] inscription. Another man might have thought he had gone mad, but Brown

[002-072] did not. When [[romance-as-adventure|romantic]] ladies gushed over his V.C. and his military

[002-073] exploits, he sometimes felt himself to be a painfully prosaic person,

[002-074] but by the same token he knew he was incurably sane. Another man, again,

[002-075] might have thought himself a victim of a passing practical joke,

[002-076] but Brown could not easily believe this. He knew from his own quaint

[002-077] learning that the garden arrangement was an elaborate and expensive one;

[002-078] he thought it extravagantly improbable that any one would pour out money

[002-079] like water for a joke against him. Having no explanation whatever to

[002-080] offer, he admitted the fact to himself, like a clear-headed man, and

[002-081] waited as he would have done in the presence of a man with six legs.

[002-082] At this moment the stout old man with white whiskers looked up, and

[002-083] the watering can fell from his hand, shooting a swirl of water down the

[002-084] gravel path.

[002-085] “Who on earth are you?” he gasped, trembling violently.

[002-086] “I am Major Brown,” said that individual, who was always cool in the

[002-087] hour of action.

[002-088] The old man gaped helplessly like some monstrous fish. At last he

[002-089] stammered wildly, “Come down--come down here!”

[002-090] “At your service,” said the Major, and alighted at a bound on the grass

[002-091] beside him, without disarranging his silk hat.

[002-092] The old man turned his broad back and set off at a sort of waddling run

[002-093] towards the house, followed with swift steps by the Major. His guide

[002-094] led him through the back passages of a gloomy, but gorgeously appointed

[002-095] house, until they reached the door of the front room. Then the old man

[002-096] turned with a face of apoplectic terror dimly showing in the twilight.

[002-097] “For heaven's sake,” he said, “don't mention jackals.”

[002-098] Then he threw open the door, releasing a burst of red lamplight, and ran

[002-099] downstairs with a clatter.

[002-100] The Major stepped into a rich, glowing room, full of red copper, and

[002-101] peacock and purple hangings, hat in hand. He had the finest manners in

[002-102] the world, and, though mystified, was not in the least embarrassed to

[002-103] see that the only occupant was a lady, sitting by the window, looking

[002-104] out.

[002-105] “Madam,” he said, bowing simply, “I am Major Brown.”

[002-106] “Sit down,” said the lady; but she did not turn her head.

[002-107] She was a graceful, green-clad figure, with fiery red hair and a flavour

[002-108] of Bedford Park. “You have come, I suppose,” she said mournfully, “to

[002-109] tax me about the hateful title-deeds.”

[002-110] “I have come, madam,” he said, “to know what is the matter. To know why

[002-111] my name is written across your garden. Not amicably either.”

[002-112] He spoke grimly, for the thing had hit him. It is impossible to describe

[002-113] the effect produced on the mind by that quiet and sunny garden scene,

[002-114] the frame for a stunning and brutal personality. The evening air was

[002-115] still, and the grass was golden in the place where the little flowers he

[002-116] studied cried to heaven for his blood.

[002-117] “You know I must not turn round,” said the lady; “every afternoon till

[002-118] the stroke of six I must keep my face turned to the street.”

[002-119] Some queer and unusual inspiration made the prosaic soldier resolute to

[002-120] accept these outrageous riddles without surprise.

[002-121] “It is almost six,” he said; and even as he spoke the barbaric copper

[002-122] clock upon the wall clanged the first stroke of the hour. At the sixth

[002-123] the lady sprang up and turned on the Major one of the queerest and

[002-124] yet most attractive faces he had ever seen in his life; open, and yet

[002-125] tantalising, the face of an elf.

[002-126] “That makes the third year I have waited,” she cried. “This is an

[002-127] anniversary. The waiting almost makes one wish the frightful thing would

[002-128] happen once and for all.”

[002-129] And even as she spoke, a sudden rending cry broke the stillness. From

[002-130] low down on the pavement of the dim street (it was already twilight) a

[002-131] voice cried out with a raucous and merciless distinctness:

[002-132] “Major Brown, Major Brown, where does the jackal dwell?”

[002-133] Brown was decisive and silent in action. He strode to the front door

[002-134] and looked out. There was no sign of life in the blue gloaming of the

[002-135] street, where one or two lamps were beginning to light their lemon

[002-136] sparks. On returning, he found the lady in green trembling.

[002-137] “It is the end,” she cried, with shaking lips; “it may be death for both

[002-138] of us. Whenever--”

[002-139] But even as she spoke her speech was cloven by another hoarse

[002-140] proclamation from the dark street, again horribly articulate.

[002-141] “Major Brown, Major Brown, how did the jackal die?”

[002-142] Brown dashed out of the door and down the steps, but again he was

[002-143] frustrated; there was no figure in sight, and the street was far too

[002-144] long and empty for the shouter to have run away. Even the rational

[002-145] Major was a little shaken as he returned in a certain time to the

[002-146] drawing-room. Scarcely had he done so than the terrific voice came:

[002-147] “Major Brown, Major Brown, where did--”

[002-148] Brown was in the street almost at a bound, and he was in time--in

[002-149] time to see something which at first glance froze the blood. The cries

[002-150] appeared to come from a decapitated head resting on the pavement.

[002-151] The next moment the pale Major understood. It was the head of a man

[002-152] thrust through the coal-hole in the street. The next moment, again,

[002-153] it had vanished, and Major Brown turned to the lady. “Where's your

[002-154] coal-cellar?” he said, and stepped out into the passage.

[002-155] She looked at him with wild grey eyes. “You will not go down,” she

[002-156] cried, “alone, into the dark hole, with that beast?”

[002-157] “Is this the way?” replied Brown, and descended the kitchen stairs three

[002-158] at a time. He flung open the door of a black cavity and stepped in,

[002-159] feeling in his pocket for matches. As his right hand was thus occupied,

[002-160] a pair of great slimy hands came out of the darkness, hands clearly

[002-161] belonging to a man of gigantic stature, and seized him by the back of

[002-162] the head. They forced him down, down in the suffocating darkness, a

[002-163] brutal image of destiny. But the Major's head, though upside down, was

[002-164] perfectly clear and intellectual. He gave quietly under the pressure

[002-165] until he had slid down almost to his hands and knees. Then finding the

[002-166] knees of the invisible monster within a foot of him, he simply put out

[002-167] one of his long, bony, and skilful hands, and gripping the leg by a

[002-168] muscle pulled it off the ground and laid the huge living man, with a

[002-169] crash, along the floor. He strove to rise, but Brown was on top like a

[002-170] cat. They rolled over and over. Big as the man was, he had evidently now

[002-171] no desire but to escape; he made sprawls hither and thither to get past

[002-172] the Major to the door, but that tenacious person had him hard by the

[002-173] coat collar and hung with the other hand to a beam. At length there came

[002-174] a strain in holding back this human bull, a strain under which Brown

[002-175] expected his hand to rend and part from the arm. But something else

[002-176] rent and parted; and the dim fat figure of the giant vanished out of the

[002-177] cellar, leaving the torn coat in the Major's hand; the only fruit of his

[002-178] adventure and the only clue to the [[mystery-in-fiction|mystery]]. For when he went up and out

[002-179] at the front door, the lady, the rich hangings, and the whole equipment

[002-180] of the house had disappeared. It had only bare boards and whitewashed

[002-181] walls.
