# Mr Wimpole

**Mr Wimpole** is a character in G.K. Chesterton's *The Club of Queer Trades* (1905), appearing in "The Painful Fall of a Great Reputation" (ch006-ch007). His identity is the subject of considerable mystery and comic debate among the characters -- a deliberate ambiguity that is characteristic of Chesterton's technique of withholding the true nature of a queer trade until the story's climax. Wimpole's very name becomes a kind of floating signifier, attached to a person whose function no one can quite name.

## The Mystery of Identity

Wimpole is introduced with dramatic solemnity. [[lord-beaumont|Lord Beaumont]] declares: "'I have Wimpole here'" (006-071). The immediate response -- "'And who is Wimpole?'" (006-072) -- is the question the entire gathering cannot answer. What follows is a cascade of increasingly absurd comparisons as the company struggles to categorise this elusive figure:

> "'Wimpole? Who was Shakespeare?'" (006-075)

The comparison to Shakespeare is simultaneously flattering and absurd -- suggesting greatness while evading definition. The debate escalates: someone invokes "Mary Queen of Scots. But as to who Wimpole is --" (006-078), and the sentence trails off into unresolved mystery. Lord Beaumont can only cry "'Wimpole!'" (006-080) in a tone that conveys urgency without explanation.

The narrator, struggling to make sense of the evening's events, later offers his own "realistic account of the history of Wimpole which he had given. Still, I" (007-031) -- an attempt at rationalisation that, like all attempts to explain the queer trades in conventional terms, falls short.

## Wimpole in Performance

When Wimpole finally speaks, he does so as a performer. [[miss-beaumont|Muriel Beaumont]] addresses him directly: "'What I can't understand, Mr Wimpole,' said Muriel Beaumont eagerly" (006-154) -- her eagerness suggesting both fascination and frustration with his opacity.

Wimpole responds with theatrical authority: "'keep your countenance,' cried Mr Wimpole, with an air of" (006-161) -- his command to maintain composure delivered in a tone that is itself a performance. The fragment "rapturously listening to Wimpole" (006-172) captures the hold he exerts over his audience, however little they understand what they are witnessing.

Later, Basil Grant turns to the narrator with a characteristic challenge: "'common sense. What do you make of Wimpole?'" (006-182). The question is rhetorical -- common sense, Grant implies, is precisely the wrong tool for understanding someone like Wimpole.

## The Queer Trade Revealed

Without spoiling the story's resolution, it can be said that Wimpole is an operative of a queer trade -- one whose very nature requires him to be what he appears to be: a man of ambiguous identity, a performer whose performance and self are indistinguishable. The comparisons to Shakespeare and Mary Queen of Scots, absurd as they seem, are not entirely wrong: Wimpole is, in his way, both a great performer and a figure of contested identity.

## Character

Wimpole embodies one of Chesterton's recurring insights: that identity itself can be a profession. In a world where the queer trades have systematised the inexplicable, a man whose identity cannot be pinned down is not a fraud but a professional -- someone whose business is the management of appearances. His "air of" authority (006-161) is genuine authority; it is simply authority in a domain that conventional society has no name for.

The name "Wimpole" -- evocative of "wimple," a garment that conceals -- is itself suggestive of concealment and revelation, of something worn that both hides and signals identity.
