# Mr. Bingham

Mr. Bingham is a character in "The Eccentric Seclusion of the Old Lady," the sixth and final story of *The Club of Queer Trades*. He is a family member — or perhaps a family representative — who approaches the authorities for help with the baffling behaviour of an elderly gentleman who will not speak but merely dances (018-001).

## Role / Identity

Bingham appears at the beginning of the story in a state of desperation. The old man — later revealed as part of a queer trade — has been dancing continuously without speaking, and Bingham, along with others, is at his wits' end trying to understand why (018-001).

He is described as having a "sensitive face" that takes on "an extra expression of distress" as the mystery deepens (018-023). His bewilderment is the reader's bewilderment: the situation is genuinely inexplicable, and Bingham's reasonable desire for an explanation is met with [[basil-grant|Basil Grant]]'s characteristically unorthodox response.

## Appearances

Bingham appears in Section 18 ("The Eccentric Seclusion of the Old Lady") of the collection. He is present throughout the key scenes in which Basil Grant demonstrates his peculiar method — first by cavorting absurdly (mimicking the dancing man) and then by explaining his reasoning (018-048).

## Relationships

At the story's conclusion, Bingham holds out his hand cordially to Grant, acknowledging the correctness of Basil's seemingly insane intervention (018-048). He interacts with [[basil-grant|Basil Grant]] and [[dr-colman|Dr. Colman]], the physician also called in to examine the dancing man.

## Significance

Bingham represents the sane, ordinary world confronted with something it cannot categorize. His first cry — "But why does he dance? Why doesn't he answer us?" (018-001) — is the question the story exists to answer. His role is that of the straight man, the foil against which Basil Grant's intuitive genius shines. He is one of several characters in the collection who demonstrate that conventional rationality cannot solve Chestertonian mysteries — only imaginative sympathy can.
