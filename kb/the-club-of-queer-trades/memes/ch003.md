[003-001] “[[the-reverend-vicar|The]] lady was in the conspiracy, of course,” said [[rupert-grant|Rupert]], nodding. [[major-brown|Major]]

[003-002] Brown turned brick red. “I beg your pardon,” he said, “I think not.”

[003-003] Rupert raised his eyebrows and looked at him for a moment, but said

[003-004] nothing. When next he spoke he asked:

[003-005] “Was there anything in the pockets of the coat?”

[003-006] “There was sevenpence halfpenny in coppers and a threepenny-bit,” said

[003-007] the Major carefully; “there was a cigarette-holder, a piece of string,

[003-008] and this letter,” and he laid it on the table. It ran as follows:

[003-009] Dear Mr Plover,

[003-010] I am annoyed to hear that some delay has occurred in the arrangements re

[003-011] Major Brown. Please see that he is attacked as per arrangement tomorrow.

[003-012] The coal-cellar, of course.

[003-013] Yours faithfully, [[northover|P. G. Northover]].

[003-014] Rupert [[basil-grant|Grant]] was leaning forward listening with hawk-like eyes. He cut

[003-015] in:

[003-016] “Is it dated from anywhere?”

[003-017] “No--oh, yes!” replied Brown, glancing upon the paper; “14 Tanner's

[003-018] Court, North--”

[003-019] Rupert sprang up and struck his hands together.

[003-020] “Then why are we hanging here? Let's get along. Basil, lend me your

[003-021] revolver.”

[003-022] Basil was staring into the embers like a man in a trance; and it was

[003-023] some time before he answered:

[003-024] “I don't think you'll need it.”

[003-025] “Perhaps not,” said Rupert, getting into his fur coat. “One never knows.

[003-026] But going down a dark court to see criminals--”

[003-027] “Do you think they are criminals?” asked his brother.

[003-028] Rupert laughed stoutly. “Giving orders to a subordinate to strangle a

[003-029] harmless stranger in a coal-cellar may strike you as a very blameless

[003-030] experiment, but--”

[003-031] “Do you think they wanted to strangle the Major?” asked Basil, in the

[003-032] same distant and monotonous voice.

[003-033] “My dear fellow, you've been asleep. Look at the letter.”

[003-034] “I am looking at the letter,” said the mad judge calmly; though, as a

[003-035] matter of fact, he was looking at the fire. “I don't think it's the sort

[003-036] of letter one [[crime-in-fiction|criminal]] would write to another.”

[003-037] “My dear boy, you are glorious,” cried Rupert, turning round, with

[003-038] laughter in his blue bright eyes. “Your methods amaze me. Why, there

[003-039] is the letter. It is written, and it does give orders for a crime. You

[003-040] might as well say that the Nelson Column was not at all the sort of

[003-041] thing that was likely to be set up in Trafalgar Square.”

[003-042] Basil Grant shook all over with a sort of silent laughter, but did not

[003-043] otherwise move.

[003-044] “That's rather good,” he said; “but, of course, logic like that's not

[003-045] what is really wanted. It's a question of spiritual atmosphere. It's not

[003-046] a criminal letter.”

[003-047] “It is. It's a matter of fact,” cried the other in an agony of

[003-048] reasonableness.

[003-049] “Facts,” murmured Basil, like one mentioning some strange, far-off

[003-050] animals, “how facts obscure the [[truth-and-fiction|truth]]. I may be silly--in fact, I'm

[003-051] off my head--but I never could believe in that man--what's his name,

[003-052] in those capital stories?--Sherlock Holmes. Every detail points to

[003-053] something, certainly; but generally to the wrong thing. Facts point in

[003-054] all directions, it seems to me, like the thousands of twigs on a tree.

[003-055] It's only the life of the tree that has unity and goes up--only the

[003-056] green blood that springs, like a fountain, at the stars.”

[003-057] “But what the deuce else can the letter be but criminal?”

[003-058] “We have eternity to stretch our legs in,” replied the mystic. “It can

[003-059] be an infinity of things. I haven't seen any of them--I've only seen the

[003-060] letter. I look at that, and say it's not criminal.”

[003-061] “Then what's the origin of it?”

[003-062] “I haven't the vaguest idea.”

[003-063] “Then why don't you accept the ordinary explanation?”

[003-064] Basil continued for a little to glare at the coals, and seemed

[003-065] collecting his thoughts in a humble and even painful way. Then he said:

[003-066] “Suppose you went out into the moonlight. Suppose you passed through

[003-067] silent, silvery streets and squares until you came into an open and

[003-068] deserted space, set with a few monuments, and you beheld one dressed as

[003-069] a ballet girl dancing in the argent glimmer. And suppose you looked, and

[003-070] saw it was a man disguised. And suppose you looked again, and saw it was

[003-071] Lord Kitchener. What would you think?”

[003-072] He paused a moment, and went on:

[003-073] “You could not adopt the ordinary explanation. The ordinary explanation

[003-074] of putting on singular clothes is that you look nice in them; you would

[003-075] not think that Lord Kitchener dressed up like a ballet girl out of

[003-076] ordinary personal vanity. You would think it much more likely that

[003-077] he inherited a dancing [[madness-and-sanity|madness]] from a great grandmother; or had been

[003-078] hypnotised at a seance; or threatened by a [[secret-in-fiction|secret society]] with death if

[003-079] he refused the ordeal. With Baden-Powell, say, it might be a bet--but

[003-080] not with Kitchener. I should know all that, because in my public days

[003-081] I knew him quite well. So I know that letter quite well, and criminals

[003-082] quite well. It's not a criminal's letter. It's all atmospheres.” And he

[003-083] closed his eyes and passed his hand over his forehead.

[003-084] Rupert and the Major were regarding him with a mixture of respect and

[003-085] pity. The former said,

[003-086] “Well, I'm going, anyhow, and shall continue to think--until your

[003-087] [[mystery-in-fiction|spiritual mystery]] turns up--that a man who sends a note recommending a

[003-088] crime, that is, actually a crime that is actually carried out, at

[003-089] least tentatively, is, in all probability, a little casual in his [[moral-philosophy|moral]]

[003-090] tastes. Can I have that revolver?”

[003-091] “Certainly,” said Basil, getting up. “But I am coming with you.” And he

[003-092] flung an old cape or cloak round him, and took a sword-stick from the

[003-093] corner.

[003-094] “You!” said Rupert, with some surprise, “you scarcely ever leave your

[003-095] hole to look at anything on the face of the earth.”

[003-096] Basil fitted on a formidable old white hat.

[003-097] “I scarcely ever,” he said, with an unconscious and colossal arrogance,

[003-098] “hear of anything on the face of the earth that I do not understand at

[003-099] once, without going to see it.”

[003-100] And he led the way out into the purple night.

[003-101] We four swung along the flaring [[lambeth|Lambeth]] streets, across Westminster

[003-102] Bridge, and along the Embankment in the direction of that part of Fleet

[003-103] Street which contained [[tanners-court-fleet-street|Tanner's Court]]. The erect, black figure of Major

[003-104] Brown, seen from behind, was a quaint contrast to the hound-like stoop

[003-105] and flapping mantle of young Rupert Grant, who adopted, with childlike

[003-106] delight, all the dramatic poses of the [[detective-in-fiction|detective]] of [[fiction-as-truth|fiction]]. The finest

[003-107] among his many fine qualities was his boyish appetite for the colour and

[003-108] poetry of London. Basil, who walked behind, with his face turned blindly

[003-109] to the stars, had the look of a somnambulist.

[003-110] Rupert paused at the corner of Tanner's Court, with a quiver of delight

[003-111] at danger, and gripped Basil's revolver in his great-coat pocket.

[003-112] “Shall we go in now?” he asked.

[003-113] “Not get police?” asked Major Brown, glancing sharply up and down the

[003-114] street.

[003-115] “I am not sure,” answered Rupert, knitting his brows. “Of course, it's

[003-116] quite clear, the thing's all crooked. But there are three of us, and--”

[003-117] “I shouldn't get the police,” said Basil in a queer voice. Rupert

[003-118] glanced at him and stared hard.

[003-119] “Basil,” he cried, “you're trembling. What's the matter--are you

[003-120] afraid?”

[003-121] “Cold, perhaps,” said the Major, eyeing him. There was no doubt that he

[003-122] was shaking.

[003-123] At last, after a few moments' scrutiny, Rupert broke into a curse.

[003-124] “You're laughing,” he cried. “I know that confounded, silent, shaky

[003-125] laugh of yours. What the deuce is the amusement, Basil? Here we are, all

[003-126] three of us, within a yard of a den of ruffians--”

[003-127] “But I shouldn't call the police,” said Basil. “We four heroes are quite

[003-128] equal to a host,” and he continued to quake with his mysterious mirth.

[003-129] Rupert turned with impatience and strode swiftly down the court, the

[003-130] rest of us following. When he reached the door of No. 14 he turned

[003-131] abruptly, the revolver glittering in his hand.

[003-132] “Stand close,” he said in the voice of a commander. “The scoundrel may

[003-133] be attempting an escape at this moment. We must fling open the door and

[003-134] rush in.”

[003-135] The four of us cowered instantly under the archway, rigid, except for

[003-136] the old judge and his convulsion of merriment.

[003-137] “Now,” hissed Rupert Grant, turning his pale face and burning eyes

[003-138] suddenly over his shoulder, “when I say 'Four', follow me with a rush.

[003-139] If I say 'Hold him', pin the fellows down, whoever they are. If I say

[003-140] 'Stop', stop. I shall say that if there are more than three. If

[003-141] they attack us I shall empty my revolver on them. Basil, have your

[003-142] sword-stick ready. Now--one, two, three, four!”

[003-143] With the sound of the word the door burst open, and we fell into the

[003-144] room like an invasion, only to stop dead.

[003-145] The room, which was an ordinary and neatly appointed office, appeared,

[003-146] at the first glance, to be empty. But on a second and more careful

[003-147] glance, we saw seated behind a very large desk with pigeonholes and

[003-148] drawers of bewildering multiplicity, a small man with a black waxed

[003-149] moustache, and the air of a very average clerk, writing hard. He looked

[003-150] up as we came to a standstill.

[003-151] “Did you knock?” he asked pleasantly. “I am sorry if I did not hear.

[003-152] What can I do for you?”

[003-153] There was a doubtful pause, and then, by general consent, the Major

[003-154] himself, the victim of the outrage, stepped forward.

[003-155] The letter was in his hand, and he looked unusually grim.

[003-156] “Is your name P. G. Northover?” he asked.

[003-157] “That is my name,” replied the other, smiling.

[003-158] “I think,” said Major Brown, with an increase in the dark glow of his

[003-159] face, “that this letter was written by you.” And with a loud clap he

[003-160] struck open the letter on the desk with his clenched fist. The man

[003-161] called Northover looked at it with unaffected interest and merely

[003-162] nodded.

[003-163] “Well, sir,” said the Major, breathing hard, “what about that?”

[003-164] “What about it, precisely,” said the man with the moustache.

[003-165] “I am Major Brown,” said that gentleman sternly.

[003-166] Northover bowed. “Pleased to meet you, sir. What have you to say to me?”

[003-167] “Say!” cried the Major, loosing a sudden tempest; “why, I want this

[003-168] confounded thing settled. I want--”

[003-169] “Certainly, sir,” said Northover, jumping up with a slight elevation of

[003-170] the eyebrows. “Will you take a chair for a moment.” And he pressed

[003-171] an electric bell just above him, which thrilled and tinkled in a room

[003-172] beyond. The Major put his hand on the back of the chair offered him, but

[003-173] stood chafing and beating the floor with his polished boot.

[003-174] The next moment an inner glass door was opened, and a fair, weedy, young

[003-175] man, in a frock-coat, entered from within.

[003-176] “Mr Hopson,” said Northover, “this is Major Brown. Will you please

[003-177] finish that thing for him I gave you this morning and bring it in?”

[003-178] “Yes, sir,” said Mr Hopson, and vanished like lightning.

[003-179] “You will excuse me, gentlemen,” said the egregious Northover, with his

[003-180] radiant smile, “if I continue to [[profession-and-trade|work]] until Mr Hopson is ready. I have

[003-181] some books that must be cleared up before I get away on my holiday

[003-182] tomorrow. And we all like a whiff of the country, don't we? Ha! ha!”

[003-183] The criminal took up his pen with a childlike laugh, and a silence

[003-184] ensued; a placid and busy silence on the part of Mr P. G. Northover; a

[003-185] raging silence on the part of everybody else.

[003-186] At length the scratching of Northover's pen in the stillness was mingled

[003-187] with a knock at the door, almost simultaneous with the turning of the

[003-188] handle, and Mr Hopson came in again with the same silent rapidity,

[003-189] placed a paper before his principal, and disappeared again.

[003-190] The man at the desk pulled and twisted his spiky moustache for a few

[003-191] moments as he ran his eye up and down the paper presented to him.

[003-192] He took up his pen, with a slight, instantaneous frown, and altered

[003-193] something, muttering--“Careless.” Then he read it again with the same

[003-194] impenetrable reflectiveness, and finally handed it to the frantic Brown,

[003-195] whose hand was beating the devil's tattoo on the back of the chair.
