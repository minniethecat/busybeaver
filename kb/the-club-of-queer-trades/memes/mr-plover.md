# Mr. Plover

**Mr. Plover** is an employee of the [[adventure-and-romance-agency|Adventure and Romance Agency, Limited]], appearing in the first story of G.K. Chesterton's *The Club of Queer Trades*. His role is revealed through the agency's business correspondence and financial records, which expose the machinery behind a manufactured adventure.

## The Letter from Northover

Mr. Plover is first mentioned in a letter discovered by Major Brown among the contents of his coat pocket — a letter that proves pivotal to unraveling the mystery of the Major's strange experiences. The letter, from the agency's proprietor P.G. Northover, reads: "Dear Mr Plover, I am annoyed to hear that some delay has occurred in the arrangements re Major Brown. Please see that he is attacked as per arrangement tomorrow. The coal-cellar, of course. Yours faithfully, P. G. Northover" (003-009).

The letter's matter-of-fact tone — treating an arranged assault as a routine business matter — is Chesterton's comic device for revealing the central paradox of the Adventure and Romance Agency: that adventure, the supposed antithesis of bureaucratic order, is here produced through precisely the administrative machinery it pretends to escape.

## In the Agency's Accounts

Mr. Plover appears a second time in the detailed financial statement that Northover presents to Major Brown, which itemizes every expense of the manufactured adventure. Under salaries, the account lists: "To salary of Mr Plover 1 0 0" (004-012) — one pound for his services.

The presence of this line item in a formal invoice transforms Plover from a shadowy conspirator into a salaried professional. He is not a criminal but an employee; the attack he was meant to execute was not a crime but a service for which the customer (however unknowingly) paid.

## Significance

Like [[miss-jameson|Miss Jameson]] and other employees of the Adventure and Romance Agency, Mr. Plover represents Chesterton's insight that the modern economy has professionalized experiences previously thought to be spontaneous or authentic. Adventure itself has become a product, and Plover — with his salary of one pound — is a small but telling cog in the machinery of manufactured romance.
