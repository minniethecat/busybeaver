## Character Overview

The Reverend Ellis Shorter is introduced in the story "The Queer Feet" through a visiting card bearing the inscription "Rev. Ellis Shorter" (008-024). He is a man of acute nervous sensibility -- "feverishly flapping his plain gloves" when first encountered (008-053) -- and his defining characteristic is a kind of helpless astonishment at the world's refusal to conform to his expectations. His short-sightedness, both literal and metaphorical, is a recurring motif: he "peered short-sightedly at his papers" when recounting his ordeal (009-007).

## The Adventure

Shorter's improbable adventure begins when he visits a household of unmarried ladies -- the Misses James -- on what he believes to be a routine pastoral call. The situation rapidly devolves into a waking nightmare. The ladies, far from being the quiet spinsters he expected, greet him with incomprehensible slang and threats of physical violence.

When Shorter politely tries to excuse himself -- "I am so sorry to disturb you, Miss James, but I must really be going" -- the reply he receives is unforgettable: "Chuck it, fat 'ead" (009-009). The situation escalates as another woman, Miss Brett, adds: "Put 'is old 'ead in a bag, Sam, and tie 'im up before you start jawin'" (009-013).

Shorter's narration of these events is a masterpiece of comic understatement. He records the incomprehensible dialogue "phonetically," with the detachment of a naturalist documenting an unfamiliar species: "I have not the least idea of their meaning; so I have only been able to render them phonetically" (009-007). His bewilderment reaches its philosophical peak when he wonders "was it really true... that unmarried ladies had some dreadful riotous society of their own from which all others were excluded?" (009-017).

## Character Significance

Shorter is one of Chesterton's finest comic creations: a man whose innocence is so complete that it becomes a kind of blindness. Yet Chesterton's treatment is never cruel. Shorter's terror is real, his confusion genuine, and his eventual rescue by Basil Grant is an act of genuine compassion. The character embodies Chesterton's conviction that the ordinary person -- the clergyman making pastoral calls, the gentleman who expects spinsters to serve tea -- is perpetually on the edge of discovering that the world is far stranger than his categories can accommodate.

The irony of Shorter's situation is that he is the "normal" one in a world of eccentrics -- and it is precisely his normality that makes him vulnerable. The spinsters, with their criminal slang and exuberant threats, are simply another variant of the Club of Queer Trades principle: everyone has a hidden life, and the most dangerous assumption is that anyone is exactly what they appear.
