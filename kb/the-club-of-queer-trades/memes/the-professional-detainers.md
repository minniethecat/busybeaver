# The Professional Detainers

**The Professional Detainers** are a [[queer-trades|queer trade]] whose agents are paid by clients to detain designated persons in conversation on harmless pretexts, keeping them occupied for a specified duration while the client pursues some private purpose. The trade is revealed at the [[the-club-dinner|Club dinner]] in "The Singular Speculation of the House-Agent."

It represents one of the more psychologically subtle of the queer trades — its effectiveness depends not on spectacle but on the mundane impossibility of escaping a determined bore.

## Overview

The Professional Detainers operate a bureau that dispatches agents to waylay and occupy persons whom a paying client wishes to have "out of the way for a few hours" (011-012). The agents assume personae calibrated to the social situation: a hunting bore for the impatient bachelor, a parish worker for the couple trying to leave for an engagement, a vicar for the highest-stakes assignments.

The trade functions on a tariff system, with the most demanding impersonations commanding the highest fees.

The trade's existence is first hinted at in "The Awful Reason of the Vicar's Visit," where the entire mystery — the kidnapping of the vicar, the strange imposture at his home — is retrospectively revealed as a Professional Detainers operation.

The "vicar" who visits the narrator is in fact a Detainer, and his colleague is simultaneously detaining Rupert Grant at his own flat. The elaborate religious imposture, complete with the tale of Miss Brett and the threat to the church plate, is nothing more than a paid assignment to keep the narrator at home while Captain Fraser enjoys an uninterrupted dinner with a lady.

## Operation and Tariff

The Professional Detainers' business model is explained at the Club dinner by the two agents themselves, who drop their vicar personae and speak in "the brisk sharp tones of a young city man" (011-011):

> "It is really nothing very important. We are paid by our clients to detain in conversation, on some harmless pretext, people whom they want out of the way for a few hours." [011-011--011-013]

The agents reveal their pricing structure. Vicars command five guineas a visit — the highest rate on the tariff — because the role is "the most respectable and more of a strain" (011-047). Colonels, the next tier down, are four guineas. The scale descends through progressively less demanding impersonations: the hunting bore, the garrulous parish worker, the determined anecdotalist.

The trade's reach into ordinary life is made explicit by one of the agents:

> "When an old bachelor goes on boring you with hunting stories, when you're burning to be introduced to somebody, he's from our bureau. When a lady calls on parish work and stops hours, just when you wanted to go to the Robinsons', she's from our bureau." [011-034--011-038]

This passage recasts the minor annoyances of social life as a hidden economy: every tedious conversationalist may be a paid professional. The conceit is characteristically Chestertonian — it takes a universal social experience (being trapped by a bore) and, by reinterpreting it as a commercial service, reveals something true about the hidden structures of ordinary life.

## Members and Associates

The two Professional Detainers encountered in the stories are "brisk young men who were occasionally vicars, and always Professional Detainers" (022-037). One poses as the Rev. Ellis Shorter of Chuntsey, in Essex — a real clergyman whose identity is borrowed for the impersonation. The other poses as the unnamed vicar who visits Rupert Grant.

Both agents are members of [[the-club-of-queer-trades-org|The Club of Queer Trades]], appearing at the climactic Club dinner alongside [[northover|P. G. Northover]], [[professor-chadd|Professor Chadd]], and the other queer tradesmen. Their presence at the dinner table confirms that the Club's membership extends beyond the inventors of the trades to include their principal operatives.

The trade also employs or coordinates with [[miss-brett|Miss Brett]], whose imposture as a charitable church lady is part of the elaborate deception constructed to detain [[the-reverend-vicar|the narrator]].

## Queer Trade Analysis

The Professional Detainers exemplify Chesterton's definition of a queer trade in its most paradoxical form: a profession that is simultaneously entirely fraudulent (the vicar is not a vicar, the bore is not really boring) and entirely genuine (the service is actually rendered, the client is actually helped). The trade does not steal or deceive for gain; it deceives in order to provide a service that, by its nature, can only be provided through deception.

The trade also illuminates the Club's founding principle: that a queer trade must be a "genuine commercial source of income, the support of its inventor" [001-035--001-036]. The Professional Detainers operate a real office, maintain a published tariff, and serve paying clients. Their trade is absurd, but it is also a functioning business — the absurdity is the point.

## Chestertonian Context

The Professional Detainers trade enacts two of Chesterton's recurring philosophical preoccupations.

First, it illustrates his [[paradox-in-fiction|paradoxical]] claim that the ordinary is more extraordinary than the exotic: the most effective disguise is not the sensational but the mundane. A vicar is a better cover than a spy because no one questions a vicar.

Second, the trade embodies Chesterton's [[ordinary-life|defense of ordinariness]] — the Detainers do not harm their targets; they merely occupy them, and the occupation is itself a form of social interaction (conversation) that, in a healthier society, would be recognized as a good in itself.

The trade also functions as a satire of professionalism — the very idea that social interaction could be standardized, priced, and delivered by paid agents is both comic and unnerving. In the world of the Professional Detainers, every conversation is potentially a transaction, and every bore may be on the clock.

The fact that the trade is revealed at the Club dinner — where storytelling is supposed to be free and convivial — adds an additional layer of irony: even at the celebration of amateurism, the professionals have a seat at the table.
