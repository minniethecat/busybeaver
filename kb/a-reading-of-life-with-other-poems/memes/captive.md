## Overview

The captive in Meredith's poetry appears in two sharply contrasting forms: the unresisting victim who accepts capture with eerie calm, and the wild creature whose captivity is a temporary state before violent liberation.

These two modes — resignation and rebellion — structure Meredith's thinking about imprisonment as both a physical condition and a psychological one.

The web-caught insect in the Arachne poem accepts its fate with the words "You have me; I am yours" (015-049), while the Camargue horses, "by treason snared" (018-013), "flung their riders to the ground" and gallop free.

Between these poles, Meredith examines captivity as a test of character — what one does when caught reveals more than what one did when free.

## In the Poems

### The Willing Captive (Arachne's Web)

In the unnamed spider poem of Section 5, Meredith presents capture as an almost philosophical transaction.

Arachne, "this matron of the unnumbered threads," catches not a fly but "A monster in the form of ball" — the "mildest captive ever snared, / That neither struggled nor despaired" (015-049).

The captive's response is extraordinary: "Remarked, 'You have me; I am yours.'"

This is captivity without resistance, a surrender so complete it becomes a form of power — the captive, by accepting its state with dignity, reverses the expected dynamic.

Meredith contrasts this calm with the weaver who "cursed her lures" below, suggesting that the captor is more disturbed by the capture than the captive.

The episode reads as a miniature fable: true freedom may consist not in avoiding capture but in how one meets it.

### The Rebellious Captive (The Mares of the Camargue)

The Camargue horses invert the spider's-captive model entirely.

These "hundred mares, all white," with manes "Like mace-reed of the marshy plains," represent wildness that cannot be domesticated.

Meredith describes how they were taken: "By treason snared, some captives shown; / Expatriate from their native Rhone, / Led off, their saline pastures far from view" (018-013).

The word "treason" is crucial — the captivity is illegitimate, a violation of natural order rather than the spider's honest predation.

Unlike the insect, the horses do not accept: "on a day, with prompt rebound, / They have flung their riders to the ground, / And at a single gallop, scouring free."

The captive state is exposed as contingent, awaiting only the moment when the creature's nature reasserts itself.

### Captivity as Moral Spectrum

Meredith's two captives trace a moral spectrum: at one end, acceptance that transforms capture into a kind of grace; at the other, the irrepressible impulse toward freedom that cannot be extinguished by chains.

Neither is presented as simply superior — the spider's captive achieves a strange dignity, while the horses achieve heroic liberation.

Together they suggest that captivity, in Meredith's vision, is never merely a physical condition but always a relationship between captor and captured, defined as much by the captive's response as by the captor's power.
