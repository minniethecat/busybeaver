## Overview

The lion appears in Meredith's poetry as an emblem of concentrated ferocity — the predator that haunts the thickets, the burning vitality of spring, and the classical measure of heroic courage. Meredith's lions are never merely decorative; each appearance condenses a relationship between power and fear, between the noble and the savage. "The lion-haunted thickets hold apart" (003-230) opens a space of primal danger at the edge of the civilized world. The composite beast "made up of lion, bear and fox" (005-034) fuses predation with cunning in a satiric portrait of human corruption. And in the prayer-like "Dear God of Mercy, and if lion-like" (013-013), the lion becomes the register of divine power — terrifying and merciful in equal measure. Across these varied deployments, the lion is Meredith's shorthand for force at its most elemental.

## In the Poems

### Lion as Predatory Threat

The most atmospherically potent lion image in the collection evokes a landscape defined by hidden danger: "The lion-haunted thickets hold apart" (003-230). The thickets are not merely inhabited by lions; they are *haunted* by them — the lion is a presence felt even when unseen, a dread that shapes the behavior of all other creatures. The thickets "hold apart" because the lion's presence creates exclusion zones, spaces where the ordinary commerce of life cannot proceed. In the Homeric register, the lion's role as predator becomes explicit: "goats bleat in flight from a lion" (017-120). The bleating and flight are the lion's signature as much as the attack itself — the predator is defined by the terror it induces, by the disruption that precedes and follows its presence.

### Lion as Composite of Power and Cunning

In one of the collection's most striking satiric passages, Meredith constructs a chimeric portrait: "These two made up of lion, bear and fox" (005-034). The composite beast is not a literal animal but a figure for human types — those who combine the lion's ferocity, the bear's brute strength, and the fox's cunning. The lion component supplies nobility to an otherwise ignoble amalgam: the bear is merely strong, the fox merely sly, but the lion confers a regal danger that elevates the composite above mere thuggery. This usage draws on the classical and medieval bestiary tradition where the lion is king of beasts, while subjecting that tradition to satiric deformation.

### Lion as Vital Fire

"A burning lion for the spring" (005-143) is among Meredith's most compressed and vivid images. The lion is not merely present in spring — it *is* spring, the season's ferocious vitality concentrated into a single burning body. The adjective "burning" does double work: it denotes the lion's tawny color (burning gold) and the intensity of its life-force (burning with energy). This lion is not predatory but generative — the spring that renews the world is lion-like in its irresistible forward surge. The image participates in a long tradition associating the lion with solar and vernal power, from zodiacal Leo to the lion of alchemical transformation.

### Lion and Divine Power

The poem "The Promise in Disturbance" invokes the lion in its most theologically charged context: "Dear God of Mercy, and if lion-like" (013-013). The conditional "if" is crucial — the speaker does not assert that God is lion-like but entertains the possibility, holding in tension the God of Mercy and the God of leonine power. The lion here belongs to the ancient tradition of Yahweh as lion (Hosea, Amos), where divine ferocity and divine mercy are not opposites but aspects of a single overwhelming presence. The line that follows — "Thou treadest us to make our dust" — completes the lion's work: the tread of the lion's paw is also the act of creation-through-destruction that returns the human to its constitutive elements.
