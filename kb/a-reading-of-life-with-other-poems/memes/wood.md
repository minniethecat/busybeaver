## Overview

The wood in Meredith's poetry is the counter-space to the settled homestead — a realm of tangled growth, hidden song, and transformative encounter. It appears across the collection as site of first words and last refuges, of "song no sermon's drone" (014-137) and of "woodland woven-boughed" (006-005) mystery. Meredith's woods are drawn from classical pastoral convention — the wood-nymph, the brushwood cavern — but also from a recognisably English landscape of "woody glen" and "unclipped woodland." The wood is where the poet finds what the cleared plain cannot offer: a density of presence and meaning resistant to parsing, a place where "no sermon's drone" can reach.

## In the Poems

### The Wood as Primal Space

Meredith's woods function as a threshold between civilisation and wilderness. "Down my woodland walk she led" (016-019) — the first line of first words, the wood is where speech begins. It is the space of beginnings, before the clearing, before the sermon, before the law. The "woody glen" (002-014) of the early poems establishes the wood as a recurring geography: not merely a setting but an argument about what lies beyond the cultivated.

### Nymph and Cavern: Classical Woodland

The classical register is unmistakable. A "wood-nymph lightly skips" (003-104) through the verse, and Ossa holds a "brushwood cavern" (005-053) where divine encounters happen. The "cool wood-side" with its "balm and flutes" (005-145) activates the full pastoral repertoire — healing, music, shade. Meredith uses these classical furnishings not as ornament but as shorthand for a mode of being that civilisation has not yet disciplined.

### Song Against Sermon

The most pointed opposition in Meredith's woodland appears in "The Sage Enamoured and the Honest Lady": "Now deep in woods, with song no sermon's drone" (014-137). The wood is where song — free, natural, ungoverned — replaces the sermon with its drone of moral instruction. This is the wood as anti-church, a natural cathedral whose liturgy is spontaneous. The "unclipped woodland" (017-102) that elsewhere "catches" the grazing life reinforces this sense of the wood as uncut, unshaped by human design.

### The Woodland's Reach

Across the collection, the wood gathers force as a presiding presence. The "leagues of woodland held them wronged" (014-114) — the forest becomes an agent, holding, keeping, accusing. It "surges to kindle a woodland" (017-155) with the energy of growth itself. And in "The Empty Purse," the "woodland woven-boughed" (006-005) offers a shelter of interlaced branches, a space woven rather than built.
