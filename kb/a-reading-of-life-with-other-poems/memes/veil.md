## Overview

The veil is the governing image of Meredith's "With the Persuader" — a poem about the approach, concealment, and ultimate revelation of divine beauty. Across three stages, the veil moves from obscuring presence to rent barrier to lifted covering, tracing an arc from mystery through crisis to disclosure. Meredith inherits the veil from multiple traditions — the biblical veil of the Temple, the erotic veil of the Song of Songs, the mystical veil of Neoplatonic philosophy — and fuses them into a single dramatic sequence.

What makes Meredith's veil distinctive is its animation. It is not a static cloth but "A veil with eyes that waver through" (003-004) — watching, wavering, alive with the presence it conceals. The veil does not simply hide; it signals that something hides behind it, and the quality of its concealment — its wavering, its eye-filled surface — is itself a kind of promise. Revelation, when it comes, is not the stripping away of an obstacle but the fulfillment of a relationship that the veil has mediated from the beginning.

## In the Poems

### The Veil with Eyes: Concealment as Invitation

"With the Persuader" opens with a question about an unseen presence: "Who murmurs, hither, hither: who / Where nought is audible so fills the ear?" (003-001–002). The answer comes through paradox: "Where nought is visible can make appear / A veil with eyes that waver through" (003-003–004). The invisible *makes appear* a veil — and the veil has eyes, suggesting that what is concealed is itself watching the one who seeks it.

The veil's eyes "waver" — they are not steady, not fully revealed, trembling between presence and absence. The veil is thus not a barrier between seeker and sought but the first form of their encounter. It conceals, but in concealing it reveals that there is something to conceal, and its eyes — wavering, uncertain, alive — communicate across the barrier. Meredith's veil is the condition of spiritual longing: what we seek hides from us, but its hiding looks back at us through the fabric of its own concealment.

### The Veil Rent: Crisis and Fulfillment

The poem's central crisis occurs when the accumulated pressure of longing breaks through: "The murmur hushes down, the veil is rent" (003-042). The verb "rent" (torn, split apart) is violent — this is not a gentle unveiling but a rupture, the fabric giving way under strain. The murmur that opened the poem — the ambiguous call of the unseen presence — falls silent at the moment of revelation, as if the voice's work is done once the veil is torn.

What follows the rending is a triple consent: "Man's cry, earth's answer, heaven's consent" (003-043). The veil's tearing aligns the cosmic registers — human, terrestrial, celestial — into a single chord. "Her form is given to pardoned sight" (003-044): the vision is a gift, not a conquest, and the sight that receives it is "pardoned" — forgiven, absolved, granted access it did not earn. The rending of the veil is an act of grace, not of force.

### Unveiled to Nuptial Sight: The Marriage of Vision

The poem's final veil image is its resolution: "She stands unveiled to nuptial sight, / Sweet in her disregard of aid / Divine to conquer or persuade" (003-055–057). The unveiling is complete — she "stands unveiled" — and the sight that beholds her is "nuptial," the sight of union, of marriage between perceiver and perceived.

"Sweet in her disregard of aid" is Meredith's most striking characterization of the unveiled beloved. She does not need help — not from divine power ("to conquer") nor from divine eloquence ("to persuade"). Her unveiled presence is sufficient; she stands in her own authority, neither commanding nor pleading, simply being. The nuptial sight is the reward for the long approach through the veiled stages — the seeker who followed the wavering eyes through concealment and crisis is granted the vision of what was always present behind the veil, now standing clear in the light of noon.
