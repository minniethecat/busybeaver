## Overview

The arrow in Meredith's poetry functions as a figure of directed intention — force given aim, energy provided with trajectory. Unlike the sword, which cuts at close range and demands the wielder's embodied presence, the arrow acts at distance: it is the extension of will across space. Meredith deploys the arrow in three principal registers: the mythological (Artemis the "Huntress, arrowy to pursue"), the natural-philosophical (an arrow pushed "through the stream"), and the epic-heroic ("thy bow and thy thick shot of arrows"). Across these, the arrow consistently represents the junction of purpose and motion — the moment when intention becomes trajectory.

## In the Poems

### The Divine Huntress and the Mythic Arrow

Meredith opens the arrow's symbolic range with the figure of the divine huntress, described as "Huntress, arrowy to pursue" (002-013, 002-049). The coinage "arrowy" — an adjective made from the weapon itself — fuses the archer with her instrument: she is not merely carrying arrows but is herself arrow-like, her very being constituted by directed pursuit. This is the arrow at its most mythic: not a tool wielded by a hunter but the quality of divine intention itself, swift and unerring. Meredith's repetition of the phrase across two sections suggests its importance as a fixed epithet in his mythological vocabulary.

### The Arrow Through the Stream

A more philosophical deployment appears in the image of one who would "push his arrow through the stream" (006-012). Unlike the mythic huntress whose arrows fly unopposed through air, this arrow meets resistance — the stream's current, the medium's density. The verb "push" rather than "shoot" emphasizes the effort involved: this is not the effortless flight of divine intent but the laborious passage of mortal purpose through a resistant world. The stream here functions as Meredith's figure for the medium of existence itself — that which must be traversed, that which opposes and shapes every directed action.

### Epic Arrows and the Heroic Register

In the collection's most epic passage, Meredith invokes "thy bow and thy thick shot of arrows" (017-124) in a context of martial confrontation. The phrase "thick shot" conveys density of fire — not a single arrow carefully aimed but a volley, a saturation of the air with directed force. This is the arrow in its collective, military aspect, where individual aim is subsumed into the massed purpose of battle. The shift from the singular arrow of the huntress to the "thick shot" of the battlefield maps Meredith's movement from lyric to epic registers, from the precision of myth to the scale of history.

### Arrows and the Landscape of Flight

The arrow also appears in pastoral flight: "On grassy sweeps and flying arrows, thick" (014-103) places the arrow not in war but in a landscape of play, where the grassy expanse and the flying shaft together compose a scene of freedom and motion. The adjective "thick" applied to arrows in this peaceful context echoes its martial use — but here thickness suggests abundance rather than violence, the joyful density of arrows in flight across meadow.
