## Overview

The kiss in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is not a single act but a spectrum. At one end lies the devotional kiss — the kiss pressed to the feet of an exalted figure, a gesture of worship and self-abasement. At the other end lies the kiss of nature — the sun's kiss on ripening fruit, the zephyr's kiss on a bent neck, the snowflake's kiss on the snowdrop. Between these poles, the human kiss moves through registers of submission, passion, and tenderness.

Meredith's treatment of the kiss is notable for what it refuses: there is no simple romantic kiss in this collection, no mutual meeting of lips between equal lovers. The kiss is always asymmetrical — offered to something higher, received from something elemental, or yielding to a "high Lady's mandate" (003-247). Desire, in Meredith's world, bends; it does not stand level.

## In the Poems

### The Devotional Kiss

The collection's most charged statement about the kiss appears early, in chapter 3: "And we, afire to kiss her feet, no more" (003-021). The syntax is elliptical and arresting. "Afire to kiss her feet" — the desire is ardent, consuming — but "no more" cuts it short. The kiss is desired but denied, or perhaps the desire itself is the entire experience, the kiss's consummation unnecessary to the poem's logic. The feet are the lowest point of the beloved body; to kiss them is to place oneself in the posture of the supplicant. This is not eroticism but devotion, and the fire that drives it is closer to religious ardor than to romantic passion.

The devotional register returns later in the same chapter: "In her high Lady's mandate, yields the kiss" (003-247). The kiss is "yielded" — not given freely but conceded under authority, the "mandate" of a lady who stands above the one who receives it. The verb choice is telling: a yielded kiss is not a gift; it is a tribute.

### The Solar Kiss

Chapter 4 shifts the kiss from the human to the cosmic register. In a passage describing a figure in perfect harmony with nature, Meredith writes that he is "As peaches, that have caught the sun's uprise / And kissed warm gold till noonday, even as vines" (004-157–158). The sun kisses the peaches into ripeness; the fruit holds the warmth as a kind of memory of the contact. This is the kiss as natural process — unhurried, generative, without the agonies of human wanting. The comparison to vines reinforces the agricultural frame: the kiss is how the natural world transfers vitality, how one thing ripens another through sustained contact.

### The Fragile Kisses

In the shorter, more delicate poems, the kiss becomes miniature and precarious. Chapter 5 offers an image of vulnerability: "With gold necks bent for any zephyr's kiss" (005-137). The necks are bent in anticipation, offered to a breeze that may or may not come. The kiss here is something awaited, something the body postures itself to receive. In chapter 7, the register shifts to the botanical: "When sister snowflake sister snowdrop kissed, / And one passed out, and one the bell-head hung" (007-031–032). The snowflake kisses the snowdrop and melts; the snowdrop remains, bearing the bell of its flower. It is a kiss of mutual transformation — one partner dissolves, the other endures — a miniature allegory of how contact changes both parties, not always equally.
