## Overview

Love in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is not the simple romantic sentiment of conventional Victorian verse. It is a complex, often agonizing force that operates simultaneously on the physical, emotional, and spiritual planes. Meredith presents love as the central human drama — the arena where Nature's dual claims (body versus spirit, earth versus sky) must be reconciled.

The collection traces a philosophical arc: from the youthful choice between Aphrodite (love of the body) and Artemis (love of purity), through the torments of divided desire, to a mature integration where "The body's love and mind's" unite into the soul's love (004-216). This is Meredith's distinctive contribution to the Victorian poetry of love: not renunciation of the physical for the spiritual, but their hard-won union.

## In the Poems

### The Vital Choice: Aphrodite or Artemis

The collection opens by framing life's fundamental choice in terms of love. In *The Vital Choice* (chapter 1), Meredith poses the question: "Or shall we run with Artemis / Or yield the breast to Aphrodite?" (001-002). Both goddesses "give bliss" (001-005), yet "Each can torture if divided" (001-006). The core problem is not choosing one over the other, but the suffering caused by division — worshipping one while denying the other. This sets up the dialectic that the entire collection seeks to resolve.

### The Huntress: Love Transcending Sex

*With the Huntress* (chapter 2) presents Artemis not as cold virginity but as a form of love "Sweet beyond the thrill of sex; / Hallowed by the sex confessed" (002-047). Under her leadership, maid and man become "comrades, led by her, / Maid-preserver, man-maker" (002-073). This is love purified — not the absence of desire but its channeling into higher purpose.

### The Persuader: Love as Allurement

*With the Persuader* (chapter 3) presents Aphrodite's call as an irresistible inner force — "The very she called forth by ripened blood / For its next breath of being" (003-026). She is "Maid, woman and divinity" (003-031), "our hunger and our fruit / Untasted" (003-033). This is love as the driving life-force, the "stream within us urged to flood" (003-029).

### The Hueless Love: Beyond Appearances

*The Hueless Love* (chapter 7) represents Meredith's most mature statement on love. The "hueless" love is one stripped of mere attraction and passion — a love that sees through appearances to essential worth. It represents the integration achieved after the battles of the earlier poems.

### Love in The Test of Manhood

The climactic resolution comes in *The Test of Manhood* (chapter 4). After the long internal war between the claims of body and spirit, the matured man "knows he Love, that beckons and controls; / The star of sky upon his footway cast" (004-213). The integration is complete: "Then match in him who holds his tempters fast, / The body's love and mind's, whereof the soul's" (004-215). Love becomes the force that unifies the divided self.

### Love and Earth's Purpose

In the collection's final vision, love serves a cosmic purpose. "Then Earth her man for woman finds at last, / To speed the pair unto her goal of goals" (004-217). Human love, properly integrated, becomes the instrument through which Nature achieves her evolutionary aim.
