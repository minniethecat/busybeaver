## Overview

The figure of the hunter runs through Meredith's poetry as a complex reversal pattern: the one who pursues becomes the one who is pursued. Nature appears as a "Huntress, arrowy to pursue" (002-013, 002-049), yet she also "will tempt pursuit" (003-249) — snaring the hunter in his own dynamic of desire. This chiastic structure — hunter hunted, pursuer pursued — is central to Meredith's understanding of eros, nature, and the relationship between the human subject and the forces that draw it forward.

The hunter motif draws on classical sources — Diana/Artemis the huntress, the boar hunt of myth, the baying hounds of epic — but Meredith reconfigures these materials into a philosophical psychology. The hunt is not merely an external activity but an internal structure: the soul hunts for truth, beauty hunts the beholder, and every pursuit contains the possibility of reversal. The hunter's horn that "might wind a jocund ring" but "held in ear it had a chilly clang" (014-109–110) epitomizes the double register of the image — joy and threat, invitation and warning, heard in the same sound.

## In the Poems

### The Divine Huntress: Nature as Pursuer

The figure of the Huntress appears in the framing poems of the collection as a personification of the force that drives the seeker. "Huntress, arrowy to pursue" (002-013) — the arrow is both weapon and directional sign, pointing toward what it strikes. Nature as huntress does not merely chase; she *aims*, and her arrows are the impulses that set human desire in motion. The repetition of the epithet at 002-049 confirms its importance: the huntress is not a passing figure but a structural presence, the agent who initiates the pursuit that constitutes the poetic quest.

The huntress's arrows are "arrowy" — a Meredith coinage that makes the quality of being arrow-like into an adjective of active force. She is not merely armed with arrows; she is *arrowy*, defined by the projective, directional, piercing quality of her instruments. To be pursued by such a figure is to be aimed at, targeted, singled out for the chase that is also a calling.

### The Hunter's Shouts: Violence and the Crimsoned Flower

The hunt's violence enters the poetry most directly in a passage from "With the Persuader": "Grunt of the tusky boar, the baying hounds, / The hunter's shouts, are heard afar, and bring / Dead on her heart her crimsoned flower of Spring" (003-129–131). The sonic sequence — grunt, baying, shouts — builds a cacophony of pursuit that culminates in an image of wounding. The "crimsoned flower of Spring" is killed by the very sounds of the hunt, brought "dead on her heart" as if the noise itself were the agent of destruction.

The hunters here are "the irreverent of Life's design" (003-132) — those who violate the sacred order of natural beauty through their aggressive pursuit. The hunt, in this register, is not the noble chase of classical tradition but a desecration, a violence against the spring that ought to be protected. Meredith's critique of the hunt is thus also a defense of the contemplative relation to beauty against the appropriative one.

### Snared as Hunter: The Reversal of Pursuit

The most philosophically compressed hunter passage turns the figure inside out: "And is it needed that Love's daintier brute / Be snared as hunter, she will tempt pursuit" (003-248–249). The agent here is Nature — "she" who "will tempt pursuit" — and her method is to make the hunter himself the snared. The "daintier brute" (love, desire, the erotic impulse in its refined form) is captured by the very dynamic it thought to control.

The verb "snared" is precise: a snare is a trap that closes around the pursuer, a noose that tightens with movement. To be "snared as hunter" is to be caught in one's own hunting — the pursuit becomes the capture, the chaser becomes the chased. This is Meredith's version of the classical topos of the hunter ensnared by love: Actaeon torn by his own hounds, but translated into the psychological register of erotic reversal.

### The Hunter's Horn: Joy and Chill

The horn sounds across the collection as the auditory signature of the hunt: "Peals the horn exulting, peals" (002-011) in the opening chase, and "The hunter's horn might wind a jocund ring" (014-109) in a later forest scene. The horn is exultant, jocund — it announces the hunt as celebration, as communal joy, as the music of pursuit.

But Meredith characteristically undercuts the celebration: "But held in ear it had a chilly clang" (014-110). The horn heard at a distance is jocund; the horn held close to the ear — examined, attended to, truly listened to — has a "chilly clang." The chill is the awareness of what the hunt means: pursuit, violence, the crimsoned flower. The same sound that rings with joy at a distance rings with cold when brought near. Meredith's hunter is never far from this chill — the awareness that every pursuit carries the seed of its own reversal.
