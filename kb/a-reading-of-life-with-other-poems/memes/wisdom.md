## Overview

Wisdom in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is never a gift.

It is "spun" from "vile relapsing" (004-236), born of "hard Necessity" (016-265), and won by those who can "gravely dread without the craven's pulse." Meredith's wisdom is explicitly anti-sentimental: it does not come from books or contemplation but from the friction of repeated failure, the "slough" through which passage must be made.

This conception places Meredith in the tradition of Victorian moral earnestness while distancing him from its more comfortable assumptions. Wisdom is not the reward of virtue but the residue of error — not what the good accumulate but what the fallen salvage.

The figure that recurs across the collection is the wise being as craftsperson: the spider working with "sagest craft" (015-001), the singer who is an "anointed sage" (016-243). Wisdom is technique, not theology; it is knowing how rather than knowing that.

## In the Poems

### Wisdom from Relapse

In "The Test of Manhood," Meredith traces the genealogy of wisdom through failure: "With such a grasp upon his brute as tells / Of wisdom from that vile relapsing spun" (004-236). The verb "spun" is crucial: wisdom is not discovered or received but manufactured, drawn out of raw material through effort.

The raw material is "vile relapsing" — the cyclical return to baser impulses, the pattern of ascension and fall that marks the moral life. To "spin" wisdom from relapse is to transform the waste product of moral failure into the thread of moral understanding.

The metaphor is alchemical: base matter becomes gold through art, and the artist is the sufferer who refuses to let suffering go to waste.

### Necessity's Daughter

"Foresight and Patience" develops the genealogy further: "Much have I studied hard Necessity! / To know her Wisdom's mother, and that we / May deem the harshness of her later cries / In labour a sure goad to prick the wise" (016-265). The speaker has "studied" Necessity — not worshipped, not accepted, but studied, with the implication of close observation over time.

What this study yields is the knowledge that Wisdom's mother is Necessity, that the capacity to choose wisely is born from the condition of having no choice. The "harshness of her later cries / In labour" is the pain of Necessity giving birth to Wisdom — labour as both work and childbirth, the double meaning making the metaphor visceral and bodily.

### The Passage Through

The culminating image of Meredith's wisdom is the passage: "The wisdom making passage through our slough" (016-265). A slough (pronounced "sluff") is a bog or mire — the place where movement becomes impossible, where every step sinks deeper. Wisdom is what "makes passage" through this terrain, finding firm ground where none is visible.

The phrase echoes Bunyan's Slough of Despond but secularizes it: Meredith's slough is not sin but the general condition of human limitation, and wisdom is not grace but the skill of navigation, earned through experience and applied with effort.

### The Sagest Craft

In "A Garden Idyl," wisdom appears in its most embodied form: "WITH sagest craft Arachne worked / Her web, and at a corner lurked" (015-001). The superlative "sagest" attaches to "craft" rather than to Arachne herself — it is the making that is wise, not the maker.

The spider's wisdom is entirely practical: knowing where to place the web, when to lurk, what will "plump her soon" (015-003). This is wisdom as predation, as patience, as the intelligence of the body rather than the mind.

Meredith's pairing of the sublime "wisdom" with the domestic spider is characteristic of his method: the highest human faculty has its analogue in the humblest corner of the garden, and the garden teaches what the library cannot.
