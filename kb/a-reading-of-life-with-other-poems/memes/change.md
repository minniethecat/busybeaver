## Overview

Change in Meredith's poetry is not merely alteration but the constitutive rhythm of being — the principle that ensures nothing stays still and everything renews. The word appears most densely in the early philosophical poems where the relation between the feminine principle (Nature) and the masculine seeker is figured as mutual, unending motion: "Unresting she, unresting he, from change / To change, as rain of cloud, as fruit of rain" (004-193, 004-194). This formulation makes change cyclical rather than linear — a precipitation and a harvest, not a departure and an arrival. In the later poems, the concept deepens into an ethical principle: the "Exchange of powers of this conflict" (005-094) transforms change from mere flux into productive exchange, where opposing forces enrich each other through their opposition. Meredith's change is fundamentally optimistic — not the Heraclitean flux that sweeps all away but a generative reciprocity that builds.

## In the Poems

### Change as Cosmic Rhythm

The central statement on change comes in the paired lines that describe the masculine and feminine principles locked in mutual motion: "Unresting she, unresting he, from change / To change, as rain of cloud, as fruit of rain" (004-193, 004-194). The repetition of "unresting" across the gender boundary establishes change as the one law that governs both halves of being. The similes that follow — rain from cloud, fruit from rain — are not random transformations but stages in a cycle, each dependent on the prior, each generating the next. Change, in this vision, is not chaotic but structured as recurrence, as natural as the water cycle and as productive as agriculture. Meredith insists that this cosmic restlessness is not a flaw but a feature: "She feels her blood-tree throbbing in her grain, / Yet skyward branched" — change is growth, the tree's pulse felt in its seed.

### Change as Exchange in Conflict

The second major passage reframes change through the lens of conflict and exchange. In describing the union of opposed forces, Meredith writes of "Exchange of powers of this conflict came; / Defacement none, nor ever squandered force" (005-094). The key move here is from *change* to *exchange* — not one-sided transformation but reciprocal transfer. The conflict between masculine and feminine, earth and sky, defeat and victory, is productive precisely because it is an exchange, not a destruction: "By loss enriched, by overthrow restored." This economy of change — nothing lost, only converted — is Meredith's antidote to the fear of flux. The forces that oppose each other are not enemies but trading partners, and change is the currency of their trade.

### The Mandate of Forward Motion

Change in Meredith is tied to the imperative of forward movement — the refusal to be static. "Strike camp, and onward, like the wind's cloud-fleets" (004-193, preceding context) frames change as a military march, the deliberate abandonment of settlement for advance. The cloud-fleets that drive across the sky are images of purposeful motion, not drift. This martial language situates change within Meredith's broader ethic of resolute striving: to change is not to be passively altered by circumstances but to actively strike camp, to choose the onward path over the comfortable encampment.
