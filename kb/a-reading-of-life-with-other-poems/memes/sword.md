## Overview

The sword in Meredith's poetry operates as a symbol of irreducible doubleness. It is simultaneously an instrument of martial heroism — the classical weapon of the epic warrior — and a metaphor for the cutting edge of experience itself, where sweetness and pain meet along the same keen line.

Meredith deploys the sword in contexts that deliberately unsettle its conventional heroic associations. The "sword's edge of sweetness" (003-040) collapses the distance between pleasure and wounding; the driven sword (003-310) becomes the culminating act in a sequence of glory; the sword as metaphor for gifts bestowed (004-068) suggests that what is given may also cut.

Across these deployments, Meredith refuses the sword's simple identification with power or violence. Instead, he treats it as a figure for the condition of mortal life itself — sharp, double-edged, incapable of being grasped without consequence.

## In the Poems

### The Sweet Edge: Pleasure and Pain United

Meredith's most compressed sword image appears early in the collection: "a sword's edge of sweetness keen" (003-040). The phrase binds sweetness and sharpness into a single sensory figure — the sword's edge does not merely represent danger or violence but becomes the very medium through which sweetness is experienced.

The word "keen" does double work: it describes the blade's sharpness while simultaneously suggesting eagerness, intensity, a quality of heightened perception. The sword here is not a weapon wielded in combat but a condition of awareness — sweetness known only because it cuts.

This paradoxical union of pleasure and wounding recurs throughout Meredith's philosophical poetry, where joy and grief are rarely separable and the deepest experiences arrive edged.

### The Driven Sword: Glory's Terminal Act

In a more conventionally heroic context, Meredith writes: "A sword is driven; for those most glorious" (003-310). The passive construction — "a sword is driven" rather than "he drives a sword" — abstracts the act from any individual agent, making the sword's motion itself the subject.

The sentence structure subordinates human agency to the weapon's trajectory. What matters is not who drives the sword but that the sword is driven — an impersonal force that concludes the arc of glory. The "most glorious" are those for whom the sword becomes, paradoxically, both the instrument of their fame and its termination.

### Gifts Bestowed but on the Sword

Meredith extends the sword metaphor into the domain of gift and obligation: "final gifts bestowed but on the sword" (004-068). The word "final" compounds the sword's association with endings; "bestowed" introduces the language of honor and ceremonial giving; "but on" carries the restrictive force of exclusion — these gifts come only through, or only to, the sword.

The line compresses an entire heroic economy into a single phrase: what is given in the heroic world is given to and through the weapon. The sword receives what the man cannot — gifts that arrive only at the point of severance.

### Hewed with Sharp Edge: The Sword in Epic Context

In the epic register of the collection's later poems, Meredith writes of something "hewed with the sword's sharp edge" (017-100). The verb "hewed" — archaic, forceful, recalling Anglo-Saxon poetry — places the sword within a tradition of heroic craft where the blade shapes as much as it destroys.

Hewing is not random violence but directed force: the sword's edge is applied with purpose, cutting along a determined line. In Meredith's hands, this becomes a figure for poetic making itself — the sharp discrimination that separates essential from inessential, the cut that defines form.
