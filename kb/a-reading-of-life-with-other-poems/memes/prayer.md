## Overview

Meredith uses the word "prayer" three times across two poems, and each occurrence marks a stage in a deliberate theological movement. The first appearance is aesthetic: prayer as a quality of beauty, "worshipful as prayer" (003-299). The second is metaphysical: prayer as the channel through which divinity flows into the human, "the spring of prayer" (004-061). The third is ethical: a prayer not for rescue but for strength, the capacity to endure and to act (004-126). Together these three moments trace a transformation — from prayer as ornament to prayer as ontology to prayer as empowerment.

Meredith's treatment of prayer is characteristically revisionary. He strips the concept of its conventional associations — petition, intercession, the asking-for of things — and reconstitutes it as something closer to alignment: the attunement of the human will to a divine purpose. His prayer is not a request but a commitment, not a wish but a resolve.

## In the Poems

### Worshipful as Prayer: The Aesthetic Register

Meredith's first use of "prayer" in the corpus appears not as an act but as a quality: something is described as "worshipful as prayer" (003-299). The comparison — "as prayer" — treats prayer as the gold standard of worshipfulness, the thing against which all other reverence is measured. But the phrasing is also subtly distancing: the thing described is not prayer itself but "as prayer," metaphorically prayer-like. Meredith is here establishing prayer as a category of value, a mode of being marked by reverence and intensity, before he goes on to redefine what prayer actually means.

### The Spring of Prayer: Prayer as Ontological Channel

The most theologically dense occurrence appears in chapter 4: "So to Divinity the spring of prayer" (004-061). The image of a "spring" — a source, a welling-up, an origin — positions prayer as the point at which the divine enters human experience. Prayer is not a message sent upward from human to God but a spring flowing downward from Divinity into the human soul. This reversal is characteristic of Meredith's immanentist theology: the divine is not remote, to be petitioned from afar, but present and available, to be received through the openness that prayer represents.

The full phrase — "to Divinity the spring of prayer" — carries an ambiguity. Prayer could be the spring that leads to Divinity, or Divinity itself could be the spring from which prayer flows. Meredith seems to intend both readings simultaneously: prayer is the path upward and Divinity is the source downward, and the two meet in the single act of spiritual attention.

### No Prayer Save for Strength: The Ethical Turn

Meredith's most radical revision of prayer comes in the declaration: "No prayer, save for strength to keep his..." (004-126). The poet explicitly refuses all other forms of prayer — for deliverance, for comfort, for the removal of difficulty — and retains only one: the prayer for strength. This is prayer stripped to its ethical core. What the soul needs from God is not a change in circumstances but a change in capacity — not less burden but more strength to bear it.

This prayer for strength aligns with Meredith's broader ethical vision, in which human dignity consists precisely in the capacity to endure, to struggle, to grow through difficulty. To pray for anything else — for ease, for escape — would be to pray against one's own spiritual development.
