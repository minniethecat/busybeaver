## Overview

Spark appears three times in Meredith's poetry, across chapters 2, 15, and 16, each time at a different scale of transformation. In chapter 2, the spark is a celestial signal — a "wavy sparkle" that beckons the runner onward under divine guidance (002-007). In chapter 15, the spark is the last flicker of a mind fading into ash, a miniature tragedy of consciousness expiring (015-073). In chapter 16, the spark is the incendiary point that rolls into consuming fire, the small beginning of vast destruction (016-112). Between the beacon, the ember, and the fuse, Meredith traces the spark's full range: the spark that leads, the spark that dies, and the spark that destroys.

Meredith's sparks are never merely decorative. Each one is a threshold — the point at which one state crosses into another. The sparkle beckons and the runner moves; the mind's spark fails and the self dissolves; the combustible spark catches and the mountain burns. In every case, the spark is the smallest visible unit of a larger transformation, the visible sign of invisible forces at work.

## In the Poems

### The Beckoning Sparkle: Divine Guidance

The first spark in Meredith's poetry is not named as "spark" but as "sparkle" — a verb become noun, action crystallized into image: "See yon wavy sparkle beck" (002-007). The sparkle appears as a sign during urgent motion. The preceding line — "Ravenous all the line for speed" (002-006) — establishes a scene of desperate running, of bodies straining forward. Into this frenzy, the sparkle enters as a directional signal, and the following line names its source: "Sign of the Virgin Lady's lead" (002-008).

The sparkle is thus a Marian signal — a glint of the divine feminine guiding mortal movement. It is "wavy," which suggests both the flicker of light on water and the undulation of a beckoning hand. The sparkle does not illuminate the whole scene; it is a point, not a flood. But that point is enough to orient the runner. Meredith's sparkle is the minimal unit of guidance — the smallest visible sign that carries the largest directional meaning.

### The Last Spark of Mind: Consciousness Expiring

In chapter 15, the spark shifts from external signal to internal faculty: "He seemed to her last spark of mind" (015-073). The context is one of depletion and decline. The preceding line describes the subject as "Ridiculously volatile" (015-072), suggesting instability, unpredictability, a self already coming apart. The spark of mind is what remains — the last point of coherent consciousness in a dissolving personality. And the following line confirms the trajectory: "And that in pallid ash declined" (015-074).

The "pallid ash" is the aftermath of a fire that has burned out. The spark was the final glow before extinction. Meredith's phrasing — "last spark of mind" — carries the weight of a terminal diagnosis. The spark here is not the beginning of anything but the end of everything: the final flicker of a consciousness that, once extinguished, leaves only pallid ash behind. The adjective "pallid" is carefully chosen: not the black of mourning or the grey of distance, but the sickly pale of something that was once alive and is now definitively not.

### The Spark That Rolls to Fire: Ignition of Destruction

Chapter 16 presents the spark at its most dangerous: "Run piling, for one spark to roll in fire / The mountain-torrent of infernal ire" (016-112, 016-113). The combustibles "run piling" — they accumulate, they heap together, they create the conditions for catastrophe. And then a single spark is all it takes. The phrase "one spark" emphasizes the disproportion between cause and effect: the spark is small, singular, almost negligible, but what it ignites is a "mountain-torrent" of infernal fire.

Meredith's image compounds two scales: the mountain (height, mass, permanence) and the torrent (speed, force, inundation). A mountain-torrent is not a gentle stream but a overwhelming flood, and when that flood is made of fire — "infernal ire" — the result is apocalyptic. The spark is the threshold between the merely combustible and the actually combusted. It is the point at which potential becomes actual, at which piled fuel becomes consuming flame. In this sense, the spark of chapter 16 is the dark twin of the sparkle of chapter 2: where one beckons toward salvation, the other ignites toward destruction.
