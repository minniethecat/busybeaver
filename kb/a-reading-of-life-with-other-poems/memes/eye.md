## Overview

The eye occupies a privileged position in Meredith's poetic vocabulary, appearing from the very first line of the collection — "Through the water-eye of night" (002-001) — as the threshold between the visible world and what lies beyond it. Meredith deploys ocular imagery across multiple registers: the mortal eye that receives sensory data, the reproachful eye of moral judgment, the eye of Nature that quickens perception, and the myriad-eyed divine that sees all. The eye is never a passive receptor in this poetry; it is an active agent of interpretation, a site where the external world is transformed into inner vision. The tension between mortal limitation and the aspiration toward deeper sight — being "Quickened of Nature's eye and ear" (003-320) — runs through the collection as a central philosophical concern.

## In the Poems

### The Threshold of Vision

The collection opens with the image that establishes the eye as a liminal organ: "Through the water-eye of night, / Midway between the two white lines" (002-001). This nocturnal eye is neither fully dark nor fully illuminated — it occupies the space between, where vision is possible but partial. The "veil with eyes that waver through" (003-004) extends this motif, suggesting that perception itself is mediated by membranes that both reveal and distort. Meredith's epistemology, as expressed through ocular imagery, acknowledges the incompleteness of mortal sight while insisting on its necessity: "And lets our mortal eyes receive / The inner light" (003-045).

### Eyes of Judgment and Desire

Meredith endows eyes with moral and emotional agency throughout the collection. The "reproachful eyes, and lips / Of lemon-poisoned scorn" (003-102) make the eye an instrument of ethical evaluation, while "the very eye of passion drowsed by excess" (005-142) presents the eye as the seat of desire itself. In "The Test of Manhood," the hero's worth is measured through the female gaze: "it is he rightly manful in her eyes" (004-153). The eye here is not merely observing but constituting — it brings value into being through the act of regarding.

### The Divine and Natural Eye

Meredith moves between the singular mortal eye and the multiplied divine vision. The figure "He of the myriad eyes, the myriad hands" (004-173) suggests an omniscient perspective that contrasts with human limitation. Yet Nature too possesses the power of sight: being "Quickened of Nature's eye and ear" (003-320) describes a mode of perception that transcends individual sensory capacity. The eye becomes the symbol of a deeper attunement to the world, where seeing and understanding converge.

### Eyes in Classical Context

Meredith's Homeric engagement brings the eye into epic register. The direct address "Menelaos, thee too, thou dog-eyed!" (017-013) borrows from Homeric epithet tradition, where the modifier attached to the eye defines moral character. The goatherds "eyeing their vast flocks of goats" (017-083) presents the pastoral gaze — watchful, possessive, routine. These classical deployments ground Meredith's more abstract ocular philosophy in the concrete visual world of epic and pastoral poetry.
