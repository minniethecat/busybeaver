## Overview

The seed in Meredith's poetry functions as the ultimate figure of potential — life compressed into minimal form, awaiting the conditions of its unfolding. Unlike the flower, which displays achieved beauty, or the vine, which demonstrates active persistence, the seed represents the phase of pure latency: what has not yet become but carries within itself everything it might be.

Meredith deploys the seed across a range of registers — cosmic, agricultural, ethical — each time emphasizing a different aspect of its paradox. The seed is "in blindness, as an earthy seed" (004-115), buried without sight of what it will become. It is scattered into soil (012-007) as a figure for what might have been. It "must" grow (016-064) under no external governance. And it holds "golden promise over leagues of seed" (016-226), a future distributed across measureless ground.

Through these variations, the seed becomes Meredith's emblem for the human condition itself — planted in darkness, governed by an inner imperative, reaching toward a harvest it cannot see.

## In the Poems

### Buried in Blindness: The Earthy Seed

Meredith's most compressed image of human limitation appears in the simile "in blindness, as an earthy seed" (004-115). The seed is placed in the ground — "earthy" linking it to the soil that both buries and nourishes it — and it cannot see. It does not know what it will become, what the world above ground looks like, or whether the conditions for germination will arrive.

The blindness is both literal (the buried seed receives no light) and metaphysical (the seed has no knowledge of its own destiny). Meredith uses the seed here not as a figure of growth but of radical uncertainty — the condition of being committed to a process whose outcome is unknowable from within.

### Might Have Been: The Scattered Seed of Counterfactual

In a passage reflecting on what could have been, Meredith imagines: "might we have been unto seed under soil, or have scattered" (012-007). The "might have been" frames the seed as a counterfactual — what we did not become, what was not planted, what was scattered rather than gathered.

The verb "scattered" carries the suggestion of dispersal without purpose, sowing without harvest. To be scattered is to be seed without soil, potential without the conditions of realization. Meredith's counterfactual seed becomes a meditation on unrealized human possibility — all the lives not lived, the capacities not cultivated, the futures not planted.

### Governed by None: The Seed's Inner Law

Meredith articulates the seed's autonomy in a striking negative formulation: "none governs, and must the seeds" (016-064). The syntax reverses expected agency: rather than "the seeds must grow," Meredith writes "must the seeds" — the compulsion comes first, the subject second. The seeds do not choose to grow; they are chosen by growth itself.

"None governs" establishes the seed's radical independence. Unlike the warrior who serves or the king who commands, the seed answers to no external authority. Its imperative is internal, biological, irresistible — a model of action that Meredith contrasts with the social constraints governing human behavior. The seed does what it must, and in doing so, is free.

### Golden Promise over Leagues: The Distributed Future

Meredith's most visionary seed image reaches across space: "golden promise over leagues of seed" (016-226). The adjective "golden" imports the alchemical transformation of time and sun — what was brown and dormant becomes gold through the process of ripening. "Promise" converts biological potential into ethical category: the seed does not merely contain a plant but makes a commitment to the future.

"Leagues of seed" extends the image across distance. The promise is not concentrated in a single seed but distributed across an entire planted field — a collective future, each seed bearing its share of the golden yield. Meredith's seed-field becomes a figure for civilization itself: distributed promise, ripening across generations, governed by no single hand.
