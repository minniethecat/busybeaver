## Biography

Homer (c. 8th century BCE) is the legendary author to whom the ancient Greek epic poems the *Iliad* and the *Odyssey* are attributed. Nothing certain is known of his life — his birthplace was claimed by numerous Greek cities, and traditions describe him as a blind bard.

Modern scholarship regards the Homeric poems as the culmination of a long oral tradition, composed and refined over generations before being committed to writing. The *Iliad*, set during the Trojan War, centers on the wrath of Achilles and its consequences. The *Odyssey* follows the hero Odysseus's ten-year journey home after the fall of Troy.

Together, these epics form the foundation of Western literature, establishing narrative patterns, character types, and poetic techniques that have influenced writers for nearly three millennia. From Chapman's Renaissance translations through Pope's Augustan couplets to Tennyson's Victorian classical verse, each generation has reinterpreted Homer for its own age.

## Works

- *The Iliad* — epic of the Trojan War, focusing on the wrath of Achilles
- *The Odyssey* — epic of Odysseus's return from Troy
- The *Homeric Hymns* — a collection of hymns to various gods, traditionally attributed to Homer

## In This Wiki

Meredith's longest single contribution to *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is *Fragments of the Iliad in English Hexameter Verse* (chapter 17), a substantial translation running to over 500 lines. The translation covers several passages from the *Iliad*:

- **The Invective of Achilles** (Iliad I.149) — Achilles's furious response to Agamemnon's demand for Briseis (017-001)
- **Marshalling of the Achaians** — the catalogue of Greek forces preparing for battle
- **The Duel of Paris and Menelaus** — the single combat to decide the war
- **Achilles Praying to Zeus** — the hero's plea for his mother Thetis to intercede

Meredith chose the English hexameter — a six-beat line — in an attempt to capture something of Homer's original dactylic hexameter rhythm. This was an ambitious and unusual metrical choice, following the recommendations of Matthew Arnold for rendering Homer's "rapidity" and "plainness of thought" into English verse.

The Iliad fragments serve multiple purposes: they connect Meredith's philosophical poetry to the classical epic tradition, they demonstrate the continuity of themes such as war and honor, and they position Meredith within the Victorian debate about translating Homer.

Key divine figures from the Homeric world receive extended treatment in this wiki: [[ares]], the war-god whose imprisonment Meredith dramatizes in *The Cageing of Ares* (chapter 5), and [[zeus]], the Olympian sovereign whose counsels govern the fate of armies in the Iliad fragments.
