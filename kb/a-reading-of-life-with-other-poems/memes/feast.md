## Overview

The feast in Meredith's poetry is not a specific meal but a state of radiant abundance — a condition in which beauty, nature, and satisfaction converge in a moment of celebratory presence. The word appears in two sharply contrasted settings: the cosmic feast of "With the Persuader," where Nature's couples face East "Reflecting radiance, even when from her feast / Their outstretched arms brown deserts disunite" (003-227–229), and the domestic feast of "Arachne," where the spider builds her web "Inside my chalet-porch" and makes it "her feast" (015-007). The movement from cosmic to domestic registers the feast's range: it is at once the most expansive and the most intimate of Meredith's images of fulfillment.

Unlike the related images of harvest (seasonal, cyclical) and fruit (organic, consumable), the feast emphasizes the social and the radiant — it is a shared condition, an overflow that includes the observer in its abundance. To be at the feast is to be in the presence of a generosity that exceeds mere sustenance and becomes celebration.

## In the Poems

### The Radiant Feast: Abundance Across Distance

The richest single passage appears in "With the Persuader," at the moment when Nature's couples are described: "She bids her couples face full East, / Reflecting radiance, even when from her feast / Their outstretched arms brown deserts disunite, / The lion-haunted thickets hold apart" (003-227–230). The feast here is Nature's own — the abundance she offers to her paired creatures — and its defining quality is radiance. The couples at the feast "reflect" light, they face East (the direction of sunrise, of illumination, of beginning), and their radiance persists even across separation.

The "brown deserts" and "lion-haunted thickets" that "disunite" and "hold apart" the feasters are the obstacles that distance places between those who belong together. Yet the radiance of the feast survives this separation — the light reflected from the feast crosses the deserts, penetrates the thickets. The feast is thus a principle of connection across distance, a shared radiance that unites what geography divides.

### The Spider's Feast: Domestic Abundance

In "Arachne," the feast is miniaturized to the dimensions of a chalet porch: "Sagaciously her home she chose / For visits that would never close; / Inside my chalet-porch her feast / Plucked all the winds but chill North-east" (015-005–008). The spider's feast is her web — the structure she builds to capture her prey, but also the site of her dwelling, her hospitality, her abundant presence. The feast "plucks all the winds" — gathers them, selects them, tastes them — and excludes only the "chill North-east," the wind of cold and denial.

"Her banquet in her victory" (015-040) confirms the identification of feast with triumph. The spider's web is both her means of capture and her celebratory meal — the feast is the form that victory takes when it is complete. The domestic register of this feast — chalet, porch, web — makes it available as a model for smaller, more intimate forms of abundance. One does not need a cosmic banquet; the spider's feast is sufficient, if it is a feast of victory.

### Hunger and Fruit: The Feast's Precondition

The feast presupposes hunger. "Unmated; she, our hunger and our fruit" (003-033) compresses the entire logic of desire and satisfaction into a single line. Nature — "she" — is both the hunger and the fruit, the need and its fulfillment. The feast is the moment when hunger meets fruit, when the longing the beloved generates is also the longing she satisfies.

"Consume the fruit in bloom; not disobeyed" (003-097) adds the dimension of permission. The fruit is to be consumed "in bloom" — at its peak, in its fullness — and this consumption is not disobedience but obedience, not transgression but fulfillment. The feast is authorized abundance, the permitted consumption of what has ripened to its moment.

### The Sweet Cup: Pleasure's Invitation

The feast's sensory register extends to taste and sweetness: "For Pleasure witched him her sweet cup to drain" (004-039) captures the seductive quality of the feast's offer. The cup is "sweet" and the verb is "drain" — total consumption, the refusal to leave anything behind. "He tastes, as doth the bridegroom rich in youth" (004-212) links the feast's tasting to the nuptial — the bridegroom tasting the richness of the marriage feast, the youth that makes the tasting possible. And "Like flower-bell to honey-bee" (003-274) provides the natural model for the feast's attraction: the flower offers sweetness, the bee receives it, and both are fulfilled in the exchange.

The feast, in this sensory register, is not merely seen (radiance) but tasted, drunk, consumed. It engages the full sensorium of satisfaction, making the point that abundance is not an abstract condition but a bodily experience — something taken into the mouth, the throat, the self.
