## Overview

God in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is more often an adjective than a noun. The collection speaks less of God than of the "godlike" and the "godhead" — divinity dispersed into qualities rather than concentrated in a person. Meredith writes in the long shadow of Victorian doubt, after Darwin and after the Higher Criticism, and his poems register that crisis without succumbing to simple atheism. The divine survives as an attribute of human and natural excellence: the "godlike leaps" (003-204), the "godlike fury" (005-049), the "godhead visible" (004-046).

This adjectival theology is Meredith's solution to the problem of belief in a disenchanted age. If God cannot be affirmed as a being, divinity can still be recognized as a quality — a way of being in the world marked by intensity, purpose, and the capacity to exceed oneself. The poems thus preserve the vocabulary of the sacred while relocating its referent from heaven to earth.

## In the Poems

The divine appears across the collection in three principal modes:

### As Diffused Godhead

Meredith repeatedly invokes "godhead" not as a person but as a state or quality. "Resisting in her godhead nature's truth" (003-151) attributes godhead to a female figure who resists — divinity as defiance rather than submission. "On godhead visible, on godhead scree—" (004-046) breaks off mid-word, as if the full name of the divine exceeds what language can capture. The word "godhead" itself — rather than "God" — is Meredith's preferred term, etymologically a state or condition (-head, cognate with -hood) rather than an entity.

### As Human Excellence

The "godlike" is Meredith's bridge between the divine and the human. "A smile, and he is up on godlike leaps" (003-204) makes the godlike a matter of movement — a quality of action rather than essence. One does not need to be a god to leap like one. Similarly, "His godlike fury startled from amaze" (005-049) locates the divine in emotional intensity, in a fury that breaks through the paralysis of amazement. The godlike is accessible to human beings willing to risk extremity.

### As Fragile Achievement

The divine is precarious in Meredith's world. "Lest it might be their godhead undermined" (005-012) acknowledges that godhead can be lost — it is not a permanent endowment but a condition that must be maintained against threat. The same note sounds in "This godlike — as the rock in ocean stands" (004-172), where the comparison to rock suggests that the godlike must achieve rock's stability in an ocean of flux. Divinity is not given but achieved and defended.
