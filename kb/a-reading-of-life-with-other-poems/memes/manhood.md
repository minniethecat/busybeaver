## Overview

Manhood in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is not a birthright but an attainment.

The title of the fourth poem in the collection, "The Test of Manhood," signals the concept's centrality: manhood is what must be tested, what must be earned, and what can be failed. Across the opening sequence, Meredith traces a progression from the "beast in manhood tame" (002-039) to the fully competent self who "in manhood must he find his competence" (004-131). The arc is neither linear nor guaranteed; manhood is the prize of a struggle that many lose.

Meredith's manhood is explicitly a middle state. It stands between the beastly and the divine, between the "goodly beast" of instinct and the "spiritual food" of the "clear mind" (004-132). To be a man is to hold these two forces in tension — neither denying the animal nor abandoning the spirit, but making both serviceable to a unified self.

This conception is distinctively Victorian in its emphasis on character as achievement, yet it departs from Victorian moralism in refusing to sentimentalize the process. Manhood is found through "sweat of anguish" (004-033), through humiliation and weeping (003-202), not through piety or self-discipline alone.

## In the Poems

### The Middle State

In "With the Huntress," Meredith establishes manhood as a mediating term between beast and something higher. The line "Beast, and beast in manhood tame" (002-039) places manhood in apposition to both beast-states: there is the raw beast, and there is the beast that has been brought under control — "tame" — through the discipline of manhood. The syntax is compressed to the point of paradox; manhood is the site where the beast is both present and governed.

The Huntress and the Persuader represent the two poles between which manhood must navigate. "Not far those two great Powers of Nature speed / Disciple steps on earth when sole they lead; / Nor either points for us the way of flame" (004-021–023). Neither instinct alone nor persuasion alone can produce the complete man; each, followed exclusively, leads away from "the way of flame" — the path of integrated selfhood.

### Humiliation as Knowledge

The Persuader's role in the formation of manhood involves a necessary humiliation. In "With the Persuader," the goddess-figure "reads him when his humbled manhood weeps / To her invoked" (003-202–203). The verb "reads" is crucial: the Persuader does not comfort the weeping man but interprets him, extracting knowledge from his abasement.

Humiliation here is not mere shame but exposure — the moment when the aspiring self is "read" by a force greater than itself. The weeping of manhood is a kind of unmaking that precedes remaking. What the man loses in this moment is the illusion of self-sufficiency; what he gains is the beginning of true competence, which can only be built on the foundation of self-knowledge.

The passage echoes the broader Meredithian theme that wisdom comes from "vile relapsing" (004-236) rather than from unbroken success. Manhood, like wisdom, is "spun" from failure.

### Competence as the Mark

The culminating definition arrives in "The Test of Manhood": "In manhood must he find his competence; / In his clear mind the spiritual food" (004-131–132). The word "competence" carries its older sense of sufficiency — what is adequate, what meets the demands of life without excess or deficiency.

Manhood as competence is a strikingly unromantic ideal. It is not heroism, not transcendence, not the conquest of passion but its governance. The "clear mind" that provides "spiritual food" is the mind that has passed through the "sweat of anguish in that strife / To hold them fast conjoined within him still" (004-033–034) — the strife of holding beast and spirit, instinct and reason, in productive tension rather than mutual destruction.

The phrase "must he find" is imperative: competence is not conferred but discovered through effort. The verb "find" rather than "achieve" or "earn" suggests that competence is latent in the human condition, waiting to be uncovered by those who undertake the test.

### The Test as Ongoing Condition

Throughout the sequence, Meredith suggests that the test of manhood is never definitively passed. The man who wavers — "marvel not he wavered if at whiles / The forward step met frowns, the backward smiles" (004-037–038) — is not condemned but understood. The test is the condition of manhood itself, not a gate to be crossed once.

This open-endedness distinguishes Meredith's manhood from simpler Victorian ideals of achieved character. Manhood is the perpetual work of holding opposites together, and "his task to hold them both in breast, and yield / Their dues to each, and of their war be field" (004-025–026). To be the field of war between opposing forces, and yet to remain whole — this is manhood as Meredith defines it.
