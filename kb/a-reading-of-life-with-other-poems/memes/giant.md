## Overview

The word "giant" appears seven times across six poems in Meredith's corpus, spanning mythological, natural, and psychological registers. In classical myth, the Giants were a primordial race born from Earth, defeated by the Olympians — and Meredith draws on this tradition while subtly revising it. His giants are not merely antagonists to divine order but figures for a kind of power that is simultaneously threatening and generative: the "giant power" that lifts the breast (002-069), the "giant cubs" who gambol in creative play (005-051), the "giant hag-fiend" of nightmare (014-024).

Across these varied appearances, the giant represents what is older, larger, and less refined than the human — the ancestral force that civilization has not eliminated but only overlaid. Meredith consistently implies that this force is not to be despised but acknowledged, channeled, and in some sense preserved.

## In the Poems

### Giant Power: The Breast Lifted

The earliest and most affirmative use appears in "With the Pursuader" (002-069): "Lifts the breast to giant power." The syntax places the giant not as an external antagonist but as a quality of power that elevates the human breast — the seat of emotion, courage, and vital force. To be lifted to "giant power" is to be enlarged beyond ordinary human scale, to participate in something older and stronger than the civilized self. The implication is that such power is accessible, even desirable — not a threat to be feared but a height to be attained.

### The Race of Giants: Vanished but Not Extinct

Meredith explicitly addresses the mythological tradition in a compressed line: "No race of giants!" (003-242). The exclamation mark carries both denial and regret — there is no race of giants *now*, but the very assertion registers their absence as significant. The line continues: "In the woman's veins" — the giant inheritance has not vanished but migrated inward, into the blood. The giant is no longer a separate species striding across the landscape; it has become a quality within, carried in the veins of women (and by implication, of all humans). This is Meredith's evolutionary reading of myth: the giant is not dead but internalized.

A related passage invokes the giants' creative aspect: "The giant cubs, who gambolled and who s…" (005-051). The word "cubs" domesticates the giant, rendering it young, playful, still growing. And they "gambolled" — played, frolicked — suggesting that even primordial power has its joyful, unthreatening mode. The truncated line (the snippet breaks off at "s…") is suggestive: whatever the giant cubs also did remains incomplete, as if the archaic record itself is partial.

### The Giant Brood: Creation and Threat

Meredith draws a distinction between two kinds of giant inheritance: "Not of the giant brood, who did create" (005-121). The "giant brood" who "did create" are the creative giants — those whose power was not merely destructive but generative, world-making. To be "not of" this brood is to belong to a lesser lineage. The passage implies a hierarchy: the creative giants are the highest, and those who do not descend from them lack something essential.

### The Giant as Nightmare: Terror and Awe

The most disturbing giant image appears in a visionary passage: "It seemed a giant hag-fiend, churning spite" (014-024). Here the giant is feminine ("hag"), demonic ("fiend"), and actively malevolent ("churning spite"). The verb "churning" suggests continuous, agitated motion — a kind of cosmic搅动 that never settles. This is the giant at its most archaic and terrifying: not the playful cubs of 005-051 but the nightmare that haunts the edge of vision.

A similarly ominous note sounds in "Else in a giant's grasp until the end" (010-003), where the giant's grasp is a fate to be endured, a captivity that lasts "until the end." The giant here is force without mercy, the crushing hand of the archaic.

### Giant Limb: The Pride of Scale

In a passage concerned with physical prowess, Meredith notes the desire "to race past the pride in giant limb" (004-166). The "giant limb" is a synecdoche for physical power on a superhuman scale, and "pride" in such limbs is the natural accompaniment of physical superiority. To "race past" this pride is to move beyond the merely physical — to acknowledge giant strength but to outrun the vanity that accompanies it. The image encapsulates Meredith's ambivalence: giant power is real and admirable, but it must be transcended, not rested in.
