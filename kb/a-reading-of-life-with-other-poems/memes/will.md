## Overview

The will in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the driving force of human agency — what makes action possible and what must itself be made serviceable to reason.

Meredith's treatment of the will spans the full range from cosmic force to intimate submission. Nature herself operates through will: the goddess-figure uses "natural snares to make her will supreme" (003-063). The human will is called to be "submissive to his will / Along the road of life" (004-035–036) — submissive not to external command but to the higher self that reason discerns. And in the Homeric fragments, the will appears as the collective cry of an army: "Loud will the cry for Achilles burst from the sons of Achaia" (017-047).

What unites these diverse manifestations is the concept of directed force. Will is not mere energy — it is energy with a vector, energy aimed at an end. The moral question for Meredith is not whether the will is strong but whether it is aimed at the right object. A misdirected will is more dangerous than a weak one.

The will's relationship to the other faculties forms a recurring thematic structure across the collection: will must be "submissive" to reason, must govern passion without extinguishing it, must drive desire toward its proper end, and must find its competence in manhood. Will is the executive — it does not set the ends but it provides the motion.

## In the Poems

### The Supreme Will of Nature

The collection's treatment of will begins at the cosmic level. In "With the Persuader," the goddess-figure — who is both Nature and something beyond Nature — operates through will: she deploys "natural snares to make her will supreme" (003-063). The phrase "make her will supreme" identifies will with sovereignty; to have a will is to seek mastery, and the goddess's mastery is achieved through "snares" — through attraction and capture rather than through force.

This feminine model of will-as-attraction contrasts with the masculine model of will-as-force that appears elsewhere in the collection. The goddess does not compel; she allures. Her will is supreme not because it overpowers opposition but because it makes opposition irrelevant — those who follow her snares do so willingly, even eagerly.

The erotic dimension of this model is explicit: "The wooer, and all roseate will she burst" (003-071). The will that "bursts" is not the goddess's but the wooer's, released by her presence. The goddess's will is made supreme not by imposing her own desire but by awakening desire in others — a paradox of power that operates through elicitation rather than command.

Later in the same poem, the darker side of this dynamic appears: "Be snared as hunter, she will tempt pursuit" (003-249). The reversal is elegant — the hunter becomes the snared, and the goddess "tempts pursuit" rather than fleeing it. Will in this context is the structure of a chase whose outcome is predetermined by the superior craft of the pursued.

### The Submissive Will

The key moral claim about the will appears in "The Test of Manhood," where the aspiring self must hold opposing forces in tension: "Submissive to his will / Along the road of life" (004-035–036). The phrase is deliberately ambiguous: whose will is submissive to whose? The immediate context suggests that the warring forces within the man — beast and spirit, instinct and reason — are made "submissive to his will." The will is the sovereign that commands the internal multiplicity.

But a deeper reading suggests that "his will" must itself be submissive — to the "road of life," to the demands of right reason, to the "clear mind" that provides "spiritual food" (004-132). The will is both master and servant: it masters the passions but serves the reason.

The poem explores what happens when the will wavers: "That she will cast him drunken down the steep" (004-160). The pronoun "she" refers to Nature or Pleasure, and the image is of a will that has lost its hold — the man cast "drunken down the steep" because his will failed to maintain its grip. The will is not eliminated by this failure; it is overwhelmed, swamped by forces it should have governed.

The counter-image follows immediately: "But life gone breathless will she reinstate" (004-188). The same "she" that casts the will-less man down will also restore him — but only after the fall. The will's submission is a discipline that prevents catastrophe; without it, restoration is possible but costly.

### The Mighty Will

A striking alternative vision of the will appears in "Written for the Charing Cross Album," where the speaker reflects: "I passed, and saw the mighty will / Earth will not hear her children's wail" (012-013, 012-024). Here the "mighty will" is an impersonal force — perhaps the will of history, of nature, or of the collective — that is indifferent to individual suffering.

Earth's refusal to "hear her children's wail" is the cosmic form of the will's indifference. The "mighty will" that the speaker "passed" and "saw" is not a personal agency but a structural fact about existence: the world does not bend to human desire, and the will must operate within constraints it did not choose.

This poem connects to the later meditation in "Forest History," where the speaker notes "The reverence that ne'er will overrun / Its due proportion" (014-143). The will that "ne'er will overrun" is the will governed by reverence — a will that knows its limits and respects proportion. The double negative ("ne'er will overrun") makes the point by negation: the absence of excess is itself a positive quality of the properly governed will.

### The Will to Mastery

The darkest vision of the will appears in "Foresight and Patience," where the speaker describes those who seek power: "to o'ermatch and have mankind at will" (016-137). Here "at will" means "at one's disposal" — the will of the tyrant who would reduce all others to instruments of his own volition.

This is the shadow form of the "mighty will" — will that has broken free of right reason and seeks only its own expansion. The phrase "have mankind at will" captures the nightmare of unchecked agency: a single will absorbing all others into itself.

The poem's response is not to condemn will as such but to insist on its proper subordination. The masses who are "indifferent" — the "massed indifferents" — will eventually "learn to quake" (016-174) when they realize what unchecked will can do. The will must be governed not because it is weak but because it is strong, and strength without direction is destruction.

### The Victor's Boast and the Cancelled Blessing

"At the Close" offers a compressed image of the will's triumph: "Till on a day will ring the victor's boast" (013-006). The "victor's boast" is the voice of the will that has achieved its end — but the word "boast" carries moral ambiguity. Is the boast justified or hollow? Has the victory been won through right means or through expedience?

The question is left open, but the poem's placement as the closing lyric of the main sequence suggests a note of qualified triumph. The will can achieve victory, but the boast that follows victory must be examined.

"Foresight and Patience" offers the most sobering judgment: "And cancelled blessings will the current length / Of our supineness show" (016-230). The "cancelled blessings" are the goods that were possible but not achieved — the opportunities that the will, through "supineness" (laziness, passivity), allowed to pass. The will's failure is not primarily a failure of action but a failure of attention; we lose blessings not because we choose badly but because we fail to choose at all.
