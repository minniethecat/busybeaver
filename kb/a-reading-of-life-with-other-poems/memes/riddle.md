## Overview

Meredith uses the word "riddle" at two critical moments in the corpus, both in poems that address the relationship between human perception and nature's deeper truths. In Meredith's poetic vocabulary, a riddle is not a trivial puzzle but a fundamental epistemological challenge: nature's appearances are savage and inscrutable on the surface, yet they contain a hidden coherence accessible to those who read correctly. The concept stands at the intersection of Meredith's natural philosophy and his poetic method — both nature and poetry require interpretation, and both reward the attentive reader with a glimpse of order beneath apparent chaos.

The two occurrences span distinct poetic contexts: one addressing the persuasive power of beauty ("With the Persuader," 003) and one reflecting on nature's apparent cruelty ("The Test of Manhood," 004). Together they articulate a consistent position: the riddle is not an obstacle to knowledge but its very form.

## In the Poems

### Beauty as Solver of Riddles

In "With the Persuader" (003), Meredith frames the riddle in relation to feminine beauty's power of persuasion. The speaker reflects that beauty "may solve the riddle, that she lends" (003-216) — suggesting that the persuasive force of beauty offers not just aesthetic pleasure but cognitive resolution. The verb "lends" is significant: beauty does not own the solution but offers it provisionally, as a gift or loan, to the perceiver. The riddle, in this context, is whatever perplexity the human mind brings to its encounter with the world; beauty, by captivating attention and stilling the analytic impulse, allows the riddle to dissolve.

The passage places the riddle within Meredith's larger meditation on the relationship between eros and understanding. Beauty does not argue; it persuades by presence. And in persuading, it solves — not by analysis but by a kind of perceptual reorientation that makes the problem disappear rather than resolving it discursively.

### Nature's Savage Riddles Kindly Solved

The second occurrence appears in "The Test of Manhood" (004), where Meredith makes his most compressed statement about nature's deeper character: "Shows Nature's savage riddles kindly solved" (004-144). The line is a thesis in miniature. Nature's riddles are "savage" — fierce, untamed, apparently cruel — but their solution is "kindly," both in the sense of benevolent and in the older sense of natural, proper to the kind.

The adjective "kindly" does the philosophical work. It suggests that the solution to nature's apparent cruelty is not to deny the cruelty but to see it as belonging to a larger pattern of natural kinship — a pattern in which what appears savage from one angle is revealed as caring from another. This is Meredith's evolutionary theodicy in a single line: nature's processes, rightly understood, tend toward the good, but only when read as a whole rather than isolated in their savage moments.

### The Interpretive Stance

Across both occurrences, the riddle concept implies an interpretive stance rather than a solved equation. The point is not that riddles have answers — they do — but that the answers are revealed through a mode of attention (beauty's persuasion, nature's kindly unfolding) rather than through analytic aggression. To solve a riddle in Meredith's sense is not to crack a code but to see differently.
