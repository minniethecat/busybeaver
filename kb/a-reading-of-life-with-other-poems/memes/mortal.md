## Overview

Mortal appears eleven times in Meredith's poetry, threading through chapters 3, 5, 12, 14, and 17, and in every instance it names a boundary. The mortal is defined by what it is not — not immortal, not divine, not eternal — yet Meredith's mortals are never merely defined by lack. They receive divine vision (003-045), contend with Love's force (003-188), are granted gifts from the gods (017-181), and produce concepts of earth's divinity from within their finite frame (005-104). The mortal condition is limitation that reaches beyond itself.

Meredith's handling of the mortal-immortal axis draws on classical sources — Homeric echoes in chapter 17's divine-mortal naming distinction (017-148) — but the governing tension is philosophical rather than mythological. The mortal mind cannot escape its finitude, but it can conceive the infinite. The mortal frame suffers diseases that find "never healing" (012-004), but the mortal eyes can receive the vision that the poem offers. Meredith's mortal is the site of a permanent double movement: descent into bodily limitation and ascent toward immortal understanding.

## In the Poems

### The Mortal Gaze: Perception and Limitation

Meredith's first mortal moment is a scene of vision granted: "And lets our mortal eyes receive" (003-045). The line is concessive — the mortal eyes are permitted to see what they could not, by their own nature, apprehend. The verb "lets" establishes the hierarchy: a higher power grants, the mortal receives. What the mortal eyes receive is not specified in the line itself but emerges from the surrounding passage as a vision of divine order mediated through natural beauty.

The mortal gaze is not condemned for its limitation but dignified by its receptivity. The mortal cannot see as the gods see, but being mortal does not mean being blind. Meredith's phrasing — "mortal eyes" rather than "human eyes" or "finite eyes" — keeps the metaphysical frame in view. These are eyes that will close in death, and that very transience is what makes the vision granted to them significant.

### Mortal and Immortal: The Defining Contrast

Throughout the corpus, Meredith deploys "mortal" in explicit contrast with "immortal," and the contrast structures a philosophy of human aspiration. At 003-263, the immortal smile belongs to a goddess — "Doth she rely for her immortal smile" — and the mortal dwarf receives it in submission (003-246). At 005-060, immortals are defied by mortal will — "Vowed to defy Immortals" — and at 005-117, the mortal mind pushes "Up to the farthest bourne: immortal still." The boundary between mortal and immortal is always under pressure from the mortal side.

The most compressed statement of this tension comes at 005-104: "The mortal mind's concept of earth's divine." The mortal mind cannot be divine, but it can conceive divinity. The concept itself — the mental representation of what lies beyond the mental — is a mortal product, and Meredith does not resolve whether this makes it less true or more remarkable. The mortal mind builds what it cannot inhabit.

### The Mortal Body: Suffering and the Divine Gift

Two later occurrences shift the emphasis from metaphysical contrast to bodily experience. At 012-004, the mortal is identified with disease: "They of our mortal diseases find never healing." The mortal body is the body that sickens, that cannot be cured, that carries within it the principle of its own dissolution. The phrase "never healing" is absolute — there is no remedy for mortality itself.

Yet at 017-181, the mortal receives what the gods bestow: "gave we to Peleus you, to a mortal." The structure is one of gift and descent — the divine comes down to the mortal, and the mortal is worthy of the gift precisely because the gift crosses the boundary. The mortal in chapter 17 is the recipient of what the immortal world chooses to share. Between the incurable disease of chapter 12 and the unmerited gift of chapter 17, Meredith's mortal spans the full range: creature of pathos and object of grace.
