## Overview

The deed in Meredith's poetry is the irreducible unit of moral action — the point at which interior virtue becomes exterior fact, visible to others and to the Muse who records it. Unlike mere intention, which remains private and unjudged, the deed enters the world and must answer for itself. Meredith traces the deed across a spectrum: from the quotidian ("daily deeds," 004-170) to the cosmogonic ("the serious naked deed / Had done the deed to strike the Titan host," 005-030–037), from the inspired ("deeds inspired of God," 012-032) to the nocturnal and hidden ("what deeds of night," 015-097). Across this range, the deed is never merely an event; it is always an index of the doer's quality, the visible signature of invisible character.

## In the Poems

### Daily Deeds and the Evening Muse

Meredith's most tender treatment of the deed places it in the domestic diurnal cycle: "In daily deeds, and she their evening Muse" (004-170). The Muse here is not the inspirer of epic song but the witness of everyday action — she attends to what people actually do, day by day, and her presence transforms the ordinary deed into material worthy of reflection. The word "daily" demystifies the deed: heroism is not reserved for the exceptional moment but distributed across the ordinary acts that compose a life. The Muse who comes in the evening to review the day's deeds is Meredith's figure for moral self-consciousness, the reflective capacity that turns action into character.

### The Naked Deed and the Titan Strike

At the opposite extreme stands the cosmogonic deed — the primordial act of violence that reshapes the order of things. Meredith describes how the deed was done "to strike the Titan host" (005-037), a deed preceded by an earlier characterization as "the serious naked deed" (005-030). The adjective "naked" strips the deed of ornament, justification, and context — it is pure act, irreducible to anything but itself. The repetition of "deed" across two lines ("the serious naked deed, / Had done the deed") enacts the deed's own structure: the first occurrence names the category, the second confirms the execution. This is the deed at its most archaic — the founding violence that establishes a new order, the Titanomachy refigured as the primal ethical event.

### Deeds Inspired and Deeds Concealed

Between daily and cosmogonic, Meredith maps two further modalities. The "deeds inspired of God" that "outstep a Paul" (012-032) present the deed as transcendent in origin — not the doer's own achievement but a force working through the doer. The verb "outstep" captures the deed's capacity to exceed the doer's normal range, to carry action beyond the boundary of the self. In contrast, the "deeds of night" (015-097) invoke the hidden act, the deed performed away from witness and therefore from judgment. The nocturnal deed is the test case for Meredith's ethics: if character is measured by deeds, then deeds performed in darkness, where no Muse watches, are the truest measure.

### The Deed's Reflection

In a culminating image, Meredith describes how the deed "sits in a smooth lake upon the deed" (016-227) — the deed reflecting upon itself, action mirrored in stillness. The "smooth lake" is the mind after action, the reflective surface that receives the deed's image and holds it for contemplation. The doubled "deed" (the deed sits upon the deed) suggests that every action generates its own contemplative double, that doing and reflecting-on-doing are inseparable moments of the same moral process.
