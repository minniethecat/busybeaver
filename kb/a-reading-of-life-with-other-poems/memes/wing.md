## Overview

Wings appear throughout Meredith's poetry as emblems of aspiration, freedom, and the natural world's capacity for movement beyond human limitation. From the "eagle wings" (002-055) that sweep across elevated heights to the "lame wings" (005-116) that struggle against wintry adversity, wing imagery charts a spectrum from transcendent flight to thwarted ambition. Meredith deploys wings in mythological, avian, and metaphysical registers: they belong to birds, to divine messengers, and — metaphorically — to human hopes and artistic endeavors. The nestling "still its wings untried" (004-083) captures a developmental arc that recurs across the collection: wings must be grown, tested, and sometimes broken before they can bear weight.

## In the Poems

### Wings of Aspiration and Transcendence

The most elevated wing imagery appears in the collection's framing poems, where "High o'er sweep of eagle wings" (002-055) establishes flight as the vantage point of visionary perception. The eagle's wing connotes sovereignty, clarity, and distance from earthly concerns. A complementary image in "The Test of Manhood" presents something "demanding birth to light and wing" (004-207), where wing is the final term of a developmental triad — birth, illumination, flight — that maps onto Meredith's philosophy of progressive spiritual evolution. The dove that "wings to nest" with "joy" (009-008) offers a gentler variant: winged movement as the fulfillment of a homing instinct, flight in service of domestic return rather than heroic aspiration.

### Wings in Struggle and Limitation

Counterpoint to the soaring eagle is the motif of damaged or ineffective flight. "Clap lame wings across a wintry haze" (005-116) condenses futility into a single image: the wings are present, the will to fly is intact, but the body cannot deliver. This line, embedded in a passage of social critique, uses crippled flight as a figure for unjust limitation — those who "reap, not sowing" (005-107) are the ones who clip others' wings. The nestling with "still its wings untried" (004-083) is not yet broken but not yet capable, suspended between potential and actual flight. In "The Thrush in February," the thrush "flew on it, then folded wings" (006-031), a moment of voluntary descent after achievement — wings folded not in defeat but in satisfied arrival.

### Wings in the Homeric Poems

The later, Homerically-inflected poems expand wing imagery into epic registers. "Multitudinous winged flocks" (017-061) transforms the avian into a military metaphor: the Trojan plain fills with warriors as the sky fills with birds, wing becoming a figure for massed, coordinated movement. In the battle scenes, the desperate hope that the Trojans might "pluck a new breath" follows an image of wings in combat context (017-119), linking wing and breath as the two means by which life sustains itself under mortal threat. The athletic contests of "The Wrestling" similarly invoke wing imagery through rapid motion: "plunging, whizzing, till his wings / webbed" (015-037, 015-038), where wings become a metaphor for the wrestler's arms in their blur of action.

### Wings and the Future

Several passages partner wing imagery with temporal projection. "In beating wing, the future I would paint" (016-173) is a direct statement of artistic method: the wing's rhythmic beat is the stroke of the poet's brush, flight forward is the movement into futurity. "Of wing shall master till men hear heart's accord" (016-034) similarly pairs winged motion with prophetic utterance — the poet's voice will carry on wing until it reaches and transforms its audience. These metapoetic usages make wing a symbol of artistic ambition itself, the medium through which poetry transcends its present moment.
