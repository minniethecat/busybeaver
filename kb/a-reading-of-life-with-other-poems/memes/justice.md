## Overview

Justice in Meredith's poetry appears at the intersection of the moral and the martial — a cry from the wounded and a demand made of the powerful. The word emerges in two sharply contrasting contexts: the prophetic register where "The gory chariot-wheel on cries for justice" (005-070) personifies the crushing engine of war as it calls forth demands for reckoning, and the Homeric register where justice is something to be "plucked from the Dardans" for a wronged king (017-012). Between these two, Meredith maps the distance between justice as a cosmic principle — something owed by the order of things — and justice as a spoil of war, claimed by the victor. The tension is never resolved; rather, Meredith lets the two meanings of justice chafe against each other, exposing the gap between the ideal and the instrumental.

## In the Poems

### The Cry for Justice Beneath the Chariot-Wheel

In the early philosophical poems, justice appears within a catalogue of the devastations wrought by an unnamed destructive force — "A desolating fire to blind the sight / With splendour built of fruitful things in ashes; / The gory chariot-wheel on cries for justice" (005-070). The chariot-wheel, gory with the blood of the crushed, is an image of overwhelming violent power. Yet the wheel itself "cries for justice" — the very instrument of destruction becomes, paradoxically, the voice demanding redress. Meredith constructs a complex moral tableau in which violence generates its own counter-claim: every act of destruction is also, implicitly, a cry for the justice that would prevent it. The passage goes on to identify this voice as "Her deepest planted and her liveliest voice, / Heard from the babe as from the broken crone," universalizing the demand for justice across the human lifespan.

### Homeric Justice as Spoils

The second major occurrence transposes justice into the epic register, where it becomes not a moral demand but a political objective. In the Homeric quarrel, the speaker reminds his antagonist that they followed him to Troy "to hearten thee, justice / Pluck from the Dardans for him, Menelaos, thee too, thou dog-eyed!" (017-012). Justice here is something to be *plucked* — the verb is agricultural, as if justice were a fruit to be harvested from the enemy's orchard. The embedding of justice within a speech of bitter recrimination — "thou dog-eyed!" — further complicates the term: the speaker claims the moral high ground of justice-seeking even as his language descends into insult. Meredith, faithful to his Homeric source, does not resolve this irony but lets it stand as a permanent feature of the human appeal to justice: it is always, in part, a mask for narrower grievances.
