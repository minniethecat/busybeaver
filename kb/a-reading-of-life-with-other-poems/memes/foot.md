## Overview

Foot in Meredith's poetry operates across several registers: as the literal means of forward motion through a landscape and through a life, as the metrical unit that gives verse its measured stride, and as a classical inheritance from Pan's goat-foot to the dust-covered footsoles of Homeric combatants. The word appears most pointedly in the journey poems where the "foot forward through the storm" (004-090) embodies the poet's ethic of resolute advance, and in the epic register of the later poems where footmen fall and footsoles bear the imprint of battle. Meredith consistently positions the foot not as the body's lowliest part but as the point of contact between will and ground — the place where intention meets terrain.

## In the Poems

### Foot as the Instrument of the Journey

The foot appears in Meredith's voyage and quest poetry as the vehicle of trust and forward motion. The poet describes one who, after long trial, places "trust in his foot forward through the storm" (004-090), a formulation that makes the foot an agent of faith rather than mere locomotion. Earlier, "Before the leader foot shall dip in stream" (003-065) positions the foot at the threshold of crossing — the moment before commitment, when the sole still hovers above the water. The "footway" upon which "the star of sky" casts its light (004-214) transforms the path into something at once earthly and celestial, the foot traversing ground that heaven illuminates. In "A Reading of Life," the speaker's "doubtful foot" that will "circle if much stirred" (016-073) introduces hesitation into the journey — the foot that questions its own advance — and thereby acknowledges that forward motion is never purely mechanical but always subject to the mind's uncertainty.

### The Classical Foot: Pan and Homer

The goat-foot of classical mythology makes a brief but vivid appearance in a poem of metamorphosis where "Goat-foot, antlers laid on neck" (002-005) introduces the chimerical body of Pan into Meredith's symbolic landscape. The goat-foot here is not merely decorative; it signals the poet's engagement with the classical tradition of hybrid divinity — the foot that is not quite human, not quite beast, and therefore capable of traversing the boundary between wild nature and cultivated art. In the epic register of the later poems, the foot becomes martial: "Foot then footmen slew, that were flying from" (017-096) places the foot in the chaos of Homeric battle, where footmen — soldiers on foot — kill and are killed. The "graze of my footsole" (017-125) introduces the body's vulnerability into the epic frame, a moment of intimate physical awareness amid the scale of heroic combat.

### Foot and Measure

Meredith, a poet deeply conscious of prosody, invites the reader to hear "foot" as a pun on the metrical unit. The forward-striving foot of the journey poems doubles as the forward motion of the verse line itself — each metrical foot a step forward through the poem's argument. When the speaker declares trust in the foot forward, the declaration is simultaneously about living and about writing: the poet trusts the meter to carry him through, foot by foot. This self-referential dimension connects Meredith's foot imagery to his broader meditation on poetic making, where the physical journey and the act of composition share a common rhythm.
