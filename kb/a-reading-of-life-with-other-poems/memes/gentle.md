## Overview

Gentle appears three times in Meredith's poetry, across chapters 3, 5, and 14, and in each occurrence the word qualifies something that might otherwise be harsh: war, restlessness, a roaring world. Meredith's "gentle" is never mere softness; it is the deliberate modulation of force, the choice to exert power without cruelty. The word's three appearances trace a widening arc — from the gentleness that imparts ease to a god, through the gentle war that must be waged daily, to the gentle maidens who flee a world too loud for them.

In the Victorian poetic tradition, "gentle" carried strong class and gender connotations — the gentlewoman, the gentleman, the gentleness expected of the weaker sex. Meredith works against these connotations without entirely escaping them. His gentleness is a quality of action, not of station; his gentle war is a paradox that resolves into a practice; his gentle maidens are refugees, not ornaments.

## In the Poems

### Gentleness that Imparts Ease

The word enters Meredith's poetic vocabulary at 003-184: "Her gentleness imparts exhaling ease." The "Her" is the Goddess or the beloved — the figure around whom the early poem's cosmology revolves — and her gentleness is not weakness but agency. It "imparts" — a verb of active transmission — and what it imparts is "exhaling ease," the release of tension as breath leaves the body. Gentleness here is therapeutic, almost medical: it treats the wound that the poem's earlier intensities have opened.

The word "exhaling" is chosen with care. Ease is not a permanent state but a rhythmic one — it comes with the out-breath and must be renewed with each cycle. Gentleness, by implication, is not a one-time gift but a sustained practice, each exhalation re-imparting the ease that the next inhalation's tension will require.

### The Gentle War: Daily Discipline

At 005-085, Meredith coins the phrase that most economically captures his paradox: "War with her, gentle war with her, each day." The repetition of "with her" frames the war as intimate and ongoing, and the qualifier "gentle" transforms war from destruction into discipline. This is not the war that kills but the war that sharpens — the daily contest with the beloved, with the goddess, with the force that the poem addresses, conducted without cruelty and sustained across time.

The phrase "each day" is the key to the gentleness. A war fought once might be brutal; a war fought daily, with the same opponent, necessarily becomes gentle — not because the stakes are lower but because the combatants know each other. Gentleness is the form that war takes when it is conducted at close quarters, over time, with respect.

### Gentle Maidens Fleeing the Roaring World

At 014-044, the word describes those who withdraw: "Where gentle maidens fled a roaring world." The line sets two sound-registers against each other — the gentle (soft, modulated, restrained) against the roaring (loud, overwhelming, chaotic). The maidens are gentle not by nature but by contrast; they are defined as gentle by the world that is not. Their flight is not cowardice but discernment — they know what they cannot survive, and they leave.

Meredith's "roaring world" is the world of epic and history, the world where lords are slain and ladies left widowed (014-077). The gentle maidens who flee it choose silence over clamor, retreat over confrontation. Whether Meredith endorses their choice or mourns it is left deliberately unresolved — the line offers description without verdict, leaving the reader to judge whether gentleness that must flee is gentleness at all.
