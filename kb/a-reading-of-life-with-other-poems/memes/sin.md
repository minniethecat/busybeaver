## Overview

Sin in Meredith's poetry is handled with a characteristic blend of inherited religious vocabulary and secular revaluation. The poet inherits the Christian framework — sin, guilt, repentance, the sinner — but deploys it within a naturalistic moral psychology where sin is less a crime against divine law than a distortion of human flourishing. "Our sins of omission" (012-002) names the ethical register directly, while "the sinner's guilt" (016-115) and the "city charged with sin" (016-121) extend the concept from the individual conscience to the collective body. Meredith neither dismisses sin as obsolete nor enforces its theological severity; instead he treats it as a structural feature of social and psychological life, a given of the human condition whose acknowledgment is the beginning of wisdom.

## In the Poems

### Sins of Omission

The most striking formulation appears in "The Thrush in February," where Meredith writes of "our sins of omission" (012-002) as something "historic within us." The phrase reframes sin not as a discrete forbidden act but as a failure to act — what we did not do, the good we withheld. The adjective "historic" embeds these omissions in the longue durée; they are not momentary lapses but accumulated inheritances, "within us" as constitutive of character rather than external stain. This is a deeply psychological account of sin, closer to modern notions of guilt and regret than to doctrinal transgression.

### The Sinner and the City

Meredith moves between the individual and the collective with ease. The "sinner's guilt" is "perceptive of a doom" (016-115) — guilt is not merely felt but perceptive, a way of seeing, an epistemology of consequence. The sinner knows something the innocent cannot: the shape of what is coming. When the poet writes of a "city charged with sin" (016-121), the metaphor is electrical — the city is charged, energised, animated by its moral failures. Sin is not a dead weight but a live current running through the civic body.

### Repentance and Transformation

The poet's moral psychology comes to a point in the concept of repentance. "Repentance offered ecstasy in pain" (004-040) — the line fuses the acknowledgment of wrong with a transformative intensity. Sin is the precondition for this experience; without the fault, there is no turning. The "vile relapsing" from which wisdom is "spun" (004-236) completes the arc: even the return to sin becomes material for the weaving of understanding. Meredith's sin is not a terminus but a pivot, the point at which the moral life changes direction.
