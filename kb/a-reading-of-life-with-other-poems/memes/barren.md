## Overview

The barren in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is not simply the absence of life but a specific kind of presence: the presence of potential that has been blocked, of ground that ought to bear but does not. Meredith applies the concept across three distinct domains — the agricultural, the cognitive, and the mythic — and in each case, barrenness is not a natural state but a condition produced by force, whether the force of war, of vanity, or of failed discipline.

The conceptual richness of the barren in Meredith comes from its dual valence. On one side, barrenness is a curse: the war-god as "barren furrower" (005-043) who plows fields not to plant but to destroy. On the other, barrenness can be a choice or a discipline: the "barren Huntress" (004-222) who forgoes fertility for other powers. Meredith never resolves this ambivalence, and the tension between barrenness-as-curse and barrenness-as-vocation is one of the collection's most provocative unresolved questions.

## In the Poems

### The Vanity of Barrenness

In chapter 3, barrenness appears as a posture that some adopt and even boast of: "Would cast; these vaunting barrenness for best" (003-134). The verb "vaunting" is damning — to boast of barrenness is to make a virtue of emptiness, to mistake the absence of fruit for superiority. Meredith's target here is likely the ascetic tradition that denigrates fertility and embodiment in favor of a sterile purity. Against this, his poetics consistently affirm the generative, the embodied, the fruitful. Barrenness vaunted is barrenness that has become ideology.

### The Barren Huntress

The most enigmatic instance of the barren appears in *The Test of Manhood* (chapter 4), where Meredith describes "the barren Huntress" (004-222) who alternates with "the sly Persuader snaky in his blood." The Huntress is Artemis/Diana, the virgin goddess of the hunt, and her barrenness is willed — it is the condition of her independence, her freedom from the domestic and the reproductive. She "alternates" with the "sly Persuader" — perhaps Aphrodite or erotic temptation — and the alternation suggests a rhythm within the psyche between the barren discipline of the hunt and the seductive pull of desire. Meredith does not condemn the Huntress; he presents her barrenness as one term in a necessary oscillation, a force that checks the "snaky" persuasions of the blood.

### The Barren Furrower: War as Anti-Agriculture

Chapter 5 deploys the barren in its most overtly political register. The war-god is described as "The barren furrower of anointed fields; / The scarlet heel in towns, foul smoke to sky" (005-043–044). The image is precise and devastating: a furrower is one who cuts the earth to receive seed, but this furrower is "barren" — he cuts not to plant but to ruin. The fields are "anointed," consecrated, set apart for sacred use, and the war-god tramples them with his "scarlet heel." War, in this figuration, is the perversion of agriculture: the same act of breaking the earth, performed not for life but for death.

### The Barren Brain

In chapter 6, Meredith offers a surprising inversion: "Impalpable, not barren, stuff" (006-038). The context describes something intangible (impalpable) that is "not barren" — capable of generating, despite having no material substance. This is thought, spirit, the immaterial ground of mind. The line is a quiet defense of the non-physical against the charge of emptiness: what is impalpable need not be barren.

The collection's final treatment of the barren returns to the cognitive domain. Chapter 16 speaks of seeds that "Lie barren at the doorway of the brain" (016-066) — ideas that fail to germinate, potentials that never enter consciousness. The image is spatial and architectural: the brain has a doorway, and at that threshold, seeds lie inert. The passage that follows is urgent: "Let stout contention drive deep furrows, blood / Moisten, and make new channels of its flood" (016-067–068). Barrenness in the mind is cured not by patience but by violence — by "stout contention" and "blood" that moistens the dry ground of thought.
