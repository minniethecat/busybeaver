## Overview

Chance threads through Meredith's poetry as the unpredictable element that disturbs both divine order and human planning. In six occurrences spanning chapters 3, 5, 14, 15, and 16, "chance" and its archaic form "perchance" mark moments where causation escapes mortal control — where the gods may be defied, where a lady may be found, where Arachne may crouch unmoved, and where a race may be overthrown. Meredith does not reduce chance to mere randomness; rather, chance is the seam where human action meets forces too large to predict.

The word appears predominantly in its archaic form "perchance" — a word that carries the weight of older poetic diction, the language of romance and epic. This choice is deliberate: "perchance" does not promise randomness but rather possibility, the open door that may or may not swing. In Meredith's cosmos of immortal powers and mortal defiance, chance is what the strong seize and the weak suffer.

## In the Poems

### Perchance and the Mortal Wound

The first occurrence sets chance in the intimate register of love and vulnerability. At 003-198, the speaker reflects on a lover's wound: "Perchance his wound is deep; she listens." The "perchance" does not indicate ignorance — it indicates the boundary of what one lover can know of another's suffering. The wound may be deep, or it may not; what matters is the listening, the attention that follows the admission of uncertainty. Chance here is the gap between two minds, the space across which love must travel without guarantees.

### Chance and the Defiance of Immortals

The most pivotal deployment of chance comes at 005-060, in a passage of extraordinary compression: "Vowed to defy Immortals. So it chanced." The clause break after "Immortals" creates a caesura — the vow is absolute, the defiers have committed themselves — and then "So it chanced" arrives as the hinge between intention and outcome. The defiance of immortals is not guaranteed success; it "chanced," meaning the outcome was not written in advance. Meredith gives the defiers their dignity — they acted — but he does not pretend that action determines its own result. Chance is the collaborator or adversary that even the boldest vow must reckon with.

### Perchance and the Lady Sweet

At 014-077, chance enters the social world: "Perchance a lady sweet, whose lord lay slain." The line evokes the world of medieval romance, where ladies are left widowed by wars they did not start, and the "perchance" captures the arbitrariness of their fate. The lady sweet is not singled out by divine decree — it merely chanced that her lord fell. The archaic diction distances the grief, placing it in a legendary framework, but the structure of feeling is immediate: chance determines who survives, who mourns, and who is remembered.

### Perchance as Narrative Engine

In the later books, "perchance" becomes a narrative device for epic-scale possibility. At 015-093, "Arachne crouched unmoved; perchance" — the line breaks on the word itself, leaving the outcome suspended. At 016-147, "To overthrow, perchance uproot the race," chance is the weight that tips the balance between overthrow and uprooting, between one kind of destruction and another. And at 016-206, "Perchance among them shoots a lustrous star" — here chance becomes the vehicle of hope, the possibility that among the fallen something luminous may rise.
