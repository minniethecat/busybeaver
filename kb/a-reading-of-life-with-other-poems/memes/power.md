## Overview

Power in Meredith's poetry is not a single, stable attribute but a spectrum of forces that animate both the cosmos and the human spirit. It appears as "giant power" that lifts the breast (002-069), as persuasion that overpowers resistance (003-141), as the "power within to strike without" (006-058), and as the exchange of powers in conflict (005-094). Meredith's treatment reflects the Victorian preoccupation with energy, will, and force — both natural and moral — while drawing on classical sources that personify power as a divine attribute. The concept is inseparable from the poet's larger inquiry into what drives human action and what constrains it.

## In the Poems

### Power as Vital Uplift

The most immediate form of power in Meredith's verse is the vital, almost ecstatic force that animates the body and spirit. The phrase "Lifts the breast to giant power" (002-069) captures this sense of being seized and elevated by a force larger than the self. This is not power exercised over others but power received — an influx of energy that expands human capacity and aligns the individual with the cosmic order.

### Persuasion and the Power of Influence

A subtler form of power appears in "The Test of Manhood," where Meredith writes of one "Enkindled by persuasion overpowers" (003-141). Here power operates through influence rather than force, through the kindling of conviction rather than the imposition of will. This aligns with the poem's larger concern with how the Goddess (Earth/Nature) guides humanity not through coercion but through attraction and inspiration.

### The Inner Arsenal

In "A Reading of Life," power becomes internalized. The "power within to strike without" (006-058) defines a latent capability that awaits its moment of expression. This inward power is contrasted with the "Exchange of powers of this conflict came" (005-094), where power is transactional, flowing between antagonists in the drama of moral growth. The movement from external to internal power traces the arc of spiritual maturation that the collection as a whole charts.

### Power Denied

Meredith also attends to the experience of powerlessness. The lament of one who must "chafe powerless, though in an anguish" (017-048) — drawn from the classical material of *Iliad* Book 1 — gives the concept its tragic dimension. To be deprived of power when the situation demands action is, for Meredith, a form of existential suffering that tests the character as severely as battle itself.
