## Overview

The heart is the most frequently invoked inward organ in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901), appearing across five chapters and serving as the symbolic center of the poet's moral psychology. Unlike the modern association of the heart with sentiment alone, Meredith's usage draws on a richer tradition in which the heart is the seat of courage, the organ of authentic desire, and the locus where moral decisions are made before they reach conscious thought. "The direst blow on human heart she deals" (003-083) — the heart is where life's most consequential strikes land.

Meredith's heart is dialectical: it can be "coward" (004-092) or "great" (003-231), "envious" (005-038) or "enlarged beyond its ribbed domain" (004-102). It is the organ of both vulnerability and expansion. The heart's "fulness" (003-041) is a state of blessed overflow, while its "excess" (003-187) suggests a passion that exceeds rational bounds. This range makes the heart Meredith's most versatile symbol for the human condition.

## In the Poems

The heart appears throughout the collection (Chapters 2 through 5), explored across emotional, moral, and cosmic dimensions.

### The Heart's Desire

The heart is the organ of wanting: "Or heart's desire of her, all earth in it" (003-016). Here, the heart's desire encompasses the whole earth — a single heart's longing contains the cosmos. The compression is characteristically Meredithian: desire is not small or private but cosmically expansive. Similarly, "Our breath for bliss, our hearts for fulness break" (003-041) links the heart's capacity to the breath of life itself. The heart's "fulness" is a state of completion, of being filled to capacity — yet it is described as a "break," suggesting that fulfilment and shattering are two sides of the same experience.

### The Heart's Courage and Cowardice

The moral quality of the heart is a central concern. "That ere it lightened smote a coward heart" (004-092) describes a lightning-like moral strike that reveals the heart's fear before judgment arrives. The coward heart is not necessarily culpable — it is a condition to be overcome, illuminated, and transformed. In contrast, "the ruddy hue declares great heart" (003-231) reads the body's outward signs as evidence of inner nobility. The great heart is legible on the face; courage cannot be hidden. The heart thus becomes the link between inner moral reality and outer physical expression.

### The Heart as Battlefield

Meredith repeatedly situates the heart as the terrain of spiritual and moral conflict. "In the forest's cavern heart" (002-058) spatializes the heart as a wild interior — the chase, the hunt, the rally all occur within the heart's dark depth. "Dead on her heart her crimsoned flower of Spring" (003-131) presents an image of love and beauty dying at the very center of the beloved — the heart as grave as well as cradle. And in "an envious heart elate" (005-038), the heart holds contradictory emotions simultaneously, proving that Meredith's heart can contain multitudes without resolving them.

### The Enlarged Heart

The collection's most hopeful image of the heart comes in Chapter 4: "His heart enlarged beyond its ribbed domain" (004-102). The heart that grows beyond its physical container — beyond the ribs that define its anatomical limit — represents moral and spiritual expansion. This enlargement is not metaphor but transformation: the heart literally outgrows its cage. "Close on the heart of Earth his bosom beats" (004-189) extends this image outward, synchronizing the human heart with the planetary one. The individual heart beating against Earth's heart suggests a cosmic intimacy that is Meredith's ultimate answer to isolation.
