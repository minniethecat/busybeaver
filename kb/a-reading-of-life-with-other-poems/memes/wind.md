## Overview

The wind in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the invisible agent of motion and transformation. It moves through the collection as a presence that can be felt but not seen — "the light wind's libertine" (015-077) that dances, carries, and tests. Meredith's wind ranges from the gentle ("gracefully a light wind bore", 015-046) to the formidable ("the great whirl of the wind", 017-103), from the nurturing breeze that aids growth to the harsh Northwind that strips and challenges. The wind is Nature's breath, the atmospheric counterpart to the blood that flows within living bodies — both are currents, one external and one internal, that drive the ongoing process of life.

In Meredith's natural philosophy, the wind embodies the principle of invisible causation: forces that cannot be seen produce visible effects, just as the inner life of mind and spirit manifests in outward action. The wind is thus both a physical phenomenon and a figure for the hidden energies — desire, inspiration, fate — that shape human existence.

## In the Poems

The wind appears across the collection in three distinct modalities:

### As the Gentle Mover

The lighter registers of wind carry grace, music, and the fragrances of the natural world. "And gracefully a light wind bore" (015-046) describes a breeze that transports without violence, an invisible hand that distributes scent and sound across the landscape. The figure of the "light wind's libertine" (015-077) personifies this gentler wind as a free spirit, a dancer unbound by gravity or restraint: "Who merely danced when light winds blew" (015-080). This wind is associated with freedom, spontaneity, and the unforced movements of the natural world — it is the spirit of play in Meredith's otherwise earnest philosophical landscape. The hunter's horn "might wind a jocund ring" (014-109), linking the verb form to the celebratory sound that wind carries and disperses.

### As the Testing Force

Wind escalates from gentle motion to formidable challenge. The "Northwind" (017-153) appears as a specific, named force — directional, purposeful, and harsh. It is the wind that tests endurance, the atmospheric equivalent of the moral trials that Meredith's philosophy demands. "Plucked all the winds but chill North-east" (015-008) figures the selective harshness of experience: life does not subject us to all adversities at once but chooses the coldest, most challenging wind to send. The sea-winds carry special force in the collection: "the stretch of the windy sea-waters" (017-011) merges wind and wave into a single image of vast, impersonal power. To stand against such wind is to prove one's strength; to bend before it is wisdom.

### As the Vehicle of Movement and Change

The wind's most abstract role is as the agent of transition. "Onward, like the wind's cloud-fleets" (004-192) images the wind as the driver of clouds — it moves the sky's furniture, perpetually rearranging the visible world. The "winding snake" (003-253) shares its adjective with the wind's motion, suggesting that winding — the sinuous, indirect path — is the characteristic movement of natural forces. The horse imagery of "Wide-nostril'd to the wind, twice ten" (018-019) captures the animal's instinctive orientation toward the wind, reading the air for information about distant events. Wind is thus the medium of prophecy and anticipation, carrying news before the event itself arrives.
