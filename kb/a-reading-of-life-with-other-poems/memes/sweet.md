## Overview

Sweet appears eight times in Meredith's poetry, across chapters 2 through 16, and in no instance is it merely the mild pleasantness that the word conventionally denotes. Meredith's sweetness is a paradox: it cuts with a "sword's edge" (003-040), it is the bait of Pleasure's trap (004-039), it belongs to silence more than to song (006-059), and it reaches its superlative form in companionship renewed (016-273). Across these occurrences, sweet is never sentimental and never safe — it is a quality that intensifies whatever it touches, whether danger or delight.

The word's distribution across the corpus is telling. Sweet appears in chapters of high poetic intensity — song (002), love's wounding (003), Pleasure's temptation (004), the Great Mother's beauty (005), forest silence (006), elegy (014), and renewal (016). No single domain contains it. Sweet is the quality that crosses categories: it belongs to sound and to silence, to pleasure and to pain, to the natural and the supernatural. Meredith's sweet is a mode of intensity, not a flavor.

## In the Poems

### The Edge of Sweetness: Love's Wounding

Meredith's most arresting sweet moment transforms the quality into a weapon: "With a sword's edge of sweetness keen to take" (003-040). The sweetness here does not soothe; it cuts. The "sword's edge" is the sharpened boundary of a blade, and sweetness is that edge — the point at which pleasure becomes pain, at which what attracts also wounds. The word "keen" reinforces the double meaning: keen as sharp (the physical edge) and keen as eager (sweetness actively desires to take).

The context of chapter 3 is Love's operation on the human subject, and sweetness is one of Love's instruments. It is the pleasurable surface that conceals the wounding depth, or perhaps the pleasurable surface that is itself the wound. The line that opens the passage — "With a sword's edge of sweetness keen to take" — leaves the object of "take" unspecified, making sweetness an absolute quality rather than a directed action. Sweetness takes; what it takes is whatever the lover has.

### The Sweet Cup: Pleasure's Temptation

In chapter 4, sweetness is explicitly the mechanism of temptation: "For Pleasure witched him her sweet cup to drain" (004-039). Pleasure is the agent, the cup is the instrument, and sweetness is the quality that makes the cup irresistible. The verb "witched" — enchanted, bewitched — places the transaction in a supernatural frame. The sweet cup is a magic object, and to drain it is to submit to Pleasure's spell.

The sweet cup is an ancient topos — Circe's cup, the temptress's draught — but Meredith gives it a specifically interior turn. The cup is sweet, and sweetness is what makes the draining inevitable. The sweetness is not a disguise for poison; it is the poison. To taste is to be lost, and the sweetness is precisely what undoes the taster.

### Sweet Silence: The Quality Beyond Song

The most surprising sweet in the corpus belongs to silence: "But most the silences were sweet" (006-059). Chapter 6 is a meditation on sound and its absence, and this line arrives as the climactic judgment. The silences — not the songs, not the speech, not the music — are "most" sweet. Sweetness here is not the quality of audible pleasure but of its suspension.

The line turns the conventional association of sweetness with song inside out. Meredith's sweet silence is not the absence of sweetness but its concentration. The silences are sweet because they hold what sound cannot carry — the unspoken, the ineffable, the space between notes where meaning gathers. Sweetness in this register is a quality of restraint rather than abundance, of withholding rather than giving.
