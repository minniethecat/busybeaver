## Overview

Meredith uses the word "miracle" sparingly — three times across the entire corpus — but each occurrence marks a strategic turning point in his argument about the relationship between nature and spirit. The word appears in two poems that form a philosophical diptych: "The Test of Manhood" (004) and the later philosophical meditation numbered 016 in the collection. Together they articulate a carefully differentiated position: nature's own processes, however wondrous, are not miracles; the true miracle is the human capacity to rise from brute existence toward the divine.

The distinction is stated with epigrammatic force in "The Test of Manhood": "No miracle the sprout of wheat from clod, / She knows" (004-197–198); "But he, the flower at head and soil at root, / Is miracle, guides he the brute to God" (004-199–200). Nature understands her own growth — the wheat sprouting, the man emerging from animal ancestry — as lawful process. Only the spiritual direction of that growth, its orientation toward transcendence, constitutes miracle. The concept thus functions as a hinge between Meredith's evolutionary naturalism and his religious aspiration.

## In the Poems

### Nature's Self-Knowledge: No Miracle in Process

The key passage in "The Test of Manhood" (004-193–202) traces a movement from natural change through biological growth to spiritual destination. Nature feels "her blood-tree throbbing in her grain, / Yet skyward branched, with loftier mark and range" (004-195–196) — the tree of life pulses within her, and its branching is upward. But this upward movement is not miraculous in itself; it is inherent in the grain, the seed, the clod. "No miracle the sprout of wheat from clod, / She knows, nor growth of man in grisly brute" (004-197–198).

Nature's knowledge is the key. She *knows* her processes; they are law, not wonder, to her. This distinguishes Meredith's position from a simple nature-worship that would find the miraculous in every sprouting seed. The natural order is wondrous to human perception — "Landward did the wonder flit" (003-015), "Itself, and at new wonders chuckling went" (014-088) — but wonder is the observer's response, not a property of the process itself. Nature, from within, experiences her transformations as continuity, not miracle.

### The True Miracle: Man Guided to God

Against nature's self-sufficient process, Meredith places man's spiritual trajectory: "But he, the flower at head and soil at root, / Is miracle, guides he the brute to God" (004-199–200). The syntax is compressed but the logic is clear: man is both flower (at his highest, the blossom of creation) and soil (at his root, the brute matter from which he emerged). When this composite being directs himself — and the animal within him — toward the divine, that is the miracle.

The verb "guides" is active and moral. Miracle is not a passive reception of grace but an act of guidance, of will, of orientation. The brute — the animal inheritance, the "grisly brute" of 004-198 — is not denied or expelled but *guided*, led upward, transformed by direction rather than by erasure. This is Meredith's evolutionary spirituality in miniature: the raw material of nature is not miraculous, but the act of directing it toward God is.

### Doubt's Appetite: The Consumption of Miracles

The third occurrence of "miracle" appears in a context of philosophical polemic: "Contemptuous of the impalpable, it swells / On Doubt; for pastime swallows miracles" (016-196–197). The materialist mind, "encased in matter" (016-195), feeds on doubt as its nourishment — and "swallows miracles" as a pastime, a casual consumption that destroys what it consumes. The verb "swallows" suggests both ingestion and obliteration: the skeptical intelligence devours the miraculous and leaves nothing behind.

This is the negative counterpart to the positive miracle of 004-200. If the true miracle is the guidance of the brute toward God, the false position is an appetite for doubt that consumes the very possibility of miracle. The passage is one of Meredith's most compressed critiques of scientific materialism: not that it is false, but that it is hungry — it feeds on its own negation.

### Wonder and Marvel: The Register of Perception

Surrounding the three direct uses of "miracle" is a wider vocabulary of wonder and marvel that registers human response to the extraordinary. "Signification marvellous she caught" (005-027) describes a moment of interpretive insight; "Then haply marvel, groan mayhap" (015-015) captures the ambiguous response to beauty — wonder and pain in the same breath. Wonder in Meredith is always on the edge between pleasure and disturbance, like the "wonder" that "flits" landward in "With the Persuader" (003-015) — a movement seen, a presence felt, a meaning half-apprehended.

The distinction between wonder and miracle is essential to Meredith's thought. Wonder is subjective, the human affect of encountering the extraordinary; miracle is objective, the real orientation of the human toward the divine. Wonder may signal miracle, but it is not itself miraculous — just as the sprouting wheat may provoke wonder without being a miracle in nature's own knowledge.
