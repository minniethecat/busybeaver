## Overview

Youth in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) appears as both promise and predicament. It is the season of maximum intensity — "so royal, unuttered, is youth's dream" (006-057) — yet it is also the season most vulnerable to waste and loss: "their youth a frost" (003-153). Meredith, writing in his later years, regards youth with the double vision of one who has left it behind: he remembers its splendor but also sees, with the clarity of distance, how easily that splendor can be misspent.

The collection's treatment of youth belongs to a long elegiac tradition — the *carpe diem* poets, the Romantic celebration of childhood — but Meredith adds a specifically Victorian moral urgency. Youth is not merely fleeting; it is *formative*. What happens in youth determines the shape of the life that follows, and a youth wasted is a life permanently diminished.

## In the Poems

Meredith's treatment of youth moves through several emotional registers:

### Youth Blighted

The starkest image of youth in the collection is also the most compressed: "Their youth a frost, their age the dirge" (003-153). The line equates youth with spring growth and then declares that growth frozen before it could flower. A "frost" youth means a life that never properly began — the warmth that should have sustained early development was withheld. And the consequence extends forward: an age that should be harvest becomes merely "the dirge," a funeral song for what never lived. This is Meredith at his most unsparing about the damage that adverse circumstances inflict on the young.

### Youth as Romantic Sacrifice

In a more intimate register, Meredith writes of "the woman's marrow in some dear youth's name" (003-179). The word "marrow" — the innermost substance of the body — suggests a depth of emotional investment that goes beyond sentiment. The woman's very core is given over to the youth's name, his identity. This is romantic love at its most consuming, and Meredith captures without either endorsing or condemning it: he simply registers the fact that youth, in both sexes, is the season when such total investments are made.

### Youth as Nuptial Promise

The collection's most hopeful image of youth appears in a marital context: "rich in youth doth the bridegroom" (004-212). Here youth is wealth — a resource to be brought to the shared life of marriage. The bridegroom's youth is not for himself alone but for the union he is forming. This is youth properly spent: invested in bonds that will outlast the season of youthful vigor. The image balances the earlier warnings — youth can be blighted, but it can also be consecrated.

### Youth's Interior Kingdom

The most beautiful single line about youth in the collection reaches toward the ineffable: "So royal, unuttered, is youth's dream" (006-057). Youth is "royal" — sovereign over its own interior kingdom — and its dream is "unuttered" because the language to express it has not yet developed, or because the dream itself resists translation into the coarser medium of speech. This is Meredith's tribute to the inwardness of the young, their possession of a world that adults have lost access to and cannot adequately describe.
