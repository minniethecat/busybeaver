## Overview

Home in Meredith's poetry is never a simple place of rest. It appears as shelter and as trap, as the cultivated clearing against wild nature and as the absent centre that defines the wanderer's longing. The poet traces home across several registers: the neighbouring homesteads that mark civilised space, the instinctual "home-bred" character of the yeoman, and the deeper existential claim "I knew my home where I had choice to feel" (016-085). In each case, home is less a location than a relationship — something chosen, defended, and capable of being lost. The concept threads through Meredith's rural landscape and his moral psychology alike, binding the physical architecture of dwelling to the inward architecture of belonging.

## In the Poems

### The Homestead as Civilisation's Mark

In "The Sage Enamoured and the Honest Lady," Meredith maps the homestead onto the landscape as an index of cultivation. The "trim homesteads bordered spaces green" (014-062) are the settled form of a world that once offered only "clothing, ambush, refuge, home" (014-054) — a movement from raw utility to ordered life. The home-bred yeoman, who "hailed the home-bred yeoman mate" (014-095), embodies a rootedness that the poem treats as both strength and limitation: his "dearer home was there" (014-105), in the familiar, and the poet neither simply celebrates nor ironises this attachment.

### Home as Chosen Refuge

Meredith's most personal registration of home comes in "The Test of Manhood," where the speaker declares "I knew my home where I had choice to feel" (016-085). The line insists on agency — home is not inherited but elected, a site where feeling itself becomes possible. The counter-pressure appears immediately: "No home is here for peace while evil …" (016-063), acknowledging that the domestic refuge is always under threat from forces that cannot be shut out. The poet holds these two truths in tension: home is necessary and home is fragile.

### The Wisdom of Placement

In "Arachne," the spider "sagaciously her home she chose" (015-005), blending instinct with intelligence. The choice of home becomes a figure for wisdom itself — to know where and how to dwell. The neighbouring impulse, described earlier in the collection where figures "neighboured home" (005-005), establishes the social dimension: home is never wholly private, always implicated in the lives of others.
