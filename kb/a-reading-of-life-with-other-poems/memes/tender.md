## Overview

In Meredith's poetic vocabulary, tenderness is never simple softness. It operates as a cosmic paradox.

The Goddess Tenderness in "With the Persuader" is "pitiless to them / Resisting in her godhead nature's truth" (003-150).

She only becomes recognizably tender — "maternal," with "a sister's tones / Benign to soothe intemperate distress" (003-180) — when approached through surrendered [[love]].

This dual nature places tenderness at the centre of Meredith's ethics of relationships: it is both the highest expression of human connection and an impersonal law that punishes those who refuse it.

## In the Poems

### The Pitiless Goddess (With the Persuader)

Meredith personifies tenderness as a goddess whose apparent cruelty is a form of cosmic justice.

Those who resist nature's imperative toward union experience her not as comfort but as torment: "No flower their face shall be, but writhen stem; / Their youth a frost, their age the dirge for youth" (003-150).

The language is uncompromising — tenderness denied becomes a curse that withers the resister across their entire lifespan.

Meredith's point is not that tenderness is absent from these lives but that its presence, when rejected, manifests as pain: "These miserably disinclined, / The lamentably unembraced, / Insult the Pleasures Earth designed."

The passage functions as a warning: tenderness is not optional in Meredith's moral universe; it is built into the structure of things.

### The Maternal Turn (With the Persuader)

The poem pivots when tenderness is authentically received. The goddess becomes maternal when "the woman's marrow in some dear youth's name" is confessed with "eyes of embers, breath of incense myrrh" (003-180).

The transformation is immediate: "Then is the Goddess tenderness / Maternal, and she has a sister's tones / Benign to soothe intemperate distress."

Meredith does not present this as a contradiction — the pitilessness and the maternity are two faces of the same divinity, differentiated only by the human response to her.

To embrace love is to experience tenderness as maternal solace; to resist it is to experience the same force as pitiless punishment.

### Tenderness and the Couple

The broader context of the poem establishes tenderness as the force that binds Meredith's celebrated "couples" — those who "play the music made of two."

These paired lovers become mirrors of her beauty: "Like orchard blossoms, apple, pear and plum, / Free of the cloud, beneath the flood of gold" (003-180).

Tenderness is thus not merely an emotion between individuals but a cosmic principle that aligns human intimacy with natural abundance.

The orchard imagery suggests that tenderness, properly enacted, makes the lovers part of Earth's generative order rather than isolated exceptions to it.
