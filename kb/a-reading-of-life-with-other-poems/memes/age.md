## Overview

Age in Meredith's poetry carries a double meaning: it is the historical era that shapes civilizations and the personal passage of years that marks each individual life. The poet's characteristic compression of the cosmic and the personal is nowhere more evident than in his treatment of age. "Their youth a frost, their age the dirge for youth" (003-153) condenses an entire philosophy of mortal time into a single line, while the imagery of the "splendid image built of man" that "has flown" (012-031) speaks to the transience of historical achievement. Age is for Meredith not merely a biological fact but a philosophical problem — the medium through which human meaning is both created and dissolved.

## In the Poems

### Youth and Its Dirge

The most striking formulation of age in Meredith's work appears in "The Test of Manhood": "Their youth a frost, their age the dirge for youth" (003-153). The line enacts a terrible symmetry — youth is frozen before it can flower, and age becomes not wisdom but lamentation for what was lost. This is Meredith at his most unsparing, refusing the consolations of conventional Victorian age-wisdom in favor of a starker vision where the passage of time is a site of grief rather than fulfillment.

### The Primal and the Reclaimed

Meredith traces the arc of civilization as a movement through ages, from the primitive toward the cultivated. The figure "whom from utter savage she reclaimed" (003-303) stands at the threshold between natural brutality and civilized life — yet the poet immediately adds the caution that "Half savage must he stay, would he be crowned" (003-304). Age, in this civilizational sense, is not pure progress but a fraught negotiation between the wild and the tamed. The "lesser savage" who "offered bogs and trees" (014-055) belongs to an earlier age of human development that the poet regards with neither nostalgia nor contempt.

### The Splendid Image Flown

In "The Thrush in February," Meredith sustains the elegiac note: "A splendid image built of man has flown" (012-031). The image — of an age, a civilization, a mode of being — dissolves into the temporal flow. The poem's thrush, singing in winter, becomes the counter-image to historical loss: nature's cycle persists while human ages rise and fall. The contrast between natural recurrence and historical linearity is one of Meredith's most persistent themes.

### Arachne and the Weight of Years

The figure of Arachne in "Arachne" provides the most intimate portrait of age in the collection. She is "humped and aged" (015-069), "wrinkled, past enraged" (015-070) — the physical marks of time written on the body. Yet her craft persists; age brings not only diminishment but a concentrated wisdom that the spinner's art embodies.
