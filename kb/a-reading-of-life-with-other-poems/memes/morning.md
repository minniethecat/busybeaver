## Overview

Morning in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is never simply the start of day. It is the threshold where darkness yields to revelation, where "Ethereal Beauty in full morningtide" (004-014) becomes visible, and where the moral quality of light — as Reverence, as truth, as exposure — is most intensely felt.

Meredith's mornings span the full range from the mythic ("Midway between eve and dawn" [002-002]) to the brutally physical ("At dawn the deer's wet nostrils blew their last" [014-098]).

The Victorian poetic tradition inherited the Romantic veneration of dawn as the moment of imaginative possibility, and Meredith draws on this lineage while adding his own characteristic note of ambivalence. Morning reveals, but what it reveals is not always welcome.

The "morning star" of bliss is asked to "fly not too fast" (003-192); dawn is as much an ending — of night's pleasures, of sleep's oblivion — as it is a beginning. This double nature makes morning one of the most complex temporal figures in the collection.

## In the Poems

### Midnight's Morning Bird

One of Meredith's most compressed temporal paradoxes appears in "With the Persuader": "We answer as the midnight's morning's bird" (003-089). The double genitive — "midnight's morning's bird" — nests two time-markers inside each other: the bird belongs to morning, but morning belongs to midnight.

The bird that sings at midnight is the bird that anticipates morning while still in darkness. Meredith uses this image to describe the human response to love's call — we answer not as the morning bird but as the bird that sings of morning while it is still midnight, responding in faith to a light not yet seen.

### The Lingering Star

The desire to prolong the threshold appears in the prayer "that swiftly may her star of eve descend, / Her lustrous morning star fly not too fast" (003-192). The evening star and the morning star are the same celestial body — Venus — seen at different moments.

To ask the evening star to descend swiftly and the morning star to linger is to ask for the night to be as long as possible. The "lustrous morning star" is beautiful precisely because it belongs to the moment before full day; its light is gentler than the sun's, its presence a promise rather than a fulfillment.

### The Dawn of Reverence

In "The Test of Manhood," Meredith traces a moral genealogy: "That quiet dawn was Reverence; whereof sprang / Ethereal Beauty in full morningtide" (004-013). Dawn is not just a time of day but a moral state — Reverence — and from this state springs Beauty in its "full morningtide" intensity.

The progression is from quiet to fullness, from dawn to morning, from Reverence to Beauty. The adjective "quiet" for dawn is significant: Reverence, like dawn, arrives without noise, transforming the landscape before anyone notices the change.

### The Hunter's Dawn

The most unsentimental dawn in the collection appears in "Forest History": "Before the blackbird pecked the turf they woke; / At dawn the deer's wet nostrils blew their last" (014-098). This is dawn as terminus, the moment of death for the hunted animal.

The detail of the "wet nostrils" is visceral — moisture still present at the moment of expiration, life and death separated by the thinnest membrane. "Blew their last" is breath becoming wind, the living exhalation becoming the final one.

This dawn has no reverence, no ethereal beauty; it is the dawn of the food chain, the morning that the hunter welcomes and the hunted does not see.

### The Brave To-Be

A different register sounds in "Foresight and Patience," where dawn becomes a figure of hope: "A brave To-be is dawn upon my brow" (016-029). Dawn is equated with the "To-be" — the not-yet, the future tense made visible. The adjective "brave" personifies the dawn as courageous, facing the day that must be endured.

And "upon my brow" brings the cosmic event into intimate contact with the speaker's body: dawn is not a distant horizon but a pressure on the forehead, a weight and a promise felt physically.
