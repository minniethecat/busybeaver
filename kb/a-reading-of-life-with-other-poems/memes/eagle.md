## Overview

Eagle appears twice in Meredith's poetry, in chapters 2 and 16, and the two occurrences map the full arc of the bird's symbolic range — from the zenith of divine power to the limit of natural aspiration. In chapter 2, the eagle sweeps high above, its wings associated with the bliss of gods (002-055). In chapter 16, the eagle is a figure of upward striving that, for all its majesty, cannot match the daisy's humble intimacy with the sun (016-032). From the companion of gods to the surpassed aspirant, Meredith's eagle traces a downward curve: the highest of birds brought low by comparison with the lowest of flowers.

Meredith's eagle belongs to the classical tradition — Zeus's bird, the king of the air, the creature whose flight is synonymous with sovereignty. But Meredith subjects this tradition to a characteristic reversal. The eagle may mount toward heaven, but the daisy already knows what the eagle strains to reach. Height, in Meredith's revision, is not the same as knowledge.

## In the Poems

### The Sweep of Eagle Wings: Divine Height

The eagle's first appearance is in a passage of exalted classical energy: "Blood the bliss of Gods to share, / High o'er sweep of eagle wings" (002-054, 002-055). The context is one of divine participation — mortal blood aspiring to share the bliss of Gods. The eagle's wings sweep "high o'er," their span a measure of the distance between the human and the divine. To share the bliss of Gods is to rise to the height of eagle wings, to enter the air that only the sovereign bird and the immortals inhabit.

The verb "sweep" conveys both breadth and ease. Eagle wings do not flutter or struggle; they sweep, in a single continuous motion that covers vast arcs of sky. The eagle's flight is the image of effortless power, of height achieved without strain. And this effortlessness is precisely what associates the eagle with the divine: gods do not labor; they simply are, and the eagle's sweeping flight is the closest mortal nature comes to this divine economy of motion.

The line that follows — "Like the run with her, when rings" (002-056) — shifts from the eagle's solitary height to a shared chase, a "run with her" that suggests erotic pursuit. The eagle's height, then, is not the end of the passage but its vantage: from the sweep of eagle wings, the poem descends into the pursuit, the chase, the human drama that plays out below.

### The Eagle That Mounts: Aspiration Surpassed

Chapter 16 returns to the eagle, but in a strikingly diminished key: "My daisy better knows her God of beams / Than doth an eagle that to mount him seems" (016-031, 016-032). The daisy — the humblest of flowers, ground-hugging, common, small — "better knows" the sun than the eagle that appears to mount toward it. The eagle "to mount him seems" — the verb "seems" is crucial. The eagle's mounting is apparent, not real; it seems to ascend toward the sun, but its ascent is an illusion of perspective. The daisy, rooted in earth, actually knows the sun better.

This is a radical inversion of the traditional hierarchy. In classical and Christian symbolism alike, the eagle is the bird that gazes directly at the sun, the creature of unmediated vision. Meredith gives this privilege to the daisy instead. The following line — "She hath the secret never fieriest reach" (016-033) — confirms the daisy's advantage: she possesses a secret that even the most fiery reach cannot attain. The eagle's height, which seemed so magnificent in chapter 2, is revealed in chapter 16 as a kind of distance — the farther you rise, the further you are from the secret the daisy knows by staying close to earth.

The eagle thus becomes, in Meredith's hands, a figure of limitation rather than transcendence. Its sweep of wings, so impressive from below, is from another angle merely the sign of a distance that cannot be closed. The daisy knows what the eagle only seems to seek.
