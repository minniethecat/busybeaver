## Overview

Field appears in Meredith's poetry across three overlapping domains: the Homeric battlefield where warriors contend, the agricultural field where growth and harvest unfold, and the metaphorical field where inner and outer realities confront one another. The "fair-flower'd field of Scamander" (017-073) invokes the Trojan plain in its epic guise, while "the barren furrower of anointed fields" (005-043) draws on agricultural imagery to indict corrupted priesthood. Most distinctively, Meredith develops the "field of tares" (016-148, 016-165) as a figure for moral confusion — the field where wheat and weed grow indistinguishable, where what appears to be pasture is in fact poison. Across these registers, the field is the ground where forces meet: armies in combat, seed in soil, truth and falsehood in the human heart.

## In the Poems

### Field of War

The classical field is dominated by martial contest. The Trojans assemble on "the fair-flower'd field of Scamander" (017-073), where the river Scamander flows through the Trojan plain — a landscape at once beautiful and destined for slaughter. The epithet "fair-flower'd" sharpens the tragic contrast: the field blooms even as armies mass upon it. The "roadway gaps of the war-field" (017-108) introduce a different spatial logic, where the field is fissured by the passages through which combatants move — the field not as open expanse but as terrain defined by its breaches and vulnerabilities. In "The Test of Manhood," the phrase "of their war be field" (004-026) makes field the very condition of conflict: without the field, there is no war, and the field itself becomes synonymous with the struggle it hosts.

### Field as Agricultural Metaphor

Meredith draws on the deep literary tradition of the field as agricultural ground to develop moral and social arguments. "The barren furrower of anointed fields" (005-043) is a compressed satiric image: the furrower moves across fields that are consecrated ("anointed") yet yields nothing ("barren"). The paradox of anointed barrenness is the poem's indictment of institutional religion — sacred ground that produces no harvest. The contrast between appearance and reality intensifies in the "field of tares" passages, where "Within, without, they are a field of tares" (016-148) collapses the distinction between inner and outer: the field is not a place but a condition, a pervasive weed-choked state. "Their field of tares they take for pasture ground" (016-165) sharpens the epistemological stakes — misrecognition of the field as pasture is a cognitive error with mortal consequences.

### Field as Contested Moral Terrain

The most philosophically charged field imagery in the collection appears in "A Reading of Life," where the speaker urges: "look this way, across the fields that show" (016-081). Here the field is a site of visibility — what can be seen if one only turns to look. The fields "show" something; the question is whether the observer will attend. In the companion passage, what is rendered "visible as life on either field" (016-151) makes the field a medium of revelation, where the opposed possibilities of human existence become unmistakably clear. This usage transforms field from a spatial category into an existential one: the field is wherever the stakes of a life are played out, wherever wheat and tare grow side by side and demand discrimination.
