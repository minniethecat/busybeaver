## Overview

The forest in Meredith's poetry is never merely a collection of trees. It is a charged landscape: a site of mythological presence, psychological encounter, and transformative experience. From the opening of the collection — "In deep forest; oread, faun" (002-004) — the forest is populated by classical spirits, framing the natural world as inhabited and animate. Across the poems, the forest oscillates between idyllic refuge and threatening unknown, between childhood wonder and adult fear. Meredith's forests are at once the literal woodlands of English pastoral and the symbolic *selva oscura* of the Western poetic tradition, a space where characters lose and find themselves.

## In the Poems

### The Mythological Forest

Meredith opens the collection by placing the forest within a classical frame: "In deep forest; oread, faun" (002-004). Oreads (mountain nymphs) and fauns inhabit this woodland, making it a space where the mythic and the natural coincide. "She and forest, where she skims" (002-017) presents the forest as the feminine principle's native habitat — she moves through it with the ease of a native, not the hesitation of a visitor. The "forest's cavern heart" (002-058) deepens the mythological register: the forest has an interior, a heart, a cavern that evokes both the womb and the underworld. A "tremor of night's forest horn" (003-125) sounds across the boundary between forest and human settlement, a call from the wild that registers as both invitation and warning.

### Forest Fear and Psychological Landscape

The most sustained forest sequence occurs in the "Forest Fear" passages of the later poems, where the forest becomes a psychological terrain. "More intimate became the forest fear" (014-012) traces a progression: fear is not an immediate reaction but something that grows with familiarity, becoming "intimate." The "forest's heart of fog on mossed morass" (014-018) presents the forest interior as murky, uncertain, a landscape of obscured vision. Yet daylight transforms the scene: "By daylight now the forest fear could read" (014-087), as if the forest were a text requiring illumination to be deciphered. The fear recedes not because the forest changes but because the perceiver's relationship to it matures.

### Forest and City

A distinctive tension in Meredith's forest imagery is its relationship to urban space. "The city urchin mooned on forest air" (014-102) captures a child's yearning for a nature he has never fully known — the forest is present only as "air," a distant atmosphere rather than a visited place. The forest and city are not simply opposed but intertwined: "In whom the city over forest flowered, / The forest wreathed the city's drama-mark" (014-129, 014-130). Each transforms the other; urban experience blooms out of woodland roots, and the forest weaves itself into the fabric of civic life. The "pensioned forester beside his crutch" (014-124) embodies this fusion — a forest figure placed within an economic and social frame.

### Forest as Journey

The forest is also the terrain of pilgrimage and passage. "Wearifully through forest-tracts unsown" (004-203) uses the forest as the landscape of arduous progress, the "unsown" modifier emphasizing its wildness — this is not cultivated land but unmapped territory. "A peak, a forest mound, a valley's gleam" (005-025) arrays the forest as one element in a layered landscape, a waypoint on a journey that also includes heights and depths. In the epic register of the Homeric poems, the forest becomes the fuel of catastrophe: "terrible fire feeds fast on a forest enormous" (017-056), the Homeric simile transforming woodland into kindling for the conflagration of war.
