## Overview

The cry in Meredith's poetry is the most elemental form of human speech — not yet language but already meaning. It rises from the body before the mind can censor it and reaches outward toward an uncertain audience: earth, heaven, other humans. Meredith deploys the cry across the collection's full range, from the metaphysical "Man's cry, earth's answer, heaven's consent" (003-043) to the battlefield's "sharp cry" (014-073) and the epic's "Loud will the cry for Achilles burst" (017-047). In every register, the cry is the sound of creatureliness — of need that cannot be met by silence, of grief that will not stay contained. The poet treats it with neither embarrassment nor sentimentality, recognizing the cry as the authentic voice of the human condition.

## In the Poems

### The Metaphysical Cry

The central cry-motif in Meredith's work appears as a triadic formula: "Man's cry, earth's answer, heaven's consent" (003-043). The line recurs with variations — "heaven's consent unto man's cry" (003-318) — establishing the cry as the opening move in a cosmic dialogue. Earth answers because the cry is bodily, rooted in material need; heaven consents because the cry is also spiritual, a reaching beyond the given. The poet's compression of this pattern into a single line reflects his characteristic ambition to make the lyric do metaphysical work.

### Nature's Cry

Not all cries are human. Meredith speaks of what "licence called it Nature's cry" (004-041), acknowledging that the natural world has its own utterances — the predator's shriek, the mating call, the wind's howl. The qualifying "licence called it" introduces a note of epistemological caution: what we hear as nature's cry may be our own projection. The cry, in this context, marks the uncertain boundary between human meaning and the indifferent sounds of the non-human world.

### The Cry of Battle

The cry sharpens into violence in two contexts. In the epic register, "Loud will the cry for Achilles burst from the sons of the Hellenes" (017-047) invokes the Homeric battlefield where the cry is both war-shout and lament for the absent hero. The Trojan and Achaean cries merge into a single "stupendous" sound (017-158) that transcends partisan allegiance — war reduced to its pure sonic signature. In the more intimate register, "each came edged, and at sharp cry" (014-073) registers the wound's sudden announcement, the body speaking before the mind can process pain.

### The Cry for Help

The cry reaches its most naked form as supplication. "Confesses in a cry for help shrill loud" (016-116) strips away all dignity and indirection: the cry is confession, admission of helplessness, the sound that pride would suppress but need cannot. Meredith treats this as morally significant — the ability to cry for help is the precondition of receiving it, and the refusal to cry is a form of self-defeating pride.
