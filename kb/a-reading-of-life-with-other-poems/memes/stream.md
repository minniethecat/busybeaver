## Overview

Meredith uses the word "stream" with striking frequency — seven distinct occurrences across eight poems — making it one of his more versatile natural images. The stream appears in three primary registers: as a figure for physical motion in the hunt ("streams the chase," 002-026); as a metaphor for inner vitality and passion ("the stream within us urged to flood," 003-029); and as an image of life-giving flow, both paternal and biological ("the stream of the paternal smile," 016-217). Across these varied contexts, the stream consistently figures a force that is simultaneously natural and human, binding the outer landscape to the inner life of blood, desire, and inheritance.

Unlike the more static images of earth or mountain, the stream is fundamentally dynamic — it flows, urges, floods, runs. This dynamism makes it a natural vehicle for Meredith's evolutionary vision, in which life itself is a current that cannot be dammed or denied.

## In the Poems

### The Stream of the Chase: Motion and Pursuit

The earliest appearance of "stream" occurs in the hunting imagery of "With the Persuader" (002-026): "Many rearward; streams the chase." Here the stream is not water but motion itself — the streaming movement of the hunt across the landscape. The verb form ("streams") is crucial: the chase does not merely proceed, it streams, becoming fluid, continuous, a current of bodies rather than a sequence of discrete actions. The image aligns the human activity of the hunt with the natural flow of water, suggesting that pursuit — whether of game, of beauty, or of truth — participates in the same dynamics that govern rivers and tides.

### The Inner Stream: Passion and Vitality

The stream turns inward in "With the Persuader" (003-029): "The stream within us urged to flood." This is Meredith's most compressed statement of the relationship between natural force and human passion. The stream is *within us* — it is not an external metaphor applied to human experience but an internal reality, a current of vitality that "urges" toward overflow. The word "urged" carries the sense of both pressure and purpose: the stream does not merely exist but presses toward expression, toward flooding its banks.

A related passage (003-065) places the leader at the stream's edge: "the leader foot shall dip in stream." The act of dipping — partial immersion, testing the current — suggests that engagement with the stream of life is not a passive surrender but a deliberate act, led by those with the courage to enter first. The stream is there to be entered, but the entry is a choice.

### The Torrent and the Fountain: Transformation of Form

Meredith varies the scale of the stream image across the corpus. In "The Test of Manhood" (004-225), the stream becomes torrential: "And as a torrent stream where cattle grazed." The pastoral calm of grazing cattle is disrupted by the sudden transformation of gentle stream into torrent — a figure for the sudden irruptions of passion or revelation that overturn the tranquil surface of ordinary life. Conversely, in a later poem (005-080), the stream has its origin in a fountain: "as where the fountain runs a stream." The fountain-to-stream progression models the relationship between source and expression, origin and outflow — the stream is what the fountain becomes when it enters the world.

### The Stream of Inheritance: Blood and Lineage

The stream's most intimate register appears in the image of blood and paternal inheritance. "Spring of the blood-stream" (013-004) identifies the stream directly with biological inheritance — the blood that flows through generations, carrying life forward. The paternal image deepens in a philosophical meditation (016-217): "Though not the stream of the paternal smile." Here the stream is not blood but approval, love, the flow of recognition from father to child. The line is tinged with loss — "Though not" — suggesting that the stream of paternal blessing is not always available, and that its absence is a kind of drought.

A contrasting image of the stream appears in "swallows o'er smooth streams" (014-104), where the birds skim the surface. The smoothness of the stream here contrasts with the torrent of 004-225 — the same element can be placid or violent, a mirror or a flood. This doubleness — stream as both calm surface and disruptive current — is essential to Meredith's use of the image.
