## Overview

The embrace is one of the most philosophically charged gestures in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901). It is at once the most intimate of human actions — two bodies closing the distance between them — and the figure Meredith reaches for when describing the largest cosmic processes: the union of heaven and earth, the relation of the individual soul to the world, the generative meeting of opposites. The embrace bridges scales, from the erotic to the cosmological, without losing its bodily ground.

Meredith's treatment of the embrace is notable for its attention to what the embrace *fails* to reach. The word "unembraced" appears twice in the collection (002-044, 003-155), naming a condition of isolation that is the embrace's negative image. The embrace is always shadowed by its absence: to name what is embraced is also to name what remains outside the circle of arms.

## In the Poems

### The Cosmic Embrace

The most expansive treatment of the embrace appears in a line that compresses creation into a single gesture: "Rich issue of the embrace of heaven and earth" (005-141). The embrace here is generative — it produces "rich issue," the abundant fruit of a union between the celestial and the terrestrial. This is not a metaphor drawn from human love but a cosmological principle: creation itself is the offspring of embrace. Heaven and earth do not merely touch; they clasp, and what issues from their clasping is the world's plenitude.

A related passage generalizes this cosmic intimacy: "Delighted with our world's embrace" (006-016). Here the embrace is not the act but the state — the world holds us in an ongoing clasp, and our proper response is delight. The world's embrace is given, not earned; the task is to recognize it and take pleasure in it.

### The Erotic Embrace and Its Obstacles

In the long poem of chapter 3, Meredith traces the difficulty of the human embrace. One line describes "Forbidding love's devised embrace" (003-332): the embrace is "devised" — planned, schemed, constructed — but love forbids it. The paradox is sharp. Love, whose natural expression is the embrace, here blocks its own fulfillment. Why? The surrounding context suggests that love, once it becomes self-conscious, once it "devises," loses the immediacy that the embrace requires. The devised embrace is a contradiction in terms.

A later meditation in chapter 4 offers a different perspective: "This gift of penetration and embrace" (004-121). Here the embrace is paired with penetration — a double gift that combines inwardness (penetration into the essence of things) with outwardness (embrace as encompassing). The embrace in this formulation is not possessive but generous: it is a gift, something given rather than seized.

### The Unembraced

Twice in the collection Meredith names the condition of being outside the embrace. In an early passage, something "Sanctifies the unembraced" (002-044), suggesting that even those who are not held are made sacred — perhaps by the very longing for embrace. A later variation sharpens the lament: "The lamentably unembraced" (003-155). The adverb *lamentably* makes explicit the sorrow of the condition. To be unembraced is to be lamentable — not morally culpable but pitiable, a state of lack that calls for compassion. The final passage, from a poem near the collection's close, reaches toward resolution: "One, more one than the bridally embraced" (009-012). The embrace here is nuptial — the "bridally embraced" — but the poem proposes a unity deeper even than this, a oneness that exceeds embrace. The embrace, even at its most intimate, is only an approximation of the unity it figures.
