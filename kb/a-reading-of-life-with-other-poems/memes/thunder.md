## Overview

Thunder in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the sound of power beyond the human scale. It enters the collection through Meredith's Homeric translations, where Zeus "glows glad in his thunder" (017-089), and echoes into his original poetry as the extreme limit of natural force.

Thunder shares conceptual territory with [[storm]] but is more focused: where the storm is the entire system of violent weather, thunder is its voice — the acoustic signature of divine or elemental will.

Meredith's thunder is fundamentally auditory, not visual. The lightning that accompanies it is secondary; what matters is the sound that shakes the world and the silence that follows.

In the epic tradition, thunder is the language in which gods address mortals who cannot bear direct speech. Meredith preserves this hieratic quality while also treating thunder as a natural phenomenon, a sound to be measured against the "stress of the gale in the oak-trees" (017-156).

## In the Poems

### Zeus and Divine Thunder

The most concentrated thunder imagery appears in the Homeric passages. "Zeus glows glad in his thunder" (017-089) is a remarkable image: the god does not merely wield thunder as a weapon but glows with it, his divine radiance and his thunderous power becoming a single phenomenon.

The verb "glows" suggests that thunder is, for Zeus, an expression of joy rather than anger — he is "glad" in it. This is thunder not as punishment but as the overflow of divine vitality.

The thunder is further described as "tonant" (017-156) — a Latinate word meaning "thundering" that reaches back through Virgil to the oldest stratum of European poetry. By choosing "tonant" over "thundering," Meredith signals his participation in a continuous literary tradition that runs from Homer through the Roman epic to the Victorian present.

### The Thunder-Cloud of War

The Homeric passages also describe how "raised up cloud-thick by the thundering" (017-098) creates a visual density in the air. The compound "cloud-thick" compresses cause and effect: the thunder creates cloud, and the cloud becomes thick with thunder.

On the battlefield, this atmospheric density mirrors the density of combat — warriors fight within a medium thickened by sound and dust and violence. Thunder is the natural analogue of battle's acoustic chaos.

### Thunder and Lightning in Original Verse

In Meredith's original poetry, thunder appears in close coupling with lightning: "Were thunder spitting lightnings on the world" (004-169). The unusual construction — thunder "spitting" lightning rather than the god or sky — blurs agency between the sound and the flash.

Thunder is personified as the active agent, lightning as the projectile it hurls. The subjunctive "Were" frames the image as hypothetical, a possibility entertained rather than an event described.

The lightning image also appears independently in the collection. In an early lyric passage, Meredith writes of a divine figure: "Those are her white-lightning limbs" (002-019) — lightning here is bodily radiance, a whiteness so intense it becomes a form of light.

Later, the hoped-for arrival of a hero is imagined "like lightning's blue" (014-079), the color shift from white to blue suggesting different registers: white-lightning for divine presence, blue lightning for heroic velocity.

These lightning images form the visual counterpart to thunder's acoustic power — the flash that precedes and outruns the sound. The thunder of Meredith's own voice, however, is not the thunder of Zeus. Where the Homeric passages celebrate divine thunder as a mode of presence, the original poems treat it as a reminder of human limitation.
