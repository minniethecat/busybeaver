## Overview

Grief in Meredith's poetry appears almost exclusively in the Homeric register of the later poems, where it surfaces not as a personal emotion but as a cosmic response to the fundamental wrongness of the mortal condition. The key passage (017-180, 017-184) presents Kronos — the father of Zeus — beholding immortal horses yoked to a mortal master and shaking his head in pity. The grief here is not the horses' (who are immortal and therefore beyond suffering) but the god's, a divine sorrow occasioned by the sight of deathlessness subjected to death. The compound "heart-grief" (017-184), coined or carried over from Meredith's Homeric source, intensifies the emotion by locating it in the body's core, making grief not a passing mood but a cardiac condition. In Meredith's treatment, grief is the appropriate response to the ontological scandal of the mortal condition — the mismatch between immortal essence and fleshly limit.

## In the Poems

### Divine Pity and the Yoke of Mortality

The sole sustained treatment of grief occurs in the Homeric episode of the immortal horses. Kronos beholds the horses — ageless and deathless — yoked to the mortal Peleus and subjected to human sorrow: "Now when the issue of Kronos beheld that sorrow, his head shook / Pitying them for their grief, these words then he spake in his bosom" (017-180). The scene is layered: the horses experience sorrow at their subjection, Kronos experiences grief at the sight of their sorrow, and the reader experiences something like pity at the entire chain of compassionate response. Kronos' head-shaking — a gesture that precedes speech, that is almost involuntary — captures grief as a physical reflex before it becomes an articulate emotion.

### Heart-Grief and the Wretchedness of Mortals

Kronos' speech pivots on the compound "heart-grief" (017-184), a word that concentrates the entire pathos of the scene into a single term: "'Tis most true, than the race of these men is there wretcheder nowhere / Aught over earth's range found that is gifted with breath and has movement." The god's conclusion is not that the horses suffer uniquely but that the mortal condition itself — being "gifted with breath and movement" — is the most wretched state in existence. Grief here becomes cosmic in scope: it is not an individual affliction but the signature emotion of embodied consciousness, the price of having breath at all. Meredith, through the Homeric ventriloquism, offers no consolation — only the grim dignity of having the truth of the human condition acknowledged by a god.

### Grief and the Structure of Compassion

The passage models a chain of compassionate response: the horses suffer, Kronos pities them, and the narrator records the pity. Grief in this triadic structure is not isolating but connective — it links sufferer, witness, and teller in a chain of recognition. This is Meredith's implicit argument about the function of poetry itself: to extend the chain of grief-pity-recognition from the mythic past into the reader's present. The Homeric episode, filtered through Meredith's Victorian sensibility, becomes a meditation on what it means to witness suffering and to be moved by it.
