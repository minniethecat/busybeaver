## Overview

Sign appears in Meredith's poetry as the visible evidence of an invisible intention — the mark by which mortals read the disposition of Heaven, Nature, and Earth. Across four genuine occurrences spanning chapters 3, 4, 5, and 14, "sign" and its cognates "signal" and "signalled" trace a movement from human interpretation of divine indifference to the self-announcing heat of earthly identity. Meredith's signs are never straightforward; they require reading, and the reader may be wrong.

The word operates at the junction of epistemology and theology. A sign is what a god or a force deigns to show; a signal is what a master-adventurer sends; signs are what authenticate the "red-hot son of Earth." In each case, the sign mediates between a hidden source and a public surface. Meredith's poetry, itself a tissue of signs that point toward meanings not fully stated, uses the word with a precision that rewards close attention.

## In the Poems

### The Divine Sign: Heaven's Indifference

The most concentrated use of the word comes at 014-030: "By sign of Heaven indifferent, Nature foe." The line places "sign" at the pivot of a cosmic disjunction. Heaven gives a sign, but the sign communicates indifference — not wrath, not blessing, but a terrifying neutrality. Nature, meanwhile, is foe. The sign from Heaven does not promise rescue or punishment; it merely indicates that Heaven exists and does not care. Meredith reverses the traditional expectation of divine signs as messages of favor or warning. Here the sign is the form that indifference takes when it becomes visible.

The construction "By sign of" grants the phrase an almost legal weight — as if the speaker were presenting evidence in a cosmic court, establishing Heaven's indifference as a matter of public record. The sign proves nothing except that proof itself is cold comfort.

### Signals of Adventurous Mastery

At 005-010, the word appears in its active, verbal form: "It signalled some adventurous master-hand." The "It" is the vessel or the work of art that Meredith's poem describes — an object that announces its maker through its excellence. Unlike the sign of Heaven's indifference, this signal is affirmative: it declares mastery, adventure, the mark of a shaping intelligence. The shift from divine sign (passive, received, interpreted) to human signal (active, sent, declared) traces the arc of the poem's own argument about mortal creativity. Mortals may not receive favorable signs from Heaven, but they can send signals of their own making.

### Signs of Earthly Identity

At 004-155, the "red-hot son of Earth" is known "by all her signs." The signs here are not messages sent but attributes displayed — the heat, the redness, the filiation with Earth itself. These signs authenticate identity. They function as evidence that does not need to be spoken because it is embodied. The "red-hot son" does not announce himself; his signs announce him. Meredith's language here draws on the ancient notion of the hero marked by visible tokens of parentage — the scar, the birthmark, the inherited weapon — but transforms it into a poetics of inherent, radiating selfhood.

### Where Innocence Signals Nay

The earliest occurrence, at 003-069, uses the verbal form in a compressed ethical drama: "Where innocence, not nature, signals nay." The line distinguishes two sources of refusal — innocence and nature — and awards the signal to innocence alone. Nature does not refuse; innocence does. The verb "signals" suggests communication without speech, a gesture or a presence that communicates prohibition. In the moral economy of the early poem, innocence has a signaling power that nature lacks, and the sign it sends is the limit that nature would not set.
