## Overview

The wall in Meredith's poetry is rarely a mere architectural feature. It functions as a figure for the limits that structure experience: the walls of distance that separate the perceiver from the perceived, the webster's wall that frames the artist's work, the swallowed byways that mark the edge of the known. Meredith inherits the wall from a long poetic tradition — the walls of Troy, the walls of the prison, the walls of the garden — and he adapts it to his own philosophical purposes. The wall is never simply an obstacle in his work; it is a condition of seeing, the frame that makes perception possible even as it limits it. To know a wall is to know one's own boundedness, and for Meredith this knowledge is the beginning of wisdom rather than its defeat.

## In the Poems

### Walls of Distance

In "The Sage Enamoured and the Honest Lady," Meredith develops the image of walls as perceptual boundaries. "Off walls of distance, left each mount" (014-023) transforms physical geography into a figure for cognitive limitation: what we see is what bounces back from the walls that surround us. The mount — a natural elevation, presumably offering a broader view — is nevertheless bounded by walls of distance, the horizon that perception cannot penetrate. The poet does not lament this condition; he maps it, insisting on clarity about what can and cannot be known.

### Arachne's Web and Wall

The most sustained architectural meditation comes in "Arachne," where the spider builds "Direct upon my webster's wall" (015-047). The wall here is the canvas, the ground of artistic production — not a barrier but a surface of inscription. The webster (weaver, poet) works directly upon the wall, transforming the blank vertical plane into pattern and meaning. The wall that confines is also the wall that enables: without it, there is nothing to build upon. Meredith's implicit identification of poet with spider makes the wall a figure for the conditions of art itself.

### The Swallowed Byway

The collection registers the wall's negative through the image of the byway that is swallowed, the opening that is closed. "Revealed where lured the swallower byway" (014-020) and "The posts that named the swallowed mile" (006-042) use the vocabulary of consumption to describe the closing of paths — the wall as devourer of possibility. The swallowed byway is the road not taken because it was never visible, the option that the walls of circumstance have already foreclosed.

### The Wall as Sceptical Figure

In "The Test of Manhood," the imagery of walls feeds into a broader scepticism. "On Doubt; for pastime swallows miracles" (016-197) extends the swallowing motif into epistemology — doubt swallows miracles as the wall swallows the byway. The wall, in this philosophical register, is the limit of credulity, the boundary that the mind cannot cross without abandoning its integrity. Meredith's characteristic move is to treat this boundedness not as tragedy but as honesty, the clear-eyed acceptance of creaturely limits.
