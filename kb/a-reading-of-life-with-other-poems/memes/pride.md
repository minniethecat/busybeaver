## Overview

Pride in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the most ambivalent of the virtues. It is what drives the hero forward — "pricked to race past the pride in giant limb" (004-166) — and what threatens to undo him: "his pitch of pride would not disown" (004-177) the very limitations that will bring him down. Meredith inherits the classical understanding of pride (*superbia* in the Latin tradition) as the sin that invites divine correction, but he complicates it with a modern psychological interest in pride as a necessary component of selfhood.

To have no pride is to have no self worth defending; to have too much is to defend a self that has become a fortress against growth. Meredith's poetry maps this tension across physical, moral, and natural domains, never resolving it into a simple moral formula.

## In the Poems

Pride appears in the collection in three distinct manifestations:

### Pride as Competitive Drive

Meredith describes a figure "pricked to race past the pride in giant limb" (004-166). The verb "pricked" suggests a goad, a stimulus that is almost physical — pride here is not a settled attitude but an active spur. It drives the self beyond its current limits, making the "giant limb" not a terminal achievement but a marker to be surpassed. This is pride in its most positive aspect: the refusal to accept one's present capacities as final.

### Pride as Refusal to Bend

The counter-movement appears immediately: "Withal his pitch of pride would not disown" (004-177). The word "pitch" — a term from falconry for the height a bird reaches before stooping — suggests pride as altitude, a position attained through effort. But the refusal to "disown" this pitch means that pride becomes a trap: having climbed so high, the self cannot descend without losing what it has defined itself by. This is the classical pattern: pride raises you up, then prevents you from stepping back from the edge.

### Pride as Natural Vitality

In a lighter register, Meredith captures pride as sheer physical exuberance: "A pride of legs in motion kept" (006-039). This is pride without moral weight — the body's delight in its own capacity. The phrase "pride of legs" is almost comic, a celebration of locomotion for its own sake. Here Meredith acknowledges that not all pride is morally charged; some of it is simply the animal pleasure of being alive and capable.

### Pride in Homeric Flight

The translated Homeric passages offer a final register: "on they fly, rejoicing in pride of their pinions" (017-065). The pride belongs to birds in flight, and it is "rejoicing" — an active, joyful possession of capacity. This is pride as the natural accompaniment of power rightly used. In the epic world, pride is not a sin but a fact: the strong are proud, and their pride is part of what makes them admirable.
