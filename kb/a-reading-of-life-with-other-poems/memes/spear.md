## Overview

The spear in Meredith's poetry operates at the intersection of classical inheritance and psychological insight. As the weapon of Ares and the defining arm of Homeric warriors, the spear carries the weight of the epic tradition — but Meredith does not simply invoke it as period decoration. He uses the spear to probe the relationship between force and domination, between the weapon and the hand that wields it, and between the epic past and the moral questions it leaves unresolved.

The spear appears in two distinct contexts: the mythological narrative of "The Cageing of Ares," where it is the war god's own instrument, "implacable" (005-047) and "pointed" toward domination (005-058); and Meredith's translation of the *Iliad*, where the "spear-armed Trojans" (017-006) are invoked by Achilles in his great invective. Across both, the spear serves as Meredith's figure for the concentration of martial force — a single point that can dominate multitudes, yet one that can also be seized, redirected, or rendered helpless.

## In the Poems

### The God's Own Weapon: Ares Disarmed

Meredith's most vivid spear passage occurs in "The Cageing of Ares," where the war god's "implacable spear" (005-047) is seized by the giant twins who ambush him. The adjective "implacable" is crucial — the spear is not merely sharp but unappeasable, driven by a will that cannot be negotiated with or turned aside. It is the physical extension of Ares' own nature: relentless, pitiless, beyond reason.

Yet the spear is taken from him. The twins "seized on his implacable spear, / Hugged him to reedy helplessness despite / His godlike fury" (005-047–049). The weapon that should guarantee invincibility becomes the instrument of the god's humiliation — seized, held, rendered useless. Meredith's irony is precise: the god of war, defined by his weapon, is nothing without it. The spear that points outward at enemies cannot protect him from being grasped from behind.

### Pointed Toward Domination: The Spear as Social Instrument

Later in the same passage, Ares watches the giant twins at play and imagines them trained to arms: "Good servitors of Ares they would be, / And ply the pointed spear to dominate / Their rebel restless fellows" (005-057–059). Here the spear shifts from mythic attribute to political instrument. To "ply the pointed spear" is not merely to fight but to "dominate" — the spear becomes the tool by which social order is enforced, rebellion suppressed, hierarchy maintained.

Meredith's choice of "ply" suggests habitual, almost professional use — the spear is not wielded in a single heroic moment but applied systematically, as a trade or craft. The domination it enables is not the glory of epic combat but the drudgery of control. In this, Meredith anticipates a modern critique of military power: the spear is not an end but a means, and its end is subjugation.

### Achilles and the Spear-Armed Enemy

In Meredith's translation of the *Iliad* — "Fragments of the Iliad in English Hexameter Verse" — the spear appears in Achilles' invective against Agamemnon: "I, not hither I fared on account of the spear-armed Trojans, / Pledged to the combat; they unto me have in nowise a harm done" (017-006–007). The "spear-armed Trojans" are the ostensible enemy, yet Achilles insists they have done him no personal injury — his true grievance is with his own commander.

The epithet "spear-armed" functions as a Homeric formula, but Meredith's hexameter rendering gives it new weight. The Trojans are defined by their weaponry — it is what makes them a threat, what gives them identity as an enemy. Yet Achilles' point is precisely that the spear does not tell the whole story: the Trojans have not stolen his horses or ravaged his harvests. The spear signals martial identity, but martial identity is not the source of Achilles' rage.
