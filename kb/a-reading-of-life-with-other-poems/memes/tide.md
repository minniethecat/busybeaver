## Overview

The word "tide" appears six times across four poems in Meredith's corpus, and in each occurrence it carries the weight of rhythmic inevitability. The tide is not merely water in motion but water governed by law — the gravitational pull of moon and sun that makes the sea rise and fall with predictive certainty. Meredith exploits this double character throughout: the tide is both wild (a "hot tide," a "high tide" of sap) and orderly (a "morningtide" that brings beauty, a "tide of nourishment" that sustains). This tension between passion and pattern makes the tide one of Meredith's most philosophically productive natural images.

The tide differs from the stream or river in one crucial respect: it returns. A river flows one way; a tide ebbs and floods, withdraws and advances. This cyclical structure makes the tide a natural figure for hope, for the return of what has been lost, for the rhythm of feeling that Meredith tracks across his poetry.

## In the Poems

### The Hot Tide: Passion's Surge

The earliest occurrence is also the most intense: "One hot tide the rapturous race" (002-028). The tide here is "hot" — heated by passion, by the chase, by the urgency of pursuit. It is also singular: "one hot tide," a single overwhelming surge that carries the "rapturous race" (the hunt, but also the human race in its pursuit of beauty and truth). The adjective "rapturous" aligns the tide with ecstasy, with being carried beyond oneself. The tide is not something observed from shore but something entered, surrendered to, a force that lifts and bears the swimmer.

### Sea-Nymphs and the Flushing Tide: Mythological Register

The tide acquires mythological resonance in a compressed image: "shoal of sea-nymphs flush the tide" (003-020). The sea-nymphs — Nereids, daughters of the sea — do not merely ride the tide but actively "flush" it, causing it to rise and surge. The mythological register elevates the tide from natural phenomenon to divine agency: the tide is not impersonal physics but the work of living presences, nymphs who inhabit and animate the water. This is Meredith at his most classically allusive, but the image also serves his larger purpose of refusing the modern divorce between spirit and nature.

### Life's Tide Arrested: The Gathering of Strength

A crucial philosophical use of the tide image appears in a line that concerns the conservation of vital force: "of gathered strength Life's tide arrest" (003-135). The syntax is compressed but the sense is clear: life's tide can be arrested — checked, held back — by gathered strength. The verb "arrest" is legal as well as physical: to arrest is to stop by authority, to bring under control. The implication is that life's tide, left to itself, would flood unchecked; it requires strength — gathered, concentrated, deliberate — to hold it back, to channel it, to prevent it from becoming destructive.

The image reverses the usual valuation: it is not the tide that is strong but the strength that arrests it. The human capacity for self-governance, for the channeling of vital force, is what Meredith celebrates here.

### High Tide of Sap: The Wild Surge Within

The tide turns inward and upward in a visceral image: "When the wild sap at high tide smites" (003-321). The sap — the life-force within the tree, within the body — rises to "high tide" and "smites," strikes, hits with force. The verb "smites" is violent, even biblical: this is not a gentle rising but a blow, an impact. The "wild sap" is the tide of vitality at its most untamed, and its high tide is a moment of maximum pressure, maximum danger, maximum creative potential. Meredith's honesty about the violence of inner life — its smiting force — distinguishes his nature poetry from mere pastoral prettiness.

### Morningtide: Beauty's Fullness

A gentler but equally significant use transforms the word into a compound: "aethereal Beauty in full morningtide" (004-014). Here "tide" becomes "morningtide" — the tide of morning, the fullness of dawn. Beauty is "aethereal" (ethereal, heavenly, of the upper air) and it appears "in full morningtide" — at the peak moment of the morning's tide. The compound word fuses time and water: morning is not merely a time of day but a tide, a rising, a fullness that comes and (by implication) will go. That beauty is most fully present at the tide's peak — not at its ebb — suggests that the aesthetic life, like the tidal life, has its rhythms of fullness and withdrawal.

### The Tide of Nourishment: Paternal Care

The final occurrence returns the tide to its most elemental function — sustenance: "where his tide of nourishment he drives" (016-218). The tide here is explicitly paternal: "his tide of nourishment" is the father's sustaining flow, the current of care and provision that "he drives" toward his dependents. The verb "drives" is active and forceful — nourishment is not passively offered but actively propelled, like a tide pushed by gravitational force. This is the tide at its most benevolent: not the hot tide of passion nor the smiting tide of wild sap, but the steady, rhythmic, sustaining current of love.
