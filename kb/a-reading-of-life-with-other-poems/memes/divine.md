## Overview

Divinity in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is less a theological claim than an epistemic one. The word "divine" and its variants appear across the collection not as the name of a being but as the description of a human activity: divining. To divine is to perceive what is hidden, to read signs without full evidence, to know through insight rather than demonstration. Meredith treats the divine as the limit case of human knowing — what we reach toward when ordinary perception fails.

This is a characteristically Victorian agnosticism, but with a positive valence. Meredith is not mourning the loss of traditional deity; he is celebrating the human capacity for divination — for perceiving meaning and order in a world that does not directly reveal them.

## In the Poems

### The Act of Divining

In *A Reading of Life* (chapter 3), Meredith describes a mode of perception that operates without sensory evidence: "Unread, divined; unseen, beheld" (003-036). The chiasmus is exact: what is unread is divined, what is unseen is beheld. Divination is the cognitive operation that compensates for the limits of reading and seeing. It is not supernatural — it is the natural human capacity to infer pattern and meaning from fragmentary evidence. Meredith presents divining as a legitimate and necessary mode of knowledge, not a poor substitute for direct observation.

### The Division Between Orders

The poem confronts the philosophical problem directly: "Division between natural and divine" (003-133). The line names the gap that traditional theology bridges with revelation. But Meredith does not offer a bridge; he offers a question: "Doth man divide divine Necessity" (003-308). The verb "divide" appears twice — first as a noun naming the separation between realms, then as a verb asking whether humanity itself creates this division. The implication is radical: the line between natural and divine may be a human imposition rather than a feature of reality. We divide divine necessity; we create the categories that we then struggle to reconcile.

### The Known God

In *The Test of Manhood* (chapter 4), Meredith develops a theology of the known: "His God the Known, diviner to adore" (004-143). The phrase "God the Known" is Meredith's most direct theological statement — divinity is not the unknown mystery but what is known through divination. And the comparative "diviner" (more divine) attaches to the act of adoration itself: to adore is to become more divine. The divine is not a static attribute of a distant being but a quality that increases through human reverence and attention. A related passage deepens this: "A stranger still, religiously divined" (004-095) — the divine is "divined" through a "religious" attitude, but the object of divination remains "a stranger," irreducible to full knowledge.

### The Divine Conceit

In a lighter register, chapter 6 describes something that "lived in such divine conceit" (006-061). "Conceit" here carries its double sense: a witty poetic idea and a self-regarding pride. To live in divine conceit is to inhabit a creative self-understanding that is audacious, perhaps hubristic, but also generative. The phrase suggests that the divine, in Meredith's vocabulary, is entangled with the aesthetic — with the poet's own capacity to make and to imagine.
