## Overview

Reason in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is never merely instrumental. It is "right reason" — a normative faculty whose proper operation unites knowledge with virtue.

The phrase "right reason" appears at the heart of "Foresight and Patience," the collection's most sustained philosophical argument: "Whose union is right reason: join they hands, / The world shall know itself and where it stands" (016-007–008). The union in question is that of Foresight and Patience — the capacity to see ahead and the capacity to endure. Right reason is not a third faculty added to these two but their mode of conjunction, the form their cooperation takes.

This is a deeply ethical conception of reason, closer to the classical *phronesis* than to Enlightenment rationalism. Reason is not the manipulation of abstract propositions but the skill of living well, the ability to "hold the reason in perceptive thought" (003-117) — to keep reason engaged with the concrete particulars of experience rather than letting it float into empty abstraction.

Meredith's reason is also a political faculty. When the world "knows itself and where it stands," the result is not merely intellectual satisfaction but existential orientation — a people that knows its position can act effectively. Reason is what makes collective action possible by providing shared understanding.

## In the Poems

### Right Reason and Its Union

The pivotal claim of "Foresight and Patience" is that reason is not a solitary achievement but the product of a union. Foresight — the capacity to anticipate consequences — and Patience — the capacity to endure delay — must "join hands" for right reason to emerge.

The bodily metaphor of joined hands is deliberate. Reason is not abstract; it is embodied in relationship, in the cooperation of distinct faculties that remain distinct even as they work together. Foresight and Patience do not merge into a single undifferentiated power; they join hands while remaining themselves.

The consequence of this union is world-disclosing: "The world shall know itself and where it stands." Reason reveals reality not as a set of isolated facts but as a positioned whole. To know "where it stands" is to know one's place within a larger order — a kind of knowledge that is simultaneously intellectual, moral, and existential.

### Reason Versus the Age

The dialogue in "Foresight and Patience" turns to the state of reason in the modern world: "Its learning is through Science to despair" (016-091). The Age — the Victorian present — has abundant "learning" but its learning leads to "despair" rather than to wisdom. Science, pursued without right reason, becomes a path to meaninglessness.

The response to this despair is not to abandon learning but to reorient it: "Bright as you are, to me the Age will turn" (016-089). Foresight predicts that the Age, having exhausted the path of despair, will eventually "turn" to Patience — that is, to the kind of knowledge that requires waiting, enduring, and remaining in the difficulty rather than escaping into premature closure.

This turning is the work of right reason. It is not a rejection of Science but a correction of its trajectory, a reconnection of knowledge to the moral virtues that give knowledge its proper use.

### Treason Versus Reason

In "The Test of Manhood," reason appears in a darker context — the context of betrayal: "Whereby he smelt his treason, who implored" (004-067). The line is gnomic, but its thrust is clear: reason enables the detection of treason, the recognition of betrayal. The verb "smelt" is sensory and animal — reason operates through a kind of trained instinct, an olfactory sensitivity to moral danger.

The man who "implored" — who begged for supernatural aid — is the man who "smelt his treason" through reason. His treason was precisely the abandonment of his own rational capacity in favor of "imagined succour" (004-053), the fantasy of divine rescue that "slew / The soul of brotherhood whence Reverence drew" (004-053–054).

This passage connects reason to the political order. When men abandon reason for imagined supernatural aid, they destroy the "soul of brotherhood" — the horizontal bonds between persons that are the foundation of genuine reverence. Reason is what keeps reverence grounded in human relationship rather than in fantasy.

### Reason in Perceptive Thought

"With the Persuader" offers the most compact formulation of Meredith's epistemology: the goddess-figure "holds the reason in perceptive thought" (003-117). The verb "holds" suggests both possession and restraint — reason is not permitted to float free but is kept within "perceptive thought," thought that remains anchored in perception, in the concrete particulars of experience.

The line appears in the context of the goddess's dual nature — she is both the force of attraction and the force of discernment. She teaches the aspiring soul to "reason" (003-306) not as an abstract exercise but as a mode of engagement with what is actually present: "He reasons; and the totterer Earth detests" (003-306). Reason properly exercised leads to contempt for what is unstable and false — the "totterer Earth" of mere appearances.

This percipient reason is the faculty by which the lover distinguishes the goddess from her counterfeits, the real from the illusory. It is reason in service of love, not reason as love's rival.

### The Vanity of Unheeded Reason

The later poems strike a darker note. "Foresight and Patience" laments that the Age calls "To conscience, reason, human love; in vain" (016-118). The triad — conscience, reason, human love — represents Meredith's summation of the moral faculties, and all three are summoned "in vain" by an Age that will not hear them.

The phrase "in vain" is the poem's bleakest judgment. It is not that reason is powerless but that it is unheeded — the Age has the capacity to hear reason but chooses not to. This is a political diagnosis as much as a philosophical one: the modern world suffers not from a deficit of rationality but from a deficit of the will to be rational.

The final image of reason betrayed appears in "The Mares of the Camargue": "By treason snared, some captives shown" (018-013). The "captives" are those who have been caught by "treason" — by the betrayal of reason in favor of something baser. The image of captivity suggests that the abandonment of reason is not liberation but enslavement, a falling into bonds that reason would have detected and avoided.
