## Overview

Clouds in Meredith's poetry serve as agents of transformation and mediation. They gather, obscure, dissolve, and release — their lifecycle mirroring the patterns of thought, emotion, and natural process that the poems explore. A cloud can be a weight that "opens" the mind (004-084), a "mountain" of laughter (005-031), or the raw material of rain and fruit (004-194). Meredith draws on the Romantic tradition of cloud symbolism — Shelley's "The Cloud" is a clear antecedent — while adapting it to his own philosophical concerns. Clouds mark the threshold between visibility and concealment, between the formless vapour and the formed precipitation, making them apt figures for the processes of poetic creation and intellectual illumination.

## In the Poems

### Cloud as Burden and Revelation

The most philosophically concentrated cloud passage appears in "The Test of Manhood," where a mind "opened under weight of cloud" (004-084). The image inverts expectation: cloud, typically a figure of obscurity, here becomes the catalyst for opening. The weight does not crush but induces expansion, as if pressure from above forces the mind to unfold. This paradox recurs in "a clouded lantern-ray" (014-017), where the cloud modifies but does not extinguish the light — the lantern still shines, its beam diffused rather than blocked. In the epic context, "the chillness of a passing cloud" (016-117) is momentary, transient, a shadow that moves across the spirit and then passes, suggesting that even the darkest moods are temporary visitations.

### Cloud and the Cyclical Economy of Nature

"To change, as rain of cloud, as fruit of rain" (004-194) condenses a complete natural cycle into a single line. Cloud becomes rain, rain becomes fruit — each transformation is both an ending and a beginning. This chain of metamorphosis is Meredith's shorthand for the productive logic of nature, where nothing is wasted and every dissolution feeds a new formation. The poem "The Thrush in February" opens with "folded showers in streamer cloud" (006-003), presenting cloud as the container of precipitation, the cloud's streamers trailing rain not yet released. The cloud holds, then yields; its nature is to gather and to give.

### Cloud in Epic and Battle Imagery

The Homeric register of the later poems deploys cloud at monumental scale. The "dust-cloud" that mounts "up off the plain, raised up cloud-thick by the thundering" (017-097, 017-098) transforms a natural atmospheric phenomenon into an image of martial chaos. The dust raised by armies becomes a cloud, and the cloud in turn becomes a figure for the obscurity of battle — the combatants hidden from view, the outcome uncertain. This usage bridges the natural and the martial: cloud is at once meteorological fact and epic simile, a real atmospheric condition and a figure for the fog of war.

### Cloud and the Golden Light

Meredith often positions cloud in relation to gold or light. "Free of the cloud, beneath the flood of gold" (003-165) presents cloud as that from which one must be freed to experience illumination. Gold is the reward for emerging from cloud-cover. The "mountain-cloud of laughter banked around" (005-031) domesticates the sublime: laughter, not storm, is what this cloud contains, and it is "banked" — stored, reserved, held in place — rather than threatening. The cloud here is a reservoir of joy, its eventual release promising not destruction but delight. "Onward, like the wind's cloud-fleets" (004-192) offers the gentlest image: clouds as a fleet sailing across the sky, driven by wind, purposeful in their passage, their destination implicit in their motion.
