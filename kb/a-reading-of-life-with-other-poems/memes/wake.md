## Overview

Wake in Meredith's poetry operates at the boundary between states: between sleep and waking, between death and life, between ignorance and knowledge. The word carries its full English ambiguity — to wake is to rouse from slumber, but a wake is also the vigil kept over the dead. Meredith exploits both senses. When "she wakens gleeful cries" (003-090) the verb names the birth of consciousness out of unfeeling matter; when the poet speaks of those who "wake a sigh" (008-006) the noun hovers between the two meanings. Across the collection, waking is never neutral — it is always a passage into a more demanding condition, whether the demand is joy, grief, or moral responsibility. The poet treats unconsciousness as a temptation and waking as a discipline, aligning the concept with his larger concern for the strenuous life of full awareness.

## In the Poems

### Waking as Moral Imperative

In "The Test of Manhood," Meredith frames waking as an ethical demand. Earth "would have us wallow" (001-008), the inertia of the sensual pulling downward into unconsciousness, while the poet insists on the harder path of alertness. The line "Still is she unaware she wakens thirst" (003-073) captures the paradox — nature itself can be sleepwalking, generating desire without knowing it. To wake, in Meredith's moral vocabulary, is to know what one wants and why, to move from instinct to intention.

### The Gleeful Cry

Nature's waking is not always solemn. "She laughs, she wakens gleeful cries" (003-090) celebrates the spontaneity of consciousness bursting forth. The line belongs to a passage where Meredith traces the emergence of mind from matter, and the glee of the cry registers the sheer creaturely pleasure of being alive. The waking here is not from sleep but from the long unconsciousness of pre-human nature — an evolutionary awakening that the poet treats with something close to wonder.

### Waking and Blood

The most troubling registration of wake in the collection comes through the image of blood. Meredith speaks of "living by an abject blood, that waked" (005-123), where waking is tied to the violent substrate of existence — blood as the life-principle that rouses, but also as the mark of predation and sacrifice. The poet does not flinch from the cost of consciousness: to be awake is to be implicated in the economy of life and death that sustains the natural order.

### The Sigh of Awakening

Elsewhere the tone softens: "They wake a sigh" (008-006) and "How waken them that have not any bent" (016-166). These lines attend to the difficulty of rousing those who lack inclination — a problem as much pedagogical as existential. The final image of the collection registers "another mass awake" (016-175), suggesting that waking is not solitary but collective, a shared condition of humanity that the poet both urges and mourns.
