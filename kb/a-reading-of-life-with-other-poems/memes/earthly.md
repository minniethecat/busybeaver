## Overview

The word "earthly" in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) marks a crucial boundary in his philosophical vocabulary. It names the realm of embodied experience — the domain of bodies, love, and mortal limitation — and sets it in productive tension with what lies beyond. Meredith does not treat the earthly as something to be escaped. Instead, his compound adjectives "over-earthly" and "inner-earthly" (003-032) suggest that the earthly contains dimensions that reach beyond and within it simultaneously.

For a poet who insistently grounds value in the material and the bodily, "earthly" is not a term of diminishment. It is the adjective of the real. To be earthly is to be fully present in the conditions that define human existence.

## In the Poems

### The Compound Self

Meredith's most concentrated meditation on the earthly appears in *A Reading of Life* (chapter 3), where he describes humanity's "over-earthly, inner-earthly mate" (003-032). The hyphenated compounds are precise: human beings are simultaneously above the earthly (aspiring, reaching beyond) and within it (interior to the material condition). The doubling — "over" and "inner" — refuses the simple binary of matter and spirit. There is no pure transcendence for Meredith; even our highest aspirations are shaped by and rooted in the earthly substance of our being.

### Love's Terrestrial Quality

In *The Sage Enamoured and the Honest Lady* (chapter 7), Meredith examines the nature of love and finds it irreducibly earthly. He describes a love that "Was all of earthly in their love untold, / Beyond all earthly known to them who wed" (007-027–028). The paradox is deliberate: love is entirely earthly — made of the stuff of bodies, time, and mortal recognition — yet it exceeds what the conventionally married know of the earthly. The "untold" love is not less earthly but more deeply so; its earthliness is its authenticity. The couple who merely wed have not plumbed the earthly depths that true love reveals.

### Embodied Knowledge

Across these passages, Meredith advances a claim about knowledge. The earthly is not a barrier to understanding but its medium. To know something "over-earthly" is to know it through the earthly, not despite it. The "inner-earthly" is the dimension of experience accessible only through embodied presence — through touch, sight, and the particularities of mortal existence. This is Meredith's corrective to Victorian spiritualism: the divine, if it exists, must be sought within and through the earthly, not by escaping it.
