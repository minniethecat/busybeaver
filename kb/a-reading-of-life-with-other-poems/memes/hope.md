## Overview

Hope in Meredith's poetry is never simple optimism. It is a virtue that operates under pressure — hedged by despair, deferred by circumstance, and shadowed by mortality. The line "Divide despair from hope, and sighs from moans" (003-183) captures the narrowness of the distinction: hope and despair are separated not by a gulf but by a partition that must be actively maintained. Meredith's treatment of hope reflects the Victorian crisis of faith while refusing the easy consolations of either religious certainty or nihilistic resignation. Hope is not a guarantee but a posture — a way of facing forward when the outcome is unknown. "Content, but how to savour hope deferred" (016-050) names the central difficulty: hope that does not arrive tests the soul more than outright despair.

## In the Poems

### The Partition of Hope and Despair

Meredith's most concentrated reflection on hope appears in "The Test of Manhood" with the imperative "Divide despair from hope, and sighs from moans" (003-183). The verb "divide" implies active labor — hope is not a state into which one falls but a boundary that must be drawn and defended. The pairing of "despair from hope" with "sighs from moans" aligns the emotional with the somatic: hope and despair register in the body as different qualities of breath. A sigh is still respiration; a moan is closer to the inarticulate sound of suffering.

### The Ambush and the Rebel Heart

Hope often appears in Meredith's poetry under the sign of threat or defiance. "An ambush hoped, as heartless night remain" (003-024) places hope in a context of concealed danger — what is hoped for may be hostile. The question "Be rebel at the core? What hope is there?" (004-230) poses the existential challenge directly: if the self is inwardly rebellious against its own condition, on what grounds can hope be sustained? This is hope interrogated rather than hope asserted, characteristic of Meredith's philosophical method.

### Hope Deferred and Hope Beyond Guile

The later poems in the collection develop a more resigned but not defeated relationship to hope. "Content, but how to savour hope deferred" (016-050) acknowledges the difficulty of maintaining hope when its object recedes, while "Beyond disgust or hope in guile" (015-071) gestures toward a state that transcends both the cynicism of disgust and the naivety of hope placed in deceptive objects. "Past the one hope to repair" (012-005) marks the limit case — a situation where hope itself has been exhausted and what remains is endurance without the consolations of expectation.

### The Hopeless Wrestler

The image of "A hopeless wrestler shall thy soul contend" (010-004) presents hope's absence not as passivity but as continued struggle. The wrestler is hopeless — the outcome is foreclosed — yet the wrestling continues. This is Meredith's stoic inflection: when hope is gone, effort remains. The soul contends even without expectation of victory, and this contending is itself a form of virtue that transcends the hope-despair binary.
