## Overview

The "world" in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the most comprehensive term in his poetic vocabulary, designating the entire created order within which human life unfolds. It encompasses earth and heaven, nature and civilization, the physical and the moral. Meredith's world is dynamic, even turbulent — "the headlong world's imperious force" (016-017) — yet also capable of beauty and order: "Flushing the world with odorous flowers" (003-326). The world is the stage on which all the collection's dramas play out.

Meredith's treatment of the world reflects his philosophical ambition. He is not content with a merely picturesque world; he wants a world that can be read, interpreted, and understood. "The world shall know itself and where it stands" (016-008) is both prophecy and program — the poet's task is to bring the world to self-knowledge. This makes "world" not just a noun of scale but a concept of moral and epistemological significance.

## In the Poems

The world appears across at least five distinct chapters (003, 004, 006, 014, 015, 016), making it one of the most widely distributed concepts in the collection.

### The Underworld and the Depths

Meredith repeatedly gestures toward an "underworld" beneath the visible surface of things. "In dusk of the underworld's perpetual eve" (003-048) and "Down on the underworld's perpetual eve" (003-076) both locate a realm of darkness and mystery beneath the daylight world. This underworld is "perpetual" — its twilight never yields to dawn. It represents not hell in the Christian sense but something closer to the classical Hades: a realm of shadows that underlies and conditions the visible world. To know the world fully, Meredith implies, one must acknowledge what lies beneath it.

### The World Made Beautiful

Against the underworld's darkness, Meredith sets images of the world's capacity for beauty. "Flushing the world with odorous flowers" (003-326) presents a world suddenly suffused with colour and scent — a spring-like eruption of beauty that transforms the entire order. The verb "flushing" suggests both the rush of colour (as in a blush) and the suddenness of the transformation. "By heavenly: and the world is hers" (003-328) follows immediately, linking the world's beauty to a feminine divine principle — the world belongs to the one who can make it beautiful.

### The World's Disorder

Meredith does not flinch from the world's darkness. "Whence Evil in a world unread before" (004-141) poses the problem of evil as a question of reading — evil arises in a world that has not yet been interpreted, understood, or brought to moral legibility. "His tumbled world" (004-226) captures the experience of personal catastrophe in a single adjective: the world tumbles, falls into disorder, and must be rebuilt. The "roaring world" (014-044) from which gentle maidens flee suggests a world of noise and violence that threatens the delicate and the peaceful.

### The World in Calm and Order

Meredith also envisions a world at equilibrium. "A sober world that walks the balanced mean" (004-178) is a vision of moral and cosmic moderation — the world as Aristotle might have wished it, walking the golden mean. This sober world is not dull but "balanced," a state of dynamic equilibrium rather than stasis. "I watched amid a world in flower" (015-088) presents the lyrical counterpoint: a world of pure aesthetic presence, in bloom, to be contemplated rather than struggled against.

### The World's Self-Knowledge

The collection's teleological vision for the world emerges in Chapter 16: "The world shall know itself and where it stands" (016-008). This knowledge is the world's destined achievement — not humanity's knowledge of the world, but the world's own self-knowledge. The poet's "true pupils" are those who learn "while the world is brute" (016-045), suggesting that the world's present state is one of unrefined power and that education — the poet's gift — is what transforms brute force into self-aware order. "Delighted with our world's embrace" (006-016) captures the reciprocal joy of a world that can at last embrace its inhabitants knowingly.
