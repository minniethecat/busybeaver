## Biography

Ares is the Greek god of war, the son of [[zeus]] and Hera in the Olympian pantheon. Unlike his Roman counterpart Mars, who was venerated as a guardian of the state, the Greek Ares embodied war in its most destructive and chaotic dimensions — the "blood-shedder, the terrible Lord of War" as Meredith renders him (005-041). In Greek mythology he appears as a figure of brute force rather than strategic command, often humiliated despite his fearsome reputation.

In the Homeric epics, Ares fights on the side of the Trojans but is wounded by Diomedes — with Athena's aid — and forced to retreat to Olympus. His affair with [[aphrodite]], the goddess of love, and their exposure before the laughing gods by Hephaestus is one of the most memorable episodes in the *Odyssey*. This duality — fearsome power yoked to vulnerability — makes Ares a natural subject for Meredith's exploration of strength and its limits.

## The Cageing of Ares

Meredith's poem *The Cageing of Ares* (chapter 5 in this collection, dedicated to the Council at The Hague) reimagines the myth of Ares's imprisonment by the Aloadae — the giant brothers Otos and Ephialtes, sons of the Earth goddess Gaea. In Meredith's telling, the giants are not monstrous but playful, "sportive, suckling mammoths" (005-035), who combine "strength and craft" (005-039) to ensnare the god:

> Great Ares. And they gagged his trumpet mouth
> When they had seized on his implacable spear,
> Hugged him to reedy helplessness despite
> His godlike fury startled from amaze. (005-046)

The poem interprets the caging not as a mere mythological anecdote but as an allegory of civilization's triumph over the war instinct. Gaea — Mother Earth — "loathed him" (005-066), knowing Ares as "the famine, pestilence and waste; / A desolating fire" (005-067). Once caged, the world enters a "glad year" (005-083) of peaceful cultivation, where "battle" becomes a metaphor for creative labor rather than bloodshed:

> Exchange of powers of this conflict came;
> Defacement none, nor ever squandered force. (005-094)

## Ares in the Iliad Fragments

Ares appears more briefly but still significantly in Meredith's *Fragments of the Iliad in English Hexameter Verse* (chapter 17). In the Marshalling of the Achaians, King Agamemnon is described with a compound simile that invokes multiple deities:

> He with the girdle of Ares, he with the breast of Poseidon. (017-090)

This physical metaphor — the "girdle of Ares" — conveys martial prowess worn as equipment rather than innate quality, suggesting war as something a leader puts on and (by implication) may choose to take off. The image resonates with the earlier poem's portrayal of Ares as a force that can be "caged" — contained and controlled — by those who understand its nature.

## Symbolism in Meredith's Poetry

Meredith's treatment of Ares operates on three interconnected levels:

| Level | Meaning | Evidence |
|-------|---------|----------|
| Mythological | The imprisoned war-god as classical figure | Otos and Ephialtes bind Ares in the cave (005-046) |
| Political | War contained by international law | Dedication to the Council at The Hague (005-002) |
| Philosophical | The war instinct as an evolutionary force that can be sublimated | "Is battle nature's mandate, here it reigned, / As music unto the hand that smote the strings" (005-096) |

The poem's dedication to the 1899 Hague Peace Conference places Meredith's mythological imagination in direct dialogue with contemporary politics. The "thirteen songful months" (005-077) of Ares's imprisonment evoke the thirteen months of the pre-conference year, and the eventual release of Ares — hinted at when Hermes is prompted to "shatter earth's delirious holiday" (005-079) — suggests the poet's clear-eyed recognition that peace is a fragile interval, not a permanent condition.
