## Overview

Spring in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is more than a season — it is the collection's governing metaphor for the irrepressible character of life itself. "Resplendent springs, to faith refreshed compels" (004-238) names the renewing power at its most forceful: spring compels belief, it leaves no room for doubt, it is the visible refutation of winter's apparent finality. Meredith's spring is not delicate or tentative; it is a "burning lion" (005-143) — fierce, urgent, claiming its ground.

The word "spring" resonates across the collection in three interrelated registers: the season of renewal, the verb of sudden emergence ("to spring"), and the noun of origin ("the spring of life"). Meredith exploits all three, weaving them into a unified vision of life as that which eternally springs forth from hidden sources, that which cannot be permanently suppressed or exhausted.

## In the Poems

### The Seasonal Spring

Meredith's seasonal spring arrives with violence, not gentleness. "And yet a burning lion for the spring" (005-143) images the season as a predatory beast, burning with the heat of renewed life. This is not the mild spring of pastoral tradition but a spring that seizes and transforms, that demands as much as it gives. The "burning lion" is an unsettling figure for a season usually associated with lambs and flowers, and the dissonance is deliberate — Meredith wants to recover the ferocity of genuine renewal.

"In the bountiful season of spring-time" (017-079) offers the more conventional register, but it appears in a Homeric context where the "bountiful" season serves as the backdrop for martial action. The spring is not an escape from the world's conflicts but the condition in which they unfold — the vital setting that makes action, including violent action, possible.

### Spring as Sudden Emergence

The verb "to spring" — meaning to leap, to burst forth, to arise suddenly — recurs throughout the collection and carries much of its energetic charge. "For the one whelming wave to spring aloof" (003-149) images the wave's upward leap, a vertical motion that counters the horizontal sweep of the sea. "She breathes low, he springs" (003-210) pairs feminine exhalation with masculine leaping, the genders distinguished by their mode of vital expression.

"Lustily spring off soil for the shoot up aloft" (017-143) is the most explicitly botanical use of the verb: the plant springs from the soil with "lusty" energy, the adverb insisting on the erotic character of growth. Meredith's spring is always a desire made visible — the shoot springs because it wants to, because life's nature is to reach upward.

### Spring as Origin

"A fountain jets from moss; a flower" (003-058) images the hidden spring — the water-source — as the origin of both the fountain's jet and the flower's bloom. The spring is what lies beneath, the invisible source that feeds the visible display. "Rocks that spout their springs" (003-313) makes the geological literal: even stone, the image of permanence and solidity, contains springs that burst forth. Nothing in Meredith's world is entirely sealed; everything has its hidden outlet, its secret source of flow.

"Of whence we spring and what we are" (012-019) pivots from physical to metaphysical origin. To ask "whence we spring" is to inquire into the ultimate source of human being. Meredith's answer, developed across the collection, is that humanity springs from the same vital ground as nature — from blood and earth and the "primal strata" (015-027) that antedate consciousness. "Out of the primal strata springs" (015-027) — beauty, consciousness, the human itself — all "spring" from the geological deep.

### The Prayer and the Source

"So to Divinity the spring of prayer" (004-061) identifies prayer's origin not in human need but in divine abundance. Prayer springs from Divinity back to Divinity — a circulation rather than a one-way petition. The "spring of prayer" is both prayer's source and prayer's character: it is a welling-up, a spontaneous overflow, not a calculated transaction.

"That mystery to simple springs resolved" (004-142) describes the mind's work of tracing complexity back to its simple origins. The "springs" are the elementary sources from which elaborate systems of thought and feeling derive. To resolve mystery to its springs is not to debunk it but to understand its genealogy — to see that the most complex phenomena have their origin in the simplest vital impulses.
