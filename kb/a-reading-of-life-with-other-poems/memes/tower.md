## Overview

The tower appears twice in Meredith's corpus, each time in a distinct register: once as an image of divine election and once as a figure for natural predation. This duality captures a tension that runs through Meredith's work: the human impulse to build upward — toward God, toward power, toward transcendence — and the natural world's indifference to those structures. The tower is both "chosen" and contested, a mark of aspiration and a site of vulnerability.

## In the Poems

### Chosen Towers Uppermost

The first occurrence frames the tower in explicitly theological terms: "That 'tis Thy chosen towers uppermost" (013-007). The apostrophe to the divine ("Thy") positions the tower as a structure belonging to God — chosen, elevated, set apart. "Uppermost" carries both spatial and spiritual meaning: the tower is physically highest and symbolically supreme, the point at which the human-made touches the divine and becomes, in some sense, sacred.

This image participates in a long tradition of tower symbolism — Babel, the watchtower, the church spire — but Meredith's compression leaves the referent open. The tower could be literal (a castle's defense), ecclesiastical (a spire reaching toward heaven), or metaphorical (the elevated station of the poet or thinker). What matters is the relationship of election: God has chosen these towers, marked them as His own, and in doing so has sanctified the human impulse to build upward.

### The Falcon's Castle Tower

The second occurrence shifts from the theological to the predatory: Meredith describes how "a castle's falcon towers" (014-064), using "towers" as a verb rather than a noun. The falcon — a bird of prey, associated with nobility and hunting — does not merely sit on the castle but "towers" from it, making the castle an extension of the bird's predatory height. The image fuses architecture and animal: the castle's tower and the falcon's towering flight become a single gesture of elevated power.

This usage reverses the spiritual trajectory of 013-007. Where the chosen tower reaches toward God, the falcon's tower reaches toward prey. Height in this passage is not sanctity but vantage — the strategic elevation from which the hunter sees and strikes. The tower, in this sense, is an instrument of natural power rather than a symbol of spiritual aspiration.

### The Double Life of Height

Taken together, the two occurrences reveal the tower as an image with a double life. Upward movement can be toward God (013-007) or toward domination (014-064); the same structure can signify election and predation. This ambivalence is characteristic of Meredith's poetic method, which rarely allows a symbol to settle into a single meaning. The tower is what humans build to rise above their condition — and like all human structures, it can serve opposed purposes depending on who occupies it and what they seek from the height.
