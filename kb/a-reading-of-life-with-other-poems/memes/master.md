## Overview

Mastery in Meredith's poetry is never simply a matter of domination. It appears as the "mastering mind" that has been shaped by tempests (004-109), the artistic capacity to resolve "mastered discords" into harmonious form (003-173), and the ambiguous "masterful" figures who hover at the edge of lawlessness (005-110). Across the collection, Meredith traces a philosophy of mastery that distinguishes between control won through struggle and control imposed by force. The master's grip (004-103) is both a mark of hard-won competence and a potential slide into tyranny. At the visionary horizon, the promise is that "wing shall master till men hear her teach" (016-034) — mastery not as command but as instruction, the flight that teaches by example rather than decree.

## In the Poems

### Artistic Mastery: Discord into Harmony

The earliest and most aesthetically significant treatment of mastery appears in the image of "mastered discords" (003-173). The phrasing is precise: discords are not eliminated but mastered — brought under a governing intelligence that makes their tension productive rather than destructive. This is Meredith's definition of the artistic act itself, and it resonates with his own metrical practice, in which irregular rhythms and dense syntax are held in check by formal control. The musician or poet who masters discord does not silence it; he conducts it into music. This conception of mastery as productive control rather than suppression runs through the collection and distinguishes Meredith's treatment from simpler celebrations of power.

### The Mastering Mind Tempered by Trial

The "mastering mind in him, by tempests blown" (004-109) is the mind that has earned its governance through exposure to force. The tempests are not merely external weather but the inward storms of passion and doubt; mastery is not native talent but the residue of what has been survived. The same passage traces how this mind "had come of many a grip in mastery" (004-103), where "grip" suggests both the wrestler's hold and the craftsman's practiced grasp of his tool. Mastery here is somatic — it inheres in the hand as much as in the intellect. Yet in the same poem-sequence, Meredith complicates the picture: the "masterful" figures who are also "lawless" (005-110) embody mastery without moral constraint. "Those beautiful, those masterful" (005-105) is a description edged with ambivalence — beauty and mastery together can produce either the sublime or the monstrous.

### The Master-Trick and the Ambiguity of Skill

"Some adventurous master-trick" (005-010) introduces mastery as cunning — the clever stroke, the strategic feint. In the context of the giants' rebellion (Ephialtes and Otos), the master-trick is the bold move that might overturn the cosmic order. Meredith does not moralize against it; instead, he registers both its daring and its danger. The master-trick is mastery applied to a single decisive moment, and its outcome determines whether the master is remembered as hero or as cautionary tale. This strand of the mastery theme connects to the Homeric episodes later in the collection, where battlefield cunning and strategic mastery decide the fates of nations.

### Wing as Master: The Visionary Horizon

The collection's culminating vision of mastery appears in the prophetic voice of "A Reading of Life": "Of wing shall master till men hear her teach" (016-034). Here mastery is figured as flight — not the grip that holds down but the wing that rises. The promise is eschatological: mastery will eventually transform into teaching, the master into the instructor, and force into persuasion. This is Meredith's resolution of the mastery paradox: true mastery abolishes its own coercive dimension and becomes the gentle authority of example.
