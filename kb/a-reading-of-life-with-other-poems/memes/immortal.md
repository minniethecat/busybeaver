## Overview

The immortal in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is not a theological promise but a natural claim. Nature herself "doth she rely for her immortal smile" (003-263) — her permanence needs no supernatural guarantee. The immortal is what persists: the species, the vital force, the beauty that outlasts any single vessel. Yet Meredith also uses the immortal to mark the limit of human aspiration, the "farthest bourne" (005-117) toward which the mortal spirit reaches. The immortal is thus both Nature's default possession and humanity's distant goal.

This dual usage reflects the collection's central ethical drama. Immortality belongs to Nature by right — she is the undying process. Humanity must earn its share of the immortal through the quality of its living, not through escape from death. The "immortal smile" is Nature's; the mortal's task is to become worthy of seeing it.

## In the Poems

### Nature's Immortal Smile

In an early passage (chapter 3), Meredith writes of Nature: "Less upon her guile / Doth she rely for her immortal smile" (003-263). The word "guile" is telling — Nature may use deception, but her immortality does not depend on it. Her permanence is real, not a trick. The "immortal smile" suggests a serene confidence: Nature knows she will outlast any individual life, and this knowledge expresses itself not as cruelty but as a kind of cosmic amusement. Meredith's Nature is neither benevolent nor malevolent; she is simply deathless, and her immortality places her beyond human moral categories.

The smile also carries an aesthetic charge. It is not merely that Nature endures; she endures beautifully. The immortal is, for Meredith, inseparable from the beautiful — what lasts is what deserves to last, and the standard of desert is aesthetic and vital rather than moral.

### The Farthest Bourne

In a later poem (chapter 5), Meredith describes a figure who reaches "Up to the farthest bourne: immortal still, / Thenceforth innocuous; lovelier than when ruled" (005-117). The "farthest bourne" — the ultimate boundary or destination — is reached not through death but through a transformation that renders the figure "immortal still." The word "still" carries a double meaning: continuing to be immortal, and now at peace (immortal and still). Having passed beyond the realm of conflict ("ruled"), the immortal being becomes "innocuous" — harmless, no longer a threat — and "lovelier than when ruled." Immortality here is a release from struggle into beauty.

The passage resonates with the classical theme of apotheosis: the mortal transformed into something deathless, not through divine intervention but through having reached the limit of mortal striving. Meredith's immortal is thus closer to the Greek concept of *kleos* (undying glory) than to Christian resurrection.
