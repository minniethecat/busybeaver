## Overview

The angel in Meredith's poetry is never simply the conventional messenger of light. Across three appearances — in "Union in Disseverance" and twice in "Foresight and Patience" — the angel oscillates between beauty and terror, elevation and fall, the celestial and the bestial. Meredith uses the angel to probe the composite nature of humanity: "What cowering angel and what upright beast / Make man" (016-009–010).

The angelic in Meredith is always paired with its opposite. The beloved has "the front of angel" (009-003) but is a star, not a celestial being — her angelic quality is borrowed, phenomenal, a matter of appearance. The angel within man "cowers" (016-009) — hardly the posture of heavenly confidence. And the rebel angels are "Black" (016-026), rained from the sky in punitive descent. Meredith's angels are fallen, compromised, ambiguous — figures not of pure transcendence but of the human condition's internal contradiction.

## In the Poems

### Angel-Fronted Beauty: The Star as Angel

In "Union in Disseverance," Meredith describes the beloved in cosmological terms: "white star with the front of angel she" (009-003). The image fuses celestial and angelic registers — she is a star, but her "front" (her face, her forward-facing presence) is that of an angel. The syntax is careful: she is not an angel but has the front of one, borrowing angelic radiance without claiming angelic nature.

"White star" evokes purity, distance, and cold light — the star shines but does not warm. The "front of angel" adds the dimension of personality, of face, of presence turned toward the viewer. Meredith's beloved is doubly removed from the earthly: she is stellar in substance, angelic in aspect. The compound image suggests that the highest beauty accessible to human perception is a convergence of cosmic order (star) and divine messenger (angel) — neither fully reachable, both visible only at a distance.

### The Cowering Angel and the Upright Beast: The Anthropology of Contradiction

Meredith's most concentrated anthropology appears in "Foresight and Patience": "What cowering angel and what upright beast / Make man" (016-009–010). The chiasmus is exact — angel cowers, beast stands upright — and each term contradicts its traditional association. Angels should soar; this one cowers. Beasts should crawl; this one stands. Man is constituted by these two inversions, each component behaving against its nature.

The "cowering angel" suggests the divine element in humanity reduced to fear, hiding from its own potential. The "upright beast" suggests the animal element elevated, given posture and dignity it does not naturally possess. Together they "make man" — not as a harmonious synthesis but as a living contradiction. Meredith's anthropology refuses both the optimistic view (man as little lower than the angels) and the pessimistic view (man as merely beast). Instead, man is the site where angel and beast exchange postures, where neither is what it should be, and where the tension between them constitutes humanity itself.

### The Rebel Angels: Blackened by Fall

The third angelic image in "Foresight and Patience" draws on the tradition of the War in Heaven: "Black as the rebel angels rained from sky!" (016-026). The speaker, looking back at past suffering, describes himself as a "broken pinion" (016-025) — a wing snapped, flight impossible — and then intensifies the image: the rebel angels, cast out of heaven, rained down blackened by their fall.

"Rained from sky" compresses the narrative of celestial warfare into a single verb. The angels do not fall — they are rained, poured down like weather, an atmospheric event rather than a moral drama. And they are "Black" — not the luminous darkness of Milton's Satan but the charred blackness of something burned in passage through the upper air. Meredith's rebel angels are not majestic in their defiance; they are the residue of a catastrophe, the dark precipitate of a conflict whose glory has been consumed in the fall itself. The speaker identifies with them — "Look I once back, a broken pinion I" — claiming solidarity with the fallen, the broken-winged, the blackened-by-descent.
