## Overview

Strength in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is never merely physical power — it is the compound of bodily capacity, moral resolve, and the wisdom to know how strength should be spent. "If that thou hast the gift of strength, then know" (010-001) opens a poem with a conditional that defines the collection's entire treatment of the theme: strength is a gift, not an achievement, and it comes with an obligation to understand its proper use. Meredith's strength is tested in battle ("battle nature's mandate, here it reigns", 005-096), proved in endurance, and measured by its capacity to serve purity: "How strength may serve for purity is shown" (004-175).

The collection traces a progression from raw strength — the "gathered strength" (003-135) that accumulates in life's veins — through the strength that combines with craft ("strength and craft combining", 005-039), to the mature strength that knows its own limits and prays only "for strength to keep his ground" (004-126). This is the arc from youthful force through strategic power to seasoned fortitude.

## In the Poems

Strength appears in a developmental sequence across the collection:

### As Innate Vital Force

At its most elemental, strength is the pressure of life itself. "In veins of gathered strength Life's tide arrest" (003-135) images strength as a tide that gathers in the body's channels — it is not created ex nihilo but accumulated, built up through the experience of living. This gathered strength is what enables life to "arrest" its own tide, to pause and redirect its energies rather than simply flowing onward. The biological metaphor is precise: strength is to the spirit what blood is to the body — the hydraulic force that enables action. "To brute, did strength and craft combining strike" (013-010) acknowledges that strength in its raw form belongs to the animal realm; it is the addition of craft — intelligence, strategy — that elevates strength from brute force to human capability.

### As the Substance of Moral Trial

Strength in Meredith is inseparable from the trials that test it. "No prayer, save for strength to keep his ground" (004-126) is the prayer of a soul that does not ask for victory, only for the fortitude to hold its position. This is the strength of endurance rather than conquest — the strength that matters most in Meredith's moral universe. "Of strength well spent, which is the strength" (004-130) contains the collection's essential definition: strength is not what one possesses but what one spends, and spending it well is itself a form of strength. The tautology is deliberate — strength spent well becomes more strength, not less, because it builds the character that is the true source of power.

### As the Foundation of Civilization

Meredith elevates strength from personal virtue to civilizational necessity. "To strengthen our foundations is the task" (016-208) places the cultivation of strength at the center of human progress. Foundations require strengthening because they bear weight — the weight of institutions, of justice, of inherited wisdom. A civilization that neglects the strength of its foundations invites collapse. The qualification follows immediately: "till they know need of shaping strength" (016-231), suggesting that strength must not only be accumulated but shaped — given form, direction, and purpose. Unshaped strength is dangerous; shaped strength is the cornerstone of lasting achievement.

### As the Partner of Craft

The pairing of strength with craft recurs throughout the collection. "These two combining strength and craft had snared" (005-039) describes how strength alone is insufficient — it must be allied with cunning, with skill, with the intelligence that directs force toward its target. The "snare" requires both: craft to devise the trap, strength to spring it. "In their strength they stood to challenge" (005-087) shifts the emphasis back to the primary term: after craft has done its work, strength must be present to meet the challenge directly. The two faculties are complementary, neither sufficient without the other.
