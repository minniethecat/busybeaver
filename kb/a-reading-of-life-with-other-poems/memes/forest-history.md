## FOREST HISTORY

[014-001] I

[014-002] BENEATH the vans of doom did men pass in.

[014-003] Heroic who came out; for round them hung

[014-004] A wavering phantom's red volcano tongue,

[014-005] With league-long lizard tail and fishy fin:

[014-006] II

[014-007] Old Earth's original Dragon; there retired

[014-008] To his last fastness; overthrown by few.

[014-009] Him a laborious thrust of roadway slew.

[014-010] Then man to play devorant straight was fired.

[014-011] III

[014-012] More intimate became the forest fear

[014-013] While pillared darkness hatched malicious life

[014-014] At either elbow, wolf or gnome or knife

[014-015] And wary slid the glance from ear to ear.

[014-016] IV

[014-017] In chillness, like a clouded lantern-ray,

[014-018] The forest's heart of fog on mossed morass,

[014-019] On purple pool and silky cotton-grass,

[014-020] Revealed where lured the swallower byway.

[014-021] V

[014-022] Dead outlook, flattened back with hard rebound

[014-023] Off walls of distance, left each mounted height.

[014-024] It seemed a giant hag-fiend, churning spite

[014-025] Of humble human being, held the ground.

[014-026] VI

[014-027] Through friendless wastes, through treacherous woodland, slow

[014-028] The feet sustained by track of feet pursued

[014-029] Pained steps, and found the common brotherhood

[014-030] By sign of Heaven indifferent, Nature foe.

[014-031] VII

[014-032] Anon a mason's work amazed the sight,

[014-033] And long-frocked men, called Brothers, there abode.

[014-034] They pointed up, bowed head, and dug and sowed;

[014-035] Whereof was shelter, loaf, and warm firelight.

[014-036] VIII

[014-037] What words they taught were nails to scratch the head.

[014-038] Benignant works explained the chanting brood.

[014-039] Their monastery lit black solitude,

[014-040] As one might think a star that heavenward led.

[014-041] IX

[014-042] Uprose a fairer nest for weary feet,

[014-043] Like some gold flower nightly inward curled,

[014-044] Where gentle maidens fled a roaring world,

[014-045] Or played with it, and had their white retreat.

[014-046] X

[014-047] Into big books of metal clasps they pored.

[014-048] They governed, even as men; they welcomed lays.

[014-049] The treasures women are whose aim is praise,

[014-050] Was shown in them: the Garden half restored.

[014-051] XI

[014-052] A deluge billow scoured the land off seas,

[014-053] With widened jaws, and slaughter was its foam.

[014-054] For food, for clothing, ambush, refuge, home,

[014-055] The lesser savage offered bogs and trees.

[014-056] XII

[014-057] Whence reverence round grey-haired story grew:

[014-058] And inmost spots of ancient horror shone

[014-059] As temples under beams of trials bygone;

[014-060] For in them sang brave times with God in view.

[014-061] XIII

[014-062] Till now trim homesteads bordered spaces green,

[014-063] Like night's first little stars through clearing showers.

[014-064] Was rumoured how a castle's falcon towers

[014-065] The wilderness commanded with fierce mien.

[014-066] XIV

[014-067] Therein a serious Baron stuck his lance;

[014-068] For minstrel songs a beauteous Dame would pout.

[014-069] Gay knights and sombre, felon or devout,

[014-070] Pricked onward, bound for their unsung romance.

[014-071] XV

[014-072] It might be that two errant lords across

[014-073] The block of each came edged, and at sharp cry

[014-074] They charged forthwith, the better man to try.

[014-075] One rode his way, one couched on quiet moss.

[014-076] XVI

[014-077] Perchance a lady sweet, whose lord lay slain,

[014-078] The robbers into gruesome durance drew.

[014-079] Swift should her hero come, like lightning's blue!

[014-080] She prayed for him, as crackling drought for rain.

[014-081] XVII

[014-082] As we, that ere the worst her hero haps,

[014-083] Of Angels guided, nigh that loathly den:

[014-084] A toady cave beside an ague fen,

[014-085] Where long forlorn the lone dog whines and yaps.

[014-086] XVIII

[014-087] By daylight now the forest fear could read

[014-088] Itself, and at new wonders chuckling went.

[014-089] Straight for the roebuck's neck the bowman spent

[014-090] A dart that laughed at distance and at speed.

[014-091] XIX

[014-092] Right loud the bugle's hallali elate

[014-093] Rang forth of merry dingles round the tors;

[014-094] And deftest hand was he from foreign wars,

[014-095] But soon he hailed the home-bred yeoman mate.

[014-096] XX

[014-097] Before the blackbird pecked the turf they woke;

[014-098] At dawn the deer's wet nostrils blew their last.

[014-099] To forest, haunt of runs and prime repast,

[014-100] With paying blows, the yokel strained his yoke.

[014-101] XXI

[014-102] The city urchin mooned on forest air,

[014-103] On grassy sweeps and flying arrows, thick

[014-104] As swallows o'er smooth streams, and sighed him sick

[014-105] For thinking that his dearer home was there.

[014-106] XXII

[014-107] Familiar, still unseized, the forest sprang

[014-108] An old-world echo, like no mortal thing.

[014-109] The hunter's horn might wind a jocund ring,

[014-110] But held in ear it had a chilly clang.

[014-111] XXIII

[014-112] Some shadow lurked aloof of ancient time;

[014-113] Some warning haunted any sound prolonged,

[014-114] As though the leagues of woodland held them wronged

[014-115] To hear an axe and see a township climb.

[014-116] XXIV

[014-117] The forest's erewhile emperor at eve

[014-118] Had voice when lowered heavens drummed for gales.

[014-119] At midnight a small people danced the dales,

[014-120] So thin that they might dwindle through a sieve

[014-121] XXV

[014-122] Ringed mushrooms told of them, and in their throats,

[014-123] Old wives that gathered herbs and knew too much.

[014-124] The pensioned forester beside his crutch,

[014-125] Struck showers from embers at those bodeful notes.

[014-126] XXVI

[014-127] Came then the one, all ear, all eye, all heart;

[014-128] Devourer, and insensibly devoured;

[014-129] In whom the city over forest flowered,

[014-130] The forest wreathed the city's drama-mart.

[014-131] XXVII

[014-132] There found he in new form that Dragon old,

[014-133] From tangled solitudes expelled; and taught

[014-134] How blindly each its antidote besought;

[014-135] For either's breath the needs of either told.

[014-136] XXVIII

[014-137] Now deep in woods, with song no sermon's drone,

[014-138] He showed what charm the human concourse works:

[014-139] Amid the press of men, what virtue lurks

[014-140] Where bubble sacred wells of wildness lone.

[014-141] XXIX

[014-142] Our conquest these: if haply we retain

[014-143] The reverence that ne'er will overrun

[014-144] Due boundaries of realms from Nature won,

[014-145] Nor let the poet's awe in rapture wane.
