## Overview

Sacredness in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is relocated from church to landscape. The sacred is what dwells on the mountain, what bubbles up from hidden wells, what is forbidden to casual approach. Meredith inherits the Romantic re-sacralization of nature — Wordsworth's "something far more deeply interfused" — but gives it a classical severity. His sacred places are not gentle; they command, refuse, and demand ascent.

The word "sacred" appears sparingly in the collection, always attached to natural features (mounts, wells) rather than persons or doctrines. This pattern is significant: Meredith's sacred is immanent in the world but accessible only through effort and attention.

## In the Poems

### The Forbidden Summit

In *The Test of Manhood* (chapter 4), Meredith issues the admonition: "Ascend no sacred Mounts" (004-057). The capitalization of "Mounts" elevates the image beyond geography into symbol. The command is paradoxical — it recognizes the sacredness of the heights while warning against the attempt to climb them. The prohibition is not dismissive but reverent: the sacred mount is genuinely sacred, which is precisely why casual ascent is forbidden. One does not conquer the sacred; one acknowledges it from below. The line participates in a long tradition of tabooed summits, from Sinai to Parnassus, but Meredith's tone is moral rather than mythological.

### The Waters of Wildness

Chapter 14 returns to the sacred in a different register: "Where bubble sacred wells of wildness lone" (014-140). Here the sacred is not high but deep — a well rather than a mount. The verb "bubble" is sensuous and immediate; sacredness wells up from underground, rising into visibility through its own pressure. The phrase "wildness lone" reinforces the isolation of the sacred. Sacred wells are not in cultivated gardens or temple precincts but in the untended wild, accessible only to those who venture there.

### The Springs at the Summit

Earlier in chapter 3, Meredith had already linked the sacred to upward movement and water: "at spout their springs to the sacred mounts" (003-313). Springs spout toward sacred mounts — the water rises toward the height, and the height is sacred because the living water seeks it. The image synthesizes the two motifs: the mountain and the well, height and depth, are not opposed but continuous in a single sacred landscape where water travels upward and the summit receives it.
