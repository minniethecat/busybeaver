## Overview

Meredith deploys the image of prison on two distinct registers: the psychological interior, where "Imprisoned Powers warring unappeased" (004-076) describe the soul's trapped energies, and the mythological exterior, where the giant Otos "aped the prisoner's wrists and knees" (005-017) in a performance of captivity.

This doubling — prison as both metaphor and theatre — reveals Meredith's understanding of confinement as fundamentally ambivalent.

The internal prison is genuinely agonizing, a state of spiritual siege; the external prison, as performed by Otos, is almost comic, a gesture toward bondage that contains the seed of its own undoing.

## In the Poems

### The Inner Prison (The Test of Manhood)

In "The Test of Manhood," Meredith maps the landscape of psychological imprisonment with clinical precision.

The passage describes a man in the grip of internal conflict: "Out of that agony, misread for those / Imprisoned Powers warring unappeased, / The ghost of his black adversary rose, / To smother light, shut heaven, show earth diseased" (004-076).

The "Imprisoned Powers" are aspects of the self — desires, fears, repressed impulses — that, because they cannot find outward expression, turn inward as destructive forces.

Meredith's insight is that imprisonment here is self-imposed: the man is both jailer and jailed.

His agony comes from the war between parts of himself that cannot be reconciled.

The "black adversary" is a projection of this internal conflict, a ghost that rises not from without but from within the prison of the divided self.

### The Performed Prison (The Cageing of Ares)

The tone shifts dramatically in "The Cageing of Ares," where the giant Otos transforms imprisonment into farce.

Having learned of Ares's capture, Otos "aped the prisoner's wrists and knees, / With doleful sniffs between recurrent howls" (005-017).

The verb "aped" is key — Otos is not a prisoner but mimics one, turning captivity into a performance for the amusement of the Olympians.

Meredith presents this theatrical imprisonment as a form of mockery that undermines the seriousness of Ares's actual cageing.

Where the inner prison in "The Test of Manhood" is genuinely agonizing, Otos's mimed prison is a comic riff that exposes the absurdity of the gods' power struggles.

### Prison and Release

Across both poems, Meredith implies that the prison's power is contingent on the prisoner's belief in it.

The man in "The Test of Manhood" is trapped because he cannot recognize his "black adversary" as a projection; his prison is real because he experiences it as real.

Otos, by contrast, treats imprisonment as a joke, demonstrating that the prison's walls are only as solid as the prisoner's conviction.

This convergence of psychological and mythological registers is characteristic of Meredith's method: the prison connects the inner life of the psyche to the outer drama of myth.
