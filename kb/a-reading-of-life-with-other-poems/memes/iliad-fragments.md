## FRAGMENTS OF THE ILIAD IN ENGLISH HEXAMETER VERSE

[017-001] THE INVECTIVE OF ACHILLES

[017-002] ILIAD, B. I. V. 149

[017-003] "HEIGH me! brazen of front, thou glutton for plunder, how can one,

[017-004] Servant here to thy mandates, heed thee among our Achaians,

[017-005] Either the mission hie on or stoutly do fight with the foemen?

[017-006] I, not hither I fared on account of the spear-armed Trojans,

[017-007] Pledged to the combat; they unto me have in nowise a harm done;

[017-008] Never have they, of a truth, come lifting my horses or oxen;

[017-009] Never in deep-soiled Phthia, the nurser of heroes, my harvests

[017-010] Ravaged, they; for between us is numbered full many a darksome

[017-011] Mountain, ay, therewith too the stretch of the windy sea-waters.

[017-012] O hugely shameless! thee did we follow to hearten thee, justice

[017-013] Pluck from the Dardans for him, Menelaos, thee too, thou dog-eyed!

[017-014] Whereof little thy thought is, nought whatever thou reckest.

[017-015] Worse, it is thou whose threat 'tis to ravish my prize from me,

[017-016] portion

[017-017] Won with much labour, the which my gift from the sons of Achaia.

[017-018] Never, in sooth, have I known my prize equal thine when Achaians

[017-019] Gave some flourishing populous Trojan town up to pillage.

[017-020] Nay, sure, mine were the hands did most in the storm of the combat,

[017-021] Yet when came peradventure share of the booty amongst us,

[017-022] Bigger to thee went the prize, while I some small blessed thing bore

[017-023] Off to the ships, my share of reward for my toil in the bloodshed!

[017-024] So now go I to Phthia, for better by much it beseems me

[017-025] Homeward go with my beaked ships now, and I hold not in prospect,

[017-026] I being outraged, thou mayst gather here plunder and wealth-store."

[017-027] V. 225

[017-028] "Bibber besotted, with scowl of a cur, having heart of a deer, thou!

[017-029] Never to join to thy warriors armed for the press of the conflict,

[017-030] Never for ambush forth with the princeliest sons of Achaia

[017-031] Dared thy soul, for to thee that thing would have looked as a

[017-032] death-stroke.

[017-033] Sooth, more easy it seems, down the lengthened array of Achaians,

[017-034] Snatch at the prize of the one whose voice has been lifted against

[017-035] thee.

[017-036] Ravening king of the folk, for that thou hast thy rule over abjects;

[017-037] Else, son of Atreus, now were this outrage on me thy last one.

[017-038] Nay, but I tell thee, and I do swear a big oath on it likewise:

[017-039] Yea, by the sceptre here, and it surely bears branches and leaf-buds

[017-040] Never again, since first it was lopped from its trunk on the

[017-041] mountains,

[017-042] No more sprouting; for round it all clean has the sharp metal clipped

[017-043] off

[017-044] Leaves and the bark; ay, verify now do the sons of Achaia,

[017-045] Guardian hands of the counsels of Zeus, pronouncing the judgement,

[017-046] Hold it aloft; so now unto thee shall the oath have its portent;

[017-047] Loud will the cry for Achilles burst from the sons of Achaia

[017-048] Throughout the army, and thou chafe powerless, though in an anguish,

[017-049] How to give succour when vast crops down under man-slaying Hector

[017-050] Tumble expiring; and thou deep in thee shalt tear at thy

[017-051] heart-strings,

[017-052] Rage-wrung, thou, that in nought thou didst honour the flower of

[017-053] Achaians."

[017-054] MARSHALLING OF THE ACHAIANS

[017-055] ILIAD, B. II V. 455

[017-056] LIKE as a terrible fire feeds fast on a forest enormous,

[017-057] Up on a mountain height, and the blaze of it radiates round far,

[017-058] So on the bright blest arms of the host in their march did the

[017-059] splendour

[017-060] Gleam wide round through the circle of air right up to the sky-vault.

[017-061] They, now, as when swarm thick in the air multitudinous winged flocks,

[017-062] Be it of geese or of cranes or the long-necked troops of the

[017-063] wild-swans,

[017-064] Off that Asian mead, by the flow of the waters of Kaistros;

[017-065] Hither and yon fly they, and rejoicing in pride of their pinions,

[017-066] Clamour, shaped to their ranks, and the mead all about them

[017-067] resoundeth;

[017-068] So those numerous tribes from their ships and their shelterings poured

[017-069] forth

[017-070] On that plain of Scamander, and horrible rumbled beneath them

[017-071] Earth to the quick-paced feet of the men and the tramp of the

[017-072] horse-hooves.

[017-073] Stopped they then on the fair-flower'd field of Scamander, their

[017-074] thousands

[017-075] Many as leaves and the blossoms born of the flowerful season.

[017-076] Even as countless hot-pressed flies in their multitudes traverse,

[017-077] Clouds of them, under some herdsman's wonning, where then are the

[017-078] milk-pails

[017-079] Also, full of their milk, in the bountiful season of spring-time;

[017-080] Even so thickly the long-haired sons of Achaia the plain held,

[017-081] Prompt for the dash at the Trojan host, with the passion to crush

[017-082] them.

[017-083] Those, likewise, as the goatherds, eyeing their vast flocks of goats,

[017-084] know

[017-085] Easily one from the other when all get mixed o'er the pasture,

[017-086] So did the chieftains rank them here there in their places for

[017-087] onslaught,

[017-088] Hard on the push of the fray; and among them King Agamemnon,

[017-089] He, for his eyes and his head, as when Zeus glows glad in his thunder,

[017-090] He with the girdle of Ares, he with the breast of Poseidon.

[017-091] AGAMEMNON IN THE FIGHT

[017-092] ILIAD, B. XI. V. 148

[017-093] THESE, then, he left, and away where ranks were now clashing the

[017-094] thickest,

[017-095] Onward rushed, and with him rushed all of the bright-greaved Achaians.

[017-096] Foot then footmen slew, that were flying from direful compulsion,

[017-097] Horse at the horsemen (up from off under them mounted the dust-cloud,

[017-098] Up off the plain, raised up cloud-thick by the thundering

[017-099] horse-hooves)

[017-100] Hewed with the sword's sharp edge; and so meanwhile Lord Agamemnon

[017-101] Followed, chasing and slaughtering aye, on-urgeing the Argives.

[017-102] Now, as when fire voracious catches the unclipped woodland,

[017-103] This way bears it and that the great whirl of the wind, and the

[017-104] scrubwood

[017-105] Stretches uptorn, flung forward alength by the fire's fury rageing,

[017-106] So beneath Atreides Agamemnon heads of the scattered

[017-107] Trojans fell; and in numbers amany the horses, neck-stiffened,

[017-108] Rattled their vacant cars down the roadway gaps of the war-field,

[017-109] Missing the blameless charioteers, but, for these, they were

[017-110] outstretched

[017-111] Flat upon earth, far dearer to vultures than to their home-mates.

[017-112] PARIS AND DIOMEDES

[017-113] ILIAD; B. XI V. 378

[017-114] SO he, with a clear shout of laughter,

[017-115] Forth of his ambush leapt, and he vaunted him, uttering thiswise:

[017-116] "Hit thou art! not in vain flew the shaft; how by rights it had

[017-117] pierced thee

[017-118] Into the undermost gut, therewith to have rived thee of life-breath!

[017-119] Following that had the Trojans plucked a new breath from their direst,

[017-120] They all frighted of thee, as the goats bleat in flight from a lion."

[017-121] Then unto him untroubled made answer stout Diomedes:

[017-122] "Bow-puller, jiber, thy bow for thy glorying, spyer at virgins!

[017-123] If that thou dared'st face me here out in the open with weapons,

[017-124] Nothing then would avail thee thy bow and thy thick shot of arrows.

[017-125] Now thou plumest thee vainly because of a graze of my footsole;

[017-126] Reck I as were that stroke from a woman or some pettish infant.

[017-127] Aye flies blunted the dart of the man that's emasculate, noughtworth!

[017-128] Otherwise hits, forth flying from me, and but strikes it the

[017-129] slightest,

[017-130] My keen shaft, and it numbers a man of the dead fallen straightway.

[017-131] Torn, troth, then are the cheeks of the wife of that man fallen

[017-132] slaughtered,

[017-133] Orphans his babes, full surely he reddens the earth with his

[017-134] blood-drops,

[017-135] Rotting, round him the birds, more numerous they than the women."

[017-136] HYPNOS ON IDA

[017-137] ILIAD, B. XIV. V. 283

[017-138] THEY then to fountain-abundant Ida, mother of wild beasts,

[017-139] Came, and they first left ocean to fare over mainland at Lektos,

[017-140] Where underneath of their feet waved loftiest growths of the woodland.

[017-141] There hung Hypnos fast, ere the vision of Zeus was observant,

[017-142] Mounted upon a tall pine-tree, tallest of pines that on Ida

[017-143] Lustily spring off soil for the shoot up aloft into aether.

[017-144] There did he sit well-cloaked by the wide-branched pine for

[017-145] concealment,

[017-146] That loud bird, in his form like, that perched high up in the

[017-147] mountains,

[017-148] Chalkis is named by the Gods, but of mortals known as Kymindis.

[017-149] CLASH IN ARMS OF THE ACHAIANS AND TROJANS

[017-150] ILIAD, B. XIV. V. 394

[017-151] NOT the sea-wave so bellows abroad when it bursts upon shingle,

[017-152] Whipped from the sea's deeps up by the terrible blast of the

[017-153] Northwind;

[017-154] Nay, nor is ever the roar of the fierce fire's rush so arousing,

[017-155] Down along mountain-glades, when it surges to kindle a woodland;

[017-156] Nay, nor so tonant thunders the stress of the gale in the oak-trees'

[017-157] Foliage-tresses high, when it rages to raveing its utmost;

[017-158] As rose then stupendous the Trojan's cry and Achaians',

[017-159] Dread upshouting as one when together they clashed in the conflict.

[017-160] THE HORSES OF ACHILLES

[017-161] ILIAD, B. XVII. V. 426

[017-162] SO now the horses of Aiakides, off wide of the war-ground,

[017-163] Wept, since first they were ware of their charioteer overthrown there,

[017-164] Cast down low in the whirl of the dust under man-slaying Hector.

[017-165] Sooth, meanwhile, then did Automedon, brave son of Diores,

[017-166] Oft, on the one hand, urge them with flicks of the swift whip, and

[017-167] oft, too,

[017-168] Coax entreatingly, hurriedly; whiles did he angrily threaten.

[017-169] Vainly, for these would not to the ships, to the Hellespont spacious,

[017-170] Backward turn, nor be whipped to the battle among the Achaians.

[017-171] Nay, as a pillar remains immovable, fixed on the tombstone,

[017-172] Haply, of some dead man or it may be a woman there-under;

[017-173] Even like hard stood they there attached to the glorious war-car,

[017-174] Earthward bowed with their heads; and of them so lamenting incessant

[017-175] Ran the hot teardrops downward on to the earth from their eyelids,

[017-176] Mourning their charioteer; all their lustrous manes dusty-clotted,

[017-177] Right side and left of the yoke-ring tossed, to the breadth of the

[017-178] yoke-bow.

[017-179] Now when the issue of Kronos beheld that sorrow, his head shook

[017-180] Pitying them for their grief, these words then he spake in his bosom;

[017-181] "Why, ye hapless, gave we to Peleus you, to a mortal

[017-182] Master; ye that are ageless both, ye both of you deathless!

[017-183] Was it that ye among men most wretched should come to have

[017-184] heart-grief?

[017-185] 'Tis most true, than the race of these men is there wretcheder nowhere

[017-186] Aught over earth's range found that is gifted with breath and has

[017-187] movement."
