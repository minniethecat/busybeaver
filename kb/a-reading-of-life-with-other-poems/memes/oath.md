## Overview

The oath in Meredith's poetry is the speech-act that binds — the word given that creates an obligation not previously existing and that carries consequences if broken. Unlike the promise, which belongs to the domestic sphere of personal reliability, the oath in Meredith's usage is an archaic and epic form, sworn with cosmic witnesses and carrying the weight of "portent" (017-046).

But Meredith also employs the related language of vowing and avowal in other registers — the "vigilant solicitude" of one "Avowed" (007-013) to duty, the "vowed to Gods obscene" (016-195) that marks a corrupted form of sacred pledge.

Across these variations, the oath-family of speech-acts consistently marks the boundary where word becomes bond, where utterance creates an obligation that the speaker cannot revoke.

## In the Poems

### The Big Oath: Swearing in the Epic Mode

Meredith's principal treatment of the oath occurs in an extended epic passage where the speaker declares "I do swear a big oath on it likewise" (017-038). The adjective "big" is deliberately unadorned — not "solemn" or "sacred" or "terrible" but simply "big," the plain word for scale applied to a speech-act.

This choice captures the oath's dual nature: it is at once momentous (big in consequence) and vernacular (big in register), available to human speakers rather than reserved for gods and kings. The speaker swears "on it" — on the matter at hand, the immediate point of contention — making the oath not an abstract invocation but a concrete stake placed on a specific claim.

### Portent and Consequence

The oath immediately generates its consequence: "so now unto thee shall the oath have its portent" (017-046). The word "portent" is the oath's shadow — the sign or omen that confirms the oath's seriousness and foreshadows what will happen if it is broken.

In Meredith's construction, the oath and its portent are inseparable: to swear is to summon the sign that will mark fulfilment or violation.

This linkage belongs to an archaic world in which words have causal force, where the utterance itself alters reality rather than merely describing it. Meredith's retention of this archaic logic within a modern poem is characteristic of his method.

### The Oath as Bond Between Unequals

The oath-passage is embedded in a scene of confrontation between figures of unequal power, and the oath serves to level the asymmetry — the weaker party, by swearing, creates a bond that the stronger party must acknowledge.

The swearing speaker is not the dominant figure in the scene, yet the oath grants him a kind of standing: his word, once sworn, demands a response. The oath is thus a democratic speech-act in an aristocratic world, a way for the less powerful to create obligation in the more powerful through the mere act of speaking.

### Avowal and Sacred Pledge: The Wider Vocabulary of Binding

Beyond the explicit oath, Meredith deploys the language of vowing across distinct ethical registers. One figure appears "Avowed in vigilant solicitude" (007-013) — the avowal here is not a single dramatic speech-act but a continuous posture of watchful care, a pledged attentiveness maintained over time rather than declared in a moment.

The past participle "Avowed" makes the pledge a settled condition rather than an ongoing negotiation.

At the opposite extreme stands the figure "vowed to Gods obscene" (016-195), where the vow is corrupted by its object — the sacred structure of binding remains, but the gods are debased. The binding power of the oath does not depend on the worthiness of what one binds oneself to.
