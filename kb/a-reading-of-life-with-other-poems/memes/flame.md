## Overview

Meredith uses the word "flame" six times across four poems, and in each occurrence the image occupies a distinct symbolic register. The flame appears as a guiding light ("silver flame," 002-040), as the residue of extinguished passion ("torch-smoke after flame," 003-079), as the mark of divine impress upon the natural world ("green leaves caught with flames," 003-189), as the path of spiritual ascent ("the way of flame," 004-023), and as the medium of both creation and revelation in the corpus's philosophical climax (016-203, 016-206). Together these occurrences trace a trajectory from the aesthetic to the cosmological — from flame as a pretty image to flame as the very substance of divine manifestation.

In Meredith's poetic vocabulary, flame is distinguished from mere fire by its elevation. Fire burns; flame rises. Fire consumes; flame illuminates. This upward tendency aligns flame with the spiritual and the aspirational, making it a natural figure for the human movement toward God that Meredith so persistently traces.

## In the Poems

### The Silver Flame: Following the Guiding Light

The first occurrence sets the tone for all that follow: "Follow we their silver flame" (002-040). The flame here is "silver" — precious, lunar, reflective rather than consuming. It belongs to "they," an antecedent that places the flame in the possession of guiding figures (the persuaders, the beautiful, the divinely touched). To "follow" this flame is to accept guidance, to orient oneself by a light that is not one's own. The adjective "silver" carries the suggestion of purity and value: this is not the red flame of destruction but the cool light of beauty and truth.

### Smoke After Flame: The Trace of Passion Spent

A more melancholic image appears in "With the Persuader" (003-079): "float like torch-smoke after flame." Here the flame is past; what remains is smoke — the residue, the trace, the memory of what burned. The verb "float" captures the aimless, unmoored quality of the aftermath. After passion (the flame), consciousness drifts (the smoke), still carrying the shape and scent of what has gone. The image is a miniature of Meredith's larger concern with what persists after intensity fades — the work of memory, of art, of reflection, all of which are forms of torch-smoke.

### Green Leaves Caught with Flames: The Divine Impress

One of Meredith's most striking images of divine immanence appears in a compressed phrase: "Like green leaves caught with flames by his impress" (003-189). The leaves are green — alive, natural, organic — but they are "caught with flames," touched and transformed by fire without being consumed. The agent is "his impress," the mark or stamp of a divine presence. The image echoes the burning bush of Exodus — aflame but not consumed — but Meredith naturalizes it: the flame of the divine does not destroy nature but illuminates it, catches its surfaces, makes it luminous. The living green and the living flame coexist; nature is not replaced by spirit but kindled by it.

### The Way of Flame: Spiritual Direction

In "The Test of Manhood" (004-023), Meredith compresses the flame into a directional image: "points for us the way of flame." The flame is not a destination but a path — a "way" that must be followed. The verb "points" suggests a guide, a signpost, an indication rather than a command. The "way of flame" is both beautiful and dangerous: it leads upward, toward light, but it is also the way of burning, of transformation through intensity. To walk the way of flame is to accept that the spiritual life is not comfortable — it is a path of heat and light and risk.

### Flame, Slime, and the Lustrous Birth

The final two occurrences of "flame" appear in the philosophical poem numbered 016, and they frame Meredith's cosmology of creation. The first: "were his offspring, born of flame on slime" (016-203) — fire upon mud, spirit upon matter, the luminous born from the base. The pairing of "flame" and "slime" is deliberate and shocking: the highest and the lowest, joined in a single act of generation. This is Meredith's evolutionary theology at its most compressed — the divine does not create from above but from within, flame kindling upon the slime of the material world.

The complementary passage completes the image: "among them shoots a lustrous flame" (016-206). The flame here is "lustrous" — shining, glorious, beautiful — and it "shoots" upward with the suddenness and vitality of a living thing. Among the offspring born of flame on slime, a new flame arises — creation generates further creation, flame begets flame. The image closes the circle: what began as divine impress upon the world becomes, within the world, a new source of light.
