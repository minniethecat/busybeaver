## Biography

Zeus is the supreme deity of the Greek pantheon, the son of Kronos and Rhea, and the ruler of Mount Olympus. After overthrowing his father and the Titans, Zeus established the Olympian order and apportioned domains among his siblings — Poseidon the sea, Hades the underworld, and himself the sky. His primary attributes are the thunderbolt, the eagle, and the sceptre of authority.

In the Homeric epics, Zeus occupies a distinctive narrative position: he is both the most powerful of gods and yet bound by fate, the guardian of cosmic order who must sometimes watch helplessly as events unfold against his preference. When his son Sarpedon is fated to die at Troy, Zeus can only weep tears of blood — an image of sovereignty constrained that deeply influenced the Western literary imagination.

Meredith engages with Zeus primarily through the Iliad fragments, where the god appears as the source of legitimate authority ("the counsels of Zeus") and as the cosmic observer whose "vision" oversees human and divine affairs alike.

## Zeus in the Iliad Fragments

Meredith's *Fragments of the Iliad in English Hexameter Verse* (chapter 17 of this collection) contains three significant appearances of Zeus, each revealing a different aspect of the god's function in epic poetry.

### Guardian of Counsels

In Achilles's great oath against Agamemnon, the sceptre held by the assembled Achaians is described as grasped by:

> Guardian hands of the counsels of Zeus, pronouncing the judgement. (017-045)

The phrase "counsels of Zeus" (Διὸς βουλή in Homer's Greek) is programmatic — the *Iliad* opens by announcing that the poem's events fulfill "the will of Zeus." Meredith's translation captures the dual sense of divine oversight and juridical authority: the sceptre is not merely a symbol of kingship but a conduit of Zeus's ordering intelligence.

### The Gleam of Thunder

When Agamemnon is described marshalling the Achaians, the simile reaches for the divine:

> He, for his eyes and his head, as when Zeus glows glad in his thunder. (017-089)

This comparison does more than flatter the mortal king. By likening Agamemnon's martial radiance to Zeus's thunder-glow, Meredith activates the epic convention of the hero as a momentary incarnation of divine power — and simultaneously foreshadows the fragility of that identification, for Agamemnon's authority will soon be challenged by Achilles.

### Hypnos on Ida

The most extended Zeus episode in the fragments is the vision of Hypnos (Sleep) perched in a pine tree on Mount Ida:

> There hung Hypnos fast, ere the vision of Zeus was observant,
> Mounted upon a tall pine-tree, tallest of pines that on Ida
> Lustily spring off soil for the shoot up aloft into aether. (017-141)

Hypnos hides from "the vision of Zeus" — a phrase that suggests Zeus's sight as an active, searching force that must be evaded. The episode draws on *Iliad* Book XIV, where Hera enlists Sleep to help her seduce Zeus and distract him from the battlefield. Meredith's choice to include this passage among his fragments highlights the theme of divine observation and its limits — a concern that runs through the entire collection.

## Poetic Function

Zeus serves three distinct functions in Meredith's collection:

| Function | Description | Key passage |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Cosmic order | Source of legitimate authority and the counsels that govern fate | "Guardian hands of the counsels of Zeus" (017-045) |
| Poetic simile | Divine radiance as a measure of mortal heroism | "as when Zeus glows glad in his thunder" (017-089) |
| Watcher and watched | The all-seeing god whose vision can be evaded | "ere the vision of Zeus was observant" (017-141) |

This tripartite presence — Zeus as ruler, as poetic comparison, and as cosmic observer — reflects the layered way Homeric epic builds divine meaning. Meredith, translating into a post-Christian Victorian idiom, retains the mythic apparatus without demythologizing it, trusting the poetry to carry the theological weight.
