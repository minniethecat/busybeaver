## Overview

Fire in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the element of transformation. It burns through the collection in two registers: as the inner fire of human passion and aspiration, and as the cosmic fire of destruction and renewal. Meredith inherits fire's rich symbolic tradition — from Heraclitus to the Prometheus myth — and adapts it to his post-Darwinian vision of humanity's struggle toward higher consciousness.

Fire's duality is essential to Meredith's moral scheme. The same flame that illuminates can blind: "A desolating fire to blind the sight" (005-068). The same heat that warms can consume voraciously (017-056). Mastery consists not in extinguishing fire but in directing it — channeling its energy toward worthy ends rather than letting it rage uncontrolled.

## In the Poems

Fire manifests across the collection in several distinct modes:

### As Inner Passion

The most pervasive form of fire is the internal flame of human emotion and ambition. Meredith's speakers are "armed of inner fires" (006-067), carrying within themselves the heat that drives action. These fires arise from "inner fire" that projects onto "the vacant air" (003-099), shaping the world according to human desire. The vocabulary of inward burning recurs: characters are "afire to kiss her feet" (003-021), seized by a heat that demands expression.

### As the Path of Purification

Fire is the medium through which love must pass to achieve its higher form. The collection's most compressed statement of this principle appears as a section heading: "INTO that love must we through fire attain" (007-001). Love is not a gentle arrival but a passage through burning. This echoes both Dante's refining fire and the alchemical tradition of purification through heat. The sharing of fire between beings becomes a profound intimacy: "Can share his fires, not always goads and rends" (003-207) — fire need not only torment; it can also connect those who learn to tend it together.

### As Destructive Consumption

Fire's destructive face is vividly rendered through extended Homeric similes. "LIKE as a terrible fire feeds fast on a forest enormous" (017-056) opens a passage that translates the Iliad's imagery of consuming flame into Meredith's own idiom. The "fire voracious catches the unclipped woodland" (017-102), driven forward by "the fire's fury rageing" (017-105). This is fire as pure annihilating force — the aspect that must be respected and feared. The "roar of the fierce fire's rush" (017-154) is the sound of destruction beyond human control, an ancient power that civilization only precariously contains.

### As Heavenly Draught

Fire also flows downward from the divine realm. In one of the collection's most compressed images, the "fire of heaven" is drunk by the grape to "swell" it (005-139) — celestial heat entering earthly fruit. This downward movement of fire complements the upward striving of inner flame. "A Sun goes down in wasted fire, a Sun" (004-237) images the daily solar cycle as fire's expenditure, suggesting that even cosmic fires exhaust themselves and must be renewed.
