## Overview

The river in Meredith's poetry is an image of irresistible motion — a force that cuts through landscape and psyche alike. Opening "The Test of Manhood" with the simile "Like a flood river whirled at rocky banks" (004-001), Meredith establishes the river as the figure for life's turbulent forward momentum. The river is simultaneously natural description and existential metaphor: it is "the river's crooks and snaky coils" (005-026) in a pastoral scene, and it is the inward current of feeling that, when blocked, leaves one "More pitiable, like rivers lacking rains" (003-118). Meredith's rivers flow through multiple poetic modes — the pastoral, the philosophical, the elegiac — without ever losing their fundamental character as figures of passage, change, and the difficult navigation of existence.

## In the Poems

### The Flood River of Life

The collection's most powerful river image opens the fourth section of "The Test of Manhood": "Like a flood river whirled at rocky banks" (004-001). This is not a gentle stream but a flood — overwhelming, dangerous, barely containable. The "rocky banks" suggest resistance and constraint, the structures that channel but cannot stop the current. As a simile for the human condition, the flood river captures Meredith's sense of life as something that sweeps the individual along, where the challenge is not to stop the flow but to navigate it without being destroyed.

### The Course and Its Crooks

Meredith attends to the specific geography of rivers — their "crooks and snaky coils" (005-026). The river's meandering path becomes a figure for indirection and complexity: life does not run straight. The pastoral setting of "grass and the river's crooks" domesticates the earlier flood imagery, placing the river within a landscape that can be observed, appreciated, and aesthetically ordered. Yet the snake metaphor lurking in "snaky coils" reminds the reader that the river, like the serpent, is not entirely trustworthy.

### Aridity and Abandonment

The negative image of the river — its absence — is equally significant. "More pitiable, like rivers lacking rains" (003-118) uses the dried riverbed as a figure for spiritual and emotional depletion. The line "Abandoned; near the river's source" (006-044) compounds this with the paradox of being close to the origin yet cut off from the flow. To be near the river's source and yet abandoned is a peculiarly Meredithian predicament — proximity to vitality that does not guarantee participation in it. The "haws beside the river's brink" (006-010) mark the boundary between fruition and the unattainable current.

### Cowards Shunning Strife

The river appears in a moral register in the line "cowards shunning strife or strivers tired" (016-040). Here the river is implicitly present as the alternative to strife — those who avoid the flood-like struggle of existence, or those exhausted by it. The river is the current that one either fights or yields to; there is no third option. This ethical dimension of the river image — the demand that one engage rather than retreat — aligns with Meredith's broader celebration of active virtue and his suspicion of passivity.
