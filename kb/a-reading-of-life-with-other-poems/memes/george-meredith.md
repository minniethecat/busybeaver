## Biography

George Meredith, OM (12 February 1828 – 18 May 1909) was an English novelist and poet of the Victorian era. Born in Portsmouth, Hampshire, he was the son of a naval outfitter. His mother died when he was five, and his early life was marked by financial instability after his father's bankruptcy. These experiences of social displacement and loss would shape his literary sensibility.

Meredith was educated briefly in Germany at the Moravian school in Neuwied, where he absorbed the German Romantic tradition that would influence his philosophical outlook. Returning to England, he was articled to a solicitor but soon abandoned law for literature. His early career was a struggle — his first major poem, *Poems* (1851), received little attention, and his early novels were often controversial for their frank treatment of sexual politics.

His major novels include *The Ordeal of Richard Feverel* (1859), *The Egoist* (1879), and *Diana of the Crossways* (1885). As a novelist, Meredith was known for his psychological depth, his critique of Victorian sexual hypocrisy, and his densely allusive prose style. His work influenced later modernists including Virginia Woolf and E. M. Forster.

In poetry, Meredith's reputation rests largely on *Modern Love* (1862), a sonnet sequence of 50 sixteen-line poems tracing the dissolution of a marriage, widely regarded as one of the great achievements of Victorian poetry. His later collections, including *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901), continued to explore the philosophical themes — the relationship between Nature and spirit, the evolution of human consciousness, and the struggle toward moral maturity — that animated all his work.

Meredith succeeded Alfred, Lord Tennyson as president of the Society of Authors in 1892. He was awarded the Order of Merit in 1905. He died at his home, Flint Cottage, near Box Hill in Surrey, in 1909.

## Works

### Major Poetry Collections

- *Poems* (1851)
- *Modern Love* (1862) — a 50-poem sonnet sequence on marital breakdown
- *Poems and Lyrics of the Joy of Earth* (1883)
- *Ballads and Poems of Tragic Life* (1887)
- *A Reading of Earth* (1888)
- *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) — the collection analyzed in this wiki

### Major Novels

- *The Ordeal of Richard Feverel* (1859)
- *Evan Harrington* (1861)
- *The Egoist* (1879) — often considered his masterpiece
- *Diana of the Crossways* (1885)

## In This Wiki

Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the subject of this wiki. The collection consists of 18 poems, including the four-part title sequence (*The Vital Choice*, *With the Huntress*, *With the Persuader*, *The Test of Manhood*) along with standalone pieces such as *The Cageing of Ares*, *The Night-Walk*, *Forest History*, and translations from Homer and Mistral.

Key themes explored in this wiki include:
- [[nature]] — Meredith's vision of Nature as both nurturing mother and indifferent law-giver
- [[love]] — the reconciliation of bodily and spiritual love
- [[manhood]] — the moral and spiritual evolution of the human being
- [[war]] — the internal and external conflicts that drive human development

The mythological figures that populate Meredith's poems — [[ares]], the imprisoned war-god; [[zeus]], the Olympian sovereign — are drawn from the classical tradition that Meredith reinterprets for his Victorian audience.
