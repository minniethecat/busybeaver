## Overview

The word "flock" appears twice in Meredith's corpus, both times in a passage of sustained classical imagery that invokes the pastoral and epic traditions of ancient Greece. In Meredith's usage, the flock is simultaneously a natural fact — birds in flight, goats on a hillside — and a cultural symbol, carrying the weight of pastoral poetry, Homeric epic, and the long tradition of representing human society through animal collectives. The thinness of the term's occurrence belies the density of its associations.

## In the Poems

### Winged Flocks of the Air

The first occurrence describes "the air multitudinous winged flocks" (017-061), an image of birds in flight that combines numerical vastness ("multitudinous") with aerial grace. The phrase compresses two perspectives: the individual bird, defined by its wing, and the collective, defined by its multitude. Meredith's choice of "flocks" rather than the more ornithological "flocks of birds" is deliberate — "flock" carries pastoral and biblical resonance that "flight" or "swarm" would lack.

The "wingèd flocks" of the air participate in a poetic tradition stretching back to Homer's bird omens and forward to the Romantic fascination with avian freedom. In Meredith's handling, the emphasis falls on multiplicity rather than on the individual bird's lyric potential: these are flocks, not songbirds, and their significance lies in their collective organization rather than in any single member's flight.

### Vast Flocks of Goats

The second occurrence shifts from the aerial to the terrestrial: "eye their vast flocks of goats" (017-083). The goatherds who "eye" their flocks are figures of watchful pastoral authority — they survey, guard, and govern the animal multitude entrusted to them. The adjective "vast" reinforces the scale: these are not modest village herds but epic quantities, appropriate to the heroic world Meredith is invoking.

The image of goatherds watching over vast flocks taps into a deep classical reservoir — the pastoral poetry of Theocritus and Virgil, the Homeric similes that compare armies to flocks, the biblical language of shepherd and sheep. By placing this image in a passage that also names "the nurser of heroes" (017-009), Meredith connects animal husbandry to the nurture of civilisation itself: the goatherd's vigilance over his flock is a miniature of the ruler's care for his people.

### The Natural Collective

Both occurrences — the winged flocks of the air and the goatherds' vast flocks — position the flock as a figure for collective life in its natural state. The flock is organized but not individually differentiated; it is governed (by the goatherd) or self-organizing (in flight); it represents abundance, multiplicity, and the kind of order that emerges from aggregation rather than command. For Meredith, who is deeply interested in the relationship between individual and collective, the flock offers a natural image of how the many compose a whole without losing their multiplicity.
