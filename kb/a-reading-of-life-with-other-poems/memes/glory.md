## Overview

Glory in Meredith's poetry spans two distinct registers: the cosmic and the Homeric. In the lyric register, glory describes the sun's self-consuming radiance — "He undone in his rays of glory spent" (009-004) — where the word names a splendour so intense it undoes its source. In the epic register of the later poems, glory descends into "glorying" (017-122), the boastful self-congratulation of the archer who vaunts over a minor wound. Between these poles, Meredith traces glory's trajectory from the genuinely luminous to the emptily self-regarding, suggesting that glory, like the sun that embodies it, must set. The concept thus becomes a quiet critique of the heroic tradition from within — glory is real, but it is also transient, and its pursuit easily degrades into vanity.

## In the Poems

### Solar Glory and Self-Undoing

The most concentrated treatment of glory appears in a sunset passage where Meredith describes the dying sun: "He undone in his rays of glory spent / Halo, fair as the bow-shot at his rise, / He casts round her, and knows his hour of rest / Incomplete, were the light for which he dies" (009-004). The syntax is crucial: the sun is *undone* in his own glory — the radiance that defines him is also what exhausts him. Glory here is not a possession but a process of expenditure, a spending that cannot be recovered. The "halo" he casts around the feminine star is the last gift of his dying light, and the phrase "the light for which he dies" makes explicit what the word "glory" has always contained: the implication of sacrifice. To be glorious is to burn out, not to endure.

### Homeric Vainglory and the Archer's Boast

In the Homeric register of the later poems, glory undergoes a sharp deflation. Diomedes answers the archer's taunt with contempt: "Bow-puller, jiber, thy bow for thy glorying, spyer at virgins!" (017-122). The archaic "-ing" form turns glory from a noun into an action — not something one *has* but something one *does*, and something contemptible at that. Diomedes' speech systematically dismantles the archer's claim to martial glory: the bow is a coward's weapon, the "glorying" is hollow, the wound inflicted is "from a woman or some pettish infant." This is Meredith subjecting the heroic code to the reality test of the battlefield, where glory is revealed as a rhetorical posture rather than a quality of action. The distance from "rays of glory spent" to "thy bow for thy glorying" is the distance from the cosmic to the comic, from genuine radiance to self-deceiving boast.
