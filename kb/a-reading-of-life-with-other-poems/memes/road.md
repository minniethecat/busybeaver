## Overview

The road in Meredith's poetry is the archetypal figure for mortal life — a path that must be travelled, a way that can be missed, a surface that bears the marks of those who have passed before. The poet draws on a tradition that stretches from Homer through Dante to the Victorian present, but he gives the figure his own inflection. In Meredith's hands, the road is not simply the pilgrimage route toward salvation or the journey toward wisdom; it is also the place of accident, the site where "a laborious thrust of roadway slew" (014-009) the unwary traveller. The road is life and the road is death, the path of meaning and the zone of meaningless fatality. Meredith holds these contraries in tension, refusing to let the metaphor settle into easy consolation.

## In the Poems

### The Road of Life

The most direct invocation appears in "The Test of Manhood": "Along the road of life!" (004-036) — the exclamation mark registering not excitement but the compressed intensity of the journey. The road of life is the single path each mortal must walk, and the poet's interest lies in what happens along the way: the choices, the obstacles, the fellow-travellers. The line "That way the road" (004-201) — characteristically Meredithian in its compression — suggests the road as constraining frame: life moves in one direction only, toward an end that cannot be evaded.

### The Midway Road

Meredith explicitly invokes the Dantean tradition in "The Sage Enamoured": "Midway the road of our life's term they met" (007-005). The echo of the *Inferno*'s opening — "Nel mezzo del cammin di nostra vita" — places Meredith's poem in the long tradition of the midlife journey narrative. But where Dante's midway is the dark wood of spiritual crisis, Meredith's is the site of a meeting — a social, dialogical event rather than a solitary ordeal. The road, in this recasting, is not simply the individual's path but the shared space where human beings encounter each other.

### The Roadway Missed

The road carries the constant possibility of error. "The roadway missed" (006-046) condenses an entire life's tragedy into three words — the path not seen, the direction not taken, the destination never reached. The passive construction ("was missed") distributes responsibility ambiguously: was it carelessness, or was the road simply invisible? Meredith does not resolve the ambiguity, and the refusal is itself philosophical. Some failures of navigation are moral; others are built into the structure of finite perception.

### The Laborious Thrust

The most violent registration of the road comes in a single line: "Him a laborious thrust of roadway slew" (014-009). The road is not here the passive surface of travel but an active agent of death — a "thrust" that kills. The adjective "laborious" adds a note of the grotesque: the road works at its killing, as if it had purpose. The line belongs to Meredith's unsentimental vision of accident, where the structures built for human purpose — roads for travel — can turn against their makers with lethal indifference.

### The War-Field Roadway

In the Homeric register of "The Centenary of Lincoln," the road becomes the site of military movement: "they led their vacant cars down the roadway gaps of the war-field" (017-108). The vacant cars, the gaps in the roadway, the war-field as destination — the road here leads not to home or wisdom but to the organized violence that is civilization's darkest achievement. The road, in this final transformation, is revealed as a technology of war no less than of peace.
