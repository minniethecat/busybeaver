## Overview

Despair in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is not a mood but a decision — the choice to stop fighting, to lie down, to accept that hope is impossible. It is the great temptation that the collection's central argument about manhood is designed to counter. Meredith treats despair with a mixture of diagnostic clarity and moral urgency: he wants to understand exactly what despair is so that he can tell us exactly why we must refuse it.

The collection distinguishes between two kinds of despair: the emotional despair that arises from personal suffering, and the intellectual despair that arises from a scientific worldview emptied of meaning. Against both, Meredith's answer is the same: despair is a posture the spirit adopts, and the spirit can — must — adopt a different one.

## In the Poems

### Despair Divided from Hope

The collection's first treatment of despair appears in chapter 3, where a maternal figure is invoked with the power to "Divide despair from hope, and sighs from moans" (003-183). The figure — likely Nature or a personification of cosmic order — performs a therapeutic function: she separates what suffering has tangled together. Despair and hope, in the untended heart, blur into each other; the maternal presence draws a line between them, making it possible to name despair as despair and hope as hope. This is Meredith at his most psychologically acute: the first step in resisting despair is recognizing it, distinguishing it from the mere sadness or sighing with which it can be confused.

The pairing of "despair from hope" with "sighs from moans" (003-183) is instructive. Sighs, in Meredith's acoustic vocabulary, belong to the side of hope — they are grief that still breathes, still expects an answer. Moans belong to despair — they are grief that has given up on being heard. The act of division is itself the beginning of healing.

### The Suicide Despair

In *The Test of Manhood* (chapter 4), despair is named in its most dangerous form: "While he rejects the suicide despair" (004-135). The adjective "suicide" is precise — this is despair that aims at self-destruction, the despair that says: better not to be than to suffer. Meredith places this rejection in the context of spiritual discipline: "Throughout his mind the Master Mind being there, / While he rejects the suicide despair; / Accepts the spur of explicable pains" (004-134–136). The alternative to suicide despair is the "spur" — pain understood, pain that has meaning, pain that drives forward rather than downward. "Explicable" is the key word: what makes pain bearable is not its removal but its intelligibility. Despair is pain that has lost its explanation; hope is pain that still makes sense.

### The Captive Who Did Not Despair

A curious counter-example appears late in the collection, in chapter 15, where Meredith describes "A monster in the form of ball; / The mildest captive ever snared, / That neither struggled nor despaired" (015-048–050). The creature — a hedgehog or similar animal rolled into a defensive sphere — is captured but does not despair. Its stillness is not surrender but a different strategy: it simply waits. The absence of despair here is almost pre-moral, an animal quiet that precedes the human drama of hope and hopelessness. Meredith presents it with something like admiration: there is a way of enduring captivity that neither fights nor collapses, and it belongs to the creaturely world that humans, with all their spiritual agonies, have difficulty accessing.

### Science to Despair

The collection's most pointed cultural criticism comes in chapter 16, where Meredith writes: "Its learning is through Science to despair" (016-091). The line is spoken by one character to another, diagnosing the temper of the age. Science, in the late Victorian moment, had stripped the cosmos of its religious meaning, and the result — for those who could not find an alternative source of significance — was despair. But Meredith does not leave the matter there. The response is immediate: "Despair lies down and grovels, grapples not / With evil, casts the burden of its lot" (016-092–093). Despair is not merely unhappy; it is passive. It "lies down," it "grovels," it refuses to "grapple" with the real challenges of existence. This is the moral core of Meredith's anti-despair position: despair is not a conclusion reached by reason but an abdication of responsibility. The world may be difficult; the answer to difficulty is not to lie down.
