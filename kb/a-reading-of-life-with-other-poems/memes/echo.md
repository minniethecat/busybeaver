## Overview

The echo in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is a figure for the way the past persists into the present. An echo is not the original sound but its after-effect — diminished, transformed, yet recognizably connected to its source. Meredith uses the echo to think about memory, tradition, and the way earlier ages continue to shape modern perception. The forest "sprang / An old-world echo, like no mortal thing" (014-108), and the "echoes of it wail" (016-098) through the deeper reaches of experience.

The echo is distinguished from the ancient primarily by its acoustic metaphor. Where the ancient exerts pressure as a lurking presence or a deep groove, the echo works through sound — it rings, it wails, it carries. This acoustic quality gives the echo a more ephemeral, more melancholy character than the ancient. An echo is already fading; to hear it is to catch something on the verge of disappearing.

## In the Poems

### The Old-World Echo of the Forest

In *Forest History* (chapter 14), Meredith describes the forest as a place where the primeval persists as echo: "Familiar, still unseized, the forest sprang / An old-world echo, like no mortal thing" (014-108). The forest is "familiar" — recognizable, known from old stories — yet "still unseized," never fully captured or tamed. It "sprang" into being as an echo, not as an original presence. The echo here is the forest's mode of existence: it is the after-sound of a pre-human world, still ringing in the modern ear. The comparison "like no mortal thing" elevates the echo beyond mere acoustics — it becomes a category of being, the mode in which the ancient world persists.

The hunter's horn that follows — "The hunter's horn might wind a jocund ring" — contrasts with the forest's deeper echo. The human sound is "jocund," cheerful, immediate; the forest's echo is older, stranger, not human in its resonance.

### The Wailing Echoes of the Deep

In a passage from chapter 16, the echo takes on a darker tone: "That know I, though the echoes of it wail, / For one step upward on the crags you scale" (016-098). The "it" refers to "the lower deeps" and to Happiness itself, which "laughs" at those who seek it. The echoes here wail — they are sounds of grief and warning, bouncing off the crags that the climber scales. Meredith suggests that the sound of past suffering never fully dies; it echoes through the deep places of experience, audible to anyone who ventures far enough from the comfortable surface.

The echo, in this darker usage, is the price of depth. To scale the crags — to achieve any "step upward" — is to enter a region where the echoes of past pain are audible. The echo connects the climber to all who have climbed before and suffered in the climbing.
