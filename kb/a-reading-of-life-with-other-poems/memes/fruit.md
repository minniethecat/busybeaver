## Overview

Fruit in Meredith's poetry is never merely the sweet product of a tree. It figures across the collection as the emblem of desire and its fulfillment — "she, our hunger and our fruit" (003-033) — as the cautionary image of premature consumption that "consumes the fruit in bloom" (003-097), and as the sign of consequence itself: "as rain of cloud, as fruit of rain" (004-194). In the agricultural register, fruit is the reward of labor, the harvest drawn from "ploughed and harrowed" ground (005-099). And in the most paradoxical formulation, fruitfulness emerges from ashes — "splendour built of fruitful things in ashes" (005-069) — so that destruction becomes the soil of new growth. Across all of these inflections, Meredith treats fruit as the visible expression of a prior process, the end that reveals the meaning of the beginning.

## In the Poems

### Fruit as Desire and Fulfillment

The identification of fruit with the object of desire appears early and decisively: "she, our hunger and our fruit" (003-033). The apposition is the point — hunger and fruit are not successive states but simultaneous aspects of the same condition. To hunger is already to contain the form of what would satisfy the hunger, and fruit is hunger's implicit shape made visible. This formulation belongs to a poem of courtship and natural affection, where the beloved is at once the source of longing and the answer to it. The line distills a philosophy of desire: fulfillment is not the abolition of hunger but hunger's completion, and fruit is hunger understood.

### Premature Consumption and Its Cost

The warning register sounds in the image of one who would "consume the fruit in bloom" (003-097). To take the fruit while it is still in flower is to destroy both flower and fruit — to sacrifice the future to an impatient present. The passage in which this appears concerns the temptation to seize pleasure before its proper season, and the "fruit in bloom" becomes a figure for all that is ruined by haste. Meredith's agricultural metaphors consistently carry ethical weight: the rhythm of planting, growth, and harvest is also the rhythm of a well-lived life, and to violate that rhythm is to court ruin.

### Fruit as Consequence

"As rain of cloud, as fruit of rain" (004-194) embeds fruit in a chain of natural causation. Cloud produces rain, rain produces fruit — each stage is both a consequence of what came before and a cause of what comes after. This is fruit understood not as reward but as inevitable outcome, the natural result of processes set in motion upstream. In the same poem-sequence, the "fruitful garden camp" (004-119) where all is "choral" suggests a community organized around shared abundance, where fruitfulness is not merely agricultural but social. The fruitful garden is the image of a common life well ordered, where each member contributes to and draws from the shared harvest.

### Fruitfulness from Ashes

The most striking formulation of the fruit theme appears in the line "splendour built of fruitful things in ashes" (005-069). Here fruitfulness is retroactive — the things that were fruitful have become ashes, and from those ashes splendour is built. The logic is phoenix-like: destruction is not the opposite of fruitfulness but its precondition, and the most radiant creations arise from what has been consumed. In the agricultural companion passage, the ground is "fruitful from her ploughed and harrowed" (005-099), where the violence done to the soil — ploughing, harrowing — is precisely what makes it fruitful. Meredith thus arrives at a vision of creativity that does not flee from destruction but metabolizes it: fruit grows from ground that has been cut open.
