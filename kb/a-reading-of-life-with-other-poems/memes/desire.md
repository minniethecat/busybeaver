## Overview

Desire in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is the fundamental vector of human motion — what pulls the self out of its present state and toward something beyond.

The collection opens with desire in its most elevated form: the speaker of "With the Persuader" is drawn toward a goddess-figure who "leaves the thrilled desire" (003-008) in those who glimpse her. "Thrilled" is the precise word — desire is not mere lack but a state of heightened receptivity, a vibration of the whole being in response to something that exceeds it. This thrilled desire is the originating impulse of the entire poetic sequence.

Yet Meredith does not sentimentalize desire. Across the collection, desire appears in multiple registers: as the goddess-pursuing "heart's desire of her, all earth in it" (003-016), as the "ripe desires" that remain "unbled" in the reflective soul (006-068), as the "Desireing and desireable" splendor of the achieved self (004-156), and as the "picture of the settled peace desired" that cowards mistake for virtue (016-039). Desire can lead upward or downward; its moral quality depends on its object and its governance.

The continuum from desire through passion to love is one of the collection's persistent thematic structures. Desire is the beginning — the raw reaching — and its proper end is love. But the path from one to the other is neither automatic nor guaranteed.

## In the Poems

### The Thrilled Origin

The opening movement of "With the Persuader" establishes desire as the primary response to the goddess-figure who animates the collection. The goddess "is lost, and leaves the thrilled desire / To clasp and strike a slackened lyre" (003-008–009). The sequence is telling: presence, loss, and then desire as the residue of loss. Desire is what remains when the beloved object withdraws.

The verb "leaves" makes desire a kind of gift or legacy — the goddess departs but leaves desire behind as the trace of her passage. And desire's first impulse is creative: "to clasp and strike a slackened lyre" — to embrace the instrument of song and to strike it into sound. Desire is the condition of poetic making; without the loss that produces desire, the lyre remains slack.

The poem intensifies this theme a few lines later: "Or heart's desire of her, all earth in it" (003-016). The phrase compresses an extraordinary claim into six words: the heart's desire for the goddess contains "all earth" within it. Desire is not a narrowing but an expansion; to desire the divine is to find the entire world contained in that desire.

### Daring to Desire

Meredith presents desire as requiring courage. In a key passage near the end of "With the Persuader," the speaker reflects that the goddess-figure is "glorious Goddess, and we dared desire" (003-101). The verb "dared" is loaded: to desire the highest is to take a risk, to expose oneself to the possibility of failure and humiliation.

The line appears in the context of the goddess's dangerous radiance — she is glorious but unapproachable, and those who desire her do so at their peril. Yet the poem insists that this daring is the specifically human act. To refuse desire out of caution would be to refuse the condition of human life itself.

This theme connects to the broader Meredithian emphasis on risk and trial. Manhood must be tested (004-131); wisdom must be "spun" from failure (004-236); and desire must be dared. In each case, the human good is not a state of achieved safety but the willingness to venture.

### Desire's Dual Nature

"The Test of Manhood" presents the achieved self in terms that fuse activity and passivity: "Desireing and desireable he shines" (004-156). The man who has found his competence is both the subject of desire (he desires) and its object (he is desirable). The chiasmus is exact: to be fully human is to occupy both positions simultaneously.

This formulation echoes the classical ideal of the *kalos kagathos* — the person who is both good and beautiful, both agent and exemplar. But Meredith gives it a specifically modern inflection: the double position is not a static perfection but a dynamic tension. To desire and to be desired are forces that pull in opposite directions, and to hold them together is an achievement of character.

The dark side of desire appears in "The Night-Walk," where the speaker describes desires that are "unbled" — "Unbled in us the ripe desires" (006-068). The physiological metaphor is striking: desires that are "ripe" but "unbled" are desires that have matured but found no outlet, swelling toward a dangerous fullness. To "bleed" desire is to release its pressure; unbled desire accumulates force that can become destructive.

### The Coward's Peace

"Foresight and Patience" offers the most pointed critique of desire's absence. The speaker describes "A picture of the settled peace desired / By cowards shunning strife or strivers tired" (016-039–040). The "settled peace" that some desire is not the fruit of achievement but the fantasy of escape — the peace of those who "shun strife" or the exhausted who can strive no more.

Here desire is revealed as morally neutral in itself. What matters is not whether one desires but what one desires. The coward desires peace without struggle; the striver desires peace as the fruit of struggle. The same word — "desired" — names both longings, but they lead to opposite destinations.

The poem does not condemn desire as such but redirects it. The proper object of desire is not the absence of difficulty but the presence of achieved good. "Whose union is right reason: join they hands, / The world shall know itself and where it stands" (016-007–008) — the union of Foresight and Patience is the true object of desire, not the escape from the conditions that make them necessary.
