## Overview

Law in Meredith's poetry is a site of sustained ambivalence. On one hand, law is the structural principle of the cosmos — the "rigid laws" (004-146) to which even the natural world must bow, the moral order that distinguishes civilization from chaos. On the other hand, law is the object of sardonic critique — "shallow laws" (003-331) provoke laughter, and the most compelling figures in the mythic landscape are precisely "those lawless" (005-106, 005-110, 005-122). Meredith inherits the classical tension between *nomos* (convention, law) and *physis* (nature, impulse) and gives it a distinctively Victorian inflection: the question is not whether law binds, but whether the laws that bind are adequate to the life they govern. The "lawless Gods" of the collection's mythic satire are not merely transgressors; they represent an alternative order, one whose freedom from law is also a freedom from the shallowness that law, in its rigid application, can enforce.

## In the Poems

### Divine and Natural Law

The most philosophically elevated treatment of law appears in "The Test of Manhood," where the feminine principle — Earth, Nature, or a personified cosmic order — is described as having "rigid laws" to which "her lord" must bow (004-138). The gendering is significant: law is figured as feminine, a constraining force that the masculine principle must submit to if it is to achieve authentic relation. The laws are "iron" (004-146) — inflexible, ancient, indifferent to preference — yet they are also the ground of "rapturous fair" form (004-146). Law here is not opposed to beauty but is its precondition: the iron framework within which rapture becomes possible. This paradox — that freedom requires law, that rapture depends on rigidity — is Meredith's most distinctive contribution to the Victorian discourse on law and liberty.

### Shallow Laws and the Laughing Response

Against the cosmic dignity of natural law, Meredith sets the deflationary force of laughter. The figure who "fills, with laugh at shallow laws" (003-331) does not reject law as such but rejects *shallow* laws — laws that fail to grasp the depth and complexity of the life they purport to regulate. The laugh is an epistemological instrument: it measures the gap between law's pretension and law's adequacy. This is not anarchic rejection but discriminating critique — the laugh spares deep laws (the iron ones of cosmic order) while puncturing shallow ones (the arbitrary conventions of social arrangement). The distinction between shallow and deep law is one the poems invite the reader to internalize as a hermeneutic principle.

### The Lawless Gods

The most memorable figures in Meredith's mythic landscape are defined by their relation to law's absence. Three times in close succession, the satiric passage of "The Test of Manhood" names "those lawless" (005-106, 005-110, 005-122) — the lawless ones who "high they sit," who are "beautiful," "masterful," and finally "first offspring of" something primordial. The anaphoric repetition makes lawlessness a kind of title, an achieved condition rather than a mere privation. These lawless Gods represent a pre-Olympian order — older, wilder, more vital than the regime of law that supplants them. Their beauty and mastery are not despite their lawlessness but because of it. The passage draws on the theogonic tradition (Hesiod's account of successive divine regimes) while posing an uncomfortable question: is the transition from lawlessness to law an unambiguous gain, or is something magnificent lost in the exchange?

### Law and the Natural World

In the nature poems, law recedes from explicit naming but governs the implicit order of the seasons. The "impervious to beak and claws" passage (015-081) describes a resilience that operates according to laws of its own — not the iron laws of cosmic order but the biological laws of adaptation and survival. The poem's speaker observes natural creatures navigating their world according to instinctual law, a law that requires no legislation because it is inscribed in the organism. This unspoken natural law provides a quiet counterpoint to the explicit legal language of the philosophical poems — law as lived pattern rather than imposed constraint.
