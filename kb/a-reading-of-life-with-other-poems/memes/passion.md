## Overview

Passion in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) occupies a double position: it is both the dangerous force that must be governed and the indispensable fire without which life has no motive power.

Meredith's treatment of passion resists the simple Victorian dichotomy of reason versus emotion. Passion is not the enemy of order but its raw material — "of rabble passions grow the chieftain Mind" (016-235). The metaphor is deliberately provocative: passions are a "rabble," a disorderly crowd, yet from this rabble the "chieftain" of Mind emerges. Governance does not come from outside passion but from within it, through a process of internal differentiation and hierarchy.

The collection's opening sequence traces passion through multiple guises: as the divine radiance of "the passion's Queen" (003-061), as the surging force that "rolled a quiet sea" in reflective moments (006-069), as the Homeric "passion to crush" enemies in battle (017-081), and as the drowsing "eye of passion" numbed by excess (005-142). Together these images map the full range of passion's manifestations — from the celestial to the brutal, from the quiescent to the explosive.

## In the Poems

### Passion as Divine Radiance

In "With the Persuader," passion appears in its most exalted form: the goddess-figure who is "with eyelids unabashed the passion's Queen" (003-061). The epithet is striking — "unabashed" suggests passion without shame, without concealment, radiant in its own right. This is not the passion that must be hidden or repressed but the passion that illuminates, that "by guerdon of her brilliance may be seen" (003-060).

Yet even this radiant Queen is "shorn of attendant Graces" (003-062) — stripped of the conventional attendants that might soften or socialize her power. Meredith's passion-queen is naked in her intensity, and the question the poem poses is whether such intensity can be borne without the mediating Graces.

The same poem later warns against letting passion usurp the place of love: "As Beauty's Queen, should passion head the rout" (003-221). The image of passion "heading the rout" — leading the disorderly crowd — anticipates the later metaphor of "rabble passions." When passion takes command rather than serving as fuel, it produces chaos rather than order. "Be sure the ruddy hue is Love's: to woo" (003-222) — the true sovereign is Love, and passion is its instrument, not its substitute.

### The Surging Force

The ocean metaphor recurs in Meredith's treatment of passion. In "With the Persuader," the speaker describes "the incessant surge / Her forces mixed of craft and passion urge / For the one whelming wave to spring aloof" (003-147–149). Passion and craft are "mixed" — blended so thoroughly that they cannot be distinguished. The "whelming wave" that springs "aloof" is the moment of breakthrough, the point at which the accumulated force of passion-craft achieves its objective.

The verb "urge" is crucial: passion in Meredith is fundamentally a driving force, an impulse toward action. It is not a state to be enjoyed or suffered but a pressure that demands outlet.

By contrast, in "The Night-Walk," passion achieves a momentary stillness: "And passion rolled a quiet sea, / Whereon was Love the phantom sail" (006-069–070). Here passion is not the surging wave but the "quiet sea" — tamed, expansive, the medium upon which Love can sail. The transformation is from turbulence to depth, from agitation to readiness.

### Passion and Excess

The dangers of passion untempered appear most clearly in "The Cageing of Ares," where Meredith describes "The very eye of passion drowsed by excess, / And yet a burning lion for the spring" (005-142–143). The image is paradoxical: passion's "eye" is "drowsed" — half-closed, narcotized — by its own excess, yet the same passion remains "a burning lion for the spring," poised to leap.

This is the paradox of passion at scale: satiety produces torpor, but the underlying force is never extinguished. The "burning lion" waits beneath the drowsed surface, ready to spring when stimulation returns. Meredith understands passion as a reservoir that can be temporarily depleted but never drained; it is a permanent feature of human nature that must be managed rather than eliminated.

The warning in "The Test of Manhood" extends this understanding to the moral realm: "It may befal his passions and his greeds / To lose their treasures, like the vein that bleeds" (004-186–187). Passion, like greed, can hemorrhage — can "lose their treasures" through the very intensity of their expenditure. The physiological metaphor ("like the vein that bleeds") grounds the abstract warning in bodily experience.

### The Rabble and the Chieftain

The culminating metaphor for Meredith's treatment of passion comes in "Foresight and Patience": "Then may sharp suffering their nature grind; / Of rabble passions grow the chieftain Mind" (016-235). The two lines form a causal sequence: suffering grinds the nature, and from the ground material — the "rabble passions" — the organized principle of Mind emerges.

"Rabble" is a political metaphor: passions are the disorderly multitude, Mind is the chieftain who rules them. But the chieftain does not come from outside the rabble; it "grows" from within, through a process of "sharp suffering" that grinds down the raw material into usable form.

This formulation is characteristically Meredithian in its refusal of dualism. Mind is not the opposite of passion but its refinement. The passions are not the enemy of reason but its prerequisite — without the rabble, there can be no chieftain.

### Homeric Passion

In the "Fragments of the Iliad," Meredith renders passion in its most elemental, martial form: the Achaean warriors are "prompt for the dash at the Trojan host, with the passion to crush them" (017-081). Here passion is undifferentiated battle-fury — the collective drive to destroy the enemy. There is no moral qualification, no refinement; this is passion as raw motive force in its Homeric original.

The juxtaposition of Homeric passion with Victorian reflection is deliberate. The Iliad fragments remind the reader that the "rabble passions" from which Mind must grow are ancient and universal; they are not a Victorian problem but a human condition.
