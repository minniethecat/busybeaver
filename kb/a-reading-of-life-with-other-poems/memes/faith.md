## Overview

Faith in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is primarily a practical virtue rather than a theological position. It is the disposition that enables forward movement when the outcome is uncertain — "So moves he forth in faith, if he has made / His inner discourse" (004-209). Faith is the bridge between inward conviction and outward action, the quality that transforms private understanding into public commitment.

Meredith's treatment of faith reflects his post-Darwinian context. He does not dismiss faith as obsolete, but he redefines it: faith is not assent to propositions but fidelity to one's own deepest insights. "Ay, be we faithful to ourselves: despise / The loud world's barter" (016-263) — this line from the collection's closing poem crystallizes the Meredithian conception. Faith is self-fidelity in a world of noise and transaction.

## In the Poems

### Faith as Forward Motion

The central passage on faith appears in *The Test of Manhood* (chapter 4). "So moves he forth in faith, if he has made / His inner discourse, and the strength may hold / To match the hope he plants in his own mould" (004-209) describes the man who has achieved inner coherence stepping into the world. Faith here is not a leap into darkness but the natural outward expression of inward integration. The "inner discourse" — the conversation one has with oneself — must precede and ground the act of faith.

The passage continues with the feminine counterpart: "What, then, the faith she feels? / She feels it, and her passage through the field / Of spousal gifts, resplendent springs, to faith refreshed compels" (004-226, 004-238). The woman's faith operates differently — felt rather than discoursed, a passage through gifts rather than a march into combat — but it too "compels" toward renewal. Faith in both its masculine and feminine modes is generative.

### Faith Shaken and Tested

Meredith does not present faith as easy or stable. "Your faith in me appals, to shake my own" (016-021) captures the interpersonal dimension of faith's fragility. Faith placed in another person is a heavy burden — "appalling" in its weight — and doubt in one quarter can "shake" faith elsewhere. The collection is honest about the cost of faith: it exposes one to disappointment and betrayal.

"Which sets faith quaking on their firmest rock" (016-153) intensifies this recognition. Even "firmest rock" — the foundations one thought unshakeable — can be made to tremble. This is not cynicism but realism. Meredith's faith is strong precisely because it has faced its own vulnerability.

### Faith as Endurance

The collection's closing vision redeems faith through endurance. "With faith, that of privations and spilt / Blood of her sons, the Mother feels no guilt" (016-250) places faith in the context of cosmic indifference. Nature — "the Mother" — feels no guilt for the suffering she prescribes. Faith in this context is not belief that suffering will cease but the resolve to continue despite it. It is the human answer to Nature's silence.

The final injunction — "Ay, be we faithful to ourselves: despise / The loud world's barter" (016-263) — returns faith to its root meaning of fidelity. To be faithful is to refuse the "barter" of self for approval, to hold fast to one's inner measure against the world's noise.
