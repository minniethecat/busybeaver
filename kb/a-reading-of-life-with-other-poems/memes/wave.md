## Overview

The wave in Meredith's poetry is a figure of rhythmic power: it rises, crests, and breaks in patterns that mirror emotional intensity, natural force, and the movement of verse itself. From the "rapturous waves" (003-018) on which a feminine figure glides to the Homeric "sea-wave" that "bellows abroad" (017-151), wave imagery spans the collection's emotional and stylistic range. Waves can be vehicles of ecstatic transport, metaphors for hesitation and doubt ("wavered," 004-037), or sublime expressions of nature's overwhelming force. The semantic field encompasses both the literal sea-wave and the figurative "wave" of feeling, thought, or collective action, drawing on a poetic tradition that runs from Homer's *polyphloisboio thalasses* through Shakespeare to the Romantics.

## In the Poems

### Waves of Rapture and Vision

The collection's early poems establish the wave as a medium of visionary experience. "On rapturous waves we saw her glide" (003-018) makes the wave not an obstacle but a conveyance — a surface across which a transcendent feminine figure moves with ease. The wave is rapturous, charged with ecstatic energy that transfers to the observers. This positive valence is complicated by "the one whelming wave to spring aloof" (003-149), where the wave threatens to overwhelm entirely — a single, catastrophic surge that demands a leap to safety. The alternation between transport and threat is characteristic: the same wave that carries can drown. "She flecks along a run of waves" (003-288) returns to the lighter register, the feminine figure skimming across wave-tops as a bird might, touching each crest without being engulfed.

### The Wavering Mind

A significant variant in Meredith's usage is "waver" — motion that is hesitant rather than powerful. "A wavering phantom's red volcano tongue" (014-004) combines indecisive motion with volcanic violence, the wavering of the phantom contrasting with the fixity of the eruption it portends. "And marvel not he wavered if at whiles" (004-037) explicitly thematizes wavering as a human response to overwhelming circumstance: to waver is to be human, to oscillate between resolve and doubt. "Listless waved on vacant air" (015-100) drains the wave of its energy entirely: the waving is "listless," the air "vacant," the motion purposeless. These usages together construct a spectrum from purposeful wave-motion through hesitant wavering to empty gesturing.

### Waves in Classical and Homeric Context

The collection's most powerful wave imagery appears in the Homeric register of the later poems. "NOT the sea-wave so bellows abroad when it bursts on the beetling / Cliff" (017-151) is a direct Homeric simile, the wave striking the headland with a roar that serves as the measure against which other sounds are calibrated. This is wave as sublime force, the *ekphrasis* of nature's most intimidating voice. The "shoulders of his brother Otos waved" (005-014) brings wave imagery into the domain of the Aloadae giants, where even the body of a mythic being moves with wave-like undulation. The wave here is not just water but a quality of motion that can characterize any massive form.

### Waves Beneath the Surface

"Where underneath of their feet waved loftiest growths of the wood" (017-140) extends wave imagery beneath the earth's surface. The "growths of the wood" — roots or subterranean vegetation — wave in an unseen medium, their motion analogous to marine plants swaying in a current. This underground wave blurs the boundary between forest and sea, suggesting that the wave-principle of rhythmic motion operates across all elements. The wave is not confined to water; it is a universal pattern of motion that manifests wherever flexible forms meet flowing force.
