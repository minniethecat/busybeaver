## Overview

Peace in George Meredith's *A Reading of Life, with Other Poems* (1901) is not a simple good. It is praised by grateful mortals as "Delightful Peace, that answers Reason's call / Harmoniously" (005-148), yet Meredith's speakers consistently expose the settled peace "desired / By cowards shunning strife" (016-039) as a dangerous illusion.

Peace that comes from avoiding conflict is not peace at all; it is vegetation, a state "beneath" consciousness.

This tension between the ideal of peace and the reality of its counterfeits runs through the collection's political and philosophical poems. Meredith writes in the aftermath of the Victorian peace — the Pax Britannica — and his skepticism toward easy peace reflects a deeper moral realism.

As the speaker declares in "Foresight and Patience": "No home is here for peace while evil breeds" (016-063). Peace is not the default condition of human life but an achievement. Like all achievements, it requires active effort rather than passive acquiescence.

## In the Poems

### The Reasonable Peace

In "The Cageing of Ares," peace appears as a divine gift received in the war-god's absence: "Then did good Gaea's children gratefully / Lift hymns to Gods they judged, but praised for peace, / Delightful Peace, that answers Reason's call / Harmoniously and images her Law" (005-148).

The capitalized abstractions — Peace, Reason, Law — and the adverb "Harmoniously" establish peace as a cosmic value, aligned with rationality and natural order. But the qualifier "judged" is Meredith's characteristic needle: the gods are thanked only after humans have judged them, and peace is praised only because it aligns with human reason.

The gratitude is conditional, earned rather than given.

### The Coward's Peace

The sharpest critique of false peace appears in "Foresight and Patience": "A picture of the settled peace desired / By cowards shunning strife or strivers tired" (016-039). The word "picture" is the first indictment — it is not peace itself but an image of peace, a representation rather than the thing.

"Settled" is the second — peace that has settled is peace that has stopped moving, stopped negotiating, stopped living. The parallel between "cowards shunning strife" and "strivers tired" is deliberately uncomfortable: the exhausted striver and the coward may arrive at the same posture, but only one warrants respect.

The sentence refuses to distinguish them visually, forcing the reader to supply the moral difference.

### No Home for Peace

Meredith extends the critique into a political maxim: "No home is here for peace while evil breeds, / While error governs, none" (016-063). The present tense — "breeds," "governs" — makes this a statement about the permanent human condition, not a temporary emergency. Evil is always breeding; error is always governing.

The double negative ("No home...none") closes the door on any hope of permanent peace within history. The lines that follow — "let stout contention drive deep furrows" (016-067) — propose the alternative: active conflict, the deep furrows of struggle, as the truer path toward whatever peace may be possible.

### The Quiet Sea Within

A quieter register appears in "The Night-Walk," where Meredith records an inner stillness: "And passion rolled a quiet sea" (006-069). The verb "rolled" retains the motion of passion — this is not the absence of feeling but feeling brought to a state of rhythmic calm.

The adjective "quiet" rather than "peaceful" is characteristic of Meredith's precision: quiet is a sound condition, the absence of noise rather than the absence of force. The sea still rolls; it simply rolls quietly, its power intact but its turbulence stilled.
