## Overview

Sister appears in Meredith's poetry in two distinct registers: as a familial relationship grounded in shared origin, and as an allegorical device through which abstract virtues speak to one another. The word recurs most densely in the dialogue poems of "A Reading of Life," where Foresight addresses her "blind sister Patience" (016-020) and the speaker reflects on "my sister" as a figure of introspective reckoning (016-164). In the nature poems, "sister snowflake" and "sister snowdrop" (007-031) extend the kinship metaphor into the seasonal world, binding natural phenomena in a web of familial relation. Across all usages, Meredith's sisters are figures of intimate knowledge — they read one another, challenge one another, and owe one another debts of understanding.

## In the Poems

### Sister as Allegorical Personification

The most sustained sister-relationship in the collection unfolds in "A Reading of Life," where Foresight speaks to "her blind sister Patience" (016-020). The pairing is philosophically precise: Foresight sees what is to come, but Patience — though blind — possesses the endurance necessary to reach it. "Yet must I read my sister for the How" (016-030), Foresight declares, acknowledging that vision without patience cannot translate into action. The allegorical sisters are interdependent, each supplying what the other lacks. Later in the same poem, Foresight reflects on "my sister" (016-083, 016-164, 016-212) across multiple passages that chart a progression from rivalry to mutual recognition, culminating in the acknowledgment: "This thought unto my sister do I owe" (016-277).

### Sister in the Natural World

The allegorical mode extends into the nature poetry through the arresting image of "sister snowflake sister snowdrop kissed" (007-031). Here the sister-relation bridges the inanimate and the botanical — snowflake and snowdrop are sisters not by blood but by season, by shared whiteness, by their common role as heralds of winter's end. The repetition of "sister" without conjunction creates a sense of proliferating kinship: everything in the natural world is sister to something else. The maternal figure who "has a sister's tones" (003-181) further blurs the boundary between familial roles, suggesting that sisterly intimacy can inhere in relationships not literally sororal.

### Sister and the Feminine Principle

Throughout the collection, sister overlaps with Meredith's broader treatment of the feminine. "She, that breath of upper air" (002-051) is not named as sister but moves through the poems with a sister's intimacy toward the natural world. The "mantled sister, Day" (006-027) makes the sister-relation cosmic: Day is sister to Night, each mantled in the other's absence. This cosmic sisterhood frames the daily cycle as a familial alternation rather than a hostile opposition. In the Homeric register of the later poems, the sister relation acquires martial stakes — "My sister calls for battle; is it she?" (016-058) — where the sister's voice becomes the summons to heroic action.
