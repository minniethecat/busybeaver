## Overview

Midnight appears twice in Meredith's poetry, in chapters 3 and 14, and both instances configure it as a time out of time — an hour when the ordinary rules of day and night are suspended. In chapter 3, midnight is the paradoxical moment when a bird answers as if it were morning, the darkness speaking with the voice of dawn (003-089). In chapter 14, midnight is the hour of a fairy dance, when "a small people" — thin enough to dwindle through a sieve — emerge to dance the dales (014-119). Between the prophetic bird and the dancing small people, Meredith's midnight is the hour of threshold beings: creatures that belong neither to day nor to night, neither to the human nor to the fully other.

Meredith draws on the deep folklore tradition of midnight as the witching hour, the time when spirits walk and transformations occur. But his midnights are neither gothic nor terrifying. They are moments of uncanny vitality — the bird that answers as if it were already morning, the dancers so thin they barely exist. Midnight in Meredith is not the hour of death but the hour of improbable life.

## In the Poems

### The Midnight's Morning Bird: Prophetic Threshold

The first midnight is embedded in a scene of laughter and sylvan concealment: "Through shelter leaves, the laughter from her throat, / We answer as the midnight's morning's bird" (003-088, 003-089). A woman laughs through sheltering leaves, and "we" — the collective voice of the poem — answer her. But the answer is strange: they answer "as the midnight's morning's bird." The double genitive — "midnight's morning's bird" — compresses two times into one creature. The bird belongs to midnight, but it also belongs to morning; it is the bird that, at the darkest hour, already sings with the voice of dawn.

This is a prophetic bird. It does not wait for the sun to rise; it announces the sun while the sun is still below the horizon. To answer "as the midnight's morning's bird" is to respond to present joy with future-oriented song — to hear laughter now and answer with the promise of more laughter to come. The line that follows — "She laughs, she wakens gleeful cries" (003-090) — confirms the movement from midnight toward morning, from the solitary laugh toward the general wakening.

The bird of midnight's morning is a creature of anticipation. It lives in the dark but sings the light. Meredith's midnight, in this first occurrence, is not a terminus but a turning point — the moment when the direction of time begins to bend back toward the sun.

### The Small People Dancing: Folklore Threshold

Chapter 14 shifts from prophetic bird to folkloric vision: "At midnight a small people danced the dales" (014-119). The scene follows an evocation of storm and thunder — "Had voice when lowered heavens drummed for gales" (014-118) — and the midnight dance answers the storm's percussion with human (or near-human) motion. The "small people" are diminutive, perhaps fairy-like, and the following line emphasizes their insubstantiality: "So thin that they might dwindle through a sieve" (014-120).

These dancers are threshold beings in the most literal sense: they are thin enough to pass through the mesh of a sieve, barely substantial, hovering between existence and nonexistence. They dance the dales — the valleys, the low places — at midnight, the low hour. Their dance is not majestic but precarious, the motion of beings who might at any moment dwindle into nothing.

The juxtaposition of storm and dance is telling. The heavens drum "for gales" — the music of the sky is violence. And the small people answer not with flight or fear but with dancing. Their dance is an assertion of presence against the overwhelming force of the storm, a thin-bodied defiance. Midnight is their hour because midnight is when the boundary between the real and the unreal is thinnest — when small people can pass through sieves and dance in dales, unobserved by the daylight world.
