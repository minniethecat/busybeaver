## Overview

The throne appears at two closely spaced moments in "With the Persuader" (003), both in passages that examine the costs of sovereignty. In Meredith's handling, the throne is never merely a piece of furniture or a neutral symbol of authority: it is a station that imposes constraints on its occupant, a height from which one may fall, a seat that elevates and encloses in equal measure. The two occurrences together articulate a subtle theory of power — that sovereignty is not freedom but a form of enclosure, and that the enthroned figure is as much imprisoned as empowered.

## In the Poems

### Quitting the Throne

The first occurrence describes a figure who "quits a throne, and must enclose" (003-208). The verb "quits" is decisive: this is not abdication under pressure but an act of voluntary departure. Yet the departure is immediately qualified by obligation — "must enclose." To leave the throne is not to escape into freedom but to exchange one form of enclosure for another. The sovereign who steps down does not enter an open field but a different kind of confinement.

This framing complicates any simple reading of power as liberation. In Meredith's vision, the throne confers authority but at the price of boundedness; to sit on it is to be defined by it, and to leave it is to discover that the definition was not imposed by the seat alone but by the structure of relations that placed one there. Quitting the throne does not quit the condition of being a former sovereign.

### The Throned Figure

The second occurrence is compressed into a single epithet: "the throned" (003-312). The past participle transforms the throne from an object one sits on into a condition one inhabits. To be "throned" is not merely to occupy a seat of power but to have become identified with that seat — the throne has become an attribute of the person rather than an external object. This is sovereignty as state of being rather than as possession.

The passage in which "throned" appears is concerned with perishing and endurance: what perishes is a particular configuration of self, while the throned figure — the essential self identified with its station — persists. The throne, in this reading, is not just a symbol of political authority but a figure for the soul's relation to its own identity. To be throned is to be settled, established, fixed in one's nature — for better and for worse.

### Sovereignty as Condition

The two occurrences, read together, present a nuanced account of what it means to hold power. The throne both elevates (one is "throned," raised above others) and encloses (one "must enclose," bounded by the very station that confers authority). Sovereignty in Meredith's poetry is never simple dominion; it is always also subjection — to the role, to its demands, to the structure of expectation that surrounds the enthroned figure. This is consistent with Meredith's broader tendency to expose the costs that attend every form of elevation.
