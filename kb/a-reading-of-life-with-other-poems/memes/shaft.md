## Overview

The shaft — an arrow or spear in flight — appears in Meredith's poetry as a concentrated image of directed force, mortal consequence, and gendered contest.

Unlike the diffuse radiance of [[light]] or the elemental sweep of [[wind]], the shaft is a pinpoint instrument: it travels from agent to target along a trajectory that Meredith exploits for dramatic irony.

In "With the Huntress" the shaft belongs to Artemis, goddess of the chase, strung with "archer's craft" (002-031).

In the Homeric fragments of the Iliad, the shaft becomes the fulcrum of a warrior's honour code, where Paris's grazing arrow and Diomedes's "keen shaft" (017-130) draw a moral line between the archer who boasts and the spearman who kills.

## In the Poems

### The Huntress's Shaft (With the Huntress)

Meredith opens the shaft image through Artemis, whose depiction as "Quiver-charged and crescent-crowned" aligns the weapon with divine femininity.

The line "Leaps she, bow to shoulder, shaft / Strung to barb with archer's craft" (002-031) compresses the act of aiming into a single kinetic moment.

Unlike the static bow, the shaft implies motion already begun — a commitment to release that cannot be recalled.

Meredith's choice to name the "shaft" rather than the arrow emphasizes the projectile's physicality: it is a length of wood, feathered and barbed, as much a crafted object as a symbol.

### The Battlefield Shaft (Fragments of the Iliad)

The Iliad fragments reframe the shaft as a test of masculine worth.

Paris, having grazed Diomedes with an arrow, boasts "not in vain flew the shaft; how by rights it had pierced thee / Into the undermost gut" (017-116).

His vaunting reveals the shaft as proxy for the warrior himself — to land a shaft is to claim superiority.

Diomedes's retort demolishes this logic: "Aye flies blunted the dart of the man that's emasculate, noughtworth! / Otherwise hits, forth flying from me ... My keen shaft, and it numbers a man of the dead fallen straightway" (017-130).

The contrast is structural: Paris's grazing shaft against Diomedes's lethal one, the archer against the spearman, boast against deed.

Meredith uses the Homeric material to examine the shaft not merely as weapon but as a moral index — the same object signifies cowardice or courage depending on whose hand releases it.

### The Gendered Shaft

Across the two poems, Meredith constructs an implicit parallel between Artemis the divine huntress and Diomedes the mortal warrior.

Both wield the shaft with lethal certainty; both are contrasted with lesser figures — the fleeing beasts for Artemis, Paris for Diomedes.

The huntress's shaft flies in the realm of nature and myth; the warrior's in the realm of men and history.

Together they establish the shaft as a poetic image that bridges the feminine-divine and masculine-heroic without collapsing one into the other.
