## Overview

The dance in Meredith's poetry figures the transformation of raw motion into patterned form. To dance is to subject the body's energies to rhythm and design, to make of movement something communicable and shared. Meredith deploys the dance across a striking range of scales: from the mythic-cosmic ("Theirs the dance to tame the rude," 002-038) to the folkloric ("At midnight a small people danced the dales," 014-119), from the comic ("Her visitor performed a dance," 015-094) to the merely kinetic ("Who merely danced when light winds blew," 015-080). Across these variations, the dance consistently marks the boundary between the human and the formless — where dance is, meaning has entered motion.

## In the Poems

### Dance as Cosmic Order

The collection's first dance is attributed to divine powers whose province is the dance: "Theirs the dance to tame the rude" (002-038). The verb "tame" identifies the dance's fundamental work — it is a civilizing force, a way of bringing the "rude" (the raw, the unformed, the violent) into the domain of order. This is dance at its most ambitious: not entertainment but cosmogony, the rhythmic patterning through which chaos becomes cosmos. Meredith's phrasing grants the dance agency — it is not that the gods dance but that "theirs" is the dance as instrument of transformation, as tool of taming. The dance preceded the tamed world.

### The Midnight Dance of the Small People

In a passage of folkloric strangeness, Meredith describes how "At midnight a small people danced the dales" (014-119). The "small people" suggest fairies, elves, or the diminished pagan deities who survive in folklore — beings for whom dance is not performance but mode of existence. They dance "the dales" (the valleys themselves are the dance floor, the landscape their ballroom), and they dance at midnight, the threshold hour when the ordinary world's claim on order loosens. The dance here is liminal, connecting the human world of daylight labor to the unseen world of nocturnal animation. Meredith's "small people" are the custodians of a dance older than human memory.

### Dance and Shadow: The Kinetic and the Meaningful

Meredith draws a subtle distinction between mere motion and genuine dance. The figure who "merely danced when light winds blew" (015-080) is placed in contrast with a visitor who "performed a dance" (015-094). "Merely danced" suggests automatic, unshaped response to stimulus — the body swaying because the wind blows — while "performed a dance" implies intention, repertoire, the execution of a form. The difference is the difference between being moved and moving oneself, between passivity and agency. Meredith's interest is in the threshold where motion becomes dance, where the body's movement acquires the quality of an act.

### Shadows Dancing: The Inanimate Animated

The dance also appears as a figure for the play of light and shadow in nature: "mountain crags their shadows danced" (005-007). Here it is not living beings but shadows that dance — the inanimate animated by the sun's movement, geology made kinetic by optics. This image extends the dance's domain beyond the human, the folkloric, and the divine into the mineral world itself. If shadows can dance, then the capacity for patterned motion is latent in all things, awaiting only the right angle of light.
