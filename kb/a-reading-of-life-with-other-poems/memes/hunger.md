## Overview

Hunger in Meredith's poetry is not mere appetite but a philosophical principle — the driving force that propels human beings toward what they lack. It is "unmated" (003-033), a desire without predetermined object; it "spurs" the pursuit of Beauty (003-329); it stands alongside fear as one of the two great human motivators (004-058); and it "grandly marches" (016-075), transforming bodily need into collective motion.

Meredith treats hunger as a fundamental category of human experience, prior to reason and irreducible to satisfaction. Unlike thirst, which can be quenched, hunger in Meredith's lexicon is a permanent condition — what drives us is precisely what we cannot permanently satisfy. The "hunger after Beauty" is not a problem to be solved but a structure of human consciousness.

## In the Poems

### Unmated Hunger: The Unpaired Drive

Meredith's most compressed formulation of hunger appears as "she, our hunger and our fruit" (003-033). The pronoun "she" refers to an unnamed feminine principle — possibly Nature, possibly Beauty, possibly the beloved — who is simultaneously the source of hunger and its satisfaction. She is "our hunger" (what we lack) and "our fruit" (what fulfills), collapsed into a single figure.

The preceding adjective "unmated" intensifies the paradox: hunger here is desire without a partner, a reaching-out that finds no complementary reaching-back. To be unmated is to be incomplete without the guarantee of completion — hunger as existential condition rather than temporary state.

### Hunger After Beauty

Meredith extends hunger into the aesthetic domain: "while hunger after Beauty spurs" (003-329). The preposition "after" converts hunger from a static condition into a directional force — it is not hunger for something possessed but hunger pursuing something ahead, always receding.

"Spurs" introduces the language of horsemanship and chivalry: hunger is the spur that drives the rider forward, the sharp prompt that will not permit rest. Beauty in this scheme is not a possession but a pursuit, known not through attainment but through the quality of the chase.

### Hunger and Fear: The Twin Motivators

In a catalog of fundamental human drives, Meredith pairs "our hungers or our fears" (004-058) as the two forces that shape action. Hunger pulls toward; fear pushes away. Together they describe the full range of motivated behavior — approach and avoidance, desire and dread.

By placing hunger before fear, Meredith suggests its primacy. Fear is reaction; hunger is initiative. The hungry organism moves into the world seeking what it lacks; the fearful organism retreats from what threatens. Meredith's philosophical anthropology begins with the former.

### Prompting Hungers: The Grand March

In the collected motion of human masses, Meredith observes hungers "prompting" action — "and they grandly march" (016-075). The verb "prompting" grants hunger a linguistic function: it is not a blind urge but a directive, a cue that initiates response.

"Grandly march" transforms individual appetite into collective pageantry. What begins as bodily lack becomes, when aggregated across a population, historical motion — armies, migrations, civilizations on the move. Meredith's hunger is not merely personal but civilizational, the engine beneath the grandest human spectacles.
