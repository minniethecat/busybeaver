## Overview

Dawn in Meredith's poetry functions as a threshold — the liminal hour suspended between night's inwardness and day's demand. It is the moment where the world reconstitutes itself, where animal life stirs into visible form, and where human consciousness confronts the promise or threat of what is to come.

Meredith treats dawn not as a simple symbol of hope or renewal — though it carries those associations — but as a charged interval of potential. "Midway between eve and dawn" (002-002) positions the speaker at a point of equipoise; "quiet dawn was Reverence" (004-013) personifies the hour as a moral quality; the deer's wet nostrils at dawn (014-098) register the animal sensorium awakening; "a brave To-be is dawn upon my brow" (016-029) makes dawn an inward condition — a future pressing against the forehead.

Across these variations, dawn is never merely backdrop. It is an event in consciousness, a daily re-negotiation of the boundary between darkness and light.

## In the Poems

### The Poised Interval: Between Eve and Dawn

Meredith places his speaker "midway between eve and dawn" (002-002) — not at either terminus but in the passage between them. The word "midway" insists on suspension: the speaker inhabits neither the completed day nor the approaching one, neither darkness nor light, but the transit itself.

This betweenness is characteristic of Meredith's philosophical temper. His speakers rarely occupy fixed positions; they are perpetually in motion, crossing from one state to another, and it is in the crossing — not the arrival — that meaning resides.

### Dawn as Reverence

In one of Meredith's most compressed metaphysical formulations, he describes a "quiet dawn" that "was Reverence" (004-013). The capital letter on "Reverence" elevates the abstraction to the status of a personified presence — dawn not as a time of day but as a moral condition made visible.

The adjective "quiet" is essential: this is not the spectacular sunrise of Romantic convention but a subdued, almost inward illumination. Reverence here is not performed but inhabited, a quality of attention rather than a gesture of worship. Meredith's dawn-reverence suggests that the world's beauty arrives not as spectacle but as quiet demand.

### Animal Dawn: The Deer's Wet Nostrils

Meredith's most sensuous dawn image is zoological rather than theological: "at dawn the deer's wet nostrils" (014-098). The line registers dawn through an animal body — not what the human observer sees but what the deer smells, the moisture condensing on its nose in the cool morning air.

This shift from visual to olfactory experience is characteristic of Meredith's naturalism. Dawn is not primarily a spectacle of light but a change in the quality of the air, registered by the body before the mind. The deer knows dawn through its nostrils; the poet learns from the deer.

### Dawn on the Brow: The Inward Future

Meredith's most compressed dawn figure transforms the phenomenon into an interior event: "a brave To-be is dawn upon my brow" (016-029). Dawn here is not an external sunrise but a future pressing against the skin — "upon my brow" locating the sensation at the forehead, the traditional seat of thought and identity.

The hyphenated "To-be" turns the infinitive into a noun, a substance: what is to come has the tangible pressure of dawn light on the face. "Brave" colors the future with courage — not the absence of fear but the determination to meet what comes. Meredith's dawn-brow becomes a figure for the heroic temperament itself: the capacity to face the coming day as an act of will.
