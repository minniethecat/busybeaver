## Overview

The frost in Meredith's poetry operates across three distinct registers — natural, psychological, and philosophical — each deepening the others. It is the "frosty wail" (002-022) of a winter landscape haunted by the Huntress; the existential cold of those whose "youth a frost, their age the dirge for youth" (003-153); and the moral condition that Philosophy must overcome, making us "Warm in the frost" (016-272).

Unlike the simple seasonal progression from cold to warmth, Meredith's frost is a permanent possibility — a condition that can afflict youth as much as age, that can be found in the moral world as much as the natural one. The frost is not something one simply endures until spring arrives; it is something one must learn to be warm within, an act of will and wisdom that defines the human response to a world that does not guarantee warmth.

## In the Poems

### The Huntress and the Frosty Wail

In "With the Huntress," Meredith describes a mountain landscape haunted by the titular goddess: "Mountains hear her and call back, / Shrewd with night: a frosty wail" (002-021–022). The "frosty wail" is an acoustic image — cold given voice — and it is "shrewd with night," suggesting not merely temperature but intelligence, cunning, the knowing quality of winter darkness.

The Huntress (Artemis/Diana) is associated with the wild, the untamed, the cold heights where human warmth does not reach. Her "frosty wail" is the sound of nature in its inhospitable aspect — beautiful but indifferent, calling across mountains that echo but do not answer. The frost here is the signature of the divine as something distant and untouchable, a cold that awes without comforting.

### Frost as the Condition of the Unloved

In "With the Persuader," Meredith turns frost into a figure for emotional atrophy. Those who resist love — the "miserably disinclined, / The lamentably unembraced" (003-154–155) — are described in terms of frozen life: "Their youth a frost, their age the dirge for youth" (003-153). The frost is not external cold but internal arrest: youth, which should be the season of warmth and flowering, has been frozen into sterility.

The parallel construction — "their youth a frost, their age the dirge" — links the two halves of a failed life. Because youth was frost (cold, unproductive, without flower), age becomes nothing more than mourning for what youth should have been. The frost is not merely a condition of one season but a blight that propagates forward, turning the entire life into elegy. Meredith's diagnosis is severe: to refuse love is to freeze oneself, and what freezes in youth produces nothing but grief in age.

### Philosophy's Warmth Against the Frost

In "Foresight and Patience," the frost appears in its philosophical dimension. Meredith's great hymn to Philosophy declares: "Philosophy, shall light us in the shade, / Warm in the frost, make Good our aim and aid" (016-271–272). Here the frost is the default condition of existence — the cold that surrounds human life in a universe that does not naturally provide warmth or meaning.

"Warm in the frost" is a striking formulation: Philosophy does not eliminate the frost, does not promise spring or dispel winter. It makes us warm *within* the frost, maintaining moral heat — "make Good our aim and aid" — despite conditions that would freeze lesser resolves. The frost becomes Meredith's figure for the indifferent universe, and human warmth becomes the achievement of conscious moral effort. This is not stoic endurance but active, philosophically-grounded resistance: to be warm in the frost is to be human at full stretch.
