## Overview

Ghost appears four times in Meredith's poetry, concentrated in chapters 4, 6, and 16, and each occurrence pulls the word in a different direction. In chapter 4, the ghost is the black adversary — a malevolent presence that prevails and rises, an internal enemy given external form (004-072, 004-077). In chapter 6, the ghostly is aestheticized — "ghostly bloom" on silken heights, the spectral made beautiful (006-019). And in chapter 16, the ghost appears in its most vernacular form — "giving up the ghost," the idiom of death that carries the weight of mortal fear (016-170). Between the adversary, the bloom, and the final breath, Meredith's ghost spans the range from active menace through aesthetic category to terminal surrender.

Unlike the phantom — which is insubstantial form without body — the ghost in Meredith carries agency. The black adversary's ghost does not merely appear; it prevails and rises. The ghostly bloom does not merely exist; it adorns the heights. The ghost given up is not merely lost; it is surrendered. Meredith's ghost is presence, not absence — the thing that comes when something else departs.

## In the Poems

### The Black Adversary's Ghost: The Enemy Within

Two of Meredith's four ghost occurrences appear in quick succession in chapter 4, and together they form a miniature narrative of confrontation and defeat. First, the ghost appears as a vision of triumph: "See a black adversary's ghost prevail" (004-072). The imperative "See" demands witness — the ghost's prevailing is something that must be looked at, not looked away from. The adversary is "black," a color that in Meredith's palette carries moral weight — not merely dark but opposed to light, not merely unknown but known to be hostile.

Four lines later, the ghost rises: "The ghost of his black adversary rose" (004-077). The rising completes the action that the prevailing initiated. The ghost is now vertical, active, occupying space that the self had claimed. The possessive "his" — the ghost of *his* black adversary — makes the adversary personal, internal, a property of the self that it cannot disown. The black adversary is not an external invader but a resident antagonist, and its ghost is the form it takes when it rises to assert dominion.

The two occurrences together map a sequence: the ghost prevails (it wins, it dominates, it establishes superiority) and then the ghost rises (it ascends, it occupies vertical space, it becomes the commanding presence). Between prevailing and rising, the self is displaced — the ghost of the adversary replaces the self as the occupant of psychic space.

### Ghostly Bloom: The Spectral Made Beautiful

Chapter 6 offers Meredith's only adjectival ghost: "The silken heights, of ghostly bloom" (006-019). The "ghostly" here is not menacing but aesthetic — a quality of bloom that is pale, delicate, otherworldly. The silken heights are elevated — vertical, aspirational — and their bloom is ghostly because it belongs to a register beyond the merely material. The ghostly bloom is the beauty of what is almost not there.

The word "silken" reinforces the delicacy: silk is smooth, fine, expensive, the fabric of luxury and refinement. The silken heights are not the rough peaks of granite but the elevated zones of refinement where experience takes on the quality of art. The ghostly bloom that adorns them is the flowering of a world made strange — beautiful precisely because it hovers at the edge of presence, refusing to commit to full existence.

### Giving Up the Ghost: The Fear of Death

The fourth ghost occurrence is the most bodily and the most idiomatic: "have much fear of giving up the ghost" (016-170). "Giving up the ghost" is the vernacular English phrase for dying — the ghost is the breath, the spirit, the animating principle that departs at the moment of death. To give it up is to surrender life, to release the hold on existence, to let the ghost go.

Meredith's line names not the act of giving up the ghost but the fear of it — "have much fear of giving up the ghost." The fear is the anticipatory emotion, the dread that precedes the act. The ghost here is the self considered as detachable — the part of the person that can be given up, that leaves the body when the body can no longer hold it. To fear giving up the ghost is to fear the moment when the self becomes separable, when what animated the frame departs and what remains is no longer a person.
