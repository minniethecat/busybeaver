[006-001] “THERE I dreamed, and I saw myself changing into a stag in dream, and

[006-002] I felt in dream the beating of a new heart within me, and in dream I

[006-003] arched my neck and braced my powerful limbs.

[006-004] “I awoke from the dream, and I was that which I had dreamed.

[006-005] “I stood a while stamping upon a rock, with my bristling head swung

[006-006] high, breathing through wide nostrils all the savour of the world. For

[006-007] I had come marvellously from decrepitude to strength. I had writhed from

[006-008] the bonds of age and was young again. I smelled the turf and knew for

[006-009] the first time how sweet that smelled. And like lightning my moving nose

[006-010] sniffed all things to my heart and separated them into knowledge.

[006-011] “Long I stood there, ringing my iron hoof on stone, and learning all

[006-012] things through my nose. Each breeze that came from the right hand or the

[006-013] left brought me a tale. A wind carried me the tang of wolf, and against

[006-014] that smell I stared and stamped. And on a wind there came the scent of

[006-015] my own kind, and at that I belled. Oh, loud and clear and sweet was the

[006-016] voice of the great stag. With what ease my lovely note went lilting.

[006-017] With what joy I heard the answering call. With what delight I bounded,

[006-018] bounded, bounded; light as a bird’s plume, powerful as a storm, untiring

[006-019] as the sea.

[006-020] “Here now was ease in ten-yard springings, with a swinging head, with

[006-021] the rise and fall of a swallow, with the curve and flow and urge of an

[006-022] otter of the sea. What a tingle dwelt about my heart! What a thrill spun

[006-023] to the lofty points of my antlers! How the world was new! How the sun

[006-024] was new! How the wind caressed me!

[006-025] “With unswerving forehead and steady eye I met all that came. The old,

[006-026] lone wolf leaped sideways, snarling, and slunk away. The lumbering bear

[006-027] swung his head of hesitations and thought again; he trotted his small

[006-028] red eye away with him to a near-by brake. The stags of my race fled from

[006-029] my rocky forehead, or were pushed back and back until their legs broke

[006-030] under them and I trampled them to death. I was the beloved, the well

[006-031] known, the leader of the herds of Ireland.

[006-032] “And at times I came back from my boundings about Eire’, for the strings

[006-033] of my heart were drawn to [[ulster|Ulster]]; and, standing away, my wide nose took

[006-034] the air, while I knew with joy, with terror, that men were blown on the

[006-035] wind. A proud head hung to the turf then, and the tears of memory rolled

[006-036] from a large, bright eye.

[006-037] “At times I drew near, delicately, standing among thick leaves or

[006-038] crouched in long grown grasses, and I stared and mourned as I looked on

[006-039] men. For Nemed and four couples had been saved from that fierce storm,

[006-040] and I saw them increase and multiply until four thousand couples lived

[006-041] and laughed and were riotous in the sun, for the people of Nemed had

[006-042] small minds but great activity. They were savage fighters and hunters.

[006-043] “But one time I came, drawn by that intolerable anguish of memory, and

[006-044] all of these people were gone: the place that knew them was silent: in

[006-045] the land where they had moved there was nothing of them but their bones

[006-046] that glinted in the sun.

[006-047] “Old age came on me there. Among these bones weariness crept into my

[006-048] limbs. My head grew heavy, my eyes dim, my knees jerked and trembled,

[006-049] and there the wolves dared chase me.

[006-050] “I went again to the cave that had been my home when I was an old man.

[006-051] “One day I stole from the cave to snatch a mouthful of grass, for I was

[006-052] closely besieged by wolves. They made their rush, and I barely escaped

[006-053] from them. They sat beyond the cave staring at me.

[006-054] “I knew their tongue. I knew all that they said to each other, and all

[006-055] that they said to me. But there was yet a thud left in my forehead, a

[006-056] deadly trample in my hoof. They did not dare come into the cave.

[006-057] “‘To-morrow,’ they said, ‘we will tear out your throat, and gnaw on your

[006-058] living haunch’.”
