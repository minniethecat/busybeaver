[049-001] “Cael, my pulse,” said the Carl, “we had better build a house or a hut

[049-002] to pass the night in.”

[049-003] “I’Il build nothing,” Cael replied, looking on the Carl with great

[049-004] disfavour.

[049-005] “No!”

[049-006] “I won’t build house or hut for the sake of passing one night here, for

[049-007] I hope never to see this place again.”

[049-008] “I’Il build a house myself,” said the Carl, “and the man who does not

[049-009] help in the building can stay outside of the house.”

[049-010] [[the-carl|The Carl]] stumped to a near-by wood, and he never rested until he had

[049-011] felled and tied together twenty-four couples of big timber. He thrust

[049-012] these under one arm and under the other he tucked a bundle of rushes for

[049-013] his bed, and with that one load he rushed up a house, well thatched and

[049-014] snug, and with the timber that remained over he made a bonfire on the

[049-015] floor of the house.

[049-016] His companion sat at a distance regarding the work with rage and

[049-017] aversion.

[049-018] “Now Cael, my darling,” said the Carl, “if you are a man help me to look

[049-019] for something to eat, for there is game here.”

[049-020] “Help yourself,” roared Cael, “for all that I want is not to be near

[049-021] you.”

[049-022] “The tooth that does not help gets no helping,” the other replied.

[049-023] In a short time the Carl returned with a wild boar which he had run

[049-024] down. He cooked the beast over his bonfire and ate one half of it,

[049-025] leaving the other half for his breakfast. Then he lay down on the

[049-026] rushes, and in two turns he fell asleep.

[049-027] But Cael lay out on the side of the hill, and if he went to sleep that

[049-028] night he slept fasting. It was he, however, who awakened the Carl in the

[049-029] morning.

[049-030] “Get up, beggarman, if you are going to run against me.”

[049-031] The Carl rubbed his eyes.

[049-032] “I never get up until I have had my fill of sleep, and there is another

[049-033] hour of it due to me. But if you are in a hurry, my delight, you can

[049-034] start running now with a blessing. I will trot on your track when I

[049-035] waken up.”

[049-036] Cael began to race then, and he was glad of the start, for his

[049-037] antagonist made so little account of him that he did not know what to

[049-038] expect when the Carl would begin to run.

[049-039] “Yet,” said Cael to himself, “with an hour’s start the beggarman will

[049-040] have to move his bones if he wants to catch on me,” and he settled down

[049-041] to a good, pelting race.
