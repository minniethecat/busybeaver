## Overview

Conan mac Morna — called [[conan-the-swearer|Cona'n the Swearer]] and Cona'n Mael (Bald Cona'n) — is the brother of [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] and one of the most memorable supporting characters in the Fenian tales. Bald, bearded, and possessed of an acid tongue and ever-ready fists, Cona'n is a perpetual irritant and an indomitable warrior. His verbal provocations at Fionn's banquet at [[allen|Allen]] spark the chain of insults that becomes the Brawl, and he stands beside his brother Goll throughout the tale's aftermath.

## Identity

Cona'n is of [[clann-morna|Clann Morna]], brother to [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] and to Art Og mac Morna [044-083]. His epithets — "the Swearer" and "Mael" (the Bald) — reflect his two most prominent features: a readiness to swear oaths and a shining bald head that he deploys as a weapon of provocation [044-082]. Stephens describes him as "gruff as a badger" with a "beard that poked all around his face" [043-010]–[043-011]. He is blunt, argumentative, and utterly fearless.

## In the Tales

### The Little Brawl at Allen

Cona'n's role in the Brawl is pivotal. It is his needling of Cairell Whiteskin — Fionn's brother — that turns words into blows. When Cairell tries to argue that Fionn had the rights of the Brawl dispute, Cona'n points out amiably, "He had eleven hundred killed, and you may call that the rights of it if you like" [043-003]–[043-004]. When Cairell protests it was Cona'n who started it, Cona'n deflects: "No, for you hit me first" [043-008].

The confrontation escalates at the banquet hall when Goll's brother turns "a savage eye" on Cairell and declares that the least of Goll's hundred-and-one men could put Cairell down easily enough [044-062]–[044-066]. When Cairell calls him "old scaldhead," Cona'n promises to "prove on your hide" his point, and on the instant Cairell looses a furious buffet which Cona'n returns with "a fist so big that every part of Cairell's face was hit with the one blow" [044-070]–[044-074].

The two then grapple, and soon Cona'n is attacked by [[oscar|Oscar]]'s sons, then [[oscar|Oscar]] himself. When [[oscar|Oscar]] enters, Cona'n greets him grimly: "I thank the gods for the chance of killing yourself, [[oscar|Oscar]]" [044-081]. But [[oscar|Oscar]] proves the stronger: [[oscar|Oscar]] "knocked a groan of distress out of Cona'n," who must look "appealingly at his brother Art og mac Morna" for rescue [044-082]–[044-083]. Art Og wounds [[oscar|Oscar]], drawing Oisin into the fray [044-084].

At the [[tara|Tara]] judgement, it is established beyond dispute that "Fionn's brother Cairell struck Cona'n mac Morna" first, precipitating the battle [045-021]–[045-022].

### The Carl of the Drab Coat

Cona'n appears alongside Fionn when the mysterious Carl arrives. When the Carl makes his extraordinary boasts, Cona'n mutters, "It is no small boast," staring at him [carl-ch01, PN=057-017]. He later counsels pragmatic caution: "Let them wear whiskers or not wear them. But let us look at them no more than we can help." When danger looms, Cona'n self-preservingly explains, "I am not frightened. I only want to keep my good health."

### The Enchanted Cave of Cesh Corran

Cona'n accompanies Fionn and a small party to investigate the cave of [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]]. When the party encounters the daughters of [[conaran|Conaran]] the druid, Cona'n is behind Fionn as they advance. When the supernatural trap is sprung, Cona'n too is ensnared, crying out "What's wrong at all?" as he tumbles. Fionn calls to his comrade, "Cona'n, my dear love, give the warning whistle so that the [[fianna|Fianna]] will know we are in danger." After [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] arrives to rescue them, Cona'n and the others are released.

## Significance

Cona'n mac Morna is the archetypal provocateur — the blunt, bald veteran who speaks his mind regardless of consequence and fights with the same lack of restraint. His role in the [[brawl-at-allen|Brawl at Allen]] is structurally essential: without his sharp rejoinders and refusal to back down, the banquet would have remained a diplomatic occasion. He is the comic foil to his brother Goll's grandeur — where Goll is magnificent and generous, Cona'n is grumbling and combative — but he is loyal to his clan and to his brother through every fight. The affection between the two is unmistakable: Cona'n fights beside Goll, and Goll watches over him as he does all his people.
