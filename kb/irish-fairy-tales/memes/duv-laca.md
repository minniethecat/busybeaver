## Overview

Duv Laca — called Duv Laca of the White Hand — is the wife of [[mongan|Mongan mac Fiachna]], the prince of [[ulster|Ulster]]. Unlike many women in Irish myth who are prizes or passive figures, Duv Laca is a fully realized character: sharp-witted, self-assured, and unafraid to claim her own desires. Her name is the one [[mongan|Mongan]] speaks "oftener than the others" [071-032], and his love for her — and her later abduction by [[branduv|Branduv]], King of [[leinster|Leinster]] — forms the emotional engine of "[[mongan|Mongan's Frenzy]]."

## Identity

Duv Laca is the daughter of [[fiachna-finn|Fiachna Finn]], a powerful lord, and was given in marriage to [[mongan|Mongan mac Fiachna]] [077-021]. Nothing in the text suggests she is other than human, though her name — "the White Hand" — carries the aura of royalty. She keeps a foster-sister as her young attendant [082-023]. She is a queen in her own right: when [[mongan|Mongan]] speaks of her, he describes her as "the most beautiful woman in Ireland" [079-044]. Duv Laca is discerning, direct, and not easily fooled — she challenges [[mongan|Mongan]] when he conceals things, and she negotiates her own terms with the King of [[leinster|Leinster]].

## In the Tales

### Mongan's Frenzy

#### The Chess Games

Duv Laca appears prominently in "[[mongan|Mongan's Frenzy]]," where she spends her days playing chess with [[mongan|Mongan]] in their palace. The picture painted is one of domestic intimacy: "One day [[mongan|Mongan]] and Duv Laca were playing chess in their palace" [078-001]. But [[mongan|Mongan]] notices that she seems discontented [078-003], and it is then that a small black-faced cleric appears over her shoulder and delivers his prophecy about cows and kings [078-004].

When the King of [[leinster|Leinster]] arrives, Duv Laca is the first to go to the window and identify the visitor: "The noise must announce the visit of a king" [080-014]. She urges hospitality: "Let us go out and welcome him properly" [080-021]. But she soon realizes the king's purpose. At the feast, when the King of [[leinster|Leinster]] openly declares "I want Duv Laca," she is the one who must navigate the consequences [080-].

#### The Bargain

[[mongan|Mongan]], bound by his word to repay the King of [[leinster|Leinster]] for a gift, must consent to [[branduv|Branduv]] taking Duv Laca away. Duv Laca does not weep or rage. Instead, she negotiates her terms directly with [[branduv|Branduv]]:

> "And I," said Duv Laca, "am the happiest woman in the world." [081-009]
>
> "In love with you," replied Duv Laca. [081-018]
>
> "But, by my word," said Duv Laca, "I will not go with you unless you give me your pledge…" [081-020]

She binds the King of [[leinster|Leinster]] by oath to grant her every request — a shrewd political move that preserves her agency even as she changes husbands [080-050]. She also secures the protection of her foster-sister and attendant, showing her care for those who serve her.

#### The Recovery

When [[mongan|Mongan]] — disguised as the cleric Tibraide — finally reaches Duv Laca in [[leinster|Leinster]], she immediately recognizes him as her husband. When [[branduv|Branduv]] confronts her about letting another man near her, Duv Laca replies with magnificent frankness: "he is my own husband" [086-046]. Her loyalty to [[mongan|Mongan]] is unwavering despite the circumstances, and she returns to [[ulster|Ulster]] with him behind her on the horse.

## Significance

Duv Laca of the White Hand is distinctive among the women of Irish myth for her active agency. She is neither the passive prize nor the otherworldly enchantress. She plays chess with her husband as an equal, speaks her mind when she discerns deception, negotiates binding oaths from kings, and maintains her loyalty through circumstances beyond her control. In a tale populated by shape-changers, druid-enchanters, and warrior-kings, Duv Laca stands as the most grounded presence — the human heart whose love drives the story's stakes and whose wit ensures its resolution.
