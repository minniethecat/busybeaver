## Overview

Fionn mac Uail (also known as Finn mac Cumhaill) is the paramount hero of the Fenian Cycle and the most frequently mentioned character in Irish mythology. Across James Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales*, he appears in eight of the ten tales, serving as the unifying thread of Irish heroic narrative — a hunter, warrior, poet, and wise leader whose story spans from hidden childhood to the captaincy of the [[fianna|Fianna]].

## Identity

Fionn is the son of Uail (also called Cumhall), former captain of the [[fianna|Fianna of Ireland]], and [[muirne|Muirne]], a woman of [[faery|Faery]] lineage — her brother was [[lugh|Lugh of the Long Hand]] himself [012-017]. Through his mother, Fionn descends from the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], giving him a dual heritage of mortal heroism and supernatural power.

His original name was Deimne, but he acquired the name "Fionn" (meaning "the Fair One") from his foster-brothers for his light-colored hair and fair appearance [019-029]–[019-030]. This epithet is preserved in the Latinized form "Finn" used in many English retellings.

After his father Uail was killed by the clann-Morna in their bid to seize control of the [[fianna|Fianna]] [012-009]–[012-013], Fionn's mother [[muirne|Muirne]] placed him in the care of two women [[druid|druids]], [[bovmall|Bovmall]] and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]], who raised him in secret in the [[slieve-bloom|woods of Slieve Bloom]] [012-023]–[012-026].

## In the Tales

### The Boyhood of Fionn

Fionn's early life was shaped by concealment and training. Hidden from his father's enemies, he grew up in the forest under the tutelage of [[bovmall|Bovmall]] and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]], absorbing the wild knowledge of birds, beasts, and the changing seasons [012-035]–[012-053]. He later studied with the poet [[finegas|Finegas]] on the banks of the [[boyne|River Boyne]], where fate granted him the greatest gift of Irish myth: [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge.

[[finegas|Finegas]] had spent seven years seeking this legendary fish, whose flesh conferred all wisdom upon the eater. When Fionn — then still called Deimne — was tasked with cooking the salmon, he burned his thumb on its skin and instinctively put it in his mouth, unknowingly receiving the wisdom intended for his master [021-053]–[021-071]. [[finegas|Finegas]], recognizing destiny, then gave Fionn the entire salmon to eat, prophesying that he would become the greatest leader in Ireland [021-085]–[021-101].

Fionn's prowess was evident early: he surpassed all other youths at swimming, running, and combat [019-024]–[019-041], and served as a hunter for the King of Finntraigh — a kingdom, the text noted, that may have been named after Fionn himself [019-047]. His hunting skill bordered on the supernatural; "the deer that Fionn got the track of did not get away" [019-054]–[019-055].

### Later Adventures

As captain of the [[fianna|Fianna]], Fionn appears throughout the tales as the wise arbiter of disputes, the leader of the hunt, and the protector of Ireland against supernatural threats. In *The [[cesh-corran|Enchanted Cave of Cesh Corran]]*, he is entrapped by the daughters of [[conaran|Conaran]] but maintains his characteristic composure. In *The [[brawl-at-allen|Little Brawl at Allen]]*, he mediates the conflict between the clans. In *[[mongan|Mongan's Frenzy]]*, his role as the reincarnated figure of ancient heroism drives much of the narrative's mythic resonance.

## Significance

Fionn mac Uail is the most important character of the Fenian Cycle and the connective tissue of Irish mythological narrative. His story arc — from hidden child to wise leader — embodies the Irish heroic ideal: not merely strength in arms, but wisdom won through experience and, crucially, through the supernatural gift of [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge. The 1920 James Stephens retelling preserves Fionn's complexity: a figure who is at once divinely descended, mythically wise, and profoundly human in his pride, his loyalties, and his limitations.
