## Identity

Saeve is a woman of [[faery|Faery]] who became the beloved of [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] and the mother of [[oisin|Oisin]], the great poet-warrior of the [[fianna|Fianna]]. Her story is one of the most poignant in the Fenian Cycle: a love won, a supernatural deception, and a mother lost to enchantment, reunited with her child only through Fionn's grief-stricken search across seven years.

Saeve introduces herself plainly: "My name is Saeve, and I am a woman of Faery" [032-032]. She is of the [[sidhe-mound|Shi']], the supernatural realm closest to the mortal world, and her unearthly beauty marks her as belonging to that other sphere. Fionn, seeing her for the first time, describes her inwardly as "the Sky-woman of the Dawn," "the light on the foam," and "white and odorous as an apple-blossom" [032-010]–[032-011].

Her epithet, "the Flower of Allen," ties her to [[wide-allen-of-leinster|Allen of Leinster]], Fionn's great stronghold [033-072]. The name is used by Fionn's household and by the narrator alike, encoding both her beauty and her brief but radiant presence at the heart of the [[fianna|Fianna]].

## In the Tales

### Arrival at Allen

Saeve's story spans the tale *[[oisin|Oisin's Mother]]*. She first appears late at night, when the door of Fionn's chamber "opened gently and a young woman came into the room" [032-002]. She seeks Fionn's protection against the [[fear-doirche|Fear Doirche]], the Dark Man of the Shi', a [[faery|Faery]] magician who has pursued her relentlessly: "He is everywhere," she whispers; "He is in the bushes, and on the hill. He looked up at me from the water, and he stared down on me from the sky" [032-047]–[032-049].

Saeve tells Fionn that while many men of the Shi' gave her their love, she gave her love to no man of her own country — but to a mortal champion of Ireland, a man known to Fionn [032-037]–[032-041]. The champion she speaks of is Fionn himself, and when she reveals this, Fionn responds with immediate, overwhelming love [032-068]–[032-071].

### Life with Fionn

Fionn's devotion to Saeve is absolute. The text states plainly: "Fionn loved Saeve as he had not loved a woman before and would never love one again" [032-071]–[032-072]. Her voice is sweeter to him than the singing of a lark [032-082]–[032-083]; "there was magic in the tips of her fingers" [032-083]–[032-084]; and she fills him with "wonder and surmise" [032-083]. Fionn abandons the hunt and the songs of poets, for "all of these were in his wife, and something that was beyond these was in her also" [032-090]–[032-092].

### The Deception and Transformation

While Fionn is away fighting the [[lochlann|Lochlannachs]] (Northmen) who have invaded Ireland, the [[fear-doirche|Fear Doirche]] enacts his revenge. Saeve, waiting at the Dun of [[wide-allen-of-leinster|Allen]], sees a figure approaching that bears Fionn's exact shape, armour, and face, accompanied by [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran and Sceolan]], Fionn's hounds [034-007]–[034-008]. She runs from the keep to meet what she believes is her returning husband, crying: "Let me go to meet my husband, the father of the child that is not born" [034-021]–[034-022] — revealing she is pregnant with Fionn's child.

When she reaches the false Fionn, "the figure lifted its hand. It touched her with a hazel rod, and, while we looked, she disappeared, and where she had been there was a fawn standing and shivering" [034-029]–[034-030]. The [[hazel-rod|hazel rod]] — the instrument of the Dark Magician's [[enchantment|enchantment]] — transforms Saeve into a deer. The hounds seize her by the throat and drag her back, and she vanishes with the false Fionn and the dogs [034-039]–[034-042].

### The Seven-Year Search and Oisin

Fionn, upon his return, is devastated. "For many years" he searched "through the length and breadth of the country in the hope that he might again chance on his lovely lady from the Shi'" [035-001]–[035-004]. He "slept in misery each night and he rose each day to grief" [035-004]–[035-005]. He used only his most trusted hounds — [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran, Sceolan]], and three others — for "if a fawn was chased each of these five great dogs would know if that was a fawn to be killed or one to be protected" [035-007]–[035-009].

After seven years, while hunting on [[ben-gulbain|Ben Gulbain]], the five hounds are found protecting a wild, naked boy with long, beautiful hair [035-029]–[035-032]. Fionn recognizes in the child's face "the very eye of Saeve" [035-055], and his grief is replaced by joy. The bond between father and son is immediate: "where before he could not be parted from Saeve, so now he could not be parted from Oisin" [036-001]–[036-002].

The boy — [[oisin|Oisin]] — later tells his story: he lived in a cliff-walled valley of the [[faery|Shi']] with a deer that loved him and that he loved [037-010]–[037-013]. A "dark stern man" — the [[fear-doirche|Fear Doirche]] — came repeatedly, speaking "gently and angrily" to the deer, until at last he struck her with a hazel rod and forced her to follow him away [037-015]–[037-026]. The deer, weeping, looked back at her son until she was gone. The boy woke in the mortal world, on the mountain, among the hounds [037-030]–[037-031].

Thus Saeve's fate is left unresolved — still a deer, still in the power of the Dark Magician, but her son delivered into Fionn's hands.

## Significance

Saeve's story bridges the mortal and Faery worlds through the most intimate of bonds: love, pregnancy, and maternal sacrifice. Her transformation by the hazel rod is a classic motif of Irish mythology — the weaponization of shape-changing against the vulnerable — and her son [[oisin|Oisin]] ("Little Fawn") carries her faery heritage into his own destiny as the greatest poet of the [[fianna|Fianna]] and the teller of these very tales [037-032]–[037-036]. The epithet "Flower of Allen" captures both the beauty and the brevity of her presence: she bloomed at the heart of Fionn's world and was taken as suddenly as she appeared.

### The Faery-Woman Motif

Saeve belongs to a distinctive tradition in Irish mythology: the supernatural woman who crosses from the Otherworld to love a mortal hero, bringing both blessing and tragedy. Her self-introduction — "my name is Saeve, and I am a woman of Faery" [032-032] — declares her otherworldly origin without apology, and her choice of a mortal lover over the men of her own country [032-037]–[032-041] repeats a pattern found across the mythological and Fenian cycles. Unlike the more assertive [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]], however, Saeve is a figure of vulnerability: pursued, transformed, and ultimately lost, she embodies the fragility of faery-mortal unions when threatened by the darker powers of the [[sidhe-mound]] world. Her story's lack of resolution — she is never restored to human form — sets it apart from tales where enchantment is broken by love or heroism, marking it instead as a meditation on irreversible loss and the cost of crossing between worlds.
