[073-001] There is a difference between this world and the world of [[faery|Faery]], but it

[073-002] is not immediately perceptible. Everything that is here is there, but

[073-003] the things that are there are better than those that are here. All

[073-004] things that are bright are there brighter. There is more gold in the

[073-005] sun and more silver in the moon of that land. There is more scent in the

[073-006] flowers, more savour in the fruit. There is more comeliness in the men

[073-007] and more tenderness in the women. Everything in Faery is better by this

[073-008] one wonderful degree, and it is by this betterness you will know that

[073-009] you are there if you should ever happen to get there.

[073-010] [[mongan|Mongan]] and his companions stepped from the world of storm into sunshine

[073-011] and a scented world. The instant they stepped they stood, bewildered,

[073-012] looking at each other silently, questioningly, and then with one accord

[073-013] they turned to look back whence they had come.

[073-014] There was no storm behind them. The sunlight drowsed there as it did in

[073-015] front, a peaceful flooding of living gold. They saw the shapes of

[073-016] the country to which their eyes were accustomed, and recognised the

[073-017] well-known landmarks, but it seemed that the distant hills were a trifle

[073-018] higher, and the grass which clothed them and stretched between was

[073-019] greener, was more velvety: that the trees were better clothed and had

[073-020] more of peace as they hung over the quiet ground.

[073-021] But Mongan knew what had happened, and he smiled with glee as he watched

[073-022] his astonished companions, and he sniffed that balmy air as one whose

[073-023] nostrils remembered it.

[073-024] “You had better come with me,” he said.

[073-025] “Where are we?” his wife asked. “Why, we are here,” cried Mongan; “where

[073-026] else should we be?”

[073-027] He set off then, and the others followed, staring about them cautiously,

[073-028] and each man keeping a hand on the hilt of his sword.

[073-029] “Are we in Faery?” the Flame Lady asked.

[073-030] “We are,” said Mongan.

[073-031] When they had gone a little distance they came to a grove of ancient

[073-032] trees. Mightily tail and well grown these trees were, and the trunk of

[073-033] each could not have been spanned by ten broad men. As they went among

[073-034] these quiet giants into the dappled obscurity and silence, their

[073-035] thoughts became grave, and all the motions of their minds elevated

[073-036] as though they must equal in greatness and dignity those ancient and

[073-037] glorious trees. When they passed through the grove they saw a lovely

[073-038] house before them, built of mellow wood and with a roof of bronze--it

[073-039] was like the dwelling of a king, and over the windows of the Sunny Room

[073-040] there was a balcony. There were ladies on this balcony, and when they

[073-041] saw the travellers approaching they sent messengers to welcome them.

[073-042] Mongan and his companions were then brought into the house, and all was

[073-043] done for them that could be done for honoured guests. Everything within

[073-044] the house was as excellent as all without, and it was inhabited by seven

[073-045] men and seven women, and it was evident that Mongan and these people

[073-046] were well acquainted.

[073-047] In the evening a feast was prepared, and when they had eaten well there

[073-048] was a banquet. There were seven vats of wine, and as Mongan loved wine

[073-049] he was very happy, and he drank more on that occasion than any one had

[073-050] ever noticed him to drink before.

[073-051] It was while he was in this condition of glee and expansion that the

[073-052] Flame Lady put her arms about his neck and begged he would tell her the

[073-053] story of [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]], and, being boisterous then and full of good spirits,

[073-054] he agreed to her request, and he prepared to tell the tale.

[073-055] The seven men and seven women of the Fairy Palace then took their

[073-056] places about him in a half-circle; his own seven guards sat behind

[073-057] them; his wife, the Flame Lady, sat by his side; and at the back of

[073-058] all Cairid, his story-teller sat, listening with all his ears, and

[073-059] remembering every word that was uttered.
