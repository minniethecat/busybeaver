## Overview

Finegas is the druid, poet, and sage who teaches the young [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] on the banks of the [[boyne|River Boyne]]. He is the central figure of one of the most famous episodes in Irish mythology: the catching of [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge. Finegas has spent seven years fishing for this salmon, which has eaten the nuts of the nine hazel trees of wisdom that grew around the [[well-of-segais|Well of Segais]]. When he finally catches it, he gives it to Fionn to cook — and through Fionn's accidental taste of the cooking fish, the boy receives the gift of prophetic knowledge that was meant for his master.

## Identity

Finegas lives "on a bank of [[boyne|the Boyne]] Water" [020-004], where he has made his home a place of learning and contemplation. He is a druid — a combination of priest, scholar, and poet — and the text describes his teaching as both practical and spiritual. Fionn "already venerated Finegas" [020-048], and the affection between teacher and pupil is genuine and deep. When his pupil shows understanding beyond his years, "Finegas beamed on his pupil" [020-027].

Finegas's defining characteristic is his long patience. He has fished [[boyne|the Boyne]] for [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge for seven years, sustained by a prophecy: "A prophecy was made to me. I was told that I should catch [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge, and that I should live to eat it" [020-030]. His dedication is absolute — until the moment of greatest irony, when the salmon he has waited a lifetime for is consumed not by himself but by his pupil.

## In the Tales

### The Boyhood of Fionn

#### The Teacher

Fionn comes to Finegas in his youth, seeking education. The druid accepts him and teaches him everything he knows. Finegas's relationship with Fionn is characterized by mutual respect and genuine warmth. When Fionn asks a profound question about [[bardic-tradition|poetry]], Finegas smiles: "A weighty question. I could answer it if I had time" [020-038]. He speaks to the boy not as a master to a servant but as one mind to another.

Finegas holds a deep reverence for knowledge. "Let us pray for that," he says fervently when discussing the highest understanding [020-061]. His passion for learning infects Fionn, who pursues "Knowledge as eagerly as his master did" [020-048].

#### The Salmon of Knowledge

One day, Finegas comes to the river bank in a state of great excitement. "It is The Salmon," he says "with a great sigh" [021-036]. After seven years of patient waiting, the prophecy has been fulfilled: he has caught the fabled fish. He entrusts the cooking to Fionn with a strict warning not to eat any of it.

Fionn cooks the salmon carefully, turning it on a spit. At one point a blister rises on the fish's skin, and Fionn presses it down with his thumb. He burns his thumb and instinctively puts it in his mouth — and in that instant, the knowledge of the world flows into him.

When Finegas returns, he looks at Fionn and immediately knows what has happened. It is the moment that would break a lesser man — the knowledge meant for him, the prophecy's fulfillment stolen by accident. Finegas's reaction is remarkable:

> "So," said Finegas, "you did not eat it on me after all?" [021-061]
>
> "Did I not?" said Fionn. [021-061]
>
> "It was given to you," Finegas answered. "It was given to Fionn, the son of Uail. You shall have all the knowledge that was gathered for me. Why should you not take it?" [021-094]

Finegas then explains the gift: Fionn need only put his thumb between his teeth, and whatever knowledge he seeks will come to him. There is no bitterness in Finegas's voice — only acceptance that destiny has chosen differently than prophecy foretold. "He had received all that he could get from Finegas," the text concludes. "His education was complete" [022-001].

## Significance

Finegas embodies a rare and noble archetype: the teacher who accepts that the gift he prepared for himself has passed to his pupil. His seven years of patient fishing — and his gracious surrender when the knowledge flows to Fionn instead — mark him as one of the most selfless figures in Irish myth. Without Finegas, there would be no Fionn of the prophetic thumb, no Fenian Cycle as we know it. He is the quiet vessel through which destiny flows, asking for nothing and receiving, in the end, what he most values: the success of his pupil.
