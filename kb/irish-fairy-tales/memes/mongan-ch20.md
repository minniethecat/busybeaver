[089-001] The doorkeeper brought word to the King of [[leinster|Leinster]] that the son of the

[089-002] King of [[connacht|Connaught]], Ae the Beautiful, and his wife, Ivell of the Shining

[089-003] Cheeks, were at the door, that they had been banished from Connaught

[089-004] by Ae’s father, and they were seeking the protection of the King of

[089-005] Leinster.

[089-006] [[branduv|Branduv]] came to the door himself to welcome them, and the minute he

[089-007] looked on [[ivell-of-the-shining-cheeks|Ivell of the Shining Cheeks]] it was plain that he liked looking

[089-008] at her.

[089-009] It was now drawing towards evening, and a feast was prepared for the

[089-010] guests with a banquet to follow it. At the feast [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]] sat beside the

[089-011] King of Leinster, but [[mongan|Mongan]] sat opposite him with Ivell, and Mongan put

[089-012] more and more [[enchantment|magic]] into the hag, so that her cheeks shone and her eyes

[089-013] gleamed, and she was utterly bewitching to the eye; and when Branduv

[089-014] looked at her she seemed to grow more and more lovely and more and more

[089-015] desirable, and at last there was not a bone in his body as big as an

[089-016] inch that was not filled with love and longing for the girl.

[089-017] Every few minutes he gave a great sigh as if he had eaten too much, and

[089-018] when Duv Laca asked him if he had eaten too much he said he had but

[089-019] that he had not drunk enough, and by that he meant that he had not drunk

[089-020] enough from the eyes of the girl before him.

[089-021] At the banquet which was then held he looked at her again, and every

[089-022] time he took a drink he toasted Ivell across the brim of his goblet, and

[089-023] in a little while she began to toast him back across the rim of her

[089-024] cup, for he was drinking ale, but she was drinking mead. Then he sent a

[089-025] messenger to her to say that it was a far better thing to be the wife

[089-026] of the King of Leinster than to be the wife of the son of the King of

[089-027] Connaught, for a king is better than a prince, and Ivell thought that

[089-028] this was as wise a thing as anybody had ever said. And then he sent a

[089-029] message to say that he loved her so much that he would certainly burst

[089-030] of love if it did not stop.

[089-031] Mongan heard the whispering, and he told the hag that if she did what he

[089-032] advised she would certainly get either himself or the King of Leinster

[089-033] for a husband.

[089-034] “Either of you will be welcome,” said the hag.

[089-035] “When the king says he loves you, ask him to prove it by gifts; ask for

[089-036] his drinking-horn first.”

[089-037] She asked for that, and he sent it to her filled with good liquor; then

[089-038] she asked for his girdle, and he sent her that.

[089-039] His people argued with him and said it was not right that he should give

[089-040] away the treasures of Leinster to the wife of the King of Connaught’s

[089-041] son; but he said that it did not matter, for when he got the girl he

[089-042] would get his treasures with her. But every time he sent anything to the

[089-043] hag, mac an Da’v snatched it out of her lap and put it in his pocket.

[089-044] “Now,” said Mongan to the hag, “tell the servant to say that you would

[089-045] not leave your own husband for all the wealth of the world.”

[089-046] She told the servant that, and the servant told it to the king. When

[089-047] Branduv heard it he nearly went mad with love and longing and jealousy,

[089-048] and with rage also, because of the treasure he had given her and might

[089-049] not get back. He called Mongan over to him, and spoke to him very

[089-050] threateningly and ragingly.

[089-051] “I am not one who takes a thing without giving a thing,” said he.

[089-052] “Nobody could say you were,” agreed Mongan.

[089-053] “Do you see this woman sitting beside me?” he continued, pointing to Duv

[089-054] Laca.

[089-055] “I do indeed,” said Mongan.

[089-056] “Well,” said Branduv, “this woman is Duv Laca of the White Hand that I

[089-057] took away from Mongan; she is just going to marry me, but if you will

[089-058] make an exchange, you can marry this Duv Laca here, and I will marry

[089-059] that Ivell of the Shining Cheeks yonder.”

[089-060] Mongan pretended to be very angry then.

[089-061] “If I had come here with horses and treasure you would be in your right

[089-062] to take these from me, but you have no right to ask for what you are now

[089-063] asking.”

[089-064] “I do ask for it,” said Branduv menacingly, “and you must not refuse a

[089-065] lord.”

[089-066] “Very well,” said Mongan reluctantly, and as if in great fear; “if you

[089-067] will make the exchange I will make it, although it breaks my heart.”

[089-068] He brought Ivell over to the king then and gave her three kisses.

[089-069] “The king would suspect something if I did not kiss you,” said he, and

[089-070] then he gave the hag over to the king. After that they all got drunk and

[089-071] merry, and soon there was a great snoring and snorting, and very soon

[089-072] all the servants fell asleep also, so that Mongan could not get anything

[089-073] to drink. Mac an Da’v said it was a great shame, and he kicked some of

[089-074] the servants, but they did not budge, and then he slipped out to the

[089-075] stables and saddled two mares. He got on one with his wife behind him

[089-076] and Mongan got on the other with Duv Laca behind him, and they rode away

[089-077] towards [[ulster|Ulster]] like the wind, singing this song: The King of Leinster

[089-078] was married to-day, Married to-day, married to-day, The King of Leinster

[089-079] was married to-day, And every one wishes him joy.

[089-080] In the morning the servants came to waken the King of Leinster, and when

[089-081] they saw the face of the hag lying on the pillow beside the king, and

[089-082] her nose all covered with whiskers, and her big foot and little foot

[089-083] sticking away out at the end of the bed, they began to laugh, and poke

[089-084] one another in the stomachs and thump one another on the shoulders, so

[089-085] that the noise awakened the king, and he asked what was the matter with

[089-086] them at all. It was then he saw the hag lying beside him, and he gave a

[089-087] great screech and jumped out of the bed.

[089-088] “Aren’t you the Hag of the Mill?” said he.

[089-089] “I am indeed,” she replied, “and I love you dearly.”

[089-090] “I wish I didn’t see you,” said Branduv.

[089-091] That was the end of the story, and when he had told it Mongan began to

[089-092] laugh uproariously and called for more wine. He drank this deeply, as

[089-093] though he was full of thirst and despair and a wild jollity, but when

[089-094] the Flame Lady began to weep he took her in his arms and caressed her,

[089-095] and said that she was the love of his heart and the one treasure of the

[089-096] world.

[089-097] After that they feasted in great contentment, and at the end of the

[089-098] feasting they went away from [[faery|Faery]] and returned to the world of men.

[089-099] They came to Mongan’s palace at Moy Linney, and it was not until they

[089-100] reached the palace that they found they had been away one whole year,

[089-101] for they had thought they were only away one night. They lived

[089-102] then peacefully and lovingly together, and that ends the story, but

[089-103] Bro’tiarna did not know that Mongan was Fionn.

[089-104] The abbot leaned forward.

[089-105] “Was Mongan Fionn?” he asked in a whisper.

[089-106] “He was,” replied [[cairide|Cairide]]’.

[089-107] “Indeed, indeed!” said the abbot.

[089-108] After a while he continued: “There is only one part of your story that I

[089-109] do not like.”

[089-110] “What part is that?” asked Cairide’.

[089-111] “It is the part where the holy man Tibraide’ was ill treated by that

[089-112] rap--by that--by Mongan.”

[089-113] Cairide’ agreed that it was ill done, but to himself he said gleefully

[089-114] that whenever he was asked to tell the story of how he told the story of

[089-115] Mongan he would remember what the abbot said.
