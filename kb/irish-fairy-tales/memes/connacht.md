# Connacht

## Overview

Connacht is the western province of Ireland, the homeland of the fierce Connacht warriors who formed the backbone of Goll Mac Morna's faction within the [[fianna|Fianna]]. In Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales*, Connachtmen are characterized by their wild, untameable nature, and the province is also the site of one of Ireland's three great pre-Christian burial-mounds — the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] of Cruachan Ahi.

## Location

- **Region**: Connacht (western Ireland)
- **Modern equivalent**: The province of Connacht comprises five counties in western Ireland — Galway, Mayo, Roscommon, Sligo, and Leitrim
- **Type of place**: Ancient province and kingdom, containing [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] mounds, royal gathering sites, and rugged western landscapes

## In the Tales

Connacht appears in "The Boyhood of Fionn" through the figure of Goll Mac Morna and his followers. Stephens describes Goll as "the huge-shouldered, stern-eyed, violent Connachtman" [014-007], using the provincial identity as shorthand for a particular kind of fierce, unbending warrior. The Connachtmen under Goll's command are described collectively as "wild Connachtmen all, as untameable, as unaccountable" [014-020], reinforcing the province's reputation for producing warriors of fierce independence.

In "[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]," Connacht is named among the three great burial-places of Ireland: "the Shi' mound of Cruachan Ahi, where [[ethal-anbual|Ethal Anbual]] presides over the underworld of Connacht" [061-015]. Cruachan Ahi (modern Rathcroghan) was the royal seat of Connacht, and its [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] mound is described as a portal to the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]], presided over by [[ethal-anbual|Ethal Anbual]], a [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] deity.

Connacht is also listed alongside the other provinces in the enumeration of Ireland's sacred and royal geography, appearing between [[ulster|Ulster]]'s [[boyne|Boyne]] and "Tailltin, in [[meath|Royal Meath]]" [061-013]–[061-018].

## Mythological Significance

Connacht's association with the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] and the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] is a key element of its mythological identity. Cruachan Ahi, the ancient capital, was believed to contain a passage to the underworld, and its [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] mound was one of the most important supernatural sites in Ireland.

The province's reputation for producing fierce warriors — the "wild Connachtmen" — aligns with the historical Connacht of Irish legend, particularly the Connacht of Queen Medb in the [[ulster|Ulster]] Cycle. While Stephens' tales focus on Fenian [[rath]]er than [[ulster|Ulster]] Cycle material, the characterization of Connachtmen as "untameable" and "unaccountable" carries forward the province's legendary association with martial ferocity.
