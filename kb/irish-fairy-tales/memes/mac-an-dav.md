# Mac an Dáv

## Overview

Mac an Dáv was a stout-hearted servant in the household of [[mongan|Mongan]], the prince of [[ulster|Ulster]]. He appears in *[[mongan|Mongan's Frenzy]]* as [[mongan|Mongan]]'s loyal companion, carrying the prince on his back on a long and uneasy journey, and speaking with a plain, sometimes comic directness that marks him as a distinctly human figure among the tale's more elevated characters. [^1] He was also the husband of a woman who accompanied [[mongan|Mongan]]'s wife [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]] to [[leinster|Leinster]] — a domestic parallel to his master's own marital entanglement. [^2]

## Identity

Mac an Dáv was a mortal man, apparently of common birth but of stout heart and goodwill. He served [[mongan|Mongan]] as both body-servant and travelling companion, a role that mixed physical labour — carrying [[mongan|Mongan]] bodily from [[ulster|Ulster]] — with the frank counsel of a trusted retainer. His speech is marked by a colloquial bluntness: he calls a stranger "you flat-faced, nosey person" when questioned about his groaning, and addresses [[mongan|Mongan]] forthrightly as "Master." [^1]

The name "mac an Dáv" suggests a patronymic rather than a clan affiliation — he is known by his father's name rather than his own lineage, consistent with his status as a commoner in a world dominated by aristocratic warriors. His wife, who accompanied [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]] to [[leinster|Leinster]], is unnamed in the corpus — her identity subsumed into her husband's — yet she participates actively in the escape from [[branduv|Branduv]]'s fortress, fleeing alongside her husband and [[mongan|Mongan]] with the recovered women. [^3]

## In the Tales

Mac an Dáv appears in several passages of *[[mongan|Mongan's Frenzy]]*. In one extended episode, he carries [[mongan|Mongan]] on his back on the long road from [[ulster|Ulster]] — an act that, as the narrator notes, no man can do easily. As they travel, he questions [[mongan|Mongan]] about the sighs the prince gives, and is told they are approaching the King of [[leinster|Leinster]], the very man who took [[mongan|Mongan]]'s wife. Mac an Dáv's response is one of great pity for his master. [^1]

As the king and his troop ride past, Mac an Dáv groans a deep and dejected groan — "Ocón! Ochón-i-o-go-deó!" — a traditional keen of lament. When a stranger asks if he is in pain, he replies with characteristic bluntness that he is not, and tells the inquisitive man to go away. [^1] The keening groan is performative: it signals grief without specifying its cause, performing the public ritual of lament while concealing the private strategy of recovery that [[mongan|Mongan]] is about to execute.

He also appears later in the tale, asking practical questions about how to get into a castle, showing his role as the voice of practical sense among the story's more [[enchantment|magic]]al events. During the Ivell deception at [[branduv|Branduv]]'s fortress, Mac an Dáv serves as the quiet receiver of the treasures that the false Ivell extracts from the besotted king — the drinking-horn and the girdle that [[mongan|Mongan]]'s scheme accumulates along the way to recovering [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]]. [^3]

## Significance

Mac an Dáv is a rare example in Irish myth of a named commoner who is neither warrior nor noble. His presence in the tale provides a human-scale perspective on the extraordinary events surrounding [[mongan|Mongan]] — a loyal, plain-spoken servant whose groans and grumbles ground the fairy-tale narrative in recognisable human emotion. [^1] The inclusion of his wife in the narrative — and her active participation in the escape — adds a domestic dimension to the political intrigue, suggesting that the bonds of servant and master extend beyond feudal obligation into something approaching shared family fate.
