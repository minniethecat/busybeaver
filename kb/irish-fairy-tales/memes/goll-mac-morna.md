## Overview

Goll mac Morna is the towering chief of the clann-Morna and a central figure in the rivalries that shape the [[fianna|Fianna of Ireland]]. Described as "huge-shouldered" [014-008] and possessed of a "merry stare" [016-060], Goll is both a fearsome warrior and a figure of boisterous joviality — a man who can slaughter sixty-one of Fionn's people in a single battle and then laugh across the banquet table at [[tara|Tara]].

## Identity

Goll is the leader of clann-Morna, the rival faction within the [[fianna|Fianna]]. His father and his clan orchestrated the killing of Fionn's father Uail in order to seize the captaincy of the [[fianna|Fianna]] [012-009]–[012-013] — an act that established the central enmity of the Fenian Cycle and forced the infant Fionn into hiding.

Despite this history, Goll is not a simple villain. He serves as Fionn's chief lieutenant, sitting at Fionn's left hand at the great banquet of the [[fianna|Fianna]] [022-065]. His one-eyed gaze — he is called "Goll" which means "one-eyed" — and his huge physical presence make him one of the most vividly drawn characters in the tales. His disposition is described repeatedly as "mirthful" and "merry" [043-033], a temperament that contrasts sharply with his capacity for battlefield violence.

## In the Tales

### The Little Brawl at Allen

Goll's role in this tale defines the fraught politics of the [[fianna|Fianna]]. When the brawl between Fionn's people and clann-Morna escalates into a full battle, "Goll mac Morna covered his people with his shield. All that slaughter was done by him" [045-030]–[045-031]. Sixty-one of Goll's men died against eleven hundred of Fionn's — a disparity that speaks to Goll's skill as a defensive warrior. At the subsequent judgement at [[tara|Tara]], Goll boldly objects to Fionn giving evidence against him, trusting instead the poet [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus True-Lips]], whose poetic oath binds him to truth [045-005]–[045-019].

### Banquets and Generosity

The banquet scenes reveal another side of Goll. At the feast where poets sang the "Forts, the Destructions, the Raids, and the Wooings of clann-Morna," Goll "grew more and more jovial and contented" [043-060]–[043-062]. His generosity matched his physique: "much as Fionn had given, Goll gave twice as much" to the poets and harpers [043-072]–[043-073]. As the banquet continued, "Goll gave, whether it was to harpers or poor men, more than any one else could give" [043-074].

## Significance

Goll mac Morna embodies the complex dynamic of rivalry and loyalty that defines the [[fianna|Fianna]]. As the leader of the clan that murdered Fionn's father, he is the villain of Fionn's origin story; as Fionn's lieutenant and fellow-feaster, he is an essential part of the heroic establishment. His mirthful, generous, and physically imposing character provides a foil to Fionn's more measured wisdom — together they represent the twin pillars of the [[fianna|Fianna]]: strength and laughter.
