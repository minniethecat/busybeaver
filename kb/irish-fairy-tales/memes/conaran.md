## Overview

Conaran, son of Imidel, was the king of the [[cesh-corran|Shi' of Cesh Corran]], a
[[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]] lord who dwelt in the hollow hills of northern
Connacht following the defeat of his people by the [[milesians|Milesians]].
He stands as one of the few antagonists in the Fenian cycle who
defeated Fionn mac Uail not through combat but through sheer magical
craft.

His enmity toward [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] was deeply personal, and his
method — ensnaring the heroes with enchanted threads spun by his three
daughters — made the encounter at [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]] one of the Fianna's
most complete humiliations.

## The Sidhe Kings

By the time of the Fenian tales, the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]] had long
since retreated into the [[sidhe-mound|sidhe mounds]], the hollow hills that dotted
the Irish landscape. These beings were no longer the gods of Ireland but
a diminished people, living underground, nursing ancient grudges against
the mortal races that had supplanted them.

Conaran represents this diminished but still-dangerous power. As king of
the [[cesh-corran|Shi' of Cesh Corran]], he commanded the supernatural resources of
his realm: glamour, transformation, and binding magic. His three
daughters, skilled in the spinning of enchanted threads, served as his
instruments.

## In the Tales

### The Enmity

The text is explicit that Conaran's hatred of Fionn was personal: "he
did not like Fionn," the narrator tells us, and "we do not know what
Fionn had done to Conaran, but it must have been bad" [055-004]–[055-007].
This deliberate ambiguity — the cause unknown, the effect devastating —
gives the conflict a mythic quality. Some offense, ancient and
unforgotten, had passed between them.

### The Enchantment

The trap was elegant in its simplicity. Conaran's three daughters sat at
the mouth of the cave, spinning — an image of domestic industry that
disarmed suspicion. When Fionn and his companions approached, Conaran
worked his magic from within: "by his arts Conaran changed the sight of
Fionn's eyes" [056-001]. The warriors' perception was altered; they saw
only what the Sidhe king willed them to see.

One by one the [[fianna|Fianna]] warriors entered the cave and were bound by
the daughters' enchanted threads [056-025]. [[conan-the-swearer|Cona'n the Swearer]],
[[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]] himself, and several others were rendered as helpless as
children, their legendary strength and skill rendered meaningless by
magic.

### The Defeat

It was [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]], arriving late and alone, who broke the
enchantment. Where Fionn and the others had been seduced by the
daughters' glamour, Goll — the one-eyed warrior — saw through it. He
killed two of the daughters and forced the third to release the
captives. Conaran's power was broken, and the [[fianna|Fianna]] emerged from
the cave, their ordeal ended but their pride seriously wounded.

## Significance

Conaran represents a recurring theme in Irish mythology: the danger of
the old powers, the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], who though defeated could
still destroy the unwary through enchantment. His tale serves as a
reminder that in the world of Irish legend, physical strength is
insufficient against the supernatural — a lesson that even [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]],
the greatest hero of the Fenian cycle, had to learn.
