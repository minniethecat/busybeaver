## Overview

Ben Edair — modern Howth Head, the rocky peninsula that guards the northern entrance to Dublin Bay — serves in *Irish Fairy Tales* as the threshold between Ireland and the supernatural sea-realms beyond. It is from Ben Edair that kings and heroes set forth on voyages into [[faery|Faery]], and it is upon the rocks of Ben Edair that they return, transformed by what they have encountered.

## Location

The Hill of Ben Edair rises at the northern edge of Dublin Bay, in the ancient [[leinster|province of Leinster]]. In the mythological geography of the tales, it is both a real place — a promontory of rock and green overlooking the Irish Sea — and a liminal zone where the boundaries between worlds grow thin. The rocks at the foot of Ben Edair are described with nautical precision: "swung in low tide against a rock at the foot of Ben Edair" [060-062], suggesting an intimate familiarity with the actual topography of Howth.

## In the Tales

### Becuma's Banishment

When [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]] was expelled from the [[faery|Many-Coloured Land]], it was to Ben Edair that she was sent: she "was forced to appear in the world" and arrived at the shore beneath this promontory, "swung in low tide against a rock at the foot of Ben Edair" [060-044]–[060-062]. From there she proceeded to [[tara|Tara]], carrying the supernatural beauty and moral contagion that would bring blight to Ireland.

### Art's Voyage

To reverse the curse that Becuma brought, Art — the High King's son — "went to Ben Edair. He stepped into a coracle and pushed out to the sea" to seek the means of Ireland's restoration [064-005]. Ben Edair is thus the departure point for quests that determine the fate of the kingdom: a place where the mortal realm's most desperate hopes are launched into the unknown.

### The Carl's Journey

In *[[the-carl|The Carl]] of the Drab Coat*, the mysterious Carl moves "towards Ben Edair as a lover runs to his maiden or as a bee flies to his hive" [050-058]. The comparison to a lover's urgency captures the pull that this place exerts on the supernatural beings who pass through it.

## Mythological Significance

Ben Edair functions as a portal site — one of those places in Irish topography where the boundary between worlds is negotiable. Unlike [[tara|Tara]] (the center of earthly power) or [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]] (a cave-entrance to [[faery|Faery]]), Ben Edair is specifically a maritime threshold. Its association with departure and return, banishment and homecoming, gives it a distinct emotional register in the tales: it is a place of leave-taking, of risk undertaken, and of the precarious hope that those who set forth will return.
