# Flann

## Identity

Flann was a young warrior who appears in the latter portion of *Mongan's Frenzy* as the lover of Dubh Laca, the wife of [[mongan|Mongan]] himself. His name, meaning "blood-red" in Old Irish, was common among the warrior class, and the young man Flann of this tale is defined not by his lineage but by the romantic entanglement that becomes the instrument of Mongan's most elaborate supernatural jest. [^1]

## In the Tales

Flann's role in *Mongan's Frenzy* is that of the unwitting rival — a young man whose love for Dubh Laca draws him into a confrontation with a king who possesses powers far beyond the mortal. Mongan, discovering the attachment between Flann and his wife, does not respond with violence but with illusion: using the shapeshifting powers inherited from his divine father [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannan mac Lir]], Mongan takes on the appearance of Flann himself, confounding both Dubh Laca and the real Flann in a demonstration of supernatural mastery that is at once comic and unsettling. [^1]

> "Come out, Flann, come out, my heart!" [040-003]

The tale reaches its emotional climax when Dubh Laca, believing she embraces Flann, throws her arms around Mongan in Flann's shape. The discovery that follows is a moment of profound confusion for all involved — a confusion that Mongan, unlike a mortal king, has the power to resolve not through punishment but through the very magic that created it. [^1]

> He whispered: "Besides the young man, named Flann, who was not slain, and her arms were about the neck of Flann." [042-005]

## Significance

Flann is a minor figure whose narrative function is to be the occasion for Mongan's display of Otherworldly power. He is not villainous — his love for Dubh Laca appears genuine — but he is hopelessly outmatched by a rival who can assume his very shape. In the economy of the tale, Flann represents ordinary mortal desire, the kind that belongs to a world without magic, and his helplessness before Mongan's enchantment underscores the gulf between the human and the Sidhe-touched.
