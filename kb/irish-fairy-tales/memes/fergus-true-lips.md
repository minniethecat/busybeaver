## Overview

Fergus True-Lips — called Fergus Fionnliath in the Irish — is the chief poet and truth-speaker of the [[fianna|Fianna-Finn]]. A figure of memorable eccentricities and unshakeable authority, he appears in two tales: as the reluctant dog-keeper in "The [[wooing-of-becfola|Wooing of Becfola]]," and as the poetic peacemaker and sworn witness at the judgement that follows "The [[brawl-at-allen|Little Brawl at Allen]]." His nickname "True-Lips" reflects his defining trait: as a poet bound by sacred oath, his words cannot be false.

## Identity

Fergus Fionnliath lived in a [[dun|stronghold]] near the harbour of Galway [026-007]. He was a gentleman of the [[fianna|Fianna]] and its chief poet, commanding the company of [[bardic-tradition|bard]]s who accompanied the warrior-band. His most distinctive characteristic was his violent hatred of dogs: "whenever a dog barked he would leap out of his seat, and he would throw everything that he owned out of the window in the direction of the bark" [026-007]. He gave prizes to servants who disliked dogs, and when he heard that a man had drowned a litter of pups, he "used to visit that person and try to marry his daughter" [026-007].

This hatred forms a comic counterpoint to [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]]'s well-known love of dogs: Fionn "delighted in dogs, and he knew everything about them" [026-013], while Fergus was "the reverse of Fergus Fionnliath in this" [026-013].

## In the Tales

### The Wooing of Becfola

Fergus appears in the tale of [[becfola|Becfola]] through one of the most peculiar episodes in Irish myth. The fairy woman [[uct-dealv|Uct Dealv]], wishing to dispose of a hound given to her by Fionn, brings the animal to Fergus Fionnliath's [[dun|stronghold]] because "he was reputed to be the unfriendliest man in the world to a dog" [027-080]. She hopes Fergus will take vengeance on the hound for her.

When Uct Dealv presents the dog, Fergus demands she kill it herself. She instead tricks him into taking custody of the hound. Over the following days, Fergus is tormented by the creature's mournful presence. His servant reports the dog will neither eat nor drink, and Fergus, in despair, declares "Its legs will drop off. Fionn will blame me" [029-027]. In the end, Fergus himself must kiss the hound — an act so contrary to his nature that he takes to his bed for a year afterwards [030-054].

### The Little Brawl at Allen

Fergus's most significant role unfolds during the great banquet brawl between Fionn's people and clann-Morna. As the combat rages, "Fergus True-Lips gathered about him all the poets of the [[fianna|Fianna]], and they surrounded the combatants" [044-126]–[044-127]. The poets "began to chant and intone long, heavy rhymes and incantations, until the rhythmic beating of their voices covered even the noise of war" [044-128]–[044-129]. The combatants, overwhelmed by the power of the verse, "stopped hacking and hewing, and let their weapons drop from their hands" [044-130]–[044-131]. The poets then picked up the weapons and brokered a reconciliation [044-131]–[044-132].

Before the brawl, Fergus had also performed at the feast, singing of Fionn's ancestors and their deeds, and then of "the Forts, the Destructions, the Raids, and the Wooings of clann-Morna" [043-060]. His songs drew lavish gifts from both Fionn and Goll, with Goll giving "twice as much" as Fionn [043-072]–[043-073].

### The Judgement at Tara

At the formal judgement before [[cormac-mac-art|Cormac mac Art]], [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] chooses Fergus as the one witness he trusts. "I will trust to the evidence of Fergus True-Lips," Goll declares. "He is Fionn's poet, and will tell no lie against his master; he is a poet, and will tell no lie against any one" [045-005]–[045-008]. Goll requires that Fergus swear before the court "by his gods, that he will do justice between us" [045-010]–[045-012].

Fergus, duly sworn, gives his evidence with precision: Cairell struck Cona'n first, Goll's sons intervened for Cona'n, [[oscar|Oscar]] for Cairell, and from there "what had started as a brawl ended as a battle" [045-018]. When Cormac marvels that clann-Morna lost only sixty-one against eleven hundred of Fionn's men, Fergus delivers the decisive testimony: "[[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] covered his people with his shield. All that slaughter was done by him" [045-030]–[045-031].

## Significance

Fergus True-Lips embodies the unique role of the poet in early Irish society — not merely an entertainer but a figure of ritual power and binding truth. His verse can stop a battle; his oath is accepted as evidence in the High King's court. His dog-hating eccentricity humanizes him, but his function in the tales is profoundly serious: he is the voice that can speak truth to power, trusted by Fionn and Goll alike because his poetic office transcends faction.
