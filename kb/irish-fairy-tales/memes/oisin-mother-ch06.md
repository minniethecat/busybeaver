[036-001] Just as at one time he could not be parted from [[saeve|Saeve]], so now he could

[036-002] not be separated from this boy. He had a thousand names for him, each

[036-003] one more tender than the last: “My Fawn, My Pulse, My Secret Little

[036-004] Treasure,” or he would call him “My Music, My Blossoming Branch, My

[036-005] Store in the Heart, My Soul.” And the dogs were as wild for the boy as

[036-006] Fionn was. He could sit in safety among a pack that would have torn any

[036-007] man to pieces, and the reason was that [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan, with their

[036-008] three whelps, followed him about like shadows. When he was with the pack

[036-009] these five were with him, and woeful indeed was the eye they turned

[036-010] on their comrades when these pushed too closely or were not properly

[036-011] humble. They thrashed the pack severally and collectively until every

[036-012] hound in Fionn’s kennels knew that the little lad was their master, and

[036-013] that there was nothing in the world so sacred as he was.

[036-014] In no long time the five wise hounds could have given over their

[036-015] guardianship, so complete was the recognition of their young lord. But

[036-016] they did not so give over, for it was not love they gave the lad but

[036-017] adoration.

[036-018] Fionn even may have been embarrassed by their too close attendance. If

[036-019] he had been able to do so he might have spoken harshly to his dogs, but

[036-020] he could not; it was unthinkable that he should; and the boy might have

[036-021] spoken harshly to him if he had dared to do it. For this was the order

[036-022] of Fionn’s affection: first there was the boy; next, Bran and Sceo’lan

[036-023] with their three whelps; then Caelte mac Rona’n, and from him down

[036-024] through the champions. He loved them all, but it was along that

[036-025] precedence his affections ran. The thorn that went into Bran’s foot ran

[036-026] into Fionn’s also. The world knew it, and there was not a champion but

[036-027] admitted sorrowfully that there was reason for his love.

[036-028] Little by little the boy came to understand their speech and to speak it

[036-029] himself, and at last he was able to tell his story to Fionn.

[036-030] There were many blanks in the tale, for a young child does not remember

[036-031] very well. Deeds grow old in a day and are buried in a night. New

[036-032] memories come crowding on old ones, and one must learn to forget as well

[036-033] as to remember. A whole new life had come on this boy, a life that was

[036-034] instant and memorable, so that his present memories blended into and

[036-035] obscured the past, and he could not be quite sure if that which he told

[036-036] of had happened in this world or in the world he had left.
