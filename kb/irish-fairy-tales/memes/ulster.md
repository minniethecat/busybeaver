# Ulster

## Overview

Ulster is the northern province of Ireland, featured prominently in Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales* as the homeland of kings, warriors, and shapeshifters. In "The Story of [[tuan-mac-cairill|Tuan Mac Cairill]]," it is the territory of [[cairill|Cairill]] the King, whose fisherman captured Tuan in his salmon form [011-001]. In "The Birth of [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]]," Ulster is the domain of [[iollan-eachtach|Iollan Eachtach]], the gentleman who marries Fionn's sister [[tuiren|Tuiren]] [026-045]–[026-048].

## Location

- **Region**: Ulster (northern Ireland)
- **Modern equivalent**: The historic province of Ulster spans nine counties — six in modern Northern Ireland and three in the Republic of Ireland
- **Type of place**: Ancient province and kingdom, encompassing coastlines, caves, royal forts, and the sacred Brugh of [[boyne|the Boyne]]

## In the Tales

Ulster appears across multiple tales, most vividly through [[tuan-mac-cairill|Tuan Mac Cairill]]'s narrative. In his long life as stag, boar, hawk, and salmon, Ulster was his anchoring point — the place his animal instincts always drew him back to. As a hawk, "my heart were drawn to Ulster; and, standing away, my wide nose took the air, while I knew with joy, with terror, that men were blown on the wind towards me" [006-033]–[006-034]. He retreated to his "Ulster cave" to dream himself into new forms, first becoming a hawk [008-002]–[008-003] and later, in old age, dreaming in "my Ulster cave close to the sea" to become a salmon [008-024]–[008-025].

As a salmon in distant waters, a homesick longing seized him: "far away in the sea, I remembered Ulster, and there came on me an instant, uncontrollable anguish to be there" [009-019]–[009-020]. He fought his way back through rock and sickness: "I fought my way to Ulster from the sea" [009-024], straining "through a rock as I strained towards Ulster from the sea" [009-029].

In his salmon form, Tuan was caught by "the fisherman of [[cairill|Cairill]], the King of Ulster" [011-001] and brought to the king's hall, where he was transformed back to human shape and told his life story. In the tale's closing lines, [[finnian|Finnian]] wonders whether [[cairill|Cairill]] "still keeps his fort in Ulster, watching all things" [011-039]–[011-040].

Ulster also appears in "The Birth of [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]]," where [[tuiren|Tuiren]], Fionn's sister, "gave her love" to "an Ulster gentleman, [[iollan-eachtach|Iollan Eachtach]]" [026-045]. After their marriage "they went to Ulster, and they lived" together [027-001]. Iollan later promises an "Ulster feast" for [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus True-Lips]] [027-068], reflecting the province's tradition of hospitality.

In "[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]," the three great burial-places of Ireland are enumerated: "the Brugh of [[boyne|the Boyne]] in Ulster, over which [[angus-og|Angus Og]] is chief and god" stands first among them [061-013].

## Mythological Significance

Ulster gives its name to the Ulster Cycle of Irish mythology, though Stephens' tales draw more from the Fenian and Mythological cycles. In these stories, Ulster is the province where [[shapeshifting|transformation]] and memory intertwine — Tuan's cave is a threshold between animal and human consciousness, and [[cairill|Cairill]]'s fort is where the ancient past is preserved through storytelling.

The Brugh of [[boyne|the Boyne]] (Newgrange), a Neolithic passage tomb in Ulster, is described as one of Ireland's three great pre-Christian burial sites, ruled by the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] god [[angus-og|Angus Og]] [061-013].
