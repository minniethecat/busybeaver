[020-001] All desires save one are fleeting, but that one lasts for ever. Fionn,

[020-002] with all desires, had the lasting one, for he would go anywhere and

[020-003] forsake anything for wisdom; and it was in search of this that he went

[020-004] to the place where [[finegas|Finegas]] lived on a bank of [[boyne|the Boyne]] Water. But

[020-005] for dread of the clann-Morna he did not go as Fionn. He called himself

[020-006] [[fionn-mac-uail|Deimne]] on that journey.

[020-007] We get wise by asking questions, and even if these are not answered we

[020-008] get wise, for a well-packed question carries its answer on its back as

[020-009] a snail carries its shell. Fionn asked every question he could think of,

[020-010] and his master, who was a poet, and so an honourable man, answered them

[020-011] all, not to the limit of his patience, for it was limitless, but to the

[020-012] limit of his ability.

[020-013] “Why do you live on the bank of a river?” was one of these questions.

[020-014] “Because a poem is a revelation, and it is by the brink of running water

[020-015] that [[bardic-tradition|poetry]] is revealed to the mind.”

[020-016] “How long have you been here?” was the next query. “Seven years,” the

[020-017] poet answered.

[020-018] “It is a long time,” said wondering Fionn.

[020-019] “I would wait twice as long for a poem,” said the inveterate bard.

[020-020] “Have you caught good poems?” Fionn asked him.

[020-021] “The poems I am fit for,” said the mild master. “No person can get more

[020-022] than that, for a man’s readiness is his limit.”

[020-023] “Would you have got as good poems by the Shannon or the Suir or by sweet

[020-024] Ana Life’?”

[020-025] “They are good rivers,” was the answer. “They all belong to good gods.”

[020-026] “But why did you choose this river out of all the rivers?”

[020-027] Finegas beamed on his pupil.

[020-028] “I would tell you anything,” said he, “and I will tell you that.”

[020-029] Fionn sat at the kindly man’s feet, his hands absent among tall grasses,

[020-030] and listening with all his ears. “A prophecy was made to me,” Finegas

[020-031] began. “A man of knowledge foretold that I should catch [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of

[020-032] Knowledge in the Boyne Water.”

[020-033] “And then?” said Fionn eagerly.

[020-034] “Then I would have All Knowledge.”

[020-035] “And after that?” the boy insisted.

[020-036] “What should there be after that?” the poet retorted.

[020-037] “I mean, what would you do with All Knowledge?”

[020-038] “A weighty question,” said Finegas smilingly. “I could answer it if I

[020-039] had All Knowledge, but not until then. What would you do, my dear?”

[020-040] “I would make a poem,” Fionn cried.

[020-041] “I think too,” said the poet, “that that is what would be done.”

[020-042] In return for instruction Fionn had taken over the service of his

[020-043] master’s hut, and as he went about the household duties, drawing the

[020-044] water, lighting the fire, and carrying rushes for the floor and the

[020-045] beds, he thought over all the poet had taught him, and his mind dwelt on

[020-046] the rules of metre, the cunningness of words, and the need for a clean,

[020-047] brave mind. But in his thousand thoughts he yet remembered the Salmon of

[020-048] Knowledge as eagerly as his master did. He already venerated Finegas

[020-049] for his great learning, his poetic skill, for an hundred reasons; but,

[020-050] looking on him as the ordained eater of the Salmon of Knowledge, he

[020-051] venerated him to the edge of measure. Indeed, he loved as well as

[020-052] venerated this master because of his unfailing kindness, his patience,

[020-053] his readiness to teach, and his skill in teaching.

[020-054] “I have learned much from you, dear master,” said Fionn gratefully.

[020-055] “All that I have is yours if you can take it,” the poet answered, “for

[020-056] you are entitled to all that you can take, but to no more than that.

[020-057] Take, so, with both hands.”

[020-058] “You may catch the salmon while I am with you,” the hopeful boy mused.

[020-059] “Would not that be a great happening!” and he stared in ecstasy across

[020-060] the grass at those visions which a boy’s mind knows.

[020-061] “Let us pray for that,” said Finegas fervently.

[020-062] “Here is a question,” Fionn continued. “How does this salmon get wisdom

[020-063] into his flesh?”

[020-064] “There is a hazel bush overhanging a secret pool in a secret place. The

[020-065] Nuts of Knowledge drop from the Sacred Bush into the pool, and as they

[020-066] float, a salmon takes them in his mouth and eats them.”

[020-067] “It would be almost as easy,” the boy submitted, “if one were to set on

[020-068] the track of the Sacred Hazel and eat the nuts straight from the bush.”

[020-069] “That would not be very easy,” said the poet, “and yet it is not as easy

[020-070] as that, for the bush can only be found by its own knowledge, and that

[020-071] knowledge can only be got by eating the nuts, and the nuts can only be

[020-072] got by eating the salmon.”

[020-073] “We must wait for the salmon,” said Fionn in a rage of resignation.
