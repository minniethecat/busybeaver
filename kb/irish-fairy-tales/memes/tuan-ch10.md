[010-001] “Delight and strength came to me again, and now I explored all the

[010-002] inland ways, the great lakes of Ireland, and her swift brown rivers.

[010-003] “What a joy to lie under an inch of water basking in the sun, or beneath

[010-004] a shady ledge to watch the small creatures that speed like lightning on

[010-005] the rippling top. I saw the dragon-flies flash and dart and turn, with

[010-006] a poise, with a speed that no other winged thing knows: I saw the hawk

[010-007] hover and stare and swoop: he fell like a falling stone, but he could

[010-008] not catch the king of the salmon: I saw the cold-eyed cat stretching

[010-009] along a bough level with the water, eager to hook and lift the creatures

[010-010] of the river. And I saw men.

[010-011] “They saw me also. They came to know me and look for me. They lay in

[010-012] wait at the waterfalls up which I leaped like a silver flash. They held

[010-013] out nets for me; they hid traps under leaves; they made cords of the

[010-014] colour of water, of the colour of weeds--but this salmon had a nose that

[010-015] knew how a weed felt and how a string--they drifted meat on a sightless

[010-016] string, but I knew of the hook; they thrust spears at me, and threw

[010-017] lances which they drew back again with a cord. Many a wound I got from

[010-018] men, many a sorrowful scar.

[010-019] “Every beast pursued me in the waters and along the banks; the barking,

[010-020] black-skinned otter came after me in lust and gust and swirl; the wild

[010-021] cat fished for me; the hawk and the steep-winged, spear-beaked birds

[010-022] dived down on me, and men crept on me with nets the width of a river,

[010-023] so that I got no rest. My life became a ceaseless scurry and wound and

[010-024] escape, a burden and anguish of watchfulness--and then I was caught.”
