## Identity and Origins

Tuan Mac Cairill is the central figure of *The Story of Tuan Mac Cairill*, the opening tale of James Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales*. He is a living witness to the entire sweep of Ireland's legendary history — from the first settlers after the Flood to the arrival of Christianity with Saint Finnian. His unique lifespan spans multiple eras through a cycle of magical animal transformations: stag, boar, hawk, and salmon, followed by human rebirth as the son of Cairill, King of Ulster. Unlike any other character in the Irish mythological corpus, Tuan bridges every epoch, making him both the oldest human inhabitant of Ireland and the longest continuous thread in its mythological memory.

Tuan's identity is layered across genealogies and eras. In the time of the story's present, he is Tuan, son of Cairill, son of Muredac Red-neck, a powerful Ulster gentleman who resists the new Christian faith [001-032], [003-024]–[003-025]. But his true lineage reaches much farther back: he is Tuan mac Starn, mac Sera, whose brother was Partholon himself — the leader of Ireland's first post-Flood settlement [003-032]–[003-033], [003-039]–[003-040]. When Finnian hears this claim, his immediate reaction is disbelief: Partholon came to Ireland "not long after the Flood," yet Tuan calmly confirms, "I came with him" [003-043]–[003-045].

His dual genealogy — that of Cairill's son and Starn's son — reflects the central miracle of his life: he was born as Tuan mac Starn in primordial Ireland, lived through thousands of years of transformations, and was reborn in historical times as Tuan mac Cairill.

## In the Tales

### The Siege of Finnian

Finnian, the Abbott of [[moville|Moville]], arrived at Tuan's stronghold in Ulster, determined to convert this gentleman who kept the old gods [001-001]–[001-018]. Tuan barricaded himself inside, refusing to hear the new doctrine [001-022]–[001-026]. Finnian fasted outside Tuan's door, a siege of will that lasted until Tuan — unable to bear the thought of a stranger dying on his doorstep — admitted him [002-001]–[002-013], [002-056]–[002-059]. After recovering, Finnian set about converting Tuan, and his campaign succeeded: Tuan's heart opened, and Finnian "marched there to do the will of God" [002-063]–[002-069].

### The Telling of the Past

Once converted, Tuan and Finnian's relationship reversed: Tuan became as eager for Christian instruction as Finnian had been for his conversion [003-001]–[003-004]. But Finnian, seeking to understand his convert, insisted Tuan first tell his own story: "a man is his past, and is to be known by it" [003-014]–[003-015]. Reluctantly — for he believed "man needs forgetfulness as well as memory" [003-016]–[003-017] — Tuan began his extraordinary narrative.

### Partholon and the First Days

Tuan recounted arriving in Ireland with Partholon, son of Noah's son, aboard a ship carrying twenty-four men and twenty-four women [004-014]–[004-015]. Ireland then was an unending forest, alive with the singing of birds, the land appearing to sea-weary eyes like Paradise [004-016]–[004-021]. Partholon's people settled, multiplied to five thousand, and lived "in amity and contentment although they had no wits" [004-031]–[004-033].

Then, sudden as a rising wind, a plague struck. On the seventh day "all of the race of Partholon were dead, save one man only" — and Tuan was that man [004-038]–[004-042].

### The Wild Years

Alone in Ireland for twenty-two years, Tuan descended into a feral existence. The creatures of the forest scented his solitude and hunted him; there was "no creature so weak but it might hunt me, there was no creature so timid but it might outface me" [005-006]–[005-010]. He learned to pad silently, to smell danger in his sleep, to bark and growl and tear with his teeth — until he "knew all that a beast surmises and had forgotten all that a man had known" [005-011]–[005-016].

When Nemed son of Agnoman arrived with his great fleet, Tuan caught his own reflection in a pool and saw a creature "hairy and tufty and bristled as a savage boar," lean, withered, and clawed — "like nothing that was known, like nothing that was animal or divine" [005-019]–[005-032]. A terrible storm destroyed Nemed's fleet, sparing only Nemed and four couples [005-037]–[005-061], [006-039]–[006-040].

## Transformations

Tuan's transformations are the core of his story. Each occurs as old age overtakes him in one form; he dreams a change, and awakens in a new body. He passes through four animal forms, experiencing the world through each creature's senses and powers.

### Stag [006-001]–[006-031]

"I dreamed, and I saw myself changing into a stag in dream, and I felt in dream the beating of a new heart within me" [006-001]–[006-002]. He awoke from the dream "and I was that which I had dreamed" [006-004]. As a stag, he experienced a rebirth of the senses: his nose "sniffed all things to my heart and separated them into knowledge" [006-009]–[006-010]. He became "the beloved, the well known, the leader of the herds of Ireland" [006-030]–[006-031], ruling over his kind with an unswerving forehead that drove away wolves and trampled rivals.

During his time as a stag, he watched the people of Nemed multiply to four thousand couples, then vanish, leaving only bones "that glinted in the sun" [006-039]–[006-046]. Old age returned, and wolves besieged him in his cave, promising: "To-morrow we will tear out your throat" [006-057]–[006-058].

### Boar [007-005]–[007-018]

"I slept, and I saw myself changing into a boar in dream" [007-005]. He awoke as a boar — a "killer, a champion, a boar who could not be defied" [007-017]. Where the wolves had cornered the old stag, they now fled in terror from the bristling, tusked boar [007-012]–[007-015]. He "took the lordship of the boars of Ireland" [007-018] and challenged every creature except men — for the people of Semion (ancestors of the Fir Bolg and Gaileoin) had now come to Ireland [007-029]–[007-031].

### Hawk [008-001]–[008-023]

When age overtook him as a boar, he dreamed again and "changed into a hawk" [008-001]–[008-003]. As a hawk, "the sweet air was my kingdom, and my bright eye stared on a hundred miles" [008-004]–[008-005]. He witnessed the arrival of Beothach, son of Iarbonel the Prophet, and the great battle between his people and the children of Semion — "seeing every spear that hurtled, every stone that whizzed from a sling" [008-008]–[008-012]. From this conflict emerged the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], whom Tuan identifies as "the people of Faery. All these are the gods" [008-016]–[008-017].

He remained a hawk "for long, long years," learning "every hill and stream; every field and glen of Ireland," and was still a hawk when the Sons of Mil drove the Tuatha De Danann underground, marking "the coming of men and the beginning of genealogies" [008-018]–[008-023].

### Salmon [008-024]–[009-001]

In his Ulster cave by the sea, Tuan dreamed his final animal transformation: "in it I became a salmon" [008-024]–[008-025]. He reflected on the completeness of the fish form compared to all his others: "I had been a man, a stag, a boar, a bird, and now I was a fish" [008-028]. Of all forms, the salmon was supreme: "the fish has but one piece from his nose to his tail. He is complete, single and unencumbered" [008-034]–[008-036].

As king of the salmon, he ranged the tides of the world, explored "the secret caves where ocean roars to ocean," and beheld the monsters of the deep [009-001]–[009-018]. Then, far away in the sea, an "instant, uncontrollable anguish" drew him back to Ulster, and he fought his way through the waves in a heroic journey home [009-019]–[009-039].

### Human Rebirth [011-001]–[011-034]

The fisherman of Cairill, the King of Ulster, caught the great salmon in his net [011-001]–[011-003]. The king's wife desired the fish; it was roasted, and she ate it [011-029]–[011-030]. Through her, Tuan was reborn: "when time passed she gave birth to me, and I was her son and the son of Cairill the king" [011-030]–[011-031]. Crucially, he retained all memory of his previous existences: "All that happened I remember, from the time I was on the gridiron until the time I was born. I forget nothing of these things" [011-032]–[011-034].

## Significance and Legacy

Upon hearing Tuan's entire story, Finnian declared that Tuan would "be born again" through Christian baptism into "the family of the Living God" [011-035]–[011-036]. The tale ends with an open question: no one knows whether Tuan died in Finnian's time, "or if he still keeps his fort in Ulster, watching all things, and remembering them for the glory of God and the honour of Ireland" [011-038]–[011-040].

Tuan Mac Cairill is unique in Irish mythology as a figure who bridges every epoch of the island's legendary history. His transformations — stag, boar, hawk, salmon — trace a deliberate arc through the animal kingdom, each form granting him a different mode of perception and power. His story serves as a mythological framework for the entire Irish settlement narrative (the Lebor Gabala Erenn tradition) and ultimately as a conversion narrative: the oldest living memory of Ireland is baptized into Christianity, merging the pagan past with the Christian present.

### The Arc of Transformation

Tuan's sequence of animal forms traces a deliberate metaphysical arc from earthbound to ethereal. The stag, rooted in the forest floor, is a creature of scent and herd — his knowledge comes through the nose, "sniffing all things to his heart" [006-009]–[006-010]. The boar is more assertive: a "killer, a champion" whose tusks command fear [007-017]. The hawk ascends beyond the earth entirely, its "bright eye staring on a hundred miles" [008-004]–[008-005] — perception becomes vision rather than smell or force. Finally, the salmon completes the sequence: "complete, single and unencumbered" [008-034]–[008-036], it inhabits the element that encircles the world, ranging "the secret caves where ocean roars to ocean" [009-016]–[009-018]. This progression — from land to air to water, from herd to solitary, from hunted to hunter to observer to oceanic voyager — mirrors Tuan's spiritual journey from feral oblivion to the cosmic consciousness that prepares him for human rebirth and, ultimately, Christian conversion. Finnian's insistence that "a man is his past" [003-014]–[003-015] finds its fullest expression here: Tuan is not merely a man who *remembers* history — his body *is* history, accumulated through form after form.
