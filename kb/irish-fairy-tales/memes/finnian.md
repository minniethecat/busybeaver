# Finnian

## Overview

Finnian, the Abbott of Moville, is the catalyst figure who opens *The Story of Tuan mac [[cairill|Cairill]]*. Upon hearing that there were still people in his province who worshipped the old gods, Finnian travelled south and east from [[donegal|Donegal]] in great haste, determined to confront and convert the last pagan holdouts. His encounter with Tuan mac [[cairill|Cairill]]—a being thousands of years old—sets the entire tale in motion. [001-001]

## The Mission to Donegal

News reached Finnian in [[donegal|Donegal]] of a powerful gentleman who observed neither Saint's day nor Sunday—a man reputed to be wise, hardy, and a [[enchantment|magic]]ian who followed the ancient ways. Finnian's response was characteristically zealous: "We shall try this person's power," and "I will [[enchantment|magic]]ian him." He demanded to know the man's location and set out immediately. [001-001]

When Finnian arrived at the [[dun|stronghold]], he demanded admittance to preach the new God and banish the old one. But the [[ulster|Ulster]] gentleman—who turned out to be Tuan mac [[cairill|Cairill]]—refused him entry, barricading his house and shuttering his windows. Tuan continued the practices of ten thousand years behind his locked doors, ignoring Finnian's calls at the window. [001-022]

## The Siege

Undeterred, Finnian laid siege to Tuan's [[dun|stronghold]]. He could not abide that any person should resist both the Gospel and himself. At first Tuan thought the contest would be simple—he expected Finnian to grow hungry and leave. But Tuan did not know Finnian. The great abbot sat down on a stone outside the door and refused to move. [002-001]

A battle of wills ensued between the two men. Tuan attempted to outlast his unwelcome guest, but Finnian's patience proved inexhaustible. The siege continued until at last Tuan's heart opened—wearied and acknowledging the abbot's determination, he admitted Finnian. [002-057]

## Recording Tuan's Story

Once inside, Finnian's persistence paid a scholarly dividend. He asked Tuan to recount his life, and the ancient being told a story spanning millennia—from the days of the Partholonians through the Nemedians, the Fir Bolg, and the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]], all the way to the coming of the Milesians and the saints. Finnian listened and, we may presume, recorded or preserved this extraordinary oral history, making him as much a chronicler as a converter. [003-008]

## Significance

Finnian represents the collision between the old pagan Ireland and the new Christian order. His zeal is fierce but not mindless—he is willing to sit on a stone for as long as it takes, matching Tuan's ancient patience with monastic discipline. His role as the recorder of Tuan's story makes him the narrative frame through which the reader accesses Ireland's mythological deep past.
