[017-001] But Fionn was gone. He was away, bound with his band of poets for the

[017-002] Galtees.

[017-003] It is likely they were junior poets come to the end of a year’s

[017-004] training, and returning to their own province to see again the people at

[017-005] home, and to be wondered at and exclaimed at as they exhibited bits of

[017-006] the knowledge which they had brought from the great schools. They would

[017-007] know tags of rhyme and tricks about learning which Fionn would hear of;

[017-008] and now and again, as they rested in a glade or by the brink of a river,

[017-009] they might try their lessons over. They might even refer to the ogham

[017-010] wands on which the first words of their tasks and the opening lines of

[017-011] poems were cut; and it is likely that, being new to these things, they

[017-012] would talk of them to a youngster, and, thinking that his wits could be

[017-013] no better than their own, they might have explained to him how ogham was

[017-014] written. But it is far more likely that his women guardians had already

[017-015] started him at those lessons.

[017-016] Still this band of young bards would have been of infinite interest to

[017-017] Fionn, not on account of what they had learned, but because of what they

[017-018] knew. All the things that he should have known as by nature: the look,

[017-019] the movement, the feeling of crowds; the shouldering and intercourse of

[017-020] man with man; the clustering of houses and how people bore themselves

[017-021] in and about them; the movement of armed men, and the homecoming look

[017-022] of wounds; tales of births, and marriages and deaths; the chase with its

[017-023] multitudes of men and dogs; all the noise, the dust, the excitement of

[017-024] mere living. These, to Fionn, new come from leaves and shadows and the

[017-025] dipple and dapple of a wood, would have seemed wonderful; and the tales

[017-026] they would have told of their masters, their looks, fads, severities,

[017-027] sillinesses, would have been wonderful also.

[017-028] That band should have chattered like a rookery.

[017-029] They must have been young, for one time a Leinsterman came on them, a

[017-030] great robber named [[fiacuil-mac-cona|Fiacuil mac Cona]], and he killed the poets. He chopped

[017-031] them up and chopped them down. He did not leave one poeteen of them

[017-032] all. He put them out of the world and out of life, so that they stopped

[017-033] being, and no one could tell where they went or what had really happened

[017-034] to them; and it is a wonder indeed that one can do that to anything let

[017-035] alone a band. If they were not youngsters, the bold Fiacuil could not

[017-036] have managed them all. Or, perhaps, he too had a band, although the

[017-037] record does not say so; but kill them he did, and they died that way.

[017-038] Fionn saw that deed, and his blood may have been cold enough as he

[017-039] watched the great robber coursing the poets as a wild dog rages in a

[017-040] flock. And when his turn came, when they were all dead, and the grim,

[017-041] red-handed man trod at him, Fionn may have shivered, but he would have

[017-042] shown his teeth and laid roundly on the monster with his hands. Perhaps

[017-043] he did that, and perhaps for that he was spared.

[017-044] “Who are you?” roared the staring black-mouth with the red tongue

[017-045] squirming in it like a frisky fish.

[017-046] “The son of Uail, son of Baiscne,” quoth hardy Fionn. And at that the

[017-047] robber ceased to be a robber, the murderer disappeared, the black-rimmed

[017-048] chasm packed with red fish and precipices changed to something else, and

[017-049] the round eyes that had been popping out of their sockets and trying

[017-050] to bite, changed also. There remained a laughing and crying and loving

[017-051] servant who wanted to tie himself into knots if that would please the

[017-052] son of his great captain. Fionn went home on the robber’s shoulder, and

[017-053] the robber gave great snorts and made great jumps and behaved like a

[017-054] first-rate horse. For this same Fiacuil was the husband of [[bovmall|Bovmall]],

[017-055] Fionn’s aunt. He had taken to the wilds when clann-Baiscne was broken,

[017-056] and he was at war with a world that had dared to kill his Chief.
