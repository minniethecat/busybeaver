[033-001] It happened that the men of Lochlann came on an expedition against

[033-002] Ireland. A monstrous fleet rounded the bluffs of [[ben-edair|Ben Edair]], and the

[033-003] Danes landed there, to prepare an attack which would render them masters

[033-004] of the country. Fionn and the [[fianna|Fianna-Finn]] marched against them. He did

[033-005] not like the men of Lochlann at any time, but this time he moved against

[033-006] them in wrath, for not only were they attacking Ireland, but they had

[033-007] come between him and the deepest joy his life had known.

[033-008] It was a hard fight, but a short one. The Lochlannachs were driven back

[033-009] to their ships, and within a week the only Danes remaining in Ireland

[033-010] were those that had been buried there.

[033-011] That finished, he left the victorious Fianna and returned swiftly to the

[033-012] [[allen|plain of Allen]], for he could not bear to be one unnecessary day parted

[033-013] from [[saeve|Saeve]].

[033-014] “You are not leaving us!” exclaimed [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]].

[033-015] “I must go,” Fionn replied.

[033-016] “You will not desert the victory feast,” [[conan-the-swearer|Conan]] reproached him.

[033-017] “Stay with us, Chief,” Caelte begged.

[033-018] “What is a feast without Fionn?” they complained.

[033-019] But he would not stay.

[033-020] “By my hand,” he cried, “I must go. She will be looking for me from the

[033-021] window.”

[033-022] “That will happen indeed,” Goll admitted.

[033-023] “That will happen,” cried Fionn. “And when she sees me far out on the

[033-024] plain, she will run through the great gate to meet me.”

[033-025] “It would be the queer wife would neglect that run,” Cona’n growled.

[033-026] “I shall hold her hand again,” Fionn entrusted to Caelte’s ear.

[033-027] “You will do that, surely.”

[033-028] “I shall look into her face,” his lord insisted. But he saw that not

[033-029] even beloved Caelte understood the meaning of that, and he knew sadly

[033-030] and yet proudly that what he meant could not be explained by any one and

[033-031] could not be comprehended by any one.

[033-032] “You are in love, dear heart,” said Caelte.

[033-033] “In love he is,” Cona’n grumbled. “A cordial for women, a disease for

[033-034] men, a state of wretchedness.”

[033-035] “Wretched in truth,” the Chief murmured. “Love makes us poor We have not

[033-036] eyes enough to see all that is to be seen, nor hands enough to seize the

[033-037] tenth of all we want. When I look in her eyes I am tormented because I

[033-038] am not looking at her lips, and when I see her lips my soul cries out,

[033-039] ‘Look at her eyes, look at her eyes.’”

[033-040] “That is how it happens,” said Goll rememberingly.

[033-041] “That way and no other,” Caelte agreed.

[033-042] And the champions looked backwards in time on these lips and those, and

[033-043] knew their Chief would go.

[033-044] When Fionn came in sight of the great keep his blood and his feet

[033-045] quickened, and now and again he waved a spear in the air.

[033-046] “She does not see me yet,” he thought mournfully.

[033-047] “She cannot see me yet,” he amended, reproaching himself.

[033-048] But his mind was troubled, for he thought also, or he felt without

[033-049] thinking, that had the positions been changed he would have seen her at

[033-050] twice the distance.

[033-051] “She thinks I have been unable to get away from the battle, or that I

[033-052] was forced to remain for the feast.”

[033-053] And, without thinking it, he thought that had the positions been changed

[033-054] he would have known that nothing could retain the one that was absent.

[033-055] “Women,” he said, “are shamefaced, they do not like to appear eager when

[033-056] others are observing them.”

[033-057] But he knew that he would not have known if others were observing him,

[033-058] and that he would not have cared about it if he had known. And he knew

[033-059] that his Saeve would not have seen, and would not have cared for any

[033-060] eyes than his.

[033-061] He gripped his spear on that reflection, and ran as he had not run in

[033-062] his life, so that it was a panting, dishevelled man that raced heavily

[033-063] through the gates of the great Dun.

[033-064] Within the Dun there was disorder. Servants were shouting to one

[033-065] another, and women were running to and fro aimlessly, wringing their

[033-066] hands and screaming; and, when they saw the Champion, those nearest to

[033-067] him ran away, and there was a general effort on the part of every

[033-068] person to get behind every other person. But Fionn caught the eye of his

[033-069] butler, Gariv Crona’n, the Rough Buzzer, and held it.

[033-070] “Come you here,” he said.

[033-071] And the Rough Buzzer came to him without a single buzz in his body.

[033-072] “Where is the Flower of Allen?” his master demanded.

[033-073] “I do not know, master,” the terrified servant replied.

[033-074] “You do not know!” said Fionn. “Tell what you do know.”

[033-075] And the man told him this story.
