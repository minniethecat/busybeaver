[058-001] Within the cave there was silence except for the voices of the hags and

[058-002] the scarcely audible moaning of the [[fianna|Fianna-Finn]], but without there was

[058-003] a dreadful uproar, for as each man returned from the chase his dogs came

[058-004] with him, and although the men went into the cave the dogs did not.

[058-005] They were too wise.

[058-006] They stood outside, filled with savagery and terror, for they could

[058-007] scent their masters and their masters’ danger, and perhaps they could

[058-008] get from the cave smells till then unknown and full of alarm.

[058-009] From the troop of dogs there arose a baying and barking, a snarling and

[058-010] howling and growling, a yelping and squealing and bawling for which no

[058-011] words can be found. Now and again a dog nosed among a thousand smells

[058-012] and scented his master; the ruff of his neck stood up like a hog’s

[058-013] bristles and a netty ridge prickled along his spine. Then with red eyes,

[058-014] with bared fangs, with a hoarse, deep snort and growl he rushed at the

[058-015] cave, and then he halted and sneaked back again with all his ruffles

[058-016] smoothed, his tail between his legs, his eyes screwed sideways in

[058-017] miserable apology and alarm, and a long thin whine of woe dribbling out

[058-018] of his nose.

[058-019] The three sisters took their wide-channelled, hard-tempered swords in

[058-020] their hands, and prepared to slay the Fianna, but before doing so they

[058-021] gave one more look from the door of the cave to see if there might be a

[058-022] straggler of the Fianna who was escaping death by straggling, and they

[058-023] saw one coming towards them with [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan leaping beside him,

[058-024] while all the other dogs began to burst their throats with barks and

[058-025] split their noses with snorts and wag their tails off at sight of the

[058-026] tall, valiant, white-toothed champion, Goll mor mac Morna. “We will kill

[058-027] that one first,” said Caevo’g.

[058-028] “There is only one of him,” said Cuillen.

[058-029] “And each of us three is the match for an hundred,” said Iaran.

[058-030] The uncanny, misbehaved, and outrageous harridans advanced then to meet

[058-031] the son of Morna, and when he saw these three Goll whipped the sword

[058-032] from his thigh, swung his buckler round, and got to them in ten great

[058-033] leaps.

[058-034] Silence fell on the world during that conflict. The wind went down; the

[058-035] clouds stood still; the old hill itself held its breath; the warriors

[058-036] within ceased to be men and became each an ear; and the dogs sat in

[058-037] a vast circle round the combatants, with their heads all to one side,

[058-038] their noses poked forward, their mouths half open, and their tails

[058-039] forgotten. Now and again a dog whined in a whisper and snapped a

[058-040] little snap on the air, but except for that there was neither sound nor

[058-041] movement.

[058-042] It was a long fight. It was a hard and a tricky fight, and Goll won it

[058-043] by bravery and strategy and great good luck; for with one shrewd slice

[058-044] of his blade he carved two of these mighty termagants into equal halves,

[058-045] so that there were noses and whiskers to his right hand and knees and

[058-046] toes to his left: and that stroke was known afterwards as one of the

[058-047] three great sword-strokes of Ireland. The third hag, however, had

[058-048] managed to get behind Goll, and she leaped on to his back with the bound

[058-049] of a panther, and hung here with the skilful, many-legged, tight-twisted

[058-050] clutching of a spider. But the great champion gave a twist of his hips

[058-051] and a swing of his shoulders that whirled her around him like a sack.

[058-052] He got her on the ground and tied her hands with the straps of a shield,

[058-053] and he was going to give her the last blow when she appealed to his

[058-054] honour and bravery.

[058-055] “I put my life under your protection,” said she. “And if you let me go

[058-056] free I will lift the enchantment from the Fianna-[[fionn-mac-uail|Finn]] and will give them

[058-057] all back to you again.”

[058-058] “I agree to that,” said Goll, and he untied her straps. The harridan did

[058-059] as she had promised, and in a short time Fionn and Oisi’n and [[oscar|Oscar]] and

[058-060] Cona’n were released, and after that all the Fianna were released.
