[062-001] Being a king he had authority to ask questions. Conn asked her,

[062-002] therefore, all the questions that he could think of, for it is not every

[062-003] day that a lady drives from the sea, and she wearing a golden-fringed

[062-004] cloak of green silk through which a red satin smock peeped at the

[062-005] openings. She replied to his questions, but she did not tell him all the

[062-006] truth; for, indeed, she could not afford to.

[062-007] She knew who he was, for she retained some of the powers proper to the

[062-008] worlds she had left, and as he looked on her soft yellow hair and on her

[062-009] thin red lips, Conn recognised, as all men do, that one who is lovely

[062-010] must also be good, and so he did not frame any inquiry on that count;

[062-011] for everything is forgotten in the presence of a pretty woman, and a

[062-012] magician can be bewitched also.

[062-013] She told Conn that the fame of his son Art had reached even the

[062-014] [[faery|Many-Coloured Land]], and that she had fallen in love with the boy. This

[062-015] did not seem unreasonable to one who had himself ventured much in Faery,

[062-016] and who had known so many of the people of that world leave their own

[062-017] land for the love of a mortal.

[062-018] “What is your name, my sweet lady?” said the king.

[062-019] “I am called Delvcaem (Fair Shape) and I am the daughter of Morgan,” she

[062-020] replied.

[062-021] “I have heard much of Morgan,” said the king. “He is a very great

[062-022] magician.”

[062-023] During this conversation Conn had been regarding her with the minute

[062-024] freedom which is right only in a king. At what precise instant he forgot

[062-025] his dead consort we do not know, but it is certain that at this moment

[062-026] his mind was no longer burdened with that dear and lovely memory. His

[062-027] voice was melancholy when he spoke again.

[062-028] “You love my son!”

[062-029] “Who could avoid loving him?” she murmured.

[062-030] “When a woman speaks to a man about the love she feels for another man

[062-031] she is not liked. And,” he continued, “when she speaks to a man who has

[062-032] no wife of his own about her love for another man then she is disliked.”

[062-033] “I would not be disliked by you,” Becuma murmured.

[062-034] “Nevertheless,” said he regally, “I will not come between a woman and

[062-035] her choice.”

[062-036] “I did not know you lacked a wife,” said Becuma, but indeed she did.

[062-037] “You know it now,” the king replied sternly.

[062-038] “What shall I do?” she inquired, “am I to wed you or your son?”

[062-039] “You must choose,” Conn answered.

[062-040] “If you allow me to choose it means that you do not want me very badly,”

[062-041] said she with a smile.

[062-042] “Then I will not allow you to choose,” cried the king, “and it is with

[062-043] myself you shall marry.”

[062-044] He took her hand in his and kissed it.

[062-045] “Lovely is this pale thin hand. Lovely is the slender foot that I see in

[062-046] a small bronze shoe,” said the king.

[062-047] After a suitable time she continued:

[062-048] “I should not like your son to be at [[tara|Tara]] when I am there, or for a year

[062-049] afterwards, for I do not wish to meet him until I have forgotten him and

[062-050] have come to know you well.”

[062-051] “I do not wish to banish my son,” the king protested.

[062-052] “It would not really be a banishment,” she said. “A prince’s duty could

[062-053] be set him, and in such an absence he would improve his knowledge both

[062-054] of Ireland and of men. Further,” she continued with downcast eyes,

[062-055] “when you remember the reason that brought me here you will see that his

[062-056] presence would be an embarrassment to us both, and my presence would be

[062-057] unpleasant to him if he remembers his mother.”

[062-058] “Nevertheless,” said Conn stubbornly, “I do not wish to banish my son;

[062-059] it is awkward and unnecessary.”

[062-060] “For a year only,” she pleaded.

[062-061] “It is yet,” he continued thoughtfully, “a reasonable reason that you

[062-062] give and I will do what you ask, but by my hand and word I don’t like

[062-063] doing it.”

[062-064] They set out then briskly and joyfully on the homeward journey, and in

[062-065] due time they reached Tara of the Kings.
