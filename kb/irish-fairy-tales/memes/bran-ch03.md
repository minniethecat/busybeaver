[028-001] As they paced along Uct Dealv railed bitterly against the hound, and

[028-002] shook and jerked her chain. Many a sharp cry the hound gave in that

[028-003] journey, many a mild lament.

[028-004] “Ah, supplanter! Ah, taker of another girl’s sweetheart!” said Uct Dealv

[028-005] fiercely. “How would your lover take it if he could see you now? How

[028-006] would he look if he saw your pointy ears, your long thin snout, your

[028-007] shivering, skinny legs, and your long grey tail. He would not love you

[028-008] now, bad girl!”

[028-009] “Have you heard of [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus Fionnliath]],” she said again, “the man who does

[028-010] not like dogs?”

[028-011] [[tuiren|Tuiren]] had indeed heard of him.

[028-012] “It is to Fergus I shall bring you,” cried Uct Dealv. “He will throw

[028-013] stones at you. You have never had a stone thrown at you. Ah, bad girl!

[028-014] You do not know how a stone sounds as it nips the ear with a whirling

[028-015] buzz, nor how jagged and heavy it feels as it thumps against a skinny

[028-016] leg. Robber! Mortal! Bad girl! You have never been whipped, but you will

[028-017] be whipped now. You shall hear the song of a lash as it curls forward

[028-018] and bites inward and drags backward. You shall dig up old bones

[028-019] stealthily at night, and chew them against famine. You shall whine and

[028-020] squeal at the moon, and shiver in the cold, and you will never take

[028-021] another girl’s sweetheart again.”

[028-022] And it was in those terms and in that tone that she spoke to Tuiren

[028-023] as they journeyed forward, so that the hound trembled and shrank, and

[028-024] whined pitifully and in despair.

[028-025] They came to Fergus Fionnliath’s [[dun|stronghold]], and Uct Dealv demanded

[028-026] admittance.

[028-027] “Leave that dog outside,” said the servant.

[028-028] “I will not do so,” said the pretended messenger.

[028-029] “You can come in without the dog, or you can stay out with the dog,”

[028-030] said the surly guardian.

[028-031] “By my hand,” cried Uct Dealv, “I will come in with this dog, or your

[028-032] master shall answer for it to Fionn.”

[028-033] At the name of Fionn the servant almost fell out of his standing. He

[028-034] flew to acquaint his master, and Fergus himself came to the great door

[028-035] of the stronghold.

[028-036] “By my faith,” he cried in amazement, “it is a dog.”

[028-037] “A dog it is,” growled the glum servant.

[028-038] “Go you away,” said Fergus to Uct Dealv, “and when you have killed the

[028-039] dog come back to me and I will give you a present.”

[028-040] “Life and health, my good master, from Fionn, the son of Uail, the son

[028-041] of Baiscne,” said she to Fergus.

[028-042] “Life and health back to Fionn,” he replied. “Come into the house and

[028-043] give your message, but leave the dog outside, for I don’t like dogs.”

[028-044] “The dog comes in,” the messenger replied.

[028-045] “How is that?” cried Fergus angrily.

[028-046] “Fionn sends you this hound to take care of until he comes for her,”

[028-047] said the messenger.

[028-048] “I wonder at that,” Fergus growled, “for Fionn knows well that there is

[028-049] not a man in the world has less of a liking for dogs than I have.”

[028-050] “However that may be, master, I have given Fionn’s message, and here at

[028-051] my heel is the dog. Do you take her or refuse her?”

[028-052] “If I could refuse anything to Fionn it would be a dog,” said Fergus,

[028-053] “but I could not refuse anything to Fionn, so give me the hound.”

[028-054] Uct Dealv put the chain in his hand.

[028-055] “Ah, bad dog!” said she.

[028-056] And then she went away well satisfied with her revenge, and returned to

[028-057] her own people in the Shi.
