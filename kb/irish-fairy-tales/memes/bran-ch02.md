[027-001] When Iollan and [[tuiren|Tuiren]] were married they went to [[ulster|Ulster]], and they lived

[027-002] together very happily. But the law of life is change; nothing

[027-003] continues in the same way for any length of time; happiness must become

[027-004] unhappiness, and will be succeeded again by the joy it had displaced.

[027-005] The past also must be reckoned with; it is seldom as far behind us as we

[027-006] could wish: it is more often in front, blocking the way, and the future

[027-007] trips over it just when we think that the road is clear and joy our own.

[027-008] Iollan had a past. He was not ashamed of it; he merely thought it

[027-009] was finished, although in truth it was only beginning, for it is that

[027-010] perpetual beginning of the past that we call the future.

[027-011] Before he joined the [[fianna|Fianna]] he had been in love with a lady of the Shi’,

[027-012] named Uct Dealv (Fair Breast), and they had been sweethearts for years.

[027-013] How often he had visited his sweetheart in [[faery|Faery]]! With what eagerness

[027-014] and anticipation he had gone there; the lover’s whistle that he used to

[027-015] give was known to every person in that Shi’, and he had been discussed

[027-016] by more than one of the delicate sweet ladies of Faery. “That is your

[027-017] whistle, Fair Breast,” her sister of the Shi’ would say.

[027-018] And Uct Dealv would reply: “Yes, that is my mortal, my lover, my pulse,

[027-019] and my one treasure.”

[027-020] She laid her spinning aside, or her embroidery if she was at that, or if

[027-021] she were baking a cake of fine wheaten bread mixed with honey she would

[027-022] leave the cake to bake itself and fly to Iollan. Then they went hand in

[027-023] hand in the country that smells of apple-blossom and honey, looking on

[027-024] heavy-boughed trees and on dancing and beaming clouds. Or they stood

[027-025] dreaming together, locked in a clasping of arms and eyes, gazing up

[027-026] and down on each other, Iollan staring down into sweet grey wells that

[027-027] peeped and flickered under thin brows, and Uct Dealv looking up into

[027-028] great black ones that went dreamy and went hot in endless alternation.

[027-029] Then Iollan would go back to the world of men, and Uct Dealv would

[027-030] return to her occupations in the Land of the Ever Young.

[027-031] “What did he say?” her sister of the Shi’ would ask.

[027-032] “He said I was the Berry of the Mountain, the Star of Knowledge, and the

[027-033] Blossom of the Raspberry.”

[027-034] “They always say the same thing,” her sister pouted.

[027-035] “But they look other things,” Uct Dealv insisted. “They feel other

[027-036] things,” she murmured; and an endless conversation recommenced.

[027-037] Then for some time Iollan did not come to Faery, and Uct Dealv marvelled

[027-038] at that, while her sister made an hundred surmises, each one worse than

[027-039] the last.

[027-040] “He is not dead or he would be here,” she said. “He has forgotten you,

[027-041] my darling.”

[027-042] News was brought to Tlr na n-Og of the marriage of Iollan and Tuiren,

[027-043] and when Uct Dealv heard that news her heart ceased to beat for a

[027-044] moment, and she closed her eyes.

[027-045] “Now!” said her sister of the Shi’. “That is how long the love of a

[027-046] mortal lasts,” she added, in the voice of sad triumph which is proper to

[027-047] sisters.

[027-048] But on Uct Dealv there came a rage of jealousy and despair such as no

[027-049] person in the Shi’ had ever heard of, and from that moment she

[027-050] became capable of every ill deed; for there are two things not easily

[027-051] controlled, and they are hunger and jealousy. She determined that the

[027-052] woman who had supplanted her in Iollan’s affections should rue the day

[027-053] she did it. She pondered and brooded revenge in her heart, sitting in

[027-054] thoughtful solitude and bitter collectedness until at last she had a

[027-055] plan.

[027-056] She understood the arts of [[enchantment|magic]] and [[shapeshifting|shape-changing]], so she changed

[027-057] her shape into that of Fionn’s female runner, the best-known woman in

[027-058] Ireland; then she set out from Faery and appeared in the world. She

[027-059] travelled in the direction of Iollan’s [[dun|stronghold]].

[027-060] Iollan knew the appearance of Fionn’s messenger, but he was surprised to

[027-061] see her.

[027-062] She saluted him.

[027-063] “Health and long life, my master.”.

[027-064] “Health and good days,” he replied. “What brings you here, dear heart?”

[027-065] “I come from Fionn.”

[027-066] “And your message?” said he.

[027-067] “The royal captain intends to visit you.”

[027-068] “He will be welcome,” said Iollan. “We shall give him an Ulster feast.”

[027-069] “The world knows what that is,” said the messenger courteously. “And

[027-070] now,” she continued, “I have messages for your queen.”

[027-071] Tuiren then walked from the house with the messenger, but when they had

[027-072] gone a short distance Uct Dealv drew a hazel rod from beneath her cloak

[027-073] and struck it on the queen’s shoulder, and on the instant Tuiren’s

[027-074] figure trembled and quivered, and it began to whirl inwards and

[027-075] downwards, and she changed into the appearance of a hound.

[027-076] It was sad to see the beautiful, slender dog standing shivering and

[027-077] astonished, and sad to see the lovely eyes that looked out pitifully

[027-078] in terror and amazement. But Uct Dealv did not feel sad. She clasped

[027-079] a chain about the hound’s neck, and they set off westward towards the

[027-080] house of [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus Fionnliath]], who was reputed to be the unfriendliest man

[027-081] in the world to a dog. It was because of his reputation that Uct Dealv

[027-082] was bringing the hound to him. She did not want a good home for this

[027-083] dog: she wanted the worst home that could be found in the world, and she

[027-084] thought that Fergus would revenge for her the rage and jealousy which

[027-085] she felt towards Tuiren.
