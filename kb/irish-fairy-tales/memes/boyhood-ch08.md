[019-001] Fionn went away, and now he was alone. But he was as fitted for

[019-002] loneliness as the crane is that haunts the solitudes and bleak wastes

[019-003] of the sea; for the man with a thought has a comrade, and Fionn’s mind

[019-004] worked as featly as his body did. To be alone was no trouble to him who,

[019-005] however surrounded, was to be lonely his life long; for this will be

[019-006] said of Fionn when all is said, that all that came to him went from him,

[019-007] and that happiness was never his companion for more than a moment.

[019-008] But he was not now looking for loneliness. He was seeking the

[019-009] instruction of a crowd, and therefore when he met a crowd he went into

[019-010] it. His eyes were skilled to observe in the moving dusk and dapple of

[019-011] green woods. They were trained to pick out of shadows birds that were

[019-012] themselves [[dun]]-coloured shades, and to see among trees the animals that

[019-013] are coloured like the bark of trees. The hare crouching in the fronds

[019-014] was visible to him, and the fish that swayed in-visibly in the sway and

[019-015] flicker of a green bank. He would see all that was to be seen, and he

[019-016] would see all that is passed by the eye that is half blind from use and

[019-017] wont.

[019-018] At Moy Life’ he came on lads swimming in a pool; and, as he looked

[019-019] on them sporting in the flush tide, he thought that the tricks they

[019-020] performed were not hard for him, and that he could have shown them new

[019-021] ones.

[019-022] Boys must know what another boy can do, and they will match themselves

[019-023] against everything. They did their best under these observing eyes, and

[019-024] it was not long until he was invited to compete with them and show his

[019-025] mettle. Such an invitation is a challenge; it is almost, among boys, a

[019-026] declaration of war. But Fionn was so far beyond them in swimming that

[019-027] even the word master did not apply to that superiority.

[019-028] While he was swimming one remarked: “He is fair and well shaped,” and

[019-029] thereafter he was called “Fionn” or [[fionn-mac-uail|the Fair One]]. His name came from

[019-030] boys, and will, perhaps, be preserved by them.

[019-031] He stayed with these lads for some time, and it may be that they

[019-032] idolised him at first, for it is the way with boys to be astounded and

[019-033] enraptured by feats; but in the end, and that was inevitable, they grew

[019-034] jealous of the stranger. Those who had been the champions before he came

[019-035] would marshal each other, and, by social pressure, would muster all the

[019-036] others against him; so that in the end not a friendly eye was turned on

[019-037] Fionn in that assembly. For not only did he beat them at swimming, he

[019-038] beat their best at running and jumping, and when the sport degenerated

[019-039] into violence, as it was bound to, the roughness of Fionn would be ten

[019-040] times as rough as the roughness of the roughest rough they could put

[019-041] forward. Bravery is pride when one is young, and Fionn was proud.

[019-042] There must have been anger in his mind as he went away leaving that lake

[019-043] behind him, and those snarling and scowling boys, but there would have

[019-044] been disappointment also, for his desire at this time should have been

[019-045] towards friendliness.

[019-046] He went thence to Lock Le’in and took service with the King of

[019-047] Finntraigh. That kingdom may have been thus called from Fionn himself

[019-048] and would have been known by another name when he arrived there.

[019-049] He hunted for the King of Finntraigh, and it soon grew evident that

[019-050] there was no hunter in his service to equal Fionn. More, there was no

[019-051] hunter of them all who even distantly approached him in excellence. The

[019-052] others ran after deer, using the speed of their legs, the noses of their

[019-053] dogs and a thousand well-worn tricks to bring them within reach, and,

[019-054] often enough, the animal escaped them. But the deer that Fionn got the

[019-055] track of did not get away, and it seemed even that the animals sought

[019-056] him so many did he catch.

[019-057] The king marvelled at the stories that were told of this new hunter, but

[019-058] as kings are greater than other people so they are more curious; and,

[019-059] being on the plane of excellence, they must see all that is excellently

[019-060] told of.

[019-061] The king wished to see him, and Fionn must have wondered what the king

[019-062] thought as that gracious lord looked on him. Whatever was thought, what

[019-063] the king said was as direct in utterance as it was in observation.

[019-064] “If Uail the son of Baiscne has a son,” said the king, “you would surely

[019-065] be that son.”

[019-066] We are not told if the King of Finntraigh said anything more, but we

[019-067] know that Fionn left his service soon afterwards.

[019-068] He went southwards and was next in the employment of the King of [[kerry|Kerry]],

[019-069] the same lord who had married his own mother. In that service he came to

[019-070] such consideration that we hear of him as playing a match of chess with

[019-071] the king, and by this game we know that he was still a boy in his mind

[019-072] however mightily his limbs were spreading. Able as he was in sports and

[019-073] huntings, he was yet too young to be politic, but he remained impolitic

[019-074] to the end of his days, for whatever he was able to do he would do, no

[019-075] matter who was offended thereat; and whatever he was not able to do he

[019-076] would do also. That was Fionn.

[019-077] Once, as they rested on a chase, a debate arose among the [[fianna|Fianna-Finn]] as

[019-078] to what was the finest music in the world.

[019-079] “Tell us that,” said Fionn turning to Oisi’n [pronounced Usheen]

[019-080] “The cuckoo calling from the tree that is highest in the hedge,” cried

[019-081] his merry son.

[019-082] “A good sound,” said Fionn. “And you, [[oscar|Oscar]],” he asked, “what is to your

[019-083] mind the finest of music?”

[019-084] “The top of music is the ring of a spear on a shield,” cried the stout

[019-085] lad.

[019-086] “It is a good sound,” said Fionn. And the other champions told their

[019-087] delight; the belling of a stag across water, the baying of a tuneful

[019-088] pack heard in the distance, the song of a lark, the laugh of a gleeful

[019-089] girl, or the whisper of a moved one.

[019-090] “They are good sounds all,” said Fionn.

[019-091] “Tell us, chief,” one ventured, “what you think?”

[019-092] “The music of what happens,” said great Fionn, “that is the finest music

[019-093] in the world.”

[019-094] He loved “what happened,” and would not evade it by the swerve of

[019-095] a hair; so on this occasion what was occurring he would have occur,

[019-096] although a king was his rival and his master. It may be that his mother

[019-097] was watching the match and that he could not but exhibit his skill

[019-098] before her. He committed the enormity of winning seven games in

[019-099] succession from the king himself!!!

[019-100] It is seldom indeed that a subject can beat a king at chess, and this

[019-101] monarch was properly amazed.

[019-102] “Who are you at all?” he cried, starting back from the chessboard and

[019-103] staring on Fionn.

[019-104] “I am the son of a countryman of the Luigne of [[tara|Tara]],” said Fionn.

[019-105] He may have blushed as he said it, for the king, possibly for the first

[019-106] time, was really looking at him, and was looking back through twenty

[019-107] years of time as he did so. The observation of a king is faultless--it

[019-108] is proved a thousand times over in the tales, and this king’s equipment

[019-109] was as royal as the next.

[019-110] “You are no such son,” said the indignant monarch, “but you are the son

[019-111] that [[muirne|Muirne]] my wife bore to Uall mac Balscne.”

[019-112] And at that Fionn had no more to say; but his eyes may have flown to his

[019-113] mother and stayed there.

[019-114] “You cannot remain here,” his step-father continued. “I do not want you

[019-115] killed under my protection,” he explained, or complained.

[019-116] Perhaps it was on Fionn’s account he dreaded the [[clann-morna|sons of Morna]], but no

[019-117] one knows what Fionn thought of him for he never thereafter spoke of his

[019-118] step-father. As for Muirne she must have loved her lord; or she may have

[019-119] been terrified in truth of the sons of Morna and for Fionn; but it is so

[019-120] also, that if a woman loves her second husband she can dislike all that

[019-121] reminds her of the first one. Fionn went on his travels again.
