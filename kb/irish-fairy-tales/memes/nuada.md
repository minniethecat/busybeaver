## Overview

Nuada is a major figure of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]], appearing in the
Stephens corpus as the father of Teigue and grandfather of Ethlinn, making him
an ancestor of [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]. The single corpus reference — that
Ethlinn's father Teigue was son of "Nuada from Faery" — places him among the
highest tier of Irish divine genealogy [012-016]. Beyond the corpus, Nuada is
one of the most significant gods in Irish mythology: the first king of the
Tuatha Dé Danann, famed for the silver hand that gave him his epithet.

## Identity

In the corpus, Nuada is described as dwelling "from [[faery|Faery]]" — the
[[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] of the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]]. This places him among the ancient
divine race who retreated into the fairy mounds after their defeat by the
Milesians. The text traces the lineage: Teigue, son of Nuada, fathered Ethlinn;
Ethlinn bore [[becuma|Becuma]]; and Ethlinn's brother was [[lugh|Lugh of the Long Hand]],
"a god, and such a god" [012-016]. Nuada thus stands at the apex of a divine
lineage connecting the Otherworld to the mortal realm.

In the broader Irish mythological tradition, Nuada is Nuada Airgetlám — Nuada
of the Silver Hand. He lost his right hand in the First Battle of Mag Tuired
against the Fir Bolg. Because a king of the Tuatha Dé Danann had to be
physically unblemished, Nuada was temporarily deposed and replaced by the
half-Fomorian Bres. The physician Dian Cécht crafted a fully functioning silver
hand for Nuada, and with this prosthesis — a marvel that satisfied the
requirement of physical perfection — Nuada reclaimed his throne. This episode
is not recounted in the Stephens corpus but is fundamental to Nuada's identity
in the broader mythological landscape.

## In the Tales

Nuada appears in the corpus only through genealogical reference, in the passage
that introduces [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]. When Becuma is named, her
ancestry is traced upward: Becuma is the daughter of Ethlinn, who is the
daughter of Teigue, who is the son of Nuada from [[faery|Faery]]. This lineage
establishes Becuma's divine heritage and explains her connection to the
[[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] — a connection central to the plot of her tale.
The casual manner of the reference suggests that the audience was expected to
recognize Nuada's name without further explanation, testifying to his enduring
presence in Irish oral tradition [012-016].

## Significance

That Nuada receives only a single direct mention in the Stephens corpus does
not diminish his importance to the mythological world the tales inhabit. By
naming Nuada as an ancestor of Becuma, the text anchors her story — a
relatively late Fenian-era tale of exile and supernatural intrigue — in the deep
genealogy of the Tuatha Dé Danann. The reference performs a kind of
mythological anchoring: it connects the mortal conflicts of Becuma's present to
the ancient divine history from which all Irish nobility claimed descent. Nuada
is thus a presence felt through absence — the silent patriarch whose name
alone carries the weight of an entire mythological cycle.
