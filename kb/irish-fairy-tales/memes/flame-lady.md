# Flame Lady

## Identity

The Flame Lady, whose true name was Bro'tiarna, was the wife of [[mongan|Mongan]], the King of Ulster and son of [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannán mac Lir]]. She earned her epithet from two qualities: the sudden flush of blood that would flood to her cheek, transforming her from a lily to a rose in an instant; and the passionate, fierce nature with which she loved her husband. "She was passionate and fierce, and because the blood would flood suddenly to her cheek, so that she who had seemed a lily became, while you looked upon her, a rose, she was called Flame Lady." [^1]

## In the Tales

### The Queen at Court

The Flame Lady appears throughout *Mongan's Frenzy*, the longest and most complex of the Irish fairy tales in the Stephens corpus. As Mongan's queen, she is present at court gatherings and participates in the storytelling sessions that form the narrative frame of the tale. When Mongan's storyteller [[cairide|Cairide]] relates the history of [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]], the Flame Lady sits by her husband's side, listening intently.

Her presence in these scenes is not merely decorative. She is an active participant in the court's life, pressing Mongan to continue the tale of Duv Laca when he falls silent, and reacting with visible emotion to the story's twists. "You have not told me one word about Duv Laca," she reproaches him at one point, her impatience flaring with characteristic heat. [^1]

### The Journey to Faery

When Mongan and his companions entered [[faery|Faery]] — the Otherworld realm of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]] — the Flame Lady accompanied him. Upon crossing the threshold, she asked with wonder, "Are we in Faery?" [^1] The question captures her mortal perspective: unlike the Sidhe-born figures who move freely between worlds, the Flame Lady experiences the Otherworld as strange and marvellous, a place of enchantment that draws forth her awe even as she remains grounded in her fierce, human passion.

## The Name and the Nature

Bro'tiarna — her true name — has been variously translated and glossed. The element *bro'* may derive from Old Irish *brú* ("belly, breast, womb"), suggesting fertility and abundance, while *tiarna* is transparently "lord" or "lady." The compound evokes a figure of embodied power — not merely titled nobility but a person whose authority is written into her flesh. The epithet "Flame Lady" translates this corporeal quality into imagery: the blood that floods her cheek is not a blush of modesty but a visible sign of the passion that governs her.

This fusion of name and body distinguishes her from other queens in the Irish corpus. [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]] is defined by the colour of her flesh — white, supernatural, untouched — marking her as a being from outside the mortal order. The Flame Lady, by contrast, is defined by the *change* in her flesh: the sudden transformation from lily to rose that happens before the watcher's eyes. Becuma is static whiteness; the Flame Lady is dynamic heat. One belongs to the Otherworld of fixed, eternal forms; the other to the mortal world of passion, change, and time. Her epithet is thus not merely descriptive — it is argumentative, staking a claim about the nature of mortality itself.

## Her Legacy in the Tale

The Flame Lady's role in *Mongan's Frenzy* is often read as ancillary — the wife who listens while the men tell tales — but this misreads her narrative function. It is she who demands that the story continue when Mongan falls silent: "You have not told me one word about Duv Laca" [081-003]. Her impatience is the engine that drives the narrative forward, and her visible reactions — the flushing cheek, the sharp question, the fierce attention — model for the reader how the tale should be received. She is the internal audience, and her emotional engagement authorizes our own.

The flame imagery carries a further implication. Fire in Irish myth is an element of transformation and passage — it is the barrier "that must be passed ere the Land of Wonder is attained" — and the Flame Lady, who bears fire in her very name, is herself a threshold figure. Her love for Mongan is described as ecstatic and abandoned [025-058], suggesting that through her, Mongan accesses a mortal intensity that his divine heritage — as son of [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannán mac Lir]] — might otherwise exclude. She is the fire through which he passes into full humanity.

## Significance

The Flame Lady's epithet — derived from the visible flush of her skin — embodies a central tension in her character: she is at once queenly and wild, capable of the decorum required of a king's wife and of the fierce, ungovernable emotion that makes her memorable. She loved Mongan "with ecstasy and abandon, and for that also he called her Flame Lady." [^1] In a corpus filled with supernatural women — Sidhe lovers, transformed fawns, exiled goddesses — the Flame Lady stands apart as a mortal woman whose supernatural quality lies not in magic but in the intensity of her feeling.
