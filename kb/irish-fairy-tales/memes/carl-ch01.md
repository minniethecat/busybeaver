[046-001] One day something happened to Fionn, the son of Uail; that is, he

[046-002] departed from the world of men, and was set wandering in great distress

[046-003] of mind through [[faery|Faery]]. He had days and nights there and adventures

[046-004] there, and was able to bring back the memory of these.

[046-005] That, by itself, is wonderful, for there are few people who remember

[046-006] that they have been to Faery or aught of all that happened to them in

[046-007] that state.

[046-008] In truth we do not go to Faery, we become Faery, and in the beating of a

[046-009] pulse we may live for a year or a thousand years. But when we return

[046-010] the memory is quickly clouded, and we seem to have had a dream or seen a

[046-011] vision, although we have verily been in Faery.

[046-012] It was wonderful, then, that Fionn should have remembered all that

[046-013] happened to him in that wide-spun moment, but in this tale there is yet

[046-014] more to marvel at; for not only did Fionn go to Faery, but the great

[046-015] army which he had marshalled to [[ben-edair|Ben Edair]] [The Hill of Howth] were

[046-016] translated also, and neither he nor they were aware that they had

[046-017] departed from the world until they came back to it.

[046-018] Fourteen battles, seven of the reserve and seven of the regular [[fianna|Fianna]],

[046-019] had been taken by the Chief on a great march and manoeuvre. When they

[046-020] reached Ben Edair it was decided to pitch camp so that the troops

[046-021] might rest in view of the warlike plan which Fionn had imagined for the

[046-022] morrow. The camp was chosen, and each squadron and company of the host

[046-023] were lodged into an appropriate place, so there was no overcrowding and

[046-024] no halt or interruption of the march; for where a company halted that

[046-025] was its place of rest, and in that place it hindered no other company,

[046-026] and was at its own ease.

[046-027] When this was accomplished the leaders of battalions gathered on a

[046-028] level, grassy plateau overlooking the sea, where a consultation began

[046-029] as to the next day’s manoeuvres, and during this discussion they looked

[046-030] often on the wide water that lay wrinkling and twinkling below them.

[046-031] A roomy ship under great press of sall was bearing on Ben Edair from the

[046-032] east.

[046-033] Now and again, in a lull of the discussion, a champion would look and

[046-034] remark on the hurrying vessel; and it may have been during one of these

[046-035] moments that the adventure happened to Fionn and the Fianna.

[046-036] “I wonder where that ship comes from?” said Cona’n idly.

[046-037] But no person could surmise anything about it beyond that it was a

[046-038] vessel well equipped for war.

[046-039] As the ship drew by the shore the watchers observed a tall man swing

[046-040] from the side by means of his spear shafts, and in a little while this

[046-041] gentleman was announced to Fionn, and was brought into his presence.

[046-042] A sturdy, bellicose, forthright personage he was indeed. He was equipped

[046-043] in a wonderful solidity of armour, with a hard, carven helmet on

[046-044] his head, a splendid red-bossed shield swinging on his shoulder, a

[046-045] wide-grooved, straight sword clashing along his thigh. On his shoulders

[046-046] under the shield he carried a splendid scarlet mantle; over his breast

[046-047] was a great brooch of burnt gold, and in his fist he gripped a pair of

[046-048] thick-shafted, unburnished spears.

[046-049] Fionn and the champions looked on this gentleman, and they admired

[046-050] exceedingly his bearing and equipment.

[046-051] “Of what blood are you, young gentleman?” Fionn demanded, “and from

[046-052] which of the four corners of the world do you come?”

[046-053] “My name is [[cael|Cael of the Iron]],” the stranger answered, “and I am son to

[046-054] the King of Thessaly.”

[046-055] “What errand has brought you here?”

[046-056] “I do not go on errands,” the man replied sternly, “but on the affairs

[046-057] that please me.”

[046-058] “Be it so. What is the pleasing affair which brings you to this land?”

[046-059] “Since I left my own country I have not gone from a land or an island

[046-060] until it paid tribute to me and acknowledged my lordship.”

[046-061] “And you have come to this realm,” cried Fionn, doubting his ears.

[046-062] “For tribute and sovereignty,” growled that other, and he struck the

[046-063] haft of his spear violently on the ground.

[046-064] “By my hand,” said Cona’n, “we have never heard of a warrior, however

[046-065] great, but his peer was found in Ireland, and the funeral songs of all

[046-066] such have been chanted by the women of this land.”

[046-067] “By my hand and word,” said the harsh stranger, “your talk makes me

[046-068] think of a small boy or of an idiot.”

[046-069] “Take heed, sir,” said Fionn, “for the champions and great dragons of

[046-070] the Gael are standing by you, and around us there are fourteen battles

[046-071] of the Fianna of Ireland.”

[046-072] “If all the Fianna who have died in the last seven years were added to

[046-073] all that are now here,” the stranger asserted, “I would treat all of

[046-074] these and those grievously, and would curtail their limbs and their

[046-075] lives.”

[046-076] “It is no small boast,” Cona’n murmured, staring at him.

[046-077] “It is no boast at all,” said Cael, “and, to show my quality and

[046-078] standing, I will propose a deed to you.”

[046-079] “Give out your deed,” Fionn commanded.

[046-080] “Thus,” said Cael with cold savagery. “If you can find a man among your

[046-081] fourteen battalions who can outrun or outwrestle or outfight me, I will

[046-082] take myself off to my own country, and will trouble you no more.”

[046-083] And so harshly did he speak, and with such a belligerent eye did he

[046-084] stare, that dismay began to seize on the champions, and even Fionn felt

[046-085] that his breath had halted.

[046-086] “It is spoken like a hero,” he admitted after a moment, “and if you

[046-087] cannot be matched on those terms it will not be from a dearth of

[046-088] applicants.”

[046-089] “In running alone,” Fionn continued thoughtfully, “we have a notable

[046-090] champion, Caelte mac Rona’n.”

[046-091] “This son of Rona’n will not long be notable,” the stranger asserted.

[046-092] “He can outstrip the red deer,” said Cona’n.

[046-093] “He can outrun the wind,” cried Fionn.

[046-094] “He will not be asked to outrun the red deer or the wind,” the stranger

[046-095] sneered. “He will be asked to outrun me,” he thundered. “Produce this

[046-096] runner, and we shall discover if he keeps as great heart in his feet as

[046-097] he has made you think.”

[046-098] “He is not with us,” Cona’n lamented.

[046-099] “These notable warriors are never with us when the call is made,” said

[046-100] the grim stranger.

[046-101] “By my hand,” cried Fionn, “he shall be here in no great time, for I

[046-102] will fetch him myself.”

[046-103] “Be it so,” said Cael. “And during my absence,” Fionn continued, “I

[046-104] leave this as a compact, that you make friends with the Fianna here

[046-105] present, and that you observe all the conditions and ceremonies of

[046-106] friendship.”

[046-107] Cael agreed to that.

[046-108] “I will not hurt any of these people until you return,” he said.

[046-109] Fionn then set out towards [[tara|Tara of the Kings]], for he thought Caelte mac

[046-110] Romin would surely be there; “and if he is not there,” said the champion

[046-111] to himself, “then I shall find him at [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]] of the Fianna.”
