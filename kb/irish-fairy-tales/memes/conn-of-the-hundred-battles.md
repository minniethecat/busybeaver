## Overview

Conn of the Hundred Battles — Conn Cétchathach — is the High King of Ireland during the era of [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] and the [[fianna|Fianna]]. In James Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales*, he appears primarily in two contexts: as the monarch presiding over the [[feast-of-tara|Feast of Tara]] where Fionn comes of age, and as the heart of the dark tale *[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]*, where his judgment as king and father is tested by a Sidhe woman's arrival. Conn represents the mortal order of Irish kingship — wise but fallible, powerful but subject to forces beyond the purely political.

## Identity

Conn's title — "of the Hundred Battles" — is both a boast and a biography. He is a king born of warfare, whose authority rests on military triumph. Yet in Stephens' telling, Conn is most vivid not as a conqueror but as a man caught between duties: to his throne, to his son Art, and to the supernatural world that intrudes upon his rule.

> Conn the Hundred Fighter, Ard-Ri' of Ireland, was in the worst humour that could afflict a king. — [061-001]

This opening line of the Becuma tale situates Conn's power as inseparable from his vulnerability. Grief over his deceased queen darkens his judgment, and the arrival of Becuma — a woman exiled from the Shi' for a crime — exploits this weakness with precision.

## At the Feast of Tara

Conn presides over the [[feast-of-tara|Feast of Tara]], the great gathering at which [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] first appears before the assembled nobility of Ireland. In this scene, Conn's manner is formal, kingly, and decisive:

> "Friends and heroes," said Conn, "Aillen, the son of Midna, will come tonight from the Shi' of Finnachy." — [023-026]

It is Conn who announces the supernatural threat — the fire-breathing Aillen — and Conn who offers the prize of [[fianna|Fianna]] captaincy to whoever can defeat the Sidhe attacker [023-026]. His kingship is defined by the intersection of mortal politics and fairy-tale danger.

When Fionn succeeds where others failed, Conn confirms the new order:

> "Make your choice," said Conn to Goll Mor; "you will leave Ireland or enter my service." — [025-085]

This moment solidifies the transfer of power from the clann-Morna to Fionn's new [[fianna|Fianna]]. Conn is the fulcrum: the law-giver whose word transforms individual victory into institutional change.

## Becuma of the White Skin

Conn's most complex role comes in *[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]*, where he becomes entangled with a woman from the Shi'. [[becuma|Becuma Cneisgel]] arrives from the fairy realm, exiled for a crime [060-027]–[060-033]. She presents herself to Conn as a potential queen, and Conn — still mourning his wife — is captivated:

> She told Conn that the fame of his son Art had reached even the Land of the Shi' [062-013]

| Aspect | Conn's Response |
|--------|----------------|
| Becuma's beauty | "Conn had been regarding her with the deepest attention" [062-023] |
| His wife's memory | "It is not right that I should marry the second time" [062-039] |
| Becuma's demand | "You must choose," Conn answered [062-039] |
| The marriage | He marries Becuma, bringing calamity upon Ireland |

His union with Becuma proves disastrous. The land withers; the harvest fails. Becuma's jealousy of Art, Conn's son, drives her to demand the prince's exile. Conn, caught between love and duty, places the fateful chess-game wager that will determine Art's future:

> "I have been informed," Conn explained, "that if the son of a king should win a game of chess against Becuma, she would be bound by that." — [065-038]

Conn's hope is that Art will win and break Becuma's power. But his decision-making throughout the episode reveals a king who cannot quite master the forces — Sidhe [[enchantment|magic]], feminine enchantment, his own grief — that swirl around his throne.

## Significance

Conn of the Hundred Battles embodies the mortal kingship that stands between the heroic age of the [[fianna|Fianna]] and the older, stranger world of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]] and the Shi'. In Stephens' retelling, Conn is not a remote figurehead but a man whose private sorrows become public catastrophes. His grief for his queen opens the door to Becuma; his love for his son Art cannot fully undo the damage. The image of Conn summoning his [[enchantment|magic]]ians and poets to divine a solution [066-001] captures the limits of even the highest mortal authority in a world where "there is the life and the other life" [060-015].
