[040-001] But after some time the moon arose and the wolves went away, for their

[040-002] leader, a sagacious and crafty chief, declared that as long as they

[040-003] remained where they were, the lady would remain where she was; and so,

[040-004] with a hearty curse on trees, the troop departed. [[becfola|Becfola]] had pains in

[040-005] her legs from the way she had wrapped them about the branch, but there

[040-006] was no part of her that did not ache, for a lady does not sit with any

[040-007] ease upon a tree.

[040-008] For some time she did not care to come down from the branch. “Those

[040-009] wolves may return,” she said, “for their chief is crafty and sagacious,

[040-010] and it is certain, from the look I caught in his eye as he departed,

[040-011] that he would rather taste of me than cat any woman he has met.”

[040-012] She looked carefully in every direction to see if one might discover

[040-013] them in hiding; she looked closely and lingeringly at the shadows under

[040-014] distant trees to see if these shadows moved; and she listened on every

[040-015] wind to try if she could distinguish a yap or a yawn or a sneeze. But

[040-016] she saw or heard nothing; and little by little tranquillity crept into

[040-017] her mind, and she began to consider that a danger which is past is a

[040-018] danger that may be neglected.

[040-019] Yet ere she descended she looked again on the world of jet and silver

[040-020] that dozed about her, and she spied a red glimmer among distant trees.

[040-021] “There is no danger where there is light,” she said, and she thereupon

[040-022] came from the tree and ran in the direction that she had noted.

[040-023] In a spot between three great oaks she came upon a man who was roasting

[040-024] a wild boar over a fire. She saluted this youth and sat beside him. But

[040-025] after the first glance and greeting he did not look at her again, nor

[040-026] did he speak.

[040-027] When the boar was cooked he ate of it and she had her share. Then he

[040-028] arose from the fire and walked away among the trees. Becfola followed,

[040-029] feeling ruefully that something new to her experience had arrived;

[040-030] “for,” she thought, “it is usual that young men should not speak to me

[040-031] now that I am the mate of a king, but it is very unusual that young men

[040-032] should not look at me.”

[040-033] But if the young man did not look at her she looked well at him, and

[040-034] what she saw pleased her so much that she had no time for further

[040-035] cogitation. For if Crimthann had been beautiful, this youth was ten

[040-036] times more beautiful. The curls on Crimthann’s head had been indeed as

[040-037] a benediction to the queen’s eye, so that she had eaten the better and

[040-038] slept the sounder for seeing him. But the sight of this youth left her

[040-039] without the desire to eat, and, as for sleep, she dreaded it, for if she

[040-040] closed an eye she would be robbed of the one delight in time, which was

[040-041] to look at this young man, and not to cease looking at him while her eye

[040-042] could peer or her head could remain upright.

[040-043] They came to an inlet of the sea all sweet and calm under the round,

[040-044] silver-flooding moon, and the young man, with Becfola treading on his

[040-045] heel, stepped into a boat and rowed to a high-jutting, pleasant island.

[040-046] There they went inland towards a vast palace, in which there was no

[040-047] person but themselves alone, and there the young man went to sleep,

[040-048] while Becfola sat staring at him until the unavoidable peace pressed

[040-049] down her eyelids and she too slumbered.

[040-050] She was awakened in the morning by a great shout.

[040-051] “Come out, Flann, come out, my heart!”

[040-052] The young man leaped from his couch, girded on his harness, and strode

[040-053] out. Three young men met him, each in battle harness, and these four

[040-054] advanced to meet four other men who awaited them at a little distance on

[040-055] the lawn. Then these two sets of four fought togethor with every warlike

[040-056] courtesy but with every warlike severity, and at the end of that combat

[040-057] there was but one man standing, and the other seven lay tossed in death.

[040-058] Becfola spoke to the youth.

[040-059] “Your combat has indeed been gallant,” she said.

[040-060] “Alas,” he replied, “if it has been a gallant deed it has not been a

[040-061] good one, for my three brothers are dead and my four nephews are dead.”

[040-062] “Ah me!” cried Becfola, “why did you fight that fight?”

[040-063] “For the lordship of this island, the Isle of Fedach, son of Dali.”

[040-064] But, although Becfola was moved and horrified by this battle, it was in

[040-065] another direction that her interest lay; therefore she soon asked the

[040-066] question which lay next her heart:

[040-067] “Why would you not speak to me or look at me?”

[040-068] “Until I have won the kingship of this land from all claimants, I am no

[040-069] match for the mate of the High King of Ireland,” he replied.

[040-070] And that reply was llke balm to the heart of Becfola.

[040-071] “What shall I do?” she inquired radiantly. “Return to your home,” he

[040-072] counselled. “I will escort you there with your maid, for she is not

[040-073] really dead, and when I have won my lordship I will go seek you in

[040-074] [[tara|Tara]].”

[040-075] “You will surely come,” she insisted.

[040-076] “By my hand,” quoth he, “I will come.”

[040-077] These three returned then, and at the end of a day and night they saw

[040-078] far off the mighty roofs of Tara massed in the morning haze. The

[040-079] young man left them, and with many a backward look and with dragging,

[040-080] reluctant feet, Becfola crossed the threshold of the palace, wondering

[040-081] what she should say to Dermod and how she could account for an absence

[040-082] of three days’ duration.
