## Identity

Angus Og, son of the Dagda, is the Tuatha De Danann deity of love, youth, and beauty in Irish
mythology. His epithet "Og" means "young," reflecting his eternal youthfulness. He is
associated with the Brugh of the Boyne, a great neolithic passage tomb and one of the most
sacred sites in ancient Ireland, over which he presides as chief and god.

In the mythological hierarchy, Angus Og holds dominion over the Brugh na Boinne
(Newgrange), the most renowned of the three great burying-places of Ireland. The other two
are the Shi' mound of Cruachan Ahi in Connacht, ruled by [[ethal-anbual|Ethal Anbual]],
and Tailltin in Royal [[meath|Meath]], the sacred place where the High King Conn laid
his wife Eithne to rest after her death [061-013].

## In the Tales

### The Three Great Burying-Places

The Brugh of the [[boyne|Boyne]] in [[ulster|Ulster]], over which Angus Og is chief and god,
was one of the three great burying-places of Ireland. When Eithne, wife of Conn of the
Hundred Battles, died, she was laid to rest at Tailltin in Royal Meath rather than at the
Brugh, for Tailltin was Conn's own sacred lordship. Her funeral games lasted nine days,
and poets and harpers sang her keen as a cairn ten acres wide was heaped over her clay
[061-013].

### Becuma's Rejection

When Becuma was banished from the [[faery|Many-Coloured Land]] for an undisclosed
transgression, she traveled from [[tara|Tara]] northward and eastward seeking refuge.
She came to "the dewy, sparkling Brugh of Angus mac an Og in [[ulster|Ulster]], but
she was not admitted there" [067-060]. The Brugh, under Angus's authority, remained
closed to her — a significant rejection, for the Tuatha De Danann lords were under no
obligation to shelter one who had been exiled from the Land of Promise.

Denied entry by Angus, Becuma went instead to the Shi' ruled by Eogabal. Though
Eogabal himself would not admit her, his daughter Aine', who was Becuma's
foster-sister, granted her passage into [[faery|Faery]] [067-060].

### The Final Banishment

After Becuma's schemes in Ireland were uncovered — she had brought misfortune upon
the land and seduced the High King's son — she faced a final judgment. She was
forbidden entry to the Shi' by Angus Og himself, and could not remain in Ireland
[069-123]. With the Many-Coloured Land and all the Shi' mounds barred to her, and
Ireland no longer a refuge, Becuma crossed the sea to Sasana (England), where she
became a queen and fostered a lasting enmity against the Holy Land.

## Through Others' Eyes

Angus Og, though he speaks no line of dialogue in the tales, is witnessed and judged
by every character who approaches his threshold. These perspectives — the exile's,
the mortal ruler's, the foster-sister's — together form a composite portrait richer
than any single narrator could provide.

### Becuma's Perspective: The Silent Gatekeeper

For Becuma, Angus Og represents an implacable boundary. She arrives at his Brugh
fresh from banishment, still carrying the hope that her beauty and status will open
doors. But Angus does not even grant her a hearing — "she was not admitted there"
[067-060]. From Becuma's point of view, Angus is neither merciful nor cruel; he is
simply closed, a door that does not open. His silence is more devastating than
any pronouncement, for it offers no ground for appeal.

Later, after her exposure, Angus administers the final judgment: she is "forbidden
entry to the Shi'" [069-123]. Again, he offers no speech — only the fact of
exclusion. Becuma's experience of Angus is thus one of pure negation: she never
sees him, never hears him, never receives acknowledgment. He embodies the
absolute finality of Otherworld law.

### Aine's Contrast: Authority vs. Kinship

Aine, daughter of Eogabal, provides the counterpoint that illuminates Angus's
role by contrast. Where Angus — the official guardian of a great sidhe — refuses
Becuma entry as a matter of principle, Aine grants passage through personal
loyalty as Becuma's foster-sister [067-060]. This juxtaposition reveals the
tension in Irish supernatural law between formal authority (Angus's gatekeeping)
and the bonds of [[fosterage|fosterage]] (Aine's intervention). Both are
legitimate forces; they simply operate on different planes.

### The High King's Perspective: Shared Guardianship

Conn of the Hundred Battles, the Ard-Righ of Ireland, shares with Angus a type of
threshold authority. Both men are custodians of ancient sacred sites — Conn of
[[tara|Tara]], Angus of the Brugh — and both must decide whom to admit and whom
to refuse. When Conn takes Becuma as his wife, he opens a door that Angus kept
closed, and the consequence is the withering of Ireland's harvests. Had Conn
exercised Angus's severity, the land might have been spared. The parallel suggests
that gatekeeping, however harsh it appears, is sometimes the wiser course.

### How These Perspectives Converge

Taken together, these three views — the exile's rejection, the foster-sister's
counter-gesture, and the mortal king's contrasting choice — build a portrait of
Angus Og as the archetypal Otherworld guardian. He is not a personality-driven
character like [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]] or [[mongan|Mongan]]; he is a function
made divine: the implacable boundary between realms. Each person who encounters
that boundary sees something different in it — some see cruelty, some see law,
some see wisdom — but all are shaped by the encounter.

## Role and Significance

Angus Og's role in the tales is that of a guardian of sacred boundaries. As the chief
and god of the Brugh, he controls access to one of the most hallowed sites in Ireland.
His denial of entry to Becuma — both upon her initial arrival and after her schemes
were exposed — marks him as an arbiter of who may pass between the mortal realm and
the Otherworld domains of the Tuatha De Danann.

In the wider mythological tradition, Angus Og is known as a patron of lovers and a
trickster figure who uses his wits to secure what he desires. His most famous myth,
the winning of the Brugh from his father the Dagda through a clever play on words,
is not recounted in the Irish Fairy Tales corpus but is fundamental to his character
as a deity of youth and cunning.

### The Brugh as Otherworld Threshold

The Brugh of the Boyne is more than a burial place — it is a threshold between the
mortal world and the Otherworld. In Irish mythological geography, the Brugh na Boinne
(modern Newgrange) was understood as a *sidhe* mound, one of the entrances through
which the Tuatha De Danann passed when they retreated underground after the coming of
the Milesians. As chief and god of the Brugh, Angus Og is not merely a territorial lord
but a guardian of the boundary between realms — a gatekeeper who decides who may cross
and who must remain outside.

This threshold-guardian role illuminates the gravity of Becuma's rejection. When Angus
refused her entry, he was exercising more than hospitality: he was enforcing a cosmic
boundary. Becuma, having been exiled from the [[faery|Many-Coloured Land]], carried a
taint of transgression that no *sidhe* lord could permit to cross his threshold. The
Brugh's closure signified that her exile was absolute — neither the mortal realm
(Ard-Righ Conn's court at Tara) nor the Otherworld would properly receive her. Only
Aine', acting through personal loyalty rather than official authority, found a way to
grant her passage.

This pattern — a *sidhe* lord standing at the gate, deciding whether to admit or
refuse — recurs throughout Irish myth. The *sidhe* mounds were places where the
normal laws of space and time were suspended, where mortals might wander into the
Otherworld and return aged by centuries. To hold the keys to such a place was to
hold immense spiritual authority. Stephens' portrait of Angus Og — silent, serene,
and unbending in his refusals — reflects the weight of that responsibility, and the
stillness of a deity who has stood at the same threshold since before mortal memory
began.
