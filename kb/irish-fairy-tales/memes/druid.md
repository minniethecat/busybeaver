# Druid

## Overview

A member of the learned priestly class of pre-Christian Ireland. Druids appear in the tales as poets, prophets, and guardians — wielding both wisdom and [[enchantment|magic]]. The druidic role in Irish mythology combines several functions: teacher of sacred knowledge, keeper of [[prophecy]], practitioner of magical arts, and protector of those destined for greatness. Both men and women served as druids, and their authority was recognized even by those outside the tradition — when [[finnian|St. Finnian]] came to Tuan's stronghold, the pagan household described the Christian saint simply as "the new druid" [002-024].

Key druids in the corpus include [[finegas|Finegas]], the druid-poet who mentored [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] and pursued the [[salmon-of-knowledge|Salmon of Knowledge]] for seven years, and the women druids [[bovmall|Bovmall]] and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]], who guarded Fionn's childhood in the woods of [[slieve-bloom|Slieve Bloom]].

## Powers and Practices

Druids in the tales wield a range of abilities centered on knowledge, prophecy, and guardianship.

### Prophecy

The druidic power of [[prophecy]] is among the most significant. [[finegas|Finegas]] had received "a prophecy" that he would catch the [[salmon-of-knowledge|Salmon of Knowledge]] and live to eat it [020-030]. This foreknowledge drove him to spend seven years fishing [[boyne|the Boyne]], demonstrating how prophecy shaped a druid's entire life and purpose. The druidic understanding of prophecy acknowledged that a foretelling might be true yet incomplete — [[finegas|Finegas]] later reflected that "the prophecy, and that omission was the lie" when he realized the salmon's knowledge was destined for his pupil instead [021-090].

### Teaching and Poetic Wisdom

The druidic gift of [[bardic-tradition|poetry]] and wisdom is transmitted through teaching. [[finegas|Finegas]] instructed the young [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]] that "poetry is revealed to the mind" [020-015]. His method was rigorous and dialectical: Fionn had to answer his own questions first, and then [[finegas|Finegas]] would "take the matter up and make clear to him where the query was badly formed or at what point the answer had begun to go astray" [021-022]. Through this training, Fionn came to understand "by what successions a good question grows at last to a good answer" [021-022].

### Guardianship and Physical Training

Druids served as guardians and protectors. The women druids [[bovmall|Bovmall]] and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]] took the infant [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]] into the woods of [[slieve-bloom|Slieve Bloom]] to hide him from the clann-Morna, who had killed his father [012-004]. Their role combined physical protection with education, for "Fionn got his first training among women" who understood that "fighting is a necessary art" [012-004]. Only a blood-tie — [[bovmall|Bovmall]] was Uail's sister — could have bound them to "go hide with a baby in a wood; and to live, as they must have lived, in terror" [014-001].

The druidic training of the body was as rigorous as the training of the mind. The women druids "had switched his legs around a tree" to discipline young Fionn [021-014]. Later, [[finegas|Finegas]] continued this tradition: "as the druid women had switched his legs around a tree, so Finegas chased his mind, demanding sense in his questions and understanding in his replies" [021-014]. The parallel between physical and mental discipline — legs switched around a tree, mind chased through questions — reveals the druidic approach to education as whole and demanding.

## In the Tales

### Finegas: The Druid-Poet and the Salmon of Knowledge

The most prominent druid in the corpus is [[finegas|Finegas]], who lived "on a bank of [[boyne|the Boyne]] Water" [020-004]. He was "a poet, and so an honourable man" [020-010], who accepted the young [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]] as his pupil. [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]] "already venerated Finegas" for "his great learning, his poetic skill, for an hundred reasons" [020-048][020-049].

[[finegas|Finegas]] had spent seven years fishing the [[boyne|Boyne]] for the [[salmon-of-knowledge|Salmon of Knowledge]], sustained by a prophecy that he would catch and eat it [020-030]. When he finally caught the fabled fish, he entrusted its cooking to Fionn with a strict warning. But Fionn accidentally tasted a drop of the salmon's juices when he pressed down a blister on the cooking fish and put his burned thumb to his mouth.

When [[finegas|Finegas]] returned and saw the change in his pupil's face, he knew immediately what had happened. His response was remarkable for its grace:

> "A prophecy was made to me," Finegas said. "I was told that I should catch the Salmon of Knowledge, and that I should live to eat it" [020-030].

Despite seven years of waiting, [[finegas|Finegas]] did not rage against fate. "It was given to you," he told Fionn. "It was given to Fionn, the son of Uail. You shall have all the knowledge that was gathered for me. Why should you not take it?" [021-094]. After this, "he had received all that he could get from Finegas. His education was complete" [022-001].

### Bovmall and Lia Luachra: The Women Druids

The druidic tradition included women of considerable power. "These were the women druids, Bovmall and Lia Luachra" [012-004]. They served as Fionn's guardians and first teachers when his mother Muirne could not keep him, for fear of the clann-Morna who had killed Fionn's father Uail [012-004].

> "The women druids, his guardians, belonged to his father's people. Bovmall was Uail's sister, and, consequently, Fionn's aunt" [014-001].

They raised Fionn in the woods of [[slieve-bloom|Slieve Bloom]], teaching him survival and combat. Their methods were physical and demanding — they switched his legs around a tree as part of his training [021-014]. Through their protection, Fionn survived the years of danger that followed his father's death and grew strong enough to eventually claim his birthright.

### The "New Druid" in Tuan's Tale

In the tale of [[tuan-mac-cairill|Tuan Mac Cairill]], when [[finnian|St. Finnian]] came to Tuan's stronghold and sat fasting outside the door, the household servants repeatedly checked whether he had departed. Each time they reported back: "The new druid is still there" [002-024]. This usage reveals that by the time the tales are set — the transition from paganism to Christianity — "druid" had become the natural term for any figure wielding spiritual authority through supernatural means. The Christian saint, in the eyes of the pagan household, was simply a druid serving a different god. The servants understood his fasting as "a combat of the gods," a phrase that acknowledges both the old druidic framework and the new Christian power operating within it [002-024].
