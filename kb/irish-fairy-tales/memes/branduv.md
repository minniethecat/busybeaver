## Overview

Branduv mac Echach was the King of [[leinster|Leinster]] and the primary antagonist of
*[[mongan|Mongan's Frenzy]]*. A man of royal stature whose passions overwhelmed his
judgment, Branduv's obsessive love for [[duv-laca|Duv Laca of the White Hand]] set in
motion a chain of schemes and reversals that drove much of the tale's dramatic
action. Though a formidable king in his own right, he was repeatedly outwitted by
[[mongan|Mongan]] and his supernatural allies.

## Identity

Branduv was the son of Echach and ruled the province of Leinster as its king.
He presided over a court known for its hospitality, yet his character was marked
by intense and often reckless desire. When he encountered something — or someone
— he wanted, restraint abandoned him entirely. His dealings with [[mongan|Mongan]]
revealed a man whose royal pride made him vulnerable to humiliation, and whose
inability to see through deception left him perpetually one step behind his
adversary.

## In the Tales

### The King of Leinster

"The King of Leinster at that time was Branduv, the son of Echach" [079-014].
From his royal seat, Branduv wielded power over one of Ireland's great provinces,
receiving guests with open hospitality. His court was a place of welcome — until
desire transformed him into something less than a generous host.

### The Obsession with Duv Laca

Branduv's fate turned upon seeing [[duv-laca|Duv Laca of the White Hand]]. She was
"utterly bewitching to the eye; and when Branduv" beheld her, desire consumed
him [089-013]. He had loved her from afar for years, and upon learning her
identity, his passion became uncontrollable. When others described her beauty to
him, Branduv "nearly went mad with love and longing and jealousy" [089-047].

Confronting [[mongan|Mongan]], Branduv demanded that Duv Laca be given to him:
"I do ask for it, and you must not refuse a king's request" [089-064]. His
appeal to royal prerogative revealed the arrogance underlying his obsession —
he believed his kingship entitled him to another man's wife.

### Desperate Measures

Branduv's obsession drove him to increasingly reckless offers. "All that I have,
and all that every-body has," he declared [081-022], offering everything in his
power in pursuit of his desires. His desperation was matched only by his
credulity; in each negotiation with [[mongan|Mongan]], Branduv believed he had the
upper hand, only to discover too late that he had been manipulated.

When [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]] was brought to [[leinster|Leinster]], Branduv "came to the door
himself to welcome her" [089-006], exulting in what he believed was his
triumph. He did not recognize that the victory had been orchestrated to lead
him toward a greater defeat.

### A King Undone

In the end, Branduv was a hapless adversary — a king of genuine power who
could not match wits with [[mongan|Mongan]]'s supernatural cunning. When Mongan
arrived in disguise at the Leinster court, Branduv welcomed him without
suspicion. His final words in the tale capture his defeat: "I wish I didn't
see you" [089-090]. A king whose passions had promised everything delivered
only ruin.

## Significance

Branduv serves as the archetypal antagonist whose flaw is desire itself. He is
not evil in the way of a supernatural villain — he is a mortal king undone by
his own obsessions. His role in *Mongan's Frenzy* is structurally essential: he
provides the external threat that sets the tale's reversals in motion, yet each
of his schemes collapses against Mongan's resourcefulness. In the economy of
the narrative, Branduv is the foil who proves, by contrast, the superiority of
Mongan's otherworldly connections and quick intelligence over mere political
power.
