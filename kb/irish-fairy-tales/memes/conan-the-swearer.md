## Overview

Cona'n the Swearer was a warrior of the [[fianna|Fianna]], the legendary war-band led by
[[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] during the reign of Cormac mac Airt. His epithet, "the
Swearer," marks him as a man of emphatic speech whose oaths and
exclamations give voice to the common warrior's perspective when faced
with danger and the supernatural.

He is one of the named companions who accompanied Fionn to the cave of
[[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]] in what would become one of the Fianna's most
humiliating defeats — an episode where the heroes were ensnared not by
force of arms but by enchantment and guile.

## Character

Cona'n's defining trait is his readiness to voice despair when the
situation turns grim. In the hierarchical culture of the Fianna, where
stoicism and courage were prized above all, Cona'n's willingness to
utter a simple "Alas!" when hope faltered makes him a distinctly human
figure among legendary warriors. He does not hide his fear; he
articulates it, making him a relatable presence in tales otherwise
dominated by larger-than-life heroes.

His role illustrates an important aspect of Irish storytelling: the
inclusion of characters who react to supernatural terror as ordinary
people would, grounding the narrative in recognizable emotion.

## In the Tales

### The Cave of Cesh Corran

When Fionn and his hunting party came to the [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]] district in
northern Connacht, they encountered the three daughters of
[[conaran|Conaran son of Imidel]], king of the local [[tuatha-de-danann|sidhe]]. The daughters sat
spinning at the mouth of a cave, and Fionn's men were drawn to
investigate, suspecting nothing of their supernatural nature.

Once within reach of the enchanted threads, the warriors were bound and
rendered helpless. Cona'n was among those snared, his warrior's
strength useless against the Sidhe magic. The text records his presence
among the captured: he stood behind Fionn as the party was forced to
march deeper into the cave under the daughters' power [056-045].

When the full horror of their predicament became clear — the greatest
warriors of Ireland trussed like game birds by three spinning-women in
a cave — Cona'n gave his characteristic cry: "Alas!" [057-011]. This
single word, in the economy of Irish storytelling, conveys the
collective despair of the party.

### Rescue

The Fianna were eventually rescued by [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]], the one-eyed
warrior who arrived after the others had been captured. Where Fionn and
his companions — Cona'n among them — had been overcome by the daughters'
glamour, Goll's method was direct: he slew two of the daughters and
forced [[conaran|Conaran]] to release the captives. Cona'n and the others were
freed from the cave, their ordeal ended by the intervention of the one
warrior the enchantment could not touch.
