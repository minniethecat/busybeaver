[087-001] [[mongan|Mongan]] and his servant went home, and (for what pleasure is greater than

[087-002] that of memory exercised in conversation?) for a time the feeling of an

[087-003] adventure well accomplished kept him in some contentment. But at the

[087-004] end of a time that pleasure was worn out, and Mongan grew at first

[087-005] dispirited and then sullen, and after that as ill as he had been on the

[087-006] previous occasion. For he could not forget [[duv-laca|Duv Laca of the White Hand]],

[087-007] and he could not remember her without longing and despair.

[087-008] It was in the illness which comes from longing and despair that he sat

[087-009] one day looking on a world that was black although the sun shone, and

[087-010] that was lean and unwholesome although autumn fruits were heavy on the

[087-011] earth and the joys of harvest were about him.

[087-012] “Winter is in my heart,” quoth he, “and I am cold already.”

[087-013] He thought too that some day he would die, and the thought was not

[087-014] unpleasant, for one half of his life was away in the territories of the

[087-015] King of [[leinster|Leinster]], and the half that he kept in himself had no spice in

[087-016] it.

[087-017] He was thinking in this way when mac an Da’v came towards him over the

[087-018] lawn, and he noticed that mac an Da’v was walking like an old man.

[087-019] He took little slow steps, and he did not loosen his knees when he

[087-020] walked, so he went stiffly. One of his feet turned pitifully outwards,

[087-021] and the other turned lamentably in. His chest was pulled inwards, and

[087-022] his head was stuck outwards and hung down in the place where his chest

[087-023] should have been, and his arms were crooked in front of him with the

[087-024] hands turned wrongly, so that one palm was shown to the east of the

[087-025] world and the other one was turned to the west.

[087-026] “How goes it, mac an Da’v?” said the king.

[087-027] “Bad,” said mac an Da’v.

[087-028] “Is that the sun I see shining, my friend?” the king asked.

[087-029] “It may be the sun,” replied mac an Da’v, peering curiously at the

[087-030] golden radiance that dozed about them, “but maybe it’s a yellow fog.”

[087-031] “What is life at all?” said the king.

[087-032] “It is a weariness and a tiredness,” said mac an Da’v. “It is a long

[087-033] yawn without sleepiness. It is a bee, lost at midnight and buzzing on

[087-034] a pane. It is the noise made by a tied-up dog. It is nothing worth

[087-035] dreaming about. It is nothing at all.”

[087-036] “How well you explain my feelings about Duv Laca,” said the king.

[087-037] “I was thinking about my own lamb,” said mac an Da’v. “I was thinking

[087-038] about my own treasure, my cup of cheeriness, and the pulse of my heart.”

[087-039] And with that he burst into tears.

[087-040] “Alas!” said the king.

[087-041] “But,” sobbed mac an Da’v, “what right have I to complain? I am only

[087-042] the servant, and although I didn’t make any bargain with the King of

[087-043] Leinster or with any king of them all, yet my wife is gone away as if

[087-044] she was the consort of a potentate the same as Duv Laca is.”

[087-045] Mongan was sorry then for his servant, and he roused himself.

[087-046] “I am going to send you to Duv Laca.”

[087-047] “Where the one is the other will be,” cried mac an Da’v joyously.

[087-048] “Go,” said Mongan, “to Rath Descirt of Bregia; you know that place?”

[087-049] “As well as my tongue knows my teeth.”

[087-050] “Duv Laca is there; see her, and ask her what she wants me to do.”

[087-051] Mac an Da’v went there and returned.

[087-052] “Duv Laca says that you are to come at once, for the King of Leinster is

[087-053] journeying around his territory, and Kevin Cochlach, the charioteer, is

[087-054] making bitter love to her and wants her to run away with him.”

[087-055] Mongan set out, and in no great time, for they travelled day and night,

[087-056] they came to Bregla, and gained admittance to the fortress, but just

[087-057] as he got in he had to go out again, for the King of Leinster had been

[087-058] warned of Mongan’s journey, and came back to his fortress in the nick of

[087-059] time.

[087-060] When the men of [[ulster|Ulster]] saw the condition into which Mongan fell they

[087-061] were in great distress, and they all got sick through compassion for

[087-062] their king. The nobles suggested to him that they should march against

[087-063] Leinster and kill that king and bring back Duv Laca, but Mongan would

[087-064] not consent to this plan.

[087-065] “For,” said he, “the thing I lost through my own folly I shall get back

[087-066] through my own craft.”

[087-067] And when he said that his spirits revived, and he called for mac an

[087-068] Da’v.

[087-069] “You know, my friend,” said Mongan, “that I can’t get Duv Laca back

[087-070] unless the King of Leinster asks me to take her back, for a bargain is a

[087-071] bargain.”

[087-072] “That will happen when pigs fly,” said mac an Da’v, “and,” said he, “I

[087-073] did not make any bargain with any king that is in the world.”

[087-074] “I heard you say that before,” said Mongan.

[087-075] “I will say it till Doom,” cried his servant, “for my wife has gone

[087-076] away with that pestilent king, and he has got the double of your bad

[087-077] bargain.”

[087-078] Mongan and his servant then set out for Leinster.

[087-079] When they neared that country they found a great crowd going on the road

[087-080] with them, and they learned that the king was giving a feast in honour

[087-081] of his marriage to Duv Laca, for the year of waiting was nearly out, and

[087-082] the king had sworn he would delay no longer.

[087-083] They went on, therefore, but in low spirits, and at last they saw the

[087-084] walls of the king’s castle towering before them, and a noble company

[087-085] going to and fro on the lawn.
