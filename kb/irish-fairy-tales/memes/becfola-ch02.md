[039-001] They were married in a haste which equalled the king’s desire; and as he

[039-002] did not again ask her name, and as she did not volunteer to give it, and

[039-003] as she brought no dowry to her husband and received none from him, she

[039-004] was called [[becfola|Becfola]], the Dowerless.

[039-005] Time passed, and the king’s happiness was as great as his expectation

[039-006] of it had promised. But on the part of Becfola no similar tidings can be

[039-007] given.

[039-008] There are those whose happiness lies in ambition and station, and to

[039-009] such a one the fact of being queen to the High King of Ireland is a

[039-010] satisfaction at which desire is sated. But the mind of Becfola was not

[039-011] of this temperate quality, and, lacking Crimthann, it seemed to her that

[039-012] she possessed nothing.

[039-013] For to her mind he was the sunlight in the sun, the brightness in the

[039-014] moonbeam; he was the savour in fruit and the taste in honey; and when

[039-015] she looked from Crimthann to the king she could not but consider that

[039-016] the right man was in the wrong place. She thought that crowned only with

[039-017] his curls Crlmthann mac Ae was more nobly diademed than are the masters

[039-018] of the world, and she told him so.

[039-019] His terror on hearing this unexpected news was so great that he

[039-020] meditated immediate flight from [[tara|Tara]]; but when a thing has been uttered

[039-021] once it is easier said the second time and on the third repetition it is

[039-022] patiently listened to.

[039-023] After no great delay Crimthann mac Ae agreed and arranged that he and

[039-024] Becfola should fly from Tara, and it was part of their understanding

[039-025] that they should live happily ever after.

[039-026] One morning, when not even a bird was astir, the king felt that his dear

[039-027] companion was rising. He looked with one eye at the light that stole

[039-028] greyly through the window, and recognised that it could not in justice

[039-029] be called light.

[039-030] “There is not even a bird up,” he murmured.

[039-031] And then to Becfola.

[039-032] “What is the early rising for, dear heart?”

[039-033] “An engagement I have,” she replied.

[039-034] “This is not a time for engagements,” said the calm monarch.

[039-035] “Let it be so,” she replied, and she dressed rapidly.

[039-036] “And what is the engagement?” he pursued.

[039-037] “Raiment that I left at a certain place and must have. Eight silken

[039-038] smocks embroidered with gold, eight precious brooches of beaten gold,

[039-039] three diadems of pure gold.”

[039-040] “At this hour,” said the patient king, “the bed is better than the

[039-041] road.”

[039-042] “Let it be so,” said she.

[039-043] “And moreover,” he continued, “a Sunday journey brings bad luck.”

[039-044] “Let the luck come that will come,” she answered.

[039-045] “To keep a cat from cream or a woman from her gear is not work for a

[039-046] king,” said the monarch severely.

[039-047] The Ard-Ri’ could look on all things with composure, and regard all

[039-048] beings with a tranquil eye; but it should be known that there was one

[039-049] deed entirely hateful to him, and he would punish its commission with

[039-050] the very last rigour--this was, a transgression of the Sunday. During

[039-051] six days of the week all that could happen might happen, so far as

[039-052] Dermod was concerned, but on the seventh day nothing should happen at

[039-053] all if the High King could restrain it. Had it been possible he would

[039-054] have tethered the birds to their own green branches on that day, and

[039-055] forbidden the clouds to pack the upper world with stir and colour. These

[039-056] the king permitted, with a tight lip, perhaps, but all else that came

[039-057] under his hand felt his control.

[039-058] It was his custom when he arose on the morn of Sunday to climb to the

[039-059] most elevated point of Tara, and gaze thence on every side, so that

[039-060] he might see if any fairies or people of the Shi’ were disporting

[039-061] themselves in his lordship; for he absolutely prohibited the usage of

[039-062] the earth to these beings on the Sunday, and woe’s worth was it for the

[039-063] sweet being he discovered breaking his law.

[039-064] We do not know what ill he could do to the fairies, but during Dermod’s

[039-065] reign the world said its prayers on Sunday and the Shi’ folk stayed in

[039-066] their hills.

[039-067] It may be imagined, therefore, with what wrath he saw his wife’s

[039-068] preparations for her journey, but, although a king can do everything,

[039-069] what can a husband do...? He rearranged himself for slumber.

[039-070] “I am no party to this untimely journey,” he said angrily.

[039-071] “Let it be so,” said Becfola.

[039-072] She left the palace with one maid, and as she crossed the doorway

[039-073] something happened to her, but by what means it happened would be hard

[039-074] to tell; for in the one pace she passed out of the palace and out of the

[039-075] world, and the second step she trod was in [[faery|Faery]], but she did not know

[039-076] this.

[039-077] Her intention was to go to Cluain da chaillech to meet Crimthann, but

[039-078] when she left the palace she did not remember Crimthann any more.

[039-079] To her eye and to the eye of her maid the world was as it always had

[039-080] been, and the landmarks they knew were about them. But the object for

[039-081] which they were travelling was different, although unknown, and the

[039-082] people they passed on the roads were unknown, and were yet people that

[039-083] they knew.

[039-084] They set out southwards from Tara into the Duffry of [[leinster|Leinster]], and after

[039-085] some time they came into wild country and went astray. At last Becfola

[039-086] halted, saying:

[039-087] “I do not know where we are.”

[039-088] The maid replied that she also did not know.

[039-089] “Yet,” said Becfola, “if we continue to walk straight on we shall arrive

[039-090] somewhere.”

[039-091] They went on, and the maid watered the road with her tears.

[039-092] Night drew on them; a grey chill, a grey silence, and they were

[039-093] enveloped in that chill and silence; and they began to go in expectation

[039-094] and terror, for they both knew and did not know that which they were

[039-095] bound for.

[039-096] As they toiled desolately up the rustling and whispering side of a

[039-097] low hill the maid chanced to look back, and when she looked back she

[039-098] screamed and pointed, and clung to Becfola’s arm. Becfola followed the

[039-099] pointing finger, and saw below a large black mass that moved jerkily

[039-100] forward.

[039-101] “Wolves!” cried the maid. “Run to the trees yonder,” her mistress

[039-102] ordered. “We will climb them and sit among the branches.”

[039-103] They ran then, the maid moaning and lamenting all the while.

[039-104] “I cannot climb a tree,” she sobbed, “I shall be eaten by the wolves.”

[039-105] And that was true.

[039-106] But her mistress climbed a tree, and drew by a hand’s breadth from the

[039-107] rap and snap and slaver of those steel jaws. Then, sitting on a branch,

[039-108] she looked with angry woe at the straining and snarling horde below,

[039-109] seeing many a white fang in those grinning jowls, and the smouldering,

[039-110] red blink of those leaping and prowling eyes.
