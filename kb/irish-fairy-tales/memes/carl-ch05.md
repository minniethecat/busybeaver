[050-001] At the end of an hour the Carl awoke. He ate the second half of the

[050-002] boar, and he tied the unpicked bones in the tail of his coat. Then with

[050-003] a great rattling of the boar’s bones he started.

[050-004] It is hard to tell how he ran or at what speed he ran, but he went

[050-005] forward in great two-legged jumps, and at times he moved in

[050-006] immense one-legged, mud-spattering hops, and at times again, with

[050-007] wide-stretched, far-flung, terrible-tramping, space-destroying legs he

[050-008] ran.

[050-009] He left the swallows behind as if they were asleep. He caught up on

[050-010] a red deer, jumped over it, and left it standing. The wind was always

[050-011] behind him, for he outran it every time; and he caught up in jumps and

[050-012] bounces on [[cael|Cael of the Iron]], although Cael was running well, with

[050-013] his fists up and his head back and his two legs flying in and out so

[050-014] vigorously that you could not see them because of that speedy movement.

[050-015] Trotting by the side of Cael, the Carl thrust a hand into the tail of

[050-016] his coat and pulled out a fistfull of red bones.

[050-017] “Here, my heart, is a meaty bone,” said he, “for you fasted all night,

[050-018] poor friend, and if you pick a bit off the bone your stomach will get a

[050-019] rest.”

[050-020] “Keep your filth, beggarman,” the other replied, “for I would rather be

[050-021] hanged than gnaw on a bone that you have browsed.”

[050-022] “Why don’t you run, my pulse?” said the Carl earnestly; “why don’t you

[050-023] try to win the race?”

[050-024] Cael then began to move his limbs as if they were the wings of a fly,

[050-025] or the fins of a little fish, or as if they were the six legs of a

[050-026] terrified spider.

[050-027] “I am running,” he gasped.

[050-028] “But try and run like this,” the Carl admonished, and he gave a

[050-029] wriggling bound and a sudden outstretching and scurrying of shanks, and

[050-030] he disappeared from Cael’s sight in one wild spatter of big boots.

[050-031] Despair fell on Cael of the Iron, but he had a great heart. “I will run

[050-032] until I burst,” he shrieked, “and when I burst, may I burst to a great

[050-033] distance, and may I trip that beggar-man up with my burstings and make

[050-034] him break his leg.”

[050-035] He settled then to a determined, savage, implacable trot. He caught up

[050-036] on the Carl at last, for the latter had stopped to eat blackberries from

[050-037] the bushes on the road, and when he drew nigh, Cael began to jeer and

[050-038] sneer angrily at the Carl.

[050-039] “Who lost the tails of his coat?” he roared.

[050-040] “Don’t ask riddles of a man that’s eating blackberries,” the Carl

[050-041] rebuked him.

[050-042] “The dog without a tall and the coat without a tail,” cried Cael.

[050-043] “I give it up,” the Carl mumbled.

[050-044] “It’s yourself, beggarman,” jeered Cael.

[050-045] “I am myself,” the Carl gurgled through a mouthful of blackberries,

[050-046] “and as I am myself, how can it be myself? That is a silly riddle,” he

[050-047] burbled.

[050-048] “Look at your coat, tub of grease?”

[050-049] [[the-carl|The Carl]] did so.

[050-050] “My faith,” said he, “where are the two tails of my coat?” “I could

[050-051] smell one of them and it wrapped around a little tree thirty miles

[050-052] back,” said Cael, “and the other one was dishonouring a bush ten miles

[050-053] behind that.”

[050-054] “It is bad luck to be separated from the tails of your own coat,” the

[050-055] Carl grumbled. “I’ll have to go back for them. Wait here, beloved, and

[050-056] eat blackberries until I come back, and we’ll both start fair.”

[050-057] “Not half a second will I wait,” Cael replied, and he began to run

[050-058] towards [[ben-edair|Ben Edair]] as a lover runs to his maiden or as a bee flies to his

[050-059] hive.

[050-060] “I haven’t had half my share of blackberries either,” the Carl lamented

[050-061] as he started to run backwards for his coat-tails.

[050-062] He ran determinedly on that backward journey, and as the path he had

[050-063] travelled was beaten out as if it had been trampled by an hundred bulls

[050-064] yoked neck to neck, he was able to find the two bushes and the two

[050-065] coat-tails. He sewed them on his coat.

[050-066] Then he sprang up, and he took to a fit and a vortex and an exasperation

[050-067] of running for which no description may be found. The thumping of his

[050-068] big boots grew as con-tinuous as the pattering of hailstones on a

[050-069] roof, and the wind of his passage blew trees down. The beasts that were

[050-070] ranging beside his path dropped dead from concussion, and the steam that

[050-071] snored from his nose blew birds into bits and made great lumps of cloud

[050-072] fall out of the sky.

[050-073] He again caught up on Cael, who was running with his head down and his

[050-074] toes up.

[050-075] “If you won’t try to run, my treasure,” said the Carl, “you will never

[050-076] get your tribute.”

[050-077] And with that he incensed and exploded himself into an eye-blinding,

[050-078] continuous, waggle and complexity of boots that left Cael behind him in

[050-079] a flash.

[050-080] “I will run until I burst,” sobbed Cael, and he screwed agitation and

[050-081] despair into his legs until he hummed and buzzed like a blue-bottle on a

[050-082] window.

[050-083] Five miles from Ben Edair the Carl stopped, for he had again come among

[050-084] blackberries.

[050-085] He ate of these until he was no more than a sack of juice, and when

[050-086] he heard the humming and buzzing of Cael of the Iron he mourned and

[050-087] lamented that he could not wait to eat his fill He took off his coat,

[050-088] stuffed it full of blackberries, swung it on his shoulders, and went

[050-089] bounding stoutly and nimbly for Ben Edair.
