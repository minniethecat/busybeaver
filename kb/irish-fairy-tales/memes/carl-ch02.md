[047-001] He had not gone a great distance from [[ben-edair|Ben Edair]] when he came to

[047-002] an intricate, gloomy wood, where the trees grew so thickly and the

[047-003] undergrowth was such a sprout and tangle that one could scarcely pass

[047-004] through it. He remembered that a path had once been hacked through the

[047-005] wood, and he sought for this. It was a deeply scooped, hollow way, and

[047-006] it ran or wriggled through the entire length of the wood.

[047-007] Into this gloomy drain Fionn descended and made progress, but when he

[047-008] had penetrated deeply in the dank forest he heard a sound of thumping

[047-009] and squelching footsteps, and he saw coming towards him a horrible,

[047-010] evil-visaged being; a wild, monstrous, yellow-skinned, big-boned giant,

[047-011] dressed in nothing but an ill-made, mud-plastered, drab-coloured coat,

[047-012] which swaggled and clapped against the calves of his big bare legs. On

[047-013] his stamping feet there were great brogues of boots that were shaped

[047-014] like, but were bigger than, a boat, and each time he put a foot down it

[047-015] squashed and squirted a barrelful of mud from the sunk road.

[047-016] Fionn had never seen the like of this vast person, and he stood gazing

[047-017] on him, lost in a stare of astonishment.

[047-018] The great man saluted him.

[047-019] “All alone, Fionn?” he cried. “How does it happen that not one Fenian

[047-020] of the [[fianna|Fianna]] is at the side of his captain?” At this inquiry Fionn got

[047-021] back his wits.

[047-022] “That is too long a story and it is too intricate and pressing to be

[047-023] told, also I have no time to spare now.”

[047-024] “Yet tell it now,” the monstrous man insisted.

[047-025] Fionn, thus pressed, told of the coming of [[cael|Cael of the Iron]], of the

[047-026] challenge the latter had issued, and that he, Fionn, was off to [[tara|Tara]] of

[047-027] the Kings to find Caelte mac Rona’n.

[047-028] “I know that foreigner well,” the big man commented.

[047-029] “Is he the champion he makes himself out to be?” Fionn inquired.

[047-030] “He can do twice as much as he said he would do,” the monster replied.

[047-031] “He won’t outrun Caelte mac Rona’n,” Fionn asserted. The big man jeered.

[047-032] “Say that he won’t outrun a hedgehog, dear heart. This Cael will end the

[047-033] course by the time your Caelte begins to think of starting.”

[047-034] “Then,” said Fionn, “I no longer know where to turn, or how to protect

[047-035] the honour of Ireland.”

[047-036] “I know how to do these things,” the other man commented with a slow nod

[047-037] of the head.

[047-038] “If you do,” Fionn pleaded, “tell it to me upon your honour.”

[047-039] “I will do that,” the man replied.

[047-040] “Do not look any further for the rusty-kneed, slow-trotting son of

[047-041] Rona’n,” he continued, “but ask me to run your race, and, by this hand,

[047-042] I will be first at the post.”

[047-043] At this the Chief began to laugh.

[047-044] “My good friend, you have work enough to carry the two tons of mud that

[047-045] are plastered on each of your coat-tails, to say nothing of your weighty

[047-046] boots.”

[047-047] “By my hand,” the man cried, “there is no person in Ireland but myself

[047-048] can win that race. I claim a chance.”

[047-049] Fionn agreed then. “Be it so,” said he. “And now, tell me your name?”

[047-050] “I am known as the Carl of the Drab Coat.”

[047-051] “All names are names,” Fionn responded, “and that also is a name.”

[047-052] They returned then to Ben Edair.
