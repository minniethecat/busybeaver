## Overview

Manannán mac Lir is the great sea deity of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]], whose realm encompasses the waters between Ireland and the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]]. Though he appears only briefly in these tales — as the father of a [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] suitor in "[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]" — his mythological stature as one of the most significant gods of the Irish pantheon places him at the foundation of the supernatural order that pervades these stories.

## Identity

Manannán is one of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]], the divine race who retreated into the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] mounds after their defeat by the Milesians. His domain is the sea: his name is etymologically connected to the Isle of Man, and his traditional home is said to lie beneath the waves or on enchanted islands beyond the western horizon. He is the son of Lir, the personification of the ocean itself, and his powers include control of the waves, the ability to shroud himself and others in mist, and ownership of [[enchantment|magic]]al possessions such as the crane-bag of treasures and the self-navigating boat.

## In the Tales

### Becuma of the White Skin

Manannán appears in these tales only through a genealogical reference: [[becuma|Becuma Cneisgel]]'s original lover in the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] was one of "the sons of Mananna'n mac Lir" [060-019]. Her transgression with this son of the sea god — an affair judged criminal in the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] — is the act that leads to her banishment to the mortal world. The reference, though brief, anchors the entire tale in the divine genealogy of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] and establishes Becuma's status as a figure who moved in the highest circles of the supernatural realm before her fall.

The son of Manannán is not named in the text, but the invocation of the sea god's lineage carries weight: a crime committed against the household of Manannán is no small matter, and Becuma's banishment, though described as harsh, reflects the gravity of offending one of the foremost powers of the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]].

## Significance

Manannán mac Lir represents the vast mythological backdrop against which the Fenian tales unfold. While the Fenian Cycle focuses on mortal heroes, the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] linger at the edges — in the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] mounds, across the sea, in the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] — and Manannán is among the most powerful of these lingering presences. His single genealogical mention in these tales serves as a reminder that the world of Fionn and the [[fianna|Fianna]] is still haunted by the older gods, whose bloodlines and grudges continue to shape mortal affairs.
