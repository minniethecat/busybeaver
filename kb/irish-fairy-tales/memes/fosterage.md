# Fosterage

**Fosterage** (*altram* in Old Irish) was a central social institution in early Irish society, where children of noble and warrior families were sent to be raised by foster-parents from allied clans. This practice strengthened political bonds, provided specialized education, and created lifelong loyalties between foster-kin.

## The Fosterage System

In Irish customary law, fosterage was not merely a domestic arrangement but a deeply formalized social contract. Foster-parents were obligated to educate the child according to their station: sons of kings learned statecraft, warriors-in-training mastered arms, and future poets (*[[bardic-tradition|fili]]*) memorized the vast corpus of traditional lore. The bond between foster-siblings was considered as sacred as blood kinship, and the obligations between foster-parent and foster-child endured for life.

## In the Tales

The fosterage system appears throughout *Irish Fairy Tales* as the framework for several key relationships.

The most prominent example is the upbringing of [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]]. After the death of his father, the infant Fionn was entrusted to two foster-mothers—the druid women [[bovmall|Bovmall]] and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]]—who hid him in the forests of [[slieve-bloom|Slieve Bloom]] to protect him from [[clann-morna|Clann Morna]]. Under their care, Fionn learned the skills that would make him the leader of the [[fianna|Fianna]].

> He was unmarried, but he had many foster-sons [038-007]

The text also notes that [[mongan|Mongan]], son of [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannán mac Lir]], was fostered by a robber among the marshes:

> the great robber who had fostered him among the marshes. [024-042]

The depth of the foster-bond is shown in how a king imparts knowledge to his foster-son:

> the king imparted to his foster-son his own wide knowledge of... [038-017]

[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]] sought counsel from her foster-sister Áine, demonstrating that foster-kin ties endured even in the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]]:

> daughter Aine', who was her foster-sister, let her into Faery. [067-063]

## Significance

Fosterage served multiple functions in Irish society: it was a system of political alliance-building, a method of specialized education, and a safeguard for the continuity of noble lines. The foster-relationship carried legal weight in the Brehon laws, with specific obligations defined for both parties. The phrase "foster-brother of Time" [001-029] in Tuan mac [[cairill|Cairill]]'s reflection suggests how deeply the concept of fosterage permeated Irish thinking—even Time itself could be understood through the fosterage framework.
