[060-001] There are more worlds than one, and in many ways they are unlike each

[060-002] other. But joy and sorrow, or, in other words, good and evil, are not

[060-003] absent in their degree from any of the worlds, for wherever there is

[060-004] life there is action, and action is but the expression of one or other

[060-005] of these qualities.

[060-006] After this Earth there is the world of the Shi’. Beyond it again lies

[060-007] the [[faery|Many-Coloured Land]]. Next comes the Land of Wonder, and after that

[060-008] the [[land-of-promise|Land of Promise]] awaits us. You will cross clay to get into the Shi’;

[060-009] you will cross water to attain the Many-Coloured Land; fire must be

[060-010] passed ere the Land of Wonder is attained, but we do not know what will

[060-011] be crossed for the fourth world.

[060-012] This adventure of [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn the Hundred Fighter]] and his son Art was by the

[060-013] way of water, and therefore he was more advanced in [[enchantment|magic]] than Fionn

[060-014] was, all of whose adventures were by the path of clay and into Faery

[060-015] only, but Conn was the High King and so the arch-magician of Ireland.

[060-016] A council had been called in the Many-Coloured Land to discuss the case

[060-017] of a lady named [[becuma|Becuma Cneisgel]], that is, Becuma of the White Skin, the

[060-018] daughter of Eogan Inver. She had run away from her husband Labraid and

[060-019] had taken refuge with Gadiar, one of the sons of Mananna’n mac Lir, the

[060-020] god of the sea, and the ruler, therefore, of that sphere.

[060-021] It seems, then, that there is marriage in two other spheres. In the

[060-022] Shi’ matrimony is recorded as being parallel in every respect with

[060-023] earth-marriage, and the desire which urges to it seems to be as violent

[060-024] and inconstant as it is with us; but in the Many-Coloured Land marriage

[060-025] is but a contemplation of beauty, a brooding and meditation wherein all

[060-026] grosser desire is unknown and children are born to sinless parents.

[060-027] In the Shi’ the crime of Becuma would have been lightly considered, and

[060-028] would have received none or but a nominal punishment, but in the second

[060-029] world a horrid gravity attaches to such a lapse, and the retribution

[060-030] meted is implacable and grim. It may be dissolution by fire, and that

[060-031] can note a destruction too final for the mind to contemplate; or it may

[060-032] be banishment from that sphere to a lower and worse one.

[060-033] This was the fate of Becuma of the White Skin.

[060-034] One may wonder how, having attained to that sphere, she could have

[060-035] carried with her so strong a memory of the earth. It is certain that she

[060-036] was not a fit person to exist in the Many-Coloured Land, and it is to be

[060-037] feared that she was organised too grossly even for life in the Shi’.

[060-038] She was an earth-woman, and she was banished to the earth.

[060-039] Word was sent to the Shi’s of Ireland that this lady should not be

[060-040] permitted to enter any of them; from which it would seem that the

[060-041] ordinances of the Shi come from the higher world, and, it might follow,

[060-042] that the conduct of earth lies in the Shi’.

[060-043] In that way, the gates of her own world and the innumerable doors of

[060-044] Faery being closed against her, Becuma was forced to appear in the world

[060-045] of men.

[060-046] It is pleasant, however, notwithstanding her terrible crime and her

[060-047] woeful punishment, to think how courageous she was. When she was told

[060-048] her sentence, nay, her doom, she made no outcry, nor did she waste any

[060-049] time in sorrow. She went home and put on her nicest clothes.

[060-050] She wore a red satin smock, and, over this, a cloak of green silk out of

[060-051] which long fringes of gold swung and sparkled, and she had light sandals

[060-052] of white bronze on her thin, shapely feet. She had long soft hair that

[060-053] was yellow as gold, and soft as the curling foam of the sea. Her eyes

[060-054] were wide and clear as water and were grey as a dove’s breast. Her teeth

[060-055] were white as snow and of an evenness to marvel at. Her lips were thin

[060-056] and beautifully curved: red lips in truth, red as winter berries and

[060-057] tempting as the fruits of summer. The people who superintended her

[060-058] departure said mournfully that when she was gone there would be no more

[060-059] beauty left in their world.

[060-060] She stepped into a coracle, it was pushed on the enchanted waters, and

[060-061] it went forward, world within world, until land appeared, and her boat

[060-062] swung in low tide against a rock at the foot of [[ben-edair|Ben Edair]].

[060-063] So far for her.
