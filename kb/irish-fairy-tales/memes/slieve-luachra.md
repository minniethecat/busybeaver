# Slieve Luachra

## Overview

Slieve Luachra is a mountain range in [[munster|Munster]], appearing in Stephens' "[[the-carl|The Carl]] of the Drab Coat" as the destination of a sixty-mile journey undertaken by the Carl and his young companion. Located at the crossroads of three modern counties, Slieve Luachra serves as a wilderness waypoint where two travelers rest before their mysterious adventure continues.

## Location

- **Region**: [[munster|Munster]], southern Ireland
- **Modern equivalent**: The Slieve Luachra mountains straddle the border of counties Cork, [[kerry|Kerry]], and Limerick in southwest Ireland
- **Type of place**: Mountain range — a remote, elevated wilderness

## In the Tales

In "[[the-carl|The Carl]] of the Drab Coat," the Carl performs a remarkable feat of geographical knowledge, telling his young companion: "to the Hill of the Rushes, Slieve Luachra of [[munster|Munster]], is exactly sixty miles from this place" [048-026]. The precision of this statement — "exactly sixty miles" — hints at the Carl's supernatural nature, as no ordinary man could know such a distance with certainty.

The young companion is eager to test the Carl's claim, and the Carl agrees: "we may go off to Slieve Luachra now, and in the morning we shall know" [048-029]. This initiates a journey across [[munster|Munster]] that tests endurance. The two set out, covering the full distance in a single day: "These two set out then for [[munster|Munster]], and as the sun was setting they reached Slieve Luachra and prepared to spend the night there" [048-032]–[048-033].

Slieve Luachra thus functions as both a destination and a threshold — the point where the travelers pause before whatever the morning will reveal. The night spent on the mountain carries the weight of anticipation.

## Mythological Significance

Slieve Luachra's significance in the tale is primarily as a test of the Carl's supernatural knowledge and endurance. The "exactly sixty miles" measurement establishes a mythical geography where distances are precise and known to the mysterious giant, setting him apart from ordinary travelers.

The mountain's association with rushes — "the Hill of the Rushes" — connects it to the natural imagery that pervades Irish place-lore. In the broader landscape of Stephens' tales, Slieve Luachra stands as one of the named wild places where the boundary between mundane travel and [[enchantment|magic]]al journey thins.
