## Overview

Cairill was the King of [[ulster|Ulster]] during the time of St. [[finnian|Finnian]] of
[[moville|Moville]], placing his reign in the early Christian period of Ireland's
history. Though he appears only as a background figure in "The Story of
Tuan mac Cairill," his role is essential: his court provides the setting
where the most ancient history of Ireland is finally told aloud and
recorded.

Tuan mac Cairill bears Cairill's name as a patronymic, and it was
Cairill's fisherman who caught the shapeshifting sage in salmon form —
an event that bridged the pagan past and the Christian present of
Ireland.

## Historical Context

Cairill's kingdom of [[ulster|Ulster]] was one of the five ancient provinces of
Ireland, with its royal seat at Emain Macha. By the time of Cairill's
reign, the province had been reduced from its former dominance — Emain
Macha had fallen centuries earlier — but its kings still commanded
respect and territory in the north of Ireland.

The presence of St. [[finnian|Finnian]] of [[moville|Moville]] at Cairill's court places
these events in the sixth century, a period when Ireland was
transitioning from its pagan past to its Christian future. Cairill
himself is presented as a figure who straddles both worlds: a king of
the old order who nevertheless welcomes the Christian saint and the
ancient wisdom brought to his hall.

## In the Tales

### Ancestry

Tuan introduces himself as "Tuan, the son of Cairill, the son of
Muredac Red-neck" [001-032]. This genealogy is significant: the name
Cairill appears both as the king of [[ulster|Ulster]] and as Tuan's father's
name, creating a deliberate parallel between the ancient lineage of the
shapeshifter and the present-day royal house.

### Capturing the Salmon

The pivotal event of Cairill's role in the tale comes through his
fisherman. Tuan, who had lived through successive animal
transformations — stag, boar, hawk — had finally taken the form of a
salmon. In this form he was caught:

> The fisherman of Cairill, the King of [[ulster|Ulster]], took me in his net. Ah, that was a happy
> man when he saw me! He shouted for joy when he saw the great salmon in
> his net [011-001]–[011-003].

The salmon was brought to Cairill's household, where the queen ate the
fish and conceived Tuan — a motif found across Celtic mythology where
consumption of a magical fish leads to rebirth. Thus Cairill became, in
a sense, the foster-father of the reborn sage, and Tuan took his
patronymic in recognition of this bond.

### The Court as Chronicle

Cairill's court became the setting for one of the most remarkable
narrative frames in Irish literature. In his hall, before St.
[[finnian|Finnian]] and the assembled nobles of [[ulster|Ulster]], Tuan — now an ancient
man — recounted the entire history of Ireland's invasions: the coming of
[[partholon|Partholon]], the people of Nemed, the Fir Bolg, the
[[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], and finally the [[milesians|Milesians]].

Without Cairill's fisherman, the salmon would not have been caught.
Without Cairill's court, the story would not have been heard. The king
thus serves as the unwitting patron of Ireland's oldest living memory.
