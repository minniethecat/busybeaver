[076-001] “It is not nice of you to laugh at us,” said [[fiachna-finn|Fiachna Finn]].

[076-002] “Who could help laughing at a king hunkering on a branch and his army

[076-003] roosting around him like hens?” said the stranger.

[076-004] “Nevertheless,” the king replied, “it would be courteous of you not to

[076-005] laugh at misfortune.”

[076-006] “We laugh when we can,” commented the stranger, “and are thankful for

[076-007] the chance.”

[076-008] “You may come up into the tree,” said Fiachna, “for I perceive that you

[076-009] are a mannerly person, and I see that some of the venomous sheep are

[076-010] charging in this direction. I would rather protect you,” he continued,

[076-011] “than see you killed; for,” said he lamentably, “I am getting down now

[076-012] to fight the sheep.”

[076-013] “They will not hurt me,” said the stranger. “Who are you?” the king

[076-014] asked.

[076-015] “I am Mananna’n, the son of Lir.”

[076-016] Fiachna knew then that the stranger could not be hurt.

[076-017] “What will you give me if I deliver you from the sheep?” asked

[076-018] Mananna’n.

[076-019] “I will give you anything you ask, if I have that thing.”

[076-020] “I ask the rights of your crown and of your household for one day.”

[076-021] Fiachna’s breath was taken away by that request, and he took a little

[076-022] time to compose himself, then he said mildly:

[076-023] “I will not have one man of Ireland killed if I can save him. All that

[076-024] I have they give me, all that I have I give to them, and if I must give

[076-025] this also, then I will give this, although it would be easier for me to

[076-026] give my life.” “That is agreed,” said Mannana’n.

[076-027] He had something wrapped in a fold of his cloak, and he unwrapped and

[076-028] produced this thing.

[076-029] It was a dog.

[076-030] Now if the sheep were venomous, this dog was more venomous still, for it

[076-031] was fearful to look at. In body it was not large, but its head was of a

[076-032] great size, and the mouth that was shaped in that head was able to open

[076-033] like the lid of a pot. It was not teeth which were in that head, but

[076-034] hooks and fangs and prongs. Dreadful was that mouth to look at, terrible

[076-035] to look into, woeful to think about; and from it, or from the broad,

[076-036] loose nose that waggled above it, there came a sound which no word of

[076-037] man could describe, for it was not a snarl, nor was it a howl, although

[076-038] it was both of these. It was neither a growl nor a grunt, although it

[076-039] was both of these; it was not a yowl nor a groan, although it was both

[076-040] of these: for it was one sound made up of these sounds, and there was in

[076-041] it, too, a whine and a yelp, and a long-drawn snoring noise, and a deep

[076-042] purring noise, and a noise that was like the squeal of a rusty hinge,

[076-043] and there were other noises in it also.

[076-044] “The gods be praised!” said the man who was in the branch above the

[076-045] king.

[076-046] “What for this time?” said the king.

[076-047] “Because that dog cannot climb a tree,” said the man.

[076-048] And the man on a branch yet above him groaned out “Amen!”

[076-049] “There is nothing to frighten sheep like a dog,” said Mananna’n, “and

[076-050] there is nothing to frighten these sheep like this dog.”

[076-051] He put the dog on the ground then.

[076-052] “Little dogeen, little treasure,” said he, “go and kill the sheep.”

[076-053] And when he said that the dog put an addition and an addendum on to the

[076-054] noise he had been making before, so that the men of Ireland stuck their

[076-055] fingers into their ears and turned the whites of their eyes upwards, and

[076-056] nearly fell off their branches with the fear and the fright which that

[076-057] sound put into them.

[076-058] It did not take the dog long to do what he had been ordered. He went

[076-059] forward, at first, with a slow waddle, and as the venomous sheep came to

[076-060] meet him in bounces, he then went to meet them in wriggles; so that in a

[076-061] while he went so fast that you could see nothing of him but a head and

[076-062] a wriggle. He dealt with the sheep in this way, a jump and a chop for

[076-063] each, and he never missed his jump and he never missed his chop. When he

[076-064] got his grip he swung round on it as if it was a hinge. The swing began

[076-065] with the chop, and it ended with the bit loose and the sheep giving its

[076-066] last kick. At the end of ten minutes all the sheep were lying on the

[076-067] ground, and the same bit was out of every sheep, and every sheep was

[076-068] dead.

[076-069] “You can come down now,” said Mananna’n.

[076-070] “That dog can’t climb a tree,” said the man in the branch above the king

[076-071] warningly.

[076-072] “Praise be to the gods!” said the man who was above him.

[076-073] “Amen!” said the warrior who was higher up than that. And the man in the

[076-074] next tree said:

[076-075] “Don’t move a hand or a foot until the dog chokes himself to death on

[076-076] the dead meat.”

[076-077] The dog, however, did not eat a bit of the meat. He trotted to his

[076-078] master, and Mananna’n took him up and wrapped him in his cloak.

[076-079] “Now you can come down,” said he.

[076-080] “I wish that dog was dead!” said the king.

[076-081] But he swung himself out of the tree all the same, for he did not wish

[076-082] to seem frightened before Mananna’n. “You can go now and beat the men

[076-083] of Lochlann,” said Mananna’n. “You will be King of Lochlann before

[076-084] nightfall.”

[076-085] “I wouldn’t mind that,” said the king. “It’s no threat,” said Mananna’n.

[076-086] The son of Lir turned then and went away in the direction of Ireland to

[076-087] take up his one-day rights, and Fiachna continued his battle with the

[076-088] Lochlannachs.

[076-089] He beat them before nightfall, and by that victory he became King of

[076-090] Lochlann and King of the Saxons and the Britons.

[076-091] He gave the Black Hag seven castles with their territories, and he gave

[076-092] her one hundred of every sort of cattle that he had captured. She was

[076-093] satisfied.

[076-094] Then he went back to Ireland, and after he had been there for some time

[076-095] his wife gave birth to a son.
