# Milesians

## Overview

The Milesians (Sons of Míl Espáine) are, in Irish mythological tradition, the final race to invade and settle Ireland. They defeated the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], the divine inhabitants who preceded them, forcing the Tuatha De to retreat into the [[faery|sidhe-mounds]] where they became the People of Faery. The Milesians are thus presented as the ancestors of the Gaelic Irish people — mortal successors to the godlike earlier races. [^1]

## Identity

The Milesians represent the transition from myth to history in the Irish origin narrative. Unlike the supernaturally-gifted [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], they are mortal beings who must conquer and negotiate. Their victory over the divine race marks the end of the Mythological era and the beginning of the age of men. In the Stephens corpus, they appear primarily as a historical reference point — the "coming of the Milesians" serves as a chronological marker for the deep past of Ireland's legendary history.

## In the Tales

The Milesians are referenced in *The Story of Tuan Mac Cairill*, where Tuan's millennia-spanning life story encompasses "the days of the Partholonians through the Nemedians, the Fir Bolg, and the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], all the way to the coming of the Milesians and the saints." [^1] This places the Milesian arrival as a pivotal point in Ireland's mythological timeline — the boundary between the age of gods and the age of mortals.

## Mythological Significance

The Milesian conquest is fundamental to Irish mythology's structure. It explains why the gods of the Tuatha De Danann are no longer visibly present in the world — they were not destroyed but displaced, retreating into an invisible parallel realm. This framework allowed medieval Irish monks to reconcile the pagan supernatural traditions with Christian history, presenting the ancient gods as a historical antecedent rather than objects of worship.

## The Division of Ireland

The settlement myth records that after their victory, the Milesians divided Ireland with the defeated Tuatha De Danann: the mortals took the surface world — the fields, hills, and shores — while the divine race withdrew underground into the *sidhe* mounds, the hollow hills that serve as portals to the Otherworld. This division is not merely geographical but ontological: it establishes a cosmology in which two orders of being coexist in the same land, separated by the thin membrane of the mound-entrance. The Brugh of the Boyne, Cruachan of Connacht, and other *sidhe* sites thus become places where the boundary between mortal and immortal is permeable — where a human might stumble into Faery, or a Tuatha De Danaan might cross into the human world to intervene in mortal affairs.

This two-worlds arrangement shapes all subsequent Irish myth. When [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]] is exiled from the Many-Coloured Land and seeks entry at various *sidhe* mounds, she is navigating the geopolitical geography of this ancient division. When Angus Og bars the Brugh to her, he is enforcing the boundary first drawn at the Milesian victory. The Milesians, though barely present as characters in the tales, are thus the architects of the mythological landscape within which all the stories unfold — the ones who drew the line between this world and the Other, and who made the gods into neighbours rather than rulers.
