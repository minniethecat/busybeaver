[055-001] Now the king of the Shi’ of [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]], [[conaran|Conaran]], son of Imidel, was

[055-002] also watching the hunt, but Fionn did not see him, for we cannot see the

[055-003] people of [[faery|Faery]] until we enter their realm, and Fionn was not thinking

[055-004] of Faery at that moment. Conaran did not like Fionn, and, seeing that

[055-005] the great champion was alone, save for Cona’n and the two hounds [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]]

[055-006] and Sceo’lan, he thought the time had come to get Fionn into his power.

[055-007] We do not know what Fionn had done to Conaran, but it must have been bad

[055-008] enough, for the king of the Shi’ of Cesh Cotran was filled with joy

[055-009] at the sight of Fionn thus close to him, thus unprotected, thus

[055-010] unsuspicious.

[055-011] This Conaran had four daughters. He was fond of them and proud of them,

[055-012] but if one were to search the Shi’s of Ireland or the land of Ireland,

[055-013] the equal of these four would not be found for ugliness and bad humour

[055-014] and twisted temperaments.

[055-015] Their hair was black as ink and tough as wire: it stuck up and poked out

[055-016] and hung down about their heads in bushes and spikes and tangles. Their

[055-017] eyes were bleary and red. Their mouths were black and twisted, and in

[055-018] each of these mouths there was a hedge of curved yellow fangs. They had

[055-019] long scraggy necks that could turn all the way round like the neck of

[055-020] a hen. Their arms were long and skinny and muscular, and at the end of

[055-021] each finger they had a spiked nail that was as hard as horn and as sharp

[055-022] as a briar. Their bodies were covered with a bristle of hair and fur

[055-023] and fluff, so that they looked like dogs in some parts and like cats

[055-024] in others, and in other parts again they looked like chickens. They had

[055-025] moustaches poking under their noses and woolly wads growing out of their

[055-026] ears, so that when you looked at them the first time you never wanted

[055-027] to look at them again, and if you had to look at them a second time you

[055-028] were likely to die of the sight.

[055-029] They were called Caevo’g, Cuillen, and Iaran. The fourth daughter,

[055-030] Iarnach, was not present at that moment, so nothing need be said of her

[055-031] yet.

[055-032] Conaran called these three to him.

[055-033] “Fionn is alone,” said he. “Fionn is alone, my treasures.”

[055-034] “Ah!” said Caevo’g, and her jaw crunched upwards and stuck outwards, as

[055-035] was usual with her when she was satisfied.

[055-036] “When the chance comes take it,” Conaran continued, and he smiled a

[055-037] black, beetle-browed, unbenevolent smile.

[055-038] “It’s a good word,” quoth Cuillen, and she swung her jaw loose and made

[055-039] it waggle up and down, for that was the way she smiled.

[055-040] “And here is the chance,” her father added.

[055-041] “The chance is here,” Iaran echoed, with a smile that was very like

[055-042] her sister’s, only that it was worse, and the wen that grew on her nose

[055-043] joggled to and fro and did not get its balance again for a long time.

[055-044] Then they smiled a smile that was agreeable to their own eyes, but which

[055-045] would have been a deadly thing for anybody else to see.

[055-046] “But Fionn cannot see us,” Caevo’g objected, and her brow set downwards

[055-047] and her chin set upwards and her mouth squeezed sidewards, so that her

[055-048] face looked like a badly disappointed nut.

[055-049] “And we are worth seeing,” Cuillen continued, and the disappointment

[055-050] that was set in her sister’s face got carved and twisted into hers, but

[055-051] it was worse in her case.

[055-052] “That is the truth,” said Iaran in a voice of lamentation, and her face

[055-053] took on a gnarl and a writhe and a solidity of ugly woe that beat the

[055-054] other two and made even her father marvel.

[055-055] “He cannot see us now,” Conaran replied, “but he will see us in a

[055-056] minute.”

[055-057] “Won’t Fionn be glad when he sees us!” said the three sisters.

[055-058] And then they joined hands and danced joyfully around their father, and

[055-059] they sang a song, the first line of which is:

[055-060] “Fionn thinks he is safe. But who knows when the sky will

[055-061] fall?”

[055-062] Lots of the people in the Shi’ learned that song by heart, and they

[055-063] applied it to every kind of circumstance.
