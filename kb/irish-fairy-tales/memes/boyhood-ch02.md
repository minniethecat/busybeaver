[013-001] Were his nurses comely or harsh-looking? Fionn would not know. This was

[013-002] the one who picked him up when he fell, and that was the one who patted

[013-003] the bruise. This one said: “Mind you do not tumble in the well!”

[013-004] And that one: “Mind the little knees among the nettles.”

[013-005] But he did tumble and record that the only notable thing about a well

[013-006] is that it is wet. And as for nettles, if they hit him he hit back. He

[013-007] slashed into them with a stick and brought them low. There was nothing

[013-008] in wells or nettles, only women dreaded them. One patronised women and

[013-009] instructed them and comforted them, for they were afraid about one.

[013-010] They thought that one should not climb a tree!

[013-011] “Next week,” they said at last, “you may climb this one,” and “next

[013-012] week” lived at the end of the world!

[013-013] But the tree that was climbed was not worth while when it had been

[013-014] climbed twice. There was a bigger one near by. There were trees that no

[013-015] one could climb, with vast shadow on one side and vaster sunshine on

[013-016] the other. It took a long time to walk round them, and you could not see

[013-017] their tops.

[013-018] It was pleasant to stand on a branch that swayed and sprung, and it was

[013-019] good to stare at an impenetrable roof of leaves and then climb into it.

[013-020] How wonderful the loneliness was up there! When he looked down there

[013-021] was an undulating floor of leaves, green and green and greener to a very

[013-022] blackness of greeniness; and when he looked up there were leaves

[013-023] again, green and less green and not green at all, up to a very snow and

[013-024] blindness of greeniness; and above and below and around there was sway

[013-025] and motion, the whisper of leaf on leaf, and the eternal silence to

[013-026] which one listened and at which one tried to look.

[013-027] When he was six years of age his mother, beautiful, long-haired [[muirne|Muirne]],

[013-028] came to see him. She came secretly, for she feared the [[clann-morna|sons of Morna]],

[013-029] and she had paced through lonely places in many counties before she

[013-030] reached the hut in the wood, and the cot where he lay with his fists

[013-031] shut and sleep gripped in them.

[013-032] He awakened to be sure. He would have one ear that would catch an

[013-033] unusual voice, one eye that would open, however sleepy the other one

[013-034] was. She took him in her arms and kissed him, and she sang a sleepy song

[013-035] until the small boy slept again.

[013-036] We may be sure that the eye that could stay open stayed open that night

[013-037] as long as it could, and that the one ear listened to the sleepy song

[013-038] until the song got too low to be heard, until it was too tender to be

[013-039] felt vibrating along those soft arms, until Fionn was asleep again, with

[013-040] a new picture in his little head and a new notion to ponder on.

[013-041] The mother of himself! His own mother!

[013-042] But when he awakened she was gone.

[013-043] She was going back secretly, in dread of the sons of Morna, slipping

[013-044] through gloomy woods, keeping away from habitations, getting by desolate

[013-045] and lonely ways to her lord in [[kerry|Kerry]].

[013-046] Perhaps it was he that was afraid of the sons of Morna, and perhaps she

[013-047] loved him.
