[044-001] [This version of the death of Uail is not correct. Also Cnocha is not in

[044-002] Lochlann but in Ireland.]

[044-003] The wonderful gift-giving of Goll continued, and an uneasiness and

[044-004] embarrassment began to creep through the great banqueting hall.

[044-005] Gentlemen looked at each other questioningly, and then spoke again on

[044-006] indifferent matters, but only with half of their minds. The singers, the

[044-007] harpers, and jugglers submitted to that constraint, so that every person

[044-008] felt awkward and no one knew what should be done or what would happen,

[044-009] and from that doubt dulness came, with silence following on its heels.

[044-010] There is nothing more terrible than silence. Shame grows in that blank,

[044-011] or anger gathers there, and we must choose which of these is to be our

[044-012] master.

[044-013] That choice lay before Fionn, who never knew shame.

[044-014] “Goll,” said he, “how long have you been taking tribute from the people

[044-015] of Lochlann?”

[044-016] “A long time now,” said Goll.

[044-017] And he looked into an eye that was stern and unfriendly.

[044-018] “I thought that my rent was the only one those people had to pay,” Fionn

[044-019] continued.

[044-020] “Your memory is at fault,” said Goll.

[044-021] “Let it be so,” said Fionn. “How did your tribute arise?”

[044-022] “Long ago, Fionn, in the days when your father forced war on me.”

[044-023] “Ah!” said Fionn.

[044-024] “When he raised the High King against me and banished me from Ireland.”

[044-025] “Continue,” said Fionn, and he held Goll’s eye under the great beetle of

[044-026] his brow.

[044-027] “I went into Britain,” said Goll, “and your father followed me there. I

[044-028] went into White Lochlann (Norway) and took it. Your father banished me

[044-029] thence also.”

[044-030] “I know it,” said Fionn.

[044-031] “I went into the land of the Saxons and your father chased me out of

[044-032] that land. And then, in Lochlann, at the battle of Cnocha your father

[044-033] and I met at last, foot to foot, eye to eye, and there, Fionn!”

[044-034] “And there, Goll?”

[044-035] “And there I killed your father.”

[044-036] Fionn sat rigid and unmoving, his face stony and terrible as the face of

[044-037] a monument carved on the side of a cliff.

[044-038] “Tell all your tale,” said he.

[044-039] “At that battle I beat the Lochlannachs. I penetrated to the hold of the

[044-040] Danish king, and I took out of his dungeon the men who had lain

[044-041] there for a year and were awaiting their deaths. I liberated fifteen

[044-042] prisoners, and one of them was Fionn.”

[044-043] “It is true,” said Fionn.

[044-044] Goll’s anger fled at the word.

[044-045] “Do not be jealous of me, dear heart, for if I had twice the tribute I

[044-046] would give it to you and to Ireland.”

[044-047] But at the word jealous the Chief’s anger revived.

[044-048] “It is an impertinence,” he cried, “to boast at this table that you

[044-049] killed my father.”

[044-050] “By my hand,” Goll replied, “if Fionn were to treat me as his father did

[044-051] I would treat Fionn the way I treated Fionn’s father.”

[044-052] Fionn closed his eyes and beat away the anger that was rising within

[044-053] him. He smiled grimly.

[044-054] “If I were so minded, I would not let that last word go with you, Goll,

[044-055] for I have here an hundred men for every man of yours.”

[044-056] Goll laughed aloud.

[044-057] “So had your father,” he said.

[044-058] Fionn’s brother, Cairell Whiteskin, broke into the conversation with a

[044-059] harsh laugh.

[044-060] “How many of Fionn’s household has the wonderful Goll put down?” he

[044-061] cried.

[044-062] But Goll’s brother, bald Cona’n the Swearer, turned a savage eye on

[044-063] Cairell.

[044-064] “By my weapons,” said he, “there were never less than an hundred-and-one

[044-065] men with Goll, and the least of them could have put you down easily

[044-066] enough.”

[044-067] “Ah?” cried Cairell. “And are you one of the hundred-and-one, old

[044-068] scaldhead?”

[044-069] “One indeed, my thick-witted, thin-livered Cairell, and I undertake to

[044-070] prove on your hide that what my brother said was true and that what your

[044-071] brother said was false.”

[044-072] “You undertake that,” growled Cairell, and on the word he loosed a

[044-073] furious buffet at Con’an, which Cona’n returned with a fist so big that

[044-074] every part of Cairell’s face was hit with the one blow. The two then

[044-075] fell into grips, and went lurching and punching about the great hall.

[044-076] Two of [[oscar|Oscar]]’s sons could not bear to see their uncle being worsted, and

[044-077] they leaped at Cona’n, and two of Goll’s sons rushed at them. Then Oscar

[044-078] himself leaped up, and with a hammer in either hand he went battering

[044-079] into the melee.

[044-080] “I thank the gods,” said Cona’n, “for the chance of killing yourself,

[044-081] Oscar.”

[044-082] These two encountered then, and Oscar knocked a groan of distress out of

[044-083] Cona’n. He looked appealingly at his brother Art og mac Morna, and that

[044-084] powerful champion flew to his aid and wounded Oscar. Oisi’n, Oscar’s

[044-085] father, could not abide that; he dashed in and quelled Art Og. Then

[044-086] Rough Hair mac Morna wounded Oisin and was himself tumbled by mac Lugac,

[044-087] who was again wounded by Gara mac Morna.

[044-088] The banqueting hall was in tumult. In every part of it men were giving

[044-089] and taking blows. Here two champions with their arms round each other’s

[044-090] necks were stamping round and round in a slow, sad dance. Here were two

[044-091] crouching against each other, looking for a soft place to hit. Yonder a

[044-092] big-shouldered person lifted another man in his arms and threw him at a

[044-093] small group that charged him. In a retired corner a gentleman stood in

[044-094] a thoughtful attitude while he tried to pull out a tooth that had been

[044-095] knocked loose.

[044-096] “You can’t fight,” he mumbled, “with a loose shoe or a loose tooth.”

[044-097] “Hurry up with that tooth,” the man in front of him grum-bled, “for I

[044-098] want to knock out another one.”

[044-099] Pressed against the wall was a bevy of ladies, some of whom were

[044-100] screaming and some laughing and all of whom were calling on the men to

[044-101] go back to their seats.

[044-102] Only two people remained seated in the hall.

[044-103] Goll sat twisted round watching the progress of the brawl critically,

[044-104] and Fionn, sitting opposite, watched Goll.

[044-105] Just then Faelan, another of Fionn’s sons, stormed the hall with three

[044-106] hundred of the [[fianna|Fianna]], and by this force all Goll’s people were put out

[044-107] of doors, where the fight continued.

[044-108] Goll looked then calmly on Fionn.

[044-109] “Your people are using their weapons,” said he.

[044-110] “Are they?” Fionn inquired as calmly, and as though addressing the air.

[044-111] “In the matter of weapons--!” said Goll.

[044-112] And the hard-fighting pillar of battle turned to where his arms hung on

[044-113] the wall behind him. He took his solid, well-balanced sword in his fist,

[044-114] over his left arm his ample, bossy shield, and, with another side-look

[044-115] at Fionn, he left the hall and charged irresistibly into the fray.

[044-116] Fionn then arose. He took his accoutrements from the wall also and

[044-117] strode out. Then he raised the triumphant Fenian shout and went into the

[044-118] combat.

[044-119] That was no place for a sick person to be. It was not the corner which

[044-120] a slender-fingered woman would choose to do up her hair; nor was it the

[044-121] spot an ancient man would select to think quietly in, for the tumult of

[044-122] sword on sword, of axe on shield, the roar of the contending parties,

[044-123] the crying of wounded men, and the screaming of frightened women

[044-124] destroyed peace, and over all was the rallying cry of [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] and

[044-125] the great shout of Fionn.

[044-126] Then [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus True-Lips]] gathered about him all the poets of the Fianna,

[044-127] and they surrounded the combatants. They began to chant and intone

[044-128] long, heavy rhymes and incantations, until the rhythmic beating of their

[044-129] voices covered even the noise of war, so that the men stopped hacking

[044-130] and hewing, and let their weapons drop from their hands. These were

[044-131] picked up by the poets and a reconciliation was effected between the two

[044-132] parties.

[044-133] But Fionn affirmed that he would make no peace with clann-Morna until

[044-134] the matter had been judged by the king, [[cormac-mac-art|Cormac mac Art]], and by his

[044-135] daughter Ailve, and by his son Cairbre of Ana Life’ and by Fintan the

[044-136] chief poet. Goll agreed that the affair should be submitted to that

[044-137] court, and a day was appointed, a fortnight from that date, to meet

[044-138] at [[tara|Tara of the Kings]] for judgement. Then the hall was cleansed and the

[044-139] banquet recommenced.

[044-140] Of Fionn’s people eleven hundred of men and women were dead, while of

[044-141] Goll’s people eleven men and fifty women were dead. But it was through

[044-142] fright the women died, for not one of them had a wound or a bruise or a

[044-143] mark.
