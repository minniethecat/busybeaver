[003-001] One day they were talking together about the majesty of God and His

[003-002] love, for although Tuan had now received much instruction on this

[003-003] subject he yet needed more, and he laid as close a siege on [[finnian|Finnian]]

[003-004] as Finnian had before that laid on him. But man works outwardly and

[003-005] inwardly. After rest he has energy, after energy he needs repose; so,

[003-006] when we have given instruction for a time, we need instruction, and must

[003-007] receive it or the spirit faints and wisdom herself grows bitter.

[003-008] Therefore Finnian said: “Tell me now about yourself, dear heart.”

[003-009] But Tuan was avid of information about the True God. “No, no,” he

[003-010] said, “the past has nothing more of interest for me, and I do not wish

[003-011] anything to come between my soul and its instruction; continue to teach

[003-012] me, dear friend and saintly father.”

[003-013] “I will do that,” Finnian replied, “but I must first meditate deeply on

[003-014] you, and must know you well. Tell me your past, my beloved, for a man is

[003-015] his past, and is to be known by it.”

[003-016] But Tuan pleaded: “Let the past be content with itself, for man needs

[003-017] forgetfulness as well as memory.”

[003-018] “My son,” said Finnian, “all that has ever been done has been done for

[003-019] the glory of God, and to confess our good and evil deeds is part of

[003-020] instruction; for the soul must recall its acts and abide by them, or

[003-021] renounce them by confession and penitence. Tell me your genealogy first,

[003-022] and by what descent you occupy these lands and [[dun|stronghold]], and then I

[003-023] will examine your acts and your conscience.”

[003-024] Tuan replied obediently: “I am known as [[tuan-mac-cairill|Tuan, son of Cairill]], son of

[003-025] Muredac Red-neck, and these are the hereditary lands of my father.”

[003-026] The saint nodded.

[003-027] “I am not as well acquainted with [[ulster|Ulster]] genealogies as I should be, yet

[003-028] I know something of them. I am by blood a Leinsterman,” he continued.

[003-029] “Mine is a long pedigree,” Tuan murmured.

[003-030] Finnian received that information with respect and interest.

[003-031] “I also,” he said, “have an honourable record.”

[003-032] His host continued: “I am indeed Tuan, the son of Starn, the son of

[003-033] Sera, who was brother to Partholon.”

[003-034] “But,” said Finnian in bewilderment, “there is an error here, for you

[003-035] have recited two different genealogies.”

[003-036] “Different genealogies, indeed,” replied Tuan thoughtfully, “but they

[003-037] are my genealogies.”

[003-038] “I do not understand this,” Finnian declared roundly.

[003-039] “I am now known as Tuan mac [[cairill|Cairill]],” the other replied, “but in the

[003-040] days of old I was known as Tuan mac Starn, mac Sera.”

[003-041] “The brother of Partholon,” the saint gasped.

[003-042] “That is my pedigree,” Tuan said.

[003-043] “But,” Finnian objected in bewilderment, “Partholon came to Ireland not

[003-044] long after the Flood.”

[003-045] “I came with him,” said Tuan mildly.

[003-046] The saint pushed his chair back hastily, and sat staring at his host,

[003-047] and as he stared the blood grew chill in his veins, and his hair crept

[003-048] along his scalp and stood on end.
