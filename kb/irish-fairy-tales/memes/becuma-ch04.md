[063-001] It is part of the education of a prince to be a good chess player, and

[063-002] to continually exercise his mind in view of the judgements that he will

[063-003] be called upon to give and the knotty, tortuous, and perplexing matters

[063-004] which will obscure the issues which he must judge. Art, the son of Conn,

[063-005] was sitting at chess with Cromdes, his father’s magician.

[063-006] “Be very careful about the move you are going to make,” said Cromdes.

[063-007] “CAN I be careful?” Art inquired. “Is the move that you are thinking of

[063-008] in my power?”

[063-009] “It is not,” the other admitted.

[063-010] “Then I need not be more careful than usual,” Art replied, and he made

[063-011] his move.

[063-012] “It is a move of banishment,” said Cromdes.

[063-013] “As I will not banish myself, I suppose my father will do it, but I do

[063-014] not know why he should.”

[063-015] “Your father will not banish you.”

[063-016] “Who then?” “Your mother.”

[063-017] “My mother is dead.”

[063-018] “You have a new one,” said the magician.

[063-019] “Here is news,” said Art. “I think I shall not love my new mother.”

[063-020] “You will yet love her better than she loves you,” said Cromdes, meaning

[063-021] thereby that they would hate each other.

[063-022] While they spoke the king and Becuma entered the palace.

[063-023] “I had better go to greet my father,” said the young man.

[063-024] “You had better wait until he sends for you,” his companion advised, and

[063-025] they returned to their game.

[063-026] In due time a messenger came from the king directing Art to leave [[tara|Tara]]

[063-027] instantly, and to leave Ireland for one full year.

[063-028] He left Tara that night, and for the space of a year he was not seen

[063-029] again in Ireland. But during that period things did not go well with the

[063-030] king nor with Ireland. Every year before that time three crops of corn

[063-031] used to be lifted off the land, but during Art’s absence there was no

[063-032] corn in Ireland and there was no milk. The whole land went hungry.

[063-033] Lean people were in every house, lean cattle in every field; the bushes

[063-034] did not swing out their timely berries or seasonable nuts; the bees went

[063-035] abroad as busily as ever, but each night they returned languidly, with

[063-036] empty pouches, and there was no honey in their hives when the honey

[063-037] season came. People began to look at each other questioningly,

[063-038] meaningly, and dark remarks passed between them, for they knew that a

[063-039] bad harvest means, somehow, a bad king, and, although this belief can be

[063-040] combated, it is too firmly rooted in wisdom to be dismissed.

[063-041] The poets and magicians met to consider why this disaster should have

[063-042] befallen the country and by their arts they discovered the truth about

[063-043] the king’s wife, and that she was [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]], and they

[063-044] discovered also the cause of her banishment from the [[faery|Many-Coloured Land]]

[063-045] that is beyond the sea, which is beyond even the grave.

[063-046] They told the truth to the king, but he could not bear to be parted from

[063-047] that slender-handed, gold-haired, thin-lipped, blithe enchantress, and

[063-048] he required them to discover some means whereby he might retain his wife

[063-049] and his crown. There was a way and the magicians told him of it.

[063-050] “If the son of a sinless couple can be found and if his blood be mixed

[063-051] with the soll of Tara the blight and ruin will depart from Ireland,”

[063-052] said the magicians.

[063-053] “If there is such a boy I will find him,” cried the Hundred Fighter.

[063-054] At the end of a year Art returned to Tara. His father delivered to him

[063-055] the sceptre of Ireland, and he set out on a journey to find the son of a

[063-056] sinless couple such as he had been told of.
