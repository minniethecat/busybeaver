[018-001] A new life for Fionn in the robber’s den that was hidden in a vast cold

[018-002] marsh.

[018-003] A tricky place that would be, with sudden exits and even suddener

[018-004] entrances, and with damp, winding, spidery places to hoard treasure in,

[018-005] or to hide oneself in.

[018-006] If the robber was a solitary he would, for lack of someone else,

[018-007] have talked greatly to Fionn. He would have shown his weapons and

[018-008] demonstrated how he used them, and with what slash he chipped his

[018-009] victim, and with what slice he chopped him. He would have told why a

[018-010] slash was enough for this man and why that man should be sliced. All men

[018-011] are masters when one is young, and Fionn would have found knowledge here

[018-012] also. He would have seen Fiacuil’s great spear that had thirty rivets

[018-013] of Arabian gold in its socket, and that had to be kept wrapped up and

[018-014] tied down so that it would not kill people out of mere spitefulness. It

[018-015] had come from [[faery|Faery]], out of the Shi’ of [[aillen-mac-midna|Aillen mac Midna]], and it would

[018-016] be brought back again later on between the same man’s shoulder-blades.

[018-017] What tales that man could tell a boy, and what questions a boy could ask

[018-018] him. He would have known a thousand tricks, and because our instinct is

[018-019] to teach, and because no man can keep a trick from a boy, he would show

[018-020] them to Fionn.

[018-021] There was the marsh too; a whole new life to be learned; a complicated,

[018-022] mysterious, dank, slippery, reedy, treacherous life, but with its own

[018-023] beauty and an allurement that could grow on one, so that you could

[018-024] forget the solid world and love only that which quaked and gurgled.

[018-025] In this place you may swim. By this sign and this you will know if it is

[018-026] safe to do so, said [[fiacuil-mac-cona|Fiacuil mac Cona]]; but in this place, with this sign

[018-027] on it and that, you must not venture a toe.

[018-028] But where Fionn would venture his toes his ears would follow.

[018-029] There are coiling weeds down there, the robber counselled him; there are

[018-030] thin, tough, snaky binders that will trip you and grip you, that will

[018-031] pull you and will not let you go again until you are drowned; until

[018-032] you are swaying and swinging away below, with outstretched arms, with

[018-033] outstretched legs, with a face all stares and smiles and jockeyings,

[018-034] gripped in those leathery arms, until there is no more to be gripped of

[018-035] you even by them.

[018-036] “Watch these and this and that,” Fionn would have been told, “and always

[018-037] swim with a knife in your teeth.”

[018-038] He lived there until his guardians found out where he was and came after

[018-039] him. Fiacuil gave him up to them, and he was brought home again to

[018-040] the [[slieve-bloom|woods of Slieve Bloom]], but he had gathered great knowledge and new

[018-041] supplenesses.

[018-042] The [[clann-morna|sons of Morna]] left him alone for a long time. Having made their

[018-043] essay they grew careless.

[018-044] “Let him be,” they said. “He will come to us when the time comes.”

[018-045] But it is likely too that they had had their own means of getting

[018-046] information about him. How he shaped? what muscles he had? and did

[018-047] he spring clean from the mark or had he to get off with a push? Fionn

[018-048] stayed with his guardians and hunted for them. He could run a deer down

[018-049] and haul it home by the reluctant skull. “Come on, Goll,” he would say

[018-050] to his stag, or, lifting it over a tussock with a tough grip on the

[018-051] snout, “Are you coming, bald Cona’n, or shall I kick you in the neck?”

[018-052] The time must have been nigh when he would think of taking the world

[018-053] itself by the nose, to haul it over tussocks and drag it into his

[018-054] pen; for he was of the breed in whom mastery is born, and who are good

[018-055] masters.

[018-056] But reports of his prowess were getting abroad. Clann-Morna began to

[018-057] stretch itself uneasily, and, one day, his guardians sent him on his

[018-058] travels.

[018-059] “It is best for you to leave us now,” they said to the tall stripling,

[018-060] “for the sons of Morna are watching again to kill you.”

[018-061] The woods at that may have seemed haunted. A stone might sling at one

[018-062] from a tree-top; but from which tree of a thousand trees did it come? An

[018-063] arrow buzzing by one’s ear would slide into the ground and quiver there

[018-064] silently, menacingly, hinting of the brothers it had left in the quiver

[018-065] behind; to the right? to the left? how many brothers? in how many

[018-066] quivers...? Fionn was a woodsman, but he had only two eyes to look with,

[018-067] one set of feet to carry him in one sole direction. But when he was

[018-068] looking to the front what, or how many whats, could be staring at him

[018-069] from the back? He might face in this direction, away from, or towards a

[018-070] smile on a hidden face and a finger on a string. A lance might slide at

[018-071] him from this bush or from the one yonder.. In the night he might have

[018-072] fought them; his ears against theirs; his noiseless feet against their

[018-073] lurking ones; his knowledge of the wood against their legion: but during

[018-074] the day he had no chance.

[018-075] Fionn went to seek his fortune, to match himself against all that might

[018-076] happen, and to carve a name for himself that will live while Time has an

[018-077] ear and knows an Irishman.
