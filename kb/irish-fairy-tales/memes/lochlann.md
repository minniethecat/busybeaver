## Overview

Lochlann is the Irish name for the land of the Northmen — the Viking raiders who appear throughout the Fenian tales as both fearsome invaders and worthy adversaries. It encompasses both the mortal realm of Scandinavia (Norway, referred to as "White Lochlann") and carries echoes of the Otherworld in its remote, sea-girt mystery. The "Lochlannachs" (men of Lochlann) are recurring antagonists in the tales, their invasions providing the backdrop for key events in the life of [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]].

## Location

Lochlann lies across the sea from Ireland. The text distinguishes "White Lochlann" — explicitly glossed as Norway — from a broader, perhaps more mythologized Lochlann [044-028]. Its court is a place of "great ease" where warriors and visitors are received with hospitality [074-009], and it possesses its own royal lineage and internal politics. The geography is never described in detail, but the journey to Lochlann typically takes "three days' time" by ship [075-021].

## In the Tales

### The Invasion during Oisin's Mother

The men of Lochlann launch an invasion of Ireland that serves as the catalyst for Saeve's tragedy in *Oisin's Mother*:

> "It happened that the men of Lochlann came on an expedition against Ireland." [033-001]

Fionn departs to fight them, leaving Saeve at [[wide-allen-of-leinster|Allen]]. The Lochlannachs are driven back in "a short one" fight [033-008], but Fionn's absence provides the opportunity for the [[fear-doirche|Fear Doirche]] to enact his deception. While the Fianna occupy the coast awaiting further raids — "we went to Ben Edar or encountered the Lochlannachs" [034-014] — Saeve is transformed into a fawn.

### The Battle of Cnocha

Lochlann is the site of a pivotal battle in Fionn's lineage. Goll mac Morna tells Fionn directly: "At that battle I beat the Lochlannachs. I penetrated to the hold of the King of Lochlann" [044-039]. It was at "the battle of Cnocha" in Lochlann that Fionn's father [[cumhal|Cumhal]] fell [044-032] — a revelation that deepens the complicated relationship between [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] and Fionn.

### White Lochlann

Fionn's own martial prowess extends to Lochlann: "Your father went into White Lochlann (Norway) and took it" [044-028]. This conquest places Lochlann within Fionn's sphere of influence, transforming it from purely antagonistic territory into a subject kingdom.

### The Court of Lochlann

Later in the tales, the Court of Lochlann appears as a place of refuge and diplomatic activity. A character "stayed at the Court of Lochlann in great ease" [074-009], and the court harbors "the most jealous and cantankerous female in Lochlann, the Black Hag" [074-027] — indicating that Lochlann has its own complex society, rivalries, and supernatural denizens.

### The Three Hundred

In battle against Lochlann forces, "three hundred of the men of Lochlann were killed" [075-026], demonstrating both the scale of the conflict and the formidable nature of Irish resistance.

## Mythological Significance

Lochlann occupies a unique position in Irish mythology — it is simultaneously a real geographic referent (Scandinavia, the source of Viking raids) and a mythologized Otherworld across the sea. Its dual nature mirrors other Irish Otherworld locations ([[faery|Faery]], [[land-of-promise|Land of Promise]]) while remaining distinctly martial. Unlike the magical Otherworld of the Tuatha De Danann, Lochlann is a realm conquered by strength of arms — Fionn "took it," Goll "penetrated to the hold." It represents the external threat that unites the Fianna and the proving ground where Irish heroes demonstrate their worth against foreign power.
