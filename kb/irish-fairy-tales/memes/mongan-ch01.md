[070-001] The abbot of the [[moville|Monastery of Moville]] sent word to the story-tellers of

[070-002] Ireland that when they were in his neighbourhood they should call at

[070-003] the monastery, for he wished to collect and write down the stories which

[070-004] were in danger of being forgotten.

[070-005] “These things also must be told,” said he.

[070-006] In particular he wished to gather tales which told of the deeds that had

[070-007] been done before the Gospel came to Ireland.

[070-008] “For,” said he, “there are very good tales among those ones, and it

[070-009] would be a pity if the people who come after us should be ignorant of

[070-010] what happened long ago, and of the deeds of their fathers.”

[070-011] So, whenever a story-teller chanced in that neighbourhood he was

[070-012] directed to the monastery, and there he received a welcome and his fill

[070-013] of all that is good for man.

[070-014] The abbot’s manuscript boxes began to fill up, and he used to regard

[070-015] that growing store with pride and joy. In the evenings, when the days

[070-016] grew short and the light went early, he would call for some one of these

[070-017] manuscripts and have it read to him by candle-light, in order that he

[070-018] might satisfy himself that it was as good as he had judged it to be on

[070-019] the previous hearing.

[070-020] One day a story-teller came to the monastery, and, like all the others,

[070-021] he was heartily welcomed and given a great deal more than his need.

[070-022] He said that his name was [[cairide|Cairide]]’, and that he had a story to tell

[070-023] which could not be bettered among the stories of Ireland.

[070-024] The abbot’s eyes glistened when he heard that. He rubbed his hands

[070-025] together and smiled on his guest.

[070-026] “What is the name of your story?” he asked.

[070-027] “It is called ‘[[mongan|Mongan]]’s Frenzy.’”

[070-028] “I never heard of it before,” cried the abbot joyfully.

[070-029] “I am the only man that knows it,” Cairide’ replied.

[070-030] “But how does that come about?” the abbot inquired.

[070-031] “Because it belongs to my family,” the story-teller answered. “There

[070-032] was a Cairide’ of my nation with Mongan when he went into [[faery|Faery]]. This

[070-033] Cairide’ listened to the story when it was first told. Then he told

[070-034] it to his son, and his son told it to his son, and that son’s

[070-035] great-great-grandson’s son told it to his son’s son, and he told it to

[070-036] my father, and my father told it to me.”

[070-037] “And you shall tell it to me,” cried the abbot triumphantly.

[070-038] “I will indeed,” said Cairide’. Vellum was then brought and quills. The

[070-039] copyists sat at their tables. Ale was placed beside the story-teller,

[070-040] and he told this tale to the abbot.
