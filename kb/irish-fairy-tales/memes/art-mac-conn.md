## Overview

Art, the son of [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]], is the crown prince of Ireland who becomes a central figure in "[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]." When Conn is seduced away to [[faery|Faery]] by Becuma's enchantment, Art must rule as regent — and when his father returns with a supernatural wife, Art must navigate the political and moral crisis that follows. He appears alongside [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] as a defender of honour against expediency.

## Identity

Art is the son of [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn Cétchathach]] — [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]] — the High King of Ireland. When Conn departs for [[faery|Faery]], he instructs "his son Art to take the rule during his absence" [061-037]. Art's fame spreads widely: Becuma herself tells Conn that "the fame of his son Art had reached even the Many-coloured Land" [062-013]. He is described as wise and careful in judgement — when faced with a difficult chess-like move, Art inquires "CAN I be careful?" before making it [063-007]–[063-008]. His return to [[tara|Tara]] after the year of regency restores joy to his father, who has suffered greatly under Becuma's influence [063-054].

## In the Tales

### Becuma of the White Skin

#### Regency

When Conn is lured away to [[faery|Faery]] by Becuma, Art is left as regent. He dispatches a message to his father at the end of a year directing Art to "leave [[tara|Tara]] instantly and to go into exile" — a consequence of Becuma's malice [063-026]. Art obeys and departs, but "during Art's absence there was no peace in Ireland" [063-031]. The land itself suffers without its rightful heir.

When Art returns to [[tara|Tara]] at the year's end, he finds his father aged and wearied. Conn's joy at seeing his son is described as a moment of restoration: "At the end of a year Art returned to [[tara|Tara]]. His father wept on his shoulder" [063-054].

#### The Council on Segda's Life

Art's most prominent moment of moral conviction comes during the council that decides the fate of young Segda — the "son of a virgin" whose blood, the [[druid|druids]] claim, must be spilled to restore Ireland's fertility. The council is divided: the [[enchantment|magic]]ians and the common people argue that the national good requires the boy's death, while "Art, and Fionn the son of Uail, and the princes of the land were outraged at the idea that one who had been placed under their protection should be hurt by any hand" [066-020]–[066-022].

Art's position is one of absolute principle: the King's oath of protection is inviolable, and no circumstance — not even the suffering of the land — can justify the murder of a protected guest. This pits him against the [[druid|druids]] and the crowd, who argue that "national honour dissolved and absolved all personal honour" [066-027]–[066-028]. The conflict is profound: Art stands for the position that "no man had aught but his personal honour, and that above it not the gods, not even Ireland, could be placed" [066-029]–[066-030].

Angry parties form around these positions, with Art and Fionn on one side and the men of Ireland on the other [066-020]. The debate spills from the Council Hall into the marketplace and streets of [[tara|Tara]] [066-026]–[066-027].

## Significance

Art mac Conn represents the principle of personal honour against the claims of collective necessity — one of the deepest moral tensions in Irish mythological tradition. His refusal to sacrifice Segda, even when the [[druid|druids]] and popular opinion demand it, marks him as a king who values oath-keeping above expediency. He is the bridge between his father Conn's heroic generation and the next era: Art mac Conn will himself become High King, and his son [[cormac-mac-art|Cormac mac Art]] will be the greatest of the pre-Christian Irish kings.
