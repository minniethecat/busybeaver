[035-001] For many years after that time, when he was not fighting against the

[035-002] enemies of Ireland, Fionn was searching and hunting through the length

[035-003] and breadth of the country in the hope that he might again chance on his

[035-004] lovely lady from the Shi’. Through all that time he slept in misery each

[035-005] night and he rose each day to grief. Whenever he hunted he brought only

[035-006] the hounds that he trusted, [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan, Lomaire, Brod, and Lomlu;

[035-007] for if a fawn was chased each of these five great dogs would know if

[035-008] that was a fawn to be killed or one to be protected, and so there was

[035-009] small danger to [[saeve|Saeve]] and a small hope of finding her.

[035-010] Once, when seven years had passed in fruitless search, Fionn and the

[035-011] chief nobles of the [[fianna|Fianna]] were hunting Ben Gulbain. All the hounds of

[035-012] the Fianna were out, for Fionn had now given up hope of encountering

[035-013] the Flower of [[allen|Allen]]. As the hunt swept along the sides of the hill there

[035-014] arose a great outcry of hounds from a narrow place high on the slope

[035-015] and, over all that uproar there came the savage baying of Fionn’s own

[035-016] dogs.

[035-017] “What is this for?” said Fionn, and with his companions he pressed to

[035-018] the spot whence the noise came.

[035-019] “They are fighting all the hounds of the Fianna,” cried a champion.

[035-020] And they were. The five wise hounds were in a circle and were giving

[035-021] battle to an hundred dogs at once. They were bristling and terrible, and

[035-022] each bite from those great, keen jaws was woe to the beast that received

[035-023] it. Nor did they fight in silence as was their custom and training, but

[035-024] between each onslaught the great heads were uplifted, and they pealed

[035-025] loudly, mournfully, urgently, for their master.

[035-026] “They are calling on me,” he roared.

[035-027] And with that he ran, as he had only once before run, and the men who

[035-028] were nigh to him went racing as they would not have run for their lives.

[035-029] They came to the narrow place on the slope of the mountain, and they saw

[035-030] the five great hounds in a circle keeping off the other dogs, and in

[035-031] the middle of the ring a little boy was standing. He had long, beautiful

[035-032] hair, and he was naked. He was not daunted by the terrible combat and

[035-033] clamour of the hounds. He did not look at the hounds, but he stared like

[035-034] a young prince at Fionn and the champions as they rushed towards him

[035-035] scattering the pack with the butts of their spears. When the fight was

[035-036] over, Bran and Sceo’lan ran whining to the little boy and licked his

[035-037] hands.

[035-038] “They do that to no one,” said a bystander. “What new master is this

[035-039] they have found?”

[035-040] Fionn bent to the boy. “Tell me, my little prince and pulse, what your

[035-041] name is, and how you have come into the middle of a hunting-pack, and

[035-042] why you are naked?”

[035-043] But the boy did not understand the language of the men of Ireland. He

[035-044] put his hand into Fionn’s, and the Chief felt as if that little hand had

[035-045] been put into his heart. He lifted the lad to his great shoulder.

[035-046] “We have caught something on this hunt,” said he to [[caelte-mac-ronan|Caelte mac Rongn]].

[035-047] “We must bring this treasure home. You shall be one of the Fianna-[[fionn-mac-uail|Finn]],

[035-048] my darling,” he called upwards.

[035-049] The boy looked down on him, and in the noble trust and fearlessness of

[035-050] that regard Fionn’s heart melted away.

[035-051] “My little fawn!” he said.

[035-052] And he remembered that other fawn. He set the boy between his knees and

[035-053] stared at him earnestly and long.

[035-054] “There is surely the same look,” he said to his wakening heart; “that is

[035-055] the very eye of Saeve.”

[035-056] The grief flooded out of his heart as at a stroke, and joy foamed into

[035-057] it in one great tide. He marched back singing to the encampment, and men

[035-058] saw once more the merry Chief they had almost forgotten.
