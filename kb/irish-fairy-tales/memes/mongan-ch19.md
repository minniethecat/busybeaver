[088-001] THEY sat in a place where they could watch the castle and compose

[088-002] themselves after their journey.

[088-003] “How are we going to get into the castle?” asked mac an Da’v.

[088-004] For there were hatchetmen on guard in the big gateway, and there were

[088-005] spearmen at short intervals around the walls, and men to throw hot

[088-006] porridge off the roof were standing in the right places.

[088-007] “If we cannot get in by hook, we will get in by crook,” said [[mongan|Mongan]].

[088-008] “They are both good ways,” said Mac an Da’v, “and whichever of them you

[088-009] decide on I’ll stick by.”

[088-010] Just then they saw [[ivell-of-the-shining-cheeks|the Hag of the Mill]] coming out of the mill which was

[088-011] down the road a little.

[088-012] Now the Hag of the Mill was a bony, thin pole of a hag with odd feet.

[088-013] That is, she had one foot that was too big for her, so that when she

[088-014] lifted it up it pulled her over; and she had one foot that was too small

[088-015] for her, so that when she lifted it up she didn’t know what to do with

[088-016] it. She was so long that you thought you would never see the end of her,

[088-017] and she was so thin that you thought you didn’t see her at all. One of

[088-018] her eyes was set where her nose should be and there was an ear in its

[088-019] place, and her nose itself was hanging out of her chin, and she had

[088-020] whiskers round it. She was dressed in a red rag that was really a hole

[088-021] with a fringe on it, and she was singing “Oh, hush thee, my one love” to

[088-022] a cat that was yelping on her shoulder.

[088-023] She had a tall skinny dog behind her called Brotar. It hadn’t a tooth in

[088-024] its head except one, and it had the toothache in that tooth. Every few

[088-025] steps it used to sit down on its hunkers and point its nose straight

[088-026] upwards, and make a long, sad complaint about its tooth; and after that

[088-027] it used to reach its hind leg round and try to scratch out its tooth;

[088-028] and then it used to be pulled on again by the straw rope that was round

[088-029] its neck, and which was tied at the other end to the hag’s heaviest

[088-030] foot.

[088-031] There was an old, knock-kneed, raw-boned, one-eyed, little-winded,

[088-032] heavy-headed mare with her also. Every time it put a front leg forward

[088-033] it shivered all over the rest of its legs backwards, and when it put a

[088-034] hind leg forward it shivered all over the rest of its legs frontwards,

[088-035] and it used to give a great whistle through its nose when it was out of

[088-036] breath, and a big, thin hen was sitting on its croup. Mongan looked on

[088-037] the Hag of the Mill with delight and affection.

[088-038] “This time,” said he to mac an Da’v, “I’ll get back my wife.”

[088-039] “You will indeed,” said mac an Da’v heartily, “and you’ll get mine back

[088-040] too.”

[088-041] “Go over yonder,” said Mongan, “and tell the Hag of the Mill that I want

[088-042] to talk to her.”

[088-043] Mac an Da’v brought her over to him.

[088-044] “Is it true what the servant man said?” she asked.

[088-045] “What did he say?” said Mongan.

[088-046] “He said you wanted to talk to me.”

[088-047] “It is true,” said Mongan.

[088-048] “This is a wonderful hour and a glorious minute,” said the hag, “for

[088-049] this is the first time in sixty years that any one wanted to talk to me.

[088-050] Talk on now,” said she, “and I’ll listen to you if I can remember how to

[088-051] do it. Talk gently,” said she, “the way you won’t disturb the animals,

[088-052] for they are all sick.”

[088-053] “They are sick indeed,” said mac an Da’v pityingly.

[088-054] “The cat has a sore tail,” said she, “by reason of sitting too close to

[088-055] a part of the hob that was hot. The dog has a toothache, the horse has a

[088-056] pain in her stomach, and the hen has the pip.”

[088-057] “Ah, it’s a sad world,” said mac an Da’v.

[088-058] “There you are!” said the hag.

[088-059] “Tell me,” Mongan commenced, “if you got a wish, what it is you would

[088-060] wish for?”

[088-061] The hag took the cat off her shoulder and gave it to mac an Da’v.

[088-062] “Hold that for me while I think,” said she.

[088-063] “Would you like to be a lovely young girl?” asked Mongan.

[088-064] “I’d sooner be that than a skinned eel,” said she.

[088-065] “And would you like to marry me or the King of [[leinster|Leinster]]?” “I’d like to

[088-066] marry either of you, or both of you, or whichever of you came first.”

[088-067] “Very well,” said Mongan, “you shall have your wish.”

[088-068] He touched her with his finger, and the instant he touched her all

[088-069] dilapidation and wryness and age went from her, and she became so

[088-070] beautiful that one dared scarcely look on her, and so young that she

[088-071] seemed but sixteen years of age.

[088-072] “You are not the Hag of the Mill any longer,” said Mongan, “you are

[088-073] Ivell of the Shining Cheeks, daughter of the King of [[munster|Munster]].”

[088-074] He touched the dog too, and it became a little silky lapdog that could

[088-075] nestle in your palm. Then he changed the old mare into a brisk, piebald

[088-076] palfrey. Then he changed himself so that he became the living image of

[088-077] Ae, the son of the King of [[connacht|Connaught]], who had just been married to Ivell

[088-078] of the Shining Cheeks, and then he changed mac an Da’v into the likeness

[088-079] of Ae’s attendant, and then they all set off towards the fortress,

[088-080] singing the song that begins: My wife is nicer than any one’s wife, Any

[088-081] one’s wife, any one’s wife, My wife is nicer than any one’s wife, Which

[088-082] nobody can deny.
