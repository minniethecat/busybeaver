[030-001] In course of time the news came to Fionn that his mother’s sister

[030-002] was not living with Iollan. He at once sent a messenger calling

[030-003] for fulfilment of the pledge that had been given to the [[fianna|Fianna]], and

[030-004] demanding the instant return of [[tuiren|Tuiren]]. Iollan was in a sad condition

[030-005] when this demand was made. He guessed that Uct Dealv had a hand in the

[030-006] disappearance of his queen, and he begged that time should be given him

[030-007] in which to find the lost girl. He promised if he could not discover

[030-008] her within a certain period that he would deliver his body into Fionn’s

[030-009] hands, and would abide by whatever judgement Fionn might pronounce. The

[030-010] great captain agreed to that.

[030-011] “Tell the wife-loser that I will have the girl or I will have his head,”

[030-012] said Fionn.

[030-013] Iollan set out then for [[faery|Faery]]. He knew the way, and in no great time he

[030-014] came to the hill where Uct Dealv was.

[030-015] It was hard to get Uct Dealv to meet him, but at last she consented, and

[030-016] they met under the apple boughs of Faery.

[030-017] “Well!” said Uct Dealv. “Ah! Breaker of Vows and Traitor to Love,” said

[030-018] she.

[030-019] “Hail and a blessing,” said Iollan humbly.

[030-020] “By my hand,” she cried, “I will give you no blessing, for it was no

[030-021] blessing you left with me when we parted.”

[030-022] “I am in danger,” said Iollan.

[030-023] “What is that to me?” she replied fiercely.

[030-024] “Fionn may claim my head,” he murmured.

[030-025] “Let him claim what he can take,” said she.

[030-026] “No,” said Iollan proudly, “he will claim what I can give.”

[030-027] “Tell me your tale,” said she coldly.

[030-028] Iollan told his story then, and, he concluded, “I am certain that you

[030-029] have hidden the girl.”

[030-030] “If I save your head from Fionn,” the woman of the Shi’ replied, “then

[030-031] your head will belong to me.”

[030-032] “That is true,” said Iollan.

[030-033] “And if your head is mine, the body that goes under it is mine. Do you

[030-034] agree to that?”

[030-035] “I do,” said Iollan.

[030-036] “Give me your pledge,” said Uct Dealv, “that if I save you from this

[030-037] danger you will keep me as your sweetheart until the end of life and

[030-038] time.”

[030-039] “I give that pledge,” said Iollan.

[030-040] Uct Dealv went then to the house of [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus Fionnliath]], and she broke the

[030-041] enchantment that was on the hound, so that Tuiren’s own shape came back

[030-042] to her; but in the matter of two small whelps, to which the hound had

[030-043] given birth, the enchantment could not be broken, so they had to remain

[030-044] as they were. These two whelps were [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan. They were sent

[030-045] to Fionn, and he loved them for ever after, for they were loyal and

[030-046] affectionate, as only dogs can be, and they were as intelligent as human

[030-047] beings. Besides that, they were Fionn’s own cousins.

[030-048] Tuiren was then asked in marriage by Lugaidh who had loved her so long.

[030-049] He had to prove to her that he was not any other woman’s sweetheart,

[030-050] and when he proved that they were married, and they lived happily ever

[030-051] after, which is the proper way to live. He wrote a poem beginning:

[030-052] “Lovely the day. Dear is the eye of the dawn--”

[030-053] And a thousand merry people learned it after him.

[030-054] But as to Fergus Fionnliath, he took to his bed, and he stayed there for

[030-055] a year and a day suffering from blighted affection, and he would have

[030-056] died in the bed only that Fionn sent him a special pup, and in a week

[030-057] that young hound became the Star of Fortune and the very Pulse of his

[030-058] Heart, so that he got well again, and he also lived happily ever after.
