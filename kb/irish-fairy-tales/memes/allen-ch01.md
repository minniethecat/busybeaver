[043-001] “I think,” said Cairell Whiteskin, “that although judgement was given

[043-002] against Fionn, it was Fionn had the rights of it.”

[043-003] “He had eleven hundred killed,” said Cona’n amiably, “and you may call

[043-004] that the rights of it if you like.”

[043-005] “All the same--” Cairell began argumentatively.

[043-006] “And it was you that commenced it,” Cona’n continued.

[043-007] “Ho! Ho!” Cairell cried. “Why, you are as much to blame as I am.”

[043-008] “No,” said Cona’n, “for you hit me first.”

[043-009] “And if we had not been separated--” the other growled.

[043-010] “Separated!” said Cona’n, with a grin that made his beard poke all

[043-011] around his face.

[043-012] “Yes, separated. If they had not come between us I still think--”

[043-013] “Don’t think out loud, dear heart, for you and I are at peace by law.”

[043-014] “That is true,” said Cairell, “and a man must stick by a judgement. Come

[043-015] with me, my dear, and let us see how the youngsters are shaping in the

[043-016] school. One of them has rather a way with him as a swordsman.”

[043-017] “No youngster is any good with a sword,” [[conan-the-swearer|Conan]] replied.

[043-018] “You are right there,” said Cairell. “It takes a good ripe man for that

[043-019] weapon.”

[043-020] “Boys are good enough with slings,” Confro continued, “but except for

[043-021] eating their fill and running away from a fight, you can’t count on

[043-022] boys.”

[043-023] The two bulky men turned towards the school of the [[fianna|Fianna]].

[043-024] It happened that [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] had summoned the gentlemen of the Fianna

[043-025] and their wives to a banquet. Everybody came, for a banquet given by

[043-026] Fionn was not a thing to be missed. There was Goll mor mac Morna and his

[043-027] people; Fionn’s son Oisi’n and his grandson [[oscar|Oscar]]. There was Dermod of

[043-028] the Gay Face, [[caelte-mac-ronan|Caelte mac Ronan]]--but indeed there were too many to be

[043-029] told of, for all the pillars of war and battle-torches of the Gael were

[043-030] there.

[043-031] The banquet began.

[043-032] Fionn sat in the Chief Captain’s seat in the middle of the fort; and

[043-033] facing him, in the place of honour, he placed the mirthful Goll mac

[043-034] Morna; and from these, ranging on either side, the nobles of the Fianna

[043-035] took each the place that fitted his degree and patrimony.

[043-036] After good eating, good conversation; and after good conversation,

[043-037] sleep--that is the order of a banquet: so when each person had been

[043-038] served with food to the limit of desire the butlers carried in shining,

[043-039] and jewelled drinking-horns, each having its tide of smooth, heady

[043-040] liquor. Then the young heroes grew merry and audacious, the ladies

[043-041] became gentle and kind, and the poets became wonders of knowledge and

[043-042] prophecy. Every eye beamed in that assembly, and on Fionn every eye was

[043-043] turned continually in the hope of a glance from the great, mild hero.

[043-044] Goll spoke to him across the table enthusiastically.

[043-045] “There is nothing wanting to this banquet, O Chief,” said he.

[043-046] And Fionn smiled back into that eye which seemed a well of tenderness

[043-047] and friendship.

[043-048] “Nothing is wanting,” he replied, “but a well-shaped poem.” A crier

[043-049] stood up then, holding in one hand a length of coarse iron links and in

[043-050] the other a chain of delicate, antique silver. He shook the iron chain

[043-051] so that the servants and followers of the household should be silent,

[043-052] and he shook the silver one so that the nobles and poets should hearken

[043-053] also.

[043-054] Fergus, called True-Lips, the poet of the Fianna-Finn, then sang of

[043-055] Fionn and his ancestors and their deeds. When he had finished Fionn and

[043-056] Oisi’n and Oscar and mac Lugac of the Terrible Hand gave him rare and

[043-057] costly presents, so that every person wondered at their munificence, and

[043-058] even the poet, accustomed to the liberality of kings and princes, was

[043-059] astonished at his gifts.

[043-060] Fergus then turned to the side of [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]], and he sang of the

[043-061] Forts, the Destructions, the Raids, and the Wooings of clann-Morna; and

[043-062] as the poems succeeded each other, Goll grew more and more jovial and

[043-063] contented. When the songs were finished Goll turned in his seat.

[043-064] “Where is my runner?” he cried.

[043-065] He had a woman runner, a marvel for swiftness and trust. She stepped

[043-066] forward.

[043-067] “I am here, royal captain.”

[043-068] “Have you collected my tribute from Denmark?”

[043-069] “It is here.”

[043-070] And, with help, she laid beside him the load of three men of doubly

[043-071] refined gold. Out of this treasure, and from the treasure of rings and

[043-072] bracelets and torques that were with him, Goll mac Morna paid Fergus for

[043-073] his songs, and, much as Fionn had given, Goll gave twice as much.

[043-074] But, as the banquet proceeded, Goll gave, whether it was to harpers or

[043-075] prophets or jugglers, more than any one else gave, so that Fionn became

[043-076] displeased, and as the banquet proceeded he grew stern and silent.
