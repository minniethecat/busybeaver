## Overview

Becuma of the White Skin is a woman of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] whose fall from grace drives one of the strangest and most haunting tales in Irish mythology. Banished from the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] for a love affair with a son of [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannán mac Lir]], she descends to the mortal world, marries the [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|High King Conn]] of the Hundred Battles, and brings a wasting curse upon Ireland — a blight that can only be lifted when she herself is cast out.

## Identity

Becuma Cneisgel — "Becuma of the White Skin" — is a woman of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]], the divine beings who dwell in the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] mounds of Ireland. Her name emphasizes the unearthly beauty that marks her as belonging to the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] [[rath]]er than the mortal realm. She is a figure of ambiguous morality: neither wholly villainous nor sympathetic, she acts throughout her tale with a mixture of calculation, self-preservation, and occasional cruelty that reflects both her supernatural origins and her fundamentally human predicament.

## In the Tales

### Banishment from the Sídhe

Becuma's tale begins with her crime: an affair with one of the sons of [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannán mac Lir]] [060-017]–[060-019]. In the strict moral order of the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]], this transgression could not be lightly forgiven. She was sentenced to banishment: "every door and gate of the Shi' was shut against her, and every way was barred" [060-027]–[060-033]. Becuma "was forced to appear in the world of men" [060-044], arriving in Ireland as an exile stripped of her supernatural privileges.

### Marriage to Conn

Becuma presents herself to the court of the High King at [[tara|Tara]]. She murmurs to Conn that she "would not be disliked" by him [062-033], and though she knows he lacks a wife, "indeed she did" already know this before arriving [062-036]. Conn marries her and brings her into the royal household. But Becuma's presence brings calamity: the land of Ireland begins to wither. The harvests fail. The king's people ask why misfortune has fallen, and the answer is clear — "Becuma is the cause, and she must be the remedy" [066-076].

### Jealousy and Plotting

Banishment did not make Becuma repentant: "the evil conditions which had fallen on Becuma did not leave her repentant" [067-004]. Instead, "her eyes grew mean, and her heart peevish" [067-011]. When Conn's son Art — a young man of surpassing beauty — returns to [[tara|Tara]], Becuma becomes fixated on him. She watches him from the palace lawn, and at a game of chess between Art and a stranger, "Becuma grew thoughtful" [067-052] — plotting her next move.

### The Binding of Art

Becuma's most dangerous act is the supernatural binding she places on Art. Invoking her [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] powers, she declares: "I bind you to eat no food in Ireland until you find and bring to me the wand of Curoi" [067-087] — sending the king's son on what she believes to be a fatal quest. Her foster-sister Aine attempts to aid Art, but Becuma's binding curse is formidable.

### Exile

In the end, Becuma's schemes unravel. Conn, faced with the continued blight on his kingdom, orders her to depart. The tale ends with Becuma driven from [[tara|Tara]] — a twice-exiled woman, cast out first from the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] and then from the mortal court, her supernatural beauty insufficient to sustain her in either world.

## Significance

Becuma stands apart from the warriors and kings who dominate Irish myth. She is neither hero nor monster but a complex agent of disorder whose actions, driven by self-interest and wounded pride, set in motion a chain of events that afflicts an entire kingdom. Her story also serves as a bridge between the divine realm of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] and the mortal world of the High Kings, illustrating how the supernatural intrusions of the older gods continue to shape human destiny in the Fenian Cycle.
