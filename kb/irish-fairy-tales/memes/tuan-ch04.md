[004-001] But [[finnian|Finnian]] was not one who remained long in bewilderment. He thought on

[004-002] the might of God and he became that might, and was tranquil.

[004-003] He was one who loved God and Ireland, and to the person who could

[004-004] instruct him in these great themes he gave all the interest of his mind

[004-005] and the sympathy of his heart.

[004-006] “It is a wonder you tell me, my beloved,” he said. “And now you must

[004-007] tell me more.”

[004-008] “What must I tell?” asked Tuan resignedly.

[004-009] “Tell me of the beginning of time in Ireland, and of the bearing of

[004-010] Partholon, the son of Noah’s son.”

[004-011] “I have almost forgotten him,” said Tuan. “A greatly bearded, greatly

[004-012] shouldered man he was. A man of sweet deeds and sweet ways.”

[004-013] “Continue, my love,” said Finnian.

[004-014] “He came to Ireland in a ship. Twenty-four men and twenty-four women

[004-015] came with him. But before that time no man had come to Ireland, and in

[004-016] the western parts of the world no human being lived or moved. As we drew

[004-017] on Ireland from the sea the country seemed like an unending forest. Far

[004-018] as the eye could reach, and in whatever direction, there were trees; and

[004-019] from these there came the unceasing singing of birds. Over all that land

[004-020] the sun shone warm and beautiful, so that to our sea-weary eyes, our

[004-021] wind-tormented ears, it seemed as if we were driving on Paradise.

[004-022] “We landed and we heard the rumble of water going gloomily through the

[004-023] darkness of the forest. Following the water we came to a glade where

[004-024] the sun shone and where the earth was warmed, and there Partholon rested

[004-025] with his twenty-four couples, and made a city and a livelihood.

[004-026] “There were fish in the rivers of Eire’, there were animals in her

[004-027] coverts. Wild and shy and monstrous creatures ranged in her plains and

[004-028] forests. Creatures that one could see through and walk through. Long we

[004-029] lived in ease, and we saw new animals grow,--the bear, the wolf, the

[004-030] badger, the deer, and the boar.

[004-031] “Partholon’s people increased until from twenty-four couples there came

[004-032] five thousand people, who lived in amity and contentment although they

[004-033] had no wits.”

[004-034] “They had no wits!” Finnian commented.

[004-035] “They had no need of wits,” Tuan said.

[004-036] “I have heard that the first-born were mindless,” said Finnian.

[004-037] “Continue your story, my beloved.”

[004-038] “Then, sudden as a rising wind, between one night and a morning, there

[004-039] came a sickness that bloated the stomach and purpled the skin, and on

[004-040] the seventh day all of the race of Partholon were dead, save one man

[004-041] only.” “There always escapes one man,” said Finnian thoughtfully.

[004-042] “And I am that man,” his companion affirmed.

[004-043] Tuan shaded his brow with his hand, and he remembered backwards through

[004-044] incredible ages to the beginning of the world and the first days of

[004-045] Eire’. And Finnian, with his blood again running chill and his scalp

[004-046] crawling uneasily, stared backwards with him.
