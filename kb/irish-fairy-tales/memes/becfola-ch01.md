[038-001] We do not know where [[becfola|Becfola]] came from. Nor do we know for certain where

[038-002] she went to. We do not even know her real name, for the name Becfola,

[038-003] “Dowerless” or “Small-dowered,” was given to her as a nickname. This

[038-004] only is certain, that she disappeared from the world we know of, and

[038-005] that she went to a realm where even conjecture may not follow her.

[038-006] It happened in the days when Dermod, son of the famous Ae of Slane, was

[038-007] monarch of all Ireland. He was unmarried, but he had many foster-sons,

[038-008] princes from the Four Provinces, who were sent by their fathers as

[038-009] tokens of loyalty and affection to the Ard-Ri, and his duties as a

[038-010] foster-father were righteously acquitted. Among the young princes of his

[038-011] household there was one, Crimthann, son of Ae, King of [[leinster|Leinster]], whom

[038-012] the High King preferred to the others over whom he held fatherly sway.

[038-013] Nor was this wonderful, for the lad loved him also, and was as eager and

[038-014] intelligent and modest as becomes a prince.

[038-015] The High King and Crimthann would often set out from [[tara|Tara]] to hunt and

[038-016] hawk, sometimes unaccompanied even by a servant; and on these excursions

[038-017] the king imparted to his foster-son his own wide knowledge of forest

[038-018] craft, and advised him generally as to the bearing and duties of a

[038-019] prince, the conduct of a court, and the care of a people.

[038-020] Dermod mac Ae delighted in these solitary adventures, and when he

[038-021] could steal a day from policy and affairs he would send word privily to

[038-022] Crimthann. The boy, having donned his hunting gear, would join the king

[038-023] at a place arranged between them, and then they ranged abroad as chance

[038-024] might direct.

[038-025] On one of these adventures, as they searched a flooded river to find the

[038-026] ford, they saw a solitary woman in a chariot driving from the west.

[038-027] “I wonder what that means?” the king exclaimed thoughtfully.

[038-028] “Why should you wonder at a woman in a chariot?” his companion inquired,

[038-029] for Crimthann loved and would have knowledge.

[038-030] “Good, my Treasure,” Dermod answered, “our minds are astonished when we

[038-031] see a woman able to drive a cow to pasture, for it has always seemed to

[038-032] us that they do not drive well.”

[038-033] Crimthann absorbed instruction like a sponge and digested it as rapidly.

[038-034] “I think that is justly said,” he agreed.

[038-035] “But,” Dermod continued, “when we see a woman driving a chariot of two

[038-036] horses, then we are amazed indeed.”

[038-037] When the machinery of anything is explained to us we grow interested,

[038-038] and Crimthann became, by instruction, as astonished as the king was.

[038-039] “In good truth,” said he, “the woman is driving two horses.”

[038-040] “Had you not observed it before?” his master asked with kindly malice.

[038-041] “I had observed but not noticed,” the young man admitted.

[038-042] “Further,” said the king, “surmise is aroused in us when we discover a

[038-043] woman far from a house; for you will have both observed and noticed that

[038-044] women are home-dwellers, and that a house without a woman or a woman

[038-045] without a house are imperfect objects, and although they be but half

[038-046] observed, they are noticed on the double.”

[038-047] “There is no doubting it,” the prince answered from a knitted and

[038-048] thought-tormented brow.

[038-049] “We shall ask this woman for information about herself,” said the king

[038-050] decidedly.

[038-051] “Let us do so,” his ward agreed

[038-052] “The king’s majesty uses the words ‘we’ and ‘us’ when referring to

[038-053] the king’s majesty,” said Dermod, “but princes who do not yet

[038-054] rule territories must use another form of speech when referring to

[038-055] themselves.”

[038-056] “I am very thoughtless,” said Crimthann humbly.

[038-057] The king kissed him on both cheeks.

[038-058] “Indeed, my dear heart and my son, we are not scolding you, but you must

[038-059] try not to look so terribly thoughtful when you think. It is part of the

[038-060] art of a ruler.”

[038-061] “I shall never master that hard art,” lamented his fosterling.

[038-062] “We must all master it,” Dermod replied. “We may think with our minds

[038-063] and with our tongues, but we should never think with our noses and with

[038-064] our eyebrows.”

[038-065] The woman in the chariot had drawn nigh to the ford by which they were

[038-066] standing, and, without pause, she swung her steeds into the shallows and

[038-067] came across the river in a tumult of foam and spray.

[038-068] “Does she not drive well?” cried Crimthann admiringly.

[038-069] “When you are older,” the king counselled him, “you will admire that

[038-070] which is truly admirable, for although the driving is good the lady is

[038-071] better.”

[038-072] He continued with enthusiasm.

[038-073] “She is in truth a wonder of the world and an endless delight to the

[038-074] eye.”

[038-075] She was all that and more, and, as she took the horses through the river

[038-076] and lifted them up the bank, her flying hair and parted lips and all the

[038-077] young strength and grace of her body went into the king’s eye and could

[038-078] not easily come out again.

[038-079] Nevertheless, it was upon his ward that the lady’s gaze rested, and if

[038-080] the king could scarcely look away from her, she could, but only with an

[038-081] equal effort, look away from Crimthann.

[038-082] “Halt there!” cried the king.

[038-083] “Who should I halt for?” the lady demanded, halting all the same, as is

[038-084] the manner of women, who rebel against command and yet receive it.

[038-085] “Halt for Dermod!”

[038-086] “There are Dermods and Dermods in this world,” she quoted.

[038-087] “There is yet but one Ard-Ri’,” the monarch answered.

[038-088] She then descended from the chariot and made her reverence.

[038-089] “I wish to know your name?” said he.

[038-090] But at this demand the lady frowned and answered decidedly:

[038-091] “I do not wish to tell it.”

[038-092] “I wish to know also where you come from and to what place you are

[038-093] going?”

[038-094] “I do not wish to tell any of these things.”

[038-095] “Not to the king!”

[038-096] “I do not wish to tell them to any one.”

[038-097] Crimthann was scandalised.

[038-098] “Lady,” he pleaded, “you will surely not withhold information from the

[038-099] Ard-Ri’?”

[038-100] But the lady stared as royally on the High King as the High King did on

[038-101] her, and, whatever it was he saw in those lovely eyes, the king did not

[038-102] insist.

[038-103] He drew Crimthann apart, for he withheld no instruction from that lad.

[038-104] “My heart,” he said, “we must always try to act wisely, and we should

[038-105] only insist on receiving answers to questions in which we are personally

[038-106] concerned.”

[038-107] Crimthann imbibed all the justice of that remark.

[038-108] “Thus I do not really require to know this lady’s name, nor do I care

[038-109] from what direction she comes.”

[038-110] “You do not?” Crimthann asked.

[038-111] “No, but what I do wish to know is, Will she marry me?”

[038-112] “By my hand that is a notable question,” his companion stammered.

[038-113] “It is a question that must be answered,” the king cried triumphantly.

[038-114] “But,” he continued, “to learn what woman she is, or where she comes

[038-115] from, might bring us torment as well as information. Who knows in what

[038-116] adventures the past has engaged her!”

[038-117] And he stared for a profound moment on disturbing, sinister horizons,

[038-118] and Crimthann meditated there with him.

[038-119] “The past is hers,” he concluded, “but the future is ours, and we shall

[038-120] only demand that which is pertinent to the future.”

[038-121] He returned to the lady.

[038-122] “We wish you to be our wife,” he said. And he gazed on her benevolently

[038-123] and firmly and carefully when he said that, so that her regard could

[038-124] not stray otherwhere. Yet, even as he looked, a tear did well into those

[038-125] lovely eyes, and behind her brow a thought moved of the beautiful boy

[038-126] who was looking at her from the king’s side.

[038-127] But when the High King of Ireland asks us to marry him we do not refuse,

[038-128] for it is not a thing that we shall be asked to do every day in the

[038-129] week, and there is no woman in the world but would love to rule it in

[038-130] Tara.

[038-131] No second tear crept on the lady’s lashes, and, with her hand in the

[038-132] king’s hand, they paced together towards the palace, while behind them,

[038-133] in melancholy mood, Crimthann mac Ae led the horses and the chariot.
