# Samhain

## Overview

Samhain (pronounced "Sow-in") is one of the four great seasonal festivals of early Ireland, marking the transition from the light half of the year to the dark half — the end of harvest and the onset of winter. In Irish mythology, Samhain is far more than a calendrical marker: it is a liminal time when the boundaries between the mortal world and the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] dissolve, allowing supernatural beings to cross freely, and when the normal rules of human society are suspended. The [[feast-of-tara|Feast of Tara]], the great assembly of kings and warriors, was held at Samhain-tide, making it simultaneously a political, social, and supernatural occasion.

## Nature

- **Type of concept**: Ritual / festival — a recurring seasonal observance combining feasting, assembly, and the acknowledgment of supernatural forces
- **Associated characters**: [[becfola|Becfola]] (whose adventure at [[tara|Tara]] occurs at Samhain), the High King at [[tara|Tara]]
- **Cycles**: Fenian and Mythological — Samhain appears as a backdrop across both cycles
- **Historical counterpart**: Corresponds to the modern Halloween and the Christian All Saints' Day, which overlay the pre-Christian festival

## In the Tales

Samhain provides the setting for the opening of *The [[wooing-of-becfola|Wooing of Becfola]]*. The corpus describes the scale and significance of the occasion: "It was Samhain-tide, and the feast of [[tara|Tara]] was being held" — the conjunction of the seasonal festival with the royal assembly at the symbolic center of Ireland [022-005]. The gathering was immense: "For weeks before Samhain an endless stream of passengers" traveled toward [[tara|Tara]] [022-015], a description that conveys the festival's role as the year's great social convergence.

The supernatural dimension is explicit: "It is to be known that on the night of the Feast of Samhain the doors" of the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] were open [023-001], and the peril was real — "of Samhain none but a madman would quit the shelter of a house" [024-005]. This passage establishes Samhain as a time when the normal protections of civilization — walls, doors, the company of others — were all that stood between a mortal and the chaos of a world suddenly permeable to supernatural forces.

> It was Samhain-tide, and the feast of Tara was being held, at which all the princes and nobles of Ireland were assembled [022-005].

## Significance

Samhain embodies one of the deepest structural principles of Irish mythology: the existence of thresholds — temporal as well as spatial — across which the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] intrudes into mortal affairs. The festival's dual nature, as both a celebration of community and a moment of maximum supernatural danger, mirrors the broader Irish mythological pattern in which the greatest feasts (see also [[feast-of-tara]]) are the settings for the greatest crises. Samhain is not merely a date on the calendar; it is a narrative device that authors use to justify the eruption of wonder and terror into an otherwise orderly world.
