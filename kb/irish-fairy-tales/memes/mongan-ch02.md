[071-001] Said [[cairide|Cairide]]’:

[071-002] [[mongan|Mongan]]’s wife at that time was Bro’tiarna, the Flame Lady. She was

[071-003] passionate and fierce, and because the blood would flood suddenly to her

[071-004] cheek, so that she who had seemed a lily became, while you looked upon

[071-005] her, a rose, she was called Flame Lady. She loved Mongan with ecstasy

[071-006] and abandon, and for that also he called her Flame Lady.

[071-007] But there may have been something of calculation even in her wildest

[071-008] moment, for if she was delighted in her affection she was tormented in

[071-009] it also, as are all those who love the great ones of life and strive to

[071-010] equal themselves where equality is not possible.

[071-011] For her husband was at once more than himself and less than himself.

[071-012] He was less than himself because he was now Mongan. He was more than

[071-013] himself because he was one who had long disappeared from the world of

[071-014] men. His lament had been sung and his funeral games played many,

[071-015] many years before, and Bro’tiarna sensed in him secrets, experiences,

[071-016] knowledges in which she could have no part, and for which she was

[071-017] greedily envious.

[071-018] So she was continually asking him little, simple questions a’ propos of

[071-019] every kind of thing.

[071-020] She weighed all that he said on whatever subject, and when he talked in

[071-021] his sleep she listened to his dream.

[071-022] The knowledge that she gleaned from those listenings tormented her

[071-023] far more than it satisfied her, for the names of other women were

[071-024] continually on his lips, sometimes in terms of dear affection, sometimes

[071-025] in accents of anger or despair, and in his sleep he spoke familiarly

[071-026] of people whom the story-tellers told of, but who had been dead for

[071-027] centuries. Therefore she was perplexed, and became filled with a very

[071-028] rage of curiosity.

[071-029] Among the names which her husband mentioned there was one which, because

[071-030] of the frequency with which it appeared, and because of the tone of

[071-031] anguish and love and longing in which it was uttered, she thought of

[071-032] oftener than the others: this name was [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]]. Although she questioned

[071-033] and cross-questioned Cairide’, her story-teller, she could discover

[071-034] nothing about a lady who had been known as the Black Duck. But one night

[071-035] when Mongan seemed to speak with Duv Laca he mentioned her father as

[071-036] Fiachna Duv mac Demain, and the story-teller said that king had been

[071-037] dead for a vast number of years.

[071-038] She asked her husband then, boldly, to tell her the story of Duv Laca,

[071-039] and under the influence of their mutual love he promised to tell it to

[071-040] her some time, but each time she reminded him of his promise he became

[071-041] confused, and said that he would tell it some other time.

[071-042] As time went on the poor Flame Lady grew more and more jealous of Duv

[071-043] Laca, and more and more certain that, if only she could know what

[071-044] had happened, she would get some ease to her tormented heart and some

[071-045] assuagement of her perfectly natural curiosity. Therefore she lost no

[071-046] opportunity of reminding Mongan of his promise, and on each occasion he

[071-047] renewed the promise and put it back to another time.
