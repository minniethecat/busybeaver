# Battle of Cnocha

The **Battle of Cnocha** (*Cath Cnocha* in Irish) was a pivotal conflict fought in Lochlann (the Norse lands) [044-032], in which Uail mac Baiscne, captain of the [[fianna|Fianna]] and father of [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]], met his death. The battle shattered Clann Baiscne, placed [[clann-morna|Clann Morna]] in control of the [[fianna|Fianna]], and forced the infant Fionn into a hidden childhood in the forests of [[slieve-bloom|Slieve Bloom]] — setting in motion the entire arc of Fionn's rise to leadership.

## Background

Before the battle, Uail mac Baiscne held the captaincy of the [[fianna|Fianna of Ireland]], the elite warrior-band that defended the high kingship. Clann Morna, a rival warrior clan led by the formidable [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]], sought to oust Uail and seize control. The resulting feud between clann-Baiscne and clann-Morna was "deadly" [054-017], a blood-feud that would shape the political landscape of the Fenian world for a generation.

The battle itself took place not on Irish soil but in Lochlann — the Norse lands across the sea — suggesting that the conflict between the two clans had drawn in foreign powers or that the Fianna's campaigns extended beyond Ireland's shores [044-032].

## The Battle

> And then, in Lochlann, at the battle of Cnocha your father [044-032]

At Cnocha, Uail mac Baiscne fell. The exact circumstances of his death are contested in the narrative tradition — one version of events is explicitly noted as "not correct" [044-001], and the location of Cnocha itself is disputed, with the text recording disagreement about whether it lay in Lochlann or elsewhere [044-001]. This narrative uncertainty reflects the passage of events through oral tradition before their written recording.

What is certain is the outcome: Uail was killed, and his clan was broken. "He had taken to the wilds when clann-Baiscne was broken" [017-055] describes the fate of one of Uail's followers, indicating the thoroughness of the defeat. The breaking of a clan was not merely military — it meant the dissolution of its social bonds, the scattering of its members, and the forfeiture of its rank and standing.

## Aftermath

The consequences of Cnocha radiated through every corner of the Fenian world:

**The Captaincy Lost.** Clann Morna seized the leadership of the [[fianna|Fianna]], placing it under the control of the clan that had killed Fionn's father. The enmity between the two clans would simmer beneath every subsequent gathering of the Fianna, erupting decades later in events like the [[brawl-at-allen|Brawl at Allen]].

**The Hidden Heir.** Fionn, the infant son of Uail, became a hunted child. His mother [[muirne|Muirne]] could not keep him, for the victorious Clann Morna would have killed any heir of Baiscne. Instead, two women druids — [[bovmall|Bovmall]], who was Uail's sister and thus Fionn's aunt [014-001], and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]] — took the baby into the deep woods of [[slieve-bloom|Slieve Bloom]] [012-004]. There, under the [[fosterage]] system, they raised him in secrecy, training his body and mind until he was strong enough to reclaim his birthright.

**The Blood-Feud.** The deadly feud between clann-Baiscne and clann-Morna [054-017] persisted for decades. It colored every interaction between [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn]] and [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] after Fionn eventually assumed the captaincy. Even Fionn's closest followers remained divided by the old loyalties, and the tension between the clans is a recurring thread throughout the Fenian tales.

## Legacy

The Battle of Cnocha is the foundational tragedy of the Fenian Cycle. Without it, there is no hidden childhood, no fosterage in the wild, no slow rise of the boy-hero to reclaim his father's place. The entire arc of Fionn's story — from hunted infant to captain of the Fianna to the wise and prudent leader who appears throughout the tales — begins in the blood and breaking of Cnocha.

Fionn himself never forgot his lineage. Throughout his life, he identified himself proudly as "Fionn, the son of Uail, the son of Baiscne" [022-092], a formula that honored the father he never knew and the clan whose breaking defined his earliest years.
