[059-001] As each man came out of the cave he gave a jump and a shout; the courage

[059-002] of the world went into him and he felt that he could fight twenty. But

[059-003] while they were talking over the adventure and explaining how it had

[059-004] happened, a vast figure strode over the side of the hill and descended

[059-005] among them. It was [[conaran|Conaran]]’s fourth daughter.

[059-006] If the other three had been terrible to look on, this one was more

[059-007] terrible than the three together. She was clad in iron plate, and she

[059-008] had a wicked sword by her side and a knobby club in her hand She halted

[059-009] by the bodies of her sisters, and bitter tears streamed down into her

[059-010] beard.

[059-011] “Alas, my sweet ones,” said she, “I am too late.”

[059-012] And then she stared fiercely at Fionn.

[059-013] “I demand a combat,” she roared.

[059-014] “It is your right,” said Fionn. He turned to his son.

[059-015] “Oisi’n, my heart, kill me this honourable hag.” But for the only time

[059-016] in his life Oisi’n shrank from a combat.

[059-017] “I cannot do it,” he said, “I feel too weak.”

[059-018] Fionn was astounded. “[[oscar|Oscar]],” he said, “will you kill me this great

[059-019] hag?”

[059-020] Oscar stammered miserably. “I would not be able to,” he said.

[059-021] Cona’n also refused, and so did Caelte mac Rona’n and mac Lugac, for

[059-022] there was no man there but was terrified by the sight of that mighty and

[059-023] valiant harridan.

[059-024] Fionn rose to his feet. “I will take this combat myself,” he said

[059-025] sternly.

[059-026] And he swung his buckler forward and stretched his right hand to the

[059-027] sword. But at that terrible sight Goll mae Morna blushed deeply and

[059-028] leaped from the ground.

[059-029] “No, no,” he cried; “no, my soul, Fionn, this would not be a proper

[059-030] combat for you. I take this fight.”

[059-031] “You have done your share, Goll,” said the captain.

[059-032] “I should finish the fight I began,” Goll continued, “for it was I who

[059-033] killed the two sisters of this valiant hag, and it is against me the

[059-034] feud lies.”

[059-035] “That will do for me,” said the horrible daughter of Conaran. “I will

[059-036] kill Goll mor mac Morna first, and after that I will kill Fionn, and

[059-037] after that I will kill every Fenian of the [[fianna|Fianna-Finn]].”

[059-038] “You may begin, Goll,” said Fionn, “and I give you my blessing.”

[059-039] Goll then strode forward to the fight, and the hag moved against him

[059-040] with equal alacrity. In a moment the heavens rang to the clash of swords

[059-041] on bucklers. It was hard to with-stand the terrific blows of that mighty

[059-042] female, for her sword played with the quickness of lightning and smote

[059-043] like the heavy crashing of a storm. But into that din and encirclement

[059-044] Goll pressed and ventured, steady as a rock in water, agile as a

[059-045] creature of the sea, and when one of the combatants retreated it was

[059-046] the hag that gave backwards. As her foot moved a great shout of joy rose

[059-047] from the Fianna. A snarl went over the huge face of the monster and

[059-048] she leaped forward again, but she met Goll’s point in the road; it went

[059-049] through her, and in another moment Goll took her head from its shoulders

[059-050] and swung it on high before Fionn.

[059-051] As the Fianna turned homewards Fionn spoke to his great champion and

[059-052] enemy.

[059-053] “Goll,” he said, “I have a daughter.”

[059-054] “A lovely girl, a blossom of the dawn,” said Goll.

[059-055] “Would she please you as a wife?” the chief demanded.

[059-056] “She would please me,” said Goll.

[059-057] “She is your wife,” said Fionn.

[059-058] But that did not prevent Goll from killing Fionn’s brother Cairell later

[059-059] on, nor did it prevent Fionn from killing Goll later on again, and

[059-060] the last did not prevent Goll from rescuing Fionn out of hell when the

[059-061] Fianna-[[fionn-mac-uail|Finn]] were sent there under the new God. Nor is there any reason

[059-062] to complain or to be astonished at these things, for it is a mutual

[059-063] world we llve in, a give-and-take world, and there is no great harm in

[059-064] it.
