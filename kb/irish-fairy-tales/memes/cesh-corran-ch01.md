[054-001] [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] was the most prudent chief of an army in the world, but

[054-002] he was not always prudent on his own account. Discipline sometimes

[054-003] irked him, and he would then take any opportunity that presented for an

[054-004] adventure; for he was not only a soldier, he was a poet also, that is, a

[054-005] man of science, and whatever was strange or unusual had an irresistible

[054-006] at-traction for him. Such a soldier was he that, single-handed, he could

[054-007] take the [[fianna|Fianna]] out of any hole they got into, but such an inveterate

[054-008] poet was he that all the Fianna together could scarcely retrieve him

[054-009] from the abysses into which he tumbled. It took him to keep the Fianna

[054-010] safe, but it took all the Fianna to keep their captain out of danger.

[054-011] They did not complain of this, for they loved every hair of Fionn’s head

[054-012] more than they loved their wives and children, and that was reasonable

[054-013] for there was never in the world a person more worthy of love than Fionn

[054-014] was.

[054-015] [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] did not admit so much in words, but he admitted it in

[054-016] all his actions, for although he never lost an opportunity of killing

[054-017] a member of Fionn’s family (there was deadly feud between clann-Baiscne

[054-018] and clann-Morna), yet a call from Fionn brought Goll raging to his

[054-019] assistance like a lion that rages tenderly by his mate. Not even a call

[054-020] was necessary, for Goll felt in his heart when Fionn was threatened, and

[054-021] he would leave Fionn’s own brother only half-killed to fly where his arm

[054-022] was wanted. He was never thanked, of course, for although Fionn loved

[054-023] Goll he did not like him, and that was how Goll felt towards Fionn.

[054-024] Fionn, with Cona’n the Swearer and the dogs [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan, was

[054-025] sitting on the hunting-mound at the top of [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]]. Below and around

[054-026] on every side the Fianna were beating the coverts in Legney and Brefny,

[054-027] ranging the fastnesses of Glen Dallan, creeping in the nut and beech

[054-028] forests of Carbury, spying among the woods of Kyle Conor, and ranging

[054-029] the wide plain of Moy Conal.

[054-030] The great captain was happy: his eyes were resting on the sights he

[054-031] liked best--the sunlight of a clear day, the waving trees, the pure

[054-032] sky, and the lovely movement of the earth; and his ears were filled with

[054-033] delectable sounds--the baying of eager dogs, the clear calling of young

[054-034] men, the shrill whistling that came from every side, and each sound of

[054-035] which told a definite thing about the hunt. There was also the plunge

[054-036] and scurry of the deer, the yapping of badgers, and the whirr of birds

[054-037] driven into reluctant flight.
