[032-001] Late that night, when he was preparing for rest, the door of Fionn’s

[032-002] chamber opened gently and a young woman came into the room. The captain

[032-003] stared at her, as he well might, for he had never seen or imagined to

[032-004] see a woman so beautiful as this was. Indeed, she was not a woman, but

[032-005] a young girl, and her bearing was so gently noble, her look so modestly

[032-006] high, that the champion dared scarcely look at her, although he could

[032-007] not by any means have looked away.

[032-008] As she stood within the doorway, smiling, and shy as a flower,

[032-009] beautifully timid as a fawn, the Chief communed with his heart.

[032-010] “She is the Sky-woman of the Dawn,” he said. “She is the light on the

[032-011] foam. She is white and odorous as an apple-blossom. She smells of spice

[032-012] and honey. She is my beloved beyond the women of the world. She shall

[032-013] never be taken from me.”

[032-014] And that thought was delight and anguish to him: delight because of such

[032-015] sweet prospect, anguish because it was not yet realised, and might not

[032-016] be.

[032-017] As the dogs had looked at him on the chase with a look that he did not

[032-018] understand, so she looked at him, and in her regard there was a question

[032-019] that baffled him and a statement which he could not follow.

[032-020] He spoke to her then, mastering his heart to do it.

[032-021] “I do not seem to know you,” he said.

[032-022] “You do not know me indeed,” she replied.

[032-023] “It is the more wonderful,” he continued gently, “for I should know

[032-024] every person that is here. What do you require from me?”

[032-025] “I beg your protection, royal captain.”

[032-026] “I give that to all,” he answered. “Against whom do you desire

[032-027] protection?”

[032-028] “I am in terror of the Fear Doirche.”

[032-029] “The Dark Man of the Shi?”

[032-030] “He is my enemy,” she said.

[032-031] “He is mine now,” said Fionn. “Tell me your story.”

[032-032] “My name is [[saeve|Saeve]], and I am a woman of [[faery|Faery]],” she commenced. “In the

[032-033] Shi’ many men gave me their love, but I gave my love to no man of my

[032-034] country.”

[032-035] “That was not reasonable,” the other chided with a blithe heart.

[032-036] “I was contented,” she replied, “and what we do not want we do not lack.

[032-037] But if my love went anywhere it went to a mortal, a man of the men of

[032-038] Ireland.”

[032-039] “By my hand,” said Fionn in mortal distress, “I marvel who that man can

[032-040] be!”

[032-041] “He is known to you,” she murmured. “I lived thus in the peace of Faery,

[032-042] hearing often of my mortal champion, for the rumour of his great deeds

[032-043] had gone through the Shi’, until a day came when the Black Magician

[032-044] of the Men of God put his eye on me, and, after that day, in whatever

[032-045] direction I looked I saw his eye.”

[032-046] She stopped at that, and the terror that was in her heart was on her

[032-047] face. “He is everywhere,” she whispered. “He is in the bushes, and on

[032-048] the hill. He looked up at me from the water, and he stared down on

[032-049] me from the sky. His voice commands out of the spaces, and it demands

[032-050] secretly in the heart. He is not here or there, he is in all places at

[032-051] all times. I cannot escape from him,” she said, “and I am afraid,” and

[032-052] at that she wept noiselessly and stared on Fionn.

[032-053] “He is my enemy,” Fionn growled. “I name him as my enemy.”

[032-054] “You will protect me,” she implored.

[032-055] “Where I am let him not come,” said Fionn. “I also have knowledge. I am

[032-056] Fionn, the son of Uail, the son of Baiscne, a man among men and a god

[032-057] where the gods are.”

[032-058] “He asked me in marriage,” she continued, “but my mind was full of my

[032-059] own dear hero, and I refused the Dark Man.”

[032-060] “That was your right, and I swear by my hand that if the man you desire

[032-061] is alive and unmarried he shall marry you or he will answer to me for

[032-062] the refusal.”

[032-063] “He is not married,” said Saeve, “and you have small control over him.”

[032-064] The Chief frowned thoughtfully. “Except the High King and the kings I

[032-065] have authority in this land.”

[032-066] “What man has authority over himself?” said Saeve.

[032-067] “Do you mean that I am the man you seek?” said Fionn.

[032-068] “It is to yourself I gave my love,” she replied. “This is good news,”

[032-069] Fionn cried joyfully, “for the moment you came through the door I loved

[032-070] and desired you, and the thought that you wished for another man went

[032-071] into my heart like a sword.” Indeed, Fionn loved Saeve as he had not

[032-072] loved a woman before and would never love one again. He loved her as he

[032-073] had never loved anything before. He could not bear to be away from her.

[032-074] When he saw her he did not see the world, and when he saw the world

[032-075] without her it was as though he saw nothing, or as if he looked on a

[032-076] prospect that was bleak and depressing. The belling of a stag had been

[032-077] music to Fionn, but when Saeve spoke that was sound enough for him. He

[032-078] had loved to hear the cuckoo calling in the spring from the tree that

[032-079] is highest in the hedge, or the blackbird’s jolly whistle in an autumn

[032-080] bush, or the thin, sweet enchantment that comes to the mind when a lark

[032-081] thrills out of sight in the air and the hushed fields listen to the

[032-082] song. But his wife’s voice was sweeter to Fionn than the singing of a

[032-083] lark. She filled him with wonder and surmise. There was [[enchantment|magic]] in the

[032-084] tips of her fingers. Her thin palm ravished him. Her slender foot set

[032-085] his heart beating; and whatever way her head moved there came a new

[032-086] shape of beauty to her face.

[032-087] “She is always new,” said Fionn. “She is always better than any other

[032-088] woman; she is always better than herself.”

[032-089] He attended no more to the [[fianna|Fianna]]. He ceased to hunt. He did not listen

[032-090] to the songs of poets or the curious sayings of magicians, for all of

[032-091] these were in his wife, and something that was beyond these was in her

[032-092] also.

[032-093] “She is this world and the next one; she is completion,” said Fionn.
