[002-001] Now [[finnian|Finnian]] could not abide that any person should resist both the

[002-002] Gospel and himself, and he proceeded to force the [[dun|stronghold]] by peaceful

[002-003] but powerful methods. He fasted on the gentleman, and he did so to such

[002-004] purpose that he was admitted to the house; for to an hospitable heart

[002-005] the idea that a stranger may expire on your doorstep from sheer famine

[002-006] cannot be tolerated. The gentleman, however, did not give in without a

[002-007] struggle: he thought that when Finnian had grown sufficiently hungry he

[002-008] would lift the siege and take himself off to some place where he might

[002-009] get food. But he did not know Finnian. The great abbot sat down on a

[002-010] spot just beyond the door, and composed himself to all that might follow

[002-011] from his action. He bent his gaze on the ground between his feet,

[002-012] and entered into a meditation from which he would Only be released by

[002-013] admission or death.

[002-014] The first day passed quietly.

[002-015] Often the gentleman would send a servitor to spy if that deserter of the

[002-016] gods was still before his door, and each time the servant replied that

[002-017] he was still there.

[002-018] “He will be gone in the morning,” said the hopeful master.

[002-019] On the morrow the state of siege continued, and through that day the

[002-020] servants were sent many times to observe through spy-holes.

[002-021] “Go,” he would say, “and find out if the worshipper of new gods has

[002-022] taken himself away.”

[002-023] But the servants returned each time with the same information.

[002-024] “The new druid is still there,” they said.

[002-025] All through that day no one could leave the stronghold. And the enforced

[002-026] seclusion wrought on the minds of the servants, while the cessation

[002-027] of all work banded them together in small groups that whispered and

[002-028] discussed and disputed. Then these groups would disperse to peep through

[002-029] the spy-hole at the patient, immobile figure seated before the door,

[002-030] wrapped in a meditation that was timeless and unconcerned. They

[002-031] took fright at the spectacle, and once or twice a woman screamed

[002-032] hysterically, and was bundled away with a companion’s hand clapped on

[002-033] her mouth, so that the ear of their master should not be affronted.

[002-034] “He has his own troubles,” they said. “It is a combat of the gods that

[002-035] is taking place.”

[002-036] So much for the women; but the men also were uneasy. They prowled up and

[002-037] down, tramping from the spy-hole to the kitchen, and from the kitchen

[002-038] to the turreted roof. And from the roof they would look down on the

[002-039] motionless figure below, and speculate on many things, including

[002-040] the staunchness of man, the qualities of their master, and even the

[002-041] possibility that the new gods might be as powerful as the old.

[002-042] From these peepings and discussions they would return languid and

[002-043] discouraged.

[002-044] “If,” said one irritable guard, “if we buzzed a spear at the persistent

[002-045] stranger, or if one slung at him with a jagged pebble!”

[002-046] “What!” his master demanded wrathfully, “is a spear to be thrown at

[002-047] an unarmed stranger? And from this house!” And he soundly cuffed that

[002-048] indelicate servant.

[002-049] “Be at peace all of you,” he said, “for hunger has a whip, and he will

[002-050] drive the stranger away in the night.”

[002-051] The household retired to wretched beds; but for the master of the house

[002-052] there was no sleep. He marched his halls all night, going often to

[002-053] the spy-hole to see if that shadow was still sitting in the shade, and

[002-054] pacing thence, tormented, preoccupied, refusing even the nose of his

[002-055] favourite dog as it pressed lovingly into his closed palm.

[002-056] On the morrow he gave in.

[002-057] The great door was swung wide, and two of his servants carried Finnian

[002-058] into the house, for the saint could no longer walk or stand upright by

[002-059] reason of the hunger and exposure to which he had submitted. But his

[002-060] frame was tough as the unconquerable spirit that dwelt within it, and

[002-061] in no long time he was ready for whatever might come of dispute or

[002-062] anathema.

[002-063] Being quite re-established he undertook the conversion of the master of

[002-064] the house, and the siege he laid against that notable intelligence was

[002-065] long spoken of among those who are interested in such things.

[002-066] He had beaten the disease of Mugain; he had beaten his own pupil the

[002-067] great Colm Cille; he beat Tuan also, and just as the latter’s door had

[002-068] opened to the persistent stranger, so his heart opened, and Finnian

[002-069] marched there to do the will of God, and his own will.
