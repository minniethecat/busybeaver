[026-001] There are people who do not like dogs a bit--they are usually women--but

[026-002] in this story there is a man who did not like dogs. In fact, he hated

[026-003] them. When he saw one he used to go black in the face, and he threw

[026-004] rocks at it until it got out of sight. But the Power that protects all

[026-005] creatures had put a squint into this man’s eye, so that he always threw

[026-006] crooked.

[026-007] This gentleman’s name was [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus Fionnliath]], and his [[dun|stronghold]] was near

[026-008] the harbour of Galway. Whenever a dog barked he would leap out of his

[026-009] seat, and he would throw everything that he owned out of the window in

[026-010] the direction of the bark. He gave prizes to servants who disliked dogs,

[026-011] and when he heard that a man had drowned a litter of pups he used to

[026-012] visit that person and try to marry his daughter.

[026-013] Now Fionn, the son of Uail, was the reverse of Fergus Fionnliath in this

[026-014] matter, for he delighted in dogs, and he knew everything about them from

[026-015] the setting of the first little white tooth to the rocking of the last

[026-016] long yellow one. He knew the affections and antipathies which are proper

[026-017] in a dog; the degree of obedience to which dogs may be trained without

[026-018] losing their honourable qualities or becoming servile and suspicious;

[026-019] he knew the hopes that animate them, the apprehensions which tingle in

[026-020] their blood, and all that is to be demanded from, or forgiven in, a

[026-021] paw, an ear, a nose, an eye, or a tooth; and he understood these things

[026-022] because he loved dogs, for it is by love alone that we understand

[026-023] anything.

[026-024] Among the three hundred dogs which Fionn owned there were two to whom

[026-025] he gave an especial tenderness, and who were his daily and nightly

[026-026] companions. These two were [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan, but if a person were to

[026-027] guess for twenty years he would not find out why Fionn loved these two

[026-028] dogs and why he would never be separated from them.

[026-029] Fionn’s mother, [[muirne|Muirne]], went to [[allen|wide Allen of Leinster]] to visit her son,

[026-030] and she brought her young sister [[tuiren|Tuiren]] with her. The mother and aunt

[026-031] of the great captain were well treated among the [[fianna|Fianna]], first, because

[026-032] they were parents to Fionn, and second, because they were beautiful and

[026-033] noble women.

[026-034] No words can describe how delightful Muirne was--she took the branch;

[026-035] and as to Tuiren, a man could not look at her without becoming angry

[026-036] or dejected. Her face was fresh as a spring morning; her voice more

[026-037] cheerful than the cuckoo calling from the branch that is highest in the

[026-038] hedge; and her form swayed like a reed and flowed like a river, so that

[026-039] each person thought she would surely flow to him.

[026-040] Men who had wives of their own grew moody and downcast because they

[026-041] could not hope to marry her, while the bachelors of the Fianna stared at

[026-042] each other with truculent, bloodshot eyes, and then they gazed on Tuiren

[026-043] so gently that she may have imagined she was being beamed on by the mild

[026-044] eyes of the dawn.

[026-045] It was to an [[ulster|Ulster]] gentleman, [[iollan-eachtach|Iollan Eachtach]], that she gave her love,

[026-046] and this chief stated his rights and qualities and asked for her in

[026-047] marriage.

[026-048] Now Fionn did not dislike the man of Ulster, but either he did not

[026-049] know them well or else he knew them too well, for he made a curious

[026-050] stipulation before consenting to the marriage. He bound Iollan to return

[026-051] the lady if there should be occasion to think her unhappy, and Iollan

[026-052] agreed to do so. The sureties to this bargain were [[caelte-mac-ronan|Caelte mac Ronan]],

[026-053] [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]], and Lugaidh. Lugaidh himself gave the bride away, but

[026-054] it was not a pleasant ceremony for him, because he also was in love with

[026-055] the lady, and he would have preferred keeping her to giving her away.

[026-056] When she had gone he made a poem about her, beginning:

[026-057] “There is no more light in the sky--”

[026-058] And hundreds of sad people learned the poem by heart.
