[016-001] ONE day his guardians were agitated: they held confabulations at which

[016-002] Fionn was not permitted to assist. A man who passed by in the morning

[016-003] had spoken to them. They fed the man, and during his feeding Fionn had

[016-004] been shooed from the door as if he were a chicken. When the stranger

[016-005] took his road the women went with him a short distance. As they passed

[016-006] the man lifted a hand and bent a knee to Fionn.

[016-007] “My soul to you, young master,” he said, and as he said it, Fionn

[016-008] knew that he could have the man’s soul, or his boots, or his feet, or

[016-009] anything that belonged to him.

[016-010] When the women returned they were mysterious and whispery. They chased

[016-011] Fionn into the house, and when they got him in they chased him out

[016-012] again. They chased each other around the house for another whisper. They

[016-013] calculated things by the shape of clouds, by lengths of shadows, by the

[016-014] flight of birds, by two flies racing on a flat stone, by throwing bones

[016-015] over their left shoulders, and by every kind of trick and game and

[016-016] chance that you could put a mind to.

[016-017] They told Fionn he must sleep in a tree that night, and they put him

[016-018] under bonds not to sing or whistle or cough or sneeze until the morning.

[016-019] Fionn did sneeze. He never sneezed so much in his life. He sat up in his

[016-020] tree and nearly sneezed himself out of it. Flies got up his nose, two

[016-021] at a time, one up each nose, and his head nearly fell off the way he

[016-022] sneezed.

[016-023] “You are doing that on purpose,” said a savage whisper from the foot of

[016-024] the tree.

[016-025] But Fionn was not doing it on purpose. He tucked himself into a fork the

[016-026] way he had been taught, and he passed the crawliest, tickliest night he

[016-027] had ever known. After a while he did not want to sneeze, he wanted to

[016-028] scream: and in particular he wanted to come down from the tree. But he

[016-029] did not scream, nor did he leave the tree. His word was passed, and he

[016-030] stayed in his tree as silent as a mouse and as watchful, until he fell

[016-031] out of it.

[016-032] In the morning a band of travelling poets were passing, and the

[016-033] women handed Fionn over to them. This time they could not prevent him

[016-034] overhearing.

[016-035] “The [[clann-morna|sons of Morna]]!” they said.

[016-036] And Fionn’s heart might have swelled with rage, but that it was already

[016-037] swollen with adventure. And also the expected was happening. Behind

[016-038] every hour of their day and every moment of their lives lay the sons of

[016-039] Morna. Fionn had run after them as deer: he jumped after them as hares:

[016-040] he dived after them as fish. They lived in the house with him: they

[016-041] sat at the table and ate his meat. One dreamed of them, and they were

[016-042] expected in the morning as the sun is. They knew only too well that the

[016-043] son of Uail was living, and they knew that their own sons would know

[016-044] no ease while that son lived; for they believed in those days that like

[016-045] breeds like, and that the son of Uail would be Uail with additions.

[016-046] His guardians knew that their hiding-place must at last be discovered,

[016-047] and that, when it was found, the sons of Morna would come. They had

[016-048] no doubt of that, and every action of their lives was based on that

[016-049] certainty. For no secret can remain secret. Some broken soldier tramping

[016-050] home to his people will find it out; a herd seeking his strayed cattle

[016-051] or a band of travelling musicians will get the wind of it. How many

[016-052] people will move through even the remotest wood in a year! The crows

[016-053] will tell a secret if no one else does; and under a bush, behind a clump

[016-054] of bracken, what eyes may there not be! But if your secret is legged

[016-055] like a young goat! If it is tongued like a wolf! One can hide a baby,

[016-056] but you cannot hide a boy. He will rove unless you tie him to a post,

[016-057] and he will whistle then.

[016-058] The sons of Morna came, but there were only two grim women living in a

[016-059] lonely hut to greet them. We may be sure they were well greeted. One can

[016-060] imagine Goll’s merry stare taking in all that could be seen; Cona’n’s

[016-061] grim eye raking the women’s faces while his tongue raked them again; the

[016-062] Rough mac Morna shouldering here and there in the house and about it,

[016-063] with maybe a hatchet in his hand, and Art Og coursing further afield and

[016-064] vowing that if the cub was there he would find him.
