# Fiachna Duv

## Identity

Fiachna Duv mac Demain ("Fiachna the Dark") was the ruler of the Dal Fiatach, a kingdom in the north of Ireland. He was the neighbour, rival, and sworn enemy of [[fiachna-finn|Fiachna Finn mac Baltan]], the King of Ulster and mortal father of [[mongan|Mongan]]. The two Fiachnas — one Fair, one Dark — stood on opposite sides of a bitter feud that shaped the politics of ancient Ulster and provided the backdrop for the birth and betrothal of Duv Laca.

## In the Tales

### The Feud with Fiachna Finn

Fiachna Duv appears in *Mongan's Frenzy* as the principal antagonist to Mongan's father. "For a long time he had been at enmity and spiteful warfare with Fiachna Finn." [^1] The conflict between the two rulers was not merely a border skirmish but a deep and abiding hatred — the kind of enmity that passes from generation to generation and can only be settled by blood or marriage.

### The Cradle Betrothal

In a remarkable twist of fate, on the same night that Fiachna Finn's son Mongan was born, Fiachna Duv also had a child — a daughter named [[duv-laca|Duv Laca of the White Hand]]. "To this Fiachna Duv there was born in the same night a daughter, and this girl was named Duv Laca of the White Hand." [^1] The synchrony of the two births was seen as an omen, and the men of Ireland seized upon it as an opportunity for peace.

"In order to end the trouble between Fiachna Finn and Fiachna Duv the babies were affianced to each other in the cradle on the day after they were born, and the men of Ireland rejoiced at that deed and at that news." [^1] Thus the infant Mongan was betrothed to the infant Duv Laca — a cradle-match that would bind the warring houses and produce one of the great love stories of Irish myth.

### Downfall

The peace proved fragile. When Mongan came of age, he chose to pursue his own desires rather than honour the cradle-betrothal, taking the Flame Lady as his wife and leaving Duv Laca unclaimed. Fiachna Duv, affronted by this rejection of his daughter and the insult to his house, went to war. He burned Fiachna Finn's palace and killed the King of Ulster, seizing the crown for himself.

But his reign was short-lived. Mongan gathered his forces and retaliated with devastating effect: "He collected his forces then, and he burned Fiachna Duv's fortress, and he killed Fiachna Duv, and he was crowned King of Ulster." [^1] The cycle of vengeance came full circle — the feud that had begun with enmity between two kings ended in fire and blood, with the son avenging the father and claiming the throne that was his birthright.

## Significance

Fiachna Duv is more than a simple antagonist. He is the dark mirror of Fiachna Finn — "Duv" meaning dark, "Finn" meaning fair — and their rivalry embodies the archetypal conflict between opposed houses that drives so much of Irish legendary history. His daughter Duv Laca, whose beauty and sorrow become the emotional centre of Mongan's tale, is his most significant legacy: through her, the Dark Fiachna's bloodline enters the great love story that Cairide the storyteller preserves for all time.
