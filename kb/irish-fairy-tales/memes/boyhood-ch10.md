[021-001] Life continued for him in a round of timeless time, wherein days and

[021-002] nights were uneventful and were yet filled with interest. As the day

[021-003] packed its load of strength into his frame, so it added its store of

[021-004] knowledge to his mind, and each night sealed the twain, for it is in the

[021-005] night that we make secure what we have gathered in the day.

[021-006] If he had told of these days he would have told of a succession of meals

[021-007] and sleeps, and of an endless conversation, from which his mind would

[021-008] now and again slip away to a solitude of its own, where, in large hazy

[021-009] atmospheres, it swung and drifted and reposed. Then he would be back

[021-010] again, and it was a pleasure for him to catch up on the thought that was

[021-011] forward and re-create for it all the matter he had missed. But he could

[021-012] not often make these sleepy sallies; his master was too experienced a

[021-013] teacher to allow any such bright-faced, eager-eyed abstractions, and as

[021-014] the druid women had switched his legs around a tree, so [[finegas|Finegas]] chased

[021-015] his mind, demanding sense in his questions and understanding in his

[021-016] replies.

[021-017] To ask questions can become the laziest and wobbliest occupation of a

[021-018] mind, but when you must yourself answer the problem that you have posed,

[021-019] you will meditate your question with care and frame it with precision.

[021-020] Fionn’s mind learned to jump in a bumpier field than that in which he

[021-021] had chased rabbits. And when he had asked his question, and given his

[021-022] own answer to it, Finegas would take the matter up and make clear to him

[021-023] where the query was badly formed or at what point the answer had begun

[021-024] to go astray, so that Fionn came to understand by what successions a

[021-025] good question grows at last to a good answer.

[021-026] One day, not long after the conversation told of, Finegas came to the

[021-027] place where Fionn was. The poet had a shallow osier basket on his arm,

[021-028] and on his face there was a look that was at once triumphant and gloomy.

[021-029] He was excited certainly, but he was sad also, and as he stood gazing on

[021-030] Fionn his eyes were so kind that the boy was touched, and they were yet

[021-031] so melancholy that it almost made Fionn weep. “What is it, my master?”

[021-032] said the alarmed boy.

[021-033] The poet placed his osier basket on the grass.

[021-034] “Look in the basket, dear son,” he said. Fionn looked.

[021-035] “There is a salmon in the basket.”

[021-036] “It is The Salmon,” said Finegas with a great sigh. Fionn leaped for

[021-037] delight.

[021-038] “I am glad for you, master,” he cried. “Indeed I am glad for you.”

[021-039] “And I am glad, my dear soul,” the master rejoined.

[021-040] But, having said it, he bent his brow to his hand and for a long time he

[021-041] was silent and gathered into himself.

[021-042] “What should be done now?” Fionn demanded, as he stared on the beautiful

[021-043] fish.

[021-044] Finegas rose from where he sat by the osier basket.

[021-045] “I will be back in a short time,” he said heavily. “While I am away you

[021-046] may roast the salmon, so that it will be ready against my return.”

[021-047] “I will roast it indeed,” said Fionn.

[021-048] The poet gazed long and earnestly on him.

[021-049] “You will not eat any of my salmon while I am away?” he asked.

[021-050] “I will not eat the littlest piece,” said Fionn.

[021-051] “I am sure you will not,” the other murmured, as he turned and walked

[021-052] slowly across the grass and behind the sheltering bushes on the ridge.

[021-053] Fionn cooked the salmon. It was beautiful and tempting and savoury as

[021-054] it smoked on a wooden platter among cool green leaves; and it looked all

[021-055] these to Finegas when he came from behind the fringing bushes and sat

[021-056] in the grass outside his door. He gazed on the fish with more than his

[021-057] eyes. He looked on it with his heart, with his soul in his eyes, and

[021-058] when he turned to look on Fionn the boy did not know whether the love

[021-059] that was in his eyes was for the fish or for himself. Yet he did know

[021-060] that a great moment had arrived for the poet.

[021-061] “So,” said Finegas, “you did not eat it on me after all?” “Did I not

[021-062] promise?” Fionn replied.

[021-063] “And yet,” his master continued, “I went away so that you might eat the

[021-064] fish if you felt you had to.”

[021-065] “Why should I want another man’s fish?” said proud Fionn.

[021-066] “Because young people have strong desires. I thought you might have

[021-067] tasted it, and then you would have eaten it on me.”

[021-068] “I did taste it by chance,” Fionn laughed, “for while the fish was

[021-069] roasting a great blister rose on its skin. I did not like the look of

[021-070] that blister, and I pressed it down with my thumb. That burned my thumb,

[021-071] so I popped it in my mouth to heal the smart. If your salmon tastes as

[021-072] nice as my thumb did,” he laughed, “it will taste very nice.”

[021-073] “What did you say your name was, dear heart?” the poet asked.

[021-074] “I said my name was [[fionn-mac-uail|Deimne]].”

[021-075] “Your name is not Deimne,” said the mild man, “your name is Fionn.”

[021-076] “That is true,” the boy answered, “but I do not know how you know it.”

[021-077] “Even if I have not eaten [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge I have some small

[021-078] science of my own.”

[021-079] “It is very clever to know things as you know them,” Fionn replied

[021-080] wonderingly. “What more do you know of me, dear master?”

[021-081] “I know that I did not tell you the truth,” said the heavy-hearted man.

[021-082] “What did you tell me instead of it?”

[021-083] “I told you a lie.”

[021-084] “It is not a good thing to do,” Fionn admitted. “What sort of a lie was

[021-085] the lie, master?” “I told you that the Salmon of Knowledge was to be

[021-086] caught by me, according to the prophecy.”

[021-087] “Yes.”

[021-088] “That was true indeed, and I have caught the fish. But I did not tell

[021-089] you that the salmon was not to be eaten by me, although that also was in

[021-090] the prophecy, and that omission was the lie.”

[021-091] “It is not a great lie,” said Fionn soothingly.

[021-092] “It must not become a greater one,” the poet replied sternly.

[021-093] “Who was the fish given to?” his companion wondered.

[021-094] “It was given to you,” Finegas answered. “It was given to Fionn, the son

[021-095] of Uail, the son of Baiscne, and it will be given to him.”

[021-096] “You shall have a half of the fish,” cried Fionn.

[021-097] “I will not eat a piece of its skin that is as small as the point of its

[021-098] smallest bone,” said the resolute and trembling [[bardic-tradition|bard]]. “Let you now eat

[021-099] up the fish, and I shall watch you and give praise to the gods of the

[021-100] Underworld and of the Elements.”

[021-101] Fionn then ate the Salmon of Knowledge, and when it had disappeared a

[021-102] great jollity and tranquillity and exuberance returned to the poet.

[021-103] “Ah,” said he, “I had a great combat with that fish.”

[021-104] “Did it fight for its life?” Fionn inquired.

[021-105] “It did, but that was not the fight I meant.”

[021-106] “You shall eat a Salmon of Knowledge too,” Fionn assured him.

[021-107] “You have eaten one,” cried the blithe poet, “and if you make such a

[021-108] promise it will be because you know.”

[021-109] “I promise it and know it,” said Fionn, “you shall eat a Salmon of

[021-110] Knowledge yet.”
