[011-001] “THE fisherman of [[cairill|Cairill]], the King of [[ulster|Ulster]], took me in his net. Ah,

[011-002] that was a happy man when he saw me! He shouted for joy when he saw the

[011-003] great salmon in his net.

[011-004] “I was still in the water as he hauled delicately. I was still in the

[011-005] water as he pulled me to the bank. My nose touched air and spun from it

[011-006] as from fire, and I dived with all my might against the bottom of the

[011-007] net, holding yet to the water, loving it, mad with terror that I must

[011-008] quit that loveliness. But the net held and I came up.

[011-009] “‘Be quiet, King of the River,’ said the fisherman, ‘give in to Doom,’

[011-010] said he.

[011-011] “I was in air, and it was as though I were in fire. The air pressed on

[011-012] me like a fiery mountain. It beat on my scales and scorched them. It

[011-013] rushed down my throat and scalded me. It weighed on me and squeezed me,

[011-014] so that my eyes felt as though they must burst from my head, my head as

[011-015] though it would leap from my body, and my body as though it would swell

[011-016] and expand and fly in a thousand pieces.

[011-017] “The light blinded me, the heat tormented me, the dry air made me

[011-018] shrivel and gasp; and, as he lay on the grass, the great salmon whirled

[011-019] his desperate nose once more to the river, and leaped, leaped, leaped,

[011-020] even under the mountain of air. He could leap upwards, but not forwards,

[011-021] and yet he leaped, for in each rise he could see the twinkling waves,

[011-022] the rippling and curling waters.

[011-023] “‘Be at ease, O King,’ said the fisherman. ‘Be at rest, my beloved. Let

[011-024] go the stream. Let the oozy marge be forgotten, and the sandy bed where

[011-025] the shades dance all in green and gloom, and the brown flood sings

[011-026] along.’

[011-027] “And as he carried me to the palace he sang a song of the river, and a

[011-028] song of Doom, and a song in praise of the King of the Waters.

[011-029] “When the king’s wife saw me she desired me. I was put over a fire and

[011-030] roasted, and she ate me. And when time passed she gave birth to me, and

[011-031] I was her son and the son of Cairill the king. I remember warmth and

[011-032] darkness and movement and unseen sounds. All that happened I remember,

[011-033] from the time I was on the gridiron until the time I was born. I forget

[011-034] nothing of these things.”

[011-035] “And now,” said [[finnian|Finnian]], “you will be born again, for I shall baptize

[011-036] you into the family of the Living God.” ---- So far the story of Tuan,

[011-037] the son of Cairill.

[011-038] No man knows if he died in those distant ages when Finnian was Abbot of

[011-039] [[moville|Moville]], or if he still keeps his fort in Ulster, watching all things,

[011-040] and remembering them for the glory of God and the honour of Ireland.

[011-041] THE BOYHOOD OF FIONN

[011-042] He was a king, a seer and a poet. He was a lord with a manifold and

[011-043] great train. He was our magician, our knowledgable one, our soothsayer.

[011-044] All that he did was sweet with him. And, however ye deem my testimony

[011-045] of Fionn excessive, and, although ye hold my praising overstrained,

[011-046] nevertheless, and by the King that is above me, he was three times

[011-047] better than all I say.--Saint PATRICK.
