# Crimthann mac Ae

**Crimthann**, son of Ae, King of Leinster, was a young prince fostered at the court of the High King at Tara. He was the preferred foster-son of Dermod mac Ae, the Ard-Ri, and accompanied the king on hunting expeditions where important events transpired.

## A Favoured Foster-Son

Among the young princes sent as tokens of loyalty to the High King, Crimthann stood out:

> Among the young princes of his household there was one, Crimthann, son of Ae, King of Leinster, whom the High King preferred to the others over whom he held fatherly sway. Nor was this wonderful, for the lad loved him also, and was as eager and intelligent and modest as becomes a prince.

## Companion of the King

The High King and Crimthann would often set out from Tara to hunt and hawk, sometimes unaccompanied even by a servant. On these excursions, the king imparted to his foster-son his own wide knowledge of forest craft, the conduct of a court, and the care of a people.

Dermod mac Ae delighted in these solitary adventures, and when he could steal a day from affairs, he would send word privily to Crimthann. The boy, having donned his hunting gear, would join the king at a prearranged place.

## The Encounter with Becfola

It was during one of these hunting expeditions with Crimthann that the High King encountered the mysterious woman in a chariot — Becfola — who would become central to the tale. Crimthann's curiosity about the woman driving two horses prompted the king to explain the wonder:

> "When the machinery of anything is explained to us we grow interested," and Crimthann became, by instruction, as astonished as the king was.

> "Does she not drive well?" cried Crimthann admiringly.
> "When you are older," the king counselled him, "you will admire that which is truly admirable, for although the driving is good the lady is better."

## Character

Crimthann is portrayed as an eager student who absorbed instruction "like a sponge and digested it as rapidly." His humility, willingness to learn, and the genuine affection between him and his foster-father paint a picture of the ideal young prince in the Irish tradition.
