## Overview

"Becuma of the White Skin" is the ninth tale in James Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales* (1920), spanning ten chapters. It tells the story of [[becuma|Becuma Cneisgel]], a woman of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] who is banished from the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] for a transgression and descends to the mortal world. She marries [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]], the High King of Ireland, and brings a wasting curse upon the land — a blight that can only be lifted through her exile, secured by the wisdom and courage of Artan mac Cumail, Conn's own son.

The tale weaves together themes of exile, purification, hospitality, and the tension between mortal law and Otherworldly magic. It stands apart from the more action-driven tales of the collection, offering instead a meditation on justice, sacrifice, and the cost of wrongdoing.

## Key Characters

- **[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]** — The protagonist, a sídhe woman banished from the Otherworld
- **[[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]]** — The High King of Ireland who marries Becuma
- **Artan mac Cumail** — Conn's son, whose wisdom and sacrifice resolve the tale
- **[[manannan-mac-lir|Manannán mac Lir]]** — The sea god whose son Becuma transgressed with
- **[[becfola|Becfola]]** — Another sídhe woman, referenced in connection with Otherworld exile

## Themes

The tale explores the concept of **purification through sacrifice** — Becuma's banishment mirrors a larger cosmic pattern of transgression and restitution. The blight she brings to Ireland is not merely physical but spiritual, a wasting of the land's fertility that reflects the moral disorder introduced by her presence. The resolution, secured through Artan's wisdom, restores balance and prosperity to the kingdom.
