# Sídhe Mound

The *sídhe* (pronounced "shee"), or fairy mound, is the Otherworld dwelling of the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] — a supernatural realm existing beneath the visible landscape of Ireland. These mounds are simultaneously physical places (ancient burial mounds and earthworks) and portals to the Irish [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]], a realm of eternal youth, beauty, and danger. The sídhe are inhabited by the "people of Faery," who are themselves "the gods" of the old Irish pantheon [008-017].

> On the night of the Feast of Samhain the doors of the sídhe were open [023-001].

## Nature and Origin

The sídhe came into being when the mortal sons of Míl conquered Ireland and "drove the Tuatha Dé Danann under the ground" [008-021]. The defeated divine race retreated into the hills and mounds, becoming the hidden folk of the sídhe. What had been a conquest became a transformation: the Tuatha Dé Danann did not cease to exist but passed into an invisible dimension layered beneath the mortal world.

The sídhe are not merely geographical features but are supernatural territories with their own kings, laws, and inhabitants. The text refers to "the king of the Shi' of Cesh Corran, Conaran, son of Imidel" [055-001] and speaks of "the Shi's of Ireland" in the plural [055-012], indicating that each sídhe is a distinct realm with its own sovereignty. Beings from the sídhe are described as coming "out of Shi' Finnachy" [023-006] or "out of the Shi' of Aillen mac Midna" [018-015], each named sídhe functioning as both a dwelling and a domain of origin.

A crucial property of the sídhe is their invisibility to mortal eyes: "we cannot see the people of Faery until we enter their realm" [055-002–003]. The inhabitants of the sídhe can observe the mortal world without being seen, as when Conaran watched Fionn on the hunting-mound at Cesh Corran [054-025] without Fionn's knowledge [055-001–002].

## Access and Liminality

The boundary between the mortal world and the sídhe is not absolute. The most famous window of access is the Feast of [[samhain|Samhain]], when "the doors separating this world and the next one are opened, and the inhabitants of either world can leave their respective spheres and appear in the world of the other beings" [023-001–003].

Physical mounds could also contain literal doorways. At Cesh Corran, a "great hole like a doorway" opened in the side of the mound, and within it the daughters of Conaran sat spinning enchantment [056-024–027]. Fionn and Cona'n entered this doorway and passed into Faery without initially realizing they had left the mortal world [056-003–005].

Entry into the sídhe realm could also occur imperceptibly. When Becfola left [[tara|Tara's]] palace on a Sunday morning, "in the one pace she passed out of the palace and out of the world, and the second step she trod was in Faery, but she did not know this" [039-074–076]. The transition was seamless: "to her eye and to the eye of her maid the world was as it always had been, and the landmarks they knew were about them" [039-080], yet she had crossed into an entirely different sphere of existence.

## Music and Enchantment

Music emanating from the sídhe carries a supernatural power. Aillen mac Midna "comes out of the Shi' playing sweet, low music on a timpan and a pipe, and all who hear this music fall asleep" [024-055–056]. The melody is described as "the music of another world! the unearthly, dear melody of the Shi'! So sweet it was that the sense strained to it, and having reached must follow drowsily in its wake, and would merge in it, and could not return again to its own place until that strange harmony was finished" [025-034–038].

This musical enchantment is characteristic of sídhe power: it lulls mortals into a state of helplessness, allowing the sídhe-dwellers to act with impunity. Fionn alone was able to resist it, by pressing his face to the venomous head of the spear Birgha, whose "heat" and "stench" counteracted the soporific magic [024-078–081].

## Key Characteristics

The phenomena described above — the plurality of sídhe realms, their invisibility, liminal access through Samhain, and enchantment through music — reflect consistent properties of the sídhe that emerge across the tales:

1. **Plurality**: There is not one sídhe but many — "the Shi's of Ireland" [055-012], each with its own king, territory, and inhabitants. Known sídhe include Shi' Finnachy in the north [023-006], the Shi' of Cesh Corran [055-001], and the Shi' of Aillen mac Midna [018-015].

2. **Invisibility**: Mortals cannot perceive sídhe-dwellers unless they have entered the sídhe realm or the dwellers choose to reveal themselves [055-002–003].

3. **Liminal access**: The boundary is normally closed but opens at Samhain [023-001–003], and at certain mounds physical entrances may manifest [056-024].

4. **Timelessness**: Those who enter the sídhe may lose track of mortal time; Becfola returned to Tara after an absence of "three days" [040-082] though within Faery her experience seemed brief.

5. **Enchantment and danger**: Sídhe-dwellers wield powerful magic, including the ability to alter perception [056-001], weave binding enchantments [056-027], and produce sleep-inducing music [024-055–056]. Mortals who enter the sídhe without protection are at the mercy of its inhabitants.

6. **Physical correspondence**: Sídhe mounds are real, physical hills and earthworks in the Irish landscape — such as the hunting-mound of Cesh Corran [054-025] and the hills where "the Shi' folk stayed" [039-066] — that simultaneously exist as gateways to the invisible Otherworld.

## In the Tales

### Aillen mac Midna and the Burning of Tara

Aillen mac Midna, a being "out of Shi' Finnachy" and grandson to "the Dagda Mor, the Lord of the Underworld" [023-005–006], attacked [[tara|Tara]] each Samhain for nine years. He emerged from his sídhe playing music that put the defenders to sleep [024-055–056], then "blows a dart of fire out of his mouth, and everything that is touched by that fire is destroyed" [024-060–061]. The spear Birgha, used to defeat him, had itself "come from Faery, out of the Shi' of Aillen mac Midna" [018-015] and was later "taken out of his Shi' by your father" [024-075]. Fionn pursued the fleeing Aillen and "it was but at the entrance to his own Shi' that the pursuer got close enough" to kill him with his own spear [025-070–074].

### The Enchanted Cave of Cesh Corran

This entire tale centers on a sídhe mound. While Fionn sat "on the hunting-mound at the top of Cesh Corran" [054-025], Conaran, the king of that sídhe, used his arts to create an illusory doorway in the hillside [056-024]. His three hideous daughters sat within, weaving enchantment on holly sticks [056-025–027]. Fionn and the [[fianna|Fianna]] who entered were bound and captured [057-008–009]; they were only rescued when [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] arrived and slew two of the daughters in combat, while the third, to save her life, agreed to "lift the enchantment from the Fianna-Finn" [058-055–057]. The tale demonstrates that each sídhe has its own political structure (a king and his subjects) and that sídhe-dwellers possess formidable magical powers but can be defeated through mortal combat.

### Becfola's Passage into Faery

Becfola's journey in *The Wooing of Becfola* provides the most detailed account of crossing into the sídhe realm. King Dermod of Tara was known to police the boundary: "he absolutely prohibited the usage of the earth to these beings on the Sunday" [039-061], and "during Dermod's reign the world said its prayers on Sunday and the Shi' folk stayed in their hills" [039-065]. Every Sunday morning, Dermod would climb "to the most elevated point of Tara, and gaze thence on every side, so that he might see if any fairies or people of the Shi' were disporting themselves in his lordship" [039-058–060].

When Becfola left the palace one Sunday, she stepped unknowingly into Faery [039-074–075]. Later, she was discovered by clerics who accused her before the king: "Either she is a woman of this world to be punished, or she is a woman of the Shi' to be banished, but this holy morning she was in the Shi', and her arms were about the neck of Flann" [042-073–074]. The episode illustrates the ambiguous status of sídhe women in mortal society and the porous boundary between the two worlds.

### Fionn's Heritage in the Sídhe

Fionn mac Uail had blood ties to the sídhe through his mother: "through his mother, he belonged to the tribes of Dana" [023-065]. This dual heritage — mortal on his father's side, divine on his mother's — gave Fionn a unique position between the worlds. He could "look for help to the people of the Shi'" [023-064] because of his lineage, yet he ultimately stood as the champion of the mortal world against the beings who emerged from the sídhe with hostile intent.

## The Sídhe in Irish Tradition

The sídhe concept is one of the most enduring elements of Irish mythology, surviving the Christianization of Ireland far more intact than the worship of named deities. While the Tuatha Dé Danann were gradually euhemerized into historical kings and heroes by medieval scribes, the sídhe themselves — their mounds, their invisibility, their dangerous enchantments, and their liminal access at Samhain — persisted in folk belief well into the modern period as the *aos sí* or "fairy folk." The Stephens tales, written in the early twentieth century, draw on this living folk tradition as much as on the medieval manuscript sources, making the sídhe one of the few mythological concepts in the corpus with an unbroken chain of belief from pre-Christian Ireland to the present day.
