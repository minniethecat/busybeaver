## Overview

The Boyne appears in Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales* in two distinct contexts. It is first the humble riverside dwelling of [[finegas|Finegas]] the poet, where the young Fionn comes seeking wisdom [020-004]. It is also the site of the Brugh, one of Ireland's three great pre-Christian burial-places, ruled by [[angus-og|Angus Og]] [061-013]. The river thus spans the mortal and the divine — a place where a boy learns to ask questions and where a god presides over the dead.

## Location

The Boyne Water flows through [[ulster|Ulster]], emptying into the Irish Sea near modern-day Drogheda. In the tales, its most significant feature is not its course but what stands beside it: the Brugh (modern Newgrange, a passage tomb in the Brú na Bóinne complex). [[finegas|Finegas]]'s dwelling — "a bank of the Boyne Water" [020-004] — places the poet in the shadow of one of Ireland's most ancient sacred landscapes.

## In the Tales

### The Boyhood of Fionn

[[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]], travelling in disguise as [[fionn-mac-uail|Deimne]] to evade the clann-Morna, came "to the place where [[finegas|Finegas]] lived on a bank of the Boyne Water" [020-004]. It was here, at the feet of the poet, that the boy learned the art of asking questions: "We get wise by asking questions, and even if these are not answered we get wise, for a well-packed question carries its answer on its back as a snail carries its shell" [020-005]–[020-007].

The Boyne is also where [[finegas|Finegas]] spoke his prophecy — "A man of knowledge foretold that I should catch [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge in the Boyne Water" [020-032] — the prophecy that would ultimately pass the gift of All Knowledge to Fionn [[rath]]er than to the poet who had waited seven years for it.

### Becuma of the White Skin

After the death of Conn's wife Eithne, Stephens notes the three great burial-places of Ireland: "the Brugh of the Boyne in [[ulster|Ulster]], over which [[angus-og|Angus Og]] is chief and god; the Shi' mound of Cruachan Ahi, where [[ethal-anbual|Ethal Anbual]] presides over the underworld of [[connacht|Connacht]], and Tailltin, in [[meath|Royal Meath]]" [061-013]–[061-016]. Conn chose Tailltin for Eithne's grave, but the enumeration establishes the Boyne's Brugh as the pre-eminent [[ulster|Ulster]] burial site, sacred to [[angus-og|Angus Og]].

## Mythological Significance

The Boyne carries a layered significance. In the Fionn cycle it is the waters of wisdom — [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] that ate the nuts of the hazel trees of knowledge swam here, and it was from the Boyne that [[finegas|Finegas]] drew the fish that would give Fionn his legendary insight. In the broader mythological landscape it is also a royal burial ground, the Brugh (Newgrange), which in Irish tradition is the dwelling of [[angus-og|Angus Og]], the god of love and youth. The river connects the mundane act of a boy learning to ask questions with the cosmic order of death and the gods.
