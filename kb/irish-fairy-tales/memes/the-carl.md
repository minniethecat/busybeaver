# The Carl of the Drab Coat

## Identity

The Carl of the Drab Coat is the mysterious protagonist of the tale that bears his name. He appears first as a hideous, mud-splattered giant in a shapeless brown coat, but this grotesque form conceals his true identity: he is the "ruler of the Shi' of Rath Cruachan" [053-053], a supernatural lord of the sidhe who transforms at the tale's end into a shape "of splendour and delight" [053-051]--[053-052]. Fionn mac Uail encounters him by chance in a gloomy wood while seeking Caelte mac Ronan to race against the foreign champion Cael of the Iron, and the Carl volunteers to run in Caelte's place, single-handedly defeating the invader and securing Ireland's sovereignty.

The Carl is a sidhe-ruler in disguise — a supernatural being who walks the mortal world in the form of a "wild, monstrous, yellow-skinned, big-boned giant" [047-010]. His disguise is deliberately unimpressive: he wears "nothing but an ill-made, mud-plastered, drab-coloured coat, which swaggled and clapped against the calves of his big bare legs" [047-011]--[047-012], and on his feet are "great brogues of boots that were shaped like, but were bigger than, a boat" [047-013]--[047-014]. His true nature is revealed only at the tale's climax, when "the Carl's shape changed into one of splendour and delight" and he declares himself "ruler of the Shi' of Rath Cruachan" [053-051]--[053-053]. Fionn then recognizes him for what he is, making "a feast and a banquet for the jovial god" [053-054]--[053-055].

### The Disguise Motif

The Carl's appearance as a repulsively ugly wanderer before revealing himself as a divine being follows a well-established pattern in Irish mythology: the *loathly servant* or *disguised supernatural helper*. His "ill-made, mud-plastered, drab-coloured coat" [047-011]--[047-012] is an anti-heroic costume that tests the Fianna's hospitality and judgement. Where lesser warriors sneer "What can we hope from that Carl?" [051-024] and call him "a greasy, big-hoofed, ill-assembled resemblance of a beggarman" [048-015]--[048-016], Fionn alone treats him with grudging respect — a test of character that the Carl, as sidhe-ruler, is administering to the mortal defenders of Ireland. The word "carl" itself (from Old Irish *cerdd*, meaning a churl or rustic labourer) underscores the deliberate inversion: Ireland's champion is not a polished nobleman but a muddy giant in a sack-like coat.

## Character and Appetite

The Carl's personality is marked by boisterous joviality and an enormous appetite. He addresses friend and foe alike with ironic endearments: "my pulse" [049-001], "dear heart" [048-010], "my darling" [048-019], and "my treasure" [050-075]. When Cael insults him as a "greasy, big-hoofed, ill-assembled resemblance of a beggarman," the Carl "burst into a roar of laughter, so that the eardrums of the warriors present almost burst inside of their heads" [048-017]--[048-018].

His hunger is as prodigious as his speed. He devours half a wild boar for supper [049-024]--[049-025], consumes the other half for breakfast [050-001]--[050-002], gorges on blackberries until he is "no more than a sack of juice" [050-085], and upon reaching camp demands meal to mix with his squashed blackberries until the pile reaches "up to his shoulders," which he then crams into his mouth with contented sighs and gurgles [053-019]--[053-024].

## In the Tales

### Victory over Cael of the Iron

The Carl's defining act is his defeat of [[cael|Cael of the Iron]], a Thessalian prince who arrives at Ben Edair demanding tribute and lordship over Ireland [046-059]--[046-062]. Fionn, unable to find his champion runner Caelte mac Ronan, encounters the Carl by chance in a forest and reluctantly accepts his offer to race [047-049]--[047-052].

The two agree to run sixty miles from Slieve Luachra in Munster to Ben Edair [048-025]--[048-027]. The Carl displays casual indifference to the contest: after building a house single-handedly by felling and carrying twenty-four couples of timber under one arm [049-010]--[049-014] and hunting a wild boar [049-023]--[049-024], he sleeps an extra hour while Cael starts running with a head start [049-032]--[049-035].

When the Carl finally runs, his speed is supernatural. "He left the swallows behind as if they were asleep. He caught up on a red deer, jumped over it, and left it standing. The wind was always behind him, for he outran it every time" [050-009]--[050-011]. He quickly overtakes Cael, offers him a meaty bone [050-015]--[050-017], shows him how to run properly, then "disappeared from Cael's sight in one wild spatter of big boots" [050-029]--[050-030].

The Carl's race is interrupted by two stops for blackberries [050-036]--[050-037], [050-083]--[050-085], and a thirty-mile backtrack to retrieve the lost tails of his coat [050-054]--[050-065]. Despite these detours, he reaches Ben Edair first, carrying a coat stuffed full of blackberries [050-088], [052-053]--[052-057]. The Fianna, who had despaired of his success, greet him with adoration and tears [053-001]--[053-003].

When Cael arrives in a fury with drawn sword, the Carl casually throws "a pawful of his oozy slop" of meal and blackberries at him with such force that "the man's head spun off his shoulders and hopped along the ground" [053-032]--[053-034]. He then picks up the head and throws it back onto Cael's body — backwards [053-035]--[053-038]. After binding Cael, he forces the Thessalian to swear yearly tribute to Fionn [053-043]--[053-045] and kicks his ship seven leagues out to sea with one boot [053-046]--[053-048].

## Significance

The Carl of the Drab Coat belongs to the tradition of the "loathly servant" or disguised supernatural helper in Irish mythology — a being of great power who appears in a humble or repulsive form and whose true identity is revealed only after they have proven their worth. His tale is one of mistaken appearances: the Fianna mock and despair of him [048-001]--[048-006], [051-026]--[051-034], only to discover that their champion is a "jovial god" and sidhe-ruler [053-051]--[053-055]. In defending Ireland's honour through a footrace rather than in battle, the Carl also embodies the Fenian motif of the contest of champions resolved by extraordinary physical prowess — though his own prowess is so far beyond mortal limits that it can only be explained by his supernatural origin.
