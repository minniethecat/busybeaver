[083-001] It was a long, uneasy journey, for although mac an Da’v was of stout

[083-002] heart and goodwill, yet no man can carry another on his back from [[ulster|Ulster]]

[083-003] to [[leinster|Leinster]] and go quick. Still, if you keep on driving a pig or a story

[083-004] they will get at last to where you wish them to go, and the man who

[083-005] continues putting one foot in front of the other will leave his home

[083-006] behind, and will come at last to the edge of the sea and the end of the

[083-007] world.

[083-008] When they reached Leinster the feast of Moy Life’ was being held, and

[083-009] they pushed on by forced marches and long stages so as to be in time,

[083-010] and thus they came to the Moy of Cell Camain, and they mixed with the

[083-011] crowd that were going to the feast.

[083-012] A great and joyous concourse of people streamed about them. There were

[083-013] young men and young girls, and when these were not holding each other’s

[083-014] hands it was because their arms were round each other’s necks. There

[083-015] were old, lusty women going by, and when these were not talking together

[083-016] it was because their mouths were mutually filled with apples and

[083-017] meat-pies. There were young warriors with mantles of green and purple

[083-018] and red flying behind them on the breeze, and when these were not

[083-019] looking disdainfully on older soldiers it was because the older soldiers

[083-020] happened at the moment to be looking at them. There were old warriors

[083-021] with yard-long beards flying behind their shoulders llke wisps of hay,

[083-022] and when these were not nursing a broken arm or a cracked skull, it was

[083-023] because they were nursing wounds in their stomachs or their legs. There

[083-024] were troops of young women who giggled as long as their breaths lasted

[083-025] and beamed when it gave out. Bands of boys who whispered mysteriously

[083-026] together and pointed with their fingers in every direction at once, and

[083-027] would suddenly begin to run like a herd of stampeded horses. There were

[083-028] men with carts full of roasted meats. Women with little vats full of

[083-029] mead, and others carrying milk and beer. Folk of both sorts with towers

[083-030] swaying on their heads, and they dripping with honey. Children having

[083-031] baskets piled with red apples, and old women who peddled shell-fish and

[083-032] boiled lobsters. There were people who sold twenty kinds of bread, with

[083-033] butter thrown in. Sellers of onions and cheese, and others who supplied

[083-034] spare bits of armour, odd scabbards, spear handles, breastplate-laces.

[083-035] People who cut your hair or told your fortune or gave you a hot bath in

[083-036] a pot. Others who put a shoe on your horse or a piece of embroidery on

[083-037] your mantle; and others, again, who took stains off your sword or dyed

[083-038] your finger-nails or sold you a hound.

[083-039] It was a great and joyous gathering that was going to the feast.

[083-040] [[mongan|Mongan]] and his servant sat against a grassy hedge by the roadside and

[083-041] watched the multitude streaming past.

[083-042] Just then Mongan glanced to the right whence the people were coming.

[083-043] Then he pulled the hood of his cloak over his ears and over his brow.

[083-044] “Alas!” said he in a deep and anguished voice.

[083-045] Mac an Da’v turned to him.

[083-046] “Is it a pain in your stomach, master?”

[083-047] “It is not,” said Mongan. “Well, what made you make that brutal and

[083-048] belching noise?”

[083-049] “It was a sigh I gave,” said Mongan.

[083-050] “Whatever it was,” said mac an Da’v, “what was it?”

[083-051] “Look down the road on this side and tell me who is coming,” said his

[083-052] master.

[083-053] “It is a lord with his troop.”

[083-054] “It is the King of Leinster,” said Mongan. “The man,” said mac an Da’v

[083-055] in a tone of great pity, “the man that took away your wife! And,” he

[083-056] roared in a voice of extraordinary savagery, “the man that took away my

[083-057] wife into the bargain, and she not in the bargain.”

[083-058] “Hush,” said Mongan, for a man who heard his shout stopped to tie a

[083-059] sandie, or to listen.

[083-060] “Master,” said mac an Da’v as the troop drew abreast and moved past.

[083-061] “What is it, my good friend?”

[083-062] “Let me throw a little small piece of a rock at the King of Leinster.”

[083-063] “I will not.”

[083-064] “A little bit only, a small bit about twice the size of my head.”

[083-065] “I will not let you,” said Mongan.

[083-066] When the king had gone by mac an Da’v groaned a deep and dejected groan.

[083-067] “Oco’n!” said he. “Oco’n-i’o-go-deo’!” said he.

[083-068] The man who had tied his sandal said then: “Are you in pain, honest

[083-069] man?”

[083-070] “I am not in pain,” said mac an Da’v.

[083-071] “Well, what was it that knocked a howl out of you like the yelp of a

[083-072] sick dog, honest man?”

[083-073] “Go away,” said mac an Da’v, “go away, you flat-faced, nosey person.”

[083-074] “There is no politeness left in this country,” said the stranger, and he

[083-075] went away to a certain distance, and from thence he threw a stone at mac

[083-076] an Da’v’s nose, and hit it.
