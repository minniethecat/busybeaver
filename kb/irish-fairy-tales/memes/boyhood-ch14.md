[025-001] He listened to the retreating footsteps until they could be heard no

[025-002] more, and the one sound that came to his tense ears was the beating of

[025-003] his own heart.

[025-004] Even the wind had ceased, and there seemed to be nothing in the world

[025-005] but the darkness and himself. In that gigantic blackness, in that unseen

[025-006] quietude and vacancy, the mind could cease to be personal to itself. It

[025-007] could be overwhelmed and merged in space, so that consciousness would

[025-008] be transferred or dissipated, and one might sleep standing; for the mind

[025-009] fears loneliness more than all else, and will escape to the moon rather

[025-010] than be driven inwards on its own being.

[025-011] But Fionn was not lonely, and he was not afraid when the son of Midna

[025-012] came.

[025-013] A long stretch of the silent night had gone by, minute following minute

[025-014] in a slow sequence, wherein as there was no change there was no time;

[025-015] wherein there was no past and no future, but a stupefying, endless

[025-016] present which is almost the annihilation of consciousness. A change

[025-017] came then, for the clouds had also been moving and the moon at last was

[025-018] sensed behind them--not as a radiance, but as a percolation of light,

[025-019] a gleam that was strained through matter after matter and was less than

[025-020] the very wraith or remembrance of itself; a thing seen so narrowly,

[025-021] so sparsely, that the eye could doubt if it was or was not seeing, and

[025-022] might conceive that its own memory was re-creating that which was still

[025-023] absent.

[025-024] But Fionn’s eye was the eye of a wild creature that spies on darkness

[025-025] and moves there wittingly. He saw, then, not a thing but a movement;

[025-026] something that was darker than the darkness it loomed on; not a being

[025-027] but a presence, and, as it were, impending pressure. And in a little he

[025-028] heard the deliberate pace of that great being.

[025-029] Fionn bent to his spear and unloosed its coverings.

[025-030] Then from the darkness there came another sound; a low, sweet sound;

[025-031] thrillingly joyous, thrillingly low; so low the ear could scarcely note

[025-032] it, so sweet the ear wished to catch nothing else and would strive to

[025-033] hear it rather than all sounds that may be heard by man: the music of

[025-034] another world! the unearthly, dear melody of the Shi’! So sweet it was

[025-035] that the sense strained to it, and having reached must follow drowsily

[025-036] in its wake, and would merge in it, and could not return again to its

[025-037] own place until that strange harmony was finished and the ear restored

[025-038] to freedom.

[025-039] But Fionn had taken the covering from his spear, and with his brow

[025-040] pressed close to it he kept his mind and all his senses engaged on that

[025-041] sizzling, murderous point.

[025-042] The music ceased and Aillen hissed a fierce blue flame from his mouth,

[025-043] and it was as though he hissed lightning.

[025-044] Here it would seem that Fionn used [[enchantment|magic]], for spreading out his fringed

[025-045] mantle he caught the flame. Rather he stopped it, for it slid from the

[025-046] mantle and sped down into the earth to the depth of twenty-six spans;

[025-047] from which that slope is still called the Glen of the Mantle, and the

[025-048] rise on which Aillen stood is known as the Ard of Fire.

[025-049] One can imagine the surprise of [[aillen-mac-midna|Aillen mac Midna]], seeing his fire caught

[025-050] and quenched by an invisible hand. And one can imagine that at this

[025-051] check he might be frightened, for who would be more terrified than a

[025-052] magician who sees his magic fail, and who, knowing of power, will guess

[025-053] at powers of which he has no conception and may well dread.

[025-054] Everything had been done by him as it should be done. His pipe had been

[025-055] played and his timpan, all who heard that music should be asleep, and

[025-056] yet his fire was caught in full course and was quenched.

[025-057] Aillen, with all the terrific strength of which he was master, blew

[025-058] again, and the great jet of blue flame came roaring and whistling from

[025-059] him and was caught and disappeared.

[025-060] Panic swirled into the man from [[faery|Faery]]; he turned from that terrible spot

[025-061] and fled, not knowing what might be behind, but dreading it as he

[025-062] had never before dreaded anything, and the unknown pursued him; that

[025-063] terrible defence became offence and hung to his heel as a wolf pads by

[025-064] the flank of a bull.

[025-065] And Aillen was not in his own world! He was in the world of men, where

[025-066] movement is not easy and the very air a burden. In his own sphere, in

[025-067] his own element, he might have outrun Fionn, but this was Fionn’s world,

[025-068] Fionn’s element, and the flying god was not gross enough to outstrip

[025-069] him. Yet what a race he gave, for it was but at the entrance to his

[025-070] own Shi’ that the pursuer got close enough. Fionn put a finger into

[025-071] the thong of the great spear, and at that cast night fell on Aillen

[025-072] mac Midna. His eyes went black, his mind whirled and ceased, there

[025-073] came nothingness where he had been, and as the Birgha whistled into his

[025-074] shoulder-blades he withered away, he tumbled emptily and was dead. Fionn

[025-075] took his lovely head from its shoulders and went back through the night

[025-076] to [[tara|Tara]].

[025-077] Triumphant Fionn, who had dealt death to a god, and to whom death would

[025-078] be dealt, and who is now dead!

[025-079] He reached the palace at sunrise.

[025-080] On that morning all were astir early. They wished to see what

[025-081] destruction had been wrought by the great being, but it was young Fionn

[025-082] they saw and that redoubtable head swinging by its hair. “What is your

[025-083] demand?” said the Ard-Ri’. “The thing that it is right I should ask,”

[025-084] said Fionn: “the command of the [[fianna|Fianna of Ireland]].”

[025-085] “Make your choice,” said Conn to Goll Mor; “you will leave Ireland, or

[025-086] you will place your hand in the hand of this champion and be his man.”

[025-087] Goll could do a thing that would be hard for another person, and he

[025-088] could do it so beautifully that he was not diminished by any action.

[025-089] “Here is my hand,” said Goll.

[025-090] And he twinkled at the stern, young eyes that gazed on him as he made

[025-091] his submission.
