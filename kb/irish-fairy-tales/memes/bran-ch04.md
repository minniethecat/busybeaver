[029-001] On the following day Fergus called his servant.

[029-002] “Has that dog stopped shivering yet?” he asked.

[029-003] “It has not, sir,” said the servant.

[029-004] “Bring the beast here,” said his master, “for whoever else is

[029-005] dissatisfied Fionn must be satisfied.”

[029-006] The dog was brought, and he examined it with a jaundiced and bitter eye.

[029-007] “It has the shivers indeed,” he said.

[029-008] “The shivers it has,” said the servant.

[029-009] “How do you cure the shivers?” his master demanded, for he thought that

[029-010] if the animal’s legs dropped off Fionn would not be satisfied.

[029-011] “There is a way,” said the servant doubtfully.

[029-012] “If there is a way, tell it to me,” cried his master angrily.

[029-013] “If you were to take the beast up in your arms and hug it and kiss it,

[029-014] the shivers would stop,” said the man.

[029-015] “Do you mean--?” his master thundered, and he stretched his hand for a

[029-016] club.

[029-017] “I heard that,” said the servant humbly.

[029-018] “Take that dog up,” Fergus commanded, “and hug it and kiss it, and if I

[029-019] find a single shiver left in the beast I’ll break your head.”

[029-020] The man bent to the hound, but it snapped a piece out of his hand, and

[029-021] nearly bit his nose off as well.

[029-022] “That dog doesn’t like me,” said the man.

[029-023] “Nor do I,” roared Fergus; “get out of my sight.”

[029-024] The man went away and Fergus was left alone with the hound, but the poor

[029-025] creature was so terrified that it began to tremble ten times worse than

[029-026] before.

[029-027] “Its legs will drop off,” said Fergus. “Fionn will blame me,” he cried

[029-028] in despair.

[029-029] He walked to the hound.

[029-030] “If you snap at my nose, or if you put as much as the start of a tooth

[029-031] into the beginning of a finger!” he growled.

[029-032] He picked up the dog, but it did not snap, it only trembled. He held it

[029-033] gingerly for a few moments.

[029-034] “If it has to be hugged,” he said, “I’ll hug it. I’d do more than that

[029-035] for Fionn.”

[029-036] He tucked and tightened the animal into his breast, and marched moodily

[029-037] up and down the room. The dog’s nose lay along his breast under his

[029-038] chin, and as he gave it dutiful hugs, one hug to every five paces, the

[029-039] dog put out its tongue and licked him timidly under the chin.

[029-040] “Stop,” roared Fergus, “stop that forever,” and he grew very red in

[029-041] the face, and stared truculently down along his nose. A soft brown eye

[029-042] looked up at him and the shy tongue touched again on his chin.

[029-043] “If it has to be kissed,” said Fergus gloomily, “I’ll kiss it; I’d do

[029-044] more than that for Fionn,” he groaned.

[029-045] He bent his head, shut his eyes, and brought the dog’s jaw against his

[029-046] lips. And at that the dog gave little wriggles in his arms, and little

[029-047] barks, and little licks, so that he could scarcely hold her. He put the

[029-048] hound down at last.

[029-049] “There is not a single shiver left in her,” he said.

[029-050] And that was true.

[029-051] Everywhere he walked the dog followed him, giving little prances and

[029-052] little pats against him, and keeping her eyes fixed on his with such

[029-053] eagerness and intelligence that he marvelled.

[029-054] “That dog likes me,” he murmured in amazement.

[029-055] “By my hand,” he cried next day, “I like that dog.”

[029-056] The day after that he was calling her “My One Treasure, My Little

[029-057] Branch.” And within a week he could not bear her to be out of his sight

[029-058] for an instant.

[029-059] He was tormented by the idea that some evil person might throw a stone

[029-060] at the hound, so he assembled his servants and retainers and addressed

[029-061] them.

[029-062] He told them that the hound was the Queen of Creatures, the Pulse of his

[029-063] Heart, and the Apple of his Eye, and he warned them that the person who

[029-064] as much as looked sideways on her, or knocked one shiver out of her,

[029-065] would answer for the deed with pains and indignities. He recited a list

[029-066] of calamities which would befall such a miscreant, and these woes began

[029-067] with flaying and ended with dismemberment, and had inside bits of such

[029-068] complicated and ingenious torment that the blood of the men who heard it

[029-069] ran chill in their veins, and the women of the household fainted where

[029-070] they stood.
