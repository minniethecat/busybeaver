[068-001] Art, as his father had done before him, set out for the Many-Coloured

[068-002] Land, but it was from Inver Colpa he embarked and not from [[ben-edair|Ben Edair]].

[068-003] At a certain time he passed from the rough green ridges of the sea to

[068-004] enchanted waters, and he roamed from island to island asking all people

[068-005] how he might come to Delvcaem, the daughter of Morgan. But he got no

[068-006] news from any one, until he reached an island that was fragrant with

[068-007] wild apples, gay with flowers, and joyous with the song of birds and the

[068-008] deep mellow drumming of the bees. In this island he was met by a lady,

[068-009] Crede’, the Truly Beautiful, and when they had exchanged kisses, he told

[068-010] her who he was and on what errand he was bent.

[068-011] “We have been expecting you,” said Crede’, “but alas, poor soul, it is

[068-012] a hard, and a long, bad way that you must go; for there is sea and land,

[068-013] danger and difficulty between you and the daughter of Morgan.”

[068-014] “Yet I must go there,” he answered.

[068-015] “There is a wild dark ocean to be crossed. There is a dense wood where

[068-016] every thorn on every tree is sharp as a spear-point and is curved and

[068-017] clutching. There is a deep gulf to be gone through,” she said, “a place

[068-018] of silence and terror, full of dumb, venomous monsters. There is an

[068-019] immense oak forest--dark, dense, thorny, a place to be strayed in,

[068-020] a place to be utterly bewildered and lost in. There is a vast dark

[068-021] wilderness, and therein is a dark house, lonely and full of echoes, and

[068-022] in it there are seven gloomy hags, who are warned already of your coming

[068-023] and are waiting to plunge you in a bath of molten lead.”

[068-024] “It is not a choice journey,” said Art, “but I have no choice and must

[068-025] go.”

[068-026] “Should you pass those hags,” she continued, “and no one has yet passed

[068-027] them, you must meet Ailill of the Black Teeth, the son of [[mongan|Mongan]] Tender

[068-028] Blossom, and who could pass that gigantic and terrible fighter?”

[068-029] “It is not easy to find the daughter of Morgan,” said Art in a

[068-030] melancholy voice.

[068-031] “It is not easy,” Crede’ replied eagerly, “and if you will take my

[068-032] advice--”

[068-033] “Advise me,” he broke in, “for in truth there is no man standing in such

[068-034] need of counsel as I do.”

[068-035] “I would advise you,” said Crede’ in a low voice, “to seek no more for

[068-036] the sweet daughter of Morgan, but to stay in this place where all that

[068-037] is lovely is at your service.”

[068-038] “But, but--” cried Art in astonishment.

[068-039] “Am I not as sweet as the daughter of Morgan?” she demanded, and she

[068-040] stood before him queenly and pleadingly, and her eyes took his with

[068-041] imperious tenderness.

[068-042] “By my hand,” he answered, “you are sweeter and lovelier than any being

[068-043] under the sun, but--”

[068-044] “And with me,” she said, “you will forget Ireland.”

[068-045] “I am under bonds,” cried Art, “I have passed my word, and I would

[068-046] not forget Ireland or cut myself from it for all the kingdoms of the

[068-047] [[faery|Many-Coloured Land]].”

[068-048] Crede’ urged no more at that time, but as they were parting she

[068-049] whispered, “There are two girls, sisters of my own, in Morgan’s palace.

[068-050] They will come to you with a cup in either hand; one cup will be filled

[068-051] with wine and one with poison. Drink from the right-hand cup, O my

[068-052] dear.”

[068-053] Art stepped into his coracle, and then, wringing her hands, she made yet

[068-054] an attempt to dissuade him from that drear journey.

[068-055] “Do not leave me,” she urged. “Do not affront these dangers. Around the

[068-056] palace of Morgan there is a palisade of copper spikes, and on the top of

[068-057] each spike the head of a man grins and shrivels. There is one spike only

[068-058] which bears no head, and it is for your head that spike is waiting. Do

[068-059] not go there, my love.”

[068-060] “I must go indeed,” said. Art earnestly.

[068-061] “There is yet a danger,” she called. “Beware of Delvcaem’s mother, Dog

[068-062] Head, daughter of the King of the Dog Heads. Beware of her.”

[068-063] “Indeed,” said Art to himself, “there is so much to beware of that I

[068-064] will beware of nothing. I will go about my business,” he said to the

[068-065] waves, “and I will let those beings and monsters and the people of the

[068-066] Dog Heads go about their business.”
