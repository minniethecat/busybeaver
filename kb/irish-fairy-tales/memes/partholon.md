# Partholon

**Partholon** was the leader of the first legendary invasion of Ireland after the Biblical Flood. His story is preserved in the account of Tuan mac Cairill, the sole survivor of Partholon's race, who recounted the tale to St. Finnian centuries later.

## Arrival in Ireland

According to Tuan mac Cairill, who claimed to be Partholon's nephew, Partholon was the son of Sera, who was the son of one of Noah's sons. He came to Ireland not long after the Flood with twenty-four couples:

> We landed and we heard the rumble of water going gloomily through the darkness of the forest. Following the water we came to a glade where the sun shone and where the earth was warmed, and there Partholon rested with his twenty-four couples, and made a city and a livelihood.

## A Man of Sweet Deeds

Tuan's memory of Partholon, though faded by the vast passage of time, was fond:

> "A greatly bearded, greatly shouldered man he was. A man of sweet deeds and sweet ways."

Under Partholon's leadership, the settlement flourished. There were fish in the rivers and animals in the forests of Ireland. Wild and monstrous creatures roamed the plains — creatures "that one could see through and walk through" — but Partholon's people lived in ease and multiplied.

## The Growth of a Civilization

Partholon's people increased dramatically:

> Partholon's people increased until from twenty-four couples there came five thousand people, who lived in amity and contentment although they had no wits.

In Tuan's telling, the people "had no wits" — a condition that set them apart from the later races who brought intelligence and craft to Ireland.

## The Plague and Legacy

Partholon's civilization came to a sudden and catastrophic end:

> Then, sudden as a rising wind, between one night and a morning, there came a sickness that bloated the stomach and purpled the skin, and on the seventh day all of the race of Partholon were dead, save one man only.

That one survivor was Tuan mac Cairill himself, who lived on through successive ages — as a stag, a boar, an eagle, and finally as a man again — to tell the tale to St. Finnian. Through Tuan's enduring memory, Partholon's place as the first settler of post-diluvian Ireland is preserved in the mythological record.
