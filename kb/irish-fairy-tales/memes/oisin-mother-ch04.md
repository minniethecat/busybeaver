[034-001] “When you had been away for a day the guards were surprised. They were

[034-002] looking from the heights of the Dun, and [[saeve|the Flower of Allen]] was with

[034-003] them. She, for she had a quest’s eye, called out that the master of the

[034-004] [[fianna|Fianna]] was coming over the ridges to the Dun, and she ran from the keep

[034-005] to meet you.”

[034-006] “It was not I,” said Fionn.

[034-007] “It bore your shape,” replied Gariv Cronan. “It had your armour and your

[034-008] face, and the dogs, [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan, were with it.”

[034-009] “They were with me,” said Fionn.

[034-010] “They seemed to be with it,” said the servant humbly

[034-011] “Tell us this tale,” cried Fionn.

[034-012] “We were distrustful,” the servant continued. “We had never known Fionn

[034-013] to return from a combat before it had been fought, and we knew you could

[034-014] not have reached Ben Edar or encountered the Lochlannachs. So we urged

[034-015] our lady to let us go out to meet you, but to remain herself in the

[034-016] Dun.”

[034-017] “It was good urging,” Fionn assented.

[034-018] “She would not be advised,” the servant wailed. “She cried to us, ‘Let

[034-019] me go to meet my love’.”

[034-020] “Alas!” said Fionn.

[034-021] “She cried on us, ‘Let me go to meet my husband, the father of the child

[034-022] that is not born.’”

[034-023] “Alas!” groaned deep-wounded Fionn. “She ran towards your appearance

[034-024] that had your arms stretched out to her.”

[034-025] At that wise Fionn put his hand before his eyes, seeing all that

[034-026] happened.

[034-027] “Tell on your tale,” said he.

[034-028] “She ran to those arms, and when she reached them the figure lifted

[034-029] its hand. It touched her with a hazel rod, and, while we looked, she

[034-030] disappeared, and where she had been there was a fawn standing and

[034-031] shivering. The fawn turned and bounded towards the gate of the Dun, but

[034-032] the hounds that were by flew after her.”

[034-033] Fionn stared on him like a lost man.

[034-034] “They took her by the throat--” the shivering servant whispered.

[034-035] “Ah!” cried Fionn in a terrible voice.

[034-036] “And they dragged her back to the figure that seemed to be Fionn. Three

[034-037] times she broke away and came bounding to us, and three times the dogs

[034-038] took her by the throat and dragged her back.”

[034-039] “You stood to look!” the Chief snarled.

[034-040] “No, master, we ran, but she vanished as we got to her; the great hounds

[034-041] vanished away, and that being that seemed to be Fionn disappeared with

[034-042] them. We were left in the rough grass, staring about us and at each

[034-043] other, and listening to the moan of the wind and the terror of our

[034-044] hearts.”

[034-045] “Forgive us, dear master,” the servant cried. But the great captain made

[034-046] him no answer. He stood as though he were dumb and blind, and now and

[034-047] again he beat terribly on his breast with his closed fist, as though he

[034-048] would kill that within him which should be dead and could not die. He

[034-049] went so, beating on his breast, to his inner room in the Dun, and he was

[034-050] not seen again for the rest of that day, nor until the sun rose over Moy

[034-051] Life’ in the morning.
