[053-001] [[the-carl|The Carl of the Drab Coat]] came bumping and stumping and clumping into

[053-002] the camp, and was surrounded by a multitude that adored him and hailed

[053-003] him with tears.

[053-004] “Meal!” he bawled, “meal for the love of the stars!”

[053-005] And he bawled, “Meal, meal!” until he bawled everybody into silence.

[053-006] Fionn addressed him.

[053-007] “What for the meal, dear heart?”

[053-008] “For the inside of my mouth,” said the Carl, “for the recesses and

[053-009] crannies and deep-down profundities of my stomach. Meal, meal!” he

[053-010] lamented.

[053-011] Meal was brought.

[053-012] The Carl put his coat on the ground, opened it carefully, and revealed

[053-013] a store of blackberries, squashed, crushed, mangled, democratic,

[053-014] ill-looking.

[053-015] “The meal!” he groaned, “the meal!”

[053-016] It was given to him.

[053-017] “What of the race, my pulse?” said Fionn.

[053-018] “Wait, wait,” cried the Carl. “I die, I die for meal and blackberries.”

[053-019] Into the centre of the mess of blackberries he discharged a barrel of

[053-020] meal, and be mixed the two up and through, and round and down, until

[053-021] the pile of white-black, red-brown slibber-slobber reached up to his

[053-022] shoulders. Then he commenced to paw and impel and project and cram the

[053-023] mixture into his mouth, and between each mouthful he sighed a contented

[053-024] sigh, and during every mouthful he gurgled an oozy gurgle.

[053-025] But while Fionn and the [[fianna|Fianna]] stared like lost minds upon the Carl,

[053-026] there came a sound of buzzing, as if a hornet or a queen of the wasps or

[053-027] a savage, steep-winged griffin was hovering about them, and looking away

[053-028] they saw [[cael|Cael of the Iron]] charging on them with a monstrous extension

[053-029] and scurry of his legs. He had a sword in his hand, and there was

[053-030] nothing in his face but redness and ferocity.

[053-031] Fear fell llke night around the Fianna, and they stood with slack knees

[053-032] and hanging hands waiting for death. But the Carl lifted a pawful of his

[053-033] oozy slop and discharged this at Cael with such a smash that the man’s

[053-034] head spun off his shoulders and hopped along the ground. The Carl then

[053-035] picked up the head and threw it at the body with such aim and force

[053-036] that the neck part of the head jammed into the neck part of the body and

[053-037] stuck there, as good a head as ever, you would have said, but that it

[053-038] bad got twisted the wrong way round. The Carl then lashed his opponent

[053-039] hand and foot.

[053-040] “Now, dear heart, do you still claim tribute and lordship of Ireland?”

[053-041] said he.

[053-042] “Let me go home,” groaned Cael, “I want to go home.”

[053-043] “Swear by the sun and moon, if I let you go home, that you will send to

[053-044] Fionn, yearly and every year, the rent of the land of Thessaly.”

[053-045] “I swear that,” said Cael, “and I would swear anything to get home.”

[053-046] The Carl lifted him then and put him sitting into his ship. Then he

[053-047] raised his big boot and gave the boat a kick that drove it seven leagues

[053-048] out into the sea, and that was how the adventure of Cael of the Iron

[053-049] finished.

[053-050] “Who are you, sir?” said Fionn to the Carl.

[053-051] But before answering the Carl’s shape changed into one of splendour and

[053-052] delight.

[053-053] “I am ruler of the Shi’ of Rath Cruachan,” he said.

[053-054] Then [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] made a feast and a banquet for the jovial god, and

[053-055] with that the tale is ended of the King of Thessaly’s son and the Carl

[053-056] of the Drab Coat.
