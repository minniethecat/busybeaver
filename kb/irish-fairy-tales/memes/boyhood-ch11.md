[022-001] He had received all that he could get from [[finegas|Finegas]]. His education was

[022-002] finished and the time had come to test it, and to try all else that he

[022-003] had of mind and body. He bade farewell to the gentle poet, and set out

[022-004] for [[tara|Tara of the Kings]].

[022-005] It was [[samhain|Samhain-tide]], and the feast of Tara was being held, at which all

[022-006] that was wise or skilful or well-born in Ireland were gathered together.

[022-007] This is how Tara was when Tara was. There was the High King’s palace

[022-008] with its fortification; without it was another fortification enclosing

[022-009] the four minor palaces, each of which was maintained by one of the four

[022-010] provincial kings; without that again was the great banqueting hall, and

[022-011] around it and enclosing all of the sacred hill in its gigantic bound ran

[022-012] the main outer ramparts of Tara. From it, the centre of Ireland, four

[022-013] great roads went, north, south, east, and west, and along these roads,

[022-014] from the top and the bottom and the two sides of Ireland, there moved

[022-015] for weeks before Samhain an endless stream of passengers.

[022-016] Here a gay band went carrying rich treasure to decorate the pavilion of

[022-017] a [[munster|Munster]] lord. On another road a vat of seasoned yew, monstrous as a

[022-018] house on wheels and drawn by an hundred laborious oxen, came bumping and

[022-019] joggling the ale that thirsty [[connacht|Connaught]] princes would drink. On a road

[022-020] again the learned men of [[leinster|Leinster]], each with an idea in his head that

[022-021] would discomfit a northern ollav and make a southern one gape and

[022-022] fidget, would be marching solemnly, each by a horse that was piled high

[022-023] on the back and widely at the sides with clean-peeled willow or oaken

[022-024] wands, that were carved from the top to the bottom with the ogham signs;

[022-025] the first lines of poems (for it was an offence against wisdom to commit

[022-026] more than initial lines to writing), the names and dates of kings, the

[022-027] procession of laws of Tara and of the sub-kingdoms, the names of places

[022-028] and their meanings. On the brown stallion ambling peacefully yonder

[022-029] there might go the warring of the gods for two or ten thousand years;

[022-030] this mare with the dainty pace and the vicious eye might be sidling

[022-031] under a load of oaken odes in honour of her owner’s family, with a

[022-032] few bundles of tales of wonder added in case they might be useful; and

[022-033] perhaps the restive piebald was backing the history of Ireland into a

[022-034] ditch.

[022-035] On such a journey all people spoke together, for all were friends, and

[022-036] no person regarded the weapon in another man’s hand other than as an

[022-037] implement to poke a reluctant cow with, or to pacify with loud wallops

[022-038] some hoof-proud colt.

[022-039] Into this teem and profusion of jolly humanity Fionn slipped, and if his

[022-040] mood had been as bellicose as a wounded boar he would yet have found

[022-041] no man to quarrel with, and if his eye had been as sharp as a jealous

[022-042] husband’s he would have found no eye to meet it with calculation or

[022-043] menace or fear; for the Peace of Ireland was in being, and for six weeks

[022-044] man was neighbour to man, and the nation was the guest of the High King.

[022-045] Fionn went in with the notables.

[022-046] His arrival had been timed for the opening day and the great feast of

[022-047] welcome. He may have marvelled, looking on the bright city, with its

[022-048] pillars of gleaming bronze and the roofs that were painted in many

[022-049] colours, so that each house seemed to be covered by the spreading wings

[022-050] of some gigantic and gorgeous bird. And the palaces themselves, mellow

[022-051] with red oak, polished within and without by the wear and the care of

[022-052] a thousand years, and carved with the patient skill of unending

[022-053] generations of the most famous artists of the most artistic country of

[022-054] the western world, would have given him much to marvel at also. It

[022-055] must have seemed like a city of dream, a city to catch the heart, when,

[022-056] coming over the great plain, Fionn saw Tara of the Kings held on its

[022-057] hill as in a hand to gather all the gold of the falling sun, and to

[022-058] restore a brightness as mellow and tender as that universal largess.

[022-059] In the great banqueting hall everything was in order for the feast. The

[022-060] nobles of Ireland with their winsome consorts, the learned and artistic

[022-061] professions represented by the pick of their time were in place. The

[022-062] Ard-Ri, Corm of the Hundred Battles, had taken his place on the raised

[022-063] dais which commanded the whole of that vast hall. At his Right hand his

[022-064] son Art, to be afterwards as famous as his famous father, took his seat,

[022-065] and on his left Goll mor mac Morna, chief of the [[fianna|Fianna of Ireland]], had

[022-066] the seat of honour. As the High King took his place he could see every

[022-067] person who was noted in the land for any reason. He would know every one

[022-068] who was present, for the fame of all men is sealed at Tara, and behind

[022-069] his chair a herald stood to tell anything the king might not know or had

[022-070] forgotten.

[022-071] Conn gave the signal and his guests seated themselves.

[022-072] The time had come for the squires to take their stations behind their

[022-073] masters and mistresses. But, for the moment, the great room was seated,

[022-074] and the doors were held to allow a moment of respect to pass before the

[022-075] servers and squires came in.

[022-076] Looking over his guests, Conn observed that a young man was yet

[022-077] standing.

[022-078] “There is a gentleman,” he murmured, “for whom no seat has been found.”

[022-079] We may be sure that the Master of the Banquet blushed at that.

[022-080] “And,” the king continued, “I do not seem to know the young man.”

[022-081] Nor did his herald, nor did the unfortunate Master, nor did anybody; for

[022-082] the eyes of all were now turned where the king’s went.

[022-083] “Give me my horn,” said the gracious monarch.

[022-084] The horn of state was put to his hand.

[022-085] “Young gentleman,” he called to the stranger, “I wish to drink to your

[022-086] health and to welcome you to Tara.”

[022-087] The young man came forward then, greater-shouldered than any mighty

[022-088] man of that gathering, longer and cleaner limbed, with his fair curls

[022-089] dancing about his beardless face. The king put the great horn into his

[022-090] hand.

[022-091] “Tell me your name,” he commanded gently.

[022-092] “I am Fionn, the son of Uail, the son of Baiscne,” said the youth.

[022-093] And at that saying a touch as of lightning went through the gathering

[022-094] so that each person quivered, and the son of the great, murdered captain

[022-095] looked by the king’s shoulder into the twinkling eye of Goll. But no

[022-096] word was uttered, no movement made except the movement and the utterance

[022-097] of the Ard-Ri’.

[022-098] “You are the son of a friend,” said the great-hearted monarch. “You

[022-099] shall have the seat of a friend.”

[022-100] He placed Fionn at the right hand of his own son Art.
