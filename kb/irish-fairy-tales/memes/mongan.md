## Overview

Mongan is the central figure of "Mongan's Frenzy," the longest tale in James Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales*. A prince of [[ulster|Ulster]] with a dual identity — publicly the son of King [[fiachna-finn|Fiachna Finn]], secretly the reincarnation of the hero [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] — Mongan's story is one of supernatural heritage, passionate love, and the wild pursuit of what has been taken from him.

## Identity

Mongan was born to the wife of [[fiachna-finn|Fiachna Finn]], King of [[ulster|Ulster]], on the same night that his servant An Da'v's wife also gave birth to a son, called [[mac-an-dav|mac an Da'v]] [077-008]–[077-011]. But Mongan's true lineage is far stranger: his real father was [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannan]] mac Lir, the god of the sea and ruler of the [[land-of-promise|Land of Promise]] [077-030]–[077-033].

When Mongan was three days old, [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannan]] appeared in the palace, wrapped the infant in his green cloak, and bore him away to the [[land-of-promise|Land of Promise]] — "beyond the sea that is at the other side of the grave" — to rear and train him [077-030]–[077-033]. Mongan's identity thus bridges the mortal and divine realms, the human kingdom of [[ulster|Ulster]] and the supernatural domain of [[faery|Faery]].

The secret at the heart of Mongan's identity is that he is the reincarnation of [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]]. When a companion whispered to the poet Bro'tiarna, "Was Mongan Fionn?", the revelation shook the very foundation of how the tales understand heroism — the great hero returns, in a new form, to live again among mortals [089-103]–[089-105].

## In the Tales

### Mongan's Frenzy

Mongan was affianced at birth to [[duv-laca|Duv Laca of the White Hand]], daughter of his father's rival, Fiachna Duv of the Dal Fiatach — a political union designed to end the feud between the two kings [077-018]–[077-028]. But when Mongan was taken to [[faery|Faery]] by [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannan]], the infant [[mac-an-dav|mac an Da'v]] was substituted in his place, and [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]] eventually married the wrong man.

The central conflict of "Mongan's Frenzy" is Mongan's return from the [[land-of-promise|Otherworld]] to reclaim his identity and his wife. The tale follows his wild, shape-shifting, [[enchantment|magic]]-wielding campaign to win back [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]] — a frenzy that is as much about reclaiming a stolen life as it is about love.

Throughout his adventures, Mongan displays the supernatural abilities inherited from his divine father: he can change form, command the forces of [[faery|Faery]], and outwit kings and [[druid|druids]] alike. His companion [[cairide|Cairide]]' — the ancestor of the story-teller who narrates the tale to the abbot of [[moville|Moville]] — accompanies him into [[faery|Faery]] and bears witness to the whole extraordinary story [070-031]–[070-034].

## Significance

Mongan represents one of the most sophisticated narrative concepts in Irish mythology: the hero who is simultaneously a mortal prince, a god's son, and the reincarnation of Ireland's greatest hero. His tale bridges the world of heroic action (the [[fianna|Fianna]], the battles of [[ulster|Ulster]]) with the world of [[faery|Faery]] [[enchantment|magic]] and divine intervention. The framing device — a story-teller recounting the tale to the abbot of [[moville|Moville]] — adds a layer of meta-narrative about the preservation of pre-Christian stories [070-005]–[070-009].
