## Overview

Faery is not a single place but a cosmology. Stephens' *Irish Fairy Tales* presents it as a layered supernatural realm, revealed most explicitly in *[[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]*: "After this Earth there is the world of the Shi'. Beyond it again lies the Many-Coloured Land. Next comes the Land of Wonder, and after that the [[land-of-promise|Land of Promise]] awaits us" [060-006]–[060-008]. Each layer is reached through a different element — clay for the Shi', water for the Many-Coloured Land, fire for the Land of Wonder [060-008]–[060-010] — and each operates under increasingly refined laws of existence.

## Location

Faery has no earthly coordinates, but its entrances are everywhere in the Irish landscape. Sídhe mounds, caves (like [[cesh-corran|Cesh Corran]]), and certain bodies of water serve as thresholds. Fionn's adventures are "by the path of clay and into Faery only" [060-014], while [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]], being High King and "arch-[[enchantment|magic]]ian of Ireland" [060-015], could travel the water-path to the Many-Coloured Land. The [[land-of-promise|Land of Promise]] — the deepest Faery realm — lies "beyond the sea that is at the other side of the grave" [077-033], a phrase that collapses the boundary between geography and death itself.

## In the Tales

### The Shi'

The Shi' (also [[enchantment|spell]]ed Sídhe) is the Faery layer closest to the mortal world. Its inhabitants include the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha De Danann]], who retreated into the mounds after their defeat by the Milesians, and beings like Becuma, who was banished from higher realms. The Shi' operates under laws parallel to Earth: "In the Shi' matrimony is recorded as being parallel in every respect with earth-marriage, and the desire which urges to it seems to be as violent and inconstant as it is with us" [060-021]–[060-024]. It is from the Shi' that the People of Faery emerge to interact with — and trouble — the mortal world.

### The Many-Coloured Land

The Many-Coloured Land is a higher sphere where marriage becomes "but a contemplation of beauty, a brooding and meditation wherein all grosser desire is unknown and children are born to sinless parents" [060-024]–[060-026]. It is from this refined realm that [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]] was banished for her infidelity — a crime that in the Shi' would have been "lightly considered" but in the Many-Coloured Land drew "implacable and grim" retribution [060-027]–[060-032].

### The Land of Promise

The deepest accessible Faery realm, ruled by [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannan]] mac Lir, god of the sea. It is here that the infant [[mongan|Mongan]] was taken by his true father to be raised and trained [077-030]–[077-033], and it is from here that the supernatural gifts and protections enjoyed by heroes like Fionn and his descendants originate.

## Mythological Significance

Faery in Stephens' retelling is a sophisticated metaphysical architecture, not merely a "[[enchantment|magic]]al land." Its layered structure — with increasingly refined moral and ontological laws at each level — suggests a cosmos where spiritual advancement maps onto physical traversal. The permeability between Faery and Ireland is the engine of most narrative conflict in the tales: Faery beings enter the mortal world bearing gifts, curses, and impossible beauty, and mortal heroes venture into Faery to retrieve what has been lost.
