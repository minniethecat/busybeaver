[075-001] A year passed, and one day as he was sitting at judgement there came

[075-002] a great noise from without, and this noise was so persistent that the

[075-003] people and suitors were scandalised, and Fiachna at last ordered that

[075-004] the noisy person should be brought before him to be judged.

[075-005] It was done, and to his surprise the person turned out to be the Black

[075-006] Hag.

[075-007] She blamed him in the court before his people, and complained that he

[075-008] had taken away her cow, and that she had not been paid the four cows he

[075-009] had gone bail for, and she demanded judgement from him and justice.

[075-010] “If you will consider it to be justice, I will give you twenty cows

[075-011] myself,” said Fiachna.

[075-012] “I would not take all the cows in [[ulster|Ulster]],” she screamed.

[075-013] “Pronounce judgement yourself,” said the king, “and if I can do what you

[075-014] demand I will do it.” For he did not like to be in the wrong, and he did

[075-015] not wish that any person should have an unsatisfied claim upon him.

[075-016] The Black Hag then pronounced judgement, and the king had to fulfil it.

[075-017] “I have come,” said she, “from the east to the west; you must come from

[075-018] the west to the east and make war for me, and revenge me on the King of

[075-019] Lochlann.”

[075-020] Fiachna had to do as she demanded, and, although it was with a heavy

[075-021] heart, he set out in three days’ time for Lochlann, and he brought with

[075-022] him ten battalions.

[075-023] He sent messengers before him to Big Eolgarg warning him of his coming,

[075-024] of his intention, and of the number of troops he was bringing; and when

[075-025] he landed Eolgarg met him with an equal force, and they fought together.

[075-026] In the first battle three hundred of the men of Lochlann were killed,

[075-027] but in the next battle Eolgarg Mor did not fight fair, for he let some

[075-028] venomous sheep out of a tent, and these attacked the men of Ulster and

[075-029] killed nine hundred of them.

[075-030] So vast was the slaughter made by these sheep and so great the terror

[075-031] they caused, that no one could stand before them, but by great good luck

[075-032] there was a wood at hand, and the men of Ulster, warriors and princes

[075-033] and charioteers, were forced to climb up the trees, and they roosted

[075-034] among the branches like great birds, while the venomous sheep ranged

[075-035] below bleating terribly and tearing up the ground.

[075-036] Fiachna Fim was also sitting in a tree, very high up, and he was

[075-037] disconsolate.

[075-038] “We are disgraced,” said he.

[075-039] “It is very lucky,” said the man in the branch below, “that a sheep

[075-040] cannot climb a tree.”

[075-041] “We are disgraced for ever,” said the King of Ulster.

[075-042] “If those sheep learn how to climb, we are undone surely,” said the man

[075-043] below.

[075-044] “I will go down and fight the sheep,” said Fiachna. But the others would

[075-045] not let the king go.

[075-046] “It is not right,” they said, “that you should fight sheep.”

[075-047] “Some one must fight them,” said [[fiachna-finn|Fiachna Finn]], “but no more of my men

[075-048] shall die until I fight myself; for if I am fated to die, I will die and

[075-049] I cannot escape it, and if it is the sheep’s fate to die, then die they

[075-050] will; for there is no man can avoid destiny, and there is no sheep can

[075-051] dodge it either.”

[075-052] “Praise be to god!” said the warrior that was higher up.

[075-053] “Amen!” said the man who was higher than he, and the rest of the

[075-054] warriors wished good luck to the king.

[075-055] He started then to climb down the tree with a heavy heart, but while

[075-056] he hung from the last branch and was about to let go, he noticed a tall

[075-057] warrior walking towards him. The king pulled himself up on the branch

[075-058] again and sat dangle-legged on it to see what the warrior would do.

[075-059] The stranger was a very tall man, dressed in a green cloak with a silver

[075-060] brooch at the shoulder. He had a golden band about his hair and golden

[075-061] sandals on his feet, and he was laughing heartily at the plight of the

[075-062] men of Ireland.
