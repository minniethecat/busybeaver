[012-001] Fionn [pronounce Fewn to rhyme with “tune”] got his first training among

[012-002] women. There is no wonder in that, for it is the pup’s mother teaches it

[012-003] to fight, and women know that fighting is a necessary art although men

[012-004] pretend there are others that are better. These were the women [[druid|druids]],

[012-005] [[bovmall|Bovmall]] and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]]. It will be wondered why his own mother did not

[012-006] train him in the first natural savageries of existence, but she could

[012-007] not do it. She could not keep him with her for dread of the clann-Morna.

[012-008] The [[clann-morna|sons of Morna]] had been fighting and intriguing for a long time to

[012-009] oust her husband, Uail, from the captaincy of the [[fianna|Fianna of Ireland]],

[012-010] and they had ousted him at last by killing him. It was the only way

[012-011] they could get rid of such a man; but it was not an easy way, for what

[012-012] Fionn’s father did not know in arms could not be taught to him even by

[012-013] Morna. Still, the hound that can wait will catch a hare at last, and

[012-014] even Manana’nn sleeps. Fionn’s mother was beautiful, long-haired [[muirne|Muirne]]:

[012-015] so she is always referred to. She was the daughter of Teigue, the son of

[012-016] [[nuada|Nuada]] from [[faery|Faery]], and her mother was Ethlinn. That is, her brother

[012-017] was [[lugh|Lugh of the Long Hand]] himself, and with a god, and such a god, for

[012-018] brother we may marvel that she could have been in dread of Morna or his

[012-019] sons, or of any one. But women have strange loves, strange fears, and

[012-020] these are so bound up with one another that the thing which is presented

[012-021] to us is not often the thing that is to be seen.

[012-022] However it may be, when Uall died Muirne got married again to the King

[012-023] of [[kerry|Kerry]]. She gave the child to Bovmall and Lia Luachra to rear, and we

[012-024] may be sure that she gave injunctions with him, and many of them. The

[012-025] youngster was brought to the [[slieve-bloom|woods of Slieve Bloom]] and was nursed there

[012-026] in secret.

[012-027] It is likely the women were fond of him, for other than Fionn there

[012-028] was no life about them. He would be their life; and their eyes may

[012-029] have seemed as twin benedictions resting on the small fair head. He was

[012-030] fair-haired, and it was for his fairness that he was afterwards called

[012-031] Fionn; but at this period he was known as [[fionn-mac-uail|Deimne]]. They saw the food they

[012-032] put into his little frame reproduce itself length-ways and sideways in

[012-033] tough inches, and in springs and energies that crawled at first, and

[012-034] then toddled, and then ran. He had birds for playmates, but all the

[012-035] creatures that live in a wood must have been his comrades. There would

[012-036] have been for little Fionn long hours of lonely sunshine, when the world

[012-037] seemed just sunshine and a sky. There would have been hours as long,

[012-038] when existence passed like a shade among shadows, in the multitudinous

[012-039] tappings of rain that dripped from leaf to leaf in the wood, and slipped

[012-040] so to the ground. He would have known little snaky paths, narrow enough

[012-041] to be filled by his own small feet, or a goat’s; and he would have

[012-042] wondered where they went, and have marvelled again to find that,

[012-043] wherever they went, they came at last, through loops and twists of the

[012-044] branchy wood, to his own door. He may have thought of his own door as

[012-045] the beginning and end of the world, whence all things went, and whither

[012-046] all things came.

[012-047] Perhaps he did not see the lark for a long time, but he would have heard

[012-048] him, far out of sight in the endless sky, thrilling and thrilling until

[012-049] the world seemed to have no other sound but that clear sweetness; and

[012-050] what a world it was to make that sound! Whistles and chirps, coos and

[012-051] caws and croaks, would have grown familiar to him. And he could at last

[012-052] have told which brother of the great brotherhood was making the noise

[012-053] he heard at any moment. The wind too: he would have listened to its

[012-054] thousand voices as it moved in all seasons and in all moods. Perhaps a

[012-055] horse would stray into the thick screen about his home, and would look

[012-056] as solemnly on Fionn as Fionn did on it. Or, coming suddenly on him,

[012-057] the horse might stare, all a-cock with eyes and ears and nose, one

[012-058] long-drawn facial extension, ere he turned and bounded away with

[012-059] manes all over him and hoofs all under him and tails all round him. A

[012-060] solemn-nosed, stern-eyed cow would amble and stamp in his wood to find a

[012-061] flyless shadow; or a strayed sheep would poke its gentle muzzle through

[012-062] leaves.

[012-063] “A boy,” he might think, as he stared on a staring horse, “a boy cannot

[012-064] wag his tail to keep the flies off,” and that lack may have saddened

[012-065] him. He may have thought that a cow can snort and be dignified at

[012-066] the one moment, and that timidity is comely in a sheep. He would have

[012-067] scolded the jackdaw, and tried to out-whistle the throstle, and wondered

[012-068] why his pipe got tired when the blackbird’s didn’t. There would be flies

[012-069] to be watched, slender atoms in yellow gauze that flew, and filmy specks

[012-070] that flittered, and sturdy, thick-ribbed brutes that pounced like cats

[012-071] and bit like dogs and flew like lightning. He may have mourned for the

[012-072] spider in bad luck who caught that fly. There would be much to see and

[012-073] remember and compare, and there would be, always, his two guardians. The

[012-074] flies change from second to second; one cannot tell if this bird is a

[012-075] visitor or an inhabitant, and a sheep is just sister to a sheep; but the

[012-076] women were as rooted as the house itself.
