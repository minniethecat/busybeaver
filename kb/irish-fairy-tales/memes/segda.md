# Segda

## Identity

Segda, whose name means "Sweet Speech," was the son of Daire Degamra ([[daire|Daire]]) and his queen, the daughter of Lodan, rulers of a paradisal island that lay beyond the shores of Ireland. He was named for the quality of his voice and the gentleness of his manner — qualities that would prove decisive in the political and supernatural crises that followed the arrival of the High King [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]] on his father's island. [^1]

## In the Tales

### The Geas Impasse

When Conn of the Hundred Battles arrived on the island of Daire Degamra during his search for a new queen, he was offered food by the queen. Conn refused, stating that it was [[geasa|geasa]] (taboo) for him to eat alone — a sacred prohibition binding upon the High King of [[tara|Tara]]. The queen replied that she and her household never ate together, creating what seemed to be an irresolvable conflict between the king's geasa and the customs of his hosts. [^1]

> "I will eat with you," said Segda (Sweet Speech), "and thus, while you are saved from the violation of your geasa, the custom of this household will not be broken." [065-006]

Segda's intervention was not merely diplomatic but cosmologically significant: by creating a third path where only two seemed to exist, he demonstrated the Irish mythological principle that geasa — however absolute — could be navigated with wit and generosity, never by simple violation.

### Journey to Tara

Conn, grateful for Segda's solution, brought the youth back with him to [[tara|Tara]], where Segda became entangled in the wider drama of [[becuma|Becuma of the White Skin]]'s presence at the High King's court. When Becuma's scheme against Conn was uncovered, Segda found himself at the centre of the ensuing debate — whether Becuma should be punished for her transgression, and what the nature of justice demanded in a world where mortal law and supernatural law intersected. [^1]

### The Debate

The debate over Becuma's fate brought Segda into direct engagement with the highest authorities of the land. He gave his opinion with the clarity that his name promised, and when the matter was concluded, he received three kisses on each cheek from those who had been his opponents — a gesture that in the Irish tradition signified not merely reconciliation but the recognition of honour. [^1]

> "Indeed," said Segda, returning those kisses, "indeed I had not thought that a man could speak so fairly against me and yet remain my friend." [066-007]

## Significance

Segda embodies the Irish ideal of *sweet speech* — the power of well-chosen words to dissolve conflicts that force cannot touch. In a mythological world where geasa bind with the force of natural law, Segda's ability to find a path between competing obligations makes him a quietly heroic figure. He is not a warrior, a king, or a druid — he is a youth whose gift is clarity, and whose clarity saves a king from violating his sacred bond and a household from breaking its customs.
