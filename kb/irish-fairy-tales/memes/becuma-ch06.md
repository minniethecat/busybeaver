[065-001] Having gone some distance by these pleasant ways he saw a shapely house

[065-002] dozing in the sunlight.

[065-003] It was thatched with the wings of birds, blue wings and yellow and white

[065-004] wings, and in the centre of the house there was a door of crystal set in

[065-005] posts of bronze.

[065-006] The queen of this island lived there, Rigru (Large-eyed), the daughter

[065-007] of Lodan, and wife of Daire Degamra. She was seated on a crystal

[065-008] throne with her son Segda by her side, and they welcomed the High King

[065-009] courteously.

[065-010] There were no servants in this palace; nor was there need for them. The

[065-011] High King found that his hands had washed themselves, and when later on

[065-012] he noticed that food had been placed before him he noticed also that

[065-013] it had come without the assistance of servile hands. A cloak was laid

[065-014] gently about his shoulders, and he was glad of it, for his own was

[065-015] soiled by exposure to sun and wind and water, and was not worthy of a

[065-016] lady’s eye.

[065-017] Then he was invited to eat.

[065-018] He noticed, however, that food had been set for no one but himself, and

[065-019] this did not please him, for to eat alone was contrary to the hospitable

[065-020] usage of a king, and was contrary also to his contract with the gods.

[065-021] “Good, my hosts,” he remonstrated, “it is geasa ([[geasa|taboo]]) for me to eat

[065-022] alone.”

[065-023] “But we never eat together,” the queen replied.

[065-024] “I cannot violate my geasa,” said the High King.

[065-025] “I will eat with you,” said Segda (Sweet Speech), “and thus, while you

[065-026] are our guest you will not do violence to your vows.”

[065-027] “Indeed,” said Conn, “that will be a great satisfaction, for I have

[065-028] already all the trouble that I can cope with and have no wish to add to

[065-029] it by offending the gods.”

[065-030] “What is your trouble?” the gentle queen asked. “During a year,” Conn

[065-031] replied, “there has been neither corn nor milk in Ireland. The land is

[065-032] parched, the trees are withered, the birds do not sing in Ireland, and

[065-033] the bees do not make honey.”

[065-034] “You are certainly in trouble,” the queen assented.

[065-035] “But,” she continued, “for what purpose have you come to our island?”

[065-036] “I have come to ask for the loan of your son.”

[065-037] “A loan of my son!”

[065-038] “I have been informed,” Conn explained, “that if the son of a sinless

[065-039] couple is brought to [[tara|Tara]] and is bathed in the waters of Ireland the

[065-040] land will be delivered from those ills.”

[065-041] The king of this island, Daire, had not hitherto spoken, but he now did

[065-042] so with astonishment and emphasis.

[065-043] “We would not lend our son to any one, not even to gain the kingship of

[065-044] the world,” said he.

[065-045] But Segda, observing that the guest’s countenance was discomposed, broke

[065-046] in:

[065-047] “It is not kind to refuse a thing that the Ard-Ri’ of Ireland asks for,

[065-048] and I will go with him.”

[065-049] “Do not go, my pulse,” his father advised.

[065-050] “Do not go, my one treasure,” his mother pleaded.

[065-051] “I must go indeed,” the boy replied, “for it is to do good I am

[065-052] required, and no person may shirk such a requirement.”

[065-053] “Go then,” said his father, “but I will place you under the protection

[065-054] of the High King and of the Four Provincial Kings of Ireland, and under

[065-055] the protection of Art, the son of Conn, and of Fionn, the son of Uail,

[065-056] and under the protection of the magicians and poets and the men of art

[065-057] in Ireland.” And he thereupon bound these protections and safeguards on

[065-058] the Ard-Ri’ with an oath.

[065-059] “I will answer for these protections,” said Conn.

[065-060] He departed then from the island with Segda and in three days they

[065-061] reached Ireland, and in due time they arrived at Tara.
