[066-001] On reaching the palace Conn called his magicians and poets to a council

[066-002] and informed them that he had found the boy they sought--the son of a

[066-003] virgin. These learned people consulted together, and they stated that

[066-004] the young man must be killed, and that his blood should be mixed with

[066-005] the earth of [[tara|Tara]] and sprinkled under the withered trees.

[066-006] When Segda heard this he was astonished and defiant; then, seeing that

[066-007] he was alone and without prospect of succour, he grew downcast and was

[066-008] in great fear for his life. But remembering the safeguards under which

[066-009] he had been placed, he enumerated these to the assembly, and called on

[066-010] the High King to grant him the protections that were his due.

[066-011] Conn was greatly perturbed, but, as in duty bound, he placed the boy

[066-012] under the various protections that were in his oath, and, with the

[066-013] courage of one who has no more to gain or lose, he placed Segda,

[066-014] furthermore, under the protection of all the men of Ireland.

[066-015] But the men of Ireland refused to accept that bond, saying that although

[066-016] the Ard-Ri’ was acting justly towards the boy he was not acting justly

[066-017] towards Ireland.

[066-018] “We do not wish to slay this prince for our pleasure,” they argued, “but

[066-019] for the safety of Ireland he must be killed.”

[066-020] Angry parties were formed. Art, and Fionn the son of Uail, and the

[066-021] princes of the land were outraged at the idea that one who had been

[066-022] placed under their protection should be hurt by any hand. But the men of

[066-023] Ireland and the magicians stated that the king had gone to [[faery|Faery]] for a

[066-024] special purpose, and that his acts outside or contrary to that purpose

[066-025] were illegal, and committed no person to obedience.

[066-026] There were debates in the Council Hall, in the market-place, in the

[066-027] streets of Tara, some holding that national honour dissolved and

[066-028] absolved all personal honour, and others protesting that no man had

[066-029] aught but his personal honour, and that above it not the gods, not even

[066-030] Ireland, could be placed--for it is to be known that Ireland is a god.

[066-031] Such a debate was in course, and Segda, to whom both sides addressed

[066-032] gentle and courteous arguments, grew more and more disconsolate.

[066-033] “You shall die for Ireland, dear heart,” said one of them, and he gave

[066-034] Segda three kisses on each cheek.

[066-035] “Indeed,” said Segda, returning those kisses, “indeed I had not

[066-036] bargained to die for Ireland, but only to bathe in her waters and to

[066-037] remove her pestilence.”

[066-038] “But dear child and prince,” said another, kissing him likewise, “if any

[066-039] one of us could save Ireland by dying for her how cheerfully we would

[066-040] die.”

[066-041] And Segda, returning his three kisses, agreed that the death was noble,

[066-042] but that it was not in his undertaking.

[066-043] Then, observing the stricken countenances about him, and the faces of

[066-044] men and women hewn thin by hunger, his resolution melted away, and he

[066-045] said:

[066-046] “I think I must die for you,” and then he said:

[066-047] “I will die for you.”

[066-048] And when he had said that, all the people present touched his cheek with

[066-049] their lips, and the love and peace of Ireland entered into his soul, so

[066-050] that he was tranquil and proud and happy.

[066-051] The executioner drew his wide, thin blade and all those present covered

[066-052] their eyes with their cloaks, when a wailing voice called on the

[066-053] executioner to delay yet a moment. The High King uncovered his eyes and

[066-054] saw that a woman had approached driving a cow before her.

[066-055] “Why are you killing the boy?” she demanded.

[066-056] The reason for this slaying was explained to her.

[066-057] “Are you sure,” she asked, “that the poets and magicians really know

[066-058] everything?”

[066-059] “Do they not?” the king inquired.

[066-060] “Do they?” she insisted.

[066-061] And then turning to the magicians:

[066-062] “Let one magician of the magicians tell me what is hidden in the bags

[066-063] that are lying across the back of my cow.”

[066-064] But no magician could tell it, nor did they try to.

[066-065] “Questions are not answered thus,” they said. “There is formulae, and

[066-066] the calling up of spirits, and lengthy complicated preparations in our

[066-067] art.”

[066-068] “I am not badly learned in these arts,” said the woman, “and I say that

[066-069] if you slay this cow the effect will be the same as if you had killed

[066-070] the boy.”

[066-071] “We would prefer to kill a cow or a thousand cows rather than harm this

[066-072] young prince,” said Conn, “but if we spare the boy will these evils

[066-073] return?”

[066-074] “They will not be banished until you have banished their cause.”

[066-075] “And what is their cause?”

[066-076] “Becuma is the cause, and she must be banished.”

[066-077] “If you must tell me what to do,” said Conn, “tell me at least to do

[066-078] something that I can do.”

[066-079] “I will tell you certainly. You can keep Becuma and your ills as long as

[066-080] you want to. It does not matter to me. Come, my son,” she said to

[066-081] Segda, for it was Segda’s mother who had come to save him; and then

[066-082] that sinless queen and her son went back to their home of enchantment,

[066-083] leaving the king and Fionn and the magicians and nobles of Ireland

[066-084] astonished and ashamed.
