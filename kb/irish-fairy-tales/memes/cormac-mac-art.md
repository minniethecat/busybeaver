## Overview

Cormac mac Art, the High King of Ireland, appears in the Fenian tales primarily as an arbiter of justice — the sovereign authority to whom even [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]] and [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] must submit their disputes. His seat at [[tara|Tara]] is the symbolic centre of law and order in the mortal world, standing in contrast to the wilder justice of the warrior-bands and the capricious supernatural order of the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]].

## Identity

Cormac mac Art is the son of [[art-mac-conn|Art mac Conn]] and the grandson of [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]], placing him in the direct line of the High Kings of Ireland. His seat is [[tara|Tara of the Kings]], the ancient ceremonial and judicial centre from which the High King dispensed judgement over the provinces. He is a figure of established authority — neither a warrior like Fionn nor a rival like Goll, but the institutional power that stands above faction.

## In the Tales

### The Judgement at Tara

Cormac's most prominent role comes in the aftermath of the [[brawl-at-allen|Brawl at Allen]]. After Fionn and Goll agree that "the matter had been judged by the king, Cormac mac Art, and by his daughter Ailve, and by his son Cairbre of Ana Life' and by Fintan the chief poet" [044-134]–[044-136], a formal court convenes at [[tara|Tara]].

Cormac presides with measured authority. When Fionn attempts to give evidence — his own version of events — Goll objects. Cormac demands: "If you object to the testimony of the chief person present, in what way are we to obtain evidence?" [045-001]–[045-003]. The question is practical, judicial — he is seeking a path to truth, not taking sides.

When Goll proposes [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus True-Lips]] as a witness, and Fergus gives his account of the battle's casualties — sixty-one of Goll's men against eleven hundred of Fionn's — Cormac's response is telling. "I marvel," he says "in a discontented voice," that "considering the numbers against them, the losses of clann-Morna should be so small" [045-022]–[045-024]. Fionn blushes at this observation — Cormac has spoken the truth that Fionn would prefer unspoken, and he has done so not as a partisan but as a judge weighing the evidence.

He presses further: "What is your judgement?" he demands of his fellow-judges [045-036]. When the penalty against Goll is set at a level that would ruin him, Cormac asks simply: "Why?" [045-039]. His question is neither challenge nor defense — it is the demand of a just king that every judgement have a reason.

## Significance

Cormac mac Art embodies the principle of law over force in the Fenian world. Where Fionn represents martial authority and the bonds of the warrior-band, Cormac represents the institutional authority of the High Kingship. His ability to command both Fionn and Goll to submit to judgement, and his careful, questioning approach to that judgement, establishes that even the greatest warriors of the [[fianna|Fianna]] answer to a higher civil power.
