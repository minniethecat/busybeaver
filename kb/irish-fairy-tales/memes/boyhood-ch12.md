[023-001] It is to be known that on the night of the [[samhain|Feast of Samhain]] the doors

[023-002] separating this world and the next one are opened, and the inhabitants

[023-003] of either world can leave their respective spheres and appear in the

[023-004] world of the other beings.

[023-005] Now there was a grandson to the Dagda Mor, the Lord of the Underworld,

[023-006] and he was named [[aillen-mac-midna|Aillen mac Midna]], out of Shi’ Finnachy, and this Aillen

[023-007] bore an implacable enmity to [[tara|Tara]] and the Ard-Ri’.

[023-008] As well as being monarch of Ireland her High King was chief of the

[023-009] people learned in [[enchantment|magic]], and it is possible that at some time Conn had

[023-010] adventured into Tir na n-Og, the Land of the Young, and had done some

[023-011] deed or misdeed in Aillen’s lordship or in his family. It must have been

[023-012] an ill deed in truth, for it was in a very rage of revenge that Aillen

[023-013] came yearly at the permitted time to ravage Tara.

[023-014] Nine times he had come on this mission of revenge, but it is not to be

[023-015] supposed that he could actually destroy the holy city: the Ard-Ri’

[023-016] and magicians could prevent that, but he could yet do a damage so

[023-017] considerable that it was worth Conn’s while to take special extra

[023-018] precautions against him, including the precaution of chance.

[023-019] Therefore, when the feast was over and the banquet had commenced, the

[023-020] Hundred Fighter stood from his throne and looked over his assembled

[023-021] people.

[023-022] The Chain of Silence was shaken by the attendant whose duty and honour

[023-023] was the Silver Chain, and at that delicate chime the halt went silent,

[023-024] and a general wonder ensued as to what matter the High King would submit

[023-025] to his people.

[023-026] “Friends and heroes,” said Conn, “Aillen, the son of Midna, will come

[023-027] to-night from Slieve Fuaid with occult, terrible fire against our

[023-028] city. Is there among you one who loves Tara and the king, and who will

[023-029] undertake our defence against that being?”

[023-030] He spoke in silence, and when he had finished he listened to the same

[023-031] silence, but it was now deep, ominous, agonized. Each man glanced

[023-032] uneasily on his neighbour and then stared at his wine-cup or his

[023-033] fingers. The hearts of young men went hot for a gallant moment and were

[023-034] chilled in the succeeding one, for they had all heard of Aillen out of

[023-035] Shl Finnachy in the north. The lesser gentlemen looked under their brows

[023-036] at the greater champions, and these peered furtively at the greatest of

[023-037] all. Art og mac Morna of the Hard Strokes fell to biting his fingers,

[023-038] Cona’n the Swearer and Garra mac Morna grumbled irritably to each other

[023-039] and at their neighbours, even Caelte, the son of Rona’n, looked down

[023-040] into his own lap, and Goll Mor sipped at his wine without any twinkle

[023-041] in his eye. A horrid embarrassment came into the great hall, and as the

[023-042] High King stood in that palpitating silence his noble face changed

[023-043] from kindly to grave and from that to a terrible sternness. In another

[023-044] moment, to the undying shame of every person present, he would have been

[023-045] compelled to lift his own challenge and declare himself the champion of

[023-046] Tara for that night, but the shame that was on the faces of his people

[023-047] would remain in the heart of their king. Goll’s merry mind would help

[023-048] him to forget, but even his heart would be wrung by a memory that he

[023-049] would not dare to face. It was at that terrible moment that Fionn stood

[023-050] up.

[023-051] “What,” said he, “will be given to the man who undertakes this defence?”

[023-052] “All that can be rightly asked will be royally bestowed,” was the king’s

[023-053] answer.

[023-054] “Who are the sureties?” said Fionn.

[023-055] “The kings of Ireland, and Red Cith with his magicians.”

[023-056] “I will undertake the defence,” said Fionn. And on that, the kings and

[023-057] magicians who were present bound themselves to the fulfilment of the

[023-058] bargain.

[023-059] Fionn marched from the banqueting hall, and as he went, all who were

[023-060] present of nobles and retainers and servants acclaimed him and wished

[023-061] him luck. But in their hearts they were bidding him good-bye, for all

[023-062] were assured that the lad was marching to a death so unescapeable that

[023-063] he might already be counted as a dead man.

[023-064] It is likely that Fionn looked for help to the people of the Shi’

[023-065] themselves, for, through his mother, he belonged to the tribes of Dana,

[023-066] although, on the father’s side, his blood was well compounded with

[023-067] mortal clay. It may be, too, that he knew how events would turn, for he

[023-068] had eaten [[salmon-of-knowledge|the Salmon]] of Knowledge. Yet it is not recorded that on this

[023-069] occasion he invoked any magical art as he did on other adventures.

[023-070] Fionn’s way of discovering whatever was happening and hidden was always

[023-071] the same and is many times referred to. A shallow, oblong dish of pure,

[023-072] pale gold was brought to him. This dish was filled with clear water.

[023-073] Then Fionn would bend his head and stare into the water, and as he

[023-074] stared he would place his thumb in his mouth under his “Tooth of

[023-075] Knowledge,” his “wisdom tooth.”

[023-076] Knowledge, may it be said, is higher than magic and is more to be

[023-077] sought. It is quite possible to see what is happening and yet not know

[023-078] what is forward, for while seeing is believing it does not follow that

[023-079] either seeing or believing is knowing. Many a person can see a thing and

[023-080] believe a thing and know just as little about it as the person who does

[023-081] neither. But Fionn would see and know, or he would under-stand a decent

[023-082] ratio of his visions. That he was versed in magic is true, for he was

[023-083] ever known as the Knowledgeable man, and later he had two magicians

[023-084] in his household named Dirim and mac-Reith to do the rough work of

[023-085] knowledge for their busy master.

[023-086] It was not from the Shi’, however, that assistance came to Fionn.
