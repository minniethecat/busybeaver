# Tailltin

## Overview

Tailltin (Tailtiu, modern Teltown) is a sacred place in Royal Meath, significant in the tale of [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn of the Hundred Battles]]. It was here that Conn buried his wife and held nine days of funeral games — an event that echoes the historical Óenach Tailtenn, the great assembly and fair of ancient Ireland. [^1]

## Location

Tailltin lies in Royal Meath, the central province and seat of the High Kings at nearby [[tara|Tara]]. The text describes it as "the sacred place of his own lordship," underlining its special status as a royal site under Conn's direct authority rather than a territory governed by a subordinate lord. [^1] It is listed alongside two other great sacred sites of Ireland: the Boyne in Ulster — associated with the [[tuatha-de-danann|Tuatha Dé Danann]] and the Brugh na Bóinne — and Cruachan in Connacht — the royal seat of Queen Medb and entrance to the [[sidhe-mound|sídhe]] of the west. That Tailltin stands in this company marks it as one of the three great ceremonial centers of ancient Ireland.

## In the Tales

### Becuma of the White Skin

In *Becuma of the White Skin*, Tailltin is mentioned as one of the significant places of Ireland alongside the Boyne in Ulster and Cruachan in Connacht. [^1] It is identified as Conn's own sacred ground, where he buried his wife — [[becuma|Becuma]]'s predecessor as queen:

> "It was in this last, the sacred place of his own lordship, that Conn laid his wife to rest." [^1]

The funeral rites were elaborate: "Her funeral games were played during nine days. Her keen was sung by poets and harpers, and a cairn ten acres wide was heaved over her clay." [^1] Princes of the Five Provinces attended, marking Tailltin as a site of national significance. The scale of the cairn — ten acres wide — suggests a monumental construction that would have dominated the landscape of Meath, a permanent marker of royal grief and communal memory. The nine days of games set a precedent for the pattern of ritualized mourning among the Irish kings: a public spectacle that transformed private sorrow into a celebration of sovereignty, binding the provinces to the central authority of [[tara|Tara]] through shared ceremony.

## Mythological Significance

Tailltin gives its name to the Óenach Tailtenn (the Fair of Tailtiu), one of the great assemblies of ancient Ireland where laws were proclaimed, games were held, and the people of Ireland gathered. The tale preserves the mythical foundation of this tradition in Conn's funeral games for his queen. As a site in Royal Meath — the symbolic center of Ireland — Tailltin represents the union of royal authority, sacred ritual, and communal memory.

The historical Óenach Tailtenn survived well into the medieval period, held annually at Teltown on the Blackwater River in County Meath. It was traditionally inaugurated by Lugh Lámhfhada in honor of his foster-mother Tailtiu — a different mythological figure from Conn's queen — but the Stephens corpus reworks the foundation story to center on Conn rather than Lugh, reflecting the author's focus on the Fenian and historical cycles rather than the mythological. This layering of traditions — Tuatha Dé Danann foundation, royal funeral games, historical fair — makes Tailltin a palimpsest of Irish cultural memory.
