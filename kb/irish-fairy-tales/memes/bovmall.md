## Identity

Bovmall was a druid-woman of the clann-Baiscne, the sister of Uail mac
Baiscne and thus the aunt of [[fionn-mac-uail|Fionn mac Uail]]. "Bovmall was
Uail's sister, and, consequently, Fionn's aunt. Only such a blood-tie could
have bound them to the clann-Baiscne, for it is not easy, having moved in
the world of court and camp, to go hide with a baby in a wood; and to live,
as they must have lived, in terror" [013-041].

Her husband was [[fiacuil-mac-cona|Fiacuil mac Cona]], a fierce warrior who
took to the wilds as an outlaw after the clann-Baiscne was broken by the
sons of Morna. "He had taken to the wilds when clann-Baiscne was broken,
and he was at war with a world that had dared to kill his Chief" [016-033].
This marriage bound Bovmall doubly to the cause of Uail's line — by blood
and by marriage.

## In the Tales

### Fostering Fionn

After Uail mac Baiscne was killed by the clann-Morna, [[muirne|Muirne]]
could not keep her infant son safely with her. The sons of Morna sought
to destroy the heir of Uail. Muirne therefore "gave the child to Bovmall
and [[lia-luachra|Lia Luachra]] to rear" [012-023]. The two druid-women
brought the infant — then called Deimne — to the [[slieve-bloom|woods of
Slieve Bloom]] and nursed him there in secret.

The women were the sole human company of the growing boy: "other than Fionn
there was no life about them. He would be their life; and their eyes may
have seemed as twin benedictions resting on the small fair head" [012-025].
They watched his growth from crawling to toddling to running, teaching him
the first arts of survival.

### The Druid-Women's Teaching

The text notes that "it is the pup's mother teaches it to fight, and women
know that fighting is a necessary art" [012-016]. Bovmall and Lia Luachra,
as druids, were more than nurses — they were Fionn's first teachers in
"the first natural savageries of existence" [012-018], laying the foundation
for the warrior he would become.

### Life in Terror

Living in hiding with the infant heir of clann-Baiscne meant constant fear
of the sons of Morna. What stories Bovmall and Lia Luachra would have told
the child — of Morna himself, the huge-shouldered, stern-eyed Connachtman,
and of his son [[goll-mac-morna|Goll Mor mac Morna]], as fierce and
huge-shouldered as his father yet merry-eyed in repose [013-042]. These
stories shaped Fionn's understanding of the feud that had cost him his
father and driven his foster-mothers into the wilderness.

### Sending Fionn Forth

When Fionn had grown, Bovmall sent him away from the Slieve Bloom woods to
find his own path. Muirne, hearing of this, "went in sorrowful and lonely
ways to her lord in [[kerry|Kerry]]" [013-045].

### The Foster-Mother's Bond

The partnership between Bovmall and Lia Luachra is presented as a seamless
joint enterprise. Stephens refers to them throughout as a compound name —
"Bovmall and Lia Luachra" [012-005] — suggesting that their work of
fosterage was so thoroughly shared that neither woman's contribution can be
separated from the other's. Bovmall brought the blood-tie to Uail's line;
Lia Luachra, whose own lineage the text does not detail, brought a
complementary wisdom. Together they formed the hidden foundation upon which
the Fenian cycle rests: without their years of secret nurture in the woods
of Slieve Bloom, there would have been no Fionn to lead the Fianna.

As druid-women, Bovmall and Lia Luachra were more than nurses or guardians.
The text's reflection that "it is the pup's mother teaches it to fight"
[012-016] points to a deeper transmission: through their stories of Morna
and Goll, of Uail's bravery and the clan's shattering, the two women gave
the boy Deimne the identity he would need to become Fionn mac Uail. Their
fosterage was not merely physical preservation but cultural transmission —
the passing of a clan's memory from one generation to the next, accomplished
in secret while the clan's enemies hunted for the heir.
