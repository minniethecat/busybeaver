[037-001] “I used to live,” he said, “in a wide, beautiful place. There were hills

[037-002] and valleys there, and woods and streams, but in whatever direction I

[037-003] went I came always to a cliff, so tall it seemed to lean against the

[037-004] sky, and so straight that even a goat would not have imagined to climb

[037-005] it.”

[037-006] “I do not know of any such place,” Fionn mused.

[037-007] “There is no such place in Ireland,” said Caelte, “but in the Shi’ there

[037-008] is such a place.”

[037-009] “There is in truth,” said Fionn.

[037-010] “I used to eat fruits and roots in the summer,” the boy continued, “but

[037-011] in the winter food was left for me in a cave.”

[037-012] “Was there no one with you?” Fionn asked.

[037-013] “No one but a deer that loved me, and that I loved.”

[037-014] “Ah me!” cried Fionn in anguish, “tell me your tale, my son.”

[037-015] “A dark stern man came often after us, and he used to speak with the

[037-016] deer. Sometimes he talked gently and softly and coaxingly, but at times

[037-017] again he would shout loudly and in a harsh, angry voice. But whatever

[037-018] way he talked the deer would draw away from him in dread, and he always

[037-019] left her at last furiously.”

[037-020] “It is the Dark Magician of the Men of God,” cried Fionn despairingly.

[037-021] “It is indeed, my soul,” said Caelte.

[037-022] “The last time I saw the deer,” the child continued, “the dark man was

[037-023] speaking to her. He spoke for a long time. He spoke gently and angrily,

[037-024] and gently and angrily, so that I thought he would never stop talking,

[037-025] but in the end he struck her with a hazel rod, so that she was forced

[037-026] to follow him when he went away. She was looking back at me all the time

[037-027] and she was crying so bitterly that any one would pity her. I tried to

[037-028] follow her also, but I could not move, and I cried after her too, with

[037-029] rage and grief, until I could see her no more and hear her no more. Then

[037-030] I fell on the grass, my senses went away from me, and when I awoke I was

[037-031] on the hill in the middle of the hounds where you found me.”

[037-032] That was the boy whom the [[fianna|Fianna]] called Oisi’n, or the Little Fawn. He

[037-033] grew to be a great fighter afterwards, and he was the chief maker of

[037-034] poems in the world. But he was not yet finished with the Shi. He was to

[037-035] go back into [[faery|Faery]] when the time came, and to come thence again to tell

[037-036] these tales, for it was by him these tales were told.
