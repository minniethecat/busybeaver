[008-001] “OLD age again overtook me. Weariness stole into my limbs, and anguish

[008-002] dozed into my mind. I went to my [[ulster|Ulster]] cave and dreamed my dream, and I

[008-003] changed into a hawk.

[008-004] “I left the ground. The sweet air was my kingdom, and my bright eye

[008-005] stared on a hundred miles. I soared, I swooped; I hung, motionless as a

[008-006] living stone, over the abyss; I lived in joy and slept in peace, and had

[008-007] my fill of the sweetness of life.

[008-008] “During that time Beothach, the son of Iarbonel the Prophet, came to

[008-009] Ireland with his people, and there was a great battle between his men

[008-010] and the children of Semion. Long I hung over that combat, seeing every

[008-011] spear that hurtled, every stone that whizzed from a sling, every sword

[008-012] that flashed up and down, and the endless glittering of the shields. And

[008-013] at the end I saw that the victory was with Iarbonel. And from his people

[008-014] the Tuatha De’ and the Ande’ came, although their origin is forgotten,

[008-015] and learned people, because of their excellent wisdom and intelligence,

[008-016] say that they came from heaven.

[008-017] “These are the people of [[faery|Faery]]. All these are the gods.

[008-018] “For long, long years I was a hawk. I knew every hill and stream; every

[008-019] field and glen of Ireland. I knew the shape of cliffs and coasts, and

[008-020] how all places looked under the sun or moon. And I was still a hawk when

[008-021] the sons of Mil drove the Tuatha De’ Danann under the ground, and held

[008-022] Ireland against arms or wizardry; and this was the coming of men and the

[008-023] beginning of genealogies.

[008-024] “Then I grew old, and in my Ulster cave close to the sea I dreamed my

[008-025] dream, and in it I became a salmon. The green tides of ocean rose over

[008-026] me and my dream, so that I drowned in the sea and did not die, for I

[008-027] awoke in deep waters, and I was that which I dreamed. I had been a man,

[008-028] a stag, a boar, a bird, and now I was a fish. In all my changes I had

[008-029] joy and fulness of life. But in the water joy lay deeper, life pulsed

[008-030] deeper. For on land or air there is always something excessive and

[008-031] hindering; as arms that swing at the sides of a man, and which the

[008-032] mind must remember. The stag has legs to be tucked away for sleep, and

[008-033] untucked for movement; and the bird has wings that must be folded and

[008-034] pecked and cared for. But the fish has but one piece from his nose to

[008-035] his tail. He is complete, single and unencumbered. He turns in one turn,

[008-036] and goes up and down and round in one sole movement.

[008-037] “How I flew through the soft element: how I joyed in the country where

[008-038] there is no harshness: in the element which upholds and gives way; which

[008-039] caresses and lets go, and will not let you fall. For man may stumble in

[008-040] a furrow; the stag tumble from a cliff; the hawk, wing-weary and beaten,

[008-041] with darkness around him and the storm behind, may dash his brains

[008-042] against a tree. But the home of the salmon is his delight, and the sea

[008-043] guards all her creatures.”
