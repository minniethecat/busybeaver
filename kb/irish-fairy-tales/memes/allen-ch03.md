[045-001] AT the end of a fortnight Fionn and Goll and the chief men of the [[fianna|Fianna]]

[045-002] attended at [[tara|Tara]]. The king, his son and daughter, with Flahri, Feehal,

[045-003] and Fintan mac Bocna sat in the place of judgement, and Cormac called on

[045-004] the witnesses for evidence.

[045-005] Fionn stood up, but the moment he did so [[goll-mac-morna|Goll mac Morna]] arose also.

[045-006] “I object to Fionn giving evidence,” said he.

[045-007] “Why so?” the king asked.

[045-008] “Because in any matter that concerned me Fionn would turn a lie into

[045-009] truth and the truth into a lie.”

[045-010] “I do not think that is so,” said Fionn.

[045-011] “You see, he has already commenced it,” cried Goll.

[045-012] “If you object to the testimony of the chief person present, in what way

[045-013] are we to obtain evidence?” the king demanded.

[045-014] “I,” said Goll, “will trust to the evidence of [[fergus-true-lips|Fergus True-Lips]]. He is

[045-015] Fionn’s poet, and will tell no lie against his master; he is a poet, and

[045-016] will tell no lie against any one.”

[045-017] “I agree to that,” said Fionn.

[045-018] “I require, nevertheless,” Goll continued, “that Fergus should swear

[045-019] before the Court, by his gods, that he will do justice between us.”

[045-020] Fergus was accordingly sworn, and gave his evidence. He stated that

[045-021] Fionn’s brother Cairell struck Cona’n mac Morna, that Goll’s two sons

[045-022] came to help Cona’n, that [[oscar|Oscar]] went to help Cairell, and with that

[045-023] Fionn’s people and the clann-Morna rose at each other, and what had

[045-024] started as a brawl ended as a battle with eleven hundred of Fionn’s

[045-025] people and sixty-one of Goll’s people dead.

[045-026] “I marvel,” said the king in a discontented voice, “that, considering

[045-027] the numbers against them, the losses of clann-Morna should be so small.”

[045-028] Fionn blushed when he heard that.

[045-029] Fergus replied:

[045-030] “Goll mac Morna covered his people with his shield. All that slaughter

[045-031] was done by him.”

[045-032] “The press was too great,” Fionn grumbled. “I could not get at him in

[045-033] time or---”

[045-034] “Or what?” said Goll with a great laugh.

[045-035] Fionn shook his head sternly and said no more.

[045-036] “What is your judgement?” Cormac demanded of his fellow-judges.

[045-037] Flahri pronounced first.

[045-038] “I give damages to clann-Morna.”

[045-039] “Why?” said Cormac.

[045-040] “Because they were attacked first.”

[045-041] Cormac looked at him stubbornly.

[045-042] “I do not agree with your judgement,” he said.

[045-043] “What is there faulty in it?” Flahri asked.

[045-044] “You have not considered,” the king replied, “that a soldier owes

[045-045] obedience to his captain, and that, given the time and the place, Fionn

[045-046] was the captain and Goll was only a simple soldier.”

[045-047] Flahri considered the king’s suggestion.

[045-048] “That,” he said, “would hold good for the white-striking or blows of

[045-049] fists, but not for the red-striking or sword-strokes.”

[045-050] “What is your judgement?” the king asked Feehal. Feehal then pronounced:

[045-051] “I hold that clann-Morna were attacked first, and that they are to be

[045-052] free from payment of damages.”

[045-053] “And as regards Fionn?” said Cormac.

[045-054] “I hold that on account of his great losses Fionn is to be exempt

[045-055] from payment of damages, and that his losses are to be considered as

[045-056] damages.”

[045-057] “I agree in that judgement,” said Fintan.

[045-058] The king and his son also agreed, and the decision was imparted to the

[045-059] Fianna.

[045-060] “One must abide by a judgement,” said Fionn.

[045-061] “Do you abide by it?” Goll demanded.

[045-062] “I do,” said Fionn.

[045-063] Goll and Fionn then kissed each other, and thus peace was made. For,

[045-064] notwithstanding the endless bicker of these two heroes, they loved each

[045-065] other well.

[045-066] Yet, now that the years have gone by, I think the fault lay with Goll

[045-067] and not with Fionn, and that the judgement given did not consider

[045-068] everything. For at that table Goll should not have given greater gifts

[045-069] than his master and host did. And it was not right of Goll to take by

[045-070] force the position of greatest gift-giver of the Fianna, for there was

[045-071] never in the world one greater at giving gifts, or giving battle, or

[045-072] making poems than Fionn was.

[045-073] That side of the affair was not brought before the Court. But perhaps it

[045-074] was suppressed out of delicacy for Fionn, for if Goll could be accused

[045-075] of ostentation, Fionn was open to the uglier charge of jealousy. It

[045-076] was, nevertheless, Goll’s forward and impish temper which commenced the

[045-077] brawl, and the verdict of time must be to exonerate Fionn and to let the

[045-078] blame go where it is merited.

[045-079] There is, however, this to be added and remembered, that whenever Fionn

[045-080] was in a tight corner it was Goll that plucked him out of it; and, later

[045-081] on, when time did his worst on them all and the Fianna were sent to hell

[045-082] as unbelievers, it was Goll mac Morna who assaulted hell, with a chain

[045-083] in his great fist and three iron balls swinging from it, and it was

[045-084] he who attacked the hosts of great devils and brought Fionn and the

[045-085] Fianna-[[fionn-mac-uail|Finn]] out with him.
