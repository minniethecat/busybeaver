[061-001] [[conn-of-the-hundred-battles|Conn the Hundred Fighter]], Ard-Ri’ of Ireland, was in the lowest spirits

[061-002] that can be imagined, for his wife was dead. He had been Ard-Ri for nine

[061-003] years, and during his term the corn used to be reaped three times in

[061-004] each year, and there was full and plenty of everything. There are few

[061-005] kings who can boast of more kingly results than he can, but there was

[061-006] sore trouble in store for him.

[061-007] He had been married to Eithne, the daughter of Brisland Binn, King of

[061-008] Norway, and, next to his subjects, he loved his wife more than all that

[061-009] was lovable in the world. But the term of man and woman, of king or

[061-010] queen, is set in the stars, and there is no escaping Doom for any one;

[061-011] so, when her time came, Eithne died.

[061-012] Now there were three great burying-places in Ireland--the Brugh of the

[061-013] [[boyne|Boyne]] in [[ulster|Ulster]], over which [[angus-og|Angus Og]] is chief and god; the Shi’ mound

[061-014] of Cruachan Ahi, where [[ethal-anbual|Ethal Anbual]] presides over the underworld of

[061-015] [[connacht|Connacht]], and Tailltin, in [[meath|Royal Meath]]. It was in this last, the sacred

[061-016] place of his own lordship, that Conn laid his wife to rest.

[061-017] Her funeral games were played during nine days. Her keen was sung by

[061-018] poets and harpers, and a cairn ten acres wide was heaved over her clay.

[061-019] Then the keening ceased and the games drew to an end; the princes of the

[061-020] Five Prov-inces returned by horse or by chariot to their own places;

[061-021] the concourse of mourners melted away, and there was nothing left by

[061-022] the great cairn but the sun that dozed upon it in the daytime, the heavy

[061-023] clouds that brooded on it in the night, and the desolate, memoried king.

[061-024] For the dead queen had been so lovely that Conn could not forget her;

[061-025] she had been so kind at every moment that he could not but miss her at

[061-026] every moment; but it was in the Council Chamber and the Judgement

[061-027] Hall that he most pondered her memory. For she had also been wise, and

[061-028] lack-ing her guidance, all grave affairs seemed graver, shadowing each

[061-029] day and going with him to the pillow at night.

[061-030] The trouble of the king becomes the trouble of the subject, for how

[061-031] shall we live if judgement is withheld, or if faulty decisions are

[061-032] promulgated? Therefore, with the sorrow of the king, all Ireland was in

[061-033] grief, and it was the wish of every person that he should marry again.

[061-034] Such an idea, however, did not occur to him, for he could not conceive

[061-035] how any woman should fill the place his queen had vacated. He grew more

[061-036] and more despondent, and less and less fitted to cope with affairs of

[061-037] state, and one day he instructed his son Art to take the rule during his

[061-038] absence, and he set out for [[ben-edair|Ben Edair]].

[061-039] For a great wish had come upon him to walk beside the sea; to listen

[061-040] to the roll and boom of long, grey breakers; to gaze on an unfruitful,

[061-041] desolate wilderness of waters; and to forget in those sights all that

[061-042] he could forget, and if he could not forget then to remember all that he

[061-043] should remember.

[061-044] He was thus gazing and brooding when one day he observed a coracle

[061-045] drawing to the shore. A young girl stepped from it and walked to him

[061-046] among black boulders and patches of yellow sand.
