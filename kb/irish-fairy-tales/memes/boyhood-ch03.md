[014-001] THE women [[druid|druids]], his guardians, belonged to his father’s people.

[014-002] [[bovmall|Bovmall]] was Uail’s sister, and, consequently, Fionn’s aunt. Only such

[014-003] a blood-tie could have bound them to the clann-Baiscne, for it is not

[014-004] easy, having moved in the world of court and camp, to go hide with a

[014-005] baby in a wood; and to live, as they must have lived, in terror.

[014-006] What stories they would have told the child of the [[clann-morna|sons of Morna]]. Of

[014-007] Morna himself, the huge-shouldered, stern-eyed, violent Connachtman; and

[014-008] of his sons--young [[goll-mac-morna|Goll Mor mac Morna]] in particular, as huge-shouldered

[014-009] as his father, as fierce in the onset, but merry-eyed when the other

[014-010] was grim, and bubbling with a laughter that made men forgive even his

[014-011] butcheries. Of Cona’n Mael mac Morna his brother, gruff as a badger,

[014-012] bearded like a boar, bald as a crow, and with a tongue that could manage

[014-013] an insult where another man would not find even a stammer. His boast was

[014-014] that when he saw an open door he went into it, and when he saw a closed

[014-015] door he went into it. When he saw a peaceful man he insulted him, and

[014-016] when he met a man who was not peaceful he insulted him. There was Garra

[014-017] Duv mac Morna, and savage Art Og, who cared as little for their own

[014-018] skins as they did for the next man’s, and Garra must have been rough

[014-019] indeed to have earned in that clan the name of the Rough mac Morna.

[014-020] There were others: wild Connachtmen all, as untameable, as unaccountable

[014-021] as their own wonderful countryside.

[014-022] Fionn would have heard much of them, and it is likely that he practised

[014-023] on a nettle at taking the head off Goll, and that he hunted a sheep

[014-024] from cover in the implacable manner he intended later on for Cona’n the

[014-025] Swearer.

[014-026] But it is of Uail mac Baiscne he would have heard most. With what a

[014-027] dilation of spirit the ladies would have told tales of him, Fionn’s

[014-028] father. How their voices would have become a chant as feat was added

[014-029] to feat, glory piled on glory. The most famous of men and the most

[014-030] beautiful; the hardest fighter; the easiest giver; the kingly champion;

[014-031] the chief of the [[fianna|Fianna]] na h-Eirinn. Tales of how he had been way-laid

[014-032] and got free; of how he had been generous and got free; of how he had

[014-033] been angry and went marching with the speed of an eagle and the direct

[014-034] onfall of a storm; while in front and at the sides, angled from the prow

[014-035] of his terrific advance, were fleeing multitudes who did not dare to

[014-036] wait and scarce had time to run. And of how at last, when the time

[014-037] came to quell him, nothing less than the whole might of Ireland was

[014-038] sufficient for that great downfall.

[014-039] We may be sure that on these adventures Fionn was with his father, going

[014-040] step for step with the long-striding hero, and heartening him mightily.
