[024-001] He marched through the successive fortifications until he came to the

[024-002] outer, great wall, the boundary of the city, and when he had passed this

[024-003] he was on the wide plain of [[tara|Tara]].

[024-004] Other than himself no person was abroad, for on the night of the Feast

[024-005] of [[samhain|Samhain]] none but a madman would quit the shelter of a house even if

[024-006] it were on fire; for whatever disasters might be within a house would be

[024-007] as nothing to the calamities without it.

[024-008] The noise of the banquet was not now audible to Fionn--it is possible,

[024-009] however, that there was a shamefaced silence in the great hall--and the

[024-010] lights of the city were hidden by the successive great ramparts. The sky

[024-011] was over him; the earth under him; and than these there was nothing, or

[024-012] there was but the darkness and the wind.

[024-013] But darkness was not a thing to terrify him, bred in the nightness of

[024-014] a wood and the very fosterling of gloom; nor could the wind afflict his

[024-015] ear or his heart. There was no note in its orchestra that he had not

[024-016] brooded on and become, which becoming is [[enchantment|magic]]. The long-drawn moan of

[024-017] it; the thrilling whisper and hush; the shrill, sweet whistle, so thin

[024-018] it can scarcely be heard, and is taken more by the nerves than by the

[024-019] ear; the screech, sudden as a devil’s yell and loud as ten thunders; the

[024-020] cry as of one who flies with backward look to the shelter of leaves and

[024-021] darkness; and the sob as of one stricken with an age-long misery, only

[024-022] at times remembered, but remembered then with what a pang! His ear

[024-023] knew by what successions they arrived, and by what stages they grew and

[024-024] diminished. Listening in the dark to the bundle of noises which make a

[024-025] noise he could disentangle them and assign a place and a reason to each

[024-026] gradation of sound that formed the chorus: there was the patter of a

[024-027] rabbit, and there the scurrying of a hare; a bush rustled yonder,

[024-028] but that brief rustle was a bird; that pressure was a wolf, and this

[024-029] hesitation a fox; the scraping yonder was but a rough leaf against bark,

[024-030] and the scratching beyond it was a ferret’s claw.

[024-031] Fear cannot be where knowledge is, and Fionn was not fearful.

[024-032] His mind, quietly busy on all sides, picked up one sound and dwelt on

[024-033] it. “A man,” said Fionn, and he listened in that direction, back towards

[024-034] the city.

[024-035] A man it was, almost as skilled in darkness as Fionn himself “This is no

[024-036] enemy,” Fionn thought; “his walking is open.”

[024-037] “Who comes?” he called.

[024-038] “A friend,” said the newcomer.

[024-039] “Give a friend’s name,” said Fionn.

[024-040] “[[fiacuil-mac-cona|Fiacuil mac Cona]],” was the answer.

[024-041] “Ah, my pulse and heart!” cried Fionn, and he strode a few paces to meet

[024-042] the great robber who had fostered him among the marshes.

[024-043] “So you are not afraid,” he said joyfully.

[024-044] “I am afraid in good truth,” Fiacuil whispered, “and the minute my

[024-045] business with you is finished I will trot back as quick as legs will

[024-046] carry me. May the gods protect my going as they protected my coming,”

[024-047] said the robber piously.

[024-048] “Amen,” said Fionn, “and now, tell me what you have come for?”

[024-049] “Have you any plan against this lord of the Shl?” Fiacuil whispered.

[024-050] “I will attack him,” said Fionn.

[024-051] “That is not a plan,” the other groaned, “we do not plan to deliver an

[024-052] attack but to win a victory.”

[024-053] “Is this a very terrible person?” Fionn asked.

[024-054] “Terrible indeed. No one can get near him or away from him. He comes out

[024-055] of the Shi’ playing sweet, low music on a timpan and a pipe, and all who

[024-056] hear this music fall asleep.”

[024-057] “I will not fall asleep,” said Fionn.

[024-058] “You will indeed, for everybody does.”

[024-059] “What happens then?” Fionn asked.

[024-060] “When all are asleep [[aillen-mac-midna|Aillen mac Midna]] blows a dart of fire out of his

[024-061] mouth, and everything that is touched by that fire is destroyed, and he

[024-062] can blow his fire to an incredible distance and to any direction.”

[024-063] “You are very brave to come to help me,” Fionn murmured, “especially

[024-064] when you are not able to help me at all.”

[024-065] “I can help,” Fiacuil replied, “but I must be paid.”

[024-066] “What payment?”

[024-067] “A third of all you earn and a seat at your council.”

[024-068] “I grant that,” said Fionn, “and now, tell me your plan?”

[024-069] “You remember my spear with the thirty rivets of Arabian gold in its

[024-070] socket?”

[024-071] “The one,” Fionn queried, “that had its head wrapped in a blanket and

[024-072] was stuck in a bucket of water and was chained to a wall as well--the

[024-073] venomous Birgha?” “That one,” Fiacuil replied.

[024-074] “It is Aillen mac Midna’s own spear,” he continued, “and it was taken

[024-075] out of his Shi’ by your father.”

[024-076] “Well?” said Fionn, wondering nevertheless where Fiacuil got the spear,

[024-077] but too generous to ask.

[024-078] “When you hear the great man of the Shi’ coming, take the wrappings off

[024-079] the head of the spear and bend your face over it; the heat of the spear,

[024-080] the stench of it, all its pernicious and acrid qualities will prevent

[024-081] you from going to sleep.”

[024-082] “Are you sure of that?” said Fionn.

[024-083] “You couldn’t go to sleep close to that stench; nobody could,” Fiacuil

[024-084] replied decidedly.

[024-085] He continued: “Aillen mac Midna will be off his guard when he stops

[024-086] playing and begins to blow his fire; he will think everybody is asleep;

[024-087] then you can deliver the attack you were speaking of, and all good luck

[024-088] go with it.”

[024-089] “I will give him back his spear,” said Fionn.

[024-090] “Here it is,” said Fiacuil, taking the Birgha from under his cloak. “But

[024-091] be as careful of it, my pulse, be as frightened of it as you are of the

[024-092] man of Dana.”

[024-093] “I will be frightened of nothing,” said Fionn, “and the only person I

[024-094] will be sorry for is that Aillen mac Midna, who is going to get his own

[024-095] spear back.”

[024-096] “I will go away now,” his companion whispered, “for it is growing darker

[024-097] where you would have thought there was no more room for darkness, and

[024-098] there is an eerie feeling abroad which I do not like. That man from the

[024-099] Shi’ may come any minute, and if I catch one sound of his music I am

[024-100] done for.”

[024-101] The robber went away and again Fionn was alone.
