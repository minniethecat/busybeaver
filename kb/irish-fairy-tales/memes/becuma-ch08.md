[067-001] There are good and evil people in this and in every other world, and the

[067-002] person who goes hence will go to the good or the evil that is native

[067-003] to him, while those who return come as surely to their due. The trouble

[067-004] which had fallen on Becuma did not leave her repentant, and the sweet

[067-005] lady began to do wrong as instantly and innocently as a flower begins

[067-006] to grow. It was she who was responsible for the ills which had come on

[067-007] Ireland, and we may wonder why she brought these plagues and droughts to

[067-008] what was now her own country.

[067-009] Under all wrong-doing lies personal vanity or the feeling that we are

[067-010] endowed and privileged beyond our fellows. It is probable that, however

[067-011] courageously she had accepted fate, Becuma had been sharply stricken in

[067-012] her pride; in the sense of personal strength, aloofness, and identity,

[067-013] in which the mind likens itself to god and will resist every domination

[067-014] but its own. She had been punished, that is, she had submitted to

[067-015] control, and her sense of freedom, of privilege, of very being, was

[067-016] outraged. The mind flinches even from the control of natural law, and

[067-017] how much more from the despotism of its own separated likenesses, for if

[067-018] another can control me that other has usurped me, has become me, and how

[067-019] terribly I seem diminished by the seeming addition!

[067-020] This sense of separateness is vanity, and is the bed of all wrong-doing.

[067-021] For we are not freedom, we are control, and we must submit to our own

[067-022] function ere we can exercise it. Even unconsciously we accept the rights

[067-023] of others to all that we have, and if we will not share our good with

[067-024] them, it is because we cannot, having none; but we will yet give what

[067-025] we have, although that be evil. To insist on other people sharing in

[067-026] our personal torment is the first step towards insisting that they shall

[067-027] share in our joy, as we shall insist when we get it.

[067-028] Becuma considered that if she must suffer all else she met should suffer

[067-029] also. She raged, therefore, against Ireland, and in particular she raged

[067-030] against young Art, her husband’s son, and she left undone nothing that

[067-031] could afflict Ireland or the prince. She may have felt that she could

[067-032] not make them suffer, and that is a maddening thought to any woman. Or

[067-033] perhaps she had really desired the son instead of the father, and her

[067-034] thwarted desire had perpetuated itself as hate. But it is true that Art

[067-035] regarded his mother’s successor with intense dislike, and it is true

[067-036] that she actively returned it.

[067-037] One day Becuma came on the lawn before the palace, and seeing that Art

[067-038] was at chess with Cromdes she walked to the table on which the match was

[067-039] being played and for some time regarded the game. But the young prince

[067-040] did not take any notice of her while she stood by the board, for he knew

[067-041] that this girl was the enemy of Ireland, and he could not bring himself

[067-042] even to look at her.

[067-043] Becuma, looking down on his beautiful head, smiled as much in rage as in

[067-044] disdain.

[067-045] “O son of a king,” said she, “I demand a game with you for stakes.”

[067-046] Art then raised his head and stood up courteously, but he did not look

[067-047] at her.

[067-048] “Whatever the queen demands I will do,” said he.

[067-049] “Am I not your mother also?” she replied mockingly, as she took the seat

[067-050] which the chief magician leaped from.

[067-051] The game was set then, and her play was so skilful that Art was hard put

[067-052] to counter her moves. But at a point of the game Becuma grew thoughtful,

[067-053] and, as by a lapse of memory, she made a move which gave the victory to

[067-054] her opponent. But she had intended that. She sat then, biting on her lip

[067-055] with her white small teeth and staring angrily at Art.

[067-056] “What do you demand from me?” she asked.

[067-057] “I bind you to eat no food in Ireland until you find the wand of Curoi,

[067-058] son of Dare’.”

[067-059] Becuma then put a cloak about her and she went from [[tara|Tara]] northward and

[067-060] eastward until she came to the dewy, sparkling Brugh of [[angus-og|Angus mac an Og]]

[067-061] in [[ulster|Ulster]], but she was not admitted there. She went thence to the Shi’

[067-062] ruled over by Eogabal, and although this lord would not admit her, his

[067-063] daughter Aine’, who was her foster-sister, let her into [[faery|Faery]].

[067-064] She made inquiries and was informed where the [[dun]] of Curoi mac Dare’

[067-065] was, and when she had received this intelligence she set out for Sliev

[067-066] Mis. By what arts she coaxed Curoi to give up his wand it matters not,

[067-067] enough that she was able to return in triumph to Tara. When she handed

[067-068] the wand to Art, she said:

[067-069] “I claim my game of revenge.”

[067-070] “It is due to you,” said Art, and they sat on the lawn before the palace

[067-071] and played.

[067-072] A hard game that was, and at times each of the combatants sat for an

[067-073] hour staring on the board before the next move was made, and at times

[067-074] they looked from the board and for hours stared on the sky seeking as

[067-075] though in heaven for advice. But Becuma’s foster-sister, Aine’, came

[067-076] from the Shi’, and, unseen by any, she interfered with Art’s play, so

[067-077] that, suddenly, when he looked again on the board, his face went pale,

[067-078] for he saw that the game was lost.

[067-079] “I didn’t move that piece,” said he sternly.

[067-080] “Nor did I,” Becuma replied, and she called on the onlookers to confirm

[067-081] that statement.

[067-082] She was smiling to herself secretly, for she had seen what the mortal

[067-083] eyes around could not see.

[067-084] “I think the game is mine,” she insisted softly.

[067-085] “I think that your friends in Faery have cheated,” he replied, “but the

[067-086] game is yours if you are content to win it that way.”

[067-087] “I bind you,” said Becuma, “to eat no food in Ireland until you have

[067-088] found Delvcaem, the daughter of Morgan.”

[067-089] “Where do I look for her?” said Art in despair.

[067-090] “She is in one of the islands of the sea,” Becuma replied, “that is

[067-091] all I will tell you,” and she looked at him maliciously, joyously,

[067-092] contentedly, for she thought he would never return from that journey,

[067-093] and that Morgan would see to it.
