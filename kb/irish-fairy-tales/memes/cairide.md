## Overview

Cairide was the story-teller (*seanchaí*) and chronicler of the court of
[[mongan|Mongan]], King of [[ulster|Ulster]]. In the literary architecture of
"Mongan's Frenzy," he serves a dual role: as a character within the
frame narrative and as the in-universe source of the inner tale — the
scribe who preserved the account of [[mongan|Mongan]]'s journey into
[[faery|Faery]] with [[manannan-mac-lir|Manannan mac Lir]].

His presence in the tale is a deliberate narrative device. By
attributing the story to a named chronicler at Mongan's own court, the
tale claims the authority of eyewitness testimony while simultaneously
acknowledging its own nature as a story told and retold — a sophisticated
piece of meta-narrative that sets "Mongan's Frenzy" apart from simpler
tales in the collection.

## The Role of the Seanchaí

In medieval Irish society, the *seanchaí* (story-teller or historian)
occupied a position of considerable prestige. These were not mere
entertainers but the custodians of genealogies, histories, and legal
precedents — the living memory of the kingdom. A king's *seanchaí* knew
the lineage of every noble house, the outcome of every significant
battle, and the terms of every treaty.

Cairide embodies this tradition. His claim to unique knowledge — "I am
the only man that knows it" — is not boastful but professional: as the
court's designated keeper of stories, it was his duty to know what
others had forgotten.

## In the Tales

### The Inherited Story

Cairide explained that his knowledge came through a lineage of
storytellers: a previous Cairide of his nation had accompanied
[[mongan|Mongan]] when the king went into [[faery|Faery]], and had listened to the
story when it was first told [070-032]–[070-033]. This detail is
significant — it establishes a chain of custody for the narrative,
suggesting that what we hear has passed through the hands of
professional preservers of oral tradition before reaching the page.

### Recording the Faery Journey

When Cairide was asked to share his tale, the court prepared for a
formal recording. Vellum was brought, quills were prepared, and
Cairide began to dictate [070-038]. This moment — the transition from
oral performance to written record — marks the birth of the story as
we have it. Cairide's narration forms the innermost layer of
*[[mongan|Mongan's Frenzy]]*, a story within a story within a story: the
medieval scribe records Cairide's dictation of the tale that the
earlier Cairide had heard [[mongan|Mongan]] tell of his own adventures.

### The Queen's Inquiry

Cairide's knowledge extended beyond stories to the delicate politics of
the court. The Queen of [[ulster|Ulster]], [[duv-laca|Duv Laca]], cross-questioned
Cairide in an attempt to discover the truth about her husband's
mysterious activities and his connection to the supernatural realm
[071-033]. As the keeper of secrets as well as stories, Cairide walked a
careful line between his loyalty to the king and his duty to the queen.

### The Wise Observer

In later episodes, Cairide appeared as a knowing observer of the
political intrigues surrounding [[mongan|Mongan]]. When asked about delicate
matters, he replied with diplomatic precision — agreeing that certain
deeds were "ill done" while privately finding satisfaction in how events
had unfolded [089-106]–[089-113]. This quality of shrewd observation
marks him as more than a passive recorder; he was an astute judge of
character and motive whose perspective enriches the tale.

## Legacy

Without Cairide, "Mongan's Frenzy" would not exist in the form we have
it. His role as the bridge between oral tradition and written record —
between the living voice of the storyteller and the permanent mark of
ink on vellum — represents a crucial moment in Irish literary culture.
He is, in a sense, the patron saint of every scribe who ever sat down to
preserve a fading story.
