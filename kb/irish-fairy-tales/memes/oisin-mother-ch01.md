[031-001] EVENING was drawing nigh, and the [[fianna|Fianna-Finn]] had decided to hunt no

[031-002] more that day. The hounds were whistled to heel, and a sober, homeward

[031-003] march began. For men will walk soberly in the evening, however they go

[031-004] in the day, and dogs will take the mood from their masters. They were

[031-005] pacing so, through the golden-shafted, tender-coloured eve, when a

[031-006] fawn leaped suddenly from covert, and, with that leap, all quietness

[031-007] vanished: the men shouted, the dogs gave tongue, and a furious chase

[031-008] commenced.

[031-009] Fionn loved a chase at any hour, and, with [[bran-and-sceolan|Bran]] and Sceo’lan, he

[031-010] outstripped the men and dogs of his troop, until nothing remained in the

[031-011] limpid world but Fionn, the two hounds, and the nimble, beautiful fawn.

[031-012] These, and the occasional boulders, round which they raced, or over

[031-013] which they scrambled; the solitary tree which dozed aloof and beautiful

[031-014] in the path, the occasional clump of trees that hived sweet shadow as

[031-015] a hive hoards honey, and the rustling grass that stretched to infinity,

[031-016] and that moved and crept and swung under the breeze in endless, rhythmic

[031-017] billowings.

[031-018] In his wildest moment Fionn was thoughtful, and now, although running

[031-019] hard, he was thoughtful. There was no movement of his beloved hounds

[031-020] that he did not know; not a twitch or fling of the head, not a cock

[031-021] of the ears or tail that was not significant to him. But on this chase

[031-022] whatever signs the dogs gave were not understood by their master.

[031-023] He had never seen them in such eager flight. They were almost utterly

[031-024] absorbed in it, but they did not whine with eagerness, nor did they cast

[031-025] any glance towards him for the encouraging word which he never failed to

[031-026] give when they sought it.

[031-027] They did look at him, but it was a look which he could not comprehend.

[031-028] There was a question and a statement in those deep eyes, and he could

[031-029] not understand what that question might be, nor what it was they sought

[031-030] to convey. Now and again one of the dogs turned a head in full flight,

[031-031] and stared, not at Fionn, but distantly backwards, over the spreading

[031-032] and swelling plain where their companions of the hunt had disappeared.

[031-033] “They are looking for the other hounds,” said Fionn.

[031-034] “And yet they do not give tongue! Tongue it, a Vran!” he shouted, “Bell

[031-035] it out, a Heo’lan!”

[031-036] It was then they looked at him, the look which he could not understand

[031-037] and had never seen on a chase. They did not tongue it, nor bell it, but

[031-038] they added silence to silence and speed to speed, until the lean grey

[031-039] bodies were one pucker and lashing of movement.

[031-040] Fionn marvelled. “They do not want the other dogs to hear or to come on

[031-041] this chase,” he murmured, and he wondered what might be passing within

[031-042] those slender heads.

[031-043] “The fawn runs well,” his thought continued. “What is it, a Vran, my

[031-044] heart? After her, a Heo’lan! Hist and away, my loves!”

[031-045] “There is going and to spare in that beast yet,” his mind went on. “She

[031-046] is not stretched to the full, nor half stretched. She may outrun even

[031-047] Bran,” he thought ragingly.

[031-048] They were racing through a smooth valley in a steady, beautiful, speedy

[031-049] flight when, suddenly, the fawn stopped and lay on the grass, and it lay

[031-050] with the calm of an animal that has no fear, and the leisure of one that

[031-051] is not pressed.

[031-052] “Here is a change,” said Fionn, staring in astonishment.

[031-053] “She is not winded,” he said. “What is she lying down for?” But Bran and

[031-054] Sceo’lan did not stop; they added another inch to their long-stretched

[031-055] easy bodies, and came up on the fawn.

[031-056] “It is an easy kill,” said Fionn regretfully. “They have her,” he cried.

[031-057] But he was again astonished, for the dogs did not kill. They leaped and

[031-058] played about the fawn, licking its face, and rubbing delighted noses

[031-059] against its neck.

[031-060] Fionn came up then. His long spear was lowered in his fist at the

[031-061] thrust, and his sharp knife was in its sheath, but he did not use them,

[031-062] for the fawn and the two hounds began to play round him, and the fawn

[031-063] was as affectionate towards him as the hounds were; so that when a

[031-064] velvet nose was thrust in his palm, it was as often a fawn’s muzzle as a

[031-065] hound’s.

[031-066] In that joyous company he came to [[allen|wide Allen of Leinster]], where the

[031-067] people were surprised to see the hounds and the fawn and the Chief and

[031-068] none other of the hunters that had set out with them.

[031-069] When the others reached home, the Chief told of his chase, and it was

[031-070] agreed that such a fawn must not be killed, but that it should be kept

[031-071] and well treated, and that it should be the pet fawn of the Fianna.

[031-072] But some of those who remembered Brah’s parentage thought that as Bran

[031-073] herself had come from the Shi so this fawn might have come out of the

[031-074] Shi also.
