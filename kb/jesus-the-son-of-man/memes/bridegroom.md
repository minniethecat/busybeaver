## Overview

The image of Jesus as bridegroom runs through *Jesus the Son of Man* as one of its most joyous Christological metaphors. Ephraim of Jericho, recalling the wedding at Cana, presents Jesus not as the suffering servant or the austere teacher but as a figure of celebration — the one whose very presence turns water into wine and ordinary gatherings into feasts. (043-001)(043-002)

## The Wedding at Cana

Ephraim's testimony centers on the first miracle of Jesus's ministry:

> He was the bridegroom at Cana, though no one knew it.

The claim is startling: Jesus is not merely a guest at the wedding but its true bridegroom. (043-002) Ephraim reads the miracle of water-into-wine as an epiphany — the bridegroom is revealed in his act of giving, even as he remains unrecognized by most of the guests. Rafca, the bride of Cana, recalls Jesus at her wedding, eating and drinking and smiling upon the guests. (009-014) When the wine ran out, the father of the bridegroom was told: "You have kept the best wine till the end of the feast." (009-023)

## The Joyful Christ

The bridegroom metaphor restores a dimension of Jesus often lost in more somber portrayals: joy. John the Baptist, speaking to one of his disciples, compares himself to the friend of the bridegroom who "rejoices greatly at the bridegroom's voice" (John 3:29). Gibran picks up this thread and weaves it through his portrait — Jesus is not only the one who suffers and dies but the one who dances at weddings.

## Gibran's Distinctive Emphasis

Where orthodox Christology tends to prioritize the crucified Christ, Gibran gives equal weight to the festive Christ. The bridegroom image balances the book's passion narrative with a counter-narrative of abundance, joy, and transformation. Just as bread and wine signify self-giving, the bridegroom signifies self-sharing — love that overflows.
