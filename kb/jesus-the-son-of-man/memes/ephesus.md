Ephesus provides the dramatic setting for one of the book's seventy-nine
testimonies: Bartholomew delivers his defense of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s social reach
from this great city of the Roman province of Asia.

## Geography

Ephesus stood near the mouth of the Cayster River on the western coast of
Asia Minor, in what is now Turkey. It was one of the largest cities of
[[romans|the Roman Empire]], a major commercial port, and home to the Temple of
Artemis — one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient world. By Gibran's
time, the ruins of Ephesus were already a site of archaeological
pilgrimage.

## In the Narrative

Bartholomew speaks from Ephesus, addressing an audience that has heard
the accusation that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] "addressed His appeal to slaves and outcasts"
(055-006). From this great Hellenistic city, Bartholomew mounts a
rhetorical counterargument: the gospel reached the highborn men and women
of "[[antioch|Antioch]] and [[byzantium|Byzantium]] and Athens and [[rome|Rome]]" (055-006), not only the
lowly, and [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] "was not with the servant against his master; neither
was He with the master against his servant" (055-006).

The chapter closes with an Ephesian image: "Even now He is crowned every
day by the priestesses of Artemis in the secret places of her temple"
(055-011). The Temple of Artemis, the city's crowning glory, becomes the
unexpected site of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s ongoing coronation — the pagan priestesses
unwittingly honoring [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Nazarene]].

## Significance

Ephesus serves a double function in the text. As the physical location of
Bartholomew's speech, it grounds the testimony in a real city of the
empire. As the home of the Artemis cult, it supplies the closing image:
[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] being crowned in the very temple of a pagan goddess, suggesting
that His influence transcends the boundaries of religion and nation.

## Events

No narrative events concerning [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] take place in Ephesus — the city is
never visited by Him in the gospel accounts or in Gibran's text. It
serves purely as the apostolic setting for Bartholomew's post-resurrection
testimony.

## Quotation

Bartholomew, speaking from Ephesus, declares the stature of Jesus:

> If nobility lies in being protective, He was the noblest of all men. If freedom is in thought and word and action, He was the freest of all men. If high birth is in pride that yields only to love and in aloofness that is ever gentle and gracious, then He was of all men the highest born.
>
> (055-009)
