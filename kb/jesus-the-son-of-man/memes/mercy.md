# Mercy

## Definition

Mercy in Gibran's book is the active face of love — love responding to suffering and sin with healing rather than judgment. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s mercy is inseparable from his power: he heals the sick, raises the dead, and forgives the sinner in a single gesture of comprehensive compassion. His critics see this mercy as lawlessness — "they say that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] heeded not the law of Moses" (008-005) — but for those who receive it, it is the sign that God's kingdom has arrived.

## In the Text

Mercy manifests in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s encounters with the marginalized. He raises a widow's son, forgives a woman taken in adultery, heals the daughter of a Roman officer. Each act of mercy transgresses a social boundary — Jew and Gentile, righteous and sinner, clean and unclean. Joanna recalls that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] "loved children as they would be loved in faith and understanding" (008-002), and this tenderness toward the vulnerable is mercy's signature. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] does not merely feel compassion; he acts on it, and his actions transform lives. Andrew recalls how [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] raised a mourner: "[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] walked over to her and He raised her face to His face and He said to her: You are the mother of all these" (006-035).
Jesus's mercy transforms grief into joy in a single gesture:

> Then Jesus walked over to her and He raised her face to His face and He said to her: "You are the mother of all these. You weep for joy, and I will keep your tears in my memory."
>
> (006-035)

## Significance

Gibran presents mercy as the test of whether religion serves God or itself. When religious authorities condemn [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] for healing on the [[sabbath|Sabbath]] and forgiving without temple sacrifice, they reveal that their religion has become an end in itself. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s mercy exposes the hollowness of a piety that values rules over people. In the book's vision, mercy is not a softening of justice but its fulfillment — the recognition that every person deserves to be seen and loved, not judged.
