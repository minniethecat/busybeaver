## Overview
> Greek god of beauty and desire, woven into Gibran's syncretic tapestry of mythological and religious figures.

In *Jesus the Son of Man*, Gibran invokes Adonis among a constellation of classical and Near Eastern deities, reflecting his broader literary project of synthesizing diverse spiritual traditions.

Mannus the Pompeiian, writing to a Greek friend, laments that the people of Palestine "would discover another Adonis, a god slain in the forest, and they would celebrate his slaying" (053-010).

Annas the High Priest, in his hostile assessment of Jesus, groups Adonis with [[ashtarte|Ashtarte]] as deities of the "North Country" from which Jesus came — alien powers that "still claim power against Israel and the God of Israel" (069-003).

The Adonis reference thus serves a double function: it locates Jesus within a world of dying-and-rising gods familiar to the Hellenistic imagination, and it marks the distance between Galilean syncretism and Jerusalem orthodoxy.
