## Overview

In Kahlil Gibran's *Jesus the Son of Man*, demons appear as a brief but significant motif — the shadow-side of the book's dominant themes of love, forgiveness, and spiritual freedom. The most pointed reference comes from Joseph Surnamed Justus, who recalls that Jesus "cast out devils" (063-003), evoking the Gospel accounts where exorcism was a primary sign of Jesus's authority.

## The Gospel Background

The Synoptic Gospels present Jesus's exorcisms as demonstrations of his authority over the spiritual realm. The Pharisees accused him of casting out demons "by the prince of demons" (Matthew 12:24; Mark 3:22) — a controversy Gibran's character Joseph Justus alludes to directly:

> They said He cast out devils by Beelzebub, the prince of devils. (063-003)

This accusation — that Jesus's power was demonic rather than divine — was one of the most serious charges leveled against him, and it appears in all three Synoptic Gospels.

## Gibran's Treatment

Gibran does not dwell on the demonic. Unlike the Gospels, where exorcism stories occupy significant narrative space, *Jesus the Son of Man* treats demons primarily as a foil — the darkness against which Jesus's light is defined. The book's focus remains on positive transformation rather than combat with evil.

Joseph Justus's testimony reflects the perspective of a cautious observer: he reports the accusation without endorsing or refuting it, leaving the reader to judge. This ambiguity is characteristic of Gibran's approach — he presents multiple viewpoints without authorial judgment.

## Symbolic Reading

Given Gibran's symbolic and poetic sensibility, demons in the book can also be read psychologically: as the "demons" of doubt, fear, hatred, and spiritual blindness that Jesus's teaching addresses. Mary Magdalene speaks of being freed not from literal possession but from a life of empty sensuality (013-001–013-010). The true exorcism in Gibran's world is the transformation of the human heart.

## Relation to Gibran's Theology

If God is love and kinship, demons represent the antithesis: separation, accusation, and division. The Christ who casts out demons is the same Christ who declares that "in my kingdom there are no strangers" (040-006). The casting out of demons is therefore not merely a display of supernatural power but an act of reunification — restoring the possessed to community and to themselves.
