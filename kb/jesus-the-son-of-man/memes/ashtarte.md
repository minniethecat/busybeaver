## Overview
> Phoenician and Canaanite goddess of fertility, love, and war, invoked within Gibran's poetic synthesis of ancient Near Eastern spirituality.

Ashtarte appears among the array of ancient deities that Gibran weaves into the symbolic fabric of *[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]*. Her presence underscores the deep cultural and religious strata of the Levantine world in which the [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] story unfolds.

## In the Narrative

Ashtarte is invoked in three distinct contexts within Gibran's work. [[pontius-pilate|Pontius Pilatus]], reflecting on the Eastern cults that had captivated Roman women including his wife, recalls how "Greece was overcome and fell to dust when Ashtarte and her seven maidens came from the [[syria|Syrian]] shores" (054-003). For Pilatus, Ashtarte represents the seductive and corrosive power of Oriental religion threatening the Empire.

[[annas|Annas the High Priest]], in his hostile condemnation of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], groups Ashtarte with [[adonis|Adonis]] as deities of the "North Country" — alien gods from [[galilee|Galilee]] that "still claim power against Israel and the [[god|God]] of Israel" (069-003). This accusation positions Ashtarte worship as evidence of Galilean religious syncretism and impurity.

The most extended invocation comes from the Woman of Byblos, who opens her lament for [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] with a ritual cry to the goddess: "Weep with me, ye daughters of Ashtarte, and all ye lovers of [[tamouz|Tamouz]]" (077-001). Her elegy repeats this refrain four times, explicitly framing the death of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] within the ancient Near Eastern pattern of the dying-and-rising god — a motif associated with [[tamouz|Tamouz]] (Dumuzi), [[adonis|Adonis]], and other consorts of the great goddess.

## The Lament of the Woman of Byblos

[[woman-of-byblos|The Woman of Byblos]] channels her grief through the idiom of Ashtarte's cult:

> Weep with me, ye daughters of Ashtarte, and all ye lovers of Tamouz.
> Bid your heart melt and rise and run blood-tears,
> For He who was made of gold and ivory is no more.
>
> (077-001)

Her lament continues through twenty-four verses, interweaving the death of [[tamouz|Tamouz]] with the crucifixion of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], until the final revelation:

> I charge ye, daughters of Ashtarte, and all ye lovers of Tamouz,
> Bare your breasts and weep and comfort me,
> For Jesus of Nazareth is dead.
>
> (077-022)

## Symbolic Significance

In Gibran's syncretic vision, Ashtarte serves as a bridge between the old pagan world and the new [[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]]ian revelation. Her myths of love, death, and resurrection prefigure the Passion narrative, while her cult's geographic association with the North Country ([[syria|Syria]]-Phoenicia) reinforces the Galilean identity of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] as an outsider to [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] orthodoxy. By placing the ancient goddess's ritual lament in the mouth of a mourner for [[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]], Gibran suggests that all the old religions were, in their way, reaching toward the same mystery.
