## Overview

> The carpenter of Nazareth who served as the earthly father of Jesus, a figure of quiet dignity and humble devotion.

In *Jesus the Son of Man*, Gibran portrays Joseph through the lens of familial intimacy and human tenderness. His presence anchors Jesus's human origins and provides the domestic foundation from which the divine mission emerges.

## In the Narrative

Joseph appears through the recollections of Susannah of Nazareth, a neighbour of Mary. She remembers the young Mary "before she became the wife of Joseph the carpenter, when we were both girls together in the vineyards" (062-001). This glimpse of village life — Mary and Susannah as girls among the vines, before marriage and motherhood — is one of the few domestic scenes in Gibran's narrative.

Susannah recalls later that "Mary was betrothed to Joseph the carpenter" (062-008), framing the marriage as a known event in the village's collective memory. Joseph himself never speaks in the book; he is present only through the recollections of others, a quiet figure whose carpentry workshop in Nazareth formed the earthly cradle of the Christian story.

## Symbolic Significance

In Gibran's hands, Joseph the carpenter becomes more than a background figure. His trade — working with wood, shaping raw material into useful forms — mirrors the divine act of creation and foreshadows the cross. His silence in the narrative reflects a humility that Gibran presents as the proper human response to proximity with the divine. Joseph's workshop in Nazareth is the hidden origin point from which the public ministry would later emerge.
