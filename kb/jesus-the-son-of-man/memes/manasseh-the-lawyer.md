> A Jerusalem lawyer who admired Jesus "as a man rather than as a leader" — the voice of the educated professional who could appreciate but not follow.

Manasseh's testimony [050-001–007] is brief but distinctive: it is the voice of a member of the Jerusalem professional class who encountered Jesus, recognized something remarkable, yet remained at a distance.

## Testimony

"I used to hear Him speak," Manasseh begins. "There was always a ready word upon His lips" (050-001). His admiration is genuine but qualified: "I admired Him as a man rather than as a leader" (050-002).

Manasseh acknowledges the force of Jesus's words while holding back from discipleship — a position that distinguishes him both from the committed apostles and from the hostile priests. He represents the educated, sympathetic observer who sees value in Jesus but cannot abandon his professional identity to follow.

## Significance

Manasseh occupies a liminal position in the book's spectrum of witnesses: neither disciple nor enemy, he is the bystander who understood enough to be moved but not enough to be changed. His presence in the narrative reminds readers that Jesus's audience included not only the poor and the outcast but also the educated elite — and that education could be as much a barrier as an aid to discipleship.

Manasseh's admiration is aesthetic rather than spiritual — charmed but never convinced:

> I was taken by His voice and His gestures, but not by the substance of His speech. He charmed me but never convinced me; for He was too vague, too distant and obscure to reach my mind.
>
> I have known other men like Him. They are never constant nor are they consistent. It is with eloquence not with principles that they hold your ear and your passing thought, but never the core of your heart.
>
> (050-003)
