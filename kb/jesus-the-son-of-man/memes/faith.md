# Faith

## Definition

In *[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]*, faith is the quality of trust that enables a person to follow [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] beyond the evidence of the senses. It is not the absence of doubt but its companion — as [[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas the Doubter]] learns: "[[doubt|Doubt]] is a pain too lonely to know that faith is his twin brother" (026-010). Faith in Gibran's portrayal is relational rather than doctrinal: it is trust in a person, not assent to propositions.

## In the Text

Faith appears most vividly in the disciples' varied responses to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]. Andrew's faith is described as deep and quiet: "My brother Andrew was a shy man, but his faith in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] was deeper than my faith" (006-020). This suggests that faith has depths invisible to others — that the quietest believer may have the strongest trust. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] himself tests and honors faith: he tells the rich merchant, "The gain shall be yours, for you have done well, and have been faithful to me and to yourself" (044-010), linking faithfulness to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] with faithfulness to one's own best self.
Thomas the Doubter gives the book's most memorable image of faith:

> Doubt is a pain too lonely to know that faith is his twin brother.
>
> (026-010)

## Significance

Gibran presents faith as a bridge between human limitation and divine possibility. It is the capacity to trust when certainty is unavailable — to follow [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] even when, like [[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas]], one "listened immovable" while others "were swayed like branches in the wind." Faith is not gullibility but the courage to act on insufficient evidence, and the book suggests that this courage is what [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] most desires from his followers.
