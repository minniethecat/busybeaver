Rome is the imperial capital that held political dominion over [[judea|Judea]] during
the life of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]. In the narrative, Rome appears primarily through the
voices of those entangled with its power — [[caiaphas|the High Priest]] [[caiaphas|Caiaphas]], the
Roman prefect [[pontius-pilate|Pontius Pilate]], and those who saw the empire's shadow over
[[galilee|Galilee]] and [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]].

## Geography

Rome is situated in Italy, across the Mediterranean Sea from [[judea|Judea]].
It was the seat of [[romans|the Roman Empire]], whose legions and governors
extended their authority to the far reaches of the known world.
The centurion Claudius speaks of serving "Caesar and Rome" (073-011),
and [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]]'s soldiers are described as "soldiers of Rome" (054-037).
In the vision of Jotham of [[nazareth|Nazareth]], Rome is destined to become
"a white skeleton in the desert" (042-012), its grandeur reduced to dust.

## In the Narrative

Rome is invoked throughout the text as the ultimate symbol of earthly
dominion. [[caiaphas|Caiaphas]] declares that "the kingdom of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] must needs
be protected by Rome" (007-001) and accuses [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] of being
"defiant to us and to Rome" (007-002). [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]], though conflicted,
ultimately acts "for the sake of Rome" (054-041) in condemning [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]].
The Persian philosopher in [[damascus|Damascus]] observes that "the gods of Greece
and Rome are vanishing into their own twilight" (010-011), placing the
empire's spiritual decline alongside its temporal reach.

For Jotham of [[nazareth|Nazareth]], Rome is the place of worldly comfort that he
rejects in favor of following [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]: he would rather be "stoned by them
rather than stay in Rome and be blest and happy" (042-002).
He prophesies that "Rome must needs fall into the dust" (042-013),
and yet acknowledges, "if Rome, when humbled and made low" (042-014),
there may yet be redemption.

In the house of [[pontius-pilate|Pontius Pilate]], the prefect reflects that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is
"a man greater than even Rome herself" (054-008). [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]]'s wife,
writing to a Roman lady, asserts that "Rome must needs prevail" (054-048),
yet her letter betrays a deeper unease with the empire's mission.

## Significance

Rome in Gibran's narrative serves as the counterpoint to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s kingdom
— the one built on legions and decrees, the other on spirit and word.
Benjamin the Scribe accuses [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] of being "the enemy of Rome and [[judea|Judea]]"
(046-001), conflating the empire's political order with the religious
establishment. The irony is that Rome, for all its might, cannot
comprehend the man standing before [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]]. As the man from [[lebanon|Lebanon]]
observes, "[[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] holds the basin and Rome the ewer" (079-048),
both serving a purpose in a drama neither fully controls.

## Events

While no specific miracles or parables are set in Rome itself, the
empire's presence shapes every political event in the narrative. The
trial before [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]] [054-001 through 054-048] is the most sustained
engagement with Roman authority. Claudius the centurion, writing after
the crucifixion, reflects on those who "are serving Caesar and Rome"
(073-011), and Bartholomew in [[ephesus|Ephesus]] imagines a voice that could hold
"Rome" among the great cities of the world (055-006).
