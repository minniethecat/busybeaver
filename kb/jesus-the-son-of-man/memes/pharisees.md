# Pharisees

## Identity

The Pharisees are a Jewish religious sect known for their meticulous adherence to the Law of Moses and the oral traditions of the elders. In first-century [[judea|Judea]] they wield considerable influence over public piety and religious instruction, their scribes serving as the authoritative interpreters of scripture. In Gibran's telling, they are less a historical institution than a moral archetype: the guardians of orthodoxy who mistake the letter of the law for its spirit.

## Role in the Narrative

The Pharisees appear from the very first scene. James the son of Zebedee recalls how [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], speaking in the marketplace of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], "accused the scribes and the Pharisees of setting snares and digging pitfalls in the path of those who long after the kingdom" (001-002). The accusation is immediate and unforgiving — the Pharisees are not merely mistaken; they are actively obstructing the way to the divine. The crowd's reaction is equally swift: "a company of men who defended the Pharisees and the scribes" sought to lay hands upon [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] (001-003).

[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] does not, however, present himself as the destroyer of Pharisaic tradition. To Matthew he explains: "Think not I came to destroy the laws of the scribes and the Pharisees; for my days among you are numbered and my words are counted, and I have but hours in which to fulfil another law and reveal a new covenant" (013-018). The distinction is crucial — [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] positions himself not against the law itself but as its fulfiller, completing what the Pharisees have only preserved.

Joseph of Arimathea records a darker exchange, in which [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] anticipates his own death at the Pharisees' instigation: "The scribes and the Pharisees say the earth is thirsty for my blood. I would quench the thirst of the earth with my blood" (019-037). The image is at once accusatory and redemptive — the Pharisees' thirst for blood becomes, in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s vision, a cosmic necessity.

Andrew recounts a vivid confrontation: "the Pharisees brought into the midst of the crowd a woman whom they called a harlot" (057-013), testing [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] with the law of adultery. The scene crystallizes the Pharisees' narrative function: they are the prosecutors who, by forcing a legal crisis, inadvertently reveal the mercy that transcends law.

## Significance

The Pharisees embody the perennial tension between institutional religion and prophetic renewal. They are not caricatures of evil but custodians of a tradition they cannot see beyond — learned, zealous, and tragically certain. Their opposition to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is the necessary friction from which the new covenant emerges. Without the Pharisees' challenge, [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s teachings on mercy, forgiveness, and the spirit of the law would lack their dramatic context and moral force.

## Related Organizations

The Pharisees are closely allied with the **[[sanhedrin|Sanhedrin]]**, the supreme council of which Pharisaic scribes are often members. Together, Pharisees and [[sanhedrin|Sanhedrin]] represent the two faces of institutional opposition: doctrinal (Pharisees) and political ([[sanhedrin|Sanhedrin]]). The **scribes**, though not a formal organization, function as the scholarly arm of the Pharisaic movement, and [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] frequently pairs them in his denunciations.
