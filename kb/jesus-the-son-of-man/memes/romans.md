# Romans

## Identity

The Romans in *[[titlepage|Jesus the Son of Man]]* are the imperial power governing Palestine — soldiers, administrators, governors, and the vast political apparatus of the Empire. They are the occupying force against which Jewish national hopes strained, and they represent the worldly power that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s kingdom of [[love]] both refused and transcended. Unlike the [[pharisees|Pharisees]] or [[sanhedrin|Sanhedrin]], the Romans are not a religious sect but a political order — their concern is not theological orthodoxy but civic stability. This makes their relationship to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] different in kind from that of the priestly authorities: they are perplexed rather than threatened, pragmatic rather than zealous.

## Role in the Narrative

The Romans appear throughout the book in multiple roles — as soldiers, as governors, as the cultural "other" against which both Jew and [[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]]ian define themselves. [[james-the-son-of-zebedee|James the Son of Zebedee]] opens the book with a political context: the crowd urges [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] to lead a revolt against [[rome|Rome]]. But [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] refuses: "[[simon-peter|Simon]] shall prevail against the Romans. If you will be the king of the Jews" (001-015) — and when [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] declines this political kingship, the seeds of [[judas-iscariot|Judas]]'s disillusionment are sown.

The central Roman figure is [[pontius-pilatus|Pontius Pilate]], the governor who orders [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s crucifixion despite finding no fault in him. [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]]'s chapter reveals a man caught between justice and expediency: "It is always wise for the governor of a Roman province not to be intolerant of the religious beliefs of a conquered race" (054-040). He is neither villain nor hero but a functionary — a man who knows the right thing and does the wrong thing because it is easier.

> It is always wise for the governor of a Roman province not to be intolerant of the religious beliefs of a conquered race. (054-040)

Other Roman voices complicate the picture. [[claudius-a-roman-centurion|Claudius the Centurion]] is a soldier stationed in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] who cannot forget the man he helped crucify — "All was well with my battalions in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]; and that night I visited the place of the skull" (073-003). [[mannus-the-pompeiian-to-a-greek|Mannus the Pompeiian]], a Roman writing to a Greek friend, reflects on the philosophical puzzle of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]: "While we Romans build marble temples to our gods, this man's temple is built in the hearts of men" (053-003). [[jotham-of-nazareth-to-a-roman|Jotham of Nazareth]] challenges a Roman to understand a man unlike any the Empire has encountered: "Do you then understand [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus of Nazareth]], a man simple and alone, who came without arms or army?" (042-004).

[[cyborea-the-mother-of-judas|Cyborea]], the mother of [[judas-iscariot|Judas]], reveals the atmosphere of occupation: "I hated our enemies, the cursèd Romans, who wear purple cloth though they were born in stables" (076-001). Her son [[judas-iscariot|Judas]] shot arrows at the Roman legions (076-002) — a detail that roots his betrayal of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] in disappointed nationalist hope. [[judas-iscariot|Judas]] wanted a liberator from [[rome|Rome]]; [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] offered a liberation of a different kind.

## Significance

The Romans in Gibran's book represent the limit case of worldly power — the empire that can kill the body but cannot touch the spirit. They are not, for the most part, evil in motive; they are functional. [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]] does not hate [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]; he simply finds it inconvenient to save him. The soldiers who nail him to [[crucifixion|the cross]] are doing their job. This makes them, in some ways, more disturbing than the zealously hostile priests — evil committed with a clean conscience is harder to recognize as evil.

Yet Gibran does not treat the Romans as a monolith. Roman voices in the book — [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]], Claudius, Mannus — are among the most reflective, and their encounter with [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] leaves them changed. The centurion who cannot forget, the governor who washes his hands but cannot wash away the memory, the philosopher who sees in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] a truth his marble temples cannot contain — these suggest that even the empire is permeable to the kingdom of [[love]]. The Romans killed [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], but they could not kill what he awakened.

> While we Romans build marble temples to our gods, this man's temple is built in the hearts of men. (053-003)

The political question that runs through the book — "who killed [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], the Romans or the priests?" — is answered by [[jonathan|Jonathan]]: "Neither the Romans slew Him, nor the priests. The whole world slew Him" (047-018). The Romans are not uniquely guilty; they are uniquely representative. They stand for every system of power that crucifies [[truth]] in the name of order.
