# Sacrifice

## Definition

Sacrifice in *[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]* is the logic of [[crucifixion|the cross]] — the giving of one life for another, whether willingly ([[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]) or unwillingly ([[barabbas-the-insurgent|Barabbas]]). It is the book's answer to the problem of how love operates in a world of violence: not by meeting violence with more violence but by absorbing it. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s death is a sacrifice not because it satisfies divine anger but because it reveals divine love — a love that gives itself completely and asks only to be remembered.

## In the Text

The theme of sacrifice crystallizes in the figure of [[barabbas-the-insurgent|Barabbas]]. Released in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s place, he becomes the unwilling beneficiary of a sacrifice he did not choose: "They released me and chose Him. Then He rose and I fell down" (072-001). His freedom is a gift he cannot refuse and cannot bear. He follows the crowd to [[crucifixion|Golgotha]], a living man walking to his own grave: "I should have fled to the desert, where shame is burned out by the sun" (072-004). The shepherd to whom [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] says "From this day you shall love this sheep more than any other in your flock, for she was lost and now she is found" (017-014) learns that sacrifice transforms the ordinary into the sacred.

## Significance

Gibran's treatment of sacrifice is distinctive: he does not present it as a transaction ([[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] paying a debt to God) but as a revelation ([[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] showing what love looks like when it costs everything). The witnesses who understand the sacrifice are not the theologians but the broken — [[barabbas-the-insurgent|Barabbas]], [[mary-mother-of-jesus|Mary]] Magdalene, the shepherd. Sacrifice is not explained; it is undergone, and its meaning unfolds in the lives of those it touches.

## Quotation

Zacchaeus reflects on what it means to give to Jesus:

> His roots are in God and the depths of this earth; and to Him it was not sacrifice but rather gain.
>
> (047-011)


Barabbas, freed in Jesus's place, experiences vicarious sacrifice as living death:

> I was freed from my chains, and walked with the throng behind Him, but I was a living man going to my own grave.
>
> (072-003)
