The Crucifixion is the gravitational center of Gibran's *[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] the Son of
Man*. Though never narrated directly as a single continuous account, it is
invoked by witness after witness — [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]], Annas, [[barabbas-the-insurgent|Barabbas]], [[simon-peter|Simon]] of
Cyrene, and others — each refracting the event through a distinct moral
lens. The cumulative effect is polyphonic: the execution is at once a
judicial murder, a cosmic paradox, and the moment that transforms every
speaker who touches it.

## The Trial and Sentence

[[pontius-pilate|Pontius Pilatus]], the Roman governor of [[judea|Judea]], provides the most extended
account of the legal proceedings. Summoned before him, [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] stands
silent while the crowd outside demands His death. [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]] goes out to
address them: "What would you with this man?" And they shout "as if with
one throat: 'We would crucify Him. He is our enemy and the enemy of
[[rome|Rome]]'" (054-013). When [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]] brings [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] out onto the palace steps, the
crowd roars "like the roaring thunder," and [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]] hears "naught save
'Crucify Him, crucify Him!'" (054-036).

[[pontius-pilate|Pilate]] yields [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] to the priests, decreeing the titulus above the cross
to read "[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus of Nazareth]], King of the Jews" — a title he later reflects
should have read "[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus of Nazareth]], a King" (054-036). He acknowledges
that "it would have been within my power to save Him, but saving Him
would have caused a revolution" (054-036). The governor's testimony is a
confession dressed as a political defense: he knew the man was innocent
and condemned Him anyway, for the sake of [[rome|Rome]].

## The Witness of the High Priest

Annas [[caiaphas|the High Priest]] speaks with chilling candor: "I had Him crucified,
and His crucifixion was a signal and warning unto those others who are
possessed by the same demon" (069-009). Where [[pontius-pilate|Pilate]] offers regret
disguised as realpolitik, Annas offers conviction — the crucifixion as a
deliberate act of institutional power meant to terrify imitators.

## Barabbas and the Exchange

[[barabbas-the-insurgent|Barabbas]], the thief released in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s place, testifies to the
paradoxical grace of the event. He speaks of "beseeching forgiveness for
the crucifixion of this one man" (072-019), and observes that "His
crucifixion endured but for an hour" (072-025) — a span of suffering
dwarfed by the eternal weight it carried.

## Simon of Cyrene

[[simon-peter|Simon]], compelled to carry the crossbeam, recalls the ascent: "there they
were to crucify Him" (075-012). His testimony anchors the event in
physical detail — the hilltop, the wood, the procession — among so many
other voices that render it in theological or political terms.

## The Cosmic Frame

The final chapter of the book, spoken by A Man from [[lebanon|Lebanon]], places the
[[death|Crucifixion]] in a metaphysical frame: "not crucified with the great
crucifixion" (079-115) — distinguishing the one historical death from a
larger, ongoing pattern of divine suffering that, in Gibran's vision, the
Nazarene both embodies and transcends.

## Significance

Gibran's treatment of the [[death|Crucifixion]] reverses the gospel convention.
There is no single authoritative account. Instead seven decades of
witnesses — the powerful and the powerless, the guilty and the absolved —
circle the event, each confessing their own role in it. The Crucifixion
becomes less a past event than a moral mirror: every speaker who
describes it reveals more about themselves than about the man on the
cross.
