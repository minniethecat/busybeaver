## Overview

The soul — the inner core of human identity — is the primary arena of Gibran's spirituality. In *Jesus the Son of Man*, it is not institutions, rituals, or doctrines that matter but the condition of the soul. Joseph of Arimathea speaks of "two streams" flowing in Jesus, both reaching toward the soul's deepest longing (039-002).

## The Divine Spark

Gibran consistently treats the soul as bearing a divine spark — not identical with God but kin to God. John the Son of Zebedee expresses this vision most fully:

> The Christ is the flame of God that dwells in the spirit of man. (014-003)

The soul is not saved from outside; it is awakened from within. Jesus does not confer something the soul lacks but reveals something it already possesses.

## Soul-Making

Gibran's anthropology is closer to the Romantic tradition of "soul-making" than to orthodox Christian soteriology. The soul is not a fixed substance but a capacity that grows through encounter, suffering, and love. Mary Magdalene's three testimonies trace the arc of a soul in formation — from infatuation to awe to mature, articulate faith.

## The Two Streams

Joseph of Arimathea's image of "two streams" — one of kinship with God, one of rapture called the Kingdom of Heaven — captures the soul's dual orientation. The soul reaches both upward (toward God) and outward (toward the world). Health consists in keeping both streams flowing; sickness is the blockage of either.

For Gibran, the ultimate question is not "What must I believe?" but "What is the state of my soul?"

The encounter with Jesus stirs the soul at its deepest level:

> And I gazed at Him, and my soul quivered within me, for He was beautiful.
>
> (004-008)
