Lebanon is the mountainous region to the north of [[galilee|Galilee]], celebrated
in the biblical and poetic imagination for its cedars, snow-capped peaks,
and fertile slopes. In Gibran's narrative, Lebanon provides the natural
imagery that suffuses the text — a landscape of majesty and purity that
frames the spiritual drama unfolding to the south.

## Geography

Lebanon is defined by its mountains and their "slopes" (019-008),
stretching from the Mediterranean coast to the high peaks. James the
Son of Zebedee evokes "the snows of Lebanon" descending to the valleys
(001-006), and Salome compares a beloved presence to "snow upon
Lebanon" (022-025). The cedars of Lebanon are referenced by Rumanous
the Greek poet as one of the wonders his song cannot surpass (029-003).
Jonathan recalls being upon "the hills of Lebanon" with the sweet
waters about them (048-001).

## In the Narrative

Lebanon is home to several of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s followers and a source of the
region's richest natural poetry. [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] speaks of "the slopes
of Lebanon where the white lilies are heedless of the caravans" (019-008),
contrasting the timeless landscape with human affairs. The shepherds
and disciples from Lebanon are described as men "from [[galilee|Galilee]] and from
the slopes of Lebanon" (047-005).

The region's produce is woven into the sensory texture of the narrative.
Cyborea remembers putting away her son's first sandals when she was
"fresh then like the grapes of Lebanon" (076-006). Susannah of [[nazareth|Nazareth]]
invokes "the snow of Lebanon" as a possible shroud (062-074), its
whiteness both a blessing and a portent. Levi recalls [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] speaking
of "the rocky heights of Lebanon" (030-013) as a metaphor for spiritual
ascent.

## Significance

Lebanon in this narrative serves as a poetic benchmark — its cedars,
snows, and slopes are the measure of natural beauty against which
spiritual truth is articulated. Bartholomew in [[ephesus|Ephesus]] observes that
"the proud princes of Lebanon and Armenia have forgotten" something
the humblest disciple now knows (055-005), drawing a contrast between
worldly elevation and spiritual insight. The region embodies a kind of
pristine witness to the events in [[galilee|Galilee]] and [[judea|Judea]] — distant enough
to remain untouched, close enough to provide disciples and imagery.

## Events

No specific miracle or confrontation is set in Lebanon itself, but
its landscape provides the backdrop for the gathering of disciples
and the imagery of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s teaching. The "shepherd in South Lebanon"
is among the early witnesses, and the region's sons are counted
among those who followed [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] south to [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]].
