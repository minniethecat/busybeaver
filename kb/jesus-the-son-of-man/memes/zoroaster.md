## Overview

> The prophet of ancient Persia whose teachings on cosmic dualism and moral choice shaped the spiritual landscape of the Near East.

Gibran references Zoroaster among the great spiritual teachers whose wisdom he weaves into the philosophical fabric of *Jesus the Son of Man*. The Persian sage's influence on concepts of light, darkness, and moral struggle resonates with themes Gibran explores throughout the work.

## In the Narrative

Zoroaster is invoked twice in the book. John the Son of Zebedee, in a sweeping catalogue of ancient spiritual traditions that prefigured Jesus, places him alongside Mithra: "the prophet of Mithra the king, and of Zoroaster the prophet of the Persians" (014-013). This grouping positions Zoroaster among the great forerunners whose teachings anticipated elements of the Christian revelation.

The Persian Philosopher in Damascus offers a more personal testimony. Speaking as a follower of the ancient Persian faith, he declares his contentment with his own tradition while acknowledging the power of Jesus's vision:

> But my God is the God of Zoroaster, the God who is the sun in the sky and fire upon the earth and light in the bosom of man. And I am content. I need no other God.
>
> (010-015)

This quiet fidelity to the Zoroastrian God — presented without hostility to Jesus — exemplifies Gibran's inclusive spiritual vision, where diverse traditions coexist in mutual recognition rather than competition.

## Symbolic Significance

In Gibran's syncretic framework, Zoroaster represents the ancient wisdom of Persia — a tradition centered on light, fire, and the cosmic struggle between good and evil. The Zoroastrian concept of light as divine presence resonates with Jesus's own declaration "I am the light of the world," and Gibran seems to suggest that the Persian prophet's insight was a genuine, if partial, apprehension of the same truth that Jesus embodied fully.
