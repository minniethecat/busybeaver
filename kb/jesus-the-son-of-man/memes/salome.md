> A woman disciple whose epistle transforms Jesus into a cascade of images — poplars in the sun, a lake among hills, snow upon the mountains.

Salome's testimony [022-001–052] is among the longest and most poetic in the book. Addressed "to a Woman Friend," it is a sustained act of praise in which Jesus is rendered through simile after simile drawn from the natural world of [[galilee|Galilee]] and [[lebanon|Lebanon]].

## Testimony

Salome describes Jesus as "like poplars shimmering in the sun," "like a lake among the lonely hills," "like snow upon the mountains," and "like pearls in the sunlight" [022-001–004]. Her imagery is drawn from the landscape — trees, water, mountains, light — rather than from scripture or theology.

Later she shifts to more intimate figures: Jesus is "a mother who hushes her own sorrow to share in the joy of her child" (022-021), and one who "loved children as they would be loved in faith and understanding" (022-030).

Salome's testimony opens with a lyrical confession of love and fear:

> He was like poplars shimmering in the sun;
>
> And like a lake among the lonely hills,
>
> Shining in the sun;
>
> And like snow upon the mountain heights,
>
> White, white in the sun.
>
> (022-001)

## Significance

Salome's voice is important for what it is not: it is not doctrinal, not narrative, not argumentative. It is pure lyric — a mode of testimony that Gibran himself practiced as a poet. In her, the book achieves one of its highest expressions of the devotional imagination, seeing Jesus through the transfigured lens of love and landscape.
