> Brother of Simon Peter, apostle, whose single chapter is a hymn of loss — "the bitterness of death is less bitter than life without Him."

Andrew's testimony [057-001–025] is a sustained poetic lament. Unlike his brother [[simon-peter|Simon Peter]], whose voice is dominant and action-oriented, Andrew speaks from the void left by Jesus's death.

## Testimony

"Death is less bitter than life without Him," Andrew begins (057-001). His testimony is not narrative but lyrical — a recollection of Jesus's voice, his words, the echo in memory.

The chapter is structured around absence. Andrew's Jesus is not the miracle-worker or the disputant with Pharisees but the voice that once filled the silence and now leaves only its resonance.

Andrew recalls how Jesus's words disturbed the comfortable: "He would say, 'The [[pharisees|Pharisee]] is like a man who stands in clear water and yet is thirsty'" (057-008).

Andrew recalls Jesus's teaching on the natural world and divine providence:

> Once I heard Him say: "Go forth in your longing to the fields, and sit by the lilies, and you shall hear them humming in the sun. They weave not cloth for raiment, nor do they raise wood or stone for shelter; yet they sing.
>
> "He who works in the night fulfils their needs and the dew of His grace is upon their petals.
>
> "And are not you also His care who never wearies nor rests?"
>
> (057-002)

## Significance

Andrew's chapter represents the interior, contemplative response to Jesus — grief that does not seek action but absorption in memory. He balances the book's more active witnesses, contributing the stillness of loss to Gibran's polyphonic portrait.
