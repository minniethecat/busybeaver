## Overview

> Roman emperor whose political dominion forms the imperial backdrop to the life and ministry of Jesus.

In *Jesus the Son of Man*, Caesar stands as the embodiment of earthly power and imperial authority, the political reality against which the spiritual kingdom proclaimed by Jesus is implicitly contrasted. Gibran uses this figure to explore the tension between temporal rule and divine sovereignty.

## In the Narrative

Caesar appears as the ultimate symbol of Roman political authority against which Jesus's spiritual kingship is measured. When Jesus is brought before Pilatus, the crowd weaponizes their loyalty to Caesar against Him: "We will have no king but Caesar" (054-014). Pilatus himself, confronting the Nazarene, experiences a moment of awe: "I felt as if Caesar had entered the Hall, a man greater than even Rome herself" (054-008).

The opposition between Caesar's kingdom and Jesus's kingdom is drawn most sharply in the trial scene. Jesus is accused of claiming kingship, and the priests frame this as sedition against Caesar's authority. The political charge — that Jesus called Himself a king — is what ultimately secures the death sentence, though Pilatus himself is unconvinced.

## A Greater Commander

The centurion Claudius, who guarded Jesus on the night before the crucifixion, offers the most remarkable assessment. Reflecting thirty years later, the old soldier compares the Galilean to Rome's greatest generals:

> I think truly that neither Pompey nor Caesar was so great a commander as that Man of Galilee.
>
> (073-013)

Claudius elaborates that "since His unresisting death an army has risen out of the earth to fight for Him … And He is better served by them, though dead, than ever Pompey or Caesar was served, though living" (073-014). This judgment — from a Roman officer who served both Caesar and the Empire — is perhaps the most subversive political statement in the entire book.

## Render unto Caesar

The famous Gospel teaching is alluded to by Nicodemus the Poet, who recalls that Jesus "said also, 'Render unto Caesar that which is Caesar's'" (038-015). A man outside Jerusalem echoes this in his regret: if Jesus "would even have rendered unto Caesar that which is Caesar's, then He would not have been slain" (067-010). These references reinforce Caesar as the fixed point of temporal obligation against which the claims of the spirit must be negotiated.
