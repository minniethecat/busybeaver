# Son of Man

## Definition

"Son of Man" is the title [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] uses most consistently to refer to himself throughout Gibran's narrative. Unlike "[[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]]" or "Messiah," which carry fixed theological expectations, "Son of Man" holds a deliberate ambiguity — it points to one who is fully human yet invested with a mission that transcends the human. It is at once the humblest and the most exalted of his self-designations: a title of incarnation, not coronation.

## In the Text

The phrase first appears in the opening chapter, where [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], challenged in the marketplace, answers with a stark image of homelessness: "the foxes shall have their holes, and the birds of the air their nests, but [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]] has not where on earth to lay his head" (001-041). The title here is bound not to glory but to dispossession — [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]] belongs nowhere, and therefore everywhere.

John the son of Zebedee explores the title's meaning most directly. He notes the variety of names applied to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] — "[[jesus-of-nazareth|the Nazarene]], and still others [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]" (014-001) — and asks: "Now you would know why some of us call Him [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]" (014-023). His answer is deceptively simple: "The Son of Man was [[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]] the Gracious, who would be with us all" (014-025). The title's power lies not in doctrinal precision but in presence — [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]] is the one who stays.

[[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas]] speaks of [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]] as the dividing line between death and life: "The dead in me buried their dead; and the living shall live for the Anointed King, even for Him who was [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]" (026-015). For [[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas]] the skeptic, the title marks the boundary between his former self and the one awakened by encounter.

Andrew records [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] using the title in a moment of unexpected mercy. When the woman taken in adultery is brought before him, [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] says: "if it is in your desire to be wise even as you are loving, then seek me; for [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]] will not judge you" (057-021). The title here is linked to refusal of judgment — [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]'s authority is exercised as release, not condemnation.

James the brother of the Lord preserves the most intimate cluster of uses. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] tells his followers: "The Son of Man who has chosen you for His brethren" (074-013), redefining the title as a bond of kinship. He adds: "It is in your longing that you shall find [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]. For longing is the fountainhead of ecstasy, and it is the path to the Father" (074-028). The Son of Man is not found through doctrine but through desire — he is the object of a hunger that itself becomes the way.

## Significance

The title "Son of Man" functions in Gibran's book as the bridge between [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s humanity and his mystery. It refuses to settle into either category alone. When [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] calls himself [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]], he locates himself among the homeless, the longing, and the judged — and simultaneously claims an authority that transcends them all. This tension is the theological heart of Gibran's portrait: the divine is not above the human but woven through it, and the most exalted title is the one that names our shared condition.
