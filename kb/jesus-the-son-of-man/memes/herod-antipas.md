## Herod Antipas

Herod Antipas was the tetrarch of [[galilee|Galilee]] and Perea during the time of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] and [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]]. He appears in the narrative chiefly through [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s words after the death of [[john-the-baptist|John]], spoken with pity rather than condemnation (032-010).

## Historical Context

Herod Antipas was a son of Herod the Great and ruled as a client king under [[romans|the Roman Empire]]. His territory included [[galilee|Galilee]], where [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] conducted much of his ministry, and Perea, where [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] was imprisoned and executed.

## Role in the Narrative

In Gibran's work, Herod Antipas appears in connection with the execution of [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]]. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] speaks of him with a tone of pity rather than condemnation:

> I grieve not for John, but rather for Herod, who let fall the sword. Poor king, like an animal caught and led with a ring and a rope. (032-010)

[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] characterizes Herod as a weak ruler trapped by his own position — a "poor petty tetrarch" lost in darkness, stumbling and falling (032-011). The execution of John is portrayed not as an act of strength but as the pathetic compliance of a tetrarch with the whims of his court.

## Significance

Herod Antipas serves as a foil in the narrative — a representative of worldly power that is, in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s view, hollow and captive to its own trappings. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s response to John's death focuses on grief for the executioner rather than the victim (032-010), emphasizing the [[soul|spirit]]ual poverty of temporal authority.
