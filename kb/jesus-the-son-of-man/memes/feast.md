# Feast

## Context

Feasting runs through *[[titlepage|Jesus the Son of Man]]* as a recurring motif — a pattern of shared meals, wedding banquets, and festive gatherings that together form one of the book's most distinctive [[list-of-concepts|themes]]. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is repeatedly depicted at table, not as an ascetic refraining from pleasure but as a participant who deepens the joy of those around him. The feast is not incidental to his ministry; it is one of its primary modes. Where [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]] came fasting, [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] came feasting — and both were revelations of [[god|God]], differently tuned.

> His words were like a feast to our ears, and to hear them was to forget the wine. (based on 010)

## Narrative

The feast appears in multiple chapters as setting and symbol. At the [[wedding-at-cana|wedding at Cana]], [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] transforms water into wine and the feast becomes the vehicle of his first public sign: "the best wine till the end of the feast. Other hosts do not so" (009-023). The wedding feast is the paradigmatic instance, but it is far from the only one.

[[naaman-of-the-gadarenes|Naaman of the Gadarenes]] recalls [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s presence at a feast: "He was like [[bridegroom|the bridegroom]] at the wedding-feast. For He gave them also the lees of the wine of His joy" (025-002). This image of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] as [[bridegroom|the bridegroom]] at the feast — the source and centre of the celebration — recurs throughout the book. He does not merely attend feasts; he becomes the feast, the one whose presence transforms an ordinary meal into a [[bread-and-wine|communion]].

The feast is also a site of controversy. [[one-of-the-marys|One of the Marys]] recalls how "His lips would sing at the wedding-feast" (028-006), a detail that scandalized the pious who expected a prophet to be solemn. For Gibran's [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], joy is not the opposite of holiness but its expression — the feast is the kingdom of [[love]] made tangible, a foretaste of the eternal banquet where all are welcome and none are turned away.

> He was like the bridegroom at the wedding-feast. For He gave them also the lees of the wine of His joy. (025-002)

The feast reaches its most charged expression in the final meal with his disciples — the [[bread-and-wine|Last Supper]] — where bread becomes body and wine becomes blood. This is the feast transfigured into [[sacrifice]], the celebration that contains within it the shadow of the [[crucifixion]]. Yet even here, the dominant note is not sorrow but gift — [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] gives himself as he has always given himself: at table, among friends, in the sharing of bread and wine.

## Significance

The feast in Gibran's book is the enacted parable of the kingdom. Where earthly kingdoms are built with swords and taxes, [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s kingdom is built with shared bread and poured wine. The feast embodies the central values of his teaching: abundance over scarcity, inclusion over exclusion, joy over duty, presence over ritual. Every feast in the book is a small revolution — the world's order, in which some are in and some are out, is overturned by a table where all are guests.

The recurring contrast between [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s feasting and [[john-the-baptist|John the Baptist]]'s fasting is deliberate. Gibran includes John's own voice in the book, speaking of his austere mission: "I am indeed an ally to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus of Nazareth]], and He shall lead my armies" (018-012). John prepares the way with fasting and repentance; [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] fulfills it with feasting and forgiveness. Both are necessary, but the feast is the destination — the end toward which the fasting points.

The "lees of the wine of His joy" (025-002) is a striking image — the dregs, the sediment, the very last of the wine. Even this, in [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s hands, becomes a gift. He gives not only the best wine but the lees as well — the whole of his joy, down to its final drop. This is the logic of the feast: nothing is held back, everything is shared.
