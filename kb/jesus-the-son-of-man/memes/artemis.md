## Overview
> Greek goddess of the hunt and the moon, known to the Ephesians as Diana, appearing in Gibran's mythological landscape.

Gibran references Artemis within the context of the ancient Mediterranean world that forms the backdrop of his Jesus narrative.

The goddess, worshipped fervently at [[ephesus|Ephesus]], represents the polytheistic milieu against which early Christianity defined itself.

Bartholomew, preaching in Ephesus, declares that even in that city of Artemis, Jesus is "crowned every day by the priestesses of Artemis in the secret places of her temple" (055-011).

The image is characteristically Gibranian: the old gods do not vanish but are absorbed into the new — Artemis's priestesses, unknowingly, crown Christ.

## In the Narrative

Artemis appears in Bartholomew's discourse at Ephesus, the city whose great temple to the goddess was one of the seven wonders of the ancient world. Bartholomew, addressing skeptics who "do not understand that the Beginning and the End are one thing," invokes the priestesses of Artemis as unwitting witnesses to Christ's cosmic sovereignty — they crown him "in the secret places of her temple" (055-011). This remarkable image transforms the cult of Artemis from a rival religion into an unconscious vehicle of Christian truth, a hallmark of Gibran's universalist vision.
