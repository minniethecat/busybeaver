## Overview

**Peace** is one of the most pervasive themes in Kahlil Gibran's *Jesus the Son of Man*. It appears across multiple testimonies — in Jesus's own words, in the reflections of his disciples, and in the experience of those he healed. In Gibran's rendering, peace is not merely the absence of conflict but an active, indwelling state of the soul, closely tied to the [[kingdom-of-heaven|Kingdom of Heaven]].

## Jesus's Teaching on Peace

In his discourse on the Kingdom of Heaven, Jesus describes a realm where peace is the natural atmosphere, not an enforced condition:

> "My face and your faces shall not be masked; our hand shall hold neither sword nor sceptre, and our subjects shall love us in peace and shall not be in fear of us." (045-006)

This vision rejects both political power (the sceptre) and violent force (the sword), proposing instead a kingdom founded on love and mutual goodwill.

Jesus also blessed the peacemakers in terms that connect peace with transformation:

> "Blessed are the peacemakers, for their spirit shall dwell above the battle, and they shall turn the potter's field into a garden." (059-012)

The image of transforming a potter's field — a burial ground for strangers and the poor — into a garden captures the redemptive, creative nature of peace in Jesus's teaching.

## Peace and Healing

Several witnesses describe Jesus's healing ministry as an act of bringing peace. [[thomas|Thomas]] reflects on the calming, restorative quality of Jesus's presence:

> Methinks it was by the power of opposition and resistance that He healed the sick, but in a manner unknown to our philosophers. He astonished fever with His snow-like touch and it retreated; and He surprised the hardened limbs with His own calm and they yielded to Him and were at peace. (060-008)

Later, Thomas describes the inner peace that followed his own spiritual healing:

> But now I see clearly, and I walk erect. I am at peace, and I live to witness and proclaim my own being every hour of the day. (035-016)

## Peace as a Gift and Calling

For [[john-the-beloved-disciple|John the Beloved]], peace was both the gift Jesus offered and the reason for following him. Jesus's words "Peace be to this house, and to those who dwell in it" were a benediction that opened every encounter, and his invitation "Take up my yoke and learn of me, for in my heart is peace, and your soul shall find abundance and a homecoming" (041-010) framed discipleship as a journey toward inner rest.

Yet peace in Gibran's portrait is not passive. The angels of peace are described as driving out "the devils, these restless elements in our life" (035-007) — peace is an active, conquering force that displaces chaos and restores order to the soul.
