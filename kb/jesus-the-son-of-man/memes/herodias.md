## Overview

Wife of Herod Antipas whose manipulation led to the death of John the Baptist, a figure of courtly intrigue in the Jesus story.

Herodias appears in Gibran's narrative as part of the political world surrounding Jesus's ministry. Her role in the beheading of John the Baptist marks a critical turning point in the Gospel drama that Gibran reimagines through poetic and philosophical lenses.

Herodias does not appear by name in Gibran's text, but her presence is felt through the narratives of Herod Antipas and John the Baptist, whose death she orchestrated according to the gospel accounts. Joanna, the wife of Herod's steward, provides an insider's perspective on the Herodian court where Herodias wielded her influence.

## In the Narrative

Although never named directly in *Jesus the Son of Man*, Herodias's shadow falls across several chapters. Herod Antipas, in his tortured reflections, is haunted by the death of John the Baptist — an execution that the Gospel tradition attributes to Herodias's manipulation through her daughter Salome's dance. John the Baptist himself, addressing one of his disciples from prison, speaks of the corrupt court that has imprisoned him — the same court where Herodias held sway. Joanna, the wife of Herod's steward, offers glimpses into the domestic politics of the Herodian household, the world in which Herodias moved as queen consort.

## Symbolic Significance

Herodias represents the corrupting influence of political power and sensual manipulation in Gibran's moral universe. She is the inverse of the women disciples who follow Jesus — where Mary Magdalene, Joanna, and Susannah use their positions to support the new spiritual movement, Herodias uses hers to destroy its herald. Her absence as a named character paradoxically amplifies her presence: she is the unnamed malice that the Baptist's righteousness must overcome.
