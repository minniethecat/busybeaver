## Overview

> Wise men from the East who journeyed to Bethlehem guided by a star, bringing gifts of gold, frankincense, and myrrh.

Gibran invokes the Magi as representatives of the wider world's recognition of Jesus's significance. Their journey from distant lands symbolizes the universal reach of the spiritual event that the nativity represents, a theme Gibran amplifies with poetic reverence.

## In the Narrative

John the Son of Zebedee, in his lyrical meditation on Jesus's universal significance, asks rhetorically whether the Master's spirit had not been known "in the land of the Magi, and upon the sands of Egypt" (014-011). This invocation places the Magi alongside other ancient centers of wisdom — India and Egypt — as witnesses to a truth that predates and transcends its incarnation in Jesus of Nazareth.

Melachi of Babylon, an astronomer, speaks in the voice of one of the Magi who visited the infant Jesus. He recounts the journey "to bring gifts to the newly-born King of the Jews" (035-002), offering the perspective of a Persian sage who read the prophecy in the stars and traveled to Bethlehem to honor the child.

## Symbolic Significance

The Magi represent the recognition of divine truth by the wisdom traditions of the East. Their journey — guided by a star and culminating in homage to an infant in a manger — embodies Gibran's theme that spiritual truth transcends religious and cultural boundaries. The gifts of gold, frankincense, and myrrh acknowledge kingship, divinity, and mortality — a triple prophecy of Jesus's identity and fate.
