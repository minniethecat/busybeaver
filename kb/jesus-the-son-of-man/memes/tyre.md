Tyre appears in Gibran's narrative as a place of departure — a port
city where sons go and from which they do not return. Though mentioned
only in passing, its name carries the weight of separation and the
lure of the sea.

## Geography

Tyre lay on the Mediterranean coast of Phoenicia, north of [[galilee|Galilee]],
in what is now southern [[lebanon|Lebanon]]. It was one of the great port cities
of the ancient world, known for its maritime trade and its purple dye.
In the narrative, it is a distant destination reached by ship — a
place beyond the hills of [[nazareth|Nazareth]] and the familiar landscape of
[[galilee|Galilee]].

## In the Narrative

Tyre enters the story through the grief of Susannah of [[nazareth|Nazareth]], a
neighbour of [[mary-mother-of-jesus|Mary]]. In the autumn after [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s death, she confides:
"Two [[sabbath|Sabbath]]s ago my heart was as a stone in my breast, for my son
had left me for a ship in Tyre. He would be a sailor. And he said he
would return no more" (062-085). The city is not described — it is
simply the place that takes a son from his mother, the horizon beyond
which he disappears.

## Significance

Tyre functions in the narrative as a symbol of distance and loss. It
is paired with the sea and with departure — a counterpoint to the
rootedness of [[nazareth|Nazareth]], where [[mary-mother-of-jesus|Mary]] still sits at her loom. Susannah's
sorrow over her son's sailing to Tyre echoes, in a minor key, the
larger grief of [[mary-mother-of-jesus|Mary]] over her own son, creating a resonance between
the mundane loss of a sailor and the cosmic loss of the [[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]].

## Events

No events occur in Tyre itself. Its sole narrative function is to be
the destination of Susannah's son, a place invoked only to mark an
absence (062-085).
