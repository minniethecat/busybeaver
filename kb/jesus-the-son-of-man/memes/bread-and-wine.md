## Overview

Bread and wine — the elements of the Last Supper — appear throughout Gibran's *Jesus the Son of Man* as symbols of shared sustenance, spiritual nourishment, and the intimate communion between teacher and disciples. Gibran treats the Eucharist not as a theological transaction (atonement, transubstantiation) but as a poetic act of self-giving.

## Peter's Memory

Peter, in his first testimony, recalls the meal with a simplicity that strips away centuries of doctrinal overlay:

> He broke the bread and gave us of the wine, and He said: "This is my body and my blood." (034-007)

The words are recorded without theological commentary. The emphasis falls not on what the bread and wine *become* but on what they *do*: they create communion. Peter remembers the act, not the doctrine.

## A Gibranian Eucharist

Gibran's treatment of the Eucharist is characteristically anti-dogmatic. The bread and wine are not objects of metaphysical transformation but vehicles of relationship. Jesus gives himself — his body and blood — not as a sacrifice to satisfy divine justice but as a gift to nourish his friends. This aligns with Gibran's broader portrayal of Jesus as one who gives rather than one who *pays*.

## Cross-Chapter Imagery

The bread and wine motif extends beyond Peter's testimony:
- **Peter 1** (034-007): The Last Supper account
- **Peter 2** (035): The shared meal as paradigm of community
- **A Man from Lebanon** (079): The book's closing prayer asks God's blessing on "the womb that held you, and the breasts that gave you milk" — extending the nourishment imagery to the Incarnation itself

For Gibran, bread and wine are the language of love made edible.

The sharing of bread and wine becomes, in Gibran's telling, an image of hospitality and communion:

> "Come into my house and share bread and wine with me."
>
> (004-025)
