> A pagan mourner from Byblos who weeps for Jesus in the language of the dying god — "Weep with me, ye daughters of Ashtarte, and all ye lovers of Tamouz."

The Woman of Byblos opens her lament (077-001) by invoking the ancient fertility deities of Phoenicia — [[ashtarte|Ashtarte]] (Astarte) and Tamouz ([[adonis|Adonis]]) — the goddess and her slain lover whose cult of dying-and-rising was among the most persistent in the ancient Near East.

## Testimony

Her lament [077-001–024] is structured as a ritual mourning. She calls upon the daughters of Ashtarte and the lovers of Tamouz to join her grief, framing Jesus's death within the mythic pattern of the dying god.

"He who was made of gold and ivory" (077-003) — her language transposes Jesus into the idiom of cult statuary and precious materials, a mode of perception wholly outside Jewish messianic categories.

## Significance

The Woman of Byblos represents Gibran's most explicit fusion of Christianity with the pagan mystery religions. Her inclusion in the book is deliberate and theological: it suggests that Jesus belongs not to one tradition but to the perennial pattern of divine death and renewal recognized across cultures. She is the voice of [[lebanon|Lebanon]]'s ancient religious imagination, claiming Jesus as its own.

The Woman of Byblos opens her lament with an invocation to the goddess and her followers:

> Weep with me, ye daughters of Ashtarte, and all ye lovers of Tamouz.
>
> Bid your heart melt and rise and run blood-tears,
>
> For He who was made of gold and ivory is no more.
>
> (077-001)
