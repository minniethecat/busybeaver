## Overview

Light is perhaps the most pervasive symbol in *Jesus the Son of Man* — the divine radiance that Jesus embodies and imparts. Birbarah of Yammouni speaks of Jesus as one who carried light within him, and John the Son of Zebedee describes the Christ as "the flame of God that dwells in the spirit of man" (014-003).

## The Inner Light

Gibran's treatment of light is characteristically inward and mystical. It is not the light of doctrinal certainty but the illumination of the soul — a quality of awareness rather than a set of propositions. Birbarah says simply: "He was light" (032-001). The statement is ontological, not metaphorical.

## John's Cosmic Vision

John the Son of Zebedee offers the book's most fully developed theology of light:

> The Christ, He who was in the ancient of days, is the flame of God that dwells in the spirit of man. He is the breath of life that visits us in our waking hours and in our sleep. (014-003)

Light and life are one reality. Jesus does not bring light as an external gift; he *is* light, and his presence awakens the same light in those who receive him.

## Light and Darkness

The light/darkness dualism that pervades John's Gospel is present in Gibran but softened — darkness is not an active force of evil so much as the mere absence of light. When Jesus dies, the disciples experience darkness; when they remember his words, light returns. The pattern is psychological and spiritual rather than cosmic.

## Gibran's Mysticism

Gibran's light imagery draws on multiple traditions: the Johannine "light of the world," the Sufi concept of the divine light (*nur*), and the Romantic poets' celebration of inner illumination. The result is a syncretic mysticism in which light is the universal language of the divine.
