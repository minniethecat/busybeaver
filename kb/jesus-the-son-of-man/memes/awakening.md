## Overview

Awakening is a recurring motif in Kahlil Gibran's *Jesus the Son of Man* — the transformative moment when a soul becomes fully alive to divine truth. Unlike the gradual process of rebirth, awakening is sudden: a flash of recognition that reorders the world. Benjamin the Scribe testifies that Jesus's words "wakened the spirit" even in those who resisted him.

## The Scribe's Testimony

Benjamin the Scribe, a man of law and precedent, speaks of being stirred from a kind of spiritual sleep:

> Even the dead bury their dead, but the living live in the spirit. [Benjamin the Scribe, 062-009]

For Benjamin, awakening means recognizing that the letter of the law is dead without the spirit. Jesus's presence acts as an alarm — disturbing comfortable certainties and demanding a response.

## Across the Testimonies

The awakening motif surfaces across multiple voices:
- **Benjamin the Scribe** (062-009): The dead vs. the living in spirit
- **Mary Magdalene** (013-001): Her first encounter with Jesus — "I hated Him and I was afraid" — is the prelude to an awakening that takes thirty years to unfold
- **John the Beloved Disciple** (052-011): Creation itself as a divine speech-act that awakens spirit from non-being

## Gibran's Poetic Theology

In Gibran's hands, awakening is not doctrinal conversion but aesthetic and relational encounter. One does not "accept Jesus as Lord" — one *sees* him, and in seeing, is changed. This aligns with Gibran's broader theology of presence: the divine does not need to be argued for, only attended to.
