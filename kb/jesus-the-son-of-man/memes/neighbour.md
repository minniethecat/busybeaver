## Overview

The commandment to "love thy neighbour as thyself" runs through *Jesus the Son of Man* as the practical heart of Jesus's ethical teaching. Peter, in his second testimony, records Jesus's words on the neighbour with a directness that captures both their simplicity and their radical demand.

## Peter's Testimony

Peter's second monologue (Chapter 35) centers on the teaching of neighbour-love:

> Your neighbour is your other self dwelling behind a wall. In understanding, all walls shall fall down. (035-003)

For Gibran's Jesus, the neighbour is not a separate entity to be tolerated or patronized but an extension of the self. The wall between self and other is an illusion — and love is the act of seeing through it.

## Beyond Tribalism

The ethic of the neighbour in Gibran's hands transcends tribal and religious boundaries. John the Son of Zebedee records Jesus's parable of the vineyard owner who pays all workers equally "out of his own purse and with his own will" (057-007) — a direct challenge to the insider/outsider binary. Combined with the teaching that "in my kingdom there are no strangers" (040-006), the neighbour ethic becomes universal.

## Gibran's Humanism

Gibran's treatment of neighbour-love reflects his broader humanism. Writing as a Syrian immigrant in America, he knew firsthand the experience of being the outsider, the "other." Through Jesus's words, he offers a vision of society in which the very category of "neighbour" expands to encompass all humanity. The practical test of love of God is love of the person next to you.
