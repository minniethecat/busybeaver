## Overview

Hypocrisy — the gap between public piety and private reality — emerges as one of the most consistent targets of Jesus's teaching in *Jesus the Son of Man*. Birbarah of Yammouni speaks of Jesus seeing through the masks people wear, and the theme echoes throughout the testimonies: the elders who pray loudly but scheme quietly, the righteous who tithe mint and forget mercy.

> He did not utterly condemn the liar or the thief or the murderer, but He did utterly condemn the hypocrite whose face is masked and whose hand is gloved.
>
> (012-006)

## Birbarah's Witness

Birbarah of Yammouni, a simple woman from the hill country, captures the contrast between hypocrisy and authenticity with remarkable directness. She observes that Jesus "looked at men and saw what was in them" (032-004). In his presence, pretense became impossible — and this, more than any doctrinal dispute, explains why the religious establishment found him intolerable.

## The Cleansing of the Temple

The theme of hypocrisy crystallizes in the temple cleansing narrative. Matthew's testimony (Chapter 13) records Jesus's anger at those who "turn my Father's house into a marketplace" — religious commerce that looks pious but serves greed. For Gibran, this is the archetypal exposure of hypocrisy: the outward form of worship maintained while its inward substance is betrayed.

> But I tolerate not the hypocrite, because he himself lays a yoke upon the guileless and the yielding.
>
> Weaklings, whom you call sinners, are like the featherless young that fall from the nest. The hypocrite is the vulture waiting upon a rock for the death of the prey.
>
> Weaklings are men lost in a desert. But the hypocrite is not lost. He knows the way yet he laughs between the sand and the wind.
>
> (012-010)

## Gibran's Lens

Gibran, himself deeply suspicious of institutional religion, gives the hypocrisy theme a distinctly modern resonance. Writing in the early twentieth century — an era of rapid secularization and religious critique — he presents Jesus not as the founder of a new religion but as the exposer of religion's failures. Hypocrisy is not merely a first-century Jewish problem; it is a perennial human temptation.
