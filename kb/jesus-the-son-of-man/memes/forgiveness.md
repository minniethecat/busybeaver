# Forgiveness

## Definition

Forgiveness in *[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] [[jesus-of-nazareth|the Son of Man]]* is the divine generosity that scandalizes the righteous. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is remembered as one who "heeded not the law of Moses, and that He was over-forgiving to the prostitutes of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] and the countryside" (008-005). His critics saw his forgiveness as lawlessness; his followers experienced it as liberation. The book explores whether forgiveness can extend to the unforgivable — to [[judas-iscariot|Judas]]'s betrayal, to [[simon-peter|Peter]]'s denial, to the executioners themselves.

## In the Text

Forgiveness appears most powerfully in the aftermath of failure. [[simon-peter|Peter]], who denied [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] three times, is restored by the resurrected [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] on the lakeshore — three questions of love answering three denials. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] places his hand "upon [[simon-peter|Simon Peter]]'s shoulder, and smiled" (079-031), a wordless absolution that needs no explanation. A woman taken in adultery finds [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s forgiveness offered without condition. The narrator of [[judas-iscariot|Judas]]'s chapter concludes: "I have pondered long since that day, and I understand [[judas-iscariot|Judas]]" (067-034) — suggesting that even the betrayer may be understood, if not absolved, when seen in the full context of his "little life."

## Significance

Gibran presents forgiveness as the test of whether [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s kingdom is real. If love can forgive betrayal, then the kingdom [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] proclaimed is more than words. The forgiveness extended to [[simon-peter|Peter]], to the adulterous woman, and perhaps even to [[judas-iscariot|Judas]], is the book's answer to the violence of the crucifixion: not revenge but restoration. Forgiveness is the mechanism by which the kingdom of love survives the world's rejection.
