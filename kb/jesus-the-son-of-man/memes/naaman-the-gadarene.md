> A voice from the Decapolis who bears witness to the scattering — "His disciples are dispersed. He gave them the legacy of pain ere He Himself was put to death."

Naaman's testimony [025-001–013] records the immediate aftermath of the crucifixion from the perspective of the hunted. His chapter opens not with praise or theology but with the raw facts of persecution.

## Testimony

"The disciples are dispersed," Naaman reports. "They are hunted like the deer, and the foxes of the fields, and the quail" (025-001). His language is that of the fugitive — men and women driven from their homes, their Teacher dead, their community shattered.

Naaman locates himself in the Gadarenes, the region east of the Jordan in the Decapolis — Gentile territory where Jesus had once healed a demoniac. His presence in the narrative extends the geographical reach of Jesus's impact beyond [[galilee|Galilee]] and [[judea|Judea]] into the Hellenistic cities of the Decapolis.

Naaman bears witness to the martyrdom of Stephen, the first to die for the name of Jesus:

> And when Stephen fell to earth he outstretched his arms as if he would die as his Master had died. His arms were spread like wings ready for flight. And when the last gleam of light was fading in his eyes, with my own eyes I saw a smile upon his lips. It was a smile like the breath that comes before the end of winter for a pledge and a promise of spring.
>
> (025-004)

## Significance

Naaman's chapter is one of the book's few windows into the persecution that followed the crucifixion. Where other witnesses meditate on Jesus's identity or teachings, Naaman documents the cost of discipleship — "the legacy of pain" (025-001) that Jesus left to his followers.
