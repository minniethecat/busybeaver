# Truth

## Definition

Truth in Gibran's book is not propositional but personal — it is what [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is, not merely what he says. [[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas]]'s grandfather taught him: "Let us observe truth, but only when truth is made manifest unto us" (026-001). This legal standard of verification is the starting point from which [[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas]] must journey toward a deeper truth — one that does not wait for evidence but reveals itself in relationship. [[nicodemus|Nicodemus]], the poet-elder, recognizes that those who mock [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] as confused reveal only their own limitation: "Many indeed are the owls who know no song unlike their own hooting" (038-002).
Nicodemus the poet-elder diagnoses the resistance to truth with scorn and pity:

> Many are the fools who say that Jesus stood in His own path and opposed Himself; that He knew not His own mind, and in the absence of that knowledge confounded Himself.
>
> Many indeed are the owls who know no song unlike their own hooting.
>
> (038-001)(038-002)

## In the Text

Truth appears in the book as the object of a search that leads different seekers to different conclusions. For [[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas]], truth must be verified; for [[nicodemus|Nicodemus]], it must be intuited; for [[caiaphas|Caiaphas]], it is identical with the law; for the Roman governor, it is an unanswerable question. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] himself embodies a truth that transcends all these approaches — a truth that cannot be reduced to a formula or enforced by an institution. His presence is the truth, and the varied responses to him are the measure of each witness's capacity for truth.

## Significance

The book suggests that truth is ultimately relational — known not through argument but through encounter. Each witness who meets [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is confronted with a truth that judges them even as they try to judge it. This makes truth not a possession but a relationship: one does not have the truth so much as one is had by it. In Gibran's vision, [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is the truth because he brings every person face to face with what they most truly are.
