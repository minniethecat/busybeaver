Byzantium appears in Gibran's narrative through the voice of Bartholomew,
who names it among the great cities of the Empire whose nobility
recognized the authority of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]].

## Geography

Byzantium stood on the Bosporus strait, commanding the passage between
the Mediterranean and the Black Sea. At the time of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] it was a Greek
trading colony; within a few centuries it would be refounded as
Constantinople, the eastern capital of [[romans|the Roman Empire]]. For Gibran's
readers in 1928, the name Byzantium would have carried the full weight
of that later imperial and ecclesiastical history.

## In the Narrative

Bartholomew, preaching in [[ephesus|Ephesus]], challenges his audience with a
rhetorical question about the social reach of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s movement: "Or
think you the highborn men and women of [[antioch|Antioch]] and Byzantium and Athens
and [[rome|Rome]] could be held by the voice of a leader of slaves?" (055-006).

The city is listed in a quartet of imperial capitals — [[antioch|Antioch]], Byzantium,
Athens, [[rome|Rome]] — each representing a distinct center of power in the
Mediterranean world. Bartholomew's argument is that the gospel was not a
slave revolt; it attracted the noble and the powerful as readily as the
poor, and the elites of cities like Byzantium were proof of its
transcendence of class.

## Significance

In Bartholomew's catalogue, Byzantium holds a specific position. [[rome|Rome]] is
the seat of political empire, Athens of philosophical wisdom, [[antioch|Antioch]] of
commerce and art. Byzantium, the city that would become the seat of
eastern [[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]]endom, represents the future: a world in which the
Nazarene's voice would be heard not in the margins but at the imperial
center.

## Events

No narrative events take place in Byzantium. It is invoked purely as a
symbol of imperial reach — evidence that the gospel's audience extended
far beyond the villages of [[galilee|Galilee]].
