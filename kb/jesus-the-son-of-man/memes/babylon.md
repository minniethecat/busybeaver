Babylon appears in Gibran's narrative in two distinct registers: as a
moral exemplar in a pronouncement of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], and as the ancestral home
of one of the narrators who testifies about Him.

## Geography

Babylon was the ancient capital of Mesopotamia, situated on the Euphrates
River south of modern Baghdad. By the time of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] it had long since
declined from its imperial zenith, but its name endured in the
Mediterranean imagination as the archetype of a great city brought low —
a trope that Gibran draws upon directly.

## In the Narrative

[[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] Himself invokes Babylon during a confrontation with the [[pharisees|Pharisees]]
at the house of Levi the publican. When they question His presence among
tax-gatherers and sinners, He rebukes them sharply: "Babylon was not put
to waste by her prostitutes; Babylon fell to ashes that the eyes of her
hypocrites might no longer see the light of day" (030-015).

The pronouncement is layered. On its surface it is a defense of the
visibly sinful — the prostitutes of Babylon, like the publicans at
Levi's table, are not the ones who bring destruction. It is the
hypocrites, those who hide their corruption behind righteousness, who
reduce a city to ashes. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] turns the familiar trope of Babylon's
fall into a mirror for His accusers.

Separately, one of the book's seventy-nine narrators is Melachi of
Babylon, an astronomer who questions [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s miracles (035-001). His
identity as "of Babylon" anchors the ancient city's name in the living
memory of Gibran's cast of witnesses.

## Significance

Babylon functions in the text as a double symbol: historically, it is the
paradigm of a great civilization that collapsed under its own moral
weight; rhetorically, [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] deploys it to distinguish between outward sin
and inward hypocrisy. The passage is one of the few moments in the book
where [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] speaks in prophetic judgment rather than parable or blessing.

## Events

No narrative events take place in Babylon. [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] invokes it as a
remembered example, and Melachi carries its name into the present tense
of his testimony.
