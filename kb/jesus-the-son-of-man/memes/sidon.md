## Overview

Sidon was one of the great Phoenician city-states of the ancient Mediterranean world, situated on the coast of modern-day Lebanon. In Gibran's *Jesus the Son of Man*, Sidon is the seat of **[[phumiah-the-high-priestess-of-sidon|Phumiah]]**, a high priestess who sings a poetic tribute to Jesus, celebrating him as a dauntless figure who overcame darkness.

## Sidon in the Ancient World

Sidon was renowned in antiquity as a center of maritime trade, purple dye production, and religious cults. Along with its sister city [[tyre|Tyre]], Sidon dominated the Phoenician coast and established colonies throughout the Mediterranean. Its people were known as skilled sailors, craftsmen, and merchants.

## Phumiah's Testimony

The only direct voice from Sidon in the gospel narrative comes through **Phumiah**, who is identified as the High Priestess of Sidon. Her chapter opens with a striking invocation:

> Take your harps and let me sing. Beat your strings, the silver and the gold; For I would sing the dauntless Man Who slew the dragon of the valley. (079-007)

Phumiah's poetic testimony portrays Jesus not as a god but as a heroic human figure — a "dauntless Man" who confronted and defeated darkness. Her perspective is notable for its artistic, celebratory tone, distinct from the more doctrinal or narrative testimonies elsewhere in the book.

## Connections

Sidon is mentioned in the context of the broader [[lebanon|Lebanese]] landscape and is linked to [[tyre|Tyre]] as its sister city on the Phoenician coast. The figure of [[phumiah-the-high-priestess-of-sidon|Phumiah]] provides the living connection between the city and the Jesus narrative.
