# Rachael, a Woman Disciple

## Identity

Rachael is a woman disciple of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], one of the few female voices among the seventy-nine witnesses. Her testimony is philosophical rather than narrative — she does not recount events so much as meditate on the nature of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s being. She opens with a question that haunts the entire book: "I often wonder whether [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] was a man of flesh and blood like ourselves, or a thought without a body, in the mind, or an idea that visits the vision of man" (023-001). She suggests that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] may have been "but a dream dreamed by the countless men and women at the same time in a sleep deeper than sleep" (023-002).

> I often wonder whether Jesus was a man of flesh and blood like ourselves, or a thought without a body, in the mind, or an idea that visits the vision of man.
>
> Often it seems to me that He was but a dream dreamed by the countless men and women at the same time in a sleep deeper than sleep and a dawn more serene than all dawns.
>
> (023-001)

## Relationship to Jesus

Rachael's relationship with [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] transcends the personal. She does not claim intimacy or special knowledge; instead, she ponders his ontological mystery. Was he human? Was he divine? Was he something in between? Her questions are not doubts but wonderments — the kind of deep reflection that only a sustained encounter with [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] could provoke. She represents the contemplative response to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]], distinct from [[simon-peter|Peter]]'s active devotion, [[thomas-the-disciple|Thomas]]'s skepticism, or [[caiaphas|Caiaphas]]'s hostility.

## Narrative

Rachael's chapter is less a story than a meditation. She reflects on [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s presence among the disciples, the quality of his being, and the impossibility of categorizing him. She recalls: "We touched His hands, and we followed Him from one place to another. We heard His discourses and witnessed His deeds" (023-005). Her voice is quiet but persistent — she does not need to see miracles or hear sermons to be transformed. The very fact of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]]'s existence, the mystery of his person, is enough to sustain her contemplation. Her chapter is one of the book's most philosophical, suggesting that the deepest response to [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] may not be action or declaration but sustained wondering.

> He was a mountain burning in the night, yet He was a soft glow beyond the hills. He was a tempest in the sky, yet He was a murmur in the mist of daybreak.
>
> He was a torrent pouring from the heights to the plains to destroy all things in its path. And He was like the laughter of children.
>
> (023-009)

## Significance

Rachael represents the contemplative dimension of discipleship — the truth that one can follow [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] not only with one's hands and feet but with one's mind and heart. She is the patron of all who cannot answer their own questions about [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] but cannot stop asking them either. Her testimony suggests that wonder is a form of faithfulness, and that the unanswerable question may be closer to truth than the confident answer.
