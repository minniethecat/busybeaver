> Former High Priest, father-in-law to Caiaphas, who saw in Jesus a threat to the religious order of Israel.

Annas speaks in a single, searing chapter [069-001–012] that functions as one of the book's most concentrated expressions of institutional hostility toward Jesus.

## Character

Annas was the patriarch of the high-priestly family that dominated the Temple in Jesus's time. Though no longer the sitting High Priest, his authority remained immense — [[caiaphas|Caiaphas]], the reigning High Priest, was his son-in-law [007-001–009].

## Testimony

Annas's testimony is a litany of contempt. He dismisses Jesus as "of the rabble, a brigand, a mountebank and a self-trumpeter" who "appealed only to the unclean and the disinherited" (069-001).

He locates Jesus's origins in [[galilee|Galilee]] of the Gentiles, "the North Country where Adonis and Ashtarte still claim power against Israel and the God of Israel" (069-003) — a rhetorical move that marks Jesus as religiously contaminated, an alien to pure Judaism.

## Significance

The Annas chapter is structurally important: it places the institutional case against Jesus alongside the personal testimonies of followers and witnesses. His voice represents the Temple's verdict — that Jesus was a dangerous syncretist whose execution was necessary for Israel's survival. The chapter thus balances the book's polyphonic structure, ensuring that condemnation is heard as clearly as devotion.

Annas's contempt for Jesus blends personal scorn with institutional alarm:

> He was of the rabble, a brigand, a mountebank and a self-trumpeter. He appealed only to the unclean and the disinherited, and for this He had to go the way of all the tainted and the defiled.
>
> He made sport of us and of our laws; He mocked at our honour and jeered at our dignity. He even said He would destroy the temple and desecrate the holy places. He was shameless, and for this He had to die a shameful death.
>
> (069-001)
