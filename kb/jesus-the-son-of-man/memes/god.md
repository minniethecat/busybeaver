## Overview

In Kahlil Gibran's *Jesus the Son of Man*, God is not the stern, jealous deity of the Hebrew scriptures but a tender, forgiving Father whose nature is most fully expressed through Jesus. Gibran's God embodies love rather than law, mercy rather than sacrifice, and kinship rather than distance. This conception runs as a unifying thread through the book's many voices.

## A Contrast of Two Gods

The Persian philosopher in Damascus draws the sharpest contrast between the Old Testament God and the God Jesus reveals:

> The ancient God of Israel is harsh and relentless. Israel should have another God; one who is gentle and forgiving, who would look down upon them with pity. (010-002)–(010-003)

> Now the God of Jesus, whom He called Father, would not be a jealous God. (010-009)

This passage crystallizes a central theme of the book: Jesus did not merely teach about God — he *revealed* a fundamentally different conception of the divine character.

## God as Father

Throughout the testimonies, Jesus consistently refers to God as "Father" — a term of intimacy that scandalized the religious authorities. John the Beloved Disciple's cosmic prologue describes creation itself as an act of divine speech and love:

> God spoke thus, and His words were man, and man was a spirit begotten by God's Spirit. And when God spoke thus, the Christ was His first Word and that Word was perfect. (052-011)–(052-012)

The Fatherhood of God is not hierarchical but creative and relational. In Gibran's rendering, Jesus teaches that God cares for birds and lilies (057-004)–(057-005), pays the late labourer the same wage as the early one (057-006)–(057-007), and opens "the gate of His mansion at the knocking of the Gentiles even as at your knocking" (057-008).

## God of Forgiveness

In the crucifixion chapters, God's nature shifts from paternal care to radical forgiveness. Barabbas, watching Jesus die in his place, hears words that reframe God as the ultimate forgiver:

> I saw all men prostrated before God beseeching forgiveness for the crucifixion of this one man. (072-019)

The final lines of the book, spoken by A Man from Lebanon, close with a prayer that encapsulates the Gibranian God: "May God forgive us all" (079-169).

## The Kinship Stream

Joseph of Arimathea (his second testimony) identifies two "streams" flowing in Jesus's heart — one of kinship with God, and one of rapture called the Kingdom of Heaven (039-002). This image of *kinship* between humanity and divinity is perhaps Gibran's most distinctive theological contribution: God is not remote but akin.

## Gibran's Synthesis

Gibran's God reconciles:
- The prophetic God of Israel with universal fatherhood
- Divine transcendence with intimate immanence ("the flame of God that dwells in the spirit of man" (014-003))
- Justice with mercy (the vineyard owner paying all workers equally (057-006)–(057-007))

The result is a poetic theology that appealed to Gibran's early-20th-century audience — Christian, Muslim, and secular alike — by presenting God as Love itself, stripped of institutional doctrinal apparatus.
