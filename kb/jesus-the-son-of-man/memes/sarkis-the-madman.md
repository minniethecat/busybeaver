# Sarkis, the Madman

## Identity

Sarkis is an old Greek shepherd known as "the madman" — an outsider to Jewish religion and society whose madness grants him a unique clarity. He is neither disciple nor opponent, neither Jew nor Roman, but a pagan shepherd whose vision of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] bypasses theology entirely. His testimony opens with a dream: "In a dream I saw [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] and my god Pan sitting together in the heart of the forest" (068-001). The image is audacious — [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] and the Greek nature god laughing together, "the laughter of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] the merrier" (068-002).

## Relationship to Jesus

Sarkis has no personal relationship with [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] in the conventional sense — he is not a follower, not a witness to the ministry, not present at the crucifixion. His encounter is entirely visionary, occurring in the liminal space of dream. Yet this distance is his gift: unencumbered by Jewish expectations or Roman politics, he sees [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] purely. His [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is a figure of cosmic joy who can converse with Pan about "earth and her secrets" while Pan remains bound to nature and [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] points beyond it. The madman's [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is the most free — uncontained by any religious system.

## Narrative

Sarkis's dream narrative places [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] in the forest with Pan, where "they conversed long" (068-002). Pan speaks of "his hoofèd brothers and his hornèd sisters" and the mysteries of the earth; [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] speaks of other things — the dream does not specify what, but the contrast is clear. Pan is earthbound; [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] transcends. When Sarkis wakes, the vision lingers. He is called mad, but his madness is the sanity of one who sees what the respectable cannot: that truth wears many faces, and that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] belongs not to one tradition but to all.

## Significance

Sarkis embodies the universal reach of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] beyond the boundaries of Judaism and [[jesus-of-nazareth|Christ]]ianity. His testimony suggests that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] can be encountered through dream and vision, not only through historical witness. The image of [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] and Pan laughing together is Gibran's most radical gesture — a declaration that the sacred is not the property of any one religion. The madman, in his holy folly, sees what the sane cannot: that [[jesus-of-nazareth|Jesus]] is larger than the church that will claim him.

## Quotation

Sarkis's dream opens with an audacious vision that places Jesus alongside the Greek god Pan:

> In a dream I saw Jesus and my god Pan sitting together in the heart of the forest.
