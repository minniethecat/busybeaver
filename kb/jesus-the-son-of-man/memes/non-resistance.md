## Overview

Non-resistance — the teaching that one should not retaliate against evil but overcome it with good — is among the most distinctive and counter-intuitive elements of Jesus's ethical teaching in *Jesus the Son of Man*. David, one of Jesus's followers, witnesses this teaching firsthand and records its quiet radicalism.

## David's Witness

David's testimony (Chapter 62) is brief but focused. He recalls Jesus teaching:

> You have heard it said: an eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth. But I say unto you: resist not evil. (062-005)

The words are familiar from the Sermon on the Mount, but Gibran presents them through the memory of a disciple who had to live them — a man who followed a non-resistant teacher to a violent death.

## The Third Way

Gibran's Jesus does not advocate passivity or cowardice. Non-resistance is not submission to evil but a creative third way between violent resistance and passive acceptance. It is the refusal to play by the enemy's rules — to meet hatred with love, violence with forgiveness. This is not weakness but a different kind of strength.

## Political Implications

In the context of Roman-occupied Judea, non-resistance was politically explosive. The Zealots advocated armed rebellion; the establishment advocated collaboration. Jesus's "resist not evil" rejected both paths. Gibran, writing in the aftermath of World War I, presents this teaching with renewed urgency — a plea for a world that had just demonstrated the full horror of organized violence.
