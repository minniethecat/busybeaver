## Overview

> Roman general whose conquest of Jerusalem brought Judea under Roman dominion, setting the political stage for the world into which Jesus was born.

Gibran references Pompey as part of the deep historical memory that shapes the political landscape of first-century Palestine. His capture of the Temple and subjugation of the Hasmonean kingdom represent the beginning of Roman hegemony over the land where Jesus would later walk.

## In the Narrative

Pompey appears in the testimony of Claudius, the Roman centurion who guarded Jesus on the night before the crucifixion. Now an old man, Claudius reflects on the greatest commanders he has known and makes a startling comparison:

> I think truly that neither Pompey nor Caesar was so great a commander as that Man of Galilee.
>
> (073-013)

He elaborates that "since His unresisting death an army has risen out of the earth to fight for Him … And He is better served by them, though dead, than ever Pompey or Caesar was served, though living" (073-014). For a Roman soldier who served under the Republic's greatest generals, this judgment represents the most profound conversion — the recognition that spiritual leadership surpasses military conquest.

## Symbolic Significance

Pompey and Caesar together represent the summit of Roman martial glory. By placing Jesus above both, Gibran — through Claudius — makes a radical political and spiritual claim: the unarmed Galilean who submitted to death commands a loyalty that Rome's greatest conquerors could never inspire. Pompey's conquest of Jerusalem in 63 BCE established the Roman presence in Judea; Jesus's "conquest" from the cross, a century later, would transform the Empire itself.
