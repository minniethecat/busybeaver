## Overview

The Comtesse de Granville is the mother of [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]] and [[marie-eugenie-du-tillet|Marie-Eugénie]], and the wife of the [[comte-de-granville|Comte de Granville]]. Though she appears only briefly in *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), her influence pervades the novel — the strict religious upbringing she imposed on her daughters is the formative condition of their characters and the source of both their virtues and their vulnerabilities.

## A Pious Mother

The Comtesse de Granville is described as a woman of extreme piety. She raised her daughters in an atmosphere of severe religious devotion, shielding them from the world and its temptations. The sisters' education — supervised by their mother, centered on religious observance, cut off from worldly knowledge — produced in Marie-Angélique a profound innocence that proves dangerously fragile when exposed to Parisian society.

The Comtesse belongs to the legitimist, Catholic aristocracy of the Restoration — a world of closed salons, elderly priests, and newspapers like [[la-quotidienne|*La Quotidienne*]]. Her household is a preserve of the old order, insulated from the intellectual and social currents transforming France.

## The Marriage Arrangement

Following aristocratic custom, the Comtesse arranged Marie-Angélique's marriage to [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]]. The match was socially advantageous — Félix was a count, a man of property and position — but it was not a love match. Marie-Angélique entered marriage as she had entered everything in her life: obediently, innocently, without having been consulted about her own desires.

The Comtesse's decision, made according to the norms of her class and the dictates of her conscience, inadvertently sets the stage for the novel's central drama. Marie-Angélique's susceptibility to Nathan's seduction is in part a consequence of having been married to a man she did not choose, without any preparation for the emotional complexities of adult life.

## A Story of Temptation

At a critical moment in the novel, the Comtesse de Granville reveals to her daughter a story from her own past — a temptation she herself faced and resisted. This confession is meant as a warning, a lesson in the cost of fidelity. But the revelation has a complex effect: it shows Marie-Angélique that even her devout mother was not immune to passion, and that the price of virtue can be a lifetime of quiet unhappiness.

## Significance

The Comtesse de Granville represents the old order — aristocratic, Catholic, and rigid — that shapes but cannot protect her daughters. Her piety is genuine, her intentions are good, but the world she has prepared her daughters for no longer exists. In Balzac's social vision, the failure of the older generation to equip the younger for the realities of modern life is not a personal failing but a historical condition.
