# Dinner at Véry's

The midnight supper at Véry's restaurant in the aftermath of Mme. de Montcornet's concert is one of the novel's most revealing scenes — a private tableau of male cynicism in which three distinctive varieties of Parisian worldliness dissect an innocent woman's romantic awakening over champagne. It is the moment when Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse's nascent passion is first framed, by the men who will determine its course, as a conquest to be managed rather than a love to be honored.

## The Setting

The supper follows immediately upon Raoul Nathan's triumphant debut at the Comtesse de Montcornet's salon. Nathan has just been told by Mme. d'Espard that the Comtesse de Vandenesse has "fallen wildly in love" with him, and Blondet has confirmed it with a laugh: "Ah! but you will see her, you rascal. Lady Dudley has invited you to her great ball with the very purpose of bringing about a meeting" [004-029, 004-031].

As the guests depart, Raoul and Blondet leave together. They encounter Rastignac, who offers them a place in his carriage. The three make "merry over this conjunction of an eclectic Undersecretary of State with a fierce Republican and a political sceptic" [004-032]. It is Blondet, the perpetual ironist, who proposes supper: "Suppose we sup at the expense of law and order?"

Rastignac takes them to Véry's, dismisses his carriage, and the three settle in for an evening of what Balzac describes as "Rabelaisian laughter" as they "set themselves to pull to pieces their contemporaries" [004-034].

## The Caricature of Marie

It is during this supper that the figure of Marie de Vandenesse becomes the evening's central subject. Balzac's narrative voice intervenes with unusual moral force:

> The story of Marie de Vandenesse was caricatured by these two profligates, who applied the scalpel of epigram and the keen edge of mockery to that transparent childhood, that happy marriage. [004-034]

Blondet, with his characteristic blend of insight and cruelty, congratulates Raoul on his good fortune. His description of Marie is a masterpiece of reductive wit: she is a woman who has been "guilty only of execrable red-chalk drawings and feeble watercolor landscapes, of embroidering slippers for her husband, and performing sonatas with a most ladylike absence of passion." She has been "tied for eighteen years to her mother's apron-strings, pickled in devotion, trained by Vandenesse, and cooked to a turn by marriage for the palate of love" [004-034].

The culinary metaphor — "cooked to a turn" — is both crude and precise. In Blondet's telling, Marie is not an autonomous being with desires and scruples but a dish prepared by others and now ready for consumption. The innocence that makes her vulnerable is precisely what makes her desirable to men who have exhausted the pleasures of the demi-monde.

## Nathan's Poetic Pretensions: Beatrice and Laura

It is at this point, when "the third bottle of champagne" has been reached, that Raoul Nathan becomes "more expansive than he had ever shown himself before" [004-034]. His speech is the emotional center of the supper scene and one of the most self-revealing passages in the novel:

> "My dear friends," he said, "you know my relations with Florine, you know my life, you will not be surprised to hear me confess that I have never yet seen the color of a Countess' love. It has often been a humiliating thought to me that only in poetry could I find a Beatrice, a Laura! A pure and noble woman is like a spotless conscience, she raises us in our own estimation. Elsewhere we may be soiled, with her we keep our honor, pride, and purity. Elsewhere life is a wild frenzy, with her we breathe the peace, the freshness, the bloom of the oasis." [004-035]

The invocation of Beatrice and Laura — Dante's and Petrarch's immortalized beloved women — is characteristic of Nathan's literary sensibility and his capacity for self-deception. He frames his pursuit of Marie in the language of the highest poetic tradition, casting himself as a Dante in search of his beatific guide. But the context — a drunken supper with two cynics in a restaurant — fatally undermines the pretension.

Nathan's speech reveals the central contradiction of his character: he genuinely longs for the purity he describes, but he approaches it through the categories of conquest and possession. He wants a Beatrice, but he also wants a Countess — a social trophy whose very inaccessibility confirms his own importance.

## Rastignac's Mockery

Rastignac, the practical politician who has already made his own calculated marriage to the daughter of the Baron de Nucingen, is unmoved by Nathan's poetic effusion. His response is brief and brutal:

> "Come, come, my good soul, shift the prayer of Moses on to the high notes, as Paganini does." [004-036]

The reference to Paganini — the violin virtuoso famous for his technical wizardry and theatrical showmanship — is a devastating comparison. Rastignac reduces Nathan's spiritual language to mere performance, a "prayer of Moses" transposed to a higher key for effect. Nathan, stung, can only respond: "This beast of a 'prentice minister does not understand me!" [004-038].

The exchange captures the essential difference between the two men. Rastignac has mastered the art of treating all ideals as instruments; Nathan still needs to believe in his own rhetoric, even as he prepares to use it for seduction.

## The Narrative Judgment

At this moment, Balzac's narrator steps forward with one of the novel's most famous moral interventions. While these "three shameless spirits trampled on the tender white blossoms of her opening love" at Véry's, the innocent Marie lies in her bed on the Rue du Rocher, "swathed in shame, terrified at the delight which had filled her while listening to this poetic pretender, hovering between the stern voice of gratitude to Vandenesse and the flattering tongue of the serpent" [004-039].

The narrator's outrage is explicit: "Ah! if women knew how cynical those men can be behind their backs, who show themselves all meekness and cajolery when by their side! if they knew how they mock their idols!" Yet the narrator immediately complicates this moral by observing that the woman triumphs even through this exposure: "Fresh, lovely, and timid creature, whose charms lie at the mercy of some graceless buffoon! And yet she triumphs! The more the veils are rent, the clearer her beauty shines" [004-039].

This paradoxical conclusion — that Marie's beauty is enhanced rather than diminished by her victimization — is characteristically Balzacian. The innocence that makes her vulnerable is also what makes her valuable, and the cynical men who exploit it are, in the final analysis, smaller than what they consume.

## The Three Men as Types

The supper at Véry's functions as a character study of three distinct modes of worldly corruption:

| Diner | Type | Approach to Marie |
|-------|------|-------------------|
| **Rastignac** | The calculating politician | Urges Nathan to pursue the conquest for the social advantage it confers; reduces all sentiment to strategy |
| **Blondet** | The cynical ironist | Mocks Marie's innocence and Nathan's pretensions with equal wit; takes pleasure in exposing the machinery beneath the romance |
| **Nathan** | The poetic self-deceiver | Wraps his predatory intention in the language of Dante and Petrarch; needs to believe in his own idealism even as he plans seduction |

## Marie's Parallel Soliloquy

Balzac immediately cuts from the restaurant to Marie's bedchamber, where she is comparing Raoul and Félix, "all-ignorant of the danger to her heart in such a process" [004-040]. She contrasts Félix's polished refinement — "his clothes fitting like a glove, the finish of a fine lady in his person" — with Raoul's "robust and unconventional" presence. The narrator observes that "a contrast like this pleases the fancy of a woman, ever ready to fly from one extreme to another" [004-040].

Marie takes herself to task for "base ingratitude" the next morning, but the seed is planted. When she asks Félix what he thinks of Nathan, the Count replies with devastating accuracy: "He is a charlatan, one of those volcanoes which a sprinkling of gold-dust will keep tranquil" [004-042]. Félix supports his verdict with proofs drawn from Nathan's life, "in which Florine, a well-known actress, played a large part" [004-043].

The supper at Véry's thus establishes the novel's central dramatic irony: the men who will determine Marie's fate have already appraised her, categorized her, and assigned her a role in their own ambitions, even as she lies awake struggling with feelings she does not yet understand.

## Significance

Véry's restaurant — a real Parisian establishment of the period — serves as the novel's symbolic antechamber to seduction. It is a male space, closed to the women who are its subject, where the language of poetry and the language of predation become indistinguishable. Nathan's invocation of Beatrice and Laura in this setting is not a mark of his idealism but of his bad faith: he borrows the vocabulary of spiritual love to dignify what is, at bottom, a social and sexual campaign.

The scene is also Balzac's most concentrated statement of his recurring theme: that the private conversations of men about women are the hidden engine of much that passes for romance in the drawing-room. What Marie experiences as the thrilling mystery of attraction is, on the other side of the gender divide, a strategic calculation conducted over champagne at the expense of law and order.
