The Pont Neuf — the "New Bridge" that is in fact the oldest bridge in Paris — spans the Seine at the western tip of the Île de la Cité. In Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), it is part of the Parisian geography through which the novel's characters move.

The Pont Neuf was, in Balzac's day, a bustling thoroughfare lined with stalls and frequented by every class of Parisian. Unlike the aristocratic enclosures of the Faubourg Saint-Germain, the Pont Neuf was a democratic space — a place where the city's diverse population mingled. Its mention in the novel's implicit geography is another element of Balzac's comprehensive mapping of Parisian space.
