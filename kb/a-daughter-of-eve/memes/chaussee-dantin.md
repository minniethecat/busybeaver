The Chaussée d'Antin was the fashionable neighborhood of bankers and financiers in Balzac's Paris — the home territory of the Nucingen family and others of their class. In contrast to the aristocratic Faubourg Saint-Germain, the Chaussée d'Antin represented new money: the wealth of speculation, banking, and commerce that was reshaping French society under the July Monarchy.

In *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), the Chaussée d'Antin is the implicit geography of [[baron-de-nucingen|Baron de Nucingen]]'s world — the financial district where the schemes and counter-schemes of the novel's money plot are hatched. Balzac's mapping of Parisian neighborhoods is always a mapping of social power, and the Chaussée d'Antin represents the ascendant force of finance.
