## Overview

The Palais-Royal was one of the most important sites in Balzac's Paris — a vast complex of arcades, shops, restaurants, and entertainments that served as the commercial and social heart of the city. In *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), it is the location of [[very-restaurant|Véry's Restaurant]], where the novel's most cynical supper takes place.

## The Arcades

The Palais-Royal's arcades — covered galleries lined with shops, cafés, and establishments of every kind — were a quintessential Parisian institution. They represented the new commercial public sphere of the nineteenth century: a space where classes mixed, where money circulated, and where the boundaries between respectable and disreputable were perpetually blurred.

For Balzac, the Palais-Royal was a site of modernity — a place where the old order of aristocratic exclusivity gave way to the democratic promiscuity of commerce. It is appropriate that the cynical supper at Véry's takes place here, in the heart of the commercial world that is displacing the aristocratic values Marie-Angélique represents.

## Restaurants and Social Life

Véry's was one of several celebrated restaurants in the Palais-Royal. The rise of the restaurant as a social institution — a place where men of different classes could dine together, discuss politics, and conduct business — was a distinctive feature of post-Revolutionary Paris. The supper at Véry's, with its mixture of political figures, journalists, and social aspirants, exemplifies the new sociability of the restaurant.

## In Balzac's Paris

The Palais-Royal appears throughout Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine* as a site of ambition, pleasure, and moral danger. It is the Paris of Rastignac's ascent, of Lucien de Rubempré's corruption, of the gambler, the speculator, and the courtesan. Its brief appearance in *A Daughter of Eve* connects the novel to this larger Balzacian geography.
