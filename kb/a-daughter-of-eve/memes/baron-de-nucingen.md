# Baron de Nucingen

The Baron de Nucingen is one of the most significant recurring characters in [[la-comedie-humaine|La Comédie Humaine]] — the great Alsatian-born banker whose financial operations, heavily-accented speech, and vast wealth make him a central figure in Balzac's depiction of the July Monarchy's moneyed aristocracy. In *A Daughter of Eve*, his role is indirect but structurally important: he is the financial power behind the political ambitions that drive the novel's subplot, and his wife becomes an instrument in Marie-Eugénie du Tillet's scheme to rescue Nathan.

## The Power of Money

Nucingen's wealth is presented as a force of nature — immense, impersonal, and indifferent to the human lives it touches. Marie-Eugénie describes the scale of his operations with a mixture of awe and resentment:

> "He and the Baron de Nucingen, another of these rich bankers, are accustomed to handle millions. Nucingen and he make as little of ruining a man as I do of all their display. And du Tillet and Nucingen scatter banknotes in handfuls for any whim that takes their fancy." [002-016]

The repetition of "Nucingen and he" — referring to [[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]] — pairs the two financiers as twin operators in a world where money has replaced birth as the true source of power.

## Political Transition

At the time of the novel, Nucingen is a peer of the realm who is resigning his seat in the [[chamber-of-deputies|Chamber of Deputies]] to du Tillet:

> "Mme. du Tillet dines tomorrow with a future peer of the realm, Baron de Nucingen, who is resigning to me his seat in the Chamber of Deputies." [002-027]

This transfer of political power from one financier to another exemplifies Balzac's critique of the July Monarchy, where parliamentary seats are commodities traded among the wealthy rather than offices of public trust.

## Madame de Nucingen's Role

Nucingen's wife, the Baronne de Nucingen, plays a more direct role in the novel's plot. She is the financial intermediary through whom Marie-Eugénie arranges to raise forty thousand francs to pay Nathan's debts:

> "Mme. de Nucingen will take four bills, each for ten thousand francs, signed by anyone you like, so as not to compromise you." [008-018]

The arrangement requires Marie-Angélique to sign promissory notes — a device that will later become the instrument of her exposure when Félix discovers the hidden correspondence.

## The Palace Under Construction

A small but telling detail: Nucingen is building a new house. "Nucingen is tired of his house in the Rue Saint-Lazare and is having a palace built" [002-016]. Later, Mme. de Nucingen confirms: "M. de Nucingen is going to deprive me of it; he is getting a new house built" [008-093]. The endless construction — wealth perpetually remaking its own physical setting — is a minor key in which Balzac sounds the theme of impermanence beneath the surface of Restoration stability.

## In La Comédie Humaine

Baron de Nucingen appears in over thirty of Balzac's novels, most prominently in *La Maison Nucingen*, *Splendeurs et misères des courtisanes*, and *Le Père Goriot*. His famous Alsatian accent and garbled French — "I am ein great financier" — make him one of Balzac's most recognizable creations. In *A Daughter of Eve*, he is in late career: a peer, a power-broker, and a man for whom money has long since ceased to be a means and become an end in itself.
