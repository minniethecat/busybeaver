# Marie-Eugénie du Tillet

Marie-Eugénie du Tillet (née Granville) is the elder sister of [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse]] and the wife of the wealthy banker [[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]]. Her marriage — a financial transaction disguised as a social union — serves as the novel's darkest portrait of conjugal misery.

## A Prisoner of Marriage

Eugénie's marriage is presented as a gilded cage. Her husband is "one of the richest bankers in Paris," and her house displays "opulence and good taste in all its details" [001-003]. But the luxury is a façade. When she sees her sister, she "pressed her sister's hand to her lips" — a gesture of silent desperation that speaks volumes [001-004].

Balzac's portrait of her is one of terrified submission. "Mme. du Tillet, terrified, had hidden her face behind a cup of tea" [002-010]. She fears her husband viscerally, and that fear shapes every aspect of her existence. Her wealth, which outsiders envy, is entirely controlled by du Tillet: "I have not a sou of my own" [002-012].

## Relationship with Marie-Angélique

The sisterly bond between Eugénie and Marie-Angélique is one of the novel's most tender relationships — and one of its most painful. "You poor darling," Eugénie says to her sister, "what strange delusion as to me have you yielded to?" [002-003]. She recognizes that Marie-Angélique envies her wealth without understanding its cost.

The sisters live "in opposing camps" [002-008] — divided by their husbands' enmity and by Eugénie's fear of du Tillet's wrath. When she visits her sister's house, the visit is clandestine, conducted while du Tillet is away: "sure to disobey me," du Tillet mutters to himself, already anticipating her defiance [002-044].

## The Controlling Husband

Du Tillet's control over Eugénie is absolute. He "took his wife's arm and, dragging her from Marie-Angélique's house" [002-036], reminds her of her subordination. "I forbid you to have anything to do with her" [002-042], he says, treating his wife as a possession rather than a partner.

When Marie-Angélique asks for financial help, du Tillet's response is revealing: the money is for *him* to dispense — "How much do you want?" [002-024] — and Eugénie's own wishes are irrelevant. His "honeyed acerbity" [002-022] is the voice of a man who equates marriage with ownership.

## Foil to Marie-Angélique

Eugénie functions as a foil to her younger sister. Where Marie-Angélique rebels (however secretly), Eugénie submits. Where Marie-Angélique dreams of romantic escape, Eugénie has stopped dreaming. "I should like to see you as happy as I am miserable," she tells her sister [002-033] — a line that inverts the expected formula and reveals her self-awareness.

Her warning is prophetic: Marie-Angélique's own marriage to [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]], while gentler, contains the same structural emptiness. The two [[marriage|marriages]] mirror each other — one cold and explicit in its cruelty, the other warm and implicit in its neglect.

## Significance

Eugénie represents a recurring Balzacian type: the aristocratic wife whose legal status as property renders her invisible. She is wealthy, beautiful, and utterly without agency. Her presence in the novel darkens the romantic plot — reminding us that for every Marie-Angélique who reaches toward passion, there is an Eugénie who has been crushed by the attempt.
