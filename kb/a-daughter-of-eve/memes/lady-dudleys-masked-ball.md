# Lady Dudley's Masked Ball

Lady Dudley's masked ball is the climactic event of *A Daughter of Eve* — the social occasion where the novel's converging plots of romance, finance, and political [[ambition]] reach their crisis point. The masquerade format, with its deliberate confusion of identities, provides Balzac with the perfect theatrical frame for a novel about the distance between appearance and reality.

## The Event

The masked ball is hosted by Lady Dudley, one of the "wealthy foreigners" who populated Parisian high [[society]] [003-027]. The guest list includes the novel's entire cast: Nathan, du Tillet, the Vandenesse sisters, and the network of aristocrats, bankers, and politicians who constitute the Restoration elite.

The masquerade convention structures the evening: attendees wear masks and domino cloaks, identities are deliberately obscured, and the usual social protocols are suspended — or rather, transformed into a game of recognition and misrecognition.

## Dramatic Function

### The Climax

The masked ball brings the novel's tensions to their breaking point. Nathan attends with Marie-Angélique, while du Tillet and his wife move through the same crowd. The physical proximity of the antagonists, combined with the anonymity of the masks, creates an atmosphere of barely suppressed danger.

The narrator describes Nathan "pacing the promenade at the masked ball, giving his arm to a domino" [009-062] — the image of purposeful movement through a disguised crowd captures the novel's central anxiety: in a [[society]] where everyone performs, how can anyone know the truth?

### The Unmasking

The masked ball's symbolic function is explicit. Throughout the novel, characters have worn masks — not literal ones but social ones: Nathan the celebrated artist (actually in debt), du Tillet the respectable banker (actually a schemer), Marie-Angélique the dutiful wife (actually in love with another man). The ball literalizes this theme.

The moment of reckoning comes when Mme. du Tillet confronts the truth about her husband's schemes. Her sister has come to her for help, and the masked ball provides the setting for their reconciliation — or its failure.

## Significance

### The Masquerade as Metaphor

| Element | Symbolic Meaning |
|---------|-----------------|
| The mask | Social performance; the gap between public face and private truth |
| The domino cloak | Uniformity of the aristocracy; the erasure of individuality |
| The promenade | Movement without destination; the circular nature of social ambition |
| The unmasking | Truth revealed; the collapse of performance |

### Counterpoint to the Concert

The masked ball forms a structural pair with Mme. de Montcornet's concert [see [[montcornets-concert]]]. The concert opened the novel's romantic plot — it was where Nathan and Marie-Angélique first met. The masked ball closes it — it is where the consequences of that meeting come due. Between these two events, the novel traces an arc from hopeful beginning to bitter resolution.

## Historical Context

Lady Dudley, as a wealthy Englishwoman in Paris, represents the international character of Restoration high society. The English aristocracy's tradition of masked entertainments — the masquerade — merged with French salon culture to produce hybrid events that were simultaneously social rituals and opportunities for intrigue.
