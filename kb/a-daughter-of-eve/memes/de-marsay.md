# de Marsay

De Marsay is the Prime Minister and head of government during the events of *A Daughter of Eve*. A figure of immense political authority and intellectual force, he presides over the party in power in the years following the [[july-revolution-1830|July Revolution of 1830]]. His death midway through the novel — he makes "his last appearance in society" at [[lady-dudleys-masked-ball|Lady Dudley's great ball]] [004-047] — triggers a governmental collapse that reshapes the political landscape and affects the fortunes of nearly every character.

## Head of Government

De Marsay is introduced indirectly, through the narrator's description of those who held power: "the great de Marsay, then at the head of affairs" [004-015]. He presides over a coalition that includes [[raoul-nathan|Rastignac]] and la Roche-Hugon, men who constitute "the party in power" [004-021]. Balzac's narrator treats him with a respect bordering on awe, presenting him as the supreme operator in a political world defined by calculation and maneuver.

His death is presented as a watershed moment. "Two months later he died, leaving the reputation of a statesman so profound that, as Blondet said, he was unfathomable" [004-047]. The word "unfathomable" is carefully chosen — de Marsay's genius was precisely that it could not be reduced to any simple formula or ideology. He was too deep for his contemporaries to sound.

The political consequences of his death reverberate through the second half of the novel. "Rastignac, who had fallen with the Government which went to pieces on de Marsay's death, leant upon Raoul" [007-024] — a single sentence that captures the vacuum he left behind. The ministerial crisis that follows opens the door for new alliances and realignments, creating the conditions in which Nathan's political ambitions briefly seem plausible.

## View of Literary Men

De Marsay's most revealing character trait is his contempt for authors. The narrator tells us directly that he "had a low opinion of authors as lacking in what Richelieu called the logical spirit, or rather in coherence of ideas" [004-015]. This is not mere aristocratic snobbery; it is a political judgment. For de Marsay, writers lack the one quality essential to statecraft: the ability to hold a consistent line over time.

He applies this judgment practically to Nathan. "Besides, no minister could have failed to reckon on Raoul's constant pecuniary difficulties which, sooner or later, would drive him into the position of accepting rather than imposing conditions" [004-015]. De Marsay's calculation is chilling in its accuracy: he sees that Nathan's financial vulnerability makes him inherently unreliable, a man who can be bought because he is always in debt. The novel's plot proves him right.

This view of authors places de Marsay in direct opposition to the Romantic cult of the writer as prophet and seer. In the world of *A Daughter of Eve*, where literary celebrity and political power are tangling in new and unstable ways, de Marsay represents the old guard's skepticism — the conviction that poets make bad politicians.

## The Political Triangle Quip

De Marsay's most memorable line in the novel is spoken not to Nathan but to Émile Blondet, the novel's chorus and political sceptic:

"You are a political triangle," said de Marsay, with a laugh, when he met him at the Opera; "that geometrical form is the peculiar property of the deity, who can afford to be idle; but a man who wants to get on should adopt a curve, which is the shortest road in politics." [004-022]

The quip is a masterpiece of political wisdom in miniature. Blondet, with his indecision and his refusal to commit to any party, is geometrically a triangle — stable, self-contained, but static. The deity may be a triangle because omnipotence has no need of movement. But a mortal who wishes to advance must adopt the curve: indirect, adaptable, never presenting a straight line for an enemy to intercept.

This aphorism encapsulates de Marsay's political philosophy. Success in politics is not about principles but about flexibility — the capacity to bend without breaking. The remark is also characteristic of his style: witty, patronizing, and devastatingly accurate. He says it "with a laugh," but the laughter contains a blade.

## At Mme. d'Espard's Salon

De Marsay's most sustained appearance occurs in Chapter VI, at the salon of Mme. d'Espard. He arrives as Prime Minister, his presence dominating the room. "The Prime Minister," says Mme. d'Espard, "with a glance at de Marsay, as she drew Nathan into the conversation, 'was just declaring, when you came in, that there is an understanding between the Royalists and Republicans'" [006-049].

Nathan, playing the Republican, answers boldly: "Supposing it were so, where would be the harm? The object of our animosity is the same; we agree in our hatred, and differ only in what we love" [006-050]. But de Marsay is not interested in debating Nathan's politics. His response cuts in a different direction entirely.

"The alliance is at least singular," said de Marsay, with a glance which embraced Raoul and the Comtesse Félix [006-051].

The glance is the point. De Marsay has seen instantly what no one else in the room has perceived — the connection between Nathan and Marie-Angélique. His remark is ostensibly about politics, but it is really about the private drama unfolding before him. He exposes the lovers with a single look, turning a political argument into a comment on their clandestine relationship.

When Marie demurs that she knows "nothing about politics," de Marsay's reply is sharp: "You will learn, madame, and then you will be doubly our enemy" [006-055]. The epigram is double-edged: she will learn politics from Nathan, her lover, and she will learn the ways of love — both forms of knowledge that will make her dangerous to the established order de Marsay represents.

Neither Nathan nor Marie "understood de Marsay's sally till he had gone" [006-056]. This is characteristic: de Marsay speaks in riddles that reveal themselves only in retrospect. He is always several moves ahead.

## Relationship with Rastignac

De Marsay's relationship with Rastignac is that of patron to protégé, though Balzac presents it elliptically. Rastignac is among those who constitute "the party in power" alongside de Marsay [004-021]. When de Marsay leaves Mme. d'Espard's salon, "Rastignac followed him" [006-056] — a small but telling detail that establishes Rastignac as de Marsay's political shadow, the younger man who attends the master.

Nathan's cruel joke after de Marsay departs confirms the dynamic: "Don't you see that he is at the last gasp? Rastignac is acting as nurse, and hopes to be mentioned in the will" [006-062]. The jest is vicious but not inaccurate. Rastignac's political career is built on proximity to de Marsay, and de Marsay's death leaves him politically orphaned.

After de Marsay dies, "Rastignac, who had fallen with the Government which went to pieces on de Marsay's death, leant upon Raoul" [007-024]. The verb "leant" is revealing: Rastignac, without de Marsay's support, must find a new prop. His turn to Nathan is a measure of his desperation, not his conviction.

## The Posthumous Tribute

De Marsay's influence extends beyond his death. Marie-Angélique, now deeply invested in Nathan's career, "advised Raoul to deal generously with de Marsay when he died, and she read with dizzy pride the fine dignified tribute which he paid the late minister, while deploring his Machiavellianism and hatred of the masses" [006-113].

The tribute is an act of political positioning. Nathan, the Republican critic, must now speak well of the dead Prime Minister — partly because magnanimity becomes a rising man, and partly because de Marsay's stature demands it even from his opponents. The phrase "Machiavellianism and hatred of the masses" captures the ambivalence de Marsay inspired: admired as a statesman, condemned as an enemy of the people.

## Significance in La Comédie Humaine

De Marsay belongs to the type of the supreme political operator that recurs across Balzac's novels. He is kin to Talleyrand — whom the narrator explicitly groups him with as "the leader of the sect" [004-053] — in his amorality and his genius. He represents the purest form of political intelligence in Balzac's world: entirely instrumental, unencumbered by conviction, seeing men as tools and ideas as counters.

His death mid-novel serves a structural function. It removes the figure of established authority, creating the vacuum into which lesser men — Rastignac, Nathan, du Tillet — rush. The novel's second half is, in part, a study of what happens when the unfathomable statesman is no longer there to maintain order. The scramble for his legacy is itself a comment on the fragility of the political settlement he embodied.
