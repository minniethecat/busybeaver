# Étienne Lousteau

Étienne Lousteau is a cynical Parisian journalist in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), described by the narrator as "the very incarnation of envy" [007-018]. A minor figure in this novel but a significant recurring character across [[la-comedie-humaine|La Comédie Humaine]], Lousteau belongs to the circle of writers, critics, and hangers-on who orbit [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]] and the actress [[florine|Florine]]. He embodies the corrosive spirit of the Parisian press — witty, resentful, and incapable of genuine loyalty.

## In Florine's Circle

Lousteau first appears in the roll-call of guests at Florine's apartment in chapter 5. Balzac enumerates the regulars: "Blondet, Finot, Étienne Lousteau, her seventh lover, who believed himself to be the first, Félicien Vernou, the journalist, Couture, Bixiou, Rastignac formerly, Claude Vignon, the critic, Nucingen the banker, du Tillet, Conti the composer" [005-014].

The parenthetical aside — "her seventh lover, who believed himself to be the first" — is a masterstroke of characterization in miniature. It establishes Lousteau as one in a long succession of men who have passed through Florine's life, but it also reveals his vanity: each of these men, Balzac implies, imagined himself uniquely significant to her, and each was mistaken. Lousteau's self-deception is at once comic and, in the novel's larger scheme, symptomatic of a world in which everyone overestimates their own importance.

Florine's salon is described as "a sort of brothel of wit, a galleys of the mind" [005-015], and Lousteau is part of the "diabolic legion of ferocious egotists in every walk of life" [005-014] who gather there. In this environment, friendship and enmity are indistinguishable and interchangeable: "Every member of this society hated or loved every other member according to circumstances" [005-015]. Lousteau fits perfectly into this moral ecosystem, where loyalties shift with every transaction and no one can be trusted for long.

## The Newspaper Venture

When Nathan decides to launch his political newspaper, Lousteau is recruited for a specific role. [[emile-blondet|Blondet]] outlines the editorial division of labor to Florine: "We will get a meeting here with Étienne Lousteau, who will do the light stuff, and Claude Vignon for the serious criticism" [005-043].

The assignment is revealing. Lousteau is given the "light stuff" — the feuilletons, the gossip, the entertainment journalism that filled the columns of nineteenth-century Parisian newspapers and provided their commercial appeal. He is not trusted with the serious criticism, which goes to [[claude-vignon|Claude Vignon]], nor with the political direction, which Nathan reserves for himself. He is, in the hierarchy of the paper, a hired hand — a witty pen for hire whose talents are useful but not respected.

This arrangement reflects Lousteau's position in the novel's social world more broadly. He is present, he is useful, he is even acknowledged as clever — but he is never central. He belongs to the supporting cast that makes the drama possible without ever taking the leading role.

## "The Very Incarnation of Envy"

Lousteau's defining moment in the novel comes in chapter 7, when [[du-tillet|du Tillet]] watches [[raoul-nathan|Nathan]] depart from Florine's apartment. Nathan climbs into his cabriolet, the picture of success — dandyish, self-assured, apparently master of his world. Du Tillet turns to Lousteau with a remark freighted with dramatic irony:

"There goes our dandy off to the Bois in tiptop style, and in six months he may be laid by the heels in Clichy" [007-018].

The narrator identifies Lousteau at this moment with a single devastating phrase: "the very incarnation of envy" [007-018]. The epithet does more than describe a character; it diagnoses a condition. Lousteau is not merely envious — he *is* envy, envy made flesh, envy as a mode of being. In Balzac's moral taxonomy, envy is a social emotion, produced by proximity and comparison. Lousteau envies Nathan because Nathan has what he does not: success, celebrity, a cabriolet, and the admiration of women.

Lousteau's response to du Tillet's prophecy is swift and defensive: "Not he! think of Florine" [007-019]. The reply reveals Lousteau's characteristic mode of reasoning. He does not defend Nathan out of friendship or loyalty but out of a kind of cynical calculation: Nathan is protected by his mistress's resources, and that protection insulates him from the consequences that would fell an ordinary man. Lousteau understands the world of the demi-monde well enough to know that an actress's fortune can buy a journalist time — but not well enough, or not loyally enough, to warn Nathan of the trap closing around him.

Du Tillet then turns the screw: "And how do you know, my good fellow, that he'll keep Florine? I tell you, you're worth a thousand of him, and I expect six months will see you in the editorial chair" [007-020]. The flattery is transparent and effective. Du Tillet plays on Lousteau's envy and ambition, holding out the prospect of Nathan's fall and Lousteau's rise — a dream that, in the event, Lousteau will never realize.

## Marie's Misreading

One of the novel's subtler ironies involves Lousteau's reception by [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse]]. In chapter 6, as she becomes increasingly absorbed in Nathan's world, she reads the newspaper every morning and "trumpeted the praises of Étienne Lousteau the feuilletonist, whom she thought charming" [006-113].

The detail is small but revealing. Marie, the sheltered aristocratic wife, reads Lousteau's light journalism and finds him "charming" — responding, in other words, to the surface wit and polish that are the feuilletonist's stock in trade. She does not see the envy, the cynicism, the corrosive resentment that defines him in the narrator's eyes. Her misreading of Lousteau is part of a larger pattern: she consistently misreads the journalistic world into which Nathan has drawn her, mistaking its cleverness for intelligence, its gossip for insight, and its performers for artists.

This blindness is not unique to Marie. It is, Balzac suggests, the blindness of her class — the aristocratic inability to see through the surface charm of the press to the moral vacancy beneath. Lousteau, who is nothing if not surface, is perfectly suited to exploit this blindness.

## Lousteau Across La Comédie Humaine

Although Lousteau is a minor character in *A Daughter of Eve*, he is a significant figure in the broader cycle of [[la-comedie-humaine|La Comédie Humaine]]. He appears in *Lost Illusions* (*Illusions perdues*) as a journalist who corrupts the provincial poet Lucien de Rubempré, initiating him into the venal world of the Parisian press. In *A Harlot High and Low* (*Splendeurs et misères des courtisanes*), he continues his career as a literary hack and occasional blackmailer.

In all of his appearances, Lousteau remains consistent: witty, envious, unreliable, and permanently embittered by his failure to rise above the minor journalism to which his talents — real but limited — have consigned him. He is Balzac's definitive portrait of the journalist as failed artist, the man whose gift for words has become a commodity to be sold by the column-inch, and whose envy of greater talents has curdled into a permanent condition of the soul.
