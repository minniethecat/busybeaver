# La Comédie Humaine

*A Daughter of Eve* (1839) belongs to Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*, the vast interconnected cycle of over ninety novels and stories that constitutes the most ambitious literary project of the nineteenth century. Understanding the novel requires understanding its place within this larger architecture.

## The Project

Balzac conceived *La Comédie Humaine* as a comprehensive portrait of French society from the Revolution through the July Monarchy. The cycle is organized into three major divisions: *Études de mœurs* (Studies of Manners), *Études philosophiques* (Philosophical Studies), and *Études analytiques* (Analytical Studies). *A Daughter of Eve* falls within the *Scènes de la vie privée* (Scenes of Private Life), a subset of the *Études de mœurs* that focuses on the domestic sphere — marriage, family, and the intimate consequences of social forces.

## Recurring Characters

One of Balzac's innovations was the system of recurring characters — figures who appear across multiple novels, their lives unfolding across the cycle like threads in a tapestry. [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse]]'s husband Félix is the protagonist of *Le Lys dans la vallée* (1835); her father the [[comte-de-granville|Comte de Granville]] appears as a magistrate in *Splendeurs et misères des courtisanes*. This interconnection means that *A Daughter of Eve* gains resonance from its place within the larger work — every character carries a history that extends beyond the novel's boundaries.

## Themes in Context

The themes of *A Daughter of Eve* — marriage, passion, social ambition — are central to the entire *Comédie Humaine*. In this novel, as across the cycle, Balzac treats love not as a private emotion but as a social force, shaped by money, class, and the inexorable logic of a society in transition from aristocratic to bourgeois values.
