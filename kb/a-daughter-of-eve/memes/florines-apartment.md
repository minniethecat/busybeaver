## Overview

Florine's apartment serves as an important setting in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). The residence of the actress [[florine|Florine]], shared with [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]], it represents the bohemian, demi-mondaine Paris of artists and journalists that contrasts with the novel's aristocratic salons.

## A Bohemian Interior

The apartment is a space of irregular hours, impromptu gatherings, and the traffic of journalists, writers, and theatrical figures. Nathan's literary circle congregates here — [[emile-blondet|Émile Blondet]], [[etienne-lousteau|Étienne Lousteau]], and others — to discuss the newspaper venture and navigate the intersecting worlds of journalism and politics. The atmosphere reflects Nathan's own character: brilliant, disordered, and perpetually on the edge of financial collapse [005-014].

## A Stage for Intrigue

Key conversations about Nathan's planned newspaper unfold in this setting, involving du Tillet's financial backing and Massol's legal participation. The apartment is thus a site of professional and political maneuvering, not merely a domestic space.

## Contrast with the Rue du Rocher

The contrast with the Vandenesse residence on [[rue-du-rocher|Rue du Rocher]] is structurally important. Marie-Angélique's world is one of order and respectability; Florine's is one of freedom, disorder, and frank sexuality. When Marie visits this apartment seeking Nathan, she crosses from one world into the other.

## In Balzac's Paris

Florine's apartment represents the Paris that Marie has been protected from knowing: the Paris of kept women, struggling artists, and the moral compromises of literary ambition. Its presence is part of Balzac's comprehensive mapping of Parisian social space.
