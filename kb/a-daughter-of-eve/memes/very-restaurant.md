## Overview

Véry's was one of the most celebrated restaurants of Restoration Paris, located in the arcades of the Palais-Royal. In *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), it is the setting for the morally charged supper at which [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]], [[eugene-de-rastignac|Rastignac]], and [[emile-blondet|Émile Blondet]] cynically discuss [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie de Vandenesse]] as a romantic conquest [004-032].

## The Supper Scene

After Lady Dudley's ball, Rastignac offers Nathan and Blondet a place in his carriage. "Suppose we sup at the expense of law and order?" Blondet proposes, and Rastignac takes them to Véry's [004-034]. Over champagne, the three men dissect the evening's events.

Blondet and Rastignac caricatured Marie's story, applying "the scalpel of epigram and the keen edge of mockery to that transparent childhood, that happy marriage" [004-034]. Blondet congratulates Nathan on finding a woman "cooked to a turn by marriage for the palate of love."

Nathan, intoxicated, responds with poetic pretension: "A pure and noble woman is like a spotless conscience... Elsewhere life is a wild frenzy, with her we breathe the peace, the freshness, the bloom of the oasis" [004-035]. Rastignac dismisses this: "shift the prayer of Moses on to the high notes, as Paganini does" [004-036].

## A Moral Theater

While Marie lies in bed "swathed in shame, terrified at the delight" she felt in Nathan's presence, these "three shameless spirits trampled on the tender white blossoms of her opening love" [004-039]. The scene exposes the gap between romantic illusion and cynical reality.

## Véry's in Balzac's Paris

Véry's was a real restaurant, famous in Balzac's day, located in the Palais-Royal. Its appearance is part of Balzac's documentary realism, anchoring fiction in actual geography. The restaurant represented the masculine world of politics and journalism from which women were excluded — a parallel sphere where reputations were made and unmade over dinner.
