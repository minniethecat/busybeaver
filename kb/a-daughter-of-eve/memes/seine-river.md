The Seine is the river that divides Paris into the Right Bank and Left Bank — a geographical fact that structures the social geography of Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). The Right Bank (*rive droite*) is the world of finance, commerce, and the fashionable boulevards; the Left Bank (*rive gauche*) is the world of the old aristocracy, the university, and bohemian poverty.

The Seine functions in the novel not merely as a geographical feature but as a symbolic boundary. The crossing from one bank to the other — from the Chaussée d'Antin to the Faubourg Saint-Germain, from Florine's apartment to the Vandenesse residence — maps onto the novel's larger crossings between worlds: between innocence and experience, between romantic illusion and disillusion, between the old France and the new.
