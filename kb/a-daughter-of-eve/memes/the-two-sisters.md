The relationship between the two Granville sisters — [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]] and [[marie-eugenie-du-tillet|Marie-Eugénie]] — forms the emotional foundation of Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). The novel opens with their shared history, traces their diverging paths, and culminates in the crisis that tests their bond.

## The Childhood Bond

The sisters were raised together under the severe piety of their mother, the [[comtesse-de-granville|Comtesse de Granville]]. Their shared education, their music lessons with [[schmucke|Schmucke]], and their mutual protection against their mother's coldness created a bond of unusual intensity. The novel's opening chapters establish this childhood world as a lost Eden — a time before marriage separated them and before the temptations of Paris tested their loyalty.

## Diverging Paths

Marie-Angélique married [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]], entering the world of aristocratic respectability. Marie-Eugénie married [[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]], a wealthy banker whose fortune was built on questionable dealings. The sisters' marriages map the two paths available to women of their class: the path of honor (Marie-Angélique) and the path of wealth (Marie-Eugénie).

## The Crisis of Loyalty

When Marie-Angélique becomes entangled with Nathan's debts, she turns to her sister for help. Marie-Eugénie, bound by old loyalty and perhaps by guilt over her own compromised position, assists in concealing the financial entanglement — an act that makes her complicit in her sister's deception of Félix. The crisis tests and ultimately reaffirms the sisterly bond, even as it exposes the moral complexities of family loyalty in a world where every relationship has its price.
