# The Two Marriages

The marriages of the Granville sisters form the structural backbone of *A Daughter of Eve*. Two sisters, two husbands, two fates — Balzac uses the contrast to explore the institution of marriage from every angle.

## The First Marriage: Marie-Angélique and Félix

Marie-Angélique's marriage to Comte Félix de Vandenesse is presented as a conventional aristocratic union. Félix, having "resolved to cut short by marriage pleasures which had begun to weary him," selects his wife as he might select a piece of furniture — for suitability, not for love (003-004). The marriage produces comfort without intimacy, security without passion. After six years, Marie-Angélique has become "sophisticated" in the worst sense: knowledgeable about the emptiness of her situation but powerless to change it (002-002).

## The Second Marriage: Marie-Eugénie and Ferdinand

Marie-Eugénie's marriage to the banker Ferdinand du Tillet follows a different logic. Where her sister's union is aristocratic and hollow, Marie-Eugénie's is bourgeois and functional. Du Tillet, a self-made man who understands that marriage is a business transaction, brings to it "the assiduity of a lover, a father, a master, and a husband" — a catalog that reveals the performative nature of conjugal affection in a society where every relationship is also an exchange (003-006).

## Counterpoint

The two marriages operate as thesis and antithesis: aristocratic versus bourgeois, passionate versus pragmatic, tragic versus successful. Balzac's insight is that neither model serves the women who inhabit it. Marie-Angélique is destroyed by her marriage's emotional vacancy; Marie-Eugénie is preserved by her marriage's emotional nullity. The novel offers no third option.
