# The July Revolution of 1830

The July Revolution of 1830 — the *Trois Glorieuses* of July 27–29 — is the historical event that divides the world Balzac describes from the world in which he writes. Though the characters of *A Daughter of Eve* move through Restoration society unaware of their approaching political horizon, Balzac's readers in 1839 know what follows: the barricades, the abdication, and the installation of a new monarchy.

## The Revolution

Charles X's increasingly reactionary policies — censorship of the press, dissolution of the Chamber of Deputies, restriction of the franchise — provoked three days of insurrection in Paris. The uprising succeeded in toppling the Bourbon monarchy and installing Louis-Philippe, the "Citizen King," at the head of a constitutional July Monarchy.

## Impact on the Novel's World

The revolution that Balzac's characters do not yet see would transform every institution they inhabit:

| Institution | Under Restoration | After 1830 |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| **The press** | Licenced, censored | Relatively free, expanded |
| **The aristocracy** | Dominant in society | Increasingly ceremonial |
| **Finance** | Rising but contained | Dominant, unchecked |
| **The court** | Bourbon, legitimist | Orléanist, bourgeois |

## Balzac's Retrospective Gaze

Writing in 1839, Balzac positions his narrative at a precise historical inflection point. His characters — the ambitious Nathan, the calculating du Tillet, the aristocratic Vandenesse brothers — are all, unknowingly, creatures of a dying order. The July Revolution is the event that will accelerate the forces already visible in the novel: the triumph of [[money]] over birth, of speculation over inheritance, of the journalist over the courtier.

## Connection to the Novel's Themes

The 1830 Revolution is the historical correlate of the novel's personal dramas:
- Just as du Tillet's [[du-tillets-financial-scheme|financial scheme]] destroys Nathan through invisible mechanisms, the Revolution destroys a dynasty through collective action
- Just as [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]] must choose between romantic illusion and conjugal reality, France must choose between reactionary nostalgia and bourgeois modernity
- The world after 1830 is a world where du Tillet's type — the financier without birth — will rule

## Aftermath

The July Monarchy (1830–1848) that followed the Revolution would prove to be the golden age of the French bourgeoisie — precisely the class whose ascendance Balzac chronicles with such ambivalent precision throughout *La Comédie Humaine*.
