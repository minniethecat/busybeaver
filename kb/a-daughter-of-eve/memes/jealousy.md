## Overview

Jealousy and envy pervade the social world of Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). The novel anatomizes these emotions across characters and classes, from Nathan's consuming envy of political rivals to the competitive jealousies of Paris salon society, revealing jealousy as one of the driving forces of ambition and social striving in Balzac's world.

## Nathan's Professional Envy

Raoul Nathan's character is fundamentally shaped by envy. The July Revolution of 1830 brought to power "a dozen authors, professors, metaphysicians, and historians" whose success "filled him with consuming envy" [004-015]. Nathan "belongs to the type of man who covets everything and looks on all success as a fraud on himself" [004-015]. This envy drives his political ambitions and poisons his satisfaction with his own considerable achievements.

Nathan's envy extends to the literary sphere, where he resents those who achieve the lasting reputation that eludes him. He "lost no opportunity of getting [his novel] puffed as the 'finest book of the period'" [004-013], a compensation for the recognition he knows he has not earned.

## Social Rivalry

The salons of Paris are theaters of competitive jealousy. The "jealousy-ridden age" [004-020] Balzac describes is one "in which a thousand personal rivalries, cloaking themselves under the name of schools, make profit out of their failures." Women compete for social precedence; hostesses compete for distinguished guests; aspiring figures compete for entry to exclusive circles.

The novel's minor characters enact this dynamic: Mme. de Montcornet's rigorous admission standards protect her salon from contamination by inferiors [004-002]; the unnamed women who notice Marie's reaction to Nathan and "did not escape her so-called friends" [004-024] represent the surveillance and competitive gossip of salon society.

## Romantic Jealousy

Romantic jealousy appears most acutely in Marie's discovery of Nathan's relationship with [[florine|Florine]]. The realization that Nathan maintains a long-standing liaison with an actress shatters Marie's illusion of exclusive devotion. Balzac treats this jealousy not as possessiveness but as the natural response to discovering that one has been deceived — Marie's jealousy is a moral awakening as much as an emotional wound.

## Jealousy and the Social Order

Balzac connects jealousy to the competitive structure of post-Revolutionary French society. The old order of inherited status has been replaced by a scramble for position: "When so many young ambitions start on foot to meet at the same goal, there must be competing wills, frightful destitution, and a relentless struggle. In this merciless combat it is the fiercest or the adroitest selfishness which wins" [004-020].

## The Gendered Dimension

Women's jealousy in the novel operates differently from men's. While men compete for power, wealth, and fame, women compete for social position and romantic attention. The Marquise d'Espard's interest in the Nathan-Marie affair carries an undertone of competitive satisfaction — the grande dame observing the fall of a younger, more innocent woman.
