# Vice

Vice in *A Daughter of Eve* is treated not as theological sin but as sociological fact. Balzac catalogues the moral degradations of Restoration Paris with the detachment of a naturalist — this is simply how the world works, and the interesting question is not how to escape vice but how to survive its pervasive presence.

## The Taxonomy of Vice

The novel presents vice as organized by profession and social position. The literary world has its characteristic degradations: Nathan "had made his badge" of the "hail-fellow-well-met with every vice, every degradation, every treachery" [004-017] — his Bohemianism is not liberation but a different kind of bondage. Financial vice, embodied by [[du-tillet|du Tillet]], is colder and more systematic: the banker's sins are calculated rather than passionate.

The novel's most striking image of this moral ecology is the salon, where "every taste, every vice, every opinion, finds a welcome" [005-015]. This is not tolerance — it is indifference. The salon is a moral marketplace where everything is for sale and nothing is condemned.

## Vice and Novices

The novel is particularly attentive to the relationship between vice and inexperience. [[raoul-nathan|Nathan]] is described as "like all novices" in his political enthusiasm [006-052] — the word "novice" recurs throughout Balzac's work as a diagnosis. Novices mistake the intensity of their feelings for the rightness of their judgments. They are vulnerable precisely because they believe in things.

This pattern extends beyond Nathan. Marie-Angélique is a novice in love, and her innocence makes her prey. The novel suggests that virtue without worldly knowledge is not virtue but vulnerability — a dangerous innocence that the machinery of [[society]] will inevitably exploit.

## Moral Beauty

Against this backdrop of pervasive vice, Balzac sets the concept of "moral beauty" [004-016]. The phrase is carefully chosen: "the grace of moral beauty and the language of self-respect" suggests a kind of virtue that is aesthetic rather than doctrinal. It is not about following rules but about maintaining a certain quality of being — a composure that vice corrodes.

This is the standard by which Marie-Angélique is ultimately measured. Her affair with Nathan is not condemned on religious grounds — Balzac has little use for conventional piety — but on aesthetic grounds. The affair makes her life uglier, narrower, more desperate. Vice in this novel is defined not by its departure from divine law but by its degradation of human dignity.
