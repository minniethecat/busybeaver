The Faubourg Saint-Germain was the traditional stronghold of the old French aristocracy in Balzac's Paris. On the Left Bank, centered on the Rue de Grenelle and Rue de Varenne, it was home to families like the Grandlieus, the Listomères, and other representatives of the *ancien régime* nobility. In *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), it is the social world to which the [[marquise-d-espard|Marquise d'Espard]] and [[duchesse-de-grandlieu|Duchesse de Grandlieu]] belong.

The Faubourg Saint-Germain stood in deliberate opposition to the Chaussée d'Antin on the Right Bank — old money versus new, birth versus wealth, exclusivity versus display. Balzac's novels chronicle the gradual erosion of the Faubourg's power as financial wealth displaced inherited status, a historical process registered in the novel's portrait of a society in transition.

Marie-Angélique's marriage to [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]] places her within this aristocratic world, but her temptation by [[raoul-nathan|Nathan]] — a man of talent without birth — represents the intrusion of the new Paris into the old.
