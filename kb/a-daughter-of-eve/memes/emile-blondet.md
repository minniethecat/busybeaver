# Émile Blondet

Émile Blondet occupies a singular position in *A Daughter of Eve*: he is the observer who sees everything and commits to nothing. A journalist and literary critic of formidable intelligence, Blondet moves through every circle of the novel — the salons of the aristocracy, the Bohemian revelry of Florine's apartment, the backroom dealings of du Tillet's financial schemes — yet remains aligned with no party, bound to no faction, and committed to no cause beyond his own sardonic clarity.

## The Man of Few Illusions

Balzac introduces Blondet in Chapter IV as "a very brilliant but also very indolent writer" who serves as Raoul Nathan's sponsor into the Comtesse de Montcornet's salon [004-002]. Unlike Nathan, whose ambition burns visibly and destructively, Blondet has made peace with his own obscurity: "Blondet too was a celebrity, but only among the initiated few; much made of by the critics, he was unknown to the general public. Blondet was perfectly aware of this, and in general was a man of few illusions." This self-knowledge — this refusal to deceive himself about fame, which he called "a poison best taken in small doses" — distinguishes him from every other ambitious figure in the novel.

His defining flaw is not ignorance but paralysis. Balzac captures this in a single devastating sentence: "Émile Blondet, handicapped by his fatal indecision and dislike of action where his own affairs were concerned, stuck to his trade of scoffer and took sides with no party, while on good terms with all" [004-021]. He is friend to Raoul, to Rastignac, and to Montcornet alike — a man who can sit at any table but commands none.

## The Political Triangle

The novel's master politician, de Marsay, diagnoses Blondet with memorable precision when they meet at the Opera: "You are a political triangle. That geometrical form is the peculiar property of the deity, who can afford to be idle; but a man who wants to get on should adopt a curve, which is the shortest road in politics" [004-022]. The epithet is perfect: Blondet's three-sided nature — connected to Republicans, Royalists, and the *juste milieu* alike — makes him geometrically stable but politically inert. He is the deity of the drawing-room, omnipresent and powerless.

This political scepticism is dramatized at the supper at Véry's, where Blondet joins Rastignac and Nathan in a scene of Rabelaisian mockery. Balzac notes the irony: "the three made merry over this conjunction of an eclectic Undersecretary of State with a fierce Republican and a political sceptic" [004-032]. Blondet is explicitly the "political sceptic" — the third term in a triangle that defines the novel's political geometry.

## Nathan's Confidant and Conscience

Blondet's most sustained role in the novel is as Raoul Nathan's friend, advisor, and occasional conscience. Their relationship spans the entire arc of Nathan's rise and fall, and Blondet's interventions mark its key turning points.

### The Literary Critic

In the earliest scene, when Nathan's great drama fails at the Théâtre Français, it is Blondet who delivers the wounding consolation: "One more such failure, and you will be immortal" [004-012]. The epigram is characteristic — witty, truthful, and utterly unhelpful to a man who needs money, not immortality.

### The Political Analyst

In Florine's boudoir, where the friends dissect the political situation of France in 1834, Blondet displays his true gift: analysis. "Blondet, the man of his day with most judgment, although, like a barrister unable to plead his own cause, he could use it only on behalf of others, was magnificent in these friendly discussions" [005-024]. His advice to Nathan is characteristically pragmatic — do not abandon the opposition too abruptly, build a left-centre position, and navigate the chaos of post-Revolutionary politics with strategic patience.

When Nathan, electrified by ambition, declares "I shall succeed!" despite having no capital, Blondet punctures him with monosyllabic realism: "You haven't a penny." When Nathan replies he will write a play, Blondet answers: "It will fall dead" [005-028, 005-030]. He is the voice of experience in a room full of delusion.

### The Voice of Doubt

Blondet's analytical mind makes him the only character who consistently questions du Tillet's motives. "How can one suspect a friend?" Nathan protests, "when now and again Blondet would give utterance to doubts, which were natural to his analytic turn of mind" [007-014]. Blondet sees the trap being laid for Nathan, but his characteristic flaw — that inability to act on his own judgment — prevents him from intervening effectively.

Even so, he offers moments of clear-sighted counsel. When Nathan borrows from the usurer Gigonnet at fifteen percent, Blondet provides the novel's taxonomy of usury: "He deserves your thanks. At twenty-five they cease to be gentlemen; at fifty, usury begins; at this figure they are only contemptible!" [007-033]. The remark is both a joke and a warning — and Nathan, characteristically, hears only the joke.

## The Chorus of Society

In the salons, Blondet functions as a kind of Greek chorus, commenting on the action with epigrams that clarify its meaning. When Nathan, at Mme d'Espard's, gives rein to his anger in "brilliant oratory" — "this Orlando-like rage, this cutting and slashing wit" — it is Blondet who restores proportion with a single line: "Hit out as much as you like, you can't clear the ring" [006-040]. The metaphor is that of a boxing match, and it reminds Nathan — and the reader — that social combat has rules the artist refuses to learn.

Later, when Nathan takes offense at the word "amusing," Blondet takes pity on him and delivers the novel's most explicit lesson in social conduct: "My dear fellow, you behave in polite society exactly as you might at Florine's. Here nobody flies into a passion, nobody lectures... love proceeds by rule. Either carry off Mme. de Vandenesse or behave like a gentleman. You are too much the lover of one of your own romances" [006-044]. The diagnosis is precise: Nathan has confused the world of art with the world of manners, and Blondet — who inhabits both — can see the difference.

## The Final Verdict

Blondet's last significant appearance comes after Nathan's ruin, when the defeated journalist unburdens his soul, discoursing of Laura and Beatrice and paraphrasing poetic laments for his lost love. Blondet's response is the novel's epitaph on Nathan's romantic pretensions:

"You're driveling, old boy. I grant you there was a pretty enough flower, only it has nothing to do with the soul; and instead of crooning like a blind man before an empty shrine, you had better be thinking how to get out of this scrape, so as to put yourself straight with the authorities and settle down. You are too much of the artist to make a politician. You have been played on by men who are your inferiors. Go and get yourself played on some other stage" [009-108].

The speech is a masterpiece of Blondet's art: brutal, accurate, and delivered with the affection of a friend who has watched the disaster unfold from the beginning and never once been surprised. When Nathan protests that "Marie can't prevent my loving her; she shall be my Beatrice," Blondet's rebuttal is definitive: "Beatrice was a child of twelve, whom Dante never saw again; otherwise, would she have been Beatrice? If we are to make a divinity of a woman, we must not see her today in a mantle, tomorrow in a low-necked dress" [009-109, 009-110]. The realist trumps the romantic with the simplest of arguments: proximity kills poetry.

## Significance in the Novel

Émile Blondet is the novel's diagnostician — the character who understands everything but changes nothing. His tragedy, if tragedy it can be called for a man so determinedly amused by life, is the tragedy of intelligence without will. He sees du Tillet's schemes, Nathan's delusions, Florine's vulnerability, and Marie-Angélique's danger with perfect clarity, yet his "fatal indecision" prevents him from intervening in any of them.

In this sense, Blondet embodies the moral condition that Balzac diagnoses across the *Comédie Humaine*: the paralysis of the observer in a society that rewards only action. He is the man who can "use judgment only on behalf of others" — a brilliant second to everyone's duel, and the principal in none of his own. His presence in the novel is a standing rebuke to Nathan's heedless ambition and a reminder that to see clearly is not the same as to live well.
