# Girodet's Portrait of Chateaubriand (1808)

Anne-Louis Girodet de Roussy-Trioson's portrait of François-René de Chateaubriand, painted in 1808 and now housed at the Musée d'Histoire de la Ville et du Pays Malouin in Saint-Malo, is one of the iconic images of French Romanticism. The painting depicts the author of *René* and *Le Génie du christianisme* in a pose of brooding meditation: his dark hair tousled by an unseen wind, his face half in shadow, and -- most memorably -- his right hand thrust into his open waistcoat, resting against his chest in a gesture that suggests both inner torment and heroic self-containment. This pose, at once studied and seemingly spontaneous, became one of the most imitated and parodied gestures of the nineteenth century.

## The Pose in Balzac's Novel

In *A Daughter of Eve*, the Girodet portrait appears at [004-004] as part of the exhaustive physical description of [[raoul-nathan]]. Balzac writes that Nathan "habitually thrusts one hand into his open waistcoat in the pose which Girodet's portrait of Chateaubriand has made famous, but not so much for the sake of copying Chateaubriand (he would disdain to copy anyone) as to take the stiffness out of his shirt front." The parenthetical disclaimer is crucial to the passage's ironic effect. Balzac simultaneously identifies the source of Nathan's gesture and grants Nathan the vanity of believing himself original. The hand-in-waistcoat pose, even as Nathan performs it, is already a citation -- a borrowed signifier of Romantic genius that has circulated through French visual culture for three decades.

## Girodet and Chateaubriand

Girodet (1767--1824) was one of the most original painters of the Napoleonic era. A student of Jacques-Louis David, he broke with his master's severe neoclassicism to explore more atmospheric, emotionally charged subjects. His *Sleep of Endymion* (1791) and *The Burial of Atala* (1808) -- the latter based on Chateaubriand's novella -- established him as a painter who could translate literary Romanticism into visual form. The portrait of Chateaubriand was commissioned at the height of the writer's fame, during the period when *Le Génie du christianisme* (1802) had made him the intellectual hero of Catholic revival and *René* had created the archetype of the Romantic melancholic.

The portrait presents Chateaubriand not as a man of letters at his desk but as a figure of almost Byronic alienation. The wind-blown hair, the shadowed eyes, the hand pressed to the chest -- all suggest a soul too large for its circumstances, a genius in exile from the ordinary world. The pose was so distinctive and so frequently reproduced through engravings that by the 1830s it had become a visual shorthand for literary genius, available for quotation by anyone who wished to signal artistic depth.

## Nathan's Borrowed Genius

The Girodet reference in *A Daughter of Eve* belongs to Balzac's systematic unmasking of Nathan's performative genius. Nathan has constructed his public identity from a repertoire of Romantic signs: the Byronic visage, the Napoleonic gaze, the medievalist costume, and the Chateaubriand pose. Each element is chosen for its legibility -- it communicates "genius" to an audience trained to read such signs. But Balzac's narrator insists on exposing the scaffolding. The hand in the waistcoat, we are told, is not there to express inner torment but "to take the stiffness out of his shirt front." The sublime collapses into the practical; the gesture of Romantic introspection reveals itself as a sartorial adjustment.

This deflation is characteristic of Balzac's realist method. Where Romanticism sees the external as a direct expression of the internal (the disheveled hair *is* the tormented soul), Balzac sees the external as a performance that may or may not correspond to internal reality. Nathan's genius is constructed, curated, and maintained -- a "magnificent stage-setting" (as the narrator describes the furnishings of Nathan's world) that impresses the naive observer but cannot survive the analytical gaze.

## The Portrait's Cultural Afterlife

The Girodet portrait's influence extended far beyond Balzac's novel. Throughout the nineteenth century, the hand-in-waistcoat pose was adopted by politicians, artists, and public figures seeking to project an air of thoughtful authority. Napoleon himself had been painted in a similar attitude by David (1812), though with the hand resting on the stomach rather than thrust into the waistcoat. By the time of *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), the pose had become sufficiently commonplace to function as a recognizable social signal -- a gesture that announced literary or intellectual pretension before the wearer spoke a word.

Nathan's adoption of the pose also carries a specific political charge. Chateaubriand was, by the 1830s, the grand old man of French legitimism -- a defender of the Bourbon cause, an enemy of the July Monarchy, and the author of the posthumous *Mémoires d'outre-tombe*. For Nathan, a Republican journalist who navigates between parties, borrowing Chateaubriand's pose involves no political commitment. It is merely another costume, like the medievalist affectations of "young France," that can be put on and taken off as circumstances require.
