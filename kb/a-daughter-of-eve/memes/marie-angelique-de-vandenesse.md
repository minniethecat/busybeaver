# Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse

The eldest daughter of the Comte de Granville, Marie-Angélique enters Balzac's novel as a young wife whose six years of marriage to Comte Félix de Vandenesse have left her emotionally starved and vulnerable to the first serious romantic temptation that presents itself.

## Role in the Novel

Marie-Angélique is the novel's moral and emotional center — a "daughter of Eve" whose fall from marital innocence structures the entire narrative. Her "early home" with her sister Marie-Eugénie was shaped by a mother whose severity prepared both daughters for the cold realities of aristocratic marriage without equipping them for the emotional demands of adult life (001-005). When she marries Félix de Vandenesse, she passes "at once from the maternal purgatory to the conjugal paradise, without suspecting that paradise might have its serpents" — a transition Balzac narrates with evident sympathy for her innocence and equal clarity about its inevitable destruction (003-006).

Her entanglement with the journalist Raoul Nathan is presented not as moral failure but as almost mechanical consequence: a woman denied emotional sustenance within marriage will seek it elsewhere. Her "feelings towards her husband" after the crisis reveal a character who has been forced to grow up rapidly, trading innocence for a bitter knowledge that is the novel's true subject (003-007).

## Social Position

As the wife of a count and daughter of another, Marie-Angélique occupies the upper tier of Parisian society. Her husband Félix "was one of the band of distinguished men who owed their fortune to the Restoration," giving her access to the political and social elite of the July Monarchy (003-004). Yet this position, far from protecting her, becomes the instrument of her confinement: the higher one stands in Balzac's Paris, the more visible one's transgressions become.

## Relationships

- **[[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Comte Félix de Vandenesse]]**: Husband. A political man whose absorption in public life leaves his wife emotionally abandoned. Their marriage is functional but hollow (003-004).
- **[[marie-eugenie-du-tillet|Marie-Eugénie du Tillet]]**: Younger sister and foil. Where Marie-Angélique succumbs to romantic temptation, Marie-Eugénie embraces pragmatic marriage to the banker Ferdinand du Tillet (001-022).
- **[[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]]**: The object of her romantic passion. A journalist and literary figure who represents everything her husband is not — artistic, passionate, dangerous.
- **[[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]]**: Brother-in-law. The calculating banker whose marriage to her sister represents the triumph of social ambition over romantic feeling.

## Character Arc

Marie-Angélique's trajectory follows the classic Balzacian pattern: innocence → temptation → crisis → disillusionment. She begins the novel "simple" in her expectations — a "girl of the type of Marie-Angélique and Marie-Eugénie" who has been shaped entirely by the forces around her (001-022). By the novel's end, she has been forced to confront the gap between romantic ideals and social reality, emerging neither triumphant nor destroyed but permanently altered.

## Significance in *La Comédie Humaine*

Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse appears in multiple Balzac novels as part of the interconnected Vandenesse-Granville family network. Her story in *A Daughter of Eve* is one episode in the larger chronicle of aristocratic decline that Balzac traces across *La Comédie Humaine*. Her husband Félix is a major figure in *Le Lys dans la vallée*, and her father the Comte de Granville appears in several works as a magistrate of the Restoration period.
