# *Ivanhoe* (1819)

*Ivanhoe: A Romance* is a historical novel by Sir Walter Scott, published in December 1819. Set in 12th-century England during the reign of Richard the Lionheart, the novel follows the dispossessed Saxon knight Wilfred of Ivanhoe as he navigates the racial and political tensions between Saxons and Normans. It was Scott's first novel to venture beyond Scottish settings into English medieval history, and it became one of the most influential works of nineteenth-century European literature, shaping the historical novel as a genre and inspiring generations of writers, painters, and composers across the continent.

## The Rebecca Reference in *A Daughter of Eve*

In Balzac's novel, *Ivanhoe* appears at a crucial moment in Raoul Nathan's seduction of Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse. At [004-057], the narrator describes how Nathan, deploying his full rhetorical arsenal, "unfolded the tale of his hardships, described his struggle with men and things, opened up glimpses of his unstable success, his political genius wasting in obscurity, his life unblessed by any generous affection." The passage continues: "Without a word directly to that effect, he conveyed to this gracious lady the suggestion that she might play for him the noble part of Rebecca in *Ivanhoe*, might love and shelter him."

This allusion is exquisitely calculated -- both by Nathan within the fiction and by Balzac as novelist. Rebecca of York, the Jewish heroine of Scott's novel, is a figure of beauty, intelligence, and moral courage who nurses the wounded Ivanhoe back to health after the tournament at Ashby. She loves him with a pure, selfless devotion, yet their love is impossible: Rebecca is Jewish, Ivanhoe is Christian, and Scott's medieval world offers no path to union. Rebecca tends to Ivanhoe, saves him, and ultimately departs into exile, her love unrequited but her dignity intact.

By casting Marie-Angélique as Rebecca to his Ivanhoe, Nathan flatters her vanity while masking his own predatory intentions. The comparison implies that Marie, like Rebecca, possesses a noble soul capable of redeeming a wounded warrior. It casts Nathan's financial and professional troubles as chivalric wounds suffered in honorable combat. And it frames their potential relationship in the language of medieval romance -- elevating what is, in truth, a calculating seduction into the realm of literary idealism.

## Scott's Influence on Balzac

Walter Scott exercised an enormous influence on Balzac and on the entire project of [[la-comedie-humaine]]. Scott had demonstrated that the historical novel could serve as a comprehensive portrait of a society, weaving together characters from every social station around a core of historical events. Balzac adapted this method to contemporary France, replacing Scott's medieval settings with the drawing-rooms, counting-houses, and newspaper offices of Restoration and July Monarchy Paris. The panoramic ambition of the *Comédie Humaine* -- its attempt to catalog every species of social type -- is unthinkable without Scott's example.

The *Ivanhoe* reference in *A Daughter of Eve* operates on multiple levels. Within the plot, it reveals Nathan's skill at literary flattery and Marie's susceptibility to romantic archetypes drawn from her reading. At the level of literary technique, it signals Balzac's debt to and distance from Scott. Where Scott's Rebecca is genuinely heroic and her love genuinely pure, Balzac's Marie-Angélique is being manipulated by a man who "knew his Shakespeare" and his Scott primarily as arsenals of seductive rhetoric. The gesture toward *Ivanhoe* thus becomes an ironic commentary on the distance between literary romance and Parisian reality.

## The Novel's European Reception

*Ivanhoe* was immediately and enormously popular across Europe. In France, it went through multiple translations and adaptations. The novel's medievalism fed directly into the Romantic fascination with the Middle Ages that Balzac satirizes in his portrait of Nathan as a member of "young France" -- those "exquisites, who swore by the Middle Ages" ([004-003]). Scott's influence permeated French theatre, opera (Rossini's *Ivanhoé*, 1826), and painting. By 1839, when *A Daughter of Eve* appeared, invoking *Ivanhoe* was a shorthand for a certain kind of romantic sensibility -- one that Nathan exploits and Balzac coolly dissects.
