# Devotion

Devotion in *A Daughter of Eve* is the novel's central moral category, and Balzac treats it with characteristic ambiguity. It is at once the highest virtue his characters can practice and the most dangerous — because devotion, once awakened, seeks an object, and the choice of that object determines everything.

## Devotion as Inheritance

Marie-Angélique's capacity for devotion is established before she appears as an adult. The Granville household is described as steeped in "the frost of devotion" [001-011], an image that captures both the rigor and the coldness of her religious upbringing. Devotion here is not warmth but discipline — a training of the soul that leaves it "impregnated with an atmosphere of devotion" [001-022].

This atmosphere is her mother's legacy. Marie-Angélique is "pickled in devotion, trained by Vandenesse" [004-034] — the verb "pickled" is striking. Devotion preserves but also arrests; it suspends the soul in a solution that prevents decay but also prevents growth. The training by her husband extends the pattern: she moves from one devotional regime to another.

## Devotion Transferred

The novel's central psychological event is the transfer of Marie-Angélique's devotion from God and husband to Nathan. What makes this transfer possible is the very strength of her devotional capacity — a woman less trained in devotion would not have had so much to transfer.

The countess's devotion to Nathan is described as "generous and absolute" [006-056]. The adjective "absolute" is the key — devotion, by its nature, does not admit of partiality. It is total or it is nothing. "For so many unnoticed acts of devotion, he found only the gentlest of reproaches" [006-069] — the asymmetry is painful: her devotion is enacted in unnoticed acts, his response in gentleness that falls short of reciprocation.

## Devotion to the Ideal

The novel offers a significant meditation on what devotion should be directed toward. "Marie, our devotion to the ideal is more ardent than ever" [007-058] — the phrase, spoken by a character who contrasts with Nathan, proposes that devotion properly belongs to the ideal, not to any mortal person. It is a critique of Marie-Angélique's mistake: she has given to a man what should be reserved for God, art, or principle.

## Political and Silent Devotion

Devotion in the novel is not only romantic and religious. "You know my devotion to the legitimist party" [009-006] shows devotion operating in the political sphere — loyalty to a cause, a party, a principle. This form of devotion is presented as more stable than romantic devotion, precisely because its object is abstract.

But the novel's most poignant insight concerns "silent devotion" [009-045] — devotion that goes "unrewarded." This is the devotion of wives who maintain households, of women who support men's careers from the shadows, of all those whose devotion is assumed rather than acknowledged. Balzac sees this clearly: silent devotion is the social order's unacknowledged debt.

## The Danger

The danger of devotion, in Balzac's treatment, is not that it exists but that it is indiscriminate. A soul trained to devotion will find something to be devoted to — and if the proper objects (God, duty, family) are unavailable or unsatisfying, it will attach itself to whatever appears worthy. Marie-Angélique's tragedy is not that she is devoted but that she was never taught to choose the object of her devotion wisely.
