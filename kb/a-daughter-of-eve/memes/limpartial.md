# L'Impartial

*L'Impartial* was one of the ephemeral newspapers of Restoration and early July Monarchy Paris — one of the many short-lived publications that flickered across the journalistic landscape of Balzac's era and that form the backdrop to the media world of *A Daughter of Eve*. The newspaper appears in the novel not as an active institution but as a point of reference within the crowded, competitive, and often short-lived ecosystem of the Parisian press.

## Role in the Novel

*L'Impartial* is mentioned in Chapter 5 ("Florine") during the description of the lavish celebration that marks the christening of Raoul Nathan's new political newspaper. The narrator, cataloguing the ephemeral names of forgotten journals, lists *L'Impartial* among them:

> The ceremony of christening the paper was then performed amidst floods of wine and wit, oaths of fidelity, of good fellowship, and of serious alliance. The name, forgotten now, like the *Libéral*, the *Communal*, the *Départemental*, the *Garde National*, the *Fédéral*, the *Impartial*, was something which ended in *al*, and was bound not to take. [005-052]

This passage places *L'Impartial* among a roster of short-lived newspapers whose names — all ending in "-al" — have faded from memory. The narrator's sardonic aside suggests that Nathan's own paper, whose name also ended in "-al," was destined for the same oblivion. The list functions as a memento mori for journalistic ambition: for every newspaper that survived to shape public opinion, dozens perished, leaving behind nothing but empty wine bottles and unpaid creditors.

The art concept page for the wiki lists *L'Impartial* alongside Nathan's paper in a table of artistic media, noting that journalism in the novel functions as a "political weapon, financial trap" [006-067]. The reference to *L'Impartial* in this context underscores the instrumental nature of the press in Balzac's world — newspapers were not primarily vehicles for information or ideas but instruments of political and financial manoeuvre.

## Historical Context

The Parisian press of the 1820s and 1830s was extraordinarily fertile and equally volatile. The relaxation of censorship following the July Revolution of 1830 produced a flood of new publications, many of them aligned with particular political factions, financial interests, or personal ambitions. Newspapers were founded, merged, renamed, and extinguished with dizzying speed. A title like *L'Impartial* — promising neutrality and objectivity — was characteristic of the period's journalistic rhetoric, even when the publication in question was anything but impartial in practice.

The name *L'Impartial* is also significant in the context of French press history. The claim to impartiality was a common marketing strategy for newspapers that sought to distinguish themselves from the openly partisan press of the Restoration. Yet in Balzac's world, such claims are invariably hollow. Every newspaper serves someone's interest, and the pretense of impartiality is merely another form of positioning.

## Significance in the Novel

The inclusion of *L'Impartial* in the catalogue of forgotten newspapers serves several functions in *A Daughter of Eve*. First, it establishes the temporal distance between the narrated events (set in the early 1830s) and the moment of narration — the narrator speaks of names "forgotten now," placing the reader in a present where these publications have already vanished. This retrospective irony is characteristic of Balzac's narrative voice, which constantly reminds the reader that the ambitions and struggles of the characters are set against the larger erosion of time.

Second, the list of "-al" newspapers creates a linguistic pattern that emphasizes the interchangeable, commodity nature of the press. One newspaper is much like another; their names blur together; their fates are identical. Nathan's paper, whatever its high-minded pretensions, is merely the latest entry in a long line of transient publications.

Third, the reference to *L'Impartial* contributes to the novel's broader anatomy of the press as an institution. Throughout *A Daughter of Eve*, journalism appears as a realm of moral compromise, financial instability, and political corruption — a world where talent is exploited, principles are traded, and survival depends on the willingness to serve whoever holds power or purse strings. The forgotten newspapers, *L'Impartial* among them, are the casualties of this system.

## The Press in La Comédie Humaine

Across *La Comédie Humaine*, Balzac returns repeatedly to the world of journalism, which he knew intimately from his own experience as a printer, publisher, and journalist. His portrayal of the press is almost uniformly dark: newspapers are engines of blackmail, vehicles of political manipulation, and graveyards of literary talent. *L'Impartial*, in its brief cameo in *A Daughter of Eve*, participates in this larger critique. Its very name — promising a neutrality that never existed — encapsulates the gap between journalistic pretension and journalistic reality that Balzac exposed throughout his work.
