The Rue de la Paix is one of the most elegant streets in Paris, running from the Place Vendôme to the Opéra. In Balzac's day as now, it was associated with luxury, fashion, and the commerce of refinement — the kind of street where a woman like [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie de Vandenesse]] might shop and where the material culture of aristocratic Paris was displayed.

The Rue de la Paix represents the commercial face of aristocratic Paris — a space where wealth is made visible through the display of goods, and where the boundary between old money and new is negotiated through taste. Its presence in the novel's implicit geography is part of the Balzacian map of a city where every street tells a social story.
