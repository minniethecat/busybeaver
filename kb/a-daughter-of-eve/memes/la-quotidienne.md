# La Quotidienne

*La Quotidienne* was a French legitimist newspaper that appeared in the context of *A Daughter of Eve* as one of the few periodicals permitted within the severe, religiously austere household of the Comte and Comtesse de Granville. Its presence in the novel is brief but telling — a single reference that illuminates the closed intellectual world in which the two Marie sisters were raised and against which they would eventually rebel.

## Role in the Novel

The reference to *La Quotidienne* occurs early in the novel, during the account of the Granville sisters' childhood and education. The narrator describes the stultifying atmosphere of the maternal household and the narrow circle of religious acquaintances who populated it:

> Their mother's friends, far from providing intellectual stimulus or calling forth their sympathies, only deepened the surrounding gloom. They were stiff-backed old ladies, dry and rigid, whose conversation turned on their ailments, on the shades of difference between preachers or confessors, or on the most trifling events in the religious world, which might be found in the pages of *La Quotidienne* or *L'Ami de la Religion*. [001-007]

The newspaper appears here as part of a portrait of intellectual and spiritual confinement. *La Quotidienne*, alongside *L'Ami de la Religion* (a Catholic ecclesiastical journal), defined the boundaries of acceptable discourse in the Granville household — a world where conversation was restricted to ailments, clerical personalities, and the narrowly religious news that these legitimist and Catholic publications chose to print. The two young girls, Marie-Angélique and Marie-Eugénie, were given no wider window onto the political, literary, or social life of Restoration Paris.

## Historical Background

*La Quotidienne* was founded in 1792, during the French Revolution, as a royalist counter-revolutionary journal. Its title — "The Daily" — belied its political commitments: it was one of the most consistently legitimist voices in French journalism throughout the Revolutionary and Napoleonic periods, surviving repression and censorship to emerge as a significant organ of ultra-royalist opinion during the Bourbon Restoration (1814–1830).

Under the Restoration, *La Quotidienne* aligned itself with the ultra-royalist faction — those who believed the restored Bourbon monarchy had not gone far enough in reversing the changes of the Revolution and who advocated for a return to the full prerogatives of throne and altar. The newspaper was closely associated with the Catholic revival that characterized the Restoration period, promoting the alliance of church and crown and defending the moral and political authority of the clergy. Its pages combined political polemic with religious commentary, literary reviews written from a Catholic perspective, and reports on the ecclesiastical world that so absorbed the attention of the Comtesse de Granville's elderly friends.

After the July Revolution of 1830, which overthrew Charles X and installed Louis-Philippe, *La Quotidienne* continued to publish as a legitimist opposition newspaper, now defending the cause of the exiled senior Bourbon line against the Orleanist monarchy. By the time *A Daughter of Eve* was published in 1839, the newspaper was thus a voice of the defeated legitimist cause, a remnant of the world that the July Monarchy had displaced.

## Significance in the Novel

The single mention of *La Quotidienne* serves a precise narrative purpose: it characterizes the intellectual environment against which Marie-Angélique and Marie-Eugénie must define themselves. The Granville household is not merely religious — it is intellectually sealed. The newspapers permitted within it filter the world through a legitimist and ultramontane Catholic lens that excludes everything Balzac's novel considers vital: literature, art, passion, ambition, the clash of ideas. When Marie-Angélique later encounters Raoul Nathan and the glittering, dangerous world of Parisian journalism and society, the contrast with the arid reading matter of her childhood is absolute.

The reference also anchors the novel in the political realities of the Restoration period. *La Quotidienne* was not a neutral choice of newspaper — it was a political statement. To subscribe to it and to permit only it signaled adherence to the ultra-royalist, counter-revolutionary position. The Comtesse de Granville's choice of reading matter is thus part of Balzac's larger portrait of her as a woman whose piety has hardened into a form of social and intellectual repression, a Jansenist severity that crushes the spirits of her daughters even as it claims to protect their souls.

## The Press in Balzac's Paris

*La Quotidienne* occupies a specific place in the rich journalistic ecology of Balzac's Paris. While the novel's plot revolves around the newer, more commercially aggressive press — the newspapers that Raoul Nathan founds, the journalism that serves as a springboard to political power — *La Quotidienne* represents the older world of ideologically committed, politically aligned journalism. It is the press of the Restoration aristocracy and clergy, not of the rising bourgeoisie; the press of conviction, not of commerce. Its brief appearance in the novel serves as a reminder of the world the Granville sisters leave behind and of the intellectual impoverishment that made them vulnerable to the seductions of a more glamorous but equally dangerous world.
