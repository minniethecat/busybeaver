# Schmucke

Schmucke (Wilhelm Schmucke) is the German music-master whose luminous goodness forms one of the quiet moral centers of Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve*. An elderly musician living in extreme poverty on the Rue de Nevers, he appears only briefly in the narrative but leaves an indelible impression on the two sisters who adore him and on any reader who recognizes in him a secular saint.

He signs four promissory notes at Marie-Angélique's tearful entreaty, an act of blind devotion that nearly destroys him. He performs this act without ever understanding its purpose or its danger. In the architecture of Balzac's vast fictional universe, Schmucke is a recurring figure of transcendent innocence, most famously as the title character of *Le Cousin Pons* (1847).

## A German Musician in Paris

Schmucke enters the Granville household as the music-master to the two young Maries when the elder is ten years old. The Comte de Granville, recognizing the inadequacy of the "spectacled lady, who professed sol-fa and the piano at a neighboring convent," insisted on finding a proper master [001-008].

The man who answered this call was an extraordinary figure. Balzac describes him as "a Catholic German, one of those men who are born old and will always remain fifty, even if they live to be eighty" [001-010]. His credentials were impressive: he had been "formerly choirmaster to the Margrave of Anspach, a learned man who underwent inspection from a council of piety" [001-010].

When asked whether he fasted, he was "tempted to reply, 'Look at me!'" — a response he wisely suppressed. The narrator adds dryly: "it is ill work jesting with saints and Jansenist confessors" [001-010]. The passage distills Schmucke's essence: a man of genuine learning and piety who cannot help but see the absurdity in the pious pretensions of others.

Balzac's physical description reveals the fusion of body and soul that defines this character. "His hollowed, wrinkled, swarthy face had kept something childlike and simple in its darkest folds. The blue of innocence sparkled in his eyes, and the gay smile of spring dwelt on his lips" [001-010].

His gray hair "fell in natural curls, like those of Jesus Christ," adding to his "ecstatic air a vague solemnity which was highly misleading, for he was a man to make a fool of himself with the most exemplar gravity" [001-010]. The Christological imagery establishes Schmucke as a holy fool whose unworldliness is the visible form of his sanctity.

His clothes were "a necessary envelope to which he paid no attention, for his gaze soared too high in the clouds to come in contact with material things" [001-010]. His hands "were of the kind which look dirty after washing." His body gave "ocular proof how far a man's body can become a mere accessory to his mind" [001-010].

Balzac concludes the portrait with a nod to Hoffmann, "a German, who has made himself the poet of all which appears lifeless and yet lives" [001-010] — acknowledging that Schmucke belongs to a category of being that realist fiction struggles to capture.

## The Music-Master and His Saint Cecilias

Schmucke's relationship with Marie-Angélique and Marie-Eugénie is the novel's single instance of love without transaction, devotion without calculation. In the "profound solitude" of their childhood, hemmed in by maternal severity and the frost of Jansenist devotion, the two girls found in Schmucke a figure of uncomplicated warmth [001-011].

"This apocryphal old man held so large a place in the life of the two Maries — they became so much attached to the great simple-minded artist whose sole interest was in his art — that, after they were married, each bestowed on him an annuity of three hundred francs, a sum which sufficed for his lodging, his beer, his pipe, and his clothes" [001-011].

The annuities reveal the sisters' enduring gratitude, but the deeper revelation is Schmucke's own feeling. "Six hundred francs a year and his lessons were a Paradise for Schmucke" [001-011]. He had confided his poverty and his hopes to no one except "these two charming children, whose hearts had blossomed under the snow of maternal rigor" [001-011].

Balzac remarks that "this fact by itself sums up the character of Schmucke and the childhood of the two Maries" [001-011]. The mutual recognition — the poor old man confiding in the poor rich girls — forms the emotional foundation of everything that follows.

Schmucke called them his "Saint Cecilias," after the patron saint of music [001-013]. His lessons opened a world that the maternal convent had sealed shut. "Melody, harmony, and composition, those three daughters of the skies, rewarded their labors, making a rampart for them with their aerial dances" [001-013].

The narrator calls him an "old Catholic faun, intoxicated by music" who "led the chorus" [001-013]. The phrase captures Schmucke's paradox — pagan in his joy, Catholic in his discipline, and childlike in both. Mozart, Beethoven, Haydn, and other masters "developed in them sensations which never passed beyond the modest limit of their veiled bosoms" [001-013].

Once the Comtesse de Granville's endorsement became known, Schmucke's reputation spread rapidly. "Schmucke had thirty houses in the Marais" [001-012]. This modest prosperity "displayed itself in slippers with bronze steel buckles and lined with horsehair soles, and in a more frequent change of shirt" [001-012].

His "childlike gaiety, long repressed by an honorable and seemly poverty, bubbled forth afresh" [001-012]. He would tell his pupils, in his inimitable Germano-Gallic lingo: "Younc ladies, de gads subbed off de dirt off Barees" — a joke about cats eating Paris mud after a frost that he delivered with "an air of such ineffable roguishness in presenting it that mockery was disarmed" [001-012].

The narrator adds that "there was no commonplace so vulgar that the warmth of his heart could not infuse it with fresh meaning" and that "the radiance of his smile might have turned the mire of the highway to gold" [001-012].

## The Signature: Innocence Conscripted

The pivotal scene occurs in Chapter VIII. Marie-Angélique, desperate to save Raoul Nathan from debtors' prison, follows her sister's suggestion and visits her old master to obtain his signature on four bills of exchange, each for ten thousand francs [008-017].

Marie-Eugénie had told the Baronne de Nucingen that her sister "wanted to do a kindness to our old music-master, a German who was in trouble" [008-017]. The fiction inverts the truth: it is Schmucke who will do the kindness, and Schmucke who will be put in trouble by it.

The visit to "the little Rue de Nevers, on the Quai Conti" [008-019] is one of the great set-pieces of Balzacian realism. The Countess "almost leapt from the step of the carriage on to the dirty and dilapidated entrance of the grimy old house, which was held together by iron clamps, like a poor man's crockery" [008-041].

Inside, on the fourth floor, she discovers an existence "such as that suddenly laid bare to her, though she had long known Schmucke's scorn for appearances" [008-042]. Balzac calls him "a musical Diogenes" who "felt no shame for the hugger-mugger in which he lived; indeed, custom had made him insensible to it" [008-042].

The room is a symphony of squalor. The German pipe has yellowed the ceiling. The cat Mirr, named "for do glorivy our creat Hoffmann at Berlin" [008-062], does the honors of the house. The piano "cased in a good wood, painted black and gold" has keys "worn like the teeth of an old horse" [008-043].

The brick floor is "strewn with dried mud, torn paper, pipe ashes, and odds and ends that defy description" [008-043]. "Chestnut parings, scraps of apple peel, and shells of Easter eggs" form "a carpet of dusty filth" [008-043]. Two armchairs, a flattened couch, a worm-eaten chest of drawers, and a maimed table complete the furniture, "unpretending as that of a Mohican wigwam" [008-043].

Yet Schmucke's presence transfigures the squalor. "Schmucke's eyes and smile transformed the sordid scene with heavenly rays, that gave a glory to the dingy tones and animation to the chaos. The soul of this man, who seemed to belong to another world, radiated light like a sun" [008-045].

When Marie-Angélique asks him to sign, he replies with terrifying alacrity: "Qvick; qvick! qvick! qvick! ... vot is dis dat I mosd to? Mosd I to de teufel go?" [008-055]. He signs "with a lamblike docility" [008-057].

The narrator observes that "the good man signed with the innocence of a child doing its mother's bidding, utterly ignorant what it is about, but sure that all will be right" [008-063].

Schmucke is "far more taken up with presenting the cat to the Countess than with the papers, which, by the laws relating to foreigners, might have deprived him forever of liberty" [008-063]. He asks only: "I ask if dese leetl stambed babers vill plees do Montame ti Dilet?" [008-066].

The Countess assures him they will. He replies: "I am fer habby do pe coot do her for zomting. Com, do mein music!" [008-068]. His hands fly over the worn keys, and "in a moment the sweetest of songs blossomed in the air and penetrated the soul" [008-069].

The scene is a cruel and beautiful inversion. The purest soul in the novel is made an unwitting accomplice to its central deception, and he plays celestial music while the ink dries on the instruments of his own potential ruin.

## Poverty, Devotion, and the Holy Fool

Schmucke's poverty is not incidental to his character but constitutive of it. He lives without thought for the morrow, without storehouses, without anxiety. His room is a "den of refuse" that becomes "a temple for two goddesses" when his former pupils visit [008-082].

When Marie-Angélique, bills secured, prepares to leave, Schmucke stands like "a faithful dog, waiting for a word" [008-072]. She apologizes for the brevity of her visit. He replies with gentle reproach: "Efer de zame! ... Go den! try de tears ov oders! Know dat de poor Schmucke counts your fisit for more dan your pounty" [008-074].

The meaning is unmistakable: you come only when you need something, and I love you enough to accept that. She promises to see him every Sunday. He answers that his "habbiness vill be den gomplete, vor I tid not zee you put at de Champes-Hailysées, ven you passed in de carrisch, fery rarely" [008-078].

This quiet revelation — that he has been watching for her from afar, receiving nothing, expecting nothing — is devastating. When she asks if he sometimes thinks of them, he answers: "Efery time ven I mein pret eat! ... Virst ass mein pountivul laties, ant den ass de two virst young girls vurty of luf dat I haf zeen" [008-081].

The Countess dares say no more. The narrator adds: "There was a marvelous and respectful solemnity in these words, as though they formed part of some religious service, breathing fidelity" [008-082]. Schmucke then stands "transfixed long after the carriage had disappeared" [008-084].

The image is definitive: stationary devotion in a novel of constant motion, the one character who remains exactly where he has always been, loving what he has always loved. His cat Mirr, his pipe, his piano, and his memories are sufficient wealth.

The sisters, at twelve years of age, had "given up making fun of Schmucke" and begun to understand the sorrows around them [001-018]. To outgrow mockery of Schmucke is to begin to understand suffering. The music-master, in his absurdity and his holiness, is the novel's quiet pedagogue in the school of compassion.

## Aftermath and Near-Destruction

The bills enter the machinery of Parisian finance. When the cashier at Mme. de Nucingen's bank examines the signatures, he notes: "Dis Schmucke iss ein musician vrom Ansbach" [008-096] — a remark "which sent a shiver through the Countess," for it reminds her that Schmucke's foreign status makes him especially vulnerable to the law.

The crisis is averted only by the intervention of [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Comte Félix de Vandenesse]], who discovers the scheme. In the great recognition scene of Chapter IX, he "drew from his pocket her guarantee and Schmucke's four bills, which the Countess recognized, and threw them into the fire" [009-044].

The burning of the bills is the symbolic annulment of Marie-Angélique's most profound betrayal — not of her husband, but of her music-master. Later, a reference to "Florine's possession of Schmucke's guarantee — plainly through the intervention of Vandenesse" [009-106] suggests how close the old man came to catastrophe.

Schmucke himself never learns what was done in his name. He remains in his garret, with his cat, his pipe, and his memories — the novel's one inviolate soul. That the Countess forgets his lessons when passion blinds her is the novel's central tragedy. That he forgives her without knowing he has anything to forgive is its central grace.

## Schmucke in *La Comédie Humaine*

Schmucke is one of Balzac's recurring characters, appearing across multiple novels of [[la-comedie-humaine|*La Comédie Humaine*]]. His most significant role comes in *Le Cousin Pons* (1847), where he is the title character's closest friend and the novel's moral anchor.

In that later work, Balzac develops fully the themes only sketched in *A Daughter of Eve*: the vulnerability of innocence in a predatory world, the sanctity of male friendship, and the peculiar cruelty with which society treats those who possess nothing but goodness.

Within *A Daughter of Eve* itself, Schmucke serves a precise structural function. He is the counterweight to every other major character: to [[du-tillet|du Tillet]]'s calculation, to [[raoul-nathan|Nathan]]'s vanity, to Marie-Angélique's romantic self-deception, even to Félix's worldly wisdom.

He alone asks for nothing, schemes for nothing, and understands nothing of the plots that swirl around him. His Germano-Gallic lingo — "de boor peast vich lifs mit de boor Schmucke" — marks him as permanently foreign, not merely to France but to the entire world of social calculation that the novel anatomizes [008-057].

Schmucke belongs to that "generous race of the absentminded, who give their time and their hearts to others, just as they drop their gloves on every table, their umbrellas at every door" [001-010]. In Balzac's taxonomy of human types, he represents the [[innocence|innocent]] — not naive, but radically and willfully unworldly.

His [[devotion]] is absolute because it expects nothing. His art is pure because it serves no ambition. In a novel where every other character is compromised by desire — for money, for status, for love, for power — Schmucke desires only to be permitted to love and to play.

He is, in the deepest sense, the novel's answer to its own question: what would a human being look like who had never eaten of the fruit of knowledge?
