# Beauty

Beauty in *A Daughter of Eve* functions as both gift and burden. For the novel's women — [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]], [[florine|Florine]], Eugénie — physical attractiveness is the primary currency they possess, and Balzac examines its circulation with the same analytical precision he brings to [[money|finance]].

## Beauty as Capital

Marie-Angélique is introduced with "more than the promise of beauty" [P01-002] — the phrasing is significant. Beauty is a promise, an asset whose value is realized only in social exchange. Her "speaking smile" proclaims what her social position forbids her to say directly. Beauty is the vocabulary of the unspeakable.

For Florine, beauty is more explicitly transactional. "With her beauty, Florine had remained seven years on the stage" [005-010] — her career depends on her appearance. The novel is clear-eyed about this economy: beauty is work, requiring maintenance and yielding returns. Florine understands this; Marie-Angélique does not, and her innocence about her own beauty's function is part of her tragedy.

## Moral Beauty

Balzac distinguishes between physical and moral beauty throughout the novel. "The grace of moral beauty and the language of self-respect" [004-016] describes a quality that has nothing to do with physical appearance. It is composure, dignity, the capacity to remain intact amid the corruptions of [[society]].

This distinction structures the novel's moral hierarchy. Marie-Angélique possesses both physical and moral beauty — but the latter is threatened by her entanglement with Nathan. Florine possesses physical beauty but the novel is ambivalent about her moral beauty. The married women of the salons possess neither — their beauty has been consumed by their [[marriage|marriages]].

## Beauty and Social Class

The novel maps beauty onto the social hierarchy. The Granville daughters' beauty is initially hidden — their mother's regime "instead of setting off their beauty, reminded one of an Egyptian mummy" [001-014], a startling image that equates parental control with aesthetic death. It is only after marriage that Marie-Angélique's beauty blooms — and the novel implies that this blooming coincides with her awakening to [[passion|desire]].

Florine's beauty has a different class signature: "the refinement of her beauty allied her to the upper classes" [005-012]. Beauty can cross class boundaries — it is the one form of capital that is genuinely portable. But the novel never lets us forget that this portability is precarious. Beauty fades; class endures.

## The Trap of Beauty

The novel's deepest insight about beauty is that it traps women in a paradox. To be beautiful is to be visible, and to be visible is to be vulnerable. "Any woman, astonished at her beauty, might have greeted her" [008-043] — the phrase captures the double bind. Beauty astonishes, but astonishment is a form of objectification. The beautiful woman is always being looked at, never looking back.
