# Molière as Literary Influence

Jean-Baptiste Poquelin, known as Molière (1622--1673), is the supreme figure of French classical comedy. His plays -- *Tartuffe*, *Le Misanthrope*, *L'Avare*, *Le Bourgeois gentilhomme*, *Les Femmes savantes* -- defined a tradition of social satire rooted in the observation of character types and the exposure of hypocrisy. For Honoré de Balzac, Molière represented both a model and a measuring-rod: the great precursor whose comprehensive portrait of seventeenth-century French society anticipated the ambitions of [[la-comedie-humaine]]. In *A Daughter of Eve*, Molière is invoked repeatedly, always as a standard against which characters and their pretensions are judged.

## The Éliante Reference: Slouching Toward Genius

The first Molière allusion is embedded in Balzac's initial description of [[raoul-nathan]] at [004-004]: "He attracts attention by his negligee if one may borrow from Molière the word used in Éliante to describe personal slovenliness." Éliante is a character in Molière's *Le Misanthrope* -- Célimène's cousin, a woman of good sense and genuine feeling who admires Alceste's sincerity even as she recognizes his excesses. In Act II, scene 4, Éliante delivers a speech about love's tendency to transfigure the beloved's faults into virtues: the thin lover is "svelte," the stout one has "a noble bearing," and the slovenly one is praised for his "negligee."

Balzac's invocation of Éliante's word is characteristically double-edged: it dignifies Nathan's slovenliness with a classical pedigree while reminding the reader that the word belongs to a character who sees through the very delusion she describes.

## Alceste and Philinte: Nathan's Double Nature

The most analytically significant Molière reference comes at [004-016], where the narrator dissects Nathan's character: "None knows better how to play at sentiment or to deck himself out in a shoddy greatness. The grace of moral beauty and the language of self-respect are at his command, he is a very Alceste in pose, while acting like Philinte." This single sentence encodes a devastating moral judgment accessible only to readers familiar with *Le Misanthrope*.

Alceste, the "misanthrope" of the title, is a man of inflexible honesty who refuses to flatter, dissemble, or compromise. He loves Célimène despite her worldliness and demands a sincerity that polite society cannot accommodate. Philinte, Alceste's friend, is the voice of pragmatic accommodation -- he accepts social conventions as necessary lubricants for human intercourse, preferring politeness to confrontational truth.

Nathan's genius for self-presentation consists in adopting the *pose* of Alceste (the noble truth-teller, the man too pure for the world's corruptions) while *acting* like Philinte (the compromiser, the accommodator, the man who knows how to work the system). The "painted cardboard" of Nathan's Alceste persona conceals a Philinte-like selfishness that "ambles along" toward its objectives. Balzac's use of Molière here performs a critical surgery, cutting through Nathan's self-dramatization to reveal the cynicism beneath.

## Molière as Moral Touchstone

Molière's name appears three more times in the novel, each instance reinforcing his function as a standard of judgment. At [004-020], discussing the competitive viciousness of literary Paris, the narrator notes: "spite of bawling, as Molière would say, it acquits and follows suit." The phrase evokes Molière's recurring theme of self-interested hypocrisy dressed in moral language. At [005-003], in a digression on the nature of actors, the narrator pairs "Molière and Talma" as examples of great artists who "loved more passionately in their old age than is usual with ordinary mortals."

And at [005-011], describing the actress Florine, we read of her "slender nose, indented by pink, quivering nostrils, which seemed to breathe the sarcasm and mockery of Molière's waiting-maids" -- those clever, impertinent servants (Dorine in *Tartuffe*, Toinette in *Le Malade imaginaire*) whose sharp tongues speak truths their social superiors cannot.

## "Not a Molière Yet"

The last and perhaps most cutting Molière allusion comes at [006-120], during a salon conversation about Nathan's theatrical work: "'M. Nathan is not a Molière yet, but--' said Mme. d'Espard, with a look at the Countess." The unfinished sentence, with its pointed look toward Marie-Angélique, enacts the social cruelty of the salon. To say someone is "not a Molière yet" is to damn with the faintest possible praise -- it acknowledges ambition while foreclosing achievement, placing Nathan in the vast category of writers who will never reach the summit implied by the comparison. The "yet" is the blade: it pretends to hold open a possibility that the speaker knows is closed.

## Molière and Balzac's Realism

Balzac's persistent reference to Molière throughout *A Daughter of Eve* serves a larger purpose within the aesthetic program of [[la-comedie-humaine]]. Molière had demonstrated that comedy could serve as a comprehensive social anatomy, that types and individuals could be rendered with equal vividness, and that the exposure of hypocrisy was among the highest callings of literature. Balzac, writing two centuries later, adapts Molière's comic vision to the novel form, creating characters who are both types (the journalist, the banker, the ambitious wife) and individuals whose psychological depth exceeds anything in classical comedy. The Molière references in *A Daughter of Eve* are not decorative -- they are structural, anchoring Balzac's realist project in the great tradition of French moral observation.
