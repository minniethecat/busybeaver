# Mademoiselle des Touches

Mademoiselle des Touches — known to the literary world as Camille Maupin — is one of the most remarkable female characters in Balzac's [[la-comedie-humaine|La Comédie Humaine]]. A writer of serious talent and a hostess of one of the most intellectually distinguished salons in Paris, she occupies a uniquely independent position in Restoration society: a woman who has achieved, through genius and force of will, the artistic autonomy normally reserved for men. In *A Daughter of Eve*, she appears in a minor but significant role as one of the voices of worldly wisdom whose conversation helps shape the romantic education — and undoing — of [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse]].

## In A Daughter of Eve

### The Salon Debate on Love and Marriage

Mademoiselle des Touches makes her most vivid appearance in chapter three, during a salon conversation in which several grandes dames debate the relative merits of marriage and love. When [[lady-dudley|Lady Dudley]] declares that "Marriage, my child, is our purgatory, love is paradise" [003-022], des Touches delivers a sharp rejoinder:

> "Don't believe her," cried Mlle. des Touches, "it's hell!" [003-023]

This single exclamation encapsulates the character's hard-won wisdom. Unlike the other women in the circle, des Touches speaks from the position of one who has lived independently, made her own way in the world of letters, and paid the price for freedom. Her voice cuts through romantic idealization — she does not celebrate love as paradise nor marriage as purgatory, but names both for what they can become: a form of suffering.

The exchange sets the tone for the entire novel's treatment of romantic love. The women who follow — the Marquise de Rochefide with her talk of "a hell with love in it" and martyrs, the [[marquise-d-espard|Marquise d'Espard]] with her psychological distinctions — are all elaborating on a theme that des Touches has crystallized in a single phrase.

### The Salon as Institution

Beyond her spoken line, Mademoiselle des Touches appears in the novel's social cartography as one of the select hostesses whose drawing-rooms remained open during the post-1830 period. Balzac lists her salon alongside those of the [[marquise-d-espard|Marquise d'Espard]], Mme. de Listomère, and the [[duchesse-de-grandlieu|Duchesse de Grandlieu]] [003-027], [004-002]. Her inclusion in this list is significant: unlike the others, whose prestige derived from aristocratic birth, des Touches commanded respect through intellectual achievement. Her salon bridged the worlds of art and aristocracy, offering a space where writers and thinkers could mingle with the old nobility on terms approaching equality.

This positioning makes her a transitional figure in Balzac's social taxonomy. The des Touches salon is neither as mixed as the [[comtesse-de-montcornet|Montcornet]] gatherings — which welcomed "commercial princes" alongside artists — nor as rigidly exclusive as the Grandlieu drawing-room. It represents a third term: the intellectual salon, governed by merit rather than birth, where the old hierarchies were suspended in deference to talent.

## Across La Comédie Humaine

Mademoiselle des Touches is a major recurring character across Balzac's cycle, appearing prominently in *Illusions perdues*, *Béatrix*, and *Autre étude de femme*. She writes under the pseudonym Camille Maupin and is widely recognized as one of the most gifted authors of her generation — a fictional counterpart to George Sand, whose literary celebrity and unconventional life made her one of the most discussed figures of the July Monarchy.

Balzac's portrait of des Touches is notably sympathetic. Unlike many of his ambitious women — whose pursuit of independence ends in ruin or compromise — des Touches achieves genuine autonomy, though at the cost of romantic fulfillment. Her famous line in this novel — "it's hell!" — resonates across the cycle as the verdict of a woman who has seen both love and art at close quarters and refuses to sentimentalize either.

## Character Significance

In *A Daughter of Eve*, Mademoiselle des Touches functions as a truth-teller amid a chorus of seductive half-truths. The novel's central drama depends on [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]]'s susceptibility to romantic fantasies — fantasies carefully cultivated by society and embodied in the figure of [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]]. Des Touches is the one voice in the salon that refuses to participate in the work of romantic mystification. Her warning goes unheeded, as such warnings typically do in Balzac's moral economy, but it stands as a marker of what the novel considers wisdom: the recognition that passion, however glorious it appears from a distance, is more likely to consume than to fulfill.

That Balzac entrusts this truth to a woman writer — one modeled on the most scandalously independent woman of his age — is a measure both of his respect for female intelligence and of his conviction that wisdom about love can only be earned through experience, never inherited through instruction.
