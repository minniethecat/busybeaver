# Mme. Octave de Camps

Mme. Octave de Camps is one of those secondary characters in Balzac's fictional world whose presence is small in word-count but large in moral significance. She appears in *A Daughter of Eve* as a devoted companion to [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse]], offering the steady, unobtrusive support that the novel's more brilliant and dangerous figures conspicuously fail to provide. In a world of seduction, gossip, and manipulation, Mme. de Camps stands out for her simple goodness — a quality Balzac describes with unusual warmth.

## In the Novel

### The Montcornet Concert

Mme. Octave de Camps makes her first appearance during the pivotal concert at the [[comtesse-de-montcornet|Comtesse de Montcornet]]'s salon, the social event at which Marie-Angélique first encounters [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]] and feels the immediate shock of attraction:

> Marie, on seeing Raoul, at once felt that inward shock, the violence of which is almost terrifying. This would-be great man, by a mere glance, sent a thrill right through to her heart, causing a delicious flutter there... The Countess drew near the ottoman in the centre of the room where Raoul was perorating. She remained standing, her arm linked in that of Mme. Octave de Camps, an excellent woman, who kept the secret of the nervous quivering by which Marie betrayed her strong emotion. [004-024]

This passage reveals the essential duality of Mme. de Camps's role. She is physically present — her arm linked with Marie's — providing both literal and metaphorical support. But she is also discreet: she "kept the secret" of the Countess's nervous trembling, neither drawing attention to it nor exploiting it for gossip. In a salon full of sharp-eyed observers like the [[marquise-d-espard|Marquise d'Espard]], this discretion is a form of protection.

Balzac's epithet — "an excellent woman" — is one of the few unambiguously positive moral judgments in a novel where most characters are described with irony or ambivalence. It signals that Mme. de Camps belongs to a different order of being from the intriguers who surround Marie-Angélique.

Immediately after this moment, it is Mme. de Camps who urges Marie to withdraw from danger:

> "Take care, dear, you had better come away," her fair companion, sweetest of women, whispered to Marie. [004-025]

The warning is gentle but clear: she recognizes the power of the attraction Marie has just experienced and moves to shield her from its consequences. The Countess heeds the advice and asks her husband for his arm, leaving the room — but the damage has been done, and the seed of romantic obsession has been planted.

### The Warning at Mme. d'Espard's

Mme. de Camps appears again in chapter six, after the rumor of Marie-Angélique's passion for Nathan has begun to circulate through Parisian society. At the Marquise d'Espard's gathering, the Countess is "warned against malicious gossips by Mme. Octave de Camps" [006-037], who helps her "realize her critical position before society" and encourages her to communicate this awareness to Nathan.

This second intervention shows Mme. de Camps acting as a kind of social guardian — not judging Marie for her feelings, but helping her navigate the treacherous waters of public opinion. In a world where friends like Lady Dudley and Mme. d'Espard use information as a weapon, Mme. de Camps uses it as a shield.

## Character Significance

Mme. Octave de Camps represents a Balzacian type that appears in many of his novels: the truly good woman whose goodness is expressed not in grand gestures but in small acts of loyalty and discretion. She is the opposite of the salon intriguer — she neither schemes nor gossips, and her presence in the novel provides a moral baseline against which the more complex characters can be measured.

Her role, though limited, is structurally important. At the two moments of Marie-Angélique's greatest vulnerability — the moment of first attraction and the moment of public exposure — it is Mme. de Camps who stands beside her, offering a steadying arm and a whispered warning. That Marie ultimately ignores these warnings to pursue her passion is not a failure of the friendship but a testament to the overwhelming force of the romantic impulse that Balzac anatomizes throughout the novel.

In the economy of *A Daughter of Eve*, Mme. de Camps is what the novel's fallen world most lacks and most needs: a friend whose loyalty requires no repayment and whose discretion invites no betrayal.
