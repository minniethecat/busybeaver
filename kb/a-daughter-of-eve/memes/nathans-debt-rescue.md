# Nathan's Debt Rescue

When Raoul Nathan's financial house of cards collapsed under du Tillet's orchestrated pressure, Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse and her sister Marie-Eugénie du Tillet mounted an audacious rescue operation — raising forty thousand francs through a chain of forged promissory notes, secret guarantees, and desperate midnight visits that would test the bonds of sisterly love against the machinery of Parisian finance [007-032, 008-005].

## The Precipitating Crisis

Nathan's debts had reached a critical point by the winter of the novel's second year. Du Tillet, who had advanced a hundred thousand francs and renewed bills at escalating rates, now called in his loans. He directed Nathan to the usurer Gigonnet, who lent at twenty-five percent, then secretly instructed Gigonnet to refuse all further quarter. Nathan, whose newspaper was hemorrhaging money and whose political ambitions consumed his remaining resources, was reduced to forty thousand francs of desperate debt and the imminent threat of arrest [007-032, 007-037].

> "There goes our dandy off to the Bois in tiptop style," [du Tillet] said to Lousteau, the very incarnation of envy, "and in six months he may be laid by the heels in Clichy." [007-018]

## The Discovery

Marie-Eugénie du Tillet learned of the crisis on the evening of Nathan's suicide attempt. Her husband Ferdinand, returning home, casually remarked that Nathan was about to be arrested for debt. Marie-Eugénie, who had been entrusted with her sister's secret, immediately understood the peril: if Nathan were imprisoned, Marie-Angélique's reputation — and perhaps her life — would be destroyed. She hastened to the Rue du Mail, where she found Nathan recovering from charcoal asphyxiation under the name of François Quillet, and extracted from him the precise sum needed: forty thousand francs [008-002, 008-089].

## The Conspiracy

The sisters devised a scheme of remarkable ingenuity. Mme de Nucingen, the banker's wife, agreed to provide the forty thousand francs against four promissory notes of ten thousand francs each. But the notes required a signatory — someone whose name would not implicate either sister. Marie-Eugénie proposed their childhood music-master, the elderly German Schmucke, whose guileless nature made him the perfect unwitting accomplice [008-017].

> "Only it occurred to me that perhaps our old master, Schmucke, might be useful to us now; he would sign them. If, in addition to these four securities, you write a letter guaranteeing their payment to Mme. de Nucingen, she will hand you the money tomorrow." [008-017]

Marie-Angélique visited Schmucke in his humble lodgings on the Rue de Nevers, and the old man — devoted to his former pupils without question — signed the four notes, each inscribed "I promise to pay ten thousand francs," without understanding their purpose [008-041, 008-043].

## The Opera Signal

On the evening of the same day, at the Opera, Marie-Angélique saw Nathan — pale, ashen, leaning on the balustrade, searching for her in her empty box. She was seated with her sister in the du Tillets' box. At all risks, she fixed Nathan with a steady magnetic gaze, then seized the diamond cross at her neck and directed his attention to it with a swift, meaning-laden smile. The brilliance of the gem radiated upon Nathan's forehead, and he understood: the money would be secured [008-023, 008-026].

## Consequences

The rescue bought Nathan three months of freedom. But the bill scheme's secrecy was fragile. Marie-Angélique's husband, Félix de Vandenesse, soon learned of the transaction from Mme de Nucingen. He repaid the forty thousand francs himself, retrieved his wife's guarantee letter, and used the recovered bills as leverage in his own counter-plot — a scheme that would ultimately expose Nathan's relationship with Florine and reclaim Marie-Angélique's honor [009-002, 009-016].

## Narrative Function

The debt rescue serves as the novel's central hinge between crisis and resolution. It demonstrates the sisters' bond — forged in the shared misery of their conventual childhood — as the one moral force capable of operating within Parisian finance. It also sets in motion Félix de Vandenesse's counter-plot, which transforms him from the deceived husband into the novel's most effective strategist. Balzac uses the episode to dramatize the collision between private passion and public credit: love, in the world of *A Daughter of Eve*, is priced in francs.
