## Overview

Moïna de Saint-Heren is a minor character in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). A pretty young woman in Parisian high society, she participates in the salon conversations about love and marriage that form the novel's moral backdrop. Her frank declaration about the appeal of forbidden love contributes to the atmosphere of worldly cynicism surrounding [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie de Vandenesse]].

## "The Forbidden Fruit"

In a salon conversation about love and marriage, Moïna declares: "A lover is the forbidden fruit, and that's enough for me!" This candid confession — free of guilt, free of moral qualm — represents the worldly acceptance of infidelity that characterizes Parisian high society in the novel. For a woman like Moïna, love outside marriage is not a sin but an adventure, a pleasure to be pursued for its own sake.

The remark is one of several cynical aphorisms that circulate through the novel's salons, creating a climate in which Marie-Angélique's temptation seems almost inevitable. Voices like Moïna's normalize what Marie-Angélique's religious upbringing has taught her to regard as mortal sin.

## Observer of Marie

In the novel's salon scenes, Moïna is among the women who observe and comment on Marie-Angélique's evident infatuation with [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]]. The salons of Balzac's Paris function as theaters of surveillance, where every glance and blush is noted, interpreted, and gossiped about. Moïna participates in this social gaze, her attention contributing to the pressure that surrounds Marie.

## Significance

Moïna de Saint-Heren is a minor figure, but her brief appearance serves an important function in the novel's moral architecture. She represents the worldly, unreflective embrace of pleasure that stands in counterpoint to Marie-Angélique's anguished conscience. She is one of the voices of the world — charming, attractive, and morally indifferent — against which the novel measures its heroine's spiritual drama.
