# Ferdinand du Tillet

Ferdinand du Tillet is one of the richest bankers in [[paris|Paris]] and a central antagonist in [[la-comedie-humaine|Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*]] cycle. In *A Daughter of Eve*, he is the husband of Marie-Angélique's sister and uses his financial power to ensnare [[love|Raoul Nathan]] — the man both sisters secretly admire — in a web of debt designed to destroy him politically and personally.

## Social Position

Du Tillet occupies the highest tier of Parisian finance. He is introduced as "one of the richest bankers in Paris" whose house displays all the signs of "opulence and good taste" [001-003]. His wealth buys him entry to the most exclusive salons, including those of [[the-salons-of-paris|Mme. de Montcornet and Mme. d'Espard]], where he moves alongside Nucingen, Rastignac, and Blondet [005-014].

His social ambition is explicit: "du Tillet wants a seat in the Chamber of Deputies" [005-043]. He views politics as an extension of banking — both are mechanisms of control.

## Character Arc

Du Tillet appears initially as a cold but seemingly reasonable relation. When Marie-Angélique's husband Félix needs money, du Tillet is "not sorry at the prospect" of lending — it gives him leverage [002-024]. His tone is one of "honeyed acerbity" [002-022], politeness that barely conceals contempt.

As the novel progresses, his mask slips. When Nathan's bills come due in October, "du Tillet graciously renewed them" — but only to tighten his grip [007-021]. By December, he demands full payment with calculated precision [007-032]. Nathan, unaware of the trap, declares himself "heartily glad to be quit of du Tillet's debt" [007-035] — not realizing du Tillet has orchestrated his ruin through the usurer Gigonnet [008-002].

His wife confronts him late in the novel, and his response reveals his philosophy: "Balzac observes that when du Tillet plots against anyone, as he did against Nathan, he makes use of every instrument at hand" [008-105]. The electoral hopes he built through Nathan's destruction ultimately prove "abortive" [008-106] — a moment of poetic justice that leaves "a look of despair" on his face [008-113].

## The Du Tillet Marriage

Du Tillet's marriage to Eugénie (Marie-Angélique's sister) is presented as a prison. His wife "pressed her sister's hand to her lips" in silent desperation [001-004], and the sisters live "in opposing camps" [002-008]. Balzac uses the contrast between the two marriages — du Tillet's cold financial union versus Vandenesse's more complex but still troubled relationship — to explore the varieties of [[marriage|marital unhappiness]] in the French aristocracy.

## Significance Across *La Comédie Humaine*

Du Tillet appears in multiple Balzac novels as a recurring antagonist. His character represents the fusion of finance and politics that Balzac saw as corrupting post-Napoleonic France. The name "du Tillet" itself suggests "of the lime tree" — an ironic gentleness for a character of such metallic coldness. His method of destroying rivals through orchestrated debt appears across the *Comédie Humaine* cycle, making him one of Balzac's most fully realized portraits of financial evil.

As a literary archetype, du Tillet descends from the tradition of the scheming banker — a figure who uses money not for pleasure but for power, and who views human relationships as instruments rather than ends. Balzac's innovation is to show how this type operates within the specific social machinery of the [[society|Restoration aristocracy]], where salons, newspapers, and parliamentary seats are all fungible assets.
