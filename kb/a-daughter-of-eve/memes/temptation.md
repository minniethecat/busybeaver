## Overview

Temptation is the central moral drama of Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). The novel's title itself — evoking the biblical Eve — frames the story as one of temptation and fall. [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie de Vandenesse]], the "daughter of Eve," is tempted away from her virtuous but sheltered marriage by the charismatic [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]].

## The Biblical Framework

Balzac explicitly invokes the Genesis narrative. Marie is described as "this Eve, listless in her paradise of the Rue du Rocher," while Nathan is "the insidious serpent, bright to the eye and flattering to the ear, with magnetic gaze and graceful motion, who ruined the first woman" [004-023]. This framing establishes temptation not merely as a social or romantic matter but as a theological one — a test of virtue with cosmic stakes.

## The Mechanism of Temptation

Balzac analyzes temptation with psychological precision. Marie's vulnerability stems from her innocence and inexperience. Raised under her mother's strict religious supervision and married to a man who treats her with protective condescension, she has never encountered the world. Nathan's appeal lies precisely in his difference from her husband [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix]]: where Félix is polished and controlled, Nathan is raw, passionate, and unconventional [004-040].

The novel traces temptation's progression: first the "inward shock" of attraction at Mme. de Montcornet's concert [004-024], then the internal debate between gratitude to her husband and fascination with Nathan [004-039], then the surrender at [[lady-dudleys-salon|Lady Dudley's ball]], and finally the deepening entanglement that threatens to destroy her.

## Temptation and Society

Temptation in the novel is not purely private. Society — in the form of the [[marquise-d-espard|Marquise d'Espard]], [[lady-dudley|Lady Dudley]], and the cynical [[emile-blondet|Émile Blondet]] — actively facilitates the affair. The supper at Véry's, where Rastignac and Blondet urge Nathan to pursue Marie as a conquest [004-034], reveals how aristocratic society treats temptation as entertainment. The "three shameless spirits trampled on the tender white blossoms of her opening love" [004-039].

## Consequence and Redemption

Balzac does not treat temptation as a simple moral failure. Marie's fall is shown with sympathy — she is a victim of her own innocence and of social manipulation. The novel's resolution, in which Marie confesses to her husband and is forgiven, suggests that temptation can be survived and that the fall need not be final. The [[comtesse-de-granville|Comtesse de Granville]]'s own story of past temptation provides a parallel that deepens the theme.

## Theological and Moral Dimensions

The novel engages with Catholic moral theology — Marie's devout upbringing, her sense of sin, her prayers — but Balzac's treatment is characteristically worldly. Temptation is simultaneously a spiritual trial and a social drama. The word "devotion" recurs throughout, linking religious devotion (to God) with marital devotion (to one's husband) and romantic devotion (to one's lover), creating a complex moral field.
