# Rue de Clichy

The Rue de Clichy appears in Honoré de Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839) not primarily as a street but as the location of the notorious debtors' prison of Paris -- the prison de Clichy, where insolvent debtors were confined at the request of their creditors. In the novel, the threat of imprisonment in "Clichy" hangs over Raoul Nathan like a sword, representing the financial ruin that awaits him at the hands of Ferdinand du Tillet.

## In *A Daughter of Eve*

The Rue de Clichy is first invoked by du Tillet himself, who confronts his wife Marie-Eugénie with cold, calculated precision after discovering that her sister has come to borrow money. The banker reveals that he knows precisely what is at stake: "Your sister came to borrow forty thousand francs to pay the debts of a man in whom she is interested, and who, within three days, will be under lock and key in the Rue de Clichy. He's too precious to be left loose." (PN [002-037]). This is du Tillet speaking as the predator he is -- he knows exactly how close Nathan is to ruin because he has engineered that ruin himself.

The second reference comes later in the novel, as Nathan's financial situation deteriorates. Du Tillet, watching Nathan depart from Florine's house in his cabriolet, remarks to the envious journalist Lousteau with savage irony: "There goes our dandy off to the Bois in tiptop style, and in six months he may be laid by the heels in Clichy." (PN [007-018]). The contrast between Nathan's ostentatious public appearance and his impending imprisonment underscores the novel's theme of appearances versus reality -- the gulf between what Parisian society sees and what lurks beneath the surface.

## The Prison de Clichy

The debtors' prison on the Rue de Clichy was a peculiar institution of 19th-century French law, a remnant of the ancien régime's practice of imprisoning those who could not pay their debts. Unlike common criminals, debtors were not considered morally guilty; their confinement was a civil matter, a mechanism by which creditors could pressure them -- or their families and friends -- into payment. The prison occupied a building at 66-68 Rue de Clichy (now in the 9th arrondissement), and operated from 1834 until 1867, when imprisonment for debt was abolished in France.

Conditions at Clichy were notably more comfortable than in ordinary prisons. Debtors who could pay for better accommodations lived in relative comfort, receiving visitors, conducting business, and even dining well. Those without means, however, endured crowded, squalid conditions. The prison thus reproduced the class hierarchies of the outside world within its walls -- a detail that Balzac, ever the keen observer of money's social power, would have appreciated.

## Symbolic significance

In Balzac's novel, "Clichy" functions as more than a geographical location; it is the ultimate symbol of financial disgrace, the endpoint of the trajectory of debt that threatens to consume Nathan's political ambitions, his literary career, and his relationship with Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse. The prison represents the material reality that poetic idealism cannot escape: however lofty Nathan's aspirations, the laws of credit and collateral will bring him down.

The two references to Clichy are strategically placed -- the first by du Tillet in a moment of domestic intimidation, the second by du Tillet in a moment of cynical prediction to a fellow journalist. In both cases, the name of the prison serves as a weapon, a reminder of the power that money exercises over talent, ambition, and even love in Balzac's Paris.
