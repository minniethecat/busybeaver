# Rue du Mail

The Rue du Mail is a street in central Paris, near the Place des Victoires in the 2nd arrondissement. In *A Daughter of Eve*, the street appears as the location of the Hôtel du Mail — a modest lodging house that becomes the site of the novel's climactic revelations.

## The Hôtel du Mail

The Hôtel du Mail serves as a temporary refuge for [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]] after his failed suicide attempt. When questioned about Nathan's whereabouts, the reply is direct: "No! Didn't I tell you Rue du Mail, at the hotel?" [008-113]. The hotel's unremarkable character — neither fashionable nor squalid — marks it as a place of transition and anonymity, far from the salons and theatres where Nathan's public life unfolds.

## Discovery and Exposure

The Rue du Mail address becomes central to the novel's final crisis when [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]] reveals his knowledge of his wife's affair:

> "Well, you can go and ask at the Hôtel du Mail, Rue du Mail, whether he was not brought there at the point of death by a beautiful lady, with whom he has had intimate relations for a year; the letters of your rival are hidden in your house, under your very nose." [009-073]

This single sentence unravels the entire secret structure of [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]]'s double life: the hotel, the rescue, the hidden correspondence, and her husband's complete knowledge of her transgression.

## Symbolic Geography

The Rue du Mail stands in opposition to the [[rue-du-rocher|Rue du Rocher]], the Vandenesse residence. Where the Rue du Rocher represents aristocratic order, wealth, and the gilded cage of Marie-Angélique's marriage, the Rue du Mail represents the hidden world of secrets, indiscretions, and the consequences of [[passion]]. The physical distance between the two streets maps onto the social and moral distance Marie-Angélique has traveled.

## Significance

The Rue du Mail functions in the novel less as a described place than as an *address of exposure* — a coordinate in Balzac's mapping of Paris that signifies the collapse of private secrets into public knowledge. Its very ordinariness is the point: passion's consequences do not play out in grand settings but in anonymous hotel rooms.
