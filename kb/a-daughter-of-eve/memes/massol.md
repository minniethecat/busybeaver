# Massol

Massol is a Parisian barrister and publicist who serves as a partner in [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]]'s newspaper venture in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). Alongside the banker [[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]], Massol is one of the two men who secretly conspire to exploit Nathan's literary energy for their own ends, intending to wrest control of the paper once it has served their purposes. He represents a particular type in Balzac's taxonomy of ambition: the mediocrity who advances not through talent but through patience, prolixity, and the cultivation of powerful patrons.

## The Barrister as Blight

Balzac introduces Massol with one of the novel's most acid character sketches. He belongs, the narrator tells us, to "the type of barrister who takes a flux of words for eloquence and can weary any audience by his prolixity, who in every gathering of men acts as a blight, shriveling up their enthusiasm, yet who is determined at all costs to be a somebody" [007-005].

The diagnosis is precise and devastating. Massol mistakes verbal abundance for rhetorical power — a confusion that the legal profession, with its premium on procedural verbosity, actively encourages. But his effect on others is not merely boring; it is *blighting*. He kills enthusiasm wherever he goes, not through malicious intent but through the sheer dead weight of his mediocrity. And yet — this is the crucial point — his determination to be "a somebody" never wavers. Massol is a case study in how ambition can flourish independently of ability, sustained by nothing more than stubbornness and a willingness to serve the interests of more powerful men.

## The Calculus of Ambition

Massol's career ambitions have been shaped by observation as much as by desire. "Within four years he had seen five or six men clothed with the robes of office, and this had cured him of the fancy" [007-005]. The remark is characteristically Balzacian in its worldly cynicism. Massol once dreamed of the ministry of justice — the highest legal office in the land — but he has watched too many ministers come and go to sustain the illusion that such heights are attainable by mere mortals.

His ambition, accordingly, has been recalibrated to more modest targets. He is now "ready to accept, as something in hand, a professorship or a post under the Council, with of course the Cross of the Legion of Honor to season the dish" [007-005]. The phrase "as something in hand" is significant: Massol is a practical man, willing to settle for what he can get rather than risk everything on an unattainable prize. The Legion of Honor, that ubiquitous decoration of the July Monarchy's functionaries, is the garnish that makes the meal palatable.

## The Duplicitous Partner

The conspiracy against Nathan is outlined with brutal clarity in chapter 7. Massol and du Tillet "had conceived the idea of harnessing his energy to the car in which they should loll at ease, with the full intention of turning him adrift as soon as his resources failed to keep the paper going, or of wresting it from his hands the moment they saw their way to using this powerful instrument for their own purposes" [007-004].

The metaphor is precise: Nathan is the horse, supplying the motive power, while Massol and du Tillet "loll at ease" in the carriage. Nathan, with his characteristic blindness to the motives of those around him, believes he is using *them*. "To their minds, Nathan represented so much capital to run through, a literary force, equal to that of ten ordinary writers, to exploit" [007-004]. The language of capital and exploitation makes explicit what the novel elsewhere implies: in Balzac's Paris, talent is a commodity, and those who control the means of its distribution profit from it more than those who possess it.

Massol's betrayal is particularly cynical because he has been bought. "Du Tillet and the Baron de Nucingen had guaranteed him the Cross and the desired post if he fell in with their views; and as he judged them to be in a better position than Nathan for fulfilling their promises, he followed them blindly" [007-005]. He is, in effect, a bribed partner — his loyalty to Nathan has been purchased in advance by the promise of decorations and office.

The verb "blindly" is telling. Massol does not merely cooperate with du Tillet and [[baron-de-nucingen|Nucingen]]; he "follows" them, abdicating his own judgment in exchange for the certainty of reward. He is not a co-conspirator so much as a purchased instrument, and his mediocrity makes him the perfect tool: a man of real intelligence might have scruples, but Massol has only ambition.

## Role in the Newspaper

In chapter 5, when [[emile-blondet|Blondet]] outlines the editorial team for Nathan's paper, he assigns Massol a supporting role: "I will get Massol to help him. He's a barrister, and, like the rest of them, intends to have a turn at being Minister of Justice" [005-043]. The slighting tone — "like the rest of them" — captures the contempt in which the more talented journalists hold him. Massol will "sweat for" the paper [005-043], providing the legal and administrative labor that the literary men consider beneath them.

The division of labor is instructive. Nathan supplies the genius, Lousteau the light entertainment, Vignon the serious criticism, and Massol — Massol supplies the drudgery. He is necessary but not valued, exploited but never respected. His ambition, in the end, is to escape the very position he has been assigned.

## The Final Revelation

Massol's role in the conspiracy is fully exposed only in chapter 9, when [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]] reveals to his wife the mechanism by which Nathan has been destroyed. The count explains that Massol, du Tillet, and Nucingen "have sold the paper in advance to the Government, so confident are they of dispossessing the great man" [009-027].

The revelation is the culmination of the plot that was set in motion in chapter 7. Massol, having used Nathan's talent to build a viable newspaper, has conspired to sell it — and Nathan with it — to the very political establishment that the paper was ostensibly designed to challenge. The betrayal of ideals is financial as well as personal: all the money that Florine sacrificed, "the whole of which she gave to Nathan for starting his paper," has "in the long run, all goes into the pockets of du Tillet, Nucingen, and Massol" [009-027].

Massol thus embodies a particular kind of modern villainy: not the passionate evil of a Grandet or a Vautrin, but the bureaucratic betrayal of a man whose mediocrity becomes, in the right circumstances, indistinguishable from predation.
