## Overview

The Marquise de Listomère is mentioned among the exclusive salon hostesses of Restoration Paris in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). Alongside [[marquise-d-espard|Mme. d'Espard]], [[mademoiselle-des-touches|Mlle. des Touches]], and the [[duchesse-de-grandlieu|Duchesse de Grandlieu]], her drawing-room was one of those that "had picked up recruits from among the latest celebrities in art, science, literature, and politics" during the winter season [004-002].

## A Grande Dame of the Faubourg Saint-Germain

The Marquise de Listomère belongs to the highest stratum of Parisian aristocracy — the Faubourg Saint-Germain, the traditional stronghold of the old nobility. Her salon, like those of her peers, functioned as a gatekeeping institution: admission signaled social recognition, exclusion signaled social death. The rigorous tests to which salon hostesses subjected potential guests ensured that "the most exclusive need not fear rubbing up against persons of an inferior grade" [004-002].

## Role in the Novel

The Marquise de Listomère does not appear directly in the novel's action but is invoked as part of the social firmament against which the characters move. Her name, listed among the great hostesses, establishes the world of exclusive sociability that Marie-Angélique inhabits and that Nathan aspires to penetrate. She is part of the architecture of social reference that Balzac uses to create the density of his fictional world.

## The Salon System

The Marquise de Listomère represents the salon system that was central to French aristocratic life. Salons were not merely social gatherings but institutions of cultural and political power — places where reputations were made, alliances forged, and careers advanced or destroyed. The hostess who presided over such a salon wielded considerable informal influence, shaping the conversation of her class and, through it, the direction of society.
