## Overview

Madame de Manerville is a minor but revealing figure in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). A former mistress of [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]], her presence at Lady Dudley's ball serves to illuminate Félix's romantic past and to provoke a moment of jealousy in his wife Marie.

## At Lady Dudley's Ball

At the grand ball hosted by [[lady-dudley|Lady Dudley]] — the event where [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie de Vandenesse]] and [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]] first encounter each other face to face — Mme. de Manerville appears among the guests. Lady Dudley deliberately detains Félix in conversation with his former mistress, a strategic maneuver designed to leave Marie alone and vulnerable to Nathan's approach.

The moment does not go unnoticed by Marie, who experiences a pang of jealous apprehension at seeing her husband engaged with a woman from his past. This small jealousy foreshadows the larger drama of infidelity and suspicion that will unfold.

## In Salon Society

Mme. de Manerville is noted among the witty aristocratic women who trade barbs in the novel's salon scenes. She belongs to the social circle that includes [[marquise-d-espard|Mme. d'Espard]], [[comtesse-de-montcornet|Mme. de Montcornet]], and other grandes dames of Restoration Paris. Her presence in these circles testifies to the interconnected nature of Parisian high society, where past liaisons are not easily forgotten and former lovers continue to move in the same orbits.

## Significance

As a character, Mme. de Manerville represents the shadow of Félix's romantic history — a reminder that the novel's world of marriage and fidelity exists against a background of past affairs and lingering attachments. Her brief appearance contributes to the novel's texture of social interconnectedness, where every salon gathering contains hidden histories and unspoken tensions.
