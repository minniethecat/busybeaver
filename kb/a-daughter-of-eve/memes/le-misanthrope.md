# *Le Misanthrope* (1666)

*Le Misanthrope, ou l'Atrabilaire amoureux* is a comedy of manners in five acts by Molière, first performed at the Théâtre du Palais-Royal on 4 June 1666. Universally regarded as one of the summits of French classical theatre, the play dramatizes the conflict between Alceste, a man who demands absolute honesty and refuses all social compromise, and the polite society of seventeenth-century Paris, which runs on flattery, dissimulation, and strategic silence. Alceste's love for the coquettish Célimène, who embodies everything he claims to despise, generates the play's central tension between principle and passion.

## The Play's Core Argument

The play opens with Alceste berating his friend Philinte for embracing a man he barely knows: "I want to be distinguished; and, to cut matters short, the friend of all mankind is no friend of mine." Philinte counsels accommodation -- human society cannot function without a degree of polite insincerity. The action that follows tests both positions. Alceste's refusal to flatter the sonnet of the foppish Oronte lands him in legal trouble; his demand that Célimène renounce her social world costs him her love; and his final retreat to a desert "where one may be an honest man" leaves open the question of whether the misanthrope's virtue is heroism or folly.

The play refuses to resolve its central argument. Molière gives the most eloquent speeches to Alceste but the most practical wisdom to Philinte. The character of Éliante -- Célimène's sensible cousin who loves Alceste without illusions -- offers a middle path, admiring sincerity while recognizing that love requires a certain blindness to the beloved's faults. It is from Éliante's great speech in Act II that Balzac borrows the word "negligee" to describe Nathan's studied slovenliness.

## The Alceste/Philinte Contrast in *A Daughter of Eve*

Balzac's deployment of Molière's characters is concentrated at [004-016], the passage that anatomizes [[raoul-nathan]]'s moral duplicity: "he is a very Alceste in pose, while acting like Philinte." This sentence is the interpretive key to Nathan's character and, by extension, to Balzac's critique of the literary-political world of the July Monarchy.

Alceste represents the posture Nathan adopts in public: the uncompromising truth-teller, the man whose "shoddy greatness" refuses the corruptions of the age. Nathan's Republican posturing, his "puritan ruggedness assumed by champions of the popular cause," his cultivation of eccentric dress and manner -- all of these constitute an Alceste performance, a claim to moral seriousness that distinguishes him from the mere entertainers and hacks among whom he moves.

Philinte represents the reality. Nathan "traffics with his honor," "takes liberties with his friends, which are nothing short of a moral outrage," and is "hail-fellow-well-met with every vice, every degradation, every treachery, every party." Like Philinte, he makes his peace with the world as it is while claiming the moral high ground. The Alceste pose is precisely what enables the Philinte practice: by performing uncompromising integrity, Nathan purchases the license to compromise everywhere.

This analysis extends beyond individual character to the novel's larger social diagnosis. Balzac's Paris is a world where everyone plays Alceste (the language of honor, principle, and sincerity is universal) while everyone acts Philinte (accommodation, compromise, and strategic betrayal are the actual currency of social life). The distance between the two is the measure of the society's corruption, and Molière's comedy provides Balzac with the classical vocabulary to make this distance visible.

## Éliante and the Grammar of Love

A second Molière character illuminates the novel's emotional dynamics. Balzac's reference at [004-004] to "the word used in Éliante to describe personal slovenliness" evokes a specific passage in *Le Misanthrope* where Éliante catalogs the transformations wrought by love:

> The lover sees his mistress as perfection,
> Finds charms and hidden graces in each section;
> All faults are given a new and flattering name:
> The pale one has the jasmine's white to claim...

Éliante understands that love is a creative act of perception, a willing suspension of judgment. Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse's love for Nathan follows exactly this pattern. She sees the disheveled, debt-ridden, morally compromised journalist and transforms him -- through the alchemy of romantic imagination -- into a misunderstood genius, a wounded Byronic hero whom only her devotion can save. Balzac, by invoking Éliante's name in the initial description of Nathan, plants the seed of this critique before the love plot has even begun.

## The Unspoken Presence

*Le Misanthrope* is never named directly in *A Daughter of Eve*. Balzac trusts his readers to recognize Alceste and Philinte, to recall Éliante's speech, and to draw the appropriate conclusions. This unspoken quality makes the play an undercurrent rather than a topical reference -- a classical framework that operates beneath the surface of the realist narration, providing moral coordinates for a world that has lost its bearings. In a novel about the gap between appearance and reality, Molière's great comedy of sincerity and dissimulation is the indispensable subtext.
