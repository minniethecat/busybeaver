The Nucingen family plays a crucial supporting role in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). Headed by the Baron de Nucingen, the great banker, and extended through his wife Delphine and their daughter (engaged to [[eugene-de-rastignac|Rastignac]]), the Nucingens represent the power of finance in July Monarchy Paris.

The family's enabling role in the Nathan debt rescue is central to the novel's plot. [[baronne-de-nucingen|Delphine de Nucingen]] provides the 40,000 francs — through the [[schmucke|Schmucke]] bills and [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]]'s guarantee — that saves Nathan from imprisonment. This transaction links the private drama of the Vandenesse marriage to the larger world of banking and credit that Balzac's fiction maps with such precision.

The Nucingens are among Balzac's most important recurring characters, appearing throughout *La Comédie Humaine* as embodiments of the financial forces transforming French society. In *A Daughter of Eve*, their role is discreet but decisive — the quiet power of money operating behind the scenes of the romantic drama.
