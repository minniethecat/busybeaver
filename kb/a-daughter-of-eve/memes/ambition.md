# Ambition

Ambition is the engine of *A Daughter of Eve*. Every major character — Raoul Nathan, Ferdinand du Tillet, even Marie-Angélique in her romantic longings — is driven by the desire for more than their current position. Balzac presents ambition not as a moral failing but as the structural condition of post-Napoleonic [[society]], where the old aristocratic order has collapsed and new paths to power have opened — or seemed to.

## Ambition's Double Face

Balzac distinguishes between two kinds of ambition: the creative and the financial. Nathan represents the first — "in all the efforts of his ambition, in the great part he plays upon the stage of life" [002-009]. His ambition is theatrical, performative, tied to his art. Du Tillet represents the second: quieter, more patient, but ultimately more effective. He "wants a seat in the Chamber of Deputies" [005-043] and pursues it methodically through debt and manipulation.

The novel's cruel insight is that financial ambition always defeats creative ambition. Nathan's literary celebrity means nothing when du Tillet calls in his loans. The artist "looked at Rastignac, whose success had redoubled his ambition" [004-053], but Rastignac succeeded by mastering the financial game, not the artistic one.

## Ambition and Gender

| Character | Ambition Type | Outcome |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| Raoul Nathan | Artistic → Political | Destroyed by debt, rescued by Marie-Angélique's intervention |
| Ferdinand du Tillet | Financial → Political | Thwarted — "abortive" electoral hopes [008-106] |
| Marie-Angélique | Romantic → Social | Disillusionment — her ambition for [[love]] is crushed by reality |
| Florine | Theatrical → Financial | Survival — her ambition is modest but effective |
| Rastignac | Political → Financial | Success — mentioned as a model Nathan envies [004-053] |

Women's ambition in the novel takes different forms. Marie-Angélique's ambition is romantic — she wants a [[passion|passionate]] life, not a dutiful [[marriage]]. Her sister Eugénie (Mme. du Tillet) has no ambition at all — she has been crushed by her husband. Florine's ambition is the most honest: she wants to maintain her position and knows exactly what that costs.

## The Galleys of Ambition

The novel's most striking image for ambition comes from the narrator: "the galleys of ambition" [006-063]. The metaphor is precise — the ambitious man is a galley slave, chained to his oar, unable to stop rowing. Nathan, once embarked on his political course, cannot turn back. Each loan from du Tillet is another link in the chain.

> "When so many young ambitions start on foot to meet at the same point, few arrive" [004-020]

Balzac does not moralize. He simply observes: ambition is the condition of modern life, and the question is not whether to be ambitious but how to survive one's own ambition.

## Ambition's Structural Role

Ambition in the novel serves as a connecting tissue between otherwise separate domains. Nathan's ambition links the world of [[art]] (his writing) to the world of politics (his newspaper) to the world of finance (du Tillet's loans). Marie-Angélique's ambition links the domestic sphere (her marriage) to the public sphere (her salon, her intervention in Nathan's affairs). Ambition is what makes the novel's [[society]] cohere as a single system rather than a collection of isolated spheres.
