# Comte de Granville

Comte de Granville is the father of [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse]] and [[marie-eugenie-du-tillet|Marie-Eugénie du Tillet]]. Though he appears only in the novel's early chapters and in retrospective references, his patriarchal authority casts a long shadow over both daughters' lives and marriages.

## A Severe Patriarch

Granville is introduced as a figure of formidable authority. "The Comte de Granville had a great reputation as a judge" [001-015] — a professional distinction that shapes his domestic manner. He brings the courtroom into the household, treating family decisions as verdicts.

When his daughters were young, he "pointed out the necessity of sending their eldest daughter to boarding-school" [001-008]. The decision was presented as a ruling, not a discussion. His wife "could not deny it" [001-009] — not because she agreed, but because Granville's authority brooked no opposition.

## The Education of His Daughters

Granville's approach to his daughters' upbringing reflects his era's conventions about female education — and Balzac's critique of them. The daughters were raised under a regime that combined neglect with intermittent severity. "He could hear how the Comtesse de Granville brought up her daughters" but rarely intervened [003-004], leaving the day-to-day work of parenting to a mother whose own authority was subordinate to his.

This hands-off paternal style produced daughters who were outwardly accomplished but inwardly unprepared for the realities of [[marriage|aristocratic marriage]]. Both Marie-Angélique and Eugénie enter marriages with powerful men — one a banker, one a statesman — without the emotional resources to navigate those relationships.

## Marriage as Legal Transaction

Granville's role in his daughters' marriages exemplifies the Balzacian theme of marriage as a legal and financial arrangement rather than an emotional bond. His elder daughter was married to [[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]], a wealthy banker — a match that served family interests but condemned Eugénie to misery. His younger daughter's marriage to [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]] was more prestigious but no less transactional in its origins.

Balzac's irony is pointed: Granville's reputation as a great legal mind did not prevent him from arranging marriages that became case studies in legal but loveless unions. The man who dispensed justice in the courtroom could not see the injustice in his own household.

## The Mother

The Comtesse de Granville appears as a shadowy figure — "not without affection for her daughters" [001-005] but equally powerless against her husband's will. She "had found a music-master for her daughters in spite of the difficulties raised by her husband" [001-012], a small act of maternal defiance that reveals the daily negotiations of power within the Granville household.

By the novel's end, "the Comtesse de Granville had gone to live in retirement, entirely occupied in the practice of her religious duties" [008-005] — a withdrawal that speaks to a lifetime of subordination.

## Significance

Granville is a minor character but a structural necessity in Balzac's sociology of the family. He represents the patriarchal system that produces, however unintentionally, the conditions for his daughters' unhappiness. He is not cruel — he is conventional, and his conventionality is precisely what Balzac indicts. The novel suggests that the tragedy of the Granville daughters begins not in their marriages but in their childhood, in a household where paternal authority was absolute and maternal affection was circumscribed.
