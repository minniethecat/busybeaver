# Passion

In *A Daughter of Eve*, passion is love stripped of its social legitimacy — an ungovernable force that Balzac depicts with equal measures of sympathy and warning. It is what remains when marriage has evacuated all feeling from a life, and it is what destroys that life when it arrives.

## In the Novel

Passion enters Marie-Angélique's life as a response to emptiness. After years in a marriage that offers security without warmth, she is vulnerable to the "torch of love" — a flame that Balzac describes society as working to extinguish, not because passion is wrong but because it is uncontrollable (001-007). Her husband Félix, by contrast, has already made his peace with passion's absence: having "resolved to cut short by marriage pleasures which had begun to weary him," he treats the domestication of passion as a form of adult competence (003-004).

The crisis of the novel unfolds when Marie-Angélique encounters Raoul Nathan. In Balzac's rendering, she does not fall in love so much as she is ambushed by passion — "the master passion, according to Mme. de Staël, is the only real thing in a woman's life" (003-020). This invocation of de Staël is characteristic of Balzac's method: passion is always being theorized even as it is being experienced, as if understanding it might provide some defense against its power.

The denouement demonstrates that it cannot. Marie-Angélique's "most impassioned epithet" — the strongest language her innocence can summon — is to call her husband by his title rather than his name, a gesture whose pathos lies in its very inadequacy (003-014).

## Balzac's Treatment

Balzac treats passion as a naturalist would treat a flood: he documents its causes, charts its course, and records its damage without moralizing. His narrative voice never condemns Marie-Angélique for feeling passion; it condemns the society that has left her so unprepared for it. The novel's deep insight is that passion is produced by the very institutions designed to suppress it — that a marriage market which treats women as economic assets inevitably generates the romantic rebellion that will destroy it.

## Contrast With

- **Love**: Passion is love at temperature — love intensified beyond social control. Balzac distinguishes between sustainable affection and the "paroxysm of passion" that consumes its host.
- **Marriage**: Passion is what marriage is designed to exclude. The novel's tragedy is that excluding passion does not eliminate the *need* for it.
- **Duty**: The antithesis of passion. Where duty demands restraint, passion demands release.

## In 19th-Century Context

Balzac wrote during the height of French Romanticism, when passion had been elevated by writers like George Sand and Stendhal into a moral value in its own right. His innovation in *A Daughter of Eve* is to treat passion not as a virtue but as a *symptom* — an inevitable byproduct of a social order that denies women agency while encouraging them to feel deeply. In this, he anticipates the psychological realism of Flaubert's *Madame Bovary* (1856), which would explore the same territory with even more devastating precision.
