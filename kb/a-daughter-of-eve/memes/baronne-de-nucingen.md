# Baronne de Nucingen

The Baronne de Nucingen, born Delphine Goriot, is one of the most significant recurring female characters in [[la-comedie-humaine|Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*]]. The younger daughter of Père Goriot — the tragic vermicelli-merchant whose story forms the core of Balzac's most famous novel — Delphine has, by the time of *A Daughter of Eve*, been married for many years to the great Alsatian banker [[baron-de-nucingen|Baron de Nucingen]]. In this novel, set in the early 1830s, she appears not as a protagonist but as a quietly decisive figure whose financial resources and worldly discretion enable the central plot of Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse's attempt to save [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]].

## Social Position

The Baronne de Nucingen occupies a paradoxical station in Parisian society. She is the wife of one of the wealthiest men in France — a peer of the realm whose millions command access to every salon and every drawing-room — yet as the daughter of a tradesman who made his fortune in flour and pasta, she bears the invisible stain of bourgeois origin that the Faubourg Saint-Germain never entirely forgets. Balzac does not dwell on this tension in *A Daughter of Eve*, but it informs everything about her: her worldly wisdom, her sympathy for those in difficult entanglements, and her readiness to act outside the rigid protocols of aristocratic propriety.

Her household is one of conspicuous wealth. She receives the Comtesse de Vandenesse in a room she herself describes as destined for demolition: "M. de Nucingen is going to deprive me of it; he is getting a new house built" [008-092]. The detail is characteristic of the Nucingen universe — wealth perpetually remaking its own physical setting, the banker's palace under construction mirroring the restless motion of the capital his operations drive.

## The Debt Rescue

The Baronne de Nucingen's critical role in the novel unfolds in [[ch08-a-lover-saved-and-lost|Chapter VIII]], when she becomes the financial instrument through which Marie-Angélique's rescue of Nathan is accomplished. The scheme, devised by [[marie-eugenie-du-tillet|Marie-Eugénie du Tillet]], requires an intermediary willing to advance forty thousand francs against promissory notes of dubious provenance, asking no questions and keeping no record that might implicate the borrower.

Marie-Eugénie explains the arrangement to her sister at the Opera: "Mme. de Nucingen will take four bills, each for ten thousand francs, signed by anyone you like, so as not to compromise you. She has explained to me how they are to be made out" [008-017]. The notes are to be signed by Schmucke, the sisters' old German music-master, and secured by Marie-Angélique's personal guarantee — a letter promising repayment that will later become the instrument of her exposure.

Early the next morning, the Comtesse de Vandenesse arrives at the Nucingen residence. "The Baroness was not yet up; but, in order not to keep a lady of position waiting, she flung round her a shawl and dressing gown" [008-085]. The domestic intimacy of the scene — a great lady received in her hostess's dressing gown — underscores the conspiratorial nature of the transaction. The Baronne greets her with warmth: "I am only too happy," she says, taking the four papers and the Countess's guarantee [008-087].

She rings for her maid and gives the order: "Theresa, tell the cashier to bring me up himself at once forty thousand francs" [008-089]. When the cashier arrives and begins to examine the signatures — noting that "Dis Schmucke iss ein musician vrom Ansbach" — the Baronne cuts him off with haughty finality: "Do you suppose I am transacting business? This is my affair" [008-094, 008-097]. The rebuke is effective in more than one sense: it silences the cashier's curiosity while simultaneously signaling to the Countess that the Baronne understands perfectly well that this is not, in truth, "her affair" at all — that she is acting as a discreet accomplice in a transaction whose real beneficiary she has shrewdly guessed.

## Discretion and Worldly Wisdom

The Baronne's handling of the aftermath reveals her experience in matters requiring secrecy. She keeps the Countess with her for a few minutes after the cashier leaves, "so that you may not seem to have anything to do with this matter" [008-099]. She arranges for the Countess's carriage to be sent to the end of the garden, and the two women cross the garden together so that "no one will see you leave this. The whole thing will remain a mystery" [008-101].

The narrator's commentary on her motives is revealing: "She knew, however, that Raoul was in difficulties, and was not deceived therefore by the sisters; she suspected shrewdly into whose hands the money would pass, and it gave her real pleasure to help the Countess. Entanglements of the kind always roused her deepest sympathy" [008-105]. The word "entanglements" is chosen with care — it is the word of a woman who has navigated her own share of complicated attachments, and who recognizes in another woman's predicament a predicament she understands from within.

When the Countess thanks her for her thoughtfulness, the Baronne's reply carries the weight of lived experience: "I don't know about thoughtfulness, but I have suffered a great deal; you, I trust, have paid less dearly for yours" [008-103]. The remark is at once generous and oblique — it acknowledges sympathy without demanding confidence, and it hints at a personal history of pain that the novel does not directly narrate but which any reader of *Le Père Goriot* would recognize.

## Between Rastignac and Vandenesse

The Baronne's entanglement in the affair does not end with the transaction. She lunches with [[eugene-de-rastignac|Eugène de Rastignac]], with whom she shares a long intimacy dating back to her youth in the Maison Vauquer. "Delphine and Rastignac had no secrets from each other, and she told him of her interview with the Countess" [008-106]. Rastignac, grasping the political implications immediately, explains "that she had this morning in all probability demolished the electoral hopes of du Tillet and rendered abortive the foul play and sacrifices of a whole year" [008-106].

Her reaction is characteristically practical: "If only the cashier does not speak of it to Nucingen" [008-107]. The danger, she intuits, lies not in the moral quality of what she has done but in the domestic and political machinery that her act of feminine solidarity has unwittingly disrupted.

When [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]] arrives in [[ch09-a-husbands-triumph|Chapter IX]] to return the forty thousand francs — having discovered his wife's secret — the Baronne receives him with composure. She returns the guarantee and the four bills, and when he attempts to explain the loan, she cuts him off: "Do not trouble to explain, since Mme. de Vandenesse has told you about it" [009-003]. The line is a masterpiece of social misdirection, allowing Vandenesse to believe his wife has been truthful when in fact the Baronne is merely declining to contradict whatever story he has been told.

Vandenesse, recognizing an opportunity, pivots to negotiation. He offers the Baronne a political quid pro quo: if a legitimist restoration should occur, he would use his influence to secure for M. de Nucingen "the peerage to which he aspires" [009-008]. In exchange, he asks only for what she knows about Nathan. The Baronne consents, and the transaction circles back upon itself — the money she lent to save Nathan becomes, in Vandenesse's hands, the means of destroying him.

## A Figure Between Worlds

The Baronne de Nucingen occupies a distinctive position in *A Daughter of Eve* — neither protagonist nor antagonist, neither insider nor outsider, she moves through the novel's intrigues with the ease of someone who has learned to inhabit the interstices of power without ever being fully of it. Her wealth opens every door; her birth ensures she will never be entirely at home behind any of them. This doubleness is the source of her acuity.

In the world of the novel, where Marie-Angélique's innocence and Marie-Eugénie's rectitude each prove insufficient to the crisis at hand, the Baronne's combination of worldly resources and unworldly sympathy provides the one thing the sisters cannot supply for themselves: the practical means of action. She asks no moral questions, imposes no conditions, and exacts no gratitude. Her forty thousand francs buy Nathan three months of borrowed time — time enough, as it turns out, for the truth to surface through other channels and for the Countess's romantic illusion to dissolve under the acid of her husband's counterplot.

## In La Comédie Humaine

The Baronne de Nucingen is one of Balzac's most fully realized recurring characters. She first appears as the unhappy younger daughter of Père Goriot in *Le Père Goriot* (1835), where her father bankrupts himself to provide her dowry and sustain her extravagant tastes, only to be abandoned by both daughters on his deathbed. Her long affair with Rastignac, which began in that novel, is still an active intimacy in *A Daughter of Eve*, where Rastignac remains her confidant and lunch companion.

She figures prominently in numerous other novels of the cycle, including *La Maison Nucingen*, *Splendeurs et misères des courtisanes*, *Le Contrat de mariage*, and *Les Secrets de la princesse de Cadignan*. Through her, Balzac traces the moral trajectory of a woman who has traded love for money and found the bargain insufficient — a figure whose suffering has taught her not bitterness but a wry, unsentimental compassion for others caught in the same machinery.
