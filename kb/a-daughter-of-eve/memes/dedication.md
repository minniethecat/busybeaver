The dedication of *A Daughter of Eve* by Honoré de Balzac is addressed to the Comtesse Bolognini, née Vimercati. This brief paratext — a few lines of elegant tribute — situates the novel within the network of aristocratic patronage and personal relationships that sustained Balzac's literary career.

The dedication reads as a gesture of homage and affection, dedicating "this work" to a woman identified only by her married and maiden names. Like many of Balzac's dedications, it operates as a private communication made public — a trace of the social world behind the fiction, where the author's relationships with aristocratic women shaped both his access to the society he described and his understanding of its inner dynamics.

In the context of the novel it introduces, the dedication to a woman — a countess — echoes the novel's central concerns: the position of women in aristocratic society, the power of names and titles, and the tension between public performance and private truth that defines Marie-Angélique's story.
