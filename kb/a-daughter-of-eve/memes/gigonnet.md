# Gigonnet

Gigonnet is an elderly moneylender and usurer who serves as an agent in [[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]]'s financial scheme against [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]] in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). A minor but vividly drawn figure, he embodies the cold, mechanical logic of credit in the novel's financial underworld — a man whose entire existence seems drained of human warmth in proportion to his accumulation of other people's obligations.

## The Instrument of Du Tillet's Trap

Gigonnet enters the plot when du Tillet, having already ensnared Nathan in debt, tightens the noose. In late December, the banker approaches Nathan with a show of friendly concern: he himself is hard up, but he recommends Nathan borrow the required sum from "a moneylender called Gigonnet — a twenty-five percent Providence for all young men in difficulties" [007-032]. Du Tillet provides Nathan with a letter of introduction, and Gigonnet obligingly hands over the amount, taking bills payable in twenty days.

The trap is perfectly baited. Nathan, far from suspecting collusion between the banker and the usurer, is "only vexed that he had not asked for more" [007-032]. Balzac notes with characteristic irony that men of great intellectual power "see only matter for pleasantry in a grave predicament, and reserve their wits for writing books" [007-032]. The loan is in fact the lynchpin of du Tillet's scheme to ruin Nathan financially and politically.

## The Usurer's Den

Nathan later regales [[emile-blondet|Blondet]] and Florine with a detailed description of Gigonnet and his surroundings that is one of the novel's most evocative minor sketches. He draws "a faithful picture of Gigonnet and his surroundings, his cheap fleur-de-lys wallpaper, his staircase, his asthmatic bell, his stag-foot knocker, his worn little door mat, his hearth as devoid of fire as his eye" [007-032].

Every detail Balzac selects suggests decay and coldness. The fleur-de-lys wallpaper — a royalist emblem — is cheap, a shabby relic of the Bourbon Restoration that the usurer inhabits. The bell is asthmatic, wheezing rather than ringing. The hearth, traditionally the warm heart of a home, holds no fire — just as Gigonnet's eye, Nathan notes, holds no warmth. The entire passage constructs usury not as a crime but as an atmosphere, a way of being in the world that has extinguished every human spark.

## Blondet's Taxonomy of Usury

When Nathan recounts his transaction to his friends, Blondet offers what amounts to the novel's moral taxonomy of moneylending. Gigonnet has charged only fifteen percent, and Blondet declares: "He deserves your thanks. At twenty-five they cease to be gentlemen; at fifty, usury begins; at this figure they are only contemptible!" [007-033].

The joke is characteristically Blondet's — witty, detached, a shade too pleased with its own cleverness. But it also encodes a serious social diagnosis. In Balzac's Paris, the boundary between legitimate banking and predatory lending is not a matter of law but of degree. [[emile-blondet|Blondet]]'s sliding scale implies that all credit is usury in some measure; the only question is how much polish the lender applies. Gigonnet at fifteen percent is merely the honest face of a system that [[du-tillet|du Tillet]], with his "opulence and good taste," practices behind a respectable facade.

## "Old Shylock" at Du Tillet's

Gigonnet's most significant scene occurs in chapter 8, when he calls at [[du-tillet|du Tillet]]'s house to report on Nathan's arrest. The banker greets him with the epithet "old Shylock" — a term that simultaneously acknowledges Gigonnet's function and distances du Tillet from it [008-109]. The greeting is also a question: "Well, old Shylock, is our man under lock and key?" Du Tillet expects the trap to have sprung.

But Gigonnet has the opposite news. "He has paid," the usurer says, "drawing from his pocketbook forty banknotes" [008-112]. The gesture is theatrical in its simplicity — the forty notes produced from the pocketbook, the concrete evidence that Nathan's debts have been mysteriously satisfied, that [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]]'s intervention has undone months of careful plotting.

Du Tillet's reaction is immediate and revealing: "A look of despair passed over du Tillet's face" [008-113]. The mask of the genial banker slips, if only for a moment. Gigonnet, by contrast, remains utterly unmoved. "You should never look askance at good money," he observes; "it's unlucky" [008-114]. The remark is gnomic, almost philosophical — the usurer's credo that money, whatever its source, has no moral quality.

When du Tillet, enraged, turns on his wife [[marie-eugenie-du-tillet|Marie-Eugénie]] and accuses her of undermining his plans, Gigonnet's detachment becomes a kind of comic relief. As the banker jumps up in fury, Gigonnet warns: "You will upset your lunch," laying hold of the tablecloth that has caught in du Tillet's dressing-gown [008-118]. Then, as the domestic argument escalates, he simply "saluted Mme. du Tillet and went out" — for he "took no interest in family broils" [008-125].

This exit is entirely characteristic. Gigonnet is interested in one thing — the movement of money — and once the transaction is complete, the human drama surrounding it holds no claim on his attention. In this he is, in a sense, the most honest character in the novel: he never pretends to be anything other than what he is.

## Function in the Novel

Gigonnet is more than a plot device, though he functions efficiently as one. As an instrument of du Tillet's scheme, he enables the financial machinery of the plot to tighten around Nathan. But as a character — with his cheap wallpaper, his fireless hearth, his pocketbook, and his indifference — he also serves as a moral emblem. He represents the pure, unadorned form of the credit economy that governs Balzac's Paris: a system in which every human bond is reducible to a debt, and every debt to a bill that must be paid.

In this reading, Gigonnet and [[du-tillet|du Tillet]] are two faces of the same coin. The banker conceals his predation behind the manners of a gentleman; the usurer displays it openly in every detail of his shabby house. The novel is never quite sure which is more contemptible.
