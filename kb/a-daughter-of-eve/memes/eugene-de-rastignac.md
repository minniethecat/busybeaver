# Eugène de Rastignac

Eugène de Rastignac is a recurring figure across [[la-comedie-humaine|Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*]], best known as the archetypal provincial arriviste who conquers Paris through charm, cunning, and the patronage of powerful women. In *A Daughter of Eve*, set in the early 1830s, Rastignac has already ascended to the position of Undersecretary of State in [[politics|Henri de Marsay's]] doctrinaire ministry, marking a mature phase in a career launched years earlier in *Le Père Goriot*.

## Political Position

Rastignac is one of the inner circle of de Marsay's government, grouped with "the de Marsays, the Rastignacs, and the la Roche-Hugons, who constituted the party in power" [004-021]. He is described as an Undersecretary of State — a role that places him at the nexus of ministerial power while still carrying the unmistakable ambition of a man not yet at the summit.

His political style is that of a novice struggling to appear seasoned. At Mme. d'Espard's salon, during a discussion about the unlikely alignment between Royalists and Republicans, "Rastignac, who, like all novices, took his politics a little too seriously," pronounces that the alliance "will not last" [006-052]. This assessment marks him as earnest but lacking the ironic detachment of seasoned operators like de Marsay.

## The Supper at Véry's

One of Rastignac's most revealing scenes occurs after Lady Dudley's great ball, the occasion of de Marsay's last appearance in society. Raoul Nathan and Émile Blondet leave the ball together, and "joining Rastignac, who offered them a place in his carriage, the three made merry over this conjunction of an eclectic Undersecretary of State with a fierce Republican and a political sceptic" [004-032].

Blondet proposes they "sup at the expense of law and order," and Rastignac takes them to Véry's, dismissing his carriage. Over champagne, the three men proceed to anatomize Parisian society. Rastignac and Blondet urge Nathan not to neglect the magnificent opportunity thrown in his way — meaning the infatuation of [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse]].

The two profligates "caricatured" Marie's "transparent childhood, that happy marriage," applying "the scalpel of epigram and the keen edge of mockery" [004-034]. When Nathan waxes poetic about finding a pure woman — a Beatrice, a Laura — Rastignac cuts him down with the irreverent quip, "Come, come, my good soul, shift the prayer of Moses on to the high notes, as Paganini does" [004-036]. Nathan retorts by calling him a "beast of a 'prentice minister" [004-038].

## Rastignac and de Marsay's Decline

As de Marsay's health fails, Rastignac positions himself as the dying minister's attendant. Nathan cynically observes that "Rastignac is acting as nurse, and hopes to be mentioned in the will" [006-062]. This remark captures both Nathan's contempt for political maneuvering and the perception that Rastignac's loyalty is inseparable from self-interest.

The strategy does not pay off immediately. "Rastignac, who had fallen with the Government which went to pieces on de Marsay's death, leant upon Raoul, and in return gave him the support of his good word" [007-024]. Out of power, Rastignac cultivates rising men like Nathan as stepping-stones back to influence. He is among those who "beguiled" Nathan into lending "ostentatious support to the doctrinaire party in the formation of one of their ephemeral cabinets" [007-008].

## The Nucingen Connection

Rastignac's long liaison with Delphine de Nucingen (née Goriot) is one of the throughlines of *La Comédie Humaine*. By the time of *A Daughter of Eve*, the relationship has evolved into a quasi-marital partnership: "Delphine and Rastignac had no secrets from each other" [008-106].

Yet Rastignac's ambitions are now fixed on the next generation. The Countess de Vandenesse casually mentions that Mme. de Nucingen's daughter "is engaged to M. de Rastignac" [008-093]. This betrothal — marrying the daughter of the woman who was his mistress — is a characteristically Balzacian arrangement, blending affection, convenience, and social strategy into a single transaction.

The intimacy between Rastignac and Delphine makes them a formidable intelligence-gathering unit. When Mme. de Nucingen unwittingly helps the Countess de Vandenesse rescue Nathan from financial ruin, she tells Rastignac everything at lunch. Rastignac, "who was playing the detective on the intrigues of the two bankers" — meaning [[du-tillet|du Tillet]] and [[baron-de-nucingen|Nucingen]] — immediately grasps that she has "demolished the electoral hopes of du Tillet and rendered abortive the foul play and sacrifices of a whole year" [008-106].

## Information Broker

Rastignac's role in the novel's resolution is that of an information conduit. The Baroness de Nucingen repeats to [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]] "her conversation of the morning with Rastignac" [009-011], providing crucial details about [[raoul-nathan|Nathan's]] entanglement. Vandenesse, understanding Rastignac's transactional nature, "dangled" before him the promise of a peerage for Nucingen "in order to extract from him further information" [009-013].

This double game — loyal to Delphine, cooperative with Vandenesse, aligned with du Tillet when it suits him — exemplifies Rastignac's political method. He operates at the intersection of every significant circle in the novel: the aristocratic salons of [[the-salons-of-paris|Mme. d'Espard and Lady Dudley]], the Bohemian revelry of [[florine|Florine's]] establishment, the financial world of Nucingen and du Tillet, and the corridors of ministerial power.

## The Rastignac Character

Rastignac in *A Daughter of Eve* is a man in mid-ascent — no longer the raw provincial student of *Le Père Goriot*, not yet the peer and minister he will become in later Balzac novels. He has acquired the carriage, the connections, and the air of command, but his ambition remains naked enough to be visible. The narrator's aside — "like all novices" — is both forgiving and damning.

His worldliness is complete. He moves easily between Florine's salon, where he is listed among the "diabolic legion of ferocious egotists" who frequent her house [005-014], and the gilded drawing-rooms of the Faubourg Saint-Germain. He is equally comfortable dining with actresses and brokering political futures over lunch with a baroness.

What distinguishes Rastignac from the other ambitious men in the novel is his patience. Unlike Nathan, who burns through his resources in a blaze of glory-seeking, or du Tillet, who schemes with the cold precision of a financier, Rastignac plays the long game. He attaches himself to the dying de Marsay, cultivates Nathan when Nathan is rising, draws intelligence from Delphine, and hedges his bets across every faction. He is not yet a master of Paris — but he is no longer anyone's student.
