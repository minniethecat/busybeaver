## Overview

The Marquise de Vandenesse is a minor character in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839). Likely the wife of Félix de Vandenesse's brother Charles (the Marquis de Vandenesse), she appears in salon conversations where her worldly wisdom contrasts with Marie-Angélique's innocence.

## Cynical Wisdom

The Marquise delivers one of the novel's most memorable cynical maxims about marriage and love. "With one's husband," she remarks, "one simply rubs along; with a lover it's life." This aphorism — worldly, disillusioned, and sharp — encapsulates the attitude of experienced Parisian society women toward the institution of marriage.

The words lodge in Marie-Angélique's mind, contributing to the climate of suggestion and temptation that surrounds her. The Marquise's casual cynicism is one of the many voices that normalize infidelity in the social world the novel depicts.

## Challenging Raoul

At one of the salon gatherings, the Marquise directly challenges [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]] about his eccentric behavior, asking him why he behaves so strangely. Nathan's pompous reply — "Are not pearls found in oyster shells?" [004-007] — is delivered partly for her benefit, exemplifying his theatrical self-presentation before aristocratic women.

## Significance

As a minor character, the Marquise de Vandenesse contributes to the novel's texture of worldly commentary. She is one of the chorus of voices — alongside [[marquise-d-espard|Mme. d'Espard]], [[lady-dudley|Lady Dudley]], and others — who articulate the knowing, disillusioned perspective of experienced society against which Marie-Angélique's innocence is measured and found wanting.
