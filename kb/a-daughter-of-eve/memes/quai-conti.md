# Quai Conti

The Quai Conti is a quay along the Left Bank of the Seine in Paris, situated between the Pont Neuf and the Pont des Arts, directly across the river from the Louvre. In Honoré de Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), the Quai Conti appears as the location of the aging German music-master Schmucke's humble lodgings -- a crucial destination in the sisters' desperate scheme to rescue Raoul Nathan from financial ruin.

## In *A Daughter of Eve*

The Quai Conti enters the novel at a moment of high dramatic tension. Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse and her sister Marie-Eugénie du Tillet have concocted a plan to raise forty thousand francs through forged bills of exchange, using the signatures of their old music-master Schmucke and the Baronne de Nucingen as guarantors. It is Marie-Eugénie who, during a whispered conversation at the Opera, gives her sister the address: "Schmucke lives in the little Rue de Nevers, on the Quai Conti; don't forget, and go yourself." (PN [008-019]).

The following morning, the Comtesse de Vandenesse acts on these instructions with characteristic determination. Balzac narrates: "She rose, took the arm of Vandenesse, who was waiting in the passage, and returned jubilant to her box; by and by she left the Opera, ordered her carriage for the next morning before eight o'clock, and found herself at half-past eight on the Quai Conti, having called at the Rue du Mail on her way." (PN [008-040]).

The physical description of the Quai Conti and its adjoining streets underscores the social descent that Marie-Angélique must undertake for the sake of her lover. "The carriage could not enter the narrow Rue de Nevers; but, as Schmucke's house stood at the corner of the Quay, the Countess was not obliged to walk to it through the mud. She almost leapt from the step of the carriage on to the dirty and dilapidated entrance of the grimy old house, which was held together by iron clamps, like a poor man's crockery, and overhung the street in quite an alarming fashion." (PN [008-041]).

## Symbolic significance

The contrast between the Quai Conti and the aristocratic quarters of the Right Bank is striking and deliberate. Marie-Angélique, the Comtesse de Vandenesse, descends from her elegant home in the Rue du Rocher into the dilapidated, cramped quarters of the Quai Conti. This physical journey mirrors her moral descent -- she is committing a form of fraud, however nobly motivated, to save the man she loves. The grime and poverty of Schmucke's neighborhood, with its "iron clamps" and overhanging architecture, stand in stark opposition to the blue velvet boudoir of her sister's house in the Rue Neuve-des-Mathurins and her own "paradise" in the Rue du Rocher.

The Quai Conti location also situates Schmucke -- a pure-hearted, unworldly musician -- in the Bohemian, artistic Left Bank, far from the salons of the Faubourg Saint-Germain. This geographical placement reflects his character: he is untouched by the corruptions of Parisian society, living in contented squalor with his cat and his pipe, his windows offering "a beautiful view of the Seine, from the Pont Neuf to the rising ground of Chaillot."

## Historical context

The Quai Conti takes its name from the Hôtel de Conti, the former townhouse of the Princes of Conti, a cadet branch of the Bourbon dynasty. The quay was constructed in the 17th century and by Balzac's time was a mixed neighborhood of modest dwellings and older aristocratic structures. Its position facing the Louvre made it a picturesque but unfashionable quarter, appropriate for a character like Schmucke whose talent far exceeds his worldly standing. The area was also known for its booksellers and print shops, linking it to the literary and intellectual life of the Left Bank.
