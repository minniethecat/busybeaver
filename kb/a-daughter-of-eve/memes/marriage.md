# Marriage

In Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve*, marriage is the axis around which all female existence turns. It is at once the only path to independence, the foundation of social identity, and the prison from which the novel's heroine seeks to escape.

## In the Novel

Marriage enters the story not as romance but as economic necessity. The two sisters are raised by a mother who teaches them that "independence meant marriage" — a lesson that strips the institution of any romantic illusion and presents it as the sole mechanism by which a woman of their class can achieve adulthood (001-006). The elder sister's marriage to Comte Félix de Vandenesse is narrated as a transaction that precedes the novel's action, its consequences unfolding across the chapters that follow (001-003).

After six years of marriage, Marie-Angélique has become "sophisticated" — a word Balzac uses to suggest not wisdom but a kind of spiritual exhaustion acquired through the daily negotiation of a loveless union (002-002). Her husband, Félix, had himself "resolved to cut short by marriage pleasures which had begun to weary him," treating the institution as a convenient terminus for a dissipated bachelorhood rather than the beginning of genuine partnership (003-004).

The marriage of the younger sister, Marie-Eugénie, to the banker Ferdinand du Tillet presents a different model: a union "welded together" by shared social ambition rather than torn apart by private longing (001-022). This marriage prospers not because it contains love but because it never expected to — it is a business partnership dressed in domestic form.

## Balzac's Treatment

Balzac examines marriage with the same unsentimental precision he brings to finance or politics. His innovation is to treat marriage not as the *ending* of a story but as its *beginning* — the condition from which all subsequent drama arises. He exposes the legal and economic architecture beneath the sentimental rhetoric, showing how the institution shapes and often deforms the emotional lives it encloses.

## Key Passages

> …independence meant marriage, and to this they looked as slaves look to freedom. (001-006)

> She was sophisticated after six years of marriage. (002-002)

> …resolved to cut short by marriage pleasures which had begun to weary him. (003-004)

> The marriage of both sisters — welded together by one and the same disaster… (001-022)

## Contrast With

- **Love**: Marriage and love are distinct and often opposed in the novel. Marriage is society's structure; love is the individual's rebellion against it.
- **Independence**: Paradoxically, marriage *is* the form independence takes for Balzac's women — a paradox the novel explores without resolving.

## In 19th-Century Context

Under the Napoleonic Code, married women had virtually no legal autonomy. They could not own property, sign contracts, or initiate legal proceedings without their husband's consent. Divorce, legalized during the Revolution, was abolished in 1816 and not reinstated until 1884. Balzac's focus on the economic dimension of marriage reflects this legal reality: for a woman of the upper bourgeoisie, marriage was the only economic transaction she would ever conduct in her own name.
