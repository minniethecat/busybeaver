The *Lettres édifiantes et curieuses* (Edifying and Curious Letters) were a collection of missionary reports published by the Jesuit order from 1702 to 1776. Mentioned in Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), they belong to the religious and moral literature that formed part of the Granville sisters' upbringing under their pious mother.

The *Lettres édifiantes* represented the Catholic missionary enterprise — reports from China, the Americas, and the Near East that combined religious edification with exotic curiosity. Their presence in the novel's cultural references evokes the enclosed, devout world of the [[comtesse-de-granville|Comtesse de Granville]]'s household, where such works constituted approved reading for young women being sheltered from the corruptions of modern literature.

The contrast between the *Lettres édifiantes* and the worldly culture of Parisian salons and journalism measures the distance Marie-Angélique must travel from her mother's world to the world of Nathan and Florine.
