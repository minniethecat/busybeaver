# Nathan's Suicide Attempt

Raoul Nathan's attempted suicide by charcoal asphyxiation is the emotional nadir of *A Daughter of Eve* — the moment when the novelist-journalist's financial collapse, political ambitions, and romantic entanglements converge into a single act of despair.

## The Crisis

Nathan's financial situation had become untenable. Dogged by creditors, with Florine's furniture distrained and his political prospects threatened by imminent arrest, Nathan faced the complete collapse of the world he had built. His self-confidence, always precarious, deserted him entirely [007-041].

## The Failed Escape

In his desperation, Nathan attempted to end his life by stifling himself with charcoal — a method Balzac presents with clinical detachment. The attempt failed, but its disclosure becomes a pivotal revelation in the novel's final act. Florine reveals the attempt to Marie-Angélique, exposing the full depth of Nathan's degradation [009-069].

## Marie-Angélique's Role

The suicide attempt marks a turning point in Marie-Angélique's education in the realities of [[passion]]. She had idealized Nathan as a genius; the revelation of his attempted suicide forces her to confront the consequences of the financial and emotional web in which they are all entangled.

## Aftermath

Balzac notes drily that "when suicide does not cure a man of life altogether, it cures him of a self-sought death" — Nathan never again contemplates self-destruction [009-106]. His rescue comes not through his own resilience but through the intervention of others: Marie-Angélique's husband Félix de Vandenesse settles Nathan's debts, extinguishing the immediate crisis while extracting a permanent obligation.

## Significance

The suicide attempt crystallizes several of the novel's central themes:

| Theme | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **Money and honor** | Nathan's financial ruin destroys his social standing; suicide appears as the only honorable exit |
| **Passion versus prudence** | Marie-Angélique's romantic illusions collide with the brutal reality of Nathan's desperation |
| **The fragility of genius** | Nathan's artistic self-image cannot survive material collapse |
| **Marriage as transaction** | Félix's bailout of Nathan is a purchase — of his wife's safety and Nathan's permanent submission |
