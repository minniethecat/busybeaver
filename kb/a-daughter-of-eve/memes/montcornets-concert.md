# Mme. de Montcornet's Concert

The concert given by the Comtesse de Montcornet is the pivotal social event of *A Daughter of Eve* — it is here, "at a concert given by the Comtesse de Montcornet, that Raoul Nathan, a well-known name in literature and politics," first encounters Marie-Angélique de Vandenesse [004-002]. This meeting initiates the central romantic plot and sets in motion the chain of events that will lead to Nathan's financial ruin and Marie-Angélique's moral crisis.

## Background

The Restoration [[society|Parisian social season]] revolved around a circuit of salons, concerts, and balls. The Comtesse de Montcornet's establishment was among the most prestigious. Her salon was known as a gathering place for the literary and political elite — "critics, Nucingen the banker, du Tillet, Conti the composer" [005-014] — a space where art and power mingled freely.

## The Event

The concert provided the setting for the first significant exchange between the novel's protagonist and antagonist. Nathan, already a celebrated literary figure, attended as a guest of standing. Marie-Angélique, the young and romantically dissatisfied wife of the Comte de Vandenesse, attended as part of her social obligations. The concert's formal structure — performers on stage, audience in attendance — created a theatrical frame within which their first glances and words acquired heightened significance.

The narrator notes the social dynamic precisely: "toward the end of the winter, Raoul Nathan, a well-known name in literature and politics" appeared at the concert [004-002]. The timing is significant — late winter, when the social season was at its peak and when Marie-Angélique's domestic discontent had reached its fullest expression.

## Significance

### The Romantic Catalyst

The concert functions as the novel's inciting romantic event. Before it, Marie-Angélique's dissatisfaction with her [[marriage]] was private and formless. After it, she has a specific object for her romantic longing — Nathan — and the plot acquires its direction.

### The Social Stage

The concert setting exemplifies how Balzac uses social events as dramatic stages. The formal concert, with its prescribed roles (performer, audience, host) and its expectation of controlled behavior, becomes the backdrop against which the most uncontrolled of emotions — romantic [[passion]] — begins to develop. The irony is characteristic of Balzac: the most socially regulated space produces the most socially transgressive desire.

### The Political Dimension

Nathan's presence at the concert is not merely social. He is "a well-known name in literature *and politics*" [004-002; emphasis added]. The concert represents his entry point into the aristocratic circles whose political support he needs. His romance with Marie-Angélique cannot be separated from his political [[ambition]] — the concert is where both pursuits converge.

## Consequences

| Consequence | Detail |
|------------|--------|
