## Overview

Fidelity — marital, moral, and political — is the virtue against which the novel's betrayals are measured. In Balzac's *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), fidelity is tested, questioned, and ultimately reaffirmed as the foundation of social and personal order. The novel does not treat fidelity as simple or costless; rather, it explores what fidelity demands and what it costs.

## Marital Fidelity

The novel presents a spectrum of marital fidelity. At one extreme stands [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie de Vandenesse]], whose fidelity to her husband is compromised emotionally and financially, though the novel leaves ambiguous whether the affair was physically consummated. At the other stands [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]], whose fidelity to Marie — both before and during the crisis — is presented as exemplary. His response to her betrayal — neither vengeful nor indifferent but protective and ultimately forgiving — represents a fidelity that transcends the merely contractual.

The [[comtesse-de-granville|Comtesse de Granville]]'s story provides a parallel. She too faced temptation in her youth and remained faithful, but at the cost of lasting unhappiness. Her fidelity was to principle rather than to a beloved husband, and the novel suggests this is a colder, more austere virtue.

## Fidelity and Honor

For Balzac's aristocrats, fidelity is bound up with honor. Félix de Vandenesse's conduct is governed not only by love for his wife but by the aristocratic code that demands a husband protect his name and household. When he discovers the financial entanglement Marie has entered into, his response is to manage the situation discreetly — fidelity to the social order as well as to his marriage.

## Political Fidelity

The novel contrasts genuine political fidelity with the opportunistic shifting of [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]], who moves from Saint-Simonism to Republicanism to potential Ministerialism [004-015]. Nathan's political inconstancy mirrors his personal inconstancy — the same lack of "coherence of ideas" [004-015] that de Marsay diagnoses in him politically manifests in his personal life.

## Fidelity and Art

Nathan's artistic betrayals — his compromises with commercial theater, his hack journalism — represent infidelity to his own talent. The novel suggests that fidelity to one's art is a moral quality: "continuous labor applied to the construction of a lasting fabric is beyond his conception" [004-016]. True artistic achievement, Balzac implies, requires the same constancy that true marriage requires.

## The Cost of Fidelity

Balzac is too honest a novelist to present fidelity as easy. Marie's fidelity to her husband costs her the excitement and passion that Nathan represents. The Comtesse de Granville's fidelity cost her happiness. Even Félix's fidelity costs him — he must absorb the wound of his wife's betrayal and respond with magnanimity rather than the vengeance that might feel more satisfying. Fidelity, in Balzac's world, is a discipline that exacts a price.
