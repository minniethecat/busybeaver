# *Pinto* (1801)

*Pinto, ou la Journée d'une conspiration* is a historical comedy in five acts by Népomucène Lemercier, first performed at the Théâtre Français on 22 March 1801. The play dramatizes the conspiracy that led to Portugal's liberation from Spanish rule in 1640, centering on the figure of João Pinto Ribeiro. It was a landmark work in French theatrical history, bridging the neoclassical tradition and the emerging Romantic sensibility. Its innovative structure -- mixing historical seriousness with comic elements -- made it a touchstone for later Romantic dramatists.

## Significance in *A Daughter of Eve*

In Balzac's novel, *Pinto* is invoked as a model for [[raoul-nathan]]'s earliest literary ambitions. At [004-011], we learn that Nathan, early in his career, "had already tried the theatre which represents the fine traditions of the French drama with a splendid romantic play in the style of *Pinto*." This reference is carefully chosen. Lemercier's play represented a kind of respectable historical drama that could challenge the classical unities without falling into the excesses that would later characterize the Romantic battle at the Théâtre Français. For Nathan, emulating *Pinto* signals an ambition to write serious, historically-grounded drama within the French tradition, not merely fashionable melodrama.

The play's fate in the novel mirrors Nathan's own career trajectory. Nathan's *Pinto*-style play was staged at the Odéon, and "the result was that the Odéon became for three nights the scene of such disorder that the piece had to be stopped." This theatrical riot prefigures the more famous *bataille d'Hernani* of 1830, positioning Nathan as a precursor to the Romantic revolution in French theatre. Yet unlike Victor Hugo, Nathan could not sustain the fight: "instead of sticking to this arduous path, he was driven by stress of poverty to fall back upon more profitable work."

## The Play and Its Author

Lemercier (1771--1840) was a transitional figure in French letters. A protégé of Marie-Joseph Chénier, he achieved early fame with *Pinto* and later with *Agamemnon* (1797). His work resisted easy classification: he admired the classical tradition but experimented with historical subject matter and mixed tones that anticipated Romantic drama. Napoleon admired *Pinto* but Lemercier's independent spirit eventually cost him imperial favor. By the 1820s, younger Romantics saw him as a precursor, though he never fully aligned with their movement.

The play itself unfolds across a single day in Lisbon, following Pinto as he orchestrates the conspirators who will overthrow Spanish rule. Its innovations included the use of prose for comic scenes and verse for elevated moments, a large cast of historical figures, and rapid scene changes that strained neoclassical conventions. These formal experiments made *Pinto* a natural reference point for ambitious young playwrights like the fictional Nathan who sought to modernize French drama without wholly abandoning its traditions.

## Literary-Historical Context

For Balzac's readers in 1839, the reference to *Pinto* carried specific weight. By then, the Romantic revolution in theatre had been fought and largely won. Hugo's *Hernani* (1830) and Dumas's *Henri III et sa cour* (1829) had established the new dramatic idiom. Looking back to Lemercier's 1801 play allowed Balzac to suggest that Nathan belonged to an earlier, more idealistic phase of the Romantic movement -- a time when writing a historical drama in the style of *Pinto* still seemed like a path to artistic glory rather than commercial success.

The failure of Nathan's *Pinto*-style play at the Odéon also signals a recurring theme in Balzac's [[la-comedie-humaine]]: the tension between artistic integrity and the marketplace. Nathan possesses real talent -- his play "seemed to many people a masterpiece" -- but the commercial logic of Parisian theatre, which demanded light vaudevilles and spectacular pieces, eventually consumed his serious ambitions.
