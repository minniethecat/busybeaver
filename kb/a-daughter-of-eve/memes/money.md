# Money

Money in *A Daughter of Eve* is not merely a plot device — it is the invisible force that shapes every character's choices and ultimately determines the story's outcome. Balzac, the great sociologist of nineteenth-century capitalism, understood that in post-Restoration [[society|France]], money had replaced blood as the organizing principle of social life.

## Money as Plot Engine

The novel's central plot is a financial transaction disguised as a love story. [[raoul-nathan|Raoul Nathan]]'s debts to [[du-tillet|Ferdinand du Tillet]] are the mechanism through which the banker controls the writer. When du Tillet "graciously renewed" Nathan's bills in October [007-021], he was not being generous — he was tightening his grip. By December, he "asked for his money" with calculated precision [007-032], knowing Nathan could not pay.

The chain of financial dependency runs through the entire cast. [[marie-angelique-de-vandenesse|Marie-Angélique]] sells her diamonds to pay Nathan's debts — "Where is the money to come from?" she asks [005-040], and the answer is her own jewelry, sold in secret. [[florine|Florine]], the actress, also contributes: "the money given by Florine, the third part of which went for interest" [007-009]. Even love is denominated in francs.

## The Moneylender's World

Balzac populates his novel with the apparatus of credit: moneylenders, usurers, and the shadow economy of Parisian finance. Nathan's "uncle" — "the word meant a moneylender, just as 'my aunt' was used for a female usurer" [005-042] — is a figure who exists in the margins of polite society but determines its fate. When Nathan can "no longer look to moneylenders" [007-040], his collapse is inevitable.

The banker du Tillet embodies the fusion of respectability and predation that Balzac saw as characteristic of modern finance. His house displays "opulence and good taste" [001-003], but his methods are indistinguishable from those of the usurer Gigonnet. The boundary between legitimate banking and criminal lending is, in Balzac's Paris, purely social.

## Marriage as Financial Contract

Every [[marriage]] in the novel is, at its foundation, a financial arrangement. Marie-Angélique's marriage to [[comte-felix-de-vandenesse|Félix de Vandenesse]] brought her social position but no independent wealth — she must ask her husband for money and be refused. Eugénie's marriage to du Tillet made her rich in name but penniless in fact: "I have not a sou of my own" [002-012].

Balzac's insight is that the financial structure of marriage determines its emotional possibilities. A wife without independent means cannot leave, cannot act, cannot even help her sister without subterfuge. The novel's women are trapped not by law but by money — or its absence.

## Money's Moral Ambiguity

Balzac refuses to moralize about money. The novel's final scenes are revealing: "I would never look askance at good money" [008-114], says a character, and the statement is neither condemned nor celebrated. Money is simply the medium in which social life occurs. The question is not whether to handle money but how to handle it without being destroyed.

This ambivalence extends to the novel's resolution. Marie-Angélique's intervention saves Nathan from financial ruin, but the money comes from Vandenesse, who "returned her the money" [009-002] — a gesture that restores the marital order by reaffirming the husband's financial control. Even salvation is a transaction.
