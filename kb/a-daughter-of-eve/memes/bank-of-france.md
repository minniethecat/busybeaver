# Bank of France

The Bank of France (Banque de France) was the central financial institution of Restoration and July Monarchy France, founded by Napoleon Bonaparte in 1800 to stabilize the French economy after the turbulence of the Revolutionary period. In *A Daughter of Eve*, the Bank of France appears at a critical moment in the novel's financial plot — not as a character or a setting, but as the ultimate guarantor of liquidity, the institution to which a man of means turns when he must move large sums of money with speed and discretion.

## Role in the Novel

The Bank of France enters the novel in Chapter 9 ("A Husband's Triumph"), at the moment when Comte Félix de Vandenesse discovers the full extent of his wife's secret financial entanglement with Raoul Nathan. Having learned from the Baronne de Nucingen that Marie-Angélique has guaranteed forty thousand francs in promissory notes to rescue Nathan from debtors' prison, Félix acts with the swiftness of a man accustomed to command:

> Félix, on his part, went at once to draw forty thousand francs from the Bank of France, and then hastened to Mme. de Nucingen. He found her at home, thanked her for the confidence she had shown in his wife, and returned her the money. He gave, as the reason for this mysterious loan, an excessive almsgiving, on which he had wished to impose some limit. [009-002]

The transaction is revealing on multiple levels. Félix does not need to arrange a loan, sell securities, or negotiate with a private banker — he simply goes to the Bank of France and draws forty thousand francs, a sum that represents a small fortune in the 1830s. The ease of this transaction demonstrates his financial solidity and his standing in the world of established wealth. The Bank of France here functions as the repository of the kind of capital that is never at risk, the wealth that can be mobilized instantly to cover a crisis.

The counterplot page elaborates on the aftermath: "Félix, concealing his shock, repaid the forty thousand francs from the Bank of France on the spot, recovering every piece of paper that could compromise his wife" [009-002, 009-005]. The Bank of France is thus the instrument through which Félix reasserts control — he extinguishes his wife's debts with the same institutional efficiency with which he manages his political affairs.

## The Bank of France in Balzac's World

The Bank of France appears throughout *La Comédie Humaine* as the anchor of the financial system, the solid center around which the more speculative activities of private bankers, moneylenders, and stock-jobbers revolve. In a fictional universe obsessively concerned with money — its creation, its circulation, its loss, its power — the Bank of France represents institutional stability and official legitimacy. It is the bank of the state and of the established aristocracy, as opposed to the private banking houses of men like Nucingen and du Tillet, which operate in the more speculative and morally ambiguous territory of high finance.

The contrast is significant in *A Daughter of Eve*. Du Tillet's financial scheme against Nathan operates through the shadowy mechanisms of discounted bills, accommodation paper, and legal pressure — the instruments of private banking and commercial credit. When Félix de Vandenesse intervenes, he does so through the Bank of France, the institution of official wealth. The contrast between these two financial worlds — the speculative and the solid, the aggressive and the established — mirrors the novel's larger opposition between the new men of finance (du Tillet) and the old aristocracy (Vandenesse).

## Historical Context

Founded in 1800, the Bank of France was granted the exclusive right to issue banknotes in Paris, a privilege that was gradually extended to the entire country. By the 1830s, the period of *A Daughter of Eve*, the Bank was a powerful institution with branches in major provincial cities, though its note issue remained limited and metallic reserves (gold and silver) still dominated everyday transactions. The Bank's discount rate and its willingness to accept commercial paper were critical determinants of credit conditions throughout the French economy.

The Bank of France was closely tied to the regime in power. Under the Bourbon Restoration, it was associated with the financial interests that supported the monarchy; under the July Monarchy, it became linked to the haute bourgeoisie that formed Louis-Philippe's political base. For a man like Félix de Vandenesse — a count, a political figure, a man of established fortune — the Bank of France was a natural financial home, an institution that recognized and served his class.

## Significance in the Novel

The Bank of France's role in *A Daughter of Eve*, though confined to a single transaction, illuminates the novel's financial architecture. Money in Balzac's world has different qualities depending on its source and the institution through which it moves. The forty thousand francs that Marie-Angélique raises through secret bills, Nucingen's guarantee, and her sister's complicity is tainted money — money generated through deception that carries with it the threat of exposure and disgrace. The forty thousand francs that Félix draws from the Bank of France is clean money — institutional, official, above suspicion. By repaying Nucingen with Bank of France funds, Félix not only recovers the compromising documents but symbolically launders the entire transaction, replacing the murky instruments of private credit with the solid currency of the state.

The episode also demonstrates the asymmetry of financial power in Balzac's Paris. Marie-Angélique must risk her reputation, her marriage, and her sister's complicity to raise forty thousand francs. Félix simply walks to the Bank of France and withdraws the sum. The Bank is thus an instrument of class power as much as financial infrastructure — an institution that serves those who already possess wealth and standing, while remaining inaccessible to those, like Nathan, who most desperately need it.
