*Les Aventures de Télémaque* (The Adventures of Telemachus, 1699) by François Fénelon was one of the most widely read books of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. In *A Daughter of Eve* (1839), its mention places Marie-Angélique's education within the classical and moral curriculum typical of aristocratic young women of the Restoration.

Fénelon's novel, based on the story of Telemachus searching for his father Odysseus, was both an adventure and a moral treatise — a "mirror for princes" disguised as fiction. Its presence in the novel's cultural references evokes the world of devout, classical education that shaped Marie-Angélique and left her unprepared for the temptations of modern Paris.
