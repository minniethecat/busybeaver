# Stone Angel

## Definition

The stone angel is the central symbol of *Look Homeward, Angel*. It first appears in the novel's opening paragraphs as Oliver Gant — Eugene's father — watches a stone angel in a Baltimore shop window, and is seized by an unquenchable desire to carve "an angel's head" from cold marble. The angel embodies the aesthetic ideal that haunts the Gant family: a dream of beauty, permanence, and transcendence that is forever just out of reach.

Wolfe uses the angel both as a literal object — the fly-specked marble statue that stands on the porch of Gant's stonecutting shop in Altamont — and as a metaphysical symbol of the artist's hunger. The angel is never perfectly realized; Oliver Gant dies having never carved the angel's head he dreamed of in his youth. The novel's title — *Look Homeward, Angel* — is drawn from Milton's "Lycidas" and invokes the angel as both a guardian spirit and an object of longing for a lost home.

## Textual Evidence

The angel motif is introduced in the novel's celebrated overture, linking Oliver Gant's origins in England and Pennsylvania to the "soft stone smile of an angel" that becomes his obsession:

> (001-001) A destiny that leads the English to the Dutch is strange enough; but one that leads from Epsom into Pennsylvania, and thence into the hills that shut in Altamont over the proud coral cry of the cock, and the soft stone smile of an angel, is touched by that dark miracle of chance which makes new magic in a dusty world.

The boy Oliver first encounters the angel in a Baltimore shop window, and the experience marks him permanently:

> (001-007) And the year the war had ended, when he was still fifteen, he had walked along a street in Baltimore, and seen within a little shop smooth granite slabs of death, carved lambs and cherubim, and an angel poised upon cold phthisic feet, with a smile of soft stone idiocy... He felt that he wanted, more than anything in the world, to carve delicately with a chisel. He wanted to wreak something dark and unspeakable in him into cold stone. He wanted to carve an angel's head.

The novel makes clear that the angel is a symbol of the unattainable — Oliver never achieves his goal:

> (001-009) He never found it. He never learned to carve an angel's head. The dove, the lamb, the smooth joined marble hands of death, and letters fair and fine — but not the angel. And of all the years of waste and loss... these are blind steps and gropings of our exile, the painting of our hunger as, remembering speechlessly, we seek the great forgotten language, the lost lane-end into heaven, a stone, a leaf, a door. Where? When?

The angel is also a recurring presence on the porch of the shop. Gant both loves and curses it:

> (019-064) No one knew how fond he was of the angel. Publicly he called it his White Elephant. He cursed it and said he had been a fool to order it. For six years it had stood on the porch, weathering, in all the wind and the rain. It was now brown and fly-specked. But it had come from Carrara in Italy, and it held a stone lily delicately in one hand. The other hand was lifted in benediction, it was poised clumsily upon the ball of one phthisic foot, and its stupid white face wore a smile of soft stone idiocy.

In his rages, Gant's fury turns toward the angel:

> (019-065) "Fiend out of Hell!" he roared. "You have impoverished me, you have ruined me, you have cursed my declining years, and now you will crush me to death, fearful, awful, and unnatural monster that you are."

The angel's absence is felt as a void:

> (019-070) He was silent, thinking for a moment of the place where the angel stood. He knew he had nothing to cover or obliterate that place — it left a barren crater in his heart.

The angel reappears in the novel's final passages, now in the moonlight of Eugene's last night in Altamont:

> (040-004) Eugene saw his father's name, faded, on the old brick in moonlight. On the stone porch of the shop, the angels held their marble posture. They seemed to have frozen, in the moonlight.

In one of the most surreal and moving moments of the novel — Eugene's final conversation with the ghost of his dead brother Ben — the stone angel comes to life:

> (040-029) With a strong rustle of marble and a cold sigh of weariness, the angel nearest Eugene moved her stone foot and lifted her arm to a higher balance. The slender lily stipe shook stiffly in her elegant cold fingers.

> (040-033) "What of it?" Ben asked irritably. "It has a right to, hasn't it? You know," he added with biting sarcasm, "there's no law against an angel lifting its arm if it wants to."

## Analysis

The stone angel operates on multiple symbolic levels. First, it represents **the unattainable artistic ideal** — Oliver Gant's dream of carving beauty from stone, a dream he never fulfills. Wolfe identifies Gant's hunger as the same impulse that drives the artist, the poet, the seeker of lost things. The angel's "soft stone idiocy" — its vacant, meaningless smile — suggests that the ideal itself may be empty, yet the hunger for it is what drives human striving.

Second, the angel embodies **the tension between permanence and transience**. Stone seems eternal, but Gant's marbles erode, become fly-specked, and his name fades on the brick wall of his shop. The angel outlasts the man who bought it — it remains standing after Gant's death — yet it cannot confer immortality. Only the memory of the quest survives.

Third, the angel is linked to **the novel's structure and title**. The phrase "Look Homeward, Angel" is drawn from John Milton's elegy "Lycidas," a poem about a drowned poet. In Wolfe's novel, the angel looks homeward — toward a home that cannot be found, toward a lost Eden of childhood and belonging. The angel looks toward what has been lost, just as Eugene, at the novel's end, looks back at Altamont before departing.

The motif's recurrence in the final ghostly scene with Ben — where the angel actually moves — signals that the boundary between the living and the dead, the real and the imagined, has dissolved. The angel, frozen for decades, finally stirs at the moment Eugene must leave home forever.

## Connections

### Other Concepts
- **Art and creation** — the angel is Wolfe's primary symbol for the artist's doomed hunger
- **Death and mortality** — the angel is a funerary monument, linked to Gant's profession as a stonecutter of tombstones
- **The lost father / searching for a father** — Oliver's quest for the angel's head becomes Eugene's inheritance as an artist

### Characters
- **Oliver Gant** — the stonecutter who dreams of carving the angel's head and dies without achieving it; the angel is his soul's outward sign
- **Eugene Gant** — inherits his father's hunger for the ideal, transformed into a writer's search for the right words rather than the right stone
- **Ben Gant** — in death, becomes the mediator between Eugene and the animated angel, speaking the novel's most enigmatic line about its right to move

### Places
- **Gant's stone shop on the Square** — the angel's physical home for decades, the threshold between the world of the living and the world of art
- **Baltimore shop window** — where young Oliver first sees the angel and receives his life's calling
- **Altamont cemetery** — where Gant is buried and where his stone carvings — lambs, doves, cherubim, and the never-completed angel — haunt the landscape
