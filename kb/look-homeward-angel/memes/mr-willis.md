# Mr. Willis

## Synopsis

Mr. Willis appears in a single brief scene in Chapter 38 — a classroom exchange in which he is called upon to define a stick and offers the answer "a piece of wood," only to be gently dismantled by the teacher's Socratic follow-up. His appearance, though vanishingly brief, is typical of Wolfe's technique of populating even minor scenes with named individuals whose small failures of thought become moments of collective classroom drama. The formal address "Mr. Willis" — used by the teacher — reflects the mock-serious courtesy with which the school treats its pupils, a detail that places the scene in its early-twentieth-century pedagogical context.

## Role in the Novel

### The Classroom Exchange (Chapter 38)

Mr. Willis's entire presence in *Look Homeward, Angel* consists of four lines of dialogue and a narrative frame that together form a miniature comedy of incomplete definition. The teacher calls on him:

> "Mr. Willis?" [038-043]

The question mark alone signals that this is a quiz — a demand for a definition. Another student provides the context:

> "Mr. Willis says a stick is a piece of wood." [038-051]

This reported speech, filtered through a classmate, already distances Mr. Willis from his own answer. He is not speaking for himself; his definition is being recited back to the class as evidence. When pressed, Mr. Willis defends himself:

> "But a stick is a piece of wood," says Mr. Willis. [038-053]

The protest is reasonable — his definition is not *wrong*, merely insufficient. And the teacher's rejoinder is swift and devastating:

> "So is a tree or a telephone-pole. No, I'm afraid that will not do. Does the class agree with Mr. Willis?" [038-054]

The teacher's counterexample — a tree, a telephone-pole — exposes the logical flaw by widening the category: if a stick is a piece of wood, is everything made of wood equally a stick? The teacher's rhetorical question to the class completes the dismissal by turning Mr. Willis's peers into the jury that confirms his failure.

### Significance

Mr. Willis's four-line appearance serves a precise narrative purpose. In a chapter set at the Altamont school — one of many that chronicle Eugene's education — the exchange dramatizes the Socratic method in miniature. Mr. Willis is not a character in any conventional sense; he is a pedagogical foil, the student whose inadequate answer provides the occasion for the teacher's wit. Yet Wolfe gives him a name and a formal address, dignifying the moment and preserving it as one of the thousand small dramas of classroom life.

The scene also reflects a recurring Wolfean interest in language and definition. Throughout the novel, characters struggle with the gap between words and the things they name. Mr. Willis's "a stick is a piece of wood" is a child's first approximation of definition — true but useless, a tautology disguised as an explanation. The teacher's objection — that the definition fails to distinguish a stick from a tree — is the first lesson in the art of precision, and Mr. Willis, however briefly, is the vehicle through which that lesson is delivered.
