# Hunger for Voyages

## Definition

The hunger for voyages — the restless, insatiable need to move, to travel, to depart — is one of the novel's most persistent motifs. Oliver Gant's life is a series of journeys: from England to Pennsylvania, from Pennsylvania to Baltimore, from Baltimore to the South, from Altamont to California on his "last great voyage." Eugene inherits this hunger as a spiritual restlessness, a conviction that somewhere beyond the hills that "shut in Altamont" there exists a larger life.

Wolfe connects the hunger for voyages to the novel's central theme of searching. The physical journey — by train, by foot, by car — is the outward expression of an inward quest. The characters travel not to arrive but to seek; the destination is always "a stone, a leaf, an unfound door."

## Textual Evidence

The novel begins with a journey — destiny drawing Oliver across oceans and states:

> (001-001) A destiny that leads the English to the Dutch is strange enough; but one that leads from Epsom into Pennsylvania, and thence into the hills that shut in Altamont over the proud coral cry of the cock, and the soft stone smile of an angel, is touched by that dark miracle of chance which makes new magic in a dusty world.

Oliver's "aimless drift along the continent" after losing Cynthia defines him as a perpetual wanderer:

> (001-015) So, alone and lost again, having found neither order nor establishment in the world, and with the earth cut away from his feet, Oliver resumed his aimless drift along the continent. He turned westward toward the great fortress of the hills.

Gant's last great voyage — to California at age fifty-six — is described as a final assertion of the hunger:

> (007-001) This journey to California was Gant's last great voyage. He made it two years after Eliza's return from St. Louis, when he was fifty-six years old. In the great frame was already stirring the chemistry of dissolution.

Eugene, packing to leave Altamont, lingers with "desperate pain" at the threshold of departure:

> (039-031) He still loitered, although his baggage had been packed for days. With a desperate pain, he faced departure from that Arcadian wilderness where he had known so much joy.

The novel's final vision pairs departure with the ghostly summons — the dead Ben telling Eugene he must go:

> (040-029) With a strong rustle of marble and a cold sigh of weariness, the angel nearest Eugene moved her stone foot and lifted her arm to a higher balance.

Eugene's walk through the Square on his last night captures the hunger for voyages as a form of visionary restlessness:

> (035-027) The powerful car roared up into the chill dead Square. The feeling of unreality grew upon the boy. He sought for his life, for the bright lost years, in this mean cramped huddle of brick and stone.

## Analysis

The hunger for voyages in Wolfe's novel is not tourism or adventure but a metaphysical condition. The Gants move because they cannot stay still — because stillness feels like death. This restlessness is inherited from Oliver, whose life is a chronicle of departures: leaving England, leaving Baltimore, leaving Cynthia (or being left by her), leaving sobriety for drunkenness and back again.

For Eugene, the hunger for voyages becomes the writer's hunger. He leaves Altamont not merely to go somewhere else but to transform the experience of leaving into art. The journey outward is also a journey inward — toward the material of the novel we are reading.

Wolfe's treatment of the voyage motif connects to the American literary tradition of the open road, but with a crucial difference: his voyagers are never free. They carry their past with them, and every departure is also a loss.
