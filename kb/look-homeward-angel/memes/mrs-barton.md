# Mrs. Barton

## Synopsis

Mrs. Barton is the mother of Hugh Barton and a significant presence in the wedding chapter of *Look Homeward, Angel*. She is described with characteristic Wolfean physicality as a large, kindly, authoritative woman whose "slow but emphatic approval" of her son's opinions defines her role in the family. Her near-fatal digestive collapse on Helen's wedding night — the result of "prenuptial banqueting" — becomes the first crisis of the marriage, delaying the honeymoon and forcing Helen into the role of nurse before she has had a single day as a wife. In this, Mrs. Barton serves as an omen: the mother-in-law whose body, quite literally, interposes itself between the newlyweds and their future.

## Role in the Novel

### The Wedding (Chapter 27)

Mrs. Barton appears at the wedding celebrations, where her primary function is to serve as a mirror for Hugh's virtues:

> Mrs. Barton wagged her great head with the slow but emphatic approval she accorded all her son's opinions. [027-133]

The verb "wagged" combined with "great head" and "slow but emphatic approval" creates a portrait of bovine maternal devotion — she is not a thinker but a believer, and the object of her belief is her son. Wolfe extends this characterization:

> Mrs. Barton, too, felt the extent of her loss. She wanted to be sure that Helen realized the extent of her acquisition of one of the latter-day saints. [027-142]

The phrase "one of the latter-day saints" is steeped in Wolfe's irony — Hugh Barton is no saint, but his mother's devotion transforms him into one. Helen's reaction is characteristically sharp: "I don't think it's such a bad bargain for him either, you know. I think pretty well of myself, too" (027-145). The tension between mother-in-law and daughter-in-law is established in a single exchange: Mrs. Barton wants gratitude; Helen wants equality.

Mrs. Barton's authority is expressed in a brief call from the veranda — "kindly but authoritative" (027-150) — that summons the wedding party. It is the voice of the matriarch, accustomed to command, softened by affection but undiluted by doubt.

### The Collapse

The most dramatic event of Mrs. Barton's narrative arc occurs on Helen's wedding night:

> But Hugh and Helen did not go honeymooning the next morning, as they had planned. During the night, as she lay at Dixieland, old Mrs. Barton was taken with a violent, a retching sickness. For once, her massive digestive mechanism failed to meet the heavy demands she had put upon it during the prenuptial banqueting. She came near death. [027-172]

The phrase "massive digestive mechanism" is classic Wolfe — a clinical description that magnifies rather than diminishes its subject, transforming indigestion into something almost mythic. The detail of "prenuptial banqueting" as the cause links Mrs. Barton's physical excess to the social excess of the wedding itself: she has consumed too much, celebrated too hard, and her body has rebelled.

Helen's response to the crisis is magnificent. Wolfe describes how she "hurled her vitality into the sick woman's care; dominant, furious, all-mastering, she blew back her life into her" (027-173). The language of violent salvation — "hurled," "furious," "all-mastering," "blew back her life" — captures the paradox of Helen's character: she saves through force, heals through domination. Within three days Mrs. Barton is out of danger, but "her complete recovery was slow, ugly, and painful" (027-173).

### The Thwarted Honeymoon

The consequence of Mrs. Barton's illness is a honeymoon that never happens. As the days drag on, Helen becomes "more and more bitter over her thwarted honeymoon," rushing out of the sickroom with "writhen face" (027-173). Mrs. Barton's feeble voice — "Hel-en! Oh Hel'en!" (027-179) — calls her back, a chain of obligation that is also a chain of resentment.

The delayed honeymoon becomes one of the first cracks in the Barton marriage — a small thing, perhaps, but emblematic of the larger pattern in which family obligations, maternal claims, and bodily frailty conspire to thwart Helen's hunger for a life of her own.

### A Late Appearance

Mrs. Barton reappears briefly in Chapter 31, mentioned in a letter from Laura James to Eugene: "Give my love to Mrs. Barton, and tell your mother I said she must not work so hard in that hot kitchen" (031-028). The casual mention places Mrs. Barton in the ongoing fabric of Altamont life — still present, still the mother-in-law, still part of the world Eugene has left behind.
