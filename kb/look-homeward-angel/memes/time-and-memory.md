# Time and Memory

## Definition

Time in *Look Homeward, Angel* is not a straight line but a vast, enfolding presence. The past is not behind us but alongside us — a claim the novel makes narratively through its montage-like structure, where decades compress into paragraphs and a single moment can expand to fill pages. Wolfe treats memory as the artist's primary material: what the writer does is recover the past, fix it in language, make it permanent.

The novel's famous overture compresses generations of Gant family history into a few pages, moving from England to Pennsylvania to Altamont in a rush of images that establish the novel's temporal ambition: this is not just one boy's story but the accumulated weight of all the lives that made him possible.

## Textual Evidence

The novel's opening immediately establishes time as something vast and strange — a "dark miracle of chance" linking past to present:

> (001-001) A destiny that leads the English to the Dutch is strange enough; but one that leads from Epsom into Pennsylvania, and thence into the hills that shut in Altamont over the proud coral cry of the cock, and the soft stone smile of an angel, is touched by that dark miracle of chance which makes new magic in a dusty world.

Oliver, near death, sees his life as shaped by a "series of accidents" — time as chaos, not order:

> (001-019) Suddenly, he saw that his life had been channelled by a series of accidents: a mad Rebel singing of Armageddon, the sound of a bugle on the road, the mule-hoofs of the army, the silly white face of an angel in a dusty shop, a slut's pert wiggle of her hams as she passed by.

The past erupts into the present as "the grisly parade of the spectre years":

> (001-019) The grisly parade of the spectre years trooped through his brain.

Eugene, as a young boy, already experiences time as something that overtakes him — the future rushing toward him:

> (004-034) He had been sent from one mystery into another: somewhere within or without his consciousness he heard a great bell ringing faintly, as if it sounded undersea.

Memory is inextricable from place — Eugene returning to Altamont feels the unreality of time's passage:

> (035-027) The feeling of unreality grew upon the boy. He sought for his life, for the bright lost years, in this mean cramped huddle of brick and stone.

Gant's name fading on the shop wall is the novel's image of time's erosion — and art's resistance to it:

> (040-004) Eugene saw his father's name, faded, on the old brick in moonlight.

The novel's final passages — "a stone, a leaf, an unfound door" — are Wolfe's attempt to fix the vanished world of Altamont in language that will outlast the physical town:

> (001-009) These are blind steps and gropings of our exile, the painting of our hunger as, remembering speechlessly, we seek the great forgotten language, the lost lane-end into heaven, a stone, a leaf, a door. Where? When?

The novel's concluding vision asserts the return of what has passed — a cyclical understanding of time:

> (037-099) And pain and pride and death will pass, and will not come again. And light and dawn will come again, and the laurel and the lizard and the stone will come again.

## Analysis

Wolfe's treatment of time is the novel's most radical formal feature. The narrative does not unfold chronologically so much as it accumulates — scenes pile onto scenes, memories interrupt memories, and the reader experiences time as the characters do: as a flood of associations, not a sequence of events. This technique, which Wolfe would push even further in *Of Time and the River*, is the novel's most important contribution to modernist fiction.

Memory is not neutral recollection but an act of creation. When Eugene remembers his father, he remakes him — Gant becomes larger than life, a mythic figure striding through Altamont. The novel is, in this sense, a monument: carved not from marble but from language, meant to outlast the lives it describes.

Time's erosive power — the faded name on the brick, the fly-specked angel, the dead leaves on Ben's grave — is inevitably counterposed against art's preservative ambition. The novel itself is the answer: it exists because Wolfe refused to let the past be past.

### Other Concepts
- **O Lost** — lostness is the condition of being severed from one's own past; memory is the attempt to bridge the gap
- **Death and Mortality** — death gives time its edge; the dead live on only through memory
- **Art and Creation** — art is the preservative that rescues time from oblivion

### Characters
- **Eugene Gant** — the rememberer, the artist whose vocation is to fix the past in language
- **Oliver Gant** — whose name fades on the brick wall, whose life becomes his son's material
