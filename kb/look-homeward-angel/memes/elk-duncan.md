## Synopsis

Elk Duncan represents an aspirational figure in Eugene Gant's adolescent social universe — older, taller, connected to the legal establishment through his father, yet eccentric enough to be accessible to a younger boy seeking models of adult masculinity. The son of "an Altamont attorney, a small Democratic politician, mighty in county affairs" [032-019], Duncan is both a product of and a departure from his class: he studies law at Pulpit Hill but spends his hours trading absurdist historical banter rather than memorizing Torts.

His friendship with Eugene is described as "one of the proud summits of his life" [032-021], capturing the adolescent hunger for association with older, more established figures. Duncan, along with Harold Gay, forms part of what the narrator calls "a more precious congress of freaks" [032-018] — a circle defined by intellectual eccentricity and a shared disregard for conventional ambition.

## Biography

Elk Duncan enters the narrative at twenty-four, already a figure of local consequence by inheritance if not yet by achievement. The narrator's physical description is vivid and peculiar:

> [032-019] Elk Duncan was the son of an Altamont attorney, a small Democratic politician, mighty in county affairs. Elk Duncan was tall — an inch or two over six feet — and incredibly thin, or rather narrow. He was a Law student.

His physical narrowness mirrors a certain narrowness of application — he is described as "a bright bird held by the stare of that hypnotic snake, the law" [032-033], suggesting a bright mind held captive by a discipline that does not naturally engage it.

Eugene's admiration for Duncan is colored by a consciousness of class difference. The narrator notes that "his association with Elk Duncan was one of the proud summits of his life: he weltered in the purple calcium which bathed that worthy, he smoked cigarettes with a debauched and devilish knowingness" [032-021]. The theatrical vocabulary — "purple calcium," "debauched and devilish" — is Eugene's own, filtered through the ironic retrospective voice that characterizes the novel.

The surviving scenes of Duncan in action show him engaged in comic historical invention. When Eugene bursts into his study, Duncan is found "with unaccustomed eye bent dully upon a page of Torts" [032-033] — the law holds him but barely. His dialogue consists of mock-genealogical fantasy:

> [032-041] "On his Uncle Jasper's side," said Elk Duncan, "he was related to Paul the Poxy and Genevieve the Ungenerous."

And:

> [032-043] "...Canterbury, Dr. Gay, who was elected Pope," said Elk Duncan. "This caused a great schism in the Church."

These flights of absurdist erudition define Duncan's appeal: he offers Eugene a model of masculinity that is learned but unserious, rooted in the town's establishment yet mocking it from within.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Duncan's significance to Eugene is measured by the distance between them — in age, in social standing, in worldliness. Eugene's association with him is a form of aspirational belonging, and Duncan seems to accept the younger boy's company without condescension. Their friendship operates through shared humor rather than mentorship.

### Harold Gay

Duncan and Harold Gay form a comic duo, trading absurdist genealogies and historical fabrications. When Gay's verbal excess threatens to become genuinely offensive, Duncan plays the straight man: "'Harold! Harold!' said Elk Duncan reprovingly. 'Damn, son! You're getting hard!'" [032-022]. Their dynamic is one of mutual performance — two young men entertaining each other with displays of wit that the law will eventually domesticate.

### His Father

Duncan's father, though unnamed in the surviving passages, is identified as "an Altamont attorney, a small Democratic politician, mighty in county affairs" [032-019]. This lineage places Duncan within the town's power structure while his own eccentricity suggests an incomplete fit with it.
