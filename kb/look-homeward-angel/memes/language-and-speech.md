# Language and Speech

## Definition

Speech in *Look Homeward, Angel* is a form of action — as consequential as violence, as revealing as confession, as creative as stone-carving. Oliver Gant's "purple flashes of the old rhetoric," his "pentameter curse at Rebel ways," his Shakespearean ranting — these are not decoration but identity. The Gant family is constituted through speech: Oliver's tirades, Eliza's fretful complaints, Ben's bitter sarcasm, Eugene's hungry listening.

Wolfe, more than any other American novelist of his generation, treats language as a physical substance. Words have weight, taste, and texture. The novel is saturated with quoted speech, remembered phrases, fragments of song and verse — a linguistic world as dense and particular as the physical world of the town.

## Textual Evidence

Oliver's speech is described as "purple flashes of the old rhetoric, the ghost of the old earth":

> (001-029) His voice was heard in the land once more, there were purple flashes of the old rhetoric, the ghost of the old earth.

His "pentameter curse" identifies his speech with Shakespeare — he speaks in verse, not prose:

> (001-012) He walked through the streets at dark, yelling his pentameter curse at Rebel ways.

The novel's title is drawn from Milton's "Lycidas" — a poem about a drowned poet — embedding literary language at the novel's foundation.

Ben's speech is the opposite of Oliver's — dry, sardonic, deflating — but equally distinctive:

> (020-033) "What of it?" Ben asked irritably. "It has a right to, hasn't it?"

Eugene's struggle to find words — the writer's hunger — is the novel's own genetic code:

> (004-037) Holding them clumsily in his tiny hands, he studied for hours the symbols of speech, knowing that he had here the stones of the temple of language, and striving desperately to find the key that would unlock it.

## Analysis

The novel is structured around a tension between two kinds of language: Oliver's grandiloquent, theatrical, voracious rhetoric — language as performance, as curse, as alchemy — and Eliza's clipped, practical, withholding speech. Eugene inherits both and must find a third way: the writer's language, which is neither ranting nor silence but something in between — descriptive, elegiac, memorial.

"Language" here includes the act of naming. To write about Altamont, to call Gant "Gant" and Eliza "Eliza," is to capture them in words that outlast their physical lives. The novel is a vast act of naming — a novel-length version of the child Eugene studying "the symbols of speech" and trying to unlock them.
