## Synopsis

Max Isaacs is Eugene Gant's first close friend, the companion of his childhood adventures and early schooling. A year older than Eugene, Max represents the world of boyhood camaraderie — tree-climbing, cellar games, shared books, and the rough physicality of growing up male in Altamont. Their friendship, forged in the intimate routines of a small town childhood, gradually loosens as Eugene's intellectual development and family upheavals carry him into a different world. Yet Max reappears at key moments, his enduring ordinariness serving as a measure of how far Eugene has traveled from his origins.

## Biography

Max enters Eugene's life as a playmate during the years when the Gant children are growing up:

> [006-022] The children were growing up — Eugene had found playmates — Harry Tarkinton and Max Isaacs.

Their friendship deepens through shared experiences of school. When Eugene starts at the local academy, it is Max's presence that makes the transition bearable:

> [008-002] Eliza did not want him to go, but his only close companion, Max Isaacs, a year his senior, was going, and there was in his heart a constricting terror of loneliness.

In the classroom, Max is witness to Eugene's dawning difference — his sudden, precocious literacy. Max looks at Eugene's exercise sheet and sees "the jagged line" of writing that sets Eugene apart. Their after-school hours are filled with the adventures of boyhood:

> [008-044] During his unoccupied hours, he lived for months in the cellar with Harry Tarkinton and Max Isaacs: they strung the ladders on wires above the wagon.

As Eugene enters adolescence, the friendship fades. Max continues to interest Eugene "as an athlete long after he had ceased to interest him as a person." Max's gift is baseball — a physical talent that Eugene, increasingly drawn to books and language, cannot match:

> [015-077] The game Max Isaacs excelled in was baseball.

Their final significant encounter occurs years later, when Max invites Eugene on a trip to Charleston with a group of young people. The episode, which includes Eugene's first extended experience with girls and a visit to the Navy Yard, is tinged with Max's awkward, small-town decency:

> [026-033] Max Isaacs and Malvin Bowden turned at once toward the Navy Yard.

Max's conversation during this trip — his "sagely" comments about Northern capital and Southern ladies, his awkward curiosity about the older woman Mrs. Bowden — captures the limited horizons of a young man who will remain in the world Eugene is leaving behind.
