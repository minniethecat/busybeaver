# World War I & The American Army

## Synopsis

World War I arrives in the novel not as battlefield narrative but as a distant, transformative pressure — "the yeasty ferment of war" that seethes through the nation, converting colleges into training camps, stationing young men in tobacco towns, and marking the boundary between Eugene's provincial childhood and the larger historical forces that will carry him forward. The war is both a concrete historical event (America's entry in 1917, the mobilization of campuses, the Armistice) and a recurrent Wolfean theme: the collision of individual lives with the impersonal machinery of history.

## The Civil War Precedent

Before World War I, the novel establishes war as the formative trauma of the parental generation. Oliver Gant's earliest memory is of the Civil War:

> (001-007) How this boy stood by the roadside near his mother's farm, and saw the dusty Rebels march past on their way to Gettysburg, how his cold eyes darkened when he heard the great name of Virginia.

The Pentland clan's identity is forged in the Civil War. Major Thomas Pentland earned his title "honestly if inconspicuously" at the Seven Days battles, while his brother Bacchus was "blistering his hard hands at Shiloh" (001-064). Another brother was killed. The war's aftermath shaped the generation that raised Eugene:

> (001-068) The four oldest children, Henry, Will, Eliza, and Jim, had passed their childhood in the years following the war. The poverty and privation of these years had been so terrible that none of them ever spoke of it now, but the bitter steel had sheared into their hearts, leaving scars that would not heal.

In Oliver's dying vision, war is one of the "series of accidents" that channeled a life:

> (001-019) Suddenly, he saw that his life had been channelled by a series of accidents: a mad Rebel singing of Armageddon, the sound of a bugle on the road, the mule-hoofs of the army.

## America Enters the Great War

The novel's treatment of World War I begins in earnest in chapter 34. The transformation is total and rapid — the nation becomes one vast military apparatus:

> (034-001) Eugene returned to Altamont two weeks before the term began at Pulpit Hill. The town and the nation seethed in the yeasty ferment of war. The country was turning into one huge camp. The colleges and universities were being converted into training-camps for officers. Everyone was "doing his bit."

Ben Gant is stationed "one hundred miles away, in the tobacco town":

> (033-003) The boy had two short awkward letters from Ben, who was now stationed one hundred miles away, in the tobacco town. At Easter, Eugene visited him, staying at his lodgings, where again his unerring destiny had thrown him into the welcoming arms of a gray-haired widow.

The war's presence is felt largely through absence — Ben is gone, the campus is militarized, the ordinary rhythms of college life are suspended. Eugene's world is simultaneously contracting (the loss of his brother to the army) and expanding (the war as a force that will eventually carry him beyond Altamont).

## The War and Eugene's Generation

For Eugene's generation, the war is a crucible that separates childhood from adulthood. It marks the end of the old Altamont — a town whose existence was defined by the Civil War and its aftermath — and the beginning of something new, though what that "something" is remains unclear. Wolfe does not send Eugene to the trenches; instead, the war functions as a horizon, a limit, a force that reshapes the world while Eugene is still forming within it.

The war's end is rendered not as celebration but as anti-climax — part of the larger pattern of departures and terminations that structure the novel's closing movement. By the time Eugene stands on the commencement stage (039-003), the war is already receding into the past, another ghost in a novel full of them.

## Generational War Structure

| war | generation | function in the novel |
|-----|-----------|----------------------|
| **Civil War** | Oliver, Major Pentland, Eliza's parents | Formative trauma; the source of the Pentland military identity and the poverty that shaped Eliza's ambition |
| **World War I** | Ben, Eugene, their classmates | Disruption of provincial life; the conversion of colleges into camps; the beginning of Eugene's departure from Altamont |

## The Military in the Novel's Social Imagination

The military runs through the novel as a recurring institution — part of the civic and familial fabric of Altamont:

| figure | military connection | significance |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| Major Thomas Pentland | Civil War (Seven Days) | Earned title "honestly if inconspicuously" |
| Corporal Bacchus Pentland | Civil War (Shiloh) | "Blistering his hard hands" |
| Oliver Gant | Witnessed Rebels marching to Gettysburg | War as boyhood memory and lifelong scar |
| Ben Gant | Stationed in tobacco town, WWI | The war takes Eugene's brother away |
| "Everyone" at Pulpit Hill | Training camps | "Doing his bit" — the war as total mobilization |

## Symbolic Significance

| theme | expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **History as accident** | Oliver's life was "channelled by a series of accidents" — a Rebel singing, a bugle, the army's mule-hoofs |
| **War as generational marker** | The Civil War defines the parents; the Great War defines the children |
| **Absence and loss** | Ben's stationing removes him from Eugene; the war's primary effect in the novel is to create distance |
| **The individual and the mass** | "Everyone was doing his bit" — war as the erasure of individual identity into collective mobilization |
