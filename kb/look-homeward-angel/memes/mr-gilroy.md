# Mr. Gilroy

## Synopsis

Mr. Gilroy is a minor character in *Look Homeward, Angel*, appearing in a single scene at [[eliza-gant|Eliza Gant]]'s boarding house, Dixieland. He serves as one of the attendants accompanying [[simon]], the eccentric and possibly mentally unstable resident whom Eliza has taken in. Alongside [[mr-flannagan|Mr. Flannagan]], Mr. Gilroy acts as a reassuring presence, his cheerful demeanor softening the unsettling impression left by Simon's grandiose and cryptic behavior.

Though his appearance is brief, Mr. Gilroy's function in the narrative is to normalize the strange circumstances surrounding Simon. His repeated affirmations that Simon "wouldn't hurt a fly" and his cheerful nonchalance about Simon's liberal scattering of coins serve to defuse the tension felt by the Gant family, particularly the young [[eugene-gant|Eugene]].

## Biography

Mr. Gilroy appears in Chapter 15, during the spring when Eugene is nearly twelve years old. He arrives at Dixieland in the company of Simon and Mr. Flannagan, descending the steps after what appears to have been some physical exertion—the two guards are described as "red in the face, and breathed stertorously as if from some recent exertion" (015-049), while Simon himself remains immaculately composed.

The scene unfolds as Simon makes a theatrical entrance before the gathered Gant family. When the young Eugene is summoned forward to meet Simon, Mr. Gilroy offers a gentle reassurance:

> [015-052] "It's all right," remarked Mr. Gilroy, encouragingly. "He wouldn't hurt a fly."

This interjection reveals Mr. Gilroy's protective instinct toward the frightened boy, as well as his familiarity with Simon's harmless nature beneath the unsettling exterior.

When Simon produces a handful of coins from his pocket and pours them into Eugene's cupped hands, the gathered adults look "doubtfully" at Mr. Gilroy (015-058), seeking his confirmation that this generosity is legitimate. Mr. Gilroy's response is characteristically breezy:

> [015-059] "Oh, that's all right!" said Mr. Gilroy cheerfully. "He'll never miss it. There's lots more where that came from."

Mr. Flannagan elaborates that Simon is a "mul-tye-millionaire" and that the guards give him "four or five dollars in small change every morning just to throw away" (015-060). When Gant grins at Simon's cryptic warning to "Look out for the Stingaree," Mr. Gilroy provides a folksy clarification:

> [015-064] "That's right," said Mr. Gilroy, noting Gant's grin. "The Stingaree's a fish. They have them in Florida."

## Character Analysis

Mr. Gilroy embodies a pragmatic, unflappable working-class sensibility. Where Simon represents mystery, theatricality, and the unsettling presence of madness, Mr. Gilroy represents the ordinary world's capacity to absorb and normalize eccentricity. His dialogue is marked by colloquial reassurance—"It's all right," "Oh, that's all right!"—and a cheerful indifference to Simon's profligacy with money.

His role is structurally that of a mediator: he bridges the gap between Simon's strange world and the Gant family's domestic reality. His explanation that "The Stingaree's a fish" (015-064) is emblematic—he translates Simon's cryptic pronouncements into ordinary, comprehensible terms.
