## Synopsis

Thelma appears in two guises across the novel, linked by name if not explicitly by identity. In chapter 24, she is Mrs. Thelma Jarvis, a milliner glimpsed on the street — "thick brown eyes above her straw regardant" — passing the boys with "a remote smile" that provokes their knowing grins. In chapter 29, she reappears as simply "Thelma," a "small woman, slightly built" with a "sharp wrenny face" who works at the brothel Jim Trivett takes Eugene to visit. Whether these are the same woman or two different Thelmas is left ambiguous — a Wolfean indeterminacy that underscores the porous boundary between Altamont's respectable and disreputable worlds. The brothel Thelma is vivid, insolent, and professionally affectionate: she studies Eugene's towering height with mock wonder, presses his hand in farewell, and vanishes into the foggy night.

## Biography

Thelma first appears in her respectable incarnation as Mrs. Thelma Jarvis, the milliner, a figure of small-town elegance observed during the boys' after-school walks:

> [024-304] Seated alone, with thick brown eyes above her straw regardant, Mrs. Thelma Jarvis, the milliner, drew, in one swizzling guzzle, the last beaded chain of linked sweetness long drawn out from the bottom of her glass.

The boys watch her pass with a mixture of amusement and fascination — a woman of ambiguous social status whose presence on the street provokes "grinning quietly" and the murmured observation: "We'll be waiting for you when you come out."

In chapter 29, Thelma reappears in a very different context. Jim Trivett, the town pressman, has taken Eugene on a nighttime excursion to a brothel on Eagle Crescent. Thelma emerges from a side room "followed by high empty yokel laughter":

> [029-079] "My Gawd!" said Thelma, in a tinny voice. "What've we got here?" She thrust out her sharp wrenny face, and studied Eugene insolently.

Her reaction to Eugene's height is a running joke — she insists he is "seven foot tall or I'm a liar" — and the scene unfolds with the coarse, jocular warmth of the brothel's professional affections. She goes into a room with Jim Trivett, and at the end of the night, as the fog rolls in and the group slips quietly away, she presses Eugene's hand:

> [029-110] "Come again, Slats," Thelma murmured, pressing his hand.

The episode leaves Eugene "haunted by his own lost ghost," convinced that his "sin" is published by every gesture. Thelma, like Louise in Charleston, becomes one of the figures through whom Eugene confronts the adult world of sexuality and its attendant guilts.
