## Synopsis

Sinker Jordan is a minor figure whose appearance in the Newport News chapter (33) serves to dramatize the economics of survival that shape Eugene Gant's early adulthood. Found "upon the beach" in Virginia, Jordan becomes Eugene's companion in a sequence of misadventures — selling and re-buying peddler's tools, surrendering their remaining money to a landlady, cadging meals from fellow dockworkers — that capture the improvisational poverty of the World War I labor market.

Jordan functions narratively as a contrast to Eugene: where Eugene is immobilized by "the powerful brooding inertia of his temper" [033-095], Jordan acts — hurling tools, cursing fate, cadging small sums — even when his actions are self-defeating. Their partnership ends when Jordan comes into an inheritance on his twenty-first birthday and departs for Altamont, leaving Eugene alone on the pier. The asymmetry is characteristic of Wolfe's world: some people have inheritances, literal and otherwise; others only the "brooding inertia" that makes them stay.

## Biography

Sinker Jordan enters Eugene's life during the Newport News episode, after Eugene has spent his war-work earnings in "a week of gaudy riot in Norfolk and on the Virginia beaches" [033-088]. The narrator's introduction is characteristically frank:

> [033-088] This worthy, whose name was Sinker Jordan, was three years older than Eugene. He was a handsome reckless boy, small in stature, and limping from an injury he had received in a football game. His character was weak and volatile — he hated effort, and was obstinate only in cursing ill-fortune.

The physical details — the limp from "a football game," the "small in stature" frame that contrasts with Eugene's lanky height — establish a boy whose body already bears the marks of his recklessness.

Together, Jordan and Eugene attempt to sell household tools door to door. The enterprise collapses quickly:

> [033-090] They were quite moneyless now, and once on the street again, Sinker Jordan had hurled upon the pavement the fatal tools, cursing savagely the folly that threatened now to keep them hungry.

Jordan's theatrical despair — hurling the tools, "cursing savagely" — is contrasted with Eugene's practical response: picking the tools up and reselling them back to the dealer. This division of temperament — Jordan's impulsive gesture versus Eugene's grudging persistence — defines their brief partnership.

The nadir of their shared poverty comes when Jordan "surrendered their remaining capital into the greedy palm of the landlady" [033-091], an act of reckless trust that leaves them both penniless. The narrator's wry aside — "and a real lady too, she admitted" — captures the comic-ironic register through which the novel filters even its grimmest moments.

As conditions worsen on the pier, with Eugene "unable to beg or borrow" and immobilized by "the powerful brooding inertia of his temper," Jordan survives by his wits:

> [033-095] Sinker Jordan cadged small sums from the other checkers, and lived miserably on bottled pop and cheese at a little grocery across the road from the pier.

The verb "cadged" is precise — Jordan is a survivor of a type Eugene can only observe. When a coworker lends five dollars, both boys survive until payday, and then:

> [033-109] after dining together on four pounds of steak, Sinker Jordan departed for Altamont and the enjoyment of an inheritance which had fallen due a few days before, on his twenty-first birthday. Eugene stayed on.

The inheritance is the deus ex machina of class: Jordan, for all his recklessness, has a birthright waiting in Altamont. Eugene, who has no inheritance, stays on the pier. The "four pounds of steak" — a last feast before divergent fates — is one of the novel's small, perfect gestures of temporary solidarity.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Jordan and Eugene are companions of circumstance rather than friends. They share a room, a hunger, and a brief chapter of the novel, but the connection is situational — two boys from Altamont, broke in a strange city, improvising survival. The relationship's defining quality is asymmetry: Jordan can cadge, can curse, can leave; Eugene can only endure.
