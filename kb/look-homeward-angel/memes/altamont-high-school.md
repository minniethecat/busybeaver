# Altamont High School & Leonard's Fitting School

## Synopsis

The educational institutions of Altamont form the crucible of Eugene Gant's intellectual awakening. The public high school, attended by the older Gant children, represents the ordinary rhythms of provincial education; the private Fitting School, founded by the Leonards, becomes Eugene's sanctuary — a place where books, ideas, and Margaret Leonard's fierce spiritual mentorship open the door to a world beyond the mountains.

## Altamont High School

The public high school serves the Gant children in their turn. Daisy Gant, the eldest daughter, is a diligent student:

> (005-002) She was seventeen and in her last year at high school. She was a timid, sensitive girl, looking like her name — Daisy-ish industrious and thorough in her studies.

Steve Gant's experience is far less placid. His truancy and insubordination lead to a confrontation that prefigures his lifelong refusal of institutional authority:

> (005-003) When he was fourteen, he was summoned by the school principal to his little office, to take a thrashing for truancy and insubordination. But the spirit of acquiescence was not in him: he snatched the rod from the man's hand, broke it.

The high school also produces Bob Sterling, Eugene's roommate at the university and a figure of sober diligence:

> (032-002) Eugene began the year earnestly as roommate of a young man who had been the best student in the Altamont High School. His name was Bob Sterling. Bob Sterling was nineteen years old, the son of a widow.

## The Fitting School

The Altamont Fitting School, founded by John Dorsey Leonard and his wife Margaret, is the novel's most important educational institution. It represents everything the public school is not: intimate, intellectually ambitious, and spiritually transformative.

> (016-069) The Altamont Fitting School was the greatest venture of their lives. All the delayed success that Leonard had dreamed of as a younger man he hoped to realize now. For him the school was independence, mastership, power, and, he hoped, prosperity. For her, teaching was its own exceeding great reward.

The school occupies a squat, rambling old house surrounded by huge trees — a physical setting that Wolfe renders with characteristic atmospheric density:

> (016-046) The huge trees made sad autumn music as they entered the grounds. In the broad hall of the squat rambling old house Eugene for the first time saw Margaret Leonard. She held a broom in her hands, and was aproned. But his first impression was of her shocking fragility.

## Eugene's Transformation

It is at the Fitting School that Eugene undergoes his most profound transformation. Under Margaret Leonard's tutelage, he discovers the life of the mind and a lineage of literary ancestors:

> (017-173) But the school had become the centre of his heart and life — Margaret Leonard his spiritual mother. He liked to be there most in the afternoons when the crowd of boys had gone, and when he was free to wander about the old house, under the singing majesty of great trees, exultant in the proud solitude of his own spirit.

Margaret Leonard's pedagogical method is not disciplinary but inspiriting. She watches over Eugene's health with maternal anxiety, yet her real gift is to see the essential quality in a person and nurture it:

> (017-174) Margaret Leonard watched his health jealously, almost morbidly, warning him constantly of the terrible consequences that followed physical depletion, the years required to build back what had once been thrown carelessly away.

## Physical Setting

The school building itself is a character in Wolfe's rendering — a decaying mansion with broad halls, great trees, and an atmosphere of faded grandeur that mirrors the aspirations of its founders:

| feature | description |
|---------|------------|
| Building | Squat, rambling old house with broad hallway |
| Grounds | Huge trees making "sad autumn music" |
| Location | On a hill above Altamont |
| Atmosphere | A "proud solitude" — scholarly, elegiac, removed from town life |

## Symbolic Significance

The schools of Altamont embody the novel's central tension between provincial limitation and the hunger for a larger world:

| theme | expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **Intellectual awakening** | The Fitting School is the portal through which Eugene discovers literature, ideas, and his own vocation |
| **Class and ambition** | Public high school vs. private Fitting School maps onto Altamont's social stratification |
| **The teacher as savior** | Margaret Leonard exemplifies the redemptive power of a great teacher |
| **Escape and return** | Education is Eugene's path out of Altamont — and the source of his lifelong alienation |
