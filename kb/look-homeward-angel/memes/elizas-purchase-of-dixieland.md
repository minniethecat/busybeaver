# Eliza's Purchase of Dixieland

## Synopsis

Eliza Gant's purchase of Dixieland is one of the most consequential events in the novel's domestic history. The acquisition of the rambling, drafty boarding house — "five minutes from the public square, on a pleasant sloping middle-class street" — transforms the Gant family's circumstances, establishing Eliza as an independent economic operator and creating the physical and social environment in which Eugene Gant spends his formative years. The purchase is an act of will, a declaration of independence from Oliver Gant, and the foundation of Eliza's real estate empire.

## Background

Eliza's ambition has always exceeded the boundaries of Gant's stone shop and the family home on Woodson Street. The purchase of Dixieland is the culmination of years of planning, saving, and a sharp instinct for property value. The house itself is a characteristic specimen of Altamont architecture — large, badly built, and full of possibility:

> (011-004) Thus, she began to think of Dixieland. It was situated five minutes from the public square, on a pleasant sloping middle-class street of small homes and boardinghouses. Dixieland was a big cheaply constructed frame house of eighteen or twenty drafty high-ceilinged rooms: it had a rambling, unplanned, gabular appearance, and was painted a dirty yellow.

The house's flaws are rendered with almost loving specificity. In winter:

> (011-005) In winter, the wind blew howling blasts under the skirts of Dixieland: its back end was built high off the ground on wet columns of rotting brick. Its big rooms were heated by a small furnace which sent up, when charged with fire, a hot dry enervation to the rooms of the first floor, and a gaseous-burnt lungful of air to those upstairs.

## The Previous Owner

The Reverend Wellington Hodge, Dixieland's previous owner, is one of Wolfe's most vivid minor grotesques — a Methodist minister undone by drink, whose disintegration prefigures the house's haunted atmosphere:

> (011-006) The place was for sale. Its owner was a middle-aged horse-faced gentleman whose name was the Reverend Wellington Hodge: he had begun life favorably in Altamont as a Methodist minister, but had run foul of trouble when he began to do double service to the Lord God of Hosts and John Barleycorn.

Hodge's decline mirrors the house's physical decay, and he comes to believe the house itself is malign:

> (011-007) Besides, the sheltering walls of Dixieland inspired him with horror — he felt that the malign influence of the house had governed his own disintegration. He was a sensitive man, and his promenades about his estate were checked by inhibited places: the cornice of the long girdling porch where a lodger had hanged himself.

## The Move

Eliza's entry into Dixieland is rendered as a moment of noisy, purposeful triumph:

> (011-019) In the young autumn when the maples were still full and green, and the migratory swallows filled secretly the trees with clamor, and swooped of an evening in a black whirlwind down, drifting at its funnel end, like dead leaves, into their chosen chimney, Eliza moved into Dixieland. There was clangor, excitement, incessant labor.

## Economic Significance

The purchase marks Eliza's emergence as an independent economic force. Before long, she has paid off the mortgage:

> (015-015) Eliza's earning power the first few years at Dixieland had been injured by her illnesses. Now, however, she had recovered, and had paid off the last installment on the house. It was entirely hers. The property at this time was worth perhaps $12,000.

Combined with Gant's shop earnings, the family's wealth grows substantially:

> (015-019) Thus, at the beginning of 1912, before the rapid and intensive development of Southern industry, and the consequent tripling of Altamont's population, and before the multiplication of her land values, the wealth of Gant and Eliza amounted to about $100,000, the great bulk of which was solidly founded in Eliza's property.

Eliza's entrepreneurial spirit is undimmed. She prints advertising cards and presses them on Eugene:

> (013-023) She gave him a pocketful of printed cards, which bore this inscription: Spend your summers at Dixieland In Beautiful Altamont, America's Switzerland. Rates Reasonable — Both Transient and Tourist. Apply Eliza E. Gant, Proprietor.

## Eugene's Ambivalence

For Eugene, Dixieland is a source of deep shame. The boarding-house life — the lack of privacy, the parade of strangers, the shabby gentility — wounds his adolescent dignity:

> (011-076) Eugene was ashamed of Dixieland. And he was again afraid to express his shame. As with The Post, he felt thwarted, netted, trapped. He hated the indecency of his life, the loss of dignity and seclusion, the surrender to the tumultuous rabble of the four walls which shield us from them.

Winter at Dixieland intensifies the misery:

> (012-001) It was the winter, and the sullen dying autumn that he hated most at Dixieland — the dim fly-specked lights, the wretched progress about the house in search of warmth, Eliza untidily wrapped in an old sweater, a dirty muffler, a cast-off man's coat. She glycerined her cold-cracked hands.

Yet Dixieland is also the theater of Eugene's childhood — the place where he discovers books, music, and the rich gallery of boarders who populate his imagination. His hatred of the house is inseparable from his absorption in it; it is simultaneously prison and world.
