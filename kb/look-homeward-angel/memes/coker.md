## Synopsis

Dr. Coker is one of Wolfe's most vividly realized secondary characters — an Altamont physician whose defining features are a long, chewed cigar, a "yellow grin" stretched across a "long malarial face," and a sardonic wit that masks a deep, weary wisdom. Nicknamed "the Lung Shark" — a moniker that captures both his medical specialization and his predatory, ironic intelligence — Coker moves through the novel as a kind of Greek chorus to the Gant family's tragedies. He is present at the morning drugstore gatherings where Altamont's professional men trade gallows humor; he examines Ben Gant and delivers, without ever saying the words directly, the news that Ben is dying; and he appears in the final pages as a figure in Eugene's memory, linked forever to the night of his brother's death. Unlike the blustering McGuire or the slick Spaugh, Coker is a man who has made his peace with the limits of medicine — and of life itself.

## Biography

Coker first appears in the novel as part of the morning gathering at the town drugstore, where doctors, journalists, and tradesmen assemble for coffee and barbed conversation. His entrance is marked by his characteristic props — the ever-present cigar, the sardonic demeanor:

> [014-056] Coker, the Lung Shark, who had not ceased to regard McGuire's bean-hunt with sardonic interest, now took the long cigar out of his devil's head and held it between his stained fingers as he tapped his companion.

His wit is caustic and precise. When McGuire drunkenly pontificates, Coker is the one who punctures the performance with dry asides. He greets the undertaker Horse Hines with mock solemnity, describing the morning's patient as "the Constipated Cop," and trades elaborate insults with McGuire that reveal the deep, comfortable familiarity of men who have worked together for years:

> [014-105] "We're getting too low and coarse for him, Hugh," said Coker. "Now that he's got drunk with some of the best families, he's in great demand socially. He's so highly thought of that he's become the official midwife to all pregnant virgins."

Coker's most significant scenes come in his private encounters with Ben Gant. When Ben visits his office seeking a medical examination for military service, the exchange becomes something far deeper — an existential interrogation in which Ben demands answers Coker cannot give:

> [025-047] "In Christ's name, Coker," he said, "what's it all about? Are you able to tell me? What in heaven's name are we here for? You're a doctor — you ought to know something."

Coker's response is weary, honest, and utterly devoid of false comfort:

> [025-051] "What do you want me to say?" said Coker. "What am I? A mind-reader? A spiritualist? I'm your physician, not your priest. I've seen them born, and I've seen them die. What happens to them before or after, I can't say."

The physical examination that follows is a masterpiece of indirection. Coker never tells Ben directly that he is unfit — or that he is dying. Instead, he prods him in the solar plexus, watches his chest collapse, and says simply, "That's all, son. I'm through with you." Ben reads the truth in Coker's "veined and weary eyes" — a moment of wordless communication between doctor and patient that Wolfe renders with devastating restraint:

> [025-080] Ben read the true answer in Coker's veined and weary eyes. His own were sick with fear.

In the final crisis of Ben's illness, Coker is called in when the family physician, Dr. Cardiac, can do no more. He attends Ben's deathbed with a gravity that belies his earlier sardonic manner, and it is Coker who delivers the final verdict to Helen:

> [035-169] Her voice was prayerful and low. Coker turned toward her slowly, taking the cigar between his big stained fingers. Then, gently, with his weary yellow smile, he answered: "Everything. Not all the king's horses, not all the doctors and nurses in the world, can help him now."

And it is Coker who speaks Ben's epitaph, in words that echo across the remainder of the novel:

> [035-177] Coker looked for a moment at the gray twisted shadow on the bed. Then, quietly, sadly, with tenderness and tired wonder, he said: "Old Ben. When shall we see his like again?"

Coker appears in the novel's aftermath — at the drugstore counter in the days following the funeral, and finally as a figure in Eugene's haunted memory, his name part of the litany of those who witnessed Ben's death.
