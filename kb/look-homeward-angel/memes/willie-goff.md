## Synopsis

Willie Goff is one of the novel's most vivid minor grotesques — a disabled pencil merchant whose "great idiot's head," crippled gait, and "gaudy handkerchief" [024-278] make him a spectacle on the streets of Altamont. His appearance outside Wood's Drug Store in Chapter 24 is a set piece of Wolfe's descriptive method: an accumulation of physical detail that builds toward a complex emotional effect, refusing the easy categories of pity, comedy, or sentiment.

Goff is both performer and performed-upon. The young men at the soda fountain greet him with "loud cheers and laughter" and pound his back "boisterously" [024-278], treating him as a mascot whose function is to be publicly amused by. Yet Goff's own responses — his "warmly, gratefully" pleasure at the attention, his "cunning grin," his knowing leer — suggest a consciousness that participates in, and perhaps manipulates, the ritual of his own comic reception. When asked if he has a woman, his reply — "'I've got several. A fellow's got to have a little Poon-Tang, hasn't he?'" [024-294] — lands as a punchline that belongs as much to Goff as to his audience.

## Biography

Willie Goff's entrance is rendered in one of the novel's most elaborate descriptive passages, a sentence that accumulates physical detail with baroque density:

> [024-278] Goggling, his great idiot's head lolling on his scrawny neck, wearing the wide-rimmed straw hat that covered him winter and summer, Willie Goff, the pencil merchant, jerked past, with inward lunges of his crippled right foot. The fingers of his withered arm pointed stiffly toward himself, beckoning to him, and touching him as he walked with stiff jerking taps, in a terrible parody of vanity.

The phrase "terrible parody of vanity" is characteristic of Wolfe's grotesque mode: Goff's withered arm's gestures mimic the preening self-regard of the able-bodied, creating a mirror in which "normal" social behavior is exposed as equally mechanical, equally absurd. The sartorial detail — "a gaudy handkerchief with blue, yellow and crimson patterns," "a huge red carnation" in his lapel, "a wide loose collar of silk barred with red and orange stripes" [024-278] — presents a figure who dresses not in spite of his disability but in defiance of the invisibility that disability might impose.

The scene that follows at the soda fountain is structured as a ritual of community inclusion that is also one of exploitation:

> [024-278] He burred ecstatically at the passersby, who grinned fondly at him, and continued down to Wood's where he was greeted with loud cheers and laughter by a group of young men who loitered at the fountain's end.

The young men's treatment of Goff — "pounding his back and drawing him up to the fountain" — is the physical language of male camaraderie, but applied to a figure who cannot reciprocate on equal terms. Goff's response is revealing: "Pleased, he looked at them warmly, gratefully. He was touched and happy" [024-278]. The question of whether Goff is genuinely moved or performing the role of the grateful mascot is left deliberately unresolved.

The ritual proceeds through its stations: a "dope and lime" ordered [024-280], a cigarette offered and lit [024-282, 024-286], a "cunning grin" [024-290] and "knowing leer" [024-293] that culminate in the scene's punchline:

> [024-294] "I've got several," said Willie Goff. "A fellow's got to have a little Poon-Tang, hasn't he?"

The crude slang is the point — Goff's claim to sexual experience both confirms the young men's expectations of male boastfulness and disrupts the premise that Goff is a sexless object of pity. He gives them what they want (a joke about sex) while claiming, for himself, a place in the economy of male desire that the town's able-bodied men might prefer to deny him.

## Critical Reception

Willie Goff has received some attention in scholarship on Wolfe's treatment of disability and the grotesque. The passage exemplifies what critics have identified as Wolfe's characteristic doubleness: a descriptive mode that is simultaneously cruel and tender, exposing the mechanisms of social exclusion while participating in the aestheticizing gaze that enables it. Goff's function in the novel is small but structurally significant — he is one of many figures in Chapter 24 who collectively create the texture of Altamont as a community defined by the tensions between belonging and spectacle.
