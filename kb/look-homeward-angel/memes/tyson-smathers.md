# Tyson Smathers

## Synopsis

Tyson Smathers appears in a single brief scene in Chapter 36 of *Look Homeward, Angel* — the dawn return of Eugene Gant to Altamont after his northern sojourn. He is a newsboy working Eugene's old paper route (number 7), and their encounter on a quiet morning street becomes a small but emotionally charged moment of recognition, memory, and generosity. Though Smathers has only a handful of lines, Wolfe renders him with characteristic vividness: his "steady Scotch-Irish face," his matter-of-fact replies, and his "puzzled, freckled grin" when Eugene gives him an unexpected dollar. He is a mirror in which Eugene sees his own boyhood — and the distance between who he was and who he has become.

## Role in the Novel

### Chapter 36: The Dawn Return

Smathers's entire role unfolds in a single sequence of eleven paragraphs (036-040 – 036-051), as Eugene walks the early-morning streets of Altamont after arriving back from his travels. The encounter is framed by Wolfe's characteristic juxtaposition of human detail against a vast natural backdrop — "In that enormous silence, birds were waking" (036-046) — giving the small exchange an almost ceremonial weight.

The scene begins when Eugene, stirred by seeing a paper carrier on the route he himself once worked, stops the boy and asks his name:

> "Tyson Smathers," said the boy, turning upon him a steady Scotch-Irish face that was full of life and business. [036-042]

The phrase "full of life and business" is quintessential Wolfe — a compressed portrait that conveys competence, energy, and a certain precocious seriousness. Smathers is not awed by Eugene; he answers directly and with self-possession.

When Eugene asks if the boy has heard of him, Smathers's reply is characteristically matter-of-fact:

> "Yes," said Tyson Smathers, "I've heard of you. You had number 7." [036-044]

The response cuts through Eugene's self-importance with a factual precision bordering on the deflating. Smathers knows Eugene only as a predecessor on the paper route — not as a writer, not as a wanderer, but as "the boy who had number 7." This small deflation is part of the scene's emotional logic: Eugene is returning to a world where his identity is grounded in the local and the specific, not in the grand ambitions he carries within him.

Eugene's response — "That was a long time ago... I was just a boy" (036-045) — is rueful and self-mocking, acknowledging the gap between his former and present selves. The gift of the dollar bill that follows is impulsive and tender:

> "Here," he said. "I carried the damn things once. Next to my brother Ben, I was the best boy they ever had. Merry Christmas, Tyson." [036-048]
> "It ain't Christmas yet," said Tyson Smathers. [036-049]

Smathers's literal-minded correction is the scene's final note of comic grounding, but Eugene's rejoinder — "You're right, Tyson, but it will be" (036-050) — transforms the exchange into something larger: a benediction, an affirmation of future possibility. The scene closes with the boy accepting the gift:

> Tyson Smathers took the money, with a puzzled, freckled grin. Then he went on down the street, throwing papers. [036-051]

The grin — "puzzled" because the generosity is unexplained, "freckled" because he is still a boy — is the final brushstroke in Wolfe's portrait. Smathers then simply continues his route, absorbed back into the ordinary morning rhythms of Altamont, unchanged by the encounter but made visible to the reader in a moment of fleeting illumination.

## Symbolic Function

Tyson Smathers serves as a figure of continuity and recurrence — the boy who now holds the paper route Eugene once held, embodying the unbroken rhythms of Altamont life that persist regardless of individual arrivals and departures. His encounter with Eugene is one of Wolfe's small epiphanies: the recognition that the self is layered, that the boy one was is never wholly lost, and that returning home means confronting these earlier selves in unexpected forms. The paper route — route number 7 — becomes a symbolic thread connecting Eugene's past to the present, and Smathers, with his steady face and freckled grin, is the living token of that connection.

The invocation of Ben Gant, Eugene's dead brother, adds a deeper resonance. "Next to my brother Ben, I was the best boy they ever had" (036-048) is one of Eugene's rare spoken tributes to Ben — brief, almost offhand, and all the more affecting for being addressed to a stranger. Smathers, unknowingly, becomes the recipient of a grief that Eugene carries into every conversation.

## Narrative Context

Chapter 36 marks Eugene's return to Altamont after his long absence in the North — a journey that parallels Wolfe's own travels. The dawn hour, the empty streets, the waking birds, and the encounter with the paperboy all contribute to the chapter's elegiac, homeward-tending atmosphere. Smathers's scene, brief as it is, is one of the chapter's emotional anchors: a moment of quiet generosity between strangers that carries the weight of everything Eugene has lost and everything he hopes to find.
