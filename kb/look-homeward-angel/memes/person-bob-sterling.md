# Bob Sterling

## Synopsis

Bob Sterling is Eugene Gant's roommate at the start of his second year at the state university. The best student at Altamont High School, neat, earnest, and universally regarded as having "a bright future," Sterling is a model of the conscientious young man Eugene is not. But Sterling carries a fatal heart condition: his pulse is slow and irregular, his lips turn blue from climbing stairs, and within weeks of the semester's start he dies. His brief appearance in the novel — and the quiet aftermath of his death — marks one of the novel's most understated treatments of mortality and the fragility of promise.

## Role in the Novel

### The Model Roommate

Sterling is introduced as a contrast to Eugene's disorderly habits. The son of a widow, he is tidy, industrious, and gently reproving:

> He remonstrated with Eugene, with good-natured seriousness, about his habits. He ought not to throw his clothes around. He ought not to let his shirts and drawers accumulate in a dirty pile. [032-003]

The room they share is decorated "with a great number of college pennants, all of which belonged to Bob Sterling" (032-004) — a detail that simultaneously conveys Sterling's pre-existing claim on the space and his conventional, aspirational nature. He is the roommate who had already established a life before Eugene arrived.

### The Failing Heart

Sterling's heart condition is introduced not as tragedy but as a striking, almost clinical moment of shared experience. After climbing the stairs to their room, Sterling collapses gasping on the landing, his face "dead white, spotted by pale freckles" and his lips "chattering and turning blue" (032-005). What follows is one of the novel's most intimate gestures:

> "Come here," said Bob Sterling with a grin. "Put your head down here." He took Eugene's head and placed it against his heart. The great pump beat slowly and irregularly, with a hissing respiration. [032-007]

Sterling's grin — the humor with which he handles his own mortality — is characteristic of Wolfe's handling of death throughout the novel. The body fails, but the person remains: "Do you hear it?" said Bob Sterling, beginning to laugh (032-009).

### Death and Aftermath

The narrative compresses Sterling's decline into a few paragraphs: he falls ill, is taken to the infirmary, lingers for several weeks "apparently not very ill, but with lips constantly blue, a slow pulse, and a subnormal temperature" (032-010). His mother takes him home. Eugene writes twice weekly and receives "short but cheerful messages" in return. Then: "one day he died" (032-011).

The aftermath is rendered in a single devastating scene. Sterling's widowed mother returns to collect his belongings. As Eugene helps her pack the pennants from the wall, she bursts into tears:

> "He was so brave," she said, "so brave. Those last days—I had not meant to—Your letters made him so happy." [032-015]

Eugene's response is characteristically inward — "She's alone now, Eugene thought" (032-016) — and the chapter turns immediately to his decision to change rooms, unable to bear "the other cot empty" (032-017).

## Symbolic Function

Bob Sterling is one of several characters in *Look Homeward, Angel* whose early death punctuates the novel's meditation on mortality. Unlike Ben Gant's extended deathbed sequence, Sterling's death is compressed, almost elliptical — a quiet disappearance from the narrative that mirrors the way death erases a person from the daily texture of shared spaces. His departure also catalyzes Eugene's next living arrangement, introducing him to Elk Duncan and Harold Gay, and through them to the broader social world of the university dormitory.

## Narrative Context

Sterling appears in the opening pages of Chapter 32, which chronicles Eugene's second year at university. The chapter is set against the backdrop of World War I — "the older ones had gone to war" (032-001) — and Sterling's death from natural causes amid a nation mobilizing for mass death creates a quiet, dissonant counterpoint. The chapter also includes the Gant family's Christmas gathering, overshadowed by W. O. Gant's terminal cancer diagnosis, making Sterling's death one thread in a tapestry of mortality that dominates the novel's later sections.
