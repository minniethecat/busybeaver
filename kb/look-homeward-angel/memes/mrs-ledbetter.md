# Mrs. Ledbetter

## Synopsis

Mrs. Ledbetter is a minor character in *Look Homeward, Angel*, the proprietor of the Belvedere, one of Altamont's many boarding houses. She appears in the marketplace scene of Chapter 14, where her acid exchange with [[mrs-coleman|Mrs. Coleman]] exposes the competitive tensions and class anxieties that govern the town's boarding-house economy.

## Biography

Mrs. Ledbetter is introduced in the panoramic marketplace sequence of Chapter 14, where the boarding-house keepers of Altamont are enumerated in a rapid-fire catalog:

> [014-225] They looked at one another hardfaced—Mrs. Barrett of the Grosvenor at Mrs. Neville of Glen View; Mrs. Ambler of the Colonial at Miss Mamie Featherstone of Ravencrest; Mrs. Ledbetter of the Belvedere—

She immediately engages Mrs. Coleman in an exchange that reveals the social pecking order among the town's landladies. After Mrs. Coleman loftily declares that her boarders are "all permanents," Mrs. Ledbetter delivers a pointed rebuttal:

> [014-228] "Well," said Mrs. Ledbetter acidly, "I could fill my house up at any time with lungers who call themselves something else, but I won't have them. I was saying the other day—"

The sentence is cut off mid-thought—Wolfe's characteristic technique of truncating minor characters' speech—but its meaning is clear. Mrs. Ledbetter's "acidly" delivered retort implies that Mrs. Coleman's "permanents" are no better than the "lungers" (tuberculosis patients) that other boarding houses reluctantly accept. The phrase "who call themselves something else" drips with social skepticism: in Mrs. Ledbetter's view, all boarders are ultimately transients seeking advantage, and any pretense to higher status is self-deception.

Her boarding house, the Belvedere—a name meaning "beautiful view" in Italian—carries its own ironic charge, suggesting aspirations to elegance in a town whose economy depends on invalids seeking mountain air.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Ledbetter's single line of dialogue does more work than many characters' entire arcs. Her "acid" tone and the social diagnosis embedded in "lungers who call themselves something else" encapsulate the economic anxieties of Altamont's boarding-house world: the dependence on sickness for income, the pretense of gentility, and the competitive hostility barely concealed beneath polite inquiry. She is a minor character in the strictest sense, but her brief appearance contributes to the novel's cumulative portrait of a town built on the fragile economics of health and weather.
