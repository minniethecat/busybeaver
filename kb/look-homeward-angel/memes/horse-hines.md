## Synopsis

Horse Hines is Altamont's undertaker, a recurring minor character whose professional role brings him into the orbit of the Gant family at moments of crisis and mortality. His physical appearance — a thin, skeletal frame in a black frock coat, with a long "lantern mouth" and prominent horse teeth — makes him one of Wolfe's more vividly grotesque character sketches. Yet beneath the macabre professionalism and thin joviality, Hines is revealed as a man capable of genuine feeling, particularly in his long connection to the Gant and Pentland families. His most significant appearance comes during the aftermath of Ben Gant's death, where he transitions from comic undertaker to solemn funeral director, serving the family with a gravity that transcends his earlier, more absurd presentation.

## Biography

Horse Hines first appears in the novel as part of the morning gathering at the town drugstore, where Altamont's professional men — doctors, journalists, and tradesmen — assemble for coffee and conversation. His entrance is memorably theatrical:

> [014-054] At this moment, Horse Hines, the undertaker, entered, producing, although he was not a thin man, the effect of a skeleton clad in a black frock coat. His long lantern mouth split horsily in a professional smile displaying big horse teeth in his white heavily starched face.

His profession defines his social persona. Among the doctors and newsmen, he occupies an ambiguous position — respected for his trade but regarded with a degree of queasy humor by men like Ben Gant, who reacts with visible nausea when Hines mentions his work:

> [014-068] "I've just finished laying the old lady out," said Horse Hines gently. "A bundle of skin and bones." He sighed regretfully, and for a moment his boiled eye moistened.

His professional cheerfulness, captured in his request for "a mug of that embalming fluid" at the coffee urn, walks a fine line between gallows humor and genuine good spirits. When the conversation turns rough, Hines is hurt beneath his jovial exterior, revealing a sensitivity that complicates his caricature-like presentation:

> [014-117] Beneath his thin joviality Horse Hines was hurt.

Hines reappears in the novel's second half after Ben Gant's death, where he encounters Eugene and Luke Gant in the days following their brother's passing. His professional demeanor takes on a more somber, respectful tone:

> [036-104] "Good morning, gentlemen," said Horse Hines, in an accent of refined sadness. "Boys," he said, coming up to them sorrowfully, "I was mighty sorry to hear of your trouble. I couldn't have thought more of that boy if he'd been my own brother."

In the funeral preparations that follow, Hines guides the brothers through the selection of a casket, balancing professional salesmanship with a genuine personal connection. He reveals his long history with the family, having done business with Oliver Gant for nearly twenty years:

> [036-133] "I take a personal interest in this funeral," said Horse Hines with gentle emotion. "I have known the Gant and Pentland families for thirty years or more. I have had business dealings with your father for nigh on to twenty years."

His final appearance comes at the graveside, where he officiates with ceremonial gravity:

> [037-009] Then, marshalled by Horse Hines, the pallbearers, young men from the paper and the town, who had known the dead man best, moved slowly out, gripping the handles of the coffin.
