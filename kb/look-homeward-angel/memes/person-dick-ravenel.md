# Dick Ravenel

## Synopsis

Dick Ravenel is a young surgeon who appears in Chapter 14 of *Look Homeward, Angel* during a morning gathering at the town pharmacy. A colleague and sometime caretaker of the brilliant but alcoholic surgeon Hugh McGuire, Ravenel is characterized by his literary inclinations, his surgical skill, and a reserved, almost hidden affection for his friend. He serves as a foil to McGuire's boisterous self-destructiveness — precise where McGuire is chaotic, restrained where McGuire is expansive, yet loyal enough to cover for his colleague's drinking before the medical establishment.

## Role in the Novel

### The Morning at the Pharmacy

Ravenel appears at the pharmacy having just returned from presenting a medical paper in Baltimore. He finds McGuire drunk, disheveled, and barely functional — yet within half an hour of a scheduled surgery at the Ravenel hospital. The scene unfolds as a comic but tense exchange, Ravenel's dry wit playing against McGuire's alcoholic grandiosity.

> "I told them," said Dick Ravenel, whose affection was like a flower that grew behind a wall, "that the best surgeon in America when he was sober was a lousy bum named Hugh McGuire who was always drunk." [014-134]

Ravenel has just presented a paper on carcinoma of the liver at a medical conference — evidence of his professional ambition and intellectual seriousness. His description of McGuire to the conference audience captures the paradox at the heart of their relationship: acknowledging the older surgeon's ruinous alcoholism while recognizing his extraordinary surgical gift.

### The Literary Sawbones

McGuire's nickname for Ravenel — "Deadeye Dick, the literary sawbones" — points to a defining trait: Ravenel reads too much, by McGuire's reckoning. The older surgeon warns him that "literature has been the ruin of many a good surgeon," a remark that simultaneously mocks Ravenel's intellectualism and betrays a trace of genuine concern. Ravenel's cultivation stands in contrast to the rough-hewn medical culture of Altamont, and his literary bent aligns him with Eugene's own sensibilities.

> "Literature, literature, Dick," McGuire returned portentously. "It's been the ruin of many a good surgeon. You read too much, Dick. Yon Cassius hath a lean and hungry look." [014-140]

### At the Hospital: The Tumor Surgery

When McGuire offers Ravenel the chance to take the surgery, Ravenel declines — an act that reveals both professional humility and clear-eyed judgment:

> "I'll stand by," said he. "I won't operate. I'm afraid of one like this. It's your job, drunk or sober." [014-147]

His laconic correction of Coker's description — "removing a woman from a tumor" rather than "removing a tumor from a woman" (014-149) — reveals a surgeon's precision of language and a grim awareness of the case's severity. When he reports that a similar operation by a Hopkins surgeon ended with the patient's death "after a very long prayer" (014-158), Ravenel's understated irony captures both medical skepticism and professional fatalism.

## Relationship with Hugh McGuire

The Ravenel-McGuire relationship is one of the novel's most interesting professional pairings. Ravenel is simultaneously McGuire's junior, his advocate, his caretaker, and his conscience. He praises McGuire as "the best surgeon in America" to the medical establishment, then urges him to bathe and shave before the operation. There is no sentimentality in Ravenel's loyalty — it is the loyalty of someone who knows exactly what McGuire is and values what he can do:

> "Hugh, for God's sake, come on," cried Dick Ravenel. [014-167]

## Narrative Context

Ravenel appears only in Chapter 14, which takes place in the early morning at the town square pharmacy — a recurring setting where the town's professional men (McGuire, Coker, Ben Gant, Harry Tugman, and others) gather to talk. The chapter interweaves bawdy masculine banter with serious medical drama, and Ravenel's presence grounds the scene in professional competence even as McGuire's alcoholic disintegration threatens to derail the upcoming surgery.
