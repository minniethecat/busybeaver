## Synopsis

Miss Ida Nelson is the assistant director of the Shakespeare pageant at Pulpit Hill, a comic minor figure whose single extended appearance in Chapter 27 of *Look Homeward, Angel* provides both structural and comic functions in one of the novel's most satirical set pieces. Tasked with marshaling the unruly student performers through Dr. George B. Rockham's grandiose historical pageant, Miss Nelson spends the afternoon "hissing loudly" in a futile attempt to impose silence on a cast more interested in bawdy jokes and flirtation than Elizabethan dignity.

## Biography

Miss Ida Nelson's entire presence in the novel is confined to the Shakespeare pageant sequence. She is introduced as she attempts to quell the horseplay of the student actors, who are costumed as various Shakespearean characters and awaiting their cues:

> [027-031] Miss Ida Nelson, the assistant director, rushed angrily among them.
>
> [027-032] "Sh!" she hissed loudly. "Sh-h!" She was very angry. She had spent the afternoon hissing loudly.

Her signature "Sh-h!" becomes a running joke. When Ralph Rolls makes a crude comment in the solemn quiet after the Sunday school procession, Miss Nelson responds with predictable fury:

> [027-043] "Sh-h! Sh-h!" hissed Miss Ida Nelson.

The pageant's climax arrives with Eugene's entrance as Prince Hal, dressed in green romantic flummery from a children's play, which immediately provokes an "unnatural silence" from Dr. Rockham:

> [027-057] Eugene saw him depart in a green blur, and presently became aware that an unnatural silence had descended upon Doctor George B. Rockham. The Voice of History was, for the moment, mute. Its long jaw, in fact, had fallen ajar.

In his moment of need, the doctor turns to his assistant — who pointedly refuses to help:

> [027-058] Dr. George B. Rockham looked wildly about him for succor. He rolled his eyes entreatingly upwards at Miss Ida Nelson. She turned her head away.

The pageant dissolves into chaos and uncontrollable laughter, and Miss Nelson disappears from the narrative — her comic function fulfilled, her hissing having been powerless against the tide of absurdity.

## Relationships

### Dr. George B. Rockham

Miss Nelson serves as Dr. Rockham's assistant director — his subordinate in the pageant hierarchy. Their relationship is marked by her dutiful attempts to execute his directions (feeding performers to him "in slow twos") and, in the crisis moment, her refusal to rescue him from the catastrophe of Eugene's entrance. Her turned head in response to his "entreating" eyes is a small but telling act of comic betrayal.

### The Student Cast

Miss Nelson's relationship with the pageant cast is one of mutual exasperation. The students — Ralph Rolls, Julius Arthur, Doc Hines, and the others — treat her orders as background noise, responding to her hissing with crude jokes:

> [027-044] "What the hell does she think she is?" said Julius Arthur, "a steam valve?"

## Critical Context

Miss Ida Nelson is one of many minor academic figures who populate the Pulpit Hill chapters. Her function is almost purely comic: she embodies futile authority, the adult attempt to impose order on youthful chaos, rendered absurd by the sheer energy of the students she cannot control. Her hissing "Sh-h!" — repeated obsessively — becomes a verbal tic that defines her character in the reader's memory despite her minimal page time.

The Shakespeare pageant sequence as a whole is one of Wolfe's most sustained comic set pieces, satirizing small-town cultural pretension, the commercialization of art (the advertising banners for Schwartzberg's and Ginsberg's), and the gap between high-flown rhetoric and bumbling reality. Miss Nelson is the pageant's comic stage manager, and her turned head at the moment of crisis is its perfect, silent punchline.
