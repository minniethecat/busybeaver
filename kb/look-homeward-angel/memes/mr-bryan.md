## Synopsis

Mr. Bryan is one of the novel's many walk-on figures — characters who pass briefly through Altamont, are observed, speculated about, and then disappear. His visit occasions a flurry of journalistic attention from Richard Gorman of the *Citizen*, and the brief exchange between reporter and subject captures something essential about small-town journalism: its earnest desire to magnify local events into news of wider significance, and its hunger for association with figures from the larger world beyond the mountains.

The brief narrative of Bryan's visit — five paragraphs in Chapter 24 — is a miniature of the novel's broader treatment of Altamont's relationship to the outside world. The town is always looking outward, always hoping to be noticed, always measuring itself against absent standards. Mr. Bryan's visit, whatever its actual purpose, becomes an occasion for this ritual of self-measurement.

## Biography

### The Visitor

Bryan appears only through the mediation of the press. His existence is recorded not as direct action but as a series of reported speech-events — introductions, statements, refusals, and the reporter's persistent questioning:

> [024-260] "Ah, there, Dick," said John Smallwood, clasping his hand affectionately, and squeezing his arm, "Just the man I was looking for. Do you know Mr. Bryan?"

The introduction establishes the social ritual that governs the encounter: Smallwood, the local minister, serves as intermediary between the town (represented by the reporter Gorman) and the visiting dignitary (Bryan). The affectionate clasping and squeezing belong to the vocabulary of small-town male sociability — warm, physical, slightly theatrical.

### The Press Encounter

The encounter that follows is a dance of journalistic persistence and subject reluctance:

> [024-264] "I'd like another statement from you before you go, Mr. Bryan," said Richard Gorman. "There's a story going the rounds that you may make your home with us in the future."

The reporter's request — for "another statement" — implies an earlier exchange, a longer visit than the five paragraphs suggest. The rumor he pursues — that Bryan might become a permanent resident — reveals the town's deepest wish: to be chosen, to be deemed worthy of settlement by someone from the outside.

> [024-265] When questioned by a Citizen reporter, Mr. Bryan refused either to confirm or deny the rumor.

Bryan's refusal to confirm or deny is the classic non-answer of the public figure — a response that preserves possibility while committing to nothing. The narrative's shift from direct quotation to reported speech at this moment ("When questioned by a *Citizen* reporter") subtly distances the reader from Bryan's actual words, as if the encounter has already begun its transformation from event into news copy.

### The Final Question

The reporter's persistence outlasts Bryan's reticence:

> [024-272] When pressed more closely by the reporter as to his future plans, Mr. Bryan replied:

The sentence trails off — incomplete, as if the reply itself matters less than the act of pressing for it. The reporter's persistence, Bryan's evasion, and the gap between them is the real subject. What Bryan actually said is secondary to the drama of the asking.

## Relationships

### Richard Gorman

The *Citizen* reporter serves as the town's designated asker of questions — the man whose professional function is to transform private encounters into public knowledge. His persistence with Bryan is both dutiful and slightly desperate, reflecting the small-town journalist's anxiety about missing a story that might connect Altamont to the currents of the larger world.

### Reverend John Smallwood

Smallwood appears in this scene as a facilitator — the man who knows everyone, who makes introductions, who bridges the gap between the visitor and the town. His affectionate clasping of Gorman's arm suggests both genuine warmth and the performative intimacy that characterizes Altamont's social rituals.
