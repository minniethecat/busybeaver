## Synopsis

Colonel James Buchanan Pettigrew is one of Wolfe's most concentrated evocations of the Civil War's long shadow over the American South. The head of the Pettigrew Military Academy — an institution that has survived since 1789 — the Colonel rides through the streets of Altamont in an open victoria, wrapped in a gray Confederate cape, leaning on a heavy polished stick. His "darting eyes" register everything that has changed: the "pimply youths, with slack jaws and a sloppy gait" who now populate his academy, so different from the 117 cadets he marched to war in "the only war that mattered." His mutter — "Not the same! Not the same! Nothing the same!" — is the novel's most economical statement of the rupture between the Old South and the New.

## Biography

Colonel Pettigrew appears in a single remarkable paragraph, but within that compressed space Wolfe renders an entire worldview. The Colonel rides past Wood's pharmacy, a figure out of another century:

> [024-332] Thinking of a more ancient war, in which he had borne himself gallantly, Colonel James Buchanan Pettigrew, head of the Pettigrew Military Academy (Est. 1789), rode by in his open victoria, behind an old black coachman.

His physical description emphasizes both dignity and frailty — "wrapped to his waist in a heavy rug," his shoulders covered with "a gray Confederate cape," his old weight bent forward upon "a heavy polished stick." The freckled hands that grip the stick are the hands of a man whose war ended sixty years ago but has never ended in his mind.

The Colonel's gaze falls on two of his own cadets among the loafers outside the pharmacy:

> [024-338] In the crowd of loafing youngsters that stood across the threshold of Wood's pharmacy, Colonel Pettigrew's darting eyes saw two of his own cadets. They were pimply youths, with slack jaws and a sloppy gait. He muttered his disgust.

What follows is a flash of memory — the 117 cadets he led, "in his proud youth, in the only war that mattered." The specificity of the number ("117, sir") is the specificity of a man who has counted his losses and found them irreplaceable. The Colonel's judgment on the present — "Not the same! Not the same! Nothing the same!" — is less a political statement than an existential lament.

When he sees the "scoundrel" who offends his sense of military decorum, his command is pure Pettigrew:

> [024-336] "Go on! Go on, you scoundrel!" said Colonel Pettigrew.

The scene is brief, but it opens a window onto the world of loss and nostalgia that underlies so much of Altamont's public life. The Colonel is a living monument to a vanished order, and his passage through the town's streets is a daily reminder of what has been lost — and what, in Wolfe's rendering, can never be recovered.
