## Synopsis

"Miss Brown" is a transient boarder at Eliza Gant's Dixieland boarding house who appears exclusively in Chapter 31. She is a prostitute traveling under an assumed name — a fact that Eliza immediately intuits ("I don't believe her name's 'Miss Brown' any more than mine is") [031-093] — and her brief interactions with the adolescent Eugene Gant constitute one of the novel's most direct depictions of sexual initiation and its attendant shame.

Though her role is confined to a single chapter, Miss Brown functions as a threshold figure in Eugene's coming of age: she introduces him to cigarettes, draws him into a clandestine encounter in her room, and extracts his cherished medals as payment. The episode crystallizes the transactional nature of desire that runs throughout the novel, and Miss Brown's calculated primness — "that slightly comic primness — that careful gentility — that marks the conduct of the prostitute incognito" [031-091] — embodies the gap between surface respectability and hidden appetite that Dixieland itself continually exposes.

## Biography

Miss Brown arrives at Dixieland as a paying guest, presenting herself as a woman of thirty-nine years from Indianapolis, Indiana [031-092]. She is "heavily built and quietly dressed," cultivating an air of refinement that Wolfe's narrator immediately undercuts by identifying her as "the prostitute incognito" [031-091]. Her physical description emphasizes a distinctly Midwestern plainness — "her face was simply permeated with the implacable dullness of the Mid-Westerner" — tempered by the "lewdness of her wide thin mouth" and a "smug" expression [031-092].

> [031-091] "Miss Brown" rocked quietly and decorously by herself. She was a heavily built and quietly dressed woman of thirty-nine years, touched with that slightly comic primness — that careful gentility — that marks the conduct of the prostitute incognito.

Her first evening at Dixieland establishes the dynamic she will maintain throughout: she sits primly apart from the other boarders, observing rather than participating, while the more glamorous Irene Mallard commands the center of attention [031-062].

The pivotal scene unfolds late in the evening. After the other boarders have dispersed, Miss Brown remains on the dark porch with Eugene. She complains of wanting a cigarette — a bold request given Eliza's strict household — and when Eugene produces one, the shared smoke becomes an intimacy freighted with transgression:

> [031-127] "I'd give anything for a smoke," muttered "Miss Brown." "I don't suppose I could here?" She looked round her.

> [031-132] "What," said "Miss Brown," with a cunning smile, "what if your mother should see us? You'd catch it!"

The flirtation escalates. Miss Brown declares herself "broad-minded" [031-134] and the two retreat from the porch into the house. When Eliza unexpectedly returns, Miss Brown smoothly performs the role of gracious guest, inviting her to sit and rest [031-145–146], before retiring to her room. Eugene later follows:

> [031-163] He groped softly back along the dark corridor until he came to the door of "Miss Brown's" room. It was slightly ajar. He went in.

The encounter concludes transactionally. Miss Brown takes possession of Eugene's medals — his prized childhood treasures — as payment or collateral for the encounter:

> [031-168] "If you don't redeem them," said "Miss Brown," "I'll give them to my own son when I go home."

This mention of her son is the only biographical detail she volunteers; whether it is true or part of her performed identity remains ambiguous. The following morning, Eugene is left frightened, ashamed, and depleted — a boy who has crossed a threshold he cannot uncross [031-190].

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Miss Brown's relationship with Eugene is simultaneously predatory and pedagogical. She initiates the adolescent into adult behaviors — smoking, sexual encounter — while extracting what little he has of value. Eugene's shame afterward is not primarily about the sex itself but about the transaction: "he was tired of the woman and didn't know how he could pay her. She had all his medals" [031-190]. The medals, symbols of his boyhood achievements and fantasies, become the price of experience.

### Eliza Gant

Eliza is immediately suspicious of Miss Brown, dismissing her assumed name outright [031-093]. Yet their direct interaction is marked by an elaborate performance of mutual politeness — Miss Brown inviting Eliza to sit, Eliza turning on the porch light and making conversation [031-145–146]. This surface cordiality masks Eliza's commercial interest (a paying boarder) and Miss Brown's need to maintain cover. The tension between the respectable widow and the prostitute-incognito mirrors the larger tension Dixieland embodies: a house that is simultaneously home and marketplace.

### Irene Mallard

The contrast between Miss Brown and Irene Mallard — the "tall elegant figure" from Tampa [031-062] — is structurally significant. Irene represents a beauty so refined that Eugene feels himself unworthy ("she seemed too high for his passion") [031-190]; Miss Brown represents the accessible, the transactional, the flesh that does not intimidate. The narrator explicitly links them: "He was afraid of her flesh; he was not afraid of 'Miss Brown's'" [031-190].
