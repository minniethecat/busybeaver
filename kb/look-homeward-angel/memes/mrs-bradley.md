# Mrs. Bradley

## Synopsis

Mrs. Bradley is a minor but vividly realized figure in the Pulpit Hill section of *Look Homeward, Angel* — a widow who runs a lodging-house near the university gates. Wolfe's description is unsparing: she is "a flabby petulant woman with a white face and heart-disease," and yet "her food was excellent" (029-003). The juxtaposition captures Wolfe's documentary eye for the way people are — unflattering physical detail alongside sincere practical compliment. Mrs. Bradley's household includes her surly son G. T., a thwarted sophomore obsessed with fraternity recognition, and the memory of a daughter, Hazel, whose "lewdness was already advertised in the town" (017-189). The Bradley clan, like the Gants themselves, is a family marked by striving, scandal, and the narrow social world of a small Southern town.

## Role in the Novel

### The Bradley Family (Chapter 17)

The Bradleys first enter the novel not through Mrs. Bradley but through her daughter Hazel, whose sexual indiscretion provides one of the novel's moments of grim small-town comedy:

> Moreover, Leonard surprised this youth one afternoon in Spring on the eastern flank of the hill, in the thick grass beneath a flowering dogwood, united in sexual congress with Miss Hazel Bradley, the daughter of a small grocer who lived below on Biltburn Avenue, and whose lewdness was already advertised in the town. Leonard, on second thought, did not go to the Bishop. He went to the Grocer. [017-189]

The euphemism — "united in sexual congress" — is followed by the devastating clause "whose lewdness was already advertised in the town." Hazel Bradley is already marked, already known; the spring afternoon merely confirms what the town has long suspected. Leonard's decision to go "to the Grocer" rather than the Bishop is a small, telling detail: secular authority, not spiritual counsel, is what this scandal requires.

Mr. Bradley's response is unexpected — a wry deflection rather than outrage:

> "Well," said Mr. Bradley, brushing his long mustache reflectively away from his mouth, "you ought to put up a no trespassin' sign." [017-190]

The line is one of the novel's small comic triumphs. Faced with his daughter's public disgrace, the grocer reaches for the language of property rights — as if the flowering dogwood were a disputed boundary rather than the scene of a moral crisis. The gesture — "brushing his long mustache reflectively" — completes the portrait of a man who has learned to absorb scandal with a kind of resigned irony.

### The Grocer's World (Chapters 19, 27)

Mr. Bradley appears in two brief, poetic glimpses as part of the texture of Altamont life. In Chapter 19, at dusk:

> In slow pulses the thick plume of fountain rose, fell upon itself, and slapped the pool in lazy rhythms. A wagon rattled leanly over the big cobbles; beyond the firemen, the grocer Bradley wound up his awning with slow creaking revolutions. [019-004]

The image — Bradley winding up his awning "with slow creaking revolutions" — is one of those small Wolfean moments where a minor character is absorbed into the rhythm of the town itself. The awning, the cobbles, the fountain: all are part of the same slow, sensory fabric.

In Chapter 27, Bradley the Grocer is immortalized in a mock-heroic catalogue of local businesses:

> They came and passed: Ginsberg's — "the glass of fashion and the mould of form"; Bradley the Grocer — "when first Pomona held her fruity horn"; The Buick Agency — "the chariots of Oxus and of Ind." [027-036]

The classical tags — Shakespeare for Ginsberg's, Pope for Bradley, Arnold for Buick — are Eugene's private joke, an overlay of literary grandeur onto the mundane commerce of Altamont. Bradley the Grocer, through Eugene's lens, becomes associated with Pomona, the Roman goddess of fruit — a comic elevation of the man who winds his awning at dusk.

### The Lodging-House (Chapter 29)

When Eugene arrives at the university, he finds lodging with the widowed Mrs. Bradley:

> With fine enthusiasm, but poor judgment, Eugene paid the widow two months in advance. Her name was Bradley: she was a flabby petulant woman with a white face and heart-disease. But her food was excellent. [029-003]

The sentence structure is characteristic Wolfe: the impulsive act ("fine enthusiasm, but poor judgment"), the blunt physical description ("flabby petulant woman with a white face and heart-disease"), and then the concession that undercuts the cruelty ("But her food was excellent"). Eugene may regret his financial imprudence, but he does not go hungry.

Mrs. Bradley's son, known by his initials, is one of Wolfe's sharpest sketches of undergraduate pathology:

> Mrs. Bradley's student son answered to his initial letters — "G. T." G. T. Bradley, a member of the sophomore class, was a surly scowling youth of nineteen — a mixture, in equal parts, of servility and insolence. His chief, but thwarted, ambition was to be elected to membership in a fraternity. Having failed to win recognition by the exercise of his natural talents, he was driven by an extraordinary obsession that fame and glory would come to him if he were known as the slave-driver of a number of Freshmen. [029-003]

The passage dissects G. T. with clinical precision: "surly scowling," "servility and insolence" in equal measure, thwarted ambition curdling into a fantasy of domination over younger students. It is a portrait of the kind of small, corrosive ambition that festers in the closed world of a university — and it reflects, by proximity, on Mrs. Bradley herself, who presides over this household of thwarted energies.

The Bradley lodging-house becomes a locus of student life, with Jim Trivett and his companions living nearby:

> Jim Trivett lived with several other students from his community in a lodging-house near Mrs. Bradley's but closer to the west gate of the university. [029-021]

The geography is precise — "closer to the west gate" — and the Bradley house becomes one of the fixed points in Eugene's map of Pulpit Hill.
