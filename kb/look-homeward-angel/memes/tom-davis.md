## Synopsis

Tom Davis is one of the more vividly realized students at John Dorsey Leonard's private academy — a boy whose skeptical, argumentative intelligence and "heavy laughter" make him a memorable presence in the classroom scenes. He functions as a kind of pragmatic foil to the classical curriculum: when Mr. Leonard expounds on Horace and Virgil, it is Tom Davis who demands to know the practical value of it all. His questions — "What's the good of all this stuff?", "a man who has studied Greek makes a better plumber than one who hasn't?" — voice a homespun empiricism that the novel neither endorses nor dismisses. In the later school scenes, his irrepressible laughter at the teachers' mannerisms marks him as a fully realized member of the boy-world Wolfe recreates with such care.

## Biography

Tom Davis appears primarily in the classroom scenes at Leonard's private academy, where Eugene Gant endures the classical curriculum under the tutelage of John Dorsey Leonard and his sister Amy. Davis is defined by his questioning disposition — an insistence on relevance that repeatedly interrupts the smooth flow of recitation:

> [017-009] "What sort?" Tom Davis insisted. "Harder than Cicero?"

When the class moves from Cicero to Horace, it is Tom Davis who presses for definitions and practical applications:

> [017-014] "He wrote Odes and Epodes," said Tom Davis. "What is an Epode, Mr. Leonard?"

His most pointed challenge comes when he questions the utility of classical education itself, in terms that resonate with the novel's broader tensions between the Old World of European culture and the New World of American practicality:

> [017-058] "What's the good of all this stuff?" said Tom Davis argumentatively.

And:

> [017-068] "According to what you say," said Tom Davis, "a man who has studied Greek makes a better plumber than one who hasn't."

His humor is physical as well as verbal. When Mr. Leonard offers up his own Latin pun — "Egibus wantibus a peckibus of cornibus" — it is Tom Davis whose "sounding laughter" validates the joke. Later, in the classroom scenes with a different teacher, his barely suppressed mirth becomes a running counterpoint to the lesson:

> [024-003] Tom Davis turned his reddening cheeks toward the window, a low sputter of laughter escaping from his screwed lips.

> [024-008] Their wild laughter leaped like freed hounds. Tom Davis hurled himself violently downward over his desk.

Tom Davis also participates in the quieter moments of classroom life — the whispered speculations about the tubercular artist and his young wife, the shared wonder at the "unsearchable riddle" of life and death that runs beneath the daily lessons.
