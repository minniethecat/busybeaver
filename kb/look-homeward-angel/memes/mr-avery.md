## Synopsis

Mr. Avery is one of the novel's most vivid minor portraits — a scholar trapped in a paralyzed body, shuffling toward the young Eugene Gant at "an inch to the second" [024-205] to deliver a Latin conjugation and a wheezing benediction. His appearance in Chapter 24 occupies less than ten paragraphs, but the image of the gasping, shuffling, stick-tattooing old man bearing down on the boy with questions about amo and amas is one of the novel's most memorable encounters between youth and age, vitality and decay, the living present and the classical past.

Avery embodies the persistence of learning in a town that has little use for it — a "very great scholar" [024-205] whose scholarship has no audience except the occasional boy he can corner on a street corner. His tragedy is not that he is paralyzed but that he has no one to talk to; his comedy is that he will talk anyway, gasping and wheezing, because the alternative is silence.

## Biography

### The Scholar Paralyzed

Mr. Avery is introduced through the physical fact of his body before any word is spoken:

> [024-205] Advancing upon him, an inch to the second, Eugene saw, ten feet away, the heavy paralyzed body of old Mr. Avery. He was a very great scholar, the narrator adds, with the characteristic Wolfean move from physical grotesquerie to spiritual claim.

The juxtaposition of "heavy paralyzed body" with "very great scholar" is the organizing tension of the portrait. The body that cannot move houses a mind that has ranged across centuries of Latin verse. The paralysis is literal but also metaphorical: Avery's learning has no field of action, trapped as it is in a town that does not recognize or require it.

### The Approach

Avery's approach to Eugene is a comic ordeal of duration:

> [024-208] Gasping a welcome, Mr. Avery bore down on him, with a violent shuffle of his feet and a palsied tattoo of his heavy stick which brought him over the intervening three yards in forty seconds.

The precision of the measurement — "three yards in forty seconds" — renders the encounter in terms at once clinical and tender. The "palsied tattoo of his heavy stick" translates physical affliction into a kind of percussive announcement: the old scholar cannot walk, but he can be heard.

### The Latin Test

When Avery finally reaches his target, he dispenses with pleasantries and goes directly to the matter at hand — a test of the boy's classical knowledge:

> [024-211] "Poeta nascitur, non fit," said Mr. Avery, and went off into a silent wheeze of laughter which brought on a fit of coughing strangulation. His eyes bulged, his tender pink skin grew crimson, he roared his terror out in a phlegmy rattle.

The quotation — "a poet is born, not made" — is both a statement of literary doctrine and a self-description. Avery himself was born a scholar; no amount of Altamont indifference could unmake him. But the laughter that follows nearly kills him, transforming mirth into "a fit of coughing strangulation" — the body, as always, reclaiming its dominion over the mind.

> [024-213] "Can you conjugate?" gasped Mr. Avery. "Here's the way I learned: 'Amo, amas, I love a lass. Amat, He loves her, too.'"

The schoolboy mnemonic — "Amo, amas, I love a lass" — is Avery's gift to Eugene, and through Eugene to the reader. It is doggerel, not poetry; it is the Latin of the schoolroom, not the scholar's study. But in Avery's gasping delivery, it becomes something more: a transmission of learning across the gulf of generations, a gift from a paralyzed body to a living one, a fragment of the classical world preserved in memory and passed on in love.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Eugene is Avery's audience of one — "just the boy he wanted" [024-205]. Their relationship is defined by asymmetry: age and youth, paralysis and vitality, erudition and inexperience. But the asymmetry runs both ways: Avery has knowledge Eugene lacks, while Eugene has a future Avery has already spent. The Latin test is less an examination than an act of transmission — the old scholar passing his beloved verbs to the next carrier of the flame.
