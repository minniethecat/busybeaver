## Synopsis

Fagg Sluder is a background figure in the social landscape of Altamont's town square — one of the aging men who populate Oliver Gant's world of shop-front gossip and afternoon idleness. A self-made man who has "salted away his fortune in two big buildings on the Square," he represents the commercial success that Gant himself never achieved [019-002]. His presence in the novel is fleeting but emblematic: he belongs to the chorus of town characters — Jannadeau, Liddell, Cardiac — whose conversations mark the slow passage of time and Gant's own gradual decline.

## Biography

Fagg Sluder appears in Chapter 19 as part of the network of idlers and gossips that surrounds Oliver Gant in his later years. He is introduced in a catalogue of Gant's daily companions:

> [019-002] He now rose a full hour later, he came to his shop punctually, but he spent long hours of the day extended on the worn leather couch of his office, or in gossip with Jannadeau, bawdy old Liddell, Cardiac, and Fagg Sluder, who had salted away his fortune in two big buildings on the Square and was at the present moment tilted comfortably in a chair before the fire department, gossiping eagerly with members of the ball club, whose chief support he was.

The portrait is one of comfortable retirement: Sluder has made his money, invested it in real estate, and now spends his afternoons tilted back in a chair, talking baseball. He is a patron of the local ball club — "whose chief support he was" — suggesting both his wealth and his investment in the town's civic life.

His only other direct appearance comes later in the same chapter, in one of Wolfe's characteristic freeze-frame passages. After a ball game, as evening settles on the square, Sluder is captured in a suspended moment:

> [019-082] All life seemed frozen in a picture: the firemen and Fagg Sluder had seen Gant, whispered, and were now looking toward him; a policeman, at the high side-porch of the Police Court, leaned on the rail and stared...

The whisper and the stare suggest that Sluder and the firemen are observing Gant with concern — aware, perhaps, of his declining health, or simply noting the approach of a familiar figure whose vitality has visibly diminished.

His wife, Mrs. Fagg Sluder, appears briefly in Chapter 21 among the ladies of the First Baptist Church who congratulate Gant outside the warehouse [021-010].

## Relationships

### Oliver Gant

Sluder is part of Gant's late-life social circle — one of the men with whom Gant whiles away the afternoons when he should be working. The contrast between Sluder (financially secure, a property owner, a patron of the ball club) and Gant (still struggling, still raging against his decline) is implicit but sharp.
