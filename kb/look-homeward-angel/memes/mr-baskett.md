# Mr. William H. Baskett

## Synopsis

Mr. William H. Baskett is a minor character in *Look Homeward, Angel*, one of the many transient boarders who pass through [[eliza-gant|Eliza Gant]]'s establishment, Dixieland. A cotton grower and banker from Hattiesburg, Mississippi, he is fifty-three years old and suffers from malaria during his stay. Though his appearances are brief, his wry observation about [[eugene-gant|Eugene]]'s hapless gardening provides a moment of dry, affectionate humor amid the chaos of the boarding house.

## Biography

Mr. Baskett is first mentioned in Chapter 14 as one of the eclectic collection of guests residing at Dixieland. The narrative catalogs the boarders room by room in a characteristic Wolfean set piece, listing Baskett alongside a lion-tamer, a candy-wheel concessionaire, chorus girls, and an alcoholic druggist's wife:

> [014-198] Mr. William H. Baskett, fifty-three, of Hattiesburg, Mississippi, cotton grower, banker, and sufferer from malaria, and his wife.

This single sentence establishes Baskett's identity with the density of a census entry—name, age, origin, occupation, affliction, and marital status compressed into a single breath. He is both a specific individual and a representative type: the Southern businessman seeking the mountain air of Altamont for his health.

He reappears in Chapter 22, during Eugene's ill-fated attempt at gardening. Eliza, exasperated at her son for "chopping down every stock of corn in the row," appeals to Baskett as a witness. The cotton merchant peers down at the boy with professional authority:

> [022-004] "Yes," Mr. Baskett said, peering over, "and missed every weed. Boy," he added judicially, "you need two months on a farm."

The irony is palpable: a cotton grower, himself laid low by malaria, prescribing farm labor as the cure for a dreamy, bookish boy's incompetence. His judgment—simultaneously accurate and absurd—captures the novel's characteristic blend of comedy and empathy.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Baskett belongs to the gallery of minor Dixieland boarders who collectively form the novel's social backdrop. Each boarder is sketched with a few telling details—Baskett's malaria, his "scraggly mustaches," his "grinning malarially" (022-003)—that create the impression of a fully inhabited world without requiring full development. He is one of the many transient figures whose brief intersection with the Gant family illuminates some aspect of their lives: here, the impossibility of making a poet out of a farmer's grandson, or a farmer out of a poet.
