# Mrs. Clarence Firkins

## Synopsis

Mrs. Clarence Firkins exists almost entirely within a single virtuoso passage of *Look Homeward, Angel* — the satirical society column that opens the Altamont section of the novel. She and her husband host a dinner dance at their "beautiful residence" Snotwood in honor of their daughter Gladys's debut, and the account of this event, rendered in the inflated prose of small-town social journalism, is one of Wolfe's most sustained exercises in comic deflation. Mrs. Firkins is less a character than a vehicle for satire — the hostess as social performer, "greeting each of the arriving guests at the threshold in a manner reviving the finest old traditions of Southern aristocracy" (014-177). The absurdity of the prose is the point: in Altamont, even the pretense to aristocracy is a kind of striving.

## Role in the Novel

### The Society Column (Chapter 14)

Mrs. Firkins appears in the novel's extended parody of small-town newspaper society pages — a passage that systematically inflates the mundane social rituals of Altamont into a mock-heroic register:

> "Members of the Younger Set were charmingly entertained last night at a dinner dance given at Snotwood, the beautiful residence of Mr. and Mrs. Clarence Firkins, in honor of their youngest daughter, Gladys, who made her debut this season. Mr. and Mrs. Firkins, accompanied by their daughter, greeted each of the arriving guests at the threshold in a manner reviving the finest old traditions of Southern aristocracy, while Mrs. Firkins' accomplished sister, Miss Catherine Hipkiss, affectionately known to members of the local younger set as Roaring Kate, supervised the checking of overcoats, evening wraps, jockstraps, and jewelry." [014-177]

Every element of the passage is double-voiced: the "beautiful residence" named Snotwood (a name that hardly needs glossing), the "finest old traditions of Southern aristocracy" (which, in a town like Altamont, are largely invented), the sister nicknamed "Roaring Kate," and the inventory of checked items that descends from "evening wraps" to the absurd intrusion of "jockstraps." The prose mimics the breathless self-importance of society journalism while systematically undermining it.

Gladys Firkins — the debutante — appears only as a name in a list:

> "Among those dancing were the Misses Aline Titsworth, Lena Ginster, Ophelia Legg, Gladys Firkins, Beatrice Slutsky, Mary Whitesides, Helen Shockett, and Lofta Barnes." [014-180]

The catalogue of names is itself a comic device — each name slightly more absurd than the last, culminating in "Lofta Barnes." The young women are rendered interchangeable, reduced to items in a list, their individuality submerged in the formulaic prose of the society column.

### The Firkins Name (Chapter 23)

The surname Firkins reappears in a very different context later in the novel — attached to a University of Chicago professor:

> Witness Professor Albert Thorndyke Firkins, of the University of Chicago, at the Falcon in Soho. Smiling bravely, he sits over a half-pint of bitter beer, in the company of a racing tout, a swaybacked barmaid, broad in the stern, with adjustable teeth, and three companionable tarts from Lisle street, who are making the best of two pints of Guinness. With eager impatience he awaits the arrival of G. K. Chesterton and E. V. Lucas. [023-021]

Whether Professor Albert Thorndyke Firkins is related to the Altamont Firkinses is never clarified — the novel leaves the connection, if any, to the reader's imagination. The academic Firkins, slumming in Soho with "companionable tarts" while awaiting literary celebrities, is a figure of satire in a different key: the provincial intellectual abroad, performing cosmopolitanism with the same awkwardness that the Altamont Firkinses perform aristocracy.

### Satirical Function

Mrs. Firkins belongs to a category of Wolfean character that exists to be satirized — the small-town social climber whose pretensions the narrator simultaneously ventriloquizes and demolishes. She is kin to the other figures in the society column and the broader Altamont social pageantry: the "Younger Set," the debutantes, the gin-ecologist Dr. Spaugh. Together they form a portrait of a town that longs to be more than it is, and whose longing is both ridiculous and — in Wolfe's treatment — oddly sympathetic.
