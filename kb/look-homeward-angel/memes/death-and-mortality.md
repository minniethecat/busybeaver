# Death and Mortality

## Definition

Death is the constant companion of every character in *Look Homeward, Angel*. Oliver Gant is a stonecutter of tombstones — his daily labor is to carve "the dove, the lamb, the smooth joined marble hands of death" for the graves of Altamont. Death is his trade, his livelihood, and ultimately his fate. Eliza remarks that "there was no money in death" because "people died too slowly," a darkly comic observation that reveals death's role as an economic reality as well as a metaphysical one.

Wolfe treats death not as a thematic abstraction but as a visceral, physical presence. Bodies decay, graves open, ghosts walk. The novel's most powerful death — Ben Gant's — occupies the entirety of Part III and transforms Eugene's understanding of loss, family, and the finality that awaits everyone.

## Textual Evidence

Oliver's first encounter with the angel is in a shop of funerary art — "smooth granite slabs of death":

> (001-007) He had walked along a street in Baltimore, and seen within a little shop smooth granite slabs of death, carved lambs and cherubim, and an angel poised upon cold phthisic feet.

Gant's trade is explicitly death-work:

> (001-009) The dove, the lamb, the smooth joined marble hands of death, and letters fair and fine — but not the angel.

Eliza's observation that "there was no money in death" reveals the grim economics of mortality:

> (002-004) But Eliza was not content with his trade: there was no money in death. People, she thought, died too slowly.

Death haunts the house physically — Gant's cancer, the wasting disease that consumes him:

> (013-012) Now, he awoke in the dark, possessed by his terror and agony, for the whole right side of his body was paralyzed by such pain as he did not know existed.

Ben's death scene is the novel's emotional center. Eugene's prayer at his brother's deathbed acknowledges the nothingness that death threatens:

> (035-204) He did not believe in God, nor in Heaven or Hell, but he was afraid they might be true. He did not believe in angels with soft faces and bright wings, but he was afraid they might exist.

And yet the novel's response to death is defiance — an assertion that some part of the dead endures:

> (035-212) We can believe in the nothingness of life, we can believe in the nothingness of death and of life after death — but who can believe in the nothingness of Ben?

Eugene, visiting Ben's grave, contemplates the emptiness of death in a landscape of dead leaves:

> (037-050) When he came to the gate of the cemetery he found it open. He went in quickly and walked swiftly up the winding road that curved around the crest of the hill. The grasses were dry and sere; a wilted wreath of flowers lay upon a grave.

The novel's closing vision asserts that the dead are not truly gone — "pain and pride and death will pass, and will not come again":

> (037-099) The laurel, the lizard, and the stone will come no more. The women weeping at the gate have gone and will not come again. And pain and pride and death will pass, and will not come again. And light and dawn will come again, and the laurel and the lizard and the stone will come again.

## Analysis

Death in Wolfe's novel is never a simple ending. It enfolds life like a garment — the tombstones Oliver carves are both monuments to death and works of art that outlast it. The central paradox is that Gant makes his living from death yet cannot accept his own mortality; he rages against it, drinks against it, and carves against it.

The novel's treatment of death connects to the stone angel motif: stone is death made visible — cold, permanent, unchanging — yet the angel carved from stone represents transcendent beauty. This is Wolfe's theology of art: what is made from the materials of death can become a vehicle for life.

Ben's death, the novel's most extended treatment of mortality, is simultaneously devastating and transformative. Eugene's loss of Ben drives him out of Altamont and into the larger world. The dead brother becomes a ghost who visits him on the night before his departure, speaking the novel's most consoling words.
