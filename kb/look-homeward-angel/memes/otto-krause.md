## Synopsis

Otto Krause is a German-American schoolboy who appears briefly but memorably in the childhood chapters of *Look Homeward, Angel*. As Eugene Gant's classmate and partner in early sexual curiosity, Otto serves as a guide into the world of boyhood mischief — the "gardens of delight" of the chapter's title. He is one of the few named characters in Eugene's grammar-school years, and his brief presence marks the beginning of Eugene's awakening to the mysteries of sex and the body.

Otto's character is drawn with vivid physical detail characteristic of Wolfe's minor portraits: his "cheese-nosed, hair-faced, inch-browed" appearance, his hoarse laugh, and his lewd scrawlings combine to create a comic but not unsympathetic figure of pre-adolescent vulgarity.

## Biography

Otto Krause is introduced in Chapter 8, during Eugene's grammar school years. He is fourteen years old, six years older than the eight-year-old Eugene, and sits next to him in the third grade:

> [008-049] There was a boy named Otto Krause, a cheese-nosed, hair-faced, inch-browed German boy, lean and swift in the legs, hoarse-voiced and full of idiot laughter, who showed him the gardens of delight. There was a girl named Bessie Barnes, a black-haired, tall, bold-figured girl of thirteen years who acted as model. Otto Krause was fourteen, Eugene was eight: they were in the third grade. The German boy sat next to him, drew obscenities on his books, and passed his furtive scrawled indecencies across the aisle to Bessie.

Otto's lewd drawings, passed to Bessie Barnes, become the focal point of a shared secret world between the boys. Together, Eugene and Otto amuse themselves by drawing obscenities in their geography textbooks:

> [008-052] In their furtive moments at school, he and Otto amused each other by drawing obscenities in their geographies, bestowing on the representations of tropical natives sagging breasts and huge organs. And they composed on tiny scraps of paper dirty little rhymes.

When Eugene's cache of obscene poetry is discovered by the principal, Otto provides a mixture of commiseration and dark humor. As Eugene anxiously watches Principal Armstrong examine the damning evidence, he turns to Otto:

> [008-055] "What does it mean? What do you reckon it means?" he whispered dryly to Otto Krause.

Otto's response is characteristically blunt and amused by Eugene's predicament:

> [008-056] "Oh, you'll ketch it now!" said Otto Krause, laughing hoarsely.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Otto is Eugene's partner in boyhood transgression — a slightly older boy who initiates the younger Eugene into the world of dirty drawings, bawdy rhymes, and furtive glances at girls. Their friendship is founded on shared mischief rather than deep emotional connection, but for Eugene, the alliance with Otto represents a step away from the solitude of his own imagination and into the rough camaraderie of boyhood.

### Bessie Barnes

Otto's relationship with Bessie Barnes is conducted entirely through furtive glances and obscene drawings passed across the classroom aisle. Bessie, described as "a black-haired, tall, bold-figured girl of thirteen years," responds to Otto's scrawled indecencies with "a lewd face, and a contemptuous blow against her shapely lifted buttock, a gesture which Otto considered as good as a promise."

## Critical Context

Otto Krause belongs to a cluster of minor childhood figures — Bessie Barnes, Max Isaacs, the schoolyard bullies — who populate the early chapters of the novel and establish the texture of Eugene's Altamont boyhood. Otto's German surname and ethnic marking ("cheese-nosed, hair-faced, inch-browed German boy") reflect the polyglot character of the fictional Altamont, modeled on Wolfe's Asheville, where German, Scots-Irish, and English settler families mingled.

The episode in which Otto and Eugene share obscene drawings has drawn critical attention for its frankness about childhood sexuality, a subject Wolfe treats with neither moralizing nor sentimentality but as a natural part of the boy's developing consciousness. Otto, as the slightly older initiator, serves a structural function: he is the companion who opens a door that Eugene will eventually walk through alone.
