# Guy Doak

## Synopsis

Guy Doak is Eugene Gant's roommate during his time at the Leonards' preparatory school, and one of Wolfe's most economically drawn studies in regional contrast. A native of Newark, New Jersey — his mother a tubercular boardinghouse mistress who has come south to recover her health — Doak embodies a crisp, shallow, city-bred rationalism that Wolfe sets in deliberate opposition to the "floridity" and "hearty violence" of Eugene's Southern classmates. Wolfe devotes a long, aphoristic paragraph to anatomizing Doak's character, and the verdict is ambivalent: Doak is "a very nice fellow" but also "sharp, obstinate, unsubtle," a "false Yankee realist" whose cynicism is as much a form of superstition as the romanticism he derides. His brief appearances in the novel — scrawling notes in fractured German, cracking jokes during schoolboy study sessions, tapping messages through the floorboards at night — capture the texture of adolescent boarding-school life with documentary precision.

## Role in the Novel

### The New Roommate (Chapter 24)

Eugene comes to room with Guy Doak during Eliza's absence from Altamont:

> During this season of Eliza's absence he roomed with Guy Doak. [024-019]

The arrangement is practical — a boarding situation, not a friendship — but it places Eugene in sustained proximity to a sensibility entirely foreign to the Gant world. Doak is introduced through a formal character sketch, one of Wolfe's set-piece descriptions:

> Guy Doak was five years his senior. He was a native of Newark, New Jersey: his speech was touched with Yankee nasality, his manner with Yankee crispness. His mother, a boardinghouse mistress, had come to Altamont a year or two before to retrieve her health: she was tubercular, and spent part of the winter in Florida. [024-020]

The details establish Doak as an outsider — a Northerner in a Southern school, his nasality and crispness marking him as other. The mention of his tubercular mother adds a somber undertone: Doak, like many of the boarding students, is displaced by adult illness.

### Physical Description

Wolfe's physical description of Doak is characteristically precise and faintly unflattering:

> Guy Doak had a trim cocky figure of medium height, black hair, bright dark eyes, a pale, very smooth oval face, somehow suggestive, Eugene thought, of a fish's belly, with somewhat unhappily full jaws which made his lower features seem larger than his upper. He was foppishly neat in his dress. People called him a good-looking boy. [024-021]

The phrase "somehow suggestive, Eugene thought, of a fish's belly" is pure Wolfe — an uncharitable simile filtered through the focalizing consciousness of Eugene, who is both observant and cruel in the way of adolescents. The concluding "People called him a good-looking boy" acts as a deflating footnote, undercutting Eugene's private unkindness with public consensus.

### The Yankee Realist

The core of Doak's character sketch is a long passage that uses him as a cultural type:

> Guy Doak had none of their floridity. He was lacking in their hearty violence. He did not laugh loudly. He had a sharp, bright, shallow mind, inflexibly dogmatic. His companions were bad Southern romantics, he was a false Yankee realist. They arrived, thus, by different means, at a common goal of superstition. [024-023]

This is Wolfe at his most essayistic — the character becomes a thesis about regional epistemology. The Southern romantics and the Yankee realist, Wolfe argues, converge on "a common goal of superstition": both modes of thought are ultimately irrational, merely wearing different costumes. The passage continues in an aphoristic register that owes something to the American transcendentalists:

> Guy Doak had already hardened into the American city-dweller's mould of infantile cynicism. He was occasionally merry with the other boys in the classic manner of the city fellow with the yokels. He was wise. Above all, he was wise. It was safe to assume, he felt, that Truth was always on the scaffold, and Wrong forever on the throne. So far from being depressed by the slaughter of the innocents, the spectacle gave him much bitter amusement. [024-023]

The repeated "He was wise. Above all, he was wise" is ironic — Wolfe's narrator does not endorse Doak's self-estimation. The "infantile cynicism" of the American city-dweller is, in Wolfe's taxonomy, a hardening of the spirit, a premature closure against experience.

### Classroom Antics

Doak's classroom appearances are brief but vivid. In one scene, he disrupts a study session with a joke:

> "Slowly the moonlight crept up the chair in which the old man was sitting, reaching his knees, his breast, and finally," — Guy Doak looked up slyly at his tutor, "giving him a good punch in the eye." [024-010]

The joke is a schoolboy's subversion of Gothic atmosphere — the slow creep of moonlight turned into slapstick. Doak's "sly" glance at the tutor captures the pleasure of the classroom comedian who knows exactly how far he can push.

In another moment, Doak passes a note with fractured German:

> Guy Doak scrawled a brief message across a crumpled wad and thrust it on Eugene's desk. Eugene read: *Gebe mir ein Stück Papier, Before I bust you on the ear.* [024-014]

The macaronic humor — German polite request followed by English threat — is typical of schoolboy wit, and the scene captures the texture of a classroom where multiple languages and registers jostle against each other.

### Boarding-House Life

The domestic arrangements at Leonard's are rendered in affectionate detail:

> Outside of this, Guy Doak was a very nice fellow — sharp, obstinate, unsubtle, and pleased with his wit. They lived on the first floor at Leonard's: at night, by a roaring wood fire, they listened carefully to the great thunder of the trees, and to the stealthy creaking footsteps of the master as he came softly down the stairs, and paused by their door. [024-024]

The concession — "outside of this, Guy Doak was a very nice fellow" — is characteristic of Wolfe's narrative voice, which can anatomize a character's limitations and then, in the next breath, offer a warm human verdict. The nighttime scene — the roaring fire, the "great thunder of the trees," the creaking footsteps of John Dorsey — is one of the novel's small atmospheric triumphs, capturing the sensory world of boarding-school life.

The paragraph continues with a catalogue of the household:

> They ate at table with Margaret, John Dorsey, Miss Amy, the two children, John Dorsey, junior, nine, and Margaret, five, and two of Leonard's Tennessee nephews — Tyson Leonard, a ferret-faced boy of eighteen, foulmouthed and sly, and Dirk Barnard, a tall slender boy, seventeen, with a bumpy face, brown merry eyes, and a quick temper. [024-024]

The dinner table is a microcosm of the school, and the physical descriptions — "ferret-faced," "bumpy face," "quick temper" — are thumbnail sketches that give each minor figure a distinct presence. The shared mischief that follows binds the boys together:

> At table they kept up a secret correspondence of innuendo and hidden movement, fleshing a fork in a grunting neighbor as John Dorsey said the blessing, and choking with smothered laughter. At night, they tapped messages on floor and ceiling, crept out for sniggering conventions in the windy dark hall, and fled to their innocent beds as John Dorsey stormed down on them. [024-024]

The "secret correspondence of innuendo," the fork in the neighbor's side during grace, the tapped messages — all of it captures a specific kind of adolescent camaraderie that exists in any boarding school, in any era. Doak is part of this world, one boy among many, his Yankee crispness subsumed into the collective mischief of boyhood.

### The Gazing Classroom

Before the character sketch unfolds, Eugene first sees Doak in the classroom:

> Guy Doak gazed solemnly at Eugene, with a forked hand stroking his grave pallid face. [024-004]

The image — "forked hand," "grave pallid face" — is faintly absurd, a parody of philosophical contemplation. Doak is performing thoughtfulness, and Eugene, even as he later becomes Doak's roommate, registers the performance.
