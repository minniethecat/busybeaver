# Dr. Hugh McGuire

## Synopsis

Dr. Hugh McGuire is Altamont's town physician — a burly, alcoholic, yet profoundly loyal doctor who delivered the Gant children, treated Oliver Gant through hundreds of drunken spells, and serves as a living chronicle of the town's bodily and spiritual ailments. His presence at the drugstore counter, impaling kidney beans with surgical precision while steeped in corn whisky, is one of the novel's most vivid recurring images of small-town medical practice in the early twentieth century.

## Biography

Dr. McGuire has been Altamont's physician for decades, his career intertwined with the Gant family from the beginning. He delivered Eugene, attended the family through typhoid and countless emergencies, and knew every member of the household intimately.

His first appearance in the narrative is during young Eugene's accident with the wagon:

> (004-060) Then, with his legs carefully straddled, he moved on, drawing the wagon beyond Eugene's body, and stopping. Both negroes awoke simultaneously; there were cries within the house, and Eliza and Gant rushed out.

> (004-064) "This looks worse than it is," observed Dr. McGuire, laying the hero upon the lounge. "Some hot water, please." Nevertheless, it took two hours to bring him round. Everyone spoke highly of the horse.

Dr. McGuire's role extends beyond emergencies; he is present at Gant's most desperate moments:

> (013-011) They had but shortly returned from Mardi Gras when, one howling night in winter, as he lay asleep at Gant's, the house was wakened by his father's terrible cries. Gant had been drinking heavily, day and night, and now he lay in the grip of an agonizing attack of inflammatory rheumatism.

## Character and Habits

McGuire's defining trait is the tension between his professional competence and his personal dissolution. He drinks heavily — corn whisky soaks the air around him — yet his surgical hands remain steady, his diagnostic instincts sound.

The drugstore scene in chapter 14 offers the fullest portrait:

> (014-051) Within, Dr. Hugh McGuire sat on a stool patiently impaling kidney beans, one at a time, upon the prongs of his fork. A strong odor of corn whisky soaked the air about him. His thick skilful butcher's hands — they had delivered half the town — moved with the deliberation of a man who has learned that speed is the enemy of precision.

In dialogue, McGuire is blunt, affectionate, and self-mocking:

> (014-078) "Listen to him," said McGuire to Coker. "A chip off the old block, isn't he? I brought that boy into the world, saw him through typhoid, got the old man over seven hundred drunks, and I've been called in on every one of them. . . ."

> (014-081) "By God, if you ever cut me open, McGuire," said Ben, "I'm going to be damned sure you can walk straight before you do."

McGuire's response is not indignation but a kind of sodden melancholy:

> (014-083) McGuire, drunkenly lost in revery, stared witlessly down at his bean plate and sighed.

## Relationship with the Gant Family

McGuire's bond with the Gants spans three generations. He delivered the children, treated Oliver Gant through _"over seven hundred drunks"_ (014-078), and attended each family crisis with gruff fidelity. His relationship with Ben is particularly intimate — Ben can joke about McGuire's drunkenness to his face, a liberty that speaks to years of shared history.

The doctor also functions as a moral anchor. When young Dr. Jefferson Spaugh arrives in patent-leather pumps, McGuire's contempt is immediate and protective:

> (014-101) "Patent-leather pumps!" said McGuire. "Hurt his feet. By God, Coker, the first time he came to town ten years ago he'd never been curried above the knees. They had to throw him down to put shoes on him."

His final gesture in the scene is one of reconciliation, pushing friendship on the young rival:

> (014-110) "Shake hands with Coker before you go, Horse," said McGuire. "He's the best friend you've ever had. You ought to give him royalties."

## Symbolic Significance

Dr. McGuire embodies several key themes of the novel:

| theme | expression |
|-------|-----------|
| **The body and mortality** | As physician, he witnesses every birth and death in Altamont; his own body is a ruin of alcohol and time |
| **Loyalty and family** | His connection to the Gants transcends professional obligation — he is practically kin |
| **The grotesque** | His drunkenness, his butcher's hands, his bean-impaling — all are rendered with Wolfe's characteristic mingling of affection and unflinching realism |
| **Time and change** | He is a relic of an older Altamont, threatened by young doctors in silk lapels and Buick roadsters |
