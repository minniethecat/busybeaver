## Synopsis

"Mr. Vanderbilt" is not a character in the conventional sense but a nickname — a label of mockery applied to Eugene Gant that crystallizes the novel's exploration of class, snobbery, and the social ambitions of the Gant family. The epithet functions as a mirror: what others see as pretension in Eugene, Eliza Gant sees as a challenge to be met.

The nickname appears sparingly but at structurally significant moments — during Eugene's early consciousness of social status, in the debates over his schooling, and in the jealous gossip of adolescent rivals. Each occurrence layers additional meaning onto what begins as a simple taunt, transforming "Mr. Vanderbilt" from a schoolyard insult into a commentary on the Gant family's relationship to the town they inhabit.

## Biography

### Origins of the Nickname

The epithet first surfaces as a narrative observation rather than quoted speech. In a passage that anatomizes the social consciousness of both Gant brothers, the narrator notes:

> [010-067] He would have been called "Mr. Vanderbilt" or "the Prince of Wales."

The conditional construction ("would have been called") suggests the nickname is less a specific historical incident than an atmospheric condition of Eugene's boyhood — the natural consequence of being perceived as putting on airs in a town that enforces democratic leveling through mockery. The passage links Eugene with his brother Ben in a shared condition of aristocratic aspiration without aristocratic means.

### The School Debate

When the question of Eugene's education arises — private school versus public — the nickname reappears as a weapon in family argument. The family's contempt for private education is expressed through the epithet:

> [016-025] The family was contemptuous. Private school! Mr. Vanderbilt! Ruin him for good!

Here "Mr. Vanderbilt" condenses an entire worldview: private education is not merely impractical but morally corrupting, a betrayal of democratic citizenship ("the public school as an incubator of citizenship" [016-025]). The nickname becomes shorthand for a whole complex of anxieties about class, education, and belonging.

### Eliza's Response

Eliza Gant's reaction to the nickname reveals the essential ambiguity of the Gant family's social position. Where others use "Mr. Vanderbilt" as a taunt, Eliza treats it as a dare:

> [016-026] Which made Eliza reflective. She had a good streak of snobbism. Mr. Vanderbilt? She was as good as any of them. They'd just see.

The narrator's diagnosis — "a good streak of snobbism" — is characteristically double-edged, acknowledging Eliza's social ambition while gently mocking its intensity. Her internal response transforms the insult into a project: if Eugene is to be called "Mr. Vanderbilt," then by God, they would make the name fit.

### Adolescent Jealousy

In the novel's middle chapters, the nickname circulates among Eugene's peers as a currency of adolescent rivalry. Harry Tugman, watching the comings and goings of the town's young men, deploys it with jealous precision:

> [014-172] As Jeff Spaugh roared off Harry Tugman said jealously: "Look at that bastard. Mr. Vanderbilt. He thinks he's hell, don't he?"

And later:

> [018-045] "I hear Mr. Vanderbilt's getting jealous," he said.

In these exchanges, "Mr. Vanderbilt" functions as a tool of social regulation — a way of cutting down anyone who rises too visibly above the common level. The irony is that Eugene, the target, is no Vanderbilt at all; the name registers not what he is but what others fear he aspires to be.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Eugene never speaks the nickname himself, nor does the narrative show him directly responding to it. His relationship to "Mr. Vanderbilt" is thus one of silent inhabitation — he lives inside the label others have given him, whether he wishes to or not. The nickname is a cage built of other people's perceptions, and Eugene's only power over it is to outgrow it.

### Eliza Gant

Eliza's relationship to the nickname is the most revealing. Where a less ambitious mother might be stung by the mockery, Eliza hears in "Mr. Vanderbilt" a challenge to her own social aspirations. Her response — "They'd just see" — makes explicit what the nickname implies: the Gants are not content with their place and will not be confined by the town's leveling humor.

### Harry Tugman and the Adolescent Circle

Tugman serves as the nickname's principal transmitter, wielding it with the particular venom of those who police class boundaries from below. His jealousy of Spaugh, his gossip about Eugene — both are expressions of the same impulse: to ensure that no one rises too far above the common level of Altamont's young men.
