## Synopsis

Miss Crane is a boarder at Dixieland during the summer of 1914 — the summer the First World War begins. A retired schoolteacher who spent thirty years teaching English in the New York City public school system, she becomes, improbably, the adolescent Eugene Gant's "closest friend at the time" [025-002]. Their friendship is forged in shared moral outrage: together they follow the newspaper accounts of the escalating European conflict, and Miss Crane's "thin red nostrils quivered with indignation" at each new report of German aggression [025-002]. She represents one of the novel's fleeting but vivid intergenerational friendships — a sharp-tongued old woman whose pedagogical instinct finds an outlet in the passionate, bookish boy who rushes to bring her the latest war news.

## Biography

Miss Crane arrives at Dixieland in the summer of 1914, when the boarding house is at its seasonal peak. She is described succinctly and memorably:

> [025-002] His closest friend at the time was a sharp old spinstress with frayed nerves, who had been for thirty years a teacher of English in a New York City public school.

Her defining trait is moral intensity. When news arrives of the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand — "the murder of the Grand Duke" — Miss Crane's reaction is physical and immediate: "Miss Crane's thin red nostrils quivered with indignation. Her old gray eyes were sharp with anger. The idea! The idea!" [025-002]. The repeated exclamation captures the rhythms of an elderly schoolteacher's moral vocabulary — a world organized into right and wrong, the correct and the outrageous.

Eugene's relationship with her is performative in a specifically adolescent way:

> [025-004] With Miss Crane he kept a face of mournful regret, but his heart drummed a martial tattoo against his ribs. The air was full of fifes and flutes; he heard the ghostly throbbing of great guns.

He presents to her the somber face she expects, while secretly thrilling to the romance of war. This doubleness — the public mourning and the private excitement — is characteristic of Eugene's wartime experience, and Miss Crane, as the audience for his performance of solemnity, is essential to the dynamic.

Through the late summer, as the German advance toward Paris confounds the optimistic newspaper accounts, Eugene "rushed to share" each new development "with the Leonards or Miss Crane" [025-012], positioning her among his trusted circle of adult confidants.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Miss Crane functions as Eugene's moral audience during the summer of 1914. He performs solemnity for her approval while privately nursing a boy's excitement about war. She seems unaware of his divided feelings, or perhaps chooses not to notice — her pedagogical habit of taking students at their presented face serving her well with an adolescent whose inner life is increasingly turbulent.

### Margaret Leonard

Miss Crane is briefly grouped with Margaret Leonard and her husband as one of Eugene's favored adult companions during the war summer: "he rushed to share it with the Leonards or Miss Crane" [025-012]. This places her, however fleetingly, in the company of Eugene's most significant mentors.
