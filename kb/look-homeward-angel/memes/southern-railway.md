# Southern Railway

## Synopsis

The railway is the novel's primary technology of passage — the iron thread that binds Altamont to the world beyond the hills. Oliver Gant arrives by train and coach; the Gant children travel to St. Louis, to California, to Baltimore; and Eugene's final departure at the novel's close is a train journey into an unknown future. The railway is never named as a corporate entity in the novel (Southern Railway is a reasonable historical attribution for western North Carolina in the early twentieth century), but its presence is pervasive: in the "musty cotton, baled and piled under long sheds of railway sidings" (007-053), in the Pullman porter's solicitous attention (005-063), and in the recurring image of a train crawling "dizzily across high trestles spanning ghostly hawsers of water" (001-021).

## Oliver's Arrival

The novel's first journey is Oliver Gant's train ride into the mountains. The railway brings him from the flatlands into a landscape of fog and hillside shacks, a passage from the known world into the enveloping strangeness of Altamont:

> (001-020) The train rattled on over the reeking earth. Rain fell steadily. A brakeman came draftily into the dirty plush coach and emptied a scuttle of coal into the big stove at the end. High empty laughter shook a group of yokels sprawled on two turned seats.

The journey continues through dusk into darkness:

> (001-021) Dusk came. The huge bulk of the hills was foggily emergent. Small smoky lights went up in the hillside shacks. The train crawled dizzily across high trestles spanning ghostly hawsers of water. Far up, far down, plumed with wisps of smoke, toy cabins stuck to bank and gulch and hillside.

This passage establishes the railway as a liminal space — a zone between worlds, where the familiar recedes and the unknown rises. Oliver completes the journey by coach:

> (001-022) The next morning he resumed his journey by coach. His destination was the little town of Altamont, twenty-four miles away beyond the rim of the great outer wall of the hills.

## The Railway as Infrastructure

Throughout the novel, the railway appears as a taken-for-granted infrastructure — the medium through which the characters' lives move. It is present in the cotton-laden sidings of the South:

> (007-053) Yes, musty cotton, baled and piled under long sheds of railway sidings; and odorous pine woodlands of the level South, saturated with brown faery light, and broken by the tall straight leafless poles of trees.

It is present in the Pullman cars that convey the Gant family to distant destinations:

> (005-063) He saw them into the train, disposed comfortably by the solicitous Pullman porter.

And it is present in young Steve Gant's vagabondage — riding freight trains in a prefiguration of the Depression-era hobo:

> (005-010) Once, with a crony, Gus Moody, son of a foundry-man, he had gone off to see the world. Grimy from vagabondage, they had crawled off a freight-train at Knoxville, Tennessee.

## The Railway in Family Life

The railway structures the novel's geography of family. Journeys by train mark the significant events of Gant family life:

| journey | PN | significance |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| Oliver's arrival in Altamont | 001-020 – 001-022 | The founding journey; brings the patriarch into the hills |
| Birth of Luke | 007-003 | Born on "the train westbound to Saint Louis" |
| Gant's last voyage to California | 007-001 | The patriarch's final great journey, by rail |
| Eugene's departure | 040-001 ff | The novel's closing journey, leaving Altamont by train |

## Symbolic Dimensions

The railway is not merely a practical convenience in Wolfe's world; it is a charged symbol operating on multiple registers:

| dimension | expression |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Arrival and departure** | The railway is the mechanism of entry and exit; every major transition in the novel involves a train |
| **Connection and isolation** | The railway connects Altamont to the world, yet the town remains enclosed by hills — the train both bridges and emphasizes the isolation |
| **Modernity and nostalgia** | The railway represents the industrial world encroaching on a pastoral South, yet Wolfe renders it with a nostalgic lyricism |
| **The journey as metaphor** | Train travel is the novel's figure for the passage of time, the movement of consciousness, and the hunger for voyages |
| **Class and comfort** | The Pullman car (005-063) signifies middle-class respectability; the freight train (005-010) signifies vagabondage and rebellion |

## The Scenic Railway

Wolfe extends the railway metaphor into the realm of amusement — the "scenic railway" at the fairgrounds, where young Eugene experiences terror transformed into thrill:

> (005-090) Once Daisy, yielding to the furtive cat-cruelty below her mild placidity, took him with her through the insane horrors of the scenic railway; they plunged bottomlessly from light into roaring blackness.

This miniature railway — a ride, not a transport — condenses the larger railway's meaning: the plunge from light into darkness, the surrender to forces beyond control, the emergence into a changed landscape.
