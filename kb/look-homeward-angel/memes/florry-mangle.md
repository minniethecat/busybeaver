# Miss Florry Mangle

## Synopsis

Miss Florry Mangle is a trained nurse who appears in two chapters of *Look Homeward, Angel* — first as the object of Jake Clapp's stealthy courtship on the porch of Dixieland, and later as the silent attendant at W.O. Gant's deathbed. She is one of Wolfe's minor grotesques: her "limp face," her "tired whine," and her morbid conversation mark her as a figure of decay and diminished vitality. Yet in her silent rocking before the parlor fire — "thirty heel-taps to the minute" — she also provides something the Gant family cannot: a still, undemanding presence that allows death to unfold at its own pace.

## Role in the Novel

### Chapter 31: The Courtship on the Porch

Florry Mangle first appears on the porch of Dixieland at dusk, being courted by Mr. Jake Clapp, a well-to-do widower from Old Hominy:

> Leaning upon the porch rail in the thickening dusk, Mr. Jake Clapp, a well-to-do widower from Old Hominy, pursued his stealthy courtship of Miss Florry Mangle, the trained nurse. Her limp face made a white blot in the darkness; she spoke in a tired whine. [031-055]

The language is precise and damning: "stealthy courtship," "limp face," "white blot," "tired whine." Wolfe gives Florry no vitality, no color, no animation — she is a figure defined by absence, by what has drained out of her. The courtship scene is rendered in a few strokes: Clapp's crude country drawl, his "lewdly decorous" manner, his physical pressing against her:

> "He'll git over it," said Jake Clapp, in a precise country drawl, streaked with a note of bawdry. "Every boy has got to go through the Calf-Love stage." He pressed his hard thigh gently against Florry, grinning widely and thinly with a few gold teeth. [031-059]

Florry's response is characteristically bleak:

> "He'd better watch out," whined Florry sadly. "I know what I'm talking about. That boy's not strong — he has no business to go prowling around to all hours the way he does. He's on the verge of — " [031-060]

The unfinished sentence — "He's on the verge of —" — is one of Wolfe's suggestive gaps, leaving the reader to supply the diagnosis (tuberculosis? nervous collapse? death?). Florry, as a trained nurse, traffics in such prognoses; her professional identity is bound up with the body's vulnerabilities, and her conversation circles perpetually around disease and decline.

### Chapter 32: The Deathbed Vigil

Florry reappears in Chapter 32, attending W.O. Gant in his final illness. Her role here is strikingly different from the porch scene — she becomes a figure of almost ritual stillness:

> His father spent most of the day staring vacantly into the parlor fire. Miss Florry Mangle, the nurse, gave him the morbid comfort of her silence: she rocked incessantly before the fire, thirty heel-taps to the minute, with arms tight-folded on her limp breasts. Occasionally she talked of death and disease. [032-074]

The phrase "morbid comfort of her silence" is a small masterstroke of paradoxical characterization. Florry's silence, rather than her speech (which circles around "death and disease"), is what provides comfort — her mere presence, rhythmic and undemanding, is a form of nursing that the Gant family cannot otherwise supply. The detail of "thirty heel-taps to the minute" is pure Wolfe: a sensory notation so precise it becomes hypnotic, matching the dying Gant's vacant stare into the fire.

The passage is immediately followed by Eugene's perception of his father's transformation:

> Gant had aged and wasted shockingly. His heavy clothes wound round his feeble shanks: his face was waxen and transparent — it was like a great beak. He looked clean and fragile. The cancer, Eugene thought, flowered in him like some terrible but beautiful plant. [032-074]

Florry Mangle, rocking and silent, is the unobtrusive witness to this flowering — her presence enabling Eugene's own moments of perception and grief.

## Symbolic Function

Florry Mangle belongs to the novel's extensive catalogue of figures associated with illness, death, and bodily decay — a thematic cluster that includes Dr. McGuire, the various deathbed scenes, and the omnipresent specter of tuberculosis. As a trained nurse, she occupies a liminal position between the professional and the domestic, the clinical and the intimate. Her courtship with Jake Clapp is a miniature of Wolfe's larger vision of love in Altamont: furtive, physical, and shadowed by mortality.

Her silence at Gant's deathbed offers a counterpoint to the novel's dominant mode of rhetorical excess. Where Eugene, Eliza, and the other Gants fill the air with speech — complaint, narration, brooding — Florry simply rocks, taps her heel, and lets death advance at its own pace. In this, she is perhaps the most effective nurse Gant could have: one who does not try to arrest what cannot be arrested.

## Narrative Context

Chapters 31 and 32 form one of the novel's great emotional arcs — the decline and death of W.O. Gant, interwoven with Eugene's own romantic turmoil. Florry Mangle's two appearances frame this arc: the porch courtship introduces her as a figure of diminished vitality, while the deathbed scene reveals her unexpected value as a silent attendant. Her presence is a reminder that the most important comfort at the end of life may come from strangers — people whose professional distance is, paradoxically, what allows them to stay.
