# The Father

## Definition

The father in *Look Homeward, Angel* is a figure of titanic proportions. Oliver Gant dominates the novel not despite his flaws but because of them: his rages are epic, his hungers are world-historical, his tenderness is buried so deep it emerges only in glimpses. For Eugene, the father is the primary mystery — the source of the hunger that will become his vocation as a writer, and the source of the pain that will fuel his work.

Wolfe's treatment of the father transcends realism. Oliver Gant is a mythic figure — "Gant the Far-Wanderer," the stonecutter who rages against God and the angel he cannot carve. He is at once the novel's greatest character and its greatest absence: dead by the novel's end, he becomes the ghost who haunts Eugene's imagination.

## Textual Evidence

Oliver's hands — enormous, capable of carving stone and of violence — are described as instruments of creation and destruction:

> (002-005) The strange figure of Oliver Gant cast its famous shadow through the town. Men heard at night and morning the great formula of his curse to Eliza. They saw him plunge to house and shop, they saw him bent above his marbles, they saw him mould in his great hands — with curse, and howl, with passionate devotion — the rich textures of the stone.

His love for the angel — kept secret, hidden beneath public curses — reveals the tenderness he cannot express:

> (019-064) No one knew how fond he was of the angel. Publicly he called it his White Elephant. He cursed it and said he had been a fool to order it.

The absence the angel would leave — the "barren crater in his heart" — is the absence the father will leave:

> (019-070) He was silent, thinking for a moment of the place where the angel stood. He knew he had nothing to cover or obliterate that place — it left a barren crater in his heart.

After his father's death, Eugene sees his name fading on the shop wall — the father becoming ghost:

> (040-004) Eugene saw his father's name, faded, on the old brick in moonlight. On the stone porch of the shop, the angels held their marble posture. They seemed to have frozen, in the moonlight.

Eugene's identification with his father's curse — "O lost!" as the cry both men share:

> (001-090) And like a man who is perishing in the polar night, he thought of the rich meadows of his youth: the corn, the plum tree, and ripe grain. Why here? O lost!

The father's legacy at its most explicit — Ben telling Eugene he is "the last hope":

> (030-263) "You're the last hope, 'Gene. Go on and finish up, if you have to steal the money. The rest of us are done for."

## Analysis

The father in Wolfe's novel operates on two levels: as the specific, overwhelming person of Oliver Gant, and as the archetypal figure of the lost father, the absent god, the source toward which the son must look. "Look Homeward, Angel" is, in one sense, Eugene's command to himself: to look back at the father, to understand him, to complete his unfinished work.

Oliver's inability to carve the angel's head becomes Eugene's mandate: the son must finish what the father started. But Eugene's medium is language, not stone — the novel is the angel's head that Oliver could not carve. This is the deepest inheritance: art as filial duty, creation as the completion of the father's failed quest.
