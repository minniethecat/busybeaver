## Synopsis

Grover Cleveland Gant is the most elegiac presence in the Gant family — a child whose brief life and early death cast a shadow across the entire novel. Born in 1892 as one of twins (with Ben), named by Oliver Gant "with a zest for politics" after Grover Cleveland, he is described by Wolfe as "the gentlest and saddest of the boys." His death from typhoid fever during the family's trip to the St. Louis World's Fair is the novel's first great tragedy, a wound from which Eliza never fully recovers. Grover haunts the margins of the narrative — his curls preserved in a tiny box, his name invoked at moments of grief, his absence a permanent feature of the family's emotional landscape.

## Biography

Grover Gant is born in 1892, one of twin boys:

> [002-007] Then, in 1892, came twins — boys — to whom Gant, always with a zest for politics, gave the names of Grover Cleveland and Benjamin Harrison.

As a child, he is part of the rough-and-tumble life of the Gant household, joining his brothers in the rituals of boyhood — prowling the kitchen for food, strung along the fence wires with neighborhood children, sent out to seek employment at the Fair Grounds. Yet even among the boisterous Gant boys, Grover stands apart:

> [005-086] This was Grover — the gentlest and saddest of the boys.

The family's trip to St. Louis becomes the setting for disaster. Grover falls ill with typhoid:

> [005-094] Grover was down with typhoid.

His death — rendered with Wolfe's characteristic indirection, through the voice of a stranger asking "Do you want to see Grover?" — is the novel's first irreparable loss:

> [006-001] The death of Grover gave Eliza the most terrible wound of her life: her courage was snapped, her slow but powerful adventure toward freedom was abruptly stopped.

Grover's memory persists through the novel. Eliza keeps his curls in a tiny box (008-039). At moments of crisis — Ben's death, Gant's final illness — she returns to the language of that earlier grief: "Child, if I had known," she weeps, "as she had wept long before at Grover's death" (035-093). Even in the novel's closing pages, Eugene remembers the lost brother he barely knew:

> [040-096] "I remember little things," said Eugene. "I remember the fly I swallowed on the peach, and the little boys on tricycles at Saint Louis..."
