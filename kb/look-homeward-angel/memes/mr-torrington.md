# Mr. Torrington

## Synopsis

Mr. Torrington is an English instructor at Pulpit Hill — the state university Eugene attends — and one of the novel's most memorable satirical portraits of academic condescension. A Rhodes Scholar (Pulpit Hill and Merton, '14), he represents everything that is genteel, effete, and intellectually timid in the academy. Wolfe renders him with devastating precision: his lisp, his "moist" smile, his weaponized politeness. In his brief but vivid appearances, Torrington becomes a foil for Eugene's raw, voracious literary appetite — the voice of sanctioned taste dismissing the writers Eugene loves as "flashy," "immature," and "not in line with the best modern thought."

## Role in the Novel

### The Rhodes Scholar (Chapter 28)

Torrington is introduced with a parenthetical that says everything: "the old Rhodes Scholar (Pulpit Hill and Merton, '14)" (028-042). The "old" is Wolfe's irony — Torrington is young, but his manner is prematurely aged, invested with the institutional weight of Oxford and its certainties. His pedagogical method is revealed in his first line of dialogue:

> "Observe," lisped Mr. Torrington, the old Rhodes Scholar (Pulpit Hill and Merton, '14), "observe how skilfully he holds suspense until the very end. Observe with what consummate art he builds up his climax, keeping his meaning hidden until the very last word." [028-042]

The repetition of "observe" — the imperative of the connoisseur — establishes Torrington's role immediately: he is not a teacher but an appreciator, a curator of taste. His verb is not "think" or "feel" but "observe," the detached gaze of the gentleman-scholar. And his lisp — that small, damning detail — feminizes his authority, makes it faintly ridiculous even as it asserts itself.

Wolfe then editorializes: "All that, Mr. Torrington would have said, was temporary and accidental. It was unsound" (028-045). The word "unsound" is Torrington's ultimate judgment — not wrong, not false, but *unsound*, as though literature were a building whose structural integrity could be assessed by a certified inspector.

### The Moist Smile

Wolfe's physical description of Torrington is characteristically lethal:

> Mr. Torrington smiled moistly at Eugene and urged him tenderly into a chair drawn intimately to his desk. [028-046]

The adverb "moistly" is pure Wolfean disgust — a smile that is wet, unctuous, faintly repellent. The chair "drawn intimately to his desk" suggests a violation of professional distance, a faux-intimacy that is really a form of control. Torrington's tenderness is a weapon.

### The Verdict

When Eugene expresses admiration for a writer Torrington considers beneath notice, the response is a masterclass in academic condescension:

> "Oh, but really! My dear boy!" said Mr. Torrington with gentle amazement. He shrugged his shoulders and became politely indifferent. [028-061]

The "My dear boy" is devastating — it reduces Eugene to a child, his enthusiasm to naivety. The shrug and the "politely indifferent" retreat are the final gestures of a man who has judged and found wanting, who need not argue because his taste is its own authority.

Torrington's critique, when elaborated, is a catalogue of genteel dismissals:

> The appeal of a man like that was mainly to the unformed taste, the uncritical judgment. He had a flashy attraction for the immature. Oh, yes! Undoubtedly an amusing fellow. Clever — yes, but hardly significant. And — didn't he think — a trifle noisy? Or had he noticed that? Yes — there was to be sure an amusing Celtic strain, not without charm, but unsound. He was not in line with the best modern thought. [028-061]

The litany of qualified concessions — "undoubtedly an amusing fellow," "clever — yes, but hardly significant," "an amusing Celtic strain, not without charm" — is the rhetoric of someone who has learned to praise in order to bury. Each compliment is a shovel of earth. The final verdict — "not in line with the best modern thought" — reveals Torrington's ultimate allegiance: not to literature but to fashion, to the sanctioned consensus of the academy.

### The Challenge to Authority

Eugene's reaction to Torrington's dismissal is one of the novel's quiet acts of intellectual defiance. He does not argue — he simply refuses to be persuaded. Torrington's authority, which rests entirely on his Oxford pedigree and his command of the rhetoric of taste, crumbles before Eugene's untheorized but passionate conviction that the books he loves are great.

The episode ends with a brief mention in a later chapter: "I thought you had Torrington," says Jim Trivett (029-029) — a throwaway line that reduces the Rhodes Scholar to a scheduling convenience, a name on a timetable. It is the novel's final, quiet revenge on a man who mistook his taste for truth.

## Thematic Significance

Torrington is one of several figures in *Look Homeward, Angel* who represent the forces that threaten to domesticate Eugene's genius — the schoolteachers, the academics, the gatekeepers of taste who would teach him to "observe" rather than to feel, to defer to authority rather than to trust his own hungers. His moist smile and lisping certainties are the face of a culture that values correctness over passion, and his defeat — quiet, unstated, but complete — is Eugene's small victory for the untutored heart.
