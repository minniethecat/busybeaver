## Synopsis

John Dorsey Leonard is the principal of the private school that Eugene Gant attends during his formative years in Altamont. A tall, white-handed man given to absently stroking students' backs while gazing into the middle distance, Leonard is rendered by Wolfe as a figure of mild absurdity — well-meaning but ineffectual, his formal instruction in Latin prose a "dry campaign over an arid waste." He exists in the novel primarily as a contrast to his wife, Margaret Leonard, whose passionate teaching of literature unlocks Eugene's imaginative life. Where Margaret gives Eugene a world, Leonard gives him only declensions.

## Biography

Leonard first appears as the head of the small private school where Eugene's parents, after considerable persuasion, agree to send him. The principal's methods of persuasion are comically ineffectual — he mentions "the sons of several fashionable and wealthy people" to the Gants, hoping social ambition will succeed where educational arguments fail:

> [016-028] Leonard mentioned the sons of several fashionable and wealthy people — of Dr. Kitchen, the eye, ear, nose and throat man, Mr. Arthur, the corporation lawyer — the subtle implication being that one's children would rub elbows there with the children of the rich and the socially distinguished.

His physical mannerisms — the white hand stroking students' backs, the abstracted gaze, the chalk-dust prints left on clothing — become a recurring motif:

> [016-035] Mr. Leonard placed his white dry hand upon Eugene's shoulders, affectionately sliding it down his back and across his kidneys, leaving white chalk prints on his clothing.

As a teacher, Leonard is competent but uninspired. His Latin instruction, two years spent on Cicero's "De Senectute" and "De Amicitia," provides Eugene with grammatical discipline but no spark:

> [017-002] From Leonard he got little — a dry campaign over an arid waste of Latin prose: first, a harsh, stiff, unintelligent skirmishing among the rules of grammar, with its prisoned, and yet vital and mysterious, symbols.

His limitations as a classicist are revealed when students ask about Virgil — Leonard reluctantly admits it requires "a different sort of Latin":

> [017-008] "Why, no," said Mr. Leonard, stroking his chin. "A different sort of Latin."
