## Synopsis

Harry Tugman is a pressman at the Altamont newspaper where Ben Gant works, and a regular at the early-morning drugstore gathering of the town's professional men. With his "powerful ink-stained lungs" and heavy-handed camaraderie, Tugman embodies the rough, masculine world of the newspaper trade — a world of press-runs, crude jokes, and provincial self-importance. He is both audience to and participant in the drugstore's running theater of wit, his heavy laughter and servile respect for doctors and authority figures marking him as a man of limited imagination but genuine good nature.

## Biography

Tugman first appears at the end of a press run, lighting a cigarette with the satisfaction of work completed:

> [014-030] Harry Tugman lit a Camel, drawing the smoke deep into his powerful ink-stained lungs as he watched the press run down.

At the drugstore counter, Tugman's interactions reveal his character — servile toward Dr. McGuire ("Doctor, what do you charge to operate?"), jealous of the news he might miss, and quick to laugh at jokes he does not fully understand:

> [014-065] "My God!" said Harry Tugman, thinking jealously of lost news. "When did she go?"

> [014-067] "God, I'm sorry to hear that," said Harry Tugman, greatly relieved.

He serves as a foil to Ben Gant's sharper, more bitter wit. While Ben's humor cuts, Tugman's is broad and physical — slapping backs, declaiming parodies, laughing heavily. His presence helps define the texture of the drugstore scenes: a man who belongs to this world completely, without Ben's restless intelligence or McGuire's drunken genius.

Tugman reappears briefly after Ben's death, one of the familiar faces at the drugstore who offers condolences:

> [036-118] "Luke," said Harry Tugman, looking up from his paper, "I was certainly sorry to hear about Ben."
