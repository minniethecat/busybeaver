## Synopsis

Vergil Weldon is one of the few adults in Eugene Gant's youth who recognizes his intellectual capacity without seeking to exploit or constrain it. An elderly teacher at the private academy, Weldon appears in a pair of scenes near the end of the novel that radiate a quiet, unhurried wisdom rare in the Gant universe. In the classroom, he teaches with gentle irony — his students parody his Harvard mannerisms ("That phrase, used now for thirty years, relic of Yale and Harvard: Royce and Munsterberg") even as they absorb his lessons. Outside the classroom, in a sun-dappled conversation beneath the great trees of his yard, he advises Oliver Gant that Eugene should attend the state university — not a prestigious Eastern college — because "a mind like yours must not be pulled green." The advice is selfless, measured, and exactly right, and it marks Weldon as one of the novel's few truly benevolent authority figures.

## Biography

Vergil Weldon's classroom is the setting for his first appearance. The scene is a discussion of martyrdom — whether one would "tell the truth" if threatened with burning — and Weldon's response, delivered "through moistening spectacles," mixes moral seriousness with theatrical self-awareness:

> [038-072] "Ah. I'd have let them burn," said the martyred Rountree through moistening spectacles.

The narrative voice notes his pedagogical pedigree: "Royce and Munsterberg" — the Harvard philosophers — are his intellectual masters, and his mannerisms ("used now for thirty years") are worn smooth with repetition. Yet the boys, even as they parody him, are learning.

Weldon's most significant scene takes place outside the classroom. Oliver Gant, risen from his "charnel-house of death for three days," has come to see his son Eugene at school. In a conversation under the great trees of Weldon's yard, over iced tea, the old teacher delivers his judgment:

> [039-024] "Yes," said Vergil Weldon approvingly. "It's the place for you, Mr. Gant. It doesn't matter about the others. They're ready now. But a mind like yours must not be pulled green. You must give it a chance to ripen."

The horticultural metaphor — "must not be pulled green" — is Weldon's own, and it captures his pedagogical philosophy in a phrase. Eugene needs time, not pressure; the state university, not the Ivy League; slow ripening, not forced precocity. It is advice that Eugene, in his rush toward experience, will not fully absorb — but the novel registers Weldon's wisdom as genuine.

Eugene's departure from Altamont, weeks later, is rendered in Weldon's terms:

> [039-032] Then one hot morning, on sudden impulse, he left. As the car that was taking him to Exeter roared down the winding street, under the hot green leafiness of June, he heard, as from the sea-depth of a dream, the mild affected voice of Vergil Weldon saying: "Mr. Gant, Mr. Gant, what a place you'd make for yourself in the world if you only had a heart."
