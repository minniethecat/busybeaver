## Synopsis

Mrs. Morgan is one of the lodgers at Dixieland, Eliza Gant's boarding house, and her brief appearance crystallizes the quiet drama of economic precarity that hums beneath the surface of the Gant household. A "railroad man's" wife from Sevier, she rocks ceaselessly in her chair as the autumn storms pass — a rhythm Eugene can measure by the sound of "her large feet as rhythmically they hit the floor." Her "tar-black eyes" glitter in the firelight, and her responses to Eliza's probing questions are sullen monosyllables. The scene between the two women — Eliza pressing for rent, Mrs. Morgan deflecting — is a small masterpiece of social tension rendered in the language of boarding-house domesticity. Eugene, observing, performs his "profound bow" and "most florid courtesy" to the lodger, his adolescent gallantry a gentle counterpoint to his mother's businesslike directness.

## Biography

Mrs. Morgan enters the novel in a single extended passage in chapter 17, during the Dixieland years. The boarding house is full of transients and semi-permanent lodgers, and Mrs. Morgan is one of the latter — a woman whose husband works on the railroad and whose presence in the house is defined by the ceaseless rhythm of her rocking:

> [017-128] Day after day the girl rocked back and forth, all through the stormy autumn. Eugene could hear her large feet as rhythmically they hit the floor, ceaselessly propelling the rocker. Her name was Mrs. Morgan.

Eliza enters the room to tend the fire, and the ensuing dialogue captures the delicate balance of power between landlady and lodger. Eliza's questions — where her husband is, whether he sends money — are delivered with the pursed-lip practicality that defines her character. Mrs. Morgan's answers are minimal, her rocking uninterrupted:

> [017-131] "Yes'm," said Mrs. Morgan sullenly. She kept on rocking.

> [017-134] "In Sevier," Mrs. Morgan said. "He's a railroad man."

The scene reaches its climax when Eliza, having established the facts of Mrs. Morgan's situation, peels off "two tens" and hands them to the woman:

> [017-147] "Well, I reckon you'll have to have a little money," said Eliza, peeling off two tens, and giving them to Mrs. Morgan.

The transaction is characteristic of Eliza — businesslike, unsentimental, yet ultimately generous. She takes Mrs. Morgan's measure, extracts what information she can, and then provides what is needed. Mrs. Morgan's response is the same monosyllable — "Thank you, ma'am" — but the money passes between them, and the rocking continues.
