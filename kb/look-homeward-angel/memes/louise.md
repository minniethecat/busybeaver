## Synopsis

Louise is a young waitress at the Charleston hotel where Eugene Gant stays during his first trip away from home — a school tour organized by Mrs. Bowden. Sixteen but claiming to be older, she enters Eugene's room in the quiet early morning and the encounter that follows is one of Wolfe's most delicately rendered scenes of adolescent sexuality. Neither predatory nor prurient, it captures the trembling threshold between childhood and experience: Eugene's earnest, awkward gallantry ("Yes, you have a pretty build, Louise — a fine build — the kind I like"), Louise's mixture of forwardness and vulnerability, the shared pretense of sophistication that barely conceals their mutual innocence. The chapter that follows registers Eugene's guilt and confusion — he is "haunted by his own lost ghost" — but the scene itself remains one of the novel's most tender intimations of the adult world waiting beyond the hills of Altamont.

## Biography

Louise appears in the Charleston sequence of chapter 26, during the school tour organized by Malvin Bowden's mother. She is introduced as one of the party — "two girls named Josie and Louise" — but it is as a hotel waitress that she comes into her own as a character.

On the morning after their arrival, Louise comes to wake Eugene. She has wanted to sleep too; she knocks gently, enters quietly. The room is "hot and stuffy" and Eugene's is "a nice big room" — the small details that become the vocabulary of their negotiation:

> [026-045] "We're all alone up here," said Louise smiling. "We've got the whole floor to ourselves."

What follows is a scene of extraordinary delicacy. Eugene, "going on sixteen," asks Louise how old she is; she deflects. They talk about build — his height, her smallness — in the coded language of adolescent attraction. He asks if her garters hurt; he stares "with keen interest" at the unfamiliar mechanics of women's clothing. Wolfe renders it all without judgment, letting the awkwardness and tenderness coexist:

> [026-065] "What do you like best, 'Gene?" Louise asked.

The encounter is brief, and Louise fades from the narrative almost immediately. But its emotional resonance persists. In the days that follow, Eugene avoids everyone, "published by every gesture, by every word" of his "sin." The "lost ghost" that haunts him is not Louise herself but the childhood self he has left behind in that hotel room — a loss that the novel treats not as moral failing but as the necessary, painful passage into adulthood.
