# Mrs. Selborne

## Synopsis

Mrs. Selborne is one of the most vividly sensual figures among the Dixieland boarders in *Look Homeward, Angel*. A South Carolinian of faded gentility who has fled the "quiet slander of a South Carolina mill-town" after multiple adulteries, she arrives at Eliza Gant's boarding-house young but worldly, bringing with her an atmosphere of lush indolence and veiled eroticism. Over the course of the novel she becomes, by turns, a magnetic presence to Helen, a lover to Luke, and an object of burning, frustrated desire for the adolescent Eugene. Wolfe renders her in language saturated with ripeness — her "heavy-bodied" blonde beauty, her "rich seductive laughter," her slow and luxuriant physicality — making her a kind of embodied principle of sensuality against which the Gant children measure their own hungers.

## Role in the Novel

### Arrival at Dixieland: The Southern Siren (Chapter 12)

Mrs. Selborne is introduced in a long, luxuriant sentence that establishes her as one of Wolfe's most fully imagined minor characters. She arrives at Dixieland at twenty-three, though "she looked older," and Wolfe's description is a catalogue of ripe physicality:

> When Mrs. Selborne first came to Dixieland from the hot South she was only twenty-three but she looked older. Ripeness with her was all: she was a tall heavy-bodied blonde, well kept and elegant. She moved leisurely with a luxurious sensual swing of the body: her smile was tender and full of vague allurement, her voice gentle, her sudden laughter, bubbling out of midnight secrecy, rich and full. [012-045]

The sentence continues into an account of her past — one of "several handsome and bacchic daughters of a depleted South Carolinian of good family," married at sixteen to "a red heavy man" who runs a livery stable, mother of two girls, and an adulteress who has "moved with wasted stealth around all the quiet slander of a South Carolina mill-town, committing adultery carefully with a mill owner, a banker, and a lumber man" (012-045). The portrait is remarkably frank for its era — Wolfe names her transgressions directly but does not condemn her; instead, he places her within a social ecosystem where "the men in particular, treated her with even more elaborate respect than a woman is usually given in a Southern town, but their eyes, behind the courteous unctions of their masks, were shiny with invitation" (012-045).

Her financial arrangements are equally candid: she receives "a small check from her husband every week, and a substantial one from the lumber man" (012-047), and her ease of living — the "wastefulness" that so attracts Helen — is underwritten by this economy of desire.

### Helen's Fascination

Helen Gant is immediately drawn to Mrs. Selborne's warmth and glamour:

> She brought a negro girl with her: she was lavish in her dispensations to the negress, and to her children: this wastefulness, ease of living, and her rich seductive laughter fascinated Helen, drew her to the older woman. [012-047]

This attraction persists throughout the novel, with Helen visiting Mrs. Selborne in South Carolina during the winters. Their friendship is one of the few sustained female relationships in the novel, and it reveals Helen's hunger for a world beyond the Gant household — a world of comfort, sensuality, and unapologetic self-indulgence.

### Luke's Devotion: The Lover (Chapter 18)

Mrs. Selborne's most significant narrative function is as the object of Luke Gant's romantic devotion. Wolfe renders Luke's infatuation with a mixture of comedy and pathos:

> He had a grand romantic feeling for Delia Selborne, the oldest daughter of Mrs. Selborne. He bought her presents, was tender and irritable by turns. [018-171]

But it is Mrs. Selborne herself who commands Luke's deepest service. Eugene observes this with jealous torment:

> Enviously, with gnawn heart, he observed Luke's ministrations to Mrs. Selborne. His service was so devout, so extravagant that even Helen grew annoyed and occasionally jealous. [018-172]

The word "ministrations" captures the quasi-religious quality of Luke's devotion. And at night, Eugene hears the evidence of Luke's success:

> And nightly, from a remote corner at Gant's or Eliza's, or from a parked automobile before the house, he heard her rich welling laughter, full of tenderness, surrender, and mystery. [018-172]

The triplet — "tenderness, surrender, and mystery" — compresses the novel's entire attitude toward feminine sexuality into three nouns. Mrs. Selborne's laughter is never merely sound; it is always revelation and concealment at once.

### Eugene's Torment: The Adolescent Gaze

For Eugene, Mrs. Selborne is the supreme object of adolescent fantasy — desirable, unattainable, and doubly tormenting because she is possessed by his brother. His desire for her is linked to her daughter Delia, in whom "her image lived again":

> He wanted the girl for himself: she was stupid, but she had the wise body and faint hovering smile of her mother. He wanted Mrs. Selborne more, he fantasied passionately about her yet, but her image lived again in Delia. [018-171]

The admission is brutally honest: Eugene's attraction to Delia is entirely derivative of his desire for her mother, and his subsequent behavior — "proud, cold, scornful and foolish before them" — is the self-protective armor of an adolescent who cannot have what he wants.

The most intense moment between Eugene and Mrs. Selborne comes in Chapter 31, in a brief physical encounter on a dark stairway:

> But on that dark stair, his footfalls numbed in the heavy carpet, he came squarely upon a woman's body that, by its fragrance, like magnolia, he knew was that of Mrs. Selborne. They held each other sharply by the arms, discovered, with caught breath. She bent toward him: a few strands of her blonde hair brushed his face, leaving it aflame. [031-160]

The magnolia fragrance — the traditional flower of the American South — identifies her even in darkness. The encounter is brief, wordless, and entirely physical; it leaves Eugene "aflame," and the passage is typical of Wolfe's ability to charge a momentary touch with enormous erotic voltage without ever becoming explicit.

### Later Appearances

Mrs. Selborne reappears throughout the novel in smaller but telling moments. In Chapter 21, her South Carolina cook, Annie, becomes a source of drama at Dixieland, with Helen fretting that "she'll tell Mrs. Selborne all about it, as soon as she goes back to Henderson" (021-066) — a reminder that Mrs. Selborne's social power extends beyond her physical presence.

She appears at Hugh Barton and Helen's wedding, "a smile of subtle mystery" on her face (027-164), and in Chapter 27 Helen visits her in Henderson, "having practically all my meals there" (027-082), a testament to their enduring bond.

In Chapter 31, she is glimpsed on the Dixieland porch "in the swing with a wealthy lumber man from Tennessee," her "low rich laughter bubbling up softly from the vat of the dark" (031-091) — the final image of her, still the siren, still the object of male attention, still laughing that rich, secretive laugh.

## Relationship to the Gant Family

Mrs. Selborne functions as a kind of litmus test for the Gant siblings. Helen is drawn to her warmth and ease; Luke is conquered by her sensuality; Eugene is consumed by frustrated desire. In a novel saturated with the Gants' turbulent emotional life, Mrs. Selborne represents an alternative mode of being — sensual, unambitious, socially graceful, and morally untroubled. She is what the Gants, with their intensities and hungers, can never be.
