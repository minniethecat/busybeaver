## Synopsis

Tom Grant is a briefly but vividly drawn figure from Eugene's late adolescence — a "thickly built boy, gaudily dressed" with "a low forehead, black hair, and a kind, stupid, indolent temper." He appears in a single extended scene, sitting with Eugene and Jim Trivett in a bar or restaurant, where his rough, affectionate humor serves as a foil to Trivett's more predatory charisma. It is Tom Grant who jokes about "Hard Boy" taking Eugene to Exeter — the code word for the brothel — and Tom Grant who, with "shaken hard fat laughter," protests that Trivett is "leading the boy astray." His presence in the novel is brief but characteristic of Wolfe's gift for rendering even the most minor characters with fullness and specificity.

## Biography

Tom Grant appears in a single chapter — 29 — during the period when Eugene, now older, has begun to move in the rougher company of Altamont's young workingmen. Jim Trivett, the pressman, is the dominant figure in this scene, but Tom Grant provides a kind of comic chorus, his slow, indolent presence a counterweight to Trivett's sharper energies.

His greeting to Eugene is warm and inclusive — "Pull up a chair, son, and sit down" — and his physical description is characteristically precise:

> [029-023] He was a thickly built boy, gaudily dressed; he had a low forehead, black hair, and a kind, stupid, indolent temper.

When Eugene begins eating with the ravenous hunger that marks so many of Wolfe's alimentary passages, it is Tom Grant who reacts with comic alarm:

> [029-026] "God!" said Tom Grant, turning slowly to look at him. "Boy, you're going to choke to death on one of those some day."

Tom Grant's function in the scene is to voice the reference to "Exeter" — the boys' code for the brothel on Eagle Crescent — with a wink and a nudge:

> [029-035] "When are you going to let Hard Boy take you to Exeter?" said Tom Grant, winking at Jim Trivett.

Yet when the joking turns toward actual corruption, Tom Grant steps back, his "hard fat laughter" registering a genuine, if mild, moral qualm:

> [029-049] "Pshaw!" said Tom Grant. "You oughtn't to do that, Hard Boy. You're leading the boy astray."

The protest is token — it changes nothing — but it marks Tom Grant as something other than merely a roué in training. He is, finally, a decent dull boy whose introduction to Eugene is a minor step in the latter's journey toward the larger world.
