# Miss Groody

## Synopsis

Miss Groody is one of the minor figures from Eugene Gant's early childhood in *Look Homeward, Angel*, remembered in a single paragraph of Chapter 8 as his elementary school teacher. A "gaunt red-faced spinster, with fierce glaring eyes" (008-052), she belongs to the gallery of vividly sketched adults who populate Eugene's school world. Though she has no dialogue and appears only through Eugene's retrospective narration, her presence is marked by the characteristic Wolfe blend of childhood memory and heightened sensory detail — Eugene sees her through a lens of childish fear and comic exaggeration, associating her with fairy-tale terrors: "the soldier and the tinder and the dogs he had to pass, with eyes like saucers, windmills, the moon."

## Role in the Novel

### Chapter 8: The Schoolroom Mischief

Miss Groody appears in a single passage of Chapter 8, embedded in Wolfe's account of Eugene and his friend Otto's schoolboy misbehavior:

> In their furtive moments at school, he and Otto amused each other by drawing obscenities in their geographies, bestowing on the representations of tropical natives sagging breasts and huge organs. And they composed on tiny scraps of paper dirty little rhymes about teachers and principal. Their teacher was a gaunt red-faced spinster, with fierce glaring eyes: Eugene thought always of the soldier and the tinder and the dogs he had to pass, with eyes like saucers, windmills, the moon. Her name was Miss Groody, and Otto, with the idiot vulgarity of little boys, wrote of her: [008-052]
>
> > Old Miss Groody
> > Has Good Toody. [008-052]

The nursery-rhyme vulgarity of Otto's couplet is characteristic of the chapter's unflinching portrait of childhood — Wolfe does not sentimentalize the young Eugene, presenting instead the "idiot vulgarity" of boys discovering the transgressive power of language. The rhyme's infantile wordplay ("Groody" / "Toody") suggests the stage at which obscenity is still a game of sound and suggestion rather than explicit content.

The passage continues with Eugene redirecting his creative energies toward the school principal:

> And Eugene, directing his fire against the principal, a plump, soft, foppish young man whose name was Armstrong, and who wore always a carnation in his coat, which, after whipping an offending boy, he was accustomed to hold delicately between his fingers, sniffing it with sensitive nostrils and lidded eyes, produced in the first rich joy of creation scores of rhymes, all to the discredit of Armstrong, his parentage, and his relations with Miss Groody. [008-053]

The detail of Armstrong sniffing the carnation after administering punishment is characteristically Wolfean — a single sensory image that compresses savagery and refinement, cruelty and aesthetics, into one gesture. Eugene's rhymes, linking Armstrong and Miss Groody in imagined scandal, mark an early flowering of the creative impulse that will later produce his literary ambitions — but here, in its nascent form, it is indistinguishable from malice and mischief.

## Symbolic Function

Miss Groody represents the adult authority figures who populate Eugene's childhood — figures who are fearsome and absurd in equal measure, seen through the distorting lens of a child's imagination. Wolfe's comparison of her to the fairy-tale obstacles of "the soldier and the tinder and the dogs" elevates the mundane reality of a stern schoolteacher into the realm of myth and nightmare. This is the child's world as Wolfe renders it: where ordinary adults acquire the proportions of giants and ogres, and where the creative impulse first expresses itself as rebellion against these looming figures.

The "relations with Miss Groody" that Eugene invents for Armstrong also mark an early instance of a pattern that runs through the novel: the tendency of the Gant family — and of Eugene in particular — to transmute the raw material of other people's lives into narrative, speculation, and art. What begins as a schoolboy's dirty rhyme becomes, in retrospect, a premonition of the novelist's larger enterprise.

## Narrative Context

Chapter 8 of *Look Homeward, Angel* covers Eugene's early school years, moving between scenes of home, street, and classroom. The Miss Groody passage is part of a larger portrait of Eugene's emerging consciousness — his discovery of language, sexuality, and the power of transgression. Wolfe's treatment of childhood in this chapter is notable for its refusal to idealize: Eugene is presented as cruel, curious, creative, and complicit in the "idiot vulgarity" of his peers.
