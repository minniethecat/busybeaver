# Judge Webster Tayloe

## Synopsis

Judge Webster Tayloe appears only twice in *Look Homeward, Angel* — and never as a speaking character — yet his presence in the novel is far larger than his page count suggests. As the "eminent, prosperous, and aristocratic corporation counsel (retired, but occasional consultations)" who presides over the "dewy richness of Lunn's Cove" from his walnut-paneled bedchamber, Judge Tayloe embodies the established social order against which the Gants' striving energy is implicitly measured. He is wealth without effort, status without advertisement, power without visible exercise — everything the Gants, with their noise and hunger and ceaseless motion, are not. Wolfe's two passages about Tayloe function as tonal counterweights: Chapter 14's satiric portrait of aristocratic complacency is balanced by Chapter 30's lyrical vision of the cove as a "rich little Eden," with Tayloe's acres as its quiet center.

## Role in the Novel

### The Aristocrat at Dawn (Chapter 14)

Judge Tayloe's first appearance is one of Wolfe's great set-piece sentences — a bravura performance of the high style turned to satiric purpose:

> Through Birdseye Gap, in the dewy richness of Lunn's Cove, Judge Webster Tayloe, the eminent, prosperous, and aristocratic corporation counsel (retired, but occasional consultations), rose in the rich walnut twilight of his bedchamber, noted approvingly, through the black lenses of the glasses that gave his long, subtle, and contemptuous face its final advantage over the rabble, that one of his country bumpkins was coming from the third pasture with a slopping pail of new milk, another was sharpening a scythe in the young glint of the sun, and another, emulating his more intelligent fellow, the horse, was backing a buggy slowly under the carriage shed. [014-191]

The sentence is a masterpiece of controlled deflation. It opens in the high rhetorical mode — "Through Birdseye Gap, in the dewy richness of Lunn's Cove" — and piles honorific upon honorific: "eminent, prosperous, and aristocratic." But the parenthetical "(retired, but occasional consultations)" introduces the first note of irony, and by the time we reach "the black lenses of the glasses that gave his long, subtle, and contemptuous face its final advantage over the rabble," the satire is fully deployed. The glasses do not correct his vision; they *enhance* his contempt. The word "rabble" is Tayloe's own, the narrator ventriloquizing the Judge's internal monologue.

The workers on his estate are "country bumpkins," and the final image — the hired man "emulating his more intelligent fellow, the horse" — completes the portrait of a man for whom other human beings are barely distinguishable from livestock. Yet the sentence never breaks its own surface; it maintains the decorum of the high style even as it hollows it out from within.

### The Rich Little Eden (Chapter 30)

Judge Tayloe's second appearance is entirely different in register — not satire but pastoral vision:

> At length, they came out above the cove, at a forking of the road. They turned left, to the north, toward the upper and smaller end. To the south, the cove widened out in a rich little Eden of farm and pasture. Small houses dotted the land, there were green meadows and a glint of water. Fields of young green wheat bent rhythmically under the wind; the young corn stood waist-high, with light clashing blades. The chimneys of Rheinhart's house showed above its obscuring grove of maples; the fat dairy cows grazed slowly across the wide pastures. And further below, half tree-and-shrub-hidden, lay the rich acres of Judge Webster Tayloe. [030-280]

The language here is lyrical and unhurried — "rich little Eden," "green meadows and a glint of water," "young green wheat bent rhythmically under the wind." The Judge's acres are "half tree-and-shrub-hidden" — visible but not ostentatious, wealth that does not need to announce itself. The passage places Tayloe within a landscape that the novel clearly loves, and by association, some of the satiric edge of Chapter 14 is softened. Tayloe is arrogant, yes, but he is also *placed* — rooted in a world of wheat and corn and grazing cows that the novel renders with unmistakable affection.

The contrast between the two portrayals captures Wolfe's characteristic doubleness: he can mock the social order and love the land it occupies, often in the same breath.

### The Social Summit

Judge Tayloe's function in the novel is primarily structural. He represents the top of Altamont's social pyramid — a position the Gants can see but never occupy. The Gants are middle-class strivers whose "mad, original, disturbing quality" (006-013) sets them apart from settled society; Tayloe is settled society incarnate. His "long, subtle, and contemptuous face" is the face of a class that does not need to strive because it already possesses.

In this sense, Tayloe is a distant cousin of the Hilliards and the other "elect" from whom the Gants are excluded — but he is even more remote, less a social actor than a feature of the landscape, like Rheinhart's chimneys or the fat dairy cows. He is aristocracy as geography.
