# Alcohol and Escape

## Definition

Alcohol in *Look Homeward, Angel* is a destructive sacrament. Oliver Gant's drinking is legendary — epic benders that shake the house on Woodson Street, that frighten his children, that alternate with periods of sobriety and stone-carving. Drinking is escape from the disappointments of his life: the wife who does not love him as he needs to be loved, the angel he cannot carve, the town that does not appreciate him.

But alcohol is also a form of inheritance. Ben drinks too, a quieter, more bitter drinking — the "opulent solitude" of the bar, the dark angel who weeps for him. Eugene will drink, will wander, will carry the family's hunger into a new generation. Drinking is the Gant family's most reliable way of coping with the "lost" condition.

## Textual Evidence

Oliver's "riotous years" of drinking and work are introduced as what consumes the years between youth and age:

> (001-009) The riotous years in Baltimore, of work and savage drunkenness, and the theatre of Booth and Salvini, which had a disastrous effect upon the stone cutter, who memorized each accent of the noble rant, and strode muttering through the streets.

Eliza catalogs the financial cost of his drinking — money that could have bought property squandered in taverns:

> (003-007) "The money he squanders every year in licker would buy a good lot: we could be well-to-do people now if we'd started at the very beginning."

Gant's rages terrify the household — his great voice "chanting obscenities" carries across the neighborhood:

> (003-035) His great voice below, chanting obscenities, carried across the neighborhood: as she heard the sudden wild roar of flame up the chimney, shaking the house in its flight, she called Steve to her side, tensely: "Son, he'll burn us all up!" she whispered.

The ritual of Eliza feeding him soup amid his drunken tirades is a recurring domestic drama:

> (003-083) "Hush! Hush!" whispered the girl fiercely, coming before him closely with the soup. She thrust a smoking ladle at his mouth, but he turned his head away to hurl another insult. She slapped him sharply across the mouth.

> (003-084) "You drink this!" she whispered. And grinning meekly as his eyes rested upon her, he began to swallow soup.

Ben's quiet, bitter drinking is contrasted with his father's theatrical binges — the "opulent solitude" he craves:

> (020-044) He wanted opulent solitude. His dark vision burned on kingdoms under the sea, on windy castle crags, and on the deep elf kingdoms at the earth's core.

Eliza's matter-of-fact accounting of the costs begins the novel's long ledger of what drink has taken:

> (003-007) "The money he squanders every year in licker would buy a good lot: we could be well-to-do people now if we'd started at the very beginning. But he's always hated the very idea of owning anything: couldn't bear it, he told me once, since he lost his money in that trade in Sydney."

## Analysis

Alcohol functions in the novel as a failed transcendence. Gant drinks to escape the confines of his life — the house that Eliza has turned into property, the angel he cannot carve, the body that will fail him — but drinking only deepens his imprisonment. The pattern is cyclical: bender, remorse, recovery, bender. It is the rhythm of the Gant household itself.

Wolfe does not moralize about drinking (though the language of Hell and damnation surrounds it). Instead, he presents it as a fact of existence — as woven into the fabric of these lives as the marble dust in Gant's shop. The question is not whether the Gants drink but whether they can survive their drinking.

The connection between drinking and art is significant: Gant's "savage drunkenness" coexists with his passion for Shakespeare, for Booth and Salvini, for the "noble rant." Alcohol both fuels and destroys the creative impulse — a paradox Eugene inherits.
