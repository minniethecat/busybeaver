## Synopsis

Pearl Hines is a supporting figure in the narrative of Helen Gant's young adulthood, representing a brief but vivid chapter in Helen's struggle for independence from the Gant household. As the daughter of "a Baptist saddlemaker" with a "powerful ragtime singing voice" [012-035], Hines offers Helen a path out of Altamont — touring the South's movie-theatre circuit as a singing duo. Their partnership embodies the tension that runs through the novel between freedom and convention: Hines's "direct and certain" intention to marry before twenty-five [018-118] stands in counterpoint to Helen's "blind hunger for variety, beauty, and independence" [018-118].

Hines is drawn with Wolfe's characteristic physical immediacy — "heavily built," "meaty face and negroid lips," "jolly and vital" [018-105] — and her ragtime performances, described with both energy and the period's racialized language, project a sensuality that contrasts with Helen's "painfully innocent" nature [018-117]. The partnership ends when Hines's life-plan (marriage) diverges from Helen's (freedom), a miniature of the novel's larger theme of relationships as temporary convergences.

## Biography

Pearl Hines enters Helen's life as part of a shift in Helen's social circle from her high-school friends (the "hardworking plain-faced" daughters of schoolmasters) to "livelier, if somewhat more vulgar, young women" [012-035]. Her first description is economical but precise:

> [012-035] Pearl Hines, daughter of a Baptist saddlemaker: she was heavy of body and face, but she had a powerful ragtime singing voice.

This pairing of physical heaviness with vocal power is emblematic of Wolfe's approach to character — the body is destiny, and Hines's voice is an instrument of escape from her body and her class.

The partnership between Helen and Pearl is professional and geographic: they "sang together at moving-picture theatres in country towns," booked through "a theatrical office in Atlanta" [018-104]. The life they lead — travel, performance, the freedom of the road — is intoxicating to Helen, who experiences it as "an instinctive groping toward a centre of life and purpose" [018-117].

Hines's musical style is described with an emphasis on embodied performance:

> [018-105] She sang ragtime and nigger songs with a natural passion, swinging her hips and shaking her breasts erotically. "Here comes my da-dad-dy now O pop, O pop, O-o pop."

The language here reflects the novel's 1929 moment — both its musical energy and its racial attitudes are artifacts of the period. Hines's "natural passion" is set against Helen's "painful innocence": Hines knows what her body communicates; Helen "walked confidently and joyously over volcanic crust, scenting only the odor of freedom, change, and adventure" [018-117].

The partnership dissolves for reasons that reveal the fundamental difference between the two women:

> [018-118] The intention of Pearl Hines' life was direct and certain. She wanted to get married, she had always wanted to get married before she was twenty-five. For Helen, the singing partnership, the exploration of new lands, had been a gesture toward freedom.

Where Hines has a destination, Helen has only direction. The novel does not dramatize their parting; it simply notes that the partnership came to an end, as all Helen's provisional escapes do. Hines flickers briefly at the periphery of the narrative thereafter — her "happy laughter" appears in a flashbulb-lit wedding scene [027-164] — before vanishing entirely.

## Relationships

### Helen Gant

Hines is Helen's partner and foil. Their professional collaboration is genuine — they perform together, travel together, share the small excitements of the theatrical life — but their temperaments diverge. Hines is "jolly and vital," her desires concrete; Helen is "painfully innocent," her desires inchoate. The partnership serves its purpose for both and dissolves without acrimony when their paths fork.

### Grace Deshaye

Hines and Grace Deshaye appear together as representatives of Helen's "livelier" social set [012-035]. Deshaye is described as "a plumber's daughter, an opulent blonde," establishing the class register of Helen's new companions: tradesmen's daughters, a step below the Gants' pretensions but more vivid in their living.

## Critical Reception

Pearl Hines has received limited scholarly attention, primarily as an element in studies of Helen Gant's character arc and of female friendship in Wolfe's fiction. Her brief presence in the novel illustrates Wolfe's technique of populating his world with characters who appear, serve a purpose in the development of a major character, and vanish — the fictional equivalent of the people one remembers from a particular season of youth.
