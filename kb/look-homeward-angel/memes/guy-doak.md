## Synopsis

Guy Doak is Eugene Gant's roommate during a season of Eliza's absence from Dixieland, a young man whose worldly sophistication — or what passes for it among Pulpit Hill undergraduates — both fascinates and unsettles Eugene. Five years older than Eugene, with a "trim cocky figure," black hair, and a pale oval face, Doak has already "hardened into the American city-dweller's mould of infantile cynicism." He is sharp, obstinate, and pleased with his wit — a type Eugene will encounter repeatedly as he moves from the provincial world of Altamont into the wider currents of American life.

## Biography

Guy Doak enters Eugene's life as a roommate during a period when Eliza is away from Dixieland. Their first interaction establishes Doak's character — a knowing, slightly mocking presence:

> [024-004] Guy Doak gazed solemnly at Eugene, with a forked hand stroking his grave pallid face.

In the classroom, Doak's humor takes the form of sly notes and dramatic recitations. He reads aloud a passage with theatrical gravity — "Slowly the moonlight crept up the chair in which the old man was sitting" — breaking the tension with a conspiratorial glance at Eugene:

> [024-014] Guy Doak scrawled a brief message across a crumpled wad and thrust it on Eugene's desk.

At night, Doak reveals another dimension of his character — a restlessness that drives him to escape through the window into the darkness beyond:

> [024-027] Often, at night, Guy Doak slid quietly out through the window on to the side porch, and escaped down the road under the concealing roar of the trees.

Doak's cynicism — "infantile" in Wolfe's telling adjective — is a mask of sophistication worn by a young man eager to appear older and wiser than he is. He is a minor but telling figure in Eugene's education: a specimen of the type of sharp, unsubtle worldliness that the wider world will offer in abundance.
