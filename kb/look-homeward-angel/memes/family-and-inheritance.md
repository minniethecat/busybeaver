# Family and Inheritance

## Definition

The Gant family is not merely the subject of *Look Homeward, Angel* — it is the novel's central metaphor for the human condition. Each member of the family inherits not just blood but a particular configuration of hungers, wounds, and gifts. Oliver passes down his rage and his art, his thirst and his stone-carving hands. Eliza passes down her shrewdness and her emotional withholding, her obsession with property and her indomitable endurance.

The novel traces how inheritance works across three generations: Oliver's father who never appears but whose "cold and shallow eyes" prefigure his son's; Oliver himself, the stonecutter; and his children, each of whom receives a different piece of the parental legacy. Eugene, as the youngest and the artist, receives the most — and suffers the most from it.

## Textual Evidence

Oliver's cold eyes — inherited from a father who is barely mentioned — set the pattern for generational transmission:

> (001-007) But I know that his cold and shallow eyes had darkened with the obscure and passionate hunger that had lived in a dead man's eyes, and that had led from Fenchurch Street past Philadelphia.

The children are differentiated by which parent they favor — "the two for whom he felt the deepest affection" (Helen and Luke):

> (020-001) Gant, during these years in which Helen and Luke, the two for whom he felt the deepest affection, were absent a large part of the time, lived a splintered existence at home and at Eliza's.

Eliza comes "through stolidly to victory" — endurance as inheritance:

> (002-009) Eliza came through stolidly to victory. As she marched down these enormous years of love and loss, stained with the rich dyes of pain and pride and death, and with the great wild flare of his alien and passionate life, her limbs faltered in the grip of ruin, but she came on, through sickness and emaciation, to victory.

The seed of destruction is inherited across generations — Wolfe's meditation on how the past shapes the present:

> (015-009) The seed of our destruction will blossom in the desert, the alexin of our cure grows by a mountain rock, and our lives are haunted by a Georgia slattern because a London cutpurse went unhung.

Ben at the end of his life, summing up Eugene's duty — to finish what the family started:

> (030-263) "You're the last hope, 'Gene. Go on and finish up, if you have to steal the money. The rest of us are done for."

Eliza, after Ben's death, is described with imagery of stone — the mother as carven monument:

> (035-145) Eliza sat stolidly, hands folded, before the parlor fire. Her dead white face had a curious carven look; the inflexible solidity of madness.

The novel's opening genealogy — the accumulation of lives that produced Eugene:

> (001-001) A destiny that leads the English to the Dutch is strange enough; but one that leads from Epsom into Pennsylvania, and thence into the hills that shut in Altamont... is touched by that dark miracle of chance which makes new magic in a dusty world.

## Analysis

Family in Wolfe's novel is both a gift and a curse — often simultaneously. Oliver's stone-carving hands are a gift to Eugene (who will become a different kind of maker), but Oliver's alcoholism and rage are a curse (which Eugene will struggle with his whole life). Inheritance is not optional; you receive what your parents were, whether you want it or not.

The novel's title itself encodes the family structure: the angel is Oliver's, but it is Eugene who looks homeward, who writes the book, who must understand what he inherited in order to become himself. The novel is the act of sorting the inheritance — keeping the hunger for creation, discarding (or at least understanding) the hunger for self-destruction.

Eliza's role is particularly complex. She is the survivor, the one who endures — but her endurance comes at a cost: emotional distance, the transformation of family into economics, a "carven" face that resembles the stone angels in Gant's shop.
