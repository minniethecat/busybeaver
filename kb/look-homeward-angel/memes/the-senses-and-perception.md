# The Senses and Perception

## Definition

*Look Homeward, Angel* is a novel of overwhelming sensory abundance. Wolfe does not describe the world; he floods it through the reader's senses. The smell of Gant's roast beef, the taste of whiskey, the clang of the carving knife, the feel of marble dust, the "proud coral cry of the cock" — the novel's world is built from sensory impressions accumulated and layered until they become a lived reality rather than a reported one.

This sensory immersion is central to Wolfe's artistic project. He is not interested in telling you what happened but in making you feel what it felt like to be there. Eugene's consciousness — the focal point of the novel — is above all a sensorium: a receptive surface onto which the world pours its sounds, smells, textures, and tastes. Memory, in Wolfe's treatment, is primarily sensory — a smell or a sound can trigger the recovery of an entire lost world.

## Textual Evidence

The novel's opening establishes sensory abundance — the "proud coral cry of the cock" as the first specific sound:

> (001-001) A destiny that leads the English to the Dutch is strange enough; but one that leads from Epsom into Pennsylvania, and thence into the hills that shut in Altamont over the proud coral cry of the cock, and the soft stone smile of an angel, is touched by that dark miracle of chance which makes new magic in a dusty world.

The town of Altamont is rendered through sensory detail — "the blown sheets of spray from the fountain," the sun, the firemen:

> (009-033) He loved the scene: the bright hill-cooled sun, the blown sheets of spray from the fountain, the garrulous firemen emerging from their hall, and the dusty throngs of week-end farmers tramping in to trade.

The stone shop is described through its tactile, physical presence — the dust, the trestles, the emery wheel:

> (009-034) Behind a wooden partition was his ware-room, layered with stone-dust — coarse wooden trestles on which he carved inscriptions, stacked tool-shelves filled with chisels, drills, mallets, a pedalled emery wheel.

Gant's ritual of polishing stone is rendered in sensory detail — pumice, water, the feel of the marble:

> (009-051) As Eugene entered, if Gant were busy on a stone, he would say gruffly, "Hello, son," and continue with his work, until he had polished the surface of the marble with pumice and water.

The dining room — the sensory heart of the Gant household — with "the clangor of steel and knife as Gant prepared to carve the roast":

> (004-049) As Eugene lay in his crib, he heard through the open door the dining-room clatter, the shrill excitement of the boys, the clangor of steel and knife as Gant prepared to carve the roast, the repetition of the morning's adventure by Steve.

The Square at night — "cold ringing pavements" and "a dead city of closed shops":

> (020-007) They came out at ten-thirty or eleven o'clock, on cold ringing pavements, into a world frozen bare — a dead city of closed shops, dressed windows, milliners' and clothiers' models posturing with waxen gaiety at congealed silence.

## Analysis

Wolfe's sensory prose is not decoration but epistemology. The world is known through the senses before it is known through the mind; Eugene's intelligence is rooted in his body's encounter with the physical world. This is why the novel is so long, why it luxuriates in details that a stricter novelist would cut: Wolfe believes that the truth of an experience resides in its sensory particularity, and that to tell what happened without telling how it smelled, sounded, and felt is to lie by omission.

The connection between sensory perception and memory is crucial. Wolfe's Proustian conviction is that sensory impressions are the keys to the lost past — that a smell of cooking, a particular quality of light, can unlock entire rooms of experience that have been sealed for years. The novel is a machine for recovering the lost sensorium of Wolfe's (Eugene's) childhood.
