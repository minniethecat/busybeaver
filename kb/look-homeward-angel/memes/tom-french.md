## Synopsis

Tom French is a minor antagonist in *Look Homeward, Angel*, appearing in two scenes that bookend Eugene Gant's social humiliation at the hands of Altamont's wealthy youth. At seventeen, French possesses the "hard insolence of money" [029-196] and uses it to assert dominance over those he perceives as socially inferior. His campaign of public mockery — first on the streets of Altamont, then at the Exeter soda shop — targets Eugene with crude sexual innuendo, but ends when Eugene confronts him directly, delivering a quiet, devastating rebuke that strips French of his social power.

French functions narratively as a representative of the class divisions that shape Altamont life. He is not a developed character but a type: the privileged bully whose wealth substitutes for character. His defeat in the Exeter scene serves as one of Eugene's early victories in the novel's long arc of self-definition against the town's social hierarchies.

## Biography

Tom French first appears outside Wood's Drug Store when, at seventeen, he and two companions — Roy Duncan, nineteen, and Carl Jones, eighteen — intercept a group of young women parading through town:

> [024-242] Meanwhile, having completed twice their parade up and down the street from the Square to the post-office, the Misses Christine Ball, Viola Powell, Aline Rollins, and Dorothy Hazzard were accosted outside Wood's Drug Store by Tom French, seventeen, Roy Duncan, nineteen, and Carl Jones, eighteen.

French immediately asserts dominance through verbal aggression: "'Where do you think you're going?' said Tom French, insolently" [024-243]. His manner is one of entitled presumption — he treats public space as his domain and women as objects to be accosted. When introducing his companions, his "proud ominous face" [024-248] signals a boy already practiced in the performance of masculine menace.

French reappears in a longer scene set at the Exeter soda shop, where he finds Eugene Gant sitting alone. The narrator's description crystallizes his character:

> [029-196] There came and sat beside him Tom French, his handsome face vested in the hard insolence of money. He was followed by his court jester, Roy Duncan, the slave with the high hard cackle.

The phrase "vested in the hard insolence of money" is key: French's good looks are inseparable from, and expressed through, his economic privilege. His greeting — "'Hello, Gant. Been to Exeter lately?'" [029-197] — is an allusion to a scandal involving Eugene and a local prostitute, delivered with a wink designed to humiliate. When this fails to provoke the desired response, French escalates to open insult: "'They say that you're as pure as the flowing sewer'" [029-202].

The confrontation reaches its climax when French, "confused and angry, with sullen boastfulness," attempts to defend himself: "'Nobody has anything on me. I've been too slick for them. Nobody knows anything about me'" [029-206, 029-207]. Eugene's reply is Swiftian in its precision:

> [029-208] "You mean," said Eugene, "that everyone knows all about you, and nobody wants to know anything about you."

The crowd laughs [029-209], and the balance of power shifts irreversibly. When Eugene tells French directly — "'I don't like you. I want you to leave me alone now. Do you hear?'" [029-212] — Roy Duncan intervenes to extract his friend, offering the face-saving excuse that Eugene "can't take a joke" [029-213]. The scene ends with French defeated, his social authority shattered by a boy with no money but superior wit.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

French's relationship with Eugene is purely antagonistic. He targets Eugene for class-based bullying, referencing the Exeter scandal as a way to publicly shame him. The dynamic reverses when Eugene's verbal wit exposes French's insecurity — his boast about being "too slick" reveals a boy who fears exposure as much as he inflicts humiliation. Eugene's final dismissal establishes that wit, not wealth, carries the day.

### Roy Duncan

French and Roy Duncan operate as a dyad: French the leader, Duncan his "court jester, the slave with the high hard cackle" [029-196]. Duncan amplifies French's aggression (the "grin" accompanying each insult) and provides the escape route when the confrontation turns against them. Their friendship illustrates the novel's critique of performative masculinity built on shared cruelty.

### Carl Jones

Jones appears only in the chapter 24 street encounter [024-242], a silent member of the trio. His presence fills out the portrait of a social set defined by casual predation.
