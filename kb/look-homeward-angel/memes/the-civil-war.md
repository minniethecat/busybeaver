# The Civil War

## Definition

This concept explores a further dimension of Wolfe's thematic universe in the novel.

## Textual Evidence

The motif appears early in the novel's expansive vision:

> (001-021) The train crawled dizzily across high trestles spanning ghostly hawsers of water. Far up, far down, plumed with wisps of smoke, toy cabins stuck to bank and gulch and hillside.

Oliver's relationship to the landscape is shaped by the changes wrought by modernity:

> (001-019) The grisly parade of the spectre years trooped through his brain. Suddenly, he saw that his life had been channelled by a series of accidents.

Spring brings healing to Oliver's wounded spirit:

> (001-029) And then the marvellous hill Spring came, green-golden, with brief spurting winds, the magic and fragrance of the blossoms, warm gusts of balsam. The great wound in Oliver began to heal.

The town square reflects the layers of history — the "convenient stopping-off place" becoming a modern Southern town:

> (001-026) This town of Altamont had been settled soon after the Revolutionary War. It had been a convenient stopping-off place for cattledrovers and farmers in their swing eastward from Tennessee into South Carolina.

The Civil War's presence lingers in the town's memory — the "Rebel" past that Oliver curses:

> (001-012) He walked through the streets at dark, yelling his pentameter curse at Rebel ways, and all their indolence.

Spring returns as a reassurance that death is not final:

> (020-016) Came a day when Spring put forth her blossoms on the earth again.

## Analysis

This concept represents one of the novel's distinctive contributions to American literature — the fusion of personal memory with historical and natural forces.

## Connections
- **O Lost** — the thematic connection to lostness and the search for home
- **Time and Memory** — how the past persists into the present
