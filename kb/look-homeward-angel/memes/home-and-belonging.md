# Home and Belonging

## Definition

Home in *Look Homeward, Angel* is both a sanctuary and a prison. Oliver Gant builds his house on Woodson Street — "the picture of his soul, the garment of his will" — yet it becomes the site of his drinking, raving, and dying. Eliza's Dixieland boardinghouse is a business disguised as a home, crowded with strangers. Eugene grows up longing to escape these houses even as they imprint themselves permanently on his imagination.

The novel's treatment of home resonates with the American literary tradition of the small-town departure narrative, but Wolfe complicates it: home is never simply left behind. It travels with you, haunts your dreams, and shapes the way you see every place that follows. The hunger to belong — to find a place that fits the shape of one's soul — is as powerful and as doomed as Oliver's hunger to carve the angel's head.

## Textual Evidence

Gant's house is the "picture of his soul" — a fusion of self and place:

> (002-003) For him the house was the picture of his soul, the garment of his will. But for Eliza it was a piece of property, whose value she shrewdly appraised, a beginning for her hoard.

Eliza purchases Dixieland and transforms it into a boardinghouse — the family home becomes a commercial enterprise:

> (012-061) Daisy had been married in the month of June following Eliza's purchase of Dixieland: the wedding was arranged on a lavish scale, and took place in the big dining-room of the house.

The town of Altamont itself is described with the ambivalent love of someone who knows every street and hates many of them:

> (001-026) This town of Altamont had been settled soon after the Revolutionary War. It had been a convenient stopping-off place for cattledrovers and farmers in their swing eastward from Tennessee into South Carolina.

Eugene, at the novel's end, stands on the porch of Gant's shop looking at the angels — the place that shaped him, the threshold he must leave:

> (040-004) Eugene saw his father's name, faded, on the old brick in moonlight. On the stone porch of the shop, the angels held their marble posture.

The house on Woodson Street becomes a site of conflict between Gant and Eliza — two incompatible visions of home:

> (002-005) The strange figure of Oliver Gant cast its famous shadow through the town. Men heard at night and morning the great formula of his curse to Eliza.

Eugene's departure — leaving Altamont forever — is the novel's final reckoning with home:

> (039-031) He still loitered, although his baggage had been packed for days. With a desperate pain, he faced departure from that Arcadian wilderness where he had known so much joy.

Dixieland as winter boardinghouse — home refashioned as a cold commercial enterprise:

> (020-004) But he remained at Dixieland as long as there was anyone to listen to him, and to the bleak little group of winter boarders he brought magic.

## Analysis

Wolfe presents home as fundamentally ambiguous. It is where you are formed and where you are deformed — the shape that fits too tightly, the garment you must eventually shed. The Gant family's various houses — the Woodson Street house, Dixieland, Gant's shop — are not neutral containers but active forces that shape the characters who inhabit them.

Eliza's transformation of the family home into the Dixieland boardinghouse is the novel's key gesture toward the failure of belonging. When home becomes a business, the family becomes strangers to itself. Eugene grows up "caged in that little round of skull" not because he is unloved but because the house offers no stable center — his father rages in one wing while boarders shuffle through the other.

The novel's title, with its invocation of Milton and the angel looking homeward, suggests that home is always elsewhere — a place you look toward rather than a place you inhabit. Eugene leaves Altamont at the novel's end, but the departure is not liberation: it is exile.
