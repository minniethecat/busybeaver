# Ralph Rolls

## Synopsis

Ralph Rolls is one of the group of young men — the "Bard's personae" — who populate Eugene Gant's school world in *Look Homeward, Angel*. He appears in two scenes: the sprawling street panorama of Chapter 24 and the Shakespeare pageant of Chapter 27. Defined by a few vivid physical details — a derby hat jammed over his pert freckled face, a ragged twist of chewing tobacco, and a coarse, audible laugh — Rolls is a minor comic figure who contributes to the novel's texture of adolescent masculine camaraderie, its rivalries, and its casual cruelties.

## Role in the Novel

### Chapter 24: The Street Gathering

Rolls first appears among the group of schoolboys who gather after classes on a spring afternoon. The scene is one of Wolfe's characteristic street panoramas, weaving together dozens of brief encounters across Altamont. Rolls is paired with Julius Arthur, his "copesmate" — the two function as a comic duo, their banter establishing the rough, teasing dynamic of the group:

> "Shall we sing our little song for Handsome Hal?" said Ralph Rolls to his copesmate Julius. He wore a derby hat jammed over his pert freckled face. As he spoke he took a ragged twist of tobacco from his pocket and bit off a large chew with a rough air of relish. [024-096]

The derby hat — an adult accessory worn with juvenile bravado — and the deliberate, performative chewing of tobacco mark Rolls as a boy affecting the mannerisms of manhood. His nickname for Eugene, "Handsome Hal," is teasing without being truly hostile; when the group disperses, his parting line is a casual, almost protective warning:

> "Well, keep Hal out of trouble," said Ralph Rolls. [024-109]

### Chapter 27: The Shakespeare Pageant

Rolls reappears in the Shakespeare pageant scene, where he is one of the costumed participants awaiting their cues. In this setting his coarseness becomes more pronounced — a deliberate counterpoint to the pretensions of the civic pageant:

> "Well, thank God for that!" said Ralph Rolls coarsely in a solemn quiet. The Bard's strewn host laughed, rustled noisily into line. [027-042]

When Eugene descends into the hollow as Prince Hal, Rolls's response blends mockery with backhanded encouragement:

> "He looks it, you mean," said Ralph Rolls. "Boy, you'll knock 'em dead," he added with an indecent laugh. [027-054]

The "indecent laugh" is Rolls's signature — a laugh that hovers between camaraderie and derision, the sound of a boy who has learned that being funny is safer than being sincere.

## Symbolic Function

Ralph Rolls represents the adolescent male culture that Eugene both participates in and stands apart from. Like Julius Arthur, Doc Hines, and the other boys of the school group, Rolls is part of a world of rough horseplay, tobacco, and casual mockery — the vernacular culture of Altamont youth. Eugene's ability to banter with them (and to endure their teasing) is a measure of his partial assimilation into this world; his deeper difference from them is measured by the artistic and intellectual aspirations that the novel's central narrative traces.

Rolls, more than some of the other boys, shows flickers of genuine goodwill toward Eugene — the "keep Hal out of trouble" line suggests a protective impulse beneath the coarse exterior. These small gestures of kindness, scattered among the novel's minor characters, complicate any simple reading of Wolfe's social world as purely hostile or alienating.

## Narrative Context

Rolls's two appearances (Chapters 24 and 27) bracket a key period in Eugene's adolescence — the spring of his growing self-awareness and the summer of Helen's wedding. Chapter 24 is the novel's great diptych of Altamont life, juxtaposing the visit of William Jennings Bryan with the texture of ordinary days. Chapter 27 transitions from the comic chaos of the Shakespeare pageant to the more somber notes of Helen's marriage to Hugh Barton.
