# The Grotesque

## Definition

Wolfe's vision is fundamentally grotesque — in the literary sense of the term: bodies are excessive, faces are distorted, appetites are monstrous. The grotesque in *Look Homeward, Angel* is not a flaw in Wolfe's style but the source of its power. Characters are rendered in extreme close-up, their physical features magnified until they become emblems of inner states. Oliver Gant's enormous hands, his "lunging" walk, his bellowing voice — these are not realistic descriptions but grotesque portraiture that reveals the soul through the body's excess.

The grotesque tradition (Rabelais, Dickens, Dostoevsky) treats the body as a site of meaning — its distortions are moral and metaphysical, not merely physical. Wolfe inherits this tradition and Americanizes it: the grotesque bodies of Altamont are the bodies of a nation in transition, swollen with new appetites and haunted by old hungers.

## Textual Evidence

The novel's opening vision already features bodily excess — the "slattern people gaping at the stations," the "gaunt" farmer:

> (001-019) He had reeled out of warmth and plenty into this barren land: as he stared out the window and saw the fallow unworked earth, the great raw lift of the Piedmont, the muddy red clay roads, and the slattern people gaping at the stations — a lean farmer gangling above his reins, a dawdling negro, a gap-toothed yokel, a hard sallow woman with a grimy baby.

Gant's body is all excess — "great hands," "enormous talking hands," his physical presence overwhelming:

> (001-009) He wanted to wreak something dark and unspeakable in him into cold stone.

The angels themselves are grotesque — "soft stone idiocy," "phthisic foot," "vacant leer":

> (019-064) It was now brown and fly-specked... it was poised clumsily upon the ball of one phthisic foot, and its stupid white face wore a smile of soft stone idiocy.

Eliza, near the end, is described as a creature of stone — "the inflexible solidity of madness":

> (035-145) Eliza sat stolidly, hands folded, before the parlor fire. Her dead white face had a curious carven look; the inflexible solidity of madness.

Gant carves "the dove, the lamb, the smooth joined marble hands of death" — funerary art that mingles the beautiful with the morbid.

## Analysis

The grotesque serves Wolfe as a mode of truth-telling. By exaggerating the body — making faces too large, hands too expressive, appetites too consuming — he captures what realism misses: the excess of lived experience, the way emotion distorts perception, the impossibility of seeing another person "objectively." Every character in the novel is seen through Eugene's hungry, distorting, adoring eyes.

The connection to the stone angel is telling: the angel is both beautiful (Carrara marble, the gesture of benediction) and grotesque (fly-specked, idiot-smiling, poised on one "phthisic" foot). The ideal and the monstrous coexist in the same object — which is Wolfe's vision of all human beings.
