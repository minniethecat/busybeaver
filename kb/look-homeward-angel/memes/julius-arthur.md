## Synopsis

Julius Arthur is one of Eugene Gant's companions at Pulpit Hill, a "friendly grinning" presence who recurs across Eugene's college years in a series of cameo appearances. From classroom banter about gullible freshmen to theatrical swordplay as Macbeth, from driving his father home at dangerous speed to rescuing a drunken Eugene from the streets, Arthur embodies a particular type in Wolfe's university world — the good-natured, physically confident young man whose easy camaraderie provides the social texture of Eugene's undergraduate life.

## Biography

Julius Arthur first appears as a voice of worldly wisdom in the schoolroom, warning a classmate about the credulity of younger students:

> [017-089] "They know you'll believe whatever they tell you," said Julius Arthur.

At Pulpit Hill, Arthur is a constant background presence in Eugene's social world. He participates in a campus theatrical production, playing Macbeth with more enthusiasm than skill:

> [027-028] Behind him, Julius Arthur (Macbeth), drew his sword with a flourish.

His most significant appearance comes on a night when Eugene, dangerously drunk, collapses in the town square. Arthur and Van Yeats find him and carry him home — an act of youthful loyalty that Wolfe renders with understated affection:

> [032-112] The next instant he was staring into the friendly grinning faces of Julius Arthur and Van Yeats.

> [032-124] "Let's take off his shoes," said Julius Arthur.

Arthur is among the Pulpit Hill students who enlist when war comes, part of the generation swept up in the mobilization:

> [034-046] All of his fraternity brothers, all of his college mates, who had not previously enlisted, and many young men who had never before thought of college, now joined.
