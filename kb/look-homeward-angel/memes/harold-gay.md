## Synopsis

Harold Gay is a minor but vividly drawn character in *Look Homeward, Angel*, appearing as one of Eugene Gant's two roommates during his first year at Pulpit Hill (the state university). The son of an Episcopal minister, Harold is twenty-two years old, several years older than Eugene, and is presented as a fundamentally innocent and childlike personality whose bravado masks deep bewilderment. His role in the novel serves both as comic relief in the dormitory scenes and as a foil to his more flamboyant roommate, Elk Duncan.

Harold's brief but memorable appearances capture the spirit of undergraduate life at the state university, with its absurd history quizzes, movie-theater escapades, and performative tough-guy posturing. Through Harold, Wolfe conveys the awkwardness of young men caught between boyhood and adulthood, between religious upbringing and worldly pretensions.

## Biography

Harold Gay is introduced in Chapter 32 when Eugene moves into a Pulpit Hill dormitory following the death of his roommate. He is twenty-two, wears spectacles, and is described as "a good soul, no older than a child":

> [032-021] The other roommate, Harold Gay, was a good soul, no older than a child. He wore spectacles, which gave the only glister to the dull grayness of his face; he was plain and ugly without any distinction: he had been puzzled so long by at least four-fifths of the phenomena of existence that he no longer made any effort to comprehend them. Instead, he concealed his shyness and bewilderment under a braying laugh that echoed at all the wrong places, and a silly grin full of an absurd and devilish knowingness.

His background as the son of an Episcopal minister gives an ironic tinge to his behavior:

> [032-018] But he roomed the remainder of the term in one of the dormitories. He had two roommates—one, an Altamont young man who answered to the name of L. K. Duncan (the L stood for Lawrence, but everyone called him "Elk") and the other, the son of an Episcopal minister, Harold Gay.

Harold's admiration for Elk Duncan transforms his social performance — he smokes cigarettes "with a debauched leer" and curses "loudly and uneasily with the accent of a depraved clergyman":

> [032-021] His association with Elk Duncan was one of the proud summits of his life: he weltered in the purple calcium which bathed that worthy, he smoked cigarettes with a debauched leer, and cursed loudly and uneasily with the accent of a depraved clergyman.

Harold participates enthusiastically in the roommates' absurd history game, quizzing Eugene on historical trivia:

> [032-037] " 'Gene!" yelled Harold Gay, hurling a thick volume from him under the spell of Elk's great names. "What do you know about history? Who signed Magna Charta, eh?"

> [032-039] "Correct!" roared Harold Gay. "Who was Æthelred the Unready?"

His final appearance in the novel finds him giving up on the intellectual game and proposing a trip to the local movie theater, the "Pic":

> [032-045] "You boys are too good for me!" yelled Harold Gay, getting up abruptly. "Come on! Who's going to the Pic?"

## Relationships

### Elk Duncan

Harold is defined largely by his adoration of his other roommate, L. K. ("Elk") Duncan. The son of an Altamont attorney, Elk is a tall, loquacious campus personality given to wild fabulism. Harold orbits Elk like a lesser satellite, eagerly adopting his mannerisms. Elk, for his part, treats Harold with affectionate condescension:

> [032-022] "Harold! Harold!" said Elk Duncan reprovingly. "Damn, son! You're getting hard! If you go on like this, you'll begin to chew gum, and fritter away your Sunday-school money at the movies."

### Eugene Gant

Harold's relationship with Eugene is collegial and warm — he calls him "'Gene" and includes him in their historical wordplay. As a younger and intellectually precocious student, Eugene participates in the game with more sophistication than Harold, but Harold accepts his role as the good-natured straight man with enthusiasm.

## Critical Context

Harold Gay belongs to a gallery of minor characters that populate the Pulpit Hill sections of the novel and give texture to Eugene's college experience. His characterization — the minister's son straining against his upbringing through performative profanity and cigarette-smoking — exemplifies Wolfe's interest in the tension between inherited identity and self-fashioning. The character's name, "Gay," may carry a submerged pun: his braying laugh and silly grin make him, in his own way, the most genuinely cheerful presence in a dormitory shadowed by Eugene's grief over his father's impending death.
