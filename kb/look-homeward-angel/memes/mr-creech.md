## Synopsis

Michael Walter Creech appears in a single, densely packed passage of Chapter 14 — one of the novel's characteristic set-pieces in which a minor character is briefly but vividly illuminated before the narrative moves on. A butcher by trade, Creech is presented in the full vigor of his morning routine: a hearty breakfast, the command of a row of negro boys waiting for his orders, the pleasure of power exercised in a small domain.

What distinguishes Creech from the novel's other merchants is the narrator's disclosure of his ancestry. The town may have "so far forgotten Mr. Creech's own African blood" [014-220], but Creech himself has not. This consciousness — of what the town chooses to overlook and what he cannot — gives the brief portrait its particular edge. Creech is a man who has achieved prosperity and respectability in a society where his ancestry would, if openly acknowledged, deny him both.

## Biography

### The Butcher at Work

The passage opens with Creech at the height of his morning power:

> [014-219] Mr. Michael Walter Creech, the butcher, having finished his hearty breakfast of calves' liver, eggs and bacon, hot biscuits and coffee, made a sign to one of the waiting row of negro boys. The line sprang forward like hounds; he stopped them with a curse and a lifted cleaver.

The scene is one of absolute command. The row of waiting boys, the sign that sets them springing forward, the curse and lifted cleaver that stop them — each detail reinforces Creech's mastery of his small domain. The "hearty breakfast" establishes physical substance, a man who consumes with the same vigor with which he dominates. The cleaver, raised in a gesture that hovers between threat and tool, is the symbol of his authority.

### The Burden of Ancestry

The narrative then pulls back from the immediate scene to disclose Creech's hidden history:

> [014-220] The town had so far forgotten Mr. Creech's own African blood (an eighth on his father's side, old Walter Creech, out of Yellow Jenny) that it was about ready to offer him political preferment; but Mr. Creech himself had not forgotten.

The parenthetical precision — "an eighth on his father's side, old Walter Creech, out of Yellow Jenny" — is characteristic of the narrator's documentary impulse. The town's forgetfulness is contrasted with Creech's own indelible memory. The phrase "about ready to offer him political preferment" captures both the town's conditional acceptance and its precariousness: Creech is always one revelation away from losing what he has gained.

### Family Resentment

The passage closes with Creech's gaze turning inward, toward his own family:

> [014-221] Mr. Creech looked venomously at Jay's yellow jowls, the fat throbbing of his jaundiced throat, the crisp singed whorl of his hair.

His brother Jay, "happily ignorant" of their shared ancestry, becomes the object of Creech's displaced resentment. The "venomous" look, the attention to "yellow jowls" and "jaundiced throat" — Creech reads in his brother's body the very marks of the ancestry he cannot forget. Jay's ignorance is, for Creech, both enviable and infuriating: a freedom he cannot share.

## Relationships

### Jay Creech

Creech's relationship with his brother is the emotional center of the portrait. Jay, described as "happily ignorant of his own African blood," represents the possibility of assimilation through forgetting — a possibility denied to Michael, whose consciousness of ancestry is too acute to permit such oblivion. The "venomous" look that closes the passage suggests a relationship poisoned by the very knowledge one brother carries and the other lacks.

### The Negro Boys

The row of waiting boys who serve Creech establish the racial hierarchy within which he operates. His power over them — the sign, the curse, the lifted cleaver — is both an expression of his achieved status and a desperate assertion of distance from the racial category that threatens to claim him. The boys are both subordinates and mirrors; in commanding them, Creech commands the part of himself the town has "forgotten."
