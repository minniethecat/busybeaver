# Jennings Ware

## Synopsis

Jennings Ware is a minor but vividly drawn character in *Look Homeward, Angel* who appears in Chapter 22 as Eugene Gant's predecessor on the morning paper route. A "robust country boy of seventeen" who has been promoted to a better position at the newspaper office, Ware trains the young Eugene over three mornings in the mechanics of delivery — and, equally, in the route's social geography. Tough, good-humored, and a little cynical, he serves as Eugene's first guide into the adult working world beyond the Gant household.

## Role in the Novel

### The Paper Route Initiation

Eugene, then a schoolboy desperate to earn money and terrified of failure, is assigned to take over the delivery route from Jennings Ware. Over three mornings they walk the route together before dawn, Ware demonstrating the practical rhythms of the job: which houses are good pay, which are deadbeats, where to collect money and where to simply leave the paper on the mat. The training is part apprenticeship and part hazing — Ware is efficient and blunt, but not unkind.

> "Check this one off," said Jennings Ware, "if you can't collect next time. She owes for six weeks now." [022-028]

> "This one," he said, flipping a paper quietly on a door mat, "is good pay. They're good niggers. You'll get your money every Wednesday." [022-029]

### A Glimpse of Adult Knowledge

Beyond the practical instruction, Ware also represents Eugene's first exposure to casual adult sensuality. When describing the sexual availability of certain women on the route, Ware's blunt talk shocks and intrigues the inexperienced Eugene. The exchange is handled with characteristic Wolfean indirection — dialogue rich with implication, the boy's response whispered and uncertain.

> Jennings Ware turned his red satirical face on him. [022-037]

> "Boy!" The word blew out of Jennings Ware's mouth like an explosion. He was silent a moment. [022-040]

> "There ain't nothing better," he said. [022-041]

### Departure

After the three mornings of training, Ware disappears from the narrative entirely. He has been "given better employment at the paper office," a promotion that opens the door for Eugene's own entry into the working world. Though brief, Ware's presence establishes a pattern that recurs throughout the novel: an older, more worldly figure who briefly illuminates a corner of adult experience for Eugene before vanishing.

## Symbolic Function

Jennings Ware functions as a threshold figure in Eugene's development — the first non-family member to initiate him into a world of labor, money, and adult knowledge. His brief appearance and abrupt departure mirror the novel's larger pattern of fleeting relationships that nonetheless leave lasting impressions on the protagonist. He is also one of several "older brother" surrogates (alongside Ben Gant and others) who guide Eugene through various rites of passage.

## Narrative Context

Ware appears exclusively in Chapter 22, which chronicles Eugene's early working life as a paper carrier. The chapter is a pivotal one in the novel's structure: it marks Eugene's first sustained contact with the world outside the Gant household and the beginning of his economic independence. Ware's pragmatic, unsentimental instruction contrasts sharply with Eliza Gant's anxious exhortations to "spruce up" and "make them think you are somebody" (022-023), embodying the street-level reality Eugene must learn to navigate.
