## Synopsis

Mrs. Tarkinton is one of the neighborhood women who populate the background of Eugene Gant's early childhood — a figure of cheerful, unreflective loyalty to Altamont and its ways. The mother of Eugene's playmate Harry Tarkinton, she belongs to the circle of Baptist families who make up the social fabric of Gant's neighborhood. Her appearances bookend two of Eliza's departures and returns: she welcomes Eliza back from California with tears and kisses, and years later she provides sanctuary when Eliza flees Gant's drunken rage. In both moments, she represents the stability of the neighborhood — the woman who "cheerfully" assures the returning Eliza that "there's no place like Altamont."

## Biography

Mrs. Tarkinton first appears during one of the novel's recurrent rituals of departure and return. Eliza has been away in California — one of her speculative real-estate ventures — and the neighborhood gathers to welcome her back. Mrs. Tarkinton's reaction captures the communal astonishment at Eliza's worldliness:

> [005-055] The Tarkintons and Duncans streamed in: there were tears and kisses. Mrs. Tarkinton regarded her with some awe. The whole neighborhood was a bit bewildered at this latest turn.

Her reassurance to Eliza is delivered with "cheerful loyalty" — a phrase that captures both the warmth and the limits of her perspective:

> [005-057] "You'll come back," said Mrs. Tarkinton with cheerful loyalty. "There's no place like Altamont."

Mrs. Tarkinton reappears in a very different register later in the novel, during one of Gant's drunken rampages. She is among the group of Baptist women who encounter Gant outside a warehouse:

> [021-010] Outside the corrugated walls of the warehouse, Gant paused for a moment to acknowledge the fervent congratulation of a group of ladies from the First Baptist Church: Mrs. Tarkinton, Mrs. Fagg Sluder.

In the chaos that follows — Gant's fury, Eliza's flight — the Tarkinton house becomes a place of refuge:

> [021-048] She kept the tree deftly between them and, when his attention was diverted for a moment to the flood of anathema, tore off on fear-quick feet, streetward to the haven of the Tarkintons' house.
