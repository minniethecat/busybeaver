## Synopsis

Major Thomas Pentland is the patriarchal figurehead of the Pentland clan and the father of Eliza Gant. Although he dies in May 1902 — before the novel's main action — his presence permeates the family mythology: he is invoked by Eliza as an emblem of Pentland temperance and respectability, and derided by Oliver Gant as a man who "never did an honest day's work in his life" [003-081]. The contradiction between these two versions of the Major — the dignified patriarch versus the indolent blowhard — encapsulates the larger tension between the Pentland and Gant family cultures that defines the novel's domestic world.

His military title, "honestly if inconspicuously earned" as commander of two companies of Home Volunteers who guarded the "stronghold of the native hills" [001-064], is a characteristic Wolfean detail: genuine but underwhelming, a distinction that simultaneously confers and undermines status. In this, Major Pentland prefigures the pattern of ambition and disappointment that will mark subsequent Pentland generations.

## Biography

Major Thomas Pentland appears in the novel primarily through retrospect and anecdote. He is introduced in Chapter 1 as "the present chieftain of the tribe," brother of the prophet Bacchus Pentland, and the father of a large family from which Eliza is the only surviving daughter [001-064, 001-067].

His Civil War service defines his public identity. Unlike his brother Bacchus, who fought at Shiloh as a corporal, the Major's war was a local affair:

> [001-064] While Bacchus, who never rose above the rank of Corporal, was blistering his hard hands at Shiloh, the Major, as commander of two companies of Home Volunteers, was guarding the stronghold of the native hills. This stronghold was never threatened until the closing days of the war, when the Volunteers, ambuscaded behind convenient trees and rocks, fired three volleys into a detachment of Sherman's stragglers, and quietly dispersed to the defense of their attendant wives and children.

The passage's ironic undertone — three volleys at stragglers, then home to the wives — establishes the gap between the Major's titular dignity and his actual military achievement. Another Pentland brother "had been killed during the Seven Days" [001-064], lending the family a genuine sacrifice that the Major's Home Volunteer service cannot quite match.

Physically, the Major is described during his first confrontation with the young Oliver Gant:

> [001-072] He was a stocky fleshy man in the middle fifties, with a ruddy face, a patriarchal beard, and the thick complacent features of his tribe.

The phrase "thick complacent features of his tribe" is characteristic of Wolfe's treatment of the Pentlands: they are a type, a clan, defined as much by their shared physical and temperamental traits as by individual character. The Major rises slowly, folds his pocket knife, puts away his apple — the deliberate, unhurried movements of a man secure in his domestic authority [001-071].

His interrogation of Gant during their first meeting — "I suppose that you've served on a great many juries?" [001-085] — is a classic Pentland gambit: outwardly polite, inwardly probing, designed to establish social dominance through indirection. It is the same technique Eliza will employ throughout her marriage.

The Major dies in May 1902, an event recorded in one of the novel's great catalogues of Gant's sleepless nights [007-003]. His death enters family legend as a dividing line: Eliza consistently invokes him as proof of Pentland virtue ("his grandfather, Major Pentland, never in his life allowed a drop in his house") [032-150], while Gant counters with the deflating retort: "Major Pentland be damned! If you'd depended on him for anything you'd have gone hungry" [032-151].

## Relationships

### Eliza Gant (née Pentland)

Eliza is the Major's only surviving daughter, and her reverence for him is a cornerstone of her identity. She invokes him as moral authority — the teetotaling patriarch who would never allow alcohol in his home — whenever Gant's drinking threatens the family's respectability [032-150]. This invocation, however, is selective: the Major's military service was negligible, and Gant's counterclaim that he was essentially useless ("never did an honest day's work") [003-081] is never directly refuted by Eliza. The Major thus functions less as a real person than as a rhetorical weapon in the ongoing Gant-Pentland marital war.

### Oliver Gant

Gant's relationship with his father-in-law is defined by contempt and competition. From their first meeting — the Major's jury-service interrogation — to Gant's posthumous dismissal decades later, Gant refuses to grant the Major the respect that Pentland family mythology demands. His accusation that the Major "never did an honest day's work in his life" [003-081] is both a class judgment (the stonecutter versus the gentleman) and a deflection from his own failings as a provider.

### Bacchus Pentland

The contrast between the two Pentland brothers is structural: Bacchus fought at Shiloh as a corporal and emerged as a prophet-like local character; the Major commanded Home Volunteers and became a patriarch. Bacchus earns his mystique through suffering and eccentricity; the Major earns his through title and progeny. Together they represent the twin poles of Pentland identity — the wild and the respectable, the visionary and the domestic.

### Will Pentland and the Pentland Sons

The Major fathered six sons — Henry, Will, Jim, Thaddeus, Elmer, and Greeley — in addition to Eliza and a younger daughter, Jane, who died young of "poor Jane's scrofula" [001-067]. Will Pentland, the second son, becomes the most vividly realized of the Major's male children, inheriting the clan's love of pocket knives, sly winks, and birdlike nods [001-071]. Greeley Pentland dies at twenty-six, a "congenital scrofulous tubercular, violinist, Pentlandian punster, petty check-forger, and six weeks' jailbird" [007-003] — the clan's tragic underside that the Major's patriarchal dignity cannot fully contain.

## Appearances

Major Pentland appears directly only in Chapter 1 (the retrospective of Gant's courtship of Eliza) but is referenced throughout the novel as a figure of family memory and argument. He dies in May 1902, before the main action begins.
