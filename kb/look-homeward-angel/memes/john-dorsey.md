## Synopsis

John Dorsey — also referred to as Mr. Leonard — is the headmaster of the private academy that Eugene Gant attends during his formative school years. A figure of chalk-dusted authority and comically ineffectual discipline, Dorsey presides over a curriculum built on Cicero, Virgil, and Horace — the classical education that Eugene both absorbs and rebels against. Wolfe renders him with a mixture of satire and affection: his "thick chalked fingers," his tentative falsetto laughter, his physical bulk squeezed into a wicker chair, his rhetoric of "sportsmanship" and "fair play" that barely contains a boiling temper. Yet Dorsey is also capable of generosity — when Eliza pleads poverty, he agrees to take Eugene for half the tuition — and his school, for all its absurdities, provides the intellectual soil in which Eugene's voracious reading takes root.

## Biography

John Dorsey runs his academy with the help of his wife Margaret and his sister Amy, who teaches mathematics and history. The school occupies a distinct place in the social geography of Altamont, drawing students from families who can afford private education. Eugene enters after his public-school years, and the transition marks a new chapter in his intellectual development.

Dorsey's pedagogical style is a blend of rote recitation and rhetorical exhortation. His Latin and Greek classes move at a glacial pace through the classics — "two years on that dull dog, Cicero" — while he intersperses the lessons with pronouncements on character and conduct. Eugene, "parroting faithfully all that John Dorsey had to say about the 'spirit of fair play,' 'sportsmanship,' 'playing the game for the game's sake,'" absorbs the language of his schoolmaster even as he begins to see through it:

> [017-185] He parroted faithfully all that John Dorsey had to say about the "spirit of fair play," "sportsmanship," "playing the game for the game's sake," "accepting defeat or victory with a smile."

The gap between Dorsey's ideals and his temper is revealed in moments of fury. When a Jewish student, Edward Michalove, becomes the target of classroom abuse — "both for John Dorsey and the boys" — Dorsey's rage erupts in physical violence that belies his moralistic rhetoric:

> [017-195] "Sit down!" yelled John Dorsey, hurling him into a desk. Then, his boiling fury unappeased, and baffled by fear of inflicting some crippling punishment on the boy, he added illogically: "Stand up!"

Dorsey's relationship with Eliza Gant reveals a more pragmatic side. When she tells him she can no longer afford the tuition, he confers with Margaret and returns with a compromise:

> [017-104] At the end of Eugene's first year at Leonard's, Eliza told John Dorsey she could no longer afford to pay the tuition. He conferred with Margaret and, returning, agreed to take the boy for half price.

In the classroom scenes of later chapters, Dorsey appears in more domestic and comic registers — squeezed into a wicker chair, drooling slightly as he laughs at a joke, rubbing his torso "from loin to chin" with "thick chalked fingers." His tentative falsetto laughter, always a beat behind the boys' own wild mirth, makes him a figure of gentle, affectionate absurdity:

> [024-008] Their wild laughter leaped like freed hounds. Tom Davis hurled himself violently downward over his desk. John Dorsey looked up, adding uncertainly his absent falsetto mirth.

Yet Wolfe does not dismiss him. In a "happy moment, touched by the genius of the place," Dorsey can arrive at an observation worthy of the novel's own rhetoric:

> [024-017] "The mountains," observed John Dorsey, touched, in a happy moment, by the genius of the place, "have been the traditional seat of Liberty."
