# Roy Duncan

## Synopsis

Roy Duncan is a minor antagonistic figure in *Look Homeward, Angel* who appears as the loyal sidekick to Tom French, the wealthy and insolent college student who torments Eugene Gant. Described as "the slave with the high hard cackle" and French's "court jester," Duncan is defined by his subservient role to a more powerful figure and by a nervous, cackling laugh that serves as his social signature. He appears at two key moments: a comic street scene in Altamont in Chapter 24, and a tense confrontation with Eugene on a train in Chapter 29.

## Role in the Novel

### Altamont Street Scene (Chapter 24)

Duncan first appears in the panoramic Chapter 24, which weaves together dozens of vignettes of Altamont life during a visit by William Jennings Bryan. Along with Tom French (seventeen) and Carl Jones (eighteen), he accosts a group of young women outside Wood's Drug Store. His contribution to the flirtation is a single, absurd line:

> "Hay's seven dollars a ton," said Roy Duncan, and immediately burst into a high cackle of laughter, in which all the others joined, merrily. [024-246]

The line is a non sequitur — an agricultural commodity price delivered as a joke — and the cackle that follows defines him. Wolfe's description of his face as "small tight freckled impish" (024-251) reinforces the impression of a figure who exists less as an individual than as a comic archetype: the jester, the hanger-on, the man who laughs at his own jokes to cue others to laugh too.

### The Train Confrontation (Chapter 29)

Duncan reappears in Chapter 29, now more firmly established in his role as French's follower. On a train returning to the university, French and Duncan confront Eugene, attempting to mock him about his social pretensions:

> There came and sat beside him Tom French, his handsome face vested in the hard insolence of money. He was followed by his court jester, Roy Duncan, the slave with the high hard cackle. [029-196]

Duncan's contribution to the exchange is characteristic: he reinforces French's jibes and tries to keep the mood light when Eugene pushes back. His line "We hear you're stepping out among them, 'Gene" (029-200) echoes French's class-based mockery, and his final intervention — "Come on, leave him alone, Tom. He can't take a joke. He takes things too seriously" (029-213) — attempts to cast Eugene as the unreasonable party while extricating French from a losing confrontation.

## Symbolic Function

Roy Duncan represents a recurring type in Wolfe's social taxonomy: the dependent personality who attaches himself to a stronger figure and derives his identity from proximity to power. As Tom French's "court jester," Duncan has traded autonomy for protection — he will never be the target of French's aggression, but he will never be taken seriously either. His function in the narrative is to amplify French's menace by providing an audience; without Duncan's cackling laughter, French's bullying would be exposed as the empty posturing it largely is.

In this sense, Duncan is a comic-satiric portrait of the enabler — the figure without whom cruelty loses its social sanction. His final line on the train ("He takes things too seriously") is the classic retreat of the bully's accomplice: reframing the victim's objection as a failure of humor.

## Narrative Context

Duncan's two appearances (Chapters 24 and 29) span the novel's middle section, bridging Eugene's Altamont adolescence and his university years. Both appearances place Duncan in the same structural position — at French's side, echoing his lines, ready with the cackling laugh. He does not develop or change; he is a static figure, defined wholly by his relationship to a more powerful character.
