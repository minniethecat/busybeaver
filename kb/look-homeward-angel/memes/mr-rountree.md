# Mr. Rountree

## Synopsis

Mr. Rountree is a minor character in *Look Homeward, Angel*, appearing in a single scene in Chapter 38 as a student in [[vergil-weldon|Vergil Weldon]]'s philosophy class at the state university. He serves as a dramatic foil in a discussion about intellectual courage and martyrdom, declaring that he would have accepted death rather than recant his beliefs—a stance that [[eugene-gant|Eugene Gant]] undercuts with pragmatic irreverence.

Rountree's brief appearance crystallizes one of the novel's recurring tensions: the gap between high-minded rhetoric and actual human behavior. His solemn declaration of martyrdom, delivered while "moistening spectacles," is gently mocked by the narrative voice and countered by Eugene's frank admission that he would have done what Galileo did—"backed out of it."

## Biography

Mr. Rountree appears in Chapter 38 during a philosophy class taught by Vergil Weldon, an aging professor whose lectures are a patchwork of Western thought from Zeno to Kant. The class discussion has turned to the historical example of Galileo, who recanted his heliocentric views under pressure from the Inquisition.

Weldon poses a hypothetical question to Rountree: what would he have done if the Inquisition had demanded he renounce the truth?

> [038-067] "I'd have told the truth," said Mr. Rountree, removing his glasses.

When pressed further—reminded that "they had built a good big fire" (038-068)—Rountree doubles down on his conviction:

> [038-069] "That doesn't matter," said Mr. Rountree, putting his glasses on again.

The scene reaches its ironic peak when Rountree, undeterred by the reminder that "they'd have burned you if you hadn't recanted" (038-071), delivers his final declaration:

> [038-072] "Ah. I'd have let them burn," said the martyred Rountree through moistening spectacles.

The narrative immediately undercuts this noble sentiment with Eugene's internal observation: "How nobly we can die for truth—in conversation" (038-070). When Weldon turns the question to Eugene, the protagonist offers a starkly different answer: "Who wants to be burned for anything? I'd have done what Galileo did—backed out of it" (038-074).

## Character Analysis

Mr. Rountree functions in the novel as a vehicle for dramatic irony. His self-image as a principled martyr—removing and replacing his glasses for rhetorical effect, eyes moistening with emotion at his own nobility—is gently deflated by the narrative's knowing aside and by Eugene's blunt pragmatism. The detail of Rountree "moistening spectacles" (038-072) is quintessential Wolfe: a small physical observation that undercuts grand rhetorical posturing.

The Rountree episode belongs to a broader pattern in the novel's university chapters, where philosophical abstractions are repeatedly tested against the messy reality of human self-interest. Eugene, who has by this point developed his "acceptable counterfeit" self (015-069), understands that the distance between proclaiming principles and acting on them is vast.
