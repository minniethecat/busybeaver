## Synopsis

George Graves is one of the most richly comic of Eugene Gant's schoolmates — a boy whose "slow ponderous rhythm" and gloomy, speculative temperament produce some of the novel's funniest dialogue. Walking home from school with Eugene, George Graves discourses with elaborate seriousness on topics ranging from the profitability of undertaking to the gold used in dental fillings, the wages of Catholic priests, and the precise resale value of the Paston family mansions. His conversation is a cascade of small-town rumor, adolescent materialism, and macabre fascination — a voice that captures the texture of boyhood in Altamont with extraordinary fidelity. Wolfe gives him a physical presence to match: he "ascended the hill with a slow ponderous rhythm, staring darkly at the ground," and his "great limbs" are "drained with laughter" after the boys' irreverent joking.

## Biography

George Graves appears in a single extended sequence — the after-school walks home from the private academy — but within that compressed space Wolfe renders him with remarkable fullness. The walk becomes a picaresque journey through the material and social landscape of Altamont, filtered through George's darkly acquisitive imagination.

The conversation that defines him begins, characteristically, with money — the estimated value of the Paston family's four large black houses:

> [024-123] "Not over $2,500, anyway," said George Graves gloomily.

His pessimism is comic but deeply felt. He moralizes about inheritance with "virtuous regret" ("It will all go to Gil Paston some day. He's not worth a damn"), then declares his religious affiliation with a pragmatism that undercuts any pretense of piety:

> [024-137] "An Episcopalian, when I go," said George Graves with irreverent laughter.

The walk becomes a kind of monetary inventory of death — George calculating the value of gold dental fillings, the profits of undertaking, the wages of priests. He speaks of mortality with the ghoulish fascination of adolescence, wondering whether the embalmed are "laid out in the room behind" Mr. Powell's establishment:

> [024-157] "I suppose," said George Graves thoughtfully, "the gold they use in people's teeth is worth a lot of money."

And:

> [024-161] "There's good money in undertaking," said George Graves. "Mr. Powell's well off."

The humor is never merely cynical. Wolfe leavens it with the physical comedy of adolescent friendship — Eugene embracing a telephone pole "erotically," George Graves leaning against it, his "great limbs drained with laughter." And there are moments of genuine moral sensitivity: when the joking turns too dark, George Graves draws back:

> [024-144] "We oughtn't to talk like that, 'Gene," said George Graves reproachfully. "Sure enough! It's not right."

The sequence ends with George's final dark speculation — that men buried alive "turned over on their faces" in the coffin — and his consoling afterthought that the embalming fluid "would kill you anyway." It is a perfect miniature of adolescent morbidity, rendered with Wolfe's characteristic blend of the grotesque and the tender.
