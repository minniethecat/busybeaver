## Synopsis

Malvin Bowden is one of Eugene's schoolmates at the private academy, but his most significant function in the novel is as a guide to a world of adult knowledge that Eugene has only begun to glimpse. It is Malvin's mother who organizes the Charleston tour — a school trip that becomes Eugene's first journey away from Altamont — and it is Malvin whose casual familiarity with beer, sex, and scandal ("she had a baby when she was eighteen," "a travelling man got her in trouble") represents a sophistication that Eugene can only observe with uneasy fascination. Wolfe draws Malvin with economical precision: the "dissipated leer" of his opening line, his eager knowledge of adult misadventure, his joyful leap from the trolley car on the Isle of Palms. He is one of those minor characters whose brief presence opens a window onto a larger world.

## Biography

Malvin Bowden's mother — referred to as Mrs. Bowden — is the organizer of the school tour to Charleston, and Malvin's presence on the trip marks him as both participant and insider. His first words in the novel establish his knowing, worldly manner:

> [026-003] "Yeah, man!" said Malvin Bowden, whose mother was conducting the tour. "You can still git beer in Charleston," he added with a dissipated leer.

The party consists of "Max Isaacs, Malvin Bowden, Eugene, two girls named Josie and Louise, and Mrs. Bowden" — a small constellation of adolescents adrift in a new city. On the morning of the trip, Malvin shares what he knows of the hotel's waitress, Louise — knowledge that carries the authority of local familiarity:

> [026-113] "I don't care," said Malvin Bowden, "she's no such thing. She's twenty-one. I reckon I ought to know. My folks have known her for five years. She had a baby when she was eighteen."

His account of Louise's past — "a travelling man got her in trouble. Then he ran away" — is delivered with the flat certainty of small-town gossip, neither condemnatory nor compassionate, simply factual. It is through Malvin's words that Eugene first learns the adult narrative that underlies his own encounter with Louise.

Throughout the trip, Malvin serves as a kind of running commentary on the scenes they pass through. He whispers about an "old lady" on the streetcar, he stares in silent incomprehension at the city's unfamiliar sights, and at the journey's end he leaps "joyously from the car" — a gesture of pure, uncomplicated boyhood that balances his earlier worldliness:

> [026-144] Malvin Bowden leaped joyously from the car.
