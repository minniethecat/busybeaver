## Synopsis

Van Yeats is a minor but warmly drawn figure in Eugene Gant's school years — a boy whose "quiet pleasant face" and stolid demeanor offer a counterpoint to the more boisterous personalities among Eugene's classmates. The son of a shoe merchant (the Van W. Yeats Shoe Company is glimpsed on a downtown street), Van Yeats belongs to the circle of boys at the private school who share walks home, horseplay, and the elaborate rituals of adolescent friendship. His most memorable moment comes later, when he and Julius Arthur discover Eugene drunk on the street and quietly, competently, get him home — a small act of loyalty that speaks to the bonds formed in those school years.

## Biography

Van Yeats first appears during the after-school walks home from the private academy, part of the knot of boys — George Graves, Julius Arthur, John Dorsey — who trade jokes, gossip, and speculation along the hillside streets of Altamont. His manner is understated, deliberate:

> [024-092] "Hey!" With high bounding exultancy Eugene came down the road to join them. Without turning, stolid Van Yeats threw up his hand impatiently and greeted the unseen with a cheer.

When the older boys tease Eugene, Van Yeats offers him quiet support:

> [024-111] "Don't let 'em climb your frame, son," said Van Yeats, turning his quiet pleasant face on Eugene. "If you need help, let me know."

The family business — the Van W. Yeats Shoe Company — is glimpsed in a passing street scene, anchoring the character in the commercial life of Altamont:

> [024-239] Across the street again, before the windows of the Van W. Yeats Shoe Company, the Reverend J. Brooks Gall, Amherst ('61)...

Van Yeats reappears later in the novel, after Eugene has grown older, in a scene that tests friendship. Eugene, drunken and reeling on the street, is discovered by Van Yeats and Julius Arthur outside Wood's pharmacy:

> [032-112] As he reached the entrance to Wood's pharmacy, a shout of laughter went up from the lounging beaux. The next instant he was staring into the friendly grinning faces of Julius Arthur and Van Yeats.

Without judgment or reproach, the two friends take charge:

> [032-117] Van Yeats propped him carefully against the wall; Julius Arthur ran swiftly into Church Street, and drew up in a moment at the curb. Eugene had a vast inclination to slump carelessly upon the nearest support. He placed his arms around their shoulders and collapsed.
