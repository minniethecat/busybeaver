## Synopsis

Dr. Jefferson Spaugh — whose full comic title is "Dr. Jefferson Reginald Alfonso Spaugh, the popular gin-ecologist" — is one of Wolfe's most economically devastating character sketches. A young physician in Altamont, Spaugh arrives at the morning drugstore gathering fresh from an all-night dance, foppishly drawing off his gloves, flicking his silk lapels, and complaining about his new patent-leather pumps. The older doctors — McGuire and Coker — demolish him with their shared sardonic wit, recounting how ten years earlier Spaugh had arrived in town so rough that "they had to throw him down to put shoes on him." The portrait is satiric but not cruel; Spaugh is a young man on the make in a small town, and his pretensions — the Buick roadster, the elegant manner, the social climbing — are rendered with the precision of someone who knows the type intimately.

## Biography

Spaugh makes his entrance in one of Wolfe's most vivid set-pieces, the morning gathering at the drugstore counter. He arrives from the opposite direction of respectability — not from a night's sleep but from a night's dancing, still in his evening clothes:

> [014-091] At the curb now, young Dr. Jefferson Spaugh brought his Buick roadster to a halt, and got out, foppishly drawing off his gloves and flicking the silk lapels of his dinner jacket. His face, whisky-red, congested with a rush of blood, shone with good-nature and a certain townsman's satisfaction.

His entrance is pure performance — a display of urbanity before the sardonic gaze of the older men. He takes his seat at the counter with exaggerated delicacy:

> [014-095] "I've been dancing all night at the Hilliards," said Spaugh elegantly. "Damn! These new patent-leather pumps have ruined my feet." He sat upon a stool, and elegantly displayed his large country feet, which were elegantly covered in lavender silk hose.

The older doctors are merciless. McGuire's retort is legendary:

> [014-101] "Patent-leather pumps!" said McGuire. "Hurt his feet. By God, Coker, the first time he came to town ten years ago he'd never been curried above the knees. They had to throw him down to put shoes on him."

Coker joins in, and the double-act dismantles Spaugh's pretensions with the precision of long practice. Yet Spaugh endures — he is good-natured, even eager to please. When McGuire's patient Ravenel appears, Spaugh offers his hotel room for the examination, "looking at Ravenel somewhat eagerly." He has his own medical wisdom to contribute:

> [014-160] "These mountain women take a lot of killing," said Jeff Spaugh sagely.

Spaugh reappears briefly in a later chapter, where Eliza sends for him during one of Gant's nighttime crises:

> [018-055] In the early hours of morning, at two or three o'clock, he would waken, and walk through the house weeping and entreating release. Eliza would send him to Spaugh at the hotel or to McGuire, at his residence on the other side of town.
