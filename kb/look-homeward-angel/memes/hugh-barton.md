# Hugh Barton

## Synopsis

Hugh Barton is Helen Gant's husband — a thin, courteous cash-register salesman who enters the Gant family circle with a Buick roadster, a keen stare, and a family of formidable women. His marriage to Helen draws him into the Gant dynasty's most intimate crises: Eliza's illness, Ben's death, and the endless gravitational pull of family obligation that defines the novel's domestic world.

## Biography

Hugh Barton enters the narrative in chapter 27 as Helen's suitor. Eliza describes him with characteristic reserve:

> (027-089) Then she told them about Mr. Hugh T. Barton, the cash register salesman. She spoke loyally and kindly of him, without great love.

His first visit to the Gant household is a spectacle. He arrives not on foot or by train but in a racing automobile, a herald of the new century:

> (027-109) Mr. Hugh Barton came up into the hills to visit his affianced kin. He came, to their huge delight, lounging in the long racing chassis of a dusty brown 1911 Buick roadster. He came, in a gaseous coil, to the roaring explosion of great engines.

The family is mesmerized. Hugh Barton's manner — deliberate, grave, faintly sardonic — projects an authority that the Gants find both impressive and faintly comic:

> (027-111) Hugh Barton bent slowly, courteously, from his thin waist. He fastened on Eliza a keen penetrating stare that discomposed her. His lips twisted again in an impressive sardonic smile. Everyone felt he was going to say something very, very important.

> (027-113) Everyone then felt that Hugh Barton had said something very, very important.

## Family Background

Hugh Barton comes from a line of formidable women — his mother, at seventy-four, has "the strength of a healthy woman of fifty, and the appetite of two of forty" (027-129). His sister, Mrs. Genevieve Selborne, casts a long shadow over his judgments:

> (027-132) "He was a beast," said Hugh Barton, "a low dog. He treated sister very badly."

This maternal-sororal dominance prefigures his marriage to Helen Gant, whose vitality and ferocity will prove more than a match for his own measured gravity.

## Marriage to Helen

The wedding is a Gant family production:

> (027-164) A flashlight: Hugh Barton and his bride limply astare — frightened; Gant, Ben, Luke, and Eugene, widely, sheepishly agrin; Eliza, high-sorrowful and sad; Mrs. Selborne and a smile of subtle mystery; the pert flower-girls; Pearl Hines' happy laughter.

After the wedding, the couple settles in Sydney, the state capital. Hugh Barton works as a salesman; Helen channels her enormous energy into domestic life. They occupy an ambiguous position in the family — close enough to be summoned for every crisis, distant enough to maintain their own household:

> (029-217) He went to see Helen and Hugh Barton several times. They lived thirty-five miles away at Sydney, the State capital. It was a town of thirty thousand people, sleepy, with quiet leafy pavements, and a capitol Square in the centre, with radial streets.

## Role in Family Crises

Hugh Barton is consistently present at the Gant family's worst moments — not as an instigator or a hero, but as a reliable, slightly exasperated participant. When Eugene's late-night visits strain the household:

> (030-118) "He has no consideration for you or anyone else," Hugh Barton growled. He had returned late from work at his office, to take Helen home. "If he can't do better than this, we'll find a house of our own. I'm not going to have you get down sick on account of it."

And when Ben lies dying:

> (035-006) "Ben's very sick," said Hugh Barton.

It is Hugh Barton who drives the family to Ben's deathbed:

> (035-013) Hugh Barton, who had always been a cautious driver, shot away with a savage grinding of gears. They roared townward through the rickety slums at fifty miles an hour.

> (035-024) "That's what they should have done at the start," growled Hugh Barton over his wheel.

## Character Assessment

Hugh Barton is one of Wolfe's most understated portraits — a decent man caught in the gravitational field of a family far more volatile, more passionate, and more doomed than his own. His caution, his courtesy, his automobile, and his growling resentments all mark him as a representative of the respectable middle-class world that the Gant children are simultaneously drawn toward and straining against. He is not a major figure, but his presence anchors several of the novel's key domestic scenes and provides a necessary counterpoint to Gantian excess.
