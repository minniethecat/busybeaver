## Synopsis

"Horse" Hines is Altamont's undertaker, a figure who haunts the margins of the town's life from the café conversations of the living to the funeral parlors of the dead. His nickname derives from his equine physiognomy — "his long lantern mouth split horsily in a professional smile displaying big horse teeth" [014-054] — but it also captures something of his social position: always present at moments of communal grief, yet never quite admitted to the inner circle of respectability he serves.

Hines appears across three distinct narrative registers. In the early chapters he is a fixture of the town's morning coffee-drinking ritual, where his professional cheerfulness is received with the same ironic tolerance as the other merchants' banter. In the pivotal Chapter 24, he is the undertaker who presides over the death of the unnamed old lady, his "boiled eye" [014-068] moistening with a grief that may or may not be genuine. And in the novel's closing movement (Chapter 36), he reappears to handle Ben Gant's funeral — the transaction that transforms him from comic background figure to an accidental custodian of the Gant family's central tragedy.

## Biography

### The Undertaker at the Morning Table

Hines is first glimpsed among the morning assembly of Altamont's businessmen, where his entrance is described with the narrator's characteristic fusion of the grotesque and the precise:

> [014-054] At this moment, Horse Hines, the undertaker, entered, producing, although he was not a thin man, the effect of a skeleton clad in a black frock coat. His long lantern mouth split horsily in a professional smile displaying big horse teeth in his white heavily starched shirt-front.

His professional identity precedes and overwhelms his personal presence — "producing the effect of a skeleton" even though he is not thin. The undertaker's trade clings to him like the black frock coat he wears, marking him as a liminal figure between the living commercial world of the café and the silent clientele he serves.

### Professional Sensitivities

Beneath the performance of professional joviality, Hines reveals a thin skin. When the café banter turns to jibes about embalming fluid and the undertaker's art, he is wounded:

> [014-117] Beneath his thin joviality Horse Hines was hurt.

His departure from the café — "'What's true then is true now,' said Horse Hines bitterly, as he left the place" [014-126] — registers a genuine bitterness. The town's willingness to employ him does not extend to granting him full membership in its social world; he is necessary but not welcome, essential but contaminating.

### The Undertaker at Work

Hines's professional domain is the Rogers-Malone Undertaking Establishment, where:

> [024-160] "Horse" Hines, tilted back in a swivel chair, with his feet thrust out on the broad window-ledge, chatted lazily with Mr. C. M. Powell, the suave silent partner.

This image — tilted back, feet on the ledge, chatting lazily — captures the peculiar rhythm of the undertaker's life: long stretches of idle waiting punctuated by intense periods of professional activity. Death is both his livelihood and his boredom.

### Ben Gant's Funeral

Hines's most significant narrative function comes in Chapter 36, where he handles the arrangements for Ben Gant's funeral. The transaction unfolds as a dark comedy of professional salesmanship meeting genuine grief:

> [036-129] "We thought we'd like to s-s-s-see you f-f-f-first, Mr. Hines, about the c-c-c-c-casket," Luke whispered nervously. "We're going to ask your advice. We want you to help us find something appropriate."

Hines navigates the Gant family's grief with practiced skill, extracting the maximum sale while maintaining the decorum of sympathetic service. Luke's stammer and the sailor's earnest insincerity ("We know you'll d-d-do your best, Mr. Hines" [036-110]) form a counterpoint to Hines's professional smoothness.

The scene culminates in an unexpected moment of authenticity:

> [036-177] "By God, Mr. Hines," said the sailor earnestly, as he wiped his eyes on his jacket, "that was one g-g-great boy."

The sailor's tears, and the simplicity of the tribute, momentarily pierce the commercial transaction. Hines, the professional manager of grief, becomes briefly the witness to it.

## Relationships

### The Gant Family

Hines's primary relationship is with the Gant family, for whom he serves as undertaker across at least two deaths — the unnamed old lady of Chapter 24 and Ben Gant in Chapter 36. His interactions with Luke and the sailor (Chapter 36) reveal the transactional intimacy of his role: he is paid to be present at moments no one else wishes to attend.

### Pearl Hines

Hines's daughter Pearl appears briefly in the narrative as "the saddlemaker's daughter" [012-035] and later as Helen Gant's traveling companion. Their relationship is suggested rather than dramatized — Pearl's brief elopement "off into the South" [018-104] with a musical partner hints at a domestic life that exists beyond the café and the funeral parlor.

### The Altamont Business Community

Hines is part of the morning coffee-drinking circle that includes Jannadeau the jeweler, Mr. Creech the butcher, and other Altamont merchants. His relationships within this group are characterized by professional banter and underlying tension — he is simultaneously one of them and apart from them, his trade setting him at a slight remove from the wholesome commerce of meat and jewelry.
