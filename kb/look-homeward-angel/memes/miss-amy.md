# Miss Amy

## Synopsis

Miss Amy is a teacher at Margaret Leonard's small private school, a figure of generous physicality and warm, sardonic intelligence who appears in several key scenes during Eugene's education. Wolfe describes her with characteristic attention to bodily presence — her "great arms," her "deep bosom," her "handsome gypsy eyes" — making her a figure of earthy womanly vitality in the otherwise austere and struggling atmosphere of Leonard's school. Her relationship with Eugene is defined by a knowing, affectionate contempt: she sees through his brilliance to the laziness beneath, and she loves him anyway.

## Role in the Novel

### The School at Leonard's (Chapter 24)

Miss Amy is introduced in the context of Leonard's struggling school, where she works alongside Margaret and John Dorsey Leonard:

> Leonard was fighting hard to keep his little school alive. He had less than twenty students the first year, and less than thirty the second. From an income of not more than $3,000 he had to pay Miss Amy, who had left a high school position to help him, a small salary. [024-025]

The detail that she "had left a high school position" is significant — it marks her commitment to the Leonards' educational experiment, a sacrifice of security for something more meaningful, if less remunerative. She is part of the small, embattled community that shapes Eugene's intellectual awakening.

### The Lazy Genius

Miss Amy's defining interactions with Eugene revolve around his paradoxical nature — brilliant but undisciplined, capable of anything but willing to do nothing. In a characteristic scene after school, she closes her grade book and regards him:

> Miss Amy closed her small beautifully kept grade book, thrust her great arms upward, and yawned. Eugene looked hopefully at her and out along the playing court, reddened by the late sun. He was wild, uncontrollable, erratic. His mad tongue leaped out in class. He could never keep peace a full day. He amazed them. They loved him, and they punished him piously, affectionately. He was never released at the dismissal hour. He was always "kept in." [024-036]

The contrast between Miss Amy's "small beautifully kept grade book" and her "great arms" captures her duality — the meticulous teacher and the expansive, physical woman. Eugene's "hopeful" look and the description of him as "wild, uncontrollable, erratic" establish their relationship in a single paragraph: he is the brilliant problem she cannot solve, and she is the warm, exasperated authority he cannot escape.

Her assessment of him is delivered with characteristic drawling contempt:

> "He can learn!" drawled Miss Amy. "He can learn anything. He's lazy — that's what it is. Just plain lazy." [024-045]

The drawl — Wolfe's dialogue attribution is precise — conveys a lifetime of Southern pedagogical experience, the weary recognition of talent squandered. But her words are also a form of praise, indirectly acknowledging the scope of Eugene's abilities even as she condemns his failure to use them.

Wolfe describes her gaze as possessing "handsome gypsy eyes" (024-049), an unusual adjective that suggests both beauty and a kind of exotic knowingness. She looks at Eugene and sees him clearly — more clearly, perhaps, than anyone at Leonard's except Margaret herself.

### The Smile

Miss Amy's most characteristic gesture is her smile, which Wolfe renders with a memorable phrase:

> Miss Amy smiled at him with indifferent sleepy cat-warmth. [024-056]

The oxymoronic quality of "indifferent sleepy cat-warmth" captures her essential nature: she is warm but not invested, affectionate but not sentimental. Like a cat, she regards Eugene with a knowing, slightly contemptuous fondness that demands nothing from him and offers nothing in return except her steady, unimpressed presence.

### The Medicine (Chapter 27)

Miss Amy makes a brief but vivid reappearance in Chapter 27, when Eugene is ill:

> "I'll fix you," said Miss Amy. "I've got just the thing for you." [027-016]

His response — "a distressed, puzzled face" (027-018) — suggests that Miss Amy's remedies, like her pedagogy, are administered with a force that brooks no refusal. Her reaction to his distress is laughter:

> Miss Amy looked. Her deep bosom heaved with full contralto laughter. [027-020]

The phrase "full contralto laughter" is pure Wolfe — synesthetic, bodily, musical. It is the laughter of a woman entirely at home in her own physicality, and it is the last image we have of Miss Amy in the novel: laughing, warm, and utterly unintimidated by the young genius before her.
