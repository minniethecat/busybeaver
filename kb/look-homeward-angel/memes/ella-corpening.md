## Synopsis

Ella Corpening is a minor but charged figure in *Look Homeward, Angel*, appearing in a single extended scene in Chapter 22 when Eugene Gant, working as a newspaper delivery boy, comes to her tenement in Niggertown to collect an overdue subscription payment. A striking mulatto woman of "Amazonian proportions," Ella becomes the conduit for Eugene's first direct sexual encounter, a scene rendered with Wolfe's characteristic blend of lush sensory detail and psychological intensity.

Ella's presence in the novel serves multiple functions: she embodies the allure and danger of Niggertown as a space of transgressive desire for the white boy Eugene; she is one of the few named Black characters in the novel; and her encounter with Eugene constitutes one of the most frank depictions of adolescent sexuality in early twentieth-century American fiction.

## Biography

Ella Corpening lives in a rickety three-story tenement in Niggertown, the Black section of Altamont. She is twenty-six years old, a mulatto with "smooth tawny skin," and she wears the cast-off garments of a former white mistress. Eugene seeks her out on a Saturday evening to collect $1.20 in overdue newspaper subscription fees:

> [022-065] One Saturday evening, in the fading red of a summer twilight, he returned to one of these tenements, a rickety three-story shack, that cropped its two lower floors down a tall clay bank at the western ledge, near the whites. Two dozen men and women lived here. He was on the search for a woman named Ella Corpening.

Her physical description is rendered in expansive detail:

> [022-066] Ella Corpening sat facing the door in a rocking chair, purring lazily in the red glow of a little kitchen range, with her big legs stretched comfortably out on the floor. She was a mulatto of twenty-six years, a handsome woman of Amazonian proportions, with smooth tawny skin.

> [022-067] She was dressed in the garments of some former mistress: she wore a brown woollen skirt, patent-leather shoes with high suede tops pearl-buttoned, and gray silk hose. Her long heavy arms shone darkly through the light texture of a freshly laundered white shirtwaist.

Ella tells Eugene she cannot pay because her "rent money" is already allocated, and promises payment the following morning when a "white gent'man" will give her a dollar. When Eugene asks what the man is paying for, her one-word answer becomes the scene's leitmotif:

> [022-081] "Jelly Roll," said Ella Corpening.

The encounter escalates swiftly. Ella locks the outer door, leads Eugene to an inner room with two rumpled beds, and, at his whispered request, undresses before him:

> [022-089] "Take off your clothes."
>
> [022-090] Her skirt fell in a ring about her feet. She took off her starched waist. In a moment, save for her hose, she stood naked before him. Her breath came quickly, her full tongue licked across her mouth.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Ella's relationship with Eugene is defined by the power dynamics of race, class, age, and sexuality. She is older (twenty-six to his approximately sixteen), Black in a segregated Southern town, and economically disadvantaged; yet in their encounter, she possesses a sexual knowingness that renders Eugene mute and trembling. Their transaction is ostensibly commercial — a newspaper collection — but quickly becomes erotic, initiated as much by Ella's knowing languor as by Eugene's desperate curiosity.

### The "White Gentleman"

Ella's unnamed white patron, who pays her a dollar presumably for sexual services, hovers at the margins of the scene. His existence underscores the economic and racial realities that structure Ella's life and, by extension, the encounter with Eugene.

## Critical Context

The Ella Corpening scene has been the subject of extensive critical discussion. Wolfe's treatment of race and sexuality in the Niggertown passages reflects both the racial attitudes of his time and his attempt to render, with unflinching honesty, the psychology of a white Southern boy's sexual awakening. Ella is simultaneously a fully realized sensual presence — "purring lazily in the red glow" — and a figure whose interiority remains largely inaccessible to the narrative, filtered as it is through Eugene's consciousness.

The scene participates in a broader pattern in the novel whereby Eugene's sexual initiation is mediated through encounters with women of lower social status — the country girls at Dixieland, the prostitutes of the town, and the women of Niggertown. Ella Corpening's "Amazonian proportions" and languid physicality make her one of Wolfe's most memorable minor characters.
