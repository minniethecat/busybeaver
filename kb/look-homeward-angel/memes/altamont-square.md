# Altamont Square

## Synopsis

The Square is the civic heart of Altamont — the physical and symbolic center around which the town's life revolves. Gant's marble shop occupies one edge; the City Hall, the fire station, and the drugstore face it from the others. The fountain plays at quarter-speed through winter ice; in summer it "dropped a fat spire of freezing water." The Square is where Oliver Gant strides through his daily rounds, where the old men gather to gossip, where the streetcar pauses before grinding up the hill — and it is the place Eugene must eventually leave behind if he is to become anything other than another Altamont ghost.

## Physical Description

The Square is rendered with Wolfe's characteristic fusion of precise observation and lyrical intensity. It is both a real place and a dreamscape:

> (007-033) The car reached the Square, jolted across the netting of north-south lines, and came to a halt on the north side, facing east. Scurfing a patch away from the glazed window, Gant looked out. The Square in the wan-gray frozen morning walled round him with frozen unnatural smallness.

In winter, the Square takes on a nightmare concreteness — a dream of hell in which a man sees his own name painted across a building:

> (007-035) The Square had the horrible concreteness of a dream. At the far southeastern edge he saw his shop: his name painted hugely in dirty scaly white across the brick near the roof: W. O. Gant — Marbles, Tombstones, Cemetery Fixtures. It was like a dream of hell, when a man finds his own name staring at him from the devil's ledger.

In spring and summer, the Square transforms:

> (009-033) More often, as Spring deepened, he went now to Gant's shop on the Square. He loved the scene: the bright hill-cooled sun, the blown sheets of spray from the fountain, the garrulous firemen emerging from the winter, the lazy sprawling draymen on his father's wooden steps, snaking their whips deftly at a passing fly.

> (020-008) On the Square the slackened fountain dropped a fat spire of freezing water into its thickening rim of ice. In summer, a tall spire blown in blue sheets of spray. When they turned it down it wilted — that was like a fountain, too.

## Social Life

The Square is Altamont's stage. It is where the young women walk:

> (019-005) Across the Square, at its other edge, the young virgins of the eastern part of town walked lightly home in chattering groups. They came to town at four o'clock in the afternoon, walked up and down the little avenue several times, entered a shop to purchase small justifications.

And where the old men gather to trade the day's rumors:

> (019-009) Gant spent delightful hours now in the gossip of dirty old men — their huddled bawdry exploded in cracked high wheezes on the Square. He came home at evening stored with gutter tidings, wetting his thumb and smiling slyly.

For young Eugene, the Square is a world unto itself, a place of sensory abundance:

> (009-052) And they would go across the Square to the cool depth of the drugstore, stand before the onyx splendor of the fountain, under the revolving wooden fans, and drink chill gaseous beverages, limeade so cold it made the head ache, or foaming ice-cream soda.

## Dream and Reality

Wolfe repeatedly renders the Square through a double lens: it is simultaneously the most concretely real place in Altamont and a site of hallucinatory intensity. In Gant's dying vision, the Square appears as a figure of cosmic order:

> (001-039) Then Oliver met Eliza. He lay one afternoon in Spring upon the smooth leather sofa of his little office, listening to the bright piping noises in the Square. A restoring peace brooded over his great extended body.

This oscillation between dreamlike enclosure and vivid sensory plenitude is central to the novel's treatment of place: Altamont is at once a prison and a paradise, and the Square is the axis around which both possibilities turn.
