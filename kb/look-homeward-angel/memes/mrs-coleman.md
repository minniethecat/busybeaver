# Mrs. Coleman

## Synopsis

Mrs. Coleman is a minor character in *Look Homeward, Angel*, one of the many boarding-house keepers who populate the social landscape of Altamont. She appears briefly in Chapter 14 during the marketplace scene, where the town's landladies prowl the stalls with "spying bargain-hunting eyes" (014-225). Her two lines of dialogue establish her as a figure of provincial pride, distinguishing herself from competitors by the social class of her clientele.

## Biography

Mrs. Coleman appears in the long marketplace set piece of Chapter 14, a virtuoso passage in which Thomas Wolfe catalogs the boarding-house keepers of Altamont as they inspect produce and exchange clipped, competitive pleasantries. The scene establishes the rigid social hierarchy among the town's accommodations:

> [014-225] Among the stalls of the market, the boardinghouse keepers of Altamont walked with spying bargain-hunting eyes and inquisitive nose.

When approached by [[mrs-ledbetter|Mrs. Ledbetter]] of the Belvedere, Mrs. Coleman responds to an inquiry about her occupancy with conspicuous self-satisfaction:

> [014-226] "I hear you're full up, Mrs. Coleman," said she inquiringly.

> [014-227] "O, I'm full up all the time," said Mrs. Coleman. "My people are all permanents, I don't want to fool with the transients," she said loftily.

Mrs. Coleman's distinction between "permanents" and "transients" encodes the social stratification of the boarding-house world. Her "permanents" are presumably established residents—businesspeople, teachers, or respectable widows—while "transients" denote the tourists and health-seekers ("lungers") who flooded Altamont each summer. Her "loftily" delivered pronouncement is immediately undercut by Mrs. Ledbetter's acid retort about "lungers who call themselves something else" (014-228), revealing the competitive insecurity beneath the surface of these exchanges.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Coleman is one of the many boarding-house keepers who collectively form a Greek chorus of Altamont's commercial and social life. Her brief appearance—two sentences of dialogue—is sufficient to sketch a complete personality: proud, status-conscious, and quietly competitive. The word "loftily" does the heavy lifting, transforming what might be a simple statement of fact into a social weapon. She is a minor figure in the novel's rich tapestry of minor figures, but her pride in her "permanents" illuminates the economics of respectability that governed the boarding-house world Eliza Gant herself inhabited.
