## Synopsis

Harry Tarkinton belongs to the small constellation of neighborhood boys who populate Eugene Gant's earliest childhood — the companions of Saturday mornings, recess periods, and the long, slow dissolution of friendships that comes with time and circumstance. The son of English immigrant parents who run a painting business, Harry is "three or four years older than Eugene, an awkward, heavy, muscular boy, smelling always of his father's paints and oils." He is remembered chiefly for one of Wolfe's more grotesque comic details: the infestation of his "thick locks" that became "the luxuriant colony of Harry Tarkinton's lice," which Eliza — superstitiously attached to Eugene's own long hair — refused to cut. By the time Eliza moves the family to Dixieland, the friendship has already begun to fray, and Harry fades from the novel as Eugene's world expands beyond the neighborhood.

## Biography

Harry Tarkinton first enters the novel as one of the "playmates" Eugene has found during the years of his early childhood, alongside Max Isaacs:

> [006-022] The children were growing up — Eugene had found playmates — Harry Tarkinton and Max Isaacs.

Wolfe's physical description of Harry is characteristically precise and vivid, capturing both the boy's working-class origins and his physical coarseness:

> [009-028] Harry Tarkinton's parents were English. He was three or four years older than Eugene, an awkward, heavy, muscular boy, smelling always of his father's paints and oils, coarse-featured, meaty sloping jaw and a thick catarrhal look about his nose and mouth.

Harry is a Baptist, like most of the neighborhood — a detail that places him in the social geography of Altamont:

> [011-091] Harry Tarkinton and Max Isaacs were Baptists, as were most of the people, the Scotch excepted, in Gant's neighborhood.

The most memorable — and grotesquely comic — detail associated with Harry is the lice infestation that becomes a proxy battle in Eliza's struggle to control Eugene's appearance:

> [008-039] Even when his thick locks had become the luxuriant colony of Harry Tarkinton's lice, she would not cut them.

The passage captures in miniature Eliza's stubbornness, the physical intimacy of childhood friendships, and the comic-grotesque register that Wolfe frequently employs in rendering the bodies of his minor characters.

Harry appears in scattered scenes — laughing hoarsely at one of Eugene's nervous tics, part of the Baptist Sunday-school world — but as Eliza's departure for Dixieland disrupts the neighborhood's established patterns, the childhood friendships loosen:

> [015-071] That Spring he was more alone than ever. Eliza's departure for Dixieland three or four years before, and the disruption of established life at Gant's, had begun the loosening of his first friendships with the neighborhood boys, Harry Tarkinton, Max Isaacs.
