# Art and Creation

## Definition

The hunger to create — to carve stone into angels, to wrestle language into meaning — runs through every generation of the Gant family as both a gift and a curse. Oliver Gant's stonecutting is the novel's primal image of creation: the craftsman bent over marble, "wreaking something dark and unspeakable in him into cold stone." Eugene inherits this hunger not for stone but for words, and the novel itself is the fulfillment of Oliver's unrealized dream — the angel's head carved not from marble but from memory and language.

Wolfe presents art-making as a doomed but necessary striving. The artist can never fully realize the vision — Oliver never carves his angel's head — yet the attempt itself is what gives life dignity. Creation is the response to loss, the effort to preserve what time erodes, and the only immortality available to mortals.

## Textual Evidence

The boy Oliver Gant first feels the creative hunger in a Baltimore shop window — a desire to carve that borders on the violent:

> (001-007) He felt that he wanted, more than anything in the world, to carve delicately with a chisel. He wanted to wreak something dark and unspeakable in him into cold stone. He wanted to carve an angel's head.

But the goal eludes him. The angel's head remains uncarved:

> (001-009) He never found it. He never learned to carve an angel's head. The dove, the lamb, the smooth joined marble hands of death, and letters fair and fine — but not the angel.

The stone shop becomes the daily ritual of creation — Gant bent over his work, polishing marble with pumice and water:

> (009-051) As Eugene entered, if Gant were busy on a stone, he would say gruffly, "Hello, son," and continue with his work, until he had polished the surface of the marble with pumice and water.

The shop itself is layered with stone-dust — the physical residue of creation:

> (009-034) Behind a wooden partition was his ware-room, layered with stone-dust — coarse wooden trestles on which he carved inscriptions, stacked tool-shelves filled with chisels, drills, mallets, a pedalled emery wheel.

Gant's great hands are instruments of creation — "moulding" marble the way a god might mould worlds:

> (002-005) Men saw him plunge to house and shop, they saw him bent above his marbles, they saw him mould in his great hands — with curse, and howl, with passionate devotion — the rich textures of the stone.

The angel itself comes from Carrara, Italy — linking the small-town stonecutter to the great tradition of Renaissance marble carving:

> (019-064) But it had come from Carrara in Italy, and it held a stone lily delicately in one hand.

Eugene's creative inheritance emerges through language rather than stone. He devours Shakespeare and feels the writer's hunger — the need to capture the world in words, as his father tried to capture it in stone.

## Analysis

The novel presents art as a form of alchemy — the transformation of pain into beauty, of the transient into the permanent. Gant's stonecutting is not a trade but a vocation, driven by the same force that animates Eugene's writing. Both are attempts to answer "O lost!" with something that endures.

But Wolfe does not romanticize the artistic life. Gant's shop is dusty, fly-specked, and his angel is "a White Elephant" that "impoverished" him. Creating is costly — it consumes the creator. Yet not creating is worse: it is to surrender to the nothingness that threatens every character.

The father-son transmission of the creative impulse is crucial. Oliver cannot carve the angel's head, but Eugene can write the novel — and in doing so, he completes his father's quest. The novel *Look Homeward, Angel* is the angel's head, carved not from Italian marble but from memory, language, and the hunger that Oliver passed down.
