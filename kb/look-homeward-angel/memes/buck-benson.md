## Synopsis

Edward Pettigrew "Buck" Benson is the Greek professor at Pulpit Hill and one of Eugene Gant's most distinguished and influential teachers during his first year at the state university. A bachelor in his mid-forties, Benson is characterized by his dandified dress, his lazy drawl, his devastating wit, and his immense personal charm. Through Benson's instruction, Eugene first encounters and is transformed by the poetry of Homer — an experience that becomes one of the novel's most lyrical passages of intellectual awakening.

Benson represents the cultivated, cosmopolitan sensibility that stands in stark contrast to the provincialism of Altamont and the state university's political machine. In Wolfe's portrait, Benson is the ideal teacher: demanding yet forgiving, caustic yet warm, and capable of opening worlds for his students.

## Biography

Buck Benson is introduced as Eugene's most distinguished instructor in his first year:

> [028-089] By far the most distinguished of his teachers this first year was Mr. Edward Pettigrew ("Buck") Benson, the Greek professor. Buck Benson was a little man in the middle-forties, a bachelor, somewhat dandified, but old-fashioned, in his dress. He wore wing collars, large plump cravats, and suede-topped shoes. His voice was low, lazy, pleasant, with an indolent drawl, but without changing its pace or its inflection he could flay a victim with as cruel a tongue as ever wagged, and in the next moment wipe out hostility, restore affection, heal all wounds by the same agency. His charm was enormous.

Benson's scholarship is described as elegant and indolent — he is the genuine article, a man who knows Greek deeply rather than mechanically:

> [028-091] He was a good Grecian—an elegant indolent scholar. Under his instruction Eugene began to read Homer. The boy knew little grammar—he had learned little at Leonard's—but, since he had had the bad judgment to begin Greek under someone other than Buck Benson, Buck Benson thought he knew even less than he did.

Benson's signature pedagogical style combines sharp criticism with genuine encouragement. When Eugene performs unexpectedly well on an examination, Benson's response is characteristic:

> [028-093] But, on the examination, he gave an excellent performance, and translated from sight beautifully. He was saved. Buck Benson commended his paper publicly with lazy astonishment, and gave him a fair grade. Thereafter, they slipped quickly into an easier relation: by Spring, he was reading Euripides with some success.

The most lasting gift Benson gives Eugene is the introduction to Homer. The experience becomes one of the novel's lyrical high points — the sound of the Homeric hexameter fused forever with Eugene's memory of his father's parlor shells:

> [028-094] But that which remained most vividly, later, in the drowning years which cover away so much of beauty, was the vast sea-surge of Homer which beat in his brain, his blood, his pulses, as did the sea-sound in Gant's parlor shells, when first he heard it to the slowly pacing feet and the hexametrical rhythm.

## Relationships

### Eugene Gant

Benson's relationship with Eugene follows a classic arc: initial skepticism, grudging respect, and eventual warm mentorship. He challenges Eugene to stop relying on translations and do his own work, then publicly celebrates his success. Through Benson, Eugene's literary imagination is permanently enriched by the Greek epic tradition.

### Pulpit Hill Faculty

Benson stands apart from the rest of the Pulpit Hill faculty by virtue of his charisma and genuine scholarly distinction. Wolfe draws a contrast between Benson's authentic cultivation and the pedantry or political careerism of other professors, making him one of the few faculty members presented with unqualified admiration.

## Teaching Legacy

Benson's Homer class proves to be one of Eugene's formative intellectual experiences. The Wolfean prose that celebrates the "vast sea-surge of Homer" stands as one of the novel's most famous passages of aesthetic rapture, and Benson is its catalyst. The professor represents the ideal of liberal education — not the accumulation of grammatical rules, but the transmission of beauty across millennia.

> [028-095] Dwaney de clangay genett, argereoyo beeoyo—above the whistle's shriek, the harsh scream of the wheel, the riveter's tattoo, the vast long music endures, and ever shall. What dissonance can quench it? What jangling violence can disturb or conquer it—entombed in our flesh when we were young, remembered now!
