## Role in the Narrative

Cara Carozza is a dancer at the Bonbonnière cabaret and one of Mabuse's most trusted accomplices. She functions as the personal link between Mabuse's criminal network and the wealthy stockbroker Edgar Hull, whom she meets through her performances at the Bonbonnière. Hull is drawn to her, and Mabuse exploits this connection to draw Hull into the rigged card game that ruins him. Wenk identifies her as a critical node in the conspiracy:

> "She knows me, then!" said Wenk to himself in astonishment. "But how could that come about, except through Cara Carozza? A State official, guardian and representative of the law, and avenger of any breach of it, himself violating its rules! It was absolutely fantastic. Yes, it must have been the Carozza girl." (003-043)

As the investigation progresses, Carozza becomes an object of surveillance. Wenk tracks her movements and notes her influence over Hull: "Wenk noticed Cara Carozza's auburn-tressed head behind" Hull at the club (003-013). She is ultimately arrested and imprisoned as a suspect in Hull's murder. Even from prison she remains significant to the plot — Wenk considers interrogating her for information about Mabuse's network, and the Countess Told is recruited to pose as a fellow inmate to extract a confession ((008-050), (008-058)).

Her narrative arc ends tragically: she is used by Mabuse as a tool of seduction and manipulation, and when she is arrested, her fate hangs in the balance between rescue and abandonment. The text describes her as "wholly given over" to Mabuse (003-007), suggesting not merely loyalty but a kind of psychological possession that defines her character throughout the novel.

## Biography / Background

Cara Carozza is introduced as "Mdlle. Cara Carozza, of the Bonbonnière" (002-006) — a performer whose stage identity provides a public-facing role. She is described as "partly Mexican" (001-076) and is known for her "jazz dances" at the Bonbonnière (001-076). Her physical appearance is noted in the text: she has an "auburn-tressed head" (003-013), a detail that marks her in crowd scenes at the gambling club.

From the town register, Wenk discovers her true identity: "Maria Strümpfli, formerly Essert, known as Cara Carozza, dancer, born in Brunn, May 1, 1892, arrived in Munich from Copenhagen" (008-020). This revelation — that the exotic "Mexican" dancer is in fact a Central European woman who has reinvented herself — mirrors the theme of disguise and false identity that runs through the entire novel.

Her relationship with Mabuse is both romantic and operational. She is referred to as his "chère amie" (002-005), and Mabuse drives to see her immediately after learning of Wenk's investigation (002-034). She is fully aware of and complicit in his schemes, gathering disguises for operations and serving as a decoy to draw wealthy victims into Mabuse's gambling traps.

## Key Appearances

- Chapter I — Performs jazz dances at the Bonbonnière, where she is introduced as a figure in Hull's orbit (001-076)
- Chapter II — Wenk is introduced to her (002-043); she is observed fully under Mabuse's influence (002-050)
- Chapter III — Wenk notices her with Hull at the club; her presence signals the connection between the entertainment world and Mabuse's schemes (003-013)
- Chapter IV — Advances toward Wenk as he holds a revolver, creating a direct physical confrontation (004-029)
- Chapter V — Gathers her disguise garments, preparing for action in Mabuse's network (005-035)
- Chapter VIII — Wenk reflects on her significance to the case (008-004); her identity and relationship to Mabuse are formally stated (008-050); Countess Told is recruited to infiltrate her prison cell (008-058)
- Chapter IX — Prison inmates describe her: "Mabuse's sweetheart" (009-002)
- Chapter XI — Wenk learns of Mabuse's order to use Carozza for Hull: "The Carozza girl can contrive it" (011-018)
- Chapter XII — Wenk asks Count Told about her, and she confronts the Countess in prison (012-021)
- Chapter XVI — Her demanded time having expired, setting temporal pressure on the plot (016-028)

## Relationships

- **Dr. Mabuse** (`dr-mabuse`): Her lover and criminal master. She is his "chère amie" and trusted accomplice, executing his instructions at the Bonbonnière and beyond. Mabuse's order — "The Carozza girl can contrive it" (011-018) — confirms her operational role in his network. Yet she is ultimately expendable to him, and the question of whether he will rescue her from prison becomes a test of his loyalty.
- **Edgar Hull** (`edgar-hull`): The wealthy stockbroker she seduces and manipulates on Mabuse's behalf. Their relationship is transactional — she is his "chère amie" and he is her mark — but Hull's genuine infatuation complicates the dynamic.
- **von Wenk** (`von-wenk`): The state prosecutor who identifies her as the key link in Mabuse's conspiracy. Their antagonism is direct: she confronts him physically and later glares at him "full of hate" during questioning (008-033).
- **Karstens** (`karstens`): A fellow accomplice in Mabuse's network who facilitates social introductions and accompanies her and Hull to gambling venues.
- **George** (`george`): Mabuse's chauffeur and enforcer, with whom she coordinates in the execution of the Hull murder plot (011-018).

## Analysis

Cara Carozza occupies the role of the *femme fatale* in Mabuse's criminal apparatus — a performer whose beauty and charm serve as instruments of manipulation. She is the conduit through which Hull, the wealthy victim, is drawn into the gambling trap. Her position at the Bonbonnière — a legitimate entertainment venue — allows her to move between respectable society and the underworld, much as Mabuse himself moves between identities. The narrator describes the effect of her presence on Hull:

> A few days afterwards Hull made acquaintance with a girl who was performing jazz dances in the Bonbonnière. She was partly Mexican, she told him. She soon effected a diversion in his thoughts, and in her company he rapidly got rid of the twenty thousand marks which he could not pay over to the stranger. (001-076)

This passage reveals Carozza's function in Mabuse's scheme: she is the "diversion" that displaces Hull's anxiety about the lost money, replacing it with a new obsession that Mabuse can exploit. Her supposed Mexican heritage is part of the exotic mystique that makes her alluring — and, as Wenk later discovers from the town register, it is entirely fabricated. The real Maria Strümpfli from Brunn is as much a constructed identity as any of Mabuse's disguises.

Her relationship with Mabuse is significant for what it reveals about his hold over his accomplices. The text describes her as "wholly given over" to him (002-050), and even after his hypnotic command to her "had exhausted its effect" she remains bound to him by loyalty and fear:

> And Cara Carozza gathered her disguising garments about her and went away in grief, downcast and heavyhearted, to sacrifice herself for him. (005-041)

Yet she is not a passive tool — she acts independently, confronting Wenk directly and gathering disguises for operations. Her imprisonment creates a pivotal tension in the plot, as Mabuse must decide whether to risk rescuing her or abandon her to the authorities. The hardness she adopts under interrogation — "suddenly hardening her attitude" (008-030) — suggests an inner resilience that belies the image of the helpless accomplice.

## Imprisonment and Interrogation

Cara Carozza's imprisonment marks a turning point in the narrative. Wenk's strategy of placing Countess Told in her cell as a fellow inmate is designed to exploit her isolation and fear. The Countess greets her with feigned warmth:

> "What, *you* here, my dear! But we know each other! What a strange coincidence!" (012-023)

The prison scenes reveal Carozza's psychological complexity. She is at once defiant and vulnerable — glaring at Wenk with hatred during questioning, yet fainting when she learns of Hull's murder: "Hull murdered! …" she exclaimed, and she sank fainting from the chair to the ground" (008-035). Her reaction to Hull's death is ambiguous: is it guilt, shock, or the realization that her own position has become far more dangerous than she anticipated?

The question of whether Mabuse will rescue her hangs over the later chapters. She has risked everything for him, but Mabuse's criminal calculus is coldly pragmatic, and Carozza's value to him now lies only in what she might confess to the authorities.
