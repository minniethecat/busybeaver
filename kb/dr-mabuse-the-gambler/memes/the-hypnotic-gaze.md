# The Hypnotic Gaze

The gaze is the primary vehicle of [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]]'s hypnotic power. Before he speaks, before he gestures, before he deploys any of the other instruments of his psychological arsenal, he looks — and the look itself is the mechanism of entry, the point at which his consciousness makes contact with the consciousness of his victim. The hypnotic gaze is the novel's most concentrated image of Mabuse's power: the eyes that meet the victim's eyes, the will that flows through the channel of vision, the self that is invaded through the aperture of the look.

## The Gaze as Technique

The hypnotic gaze is a technique — something Mabuse has cultivated through practice and can deploy at will. It is not a supernatural power but a skill, and the novel's treatment of it reflects the early twentieth-century understanding of hypnosis as a technique that could be learned, refined, and applied. Mabuse's gaze is effective not because he possesses occult powers but because he has mastered the psychological mechanisms through which the gaze can influence the mind.

## The Gaze as Power

The gaze is also a form of power — perhaps the most fundamental form, the power to look and, through looking, to affect what is looked at. The person who commands the gaze commands attention; the person who commands attention commands the first condition of influence. Mabuse's hypnotic gaze is the limit case of this general principle: the look that does not merely command attention but enters and redirects the consciousness that attends to it.

## The Gaze and the Victim

For the victim, the hypnotic gaze is experienced as a loss of agency — a progressive dissolution of the boundary between self and other, between the will that looks and the will that is looked at. [[edgar-hull|Edgar Hull]] experiences it as a "veil" that descends over his mental vision (004-002); [[countess-dusy-told|Countess Told]] experiences it as a "strange tumult of feeling" (010-016). The gaze that enters the victim's consciousness does not announce itself as an invasion; it presents itself as a sensation, an atmosphere, a change in the quality of experience that the victim cannot attribute to an external cause.

> The Countess had left the Privy Councillor's house in a strange tumult of feeling. The stranger who had so impressed her had suddenly disappeared, but his forceful personality had left its mark, and she could not free herself of it. This mysterious and compelling power of his effaced the image of Wenk, and the latter receded into the background.
>
> (010-016)

## The Gaze and the Cinema

Lang's [[dr-mabuse-the-gambler-1922|film adaptation]] would exploit the cinematic potential of the hypnotic gaze, using close-ups of Rudolf Klein-Rogge's eyes, superimpositions of Mabuse's face over the gambling scene, and the other visual resources of silent cinema to make the gaze the film's central image of power. The cinematic gaze — the camera that looks, the spectator who looks at the screen, the actor who looks into the camera — is the film's translation of the novel's hypnotic gaze, and the translation is remarkably effective.
