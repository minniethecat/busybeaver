## Description

Stuttgart appears in the narrative as a provincial German city south of the main action, functioning as a node in Mabuse's extended network of agents and commercial operations. It is referenced in connection with business dealings, travel routes, and Mabuse's escape itinerary. The city is not described in topographic detail but is invoked as a known geographical point within the German landscape of the early 1920s, valued by Mabuse's network for its position on key transportation arteries and its commercial infrastructure.

## Role in the Narrative

Stuttgart plays a logistical and commercial role in Mabuse's operations. Early in the story, Mabuse instructs an associate to "inquire of Meggers in Stuttgart about the sales" (005-104) in the context of a large-scale meat speculation scheme — a scheme involving the purchase of over a thousand head of cattle across the Bavarian-Württemberg price divide. This reference reveals that Mabuse's network maintains commercial agents in cities across Germany, not merely in Munich.

The strategic importance of Stuttgart deepens during Mabuse's flight. As the net closes around him, Stuttgart becomes a critical waypoint:

> Mabuse wanted to get to Stuttgart. His agents there had telephoned the previous day that a patient wanted to consult him. That meant that they had got hold of a rich man worth plucking. (014-045)

Even while fleeing, Mabuse's network continues to operate — Stuttgart agents identify new victims, and the city serves as a communication and coordination hub. During the final pursuit, Mabuse spends "the night in a village south of Stuttgart" (019-019), and the following day his chauffeur Spoerri is dispatched to "go to Stuttgart and buy, whatever the price may be, a two-seater aeroplane" (019-041). From Stuttgart, Mabuse flies onward to Berlin: "On the morning of the following day Mabuse flew with her from Stuttgart to Berlin" (019-034). Stuttgart thus serves as the critical transition point — from rural hiding, to aerial escape, to the final confrontation in the capital.

## Real-World Context

Stuttgart, the capital of Württemberg, was a major industrial and commercial center in Weimar-era Germany. Its position on key railway and road routes between Munich, the Swiss border (via Bregenz), and northern Germany made it a natural transit hub. The city's commercial character — home to manufacturing firms and trading houses — aligns with the narrative's depiction of Stuttgart as a place of business contacts and logistical coordination. The 1920s also saw the early development of commercial aviation in Germany, making the purchase of an aeroplane in a city like Stuttgart a plausible detail.

## Significance

The escape plan accelerates as Mabuse commands Spoerri:

> "There is just one thing left for me to do in Germany, in Europe … and that is to get hold of that lawyer, the State Attorney, Wenk, alive. I want him alive, mark you! as much alive as a fly under a glass. The Countess and I are staying here tomorrow. You will go to Stuttgart and buy, whatever the price may be, a two-seater aeroplane."
>
> (019-020)


Stuttgart's role in the narrative is modest but structurally revealing: it demonstrates the geographical reach of Mabuse's criminal enterprise. The network is not confined to a single city but spans Germany, with agents in provincial centers who can be activated for commercial dealings, communication, and escape logistics. Stuttgart's position on Mabuse's south-to-north escape route — village south of Stuttgart → Stuttgart → Berlin — maps the geography of flight in the narrative, tracing the path from rural hiding place to urban anonymity. The progression from commercial agent (Meggers) to logistical hub (aeroplane purchase) to departure point reflects the escalation of Mabuse's mobility needs as pursuit intensifies, and it underscores the decentralized, resilient nature of his criminal organization.
