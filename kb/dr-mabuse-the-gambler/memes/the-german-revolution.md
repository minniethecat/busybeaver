# The German Revolution of 1918–1919

The German Revolution of 1918–1919 — the revolutionary upheaval that forced the abdication of Kaiser Wilhelm II, established the Weimar Republic, and was marked by violent conflict between socialist revolutionaries and right-wing paramilitaries — is the immediate political context within which *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* was written and published. The revolution is not depicted directly in the novel, but its effects are everywhere: in the dissolution of traditional authority, in the anxiety about social disorder, in the sense that the old rules no longer apply and the new rules have not yet been established.

## The Revolution's Aftermath

By the time Jacques published his novel in 1921, the most violent phase of the revolution was over, but its consequences were still unfolding. The Weimar Republic had been established, but its legitimacy was contested from both left and right. The economic crisis — inflation, unemployment, the burden of reparations — was gathering force. The social fabric had been torn by war and revolution, and the institutions that might have mended it were themselves weakened and delegitimized.

This is the world into which Mabuse emerges — a world of broken institutions, contested authority, and pervasive anxiety. The novel's depiction of a society in which the traditional frameworks of order have collapsed and the new frameworks have not yet been established is a depiction, at one level, of post-revolutionary Germany. Mabuse flourishes in this world because it is a world that has been prepared for him.

## Revolution and Crime

The novel suggests a connection between revolutionary upheaval and criminal flourishing that is not simply reactionary. The revolution has not created crime — crime existed before the revolution — but it has created conditions in which crime can operate with unprecedented freedom. The police are distracted, the courts are overwhelmed, the public is focused on political threats rather than criminal ones. The revolution has opened a space for criminal enterprise that Mabuse has been quick to occupy.

This connection between political upheaval and criminal opportunity is not a moral judgment on the revolution but a structural observation: periods of rapid social change create vulnerabilities that criminal enterprises can exploit, and the period following the German Revolution was, in this respect, no different from other periods of revolutionary transition.

## The Novel as Political Document

The novel is not, in any straightforward sense, a political document — it does not advocate for a particular political program or take sides in the conflicts of its moment. But it is, in a broader sense, a political novel: a work that registers the political conditions of its moment and traces their effects on the lives of its characters. The German Revolution is the absent cause of much that happens in the novel, and the novel's depiction of social crisis is, among other things, a depiction of the political crisis that the revolution had inaugurated.
