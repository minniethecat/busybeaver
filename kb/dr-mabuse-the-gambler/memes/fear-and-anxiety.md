# Fear and Psychological Anxiety

## Definition

Fear in *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* is not merely a reaction to danger but the fundamental atmosphere in which the characters exist. It is at once personal — the terror of the individual victim confronting Mabuse's hypnotic gaze — and collective, the free-floating anxiety of a society that has lost its moral and economic bearings.

## Manifestation in the Text

The novel opens in an atmosphere of unease. Von Wenk senses that something is wrong in the city before he can articulate what it is. His investigation is driven not by a specific crime but by a diffuse sense of threat: "He saw that a danger to the people lay in gambling" (002-019). The danger is not yet visible; it must be uncovered, tracked, named.

> The first idea which occurred to him then was that henceforth his life was at stake. He accepted it calmly, for since he was combating evil, it was natural that it should be so. The conflict would be played out on the borderland between existence and annihilation. Such men are like beasts escaped from a menagerie, and it was his task, his duty, the justification for his existence, to help to make them powerless for evil. There must be no fear of men, no fear of the body and its destruction.
>
> (006-007)

Mabuse's power is fundamentally the power to inspire fear. His victims do not merely lose money — they lose their sense of agency, their confidence in their own perceptions. Hull, after his ruin, is a hollowed-out figure, his will destroyed along with his fortune. The fear Mabuse generates is not the fear of physical violence but the deeper terror of discovering that one's own mind is not one's own.

The Countess Told's storyline articulates a specifically feminine experience of anxiety. Drawn to the gambling dens by a compulsion she cannot explain, trapped in a marriage that offers no fulfillment, she embodies the existential terror of a life lived under invisible constraints. Her séances are attempts to contact a dimension beyond the oppressive reality of her daily existence.

The climax of the novel involves a confrontation in which Wenk must overcome his own fear to face Mabuse directly. The recognition scene — Wenk stripping away Mabuse's disguises with his eyes — is a moment of triumph over the anxiety that has pervaded the entire narrative. But the victory is psychological, not physical: fear is overcome by the act of seeing clearly.

## Context

The early 1920s saw the emergence of anxiety as a central theme in European culture. Freud's work on the uncanny, Heidegger's analysis of *Angst* in *Being and Time* (1927), and the expressionist movement in art and cinema all registered a profound cultural unease. The war had shattered the nineteenth century's faith in progress; the peace had brought inflation, political violence, and a pervasive sense that the ground had shifted beneath everyone's feet.

## Significance

The novel's achievement is to transform this diffuse cultural anxiety into a concrete dramatic form. Mabuse is fear personified — not a supernatural monster but a human being who has understood that fear is the most powerful weapon, more effective than violence because it never leaves a mark. His defeat is provisional because the conditions that produce such figures — economic chaos, institutional collapse, the terrifying fluidity of modern identity — have not been defeated.
