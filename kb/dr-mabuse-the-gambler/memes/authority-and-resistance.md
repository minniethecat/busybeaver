# Authority and Resistance

Authority in *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* comes in multiple forms, and the relationship between them is a central concern of the novel's political imagination. [[von-wenk|Von Wenk]] exercises legal-rational authority — the authority of the office, the procedure, the institution. [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]] exercises charismatic authority — the authority of personal magnetism, psychological power, and the capacity to compel obedience through influence rather than law. The novel stages a contest between these forms of authority and traces the possibilities and limits of resistance to each.

## The Varieties of Authority

The novel distinguishes among several types of authority. Legal authority derives from the state and operates through procedures, warrants, and the legitimate use of force. Charismatic authority derives from personal qualities and operates through influence, fascination, and the suspension of critical judgment. Traditional authority derives from social position — the aristocrat, the father, the master — and operates through custom, expectation, and the internalization of hierarchy. Each type of authority commands a different kind of obedience, and each is vulnerable to a different kind of resistance.

## The Limits of Resistance

Resistance to authority, in the novel's world, is difficult and often futile. Mabuse's operatives cannot resist his authority without risking imprisonment or death. Von Wenk's subordinates cannot resist his authority without jeopardizing their careers. The gambler cannot resist the authority of the game without acknowledging that the game is rigged — and that acknowledgment would require recognizing that one has been a victim, which is psychologically difficult. The novel documents these limits without endorsing them, tracing the mechanisms through which authority secures compliance and the costs that resistance exacts.

> He knew that none were more responsible for the existing state of affairs than these men, because, at a time when resistance was a necessity, they had been cowardly and kept out of the way. But he knew, too, that in them a new force of decision had come to birth.
>
> (002-028)

## The Investigator's Resistance

Von Wenk's investigation is itself a form of resistance — a refusal to accept the authority that Mabuse exercises over Munich's underworld. But the investigation is also an exercise of authority — the deployment of the state's power against those who challenge it. The novel's treatment of von Wenk thus complicates any simple opposition between authority and resistance: the investigator resists one form of authority by exercising another, and the boundary between resistance and authority blurs under the pressure of circumstances.

## Significance

Authority and resistance function in the novel as a political diagnostic. The contest between von Wenk's legal authority and Mabuse's charismatic authority is a contest between two models of social organization — one based on law and procedure, the other based on personality and influence. The novel's resolution — von Wenk's partial victory — is a victory for legal authority, but it is a victory that required von Wenk to supplement his legal authority with charismatic qualities, and this supplement is the novel's acknowledgment that legal authority, in its pure form, may be insufficient to the challenges it faces.
