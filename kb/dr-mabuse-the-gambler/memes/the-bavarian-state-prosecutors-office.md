# Bavarian State Prosecutor's Office

The Bavarian State Prosecutor's Office is the institutional seat of [[von-wenk|von Wenk]]'s legal authority — the space where the investigation is officially headquartered and where the procedural framework that governs it is most keenly felt. It is a space of files and forms, of legal protocols and institutional hierarchies, of the bureaucratic apparatus that translates individual investigative efforts into the formal language of the law.

## The Office as Institution

The office is the spatial expression of von Wenk's institutional identity. Here he is not the undercover operative or the disguised infiltrator but the State Prosecutor — the official whose actions must be justified within the legal framework that his office represents. The office is where warrants are requested, where evidence is filed, where the procedural requirements of the legal system are satisfied. It is the space that connects the investigation to the larger apparatus of the state.


Wenk discusses his professional domain with fellow travellers:

> Berlin, as the hotbed of crime, afforded a good field of study for a Munich criminal prosecutor. He touched lightly on the existence of Mabuse, though without naming him, and related some of his bold and shameless crimes.
>
> (019-065)
## The Office and the Field

The tension between the office and the field is one of the investigation's structuring dynamics. The office represents procedure, legality, institutional constraint; the field represents improvisation, risk, the willingness to operate outside established frameworks. Von Wenk moves between these spaces — from the office where he reviews files to the streets where he conducts surveillance, from the institutional identity of the prosecutor to the disguised identity of the undercover operative — and the movement between them is the movement of his transformation.

## The Limits of the Office

The office is also the space where the investigation's limitations are most apparent. The legal framework that the office represents — the requirements of evidence, the standards of procedure, the protections afforded to suspects — was designed for a world of conventional crime, and it is inadequate to the novel forms of criminality that Mabuse represents. The office can process the arrests of operatives and the seizure of gambling apparatus, but it cannot reach the mastermind who operates outside the categories that the office was designed to address.
