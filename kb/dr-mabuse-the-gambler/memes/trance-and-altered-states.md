# Trance and Altered States of Consciousness

The hypnotic trance is the technical core of [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]]'s power. It is the altered state of consciousness through which he suspends the will of his victims and inserts his own commands in place of their volition. The novel's representation of trance draws on early twentieth-century medical and popular understandings of hypnosis — a field that was, in 1921, simultaneously a legitimate therapeutic practice, a subject of serious scientific investigation, and a staple of stage entertainment. Mabuse exploits this ambiguity, using the prestige of medical hypnosis to disguise the coercive nature of his practice.

## Phenomenology of Trance

The novel describes the trance state with clinical precision. Victims experience a progressive dissolution of agency: first a "veil" descends over their mental vision (004-002), then their senses feel "imprisoned in a delicate, invisible web, pressing ever more and more closely upon them" (004-004). The language mingles the tactile and the visual — the trance is both something that obscures sight and something that constrains movement. It is experienced as a physical condition, a transformation of the body as much as the mind.

The victim remains conscious during trance, but their consciousness is no longer their own. They can see, hear, and act, but their perceptions and actions are directed by the hypnotist's will. This preservation of consciousness is crucial to Mabuse's method: his victims are not unconscious puppets but aware participants who have lost the ability to distinguish between their own volition and the commands they have received.

## The Trance at the Gambling Table

The novel's paradigmatic trance scene occurs at the gambling table, where [[edgar-hull|Edgar Hull]] loses his fortune while in a state of hypnotic suspension. The narrative describes his experience as a progressive loss of agency:

> He felt a good deal of excitement within, and a veil seemed to be obscuring his mental vision. His banknotes fluttered across to the stranger without his appearing aware of the fact. (004-002, 004-003)

The "veil" is the novel's recurring metaphor for the trance state — a barrier between the self and its own perceptions, a filter that allows Mabuse's commands to pass through while blocking the subject's awareness of what is happening. Hull continues to play, to lose, to ruin himself, but he experiences his actions as if from a distance, as events happening to someone else.

## Trance as Surrender

The trance state is represented as a form of surrender — a giving-over of the self to an external authority. This representation has troubling implications. The novel does not present Mabuse's victims as purely passive recipients of hypnotic force; rather, they are described as complicit in their own subjection, their wills already weakened by desire, fear, or the general exhaustion of postwar life. The trance does not create vulnerability but exploits a pre-existing susceptibility.

This complicity complicates the moral calculus of the novel. If Mabuse's victims are in some sense willing — if the trance works because they have already surrendered something of themselves — then Mabuse's crime is not the creation of vulnerability but its exploitation. He is, in this reading, not a demonic exception but a diagnostician of weakness: someone who sees where the will has already faltered and knows how to push.

## Medical Hypnosis and Its Perversion

Mabuse's use of hypnosis parasitizes the legitimate medical practice of his era. He presents himself as a doctor treating patients, and his hypnotic sessions are framed as therapeutic interventions. This medical framing serves multiple purposes: it provides cover for his criminal activities, it grants him access to vulnerable patients, and it allows him to present coercion as cure.

The novel's treatment of medical hypnosis is ambivalent. On one hand, it acknowledges hypnosis as a genuine therapeutic tool — Mabuse has apparently cured patients of severe neurosis (010-002). On the other hand, it suggests that the therapeutic relationship is inherently vulnerable to abuse: the trust that a patient places in a doctor, the surrender of autonomy that treatment requires, and the asymmetry of power between healer and sufferer all create conditions in which exploitation is possible.

## Significance

Trance and altered states function in the novel as a limit case of human agency. The trance demonstrates that the self is not a sovereign entity but a permeable membrane, vulnerable to invasion and redirection by external forces. This insight, which the novel shares with psychoanalysis, is both scientifically prescient and politically disturbing. If the will can be suspended and replaced, then freedom is not a natural condition but an achievement — one that requires constant vigilance against those who would take it away.
