# Surveillance Technology

Surveillance in *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* is both an art and a technology. The novel depicts a range of surveillance techniques, from the human (informants, disguised officers, physical observation) to the technological (the telephone, the automobile, the archive), and traces the ways in which these techniques are deployed by both the criminal network and the police. Surveillance is not the exclusive province of either side; it is a capability that both deploy, and the contest between them is partly a contest over who can observe more effectively.

## Human Surveillance

The novel's primary surveillance technology is human. Informants watch the gambling dens and report to the police; [[von-wenk|von Wenk]], in disguise, observes the operations he is investigating; operatives watch each other, reporting infractions and maintaining discipline. Human surveillance is flexible — the human observer can adapt to changing circumstances, can interpret what they see, can make judgments about significance that a mechanical system cannot — but it is also fallible: the human observer can be detected, deceived, or turned.

## Technological Surveillance

The novel registers the emerging technologies of surveillance without dwelling on them. The telephone, which enables communication at a distance, also enables interception — the possibility that a conversation can be overheard by someone who is not its intended recipient. The automobile, which enables rapid movement, also enables tracking — the possibility that a vehicle's movements can be observed and recorded. The archive, which preserves the records of past investigations, also enables the identification of patterns that would be invisible in any single case.

## The Asymmetry of Surveillance

The novel depicts an asymmetry in surveillance capabilities between the criminal network and the police — but the asymmetry is not simply in favor of one side or the other. The criminal network has advantages in surveillance: its operatives are embedded in the spaces they observe, and its methods are not constrained by legal procedures. The police have different advantages: their resources are larger, their observations can be coordinated across multiple sites, and the information they gather can be used as evidence in court. The contest between these two surveillance apparatuses is a contest between different models of observation, and the novel's outcome — von Wenk's partial victory — is a judgment, however qualified, in favor of the legal model.

## Significance

Surveillance technology functions in the novel as a preview of developments that would accelerate across the twentieth century. The techniques of observation that the novel depicts — informant networks, undercover infiltration, communications interception, pattern analysis — would become the standard repertoire of intelligence agencies and police forces, and the ethical questions the novel raises about these techniques — their legitimacy, their effectiveness, their tendency to corrupt those who deploy them — would become increasingly urgent as the technology of surveillance became increasingly powerful.
