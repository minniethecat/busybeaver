# Science and Pseudoscience

Hypnosis, in the early 1920s, occupied an ambiguous position on the boundary between science and pseudoscience. It was simultaneously a legitimate medical technique with a growing body of clinical evidence, a subject of serious scientific investigation by researchers like Charcot and Bernheim, and a staple of stage entertainment whose practitioners made claims that the scientific establishment rejected. [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]] exploits this ambiguity systematically, using the prestige of medical hypnosis to legitimize practices that belong more to the tradition of mesmerism — the manipulation of "animal magnetism" — than to scientific medicine.

## The Authority of Science

Mabuse's medical credentials — his degree, his consulting room, his therapeutic successes — lend his hypnotic practice the authority of science. Patients accept his treatments because a doctor administers them; colleagues respect his work because it produces results. The authority of science is, in this context, a form of social power, and Mabuse's deployment of it is strategic rather than sincere. He does not need to believe in the scientific validity of his methods; he only needs others to believe in it.

## The Pseudoscientific Undercurrent

Beneath the scientific surface of Mabuse's practice runs a current of pseudoscientific belief. His methods draw on traditions of mesmerism and animal magnetism that predate scientific psychology; his effects — the apparent transfer of will from hypnotist to subject — resist explanation within the scientific frameworks of his day. The novel does not resolve the question of whether Mabuse's powers are scientifically explicable or genuinely occult, and this ambiguity is strategic: it places the reader in the same position as Mabuse's victims, unable to determine whether they are witnessing science or something else.

## Weltmann and the Performance of Science

[[weltmann|Weltmann]]'s mentalist performance at Lake Constance represents the entertainment-industry version of the same ambiguity. Weltmann presents his feats as "matters of fact" (020-030), inviting the audience to draw their own conclusions about the mechanisms behind them. The performance is framed as scientific demonstration — the mentalist as researcher, the audience as experimental subjects — but the frame is transparently theatrical. The boundary between scientific demonstration and theatrical performance is as blurred in Weltmann's act as the boundary between medical treatment and criminal manipulation is in Mabuse's.

> He said that the gifts he was about to display before the Prince and Princess and their guests were matters of fact, and he thought that the guests would prefer facts rather than an attempt to explain in words what would probably never be explainable. He would first offer himself as subject, and ask someone to name a lady and a gentleman in the company.
>
> (020-030)

## Significance

Science and pseudoscience function in the novel as an epistemological warning. The authority of science can be appropriated by those who do not practice it; the language of scientific demonstration can be used to legitimize practices that are not scientific; the boundary between science and pseudoscience can be blurred by those who benefit from the blurring. The novel's treatment of this theme is particularly relevant to a historical moment — the early 1920s — when scientific psychology was establishing its professional credentials and the distinction between legitimate and illegitimate practice was being actively contested.
