## Description

Komorek is a former Hungarian aristocrat — once Prince of Komor and Komorek — who renounced his title after his family objected to his marriage to a Viennese dancer. "His people were furious! They made things so disagreeable for him that one day he said, 'All right: you've gone too far. You've done with your Prince. From today I am plain Komorek,' and then he wandered off." Independently wealthy, he settled in a mansion near Nicholas Lake outside Berlin, where he has lived for ten years with his wife, who is described as "very smart and exclusive — more exclusive than a princess." (020-001)

## Role in the Narrative

Komorek appears only in the climactic twentieth chapter, but his role is pivotal: his villa is the setting for the grand entertainment where Weltmann — actually [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]] in disguise — performs his hypnotic stage act before sixty to seventy guests. It is here that [[von-wenk|von Wenk]], brought to the party by Police Superintendent Vörös, finally recognizes Weltmann as Mabuse, setting up the story's final confrontation. (020-001)(020-005)

The villa's remote location beyond Nicholas Lake contributes to Wenk's growing unease during the long, disorienting car ride with Vörös. Wenk notes that the journey takes far longer than it should — an hour at forty-five kilometres per hour — feeding his suspicion that he is being led into a trap. Komorek himself is described as hospitable ("they are always ready for guests") and his house is filled with overcoats in the cloakroom, suggesting a large and active social circle. (020-002)(020-004)

Komorek does not participate directly in the Mabuse-Wenk conflict; he is a neutral figure whose wealth and social position provide the stage upon which the drama unfolds. His abdication of princely status — choosing love over title — may be read as a counterpoint to Mabuse's insatiable hunger for power over others.

## Significance

Komorek represents the fading Central European aristocracy in the post-World War I landscape: a prince who has voluntarily shed his title but retains his wealth and social cachet. His villa, remote and opulent, is the kind of liminal space Mabuse exploits — outside the easy reach of Berlin police jurisdiction, populated by international guests who ask few questions. Komorek's hospitality, offered indiscriminately to Hungarian expatriates, police superintendents, and master criminals alike, illustrates the porous social boundaries of the Weimar-era elite, through which Mabuse moves with ease.
