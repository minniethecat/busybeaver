# Dance and Performance

Dance and performance permeate *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* at every level. From [[cara-carozza|Cara Carozza]]'s professional dancing at the [[bonbonniere|Bonbonnière]] to the nude ballet staged as part of the Go-Ahead Institute's garde-police, from the choreographed rituals of high society to the improvised performances of criminal imposture, the novel insists that modern life is fundamentally theatrical. Dancing is not merely an activity that some characters engage in; it is a metaphor for the condition of identity itself in a world where appearance and reality have become permanently unmoored.

## Cara Carozza: The Dancing Accomplice

Cara Carozza is the novel's central figure of dance, and her trajectory illustrates the ambiguous position of the female performer in Weimar culture. She dances at the Bonbonnière, a venue that occupies the uncertain territory between legitimate entertainment and the demimonde. Her dancing is at once an artistic practice, a form of seduction, and a professional obligation — the source of her livelihood and the means of her entanglement with [[dr-mabuse|Mabuse]]'s criminal network.

> A few days afterwards Hull made acquaintance with a girl who was performing jazz dances in the Bonbonnière. She was partly Mexican, she told him. She soon effected a diversion in his thoughts, and in her company he rapidly got rid of the twenty thousand marks which he could not pay over to the stranger.
>
> (001-076)

Cara's dancing draws the gaze — of the Bonbonnière's patrons, of von Wenk, of the novel's readers. This visibility is both her power and her vulnerability. As a dancer, she commands attention and exercises a form of influence over her audience; but this same visibility makes her legible to the police, traceable, ultimately arrestable. Her body, trained to express, becomes a body that can be read — by von Wenk, who follows her movements, and by Mabuse, who controls them.

## The Go-Ahead Institute Ballet

The novel's most explicit fusion of dance and crime occurs at the Go-Ahead Institute, where the garde-police mechanism transforms a gambling den into a theatre at the press of a button. The nude ballet that arises from the transformed gambling table is a performance within a performance: a spectacle designed to cover criminal activity by becoming, itself, a different kind of spectacle.

The dancers who perform this ballet are the most exploited figures in the novel's economy of performance. Unlike Cara, who has some measure of agency within her relationship with Mabuse, the Go-Ahead dancers are pure instruments — bodies deployed to create a distraction, their artistry subordinated entirely to criminal purpose. The sequence represents the darkest terminus of the novel's logic of performance: the point at which art becomes entirely instrumental, a machine for manufacturing alibis.

## The Theatricality of Social Life

Beyond professional performance, the novel presents social life itself as inherently theatrical. The Princess of Komor, a former dancer, continues to "perform" aristocracy at her Lake Constance mansion. Von Wenk, pursuing his investigation, must learn to perform identities — "a country cousin, a dispatch-bearing cyclist, a taxicab driver, a porter, waiter, steward, window-cleaner, an 'unemployed'" (003-024) — that are not his own. Mabuse's entire criminal method depends on the ability to perform roles: the elderly gentleman at the gambling table, the respectable doctor, the imperious prince.

In this world, the distinction between performance and authenticity collapses. Everyone is performing; the question is not whether one acts but what one's acting serves. The dancer on the Bonbonnière stage, the State Prosecutor in his disguises, the criminal mastermind in his personas — all are engaged in the same fundamental activity: the construction and projection of a self.

## The Female Performer

The novel's treatment of the female performer is particularly complex. Cara, the Viennese dancer who becomes a princess, the dancers at the Go-Ahead Institute — these figures occupy a precarious position in the novel's social world. Their visibility gives them a kind of power — they can attract attention, command desire, influence events — but this power is always conditional, always subject to appropriation by the men who control the venues, the networks, the institutions.

Cara's tragedy is that her performance, which should be the source of her freedom, becomes the instrument of her bondage. Her ability to attract the gaze makes her useful to Mabuse, who deploys her as a lure and a distraction. Her art becomes her cage.

## Significance

Dance and performance function in the novel as a diagnostic of modernity. In a society where traditional structures of identity — class, profession, family — have lost their power to define the self, performance fills the void. Everyone becomes an actor; every social interaction becomes a scene. The novel's insight is to recognize that this theatricalization of social life is neither liberating nor oppressive in itself — everything depends on who writes the script, who directs the action, who controls the stage. In Weimar Germany, the novel suggests, the script is being written by figures like Mabuse, and the stage belongs to whoever can seize it.
