# Class and Society in Weimar Germany

## Definition

The social world of *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* is stratified yet permeable — a society in which the old hierarchies of birth and rank coexist uneasily with new forms of wealth, power, and notoriety. The gambling dens, where aristocrats rub shoulders with criminals and policemen in disguise, are the novel's laboratories of class dissolution.

## Manifestation in the Text

The Count and Countess Told represent the old aristocracy: titled, wealthy, but spiritually depleted. The Countess is drawn to the gambling dens by a force she cannot explain — a symptom of her class's loss of purpose in the postwar order. The Count, meanwhile, is a passive figure, a husband whose authority has been hollowed out.

Von Wenk occupies the professional bourgeoisie: educated, diligent, morally earnest. His position as State Prosecutor gives him institutional authority, but in the gambling dens he must abandon the markers of his class — he goes disguised as "a rich old gentleman from the provinces" (004-008), a persona that allows him to move between social worlds without attracting suspicion.

The criminal class, represented by Mabuse and his network, is the most fluid. Mabuse can appear as a gentleman gambler, a chauffeur, a cyclist messenger — his class identity is a costume, donned and shed at will. This mobility is the source of his power, but also his tragedy: beneath the masks, there may be no authentic self.

The gambling table itself is a space of radical class leveling. "The passion for gambling had seized and carried them all away" (002-048) — in the fever of the game, countess and clerk, millionaire and chauffeur, are equal.

> There were gentlemen in evening dress, ladies in décolletée, expensively and fashionably attired. The passion for gambling had seized and carried them all away.
>
> (002-048) Money, not birth, is the only currency that matters.

## Context

The Weimar Republic inherited a society that was formally democratic but deeply stratified. The old aristocracy retained significant social prestige even after losing its political power; the professional middle class was traumatized by inflation; the working class was radicalized by economic crisis. The novel's fascination with secret spaces — the hidden gambling den, the hypnotist's consulting room — reflects an anxiety about what happens to social order when it is no longer visible.

## Significance

The novel's treatment of class is fundamentally pessimistic. No class is portrayed as healthy or sustainable: the aristocracy is decadent, the bourgeoisie is ineffectual, the criminal class is parasitic. The social order depicted is one in which all hierarchies are breaking down — and Mabuse, the man of many faces, is both the agent and the symptom of this dissolution.
