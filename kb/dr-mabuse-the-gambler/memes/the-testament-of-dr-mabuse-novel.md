# Mabuse's Colony (Novel)

*Mabuse's Colony* (*Mabuse Kolonie*) is [[norbert-jacques|Norbert Jacques]]'s later Mabuse novel, published several years after the original *Dr. Mabuse, the Gambler* and providing material that Fritz Lang and Thea von Harbou would draw on for their 1933 film *[[the-testament-of-dr-mabuse|The Testament of Dr. Mabuse]]*. The novel extended the Mabuse mythos beyond the original work, exploring new dimensions of the character and his criminal methods, though it never achieved the cultural impact of either the original novel or Lang's film adaptations.

## The Colonial Theme

As its title suggests, *Mabuse's Colony* develops the colonial theme that was present in embryo in the original novel's [[citopomar|Citopomar]] subplot. Where the original novel presented Mabuse's Brazilian empire as a fantasy — a destination never reached, a project never realized — the later novel apparently explores the colonial dimension of Mabuse's ambition more directly. The colony of the title represents the extension of Mabuse's criminal method from the individual (the gambler at the table) to the collective (the population of a territory), and this extension marks a significant evolution in the Mabuse concept.

## Relationship to the Film

Lang's *The Testament of Dr. Mabuse* draws on elements of *Mabuse's Colony* while transforming them significantly. The film's central conceit — Mabuse confined to an asylum, directing criminal operations through written manifestos that exercise a hypnotic power over their readers — combines material from Jacques's later novel with Lang and von Harbou's original inventions. The relationship between novel and film is thus one of inspiration and transformation rather than straightforward adaptation.

## The Extended Mythos

*Mabuse's Colony* is significant primarily as evidence of the Mabuse concept's capacity for extension and variation. Jacques, having created the character in 1921, returned to him in this later work, exploring new dimensions of his criminality and new settings for his operations. The novel demonstrates that Mabuse was not a one-time creation but a figure capable of sustaining multiple narratives across multiple works — a quality that would prove essential to the character's subsequent career in film and popular culture.

## Significance

*Mabuse's Colony* occupies a secondary position in the Mabuse canon — less well-known than the original novel, less influential than Lang's films — but it is significant as evidence of the character's literary durability and as a source for the thematic developments that would shape the cinematic Mabuse. The novel represents the second generation of the Mabuse mythos, and its existence testifies to the creative fertility of Jacques's original conception.
