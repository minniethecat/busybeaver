# Will to Power

Dr. Mabuse is not motivated by greed in any ordinary sense. He does not accumulate wealth for its own sake; he does not seek the comforts that money can buy. His drive is deeper and more abstract: the will to power — the Nietzschean imperative to dominate, to impose one's will upon others, to reshape reality according to one's own design. Gambling, hypnosis, disguise, stock manipulation — all are instruments of this single, consuming drive. What makes Mabuse terrifying is not the magnitude of his crimes but the purity of his motivation: he is a criminal whose purpose is not gain but mastery, whose satisfaction derives not from possession but from the act of subjugation itself.

## Definition

The will to power, as it operates in *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler*, is the drive to dominate that has been unmoored from all ethical constraint. It is Nietzsche's concept stripped of its creative dimension and reduced to its purest form: the imposition of one will upon another, without justification, without limit, without end. Mabuse embodies this drive not as an idea but as a practice — a technique of subjugation refined through study and repetition until it becomes indistinguishable from his identity. He does not *have* power; he *is* the will to power. Everything else — the disguises, the financial schemes, the hypnotic technique — is merely instrumentation.

## Manifestation in the Text

### The Logic of Negation

Mabuse's will to power is articulated through negation: what he does *not* want is as revealing as what he does. He does not seek recognition; his disguises ensure he is never seen. He does not seek pleasure; his operations are conducted with clinical efficiency. He does not seek revenge; his victims are chosen for their utility, not their offenses. Each of these negations strips away a conventional criminal motive, revealing beneath them a drive that is irreducible to any ordinary desire — the pure will to dominate, without object and without limit.

This pattern of negation serves a structural purpose in the novel. By removing every conventional explanation for Mabuse's behavior, Jacques and Lang force the reader to confront the uncomfortable possibility that evil can be gratuitous — that the will to power is not a means to an end but an end in itself.

### The Predator's Posture

The narrator repeatedly describes Mabuse in animal terms that emphasize predatory intent over rational calculation. When von Wenk first observes him at the gaming table:

> His attitude was that of a beast of prey who has already played his victim and is only waiting to pounce upon him. He seemed to be all sinews. (003-004)

This is not the posture of a businessman calculating returns but of a predator savoring the moment before the kill. The physicality of the description — "all sinews" — suggests that Mabuse's will to power is not merely intellectual but embodied, a force that expresses itself through the body before it reaches consciousness.

The anonymous woman in the darkened corner of the gaming room arrives independently at the same perception:

> "He seemed to look at Basch like a beast of prey eyeing his victim!" (003-038)

Von Wenk immediately recognizes the accuracy of this description:

> "That was exactly the impression he gave me!" exclaimed Wenk. (003-039)

The convergence of independent observers on the same predatory metaphor suggests that Mabuse's will to power is not subtle — it is visible to anyone who looks closely enough, a quality that radiates from him despite (or perhaps because of) his attempts at disguise.

### Von Wenk: The Forging of a Counter-Will

The investigation storyline is, at its deepest level, the story of von Wenk's will being forged into an instrument capable of opposing Mabuse. After his humiliating failure at the gaming table — where he becomes so absorbed in the game that he forgets his investigative purpose — Wenk undergoes a crisis of self-reckoning that is the novel's most explicit articulation of what is required to oppose the will to power:

> "I must set to work in quite another way. Goodwill and industry are not sufficient. Self-denial and inexorable self-discipline and a little more cunning are necessary! I must make use of every ruse that my opponent displays. … I must make use of disguise and secret spying. I must be prepared to stake myself on the game … must be myself the snare, if I do not want to be caught in it like a silly pigeon." (003-022)

This moment is the pivot on which the novel turns. Wenk recognizes that opposing Mabuse requires more than professional competence — it requires a transformation of the self. He must become, in his own way, what Mabuse already is: a being defined by will, someone who stakes everything on the outcome of a single contest. The phrase "myself the snare" is exquisitely chosen — Wenk understands that the only way to catch a predator is to offer oneself as bait, to make one's own vulnerability the instrument of capture.

The transformation is enacted immediately. Wenk procures disguise materials from the Criminal Investigation Department and devotes himself to the study of deception:

> He could now make up as a country cousin, a dispatch-bearing cyclist, a taxicab driver, a porter, waiter, steward, window-cleaner, an "unemployed," and other characters. (003-024)

The catalogue of disguises is significant: Wenk is learning to multiply his identity in exactly the way Mabuse does. To oppose the will to power, he must himself become a wielder of masks — must, in effect, become more like Mabuse. The novel does not flinch from this implication: the defense of civilization may require the defender to adopt the methods of its enemy.

### The Asymmetry of Wills

The novel presents the duel between Mabuse and von Wenk as fundamentally asymmetrical. Mabuse's will is unified, undivided by doubt or scruple. It operates with the efficiency of a machine, converting every situation into an opportunity for domination. Von Wenk's will is fractured — divided between his duty as prosecutor, his attraction to the mysterious Countess, his fear of failure, his residual belief in the institutions he serves. The drama of the investigation is the drama of von Wenk's will being progressively concentrated, his distractions eliminated one by one, until he becomes — for the duration of the final confrontation — an instrument as single-minded as his adversary.

This asymmetry is not merely psychological but structural. Mabuse operates outside all institutions; his will is constrained only by his own capacity. Von Wenk operates within institutions — the police, the courts, the state — and his will is therefore mediated, diluted, slowed by the very structures that are supposed to amplify it. The novel suggests that this is the fundamental advantage of the criminal over the law: not superior intelligence or resources, but superior freedom of will.

### The Eyes as Instrument

Throughout the novel, Mabuse's will to power is channeled through his eyes. The gaze is his primary instrument of domination — the means by which his will enters and occupies the consciousness of his victims. Hull, recalling his encounter with Mabuse, describes the effect not as a memory but as a physical injury:

> "It is not so much that I have played with him, but as if he had done me some very serious internal injury, affecting my very blood; but how, and when, and where, I have no idea. It must have been in a dream." (003-035)

The "internal injury" is the wound left by Mabuse's will — a violation that leaves no external mark but that alters the victim's relationship to their own consciousness. Hull cannot remember the event because memory requires a self to do the remembering, and Mabuse's gaze has temporarily dissolved that self.

Another observer simply notes:

> "He has evil eyes." (003-036)

The statement is banal on its surface but profound in context: what makes the eyes "evil" is not their appearance but their function. They are the aperture through which the will to power enters the world, the point of contact between Mabuse's consciousness and his victim's.

## Context

Nietzsche's philosophy of the will to power was a dominant intellectual current in early 20th-century Germany. The idea that all human behavior could be explained as the expression of a fundamental drive for mastery — over others, over oneself, over nature — resonated with a generation that had experienced the total mobilization of World War I and the subsequent collapse of traditional moral frameworks. The war had demonstrated, on an industrial scale, that the will to power was not a philosophical abstraction but a historical force capable of consuming millions of lives.

Jacques and Lang translate this philosophical abstraction into the concrete figure of Mabuse, making the will to power visible and terrifying. Their achievement is to show not merely that such a drive exists but how it operates — the specific techniques (hypnosis, disguise, financial manipulation) through which the abstract will to dominate is actualized in a modern urban environment.

It is significant that the novel was published in 1921, before the Nazi Party's rise to prominence. Mabuse is not an allegory for Hitler but something more disturbing: a prophecy of the type of figure that a society in dissolution would produce. The will to power, the novel suggests, is latent in modernity itself — not a foreign import but a potential within every democratic society that has lost its moral bearings.

## Significance

The will to power is the novel's deepest explanation for Mabuse. He is not a criminal because he is poor, or desperate, or traumatized. He is a criminal because he has chosen power as his highest value — has made the domination of others the organizing principle of his existence. In this, he is less a psychological case study than a philosophical warning: this is what happens when the will to power is unmoored from any ethical constraint.

The duel between Mabuse and von Wenk is ultimately a duel between two modes of will:

| Dimension | Mabuse's Will | Von Wenk's Will |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Source | Self-generated, autonomous | Derived from institutional duty |
| Unity | Unified, undivided | Fragmented by competing obligations |
| Method | Direct psychic domination | Indirect investigation and evidence |
| Constraint | None — operates outside all structures | Multiple — law, procedure, conscience |
| Goal | Domination for its own sake | Justice as restoration of order |
| Cost | Isolation, ultimate destruction | Self-transformation into Mabuse's mirror |

The tragedy of the novel is that von Wenk can only defeat Mabuse by becoming, in crucial respects, like him — by learning disguise, by staking himself on the game, by transforming his own will into an instrument as ruthless as his adversary's. The victory over Mabuse is real, but it exacts a price: the recognition that the boundary between the defender of civilization and its enemy is thinner than either would like to believe.
