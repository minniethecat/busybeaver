## Description

Berlin appears as a significant urban setting in the concluding chapters of the narrative. It is depicted as a center of police administration — the seat of the Berlin police force — and as a destination to which characters travel in connection with Mabuse's schemes. The city is portrayed as a vast, modern metropolis where individuals can move anonymously and where police bureaucracy operates at scale. Mabuse himself is drawn to Berlin precisely because of its scale and anonymity: Munich is too small for his ambitions, and Berlin's vastness provides the ideal hunting ground.

## Role in the Narrative

Berlin functions primarily as a locus of police activity and as the final arena of the cat-and-mouse game between Mabuse and Wenk. Chief Inspector von Wenk reports to his superiors in Berlin and coordinates his investigation from the city: "he was only on leave in Berlin, for the scene of his activities lay elsewhere" (019-065). The Berlin police apparatus provides the institutional framework within which Wenk operates, even though his pursuit of Mabuse takes him far from the capital.

The city's importance deepens when Wenk realizes that Mabuse has fled to Berlin:

> Wenk stopped still, saying, "You have come just at the right time. I don't belong to Berlin, and I should like to see the real nightlife of this city just for once. Where are your dancers? Go ahead!" (019-053)

The narrator reveals Mabuse's strategy in a passage of chilling clarity:

> Mabuse in reality had never left Berlin. In all the towns to which Wenk had travelled in search of the gambler, Mabuse had doubles, persons of his own gang, instructed by him. Munich was too small for the scheme Mabuse had in hand. The abysses of Berlin would be a safer hunting-ground, and the hunt began on the very next day.
>
> (019-048)

This passage crystallizes Berlin's dual identity: it is both the seat of police authority and a labyrinth deep enough to shelter a criminal mastermind. Additionally, minor characters ground the metropolitan setting — a "Berlin taxi-driver" (020-012) provides Wenk with crucial information about Mabuse's whereabouts.

## Real-World Context

Berlin in the early 1920s was the capital of the Weimar Republic and one of Europe's most dynamic cities. It was a center of modernist culture, political ferment, and organized crime — the historical context that Norbert Jacques drew upon for his 1921 novel *Dr. Mabuse, der Spieler*. The Berlin police force (the *Berliner Polizei*) was a large and professional organization, and the city's sheer scale made it a natural setting for narratives of pursuit, anonymity, and institutional power. The novel's description of Berlin as a place of "abysses" capable of hiding a criminal mastermind reflects the Expressionist anxiety about the modern metropolis — a theme that would reach its full cinematic expression in Lang's urban panoramas.

## Significance

Berlin in the Mabuse narrative represents the tension between institutional authority and urban anonymity. The city houses the police bureaucracy that Wenk represents — the force of law and order — yet it is also a place where Mabuse can move unseen, where a criminal mastermind can exploit the impersonality of the modern metropolis. The narrator's description of Berlin's "abysses" as "a safer hunting-ground" for Mabuse encapsulates this duality: the same vastness that enables police coordination across jurisdictions also provides the cover a fugitive needs. This tension aligns with the Expressionist portrayal of the modern city as simultaneously rational and menacing, a theme Fritz Lang would develop further in his visual depiction of urban space in the 1922 film adaptation.
