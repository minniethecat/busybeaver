# The Prison Interrogation

The interrogation of arrested operatives is a recurring event in the investigation's later phases — a procedural ritual that promises revelation but delivers only fragments. The operatives who are taken into custody and questioned possess information that von Wenk needs, but that information is guarded by loyalties that interrogation cannot easily breach. The prison interrogation thus becomes a test not only of the operative's resistance but of the investigator's method: what can the law extract from those who have placed themselves outside the law?

## The Interrogation of Cara Carozza

The interrogation of [[cara-carozza|Cara Carozza]] is the most significant of these encounters. Cara, arrested after her [[cara-carozza-arrest|arrest at the Bonbonnière]], is brought to [[munich-prison|Munich Prison]] for questioning. The setting — the cell, the institutional furniture, the formal procedures of interrogation — marks the transition from the world of the criminal network to the world of state authority. Cara, who commanded attention on the Bonbonnière stage, is now the object of state attention, her body and speech subject to the protocols of institutional inquiry.

Von Wenk conducts the interrogation, and the encounter is charged with the history of their previous interactions. The investigator who was drawn to the dancer must now question the prisoner; the personal feelings that complicated the surveillance now complicate the interrogation. The novel registers this complexity without resolving it, allowing the tension between professional duty and personal history to persist throughout the questioning.

## The Limits of Interrogation

Interrogation, as the novel depicts it, is a limited instrument. The operative who is questioned may know more than they reveal; the information they provide may be incomplete, misleading, or false. The interrogation extracts fragments — a name, a location, a date — but cannot extract the larger picture that would make those fragments meaningful. That picture must be assembled by the investigator from multiple sources, and the interrogation is only one source among many.

## The Ethics of Questioning

The novel raises, implicitly, ethical questions about the interrogation process. The prisoner is in the state's power; the questions are backed by the threat of continued confinement; the answers are given under conditions of duress. The novel does not depict physical torture, but the psychological pressure of interrogation is real, and the line between legitimate questioning and coercion is not always clear. Von Wenk, as the agent of the state, operates on that line, and the novel does not exempt him from the ethical scrutiny it applies to Mabuse.
