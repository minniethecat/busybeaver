# Medicine and Ethics

Medical ethics is the framework that Mabuse's practice systematically violates. The doctor-patient relationship, founded on trust and directed toward healing, becomes in his hands a mechanism of exploitation. The patient who seeks relief from suffering is delivered instead into dependence and manipulation. The novel's treatment of this corruption is particularly pointed because Mabuse's medical credentials are, in some sense, genuine — he has successfully treated patients through hypnosis — and the ambiguity of his practice raises uncomfortable questions about the vulnerability of the therapeutic relationship to abuse.

## The Doctor as Predator

Mabuse's medical practice provides the cover for his criminal activities. The consulting room is a space of legitimate professional encounter that doubles as a space of psychological predation. The patient who undresses for examination — physically or psychologically — exposes vulnerabilities that the doctor is sworn to protect and that Mabuse is methodical in exploiting.

> Wenk mentioned his telephone number. Both the gentlemen then left, to go to a house where ether, cocaine and opium were procurable, and other more obvious vices were pandered to.
>
> (019-075)

The novel's depiction of this exploitation draws on anxieties about medical power that were current in the early twentieth century. The rise of professional medicine had concentrated enormous authority in the hands of doctors, and the patient's dependency on that authority created a structural vulnerability that the novel's plot systematically explores.

## Therapeutic Hypnosis and Its Perversion

Hypnosis occupies an ambiguous position in the novel's medical ethics. It is presented as a genuine therapeutic technique — Mabuse has treated patients successfully — but it is also the primary instrument of his predation. The ambiguity is structural: the same technique that can heal can also harm, and the line between therapeutic and predatory hypnosis is determined not by the technique itself but by the intentions of the practitioner.

## The Patient's Trust

Trust is the foundation of the therapeutic relationship, and it is the quality that makes that relationship vulnerable to abuse. The patient trusts the doctor to act in the patient's interest; the doctor who betrays that trust does not merely harm the patient but corrupts the institution of medicine itself. Mabuse's crimes are, in this sense, not merely personal but institutional — they undermine the trust that makes medical practice possible.

## Significance

Medicine and ethics function in the novel as a case study in the vulnerability of professional institutions to corruption. The medical profession's codes of conduct, its mechanisms of oversight, its traditions of ethical practice — all are inadequate to prevent the kind of abuse that Mabuse perpetrates. The novel suggests that professional ethics, like the law, is a framework that works only when those who operate within it are committed to its values — and that the figure who is not so committed can exploit the framework's protections while systematically violating its principles.
