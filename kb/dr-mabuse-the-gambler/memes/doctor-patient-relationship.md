# The Doctor-Patient Relationship

## Definition

Dr. Mabuse is not merely a criminal who happens to have a medical degree. His identity as a psychoanalyst is essential to his criminal method. The therapeutic relationship — built on trust, confidentiality, and the patient's vulnerability — provides the perfect structure for exploitation. Mabuse does not violate medical ethics incidentally; he has chosen medicine precisely because it grants him access to the inner lives of his victims.

## Manifestation in the Text

Mabuse's title — "Doctor" — is invoked throughout the novel, a constant reminder that his authority is institutional as well as personal. He has been "successfully treated by hypnotic suggestion" (010-002) — the passive construction is telling; it implies that Mabuse himself underwent hypnotic treatment, learning the technique from within the therapeutic relationship before turning it outward as a weapon.

> Mabuse had made the Privy Councillor's acquaintance through a patient of his, an aristocratic and wealthy dame, who had suffered from severe neurosis and whom Mabuse had very successfully treated by hypnotic suggestion.
>
> (010-002)

The séance scene dramatizes the perversion of therapeutic practice. A gathering that ostensibly serves scientific inquiry — "studying the effect of hypnosis on a medium" — becomes an occasion for manipulation and terror. The doctor's consulting room, which should be a space of healing, is revealed as a theater of power.

The Countess Told is the novel's most complex patient figure. She is treated by Mabuse even as her husband — the Count — is one of Mabuse's targets. The triangulation of doctor, patient, and patient's family creates opportunities for manipulation that Mabuse exploits with surgical precision. The therapeutic frame — confidentiality, trust, the suspension of ordinary social judgment — becomes a trap.

## Context

The early 1920s saw the professionalization of psychoanalysis and the emergence of the doctor-patient relationship as a site of cultural anxiety. Freud's case studies had made the intimate details of therapeutic encounters public; the figure of the psychoanalyst — who could read minds, uncover secrets, access the unconscious — was simultaneously fascinating and threatening. Mabuse literalizes this threat: he is the psychoanalyst who uses his knowledge not to heal but to dominate.

## Significance

The doctor-patient relationship is the novel's most disturbing insight into the nature of modern power. It is not coercive in any obvious sense; the patient comes willingly, pays for the privilege, and believes she is being helped. The exploitation is structural rather than personal — it is built into the asymmetry of the relationship itself. Mabuse's genius is to recognize that the institutions designed to protect the vulnerable (medicine, law, therapy) are also the most efficient instruments of exploitation, if one is willing to use them without ethical constraint.
