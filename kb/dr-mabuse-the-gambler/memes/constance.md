# Constance (Konstanz)

## Description

Constance (Konstanz) is the German city on the western shore of Lake Constance, marking the border with Switzerland. The novel references the lake as a site of criminal operations, mentioning "ten percent by Lake Constance or the Ticino frontier" (005-042). In the narrative, it serves as the threshold space — the last point of German territory before the lake crossing, the final urban environment before the natural expanse of the water.

> "Into the sea. You won't pick a quarrel with anybody. There is no point of honour on the Lake of Constance."
>
> (005-138)

## Role in the Narrative

Constance is the city where the pursuit narrows to its final focus. Von Wenk tracks Mabuse to Constance, recognizing that the city's border location makes it the logical point of attempted escape. The narrative records that "Mabuse started for Lake Constance" (014-037) as the pursuit entered its final phase. The city's railway station, streets, and lakefront become the geography of the novel's climax.

## Significance

Mabuse dismisses honour with characteristic cynicism:

> "Point of honour is an expression that doesn't exist. A point is no larger than a squashed fly, and that's the extent of a man's honour — yours too, eh? You have some honour, even if the Lake of Constance has not?"
>
> (005-137)


Constance represents the endpoint of the investigation's spatial trajectory — from Munich's labyrinthine interior to the border's edge. At Constance, there is nowhere left to hide; the city's openness prefigures the even greater openness of the lake. The border city is a space of decision: escape or capture, Switzerland or justice.
