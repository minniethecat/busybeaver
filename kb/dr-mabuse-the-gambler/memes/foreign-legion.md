## Description

The Foreign Legion (Légion étrangère) was a branch of the French Army open to foreign recruits, notorious in the post-World War I era for accepting men with checkered pasts who sought a fresh start under a new identity. In the world of *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler*, it functions as a shadow mechanism: Mabuse's agents pose as Legion recruiters — or collaborate with actual ones — to ship off discharged smugglers, informants, and other compromised associates who might otherwise talk to the German authorities. (005-116)(009-107)

## Role in the Narrative

Mabuse instructs his lieutenant [[spoerri|Spoerri]] to "pack them off into the Foreign Legion" — the "them" being the Constance workers whose high-wage smuggling jobs have suddenly ended and who, left idle and desperate, would inevitably steal, get arrested, and "blab everything in their rage." The contact for this operation is a man named Magnard, whom Spoerri is to see after finishing business in Zürich and Meran. (005-116)

State Prosecutor [[von-wenk|von Wenk]] encounters the Legion's reputation from the other side: a young car-dealer confides that rather than starve under tightening border controls, he would join the Foreign Legion — "then at least he would be safe from the German authorities." The man explains that unlike before the war, one no longer needs to travel to Belfort; recruitment offices now operate inside Germany itself. (009-064)(009-065)

The Legion appears again as [[dr-mabuse|Mabuse]] realizes his operation is crumbling: "several agents of the Foreign Legion had been discovered and arrested," tightening the investigative net around him. (009-107) Von Wenk, for his part, weaponizes the Legion's shady reputation as a press cover story: when seven of Mabuse's men are arrested at Offenburg station carrying forged passports to Alsace, he instructs police to tell reporters only that the case involved "Germans having been enticed into the Foreign Legion" — concealing the deeper conspiracy. (011-011)

In the final act, [[george|George]] — Mabuse's chauffeur and enforcer — is confronted in prison with "the young fellows whom he had tried to hand over to the Foreign Legion, all of whom instantly recognized him." George, playing a deaf mute, never speaks; he hangs himself in his cell. (019-043)

## Key Members

- Magnard — contact person for Foreign Legion recruitment, engaged by Mabuse via Spoerri (005-116)
- The unnamed young car-dealer — a desperate smuggler who saw the Legion as his only escape from destitution (009-064)(009-065)

## Significance

The Foreign Legion serves as a narrative device illustrating the permeability of Weimar-era borders and institutions. It is simultaneously a real military body and a convenient sinkhole into which inconvenient people can be made to disappear. Mabuse's exploitation of it — dangling the promise of escape to desperate men, then using Legion recruitment channels to launder away criminal liabilities — mirrors his broader method of turning legitimate structures (banks, stock exchanges, police forces) into instruments of crime. The Legion's presence on German soil (recruitment offices operating domestically after the war) reflects the weakened sovereignty of the post-Versailles German state.
