# The Hotel Room

The hotel room is the quintessential space of modern anonymity — a room that belongs to no one, occupied temporarily by travelers whose identities may or may not correspond to the names they have registered. In a novel attentive to the spatial conditions of both crime and investigation, the hotel room serves multiple functions: meeting place, hiding place, transactional space, temporary refuge. It is the spatial correlative of the mobility and anonymity that characterize modern urban life.

## The Room as Anonymity

The hotel room guarantees a certain form of anonymity. The occupant who registers under a false name, who pays in cash, who leaves no trace beyond the rumpled sheets and the empty bottle — this occupant is invisible to all but the hotel staff, and the hotel staff are trained not to notice. The hotel room is a space where identity can be suspended, where the person who enters can be someone other than who they are.

## The Room as Meeting Place

The hotel room serves as a meeting place for transactions that require privacy. The criminal conspirators who cannot meet in public, the informant who cannot be seen with the investigator, the lovers whose relationship cannot be acknowledged — all find in the hotel room a space where their encounter can occur beyond the reach of observation. The room is a temporary sanctuary, a space carved out of the public world for private purposes.

## The Room as Threshold

The hotel room is also a threshold — a space between the street and the home, between the public and the private, between the identity one presents to the world and the identity one inhabits when no one is looking. The room is not a home — it lacks the permanence, the personalization, the accumulation of history that defines domestic space — but it is also not the street. It is a space of temporary dwelling, and its temporariness is essential to its function.

## The Room and the Investigation

For the investigation, the hotel room is both a resource and a challenge. It is a resource because it provides a space for meetings that cannot be conducted in official settings. It is a challenge because its anonymity extends to the investigator as well as the criminal — the room that conceals the conspiracy also conceals the surveillance, and the investigator who uses the hotel room is subject to the same conditions of anonymity as the criminal who uses it.
