# Princess of Komor

The Princess of Komor (and Komorek) is a former Viennese dancer whose marriage to the exiled [[prince-komor|Prince of Komor]] has elevated her to the ranks of European aristocracy. Her presence at the Lake Constance gathering is central to the evening's entertainment: she participates actively in [[weltmann|Weltmann]]'s mentalist performance, naming participants and engaging with the demonstration in ways that reveal both her intelligence and her theatrical instincts — the residue of her former profession.

## From Dancer to Princess

The Princess's background is established through the dismissive commentary of von Wenk's informant, whose contempt for the Prince extends to his wife:

> "His wife is very smart and exclusive — more exclusive than a princess." (020-002)

The observation is double-edged. On its surface, it acknowledges the Princess's successful performance of aristocratic identity: she has mastered the codes of exclusivity so thoroughly that she outdoes genuine royalty. But the compliment contains an implicit dismissal — she is "more exclusive than a princess" because she is not, in fact, a princess by birth. Her exclusivity is a studied performance, a role she has learned to play.

This performative quality connects the Princess to the novel's broader interest in [[disguise-and-secret-identity|identity and imposture]]. Like [[dr-mabuse|Mabuse]], she has constructed an identity through performance; unlike Mabuse, her performance is socially sanctioned — the acceptable face of self-invention.

## Role in Weltmann's Performance

The Princess emerges as an active participant in the evening's entertainment. When Weltmann asks for a volunteer to name a participant, she immediately responds:

> Then the Princess said, "As the gentleman you want, I should like to name my neighbour, Herr von Wenk!" (020-031)

Her choice is pointed: she selects the State Prosecutor, the one person in the room whose professional identity most directly opposes the world of illusion and performance. The Princess's social instincts are acute — she understands that involving the representative of the law in the entertainment will heighten the drama for everyone present.

When the Prince reciprocates by naming his own wife as the female participant, the performance becomes a marital duet — the Prince and Princess performing their union as a public spectacle. This public dimension of their relationship, the willingness to make their marriage a theatrical event, is itself a sign of their ambiguous social position: genuine aristocrats do not need to perform their status.

## Interaction with von Wenk

The Princess's engagement with von Wenk during the performance is marked by physical proximity and a certain flirtatious energy:

> From all sides folks gazed at him, and the Princess leant towards him, so that he could distinguish the scent of the powder she used. (020-047)

The detail — the scent of her powder — registers von Wenk's heightened sensory awareness in the moment. The Princess has drawn him into an intimate proximity that blurs the line between theatrical participation and genuine social interaction. The scene captures the distinctive atmosphere of the Lake Constance gathering: a space where performance and reality, entertainment and danger, have become permanently entangled.

## Involvement in the Investigation

As the evening progresses toward its climactic revelations, the Princess's role shifts from hostess to participant in the unfolding investigation. She receives Weltmann's written block and passes it to von Wenk:

> The Princess handed him Weltmann's block, and he thrust these ideas away somewhat, though he had to struggle to see the words. (020-077)

The gesture is small but significant: the Princess has become a conduit for evidence, a participant in the process of revelation rather than merely its audience. The aristocratic hostess who began the evening as a performer of exclusivity ends it as an unwitting collaborator in the exposure of crime.

## Significance

The Princess of Komor illustrates the novel's treatment of social mobility and identity performance. Her trajectory — dancer to princess, performer to aristocrat — mirrors in benign form the darker transformations enacted by Mabuse. Both demonstrate that identity in modern society is not fixed but constructed; the difference lies in the ends to which this insight is put. The Princess uses performance to ascend; Mabuse uses it to destroy.

As a character, she also provides the novel with a moment of genuine social grace amid the gathering darkness. Her intelligence, her theatrical flair, and her ability to navigate between the worlds of art and aristocracy make her one of the more vividly realized minor figures in the novel's final sections.
