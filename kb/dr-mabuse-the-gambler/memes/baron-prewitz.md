# Baron Prewitz

Baron Prewitz is an aristocratic guest at the Lake Constance gathering hosted by the [[prince-komor|Prince of Komor]]. His role in the narrative is brief but pointed: during [[weltmann|Weltmann]]'s mentalist performance, Prewitz's identity becomes the subject of scrutiny, raising the question that haunts the entire novel — whether any person is genuinely who they claim to be.

## The Identification Scene

Prewitz appears during the climactic performance sequence in Chapter 20. As Weltmann conducts his demonstration of apparent telepathy, a moment of tension arises when a member of the company must be identified:

> "Yes, it is Baron Prewitz!" (020-054)

The exclamation is a confirmation — but the very need for confirmation, the fact that an aristocrat's identity must be verbally vouched for, speaks to the novel's deep anxiety about the stability of social identity. The Baron's presence provokes a further question from those assembled:

> "Is the Baron's being known to the Princess sufficient for the company to rule out the idea of any private understanding between him and myself?" (020-055)

This question transforms Prewitz from a mere guest into a test case. The assembled company must decide whether Prewitz's established relationship with the [[princess-komor|Princess]] — his being "known" to her — constitutes a sufficient guarantee of his bona fides, or whether, in a world of infinite [[disguise-and-secret-identity|disguise]], even an aristocratic introduction can be a cover for conspiracy.

## The Gaze of Suspicion

Weltmann's response to the uncertainty is to turn the performance's attention directly onto Prewitz (020-057):

> He looked at Prewitz, quite quietly, for a short time. Then Prewitz, with stealthy movements, left the platform and went slowly and cautiously from chair to chair, looking everyone in the face. (020-057)

The role reversal is striking. Prewitz, who came to be entertained, becomes the object of scrutiny. His "stealthy movements" and his slow examination of each face transform him from audience member into something resembling an investigator — or a suspect. The performance has destabilized the boundary between observer and observed, between the respectable guest and the potential criminal.

## Significance

Baron Prewitz, like many minor characters in the novel, is less important as an individual than as a function in the narrative's system of suspicion. His presence raises a question that the novel never fully resolves: in a society where [[criminal-mastermind|criminal masterminds]] wear the masks of gentlemen, how can anyone be trusted? The Baron's aristocratic title should guarantee his respectability, but in Mabuse's world, titles are merely another form of [[disguise-and-secret-identity|disguise]].

Prewitz also represents the aristocracy's diminished authority in Weimar Germany. Where a baron's word would once have been sufficient guarantee, the assembled company now demands additional verification. The old social order's mechanisms of trust have broken down, and nothing reliable has replaced them.
