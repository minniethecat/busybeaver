# The Hotel Lobby

The hotel lobby is a quintessentially modern space — a room designed for temporary presence, for the comings and goings of travelers who do not belong to the city they are visiting. It is neither fully public (like the street) nor fully private (like the hotel room); it occupies a transitional zone between these categories, and this transitional quality makes it useful for transactions that require a degree of privacy without the intimacy of a private space.

## The Space of Transaction

In the novel's world, the hotel lobby is a space of transaction — a place where meetings are conducted, information is exchanged, and relationships are initiated. The lobby's anonymity makes it ideal for encounters that should not be observed; its formality makes it appropriate for encounters that require a degree of social ceremony. The character who meets a contact in a hotel lobby is conducting business that is neither fully legitimate nor fully criminal — business that occupies the gray zone between these categories that the hotel lobby, as a space, exemplifies.

## The Lobby as Stage

The hotel lobby is also a stage — a space where identities are performed for an audience of strangers. The traveler who enters the lobby presents a version of themselves — the business traveler, the tourist, the visiting dignitary — that may or may not correspond to their actual identity. The lobby's audience — the other travelers, the hotel staff, the casual observers — provides the recognition that the performance requires, and the lobby's anonymity ensures that the performance will not be scrutinized too closely.

> Hull thereupon took his leave. Downstairs he asked whether there were a second Herr Balling in the hotel, but the answer was "No." Had Room 15 been occupied by any Herr Balling who had just left? "No." Was the writing in the pocketbook known? Again there was a negative reply. "For the first time in my life," thought Hull, "I find myself unable to pay a debt of honour."

## The Lobby and the Investigation

For von Wenk's investigation, the hotel lobby is a space of potential intelligence. The comings and goings of travelers, the meetings conducted in corners, the transactions that occur in the transitional space between arrival and departure — all are potential sources of information about the movements and connections of the criminal network. The lobby that conceals also reveals, to those who know how to look.
