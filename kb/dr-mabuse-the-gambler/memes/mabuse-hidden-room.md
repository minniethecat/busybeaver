# Mabuse's Hidden Room

Every identity Mabuse presents to the world requires a space where that identity can be shed. The hidden room — a concealed chamber within one of his residences, accessible only to the mastermind himself — is the spatial correlative of his psychological method: a space of absolute privacy where the performances that constitute his public existence can be suspended, where the man behind the masks can exist without observation.

## The Concealment

The hidden room is concealed not merely from the public but from the network's own operatives. Its existence may be unknown even to [[spoerri|Spoerri]] or [[pesch|Pesch]] — a spatial secret that mirrors the psychological secrets Mabuse keeps from those closest to him. The concealment of the room is an expression of the principle that governs Mabuse's entire method: information is power, and the most powerful information is the information that no one else possesses.

> When furnishing the house he had had a little secret chamber made near the doorway leading to the first floor. A carpenter belonging to his band of accomplices had done the work. This chamber was so cunningly concealed in the cleverly contrived decoration of the walls as to be invisible from the corridor outside, and on the inner side the existence of a door would never have been suspected.
>
> (017-008)

## The Function

What happens in the hidden room is not described — its function is defined by its invisibility rather than by any specific activity. It may contain records of criminal operations, evidence that would incriminate the network if discovered. It may contain the materials of disguise — the costumes, the makeup, the documents — through which Mabuse constructs his multiple identities. It may contain nothing but the space itself, a retreat from the perpetual performance that his method requires.

## The Space of the Self

The hidden room represents, in spatial form, the question that haunts the novel's treatment of identity: is there a self beneath the masks, a person behind the performances? The room suggests that there is — that Mabuse requires a space where performance can cease, where the effort of maintaining multiple identities can be temporarily suspended. But the room's content is hidden from the reader as it is hidden from the novel's characters, and the question of what, if anything, occupies the space behind Mabuse's masks remains unanswered.
