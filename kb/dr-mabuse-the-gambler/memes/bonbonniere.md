## Description

The Bonbonnière is a Parisian cabaret and nightclub that appears in the early chapters of the narrative. It is the venue where Cara Carozza performs as a jazz dancer, and it functions as a meeting point for figures connected to Mabuse's underworld. The establishment is described as a fashionable entertainment spot, frequented by wealthy patrons including the stockbroker Edgar Hull. Its name — meaning a small decorative candy box in French — hints at the surface glamour that conceals the dangerous entanglements beneath.

## Role in the Narrative

The Bonbonnière serves as the critical link between Cara Carozza and Edgar Hull. Hull visits the club and becomes acquainted with Carozza through her performances there:

> A few days afterwards Hull made acquaintance with a girl who was performing jazz dances in the Bonbonnière. She was partly Mexican, she told him. She soon effected a diversion in his thoughts, and in her company he rapidly got rid of the twenty thousand marks which he could not pay over to the stranger. (001-076)

The cabaret serves as the point of entry into Mabuse's world:

> Through the Bonbonnière lady Hull frequently spent his evenings in places where gambling was indulged in. He heard much about this swindler at play, and from many different quarters, for theatrical folk are always particularly interested in anything out of the common, especially where masquerading is concerned.
>
> (002-004)

The cabaret is not merely a neutral venue — it is the launch point for Mabuse's manipulation of Hull, the place where legitimate entertainment shades into criminal entrapment.

The club also provides Cara Carozza with a public-facing identity. She is known as "Mdlle. Cara Carozza, of the Bonbonnière" (002-006), a performer whose profession offers cover for her role as Mabuse's accomplice. The Bonbonnière thus functions as a site where legitimate social life and criminal conspiracy intersect — a characteristic pattern in the Mabuse underworld. Wenk, too, recognizes the Bonbonnière's significance: his questioning of Hull explicitly references his intimacy with "Mdlle. Cara Carozza, of the Bonbonnière" (002-007), identifying the venue as a node in the criminal network.

## Real-World Context

While no specific real-world cabaret named "Bonbonnière" is documented in 1920s Paris, the establishment strongly evokes the Parisian *cabaret artistique* tradition of the era — venues like the Folies Bergère or the Moulin Rouge, where dance performances, social mixing, and underworld connections often overlapped. The French name suggests a deliberately glamorous, slightly decadent atmosphere, consistent with the Weimar-era fascination with Parisian nightlife as epitomized in the popular culture of the time. The reference to jazz dances places the Bonbonnière squarely in the post-World War I cultural moment, when jazz — imported from America — was transforming European nightlife.

## Significance

The Bonbonnière represents the permeable boundary between entertainment and criminality in Mabuse's world. It is a legitimate business — a cabaret where people go to be entertained — but it also serves as a conduit for Mabuse's schemes. Carozza's dual identity (performer and accomplice) mirrors the dual nature of the space itself: outwardly glamorous, inwardly instrumental to crime. This doubling — surface respectability concealing darker purposes — is a recurring motif throughout the Mabuse narrative. The Bonbonnière episode also illustrates Mabuse's method: he does not directly approach his victims but instead works through intermediaries and social venues that appear harmless, making the trap invisible until it is too late.
