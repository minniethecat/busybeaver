# Citopomar

Citopomar is not a place but a fantasy of place — an imagined empire in the Brazilian primeval forests that represents the ultimate goal of [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]]'s criminal accumulation. Named only a handful of times in the novel, it nevertheless exerts a powerful gravitational pull on the narrative: it is the destination toward which Mabuse's wealth flows, the purpose that justifies his predation, the dream that transforms his crimes from mere profit-seeking into a project of world-building.

## The Dream of Empire

Citopomar is introduced through Mabuse's own consciousness, as the narrator reveals the purpose behind the mastermind's relentless extraction of wealth:

> By means of his accomplices he was collecting the money wherewith to establish his empire in the primeval forests of Brazil, the Empire of Citopomar. (005-051)

The phrasing is significant. Mabuse does not merely plan to buy property in Brazil; he plans to "establish his empire." The language of sovereignty — empire, realm, dominion — reveals the scope of his ambition. The criminal mastermind who has mastered the art of psychological manipulation in the drawing rooms and gambling dens of Munich imagines himself as the absolute ruler of a territory where no competing authority exists.

The choice of Brazil is not arbitrary. The Brazilian interior, in the European imagination of the early twentieth century, represented a space of radical possibility — a vast, unmapped territory where the constraints of European civilization did not apply, where a man with sufficient resources could create a world of his own design. Citopomar is the colonial fantasy stripped of its civilizing pretext: not a mission to bring light to the darkness but a project of pure dominion.

## The Frontier of the Self

Citopomar also represents a psychological frontier — the limit of Mabuse's self-understanding. The empire is the object of a boyhood dream:

> There, in Citopomar, the dream of his boyhood would be fulfilled — a dream which had already begun to be realized on that large and desolate island which lay cradled in the ocean yonder. (005-050)

The reference to "that large and desolate island" — possibly an earlier, smaller attempt at territorial acquisition — suggests that Citopomar is the culmination of a lifelong fantasy. Mabuse has been moving toward this imagined empire since childhood; his criminal career is the means by which he has accumulated the resources to make the fantasy real. The empire is thus not merely a goal but an identity — the answer to the question of who Mabuse ultimately is: not a doctor, not a gambler, not a criminal, but an emperor.

But the dream remains a dream. The novel ends with Mabuse's network dismantled, his operatives arrested, his wealth seized. Citopomar is never reached. The empire exists only as a name, a direction, a fantasy that drives the action without ever becoming its destination. In this sense, Citopomar is the novel's most perfect symbol of Mabuse's enterprise: a project of absolute power that is always approaching but never arriving, a destination that recedes as one advances toward it.

## The Name

The name "Citopomar" itself deserves attention. It is not Portuguese — the language of Brazil — nor German, nor any recognizable language. It is a constructed name, a neologism that belongs to no existing linguistic tradition. The act of naming a territory is an act of claiming it, and Mabuse's coinage of "Citopomar" is his first assertion of sovereignty over the empire he has not yet founded. The name is the empire's first and perhaps only reality.

## Significance

Citopomar represents the utopian dimension of Mabuse's criminality — the fantasy that transforms predation into a project, that gives meaning and direction to the accumulation of wealth. Without Citopomar, Mabuse would be merely a very successful criminal; with it, he is a visionary, a builder, a man whose crimes serve a purpose beyond themselves. The empire's status as pure fantasy — a destination never reached, a name without a territory — does not diminish its narrative function. The novel suggests that the most powerful forces in human life are fantasies that have not yet been tested against reality, and Citopomar is the most powerful fantasy Mabuse possesses.
