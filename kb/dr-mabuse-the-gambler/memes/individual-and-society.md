# Individual and Society

The relationship between the individual and society is the novel's most fundamental political concern. [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]] represents the individual will liberated from social constraint — the self that recognizes no law beyond its own desire and no limit beyond its own capacity. [[von-wenk|Von Wenk]] represents the social order that constrains individual will — the network of laws, institutions, and norms that make collective life possible. The novel stages their conflict as a contest between two principles of social organization, and its resolution — von Wenk's partial victory — is an argument for the necessity, and the fragility, of the social order that constrains the individual.

## The Unconstrained Individual

Mabuse is the novel's case study in the unconstrained individual will. He operates according to no law but his own; he recognizes no authority beyond his own judgment; he pursues his desires without regard for the effects on others. His criminality is not a deviation from individualism but its logical extreme — the individual who has fully liberated himself from social constraint and who treats society as a field for the exercise of his will.

The novel's treatment of Mabuse is thus a critique of radical individualism — not an argument against individual freedom but a demonstration of what individual freedom becomes when it is liberated from all constraint. Mabuse is free, in a sense — free from law, from morality, from the obligations that bind individuals to one another — but his freedom is predatory, exercised at the expense of the freedom of others.

## The Social Order

Von Wenk represents the social order that constrains the individual — the legal system, the police, the institutional framework that regulates behavior and protects individuals from predation. But the novel complicates this representation: the social order that von Wenk defends is depicted as fragile, inadequate, and compromised. It cannot protect its citizens from Mabuse's predation; it cannot adapt its methods to the novel forms of criminality it confronts; it cannot even maintain the boundary between its own methods and the methods of the criminals it pursues.

## The Dialectic

The novel's treatment of the individual-society relationship is dialectical rather than dogmatic. It does not simply celebrate society and condemn the individual, or celebrate the individual and condemn society. It traces the mutual dependence and mutual antagonism of the two principles, and it locates the possibility of justice in the tension between them. The individual must be constrained for society to function; society must be constrained for the individual to flourish. The novel's resolution — partial, provisional, unsatisfying — is an acknowledgment that this tension cannot be permanently resolved, only perpetually negotiated.
