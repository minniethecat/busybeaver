# Kriminalpsychologie

*Kriminalpsychologie* — criminal psychology — was an emerging field in early twentieth-century Germany, and its development provides essential context for understanding the novel's treatment of crime and the mind. The intellectual currents that the novel draws on — the psychoanalytic investigation of unconscious motivation, the forensic study of criminal behavior, the medical exploration of hypnotic states — were all part of a broader German-language discourse about the relationship between psychology and crime that was flourishing in the years surrounding the First World War.

## The German Tradition

German-speaking researchers had been central to the development of scientific psychology since the nineteenth century, and the application of psychological methods to the study of crime was a natural extension of this tradition. Figures like Hans Gross, whose *Handbuch für Untersuchungsrichter* (1893) advocated for the psychological study of criminals, and Gustav Aschaffenburg, whose *Das Verbrechen und seine Bekämpfung* (1903) analyzed criminal behavior in psychological terms, had established criminal psychology as a recognized field of inquiry by the time Jacques wrote his novel.

The novel engages with this tradition implicitly rather than explicitly — it does not name the researchers or cite the studies — but its fundamental assumptions about the psychological nature of criminality, the vulnerability of the will, and the possibility of manipulating behavior through psychological technique are all drawn from the discourse of *Kriminalpsychologie* as it existed in the early 1920s.

## The Forensic Imagination

The novel participates in what might be called the forensic imagination of its period — the conviction that crime could be understood, and therefore controlled, through the systematic application of psychological knowledge. Von Wenk's investigation embodies this conviction: he studies the criminal mind, he analyzes criminal methods, he deploys psychological insight as an investigative tool. The investigation is, in part, an exercise in applied *Kriminalpsychologie*.

But the novel also registers the limits of this forensic imagination. The criminal psychology that can classify the burglar and the forger cannot classify Mabuse; the psychological knowledge that can explain ordinary crime cannot explain the criminality that operates through psychology itself. The novel is thus both an expression of the early twentieth-century faith in psychological science and a critique of that faith — a recognition that the most dangerous criminal is the one who has mastered the very knowledge that is meant to control him.

## The Cultural Moment

*Kriminalpsychologie* flourished in a specific cultural moment — the aftermath of the First World War, when traditional frameworks for understanding human behavior had been shattered and the new sciences of the mind promised to replace them. The novel's engagement with criminal psychology reflects this moment, and its ambivalence about psychological knowledge — its simultaneous investment in and skepticism toward the project of understanding crime through the study of the mind — is characteristic of the broader cultural response to the psychological revolution of the early twentieth century.
