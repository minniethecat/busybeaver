# The Swiss Connection

Switzerland haunts the margins of the novel as a space of refuge and possibility — a country whose proximity to [[lake-constance|Lake Constance]] and whose tradition of neutrality make it an ideal base for the international dimensions of [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]]'s operations. The Swiss connection is never fully detailed — the novel's attention remains focused on Munich — but it is referenced sufficiently to establish that Mabuse's network extends beyond Germany's borders, that his resources include foreign accounts and foreign contacts, and that the possibility of escape across the Swiss border is a constant factor in the investigation's strategic calculations (005-126).

## The Border

The Swiss border represents a jurisdictional limit — the line beyond which German law does not apply. Mabuse's operations approach this border without crossing it, maintaining proximity to the possibility of escape while continuing to operate within the territory where their victims are located. The Prince of Komor's estate at Lake Constance, near the Swiss border, is a geographical expression of this strategy: close enough to Switzerland for escape to be possible, far enough within Germany for business to be conducted.

## The International Network

The Swiss connection is one element of a broader international dimension to Mabuse's operations. The criminal network that operates in Munich has links to other countries — to the sources of the capital that funds its operations, to the markets where its proceeds are invested, to the safe havens where its leadership can retreat if the German police close in. This international dimension is characteristic of modern organized crime, and the novel's registration of it — however brief — acknowledges that crime in the twentieth century is not contained by national borders.

## The Escape Route

Switzerland's most significant function in the novel is as an escape route — the destination toward which Mabuse's operatives flee when the investigation closes in. The proximity of the Swiss border to Lake Constance is not coincidental; the novel's climactic events occur where they do partly because the border is near. The possibility of escape — across the lake, across the border, into a jurisdiction where German warrants do not apply — is the permanent background condition of the investigation, and the pressure it exerts shapes every phase of the pursuit.
>

> "No, no," said Spoerri, "we are a Swiss patrol-boat tonight. I heard that the Germans were about, so I ordered the three other boats to act as convoy. One is travelling behind us, the others on each side. Nobody could reach us; we are already in Swiss waters."
>
