# Criminal Psychology

Criminal psychology in *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* is both a subject of investigation and a tool of predation. [[von-wenk|Von Wenk]] studies the criminal mind in an attempt to understand his adversary; [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]] has mastered the criminal mind because he is one — and because his criminality consists precisely in his understanding of how minds work. The novel thus stages a confrontation between two approaches to criminal psychology: the external, investigative approach that seeks to understand crime from outside, and the internal, experiential approach that understands crime because it is crime.

## The Investigator's Psychology

Von Wenk's approach to criminal psychology is systematic and taxonomic. His [[the-criminal-museum-visit|visit to the criminal museum]] represents the institutional form of this approach: the accumulation of data about criminals, the classification of criminal types, the attempt to understand crime through the study of its perpetrators. This approach fails with Mabuse because Mabuse represents a type that the museum's taxonomy does not contain. The criminal psychology that can classify the burglar and the forger cannot classify the mastermind whose criminality operates through psychological manipulation rather than physical action.

## The Criminal's Psychology

Mabuse's understanding of psychology is instrumental rather than academic. He knows how minds work because he has spent his career entering and redirecting them. His knowledge is practical, intuitive, and deeply cynical: he understands that people are governed by desires they cannot control, fears they cannot name, and vulnerabilities they cannot acknowledge.

> In that night of reflection the lawyer saw the criminal no longer as a being of an inferior order. He envisaged him as a man whose pulses raced madly along, his senses stirred by the powers of hell; a man whose lusts and appetites, demon-fed, should overreach themselves and be brought to nought, and he, Wenk, should save and deliver.
>
> (004-004)

This understanding is the foundation of his power, and the novel's detailed depiction of his methods is, in effect, a treatise in applied criminal psychology.

## The Ethics of Psychological Knowledge

The novel raises, implicitly, an ethical question about psychological knowledge: can the knowledge that enables manipulation be separated from the manipulator who uses it? Mabuse's psychological expertise is, in its content, not different from the expertise of a legitimate therapist; the difference lies in the use to which it is put. The novel suggests that psychological knowledge is ethically neutral — it can heal or harm, liberate or enslave — and that the determination of its effects depends entirely on the intentions of the practitioner.

## Significance

Criminal psychology functions in the novel as a case study in the ambivalence of expertise. The same knowledge that enables the investigation of crime also enables the commission of crime; the same understanding that heals also harms. The novel's treatment of criminal psychology is thus not merely a depiction of one criminal's methods but a meditation on the ethical responsibilities that accompany psychological knowledge in a world where the mind has become the primary target of power.
