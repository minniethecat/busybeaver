# The Murder of Edgar Hull

## Summary

Edgar Hull's murder is the novel's first killing — a targeted assassination ordered by Dr. Mabuse and executed by his criminal network in the Jägerstrasse around 2 a.m. Hull, already financially ruined by Mabuse at the baccarat table in [[the-gambling-scene|the opening gambling scene]], had been placed under police protection by State Attorney [[von-wenk|von Wenk]]. His murder transforms the investigation from a routine criminal matter into a personal confrontation between Wenk and a shadowy adversary whose reach extends into every corner of [[munich|Munich]].

## Prelude: The Target

Hull's fate was sealed in the moments after his gambling loss. In a drunken monologue overheard by [[spoerri|Spoerri]], Mabuse dictated the kill order with chilling nonchalance:

> "We'll begin with Herr Hull, for he was the first. … Let me see — Hull, yes, Edgar Hull, 34, Hubertusstrasse. Away with him, his turn first. George will look after it, and you can help him. The Carozza girl can contrive it." (005-181–182)

The division of labor was explicit: George as executor, Cara Carozza as lure, Spoerri as record-keeper. Hull was not killed in passion or chance — he was logged, assigned, and dispatched as a line item in Mabuse's accounting of enemies.

## The Crime Scene

At approximately 2 a.m., Hull emerged from a residence near the Türkenstrasse accompanied by [[cara-carozza|Cara Carozza]], the dancer who was his mistress, and another gentleman — a constable who was, in fact, one of Mabuse's men in disguise. They walked toward the Ludwigstrasse, turned into the Gabelsbergerstrasse, and vanished into the darkness of the Jägerstrasse. A second constable, trailing the group, heard what followed:

> "Suddenly I heard cries; they were shrill and then stifled. The war had taught me that that was how men in fear of death cry out." (008-004)

When Wenk arrived at the scene an hour later, Hull's body lay curled "like the gnarled root of a tree" in a gloomy street-turning behind the Wittelsbach Palace:

> "By the light of an electric torch Wenk gazed upon the corpse. There was a gaping wound from the neck down the back, and the body lay with its face to the earth. … The blood which had flowed from its wounds shone like black marble under the searching light." (008-012)

## The Investigation

Wenk's response to the murder was methodical and forensic. He arrived at the scene within the hour, examined the corpse by electric torch, photographed the details in his memory, recorded the house number, checked doors and windows for witnesses, and searched for footprints:

> "He tried to photograph the details of the scene upon his memory, getting the exact position of the corpse. He wrote down the number of the house, tried to ascertain whether all the doors and windows in the neighbourhood were closed, whether any footprints could be seen, or any objects connected with the crime found in the immediate vicinity, but nothing was to be discovered." (008-012)

The perpetrators had escaped into the Wittelsbach Palace grounds and vanished. A trace of blood on the park wall was the only physical evidence.

## The Cover-up

Recognizing Mabuse's strategy — to weaponize publicity and spread terror — Wenk made the counterintuitive decision to suppress the full story. He visited the press and requested that only a minimal report be published:

> "Behind this assault we are confronted by a gang having at their head a man of apparently enormous and comprehensive powers. … It is his aim to surround his dark deeds by a wall of dread; folks are to realize that no one who makes any attempt against *him* can escape with his life. … We are living in an age of mental and spiritual epidemics, and those who would help to bring healing must be prepared to sacrifice themselves." (008-029)

The published notice was deliberately sparse: "Edgar Hull, Esquire, was attacked and murdered in the Jägerstrasse in the early hours of this morning. The criminals have escaped. It appears to have been an act of revenge, for the murdered man frequented gambling circles" (008-036).

## The City's Reaction

The murder did not stay contained. Jacques describes its spread through Munich in pathological terms — an infection, a monster, an underground drain:

> "The report of the murder ran through the city. … A dark patch remained there, and the pavement was coloured with the blood that had been shed. … From a mere patch it became a monster, reaching from its own narrow corner to spread throughout the town. … Like a dragon it wound itself through the alleys to the broad Ludwigstrasse, crept through the squares to the very heart of the city, and began to overflow all quarters." (008-045)

## Significance

Hull's murder is the act that transforms the novel's stakes. Before it, Wenk is investigating a gambling ring; after it, he is at war with an invisible enemy who can strike at will. The murder also reveals Mabuse's operational method: he does not kill personally but through a network — George, Cara, the false constable — each performing a delegated function. Hull is dead before he knows he is a target, and his death serves a strategic purpose: it is a message that [[mabuse-criminal-network|Mabuse's organization]] will eliminate anyone who threatens it.
