# Prince of Komor

The Prince of Komor (and Komorek) is an exiled aristocrat living on the shores of Lake Constance, whose mansion serves as the setting for the novel's final social gathering. His presence introduces a cosmopolitan and slightly disreputable European aristocracy into the novel's otherwise predominantly German milieu. Like many aristocrats in Weimar-era fiction, the Prince represents a displaced old order — maintaining the forms of princely life while stripped of actual political power.

## Background

The Prince's history is relayed by von Wenk's informant, a fellow countryman who provides the details of the Prince's fall from power:

> "Now I must tell you something about our host and my fellow-countrymen out there by the Lake," he said directly the car started. "He was formerly Prince of Komor and Komorek, and he married a Viennese dancer." (020-002)

The informant's tone is dismissive — the Prince's title is a thing of the past, his marriage to a dancer a mark of social decline rather than romance. The informant continues with barely concealed contempt:

> "You've done with your Prince." (020-002)

Yet the Prince retains enough of his former wealth and status to maintain a substantial estate. The informant concedes:

> "The only thing that is still 'princely' about him is his mansion out yonder." (020-002)

The quotation marks around "princely" are the informant's own — a verbal sneer that acknowledges the gap between the Prince's present circumstances and his former dignity.

## The Princess

The Prince's wife, [[princess-komor|the Princess]], is a former Viennese dancer whose social ascent through marriage marks her as an ambiguous figure in the novel's class system. The informant observes:

> "His wife is very smart and exclusive — more exclusive than a princess." (020-002)

The comment is barbed: the dancer who has become a princess performs aristocracy more convincingly than the genuine article. This observation connects to the novel's broader concern with performance, authenticity, and social imposture — themes central to [[disguise-and-secret-identity|Mabuse's methods]] and to Weimar culture more generally.

## The Lake Constance Gathering

The Prince's evening party brings together an international cast — Hungarian aristocrats, German police officials, exiled royalty — in a setting that combines luxury with latent menace. The Prince himself presides with studied grace:

> "Good evening, Prince," said the police superintendent to a man who bowed to Wenk in what the latter considered a slightly affected way; and as his host raised his head again, Wenk looked into a swarthy face with a black moustache, strongly resembling the Hungarian type. (020-024)

The Prince's appearance — "swarthy," "Hungarian type" — marks him as foreign, exotic, slightly suspect in the context of Bavarian society. His manners, however, are impeccable:

> The Prince, who in appearance was somewhat common, possessed the most finished manners. (020-025)

## Significance

The Prince of Komor functions as a representative of the displaced European aristocracy that populated Weimar Germany's fashionable circles. Stripped of political power but retaining wealth and social pretension, such figures created a milieu in which distinctions between legitimate and illegitimate, authentic and counterfeit, became increasingly difficult to maintain — exactly the conditions in which a figure like [[dr-mabuse|Mabuse]] could flourish. The Prince's mansion, the last "princely" thing about him, becomes the setting for the unmasking of criminals — a fitting end for a space that has itself become a stage for the performance of an identity that no longer corresponds to any reality.
