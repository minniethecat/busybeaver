# Inspector Schramm

## Role in the Narrative

Inspector Schramm is von Wenk's steady counterpart — the institutional detective who provides the investigation with method, procedure, and continuity. Where von Wenk goes undercover and adopts disguise, Schramm works through official channels: informants, records, surveillance reports. Their partnership represents the collaboration between the extraordinary individual (the Staatsanwalt who personally infiltrates the underworld) and the ordinary institution (the police who sustain the investigation over time).

## Biography

Schramm is a career police officer, experienced and methodical. He maintains the network of informants that provides von Wenk with raw intelligence, and his knowledge of Munich's criminal geography is encyclopedic. He is present at key moments — the gambling den raid, the interrogations, the pursuit — but always in his institutional role, never stepping outside it as von Wenk does.

## Key Appearances

- **The investigation briefing (Ch. III)**: Schramm provides von Wenk with the institutional knowledge necessary to begin the investigation
- **The gambling den raid (Ch. VII)**: He participates in the police action, his professional competence providing the operation's backbone

## Relationships

Schramm's relationship with von Wenk is one of professional complementarity — the methodical detective supporting the brilliant prosecutor. His loyalty is to the institution rather than to any individual, making him a reliable but limited ally.

## Analysis

Schramm represents the novel's acknowledgment that individual heroism — von Wenk's undercover work, his disguises, his personal risk — is insufficient without institutional support. The investigation succeeds not because von Wenk is extraordinary but because the police apparatus, embodied by men like Schramm, provides the infrastructure that makes extraordinary action possible.
