# Von Wenk Meets Cara Carozza

The first encounter between [[von-wenk|von Wenk]] and [[cara-carozza|Cara Carozza]] is the moment when the investigation becomes personal. Von Wenk, attending the [[the-bonbonniere|Bonbonnière]] in his professional capacity — to observe, to gather intelligence, to map the geography of [[dr-mabuse|Mabuse]]'s network — finds himself drawn to Cara in a way that exceeds professional interest. The encounter initiates a relationship that will complicate every subsequent phase of the investigation, introducing the element of personal desire into what should be a purely professional pursuit.

## The Investigative Context

Von Wenk's presence at the Bonbonnière is officially investigative. Cara has been identified as an associate of Mabuse, and her performances at the club provide an opportunity for surveillance. Von Wenk watches her dance, noting her movements, her associates, her comings and goings — all the data that a professional investigator collects. But the data does not remain professional. The dancer who is the object of surveillance becomes, in the course of the evening, the object of something less easily classified.

> In the middle of the room stood a fairly large table of an oval shape, but so arranged that every occupant of an armchair was sitting in a hollowed-out niche of his own, with the leaves of the table under his elbows on both sides. The table was formed of quaint, curiously veined Kiefersfeld marble. In the middle only was there a perfectly white oval left. Around the table, behind the players' chairs, the floor was raised and the walls furnished with full-length reclining lounges, upon which rested crushed-strawberry-coloured cushions with black designs. A large shade of polished glass attached to a brass electrolier hung low over the table and reflected the electric light bulbs which gleamed forth from silver brackets. The walls above the strawberry-coloured cushions were inlaid with the same warm marble as that on the table.

## The Erotic Complication

Von Wenk's response to Cara is registered with an honesty that is characteristic of the novel's psychological realism. He is attracted to her, and the attraction troubles him. His self-admonition — "I am searching for a criminal and am about to fall in love with someone whom I may have to send to prison tomorrow. This is really idiotic!" (003-040) — is both a confession and a defense, acknowledging the attraction while attempting to contain it through self-mockery.

The attraction is not merely personal; it is structural. Cara is a dancer, and her professional identity is bound up with the production of desire. Von Wenk is an investigator, and his professional identity is bound up with the suppression of desire in favor of rational analysis. The encounter stages a collision between these two modes of being, and the collision is not resolved — it persists throughout the novel, complicating every interaction between the two characters.

## The Beginning of Entanglement

The meeting initiates a pattern of entanglement that will define both characters' trajectories. Cara, assigned by Mabuse to monitor von Wenk, becomes his unwitting source of information. Von Wenk, assigned to investigate Cara, becomes her unwitting protector. Each is using the other; each is being used; and the boundary between strategy and feeling, manipulation and genuine connection, becomes increasingly difficult to locate.
