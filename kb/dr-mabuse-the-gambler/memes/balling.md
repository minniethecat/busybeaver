# Balling

**Balling** is the false name used by [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]] during the opening card-game scene
at the club, where he manipulates [[edgar-hull|Edgar Hull]] into a debt of twenty thousand marks.
The identity of "Herr Balling, Room 15, Excelsior Hotel" is the narrative mechanism by which
Mabuse ensnares his first victim, setting the entire plot of Norbert Jacques' novel in motion.

## The Card Game

At a private gaming club, an elderly gentleman introduced by Hull himself — later giving the
name **Balling** — played a quiet, restrained game of _vingt-et-un_ (twenty-one), frequently
losing small amounts:

> Hull got his little notebook back, he read in it:
>
> > **Balling**, Room 15, Excelsior Hotel.
>
> (001-025)

Hull, who had been drinking, played recklessly and accumulated a debt of twenty thousand marks
to the stranger. Balling then rose, thanked the company, and departed:

> "I am ready to give you your revenge, Herr von Hull," said Balling, as he rose.
> "Gentlemen, may I offer you my thanks for the evening's hospitality? Good night!"
>
> (001-027)

## The Identity Misdirection

When Hull attempted to repay the debt the following day, he discovered the deception.
A man named Balling did occupy Room 15 at the Excelsior Hotel — but he was not the man
from the card game:

| Detail | The Card-Player "Balling" | The Real Balling (Room 15) |
|--------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Appearance | About sixty, large grey eyes, big nose, white whiskers, tall grey hat, imposing presence | Short and stout |
| Identity | Dr. Mabuse in disguise | A genuine hotel guest |
| Knowledge of Hull | Claims acquaintance through the game | Has never met Hull |

Hull's confusion is captured as he descends into unease:

> But this tale of Herr Balling and the twenty thousand marks had some mystery or other
> behind it. He had forgotten that it was he who introduced the stranger to the club.
> He had played as if he had lost his head. He had incurred a debt of twenty thousand
> marks, and his creditor had furnished a name and address which did actually exist
> but were not his.
>
> (001-061)

## Mabuse's Technique

The Balling persona demonstrates Mabuse's modus operandi in microcosm:

- **Psychological manipulation**: Mabuse allowed Hull to win early rounds, building false confidence
  before the stakes escalated.
- **Identity theft**: The real Balling existed — a detail that made the deception harder to detect
  ("the Balling of No. 15" (001-059)).
- **The unpayable debt**: Hull could not discharge the obligation because his creditor had vanished,
  creating a psychological burden that Mabuse exploited.
- **The I.O.U. as leverage**: The signed promissory note — "Herr Balling, I.O.U. 20,000
  (twenty thousand marks)" (003-061) — became a tool of control.

[[von-wenk|State Prosecutor von Wenk]] later used Balling's name as a key point in his
investigation:

> "But the Herr Balling in the hotel, who was quite different from the Balling at the club?"
>
> (002-012)

## The Counterfeit Context

The Balling affair acquired a deeper significance when tied to Mabuse's broader
counterfeit operation. The twenty-thousand-mark gambling debt was not merely about the
money — it demonstrated Mabuse's ability to manipulate value itself:

> Because the pseudo Herr Balling desired thus to give an example.
>
> (008-002)

The real Balling in Room 15 was, per one interpretation, an innocent bystander whose
identity Mabuse had researched and appropriated — a technique he would later employ
with other aliases and disguises throughout the narrative.

## Role in the Plot

Balling serves as the **inciting mechanism** of the novel's investigation thread.
The episode:

1. Introduces Mabuse's method: disguise, psychological manipulation, and the
   exploitation of chance encounters
2. Draws von Wenk into the case when Hull reports the suspicious circumstances
3. Establishes the pattern of Mabuse using false identities to entrap victims
4. Demonstrates that Mabuse's power extends to manipulating the very concept
   of identity and obligation
