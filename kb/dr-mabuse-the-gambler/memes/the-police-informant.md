# The Police Informant

The police informant is one of the novel's most significant unnamed characters — a figure whose identity is never revealed but whose information is essential to [[von-wenk|von Wenk]]'s investigation. He provides "a list of all the secret gambling-dens" (004-007) and later, during the [[the-train-journey|train journey to Lake Constance]], delivers the essential background about the [[prince-komor|Prince of Komor]] and his household. The informant operates in the gray zone between the criminal underworld and the forces of law, and his anonymity is both a practical necessity (he would be in danger if his identity were known) and a thematic statement (the investigation depends on relationships that cannot be openly acknowledged).

> "He was formerly Prince of Komor and Komorek, and he married a Viennese dancer. Of course, his people were furious! They made things so disagreeable for him that one day he said, 'All right: you've gone too far. You've done with your Prince. From today I am plain Komorek,' and then he wandered off. He was very rich, anyhow, and not in any way dependent on his people."
>
> (020-002)

## The Informant's Position

The informant occupies a position that is neither fully legitimate nor fully criminal. He has access to the criminal underworld — he knows the locations of the gambling dens, the identities of the operatives, the gossip of the network — but he is not, or not fully, a member of that underworld. His relationship to von Wenk is transactional: he provides information in exchange for money, protection, or the satisfaction of personal grievances that the novel does not specify.

This transactional relationship raises ethical questions that the novel acknowledges without resolving. The informant is not a citizen performing a civic duty; he is a source motivated by interests that may not align with the interests of justice. The information he provides is valuable but potentially unreliable, and von Wenk's dependence on it is both a practical necessity and a potential vulnerability.

## The Informant as Character Type

The informant belongs to a recognizable character type in crime fiction: the shadowy figure who moves between worlds, whose knowledge is both a resource and a threat, whose loyalty is always provisional. The novel's treatment of this type is notably restrained: the informant is not romanticized (he is not a noble criminal helping the police out of conscience) or demonized (he is not a treacherous villain whose information will prove to be a trap). He is simply a person who knows things and is willing to sell them, and the investigation must take him as he is.

## The Informant's Knowledge

The informant's knowledge is specific and practical: the addresses of gambling dens, the backgrounds of aristocrats, the gossip of the criminal underworld. He does not possess strategic understanding — he cannot explain how the network operates or who ultimately controls it — but he possesses the tactical intelligence that von Wenk needs to transform general suspicion into specific action. The informant is the investigation's eyes in spaces that the police cannot directly observe.
