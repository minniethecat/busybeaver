# The Bonbonnière Dressing Room

The dressing room is a space of transition — the threshold between the public identity of the performer and the private identity of the person, between the stage where [[cara-carozza|Cara Carozza]] commands the audience's gaze and the backstage where she sheds the costume and the persona of performance. It is in the dressing room that Cara is most herself — and most vulnerable. The public protections of the stage — the distance from the audience, the conventions of performance — do not apply in the dressing room, and the visitors who seek access to her there seek access to a person rather than a performer.

## The Private Space

The dressing room is Cara's only genuinely private space in the novel. The stage is public, the gambling den is criminal, the city is surveilled — but the dressing room, with its closed door and its mirror and its accumulated personal effects, is a space that belongs to her. Here she can be, momentarily, not the dancer, not the operative, not the object of the gaze — simply herself.

The novel does not linger in the dressing room, and its depictions of it are brief. But the room's existence as a private space — a space that is not accessible to the investigation, not legible to the state, not commanded by Mabuse — is significant. It represents a limit to the penetration of the various gazes that structure the novel's world, a residue of privacy that survives the erosion of privacy elsewhere.


> A few days afterwards Hull made acquaintance with a girl who was performing jazz dances in the Bonbonnière. She was partly Mexican, she told him. She soon effected a diversion in his thoughts, and in her company he rapidly got rid of the twenty thousand marks which he could not pay over to the stranger.
>

## The Dressing Room as Vulnerability

The dressing room's privacy is also its vulnerability. The space that is not observed is a space where anything can happen, and the visitors who seek access to the dressing room — Mabuse's operatives, von Wenk's investigators, the patrons who mistake professional performance for personal availability — represent the threats that privacy cannot defend against. The dressing room is a sanctuary without locks, a private space whose privacy is a function of convention rather than of security.

## The Mirror

The dressing room mirror is a recurring motif — the surface in which Cara examines herself, adjusts her appearance, and confronts the image that she presents to the world. The mirror is the instrument of self-surveillance, the technology through which the performer manages the identity that the audience will see. But the mirror also presents a question: who is the person in the mirror, and is she the same person who performs on stage, who serves Mabuse, who exists when no one is looking?
