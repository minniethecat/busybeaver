# Paranoia and Surveillance

Paranoia is the psychological condition produced by the world Mabuse has created — a world in which anyone might be watching, anyone might be reporting, and the boundary between observer and observed has become permanently unstable.

> "We are pursued, or at any rate under surveillance," he thought. "Can it be that lawyer-detective Wenk?" Calmly, yet defiantly, he got his pistols ready. In the darkness he tried to discern what flag the Rhine was carrying, but it was impossible to find out.
>
> (005-124) The novel depicts paranoia not as a pathology of individual minds but as a rational response to an environment in which surveillance is ubiquitous and the sources of surveillance are unknown.

## The Watched Self

Mabuse's operatives live in a condition of permanent surveillance. They are watched by the police, who track their movements and document their associations (009-044); they are watched by Mabuse, who monitors their loyalty and disposes of them when they become liabilities; they watch each other, reporting infractions and currying favor. The criminal network is, among other things, a surveillance apparatus — a system for ensuring that everyone within it is visible to the center.

This internal surveillance is mirrored by external surveillance. Von Wenk's investigation extends the gaze of the state into spaces that had previously been private. The gambling den, the hotel room, the private residence — all become legible to the police, their secrets extracted and filed. The novel's Munich is a city in which privacy has ceased to exist as a reliable condition.

## The Paranoia of the Investigator

Von Wenk is not exempt from the paranoia he helps to create. His investigation places him in danger, and his awareness of that danger transforms his experience of the urban environment. Every stranger is a potential threat; every encounter is a potential trap. The paranoia that should be the criminal's burden becomes the investigator's as well — the price of operating in the same gray zone as the criminals he pursues.

The novel registers this paranoia through von Wenk's increasing isolation. As the investigation progresses, he trusts fewer people, shares less information, relies more heavily on his own judgment. The network of professional relationships that sustained him at the investigation's beginning narrows to a handful of trusted subordinates. The investigator who began by consulting the criminal museum ends by consulting only himself.

## Paranoia as Epistemology

The novel suggests that paranoia, in Mabuse's world, is not a distortion of reality but an accurate perception of it. The person who believes they are being watched often is being watched (005-124); the person who suspects conspiracy often is the target of conspiracy. Paranoia is not a failure of epistemology but an adaptation to an environment in which conventional epistemological trust — the assumption that appearances are generally reliable, that strangers are generally benign — has become a liability.

## Significance

Paranoia and surveillance function in the novel as the psychological cost of the investigation — the price that both criminals and investigators pay for operating in a world of ubiquitous observation. The novel's depiction of this condition is prophetic: the surveillance society that Mabuse creates in miniature would become, in the course of the twentieth century, a feature of modern states, and the paranoia that the novel diagnoses as a rational response to that society would become a permanent feature of modern consciousness.
