# The Criminal Gaze

If the police deploy [[police-surveillance|surveillance]] against crime, crime deploys surveillance against its victims, its operatives, and the police themselves. The criminal gaze — the practice of observation from the perspective of the criminal enterprise — is one of the novel's most persistent motifs, and its treatment of this gaze complicates any simple opposition between the watching state and the watched criminal. In Mabuse's world, everyone is watching, and everyone is watched.

## The Gaze on the Victim

The criminal gaze is directed first at the victim — the gambler at the table, the patient in the consulting room, the aristocrat at the social gathering. Mabuse's operatives observe their targets, gathering the information — about desires, about vulnerabilities, about routines and relationships — that enables effective manipulation. The victim is watched before they are exploited, and the watching is the precondition of the exploitation.

## The Gaze on the Operative

The criminal gaze is also directed inward, at the network's own members. Operatives watch each other, reporting infractions and maintaining discipline; the center watches the periphery for signs of disloyalty or incompetence. The network is a surveillance apparatus as well as a criminal enterprise, and the gaze that it directs at its own members is one of the mechanisms through which it maintains control.

## The Gaze on the Police

The criminal gaze is also directed at the police — the investigators who are themselves investigators, the watchers who are themselves watched. The novel registers the possibility of counter-surveillance — the criminal observation of police operations — without dramatizing it extensively. But the possibility is always present: the police who watch the criminals may themselves be watched, and the asymmetry of observation that gives the police their advantage is always precarious.

## The Reciprocity of Gazes

The novel's treatment of the criminal gaze, in conjunction with its treatment of the police gaze, establishes a reciprocity of observation that is one of its most distinctive features. The world of the novel is a world in which everyone is looking and everyone is looked at, in which the gaze is a weapon that both sides deploy, in which the contest between crime and law is also a contest between two ways of seeing. The reciprocity of gazes is the visual expression of the novel's deeper reciprocity — the mirroring of criminal and investigator, the convergence of opposed methods, the blurring of the boundary between the watcher and the watched.
