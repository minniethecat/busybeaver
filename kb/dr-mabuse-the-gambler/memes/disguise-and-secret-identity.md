# Disguise and Secret Identity

## Definition

Disguise is the central operational method in the cat-and-mouse conflict between Dr. Mabuse and State Prosecutor von Wenk. Both the criminal and the investigator adopt false appearances — Mabuse to conceal his criminal empire, Wenk to infiltrate it. Disguise in this work is not mere costuming; it is a psychological weapon, a form of dominance over perception, and the narrative engine that drives the plot through its many reversals and revelations.

## Manifestation in the Text

Von Wenk, realizing that conventional policing is useless against Mabuse, decides to abandon his official identity entirely. In a moment of self-admonishment, he resolves: "I must make use of disguise and secret spying. I must be prepared to stake myself on the game … must be myself the snare, if I do not want to be caught in it like a silly pigeon" ((003-022)). He studies his own face in the mirror, finding that his features "lent themselves very well to disguise" ((003-023)).

> "I must set to work in quite another way," he said to himself. "Goodwill and industry are not sufficient. Self-denial and inexorable self-discipline and a little more cunning are necessary! I must make use of every ruse that my opponent displays. … I must make use of disguise and secret spying. I must be prepared to stake myself on the game … must be myself the snare, if I do not want to be caught in it like a silly pigeon. … A State official with a false beard … a Browning concealed in his fist … a jockey-cap, a tall hat, a wig, and so on, like the cinema detective."
>
> (003-022)

Wenk adopts his first major persona as "a rich old gentleman from the provinces," modeled on an elderly uncle:

> Wenk visited these places evening by evening. He went disguised as a rich old gentleman from the provinces. He had chosen this disguise, first of all, because he had an excellent example of it in an elderly uncle whom he merely had to copy.
>
> (004-008)

This disguise becomes his gateway into the gambling underworld that Mabuse controls. The transformation  is not merely external — it requires Wenk to inhabit a different psychology, to learn the mannerisms and habits of another social class.

Mabuse's mastery of disguise is even more complete. Wenk eventually comes to recognize that beneath every false identity — the sandy-bearded gambler, the chauffeur, the cyclist messenger — lurks the same man. In the climactic recognition scene, Wenk "divested the face of its false moustache, eyebrows and wig, seeing beneath it the smooth-shaven, well-formed cranium of Dr. Mabuse" ((020-091)). The verb *divested* captures the layered nature of Mabuse's deceptions: identity is something worn, like clothing, and can be shed at will.

Cara, one of the few characters to see through Wenk's disguise at the gambling club, tells him: "You were well disguised, but I could not believe that here in Munich there would be two such quaint little monkeys on a cherry-tree" ((006-028)). Her recognition by means of a distinctive piece of jewelry rather than facial features emphasizes that disguise operates on multiple levels — physical appearance, accessory, behavior, and context.

Disguise also shapes the novel's set-piece sequences. When the police raid the secret gambling den, "the uniformed police threw off their disguise and appeared in evening dress, laughing" ((007-015)), revealing that authority, too, wears masks. The entire gambling establishment is itself a theatrical construction — a hidden venue accessed through a barber shop, where the floor becomes the ceiling and appearances are systematically unreliable.

## Context

The motif of disguise draws on the rich tradition of the criminal mastermind in early 20th-century crime fiction — from Sherlock Holmes's Moriarty to Fantômas. However, Lang and Jacques push it further into the realm of psychological horror. Mabuse's disguises are not mere practical tools; they are expressions of a fractured self, anticipating later psychoanalytic theories of the divided subject. In Weimar Germany, a society rebuilding its own identity after the collapse of the Kaiserreich, the theme of unstable identity carried particular resonance. The period saw a flourishing of interest in hypnosis, suggestion, and the malleability of the self — themes that permeate the novel.

## Significance

Disguise in *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* is the narrative manifestation of the work's deepest philosophical concern: the instability of identity itself. Mabuse, who can be anyone, ultimately risks becoming no one — his self dissolved into the succession of his masks. Wenk, who must become someone else to catch him, risks the same dissolution. The theme elevates the novel from a simple crime thriller into a meditation on the nature of selfhood in the modern world.
