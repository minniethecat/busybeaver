# Visual Culture in Weimar Germany

The world of *Dr. Mabuse: the Gambler* is saturated with visual media — cinema, photography, the illustrated press, the theatrical spectacle — and the novel's preoccupation with looking and being looked at, with [[gaze-and-observation|the gaze]] and its objects, must be understood against the background of the emerging visual culture of Weimar Germany. The early 1920s were a moment of rapid transformation in the visual environment: cinema was becoming a mass medium, photography was proliferating in newspapers and magazines, and the experience of urban life was increasingly organized around visual spectacle. The novel registers this transformation at every level of its narrative.

## The Cinema

Cinema is the novel's most explicit engagement with the new visual culture. The [[go-ahead-institute|Go-Ahead Institute]]'s garde-police is described as "the most ordinary kind of cinema" (007-017) — a dismissal that registers both the familiarity of cinematic technology and its potential for criminal appropriation. The cinema that should be a medium of entertainment becomes, in Mabuse's hands, a mechanism of deception — a technology for producing illusions that the audience cannot distinguish from reality.

> "This is only an ordinary cinema," said Wenk, turning to Karstens, and whispering, "the most ordinary kind of cinema. If the police were to come, they would discover the whole trick in ten minutes."
>
> (007-017)

Lang's [[dr-mabuse-the-gambler-1922|1922 film adaptation]] would, of course, complete the circuit between the novel and the cinema, translating the novel's concern with visuality into the medium that the novel had identified as both symptom and symbol of the new visual culture.

## Photography and Surveillance

Photography, though less prominent in the novel than cinema, is implicitly present in the investigation's methods. The criminal museum that von Wenk consults contains photographs of criminals — the visual record of the state's effort to document and classify those who violate its laws. Photography is the technology of surveillance, the mechanism through which the criminal is made permanently visible to the state, and its presence in the novel — however implicit — registers the intersection of visual culture and police power.

## The Illustrated Press

The press, which the novel treats under the rubric of [[press-and-public-opinion|public opinion]], was increasingly visual in the early 1920s. Illustrated magazines proliferated; photographs accompanied news stories; the public's experience of current events was increasingly mediated by images as well as text. The novel's attention to the press's role in shaping public perception of crime reflects this visual turn in journalism, and its concern with the manipulation of perception extends to the visual register as well as the verbal.

## The Society of Spectacle

The novel anticipates, in its treatment of visual culture, the analysis of the "society of the spectacle" that would become central to twentieth-century cultural theory. The world Mabuse creates — a world of performances, illusions, and manipulated perceptions — is a world in which the visual has become the primary medium of power, and the ability to control what others see is the most effective form of control. The novel's diagnosis of this condition, a century before the age of social media and digital imagery, is remarkably prescient.
