## Description

The Four Seasons Hall is a public auditorium on Munich's Maximilianstrasse, situated between the Marstall and the grand boulevard. It is a venue for lectures, demonstrations, and public entertainments — the kind of space where a well-dressed audience of Munich society gathers for an evening of edification. On the night that matters to the narrative, the hall hosts Dr. Mabuse's lecture on hypnotism, advertised by a notice at the entrance. (016-001)(016-002)

## Role in the Narrative

State Prosecutor [[von-wenk|von Wenk]], walking from the Marstall to Maximilianstrasse, passes the Four Seasons Hall and glimpses a familiar back and shoulders in the crowd struggling to enter — the "sandy-bearded gambler" he has been hunting across Germany. The clue crystallizes when he sees a lottery advertisement with the words "Lucky Chance," which triggers his memory of the gambler. Returning to the hall, he reads the notice: "Dr. Mabuse was giving a lecture, with experiments, there that evening." (016-001)

Wenk immediately summons plainclothes officers, seals all exits, and searches the audience row by row during the lecturer's preparation — but Mabuse has vanished. Among the attendees Wenk notes Privy Councillor Wendel in the front row. The failed capture becomes a turning point: Wenk has seen his quarry in public, but Mabuse's disappearance from a locked hall deepens the mystery of his elusiveness. (016-002)(016-003)

Later, at Komorek's Nicholas Lake villa, Wenk's mind returns to the Four Seasons Hall as the place where he saw Mabuse in his "real form" — tall, stooping slightly, clean-shaven, with a broad face, large nose, and large grey eyes. He contrasts this image with the disguised Professor and the bearded gambler, the venue having provided the one unvarnished sighting of the criminal beneath his multiple masks. (020-010)

## Real-World Context

The Four Seasons Hall appears to be a fictional venue, though its name evokes Munich's Hotel Vier Jahreszeiten (Four Seasons Hotel) on Maximilianstrasse, one of the city's most prestigious addresses. Lang may have modelled the hall on the actual lecture rooms and assembly halls that lined Munich's cultural boulevards in the early 1920s, where public lectures on science, art, and the occult drew middle-class audiences. Mabuse's choice of such a venue — appearing before the public as a learned lecturer — epitomizes his audacity and his gift for hiding in plain sight.

## Significance

The Four Seasons Hall scene encapsulates the central paradox of Mabuse's criminality: he is simultaneously hidden and exposed, invisible and public. That he can stand on a stage before Munich's respectable citizens — including a Privy Councillor in the front row — and then vanish from a locked and searched auditorium demonstrates his near-supernatural capacity for evasion. For Wenk, the venue becomes a site of frustrated recognition: he has seen the man he seeks but cannot lay hands on him, a pattern that repeats throughout the investigation until the final confrontation.
