# The Automobile

The automobile is the technology of criminal mobility in Mabuse's world — the vehicle that enables rapid movement through Munich's streets, quick escape from the scenes of crimes, and the compression of space that makes the network's distributed operations possible. It is, along with the telephone and the train, one of the technologies of modernity that Mabuse's criminal enterprise both depends on and exemplifies, and its presence in the novel registers the transformation of urban life by the internal combustion engine.

## Speed and Escape

The automobile's primary function in the novel is as a vehicle of escape. The car that drives without lights through dark Munich streets, the car that accelerates away from the pursuing [[schmied|Sergeant Schmied]] on his bicycle, the car that transports operatives between the network's nodes — each is an instance of the automobile's capacity to outrun pursuit. The car is faster than the bicycle, faster than the pedestrian, faster than any mode of transportation that the police can routinely deploy.

## The Automobile and the City

The automobile transforms the experience of the city. Distances that would require hours on foot are compressed into minutes by car; neighborhoods that are separate in pedestrian experience become contiguous in automotive experience; the city's geography is reorganized around the routes and speeds that the automobile makes possible. The criminal network that deploys automobiles can operate across a larger territory, with greater speed, and with less visibility than a network dependent on foot traffic or public transportation.

## The Automobile as Status

The automobile is also a marker of status — the vehicle that distinguishes the wealthy criminal from the ordinary one, the mastermind from the operative. Mabuse's operatives drive automobiles; the police pursue on bicycles. The automobile is the technology of the criminal elite, and its presence in the novel is an index of the asymmetry of resources between crime and law enforcement.

## The Darkened Automobile

The automobile driving without lights — a recurring image in the novel — is the perfect symbol of criminal mobility: the vehicle that moves through the city unseen, its presence registered only as sound, its destination unknown, its occupants invisible. The darkened automobile is the technological expression of the secrecy that Mabuse's operations require, and its appearance in the novel is always a sign that something criminal is in motion.

## Significance

The automobile functions in the novel as a technology of modernity that has been appropriated for criminal purposes. It is not inherently criminal — the police also use automobiles, though fewer and less powerful ones — but its concentration in criminal hands is a measure of the resource asymmetry that characterizes the contest between law and crime. The automobile is the vehicle of the future, and in Mabuse's world, the future belongs to whoever can afford the fastest car.
