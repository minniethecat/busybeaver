# The Testament of Dr. Mabuse (Film)

*The Testament of Dr. Mabuse* (German: *Das Testament des Dr. Mabuse*) is Fritz Lang's 1933 sequel to his 1922 film *[[dr-mabuse-the-gambler-film|Dr. Mabuse, the Gambler]]*. Where the original film adapted Norbert Jacques's novel, the sequel was based on an original screenplay by Lang and his wife Thea von Harbou, drawing on elements from Jacques's later Mabuse novel *Mabuse's Colony*. The film represents a significant evolution in the Mabuse mythos: the criminal mastermind, now confined to an asylum, continues to direct a criminal empire through written manifestos that seem to exercise a hypnotic power over those who read them.

## Narrative

The film opens with [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]] imprisoned in the asylum of Professor Baum. Unable to speak, Mabuse spends his days furiously writing — producing detailed plans for crimes that are, impossibly, being executed in the outside world by a criminal organization that has no contact with him. The investigation, led by Commissioner Lohmann (a character Lang introduced in his 1931 film *M*), gradually uncovers the mechanism of Mabuse's continuing influence: a form of psychic domination that transcends physical confinement.

The film's central mystery — how can a man who never leaves his cell direct a criminal empire? — extends the original novel's concern with the relationship between psychological power and physical constraint. Mabuse, imprisoned, is more dangerous than Mabuse free, because imprisonment has concentrated his will into its purest form: the written word that commands and the psychic force that compels.

## Political Context

*The Testament of Dr. Mabuse* was made in the shadow of the Nazi seizure of power. Lang completed the film in early 1933, and it was promptly banned by the Nazi regime, which recognized — correctly — that the film's depiction of a criminal mastermind who exercises power through psychological manipulation and written commands was an unwelcome commentary on their own methods. The film was not released in Germany until after the war.

The film's political dimension transforms the Mabuse character from a criminal mastermind into a figure for authoritarian power as such. The manifestos that command crimes from beyond the asylum walls are, at one level, a representation of the Führerprinzip — the principle of absolute leadership that organized Nazi political practice. Lang, in exile after 1933, would later acknowledge the political intention of the film, though he was careful to note that the allegory was general rather than specific.

## Relation to the Original

*The Testament* deepens the original's treatment of [[hypnotic-suggestion|hypnosis]] and [[psychological-manipulation|psychological manipulation]] by removing the physical person of the manipulator. Mabuse in the sequel is pure will, pure command, pure text — a presence that operates without a body. This abstraction of Mabuse's power from physical presence to psychic influence represents the logical endpoint of the original's trajectory: the mastermind who began by hypnotizing individual gamblers at a card table now commands a criminal army from an asylum cell, his will transmitted through the very walls that confine him.

## Significance

*The Testament of Dr. Mabuse* is a landmark of Weimar and early Nazi-era cinema, and its suppression by the regime gives it a historical significance that extends beyond its considerable artistic merits. The film represents the culmination of Lang's engagement with the Mabuse material and, more broadly, with the themes of psychological power, criminal organization, and social chaos that animated his work throughout the Weimar period.
