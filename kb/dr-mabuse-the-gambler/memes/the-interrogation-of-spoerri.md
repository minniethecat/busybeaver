# The Interrogation of Spoerri

[[spoerri|Spoerri]], one of [[dr-mabuse|Dr. Mabuse]]'s household servants, is interrogated by the police as part of the investigation into the criminal network. His interrogation is a test of the network's compartmentalized structure: how much does a servant know, and how much of what he knows will he reveal under questioning? The answers — partial, evasive, shaped by loyalty and fear — reveal both the effectiveness of Mabuse's information management and its vulnerabilities.

## The Servant's Knowledge

Spoerri occupies a paradoxical position in the network's information economy. As a household servant, he has intimate access to Mabuse's daily life — his movements, his visitors, his habits — that no outside observer could match. But this access is confined to the domestic sphere; Spoerri knows the surface of Mabuse's operations without knowing their depth. The servant who opens the door to visitors does not know the purpose of their visits; the servant who drives the car does not know the destination of the journey.

The interrogation probes the boundaries of Spoerri's knowledge, seeking the points where domestic intimacy meets operational awareness. The information he can provide — the times of comings and goings, the descriptions of visitors, the fragments of overheard conversation — is valuable but partial, and the investigator must assemble these fragments into a larger picture that Spoerri himself cannot see.

## The Servant's Loyalty

Spoerri's loyalty to Mabuse is a factor in the interrogation. The servant who has served the master for years, who depends on the master for livelihood and identity, does not easily betray that trust — even when the master is a criminal and the interrogator represents the law. Loyalty is a habit, a structure of feeling, a relationship that persists even when its object has been exposed as unworthy.

Spoerri's appearance before Mabuse — dressed as a soldier — underscores his role as an instrument of Mabuse's will rather than an autonomous agent (014-016):

> Spoerri entered. He was dressed as a soldier. "What is that for?" asked Mabuse morosely, but he did not wait for an answer, and asked about George's movements.
>
> (014-016)

## The Limits of Interrogation

The interrogation of Spoerri demonstrates, once again, the limits of interrogation as an investigative method. The information that can be extracted through questioning is limited by the knowledge the subject possesses and the loyalty the subject maintains. The interrogation produces fragments, not confessions; clues, not revelations. The investigator who depends on interrogation must supplement it with other methods — surveillance, analysis, the cultivation of other sources — and the combination of methods, rather than any single method, is what produces results.
