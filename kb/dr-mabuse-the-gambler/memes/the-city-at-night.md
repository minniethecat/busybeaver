# The City at Night

Night transforms Munich. The streets that are busy and visible by day become empty and dark after sunset; the buildings that are transparent in daylight become opaque in shadow; the city that is organized by the rhythms of commerce and sociability becomes a space of concealment and danger. Night is Mabuse's element — the condition in which his operations become possible, his escapes become feasible, his power becomes most effective. The investigation that operates by day must learn to operate by night, and the nocturnal city is the space in which that learning occurs.

## The Darkness

Darkness is the city's nocturnal condition, and the novel treats it as a medium with specific properties. Darkness conceals — the automobile driving without lights, the figure slipping into a doorway, the meeting conducted in an unlit room. Darkness disorients — the streets that are familiar by day become strange by night, their landmarks obscured and their distances distorted. Darkness enables — the criminal activity that would be visible by day becomes possible at night, and the investigator who would be conspicuous by day becomes invisible in darkness.

## The Nocturnal Economy

The city at night has its own economy, distinct from the daytime economy of production and legitimate commerce. The gambling dens open after dark; the nightclubs fill with patrons; the streets empty of workers and fill with those who operate outside the legitimate economy. This nocturnal economy is the infrastructure of Mabuse's operations, and the investigation's penetration of it requires von Wenk to adapt to its rhythms — to become, in effect, a creature of the night himself.

## The Night Investigator

Von Wenk's adaptation to the nocturnal city is one of the investigation's most significant transformations. The prosecutor who works by day, in offices and courtrooms, learns to work by night, in streets and gambling dens. The adaptation is practical — he must be where the crime is — but it is also symbolic: the investigator who enters the night enters the criminal's domain, and the entry changes him. The night investigator is not the same person as the day prosecutor, and the novel registers the difference without resolving it.

## Significance

The city at night functions in the novel as a spatial and temporal condition of criminality. The night is when the hidden becomes possible, when the invisible becomes powerful, when the constraints that govern daytime behavior are suspended. The novel's attention to the nocturnal city is part of its larger attention to the conditions that make Mabuse's criminality possible, and the darkness that enables his operations is as important to the novel's world as the psychological knowledge that enables his methods.
