## Overview

Mr. Pounceby is Catherine Vernon's personal solicitor, who appears during the second bank crisis — the threatened collapse that occurs when Edward Vernon goes missing. He is likely the successor or junior partner at the firm of Messrs. Pounce and Seeling, which handled the legal restructuring of Vernon's Bank after the earlier crisis under John Vernon. During the climactic day-long conclave at the bank, Pounceby remains steadfastly at Catherine's side while other advisors come and go.

## Role in the Novel

Pounceby is first mentioned during the anxious period when Edward Vernon has disappeared and the bank's future hangs in the balance. Harry Vernon, temporarily in charge, is informed that "several gentlemen have been here asking for Mr. Edward" — among them "Mr. Merridew, sir, the old gentleman: Mr. Pounceby: and Mr. Fish" [038-038]. The presence of Pounceby among these concerned visitors signals that the legal dimension of the crisis is already in motion.

His most significant appearance occurs during the day-long crisis meeting at the bank, where Catherine Vernon has taken command. As the clerks observe anxiously, a succession of important visitors — "the fathers of the town" — enter and leave the inner office where Catherine works. The narrator provides a crucial detail about Pounceby's conduct:

> Mr. Pounceby, who was Catherine's solicitor, stayed by her all day long, while the others went and came. [042-042]

This single line establishes Pounceby's loyalty and professional dedication. While other advisors — the Bank of England agent, old Mr. Merridew, and various town dignitaries — attend in rotation, Pounceby alone remains present throughout. His continuous presence suggests both the legal complexity of the crisis and his personal commitment to Catherine's interests.

## Character Analysis

Pounceby functions in the novel as an embodiment of professional loyalty. Unlike the earlier Mr. Pounce, whose respect for Catherine was qualified by a certain detached professional judgment, Pounceby appears to have a more personal connection to her affairs. The narrator explicitly identifies him as "Catherine's solicitor" — the possessive pronoun signalling a relationship that is professional but also personal and enduring.

His decision to stay "all day long, while the others went and came" [042-042] distinguishes him from the other advisors and reinforces the gravity of the crisis. The legal work required to save Vernon's Bank clearly demands continuous attention, and Pounceby provides it without interruption. This dedication mirrors Catherine's own steadfastness — her "old look of command, the energy and life of old" — and suggests a professional relationship built on mutual respect.

The likely connection between Pounceby and the earlier firm of Pounce and Seeling creates a sense of institutional continuity across the novel's timeline. The legal firm that helped Catherine take control of the bank after John Vernon's disgrace is still serving her interests decades later when Edward's crisis threatens the bank again.

## Key Scenes

- **Inquiry at the bank** [038-038]: Pounceby is among those calling at the bank to ask about Edward Vernon's whereabouts, signalling the seriousness of the situation to the clerks.
- **The day-long conclave** [042-042]: During the crisis meeting, Pounceby remains at Catherine's side throughout the entire day — the only advisor to do so — while other visitors come and go.

## Literary Significance

Mr. Pounceby exemplifies a minor but structurally important character type in the Victorian novel: the loyal professional retainer whose steadfast presence signals the gravity of a crisis and the worthiness of the protagonist. His continuous attendance on Catherine during the bank crisis functions as a form of implicit character testimony — the fact that a respected solicitor stays by her side all day confirms both the seriousness of the situation and her authority to command such loyalty.

The Pounce/Pounceby succession also reflects the novel's broader interest in continuity and change within provincial institutions. The legal firm that served Catherine in her youth still serves her in her old age, providing a thread of stability through the disruptions of banking crises and family scandals.
