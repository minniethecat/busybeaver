# Ellen Vernon

## Overview

Harry's fashionable sister — a charming, slightly frivolous young woman whose marriage to a wealthy but unimpressive man illustrates the marriage market's pragmatism. Key appearances establish their role in the novel's social world and their relationship to the central conflicts between independence and obligation, passion and prudence, that drive the plot.

## Role in the Novel

As a member of the extended Vernon circle, Ellen Vernon participates in the network of relationships that constitutes the novel's social universe. Their interactions with the central characters illuminate the various strategies by which individuals navigate the constraints of dependence, family obligation, and social expectation.

## Character Analysis

Ellen Vernon represents one of the many positions available within the Vernon social order — a particular accommodation to the conditions of dependence, ambition, or loyalty that the novel maps with sociological precision.

## Key Scenes

- Key interactions with Hester and Catherine Vernon (Chapters 007-c. 1860)
- Participation in the social rituals that define Redborough society

## Literary Significance

Ellen Vernon contributes to Oliphant's comprehensive portrait of a provincial community organized around a single institution and a single dominant personality. Their presence fills in the social canvas, demonstrating the range of responses — from loyalty to resentment, from ambition to resignation — that Catherine Vernon's reign elicits.
