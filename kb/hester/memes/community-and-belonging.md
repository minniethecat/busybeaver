# Community and Belonging

## Overview

Redborough is more than a setting in *Hester*; it is a character in its own right — a community with its own history, hierarchies, and habits of judgment. The town's identity is inseparable from the Vernon family's, and the novel traces the complex symbiosis between a community and the institution that sustains it. Within this larger social world, the Vernonry functions as a kind of hothouse community — an artificial concentration of family relations in which the normal mechanisms of belonging and exclusion operate with particular intensity.

## Manifestations in the Novel

### Redborough and Vernon's Bank

The bank is Redborough's economic heart, and its fate is the town's fate. When the bank faces crisis, the entire community is affected — depositors, merchants, employees, the web of economic relationships that constitute civic life. Catherine Vernon's rise to power is simultaneously a personal triumph and a community event; she becomes "almost the most important person in Redborough" (002-051) because she has saved what the community depends on.

### The Vernonry as Enclosed Community

Within Redborough, the Vernonry forms a distinct sub-community — the old Heronry house converted into apartments for "the poor relations who made a little centre of curious life curiously subdued" (003-012). This community-within-a-community intensifies both the bonds of belonging and the pressures of exclusion. Everyone knows everyone else's business; privacy is impossible; small slights and kindnesses are magnified by proximity.

### Hester as Outsider

Hester's tragedy is that she belongs nowhere. Too proud for the Vernonry's enforced intimacy, too poor for Redborough society, too independent for Catherine's patronage — she is "a sort of outlaw from life" (012-002), excluded from the ordinary social experiences through which belonging is forged. Her struggle is not just against individual antagonists but against the condition of not fitting anywhere.

## Character Perspectives

### Catherine Vernon — The Center That Holds

Catherine is the center around which the community revolves. Her position depends on being indispensable — to the bank, to the family, to the town — and the novel explores both the power and the burden of being the person everyone depends on.

### Hester Vernon — The Person Who Doesn't Fit

Hester's inability to belong is both her wound and, paradoxically, her freedom. Because she is already excluded, she has less to lose — and this gives her a moral clarity that more embedded characters lack. But the cost is real: loneliness, misunderstanding, the exhaustion of perpetual self-assertion.

### The Vernonry Residents — Belonging by Default

For the residents of the Vernonry, belonging is not chosen but assigned. They are Vernons, and therefore they belong — in the Heronry, in Redborough, in Catherine's web of obligation. But this default belonging is also a kind of trap, because it comes with expectations of gratitude and deference that many of them cannot meet.

## Narrative Function

The novel's treatment of community operates at two levels: the public world of Redborough, where social events (the dancing teas, Catherine's Christmas party) mark the rhythms of collective life; and the private world of the Vernonry, where the same dynamics of inclusion and exclusion play out in close quarters. The contrast between these two scales allows the novel to show how community simultaneously sustains and constrains its members.

## Victorian Context

The provincial town is one of the great settings of Victorian fiction — from George Eliot's Middlemarch to Elizabeth Gaskell's Cranford. Oliphant's Redborough belongs to this tradition while reflecting the specific conditions of late-Victorian England: a town built on commerce rather than land, a community organized around a bank rather than an estate, a social world in which money matters at least as much as birth.
