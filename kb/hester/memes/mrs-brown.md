# Mrs. Brown

## Overview

Mrs. Brown is the wife of Brown, the head clerk at Vernon's Bank, appearing at Catherine Vernon's Christmas party in her ribbons alongside "the great ladies in their jewels" [030-002]. Her role in the novel is minimal in terms of page-time but significant as a narrative device: through her, Oliphant illustrates the class fictions that Catherine Vernon's hospitality creates, and the mutual incomprehension between the commercial middle class and the county aristocracy.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Brown appears exclusively at Catherine Vernon's annual Christmas party, an assembly designed to bring together "all Redborough" under the fiction of social equality [030-002]. The narrator describes the scene with deliberate irony:

> The great ladies in their jewels sat about upon the sofas, and so did Mrs. Halifax, the cashier's wife, and Mrs. Brown, the head clerk's, in their ribbons. All was supposed to be equality and happiness. [030-002]

The juxtaposition of "jewels" and "ribbons" economically establishes the class gulf that the party's "supposed" equality cannot bridge. Catherine, however, makes a point of being conspicuously attentive to Mrs. Brown — "so civil to Mrs. Brown — so very civil" — a performance of democratic goodwill that Lady Freemantle interprets as evidence that Catherine's commercial background has permanently marked her [030-002].

Mrs. Brown's own perspective is revealed in a passage of free indirect discourse:

> On her side Mrs. Brown drew conclusions quite as fallacious. "Miss Vernon is well aware how much the young gentlemen owe to Brown," that lady said afterwards, "and how devoted he is. She knows his value to the business, and I am sure she sees that a share in the bank is what he has a right to look to." [030-006]

The narrator's pointed adjective — "fallacious" — signals that Mrs. Brown's reading of Catherine's civility is as mistaken as Lady Freemantle's, though in the opposite direction. Where Lady Freemantle sees Catherine's kindness to the Browns as a sign of lowered status, Mrs. Brown sees it as recognition of her husband's indispensable value — a promotion fantasy that the narrative gently undercuts. Both readings are projections; neither captures Catherine's actual motivations.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Brown occupies a specific position in the class structure Oliphant anatomizes. As the wife of a head clerk, she is neither working class nor gentry — she belongs to the respectable lower-middle class whose economic security depends entirely on the goodwill of employers like Catherine Vernon. Her "ribbons" rather than jewels, her hopeful misreading of Catherine's attention, and her dream of a "share in the bank" all speak to the precarious aspirations of this class stratum.

Oliphant treats Mrs. Brown without contempt but without sentimentality. The narrator's identification of her conclusions as "fallacious" is a gentle correction rather than a mockery — Mrs. Brown is not foolish for hoping, merely mistaken about the meaning of what she has experienced. The brief portrait contributes to the novel's comprehensive social mapping of Redborough, in which every class level from the county aristocracy (Lady Freemantle) to the dependent poor relations (the Miss Vernon-Ridgways) is represented and anatomized.

## Key Scenes

- **Christmas Party Assembly** [030-002]: Mrs. Brown is seated among the great ladies, distinguished by her ribbons where they wear jewels, as Catherine's party enacts its "supposed" equality.

- **Mrs. Brown's Fallacious Conclusion** [030-006]: Believing that Catherine's civility signals recognition of Brown's value to the bank, Mrs. Brown expresses her hope that her husband will be given a share in the business — a hope the narrator gently identifies as deluded.
