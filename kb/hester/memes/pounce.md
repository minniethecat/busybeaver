## Overview

Mr. Pounce is the senior partner at the Redborough legal firm of Messrs. Pounce and Seeling. He appears in the novel's extended flashback to the era of John Vernon's mismanagement of the bank, where he is portrayed as a shrewd observer of the Vernon family's affairs. Though not a sentimentalist, he respects Catherine Vernon's capabilities and harbours serious doubts about her cousin John — doubts that are vindicated by the subsequent financial crisis.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Pounce is introduced in a revealing exchange that establishes both his professional standing and his attitude toward the Vernon family. When John Vernon dismisses Catherine's authority on the grounds that she is an unmarried woman, it is Pounce who defends her, as the narrator recounts:

> "Miss Vernon is not a girl any longer," said Mr. Pounce, who was its head; upon which John Vernon laughed, one of those offensive laughs with which a coarse-minded man waves the banner of his sex over an unmarried woman. [001-001]

This exchange establishes the central tension between Pounce's professional respect for Catherine and John Vernon's dismissive chauvinism. The narrator adds a significant qualification: "Mr. Pounce was not a sentimentalist, and no doubt laughed sometimes too at the unfortunate women who had thus failed in the object of their life; but he respected Miss Vernon, and he was very doubtful of her cousin" [001-001].

Pounce's concern about John Vernon is rooted in financial observation rather than personal dislike. During the period of John's extravagant spending — the grand new house, the costly furniture, the lavish entertaining — Pounce acknowledges that "personal extravagance, as long as it is merely hospitality and show, must go a very long way indeed before it touches the great revenue of such a business. It was not the diamonds nor the feasts that they were afraid of" [001-002]. His wariness is the product of professional judgment rather than moral censure.

After the bank run and John Vernon's disappearance, Pounce and his partner Seeling undertake the legal work necessary to restructure the bank's affairs: "Messrs. Pounce and Seeling had a good deal to do about the matter, and new deeds were drawn, and old deeds cancelled to a serious extent" [002-002]. This legal restructuring secures Catherine Vernon's control of the bank, which thereafter "throve in her hands."

## Character Analysis

Mr. Pounce represents the professional, institutional perspective on the Vernon family's financial affairs. Unlike the family members themselves, who are entangled in kinship loyalties and personal histories, Pounce assesses the situation with the dispassionate eye of a solicitor. His respect for Catherine is not sentimental — the narrator is careful to note that he is "not a sentimentalist" — but practical, based on his observation of her competence.

His role in the novel is structurally significant as one of the earliest external validators of Catherine Vernon's business ability. At a time when John Vernon and the broader society dismiss her because of her sex and unmarried status, Pounce — a professional man whose opinion carries weight — recognises her capability and doubts her cousin. This early external validation helps establish Catherine's exceptionality before the narrative demonstrates it through the crisis.

The distinction between Pounce and his probable successor or partner Pounceby (who serves as Catherine's solicitor during the later crisis) suggests the continuity of the legal firm's relationship with the Vernon family across generations, reflecting the enduring institutional networks of provincial business life.

## Key Scenes

- **Defence of Catherine** [001-001]: Pounce challenges John Vernon's dismissive remark about Catherine, asserting that "Miss Vernon is not a girl any longer." John responds with an offensive laugh.
- **Assessment of John's extravagance** [001-002]: Pounce's perspective on John Vernon's spending is presented: he acknowledges that the mere hospitality and show are not concerning, but that lavishness in a businessman is perilous.
- **Restructuring after the crisis** [002-002]: After the bank run, Messrs. Pounce and Seeling handle the legal work — drawing new deeds and cancelling old ones — that transfers control of the bank to Catherine.

## Literary Significance

Mr. Pounce belongs to a familiar type in Victorian fiction: the professional observer whose judgment confirms the narrative's moral alignments. Like the family solicitor in many nineteenth-century novels, he serves as a kind of institutional chorus, his respect for Catherine and wariness of John providing external validation of what the narrative otherwise demonstrates through action.

The legal firm of Pounce and Seeling also represents the institutional infrastructure of provincial capitalism — the solicitors, deeds, and contracts that underpin the world of banking and property. Their presence grounds the novel's financial plot in the material realities of Victorian commercial life.
