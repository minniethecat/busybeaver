# The Whins

## Overview

The patch of wild ground where Hester retreats to think — a rare natural space in a novel otherwise dominated by interiors, representing the possibility of escape from social constraint.

## Role in the Novel

The Whins fills in the spatial geography of Oliphant's Redborough, contributing to the novel's detailed mapping of the relationship between physical space and social position.

## Significance

Each location in *Hester* carries specific social meaning — encoding hierarchies, marking boundaries between insiders and outsiders, and materializing the economic relations that the novel anatomizes.

## Key Associations

Significant to the novel's spatial organization of power, dependence, and community.
