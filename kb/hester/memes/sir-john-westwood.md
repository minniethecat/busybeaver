# Sir John Westwood

## Overview

Sir John Westwood is a baronet who never appears directly in *Hester* but whose title functions as a recurring reference point in the social calculations of the Vernon family. As Mrs. John Vernon's cousin, he represents the aristocratic connection that gives Hester and her mother a claim to social standing beyond their reduced financial circumstances — a claim that is simultaneously potent and, as Edward Vernon's critique reveals, hollow.

## Role in the Novel

Sir John Westwood exists in the novel entirely through the speech and thoughts of other characters. He is never seen, never speaks, and takes no action in the plot; his significance lies in what he represents to those who invoke his name.

The baronet first enters the narrative consciousness through Harry Vernon's pragmatic assessment of Hester's marriageability. In calculating the advantages of marrying Hester — who has no money — Harry weighs her connections:

> Mrs. John might be a silly little woman, but she was Sir John Westwood's cousin, and a little more backing up from the county people would do the Vernons no harm. [014-002]

For Harry, Sir John Westwood is not a person but a piece of social currency: a title that can be deployed to strengthen the Vernon family's standing with the county gentry. The calculation is characteristically cold-blooded and pragmatic — Harry sees marriage, like banking, as a transaction in which assets both financial and social must be accurately valued.

Hester invokes the baronet more passionately but no less instrumentally. When defending her mother's precedence at a social gathering, she bursts out:

> "My mother should go out of the room before either of them. Do you know who she is? Sir John Westwood is her cousin: a duke's daughter once married into her family —" [023-059]

The escalation from "Sir John Westwood" to "a duke's daughter" reveals Hester's strategy: she deploys her mother's aristocratic connections as a rhetorical shield against social slight, piling title upon title in a desperate assertion of rank. The outburst is a measure of Hester's insecurity — she leans on the baronet precisely because she has so little else to lean on.

Edward Vernon's response exposes the emptiness of this appeal:

> "It is nothing that she is a perfect little gentlewoman, and has far better manners than any of us; but because she is a cousin to a heavy baronet, who is not good enough to tie her shoe —" [023-059]

And later, the narrator reflects:

> She was above them all — not because of Lady Ethelinda (she was Lady Sarah in reality) and Sir John, but herself. [023-063]

Edward's critique, endorsed by the narrative voice, reframes the question of value: Mrs. John's worth lies not in her titled relations but in her intrinsic gentility — her manners, her bearing, her quality as "a perfect little gentlewoman." The baronet, evoked so often by Hester and her mother, is exposed as a social prop — real enough in Burke's Peerage, but meaningless as a measure of human worth.

## Literary Significance

Sir John Westwood exemplifies a technique Oliphant uses throughout *Hester*: the offstage character whose social meaning exceeds their narrative presence. Like Lady Ethelinda and other aristocratic references, the baronet embodies the gravitational pull of the landed class on the commercial Vernons — a pull that the novel's moral intelligence works to resist. The baronet's function is to be invoked and then dismissed, as the narrative insists on intrinsic worth over inherited title.

The critique of aristocratic pretension embedded in Sir John's treatment aligns *Hester* with the Victorian novel's broader interrogation of class — a theme shared with Trollope, Eliot, and Gaskell. Oliphant's particular contribution is the psychological acuity with which she shows her characters using, and being used by, their titled relations.
