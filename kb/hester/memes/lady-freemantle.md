# Lady Freemantle

## Overview

Lady Freemantle is a minor but representative figure in the social world of *Hester*, appearing at Catherine Vernon's Christmas party as one of the "great ladies in their jewels" who attend the annual assembly [030-002]. She functions as a narrative device through which Oliphant exposes the class prejudices that structure Redborough society, distinguishing the county aristocracy from the commercial town elite.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Freemantle appears exclusively at Catherine Vernon's Christmas party, an event that brings together "all Redborough" across class lines [030-002]. The party is Catherine's carefully staged demonstration of social harmony — clerks mingle with magnates, their daughters dance with the "golden youth" of the Vernon family. Yet Lady Freemantle's presence, with her diamonds and her county rank, introduces an undercurrent of judgment that the narrator uses to reveal the limits of this manufactured equality.

When Catherine makes a point of being "so civil to Mrs. Brown — so very civil", Lady Freemantle responds with a whispered aside to Mrs. Merridew:

> "She is more at home with those sort of persons than she is with the county," Lady Freemantle said. [030-003]

This single remark encapsulates the class logic of the county set: despite Catherine's wealth, power, and position, her association with the commercial world of the bank marks her as fundamentally different from — and inferior to — the landed gentry. The comment invokes the long-standing tension between "trade" and "county" that pervades Victorian social fiction.

The narrator notes that this dialogue "would have given Catherine sincere enjoyment if she had heard it" [030-005], observing that Catherine divined its content from "the conjunction of Lady Freemantle's diamonds with Mrs. Merridew's lace, as they leant towards each other, and from the expression and direction of their eyes" [030-005]. Catherine's amusement rather than offense suggests her confident self-possession — she understands these dynamics perfectly and chooses to transcend them rather than be wounded by them.

## Character Analysis

Lady Freemantle represents a type Oliphant sketches with economy and precision: the county aristocrat whose social position rests on inherited title and land, not on commercial achievement. Her diamonds — mentioned twice, once as visual detail and once as metonym for her social identity — signal a form of wealth that is display-oriented rather than productive. She embodies what the Vernon family, with their banking fortune, can never quite be: old money.

Her prejudice is neither malicious nor personal; it is structural, the unthinking assumption that "those sort of persons" — the wives of clerks and cashiers — are beneath her notice, and that Catherine's willingness to treat them as equals is a mark not of democratic generosity but of diminished status. Lady Freemantle cannot see what the narrator and the reader can: that Catherine's inclusive hospitality is the exercise of a more genuine power than anything diamonds and a title can confer.

## Key Scenes

- **Christmas Party Assembly** [030-002]: Lady Freemantle is among "the great ladies in their jewels" seated on the sofas alongside Mrs. Halifax, the cashier's wife, and Mrs. Brown, the head clerk's wife, "in their ribbons" — the visual contrast between jewels and ribbons encoding the class divide Catherine's party seeks to bridge.

- **The Whispered Aside** [030-003]: Lady Freemantle's critical remark to Mrs. Merridew about Catherine being "more at home with those sort of persons than she is with the county" crystallizes the tension between old county rank and new commercial wealth.

- **Catherine's Unspoken Triumph** [030-005]: The narrator reveals that Catherine, divining the content of the whispered exchange from the body language of diamonds and lace, would have been amused rather than hurt — a moment that quietly affirms Catherine's superiority over the very aristocrats who condescend to her.
