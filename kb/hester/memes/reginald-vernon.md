## Overview

Reginald Vernon is a deceased member of the Vernon family who appears only through the legacy of his widow and children. He was an officer in the military who died sometime before the events of the novel, leaving his wife — a clergyman's daughter — and four sons to reside in the Vernonry under the protection of Catherine Vernon. Though never present in the narrative, his absence shapes the circumstances of his family's dependent position within the Vernonry community.

## Role in the Novel

Reginald Vernon's role is entirely posthumous. His widow, Mrs. Reginald Vernon, lives on the road side of the Vernonry with their four sons, forming one of the households that the garden-front residents consider socially inferior. The family's presence in the Vernonry is made possible by Catherine Vernon's charity towards her deceased cousin's widow.

The Vernonry community regards the Reginald Vernon household with a mixture of condescension and complaint. As the narrator notes, Mrs. Reginald Vernon is "the young widow of an officer, with her four children, of whom everybody complained" [003-003]. The boys are universally seen as troublesome — Mr. Mildmay Vernon "detested" them, and old Mrs. Morgan lived in constant anxiety that "little Ted, Mrs. Reginald's third boy, would be run over" [008-008].

Captain Morgan, the retired naval officer who lives on the same side of the Vernonry, takes an active interest in the boys, making them model ships — for instance, constructing one "for Alick Vernon, the middle boy of Mrs. Reginald's three" [004-004].

## Character Analysis

Reginald Vernon himself is never characterised directly. What can be inferred from his posthumous situation is that he was a Vernon by blood — his family's claim to residence in the Vernonry rests on this connection, unlike the Morgans who are related only through Catherine's mother. His choice of wife — a poor clergyman's daughter — suggests a marriage outside the social expectations of the Vernon family, which contributes to the garden-front residents' dismissive attitude toward his widow.

His death places his family in a position of genteel dependency: respectable enough for Catherine Vernon's patronage, but not elevated enough for the respect of the Miss Vernon-Ridgways or Mr. Mildmay Vernon. This liminal social position is a recurring theme in the novel's depiction of the Vernonry community.

## Key Scenes

Though Reginald Vernon never appears, his family features in several significant scenes:

- **Vernonry description** [003-003]: The initial survey of the Vernonry community establishes the Reginald Vernon household — "Mrs. Reginald Vernon, the young widow of an officer, with her four children" — as occupying the less prestigious road side of the building.
- **Harry and Ellen's arrival** [004-004]: Mrs. Reginald's "little boy, though bound under solemn penalties never to enter the gardens, came round and hung upon the gate to gaze" at the dashing phaeton and horses.
- **Hester's friendship** [008-008]: Hester Vernon, alienated from the garden-front society, finds comfort in the Reginald household. She helps the boys with their lessons — they declare her "a dab at figures" — and becomes devoted to little Katie, the youngest child.

## Literary Significance

Reginald Vernon serves as an example of Oliphant's technique of populating her fictional world with characters who exert influence through their absence. Like the elder Roland Ashton, Reginald Vernon's death creates the conditions for his family's narrative situation — a pattern of patriarchal absence that recurs throughout *Hester*. The dependent position of his widow and children also illustrates the precarious social standing of women without male protection in Victorian society, a theme Oliphant explores across her fiction.
