## Overview

Westminster Bridge, spanning the Thames between [[westminster|Westminster]] and the Surrey shore, serves as a narrative threshold in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*. It is the place where [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] and his mother arrive in [[london|London]] on the morning the riots begin, and the route by which [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] leads his division of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] towards the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]]. The bridge functions as both a geographical landmark and a narrative device — a point from which characters observe, and are swept into, the rising tide of anti-Catholic agitation.

## Description

Dickens does not linger on the physical architecture of Westminster Bridge. Instead he uses the bridge as a stage for the human spectacle of the approaching riot. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] and his mother sit "in one of the recesses on the bridge, to rest" (047-044) and watch the vast crowd streaming past with blue cockades in their hats. The scene is rendered through the bewildered perspective of newcomers: the stream of people, the silence, the sheer scale of the movement. The bridge is less a described space than an observed threshold between the quiet countryside [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] has left and the violence into which he is about to be drawn.

## Role in the Narrative

Westminster Bridge appears at three critical moments. First, as [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] and his mother arrive at its foot on the morning of Friday, 2 June 1780:

> at seven in the forenoon of Friday the second of June, one thousand seven hundred and eighty, they alighted at the foot of Westminster Bridge, bade their conductor farewell, and stood alone, together, on the scorching pavement. (047-043)

From the bridge's recesses they witness the blue-cockaded crowds pouring towards [[westminster|Westminster]] — "the stream of life was all pouring one way," a vast throng crossing from Middlesex to Surrey (047-044). An old man explains that [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] has declared he will not present the petition "unless it is attended to the door by forty thousand good and true men at least" (048-011). It is the first moment [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] and his mother understand the scale of what is unfolding.

Second, the bridge becomes a strategic route. In Chapter 49, Dickens divides [[mob-violence|the mob]] into three columns, each assigned a different Thames bridge:

> At the head of that division which had Westminster Bridge for its approach to the scene of action, Lord George Gordon took his post; with Gashford at his right hand, and sundry ruffians, of most unpromising appearance, forming a kind of staff about him. (049-002)

The choice is significant — [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] takes the most direct western route to [[parliament|Parliament]], while the more violent contingent under Tappertit, [[dennis|Dennis]], and [[hugh|Hugh]] marches via [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] through the City. The bridge assignments map a hierarchy from political theatre ([[westminster|Westminster]]) to street-level intimidation ([[london-bridge|London Bridge]]).

Third, after the petition is rejected and [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] flee by boat, they observe "the crowd passing along Westminster Bridge" and correctly deduce that the people are dispersing — a gauge of the riot's ebb and flow read from the bridge's movement (049-032). Finally, in Chapter 82, after his arrest, [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] "was taken by way of Westminster Bridge, and back over [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] (for the purpose of avoiding the main streets), to [[tower-of-london|the Tower]]" (082-017) — the bridge that once carried him in triumph now bears him as a [[imprisonment|prison]]er.

## Historical Basis

The original Westminster Bridge, opened in 1750, was the second stone bridge across the Thames in [[london|London]] (after [[london-bridge|London Bridge]]). By 1780 it was still relatively new and provided the primary crossing between [[westminster|Westminster]] and the developing Surrey shore. Dickens's three-bridge scheme for the rioters' approach to [[parliament|Parliament]] — [[westminster|Westminster]], [[blackfriars|Blackfriars]], [[london|London]] — accurately reflects the available Thames crossings in 1780. The bridge described in the novel was replaced in 1862; the current Westminster Bridge dates from that later reconstruction.
