## Definition

Sympathy in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is more than an emotion — it is the novel's most reliable moral barometer. Dickens uses the capacity for genuine fellow-feeling to separate the virtuous from the vicious, the sincere from the hypocritical. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] embodies true sympathy: "more of life and human sympathy than the whole house besides put together" (020-002). Against him stand the novel's sympathisers-in-name-only — [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], whose compassion never extends beyond a polished phrase, and [[miggs|Miggs]], whose concern is "assault under the mask of sympathy" (022-023). Throughout the novel, the presence or absence of authentic sympathy for another's suffering is the surest index of a character's moral worth, more reliable than their professed religion, social standing, or political [[loyalty|allegiance]]. As the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] sweep through [[london|London]], sympathy becomes the force that binds the shattered community back together — "the care, humanity, and sympathy of these good people" (079-007) restores what mob violence has broken.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Sympathy as Moral Measure

From the novel's opening chapters, Dickens establishes sympathy as the quality that distinguishes his heroes. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] [[gabriel-varden|the locksmith]] is introduced not through his trade or appearance but through his emotional capacity — he possesses "more of life and human sympathy than the whole house besides put together" (020-002). His workshop becomes a refuge precisely because of this quality: all who enter find "an air of kindness and sympathy for her distress" (014-024). Varden's sympathy is active, not passive — it moves him to help, to shelter, to forgive. When [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] arrives at his door in desperate circumstances, it is Varden who responds with practical compassion while others speculate or judge.

### The Performance of Sympathy

Against genuine sympathy, Dickens sets a gallery of characters who perform concern without feeling it. [[miggs|Miggs]], the Varden family's servant, specialises in counterfeit emotion — her "sympathy" for [[dolly-varden|Dolly]] is described as "assault under the mask of sympathy, that when [[dolly-varden|Dolly]], recovering, embraced her father, and went on and on about the robbery, [[miss-miggs|Miggs]] gave fresh vent to every variety of lamentation" (022-023). [[mrs-varden|Mrs. Varden]] is scarcely more sincere: "[[mrs-varden|Mrs. Varden]] expressed her sympathy for her daughter's distress by a great many incoherent exclamations" (021-041), her display designed to draw attention to herself rather than comfort its object. In the novel's moral economy, performed sympathy is a form of theft — it takes credit for virtue without paying its cost.

### Sympathy in the Crowd

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] test sympathy at the scale of a society. As law and order collapse, Dickens tracks the fate of fellow-feeling in [[mob-violence|the mob]]. Even in the chaos, moments of shared emotion break through: the crowd is "moved by a general sympathy perhaps — God knows — with all which was turned to sorrow and most wrong, in these great social convulsions" (063-005). The novel's most devastating image of sympathy's failure comes with the rioters' destruction — where there should have been "friendly murmurs of compassion, but brutal shouts and exultation" (055-030). After the riots, the rebuilding of community begins with the restoration of sympathy: neighbours offer "the care, humanity, and sympathy of these good people" (079-007), and characters acknowledge debts of feeling — "he thanked them for their sympathy" (079-053).

### Cosmic Sympathy

Beyond the social, Dickens gestures toward a deeper, almost metaphysical sympathy between human emotion and the natural world. In a storm, characters "feel a mysterious sympathy with the tumult of nature, and are so stirred and agitated by the changes of the air, the thunder and the lightning" (002-025). This cosmic sympathy suggests that the capacity to feel with others is not merely a social grace but a fundamental human attunement — those who lack it, like [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], are not merely unpleasant but somehow less than fully human. When [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] appeals to another character, "have some sympathy with mine" (045-058), he is asking not for pity but for the recognition of shared vulnerability that makes moral community possible.
