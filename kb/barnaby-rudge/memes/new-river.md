## Overview

The New River was a seventeenth-century artificial watercourse constructed by Sir [[hugh|Hugh]] Myddleton to bring fresh water from Hertfordshire into [[london|London]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it appears in two distinct registers: as a pastoral feature of the pre-indus[[justice-and-punishment|trial]] [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]] landscape, a winding stream through haymaking fields; and as a strategic target of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon rioters]], who threatened to attack New River Head and cut off the city's water supply.

## Description in the Novel

The New River first appears in the novel's nostalgic evocation of old [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]], before the sprawl of [[london|London]] had consumed its fields and farms:

> Fields were nigh at hand, through which the New River took its winding course, and where there was merry haymaking in the summer time. (004-002)

This pastoral image — the artificial river winding through working fields, haymakers labouring in summer — forms part of Dickens's extended contrast between the [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]] of 1775 and the [[london|London]] of 1841, when the novel was written. The New River here is not yet an urban feature but a ribbon of countryside threading through the northern suburbs.

The river's second appearance is altogether darker. During the riots, the authorities deploy soldiers to guard the city's water infrastructure:

> A numerous detachment of soldiers were stationed to keep guard at the New River Head, which the people had threatened to attack, and where, it was said, they meant to cut off the main-pipes, so that there might be no water for the extinction of the flames. (067-003)

## Role in the Narrative

The New River's dual role — pastoral amenity in the opening chapters, strategic infrastructure under threat in the riot chapters — encapsulates the novel's movement from domestic comedy to public catastrophe. In Chapter 4, the river is part of the idyllic setting that surrounds [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s home and workshop, a world of honest labour and natural rhythms. By Chapter 67, the same water that once nourished hayfields is a military asset to be defended at gunpoint.

The threat to New River Head demonstrates the tactical sophistication of the riots: [[mob-violence|the mob]] understood that cutting off the water supply would render the fire engines useless, allowing the flames they had set to spread unchecked. The soldiers stationed there represent the state's desperate and ultimately successful effort to prevent total catastrophe.
