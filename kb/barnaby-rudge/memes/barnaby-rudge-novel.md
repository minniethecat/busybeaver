# Barnaby Rudge (novel)

## Overview

_Barnaby Rudge: A Tale of the Riots of Eighty_ is the fifth novel by |Charles Dickens]], published in weekly installments in _Master Humphrey's Clock_ from February to November 1841. It is Dickens's first historical novel, set in [[london|London]] and the Essex village of [[chigwell|Chigwell]] between 1775 and 1780, and centres on the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] -- the most destructive outbreak of civil disorder in English history, in which an anti-Catholic mob held [[london|London]] for nearly a week in June 1780.

The novel weaves together two narrative strands. The private plot traces a twenty-seven-year-old murder mystery: the killing of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]] at the Warren, the flight of [[barnaby-rudge-senior|the steward]] Rudge, and the shadow this [[guilt|crime]] casts over the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] and Rudge fami[[deception|lies]]. The public plot follows the fomenting and eruption of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], into which many of the novel's characters are swept -- some as rioters, some as victims, and some as defenders of order. The title character, |Barnaby Rudge]], is the intellectually disabled son of the fugitive steward, whose innocence and simplicity stand in stark counterpoint to [[mob-violence|the mob]] violence that engulfs him.

Dickens's own preface describes the historical foundation: the riots were "attended by a pretty large crowd, and spontaneously exhibiting" (P03-004) the anti-Catholic fury that [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] had inflamed. Dickens insists that "reference has been had to the best authorities of that time" (P03-008) and that the account "of all the main features of the Riots, is substantially correct" (P03-008), asserting his commitment to historical accuracy while acknowledging his invention of the private plot.

## Publication History

_[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]_ was the second of two novels to appear in _Master Humphrey's Clock_, a weekly periodical Dickens launched in April 1840. The periodical was conceived as a framing device -- stories told by the members of Master Humphrey's circle -- and _The Old Curiosity Shop_ occupied the early numbers. _[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]_ began serialisation on 13 February 1841 and concluded on 27 November 1841, in 88 weekly numbers. It was published as a single volume by Chapman and Hall in December 1841.

The book was illustrated by George Cattermole and Hablot K. Browne ("Phiz"), with Cattermole contributing the architectural and atmospheric plates and Browne handling the figure illustrations. This division of labour suited Cattermole's skill at rendering the old buildings and nocturnal landscapes that give the novel much of its Gothic atmosphere.

Dickens had conceived the story as early as 1836, originally planning it under the title _[[gabriel-varden|Gabriel]] Vardon, the Locksmith of [[london|London]]_. The long gestation -- five years from conception to publication -- is reflected in the novel's unusually careful construction for an early Dickens work. The murder-mystery plot and the riot plot, though seemingly distinct, are threaded together through shared characters and parallel themes of hidden [[innocence|guilt]], public exposure, and the force of the past upon the present.

The novel's connection to _Master Humphrey's Clock_ was severed in the 1841 volume edition: the framing narrative was dropped, and the novel was published as a standalone work -- the form in which it has been read ever since.

## Plot Summary

### Book the First: The Murder at the Warren

The novel opens on a stormy night in March 1775 at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn in [[chigwell|Chigwell]], where the regulars include [[john-willet|John Willet]] the landlord, his son [[joe-willet|Joe]], and [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]] the parish clerk. A stranger arrives, seeking directions to the Warren, the country house of [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]]. [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]] tells the story of the murder: on the same date twenty-two years earlier, [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]] -- Geoffrey's elder brother -- was found murdered in his bedchamber, and the family steward, a man named Rudge, vanished with a cashbox. A gardener was wrongly [[suspicion|suspected]] and hanged, but "[[reuben-haredale|Mr. Reuben Haredale]] was found murdered in his bedchamber" (001-103) and the true killer was never caught.

In [[london|London]], the novel introduces [[gabriel-varden|the locksmith]] [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], his wife Martha, their flirtatious daughter [[dolly-varden|Dolly]], and Varden's apprentice [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]] -- a diminutive figure with vast self-regard who harbours secret ambitions. [[edward-chester|Edward Chester]], the son of the dissolute [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], is in love with [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]], [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]]'s niece; their fathers, locked in an ancient feud, forbid the match.

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] -- described in the ear[[deception|lies]]t chapter headings as "an idiot" -- lives with his mother [[mrs-rudge|Mary Rudge]] in a cottage near the Warren. He is gentle, loves animals and bright colours, and is accompanied everywhere by his pet raven, [[grip|Grip]]. His father, it is said, died long ago. Yet a mysterious figure visits [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] on a stormy night, leaving her trembling with terror (006-081).

### Book the Second: The Gathering Storm

Five years pass. The year is 1780. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]'s machinations separate [[edward-chester|Edward]] and [[emma-haredale|Emma]]. [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]], weary of his father's tyranny, quarrels with old John and leaves [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] to join the army. The [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], led by [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] and his duplicitous secretary [[gashford|Gashford]], gathers support for a petition to [[parliament|Parliament]] demanding the repeal of the Catholic Relief Act of 1778. Anti-Catholic feeling is inflamed by [[gashford|Gashford]]'s [[deception|manipulation]]s: "half disposed to pull down a Romish chapel or two" (050-036).

[[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] moves through the novel as a figure of ironic detachment, manipulating others for his amusement and advantage. His relationship with his son [[edward-chester|Edward]] is the novel's central study of bad fatherhood: cold, mocking, and self-serving, Chester treats his son as a piece on a chessboard. Opposed to him is [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]], a sombre Catholic whose life has been shadowed by his brother's murder and whose paternal [[loyalty|devotion]] to his niece [[emma-haredale|Emma]] is genuine if dour.

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], meanwhile, is recruited by [[gashford|Gashford]] to join the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]. He is lured by promises of gold and spectacle, incapable of understanding the political or religious dimensions of the cause he is joining. His mother's desperate efforts to protect him from the world's cruelties prove futile.

### Book the Third: The Riots

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] erupt on 2 June 1780. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] leads a procession of some sixty thousand people to deliver a petition to [[parliament|Parliament]]. The crowd swells; over the next six days, [[mob-violence|the mob]] burns Catholic chapels, destroys the homes of wealthy Catholics, attacks [[newgate|Newgate Prison]], and threatens the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]]. "The solitude in crowds alone awakens" (018-003), Dickens writes of the eerie psychological state the riots induce.

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], [[hugh|Hugh]] the ostler (who is later revealed to be [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]'s illegitimate son), and the brutalised hangman [[dennis|Dennis]] (who specifically calls for [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]) become swept up in the violence. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] is arrested and sentenced to death. His father -- the fugitive steward Rudge, who has lived in hiding as a ghost-like figure haunting the Warren -- emerges to confess his [[innocence|guilt]] in the hope of saving his son. The revelation shocks the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]s and Vardens: Rudge was not the murdered steward but the murderer, who had killed [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]] and assumed the identity of the dead man.

[[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] is exposed as the instigator of much of the family's suffering. In a climactic duel at the Warren, [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] kills him. The riots are suppressed by military force. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] is granted "a free [[forgiveness|pardon]]" (079-051) at the twelfth hour. [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[dennis|Dennis]] are hanged. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] is tried for high treason but acquitted.

[[joe-willet|Joe Willet]], returning from the wars with a lost arm, is reconciled with his father and marries [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]]. [[edward-chester|Edward Chester]] and [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]] are united. [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] retires to a Catholic monastery abroad, and the old animosities dissolve.

## Major Themes

### Mob Violence and the Crowd

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] provide Dickens with his first sustained treatment of crowd psychology -- a theme that would dominate his later novel _A Tale of Two Cities_. The riot scenes are remarkable for their depiction of the crowd as an entity with its own will, absorbing individual identity: "How many recollections crowded on her mind" (025-007). Dickens anatomises the varieties of mob motivation, from sincere religious grievance to opportunistic destruction to the simple intoxication of collective action.

The novel's position is clear: [[mob-violence|the mob]] is a destructive force that must be opposed by individual moral courage. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s refusal to open [[gabriel-varden|the locksmith]]'s door to the rioters is the novel's emblematic act of resistance -- the ordinary citizen standing firm. Yet Dickens also shows the social conditions that make mob recruitment possible: poverty, ignorance, and cynical [[deception|manipulation]] by leaders like [[gashford|Gashford]].

### Innocence and Corruption

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] embodies the theme of innocence in a fallen world. His intellectual disability renders him incapable of moral choice in the conventional sense; he acts from instinct, affection, and a simple love of beauty that the rioters exploit. The tragedy of his sentence to death is that it punishes a soul incapable of understanding the [[guilt|crime]]. His innocence contrasts with the knowing [[innocence|corruption]] of figures like [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], whose sin is not passion but cold calculation.

This theme extends to the novel's women. [[mrs-rudge|Mary Rudge]]'s lifelong [[deception|concealment]] of her husband's [[innocence|guilt]] is both a [[loyalty|devotion]] and a complicity. [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]]'s virtue is threatened by the chaos of the riots. [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]]'s flirtatiousness is charming but places her in danger from the predatory [[hugh|Hugh]].

### Fathers and Sons

The novel presents a gallery of [[father-son-conflict|father-son relationships]], nearly all of them damaged. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] is a father of pure narcissism who regards his son [[edward-chester|Edward]] as an inconvenience and a rival. [[john-willet|John Willet]] infantilises [[joe-willet|Joe]], calling him a "little boy" (001-025) at twenty. The steward Rudge's disappearance leaves [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] fatherless, and his return brings not comfort but the revelation that his father is a murderer. In a dark twist, [[hugh|Hugh]] -- the most violent rioter -- turns out to be Chester's illegitimate son, their estrangement the novel's most literal rendering of paternal failure.

[[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] offers the closest thing to a functional paternal model, but his relationship with [[dolly-varden|Dolly]] is tested by her romantic complications. The novel suggests that bad fatherhood is both a private tragedy and a social danger: the rioters are, in large part, a product of failed paternal authority.

### Anti-Catholicism and Religious Bigotry

Dickens, himself no friend to Catholicism, nonetheless portrays the anti-Catholic frenzy of 1780 as a monstrous eruption of [[prejudice|bigotry]]. The novel distinguishes between ordinary prejudice and the weaponised hysteria fomented by the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]. The preface explicitly notes that Dickens "has no sympathy with the Romish Church" (P03-007) but regards the riots as a disgrace to English justice and humanity.

## Historical Method

_[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]_ is Dickens's ear[[deception|lies]]t and most deliberate engagement with history-as-material. In the Preface, Dickens stakes his claim to factual [[loyalty|fidelity]]: "reference has been had to the best authorities of that time" (P03-008) and the account of the riots' main features "is substantially correct" (P03-008). He drew on published accounts of the riots, notably Thomas Holcroft's _A Plain and Succinct Narrative of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]_ (1780), and on the [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] records of [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]].

Yet Dickens is not a documentarian. He compresses the riot's chronology -- the six days of June 1780 occupy the novel's final third -- and he freely invents the private plot of murder, hidden identity, and family feud. The historical and the fictional intertwine deliberately: historical characters ([[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]], the hangman [[dennis|Dennis]]) appear alongside invented ones; real locations ([[newgate|Newgate Prison]], the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]]) host fictional scenes. This mixture is Dickens's method: history provides the frame and the moral stakes; fiction provides the human drama that makes those stakes felt.

The novel's treatment of [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] himself is notably ambivalent. He is presented not as a villain but as a sincere, simple-minded man duped by his more cunning subordinates -- a portrait consistent with the historical record of his later conversion to Judaism and eccentric old age. [[gashford|Gashford]], by contrast, is a pure invention, a figure of Machiavellian [[deception|manipulation]] who channels [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s religious sincerity into political violence.

## Critical Reception

Contemporary reviews were mixed. Edgar Allan Poe, in _Graham's Magazine_ (1842), wrote a famously astute review that praised the novel's careful construction and identified the murder-mystery plot as its true structural spine, anticipating the detective fiction that would later emerge from the Gothic tradition. Poe noted that [[grip|the raven]] [[grip|Grip]] -- with its refrain of "I'm a devil" -- inspired his own poem "The Raven" (1845).

General Victorian readers were less enthusiastic. The novel's darker tone, its historical setting (a departure from Dickens's contemporary [[london|London]]), and the perceived longueurs of its opening movement led to lower sales than _The Old Curiosity Shop_. John Forster, Dickens's friend and biographer, defended the novel as "unduly depreciated" and praised its careful architecture.

Modern criticism has been kinder. The novel's Gothic elements -- the haunted Warren, the figure of the ghost-like murderer, the stormy night atmospherics -- have attracted interest from scholars of Gothic literature. The riot scenes are increasingly read alongside the crowd-theory writings of Gustave Le Bon and the revolutionary novels of Dickens's later career. _[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]_ is now recognised as a transitional work in Dickens's development, bridging the episodic picaresque of his early fiction and the darker, more structurally ambitious novels of his maturity.

## Legacy

The novel occupies an unusual place in the Dickens canon. It is neither among his most-read works nor among the forgotten ones; scholars value it for what it reveals about Dickens's developing craft. The character of [[grip|Grip]], [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s talking raven, holds a singular place in literary history as the direct inspiration for Edgar Allan Poe's "The Raven" (1845) -- a debt Poe acknowledged in his review. [[grip|Grip]]'s stuffed body, acquired by Poe's Philadelphia acquaintance after the bird's death, is now held by the Free Library of Philadelphia.

[[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]]'s name entered the language: the "[[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]]" became a style of brightly patterned dress in the 1870s, a craze that led to a species of trout being named after her. These cultural afterlives testify to the vividness of Dickens's minor characters even in a novel that has never achieved the popular stature of his greatest works.
