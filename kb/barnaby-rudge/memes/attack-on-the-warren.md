## Overview

The Attack on the Warren was [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s destruction by fire of the ancestral [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] estate during the fourth night of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. Unlike the destruction of Catholic chapels and the burning of [[imprisonment|Newgate]], the attack was motivated by a blend of anti-Catholic fanaticism and personal animosity — inflamed by [[hugh|Hugh]], [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]], and the hangman [[dennis|Dennis]], who redirected the riot's fury against the property of [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]].

## Historical Background

[[the-warren|The Warren]] is a fictional estate in the English countryside near [[chigwell|Chigwell]], the seat of the Catholic [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] family. Within the novel, it represents the old Catholic gentry — a class that by 1780 was already an embattled minority. The attack on the Warren mirrors the pattern of historical attacks on Catholic country houses during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], though Dickens's rendering of the event is a fictional composite rather than a record of any single real-world incident.

The attack occurs on the fourth night of the riots, by which point [[mob-violence|the mob]] has already destroyed Catholic chapels in [[moorfields|Moorfields]] and Wapping. The crowd, now divided into detachments, spreads outward from [[london|London]] to target Catholic residences in the surrounding countryside.

## Depiction in the Novel

### The Mob Assembles

After the destruction of the Catholic chapels in [[london|London]], the rioters formed a solid body and marched towards the Warren. Dickens describes the moment:

> When they left the Maypole, the rioters formed into a solid body, and advanced at a quick pace towards the Warren. (055-024)

[[hugh|Hugh]] led the party that forced entry through the turret door, while the armed servants inside fired a last volley of shots in defence of the house:

> A few armed servant-men were posted in the hall, and when the rioters forced an entrance there, they fired some half-a-dozen shots. But these taking no effect, and the concourse coming on like an army of devils, they only thought of consulting their own safety, and retreated. (055-028)

### The Burning

Once inside, [[mob-violence|the mob]] set about systematic destruction. Furniture was broken and thrown from the windows to feed bonfires below; wine barrels were stove in, and men ran through the blaze "stark mad, setting fire to all they saw — often to the dresses of their own friends — and kindling the building in so many parts that some had no time for escape" (055-029). Dickens captures the demonic energy of the fire:

> The more the fire crackled and raged, the wilder and more cruel the men grew; as though moving in that element they became fiends, and changed their earthly nature for the qualities that give delight in hell. (055-029)

The burning pile illuminated the countryside for miles. [[hugh|Hugh]], emerging from the darkness "out of breath, and blackened with the smoke," reported: "We have done all we can; the fire is burning itself out; and even the corners where it hasn't spread, are as hot as flaming coals" (055-032).

### Mr. Haredale's Return

[[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]], returning from [[london|London]] where he had been searching for [[emma-haredale|his niece Emma]], saw the fire from a great distance. He rode towards it with desperate speed, never once casting his eyes upon the ground, his gaze fixed on the glowing tower of flame that marked the destruction of his home (056-017).

Arriving at the smouldering ruin that had been his ancestral seat, [[geoffrey-haredale|Mr. Haredale]] stood before the desolation. The gardens, the halls, the family portraits — all were consumed. In the ruined garden he encountered [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], the man he had long [[suspicion|suspected]] of engineering his brother's murder, and the personal hatred that had simmered for decades ignited into a deadly pursuit (056-057).

## Aftermath and Consequences

The destruction of the Warren displaced the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] household entirely. [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]] and her servant had fled before [[mob-violence|the mob]] arrived, taking refuge at the [[maypole-inn|Maypole Inn]]. [[geoffrey-haredale|Mr. Haredale]], now homeless, became consumed by his hunt for [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], whom he finally confronted and killed in a duel.

The burning of the Warren also marked a turning point in the riots: what had begun as an anti-Catholic political demonstration had become personal [[revenge|vengeance]], and [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s energies were increasingly directed by private grudges rather than public grievances. This fragmentation of purpose mirrored the historical trajectory of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], which descended from organised protest into chaotic looting and score-settling.
