# Hugh

## Overview

Hugh is the ostler at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn in [[chigwell|Chigwell]] and one of the most formidable antagonists in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*. A dark, powerfully built figure of uncertain origin, Hugh is introduced as a lazy, intimidating presence around the inn. [[john-willet|John Willet]] describes him as "a dreadful idle vagrant fellow, sir, half a gipsy" (010-009). His rough exterior conceals a deeper significance: Hugh is eventually revealed to be the illegitimate son of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]].

During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], Hugh emerges as a charismatic and brutal leader of [[mob-violence|the mob]]. His physical strength and ruthless energy make him a natural commander of the rioters, and he participates in the destruction of Catholic property and the storming of [[imprisonment|Newgate]]. His [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] alongside [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] serves as Dickens's stark moral judgment on the violence that the riots unleashed.

## Role in the Novel

Hugh first appears at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], where his rough manners and threatening presence unsettle the inn's more peaceable inhabitants.

> "Hugh looked more like a poaching ruffian than an honest ostler" (011-004)

His early scenes with [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] establish a strange bond between the two. Hugh, for all his brutality, shows a protective instinct toward the simple-minded [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], and they become companions in the riotous days ahead.

Hugh's transformation into a riot leader is gradual but inexorable. His natural aggression, pent up in the quiet life of [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], finds release in the chaos of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. When recruited to the cause, his motivation is revealed as deeply personal:

> "I'd do anything to have some revenge on him — anything. And when you told me that he and all the Catholics would suffer from those who joined together under that handbill, I said I'd make one of 'em, if their master was the devil himself. I am one of 'em. See whether I am as good as my word and turn out to be among the foremost, or no." (040-041)

His words reveal the engine of his violence: not ideology but a lifelong accumulation of grievances, a hunger for revenge against a world that has given him nothing. His partnership with [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] illustrates how personal loyalty binds the rioters into a formidable force.

His most crucial scenes come during the siege of [[imprisonment|Newgate]], where he is among those who storm the [[imprisonment|prison]]. The revelation of his parentage — that [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], the suave and heartless gentleman, is his father — adds a bitter irony to his fate. The father who abandoned him to poverty is the embodiment of the order that Hugh has sought to destroy. His arrest and [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] close a narrative arc that runs from folkloric menace to revolutionary violence to tragic waste.

## Character and Personality

Hugh is the novel's embodiment of untamed nature — "half a gipsy" in [[john-willet|John Willet]]'s phrase, a creature of instinct and appetite rather than reflection. His physical description emphasizes his darkness and strength: tall, powerfully built, with a "poaching ruffian" look that sets him apart from ordinary villagers. He is, in many ways, a figure out of folklore — the wild man at the edge of civilized society.

Yet Dickens complicates this archetype. Hugh's loyalty to [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] hints at a capacity for affection that his brutal exterior conceals. His deference to [[gashford|Gashford]], and through him to [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]], suggests a hunger for belonging that the established social order has never offered him. When his parentage is finally revealed, Hugh becomes a figure of almost Shakespearean tragedy — the bastard son who destroys his father's world without ever knowing why.

His [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] scene is one of the novel's darkest moments. Unlike [[dennis|Dennis]], who rails and pleads, Hugh meets his death with a stoic indifference that is both chilling and oddly dignified. He has lived by instinct and violence, and he dies without [[guilt|remorse]].

## Relationships

- **[[john-willet|John Willet]]** — Hugh's employer at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], who regards him with a mixture of contempt and grudging respect for his skill with horses.
- **[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]** — Hugh's companion in the riots; Hugh shows genuine protectiveness toward [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], forming one of the novel's most complex cross-class friendships.
- **[[dennis|Dennis]]** — Fellow rioter and the public hangman; their partnership ends on the same scaffold.
- **[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]** — The titular leader of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]; Hugh follows the cause without fully understanding its politics.
- **[[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]** — The gentleman who is secretly Hugh's father, a revelation that casts Hugh's entire life in a tragic light.
- **[[gashford]]** — [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s secretary, who manipulates Hugh into the riots.
- **[[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]]** — Fellow rioter and rival; both are drawn from the apprentice/ostler class that provides the riots' [[social-class|rank]] and file.
