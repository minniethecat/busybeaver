## Overview

Warwick Street, in the Golden Square area of [[london|London]], was the site of a Roman Catholic chapel that became one of the first targets of the [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] rioters. Along with the chapel in [[duke-street|Duke Street]], [[duke-street|Lincoln's Inn Fields]], it was singled out by [[gashford|Gashford]] as a rallying-point for [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s anti-Catholic violence. The destruction of these chapels, which took place on the Friday evening of the riots, marked the transition from the peaceful [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] procession at [[westminster|Westminster]] to full-scale urban devastation.

## Description

Dickens does not offer a physical description of Warwick Street itself, treating it primarily as a locus of action — a named target rather than a rendered place. The narrative follows the rioters as they move from [[westminster|Westminster]] to the streets of central [[london|London]]; the chapels in [[duke-street|Duke Street]] and Warwick Street are their first objectives after the day's political demonstration. The technique is characteristic of Dickens's treatment of the riots: the geography of [[london|London]] becomes a network of named strongpoints, each marking a stage in the escalation of violence.

## Role in the Narrative

Warwick Street appears at a pivotal narrative juncture — the moment when the anti-Catholic agitation passes from rhetoric to physical destruction. [[gashford|Gashford]], the instigator, plants the suggestion in the ears of [[hugh|Hugh]], [[dennis|Dennis]], and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]:

> "I hear — but I cannot say whether it be true or false — that the men who are loitering in the streets tonight are half disposed to pull down a Romish chapel or two, and that they only want leaders. I even heard mention of those in Duke Street, Lincoln's Inn Fields, and in Warwick Street, Golden Square; but common report, you know —" (050-036)

This feigned detachment is pure [[deception|manipulation]]; [[gashford|Gashford]] knows exactly what his words will produce. [[hugh|Hugh]] and his companions rush out immediately, and the chapels are attacked that night.

The locksmith [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], a figure of sturdy decency, fears for his apprentice [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]]'s involvement: "If he has been at Warwick Street or [[duke-street|Duke Street]] tonight —" (051-018). Tappertit's denial — "He was not at [[duke-street|Duke Street]], or at Warwick Street, G. Varden" — is technically true only because he was at [[westminster|Westminster]] instead (051-022).

Dickens tracks [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s trajectory explicitly in Chapter 52: "The people who were boisterous at [[westminster|Westminster]] upon the Friday morning, and were eagerly bent upon the work of devastation in [[duke-street|Duke Street]] and Warwick Street at night, were, in the mass, the same" (052-002). The passage makes clear that the attacks were neither spontaneous nor organised — the crowd was "the same" mob, yet "made no appointment for reassembling, had no definite purpose or design" (052-002).

In the aftermath, [[gashford|Gashford]] reveals that a witness has come forward: "a gentleman who saw the work going on in Warwick Street; a Catholic gentleman; one [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]" (053-037). This mention of [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] ties the destruction of Warwick Street directly to the novel's central conflict — [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] is both a Catholic and the sworn enemy of the senior [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]], making the chapel attack personal as well as political.

## Historical Basis

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] of June 1780 saw numerous attacks on Catholic chapels and the homes of known Catholics in [[london|London]]. The chapels attached to the Sardinian and Bavarian embassies — in [[duke-street|Duke Street]] ([[duke-street|Lincoln's Inn Fields]]) and Warwick Street (Golden Square) respectively — were among the principal targets. Both were ransacked, their furnishings burned in the streets. The Warwick Street chapel was later rebuilt and survives today as the Church of Our Lady of the Assumption and St Gregory.
