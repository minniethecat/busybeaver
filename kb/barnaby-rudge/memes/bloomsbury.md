## Overview

Bloomsbury — specifically Bloomsbury Square, then the residence of the nation's most senior judge — occupies a distinctive place in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*: it is where the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] rise to their highest pitch of symbolic violence and where, in the novel's aftermath, the law reasserts itself through the scaffold. Lord Mansfield's house in Bloomsbury Square, "the nobleman who was at that time Chief Justice of the King's Bench" (066-025), was singled out for destruction not for its Catholic associations but because it represented the legal order the rioters sought to overthrow.

Bloomsbury Square thus incarnates the novel's central tension between law and mob. The attack on Mansfield's house is an attack on the idea of impartial justice; the [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s that follow in the same square are the law's answer — symmetrical, public, and terrible. No other location in the novel so neatly compresses [[guilt|crime]] and punishment into a single address.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens renders the attack on Mansfield's house with the peculiar restraint of a narrator who knows what is coming but chooses to report it through the [[innocence|experience]] of those inside:

> At length they were heard approaching the house, and some minutes of terrible anxiety ensued. They came close up, and stopped before it; but after giving three loud yells, went on. And although they returned several times that night, creating new alarms each time, they did nothing there; having their hands full. Shortly after they had gone away for the first time, one of the scouts came running in with the news that they had stopped before Lord Mansfield's house in Bloomsbury Square. (066-025)

The three loud yells, the repeated returns, the false reprieves — the passage is built on the tension between what is threatened and what actually happens. The house is menaced repeatedly, but the destruction is reported secondhand, by a scout, not witnessed directly. This indirection is central to Dickens's method: the attack on the law is too serious to stage in full view.

When the law reasserts itself, however, Dickens makes us look directly:

> Two cripples — both mere boys — one with a leg of wood, one who dragged his twisted limbs along by the help of a crutch, were hanged in this same Bloomsbury Square. As the cart was about to glide from under them, it was observed that they stood with their faces from, not to, the house they had assisted to despoil; and their misery was protracted that this omission might be remedied. (077-060)

The detail of the boys facing the wrong way — and the authorities pausing the [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] to turn them toward the house they helped destroy — is Dickens at his most lacerating. The scaffold must face the [[guilt|crime]]; the symmetry must be visible. Bloomsbury Square becomes a theatre of [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]] in which even the orientation of the condemned matters.

## Role in the Narrative

Bloomsbury Square marks the rioters' escalation from sectarian targets to institutional ones. The attack on Catholic chapels was religious; the attack on Lord Mansfield's house was constitutional. Mansfield, as Lord Chief Justice, was the living embodiment of the common law — and his house, with its "pictures and furniture" and its "rare and beautiful collection of books" (066-025), was its material extension. To burn Mansfield's library was to burn the law's memory.

The novel then completes the arc in the [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] scenes of Chapter 77. Bloomsbury Square is chosen as the site of [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] precisely because it was a site of destruction: the hanged boys face the house they despoiled. Dickens structures this as a grim proportionality — as Mansfield's house was violated, so the violators' bodies are displayed before it:

> One of us three is to go to Bloomsbury Square. Let me be the one. (077-039)

This plea, spoken by one of the condemned, makes explicit what the novel's architecture imp[[deception|lies]]: Bloomsbury Square is a destination, a place where accounts are settled. To be sent to Bloomsbury Square is to be sent to one's death. The square has become, in the novel's symbolic geography, the place where the riot's moral bill comes due.

The final reference to Bloomsbury Square closes the circle with unexpected [[forgiveness|mercy]]. [[geoffrey-haredale|Mr Haredale]], having secured [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]'s [[forgiveness|pardon]], goes "straight from Bloomsbury Square to the [[golden-key|Golden Key]]" (079-051), leaving [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] to bring the freed boy home. The square that was a site of destruction, then of [[justice-and-punishment|execution]], becomes — for one [[imprisonment|prison]]er — the site of reprieve.

## Historical Basis

The historical Lord Mansfield — William Murray, 1st Earl of Mansfield (1705–1793) — was indeed Lord Chief Justice during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], and his house at Bloomsbury Square was among the most prominent targets of [[mob-violence|the mob]] on the night of 6–7 June 1780. Contemporary accounts record that the rioters broke into the house, smashed the furniture, and burned Mansfield's celebrated library — one of the finest private collections in England — along with his legal manuscripts and personal papers. The loss to legal scholarship was irreparable.

Bloomsbury Square itself was one of [[london|London]]'s ear[[deception|lies]]t and most fashionable squares, developed in the 1660s by the Earl of Southampton. By the late eighteenth century it was the residence of peers, judges, and wealthy merchants — precisely the symbols of established authority that the rioters' violence, once it escalated beyond sectarian targets, sought to attack. The square's association with the legal establishment made it a natural site for the retributive [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s Dickens describes.

The [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s following the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] were among the largest mass [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s in eighteenth-century [[london|London]] history. Contemporary estimates suggest that between twenty-five and thirty-five rioters were executed, many at or near the sites of their [[guilt|crime]]s — a deliberate policy of exemplary justice. The scaffold in Bloomsbury Square, facing the ruins of Mansfield's house, was the most visible of these symbolic punishments.
