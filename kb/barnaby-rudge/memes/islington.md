## Overview

Islington is a village located north of [[london|London]], presented in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as a point of transition between the city and the surrounding countryside. In the novel's geography, it functions as a threshold — a place one passes through when leaving the urban world of [[london|London]] for the rural roads and fields beyond. It appears twice in the narrative, both times in connection with characters departing the city.

## Description

Dickens does not provide an elaborate physical description of Islington itself; rather, it is referenced as a directional waypoint. The road through Islington leads northward toward [[highgate|Highgate]] and the countryside, and it is described as the "nearest point of egress" from [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]] for those seeking to escape the city (068-004). Its location on the northern fringe of [[london|London]] makes it a natural gateway between urban and rural space.

## Role in the Narrative

Islington serves two narrative functions in the novel:

First, in Chapter 31, after [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]] has left [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn following his quarrel with his father, he wanders northward in a state of despondency. Dickens records that "he went out by Islington and so on to [[highgate|Highgate]], and sat on many stones and gates, but there were no voices in the bells to bid him turn" (031-045). The reference underscores [[joe-willet|Joe]]'s isolation — even the church bells, which in folklore might ring out to guide a wandering soul homeward, remain silent. The passage through Islington marks [[joe-willet|Joe]]'s departure from everything familiar.

Second, in Chapter 68, after the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] have been suppressed, [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] and his father — the fugitive murderer — escape from [[smithfield|Smithfield Market]], where [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] has freed his father from his irons. The two "made towards [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]], and passing thence to Islington, as the nearest point of egress, were quickly in the fields" (068-004). Islington here represents the threshold of freedom: once past it, the fugitives are out of the city and into the open countryside, where they eventually find shelter in a deserted cowherd's shed near [[finchley|Finchley]].

In both instances, Islington is not a destination but a passage point — the last edge of [[london|London]] before the rural world begins.

## Characters Associated

- [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]] — passes through Islington on his journey northward after leaving [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] (Chapter 31)
- [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] — escapes through Islington with his father after the riots (Chapter 68)
- [[barnaby-rudge-senior|Mr. Rudge]] — the fugitive murderer, escapes through Islington with [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] (Chapter 68)
