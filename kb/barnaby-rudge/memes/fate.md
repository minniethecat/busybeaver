## Definition

Fate in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* takes the form of a moral providence — a chain of cause and effect that ultimately delivers [[revenge|retribution]] to the [[innocence|guilt]]y and restoration to the innocent. Dickens structures the novel's two interlocking plots (the murder mystery of the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] family and the historical arc of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]) so that private sins committed decades earlier ripple forward to determine the events of 1780. The novel's providential logic is reinforced by the riot's own arc: those who unleash [[mob-violence|collective violence]] are, in turn, consumed by it.

## Historical Context

Dickens wrote *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* between *Oliver Twist* and *David Copperfield*, in a period when his treatment of providence was shifting from the explicit moral machinery of his early work toward the more secular, psychological causality of his mature novels. The novel's historical setting allowed Dickens to use events whose outcomes were already known, reinforcing the sense that history obeys a providential pattern. The preface's emphasis on the riots as teaching a "good lesson" (P03-006) signals the novel's didactic framework.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Haredale Murder

The novel's mystery plot depends on a hidden [[guilt|crime]] — the murder of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]] — whose perpetrator ([[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] Senior) and witness ([[barnaby-rudge-senior|the steward]]) remain concealed for decades. The novel opens with a meditation on misfortunes that "never come singly," noting that "troubles are exceedingly gregarious in their nature, and flying in flocks, are apt to perch capriciously" (032-001). The eventual revelation, occurring in the midst of the riots, suggests that hidden truths possess a momentum of their own: fate, in the form of narrative necessity, compels exposure.

### Retributive Justice

The novel's most explicit providential pattern is the fate of the rioters. [[hugh|Hugh]], [[dennis|Dennis]], and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] are all captured, tried, and condemned. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] tells his son [[edward-chester|Edward]] that "all men are fortune-hunters" — a cynical philosophy that fate will ultimately repudiate (015-052). [[hugh|Hugh]]'s [[justice-and-punishment|execution]], [[dennis|Dennis]]'s grotesque death at the hands of [[mob-violence|the mob]], and the dispersal of the riot's leaders all follow a logic of poetic justice. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], the novel's most calculating villain, is murdered by his own son's half-brother — a [[revenge|retribution]] that spans two generations and unknowable family connections.

### Fortune's Wheel

Characters repeatedly invoke fortune and fate in their speech. [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]] sits "ruminating on his dismal fate and the manifold perfections of [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]]" (030-005), while the recruiting sergeant encourages him as "the boy to push your fortune" (031-040). [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] manipulates [[emma-haredale|Emma]] by claiming that "the Fates forbid" his desire to call her daughter (029-057). [[maypole-inn|The Maypole Inn]]'s convivial atmosphere is shadowed by the sense that fortune is capricious — that the same wheel that elevates [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]] to military honor can cast him into disgrace before elevating him again in marriage and inheritance.

## Key Characters

- **[[Geoffrey Haredale]]** — His family's murder is the hidden fate that shapes his entire life and the novel's gothic architecture of revelation.
- **[[Sir John Chester]]** — The master manipulator who believes himself above fate, only to discover that his past sins have produced an avenger he never knew existed.
- **[[Mrs. Rudge]]** — Her lifelong [[deception|concealment]] of her husband's [[guilt|crime]] places her at fate's intersection: the keeper of a secret that destiny will not allow to stay buried.
- **[[Dennis]]** — The hangman whose profession is to be fate's instrument for others, yet who discovers that fate has reserved a more terrible death for him.
- **[[Hugh]]** — The wild ostler whose parentage, revealed only at his [[justice-and-punishment|execution]], makes him fate's living paradox: the son of a gentleman, hanged as a rioter.

## Key Scenes

1. **The Revelation**: The disclosure of [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] Senior's identity and [[guilt|crime]], decades in the making, sets the novel's providential machinery in motion.
2. **[[dennis|Dennis]]'s Death**: The hangman's ironic end — he who executed others dies at the hands of those he would have executed.
3. **[[hugh|Hugh]]'s Execution**: The revelation of [[hugh|Hugh]]'s parentage on the scaffold unites the novel's two plots in a single moment of fateful convergence.
4. **Chester's Death**: [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] is killed by his own unacknowledged son, a [[revenge|retribution]] that spans decades and transcends any individual's intention.
