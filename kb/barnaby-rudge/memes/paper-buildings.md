## Overview

Paper Buildings is a row of chambers in the [[temple|Inner Temple]], one of the four Inns of Court in [[london|London]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it is the [[london|London]] residence of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] — the setting for his idle, elegant breakfasts and the place where his son [[edward-chester|Edward]] comes to confront him. The location embodies Chester's world of gentlemanly leisure, far removed from the honest labour of [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s locksmith shop in [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]].

## Description in the Novel

Paper Buildings is introduced as Chester's comfortable private chambers, part of a row of handsome townhouses within the Temple precinct:

> It was in a room in Paper Buildings — a row of goodly tenements, shaded in front by ancient trees, and looking, at the back, upon the Temple Gardens — that this, our idler, lounged; now taking up again the paper he had laid down a hundred times; now trifling with the fragments of his meal; now pulling forth his golden toothpick, and glancing leisurely about the room, or out at window into the trim garden walks, where a few early loiterers were already pacing to and fro. (015-005)

The setting is carefully contrasted with the world of work. While [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s shop in [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]] bustles with activity and honest trade, Chester's chambers in Paper Buildings are devoted entirely to idleness, comfort, and aesthetic pleasure. The "ancient trees" and "[[temple|Temple Gardens]]" reinforce the sense of a cloistered, privileged enclave shut off from the city's commerce and labour.

## Role in the Narrative

Paper Buildings appears in two key scenes:

1. **Chapter 15**: Chester lounges over a late breakfast in his chambers, reading the paper and using his golden toothpick, while his son [[edward-chester|Edward]] waits patiently in the garden below. The scene establishes Chester's character — his indolence, his fastidiousness, and his control over the physical spaces he occupies.

2. **Chapter 40**: [[hugh|Hugh]], sent by [[sir-john-chester|Chester]], arrives at the [[temple|Middle Temple]] gate late at night seeking "Paper Buildings" and "[[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]'s chambers" (040-006–040-008). The gate-porter's surliness and [[hugh|Hugh]]'s rough manner contrast sharply with the refined world Chester inhabits.

Paper Buildings functions as a spatial marker of Chester's social identity. [[temple|The Temple]] was (and remains) a legal precinct, and Chester's residence there aligns him with the professional and gentlemanly classes — a world of law, privilege, and polished surfaces that is the antithesis of the rough, violent, and honest worlds represented by the [[maypole-inn|Maypole Inn]] and the [[the-warren|Warren]].
