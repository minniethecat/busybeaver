## Historical Biography

Thomas Langdale was a prominent Catholic vintner and distiller in Holborn, London, whose premises became one of the most spectacular targets of the Gordon Riots. Langdale's distillery on Holborn contained large quantities of gin and other spirits, and when the mob set it alight on the night of 7 June 1780, the resulting fire was visible for miles. The burning liquor ran in flaming rivers down the street, creating one of the most memorable images of the riots.

Langdale is recorded in contemporary accounts as having escaped the attack and later giving evidence about the destruction of his property. He was one of many Catholic businessmen targeted during the riots — his prominence and the visibility of his trade made him a natural target for the anti-Catholic mob.

## In the Novel

In *Barnaby Rudge*, Langdale appears briefly but memorably. He is introduced during the riots when Gabriel Varden warns him of the danger:

> "it might get you into trouble. Langdale — that's my name — vintner and distiller" (061-033)

The attack on Langdale's premises represents the riot's transformation from targeted anti-Catholic violence into indiscriminate destruction. The mob that descends on his distillery is not driven by religious zeal but by the intoxicating combination of alcohol and arson. Dickens presents the burning of Langdale's as the point where the riots become pure chaos.

## The Fire

The destruction of Langdale's distillery is one of the riot's defining events. Contemporary accounts describe the burning spirits flowing down Holborn in streams of blue flame. The fire was so intense that it illuminated the night sky and could be seen from miles away. The distillery's destruction also had lethal consequences — rioters who drank the raw spirits from the gutters died of alcohol poisoning, and others were trapped in the collapsing buildings.

In the novel, the burning of Langdale's distillery is folded into the wider destruction of the night of 7 June, when Newgate was attacked, the Fleet and King's Bench prisons were opened, and multiple Catholic-owned properties were set ablaze.

## Significance

Langdale represents the Catholic lay community that suffered most directly from the riots. He is not a political figure or a clergyman but a tradesman whose faith made him a target. His inclusion in the novel — brief though it is — grounds the riots in economic reality: the destruction of property was not collateral damage but the point of the exercise.
