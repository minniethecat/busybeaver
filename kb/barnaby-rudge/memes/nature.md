## Definition

The natural world in *Barnaby Rudge* functions as a moral and psychological alternative to the human violence of London. The novel opens not in the city but in the countryside — at the Maypole Inn on the edge of Epping Forest — and its most peaceful moments occur in rural settings. The contrast between the organic order of nature and the manufactured chaos of the riots is one of the novel's deepest structural principles.

## The Maypole and Epping Forest

The novel's opening chapters establish the Maypole Inn and its surrounding landscape as a world apart from London. The inn stands "upon the borders of Epping Forest, at a distance of about twelve miles from London" (001-001), and Dickens lavishes descriptive attention on its setting: the ancient trees, the changing seasons, the rhythms of rural life.

This rural world is not idealized — it has its own cruelties and conflicts — but it possesses a stability that the city lacks. Trees grow, seasons turn, and the inn endures, indifferent to the political passions that consume London. The Maypole is the novel's ground note: a place where the fundamental realities of life — work, food, companionship, sleep — assert their priority over ideology.

## Barnaby and the Natural World

Barnaby Rudge, the novel's titular character, has a special relationship with the natural world. His "idiot" mind, incapable of the abstractions that drive other characters to violence, is attuned to the sensory richness of the countryside. The natural world is Barnaby's refuge and his language. When the novel's human world descends into chaos, Barnaby turns instinctively toward the non-human — toward birds, trees, and sky. His innocence is not merely a moral quality but an ecological one: he belongs to the natural order in a way that the other characters do not.

## Nature as Moral Measure

Dickens uses the natural world as a yardstick against which human behavior is measured. The riots are not merely politically destructive; they are aesthetically and morally offensive because they violate the order of nature. When [[mob-violence|the mob]] burns and loots, it is not only breaking human laws but desecrating a world that existed before those laws and will endure after them.

This is not a romanticism that denies the existence of suffering — the novel is too honest for that — but a conviction that the natural world provides a perspective from which human conflicts appear in their true proportion. The riots that convulse London are, from the perspective of Epping Forest, a temporary disturbance in a much older and more durable order.
