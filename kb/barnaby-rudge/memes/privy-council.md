## Overview

The Privy Council was the principal advisory body to the British monarch in the eighteenth century, composed of senior ministers, bishops, judges, and other dignitaries appointed by the Crown. While its routine functions were largely ceremonial by 1780, the Privy Council retained the authority to issue royal proclamations and, in moments of crisis, to direct the use of military force. During the Gordon Riots, an emergency meeting of the Privy Council made the fateful decision to deploy regular troops against the civilian mob.

## Historical Background

The Privy Council had been the central instrument of royal government since the Tudor period, though its executive functions had largely migrated to the Cabinet by the eighteenth century. Its membership included the great officers of state, the Archbishop of Canterbury, and senior judges. The Council met at Whitehall and acted under the authority of the King, though in practice its decisions were shaped by whichever ministers commanded the King's confidence. The proclamation issued on the evening of 7 June 1780, authorizing military intervention during the Gordon Riots, was one of the most consequential acts of the Council in the late eighteenth century.

## Role in the Novel

The Privy Council enters *Barnaby Rudge* at the moment of maximum crisis. After days of unchecked rioting, with Newgate burned and London in chaos, the civil authorities have proven helpless. Dickens narrates the decisive escalation:

> at nine o'clock in the evening, the Privy Council issued a solemn proclamation (067-009)

This proclamation authorizes the military to fire on rioters who fail to disperse — the turning point from hesitant containment to active suppression. The Council's deliberations are not dramatized directly, but their consequences ripple through the remaining chapters. In the aftermath, officials are "summoned that night before the Privy Council to answer for his conduct" (073-061), and arrested rioters are "conducted first before the Privy Council, and afterwards to the Horse Guards" (073-064). The Council thus serves both as the source of lawful authority for the military response and as the tribunal before which the riots' failures of governance are examined.

## Key Members

Though no Privy Council members appear as named characters in the novel, the Council's decisions are understood to represent the authority of the Crown and the government. In the historical context, its membership would have included the Prime Minister (Lord North) and other ministers of state.

## Significance

The Privy Council episode encapsulates the central political dilemma of *Barnaby Rudge*: when law fails, must the state resort to force? The Council's proclamation represents the crossing of a threshold — from persuasion to coercion, from civil authority to military power. Dickens presents this escalation not as a triumph but as a grim necessity, the price paid when magistrates like Brackley Kennett abdicate their responsibilities and the mob is permitted to run unchecked. The Council functions in the novel as a deus ex machina of order, but one whose very necessity is an indictment of the system that preceded it.
