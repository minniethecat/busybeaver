## Overview

The Associated Rememberers of Bloody Mary are among the more darkly comic of the "No Popery" societies that Dickens catalogues in *Barnaby Rudge*. Their name — with its grotesque combination of bureaucratic formality ("Associated") and sectarian blood-memory ("Rememberers of Bloody Mary") — encapsulates the novel's satirical treatment of organized bigotry. Like the other subscription societies that spring up around Lord George Gordon's movement, the Rememberers are at once absurd and menacing: a club devoted to commemorating the sixteenth-century persecution of Protestants as a pretext for eighteenth-century violence against Catholics.

## Role in the Novel

The Associated Rememberers appear in a single, devastating sentence — the list of anti-Catholic subscription societies that Lord George Gordon reviews as he tallies the movement's support:

> The Associated Rememberers of Bloody Mary, half-a-guinea. (036-020)

The detail of the half-guinea subscription fee is characteristically Dickensian. The Rememberers, like the United Bulldogs and the other Protestant clubs, have commodified their hatred: for the price of a coin, any Londoner can purchase membership in historical grievance. The reference to Bloody Mary — Queen Mary I, under whose reign nearly three hundred Protestants were burned — ties the 1780 agitation to a religious conflict two centuries old, suggesting both the depth and the absurdity of the historical resentments that fuel the riots.

## Key Members

The Rememberers are not associated with any named characters in the novel. Like most of the subscription societies Dickens lists, they exist as a collective entity — a name, a fee, and an implied membership of anonymous Londoners whose anti-Catholic fervor has been monetized.

## Significance

The Associated Rememberers of Bloody Mary illustrate one of *Barnaby Rudge*'s most important themes: the way organized hatred appropriates historical trauma for present purposes. The society's name performs an act of historical ventriloquism — it speaks in the voice of sixteenth-century martyrs to justify eighteenth-century arson. Dickens's catalogue of such societies, each with its own subscription rate, suggests a marketplace of grievance in which righteous anger is priced by the half-guinea. The Rememberers are at once ridiculous and chilling, a reminder that the infrastructure of mob violence includes not just torches and crowbars but membership rolls and subscription books.
