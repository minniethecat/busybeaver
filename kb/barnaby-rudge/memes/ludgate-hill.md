## Overview

Ludgate Hill is a street in the [[london|City of London]], running from Ludgate Circus up to [[st-pauls-cathedral|St. Paul's Cathedral]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it appears as one of the main arteries through which the [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] Rioters surged, and as a scene of opportunistic looting during the chaos.

## Description

Dickens presents Ludgate Hill as a commercial street caught in the riot's path. A shop counter becomes an impromptu stage for a looter:

> …off the counter of a shop in Ludgate Hill, and putting it down again when… (037-057)

During the riots, Ludgate Hill channels one of the two great streams of [[mob-violence|the mob]]:

> …in two great streams, one by Ludgate Hill, and one by Newgate Street… (067-014)

## Role in the Narrative

Ludgate Hill functions as a conduit for the rioters' movement through the City. Its mention alongside [[newgate-street|Newgate Street]] (067-014) places it in the grid of streets that channelled [[mob-violence|the mob]] toward St. Paul's and [[newgate|Newgate Prison]]. The shop-counter incident (037-057) is characteristic of Dickens's technique: a single, almost comic vignette of opportunistic theft set against the backdrop of mass violence, illustrating how civil order unravels at the retail level.

## Historical Basis

Ludgate Hill in the 18th century was one of the principal approaches to [[st-pauls-cathedral|St. Paul's Cathedral]] and a busy commercial street. It took its name from Ludgate, one of the ancient gates of the [[london|City of London]] wall. During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], Ludgate Hill was indeed among the streets through which rioters moved between the City and the cathedral precinct. The area around Ludgate Hill and [[st-pauls-cathedral|St. Paul's Churchyard]] was a major thoroughfare for both rioters and military forces.
