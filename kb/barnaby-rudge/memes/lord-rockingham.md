## Historical Biography

Charles Watson-Wentworth, 2nd Marquess of Rockingham (1730–1782), was a British Whig statesman who served twice as Prime Minister of Great Britain — first from 1765 to 1766, and again in 1782, the year of his death. A wealthy Yorkshire landowner, Rockingham led the "Rockingham Whigs," a parliamentary faction that opposed the policies of George III's favored ministers and supported conciliation with the American colonies. His first ministry repealed the Stamp Act in 1766. During the Gordon Riots of June 1780, Rockingham was out of office but remained a prominent public figure whose London residence was among the properties threatened by the mob.

## Role in *Barnaby Rudge*

Lord Rockingham appears in *Barnaby Rudge* as a single, telling detail in Dickens's panoramic catalogue of the rioting. When the authorities begin fortifying London against the mob, Rockingham's Grosvenor Square mansion is listed among the secured properties:

> private houses (among them, Lord Rockingham's in Grosvenor Square) (067-003)

This brief inclusion serves a narrative purpose: Grosvenor Square was the most fashionable address in London, the heart of the aristocratic West End. That the rioters had forced the fortification of houses in such a district — that even a former prime minister's residence required military protection — conveys the scale and audacity of the disorder more effectively than any abstract description. Rockingham's house, alongside other fortified properties, marks the boundary the riot had crossed: from targeting specifically Catholic or legally connected premises to threatening the governing class itself.

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens's treatment of Rockingham is entirely factual and minimal. The historical Rockingham's Grosvenor Square house was indeed among those fortified during the riots. The novel does not explore Rockingham's political career or his significance to the era. His inclusion serves purely as documentary texture — one more name in the catalogue of the threatened establishment, lending historical authenticity to the fictional narrative.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

Rockingham's appearance — brief as it is — contributes to Dickens's anatomy of social order under stress. The fortification of aristocratic houses signals the collapse of the normal protections of property and rank. When a peer of the realm, a former prime minister, must barricade his own front door, the distinction between the mob's purported targets (Catholics) and its actual effects (generalized terror) collapses. Rockingham's name, dropped among a litany of fortified houses, becomes part of the novel's argument that mob violence, once unleashed, does not discriminate.
