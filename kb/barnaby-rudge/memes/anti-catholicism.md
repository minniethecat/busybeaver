## Definition

Anti-Catholicism in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is the dominant political and social force driving the novel's central historical event—the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots of 1780]]. Dickens presents it not as a genuine religious conviction but as a "false … religious cry" raised by ambitious men for their own purposes (P03-006).

The movement, organized under the "[[protestant-association|Great Protestant Association]]," exploited the residual fear of Catholicism embedded in English political culture since the Reformation, transforming private prejudice into public violence.

## Historical Context

In late-18th-century England, anti-Catholic sentiment was codified in law. The penal laws imposed penalties including "perpetual im[[imprisonment|prison]]ment" for Roman Catholic priests who administered sacraments (037-002). The Catholic Relief Act of 1778, which removed some of these restrictions, provoked a nativist backlash.

[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] emerged as the president of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], which gathered signatures for a petition demanding repeal of the Catholic relief measures.

Dickens, writing in 1841, uses the riots to deliver what his preface calls a "good lesson" — that religious slogans are "easily raised by men who have no religion, and who in their daily lives set at naught the commonest principles of morality" (P03-006). The Preface establishes that the novel's depiction of the riots is "substantially correct" as history, even as the fictional [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] and Chester plots are woven through it.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Protestant Association as Political Machinery

The [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] serves as the organizational vehicle for anti-Catholic agitation. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s secretary, [[gashford|Gashford]], enrolls members with perfunctory questions and a show of patriotic purpose, and [[hugh|Hugh]] the ostler performs "an extemporaneous No-Popery Dance" as his initiation rite (038-026, 038-038).

Dickens satirizes the thinness of the movement's ideological conviction — members join without understanding the bill they are protesting against, as [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] and [[hugh|Hugh]] both demonstrate (038-013).

### The Cry as Rallying Mechanism

"No Popery" functions throughout the novel as a shibboleth — a shared cry that unites the disparate classes and motivations of [[mob-violence|the mob]]. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] himself uses it at public appearances, withdrawing from his coach to "gravely" pull off his hat and pronounce "Gentlemen, No Popery. Good day. God bless you" before disappearing (037-032, 037-029).

The cry appears scrawled on doors as a protection racket: "chalk 'No Popery' on your door tomorrow night, and light up your windows" — a mark that the occupant has bought safety from the very mob now policing religious orthodoxy (051-036).

### The Mob as Instrument

Dickens explicitly connects the anti-Catholic cause to the dangerous energy of the [[london|London]] crowd. The mob is described as "a creature of very mysterious existence … as difficult to follow to its various sources as the sea itself" (052-001).

By the time the riots begin in earnest, [[mob-violence|the mob]] has been divided into four divisions — [[london|London]], [[parliament|Westminster]], [[southwark|Southwark]], and Scotch — revealing a quasi-military organization beneath the surface of spontaneous outrage (049-001).

### The Human Cost

The anti-Catholic fury transforms private lives. The [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] family, being Catholic, faces suspicion and violence. [[gashford|Gashford]] probes [[geoffrey-haredale|Mr. Haredale]] with "an inquisitive look" about [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] being "a Catholic gentleman unfortunately" (043-038).

Catholic houses in [[warwick-street|Warwick Street]] and other neighborhoods are specifically targeted, illustrating how sectarian politics in the novel becomes indistinguishable from organized looting and destruction (053-037, 054-013).

## Key Characters

- **[[Lord George Gordon]]** — The historical president of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], portrayed by Dickens as sincere but vain and easily manipulated by his secretary [[gashford|Gashford]].
- **[[Gashford]]** — [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s Machiavellian secretary, the true architect of much of the agitation. He understands that the anti-Catholic cause is a means of acquiring power rather than a religious conviction.
- **[[Geoffrey Haredale]]** — A Catholic gentleman whose family becomes a target of the riots. His faith places him at mortal risk when [[mob-violence|the mob]] turns toward the Warren.
- **[[Sir John Chester]]** — A cynical observer who sees the anti-Catholic agitation as an opportunity for personal advancement while keeping his own hands clean.
- **[[Dennis]]** — The public hangman who joins the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] with professions of piety, embodying Dickens's point about the hypocrisy of those who invoke religion to justify violence.
- **[[Hugh]]** — The ostler who becomes a mob leader, joining the Association and performing the "No-Popery Dance" as a ritual of belonging rather than conviction.

## Key Scenes

1. **[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s Procession into [[london|London]]** (037-029–037-032): [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] is cheered by crowds crying "No Popery!" He responds with theatrical gravity, establishing the carnivalesque atmosphere around the movement.

2. **[[hugh|Hugh]] and [[dennis|Dennis]] Join the Association** (038-013–038-038): The initiation of two of the novel's central rioters into the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], complete with [[dennis|Dennis]]'s "No Popery, brother!" and [[hugh|Hugh]]'s dance — Dickens at his most satirical about the movement's intellectual vacuity.

3. **The Mob Forms** (049-001): The four-division structure of [[mob-violence|the mob]] is described in detail, revealing the organizational logic behind the apparent chaos.

4. **[[attack-on-the-warren|Attack on the Warren]]** (043-054): The cry "Down with the Papists!" unites a diverse crowd that had been "inactive" — the anti-Catholic slogan is the catalyst that transforms passive discontent into destructive action.

5. **Chalk on the Door** (051-036): The chilling detail of householders marking their doors "No Popery" to escape [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s attention crystallizes the movement's transformation from political petition to protection racket.
