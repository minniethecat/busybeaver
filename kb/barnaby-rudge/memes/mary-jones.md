## Historical Biography

Mary Jones was a young woman — not yet nineteen and "most remarkably handsome" — who was hanged in 1777 for the crime of shoplifting a piece of coarse linen from a draper's shop on Ludgate Hill. Her case, recounted by Sir William Meredith in a Parliamentary speech "on Frequent Executions," became a notorious example of the severity of England's penal code in the eighteenth century.

Jones's husband had been pressed into naval service during the alarm over the Falkland Islands. Their goods were seized for his debts, and she was turned into the streets with two small children to beg. In desperation, she took the linen from a shop counter and slipped it under her cloak. The shopman saw her, and she laid it down immediately — but she was arrested, tried, and sentenced to death.

## Dickens's Account

Dickens includes Mary Jones's story in the Preface to *Barnaby Rudge* not as fiction but as historical evidence of the era's brutality. He quotes Sir William Meredith's speech at length:

> "Under this act [the Shoplifting Act], one Mary Jones was executed, whose case I shall just mention; it was at the time when press warrants were issued, on the alarm about Falkland Islands. The woman's husband was pressed, their goods seized for some debts of his, and she, with two small children, turned into the streets a-begging." (P03-011)

Her defense — that she "had lived in credit, and wanted for nothing, till a press-gang came and stole her husband from her; but since then, she had no bed to lie on; nothing to give her children to eat; and they were almost naked" — was ignored. The parish officers confirmed her story, but "an example was thought necessary; and this woman was hanged for the comfort and satisfaction of shopkeepers in Ludgate Street."

Dickens reports that when she was brought to receive sentence, "she behaved in such a frantic manner, as proved her mind to be in a distracted and desponding state; and the child was sucking at her breast when she set out for Tyburn" (P03-011).

## In the Novel

Within the narrative, the hangman Dennis references Mary Jones as a professional boast:

> "I got Mary Jones, a young woman of nineteen, for a pocketbook" (037-057)

Dennis's casual pride in having executed her illustrates the moral callousness that Dickens associates with the penal system — the hangman values her as a professional trophy, not a human tragedy.

## Significance

Mary Jones serves a dual purpose in *Barnaby Rudge*:

1. **Historical Authenticity**: Dickens uses her documented case to establish that the novel's brutality — the hangings, the mob justice, the indifference to suffering — is not fictional exaggeration but historical reality
2. **Moral Argument**: Her story embodies the Preface's "good lesson" about a society that enforces property rights with capital punishment while offering no protection to the poor

Her fate at Tyburn, with an infant at her breast, is the novel's most compressed image of the injustice that the Gordon Riots both protested and reproduced.
