## Overview

St. Paul's Cathedral, designed by Sir Christopher Wren, is the Anglican cathedral of the Diocese of [[london|London]] and one of the city's most recognisable landmarks. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it serves as both a topographical reference point and a site of military action during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], where guards of soldiers were stationed to protect it from [[mob-violence|the mob]].

## Description

Dickens evokes St. Paul's as a visual constant on the [[london|London]] skyline — its dome dominating the cityscape even as the streets below descend into chaos:

> …and, high among them all, St. Paul's towers up, showing its loftiest dome and cross… (075-001)

The cathedral's churchyard serves as a gathering point for the marching crowds:

> …reet, and Cheapside, and into St. Paul's Churchyard. Arriving close… (037-031)

## Role in the Narrative

St. Paul's Cathedral features in two distinct registers. First, it is a romantic and architectural reference — the narrator evokes St. Paul's dome as a sublime presence against the [[london|London]] sky (022-001, 075-001). Second, and more dramatically, it becomes a flashpoint during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. The cathedral was a known Catholic target (though itself Anglican), and guards were deployed there:

> …f a great crowd straight upon Saint Paul's, attacked a guard of soldiers… (067-016)

This moment captures the riots' paradoxical geography: Protestant rioters menacing the nation's most prominent Protestant cathedral, the soldiers defending an edifice that the rioters theoretically supported but practically threatened. The irony is characteristic of Dickens's treatment of [[mob-violence|mob psychology]].

## Characters Associated

- The guards of soldiers stationed at St. Paul's — Defended the cathedral from the advancing mob
- The rioters — Attempted to overrun the cathedral during the riots

## Historical Basis

Wren's St. Paul's Cathedral was completed in 1710 and was less than 70 years old at the time of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. During the actual riots of June 1780, St. Paul's was indeed a concern for authorities. The [[london|City of London]] militia and regular troops were deployed to defend strategic points, and the cathedral featured in these plans. The [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] Rioters, though anti-Catholic, did not distinguish clearly between Catholic and Anglican targets once the violence escalated — [[mob-violence|the mob]] attacked property indiscriminately. Dickens's depiction of the attack on the guards at St. Paul's (067-016) reflects this historical reality.
