## Overview

The Shoplifting Act (10 Will. 3, c. 12), passed in 1699 under William III, made it a capital felony to steal goods valued at five shillings or more from a shop, warehouse, or coach-house. It was one of the notorious "Bloody Code" statutes that expanded the number of capital offenses in England from approximately fifty at the start of the eighteenth century to over two hundred by its end.

## Mary Jones

The Act's most famous victim was Mary Jones, whose execution in 1777 Dickens recounts in the Preface to *Barnaby Rudge*. Jones stole a piece of coarse linen from a draper's shop on Ludgate Hill and laid it down when the shopman saw her — but she was convicted and sentenced to death under the Shoplifting Act nonetheless. Dickens quotes Sir William Meredith's speech in Parliament:

> "Under this act [the Shoplifting Act], one Mary Jones was executed" (P03-011)

Her case was cited in Parliamentary debates as evidence of the penal code's inhumanity, and Meredith's recounting of it — with the detail that "the child was sucking at her breast when she set out for Tyburn" — became one of the most frequently quoted arguments for reform.

## In the Novel

Dickens invokes the Shoplifting Act in the Preface to establish the novel's moral framework. The Gordon rioters — who included professional hangmen like Dennis who boasted of executing women under this statute — were themselves subject to the same brutal code they claimed to protest. Dennis's pride in having "got Mary Jones" (037-057) collapses the distance between lawful execution and mob violence: the state killed Mary Jones for five shillings' worth of linen; the mob killed Catholics for the crime of their faith. Both were forms of public violence dressed in the language of justice.

## Historical Significance

The Shoplifting Act remained in force until 1820, by which time Parliamentary committees had documented the systematic injustice of a penal code that made property worth more than human life. The Act's repeal was part of the gradual dismantling of the Bloody Code that Dickens's generation — the generation that produced the Reform Bill, the Factory Acts, and the abolition of slavery — carried forward.
