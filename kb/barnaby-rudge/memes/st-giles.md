## Overview

St. Giles was one of London's most notorious "rookeries" — densely populated slums whose cramped courts and alleys housed the city's poorest inhabitants. Situated near the intersection of modern-day Oxford Street and Charing Cross Road, the parish of St. Giles-in-the-Fields had degenerated from a fashionable suburb into a byword for urban squalor by the mid-eighteenth century. The area was a natural recruiting ground for the Gordon rioters.

## In the Novel

St. Giles appears in *Barnaby Rudge* at a significant moment — the start of the riots' final and most destructive phase:

> "away by Saint Giles's to Tottenham Court Road, at a pace not to be followed" (044-004)

The mob passes through St. Giles en route to the attack on the Warren, the Haredale estate. Dickens's route-mapping is precise: the crowd moves from central London through St. Giles, then northward toward the countryside. The district functions in the narrative as a threshold — the point where the urban mob crosses from the familiar geography of the City into the rural world of the novel's private plot.

The rioters drawn from St. Giles and similar districts represent the social dimension of the Gordon Riots that Dickens emphasizes throughout: the riots were not merely a religious protest but an explosion of class resentment. The slum-dwellers of St. Giles had no stake in the Catholic question; they joined the mob because the mob offered license, loot, and the intoxicating experience of power.

## The Rookery

The St. Giles rookery was one of London's most densely populated areas. Contemporary accounts describe streets so narrow that neighbors could shake hands across them from upper windows, courtyards where sunlight never penetrated, and a population so transient that census-takers gave up counting. The area was predominantly Irish — many of its inhabitants were Catholic immigrants — which added a grim irony to the anti-Catholic mob's passage through it.

The rookery was largely cleared in the 1840s to make way for New Oxford Street, a public health measure that Dickens would have witnessed during the years he was writing *Barnaby Rudge*.
