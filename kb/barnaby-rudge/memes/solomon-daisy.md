Solomon Daisy is the parish clerk of [[chigwell|Chigwell]] and a fixture of [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn's fireside circle. He is the keeper of the novel's originating ghost story — the tale of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]]'s murder — which he tells on the stormy night that opens the novel, setting the mystery plot in motion.

## Appearance and Manner

Dickens consistently describes Solomon Daisy as "little," emphasizing his diminutive stature and gentle nature (003-001):

> the cricket-like chirrup of little Solomon Daisy

His voice and manner are mild, and his storytelling style is deliberate and self-important, easily "nettled" when interrupted or questioned (002-002). He is a creature of routine — his regular evening visits to [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] with [[tom-cobb|Tom Cobb]] and [[phil-parkes|Phil Parkes]] (called "[[phil-parkes|Long Parkes]]") are a fixed part of [[chigwell|Chigwell]] life that spans the entire novel, from Chapter 1 to the final pages (082-014).

## The Murder Story

Solomon Daisy's most significant contribution to the plot is his tale of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]]'s murder. On the night that opens the novel — the anniversary of the [[guilt|crime]] — he recounts the story to the assembled company at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] (001-083):

> That story is Solomon Daisy's story. It belongs to the house, and nobody but Solomon Daisy has ever told it under this roof.

His telling of the story (001-085–088) establishes the central mystery: the murder of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]] at [[the-warren|The Warren]] twenty-two years earlier, the disappearance of [[barnaby-rudge-senior|the steward]] Rudge, and the haunting of the scene ever since.

## Role in the Community

Solomon Daisy serves as the moral compass and institutional memory of [[chigwell|Chigwell]]. At [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], he is the one who proposes formal action (011-002):

> on the motion of little Solomon Daisy, every man (including John himself) should be fined a shilling

He also participates in the village watch and is present at key moments — notably in Chapter 33, when he arrives with his lantern to share the news of the stranger's appearance at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], his eyes "already beginning to roll and glisten" with the pleasure of bearing dramatic tidings (033-031).

## The Final Chapter

Solomon Daisy's presence in the novel's closing pages (Chapter 82) — still making his regular visits to [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] with [[tom-cobb|Tom Cobb]] and [[phil-parkes|Phil Parkes]] — signals the restoration of ordinary life after the violence of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. His survival and unchanged routine represent continuity and the quiet virtues of English village life.
