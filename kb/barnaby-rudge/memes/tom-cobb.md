Tom Cobb is the general chandler and post-office keeper of [[chigwell|Chigwell]], and a fixture of village life throughout *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*. He is introduced in the novel's opening pages and remains present until the final chapter, forming one third of [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn's regular evening company alongside [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]] and [[phil-parkes|Phil Parkes]].

## Introduction at the Maypole

Dickens introduces Tom Cobb in Chapter 1, in the midst of the stormy night that opens the novel. He is described physically with a single telling detail (001-018):

> the eyes belonging to short Tom Cobb the general chandler and post-office keeper

His stature — "short Tom Cobb" — becomes his identifying epithet. During [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]]'s tale of the murder, Tom Cobb is the most impressionable listener, reacting with whispered alarm (001-023):

> "A highwayman!" whispered Tom Cobb to Parkes the ranger.

## The Maypole Trio

Tom Cobb forms an inseparable trio with [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]] (the parish clerk) and [[phil-parkes|Phil Parkes]] (the ranger). Dickens uses the three men — with [[john-willet|John Willet]] as their host — to create a portrait of village life that spans the novel. They are present in Chapter 1 and still meeting regularly in Chapter 82 (082-014):

> To this, his new habitation, Tom Cobb, Phil Parkes, and Solomon Daisy went regularly every night.

This continuity is significant — through all the violence and upheaval of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], the rhythm of village life at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] endures.

## The Murder Story

Tom Cobb serves as the primary audience for [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]]'s tale of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]]'s murder in Chapter 1. His credulous reactions — whispers, gasps, and earnest questions — mirror the reader's own initial engagement with the novel's central mystery. In Chapter 33, when the anniversary of the murder brings the story back to the forefront, Tom Cobb's eagerness is palpable (033-055):

> "Who!" cried Parkes and Tom Cobb, looking eagerly by turns at Solomon Daisy and at Mr. Willet.

## Function in the Novel

Tom Cobb and his companions represent the stable, ordinary world against which the novel's violence is measured. They are the "chorus" of [[chigwell|Chigwell]] — present at key moments, reacting as the community would react, and providing a baseline of English village normality that makes the riots' chaos all the more striking by contrast.
