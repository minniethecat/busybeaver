## Overview

The Trial of the Rioters was the legal reckoning that followed the suppression of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. Over several weeks in June and July 1780, scores of riot participants were tried at the [[old-bailey|Old Bailey]] and other [[london|London]] sessions. A large number were condemned to death and publicly hanged at various locations across the city, including a mass [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] outside [[imprisonment|Newgate]] [[imprisonment|prison]]. Among those condemned were [[hugh|Hugh]] the ostler and [[dennis|Dennis]] the hangman — the latter facing the bitter irony of dying on the very instrument of his profession.

## Historical Background

The legal response to the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] was severe and exemplary. Of the estimated 450 rioters taken into custody, approximately 160 were brought to [[justice-and-punishment|trial]]. Some twenty to thirty were executed, mostly by hanging, at locations across [[london|London]] — at [[imprisonment|Newgate]], in [[bishopsgate-street|Bishopsgate Street]], on [[holborn|Holborn Hill]], and in [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]]. The [[justice-and-punishment|trial]]s were conducted at the [[old-bailey|Old Bailey]] sessions house with unusual speed, the authorities determined to demonstrate that riot and destruction would meet with swift and visible punishment.

A historical peculiarity noted by Dickens is that the [[justice-and-punishment|trial]]s were conducted by the very same legal establishment that the rioters had sought to defend by their anti-Catholic violence — "preserving the [[old-bailey|Old Bailey]] in all its [[innocence|purity]], and the gallows in all its pristine usefulness and moral grandeur" (070-003).

## Depiction in the Novel

### The Prisoners

In the aftermath of the riots, the principal rioters in Dickens's narrative were captured. [[dennis|Dennis]] was arrested late on a Friday evening and carried before a justice the next morning, where the testimony of [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] proved decisive. The charges against him were "numerous and weighty" and he was committed for [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] (074-001).

[[hugh|Hugh]] was taken by a patrol, and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], who had been swept up in the crowd without fully understanding the violence, was also arrested. The three men — [[hugh|Hugh]], [[dennis|Dennis]], and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] — were held in the condemned cells at [[imprisonment|Newgate]], awaiting the gallows.

### The Condemned Cell

Dickens's treatment of the condemned [[imprisonment|prison]]ers is psychologically acute. [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]], who had spent his professional life executing others, now found himself on the other side of the iron door. His sense of betrayal is existential:

> When he remembered the great estimation in which his office was held, and the constant demand for his services; when he bethought himself, how the Statute Book regarded him as a kind of Universal Medicine, applicable to men, women, and children, of every age and variety of criminal character; and how high he stood, in his official capacity, in the favour of the Crown, and both Houses of Parliament, the Mint, the Bank of England, and the Judges of the land … he felt it almost incredible that he should be brought to Newgate like a common man. (074-003)

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], by contrast, bore his [[imprisonment|confinement]] with childlike incomprehension — living in a "dim sense of the past" and never fully understanding the mechanism of justice that had ensnared him.

### Gabriel Varden's Intervention

The locksmith [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], who had been a witness against [[dennis|Dennis]], undertook a final mission of [[forgiveness|mercy]]. Visiting [[hugh|Hugh]] in his cell, he learned the secret of [[hugh|Hugh]]'s parentage — that [[hugh|Hugh]] was the illegitimate son of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], a fact that [[hugh|Hugh]] himself had only just discovered. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel]] carried this news to Sir John, hoping it would move him to intervene:

> "When he was taken to the jail, he found that his fellow-prisoner, in the same room, was a young man, Hugh by name, a leader in the riots, who had been betrayed and given up by himself. From something which fell from this unhappy creature in the course of the angry words they had at meeting, he discovered that his mother had suffered the death to which they both are now condemned." (075-048)

Sir John, coldly indifferent to his son's fate, refused to help.

### The Reprieve

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] was saved from the gallows at the final moment by the intervention of [[geoffrey-haredale|Mr. Haredale]], who obtained a last-minute reprieve. [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[dennis|Dennis]], however, were executed alongside the other condemned rioters in the mass hanging outside [[imprisonment|Newgate]] on a bright summer morning. Dickens describes the scene:

> As the warmth of the cheerful day began to shed itself upon the scanty crowd, the murmur of tongues was heard, shutters were thrown open, and blinds drawn up, and those who had slept in rooms over against the prison, where places to see the execution were let at high prices, rose hastily from their beds. (077-007)

[[dennis|Dennis]], to the last, begged desperately for his life: "Stop my [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] — for charity's sake stop my [[justice-and-punishment|execution]], gentlemen — till they can be told that I've been hangman here, nigh thirty year" (077-035). The crowd, indifferent, watched the sentence carried out.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s marked the formal conclusion of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] in the novel. For the surviving characters, the [[justice-and-punishment|trial]]s left lasting scars. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], though spared, could "never separate his condemnation and escape from the idea of a terrific dream" and never again set foot in [[london|London]] (082-019). [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], haunted by his role as witness, tempered his public duty with private charity. [[geoffrey-haredale|Mr. Haredale]], having secured [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s reprieve, fled England to a continental monastery of "rigour and severity" (082-002).

Historically, the [[justice-and-punishment|trial]]s had a deterrent effect — no comparable urban riot occurred in [[london|London]] for decades — but the underlying anti-Catholic sentiment persisted. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] himself was tried for high treason in February 1781 and acquitted, the court finding no proof of traitorous intent (082-004).
