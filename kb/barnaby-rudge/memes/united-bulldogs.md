## Overview

The United Bulldogs are one of the most prominent of the numerous "No Popery" societies that proliferated in London during the anti-Catholic agitation of 1780. Named with an aggressive, martial title that evokes both British patriotism and canine ferocity, the United Bulldogs represent the organized, branded face of the mob. Unlike the nameless crowds that surge through the streets, the Bulldogs possess an identity, a membership structure (complete with half-guinea dues), and a recognized presence in the movement's internal politics.

## Role in the Novel

The United Bulldogs make their first appearance in the catalogue of Protestant societies that Dickens presents as Lord George Gordon reviews the petition effort. They are listed among other subscription-based clubs:

> The United Bulldogs, half-a-guinea. (036-020)

Lord George Gordon himself acknowledges their significance, repeating the name with a mixture of unease and recognition (036-021). The Bulldogs are not merely a paper organization: a "detachment of United Bulldogs" appears in person during the march on Parliament, "received with very flat" enthusiasm by the more respectable elements of the crowd (039-001). Their reputation for violence precedes them, and even other rioters regard them with caution.

The Bulldogs' membership overlaps with the novel's most hardened characters. Dennis the hangman and Hugh the ostler are both identified as being "selected from the Brotherhood of United Bulldogs" (049-002), marking the organization as a recruiting ground for the riot's most ruthless participants. In the novel's ironic epilogue, Dickens reports that "the United Bulldogs were to a man all killed, imprisoned, or transported" (082-010), a dismissive footnote that underscores the organization's transient, destructive nature.

## Significance

The United Bulldogs embody Dickens's critique of organized mob violence. Their name — part patriotic symbol, part threat — captures the way political movements rebrand brutality as loyalty. The detail of the half-guinea subscription fee is characteristically Dickensian: even righteous indignation, in this world, has its price. The Bulldogs' disappearance from history at the novel's end — killed, imprisoned, or transported — makes a grim moral point: organizations built on hatred leave nothing behind but a casualty list.
