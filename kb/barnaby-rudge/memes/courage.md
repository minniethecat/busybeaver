## Definition

Courage in *Barnaby Rudge* is not a single virtue but a spectrum. The novel distinguishes between physical courage (the willingness to face danger), moral courage (the willingness to stand by one's principles), and what might be called institutional courage — the refusal to abandon one's post when the institutions of civilization are under attack. These varieties of courage are distributed unevenly among the characters, and the novel's moral judgments turn on who possesses which.

## Physical Courage

Gabriel Varden is the novel's primary exemplar of physical courage. The locksmith repeatedly places himself in danger to protect others: he confronts the mob, shields the wounded, and refuses to be intimidated by threats. His courage is instinctive rather than calculated — he acts before he thinks, and his actions are invariably on the side of protection rather than aggression.

Joe Willet, who loses an arm fighting for the Crown in the American war, represents a quieter form of physical courage — the courage of endurance rather than action. His wound is a permanent reminder that courage has costs that persist long after the moment of bravery has passed.

## Moral Courage

Geoffrey Haredale embodies moral courage — the twenty-seven-year pursuit of his brother's murderer, conducted without fanfare or self-pity. His courage is not theatrical; it is the steady, grinding courage of a man who has subordinated his life to a single purpose. When he finally confronts Rudge, there is no triumph in his voice, only the exhaustion of a long task completed.

Akerman, the keeper of Newgate, represents institutional courage. Faced with a mob that outnumbers him a thousand to one, he does not flee or bargain. He stands at his post and speaks plainly: "All I can do, good people, is to exhort you to disperse" (064-011). His courage is the courage of the functionary — unglamorous, procedural, and, in Dickens's moral universe, the highest form of virtue.

## The Cowardice of Authority

The novel's treatment of courage is sharpened by its contrasting portraits of cowardice. Lord Mayor Brackley Kennett, who hides behind bolted doors while the city burns, is the novel's supreme coward. His failure is not physical — he is not personally threatened — but institutional. He had the power to act and chose not to.

Sir John Chester represents a more insidious form of cowardice: the refusal to commit. His elegant detachment is a mask for moral nullity — he will not risk himself for any cause, any person, or any principle. In a novel that values the willingness to take a stand, Chester's perpetual ambivalence is the negation of courage.
