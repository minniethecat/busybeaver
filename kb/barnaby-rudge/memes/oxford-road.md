## Overview

Oxford Road — known today as Oxford Street — appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as a key thoroughfare of eighteenth-century [[london|London]]. It is mentioned in two distinct contexts: as the road on which the hangman encounters [[hugh|Hugh]] near a turnpike, establishing the conspiratorial networks of the novel's underworld; and as the route of [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s triumphant procession from the City to his house in [[welbeck-street|Welbeck Street]], mapping the geography of Protestant political agitation.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens gives Oxford Road no extended physical description. It appears as a functional landmark — a road that characters travel along, whose significance [[deception|lies]] in the kinds of people who use it and the destinations it connects. The first mention occurs in a night-time encounter between the hangman and [[hugh|Hugh]]:

> "Didn't I pass you near the turnpike in the Oxford Road?" (016-012)

The turnpike reference identifies Oxford Road as a main arterial route with toll-gates, linking the City to the western suburbs. The road reappears during [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s public procession:

> So along the Strand, up Swallow Street, into the Oxford Road, and thence to his house in Welbeck Street, near Cavendish Square. (037-032)

This route — [[strand|Strand]] to Swallow Street to Oxford Road to [[welbeck-street|Welbeck Street]] — traces the geography of fashionable [[london|London]], from the political centre of [[westminster|Westminster]] eastward into the aristocratic squares of the West End.

## Role in the Narrative

Oxford Road's narrative role is that of a connecting thread between the novel's disparate worlds. The hangman's encounter with [[hugh|Hugh]] near the turnpike places the road within the nocturnal geography of [[guilt|crime]] and conspiracy — a liminal space where the agents of state violence (the hangman) and popular violence ([[hugh|Hugh]]) meet in the dark. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s procession along the same road, by contrast, transforms it into a stage for public political theatre, lined with cheering crowds and waving handkerchiefs.

The road thus functions as a spatial marker of the novel's central tension: the same thoroughfare that carries a peer of the realm in triumph also shelters the hangman and the rioter in their covert dealings. [[london|London]]'s streets, in Dickens's vision, belong to everyone and to no one.
