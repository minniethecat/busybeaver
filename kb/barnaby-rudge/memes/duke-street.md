## Overview

Duke Street, in Lincoln's Inn Fields, was the location of the Roman Catholic chapel attached to the Sardinian Embassy, one of the first two targets of the [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] rioters' anti-Catholic violence on the night of Friday, 2 June 1780. Along with the [[warwick-street|Warwick Street]] chapel in [[warwick-street|Golden Square]], Duke Street was named by [[gashford|Gashford]] as a potential target, and [[mob-violence|the mob]] that had been "boisterous at [[westminster|Westminster]] upon the Friday morning" was "eagerly bent upon the work of devastation in Duke Street and [[warwick-street|Warwick Street]] at night" (052-002). The destruction of these chapels marked the first escalation from political demonstration to physical violence.

## Description

Like [[warwick-street|Warwick Street]], Duke Street is treated by Dickens primarily as a named target rather than a visually rendered place. The narrative concern is with the attack itself and its consequences, not with the architectural character of the chapel. The street is embedded within the legal quarter of [[london|London]] — Lincoln's Inn Fields is surrounded by the Inns of Court — a detail that underscores the irony of lawlessness erupting in the very precinct of the law.

## Role in the Narrative

Duke Street enters the novel through [[gashford|Gashford]]'s insinuating speech to [[hugh|Hugh]], [[dennis|Dennis]], and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] in Chapter 50. Feigning detachment, the secretary drops the names of two Catholic chapels:

> "I hear — but I cannot say whether it be true or false — that the men who are loitering in the streets tonight are half disposed to pull down a Romish chapel or two, and that they only want leaders. I even heard mention of those in Duke Street, Lincoln's Inn Fields, and in Warwick Street, Golden Square; but common report, you know — You are not going?" (050-036)

The rhetorical question at the end — "You are not going?" — is [[gashford|Gashford]]'s signature technique: planting a suggestion and then pretending to disclaim it. The rioters rush out immediately and the chapels are attacked that night.

The attack on Duke Street becomes a point of moral reckoning for [[gabriel-varden|the locksmith]] [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], who fears his apprentice's complicity: "If he has been at [[warwick-street|Warwick Street]] or Duke Street tonight —" (051-018). [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]]'s denial is characteristically evasive — "He was not at Duke Street, or at [[warwick-street|Warwick Street]], G. Varden" (051-022) — a statement that is literally true (Tappertit was at [[westminster|Westminster]] attacking members of [[parliament|Parliament]]) but morally empty.

In Chapter 52, Dickens explicitly links the two chapel attacks to the [[westminster|Westminster]] demonstration, identifying [[mob-violence|the mob]] at all three locations as "in the mass, the same" (052-002). This is a significant narrative claim: the "peaceful" [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] procession and the violent chapel destructions were not separate events but phases of a single movement. The Duke Street attack thus serves as evidence for the novel's broader argument about the inseparability of mob politics and mob violence.

## Historical Basis

The Sardinian Embassy chapel in Duke Street, Lincoln's Inn Fields, was one of the principal Catholic places of worship attacked during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. It was ransacked on the night of 2 June 1780; its furnishings, vestments, and altar fittings were dragged into the street and burned. Dickens's pairing of Duke Street and [[warwick-street|Warwick Street]] reflects the historical record: the Sardinian (Duke Street) and Bavarian ([[warwick-street|Warwick Street]]) embassy chapels were the two most prominent Catholic targets on the first night of rioting. The attack on embassy chapels raised delicate diplomatic questions, as these were technically foreign soil under the protection of Catholic powers.
