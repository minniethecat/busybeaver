## Overview

Bedlam, properly the Bethlem Royal Hospital, was [[london|London]]'s principal institution for the [[imprisonment|confinement]] of the mentally ill. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it appears as a symbol of chaos — the rioters threaten to throw open its gates and release the madmen into the streets.

## Description

Dickens does not describe Bedlam's physical premises directly. Instead, the asylum enters the novel through metaphor and threat. [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] invokes it with dark humour:

> …A friend in Bedlam, half-a-crown. Dennis the han… (036-015)

The rioters themselves are compared to Bedlam's inmates released:

> …If Bedlam gates had been flung wide open, there would not have issued forth such maniacs as the frenzy of that night had made. (055-033)

The threat becomes explicit as [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s intentions escalate:

> …they meant to throw the gates of Bedlam open, and let all the madmen loose… (067-007)

## Role in the Narrative

Bedlam functions in the novel less as a physical location than as a controlling metaphor for the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] themselves. The threat to open its gates (067-007) crystallises the fear that the riots represented: not merely property destruction, but the collapse of the boundary between order and chaos, reason and madness. The ironic observation that the rioters were madder than Bedlam's actual inmates (055-033) is one of Dickens's most pointed comments on [[mob-violence|mob psychology]].

## Characters Associated

- [[dennis]] — The hangman, who jokes darkly about having a friend in Bedlam

## Historical Basis

The Bethlem Royal Hospital moved to [[moorfields|Moorfields]] in 1676, to a grand building designed by Robert Hooke. At the time of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] (1780), it still occupied this site. The threat to throw Bedlam open was a real fear recorded in contemporary accounts — the asylum was a potent symbol in the 18th-century imagination. Dickens's use of Bedlam as a metaphor for social disorder draws on a long literary tradition. The hospital moved to St. George's Fields in [[southwark|Southwark]] in 1815, to the building that now houses the Imperial War Museum.
