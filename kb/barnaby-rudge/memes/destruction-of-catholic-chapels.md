## Overview

The Destruction of Catholic Chapels was the first phase of organised violence during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], following directly from the failed [[march-on-parliament|March on Parliament]] of 2 June 1780. Over the nights of 3–5 June, mobs of Protestant rioters systematically attacked and destroyed Roman Catholic places of worship in [[moorfields|Moorfields]], Wapping, and [[smithfield|East Smithfield]], along with the private houses of known Catholic fami[[deception|lies]]. The chapels were stripped of altars, pulpits, pews, and even flooring — the raw materials burned in great bonfires that illuminated the [[london|London]] skyline for miles.

## Historical Background

The targeting of Catholic places of worship was the most visible and symbolic act of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]' anti-Catholic phase. Since the Reformation, Catholic worship in England had been legally restricted; Catholic chapels were tolerated only in certain districts and among foreign embassy communities. The Saville Act of 1778, which sought to ease some of these restrictions, provoked the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s campaign of petitioning — and, when petitioning failed, a campaign of destruction.

The rioters carried trophies of their work openly through the streets, making no effort to [[deception|disguise]] themselves. Dickens records that the spoils were "easily recognisable" as belonging to Catholic worship, and that [[mob-violence|the mob]] moved from one target to the next with the methodical deliberation of workmen performing a day's labour.

## Depiction in the Novel

### The First Attacks

The destruction began on the Sunday evening following the march on [[parliament|Parliament]]. The mob approached the work not with frenzy but with an almost professional detachment:

> Beginning with the private houses so occupied, they broke open the doors and windows; and while they destroyed the furniture and left but the bare walls, made a sharp search for tools and engines of destruction, such as hammers, pokers, axes, saws, and suchlike instruments. Many of the rioters made belts of cord, of handkerchiefs, or any material they found at hand, and wore these weapons as openly as pioneers upon a field-day. There was not the least disguise or concealment — indeed, on this night, very little excitement or hurry. (052-031)

The chapels themselves were dismantled with the same systematic thoroughness:

> From the chapels, they tore down and took away the very altars, benches, pulpits, pews, and flooring; from the dwelling-houses, the very wainscoting and stairs. This Sunday evening's recreation they pursued like mere workmen who had a certain task to do, and did it. (052-031)

### Bonfires of Sacred Objects

The spoils of the chapels were piled into great bonfires, which became the signature image of the riots' early phase:

> Priestly garments, images of saints, rich stuffs and ornaments, altar-furniture and household goods, were cast into the flames, and shed a glare on the whole country round; but they danced and howled, and roared about the fires. (052-032)

Dickens notes with grim irony that "fifty resolute men might have turned them at any moment; a single company of soldiers could have scattered them like dust; but no man interposed, no authority restrained them" (052-031). The civil power, paralysed by the sheer scale of the disorder, stood by while the destruction proceeded unchecked.

### The Wider Campaign

The destruction was not confined to a single location. The rioters divided into detachments and fanned out across [[london|London]]. A witness records:

> The first detachment, carrying, by way of banners, some relics of the havoc they had made in Moorfields, proclaimed that they were on their way to Chelsea, whence they would return in the same order, to make of the spoil they bore, a great bonfire, near at hand. The second gave out that they were bound for Wapping, to destroy a chapel; the third, that their place of destination was East Smithfield, and their object the same. (052-030)

Even Catholic clergy — mild, elderly priests who had served their congregations for decades — were forced to flee. One old priest whose chapel was destroyed trudged away on foot, fearing no magistrate would dare commit a [[imprisonment|prison]]er to jail on his complaint (067-008).

## Aftermath and Consequences

The destruction of Catholic chapels achieved the rioters' immediate aim — the visible eradication of Catholic worship from whole districts of [[london|London]]. Catholic fami[[deception|lies]] fled the city in panic; businesses were shuttered; foreign ambassadors protested to the government. One observer noted that "people had been afraid to recognise Catholic acquaintance in the streets, lest they should be marked by spies, and burnt out, as it was called, in consequence" (067-008).

Paradoxically, the comprehensive success of the chapel-burning phase emboldened [[mob-violence|the mob]] to escalate. Having destroyed places of worship with impunity, the rioters turned to public institutions — most dramatically, [[burning-of-newgate|Newgate prison]] itself — and then to private country estates such as [[attack-on-the-warren|the Warren]]. The chapels' destruction was the pivot between political demonstration and generalised lawlessness.
