## Overview

The burning of [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] on June 6, 1780, was the most spectacular single act of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]].

A mob of thousands forced the gates, freed every inmate, and set the building ablaze.

In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, the event occupies chapters 64 and 65.
It serves as historical set piece and narrative turning point alike.

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed for his part in the riots, faces death as the fire consumes the [[imprisonment|prison]] around him.

The murderer of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]] is released into the chaos.
The [[imprisonment|prison]] that confined [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] is destroyed by the same violence threatening to consume him.

Dickens noted his account of "all the main features of the Riots, is substantially correct" (P03-008).

The destruction of Newgate is the pivot on which the riot narrative turns.
It marks the point where anti-Catholic protest becomes a general assault on the state.

## Historical Background

[[newgate|Newgate Prison]] stood at the corner of [[imprisonment|Newgate]] Street and [[old-bailey|Old Bailey]].
It was rebuilt after the Great Fire of 1666 and renovated in the 1770s.

The [[imprisonment|prison]] was notorious for overcrowding, "gaol fever" (typhus), and the mixing of debtors, felons, and the mentally ill.

Dickens's preface documents the era's barbarous penal system.
[[dennis|Dennis]]'s "allusions to the flourishing condition of his trade... have their foundation in Truth" (P03-009).

The Mary Jones case, a young mother hanged for shoplifting, is from Sir William Meredith's 1777 speech (P03-011).
It illustrates the savagery that made [[imprisonment|Newgate]] an object of popular hatred.

The mob was "designed for the attack on [[imprisonment|Newgate]]" (063-005).

It included "all the rioters who had been conspicuous in any of their former proceedings" (063-005).

Relatives and friends of felons in the jail were also among them.

Akerman, the historical keeper of [[imprisonment|Newgate]], was a Quaker.
He refused to fire on the crowd without the Lord Mayor's authorization.
This decision cost him the [[imprisonment|prison]] but arguably saved lives.

The Guards had been "conveying to [[imprisonment|Newgate]] some rioters who had been re-examined" (056-001).

The narrative is pervaded by the irony that [[imprisonment|prison]]ers were sent "to [[imprisonment|Newgate]] for safety" (049-051).
The supposed bastion of state security became the rioters' primary target.

## Depiction in the Novel

### The Forming of the Mob

Dickens devotes chapter 63 entirely to [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s assembly and march on [[imprisonment|Newgate]].

By this point, "the crowd was everywhere; all [[deception|concealment]] and [[deception|disguise]] were laid aside" (063-003).

The mob carried "old swords, pistols without ball or powder; sledgehammers, knives, axes, saws" (063-006).

They brought a "forest of iron bars and wooden clubs; long ladders for scaling the walls" (063-006).

Among the crowd were women in men's clothing seeking to rescue children or brothers.
There were sons of a man under sentence of death, and boys whose fellow-pickpockets were inside.

At the edges were "a score of miserable women, outcasts from the world" (063-005).
They sought "to release some other fallen creature as miserable as themselves" (063-005).

After abducting [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] from his home, [[mob-violence|the mob]] halted "in a dense mass before the [[imprisonment|prison]]-gate" (063-063).

### The Siege of the Prison

Chapter 64 renders the assault with an intensity rarely matched in Victorian fiction.

[[hugh|Hugh]] confronted keeper Akerman on the rooftop, demanding the [[imprisonment|prison]]ers' release.

Akerman replied: "It's my duty to keep them all. I shall do my duty" (064-009).

The mob demanded that Varden pick the lock of the great door he had built.
He refused, crying "I will not!" even as [[dennis|Dennis]] struck him down (064-030).

The cry of "Remember [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]!" (064-037) redirected [[mob-violence|the mob]] from Varden to the [[imprisonment|prison]] itself.

> The strokes began to fall like hail upon the gate, and on the strong building; for those who could not reach the door, spent their fierce rage on anything — even on the great blocks of stone, which shivered their weapons into fragments, and made their hands and arms to tingle as if the walls were active in their stout resistance, and dealt them back their blows. (064-039)

When brute force failed, [[mob-violence|the mob]] turned to fire.
They piled the keeper's furniture against the gate "to make a bonfire which should burn it down" (064-040).

The furniture, "rendered more combustible by wax and oil," took fire at once (064-041).

Dickens's description of the conflagration is among the novel's most celebrated passages:

> When it shone and gleamed so brightly that the church clock of St. Sepulchre's so often pointing to the hour of death, was legible as in broad day, and the vane upon its steeple-top glittered in the unwonted light like something richly jewelled — when blackened stone and sombre brick grew ruddy in the deep reflection, and windows shone like burnished gold — when wall and tower, and roof and chimney-stack, seemed drunk, and in the flickering glare appeared to reel and stagger — then the mob began to join the whirl, and with loud yells, and shouts, and clamour, such as happily is seldom heard, bestirred themselves to feed the fire, and keep it at its height. (064-041)

The rhythmic anaphora builds to a crescendo mirroring [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s frenzy.

The clock of St. Sepulchre's, "so often pointing to the hour of death," serves as grim moral commentary.
The instrument that marked [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s now witnesses the [[imprisonment|prison]]'s destruction.

### The Prisoners' Terror

The [[imprisonment|prison]]ers saw from their bars that "the fire caught in many places and thrived fiercely" (064-043).

Locked in strong cells, "they began to know that they were in danger of being burnt alive" (064-043).

Their cries were "so full of agony and despair, that it made the boldest tremble" (064-043).

The four condemned men were "the first to whom the dread of being burnt occurred" (064-044).
They were "throughout, the most importunate of all" (064-044).

Their two sons, hearing their father's voice, tore at the stones with bare hands.
Dickens makes historical horror intimate.

### The Breaking of the Door

The great door, weakened by fire, finally yields:

> The door sank down again: it settled deeper in the cinders — tottered — yielded — was down! (064-049)

[[hugh|Hugh]] "leapt upon the blazing heap, scattering a train of sparks into the air" and "dashed into the jail" (064-050).

[[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] followed close behind.
"So many rushed upon their track, that the fire got trodden down" (064-051).
But it no longer mattered: "inside and out, the [[imprisonment|prison]] was in flames" (064-051).

### Inside the Prison

Chapter 65 shifts to the burning [[imprisonment|prison]]'s interior.

One man suffered "a degree of fear and mental torment which had no parallel" (065-001).

Even those under sentence of death did not endure such anguish.

This was the murderer of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]], convinced [[mob-violence|the mob]] had come to tear him apart.

Dickens calls him "a Lucifer among the devils" (065-004).
Even "the very captives in the jail fell off and shrunk appalled" from him (065-004).

The [[imprisonment|prison]]er release unfolds in a cascade of images.

Men are dragged by their legs, arms, and hair.

Highwaymen pass with "fetters muffled with handkerchiefs and bands of hay" (065-007).

A famished wretch is freed into "liberty to starve and die" (065-007).

In a moment of extraordinary compression, the murderer and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] cross paths in the yard.

"The yard was filled again, and a crowd rushed on, hurrying [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] among them" (065-011).
Father and son "were glancing backward at a burning pile which someone said was [[imprisonment|Newgate]]" (065-011).

### Dennis and the Condemned Men

The most chilling sequence involves [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]].

He took keys from a cupboard, locked himself into the condemned cells' gallery, and sat "sucking the head of his stick with the utmost complacency" (065-013).

The four [[fate|doom]]ed men begged for rescue through the gratings.
[[dennis|Dennis]] lectured them: "You've had law... and yet you're not contented!" (065-025).

His counsel was grotesque: "Whatever you do... never snivel" (065-029).
He preferred a man "tear his clothes a' purpose to spile 'em before they come to me, than find him snivelling" (065-029).

When [[hugh|Hugh]] arrived to free the condemned, [[dennis|Dennis]] protested.
"Don't you respect the law — the constitootion — nothing? Let the four men be" (065-035).

[[hugh|Hugh]] forced the cells open regardless.
The four men emerged "a mere heap of chains" (065-046).
Their release was "the crowning horror of the scene" (065-047).

After all had escaped, "a melancholy heap of smoking ruins marked the spot where it had lately chafed and roared" (065-049).

## Key Participants

- **[[Hugh]]** leads the assault with savage energy. His leap into the flames, "scattering a train of sparks into the air" (064-050), is an iconic image.

- **[[Dennis]]** is the hangman whose participation is thick with irony. His appeal to "the law — the constitootion" (065-035) exposes the novel's satirical critique of the penal system.

- **[[Barnaby Rudge]]** is im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed within [[imprisonment|Newgate]]. The cry of "Remember [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]!" (064-037) redirects [[mob-violence|the mob]] to the [[imprisonment|prison]]. His fate is the human stake.

- **[[Simon Tappertit]]** leads alongside [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[dennis|Dennis]], though his role in the climax is submerged in the general fury.

- **[[Gabriel Varden]]**, [[gabriel-varden|the locksmith]], is abducted to pick the lock he built. His "I will not!" (064-030) is the novel's emblem of individual integrity against mob violence.

- **[[Akerman]]**, the keeper, faces [[mob-violence|the mob]]: "It's my duty to keep them all" (064-009). His exchange with Varden creates a pocket of moral clarity in chaos.

## Aftermath and Consequences

### In the Novel

The destruction of Newgate sets in motion the novel's final sequence.

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s [[justice-and-punishment|trial]], the exposure of the murderer's identity, and [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]'s confrontation with his brother's killer all flow from this event.

[[dennis|Dennis]]'s failure to keep the condemned for his gallows foreshadows his own [[justice-and-punishment|execution]].

The intelligence that "[[mob-violence|the mob]] were setting fire to [[imprisonment|Newgate]]" (066-006) reaches [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] in the streets.

His cry, "To [[imprisonment|Newgate]]! where that man was!" (066-007), propels the murder plot toward its climax.

The [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s of captured rioters form the novel's closing moral cadence.

[[hugh|Hugh]] and [[dennis|Dennis]] are among those hanged.
Dickens frames these deaths as both the terminus of mob violence and a system perpetuating barbarism.

### Historical Consequences

The destruction exposed the inadequacy of [[london|London]]'s policing arrangements.

The Lord Mayor was "the faintest-hearted and most timid of them all" (063-002).
This failure contributed to the movement that led to the Metropolitan Police Act of 1829.

[[About]] 25 rioters were hanged, with many more transported.

[[dennis|Dennis]]'s "flourishing" trade was no fiction.
The Mary Jones case (P03-011) reminds readers the critique targets both mob and state justice.

[[imprisonment|Newgate]] was rebuilt and housed [[imprisonment|prison]]ers until 1902.
It was demolished in 1904 for the present [[old-bailey|Old Bailey]], where a fragment of the original wall still stands.

## Literary Significance

The burning of [[imprisonment|Newgate]] is the thematic and structural pivot of *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*.

Before this, the riots escalate through attacks on Catholic chapels and houses.
With [[imprisonment|Newgate]], [[mob-violence|the mob]] turns from targeted religious violence to an assault on the institutions of the state.

Dickens stages the event as a moral inversion.

The [[imprisonment|prison]] meant to protect endangers those inside.
The mob that claims to liberate brings destruction.
The hangman who should uphold law becomes a rioter saving the condemned for his gallows.

[[dennis|Dennis]] "sucking the head of his stick with the utmost complacency" (065-013) distills the novel's argument.
A system built on capital punishment corrupts everyone it touches.

The burning fuses documentary and melodrama, anchoring fictional characters in recorded history.
This prefigures the social-problem novels of Dickens's maturity.

St. Sepulchre's clock, "so often pointing to the hour of death" (064-041), is hallmark Dickensian technique.
One detail carries the full moral weight of the scene.

It anticipates the symbolic landscapes of *Bleak House* and *Our Mutual Friend*.
In those later novels, environment becomes an active participant in moral argument.
