## Overview

Cornhill is one of the principal thoroughfares of the [[london|City of London]], running eastward from the Bank junction towards Leadenhall Market. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it appears at three critical moments: as the traditional datum from which distances are measured in the novel's very first sentence; as the labyrinth into which a fugitive plunges after a murder; and as a defensive line during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] where iron chains were drawn and soldiers concealed.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens does not pause to describe Cornhill physically; its character is established by its function as the geographical centre of the City. The novel opens with it serving as the reference point for measuring the distance to [[chigwell|Chigwell]]:

> In the year 1775, there stood upon the borders of Epping Forest, at a distance of about twelve miles from London — measuring from the Standard in Cornhill, or rather from the spot on or near to which the Standard used to be in days of yore — a house of public entertainment called the Maypole. (001-001)

The Standard in Cornhill was the traditional central point from which all distances from [[london|London]] were reckoned. By anchoring the novel's geography here, Dickens places the [[maypole-inn|Maypole Inn]] — and [[chigwell|Chigwell]] — at precisely twelve miles from the heart of the metropolis.

## Role in the Narrative

Cornhill functions in three modes: as a cartographic anchor, as a labyrinth, and as a battlefield. Its second appearance comes when the mysterious stranger who has left [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]]'s house crosses [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] into the City:

> Gliding along the silent streets, and holding his course where they were darkest and most gloomy, the man who had left the widow's house crossed London Bridge, and arriving in the City, plunged into the backways, lanes, and courts, between Cornhill and [[smithfield|Smithfield]]; with no more fixedness of purpose than to lose himself among their windings, and baffle pursuit, if anyone were dogging his steps. (018-001)

The "backways, lanes, and courts, between Cornhill and [[smithfield|Smithfield]]" map a geography of [[deception|concealment]] — the ancient, tangled streets of the City offering refuge to a man who may be [[barnaby-rudge-senior|the murderer of Reuben Haredale]].

Its third appearance transforms Cornhill from a place of [[deception|concealment]] into one of confrontation. On the decisive night of the riots:

> In the [[poultry|Poultry]], and on Cornhill, and at several other leading points, iron chains were drawn across the street; parties of soldiers were distributed in some of the old city churches while it was yet dark; and in several private houses (among them, Lord Rockingham's in Grosvenor Square); which were blockaded as though to sustain a siege, and had guns pointed from the windows. (067-003)

The chaining of Cornhill represents the militarisation of the City's commercial heart — a place of trade and measurement rendered, for one terrible night, a fortified re[[suspicion|doubt]].
