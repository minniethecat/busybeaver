## Overview

The House of Lords is the upper chamber of the Parliament of the United Kingdom, housed in the Palace of Westminster. At the time of *Barnaby Rudge*, it was the more powerful of the two houses, composed of hereditary peers and bishops of the Church of England. The Lords could veto legislation passed by the Commons, a fact that loomed over the Protestant Association's campaign to repeal the Catholic Relief Act.

## In the Novel

The Lords appear in the novel primarily as the institutional backdrop to Lord George Gordon's agitation. Gordon, himself a member of the upper house by virtue of his title, uses his parliamentary status to lend legitimacy to the Protestant Association's cause.

Gashford, Gordon's secretary, boasts to the rioters about Gordon's influence among the peers:

> "He told him, too, who some of the Lords and Commons were, by name, as they came in and out" (038-032)

The procession Gordon leads is bound for the House of Commons rather than the Lords — but the Lords' power to block repeal made it the ultimate target of the petitioners' hopes. The petition's failure in the Commons meant that the Lords never debated the repeal at all, though several peers, including Lord Mansfield, were personally targeted by the mob.

## The Gordon Riots

On 2 June 1780, Lord George Gordon addressed the House of Lords, informing them that his supporters would present a petition to the Commons. The Lords responded by adjourning, effectively refusing to engage with the substance of the petition. This high-handed dismissal of popular grievance was one of the grievances that, in Gordon's rhetoric, justified the subsequent violence.

During the riots themselves, the Lords' chamber was not directly attacked — the mob focused on the Commons side of the Palace — but the peers' town houses were systematically targeted. Lord Mansfield's house in Bloomsbury Square was burned to the ground, and his valuable library and legal manuscripts were destroyed.
