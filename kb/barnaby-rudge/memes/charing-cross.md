## Overview

Charing Cross, a well-known junction in [[westminster|Westminster]] at the south side of Trafalgar Square, appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as the location of a hotel where [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] took shelter after fleeing the destruction of his ancestral home, [[the-warren|The Warren]]. Arriving alone and destitute as night fell, [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] entered the hotel near Charing Cross to order refreshment and a bed. His exhausted appearance drew unwanted attention from the landlord and waiters, who [[suspicion|suspected]] him of being penniless — a suspicion he dispelled by producing his purse (066-004).

## Description

The hotel near Charing Cross is depicted through [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]'s [[innocence|experience]] as a fugitive seeking anonymity. Dickens emphasises the social scrutiny of public lodging: [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]'s "faint and worn appearance" attracted the attention of the landlord and his waiters, who silently questioned his ability to pay. The scene captures the precariousness of a gentleman reduced to circumstances where his credit — and even his safety — must be demonstrated with coin.

> He found himself, with the night coming on, alone in the streets; and destitute of any place in which to lay his head. He entered an hotel near Charing Cross, and ordered some refreshment and a bed. He saw that his faint and worn appearance attracted the attention of the landlord and his waiters; and thinking that they might suppose him to be penniless, took out his purse, and laid it on the table. It was not that, the landlord said, in a faltering voice. (066-004)
