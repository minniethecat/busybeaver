## Definition

Guilt in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* operates on multiple planes: criminal [[innocence|guilt]] (the murderer's secret), inherited [[innocence|guilt]] (the son's suffering for the father's crime), and psychological [[innocence|guilt]] (the haunted conscience). The novel's central paradox is articulated by [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] herself: "I am [[innocence|guilt]]y, and yet innocent; wrong, yet right; good in intention, though constrained to shield and aid the bad" (025-051). Guilt is not simply a legal category — it is a corrosive force that isolates, transforms, and ultimately destroys those who carry it, while also falling unjustly upon the innocent. Dickens traces [[innocence|guilt]]'s path from the secret crime of Chapter 1 through to the scaffold, where [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] feels "their [[innocence|guilt]] his own" (073-063).

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Murderer's Guilt

The novel's [[innocence|guilt]] narrative centers on the murder of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]], a crime whose perpetrator remains hidden for decades. [[barnaby-rudge-senior|Rudge Sr.]] carries his secret [[innocence|guilt]] as a visible stain: "His [[innocence|guilt]]y conscience instantly arrayed these men against himself" (065-003), and he stands "marked and singled out by his great [[innocence|guilt]], a Lucifer among the devils" (065-004). When confronted at last, [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] names the truth: "You, whose hands are red with my brother's blood, and that of his faithful servant, shed to conceal your own atrocious [[innocence|guilt]]" (056-057). Even the murderer's cell becomes a map of [[innocence|guilt]] — the narrator describes "at the bottom of his every thought there was an uneasy sense of [[innocence|guilt]], and dread of death" (062-001).

### Inherited Guilt and the Innocent

[[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] bears the burden of her husband's secret, fearing "some other heavy judgement on the head of my dear son, whose innocence will suffer for his mother's [[innocence|guilt]]" (025-049). This inherited [[innocence|guilt]] — the innocent suffering for the [[innocence|guilt]]y — forms the novel's deepest moral wound. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] himself, though innocent of any crime, becomes "a creature who had sprung into existence from his victim's blood" in the murderer's tormented vision (069-035). The novel insists on this distinction between legal and moral [[innocence|guilt]], between the [[innocence|guilt]]y act and the innocent person caught in its wake.

### Collective Guilt and the Riots

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] extend [[innocence|guilt]] beyond the individual to the collective. In [[tower-of-london|the Tower]] after the riots, [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] [[innocence|experience]]s a moment of profound moral clarity: "remorseful for every act that had been done by every man among the cruel crowd; feeling for the time their [[innocence|guilt]] his own, and their lives put in peril by himself" (073-063). The rioters' [[innocence|guilt]] contaminates even the physical landscape — [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s presence torments the murderer with "terrible images of that [[innocence|guilt]]y night" (069-035). The moonlight becomes one of the novel's most striking images of [[innocence|guilt]]'s relationship to grace: "through the small compass of the grated window, as through the narrow crevice of one good deed in a murky life of [[innocence|guilt]], the face of Heaven shone bright and merciful" (073-034).
