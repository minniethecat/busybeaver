## Definition

Suspicion in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* functions as both a Gothic narrative mechanism and a thematic concern. As a plot device, suspicion is the engine of the mystery: the novel repeatedly misdirects the reader's attention, casting doubt on the innocent ([[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]], [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]]) while the true murderer walks unsuspected. As a theme, suspicion explores the corrosive effect of unspoken doubt on relationships and communities. Dickens deploys dramatic irony throughout — the reader shares knowledge that characters lack, watching suspicion fall on the wrong targets while the real threat remains hidden. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] articulates the novel's ambivalent relationship with suspicion when he says, "I am loth, I tell you plainly, to leave [[edward-chester|Mr. Edward]] here" after suspicions are forced upon him (006-086).

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Suspicion as Misdirection

The novel's masterplot depends on suspicion being systematically misdirected. After the murder of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]], "slanderous tongues" whisper "that the [[innocence|guilt]] of this most foul and cruel deed was mine" — [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]]'s own (061-026). The narrative strategically diverts attention: after the stranger's violent confrontation with [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]'s steward, "it was decided that he had left the gentleman above, for dead, and had adopted this stratagem to divert suspicion or pursuit" (012-048). Yet the reader, like the characters, cannot be certain. The raven [[grip|Grip]] becomes a symbol of this thematic current — as [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] says, "If there's any wickedness going on, that raven's in it, I'll be sworn" (006-088).

### The Burden of Being Suspected

Suspicion falls most heavily on [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]], whose poverty and [[deception|secrecy]] make her vulnerable. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], though sympathetic, cannot escape doubt: "she is poor, the temptation may be great, and we daily hear of things as strange" (006-088). The novel shows how suspicion operates as a social weapon — the powerless are suspected first. Even the innocent cannot escape its shadow: [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] lives for decades under the cloud of fratricide suspicion, fearing he will "die with the suspicion of having slain his own brother, dimly gathering about him" (066-007).

### Dramatic Irony and Unspoken Doubt

Dickens exploits dramatic irony to make suspicion a source of narrative tension. When [[edward-chester|Edward Chester]]'s hesitation and confusion are attributed to fright, the narrator notes that "no suspicion of the truth occurred to him for a moment" (021-032) — but the reader knows better. [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] lives in terror of discovery: "To caution him against their visitor, to show any fear or suspicion in reference to him, would only be, she feared, to increase that interest" (047-054). The novel's resolution requires the clearing of suspicion: "It is better and more fitting that the circumstances attendant on the past, which wrought your separation, and sowed between you suspicion and distrust, should not be entered on by me" (079-035). Suspicion is what must be undone for the novel to reach its reconciliatory close.
