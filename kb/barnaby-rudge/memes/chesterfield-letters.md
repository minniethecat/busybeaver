## Overview

*Letters to His Son on the Art of Becoming a Man of the World and a Gentleman* is a collection of more than four hundred letters written by Philip Dormer Stanhope, 4th Earl of Chesterfield, to his illegitimate son, Philip Stanhope. Published posthumously in 1774, the letters became an immediate sensation and a lasting monument of eighteenth-century advice literature. Written in elegant, epigrammatic prose, they counsel the young man on manners, ambition, the arts of pleasing, and — most controversially — the strategic dissimulation necessary for worldly success. Dr. Johnson's famous condemnation — that they taught "the morals of a whore and the manners of a dancing-master" — captures the enduring ambivalence toward Chesterfield's philosophy.

## Reference in *Barnaby Rudge*

Chesterfield's *Letters* appear in a brilliant scene of characterization. Sir John Chester, the novel's most consummate hypocrite, is discovered reading the book and delivers an ecstatic endorsement:

> "My Lord Chesterfield," he said, pressing his hand tenderly upon the book as he laid it down, "if I could but have profited by your genius soon enough to have formed my son on the model you have left to all wise fathers, both he and I would have been rich men." (023-005)

Sir John goes on to declare that in "every page of this enlightened writer, I find some captivating hypocrisy which has never occurred to me before, or some superlative piece of selfishness to which I was utterly a stranger" (023-007). The irony is devastating: Sir John, who has devoted his life to selfish manipulation, finds in Chesterfield a refinement of his own vices. The praise of "captivating hypocrisy" as a virtue is Dickens's most concentrated satire of aristocratic morality.

## Significance

Chesterfield's *Letters* function in the novel as a mirror held up to Sir John Chester — and, through him, to the governing class that proved so feckless during the Gordon Riots. Sir John's reverence for a book that celebrates emotional manipulation and social performance is not incidental; it is diagnostic. Dickens suggests that the elite's moral bankruptcy has canonical texts of its own, and that Sir John Chester is not merely a bad man but the product of a bad philosophy. The *Letters* are thus more than a period detail; they are an exhibit in the novel's case against the values of the ruling order.
