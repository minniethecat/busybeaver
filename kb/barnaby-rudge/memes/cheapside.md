## Overview

Cheapside, one of the principal thoroughfares of the [[london|City of London]], appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* at both extremes of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] arc. Early in the novel, it is the route of [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s triumphal progress towards St. Paul's — a street lined with cheering crowds. Later, during the military suppression, it becomes a site of deadly combat, where soldiers form across the road and charge the rioters at bayonet point. In the riots' aftermath, Cheapside is the imagined scene of grim reprisals — rumoured hangings from lampposts that haunt the shaken citizenry.

## Description

Cheapside is not visually described by Dickens; it is rendered through the events that pass along it. In Chapter 37, it is the final leg of [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s ride — a concatenation of [[whitechapel|Whitechapel]], Leadenhall Street, Cheapside, and [[st-pauls-cathedral|St. Paul's Churchyard]], each street a deepening stage of public acclamation. In Chapter 67, the street becomes a geography of military action: chains drawn across the road, soldiers forming lines, bayonets advancing. The name is almost all Dickens needs; to his contemporary readers, Cheapside was synonymous with the commercial heart of the City, and its transformation into a battlefield carried its own rhetorical force.

## Role in the Narrative

Cheapside first appears during [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s public entry into [[london|London]]. After stopping at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] rides through the eastern suburbs and into the City:

> So they rode (to the deep and unspeakable disgust of John Grueby) the whole length of Whitechapel, Leadenhall Street, and Cheapside, and into St. Paul's Churchyard. Arriving close to the cathedral, he halted; spoke to Gashford; and looking upward at its lofty dome, shook his head, as though he said, "The Church in Danger!" (037-031)

The ride through Cheapside is the crescendo of [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s public performance — by the time he reaches St. Paul's, the crowd's acclamation is at its height. The detail of [[john-grueby|John Grueby]]'s "deep and unspeakable disgust" provides a counterpoint: the loyal servant sees through the pageantry that [[mob-violence|the mob]] embraces.

Cheapside reappears with devastating effect in the "dreadful night" of Chapter 67. During the military crackdown, soldiers pursue the rioters from the [[poultry|Poultry]] into Cheapside:

> The firing began in the Poultry, where the chain was drawn across the road, where nearly a score of people were killed on the first discharge. Their bodies having been hastily carried into St. Mildred's Church by the soldiers, the latter fired again, and following fast upon the crowd, who began to give way when they saw the execution that was done, formed across Cheapside, and charged them at the point of the bayonet. (067-012)

The geography is precise: [[poultry|Poultry]] leads directly into Cheapside; the soldiers' line cuts across the thoroughfare and drives the crowd westward. This is the turning point — after the Cheapside charge, the riot is no longer an insurrection but a rout.

In the aftermath, Cheapside becomes the locus of urban nightmare. Dickens records the rumours that swept the frightened city: "dismal stories of [[imprisonment|prison]]ers having been seen hanging on lampposts in Cheapside and [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]]" (073-005). These stories prove false — dispelled by a royal proclamation — but their circulation reveals how deeply the riots had unsettled the civic imagination. Cheapside, the street of commerce and crowds, had become, in the public mind, a place of summary [[justice-and-punishment|execution]].

## Historical Basis

Cheapside was one of the busiest commercial streets in 18th-century [[london|London]], running east–west from the [[poultry|Poultry]] to [[st-pauls-cathedral|St. Paul's Churchyard]]. During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], it lay directly in the path of the military operations described in Chapter 67. The "chain" drawn across the road at [[poultry|Poultry]] was a historical reality — iron chains were a standard means of blocking streets against mob movement, used at multiple points across the City during the riots. The transition from the [[poultry|Poultry]] chain to the Cheapside bayonet charge tracks a real spatial sequence in the City's geography.
