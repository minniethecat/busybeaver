## Historical Biography

William Murray, 1st Earl of Mansfield (1705–1793), was one of the most influential British judges of the eighteenth century. Born in Scone, Scotland, he rose through the legal profession to become Lord Chief Justice of the King's Bench in 1756, a position he held for over thirty years. His landmark rulings shaped English commercial law, and his judgment in *Somerset v Stewart* (1772) is widely understood to have established that slavery was unsupported by English common law.

Mansfield was also a prominent Whig politician, serving as Solicitor General, Attorney General, and eventually as a member of the Cabinet. He was created Earl of Mansfield in 1776. His London residence was at Bloomsbury Square, and his country seat was at Caen Wood (now Kenwood House) in Hampstead.

## Role in *Barnaby Rudge*

Lord Mansfield is a significant off-stage figure in the Gordon Riots chapters of *Barnaby Rudge*. Dickens depicts him as one of the principal targets of the anti-Catholic mob. The rioters make a deliberate stop at his Bloomsbury Square residence:

> they had stopped before Lord Mansfield's house in Bloomsbury Square. (066-025)

The mob forces entry and ransacks the property, carrying away furniture and valuables before setting the house alight. Dickens notes that the crowd then:

> marched away to Lord Mansfield's country seat at Caen Wood (066-027)

The destruction of Mansfield's property is a recurring reference point throughout the later chapters. In the aftermath of the riots, Mansfield and Lord Saville are mentioned together as figures who had incurred the mob's special enmity (073-003). The ruined house in Bloomsbury Square serves as a visual reminder of the violence, with crowds pressing toward "Lord Mansfield's ruined house" (077-058) even after the riots have been suppressed.

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens's depiction closely follows the historical record. The burning of Mansfield's Bloomsbury Square house and the attack on his Caen Wood estate are well-documented events of the Gordon Riots. Mansfield and his wife narrowly escaped the Bloomsbury Square mob through a back entrance. The novel accurately captures Mansfield's status as a hated figure among the rioters, largely because of his perceived sympathy toward Roman Catholics — a reputation stemming from his rulings against prosecutions of Catholic priests.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

Mansfield represents the authority and established order that the Gordon Riots sought to dismantle. The targeting of his home — the residence of the nation's highest judge — dramatizes the mob's rejection of law itself. In a novel centrally concerned with the thin line between order and chaos, Mansfield's fate serves as a cautionary emblem: if even the Lord Chief Justice cannot protect his own house, no one is safe.
