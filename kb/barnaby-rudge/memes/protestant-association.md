# Protestant Association

## Overview

The Protestant Association -- formally styled the Great Protestant Association of England -- was the political organization at the heart of the anti-Catholic agitation that erupted into the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] of June 1780. Founded in 1779 under the presidency of [[Lord George Gordon]], it campaigned for the repeal of the Catholic Relief Act of 1778, collecting signatures on a "Great Protestant petition" for presentation to [[Parliament]]. In Dickens's novel, the Association serves a dual function: as historical fact, it anchors the narrative in the authentic politics of late-eighteenth-century England; as literary device, it embodies the dangerous alchemy by which religious rhetoric, political ambition, and mob energy combine to produce catastrophic violence. The organization is presented neither as wholly sincere nor as purely cynical, but as a volatile compound whose secret ingredient, as the narrator reveals, was "a secret power ... mustering against the government for undefined and mighty purposes" (037-002).

## Historical Background

The Catholic Relief Act of 1778 removed several penal restrictions that had been imposed on Roman Catholics since the Reformation, allowing them to own and inherit property and to serve in the military upon swearing an oath of [[loyalty|allegiance]]. The Act left in place the prohibition on Catholics educating children in their faith and the disqualification from inheriting real property by "right of purchase or descent," as well as the penalty of "perpetual im[[imprisonment|prison]]ment" for priests who administered sacraments (037-002). Even these limited concessions provoked a fierce nativist backlash among English Protestants, many of whom feared -- or were encouraged to fear -- a return to Catholic domination.

[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]], a former naval officer and Member of [[parliament|Parliament]], emerged as the figurehead of this reaction. In 1779 he founded the Protestant Association, which quickly grew into a national organization with branches across England and Scotland. By the spring of 1780 the Association claimed to have gathered some 44,000 signatures on a petition demanding full restoration of the penal laws. On 2 June 1780, [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] led an estimated 40,000 to 60,000 supporters to the [[parliament|Houses of Parliament]] to present the petition. When [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] voted to adjourn debate, the crowd turned violent, initiating six days of rioting that left over 285 people dead and caused destruction exceeding that which Paris suffered during the entire French Revolution. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] was subsequently tried for treason and acquitted; the Association was dissolved in the aftermath.

## Role in the Novel

The Protestant Association enters the narrative in the novel's middle chapters, where Dickens traces its transformation from political movement to mob machinery. Its recruitment methods are described in one of the novel's most remarkable passages, a sustained periodic sentence in which the narrator anatomizes how fear and propaganda recruited men to a cause they did not understand:

> But when vague rumours got abroad, that in this Protestant association a secret power was mustering against the government for undefined and mighty purposes; when the air was filled with whispers of a confederacy among the Popish powers to degrade and enslave England, establish an inquisition in London, and turn the pens of Smithfield market into stakes and cauldrons; when terrors and alarms which no man understood were perpetually broached, both in and out of Parliament, by one enthusiast who did not understand himself, and bygone bugbears which had lain quietly in their graves for centuries, were raised again to haunt the ignorant and credulous; when all this was done, as it were, in the dark, and secret invitations to join the Great Protestant Association in defence of religion, life, and liberty, were dropped in the public ways, thrust under the house-doors, tossed in at windows, and pressed into the hands of those who trod the streets by night; when they glared from every wall, and shone on every post and pillar, so that stocks and stones appeared infected with the common fear, urging all men to join together blindfold in resistance of they knew not what, they knew not why; -- then the mania spread indeed, and the body, still increasing every day, grew forty thousand strong. (037-002)

This passage distills the Association's method: the deliberate cultivation of ignorance and terror as recruiting tools. Dickens emphasizes that the actual legislative question -- whether to relax the penal laws -- was "so far removed from the business and bosoms of the mass" that it would have attracted only a handful of supporters had it been presented honestly (037-002). The Association grew not through reasoned argument but through the propagation of apocalyptic fantasies about Catholic conspiracies.

The organization's inner workings are exposed in a sequence of chapters set at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn (Chapters 35-39) and later at [[the-boot|The Boot]] tavern in [[london|London]]. At [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], the reader witnesses the relationship between [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] and his manipulative secretary [[Gashford]], who flatters the president's vanity while quietly directing the Association's energies toward violent ends. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]], described as having "a restlessness of thought and purpose, singularly at variance with the studied composure and sobriety of his mien," is shown to be sincere but dangerously susceptible to flattery (035-043). [[gashford|Gashford]], by contrast, is all calculation: "He wore the aspect of a man who was always lying in wait for something that wouldn't come to pass; but he looked patient -- very patient -- and fawned like a spaniel dog" (035-043).

The enrollment of [[Hugh]] the ostler as a member by [[Dennis]] the hangman in Chapter 38 provides a satirical set piece on the Association's recruitment standards. After "asking the candidate a few unimportant questions," the secretary proceeds to enrol [[hugh|Hugh]] as a member, and [[dennis|Dennis]]'s joy is completed by "the announcement that the new member could neither read nor write" -- those arts being, in [[dennis|Dennis]]'s view, "the greatest possible curse a civilised community could know" (038-026). The toast to "[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]], [[lord-george-gordon|President of the Great Protestant Association]]" is followed by an "extemporaneous [[anti-catholicism|No-Popery]] Dance," reducing political commitment to tavern revelry (038-038).

The Association's [[Simon Tappertit|'prentice branch]] is introduced in Chapter 39, where Tappertit, the former locksmith's apprentice, appears as "chief or [[simon-tappertit|captain]] of the Bulldogs" attended by two lieutenants who were once "Prentice Knights." Their "connection with the Protestant Association of England, sanctioned by the name of [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]" arises not from religious conviction but from a desire for "a distinguished state in great political events" (039-003). This branch of the organization channels the frustrated ambition of the apprentice class into political violence.

The Association's true character is most pointedly exposed by the Catholic gentleman Mr. [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]], who confronts [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] and [[gashford|Gashford]] at the [[parliament|Houses of Parliament]]:

> "You have so much of my favour, sir," retorted Mr. Haredale, with a bitter glance at the third party in their conversation, "that I am glad to see you in such good company. You are the essence of your great Association, in yourselves." (043-029)

This judgment -- that the Association's essence is not religious principle but personal malevolence -- echoes the narrator's earlier assessment. The Association provides a respectable facade for cruelty, a political warrant for destruction.

During the riots themselves, Dickens delivers a devastating detail: as one group of rioters destroys a Catholic house, "another man with an equally tender conscience ... harangued the crowd from a pamphlet circulated by the Association, relative to the true principles of Christianity" (066-029). The juxtaposition of the Association's official propaganda with the act of destruction epitomizes Dickens's view of the organization as a machine for converting professed piety into practical savagery.

## Symbols and Rituals

The Protestant Association developed several visible symbols that Dickens deploys for ironic effect:

**The Blue Cockade.** Members wore a blue cockade in their hats as a badge of affiliation. [[john-grueby|John Grueby]], [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s servant, wears one "which he appeared to despise mightily" (035-046). During the riots, "the streets are filled with blue cockades" (050-011), transforming the symbol from a political identifier into a mark of mob membership. The blue cockade appears on hats from [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] to the final military suppression, making it the novel's most persistent visual emblem of the Association (048-002, 048-018, 052-030, 073-004).

**The Protestant Manual.** [[mrs-varden|Mrs. Varden]]'s constant companion, the Protestant Manual, represents the domestic face of the Association's ideology -- the way anti-Catholic sentiment permeated respectable households. She is "five fathoms deep in the Protestant Manual" (019-027) at meals and carries it about as a badge of her piety, though her husband [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] perceptively notes that her Protestantism is more about social performance than genuine faith.

**The Contribution Box.** The Vardens' parlour contains a box "painted in imitation of a very redbrick dwelling-house, with a yellow roof," bearing the inscription "Protestant Association" on its door (041-039). [[mrs-varden|Mrs. Varden]] laments that her husband never contributed anything to it "save once in secret ... two fragments of tobacco-pipe" (041-039). The box literalizes the Association's financial dimension: anti-Catholic agitation as a subscription-funded enterprise.

**"[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" as Shibboleth.** The cry of "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" functions as the Association's unifying slogan, chanted by everyone from [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] himself to [[grip|the raven]] [[grip|Grip]], who learns to croak "I'm a Protestant kettle, [[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]!" (057-008). At the Association's tavern meetings, [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] would "gravely pull off his hat, and say, 'Gentlemen, [[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]. Good day. God bless you'" (037-032). The cry appears chalked on doors during the riots as a protection racket: "chalk '[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]' on your door tomorrow night, and light up your windows" (051-036).

## Significance

The Protestant Association is Dickens's most sustained fictional treatment of organized political [[deception|manipulation]] -- a theme that looks forward to the Chancery suits of *Bleak House* and the Circumlocution Office of *Little Dorrit*. Several thematic threads converge in the Association:

**The Manufacture of Outrage.** Dickens's great periodic sentence in Chapter 37 (037-002) is a masterclass in how political movements are built: not through addressing genuine grievances but through the systematic cultivation of "terrors and alarms which no man understood." The Association demonstrates that "it is not the reality but the perception of threat that mobilises" -- a principle no less relevant to subsequent political history.

**The Alliance of Sincerity and Cynicism.** The Association is held together by an unstable alliance between [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s genuine fanaticism and [[gashford|Gashford]]'s cold [[deception|manipulation]]. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] believes in the cause; [[gashford|Gashford]] uses the cause. This pairing allows Dickens to show that the most dangerous political movements are those in which true believers provide the moral cover for operators who understand exactly what they are doing.

**The Marriage of Politics and Criminality.** The enrollment of [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[dennis|Dennis]] -- an illiterate ostler and a state hangman -- exposes the Association as a mechanism for recruiting social marginals into a structure that legitimizes their worst impulses. The Association does not corrupt these men so much as give them permission. The "extemporaneous [[anti-catholicism|No-Popery]] Dance" (038-038) is a perfect image of how ideology, stripped of content, becomes mere performance: a tavern reel under a political banner.

**Class Displacement.** The 'prentice branch led by Tappertit channels class resentment into sectarian hatred. These young men, denied advancement in the rigid hierarchy of Georgian [[london|London]], find in the Association a stage on which they can play at being soldiers and statesmen. Their anti-Catholicism is a displacement of their frustration with their own social condition.
