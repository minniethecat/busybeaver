## Historical Biography

Brackley Kennett (c. 1714–1782) served as Lord Mayor of London during the tumultuous year of 1780, when the Gordon Riots broke out. A wine merchant by trade, Kennett rose through the City of London's civic hierarchy to become Sheriff in 1765 and eventually Lord Mayor in 1779–1780. His mayoralty was defined and ultimately destroyed by the Gordon Riots.

Historically, Kennett's response to the riots was widely condemned. When the mob attacked Newgate Prison and other buildings, Kennett refused to read the Riot Act or to authorize the military to use lethal force without a magistrate present — and then declined to appear himself. He was later tried for negligence, found guilty, and fined £1,000. His reputation never recovered.

## Role in *Barnaby Rudge*

Kennett appears prominently in the Gordon Riots chapters of *Barnaby Rudge*, where Dickens characterizes him as the embodiment of official timidity and incompetence. The narrator introduces him with damning concision:

> the Lord Mayor, who was the faintest-hearted and most timid of all the magistrates (063-002)

Dickens places Kennett at the center of several key scenes during the riots. When a delegation urges him to take action against the mob, he vacillates and makes excuses:

> "Oh dear me!" cried the Lord Mayor. "God bless my soul—and body—oh dear me!" (061-025)

His characteristic inaction is captured in the image of him standing idle while the city burns:

> Meanwhile the Lord Mayor, with his hands in his pockets, looked on (066-029)

Kennett's cowardice is contrasted with the bravery of ordinary citizens. The novel's early foreshadowing hints at his inadequacy: even before the riots, Sim Tappertit boasts that his planned uprising would "make the Lord Mayor tremble on his throne" (004-021). After the riots, Kennett is summoned before the Privy Council and fades from the narrative (073-061, 075-007).

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens's portrayal of Kennett is notably faithful to the historical record. Contemporary accounts and the subsequent legal proceedings confirm Kennett's paralysis during the crisis. The novelist uses him less as a character needing development and more as a thematic device — a living illustration of how institutional cowardice enables mob violence. The detail of him retreating behind bolted doors (061-030) is drawn from historical testimony.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

Kennett serves a specific thematic function in *Barnaby Rudge*: he demonstrates that the Gordon Riots succeeded not merely because of the mob's ferocity, but because of the authorities' failure. His "hands in his pockets" passivity (066-029) implicates the establishment in the violence. In a novel that examines the thin line between civilization and chaos, Kennett represents what happens when those charged with defending order abdicate their responsibility.
