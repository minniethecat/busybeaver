Grip is [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]'s pet raven, one of Dickens's most famous animal characters. More than comic relief, the raven serves as [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s constant companion, a mirror of his owner's disordered mind, and — as Dickens notes in the Preface — the direct inspiration for Edgar Allan Poe's poem "The Raven."

## Dickens's Preface

In the Preface to the novel, Dickens addresses the raven's origins directly (P03-001–002):

> The raven in this story is a compound of two great originals, of whom I was, at different times, the proud possessor.

He recounts how his own pet ravens exhibited the same mischievous intelligence and capacity for mimicry that characterizes Grip, and notes Poe's admiration for the character (P03-001):

> he expressed his opinion that ravens are gradually becoming extinct

## Companion to Barnaby

Grip is inseparable from [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], travelling in a "little basket" (017-045) at his master's side. The raven's speech — a series of apparently random phrases that often carry unsettling double meanings — mirrors [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s own fragmentary consciousness. When [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] cries "Hurrah!," the raven rep[[deception|lies]] (010-083):

> "Hurrah! Hurrah!" replied the raven, "keep up your spirits. Never say die."

The raven's most famous utterance — "I'm a devil!" (017-081) — is both comic and unnerving, a moment of apparent self-knowledge from a creature positioned between the natural and supernatural worlds.

## Symbolic Function

Throughout the novel, Grip functions as an omen. Other characters are unsettled by his presence, treating him as a supernatural agent. In Chapter 6, he is described as having "perched upon the top of the garden-gate" (006-065) with an air of uncanny watchfulness. The superstition that "that raven's in it" whenever "wickedness" is afoot (006-088) makes Grip a folk-barometer of the novel's moral atmosphere.

Yet Grip is also a source of genuine comfort to [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]. When the riots reach their height, the raven remains with him, a constant in a collapsing world.

## Literary Legacy

The connection between Grip and Poe's "The Raven" (1845) is one of the most famous examples of literary influence in the nineteenth century. Poe reviewed *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* and corresponded with Dickens about the raven's potential as a symbol. Dickens's Preface acknowledges this debt, making Grip not just a character but a bridge between two major literary imaginations.
