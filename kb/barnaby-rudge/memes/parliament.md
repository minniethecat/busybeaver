# Parliament

## Overview

Parliament — the Houses of Commons and Lords sitting at [[westminster|Westminster]] — serves in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as both the institutional stage upon which the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] begin and the symbolic embodiment of the political order that [[mob-violence|the mob]] seeks to overturn. On June 2, 1780, [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] leads a crowd of forty thousand to [[westminster|Westminster]] to present the Great Protestant Petition against the Catholic Relief Act of 1778 (037-002). What begins as an act of political petitioning rapidly degenerates into a siege: members are assaulted in the streets, the lobbies are packed with rioters, and [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] chamber itself becomes a fortress whose defenders — including General Conway and Colonel [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] — draw swords against the prospect of [[mob-violence|the mob]] breaking through its doors.

Dickens uses Parliament to dramatize the collision between legitimate political process and mob coercion. The institution is presented as vulnerable, its members physically threatened, its deliberations drowned out by "the tumult, both within and without" (049-027). Yet Parliament also represents the ultimate resilience of constitutional order: the Riot Act is read outside its walls (049-044), the Crown eventually issues a proclamation authorizing military force (066-002), and by the novel's end, the institution endures.

## Historical Background

The Parliament of 1780 sat in the [[westminster|Palace of Westminster]], a complex that included [[westminster-hall|Westminster Hall]], the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] chamber (then the former St. Stephen's Chapel), and the House of Lords. The buildings were cramped, poorly ventilated, and dangerously accessible to the public — conditions that Dickens exploits to maximum dramatic effect.

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] exposed the physical vulnerability of Parliament in an era without professional policing. The [[westminster|Palace of Westminster]] had no permanent guard force; when [[mob-violence|the mob]] threatened [[house-of-commons|the Commons]], members relied on their own swords, hastily summoned soldiers, and the narrowness of the chamber's entrance as a defensive choke-point (049-037). This institutional fragility was a subject of contemporary commentary and would contribute, decades later, to reforms in [[london|London]]'s policing arrangements.

The petition that [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] brought to Parliament — carried on "a porter's knot through the lobby to the door of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]]" (049-027) — reflected a long tradition of petitioning as a constitutional right. But the scale of the crowd (forty to sixty thousand) and its menacing posture transformed that right into a confrontation between representative government and direct popular coercion.

## Role in the Novel

### The Buildup to Westminster

The march on Parliament is prepared across multiple chapters. In Chapter 37, [[gashford|Gashford]] tells [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] that "about next month, or May, when this Papist relief bill comes before the house," the Association will "walk in procession through the streets — just as an innocent display of strength — and accompanying our petition down to the door of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]]" (037-068). The word "innocent" drips with irony, as the narrative has already shown how the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s membership is composed of "the very scum and refuse of [[london|London]]" (049-026).

In Chapter 38, [[hugh|Hugh]] is taken on a reconnaissance tour of [[westminster|Westminster]], where his guide "pointed out to him significantly the weak parts of the building, how easy it was to get into the lobby, and so to the very door of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]]; and how plainly, when they marched down there in grand array, their roars and shouts would be heard by the members inside" (038-031). [[hugh|Hugh]]'s guide — almost certainly [[gashford|Gashford]] or his agent — also identifies "who some of the Lords and Commons were, by name, as they came in and out; whether they were friendly to the Papists or otherwise" (038-032). This careful intelligence-gathering reveals the organizational planning beneath the riot's apparent spontaneity.

### The Siege of the Commons (Chapter 49)

Chapter 49 contains the novel's most sustained description of Parliament under assault. Members who had not "taken the precaution to be already at their posts" are forced to fight through [[mob-violence|the mob]]:

> Through this vast throng, sprinkled doubtless here and there with honest zealots, but composed for the most part of the very scum and refuse of London, whose growth was fostered by bad criminal laws, bad prison regulations, and the worst conceivable police, such of the members of both Houses of Parliament as had not taken the precaution to be already at their posts, were compelled to fight and force their way. Their carriages were stopped and broken; the wheels wrenched off; the glasses shivered to atoms; the panels beaten in; drivers, footmen, and masters, pulled from their seats and rolled in the mud. Lords, commoners, and reverend bishops, with little distinction of person or party, were kicked and pinched and hustled; passed from hand to hand through various stages of ill-usage; and sent to their fellow-senators at last with their clothes hanging in ribbons about them, their bagwigs torn off, themselves speechless and breathless, and their persons covered with the powder which had been cuffed and beaten out of their hair. (049-026)

Inside [[house-of-commons|the Commons]], matters are worse. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s followers have packed the lobby, and "the members were not only attacked in their passage through the streets, but were set upon within the very walls of Parliament" (049-027). The noise is so overwhelming "that those who attempted to speak could scarcely hear their own voices" (049-027). When the chamber door is opened to admit a member, [[mob-violence|the mob]] grows "more wild and savage, like beasts at the sight of prey, and made a rush against the portal which strained its locks and bolts in their staples, and shook the very beams" (049-027).

### Defenders and Defiance

Two members step forward as Parliament's defenders. General Conway declares: "I am a soldier, you may tell them, and I will protect the freedom of this place with my sword" (049-037). He warns [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] that "there are men within these walls who are determined to defend that pass to the last, and before whom many lives must fall if your adherents persevere" (049-037). Colonel [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] — Lord George's own cousin — delivers an even more personal threat:

> "If a man among this crowd, whose uproar strikes us deaf, crosses the threshold of the House of Commons, I swear to run my sword that moment — not into his, but into your body!" (049-038)

These moments crystallize the novel's central political argument: the distinction between legitimate petition and violent coercion is absolute, and the constitutional order is worth defending by force of arms.

### The Riot Act and Aftermath

Outside Parliament, the military arrives. The Riot Act is read "but not a man stirred" (049-044) — the crowd's defiance of legal authority is complete. The failure of the Riot Act at this juncture marks the transition from political protest to outright insurrection. As the novel progresses, Parliament continues to function: the government assures citizens that "the sympathies of the King, the Administration, and both Houses of Parliament" are with the injured Catholics (066-002), and troops are posted at "[[westminster-hall|Westminster Hall]] and the Houses of Parliament" as defensive positions (067-003).

By the novel's close, Parliament has authorized compensation: a sum is "levied on the hundred of Ossulston, in pursuance of a vote of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]]" (073-003) to reimburse victims of the riots. The restoration of order is enacted through the same parliamentary mechanisms that the rioters sought to overthrow.

## Key Members

- **[[Lord George Gordon]]** — The president of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], who presents the petition in [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] chamber. A sitting MP himself, he uses parliamentary process to attack the institution from within.
- **[[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]** — A member of Parliament who obtained his seat through family influence: "It was quite as good as an Insolvent Act, and infinitely more genteel. So [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] was a member of Parliament" (040-021). His cynical [[deception|manipulation]] of the political system mirrors the [[innocence|corruption]] that the rioters purport to oppose.
- **[[Gashford]]** — [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s secretary, who orchestrates the [[westminster|Westminster]] reconnaissance and manipulates the parliamentary confrontation for his own ends.

## Significance

Parliament in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* functions as a symbol of constitutional order besieged by forces it cannot comprehend or control. The episode traces the boundary between legitimate petition and violent coercion — a boundary that the [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] rioters obliterate when they transform a right of address into a physical assault on the legislature.

The siege of [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] also exposes the fragility of institutional authority in an age before professional police. Dickens, writing in 1841 with the benefit of hindsight, emphasizes the paralysis of the magistrates and the inadequacy of the existing legal machinery. The Commons chamber survives — but only through the personal courage of its armed members and the narrow architecture of its entrance. In the character of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] MP, Parliament is also revealed as an institution of privilege and [[innocence|corruption]], where seats are purchased and influence is traded "infinitely more genteel" than a debtor's [[imprisonment|prison]] (040-021).

Thematically, Parliament stands opposite the [[mob-violence]] it faces. Where [[mob-violence|the mob]] acts with "the fury of a mad monster" (049-026), Parliament stands for deliberation, law, and constitutional process — however imperfectly realized. The conflict between these two forces is the central political drama of the novel.
