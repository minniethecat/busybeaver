## Definition

Disguise in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* functions as both a gothic plot device and a metaphor for the hidden truths that structure the novel's world. The central instance — [[barnaby-rudge-senior|Barnaby Rudge Senior]]'s twenty-seven-year [[deception|concealment]] as a beggar at the [[the-warren|Warren]] — is the mechanism by which the novel's originating [[guilt|crime]] remains hidden, and its eventual exposure coincides with the collapse of social order during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. Beyond this master-[[deception|disguise]], Dickens populates the novel with acts of [[deception|concealment]] ranging from [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]]'s secret double life as a 'Prentice Knight to the rioters' adoption of masquerade as a tool of destruction.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Long Disguise: Barnaby Rudge Senior

The novel's foundational secret is sustained by physical [[deception|disguise]]. [[barnaby-rudge-senior|Barnaby Rudge Senior]], having murdered [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]], conceals himself as a servant and gardener at the Warren for twenty-seven years. The [[deception|disguise]] is so complete that even [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]], who has spent his life seeking the murderer, does not recognize the man living under his roof. The gardener's face registers "drops, like dews of agony and death" (002-052), visible only to the reader — a gothic technique whereby [[deception|disguise]] conceals identity from other characters while revealing it to the audience.

### The Gentleman in Disguise

The novel's other [[deception|disguise]] episodes extend the motif into the social realm. [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]]'s secret identity as [[simon-tappertit|captain]] of the 'Prentice Knights requires a "courtly dress" kept hidden from his master [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] — a [[deception|disguise]] that literalizes his social ambition (008-040). During the riots, the erasure of [[deception|disguise]] becomes a mark of total social breakdown: "all [[deception|concealment]] and [[deception|disguise]] were laid aside, and they performed the usual functions of a crowd" (063-003). Women [[deception|disguise]]d in men's attire participate in the violence (063-005), and [[hugh|Hugh]] himself moves through the crowd "[[deception|disguise]]d as one of them" (067-033). In each case, [[deception|disguise]] enables transgression — of class, of gender, of law — and its abandonment signals the riot's descent into unmasked chaos.

### The Gentleman's Mask

Social [[deception|disguise]] extends beyond physical costume. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] practices a form of permanent [[deception|disguise]] through manner, his polished exterior concealing a ruthless interior. When a character remarks "You're a gentleman in [[deception|disguise]]" (031-026), the observation cuts both ways: Chester is a gentleman by appearance, but his [[social-class|gentility]] masks the true nature of a man who has abandoned his own son.
