## Overview

The Fleet Prison was one of London's principal debtors' prisons, located on the eastern side of Farringdon Street, near the Fleet River from which it took its name. Established in the twelfth century, by the time of *Barnaby Rudge* it was a dilapidated and overcrowded institution whose inmates survived by begging from passersby through the prison grates. The prison was demolished in 1844, just three years after the novel's publication.

## In the Novel

The Fleet Prison appears in *Barnaby Rudge* primarily as one of the prisons attacked during the Gordon Riots. On the night of 7 June 1780, the rioters forced open its gates along with those of the King's Bench Prison:

> "the gates of the King's Bench and Fleet Prisons being opened at the usual hours" (067-004)

The prison was targeted not for any political grievance but as part of the general breakdown of order — the mob, having begun with anti-Catholic targets, had by this point turned to destroying all instruments of authority. The liberation of the Fleet's debtors contributed to the atmosphere of chaos and lawlessness that characterized the riots' final phase.

## Other References

The Fleet appears tangentially in the novel through other references to the Fleet Market vicinity:

- The area around Fleet Market was a known haunt of the criminal classes (060-006, 060-008)
- Fleet Street, adjacent to the prison, was a major thoroughfare where mobs gathered during the riots (049-051)
- Sir John Chester jests darkly about marrying his son at the Fleet — a reference to the clandestine "Fleet marriages" performed by imprisoned clergymen (008-049)

## Historical Context

In the eighteenth century, the Fleet held primarily debtors who could not pay their creditors. Unlike Newgate, which housed felons awaiting trial or execution, the Fleet's population consisted of civil prisoners. Conditions were notoriously harsh: prisoners were charged fees for their own incarceration, and those unable to pay were confined to the "Common Side" in conditions of extreme squalor.

The opening of the Fleet during the Gordon Riots was part of a wider pattern — Newgate, the King's Bench, the New Bridewell, and the Clink were all forced open during the same disturbances (067-012). The mass liberation of prisoners across London constituted the most complete breakdown of criminal justice in the city's history before the nineteenth century.
