## Definition

Social class in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is not merely background but an active force that shapes character, plot, and the novel's political argument. Dickens presents a society rigidly stratified yet fundamentally unstable: the gentry ([[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]]), the respectable tradesman class ([[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]), and the urban poor who become the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]' foot soldiers. The novel traces how class resentment, long suppressed, finds violent release — and how the disruption of class hierarchy during the riots reveals the fragility of the social order.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Gentry and Their Discontents

The novel's two representative gentlemen embody opposed versions of aristocracy. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] is the cynical aristocrat who uses charm as a weapon, observing that class feeling "ran so high among all classes" (016-005) as a curious social phenomenon rather than a moral crisis. [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]], by contrast, belongs to the Catholic gentry — "men of ancient houses" who have "no privilege of exemption" from the cruelties visited upon their faith (040-021). Chester articulates the ruling-class viewpoint explicitly: "these are natural class distinctions, depend upon it" (026-025) — a statement the novel's events systematically undermine.

### The Tradesman's World

[[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], [[gabriel-varden|the locksmith]], represents the novel's ideal of the respectable tradesman — industrious, honest, and content within his station. Yet even Varden's household is riven by class anxiety: his wife [[mrs-varden|Mrs. Varden]] and her maid [[miss-miggs|Miggs]] embody the aspirational dimension of class, their pretensions to gentility a source of both comedy and discord. The Varden household's internal class drama mirrors the larger social conflict, showing that class is not only a public structure but a private obsession.

### Poverty and the Mob

The novel's treatment of poverty is unsparing. In the great crowd scenes, Dickens shows "poverty, by ignorance, by the love of" destruction driving the rioters forward (053-002). The mob's violence is partly political — anti-Catholic — but Dickens insists it is also economic: a "troublesome class of persons who, having an incurable dissatisfaction" with their lot, seize any pretext for destruction (030-001). The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] are, in this reading, a class war by other means.
