Gashford is [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s private secretary and one of the principal antagonists of *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*. Beneath a facade of pious humility, he is a cunning manipulator who quietly stokes the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] while keeping his own hands clean. Unlike [[mob-violence|the mob]] leaders who act openly, Gashford operates through whispers, insinuation, and strategic silence, making him arguably the novel's most dangerous figure.

## Character and Appearance

Gashford is introduced in Chapter 35 as [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s travelling companion on the road to [[london|London]]. Dickens provides a sharp physical description (035-043):

> Gashford, the secretary, was taller, angularly made, high-shouldered, bony, and ungraceful.

His manner is obsequious toward his patron — constantly addressing him as "my lord" and adopting a "whining" tone — yet his careful glances and silences reveal a colder intelligence at work. He flatters Lord George's vanity while quietly steering him toward destructive ends.

## Role in the Gordon Riots

Gashford serves as the hidden engineer of the riots. While Lord George provides the public face and oratory, Gashford manages the practical machinery: correspondence, coordination with local Protestant associations, and the subtle encouragement of violence. In Chapter 36, he reads aloud the list of member societies with evident satisfaction (036-024):

> "President," said Gashford, reading, "Mr. Simon Tappertit."

His management of the mayoral petition in Chapter 37 shows his tactical skill — he orchestrates the timing, the crowd, and the message while allowing Lord George to believe the momentum is spontaneous.

When Lord George expresses [[suspicion|doubt]] or exhaustion, Gashford refills his glass and renews his resolve, as in their fireside conversation after the first day's march (035-047):

> "So ends, my lord," said Gashford, filling his glass with great assiduity.

## Relationship with Lord George Gordon

The dynamic between Gashford and Lord George is central to understanding both characters. Lord George is sincere in his anti-Catholic convictions but weak-willed and susceptible to flattery. Gashford exploits this mercilessly (035-065):

> "Does my lord ask me," whined Gashford, drawing his chair nearer with an injured air.

Gashford's method is never to contradict but to amplify — he mirrors Lord George's grandiosity while ensuring that every decision leads toward violence. [[john-grueby|John Grueby]], Lord George's loyal servant, sees through Gashford immediately and warns him (035-089):

> "I know you; you're the man that keeps him here in this state — you're the man that blows the fire in him."

## Moral Character

Gashford is Dickens's study of the manipulator who hides behind a cause. He has no genuine religious conviction — the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] is merely a vehicle for his ambition. His cowardice is exposed when danger approaches: at the first sign of trouble, "Gashford had vanished long ago" (035-090).

In Chapter 36, his private soliloquy after closing the window reveals his true nature (036-052):

> "More seed, more seed," said Gashford as he closed the window. "When will the harvest come!"

This agricultural metaphor for sowing chaos confirms that Gashford views the riots not as a religious crusade but as a crop to be cultivated for his own purposes.
