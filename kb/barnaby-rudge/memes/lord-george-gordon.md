# Lord George Gordon

## Historical Biography

Lord George Gordon (26 December 1751 -- 1 November 1793) was a British politician and controversialist, the third and youngest son of Cosmo George Gordon, 3rd Duke of Gordon. Educated at Eton, he entered the Royal Navy as a midshipman before resigning his commission in 1772. He was elected Member of [[parliament|Parliament]] for Ludgershall in 1774, serving until 1780. Though a minor figure in [[parliament|Parliament]] -- described by contemporaries as erratic and something of a c[[social-class|rank]] -- his name became synonymous with the worst civil disturbance in [[london|London]]'s history before the Blitz: the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] of June 1780.

The riots had their origin in the Catholic Relief Act of 1778, a moderate reform that removed some legal disabilities from Roman Catholics willing to swear an oath of [[loyalty|allegiance]] to the Crown. Gordon, a fervent Protestant, founded the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] and launched a mass petition campaign against the Act. Anti-Catholic feeling, stoked by centuries of penal laws and popular prejudice, proved a potent force. The peaceful petition campaign Gordon intended was overtaken by [[mob-violence|the mob]] he had helped mobilize. On 2 June 1780, an estimated forty to sixty thousand people marched on [[parliament|Parliament]]. Over the following six days, [[london|London]] descended into chaos: Roman Catholic chapels were burned, the houses of prominent Catholics and government ministers were attacked, [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] was stormed and destroyed, and the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] was nearly overrun. The army was called out; more than 285 rioters were killed and over 450 arrested.

Gordon himself was arrested on 9 June 1780 on a charge of High Treason. He was im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed in [[tower-of-london|the Tower]] of [[london|London]] and tried at [[parliament|Westminster]] on 5 February 1781. After a patient investigation, he was acquitted -- the prosecution could not prove he had summoned the crowd with traitorous intent. Yet his political career was destroyed. He drifted from cause to cause in the years that followed, and in 1787, in a remarkable personal transformation, he converted to Judaism in Birmingham, taking the name Yisrael bar Avraham Avinu. He lived as an observant Jew for the remaining years of his life. In 1788 he was convicted of libelling Marie Antoinette and the French ambassador and was im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed in [[imprisonment|Newgate]], where he died of gaol fever (typhus) on 1 November 1793.

## Role in Barnaby Rudge

Lord George Gordon enters the novel in Chapter 35 as the president of the [[protestant-association|Great Protestant Association]], established overnight at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn in [[chigwell|Chigwell]], the setting to which Dickens returns him for the introduction of his character's personal habits and physical peculiarities. The narrative voice that accompanies his first appearance in Chapter 37 is one of the most remarkable set-pieces in all of Dickens -- a sustained diagnostic of how "false priests, false prophets, false doctors, false patriots, false prodigies of every kind" exploit mystery and popular credulity (037-001). Gordon is neither villain nor hero. Dickens presents him as a well-meaning but naive man whose "disordered brain" (037-003) has been captured by an idea larger than his understanding, and whose "weakness had been goaded and urged on by so many for their own purposes" (073-063).

Gordon's physical presence in the novel is rendered with Dickens's characteristic grotesque precision. He is introduced at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]]:

> Sitting bolt upright upon his bony steed, with his long, straight hair, dangling about his face and fluttering in the wind; his limbs all angular and rigid, his elbows stuck out on either side ungracefully, and his whole frame jogged and shaken at every motion of his horse's feet; a more grotesque or more ungainly figure can hardly be conceived. In lieu of whip, he carried in his hand a great gold-headed cane, as large as any footman carries in these days, and his various modes of holding this unwieldy weapon -- now upright before his face like the sabre of a horse-soldier, now over his shoulder like a musket, now between his finger and thumb, but always in some uncouth and awkward fashion -- contributed in no small degree to the absurdity of his appearance. (037-028)

The description is at once comic and oddly affectionate. Gordon is "stiff, lank, and solemn" (037-028), a figure who "might have moved the sternest looker-on to laughter" (037-028) -- yet he is also entirely genuine in his convictions. He is "quite unconscious of the effect he produced" (037-028), and this lack of self-awareness is the key to his character. When passing [[london|London]]ers cry "Hurrah Geordie! [[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]!" he "would gravely pull off his hat, and bow" (037-028). There is no guile in him; he is the sincere fanatic, and it is precisely his sincerity that makes him dangerous.

The central dramatic irony of Gordon's role is that he is manipulated by his secretary, [[gashford|Gashford]]. [[gashford|Gashford]] is a Mephistophelean figure who whispers suggestions and flattery while steering Gordon toward catastrophe. Gordon's own perception of his cause and its righteousness never falters, even as [[mob-violence|the mob]] he has called into being goes about its work of destruction. He leads the march to [[parliament|Westminster]] with the petition on 2 June (048-043, 049-002), taking his post at the head of the crowd with "[[gashford|Gashford]] at his right hand, and sundry ruffians, of most unpromising appearance, forming a kind of staff about him" (049-002). The mob sings hymns and nonsense songs under his very nose, "quite unconscious of their burden" (048-050) in his obliviousness.

His arrest, im[[imprisonment|prison]]ment, [[justice-and-punishment|trial]], and acquittal form the coda of the novel's historical narrative. Dickens gives us the image that closes Gordon's arc:

> And in the Tower, in a dreary room whose thick stone walls shut out the hum of life, and made a stillness which the records left by former prisoners with those silent witnesses seemed to deepen and intensify; remorseful for every act that had been done by every man among the cruel crowd; feeling for the time their guilt his own, and their lives put in peril by himself; and finding, amidst such reflections, little comfort in fanaticism, or in his fancied call; sat the unhappy author of all -- Lord George Gordon. (073-063)

This is one of the most powerful passages in the novel: Gordon, alone, abandoned by the forty thousand who once cheered him, no longer sustained by the fanaticism that had given his life purpose. Dickens's final report, in Chapter 82, notes his acquittal at [[parliament|Westminster]] on 5 February 1781 and the grim irony that "so many people were there, still, to whom those riots taught no lesson of reproof or moderation, that a public subscription was set on foot in Scotland to defray the cost of his defence" (082-004).

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens's Lord George Gordon is a remarkably faithful portrait in its broad outlines, but the novelist exercises considerable creative liberty in matters of psychological shading and narrative function.

**What Dickens gets right.** The basic facts are accurate. Gordon was indeed the younger son of a duke, a naval officer turned MP, president of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], and the figurehead of the petition campaign that ignited the riots. He was arrested, tried for High Treason, and acquitted. Dickens's preface to the novel attests that his account is "substantially correct" as history (P03-005). The description of the petition march (049-002), the storming of [[imprisonment|Newgate]], the attack on the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]], and the eventual military suppression of the riots all follow the historical record.

**What Dickens invents or reshapes.** The character of [[gashford|Gashford]], Gordon's manipulative secretary, is a composite fiction. While Gordon did have associates who encouraged his zealotry, the particular relationship of master and tempter -- with [[gashford|Gashford]] as a silky hypocrite who whispers flattery while nursing private contempt -- is a Dickensian dramatization. Historically, Gordon was not the naive, easily led man he appears in the novel; contemporary accounts suggest a more complex figure, at once charismatic and unstable, capable of genuinely commanding loyalties that Dickens attributes entirely to the atmosphere of fear and rumor described in Chapter 37.

The novel also omits Gordon's later conversion to Judaism, which occurred in 1787. At the time Dickens wrote _[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]_ (serialized in _[[barnaby-rudge-novel|Master Humphrey's Clock]]_, 1841), this was well-known historical fact. Its omission is significant: the novel's thematic architecture treats Protestantism and its associated prejudices as a force that enables violence, and adding Gordon's Jewish period might have complicated this framework. Intriguingly, however, Dickens plants a strange prophetic seed: on his first morning in the novel, Gordon tells [[gashford|Gashford]], "I dreamed that we were Jews, [[gashford|Gashford]]. You and I -- both of us -- Jews with long beards" (037-011). [[gashford|Gashford]] recoils in pious horror, but the historical reader knows the dream is premonitory.

Finally, Dickens's Gordon lacks the genuine political acumen that the historical Gordon possessed. Historically, Gordon was an effective populist organizer. In the novel, the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s growth is attributed to irrational fear and mysterious handbills -- "they glared from every wall, and shone on every post and pillar, so that stocks and stones appeared infected with the common fear" (037-002) -- with little agency attributed to Gordon himself.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

Lord George Gordon serves at least four interconnected thematic functions in _[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]_.

**First, he embodies the danger of sincere zealotry.** Unlike the novel's outright villains -- [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], [[gashford|Gashford]], [[dennis|Dennis]] -- Gordon is not malicious. He genuinely believes in his cause. Yet it is this very sincerity that makes him destructive: "If ever man believed with his whole heart and soul that he was engaged in a just cause, and that he was bound to stand by his leader to the last, poor [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] believed it of himself and Lord George Gordon" (049-045). Dickens's point is that sincerity is no guarantor of goodness; a man who believes absolutely in a wrong idea is more dangerous than a cynic, because he carries conviction with him.

**Second, he is a study of the leader who does not lead.** Gordon is nominally the president of the Association, but [[mob-violence|the mob]] he calls into being quickly escapes his control. This is a central theme of the novel: the forces that demagogues unleash have their own momentum, and the man at the front is as likely to be swept away as to direct events. Gordon "quickened his pace" (048-043) as the crowd swelled around him, but he never commands it -- he rides at its head as a figurehead, not a general.

**Third, he is a vehicle for one of Dickens's most sustained and devastating analyses of mass psychology.** Chapter 37's opening paragraph (037-001) is a thesis statement on how mystery and fear can transform "a cracked-brained member of the lower house, who attacked all parties and sided with none" (037-003) into the focus of a national mania. The mechanism is not reason but contagion: handbills "thrust under the house-doors, tossed in at windows" (037-002), rumors of secret armies, whispers of a Popish plot. Gordon is the empty center around which these fears cohere.

**Fourth, his arc provides the novel's moral reckoning.** The image of Gordon alone in [[tower-of-london|the Tower]], "[[guilt|remorse]]ful for every act that had been done by every man among the cruel crowd" (073-063), is the closest the novel comes to a direct statement of [[guilt|culpability]]. Dickens does not let Gordon off -- the word "unhappy" is freighted with judgment as well as [[sympathy|pity]] -- but he also shows us the machinery by which a well-intentioned man became "the unhappy author of all" (073-063). It is a more complex moral position than simple condemnation or exoneration, and it speaks to the novel's deepest concern: the ease with which ordinary prejudice, given the right conditions, can metastasize into catastrophic violence.
