## Overview

The House of Commons is the political centre of gravity in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* — the chamber where the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s petition is laid, where the fate of Catholic relief is decided, and to whose doors [[mob-violence|the mob]] marches in the tens of thousands. Dickens uses the Commons as a spatial and symbolic anchor for the novel's political narrative: every procession, every cry of "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]," every act of mob violence ultimately converges on the chamber that "overlooked the river" (048-009).

The House of Commons in the novel is both a physical place — a specific building with doors, lobbies, and a chamber — and an idea: the seat of legitimate political authority that [[mob-violence|the mob]], in its swelling numbers, threatens to overwhelm. The rioters' march to the Commons is the novel's key image of the collision between popular fury and constitutional order.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens does not describe the Commons chamber in architectural detail; his interest is in its doors and thresholds — the points of contact between the institution and [[mob-violence|the mob]]. The Commons is rendered through the pressure exerted upon it from outside:

> He pointed to where the House of Commons overlooked the river, "that'll be on fire next." (048-009)

The prediction — made by one of the riot's leaders — frames the Commons as [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s ultimate target. The building's position on the river is noted precisely because it makes it vulnerable: a landmark visible from the bridges, accessible by water.

The approach to the Commons is rendered in terms of military manoeuvre:

> Make for the House of Commons in separate detachments. (049-001)

This is not a crowd assembling; it is an army deploying. The language — "separate detachments" — is deliberately military, and the Commons becomes a fortress under siege. Dickens tracks [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s advance through the Commons' physical geography:

> The lobby to the door of the House of Commons, where it was received by two magistrates. (049-027)

The lobby is the threshold between the street and the chamber — the narrow space where [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s momentum meets the thin line of constituted authority. Two magistrates, standing in a lobby, facing thousands: the image crystallises the disproportion between the force of the crowd and the resources of the state.

The most charged moment comes when a member threatens violence on the floor of the chamber itself:

> If any man crosses the threshold of the House of Commons, I swear to run my sword that moment through his body. (049-038)

The threat — made within the Commons chamber, the very space of parliamentary privilege — marks the collapse of the distinction between political debate and physical violence. The Commons, designed as a space where words replace swords, becomes a place where swords are brandished.

After the riots, the Commons continues to appear in the novel's aftermath:

> The House of Commons, sitting on Tuesday with locked doors. (073-004)

The locked doors — a detail Dickens records without comment — are the Commons' lasting memorial to the riots. The chamber that [[mob-violence|the mob]] sought to force open now sits behind barred entrances, a fortress of parliamentary procedure that has weathered the storm but bears its marks.

## Role in the Narrative

The House of Commons serves as the destination toward which the novel's political action tends. The story's central political event — the presentation of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s petition against Catholic relief — takes place at the Commons, and all the mass mobilisation of the early riot chapters (37–49) is directed toward this single point.

The Commons first appears in the novel's paratext, where the impeachment of [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] is referenced (P03-010). This framing establishes the Commons as the arena in which the novel's political stakes will be decided. When the action reaches the Commons in the narrative present, it does so through the voice of [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] himself:

> The petition down to the door of the House of Commons. (037-068)

The petition's journey — from the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s rallying point in St. George's Fields, across the bridges, through the streets, to the very door of the Commons — is the spatial arc of the novel's political plot. The Commons door is the destination; everything after it passes is riot, not petition.

The climax at the Commons comes in Chapter 49, where [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s assault on the chamber's doors and the military's defence of the lobby create the novel's most sustained image of institutional siege. The passage through which [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] enters the Commons (043-036) and the moment when the magistrates receive [[mob-violence|the mob]] at the lobby door (049-027) bracket the crisis: [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] moves between the chamber and the crowd, translating parliamentary language into mob action and mob demands into political threats.

In the novel's aftermath, the Commons passes the vote that compensates the victims (073-003–004). The institution that [[mob-violence|the mob]] sought to destroy becomes the instrument of restitution — a return to constitutional order that Dickens presents without irony.

## Historical Basis

The House of Commons at the [[westminster|Palace of Westminster]] was, in 1780, the same building that had housed [[parliament|Parliament]] since the Middle Ages — a warren of chambers, lobbies, and corridors on the bank of the Thames. The historical [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] did indeed converge on the Commons: [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] presented the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s petition there on 2 June 1780, and the subsequent days saw the largest mobilisation of troops in [[london|London]] since the Civil War, much of it concentrated around [[westminster|Westminster]].

The locked doors that Dickens records (073-004) are a historical detail: after the riots, the Commons did sit with its doors locked as a security measure, and the practice continued for some years. The vote to compensate the victims of the riots, passed by the Commons in the aftermath, was a genuine parliamentary measure — one of the largest compensation schemes of the eighteenth century.

Dickens's account of [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s assault on the Commons is substantially faithful to the historical record, including the detail of magistrates receiving the crowd at the lobby door and members of [[parliament|Parliament]] threatening to use their swords in defence of the chamber.
