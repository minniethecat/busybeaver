## Overview

Newgate Street is the thoroughfare that runs eastward from [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] into the heart of the [[london|City of London]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it appears in two contexts — as the quarter of the [[imprisonment|prison]] where the condemned rioters await [[justice-and-punishment|execution]], and as one of the streets through which the crowd fled the military's gunfire on the decisive night of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]].

## Description in the Novel

Dickens gives Newgate Street no physical description; it is defined entirely by its relationship to the [[imprisonment|prison]] that gives it its name. Its character in the novel is shaped by the sounds and movements that pass through it — the cries of condemned men, the stampede of a fleeing crowd.

## Role in the Narrative

Newgate Street is first mentioned in connection with the [[imprisonment|prison]]ers awaiting [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] after the riots. The sounds emerging from the [[imprisonment|prison]] carry a grim geography:

> It was remarkable that these cries began in that quarter of the jail which fronted Newgate Street, where, it was well known, the men who were to suffer death on Thursday were confined. (064-044)

The precision of the detail — the cries come specifically from the quarter of the jail "which fronted Newgate Street" — maps the interior of [[newgate|Newgate]] onto its external geography. Newgate Street becomes the boundary between the living city and its condemned, the street through which the sounds of the death-row cells filter out to the world beyond.

The street reappears during the military suppression of the riots, when the crowd attempting to flee the City is channelled through its narrow length:

> At Holborn Bridge, and on Holborn Hill, the confusion was greater than in any other part; for the crowd that poured out of the city in two great streams, one by Ludgate Hill, and one by Newgate Street, united at that spot, and formed a mass so dense, that at every volley the people seemed to fall in heaps. (067-014)

Newgate Street serves as one of two arterial escape routes from the City during the military crackdown. The image of the crowd pouring out of the City "in two great streams" — one flowing down [[ludgate-hill|Ludgate Hill]], the other down Newgate Street — captures the hydraulic imagery that Dickens uses throughout the riot chapters: [[mob-violence|the mob]] as a fluid mass, channelled by the ancient street-plan of [[london|London]], converging at [[holborn|Holborn]] into a fatal density where the soldiers' volleys find their target.
