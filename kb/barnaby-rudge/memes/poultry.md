## Overview

The Poultry is a thoroughfare in the [[london|City of London]], running eastward from [[cheapside|Cheapside]] towards the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] and [[cornhill|Cornhill]]. It appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as one of the key defensive points where the military confronted the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Rioters]] on the most violent night of the riots — the scene of the first volley of musketry that killed nearly a score of people.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens does not give the Poultry a physical description in the novel; it is named as a landmark street in the [[london|City of London]], defined by its strategic position at a major crossroads and by its role as a point of military defence. Its significance emerges entirely from the events of the riots:

> In the Poultry, and on Cornhill, and at several other leading points, iron chains were drawn across the street; parties of soldiers were distributed in some of the old city churches while it was yet dark; and in several private houses (among them, Lord Rockingham's in Grosvenor Square); which were blockaded as though to sustain a siege, and had guns pointed from the windows. (067-003)

The military preparations — iron chains drawn across the street, soldiers concealed in churches, private houses fortified — transform the City's familiar commercial thoroughfares into a militarised zone.

## Role in the Narrative

The Poultry serves as one of the critical battlegrounds of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. It is here that the soldiers first open fire on the crowd:

> The firing began in the Poultry, where the chain was drawn across the road, where nearly a score of people were killed on the first discharge. (067-012)

This moment marks the decisive turn in the riots — the state's lethal response. The chain drawn across the road becomes a deadly bottleneck: the crowd, advancing along the Poultry, is met not with negotiation but with musket-fire. The tactical geography of the street — narrow enough to be closed by a chain, but wide enough to channel a large crowd into a killing zone — makes it the flashpoint where civil disorder becomes massacre.

The Poultry is also one of the ear[[deception|lies]]t locations mentioned in the novel, as a reference point for measuring distances from [[london|London]]:

> In the year 1775, there stood upon the borders of Epping Forest, at a distance of about twelve miles from London — measuring from the Standard in Cornhill, or rather from the spot on or near to which the Standard used to be in days of yore — a house of public entertainment called the Maypole. (001-001)

The Standard in [[cornhill|Cornhill]] — adjacent to the Poultry — was the traditional datum from which distances from [[london|London]] were measured, establishing the Poultry as part of the geographical centre of the City.
