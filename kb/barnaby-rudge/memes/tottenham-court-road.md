## Overview

Tottenham Court Road appears in the novel as part of the route taken by [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]] and [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] as they travelled northward from [[parliament|Parliament]] Street past St Martin's church and St Giles's. On its western side lay a place then called the Green Lanes — a retired and neglected spot leading into the fields (044-004). The detailed description of this area provides one of Dickens's small but vivid sketches of the transitional landscape between Georgian [[london|London]] and the countryside beyond.

## Description

The Green Lanes, situated at the back of Tottenham Court Road's western side, is depicted as a gloomy, semi-derelict space. Dickens catalogues its features with characteristic specificity: great heaps of ashes, stagnant pools overgrown with [[social-class|rank]] grass and duckweed, broken turnstiles, and the upright posts of palings long since carried off for firewood — a landscape of abandonment on the city's edge.

> They went up Parliament Street, past Saint Martin's church, and away by Saint Giles's to Tottenham Court Road, at the back of which, upon the western side, was then a place called the Green Lanes. This was a retired spot, not of the choicest kind, leading into the fields. Great heaps of ashes; stagnant pools, overgrown with rank grass and duckweed; broken turnstiles; and the upright posts of palings long since carried off for firewood, which menaced all heedless walkers with their jagged and rusty nails. (044-004)
