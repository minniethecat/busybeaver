## Overview

The Old Bailey is the Central Criminal Court of England, located on Old Bailey Street in the [[london|City of London]], adjacent to [[newgate|Newgate Prison]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it is the professional domain of [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] and the site where the captured [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] Rioters face justice.

## Description

Dickens mentions the Old Bailey in connection with [[dennis|Dennis]], whose familiarity with the court underscores his grim trade as public [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]er. The proximity of the court to [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] — condemned [[imprisonment|prison]]ers were led directly from their cells to the dock — makes the Old Bailey a node in the novel's geography of [[guilt|crime]] and punishment.

> …they happened to be near the Old Bailey, and Mr. Dennis knew there were certain prisoners to be tried whose cases might require his professional services. (039-064)

A later ironic passage connects the preservation of the Old Bailey to the broader theme of legal institutions standing firm against chaos:

> …the main object of preserving the Old Bailey in all its purity, and the gallows in all its pristine usefulness. (070-003)

## Role in the Narrative

The Old Bailey appears as a functional backdrop to [[dennis|Dennis]]'s character (039-064) — the hangman's knowledge of its calendar and [[imprisonment|prison]]ers establishes his institutional authority and moral vacancy. The court represents the legal machinery that will ultimately process the rioters after the violence subsides.

In the novel's satirical register, the Old Bailey serves Dickens's critique of a legal system that preserves its forms of punishment even as the city burns around it. The phrase "preserving the Old Bailey in all its [[innocence|purity]]" (070-003) carries a double edge, mocking the self-regard of institutions that dispense death.

## Characters Associated

## Historical Basis

The Old Bailey sessions house has stood on the street of the same name since the medieval period. In 1780, during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], the Old Bailey stood just north of [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] — the two buildings were physically connected. After the riots, many of the captured participants were tried here. The courtroom that [[dennis|Dennis]] would have known was destroyed in the 1780 [[burning-of-newgate|Newgate fire]] (set by the rioters), and a new sessions house was built in its place.
