## Definition

Revenge in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is the novel's most powerful narrative engine. The gothic subplot — a murder committed decades before the main action — generates consequences that ripple through two generations, culminating in a series of violent deaths that read as [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]] rather than mere accident. Dickens structures revenge not as simple eye-for-eye justice but as a providential force that exceeds any individual's intention: the avenger may not know what he avenges, yet the pattern is unmistakable. The murder of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] by his own illegitimate son [[hugh|Hugh]] is the novel's supreme example of revenge as cosmic irony.

## Historical Context

The 18th-century gothic novel, from Walpole's *The Castle of Otranto* (1764) to Radcliffe and Lewis, established revenge as the genre's central motor — the [[guilt|crime]] that generates the haunting, the secret that demands exposure. Dickens adapts gothic conventions to the historical novel, grounding the revenge plot in the specific political crisis of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] and making the riot itself the mechanism by which hidden [[guilt|crime]]s are brought to light. The preface's emphasis on [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s under the "Bloody Code" (P03-011) establishes the novel's interest in the boundary between judicial punishment and private vengeance.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Haredale Murder as Unfinished Business

The murder of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]] by [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] Senior is the novel's originating [[guilt|crime]]. For over twenty years, the murderer is concealed in the Warren, and the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] family's knowledge of the [[guilt|crime]] is suppressed. The secret is kept by [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] out of a desperate, misguided loyalty — but the weight of concealment is itself a slow-burning revenge. When the murderer at last confronts his wife, he articulates the threat in its purest form:

> "Observe," he whispered in the widow's ear: "In him, of whose existence I was ignorant until tonight, I have you in my power. Be careful how you use me. Be careful how you use me. I am destitute and starving, and a wanderer upon the earth. I may take a sure and slow revenge." (017-100)

"Slow revenge" — the phrase captures the novel's understanding of vengeance as a force that works across decades, indifferent to the intentions of those who set it in motion.

### Hugh as Unwitting Avenger

[[hugh|Hugh]]'s murder of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] is the novel's most dramatically concentrated act of revenge — and [[hugh|Hugh]] does not know he is avenging his own abandonment. He kills a man whose identity as his father is revealed only moments before the [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]. The scene collapses the distinction between revenge (motivated, personal) and [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]] (providential, impersonal): [[hugh|Hugh]] acts from rage, but the result is cosmic justice. His words to [[gashford|Gashford]] reveal the personal fuel behind his public violence:

> "I'd do anything to have some revenge on him — anything. And when you told me that he and all the Catholics would suffer from those who joined together under that handbill, I said I'd make one of 'em, if their master was the devil himself." (040-041)

This is the novel's psychology of revenge in miniature: abstract grievances attach themselves to concrete targets, and political violence is always, at some level, personal.

### The Hangman's Irony

[[dennis|Dennis]], who spent his career as the instrument of judicial [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]], dies in a grotesque parody of his own profession. The hangman who hanged others is, in effect, hanged by [[mob-violence|the mob]] — a revenge that is also a judgment on the system he served. Dickens's treatment of [[dennis|Dennis]]'s death is darkly comic, underscoring the novel's suggestion that [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]] is always, at some level, ironic.

## Key Characters

- **[[Geoffrey Haredale]]** — The surviving brother whose life is defined by the unresolved murder of [[reuben-haredale|Reuben Haredale]]. His pursuit of the truth is partly justice-seeking, partly revenge.
- **[[barnaby-rudge-senior]]** — The murderer who hides for decades, only to be exposed by the chaos of the riots — revenge delayed, not denied.
- **[[Hugh]]** — The bastard son who kills his father without knowing it, transforming accidental violence into providential [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]].
- **[[Sir John Chester]]** — The villain whose murder by his own unacknowledged son is the novel's ultimate act of revenge, one that no character consciously designed.
- **[[Dennis]]** — The hangman who becomes the hanged — [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]] as poetic justice.

## Key Scenes

1. **The Revelation of the Murderer**: The exposure of [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] Senior's identity after decades of [[deception|concealment]] — the novel's long-delayed reckoning.
2. **[[hugh|Hugh]] Kills Chester**: The illegitimate son murders his father, an act whose full meaning is revealed only on the scaffold.
3. **[[dennis|Dennis]]'s Death**: The [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]er executed — the novel's most explicit instance of retributive justice.
4. **The Aftermath**: The dispersal of the riot's leaders, each meeting a fate proportionate to their [[guilt|crime]]s.
