## Overview

Bishopsgate Street, in the [[london|City of London]] near Bishopsgate, was one of the sites where participants in the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] were publicly executed in the aftermath of the disturbances. Dickens records a single, devastating scene at the gallows erected there: a young man hanged while his aged, grey-headed father waited for him, kissed him at the foot of the gallows, and sat on the ground until the body was taken down (077-061). The passage is one of the most compressed and emotionally potent moments in the novel's treatment of the riot's human cost.

## Description

Dickens does not describe the physical setting of Bishopsgate Street; rather, the place is defined entirely by the [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] that occurs there. The gallows become the focus of a tableau of paternal grief: an old man too poor to afford a hearse or coffin, walking meekly away beside the cart that carried his son's body back to [[imprisonment|prison]], "trying, as he went, to touch its lifeless hand." The economy of the description sharpens its impact, making Bishopsgate Street a site of quiet, individual tragedy amidst the larger [[mob-violence|collective violence]] of the riots and their suppression.

> One young man was hanged in Bishopsgate Street, whose aged grey-headed father waited for him at the gallows, kissed him at its foot when he arrived, and sat there, on the ground, till they took him down. They would have given him the body of his child; but he had no hearse, no coffin, nothing to remove it in, being too poor—and walked meekly away beside the cart that took it back to prison, trying, as he went, to touch its lifeless hand. (077-061)
