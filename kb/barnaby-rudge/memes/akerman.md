## Historical Biography

Richard Akerman (c. 1722–1792) served as the Keeper (governor) of Newgate Prison from 1768 until his death. He was a figure of some standing in the City of London, known for his professionalism and integrity in an era when jailers were often corrupt and brutal.

His defining moment came on the night of 6 June 1780, when a vast mob — having already destroyed Catholic chapels and private houses across London — turned its attention to Newgate.

Akerman refused to release his prisoners despite the mob's demands. When the rioters broke into the prison, he escaped with his life, but the prison was gutted and hundreds of prisoners were freed.

Akerman was later commended for his conduct and continued to serve as Keeper for another twelve years after the riots.

## Role in *Barnaby Rudge*

Akerman appears in two key chapters of *Barnaby Rudge*, both set during the attack on Newgate Prison. He is introduced when Hugh and the mob confront him directly:

> "you," said Hugh at length, "Mr. Akerman, the head jailer here?" (064-003)

Dickens portrays Akerman as a figure of stoic professional dignity amid the chaos. Faced with an impossible situation — a vast mob demanding the release of prisoners, backed by no military support — Akerman responds with remarkable composure:

> "All I can do, good people," Akerman replied, "is to exhort you to disperse" (064-011)

When Gabriel Varden, a prisoner inside Newgate, appeals to Akerman, the keeper's integrity is further demonstrated. He refuses to release Varden despite the danger to himself:

> "Bear witness for me, Mr. Akerman, that I refuse to do it" (064-017)

Akerman's reply to Gabriel reinforces both men's commitment to duty under pressure:

> "None, Mr. Akerman. You'll do your duty, and I'll do mine" (064-019)

In the aftermath, Akerman reappears briefly, identified as a gentleman who stood by his post while others fled (077-036).

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens stays close to the historical record in his portrayal of Akerman. The real Akerman did refuse to surrender his prisoners and was widely praised for his conduct.

The novel, however, elevates Akerman's role into a moral counterpoint: his steadfastness contrasts sharply with the cowardice of Lord Mayor Brackley Kennett. In reality, Akerman was one of several officials who performed commendably; Dickens singles him out to sharpen the thematic contrast between private integrity and public failure.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

Akerman embodies the quiet, professional virtue that *Barnaby Rudge* consistently values above political posturing. Where Kennett the Lord Mayor hides behind bolted doors (061-030), Akerman the jailer stands at his post and speaks plainly to the mob.

His simple words to Gabriel Varden — "you'll do your duty, and I'll do mine" (064-019) — crystallize the novel's ethic of personal responsibility in the face of mass chaos. In a narrative dominated by mobs, riots, and collective passions, Akerman represents the saving power of individual integrity.
