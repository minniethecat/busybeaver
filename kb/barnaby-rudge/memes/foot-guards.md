## Overview

The Foot Guards were the infantry regiments of the British Army's Household Division, responsible for protecting the sovereign and the royal palaces. Like their mounted counterparts the Horse Guards, the Foot Guards were stationed in London and were readily available for deployment in civil emergencies. During the Gordon Riots of June 1780, detachments of Foot Guards were dispatched to reinforce the Horse Guards and to protect strategic locations from the mob.

## Historical Background

The Foot Guards traced their lineage to the royal bodyguard regiments established after the Restoration of Charles II in 1660. By 1780, they comprised three regiments: the First (Grenadier) Guards, the Coldstream Guards, and the Third (Scots) Guards. Their London barracks gave them a unique role in domestic order-keeping, distinct from line regiments stationed in provincial garrisons or abroad. The deployment of the Guards during the Gordon Riots marked one of the largest uses of regular soldiers for civil crowd control in eighteenth-century London.

## Role in the Novel

The Foot Guards appear in *Barnaby Rudge* at a decisive moment in the military response to the riots. As the authorities escalate their efforts to regain control of the city, Dickens notes the arrival of heavier force:

> a strong detachment of the Foot Guards, having along with them two guns (057-051)

The presence of artillery — the two guns accompanying the Foot Guards — signals a qualitative escalation. Infantry with cannon represent not crowd control but military combat, the last stage before the state treats its own citizens as an enemy force. The Foot Guards thus embody the grim terminus of the novel's trajectory from political protest to armed confrontation. Their image — disciplined ranks of soldiers with fixed bayonets, backed by field guns — is the visual antithesis of the formless, surging mob.

## Key Members

Like the Horse Guards, the Foot Guards appear institutionally rather than through named individuals. Their officers and men are anonymous agents of state authority, their effectiveness residing precisely in their interchangeability and discipline.

## Significance

The Foot Guards complete the novel's portrait of the military dimension of the Gordon Riots. Where the Horse Guards represent mobility and shock — the sudden appearance of cavalry in a crowded street — the Foot Guards represent sustained force, the capacity to hold ground that cavalry can only sweep through. The two guns that accompany them are the novel's most chilling image of state power: the government, having exhausted every lesser measure, is now prepared to fire cannon into its own streets. Dickens does not dwell on this image; it is enough that the reader understands what it means.
