## Overview

The Mansion House is the official residence of the Lord Mayor of the [[london|City of London]], located at the junction of [[poultry|Poultry]] and [[cornhill|Cornhill]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it serves as a command post during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], from which the Lord Mayor coordinated the city's response to the violence.

## Description

Dickens presents the Mansion House primarily through its function during the crisis — a place of anxious waiting and frustrated authority.

> …they went on, and reached the Mansion House soon after sunrise. (061-008)

The building becomes a focal point for citizens' fears as the riots spread:

> …they'll be setting the Mansion House afire next, and we shall have… (061-029)

The Lord Mayor uses it as his operational base:

> …several times despatched to the Mansion House to await his orders… (063-002)

## Role in the Narrative

The Mansion House appears in the novel's account of the civic response to the riots. It represents the official authority of the City — the Lord Mayor issuing orders while [[mob-violence|the mob]] advances. Dickens uses the Mansion House to dramatise the paralysis of lawful authority: messages are sent, orders awaited, but effective action is delayed. The threat that the Mansion House itself might be set alight (061-029) captures the sense that no institution was safe.

## Characters Associated

- The Lord Mayor of [[london|London]] — Directed the City's response from the Mansion House

## Historical Basis

The Mansion House was built between 1739 and 1752 to the designs of George Dance the Elder. At the time of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] (1780), it was a relatively new building. During the actual riots, the Lord Mayor Brackley Kennett famously refused to take decisive action, and his inaction from the Mansion House became a significant point of criticism. Dickens's depiction of the Mansion House as a site of anxious paralysis reflects this historical reality.
