## Overview

Lincoln's Inn is one of the four Inns of Court, the ancient legal societies of [[london|London]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it appears during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] as an institutional space commandeered for military defence — its hall and commons given over to the Northumberland Militia — while its Fields become one of the principal gathering-points for the rioters themselves.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens gives Lincoln's Inn no architectural description; it is defined by its institutional function and its strategic geography. It is first mentioned in a conversation overheard by [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]] about houses targeted by [[mob-violence|the mob]]:

> I even heard mention of those in Duke Street, Lincoln's Inn Fields, and in Warwick Street, Golden Square; but common report, you know — You are not going? (050-036)

This passing reference maps the geography of the riots across the West End and the legal quarter, linking [[duke-street|Lincoln's Inn Fields]] to other fashionable addresses threatened by [[mob-violence|the mob]].

## Role in the Narrative

Lincoln's Inn serves a dual role in the riots — as a military strongpoint and as a rioters' rendezvous. The military occupation is described in the account of [[london|London]]'s defensive preparations:

> In Lincoln's Inn, they gave up the hall and commons to the Northumberland Militia, under the command of Lord Algernon Percy; in some few of the city wards, the burgesses turned out, and without making a very fierce show, looked brave enough. (067-010)

The image of an Inn of Court — a place of law, learning, and dining — converted into a militia barracks captures the inversion of civil order that the riots represent. The hall where barristers dined now houses soldiers; the commons where students walked now serves as a garrison.

The Fields outside the Inn, however, belong to [[mob-violence|the mob]]. On the evening when the riots reach their climax, [[duke-street|Lincoln's Inn Fields]] becomes one of the principal rallying points:

> It was about six o'clock in the evening, when a vast mob poured into Lincoln's Inn Fields by every avenue, and divided — evidently in pursuance of a previous design — into several parties. (063-004)

The mob's use of [[duke-street|Lincoln's Inn Fields]] as a mustering ground, dividing into organised parties "in pursuance of a previous design," reveals the tactical intelligence behind the apparent chaos of the riots. The largest open square in the legal quarter — designed for order, procession, and dignity — becomes a marshalling yard for insurrection.
