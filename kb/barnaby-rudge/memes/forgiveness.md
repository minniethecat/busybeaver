## Definition

Forgiveness in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is the moral force that resolves the novel's conflicts, appearing most powerfully in its closing chapters when the novel pivots from [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]] to reconciliation. Unlike [[revenge|revenge]], which drives the gothic plot forward, forgiveness halts the cycle of violence — it is what the novel's best characters offer and what its worst characters cannot accept. Dickens distinguishes between true forgiveness (the quiet, personal act) and performative forgiveness (the public display), most notably in [[mrs-varden|Mrs. Varden]]'s elaborately staged "humility and forgiveness" (019-025). The novel's final movement — from the scaffold to reconciliation — traces forgiveness as the alternative to the law's justice that [[justice-and-punishment|punishment]] alone cannot provide.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Performative vs. True Forgiveness

[[mrs-varden|Mrs. Varden]] provides the novel's satire of false forgiveness. Her "mighty show of humility and forgiveness" (019-025) is a performance designed to assert moral superiority over her husband — forgiveness as power, not grace. Dickens immediately contrasts this with the genuine article: the narrator describes her as "granting him her free forgiveness (the merit whereof, she meekly" attributed to her own virtue) (019-048). This distinction between real and counterfeit forgiveness runs through the novel: true forgiveness costs the forgiver something, while counterfeit forgiveness costs nothing and serves only the ego.

### The Final Reconciliation

The novel's closing chapters turn on forgiveness. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] "heartily prayed his forgiveness" after a moment of failure (066-004), modeling the humble request for pardon that the novel endorses. The most powerful instance comes from [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]], who, confronting the murderer of his brother, urges: "if you will but implore forgiveness of Heaven and of those whom you have injured" (073-053). That even the wronged brother can urge forgiveness — rather than demanding [[revenge|vengeance]] — marks the novel's moral climax. The final scenes between characters see forgiveness offered "in no common phrase, or show of" mere politeness (079-029), and the narrator notes that one cannot depart "without your full forgiveness; for busy life and I have little time" for resentment (079-031). The novel's last domestic scene catalogs "forgivenesses of injuries, here's amicable" settlements among relations (080-018) — forgiveness as the foundation of restored community.
