# Simon Tappertit

> "If I had been born a corsair or a pirate, a brigand, genteel highwayman or patriot—and they're the same thing," thought Mr. Tappertit, musing among the ninepins, "I should have been all right. But to drag out a ignoble existence unbeknown to mankind in general—patience! I will be famous yet. A voice within me keeps on whispering Greatness. I shall burst out one of these days, and when I do, what power can keep me down? I feel my soul getting into my head at the idea." (008-060)

## Overview

Simon Tappertit — called Sim in [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s household, but always "Mr. Simon Tappertit" in his own reckoning — is the vain and ambitious apprentice locksmith of the [[golden-key|Golden Key]] who becomes one of the most memorable comic villains in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*. A "thin-faced, sleek-haired, sharp-nosed, small-eyed little fellow, very little more than five feet high" who is "thoroughly convinced in his own mind that he was above the middle size" (004-019), Sim is a masterpiece of Dickensian caricature: a man so enormous in self-regard that his tiny body can barely contain him. His defining trait is a delirious vanity about his legs, "which, in knee-breeches, were perfect curiosities of littleness" (004-019) but which he admires with "a degree amounting to enthusiasm."

As the novel progresses, Sim's private fantasies of greatness find a public outlet in the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. He rises from secret ringleader of the underground ''Prentice Knights'' to captain of the United Bulldogs, to a field commander of rioters marching on [[parliament|Parliament]]. His arc is both comic — every scene of him issuing orders in a "cracked and shrill" voice invites laughter — and darkly consequential, for the vanity that makes him ridiculous also makes him dangerous. By the novel's end, having been "burnt and bruised, and with a gunshot wound in his body" and his precious legs "crushed into shapeless ugliness" (071-069), he is reduced to a shoeblack on two wooden legs, a fate that is simultaneously pitiable and perfectly just.

## Role in the Novel

### The Apprentice

Sim first appears in Chapter 4 as [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s apprentice, and his introduction is among the most vivid character sketches in Dickens. Caught by his master preening before a scrap of looking-glass, he is described as a man who "in years just twenty, in his looks much older, and in conceit at least two hundred" (004-022). His conceit centres on his legs — he "looked over his shoulder at so much of his legs as could be reflected in that small compass, with the greatest possible complacency and satisfaction" (004-018) — and extends to a belief in the mesmerising power of his eye, a faculty he terms "eyeing her over" (004-019), though the narrator notes that he has never furnished evidence of its efficacy.

His infatuation with his master's daughter, [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]], is the engine of much of his early comedy. Watching [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel]] kiss his daughter, "Sim Tappertit" felt that "there are bounds to human endurance" and "wished the Yorkshire cake might choke him" (004-024). When he learns that [[dolly-varden|Dolly]] favours [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]], he uses his position as captain of the ''Prentice Knights'' to issue a formal "proscribing" of [[joe-willet|Joe]], "forbidding all 'Prentice Knights to succour, comfort, or hold communion with him" (008-074).

### The Captain of the 'Prentice Knights

Chapter 8 reveals Sim's secret life as the self-styled captain of the ''Prentice Knights'', a ludicrous secret society meeting in a cellar beneath a [[barbican|Barbican]] tavern. The initiation ceremonies — involving a thighbone wielded as a sceptre, skulls "cheerfully ornamenting" a chair of state, and a great clasped book resting on a human desk (008-035, 008-036) — are mock-heroic comedy of the highest order. But they also reveal the genuine menace beneath Sim's absurdity: the society exists to pursue "[[revenge|vengeance]] on their Tyrant Masters" and the restoration of ancient apprentice "rights and holidays" by "fire and sword when needful" (008-055). Sim's oath binds members "to resist and obstruct the Lord Mayor, sword-bearer, and chaplain; to despise the authority of the sheriffs; and to hold the court of aldermen as nought" (008-055).

Sim's appeal to his comrades rests on small, mean skills: he has constructed secret door-keys for the entire society, and "perhaps owed something of his influence to that mean and trivial circumstance—on such slight accidents do even men of mind depend!" (008-065). The combination of grandiose rhetoric and petty mechanism is the essence of his character.

### The Protestant Association and the Gordon Riots

By the time of the riots, Sim has advanced from apprentice to journeyman and has become "chief or captain of the Bulldogs," a detachment of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]. Dickens notes that he "seemed, physically speaking, to have grown smaller with years (particularly as to his legs, which were stupendously little), but who, in a moral point of view, in personal dignity and self-esteem, had swelled into a giant" (039-002). [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]], hearing [[gashford|Gashford]] report that Tappertit "snuffs the battle from afar, like the warhorse" and "throws his hat up in the street as if he were inspired" (036-028), declares: "Make a note of Tappertit. We may advance him to a place of trust" (036-029).

The advancement comes swiftly. On the day of the great march on [[parliament|Parliament]], Simon Tappertit leads the third and largest division — the one that sweeps through the City "in order that their numbers and their serious intentions might be the better known and appreciated by the citizens" (049-002). He is assisted by [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]], [[hugh|Hugh]], and selected subalterns from the Brotherhood of United Bulldogs. The mob stretches nearly four miles, marching three abreast.

In the aftermath of the march, a drunken Sim returns to the [[golden-key|Golden Key]], dishevelled and battered but insufferably proud. He displays a tooth he claims to have knocked from a bishop, warns [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] to "Beware!" and proclaims: "My bleeding country calls me and I go!" (051-040). When Varden tries to save him by locking him in his room, Sim fights his master, wriggles free — "drunken men who have lost the power of walking steadily, can often run" (051-050) — and flees into the night. Varden, winded on a doorstep, pronounces his epitaph: "I have done my best for thee, poor lad, and would have saved thee, but the rope is round thy neck, I fear" (051-052).

### The Kidnapping and the March on Newgate

Sim participates in the kidnapping of [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]] and [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]], and in the carriage that bears them to [[london|London]] he seizes the moment to make his long-dreamed-of proposal. Standing before the terrified [[dolly-varden|Dolly]], he delivers a speech of breathtaking presumption:

> "You meet in me, Miss V.," said Simon, laying his hand upon his breast, "not a 'prentice, not a workman, not a slave, not the wictim of your father's tyrannical behaviour, but the leader of a great people, the captain of a noble band ... Behold in me, your husband. Yes, beautiful Dolly—charmer—enslaver—S. Tappertit is all your own!" (059-056)

[[dolly-varden|Dolly]]'s response — she "wound her hands in his hair, and crying out amidst her tears that he was a dreadful little wretch, and always had been, shook, and pulled, and beat him" (059-057) — prompts [[hugh|Hugh]] to laugh harder than at anything else in the novel, and Sim to smooth his "rumpled feathers" with the reflection that she is merely "in an excited state" (059-058).

At the [[burning-of-newgate|burning of Newgate]], Sim is one of the leaders: "When all was ready, [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[dennis|Dennis]], with Simon Tappertit between them, led the way. Roaring and chafing like an angry sea, the crowd pressed after them" (063-006). He confronts his old master at the [[golden-key|Golden Key]], demanding that Varden use his locksmith's skill to pick the great door of the [[imprisonment|prison]]. Varden's reply — "When I do, my hands shall drop off at the wrists, and you shall wear them, Simon Tappertit, on your shoulders for epaulettes" (063-051) — is one of the novel's finest rebukes.

## Character and Personality

Dickens builds Sim Tappertit on a single comic disproportion: the gap between his self-conception and reality. The narrator describes this as a perpetual fermentation — "As certain liquors, confined in casks too cramped in their dimensions, will ferment, and fret, and chafe in their im[[imprisonment|prison]]ment, so the spiritual essence or soul of Mr. Tappertit would sometimes fume within that precious cask, his body" (004-020). Sim himself calls this state "my soul getting into my head" (008-060).

His vanity extends beyond his legs to his dress (he changes his steel knee-buckles for glittering paste at a convenient street-corner post on Sundays), his eye (he boasts of its power to "utterly quell and subdue the haughtiest beauty"), and his place in history (he fancies himself a "master spirit" capable of leading apprentices in a new age of glory). All of this is undercut by his absurd physical presence — the "cracked and shrill" voice (008-061), the legs so small [[stagg|Stagg]] mocks them while flattering them, the face that when attempting to look soulful resembles a man choking (004-039).

Yet Sim is not merely ridiculous. His vanity has real-world consequences. He recruits impressionable young apprentices into a society devoted to revenge against their masters. He betrays his master's household by duplicating [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel]]'s keys. He plays an active role in the kidnapping of two innocent women. And as a leader of [[mob-violence|the mob]], he bears responsibility for the destruction and violence of the riots. Dickens's achievement is to make Sim simultaneously laughable and sinister, a man whose absurdity does not quite excuse his [[innocence|guilt]].

His treatment of women reveals the uglier side of his vanity. He patronises [[miss-miggs|Miggs]], exploiting her [[loyalty|devotion]] while holding her in contempt. His pursuit of [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]] is marked by complete indifference to her feelings — he genuinely cannot comprehend that she might reject him. His proposal in Chapter 59, delivered to a captive audience, is a study in the sexual entitlement that vanity breeds: "For how many years have I looked forward to this present meeting! For how many years has it been my intention to exalt and ennoble you!" (059-056).

## Relationships

- **[[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]** — His master at the [[golden-key|Golden Key]]. Varden treats Sim with exasperated affection, even trying to save him from the gallows after the riots. Sim betrays him by stealing his master key and joining the rioters. Their final confrontation at the sack of [[imprisonment|Newgate]] — Varden refusing to pick the [[imprisonment|prison]] lock — is the moral climax of their relationship.
- **[[john-willet|Dolly Varden]]** — The locksmith's daughter, whom Sim idolises from a distance and whose rejection of him is the novel's bluntest verdict on his character. She wants nothing to do with him and tells him so with her fists.
- **[[miss-miggs|Miggs]]** — The Vardens' maidservant, who loves Sim with a [[loyalty|devotion]] he neither merits nor returns. Her clingy persistence — hiding in the garret, sabotaging Varden's gun with table-beer, throwing herself at Sim during the siege — provides much of the novel's darkest comedy. By the end, he has married someone else; she has become the fearsome turnkey of the County Bridewell.
- **[[hugh|Hugh]]** — The [[maypole-inn|Maypole]] hostler and Sim's co-leader in the riots. [[hugh|Hugh]]'s physical bulk and genuine menace contrast with Sim's small stature and theatrical posturing. [[hugh|Hugh]]'s laughter at Sim's expense — especially after [[dolly-varden|Dolly]]'s rejection — is a running joke.
- **[[john-willet|Ned Dennis]]** — The hangman and fellow rioter. Their partnership in the riots places Sim alongside the novel's most cheerfully amoral character.
- **[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]** — Fellow rioter, though Sim has little direct interaction with him. Both are in some sense victims of the manipulators ([[gashford|Gashford]], Chester) who exploit the riots for their own ends.
- **[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]** — Sim's political patron, who promotes him on [[gashford|Gashford]]'s recommendation as a man who "snuffs the battle from afar" (036-028). Sim serves as an instrument of [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s anti-Catholic agitation.
- **[[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]** — Recruits Sim in Chapter 24 to do his dirty work, amused by the little man's pretensions while exploiting his practical access to [[gabriel-varden|the locksmith]]'s keys.
- **The Widow** — Sim's eventual wife: "[[mrs-rudge|the widow]] of an eminent bone and rag collector, formerly of Millbank" (082-010). She "assisted in the business" and occasionally "retaliated by taking off his legs" during marital disputes.

## Historical Basis

Simon Tappertit is a fictional character, but he draws on a real historical type: the [[london|London]] apprentice of the eighteenth century. Apprentices in Georgian [[london|London]] were a recognised social force, with a tradition of collective action stretching back to the Elizabethan period. The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots of 1780]] were, in fact, heavily populated by apprentices and journeymen — young men whose economic frustrations and institutional resentments made them ready recruits for anti-Catholic agitation.

Dickens's portrayal of the ''Prentice Knights'' as a secret society devoted to the "restoration of their ancient rights and holidays" (008-055) echoes the actual customs of eighteenth-century apprentices, who had historically enjoyed certain customary liberties and holidays that were increasingly curtailed. The society's blend of buffoonish ritual and genuine menace captures something essential about the way righteous political energy, when mixed with personal grievance and a taste for violence, can curdle into mob brutality.

Sim's fate as a shoeblack — a trade that barely existed in 1780 but was ubiquitous in Victorian [[london|London]] — is an anachronistic joke that Dickens cannot resist. It places the would-be captain of apprentices in the lowest rung of the service economy, shining the boots of the very class of gentlemen he once dreamed of displacing.
