## Overview

Fleet Street in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* appears as one of the great arteries of the [[london|City of London]] — a street whose name carries the weight of its institutional associations (the [[temple|Temple]], the Fleet Prison, the legal trade) and whose geography makes it a natural stage for the political confrontations of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. It is first mentioned in Chapter 15 as a contrast to the quiet of the Temple precincts — "from the tumult of [[strand|the Strand]] or Fleet Street, 'Who enters here leaves noise behind'" (015-004) — and reappears in the riot chapters as a site of violence and suppression.

The street's narrative significance is inseparable from its geography. Fleet Street runs eastward from [[strand|the Strand]] toward [[ludgate-hill|Ludgate Hill]] and St Paul's, forming part of the principal east–west route through the City. During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], this route became one of the corridors along which [[mob-violence|the mob]] moved between the political spaces of [[westminster|Westminster]] and the commercial spaces of the City. The street's width, its institutional buildings, and its proximity to the Fleet Prison all contributed to its role as a contested space.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens introduces Fleet Street through the device of a contrast — the noise of the thoroughfare against the quiet of the Temple:

> "From the tumult of the Strand or Fleet Street, 'Who enters here leaves noise behind'" (015-004)

This is a description of the Temple rather than of Fleet Street itself, but the force of the contrast depends on the reader understanding Fleet Street as a place of "tumult" — a word that suggests not merely noise but disorder, confusion, the press of bodies and voices. [[temple|The Temple]], by implication, is a retreat from the commercial and political life that Fleet Street embodies.

The street's role in the riots is rendered through action rather than scenic description:

> "Coming into Fleet Street, they found it in an unusual state of excitement" (049-051)

The "unusual state of excitement" is Dickens's characteristic understatement for the approach of mob violence. By the time the rioters reach Fleet Street, they are moving through the commercial heart of the City, and the street's usual bustle has been replaced by something more dangerous. This moment in Chapter 49 marks a narrative threshold: the private tensions that have been building through the novel's earlier chapters are about to erupt into public violence.

During the military suppression that follows the riots' peak, Fleet Street becomes a site of exemplary punishment:

> "Lampposts in Cheapside and Fleet Street" (073-005)

served as improvised gibbets where captured rioters were hanged by the troops. The image of the lamppost — a feature of the modern, improving city — converted into an instrument of [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] captures the sudden reversal by which order reasserted itself. The street that had been a corridor of mob movement became a corridor of state [[justice-and-punishment|retribution]].

## Role in the Narrative

Fleet Street appears at three critical junctures in the novel. First, in Chapter 15, it establishes the boundary between the contemplative world of the Temple (where the novel's legal business touches the narrative) and the active, political world of the street. This boundary will be breached during the riots, when [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s violence penetrates even the Temple's precincts.

Second, in Chapter 49, Fleet Street is the place where the approaching crisis becomes visible. The "unusual state of excitement" (049-051) is the moment when rumour tips into recognition — when characters who have heard of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s march now see its consequences in the faces and movements of the crowd.

Third, in Chapter 73, the aftermath: the lamppost hangings "in [[cheapside|Cheapside]] and Fleet Street" (073-005) mark the restoration of order through terror. The street that bustled with commerce and the law becomes for a time a place of death, its lampposts serving as gallows — a transformation that encapsulates the riots' overturning of all normal urban life. The rioters had also "made a diversion for the same purpose in Fleet Street, and refused to take anything" (067-006) when they passed through, marking the street as one of the routes along which [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s peculiar code of selective plunder operated.

## Historical Basis

Fleet Street in 1780 was one of the principal thoroughfares of the [[london|City of London]], running eastward from [[temple-bar|Temple Bar]] (the western boundary of the City) toward [[ludgate-hill|Ludgate Hill]]. The street took its name from the River Fleet, which flowed beneath it in a covered channel. It was lined with the establishments of printers, booksellers, and legal professionals — the proximity of the Inns of Court, including the Temple, gave the street its enduring association with the law.

During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], Fleet Street was a natural axis of movement. The mobs that assembled in St George's Fields in [[southwark|Southwark]] crossed [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] and moved through the City toward [[westminster|Westminster]]; Fleet Street lay on the route between the City and the political spaces of the West End. The "lamppost hangings" that Dickens records were part of the brutal military suppression that followed the riots' peak: troops were authorised to fire on rioters, and summary [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s were carried out in the streets. Dickens's choice of Fleet Street and [[cheapside|Cheapside]] — two of the City's most prominent thoroughfares — as the sites of these [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]s emphasises the public, exemplary nature of the violence, performed where the greatest number of citizens would witness it.
