John Grueby is [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s loyal manservant and one of the novel's most sympathetic minor characters. A plain-speaking, physically imposing man, Grueby sees clearly what his master cannot — that [[gashford|Gashford]] is manipulating Lord George toward violence — but remains steadfast in his service nonetheless. In the chaos of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], Grueby represents the ordinary decency that survives amid fanaticism.

## Character and Appearance

Grueby is introduced in Chapter 35 as part of Lord George's travelling party. He is described as "bluff John Grueby" (082-008), a man of few words and strong opinions who wears a "great blue cockade" (035-046) — the symbol of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] — with reluctant duty rather than enthusiasm. His physical presence is substantial; he serves as his master's protector in the violent crowds of the riots.

## Relationship with Gashford

Grueby's most important dramatic function is as the foil to [[gashford|Gashford]]. While [[gashford|Gashford]] flatters and manipulates Lord George with whispered insinuation, Grueby speaks blunt truth (035-085):

> "Now, Mr. Gashford sir," said John Grueby in his ear, after what appeared a very lengthened and circuitous preparation, "you are the man that keeps him here in this state — you're the man that blows the fire in him."

[[gashford|Gashford]]'s smoothness and Grueby's directness are set in constant contrast. Grueby sees through [[gashford|Gashford]] immediately, and his open contempt for the secretary is one of the novel's moral signposts — Grueby's judgment is always sound, even when his master ignores it.

## Loyalty to Lord George

Despite his disapproval of the riots and his clear-eyed view of [[gashford|Gashford]], Grueby never abandons Lord George. His loyalty is grounded not in agreement with the cause but in personal [[loyalty|devotion]] to a man he recognizes as weak but not wicked (057-030):

> "My lord," John Grueby answered, "look at his dress, his condition. Do not wrong yourself by noticing him."

During the attack on [[imprisonment|Newgate]] (Chapter 64) and the subsequent unraveling of the riots, Grueby remains at Lord George's side, protecting him from both [[mob-violence|the mob]] and the consequences of his own actions (066-011):

> The other was John Grueby, who had stood by him so manfully.

## The Final Chapter

In the novel's closing pages, Grueby's steadfastness is rewarded with quiet recognition: "he was truly served by bluff John Grueby" (082-008). His survival and continued service represent one of the novel's small affirmations — that loyalty and decency endure even when the causes they serve are flawed.
