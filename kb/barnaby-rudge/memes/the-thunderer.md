## Overview

*The Thunderer* was a militant "No Popery" pamphlet that circulated among the Protestant societies of London during the anti-Catholic agitation of 1780. Its editorship was anonymous — "edited by nobody knew whom" (039-038) — and its content espoused the most extreme positions of the movement. The title itself, with its suggestion of divine or apocalyptic authority, is characteristic of the hyperbolic rhetoric that fueled the Gordon Riots.

## Reference in *Barnaby Rudge*

*The Thunderer* appears in *Barnaby Rudge* as the centerpiece of a scene of political radicalization. During the build-up to the march on Parliament, the pamphlet is held up as a rallying point:

> the great attraction was a pamphlet called The Thunderer, which espoused their own opinions (039-038)

The pamphlet's influence extends beyond the moment of agitation. Mrs. Varden, the locksmith's wife, is described as a "regular subscriber to the Thunderer" (041-038), and Dickens notes that she would have had ample opportunity to air her grievances in its pages. The detail is both comic and chilling: the respectable housewife, snug in her Clerkenwell home, funds and consumes propaganda that justifies arson and murder. *The Thunderer* thus bridges the domestic and the political, showing how the ideology of the riots seeped into ordinary households.

## Significance

In Dickens's anatomy of the Gordon Riots, *The Thunderer* represents the printed word as accelerant. The mob does not spontaneously combust; it is kindled by pamphlets, placards, and speeches. *The Thunderer* — anonymous, unaccountable, and inflammatory — embodies the irresponsible exercise of press freedom that Dickens saw as one of the enabling conditions of mob violence. Its subscription model, with Mrs. Varden as a paying customer, further suggests that hatred, like any other commodity, can be packaged and sold.
