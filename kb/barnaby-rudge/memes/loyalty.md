## Definition

Loyalty in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is not a simple virtue but a contested moral quality that reveals character through its object. Loyalty to a corrupt cause (the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]) produces violence; loyalty to a person ([[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s devotion to his mother, [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]]'s constancy to [[dolly-varden|Dolly]]) produces moral clarity. The novel's moral scheme rewards loyalty to persons over loyalty to abstractions, and Dickens is particularly interested in the loyalty of servants — characters like [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], whose fidelity to craft and community provides the novel's ethical center.

## Historical Context

In 18th-century England, loyalty was understood hierarchically: servants owed loyalty to masters, subjects to crown, apprentices to guild. The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] disrupted these hierarchies, demanding that characters choose between competing loyalties — to religion, to law, to family. The [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] exploited the language of loyalty ("defence of religion, life, and liberty") to mobilize working-class [[london|London]]ers (037-002). Dickens's own radical sympathies made him suspicious of loyalty demanded by institutions rather than freely given by individuals.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Personal Loyalty

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]'s unwavering love for his mother is the novel's purest example of loyalty. His devotion is not intellectual but instinctive — he needs no reason beyond love. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s loyalty to his craft, his family, and his community represents the novel's middle-class ideal: loyalty expressed through work and care rather than grand gestures. [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]]'s constancy to [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]], maintained through years of absence and presumed rejection, is the novel's romantic ideal of fidelity.

### Loyalty Perverted

The mob's loyalty to "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" is loyalty to a slogan emptied of meaning — the novel's sharpest critique of loyalty given without examination. [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] shifts his loyalty from the state (as [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]er) to [[mob-violence|the mob]] (as rioter), only to discover that neither object of loyalty protects him from the scaffold. [[hugh|Hugh]]'s loyalty to [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] — his one redeeming attachment — is perverted by his deeper loyalty to violence and drink.

### Loyalty as Moral Test

The novel repeatedly tests characters by demanding they choose between competing loyalties. [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]]'s loyalty to his murdered brother's memory conflicts with his duty to protect his niece. [[gashford|Gashford]]'s loyalty to [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] masks a deeper loyalty to his own advancement. In every case, the character's choice reveals their moral core.

## Key Characters

- **[[Gabriel Varden]]** — The locksmith whose loyalty to craft, family, and law provides the novel's moral center. His refusal to join either [[mob-violence|the mob]] or its suppressors is loyalty expressed as integrity.
- **[[Barnaby Rudge]]** — Whose simple, absolute loyalty to his mother is the novel's most moving example of love as moral compass.
- **[[Joe Willet]]** — Whose loyalty to [[dolly-varden|Dolly]] survives years of separation, war wounds, and apparent rejection.
- **[[Geoffrey Haredale]]** — Whose loyalty to his murdered brother's memory shapes his entire adult life, for good and ill.
- **[[Dennis]]** — The hangman whose transferable loyalty — from state to mob — exposes the emptiness of loyalty without moral conviction.

## Key Scenes

1. **[[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s Refusal**: The locksmith declines to join either the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] or the forces suppressing it — loyalty to principle over party.
2. **[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] and His Mother**: Every scene between [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] and [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] is a study in mutual loyalty, the novel's emotional keel.
3. **[[joe-willet|Joe Willet]]'s Return**: The wounded veteran returns to [[dolly-varden|Dolly]], his loyalty intact after years of war and exile.
4. **[[dennis|Dennis]] on the Scaffold**: The hangman who served both state and mob faces death alone — loyalty to institutions cannot substitute for loyalty to persons.
