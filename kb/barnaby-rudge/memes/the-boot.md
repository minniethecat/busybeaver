## Overview

The Boot is the dark twin of [[maypole-inn|the Maypole Inn]] — a [[london|London]] public house that serves, through the central chapters of *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, as the headquarters and rallying-point of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon rioters]]. Where [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] embodies rural community, convivial warmth, and settled custom, The Boot is the space where mob violence is planned, celebrated, and directed: a "lone house of public entertainment" (038-037) whose isolation from normal neighbourhood life makes it the perfect base for insurrection.

Dickens presents The Boot as a threshold space — neither fully inside nor outside the city, neither wholly criminal nor wholly respectable. Its ambiguous position reflects the ambiguous status of the riots themselves: public disorder conducted with an eerie semblance of organisation, in which rounds of drink alternate with arson and looting. The Boot is where [[hugh|Hugh]] the ostler becomes [[hugh|Captain Hugh]], where [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]] holds court as a revolutionary leader, and where the everyday rituals of tavern life — drinking, boasting, storytelling — are transposed into the idiom of mob violence.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens introduces The Boot with a description that emphasises its liminal, almost outlaw character:

> This Boot was a lone house of public entertainment, situated on the fields at the extremity of a narrow lane, and was renowned for its beef-steak puddings, and for a certain pearly old ale, much affected by the more luxurious of the neighbours. (038-037)

The phrase "lone house" is carefully chosen. The Boot stands apart — from other buildings, from domestic surveillance, from the ordinary constraints that would inhibit a mob's gathering. Its isolation is its utility. The "narrow lane" that leads to it is both a physical approach and a narrative device: characters who make their way to The Boot are moving into a space of moral exception, where the rules that govern ordinary life are suspended.

Inside, the atmosphere is one of enforced camaraderie masking barely-contained violence. The company at The Boot includes "good company" (038-036) — a phrase freighted with irony, since the "good company" consists of the riot's most dangerous figures. "Bolder spirits" who "cared nothing for the appearance" of their fellow drinkers ensure that "all noise and jest" continue (039-038), but the jollity is performative — a way of drowning out fear and conscience.

The Boot's physical position on the fields is strategically significant. It is close enough to [[london|London]] for the rioters to strike and retreat, but remote enough to escape casual detection. When the soldiers finally take possession of the house in Chapter 60, the event marks a turning point in the riots: the loss of the headquarters signals the beginning of the end for [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s organised phase.

## Role in the Narrative

The Boot functions as the organisational nerve-centre of the [[gordon-riots-1780|riots]] from their first outbreak to their military suppression. It is first proposed as a meeting-place by [[gashford|Gashford]], who tells the rioters after the [[westminster|Westminster]] confrontation that "they should repair together to The Boot" (038-036). [[gashford|Gashford]]'s choice of location is no accident: he understands, with the instinct of a political operative, that [[mob-violence|the mob]] needs a space of its own — a headquarters where loyalty can be reinforced, plans laid, and the fragile unity of the crowd maintained through alcohol and shared purpose.

The rhythms of life at The Boot mirror the rhythms of the riots themselves. In the aftermath of each night's violence, the rioters return to The Boot to drink, boast, and count their spoils. When the "excitement of the company at The Boot had not yet subsided" (039-001), the leaders know that another night of destruction can be launched. The public house becomes a kind of pulse-point for the novel's violence: the level of noise and confidence at The Boot predicts the level of destruction on [[london|London]]'s streets.

Key decisions are made within its walls. It is at The Boot that [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]] consolidates his authority over the United Bulldogs, that [[dennis|Dennis]] dispenses his macabre brand of hangman's wisdom, and that [[hugh|Hugh]] emerges from his role as [[maypole-inn|Maypole]] ostler to become one of the riot's most feared [[simon-tappertit|captain]]s. The Boot is also where [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] is drawn into the rioters' company — his innocence a stark contrast to the calculated violence of those around him.

The loss of The Boot to the soldiers in Chapter 60 is a decisive narrative turn. When [[hugh|Hugh]] learns that "the soldiers have taken possession of The Boot" (060-011), the rioters lose not just a building but their centre of gravity. From this point, the riots become increasingly desperate and disorganised — a scattering rather than a campaign.

## Historical Basis

The Boot as a specific public house does not feature in the historical record of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]; it appears to be Dickens's invention — a composite drawn from the many [[london|London]] taverns that served as meeting-places for the rioters. Contemporary accounts confirm that the rioters used public houses as organisational nodes: the rioters gathered at taverns to plan their attacks, divided into "divisions" named for different districts, and returned to drink after each night's destruction. Dickens condenses this dispersed historical reality into a single, vivid location, giving the riots a dramatic geography they lacked in fact.

The name "The Boot" was a common one for [[london|London]] taverns in the eighteenth century. The boot was a familiar signboard emblem, often associated with coaching inns. Dickens's choice of such an ordinary name for the rioters' headquarters is characteristic: the horror of the riots is domesticated, made to grow out of the everyday fabric of [[london|London]] life, rather than imported from some exotic elsewhere.
