## Definition

The Rudge Murder is the foundational crime of *Barnaby Rudge* — the killing of Reuben Haredale at his country house, the Warren, on the night of 19 March 1753. The steward, Rudge, vanished the same night and was presumed both murderer and victim, but the truth — that Rudge was the murderer and had escaped, leaving another body in his clothes — only emerges decades later. This crime sets in motion the novel's twin plots of private vengeance and public riot.

## The Crime

On the night of the murder, Reuben Haredale was found dead in his bedchamber at the Warren, the Haredale family's country estate near Chigwell. The steward Rudge — father of the infant Barnaby — was missing, and in the garden a body dressed in Rudge's clothes was discovered, "scarcely colder than the ashes of the fire" (001-105). The gardener was suspected, but no conviction was ever secured. The crime remained officially unsolved for twenty-seven years.

## The Truth

The hidden truth, preserved as a guilty secret by Mrs. Rudge and eventually confessed by the murderer himself, is that Rudge killed his master Reuben Haredale and then murdered the gardener, dressing the gardener's body in his own clothes to fake his death. Rudge escaped and lived in hiding, periodically returning to extort money from his wife through threats against their son Barnaby.

Geoffrey Haredale, Reuben's brother, spent his life pursuing the murderer. When Rudge is finally unmasked, Haredale confronts him at the Maypole: "You, Rudge, double murderer and monster" (056-057).

## Mrs. Rudge's Role

Mrs. Rudge knew the truth from the beginning. When Gabriel Varden first brought the fugitive Rudge (disguised) to her cottage, she recognized her husband immediately: "she no sooner saw him than she swooned" (004-030). Her life was thereafter defined by this terrible knowledge — she was bound to silence by Rudge's threats and by her love for Barnaby, whose fragile mind would be destroyed by the truth about his father.

## Narrative Function

The Rudge murder functions as the novel's private plot, interwoven with the public plot of the Gordon Riots:

- **Structural Parallel**: Both the private murder and the public riots involve concealed identities, secret guilt, and delayed retribution
- **Thematic Anchor**: The murder embodies the novel's preoccupation with the past's inescapable hold on the present
- **Character Engine**: The crime shapes the lives of Barnaby, Mrs. Rudge, Geoffrey Haredale, and the murderer himself across two decades

The resolution of the murder plot — Rudge's capture, confession, and execution — coincides with the suppression of the riots, uniting the novel's twin narratives in a single moment of justice.

## Key Scenes

1. **Discovery** (001-103–001-106): The gardener describes finding Reuben Haredale's body and the presumed remains of the steward
2. **Recognition** (004-030): Mrs. Rudge faints upon seeing her disguised husband, revealing to Gabriel Varden that something is amiss
3. **Confrontation** (056-057): Geoffrey Haredale unmasks Rudge at the Maypole
4. **Execution** (077): Rudge is hanged for his crime, the public execution mirroring the public resolution of the riots
