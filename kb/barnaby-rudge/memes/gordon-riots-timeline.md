# Gordon Riots Timeline

## Thesis

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] of June 1780 were not a single continuous uprising but a sequence of distinct, escalating phases — each with its own logic, leadership, and targets. Understanding the riots requires seeing them not as a monolithic "mob" but as a chain of events in which each phase created the conditions for the next. This timeline traces that chain, from the peaceful [[march-on-parliament|march on Parliament]] through the systematic destruction of Catholic property, the [[attack-on-the-warren|attack on the Warren]], the [[burning-of-newgate|storming of Newgate]], and the final reckoning of [[justice-and-punishment|trials and executions]].

## Phase 1: March on Parliament (June 2, 1780)

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] began as an orderly political demonstration. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]], president of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], led a procession of approximately 40,000–60,000 supporters to the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] to present a petition against the Catholic Relief Act of 1778.

> The crowd assembled on [[st-georges-fields|St. George's Fields]] in [[southwark|Southwark]] before marching across [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] toward [[westminster|Westminster]]. The procession was initially peaceful — many participants wore blue cockades, the symbol of the Protestant Association. (037-015)

The [[march-on-parliament|march]] turned violent when the crowd, finding the House of Commons doors barred against them, began attacking the carriages of arriving MPs. Members of the House of Lords were assaulted; [[lord-rockingham|Lord Rockingham]]'s carriage was overturned. By evening, the demonstrators had dispersed from [[westminster|Westminster]] but had not gone home — they had moved into the streets of [[london|London]].

## Phase 2: Destruction of Catholic Chapels (June 2–4, 1780)

With the political demonstration over, the crowd turned its attention to direct action against Catholic targets. On the night of June 2, the [[destruction-of-catholic-chapels|Sardinian Embassy Chapel]] in [[lincolns-inn|Lincoln's Inn Fields]] was attacked and burned.

> There was not the least disguise or concealment — indeed, on this night, very little excitement or hurry. From the chapels, they tore down and took away the very altars, benches, pulpits, pews, and flooring; from the dwelling-houses, the very wainscoting and stairs. This Sunday evening's recreation they pursued like mere workmen who had a certain task to do, and did it. (052-031)

Over the next two days, the attacks spread: the Bavarian Embassy Chapel in Warwick Street, the [[moorfields|Moorfields]] Catholic chapel, and numerous Catholic homes and businesses were destroyed. The authorities' failure to intervene — due to the legal requirement that magistrates read the Riot Act before deploying troops, and the reluctance of magistrates to act without military support — created a power vacuum that the rioters exploited.

## Phase 3: Attack on the Warren (June 5, 1780)

The [[attack-on-the-warren|attack on the Warren]] — the [[haredale|Haredale]] family estate — marked the riots' intersection with the novel's private plot. While historical rioters did attack Catholic-owned properties throughout London, Dickens makes [[the-warren|the Warren]] the symbolic site where public violence becomes personal.

> The attack on [[the-warren|the Warren]] was led by [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]] and [[hugh|Hugh]], who had personal grievances against [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] that they dressed in the language of religious zeal. The destruction of the house was both a political act and a private revenge. (055-030)

The burning of [[the-warren|the Warren]] displaced [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]], [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]], and their household, driving the Haredale plot toward its resolution while the riots continued to escalate.

## Phase 4: The Storming of Newgate (June 6–7, 1780)

The [[burning-of-newgate|storming of Newgate Prison]] on the night of June 6 was the riots' military climax. [[hugh|Hugh]], [[dennis|Dennis]], and [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]] led the attack:

> By this time, the crowd was everywhere; all concealment and disguise were laid aside, and they pervaded the whole town. (063-003)

> Old swords, and pistols without ball or powder; sledgehammers, knives, axes, saws, and weapons pillaged from the butchers' shops; a forest of iron bars and wooden clubs; long ladders for scaling the walls, each carried on the shoulders of a dozen men; lighted torches... (063-006)

The crowd breached [[newgate|Newgate]]'s walls, released all 300 prisoners, and set the [[imprisonment|prison]] ablaze. The keeper, [[akerman|Mr. Akerman]], was forced to surrender the keys. The burning of [[newgate|Newgate]] was the symbolic center of the riots — the destruction of the state's principal instrument of [[justice-and-punishment|punishment]].

Simultaneously, other targets were attacked: the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] (defended by [[john-grueby|John Grueby]] and the [[foot-guards|Foot Guards]]), the [[fleet-street|Fleet Prison]], the [[black-lion|Black Lion]] tavern, and the houses of prominent figures including [[lord-mansfield|Lord Mansfield]] in [[bloomsbury|Bloomsbury]].

## Phase 5: Suppression (June 7–9, 1780)

On June 7, [[king-george-iii|King George III]] personally ordered the military to suppress the riots with force. The [[horse-guards|Horse Guards]] and [[foot-guards|Foot Guards]], supported by militia units, deployed across [[london|London]]. The legal obstacle that had paralyzed the magistrates was removed by a Privy Council ruling that soldiers could fire on rioters without waiting for the Riot Act to be read.

> The military intervention was swift and lethal. At [[holborn|Holborn]], [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]], and [[cornhill|Cornhill]], soldiers fired on crowds. The official death toll was 285 killed and approximately 200 wounded, though contemporary estimates ranged higher. (069-040)

By June 9, the riots were effectively over. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] was arrested and sent to [[tower-of-london|the Tower of London]] (he was later acquitted of treason). The mass arrests that followed filled London's surviving prisons beyond capacity.

## Phase 6: Trials and Executions (June–July 1780)

The legal reckoning was swift and severe. [[brackley-kennett|Brackley Kennett]], the Lord Mayor of London, was tried and convicted for criminal negligence in failing to suppress the riots. [[dennis|Dennis]] and [[hugh|Hugh]] were captured, tried at the [[old-bailey|Old Bailey]], and sentenced to death. Their execution on the scaffold — the hangman hanged by his own profession's instrument — was the riots' final irony.

> Of those arrested, 62 were sentenced to death and 25–30 were actually executed. The remainder were transported, pardoned, or died in custody. (073-015)

The [[trial-of-the-rioters|trials]] established legal precedents for prosecuting riot-related offenses, but they also exposed the selectivity of the justice system: [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] was acquitted while ordinary rioters hanged.

## Conclusion

The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] were the most destructive urban uprising in 18th-century English history — and their lesson, for Dickens, was not simply that mobs are dangerous. The timeline reveals a pattern: a legitimate political grievance exploited by manipulators ([[gashford|Gashford]]), escalated by men with personal scores to settle ([[hugh|Hugh]], [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]]), enabled by institutional paralysis ([[brackley-kennett|Kennett]]'s negligence), and punished with selective severity. The mechanism that produced the riots was not crowd madness but the convergence of political failure, personal grievance, and institutional collapse.
