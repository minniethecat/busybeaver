## Definition

The Riot Act (1 Geo. 1, c. 5) was a statute passed by the British Parliament in 1715, in the aftermath of the Jacobite rising. It empowered local authorities to declare any gathering of twelve or more persons an unlawful assembly and to order them to disperse. The procedure required a magistrate to read aloud a prescribed proclamation:

> "Our Sovereign Lord the King chargeth and commandeth all persons, being assembled, immediately to disperse themselves, and peaceably to depart to their habitations, or to their lawful business, upon the pains contained in the act made in the first year of King George, for preventing tumults and riotous assemblies. God Save the King."

If the crowd failed to disperse within one hour of the reading, they became felons, and lethal force could be used against them.

## In the Novel

The Riot Act is invoked at a critical moment during the Gordon Riots, and its failure is the point at which the disorders become unstoppable. When the soldiers at last confront the mob:

> "The Riot Act was read, but not a man stirred" (049-044)

The ineffectiveness of the Riot Act during the Gordon Riots was, in Dickens's account, not a failure of the law but a failure of will. The Lord Mayor, Brackley Kennett, refused to read the Act when it might have dispersed the initial crowds. By the time the military finally acted, the mob had grown too large and too confident to be intimidated by a proclamation. The soldiers dismounted and made ready to fire, but the reading of the Act — the legal prerequisite for the use of force — was a formality that moved no one (066-026).

## Historical Reality

The Riot Act's failure during the Gordon Riots was historically real. Lord Mayor Kennett's reluctance to read the proclamation was widely condemned, and his subsequent trial for criminal negligence became a landmark case in the law of public order. The Act remained on the statute books until 1973 — a legal dinosaur that had long since been superseded by more modern public order legislation, but whose name entered the English language in the phrase "to read the Riot Act" (meaning to deliver a stern reprimand).

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

The Riot Act represents the thin line between order and chaos in *Barnaby Rudge*. The statute is, on paper, an instrument of absolute authority — one hour after its reading, the state may kill its own citizens with impunity. But authority in the novel is not ultimately a matter of statutes. It is a matter of character. Kennett, who had the power to read the Act, lacked the courage to do so. The soldiers, who had the courage to act, could not do so legally until the Act was read. The gap between legal authority and moral courage is one of the novel's deepest preoccupations.
