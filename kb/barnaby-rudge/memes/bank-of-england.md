## Overview

The Bank of England, founded in 1694 and located on Threadneedle Street in the [[london|City of London]], appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as a symbol of the financial and political establishment threatened by the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. The prospect of the Bank's destruction by [[mob-violence|the mob]] crystallises the scale of the threat the rioters posed to civil society.

## Description

Dickens does not offer a physical description of the Bank building; instead, it enters the novel through speech — imagined as smouldering ruins in the hyperbolic rhetoric of the riot's observers and defenders.

> …fire, made of the ruins of the Bank of England. (038-016)

The phrase captures the apocalyptic fears that the riots provoked among [[london|London]]'s propertied classes. The Bank's mention alongside other pillars of the state reinforces its symbolic weight:

> …of Parliament, the Mint, the Bank of England, and the Judges of the land… (074-003)

## Role in the Narrative

The Bank of England appears twice, both times in lists that enumerate the institutions [[mob-violence|the mob]] threatened. In the first instance (038-016), the imagined ruin of the Bank is part of a rhetorical flourish describing the chaos that unchecked mob violence could unleash. In the second (074-003), the Bank is grouped with [[parliament|Parliament]], the Mint, and the judiciary as institutions the riot targeted — a catalogue of the constitutional order under siege.

Although the Bank is never directly attacked in the novel's action, its presence in these passages serves Dickens's broader theme: the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] were not merely street violence but an assault on the entire fabric of Georgian governance.

## Characters Associated

- [[lord-george-gordon]] — His [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s march triggered the chain of events that brought the rioters to threaten [[london|London]]'s institutions

## Historical Basis

During the actual [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] of June 1780, the Bank of England was indeed considered a potential target. A military guard was posted at the Bank on 7 June 1780, and Wilkes's citizen militia defended it. The attack never materialised — [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s energy was spent on [[imprisonment|Newgate]], Catholic chapels, and the houses of magistrates — but the fear Dickens records was genuine. The Bank on Threadneedle Street that Dickens knew was the 1734 building by George Sampson, later enveloped by Sir John Soane's expansion.
