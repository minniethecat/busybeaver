## Definition

Catholic Emancipation was the gradual process — spanning the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries — by which Roman Catholics in Britain and Ireland were relieved of the legal disabilities imposed by the Penal Laws. These disabilities included exclusion from Parliament, the judiciary, the armed forces, the universities, and the professions, as well as restrictions on property ownership, worship, and education.

## The Novel's Historical Moment

*Barnaby Rudge* is set at the very beginning of this process. The Catholic Relief Act of 1778, sponsored by Sir George Saville, removed only a handful of the most severe restrictions: it allowed Catholics to own and inherit land and to serve in the army upon taking an oath of allegiance. This modest measure — which fell far short of full emancipation — was nonetheless sufficient to trigger the violent backlash that became the Gordon Riots.

Dickens captures the irony of this disproportion: a whisper of reform provoked an apocalypse of violence. The Protestant Association's petition demanded not merely the repeal of the 1778 Act but a reassertion of the full panoply of anti-Catholic legislation. The movement was not content to halt reform; it wanted to reverse history.

## Full Emancipation

Full Catholic Emancipation was not achieved until 1829 — nearly half a century after the Gordon Riots — when the Duke of Wellington's government passed the Roman Catholic Relief Act. That Act admitted Catholics to Parliament and to most public offices, though the monarch is still prohibited from being or marrying a Catholic. The delay was in large part attributable to the trauma of the Gordon Riots themselves: the violence of 1780 convinced generations of British politicians that Catholic relief was too dangerous to contemplate.

## In the Novel's World

Within the novel, the Catholic question is embodied in the figure of Geoffrey Haredale, a Catholic gentleman whose faith places him and his family in mortal danger. Haredale is the living refutation of anti-Catholic paranoia: he is loyal, honorable, and entirely uninterested in subverting the constitution. Yet his Catholicism is sufficient to make him a target.

Gashford, with his probing questions about whether Haredale is "a Catholic gentleman unfortunately" (043-038), represents the political exploitation of sectarian prejudice that Catholic Emancipation's advocates struggled against for decades.
