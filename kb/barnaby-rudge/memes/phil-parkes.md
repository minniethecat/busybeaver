Phil Parkes is the ranger of [[chigwell|Chigwell]], known throughout the novel as "Long Parkes" — a nickname that contrasts his tall, lean frame with the short, stocky figure of his friend [[tom-cobb|Tom Cobb]]. He is one of the three regulars at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn and, like his companions, serves as a marker of ordinary village life persisting through the novel's upheavals.

## Introduction at the Maypole

Parkes is introduced in the novel's opening paragraphs as part of the audience for [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]]'s ghost story. Dickens names him with his occupation and his defining physical characteristic in a single phrase (001-018):

> long Phil Parkes the ranger

His role in the opening scene is that of the practical-minded listener. When [[tom-cobb|Tom Cobb]] whispers "A highwayman!" during the murder story, Parkes offers a more grounded response (001-024):

> "Handsomer than that?" replied Parkes. "It's a better business than highwayman."

## The Maypole Trio

Parkes forms one third of the inseparable [[maypole-inn|Maypole]] trio with [[tom-cobb|Tom Cobb]] and [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]]. The three men are differentiated by physique and temperament: [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]] is the storyteller, small and precise; [[tom-cobb|Tom Cobb]] is short and impressionable; and Parkes is tall, pragmatic, and given to dry observations. [[john-willet|John Willet]] notes in Chapter 54 that Parkes is the "adventurous" one (054-009), willing to venture opinions that others only think.

In Chapter 33, the three men's dynamics are on full display during the anniversary of the murder (033-055):

> "Who!" cried Parkes and Tom Cobb, looking eagerly by turns at Solomon Daisy.

Parkes contributes to the village lore with his own ghost story — he "had had a ghost in his family" (033-059) — establishing himself as a minor authority on supernatural matters.

## Function in the Novel

Parkes, along with Cobb and Daisy, represents the stable, comic baseline of the novel. Through the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], the murder mystery, and the various romantic entanglements, [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] trio endures. In Chapter 82, the novel's final pages, they are still making their regular visits (082-014):

> To this, his new habitation, Tom Cobb, Phil Parkes, and Solomon Daisy went regularly every night.

This continuity is Dickens's quiet affirmation that English village life — with its predictable rhythms, minor personalities, and fireside storytelling — survives the worst that history can throw at it.
