## Historical Biography

Mary I (1516–1558) was the first queen regnant of England, reigning from 1553 until her death. The daughter of Henry VIII and Catherine of Aragon, she reversed the Protestant Reformation of her half-brother Edward VI and restored Roman Catholicism as the state religion. During her reign, nearly three hundred religious dissenters were burned at the stake, earning her the enduring epithet "Bloody Mary." Her marriage to Philip II of Spain was deeply unpopular, and her reign was marked by economic difficulty and military failure, including the loss of Calais in 1558. She died childless and was succeeded by her Protestant half-sister, Elizabeth I.

## Role in *Barnaby Rudge*

In *Barnaby Rudge*, "Bloody Mary" is invoked not as a historical personage in her own right but as a rhetorical symbol wielded by both sides of the anti-Catholic agitation. During the build-up to the Gordon Riots, Dickens shows how Mary's name was appropriated by the Protestant clubs that sprang up around the "No Popery" movement. John Willet, the landlord of the Maypole Inn, wearily attempts to shut down a discussion of the topic:

> where's the use of talking of Bloody Mary, under such circumstances as the present (035-026)

Nevertheless, the name proves inescapable. The rioting crowd itself is described in terms that evoke Mary's memory, and Dickens lists "The Associated Rememberers of Bloody Mary" among the proliferating Protestant societies (036-020), at half a guinea membership. The mob's captain, Hugh, later invokes the cry that "that woman Bloody Mary" must be answered with action (043-065). In the novel's treatment, Mary functions less as a person than as a dark totem — a name the crowd brandishes to justify their destruction.

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens does not attempt a portrait of Mary I as a character; her appearance in the novel is purely symbolic. The historical Mary's record of religious persecution needs no embellishment, and Dickens uses her epithet as shorthand for the sectarian hatred that fuels the Gordon Riots. The novel accurately reflects the historical reality that eighteenth-century anti-Catholic agitation routinely invoked Mary's reign as the archetype of Catholic tyranny. The "No Popery" clubs named after her were a genuine feature of the period's political landscape.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

The repeated invocation of Bloody Mary throughout the riot chapters serves Dickens's central theme: that mob violence feeds on symbols rather than reason. The crowd does not engage with the theology of Catholicism or the political merits of Catholic emancipation; it chants a name and burns houses. Mary's spectral presence — invoked alongside "blue cockades" and "No Popery" slogans — encapsulates how historical grievances, stripped of context, become fuel for present violence.
