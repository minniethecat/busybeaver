## Overview

Southwark — the ancient borough on the southern bank of the Thames, "not far from [[london-bridge|London Bridge]]" (005-001) — plays a double role in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*: it is both the birthplace of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] and the staging ground for their suppression. On its open ground at [[st-georges-fields|St George's Fields]], the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] gathered its thousands; in its streets and inns, the fugitive and the stranger alike could lose themselves.

Southwark's position outside the [[london|City of London]] — literally across the river, beyond the jurisdiction of the City authorities — made it a natural gathering place for dissent. It was here that [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] convened the mass meetings that preceded the march on [[parliament|Parliament]], and it was here that "no fewer than three thousand" troops (073-006) were quartered to finally crush the violence that those meetings unleashed. In the novel's geography, Southwark is both cause and cure: the place where the riot gestates and the place from which the counter-force deploys.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens's Southwark appears first as a place of obscure lodging — a "by-street in Southwark, not far from [[london-bridge|London Bridge]]" (005-001) — where the stranger can pass unnoticed. This is the novel's characteristic mode for Southwark: it is a borough of transit, of incomplete visibility, of identities that shift with each change of lodging.

The crossing of [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] into Southwark is rendered as a passage into a different urban world:

> He crossed London Bridge and passed into Southwark. As he glided down a bye street. (016-023)

The verb "glided" captures the furtiveness of movement in a district where one does not wish to be seen. Southwark's streets, narrower and less policed than those of the City, offer cover. Throughout the novel, characters who need to disappear — the fugitive, the conspirator, the man with a secret — gravitate toward Southwark as naturally as water finds a drain.

The most vivid single image of Southwark in the novel is the "obscure inn in the Borough, where he was quite unknown" (082-009). The inn is a place of last resort, the final anonymity before the novel's resolution. Southwark in this sense is the city's back room — the space where stories end, where characters who can no longer sustain their public identities fade into the general obscurity of the trans-Thames borough.

> In Southwark, no fewer than three thousand men were quartered. (073-006)

The concentration of military force in the borough is the decisive administrative fact of the riots' suppression. Three thousand soldiers — a number that dwarfs the entire population of [[chigwell|Chigwell]] — are the answer to the question the novel has been asking since [[mob-violence|the mob]] first gathered at [[st-georges-fields|St George's Fields]]: how much force does it take to restore order once order has been broken?

## Role in the Narrative

Southwark's narrative function is tied to its geographical and jurisdictional peculiarity. As a borough outside the City walls, it was subject to different magistrates, different policing, different norms of public order. The [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] exploited this jurisdictional gap: a gathering that the City authorities might have suppressed could be held with relative impunity in Southwark's open fields.

The novel's riot narrative thus begins in Southwark. The [[march-on-parliament|March on Parliament]] originates from the mass meeting at [[st-georges-fields|St George's Fields]], and the procession that crosses [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] into the City carries the political energy that will, within days, become wholesale destruction. Southwark is the cradle of the violence — and, in the novel's symmetry, also its grave. The troops that finally suppress the riots are quartered in Southwark, and it is from Southwark that they deploy across the bridge to clear the City's streets.

The division of [[london|London]] into military divisions during the riot crisis — "the [[london|London]], the [[westminster|Westminster]], the Southwark, and the Scotch" (049-001) — recognises Southwark as a distinct command, confirming its administrative separateness from the City. The "borough of Southwark" (073-003) is a jurisdiction in its own right, and its response to the crisis — the concentration of three thousand soldiers — is the decisive military fact of the riots' final phase.

## Historical Basis

Southwark in the eighteenth century was a distinct entity from the [[london|City of London]] — a borough in the county of Surrey with its own administration, its own magistrates, and its own relationship to the Crown. Its position on the southern bank of the Thames, connected to the City only by [[london-bridge|London Bridge]], gave it a strategic importance out of proportion to its size. Anyone seeking to enter or leave [[london|London]] from the south had to pass through Southwark.

The historical [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] did indeed begin with a mass meeting on St George's Fields in Southwark on 2 June 1780. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] addressed a crowd estimated at between 40,000 and 60,000 people — an extraordinary number for a city of 750,000 — and led them in procession across [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] toward the [[parliament|Houses of Parliament]]. The petition they carried, demanding the repeal of the Catholic Relief Act, was the proximate cause of the riots that followed.

Southwark was also the site of several [[imprisonment|prison]]s attacked during the riots — the Borough Clink in Tooley Street was burned (067-012), and the King's Bench Prison was threatened. The area's concentration of [[imprisonment|prison]]s, inns, and open ground made it a natural flashpoint: large crowds could gather without immediate suppression, and the targets — Catholic chapels, magistrates' houses, symbols of authority — were within easy reach.

The military response to the riots was, as Dickens records, concentrated in Southwark. The borough's open spaces — St George's Fields and the areas around the Elephant and Castle — became encampments for the troops that were eventually deployed to clear the City. The figure of three thousand soldiers (073-006) is historically plausible: contemporary accounts record that several regiments of regular infantry and cavalry, supplemented by militia units and the volunteer associations of the City and [[westminster|Westminster]], were needed to restore order.
