## Overview

The militia was a part-time military force, distinct from the regular army, composed of local men raised by county and subject to call-up in times of civil emergency. During the Gordon Riots of June 1780, the government mobilized militia battalions to supplement the regular troops — the Horse Guards and Foot Guards — who were struggling to contain the widespread disorder across London. The militia's deployment represented the broadest possible assertion of state military power against the civilian population.

## Historical Background

The militia system in eighteenth-century England was governed by the Militia Acts, which required each county to raise a quota of men by ballot. Militiamen trained periodically and could be embodied for active service during invasion threats or civil unrest. By 1780, the militia was a well-established institution, though its effectiveness varied by county. The Northumberland Militia, specifically mentioned by Dickens, was one of the county regiments called to London during the crisis.

## Role in the Novel

The militia enters *Barnaby Rudge* at the climax of the military escalation. As Dickens catalogues the forces assembled to crush the riots, the militia appears alongside the regular army in a comprehensive display of state power:

> five battalions of Militia were under arms; the Tower was fortified, the drawbridge was raised (067-003)

The specific mention of "the Northumberland Militia, under the command of Lord Algernon Percy" (067-010) anchors the fictional narrative in a verifiable historical detail — the real regiment and its real commander. The militia's presence signals that the crisis has exhausted all ordinary remedies. Unlike the Horse Guards, who are a permanent London presence, the militia must be summoned from the counties, and their arrival represents the government's acknowledgment that the situation requires forces beyond the metropolitan garrison.

## Key Members

The militia appears as an institution rather than through named characters. Their commander, Lord Algernon Percy, is mentioned in passing, but the militia's narrative function is collective rather than individual.

## Significance

The militia's deployment completes the novel's anatomy of state power under stress. The progression from civil magistrates to constables to Horse Guards to Foot Guards to militia battalions traces an escalating series of failures: each level of authority proves insufficient, requiring the next, heavier intervention. The militia — citizen-soldiers drawn from the shires — represent the final extension of the state's reach, the point at which the government treats a domestic political movement as though it were an invading army.
