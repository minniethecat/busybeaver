## Overview

The Foundling Hospital was a historic charitable institution in [[london|London]], established in the eighteenth century by Thomas Coram for the care of abandoned children. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, the hospital itself is not visited or described directly; rather, it serves as a geographical landmark for locating [[the-boot|The Boot]] — a solitary tavern situated "in the fields at the back of the Foundling Hospital" (038-037) that becomes an important gathering place for the [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] rioters.

## Description

Dickens provides no physical description of the Foundling Hospital itself, using it purely as a locational reference. The area behind the hospital is characterised as "a very solitary spot at that period, and quite deserted after dark" (038-037). The hospital's presence anchors the geography of the scene: [[the-boot|The Boot]] tavern "stood at some distance from any high road, and was approachable only by a dark and narrow lane" (038-037), making it an ideal clandestine meeting place precisely because of its isolation in the fields behind the institution.

## Role in the Narrative

The Foundling Hospital appears in the novel indirectly, through its function of locating [[the-boot|The Boot]]. In Chapter 38, after the first night of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], the hangman [[dennis|Dennis]] proposes to [[hugh|Hugh]] that they "repair together to [[the-boot|The Boot]], where there was good company and strong liquor" (038-036). Dickens then establishes the tavern's location "in the fields at the back of the Foundling Hospital" (038-037), a detail that conveys both the remoteness and the disreputable character of the establishment.

[[the-boot|The Boot]] proves to be a nest of rioters and conspirators: [[hugh|Hugh]] is "much surprised to find several people drinking there, and great merriment going on," and still more surprised "to find among them almost every face that had caught his attention in the crowd" (038-037). [[dennis|Dennis]] advises [[hugh|Hugh]] that "it was not considered good manners at [[the-boot|The Boot]] to appear at all curious about the company" (038-037), reinforcing the tavern's character as a place where the normal rules of social inquiry are suspended.

The hospital itself serves a symbolic function by contrast: a charitable institution devoted to the care of innocent children stands adjacent to a public house that shelters those who would burn, loot, and destroy. The juxtaposition of the Foundling Hospital's mission with the violence planned at [[the-boot|The Boot]] creates an implicit moral commentary on the proximity of benevolence and brutality in the [[london|London]] of the period.
