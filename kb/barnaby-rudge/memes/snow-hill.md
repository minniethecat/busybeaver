## Overview

Snow Hill is a street in the [[holborn|Holborn]] district of [[london|London]], descending from the high ground of [[holborn|Holborn Hill]] down towards [[holborn|Holborn Bridge]] and [[fleet-market|Fleet Market]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it becomes one of the principal battlegrounds of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] on their fourth and most terrible night, raked by continuous volleys of musket-fire from a large detachment of soldiers stationed at [[holborn|Holborn Bridge]].

## Description in the Novel

Dickens presents Snow Hill not through leisurely topographical description but through the chaos and terror of the riot. It appears as part of the concentration of violence around [[holborn|Holborn Bridge]], where two great streams of [[mob-violence|the mob]] converge:

> At Holborn Bridge, and on Holborn Hill, the confusion was greater than in any other part; for the crowd that poured out of the city in two great streams, one by Ludgate Hill, and one by Newgate Street, united at that spot, and formed a mass so dense, that at every volley the people seemed to fall in heaps. (067-014)

The military response was indiscriminate and devastating:

> At this place a large detachment of soldiery were posted, who fired, now up Fleet Market, now up Holborn, now up Snow Hill — constantly raking the streets in each direction. (067-014)

The three streets — [[fleet-market|Fleet Market]], [[holborn|Holborn]], and Snow Hill — form a triangle of fire, with the soldiers positioned at the apex. The passage describes a slaughter-ground where "the terrors of that terrible night seemed to be concentrated in one spot" (067-014).

## Role in the Narrative

Snow Hill is the focal point of the novel's most sustained depiction of the riots' violence. It is here that [[hugh|Hugh]] makes his famous mounted charge — "Full twenty times" (067-015) — leading the rioters against the soldiers, riding a brewer's horse caparisoned with fetters taken from [[imprisonment|Newgate]], wielding an axe. The repeated assaults and repulses, the flames of nearby fires, and the volleys of the soldiers combine to create a scene of apocalyptic urban warfare.

Snow Hill's role in the novel is thus not that of a setting in the conventional sense — it is the arena where the riots reach their climax of destructive fury, and where the military response to [[mob-violence|the mob]] becomes a massacre. The street name itself, with its suggestion of wintry blankness, becomes ironically appropriate to the scene of death that Dickens paints.
