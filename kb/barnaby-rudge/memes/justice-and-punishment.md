## Definition

Justice in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* operates on two registers: the flawed machinery of 18th-century English law, and the providential justice that exceeds human institutions. The novel is bookended by executions — the preface recounts the hanging of a woman for passing a forged note (P03-011), and the final chapters stage the mass trials and executions of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]' participants. Between these poles, Dickens examines punishment in its legal, psychological, and cosmic forms, suggesting that the law's justice is partial and sometimes cruel, while true justice emerges only through the pattern of events — what one character calls "a punishment along with it" that is self-inflicted (027-009).

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Law's Machinery

The novel presents the 18th-century English legal system as both an instrument of order and a theater of cruelty. The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]' aftermath sees "the law, which will assuredly sentence him to suffer death" deployed against the rioters (062-060). But Dickens's treatment of the trials is deeply ambivalent: the law restores order, yet the executions are public spectacles that mirror the riot's own violence. The hanging of [[dennis|Dennis]], the state executioner, by [[mob-violence|the mob]] collapses the distinction between judicial and extralegal punishment — the hangman becomes the hanged, and law's authority is rendered indistinguishable from the crowd's [[revenge|vengeance]].

### Punishment as Self-Inflicted Wound

The novel's most distinctive contribution to the theme is the idea that the worst punishment is self-generated. [[barnaby-rudge-senior|Barnaby Rudge Senior]]'s twenty-seven years of [[deception|concealment]] at the [[the-warren|Warren]] constitute a punishment that "exceeds his self-inflicted punishment" (065-006) — the murderer im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed not by law but by his own [[innocence|guilt]]. [[simon-tappertit|Sim]]ilarly, [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]'s death at the hands of his illegitimate son [[hugh|Hugh]] is a punishment he authored himself through a lifetime of cynical abandonment. Dickens suggests that the novel's true judgments are not pronounced by judges but written into character and consequence.

### The Trial of Conscience

The legal trials that follow the riots are, in Dickens's treatment, secondary to the trials of conscience that precede them. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] the younger, sentenced to death for his unwitting participation in the riot, becomes the object of efforts to secure a [[forgiveness|pardon]] — a test of the law's capacity for [[forgiveness|mercy]]. His mother's plea is "but justice" to her "joint exertions" (019-048), a justice that exists outside the courtroom and depends entirely on human [[sympathy|compassion]] rather than legal code.
