## Overview

The Strand — the historic connective artery between the [[london|City of London]] and the seat of government at [[westminster|Westminster]] — appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* in three sharply contrasting modes: as the emblem of metropolitan noise against which the [[temple|Temple]]'s quiet is defined; as the route by which [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] returns from [[parliament|Parliament]] to his house in [[welbeck-street|Welbeck Street]]; and as the street where [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] walks alone, too proud to seek shelter, after the destruction of his home. The Strand is never a destination — it is always a passage, and the manner of a character's passage along it reveals their condition.

## Description in the Novel

The Strand first enters the novel by way of contrast. In one of the most celebrated descriptive passages in the book — the meditation on the Temple's cloistered quiet — the Strand is invoked as the antithesis of everything the Temple represents:

> There is yet a drowsiness in its courts, and a dreamy dullness in its trees and gardens; those who pace its lanes and squares may yet hear the echoes of their footsteps on the sounding stones, and read upon its gates, in passing from the tumult of the Strand or Fleet Street, "Who enters here leaves noise behind." (015-004)

The "tumult of the Strand" is a single phrase, but it carries the weight of a world. The Strand is noise, bustle, commerce, crowds — everything the Temple's gates exclude. Dickens does not need to describe the Strand; he can define it purely by what the Temple is not. The "echoes of footsteps" within the Temple's courts are intelligible precisely because the Strand's noise — omnipresent, just beyond the gates — provides the silence that makes echoes possible.

When the Strand appears as a literal route, it is equally terse:

> So along the Strand, up Swallow Street, into the Oxford Road, and thence to his house in Welbeck Street, near Cavendish Square. (037-032)

[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s progress along the Strand — accompanied by "a few dozen idlers" — is rendered as a list of street names, each stage of the journey reducing the crowd until, by the time he reaches his doorstep, only the formulaic dismissal "Gentlemen, [[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]. Good day. God bless you" remains. The Strand here is the first leg of a journey from public performance to domestic retreat. It is the widest, most public stage of the route — the place where [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] is most visible, most accompanied, and most the leader of a movement.

The final appearance of the Strand is the most desolate:

> Feeling that he might have anticipated this occurrence, after what he had seen at Chigwell in the morning, where no man dared to touch a spade, though he offered a large reward to all who would come and dig among the ruins of his house, he walked along the Strand; too proud to expose himself to another refusal, and of too generous a spirit to involve in distress or ruin any honest tradesman who might be weak enough to give him shelter. (066-006)

[[geoffrey-haredale|Mr Haredale]] on the Strand is the novel's image of absolute isolation. He walks along one of the busiest streets in [[london|London]] — a thoroughfare thronged with people — and yet he is entirely alone. The "tumult of the Strand" that defined the Temple by contrast in Chapter 15 has become, in Chapter 66, the medium of [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]'s isolation. The crowd that surrounds him offers no companionship; it is an absence made visible.

## Role in the Narrative

The Strand functions in the novel as a measure of what surrounds it. In Chapter 15, it measures the Temple's quiet: the cloister is defined by the noise it excludes. In Chapter 37, it measures [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s trajectory: the public man diminishes as he travels from the Strand (public, broad, crowded) to [[welbeck-street|Welbeck Street]] (private, narrow, domestic). In Chapter 66, it measures [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]'s despair: a man can be in the middle of the Strand and be utterly cut off from the human world.

This is a characteristic Dickensian technique — the street as emotional barometer. The Strand is not described; it is inhabited. Each character who walks it brings a different Strand into being: the idler's Strand, the politician's Strand, the ruined man's Strand. The street is constant; what changes is what it means to be seen there.

The Strand's geographical position — between the City and [[westminster|Westminster]] — makes it the natural route for any character moving between [[london|London]]'s two poles of power (commerce and government). [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s procession along the Strand toward [[welbeck-street|Welbeck Street]] is a movement away from [[westminster|Westminster]] (where he has presented his petition) toward the domestic sphere where the conspiracy is actually managed. [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]]'s walk along the Strand, by contrast, is aimless — he is "walked along" (the passive construction is deliberate), propelled not by purpose but by the impossibility of staying still.

## Historical Basis

The Strand in the eighteenth century was one of [[london|London]]'s principal thoroughfares, running from [[temple-bar|Temple Bar]] (the western boundary of the City) to [[charing-cross|Charing Cross]] and thence to Whitehall and [[westminster|Westminster]]. Its name derives from the Old English "strond", meaning the bank of a river — the street originally ran along the Thames's northern shore before the construction of the Victoria Embankment in the 1860s pushed the river back. By 1780, the Strand was lined with shops, taverns, coffee houses, and the townhouses of the nobility — a continuous ribbon of commerce and fashion connecting the City's financial power to [[westminster|Westminster]]'s political authority.

During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], the Strand was one of the principal routes along which [[mob-violence|the mob]] moved between the [[parliament|Houses of Parliament]] and the City. Its width and prominence made it a natural stage for political demonstration, and contemporary accounts record the passage of large bodies of rioters along the Strand on several nights of the disturbances. It was also along the Strand that troops were deployed to protect the mansions of the wealthy from the spreading violence.

Dickens, who knew the Strand intimately from his own years as a parliamentary reporter and from his walks through [[london|London]], uses the street's symbolic resonance economically. To walk the Strand in Dickens's [[london|London]] is to be on display — and whether one welcomes that display ([[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]], attended by his idlers) or dreads it ([[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]], "too proud to expose himself") is an index of character.
