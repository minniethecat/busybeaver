## Overview

Thames Street is a historic thoroughfare in the [[london|City of London]], running along the north bank of the River Thames from Tower Hill to [[blackfriars|Blackfriars]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it appears as a commercial district of cellars, warehouses, and merchants.

## Description

Dickens mentions Thames Street without elaborate description, treating it as a known quantity to his contemporary readers — a working mercantile street in the old City, associated with wine storage and trade.

> …in some deep cellars hard by Thames Street, and who was as purple-faced… (013-031)

The street's warehouses serve as a marker of commercial identity:

> …fitted on a ware'us-door in Thames Street. (024-020)

During the riots, Thames Street reappears in connection with a vintner whose premises were threatened by the advancing mob:

> …le-faced vintner, formerly of Thames Street, now looked him in the face… (067-028)

## Role in the Narrative

Thames Street functions as local colour — one of the many streets that establish the novel's densely realised [[london|London]] geography. Its appearances are tied to characters on the margins of the main action: a purple-faced cellar-dweller (013-031), a warehouse fitter (024-020), a displaced vintner (067-028). Together, these brief mentions sketch a world of City trade that forms the backdrop to the novel's main events.

## Characters Associated

- An unnamed purple-faced man — A minor figure associated with Thames Street cellars (013-031)
- A vintner formerly of Thames Street — Displaced during the riots (067-028)

## Historical Basis

Thames Street was one of the oldest streets in the [[london|City of London]], mentioned in records as early as the 12th century. In the 18th century, it was lined with wharves, warehouses, and merchants' premises, particularly those connected with the wine and coal trades. The cellars and warehouses Dickens references were characteristic of the street's commercial character. Much of Thames Street was destroyed in the Blitz and subsequently rebuilt as Upper and Lower Thames Street.
