## Overview

The Catholic Relief Act of 1778 (18 Geo. 3, c. 60), formally titled the Papists Act, was landmark legislation sponsored by Sir George Saville that repealed certain provisions of the Penal Laws against Roman Catholics. It allowed Catholics to own property, inherit land, and join the army without taking the oath of supremacy — concessions that had been impossible in Britain since the reign of William III.

The Act was intended as a moderate measure of religious toleration and a practical necessity: the government needed Catholic recruits for the army during the American War of Independence. Instead, it provoked the most destructive urban riot in British history.

## The Backlash

The Act's passage through Parliament enraged Protestant opinion in England and Scotland. While the bill removed only a fraction of the anti-Catholic statutes on the books, its symbolism was explosive. Lord George Gordon emerged as the leader of the opposition, becoming president of the Protestant Association and gathering a petition with tens of thousands of signatures demanding repeal.

In the novel, Dickens captures the paranoia the Act generated:

> "the penal laws against Roman Catholic priests, the penalty of perpetual imprisonment" (037-002)

The irony Dickens emphasizes is that the Act had barely touched these harshest provisions. What the anti-Catholic mob imagined as a Catholic takeover was, in reality, a modest reduction of statutory persecution.

## The Petition and the Riots

On 2 June 1780, Lord George Gordon led a procession of an estimated 40,000–60,000 supporters to the House of Commons to present the petition for repeal. When the Commons refused to debate the petition immediately, the crowd turned violent, beginning six days of destruction that targeted Catholic chapels, the houses of Catholic families, the homes of magistrates, and ultimately Newgate Prison itself.

The novel dramatizes this sequence: Gordon's procession, the rejection of the petition, and the descent into mob rule that "No Popery" slogans could not contain.

## Aftermath

In the novel, Saville is mentioned with particular venom by the Protestant Association. Gashford speaks of "Saville, who introduced the bill" as a target for their "worst wishes" (040-062). The Act was never repealed — the riots ultimately strengthened the government's resolve against mob intimidation — but the trauma of June 1780 remained a cautionary tale about the volatility of sectarian politics.
