## Overview

Barbican is a district in the Cripplegate area of [[london|London]], just north of the old city wall. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, it is the location of the cellar where [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]]'s 'Prentice Knights hold their nocturnal meetings — a secret society of discontented apprentices who dream of overturning the social order. The Barbican of Dickens's novel is a warren of narrow alleys, blind courts, and decaying tenements, a suitable underworld for a conspiracy of the disenfranchised.

## Description in the Novel

Barbican is presented as a place of degradation and [[deception|concealment]], far removed from the respectable streets of [[clerkenwell|Clerkenwell]]:

> Half pausing for an instant now and then to smite his pocket and assure himself of the safety of his master key, he hurried on to Barbican, and turning into one of the narrowest of the narrow streets which diverged from that centre, slackened his pace and wiped his heated brow, as if the termination of his walk were near at hand. (008-002)

The physical environment is described as threatening to the stranger:

> It was not a very choice spot for midnight expeditions, being in truth one of more than questionable character, and of an appearance by no means inviting. From the main street he had entered, itself little better than an alley, a low-browed doorway led into a blind court, or yard, profoundly dark, unpaved, and reeking with stagnant odours. (008-003)

The 'Prentice Knights' meeting place is identified by "the rude effigy of a bottle" swinging from a "defaced and rotten front" (008-003) — an image of decay and dissolution that mirrors the moral character of the conspiracy beneath.

At dawn, the meeting breaks up to the sound of crowing cocks:

> It wanted little more than an hour of day, and all the cocks in Barbican had already begun to crow, as if their lives depended on it. (008-076)

## Role in the Narrative

Barbican is the spatial embodiment of the novel's preoccupation with hidden conspiracies and secret societies. Just as the [[maypole-inn|Maypole Inn]] represents the public, convivial England of the coaching era, Barbican's blind courts and cellar rooms represent its dark underside — a world of class resentment, male violence, and revolutionary fantasy that will erupt into the open during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]].

The 'Prentice Knights, with their absurd titles, passwords, and rituals, are partly comic — [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]]'s pompous self-importance is a source of satire. But the darkness of Barbican is real. The cellar keeper, [[stagg|Stagg]], is a blind man who traffics in information and conspiracy, and the apprentices' grievances, however ridiculous their expression, are rooted in genuine social tensions. Barbican is where the novel's comedy of manners shades into something more dangerous.
