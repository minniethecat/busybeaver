## Overview

Fleet Market was a sprawling marketplace in 18th-century [[london|London]], occupying the centre of what is now Farringdon Street. It appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* both as a minor early reference — the place where scraps for penny-broth were bought — and, far more significantly, as a gathering-point and stronghold for the [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] rioters during the novel's central insurrection sequence. [[hugh|Hugh]], [[dennis|Dennis]], and [[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]] repair there after learning that [[the-boot|The Boot]] has been taken by soldiers, and it remains the last re[[suspicion|doubt]] of the rioters before the military finally disperses them.

## Description

Dickens provides a vivid, unflattering portrait of Fleet Market in Chapter 60:

> Fleet Market, at that time, was a long irregular row of wooden sheds and penthouses, occupying the centre of what is now called Farringdon Street. They were jumbled together in a most unsightly fashion, in the middle of the road; to the great obstruction of the thoroughfare and the annoyance of passengers, who were fain to make their way, as they best could, among carts, baskets, barrows, trucks, casks, bulks, and benches, and to jostle with porters, hucksters, wagoners, and a motley crowd of buyers, sellers, pickpockets, vagrants, and idlers. The air was perfumed with the stench of rotten leaves and faded fruit; the refuse of the butchers' stalls, and offal and garbage of a hundred kinds. (060-008)

The description combines physical disarray with sensory assault — visual confusion, obstruction, and olfactory offence. Dickens's concluding remark — that "it was indispensable to most public conveniences in those days, that they should be public nuisances likewise" — extends the particular to a general satire of 18th-century civic life (060-008).

## Role in the Narrative

Fleet Market enters the novel modestly, as the source of cheap scraps from which a character retails pennyworths of broth (008-078). It returns with far greater narrative weight in the riot chapters.

When [[hugh|Hugh]], [[dennis|Dennis]], and Tappertit learn that [[the-boot|The Boot]] has been occupied by soldiers and that [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] has been taken to [[imprisonment|Newgate]], [[hugh|Hugh]] yields to his companions' arguments and agrees to "turn back and to repair to Fleet Market; for which place, it seemed, a few of their boldest associates had shaped their course, on receiving the same intelligence" (060-006). The market's sheds and baskets serve as makeshift beds and potential barricades, making it a natural rallying-point for the dispersed rioters.

During the military suppression, Fleet Market becomes a principal theatre of action. At [[holborn|Holborn Bridge]], soldiers are "posted, who fired, now up Fleet Market, now up [[holborn|Holborn]], now up [[snow-hill|Snow Hill]] — constantly raking the streets in each direction" (067-014). The soldiers subsequently "advanced into Fleet Market and were dispersing the people there" (067-018), clearing the way for the final assault on the rioters' positions.

Fleet Market's most telling appearance comes when [[hugh|Hugh]], captured and bound, is riding towards [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]. Dickens writes that "when they got into [[london|London]], and more especially into Fleet Market, lately the stronghold of the rioters, where the military were rooting out the last remnant of the crowd, he saw that this hope was gone, and felt that he was riding to his death" (069-091). The market here serves as a symbolic marker — the place where collective insurrection gives way to individual [[fate|doom]].

## Historical Basis

Fleet Market occupied the course of the River Fleet, which had been covered over in the 1730s. The road above — originally called Fleet Market, later renamed Farringdon Street — housed an open-air market that operated until the 1820s. Dickens, writing in 1841, could expect his readers to remember the old market, demolished less than two decades earlier. Its reputation as a disorderly, malodorous space was well established in contemporary accounts.
