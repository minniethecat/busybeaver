## Definition

Prejudice in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* operates at every level of the social order: religious (anti-Catholicism), class (gentleman versus commoner), and personal (the snap judgments that define characters before they speak). Dickens systematically exposes how prejudice reduces individuals to categories, making violence and injustice possible. The novel's most devastating critique is not of the rioters' bigotry but of the polite prejudice of men like [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], who use social convention as a weapon while maintaining an appearance of civility.

## Historical Context

The late 18th century saw religious prejudice encoded in English law. Catholics could not vote, hold public office, or practice their faith openly. The Catholic Relief Act of 1778, which the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] formed to oppose, removed only a fraction of these restrictions. The [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] were the most violent expression of anti-Catholic prejudice in English history. Dickens situates his novel within this specific historical moment to explore how institutional prejudice becomes mob violence.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Religious Prejudice

The "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" cry is prejudice in its purest form — a slogan adopted by men who could not articulate a single theological objection to Catholicism. The hangman [[dennis|Dennis]] declares himself "a [[anti-catholicism|No-Popery]] man" and is "ready to be sworn in for the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]" without knowing "what it means, or where it comes from" (038-013). The rioters chalk "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" on doors as a mark of conformity rather than conviction (051-036), exposing the social rather than religious nature of their prejudice.

### Class Prejudice

[[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] embodies the prejudice of the ruling class. His objection to [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]] has nothing to do with her character and everything to do with her family's social standing and religion. When he tells his son that "all men are [[fate|fortune]]-hunters," he is projecting his own class prejudice onto all humanity — universalizing his own narrowness to excuse it (015-052). [[john-willet|John Willet]]'s treatment of his son [[joe-willet|Joe]] is class prejudice writ small: the father who cannot see past his own status as landlord to recognize his son's worth.

### Prejudice Against Disability

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] is defined by others' prejudice before he speaks a word. Called "[[barnaby-rudge|the idiot]]" as though it were his name, he is judged incapable of moral agency by those whose moral agency is far more questionable. The novel's arc — [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] condemned to death for actions he could not fully comprehend, while the calculating [[gashford|Gashford]] escapes — is a sustained critique of a society that punishes disability while excusing conscious evil.

## Key Characters

- **[[Sir John Chester]]** — The novel's most polished bigot, whose class prejudice is masked by impeccable manners.
- **[[Lord George Gordon]]** — The figurehead of religious prejudice who, Dickens suggests, is himself the tool of more cynical men.
- **[[Geoffrey Haredale]]** — A Catholic gentleman who [[innocence|experience]]s prejudice from both sides: stigmatized by the Protestant majority, yet capable of his own narrow judgments.
- **[[John Willet]]** — The comic face of prejudice, whose self-importance prevents him from seeing anyone else's worth.
- **[[Simon Tappertit]]** — The apprentice whose social resentment is a form of inverted prejudice, judging all gentlemen by his [[innocence|experience]] of his master.

## Key Scenes

1. **[[dennis|Dennis]]'s Initiation** (038-013): The hangman joins the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] without understanding the bill he is opposing — a distillation of prejudice as unexamined [[loyalty|allegiance]].

2. **Chester's Fortune-Hunter Speech** (015-052): Sir John universalizes his own cynicism to justify blocking his son's marriage — class prejudice dressed as worldly wisdom.

3. **Chalk on the Door** (051-036): The marking of Catholic houses with "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" as a protective measure reveals how quickly prejudice becomes an instrument of coercion.

4. **[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] on Trial**: The legal system's prejudice against mental disability, treating [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] as fully culpable for actions he could not fully understand.
