## Overview

Temple Bar was a historic stone gateway that stood at the western boundary of the [[london|City of London]], where [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]] meets [[strand|the Strand]]. Designed by Christopher Wren and built in 1672, it marked the ceremonial divide between the [[london|City of London]] and the City of [[westminster|Westminster]]. In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, Temple Bar serves as a notable [[london|London]] landmark — both as a geographical reference point for the city's commercial character and, more distinctively, as a forbidden target in the secret oath of [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]]'s apprentice society.

## Description

Dickens references Temple Bar as a fixed point in [[london|London]]'s topography. In describing the character of the city's thoroughfares, he notes that "some of the shops, especially those to the eastward of Temple Bar, still adhered to the old practice of hanging out a sign" (016-004). This detail situates Temple Bar as a dividing line within the city's commercial geography: to the east, within the old City, the medieval tradition of hanging shop signs persisted, while westward toward [[westminster|Westminster]], more modern practices prevailed. The creaking of these signs "in their iron frames on windy nights, formed a strange and mournful concert" (016-004), contributing to the novel's evocation of old [[london|London]]'s distinctive sensory landscape.

## Role in the Narrative

Temple Bar plays its most memorable role in Chapter 8, during the elaborate ceremony in which [[simon-tappertit|Sim Tappertit]] inducts a new member into his secret society of apprentices — the 'Prentice Knights. Tappertit, in his grandiose address, describes "the oath which every member of that small remnant of a noble body took, and which was of a dreadful and impressive kind" (008-055). The oath binds each apprentice, among other rebellious commitments, "not on any account, in case the fullness of time should bring a general rising of 'prentices, to damage or in any way disfigure Temple Bar, which was strictly constitutional and always to be approached with reverence" (008-055).

This comic exemption of Temple Bar from the otherwise comprehensive programme of insubordination is characteristic of Dickens's satirical treatment of Tappertit's pretensions. In a society whose stated goals include resisting the Lord Mayor, despising the sheriffs, and holding the court of aldermen "as nought" (008-055), the solemn protection of a single stone gateway is presented with deadpan gravity. The passage simultaneously mocks the apprentice's grandiose political fantasies and preserves Temple Bar as a symbol of constitutional order — a landmark so fundamental that even imaginary revolutionaries must honour it.

The reference in Chapter 16 is more matter-of-fact, using Temple Bar as a geographical marker to distinguish the older, sign-hanging shops of the City from the rest of [[london|London]].

## Historical Basis

Temple Bar was a real gateway that stood at the junction of [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]] and [[strand|the Strand]] from 1672 until 1878, when it was dismantled to ease traffic congestion. It was indeed the ceremonial boundary of the [[london|City of London]], where the monarch would traditionally pause to request the Lord Mayor's permission to enter the City. The gateway was later re-erected at Theobalds Park in Hertfordshire, and in 2004 was returned to [[london|London]], where it now stands beside St Paul's Cathedral. Dickens's reference to shops "to the eastward of Temple Bar" (016-004) reflects the genuine distinction between the commercial character of the City and [[westminster|Westminster]] in the eighteenth century.
