## Overview

Blackfriars is a district of the [[london|City of London]] on the north bank of the Thames, named after the medieval Dominican friary. Its bridge, Blackfriars Bridge (completed 1769), appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as a strategic river crossing during the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], where [[mob-violence|the mob]] destroyed its toll-houses.

## Description

Dickens portrays Blackfriars primarily through the movement of the rioters across its bridge. The district serves as a waypoint in [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s tactical geography.

> …second party, whose route lay by Blackfriars, was entrusted to a committee… (049-002)

The bridge itself becomes a site of destruction:

> …they should proceed to Blackfriars, and, going ashore at the bridge… (049-050)

The toll-houses on the bridge are targeted by the advancing mob:

> …break open the Tollhouses on Blackfriars Bridge, and cast the money into the river. (067-016)

## Role in the Narrative

Blackfriars appears as one node in the rioters' coordinated movement through [[london|London]]. The destruction of the bridge's toll-houses (067-016) is a small but telling detail — [[mob-violence|the mob]] attacks not just Catholic targets and [[imprisonment|prison]]s but also the mundane apparatus of civic commerce. The casting of toll money into the Thames is an act of symbolic defiance against the everyday machinery of the city. Blackfriars Bridge also functions as a narrative valve, funnelling one stream of the divided mob toward their designated targets.

## Historical Basis

Blackfriars Bridge, designed by Robert Mylne, opened in 1769 and was the third bridge across the Thames in central [[london|London]]. Its toll-houses were genuinely unpopular — [[london|London]]ers resented paying tolls on bridges. During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], Blackfriars Bridge was indeed a key route for the rioters moving between the City and the Surrey bank. The toll-house destruction Dickens records was one of many acts of opportunistic vandalism that accompanied the political violence of the riots.
