## Definition

Sacrifice in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is the measure of a character's moral stature. Dickens repeatedly contrasts those who sacrifice for others with those who sacrifice others for themselves. The novel's virtuous characters — [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]], [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]], [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] — are defined by what they give up, while its villains — [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], [[dennis|Dennis]] — are defined by what they take. The motif appears across the social spectrum, from a widow's lifelong sacrifice raising a disabled son to a gentleman's sacrifice of peace for the pursuit of justice, suggesting that sacrifice is not a class-bound virtue but a universal moral language.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Widow's Sacrifice

The novel's foundational sacrifice is [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]]'s. Having lost her husband to the [[guilt|crime]] that structures the plot, she dedicates her life to raising [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]], a son with a cognitive disability, in poverty and obscurity. The narrator notes she has "sacrificed my whole store, so hardly earned" (046-046) — a sacrifice not only of resources but of identity and safety. This lifelong, invisible sacrifice is the novel's moral baseline against which other characters' actions are measured. It costs everything and expects nothing in return.

### Sacrifice for the Common Good

The novel extends the motif beyond domestic life into the public sphere. During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], characters act "at a great personal sacrifice, for their common good" (070-024) — the sacrifice of personal safety for the preservation of order. The narrator reflects on "the voluntary sacrifices of the missionaries in foreign" lands (021-061), drawing a parallel between domestic and public forms of self-denial. The novel's heroism is defined by "some voluntary sacrifice, born of a noble cause and pure" intention (077-028) — sacrifice not as suffering but as chosen moral action. [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]]'s pursuit of his brother's murderer requires the sacrifice of "sleep and comfort" (043-006), transforming his life into an instrument of justice. Throughout the novel, Dickens insists that the capacity for sacrifice — not wealth, [[social-class|rank]], or intelligence — is the true index of a character's worth.
