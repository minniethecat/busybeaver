## Overview

The Gordon Riots of June 1780 were the most destructive civil disturbance in [[london|London]]'s history until the Blitz. Triggered by [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] petition against the Catholic Relief Act of 1778, the riots lasted six days and caused more property damage than Paris suffered during the entire French Revolution.

In *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, Dickens uses the riots as both historical backdrop and narrative engine, interweaving the public catastrophe with the private drama of the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] murder mystery. His preface notes that the novel's account is "substantially correct" as history, though the fictional characters are woven through documented events (P03-005).

## Historical Background

The Catholic Relief Act of 1778 removed some penal restrictions on Catholics, allowing them to own property and serve in the military if they swore an oath of [[loyalty|allegiance]]. The [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], formed under [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s presidency, gathered approximately 44,000 signatures demanding repeal.

In the months leading up to June, the Association's agitation spread across England -- a movement into which Dickens's fictional characters are drawn as early as March 1780, when [[gashford|Gashford]] and Lord George recruit followers at [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn (037-003).

On Friday, June 2, 1780, [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] led a crowd estimated at 40,000--60,000 to the [[parliament|Houses of Parliament]] to present the petition (047-055). When [[house-of-commons|the Commons]] voted to adjourn debate, the crowd turned violent.

Over the following days, rioters attacked Catholic chapels, the houses of Catholic gentry, the residences of magistrates who had attempted suppression, and the public institutions of [[newgate|Newgate Prison]], [[fleet-street|the Fleet]] Prison, and the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]].

The authorities' response was paralyzed: magistrates hesitated to authorize the military to fire on [[mob-violence|the mob]], fearing legal consequences, while the city burned (052-001).

> The mob had been divided from its first assemblage into four divisions; the London, the Westminster, the Southwark, and the Scotch (049-001).

This four-division structure was [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s organizational skeleton. Each division had its own leaders and targets, allowing simultaneous attacks across [[london|London]] -- a tactical sophistication that accounts for the extraordinary scale of destruction achieved in less than a week.

## Depiction in the Novel

Dickens's account of the riots is both panoramic and intimate, tracing the movement from political protest through organized destruction to military suppression.

> A mob is usually a creature of very mysterious existence, particularly in a large city. Where it comes from, or whither it goes, few men can tell. Assembling, and dispersing with equal suddenness, it is as difficult to follow to its various sources as the sea itself; nor does the parallel stop here, for the ocean is not more fickle and uncertain, more terrible when roused, more unreasonable, or more cruel (052-001).

Dickens opens his analysis of [[mob-violence|mob psychology]] with this sustained metaphor of [[mob-violence|the mob]] as ocean -- unpredictable, elemental, and indifferent to moral categories. It is one of the most sustained pieces of crowd psychology in nineteenth-century fiction.

> The contagion spread like a dread fever: an infectious madness, as yet not near its height, seized on new victims every hour, and society began to tremble at their ravings (053-003).

The fever metaphor recurs throughout the riot chapters. The mob is not a rational political actor but a disease, spreading by proximity rather than conviction.

The "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" cry is "easily raised by men who have no religion" (P03-006), a grievance manufactured for destruction. [[gashford|Gashford]], [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s secretary, understands this perfectly, manipulating [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s cover-story while directing its violence toward other ends.

The mob divides into four parts (049-001), each with its appointed [[simon-tappertit|captain]]. [[hugh|Hugh]] leads the attack on the Warren and participates in the storming of [[imprisonment|Newgate]] (056-001, 060-027). [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] joins [[mob-violence|the mob]] that burns down his own workplace: "agin' all [[imprisonment|Newgate]]!" (038-011).

[[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]] leads the 'Prentice Knights (063-044). [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] is swept along, crying "[[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] forever, [[grip|Grip]]!" and "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]!" (057-009) without comprehending what he does.

The burning of [[imprisonment|Newgate]] is the novel's thematic and visual climax. Dickens renders it as sublime horror: "a vast mob poured into [[duke-street|Lincoln's Inn Fields]]" (063-002), "they bore down straight on [[imprisonment|Newgate]], and halted in a dense mass before the gate" (063-063).

The [[imprisonment|prison]] -- symbol of state power and the law -- is torn open and set ablaze. The keeper's house on [[imprisonment|Newgate]] Street is targeted because "it was well known" to contain the strongest cells (064-044). The sequence (063--065) is among the most celebrated set-pieces in Dickens's fiction.

## Key Participants

### [[Lord George Gordon]]

The historical president of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] and the figure around whom the riot's political legitimacy revolves.

In the novel, [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] is an earnest, somewhat naive enthusiast, a man of "Quaker-like simplicity" in dress and manner (037-028) who is manipulated by his secretary [[gashford|Gashford]]. Dickens emphasizes [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s genuine religious conviction and his horror at the violence his movement unleashes.

His historical acquittal on treason charges -- argued as political protest rather than criminal conspiracy -- is noted without being dramatized.

### [[Gashford]]

[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s secretary is the novel's hidden prime mover. Where [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] is sincere, [[gashford|Gashford]] is calculating; where [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] believes in the Protestant cause, [[gashford|Gashford]] understands [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s appetites and how to feed them (035-043).

He is a study in the type of political operator who manipulates idealism to achieve destructive ends. His influence over [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] is one of the novel's most tightly drawn relationships: "My lord is a little hasty," [[gashford|Gashford]] whines to observers, while privately orchestrating the chaos (035-065).

### [[Hugh]]

The savage ostler of [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn who finds his true element as a mob [[simon-tappertit|captain]]. [[hugh|Hugh]] is "as strong as a lion" -- a figure of raw, untamed physical force. He leads attacks with instinctive tactical sense and wants nothing more than the intoxication of destruction.

His participation in the storming of [[imprisonment|Newgate]] and his role in the attack on the Warren make him the novel's embodiment of [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s destructive energy (056-001, 063-044).

### [[Dennis]]

The hangman whose participation in the riots embodies the novel's darkest irony. [[dennis|Dennis]], a state functionary who earns his living by executing criminals, becomes a rioter who burns down the very [[imprisonment|prison]] where he works.

"[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]], brother!" he cries (038-018), cloaking his appetites in religious language. His eventual arrest and [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] -- by his own former trade -- is one of Dickens's most morally intricate resolutions.

### [[Simon Tappertit]]

The apprentice locksmith whose fantasies of martial glory and sexual conquest drive him into mob leadership.

Tappertit leads the 'Prentice Knights, a secret society of disaffected apprentices who join the riot as a ready-made fighting unit (063-044). His arc -- from domestic schemer to would-be revolutionary to crippled survivor -- mirrors [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s trajectory from grievance to self-destructive chaos.

### [[Barnaby Rudge]]

The novel's eponymous innocent. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] is recruited by [[hugh|Hugh]] and drawn into the riots without comprehending their meaning. His own cries -- "[[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] forever, [[grip|Grip]]!" and "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]!" (057-009) -- are learned rote phrases, repeated without understanding.

His presence in [[mob-violence|the mob]] serves Dickens's thematic argument: that political violence exploits the vulnerable and the simple, and that revolutionary movements are built on the unthinking enthusiasm of those who do not grasp what they serve.

## Dickens's Portrayal

Dickens's handling of the Gordon Riots is notable for three distinctive features.

**Historical Fidelity.** Dickens insists in his preface that the novel's account is "substantially correct" as history. The four-division mob structure, the paralyzed magistrates, the burning of [[imprisonment|Newgate]], the chalking of "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" on doors (051-036), and the military suppression are all drawn from contemporary accounts. Fictional characters move through a historically accurate frame.

**The Mob as Character.** Rather than treating [[mob-violence|the mob]] as a collection of individual rioters, Dickens personifies it. It is "a creature of very mysterious existence" (052-001), "a mad monster" that "raged and roared" (049-026).

The crowd becomes a single organism with its own moods, appetites, and destructive logic. This reflects Dickens's deep suspicion of mass political movements, grounded in his witnessing of reform-bill agitation and Chartist demonstrations, with the Gordon Riots serving as a historical proxy.

**Moral Architecture.** The riots function as a mechanism of providential justice. Villains meet their ends through the violence they helped unleash: [[hugh|Hugh]] is executed, [[dennis|Dennis]] is hanged by his own trade, [[gashford|Gashford]] disappears in disgrace.

The riot is a moral crucible -- the conflagration that burns away the deceptions and secrets of the pre-riot narrative and allows the surviving characters to emerge into clarity and resolution.

**The Religious Pretext.** Dickens is precise about the emptiness of the riot's religious justification. The "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" cry is "easily raised by men who have no religion" (P03-006). Throughout the riot chapters, the Protestant slogan functions as a social license for destruction: a phrase that transforms arson and murder into something that can be spoken of in public.

This skepticism about religious pretext in political violence was pointed in the 1840s, when the novel was written against the backdrop of renewed anti-Catholic agitation surrounding the Maynooth Grant controversy.

## Aftermath and Consequences

The riots were suppressed by military force on June 8--9, with at least 285 rioters killed and over 450 arrested. Twenty-five were executed.

[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] was tried for high treason but acquitted -- his defence arguing that his actions constituted political agitation, not criminal conspiracy. He later converted to Judaism and died in [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] in 1793, im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed for libel.

The events severely damaged the reputation of the [[london|City of London]]'s magistrates, exposed the inadequacy of [[london|London]]'s policing arrangements, and hardened political opposition to Catholic relief for decades. Catholic emancipation would not be achieved until 1829.

In the novel, the riots serve as the mechanism of narrative resolution. Secret [[guilt|crime]]s are exposed in the chaos: the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] murder mystery is solved, [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]'s machinations collapse, and the surviving characters emerge into a changed world.

The riot's violence functions as Dickens's instrument of moral reckoning, distributing fates to characters according to their pre-riot actions.

The novel closes with a brief post-riot survey (082-014), treating the riots as the watershed after which a new, quieter order takes shape. The architecture of [[london|London]] is rebuilt; the lives of the characters are rebuilt too, scarred but simplified, the old deceptions burned away.
