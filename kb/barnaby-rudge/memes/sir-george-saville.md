## Historical Biography

Sir George Savile (1726–1784) was a prominent Whig politician who served as Member of Parliament for Yorkshire for over twenty years. Born into wealth — his father was the 7th baronet of Thornhill — Savile was educated at Queens' College, Cambridge, and entered Parliament in 1757. He earned a reputation as an independent-minded reformer, supporting causes such as the abolition of press-ganging and the relief of Dissenters from legal penalties.

Savile's most consequential political act was the introduction of the Papists Act 1778 — commonly known as the Catholic Relief Bill — which proposed to remove certain legal disabilities from Roman Catholics, most notably the penalties for Catholic priests who entered England. The bill passed into law, but the Protestant Association, led by Lord George Gordon, responded with a campaign of petition and protest that escalated into the Gordon Riots. Savile's house in Leicester Fields was among those destroyed by the mob.

## Role in *Barnaby Rudge*

Sir George Savile is a background figure in *Barnaby Rudge*, but his legislative action is the proximate cause of the novel's central historical event. The novel references him first through the speech of Lord George Gordon, who names Savile as the author of the hated Catholic Relief legislation:

> to Savile, who introduces their bill, I would have you direct your attacks (040-062)

When the riots reach their height, the destruction of Savile's property becomes one of the key news items:

> another, that Sir George Savile's house in Leicester Fields was on fire (056-001)

In the aftermath, Savile is grouped with Lord Mansfield as one of the principal targets whose persecution demonstrated the rioters' scope:

> Both Lord Mansfield and Lord Saville, however, who had been great sufferers (073-003)

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens's treatment of Savile is historically accurate but economical. The novel correctly identifies Savile as the MP who introduced the Catholic Relief Bill and whose London residence was burnt as a direct consequence. However, Savile never appears as a character, and his reformist motivations receive no exploration. He exists in the novel purely as a target — a name on the rioters' list whose property, like his bill, must be destroyed.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

Savile's role in *Barnaby Rudge* highlights the political dimension of the riots. The destruction of his Leicester Fields home makes literal the connection between parliamentary action and mob reaction. His fate demonstrates how legislative reform — however well-intentioned — could unleash forces that its author could not control, a theme resonant with the novel's broader skepticism about political passion.
