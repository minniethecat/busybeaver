## Overview

The March on Parliament of 2 June 1780 was the culminating public demonstration of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]], organised by [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] to present the Great Protestant petition against the Saville Act, a measure that sought to relieve Roman Catholics of certain penal disabilities. Though the march began as an ostensibly peaceful procession, the sheer scale of the crowd and the tensions it inflamed within the Palace of [[parliament|Westminster]] turned the day into the immediate prelude to the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]].

## Historical Background

The [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] had formed in response to the Papists Act 1778 (18 Geo. 3 c. 78), which repealed certain restrictions on Roman Catholics — notably the disqualification from inheriting real property, the penalty of perpetual im[[imprisonment|prison]]ment for Catholic educators, and the prohibition on acquiring land by purchase. Dickens portrays the agitation as a phenomenon fuelled by rumour and paranoia:

> If all zealous Protestants had been publicly urged to join an association for the avowed purpose of singing a hymn or two occasionally, and hearing some indifferent speeches made, and ultimately of petitioning Parliament not to pass an act for abolishing the penal laws against Roman Catholic priests … matters so far removed from the business and bosoms of the mass, might perhaps have called together a hundred people. But when vague rumours got abroad, that in this Protestant association a secret power was mustering against the government for undefined and mighty purposes … then the mania spread indeed, and the body, still increasing every day, grew forty thousand strong. (037-002)

The organising genius behind the recruitment was [[gashford|Gashford]], Lord George's secretary, who travelled the countryside enrolling men like [[hugh|Hugh]] the ostler into the cause.

## Depiction in the Novel

### Pre-March Organisation

The plan for the procession was outlined months in advance. [[gashford|Gashford]] explains the intended strategy:

> "We shall have, we think, about next month, or May, when this Papist relief bill comes before the house, to convene our whole body for the first time. My lord has thoughts of our walking in procession through the streets — just as an innocent display of strength — and accompanying our petition down to the door of the House of Commons." (037-068)

[[hugh|Hugh]], newly recruited, was brought to [[parliament|Westminster]] to survey the ground; the surrounding crowd, the carriages, the buzz of anticipation all anticipated the confrontation to come. As Chapter 37 closes, the machinery of the march is fully in motion, and the Association's divisions — the [[london|London]], [[parliament|Westminster]], [[southwark|Southwark]], and Scotch — are being organised across the city.

### 2 June 1780: The Day of the March

On the day of the march the assembled crowd divided into four companies and crossed the Thames to converge on [[parliament|Westminster]]:

> The mob had been divided from its first assemblage into four divisions; the London, the Westminster, the Southwark, and the Scotch. Each of these divisions being subdivided into various bodies, and these bodies being drawn up in various forms and figures, the general arrangement was, except to the few chiefs and leaders, as unintelligible as the plan of a great battle to the meanest soldier in the field. (049-001)

[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] himself led the column. The immense petition — a roll signed by tens of thousands — was carried into the Palace:

> Lord George — preceded by a man who carried the immense petition on a porter's knot through the lobby to the door of the House of Commons, where it was received by two officers of the house who rolled it up to the table ready for presentation — had taken his seat at an early hour, before the Speaker went to prayers. (049-027)

### Confrontation Inside the Chamber

Within the chamber, the situation rapidly deteriorated. Members arriving were assaulted in the passages; their glimpses through the door ignited the crowd. Lord George himself, "very pale and agitated" (049-032), demanded immediate consideration of the petition:

> "Gentlemen," said Lord George, who was very pale and agitated, "we must be firm. They talk of delays, but we must have no delays. They talk of taking your petition into consideration next Tuesday, but we must have it considered now. Present appearances look bad for our success, but we must succeed and will!" (049-032)

The crisis reached its peak when Colonel [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] — Lord George's own kinsman — confronted him inside the House:

> "And my Lord George," said the other gentleman, addressing him in like manner, "I desire them to hear this, from me — Colonel Gordon — your near relation. If a man among this crowd, whose uproar strikes us deaf, crosses the threshold of the House of Commons, I swear to run my sword that moment — not into his, but into your body!" (049-038)

The Riot Act was read to the crowd massed outside, soldiers formed across the street, but the crowd refused to disperse:

> The Riot Act was read, but not a man stirred. (049-044)

## Aftermath and Consequences

The petition's failure to receive immediate attention from the House triggered the cascade of violence that became the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. Over the following six days, the crowd — now a fully unleashed mob — attacked Catholic chapels, embassies, private houses, and the [[imprisonment|Newgate]] [[imprisonment|prison]] itself. The march on [[parliament|Parliament]], conceived as "an innocent display of strength" (037-068), had ignited the most destructive urban riot in British history.

The [[parliament|House of Commons]], meeting on the following Tuesday behind locked and guarded doors, passed a resolution to take the petition into "serious consideration" — but the concession came far too late. Lord George was later tried for high treason at [[parliament|Westminster]] on 5 February 1781 and found Not Guilty, the court determining there was no proof he had called the multitude together with traitorous intentions (082-004).
