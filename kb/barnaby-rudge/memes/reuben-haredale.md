Reuben Haredale is the murdered elder brother of [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]], dead twenty-two years before the novel's opening. Though he never appears as a living character, his murder is the central mystery of *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* — the event that links the private plot of the [[geoffrey-haredale|Haredale]] family to the public drama of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]].

## The Murder

On a stormy night in 1753 — the exact date that marks the novel's opening — Reuben Haredale was found murdered in his bedchamber at [[the-warren|The Warren]]. The circumstances, as told by [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]] in Chapter 1, are both precise and haunting (001-103):

> But, that morning, Mr. Reuben Haredale was found murdered in his bedchamber; and in his hand was a piece of the good old fashion, having one side of this silver, and that of the blackened wood, which was the very steel that had lain upon the dressing-table.

The steward, a man named Rudge, vanished that same night along with a cash-box containing a considerable sum (001-105). The body of a man — presumed to be Rudge — was later found in a pond on the grounds, and the case was closed.

But the true story was far darker. The murderer was not [[barnaby-rudge-senior|the steward]] but Rudge himself — [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] Senior — who killed his employer, stole the money, murdered [[barnaby-rudge-senior|the steward]] to fake his own death, and fled, leaving his wife and infant son behind.

## The Haunting

The murder scene remains physically present throughout the novel. [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]]'s nighttime visit to [[the-warren|The Warren]] in Chapter 33 deliberately echoes the anniversary visit he made on the night of the murder. The room where Reuben died — "Mr. Reuben's own apartment, God be with us!" (034-013) — is still identifiable, and the fear it inspires is an active presence.

The image of the murdered man becomes a motif of recognition. In Chapter 33, the stranger's face seen by [[solomon-daisy|Solomon Daisy]] recalls the likeness of "a murdered man" (033-056), forging a connection between the present and the past that haunts the characters until the truth is revealed.

## Narrative Function

Reuben Haredale's murder is the hidden foundation of the novel's private plot. It explains the apparently motiveless hostility of [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] toward [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]'s family, the strange protectiveness of [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]] toward her son, and ultimately the revelation in Chapter 62 that links the novel's domestic mystery to its historical panorama. In the climactic confrontation at [[imprisonment|Newgate]], [[geoffrey-haredale|Geoffrey Haredale]] identifies the body of the hanged [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]] Senior with the words (061-026):

> "My lord," said Mr. Haredale, "the murdered gentleman was my brother; I succeeded to his inheritance; there were not those wanting who said I was his murderer."
