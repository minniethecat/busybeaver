## Overview

The Tower of London, referred to throughout *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* simply as "the Tower," is the ancient Thames-side fortress that serves as both a military stronghold and a state [[imprisonment|prison]]. During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], the Tower becomes a symbol of the Crown's defensive posture: its guns are fired, its drawbridge raised, its walls fortified. After the suppression of the riots, the Tower becomes the [[imprisonment|prison]] of [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] himself, who is held there on a charge of High Treason — a charge for which he will eventually be tried at [[westminster|Westminster]] and acquitted.

Dickens treats the Tower not as a tourist landmark but as a grim fact of state power. Its "dreary room whose thick stone walls shut out the hum of life" (073-063) is the novel's final image of im[[imprisonment|prison]]ment — the author of the riots, trapped in the silence he made.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens's descriptions of the Tower are sparse and functional, reflecting its role as an instrument of the state rather than a place of human habitation. The fortress is introduced in the context of military preparation:

> The next day was ushered in by merry peals of bells, and by the firing of the Tower guns; flags were hoisted on many of the church-steeples. (053-001)

The firing of the Tower guns — a ceremonial salute for the King's birthday — acquires an ironic charge in the context of the riots. While the city burns, the Tower signals royal celebration; the juxtaposition of "merry peals" and "firing" with the half-smouldering embers Dickens describes in the same passage underscores the distance between the Crown's rituals and the city's reality.

As the riots escalate, the Tower is fortified:

> The Tower was fortified, the drawbridge was raised. (067-003)

The sentence is characteristically brief. Dickens does not linger on the fortification — it is one item in a catalogue of defensive measures that includes the posting of soldiers at "the Lord President's in Piccadilly, at Lambeth Palace, at the Lord Chancellor's in Great Ormond Street, in the Royal Exchange, the Bank, the [[guildhall|Guildhall]], the Inns of Court, the Courts of Law, and every chamber fronting the streets near [[westminster-hall|Westminster Hall]] and the [[parliament|Houses of Parliament]]" (067-003). The Tower is first among equals in this litany of defended institutions — the ultimate re[[suspicion|doubt]] of state authority.

The novel's most extended treatment of the Tower comes after the riots, when [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] is im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed there:

> And in the Tower, in a dreary room whose thick stone walls shut out the hum of life, and made a stillness which the records left by former prisoners with those silent witnesses seemed to deepen and intensify; remorseful for every act that had been done by every man among the cruel crowd; feeling for the time their guilt his own, and their lives put in peril by himself; and finding, amidst such reflections, little comfort in fanaticism, or in his fancied call; sat the unhappy author of all — Lord George Gordon. (073-063)

This is the novel's most powerful image of the Tower. The fortress is rendered in terms of sensory deprivation: thick walls, silence, stillness. The "records left by former [[imprisonment|prison]]ers" — the grafitti and carvings of earlier inmates — are "silent witnesses" to [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s [[innocence|guilt]]. The passage makes the Tower a place of solitary moral reckoning, where the noise of [[mob-violence|the mob]] is replaced by the silence of conscience.

## Role in the Narrative

The Tower appears at three critical moments in the novel's second half, each marking a stage in the arc of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]].

First, during the riots themselves, the Tower guns are fired on the King's birthday (053-001). The timing is cruelly coincidental: as the city descends into violence, the Tower celebrates. The passage registers the disconnection between official [[london|London]] and the reality on the streets — a disconnection that will cost lives before the week is out.

Second, at the height of the crisis, the Tower is fortified and its drawbridge raised (067-003). This is part of a comprehensive military lockdown of the capital. The Tower's fortification signals that the authorities now regard the riots as an existential threat to the state — not a disturbance to be policed but an insurrection to be suppressed by arms.

Third, and most extensively, the Tower becomes [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s [[imprisonment|prison]] after his arrest (073-063, 073-064, 082-004, 082-008). [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] is taken there under "the strongest guard ever mustered" (073-064), held until the following February, and then tried at [[westminster|Westminster]]. [[john-grueby|John Grueby]] remains with his master throughout the im[[imprisonment|prison]]ment — "never left him until he was carried in his coffin to the church of Hendon" (082-008). The Tower thus becomes the setting for the novel's closing portrait of loyalty: Grueby's steadfast service to a master who has lost everything.

## Historical Basis

The Tower of London was indeed the [[imprisonment|prison]] where the historical [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] was held after the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. He was arrested on a charge of High Treason and confined in the Tower from June 1780 until his [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] at [[westminster|Westminster]] in February 1781, where he was acquitted. The Tower in the eighteenth century was still an active fortress and state [[imprisonment|prison]], though its use as a royal residence had long since ended.

The Tower guns that Dickens mentions were (and remain) a real feature of the fortress: they are fired on ceremonial occasions, including the King's (or Queen's) official birthday. The historical [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] did coincide with the King's birthday celebrations, and contemporary accounts confirm that the ceremonial gunfire created the same disorienting contrast between official festivity and civic destruction that Dickens dramatises.

The "records left by former [[imprisonment|prison]]ers" (073-063) — the grafitti carved into the walls of the Tower's [[imprisonment|prison]] rooms — were a well-known feature of the Tower. Prisoners of state, many held for years, left their names, coats of arms, and messages carved into the stone. Dickens's reference to these "silent witnesses" draws on a grim aspect of the Tower's history that would have been familiar to nineteenth-century readers.
