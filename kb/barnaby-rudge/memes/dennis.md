# Dennis

## Overview

Dennis is the public hangman of [[london|London]] — a role that makes him, in Dickens's rendering, one of the most grotesque figures in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*. First mentioned as a contributor to the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s cause — "Dennis the hangman, one shilling" (036-015) — he quickly emerges as a central figure in the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], combining professional expertise in death with an almost comic enthusiasm for destruction. His eventual fate — to be hanged on the very scaffold he had helped prepare for others — is one of the novel's most pointed instances of poetic justice.

Dickens uses Dennis to embody the brutal, hypocritical underside of the rioters' professed moral purpose. A hangman who joins a movement ostensibly defending religious liberty is a walking contradiction, and Dennis's garrulous, self-justifying chatter throughout the riots exposes the moral emptiness at the heart of mob violence.

## Role in the Novel

Dennis enters the novel through the ledgers of the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] — a shilling donation from the public [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]er that catches [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s attention. "Make a note of Dennis. I'll talk with him" (036-018). This early mention establishes him as a figure whose participation in the riots is at once natural and horrifying.

His first substantial scenes are with [[gashford]], the Association's secretary, who recognizes in Dennis a useful instrument. Dennis's eagerness to participate is almost touching in its grotesquerie:

> "The sooner the better," said Dennis, with another oath. (037-069)

His conversations with [[gashford|Gashford]] reveal a man who has thoroughly internalized his profession, speaking of hanging with the cheerful familiarity that a baker might speak of bread.

During the riots, Dennis becomes one of [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s principal [[simon-tappertit|captain]]s alongside [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]. His strategic sessions with [[gashford|Gashford]] — "Dennis's policy was mine, as you may remember" (044-027) — show him to be not merely a brute but a cunning tactician of destruction. He participates in the burning of houses, the attack on [[imprisonment|Newgate]], and the general terror of those June days with professional satisfaction.

His arrest and [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] form a grim coda to the riots. The hangman who executed so many is himself condemned to death, and his terror at the prospect reveals the cowardice beneath his bravado.

## Character and Personality

Dennis is a figure of grotesque comedy whose laughter masks a profound moral vacancy. He roars, he slaps his knees, he utters oaths with theatrical relish — "you will be the death of me" (037-044), he cries to [[gashford|Gashford]], in an unconscious irony that Dickens clearly savours. His manner is that of a man who has made his peace with horror by refusing to acknowledge it as horror at all.

His professional pride is the key to his character. Dennis speaks of hanging not as punishment but as craft — a skilled trade requiring judgment, timing, and the proper equipment. When the riots give him scope to exercise his talents on a grander scale, his enthusiasm is that of a craftsman presented with a magnificent commission.

Yet Dennis is not simply a monster. His terror at his own [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] reveals the human being beneath the professional mask. He has spent a lifetime administrating death to others without ever contemplating his own; when that contemplation is forced upon him, he collapses into abject fear. Dickens's description of Dennis on the scaffold, pleading for his life where so many had pleaded to him in vain, is one of the novel's most powerful passages of moral reckoning.
