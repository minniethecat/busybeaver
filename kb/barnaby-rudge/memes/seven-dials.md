## Overview

Seven Dials was a London district centered on a column at the convergence of seven streets in the parish of St. Giles. Built in the 1690s on what was then open farmland, it had by the mid-eighteenth century descended into one of London's most infamous slums. Its crowded tenements, narrow courts, and transient population made it synonymous with urban poverty.

## In the Novel

While Seven Dials is not named directly in *Barnaby Rudge*, the novel's geography repeatedly traces the mob's movements through precisely this quarter of London. The St. Giles rookery, of which Seven Dials was a central part, was the reservoir of desperate humanity from which the Gordon rioters drew their numbers.

Dickens's later novels — particularly *Sketches by Boz* and *Oliver Twist* — would explore Seven Dials in greater detail, but *Barnaby Rudge* already registers the district's significance as the dark underside of London's prosperity. The mob that burns Catholic chapels and attacks Parliament is, in its composition, a Seven Dials mob: the hungry, the desperate, and the casually brutal.

## Historical Context

By 1780, Seven Dials was among the most densely populated areas of London. Contemporary accounts describe families of eight or ten living in single rooms, open sewers running in the streets, and a mortality rate that made it one of the unhealthiest places in England. The district's inhabitants included Irish immigrants, discharged soldiers, street vendors, beggars, and criminals of every description.

The contrast between Seven Dials and the fashionable squares of the West End — a ten-minute walk away — was one of the most extreme wealth gaps in Europe. This proximity of wealth to poverty is fundamental to the Gordon Riots' logic: the mob knew where the rich lived because the rich lived next door.
