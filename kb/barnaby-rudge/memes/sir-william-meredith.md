## Historical Biography

Sir William Meredith (c. 1725–1790) was a British Whig politician who served in the House of Commons for various constituencies between 1754 and 1780. He held office as a Lord of the Admiralty and later as Comptroller of the Household. Meredith was known as a parliamentary reformer and an advocate for the reduction of capital punishment. In 1777, he delivered a notable speech in Parliament "on Frequent Executions," arguing against the severity of the English criminal code — the so-called "Bloody Code" — under which over two hundred offenses carried the death penalty. His speech was part of a nascent reform movement that would gain momentum in the decades to follow, culminating in the campaigns of Sir Samuel Romilly and others in the early nineteenth century.

## Role in *Barnaby Rudge*

Sir William Meredith does not appear in the narrative of *Barnaby Rudge*, but his parliamentary speech is cited by Dickens in the novel's Preface. Invoking the case of Mary Jones — a young woman of nineteen hanged for shoplifting — Dickens quotes Meredith as an authoritative witness to the harshness of eighteenth-century justice:

> as related by Sir William Meredith in a speech in Parliament, "on Frequent Executions," made in 1777 (P03-011)

Dickens then reproduces Meredith's account of the Mary Jones case in full, using it to establish the historical credibility of the novel's depiction of judicial severity. The reference serves a dual purpose: it authenticates the novel's period detail and aligns the work with the reformist parliamentary tradition that Meredith represented. By citing Meredith alongside Romilly, Dickens places *Barnaby Rudge* within a lineage of protest against legal cruelty.

## Historical vs. Fictional Depiction

Dickens's treatment of Meredith is entirely factual. Meredith is not fictionalized; he is invoked as a documentary source, much as a historian might cite a parliamentary record. The novel reproduces Meredith's own words from the 1777 debate, treating them as primary evidence. There is no creative embellishment — Dickens simply lets Meredith's parliamentary intervention speak for itself.

## Significance to the Novel's Themes

Meredith's presence in the Preface reinforces one of the novel's deepest concerns: the relationship between law, cruelty, and public order. The case Meredith cited — Mary Jones, hanged for stealing a piece of cloth — is exactly the kind of judicial brutality that, in Dickens's view, breeds contempt for the law among the poor. By invoking Meredith, Dickens signals that the novel's critique of eighteenth-century justice is not mere sensationalism but is grounded in the reformist arguments of respected parliamentarians. Meredith stands as a bridge between the world of the novel (1780) and the reform movements that were reshaping English law by the time of the novel's publication in 1841.
