## Definition

Innocence in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is not a fixed moral state but a precarious condition constantly threatened by the novel's twin engines of violence and deception. [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]], the novel's title character and its central innocent, is condemned to death for participating in a riot whose political meaning he cannot grasp. [[edward-chester|Edward Chester]]'s innocent love for [[emma-haredale|Emma Haredale]] is weaponized against him by his father. [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]]'s innocence of the world's cruelties is both her charm and her vulnerability. Dickens uses the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] — a historical event in which the innocent suffered alongside the guilty — to explore how [[mob-violence|collective violence]] erases the distinction between innocence and [[guilt|culpability]].

## Historical Context

The "Bloody Code" of 18th-century England made no meaningful distinction between levels of [[guilt|culpability]]: 160 offenses carried the death penalty, and children, the mentally disabled, and minor offenders were executed alongside murderers. Dickens's preface cites Sir William Meredith's parliamentary speech documenting this barbarity (P03-011). The novel's treatment of [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] and death sentence is a direct critique of a legal system incapable of recognizing innocence when it presented in unfamiliar form.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Barnaby Rudge as Holy Fool

[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] is innocent in the theological sense — without guile, incapable of calculating self-interest. His participation in the riots comes not from conviction or malice but from his inability to understand what is happening around him. The novel's central moral argument is that [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s innocence makes him more, not less, deserving of protection — and that a society that would hang him has forfeited its own claim to moral legitimacy. The narrator's comment that one may "learn some wisdom even from the witless, when their hearts are lifted up" (025-005) is the novel's clearest statement of its position on innocence and wisdom.

### The Corruption of the Innocent

The riots corrupt everyone they touch, but the novel is particularly attentive to how innocence is the first casualty of [[mob-violence|collective violence]]. [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]], whose innocence is partly comic (her vanity, her flirtatiousness), is subjected to genuine danger during the riots. [[joe-willet|Joe Willet]] loses an arm in military service — a physical mark of the cost of experience. [[edward-chester|Edward Chester]]'s moral innocence is punished by disinheritance, while his calculating father prospers — a pattern the novel's providential ending only partially reverses.

### The Guilt of the "Sane"

Dickens systematically inverts the expected relationship between innocence and guilt. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], the novel's most calculating intelligence, is also its most morally corrupt character. [[gashford|Gashford]]'s lucidity is the instrument of his [[deception|manipulation]]. By contrast, [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s "[[madness|idiocy]]" is the ground of his moral clarity. The novel suggests that full moral agency — the capacity to understand one's actions — paradoxically makes guilt more likely, not less.

## Key Characters

- **[[Barnaby Rudge]]** — The title character, whose innocence is the novel's moral reference point. His condemnation to death is the novel's most explicit critique of a system that cannot distinguish innocence from guilt.
- **[[Edward Chester]]** — The innocent son whose integrity is punished by his father's cynicism — innocence as social disadvantage.
- **[[Emma Haredale]]** — The innocent object of both [[edward-chester|Edward]]'s love and [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s violence, caught in the crossfire of conflicts she did not create.
- **[[Dolly Varden]]** — Whose innocence of the world's cruelties is tested by her exposure to the riots.
- **[[Hugh]]** — Begins the novel as a kind of natural innocent (his animal vitality, his loyalty to [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]) and ends it as a condemned rioter — innocence corrupted by violence.

## Key Scenes

1. **[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]] in [[imprisonment|Newgate]]**: The innocent awaiting [[justice-and-punishment|execution]] — the novel's most concentrated portrait of the gap between legal guilt and moral innocence.
2. **"I can't touch him!"** (003-055): [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s recoil from violence, establishing his innocence as physical instinct rather than intellectual choice.
3. **[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s Wisdom** (025-005): The narrator's meditation on learning "from the witless" — the novel's thematic core.
4. **The Trial**: The legal system's failure to distinguish [[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby]]'s incapacity from the guilt of the other rioters.
