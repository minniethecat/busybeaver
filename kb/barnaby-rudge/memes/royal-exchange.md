## Overview

The Royal Exchange was London's preeminent commercial center, located at the intersection of Cornhill and Threadneedle Street in the heart of the City. Founded by Sir Thomas Gresham in 1571 and rebuilt after the Great Fire of 1666, the Exchange in the time of *Barnaby Rudge* was a colonnaded courtyard where merchants, brokers, and ship-owners conducted the business of empire.

## In the Novel

The Royal Exchange appears in *Barnaby Rudge* during the account of the riots' final phase, when the mob's ambitions had swollen to encompass the entire architecture of authority:

> "notices on the gates of the Fleet and the King's Bench, many similar ones in Great Ormond Street, in the Royal Exchange, the Bank, the Guildhall, and other public buildings" (067-003)

The Exchange is grouped with the Bank of England and the Guildhall — the three pillars of London's financial and civic power. The placarding of these institutions with threatening notices signaled the mob's transition from anti-Catholic violence to a more comprehensive assault on the established order.

Dickens does not describe an actual attack on the Royal Exchange during the riots — the military presence in the City protected the financial district — but its inclusion on the list of threatened buildings conveys the scope of the terror. The mob that began by burning Catholic chapels had, within a week, come to threaten the Bank itself.

## Historical Significance

The Royal Exchange was a symbol of London's commercial dominance. In the late eighteenth century, the City of London was the center of global trade, and the Exchange was its beating heart. The fact that it appeared on the rioters' list of targets reflected the Gordon Riots' unique character: unlike most eighteenth-century disturbances, which were geographically confined, the No Popery riots spread across the entire metropolis, threatening institutions in every quarter of the capital.
