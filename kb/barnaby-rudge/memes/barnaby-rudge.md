## Overview

Barnaby Rudge is Dickens's most unusual title character: a young man with an intellectual disability, consistently called "the idiot" by the novel's other characters, whose innocence and simplicity provide the moral standard against which the novel judges everyone else. Where [[mob-violence|the mob]] is calculating in its violence, Barnaby is impulsive and gentle; where [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] is polished in his [[deception|manipulation]], Barnaby is transparent in his affections; where the legal system is rigid in its categories, Barnaby exceeds every category it tries to impose on him. His [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] for participating in the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] — [[guilt|crime]]s he could not understand — is the novel's most sustained critique of a society that punishes disability while excusing conscious evil.

## Character Analysis

### Innocence and Disability

Barnaby is introduced through the reactions of others. His first significant moment in the novel is his recoil from the threat of violence: "I can't touch him!" cried the idiot falling back, and shuddering as with a strong repugnance" (003-055). This instinctive rejection of violence — physical, visceral, pre-rational — establishes the paradox at the novel's heart: the "idiot" has a keener moral instinct than the "sane" men who will soon be burning [[london|London]]. The narrator's later observation that one may "learn some wisdom even from the witless, when their hearts are lifted up" (025-005) is Dickens's most explicit statement of the novel's position on Barnaby's value.

### Relationship with His Mother

Barnaby's [[loyalty|devotion]] to his mother is absolute and uncomplicated — the novel's purest example of love. Their bond, forged in [[mrs-rudge|Mrs. Rudge]]'s lifelong [[deception|concealment]] of her husband's [[guilt|crime]] and her protection of her disabled son, is the emotional keel of the novel. Every scene between them is a study in mutual loyalty, and Barnaby's anguish when separated from her in [[imprisonment|Newgate]] is the novel's most affecting rendering of the cost of the riots.

### Barnaby and Hugh

[[hugh|Hugh]]'s protective affection for Barnaby — "the idiot whispered in his ear" (006-033) — is the one redeeming feature of a character otherwise defined by violence. Their bond, revealed at the novel's climax to be half-brotherhood, is the novel's most complex exploration of the relationship between nature and nurture: two sons of the same father, one raised in love by a mother who sacrificed everything, the other abandoned to become a savage.

### On the Scaffold

Barnaby's [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] and death sentence is the novel's most concentrated moral argument. A legal system that cannot distinguish between Barnaby's incapacity and the [[innocence|guilt]] of [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] or [[hugh|Hugh]] [[mob-violence|the mob]] [[simon-tappertit|captain]] is a system that has forfeited its claim to justice. His eventual rescue — a deus ex machina that Dickens's providential scheme demands — is less a legal outcome than a moral one: the novel insists that innocence must be saved, even if the legal system is incapable of recognizing it.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Mrs. Rudge]]** — [[mrs-rudge|Barnaby's mother]], whose lifelong protection of her son is the novel's model of unconditional love.
- **[[Hugh]]** — Barnaby's half-brother, whose protective instinct toward Barnaby is the one moral gesture of his violent life.
- **[[barnaby-rudge-senior]]** — The father Barnaby never knows, a murderer hidden in secret [[imprisonment|confinement]] whose legacy is his son's disability.
- **[[Grip]]** — [[grip|Barnaby's raven]], his constant companion and the novel's most memorable animal presence.

## Key Scenes

1. **"I can't touch him!"** (003-055): Barnaby's first significant moment — his instinctive rejection of violence.
2. **The Whisper** (006-033): [[hugh|Hugh]]'s protective whispering to Barnaby, establishing their bond.
3. **In [[imprisonment|Newgate]]**: Barnaby im[[imprisonment|prison]]ed, dreaming — the novel's psychological center.
4. **The Trial**: Barnaby condemned for a [[guilt|crime]] he could not understand — Dickens's critique of a system that cannot recognize innocence.
5. **The Rescue**: Barnaby saved from the scaffold by forces beyond the legal system's logic.
