## Overview

Smithfield in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is the great livestock market of the [[london|City of London]] — a place where the commerce in animal bodies blurs into the violence done to human ones. Dickens introduces Smithfield as a simile rather than a setting: the [[maypole-inn|Maypole Inn]] courtyard is compared to "the courts between [[cornhill|Cornhill]] and Smithfield" (018-001), an image that imports the noise and disorder of the metropolitan market into the pastoral world of [[chigwell|Chigwell]].

During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], Smithfield becomes one of the assembly points for [[mob-violence|the mob]]. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] is reported to have threatened to "turn the pens of Smithfield market into stakes and cauldrons" (037-002) — a phrase that captures the transformation of a place of commerce into a place of [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]. When the rioters reach Smithfield itself, the market's association with blood and butchery becomes literal: a corpse is "handed from hand to hand" (068-001) as an object of triumph rather than horror. Smithfield, in Dickens's rendering, is where the line between the slaughter of animals and the killing of human beings dissolves.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens's first reference to Smithfield is one of his most compressed urban descriptions — a single line that conveys the noise, smell, and chaos of the market through analogy:

> "The courts between Cornhill and Smithfield; with no more fixedness of purpose than the wind" (018-001)

This is the description of [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] courtyard on a busy night, but the simile works by importing Smithfield's associations: the livestock market is a place of constant movement, of animals being driven and traded, of noise that has no direction because it comes from everywhere at once. The comparison dignifies [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]]'s provincial bustle by linking it to one of [[london|London]]'s great commercial institutions, even as it hints that the disorder of the market is never far from the order of the inn.

During the riots, the threat of violence at Smithfield is first articulated by an anonymous voice in the crowd:

> "Let the men of London, and turn the pens of Smithfield market into stakes and cauldrons" (037-002)

The "pens" — the enclosures where livestock were held before sale — are to be repurposed as instruments of atrocity: stakes for burning and cauldrons for boiling. The image is deliberately grotesque, and it is attributed not to [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s leaders but to the popular imagination — the kind of rumour that circulates in a crowd and becomes, by repetition, a plan.

When the rioters actually reach Smithfield, the violence has moved from the rhetorical to the physical:

> "A corpse, handed from hand to hand, stood in Smithfield, on the outskirts of the mob" (068-001)

The corpse — one of the riot's casualties — becomes an object passed through the crowd, a trophy or a warning. Smithfield, where animals were bought and sold and slaughtered, is now the stage for a human body displayed in death. The location is not accidental: the market's architecture of pens and open spaces made it a natural gathering point for crowds, and its association with butchery gave the violence performed there a grim symbolic logic.

## Role in the Narrative

Smithfield functions in the novel as a site where the boundary between the civilised and the savage is tested. The market is itself a place of sanctioned violence — the slaughter of animals for food — and Dickens uses this fact to probe the nature of [[mob-violence|the mob]]'s violence. When [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s rhetoric threatens to "turn the pens of Smithfield market into stakes and cauldrons" (037-002), the threat is shocking precisely because it proposes to redirect the market's existing machinery of death from animals to humans.

The market also serves a practical narrative function as a gathering point. Its central location, its open spaces, and its association with the working population of the City made it a natural rallying point during the riots. In the novel, Smithfield is the place where [[mob-violence|the mob]] coalesces before moving toward its targets: the Catholic chapels of the City, the houses of magistrates, and ultimately [[newgate|Newgate Prison]].

## Historical Basis

Smithfield Market — properly known as Smithfield or Smoothfield — was one of the oldest markets in [[london|London]], operating as a livestock market from at least the twelfth century. In 1780, it was the principal cattle market for the City, handling vast numbers of sheep, cattle, and pigs each week. The market's location, just north of St Paul's and west of the Charterhouse, placed it squarely within the area affected by the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]].

Smithfield was also, historically, a place of [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]. The Scottish patriot William Wallace was executed there in 1305, and the market was used as a site for burning heretics during the Marian persecutions of the 1550s — a history that gave particular resonance to the threat of turning its "pens" into "stakes and cauldrons." Dickens, steeped in [[london|London]]'s history, would have been aware of these associations, and his use of Smithfield in the novel draws on the market's layered identity as a place of commerce, butchery, and judicial killing.

The market continued to operate as a livestock market until 1855, when it was relocated to the newly built Metropolitan Cattle Market in [[islington|Islington]]. By the time Dickens was writing *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, Smithfield was still a functioning livestock market, and its sights, sounds, and smells were part of the lived [[innocence|experience]] of Victorian [[london|London]].
