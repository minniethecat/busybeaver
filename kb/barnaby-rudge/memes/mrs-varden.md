Mrs. Varden is the wife of [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]] and mother of [[dolly-varden|Dolly Varden]]. She is one of Dickens's most fully realized comic portraits of domestic discontent — a woman who, though materially comfortable and well-treated, casts herself as a "persecuted perfection" and exercises a reign of mild emotional tyranny over her household.

## Character Introduction

Dickens introduces Mrs. Varden in Chapter 7 with a brilliantly ironic character sketch (007-001):

> Mrs. Varden was a lady of what is commonly called an uncertain temper — a phrase which, being interpreted, signifies a temper tolerably certain to make everybody more or less uncomfortable.

Her world is one of imagined slights and theatrical self-[[sympathy|pity]]. She keeps a Protestant Manual prominently displayed, not from religious conviction but as a prop in her domestic performances — a sign that she belongs to the "persecuted" even as she sits comfortably by her own fireside.

## Relationship with Her Husband

The dynamic between Mrs. Varden and [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel]] is one of Dickens's sharpest studies in marital asymmetry. [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel]] is patient, cheerful, and genuinely fond of his wife, but her "uncertain temper" means he never knows which version of her he will encounter (013-063):

> the certain uncertainty of Mrs. Varden's temper, that when they were alone the good locksmith was always fidgeting in his chair and never could be quite sure whether she was going to be pleasant or disagreeable

Mrs. Varden's method is to interpret everything as an attack on herself. When [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel]] visits [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]], she does not complain directly but frames it as his wanting to escape her (022-023):

> Mrs. Varden was persecuted perfection, and her husband a creature of almost supernatural brutality.

## Domestic Comedy and Miggs

Mrs. Varden's partnership with her maid [[miss-miggs|Miggs]] creates a two-woman echo chamber of grievance. [[miss-miggs|Miggs]], "her chief aider and abettor" (007-003), amplifies every slight and feeds her mistress's sense of injury. Their exchanges are among the novel's funniest passages, as when Mrs. Varden bursts into theatrical tears over an imagined insult and [[miss-miggs|Miggs]] loudly agrees (007-020):

> "Yes!" cried Mrs. Varden, bursting into tears, "yes, nobody knows — no one can feel — the way he speaks to me!"

The comedy [[deception|lies]] in the contrast between her rhetoric of suffering and her actual comfort — she complains while "glancing at herself in a neighbouring mirror" (013-056).

## Social Ambitions

Mrs. Varden is acutely conscious of social status. Her hostility toward [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] Inn is rooted not in moral objection but in snobbery — she considers it beneath her (013-043). When [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]] visits, she is "delighted beyond measure" (019-010) and undergoes an instant transformation into "all smiles and graces" (019-050), revealing her susceptibility to class flattery.
