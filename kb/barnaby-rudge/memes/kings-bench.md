## Overview

The King's Bench Prison was a debtors' prison in Southwark, on the south bank of the Thames, one of London's oldest and largest prisons for civil offenders. Originally established to hold prisoners awaiting trial before the Court of King's Bench, by the eighteenth century it primarily housed debtors. It was distinct from Newgate (which held felons) and the Fleet (which also held debtors, on the north bank), forming with them London's triad of major detention facilities.

## In the Novel

The King's Bench appears in *Barnaby Rudge* during the account of the Gordon Riots' most destructive night, when the mob forced open multiple prisons across London:

> "the gates of the King's Bench and Fleet Prisons being opened at the usual hours" (067-004)

The prison's gates were forced on the same night as the attack on Newgate (7 June 1780), part of a coordinated assault on the institutions of criminal justice. The King's Bench was not burned, as Newgate was, but the liberation of its inmates — debtors who had committed no crime but poverty — added to the general breakdown of order.

The novel notes that the liberation of the prisons was followed by the systematic placarding of public buildings with threatening messages, including "notices on the gates of the Fleet and the King's Bench" themselves — as though the mob wanted to claim the prisons as trophies (067-006).

## Comparison with Newgate

The King's Bench represents a different dimension of justice than Newgate. Where Newgate held the condemned — murderers, thieves, rioters — the King's Bench held debtors whose only crime was insolvency. The mob's attack on both types of prison, without discrimination, suggests the indiscriminate nature of the rioters' grievances. They did not distinguish between just and unjust laws; they simply wanted the prisons open.
