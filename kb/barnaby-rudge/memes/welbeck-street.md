## Overview

Welbeck Street — "near Cavendish Square" in the parish of Marylebone — is, in the geography of *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]*, the headquarters of the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]]. It is here that [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] resides; it is from here that he departs to lead the [[march-on-parliament|March on Parliament]]; and it is from an upper window of this house that [[gashford|Gashford]], the [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]]'s secretary, watches the riot he has helped to orchestrate unfold in the streets below.

The street operates in the novel as the hidden centre of the conspiracy. Unlike [[st-georges-fields|St George's Fields]] (the riot's public origin) or [[westminster|Westminster]] (its political target), Welbeck Street is private — a place of whispers, instructions, and strategic observation. The rioters surge through the streets of [[london|London]], but their strings are pulled from a quiet Marylebone window.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens introduces Welbeck Street through a fragment of reported conversation — [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]] praising his secretary to a visitor:

> "That Dennis," said his lordship, "is an earnest man. I marked him in the crowd in Welbeck Street, last Friday." (036-016)

The remark is casual, almost banal — the great man noticing a follower in the street — but it fixes Welbeck Street as the site where [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] first observes the human material of his movement. [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]], with his "earnest" [[loyalty|devotion]], is recruited from the crowd outside [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s own door.

The most detailed rendering of Welbeck Street arrives when [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] returns home after addressing his followers:

> So along the Strand, up Swallow Street, into the Oxford Road, and thence to his house in Welbeck Street, near Cavendish Square, whither he was attended by a few dozen idlers; of whom he took leave on the steps with this brief parting, "Gentlemen, No Popery. Good day. God bless you." (037-032)

The route — from the public space of [[strand|the Strand]] to the private refuge of Welbeck Street — is a progress from popularity to privacy. The "few dozen idlers" who accompany him are dismissed on the steps with a formulaic benediction. The house in Welbeck Street is the boundary between [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s public performance and his private life — a boundary that the riot, as it escalates, will dissolve.

The street's most chilling function emerges when the riots are in full spate:

> Gashford betook himself to Welbeck Street by a different path from that which he knew the rioters would take, and sitting down behind a curtain in one of the upper windows of Lord George Gordon's house, waited impatiently for their coming. (053-059)

[[gashford|Gashford]] behind the curtain — watching the destruction he has engineered, physically separated from the violence he has authored — is one of the novel's most potent images of political [[deception|manipulation]]. Welbeck Street becomes a vantage point from which the riot can be observed without being touched. The secretary waits "impatiently" for the rioters to arrive, as if they were a spectacle laid on for his entertainment.

## Role in the Narrative

Welbeck Street's narrative function is to locate the conspiracy in a specific, respectable, domestic setting. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] does not direct the riots from a tavern in [[southwark|Southwark]] or a field in [[moorfields|Moorfields]]; he directs them — or allows them to be directed — from a gentleman's house in Marylebone. The respectability of the address is the point: violence that issues from Welbeck Street is violence that issues from within the establishment, not from outside it.

The street appears at three critical junctures. In Chapter 36, it is the recruiting ground where [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] first notices [[dennis|Dennis]]. In Chapter 37, it is the refuge to which [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] retreats after speaking to the crowd — the "brief parting" on the doorstep marking the line between leader and led. And in Chapter 52–53, after [[hugh|Hugh]] and [[mob-violence|the mob]] have sacked [[the-warren|The Warren]] and rampaged through [[moorfields|Moorfields]], Welbeck Street becomes the convergence point:

> As the main body filed off from this scene of action, and passed down Welbeck Street, they came upon Gashford, who had been a witness of their proceedings, and was walking stealthily along the pavement. (052-033)

[[gashford|Gashford]] is "stealthily" walking — the adverb is damning. He is both part of the scene and apart from it, a spectator pretending to be a passerby. [[hugh|Hugh]], who knows him for what he is, "muttered in his ear" as they pass. Welbeck Street here is the site of a secret exchange between the riot's mastermind and its muscle — a transaction conducted on the pavement, in plain sight, yet invisible to anyone who does not know what to look for.

## Historical Basis

Welbeck Street in the late eighteenth century was a relatively new development in the parish of Marylebone, part of the Portland Estate's expansion of [[london|London]]'s West End. The area around Cavendish Square had been laid out from the 1710s onward and by 1780 was a fashionable residential district populated by peers, members of [[parliament|Parliament]], and wealthy professionals. [[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]'s choice of Welbeck Street for his [[london|London]] residence was consistent with his status as the younger son of a duke — it was an address of quiet respectability, a short distance from the political centre of [[westminster|Westminster]] but not ostentatiously grand.

The historical [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s house in Welbeck Street did serve as a gathering point for [[protestant-association|Protestant Association]] supporters, though the extent to which [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] personally directed the riots from this address — as opposed to losing control of a movement he had helped create — remains a matter of historical debate. [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] was acquitted of high treason at his [[justice-and-punishment|trial]] in 1781, largely because the prosecution could not prove he had intended the violence that his movement unleashed. Dickens's portrayal, in which [[gashford|Gashford]] rather than [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] is the active conspirator, reflects this ambiguity: the house in Welbeck Street is the conspiracy's address, but [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] himself is shown as a dupe rather than a director.
