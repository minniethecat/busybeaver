## Definition

Mob violence in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* is the central destructive mechanism through which the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] unfold. Dickens presents the mob not as a faceless abstraction but as a creature with its own psychology — a living entity that assembles and disperses "with equal suddenness" (052-001).

The mob defies prediction, defies control, and ultimately defies moral categories that would assign individual [[innocence|guilt]]. It is simultaneously a political instrument wielded by agitators like [[gashford|Gashford]], a self-perpetuating force exceeding any individual's control once roused, and a moral contagion spreading "like a dread fever" through the city (053-003).

## Historical Context

Dickens drew upon contemporary accounts of the actual [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] of June 1780, which lasted six days and caused more property damage than the entirety of Paris [[innocence|experience]]d during the French Revolution. His preface notes that the novel's riot scenes are "substantially correct" as historical record.

The mobs of 1780 attacked Catholic chapels, the houses of Catholic gentry, and public institutions including [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] and the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] before being suppressed by military force. Dickens had access to eyewitness accounts through his reading of the *Annual Register* and other contemporary sources.

> "No account of the Gordon Riots having been to my knowledge introduced into any Work of Fiction, and the subject presenting very extraordinary and remarkable features, I was led to project this Tale."
>
> -- Charles Dickens, Preface to *Barnaby Rudge*

The author's framing of the riots as "shameful tumults" that "reflect indelible disgrace upon the time in which they occurred" establishes from the outset that mob violence is not merely a narrative device but a moral argument — an attempt to anatomize how ordinary men become instruments of collective destruction.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### The Mob as an Organised Force

The mob is introduced in Chapter 49 with surprising precision. Dickens describes its division into four regional bodies — [[london|London]], [[parliament|Westminster]], [[southwark|Southwark]], and Scotch — each subdivided "into various bodies, and these bodies being drawn up in various forms and figures" (049-001).

This quasi-military organisation, largely invisible to the [[social-class|rank]]-and-file participants, reveals the secret architecture behind apparent spontaneity.

The crowd "had resolved itself into three great parties, and were prepared, as had been arranged, to cross the river by different bridges, and make for the [[parliament|House of Commons]] in separate detachments" (049-001). The word "resolved" is deliberate: the mob appears to have a will of its own even as it follows hidden leaders.

> "The mob had been divided from its first assemblage into four divisions; the London, the Westminster, the Southwark, and the Scotch. Each of these divisions being subdivided into various bodies, and these bodies being drawn up in various forms and figures, the general arrangement was, except to the few chiefs and leaders, as unintelligible as the plan of a great battle to the meanest soldier in the field."
>
> -- *Barnaby Rudge*, Chapter 49 (049-001)

### The Mob as a Living Entity

Dickens's most sustained meditation on mob psychology comes in Chapter 52, where he personifies the mob as "a creature of very mysterious existence" whose movements are as unpredictable as the sea:

> "A mob is usually a creature of very mysterious existence, particularly in a large city. Where it comes from or whither it goes, few men can tell. Assembling and dispersing with equal suddenness, it is as difficult to follow to its various sources as the sea itself; nor does the parallel stop here, for the ocean is not more fickle and uncertain, more terrible when roused, more unreasonable, or more cruel."
>
> -- *Barnaby Rudge*, Chapter 52 (052-001)

This oceanic metaphor — the mob as a force of nature, at once indifferent and malevolent — runs throughout the novel. In Chapter 49, the mob "raged and roared, like a mad monster as it was" (049-026).

During the burning of [[imprisonment|Newgate]], the crowd howled "like wolves" (064-007). The cumulative effect strips the rioters of individual humanity and presents the mob itself as the novel's most terrifying antagonist.

### The Moral Contagion

A central insight of Dickens's treatment of mob violence is the idea of moral contagion — that collective violence spreads not through persuasion or ideology but through proximity and excitement. Chapter 53 describes how "sober workmen, going home from their day's labour, were seen to cast down baskets of tools and become rioters in an instant; mere boys on errands did the like" (053-003).

The metaphor is explicitly epidemiological:

> "In a word, a moral plague ran through the city. The noise, and hurry, and excitement, had for hundreds and hundreds an attraction they had no firmness to resist. The contagion spread like a dread fever: an infectious madness, as yet not near its height, seized on new victims every hour, and society began to tremble at their ravings."
>
> -- *Barnaby Rudge*, Chapter 53 (053-003)

This conception of mob violence as disease — not argument, not grievance, but a transmissible frenzy — is one of the novel's most modern and unsettling ideas. It suggests that anyone, given sufficient proximity to the crowd, can become a rioter.

### Composition and Anonymity

Dickens delineates the social composition of the mob in unsparing terms. Chapter 49 describes the crowd as "composed for the most part of the very scum and refuse of [[london|London]], whose growth was fostered by bad criminal laws, bad [[imprisonment|prison]] regulations, and the worst conceivable police" (049-026).

Yet the novel complicates this class analysis: the mob also contains "honest zealots" alongside criminals, and the "moral plague" of Chapter 53 reaches "sober workmen" and "mere boys" — not only outcasts but ordinary [[london|London]]ers drawn in by the crowd's gravitational pull.

The mob simultaneously confers anonymity and collective identity. Individual moral agency dissolves within it. [[hugh|Hugh]], a genial ostler, transforms into a savage [[simon-tappertit|captain]].

[[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]]'s absurd fantasies of martial glory find expression in real destruction. [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]], who spent his life as an instrument of state violence, becomes enmeshed in violence the state can no longer control. The slogan of "[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" provides a thin mask of legitimacy for actions driven by greed, cruelty, and the intoxicating freedom of anonymous numbers.

### Spontaneity and the Illusion of Order

Once the riots begin, Dickens notes that "from the moment of their first outbreak at [[parliament|Westminster]], every symptom of order or preconcerted arrangement among them vanished" (053-001). Each party "swelled as it went along, like rivers as they roll towards the sea" (053-001).

This tension between initial organisation and subsequent chaos is central to the novel's critique: the mob can be set in motion by design, but it cannot be steered. The leaders who think they command it — [[gashford|Gashford]], [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]], even [[hugh|Hugh]] — are ultimately its servants.

### The Burning of Newgate as Culmination

The attack on [[newgate|Newgate Prison]] (Chapters 64–65) represents the mob's fullest expression. The cry "ran through the mob" (064-038) to breach the [[imprisonment|prison]] walls; sledgehammers rattled "on the nailed and plated door" while sparks flew "off in showers" (064-038).

When the fire took hold, the mob "began to join the whirl, and with loud yells, and shouts, and clamour, such as happily is seldom heard, bestirred themselves to feed the fire, and keep it at its height" (064-041).

The burning of a [[imprisonment|prison]] — the symbol of the law itself — represents the mob's ultimate rejection of civil order. The scene's apocalyptic imagery transforms destruction into terrible beauty.

The aftermath extends mob power into daily life: "At the bidding of the mob, the houses were all illuminated that night — lighted up from top to bottom as at a time of public gaiety and joy" (065-048). For a brief period, the mob becomes the de facto government of [[london|London]], its demands obeyed by the terrified citizenry.

## Key Characters

- **[[hugh|Hugh]]** — The ostler who becomes the mob's most charismatic [[simon-tappertit|captain]]. His physical strength, fearlessness, and lack of moral reflection make him the mob's ideal instrument.

- **[[dennis|Dennis]]** — The hangman whose participation in the riots represents a grotesque inversion: the [[justice-and-punishment|execution]]er becomes the rioter.

- **[[simon-tappertit|Simon Tappertit]]** — The apprentice locksmith whose fantasies of martial glory find expression in mob leadership. His ludicrous "Prentice Knights" and the leg he loses in the riots make him a darkly comic figure.

- **[[gashford|Gashford]]** — [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]]'s secretary, the secret architect who treats mob violence as a political tool. His manipulative genius is matched only by his cowardice when the mob turns.

- **[[lord-george-gordon|Lord George Gordon]]** — The figurehead who sets the mob in motion but cannot control it. His misguided Protestant zeal provides the pretext for violence he neither anticipated nor can halt.

- **[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]** — The innocent carried along by the mob. "The only lighthearted, undesigning creature, in the whole assembly" (049-001), he is the simpleton made standard-bearer of collective evil.

- **[[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]** — The locksmith who refuses, at the risk of his life, to pick the lock of [[imprisonment|Newgate]] for the mob (064-026 through 064-028). His stand represents individual conscience in defiance of collective madness.

## Key Scenes

1. **The Mob Divided and Dispatched** (049-001): Dickens's detailed account of the four divisions of rioters marching on [[parliament|Parliament]], revealing the hidden organisational structure beneath the apparent chaos.

2. **The Attack on Members of [[parliament|Parliament]]** (049-026): Members of both Houses are pulled from carriages, beaten, and rolled in mud — the mob's first demonstration that no [[social-class|rank]] or institution is beyond its reach.

3. **The Meditation on Mob Psychology** (052-001–052-002): The novel's most sustained analysis of what a mob is — its mysterious origins, its fickleness, its cruelty, and the moral implications of collective action. The sea metaphor establishes the mob as a force of nature.

4. **The Moral Plague** (053-003–053-004): The description of how riot violence spreads like contagion through the working population of [[london|London]], transforming ordinary men into participants.

5. **"[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]" as Catalyst** (043-054): The cry that transforms an inactive crowd into a destructive mob, illustrating how political and religious slogans serve as catalysts for violence independent of genuine conviction.

6. **The March on [[chigwell|Chigwell]]** (054-023–055-027): The mob's journey to [[maypole-inn|the Maypole]] and its destruction of the inn, showing how mob violence radiates outward from [[london|London]] to the countryside.

7. **The Guards Attacked** (056-001): Soldiers conveying [[imprisonment|prison]]ers are set upon by the mob and compelled to retreat — a demonstration that the state's coercive power is, at least temporarily, broken.

8. **The Mob Rules [[london|London]]** (063-002–063-004): The description of how "the crowd was everywhere; all [[deception|concealment]] and [[deception|disguise]] were laid aside" — the mob as de facto sovereign of the city. Rumours spread that the soldiers themselves are "ripe for disobeying orders and joining the mob" (063-002).

9. **The Stand at [[imprisonment|Newgate]]** (064-007–064-028): [[gabriel-varden|Gabriel Varden]]'s refusal to pick the [[imprisonment|prison]] lock, the mob's howling like wolves, and the hammering down of the gates — the novel's central confrontation between individual conscience and collective violence.

10. **The Burning of [[imprisonment|Newgate]]** (064-038–064-045): The [[imprisonment|prison]] in flames, the mob joining "the whirl," and the terrible spectacle of the condemned [[imprisonment|prison]]ers crying for rescue — the apocalyptic climax of mob violence in the novel.

11. **The Illuminated City** (065-048): [[london|London]]'s houses lit "at the bidding of the mob" — a chilling image of civic surrender to mob rule.

## Dickens's Commentary

Dickens does not present mob violence as a neutral narrative subject; from the preface onward, he frames it as a vehicle for moral and political argument.

The preface states that "those shameful tumults" teach a lesson about how "what we falsely call a religious cry is easily raised by men who have no religion, and who in their daily practice set at nought the commonest principles of right and wrong" (Preface).

The author insists that he has "no sympathy with the Romish Church" — the disclaimer underscores his point that the riots were not truly about religion but about the exploitation of prejudice for violent ends.

Three interrelated critiques emerge from the novel's treatment of mob violence.

**The critique of instrumental violence.** [[gashford|Gashford]] embodies the danger of treating the mob as a political tool. His ability to orchestrate the riots through surrogates — [[lord-george-gordon|Gordon]] the figurehead, [[hugh|Hugh]] the enforcer, [[dennis|Dennis the hangman]] — demonstrates how elite [[deception|manipulation]] can channel popular violence toward calculated ends.

Yet the novel insists that instrumental violence is a trap for its architects: the mob that [[gashford|Gashford]] summons ultimately escapes his control.

**The critique of moral abdication.** The novel repeatedly shows how individuals shed moral responsibility when absorbed into the crowd. The "moral plague" passage (053-003) makes this explicit: ordinary men "had no firmness to resist" the crowd's pull.

The mob provides moral licence — actions unthinkable for any individual become possible, even exhilarating, when performed in concert. [[hugh|Hugh]], who as an individual is capable of loyalty and tenderness, becomes a monster when the mob gives him permission.

**The critique of the state's complicity.** Dickens draws a direct line between "bad criminal laws, bad [[imprisonment|prison]] regulations, and the worst conceivable police" (049-026) and the mob's composition.

The state's failures — its brutal penal system, its neglect of policing, its tolerance of the conditions that breed desperation — create the raw material of riot. This is not an exculpatory argument; Dickens shows no sympathy for the rioters themselves. But it is an institutional one: mob violence is, in part, what a negligent state deserves.

The preface's closing sentence extends this critique beyond 1780 into the reader's present: "perhaps we do not know it in our hearts too well, to profit by even so humble an example as the '[[anti-catholicism|No Popery]]' riots of Seventeen Hundred and Eighty" (Preface). The novel is not merely historical fiction but a cautionary tale for any society that permits prejudice to be weaponised for political ends.
