## Overview

The Temple — the ancient legal precinct comprising the Inner Temple and Middle Temple, one of [[london|London]]'s four Inns of Court — appears in *[[barnaby-rudge|Barnaby Rudge]]* as a zone of privilege and seclusion, a place where the noise of the city is held at bay and where men of influence conduct their affairs in studied leisure. It is the [[london|London]] residence of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], and its quiet gardens and chambers are the setting for some of the novel's most politically charged conversations.

The Temple occupies a liminal position in the novel's geography. It [[deception|lies]] between the commercial tumult of [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]] and the river Thames; it is physically within the [[london|City of London]] but legally and culturally separate from it. When the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]] threaten to breach its gates, "the gentlemen of the Temple, and the other Inns, mounted guard" (067-010) — a detail that captures both the precinct's vulnerability and its capacity for self-defence. The lawyers arm themselves not as soldiers but as property-owners, defending a space whose privileges [[mob-violence|the mob]] would happily erase.

## Description in the Novel

Dickens's Temple is rendered in precise sensory detail. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]'s chambers look out upon Temple Gardens, and the contrast between the interior refinement and the exterior violence is one of the novel's recurring structural principles:

> There are, still, worse places than the Temple, on a sultry day, for basking in the sun, or resting idly in the shade. (015-004)

The sentence is characteristically double — it describes a pleasant afternoon while positioning the Temple as a place where basking and idling are the primary occupations. For [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], the Temple is exactly that: a stage for the performance of leisured indifference, a retreat from the vulgar world of action and consequence.

The chambers themselves are described through the view they afford:

> Looking, at the back, upon the Temple Gardens. (015-005)

The gardens are the Temple's pastoral heart — a green space visible from Sir John's windows, a reminder of the countryside within the city. But the garden is also a vantage point for observing the city beyond:

> Through the half-opened window, the Temple Garden looks green and pleasant. (075-001)

This glimpse, in the novel's closing chapters, is the Temple restored to its customary tranquillity after the riots have passed. The garden that looked "green and pleasant" before the violence looks green and pleasant again — but the novel's readers know what has happened in between.

The Temple's boundary with the city is marked by [[temple-bar|Temple Bar]] — the historic gateway between the [[london|City of London]] and [[westminster|Westminster]] — which Dickens mentions as a landmark of the old city, one of the structures that "still adhered to the old practice" (016-004) in its lighting arrangements, resisting the modernising impulse that was slowly transforming [[london|London]].

## Role in the Narrative

The Temple serves primarily as the [[london|London]] base of [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]], the novel's most refined villain. It is here, in his dressing-room — "a sofa in his dressing-room in the Temple" (023-001) — that Chester receives visitors, dispenses advice, and orchestrates his schemes. The setting is appropriate: the Inns of Court are the headquarters of England's legal profession, and Sir John is, in his own way, a master of appearances before the bar of society. His chambers in the Temple are a kind of theatre in which he performs his urbane, detached, utterly self-interested version of civility.

The Temple's role in the riot chapters is brief but significant. When [[mob-violence|the mob]] threatens to move westward through the City, "the gentlemen of the Temple, and the other Inns, mounted guard" (067-010) — a detail that Dickens draws from the historical record. The lawyers who argue cases by day take up arms by night to defend their precinct's gates. The image of the Inns of Court under arms is one of the novel's small but pointed ironies: the profession that represents the rule of law is reduced to defending it with muskets.

[[temple-bar|Temple Bar]] also serves as a symbolic divider. To the east of [[temple-bar|Temple Bar]], the old City with its medieval street plan and its traditional liberties; to the west, the newer districts of [[westminster|Westminster]] and [[strand|the Strand]]. The riots move through both zones, and [[temple-bar|Temple Bar]] — "which was strictly constituted" (008-055) as a landmark of civic order — marks the boundary that [[mob-violence|the mob]] disregards.

## Historical Basis

The Temple is one of [[london|London]]'s most ancient institutions, occupying land that was originally the precinct of the Knights Templar until their suppression in 1312. The site was subsequently leased to lawyers, and by the fourteenth century two of the four Inns of Court — the Inner Temple and Middle Temple — were well established there. The Temple Church, built by the Templars in the twelfth century, remains at the heart of the precinct.

In the eighteenth century, the Temple was a mixed-use enclave: barristers' chambers, residential apartments, gardens, and a church, all enclosed within gates that were locked at night. The privilege of living within the Temple was jealously guarded, and the precinct operated under its own governance, separate from the [[london|City of London]]. [[sir-john-chester|Sir John Chester]]'s chambers — a "dressing-room" with a view of the gardens — would have been characteristic of a well-appointed set occupied by a gentleman of means.

During the [[gordon-riots-1780|Gordon Riots]], the Temple's gates were indeed defended by its residents. Contemporary accounts record that the lawyers and students of the Inns of Court armed themselves and stood watch, fearing that [[mob-violence|the mob]], having burned Catholic targets in the City, would move westward toward the Temple and the houses of prominent figures along [[strand|the Strand]]. [[temple-bar|Temple Bar]], the great stone gateway designed by Christopher Wren that marked the boundary between the City and [[westminster|Westminster]], was a natural defensive point — though by 1780 it had long ceased to function as a gate and served only as a ceremonial and symbolic boundary. (The Wren gateway was removed in 1878 to widen the roadway; it now stands at the entrance to Theobalds Park in Hertfordshire.)
