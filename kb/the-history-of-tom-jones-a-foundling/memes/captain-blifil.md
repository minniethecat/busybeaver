# Captain Blifil

**Captain Blifil** is the calculating husband of [[Bridget Allworthy]] and the father of [[Master Blifil]]. Though his presence in the novel is brief — he dies before the narrative proper begins — his ambition, ingratitude, and domestic plotting establish the lineage of hypocrisy that his son inherits.

## Character

Captain Blifil is driven by policy and self-interest. Fielding traces his actions to a "diabolical principle" of using others for his own advancement and then discarding them: "no sooner was he possessed of [[bridget-allworthy|Miss Bridget]], and reconciled to Allworthy, than he began to show a coldness to his brother which increased daily; till at length it grew into rudeness" (Book 1, Chapter 13).

The captain's treatment of his own brother — a doctor who had helped him secure the match with Bridget — reveals his fundamental nature. When the doctor privately remonstrated about his brother's cold behaviour, the captain replied with brutal frankness: "If you dislike anything in my brother's house, sir, you know you are at liberty to quit it." This "strange, cruel, and almost unaccountable ingratitude" broke the doctor's heart (Book 1, Chapter 13).

Fielding diagnoses the captain as "a man of great pride and fierceness" who had always treated his milder, more learned brother "with the utmost air of superiority." The captain could not bear that his brother was reputed to have the better understanding, and envy "greatly heightened by mixing with contempt" produced not gratitude for the doctor's services but indignation (Book 1, Chapter 13).

## Role in the Novel

The captain enters the narrative as a suitor to [[bridget-allworthy|Bridget Allworthy]], having calculated that marriage to [[squire-western|the squire]]'s sister would secure his fortune. His courtship succeeds, and a son — the future [[master-blifil|Master Blifil]] — is born "a month before its full time," eight months after the wedding (Book 2, Chapter 2).

During his brief tenure at Paradise Hall, Captain Blifil actively schemes against the infant [[Tom Jones]]. He bears "no great goodwill to the little foundling" and gives "visible uneasiness" to Allworthy's affection for the child. [[mrs-deborah-wilkins|Mrs Wilkins]], the housekeeper, perceives the captain as her likely future master and offers to help undermine Tom's standing with Allworthy, though the captain — displaying his characteristic wariness of servants — refuses to be drawn into an explicit conspiracy (Book 2, Chapter 5).

Eventually, the captain himself reveals to Allworthy the story of [[Partridge]] being the supposed father of [[tom-jones|the foundling]] — a [[discovery|revelation]] calculated to diminish Allworthy's attachment to Tom. The captain's death soon follows: he dies of apoplexy while walking in his garden, leaving his infant son to be raised by Allworthy alongside Tom.

## Themes

Captain Blifil is an early study in the calculating personality that Fielding anatomises throughout the novel. His ingratitude to his brother — the man who made his marriage possible — is presented as a particular species of evil because it wounds the conscience of the benefactor as well as the heart. The captain's early death, leaving his son to be raised in the household he sought to dominate, is a small instance of the providential irony that runs through the novel.
