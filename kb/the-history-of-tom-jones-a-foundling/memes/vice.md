# Vice

**Vice** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* refers not to passionate indiscretion but to cold, calculating selfishness that wears the mask of [[virtue]]. Fielding draws on the Latitudinarian Anglican tradition to distinguish between errors of the heart (which may be corrected) and errors of the will (which constitute true vice).

## In the Novel

Fielding presents vice primarily through the character of [[Master Blifil]], whose every action is calculated for personal advantage while maintaining an outward show of piety and rectitude. Unlike [[Tom Jones]], whose faults arise from impulsive [[passion]], Blifil's schemes are deliberate and self-serving.

Blifil's vice is established early. He intercepts Tom's letter to [[Sophia Western]], suppresses evidence of Tom's true parentage, and conspires with the lawyer [[Mr Dowling]] to secure Allworthy's estate for himself. Fielding describes him as possessing "a prudent and sober disposition" that masks "the deepest villainy" (Book 3, Chapter 2).

Fielding distinguishes Blifil's vice from the lesser faults of other characters. [[Lady Bellaston]]'s manipulation arises from vanity rather than malice. [[Ensign Northerton]]'s cruelty is brutish rather than calculated. Only Blifil attains what Fielding considers the complete form of vice: the systematic exploitation of others through the performance of goodness.

The novel's resolution reinforces this moral taxonomy. Blifil is exposed and expelled from Paradise Hall, while Tom — whose sins were always of passion rather than calculation — is restored to Allworthy's favor and to Sophia's hand (Book 18).
