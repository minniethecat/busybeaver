# Bridget Allworthy

**Bridget Allworthy** is the sister of [[Squire Allworthy]] and, as revealed at the novel's climax, the true mother of [[Tom Jones]]. Her decision to conceal her pregnancy and arrange for the child to be discovered in her brother's bed sets in motion the entire plot.

## Character

Bridget Allworthy is introduced as a woman "somewhat past the age of thirty," of the sort "whom you commend rather for good qualities than beauty" (Book 1, Chapter 2).

Fielding describes her as one who "never mentioned that perfection, if it can be called one, without contempt" — a woman whose professed disdain for beauty masks a sharp awareness of her own plainness (Book 1, Chapter 2).

Her outward conduct is marked by an elaborate display of virtue and religious severity.

She reads English divinity, puzzles neighbouring curates with theological debate, and maintains "so grave and solemn" a deportment that "she seemed to deserve the name of saint."

Yet beneath this austere exterior, Fielding hints at a passionate nature: she falls deeply in love with [[captain-blifil|Captain Blifil]], a man notable neither for looks nor refinement, but for the "charms of his conversation" (Book 1, Chapter 11).

## Role in the Novel

Bridget's most consequential act occurs before the novel opens: her secret affair with [[mr-summer|Mr Summer]], a young man educated at Allworthy's expense, results in pregnancy.

She arranges with [[Jenny Jones]] and her mother to conceal the birth, dispatching [[Mrs Deborah Wilkins]] to a distant part of Dorsetshire so that the housekeeper would be absent when the child was born (Book 18, Chapter 7).

After the infant is discovered in Allworthy's bed, Bridget plays her part with consummate skill (Book 1, Chapter 4). She affects ignorance, joins in condemning the unknown mother, and maintains an outward show of disapproval towards [[tom-jones|the foundling]] — while secretly showing him affection when unobserved.

She later marries [[captain-blifil|Captain Blifil]] and bears a legitimate son, [[Master Blifil]], who becomes Tom's antagonist (Book 1, Chapter 12). Both Bridget and her husband die before the novel's main action concludes, but the secret of Tom's birth survives until [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]] reveals it to Allworthy in the final book.

## Themes

Bridget Allworthy embodies the theme of **appearance versus reality** that pervades the novel. Her public persona — the severe, virtuous, religiously scrupulous spinster — is a carefully maintained fiction concealing private passions and a secret that would destroy her reputation.

Fielding uses her to expose the gap between the moral posturing of respectable society and the natural human impulses it condemns.
