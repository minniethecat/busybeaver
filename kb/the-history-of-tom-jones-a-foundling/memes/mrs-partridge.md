# Mrs Partridge

**Mrs Partridge** is the wife of [[Partridge]] the schoolmaster, and a minor but consequential figure in the backstory of *[[tom-jones|Tom Jones]]*. Her jealous rage at discovering her husband's tutelage of [[Jenny Jones]] leads directly to Jenny's dismissal, the discovery of her pregnancy, and the accusation that [[partridge|Partridge]] fathered the child — the first link in the chain of misunderstandings about the hero's parentage.

## Character

Mrs Partridge is introduced in the novel's retrospective account (Book 2, Chapters 3–4) as a woman of violent passions who had married [[partridge|Partridge]] for her fortune of twenty pounds saved from [[Squire Allworthy]]'s kitchen. Fielding paints her as a formidable termagant: her jealousy, once roused, is compared to the gout — "when such distempers are in the blood, there is never any security against their breaking out."

Her domestic tyranny is absolute. She maintains "so constant a jealousy" of her husband that "he durst hardly speak to one woman in the parish." When her suspicion of [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]] ignites — triggered by seeing the girl start up from a chair while [[partridge|Partridge]] leaned over her reading — she explodes with startling violence: flinging a trencher at Jenny's head, pursuing her with a knife, and later, upon learning of Jenny's pregnancy, attacking [[partridge|Partridge]] with "her tongue, teeth, and hands" until his face streams blood from five scratches.

Yet Fielding notes that her passions were "equally violent, whichever way they inclined; for as she could be extremely angry, so could she be altogether as fond." After her first fit of jealousy subsided, she treated her husband with unusual affection, though this was merely the remission between storms.

[[partridge|Partridge]] himself, recalling his marriage to [[Tom Jones]], summarises his experience with grim concision: "she married me, and made one of the most confounded wives in the world. However, heaven be praised, she's gone" (Book 8, Chapter 9).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Partridge appears only in the retrospective narrative of Book 2, but her actions are the catalyst for the novel's central mystery. Her jealous dismissal of [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]] sends the young woman back to her parish, where her pregnancy soon becomes apparent. When the parish women investigate, Mrs Partridge, her jealousy rekindled, becomes convinced that her husband is the father.

Her subsequent physical assault on [[partridge|Partridge]] and public accusation before the neighbours leads to the matter reaching Allworthy's ears. Though [[partridge|Partridge]] protests his innocence, the circumstantial evidence — his tutelage of Jenny, his satisfaction at her departure, Jenny's refusal to name the real father — convinces Allworthy of his guilt. [[partridge|Partridge]] is stripped of his annuity and driven from the parish, becoming the wandering barber-surgeon who later accompanies Jones on his adventures.

Thus Mrs Partridge's domestic fury, though confined to a few chapters, indirectly shapes the entire novel: it brands [[partridge|Partridge]] as the supposed father, obscures the truth of Tom's parentage for two decades, and sends [[partridge|Partridge]] onto the road where he will eventually become Jones's loyal companion.
