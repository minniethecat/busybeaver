# Love

**Love** is the animating principle of [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]], appearing in more chapters than any other thematic concept. Fielding explores love in its full complexity: not as mere sentiment, but as active benevolence, romantic devotion, and the foundation of moral life.

## Varieties of Love in Tom Jones

### Romantic Love

The central romance between [[Tom Jones]] and [[Sophia Western]] drives the novel's plot across all eighteen books. Their love is distinguished from the mercenary matches that surround them: Sophia's father [[Squire Western]] would force her into a marriage of property, while [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]] treats love as a game of intrigue. Tom and Sophia's love, by contrast, is grounded in genuine affection and mutual respect—though tested by Tom's indiscretions and Sophia's prudence.

### Benevolent Love

[[Squire Allworthy]] embodies love as active charity. His discovery of the infant Tom and his immediate decision to care for him arise from "good nature" rather than obligation. Fielding consistently distinguishes this active benevolence from the merely theoretical virtue of characters like [[mr-square|Mr Square]], whose philosophy never translates into kindness. Allworthy's love is the practical kind that feeds the hungry and shelters [[tom-jones|the foundling]].

### Filial Love

The bond between parent and child—both biological and adoptive—runs throughout the novel. Tom's love for Allworthy endures even after his banishment, and Allworthy's eventual reconciliation with Tom represents love's triumph over deception and misunderstanding.

## Love and Fielding's Moral Philosophy

Fielding draws on the tradition of Latitudinarian Anglicanism, which emphasized charity as the supreme Christian virtue. Love, in this framework, is not opposed to reason but is its highest expression. The novel's happy ending is achieved not through fortune alone, but through the convergence of love in all its forms: romantic (Tom and Sophia's marriage), benevolent (Allworthy's forgiveness), and providential (the discovery of Tom's true parentage).
