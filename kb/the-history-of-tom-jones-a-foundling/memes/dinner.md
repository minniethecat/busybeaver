# Dinner

**Dinner** scenes appear throughout [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]] as one of Fielding's most versatile narrative devices. More than mere backdrop, the dinner table is where character is revealed, conflicts erupt, and the social order of eighteenth-century England is laid bare for satirical examination.

## Dinner as Social Theater

Fielding uses dinner gatherings to bring together characters from different stations and dispositions, creating the friction that drives comic action. At [[Squire Western]]'s table, the boisterous hunting squire clashes with his refined daughter [[Sophia Western]]; at [[Squire Allworthy]]'s, the hypocrite [[Master Blifil]] and the philosophers [[mr-square|Mr Square]] and [[mr-thwackum|Mr Thwackum]] perform their virtues for their host while revealing their true natures through their appetites and conversation.

The dinner table is a stage on which Fielding's characters cannot help but act according to their ruling passions. Western's table groans with excess; Allworthy's is marked by dignified hospitality. Each meal is a miniature moral drama.

## Food and Morality

Fielding associates good food with good nature. Characters who eat heartily and share generously—like Tom and [[partridge|Partridge]]—are morally approved, while those who hoard or disdain food are suspect. The dinner table thus becomes a test of character: how one eats reveals how one lives.
