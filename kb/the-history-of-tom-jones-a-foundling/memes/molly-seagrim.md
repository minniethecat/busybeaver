# Molly Seagrim

**Molly Seagrim** is the second child and eldest daughter of [[black-george|George Seagrim]], commonly called [[black-george|Black George]], the gamekeeper on [[Squire Allworthy]]'s estate. She is [[Tom Jones]]'s first lover and the mother of his child, a figure whose boldness and vitality challenge the novel's class assumptions.

## Character

Molly is described as "one of the handsomest girls in the whole country," though her beauty "had very little of feminine in it" and "would have become a man at least as well as a woman" (Book 4, Chapter 7). Her appeal lies in her youth, florid health, and spirited temperament. Fielding explicitly notes that Jones's attraction to her was grounded more in constitution than sentiment.

Despite her humble station, Molly possesses remarkable self-possession and combative courage. The churchyard battle scene (Book 4, Chapter 8) — in which she faces down an entire mob of village women using a skull and thigh-bone as weapons — is one of the novel's most celebrated comic set-pieces, narrated in mock-heroic style that simultaneously elevates and burlesques her Amazonian fury.

Molly is no passive victim. She actively pursues Jones and "so well played her part, that Jones attributed the conquest entirely to himself" (Book 4, Chapter 10). When discovered to be pregnant, she refuses to be shamed into submission and boldly defends herself against her family's reproaches.

## Role in the Novel

Molly first appears in Book 4, where her pregnancy by Jones is discovered and she narrowly escapes being sent to the house of correction — Jones gallantly claims paternity and secures her release. Her most memorable scene is the churchyard battle (Book 4, Chapter 11), a Homeric parody in which she single-handedly defeats a crowd of female adversaries.

Molly's role in the plot is chiefly significant for its effect on [[Sophia Western]]. When Sophia learns of Jones's entanglement with Molly, it first opens her eyes to her own [[passion|feeling]]s and the "weakness of which she had been guilty" in loving him (Book 5, Chapter 6). Molly thus becomes an unwitting catalyst for Sophia's awakening and for the [[passion|emotion]]al complication that drives the novel's central romance.

After the early books, Molly largely disappears from the narrative, her function as a plot device fulfilled. She represents the class of rural poor whose lives intersect with the gentry's through bonds of service, dependency, and occasionally, intimacy.

## Themes

Molly embodies the theme of **nature versus social convention**. Her robust physicality, her refusal to internalise shame, and her frank pursuit of her desires contrast with the hypocritical morality of her respectable neighbours. Fielding presents her not as a moral exemplar but as a force of nature whose vitality exposes the cruelty of social [[reason|judgment]]. The churchyard battle, with its epic machinery applied to a village brawl, underscores Fielding's democratic comic vision — the passions of the low-born are no less worthy of literary treatment than those of heroes.
