# Parson Supple

**Parson Supple** (Mr Supple) is the curate of [[Squire Allworthy]]'s parish, a minor clerical figure whose hearty appetite, physical vigour, and pragmatic accommodation to the secular world make him a gently comic presence in the novel.

## Character

Fielding introduces Supple with characteristic economy and wit: "He was a good-natured worthy man; but chiefly remarkable for his great taciturnity at table, though his mouth was never shut at it. In short, he had one of the best appetites in the world" (Book 4, Chapter 10). The pun on "mouth" — silent in conversation, ceaseless in consumption — is typical of Fielding's treatment of clerical figures.

Once the cloth was removed, however, Supple became "a very hearty fellow; and his conversation was often entertaining, never offensive." He thus embodies a genial, unambitious country clergyman whose virtues are social rather than spiritual.

## Role in the Novel

Supple appears in several domestic scenes. At [[squire-allworthy|Squire Allworthy]]'s table he is a fixture of the company (Book 4, Chapter 10). He also attends [[Bridget Allworthy]], who is found "reading and expounding the *Gazette* to parson Supple" when [[Squire Western]] arrives with urgent business (Book 6, Chapter 4).

His most active moment comes when [[squire-western|Squire Western]], enraged at [[Tom Jones]], threatens violence. Supple, "who was a very strong man," physically restrains [[squire-western|the squire]] from attacking Jones — an act of practical peacemaking that distinguishes him from the more theoretical morality of other clerical characters (Book 6, Chapter 9).

Supple's moral pragmatism is on display in his dealings with Western. Though [[squire-western|the squire]]'s profanity "greatly shocked the pious ears of Supple," the parson "did not dare to remonstrate against a privilege which [[squire-western|the squire]] claimed as a freeborn Englishman." Fielding notes dryly that "the parson submitted to please his palate at [[squire-western|the squire]]'s table, at the expense of suffering now and then this violence to his ears." Supple later paid [[squire-western|the squire]] back "obliquely in the pulpit," and while this did not reform Western himself, "it so far operated on his conscience, that he put the laws very severely in execution against others" — making the magistrate "the only person in the parish who could swear with impunity" (Book 6, Chapter 9).

## Themes

Parson Supple illustrates Fielding's preference for practical goodness over doctrinal purity. Supple is no zealot — he compromises with the world when it suits his comfort — yet he also acts decisively to prevent violence when the moment demands it. His oblique pulpit rebukes of Western's swearing, and their perverse effect of making Western a more rigorous magistrate, are a small but characteristic Fielding irony about the gap between preaching and practice.
