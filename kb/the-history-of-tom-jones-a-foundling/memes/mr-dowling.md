# Mr Dowling

**Mr Dowling** is an attorney from Salisbury who plays a pivotal role in the plot of *[[tom-jones|Tom Jones]]* as a carrier of information between [[Master Blifil]] and [[Squire Allworthy]], and whose accidental meeting with [[Tom Jones]] at an inn provides the first clear glimpse of the machinations behind Jones's disinheritance.

## Character

Dowling is introduced as the attorney who brought news of [[bridget-allworthy|Mrs Blifil]]'s death to Allworthy. Though a professional lawyer, Fielding is careful to distinguish him from the petty-fogger who shares his table at an inn: Dowling possesses intelligence, discretion, and a certain worldly wit.

He enjoys his wine, is curious about human affairs, and, as the narrator observes, "had not divested himself of humanity by being an attorney" (Book 12, Chapter 10).

His manner is marked by guardedness and professional caution. He speaks with careful indirection, qualifies his statements with "I protest," and deploys innuendo rather than direct accusation. Yet beneath this professional veneer, he is capable of genuine [[passion|feeling]]: when Jones relates his history, Dowling is "very greatly affected."

## Role in the Novel

Dowling first appears in Book 12, Chapter 10, at an inn where he dines with Jones. Not recognising Jones at first, he listens to the petty-fogger's scurrilous account of Jones's supposed misdeeds.

The moment he learns the name of the gentleman described — Jones — his demeanour shifts dramatically: he falls silent, "biting his fingers, making faces, grinning, and looking wonderfully arch" (Book 12, Chapter 10). He then departs in evident haste.

The reason for this reaction becomes clearer when Dowling appears in [[london|London]] as Blifil's "great favourite" and Allworthy's newly appointed steward. His connection to Blifil suggests he was the intermediary through whom Blifil conveyed the poisoned account of Jones's character to Allworthy, contributing to the rupture between patron and foundling.

In the novel's climactic [[discovery|revelation]]s, Dowling's presence at Allworthy's house and his involvement in the examination of the bank-note affair connect him to the final unravelling of Blifil's schemes. His legal expertise is invoked when Allworthy asks how a person who stole bank-notes might be punished, though the reader understands that Jones is innocent of the charge.

## Themes

Dowling illustrates how legal professionals could serve as instruments of intrigue in eighteenth-century society. His careful speech, professional discretion, and loyalty to his patron Blifil make him a figure through whom injustice can be perpetuated without overt villainy — he simply does his job.

His brief moment of sympathy for Jones, quickly suppressed, reveals the tension between professional duty and personal conscience.
