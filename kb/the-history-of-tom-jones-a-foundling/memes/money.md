# Money

**Money** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* serves as a material index of moral character. How characters acquire, use, and speak about wealth reveals their ethical dispositions more reliably than their professed principles.

## In the Novel

Fielding establishes the moral significance of money through contrasts. [[Squire Allworthy]] possesses one of the largest estates in [[Somerset]] yet treats wealth as a trust for charitable use. His generosity to the poor, his hospitality to neighbors, and his provision for [[Tom Jones]] all demonstrate that money in the right hands becomes an instrument of [[virtue]].

Against this model, Fielding arrays characters whose relationship to money exposes their corruption. [[Master Blifil]] schemes to monopolize Allworthy's inheritance, revealing that his piety is a mask for avarice. [[Lady Bellaston]] uses her wealth to manipulate lovers and maintain social power in London. The lawyer [[Mr Dowling]] sells his professional integrity for [[Master Blifil]]'s patronage.

A crucial plot development turns on the discovery that Tom's mother, [[Bridget Allworthy]], left a legacy of five hundred pounds — knowledge that Blifil suppressed along with the proof of Tom's parentage (Book 18, Chapter 7). The legacy symbolizes the rightful inheritance that Tom's good nature deserves and that Blifil's [[vice]] attempts to steal.

Fielding also examines the simpler relationship to money represented by characters like [[Black George]], whose poverty leads him to theft, and the philosophical reflections of the [[Man of the Hill]], who concluded that "money only differs from money, as one kind of dirt from another" (Book 8, Chapter 13).
