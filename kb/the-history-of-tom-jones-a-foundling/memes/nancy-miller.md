# Nancy Miller

**Nancy Miller** is the elder daughter of [[Mrs Miller]], the [[london|London]] landlady. Her romance with [[Mr Nightingale]] — a courtship that nearly ends in betrayal and ruin — provides one of the novel's most [[passion|emotion]]ally charged subplots and occasions some of [[Tom Jones]]'s finest moral action.

## Character

Nancy is portrayed as modest, sensible, and genuinely devoted to Nightingale. She is "one of the best-natured and honestest of human creatures" (Book 14, Chapter 5), and her distress at Nightingale's intended departure is evident to Jones long before her mother perceives it. Her mother calls her "the darling of my soul, the delight of my eyes, the pride of my heart" (Book 14, Chapter 8).

Though she loves Nightingale deeply, she is not blind to her situation. When he abandons her, she attempts suicide twice — a testament to the extremity of her despair and the social ruin that seduction brought upon women of her station. Her eventual marriage to Nightingale, secured by Jones's intervention, is presented as a just restoration of honour rather than a romantic triumph.

## Role in the Novel

Nancy first appears in Book 14, Chapter 5, where she dines with Jones, Nightingale, and her mother. Her silent devotion to Nightingale is immediately apparent to the observant Jones. She accompanies the party to the masquerade, though her mother refuses to allow her to attend the entertainment itself.

The crisis of her story occurs in Book 14, Chapter 8, when [[mrs-miller|Mrs Miller]] discovers that Nightingale has seduced Nancy, left her pregnant, and fled the lodging-house. Her mother's anguished [[discovery|revelation]] to Jones — complete with Nightingale's cruel letter — sets in motion Jones's moral intervention. Nancy's situation becomes the vehicle for one of the novel's most sustained ethical arguments, as Jones persuades Nightingale that true honour requires him to marry the woman he has wronged.

With Jones's help — including his clever manipulation of old [[nightingale|Mr Nightingale]] in Book 14, Chapter 8 — Nancy is ultimately married to Nightingale. Her story concludes happily, but the narrowness of her escape from ruin underscores the precarious position of women without fortune or family protection.

## Themes

Nancy's story exemplifies the theme of **seduction and its social consequences**. Her near-ruin illustrates the devastating vulnerability of women without independent means, for whom the loss of reputation meant the loss of everything. The restoration of her honour through marriage, engineered by Jones's moral advocacy, demonstrates Fielding's belief that goodness — not birth or fortune — should govern human affairs. Her story also reinforces the novel's broader argument that women are not property to be used and discarded, but moral beings deserving of justice.
