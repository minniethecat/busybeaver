# Captain Waters

**Captain Waters** is an army officer whose marriage to [[Mrs Waters]] (the former [[Jenny Jones]]) provides the nominal respectability under which she travels, though the legitimacy of their union is questioned by his fellow soldiers.

## Character

Captain Waters is a shadowy figure known only through the reports of others. The serjeant at the Upton inn describes him as the husband of [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]] but adds that "some folks used indeed to doubt whether they were lawfully married in a church or no" (Book 9, Chapter 6). His own character is suggested in the serjeant's wry observation: "I fancy the captain may go to heaven when the sun shines upon a rainy day. But if he does, that is neither here nor there; for he won't want company."

Despite the doubts about his marriage, Waters is not portrayed unsympathetically. The serjeant notes that "he loves her not a bit the worse" for the rumours about her conduct with [[Ensign Northerton]], "and I am certain would run any man through the body that was to abuse her" (Book 9, Chapter 6).

## Role in the Novel

Captain Waters appears exclusively offstage. His regiment's movements provide the circumstances for [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]]'s encounter with [[Tom Jones]]. The captain's company had marched two days ahead of [[ensign-northerton|Ensign Northerton]]'s, reaching Worcester the day after Jones's confrontation with Northerton (Book 9, Chapter 7).

[[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]] and the captain had agreed that she would accompany him as far as Worcester, where they would part — she returning to Bath for the winter while he continued the campaign against the rebels. It is during her journey from Worcester that she encounters Jones at the Upton inn, and their subsequent entanglement becomes one of the novel's most consequential episodes.

Captain Waters's real significance is structural: his existence as a nominal husband allows the social fiction under which [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]], now [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]], travels the country. His absence at the critical moment enables the plot while his presence in the background preserves a veneer of respectability.

## Themes

Captain Waters exemplifies a recurring Fielding device: the offstage character whose existence enables the plot while remaining sufficiently indistinct that the reader does not invest in his fate. The ambiguity of his marriage to [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]] mirrors the novel's broader interest in legitimacy, both social and legal.
