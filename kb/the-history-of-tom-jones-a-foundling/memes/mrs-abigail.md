# Mrs Abigail

**Mrs Abigail** is the waiting-woman who serves [[Sophia Western]] after [[Mrs Honour]]'s departure from service. Fielding introduces her with deliberate casualness — "Mrs Abigail (so for shortness we will call her)" (Book 10, Chapter 4) — and uses her name throughout the novel as a generic term for a lady's maid, reflecting the eighteenth-century convention of calling waiting-women "Abigail."

## Character

Mrs Abigail is defined by her fastidiousness and social pretension. When she arrives at an inn with Sophia, her conversation with the landlady reveals her character in full: she demands fresh-laid eggs with bacon "cut very nice and thin," insists the landlady wash her hands before preparing the food, and laments the indignity of eating in a kitchen — "I do not believe I ever eat in one before" (Book 10, Chapter 4).

She is sharply conscious of social rank, dismissing the inn as fit only for "tradesmen and grasiers" and declaring, "I believe I know more of people of quality than such as you." Yet beneath her airs, she eats "very heartily for so delicate a person," revealing a healthy appetite that undercuts her pretensions.

Despite her sharp tongue, she is not without competence. She serves Sophia loyally through the journey towards [[london|London]], and Fielding notes that she is privy to Sophia's secrets through the communicative [[Mrs Honour]], who — before her departure — had "acquainted her sister Abigail with the whole history of Jones" (Book 13, Chapter 2).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Abigail first appears in Book 10, Chapter 4, at the inn where Sophia stops during her flight. In the kitchen, she encounters [[Partridge]], who unwittingly reveals that "[[tom-jones|Mr Jones]]" is in the same house. Abigail, recognising the name from [[honour|Honour]]'s confidences, drops her bacon in astonishment and hurries back to inform her mistress — a moment that brings Sophia dangerously close to an encounter with Jones.

She accompanies Sophia through subsequent adventures, including the meeting with [[Mrs Fitzpatrick]] on the road. In Book 11, Chapter 2, she shares a bed with [[mrs-fitzpatrick|Mrs Fitzpatrick]]'s maid at another inn, the two "sister Abigails" (as Fielding calls them) forming a parallel to their mistresses' reunion. During the journey to [[london|London]], she takes turns riding on [[lord-fellamar|Lord Fellamar]]'s horse, alternating with [[mrs-honour|Mrs Honour]].

Her name also becomes a generic term: Fielding repeatedly uses "Abigail" to refer to any lady's maid, and when [[mrs-fitzpatrick|Mrs Fitzpatrick]]'s maid relays gossip about Jones, the narrator notes it was "her sister Abigail" — a metonymy that blurs the line between the specific character and the type she represents.
