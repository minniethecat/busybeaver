# Reputation

**Reputation** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* is treated with characteristic Fielding ambivalence: as a social good that cannot be dismissed, yet one that must never be confused with genuine [[virtue]]. Throughout the novel, characters gain, lose, defend, and manipulate reputation — and the narrator repeatedly intervenes to distinguish the appearance of goodness from its substance.

## Reputation Versus Virtue

The novel's most sustained treatment of reputation occurs in the story of Jenny Jones. After her affair with the schoolmaster Mr Partridge, Jenny is "driven by necessity itself into a state of shame and misery" because "no persons of character will receive you into their houses" (Book I, Chapter 9). [[Squire Allworthy]] intervenes by sending her to a place "where she might enjoy the pleasure of reputation, after having tasted the ill consequences of losing it." The phrase "pleasure of reputation" is carefully chosen: reputation is a social pleasure, not a moral achievement.

Captain Blifil is described as a man who "at any time deposit" his natural roughness "and appear all gentleness and good-humour" — a man who can "put on a more serious character" at will (Book I, Chapter 10). His reputation is a costume, not a reflection of inner worth.

## Reputation and Power

The novel shows how reputation serves as an instrument of social power. [[Mr Blifil]] the younger carefully cultivates his reputation with Allworthy, knowing that his uncle's good opinion is the key to inheritance. His technique of praising his tutors behind their backs — "for he knew his uncle repeated all such compliments to the persons for whose use they were meant" (Book III, Chapter 5) — is a masterclass in reputation management.

[[Lady Bellaston]] and [[Lord Fellamar]] represent the aristocratic extreme: their reputations shield them from the consequences of actions that would destroy a person of lower station. Reputation, in their world, is a function of rank rather than conduct.

## The Loss and Recovery of Reputation

[[Tom Jones]]'s trajectory traces the loss and eventual recovery of reputation. Despite his good heart, his imprudence — the affair with Molly Seagrim, the apparent ingratitude toward Allworthy, the duel — blackens his name. The narrator's warning is direct: "If your inside be never so beautiful, you must preserve a fair outside also" (Book III, Chapter 7). Reputation requires maintenance; virtue alone cannot defend itself against "malice and envy."

## Reputation and the Narrator's Art

Fielding's narrator himself is acutely conscious of reputation — both his own as author and that of his characters. He insists that "we determined, when we first sat down to write this history, to flatter no man, but to guide our pen throughout by the directions of truth" (Book III, Chapter 1). This commitment to truthful representation over flattering depiction is itself a stance on reputation: better an honest portrait than a reputation preserved by silence.
