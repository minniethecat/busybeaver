# Mrs Miller

**Mrs Miller** is the landlady of the lodging-house in [[london|London]] where [[Tom Jones]] and [[Partridge]] take up residence. A widow with two daughters, Nancy and Betty, she is one of the most consistently virtuous characters in the novel.

## Character

Mrs Miller is defined by her generosity and maternal devotion. Despite her modest means, she repeatedly acts to relieve the distress of others. Her most notable act of charity occurs when she describes the pitiful condition of her cousin Anderson's family, prompting Jones to give her fifty pounds for their relief (Book 14, Chapter 5).

Her [[passion|emotion]]al [[truth|honesty]] is unmistakable: she weeps openly at tales of suffering, yet never allows sentiment to paralyse action. Fielding presents her as a model of practical benevolence, translating [[passion|feeling]]s into deeds.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Miller first appears in Book 14, Chapter 5, where her personal history is recounted. She becomes Jones's loyal ally when he lodges at her house. When Jones is imprisoned, she visits him with [[nightingale|Mr Nightingale]] (Book 17, Chapter 5) and later visits [[Sophia Western]] to advocate on his behalf (Book 17, Chapter 6).

Her most dramatic scene occurs in Book 17, Chapter 7, where she confronts [[Squire Allworthy]] and reveals the full extent of Jones's generosity, helping reverse Allworthy's negative [[reason|judgment]].

## Themes

Mrs Miller embodies the theme of **true charity versus hypocritical virtue**. Where characters like [[mr-thwackum|Mr Thwackum]] preach morality while acting selfishly, Mrs Miller practices benevolence without fanfare. Her actions contrast generosity of spirit with cold self-interest.
