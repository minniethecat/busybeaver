# Honour

**Honour** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* is a contested concept, appearing across nearly every book. Fielding distinguishes between the superficial honour of reputation—the gentleman's code demanding satisfaction for affronts—and true honour rooted in Christian virtue and integrity.

## Honour as Social Code

The aristocratic code of honour governs much of the novel's conflict. When [[Ensign Northerton]] strikes [[Tom Jones]] at the inn, the ensuing debate between Jones and the lieutenant in Book 7, Chapter 13 crystallizes the tension. The lieutenant insists "a man of honour can't keep" the Christian command to forgive, declaring "I love my religion very well, but I love my honour more." Jones, facing possible death from his wound, wrestles with "how terrible must it be to any one who is really a Christian, to cherish malice in his breast."

## True Honour Versus False

Fielding repeatedly contrasts genuine honour with its counterfeits. [[Squire Allworthy]] embodies honour as integrity—his [[reason|judgment]]s proceed from principle rather than social calculation. Conversely, characters like [[Lord Fellamar]] and [[Lady Bellaston]] deploy the language of honour to mask self-interest and manipulation. The narrator's ironic treatment of "honour" in the abstract serves to expose how frequently the term is invoked to justify vice rather than virtue.

## Honour and Prudence

The novel suggests that honour without prudence becomes dangerous. [[Tom Jones]]'s impulsive sense of honour—defending [[Molly Seagrim]]'s reputation, challenging Northerton—often leads him into trouble that wiser restraint might have avoided. Fielding thus positions honour as a virtue requiring the guidance of [[prudence]] and [[reason|judgment]] to function properly within society.
