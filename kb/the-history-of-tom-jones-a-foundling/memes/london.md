# London

**London** is the destination of [[Tom Jones]]'s journey and the setting for the novel's final third. In [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]], the city represents both opportunity and danger—a place where fortunes are made and lost, where love is tested by intrigue, and where the moral clarity of rural life gives way to urban ambiguity.

## London Versus the Country

Fielding structures the novel around a geographical-moral axis: Somersetshire represents pastoral virtue, while London embodies sophisticated corruption. At Paradise Hall, [[Squire Allworthy]]'s benevolent rule creates a moral order in which goodness flourishes. In London, by contrast, characters like [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]] operate through intrigue, masks, and manipulation.

Yet Fielding does not simply condemn the city. Tom's journey to London is necessary for his moral education. Only by confronting urban corruption can he develop the prudence that his rural upbringing failed to instill. London tests Tom's character—and he emerges from the trial worthy of [[Sophia Western]].

## London Society

Fielding's London is populated by a hierarchy of types: the fashionable aristocracy ([[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]], [[lord-fellamar|Lord Fellamar]]), the professional classes (lawyers, physicians), and the servant class ([[mrs-honour|Mrs Honour]], whose loyalty to Sophia is tested by city temptations). Each stratum reveals a different facet of urban moral life, from the cynical calculation of the elite to the precarious respectability of the middling sort.
