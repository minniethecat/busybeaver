# Mrs Honour

**Mrs Honour** (commonly addressed simply as "[[honour|Honour]]") is [[Sophia Western]]'s waiting-woman and confidante. Talkative, indiscreet, and loyal by turns, she serves as a comic intermediary between the novel's central lovers.

## Character

Mrs Honour is introduced as a woman of "a very communicative temper" (Book 4, Chapter 6). She cannot keep a secret, yet her garrulity often proves useful — it is through her that [[Tom Jones]] learns of Sophia's [[passion|feeling]]s, and through her that Sophia receives intelligence of Jones.

Her speech is marked by distinctive colloquialisms, most famously "Marry come up, I assure you" and the habitual qualifier "to be sure," which Fielding uses to comic effect.

Fielding describes her romantic history with characteristic irony. She once loved a nobleman's footman who "basely deserted her after a promise of marriage," and since then has kept "the broken remains of her heart" securely together, so that no man could possess any fragment of it (Book 5, Chapter 1).

She views all handsome men with "that equal regard and benevolence which a sober and virtuous mind bears to all the good." Her loyalty to Sophia is genuine, though tested. She is proud of her honest birth — her grandfather was a clergyman — and frequently reminds listeners that her parents were married.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Honour first appears in Book 4, Chapter 6, bringing Sophia news of [[molly-seagrim|Molly Seagrim]]'s pregnancy. Her report of Jones's alleged role inadvertently reveals Sophia's own [[passion|feeling]]s. In Book 6, Chapter 6, she becomes the channel through which Sophia learns of Jones's love — most memorably through the story of Jones kissing Sophia's muff.

In Book 7, Chapter 7 ("A strange resolution of Sophia, and a more strange stratagem of Mrs Honour"), she aids Sophia in her flight from her father's house. She accompanies Sophia to [[london|London]] and continues serving as a go-between, helping pass letters between the lovers.

## Themes

Mrs Honour embodies the theme of **servant as chorus** — she voices the practical, unsentimental perspective that contrasts with the romantic idealism of the main characters. Her comic verbosity serves as a vehicle for Fielding's satire of feminine discourse.
