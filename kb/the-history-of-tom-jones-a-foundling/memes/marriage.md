# Marriage

**Marriage** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* serves as the narrative's ultimate destination — every major plotline resolves toward matrimonial union — but Fielding subjects the institution to sustained moral scrutiny, examining the motives that drive characters toward the altar.

## In the Novel

The novel's most extended treatment of marriage comes in Book 1, Chapter 12, where [[Squire Allworthy]] delivers what amounts to a sermon on the proper foundations of matrimony. Rejecting both the worship of beauty and the calculation of fortune, Allworthy declares that "love is the only foundation of happiness in a married state, as it can only produce that high and tender friendship which should always be the cement of this union." He condemns marriages contracted from other motives as "a profanation of a most holy ceremony" that "generally end in disquiet and misery" (Book 1, Chapter 12).

This moral framework is immediately tested by the marriage of Captain Blifil to [[Bridget Allworthy]]. The captain's passion, the narrator wryly observes, is directed not at Bridget but at "Mr Allworthy's house and gardens, and of his lands, tenements, and hereditaments" — he "would most probably have contracted marriage with them, had he been obliged to have taken the witch of Endor into the bargain" (Book 1, Chapter 11). This fortune-driven union produces the novel's arch-villain, [[Master Blifil]], establishing a causal link between corrupted marriage and corrupted offspring.

Fielding also dramatizes troubled marriages through the [[Partridge]]s and the Westerns. Mrs Partridge's jealousy makes her husband "never master there, or anywhere else, in her presence" (Book 2, Chapter 3). Squire Western's deceased wife, meanwhile, is barely mentioned — her absence from the narrative underscores the patriarchal household's domestic imbalance. These secondary portraits build Fielding's case that marriages without mutual affection become prisons for both parties.

The novel's resolution affirms Allworthy's marriage philosophy: [[Tom Jones]] and [[Sophia Western]] unite only after Tom has proven his moral worth, while the fortune-hunting [[Master Blifil]] is expelled. Their marriage, blessed by both Allworthy and a grudgingly reconciled Squire Western, represents the union of love, [[virtue]], and prudence that Fielding holds up as the social ideal.
