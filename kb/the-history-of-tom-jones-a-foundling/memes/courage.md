# Courage

**Courage** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* is a quality Fielding both celebrates and complicates. Physical bravery wins admiration throughout the novel, yet Fielding insists that true courage — or fortitude — must be governed by moral intelligence to count as [[virtue]].

## In the Novel

[[Tom Jones]] is courageous from childhood. When [[Black George]] is caught poaching, young Tom takes the entire punishment upon himself rather than betray the gamekeeper — an act that the servants celebrate and that even [[Mr Square]] concedes "at first sight appeared like fortitude" (Book 3, Chapter 5). This instinct to shield others from harm, even at personal cost, is the seed of Tom's moral courage.

Tom's physical courage is repeatedly demonstrated in defence of others. He rescues [[Sophia Western]]'s bird, intervenes when [[Ensign Northerton]] insults Sophia, and fights to protect women on the road. Yet Fielding is careful to show that courage alone, unguided by [[prudence]], leads Tom into avoidable disasters. His impulsive challenge to Northerton at the inn results in a head wound that nearly kills him — an outcome that Fielding's narrator treats as a lesson in the limits of hot-blooded gallantry.

Fielding draws on classical and early modern distinctions between true and false courage. The narrator invokes Aristotle's observation that "the modesty and fortitude of men differ from those virtues in women; for the fortitude which becomes a woman, would be cowardice in a man" (Book 5, Chapter 6). Sophia Western, in this light, exhibits a quieter but no less genuine fortitude: her resistance to a forced marriage with [[Master Blifil]], her flight from her father's house, and her endurance of hardship on the road to London all require a moral courage that contrasts with Tom's physical impulsiveness.

The novel also explores courage's counterfeit: [[Master Blifil]] is physically cowardly — when Tom fights to defend Black George, Blifil "basely" informs on them both — yet he possesses the cold nerve to execute elaborate schemes of deception. Fielding thus distinguishes between the courage that faces danger openly and the audacity that operates in darkness.
