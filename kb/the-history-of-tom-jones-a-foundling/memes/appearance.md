# Appearance

Few themes are more pervasive in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* than the unreliability of **appearance**. Fielding constructs his entire narrative method around the gap between seeming and being: characters are repeatedly misjudged on the basis of outward signs, and the reader is trained, through the narrator's guidance, to distrust first impressions.

## Appearance in the Novel's Moral Scheme

The narrator makes the theme explicit in one of his most important direct addresses to the reader: "It is not enough that your designs, nay, that your actions, are intrinsically good; you must take care they shall appear so. If your inside be never so beautiful, you must preserve a fair outside also" (Book III, Chapter 7). This is not a counsel of hypocrisy but of [[prudence]]: virtue unprotected by a decent appearance is vulnerable to the malice of others. "Nor will Virtue herself look beautiful," he continues, "unless she be bedecked with the outward ornaments of decency and decorum."

## Appearance and the Foundling Plot

The novel's founding incident turns on appearance. The infant [[Tom Jones]] is discovered in [[Squire Allworthy]]'s bed, and his "beauty of innocence" — "appearing in those lively colours with which infancy and sleep always display it" (Book I, Chapter 3) — instantly moves Allworthy to compassion. The child's appearance of innocence, combined with Allworthy's charitable disposition, determines the entire course of the narrative. Yet this very susceptibility to appearances will later lead Allworthy to misjudge Tom's character.

## False Appearances

Fielding populates his novel with characters skilled in manipulating appearance. [[Mr Blifil]] the elder, the half-pay officer, can "at any time deposit" his natural roughness "and appear all gentleness and good-humour" (Book I, Chapter 10). His wife [[Bridget Allworthy]] "appeared, notwithstanding all her outward compliance, to be in her heart sufficiently displeased" — her religious exterior concealing resentment and partiality (Book III, Chapter 6).

[[Mr Blifil]] the younger perfects the art of false appearance. His pious declarations, his deference to authority, and his calculated praise of others are all performances designed to secure Allworthy's approval. The narrator observes that Tom, by contrast, "was not only deficient in outward tokens of respect, often forgetting to pull off his hat" (Book III, Chapter 5) — yet Tom's neglect of appearances proceeds from honesty, not disrespect.

## Appearance and Gender

Women in the novel are particularly vulnerable to judgment by appearance. The narrator describes how young women may be drawn to "cherry-cheeks, small, lily-white hands, sloe-black eyes... the outward ornaments of the person, for which men are beholden to the taylor, the laceman, the periwig-maker... and not to nature" (Book I, Chapter 11). [[Sophia Western]]'s genuine beauty is contrasted with the artificial charms of [[Lady Bellaston]], whose painted exterior mirrors her moral falseness.

## The Narrator's Own Appearance

With characteristic self-consciousness, Fielding's narrator makes a "short appearance, by way of chorus, on the stage" (Book III, Chapter 7), acknowledging that even his own authorial role is a kind of performance. He appears and withdraws, guiding the reader's judgment while warning against the very tendency to judge by appearances that his own narrative art exploits.
