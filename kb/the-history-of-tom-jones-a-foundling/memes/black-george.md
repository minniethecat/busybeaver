# Black George

**Black George** (George Seagrim) is the gamekeeper on [[Squire Allworthy]]'s estate and the father of [[Molly Seagrim]]. His family's poverty, his own legal troubles for poaching, and the loyalty [[Tom Jones]] shows toward him form a significant moral thread in the early books of the novel.

## Character

Black George is described as "in the main, a peaceable kind of fellow, and nothing choleric nor rash" (Book 4, Chapter 9). Though not without an irascible streak, he is fundamentally a poor man struggling to provide for his wife and five children. His reputation among the parish is poor — he is "universally disliked" by the servants and known to [[squire-western|Mr Western]]'s household as a poacher.

His moral character is ambiguous. While he inspires Jones's loyalty and receives considerable generosity, he also proves capable of deceit. When Jones entrusts him with the sale of a horse and other property to raise funds after being cast out, Black George keeps a portion of the proceeds for himself, demonstrating that gratitude does not always overcome self-interest.

## Role in the Novel

Black George first appears in the partridge-shooting incident (Book 3). Jones persuades him to trespass on a neighbouring manor to shoot game, and when the matter comes to light, Jones takes the blame upon himself to shield George from punishment. Though [[Master Blifil]] later reveals George's involvement, Jones's loyalty to the gamekeeper wins him the admiration of the servants.

George is subsequently dismissed by Allworthy after a separate poaching charge brought by [[Squire Western]]. Jones, moved by the sight of George's starving family, gives them money and eventually persuades Western to take George into his service as gamekeeper. The episode establishes Jones's compassionate character and his willingness to help the poor, even at cost to himself.

George also provides the narrative link to [[molly-seagrim|Molly Seagrim]], his daughter, whose affair with Jones becomes a significant subplot. When Molly's pregnancy is discovered, George's household is thrown into turmoil, and [[sophia-western|Sophia Western]]'s generous offer to take Molly into service creates a delicate situation that reveals the social tensions surrounding class and sexual morality.

## Themes

Black George embodies the moral complexity of the rural poor in Fielding's world. He is neither wholly virtuous nor irredeemably wicked — his poaching is driven by necessity, his ingratitude by desperation. Jones's persistent loyalty to him, even when George proves unworthy of it, illustrates the novel's central argument that charity should be given freely, not contingent on the recipient's merit.

The gamekeeper's story also exposes the harshness of the game laws and the precarious existence of the rural labouring class.
