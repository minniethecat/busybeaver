# Tom Jones

**Tom Jones** is the eponymous hero of Henry Fielding's [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]] (1749). Discovered as an infant in the bed of [[Squire Allworthy]], he is raised as a gentleman's son at Paradise Hall in [[Somerset]]. His journey from the rural west country to [[London]] forms one of the earliest and most influential examples of the English bildungsroman.

## Character

Tom is defined by the tension between his natural goodness and his impulsive passions. Fielding introduces him as possessing "a disposition truly honest and benevolent" yet equally prone to "the ungovernable impulses of his passions, which often hurried him into the most evident dangers" (Book 3, Chapter 2).

His virtues are spontaneous rather than calculated. Tom gives his small savings to the impoverished [[Black George]] Seagrim, risks his safety to rescue [[Sophia Western]] from her runaway horse, and refuses to abandon [[Partridge]] despite the latter's comic liability.

Fielding deliberately contrasts this instinctive [[virtue]] with the calculated piety of [[Master Blifil]] and [[Mr Square]]. Where Blifil performs morality for social advantage, Tom's goodness flows from an unguarded heart.

Tom's central flaw — sexual imprudence — becomes the engine of the plot. His entanglement with [[Molly Seagrim]], his affair with [[Lady Bellaston]] in London, and the rumors surrounding his relationship with [[Mrs Waters]] all arise from a nature that Fielding describes as "too susceptible of the tender passions" (Book 5, Chapter 10).

## Role in the Novel

Tom's narrative arc spans the entirety of the novel's eighteen books, tracing a trajectory from innocence through experience to ultimate vindication.

His origin as a foundling establishes the central mystery of the plot. Discovered by Allworthy upon returning from a three-month absence in London, the infant Tom immediately becomes the subject of village speculation (Book 1, Chapter 3). The servant [[Jenny Jones]] is initially accused of being his mother, and the schoolmaster [[Partridge]] is falsely suspected as his father.

Tom's banishment from Paradise Hall marks the novel's structural pivot. Deceived by Blifil's manipulation of Allworthy, Tom sets out on the road to [[Bristol]] intending to go to sea, initiating the picaresque journey that occupies Books 7 through 12 (Book 6, Chapter 12).

> Though he was entirely innocent of the crimes laid to his charge, yet the proofs were so strong against him, and the behaviour of Allworthy so resolute, that he thought it in vain to attempt his justification. (Book 6, Chapter 12)

Several key episodes punctuate Tom's journey. His encounter with the [[Man of the Hill]] in the woods near the Severn tests Tom's natural optimism against a philosophy of thoroughgoing misanthropy (Book 8, Chapters 10–15).

The rescue of [[Mrs Waters]] from the assassin [[Ensign Northerton]] at the Upton inn demonstrates Tom's characteristic courage, though it later becomes the source of a near-fatal misunderstanding when Mrs Waters is mistaken for Tom's mother (Book 9, Chapter 2).

In London, Tom becomes entangled with the sophisticated [[Lady Bellaston]], whose patronage temporarily compromises his devotion to Sophia and draws him into the moral ambiguities of town life (Book 13).

His imprisonment for the supposed murder of [[Mr Fitzpatrick]] brings Tom to the verge of execution, the novel's darkest moment (Book 17, Chapter 4).

The resolution turns on the revelation that Tom is the son of [[Bridget Allworthy]], the squire's deceased sister, making him Allworthy's nephew and Blifil's half-brother (Book 18, Chapter 7). This discovery restores Tom to Allworthy's favor and to Sophia's hand.

## Themes

Tom's character serves as a vehicle for several of Fielding's central philosophical concerns.

**Good Nature vs. Prudence.** Tom embodies Fielding's argument that spontaneous [[virtue]] matters more than calculated righteousness. His good heart compensates for his imprudence.

**Fortune and Providence.** Tom's journey is punctuated by accidents of [[fortune]] that Fielding uses to explore the relationship between chance, character, and providential design in human affairs.

**Appearance and Reality.** Nearly every judgment made about Tom is proven wrong, dramatizing the novel's theme that "a true judgment is seldom to be formed from external accidents" (Book 3, Chapter 1).
