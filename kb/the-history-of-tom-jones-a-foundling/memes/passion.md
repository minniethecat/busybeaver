# Passion

**Passion** is the driving psychological force in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]*. Fielding treats passion not as inherently sinful but as a natural component of [[nature|human nature]] that requires proper direction. The novel distinguishes between benevolent passions rooted in good nature and selfish passions that lead to ruin.

## The Theory of Love as Passion

In Book 6, Chapter 1, Fielding interrupts the narrative to offer a philosophical examination of the passion of [[love]]. He argues against philosophers who "pretend to have found out, that there is no such passion in the human breast," distinguishing between mere appetite—"the desire of satisfying a voracious appetite with a certain quantity of delicate white human flesh"—and genuine love, which has "gratitude and esteem for its basis." This passage establishes the novel's moral psychology: passions are real and powerful, but their moral quality depends on their object and governance.

## Passion in Character

[[Sophia Western]]'s passion for [[Tom Jones]] illustrates Fielding's ideal—her love is grounded in esteem for Tom's good nature, not mere desire. Her flight from her father's house (Book 10, Chapter 9) demonstrates passion governed by resolution and principle. Conversely, [[Squire Western]]'s hunting passion exemplifies uncontrolled appetite, while [[Master Blifil]]'s calculated self-interest shows the danger of a nature devoid of generous passion altogether.

## Passion and Gender

Fielding draws a notable contrast in how passion operates across gender. Sophia's passion, as the narrator observes in Book 4, Chapter 5, develops gradually from "some little kindness" born of observing Tom's good nature. The novel treats feminine passion as capable of constancy and moral discrimination, while masculine passion more frequently appears as impulsive and in need of restraint by [[reason]] and [[religion]].
