# Liberty

**Liberty** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* operates on multiple registers: as a political ideal, a personal right, and a narrative principle. Written in the aftermath of the Jacobite rising of 1745, the novel reflects Fielding's Whig sympathies while subjecting the rhetoric of liberty to the same ironic scrutiny it applies to all grand abstractions.

## Political Liberty

The novel's political dimension emerges most explicitly through [[Squire Western]], whose boisterous Jacobitism provides comic relief but also a serious undercurrent. Western rails against "Hanover rats" and declares "I hope the times are a coming when we shall make fools of them, and every man shall enjoy his own" (Book VI, Chapter 14). His sister [[Mrs Western]], in turn, invokes liberty against his tyrannical parenting: "English women, brother, I thank heaven, are no slaves. We are not to be locked up like the Spanish and Italian wives. We have as good a right to liberty as yourselves" (Book VI, Chapter 14). The irony is that Mrs Western's appeal to liberty serves her own project of forcing Sophia into a mercenary marriage — liberty of rhetoric masking tyranny of practice.

## Liberty and Marriage

The question of liberty is most pressing in the novel's marriage plot. [[Sophia Western]]'s resistance to the arranged match with [[Mr Blifil]] asserts a woman's right to choose her partner. When her father locks her in her chamber, the narrator frames the conflict in terms of fundamental freedom. Sophia's escape from her father's house — and later from the pursuit of [[Lord Fellamar]] — dramatizes the principle that consent, not coercion, must govern the most intimate of human bonds.

Fielding, through [[Squire Allworthy]]'s reflections on marriage, endorses "love the only foundation of happiness in a married state." Marriages contracted from other motives — lust, avarice, family ambition — are "a profanation of a most holy ceremony" (Book I, Chapter 12). This is a quiet but firm assertion of liberty within the domestic sphere.

## Liberty as Narrative Principle

Fielding extends the concept of liberty to his own practice as author. In one of the novel's most famous authorial intrusions, he declares himself "the founder of a new province of writing" and asserts: "I am at liberty to make what laws I please therein" (Book II, Chapter 1). This claim of authorial freedom — to vary chapter length, to pause the narrative for essayistic reflection, to address the reader directly — is itself an enactment of the Whig liberty the novel celebrates.

## Liberty and Its Abuses

The novel is equally attentive to the abuse of liberty. [[Tom Jones]]'s impulsive behaviour — his affair with [[Molly Seagrim]], his recklessness at [[Upton]] — represents liberty untamed by [[prudence]]. The narrator's young readers are cautioned that "no man can be good enough to enable him to neglect the rules of prudence" (Book III, Chapter 7). Liberty, like all goods in Fielding's moral universe, requires the guidance of [[reason]] and [[judgment]].
