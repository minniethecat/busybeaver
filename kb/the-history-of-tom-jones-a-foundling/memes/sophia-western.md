# Sophia Western

**Sophia Western** is the heroine of [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]], the beautiful and virtuous daughter of [[Squire Western]] whose love for [[Tom Jones]] drives the novel's romantic plot. Fielding presents her as the ideal of eighteenth-century womanhood: prudent yet passionate, dutiful yet independent when conscience demands.

## Character

Sophia is introduced in Book IV with a chapter titled "A description of Miss Sophia Western." Fielding describes her as "a middle-sized woman, but rather inclining to tall," whose features were "regular" and whose expression conveyed "sweetness and good nature" (Book 4, Chapter 2).

Her character is defined by the tension between filial duty and personal integrity. She respects her father's authority but refuses to marry [[Master Blifil]], whom she despises, even when [[Squire Western]] locks her in her chamber. Her resistance is not rebellion but conscience: she cannot vow love where she feels none.

Sophia's virtue is active rather than passive. When she learns of her aunt's scheme to force her marriage to Blifil, she flees her father's house with her maid [[Mrs Honour]], embarking on a journey to [[london|London]] that mirrors Tom's own. Fielding emphasizes that her courage is rooted in principle, not impulse.

## Role in the Novel

Sophia is the moral compass of the novel's romantic plot. Her love for Tom endures through his infidelities, her father's imprisonment, and the schemes of [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]]. Her forgiveness at the novel's close—when Tom has been revealed as Allworthy's heir—demonstrates the synthesis of love and prudence that Fielding presents as the ideal.

Her pursuit of Tom to [[london|London]] transforms her from a passive object of desire into an active agent. As Fielding notes in a chapter title, she forms "a strange resolution" and acts upon it (Book 11, Chapter 7). Her journey parallels Tom's but with greater moral clarity: where Tom stumbles into temptation, Sophia navigates the same dangers with unwavering principle.
