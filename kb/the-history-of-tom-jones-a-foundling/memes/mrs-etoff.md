# Mrs Etoff

**Mrs Etoff** is the waiting-woman of [[Lady Bellaston]] in [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]. She serves as an information conduit, gathering intelligence about [[Tom Jones]] and feeding her mistress's growing fascination with him.

## Character

Mrs Etoff is introduced as the woman "who had the honour to pin and unpin the [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]]" (Book 13, Chapter 6). Her role as a lady's waiting-woman gives her privileged access to her mistress's private chambers and, crucially, to her mistress's ear.

She is a skilled purveyor of gossip. Having "received compleat information concerning the said [[tom-jones|Mr Jones]]," she "had faithfully conveyed the same to her lady last night (or rather that morning) while she was undressing; on which accounts she had been detained in her office above the space of an hour and a half" (Book 13, Chapter 6).

Fielding notes with wry humour that [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]], "though generally well enough pleased with the narratives of Mrs Etoff at those seasons, gave an extraordinary attention to her account of Jones" (Book 13, Chapter 6). Mrs Etoff, "in her hurry, added so much to the beauty of his person to her report, that [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]] began to conceive him to be a kind of miracle in nature."

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Etoff first appears in Book 13, Chapter 6, where her report on Jones's physical attractions kindles [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]]'s interest. The intelligence she provides comes from [[Mrs Honour]], [[sophia-western|Sophia Western]]'s former maid, who had described Jones as "a very handsome fellow." Mrs Etoff amplifies this description, inadvertently (or perhaps deliberately) inflaming her mistress's desires.

Her function in the narrative is structural: she is the mechanism by which information about Jones reaches [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]], setting in motion the [[london|London]] intrigue that dominates the novel's final books. Without Mrs Etoff's gossip, [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]] would have no reason to pursue Jones, and a significant strand of the plot would unravel.

She later appears in Book 14, Chapter 2, when [[lady-bellaston|Lady Bellaston]] dispatches both [[mrs-honour|Mrs Honour]] and Mrs Etoff to the play, clearing her house for a private rendezvous with Jones.

## Themes

Mrs Etoff illustrates the **servant-class information economy** that drives much of the novel's plot. Fielding repeatedly shows how servants, maids, and waiting-women function as the nervous system of the social world, transmitting gossip and intelligence between households. Her embellishment of Jones's beauty also reflects the theme of **reputation versus reality** — the gap between who Jones is and who rumour makes him.
