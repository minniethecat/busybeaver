# Sentiment

**Sentiment** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* operates on two levels: as a narrative device through which Fielding exposes character, and as a moral concept distinguishing sincere feeling from hollow display. The term appears throughout the novel — characters declare their sentiments, the narrator analyzes them, and readers are invited to judge the distance between profession and practice.

## Sentiment as Revelation of Character

Fielding frequently uses the word "sentiments" to mark moments when characters reveal their true moral disposition. When [[Squire Allworthy]] discovers the infant [[Tom Jones]] in his bed, the narrator notes that he "soon began to be touched with sentiments of compassion for the little wretch before him" (Book I, Chapter 3). Here sentiment signifies genuine moral feeling — the benevolent impulse that defines Allworthy's character.

By contrast, Mrs Deborah Wilkins's sentiments are shown to be entirely performative: "this well-bred woman seldom opened her lips, either to her master or his sister, till she had first sounded their inclinations, with which her sentiments were always consonant" (Book I, Chapter 6). Her sentiments are not convictions but mirrors of her superiors' opinions.

## The Language of Sentiment and Its Abuses

Characters throughout the novel deploy the language of sentiment to mask self-interest. [[Mr Blifil]] "took frequent occasions of praising" his tutors behind their backs, knowing his uncle "repeated all such compliments to the persons for whose use they were meant" (Book III, Chapter 5). His sentiments are strategic; they serve ambition rather than truth. The narrator acknowledges this danger directly: "many true and just sentiments often came from the mouths of these persons" — yet this does not redeem the speaker, for "neither of these men were fools" and their principles were chiefly wrong (Book III, Chapter 3).

## Sentiment and the Education of Sophia

[[Sophia Western]]'s sentimental education is central to the novel's romantic plot. When she discovers her love for Tom Jones, the narrator quotes: "Parva leves capiunt animos — Small things affect light minds" (Book IV, Chapter 5), and notes that "from this day Sophia began to have some little kindness for Tom Jones, and no little aversion for his companion." Her sentiments develop through experience and reflection, not convention — a contrast to the arranged-marriage logic imposed by her aunt, [[Mrs Western]], who treats matrimony "not as a romantic scheme of happiness arising from love" but "rather as a fund in which prudent women deposit their fortunes" (Book VI, Chapter 14).

## True Sentiment Versus Sentimentality

Fielding distinguishes between authentic sentiment and what a later age would call sentimentality. The narrator's famous declaration — "I would rather have buried the sentiments of these two persons in eternal oblivion, than have done any injury to either of these glorious causes" (Book III, Chapter 3) — insists that genuine moral sentiments (benevolence, honour, virtue) must be protected from the ridicule that properly attaches only to their hypocritical imitations. The novel's comic method depends on this distinction: it laughs at false sentiment to clear ground for the true.
