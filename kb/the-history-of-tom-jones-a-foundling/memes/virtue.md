# Virtue

**Virtue** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* is defined as active benevolence — the disposition to do good to others — rather than conformity to abstract moral rules or religious doctrine. This conception draws on the Latitudinarian Anglican emphasis on practical charity and good works over doctrinal orthodoxy.

## In the Novel

Fielding presents two complementary models of virtue. [[Squire Allworthy]] represents the mature, reflective form: his house is "the only house in the kingdom where you was sure to gain a dinner by deserving it" (Book 1, Chapter 10). His charity is deliberate, consistent, and informed by judgment.

[[Tom Jones]] represents the instinctive form: his generosity is impulsive and uncalculated. He gives his last coin to [[Black George]], risks himself to rescue [[Mrs Waters]] from Northerton's assault, and refuses to abandon [[Partridge]] despite the latter's liability. Fielding argues that this spontaneous goodness, though prone to errors of [[passion]], is the real foundation of moral worth.

The novel's central moral argument is that Tom's warm-hearted imprudence is superior to [[Master Blifil]]'s cold prudence. Fielding does not deny that Tom's conduct falls short — his sexual entanglements with [[Molly Seagrim]] and [[Lady Bellaston]] are clearly faults — but insists that "a good heart" matters more than "a good head" (Book 3, Chapter 2).

This position places Fielding within the "benevolist" tradition of eighteenth-century moral philosophy, associated with figures such as the Earl of Shaftesbury and Francis Hutcheson, who held that virtue arises from natural affection rather than rational calculation.
