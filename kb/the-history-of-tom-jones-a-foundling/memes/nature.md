# Nature

**Nature** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* carries multiple layers of meaning: it denotes innate human character, the natural moral law, and the standard against which artifice and hypocrisy are measured. Fielding's use of the concept draws on both classical and Enlightenment sources.

## Good Nature as Moral Foundation

The novel's central moral claim is that good nature—an innate benevolent disposition—forms the necessary basis of [[virtue]]. [[Tom Jones]] is repeatedly described as possessing "good nature," and though his imprudence leads him into error, his fundamental benevolence distinguishes him from the calculating [[Master Blifil]]. As the narrator observes in Book 3, Chapter 4, Tom's faults arise from impulse and generosity, while Blifil's apparent virtue masks a cold self-interest. Fielding suggests that good nature without [[prudence]] may err, but prudence without good nature is merely cunning.

## Nature Versus Artifice

The novel consistently opposes natural goodness to social artifice. [[Sophia Western]]'s natural modesty and [[truth|sincerity]] contrast with [[Lady Bellaston]]'s sophisticated dissimulation. The city, particularly [[london|London]] in the later books, represents a realm where nature is corrupted by fashion, intrigue, and the pursuit of reputation. Fielding's narrator, claiming to write "the history of human nature" in Book 1, Chapter 1, positions the entire novel as an examination of how character reveals itself under varying circumstances.

## Nature as Moral Standard

Fielding invokes nature as a normative concept—actions are judged by their conformity to or deviation from the natural order. [[Mr Square]] attempts to justify his conduct by appealing to nature ("Nothing is indeed unfit which is not unnatural," Book 5, Chapter 5), but the novel exposes this as a perversion of the concept. True nature, for Fielding, aligns with benevolence and [[truth]], not appetite.
