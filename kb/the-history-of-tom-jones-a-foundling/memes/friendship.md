# Friendship

**Friendship** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* figures as one of the most valued yet most vulnerable human bonds. Fielding distinguishes between genuine friendship, grounded in mutual affection and virtue, and counterfeit forms that serve self-interest, ambition, or flattery. The novel's narrator repeatedly reflects on the nature of friendship, offering some of the work's most pointed moral observations on the subject.

## Friendship and Virtue

For Fielding, true friendship is inseparable from [[virtue]]. [[Squire Allworthy]] embodies this union: his friendship is extended not only to his social equals but to the unfortunate — the foundling [[Tom Jones]], the disgraced Jenny Jones, and the impoverished clergyman Mr Supple. Allworthy's friendship with Captain Blifil, by contrast, proves disastrous precisely because it is founded on misjudged character rather than genuine merit. The narrator observes that "by conferring an obligation, they do not always procure a friend, but are certain of creating many enemies," a cynical truth borne out by the captain's subsequent ingratitude.

## False Friendship and Ingratitude

The novel devotes considerable attention to the problem of false friends. Captain Blifil, having secured his fortune through his brother Dr Blifil's introduction to Allworthy, discards the doctor with the cold declaration: "If you dislike anything in my brother's house, sir, you know you are at liberty to quit it." The narrator pronounces this "strange, cruel, and almost unaccountable ingratitude" as breaking "the poor doctor's heart," observing that "ingratitude never so thoroughly pierces the human breast as when it proceeds from those in whose behalf we have been guilty of transgressions" (Book I, Chapter 13).

[[Lord Fellamar]] and [[Lady Bellaston]] represent the aristocratic abuse of friendship as a mask for seduction and manipulation. Their social charm conceals a complete absence of genuine regard.

## Friendship in Marriage

Fielding, through Allworthy, articulates an ideal in which marriage is built upon friendship. Allworthy declares: "I have always thought love the only foundation of happiness in a married state, as it can only produce that high and tender friendship which should always be the cement of this union" (Book I, Chapter 12). Marriages contracted from motives of "lust or avarice" he condemns as a "profanation of a most holy ceremony." This ideal is fulfilled in the union of Tom Jones and [[Sophia Western]], whose bond matures from romantic attraction into mutual respect and trust.

## The Fragility of Friendship

Fielding acknowledges that friendship requires forbearance. In one of the novel's most memorable reflections, the narrator muses: "I hope my friends will pardon me when I declare, I know none of them without a fault; and I should be sorry if I could imagine I had any friend who could not see mine. Forgiveness of this kind we give and demand in turn. It is an exercise of friendship, and perhaps none of the least pleasant" (Book II, Chapter 7). This tolerant wisdom — that friendship flourishes not through perfection but through mutual forgiveness — is central to the novel's moral vision.
