# Inn

The **inn** is the quintessential setting of [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]], appearing across nearly every book of the novel. Inns function as microcosms of English society where characters of every rank and disposition converge and reveal themselves.

## Inns as Crossroads

The inn occupies a unique position between public and private space. Unlike the great houses where strict social hierarchies govern behavior, the inn is a place of relative freedom and unpredictability.

Here, a foundling may dine beside a gentleman, a lady's maid may encounter her mistress's lover, and a barber-surgeon may debate theology with a soldier.

Fielding exploits this fluidity to generate comic and dramatic encounters. The inn at Upton, where [[Tom Jones]] meets [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]] and is later discovered by [[Sophia Western]], is the novel's most famous example: an entire book's worth of plot converges on a single inn over one night.

## The Journey Through Inns

Tom's journey from Paradise Hall to [[london|London]] is structured as a series of inn stops:

| Inn | Event | Significance |
|-----|-------|-------------|
| Inn at Hambrook | Tom meets a Quaker and soldiers | First taste of the world beyond Paradise Hall |
| Inn at Gloucester | Tom encounters [[Partridge]] | Beginning of their picaresque partnership |
| Inn at Upton | Crossed paths and mistaken identities | The novel's comic climax on the road |
| Various [[london|London]] inns | Tom's arrival in the city | Transition from rural to urban adventure |

## Inns and Social Commentary

Through inn scenes, Fielding satirizes the pretensions of English society. Landlords flatter the wealthy and disdain the poor; travelers boast, deceive, and condescend; and the bill of fare reveals as much about character as any philosophical dialogue.
