# Mrs Deborah Wilkins

**Mrs Deborah Wilkins** is the housekeeper at Paradise Hall, the estate of [[Squire Allworthy]]. It is she who first discovers the infant [[Tom Jones]] in her master's bed and who subsequently leads the parish investigation to identify the child's mother.

## Character

Fielding introduces Mrs Wilkins in Book 1, Chapter 3 as a woman of "the fifty-second year of her age" who had "never beheld a man without his coat." Her extreme prudery is matched by an equally extreme cruelty: upon discovering [[tom-jones|the foundling]], her first instinct is not compassion but condemnation. She calls for the mother to be "committed to Bridewell, and whipt at the cart's tail" and suggests the infant be put in a basket and left at the churchwarden's door to die (Book 1, Chapter 3).

Fielding's satire of Mrs Wilkins is sharp and sustained. He describes her as one of those persons whose nature it is to "insult and tyrannize over little people" as recompense for their "extreme servility and condescension to their superiors" (Book 1, Chapter 6). Her moral [[reason|judgment]]s are entirely determined by the inclinations of those above her: when [[bridget-allworthy|Miss Bridget]] shows affection for [[tom-jones|the foundling]], Mrs Wilkins instantly reverses her position and begins "squeezing and kissing" the child she had moments before condemned.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Wilkins's most significant contribution to the plot occurs early in Book 1. Dispatched by Allworthy to investigate the parish, she fixes suspicion upon [[Jenny Jones]], interrogating her with "the gravity of a judge, with somewhat more than his austerity" and beginning her oration with the words "You audacious strumpet!" (Book 1, Chapter 6). Jenny's confession follows, and [[tom-jones|the foundling]]'s supposed parentage is established — wrongly, as the novel ultimately reveals.

In Book 2, Chapter 2, Mrs Wilkins makes a second discovery: she learns of a quarrel between the schoolmaster [[Partridge]] and his wife, and concludes that [[partridge|Partridge]] is the father of Jenny's child. This accusation, brought to Allworthy, results in [[partridge|Partridge]]'s ruin — the removal of his annuity and the loss of his livelihood. Both of Mrs Wilkins's investigations are thus successful in their immediate aims but fundamentally mistaken in their conclusions, a pattern that underscores the novel's scepticism about officious moral certainty.

## Themes

Mrs Wilkins represents the theme of **servile cruelty** — the tendency of those who bow to power to tyrannise over those beneath them. She is Fielding's portrait of moral authoritarianism untempered by genuine [[passion|feeling]]: her rigid adherence to propriety produces not virtue but vindictiveness. Her readiness to reverse her [[reason|judgment]]s at a nod from her superiors exposes the emptiness of her moral posturing.
