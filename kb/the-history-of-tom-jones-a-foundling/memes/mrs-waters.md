# Mrs Waters

**Mrs Waters** (also known as **[[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]]**) is a significant figure in the mystery of [[Tom Jones]]'s parentage. First appearing as a distressed woman rescued by Jones, she later becomes briefly his lover — a relationship that creates one of the novel's most dramatic complications.

## Character

Mrs Waters is a woman of spirit and strong will. When insulted by a landlady who suspects her of low character, she retorts with imperious dignity: "I would have you know, creature, I have a spirit above that" (Book 9, Chapter 7).

Fielding describes her as physically robust — "her limbs were indeed full of strength and agility" — and her mind "no less animated with spirit" (Book 9, Chapter 7). She is introduced as the wife of [[captain-waters|Captain Waters]], though other soldiers hint that the marriage may not be entirely legitimate.

Her romantic history includes involvement with [[ensign-northerton|Ensign Northerton]], the same man who later attempts to rob and assault her on the Upton road. Fielding treats her moral character with characteristic ambiguity — presenting her as a woman of strong passions and practical sense.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Waters first appears in Book 9, Chapter 7, where Jones hears her screams and rescues her from Northerton, who has attacked and robbed her. Jones brings her to the inn at Upton, where she recovers and, appreciative of her handsome rescuer, seduces him (Book 10, Chapter 5).

The full account of her history is given in Book 10, Chapter 7. She had been travelling with Northerton, financing him with bank-notes and a diamond ring, only for him to betray her. Her resilience — physically fighting off her attacker until Jones arrived — demonstrates her courage.

The crucial [[discovery|revelation]] comes later: Mrs Waters is in fact **[[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]]**, the servant from [[squire-allworthy|Squire Allworthy]]'s household originally accused of being Jones's mother. This [[discovery|revelation]] causes temporary horror before the truth of Jones's actual parentage (as [[bridget-allworthy|Bridget Allworthy]]'s son) is established.

## Themes

Mrs Waters embodies the novel's concern with **uncertain identity and concealed truths**. Her dual identity as [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]] drives one of the central mystery plots. Her rescue by Jones and her role in the parentage [[discovery|revelation]] tie her to the theme of **providence and coincidence** that structures the novel.
