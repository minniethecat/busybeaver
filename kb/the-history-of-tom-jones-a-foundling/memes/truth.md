# Truth

**Truth** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* operates on two interconnected levels: as a narrative principle governing the historian-narrator's method, and as a moral quality distinguishing characters. Fielding's narrator repeatedly invokes truth to authorize the fiction, while the plot rewards truthfulness and punishes deception.

## Narrative Truth

From the novel's opening, the narrator adopts the persona of a historian committed to factual accuracy. In Book 9, Chapter 1, he reflects on the requirements of his craft: the historian "must converse with all ranks and degrees of men," learning from both high and low life, because "the follies of either rank do in reality illustrate each other." This commitment to comprehensive truth—recording [[nature|human nature]] as it is, not as convention would represent it—justifies the novel's range from country estates to [[london|London]] drawing-rooms and roadside [[inn|inns]].

## Truth and Character

The novel's moral economy consistently rewards truthfulness. [[Tom Jones]], despite his many follies, never lies about his essential nature; his openness contrasts with [[Master Blifil]]'s systematic deception. The [[discovery|revelation]] of Tom's true parentage in the final books literalizes the theme: hidden truth emerges, and characters are judged by their relationship to it. [[Squire Allworthy]]'s tragic error—believing false reports about Tom—demonstrates how even a just man can be misled when truth is deliberately obscured.

## Self-Deception

Fielding extends the treatment of truth to include self-deception. Characters like [[Mr Square]] and [[Mr Thwackum]] do not merely lie to others; they have persuaded themselves that their self-serving conduct aligns with philosophical or religious principle. The novel suggests that the most dangerous falsehoods are those we tell ourselves.
