# Mr Fitzpatrick

**Mr Fitzpatrick** is an Irish gentleman who married [[mrs-fitzpatrick|Harriet Fitzpatrick]], the cousin of [[Sophia Western]]. His jealousy, financial recklessness, and violent temper make him a disruptive figure at several key junctures in the novel.

## Character

Fitzpatrick is introduced as a man "born a gentleman, though not worth a groat" (Book 10, Chapter 6). Though Fielding grants that he was not niggardly, his generosity took the form of reckless spending: having received a handsome fortune with his wife, he "had now spent every penny of it, except some little pittance which was settled upon her."

To gain control of even that remnant, he subjected her to such cruelty, compounded by "jealousy of the bitterest kind," that she was forced to flee from him.

Fitzpatrick's mental faculties are uncharitably summarised by the narrator as one of "those compositions which nature makes up in too great a hurry, and forgets to put any brains into their head" (Book 10, Chapter 6). He requires his friend Maclachlan to suggest what ought to have been obvious — that the lady at the inn was his own wife — before he acts upon it.

## Role in the Novel

Fitzpatrick first becomes entangled in the narrative through his wife's history, recounted across several chapters of Book 11. [[mrs-fitzpatrick|Mrs Fitzpatrick]] tells Sophia of her unhappy marriage and eventual flight from her husband's cruelty.

His most dramatic intervention occurs at the Upton inn, where, upon Maclachlan's prompting, he bursts into what he believes to be his wife's chamber, only to find [[Tom Jones]] and [[Mrs Waters]] together. Discovering his mistake, he withdraws but nurses a grudge against Jones for having "bate" (beaten) him (Book 10, Chapter 2).

When [[Squire Western]] arrives at the same inn in pursuit of Sophia, Fitzpatrick attempts to curry favour with his uncle by claiming to know Sophia's whereabouts. He also volunteers his legal knowledge — having served three years as a clerk to an attorney in Ireland — to assist in drawing up a commitment against Jones on the spurious charge of stealing Sophia's muff (Book 10, Chapter 7).

Fitzpatrick later encounters Jones in [[london|London]], where a duel ensues. Jones wounds Fitzpatrick, an event that becomes a critical plot point, as it leads to Jones's imprisonment and the eventual [[discovery|revelation]] of his true parentage.

## Themes

Fitzpatrick illustrates the destructive consequences of financial irresponsibility and unrestrained jealousy within marriage. His treatment of his wife — the cruelty that drove her to flee — forms a dark counterpoint to the novel's central love story.

His character also serves as a vehicle for Fielding's satire of Irish gentlemen who attempt to live beyond their means by marrying fortunes they cannot manage.
