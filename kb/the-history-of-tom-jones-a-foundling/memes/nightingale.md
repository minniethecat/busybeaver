# Mr Nightingale

**Mr Nightingale** is a young gentleman of fortune who lodges at [[mrs-miller|Mrs Miller]]'s house in [[london|London]], where he becomes the intimate friend of [[Tom Jones]]. His courtship of [[Mrs Miller]]'s daughter Nancy, and his near-abandonment of her, forms one of the novel's key moral episodes.

## Character

Nightingale is described as a man of strict honour and [[truth|honesty]] in ordinary transactions of life, yet "somewhat loose in his morals" in affairs of love (Book 14, Chapter 5). He is good-natured, generous in sentiment, and capable of genuine affection — as shown by his real attachment to [[nancy-miller|Nancy Miller]] — but his principles are not firm enough to withstand the pressure of filial duty and worldly considerations.

His moral weakness is not malice but a failure of courage: he would rather flee than face the consequences of his actions. Jones's intervention is decisive in persuading him to marry Nancy, demonstrating that Nightingale possesses the capacity for honourable conduct when guided by a stronger moral example.

## Role in the Novel

Nightingale first appears in Book 14, Chapter 5, where he dines with Jones and the Millers. He accompanies Jones to the masquerade that evening, where he mingles with the fashionable crowd while Jones pursues his own mystery. His intimacy with Jones deepens rapidly, and he offers to share lodgings with him in Pall-mall.

The central episode of his character unfolds across Books 14–15. When [[Mrs Miller]] discovers that Nightingale has seduced Nancy and left her pregnant, she reveals the situation to Jones in anguish. Jones seeks Nightingale at his new lodgings (Book 14, Chapter 7) and delivers an impassioned moral appeal, arguing that true honour requires Nightingale to marry the woman he has wronged. Nightingale initially resists, citing the fear of public disgrace, but is ultimately persuaded by Jones's reasoning.

Jones further secures the match by visiting old Mr Nightingale (Book 14, Chapter 8) and, through a strategic misunderstanding in which the father believes Nancy to be the wealthy Miss Harris, obtains his consent. Nightingale later visits Jones in prison (Book 17, Chapter 5), proving his loyalty as a friend. By the novel's end he is married to Nancy, with the match settled to the satisfaction of all parties.

## Themes

Nightingale embodies the theme of **honour versus worldly opinion**. His internal conflict — knowing what is right but fearing social disgrace — illustrates Fielding's argument that true honour derives from goodness rather than reputation. His eventual marriage to Nancy, brought about by Jones's moral intervention, demonstrates the redemptive power of friendship and the triumph of genuine honour over worldly calculation.
