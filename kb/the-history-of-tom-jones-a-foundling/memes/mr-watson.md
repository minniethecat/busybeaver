# Mr Watson

**Mr Watson** is a figure from the [[Man of the Hill]]'s autobiographical narrative whose friendship, corruption, and ultimate betrayal form a cautionary tale about the dangers of gaming and false companionship.

## Character

Watson is introduced as a fellow-collegiate of the [[man-of-the-hill|Man of the Hill]] who greets his old friend with apparent warmth, shaking him heartily by the hand and proposing they drink together (Book 8, Chapter 12). Beneath this convivial exterior, however, Watson is a compulsive gambler trapped in a cycle of alternating wealth and poverty — "instead of making a fortune by his profession, as some others did, he was alternately rich and poor" (Book 8, Chapter 13).

His moral compass is elastic. When the [[man-of-the-hill|Man of the Hill]] confesses he has no money, Watson suggests dining and "scoring it behind the door" — leaving without paying. He encourages his friend to see theft as a "meritorious action" when the victim is rich, and introduces him to the gaming tables where both men are eventually ruined.

## Role in the Novel

Watson appears exclusively in the [[man-of-the-hill|Man of the Hill]]'s extended narrative (Books 8, Chapters 12–14). He first rescues the destitute narrator from hunger in [[london|London]], but this apparent kindness masks a design to draw him into gaming. At the tables, Watson puts down a large sum as a decoy to attract other players (Book 8, Chapter 12).

After the [[man-of-the-hill|Man of the Hill]]'s return from visiting his dying father, he finds Watson in Bath, destitute and having attempted suicide. The narrator gives him money and extracts a promise to abandon gaming, but Watson immediately breaks it. The two later join the Duke of Monmouth's rebellion together — Watson "from different motives" than patriotism, "for the spirit of a gamester will carry a man as far upon such an occasion as the spirit of patriotism" (Book 8, Chapter 14).

Watson's ultimate act is one of treachery. After escaping from the Battle of Sedgemoor together, Watson leaves the wounded narrator supposedly to find provisions — but instead betrays him to a party of King James's horse, delivering him into captivity. The [[man-of-the-hill|Man of the Hill]] calls him "this base, barbarous, treacherous villain" (Book 8, Chapter 14).

## Themes

Watson embodies the destructive power of gaming addiction and the unreliability of companions formed through vice. His arc — from generous friend to desperate suicide to treacherous betrayer — traces the moral degradation that Fielding presents as inseparable from the gaming life. The [[man-of-the-hill|Man of the Hill]]'s story uses Watson as a warning to Jones about the company men keep.
