# Justice

**Justice** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* operates on two levels: the human justice of courts and magistrates, which is shown to be fallible and often corrupt, and the providential justice of the novel's comic design, which ensures that virtue is rewarded and vice exposed.

## In the Novel

Fielding, himself a magistrate, brings professional insight to his depiction of legal institutions. The novel repeatedly demonstrates how legal processes can miscarry. The petty magistrate who attempts to prosecute [[Jenny Jones]] for bastardy in Book 1 exemplifies the gap between legal form and moral substance.

The most dramatic failure of human justice occurs when [[Tom Jones]] is imprisoned for the supposed murder of [[Mr Fitzpatrick]] (Book 17, Chapter 4). The evidence is circumstantial but damning, and Tom's own impulsive character makes the accusation plausible. He is saved not by the legal system but by the revelation of truth through private channels.

This pattern reflects Fielding's Augustan conviction that human institutions are imperfect instruments. Justice in the full sense requires the convergence of [[reason]], evidence, and good character — a combination rarely found in any single person or court.

The novel's resolution provides poetic rather than legal justice. [[Master Blifil]]'s exposure and expulsion, Tom's restoration to [[Squire Allworthy]]'s favor, and his marriage to [[Sophia Western]] together constitute Fielding's argument that a providential order, operating through [[fortune]], achieves what human law cannot.
