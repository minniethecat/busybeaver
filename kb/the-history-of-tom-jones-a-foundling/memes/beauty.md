# Beauty

**Beauty** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* functions as an epistemological problem. Physical attractiveness reliably attracts admiration but unreliably signals moral worth, forcing Fielding's characters — and his readers — to distinguish between surface and substance.

## In the Novel

Fielding establishes the moral ambiguity of beauty through paired contrasts. [[Sophia Western]] is both beautiful and virtuous, her appearance reflecting her inner goodness. Fielding describes her as possessing "a dignity beyond her years, and a sweetness which set all her other beauties in the most advantageous light" (Book 4, Chapter 2).

Yet [[Lady Bellaston]] demonstrates that beauty can coexist with corruption. Her physical allure draws [[Tom Jones]] into a morally compromising entanglement in London, illustrating how aesthetic attraction can override [[reason]]. Similarly, [[Molly Seagrim]]'s physical charms mask her promiscuity until Tom discovers her with [[Mr Square]].

Fielding extends this analysis to male beauty. [[Master Blifil]]'s "sober and regular countenance" earns him a reputation for virtue that his conduct belies. Tom's handsome features, by contrast, predispose observers to forgive his faults — a pattern Fielding treats with characteristic irony.

The novel's treatment of beauty reflects the eighteenth-century discourse on physiognomy. Fielding does not reject the idea that character can be read in appearance, but insists that such reading requires the judgment of a discerning observer — someone like [[Squire Allworthy]], who learns painfully that a fair face may conceal a foul heart.
