# Mrs Hunt

**Mrs Hunt** is a wealthy young widow who appears briefly in *[[tom-jones|Tom Jones]]* (Book 15, Chapter 11) to test the hero's constancy. Her marriage proposal, delivered by letter and backed by a considerable fortune, presents [[Tom Jones]] with a temptation that he ultimately resists out of love for [[Sophia Western]].

## Character

Mrs Hunt is introduced as a friend of [[Mrs Miller]], "intimately acquainted with the women" at the house where Jones lodges. Fielding describes her with characteristic precision: "Her age was about thirty, for she owned six-and-twenty; her face and person very good, only inclining a little too much to be fat" (Book 15, Chapter 11).

Her history is succinctly sketched. She had been "married young by her relations to an old Turkey merchant, who, having got a great fortune, had left off trade." With him she "lived without reproach, but not without pain, in a state of great self-denial, for about twelve years." Her virtue, Fielding wryly observes, "was rewarded by his dying and leaving her very rich."

Now a year into her widowhood, her circumstances and temperament converge on a single resolution: "Very good health, a very warm constitution, and a good deal of religion, made it absolutely necessary for her to marry again; and she resolved to please herself in her second husband, as she had done her friends in the first." She has passed her widowhood in retirement, "seeing only a few particular friends, and dividing her time between her devotions and novels, of which she was always extremely fond."

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Hunt's role, though confined to a single chapter, is morally significant. Having observed Jones at [[mrs-miller|Mrs Miller]]'s house, she sends him a letter proposing marriage. Her proposal is candid and generous: she offers her person, her fortune, and explicitly releases Jones from any obligation to Sophia — "I have heard of your constancy to [[sophia-western|Miss Western]]; but as I am convinced your happiness is what she has not in her power to bestow, I hope you will not think me impertinent in endeavouring to convince you that it is in the power of another."

The letter precipitates a crisis of conscience for Jones. His finances are at "a very low ebb" — of all he had received from [[Lady Bellaston]], "not above five guineas remained," and he had been "dunned by a tradesman for twice that sum" that very morning. Sophia is in her father's custody with little hope of recovery. The widow's fortune "would have been exceeding convenient to him, and he could have no objection to her in any respect. On the contrary, he liked her as well as he did any woman except Sophia."

Jones briefly entertains the notion that abandoning Sophia might be an act of friendship — releasing her from a hopeless attachment — but "that refinement was not able to stand very long against the voice of nature, which cried in his heart that such friendship was treason to love." He refuses Mrs Hunt with a letter of courteous but firm rejection, declaring that his heart is "entirely engrossed by a lady" and that "to accept your offer would be the greatest injustice to you, as I should give my hand without my heart."

After sending the letter, Jones takes out Sophia's muff, kisses it several times, and struts about his room "with more satisfaction of mind than ever any Irishman felt in carrying off a fortune of fifty thousand pounds."
