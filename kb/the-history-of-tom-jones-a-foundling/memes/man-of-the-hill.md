# Man of the Hill

The **Man of the Hill** is a reclusive figure whom [[Tom Jones]] and [[Partridge]] encounter during their journey toward Gloucester. Living in isolation with only an elderly housekeeper, he relates the long and eventful history of his life, offering one of the novel's most developed embedded narratives.

## Character

The Man of the Hill is a former gentleman who, after a life of travel, ambition, and disillusionment, has withdrawn entirely from human society. His house, filled with curiosities and weapons, reflects a man of wide experience who now lives in fear of the world he has renounced.

The country people regard him with superstitious dread, calling him a devil or a spirit, and he walks only by night to avoid being seen.

His outlook is deeply misanthropic. Having witnessed war, political intrigue, and personal betrayal, he has concluded that [[nature|human nature]] is irredeemably corrupt. His philosophical position — that the study of mankind is a study of folly and vice — forms a stark contrast to Jones's benevolent optimism.

## Role in the Novel

Jones and [[partridge|Partridge]] take shelter in the Man of the Hill's dwelling after a storm (Book 8, Chapter 11). Over three chapters (Book 8, Chapters 11–14), the hermit recounts his autobiography: his youthful follies at Oxford, his military service in the Jacobite rising of 1715, his travels through Europe, his imprisonment, and his final retreat into solitude.

The episode culminates in Book 8, Chapter 15, with "a curious discourse between [[tom-jones|Mr Jones]] and the Man of the Hill," in which the two debate the nature of humanity. The hermit argues that all men are driven by selfish passions and that virtue is a pretence; Jones counters with a defence of natural goodness and charity.

Fielding uses this debate to dramatise the novel's central philosophical question: whether benevolence is natural or artificial.

The hermit reappears briefly in Book 9, Chapter 2, when Jones, walking with him, hears a woman's cries and rushes to her aid — finding [[Mrs Waters]] (then known only as a distressed lady) under attack.

The Man of the Hill, true to his misanthropic principles, remains on the hill with his gun, watching with "great patience and unconcern," unwilling to intervene. This moment crystallises the difference between his philosophy of detachment and Jones's instinctive compassion.

## Themes

The Man of the Hill serves as a philosophical foil to Jones. Where Jones believes in active benevolence and the essential goodness of the human heart, the hermit preaches withdrawal and distrust.

Fielding does not wholly repudiate the hermit's critique — his life story contains genuine grounds for disillusionment — but the narrative ultimately endorses Jones's engaged compassion over the hermit's passive cynicism.

The episode is also a formal experiment: the embedded first-person narrative allows Fielding to explore a darker, more introspective register than the comic-ironic narration of the main plot.
