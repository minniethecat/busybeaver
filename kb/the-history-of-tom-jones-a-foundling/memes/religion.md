# Religion

**Religion** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* provides the novel's explicit moral vocabulary, yet Fielding distinguishes carefully between true Christian virtue and its counterfeits. Drawing on the Latitudinarian Anglican tradition that emphasized practical benevolence over doctrinal rigidity, Fielding uses religion as a touchstone to evaluate his characters' moral worth.

## In the Novel

Fielding announces his moral purpose in the Dedication, declaring the work contains "nothing prejudicial to the cause of religion and virtue" and that "to recommend goodness and innocence hath been my sincere endeavour." Throughout the novel, characters who perform piety without practising charity are exposed as villains, while those whose religion expresses itself in action are vindicated.

The novel's central religious foil is [[Mr Thwackum]], whose name itself suggests the harsh, punitive Christianity Fielding rejected. Thwackum invokes Scripture to justify severity — he declares that "the Christian religion was instituted for much nobler purposes than to enforce a lesson which many heathen philosophers had taught us long before" (Book 2, Chapter 5). His interpretation of charity is particularly revealing: in a debate at Allworthy's table, he argues that charity in Scripture means "candour, or the forming of a benevolent opinion of our brethren" rather than the "pitiful distribution of alms" (Book 2, Chapter 5). This conveniently absolves him of any obligation to material generosity.

[[Squire Allworthy]] embodies Fielding's ideal of practical Christianity. The narrator describes him as "a human being replete with benevolence, meditating in what manner he might render himself most acceptable to his Creator, by doing most good to his creatures" (Book 1, Chapter 4). When he discovers the infant Tom in his bed, Allworthy's first impulse is compassion, instructing that the child be cared for immediately — an instinctive charity that contrasts with Mrs Deborah Wilkins's suggestion to abandon the child at the churchwarden's door.

The tension between Christian forgiveness and the aristocratic code of [[honour]] is dramatized in Book 7, Chapter 13, when a lieutenant declares "a man of honour can't keep" the Christian command to forgive, adding "I love my religion very well, but I love my honour more." Tom Jones, confronting possible death, wrestles with "how terrible must it be to any one who is really a Christian, to cherish malice in his breast."
