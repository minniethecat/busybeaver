# Squire Western

**Squire Western** is the father of [[Sophia Western]] and the neighboring squire to [[Squire Allworthy]] in [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]].

A boisterous country gentleman devoted to hunting, drinking, and the absolute authority of his will, he serves as both comic relief and a study in the dangers of unbounded paternal power.

## Character

Squire Western is defined by his passions. Fielding portrays him as a man of "a very considerable" estate who "had a most violent affection for his daughter Sophia" (Book 4, Chapter 10).

This affection, however, expresses itself as tyranny. He loves Sophia not as a person with her own will, but as property to be disposed of advantageously.

His ruling passion is the hunt. Every morning he rides out with his hounds, returning only for his dinner and his bottle. Fielding uses this obsession to characterize Western's entire worldview: he treats Sophia's marriage as he would a horse trade.

Western's language is as coarse as his manners. He curses, shouts, and threatens. Yet Fielding grants him a certain rough [[truth|honesty]]—unlike [[Master Blifil]], Western's faults are all on the surface.

## Role in the Novel

Western's determination to marry Sophia to [[Master Blifil]]—the heir of his neighbor [[Squire Allworthy]]—sets the novel's central conflict in motion.

When Sophia refuses, Western imprisons her in her chamber, declaring that he would "lock her up in a garret" rather than let her marry a bastard like [[Tom Jones]].

His pursuit of Sophia to [[london|London]] occupies the novel's final books. When he discovers her in the city, his rage is tempered by relief, but his determination to control her remains.

Only the [[discovery|revelation]] of Tom's true parentage—making him Allworthy's heir—reconciles Western to the match, a resolution that satirizes his purely mercenary view of marriage.
