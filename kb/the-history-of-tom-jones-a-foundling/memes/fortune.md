# Fortune

**Fortune** is one of the most pervasive concepts in [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]], mentioned across nearly every book. Fielding employs the term with deliberate ambiguity: it signifies both blind chance and divine providence, the capricious goddess of classical tradition and the ordering hand of a benevolent deity.

## Fortune as Plot Mechanism

The novel's picaresque structure depends on fortune's constant reversals. [[Tom Jones]] is raised in wealth, cast out in disgrace, imprisoned, and restored—each reversal attributed to fortune's whims. Fielding explicitly invokes the goddess Fortune in his chapter titles and narrative asides, framing the plot as a series of tests through which character is revealed.

Fortune's operations are not random, however. Each seeming accident—from Tom's discovery in Allworthy's bed to the [[discovery|revelation]] of his true parentage—retrospectively reveals a providential design. What appears as misfortune (banishment) ultimately leads to Tom's moral education and his reunion with [[Sophia Western]] on [[honour|honor]]able terms.

## Fortune Versus Virtue

The novel's central moral question concerns the relationship between [[fortune]] and [[virtue]]. Can goodness flourish without favorable circumstances? Fielding's answer is complex: while virtue can survive misfortune, it requires fortune's cooperation to achieve happiness. The happy ending is not merely luck but the result of good character meeting favorable circumstance—a synthesis Fielding presents as the operation of divine providence.

## Classical and Christian Sources

Fielding draws on the classical goddess Fortuna (blind, turning her wheel, distributing gifts and blows without regard to merit) while simultaneously invoking the Christian doctrine of Providence. This dual framework allows him to maintain narrative suspense—characters face genuine risks—while affirming a moral universe in which virtue is ultimately rewarded.
