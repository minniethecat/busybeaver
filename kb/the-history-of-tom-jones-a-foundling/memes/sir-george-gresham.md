# Sir George Gresham

**Sir George Gresham** is a character in the interpolated tale told by the [[Man of the Hill]] in [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]. A wealthy and profligate young baronet at Oxford, he seduces the narrator into a life of dissipation and debt, serving as Fielding's cautionary portrait of aristocratic corruption.

## Character

Sir George Gresham is introduced as a young baronet "who was intitled to a very considerable fortune, which he was not, by the will of his father, to come into full possession of till he arrived at the age of twenty-five" (Book 8, Chapter 11).

His guardians allowed him five hundred pounds a year at university, yet he spent vastly more: "he kept his horses and his whore, and lived as wicked and as profligate a life as he could have done had he been never so entirely master of his fortune; for besides the five hundred a year which he received from his guardians, he found means to spend a thousand more" (Book 8, Chapter 11).

Above the age of twenty-one, he had no difficulty gaining credit, and his extravagance set the standard for the impressionable narrator. Fielding presents him as the archetype of the corrupting aristocratic companion — charming, reckless, and ultimately destructive to those who try to keep pace with him.

## Role in the Novel

Sir George Gresham appears exclusively within the [[Man of the Hill]]'s autobiographical narrative in Book 8, Chapters 11–14. He befriends the narrator at Oxford and draws him into a world of reckless spending, drinking, and debauchery.

The narrator, attempting to match Sir George's lifestyle, rapidly exhausts his funds and falls into debt. When his father sends remonstrances instead of money, the crisis deepens. The narrator reflects that even his father's "whole income" could not have sustained "one who kept pace with the expenses of Sir George Gresham" (Book 8, Chapter 13).

After the narrator's ruin, Sir George vanishes from the story — a pattern Fielding uses to illustrate how the wealthy libertine faces no consequences while those he corrupts suffer the full penalty. The narrator notes that Sir George "afterwards laughed at as fools and coxcombs" those he had ruined, for "vying, as he called it, with a man of his fortune" (Book 8, Chapter 14).

## Themes

Sir George Gresham embodies Fielding's critique of **aristocratic privilege without moral responsibility**. His wealth and title shield him from the consequences that destroy others. He also illustrates the novel's recurring concern with **the corrupting influence of bad company** — a theme that echoes through [[tom-jones|Tom Jones]]'s own adventures with less scrupulous companions.
