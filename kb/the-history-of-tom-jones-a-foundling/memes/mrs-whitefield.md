# Mrs Whitefield

**Mrs Whitefield** is the landlady of the Bell, an inn at Gloucester, who plays host to both [[Tom Jones]] and [[Sophia Western]] at different points in their journeys. Sister of the celebrated Methodist preacher George Whitefield, she is a friendly, well-bred woman whose opinion of Jones is swayed by the slander of a petty-fogger.

## Character

Fielding introduces Mrs Whitefield with an extended and affectionate portrait (Book 8, Chapter 8). She is the wife of the Bell's master, who is brother to "the great preacher Whitefield" but "absolutely untainted with the pernicious principles of Methodism." Mrs Whitefield herself confesses that her brother's preaching "made at first some impression upon her," and she had even purchased a long hood to attend Methodist meetings; but "having found, during an experiment of three weeks, no [[passion|emotion]]s, she says, worth a farthing, she very wisely laid by her hood, and abandoned the sect."

The narrator praises her warmly: "she is a very friendly good-natured woman; and so industrious to oblige, that the guests must be of a very morose disposition who are not extremely well satisfied in her house." Her person and deportment, Fielding adds, "might have made a shining figure in the politest assemblies."

Her good nature, however, makes her susceptible to persuasion. When the petty-fogger who dines with Jones at her inn fills her ears with lurid falsehoods about Jones's supposed crimes — seducing a maid, breaking a clergyman's arm, firing a pistol at Blifil — she initially protests that "if half what this gentleman hath said be true, [[tom-jones|Mr Jones]] hath the most deceitful countenance I ever saw; for sure his looks promise something very different." But the petty-fogger's oaths and vehemence eventually overcome her judgement, and "she accordingly gave up her skill in physiognomy, and hence-forwards conceived so ill an opinion of her guest, that she heartily wished him out of her house" (Book 8, Chapter 8).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Whitefield appears in two episodes. In Book 8, Chapter 8, she hosts Jones and [[partridge|Partridge]] at the Bell in Gloucester. Her initial warmth towards Jones — she invites him to dine with her, discerning in his air "something which distinguished him from the vulgar" — turns to coldness after the petty-fogger poisons her opinion. Jones, bewildered by the change, quits the inn that evening.

In Book 10, Chapter 9, she reappears as the hostess of [[Sophia Western]] and her maid [[Mrs Honour]], who stop at the Bell during their pursuit of Jones. Mrs Whitefield, with characteristic kindness, presses the exhausted Sophia to stay the night. But when [[honour|Honour]] reveals the name of the gentleman they seek, the landlady launches into a well-meaning but disastrous narrative of Jones's stay — including his passionate declarations of love for Sophia, which she exaggerates — embarrassing Sophia and providing [[honour|Honour]] with ammunition to disparage Jones.

Sophia leaves Mrs Whitefield's at eleven that night, continuing her journey rather than risk further indiscreet [[discovery|revelation]]s.

Mrs Whitefield thus functions as a crossroads figure: her inn is the point where the paths of Jones, Sophia, the petty-fogger, and the lawyer Dowling intersect, and where slander and truth collide with practical consequences for the principals.
