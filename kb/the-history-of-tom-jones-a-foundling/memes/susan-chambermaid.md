# Susan Chambermaid

**Susan Chambermaid** is a servant at the inn at Upton who becomes embroiled in the novel's central romantic complications. Her willingness to accept money for information — first from [[Fitzpatrick]], then from [[Sophia Western]] — makes her a pivotal, if unwitting, agent in the plot's misunderstandings.

## Character

Susan is physically formidable. Fielding describes her as "two-handed a wench" whose "form was robust and man-like, and every way made for such encounters" (Book 9, Chapter 3). Her features appear purpose-built for combat: a flat nose, large lips so hard that "a fist could hardly make any impression on them," and prominent cheekbones that "stood out, as if nature had intended them for two bastions to defend her eyes." The narrator wryly compares her to the Amazon queen Thalestris.

She is not particularly scrupulous but possesses the shrewd practicality of a servant who knows the value of information. Her decision to accept [[Fitzpatrick]]'s guineas is driven by a calculation that "she never could get money in an honester way than by restoring a wife to her husband," a piece of self-justifying reasoning that Fielding presents with characteristic irony (Book 10, Chapter 2).

## Role in the Novel

Susan first appears during the brawl at the Upton inn. When the landlady attacks [[Tom Jones]] and his party with a broomstick, Susan joins the fray with devastating effect, throwing herself upon [[Partridge]] with such fury that she "overthrown and bestrid her enemy" and continued cuffing him even after the general truce was called (Book 9, Chapter 3). Jones had to physically drag her off his companion. She and [[partridge|Partridge]] later made peace, with Susan nursing a black eye from [[partridge|Partridge]]'s first blow and [[partridge|Partridge]] bearing "many more" marks from her nails (Book 9, Chapter 4).

Her most consequential act occurs late at night when [[Fitzpatrick]] arrives at the inn searching for his wife. Finding Susan the only person still awake — "she being obliged to wash the kitchen before she retired to the arms of the fond expecting hostler" — he bribes her with a handful of guineas. Susan promptly leads him to the bedchamber of [[Mrs Waters]], where Fitzpatrick bursts in and finds Jones in bed with her (Book 10, Chapter 2). This discovery sets in motion the chain of misunderstandings that drives the novel's second half.

The following morning, [[Sophia Western]] — who has arrived at the same inn — bribes Susan with two guineas to reveal the truth about Jones's sleeping arrangements. Susan confirms that Jones is not in his own bed, delivers [[Partridge]]'s gossip about Sophia's supposed love for Jones, and even sneaks into Jones's room to verify he is absent (Book 10, Chapter 5). She then helps Sophia execute her plan to leave her muff in Jones's empty bed as a silent message.

## Themes

Susan's venality, presented without moral condemnation, reflects Fielding's comic worldview — minor characters act from ordinary self-interest, and their small choices propel the grand machinery of the plot. Her physical description as an "Amazonian fair" also exemplifies the novel's enjoyment of exaggerated, mock-heroic portraiture.
