# Mr Summer

**Mr Summer** is a posthumous character in *[[tom-jones|Tom Jones]]* whose true significance — as the biological father of [[Tom Jones]] — is revealed only in the novel's final chapters. The son of a clergyman, he was educated and patronised by [[Squire Allworthy]], and his secret liaison with [[Bridget Allworthy]] produced [[tom-jones|the foundling]] whose identity drives the entire plot.

## Character

Summer is described entirely through the recollections of others, most extensively by [[Mrs Waters]] in her climactic confession to Allworthy (Book 18, Chapter 7). He was the son of "a clergyman of great learning and virtue, for whom I had the highest friendship," as Allworthy recalls. Allworthy bred the young man up, maintained him at the university, and received him into his house after his studies were completed.

[[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]]'s description paints Summer as exceptional: "a finer man, I must say, the sun never shone upon; for, besides the handsomest person I ever saw, he was so genteel, and had so much wit and good breeding." He lived at Allworthy's house for a year "as if he had been your own son," before dying there of small-pox.

Allworthy himself later admits that he had "some passages relating to that Summer, which formerly gave me a conceit that my sister had some liking to him." He had such regard for the young man that he "should willingly have consented to a match between them," but Bridget "exprest the highest disdain of my unkind suspicion."

## Role in the Novel

Summer himself never appears in the novel's present action; he is dead before the story begins. His role is entirely genealogical and revelatory. The disclosure that Summer, not [[partridge|Partridge]] or any other, fathered [[tom-jones|Tom Jones]] is the central [[discovery|revelation]] of Book 18, Chapter 7, undoing the long-standing assumption that [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]] was the child's mother.

[[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]]'s confession to Allworthy establishes the full chain: Summer was the father of the infant, but the mother was not [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]] — it was [[bridget-allworthy|Bridget Allworthy]] herself. This [[discovery|revelation]] transforms Tom from a foundling of unknown parentage into Allworthy's nephew, restoring his social position and making possible his marriage to [[Sophia Western]].

Summer's early death by small-pox, before his son's birth, and Bridget's determination to conceal the pregnancy, set in motion the entire plot: the infant's secret placement in Allworthy's bed, [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]]'s paid assumption of motherhood, and the two decades of mystery surrounding Tom's origins.
