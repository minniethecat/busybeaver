# Compassion

**Compassion** in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling* is the central moral emotion from which virtuous action flows. Fielding treats the capacity to feel for another's suffering not as a sentimental weakness but as the foundation of all genuine goodness, and he systematically tests his characters against this standard throughout the novel.

## In the Novel

The novel's moral centre, [[Squire Allworthy]], is introduced through an act of compassion. Discovering the infant Tom in his bed, Allworthy "soon began to be touched with sentiments of compassion for the little wretch before him" (Book 1, Chapter 3). His immediate, uncalculated response — ordering the child cared for — establishes compassion as the instinctive reflex of a good heart, preceding any deliberation about duty or reputation.

[[Tom Jones]] inherits this compassionate disposition. His secret generosity to the family of [[Black George]] the gamekeeper — sacrificing first his horse, then his Bible, and even his nightgown to feed the starving children — moves Allworthy to tears when discovered. The mother testifies that "we have not had a morsel to eat, nor have these poor children had a rag to put on, but what his goodness hath bestowed on us" (Book 4, Chapter 10). Tom's compassion is impulsive and sometimes imprudent, but Fielding presents it as fundamentally right where [[Master Blifil]]'s calculated propriety is fundamentally wrong.

Fielding explicitly contrasts compassion with the false charity of doctrinal rigorists. [[Mr Thwackum]]'s notorious argument that charity in Scripture means merely thinking well of others — not giving alms — represents the evacuation of compassion from religion. [[Squire Allworthy]]'s rebuttal is Fielding's own position: "I do not think a few or many examples of ingratitude can justify a man's hardening his heart against the distresses of his fellow-creatures" (Book 2, Chapter 6).

The novel also explores the limits of compassion. The mob's desire to see [[Jenny Jones]] punished satisfies a cruel appetite disguised as moral outrage, which Fielding calls "an object for their compassion to work on" only if she were "sacrificed to ruin and infamy" (Book 1, Chapter 9). This inverted compassion — pitying only those one has first destroyed — exposes the hypocrisy of performative sympathy. True compassion, in Fielding's scheme, acts to relieve suffering directly, without first requiring the sufferer's humiliation.
