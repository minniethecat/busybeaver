# Mr Thwackum

**Mr Thwackum** is a clergyman engaged by [[Squire Allworthy]] as tutor to [[Tom Jones]] and [[Master Blifil]]. Alongside [[Mr Square]] the philosopher, he forms one half of the pedagogical duo whose harsh methods and hypocritical principles shape the boys' upbringing at Paradise Hall.

## Character

Mr Thwackum is introduced as a man whose name reflects his pedagogical philosophy: he believes that the fear of punishment is the foundation of all virtue (Book 3, Chapter 3). His theological system reduces all morality to religion, and all religion to his own narrow interpretation of doctrine.

Where Square finds virtue in the "eternal fitness of things," Thwackum finds it only in divine command and the threat of damnation (Book 3, Chapter 3).

Fielding portrays Thwackum as a study in hypocrisy. He preaches Christian charity yet shows none to those beneath him. His severity towards [[tom-jones|Tom Jones]] is relentless — he gives the boy a whipping so severe "that it possibly fell little short of the torture with which confessions are in some countries extorted from criminals" (Book 3, Chapter 2).

Yet his religious principles never prevent him from siding with the scheming Blifil when it serves his interests.

## Role in the Novel

Thwackum appears throughout the early books as part of the household at Paradise Hall. His primary function is to serve, alongside Square, as a foil to Allworthy's genuine goodness.

Where Allworthy practices benevolence instinctively, Thwackum and Square justify their cruelties through elaborate theoretical systems — one religious, one philosophical — that converge on the same self-serving conclusions.

Thwackum's disputes with Square form a recurring comic motif. The two tutors pronounce [[reason|judgment]] on Tom after an incident of poaching; their different systems produce identical condemnations, revealing that their principles are merely ornaments for prejudice (Book 3, Chapter 9).

Thwackum later disappears from the narrative as the action shifts to [[london|London]], his role as a provincial hypocrite exhausted once the true moral drama moves elsewhere.

## Themes

Mr Thwackum represents the theme of **religious hypocrisy**. His insistence on the exclusive authority of divine command masks a complete absence of Christian [[passion|feeling]]. He is one of Fielding's sharpest satirical portraits: a man whose religion is all punishment and no mercy, all doctrine and no charity.

Through Thwackum, the novel argues that institutional religion, when separated from natural benevolence, becomes an instrument of cruelty rather than virtue.
