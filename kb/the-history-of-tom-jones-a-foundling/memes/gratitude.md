# Gratitude

**Gratitude** is a central moral emotion in Henry Fielding's *The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling*, functioning as the necessary complement to benevolence. If [[compassion]] motivates the giving of benefits, gratitude is the proper response to receiving them, and Fielding uses a character's capacity for gratitude — or its absence — as a reliable index of moral worth.

## In the Novel

The novel's first book closes with a chapter titled "An Instance of Ingratitude, Which, We Hope, Will Appear Unnatural" (Book 1, Chapter 13). Captain Blifil, having been received into [[Squire Allworthy]]'s household and married to [[Bridget Allworthy]] through his brother Dr Blifil's scheming, turns on the very brother who secured his fortune. The narrator reflects that "ingratitude never so thoroughly pierces the human breast as when it proceeds from those in whose behalf we have been guilty of transgressions" (Book 1, Chapter 13). The captain's ingratitude is compounded by the doctor's complicity in deceiving Allworthy — creating a chain of moral contamination that culminates in [[Master Blifil]], the novel's supreme ingrate.

[[Tom Jones]] is defined by his capacity for gratitude. His devotion to Allworthy is explicitly grounded in the recognition of unrepayable debt: "he had more than filial obligations" toward the man who raised him from infancy (Book 6, Chapter 1). When Allworthy later banishes Tom — based on Blifil's deceptions — Tom's grief is not for his lost inheritance but for the rupture of this bond of gratitude. His most anguished self-reproach is that he may have appeared ungrateful.

Fielding is psychologically acute about the difficulty of gratitude. He observes that envy mixed with contempt toward a benefactor produces "indignation and not gratitude" (Book 1, Chapter 13). The narrator also notes that "the youth of both sexes are too apt to be deficient in their gratitude for that regard with which persons more advanced in years are sometimes so kind to honour them" (Book 5, Chapter 1). Gratitude, in Fielding's scheme, requires [[humility]] — the willingness to acknowledge dependence — which is why the proud find it impossible.

The novel's resolution is structured around the repayment of moral debts. [[Mrs Miller]]'s generous and grateful behaviour toward Tom — sheltering him after his banishment — is given its own chapter title (Book 14, Chapter 2), and her testimony to Allworthy helps restore Tom to favour. The final reunions at Paradise Hall are suffused with the language of gratitude, as each character acknowledges what they owe to others' goodness.
