# Mr Square

**Mr Square** is a philosopher engaged by [[Squire Allworthy]] as tutor to [[Tom Jones]] and [[Master Blifil]]. Alongside his colleague [[Mr Thwackum]], he represents one pole of the educational and moral debate that runs through the novel.

## Character

Mr Square is introduced as a proponent of philosophical rationalism and the "natural beauty of virtue" (Book 3, Chapter 3). Fielding presents him as a man whose professed principles of reason and moral fitness are belied by his private conduct.

He frequently engages in theological and philosophical disputes with [[mr-thwackum|Mr Thwackum]], each man representing a distorted extreme: Thwackum reduces all virtue to religion, while Square reduces all religion to abstract reason (Book 3, Chapter 3).

Square's hypocrisy is gradually exposed over the course of the novel. Despite his philosophical pretensions, he is not immune to the very human weaknesses he condemns in others. His character serves as Fielding's satire on those who substitute systems of philosophy for genuine goodness of heart.

## Role in the Novel

Square first appears as part of the household at Paradise Hall, where he and Thwackum share responsibility for the education of the two boys. Their pedagogical approach is marked by harshness towards Tom and favouritism towards Blifil. Square consistently takes Blifil's side in disputes, his philosophical [[reason|judgment]] conveniently aligning with the interests of the heir apparent.

Square and Thwackum jointly condemn Tom after an incident involving a partridge, their respective philosophies producing identical conclusions when self-interest is at stake (Book 3, Chapter 9).

Square's later appearances trace a gradual moral decline, culminating in his deathbed confession, where he repudiates his philosophical system and acknowledges the insufficiency of reason without religion (Book 18, Chapter 4).

## Themes

Mr Square embodies the theme of **hypocritical philosophy**. His rationalist system, which professes to follow the "eternal fitness of things," invariably bends to accommodate his own comfort and advancement.

Fielding uses Square to critique those who substitute intellectual systems for genuine moral [[passion|feeling]], a theme closely tied to the novel's broader argument about the primacy of good nature over abstract virtue.
