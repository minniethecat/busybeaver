# Partridge

**Partridge** (Benjamin Partridge) is a schoolmaster turned barber-surgeon who becomes the loyal traveling companion of [[Tom Jones]] through the middle books of [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]. Wrongfully accused of fathering the infant Tom through his servant [[Jenny Jones]], Partridge loses his wife, his school, and his reputation—yet emerges as one of Fielding's most endearing comic creations.

## Biography

Partridge first appears as the schoolmaster of Little Baddington, where he lives with his formidable wife, [[mrs-partridge|Mrs Partridge]], and their maid, [[jenny-jones|Jenny Jones]]. His downfall begins when Jenny—who has been receiving Latin lessons from him—is accused of being Tom's mother. [[mrs-partridge|Mrs Partridge]]'s jealousy, long dormant, erupts when a neighbor reports that Jenny has given birth to two bastards. Convinced of her husband's guilt, she attacks him with a fury Fielding describes in mock-epic terms:

> Her tongue, teeth, and hands, fell all upon him at once. His wig was in an instant torn from his head, his shirt from his back, and from his face descended five streams of blood, denoting the number of claws with which nature had unhappily armed the enemy. (Book 2, Chapter 4)

Ruined by the scandal, Partridge loses his school and his wife (who dies shortly after). He reinvents himself as a barber-surgeon and eventually encounters Tom on the road, becoming his devoted companion for the remainder of the hero's adventures.

## Character

Partridge is defined by three qualities that make him both comic and sympathetic:

- **Loyalty**: Despite his misfortunes, Partridge is unfailingly devoted to Tom, following him through [[inn|inns]], highways, and prisons. His loyalty is the one virtue that survives all his trials.
- **Superstition**: He sees omens in everything and is perpetually terrified of ghosts, gypsies, and ill portents. His credulity provides much of the novel's comic relief.
- **Latin Pedantry**: As a former schoolmaster, Partridge cannot resist displaying his classical learning, often quoting Latin tags at the most inopportune moments—a habit Fielding uses to satirize empty erudition.

His cowardice is legendary, yet his good heart is unmistakable. In a novel filled with hypocrites, Partridge's flaws are honest ones.

## Role in the Novel

Partridge appears across more chapters than almost any character except Tom himself. He functions as a kind of Sancho Panza to Tom's Quixote—a comic foil whose earthy pragmatism and superstition contrast with Tom's idealism and impulsiveness. Through Partridge, Fielding explores themes of [[fortune]], loyalty, and the gap between reputation and true character. Though the world judges Partridge harshly, the novel ultimately vindicates his essential goodness.
