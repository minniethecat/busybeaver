# Jenny Jones

**Jenny Jones** is a servant girl in the parish of [[Squire Allworthy]] who becomes the central figure in the mystery of [[Tom Jones]]'s parentage. Though initially presented as the mother of [[tom-jones|the foundling]], she is later revealed to have merely acted as a scapegoat to protect the true mother, [[Bridget Allworthy]].

## Character

Jenny Jones is remarkable not for her appearance — Fielding describes her as "no very comely girl, either in her face or person" — but for her intellect. Having lived several years as a servant with a schoolmaster, she acquired a competent skill in Latin and became "as good a scholar as most of the young men of quality of the age" (Book 1, Chapter 6). This intellectual superiority set her apart from her social equals and earned her the envy and ill-will of her neighbours.

Her conduct under accusation reveals a stoic resilience. When confronted by [[Mrs Deborah Wilkins]] and charged as the mother of [[tom-jones|the foundling]], Jenny freely confessed — not from guilt, but from a carefully arranged plan to shield the real mother. She bore the public shame and verbal abuse of the parish women with remarkable patience, only briefly lashing out when one woman mocked her plain appearance (Book 1, Chapter 6).

## Role in the Novel

Jenny Jones first appears in Book 1 when [[mrs-deborah-wilkins|Mrs Wilkins]], investigating the discovery of an infant in [[squire-allworthy|Squire Allworthy]]'s bed, fixes suspicion upon her. Summoned before Allworthy, she confesses and refuses to name the father, for which Allworthy treats her with unexpected clemency — giving her money and sending her to a place where her reputation is unknown (Book 1, Chapter 7).

She later reappears as [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]], a woman of striking appearance whom [[tom-jones|Tom Jones]] encounters at the inn at Upton (Book 9, Chapter 2). In the climactic [[discovery|revelation]] of Book 18, [[mrs-waters|Mrs Waters]] discloses to Allworthy that she was never Tom's mother: the true mother was Allworthy's own sister, [[Bridget Allworthy]], who had arranged the entire deception with Jenny and her mother. The real father was [[mr-summer|Mr Summer]], a young man Allworthy had educated who died of smallpox shortly before Tom's birth (Book 18, Chapter 7).

## Themes

Jenny Jones embodies the theme of **social injustice and class prejudice**. Her superior intellect makes her a target rather than an asset, and her low social status makes her the convenient scapegoat for a gentlewoman's secret. Her story illustrates how consequences fall unequally: [[bridget-allworthy|Bridget Allworthy]]'s reputation is preserved while Jenny bears public disgrace.
