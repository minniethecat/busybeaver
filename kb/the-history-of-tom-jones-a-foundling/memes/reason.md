# Reason

**Reason** in *[[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]]* functions as the governing faculty that ought to direct human action. Fielding, writing within the Enlightenment tradition, presents reason as essential to virtue, yet the novel repeatedly demonstrates how reason can be perverted into self-serving rationalization.

## Reason as Moral Guide

The novel's moral framework positions reason as the necessary check on [[passion]]. [[Squire Allworthy]] most consistently embodies reason in judgment—his decisions, though occasionally mistaken, proceed from careful deliberation rather than impulse. In Book 5, Chapter 5, when [[Tom Jones]] discovers [[Mr Square]] with [[Molly Seagrim]], Square attempts to justify his conduct through philosophical reasoning: "Fitness is governed by the nature of things, and not by customs, forms, or municipal laws. Nothing is indeed unfit which is not unnatural." Jones immediately recognizes this as sophistry, exposing how reason can serve appetite rather than truth.

## False Reason and Hypocrisy

The novel's sharpest satire targets those who deploy the language of reason to mask self-interest. [[Mr Square]] and [[Mr Thwackum]] function as twin caricatures: Square reduces all morality to "the eternal fitness of things" while rationalizing his own vices; Thwackum invokes religious authority with equal hypocrisy. Their debates, the narrator notes, never arrive at [[truth]] because both begin from the conclusions their self-interest requires.

## Reason and Prudence

Fielding distinguishes genuine reason from mere calculation. [[Master Blifil]] possesses a kind of cunning rationality—he consistently acts in his own interest—but lacks the moral dimension that transforms intelligence into wisdom. The novel thus aligns true reason with [[virtue]] and [[prudence]], suggesting that reason without goodness is merely clever selfishness.
