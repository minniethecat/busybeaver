# Squire Allworthy

**Squire Allworthy** is a central figure in [[The History of Tom Jones, a Foundling]], the wealthy and benevolent gentleman of [[Somerset]] who discovers the infant [[Tom Jones]] in his bed and raises him as his own son.

Allworthy embodies the novel's moral ideal of [[virtue]], justice, and good nature tempered by sound [[reason|judgment]].

## Character

Allworthy is introduced as a gentleman "who might well be called the favourite of both nature and fortune" (Book 1, Chapter 2).

[[nature|Nature]] bestowed upon him "an agreeable person, a sound constitution, a solid understanding, and a benevolent heart," while fortune granted him "one of the largest estates in the county" (Book 1, Chapter 2).

His character is defined by an exceptional generosity: his house was "the only house in the kingdom where you was sure to gain a dinner by deserving it" (Book 1, Chapter 10).

His benevolence, however, is not mere sentiment. Each evening he spent "some minutes on his knees—a custom which he never broke through on any account" (Book 1, Chapter 3).

Unlike the hypocrites who populate the novel, Allworthy's virtue is active: he is charitable to the poor, hospitable to his neighbors, and judges true merit over mere pretension.

## Role in the Novel

Allworthy serves as the moral anchor of the narrative. His decision to adopt [[tom-jones|the foundling]] [[tom-jones|Tom Jones]] sets the entire plot in motion.

When he returns from a long absence in [[london|London]] to discover an infant in his bed, his response is immediate compassion:

> He stood some time lost in astonishment at this sight; but, as good nature had always the ascendant in his mind, he soon began to be touched with sentiments of compassion for the little wretch before him. (Book 1, Chapter 3)

His [[reason|judgment]] is tested most critically when [[Master Blifil]]'s machinations lead him to banish Tom from his home. His eventual recognition of Blifil's true character and reconciliation with Tom constitutes the moral climax of the story.

## Themes

- **Virtue and Good [[nature|Nature]]**: Allworthy represents active benevolence, against the performative morality of [[Master Blifil]] and [[mr-square|Mr Square]].
- **[[fortune|Fortune]] and Providence**: His name suggests both moral character and the providential order Fielding explores.
- **Judgment and Deception**: Even the wisest [[reason|judgment]] can be misled, a key theme in Fielding's moral philosophy.
