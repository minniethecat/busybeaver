# Ensign Northerton

**Ensign Northerton** is an officer encountered by [[Tom Jones]] at an inn shortly after Jones joins the army as a volunteer. His coarse manners, quarrelsome temper, and fateful insult of [[Sophia Western]] trigger the first serious conflict of Jones's journey.

## Character

Northerton is portrayed as a rude, ill-educated soldier with a habit of profane swearing and a quickness to take offence. When the lieutenant reproaches him for his language, Northerton dismisses the criticism with contempt, revealing a man "whom nature makes up in too great a hurry" — rash, unreflective, and governed by impulse.

His jesting about [[sophia-western|Sophia Western]]'s character, claiming she had been "lain with by half the young fellows at Bath," demonstrates both his vulgarity and his disregard for the honour of women he does not know.

## Role in the Novel

Northerton first appears in Book 7, Chapter 12, at an inn where Jones, having enlisted, dines with fellow officers. The conversation turns to toasts, and when Jones names [[sophia-western|Sophia Western]] as his toast, Northerton makes the slanderous claim about her character.

Jones, though slow to anger, eventually calls him "one of the most impudent rascals upon earth," prompting Northerton to strike Jones on the head with a wine bottle, grievously wounding him (Book 7, Chapter 12).

Northerton is placed under guard while Jones recovers from his wound. That night, the centinel guarding him reports a terrifying apparition — Jones's ghost, "all over blood, vomiting fire out of his mouth and nostrils" — entering Northerton's chamber and flying away with him.

Whether through bribery, supernatural intervention, or the centinel's negligence, Northerton vanishes from the narrative, his subsequent fate unknown (Book 8, Chapter 2).

The incident serves a dual purpose in the plot: it delays Jones's military ambitions and introduces the theme of mistaken identity and rumour, as the centinel's ghost story spreads through the regiment.

## Themes

Northerton embodies the dangers of unchecked impulse and the culture of masculine aggression. His assault on Jones illustrates how easily honour-based violence can escalate from a verbal slight. His mysterious escape also introduces a Gothic element into the narrative, with the ghost story blurring the line between rational explanation and supernatural possibility.
