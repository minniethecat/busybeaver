# Mrs Western

**Mrs Western** is the sister of [[Squire Western]] and aunt to [[Sophia Western]]. She prides herself on her learning and knowledge of the world, and serves as a satirical portrait of the pretentious, half-educated gentlewoman.

## Character

Mrs Western is introduced in Book 6, Chapter 2 with a full chapter devoted to her character. She affects the language of courtly sophistication, speaks in political metaphors, and considers herself vastly superior to her country-squire brother. Her "great learning and knowledge of the world" are, in Fielding's ironic treatment, sources of error rather than wisdom.

She advocates for Sophia's marriage to [[Master Blifil]] not out of concern for her niece's happiness but from a conviction that estate and fortune are the only rational considerations in matrimony. When Sophia resists, Mrs Western interprets it as girlish folly to be overcome by authority. Her worldliness is revealed as a more polished form of the same tyranny her brother exercises more crudely.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs Western first intervenes in Sophia's affairs in Book 6, where she attempts to persuade her niece to accept Blifil as a husband. When Sophia declares her aversion, Mrs Western accuses her of being influenced by romantic notions and insists upon the match. Her authority, however, proves as ineffective as her brother's violence: Sophia flees rather than submit.

After Sophia's flight, Mrs Western pursues her to [[london|London]], where she becomes entangled in the schemes of [[Lady Bellaston]] and [[Lord Fellamar]]. In Book 16, she attempts to force a marriage between Sophia and Fellamar, employing the language of honour and family obligation. Her machinations are ultimately thwarted by the timely arrival of [[squire-western|Squire Western]] and the [[discovery|revelation]] of Jones's true parentage.

## Themes

Mrs Western satirises the theme of **false learning and worldly wisdom**. Her pretensions to political and philosophical knowledge mask a fundamental failure of moral perception — she cannot distinguish between a prudent match and a virtuous one. Fielding uses her to critique the hypocrisy of those who speak of honour while practising manipulation, demonstrating that learning without goodness is merely a more elegant form of folly.
