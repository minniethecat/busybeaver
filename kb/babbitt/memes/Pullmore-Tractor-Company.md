## Description

The Pullmore Tractor Company of Zenith is a major industrial manufacturer whose factory is described as "a steel and cement town" — a self-contained industrial fortress within the city. Its operations include filling large-scale international orders, reflecting Zenith's industrial might and global commercial reach (007-039).

## Industrial Presence

The factory at night is an imposing and militarized sight: "It hummed like a million bees, glared through its wide windows like a volcano. Along the high wire fences, searchlights played on cinder-lined yards, switch-tracks, and armed guards on patrol" (007-039). The night shift ran to fill an order of tractors for the Polish army, demonstrating Zenith's connection to international markets and military procurement (007-039).

Lewis embeds this factory portrait within a chapter that juxtaposes simultaneous moments across Zenith society — a technique that places the Pullmore plant alongside a dying Civil War veteran, striking union officials, and a casual murder in a saloon. The factory is presented not merely as a workplace but as an organic force in the city's nocturnal life: humming, glaring, guarded, and alive.

## Key Personnel

**Caleb Nixon** serves as secretary of the Pullmore Tractor Company. His significance extends beyond the factory walls: Nixon is also a Colonel in the National Guard. During the September strike that convulsed Zenith, Nixon "put on a long khaki coat and stalked through crowds, a .44 automatic in hand" (027-003). This is the same man who, "in private life," ran the Pullmore Tractor Company — the boundary between industrial management and civic-military authority is entirely dissolved.

Nixon's armed patrol is part of a broader mobilization. The strike, which began with telephone girls and linemen protesting wage reductions, escalated into a general crisis involving truck-drivers, dairy workers, and violent clashes. When the National Guard was ordered out, Zenith's business class transformed overnight into an armed militia: "Even Babbitt's friend, Clarence Drum the shoe merchant — a round and merry man who told stories at the Athletic Club — was to be seen as a waddling but ferocious captain" (027-004). Bookkeepers and grocery-clerks became "citizen-soldiers" trying "to look dangerous while small boys yelped" (027-004).

## Social Function

The Pullmore Tractor Company exemplifies the industrial dimension of Zenith's booster ideology. Like the Zeeco Motor Company and the Zenith Foundry, Pullmore provides the manufacturing muscle that gives substance to the Boosters' Club's rhetoric about Zenith's commercial supremacy. The Polish army order demonstrates that Zenith's industries participate in global geopolitics — tractors built in Zenith serve military ends abroad, and the armed guards patrolling Pullmore's fences suggest that the boundary between industrial production and military force has always been thin.

The company also illuminates the class structure of Zenith's business elite. As company secretary, Nixon occupies the managerial tier — not the ownership class, but the operational leadership that actually runs the factories. His dual role as Colonel makes explicit the ideological alignment between property rights and state violence that Lewis sees as central to American booster culture.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Pullmore Tractor Company to expose the inseparability of industrial capitalism and coercive authority in Zenith. The factory that builds tractors for a foreign army is guarded like a military installation; its manager becomes a military officer the moment labor challenges management. The "steel and cement town" is a fortress — against workers, against strikers, and symbolically against any challenge to the economic order.

The chapter structure reinforces this critique: the Pullmore factory hums "at that moment" when "a cocaine-runner and a prostitute were drinking cocktails in Healey Hanson's saloon," when "two men sat in a laboratory" working on synthetic rubber, when "four union officials" debated a coal strike, and when "a G. A. R. veteran was dying" [007-035 through 007-039]. Lewis presents the factory as one node in a web of simultaneous but disconnected lives — each pursuing its own logic, none aware of the others, all contained within the same city. The armed guards and searchlights at Pullmore are not just factory security; they are the visible edge of the social order that Zenith's business class is prepared to defend by force.
