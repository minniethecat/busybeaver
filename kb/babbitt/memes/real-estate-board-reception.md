# The Real Estate Board Reception

The annual convention of the S.A.R.E.B. — the State Association of Real Estate Boards — at Monarch, where [[george-babbitt]] delivers a paper arguing that realtors deserve recognition as "professional men," and in doing so discovers that the line between professional advocacy and self-delusion is thinner than he imagined.

## Summary

Babbitt's opportunity to address the convention arrives "by accident" (013-004). The S.A.R.E.B. — named, Lewis notes, with "the universal passion for mysterious and important-sounding initials" (013-004) — is the state organization of real-estate brokers and operators. Its annual convention is to be held at Monarch, "Zenith's chief rival among the cities of the state" (013-004). Babbitt is an official delegate; another is Cecil Rountree, chairman of the convention program-committee.

The convention unfolds across several key moments. The **composition of the paper** is a domestic drama: Babbitt "laid out a new fifteen-cent school exercise-book on his wife's collapsible sewing-table, set up for the event in the living-room" (013-004). The household is "bullied into silence" — Verona and Ted "requested to disappear," Tinka threatened with punishment. Mrs. Babbitt sits "over by the piano, making a nightgown and gazing with respect while Babbitt wrote in the exercise-book, to the rhythmical wiggling and squeaking of the sewing-table" (013-004). When Babbitt rises, "damp and jumpy, and his throat dusty from cigarettes," Myra marvels: "I don't see how you can just sit down and make up things right out of your own head!" Babbitt's reply — "Oh, it's the training in constructive imagination that a fellow gets in modern business life" (013-004) — is both self-congratulation and unwitting confession.

At Monarch, the convention's social program includes a **garden party and motor tours**. Babbitt reflects: "Myra would have enjoyed all this social agony" (013-043). But he himself "cared less for the garden party than for the motor tours which the Monarch Chamber of Commerce had arranged. Indefatigably he viewed water-reservoirs, suburban trolley-stations, and tanneries" (013-043). He "devoured the statistics which were given to him, and marveled to his roommate, W. A. Rogers, 'Of course this town isn't a patch on Zenith; it hasn't got our outlook and natural resources; but did you know — I nev' did till to-day — that they manufactured seven hundred and sixty-three million feet of lumber last year? What d' you think of that!'" (013-043).

The **speech** is the convention's centerpiece. On the platform, Babbitt is nervous: "he trembled and saw only a purple haze" (013-043). But he speaks with earnestness, and when he has finished the formal paper he talks extemporaneously, "his hands in his pockets, his spectacled face a flashing disk, like a plate set up on edge in the lamplight" (013-043). The delegates shout "That's the stuff!" and afterward refer to "our friend and brother, Mr. George F. Babbitt." In fifteen minutes he has changed "from a minor delegate to a personage almost as well known as that diplomat of business, Cecil Rountree" (013-043). Sixteen complete strangers call him "George," and three men take him into corners to confide their agreement. The next morning the Zenith Advocate-Times prints his picture and a half-column account: "Sensation at Annual Land-men's Convention. G. F. Babbitt, Prominent Ziptown Realtor, Keynoter in Fine Address" (013-043).

## Background

Babbitt's complaint to Rountree — "Makes me tired the way these doctors and profs and preachers put on lugs about being 'professional men.' A good realtor has to have more knowledge and finesse than any of 'em" (013-004) — reveals the status anxiety that drives his convention address. The real-estate man occupies an ambiguous position in the class hierarchy: wealthy but not credentialed, powerful but not prestigious.

Rountree's suggestion that Babbitt put his argument into a paper is partly genuine program-building and partly a maneuver to escape further conversation: "'Rather! That's perfectly bully! Perfectly corking! Now you write it in a paper,' said Rountree, as he rapidly and firmly moved away" (013-004).

## Consequences

The convention speech gives Babbitt the beginning of a reputation for oratory that will carry him into the local election campaign as a precinct leader and speaker (014-002). It also establishes the pattern — repeated throughout the novel — by which Babbitt's public successes fail to satisfy his private discontent. The newspaper notice and the sixteen strangers calling him "George" produce a thrill that proves temporary; by the novel's end, Babbitt will find that being a "Prominent Ziptown Realtor" does not answer the questions his restlessness keeps asking.

## Satirical Significance

The convention episode is Lewis's most sustained treatment of professional identity as performance. Babbitt's paper — seven pages of which "the other six pages were rather like the first" (013-004) — argues that realtors deserve the status of professionals because they possess "trained skill" and "public service." Yet the composition process, rendered in comic domestic detail, reveals the argument's emptiness: Babbitt is not demonstrating professional expertise but performing it, with the exercise-book and sewing-table as his stage.

The convention's structure enacts the contradictions of booster professionalism. The formal program — papers, keynote addresses, resolutions — provides the appearance of intellectual exchange. But the real convention, Lewis notes, "consisted of men muttering in [hotel] bedrooms" (013-035), making deals and trading gossip. The garden party and motor tours serve the same function as the Boosters' Club nickname buttons: they manufacture an experience of collegiality that substitutes for genuine professional community.

Babbitt's transformation — from minor delegate to "personage" in fifteen minutes — is the convention's true product. Professional identity, in Lewis's account, is not earned through demonstrated competence but conferred through social recognition. Sixteen strangers calling him "George" and the Advocate-Times headline do more to make Babbitt a "professional man" than any paper he might write.
