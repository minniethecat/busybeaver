# The Bunch

## Description

"The Bunch" is Babbitt's informal social circle — the group of men who gather regularly at Vergil Gunch's for poker, drink Prohibition-era home-brewed beer, smoke cigars, and engage in the ritualized banter that constitutes male friendship in Zenith. The group is also known as "the Roughnecks," a deliberately ironic name that claims a working-class toughness its members — all businessmen and professionals — do not possess. The Bunch represents friendship at its most mechanical and its most necessary: the human connection that Zenith's business civilization both requires and trivializes.

## Social Function

The Bunch serves as Babbitt's primary social outlet outside of formal club meetings. Its gatherings follow a rigid unwritten script: poker, beer, stories about business deals, complaints about wives, and the repetition of booster platitudes about the greatness of Zenith and the American way. The ritual is so predictable that Lewis describes it almost fondly — the Bunch is where Babbitt can "be natural and simple in a natural and simple world" (001-015), a world bounded by poker hands and the foam on a glass of beer.

The Bunch also functions as a kind of informal court, where reputations are made and broken. Babbitt's standing in the group rises and falls with his professional fortunes and his adherence to group norms. When he begins his rebellion — socializing with liberals, defending strikers — the Bunch becomes the first institution to register disapproval, its silences and sidelong glances communicating what words do not.

## Key Scenes

- **The Poker Game (Chapter I)**: The Bunch is introduced at Vergil Gunch's house, where the men play poker, drink "the tremendous home-brewed beer of the prohibition-era" (001-015), and trade the comfortable clichés of Zenith masculinity. This early scene establishes the Bunch as Babbitt's emotional home — the one place where he doesn't have to perform, because the performance has become his nature.
- **The Prohibition Debate (later chapters)**: The Bunch's conversations about Prohibition reveal the group's characteristic hypocrisy — they drink heavily while endorsing dry laws, the contradiction resolved by the unspoken understanding that the laws are for other people.
- **Babbitt's Isolation**: When Babbitt's brief rebellion alienates him from Zenith society, it is the Bunch's withdrawal that hurts most. The poker invitations stop coming; the easy camaraderie is replaced by formal politeness. The Bunch, which had been the warm center of Babbitt's social life, becomes the instrument of his punishment.

## Satirical Significance

The Bunch is Lewis's study of male friendship under the conditions of American commercial civilization. The men are genuinely fond of each other — Lewis never denies the reality of their affection — but their friendship has no language except the language of business and boosterism. They cannot express emotion except through insults disguised as jokes; they cannot discuss ideas except as slogans. The Bunch is a portrait of emotional impoverishment disguised as camaraderie, of men who have everything except the capacity to say what they feel.

The name "Roughnecks" is the key to the satire: these comfortable businessmen, with their mortgages and their country-club memberships, pretend to a working-class virility they have long since traded away for respectability. The poker game, the beer, the cigars — all are props in a performance of masculinity that no one in the room quite believes but everyone is committed to maintaining.
