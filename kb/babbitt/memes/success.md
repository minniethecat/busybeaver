# Success

In the world of *Babbitt*, success is the universal yardstick of human value — measured in square footage, cylinder count, club memberships, and the "tokens of financial and social success" that mark a man as worthy of respect.

## Definition in Context

Success in Zenith is defined almost entirely in material and social terms. Babbitt measures his own worth by his yard, his car, his house: "it was the neat yard of a successful business man of Zenith, that is, it was perfection, and made him also perfect" (001-018). Achievement is circular — success is defined by the possession of things that other successful men also possess, such as "a sleeping-porch with a sun-parlor below" (007-026). The standard is conformity to a pattern of consumption, not individual excellence.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Babbitt's material proofs** — Babbitt's self-worth depends on visible tokens: his yard, his house, his six-cylinder car. The yard "made him also perfect" (001-018), revealing how completely external possessions substitute for internal self-regard.
- **The emptiness beneath achievement** — Despite having "pretty much done all the things I ought to; supported my family, and got a good house and a six-cylinder car" (005-026), Babbitt confesses to feeling "kind of down in the mouth all day long" (005-026), exposing the spiritual poverty of purely material success.
- **The successful Regular Guy** — The ideal American is "a God-fearing, hustling, successful, two-fisted Regular Guy, who belongs to some church with pep and piety to it, who belongs to the Boosters or the Rotarians or the Kiwanis" (014-034) — success measured by organizational membership and conformity to a stereotype.
- **Religion as success tool** — The "value of the Prayer-life in attaining financial success" (017-032) illustrates how even spirituality is subordinated to the goal of getting ahead, with church participation treated as a networking opportunity.

## Key Passages

> He creaked to his feet, groaning at the waves of pain which passed behind his eyeballs. Though he waited for their scorching recurrence, he looked blurrily out at the yard. It delighted him, as always; it was the neat yard of a successful business man of Zenith, that is, it was perfection, and made him also perfect. (001-018)

> "I've pretty much done all the things I ought to; supported my family, and got a good house and a six-cylinder car, and built up a nice little business, and I haven't got any vices 's a matter of fact, and I still feel kind of down in the mouth." (005-026)

## Satirical Function

Lewis satirizes the American cult of success by showing its circular logic: success is measured by possessions that mark one as successful. Babbitt's yard "makes him also perfect" — the verb exposes the absurdity of deriving moral worth from landscaping. The deeper satire lies in Babbitt's private confession to Paul Riesling: he has checked every box of the American success formula and still feels empty. Lewis suggests that the relentless pursuit of success is not merely unsatisfying but actively destructive of the capacity for genuine fulfillment. Success in Zenith is a performance that even its most accomplished performers cannot fully believe in.
