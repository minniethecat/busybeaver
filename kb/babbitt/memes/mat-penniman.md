# Mat Penniman

"Old Mat Penniman" is the general utility man at Babbitt-Thompson Realty — a figure whose age, deference, and humble function define the lower boundary of the office hierarchy that Babbitt commands. He is not a person so much as a piece of office furniture, a presence so routine that his humanity has been absorbed into his function.

## Role in the Novel

Mat Penniman serves as a quiet structural device: the fixed point against which Babbitt's own professional rise can be measured. While Babbitt ascends through deal-making and boosterism, Penniman remains stationary — the "general utility man" (003-042) whose title captures both his indispensability and his replaceability. He collects rent, runs errands to the recorder's office, and provides the low-level labor that keeps the real-estate machine operating. Babbitt sends him to "the recorder's office to dig out the names of the owners of houses" (004-012), a task that demands patience rather than skill.

Lewis's treatment of Penniman is characteristic: he is not mocked, exactly, but he is rendered entirely through his utility. At the office, he is seen "craning around at his desk" (006-017), a posture of attentive servitude that is also a posture of permanent subordination. He is old in a business culture that values youth and energy; his presence is a reminder of what happens to those who do not rise.

## Key Scenes

- **Rent Advice** — Babbitt offers Penniman "advice on getting money out of tenants" at the rate of fifty dollars a month — far less than the value Babbitt assigns to his own expertise (004-020). The asymmetry of the valuation is the scene's quiet point: Penniman does the actual collection work, but Babbitt's "advice" is what's priced.

- **The Invitations** — In a moment of social inclusion that is also exclusion, Penniman is "among the invitations to join" a gathering (031-009). The phrasing is revealing: Penniman is on the list but not quite of the company; he is included by courtesy, not by belonging.

- **The Office Tableau** — Lewis presents the Babbitt-Thompson office in a characteristic panoramic passage: "Miss McGoun looking over her ledger, Mat Penniman craning around at his desk" (006-017). The verb "craning" is precise and devastating — Penniman is not working but waiting, his body bent in a posture of attentive readiness for tasks that have not yet been assigned. He is visible in the office scene the way a coat-rack is visible: present, useful, unremarked.

## Character Traits

Penniman's defining trait is his lack of defining traits — he is a figure defined entirely by his function. He is "old" not just in years but in type: the loyal retainer of an earlier era of business, the man whose competence consists in the fact that no one notices him. In Babbitt's world of boosterism and self-promotion, Penniman's silence is both his virtue and his fate.

## The Utility Man as Social Commentary

Penniman represents a figure largely invisible in American literary modernism: the aging clerical worker whose life has been absorbed by the firm without yielding the rewards of ownership or partnership. He is not a failure in any dramatic sense; he has simply never risen, and now, late in his working life, he never will. The "general utility" of his title (003-042) is a cruel euphemism: it dignifies what is essentially interchangeable labor with the language of versatility.

The economic relationship between Babbitt and Penniman is stark. Babbitt pays himself for "advice" at a rate far higher than what Penniman earns for the actual labor of rent collection (004-020). The distinction between "advice" and labor, between the man who thinks and the man who does, is the fundamental class distinction within the office — and Penniman, who does, occupies the lower tier permanently. His age makes the situation more pointed: Penniman has spent decades in subordinate labor and has nothing to show for it but the right to continue, "craning around at his desk" until he can crane no longer.

The invitation episode (031-009) adds a final, delicate shading to the portrait. Penniman is invited to the gathering — he is not excluded outright — but the passive construction "among the invitations to join" makes clear that his inclusion is an afterthought, a gesture of office propriety rather than genuine social welcome. He is old, he is subordinate, and the invitation extended to him is of a kind that costs the extender nothing.
