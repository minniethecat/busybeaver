# Morality

In Zenith, morality has been domesticated — transformed from a set of principles about how to live into a set of conventions about how to appear. Lewis's anatomy of this transformation is the novel's most sustained satirical achievement: the exposure of a civilization that has mistaken manners for morals and reputation for righteousness.

## Definition in Context

Morality in *Babbitt* is primarily a social phenomenon — a matter of what is "done" and "not done" rather than what is right and wrong. When Babbitt's friends describe him as "so earnest about morality" (005-031), they mean he is a reliable enforcer of conventions, not that he is a person of unusual ethical sensitivity. Lewis's insight is that this confusion of morality with respectability is not a bug in Zenith's moral system but its defining feature: the system works precisely by making the two indistinguishable.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Morality as social performance** — The novel's characters perform morality through church attendance, prohibition enforcement, and denunciations of "immorality" in literature and politics. What matters is the performance, not the conviction.
- **The immorality panic** — Zenith periodically experiences increases in "what was known as Immorality" (018-016) — a nebulous category that includes everything from jazz music to labor organizing. The panic is a ritual of moral boundary-marking rather than a response to any actual ethical crisis.
- **Business as moral framework** — Economic success is taken as evidence of moral worth. The "God-fearing, hustling, successful" businessman (014-034) is the Zenith ideal — note that "God-fearing" and "hustling" are treated as equivalents.
- **The morality of golf** — In one of the novel's sharpest satirical passages, Babbitt returns to "high morality" and speaks "with gravity to Ted" about "the delights of golf, morality, and bank-accounts" (030-007) — the three terms placed in revealing apposition.
- **Morality without content** — What Zenith morality actually requires, beyond church attendance and business boosterism, is never specified. Its power lies precisely in this vagueness: it can be deployed against any deviation without having to justify itself.

## Key Passages

> In fact you're so earnest about morality, old Georgie, that I hate to think how you'd feel if you ever found out you were on the wrong side. (005-031, paraphrased)

> He rejoiced in his return to high morality and spoke with gravity to Ted about the delights of golf, morality, and bank-accounts. (030-007, paraphrased)

> It don't pay. Can't you see, old man, it just don't pay to be anything but regular? (020-013, paraphrased — the reduction of morality to prudence)

## Satirical Function

Lewis offers a devastating critique of conventional morality without ever proposing an alternative — his satire is descriptive, not prescriptive. The novel shows morality functioning as a mechanism of social control, as a status marker, and as a source of self-deception, but it never suggests that Zenith's moral code is simply false. The more unsettling suggestion is that morality in any society may be inseparable from the social functions it serves — that the line between ethics and etiquette may be impossible to draw. Babbitt's return to "high morality" at the novel's end is genuinely comforting to him, and Lewis refuses to mock that comfort even as he anatomizes its emptiness.
