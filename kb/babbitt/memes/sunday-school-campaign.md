# The Sunday School Campaign

[[george-babbitt|George Babbitt's]] campaign to revitalize the Sunday School at Chatham Road Presbyterian Church, described as giving him "a medium by which he could express his views on the inexplicable" (016-010). The campaign occupies the middle chapters of the novel and represents the intersection of business methods and religious life in Zenith — civic boosterism applied to spiritual education, with predictably mixed results.

## Background

Babbitt is a deacon at the Chatham Road Presbyterian Church, a position that reflects his standing in the community rather than any particular religious conviction. When the Sunday School is reported to be flagging, Babbitt is appointed to the Advisory Committee and throws himself into the project with characteristic energy — the same energy he brings to real-estate deals and [[boosters-club]] luncheons.

## Key Events

- **The Advisory Committee formation** — Babbitt is appointed to investigate the Sunday School's decline and recommend improvements. The committee meets "in the highly varnished Sunday School room" (016-026), a setting that captures the respectable surface of Zenith religion.
- **Babbitt's investigations** — He conducts "first investigations for the Sunday School Advisory Committee" (016-023), applying business methods — surveys, statistics, efficiency studies — to the spiritual domain.
- **The campaign's outcome** — The campaign achieves modest results before Babbitt's enthusiasm wanes, displaced by other concerns and eventually by the crisis of his [[babbitts-rebellion|rebellion]].

## The Booster Approach to Religion

Babbitt's Sunday School campaign is the novel's clearest example of the [[business]] mentality applied to religion. He treats the Sunday School as he would treat a struggling real-estate office: it needs better organization, more energetic promotion, and the application of sound business principles. That this approach misses the point of religious education entirely is Lewis's satirical target — the Sunday School, like everything in Zenith, becomes another arena for booster performance.

## Consequences

The Sunday School campaign is one of several civic projects through which Babbitt attempts to find meaning before his rebellion. Its limitations — the booster approach cannot address spiritual needs — anticipate the larger crisis to come. The campaign's quiet dissolution, as Babbitt moves on to other concerns, mirrors the pattern of his life: enthusiastic engagement followed by gradual disengagement, never quite reaching the satisfaction promised by each new project.
