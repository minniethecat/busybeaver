# Republican-Democratic Central Committee

The joint Republican and Democratic campaign organization that manages the local election effort in Zenith, dispatching George F. Babbitt from his safe Floral Heights precinct to the working-class wards of South Zenith where his newly acquired reputation for oratory can be put to political use (014-003).

## Description

The Republican-Democratic Central Committee is the local electoral apparatus that organizes the municipal campaign. While the nation is absorbed in the presidential election of Warren G. Harding, "Zenith was less interested in the national campaign than in the local election" (014-002). The Committee deploys speakers to the city's various wards, matching oratorical talent to electoral need. Its bipartisan name — "Republican-Democratic" — reflects the local political reality that in Zenith's municipal elections, party distinctions dissolve into a unified front of business interests against the insurgent candidacy of Seneca Doane (014-002).

Babbitt serves as "precinct-leader on Floral Heights, but his district was safe and he longed for stouter battling" (014-003). His convention paper — delivered at the state real-estate convention in Monarch — has "given him the beginning of a reputation for oratory," and the Committee capitalizes on this by sending him "to the Seventh Ward and South Zenith, to address small audiences of workmen and clerks, and wives uneasy with their new votes" (014-003). The election itself is not a contest between two parties with competing platforms but between "Sound Business," embodied by Lucas Prout, and "Class Rule," the epithet affixed to Doane's progressive challenge (014-010).

## Social Function

The Committee serves as the organizational vehicle for Babbitt's brief career as a campaign orator. Through it, he is thrust outside the comfortable precinct of Floral Heights into the grittier wards where the audiences are "workmen and clerks" and the venues are halls "over a delicatessen shop, in a street banging with trolleys and smelling of onions and gasoline and fried fish" (014-003)(014-005). This displacement from Babbitt's usual environment — the Athletic Club, the Boosters' lunches, the "oaken and velvety" Chatham Road Presbyterian Church — to Turnverein Hall in South Zenith generates the novel's most concentrated portrait of class division in Zenith.

The Committee's deployment of Babbitt reveals its political calculus: Floral Heights, a safe precinct, requires no persuasion, but the Seventh Ward and the outer reaches of South Zenith are contested ground. Babbitt is sent where his talents as a "natural orator" can convert working-class votes (014-003). His effectiveness derives from conviction — "He had faith; he was certain that if Lincoln were alive, he would be electioneering for Mr. W. G. Harding — unless he came to Zenith and electioneered for Lucas Prout" (014-003). Lewis notes that Babbitt was "obviously a Good Fellow; and, rarest of all, he really liked people. He almost liked common workmen" (014-003).

The Committee's reward system reveals the transactional logic at the heart of Zenith's civic life. After the election, Babbitt receives not political office but "advance information about the extension of paved highways" — insider knowledge that feeds his real-estate business (014-010). Political activism is simply an extension of commerce, and the Committee serves as the broker between electoral mobilization and commercial advantage.

## Key Scenes

- **The Campaign Circuit**: Babbitt is dispatched to the Seventh Ward and South Zenith, delivering speeches to "workmen and clerks, and wives uneasy with their new votes." The campaign yields tangible fame — "Now and then a reporter was present at one of his meetings, and the headlines (though they were not very large) indicated that George F. Babbitt had addressed Cheering Throng" (014-003). His apotheosis arrives when the Sunday *Advocate-Times* rotogravure section runs "a photograph of Babbitt and a dozen other business men, with the caption 'Leaders of Zenith Finance and Commerce Who Back Prout'" (014-003).

- **The Turnverein Hall Speech**: The most fully dramatized of Babbitt's campaign appearances takes place at Turnverein Hall in South Zenith, a venue "over a delicatessen shop" in a "street banging with trolleys and smelling of onions and gasoline and fried fish" (014-005). Babbitt arrives with his family and the Rieslings crowded in his car, and "a new appreciation of Babbitt filled all of them, including Babbitt" (014-005). The hall's interior — "a dais with a red-plush throne and a pine altar painted watery blue, as used nightly by the Grand Masters and Supreme Potentates of innumerable lodges" (014-007) — is the visual lexicon of lodge fraternalism. The chairman who introduces him forgets his name: "The speaker? All ready, sir! Uh — let's see — what was the name, sir?" (014-007). Babbitt's oratory opens by invoking "one plain man of business" who was "brought up to the blessings of poverty and of manual labor" (014-007). The audience fractures along generational and ethnic lines: "young cynical workmen, for the most part foreigners, Jews, Swedes, Irishmen, Italians" jeer, while "the older men, the patient, bleached, stooped carpenters and mechanics, cheered him" (014-007).

- **Post-Election Victory**: The election results vindicate the Committee's strategy — "Mr. Lucas Prout and Sound Business defeated Mr. Seneca Doane and Class Rule, and Zenith was again saved" (014-010). Babbitt's political service culminates in a seat at the victory dinner: "he was one of only nineteen speakers at the dinner with which the Chamber of Commerce celebrated the victory of righteousness" (014-010).

## Satirical Significance

The Committee embodies Lewis's satire of local politics as a business enterprise. Its bipartisan name collapses the distinction between the two national parties into a local united front against the socialist-leaning Seneca Doane (014-002). Babbitt's role — a safe-precinct leader dispatched to address the working class — positions him as the voice of business prosperity speaking to audiences whose relationship to that prosperity is tenuous at best. He does not confuse audiences with nuance: "Prout represented honest industry, Seneca Doane represented whining laziness, and you could take your choice" (014-003).

The victory dinner, with its "only nineteen speakers," confirms that political success in Zenith is measured not in policy outcomes but in speaking slots at Chamber of Commerce banquets (014-010). The Committee is, finally, not a political organization in any meaningful sense — it is a mechanism for converting electoral victory into business advantage, and Babbitt's brief career as a campaign orator is the perfect expression of its function.
