# Willis Ijams

Willis Ijams is the co-owner of Ijams Brothers', the Sporting Goods Mart, a fellow member of the Boosters' Club, and a salesman whose combination of "poetry and diplomacy" makes him one of the more genial figures in Babbitt's commercial world. He embodies the novel's recurring type of the businessman whose expertise — in Ijams's case, fly-fishing — lends him a dignity that pure commerce cannot provide.

## Role in the Novel

Ijams represents the competent, amiable businessman whose specialized knowledge elevates him above the pure boosterism of figures like [[vergil-gunch|Vergil Gunch]]. When [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]] and [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]] prepare for their Maine fishing trip, Ijams's expertise with flies transforms him from a mere merchant into a connoisseur. His discussion of "the great scrap between dry flies and wet flies" is one of the few commercial interactions in the novel that Babbitt finds genuinely pleasurable rather than merely transactional. Later, Ijams appears as the new president of the Boosters' Club, having risen through the organization's ranks.

## Key Scenes

- **Buying tackle at Ijams Brothers'** — Babbitt and Paul Riesling visit Ijams Brothers' to buy tackle for their Maine fishing trip. Babbitt is "completely mad" with excitement, trumpeting and dancing at the counter. That "good old Willis Ijams himself" comes down to the floor to wait on them personally makes the experience feel like an honor rather than a purchase. (010-019)
- **The fly-fishing discourse** — Ijams discusses flies with Paul Riesling, and "even Paul lightened" during the conversation. Ijams's opening gambit — "Now, of course, you boys know the great scrap is between dry flies and wet flies" — is delivered with "poetry and diplomacy," transforming a sales pitch into a shared enthusiasm. The moment offers one of the novel's rare glimpses of genuine male camaraderie mediated by technical expertise. (010-021)
- **Introducing the sporting editor** — At a Boosters' Club gathering, Ijams serves as social facilitator, introducing Babbitt to the sporting editor of the *Press*. His role as connector reflects his easy sociability and broad acquaintance. (021-017)
- **Elevation to Boosters' Club president** — By the novel's later chapters, Ijams has become the new president of the Boosters' Club. Babbitt boasts to Myra, "Willis Ijams is the new president, but when he's away, little ole Georgie takes the gavel." The succession marks Ijams as a man whose genial competence has been rewarded with institutional authority. (023-021)

## Character Traits

Lewis gives Ijams a rare combination of qualities: commercial competence leavened by genuine enthusiasm for his product and a diplomatic touch with customers. His "poetry" — his ability to make the dry-fly/wet-fly debate compelling — lifts him out of the generic booster mold. Unlike the novel's many interchangeable businessmen, Ijams has a recognizable expertise and a personality that earns him the adjective "good old" from Babbitt without irony.

## Relationships
