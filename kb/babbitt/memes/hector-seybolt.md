# Hector Seybolt

Hector Seybolt's name is a comedy in miniature. "Hector" is the greatest warrior of Troy, the defender of a doomed city; "Seybolt" is a name that has never been sung in epic verse. For the proprietor of the Little Sweetheart Condiment Company, the heroic first name is an accident of parental ambition rather than a destiny — a reminder, in Lewis's satirical economy, that in Zenith even the most Homeric name attaches to a man who sells relishes. The name operates as what might be called a "mock-heroic diminutive": the epic associations of "Hector" are systematically undercut by the commercial triviality of the life to which it is attached, a technique Lewis deploys throughout the novel to expose the gap between American aspiration and American achievement.

## Role in the Novel

Seybolt appears in a single named reference, grouped among the Boosters' Club membership: "Boos the merchant tailor, Hector Seybolt of the Little Sweetheart Condiment Company" (021-004). The juxtaposition is characteristic of Lewis's cataloguing technique — trades and names listed side by side, each man defined by his commercial identity. Seybolt is a "merchant tailor" and the proprietor of a condiment company: a man who makes and sells things that are useful but not dignified, necessary but not noble.

The listing places Seybolt firmly within the Boosters' orbit, one of the dozens of small businessmen who constitute the organization's membership and who receive, in Lewis's prose, the briefest of nods — enough to mark their existence, not enough to grant them individuality. His single appearance in the text, embedded in a catalog of names, enacts in literary form the social condition Lewis diagnoses: the reduction of individual identity to occupational function, the transformation of a man into a line item in the Boosters' Club roster.

## The Little Sweetheart Condiment Company

The name of Seybolt's enterprise deserves attention as an instance of Lewis's gift for the satirically resonant commercial name. "Little Sweetheart" is a phrase that belongs to the vocabulary of sentimental endearment, not the lexicon of commerce; its application to a condiment company produces a faint but unmistakable queasiness — the suggestion that affection itself can be bottled, branded, and sold. The condiment, after all, is the supplement to the meal, the non-essential addition that makes the ordinary palatable. Seybolt's profession, seen through Lewis's satirical lens, is the manufacture of inessential pleasantness — a trade that mirrors, in miniature, the booster culture's project of coating commercial reality in "little sweetheart" sentiment.

The double identity — merchant tailor and condiment manufacturer — suggests a man who has not risen to a single commanding commercial identity but has instead assembled a livelihood from multiple modest enterprises. In this sense he represents the more precarious stratum of the Boosters' Club: men for whom membership in the organization is not merely a ritual of belonging but a genuinely necessary network of commercial contacts and social validation.

## Character Traits

Seybolt, like many of the novel's peripheral figures, exists as a type rather than a character: the small businessman whose identity is entirely absorbed by his trade. His two occupations — tailoring and condiment manufacture — suggest a man of practical skills and modest commercial ambition, the kind of figure who fills out the Boosters' Club roster and provides the institutional mass that makes organizations like the Boosters' Club possible. He is not important enough to be satirized individually; he is important only as a representative of the class that Lewis anatomizes.

The pairing of "Boos the merchant tailor" with "Hector Seybolt of the Little Sweetheart Condiment Company" in Lewis's single sentence (021-004) creates a miniature taxonomy within the Boosters' Club: the German-American merchant (Boos) whose name suggests conviviality, and the classically-named condiment manufacturer whose Homeric first name sits in ironic tension with his commercial reality. Together they represent the ethnic and occupational variety that the Boosters' Club absorbs and homogenizes into its single creed of business fellowship.

## Satirical Significance

Seybolt exemplifies what might be termed the "catalog satire" technique — Lewis's practice of embedding satirical commentary in the very structure of his lists and enumerations. The Boosters' Club membership roll is not merely a register of names but a compressed social analysis: each entry (tailor, condiment manufacturer, realtor, contractor) is a data point in Lewis's argument that in Zenith, a man is what he sells. The Homeric "Hector" attached to a condiment merchant is not merely incidental humor but a structural claim about the relationship between the classical ideals of heroism and the modern realities of commerce. The epic hero who fought for a city has been replaced by the small businessman who sells for one.
