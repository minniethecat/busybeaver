# Maccabee Temple

A fraternal meeting hall in Zenith, used as a venue for political rallies. Its name evokes the Maccabees, a Jewish fraternal order, and the temple served as one of the many lodge halls that dotted the industrial wards of the city. In the dense ecosystem of Zenith's fraternal life — which encompassed "the Elks, the Moose, the Red Men, the Knights of Pythias, the Woodmen, the Owls, the Eagles, the Maccabees, the Knights of Columbus" (PN 016-004) — each lodge and its hall played a specific role in organizing the loyalties, ambitions, and leisure time of the middle class.

## Description

Maccabee Temple was a lodge hall located in one of Zenith's industrial wards, used by fraternal orders for meetings and by political campaigns for rallies. During the mayoral election, it was the site of Babbitt's third campaign speech of the evening, where Mrs. Babbitt — relishing her role as campaign wife — exclaimed: "Right now they're gathering in Maccabee Temple, and *waiting* for us!" (PN 014-008). The temple was one of the many such halls that hosted the nightly meetings of "Grand Masters and Supreme Potentates of innumerable lodges" (PN 014-007), a phrase in which Lewis captures the absurd grandiosity of fraternal titles and the interchangeable quality of these institutions.

The Maccabees themselves were one of dozens of fraternal orders active in Zenith. The sheer proliferation of these organizations is one of Lewis's running jokes: at a Chamber of Commerce luncheon, Babbitt's peers came from "the Benevolent and Protective Order of Elks, the Protective and Benevolent Order of Moose, the Loyal Order of Red Men, the Woodmen, the Owls, the Eagles, the Maccabees, the Knights of Pythias, the Knights of Columbus" (PN 016-004) — a list whose repetition of the words "benevolent," "protective," and "order" suggests ritualized benevolence as a mask for banality.

## Social Function

Maccabee Temple functioned within the dense network of fraternal organizations that structured working-class and lower-middle-class social life in Zenith. Such halls served the dual purpose of providing meeting spaces for lodges and functioning as accessible venues for political campaigns seeking to reach working-class voters. The use of Maccabee Temple for Babbitt's campaign rally illustrates how the fraternal infrastructure of Zenith was co-opted by the city's political machinery.

The fraternal order system was itself a kind of parallel government of the middle class. The Elks, where Babbitt "went more regularly" (PN 016-002), offered a hierarchy of offices, rituals, and social standing that operated as an alternative to — and reinforcement of — the economic hierarchy of the business world. A man who was merely a mid-level real-estate broker by day could become an Exalted Ruler or Grand Potentate by night. The fraternal halls were the temples of this alternative social order, providing physical spaces where the fiction of earned nobility could be enacted through ritual, regalia, and mutual recognition.

## Key Scenes

- **Campaign night at Maccabee Temple** — After speaking at Turnverein Hall in South Zenith, Babbitt's party rushes to Maccabee Temple for his third speech of the evening. His wife announces the venue with excitement, underscoring the campaign's importance to the Babbitt family and the way political campaigning extended the rituals of boosterism into the working-class wards (PN 014-008)
- **The Chamber of Commerce luncheon** — Babbitt's peers gather from "the Elks, the Moose, the Red Men, the Woodmen, the Owls, the Eagles, the Maccabees, the Knights of Pythias, the Knights of Columbus" (PN 016-004), a catalog of fraternal affiliation that suggests the organizational density — and interchangeability — of Zenith's social infrastructure

## Satirical Significance

The Maccabee Temple, like Turnverein Hall and the unnamed lodges of South Zenith, represents the institutionalization of social life in Lewis's America. The proliferation of fraternal orders and their halls — each with their grandiose titles and arcane rituals — is part of the novel's broader portrait of a society substituting organizational membership for genuine community. That the temple is merely a stop on Babbitt's campaign circuit, its name mentioned but its interior never described, underscores the interchangeability of these institutions in the machinery of Zenith's civic life: one temple is much like another, and in any of them an upstanding citizen can deliver the same speech to the same kind of audience.

The fraternal order system is also the novel's most visible instance of a phenomenon Lewis anatomizes throughout: the human need for belonging and distinction, channeled into forms that neutralize both. A Babbitt who might have been a genuine community leader becomes instead a serial joiner of organizations whose names promise benevolence and protection while delivering only the rituals of mutual approval. The Maccabee Temple — a building named for ancient warriors, filled with businessmen in regalia — is the architectural expression of this diminishment.
