# Grover Butterbaugh

Grover Butterbaugh is an eminent wholesaler of plumbers' supplies and a member of [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s extended social world. He embodies the prosperous middle tier of Zenith's business class — successful enough to matter, unremarkable enough to be representative. His single named appearance in the novel encapsulates the dynamics of status, banter, and mutual surveillance that define male social life in Zenith.

## Role in the Novel

Butterbaugh appears in Chapter XVII during the aftermath of Babbitt's successful Sunday School campaign, in which Babbitt has maneuvered himself into public association with [[william-washington-eathorne|William Washington Eathorne]], the patrician president of the First State Bank. The newspapers have run flattering accounts of Babbitt's "sterling labors for religion," and "nothing had brought Babbitt quite so much credit at the Elks, the Athletic Club, and the Boosters'" (017-041). This is the context for Butterbaugh's entrance.

As Babbitt basks in the glow of his new association with old money, Orville Jones shouts across the Athletic Club dining-room, "Here's the new director of the First State Bank!" It is in this moment of collective recognition that Butterbaugh delivers his memorable line: "Wonder you mix with common folks, after holding Eathorne's hand!" (017-041). The joke is genial but not innocent — it acknowledges the social distance Eathorne represents and, by extension, the distance Babbitt has momentarily traveled. Butterbaugh is at once congratulating Babbitt and reminding him — and everyone else at the table — of his place.

Lewis uses Butterbaugh's cameo to dramatize the way social capital circulates in Zenith: through jokes, through lunchroom banter, through the thousand small acknowledgments that constitute a man's standing among his peers. Butterbaugh's "chuckle" carries the weight of the entire social system.

## Character Traits

Butterbaugh is jovial, conventional, and shrewd enough to recognize social advantage when he sees it. His epithet — "the eminent wholesaler of plumbers' supplies" — is itself a small satirical masterpiece: the comic inflation of a thoroughly unglamorous trade into something approaching dignity. He represents the settled middle — men who have found their level and are content to stay there, watching the Babbitts of the world scramble upward with amusement rather than envy.

## Appearance in Context

Butterbaugh appears in a sequence that Lewis carefully stages to show the ripple effects of Babbitt's association with Eathorne. In rapid succession after the newspaper coverage:

- Orville Jones publicly announces Babbitt's new status (017-041)
- Grover Butterbaugh delivers his teasing tribute (017-041)
- Emil Wengert, the jeweler, is "at last willing to discuss buying a house in Dorchester" (017-041)

The progression is comic but structurally significant: social recognition (Jones) → social banter (Butterbaugh) → economic transaction (Wengert). In Zenith, status is never purely symbolic — it always converts, eventually, into real estate.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt]]** — A social peer who watches Babbitt's ascent with amused admiration, his teasing functioning as both recognition and gentle boundary-policing
- **[[william-washington-eathorne]]** — Views Eathorne with the same distant reverence as the rest of the business class, the figure whose reflected glory momentarily illuminates Babbitt
- **[[Orville-Jones]]** — Fellow Athletic Club member, part of the same social circle that collectively processes and validates Babbitt's status gains

## Significance

Butterbaugh exemplifies Lewis's technique of the telling minor character. In a single sentence of dialogue, he reveals the intricate social calculus of Zenith: the precise measurement of status, the translation of old money into new credit, and the way an entire community participates in the collective work of ranking its members. His trade — plumbing supplies — is itself a joke about the material basis of Zenith's prosperity, the unglamorous infrastructure beneath the booster rhetoric.
