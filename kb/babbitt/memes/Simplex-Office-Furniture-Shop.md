# Simplex Office Furniture Shop

The Simplex Office Furniture Shop is a retail establishment in downtown Zenith selling office furniture and business equipment. It is where George Babbitt covets the latest business technology—a dictaphone and an adding-multiplying typewriter—that represent his aspirations for modern efficiency and professional status. The shop appears in a key passage of the novel's early chapters, during Babbitt's morning walk through Zenith's business district, a ritual that Lewis uses to map the geography of Babbitt's material desires.

## Description

The shop displays modern office equipment that captures Babbitt's imagination during his morning walk through Zenith's business district. Passing the Simplex Office Furniture Shop and the neighboring National Cash Register Agency, Babbitt "yearned for a dictaphone, for a typewriter which would add and multiply, as a poet yearns for quartos or a physician for radium" (PN 005-008).

The comparison Lewis draws—Babbitt's longing for office machines set against a poet's love of fine editions or a physician's need for radium—is deliberately grandiose and ironic. It elevates Babbitt's consumer desire to the level of artistic or scientific passion, while simultaneously revealing the poverty of his imaginative world: his equivalent of poetry is office efficiency, and his radium is a calculating machine. The "quartos" reference—large-format books prized by bibliophiles—emphasizes the cultural gulf between Babbitt's world and the world of art and learning.

## The Objects of Desire

Babbitt's yearnings at the Simplex window are for two specific items:

- **The Dictaphone** — a early voice-recording device used for business dictation, representing the mechanization of office communication and the prestige of having one's words important enough to record. In the 1920s, the dictaphone was a marker of the modern, efficient executive who delegated transcription to stenographers.
- **The Adding-Multiplying Typewriter** — a combination calculating and typing machine that promised to collapse two office functions into one, embodying the era's faith in technological solutions to business problems. The device represents not merely efficiency but the fantasy of the self-sufficient businessman who needs no clerical staff.

Neither item is something Babbitt practically requires. He manages his real estate business adequately without them. The yearning is for the symbolism of the objects—the dictaphone says "I am important enough to dictate," the adding typewriter says "I am modern enough to compute."

## Social Function

The Simplex Office Furniture Shop serves as a site of consumer desire in Babbitt's daily routine. The morning walk past shop windows is a ritual of urban commercial life, where the display of goods stimulates aspirations. The shop's merchandise—furniture and machines designed for the modern office—embodies the values of the era: efficiency, mechanization, and the cult of the up-to-date businessman.

The brand name "Simplex" itself is telling: it promises simplicity and ease, the reduction of business complexity to a purchasable commodity. It belongs to the same commercial naming vocabulary as "Riteway" Business College and other Zenith enterprises that brand themselves with promises of straightforward results. The name also carries an ironic charge—the promise of simplicity attached to machines that add complexity to the office, just as modern business culture promises freedom through ever more elaborate systems of conformity.

## Key Scenes

The Simplex Office Furniture Shop appears in Chapter 5, during Babbitt's morning walk to his office. This walk is one of the novel's recurring set-pieces, a passage through Zenith's commercial district that serves as both geographic description and psychological portrait. Each shop window Babbitt passes reflects a different facet of his desires and values: the Simplex window reflects his yearning for modern equipment, the National Cash Register Agency his faith in systematic accounting, and other storefronts his various other material aspirations (PN 005-008).

The passage is notable for what Lewis reveals about Babbitt through his relationship to objects. Babbitt does not think about ideas, art, or human relationships during his walk—he thinks about machines he wants to buy. His inner life, such as it is, is organized around commodities.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses Babbitt's encounter with the Simplex shop window to satirize the mechanization of desire in modern commercial society. Babbitt does not actually need a dictaphone or an adding-multiplying typewriter—he yearns for them because they represent the next rung on the ladder of business success. The yearning is for the status the objects confer, not their function. The poet/physician comparison drives home Lewis's critique: in Babbitt's world, the highest form of aspiration is the purchase of office equipment.

The passage also exemplifies what critic Sinclair Lewis called the "standardization" of American life. Babbitt's desires are not authentically his own—they are manufactured by the commercial culture that surrounds him, displayed in shop windows, advertised in trade journals, and reinforced by his peer group. The Simplex shop window is not merely a retail display; it is a site of ideological reproduction, teaching Babbitt what a successful businessman should want and how he should measure his worth.
