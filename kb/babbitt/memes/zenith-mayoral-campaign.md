# Zenith Mayoral Campaign

The Zenith mayoral campaign of 1920 is one of the novel's most politically pointed episodes, dramatizing the chasm between the city's conservative business establishment and the faint stirrings of labor radicalism. It is also, for George F. Babbitt, a moment of personal transformation: the campaign makes him, however briefly, a figure of public importance.

## Background

In the autumn of 1920, while the nation was absorbed in the Harding-Cox presidential race, Zenith's attention was fixed on its own local contest. Seneca Doane, a lawyer and State University graduate, ran for mayor on a labor ticket that the business class regarded as barely distinguishable from Bolshevism. To oppose him, the Democratic and Republican parties took the extraordinary step of uniting behind a single candidate: Lucas Prout, a mattress manufacturer "with a perfect record for sanity" (014-002).

## The Candidates

**Seneca Doane** represented the novel's liberal conscience — a figure who appears throughout the narrative as the advocate of progressive causes that Babbitt's set instinctively distrusts. His labor-backed candidacy challenged the assumption that Zenith's governance belonged by right to its businessmen.

**Lucas Prout** was the establishment's answer: a manufacturer, not a politician; a man whose chief qualification was that he was reliably, uninterestingly sane. He was supported by "the banks, the Chamber of Commerce, all the decent newspapers, and George F. Babbitt" (014-002).

## Babbitt's Role

Babbitt threw himself into the campaign with an enthusiasm that surprised even himself. As precinct-leader for solidly Republican Floral Heights, his district was safe; but the Central Committee, recognizing the oratorical promise he had shown at the real-estate convention, dispatched him to the Seventh Ward and South Zenith to address audiences of workmen and clerks.

There he discovered, to his own astonishment, that he was a natural campaigner. "He had faith; he was certain that if Lincoln were alive, he would be electioneering for Mr. W. G. Harding — unless he came to Zenith and electioneered for Lucas Prout" (014-003). The *Advocate-Times* ran his photograph among the "Leaders of Zenith Finance and Commerce Who Back Prout," and for a season Babbitt tasted a fame that was almost — but not quite — the real thing.

## Significance

The mayoral campaign crystallizes the novel's political vision. Doane's candidacy shows that Zenith is not as monolithic as it appears: there are undercurrents of dissent, voices that question the business gospel. But the united front against him — Democrats and Republicans, banks and newspapers, manufacturers and real-estate brokers — demonstrates the overwhelming force that the establishment can bring to bear against even modest challenges to its authority. Babbitt's participation, meanwhile, reveals how easily a man of no particular convictions can be swept into political engagement when it flatters his vanity and confirms his belonging.
