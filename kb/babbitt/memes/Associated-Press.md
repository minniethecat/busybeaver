# Associated Press

The Associated Press is the national news wire service whose Zenith bureau appears in the opening passage of *Babbitt*, symbolizing the city's connection to the wider world.

## Description

The Associated Press maintains an office in one of Zenith's skyscrapers. As dawn breaks over the city, the AP telegraph operators conclude their night shift, having spent the night "talking with Paris and Peking" (001-005). The operators wearily raise their celluloid eye-shades, signaling the transition from the global news cycle to the city's waking industrial rhythm.

## The Opening Scene

The AP bureau appears in the novel's first substantive description of Zenith, immediately after the initial panorama of "towers aspiring above the morning mist" (001-005). Lewis establishes the city's modern credentials through this detail — the wires of a national news service threading through Zenith's skyscrapers, connecting the Midwestern city to the capitals of Europe and Asia:

> In one of the skyscrapers the wires of the Associated Press were closing down. The telegraph operators wearily raised their celluloid eye-shades after a night of talking with Paris and Peking. Through the building crawled the scrubwomen, yawning, their old shoes slapping. The dawn mist spun away. Cues of men with lunch-boxes clumped toward the immensity of new factories. (001-005)

The passage establishes a vertical cross-section of the city: the AP operators occupy the upper reaches ("in one of the skyscrapers"), engaged in the immaterial work of global communication, while beneath them the scrubwomen and factory workers form the material base.

## From Global Wire to Local Boosterism

The raw information transmitted by the AP — dispatches from Paris and Peking — would, after editing, flow into the *Advocate-Times*, the local newspaper that arrives at Babbitt's front door each morning with a thump (001-011). For Babbitt, the newspaper is "the exhilarating drug of the *Advocate-Times* headlines" (002-017), suggesting that the AP's global reports are filtered through the booster frame before reaching Zenith's breakfast tables. What arrives in Europe as neutral news reaches Babbitt repackaged as confirmation of Zenith's greatness.

The transition from international wire service to local booster press is part of Lewis's larger critique: Zenith possesses the infrastructure of a world city — skyscrapers, telegraph wires, factories — but its intellectual culture remains provincial and self-congratulatory.

## Social Function

Lewis deploys the Associated Press office to establish Zenith's cosmopolitan credentials in the novel's very first paragraphs. The AP's presence — with its direct wires to European and Asian capitals — positions Zenith not as a provincial backwater but as a city connected to global currents of information. The immediacy of international communication contrasts with the mechanical, repetitive labor of the city's industrial workforce, introduced in the same passage.

## Key Scenes

- **Dawn in Zenith** — At the close of the night shift, AP telegraph operators finish their work communicating with Paris and Peking as scrubwomen begin their morning rounds and factory workers stream toward the new industrial plants (001-005)

## Satirical Significance

The Associated Press reference is part of Lewis's opening technique of presenting Zenith as impressive yet ironically diminished. The AP's global reach — "talking with Paris and Peking" — sounds grand, but the operators' weariness, the scrubwomen's slapping shoes, and the assembly-line rhythm of factory-bound workers undercut the glamour. The news wire, for all its reach, produces only the raw material for the booster press that will later repackage events into the self-congratulatory narrative of the *Zenith Advocate-Times*.
