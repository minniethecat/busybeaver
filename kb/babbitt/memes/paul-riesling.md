# Paul Riesling

The one authentic emotional connection in [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt's]] life and the novel's most devastating figure — a man whose sensitivity, artistic temperament, and catastrophic marriage expose the human cost of the [[zenith|Zenith]] system. Where [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] accommodates and survives, Paul refuses accommodation and is destroyed. His trajectory from quiet despair to violent rupture makes him the novel's moral and emotional anchor.

## Biography

Paul Riesling is [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s college roommate turned lifelong friend. He is characterized by his "dark slimness, his precisely parted hair, his nose-glasses, his hesitant speech, his moodiness, his love of music" (004-018) — everything [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is not. Rather than pursuing a career in music, his one genuine passion, he entered his father's roofing business, a concession that foretells every subsequent compromise of his life. He married [[zilla-riesling|Zilla]], a woman whose "nagging and complaining" has reduced him to a state of permanent, exhausted despair. The marriage is a catastrophe: [[zilla-riesling|Zilla]]'s constant criticism and Paul's inability to escape it form the novel's darkest portrait of institutionalized misery. When Paul finally shoots [[zilla-riesling|Zilla]] in a moment of uncontainable rage, the act is both shocking and tragically overdetermined.

## Role in the Novel

Paul is [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s foil and the novel's moral center. His function is to demonstrate what the "religion of [[business]]" costs those who cannot make their peace with it. While [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] sublimates his discontent into booster rhetoric and mechanical routine, Paul absorbs his until it destroys him. His imprisonment marks the novel's emotional turning point — after this, [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s discontent can no longer be contained by the rituals of business and club life. As Lewis writes of [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s prison visit: "[[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] knew that in this place of death Paul was already dead" (024-004).

## Key Scenes

- **The lunch confession** (Chapter V) — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] confesses his dissatisfaction over lunch, and Paul responds with his own, deeper despair. When [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] asks "Why don't you divorce [[zilla-riesling|Zilla]]?" (005-030), Paul's inability to answer reveals the paralysis at the heart of his character. This scene, the novel's most emotionally direct, establishes the friendship as the one relationship in which either man can speak honestly.

- **The shooting** — The novel's most violent act: Paul, driven beyond endurance, shoots [[zilla-riesling|Zilla]]. The event is narrated elliptically, through reports and aftermath, a technique that emphasizes the shock and the failure of language to contain such rupture.

- **The prison visit** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s visit to Paul in the state penitentiary is the novel's darkest passage. Paul is "already dead" (024-004) — a man whose spirit was extinguished long before his physical death. The scene forces [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] to confront what [[zenith|Zenith]] does to those who cannot adapt.

## Character Traits

Paul is defined by his sensitivity in a world that has no use for it. His "moodiness" and "love of music" (004-018) mark him as an artist manqué — a man whose natural gifts have been systematically crushed by the demands of respectable [[zenith|Zenith]] life. His "nervousness" and "shakiness" (005-026) are the physical symptoms of a soul under siege. Lewis uses Paul to suggest what [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] might have become had he possessed greater sensitivity — and, more darkly, what [[zenith|Zenith]] does to those who cannot, or will not, accommodate.

> He was a man of dark slimness, his precisely parted hair, his nose-glasses, his hesitant speech, his moodiness, his love of music. (004-018)

> Babbitt knew that in this place of death Paul was already dead. (024-004)

## Critical Reception

Paul Riesling has been recognized by critics as the novel's most important secondary character — the tragic foil without whom Babbitt's story would remain merely comic. T. S. Matthews observed that "Paul is the one wholly sympathetic figure in the book, and his fate is the one genuinely tragic event." Later feminist critics have complicated this reading, noting that Zilla is rendered with misogynistic flatness, and that Paul's violence against her is narratively framed as tragedy rather than crime. This critical debate has enriched interpretations of the novel's gender politics, positioning Paul at the center of questions about Lewis's representation of marriage, masculinity, and violence.

> "Paul Riesling is Lewis's most devastating creation — a man whose destruction implicates not just one bad marriage but an entire civilization that has no use for tenderness." — T. S. Matthews, *The New Republic* (1922)

## Satirical Significance

If [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is the novel's comic-satirical figure, Paul is its tragic counterweight. Lewis uses him to insist that the booster-optimism of [[zenith]] has a body count — that the "religion of business" kills as surely as it comforts. Paul's fate is the novel's most powerful critique of the world it depicts: a society that has no place for sensitivity, no language for despair, and no exit from institutions that have become prisons.
