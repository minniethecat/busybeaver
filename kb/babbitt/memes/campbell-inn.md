# Campbell Inn

A hotel on the South Side of Zenith, notable as the location where Paul Riesling meets his mistress. Unlike the respectable Regency Hotel where Babbitt dines alone, the Campbell Inn is a space of genuine transgression — a place where the novel's central moral crisis takes physical form.

## Description

The Campbell Inn is located "on the South Side" (019-049), a part of Zenith less respectable than Babbitt's Floral Heights. Its office is "hard, bright, new" (020-004) — the aggressive newness suggesting an establishment that does not ask questions about its guests. Paul Riesling brings his mistress here, and Babbitt, as confidant and accomplice, becomes entangled in the arrangement.

When Paul names the Inn — "Campbell Inn, on the South Side" (019-049) — the address carries weight: the South Side is not where respectable Zenith citizens go. Babbitt's involvement is limited to knowing the location and, in one charged moment, directing a taxi there: "savagely he said 'Campbell Inn!' to the taxi-driver" (020-003). The adverb "savagely" captures his conflicted state — anger, excitement, complicity.

## Social Function

The Campbell Inn functions as the underside of Zenith's respectable institutions. If the Athletic Club is where men perform civic virtue and the Union Club where old money presides, the Campbell Inn is where the arrangements those institutions pretend do not exist are made.

The hotel also marks a class boundary. It is not the elegant Regency Hotel of Babbitt's solitary dinners but a South Side establishment, suggesting that Paul's affair occupies a lower rung of Zenith's social geography — not even his transgressions can access the city's better addresses.

- **The confession** — Paul tells Babbitt about the Campbell Inn, the disclosure drawing Babbitt into his friend's secret life (019-049)
- **The taxi** — Babbitt gives the taxi-driver the address, the single word "Campbell Inn!" freighted with the violence of suppressed emotions (020-003)
- **The office** — The "hard, bright, new" office of the Inn (020-004), an impersonal space that mirrors the transaction taking place within

## Satirical Significance

The Campbell Inn is where the novel's critique of Zenith respectability becomes most explicit. The city's booster mythology insists on propriety, family values, and civic virtue; the Campbell Inn is where those values are privately violated. That Paul Riesling — a gentle, artistic man trapped in a brutal marriage — ends up here is a measure of the system's failure.

Lewis positions the Inn as a space of moral ambiguity. Babbitt is not the adulterer but the accomplice; his complicity is passive rather than active. Yet his knowledge of the Inn and his willingness to keep Paul's secret implicate him in the same hypocrisy he professes to despise.
