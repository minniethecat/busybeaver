# Cecil Rountree

Cecil Rountree is a Zenith realtor and speculative builder, a fellow delegate to the State Association of Real Estate Boards convention, and — from Babbitt's perspective — the man who has everything Babbitt wants. As chairman of the convention program-committee, Rountree occupies the professional recognition Babbitt covets. As an attendee of "the smartest dances on Royal Ridge," he possesses the social access Babbitt envies. Together these attributes make Rountree the novel's most concentrated symbol of competitive professional envy.

## Role in the Novel

Rountree appears primarily in Chapter XIII, the convention chapter, where he serves as a foil that reveals Babbitt's deepest insecurities. Babbitt "admired him for his picaresque speculative building, and hated him for his social position, for being present at the smartest dances on Royal Ridge" (013-004). The double verb — admired and hated — captures Babbitt's characteristic emotional contradiction: he cannot separate professional respect from personal resentment.

Rountree's role as program-committee chairman places him in a position of institutional authority over Babbitt at the convention — an authority that Babbitt, in his own mind, both acknowledges and resists. When Babbitt gives his impromptu speech and the convention elevates him "from a minor delegate to a personage almost as well known as that diplomat of business, Cecil Rountree," the comparison is the highest measure of professional ascent Babbitt can imagine (013-043).

## Key Scenes

- **The midnight train to Monarch** — Rountree refuses to wear the convention badges that the other delegates display: "All of them, save Cecil Rountree, who was such a snob that he never wore badges, displayed celluloid buttons the size of dollars and lettered 'We zoom for Zenith'" (013-019). His refusal marks the social distance between himself and the booster enthusiasm of his fellow delegates; he is too refined for booster paraphernalia.

- **Babbitt's convention speech** — After Babbitt addresses the convention, Lewis reports that "in fifteen minutes he had changed from a minor delegate to a personage almost as well known as that diplomat of business, Cecil Rountree" (013-043). The line is barbed: "almost as well known" suggests Babbitt has not quite caught up, and the epithet "diplomat of business" hints at the social polish Babbitt lacks.

- **The Garden Party at Mrs. Knowlton's** — "Without request, the Zenith delegates (except Rountree) gathered round a marble dancing nymph and sang 'Here we come, the fellows from Zenith, the Zip Citee'" (013-039). Rountree's absence from the booster singalong is characteristic: he stands apart from the communal displays of enthusiasm that define the other delegates.

## Character Traits

Rountree's defining trait is his apartness. He does not wear badges; he does not sing; he does not participate in the collective rituals of booster masculinity. This apartness is both the source of his social prestige and the quality Babbitt most resents. Rountree possesses what Babbitt cannot buy with booster enthusiasm: an air of natural superiority that requires no demonstration.

Babbitt's private denunciation of Rountree as "that dirty, fourflushing society sneak" (006-057) reveals more about Babbitt than about Rountree. The epithet erupts during a dinner-table argument with Ted, when Babbitt's defenses are down and his competitive instinct overrides his public booster ethic of "always speaking of each other only in the friendliest terms." Rountree is the exception that proves Babbitt's rule — the one realtor he allows himself to hate openly.

## Satirical Significance

Rountree is Lewis's portrait of the social stratification within Zenith's business class. The booster ideology preaches that all businessmen are brothers united in the gospel of growth, but Rountree's existence gives the lie to this creed. He is a realtor like Babbitt — and yet he is nothing like Babbitt. His speculative building is "picaresque," a word that suggests adventure and dash rather than the grinding slog of Babbitt's Glen Oriole development. His social position derives not from booster club membership but from an unearned ease that boosterism cannot manufacture.

The irony is that Rountree's social equipment — the refusal to wear badges, the independence from collective ritual — is precisely what Babbitt's late-novel rebellion attempts to acquire. Paul Riesling's aesthetic sensibility, Seneca Doane's political independence, Rountree's social remove: each represents a mode of resistance to booster conformity that Babbitt briefly glimpses but can never sustain.
