## Overview

Waterbury College is an educational institution in Oklahoma, mentioned once in the novel as the source of the Reverend John Jennison Drew's LL.D. degree. The college exists in the text solely as a credential on a résumé, its actual character — size, founding, academic reputation — entirely absent. This silence is itself Lewis's point: Waterbury College is part of the interchangeable landscape of small denominational and regional colleges whose degrees circulate as tokens of professional legitimacy without bearing the weight of genuine academic distinction.

## The Reverend Drew's Credentials

The Reverend John Jennison Drew, pastor of the Chatham Road Presbyterian Church, is introduced with his full academic pedigree: "the Reverend John Jennison Drew, M.A., D.D., LL.D. (The M.A. and the D.D. were from Elbert University, Nebraska, the LL.D. from Waterbury College, Oklahoma.)" (016-011). The parenthetical form of this disclosure is telling — the degrees are presented as supplementary authentication, the institutional names subordinate to the titles they confer. Drew is described as "eloquent, efficient, and versatile" (016-011), a tripartite endorsement that treats ministerial skill as though it were a product specification.

## Significance as an Institution

### Credential Inflation in the Professional Class

Lewis uses Waterbury College to illustrate the titular inflation that characterized the early-twentieth-century American clergy and professional class. Drew carries three degrees — M.A., D.D., and LL.D. — from two different institutions, neither of which the reader has heard of before or will hear of again. The degrees serve a purely ornamental function: they appear in a biographical aside to establish Drew's authority, then vanish from the narrative.

### The Geography of American Higher Education

The pairing of Elbert University (Nebraska) and Waterbury College (Oklahoma) maps a particular tier of American higher education: regional institutions in the Midwest and West that serve their communities but lack national prestige. These are not the Harvards or Yales that Zenith's boosters both envy and disparage; they are the institutions a young man from a Midwestern town attends if he seeks professional advancement without leaving his cultural world.

### Interchangeability of Institutional Identity

That Waterbury College is never described — no campus, no founding date, no notable alumni — is thematically significant. In the standardized America Lewis depicts, one small college is interchangeable with another. What matters is not the education received but the credential acquired, the string of capital letters after one's name that can be printed in the church bulletin and spoken from the pulpit.

## Comparison with Elbert University

Waterbury College occupies a lower tier than [[elbert-university]], which appears more frequently in the novel and grants two of Drew's three degrees. Elbert is the default alma mater for Babbitt's class; Waterbury fills a gap — the LL.D. that Elbert either could not or would not confer. The relationship between the two institutions maps the hierarchy of American higher education: state universities with broad constituencies, and small private colleges whose degrees round out the credential portfolios of the professional class.
