# Chatham Road Presbyterian Sunday School

The Sunday School of the Chatham Road Presbyterian Church, an institution that becomes the vehicle for George F. Babbitt's most enthusiastic application of business technique to religious life — recasting spiritual education as a "merchandizing problem" and organizing it along military lines (017-013)(017-014).

## Description

The Chatham Road Presbyterian Sunday School is the educational arm of one of Zenith's largest and wealthiest congregations. The Sunday School room itself is "highly varnished" with an overflow into the basement "decorated with varicose water-pipes and lighted by small windows high up in the oozing wall" (016-026). Its atmosphere carries "that polite stuffiness to be found only in church parlors" and the stale scent of drab lesson books — "Hetty, a Humble Heroine" and "Josephus, a Lad of Palestine" — and "high-colored text-cards which no boy wanted but no boy liked to throw away, because they were somehow sacred" (016-026).

Nothing gives Babbitt "more purification and publicity than his labors for the Sunday School" (016-010). His appointment to the Sunday School Advisory Committee by Dr. John Jennison Drew places him alongside the banker William Washington Eathorne, setting the stage for Babbitt's campaign to modernize the institution.

## Social Function

Babbitt approaches the Sunday School as a sales problem. He proposes dividing the school into "four armies, depending on age," with each participant receiving "a military rank in his own army according to how many members he brings in" (017-014). Those who fail to recruit are branded "duffers that lie down on us." His second proposal — hiring "a real paid press-agent for the Sunday School" — leads to the appointment of Kenneth Escott, a reporter on the _Advocate-Times_, who is paid for six hours a week of promotional work (017-015)(017-032).

The campaign is effective. The Sunday School "had a boom" — though it never surpassed the Central Methodist Church, which Dr. Drew scored as using "unfair, worldly, unspiritual means" (017-033). Babbitt himself is made a "Colonel" in the organization and is "plumply pleased by salutes on the street from unknown small boys" (017-035).

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's Sunday School research** — He discovers "a world of Sunday School journals" as technical and practical "as the real-estate columns or the shoe-trade magazines," learning tips on "Focusing Appeals" and "Scouting for New Members" (016-028)(016-029)(016-031)
- **The Eathorne meeting** — Babbitt and Frink visit the banker's mansion with "uneasy stillness," and Babbitt tremulously presents his militarization scheme as a "merchandizing problem" (017-007)(017-013)
- **The campaign's success** — The Sunday School booms and Babbitt receives salutes from small boys calling him "Colonel" (017-035)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Sunday School campaign to satirize the American impulse to treat every domain of life — including religious education — as a marketing challenge. Babbitt's conflation of Sunday School growth with "a merchandizing problem" (017-013) and his deployment of military ranks as membership incentives (017-014) reduce spiritual formation to salesmanship. The journals he studies promise that "the moral springs of the community's life lie deep in its Sunday Schools" (016-029) while offering the same techniques used to sell real estate. The campaign's supposed spiritual renewal is indistinguishable from a business promotion, and Babbitt measures its success not in souls but in salutes.
