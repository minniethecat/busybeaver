# Dr. Howard Littlefield

The domesticated intellectual of [[floral-heights|Floral Heights]] — a man whose Ph.D. in economics serves not to challenge the business order but to sanctify it. "His great value was as a spiritual example" (003-007): he is the living proof that education and orthodoxy can coexist, that a man with a doctorate need not be "queer" or radical, that intelligence can be put to work in the service of the status quo.

## Biography

Dr. Howard Littlefield is [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s neighbor on [[floral-heights|Floral Heights]]. He holds a doctorate in economics and is employed by the [[zenith-street-traction|Zenith Street Traction]] Company — a detail that neatly captures his function, since the traction company is one of Zenith's monopolistic utilities whose interests a genuinely critical economist might question. He is married with a daughter, [[eunice-littlefield|Eunice]], who represents the younger generation's more irreverent relationship to the conventions her father upholds. He embodies the acceptable face of intellectualism in [[zenith|Zenith]]: scholarly enough to command respect, but sufficiently orthodox to pose no threat to the business consensus.

Littlefield's career path is itself a satire in miniature. A man with a Ph.D. in economics — a discipline that might, in theory, examine the distribution of wealth, the ethics of monopoly, or the costs of industrialization — ends up working for a traction company and using his learning to justify the very system he might have been trained to critique. His daily inspection of "the grass parking between the curb and the broad cement sidewalk" (003-009) is the perfect emblem of what his intellect has been reduced to: the maintenance of appearances, the policing of the strip between public road and private property.

## Role in the Novel

Littlefield's "great value was as a spiritual example. Despite his strange learnings he was as strict a Presbyterian and as firm a Republican as George F. Babbitt" (003-007). His function is to provide intellectual cover for the business faith — he "confirmed the business men in the faith. Where they knew only by passionate instinct that their system of industry and manners was perfect, Dr. Howard Littlefield proved it to them, out of history, economics, and the confessions of reformed radicals" (003-007). He is the theologian of the religion of business, and his theology, like all successful theology, tells the powerful exactly what they want to hear.

This passage (003-007) is one of the novel's most concentrated satirical statements. The business men know their system is perfect "by passionate instinct" — that is, by faith. Littlefield's role is to supply the arguments after the conclusion has already been reached. His scholarship is not an inquiry; it is an apologia. And the detail that he draws on "the confessions of reformed radicals" is especially cruel: Littlefield's economics is the economics of the convert, the man who has seen the radical alternative and rejected it, not because his scholarship disproved it but because his career required its rejection.

## Key Scenes

- **The morning greeting** — Babbitt stops his [[automobile|car]] to greet Littlefield, who is "inspecting the grass parking between the curb and the broad cement sidewalk" (003-009). The image captures Littlefield's fusion of scholarly authority with suburban fastidiousness: the Ph.D. is deployed in the service of lawn maintenance. That Littlefield is abroad at this hour, inspecting grass rather than engaging in intellectual labor, is Lewis's quiet comment on what happens to learning in the suburbs.
- **The intellectual defense of business** — Littlefield's role in Babbitt's social circle is to provide the arguments that justify what everyone already believes, proving "out of history, economics, and the confessions of reformed radicals" (003-007) the perfection of the American business system. His arguments are never quoted directly in the novel — a telling omission. What matters is not what Littlefield says but that he says it: the doctorate is the credential, not the argument.
- **The dinner party** — At Babbitt's dinner party (008-031), Littlefield presides as the resident intellectual, offering opinions that confirm rather than challenge. His presence at the party, alongside [[chum-frink|Chum Frink]] the poet-advertiser, creates a diptych of domesticated culture: Frink the domesticated artist, Littlefield the domesticated intellectual. Together they demonstrate that Zenith can absorb any human activity — poetry, scholarship, philosophy — and turn it to the service of salesmanship.
- **Littlefield and the younger generation** — His daughter [[eunice-littlefield|Eunice]]'s irreverence and eventual marriage to [[ted-babbitt|Ted Babbitt]] suggest that Littlefield's domesticated intellectualism, like Babbitt's boosterism, is a generational product — something the young will escape not by argument but by indifference.

## Character Traits

Littlefield represents the complete domestication of intellectual life by business culture. His learning is genuine — he really does know history and economics — but it is deployed exclusively in the service of the status quo. He is the ideal intellectual for a society that distrusts intellectuals: credentialed enough to be useful, conformist enough to be safe. "Despite his strange learnings" — the word "strange" is Babbitt's, and it registers the suspicion that attends any intellectual activity in Zenith — Littlefield "was as strict a Presbyterian and as firm a Republican" as anyone (003-007). His function is to prove that education need not be dangerous.

The tragedy of Littlefield, such as it is, is that he might have been something else. A man with his training, in a different culture, might have been a genuine scholar — someone whose learning led him to question rather than to confirm. But Zenith has no use for that kind of intellectual, and Littlefield has made the accommodation that the culture demands. He is what intelligence becomes when it is measured entirely by its utility to the powerful: a credential, an ornament, a justification.

Littlefield is perhaps the novel's most devastating portrait of what happens to intelligence in a commercial civilization. He is not stupid; he is not a fraud; he is simply a man whose learning has been entirely co-opted by the system it might have criticized. His Ph.D. is real, but it functions as a credential rather than a tool of inquiry. His economics is not inquiry but apologetics. His existence proves that Zenith can absorb and neutralize any intellectual force — that a society sufficiently committed to commerce can turn even its potential critics into its most effective defenders.

The deepest cut is the detail about "the confessions of reformed radicals" (003-007). Littlefield's intellectual arsenal includes the testimony of those who once believed differently but have recanted. This is the logic of the show trial applied to economics: the radical who reforms is more valuable to the system than the lifelong conservative, because the convert's testimony carries the authority of experience. Littlefield knows what the radicals believe — he has studied it — and he has chosen the other side. His choice is presented as the verdict of scholarship, but the novel lets us see it for what it is: a career move.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]** — His neighbor and admirer, who takes "honest pride in being the neighbor of such a savant" (003-008). The word "savant" is Babbitt's — a term that elevates Littlefield into a category Babbitt can respect without understanding. Babbitt does not understand Littlefield's learning, but he understands that having a learned neighbor reflects well on the neighborhood. Littlefield is, for Babbitt, another piece of evidence that [[floral-heights|Floral Heights]] is the right kind of place to live.
- **[[verona-babbitt|Verona Babbitt]]** — Babbitt's daughter, who represents a different, less domesticated form of education. Littlefield's learning confirms; Verona's questions. The contrast is never explicit in the novel but is implicit in their shared proximity to Babbitt: one makes him proud, the other makes him uncomfortable.
- **[[eunice-littlefield|Eunice Littlefield]]** — His daughter, whose irreverence toward her father's pieties and eventual choice of [[ted-babbitt|Ted Babbitt]] are implicit rejections of her father's world. She does not argue with his ideas; she simply ignores them, which may be the more damaging response.
- **[[chum-frink|Chum Frink]]** — Fellow guest at Babbitt's dinner party; the two together form the novel's diptych of domesticated culture — the artist and the intellectual, both safely absorbed by the business order.
- **The business men** of the Boosters' Club and the Athletic Club — they value Littlefield not for what he knows but for the use they can make of it. He "confirmed the business men in the faith" (003-007) — the verb "confirmed" suggests a religious function, and Littlefield is indeed the high priest of the business religion, the man who intones the intellectual blessings on what everyone already believes.

## Critical Reception

Howard Littlefield has been read as Lewis's satire of the American academy's subservience to business interests — the credentialed intellectual who deploys his learning not to challenge but to justify. Critics have noted that Littlefield represents a distinctively American type: the expert whose expertise is always for sale, the scholar whose scholarship never disturbs a paying client. His economic determinism — "all activities whatsoever are ultimately reducible to dollars and cents" — has been cited as a prescient critique of the economization of all fields of knowledge in twentieth-century America.

> "Littlefield is the perfect intellectual for Zenith: never too interesting, never too challenging, always ready with a statistic that confirms what the businessmen already believe." — James Lundquist, *Sinclair Lewis* (1973)
