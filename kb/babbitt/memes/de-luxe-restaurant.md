# De Luxe Restaurant

A Zenith dining establishment that appears in an advertisement for a home-study public-speaking course — itself a satire within the satire — where a chance encounter between two acquaintances becomes a parable of self-improvement through the acquisition of commercial eloquence. The restaurant's name, with its French affectation, is deployed within the advertisement's rhetorical machinery to lend cosmopolitan prestige to a tale of social climbing.

## Description

The De Luxe Restaurant is known only through an anecdote recounted in an advertisement for a correspondence course in public speaking. The advertisement — one of "half a hundred advertisements of those home-study courses" (006-033) that the narrator catalogs — tells of running into an acquaintance "the other evening at the De Luxe Restaurant" (006-033). The encounter prompts the narrator to marvel at his friend's transformation from a modest clerk to a commanding speaker:

> "Him at the De Luxe!" the narrator exclaims, "And if he wasn't ordering a bang-up supper and flinging out his arms and producing a sensation!" (006-034)

The restaurant's French name — "De Luxe," literally "of luxury" — serves as shorthand for sophistication in a city where European cultural signifiers were adopted without their substance. The mere fact of dining at an establishment with a French name is presented within the advertisement as evidence of professional success.

## Social Function

The De Luxe Restaurant functions in Lewis's novel the way restaurants do throughout *Babbitt*: as stages for the performance of prosperity. Dining out, particularly at establishments with French-sounding or aspirational names, was part of Zenith's imitation of metropolitan sophistication. The restaurant's appearance solely within an advertisement — a form of commercial speech that Lewis repeatedly satirizes — doubles the ironic effect: the De Luxe is invoked not for its food or atmosphere but for the status it confers on those who dine there and the business opportunities that ostensibly arise from such encounters.

The advertisement belongs to a genre that the novel treats with particular scorn. The home-study courses promise to transform ordinary men into powerful speakers and, by extension, successful businessmen. The De Luxe Restaurant appears in this context as a stage prop in a commercial narrative: the setting where the transformed man is observed performing his new identity. The restaurant authenticates the transformation — if a man can command attention at the De Luxe, the advertisement implies, the course must have worked.

## Satirical Significance

The De Luxe Restaurant is a minor but revealing example of how Lewis uses commercial language to critique commercial values. The restaurant's name — "De Luxe," a French borrowing that promises luxury — is deployed within an advertisement that reduces a human encounter to a sales pitch. The story's logic — running into someone at an expensive restaurant proves that person's success, which in turn validates the advertised product — mirrors the circular reasoning of Zenith's booster culture, where the appearance of prosperity is taken as evidence of worth.

Lewis's satire operates on two levels simultaneously. On the surface, the advertisement is a straightforward testimonial for a public-speaking course. But the narrator's inclusion of it among a catalog of such advertisements — "half a hundred" of them — and his deadpan description of their claims as "truly philanthropic" (006-059) reveals the deeper critique. The De Luxe Restaurant, in this reading, is not a real place at all but a fictional setting within a fictional advertisement within a fictional novel — a hall of mirrors in which the emptiness of commercial rhetoric is endlessly reflected.

> "These advertisements were truly philanthropic," the narrator observes with acid irony. "They gave a man the opportunity of developing himself, of bettering his position" (006-059)

The restaurant thus embodies the circular logic of aspirational consumption: one dines at the De Luxe to demonstrate success, but success is defined in part by the ability to dine at establishments like the De Luxe. The home-study advertisement exploits this circularity, selling the dream of entry into a world whose gatekeepers are already inside, dining.
