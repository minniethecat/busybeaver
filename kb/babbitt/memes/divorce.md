# Divorce

In Zenith's culture of respectability, divorce is the unspoken terror — the failure of the domestic arrangement that undergirds the entire social order. Paul and Zilla Riesling's marriage serves as the novel's cautionary tale of what happens when the performance of respectability breaks down, and the violence that erupts when the escape valve of divorce is sealed shut by convention and cost.

## Definition in Context

Divorce in *Babbitt* is less a legal process than a social catastrophe — the visible collapse of the domestic front that Zenith's respectable citizens work so hard to maintain. Paul Riesling's miserable marriage to Zilla represents the dark alternative to Babbitt's own accommodation with Myra. Where Babbitt silently endures, Paul rebels — and the consequences are destruction. The novel suggests that the unavailability of divorce as a legitimate social option is itself a form of violence, channeling marital misery into the only outlets the culture permits: silent suffering or catastrophic rupture.

Lewis handles the theme with characteristic indirection — the word "divorce" appears sparingly, but its presence haunts every domestic scene. Babbitt's marriage to Myra is not happy; it is a truce. Paul's marriage to Zilla is not a truce; it is a war. The difference between the two marriages is not the presence or absence of misery but the management of it. Babbitt manages his misery with silence and routine; Paul cannot manage his, and it destroys him.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Paul and Zilla** — The Rieslings' marriage is the novel's central example of marital dysfunction. Zilla is described with unsparing cruelty: "a woman who had by long practice learned to nag, and who, having discovered that her husband was too gentle to beat her, had become almost professional in her talent for making his life a burden" (010-015, paraphrased). When Babbitt directly asks Paul "Why don't you divorce Zilla?" (005-030), Paul's reply reveals the trap of respectability: divorce is unthinkable not for moral reasons but because Zilla "keeps rooting for a nice expensive divorce" (005-036) — that is, she wants alimony Paul cannot afford. The economic dimension of divorce — accessible to the wealthy, ruinous for the middle class — is a detail Lewis includes with quiet precision.
- **Babbitt's vicarious experience** — Through Paul, Babbitt experiences the divorce that he himself cannot contemplate. "He had a curious sense of intimacy with Paul's troubles," Lewis notes; "they were more real to him than his own" (010-018, paraphrased). Paul's eventual catastrophe — shooting Zilla in a rage — serves as Babbitt's nightmare by proxy, the destruction he fears might be latent in his own soul.
- **The social cost** — When Paul shoots Zilla and goes to prison, he becomes a social outcast. The punishment for marital failure extends far beyond the legal system. Babbitt's attempt to defend Paul to his business associates meets with cold silence: a man who shoots his wife, whatever the provocation, is beyond the pale. The novel is unsparing in its account of what happens to those who fail the performance of domestic respectability.
- **Babbitt's own temptation** — During his rebellion, Babbitt flirts with the idea of leaving his marriage for [[tanis-judique|Tanis Judique]]. The affair is his divorce by other means — an attempt to find the emotional life his marriage denies him without paying the social cost of actual separation. But even this escape-within-convention fails him; he cannot sustain the double life, and he returns to Myra not because he loves her but because the alternative is too expensive in every sense.
- **The gender asymmetry** — Lewis registers, though he does not belabor, the different consequences of marital failure for men and women. Paul can shoot his wife; his punishment is prison. A woman who might leave an abusive husband, by contrast, would face economic ruin and social ostracism of a kind that no male character in the novel ever contemplates. The divorce that Babbitt flirts with is an option with consequences he can manage; the divorce that Zilla might seek is an existential threat.

> "Why don't you divorce Zilla?" Babbitt demanded. "Why don't you?" — Paul's silence is more eloquent than any answer. (005-030, paraphrased)

> "She keeps rooting for a nice expensive divorce." Paul's explanation reveals that divorce in Zenith is governed not by morality but by economics — it is a luxury good, available only to those who can afford it. (005-036, paraphrased)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses the Rieslings' marriage as the shadow text of Babbitt's own domestic life. Where Babbitt's marriage is a quiet truce, Paul and Zilla's is open warfare. The novel suggests that both are products of the same system — a culture that values the appearance of domestic stability over the reality of human connection. Paul's violence is not an aberration but the logical endpoint of a society that offers no legitimate escape from unhappy marriage.

The deeper satirical point is that Zenith's moral code, which officially condemns divorce, in practice makes divorce impossible for the middle class while permitting every other form of domestic dysfunction. Adultery is tolerated if discreet. Emotional cruelty is invisible. Only violence that becomes public — as Paul's does — triggers the moral machinery. The novel implies that Zenith's domestic morality is not morality at all but a system of appearances management, concerned not with whether marriages are good but whether they look good.

## Consequences and Tragedy

Paul's fate is the novel's darkest commentary on the institution of marriage in Zenith. He shoots Zilla, goes to prison, and emerges a broken man — not because he was evil but because he was trapped. "He had killed the thing he hated and the thing he loved, and now there was nothing" (024-018, paraphrased). The tragedy is not only Paul's; it is the tragedy of a society that could have offered him another way out and chose not to. Every character in the novel who maintains a marriage — Babbitt, Myra, the Littlefields, the Gunches — is, in a sense, Paul's jury: they preserve their own arrangements by condemning his.

The novel does not offer divorce as a solution — that would be too programmatic for Lewis's art. Instead, it offers the absence of divorce as a problem, a structural violence built into the architecture of Zenith's domestic life. Paul's catastrophe is the price of a system that values the appearance of marriage over the experience of it.
