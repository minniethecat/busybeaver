# Old Farm Inn

A roadhouse outside Zenith frequented by Sam Doppelbrau's set and later by Tanis Judique's "Bunch." It appears in the novel during Babbitt's period of domestic dissolution, when he abandoned his respectable routines for nightly carousing.

## Description

The Old Farm Inn was one of several roadhouses in the Zenith area that served as destinations for automobile-based entertainment. Sam Doppelbrau and his set patronized it as part of their weekend ritual of "suburban bacchanalia of alcohol, nicotine, gasoline, and kisses." Their parties typically featured "an extremely rapid motor expedition to nowhere in particular" — the Old Farm Inn being one such "nowhere" [^029-042][^029-048].

Babbitt visited the Old Farm Inn on his third visit to Doppelbrau's house. By eleven o'clock, "they all drove out to the Old Farm Inn. Babbitt sat in the back of Doppelbrau's car with Louetta Swanson." In the darkened intimacy of the car, Babbitt made advances toward Louetta, who "dropped her head on his shoulder, told him what a nagger Eddie was, and accepted Babbitt as a decent and well-trained libertine" [^029-048].

## Social Function

The Old Farm Inn represented the geography of Babbitt's rebellion against domesticity. Roadhouses like it existed at the civic margin — far enough from Zenith to escape neighborhood surveillance, but close enough for a quick drive. They functioned as zones of moral exception, where Prohibition was openly violated and marital vows suspended. For Babbitt, who had once "timorously tried to make love to [Louetta]" but now "did not try; he merely made love," the Inn marked his transformation from inhibited businessman to practicing libertine [^029-048].

## Key Members

- **[[Sam-Doppelbrau]]** — a "sporting neighbor" who introduced Babbitt to the roadhouse circuit and the Doppelbraus' culture of weekend partying
- **[[Louetta-Swanson]]** — Eddie Swanson's wife, who became Babbitt's companion in the back seat on the drive to the Old Farm Inn

## Key Scenes

- **The Drive to the Old Farm Inn** — After an evening of cocktails at Doppelbrau's, the party drove out to the Old Farm Inn. In the car's back seat, Babbitt made love to Louetta Swanson, crossing a line between flirtation and infidelity [^029-048].

## Satirical Significance

The name "Old Farm Inn" is ironic in the same vein as Bright Blossom Inn — it conjures rustic simplicity, but functions as a site of decidedly un-pastoral indulgence. Lewis uses these roadhouses to map the suburban geography of vice: respectable by day, the same businessmen who attend church and Rotary Club by night drive out to the Old Farm Inn to drink bootleg gin and pursue extramarital affairs. The roadhouse is the spatial correlative of Babbitt's divided self.
