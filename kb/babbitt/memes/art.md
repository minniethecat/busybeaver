# Art and Culture

In Zenith, art is what happens when business stops — a realm of foreign poetry, effeminate aesthetics, and the dangerous proposition that a life might be measured by something other than its bank balance. Lews portrays a civilization that is at once contemptuous of art and secretly intimidated by it, producing its own commercial simulacrum in the figure of Chum Frink.

## Definition in Context

Art in *Babbitt* appears primarily as a problem: something the business class cannot entirely ignore but cannot comfortably assimilate. Babbitt carries "small volumes of poetry in a foreign language" in his pocket (006-006) — not because he reads them but because possessing them signals a depth his business life denies him. The novel's treatment of art is organized around the gulf between genuine artistic experience and its commercial imitation, a gulf personified by T. Cholmondeley "Chum" Frink, the advertising-agent who is also Zenith's official poet.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Chum Frink, the commercial poet** — Frink writes verses for newspaper syndication, "peddling Culture as an article of commerce" (008-031). He is art domesticated, made safe for business-class consumption — a poet whose genius never threatens the social order because it is indistinguishable from advertising copy.
- **Poetry as secret aspiration** — Babbitt's pocket-sized poetry volumes (006-006) and his suggestion that real-estate ads could use "something in poetry" (004-004) reveal a genuine, if stunted, hunger for aesthetic experience. His tragedy is that he can only imagine art as another tool of salesmanship.
- **"The great art" of the Boosters' button** — The novel's most devastating aesthetic judgment comes in its description of the Boosters' Club button: "With the conciseness of great art the button displayed two words: 'Boosters — Pep!'" (001-042). The phrase is lethal — this, Lewis implies, is what counts as art in Zenith: a slogan on a lapel.
- **Standardization versus beauty** — "It has standardized all the beauty out of life" (007-048) is Paul Riesling's indictment of America, and it places art and standardization in direct opposition. Art is what resists standardization, which is why Zenith cannot truly tolerate it.
- **The missing symphony** — "They haven't any feeling of poetry and beauty" (028-017) — the lament of Babbitt's rebellion, the recognition that something essential is absent from Zenith life.

## Key Passages

> With the conciseness of great art the button displayed two words: "Boosters — Pep!" It made Babbitt feel loyal and important. (001-042)

> He had enormous and poetic admiration, though very little understanding, for the verses of T. Cholmondeley Frink. (006-003, paraphrased)

> "They haven't any feeling of poetry and beauty. That's a fact, they haven't." (028-017, paraphrased)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses art as a litmus test for the spiritual condition of his civilization. Zenith produces plenty of commerce but no genuine art; it has poets but they are also advertising-agents; it has literary pretensions but they are satisfied by syndicated newspaper verse. The satire is not that Zenith despises art — it is that Zenith has produced a version of art that is indistinguishable from commerce, a culture industry that satisfies the hunger for beauty without ever disrupting the business of making money. Chum Frink is the novel's most mordant creation: a real poet would be a threat; a fake poet is an asset.
