# Shotwell's Stationery Shop

A stationery shop occupying one of the ground-floor commercial storefronts in the Reeves Building, listed alongside a Western Union Telegraph Office, the Blue Delft Candy Shop, and the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company as a tenant on the building's Third Street side (003-035).

## Description

Shotwell's Stationery Shop is one of the businesses that line the street frontage of the Reeves Building, the fourteen-story "fireproof as a rock and efficient as a typewriter" office tower where Babbitt maintains his real-estate office (003-034). The shop shares the Third Street commercial strip with a telegraph office, a candy shop, and Babbitt's own firm, forming the dense retail ecosystem of downtown Zenith in which Babbitt's professional life is embedded (003-035).

The shop's position alongside the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company on the building's ground floor situates it within the daily visual field of Babbitt's comings and goings. The Reeves Building's "clean, upright, unornamented lines" and "yellow pressed brick" (003-034) provide the architectural frame for this small retail enterprise.

The surrounding streetscape is also highly animated: Babbitt's daily arrival involves an elaborate ritual of parallel parking on Third Street, a "virile adventure masterfully executed," after which he deploys "a thief-proof steel wedge on the front wheel" before crossing to his office (003-033). This vehicular drama unfolds directly before the ground-floor shops, including Shotwell's, making the stationery store a bystander to the daily theater of the businessman's arrival.

## Social Function

Shotwell's Stationery Shop functions primarily as a minor element in the commercial landscape Lewis uses to establish the dense material reality of downtown Zenith. In the single sentence that lists the Reeves Building's ground-floor tenants, the stationery shop serves alongside the Western Union telegraph office as a marker of the building's function as a nexus of business communication — the telegraph office for long-distance messages, the stationery shop for the paper that fuels the office machinery above.

The enumeration of these businesses — Western Union, Blue Delft Candy, Shotwell's Stationery, Babbitt-Thompson Realty — establishes the layered commercial world through which Babbitt moves. The stationery shop is neither glamorous nor satirized; it is simply present, a working piece of the city's commercial fabric.

The shop's adjacency to the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company carries an implicit parallel: both are enterprises that supply the raw material of business — stationery for correspondence, real estate for speculation. The shop's presence in the Reeves Building list is less a portrait of the business itself than an element in the building's composition as a vertical Main Street, where the "little unknown people who inhabited the Reeves Building corridors — elevator-runners, starter, engineers, superintendent, and the doubtful-looking lame man who conducted the news and cigar stand" form a "constricted valley" community complete with its own social hierarchies (003-037).

## The Reeves Building Ecosystem

Shotwell's functions within a building that Lewis treats as a self-contained village. The ground-floor shops, including the stationery store, face outward onto Third Street, serving the public city. But behind them, the building's interior corridor constitutes what Lewis calls the inhabitants' "Main Street," with "its stone floor, severe marble ceiling, and the inner windows of the shops" (003-037). This spatial division — street-front retail versus corridor-facing businesses — maps the boundary between the commercial public realm and the building's internal social world.

The liveliest establishment within this interior ecosystem is the Reeves Building Barber Shop, which Babbitt himself does not patronize — he prefers "the glittering Pompeian Barber Shop in the Hotel Thornleigh" — and this defection causes him a twinge of guilt: "every time he passed the Reeves shop — ten times a day, a hundred times — he felt untrue to his own village" (003-037). Shotwell's, in contrast, raises no such conflicted loyalties. It belongs to the public face of the building, not the intimate village within.
