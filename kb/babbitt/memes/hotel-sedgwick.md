# Hotel Sedgwick

A hotel in the city of Monarch — Zenith's chief rival among the mid-sized cities of the state — where George Babbitt stayed during the annual state real estate convention. Monarch was the host city for the convention that year, and Babbitt "was to share a room at the Hotel Sedgwick with W. A. Rogers, that shrewd, rustic-looking Zenith dealer in real estate" (013-032). The hotel featured a "Moorish Grillroom" (013-062), a detail that Sinclair Lewis uses to satirize the era's fashion for exotic-themed commercial interiors grafted onto midwestern business establishments.

## Description

The Hotel Sedgwick was located in Monarch, which the novel describes as "Zenith's chief rival among the middle cities of the state" (013-004). It served as convention housing for the visiting real-estate men from across the region. Babbitt shared a room there with W. A. Rogers, a fellow Zenith real-estate dealer described as "shrewd, rustic-looking" (013-032). The hotel's dining facilities included a "Moorish Grillroom" (013-062) — part of the 1920s vogue for themed commercial spaces that adopted superficial exotic motifs without any connection to local cultural traditions.

The convention itself was a significant civic event for Monarch. The visiting delegates were greeted "with a welcome by the mayor of Monarch" and "the pastor of the First Christian Church" also addressed them (013-036). The Monarch Chamber of Commerce organized motor tours for the delegates (013-043), and the local press covered the proceedings — Babbitt's activities were even noted in the *Monarch Herald* (013-065). The Hotel Sedgwick, as the delegates' headquarters, was at the center of this orchestrated display of inter-city commercial fellowship.

## Social Function

The Hotel Sedgwick's primary function in the novel is as the setting for the real estate convention's social activities — a space where business rivalry and boosterish camaraderie coexist. Sharing a room with W. A. Rogers placed Babbitt in the company of a dealer who, despite his rustic appearance, was a canny businessman. The convention itself, with its speeches, banquets, and organized entertainment, was an exercise in what Lewis portrays as the secular liturgy of the business class: men from competing cities gathering to reassure one another of their shared prosperity and importance.

The hotel also served as the stage for the convention's extracurricular socializing. When a fellow delegate jokingly asked Babbitt, "just kind of stayed in Monarch and threw a party, heh?" (013-058), the question hinted at the unspoken understanding that conventions were as much about pleasure and escape from domestic routine as they were about professional development. The hotel, as a space away from home and office, enabled a temporary loosening of the restraints that governed Babbitt's behavior in Zenith.

## Key Scenes

- **Arrival and lodging** — Babbitt takes the midnight train to Monarch and shares a room at the Hotel Sedgwick with W. A. Rogers (013-019, 013-032)
- **The Moorish Grillroom** — The hotel's elaborately themed dining facility is mentioned as a social setting (013-062)
- **Convention aftermath** — Fellow delegates tease Babbitt about his behavior in Monarch, implying unspoken escapades at the hotel (013-058)

## Satirical Significance

The Hotel Sedgwick, with its "Moorish Grillroom," exemplifies Lewis's satire of 1920s American commercial culture's superficial exoticism. The adoption of Moorish architectural themes in a midwestern business hotel — unrelated to any authentic cultural tradition in Monarch — represents the boosterish impulse to appear cosmopolitan through decorative imitation. The very name "Moorish Grillroom" collapses two disparate cultural references into a single commercial gesture, neither Moorish nor particularly a grillroom in any authentic sense.

The hotel's role as convention housing also reinforces the novel's theme of businessmen as a transient tribe, constantly traveling between identical commercial spaces. The convention delegates move through a circuit of hotels, banquet halls, and chambers of commerce that are interchangeable across the mid-sized cities of the state — Monarch's Hotel Sedgwick could just as easily be Zenith's Hotel Thornleigh, and the speeches delivered in one would serve equally well in the other. Lewis uses this interchangeability to suggest the spiritual emptiness beneath the boosterish rhetoric of civic uniqueness and local pride.

> His fellow delegates, after the convention, asked Babbitt: "just kind of stayed in Monarch and threw a party, heh?" (013-058)

This knowing question from a fellow real-estate man captures the convention's dual nature: officially, it was a gathering for professional improvement and inter-city goodwill; unofficially, it was an opportunity for the kind of behavior that the businessmen would not have indulged at home. The hotel, as a liminal space, made such duality possible.
