# Hafiz Book Shop

A book shop owned by Lloyd Mallam, the poet, who was "finishing a rondeau to show how diverting was life amid the feuds of medieval Italy" when encountered in the narrative (007-063). The shop, named after the 14th-century Persian poet Hafiz, represented one of the few outposts of literary culture in a city dominated by business, real estate, and boosterism.

## Description

The Hafiz Book Shop was a small bookstore owned and operated by Lloyd Mallam, who styled himself as a poet. The shop's name—invoking Hafiz, the celebrated Persian lyric poet—suggested Mallam's aspirations toward genuine literary culture, a rarity in Zenith where cultural pretensions typically manifested in the standardized "culture" of the Women's Club lecture series or the Chautauqua circuit. The shop served as both Mallam's business and his literary retreat, where he composed poetry amid the commercial surroundings of Zenith's retail district (007-063).

## Social Function

The Hafiz Book Shop occupied an ambiguous position in Zenith society. It was a business, part of the commercial fabric of the city, yet its proprietor's poetic ambitions set it apart from the straightforward commercial enterprises that surrounded it. Mallam represented the figure of the "artist" in a city that had little use for art not tied to commerce or social display. The shop's name—classical, literary, foreign—was itself a small act of cultural resistance against the standardized modernity of Zenith, though the novel suggests that Mallam's medieval rondeaux were as disconnected from genuine lived experience as Babbitt's booster slogans.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Hafiz Book Shop to illustrate the marginalization and irrelevance of literary culture in Zenith. Mallam's rondeau about "medieval Italy" could not be further removed from the concerns of Babbitt's world—real estate, automobiles, and business deals. The shop's name, drawn from Persian poetry, is an artifact of genuine world culture in a city whose cultural references are entirely local and boosterish. Yet Lewis's satire cuts both ways: Mallam's escapism into medieval aesthetics is no more authentic than Babbitt's boosterism—both are forms of refusing to engage with the actual condition of modern American life. The book shop, like the church and the club, ultimately serves its proprietor's need for self-definition rather than any larger cultural purpose.
