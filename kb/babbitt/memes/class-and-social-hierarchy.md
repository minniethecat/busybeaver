# Class and Social Hierarchy

In *Babbitt*, social class is simultaneously omnipresent and unmentionable — a system of ranking as precise as any European aristocracy but denied by the very ideology of American democracy that sustains it. Lewis devotes some of his sharpest satire to the paradox of a supposedly classless society that has invented its own elaborate hierarchy of motors, neighborhoods, and club memberships.

## Definition in Context

Lewis defines Zenith's class system through the automobile, which serves as the city's equivalent of hereditary titles. A family's motor "indicated its social rank as precisely as the grades of the peerage determined the rank of an English family" (006-027). The system functions without any formal court of precedence — "there was no court to decide whether the second son of a Pierce Arrow limousine should go in to dinner before the first son of a Buick roadster, but of their respective social importance there was no doubt" (006-027). This is hierarchy by consumption, invisible to those inside it yet ruthlessly exact in its gradations.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The automobile as coat of arms** — Lewis's extended metaphor in Chapter VI maps Zenith's entire class structure onto automobile brands: the Packard twin-six represents the pinnacle to which Babbitt's son Ted aspires, replacing the older generation's presidential ambition with a longing for "an established position in the motored gentry" (006-027).
- **The Old Families** — Zenith possesses its own aristocracy: families who arrived before 1840. Charles McKelvey belongs to this class, "a peer in the rapidly crystallizing American aristocracy, inferior only to the haughty Old Families" (015-013). Babbitt watches this world from the outside with a mixture of resentment and desperate aspiration.
- **The dinner-party calculus** — Social striving is measured in invitations: Babbitt having "risen to that metropolitan social plane on which hosts have as many as four people at dinner without planning it for more than an evening or two" (008-003), yet failing utterly to penetrate the McKelvey circle: "For a month they watched the social columns, and waited for a return dinner-invitation" (015-039).
- **The water-cooler as status symbol** — Babbitt reassures himself that "no tenant of the Reeves Building had a more expensive one," yet "could not recapture the feeling of social superiority it had given him" (003-046) — the fragility of status maintained through objects.
- **The ruling caste of Good Fellows** — Babbitt's shoulders, voice, and humor establish him "as one of the ruling caste of Good Fellows" (004-021), a distinctly American aristocracy where hearty conformity substitutes for lineage.
- **The McKelvey dinner party** — Babbitt's disastrous attempt to enter the circle of the truly wealthy exposes the unspoken rules of Zenith's upper tier: the McKelveys "did not so much ignore him as fail to see him" (015-041). The dinner party at the Babbitts' — elaborate, anxious, and ultimately unreciprocated — dramatizes the impossibility of crossing class lines through effort alone.

(006-027)

> For a month they watched the social columns, and waited for a return dinner-invitation. (015-039)

## Satirical Function

Lewis skewers the American pretense of classlessness by revealing a hierarchy more rigid for being unspoken. The novel's satire operates through juxtaposition: the language of democratic equality ("Good Fellows," "regular guy") coexists with an obsession with social rank so precise it can be read off the hood of a car. Babbitt's tragedy is that he subscribes to both systems simultaneously — he believes in the American gospel of the self-made man while spending his life calibrating his exact position in a pecking order he claims not to recognize.

The deeper satire is that Zenith's class ladder has no top. Even the McKelveys, Babbitt discovers, are insecure — looking over their shoulders at Eastern society, which looks over its shoulder at Europe. The pursuit of status is infinite, and the novel suggests that this infinity is what keeps the entire system of consumption running: there is always one more rung to climb, one more invitation to secure.
