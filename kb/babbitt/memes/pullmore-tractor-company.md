# Pullmore Tractor Company

A Zenith manufacturing enterprise that Lewis uses to evoke the city's industrial might. The Pullmore Tractor Company factory ran "night and day on orders from every continent" (007-039), serving as a symbol of Zenith's boast that it was a world-class industrial center. The company's secretary, Caleb Nixon, was among the businessmen who "put on a long khaki coat" and joined the Good Citizens' League's campaign of intimidation (027-003).

## Description

The Pullmore Tractor Company operated a factory in Zenith. Lewis describes its production as running continuously — "night and day" — with orders flowing in from "every continent" (007-039). This global reach was part of Zenith's self-image as a city of international commercial significance. The company's secretary, Caleb Nixon, was a prominent businessman who participated in the Good Citizens' League's enforcement of political conformity (027-003). Together with companies like the Zenith Steel and Machinery Company and the Doodle and Quill Company, Pullmore represented the industrial base upon which Zenith's commercial class had built its fortunes.

## Social Function

Pullmore Tractor served as an exemplar of Zenith's industrial self-conception. The claim that its factory ran on "orders from every continent" (007-039) was a point of civic pride that bolstered the city's booster narrative. The company's inclusion among the roster of Zenith industries — "the steel and cement town which composed the factory of the Pullmore Tractor Company" (007-039) — suggests a scale of operation that is as much rhetorical as actual: the factory is invoked to demonstrate Zenith's world-historical importance rather than to describe a specific manufacturing operation.

The presence of company officials like Caleb Nixon in the Good Citizens' League (027-003) illustrated how industrial and political power were intertwined — the same men who ran manufacturing plants also enforced ideological conformity. Nixon's transformation from corporate secretary to paramilitary enforcer — donning "a long khaki coat" — dramatizes Lewis's argument that the organizational structures of business and political repression were continuous in 1920s America.

- **Good Citizens' League mobilization** — Caleb Nixon, secretary of Pullmore Tractor Company, appears in "a long khaki coat" as a member of the League's enforcement apparatus, illustrating the seamless transition from corporate management to political repression (027-003)

## Satirical Significance

Pullmore Tractor Company functions primarily as a rhetorical device within the novel's satire of boosterism. Its invocation — "night and day on orders from every continent" — exemplifies the hyperbolic self-promotion that Lewis satirizes throughout the novel. The phrase's rhythm is significant: the assertion cascades from "night and day" through "every continent" without pause, piling superlative upon superlative until the claim becomes self-parodic. Lewis describes the factory as "the steel and cement town" (007-039), blurring the distinction between the company and the city itself — a deliberate conflation that mirrors the booster rhetoric equating corporate success with civic virtue.

The fact that the company's secretary is later seen in paramilitary garb enforcing political orthodoxy (027-003) underscores Lewis's critique that industrial "progress" and authoritarian conformity were two sides of the same coin in 1920s America. Nixon's khaki coat is not merely a costume but a visualization of the latent violence beneath the boosterish surface of Zenith's business culture.
