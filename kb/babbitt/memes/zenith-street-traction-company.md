# Zenith Street Traction Company

The Zenith streetcar and transit utility, which served as a focal point for the kind of insider dealing and business cronyism that defined Zenith's commercial culture. Its purchasing agent "owned twenty-eight persons, nine places, three million dollars, and was owned by forty-two persons, eleven places, and five hundred thousand dollars" (004-028). The company's terminal deal became a key plot point, testing Babbitt's moral boundaries when he was offered "a chance to take part" (017-052).

## Description

The Street Traction Company operated Zenith's streetcar system. It employed a publicity-counsel who, Lewis notes, "could, on ten hours' notice, appear before the board of aldermen or the state legislature and prove that the street traction company was a boon to the public" (003-006). The assistant general manager, Vergil Gunch, "loved the city with something of the patriotism of a traffic policeman" (008-031). The company planned to "build car-barns in the suburb of Dorchester" (019-002), which became the basis of a speculative land deal.

## Social Function

The Traction Company exemplified the intertwining of public utility and private profit in Zenith. The proposed Dorchester car-barn expansion became the vehicle for a speculative real-estate scheme in which Babbitt was offered an inside position (017-052). The deal tested his ethical boundaries — he initially joined enthusiastically but later, during his rebellion, reflected on the "strain of the Street Traction deal and his disappointment in it" (019-023). The purchasing agent's purchase of a "five thousand dollar" automobile illustrated the personal enrichment flowing from the company's dealings (019-003).

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's work with the Traction Company** — The publicity-counsel works with Babbitt on public relations, and the assistant manager Vergil Gunch is a fellow Booster (003-006, 008-031)
- **The terminal deal** — Babbitt is offered "a chance to take part in the Street Traction Company's terminal deal" (017-052), a speculative land investment
- **The Dorchester car-barn plan** — The company plans to build car-barns in Dorchester, triggering real estate speculation (019-002)
- **Aftermath** — Babbitt later reflects on "the strain of the Street Traction deal" (019-023) as part of his broader disillusionment

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Street Traction Company to expose the mechanics of civic corruption in the progressive era. The company is a public utility in name but a vehicle for private enrichment in practice — its officials double as speculators, its expansions function as insider-trading opportunities, and its public-relations apparatus exists to manufacture consent rather than inform the public. The company embodies the central irony of Zenith boosterism: institutions that ostensibly serve the public good are systematically repurposed for private gain.
