# Catalpa Inn

A hotel in Monarch, the city hosting the annual convention of the State Association of Real Estate Boards. The Catalpa Inn was one of the venues where conventioneers socialized away from the official proceedings.

## Description

The Catalpa Inn featured an "Art Room" decorated with painted rabbits, mottoes lettered on birch bark, and waitresses dressed in Dutch caps. Its aesthetic was self-consciously quaint and "artistic," serving as a tea venue for visiting businessmen during the convention. Babbitt and W. A. Rogers were invited to tea there by the Jered Sassburgers of Pioneer on the final afternoon of the convention [^013-056].

Tea at the Catalpa Inn represented a social ritual that was "sufficiently exotic to make [Babbitt] feel important," though he attended such functions with his wife at least twice a year at home [^013-057]. The Inn's glass-covered tables and lettuce sandwiches provided a respectable veneer that contrasted sharply with the bootleg drinking that followed in the Sassburgers' hotel room [^013-057].

## Social Function

The Catalpa Inn served as a staging ground for the convention's real social purpose: drinking. After the tea, the party moved to the Sassburgers' hotel room where Miriam Sassburger mixed cocktails from a "dismal, nakedly white water-pitcher." Babbitt's decision to skip the formal last-evening program and instead extend his carousing with Rogers and other delegates began with this afternoon tea at the Catalpa Inn [^013-056][^013-058].

## Key Scenes

- **Afternoon Tea with the Sassburgers** — Babbitt and Rogers attend tea at the Catalpa Inn, where Babbitt eats "insufficient lettuce sandwiches" and flirts with Mrs. Sassburger. The Inn represents a thin veneer of middle-class propriety that quickly dissolves into a night of drinking at the hotel and Bright Blossom Inn [^013-056][^013-057].

## Satirical Significance

The Catalpa Inn embodies Lewis's satire of middle-class pretension. Its "Art Room" with painted rabbits and mottoes on birch bark presents a parody of culture-seeking among businessmen whose real interest lies in bootleg cocktails. The Inn's function is entirely nominal — the tea is a prelude to drinking, and the "artistic" setting is merely decorative, serving to legitimize socializing that would otherwise appear too openly self-indulgent.
