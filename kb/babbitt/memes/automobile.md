# Automobile

In *Babbitt*, the automobile is more than transportation: it is "poetry and tragedy, love and heroism" (003-002) — the machine that defines a man's status, expresses his masculinity, and serves as the central object of consumer desire in the religion of business.

## Definition in Context

The automobile in Zenith is the ultimate consumer good and the most visible marker of a man's place in the social hierarchy. Babbitt feels his own worth reflected in his car's cylinder count. The car is also the vehicle — literally — of Babbitt's social life: his commute, his business calls, his family outings all take place within the mobile cocoon of the automobile.

The automobile economy of Zenith extends far beyond individual ownership. Every aspect of the city's life — its real-estate development, its suburban expansion, its social rituals, its advertising industry — is shaped by the car. [[babbitt-thompson-realty|Babbitt's real-estate business]] depends on the automobile to connect suburban developments like [[floral-heights|Floral Heights]] to the commercial center. The car makes possible the spatial segregation of Zenith by class, enabling the prosperous to live at a distance from the industrial districts their wealth depends upon.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The car as identity** — For Babbitt, "his motor car was poetry and tragedy, love and heroism" (003-002), compressing the entire range of human emotion into a consumer object. This sentence, one of the most famous in the novel, is Lewis's thesis statement on consumer culture: the automobile has become the medium through which modern man experiences the emotions that earlier ages found in art, religion, and human relationship.

- **The closed car as status** — The debate between Babbitt and his family about "a closed car costs more money" (006-026) reveals how automobile choices are never just practical — they are always social calculations. A closed car signals prosperity, shields its occupants from the weather and the gaze of the less fortunate, and announces that its owner has arrived at a certain level of the social hierarchy. The Babbitt family's negotiation over the car purchase is a miniature of the class anxieties that structure their entire lives.

- **The car and masculine freedom** — Driving represents one of Babbitt's few experiences of autonomy and competence. Behind the wheel, he is in control in a way he rarely is in his social or domestic life. The car is a private kingdom where Babbitt can be the master — a compensation for the thousand daily humiliations of a life spent pleasing clients, deferring to superiors, and conforming to social expectations.

- **The car as social connector** — Every significant social encounter in the novel — business meetings, club luncheons, church services, family visits — is mediated by the automobile. The car transforms the geography of social life, making possible a dispersed network of relationships that would have been unthinkable in a walking city. Babbitt's social world is held together by gasoline.

- **The car as mechanical religion** — Just as the skyscraper is the "temple-spire of the religion of business," the automobile is its mobile altar — a machine that promises freedom while binding its worshipper more tightly to the economic system that produced it. The car requires fuel, maintenance, insurance, garage space, and regular replacement — each of these is a transaction that feeds the commercial machine the car ostensibly frees its owner from.

- **The car and class segregation** — The automobile enables Babbitt to live in [[floral-heights|Floral Heights]] and work downtown, to drive through but never stop in the working-class districts of [[South-Zenith]] and [[old-town]]. The car is an instrument of social distance as much as physical mobility, allowing the prosperous to observe poverty from behind glass without ever experiencing it. The "windshield perspective" — seeing the city without touching it — is the automobile's distinctive contribution to the social psychology of the business class.

## Key Passages

> To George F. Babbitt, as to most prosperous citizens of Zenith, his motor car was poetry and tragedy, love and heroism. (003-002)

This passage, from Chapter III, is the novel's most concentrated statement on the automobile's symbolic function. The four nouns — poetry, tragedy, love, heroism — are the vocabulary of the humanities, of art and literature and religion. By attaching them to a machine, Lewis performs in miniature the satirical operation of the entire novel: the replacement of authentic human experience with the commodities that purport to deliver it.

## Satirical Function

Lewis's treatment of the automobile is a miniature of his broader satire: the car, which should be a tool of freedom, becomes another instrument of conformity. Babbitt measures himself against his neighbors by his car; he experiences "poetry" through a machine; he understands "heroism" as a matter of horsepower. The automobile, like the religion of business itself, promises transcendence and delivers only more of the same.

The automobile is also the novel's central metaphor for the paradox of consumer freedom. Every advertisement promises that a new car will bring liberty, adventure, self-expression. But the actual experience of car ownership is a cycle of payments, repairs, trade-ins, and status anxiety — a system of obligations as binding as any social convention. Babbitt's car "frees" him to commute to an office he hates, to attend social functions he dreads, to maintain a lifestyle he cannot afford. The freedom of the open road leads always back to the same garage on the same street in the same suburb.
