# The Boosters' Club Banquet

The annual banquet of the Zenith Boosters' Club — one of the novel's key set pieces for the performance of booster ideology and masculine fellowship. The banquet, with its speeches, toasts, songs, and collective rituals, is the secular equivalent of a religious feast: a gathering at which the "religion of business" is publicly affirmed and individually renewed.

## Summary

The banquet takes place in a hotel ballroom, decorated with bunting in the club's colors and banners bearing booster slogans. Tables are arranged in the banquet style, seating eight or ten men each, with the head table on a raised dais for the club's officers and honored guests.

The evening follows a fixed liturgical order: the call to order, the invocation (delivered by a clergyman who understands that brevity is a professional obligation), the meal itself (heavy, abundant, proof of prosperity made edible), the toasts (ascending in importance from junior members to the evening's keynote speaker), the speeches (lengthy performances of booster rhetoric), the songs (collective singing of booster anthems, often with comic verses composed by Chum Frink for the occasion), and the benediction (a final toast to Zenith and to business, the twin deities of the club's theology).

Lewis structures the banquet so that its religious parallels are unmistakable. The ballroom is the sanctuary; the head table is the altar; the speakers are the priesthood. The invocation, the hymns (songs), the sermon (keynote speech), and the benediction (final toast) map precisely onto Protestant worship — the form familiar to every man in the room. The Boosters' Club has not created a new religion; it has simply taken the old one and replaced its content.

The theological content of this secular liturgy is consistent: Zenith is the promised land, business is the path of righteousness, growth is the measure of virtue, and the "sorehead" — the man who criticizes, who doubts, who refuses to join — is the heretic. The banquet's ritual function is to reaffirm these articles of faith through collective performance, to make each man feel that his individual pursuit of profit is sanctified by membership in a larger enterprise.

## Background

The Boosters' Club is the central civic organization of Zenith's business class. Its weekly luncheons maintain the bonds of fellowship; its annual banquet elevates those bonds to ceremonial status. The banquet is where the club's ideology — growth, pep, anti-radicalism, business as moral good — is most explicitly articulated and most collectively celebrated.

The club's membership encompasses the full range of Zenith's commercial elite. Babbitt's own circle includes Vergil Gunch the coal-dealer, Sidney Finkelstein the ladies'-ready-to-wear buyer, Professor Pumphrey of the Riteway Business College, and Chum Frink the advertising poet [007-016, 007-030]. The annual banquet brings these men together in their most formal and self-congratulatory mode, each wearing the Boosters' Club button that "announced to the world, 'I am a solid business man and a loyal and attending member of the Boosters' Club'" (001-042).

## Ritual Elements

- **The setting** — A hotel ballroom decorated with bunting and booster slogans, the physical environment designed to inspire the "loyal and important" (001-042) feeling the button confers. The choice of venue — a commercial hotel, not a church, not a public hall — is itself significant: the banquet's sacred space is a space of commerce.
- **The toasts** — Ritual affirmations of booster values, delivered by the club's leadership and honored guests. Each toast follows a predictable template: a joke (Chum Frink's specialty), a sentiment (growth is good, Zenith is great, business is noble), and a call to drink. The structure is so familiar to the participants that variation would be felt as violation.
- **The speeches** — Extended performances of booster rhetoric, celebrating Zenith, denouncing "soreheads" and "knockers," and reaffirming the faith in growth. The keynote address, typically delivered by the club's president or a visiting dignitary, is the banquet's homiletic centerpiece — a sermon in which the congregation is reminded of its duties and its privileges.
- **The songs** — Collective singing, including booster anthems and comic verses, reinforcing solidarity through shared performance. Chum Frink's lyrics, with their jaunty rhymes and heavy-handed wit, transform booster ideology into something that can be sung — and therefore felt — rather than merely thought. The singing is the banquet's most genuinely communal moment, and Lewis treats it with a mixture of satire and something approaching respect: these men really do enjoy singing together, and the enjoyment is real even if the occasion is hollow.
- **The camaraderie** — The genuine fellowship that makes the banquet meaningful to its participants and the ideological enforcement that makes it effective as social control. Men who compete with each other during business hours embrace as brothers at the banquet. The warmth is not feigned — it is the warmth of shared belief, of membership in a congregation that knows itself to be both chosen and threatened.

## Consequences

The banquet reinforces the social bonds that will later become instruments of enforcement. When Babbitt begins his rebellion, it is precisely these bonds — the shared meals, the collective songs, the ritual affirmations — that make his ostracism so painful. The banquet, in Lewis's anatomy, is both community and trap, fellowship and conformity, and the novel never fully separates the two.

The banquet's legacy in the novel is the network of obligation it creates. Men who have sung together, toasted together, and heard the same sermons cannot easily break ranks. Babbitt's rebellion fails not because he is wrong — the novel is clear that his criticisms of Zenith are, in many respects, correct — but because he cannot bear the loneliness that dissent entails. The banquet has done its work: it has made community contingent on conformity, and Babbitt, like every man in the room, needs the community more than he needs the truth.

## Satirical Significance

The banquet is Lewis's most complete portrait of boosterism as liturgy. Every element — the setting, the toasts, the speeches, the songs — has its religious analogue, and the collective emotional experience is genuinely powerful. Lewis's satire is at its sharpest here because he recognizes that the banquet *works* — it really does make men like Babbitt feel "loyal and important" (001-042) — even as it empties civic life of any content beyond self-congratulation.

The satire's sophistication lies in its refusal to treat the banquet as merely ridiculous. The songs are silly, the speeches are bombastic, the ideology is threadbare — and yet the men who participate are not fools. They are businessmen who have built a city, created jobs, organized a community. The banquet gives them something they genuinely need: a sense that their lives have meaning beyond the balance sheet. That this meaning is manufactured, that the liturgy is a substitute for genuine civic or spiritual engagement, does not make the need any less real.

Lewis's critique is thus double-edged. He satirizes the banquet's ideological content — the boosterism, the anti-intellectualism, the conflation of prosperity with virtue — while acknowledging the human hunger that the banquet satisfies. The tragedy of the Boosters' Club is not that its members are hypocrites but that they have been given nothing better: a culture that values only success offers them only the rituals of success, and they take what they are given.
