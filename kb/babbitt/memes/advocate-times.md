# Advocate-Times

The _Advocate-Times_ is Zenith's leading morning newspaper, owned by Rutherford Snow (032-013). It serves as both a source of information and a marker of social status in Zenith: its headlines provide Babbitt with "the exhilarating drug" of engagement with world events (002-017), while its society pages document the comings and goings of Zenith's upper crust. The paper's physical presence — the thump of its rolled-up edition against the front door — punctuates the morning ritual of Zenith's solid citizens (001-011).

## Description

The _Advocate-Times_ building occupies a prominent place in downtown Zenith, its bulletin-board a gathering point for citizens tracking the day's developments. Babbitt himself "compromised on watching the _Advocate-Times_ bulletin-board" when following breaking news, standing among the sidewalk crowd (012-006). The bulletin-board functions as a public square of sorts — an analog predecessor to the news ticker, where Zenith's businessmen could absorb the headlines without the inconvenience of purchasing a paper.

The newspaper's editorial scope encompasses both local boosterism and national affairs. Babbitt reads the paper's political coverage to form his opinions — "I see by the _Advocate_ that the Presbyterian General Assembly has voted against consolidation" (008-011) — treating the paper's editorial line as indistinguishable from his own. The _Advocate-Times_ also covers civic lectures and cultural events, reporting on Babbitt's own Annual Address to the Real Estate Board "with unstinted admiration" (014-011) and documenting lectures by visiting dignitaries (014-038).

The Sunday edition features a gravure section capable of reproducing photographs — a mark of technical sophistication for a Midwestern newspaper of the era. It is in this gravure section that Babbitt's photograph appears following his real estate address, a moment of personal triumph that he savors as validation of his civic standing (014-003). The Sunday paper represents the _Advocate-Times_ at its most expansive, offering its readers not merely news but a curated image of their own importance.

The _Evening Advocate_ serves as the afternoon companion to the morning _Advocate-Times_. It carries comic strips that constitute Babbitt's favorite reading material, offering him a daily dose of "humor" that requires no intellectual engagement (006-031). The evening edition also runs editorials, including those penned by Dr. John Jennison Drew on topics such as "The Dollars and Sense Value of Christianity" (016-011), blending commercial pragmatism with religious sentiment in a manner characteristic of Zenith's public discourse.

The _Advocate-Times_ sustains Zenith's commercial culture through its business columns. Mrs. Babbitt reads the "Real Estate and Building column of the _Advocate-Times_" with an eye for property transactions (002-029), and Babbitt himself "read the statement in the _Advocate-Times_" when following business developments (004-023). The paper thus serves dual functions in the domestic sphere: it feeds Babbitt's sense of engagement with a wider world while simultaneously providing the practical business intelligence upon which his livelihood depends.

## Social Function

The _Advocate-Times_ functions as an instrument of social cohesion in Zenith, reinforcing the values and hierarchies of the business class. Its society pages, presided over by Miss Elnora Pearl Bates, define who belongs to Zenith's elite — and by omission, who does not. Miss Bates, the "popular society editor of the _Advocate-Times_," wields considerable soft power through her column, her prose reaching its "highest larknote" when chronicling the parties and travels of Zenith's prominent families. When the McKelveys give "a Singhalese dinner," Miss Bates rises to the occasion (015-040), her coverage conferring a kind of ontological validation — the dinner exists socially because the _Advocate-Times_ says it did (002-029).

The newspaper's coverage of Babbitt illustrates its dual role as mirror and amplifier of bourgeois aspiration. When Babbitt gives his Annual Address, the paper reports it "with unstinted admiration" (014-011); when his photograph appears in the Sunday gravure section, the visual recognition confirms his arrival among Zenith's worthies (014-003). Even when the paper covers a lecture by a visiting speaker, it does so with such thoroughness that Vergil Gunch tells Babbitt, "You're getting to be one of the classiest spellbinders in town" (014-038) — attributing the speaker's prestige to Babbitt by association. The _Advocate-Times_ does not simply report on Zenith society — it certifies membership in it.

At the same time, the newspaper serves as a conduit for the "exhilarating drug" of engagement with a world beyond Zenith's city limits (002-017). Babbitt consumes its headlines as a form of intellectual stimulant, absorbing a simplified version of national and international events that requires no action beyond nodding agreement. The paper thus enables a form of pseudo-engagement — the feeling of being informed without the burden of genuine thought.

The _Advocate-Times_ is embedded in the same network of influence that governs Zenith's civic life. Its owner, Colonel Rutherford Snow, sits alongside Charles McKelvey the contractor and Dr. Dilling the surgeon in the pantheon of Zenith's most formidable citizens — the men whose disapproval Babbitt most fears during his rebellion (032-013). The newspaper's willingness to lend its reporters to the Sunday School publicity campaign demonstrates the porous boundary between press and established institutions [017-019, 017-032].

During the novel's climactic labor crisis, the _Advocate-Times_ and _Evening Advocate_ distinguish themselves as the only Zenith newspapers that do *not* attribute a violent direct-action event to the American League (034-008). This restraint — whether principled or calculated — sets the Snow-owned papers apart from the rest of Zenith's press and hints at the _Advocate-Times_'s relative independence, or at least its more sophisticated understanding of its own credibility.

Kenneth Escott's career trajectory illustrates the newspaper's role as a mechanism of social integration. Beginning as a reporter (017-019), Escott is lent to the Sunday School as press-agent (017-032), taken to lunch at the Athletic Club (017-036), and eventually marries Verona Babbitt (034-019) — a sequence that traces an arc from outsider journalist to fully incorporated member of the Zenith establishment, with the _Advocate-Times_ as the vehicle of his ascent.

## Editorial Position and Key Coverage

The _Advocate-Times_ maintains a consistent editorial posture of conservative boosterism — supporting business interests, defending the established social order, and treating any deviation from orthodox opinion as suspect. The paper covered the Seneca Doane mayoral campaign with open hostility, aligning with the business establishment's view that Doane's labor sympathies threatened Zenith's prosperity. Babbitt himself absorbed the paper's anti-Doane position as his own, though he could not have articulated a single substantive policy disagreement.

The paper's political coverage reveals its function as an ideological filter. When the Presbyterian General Assembly votes on consolidation, Babbitt quotes the paper's account verbatim (008-011), treating the _Advocate-Times_'s framing as objective fact. When the paper covers labor unrest, its headlines and editorials frame strikers as threats to public order rather than workers with legitimate grievances — a perspective Babbitt internalizes without examination. The _Advocate-Times_ does not tell its readers what to think so much as it tells them what there is to think about, and within what emotional register.

The paper's real-estate and building column exemplifies the commercial symbiosis between press and business. Mrs. Babbitt consults it for property intelligence (002-029); Babbitt uses it to monitor competitors. The column serves as a kind of institutionalized rumor mill — a marketplace of information in which the paper functions as both chronicler and participant. The boundary between reporting on Zenith's development and promoting it is, by design, invisible.

The _Advocate-Times_ also covered the sensational trial of Paul Riesling, though the novel leaves the specifics of its coverage largely offstage. What matters for Lewis's purposes is not what the paper reported but that it reported — that Paul's private tragedy became public property, fed through the machinery of Zenith's press into the breakfast tables of solid citizens who would read about it between sips of coffee and then turn, untroubled, to the comic strips.

## Key Members

- **Colonel Rutherford Snow** — Owner of the _Advocate-Times_ (032-013), one of Zenith's most formidable citizens. A white-bearded patriarch whose newspaper functions as the city's conservative conscience
- **Miss Elnora Pearl Bates** — Society editor [002-029, 015-040], whose column defines Zenith's social hierarchy and whose prose is a stylistic achievement in the genre of flattery-as-journalism
- **Kenneth Escott** — Reporter (017-019) lent to the Sunday School as press-agent (017-032), later marries Verona Babbitt (034-019). His career arc from journalist to family insider traces the absorption of the press into the establishment it purports to cover
- **Managing Editor** (anonymous) — Approves Escott's loan to the Sunday School (013-014), demonstrating the institutional permeability between newspaper and church

## Key Scenes

- **The Morning Ritual** — Babbitt reads the _Advocate-Times_ as part of his daily routine. The newspaper's arrival punctuates his morning — "whistling, and the rolled-up _Advocate_ thumped the front door" (001-011). He consumes its headlines as "the exhilarating drug of the _Advocate-Times_ headlines" before leaving for the office (002-017). The ritual is as fixed and unexamined as his breakfast.
- **The Bulletin-Board** — Outside the _Advocate-Times_ building, a bulletin-board displays the latest headlines. Babbitt "compromised on watching the _Advocate-Times_ bulletin-board" when curious about unfolding events (012-006). The scene captures the communal, almost theatrical nature of news consumption in Zenith — citizens gather on the sidewalk, reading together but not conversing, united by shared attention to the headlines.
- **The Press-Agent Appointment** — Kenneth Escott, a reporter on the _Advocate-Times_ (017-019), is appointed press-agent for the Sunday School's publicity campaign. Chum Frink suggests Escott for the role, and the managing editor approves the loan (013-014). Escott subsequently writes a profile of Dr. John Jennison Drew, though he admits he "will have to wait till the managing editor is out of town, and then blackjack the copy kid" to get it into the paper (017-043). The arrangement exemplifies the porous boundary between press and pulpit in Zenith — a reporter can simultaneously serve as publicity agent for a church, with only a joking acknowledgment of any conflict.
- **Babbitt in the Gravure Section** — The Sunday _Advocate-Times_ gravure section publishes Babbitt's photograph alongside coverage of his Annual Address, with the caption identifying him among "a dozen other business men" as "one of the leaders" in Zenith's civic life [014-003, 014-011]. For Babbitt, this visual recognition represents the tangible evidence of his standing — he has been seen, photographed, and certified by Zenith's paper of record.
- **The McKelvey Singhalese Dinner** — Miss Elnora Pearl Bates covers the McKelveys' Singhalese dinner in the _Advocate-Times_ society pages, rising "to her highest larknote" in chronicling the affair (015-040). The event represents the apex of Zenith's social calendar, and its documentation in the _Advocate-Times_ is essential to its reality — without Miss Bates's column, the dinner would be merely a private gathering; with it, it becomes a public ratification of the McKelveys' social supremacy.
- **Escott and Verona's Courtship** — Several scenes in the novel trace the courtship of Kenneth Escott and Verona Babbitt through the lens of the _Advocate-Times_. Escott's admiration for Verona develops alongside his work for the paper (018-003); the couple's "long inquiries into epistemology" (018-039) occur under the Babbitt roof; and the household gradually accepts their engagement [026-020, 029-015]. Their eventual marriage (034-019) represents the incorporation of the journalist into the Zenith establishment — the newspaperman who once needed to "blackjack" his own copy kid marries the boss's daughter.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the _Advocate-Times_ to satirize the conflation of journalism with boosterism in middle-American civic life. The paper's coverage of Babbitt's speech "with unstinted admiration" (014-011) represents not objective reporting but promotional cheerleading — the newspaper serves as Zenith's public-relations arm rather than its critical conscience. The revolving door between the _Advocate-Times_ newsroom and the Sunday School publicity campaign (017-032) further blurs the line between journalism and advocacy.

The newspaper's name itself bears scrutiny. "Advocate-Times" — a paper that both advocates (for Zenith, for business, for the established order) and times (marks the rhythm of the commercial day) — captures the dual function Lewis exposes: the press as both promoter and timekeeper of bourgeois life. Babbitt does not read the news; he absorbs it as ritual, as drug, as validation.

The Escott-Verona marriage subplot serves as a structural satire: a reporter enters the Babbitt household as a press-agent, courts the daughter, and becomes a son-in-law — the press is, quite literally, domesticated. What begins as a professional arrangement between the newspaper and the Sunday School ends as a family merger, suggesting that in Zenith, all institutions ultimately converge into a single, self-perpetuating establishment.

The _Advocate-Times_'s restraint during the labor crisis (034-008) adds a final, ambiguous note to Lewis's portrait: even a booster newspaper, it turns out, has limits. Whether the Snow papers' refusal to join the anti-labor smear campaign reflects journalistic principle or merely a more sophisticated calculation, it demonstrates that the machinery of booster journalism is not monolithic — there are, even within it, degrees of complicity.
