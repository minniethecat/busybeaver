# Biddlemeier's Inn

A restaurant on the outskirts of Zenith where George Babbitt takes Tanis Judique for dinner — a setting chosen for its discretion, which becomes the stage for Babbitt's failed attempt at romantic intimacy.

## Description

Biddlemeier's Inn is located "on the outskirts of the city" (024-029), distant enough from Zenith's social center to offer the anonymity that Babbitt requires. The inn serves as the destination for Babbitt's first deliberate, planned outing with Tanis Judique: "lest some one see them and not understand, he would take her to Biddlemeier's Inn" (024-029). The phrase "lest some one see them and not understand" reveals Babbitt's consciousness of the social risk — the inn's distance is a function of shame, the geography of a man who wants to transgress without being seen transgressing.

The inn, however, cannot deliver the romantic atmosphere Babbitt intends. "At Biddlemeier's Inn they were unable to get anything to drink" (024-032), a Prohibition-era detail that strips the evening of its intended seductive quality. With alcohol unavailable, Babbitt is forced to rely on conversation, and "all the way out to Biddlemeier's Inn he tried to talk as an old friend, but he could not pierce the wall of her words" (024-032). Tanis, rather than responding to his overtures, narrates "with interminable indignation her retorts to 'that fresh head-barber'" (024-032) — the romantic dinner becomes a monologue about workplace grievances.

The drive itself — "all the way out" — extends the evening's frustration across time and distance. Babbitt's journey to the outskirts is also a journey into the limits of his own romantic imagination: he can conceive of escape as a change of venue, but not as a transformation of self. The inn's failure to provide alcohol is the novel's quiet joke at Prohibition's expense, but it is also a structural necessity: without drink, Babbitt's fantasy has no scaffolding.

## Social Function

Biddlemeier's Inn functions as a liminal space — physically on Zenith's outskirts, socially outside the surveillance of Babbitt's normal circles. Its distance from the city center creates the illusion of escape, but the inn's failure to provide alcohol (the Prohibition constraint) and Tanis's determined superficiality ensure that the "escape" remains geographic rather than existential. The inn is far enough from Zenith to promise freedom but close enough that its reality — no drinks, trivial conversation — mirrors the city's constraints.

The inn belongs to a network of marginal venues in the Babbitt-Tanis affair: the movies they attend, the walks they take, the tea at her flat. Each setting is chosen for its discretion, and each fails to deliver the transcendence Babbitt seeks. Biddlemeier's is the most explicit of these failures because it is the most deliberate — Babbitt has planned this evening, driven out of his way, and invested it with romantic expectation. The gap between intention and outcome is the comic architecture of the entire affair.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt and Tanis's dinner** — Babbitt drives Tanis to Biddlemeier's Inn for an evening he hopes will advance their relationship, but the lack of alcohol and Tanis's "interminable indignation" about her barber (024-032) defeat his romantic intentions. The scene is a miniature of the entire affair: Babbitt's fantasy of escape collides with the banality of the person he has chosen to escape with

## Satirical Significance

Biddlemeier's Inn is a minor but telling setting that illustrates the gap between Babbitt's romantic aspirations and his actual circumstances. He selects a restaurant on the city's outskirts, hoping geography will create the conditions for intimacy; instead, Prohibition and Tanis's resolute banality conspire against him. The inn — which should be a site of escape — becomes yet another venue where Babbitt's desires are frustrated by the constraints of his world. The detail that they "were unable to get anything to drink" (024-032) is a quiet joke at the expense of a man whose fantasy of seduction requires alcohol to function.

The scene also dramatizes the novel's recurrent theme that Babbitt's rebellions are fundamentally geographic rather than spiritual. He drives to the outskirts, he joins the "Bunch," he vacations in Maine — each escape is a change of address, not a change of character. Biddlemeier's Inn, with its promise of distance and its delivery of disappointment, is the architecture of Babbitt's romantic life in miniature.
