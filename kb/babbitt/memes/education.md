# Education

The tense relationship between book-learning and business sense in Zenith, where a college degree is a mark of status but too much learning — as embodied by Babbitt's daughter Verona and the scholar Howard Littlefield — threatens the anti-intellectual consensus of the business class.

## Definition in Context

Education in *Babbitt* occupies an uneasy place: it is valued as a credential but distrusted as a force that might undermine the certainties of business culture. Babbitt himself attended a state university, and his children — Verona at Bryn Mawr, Ted at college — represent the next generation's more complicated relationship with learning. The "scholar" Howard Littlefield serves as "theological" defender of business orthodoxy, proving "out of history, economics, and the confessions of reformed radicals" (003-007) what Babbitt knows by instinct.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Howard Littlefield as domesticated intellectual** — The neighbor who "confirmed the business men in the faith" by proving their system "out of history, economics, and the confessions of reformed radicals" (003-007) represents the acceptable face of learning: scholarship in service of the status quo.
- **Verona's Bryn Mawr education** — Verona, "just out of Bryn Mawr, given to solicitudes about duty and sex and God" (002-010), embodies the gap between academic education and practical business life. Her learning makes her strange to her father rather than wise.
- **Ted's college career** — Ted represents a new relationship to education, one in which college is a social experience and athletic opportunity rather than a path to business respectability.
- **Anti-intellectual consensus** — Babbitt and his peers share an unspoken agreement that "highbrow" pursuits are suspect. Poetry, ideas, and critical thinking are tolerated only when they serve business ends.

## Key Passages

> Despite his strange learnings he was as strict a Presbyterian and as firm a Republican as George F. Babbitt. He confirmed the business men in the faith. Where they knew only by passionate instinct that their system of industry and manners was perfect, Dr. Howard Littlefield proved it to them, out of history, economics, and the confessions of reformed radicals. (003-007)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses education to expose the anti-intellectualism at the heart of American business culture. Littlefield is the perfect intellectual for Zenith because his vast learning serves only to justify what Babbitt already believes. The novel suggests that in a society where business is religion, education can only be tolerated if it functions as apologetics — never as critique.
