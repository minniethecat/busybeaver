# East Side High School

The public high school attended by [[ted-babbitt|Ted Babbitt]], reflecting the priorities of Zenith's educational system and middle-class family life — where manual training and basketball count for more than Latin and English.

## Description

East Side High School was Zenith's "great" public high school (002-015). Ted Babbitt attended as a junior and later a senior. The adjective "great" is applied by the narrator with characteristic Lewis irony — the school's greatness is asserted rather than demonstrated, much like Zenith's own booster claims.

> Ted Babbitt, junior in the great East Side High School, had been making "nut conversations about what-nots and so-on-and-so-forths" to a girl named Eunice Littlefield. (002-015)

## Ted Babbitt's Education

### Academic Record

Ted's transcript encapsulates the school's inverted priorities:

> With conditions in Latin and English but with a triumphant record in manual training, basket-ball, and the organization of dances, Ted was struggling through his Senior year in the East Side High School. (018-005)

The juxtaposition of "conditions" (failing grades) in academic subjects against a "triumphant record" in practical and social activities is presented without explicit comment, but the satire is unmistakable. The word "struggling" is especially pointed — Ted's struggle is not with poverty, disability, or any external obstacle, but with the academic side of an education he does not value.

### The School as Social Training Ground

The school functions primarily as a social arena. Ted negotiates with his sister Verona for the family car to drive "couple o' girls in my class" to "the rehearsal of the school chorus," deploying the argument that "a gentleman's got to keep his social engagements" (002-018). The school chorus rehearsal is a pretext for dating; the car is the real currency of social status.

> "A gentleman's got to keep his social engagements. In high school!" (002-018)

Class identity expresses itself through Ted's judgments: he dismisses intellectual conversation as "nut conversations about what-nots and so-on-and-so-forths" (002-015) and rebukes Verona with the mechanical expertise of his gender and generation — "you don't know a generator from a differential" (002-016).

### Post-Graduation Plans

Ted's relationship to the school is defined by his rejection of its ostensible purpose: "He repeated to his tut-tutting father that he did not wish to go to college or law-school" (018-005). Babbitt is "equally disturbed by this 'shiftlessness' and by Ted's relations with Eunice Littlefield, next door" (018-005). The school has prepared Ted for Zenith life — motor-mad, dance-organized, academically indifferent — but not for the conventional path of college and profession that his father expects. Babbitt himself had urged Ted to drop out of high school entirely, telling him "a lot of boys that are making good don't amount to a damn in school" (006-033), only to reverse himself when faced with the reality of Ted actually wanting to leave.

## Social Function

The school's emphasis on manual training and athletics over academic subjects mirrors the Babbitt family's own values. George Babbitt takes pride in Ted's practical achievements while showing little concern for his academic struggles. The school thus functions as an extension of Zenith's booster culture — preparing students not for intellectual life but for social and commercial participation. It produces the next generation of Zenith citizens: "motor-mad" like Ted, "movie-mad" like [[Eunice-Littlefield]] (018-009).

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses East Side High School to satirize American education's subordination to practical and social ends. Ted's "triumphant record" in non-academic pursuits at the expense of Latin and English is presented without authorial comment, letting the reader recognize the inverted priorities. The school is the institutional counterpart to the Babbitt family: both systems value the social and the practical over the intellectual, and both produce young adults equipped for Zenith's commercial culture but unprepared for anything beyond it.

The irony is deepened by the fact that [[George-Babbitt]] himself is a product of the State University, which the narrator calls "that great department-store" (006-006) — suggesting that Zenith's educational institutions form a continuous pipeline from high school through college, all dedicated to producing standardized citizens rather than educated individuals.
