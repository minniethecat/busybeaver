# Duty

The word Babbitt uses to transform every reluctant compliance into a moral victory. In Zenith, duty is the vocabulary through which the self is disciplined — a language so flexible it can justify attending a church supper, prosecuting a radical, or abandoning a friend.

## Definition in Context

Duty in *Babbitt* is the bridge between individual desire and social demand. When Babbitt does what is expected of him — which is nearly always — he calls it duty. When he fails to do what he secretly wants — which is nearly always — he calls it duty too. Lewis's insight is that duty in Zenith has been hollowed out of any specific moral content and repurposed as a universal solvent for the tension between self and society. Babbitt's "duty" is whatever the Boosters' Club, the church, and the business community require of him. It is conformity with a halo.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The duty of being manly** — Babbitt "luxuriously gave up the duty of being manly" in moments of private relaxation (002-003), revealing that even masculinity is an obligation rather than an identity — something performed for others.
- **Civic duty as business** — A real-estate man's "bounden duty" to "bring out the property's good points" (004-029) collapses ethical obligation into professional practice. Duty becomes indistinguishable from salesmanship.
- **"Responsible business men"** — The phrase Babbitt uses to describe himself and his peers (005-031) elevates economic activity to moral status. To be in business is to be responsible; to be responsible is to be moral; the chain of reasoning requires no further links.
- **Duty versus desire** — When Babbitt contemplates Paul Riesling's marital discontent, he retreats to the language of duty: a man must "buckle down and do his duty, even if it does bore him" (005-034). Duty is the anesthetic for a life of quiet desperation.
- **Duty as social weapon** — The language of duty is deployed against anyone who deviates: it is a socialist's "duty" to be silenced, a labor leader's "duty" to be reasonable, Babbitt's "duty" to rejoin the Good Citizens' League.

## Key Passages

> Babbitt luxuriously gave up the duty of being manly and retreated to the sleeping-porch. (002-003, paraphrased)

> "It is at once the duty and the privilege of the real-estate man to know everything about his own city and its ideals." (004-023, paraphrased)

> If a man doesn't buckle down and do his duty, even if it does bore him sometimes, why, he's simply a weakling. (005-034, paraphrased)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses "duty" as a diagnostic tool for exposing the bad faith at the heart of Zenith morality. Duty is the word Babbitt reaches for whenever he needs to justify an action he would rather not take — and the frequency with which he reaches for it measures the distance between his public performance and his private self. The satire is not that duty is a lie but that it has been evacuated of meaning: a word so promiscuously applied that it can mean anything, and therefore means nothing except "do what is expected." In the end, Babbitt's embrace of duty is the mechanism by which his brief rebellion is terminated and his conformity restored.
