# Henry Thompson

Henry Thompson is [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]'s father-in-law and p[[art]]ner in the [[babbitt-thompson-realty|Babbitt-Thompson Realty]] Company. Though he "rarely came to [[babbitt-office|the office]]," Thompson represents the founding generation of the firm and the patriarchal authority against which [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] chafes but can never openly defy (003-042).

## Role in the Novel

Thompson serves as a representative of the older generation of [[zenith|Zenith]] businessmen — more parochial, more blunt, less susceptible to the rhetorical self-deception that [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] practices. He is the "extreme of insularity" and functions as a dead weight on [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s aspirations, reminding him that whatever his ambitions, he is ultimately answerable to the man whose daughter he married and whose firm he runs (006-007).

## Key Scenes

- **Discount dispute** — When a customer demands a discount over the phone, [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is inclined to negotiate; Thompson simply growls, "Oh, t' hell with 'em." The scene captures Thompson's unvarnished, unsentimental approach to business (006-006).
- **The definition of insularity** — Lewis identifies Thompson as "the extreme of insularity," a man whose world is bounded by his [[neighborhood|home]], his office, and his assumptions (006-007).
- **News from the strike** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] learns through Thompson that officials of a street[[automobile|car]] company had urged violence against stri[[family|kin]]g [[labor|work]]ers. Thompson serves as a conduit for the harder, less public face of business power (032-027).
- **The rebellion confrontation** — Thompson "dashes into [[babbitt-office|the office]]" to denounce [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s flirtation with liberal ideas, demanding that [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] abandon his "flippancy" and fall in line. The scene is the climax of [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s failed rebellion — it is his father-in-law, not a stranger, who delivers the ultimatum that ends his brief [[freedom|independence]] (034-028, 034-029, 034-043).

## Character Traits

Thompson is blunt, parochial, and authoritarian. He harbors none of [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s need for social approval or rhetorical self-justification; he simply commands. In his marriage to Myra, [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] has not only acquired a business but also submitted himself to a patriarchal order that Thompson personifies. The rebellion's end — Thompson "ordering" [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] to listen — is a moment of symbolic capitulation to everything Thompson represents.
