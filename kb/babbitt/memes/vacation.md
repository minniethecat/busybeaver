# Vacation

The organized escape from Zenith's pressures that promises renewal but delivers only "shame-faced contentment" (013-080) — a temporary reprieve that ultimately reinforces the very conformity it seeks to relieve.

## Definition in Context

For Babbitt, vacation is both necessity and disappointment. His annual trip to Maine with Paul Riesling represents the one sanctioned departure from Zenith routine, yet "Vacation doesn't seem to have done me a bit of good" (011-024) he laments. The vacation fails not because Maine is inadequate but because Babbitt carries Zenith with him — his expectations, his habits, his restless dissatisfaction cannot be left behind.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Maine retreat** — Babbitt's annual vacation to the Maine woods is the one ritual of escape that his culture permits. Away from business, he can be "natural" with Paul in ways impossible in Zenith.
- **Vacation's failure** — Despite three weeks in Maine, Babbitt complains: "Vacation doesn't seem to have done me a bit of good" (011-024). The problem is not the vacation but the man: Babbitt has lost the capacity for genuine leisure.
- **Paul as vacation companion** — The Maine trip works only because Paul is there. Paul represents everything the vacation is supposed to provide — authenticity, ease, freedom from performance — and the tragedy is that even this escape is temporary.
- **The return** — After the Maine trip, Babbitt's "desire for rebellion was partly satisfied. He had retrograded to a shame-faced contentment" (013-080). The vacation has functioned as a safety valve, releasing pressure without changing anything.

## Key Passages

> He was tired now in every cell. "Funny! Vacation doesn't seem to have done me a bit of good," he lamented. "Paul's frisky as a colt, but I swear, I'm crankier and nervouser than when I came up here." (011-024)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses the vacation motif to dramatize the central tragedy of Babbitt's life: he cannot escape himself. The Maine woods offer genuine beauty and the possibility of authentic connection with Paul, but Babbitt's psyche has been so thoroughly colonized by the business ethos that even in the wilderness he remains a restless, dissatisfied businessman. The vacation is a microcosm of the novel's larger question: can a person raised in the "religion of business" ever truly break free?
