# Turnverein Hall

Turnverein Hall is a meeting venue in the South Zenith district where George Babbitt delivers campaign speeches during his ill-fated 1920 run for mayor as a Republican candidate (014-005).

## Description

Turnverein Hall is located in South Zenith, the industrial and working-class district of the city. The hall occupies the upper floor of a building whose ground floor houses a delicatessen shop. The setting is distinctly unglamorous: "The hall was over a delicatessen shop, in a street banging with trolleys and smelling of onions and gasoline and fried fish" (014-005). The "Turnverein" name — German for "gymnastics club" — reflects the heritage of German-American immigrant institutions that dotted Midwestern cities like Zenith in the early twentieth century.

The hall itself had broad stairs leading up to it, where working-class attendees — described as "Men in black sateen shirts, their faces new-washed but with a hint of grime under their eyes" — loitered before meetings (014-007). The hall was large enough to accommodate a substantial audience: "The hall was full" on the night of Babbitt's speech (014-007).

## Social Function

Turnverein Hall served as a venue for ward-level political organizing. The Republican-Democratic Central Committee assigned Babbitt to speak in South Zenith and the Seventh Ward after his convention paper had established his local reputation for oratory (014-003). The choice of venue — a working-class ethnic hall rather than a downtown hotel ballroom or the Athletic Club — reflects the ward-by-ward retail politics of the era, where candidates were expected to appear in neighborhood venues to address small audiences of the district's residents.

Babbitt visited the hall with his family in tow: "Crowded in his car, they came driving up to Turnverein Hall, South Zenith — Babbitt, his wife, Verona, Ted, and Paul and Zilla Riesling" (014-005). The presence of his family at this working-class venue underscores the campaign's small-scale, amateur character.

## Key Scenes

- **Campaign speech for Babbitt's mayoral race** — Babbitt delivers a political address to a full house at Turnverein Hall, addressing the audience as "ladies and gentlemen of the Sixteenth Ward" before launching into a characteristically boosterish oration praising "the home for manly men and womanly women and bright kids" [014-007, 014-025]. He departs "modestly, busily" to "delicious applause" and hurries to his third audience of the evening (014-008).

## Satirical Significance

The Turnverein represents the gap between Babbitt's self-image as a public figure and the modest reality of his political operation. He campaigns not from a podium in a great auditorium but from above a delicatessen in a neighborhood that smells of "onions and gasoline and fried fish" (014-005). The venue's German name and working-class clientele are at odds with Babbitt's boosterish rhetoric about Zenith as a city of "Regular Guys" (014-025) — he is a middle-class realtor playing at tribune of the working man, and the setting quietly exposes the performance.
