## Overview

The _Zenith Advocate-Times_ is the city's principal morning newspaper, a broadsheet that both reflects and reinforces the values of the Zenith business class. Owned by the "ferocious" Colonel Rutherford Snow (032-013), it serves as the city's unofficial civic cheerleader — celebrating its commercial achievements, reporting its social events, and providing the daily reading ritual that frames Babbitt's own relationship to his city (002-017).

## Key Personnel

### Colonel Rutherford Snow

The _Advocate-Times_ is owned by the "white-bearded Colonel Rutherford Snow" (032-013). Described as "hoar and mighty" and "ferocious," the Colonel wields influence over Zenith's public discourse. When Sheldon Smeeth of the Y.M.C.A. suggests he attend a prayer meeting, the Colonel's blunt refusal — "What the hell would I do that for?" (034-007) — epitomizes the paper's pragmatic, unsentimental business-first orientation.

### Ira Runyon, Managing Editor

Old Ira Runyon serves as the managing editor, a figure of weary professional courtesy. When Babbitt presents him with a bound copy of his typed speech, Runyon "said yes, indeed yes, he was very glad to have it, and he certainly would read it all through — as soon as he could find time" (013-014). The response is a masterclass in polite dismissal disguised as editorial interest.

### Miss Elnora Pearl Bates, Society Editor

The society column is written by Miss Elnora Pearl Bates, described as "the popular society editor of the _Advocate-Times_" (002-029). Her coverage of Zenith's elite — including a Singhalese dinner given by the McKelveys during which she "rose to her highest larknote" (015-040) — defines the aspirational social register that families like the Babbitts measure themselves against.

### Kenneth Escott, Reporter

Kenneth Escott is a young reporter on the _Advocate-Times_ whom Chum Frink suggests as a part-time press-agent for the Sunday School (017-019). Escott is later appointed press-agent of the Chatham Road Presbyterian Sunday School, a role he performs with journalistic cunning — waiting "till the managing editor is out of town" to blackjack the city editor into running a flattering profile of Dr. Drew [017-032, 017-043].

## Babbitt's Relationship with the Paper

The _Advocate-Times_ is woven into the fabric of Babbitt's daily life. He begins his morning by lighting a cigar and "tasting the exhilarating drug of the _Advocate-Times_ headlines" (002-017), the newspaper as intoxicant. Mrs. Babbitt reads the Real Estate and Building column with its transactions listings (002-029). Babbitt quotes the paper's editorial line as though it were his own opinion: he chants that Zenith pays its teachers adequately "because he had read the statement in the _Advocate-Times_" (004-023).

## Role in Boosterism and Public Opinion

The _Advocate-Times_ functions as an unofficial house organ of the Zenith business establishment. When the Real Estate Board holds its annual banquet, the paper "reported this speech with unusual fullness" (014-011). When Babbitt gives a lecture, the paper covers it so thoroughly that "Vergil Gunch said to Babbitt, 'You're getting to be one of the classiest spellbinders in town'" (014-038). A photograph of "Leaders of Zenith Finance and Commerce Who Back Prout" in the Sunday rotogravure section confers legitimacy through visual association (014-003).

The paper's editorial silence is as powerful as its coverage: "Neither the _Advocate-Times_, the _Evening Advocate_, nor the _Bulletin of the Zenith Chamber of Commerce_ had ever had an editorial on the matter, and until one of them had spoken he found it hard to form an original opinion" (006-031). This passage lays bare Lewis's satire of how media shapes — or suppresses — independent thought in the standardized city.

## The Sunday Edition

The Sunday _Advocate-Times_ carries expanded features, including a rotogravure section for photographs (014-003) and space for extended coverage of civic and religious events. The profile of Dr. Drew that Kenneth Escott plants, complete with a photograph showing "Dr. Drew at his earnestest, with eyes alert, jaw as granite, and rustic lock flamboyant," provides "twenty-four hours' immortality" on the "wood-pulp tablet" of newsprint (017-043).

## Satirical Significance

Sinclair Lewis uses the _Advocate-Times_ to satirize the symbiosis between journalism and boosterism in the American city. The paper does not merely report on Zenith; it manufactures consent for its commercial class and confers legitimacy on aspiring boosters. When the paper fails to editorialize on a topic, the citizen is paralyzed — unable to "form an original opinion" (006-031) without the paper's permission. Lewis portrays a press that is not adversarial but accessory to the city's self-congratulatory narrative.
