# Drama League

A cultural organization in Zenith concerned with theater and dramatic arts. The Drama League appears only as a passing reference, but it illuminates the intellectual pretensions of Zenith's younger, college-educated generation. In a city whose cultural life was dominated by the Boosters' Club song ("Oh, here we come, the fellows from Zenith, the Zip, the Zest, the Zowie" — PN 013-021), the Drama League represented an aspiration toward something recognizably "artistic" — an aspiration that Lewis treats with his characteristic blend of sympathy and irony.

## Description

The Drama League is one of the cultural causes that interested Verona Babbitt and Kenneth Escott, who "worried, in a cultured collegiate manner, about minimum wages and the Drama League" (PN 034-019). Its pairing with minimum wages — progressive economics and amateur theater grouped as equivalent "causes" — suggests the scattershot earnestness of Zenith's collegiate youth. Verona, described earlier as "the older daughter" of the Babbitts, had returned from college with new opinions and a "conscientious" disposition that her father found fussy and unsettling (PN 001-046, PN 002-013).

## Social Function

The Drama League served as a marker of cultural aspiration within Zenith. Unlike the Boosters' Club or Chamber of Commerce — which existed for business networking — the Drama League signified a desire for genuine artistic culture. Yet Lewis's phrasing ("in a cultured collegiate manner") carries ironic weight: the League represents not deep engagement with art but the performance of cultural sophistication that defined Verona's set. Her interests extended to "filing letters at the Y.W.C.A." (PN 002-014) and discussing "Dr. John Jennison Drew's opinion of labor unions" (PN 023-006) — a grab bag of Progressive-era causes that substituted for intellectual rigor.

The League also functioned as a social context for Verona and Kenneth Escott's courtship. Escott, a reporter for the *Advocate-Times*, shared Verona's cultural interests, and the two "were always under foot" at the Babbitt home "holding long inquiries into epistemology and theosophy" (PN 018-034). The Drama League, minimum wages, epistemology, and theosophy — the sheer randomness of the list is Lewis's point. For Verona's generation, "culture" was a menu of approved topics, not a practice of genuine inquiry.

## Key Members

- **Verona Babbitt** — discussed the League with earnest concern alongside broader progressive topics; the eldest Babbitt child, college-educated, "conscientious" and given to opinions her father found irritating (PN 001-046, PN 034-019)
- **Kenneth Escott** — reporter for the *Advocate-Times*, shared Verona's interest in the Drama League and other cultural causes; their shared cultural pursuits formed the basis of their relationship and eventual engagement (PN 018-034, PN 026-020, PN 034-019)

## Key Scenes

- **After Verona's wedding** — Babbitt reflected with relief that he would no longer have to hear Verona and Kenneth "worrying, in a cultured collegiate manner, about minimum wages and the Drama League" (PN 034-019). The scene captures Babbitt's deep unease with his daughter's intellectual pretensions and his relief that marriage — the conventional destiny for Zenith women — would silence those pretensions
- **The Escott-Verona courtship** — throughout the middle chapters, Escott and Verona "held long inquiries into epistemology and theosophy" at the Babbitt home (PN 018-034), Ken neth Escott and Verona "appeared really to be engaged" by Chapter 26 (PN 026-020). The Drama League was one item in the repertoire of earnest concerns that constituted their shared identity

## Satirical Significance

The Drama League exemplifies Lewis's satire of Zenith's cultural pretensions. It is a "cultural" organization in a city whose cultural life consists largely of movies, jazz, and Chum Frink's poetry. Verona's earnest concern for the Drama League — paired incongruously with minimum wages as twin causes — reveals the shallow, performative nature of Zenith intellectual life. Babbitt's relief at escaping such discussions after Verona's marriage underscores the generational divide and the novel's broader critique: that even Zenith's "radicals" merely trade one form of conformity for another.

The Drama League also functions as a structural counterpart to the Boosters' Club at the opposite pole of Zenith's organizational life. Where the Boosters exist for business networking and conformity, the Drama League exists for the performance of nonconformity. Both are performances; neither produces genuine culture. Verona and Escott's cultural earnestness is the college-educated mirror image of Babbitt's boosterism — different vocabulary, same role, same empty piety.
