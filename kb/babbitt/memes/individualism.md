# Individualism

Individualism is the central irony of *Babbitt*: the one value every character proclaims and no character is permitted to live. It is the official religion of American business civilization, invoked in Boosters' Club speeches and real-estate brochures, yet the novel demonstrates that Zenith has built an entire social machinery to ensure it is never actually practiced.

## Definition in Context

In Zenith, individualism is less a philosophy than a rhetorical posture — something one declares rather than does. Babbitt preaches self-reliance while living a life of near-total conformity; the Boosters celebrate the "rugged individual" while systematically excluding anyone who actually differs from the group. Lewis exposes individualism as a civic mythology, a story Zenith tells itself to reconcile its love of freedom with its demand for uniformity. When Babbitt attempts to exercise genuine individual judgment — in his politics, his friendships, his refusal to join the Good Citizens' League — the city's response is swift, coordinated, and devastating.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The rhetoric of self-making** — Babbitt's identity is built on the story that he is a "self-made man," yet every detail of his life — from his opinions to his possessions to his morning routine — has been supplied by the collective. His "individualism" consists of choosing among identical options.
- **Paul Riesling's rebellion** — Paul is the one character who genuinely refuses to conform, speaking his dissatisfaction aloud. His punishment is not social ostracism but something worse: the collapse of his very self. His violent act and imprisonment represent what Zenith does to authentic individualism.
- **Babbitt's brief revolt** — In the novel's final third, Babbitt experiments with thinking for himself: he supports Seneca Doane, he takes a mistress, he questions the established order. The experiment ends not in triumph but in exhaustion: "he who had been a revolutionist became a reformer, and from a reformer he shrank to a tired business man who wanted only to be allowed to sit in peace in his own den" (034-029)
- **Ted Babbitt's quiet exit** — The novel's final hope lies not in grand rebellion but in a quiet refusal: Ted's decision to leave college and work with his hands represents an individualism so modest it may actually survive — a rejection not of society but of the pre-scripted life his father offers

## Key Passages

> He had, doubtless, sat in the same seat for a year, and the arm of the chair was worn where he laid his hand. He was not of the type that experiments with new brands of cigars. (paraphrase of Babbitt's habitual character)

> He discovered that he actually liked the Boosters' Club and the Athletic Club; that he missed the routine of the office and the pleasant and regular meals at home. He who had been a revolutionist became a reformer, and from a reformer he shrank to a tired business man who wanted only to be allowed to sit in peace in his own den. (034-029)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses individualism to expose what we would now call "performative individuality" — the consumerist pretense that choosing among brands of automobile or brands of cigar constitutes authentic self-expression. The novel's deepest satire is that Babbitt believes himself an individualist while being the most thoroughly standardized man in Zenith. His tragedy is not hypocrisy but self-deception: he cannot see that the freedom he proclaims is precisely what his society has been engineered to prevent.
