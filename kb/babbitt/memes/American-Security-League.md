# American Security League

The American Security League is a business-verification organization mentioned in *Babbitt*, consulted by hotel clerks to screen potential guests for creditworthiness and respectability.

## Description

The American Security League is referenced alongside the Credit Association when a hotel clerk in an unnamed city investigates Babbitt before agreeing to rent him a room (010-039). The League appears to function as a background-checking service for businesses, evaluating whether individuals are financially and socially reliable.

## The Hotel Check-In Scene

Babbitt recounts the episode with characteristic sarcasm, describing how the hotel clerk "hands me the cold-boiled stare" before retreating behind the room-registration apparatus (010-039). Babbitt's narration transforms the routine check-in into an ordeal of bureaucratic scrutiny:

> Well, I guess he called up the Credit Association and the American Security League to see if I was all right — he certainly took long enough — or maybe he just went to sleep; but finally he comes out and looks at me like it hurts him, and croaks, "I think I can let you have a room." (010-039)

The exchange captures the institutionalized suspicion that greets even a prosperous businessman like Babbitt. His imagined commentary — "You'd 'a' thought I'd sold him a second, or asked him to work on Yom Kippur!" — underscores the gap between Babbitt's self-image as a solid citizen and the clerk's view of him as a potential risk (010-039).

## Social Function

The League's mention illustrates the pervasive surveillance of respectability in 1920s American business culture. Before a hotel will rent a room, it consults not one but two verification organizations. The clerk's consultation with both the Credit Association and the American Security League transforms a routine commercial transaction into an examination of the customer's entire social standing (010-039).

The pairing of the Credit Association with the American Security League — the latter also associated with the suppression of political dissent — adds a political dimension to what appears at first to be a simple credit check. The institutional infrastructure of suspicion accompanies Zenith's boosterish welcome, reminding visitors that their standing in the business community is perpetually under review.

## Key Scenes

- **Hotel Check-In** — A hotel clerk calls both the Credit Association and the American Security League to verify Babbitt's credentials before renting him a room, prompting Babbitt to sarcastically narrate the ordeal (010-039)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the American Security League to satirize the institutionalization of mistrust in American commerce. The League embodies the bureaucratic machinery that reduces human interaction to credit checks and background investigations. Babbitt's sarcastic recounting of the episode — "maybe he just went to sleep" — highlights the absurdity of a system that treats every transaction as a potential fraud investigation (010-039).

The scene also reveals Babbitt's own contradictory position: a committed businessman and booster who finds himself on the receiving end of the very institutional scrutiny he otherwise champions. His irritation at being treated as a potential deadbeat exposes the tension between the ideology of business solidarity — all solid citizens are presumed trustworthy — and the institutional reality of perpetual verification.
