# Catawba

Catawba is the village where George Babbitt "had been born" (008-003) and spent his childhood. A small rural settlement at some distance from Zenith, Catawba represents everything Babbitt has left behind — and everything that, in moments of honesty, he suspects he has lost. It is the gravitational center of the novel's meditation on origins, identity, and the price of social climbing.

## Description

Catawba is small enough that Babbitt, even after years in Zenith, can still be called "the Catawba boy" (006-078) by his fellow citizens. It has at least a First Congregational Church and a Sunday School (016-026), and it functions in Babbitt's memory as a repository of "high-school pleasures" (018-015) and small-town simplicities. The village sends family members "down to" Zenith to visit — Babbitt's mother "came down from Catawba to spend three weeks" (018-038), and her youngest baby "came down from Catawba for two days" (018-039), a phrasing that measures the distance not in miles but in status.

Yet Catawba also exerts a backward pull. Every time Babbitt goes "up to Catawba" — the preposition shifts depending on whether he is moving toward or away from his origins — he feels compelled to "go around apologizing" (008-046) for having become a "city man." The village is simultaneously home and accusation.

## Role in the Novel

Catawba functions as the novel's symbolic counterweight to Zenith. Where Zenith is modern, standardized, and booster-driven, Catawba is traditional, intimate, and inert. Babbitt's trajectory from Catawba boy to Floral Heights homeowner is the American success story the boosters celebrate — yet the novel quietly asks what was surrendered along the way.

The village rarely appears as a physical setting; it operates almost entirely through memory and guilt. Babbitt thinks of Catawba when he feels most alienated from his present life — during his rebellion, his fantasies of escape, and his moments of domestic despair. It is less a place he can return to than a place he carries.

## Key Scenes Set Here

- **Babbitt's childhood Sunday School** — In memory, Babbitt is "back in the Sunday School of the First Congregational Church of Catawba" (016-026), a reverie triggered by the church social at Chatham Road, where he "went to the ole Sunday School at Catawba every chance I got" (016-030)
- **Mother's visits to Zenith** — Babbitt's mother "came down from Catawba to spend three weeks" (018-038), bringing the village with her in the form of family news and unspoken expectations
- **The return fantasy** — During his rebellion, Babbitt imagines spending "a week or ten days in Catawba" (025-010) but is "nagged by the memory" of obligations, a half-hearted attempt to flee forward by fleeing backward

## Themes

Catawba embodies the American tension between rural origins and urban identity. Lewis uses the village to anatomize the psychology of social mobility: Babbitt's embarrassment at his Catawba past coexists with a genuine affection for its simplicities, and neither feeling is wholly honest. He "certainly don't look like Catawba" (008-026), he insists — but the very vehemence of the denial proves the opposite. Catawba is the self Babbitt cannot outrun, the "hick" (008-026) he fears he still is, and the home he can never fully inhabit again.
