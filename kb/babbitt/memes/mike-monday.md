# Mike Monday

Mike Monday is the most prominent religious figure in *Babbitt* — not a settled pastor but a traveling evangelist, a former prize-fighter who has "converted over two hundred thousand lost and priceless souls at an average cost of less than ten dollars a head" (007-040). He is the novel's supreme example of religion industrialized: salvation as mass production, faith as spectacle, the Gospel as a product to be marketed like any other.

## Role in the Novel

Monday's revival campaign in Zenith is a major civic event. An expense fund of forty thousand dollars is underwritten, and a "Mike Monday Tabernacle" — seating fifteen thousand people — is erected on the County Fair Grounds (007-043). The scale of the operation is indistinguishable from a commercial enterprise: capital is raised, a venue is constructed, a product (salvation) is delivered to consumers (the congregation). Lewis's satire is not directed at religious belief itself but at its reduction to technique — the transformation of the preacher into a CEO of souls.

Monday's campaign serves a specific social function in Zenith: it channels the city's vague religious impulses into a form that reinforces, rather than challenges, the existing social order. His revivals are not about spiritual transformation but about moral ratification — the dramatic public affirmation that Zenith's way of life is righteous. When [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s Sunday School campaign needs legitimacy, Monday's endorsement carries the weight of religious authority, but it is an authority purchased and packaged like everything else in Zenith.

## The Revival Meeting

Lewis's portrait of Monday in Chapter VII is a masterpiece of satirical compression. Monday is introduced not through narration but through his own rhetoric — the voice of a man who has absorbed the techniques of the prize ring and the sales floor into his preaching:

> "Oh, there's a swell bunch of Lizzie boys and lemon-suckers and pie-faces and infidels and beer-bloated scribblers that love to fire off their filthy mouths and yip that Mike Monday is vulgar and full of mush." (007-043)

The passage is extraordinary in several respects. First, Monday's self-quotation reveals that his primary subject is himself — his celebrity, his authenticity, his defense against unnamed critics. Second, the language is pure demotic aggression: the evangelist's rhetorical strategy is to preempt elite criticism by embracing the very vulgarity he is accused of. Finally, the structure of the passage — Monday daring the crowd to doubt him — is identical to the salesman's pitch or the political stump speech: the product being sold is Mike Monday himself.

His signature challenge — "Who says Mike Monday is a fourflush and a yahoo?" (007-044) — is a rhetorical question designed to be unanswerable. The crowd's silence confirms his authority, but the question's very existence reveals the fragility of that authority. Monday must constantly perform his authenticity because it is never quite secure.

## Character Traits

Monday is bombastic, shrewd, and profoundly performative. His background as a prize-fighter (007-040) is not incidental but essential: Lewis suggests that revival preaching is a form of rhetorical violence, a pummeling of the audience into submission. His "conversion" rate — two hundred thousand souls at under ten dollars each — is presented in the language of industrial efficiency, as though salvation were a unit cost to be optimized.

The defensiveness that runs through his rhetoric — the constant need to assert that he is not a "fourflush," not "vulgar," not a fraud — reveals a man who understands that his calling sits uneasily with the more sophisticated members of his audience. He is both a figure of genuine popular power and a figure of contempt for the educated classes, and Lewis captures this ambivalence without resolving it.
