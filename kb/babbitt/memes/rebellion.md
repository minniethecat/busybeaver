# Rebellion

The force that disrupts the smooth surface of Zenith's conformist society — manifesting as Babbitt's private discontent, his brief public defiance, and the eventual crushing of his revolt by the organs of respectability.

## Definition in Context

In *Babbitt*, rebellion is less a coherent ideology than a series of impulses and actions against the "religion of business" and the relentless social conformity of middle-class Zenith. For Babbitt, rebellion begins as a private, inarticulate dissatisfaction — "a lonely feeling which perplexed and frightened him" (007-015) — before briefly erupting into public defiance, only to be healed back into conformity by the novel's end.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Babbitt's veiled rebellions** — Early in the novel, Babbitt experiences "treacherous day of veiled rebellions" (007-015), small acts of private resistance against the routine that defines his life as a successful businessman.
- **The excursion as escape** — After a trip away from Zenith, "his desire for rebellion was partly satisfied. He had retrograded to a shame-faced contentment" (013-080), demonstrating how rebellion is temporarily soothed by brief escape rather than resolved.
- **Babbitt's conscious revolt** — Awakening to his dissatisfaction, Babbitt wonders "Why, he wondered, should he be in rebellion? What was it all about? 'Why not be sensible; stop all this idiotic running around, and enjoy himself with his family, his business, the fellows at the club?'" (025-002), articulating the central tension between individual desire and social expectation.
- **The political dimension** — Though Babbitt resists being associated with radicals, "Though he was in rebellion, naturally Babbitt did not care to be seen talking with the hot-headed Doane" (026-002), revealing how even in revolt, Babbitt remains constrained by respectability.
- **Myra's quiet rebellion** — Babbitt discovers that his wife "was having a small determined rebellion of her own" (030-008), showing that the impulse to resist conformity crosses gender and temperament.
- **The healing of rebellion** — By the novel's conclusion, "the last scar of his rebellion was healed" (034-029), though Babbitt "discovered that he actually did not consider the Traction Bunch an interesting subject of conversation" (034-029), hinting that his rebellion has left permanent traces.

## Key Passages

> He awoke to stretch cheerfully as he listened to the sparrows, then to remember that everything was wrong; that he was determined to go astray, and not in the least enjoying the process. Why, he wondered, should he be in rebellion? What was it all about? "Why not be sensible; stop all this idiotic running around, and enjoy himself with his family, his business, the fellows at the club?" (025-002)

> Through all his fear ran defiance. He felt stubborn. Sometimes he decided that he had been a very devil of a fellow, as bold as Seneca Doane; sometimes he planned to call on Doane and tell him what a revolutionist he was, and never got beyond the planning. (032-032)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses Babbitt's rebellion to expose the hollowness of both Zenith's conformity and the individual's capacity to resist it. Babbitt's revolt is never ideological — he remains "in rebellion" without knowing what he wants, his defiance expressed through social slights and minor transgressions rather than principled action. The satirical force comes from the ease with which the rebellion is crushed: Babbitt's "moral fiber" is "so weakened by rebellion" (034-018) that he cannot sustain it, and the Good Citizens' League absorbs him back into conformity. Lewis suggests that rebellion in middle-class America is doomed not by tyranny but by the individual's own dependence on social approval.
