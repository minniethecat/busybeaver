# Sanitary Communion Outfit Company

A business that advertises communion sets in the pages of Sunday School journals, emblematic of the commercial spirit that Lewis saw infiltrating American religious practice during the 1920s.

## Description

The Sanitary Communion Outfit Company is known through its advertisement in a Sunday School journal that George Babbitt reads while serving on the Sunday School Advisory Committee. The company offers "an improved and satisfactory outfit throughout, including highly polished beautiful mahogany tray" (016-032). The advertisement's language—blending the hygienic ("Sanitary") with the sacred ("Communion"), and emphasizing both practical improvement and aesthetic refinement—exemplifies the rhetoric that Babbitt found so compelling in his study of Sunday School promotional materials.

## Context in the Novel

Babbitt encounters the Sanitary Communion Outfit Company's advertisement alongside a variety of other commercial solicitations in the Sunday School journals: a preparation that "takes the place of exercise for sedentary men," an industry report on the competitive Bible-selling business, and numerous tips on marketing religious education (016-032). The juxtaposition reveals Babbitt's undiscriminating enthusiasm for all varieties of promotional literature—he approaches religion with the same consumer mindset he applies to real estate, evaluating Sunday School materials by the same standards of "pep" and efficiency he uses in business. The communion outfit, with its emphasis on hygiene and beautiful mahogany, is indistinguishable in his mind from the other products being sold.

## Satirical Significance

The Sanitary Communion Outfit Company crystallizes Lewis's critique of the commercialization of American Protestantism. The word "Sanitary"—a term of modern public health—attached to "Communion"—one of Christianity's most solemn rituals—creates a dissonance that is both comic and revealing. The company's communion set, with its "highly polished beautiful mahogany tray," treats the sacred act as a consumer experience to be upgraded and improved, much as Babbitt treats his Sunday School as a business enterprise to be reorganized and promoted.
