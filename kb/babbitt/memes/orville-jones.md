# Orville Jones

Orville Jones, whose laundry was in many ways the best in Zenith, sat at Babbitt's regular lunch table at the Boosters' Club alongside Chum Frink, Howard Littlefield, Vergil Gunch, and Professor Pumphrey (005-023). He was a steady-looking, stubby, not very impressive man who belonged to the Roughnecks, the club-within-a-club that formed around Babbitt's lunch circle.

## The Rank-and-File Businessman

Jones represents the rank-and-file of Zenith's business class: solid, unremarkable, thoroughly assimilated into the booster culture. He is neither a leader like Gunch nor a celebrity like Frink — he is the ordinary businessman whose very ordinariness is the point. The novel populates its world with men like Jones precisely because they are the baseline against which Babbitt measures his own standing. Jones is not a climber like Babbitt, not a thinker like Littlefield, not a performer like Frink; he is simply there, steady and stubby, laundering the city's shirts.

## The Best Laundry in Zenith

The narration's parenthetical acknowledgment — "whose laundry was in many ways the best in Zenith" — is a miniature masterpiece of Lewis's satirical method (005-023). The qualifier "in many ways" does just enough work to suggest that even Zenith's best laundry might have competitors in those other, unnamed ways. Jones's identity is entirely subsumed by his business; he is "the laundryman," not a man who happens to own a laundry. In Zenith, what you do is who you are.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's dinner party** — Jones attended Babbitt's dinner party along with the other Roughnecks and their wives. Under the inspiration of cocktails, the conversation turned to prohibition and personal liberty (008-031). The dinner party was the social crucible in which the Roughnecks' camaraderie was tested and displayed before their wives.

- **The prohibition debate** — Jones spoke up during the dinner party discussion: "But it just goes to show the dangers of invading the rights of personal liberty" (008-033). The other guests felt it was rather in bad taste for Jones — not recognized as one of the wits of the occasion — to contribute to the discussion (008-037). This moment captures the rigid social hierarchy even within the Roughnecks: Jones has the right to sit at the table, but not the right to speak at it. When he ventures an opinion, the group's silent rebuke reminds him of his place.

## The Social Hierarchy Within the Roughnecks

The Roughnecks table was an ostensibly egalitarian space — "we're just a bunch of good fellows" — but it had its own internal ranking. At the top sat Gunch (the enforcer), Frink (the celebrity), and Littlefield (the intellectual authority). Babbitt occupied the middle tier: respected but not dominant. Jones sat near the bottom, tolerated but not consulted. The prohibition-debate incident reveals that his role was to listen and laugh, not to opine.
