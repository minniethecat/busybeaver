# Willis Ijams

Willis Ijams occupies a distinctive niche in the Boosters' Club: he is the salesman who writes poetry. In a culture that views art with suspicion and commerce with reverence, Ijams is the figure who combines them without apparent contradiction — a man who can sell sporting goods and compose verse with equal facility, and who is elected president of the Boosters' Club precisely because he makes the combination seem natural.

## Role in the Novel

Ijams first appears in connection with the "Sports Goods Mart, with the help of Willis Ijams, fellow member of the Boosters'" (010-019) — a salesman whose professional identity is inseparable from his club affiliation. Lewis's phrasing suggests that Ijams's Boosters' membership is not incidental to his business success but constitutive of it: the club is where deals are made and reputations forged.

What distinguishes Ijams is his "poetry and d—" (010-021) — the interrupted word hints at "drama" or "diction," but the interruption itself is telling. Even Paul Riesling, the novel's most cultivated sensibility, "lightened" at Ijams's presence (010-021): the poetry-writing salesman is a figure of comic incongruity, but also of genuine charm. He is not a fraud; he simply embodies a culture in which poetry and commerce coexist without strain.

His elevation to the presidency — "Willis Ijams is the new president" (021-024) — confirms his status as a man whose particular synthesis of talents is valued by his peers. By the novel's later chapters, he presides over the Boosters' Club luncheon with the authority of office: "President Willis Ijams began that Boosters' Club luncheon" (034-022).

## Key Scenes

- **The Sports Goods Deal** — Ijams's business association with the Sports Goods Mart (010-019) introduces him as a man whose commercial and social networks are identical.

- **The Poetry Salesman** — Paul Riesling's reaction to Ijams — he "lightened" (010-021) — suggests that even the novel's most disillusioned character finds something refreshing in Ijams's unselfconscious blending of poetry and salesmanship.

- **The Presidency** — Ijams's assumption of the Boosters' Club presidency (021-024, 034-022) marks the culmination of his social ascent and confirms the club's preference for genial competence over ideological purity.

## Character Traits

Ijams is defined by the absence of irony about his own contradictions. He writes poetry and sells sporting goods, and sees no tension between the two activities. In a novel structured around the gap between aspiration and reality — Babbitt's longing for escape, Paul's despair at the impossibility of beauty — Ijams is the rare figure who seems entirely comfortable in his own skin. He represents a kind of innocence that Lewis treats with unusual gentleness: the man who has never asked whether a life of poetry-plus-commerce is worth living, and is happier for not having asked.
