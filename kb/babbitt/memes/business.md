# Business

The dominant ideology and social organizing force in the world of *Babbitt*, functioning not merely as an economic activity but as a secular religion that defines identity, morality, and social standing in [[zenith]]. Lewis's decision to treat commerce as faith — complete with temple-spires, sacraments, credentialed theologians, and the threat of excommunication — is the novel's most powerful satirical device.

## Definition in Context

Business in *Babbitt* is more than commerce — it is the "religion of business, a faith passionate, exalted, surpassing common men" (001-052). For [[george-babbitt]], being a businessman defines his entire identity: "In them he was the modern business man; one who gave orders to clerks and drove a car and played occasional golf and was scholarly in regard to Salesmanship" (001-037). The business world provides the framework through which all personal worth is measured. Material success is both goal and proof of virtue; failure is moral as well as financial. Even Babbitt's physical appearance — his gold-rimmed spectacles, his "slightly plump" figure — marks him as a creature of commerce before he has spoken a word.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Babbitt's self-image** — His identity is entirely constructed around being a "successful business man of Zenith" (001-018). His yard, his car, his morning bathroom ritual all derive meaning from business success and the material comfort it provides. His private thought — that he "detested the grind of the real-estate business" (001-015) — is the heresy he dare not speak aloud.

- **Howard Littlefield as theologian** — The scholarly neighbor "confirmed the business men in the faith. Where they knew only by passionate instinct that their system of industry and manners was perfect, Dr. Howard Littlefield proved it to them, out of history, economics, and the confessions of reformed radicals" (003-007). Littlefield's function is to provide intellectual respectability for what is, at bottom, an article of faith.

- **The city as sacred space** — The physical geography of Zenith reinforces the business religion. The "clean towers" thrusting upward, "the thickening darting traffic, the crammed trolleys unloading, and high doorways of marble and polished granite" (003-030) form the sacred precincts of the commercial faith, a space where Babbitt feels genuinely inspired.

## Key Passages

> Its shining walls rose against April sky to a simple cornice like a streak of white fire. Integrity was in the tower, and decision. It bore its strength lightly as a tall soldier. As Babbitt stared, the nervousness was soothed from his face, his slack chin lifted in reverence. All he articulated was "That's one lovely sight!" but he was inspired by the rhythm of the city; his love of it renewed. He beheld the tower as a temple-spire of the religion of business, a faith passionate, exalted, surpassing common men. (001-052)

> He sulkily admitted now that there was no more escape, but he lay and detested the grind of the real-estate business, and disliked his family, and disliked himself for disliking them. (001-015)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses the "religion of business" as his central satirical vehicle for exposing the spiritual emptiness of American middle-class life. By elevating commerce to the status of faith, Lewis reveals how thoroughly Babbitt and his peers have substituted material success for genuine meaning. The irony operates on two levels: the business tower genuinely inspires Babbitt — the emotion is real — yet the very language of worship applied to a commercial building exposes the poverty of his spiritual imagination. Babbitt's private moments reveal a man who secretly "detested the grind of the real-estate business" (001-015), suggesting the business religion is a form of collective self-deception that even its most devout practitioners cannot fully sustain. The novel's genius is to hold both truths in tension: business IS Babbitt's genuine faith, AND it is a hollow substitute for real meaning.
