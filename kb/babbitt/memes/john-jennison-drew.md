# John Jennison Drew

The Reverend Dr. John Jennison Drew, M.A., D.D., LL.D., is the pastor of the [[chatham-road-presbyterian|Chatham Road Presbyterian Church]], which the [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] family attends. His three degrees, prominently displayed after his name, signal the weight of institutional credentials in [[zenith|Zenith]]'s religious [[art|culture]] (016-011).

## Role in the Novel

Dr. Drew is the official voice of religion in [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s world — a voice that, for all its eloquence, never challenges the basic premises of business civilization. Lewis uses him to satirize the comfortable alliance between Protestant [[religion|Christianity]] and the commercial order: Drew's sermons are aesthetically polished, his theology nominally Presbyterian, but his real office is to sanctify [[success|prosperity]] and assure his congregation that Christ is, in effect, the most efficient executive who ever lived (016-013, 017-043).

## Key Scenes

- **Dr. Drew's sermon** — On a Sunday when [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is unusually attentive, Drew preaches with "unusual eloquence" about the true business of [[chatham-road-presbyterian|the church]] being to "increase the world's sum of happiness." The sermon seamlessly merges Christian doctrine with the values of the [[rotary-club|Rotary Club]] (016-014, 016-015).
- **The [[religion|church]] as a business** — [[kenneth-escott|Kenneth Escott]] explains to [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] that [[chatham-road-presbyterian|Chatham Road]] Church operates with "top-notch efficiency," and Dr. Drew responds enthusiastically to the suggestion that [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] might provide "a little boosting for Doc Drew personally" — the pastor as product (017-043, 017-045).
- **[[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s confession** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] tells Drew that he has "put over" a deal for the Sunday School, and Drew calls him "brother." The language of [[business|commerce]] and the language of [[religion|faith]] become indistinguishable (017-048, 017-049).
- **[[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]]'s moral standing** — When [[paul-riesling|Paul]] shoots [[zilla-riesling|Zilla]], Drew's judgment is unambiguous: from the standpoint of the Reverend Dr. John Jennison Drew, [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]] is "a wicked man" (024-037).

## Character Traits

Dr. Drew is professionally charming, rhetorically gifted, and theologically empty. His sermons are exercises in "word-painting" — a skill that [[kenneth-escott|Escott]] the press-agent admires in precisely the terms one would admire a copywriter (017-046). Drew "bounces into the room and flops" with a calculated informality that signals his modernity, but there is nothing modern about the social function he performs: he baptizes the status quo and calls it Providence.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt]]** — Drew's parishioner and occasional booster; [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] relates to Drew with the same boyish eagerness he brings to his relationships with other men of standing
- **[[kenneth-escott]]** — [[kenneth-escott|Escott]], as a reporter and press-agent, admires Drew's pulpit technique in professional terms and helps promote him
- **[[paul-riesling]]** — Drew judges [[paul-riesling|Paul]] harshly after [[pauls-arrest|the shooting of Zilla]], embodying [[chatham-road-presbyterian|the church]]'s role in policing social morality
