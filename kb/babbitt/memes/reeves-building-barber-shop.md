# Reeves Building Barber Shop

The barber shop located in the Reeves Building, the office tower where George F. Babbitt's realty firm is headquartered. It is part of Babbitt's daily ritual until the more luxurious Pompeian Barber Shop tempts him with greater opulence, triggering a crisis of consumer loyalty.

## Description

The Reeves Building at the corner of Oberlin Avenue and Third Street, N.E., is "as fireproof as a rock and as efficient as a typewriter; fourteen stories of yellow pressed brick, with clean, upright, unornamented lines" (003-034). Within its corridors, among the "little unknown people who inhabited the Reeves Building corridors — elevator-runners, starter, engineers, superintendent, and the doubtful-looking lame man who conducted the news and cigar stand" (003-037), the barber shop serves the building's tenants and passersby.

The barber shop is Babbitt's regular grooming destination, part of the neighborhood fabric he navigates daily. His loyalty to it is habitual: "As always, he felt disloyal at deserting his neighbor, the Reeves Building Barber Shop" (024-017). When he decides to try the rival Pompeian Barber Shop, he must actively "overthrew his sense of guilt" and convinces himself: "Doggone it, I don't have to go here if I don't want to! I don't own the Reeves Building! These barbers got nothing on me!" (024-018).

## Social Function

The barber shop functions as a symbol of neighborhood loyalty and routine in Babbitt's life. His defection to the Pompeian Barber Shop represents a small but meaningful rebellion against habit — one that parallels his larger midlife restlessness. The language he uses to justify the switch — "I don't own the Reeves Building!" — echoes his growing resentment of the obligations that tie him down.

The Pompeian Barber Shop, by contrast, embodies the seductions of consumer luxury. It offers "a violet-ray facial treatment, an oil shampoo," "miraculous electrical massage-machines," and a shampoo machine "like a howitzer of polished nickel" [024-022 through 024-023]. The barber's obsequious attentions transform Babbitt into "a plump pink calif on an ingenious and adjustable throne" (024-024), appealing directly to his vanity and his reverence for modern machinery.

## Key Scenes

- **The habitual haircut** — Babbitt's fort nightly visit to the Reeves Building barber shop is part of the established routine documented in the novel's early chapters
- **The defection to Pompeian** — In Chapter XXIV, Babbitt visits the Pompeian Barber Shop and experiences its luxurious treatments, including the "best thrill" of the shampoo (024-023)
- **The manicure girl encounter** — At the Pompeian, Babbitt becomes infatuated with "the last manicure girl on the right," small and dark-haired (024-016), an attraction he acts upon by requesting a manicure after his haircut (024-025)

## Satirical Significance

The barber shop episodes satirize the intersection of consumer culture and masculine vanity in middle-class America. Babbitt's loyalty to the Reeves Building barber shop is not based on quality but on mere proximity and habit — he feels "disloyal" deserting it (024-017) as though it were a moral obligation rather than a commercial transaction.

The Pompeian Barber Shop scene is a miniature of Babbitt's consumer seduction: the elaborate machinery, the servile barber, the "adjustable throne" (024-024), all designed to make him feel important while extracting his money. His decision to request a manicure specifically to talk to the "dark-haired, smiling, tender, little" manicure girl (024-025) reveals how thoroughly his midlife restlessness channels through consumer experiences.
