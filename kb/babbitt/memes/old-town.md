# Old Town

Old Town is Zenith's industrial underbelly — a district of sooty warehouses, crowded tenements, and hidden saloons that exists in uneasy proximity to the clean towers and shining suburbs of the modern business center. It is the part of Zenith that the Boosters' Club would prefer not to discuss, but which George F. Babbitt visits whenever he needs a drink.

## Geography and Character

Lewis describes Old Town as the antithesis of Zenith's aspirational modernity. Beyond "the severe rectangular streets of the modern business center" lie "the tangled byways of Old Town — jagged blocks filled with sooty warehouses and lofts" (008-015). The district gives way, further out, to The Arbor, "once a pleasant orchard but now a morass of lodging-houses, tenements, and brothels."

This is the geography of urban decay mapped with sociological precision: the progression from commerce to industry to poverty follows a downward gradient that Babbitt traverses in his automobile, a visitor from another world.

## A Place of Illicit Pleasure

Old Town's primary function in the novel is as the site of Zenith's underground drinking establishments. Under Prohibition, alcohol is not absent from the city — it is merely hidden. When Babbitt wants gin, he drives not to a downtown bar but to Old Town, where Healey Hanson's saloon operates behind the facade of a soft-drink establishment.

The journey itself becomes a ritual of transgression. Babbitt "looked at every policeman with intense innocence, as one who loved the law, and admired the Force, and longed to stop and play with them" (008-015). The irony is rich: the city's leading real-estate broker, a man who preaches civic virtue at the Boosters' Club, must skulk through the working-class district like a criminal to obtain his evening cocktails.

## Social Significance

Old Town represents the hidden cost of Zenith's prosperity. The "clean towers" of the business center are built on a foundation of industrial labor performed in districts like this — districts that the city's respectable citizens visit only furtively and never discuss openly. The contrast between Floral Heights and The Arbor is not merely architectural; it is a map of class division rendered in brick and soot.
