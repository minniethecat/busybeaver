# Y.M.C.A.

The Zenith chapter of the Young Men's Christian Association, serving as both a religious-educational institution and a platform for the city's civic booster culture. Its educational director, [[Sheldon-Smeeth]], bridged the worlds of church and commerce, leading the choir at [[chatham-road-presbyterian-church]] while organizing classes where prominent citizens promoted their business interests.

## Description

The Zenith Y.M.C.A. offered classes in practical subjects, including a "Sales Methods" course where prominent citizens like [[George-Babbitt]] delivered lectures. Sheldon Smeeth served as its educational director, also leading the choir at Chatham Road Church (014-037). The Y.M.C.A. organized musical programs featuring its educational director as soloist (016-014).

> But in nothing was he more clearly revealed as the Prominent Citizen than in his lecture on "Brass Tacks Facts on Real Estate," as delivered before the class in Sales Methods at the Zenith Y.M.C.A. (014-037)

The institution's reach extended through Zenith's elite networks. During fundraising campaigns, prominent businessmen including [[Vergil-Gunch]], Sidney Finkelstein, and even [[Charles-McKelvey]] testified in movie theaters to the "good old Y.'s" influence on their lives (034-007). The campaign was spearheaded by Colonel Rutherford Snow, owner of the [[zenith-advocate]], who was photographed clasping hands with the governor (034-007).

## Social Function

The Y.M.C.A. bridged religious and commercial spheres in Zenith. Babbitt's lecture on "Brass Tacks Facts on Real Estate" before the Y.M.C.A. class exemplified how the institution served as a venue for the city's business elite to perform civic virtue while promoting their commercial interests.

> It helped the Y.M.C.A. to raise a two-hundred-thousand-dollar fund for a new building. Babbitt, Vergil Gunch, Sidney Finkelstein, and even Charles McKelvey told the spectators at movie theaters how great an influence for manly Christianity the "good old Y." had been in their own lives; and the hoar and mighty Colonel Rutherford Snow, owner of the Advocate-Times, was photographed clasping hands with the governor. (034-007)

The fundraising campaign exemplified the organized, publicity-driven approach to charity that Lewis satirizes throughout the novel. The Y.M.C.A. successfully combined moral uplift with business networking, creating a space where religious rhetoric served commercial ends.

## Key Members

- **[[Sheldon-Smeeth]]** — educational director of the Y.M.C.A. and choir-leader of Chatham Road Church, described as "a pale but strenuous young man with curly hair and a smile" (016-024), who was known for his damp handshake and relentless cheerfulness (032-038).
- **[[George-Babbitt]]** — lecturer in the Sales Methods course, using his Y.M.C.A. lecture to cement his status as a Prominent Citizen.
- **[[Vergil-Gunch]]** — booster and fundraiser, testified at movie theaters for the Y.M.C.A. campaign.
- **Colonel Rutherford Snow** — owner of the [[zenith-advocate]], figurehead of the fundraising campaign.

## Key Scenes

Babbitt's encounter with Sheldon Smeeth on the street reveals the Y.M.C.A. director's unsettling manner:

> With one of his damp hands Smeeth imprisoned Babbitt's thick paw while he chanted, "Brother Babbitt, we haven't seen you at church very often lately. I know you're busy with a multitude of details, but you mustn't forget your dear friends at the old church home." (032-038)

Babbitt's irritated reaction — shaking off the "affectionate clasp" and later worrying about what "the holy outfit" was whispering about him (032-038) — illustrates the social pressure the Y.M.C.A. network exerted on nonconformists in Zenith.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Y.M.C.A. to illustrate the fusion of religion, commerce, and civic boosterism in Zenith. The institution's dual identity — nominally religious but functionally a business-networking venue — mirrors Babbitt's own conflation of spiritual and commercial values. The fundraising campaign, with businessmen delivering testimonials in movie theaters, reduces religious charity to a publicity stunt. Sheldon Smeeth embodies the hollowness at the institution's core: a man whose professional cheerfulness conceals an aggressive social enforcement mechanism, pressuring those who stray from the herd.
