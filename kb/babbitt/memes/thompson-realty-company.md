# Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company

The real-estate firm co-owned by George F. Babbitt and his father-in-law Henry T. Thompson, located in the Reeves Building on Third Street, N.E., in Zenith. It is the economic engine of Babbitt's life and the source of his middle-class identity.

## Description

The Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company occupies a ground-floor office in the Reeves Building, "fourteen stories of yellow pressed brick, with clean, upright, unornamented lines" (003-034). Its storefront position along Third Street places it among other businesses including "a Western Union Telegraph Office, the Blue Delft Candy Shop, Shotwell's Stationery Shop" (003-035). The firm's advertisements declare it "Sole agents BABBITT-THOMPSON REALTY COMPANY Reeves Building" (004-010), emphasizing its modern, aggressive approach to merchandising.

The office employs nine staff members beyond the two partners (003-042). Babbitt's own office features modern business equipment including "a non-conducting fiber ice-container, a porcelain water-jar (guaranteed hygienic), a dripless non-clogging sanitary faucet, and machine-painted decorations in two tones of gold" (003-046), reflecting his pride in the material emblems of business success.

The company's business model includes both straightforward brokerage and more ethically ambiguous ventures. The Glen Oriole housing development is a case in point: "It was supposed that the Babbitt-Thompson Company were merely agents for Glen Oriole, serving the real owner, Jake Offutt, but the fact was that Babbitt and Thompson owned sixty-two per cent. of the Glen Oriole Company" (004-028). This arrangement allows them to profit from land speculation while maintaining the appearance of neutral agency.

## Social Function

The realty company serves as Babbitt's primary source of social status and self-definition. His business identity is so central that he introduces himself at social functions as "George Babbitt of the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company" (008-018), as though the firm's name confers legitimacy upon his person.

The company also embodies the intersection of business and politics in Zenith. When the Zenith Street Traction Company seeks to purchase land in Dorchester, they discover it is "held, on options, by the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company" (019-002), positioning Babbitt as a middleman between municipal infrastructure and private profit. The purchasing-agent's cryptic telephone call and the mysterious retrieval of an envelope marked "'Babbitt-Thompson' in the corner" (019-013) suggest the shadowy dealing that underlies Zenith's business culture.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company to dramatize the fusion of economic interest and moral orthodoxy in middle-class America. The firm is not merely a business but a moral universe: Babbitt's partner Henry Thompson stands between the extremes of "insularity" and "frothiness," while Babbitt occupies the virtuous center of "sound business" (006-007). This triad — Thompson, Babbitt, and the flamboyant Noël Ryland — maps the narrow bandwidth of acceptable bourgeois character.

The Glen Oriole arrangement satirizes the pretense of ethical business practice in a culture where self-dealing is routine as long as it remains technically legal. Babbitt "really did hate men recognized as swindlers" (004-028), yet participates in the same profit structure. The firm's slogan, "Yours for service" (003-057), echoes hollowly against the reality of speculative land deals and political maneuvering.
