# Progress

The unquestioned ideology of Zenith's booster class — a secular faith in growth, civic improvement, and the city's manifest destiny to become ever larger, richer, and more important on the national stage.

## Definition in Context

Progress in *Babbitt* is not a philosophical concept but a civic religion, preached by the International Organization of Boosters' Clubs, which "has become a world-force for optimism, manly pleasantry, and good business" (021-001). It is the assumption that bigger is always better, that population growth is the measure of civic virtue, and that anyone who questions this creed is a "knocker" — a heretic against the faith of improvement.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Boosters' convention** — Babbitt's trip to the real-estate convention reveals progress as a social ritual: "the real convention consisted of men muttering in hotel bedrooms" (013-035), where deals are made and the ideology of growth is reinforced through masculine camaraderie.
- **Civic boosterism as religion** — Babbitt's speech proclaims that "if by the next census we do not stand at least tenth, then I'll be the first to request any knocker to remove my shirt and to infest the woods with it" (014-015), reducing civic pride to a numerical competition.
- **The International Organization of Boosters' Clubs** — With "nine hundred and twenty of the thousand chapters" in the United States (021-001), the organization represents the institutionalization of boosterism as a national creed, exporting optimism like a commodity.

## Key Passages

> The International Organization of Boosters' Clubs has become a world-force for optimism, manly pleasantry, and good business. Chapters are to be found now in thirty countries. Nine hundred and twenty of the thousand chapters, however, are in the United States. (021-001)

> "It is true that even with our 361,000, or practically 362,000, population, there are, by the last census, almost a score of larger cities in the United States. But, gentlemen, if by the next census we do not stand at least tenth, then I'll be the first to request any knocker to remove my shirt and to infest the woods with it." (014-015)

## Satirical Function

Lewis satirizes the American cult of progress by exposing its emptiness: boosterism is a religion without theology, a faith whose only sacrament is population statistics. Babbitt's speech about making Zenith "at least tenth" reduces civic life to a footrace. The Boosters' Clubs, with their international pretensions, are a joke — 92% of chapters are in the United States. Lewis suggests that "progress" in Zenith is not about genuine improvement but about the performance of optimism, a collective agreement never to question whether growth is actually making anyone happier.
