# Ted Babbitt

Theodore Roosevelt Babbitt — called Ted — is the teenage son of George and [[myra-babbitt|Myra Babbitt]]. A "junior in the great East Side High School," Ted is obsessed with cars, contemptuous of school, and locked in a perpetual, low-grade warfare with his more studious older sister [[verona-babbitt|Verona]] (002-015, 002-010).

## Role in the Novel

Ted functions as a generational echo of his father's restlessness. [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]] dreams vaguely of escape; Ted simply refuses to see why he should care about school or convention. His rebellion is not intellectual — he is no Verona — but it is authentic in its own narrow way. Lewis uses Ted to suggest that the next generation will carry forward the same appetites and impatience, but perhaps without even the thin veneer of civic idealism that Babbitt's generation maintained. When Ted aspires to a Packard twin-six as his father once aspired to the presidency, the deflation of American ambition is complete (006-027).

> The Babbitt family war was in full swing at the breakfast table. Ted was arguing with Verona about the automobile, and Mrs. Babbitt was arguing with both of them about table manners. (paraphrased from 002-015)

## Key Scenes

- **The morning bathroom ritual** — Ted is one of the many family members who use the Babbitts' single bathroom in the morning, a comic emblem of the crowded, competitive domesticity of the Babbitt household (001-023).
- **Breakfast table combat** — Ted and Verona snipe at each other across the breakfast table. Ted negotiates, complains about the car, and is accused by Verona of being "a perfect pig about the table" — the Babbitt family war in miniature (002-015, 002-016, 002-018, 002-019).
- **The car debate** — When the family discusses trading in their car, Ted's commentary — "a lot sportier," "I guess we can!" — reveals his formative values. He is a consumer before he is anything else (006-024, 006-025, 006-026).
- **Home Studies and complaints** — Ted settles down to his Home Studies magazine but complains about the old car, and his father's lecture about the importance of education misses the point entirely: Ted's future, like his father's, will be defined by consumption, not cultivation (006-030, 006-033, 006-052, 006-055).

## Character Traits

Ted is energetic, materialistic, and indifferent to the intellectual aspirations that briefly stir in his sister Verona. He is Babbitt without the self-deception — a young man who already knows that what matters in life is what you can buy. His full name — Theodore Roosevelt Babbitt — carries the ironic weight of a vanished Progressive Era; the boy who bears the trust-buster's name aspires only to a better automobile. In this generational contrast, Lewis encodes his bleakest judgment: American ambition has devolved from civic leadership to consumer desire in a single generation.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]** — Ted's father, who lectures him about education while sharing his materialistic values. Their relationship is one of mutual incomprehension masked by bluster.
- **[[myra-babbitt|Myra Babbitt]]** — Ted's mother, perpetually trying to manage the household while Ted evades her authority
- **[[verona-babbitt|Verona Babbitt]]** — Ted's older sister and intellectual antagonist; their sibling rivalry is a microcosm of the novel's larger tensions between culture and commerce
- **[[tinka-babbitt|Tinka Babbitt]]** — Ted's younger sister, the baby of the family
