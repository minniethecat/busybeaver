# State University

The state university of Winnemac, attended by George Babbitt, Paul Riesling, and generations of Zenith's business and professional class. More a badge of respectability than a center of learning, it functions as the educational pillar of Zenith's booster mythology.

## Description

State University is the flagship public university of Winnemac, located in or near Zenith. It is repeatedly invoked by the city's businessmen as proof of Zenith's greatness — "that great department-store, the State University" (006-006) — and its graduates form the backbone of the city's professional elite. The university's identity is explicitly commercial and pragmatic: it is mentioned in the same breath as department stores, a civic asset rather than an intellectual institution.

The novel gives little detail about its actual academic life. What matters is the diploma: Babbitt "had graduated from the State University, twenty-four years ago" (006-076), and this credential is part of his identity. The university is "my own Alma Mater" to figures like Seneca Doane (014-032), and a lawyer and fellow graduate runs for mayor (014-002). It is a shared origin story for the city's ruling class.

## Social Function

State University serves three interrelated functions in the novel's social landscape:

1. **Credential factory**: A degree from State U marks a man as qualified for business and civic leadership. It is "what the State University considered scholarship" (015-012) — the phrasing implying that actual intellectual rigor is secondary to certification.

2. **Network generator**: Babbitt and Paul Riesling were "classmates, roommates, in the State University" (004-018), forging the central friendship of the novel. The university creates the personal bonds that later animate booster clubs and business partnerships.

3. **Civic totem**: Like the Zenith Athletic Club or the Chamber of Commerce, State U is a point of collective pride. Speakers at booster events invoke it to legitimize themselves and their city.

Babbitt's son Ted enters as "a freshman in the College of Arts and Sciences" (026-021), signaling generational continuity — but also the tensions to come, as Ted's mediocre academic performance and unconventional interests challenge the booster model of education-as-credential.

## Key Scenes

- **Commencement nostalgia** — Babbitt reflects on his graduation twenty-four years prior, measuring his life against youthful ambitions (006-076)
- **Ted enters college** — Ted matriculates as a freshman, provoking parental anxiety and Babbitt's hopes for "future triumphs" (026-021, 025-021)
- **Professor at dinner** — A history professor from State U appears at a dinner party, an "old man and white-bearded," representing the diminished prestige of the academic in booster society (027-019)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses State University to expose the hollowness of booster claims to culture. The university is celebrated as "great" not for its scholarship but for its size and commercial utility — it is literally compared to a department store. No character in the novel is portrayed as intellectually transformed by their education there; the university produces businessmen, not thinkers. Babbitt's college experience is remembered only for the friendship with Paul, not for any idea or discovery.

The satire extends to the second generation: Ted's entrance into State U promises only more of the same. The university is not a site of liberation or growth but a waystation on the path to respectability, as empty of genuine learning as the Boosters' Club is of genuine civic purpose.
