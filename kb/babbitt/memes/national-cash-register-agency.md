# National Cash Register Agency

A Zenith retail establishment dealing in office machinery — cash registers, dictaphones, and calculating typewriters — that occupies space within the Simplex Office Furniture Shop on Babbitt's familiar downtown route, and which provokes in him a yearning for technological marvels that Lewis renders with deliberate comic inflation. The agency embodies the intersection of commerce, technology, and desire in Babbitt's materialist imagination.

## Description

The National Cash Register Agency shares quarters with the Simplex Office Furniture Shop, a downtown Zenith retail building that Babbitt passes each morning on his drive to work. From the street, the combined establishment displays the tools of modern American commerce: office furniture, cash registers, dictaphones, and the latest in adding and multiplying typewriters (005-008). The agency is one of the specialized businesses that line Babbitt's route from his Floral Heights home to the Reeves Building, each a landmark in his mental geography of a city he experiences primarily through its commercial facade.

The Simplex building itself is a characteristic Zenith structure. The Reeves Building, where Babbitt's own office is located, is described as "as fireproof as a rock and as efficient as a typewriter; fourteen stories of yellow pressed brick" (003-034). The comparison of a building to a typewriter is quintessential Lewis: architecture evaluated not by beauty or livability but by the mechanical virtues of efficiency and reliability. The National Cash Register Agency, housed within a similar commercial structure, participates in this aesthetic of functionalist boosterism.

## Babbitt's Yearning

When Babbitt passes the National Cash Register Agency, he covets the machines on display with an intensity that Lewis renders through deliberately incongruous comparison:

> "he yearned for a dictaphone, for a typewriter which would add and multiply, as a poet yearns for quartos or a physician for radium" (005-008)

The simile is the key satirical device. By equating Babbitt's desire for office machinery with a poet's longing for rare books or a doctor's for a life-saving element, Lewis simultaneously inflates the office equipment to heroic proportions and deflates Babbitt's materialism by exposing its essential triviality. The dictaphone is not radium; it will not cure disease. But in Babbitt's emotional economy, it carries a comparable charge — the promise of efficiency, modernity, and the kind of success that can be measured in invoices and square footage.

The passage is one of the novel's most economical satirical moments, conveying in a single sentence both Babbitt's genuine emotional investment in the apparatus of business and the narrator's cool awareness of how disproportionate that investment is. Babbitt's "yearning" is real; what he yearns for is the question Lewis leaves for the reader to answer.

## Social Context

The National Cash Register Agency belongs to the ecosystem of downtown Zenith businesses that Lewis catalogs in Chapter V as Babbitt drives to work — a panoramic inventory of the city's commercial face. The agency represents the mechanization of the office, a trend that was transforming American business in the 1920s. The dictaphone, the adding typewriter, and the cash register were not merely tools; they were symbols of the "scientific management" and efficiency movements that had captured the imagination of the business class.

The agency's location within the Simplex Office Furniture Shop is itself significant. The name "Simplex" — promising simplicity in a world of growing complexity — is the kind of brand name Lewis's satire feeds on: a word that means the opposite of what the business actually sells (an increasingly elaborate array of specialized office machines). The proximity of furniture and machinery in a single establishment reflects the totalizing ambition of the commercial supply chain — everything the modern office needed, from the desk to the dictaphone, available under one roof.

Later in the novel, Babbitt's fantasy of escape from his office routine is defined in part by the absence of these very machines: "Never hear a typewriter. Or Stan Graff fussing in the next office" (011-010). And still later, Babbitt associates the city itself with the noise of mechanized business: "a city with trolleys grinding and typewriters clacking and people bothering" (025-029). The machines he yearned for in Chapter V have become, by the novel's later chapters, instruments of entrapment. The National Cash Register Agency thus marks an early point on an arc of disillusionment that the novel traces but Babbitt only dimly perceives.

## Satirical Significance

The National Cash Register Agency is one of the novel's clearest illustrations of what might be called the religion of office equipment. Lewis's Babbitt does not merely use the products of modern industry; he worships them, investing them with an almost spiritual significance. The agency's display window is a shrine, and Babbitt's morning drive past it is a form of daily devotion — the businessman paying homage to the instruments of his faith.

The satire is not directed at the machines themselves, which are neutral, but at the cultural logic that elevates them to objects of reverence. In Zenith, a calculating typewriter is not simply a tool for arithmetic; it is proof of its owner's participation in Progress. The "yearning" Babbitt feels is the desire to be counted among the modern, the efficient, the up-to-date — a desire that Lewis suggests is as insatiable as it is empty.
