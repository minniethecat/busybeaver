# Home and Family

If the Boosters' Club is the public face of Zenith, the Babbitt household is its private counterweight. The novel's domestic scenes — breakfast-table silences, bedtime rituals, the tensions between parents and children — expose the emotional costs of the booster ethos. George Babbitt's marriage to Myra, his relationship with his children, and the quiet desperation of Floral Heights evenings belong to a world the banquet speeches never acknowledge. ## About This Index

The family in *Babbitt* is less a source of comfort than a site of quiet accommodation. Lewis portrays domestic life not as a refuge from the commercial world but as its extension — a realm governed by the same unspoken rules of conformity and consumption. The pages indexed here map the network of relationships, tensions, and generational conflicts that the novel's booster characters would prefer to keep private.
