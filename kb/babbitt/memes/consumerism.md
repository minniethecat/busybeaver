# Consumerism

Consumerism is the unspoken theology of Zenith: a system of belief in which salvation is achieved through the acquisition of goods and damnation consists of falling behind the neighbors. Lewis, writing in 1922 at the dawn of modern advertising and mass consumption, produced one of the earliest and most penetrating literary critiques of consumer culture — a critique that has only grown more relevant in the century since.

## Definition in Context

In Zenith, consumption is never merely practical — it is always symbolic. Every purchase is a declaration of identity, every possession a claim to status. The novel opens not with Babbitt's thoughts or feelings but with a meticulous inventory of his bedroom's manufactured contents: the "standard bedside book" (unread), the "well-known brand of a device for curling the mustache," the "alarm-clock with the best patent alarm" (001-002). Babbitt is introduced through the objects he owns — or rather, through the objects that own him. His identity is assembled from catalog purchases rather than cultivated from within.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The inventory of the self** — Chapter I's famous catalog of Babbitt's bedroom is a tour de force of consumerist satire: every object is brand-named, standardized, and aspirational. The objects do not express Babbitt's personality; they constitute it
- **The automobile as identity** — Babbitt's relationship with his car transcends utility: "his motor-car was poetry and tragedy, love and heroism" (003-002). The automobile is at once transportation, status symbol, erotic object, and spiritual consolation — consumerism's perfect product
- **The cigar as ritual** — Babbitt's cigar-lighting ceremony at the Boosters' Club, with its specialized cutter and precise flame, transforms consumption into liturgy. The cigar is not smoked so much as performed
- **Advertising as scripture** — The "billboards with crimson goddesses nine feet tall advertising cinema films, pipe tobacco, and talcum powder" (003-029) form the visual landscape of Zenith, a secular iconography promising transformation through purchase
- **The standardized home** — The Babbitt house on Floral Heights, with its sleeping-porch, sun-parlor, and "all the latest conveniences," is a monument to the idea that the right purchases can purchase the right life — and the novel's drama lies in the slow discovery that they cannot

## Key Passages

> The towers of Zenith aspired above the morning mist; austere towers of steel and cement and limestone, sturdy as cliffs and delicate as silver rods. They were neither citadels nor churches, but frankly and beautifully office-buildings. (001-002)

> Of course I believe in using poetry and humor and all that junk when it turns the trick, but with a high-class restricted development like the Glen we must use the high-class methods. (004-005, Babbitt on the language of salesmanship)

## Satirical Function

Lewis's satire of consumerism operates at two levels. On the surface, he mocks the absurdity of a civilization in which a man's worth is measured by his cigar cutter and his bathroom fixtures. But beneath the comedy runs a darker current: consumerism is what fills the void left by the absence of genuine meaning. Babbitt's purchases are attempted purchases of a self — and they never quite succeed. The novel's most devastating insight is not that consumerism is vulgar but that it is insufficient: no quantity of goods can satisfy the hunger they were bought to satisfy.
