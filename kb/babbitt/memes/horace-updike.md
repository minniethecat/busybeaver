# Horace Updike

Horace Updike is Zenith's "professional bachelor" — a slim-waisted man of forty-six with an effeminate voice and a refined taste in flowers, cretonnes, and flappers. Unlike [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]], who belongs to the middle-class [[outing-golf-and-country-club|Outing Golf and Country Club]], Updike moves in the rarified sphere of the [[tonawanda-country-club|Tonawanda Country Club]] and the [[union-club|Union Club]], where the truly rich men of Zenith lunch.

## Role in the Novel

Updike appears only in passing, but his function is precise: he marks the social boundary that Babbitt can see but cannot cross. Babbitt loudly insists he would never join the Tonawanda even if they paid him — "Too much dog altogether" — yet the narration quietly places Updike among "[[charles-mckelvey|Charles McKelvey]], Horace Updike, and the other rich men," making Updike a fixed point in the geography of Zenith's class hierarchy.

## Key Scenes

- **The Tonawanda distinction** — When listing the Tonawanda Country Club's membership, the narration names "Charles McKelvey, Horace Updike, and the other rich men who lunched not at the Athletic but at the Union Club." Babbitt defensively explains that the Outing has "real human fellows" while the Tonawanda has "nothing but these would-be's in New York get-ups, drinking tea!" (012-011)
- **The Royal Ridge evening** — At the very moment Babbitt is elsewhere in Zenith, "Horace Updike was making love to Lucile McKelvey in her mauve drawing-room on Royal Ridge, after their return from a lecture by an eminent English novelist." (007-032) The juxtaposition is characteristic of Lewis's technique: while Babbitt fusses over middle-class concerns, Updike glides through the romantic intrigues of the truly rich.

## Character Traits

Updike is sketched with economy: a bachelor at an age when Zenith expects marriage, effeminate in a city that prizes masculine bluster, cultivated in a culture that distrusts cultivation. His "taste in flowers, cretonnes, and flappers" neatly collapses the aesthetic and the erotic, suggesting a man whose refinement extends to his romantic pursuits. He is everything Babbitt is not — slim where Babbitt is plump, quiet where Babbitt is booming, inside the inner circle where Babbitt is forever outside looking in.
