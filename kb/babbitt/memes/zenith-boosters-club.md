# Zenith Boosters' Club

A weekly lunch-club, "local chapter of a national organization which promoted sound business and friendliness among Regular Fellows" (005-016), the Boosters' Club is the quintessential Zenith institution — a place where business, boosterism, and masculine camaraderie merge into a single civic religion.

## Description

The Boosters' Club is central to the social and business life of Zenith. Its president, Vergil Gunch, is "a coal-dealer, equally powerful in the Elks and in the Boosters' Club" (008-031). The club's influence extends beyond its weekly lunches: membership is virtually mandatory for any man claiming civic standing. "Babbitt and Ryland were fellow-members of the Boosters' Club, and no Booster felt right if he bought anything from another Booster without receiving a discount" (006-006) — the club's fellowship translates directly into commercial advantage.

The club's ethos is captured in Babbitt's own self-description: "My religion is to serve my fellow men, to honor my brother as myself, and to do my bit to make life happier for one and all" (016-023) — a creed delivered in "sonorous Boosters'-Club rhetoric" that substitutes commercial fellowship for religious conviction. The club's national organization has "become a world-force for optimism, manly pleasantry, and good business" (021-001).

## Social Function

Membership in the Boosters' Club serves as a social credential. Of a "decent man in Zenith it was required that he should belong to one, preferably two or three, of the innumerous 'lodges' and prosperity-boosting lunch-clubs; to the Rotarians, the Kiwanis, or the Boosters" (016-004). The lunch-club form — combining eating with oratory — embodies the Zenith ideal of productive leisure: nothing is done purely for pleasure; everything must boost.

Babbitt wears his membership literally on his lapel: "Last, he stuck in his lapel the Boosters' Club button" (001-042), the button functioning as a visible badge of belonging that completes his morning ritual of self-presentation.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's speech on ethics** — In an address at the Boosters' Club, Babbitt admits "It is at once the duty and the privilege of the realtor to know everything about his own city and its environs" (004-023), conflating civic knowledge with commercial opportunity
- **The dinner party guest list** — Babbitt's dinner party draws its guest list from the Boosters' Club membership (008-031), revealing how the club structures Zenith's social hierarchy
- **Ted Babbitt's college speech** — Ted parrots Boosters'-Club ideology in his college oratory about "the ideal of American manhood" — the God-fearing, hustling Regular Guy who "belongs to the Boosters or the Rotarians or the Kiwanis" (014-034)

## Satirical Significance

The Boosters' Club is Lewis's master symbol for the conflation of commerce and community in American life. "Boosterism" — the ideology that local business promotion is a form of civic virtue — finds its institutional home here. The club's rhetoric transforms salesmanship into religion ("My religion is to serve my fellow men"), self-interest into altruism, and conformity into manliness. The Boosters' lunch — where "they laughed with the sound of a Boosters' luncheon" (019-039) — reduces human fellowship to a standardized commercial product, as mass-produced as the automobiles and real estate its members sell.
