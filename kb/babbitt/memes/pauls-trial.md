# Paul Riesling's Trial

The trial of [[paul-riesling]] for shooting his wife Zilla — a legal proceeding that "occupied less than fifteen minutes" (022-017) but whose consequences ripple through the remainder of the novel, transforming [[george-babbitt]]'s private discontent into the beginning of his [[babbitts-rebellion|rebellion]].

## Background

The shooting occurs during an episode of domestic conflict that has defined the Rieslings' marriage throughout the novel. Zilla Riesling is portrayed as a relentlessly nagging wife whose "deviling" (022-007) drives Paul — a sensitive, violin-playing man ill-suited to Zenith's business culture — to a moment of uncharacteristic violence. Paul tells Babbitt at the City Prison: "I didn't hardly mean to, but she got to deviling me so I went crazy, just for a second, and pulled out that old revolver you and I used to shoot rabbits with, and took a crack at her" (022-007).

Babbitt's response to the crisis is immediate and visceral. He drives to the City Prison "not blindly, but with unusual fussy care at corners, the fussiness of an old woman potting plants" (022-002), and fights through bureaucratic obstruction until he reaches Paul's cage, where his friend sits "twisted like an old beggar, legs crossed, arms in a knot, biting at his clenched fist" (022-006).

## Key Elements

- **The prison visit** — Babbitt pushes past the attendant who tells him visiting hours haven't started, demands to see the warden, and eventually finds Paul in his cell. The moment crystallizes the depth of their friendship and Babbitt's first encounter with institutional power used against someone he loves (022-002 to 022-007).
- **Babbitt's offer of perjury** — In a meeting with Paul's lawyer, P. J. Maxwell, Babbitt offers to testify falsely: "I've known and you've known many and many a case of perjury, just to annex some rotten little piece of real estate, and here where it's a case of saving Paul from going to prison, I'd perjure myself black in the face" (022-013). Maxwell refuses, noting Paul insists on pleading guilty.
- **The fifteen-minute trial** — Babbitt, who "had, doubtless from some story-book, pictured Paul's trial as a long struggle, with bitter arguments, a taut crowd, and sudden and overwhelming new testimony" (022-017), finds the reality deflatingly brief and bureaucratic. Doctors testify that Zilla will recover and Paul was temporarily insane.
- **The sentence** — Paul is sentenced to three years in the State Penitentiary and led away "quite undramatically, not handcuffed, merely plodding in a tired way beside a cheerful deputy sheriff" (022-017).

## Satirical Significance

The trial's brevity is Lewis's indictment of a legal system that processes human tragedy with bureaucratic efficiency. Babbitt expects drama — a courtroom struggle with "bitter arguments" and "overwhelming new testimony" (022-017) — but receives only procedure. The gap between the emotional magnitude of Paul's act (shooting his wife with a childhood friend's revolver) and the institutional response (fifteen minutes of medical testimony) exposes the inadequacy of legal machinery to address human suffering.

The episode also reveals the class dynamics of justice. Babbitt's instinct is to use his connections and willingness to perjure himself — tactics he knows work in real estate deals — to circumvent the law. Maxwell's ethical refusal, grounded in professional norms rather than moral conviction, highlights the arbitrary line between the "rotten little piece of real estate" perjury Babbitt cites and the perjury that might save his friend.

## Consequences

The trial is the catalyst for Babbitt's incipient rebellion. Returning from the station after saying goodbye to Paul, Babbitt "returned to his office to realize that he faced a world which, without Paul, was meaningless" (022-017). The loss of his closest friend strips away the satisfactions that previously sustained his booster identity.

Zilla's subsequent response deepens Babbitt's alienation. Visiting her later, he endures her declaration that "it's the blessing of God himself that Paul should be in prison now, and torn and humbled by punishment" and her hope "that he _dies_ in prison" (026-018). Babbitt "had itched and twisted" (026-017) through her speech, and the encounter sharpens his recognition that the respectable moral order Zilla invokes is indistinguishable from cruelty.

The trial sets in motion the sequence of events that culminates in Babbitt's [[babbitts-rebellion|open rebellion]]: his sympathy for Seneca Doane, his defense of radicalism at the [[athletic-club]], and his eventual ostracism by the very business class that once sustained him.
