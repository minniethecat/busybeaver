# Devon Woods

Devon Woods is an affluent suburban residential development on the outskirts of Zenith, representing the aspirational goal of the city's successful businessmen. It features both residential properties — homes for the business elite — and a country club that hosts social events including dances. The neighborhood appears at two distinct levels of Zenith society: as a mark of corporate career achievement for the older generation, and as a social playground for the younger set.

## Description

Devon Woods serves as a residential destination for Zenith's most successful businessmen. The president of the Street Traction Company was appointed minister to a foreign country following his success, while the first vice-president built a home in Devon Woods, signaling its status as the neighborhood of choice for the corporate upper echelon (019-003). This progression — from executive promotion to suburban home ownership to diplomatic appointment — maps the complete arc of bourgeois aspiration as Lewis understood it: a sequence of visible acquisitions and honors, each validating the one before.

The neighborhood is situated on the outskirts of Zenith, physically removed from the commercial downtown where its residents earn their fortunes. This spatial separation between work and home — between the office where money is made and the suburb where it is displayed — reflects the compartmentalization of middle-class life that the novel consistently documents. The commute from Devon Woods to downtown Zenith is itself a daily ritual of transition from domestic respectability to commercial competition and back again.

The development also features social amenities including a dance venue. Babbitt attended a Saturday evening dance at Devon Woods, taking Eunice Littlefield as his companion (034-034). The event places Devon Woods within the novel's late-stage social landscape, as Babbitt's rebellion brings him into the orbit of younger, more liberated company. Ted Babbitt also had social connections to the area, with a classmate living in Devon Woods (034-036). The presence of Ted's peer group there reinforces the neighborhood's dual role as both established-elite residence and site of generational social transition.

## Social Function

Devon Woods functions as a marker of economic achievement in Zenith's stratified society. Home ownership there signals arrival at the pinnacle of the business world — it is where vice-presidents and corporate leaders settle after reaching the highest rungs of their careers. The neighborhood operates as a visible token of having "arrived," the residential equivalent of a club membership or a college fraternity pin. To say "he lives in Devon Woods" is to make a complete statement about a man's standing — no further biographical detail is needed.

Simultaneously, Devon Woods serves as a social hub for the younger generation, with Ted Babbitt and his classmates frequenting its events. The juxtaposition is characteristically Lewisian: the same suburban development that represents the settled success of Zenith's corporate fathers also houses the dances and gatherings where their children begin to pull away from the values those fathers embody. Babbitt's own presence at the Devon Woods dance — escorting Eunice Littlefield, who is the daughter of his next-door neighbor — places him in an ambiguous middle position, neither fully of the old guard nor comfortably among the young. He goes to Devon Woods not as a resident but as a visitor, a man who has not "arrived" at this final suburb but who is sampling the social possibilities it offers.

The neighborhood's exclusivity is unstated but absolute. No character in the novel speaks of aspiring to live in Devon Woods; one either does or does not. This silence around the mechanisms of exclusion — there are no explicitly stated membership requirements or property covenants — is itself the mechanism. Devon Woods need not announce its exclusivity because everyone in Zenith already knows who belongs there.

## Key Scenes

### The Business Conversation (Chapter 19)

Devon Woods appears first as a name dropped in business conversation, invoked as an emblem of corporate success. The vice-president who builds a home there sits at the apex of the local commercial hierarchy, his residential choice mentioned in the same breath as the president's diplomatic appointment (019-003). The neighborhood functions here as shorthand — no description is needed because the name alone conveys everything: wealth, status, arrival.

### The Saturday Dance (Chapter 34)

In the novel's late chapters, Devon Woods shifts from an abstraction to a lived space. Babbitt escorts Eunice Littlefield to a Saturday evening dance at Devon Woods (034-034). The scene is brief but significant: Babbitt, in the midst of his midlife rebellion, has entered a social world different from the Athletic Club lunches and Boosters' Club meetings that defined his earlier life. The dance floor at Devon Woods represents a space where the generations mingle uneasily — where a middle-aged real estate man can find himself dancing with his neighbor's daughter, surrounded by the children of the very businessmen with whom he lunches.

Ted Babbitt's connection to the neighborhood — "a classmate of Ted's, out in Devon Woods" (034-036) — further layers the location with generational significance. Ted and his friends use Devon Woods as a social venue without aspiring to live there; for them, the neighborhood is a playground, not a goal. The contrast between the older generation's reverence for Devon Woods and the younger generation's casual appropriation of it captures the novel's broader theme of generational change.

## Satirical Significance

Devon Woods represents the materialistic aspirations that drive Babbitt's world. The progression from a successful business deal (the Street Traction Company's purchasing-agent buying a five-thousand-dollar car) to a vice-president building a home in Devon Woods to a president being appointed minister abroad illustrates the hierarchy of middle-class achievement that Lewis satirizes throughout the novel (019-003). Each rung on this ladder is signaled by a physical object or location — the car, the house, the diplomatic post — reducing career accomplishment to a series of visible purchases.

The neighborhood's double life as both establishment trophy and youth social scene adds a second satirical layer. Devon Woods is meant to represent the summit of respectable achievement, yet it is also where Babbitt, in the midst of his midlife rebellion, goes dancing with a woman not his wife. The contradiction between the neighborhood's symbolic meaning (arrival, stability, respectability) and its actual use in the narrative (a site of Babbitt's straying from domestic convention) is precisely the kind of irony Lewis deploys to expose the hollowness of bourgeois ideals. A neighborhood built to signify permanence becomes, in Babbitt's experience of it, a place of transgression.
