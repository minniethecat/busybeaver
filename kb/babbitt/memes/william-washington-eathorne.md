# William Washington Eathorne

William Washington Eathorne is the seventy-year-old president of the [[first-state-bank|First State Bank]] of Zenith and the great-grandson of one of the five men who founded Zenith in 1792. A third-generation banker who still wears the delicate side-whiskers that were the uniform of bankers in 1870, he represents the old-money aristocracy that stands entirely above the Smart Set of the [[charles-mckelvey|McKelveys]].

## Role in the Novel

If [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] was envious of the McKelveys' social standing, before Eathorne he was reverent. Eathorne embodies a Zenith that is "small, still, dry, polite, cruel" — a tiny hierarchy for which the other Zeniths unwittingly labor. His endorsement becomes the pinnacle of Babbitt's social climbing, and his later withdrawal of approval marks the definitive collapse of Babbitt's standing.

## Key Scenes

- **The Sunday School Advisory Committee** — Babbitt and [[t-cholmondeley-frink|Chum Frink]] call on Eathorne at his mansion, where they are reduced to flustered obedience by his quiet authority. Eathorne serves sherry and offers "very clear and very bad" ideas on Sunday School advancement.
- **The dinner at Reverend Drew's** — Eathorne accepts an invitation arranged by Babbitt, and for the first time departs from Sunday School topics to inquire about Babbitt's business — a moment Babbitt treasures as proof of intimacy with greatness.
- **The private loan** — When Babbitt needs capital for real-estate speculation, Eathorne provides a loan kept off the bank's books, framing it as an "investment in character."
- **The snub** — During Babbitt's social rebellion, Eathorne deliberately hesitates before giving Babbitt "a nod more contemptuous than a direct cut," sealing his fall from respectability.

## Character Traits

Eathorne is conservative, restrained, and softly paternal. He folds his hands on his "comfortable little belly and purred like an aged pussy." His power is not in force but in quiet exclusion — the ability to nod more contemptuously than a direct cut. He is a man who has never needed to raise his voice because his social position has always spoken for him.

## The Eathorne Mansion

The Eathorne Mansion on Royal Ridge preserves the memory of the "nice parts" of Zenith as they appeared from 1860 to 1900. It is a red brick immensity with gray sandstone lintels, its marble steps scrubbed daily and its lace curtains "as prim and superior as William Washington Eathorne himself." While most of the Victorian mansions have decayed into boarding-houses, the Eathorne Mansion remains "virtuous and aloof, reminiscent of London, Back Bay, Rittenhouse Square."
