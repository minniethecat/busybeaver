# Rutherford Snow

Colonel Rutherford Snow is the owner of the _Advocate-Times_, Zenith's leading newspaper, and a figure whose authority is rooted simultaneously in commerce, media, and the mysterious prestige of military rank. "White-bearded" and "hoar and mighty" (032-013, 034-007), he is the patriarch of Zenith's opinion-making apparatus — a man whose power is exercised not through legislation but through the shaping of what the city reads, believes, and discusses.

## Role in the Novel

Snow appears only twice in the novel's named references, but his significance far exceeds his page-time. He represents the invisible infrastructure of Zenith's conservative consensus: the newspaper that defines respectable opinion, the editorial voice that establishes the boundaries of acceptable dissent. When Babbitt begins to drift from orthodoxy — befriending Seneca Doane, expressing sympathy for strikers — it is the world that Snow represents that punishes him. The _Advocate-Times_ does not attack Babbitt directly; it simply continues to define the common sense from which Babbitt has now departed.

The honorific "Colonel" is telling. Lewis does not explain how Snow acquired it, and the omission is the point: in Zenith, military titles circulate as freely as business cards, conferring authority without requiring explanation. Snow's beard completes the iconography — white-bearded, he looks the part of the sage elder statesman, regardless of what his paper prints.

## Key Scenes

- **The Good Citizens' League** — Snow is among the "white-bearded Colonel Rutherford Snow" (032-013) associated with the founding of the Good Citizens' League, the vigilante organization that enforces conservative orthodoxy during the novel's climactic chapters. His presence legitimizes the League's activities: if the owner of the _Advocate-Times_ is on board, the cause must be respectable.

- **The Patriotic Consolidation** — Snow appears again, "the hoar and mighty Colonel Rutherford Snow, owner of the _Advocate-Times_" (034-007), as the forces of reaction consolidate in late chapters. The adjective "hoar" — meaning both white-haired and venerable — does double work: it describes both Snow's appearance and his ideological function. He is old in both senses: aged in years and ancient in the conservatism he represents.

## Character Traits

Snow is less a character than an institution in human form. His traits — patriarchal authority, venerable appearance, unquestioned prestige — are the traits of the newspaper he owns, projected onto the man. Lewis presents him without the satirical sharpness he directs at Babbitt or Frink; Snow is too powerful to be funny, and too essential to the novel's social machinery to be dismissed. He is the figure whom even the boosters must respect, because he owns the medium through which they are boosted.
