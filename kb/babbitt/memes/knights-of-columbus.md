# Knights of Columbus

## Description

The Knights of Columbus is a Catholic fraternal benefit organization with a council in Zenith. Founded in 1882 in New Haven, Connecticut, the Knights provided insurance, fellowship, and a counterweight to Protestant-dominated fraternal orders for Catholic men. In Zenith, the Knights occupy a distinctive position: they are listed alongside the Protestant-dominated Elks, Moose, Red Men, and Odd Fellows as legitimate male organizations (014-034), signaling the limited but real religious pluralism of the business class — a pluralism united by commerce rather than divided by theology.

## Social Function

The Knights of Columbus serves the same functions as the other fraternal orders in Zenith — insurance, ritual, social networking — but with the added dimension of Catholic identity. In the predominantly Protestant social landscape of Zenith, the Knights provide Catholic businessmen with a fraternal home that does not require them to suppress their religious identity. At the same time, the Knights' inclusion in the roll-call of accepted organizations (014-034) suggests that the business class is willing to overlook religious difference in the service of commercial solidarity.

The Knights also appear among the civic bodies represented in the mayor's Good Citizens' League initiative (016-004), which aimed to consolidate the city's respectable organizations into a single force for social order. Their participation in this ecumenical project of social control is telling: even Catholic fraternalism, in Zenith, can be mobilized in defense of the business-class status quo.

## Key Scenes

The Knights of Columbus appear only in the novel's recurring catalogs of fraternal organizations, most notably:

- In Chapter XIV, where they are listed among the organizations whose membership defines the "Regular Guy": "the Elks or Moose or Red Men or Knights of Columbus or any one of a score of organizations" (014-034).
- In Chapter XVI, where they appear in the still more exhaustive catalog of "Maccabees, Knights of Pythias, Knights of Columbus, and other secret orders" (016-004) that form the fraternal infrastructure of Zenith.

## Satirical Significance

The inclusion of the Knights of Columbus in Lewis's catalog of Zenith clubs serves a double satirical function. First, it establishes the sheer breadth of the fraternal ecosystem — even the city's Catholic minority has its own order, and even that order is folded into the same booster machinery. Second, it subtly acknowledges the religious diversity that the booster ideology otherwise ignores: by listing the Knights alongside the Protestant-dominated orders, Lewis concedes that Zenith is not quite as homogenously Protestant as its boosters pretend.

Yet the satire also cuts the other way. The Knights are listed without distinction from the Elks, Moose, or Red Men — they are, in the novel's treatment, just another fraternal order among many. Their Catholic particularity is absorbed into the general category of "male organization," their religious identity rendered irrelevant by the commercial fellowship that is the true religion of Zenith.
