# Regency Hotel

A Zenith hotel that appears during Babbitt's midlife rebellion as a setting for solitary dinners and small acts of independence. It is one of several urban spaces where Babbitt experiments with a life outside his home on Floral Heights.

## Description

The Regency Hotel is a downtown Zenith hotel notable for its "Crystal Versailles Room" (019-030), a dining room whose pretentious name signals aspirational elegance. Babbitt dines there on "Blue Point" oysters, indulging in the small luxuries available to a man of his means when he steps away from his domestic routine.

The hotel lobby and cigar-counter (019-046) provide the kind of anonymous, semi-public space where Babbitt can experience a version of urban freedom — talking to strangers, lingering without purpose, behaving in ways that would be noted and judged in his neighborhood.

## Social Function

The Regency Hotel serves as an escape valve from domesticity. When Babbitt "dined by himself at the Regency Hotel" (019-032), the solitude is significant: he is not at home with Myra, not at the Athletic Club with his booster peers, but alone in a hotel dining room, watching the city pass. The hotel offers the anonymity that Zenith's neighborhoods and clubs do not.

The cigar-counter conversation (019-046) is a small but meaningful social transaction — the kind of casual, unmonitored exchange that Babbitt's usual routines do not permit. In the hotel, he is not George Babbitt of Floral Heights and the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company; he is just a man having a cigar and a conversation.

## Key Scenes

- **Solitary dinner** — Babbitt dines alone in the Crystal Versailles Room, ordering Blue Point oysters, the meal a modest assertion of independence from domestic routine (019-030)
- **Lobby lingering** — After dinner, Babbitt "sat in the lobby afterward" (019-032), watching the hotel's transient population, experiencing a form of urban solitude
- **Cigar-counter chat** — Babbitt falls into conversation at the cigar-counter (019-046), the casual encounter a contrast to the ritualized sociability of his clubs

## Satirical Significance

The Regency Hotel is a minor site of Babbitt's rebellion, but its very modesty is the satirical point. The "Crystal Versailles Room" — a name of delusional grandeur — is where Babbitt's escape from domestic life takes the form of a plate of oysters and a cigar. The rebellion that will later threaten his marriage and his social standing begins in spaces as mundane as a hotel lobby.

Lewis suggests that for a man like Babbitt, freedom is not found in grand gestures but in small, anonymous spaces where identity loosens. The hotel is neither home nor office nor club — it is a third place where Babbitt can be momentarily undefined.
