# Don't Make Prohibition a Joke Association

A prohibition advocacy organization whose name Lewis deploys as a satirical flourish, reflecting the absurdity of organized moral reform in 1920s America. The group appears only once in the novel, but its name alone carries the full weight of Lewis's critique of the humorless bureaucratic approach to moral legislation.

## Description

The Don't Make Prohibition a Joke Association is mentioned once in the novel, when George Babbitt anxiously notes that representatives of the organization are coming to see him: "the Don't Make Prohibition a Joke Association coming to see me in a quarter of an hour" (034-015). The name itself is Lewis's satirical invention: its self-serious earnestness ("Don't Make Prohibition a Joke") inadvertently calls attention to the very possibility it seeks to forestall — that Prohibition might indeed be a joke. The more urgently the association insists that Prohibition is not a laughing matter, the more it suggests that laughter is precisely the appropriate response.

No further details about the organization's membership, activities, or agenda appear in the text. The group exists in the novel purely as a name and a threat — a disembodied voice of moral surveillance that can summon Babbitt to account for his conduct. This narrative minimalism is itself a satirical technique: the organization needs no description because its name already says everything Lewis wants to say about the culture of organized moral reform.

The historical context deepens the satire. Prohibition, enacted through the Eighteenth Amendment in 1920, was widely flouted in American life by the time of the novel's publication in 1922. Organizations that insisted on the law's solemnity were fighting a rearguard action against widespread public contempt. The association's name thus captures a historical moment when the defenders of Prohibition had become objects of ridicule even as they demanded to be taken seriously.

## Social Function

The association's function in the narrative is to dramatize Babbitt's concern with reputation management during his period of social rebellion. At the point when the organization is mentioned, Babbitt is navigating the fallout from his brief flirtation with liberalism and his association with Seneca Doane. The prospect of being visited by moral crusaders represents the social surveillance that enforces conformity in Zenith — even a satirically named group carries the threat of public censure (034-015).

The timing of the association's visit is significant. It comes in the novel's late chapters, when Babbitt's rebellion is collapsing under the weight of social pressure. The Good Citizens' League has already begun its campaign against him; his business is suffering; his wife has been ill. The Don't Make Prohibition a Joke Association arrives as one more voice in the chorus of respectability demanding that Babbitt return to the fold. That the association's name is ridiculous does not diminish its power to intimidate — if anything, the absurdity of moral reform organizations makes them more menacing, because there is no reasoning with people who cannot recognize their own ridiculousness.

The visit also illustrates the decentralized nature of social control in Zenith. The association is not a government body; it has no legal authority. Its power is entirely social — the power to visit, to inquire, to report, to make Babbitt's name the subject of disapproving conversation. In a community where reputation is currency, this informal surveillance is more effective than any legal sanction could be.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's Anxiety** — In the later chapters of the novel, Babbitt mentions "the Don't Make Prohibition a Joke Association coming to see me in a quarter of an hour," revealing his nervousness about his standing with Zenith's moral establishment. The visit is one element in the converging pressures that will ultimately force Babbitt to abandon his brief rebellion and rejoin the ranks of Zenith's solid citizens (034-015).

## Satirical Significance

The name of the organization is a compact example of Lewis's satirical method: by simply reporting the organization's title without commentary, he allows its inherent absurdity to indict the culture that produced it. The association embodies the humorless, bureaucratic approach to moral reform that the novel consistently mocks — prohibition enforced not through moral persuasion but through organized vigilance, through the implied threat of the visit, the inquiry, the report.

The organization's name operates on multiple satirical levels simultaneously. First, it reveals the anxiety underlying the Prohibition movement — the fear that the law is not taken seriously, that it is indeed "a joke." Second, it demonstrates the bureaucratic instinct to form a committee in response to any perceived problem, even one as intangible as public ridicule. Third, and most devastatingly, the name's sheer clumsiness — its earnest, multi-word, capital-letter self-importance — embodies everything Lewis found contemptible in the reformist mentality: the absence of humor, the distrust of pleasure, the reduction of complex social issues to slogans enforced by committees.

The association also serves as a counterpoint to the Good Citizens' League. Where the League represents organized business conservatism — the enforcement of conformity through economic pressure — the Don't Make Prohibition a Joke Association represents organized moral reform — the enforcement of conformity through social surveillance. Together, they form the twin pillars of the social control apparatus that Lewis saw operating in middle-American life: commerce and morality, each reinforcing the other, each demanding that the individual conform or face the consequences.
