# Alcohol

In Prohibition-era Zenith, alcohol serves as the forbidden lubricant of male social life — openly consumed in private clubs, publicly condemned from pulpits, and symbolic of the gap between the city's professed morality and its actual behavior.

## Definition in Context

Despite the legal prohibition of alcohol, drinking is pervasive among Zenith's businessmen. Babbitt plays poker at Vergil Gunch's "till midnight" (001-015), sustained by "the tremendous home-brewed beer of the prohibition-era and the cigars to which that beer enticed him" (001-015). Alcohol is the secret sacrament of masculine fellowship, consumed in defiance of the very laws that Babbitt and his peers publicly support.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The poker game ritual** — Babbitt's late-night poker sessions involve heavy drinking: the "tremendous home-brewed beer of the prohibition-era" (001-015) fuels the bonding that business and church cannot provide.
- **Public hypocrisy** — Babbitt supports Prohibition as a matter of public policy while regularly drinking in private, embodying the contradiction at the heart of Zenith's moral culture.
- **Alcohol as escape** — Drinking is Babbitt's primary mechanism for escaping the dissatisfaction of his daily life. The "beer and cigars" (001-015) provide temporary relief from "the grind of the real-estate business" (001-015).
- **The morning after** — After drinking, Babbitt is "irritable before breakfast" (001-015), and his hangovers become a physical manifestation of the moral hangover of his double life — the cost of the escape alcohol provides.

## Key Passages

> He sulkily admitted now that there was no more escape, but he lay and detested the grind of the real-estate business, and disliked his family, and disliked himself for disliking them. The evening before, he had played poker at Vergil Gunch's till midnight, and after such holidays he was irritable before breakfast. It may have been the tremendous home-brewed beer of the prohibition-era and the cigars to which that beer enticed him. (001-015)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses alcohol to expose the hypocrisy of Prohibition-era America. Babbitt and his peers publicly defend the law while privately flouting it, a contradiction that mirrors the larger gap between Zenith's professed morality and its actual behavior. The "home-brewed beer of the prohibition-era" is both a genuine pleasure and a symbol of moral compromise — a small rebellion that, unlike Babbitt's later political rebellion, carries no social cost because everyone participates in it. Lewis suggests that this kind of contained, ritualized transgression actually serves to reinforce conformity rather than challenge it.
