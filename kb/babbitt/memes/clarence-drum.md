# Clarence Drum

Clarence Drum is a shoe merchant of Zenith and a member of [[athletic-club|the Athletic Club]] — "a round and merry man" who tells stories and who "strangely resembled a Victorian pug-dog." During the general strike, he transforms into a waddling but ferocious militia captain who becomes a point of contention in Babbitt's faltering social conformity.

## Role in the Novel

Drum embodies the comic militarism of the Zenith business class when confronted with labor unrest. A man whose peacetime authority extends no further than shoe sales, he inflates into a strutting captain the moment he dons a uniform — and Babbitt's public criticism of him becomes one of the precipitating incidents in Babbitt's social ostracism.

## Key Scenes

- **The Athletic Club confrontation** — During the strike, Drum comes "pee-rading up" to the table at the Athletic Club, "all dolled up fit to kill in his nice lil cap'n's uniform." When someone asks about busting the strike, "he swells up like a pouter-pigeon" and boasts that he told the strike-leaders where to get off. Babbitt, uncharacteristically, contradicts him: "Oh, rats, Clarence, I looked 'em all over carefully, and they didn't have any more bombs 'n a rabbit." (028-020)
- **On the strike front** — Drum appears as a "waddling but ferocious captain, with his belt tight about his comfortable little belly, and his round little mouth petulant as he piped to chattering groups on corners: 'Move on there now! I can't have any of this loitering!'" (027-004)
- **Babbitt's admiration turns to criticism** — Watching the strike, Babbitt at first respectfully clamors "Great work, Captain!" as Drum rages by. But later he grumbles that the strikers "got just as much right to march as anybody else! They own the streets as much as Clarence Drum or the American Legion does!" [027-018, 027-023]
- **The offended Drum** — At the Athletic Club after Babbitt's comments, Drum swings by "splendid in khaki," and [[joseph-k-pumphrey|Professor Pumphrey]] later notes, "You hurt Clarence's feelings, George." [[vergil-gunch|Vergil Gunch]] listens to Babbitt's stammering explanation in intimidating silence. [027-023, 027-028]
- **Babbitt's retrospective regret** — During his social isolation, Babbitt wails to himself: "Good Lord, what have I done? Just played with the Bunch, and called down Clarence Drum about being such a high-and-mighty sodger." (032-032)

## Character Traits

Drum is a creature of the Athletic Club — jolly, story-telling, and entirely conventional. His transformation into a militia captain is Lewis's satire on the ease with which the business class assumes martial authority during crises. Drum's pug-dog appearance — round body, round mouth, waddling gait — makes his ferocity comic rather than threatening, but the comedy has a sharp edge: men like Drum, Lewis implies, are dangerously unserious about the power they wield.
