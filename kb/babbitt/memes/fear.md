# Fear

If conformity is the visible architecture of Zenith society, fear is its invisible foundation. Lewis portrays a world in which the fear of social judgment — of being thought odd, irregular, or "queer" — is so pervasive that it requires no enforcement because it has been internalized as conscience.

## Definition in Context

Fear in *Babbitt* is rarely named directly but is everywhere present — in the morning rituals that dare not vary, in the business handshakes that conceal mutual suspicion, in the dinner-party conversations that circle endlessly around safe topics. It is not fear of violence or poverty but fear of exposure: the terror of being seen without the armor of respectability. When Babbitt briefly rebels, he experiences a moment of liberation from "the oppressive fear of criticism" (028-002) — and the relief is so intense precisely because the fear has been so constant.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Fear of the unconventional** — Throughout the novel, the word "queer" functions as a term of social quarantine. To be thought "queer" — to hold unusual opinions, to read foreign poetry, to question the established order — is the worst fate Zenith can imagine.
- **The fear economy** — Zenith's institutions — the Boosters' Club, the Good Citizens' League, the church — all trade in fear: fear of exclusion, fear of economic reprisal, fear of being talked about. Membership is purchased not with conviction but with the surrender of independence.
- **Babbitt's hidden terror** — Beneath his boosterish confidence, Babbitt harbors a "fearful" recognition of his own emptiness. The fear of being discovered — as a fraud, as a failure, as a man who does not believe what he says — is the novel's great unspoken subject.
- **Fear as social control** — The Good Citizens' League operates not through violence but through fear: the fear of losing clients (029-014), the fear of being excluded from clubs, the fear of whispered conversations at the next table.
- **Liberation from fear** — Babbitt's rebellion, brief as it is, gives him his first taste of life without "the oppressive fear of criticism" (028-002). The tragedy is that this liberation proves unsustainable — fear reclaims him.

## Key Passages

> The oppressive fear of criticism was gone, but a loneliness was about him that was colder than fear. (028-002, paraphrased)

> His predominant fear — not from any especial fondness for his wife but from the dread of being alone — was that Myra might leave him. (029-014, paraphrased)

> The evening before his marriage was an agony, and the morning after was a dull misery — a fear of the whole woman thing that went cold and fearful through him. (006-081, paraphrased)

## Satirical Function

Lewis's treatment of fear is quietly devastating: he shows that the celebrated confidence of the American businessman is a thin membrane stretched over a deep reservoir of anxiety. Babbitt is afraid of his wife, afraid of his peers, afraid of his children, afraid of being alone, afraid of being seen — and the boosterism that is his public identity is, at its root, a defensive strategy against these fears. The novel's satire is not that Babbitt is a coward but that Zenith has made courage impossible — that a civilization built on conformity cannot produce the independence it claims to celebrate, because independence requires the capacity to tolerate fear, and Zenith has made fear intolerable.
