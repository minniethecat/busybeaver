# Old Colony Theatre

A Zenith entertainment venue whose newspaper advertisement for the burlesque troupe "The Wrollicking Wrens" provides a moment of coarse masculine laughter at a real-estate convention dinner, illustrating the kind of popular culture that Babbitt and his business associates consume — and the gap between Zenith's pretensions to civic culture and its actual entertainment preferences.

## Description

The Old Colony Theatre is a burlesque house operating in Zenith, referenced in the novel through a single print advertisement rather than through direct depiction of the venue itself. Its name — invoking the colonial heritage of New England — contrasts ironically with the risqué character of the entertainment it purveys. The theatre belongs to the commercial entertainment district of a city whose cultural life spans from the Château movie palace to vaudeville houses to burlesque theatres, each rung on the ladder catering to a different audience and a different level of social respectability.

The advertisement for "The Wrollicking Wrens" — with its deliberately misspelled name, alliterative tagline ("the bonniest bevy of beauteous bathing babes in burlesque"), and cornball humor ("Shake the Old Dogs to the WROLLICKING WRENS") — exemplifies the promotional vernacular that Lewis satirizes throughout the novel as the degraded language of American commercial culture (013-065).

## Social Function

The Old Colony Theatre's advertisement serves a social function in the novel that has little to do with the theatre itself. Discovered by Babbitt in the *Monarch Herald* during a dinner at the annual real-estate convention, the ad becomes a prop for masculine bonding: Babbitt reads it aloud "to applause and laughter" from his table of businessmen (013-065). The moment typifies the convention's social rituals, in which coarse humor and shared amusement at sexual innuendo reinforce the homosocial bonds among the delegates.

The advertisement also functions as a marker of cultural taste. The businessmen's enthusiastic reception of the ad — their applause — identifies them as consumers of burlesque rather than, say, the symphony concerts that Babbitt occasionally affects to appreciate. The Old Colony Theatre thus helps Lewis establish the distance between Zenith's official culture (lectures, church, civic pageantry) and its actual culture (burlesque, movies, vaudeville, and the rituals of commercial conviviality).

## Entertainment in Zenith

The Old Colony Theatre belongs to the landscape of popular entertainment in Zenith — a city whose amusements include motion-picture theaters like the Château, vaudeville houses, and burlesque shows. Burlesque, with its combination of broad comedy and risqué performance, occupies the lower rung of Zenith's cultural ladder: beneath the "highbrow" lectures and symphony concerts that some characters affect to appreciate, but far more honestly enjoyed by the businessmen of the Boosters' Club.

The advertisement's style — with its calculated orthographic playfulness ("Wrollicking") and its promise of "beauteous bathing babes" — represents the register of popular entertainment promotion that Lewis treats as symptomatic of a broader debasement of language in commercial America. Words, in this register, are deployed not for precision but for sensation; they promise pleasure while delivering cliché.

## Key Scenes

- **The convention dinner (Ch. XIII)** — During the annual real-estate convention in Monarch, Babbitt discovers the Old Colony Theatre's advertisement in the *Monarch Herald* and reads it aloud to his table of businessmen, earning "applause and laughter" and reinforcing the masculine conviviality of the convention's social rituals (013-065)

## Satirical Significance

The Old Colony Theatre episode illustrates Lewis's technique of satirizing American culture through its own commercial artifacts. The advertisement is presented verbatim, without authorial comment; the satire inheres in the juxtaposition between the ad's bombastic vulgarity and the businessmen's delighted reception of it. Babbitt and his associates, who present themselves as civic leaders and "Solid Citizens," are revealed in this moment as consumers of the coarsest popular entertainment — a revelation that complicates the novel's portrait of middle-class respectability.

The theatre's colonial-evoking name ("Old Colony") superimposed on its burlesque function creates an implicit satire of the American tendency to dignify low pursuits with high-sounding historical references — a pattern that runs throughout the novel, from the pseudo-classical architecture of Zenith's public buildings to the inflated rhetoric of its booster literature.
