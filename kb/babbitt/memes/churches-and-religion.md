# Churches and Religious Life

Religion in Zenith is a civic institution as much as a spiritual one. The Chatham Road Presbyterian Church, where Babbitt's family occupies a proprietary pew, is less a place of worship than a social club with hymns. Lewis's satire of middle-class Protestantism — its comfortable certainties, its equation of prosperity with piety, its pastor who doubles as a publicity man — runs through the novel. ## About Religion in Zenith

The religious world of *Babbitt* is thoroughly domesticated. Protestant Christianity, as practiced on Chatham Road, has been absorbed into the same booster machinery that sells real estate and elects aldermen. The Reverend John Jennison Drew preaches a gospel of "punch" and "pep"; the Sunday School press issues promotional pamphlets; and Babbitt's own faith, such as it is, consists of a vague confidence that God approves of prosperity. Lewis's satire here is not anti-religious but anti-hypocrisy — a critique of a faith that has forgotten its own radical roots.
