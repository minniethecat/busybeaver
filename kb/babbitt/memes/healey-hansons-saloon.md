# Healey Hanson's Saloon

## Description

Healey Hanson's Saloon was an illicit drinking establishment located on Front Street in the Old Town district of Zenith. Operating during the Prohibition era, it was a cramped, unglamorous venue—"a place curiously like the saloons of ante-prohibition days, with a long greasy bar"(008-015)—where Zenith's citizens, both high and low, obtained bootleg alcohol. The saloon was run by Healey Hanson, an elderly man with "burning brown trousers"(008-017) who maintained an air of weary authority over the establishment.

> "a cocaine-runner and a prostitute were drinking cocktails in Healey Hanson's saloon on Front Street. Since national prohibition was now in force, and since Zenith was notoriously law-abiding, they were compelled to keep the cocktails innocent by drinking them out of tea-cups." (007-035)

## Social Function

Despite—or because of—Prohibition, Healey Hanson's served as an equalizer in Zenith's stratified society. On any given evening, "a cocaine-runner and a prostitute were drinking cocktails in Healey Hanson's saloon on Front Street"(007-035)—compelled, by the pretense of law-abidingness, "to keep the cocktails innocent by drinking them out of tea-cups"(007-035). The same establishment that served the criminal underworld also catered to Zenith's most prominent businessmen. George Babbitt himself parked "a block from Healey Hanson's saloon, worrying, 'Well, rats, if anybody did see me, they'd think I was here on business'"(008-015).

The saloon embodied the hypocrisy of Prohibition-era America: laws were universally acknowledged, universally violated, and the violation was conducted with an elaborate pantomime of discretion that fooled no one.

## Key Scenes

**Babbitt's First Visit (Chapter VIII)** — Preparing for a dinner party, Babbitt drove into the tangled byways of Old Town—"jagged blocks filled with sooty warehouses and lofts; on into The Arbor, once a pleasant orchard but now a morass of lodging-houses, tenements, and brothels"(008-015)—to procure alcohol. He presented his business card to the bartender with elaborate ceremony. The bartender "held it as though it weighed ten pounds, and read it as though it were a hundred words long"(008-017) before summoning Healey Hanson from the back room.

**Tanis's Errand (Chapter XXIX)** — During Babbitt's involvement with Tanis Judique and the Bunch, Tanis sent him on an errand to replenish her liquor supply: "Dear, wouldn't you like to do something for me? I'm all out of booze, and the Bunch want to celebrate. Couldn't you just skip down to Healey Hanson's and get some?"(029-027) The errand, undertaken with the condescending companionship of Min Sonntag, underscored Babbitt's diminished status within the Bunch and his growing resentment of their pretensions.

**The Saloon as Escape (Chapter XXIX)** — Later, Babbitt sat "in the back room of a saloon, and Babbitt had a headache, was confused"(029-052), accompanying younger men on a sordid expedition with "a grubby shrieking cash-girl from Parcher and Stein's"(029-052). The scene marked the nadir of Babbitt's rebellion—a far cry from the dignified domesticity of Floral Heights.

## Satirical Significance

Healey Hanson's Saloon functions as a satirical mirror of Zenith's moral pretensions. Prohibition was enforced in name only; the city was "notoriously law-abiding"(007-035), yet its citizens drank freely—so long as they observed the ritual of discretion. The saloon's clientele, ranging from drug dealers to real-estate brokers, revealed the unacknowledged common ground between Zenith's respectable and criminal classes. Babbitt's furtive visits to the "vile and smelly old saloon"(008-032) dramatized the gap between his public persona as a solid businessman and his private appetites.
