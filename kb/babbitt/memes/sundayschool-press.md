# Chatham Road Sunday School Press Office

## Description

The Sunday School press office is the publicity apparatus of the Chatham Road Presbyterian Church's Sunday School program, operated by Kenneth Escott. The office functions as a miniature public-relations agency within the church, producing "a trickle of insinuating items about neighborliness and the Bible, about class-suppers, jolly but educational, and the value of the Prayer-life in attaining financial success" (017-032). It represents the complete merger of religious instruction, commercial publicity, and business ideology that Lewis presents as characteristic of 1920s American Protestantism.

## Social Function

The press office serves a dual purpose: it promotes church attendance through modern advertising techniques while simultaneously reinforcing the business gospel that equates financial success with divine favor. Escott's items about "the value of the Prayer-life in attaining financial success" (017-032) reduce prayer to a productivity technique — a spiritual counterpart to the business-efficiency manuals that Babbitt admires. The press office is, in effect, the public-relations department for a version of Christianity that has been thoroughly domesticated to serve commercial interests.

## Key Scenes

- **Escott's Operation (Chapter XVII)**: The press office is described as Escott's domain, where he procures "insinuating items" that blur the boundary between religious news and business propaganda. The verb "insinuating" is carefully chosen — these are not overt advertisements but subtle suggestions that religion and prosperity are natural partners (017-032).
- **The Sunday School Advisory Committee**: Babbitt serves on this committee, and its meetings take place in "the highly varnished Sunday School room" (016-026). The varnish itself is a telling detail — the room is polished to a commercial shine, as befits a religion that has been thoroughly merchandised.
- **Babbitt's Discovery**: Through the committee work, Babbitt "discovered a world of Sunday School journals, an enormous and bustling world" (016-028) — a revelation that the business of religion is itself a substantial business, with its own periodicals, conventions, and supply chains. The Sunday School press office is a node in this national network.

## Satirical Significance

The Sunday School press office is one of Lewis's most concentrated satirical creations, embodying in a single image the fusion of three forces that the novel critiques: organized religion, commercial publicity, and the ideology of success. Escott's "insinuating items" are not lies — they are worse than lies; they are the reduction of spiritual truth to advertising copy. The formula "the value of the Prayer-life in attaining financial success" (017-032) is a perfect specimen of booster Christianity: prayer is valued instrumentally, for its cash yield, and the Prayer-life (with its self-conscious capitalization) can be advertised like any other product. The press office is the logical endpoint of a religion that has made its peace with Mammon.

Babbitt's own relationship to the Sunday School exposes the contradiction: he is an indifferent churchgoer who skips Sunday services when convenient, yet he serves on the Advisory Committee, reads the Sunday School journals, and takes pride in the church's institutional success. The press office, like the church itself, matters to him not as a spiritual resource but as a social credential — another box checked on the checklist of the "Regular Guy."
