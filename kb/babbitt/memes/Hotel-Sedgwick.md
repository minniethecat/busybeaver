## Description

The Hotel Sedgwick is a hotel in Monarch, the state capital and Zenith's chief rival city, where George Babbitt stays during the annual state convention of real estate boards (013-032). The hotel represents the mid-range business accommodation typical of the traveling salesman and middle-manager class. It is not as grand as the Allen House (where the convention meetings themselves are held), nor as modest as the Eden Hotel where Zenith businessmen stay in Chicago (013-034) (019-030).

## Babbitt's Stay

Babbitt arrives in Monarch after an overnight Pullman journey, reveling in the luxury of a private washroom and the services of a valet, to whom he gives "a tip of a dollar and a half" (013-030). Disembarking at Monarch in "newly pressed clothes, with the adoring porter carrying his suit-case," Babbitt "rather hoped that he was being noticed" (013-031).

He shares a room at the Hotel Sedgwick with W. A. Rogers, "that shrewd, rustic-looking Zenith dealer in farm-lands" (013-032). Together they enjoy "a noble breakfast, with waffles, and coffee not in exiguous cups but in large pots." In an expansive mood, Babbitt "gave a bellboy a quarter to fetch a morning newspaper from the lobby, and sent to Tinka a post-card: 'Papa wishes you were here to bat round with him'" (013-032). This moment captures Babbitt's sense of freedom and self-importance while away from Zenith on business — a temporary escape from the domestic routines he finds increasingly stifling.

## Key Scenes

### The Real Estate Convention

The convention meetings are held not at the Sedgwick itself but at the ballroom of the Allen House (013-034). The convention exhibits feature "plans of the new suburbs of Sparta, pictures of the new state capitol, at Galop de Vache, and large ears of corn with the label, 'Nature's Gold, from Shelby County, the Garden Spot of God's Own Country'" (013-035). The real action, however, happens informally: "The real convention consisted of men muttering in hotel bedrooms or in groups amid the badge-spotted crowd in the hotel-lobby" (013-035). The Hotel Sedgwick thus serves as the social infrastructure of the convention — the liminal space where deals are made and relationships maintained between the formal sessions.

### Dinner in the Moorish Grillroom

The delegates dine together in the Hotel Sedgwick's Moorish Grillroom, an exotic-themed restaurant typical of 1920s American hotel design (013-062). The choice of "Moorish" décor reflects the period's fashion for Orientalist styling as a marker of cosmopolitan sophistication, however superficial.

### Entertainment of Delegates

Throughout the convention, delegates are "entertained, incessantly and firmly" (013-039). The Monarch Chamber of Commerce gives them a banquet, the Manufacturers' Association an afternoon reception "at which a chrysanthemum was presented to each of the ladies, and to each of the men a leather bill-fold inscribed 'From Monarch the Mighty Motor Mart'" (013-039). While these entertainments occur at various venues, the Hotel Sedgwick is the delegates' home base — the place they return to after the boosterish pageantry.

## Social Function

The Hotel Sedgwick functions as a temporary community for the real-estate men away from their home cities. In the hotel lobby, "badge-spotted" delegates from rival cities — Pioneer with their Elks banner, Galop de Vache with their sundial-climbing leader — mingle and compete in displays of civic pride (013-035) (013-039). The hotel is the neutral ground where the actual business of the convention — the whispered consultations, the impromptu agreements — takes place outside the formal program. It also serves as a backdrop for Babbitt's performed masculinity: the generous tipping, the expansive breakfast conversation, the postcard to his daughter that frames his absence as adventure rather than work.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Hotel Sedgwick and the Monarch convention to satirize the rituals of American business culture. The convention itself is a masterpiece of performative productivity: "the chairman of the executive committee" is "the busiest man in the convention; he was so busy that he got nothing done whatever" (013-034). While earnest speeches denounce cooperative stores and celebrate plate-glass prices (013-037), the real convention happens in hotel bedrooms and lobbies — a social ritual dressed as professional development.

The hotel also reveals the class stratification of business travel: Babbitt's enjoyment of the valet, the private washroom, and the quarter-tipped bellboy all mark him as a man who has arrived at a certain income level, yet the Hotel Sedgwick is not the finest hotel in Monarch. Babbitt's pleasure in these modest luxuries — and his hope "that he was being noticed" (013-031) — exposes the status anxiety beneath the booster's confident exterior.
