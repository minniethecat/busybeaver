# Eunice Littlefield

Eunice Littlefield is the daughter of [[howard-littlefield|Dr. Howard Littlefield]] and a friend of the [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] children. At sixteen, she is "interested in no statistics save those regarding the ages and salaries of motion-picture stars" (003-008). [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] notes with approval that "she was her father's daughter" — meaning that, like her father, she has found a way to deploy intelligence in the service of triviality.

## Role in the Novel

Eunice represents the emerging youth [[art|culture]] of the 1920s — a generation whose rebellion takes the form of popular [[art|culture]] consumption rather than intellectual questioning. Her interest in movie stars marks a cultural shift that the novel registers but does not explore in depth.

## Key Scenes

- **The neighborly observation** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s remark that "she was her father's daughter" (003-008) captures the way [[zenith|Zenith]]'s value system reproduces itself across generations, even as the content of those values shifts from economics to entertainment.
