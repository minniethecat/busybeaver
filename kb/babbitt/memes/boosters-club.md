# Boosters' Club

The central civic organization of Zenith's business class and the institutional heart of the booster ideology that the novel anatomizes. Its lapel button — "Boosters—Pep!" — makes [[george-babbitt]] "feel loyal and important. It associated him with Good Fellows, with men who were nice and human, and important in business circles" (001-042). The button is "his V.C., his Legion of Honor ribbon, his Phi Beta Kappa key" (001-042) — the highest decoration in the religion of [[business]]. The Club is at once a genuinely meaningful community and a mechanism of social control, and Lewis never resolves this ambivalence.

## Role in the Novel

The Boosters' Club is the novel's primary institution for examining the relationship between individual identity and collective belonging. Through the Club, Lewis shows how civic boosterism functions as both genuine social bond and ideological enforcement mechanism. Babbitt's relationship to the Club tracks his character arc: he begins as an enthusiastic congregant, delivering the speech that crowns his booster career; during his [[rebellion]], he criticizes the Club's leadership and is nearly ostracized; in his retreat, he returns to the fold, though with a private awareness he lacked before. The Club is where Babbitt's public self is most fully performed and most thoroughly hollow.

## Key Scenes

- **The button** (Chapter I) — Babbitt's morning ritual includes pinning on his Boosters' button, which makes him "feel loyal and important" (001-042). The button is the novel's most concentrated symbol of the way material objects confer spiritual identity in the business religion.

- **The real-estate convention speech** (Chapter XIII) — Babbitt's address, proclaiming that if Zenith doesn't reach "at least tenth" in population rankings he'll be the first to call himself a failure, is the apotheosis of booster rhetoric — and a devastating exposure of its intellectual emptiness.

- **The rebellion** (Chapter XXIX) — When Babbitt begins to question Zenith's orthodoxies, the Boosters' Club becomes the instrument of his social punishment. [[vergil-gunch]] leads the campaign to ostracize him, and the fellowship that once sustained Babbitt becomes the mechanism of his discipline.

## Relationship to the Business Religion

The Boosters' Club is the liturgical arm of the "religion of business" (001-052). If the business tower is the temple, the Boosters' Club button is the sacrament — a physical object that confers spiritual belonging. If [[howard-littlefield]] is the theologian, the Club's speakers (including Babbitt himself) are the preachers, delivering homilies on the gospel of Growth. The Club illustrates Lewis's central satirical thesis: that American civic life has been hollowed out by commercial ideology, that boosterism has replaced citizenship, and that community has been reduced to membership.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Boosters' Club to anatomize the relationship between community and conformity. The Club provides Babbitt with genuine fellowship — the lunches, the speeches, the sense of belonging to something larger than himself — and it is precisely this fellowship that makes the Club's enforcement of orthodoxy so effective. Babbitt cannot reject the Club's ideology without also rejecting the community that sustains him. The Club is Lewis's most complete picture of how social institutions can be both genuinely valuable and deeply corrupting — often in the same gesture.

> The club button made him feel loyal and important. It associated him with Good Fellows, with men who were nice and human, and important in business circles. It was his V.C., his Legion of Honor ribbon, his Phi Beta Kappa key. (001-042)
