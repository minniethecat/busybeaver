# George F. Babbitt

The central figure of Sinclair Lewis's *Babbitt* (1922), whose name became a cultural shorthand for the complacent, conformist American businessman. A successful [[zenith|Zenith]] real-estate broker outwardly devoted to the "religion of [[business]]," Babbitt privately harbors a discontent that erupts into a brief [[rebellion]] — only to be reabsorbed into the very conformity he briefly escaped. His story is both a satirical portrait of 1920s middle-class America and a surprisingly tender account of one man's thwarted search for meaning.

## Biography

George F. Babbitt is "forty-six years old now, in April, 1920, and he made nothing in particular, neither butter nor shoes nor poetry, but he was nimble in the calling of selling houses for more than people could afford to pay" (001-008). A partner in the firm of [[babbitt-thompson-realty|Babbitt-Thompson Realty]], he lives in the [[floral-heights|Floral Heights]] suburb of [[zenith]] with his wife [[myra-babbitt]] and their three children — [[verona-babbitt|Verona]], Ted, and [[tinka-babbitt|Tinka]]. He is a deacon at the [[chatham-road-presbyterian|Chatham Road Presbyterian Church]], a graduate of the State university, and a member in good standing of the [[boosters-club|Boosters' Club]], the [[athletic-club]], and [[elks-club|the Elks]]. His morning ritual — waking on his sleeping-porch to the sound of his oil-burner, admiring his "standard, advertised wares of the bathroom," and donning his "spectacles, huge, circular, frameless lenses of the very best glass" (001-037) — defines him as "the modern business man" before he has spoken a single word.

## Role in the Novel

Babbitt is both protagonist and satirical target, a dual role that gives the novel its distinctive tonal complexity. Lewis uses him to anatomize the American middle-class businessman of the 1920s — his values, his rituals, his aspirational vocabulary, and his carefully concealed dissatisfaction. The novel traces a three-part arc: **conformity** (Chapters I–V), in which Babbitt performs the role of ideal [[zenith|Zenith]] businessman with mechanical precision; **rebellion** (Chapters VI–XXIX), precipitated by [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling's]] arrest and culminating in Babbitt's affair with [[tanis-judique]] and his sympathy for striking [[labor|workers]]; and **retreat** (Chapters XXX–XXXIV), in which the threat of social ostracism drives him back into "belligerent conformity" (034-029) — though, crucially, the retreat is not total.

## Key Scenes

- **The morning routine** (Chapter I) — The novel opens with Babbitt waking in his "sleeping-porch" to the mechanical sounds of his modern house. His pride in his technological possessions is undercut by the private confession: "he lay and detested the grind of the real-estate business, and disliked his family, and disliked himself for disliking them" (001-015). This moment of private honesty, occurring before Babbitt has even risen from bed, establishes the central tension between public performance and private despair.

- **The confession to [[paul-riesling|Paul]]** (Chapter V) — Over lunch, Babbitt confesses to [[paul-riesling]] that despite having "pretty much done all the things I ought to; supported my family, and got a good house and a six-cylinder car" (005-026), he feels "kind of down in the mouth." This is the novel's most direct statement of Babbitt's spiritual crisis — and its most intimate moment of male friendship.

- **The [[boosters-club|Boosters']] speech** (Chapter XIII) — Babbitt's address to [[zenith-real-estate-convention|the real-estate convention]], proclaiming that if [[zenith|Zenith]] doesn't become "at least tenth" in population he'll be the first to call himself a failure, represents the apotheosis of the booster spirit and the rhetorical emptiness at its core.

- **The rebellion** (Chapters XX–XXIX) — [[babbitts-rebellion|Babbitt's defiance]], including his association with [[tanis-judique]], his sympathy for strikers, and his open criticism of the "bunch of bullies" at the [[boosters-club]], represents his one genuine attempt to escape the conformity that defines him. The rebellion is both exhilarating and doomed.

- **The return** (Chapter XXXIV) — Though "the last scar of his rebellion was healed" (034-029), Babbitt discovers that "he actually did not consider [[zenith-street-traction|the Traction Bunch]] an interesting subject of conversation." His rebellion has left permanent, if subtle, traces — most importantly in his silent decision that his son Ted should not repeat his life.

## Character Traits

Babbitt is defined by contradiction. He is physically comfortable and spiritually miserable. He is "nimble in the calling of selling houses for more than people could afford to pay" (001-008) — a description that simultaneously acknowledges his professional competence and indicts his ethical emptiness. His spectacles mark him as "the modern business man" — a creature entirely defined by his commercial role. His [[boosters-club|Boosters' Club]] button makes him "feel loyal and important" (001-042), revealing how completely his sense of self depends on external validation.

> His name was George F. Babbitt. He was forty-six years old now, in April, 1920, and he made nothing in particular, neither butter nor shoes nor poetry, but he was nimble in the calling of selling houses for more than people could afford to pay. (001-008)

> He was the modern business man; one who gave orders to clerks and drove a car and played occasional golf and was scholarly in regard to Salesmanship. (001-037)

## Critical Reception

Since publication, George F. Babbitt has been recognized as one of American literature's great satirical creations. H. L. Mencken praised Lewis for capturing "the whole grotesque comedy of Middle Western civilization" through Babbitt's character, while later critics such as Mark Schorer emphasized the novel's "double vision" — simultaneously satirizing Babbitt's boosterism while rendering his private suffering with genuine pathos. The name "Babbitt" entered dictionaries as a common noun denoting "a person who thinks and behaves according to middle-class conventions; a philistine," cementing the character's status as a cultural archetype. Contemporary scholarship has reconsidered Babbitt through lenses of masculinity studies, consumer culture, and the sociology of the professions, finding in his story a prescient critique of the spiritual costs of American prosperity.

> "The portrait of Babbitt is not only a devastating satire on the American businessman; it is also, in its finest moments, a heartbreaking account of a man who dimly senses that his life has been a failure in the only terms that matter." — Mark Schorer, *Sinclair Lewis: An American Life* (1961)

## Satirical Significance

Babbitt's name entered the American lexicon as a type — the "Babbitt" is the conformist businessman, the booster, the man who substitutes group membership for individual conviction. Lewis's achievement is to make this type simultaneously ridiculous and sympathetic. The reader is invited to laugh at Babbitt's booster rhetoric and to recognize in his private discontent something genuinely moving. The novel's final moment — Babbitt silently endorsing his son's freedom — suggests that even the most thoroughly standardized American soul retains, somewhere, the capacity for genuine feeling.
