# Friendship

The spectrum of male relationships in *Babbitt*, ranging from the ritualized camaraderie of the Boosters' Club to the one authentic connection in Babbitt's life — his friendship with Paul Riesling.

## Definition in Context

Lewis draws a sharp distinction between two kinds of friendship in Zenith. On one hand, there is the pervasive "mechanical friendship — back-slapping and jocular, never demanding genuine intimacy" (018-041), performed through clubs and lunch tables. On the other hand, there is the singular bond between Babbitt and Paul Riesling — the one relationship in which Babbitt can express doubt, weakness, and his longing for something beyond the business religion.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Roughnecks' Table** — Babbitt's daily lunch with his business peers is a ritual of masculine bonding that reinforces conformity while providing the appearance of connection. It is friendship as performance, requiring nothing deeper than jokes about business and politics.
- **Paul Riesling** — The sole genuine friendship in Babbitt's life. Paul is the only person to whom Babbitt can confess: "I've pretty much done all the things I ought to... and I still feel kind of down in the mouth" (005-026). Their bond, forged in college, represents the human connection that Zenith's institutionalized fellowship cannot provide.
- **Friendship as social currency** — Joe Paradise recognizes "the friendship Babbitt was offering him" (025-019) as a transaction — friendship as a tool of social navigation rather than emotional connection.
- **The cost of lost friendship** — When Paul is imprisoned, Babbitt's world collapses. The novel suggests that without genuine friendship, Babbitt has no defense against the spiritual emptiness of Zenith life.
- **Pledging friendship** — Babbitt is shown "pledging friendship with men whom he had for years privately denounced" (029-043), revealing the performative nature of most social bonds.

## Key Passages

> With Paul Riesling he could be unnatural and preening, but with men at the Roughnecks' Table he was mechanical friendship — back-slapping and jocular, never daring to risk the exposure of genuine feeling. (018-041)

> The assurance of Tanis Judique's friendship fortified Babbitt's self-approval. At the Athletic Club he became experimental with the usual rough-house greetings. (029-002)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses friendship to expose the emotional poverty of Zenith's masculine culture. The Boosters' Club, the Roughnecks' Table, the Elks — all these institutions promise fellowship while delivering only "mechanical friendship" (018-041). The tragedy of Babbitt is that he has exactly one real friend, and when Paul is taken from him, he has no emotional resources to draw upon. The novel suggests that a society built on competitive individualism and performed camaraderie leaves its members profoundly alone, unable to form the very connections they most need.
