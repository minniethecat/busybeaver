# T. Cholmondeley Frink

T. Cholmondeley Frink is a poet, advertising professional, and newspaper columnist in [[zenith|Zenith]]. He is the city's designated man of letters — the figure who reassures the businessmen of [[boosters-club|the Boosters' Club]] that [[art|culture]] and [[business|commerce]] can coexist, provided [[art|culture]] understands its subordinate role. [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] keeps a file of [[chum-frink|Frink]]'s verses and editorials, a gesture of cultural aspiration that is also an act of self-deception (001-040).

## Role in the Novel

[[chum-frink|Frink]] is Lewis's satire of the commercialized [[art]]ist — a poet whose [[art]] exists to flatter the businessmen who pay for it. He is "not only the author of the lyrics that graced [[boosters-club|the Boosters' Club]] menus but the press-agent for [[zenith|the Zenith]] Chamber of Commerce," a fusion of roles that perfectly captures the city's instrumental view of [[art|culture]] (008-031). [[chum-frink|Frink]] is [[chum-frink|the poet]] laureate of [[community|boosterism]], and his presence in the novel exposes the hollowness of [[zenith|Zenith]]'s claim to be anything more than a marketplace.

## Key Scenes

- **[[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s clippings file** — Among [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s prized possessions are "clippings of verses by T. Cholmondeley Frink and of [[zenith-advocate|the newspaper]] editorials in which he so magnificently demolished all opponents of the Open Shop." The conflation of [[art|poetry]] and anti-union propaganda in a single sentence is pure Lewis (001-040).
- **Neighbor and borrow-club member** — [[chum-frink|Frink]] is p[[art]] of [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s social circle, a neighbor whose borrowed tools are never returned. His intimacy with the [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]s illustrates how the cultural authority figure is also, inevitably, a fellow [[neighborhood|suburb]]anite with the same domestic pettiness as everyone else (008-050).
- **The genius of T. Cholmondeley Frink** — The citizens of [[zenith|Zenith]] "appreciate the genius of T. Cholmondeley Frink, but the vigor of the city's commercial life made them feel that he was perhaps a trifle too poetic, a little too much the long-haired genius." The passage captures the precise social position of the [[art]]ist in [[zenith|Zenith]]: admired but slightly suspect (009-002).
- **[[chum-frink|Frink]] to whom [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] most often turns** — When [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] seeks cultural validation for his business ideas, it is [[chum-frink|Frink]] he consults. [[chum-frink|Frink]] serves as the intellectual alibi for [[zenith|Zenith]]'s commercial instincts (013-013).

## Character Traits

[[chum-frink|Frink]] is genial, productive, and entirely [[conformity|convention]]al beneath his [[art]]istic pretensions. He supplies a product — [[art|culture]] — and is judged by the same standard of efficiency as any other [[zenith|Zenith]] businessman. His [[art|poetry]] is decoration, not [[rebellion|dissent]]; his editorials, aggression disguised as principle. Lewis does not make [[chum-frink|Frink]] a hypocrite — that would imply he knows the difference between [[art]] and advertising. He does not.
