# Street Traction Company

The Zenith Street Traction Company operates the city's streetcar system and represents the hardest, least apologetic form of corporate power in the novel. Its purchasing agent, Sanders, and its directors — referred to collectively as "the Traction Bunch" — wield an influence that extends from municipal contracts to labor relations to political campaigns. When Babbitt briefly aligns himself with liberal causes, it is the Street Traction Company that embodies the forces that reassert control (034-029).

## Role in the Novel

The Street Traction Company serves as the novel's most concentrated image of the power that underlies Zenith's booster rhetoric. The boosters speak of community and growth; the Traction Company delivers control. It is the organization that Babbitt's rebellion brushes against — and the one that, through its purchasing agent Sanders, helps orchestrate Babbitt's return to conformity. The Company is the iron fist inside the booster's velvet glove.

## Key Scenes

- **The terminal deal** — Babbitt is offered "a chance to take part in the Street Traction Company's terminal deal," a speculative opportunity that requires no labor and produces pure profit for the connected (017-052).
- **The option steal** — The Company plans to build car-barns, and the purchasing agent buys a five-thousand-dollar property whose value will multiply when the route is announced. Babbitt's moral qualms are aroused not by the corruption but by being excluded from it (019-002, 019-003, 019-021).
- **Labor surveillance** — Babbitt learns through [[henry-thompson]] that officials of the Street Traction Company are encouraging violence against striking workers. The revelation confronts Babbitt with the violence that sustains his business class (032-027).
- **The Traction Bunch's judgment** — [[jake-offut]] reports that the Traction Bunch are "dissatisfied" with how Sanders is handling the strike — an internal power struggle that reveals the Company as a site of factional intrigue (034-029).

## Satirical Significance

The Street Traction Company is Lewis's portrait of monopoly capitalism in its municipal form. Unlike the boosters' clubs, which cloak power in fellowship, the Traction Company dispenses with the rhetoric. It buys, sells, and pressures with a frankness that exposes the economic machinery the boosters work so hard to disguise.
