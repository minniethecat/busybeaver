# Archibald Purdy

Archibald Purdy is a grocer in the indecisive residential district known as Linton. A long-nosed and solemn man, he becomes the mark in one of [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt's]] most revealing real-estate transactions — a deal that exposes the predatory mechanics beneath Babbitt's self-image as an honest businessman.

## Role in the Novel

Purdy appears in a single extended scene, but that scene is one of the novel's sharpest satirical set-pieces. The transaction between Babbitt, [[conrad-lyte|Conrad Lyte]], and Purdy dramatizes the "sensitive mechanism of modern finance" by which the prosperous are enriched and the small are squeezed — all while Babbitt maintains his conviction that he is a pillar of the community who merely helps willing buyers and sellers find one another.

## Key Scenes

- **The real-estate conference** — Purdy, a grocer who wishes to open a butcher shop beside his existing store, comes to Babbitt's office to negotiate for an adjacent lot. Six months earlier, Babbitt had persuaded Conrad Lyte to purchase the lot for eleven thousand dollars — above its appraised value of nine thousand — on the theory that Purdy could eventually be forced to pay more. Babbitt greets Purdy with cries of "This way, Brother Purdy!" while plying him with cigars and strategic chair-adjustments. [004-034 through 004-037]
- **The squeeze** — Babbitt opens by claiming that butchers and chain-store operators have made "tempting offers" for the lot, threatening that "it would be hard luck if one of these cash-and-carry chain-stores got in there and started cutting prices below cost till they got rid of competition and forced you to the wall!" Purdy protests that the asking price is over twice the property's value: "Why good God, Mr. Babbitt, you're asking more 'n twice its value! And threatening to ruin me if I don't take it!" Babbitt responds with wounded dignity: "Purdy, I don't like your way of talking!" (004-037)
- **The close** — Through a choreographed routine of false generosity, Babbitt persuades Lyte to "come down" to twenty-one thousand dollars, then guides Purdy's hand to sign. Lyte makes over nine thousand dollars in profit. Babbitt earns a four-hundred-and-fifty-dollar commission. Purdy gets his lot. The narration concludes dryly: "The work of the world was being done." [004-037 through 004-038]

## Character Traits

Purdy is described as "long-nosed and solemn" and seems to care "less for Babbitt and for Vision." His thin hands move nervously in and out of his pockets during the negotiation. He is not stupid — he knows the price is extortionate and says so — but he lacks the power to resist. Lewis makes him more object than agent: a small businessman caught in the gears of the very system he presumably believes in. His plight is never commented on directly; the satire operates entirely through the contrast between Babbitt's self-congratulation and the transparent ruthlessness of the deal.
