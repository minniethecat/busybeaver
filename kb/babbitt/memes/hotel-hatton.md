# Hotel Hatton

The Hotel Hatton is a modest residential hotel in Zenith, described with devastating precision as "that glorified boarding-house filled with widows, red-plush furniture, and the sound of ice-water pitchers" (018-037). It represents a liminal space in Zenith's social geography — neither the private home of the respectable middle class nor the raw boarding-house of the working poor, but a "glorified" version that preserves the form of hotel living while betraying, in every detail, its distance from the Thornleigh or the Regency. Its clientele is revealing: widows, the elderly, the recently downsized — people who have slipped, or are slipping, from the prosperity that defines Zenith's self-image.

## Description

The Hotel Hatton enters the narrative through Babbitt's family obligations. His father- and mother-in-law, Mr. and Mrs. Henry T. Thompson, "rented their old house in the Bellevue district and moved to the Hotel Hatton" (018-037). This move from a private home to a residential hotel signals a quiet defeat — the Thompsons have aged past the point of maintaining a household, and the Hatton offers a dignified alternative to dependence on their children. The hotel's interiors — red-plush furniture and the ubiquitous sound of ice-water pitchers — are markers of a bygone gentility, the material residue of respectability maintained on a reduced budget.

The cost of the Thompsons' move is a new ritual of obligation: "every other Sunday evening the Babbitts had to dine with them, on fricasseed chicken, discouraged celery, and cornstarch ice cream, and afterward sit, polite and restrained, in the hotel lounge, while a young woman violinist played songs from the German via Broadway" (018-037). The food is "discouraged," the entertainment is ersatz, and the Babbitts' politeness is "restrained" — every adjective signals the strain of family duty performed without warmth.

## Social Function

The Hotel Hatton serves as a transitional space in Zenith's social architecture — a halfway house between the private home (the norm for the middle class) and dependency (living with adult children). For the Thompsons, the Hatton preserves a form of independence, however diminished: they have their own space, their own meals, their own Sunday entertainment. But the quality of that independence — "discouraged celery," "cornstarch ice cream" — exposes its inadequacy.

The hotel also functions as a catchment for the socially displaced: widows who have lost their economic anchor, the elderly who can no longer manage a household, and those like Tanis Judique who find themselves between social categories. Tanis, after she and Babbitt first meet, explains that "she had left the avenue district and moved to the Hotel Hatton, that glorified boarding-house" (018-037). The move from the avenue district — presumably a private apartment or house — to the Hatton represents a step down, a concession to widowhood and reduced means. Yet the Hatton is also the starting point for Babbitt's liberation from domestic routine: Tanis's residence places her in Babbitt's orbit, her ambiguous social position — neither fully respectable nor disreputable — embodied by the hotel itself.

The Hatton houses both Babbitt's in-laws (the past he is obligated to) and Tanis (the future he is drawn toward), making it a site of competing emotional forces in his life. The Sunday dinners become a theater of familial duty where no one wants to be on stage but no one can leave — a microcosm of Babbitt's larger predicament.

## Key Members

- **The widows** — Unnamed but collectively significant: "filled with widows" defines the Hatton as a repository for women who have lost the husbands that gave them social standing (018-037).

The ritual embodies the strain of family obligation stripped of affection.
- **Tanis's move to the Hatton**: After meeting Babbitt, Tanis explains that she has left the avenue district for the Hatton (018-037), a transition that marks her reduced circumstances and sets the stage for her role in Babbitt's rebellion.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis's description of the Hotel Hatton is a masterpiece of compressed social criticism. "Glorified boarding-house" — the phrase exposes the pretense in the very act of naming it a hotel. "Filled with widows" — the Hatton is a repository for women the economic system has discarded. "Red-plush furniture, and the sound of ice-water pitchers" — these sensory details evoke a world of threadbare gentility, of respectability maintained through objects rather than substance.

The Sunday dinners at the Hatton mirror the larger theme of Babbitt's life — trapped in roles he did not choose, surrounded by people he is supposed to love, performing the gestures of affection while feeling nothing but the desire to escape. The hotel's name carries no obvious irony, unlike Eden. Its very ordinariness is a statement: this is the unglamorous, undecorated reality behind Zenith's booster rhetoric of prosperity. Not everyone in Zenith lives in Floral Heights. Some live at the Hatton.
