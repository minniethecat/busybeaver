# Alvin Thayer

Alvin Thayer is a Zenith realtor, one of the five official delegates representing Zenith at the annual convention of the State Association of Real Estate Boards in Monarch. He appears in the novel as a name on a delegate list and as a reference point in Babbitt's competitive inventory of his fellow realtors' business ethics.

## Role in the Novel

Thayer is introduced among the convention delegation: "Babbitt, Rountree, W. A. Rogers, Alvin Thayer, and Elbert Wing" (013-018). The list of five names composes a miniature taxonomy of Zenith's real-estate establishment: Babbitt the anxious aspirant, Rountree the social climber, Rogers the farm-lands dealer, Wing the retail-marketing specialist, and Thayer.

Thayer's most significant appearance is negative: he does not appear on stage at all. Babbitt invokes him by name in a private moment of competitive self-justification: "I don't pad out the truth like Cecil Rountree or Thayer or the rest of these realtors" (004-029). The pairing of Thayer with Rountree — the novel's emblem of the socially elevated, ethically suspect realtor — places Thayer in a category of business practice Babbitt publicly denounces and privately approximates.

## The Convention Delegate as Social Role

The delegation to the State Association convention is worth examining as a social structure. "Besides the five official delegates to the convention — Babbitt, Rountree, W. A. Rogers, Alvin Thayer, and Elbert Wing — there were fifty unofficial delegates, most of them with their wives" (013-018). The distinction between "official" and "unofficial" delegates is itself a miniature class system: the five official delegates are the recognized leaders of the profession, while the fifty unofficial delegates are followers, spectators, men whose presence affirms the importance of the event without conferring the distinction of office.

Thayer's position among the five official delegates thus marks him as a figure of genuine standing within the real-estate community — a man whose professional reputation, however invisible to the reader, is sufficient to secure him a place among Zenith's representatives to the state body. The irony, characteristic of Lewis, is that this genuine professional standing is accompanied by a near-total absence from the narrative: Thayer is important enough to be a delegate but not important enough to be a character.

## Character Traits

Like several of the convention delegates, Thayer is a name rather than a character — a structural position in the taxonomy of Zenith business rather than a psychological portrait. Lewis uses such names to convey the density and variety of the business world Babbitt inhabits without slowing the narrative to develop each figure. Thayer exists in the novel as a data point: one more realtor in the competitive field through which Babbitt navigates.

Babbitt's private self-comparison to Thayer reveals as much about Babbitt as it does about his competitor. When Babbitt reassures himself that he does not "pad out the truth like Cecil Rountree or Thayer" (004-029), he is engaged in the characteristic booster activity of competitive self-evaluation — measuring himself against his peers and finding himself satisfactory. The fact that Thayer is named alongside Rountree, the novel's most prominent example of the socially ambitious realtor, suggests that Babbitt regards Thayer as a genuine rival — a man whose business practices invite the same scrutiny that Babbitt applies to his own.
