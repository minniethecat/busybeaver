# Martin Lumsen

Martin Lumsen is a Zenith real-estate developer and competitor to [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]] in the suburban land business. He appears in the novel primarily through Babbitt's competitive reflections on his own developments and through his young son Willy, who serves as banner-bearer at the convention departure — a small but pointed image of boosterism passed from father to son.

## Role in the Novel

Lumsen serves as Babbitt's competitive yardstick in the real-estate development market. His subdivision, Avonlea, is the development against which Babbitt measures his own Glen Oriole development. Babbitt's private satisfaction with Glen Oriole's sewage system is explicitly a satisfaction at Lumsen's expense: "It made him feel superior; it enabled him to sneer privily at the Martin Lumsen development, Avonlea, which had a cesspool" (004-027).

Yet Lewis's irony cuts both ways. Babbitt's Glen Oriole sewers "had insufficient outlet, so that waste remained in them, not very agreeably, while the Avonlea cesspool was a Waring septic tank" (004-027). The competitive satisfaction Babbitt derives from his superior sewage system is undermined by the revelation that the system doesn't actually work properly — while Lumsen's supposedly inferior cesspool uses superior technology. The passage is a miniature of Babbitt's entire booster psychology: the need to feel superior is more important than the factual basis for the superiority.

## Key Scenes

- **The Glen Oriole comparison** — Babbitt, surveying his Glen Oriole development, "righteously put in a complete sewage-system" and uses this as grounds to "sneer privily at the Martin Lumsen development, Avonlea, which had a cesspool" (004-027). The competitive triangulation is quintessential Babbitt: self-worth established not by absolute achievement but by relative position against a rival.

- **The convention departure** — At the Union Station, Lumsen's young son Willy carries "a tasseled banner inscribed 'Zenith the Zip City — Zeal, Zest and Zowie — 1,000,000 in 1935'" as the convention delegates assemble for the midnight train to Monarch (013-019). The image of a small boy carrying the booster slogan through the train station is both comic and faintly ominous: booster ideology is transmitted across generations through ritual participation.

- **The street-traction controversy** — When the Zenith Street Traction Company plans to build car-repair shops in "the Martin Lumsen neighborhood" near the Dorchester district, residents express concern about declining property values and increased noise (019-002). The neighborhood's association with Lumsen's name — it is "the Martin Lumsen neighborhood" — suggests a developer whose name has become synonymous with a particular class of housing: modest, practical, vulnerable to industrial encroachment.

## Character Traits

Lumsen is never directly described by Lewis; he exists entirely through Babbitt's competitive consciousness and through the brief appearances of his son and his neighborhood. This absence is itself characterization: Lumsen is the anonymous rival, the competitor whose name Babbitt mutters to himself as he inspects his own work, the presence felt only through the contrast he provides.
