# Miners' and Drovers' National Bank

A major Zenith financial institution whose imposing architecture and marbled interior serve Babbitt as a mirror of his own respectability, reinforcing his self-image as a "clever and solid" businessman each time he passes it on his daily commute. The bank's name — evoking the extractive industries of the nineteenth-century frontier — is itself a form of commercial rhetoric, borrowing the prestige of mining and cattle-driving for an institution whose real business is the more abstract practice of credit and investment in a modern industrial city.

## Description

The Miners' and Drovers' National Bank is one of the downtown landmarks on Babbitt's familiar morning route from his Floral Heights home to his office in the Reeves Building. The bank building is described as "marbled," a word that conveys both the physical materials of its construction and the aura of permanence and prosperity it projects (005-009). Marble, in the architectural vocabulary of American commercial buildings, signified more than durability; it was the material language of classical authority, borrowed from Greek temples and Roman forums and repurposed to sanctify the transactions of modern capitalism.

The bank is one of the more prestigious institutions in Babbitt's mental catalog of downtown Zenith. Unlike the retail shops and service establishments that fill the rest of his morning route — the Parthenon Shoe Shine Parlor, the Nobby Men's Wear Shop, the United Cigar Store — the bank commands a different order of respect. It is not a place where one purchases goods but a place where one's financial identity is established and maintained. For Babbitt, who measures his worth by his credit rating and his standing in the business community, the bank is almost a sacred space.

## Babbitt's Self-Reflection

Lewis captures Babbitt's relationship to the bank in a single sentence dense with irony:

> "He looked at his bank, the Miners' and Drovers' National, and considered how clever and solid he was to bank with so marbled an establishment" (005-009)

Every element of the sentence rewards close attention. The possessive "his bank" reveals Babbitt's habit of claiming institutional affiliation as personal attribute — the bank belongs to him in the same way that his membership in the Boosters' Club belongs to him, as an extension of identity rather than a mere commercial arrangement. The self-congratulatory "how clever and solid he was" exposes the circular logic of his self-assessment: he judges himself solid because he banks with a building that looks solid. The architecture authenticates the man.

The narrator's knowing tone, however, makes clear that this is a form of self-deception. Babbitt is a depositor, not a partner. The marble lobby does not reflect his virtues back at him; it merely provides a flattering surface in which he can admire his own reflection. Lewis's irony is gentle here — Babbitt is not being mocked for banking at a respectable institution but for mistaking the bank's grandeur for his own.

## The Bank in Babbitt's Mental Geography

The Miners' and Drovers' National appears as one stop in the catalog of commercial landmarks that structure Babbitt's morning drive: the California Building, the Parthenon Shoe Shine Parlor, the Simplex Office Furniture Shop, the National Cash Register Agency, the Nobby Men's Wear Shop, and the United Cigar Store (005-007–009). The bank, with its weighty name evoking the extractive industries of the West — mining and cattle-driving — anchors this landscape with an implication of productive substance that the surrounding retail establishments lack.

The name "Miners' and Drovers' National" is itself worth examining as an instance of Lewis's satirical method. In 1920s America, banks frequently adopted names that invoked the industries — mining, ranching, agriculture — from which regional wealth had historically derived, even when their actual business was in urban real estate loans and commercial credit. The name conjures an image of rugged individualism — the prospector with his pick, the cattleman with his herd — that has little connection to the ledgers and interest calculations conducted within the marbled interior. The frontier has been marble-ized, its rough energies tamed into architectural respectability.

## Satirical Significance

The Miners' and Drovers' National Bank is one of the novel's most concentrated examples of Babbitt's habit of proxy thinking — the tendency to measure his own qualities by the qualities of the institutions, objects, and brands with which he associates. The bank is "marbled," therefore Babbitt is solid. The bank's name evokes the productive economy of the nineteenth century, therefore Babbitt's real-estate speculation in the twentieth is grounded in authentic value creation.

Lewis is not suggesting that Babbitt should bank elsewhere, or that banking itself is corrupt. The satire targets something subtler: the way commercial culture encourages people to outsource their self-worth to the institutions that hold their money, insure their property, and furnish their offices. Babbitt does not know himself; he knows his bank, his club, his car, his cigar-cutter. The Miners' and Drovers' National, in its marbled silence, tells him who he is — and he is grateful for the answer, however incomplete.
