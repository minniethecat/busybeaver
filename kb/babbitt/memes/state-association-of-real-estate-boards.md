# State Association of Real Estate Boards

The statewide organization of real estate brokers and operators, known to its members as the S. A. R. E. B. — "with the universal passion for mysterious and important-sounding initials" (PN 013-004). The organization represents the professional aspirations of men like George Babbitt, serving as both a trade association and a theater for business boosterism. The convention at Monarch, occupying most of Chapter 13, is the novel's most sustained portrait of middle-class professional culture at play — a study in the rituals by which Babbitt's class convinces itself of its own dignity while indulging precisely the appetites that dignity is meant to suppress.

## Description

The S. A. R. E. B. was the state-level organization of realtors, holding annual conventions in rotating cities including Monarch, Zenith's chief rival. Babbitt attended as an official delegate alongside Cecil Rountree, who chaired the convention program committee (PN 013-004). The organization lobbied the state legislature on tax measures affecting realty transfers (PN 013-052) and maintained committees including one on Torrens Titles, to which Babbitt was appointed (PN 013-053). Babbitt later served on one of its committees, a responsibility he used to impress Tanis Judique with his public-spiritedness: "I'm on one of the committees of the State Association of Real Estate Boards, and that means a lot of work and responsibility" (PN 024-012).

Babbitt's own concept of the realtor's role, articulated to his Sunday-afternoon audience at his dinner party, frames property dealing as a quasi-priestly calling: "it's the duty and the privilege of the realtor to know everything about his own city and its environs" (PN 004-022). This self-mythologizing rhetoric — the realtor as "a seer of the future development of the community" (PN 004-022) — is what the S. A. R. E. B. exists to validate and amplify through its conventions, committees, and resolutions.

## Social Function

The S. A. R. E. B. conventions served multiple social functions beyond their stated professional purpose. They were occasions for civic boosterism, with delegations from Galop de Vache, Sparta, Pioneer, and Zenith competing through banners, songs, and circus parades to host the next year's convention (PN 013-039–013-049). They were also occasions for male camaraderie and escape from domestic routine — Babbitt's night of drinking with Rogers, Wing, and delegates from up-state represents the convention's unstated function as a sanctioned release from respectability (PN 013-058–013-060). The professional and the pleasurable were inseparable in the S. A. R. E. B.'s actual operations.

The convention program itself enacted the rituals of professional legitimation: Babbitt's speech arguing that "a good realtor has to have more knowledge and more ability than most of these so-called 'professional men'" (PN 013-005), the singing of the Boosters' Club Song with a special verse for the convention (PN 013-021), and the reading of a paper by "the venerable Minnemagantic realtor, Major Carlton Tuke" (PN 013-037) — all served to affirm that men whose daily work consisted of buying and selling property were engaged in something nobler than commerce. The irony is that Babbitt himself, in unguarded moments, admits the truth: he tells Paul Riesling that "sometimes I get so darned sick of the whole game" and wonders if he "might have done something worthwhile" (PN 005-030).

## Key Members

- **George F. Babbitt** — official delegate from Zenith, delivered keynote address, appointed to Committee on Torrens Titles, later served on a committee (PN 013-004, 013-044, 013-053, 024-012)
- **Cecil Rountree** — chairman of the program committee, admired by Babbitt for his speculative building and envied for his social position (PN 013-004)
- **W. A. Rogers** — Zenith dealer in farm-lands, Babbitt's convention roommate (PN 013-032)
- **Major Carlton Tuke** — "the venerable Minnemagantic realtor" who read a paper to the convention, representing the elder-statesman archetype of the trade (PN 013-037)

## Key Scenes

- **The convention at Monarch** — Babbitt attends his first S. A. R. E. B. convention, delivers a speech on the professional dignity of realtors, and gains statewide recognition (PN 013-004 through 013-055, occupying the bulk of Chapter 13)
- **Babbitt's speech** — Babbitt argues that realtors should be called "realtors" not "real-estate men" and that theirs is a true profession defined by "public service and trained skill" (PN 013-005–013-006). The speech is the novel's most concentrated depiction of Babbitt's public voice — self-important, derivative, but genuinely felt
- **The Zenith delegation's circus parade** — At the final meeting, the Zenith brokers dressed as cowpunchers, bareback riders, and Japanese jugglers, with Babbitt as a clown beating a bass drum (PN 013-048–013-049). The image of a man who has just delivered a lecture on professional dignity dressed as a clown is Lewis's visual distillation of the booster's divided nature
- **Committee service** — Babbitt mentions his S. A. R. E. B. committee work to Tanis Judique as evidence of his public importance (PN 024-012), revealing that the credential he mocked at the convention becomes useful social currency in other contexts

## Satirical Significance

The S. A. R. E. B. is a vehicle for Lewis's satire of professional pretension and business culture. The organization's very name — a string of initials pronounced with self-importance — embodies the absurdity of the professional aspirations Babbitt champions. His speech arguing that realtors constitute a "profession" defined by "public service" rather than profit-seeking is undercut by every subsequent scene: the circus parade, the drunken hotel room, the lobbying for "sane tax measures" that benefit the industry. The convention's blend of solemn resolutions about Almighty God and boozy male bonding reveals the hollowness at the core of business boosterism — a ritual of mutual self-congratulation serving interests its participants cannot acknowledge.

The convention chapter also exposes Babbitt's internal division with unusual directness. In his hotel room, drunk, he confesses to W. A. Rogers that "a fellow that didn't have any money could have a better time than we do" and that he sometimes feels his whole life is "just one darned day after another without ever really living" (PN 013-061–013-062). The S. A. R. E. B. serves simultaneously as the institution that certifies Babbitt's public identity and as the stage where that identity's insufficiency becomes most visible. It is the perfect Lewisian institution: a structure that exists to convince its members of what none of them quite believes.
