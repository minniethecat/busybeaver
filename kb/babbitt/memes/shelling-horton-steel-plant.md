# Shelling-Horton Steel Plant

A steel mill that appears briefly but significantly in *Babbitt*, serving as a focal point for the novel's exploration of conflicting value systems — the utilitarian businessman's reverence for wealth versus the artist's appreciation of industrial beauty. The scene, lasting only a few paragraphs, is among the most concentrated dramatizations of the Paul Riesling tragedy in the entire novel.

## Description

The Shelling-Horton Steel Plant is first seen from a train window as Babbitt and Paul Riesling travel with other businessmen. As they enter a city, the train passes a steel mill "which flared in scarlet and orange flame that licked at the cadaverous stacks, at the iron-sheathed walls and sullen converters" (010-045). The imagery is gothic and almost violent — "cadaverous stacks," "sullen converters," flames that "licked" at the industrial architecture. Lewis's language transforms the factory into a vision of infernal beauty, a landscape at once terrifying and sublime.

Paul Riesling, the sensitive violinist trapped in the roofing business, exclaims at its beauty: "My Lord, look at that — beautiful!" (010-046). The exclamation is spontaneous, uncalculated — a genuine aesthetic response in a novel where most utterances are formulaic and self-serving. For a moment, Paul is not the defeated husband and reluctant businessman but an artist capable of seeing beauty where others see only industry.

A fellow passenger in a velour hat responds with a businessman's assessment: "You bet it's beautiful, friend. That's the Shelling-Horton Steel Plant, and they tell me old John Shelling made a good three million bones out of munitions during the war!" (010-047). The response is a masterpiece of miscommunication. The businessman agrees that the plant is "beautiful," but his definition of beauty has nothing to do with flame and form — it is measured entirely in dollars. "Three million bones" — the slang reduces the fortune to something chewable, digestible, a bone to be gnawed. The reference to "munitions during the war" adds a darker shading: the beauty that the businessman admires is built on the profits of mass death.

When Paul clarifies that he meant "it's lovely the way the light pulls that picturesque yard, all littered with junk, right out of the darkness," the man retorts: "Well, personally, I think Shelling-Horton keep their works awful dirty. But I don't suppose there's any law against calling 'em 'picturesque' if it gets you that way!" (010-048) (010-049). The exchange is a perfect miniature of the novel's central conflict. Paul sees composition, contrast, the wresting of beauty from industrial chaos. The businessman sees dirt — a failure of housekeeping, an offense against the tidiness that Zenith values above all else. That he concedes there is "no law" against calling the plant picturesque is itself revealing: in his world, the absence of a law against something is the closest one can come to tolerating it.

Babbitt, caught between loyalty to his friend and to the business ethic, awkwardly bridges the divide by praising Paul's "great little eye for picturesque places," while reassuring the businessmen that Paul is still "in the roofing line" (010-049). The intervention is pure Babbitt — well-meaning, clumsy, and ultimately reinforcing the very conformity it pretends to mediate.

## Social Function

The Shelling-Horton Steel Plant functions as a symbolic site where two incompatible modes of seeing collide. For the businessman, the plant's value is purely economic — measured in "three million bones" of war profit. For Paul Riesling, it is an aesthetic object — a composition of flame, darkness, and industrial form. The two modes of valuation are not merely different; they are mutually unintelligible. The businessman cannot understand what Paul means by "beautiful" because beauty, for him, is a synonym for successful. Paul cannot understand why the businessman talks about money when there is a vision of flame and shadow before them.

The scene also dramatizes the social cost of aesthetic sensibility in a commercial culture. Paul's appreciation of the plant's beauty marks him as an outsider — someone whose values do not align with those of the men in the smoking car. His retreat into his newspaper after the exchange (010-049) is a gesture of defeat, a quiet acknowledgment that his way of seeing has no place in this company. The moment prefigures his eventual psychological collapse: Paul's tragedy is not that he is wrong but that he cannot make himself understood, and the effort of trying eventually destroys him.

## Key Members

- **John Shelling** — the owner, who "made a good three million bones out of munitions during the war," admired by the businessmen in the smoking car for his wealth, not his product (010-047)

## Key Scenes

- **The Train Passing the Steel Plant** — Paul Riesling's aesthetic appreciation of the flaming steel mill provokes incomprehension and hostility from his fellow passengers. The man in the velour hat can only process "beauty" as a financial metric; when Paul clarifies that he means visual beauty, the man dismisses the plant as "awful dirty." Babbitt's attempt to mediate — praising Paul's eye while reaffirming his business credentials — exemplifies the pressure to conform that defines their friendship (010-045) (010-049).

## Satirical Significance

The Shelling-Horton episode is one of the novel's sharpest dramatizations of the conflict between art and commerce. Paul sees a sublime industrial landscape; the businessmen see dollar signs and dirt. Their inability to recognize Paul's category of value — and Paul's sulky retreat into his newspaper — prefigures his eventual psychological collapse. The plant's name, linking "Shelling" (suggesting both the product and wartime shelling) with "Horton," is itself a study in industrial nomenclature: the name of a thing that makes things whose names are euphemisms for destruction.

Lewis's satirical target in this scene is not the steel plant or even the businessmen's philistinism but the deeper cultural condition that makes aesthetic experience unintelligible in commercial society. The man in the velour hat is not evil; he is simply incapable of the kind of perception Paul exercises. His world is organized around prices, not values; around cleanliness, not beauty; around what can be counted, not what can be felt. The tragedy of the scene — and of the novel — is that this incapacity is not individual but cultural. It is what Zenith does to people.

The contrast between the two modes of valuation is rendered with extraordinary economy. In the space of a few paragraphs, Lewis moves from gothic description ("cadaverous stacks") to commercial assessment ("three million bones") to aesthetic appreciation ("the light pulls that picturesque yard") to philistine dismissal ("awful dirty") — a complete traversal of the novel's moral and perceptual universe, compressed into a single train ride past a steel plant (010-045) (010-049).
