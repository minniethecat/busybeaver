# Zenith Foundry Company

The Zenith Foundry Company is a manufacturing enterprise in Zenith whose capabilities include cast-iron work. The firm appears in the novel primarily as a node in the Boosters' Club business network, where commercial dealings double as social obligations — Babbitt and his father-in-law Henry Thompson approach the foundry's secretary about "an interesting artistic project — a cast-iron fence for Linden Lane Cemetery," mixing business and civic improvement in the characteristic Zenith manner (006-006).

## Role in the Novel

The Zenith Foundry Company belongs to the industrial landscape of Zenith, a city whose identity is inseparable from its factories, foundries, and workshops. Like the [[Zeeco-Motor-Company]], the foundry represents the productive economy that underwrites Babbitt's prosperity. But Lewis's treatment of the company is characteristically deflationary: it is not celebrated as an engine of wealth but presented as one more stop on Babbitt's round of business calls — its significance measured not in output or employment but in its secretary's amenability to a Boosters' Club discount (006-006).

## Business Context

The foundry is embedded in the characteristic pattern of Zenith commerce: personal relationships among Boosters substitute for arm's-length market transactions. Babbitt and Thompson's visit to the foundry secretary is one of several stops on a day spent driving around the city, treating business as a social circuit rather than a discrete activity. The Boosters' code — "no Booster felt right if he bought anything from another Booster without receiving a discount" — transforms every commercial exchange into an occasion for reaffirming group membership (006-006).

## Key Scenes

- **The Linden Lane Cemetery project** — Babbitt and Thompson visit the foundry secretary to propose a cast-iron fence for the cemetery. The project is described as "interesting" and "artistic," language that reflects the Boosters' habit of dressing commercial transactions in civic-improvement rhetoric. The scene is one of several brief encounters that collectively depict a business community where no transaction is merely economic (006-006).

## Satirical Significance

The Zenith Foundry Company exemplifies Lewis's technique of treating industrial enterprises as expressions of social rather than economic logic. The foundry matters in the novel not because it produces goods or employs workers, but because its secretary can be approached through Boosters' Club channels. Lewis's point is that in Zenith, business is less a system of production than a system of relationships — a form of club life conducted through invoices and discounts rather than handshakes and cigars, though the cigars are present too.
