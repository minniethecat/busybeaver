# Linden Lane Cemetery

A burial ground on the outskirts of Zenith, notable in the novel not for its memorial function but as an artifact of the Thompson family's business history — Henry T. Thompson, Babbitt's father-in-law, once secured a contract for "a cast-iron fence for Linden Lane Cemetery" (006-006).

## Description

Linden Lane Cemetery is referenced only in passing, as an entry in the catalogue of Henry T. Thompson's business accomplishments. Babbitt recalls the project — "a cast-iron fence for Linden Lane Cemetery" (006-006) — alongside the family's other ventures, including the transitional housing development that gave Floral Heights its name. The cemetery contract is framed not as a solemn civic commission but as one more deal in the Thompson portfolio, equivalent in kind to a real-estate speculation.

The brief reference suggests a modest municipal cemetery on the city's periphery — the sort of project that a prosperous local contractor might secure through family connections and civic standing. The cast-iron fence, an artifact of early twentieth-century American funerary architecture, marks the boundary between the city of the living and the city of the dead.

## Social Function

The cemetery's function in the narrative is genealogical rather than spatial. It exists as a data point in Babbitt's inventory of his father-in-law's achievements — proof that the Thompsons were "pioneers" (006-006) whose business reach extended even to the city's burial grounds. The reference to the cemetery project immediately follows a mention of the "table-cloth" and "film of yellow soap" on Thompson's hands (006-005), contrasting the material dignity of cast-iron cemetery fencing with the mundane texture of the pioneer's daily life. The fence contract is one more brick in the edifice of Babbitt's claim to belong to the "best-known pioneer family" (015-012) lineage by marriage.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's recollection of Thompson's projects** — Driving past the Zeeco motel, Babbitt remembers his father-in-law's various business ventures, including "a cast-iron fence for Linden Lane Cemetery" (006-006)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis reduces a cemetery — traditionally a site of mortality, grief, and spiritual reckoning — to a line item in a business ledger. The cast-iron fence for Linden Lane Cemetery is not a memorial but a contract; death, in Babbitt's mental accounting, is just another sector of the construction industry. The juxtaposition of cemetery fencing with the "terrible tongues of flame" of the Zeeco sign (006-006) — the sacred and the commercial sharing a sentence — encapsulates the novel's insistence that in Zenith, everything, including the boundary between the living and the dead, is ultimately real estate.
