# Gruensberg Leather Company

A manufacturing business in Zenith, significant primarily as the employer of Verona Babbitt, who works there as a filing-clerk and later secretary to Mr. Gruensberg.

## Description

The Gruensberg Leather Company was one of Zenith's industrial enterprises. Its offices employed Verona Babbitt, George Babbitt's daughter, who "had for six months been filing-clerk at the Gruensberg Leather Company offices, with a prospect of becoming secretary to Mr. Gruensberg" (002-014). By the novel's later chapters, she had indeed become Mr. Gruensberg's secretary, performing "her work with the thoroughness of a mind which reveres details and never quite understands them" (018-004).

## Verona Babbitt's Employment

### From Filing-Clerk to Secretary

Verona Babbitt, "a dumpy brown-haired girl of twenty-two, just out of Bryn Mawr" (002-010), took the position at Gruensberg Leather Company as her first employment after college. Her father saw the job as a way of "getting some good out of your expensive college education till you're ready to marry and settle down" (002-014) — revealing his assumption that women's work was a temporary interlude before marriage, not a career.

Babbitt's pragmatic advice to his daughter was characteristically blunt: "If you get to be Gruensberg's secretary — and maybe you would, if you kept up your shorthand and didn't go sneaking off to concerts and talk-fests every evening — I guess you'll find thirty-five or forty bones a week worth while!" (002-014). The salary of thirty-five to forty dollars a week was presented as the ceiling of Verona's professional aspirations.

### The Secretary

By the time of the novel's later chapters, Verona had achieved the promised promotion. She "had become secretary to Mr. Gruensberg of the Gruensberg Leather Company; she did her work with the thoroughness of a mind which reveres details and never quite understands them" (018-004). Lewis adds a characteristic observation: "she was one of the people who give an agitating impression of being on the point of doing something desperate — of leaving a job or a husband — without ever doing it" (018-004).

## Social Function

The company serves as a narrative device to illustrate Verona's constrained position within both her family and Zenith society. A Bryn Mawr graduate employed as a filing-clerk and secretary in a leather factory office, Verona occupies a liminal space between her expensive education's promise and the narrow reality of women's work in a Midwestern industrial city. The Gruensberg Leather Company is the institutional setting where this contradiction plays out daily.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis's characterization of Verona's work — "the thoroughness of a mind which reveres details and never quite understands them" — is a quietly devastating sentence (018-004). It captures both Verona's dutiful nature and the intellectual limitations that her education and environment have imposed on her. The contrast between Bryn Mawr and a leather company filing room is itself a satirical comment on the gap between the Babbitt family's social aspirations and the realities of Zenith commerce.

The company also illuminates George Babbitt's conventional views on gender and work. His framing of Verona's job entirely in terms of its pre-marital utility — getting value from her education "till you're ready to marry and settle down" — reveals the era's assumptions about women's economic roles (002-014).
