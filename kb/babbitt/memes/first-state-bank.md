# First State Bank

The First State Bank of Zenith, the city's preeminent financial institution. Its president, William Washington Eathorne, embodies the old-money establishment that stands in quiet contrast to Babbitt's booster class, and the bank itself serves as a center of conservative power in the novel's social landscape.

## Description

The First State Bank is the leading bank in Zenith, presided over by the "twenty-year-old president of the First State Bank of Zenith" (016-018) — "twenty-year" here referring to his tenure, not his age; Eathorne is a figure of long-established authority. The bank is a pillar of the city's financial establishment, the kind of institution that does not advertise or hustle because it does not need to.

Its president, Eathorne, is described with a formality — "Eathorne, president of the First State Bank" (017-002) — that signals institutional weight. When Grover Butterbaugh announces "here's the new director of the First State Bank!" (017-041), the declaration has the quality of a social promotion.

## Social Function

The First State Bank functions as the financial counterpart to the Union Club: an institution of quiet, inherited power. Unlike Babbitt's real-estate office, which is all about hustling and selling, the bank is a place where money resides rather than circulates. It is the establishment.

The bank's role in the novel is primarily symbolic. Eathorne's authority derives from his position at the bank, and the bank's prestige rubs off on anyone associated with it. When Babbitt encounters the world of Eathorne and the First State Bank, he enters a realm of social status that his booster energy cannot access on its own.

- **Director announcement** — Grover Butterbaugh announces Babbitt as "the new director of the First State Bank!" (017-041), a moment that simultaneously elevates Babbitt and reveals his subordinate position within the old-money hierarchy
- **The office encounter** — Babbitt visits Eathorne at the bank, where the setting itself — the hushed corridors, the weight of institutional money — communicates the power differential between booster energy and establishment capital

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the First State Bank to delineate the class structure that booster ideology pretends does not exist. Babbitt may celebrate Zenith as a city of opportunity and democratic commerce, but the real power resides in institutions like the First State Bank, controlled by men like Eathorne who did not have to hustle for their position.

Babbitt's directorship of the bank is a minor honor, a gesture of respectability that flatters him without granting him real influence. It is typical of the novel's satire that one of Babbitt's proudest affiliations is with an institution whose president represents everything Babbitt is not: old money, quiet authority, effortless superiority.
