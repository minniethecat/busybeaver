# Religion

In *Babbitt*, religion functions less as a matter of personal faith than as a social institution that blesses the business order, polices morality, and provides the respectable framework for community life.

## Definition in Context

Babbitt's religion is indistinguishable from his business ethos. If asked about his faith, "he would have answered in the words of the Chatham Road Presbyterian Sunday School" (016-023). Lewis presents religion in Zenith as a "mechanical religion — a dry, hard church, shut off from the real world" (018-041). It is a faith that has been thoroughly commercialized, serving primarily to sanctify the existing social order rather than to challenge or transform it.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The religion of business** — The central metaphor of the novel fuses commerce and faith: Babbitt sees the skyscraper "as a temple-spire of the religion of business, a faith passionate, exalted, surpassing common men" (001-052), elevating economic activity to spiritual status.
- **Christianity Incorporated** — Babbitt's ideal is "a real virile hustling religion. Sort of Christianity Incorporated" (016-036), explicitly merging faith with corporate culture and masculine vigor.
- **Religion as social performance** — Babbitt's church work wins praise from his peers: "all of them tactfully mentioned Babbitt's sterling labors for religion" (017-040), demonstrating that religious participation is valued for its social currency rather than its spiritual sincerity.
- **The preacher as politician** — The minister is warned to "stick to straight religion and save souls, and not stir up a lot of discussion" (027-009) when his sermons threaten the political order, revealing the limits of acceptable faith.
- **Conversion as conformity** — When a character claims "I've gotten religion, George, since the terrible trouble" (026-017), the conversion is presented as a return to respectability rather than a spiritual awakening.

## Key Passages

> He beheld the tower as a temple-spire of the religion of business, a faith passionate, exalted, surpassing common men; and as he clumped down to breakfast he whistled the ballad "Oh, by gee, by gosh, by gum." (001-052)

> If you had asked Babbitt what his religion was, he would have answered in the words of the Chatham Road Presbyterian Sunday School — he would have stated the arguments learned by every sound American churchman. (016-023)

## Satirical Function

Lewis's portrayal of religion in Zenith is devastatingly satirical. By conflating faith with business ("the religion of business"), he exposes how thoroughly American Protestantism has been co-opted by capitalism. The church in Zenith does not challenge the pursuit of wealth — it blesses it. Babbitt's vague, inherited faith serves the same function as his Boosters' Club membership: it provides social belonging while demanding nothing of substance. The satire reaches its peak in the phrase "Christianity Incorporated" (016-036), which reduces the Gospel to a corporate slogan.
