# Politics

In Zenith, politics is an extension of business: the measure of a sound policy is whether it constitutes "a good—sound—economical—business—administration" (003-015). Political orthodoxy is enforced not by argument but by social consensus.

## Definition in Context

For Babbitt and his peers, political belief is inherited rather than examined. Babbitt's political philosophy consists of the conviction that the country needs "a good—sound—economical—business—administration" (003-015) — a formulation that reduces governance to efficient management and eliminates any possibility of genuine political thought. The Republican Party is less a political choice than a mark of respectability, and any deviation from conservative orthodoxy marks one as dangerous.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Babbitt's political "philosophy"** — When asked about the Republican candidate, Babbitt declares "I feel a fellow ought to keep an open mind and give all the candidates a show" (003-023), but his open-mindedness is entirely within the bounds of conservative Republicanism — he never considers any alternative to business-friendly governance.
- **Seneca Doane as the political other** — The progressive lawyer who "had turned crank, had headed farmer-labor tickets and fraternized with admitted socialists" (026-002) serves as the specter that haunts Zenith's political imagination — proof that deviation from orthodoxy leads to social exile.
- **The strike and the Red Scare** — When a labor strike hits Zenith, the response reveals the deep connection between business interests and political power. The minister is warned to "stick to straight religion and save souls, and not stir up a lot of discussion" (027-009) when he shows sympathy for strikers.
- **Politics as Love** — One character absurdly reframes labor conflict as "essentially and only a matter of Love, and of the practical application of the Christian religion" (027-012), spiritualizing away the material basis of class conflict.

## Key Passages

> "What the country needs—just at this present juncture—is neither a college president nor a lot of monkeying with foreign affairs, but a good—sound—economical—business—administration, that will give us a chance to have something like a decent turnover." (003-015)

> "It's too early to make any predictions yet... I feel a fellow ought to keep an open mind and give all the candidates a show—look 'em all over and size 'em up, and then decide carefully." (003-023)

## Satirical Function

Lewis exposes the intellectual bankruptcy of Zenith's political culture by showing that Babbitt's "open mind" extends only to different flavors of business Republicanism. Politics in Zenith is not about ideas — it is about tribal loyalty and the protection of property. The satirical high point is the character who insists that labor conflict is "only a matter of Love" (027-012), a formulation so disconnected from reality that it reveals the complete inability of Zenith's ruling class to think politically about anything that threatens their interests.
