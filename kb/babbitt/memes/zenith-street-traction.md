# Zenith Street Traction Company

The streetcar company that employs Howard Littlefield and serves as a nexus of business and political power in Zenith. The "Traction Bunch" represents the informal network of business interests that controls the city's infrastructure and politics.

## Howard Littlefield's Role

Howard Littlefield, Ph.D., served as the employment-manager and publicity-counsel of the Zenith Street Traction Company, and Babbitt was awed by Littlefield's confession that he often sat up till midnight reading Government reports (003-006). Littlefield's position was a perfect example of the revolving door between academia and regulated industry: a man armed with statistics and government documents, deployed to defend a monopoly's interests in the language of public service.

## The Infrastructure-Monopoly Nexus

The company's agent owned twenty-eight percent of all the contracts for paving in the city — an arrangement that illustrated the cozy relationship between Zenith's public utilities and municipal government (004-028). This was not corruption in the crude sense of bribery; it was the natural outcome of a system in which the men who ran the city's infrastructure also sat on the committees that awarded the contracts. The Street Traction Company was not merely a transportation provider; it was a political entity whose reach extended into paving, construction, and municipal finance.

## The Lumsen Neighborhood Controversy

Later, the company planned to build car-repair shops in the Martin Lumsen neighborhood, a decision that sparked local concern (019-002). The residents of that working-class district understood that industrial development in their neighborhood meant declining property values and increased noise — but they lacked the political leverage to stop a company whose owners controlled twenty-eight percent of the city's paving contracts.

## The Traction Bunch as Political Machine

The "Traction Bunch" was shorthand for the loose coalition of utility executives, paving contractors, and city officials who ran Zenith's infrastructure. Like the "Bank Bunch" and the "Real-Estate Bunch," it was an informal power network — no charter, no bylaws, no membership list — that nevertheless made the decisions that shaped the city's physical growth. Babbitt's own real-estate fortunes were tied to these decisions; the location of a new streetcar line could make or break a subdivision.

## The Streetcar as Symbol

In the novel's symbolic geography, the streetcar represents the nervous system of the modern city — the infrastructure that moves workers from home to factory and back, that shapes property values along its routes, that makes suburban development possible. Yet it is also a symbol of the mechanical monotony Babbitt feels but cannot name: the sense of being carried along tracks laid by others, unable to change direction, unable to get off.
