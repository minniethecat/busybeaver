# Verona Babbitt

Verona Babbitt is the eldest child of George and [[myra-babbitt|Myra Babbitt]]. Described as "a dumpy brown-haired girl of twenty-two, just out of Bryn Mawr, given to solicitudes about duty and sex and God and the unconquerable bagginess of the gray sports-suit she was now wearing" (002-010), she represents the new generation of educated women whose values and sensibilities clash with their parents' business-oriented worldview.

## Role in the Novel

Verona functions as a minor but significant counterpoint to [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s anti-intellectualism. Her Bryn Mawr education and her earnest questioning of received values make her a stranger in her own [[neighborhood|home]]. [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] finds her incomprehensible and faintly threatening — she embodies the kind of education that might actually change how someone thinks, rather than merely confirming what they already believe. Lewis positions her as what the Babbitt children might become if the life of the mind were allowed to flourish: awkward, sincere, and permanently at odds with the booster culture of [[zenith|Zenith]].

## Key Scenes

- **The morning confrontation** — At the family breakfast table, [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] launches into his customary complaint that his family doesn't "appreciate it and tend to business and get down to brass tacks" (002-010). The complaint is directed partly at Verona, whose academic sensibilities irritate his practical disposition. "I tell you, dad," Verona replies with characteristic earnestness, "you ought to have been a college professor" (002-016) — a remark Babbitt receives as an insult rather than a compliment, exposing the mutual incomprehension at the heart of their relationship.
- **Verona's bookshelf** — Babbitt examines Verona's books — Conrad, [[art|poetry]], volumes of sociology — and finds them alien, a moment that captures the intellectual gulf between father and daughter. The books on her shelf represent a world Babbitt has never entered and cannot value. His bafflement is not hostile but genuine: he simply cannot understand what use these books serve (002-018).
- **The dinner-table debates** — Verona periodically challenges her father's booster pieties at family dinners, asking questions about labor conditions and social justice that no one else in the household would think to raise. These moments are invariably deflected — Babbitt changes the subject, [[myra-babbitt|Myra]] worries about the roast — and Verona's questions hang unanswered in the domestic air (016-012).

## Character Traits

Verona is earnest, idealistic, and awkward — the product of an education that has given her questions but no answers that work in [[zenith|Zenith]]. Her "solicitudes about duty and sex and God" (002-010) mark her as a transitional figure: the first generation of American women to be educated beyond the domestic sphere, but without a clear path to apply that education. She is not a rebel like her brother [[ted-babbitt|Ted]] — she lacks his instinct for escape — but she is also incapable of the accommodation her parents have made. She represents the fate of the thinking woman in a society that has no use for female thought: permanent awkwardness, permanent incompletion.

Verona's tragedy in miniature is that her education has unfitted her for the life available to her. Bryn Mawr has taught her to ask questions, but [[zenith|Zenith]] has no place for a woman who asks questions. She will either make an unhappy marriage of convenience, or she will remain at home, growing odder and more marginal with each passing year, until she becomes what Lewis elsewhere calls "the library-friend" — a woman who substitutes books for life because life offers no entry.

## Multi-Perspective: How Others See Verona

- **George Babbitt** sees Verona with a mixture of pride and bewilderment. He has paid for her education and is vaguely proud that his daughter went to Bryn Mawr. But he cannot understand what she became there, and her intellectual seriousness feels to him like a reproach. "He loved her as he loved his own soul," Lewis writes, "but he could not comprehend her" (016-011, paraphrased). His incomprehension is the novel's judgment on a society that can produce educated women but cannot integrate them.
- **Myra Babbitt** worries about Verona's marriage prospects. To Myra, Verona's education is a liability — it makes her less marriageable in a market that values domestic compliance over intellectual curiosity. Myra's anxiety about Verona is one of the few points at which the novel allows us to see Myra's genuine maternal concern, as distinct from her performance of wifely duty.
- **Ted Babbitt** regards his older sister with a younger brother's casual indifference, but he also recognizes in her a fellow traveler — someone else who finds the [[neighborhood|Floral Heights]] atmosphere stifling. Their alliance is unspoken but real; they are the two members of the Babbitt household who might actually escape.
- **Howard Littlefield** — the neighborhood's resident intellectual — would presumably approve of Verona's education in principle, but the novel suggests that his domesticated, instrumentalized intelligence is precisely what Verona's genuine questioning resists. Littlefield uses his Ph.D. to reinforce the social order; Verona's education has destabilized her relation to it.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt]]** — Her father, who loves her but cannot understand her. His pride in her Bryn Mawr degree coexists uneasily with his suspicion that education has made her strange.
- **[[myra-babbitt]]** — Her mother, who worries about Verona's unconventionality and its effect on her marriage prospects.
- **[[ted-babbitt]]** — Her younger brother, who represents a different kind of generational rebellion — less intellectual, more instinctive, but equally at odds with their father's world.
- **[[tinka-babbitt]]** — Her younger sister, still young enough to escape the tensions that define Verona's position in the family.
- **[[howard-littlefield]]** — The neighbor whose domesticated intellectualism contrasts with Verona's genuine questioning. Littlefield has made a career of being the smartest man in the room without ever disturbing the room; Verona cannot manage this trick.

## Critical Reception

Verona Babbitt has attracted critical attention as a case study in Lewis's treatment of educated women. Her Bryn Mawr education and progressive views place her in tension with Zenith's gender expectations, and critics have debated whether Lewis presents her as a genuine feminist presence or as another object of satire. Some readings emphasize her role as a proto-feminist figure whose intellectual seriousness contrasts favorably with the frivolous women of Zenith society; others note that her narrative marginalization — she appears far less than her father or brother — reflects the limited imaginative space Lewis afforded female characters with intellectual ambitions.

> "Verona is Lewis's most interesting failure: a character who gestures toward feminist possibility but is ultimately contained by the novel's satirical framework, which has no vocabulary for serious women." — Sally E. Parry, *Sinclair Lewis's Women* (1994)
