# United Cigar Store

A Zenith retail shop whose crimson and gold signage catches George Babbitt's eye during his morning commute, prompting a moment of consumer self-reflection characteristic of his oscillating relationship to the commercial landscape of the city. Cigars, for Babbitt, are far more than a commodity — they are a ritual object, a social prop, and a recurring site of failed self-discipline throughout the novel.

## Description

The United Cigar Store is one of the downtown businesses that line Babbitt's route to his office in the Reeves Building. During his daily drive down Third Street, he passes the store and registers its "crimson and gold alertness" — a phrase that captures both the visual impact of its signage and the energy of Zenith's commercial district (005-009). The store's colors and lively presentation are part of the urban spectacle that Babbitt finds reassuring, a visual confirmation of the city's prosperity and his own place within it. It sits among a catalog of commercial landmarks: the California Building, the Parthenon Shoe Shine Parlor, the Simplex Office Furniture Shop, the National Cash Register Agency, and the Nobby Men's Wear Shop (005-007–009).

## Babbitt's Encounter

Babbitt's reaction upon seeing the United Cigar Store is revealing in its rapid oscillation:

> "Wonder if I need some cigars — idiot — plumb forgot — going t' cut down my fool smoking" (005-009)

The sequence of thought — desire, self-rebuke, memory of a resolution, and renewed self-criticism — encapsulates Babbitt's conflicted relationship with consumption. He is drawn to the goods on display, yet also aware that his appetites rarely align with his intentions. The cigar store becomes a mirror of his inner conflict between the sensual indulgence he craves and the businessman's ideal of disciplined self-control.

## Cigars as a Motif in *Babbitt*

The United Cigar Store is a single point in a network of cigar-related imagery that runs throughout the novel. Babbitt's day begins with "the gloriously satisfying first cigar of the day" (002-017), and the ritual is repeated — "illegally early" — with "his second cigar of the day" (003-010). His self-image as a solid businessman is inseparable from the prop of the large cigar: he pictures himself as a man "smoking a large cigar, driving a good motor along a lake" (003-018). Among his prized possessions are "a gold penknife, silver cigar-cutter, seven keys" (001-040) — the cigar-cutter ranked alongside the instruments of property and access.

Yet cigars are also the object of Babbitt's repeated and always-failed attempts at self-reform. He "tapered off his allowance of cigars" and "brought himself down to three cigars a day" (004-013–014), then devised "a system of leaving his cigar-case and cigarette-box in an inaccessible drawer" (004-015). The system fails, inevitably: "he rushed out and locked up his cigars, his cigarettes, and even his pipe" (004-016), but the resolution dissolves as quickly as it forms. The pattern — desire, restraint, collapse, self-reproach — is the rhythm of Babbitt's entire inner life, and the cigar is its most reliable trigger.

## Social Context

Cigar stores in 1920s America were more than retail outlets; they were social spaces where men gathered, browsed, and lingered. The United Cigar Store, with its prominent display and downtown location, participates in the masculine commercial culture that Lewis depicts throughout the novel. Cigars function as markers of male camaraderie and business negotiation — Babbitt offers his guests cigars from his office correspondence file, an act that merges hospitality with the tools of commerce (004-036). The news and cigar stand in Babbitt's own office building is "in no way city-like" (003-037), marking the boundary between Zenith's pretensions to metropolitan status and its provincial reality.

## Satirical Significance

The United Cigar Store, modest as it is, condenses one of Lewis's central satirical insights: that Babbitt's identity is constructed through the objects he desires and purchases, not through any authentic self-knowledge. The "crimson and gold alertness" of the storefront is a promise — of pleasure, of status, of the kind of man Babbitt imagines himself to be — that the product itself cannot fulfill. His self-castigation ("idiot — plumb forgot") reveals his awareness of this gap between promise and fulfillment, but awareness is not enough to break the cycle. Lewis presents Babbitt not as a victim of advertising but as a willing participant in his own commodification, a man who measures his soul in cigar-cutters and bank balances.
