## Description

The Eden Hotel is a modest lodging establishment in Chicago, not Zenith. It serves as the default accommodation for Zenith businessmen traveling to the larger city — "because Zenith business men always stayed at the Eden" (019-030). The hotel represents a deliberate choice of economy over luxury, in contrast to the grander hotels frequented by Zenith's elite when hosting visitors at home.

## Role in the Novel

### Babbitt's Chicago Trip with Ted

The Eden Hotel appears during Babbitt's trip to Chicago with his son Ted, an expedition that becomes one of the novel's most intimate father-son episodes. They arrive in Chicago late at night and "lay abed in the morning, rejoicing, 'Pretty nice not to have to get up and get down to breakfast, heh?'" (019-030). The choice of the modest Eden is explained with institutional familiarity: "Zenith business men always stayed at the Eden" (019-030). It is the established habit of a class, not an individual preference.

The choice of the Eden for lodging allows the Babbitts a theatrical contrast at dinner: "they had dinner in the brocade and crystal Versailles Room of the Regency Hotel" (019-030). Babbitt orders extravagantly — "Blue Point oysters with cocktail sauce, a tremendous steak with a tremendous platter of French fried potatoes, two pots of coffee, apple pie with ice cream for both of them and, for Ted, an extra piece of mince pie" — prompting Ted to admire, "Some feed, young fella!" (019-030) (019-031). The split between lodging at the Eden and dining at the Regency encapsulates Babbitt's contradictory ethos: save on the room, spend on the meal, and perform prosperity where it can be seen.

### The Lonely Return

After Ted returns to Zenith, Babbitt remains in Chicago on business. He dines by himself at the Regency Hotel and "sat in the lobby afterward, in a plush chair bedecked with the Saxe-Coburg arms, lighting a cigar and looking for some one who would come and play with him and save him from thinking" (019-032). It is in this lonely state that he encounters Sir Gerald Doak in the Regency lobby — a chance meeting that leads to an evening of drinking and club-hopping that briefly liberates Babbitt from his Zenith self [019-033 through 019-035]. The Eden Hotel, implicitly, is where Babbitt returns after these Regency adventures — the modest daytime self to which the nighttime reveler must revert.

## Social Function

The Eden Hotel functions as a marker of class identity and institutional habit. "Zenith business men always stayed at the Eden" — the word "always" signals that this is not a cost calculation made fresh each trip but a settled norm, a piece of shared institutional knowledge passed between businessmen. Staying at the Eden is what a Zenith businessman does in Chicago, as automatic as belonging to the Boosters' Club or living in Floral Heights.

The choice also reflects a quiet provincial anxiety. The narrator notes that after the "recent convention of the International Organization of Boosters' Clubs" in Zenith, the businessmen "had an uncomfortable feeling that they had overdone the luxurious hotels" (019-030). Staying at the modest Eden is thus a corrective, a ritual of modesty that balances the periodic excesses of booster hospitality. It is performative asceticism — the business class demonstrating to itself that it can be frugal when necessary.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Eden Hotel to expose the hierarchy of American business travel and the contradictions of the booster mentality. The split between the Eden (lodging) and the Regency (dining) reveals a class that wants to be seen spending money but not wasting it — a careful calibration of visibility and economy. The businessman who saves on his hotel room but orders "a tremendous steak" at the finest restaurant is performing two different selves for two different audiences.

The Eden also serves as a counterpoint to Zenith's own hotel culture. At home, Zenith businessmen build and patronize ever-grander hotels as monuments to civic progress — the Thornleigh, the Regency, the Sedgwick. Away from home, they lodge at the Eden. The contrast suggests that the booster's commitment to luxury is not a matter of personal taste but of civic performance: in Chicago, where no one from Zenith is watching, frugality is permissible. At home, where every hotel is a totem of the city's commercial vigor, economy would be a betrayal.

This modest hotel, with its ironic name — Eden, paradise — houses businessmen who are, in Chicago, temporarily expelled from the garden of Zenith's self-regard. They sleep at the Eden and dine at the Regency: a daily migration from modesty to pretense that mirrors the larger rhythms of their lives.
