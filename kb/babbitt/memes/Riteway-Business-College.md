# Riteway Business College

Riteway Business College is a for-profit vocational school in Zenith, owned and operated by Professor Joseph K. Pumphrey. It offers practical business-oriented courses including Public Speaking, Business English, Scenario Writing, and Commercial Law, catering to Zenith's aspiring businessmen and professionals. The institution appears in the novel primarily through its proprietor, who is a fixture in Zenith's network of booster luncheons and business social gatherings.

## Description

The college is owned by Professor Joseph K. Pumphrey, who serves as an instructor in Public Speaking, Business English, Scenario Writing, and Commercial Law (PN 005-016). The name "Riteway" itself is characteristic of Lewis's satirical naming—a phonetic spelling that suggests both "right way" and the commercial impulse toward catchy branding, reducing the dignity of education to a marketing slogan.

Pumphrey's business college is part of the ecosystem of Zenith's booster culture, where middle-class professionals gather at luncheons and clubs. The professor appears alongside other Zenith businessmen—a coal-dealer, a department-store buyer, a jeweler, a condensed-milk company executive, a tailor, a photographer, and a photo-engraver—at social gatherings where business connections are reinforced (PN 005-016, PN 021-004). This roll call of professions captures the breadth of Zenith's commercial middle class, from raw materials (coal) to retail (department store) to luxury goods (jewelry) to food processing (condensed milk) to services (tailoring, photography).

## Curriculum

The college's course offerings reveal the priorities of vocational education in Zenith:

- **Public Speaking** — essential for the booster luncheon circuit, where every businessman is expected to deliver stirring calls to civic and commercial action
- **Business English** — the language of contracts, correspondence, and commercial persuasion, stripped of literary pretension
- **Scenario Writing** — a course reflecting the era's fascination with motivational narratives and self-improvement through imaginative projection
- **Commercial Law** — the practical legal knowledge needed to navigate contracts, partnerships, and regulatory matters

The absence of any liberal arts or humanities subjects from the curriculum is itself a statement: education in Zenith exists to serve commerce, not to cultivate the mind.

## Social Function

Riteway Business College serves as a vehicle for the pragmatic, commercially-oriented education valued in Zenith. Its curriculum—focused entirely on practical business skills—reflects the city's anti-intellectual ethos, where education serves commerce rather than culture or critical thought. The college and its proprietor are fixtures in Zenith's network of booster clubs and business luncheons, where professional identity and social standing are intertwined.

The institution also functions as a credentialing mechanism for Zenith's aspiring class. In a city where "business is the science of sciences," a diploma from Riteway carries weight not for its intellectual rigor but for its demonstration of commitment to commercial values. The college certifies not knowledge but attitude—the right kind of ambition, channeled in the right direction.

## Key Members

- **Professor Joseph K. Pumphrey** — owner, proprietor, and instructor in Public Speaking, Business English, Scenario Writing, and Commercial Law (PN 005-016)

## Key Scenes

Riteway Business College does not appear directly as a setting; it is represented entirely through its proprietor. Pumphrey appears at the Babbitt dinner party among the roster of Zenith businessmen whom Babbitt hosts (PN 005-016). He appears again in a later gathering of the same booster network, alongside Dr. Walter Gorbutt, Roy Teegarten, and other fixtures of Zenith's commercial-social circuit (PN 021-004).

## Satirical Significance

Professor Pumphrey's title and the college's curriculum are objects of Lewis's satire. The gap between the academic title "Professor" and the purely commercial nature of his enterprise highlights the pretensions of Zenith's business class—the title borrows the prestige of scholarship while the enterprise itself is a for-profit venture teaching not wisdom but salesmanship. The inclusion of "Scenario Writing" alongside more conventional business subjects reflects the era's get-rich-quick educational fads, while the phonetic brand name "Riteway" satirizes the commercial impulse to reduce education to a branded product.

The college embodies what Lewis saw as the fundamental confusion of American commercial culture: the belief that education is valuable only insofar as it serves business, and that business success is the only legitimate measure of a person's worth. In Zenith, even the language of learning is co-opted—a "college" need not teach the liberal arts, a "professor" need not profess anything beyond sales technique, and the "right way" is always and only the way of commerce.
