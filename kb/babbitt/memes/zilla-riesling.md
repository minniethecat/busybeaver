# Zilla Riesling

The wife whose "horrible brassy laugh" (005-031) and ceaseless criticism destroy [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]] — and through him, threaten the emotional foundation of [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s world.

## Biography

Zilla Riesling is married to [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]], [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s closest friend. She is portrayed almost entirely through the eyes of Paul and Babbitt, who see her as a force of domestic destruction. She insists on expensive vacations "with the bright lights and the bootlegged cocktails and a bunch of lounge-lizards to dance with" (005-036), embodying the consumer desires that Paul's modest roofing business cannot satisfy.

## Role in the Novel

Zilla functions as the novel's most unambiguous antagonist — the personification of everything that destroys Paul. She "went right on talking about the littlest, smallest thing" (005-027) in public, humiliating Paul through a relentless campaign of small cruelties. Paul's eventual violence against her is the novel's darkest moment, and Zilla's role in provoking it raises uncomfortable questions about gender, victimhood, and the novel's own sympathies.

> She was a woman who had by long practice learned to nag, and who, having discovered that her husband was too gentle to beat her, had become almost professional in her talent for making his life a burden. (paraphrased from Ch. X)

## Key Scenes

- **Paul's confession about Zilla** — Paul tells Babbitt that Zilla publicly humiliates him: "I can tell you exactly how every tile looks in the ceiling of that lobby" (005-027) — he has memorized the ceiling because he cannot bear to look at the people watching his humiliation.
- **The "divorce" question** — When Babbitt asks "Why don't you divorce Zilla?" (005-030), Paul's reply reveals the trap he is in: Zilla "keeps rooting for a nice expensive vacation" (005-036) he cannot afford.
- **Paul's desperate fantasy** — Paul confesses he wishes Zilla would be unfaithful: "honestly, I'd be tickled to death if she'd really go making love with somebody" (005-031), revealing the depth of his desperation to escape the marriage through any means.
- **The shooting** — Paul's act of violence against Zilla — shooting her in a moment of uncontainable rage — is the novel's darkest event. It transforms Paul from sympathetic figure to criminal, and forces Babbitt to confront the potential for destruction latent in every Zenith marriage.

## Character Traits

Zilla is portrayed as a woman of bottomless dissatisfaction — with Paul's income, his social standing, his very existence. Her "horrible brassy laugh" (005-031) functions as an auditory symbol of her destructive presence. Lewis's portrait of Zilla reflects the period's anxieties about marriage, gender roles, and female power within the domestic sphere. Though rendered with little sympathy, Zilla represents a specific social type of the era: the middle-class wife whose ambitions outstrip her husband's means, and whose discontent becomes a weapon turned against the domestic sphere she is supposed to sanctify.

## Relationships

- **[[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]]** — Her husband, whom she systematically destroys through criticism and financial pressure
- **[[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]** — Her husband's best friend, who despises her as a threat to his own domestic peace
- **[[myra-babbitt|Myra Babbitt]]** — The wife who, unlike Zilla, maintains the appearance of domestic peace — but whose own marriage is also a quiet battlefield
