# Stanley Graff

Stanley Graff is an outside salesman at the [[babbitt-thompson-realty|Babbitt-Thompson Realty]] Company. Young, aggressive, and ultimately dishonest, Graff is the dark mirror of the sales ethos that [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]] champions: the same competitiveness [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] celebrates produces not only [[prosperity|success]] but also corruption (019-010).

## Role in the Novel

Graff functions as a cautionary figure — the salesman whose competitive drive crosses over into dishonesty, sha[[family|kin]]g [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s complacent [[religion|faith]] in the sales profession. [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is personally invested in seeing himself as the honest businessman, and Graff's exposure forces him to confront the uncomfortable proximity between sharp practice and outright theft (019-023). Graff also serves as a foil: where [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] channels his aggression into civic [[community|boosterism]], Graff channels his into larceny.

## Key Scenes

- **Office politics at [[babbitt-thompson-realty|Babbitt-Thompson]]** — Graff is first introduced as one of the nine employees of the firm, "the outside salesman — a young man of questionable habits" who taunts [[miss-mcgoun|the stenographer]] (003-040, 003-042).
- **[[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s complaint** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] grumbles to Graff about the "tan-colored voice" of [[conrad-lyte|Conrad Lyte]], a rival real-estate developer. The exchange reveals the casual [[friendship|camaraderie]] and shared grievances of [[babbitt-office|the office]] (004-007).
- **Fighting with Graff** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is described as frequently quarreling with Graff "about repairs," suggesting the fractious daily life of [[babbitt-office|the office]] (006-013).
- **The revelation of dishonesty** — Graff is definitively labeled "the dishonest one" among the outside salesmen. His dishonesty and the disappointment it causes [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] are significant enough to shake [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s [[religion|faith]] in his own judgment (019-010, 019-023).

## Character Traits

Graff is restless, inventive, and morally casual — a product of a system that rewards results over methods. His dishonesty is not presented as an aberration but as the natural terminus of a logic [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] himself subscribes to: sell, compete, win, by whatever means necessary. In Graff's fall, [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] glimpses the fragility of his own moral position.
