# Floral Heights

[[zenith|Zenith]]'s premier middle-class suburb — "a region of large houses and large ideals" (001-022) where [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]] has achieved the dream of home ownership. Floral Heights is the geography of the Babbitt class: prosperous, respectable, and relentlessly uniform.

## Description

Floral Heights lies outside the center of [[zenith]], accessible by trolley and motorcar. Its streets are "lined with maples and oaks," and the houses — mostly Dutch Colonials like the Babbitts' — stand "competent and glossy" on their neatly trimmed lots (001-051). The name itself is a piece of suburban poetry, elevating the merely comfortable to the pastoral, and it signals the aspirations of its residents as clearly as any motto.

> The roofs were bright and new; the lawns were like immaculate turf in a golf course. (paraphrased from 003-017)

The neighborhood functions as a visual demonstration of its residents' success. Each house is a statement, and the statements are identical: I have arrived. I belong. I can afford this.

## Role in the Novel

Floral Heights is more than setting — it is the physical manifestation of Babbitt's social position. His house there is his "citadel of the Babbitt clan" (002-005), proof that he has escaped the city's less desirable precincts for the respectability of the suburbs. The daily commute between Floral Heights and downtown [[zenith]] marks the boundary between domestic and commercial life, and the trolley that carries Babbitt between them is a recurring motif of his divided existence.

The neighborhood is also the stage for Babbitt's domestic life — the morning bathroom rush, the breakfast table combat, the neighborly exchanges with [[howard-littlefield|Howard Littlefield]] next door. Its very perfection is the problem: "it was not a home" (002-007), the novel observes of the Babbitt house, suggesting that Floral Heights is a neighborhood of houses rather than homes, a showcase of achievement rather than a place of genuine living.

> He had a house in Floral Heights, which is the best residential district of Zenith — which means the very best. (paraphrased from 001-018)

## Key Scenes Set Here

- **The morning departure** — Babbitt's daily exit from the house, with Myra's mechanical farewell and the children's squabbling, sets the tone for domestic life on Floral Heights (003-027).
- **The neighborly lecture** — [[howard-littlefield|Howard Littlefield]], the Babbitts' next-door neighbor and Zenith's unofficial philosopher, delivers his pontifications from the adjacent yard, confirming the businessmen of the neighborhood in their faith (007-004, 007-005).
- **The dinner party** — The Babbitts' elaborate dinner party for the McKelveys, the novel's most devastating social set piece, takes place in the Floral Heights house and reveals the anxious striving beneath the neighborhood's placid surface (008-014).
- **Babbitt's private discontent** — It is in his Floral Heights bed, in the darkness before dawn, that Babbitt secretly "detested the grind of the real-estate business" (001-015) — the neighborhood of achievement is also the neighborhood of unspoken despair.

## Associated Characters

- **[[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]** — The neighborhood's exemplary resident, who has achieved the suburban dream and privately questions its worth
- **[[myra-babbitt|Myra Babbitt]]** — The household manager, whose domain extends from the sleeping-porch to the sun-parlor
- **[[howard-littlefield|Howard Littlefield]]** — The Babbitts' next-door neighbor, "the Great Scholar" of Floral Heights
- **[[vergil-gunch|Vergil Gunch]]** — Fellow Floral Heights resident, coal-dealer, and enforcer of neighborhood orthodoxy

## Themes

- **[[conformity]]** — Floral Heights is the architecture of conformity, street after street of identical houses embodying identical values
- **[[social-class|Class and Social Hierarchy]]** — The neighborhood marks a precise rung on Zenith's social ladder: above the apartment-dwellers, below the mansion-owners, and perpetually insecure about the distinction
- **[[consumerism]]** — The houses of Floral Heights are consumer goods, purchased as proof of success rather than for the life they contain
- **[[family|Domesticity]]** — Floral Heights is where domestic ideals go to be performed; the performance is convincing, but it is still a performance
