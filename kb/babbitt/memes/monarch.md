# Monarch

Zenith's "chief rival among the lesser cities of the state" (013-004), Monarch is a medium-sized Midwestern city that mirrors Zenith's booster ethos while cultivating a marginally more cosmopolitan self-image. The real-estate convention Babbitt attends in Chapter 13 unfolds almost entirely in Monarch, making the city both setting and satirical foil.

## Description

Monarch is reached from Zenith by the midnight train, arriving just before dawn (013-029). Its outskirts consist of "small brown wooden houses" (013-078) that give way to a commercial core boasting a mayor's office, the First Christian Church (013-036), a Chamber of Commerce, and the *Monarch Herald* newspaper (013-065). A local described it to Babbitt as "a lot sportier than Zenith" (013-079) — faint praise from a rival that reveals more about the speaker's civic vanity than about either city.

The Monarch Chamber of Commerce organizes "motor tours" for visiting conventioneers (013-043), and the city's hospitality extends to a formal welcome by the mayor and a banquet described as lasting "incessantly and firmly" (013-039). The Convention itself is held in a large hall where delegates from across the state gather to celebrate the real-estate profession.

## Role in the Novel

Monarch appears almost exclusively in Chapter 13, where Babbitt attends the annual convention of the state real-estate association. The trip to Monarch is Babbitt's escape from domestic routine: a "fine, lively, manly" outing (013-008) of speeches, banquets, and "good fellows" — the prose equivalent of the Zenith Athletic Club transported to another city.

Structurally, the Monarch convention functions as a concentrated dose of booster culture, stripped of the domestic backdrop that softens it in Zenith. The speeches are more florid, the backslapping more desperate, and Babbitt's private doubts more audible. When a delegate whispers that Babbitt "just kind of stayed in Monarch and threw a party" (013-058), it lands as both accusation and confession: Babbitt did precisely that, and it was precisely what he came for.

## Key Scenes Set Here

- **The midnight train to Monarch** — Babbitt and his fellow delegates board the train at Zenith, "all of them, save Cecil Rountree" settling "in the smoking-compartment" (013-019) for an evening of cigars, cards, and masculine camaraderie
- **Arrival and civic welcome** — The porter wakes the delegates "half an hour before they reached Monarch" (013-029); Babbitt disembarks with his "suit-case" (013-031) to a formal greeting by the mayor and the pastor of the First Christian Church (013-036)
- **The convention banquet** — A prolonged affair of speeches and fellowship that runs "incessantly and firmly" (013-039), followed by motor tours arranged by the Monarch Chamber of Commerce (013-043)
- **Babbitt's unscheduled party** — Rather than attending the formal program, Babbitt "just kind of stayed in Monarch and threw a party" (013-058), glimpsed later in the *Monarch Herald* advertisement for an unnamed establishment (013-065)

## Themes

Monarch represents the interchangeability of American booster cities. Its mayor, Chamber of Commerce, newspaper, churches, and motor tours replicate the Zenith template so precisely that the rivalry between the two cities becomes a satire of civic pretension: two nearly identical places competing for distinction through the thickness of their banquet programs. That Monarch is called "sportier" — a quality Babbitt presumably values — only deepens the irony, since the "sport" in question is indistinguishable from the same masculine carousing he might have done in Zenith.
