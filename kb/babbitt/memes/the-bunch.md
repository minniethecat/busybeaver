# The Bunch

"The Bunch" is the bohemian social circle clustered around [[tanis-judique|Tanis Judique]], centered on Healey Hanson's saloon and the apartment evenings at her flat. They represent Babbitt's first taste of rebellion — a world of jazz, prohibition-defying whisky, and self-congratulatory unconventionality that both attracts and ultimately disappoints him.

## Definition in Context

The Bunch is Lewis's satirical portrait of provincial bohemianism — a counterfeit liberation that substitutes drinking, dancing, and gossip about "crabs, pikers, and poor fish" for genuine individuality. The group consists of soda-fountain clerks, a railway clerk named Fulton Bemis, several "overdressed and slightly effeminate young men," and thin, sarcastic young women like Minnie Sonntag (029-023 through 029-033). Their conversation "was exclamatory, high-colored, full of references to people whom Babbitt did not know," and "apparently they thought very comfortably of themselves. They were the Bunch, wise and beautiful and amusing; they were Bohemians and urbanites, accustomed to all the luxuries of Zenith" (029-033).

The Bunch's importance in the novel lies not in who they are but in what they reveal about Babbitt. He is initially dazzled, then progressively disenchanted, as he discovers that the Bunch's rebellion is as conformist in its own way as the Boosters' Club — "Bohemian" only in the most self-congratulatory and superficial sense.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Healey Hanson's saloon** — The Bunch's headquarters, a speakeasy where Fulton Bemis supplies "raw but vigorous whisky" (029-032). The Prohibition-defying drinking is both the Bunch's primary ritual and the engine of Babbitt's initial acceptance into the group
- **The apartment evenings** — Tanis Judique's flat serves as the Bunch's salon, where jazz records play, dancing happens on the living-room floor, and sitting on the floor is considered the height of Bohemian daring. The gatherings rotate among members, with each member "expected to telephone to every other member at least once a week" (029-037)
- **The social hierarchy within** — Despite its egalitarian pretensions, the Bunch has its own internal pecking order. The "surly older man" Fulton Bemis exercises a sullen dominance; Pete and Carrie Nork, "the most nearly intelligent of the nimble youths," serve as gatekeepers; and newcomers like Babbitt must earn their place through alcohol and agreement
- **Babbitt's disillusionment** — Three drinks into an evening, Babbitt sees the Bunch as "tender" and "philosophic"; sober, he feels "social inferiority" when comparing their seedy bohemianism against the solid prosperity of the McKelvey set (029-035). The Bunch reveals Babbitt as a man caught between two worlds, belonging fully to neither
- **Tanis as den mother** — The Bunch exists entirely through Tanis Judique's social energy. Her apartment, her telephone, her whisky supply, and her restless need for company are the group's infrastructure. When Babbitt abandons Tanis, he abandons the Bunch as well — the two are inseparable

## Key Passages

> The conversation of the Bunch was exclamatory, high-colored, full of references to people whom Babbitt did not know. Apparently they thought very comfortably of themselves. They were the Bunch, wise and beautiful and amusing; they were Bohemians and urbanites, accustomed to all the luxuries of Zenith: dance-halls, movie-theaters, and roadhouses; and in a cynical superiority to people who were "slow" or "tightwad" they cackled... (029-033)

> He began to think extremely well of the Bunch. When he mentioned his friends Sir Gerald Doak, Lord Wycombe, William Washington Eathorne, and Chum Frink, he was proud of their condescending interest. (029-035)

## Satirical Function

The Bunch is one of Lewis's most economical satires. In a single chapter, he anatomizes a certain kind of provincial bohemianism — the rebellion that is merely an inversion of conformity, the unconventionality that enforces its own rigid conventions. The Bunch's "Bohemianism" is a parody of genuine artistic or intellectual freedom: they have substituted drinking for thinking, gossip for conversation, and self-congratulation for self-awareness.

The deeper satire is directed at Babbitt himself. He is sophisticated enough to find the Boosters stifling but not sophisticated enough to see that the Bunch is merely the Boosters' mirror image — a different uniform, an identical demand for conformity. His eventual disillusionment with the Bunch is one of the novel's quietest tragedies: he has glimpsed two versions of community, and neither one fits.
