# W. A. Rogers

W. A. Rogers is a Zenith dealer in farm-lands who serves as one of the five official delegates to the Monarch real-estate convention and becomes George F. Babbitt's roommate, drinking companion, and partner in escapade during the novel's great set piece of masculine release. He is a minor figure, but a vivid one — a man whose rustic appearance belies a shrewd business sense and a genial talent for carousing.

## Role in the Novel

Rogers appears exclusively in Chapter XIII, the convention chapter, where he serves as a kind of alter ego to Babbitt: a fellow businessman who, freed from the constraints of wife and office, embraces the convention's atmosphere of boozy camaraderie with uncomplicated enthusiasm. He is introduced among the official delegates — "Babbitt, Rountree, W. A. Rogers, Alvin Thayer, and Elbert Wing" (013-018) — and immediately becomes Babbitt's companion.

At the Hotel Sedgwick, Rogers shares a room with Babbitt. Lewis describes him as "that shrewd, rustic-looking Zenith dealer in farm-lands" (013-032), a characterization that captures the duality of the man: he looks like a country rube, but he knows exactly what he is doing.

## The Convention Companion

Over the course of the convention, Rogers functions as Babbitt's partner in indulgence. He tramps the train platform with Babbitt and Wing (013-024), shares a "noble breakfast, with waffles, and coffee not in exiguous cups but in large pots" (013-032), and joins the tea with the Sassburgers at the Catalpa Inn (013-056).

When Babbitt proposes extending their stay in Monarch rather than returning to their wives, it is Rogers who replies with comradely approval: "George, you speak with the tongue of wisdom and sagashiteriferousness" (013-058). Later, at the improvised party in the hotel room, Rogers takes the lead in "kidding" the Italian waiter with absurd dinner orders: "Say, Gooseppy, I want a couple o' fried elephants' ears" (013-064). It is Rogers, too, who drawls the suggestion that sets the evening's course: "What say we go down the line and look over the girls?" (013-077).

## Character and Significance

Rogers is what Babbitt might be if he were slightly less anxious about respectability. Where Babbitt oscillates between indulgence and guilt, Rogers seems to enjoy the convention without existential torment. He is the unselfconscious Babbitt, the Babbitt who has not yet begun to question whether the Boosters' Club gospel is the whole truth. His rustic shrewdness — he deals in farm-lands, the most elemental form of real estate — suggests a man closer to the soil than to the abstractions of modern commerce, and perhaps, for that reason, more at ease with his appetites.

In the morning, Rogers awakens with a hangover and a complaint: "Ow, what a head! I certainly do feel like the wrath of God this morning. Say! I know what was the matter! Somebody went and put alcohol in my booze last night" (013-080). The joke is vintage Lewis: the man who drank whisky from teacups all evening is shocked to discover that his booze contained alcohol. Babbitt does not smile at the jest; he is already sliding back into the shame-faced contentment that will carry him home to Zenith and Myra.
