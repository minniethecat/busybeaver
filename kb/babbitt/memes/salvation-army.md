# Salvation Army

An international Christian charitable and evangelical organization founded in 1865 by William and Catherine Booth, invoked in Sinclair Lewis's *Babbitt* (1922) as a symbol of financial desperation and diminished social standing in the imagination of the American middle class.

## Description

The Salvation Army was a familiar presence in American cities by the 1920s, known for its uniformed officers, street-corner brass bands, and charitable relief programs for the urban poor. In the novel, however, the organization appears not through direct depiction of its activities but through a characteristic complaint by George F. Babbitt about his financial pressures. Babbitt remarks that with his income and expenses, "they were putting me in the Salvation Army collection-box" — a hyperbolic expression of feeling financially squeezed despite his comfortable middle-class circumstances (010-040).

The reference draws on the Salvation Army's widely recognized dual identity as both an evangelical mission and a provider of material relief. Its red collection kettles, deployed on city streets during the Christmas season, were a visible reminder of the boundary between those who gave and those who received. Babbitt's rhetorical self-positioning on the wrong side of that boundary — however facetious — reveals the anxiety that underlies his performance of prosperity.

## Social Function

In Babbitt's rhetorical usage, the Salvation Army serves as a cultural shorthand for the specter of downward mobility. To be the recipient rather than the donor of charity is, in his worldview, a mark of social failure. The reference reveals Babbitt's deep anxiety about class position: even as he enjoys the material comfort of a Floral Heights home, a car, and membership in the Boosters' Club, he fears slipping into a condition where he would need the kind of help the Salvation Army provides (010-040).

The organization's function in the novel is thus primarily symbolic rather than narrative. It marks the lower boundary of the middle-class respectability that Babbitt occupies, the feared destination of a fall he can imagine but never quite articulate. In a city where charitable organizations like the Associated Charities provide approved outlets for feminine social engagement, the Salvation Army represents something rawer — the actual interface between the prosperous and the indigent, a threshold Babbitt wants to remain firmly on the giving side of.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses Babbitt's invocation of the Salvation Army to expose the gap between Babbitt's self-pity and his actual privilege. Babbitt's complaint that his substantial income — sufficient for a house in Floral Heights, a car, and domestic help — leaves him near the condition of a charity case is darkly comic. He is a man so insulated from real poverty that he can treat the idea of modest financial pressure as equivalent to destitution (010-040).

The reference participates in the novel's broader critique of middle-class self-absorption. Babbitt's financial anxieties, while genuine to him, are revealed as grotesquely disproportionate when measured against the actual poverty that organizations like the Salvation Army existed to address. The humor of the line depends on the reader recognizing the absurdity of Babbitt's self-comparison to the genuinely indigent — a recognition that Lewis trusts his audience to make. The Salvation Army, never directly seen in the novel, functions as an implied rebuke to Babbitt's self-dramatizing discontent.
