# Geography and Settings

The geography of *Babbitt* is both literal and moral. Floral Heights is not just a neighborhood but a social aspiration; the industrial South Side is not just a district but the city's guilty conscience. Zenith itself — the "Zip City," the "finest example of American life and prosperity" — stands at the center of a spatial hierarchy that reaches outward to Monarch, Catawba, and the imagined wilderness of Maine. ## About This Index

Lewis maps Zenith meticulously — its avenues, its subdivisions, its social topography. But the map is never neutral: every address in the novel carries a social charge. Floral Heights means respectability; Royal Ridge means exclusion; Old Town means what the boosters would rather forget. The novel's geography is a sociology in spatial form, and these pages trace the connections between where characters live and who they are permitted to be.
