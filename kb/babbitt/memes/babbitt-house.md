# The Babbitt House

The Babbitt family home on Floral Heights — a five-year-old Dutch Colonial with sleeping-porch, sun-parlor, and "the neat yard of a successful business man of Zenith" (001-018). It is a showcase of modern convenience and suburban respectability. Yet "there was but one thing wrong with the Babbitt house: It was not a home" (002-007).

## Description

The house was "all as competent and glossy" as its master bedroom (002-006). It featured "the best of taste, the best of inexpensive rugs, a simple and laudable architecture, and the latest conveniences" (002-006). Electricity replaced candles and hearth-fires throughout: "Along the bedroom baseboard were three plugs for electric lamps, concealed by little brass doors. In the halls were plugs for the vacuum cleaner, and in the living-room plugs for the piano lamp, for the electric fan" (002-006).

The bathroom was "altogether royal," with "porcelain and glazed tile and metal sleek as silver" (001-022), featuring a "sensational exhibit of tooth-brush holder, shaving-brush holder, soap-dish, sponge-dish, and medicine-cabinet, so glittering and so ingenious that they resembled an electrical instrument-board" (001-022). The dining room boasted an "admirable oak buffet, its leaded-glass cupboard, its creamy plaster walls, its modest scene of a salmon expiring upon a pile of oysters" (002-006).

The master bedroom was "a masterpiece among bedrooms, right out of Cheerful Modern Houses for Medium Incomes" (002-004), with gray walls, white woodwork, a serene blue rug, and twin beds between which sat "a standard bedside book with colored illustrations — what particular book it was cannot be ascertained, since no one had ever opened it" (002-004). The room "had the air of being a very good room in a very good hotel" (002-004).

Outside, the yard featured "one sizable elm, two respectable grass-plots, a cement driveway, and a corrugated iron garage" (001-009). Babbitt reflected daily that the garage had "No class" and planned to build a frame replacement (001-018).

## Role in the Novel

The Babbitt house is the novel's primary domestic setting and a character in its own right. It represents everything Babbitt has achieved — and everything that confines him. Its modern conveniences, which Babbitt takes such pride in, are revealed as substitutes for genuine domestic warmth. The house is "competent and glossy" (002-006) but impersonal: "If people had ever lived and loved here, read thrillers at midnight and lain in beautiful indolence on a Sunday morning, there were no signs of it" (002-004).

The house is also the site of Babbitt's daily rituals — shaving, dressing, breakfasting — through which Lewis exposes the tedium and quiet desperation of middle-class life.

## Key Scenes Set Here

- **Babbitt's morning routine** — The novel's opening sequence: Babbitt wakes on the sleeping-porch, shaves in the "royal bathroom," argues with his family over towels and toast, and dons his uniform as a Solid Citizen (001-007–042)
- **The family breakfast** — Daily warfare among Verona, Ted, and Tinka, with Babbitt's irritability and Mrs. Babbitt's placid domesticity (002-009–031)
- **Babbitt's return from Maine** — After his solitary vacation, Babbitt returns to find the house unchanged — and his dissatisfaction with it intensified (007-001)
- **The dinner party** — Babbitt hosts the "highbrow" set including Chum Frink and Howard Littlefield, an evening of strained conversation and social performance (008-014–030)

## Themes

The Babbitt house embodies the central irony of Babbitt's life: it is the tangible proof of his success and the daily reminder of his emptiness. Its "latest conveniences" (002-006) — electric lamps, vacuum cleaner plugs, scientific radiators — are modern America's answer to the question of how to live. That the answer is inadequate is the novel's persistent suggestion. The house is a "standard" house for a "standard" man, and its standardized comforts cannot satisfy the longings that surface in Babbitt's dreams.
