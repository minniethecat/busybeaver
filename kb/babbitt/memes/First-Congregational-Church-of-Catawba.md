# First Congregational Church of Catawba

The First Congregational Church of Catawba is the church where Paul Riesling's father served as pastor and where George Babbitt attended Sunday School as a boy. Though it exists only in memory — visited not in the novel's present action but in Babbitt's sudden, unbidden recollection — the Catawba church serves as one of the novel's most important symbolic locations: the repository of an authentic religious experience that Zenith's commercialized Christianity has long since displaced (016-026).

## Role in the Novel

The church appears in a single, luminous passage that is among the most emotionally direct in the novel. While attending a Sunday School session in Zenith, Babbitt is suddenly flooded with sensory memories of the Catawba church — its "polite stuffiness," its "drab Sunday School books," its "high-colored text-cards." The memory provides a momentary glimpse of the boy Babbitt once was, before the boosterism and business ethics of adult Zenith overlaid every genuine feeling with the language of salesmanship (016-026).

## Babbitt's Memory

Lewis's narration of the memory is notable for its uncharacteristic directness. Babbitt is not satirized here but rendered with sympathy. The passage describes "the unforgotten and unceasingly iterated station — 'Jesus wants me for a sunbeam'" and the "irritating and cultural Tuesday lectures with stereopticon views of Palestine." The accumulation of details — the drab books, the sacred-but-unwanted text-cards — conveys the texture of a Protestant childhood that is both banal and genuinely numinous. The memory is triggered by the smell of church parlors, suggesting that religious experience, for Babbitt, is ultimately sensory and associative rather than doctrinal (016-026).

## Connection to Paul Riesling

The Catawba church is inseparable from Babbitt's friendship with Paul Riesling. Paul's father's pastoral role at the church roots their friendship in a shared childhood landscape that predates Zenith and its values. This is significant because Paul is the one person in Babbitt's life to whom he can speak honestly, without the booster's performance — and the Catawba church is the setting where that friendship first formed. The Sunday School memory thus does double work: it evokes a lost religious authenticity and a lost emotional authenticity, both casualties of adult Zenith (016-026).

## Key Scenes

- **The Sunday School trigger** — In a Zenith Sunday School, Babbitt suddenly "sees" the Catawba church of his boyhood. The scene is narrated in a register of genuine feeling rare in the novel, with Lewis suspending his satirical voice to render the texture of a remembered Protestant childhood — the stereopticon lectures, the sunbeam hymns, the text-cards too sacred to throw away. The memory functions as an implicit critique of adult Babbitt's religion, which is performed for social standing rather than experienced (016-026).

## Satirical Significance

The Catawba church serves as the novel's implicit standard for what religion might be: not a booster club with hymns but a genuine repository of childhood wonder and moral seriousness. By placing this church in the past — in Catawba, in boyhood, in memory — Lewis makes it a measure of what Zenith has lost. The fact that the church exists in the novel only as a memory is itself a satirical point: authentic religious experience belongs to a vanished America, and what remains in Zenith is the Chatham Road Presbyterian Church — a place where the Rotary Club's values are sanctified rather than questioned.
