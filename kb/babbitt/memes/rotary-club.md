# Rotary Club

## Description

The Rotary Club is an international business service organization with an active Zenith chapter. In *Babbitt*, Rotary membership functions as a credential of respectability: to be a Rotarian is to be certified as a "Regular Guy" in good standing. The club appears alongside the Kiwanis, Boosters, Elks, and Moose as one of the "organizations of good, jolly, kidding, laughing, sweaty, upstanding, lend-a-handing Royal Good Fellows" (014-034) whose membership defines the ideal American businessman.

## Social Function

The Zenith Rotary Club serves as a contact-exchange mechanism for the city's commercial class. Babbitt attends Rotary luncheons as part of his professional routine, the gatherings providing opportunities to "get together and shoot a few hot air topics" (019-041). The Rotary occupies an interesting position in Zenith's club hierarchy: it maintains a friendly rivalry with the Boosters' Club, with the "visiting secretary of the Zenith Rotary Club, a rival organization" (021-016) making an appearance at a Boosters' meeting. This inter-club competition is itself performative — the Rotarians and Boosters share the same membership base and the same ideology of commercial fellowship.

## Key Scenes

- **The Boosters' Convention (Chapter XXI)**: The Rotary secretary's visit to the Boosters' meeting becomes a moment of comic rivalry. The Rotary secretary "laughingly admitted" (021-017) the superiority of the Boosters' conviviality, and Babbitt sighs at the thought of attending yet another meeting — "Boosters and Rotarians and the Kiwanis all together" (021-017) — a rare flash of exhaustion with the relentless social machinery.
- **Luncheon Culture (Chapter XIX)**: Babbitt envisions bringing "the bunch of live wires and Real Guys" to "our next Rotary Club luncheon" (019-041), the possessive "our" revealing how deeply his identity is fused with these institutions.

## Satirical Significance

Rotary International is the quintessential Sinclair Lewis target: a genuinely idealistic organization (founded on the motto "Service Above Self") whose local chapters reduce its mission to handshakes, attendance records, and the exchange of business cards. The Rotary Club represents the suburbanization of idealism — noble rhetoric hollowed out into committee work and luncheon speeches. Lewis's satire is gentler here than with the Boosters because Rotary's national prestige makes it a subtler target: the comedy lies not in corruption but in the gulf between the international organization's professed ideals and the "jolly, kidding, laughing" reality of its Zenith chapter.
