# Babbitt-Thompson Realty

The real-estate firm of which [[george-babbitt]] is a partner, co-owned with Henry T. Thompson. Located in the business center of [[zenith]], it is the source of Babbitt's income, the stage for his professional performance, and the practical arena in which he exercises his "nimble" talent for "selling houses for more than people could afford to pay" (001-008). The firm represents the institutional framework within which Babbitt becomes "the modern business man" (001-037) — his identity is inseparable from his professional role, and the firm's fortunes are the measure of his worth.

## Role in the Novel

Babbitt-Thompson Realty is more than a setting — it is an extension of Babbitt's identity. When he is at the office, dictating letters to [[miss-mcgoun]] or meeting with clients, Babbitt is at his most performatively competent and his most existentially hollow. The office is where the "religion of [[business]]" becomes concrete: deals, commissions, contracts, the daily ritual of commercial activity that structures Babbitt's life and gives it its only meaning. Significantly, Babbitt's [[rebellion]] is accompanied by a decline in his business — his social heresy has economic consequences, and the threat of professional ruin is what ultimately drives him back to conformity.

## The Office as Stage

The firm's office is where Babbitt's professional identity is most fully performed. His morning arrival, his dictation of letters, his telephone calls with clients — these are rituals as surely as the [[boosters-club]] luncheon is a ritual. Babbitt at his desk is Babbitt playing Babbitt, a man so thoroughly identified with his professional role that there is no self separable from it. When Miss McGoun takes dictation, she is the audience for Babbitt's performance of business competence; the letters he dictates are scripts in the theater of commerce.

## The Firm and Babbitt's Rebellion

During Babbitt's period of rebellion, his business suffers. Clients begin to drift away; colleagues become wary. The connection between social conformity and commercial success is made explicit: in Zenith, to question the booster orthodoxy is to become a business risk. The threat of professional ruin — the loss of the firm, the loss of income, the loss of identity — is what ultimately forces Babbitt back into the "belligerent conformity" (034-029) from which he briefly escaped. The firm is thus both Babbitt's genuine achievement and the golden cage that ensures his spiritual imprisonment.

> He was nimble in the calling of selling houses for more than people could afford to pay. (001-008)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses Babbitt-Thompson Realty to connect the personal and the systemic. Babbitt's ethical emptiness — his talent for selling overpriced houses — is not a personal failing but a professional requirement. The firm is the mechanism through which the abstractions of the "religion of business" become material: a family burdened with an unaffordable mortgage, a neighborhood of standardized houses, a city built on the premise of perpetual growth. Babbitt is not a villain — he is a functionary in a system whose logic he has never questioned, and his professional "nimbleness" is simply his successful adaptation to that system's demands.
