# Babbitt's Rebellion

The central dramatic arc of Sinclair Lewis's *Babbitt* — [[george-babbitt|George F. Babbitt's]] brief, doomed attempt to break free from the conformity that defines his life in [[zenith]]. Triggered by [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling's]] imprisonment, sustained by his affair with [[tanis-judique]] and his sympathy for striking workers, and ultimately crushed by the combined pressure of business ostracism and domestic obligation, Babbitt's rebellion is the novel's most sustained exploration of whether individuality can survive the demands of the [[business]] religion. The answer, characteristically for Lewis, is both no and yes: the rebellion fails, but it leaves permanent traces.

## Background

Before the rebellion, Babbitt is the perfect Zenith businessman. He is a partner in [[babbitt-thompson-realty]], a deacon at Chatham Road Presbyterian, a member in good standing of the [[boosters-club]] and the [[athletic-club]], and a believer — however privately ambivalent — in the religion of business. His discontent, confessed only to Paul Riesling over lunch, is a secret he keeps even from himself: "he lay and detested the grind of the real-estate business, and disliked his family, and disliked himself for disliking them" (001-015). This suppressed discontent is the raw material of the rebellion to come.

## Catalyst: Paul's Imprisonment

The arrest and imprisonment of [[paul-riesling]] for shooting his wife [[zilla-riesling|Zilla]] is the event that shatters Babbitt's accommodation to Zenith life. Visiting Paul in the state penitentiary, Babbitt confronts what the system does to those who cannot adapt: "Unseeing he went through prison corridors stinking of carbolic acid" (024-003). The recognition that Paul is "already dead" (024-004) forces Babbitt to question the world that destroyed his friend — and his own complicity in that world.

## The Nature of the Rebellion

Babbitt's rebellion is limited in both scope and duration. He does not become a radical — "though he was in rebellion, naturally Babbitt did not care to be classed with the socialists" (026-002). His defiance is personal rather than ideological: he wants freedom for himself, not systemic change for others. This limitation is both the rebellion's weakness (it lacks conviction) and Lewis's point — Babbitt can imagine personal escape but not structural transformation, because his imagination has been shaped by the very system he briefly rejects.

## Aftermath

The rebellion ends not with a bang but with a gradual reabsorption into the world Babbitt tried to escape. The mechanism is not violence but economics and domesticity: his business suffers, his wife falls ill, his social circle closes ranks. "The last scar of his rebellion was healed" (034-029) — the medical metaphor suggesting that rebellion, in Zenith's terms, is a wound to be cured. Yet the novel's final pages complicate this closure: Babbitt discovers that "he actually did not consider the Traction Bunch an interesting subject of conversation" and silently endorses his son Ted's freedom to choose a different life. The rebellion has failed, but it has left Babbitt permanently, if subtly, changed.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses Babbitt's rebellion to ask the novel's most serious question: can an individual shaped entirely by a conformist society ever genuinely rebel against it? The rebellion's failure suggests a pessimistic answer, but the novel's final gesture — Babbitt silently granting his son the freedom he never claimed — complicates that pessimism. The rebellion may not liberate Babbitt, but it may, Lewis suggests, make liberation possible for the next generation. The "last scar" has healed, but a scar is evidence that a wound once existed — and that is not nothing.
