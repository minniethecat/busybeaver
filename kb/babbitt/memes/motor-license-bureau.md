# Motor License Bureau

The state agency responsible for issuing motor vehicle license plates, whose coveted low-number plates become emblems of social status among Zenith's business elite, and whose superintendent is the subject of Babbitt's cynical speculation about cronyism and influence-peddling.

## The Status of Low Numbers

The Motor License Bureau enters the novel through the figure of a visiting Rotary Club secretary who possesses "State Motor Car License Number 5" (021-016). The low number is a mark of distinction: the secretary admits that "wherever he drove in the state so low a number created a sensation," though he concedes that traffic policemen "remembered it only too darn well" (021-017). The license plate, in Lewis's rendering, functions as a portable status marker — a number low enough to signal the holder's importance in the civic hierarchy while remaining ambiguous about exactly how that importance was acquired.

The conversation occurs at Babbitt's Athletic Club lunch table, where the men have been discussing license numbers with the same serious attention they might give to stock prices or real estate values. The Rotary secretary's Number 5 immediately reshapes the social dynamics of the table: the visiting guest is suddenly someone to be taken seriously, his low number conferring a prestige that his Rotary affiliation alone could not command.

## Babbitt's Cynicism

Babbitt's response to the Rotary secretary's low-number plate is a miniature portrait of his moral universe. He sighs to [[joseph-k-pumphrey|Professor Pumphrey]], "Be pretty nice to have as low a number as that! Everybody'd say, 'He must be an important guy!'" (021-017). The remark reveals Babbitt's understanding of status as pure display: importance is not a matter of what one does but of what others say. The license plate is valuable not for any privilege it confers but for the story it tells about its holder.

But Babbitt's admiration quickly turns to suspicion: "Wonder how he got it? I'll bet he wined and dined the superintendent of the Motor License Bureau to a fare-you-well!" (021-017). The pivot from envy to cynicism is instantaneous and characteristic. Babbitt cannot simply admire another man's good fortune; he must explain it, and his explanation always takes the same form: the system is rigged, success is purchased, and the only difference between himself and the man with Number 5 is that the other man bought his advantage more skillfully.

What Babbitt does not do — what it would never occur to him to do — is question the system that makes a license plate number into a marker of status in the first place. His cynicism extends only to the distribution of privileges, never to the existence of the privileges themselves.

## Civic Significance

The Motor License Bureau, though glimpsed in only a few sentences of the novel, illuminates a central theme of *Babbitt*: the entanglement of public office with private influence. The bureau's low-number plates are a scarce government resource distributed through channels opaque enough to invite Babbitt's speculation — and Babbitt's speculation, whether accurate or not, reflects the understood rules of Zenith's civic life. In a city where Boosters' Club membership, church affiliation, and the right dinner invitation can secure advantages, the Motor License Bureau is one more institution where personal connections may matter more than formal rules.

The bureau also crystallizes the novel's extended meditation on the automobile as a social marker. If "a family's motor indicated its social rank as precisely as the grades of the peerage" (006-027), then the license plate is the automobile's own coat of arms — a number that announces, before the driver has even stepped out of the car, where he stands in the civic order. The Motor License Bureau is, in this sense, the College of Heralds of Zenith's motored gentry.
