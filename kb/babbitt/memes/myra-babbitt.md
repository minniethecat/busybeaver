# Myra Babbitt

The wife whose quiet management of domestic life enables [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]'s career — and whose own suppressed discontent surfaces in a "small determined rebellion of her own" (030-008).

## Biography

Myra Babbitt (née [[henry-thompson|Thompson]]) is [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]'s wife of more than twenty years. She is the mother of [[verona-babbitt|Verona]], Ted, and [[tinka-babbitt|Tinka]]. She manages the [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] [[family|household]] on [[floral-heights|Floral Heights]] with quiet efficiency, expressing "the sympathy she was too experienced to feel" (002-001). Her life is defined by the routines of middle-class [[family|domesticity]]: darning, managing servants, worrying about appearances.

Myra is a product of her class and generation — a woman educated just enough to manage a household, socialized to measure her worth by her husband's success and her children's respectability. Her father, [[henry-thompson|Henry Thompson]], was Babbitt's business partner and patron, and the marriage was in part a business arrangement — the son-in-law inheriting the father-in-law's real-estate practice. This origin, never directly discussed in the novel, hovers over the Babbitt marriage as an unspoken transaction at its foundation.

## Role in the Novel

Myra functions primarily as background — the stable domestic presence against which [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s drama of rebellion plays out. But Lewis gives her one striking moment: late in the novel, [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] discovers that she "was having a small determined rebellion of her own" (030-008). This revelation suggests that Myra's placidity conceals an inner life that the novel, focused on [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s consciousness, only occasionally glimpses.

Structurally, Myra serves as a counterpoint to Babbitt's rebellion. While he rebels loudly — making speeches, carousing with [[tanis-judique|Tanis Judique]], challenging the Boosters — Myra's rebellion is quiet, domestic, and perhaps for those reasons more effective. She does not need to announce her discontent; she simply acts on it. The novel's final irony is that Myra's "small determined rebellion" may prove more durable than Babbitt's noisy one, precisely because it does not depend on validation from the social world that both of them have served for so long.

## Key Scenes

- **The morning routine** — As [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] wakes, Myra is already up, her "soft grunts" expressing "the sympathy she was too experienced to feel" — a description that captures the emotional economy of their marriage. The passage (002-001) is remarkable for its compression: in a single sentence, Lewis conveys the twenty-year history of a marriage in which affection has been replaced by performance, and performance has become so habitual that neither party notices it anymore.

- **Myra's rebellion** — Late in the novel, Mrs. Babbitt "tore the decent cloak from her unhappiness and the astounded male discovered that she was having a small determined rebellion of her own" (030-008), revealing the parallel currents of discontent running beneath the surface of the [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] marriage. The verb "tore" is the most violent word associated with Myra in the entire novel; the "cloak" metaphor suggests that her unhappiness, like her respectability, has been a garment she can choose to remove.

- **The appendicitis episode** — Myra's sudden illness serves as the crisis that ends Babbitt's rebellion. Her appendicitis attack summons Babbitt back from his experiment in freedom, reasserting the claims of domestic obligation over the pleasures of self-expression. The timing is ambiguous — is it coincidence or psychosomatic rebellion? — but the effect is clear: Myra's body, which has served the marriage for decades, finally asserts its own needs in a way that cannot be ignored.

- **The dinner-party preparations** — Myra's meticulous management of the Babbitt dinner party (Chapter XV) reveals her skill at the social performance her class demands. She orchestrates the menu, the seating, the conversation topics — every detail calculated to project the image of prosperous respectability. This is Myra's domain of competence, the sphere in which she exercises an authority that Babbitt, for all his boasting, cannot match.

## Character Traits

Myra is defined by her competence at the domestic role and her emotional restraint. She is the enforcer of [[conformity|respectability]] — concerned with appearances, propriety, and the opinion of neighbors. Yet her "small determined rebellion" suggests that she, like her husband, has paid a price for the performance of contentment.

Her defining tension is between accommodation and suppressed desire. Unlike Babbitt, who erupts into rebellion and then subsides, Myra manages her discontent through the same domestic skills she uses to manage everything else. Her rebellion is "small" and "determined" — the adjectives are carefully chosen. It will not change the world, or even the household, but it will not be surrendered. It is the quiet intransigence of someone who has spent a lifetime learning to endure.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt]]** — Her husband, whose rebellion threatens the domestic order she maintains. Their marriage, like everything else in Babbitt's life, is a compromise between individual desire and social expectation. Myra understands this compromise better than Babbitt does, which is why her quiet endurance outlasts his noisy revolt.

- **[[verona-babbitt]]** — Her daughter, whose Bryn Mawr education creates generational tension. Verona represents a possibility Myra never had — education as a path to independence — and Myra's response to this possibility is characteristically ambivalent, mixing pride with unease.

- **[[ted-babbitt]]** — Her son, whose [[freedom|independence]] challenges her authority. Ted's casual disregard for the domestic order Myra maintains is both a generational rebellion and a gendered one — the son who takes for granted what the mother has sacrificed to provide.

- **[[tanis-judique]]** — The woman who represents an alternative to the domestic life Myra embodies. The novel never stages a direct encounter between them, but Myra's "small determined rebellion" can be read as her indirect response to the challenge Tanis represents — a reassertion of selfhood that does not require a man's validation.

- **[[henry-thompson]]** — Her father, whose business partnership with Babbitt established the economic foundation of the marriage. The Thompson connection is one of the novel's structural givens, conditioning the marriage without being directly dramatized.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis's treatment of Myra is one of the novel's most delicate achievements. She could easily have been a caricature — the nagging wife, the domestic drudge. Instead, Lewis gives her dignity and, in her "small determined rebellion," a kind of heroism that Babbitt's grander gestures never attain. Myra is the novel's quietest critic of the booster world — not because she articulates a critique but because her life demonstrates the human cost of the system Babbitt serves. The wife who "was too experienced to feel" sympathy has been shaped by decades of performing emotions she does not experience; her rebellion, when it comes, is the revenge of the authentic self against a lifetime of performance.
