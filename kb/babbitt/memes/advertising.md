# Advertising

The commercial rhetoric that powers Zenith's economy and defines Babbitt's professional identity — the language of "pep" and persuasion that transforms every human desire into a sales opportunity.

## Definition in Context

Advertising in *Babbitt* is more than a profession — it is the native language of the business civilization. Babbitt is "scholarly in regard to Salesmanship" (001-037) and his professional skill at persuasion bleeds into every aspect of his life. The language of advertising — optimistic, exaggerated, relentlessly positive — becomes indistinguishable from how Babbitt thinks about everything from his city to his marriage.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Babbitt the copywriter** — Babbitt's professional identity centers on his ability to craft persuasive language. His form letters and sales pitches represent the application of advertising rhetoric to real estate, transforming ordinary houses into objects of desire.
- **The language of "pep"** — The Boosters' Club button displays "Boosters — Pep!" (001-042), condensing the advertising ethos into two words. Pep is the emotional product advertising sells: optimism, energy, belonging.
- **Advertising as worldview** — Babbitt approaches his speeches about Zenith with the same rhetorical strategies he uses in real-estate copy: exaggeration, repetition, appeals to emotion over fact. The city becomes another product to sell.
- **The limits of persuasion** — Despite his professional skill at persuasion, Babbitt cannot sell himself on his own happiness. The gap between advertising rhetoric and genuine feeling becomes one of the novel's central tensions.

## Key Passages

> In them he was the modern business man; one who gave orders to clerks and drove a car and played occasional golf and was scholarly in regard to Salesmanship. (001-037)

> With the conciseness of great art the button displayed two words: "Boosters — Pep!" It made Babbitt feel loyal and important. It associated him with Good Fellows, with men who were nice and human, and important in business circles. (001-042)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses advertising as a metaphor for the inauthenticity at the heart of Zenith's culture. Babbitt the ad-man is Babbitt the self-deceiver: the same rhetorical techniques he uses to sell houses he uses to sell himself on the proposition that he is happy. The "great art" of the Boosters' button is its ability to compress an entire ideology — conform, consume, be cheerful — into two words. Lewis suggests that advertising has colonized the American soul, replacing genuine emotion with commercial simulacra.
