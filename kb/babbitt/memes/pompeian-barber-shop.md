# Pompeian Barber Shop

A modern, "glittering" barber shop located in the basement of the Hotel Thornleigh, the largest and most dynamically modern hotel in Zenith. Babbitt patronizes it for his fortnightly hair-trims, choosing it over the Reeves Building Barber Shop for its superior status and modern appointments (003-037).

## Description

The Pompeian Barber Shop occupied the basement of the Hotel Thornleigh, the largest hotel in Zenith. It was characterized by its "glittering" atmosphere and "powerful system" of barbering—a mechanized, efficient operation that "wafted" customers into chairs with industrial precision (024-018, 024-021). The shop employed manicure girls, at least one of whom "especially disturbed" Babbitt, though he never spoke to her (024-016). Babbitt passed this shop ten times a day, a hundred times a month, on his way through the hotel arcade (003-037).

## Social Function

The Pompeian served as a marker of class distinction in Zenith's grooming hierarchy. Babbitt deliberately chose it over the Reeves Building Barber Shop—a preference that signaled both economic status and alignment with Zenith's cult of modernity. The shop's location in the Hotel Thornleigh, the city's premier hotel, reinforced its prestige. Its industrial efficiency—the "powerful system" that brooked no waiting—mirrored Zenith's broader obsession with speed, mechanization, and the subordination of individual preference to system (024-021).

## Key Members

- **George Babbitt** — Regular patron, visiting fortnightly for hair-trims; his choice of the Pompeian over the Reeves shop reflects his aspirational self-image
- **Manicure Girl (unnamed)** — "The last manicure girl on the right" who "especially disturbed" Babbitt with attraction he never acted upon (024-016)

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's Haircut** — Babbitt's visit to the Pompeian for his regular trim, where he is "instantly wafted into a chair" by the efficient system, observes the manicure girl, and embodies the passive consumer of Zenith's mechanized services (024-016, 024-018, 024-021)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Pompeian Barber Shop to satirize the mechanization of personal care in 1920s America. The shop's "powerful system" that renders waiting "inconceivable" reduces barbering—an intimate, personal service—to an industrial process. The Pompeian's "glittering" modernity contrasts with the homelier Reeves shop, illustrating how even grooming choices become status signals in Babbitt's world. Babbitt's mute attraction to the manicure girl, whom he passes "ten times a day" without ever speaking to, underscores the emotional sterility beneath the gleaming surfaces of Zenith's consumer institutions.
