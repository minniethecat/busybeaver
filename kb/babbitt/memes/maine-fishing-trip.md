# Maine Fishing Trip

The Maine fishing trip is not an event in the conventional sense — it never actually takes place. It exists as a recurring fantasy in [[george-babbitt|Babbitt's]] imagination, a symbol of escape that haunts the early chapters of the novel, growing more urgent as Babbitt's discontent with his Zenith life deepens, and finally dissolving as the realities of marriage, business, and conformity reassert their grip.

## Summary

Babbitt's fantasy takes a simple, recurring form: "Maine.... Wear old pants, and loaf, and cuss" (006-029). He imagines himself and [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]] alone beside a lake, free from the demands of wives, offices, and social performance. The trip would involve slipping away to Maine a few days ahead of their wives, who would join later — a window of pure, unmediated male companionship before domesticity catches up.

The fantasy first surfaces in Chapter V, where Babbitt tells Paul: "I will make some excuse — say business in New York — and get up to Maine four or five days before they do, and just loaf by ourselves and smoke and cuss and be natural" (005-036). Paul, already worn down by [[zilla-riesling|Zilla's]] emotional warfare, wavers between hope and fatalism. The plan resurfaces in Chapter VI, where Babbitt dreams of "Paul Riesling beside a lake in Maine. It was as overpowering and idyllic as a new love-affair" (009-013), and again in Chapter VIII, but by Chapter IX it has receded: "he forgot his desire to run off to Maine with Paul Riesling" (008-002).

The trip's non-occurrence is the point. Maine is Babbitt's horizon — always visible, never reached.

## Background

The Maine fantasy emerges from the convergence of two pressures in Babbitt's life: his growing sense of spiritual suffocation in Zenith and his deep, almost romantic attachment to Paul Riesling. Paul is the one person with whom Babbitt can be "natural" — the word he uses repeatedly in connection with Maine (005-036). The fantasy of Maine is therefore a fantasy of authenticity: a place where the performance of boosterism can be set aside and the real self, whatever remains of it, can emerge.

The trip also has a specific marital dimension. Babbitt's wife [[myra-babbitt|Myra]] insists on coming along: "'Don't you *want* me to go to Maine with you? I shouldn't dream of going unless you wanted me'; and you'd give in to save her feelings" (005-038). Babbitt's concession is the first defeat of the fantasy — the recognition that even escape must be negotiated with the demands of domestic obligation. Paul, for his part, is trapped by Zilla's emotional manipulation in a parallel but more extreme version of the same dynamic.

Lewis also embeds the Maine fantasy in a broader pattern of Babbitt's escapism. The same impulse that drives him toward Maine also drives him toward the Bunch, toward [[tanis-judique]], and toward his brief rebellion. Maine is the purest form of the fantasy — escape without social entanglement — and its failure anticipates the failure of every other escape attempt.

## Participants

- **[[george-babbitt]]** — The dreamer; the man who needs Maine most and is least able to reach it
- **[[paul-riesling]]** — The necessary companion; the one person Babbitt wants to be "natural" with. Paul's own longing for escape mirrors Babbitt's, but his fatalism — rooted in his marriage to Zilla — makes him a reluctant co-conspirator
- **[[myra-babbitt]]** — The unwitting obstacle; her innocent insistence on joining the trip embodies the domestic claim that Babbitt cannot refuse
- **[[zilla-riesling]]** — The more explicitly destructive parallel to Myra; her emotional hold on Paul makes the Maine fantasy impossible for him

## Consequences

The Maine trip's failure to materialize has consequences precisely because it is never attempted. Babbitt's fantasy remains frozen in the optative mood — "would," "could," "might" — and this suspension becomes its own form of imprisonment. The novel traces an arc from Maine (a dream of escape *from* Zenith) through the Bunch and Tanis Judique (an attempted escape *within* Zenith) to the Good Citizens' League (the final surrender *to* Zenith). Each stage is a degradation of the original, pure fantasy.

After Paul shoots Zilla and is sent to prison, the Maine fantasy becomes not merely impossible but retrospectively tragic. The companion Babbitt needed for his escape was, all along, a man who was himself escaping toward catastrophe. Maine was never a real destination; it was a shared delusion that two unhappy men used to sustain each other through their separate unhappiness.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Maine fishing trip as an index of Babbitt's spiritual condition. The fantasy's recurrence, its growing urgency, and its eventual dissolution chart Babbitt's arc from vague discontent to explicit rebellion to final capitulation. Maine is the "elsewhere" that Zenith cannot provide — and the novel's tragic insight is that Babbitt lacks the courage to seize it.

The satire is gentler here than elsewhere in the novel. Lewis does not mock Babbitt for wanting to escape; he mourns the fact that Babbitt cannot. The Maine fantasy is genuinely touching — a middle-aged real-estate broker dreaming of old pants and lakeside loafing — and its failure is not a joke but a quiet tragedy.
