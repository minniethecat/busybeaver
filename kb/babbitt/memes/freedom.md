# Freedom

In *Babbitt*, freedom is not a political condition but a psychological one — a brief, intoxicating sensation of escape from the gravitational field of respectability. Lewis portrays it as something his characters glimpse rather than possess, a negative space defined by the absence of obligation rather than the presence of anything positive.

## Definition in Context

Freedom enters the novel not as a program or an ideal but as a feeling — the "suggestion of freedom and heroism" (001-017) Babbitt experiences behind the wheel of his automobile, the brief sensation of liberation when he leaves his wife for a vacation in Maine. Lewis consistently depicts freedom as freedom *from* — from family, from business, from the expectations of the Boosters' Club — rather than freedom *to*. This is the novel's bleakest insight: Babbitt's culture has given him no vocabulary for what freedom might be *for*, only an inchoate sense of what it must escape.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The automobile as freedom machine** — Babbitt's car represents "a suggestion to him of freedom and heroism" (001-017). The automobile is the one technology in the novel that genuinely liberates — private, mobile, unaccountable — and Babbitt's love of driving is the purest expression of his suppressed desire for autonomy.
- **The Maine vacation** — Babbitt's trip without Myra (005-037, 009-044) is an experiment in freedom that reveals its limits: "comprehending that he had won freedom, and wondering what he could do with it." Freedom without purpose becomes merely a different kind of emptiness.
- **The rebellion** — Babbitt's brief revolt against Zenith's conformity (Chapters 28–31) is his most sustained experience of freedom, and it is characterized primarily by negation: he stops attending church, he refuses the Good Citizens' League, he befriends the "queer" people. Positive freedom — a life built on different principles — eludes him.
- **The return** — The novel's conclusion is an anti-climax of freedom: "in a barren freedom of icy Northern wind he sighed" (031-019). Freedom, Lewis suggests, is cold — it provides no warmth, no community, no comfort. The comforts Babbitt returns to are the very constraints he rebelled against.
- **Freedom in business rhetoric** — Zenith's boosters use "freedom" as a slogan while organizing to crush any actual exercise of it. The Good Citizens' League is the mechanism by which freedom of thought is policed in the name of freedom of enterprise.

## Key Passages

> His car was forever a suggestion to him of freedom and heroism. (001-017, paraphrased)

> He was comprehending that he had won freedom, and wondering what he could do with it. (009-044, paraphrased)

> In a barren freedom of icy Northern wind he sighed for the warm, companionable land. (031-019, paraphrased)

## Satirical Function

Lewis's treatment of freedom is perhaps his most radical satirical move: he suggests that freedom, as understood in American ideology, may be impossible within the structures of modern society — and that its impossibility is concealed by the ritual invocation of the word. Babbitt's brief experience of freedom is genuinely transformative, but it cannot be sustained because freedom requires institutions that Zenith does not possess and a self that Babbitt has never been allowed to develop. The novel refuses either to celebrate Babbitt's rebellion as triumph or to condemn his return as cowardice — because neither freedom nor conformity, as Lewis presents them, offers a livable life.
