# Reeves Building

The office building in downtown Zenith that houses the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company, along with the barber shop where George Babbitt receives his daily shave. It is the physical center of Babbitt's working life and a stage for his professional identity.

## Description

The Reeves Building is a commercial office building in Zenith's business district. Babbitt's real-estate office occupies "the ground floor of the Reeves Building" (003-033), positioned for maximum visibility to the street. The building is described as "fireproof as a rock" (003-034), an assertion of modern solidity that aligns with Babbitt's booster sensibility — the building must be as respectable as the businesses it contains.

The elevator-runners and other building staff form a small community that Babbitt knows by sight, part of the "known people who inhabited the Reeves Building corridors" (003-037). The building's barber shop is a recurring setting: it is where Babbitt gets his daily shave, and where he later asserts independence by refusing to patronize it — "I don't own the Reeves Building!" (024-018).

## Social Function

The Reeves Building functions as a status marker for Babbitt. He notes with satisfaction that "no tenant of the Reeves Building had a more expensive" automobile (003-046), measuring his success against his immediate professional neighbors. When he sees a rival building, he reflexively judges it "less beautiful, than his own Reeves Building" (005-008) — the building becomes an extension of his ego.

The building also anchors Babbitt in the commercial establishment. His letterhead — "BABBITT-THOMPSON REALTY COMPANY / Reeves Building" (004-010) — fuses his identity with the address. When he later rebels, one of his small acts of defiance is abandoning the Reeves Building Barber Shop (024-017), a gesture that is both trivial (it's just a haircut) and symbolically loaded (it is turning away from a ritual of his daily routine).

## Key Scenes

- **Morning arrival** — Babbitt's daily entrance into the building, noting the familiar corridors and elevator operators, establishes the rhythm of his commercial life (003-033–003-037)
- **Letterhead pride** — Babbitt rejoices at the sight of his company name and building address on official stationery, a moment of pure booster satisfaction (004-010)
- **Comparative vanity** — Passing a rival office building, Babbitt judges it inferior to "his own Reeves Building," revealing the competitive narcissism of the commercial class (005-008)
- **Barber shop rebellion** — In a small act of defiance, Babbitt stops patronizing the building's barber shop, a rejection of routine that signals his broader rebellion (024-017–024-018)

## Satirical Significance

The Reeves Building is a site of what Lewis satirizes as commercial self-satisfaction. Babbitt's pride in his building — its fireproof construction, its expensive tenants, its superiority to neighboring structures — mirrors his pride in Zenith itself. Both are investments in an identity built on external markers of success. The building's barber shop becomes a subtle measure of Babbitt's psychological distance from that identity: when he leaves it, he is leaving more than a barber's chair.

Lewis has Babbitt think of the building possessively ("his own Reeves Building") despite his merely being a tenant, exposing the booster's habit of conflating occupancy with ownership, association with achievement.
