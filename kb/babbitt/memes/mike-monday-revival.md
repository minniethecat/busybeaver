# Mike Monday's Revival Meeting

The revival campaign of Mike Monday — "the distinguished evangelist, the best-known Protestant pontiff in America" (007-040) — a mass religious event in Zenith that transforms spiritual regeneration into a business enterprise, complete with efficiency metrics, advertising budgets, and the rhetorical style of a prize-fighter.

## Background

Mike Monday is a national figure, an ex-prize-fighter whose conversion narrative and pugilistic preaching style have made him "the world's greatest salesman of salvation" (007-040). His organization has "converted over two hundred thousand lost and priceless souls at an average cost of less than ten dollars a head" (007-040) — a formulation that applies the language of industrial efficiency to spiritual matters with no apparent awareness of the incongruity.

Zenith is among the last major cities to host Monday's campaign. Opposition from "certain Episcopalian and Congregationalist ministers" — whom Monday calls "a bunch of gospel-pushers with dish-water instead of blood, a gang of squealers that need more dust on the knees of their pants and more hair on their skinny old chests" (007-041) — is overcome when business leaders recognize the revival's practical utility.

## Key Elements

- **The Tabernacle** — A temporary structure erected on the County Fair Grounds, "to seat fifteen thousand people" (007-043), underwritten by an expense fund of forty thousand dollars. The scale is industrial, the financing corporate.
- **The preacher** — Mike Monday, the "Prophet with a Punch," whose boxing background supplies the metaphor for his entire ministry. He threatens his critics with "one good swift poke from Mike, with all the kick of God's Flaming Righteousness behind the wallop" (007-044).
- **The business model** — The revival is explicitly framed as a sales operation. Souls are the product, conversion is the transaction, and the cost-per-head metric is reported with the pride of an efficiency engineer.
- **The labor utility** — Monday's campaign has demonstrable economic benefits: he "had turned the minds of workmen from wages and hours to higher things, and thus averted strikes" (007-042). Religion serves as an instrument of labor discipline.

## Satirical Significance

The Mike Monday revival is Lewis's most concentrated satire of the American commercialization of religion. The revival is not a spiritual event that happens to use business methods — it is a business that happens to use spiritual language. The "$10 per head" metric, the $40,000 expense fund, the Tabernacle's seating capacity, the labor-pacification function — every element reduces faith to a transaction and the church to a corporation.

Monday himself embodies the fusion of violence and piety that Lewis sees in American evangelical culture. The ex-prize-fighter's "wallop" — now delivered with "God's Flaming Righteousness behind" it (007-044) — is not a departure from the gospel but its fulfillment in the idiom of Zenith: masculine, aggressive, and commercially effective.

The episode connects to the novel's broader anatomy of [[business]] as religion. Just as the [[boosters-club-banquet|Boosters' Club Banquet]] is the liturgy of the business faith, the Monday revival is its evangelistic crusade — a traveling sales organization for spiritual product, complete with territory managers, conversion quotas, and a charismatic CEO.

## Consequences

The revival is part of the institutional apparatus that sustains Babbitt's conformity. His religious beliefs, like his political opinions and his social values, are "determined" by authorities — the Presbyterian "priests" for religion, the Republican senators for politics, the national advertisers for taste (007-025). The Monday revival is the mechanism by which this spiritual conformity is mass-produced and periodically renewed.

When Babbitt later begins his [[babbitts-rebellion|rebellion]], it is precisely this apparatus of standardized belief — political, commercial, and religious — that he briefly challenges. The Monday revival represents the religious dimension of the conformity he must overcome.
