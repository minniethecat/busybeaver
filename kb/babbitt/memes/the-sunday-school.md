# The Sunday School

The Sunday School program at Chatham Road Presbyterian Church in Zenith, an institution that George F. Babbitt champions with the same booster enthusiasm he applies to his real-estate business. Its growth and modernization become his primary religious contribution to the community.

## Description

The Sunday School at Chatham Road Presbyterian is described as "the fourth largest in Zenith" (016-020), a fact that motivates Babbitt's ambition to make it first. The school operates out of a "highly varnished Sunday School room" with overflow classes relegated to "the basement, where was the kitchen" (016-026). Its educational program encompasses not only religious instruction but also "philosophy and Oriental ethnology" taught "by earnest spinsters" (016-026).

Babbitt approaches Sunday School administration as "a merchandizing problem" (017-013), applying the same techniques he uses in real estate. He discovers "a world of Sunday School journals, an enormous and busy domain of weeklies and monthlies which were as technical, as practical and forward-looking, as the real-estate columns or the shoe-trade magazines" (016-028). These journals offer "lucrative tips on 'Focusing Appeals,' 'Scouting for New Members,' and 'Getting Prospects to Sign up with the Sunday School'" (016-029), terminology that collapses the distinction between evangelism and salesmanship.

## Social Function

The Sunday School provides Babbitt with "purification and publicity" (016-010), satisfying both his need for moral respectability and his appetite for community recognition. His administrative zeal is inseparable from his social ambition: the school's growth validates his business methods while positioning him as a civic leader.

The institution becomes a vehicle for Babbitt's military-inspired organizational scheme. He proposes dividing "the Sunday School into four armies, depending on age" with military ranks awarded "according to how many members he brings in" (017-014), a plan that converts religious education into a competitive recruitment campaign. He further suggests hiring "a real paid press-agent for the Sunday School" to generate publicity (017-015), and the position is filled by "Young Kenneth Escott, reporter on the _Advocate-Times_" (017-032).

The campaign succeeds: "Quickened by this spiritual refreshment, it had a boom" (017-033), though it never overtakes the Central Methodist Church's program. Babbitt himself is "plumply pleased by salutes on the street from unknown small boys" calling him "Colonel" (017-035), his military rank in the Sunday School scheme providing a gratification more visceral than spiritual.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis deploys the Sunday School as a devastating satire of the American tendency to treat religion as a subsidiary of business. Babbitt's discovery that Sunday School journals resemble "the real-estate columns or the shoe-trade magazines" (016-028) exposes the thorough commodification of spiritual life. His military-rank scheme transforms children's religious education into a recruitment pyramid, and the language of "prospects" (016-029) completes the merger of evangelism and salesmanship.

The figure of William Washington Eathorne — the patrician banker who chairs the Sunday School committee from his mansion filled with "catacombs of reception-rooms" (017-007) — embodies the alliance of wealth and piety that Lewis sees as the true religion of Zenith. That Eathorne only departs from Sunday School topics to discuss banking (017-051) confirms that business, not belief, is the real object of devotion.
