# Babbitt's Speech at the Boosters' Club

The speech in which [[george-babbitt]] "had once praised him [Mike Monday] in a speech at the [[boosters-club|Boosters' Club]]" (007-041) — a representative performance of the booster oratory that defines Zenith's civic culture and, for Babbitt personally, a step on the path from private citizen to club vice-president.

## Background

The Boosters' Club is the central civic organization of Zenith's business class, a "world-force for optimism, manly pleasantry, and good business" (021-001). Its weekly luncheons combine eating, back-slapping, and speech-making into a ritual of masculine fellowship and ideological affirmation. Membership is restricted to "only two persons from each department of business," so that members might "realize the metaphysical oneness of all occupations — plumbing and portrait-painting, medicine and the manufacture of chewing-gum" (021-004).

The Boosters' luncheon is a structured affair. Each member wears "a huge celluloid button announcing his name, his nickname, and his business" (021-004). The booklet at each place setting mixes membership listings with advertisements and the admonition: "There's no rule that you have to trade with your Fellow Boosters, but get wise, boy — what's the use of letting all this good money get outside of our happy fambly?" (021-009). Lunch consists of "chicken croquettes, peas, fried potatoes, coffee, apple pie, and American cheese" (021-016).

Into this environment, Babbitt rises to speak — not once but repeatedly, his oratory becoming a recognized feature of Boosters' gatherings.

## Key Elements

- **The occasion** — Babbitt's speech praising Mike Monday is delivered at a regular Boosters' Club luncheon, the forum where Zenith's business class performs its collective identity. The speech is a civic act: to praise Monday is to affirm the alliance of business, religion, and booster optimism.
- **The content** — Babbitt praises Mike Monday, the ex-prize-fighter evangelist whose revival campaign is sweeping the nation. The speech endorses Monday's fusion of business efficiency and spiritual regeneration — souls converted at "less than ten dollars a head" (007-040) — as a model of the booster ethos applied to religion.
- **The rhetoric** — Babbitt's oratorical style mirrors the booster idiom he has absorbed: hearty, exclamatory, full of superlatives and back-slapping familiarity. At the annual election lunch where he is named vice-president, he announces: "You are now addressing the vice-president of the Boosters' Club!" and promises to "whoop 'em up and introduce the speakers — no matter if they're the governor himself" (021-024).
- **The election** — Babbitt's speech-making contributes to his rising status within the club. At the second March lunch, the most important of the year, he is elected vice-president of the Boosters (021-023), a milestone in his civic career.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis presents Babbitt's Boosters' Club speech as the paradigmatic utterance of the booster culture: public, affirmative, and completely devoid of intellectual content. The speech praises a man (Mike Monday) whose own rhetoric consists of threats to deliver "one good swift poke... with all the kick of God's Flaming Righteousness behind the wallop" (007-044), and whose ministry measures spiritual success in cost-per-head metrics.

The speech's importance, in Lewis's anatomy, lies not in what it says but in what it does: it reinforces the bonds of booster solidarity, affirms the alliance of business and religion, and advances Babbitt's career within the club hierarchy. Speech-making in Zenith is not communication — it is ritual performance, and Babbitt, like Monday, has mastered the form.

The election to vice-president that follows is the reward for this mastery. Babbitt's oratory is a form of social currency, convertible into the institutional status that will later make his [[babbitts-rebellion|rebellion]] so costly: the higher he rises in the Boosters, the farther he has to fall.

## Consequences

Babbitt's speech-making career at the Boosters' Club establishes the pattern of public performance that defines his civic identity. The man who can "whoop 'em up and introduce the speakers — no matter if they're the governor himself" (021-024) is rewarded with titles, respect, and the solidarities of booster fellowship.

But the speech praising Mike Monday also represents the conformity that Babbitt will later struggle against. The Monday he praises is an instrument of labor pacification — he "had turned the minds of workmen from wages and hours to higher things, and thus averted strikes" (007-042) — and the speech endorses this function without reservation. When Babbitt later begins his [[babbitts-rebellion|rebellion]], his defense of radicalism and his sympathy for strikers represent a direct repudiation of the orthodoxy his Boosters' speeches once affirmed.
