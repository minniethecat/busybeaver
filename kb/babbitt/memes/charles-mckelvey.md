# Charles McKelvey

Charles L. McKelvey is the acknowledged center of Zenith's Smart Set — a millionaire contractor whose collegiate glory as football captain and hammer-thrower of the Class of '96 (015-012) has been seamlessly converted into social dominance. He is the man whose dinner table Babbitt most desperately longs to sit at, the figure for whom social warmth is both a gift and a weapon.

## The Hero of the Class of '96

McKelvey's social position rests on a foundation that is distinctively American: not birth but performance. At the state university, he was "not only football captain and hammer-thrower but debater, and passable in what the State University considered scholarship" (015-012). The qualification is Lewis's own — "passable in what the State University considered scholarship" is a phrase that simultaneously honors McKelvey's achievement and quietly deflates the institution that conferred it. McKelvey is a hero of a system whose standards are themselves modest.

At the alumni dinner, McKelvey stands "warming his admirers like a furnace" (015-011) — the image is comic but also precise. McKelvey's social function is to radiate warmth, and the men who cluster around him are there to absorb it. Babbitt, working his way through the crowd, "felt slight and adoring" beside McKelvey (015-014), a telling contrast to the "ponderous and protective" feeling he has with [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]]. With Paul, Babbitt is the stronger man; with McKelvey, he is a supplicant.

## The Dinner Party

The centerpiece of McKelvey's role in the novel is the dinner party to which the Babbitts successfully maneuver an invitation — and which becomes a catastrophe of social aspiration. The episode occupies much of Chapter XV and is one of Lewis's most sustained set-pieces of social comedy.

The Babbitts' invitation is extracted through a combination of alumni nostalgia and aggressive friendliness: Babbitt reminds McKelvey of their shared college past — "Say, Charley, juh remember in Junior year how we chartered a sea-going hack and chased down to Riverdale?" (015-019) — and seizes the moment of warmth to issue the invitation. McKelvey's response is "much less vague" (015-020) than his initial politeness, and the date is set.

The McKelveys arrive "less than fifteen minutes late" (015-030) — a detail that conveys the exquisite calibration of social superiority: late enough to signal status, early enough to avoid rudeness. Babbitt's concern that the neighbors see the McKelveys' limousine and uniformed chauffeur (015-031) reveals how thoroughly the dinner is an exercise in public display rather than private hospitality.

The dinner itself is a disaster of mismatched expectations. Babbitt's overpraise — McKelvey is declared "an inspiration to ambitious youth," Mrs. McKelvey "an adornment to the social circles of Zenith, Washington, New York, Paris, and numbers of other places" (015-032, 015-033) — is so excessive it becomes self-parody. The McKelveys leave early, and the hoped-for return invitation never comes: "For a month they watched the social columns, and waited for a return dinner-invitation" (015-039).

## The Meaning of McKelvey

McKelvey is not malicious; he is simply indifferent. His geniality is genuine but limited — it extends to those within his circle and evaporates at its boundary. He is "placidly merry now with the great, the manufacturers and bankers, the land-owners and lawyers and surgeons who had chauffeurs and went to Europe" (015-014). Babbitt can approach this circle, can even briefly enter it on the strength of old acquaintance and aggressive good cheer, but he cannot belong to it. The circle's boundary is invisible but impermeable.

Lewis's subtlety lies in making McKelvey likeable — or at least not dislikable. He is not a snob in the English sense; he does not consciously exclude. He simply lives in a world whose boundaries are maintained by forces so naturalized they require no enforcement. The McKelveys' failure to return the dinner invitation is not a decision but an absence of decision — they simply do not think of the Babbitts again.

McKelvey's social world is also international in a way Babbitt's is not. He casually mentions having met "a Senator, a Balkan princess, and an English major-general" at a Washington dinner, calling the princess "Jenny" and letting it be known that "he had danced with her" (015-017). This is social capital of a different order from Babbitt's — it transcends Zenith and touches the larger world, reinforcing McKelvey's status as a man for whom the city is not a horizon but a base.

## Character Traits

McKelvey is hearty, expansive, and fundamentally empty — a man whose social warmth is entirely performance, but a performance so practiced it has become indistinguishable from the thing itself. He is not cruel, not calculating, not even especially vain. He is simply a man who has never had to ask whether his life has meaning, because his life has always been defined for him by the admiration of others.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt]]** — Views Babbitt with genial condescension; the man who can be charmed for an evening but not admitted to the circle
- **[[lucile-mckelvey]]** — His "pretty wife," red-haired and exquisite, who enforces through exquisite rudeness the social boundaries McKelvey's bonhomie blurs (013-025)
- **[[william-washington-eathorne]]** — Stands above McKelvey in the social hierarchy; new money, however magnificent, still defers to old
- **[[paul-riesling]]** — The classmate for whom McKelvey's social dominance is a matter of indifference — and who is therefore the one man at the dinner Babbitt most wants McKelvey to meet
- **[[max-kruger]]** — The banker with whom McKelvey casually plans to host titled English visitors (015-015), a glimpse of the transnational elite to which McKelvey belongs
