# Babbitt's Dinner Party

The dinner party that [[george-babbitt]] and Myra Babbitt host at their Floral Heights home in Chapter IX — a set piece of middle-class social performance in which the rituals of dining, drinking, and amateur spiritualism expose both the authentic fellowship and the quiet desperation that structure the Babbitts' social world.

## Background

The party requires advance preparation that reveals Babbitt's relationship to Prohibition-era social norms. In Chapter VIII, Babbitt visits the bootlegger Healey Hanson to procure gin for the party, a transaction that combines criminality with middle-class respectability. Babbitt introduces himself as "a great friend of Jake Offutt's" and, "melodramatically," creeps into Hanson's apartment (008-018). The procurement of alcohol — illegal but socially obligatory — establishes the party's dual character: a performance of propriety underwritten by systematic hypocrisy.

At the Babbitt home, domestic tensions precede the guests' arrival. The family dinners in Chapter VI show Babbitt already restless: "I don't know how we all get to scrapping this way. Like to go off some place and be able to hear myself think" (006-029). His daughter Verona "escaped, immediately after dinner, with no discussion" (006-029), and the dinner-gong itself, beaten by the "Lettish-Croat maid, a powerful woman" (006-022), signals the mechanical regularity of domestic routine against which the party promises a temporary reprieve.

## Key Elements

- **The gin procurement** — Babbitt's furtive visit to Healey Hanson, the bootlegger, illustrates the hypocrisy of Prohibition-era middle-class sociability: the party's conviviality depends on a crime everyone tacitly accepts (008-018).
- **The dinner** — Fried chicken, prepared by the maid Matilda, becomes the centerpiece of the meal. Afterward, Babbitt praises it as "fried to the Queen's taste. Best fried chicken I've tasted for a coon's age" (009-039).
- **The spiritualist table-tipping** — Mrs. Frink, "a small twittering woman," proposes that the guests "try and do some spiritualism and table-tipping" (009-018). The séance, conducted in dimness, gender-segregates the gathering: "the goodwives quivered and adored" while "the men were rather solemn and foolish" (009-018).
- **The bridge tables** — Before the spiritualism, Babbitt "ambled back to the bridge-tables" (009-018), too "languid this evening to pursue his duty of being a captivating (though strictly moral) male" (009-018).

## Satirical Significance

The dinner party is Lewis's anatomy of middle-class leisure as ritualized emptiness. Every element — the illegal gin, the predictable menu, the faddish spiritualism — is consumed without genuine pleasure or intellectual engagement. The party "works" socially (Mrs. Babbitt declares it the "best party this year, by a long shot" (009-039)), but only because its participants have agreed not to notice the gap between their performance of enjoyment and their actual experience.

The gender dynamics are especially revealing. The spiritualist interlude separates the sexes — the women take "command" of the spirit world, while the men, whose domain is "base things material" (009-018), are reduced to awkward participation. Lewis suggests that the Babbitts' social world offers women so little intellectual stimulation that table-tipping becomes a genuine entertainment.

## Consequences

The party succeeds on its own terms — Mrs. Babbitt is "beaming" and Babbitt, despite his private reservations, performs the expected role, declaring the soup "corking" and "best soup I've tasted since Heck was a pup" (009-039). But the success is hollow. Babbitt's voice is "seeping away" as he delivers his compliments (009-039), and the party's aftermath — the couple standing "in the hall, under the electric light in its square box-like shade of red glass bound with nickel" (009-039) — underscores the mechanical quality of their domestic happiness.

The dinner party prefigures the pattern that will define Babbitt's [[babbitts-rebellion|rebellion]]: the performance of satisfaction that temporarily suppresses but never resolves the underlying discontent. It is one of many such rituals — alongside the [[boosters-club-banquet|Boosters' Club Banquet]] and the [[sunday-school-campaign]] — that sustain the social order Babbitt will later attempt to defy.
