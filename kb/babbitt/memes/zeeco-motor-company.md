## Overview

The Zeeco Motor Company is an automobile manufacturer in Zenith. The Zeeco car is positioned between the economy and luxury segments — "above the Javelin class" — and Babbitt's advertising firm handles its promotional campaigns (009-038). The company is significant as one of the local industrial enterprises that Babbitt, as a real-estate agent and booster, visits during his sales rounds.

## Role in the Novel

### A Stop on Babbitt's Sales Tour

On a day of business calls, Babbitt and Henry Thompson visit the Zeeco Motor Company after leaving the Linden Lane Cemetery. They interview the sales-manager, Noël Ryland, "about a discount on a Zeeco car for Thompson" (006-006). The visit is part of a systematic canvassing of Zenith businesses.

### Key Personnel: Noël Ryland

Noël Ryland, the sales-manager of the Zeeco, is "a frivolous graduate of Princeton," in contrast to Babbitt who is described as "a sound and standard ware from that great department-store, the State University" (006-006). This contrast between Eastern establishment and Midwestern practicality reflects the novel's recurring tension between Zenith's provincial pride and its insecurity before older cultural centers.

### T. Cholmondeley Frink's Zeeco Advertising Campaign

The Zeeco advertising account becomes a vehicle for exploring the character of T. Cholmondeley Frink, Zenith's resident poet-advertising man. Frink tells his neighbors at Babbitt's dinner party: "I'm doing a series of ads for the Zeeco Car and I want to make each of 'em a real little gem — reg'lar stylistic stuff" (008-050). He confesses the challenge of industrial copywriting: "But the poetry of industrialism, now there's a literary line where you got to open up new territory" (008-051).

Faced with creative pressure, Frink makes an ambitious attempt at the highbrow: "So I decided to stick to the straight poetic, and I took a shot at a highbrow ad for the Zeeco" (008-054). He reads his draft aloud to the gathered dinner guests:

> "The long white trail is calling — calling — and it's over the hills and far away for every man or woman that has red blood in his veins and on his lips the ancient song of the buccaneers. It's away with dull drudging, and a fig for care. Speed — glorious Speed — it's more than just a moment's exhilaration — it's Life for you and me! This great new truth the makers of the Zeeco Car have considered as much as price and style. It's fleet as the antelope, smooth as the glide of a swallow, yet powerful as the charge of a bull-elephant. Class breathes in every line. Listen, brother! You'll never know what the high art of hiking is till you TRY LIFE'S ZIPPINGEST ZEST — THE ZEECO!" (008-054)

Despite his own satisfaction — "that's got an elegant color to it" — Frink worries that "it ain't got the originality of 'spill-of-speech!'" The passage satirizes both the pretensions of advertising copy and the cultural aspirations of the Zenith business class (008-054).

### Product Positioning

The Zeeco's market position is described explicitly by Eddie Swanson, an automobile insider: "If you want to go above the Javelin class, the Zeeco is a mighty good buy. Couple of nice features" (009-038). He goes on to relate the results of a performance test: "this was a fair, square test, they took a Zeeco stock touring-car and they slid up the Tonawanda hill on high" (009-038). This places the Zeeco as a mid-range automobile, better than entry-level but not competing with luxury marques.

### Business Network Significance

The Zeeco Motor Company is one node in the intricate web of Zenith businesses connected through the Boosters' Club. Visits to the Zeeco and similar firms are part of the daily ritual of maintaining business relationships — the "You tickle my car, I'll tickle yours" ethos that Babbitt himself articulates (006-006).
