# Lloyd Mallam

Lloyd Mallam is the owner of the Hafiz Book Shop and, as Sinclair Lewis tells us, a poet. He makes a single, luminous appearance in the novel, one that encapsulates the central tension between art and commerce, between the life of the mind and the life of the Boosters' Club. In Zenith, a poet who runs a bookshop is an anomaly — a man who lives among words in a city that values only prices.

## Role in the Novel

Mallam appears in a single sentence, but it is one of the novel's most quietly devastating passages. As Babbitt sits amid the chatter of his acquaintances, "Lloyd Mallam, the poet, owner of the Hafiz Book Shop, was finishing a rondeau to show how diverting was life amid the feuds of medieval Florence, but how dull it was in so obvious a place as Zenith" (007-063).

The sentence is a masterpiece of Lewis's satirical method. Mallam's rondeau — a fixed poetic form associated with the French Renaissance — is an act of imaginative escape from a city that offers no imaginative sustenance. The word "obvious" is the key: Zenith, in Mallam's judgment (and Lewis's), is a place without mystery, without depth, without anything that cannot be priced and catalogued.

## The Hafiz Book Shop

The name of Mallam's establishment is itself a quiet joke. Hafiz was a fourteenth-century Persian poet whose verses celebrate wine, love, and the defiance of religious orthodoxy — precisely the values that Prohibition-era Zenith exists to suppress. That Mallam's shop bears this name suggests either an aspiration toward a richer cultural life or a quiet act of literary subversion. The shop, like its proprietor, exists on the margins of Zenith's commercial life, tolerated but not celebrated.

The choice of "Hafiz" is not arbitrary. In naming his shop after a poet whose work celebrates ecstatic experience and the rejection of conventional piety, Mallam plants a small flag of cultural dissent in the heart of a city built on conventionality. The irony is that almost no one in Zenith would recognize the reference — the subversion is invisible to its intended audience, and therefore safe. The Hafiz Book Shop can exist precisely because Zenith does not know what Hafiz means.

## The Rondeau as Resistance

Mallam's chosen poetic form merits attention. The rondeau is a fixed form — governed by strict rules of rhyme, refrain, and meter — that reached its highest development in the French Renaissance. To write a rondeau in 1920s Zenith is to perform an act of double removal: from the present into the past, and from America into Europe. The form itself is a refusal of modernist fragmentation and of American informality alike.

That Mallam's rondeau takes medieval Florence as its subject is equally significant. Florence — city of Dante, of Michelangelo, of the Medici — represents in the Western imagination the supreme fusion of commerce and culture, of money and beauty. Mallam's rondeau argues, by its very existence, that Zenith's commerce produces no comparable beauty — that the Boosters' Club will never be mistaken for the Medici court. The "feuds of medieval Florence" are, in Mallam's implicit comparison, more "diverting" than the feuds of modern Zenith because they took place in a civilization that valued more than the price per front foot.

## Significance

Mallam functions in the novel as a kind of counterweight to T. Cholmondeley Frink, the syndicated poet whose verses celebrate business efficiency and civic boosterism. Where Frink turns poetry into a commodity, Mallam uses it as a means of private resistance. He does not write odes to the Zeeco Motor Company; he writes rondeaux about medieval Florence. His silence in the novel — he is observed but never speaks — reinforces his status as an outsider in a city that demands constant, cheerful noise.

The contrast between Mallam and Frink is not merely one of taste but of function. Frink's verse serves the commercial order — it is, in the strict sense, advertising in metrical form. Mallam's verse, by contrast, serves no commercial purpose whatsoever. It does not sell condiments or real estate; it does not boost Zenith or celebrate business efficiency. It exists purely for its own sake, and in this uselessness lies its moral force. In a city where everything must justify itself by its contribution to commerce, a rondeau about medieval Florence is a small act of treason.
