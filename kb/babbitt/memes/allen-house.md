# Allen House

A hotel in Monarch, the city that rivals Zenith, whose ballroom serves as the central venue for the annual convention of the state real-estate association — the professional gathering at which Babbitt delivers his celebrated oration and is swept up in the rituals of business camaraderie (013-034). The convention was held at Monarch, described as "Zenith's chief rival among the smaller cities of the state," and the Allen House was chosen as its headquarters (013-004).

## Description

The Allen House provides the primary meeting space for the state real-estate convention. The meetings were held "in the ballroom of the Allen House," while an anteroom served as "the office of the chairman of the executive committee" (013-034). This anteroom was described as "littered with crumpled papers," where the chairman, "the busiest man in the convention," was "so busy that he got nothing done whatever" (013-034). The chairman sat "at a marquetry table" in a room filled with the debris of organizational activity that produced no actual results (013-034).

The hotel is part of Monarch's effort to compete with Zenith for the convention trade. While Babbitt stays at the Hotel Sedgwick with W. A. Rogers, the Allen House is where the convention's official business unfolds — a separation that mirrors the gap between the delegates' private networking in hotel bedrooms (where "the real convention consisted of men muttering") and the public ceremonies in the ballroom (013-034)(013-035).

At the convention's conclusion, "the golden doors of the ballroom opened with a blatting of trumpets" as the crowd spilled out into the corridors, the formal proceedings dissolving into the informal exchanges that constituted the convention's true purpose (013-048).

## Social Function

The Allen House ballroom is the stage on which the conventions of professional boosterism are performed. The opening session begins with "a welcome by the mayor of Monarch," followed by a prayer from "the pastor of the First Christian Church of Monarch, a large man with a long damp frontal lock," who "informed God that the real-estate men were here now" — a prayer less supplication than civic announcement (013-036).

The formal program includes Major Carlton Tuke's denunciation of cooperative stores, William A. Larkin's "comforting prognosis" on construction prospects, and the reminder that "plate-glass prices were two points lower" (013-037). J. Harry Barmhill, the convention chairman, presided over the sessions, at one point declaring that "the Zenith real-estate men had made up their minds to grab this convention off their neighbor burgs like we've grabbed the Rouncefield pottery and the Deturey Chemical Company" — a statement of civic conquest delivered as banquet rhetoric (013-049).

The convention's official character — "The convention was on" (013-038) — is established in this ballroom, even as the "real convention" happened elsewhere (013-035). The Allen House thus serves as the architectural correlative of the divide between public performance and private dealing that structures the novel's portrait of business culture. The ballroom embodies the performative dimension of boosterism: the rituals of welcome, prayer, and formal papers that lend legitimacy to the informal networking that is the convention's actual engine.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Allen House ballroom as a site of ceremonial emptiness. The chairman "so busy that he got nothing done whatever" (013-034), the pastor treating prayer as civic press release (013-036), the formal papers on plate-glass prices and cooperative stores (013-037) — all suggest a professional gathering in which the declared purpose (improving real-estate practice) is wholly subordinate to the undeclared one (networking, self-congratulation, and escape from domestic routine).

The convention's rhetoric, culminating in Barmhill's boast that Zenith would "grab this convention off their neighbor burgs" (013-049), reveals boosterism as a form of civic aggression dressed in banquet-hall camaraderie. The golden doors that open "with a blatting of trumpets" (013-048) are the architectural equivalent of this rhetorical inflation — grand gestures that frame the small transactions of business as historic events. The Allen House's ballroom is where the fiction of professional purpose is maintained, while the hotel bedrooms host the actual business of the convention.
