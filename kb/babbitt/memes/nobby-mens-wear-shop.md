# Nobby Men's Wear Shop

A downtown Zenith clothing retailer whose storefront becomes the site of one of George F. Babbitt's small daily rituals of self-approval — a moment in which the purchase of a costly scarf confirms his identity as a successful, solvent businessman and crystallizes the novel's systematic critique of consumption as the foundation of middle-class selfhood.

## Description

The Nobby Men's Wear Shop is one of the commercial establishments that line Babbitt's morning driving route through downtown Zenith, catalogued by Lewis in the extended street-level panorama of Chapter V. Its name — with the slangy modifier "Nobby," a period colloquialism for fashionable or elegant — signals the aspirational character of the goods sold within: they promise not merely clothing but stylishness, a quality Babbitt yearns for but can only approximate through expenditure.

The shop itself is never entered or described in interior detail. Like many of the novel's commercial institutions, it exists primarily as a reflective surface — in this case, almost literally: the storefront provides the occasion for Babbitt to touch his scarf, admire his own appearance, and rehearse the mental equation between his purchases and his worth.

## Babbitt's Encounter

Each morning, Babbitt drives past the Nobby Men's Wear Shop on his route to the office. Lewis describes the moment with characteristic precision: "At the Nobby Men's Wear Shop he took his left hand off the steering-wheel to touch his scarf, and thought well of himself as one who bought expensive ties 'and could pay cash for 'em, too, by golly'" (005-009).

The gesture — taking his hand off the wheel to touch his scarf — is a small, almost unconscious act of vanity, performed daily and therefore ritualized. The interior monologue that accompanies it reveals the syllogism by which Babbitt constructs his identity: he buys expensive ties; he pays cash; therefore he is the kind of man who matters. The phrase "and could pay cash for 'em, too, by golly" carries the particular emphasis of a man for whom cash payment is not merely a transaction method but a moral credential — proof of solvency, discipline, and membership in the community of solid citizens.

## Consumer Identity

The Nobby Men's Wear Shop episode exemplifies a pattern that runs throughout the novel: Babbitt's identity is constructed through his purchases. His scarf, his car, his house in Floral Heights, his membership in the Boosters' Club — each is a possession that testifies to his standing, and each requires the constant mental work of self-reassurance that Lewis renders with such clinical precision.

The shop's function in the novel is thus metonymic: it stands for the entire system of commercial stimuli that surrounds Babbitt and that he navigates with "the fondness of familiarity" (005-007). The advertising hoardings, the shop windows, the displays of goods — these are the texts from which Babbitt reads his own value. The Nobby Men's Wear Shop, like the United Cigar Store and the other establishments on his route, does not merely sell products; it sells the possibility of becoming the person those products promise.

## Downtown Zenith Commerce

The Nobby Men's Wear Shop belongs to the parade of downtown storefronts that Lewis catalogues in the opening movement of Chapter V: the Simplex Office Furniture Shop, the National Cash Register Agency, the United Cigar Store, and others [005-007–009]. Together they form the commercial landscape that Babbitt traverses with proprietary satisfaction, each shop window offering him a reflection — sometimes literal, sometimes metaphorical — of the man he believes himself to be.

Lewis's technique in this passage is accumulative: the catalogue of storefronts builds a composite portrait of the city's commercial culture, in which every human need and desire has been commodified and given a storefront. The Nobby Men's Wear Shop, in this context, is one node in a network of consumption that constitutes the real religion of Zenith — far more potent than the Presbyterianism to which Babbitt pays Sunday-morning tribute.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's morning drive (Ch. V)** — Passing the Nobby Men's Wear Shop, Babbitt touches his scarf and mentally congratulates himself on his ability to pay cash for expensive neckwear, a daily ritual of consumer self-validation that Lewis renders as both comic and quietly damning (005-009)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis's treatment of the Nobby Men's Wear Shop illustrates his method of satirizing American consumer culture not through direct condemnation but through the precise rendering of its psychological effects. Babbitt's scarf-touching gesture is, in isolation, trivial — the kind of detail a lesser novelist might omit. But Lewis understands that identity in a consumer society is constructed precisely through such trivialities: the accumulation of small purchases, each bearing its freight of self-congratulation, that together constitute the edifice of the middle-class self.

The satire operates through the gap between Babbitt's self-perception and the reader's. Where Babbitt sees proof of his substance — "could pay cash for 'em, too, by golly" — the reader sees a man whose sense of worth is entirely contingent on the goods he has bought and the cash he has paid. The Nobby Men's Wear Shop, in the novel's moral economy, is less a clothing store than a temple of the faith Babbitt actually practices: the religion of purchase power.
