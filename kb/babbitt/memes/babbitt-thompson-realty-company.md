# Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company

George Babbitt's real estate firm, located prominently along Third Street in the Reeves Building, forming the economic foundation of his middle-class success and his identity as a Zenith business leader. The partnership with his father-in-law Henry Thompson placed Babbitt among Zenith's "substantial and forward-looking interests" (032-018) and connected him to the city's interlocking network of business, political, and social power.

## Description

The Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company occupied a storefront along Third Street in the Reeves Building, sharing the block with a Western Union Telegraph Office, the Blue Delft Candy Shop, and Shotwell's Stationery Shop (003-035). The Reeves Building itself was "as fireproof as a rock and as efficient as a typewriter; fourteen stories of yellow pressed brick, with clean, upright, unornamental lines" (003-034) — an architectural embodiment of the no-nonsense business values Babbitt professes. Babbitt took immense pride in the building: he noted with satisfaction that "the California Building across the way was three stories lower, therefore three stories less beautiful" (005-008).

The office interior reflected Babbitt's understanding of what a modern business should look like. His desk was equipped with a telephone, a dictaphone, and a cigar-lighter shaped like a nude cherub — each emblem of the mechanized, status-conscious business culture Lewis satirizes (003-046). Babbitt assured himself that "no tenant of the Reeves Building had a more expensive water-cooler" (003-046), and he employed a small staff: Miss McGoun the stenographer, Stanley Graff the outside salesman, old Mat Penniman the general utility man, and several clerks (003-042). Among the "little unknown people" who inhabited the corridors of the Reeves Building were "elevator-runners, starter, engineers, superintendent, and the doubtful-looking lame man who conducted the news and cigar stand" (003-037). These minor figures form the background of Babbitt's professional world — seen but not registered, essential to the building's operation but beneath the notice of a partner-owner.

The firm handled residential property sales throughout Zenith's expanding suburbs. Their listings included bungalows in Silver Grove, lots in Glen Oriole, and land in the new development of Dorchester [003-057, 019-002]. Babbitt's daily routine at the office combined dictation, property promotion, and performance. His form-letters deployed the breezy, promotional language of modern business culture, filled with exhortations like "let's go to it" and closing with "Yours for service" (003-057). He composed copy for the Linden Lane cemetery, describing it as "the only strictly up-to-date burial place in or near Zenith, where exquisitely gardened plots look from daisy-dotted hill-slopes" (004-009), signed with the imposing letterhead "BABBITT-THOMPSON REALTY COMPANY, Reeves Building" (004-010). After dictation concluded, "with its need of sitting and thinking instead of bustling around and making a noise and really doing something," Babbitt would sit "creakily back" (003-059) — the mental exertion of composing sales copy requiring a physical recovery.

The firm's promotional methods extended beyond form-letters into direct salesmanship. Babbitt "substantially" noted that his own firm was "the first in the city to give the free lecture on 'Why I Got a Home of My Own'" — an illustrated talk with lantern slides that pulled in prospective buyers (004-026). This blend of educational pretense and commercial purpose typified the booster approach: the lecture was not a public service but a sales funnel, the lantern slides not art but advertising, and Babbitt's own role not educator but closer. The philosophy was explicit: "Get a prospect, get a lot of prospects, keep them coming, and then talk turkey" (004-026).

## Social Function

The Babbitt-Thompson office functioned as a stage for Babbitt's performance of business authority. He timed his exits for maximum effect, "giving a thousand enjoyable fussy directions" before leaving for lunch (006-016), and he monitored his staff with a mixture of pride and anxiety. The office was also a site of social maneuvering: Babbitt reflected on old Jake Offutt's maxim that "a wise bird never goes love-making in his own office or his own home" (003-062), and the presence of Miss McGoun, his efficient and attractive stenographer, was a continuing source of both comfort and tension.

The employees formed a small, self-contained social group. Stanley Graff, the outside salesman, was "a youngish man given to cigarettes and the playing of pool" (003-042) who later fell into open disagreement with Babbitt (016-007) and was fired in a dramatic scene [019-017, 019-021]. Old Mat Penniman was "a real antique" (003-042) who represented continuity and tradition in the office. Miss McGoun, "the paragon of stenographers" (032-030), was the emotional center of the office — her efficiency, her attractiveness, and eventually her departure all registered powerfully with Babbitt.

The firm's integration with Zenith's power structure was exemplified by its relationship with the Street Traction Company. Babbitt's father-in-law Henry Thompson told him bluntly: "You've stood in, you and your father-in-law, with some of the most substantial and forward-looking interests in town, like my friends of the Street Traction Company" (032-018). This was not mere networking — it was the machinery by which Zenith's development was directed and its profits distributed. The Dorchester project illustrated the mechanism: the Street Traction Company planned to build car-repair shops in the suburb of Dorchester, but when they came to buy the land they found it held by the Babbitt-Thompson Company as agents — a situation engineered by insider foreknowledge (019-002). "Just after this Babbitt deposited three thousand dollars in the bank, the purchasing-agent of the Street Traction Company bought a five thousand dollar car" (019-003). The novel's deadpan juxtaposition of these facts — the deposit, the purchase — invites the reader to draw their own conclusions about the relationship between public utility planning and private enrichment.

Stanley Graff later confronted Babbitt directly after being fired: "if I told the prosecuting attorney what I know about this last Street Traction option steal, both you and me would go to jail" (019-021). The threat is defused, but its echo lingers through the rest of the novel. Babbitt and Henry Thompson had reason to be nervous: the Graff episode exposed the vulnerability beneath the firm's prosperous surface, the unspoken truth that Zenith's "substantial interests" depended on arrangements that could not withstand legal scrutiny.

The firm's chief rival was Sanders, Torrey and Wing, a real estate firm that competed with Babbitt-Thompson for development deals. When the Street Traction Company planned a real-estate coup, Babbitt learned through Thompson that Sanders, Torrey and Wing — not his own firm — had been selected to handle the transaction (032-027). The rivalry intensified when Babbitt's loyal secretary, Miss McGoun, left his employ to join Sanders, Torrey and Wing (032-030), a defection that wounded Babbitt's pride and signaled his declining fortunes. The episode revealed the fragility of business relationships in Zenith: loyalty was contingent on success, and a single "little rumor about your being a crank" could unravel years of client relationships (032-027).

Though the company presented itself as merely the sales agent for developments like Glen Oriole, Babbitt and his partner Henry Thompson actually owned sixty-two percent of the project, while the president of the Zenith Street Traction Company owned another twenty-eight percent — the official developer Jake Offutt held only ten percent, in exchange for fixing health and fire inspectors and a member of the State Transportation Commission (004-028). As Lewis observes, "The whole of the Glen Oriole project was a suggestion that Babbitt, though he really did hate men recognized as swindlers, was not too unreasonably honest" (004-028).

## Business Operations and Deal-Making

The daily rhythm of the office revealed the theatrical nature of Babbitt's business methods. The telephone, the dictaphone, and the carbon copies flowing through Miss McGoun's typewriter were the props; Babbitt's booming voice and hearty manner were the performance. He could "snatch from a drawer the agreement he had had Miss McGoun type out a week ago" and deploy it for maximum dramatic effect (004-037), timing his reveals with the instinct of a showman. The business of real estate in Zenith was inseparable from the business of self-presentation.

Babbitt's philosophy of property extended beyond profit into a kind of civic theology. Real estate development _was_ progress — "the whole town is a mighty sweet proposition," he told Stanley Graff, "and I don't mind saying it, I'm boosting for it morning, noon, and night" (006-037). His sales rhetoric merged seamlessly with his booster rhetoric because, for Babbitt, there was no distinction: selling lots was selling Zenith, and selling Zenith was selling lots. Every transaction was simultaneously a business deal and an act of civic devotion.

The firm's competitive position depended on insider information — the knowledge, typically conveyed through Henry Thompson, of where the Street Traction Company planned to extend its lines next. This was not corruption in the overt sense of bribery but rather the quiet operation of a system in which public utilities and private developers were interlocked through ownership, kinship, and club membership. Babbitt was honest enough to be indignant about "men recognized as swindlers" (004-028) but flexible enough to profit from arrangements that a less sympathetic observer might classify the same way.

The Glen Oriole development exemplified this blurred boundary. The deal was structured so that Jake Offutt appeared as the developer while Babbitt and Thompson held the controlling interest (004-028). Offutt's actual contribution — fixing inspectors and a transportation commissioner — was the illegal part, but the ownership structure was merely expedient. Babbitt could tell himself he was a legitimate businessman, and in the context of Zenith in the 1920s, the distinction between speculation and swindling was largely a matter of who you were and whom you knew.

## Key Members

- **[[George-Babbitt]]** — partner and the firm's public face, whose identity is inseparable from the company
- **[[Henry-Thompson]]** — Babbitt's father-in-law and business partner, whose financial acumen grounds the firm and whose connections to the Street Traction Company provide the inside track on development opportunities
- **[[Miss-McGoun]]** — Babbitt's secretary, "the paragon of stenographers" (032-030). She appears as his efficient stenographer, dashing in "to whisper, with the excitement of an approaching scene" (019-017); she types the agreements Babbitt deploys theatrically (004-037); she fields telephone calls that introduce complications into Babbitt's life (028-001). Babbitt's relationship with her is suggestively ambiguous — after a period of marital discontent, "he thought of his stenographer, Miss McGoun" (023-023) and "kept Miss McGoun after he had finished dictating" (023-035), though he immediately "utterly repudiated the view that he had been trying to discover how approachable was Miss McGoun" (023-037). Her departure is one of the novel's quiet turning points: she leaves for "excellent reasons — she needed a rest, her sister was sick, she might not do any more work for a year" (032-029), but "had, a week after leaving him, gone over to his dangerous competitors, Sanders, Torrey and Wing" (032-030). Babbitt was "jarred as by nothing else" (032-029) and "longed for Miss McGoun's return, and thought of writing to her" (032-029) — a longing that is part professional need, part something less easily named.
- **[[Stanley-Graff]]** — outside salesman, "a youngish man given to cigarettes and the playing of pool" (003-042). His falling-out with Babbitt (016-007) culminates in a confrontation where Graff threatens to expose the Street Traction deal (019-021), marking the first cracks in the firm's facade of solidity. Babbitt's own hiring criteria are inadvertently revealed when he reflects on Graff: "he had been in the office three years, he was an active salesman, and Babbitt liked to look at him — clean, sharp, a good sport, a man who was likely to succeed in business" (016-007).
- **[[Mat-Penniman]]** — "old Mat Penniman, general utility man, a real antique" (003-042), representing continuity and tradition in the office. He is "broken, silent, gray" (003-042), a living reminder of what a lifetime of service without partnership looks like

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt at his desk** — The opening chapter shows Babbitt in his private office, surrounded by the props of modern business: telephone, dictaphone, cigar-lighter, and correspondence bearing the firm's imposing letterhead (003-049). At the right moment, Babbitt could "snatch from a drawer the agreement he had had Miss McGoun type out a week ago" and deploy it for maximum dramatic effect (004-037).
- **The Glen Oriole deal** — Babbitt and Thompson's hidden ownership of sixty-two percent of Glen Oriole, with Jake Offutt as the nominal developer, reveals the insider dealing behind Zenith's real estate boom (004-028)
- **Graff's dismissal and blackmail threat** — Babbitt fires Stanley Graff in a dramatic scene, and Graff threatens to expose the Street Traction option steal [019-017, 019-021]
- **The Lyte deal** — Conrad Lyte's proposed outlying business-center development on Linton Ridge represents the firm's largest potential transaction. Babbitt visits the grocer Archibald Purdy to buy up adjacent property before Purdy learns of the development (008-017). The deal becomes the vehicle for Babbitt's finest moment of professional performance when he delivers his Annual Address to the Real Estate Board, using the Lyte deal as the centerpiece of his talk on "the ethics of the real-estate profession" (014-017). The irony is rich: Babbitt lectures on ethics using as his example a secret insider-purchase of land from an unsuspecting grocer.
- **Losing the Traction deal** — Thompson informs Babbitt that the Street Traction Company has chosen Sanders, Torrey and Wing, attributing the loss to rumors about Babbitt's political nonconformity (032-027). The moment crystallizes the connection between Babbitt's social standing and his business prospects: his brief rebellion against Zenith's orthodoxies translates directly into lost income.
- **Miss McGoun's defection** — Babbitt learns his secretary has joined the rival firm, compounding the loss of the Traction deal (032-030). The defection is both practical — he loses an excellent stenographer — and symbolic: his professional house is crumbling around him.
- **Recovery** — Jake Offutt reports the Traction Bunch are dissatisfied with Sanders and willing to return to Babbitt-Thompson; Babbitt and the purchasing-agent make plans for "the secret purchase of lots along the Evanston Road" [034-029, 034-031]. The novel ends with Babbitt back in the game, the machinery of insider dealing restored, his period of rebellion absorbed and forgotten by the system that never doubted its own endurance.

## Satirical Significance

The Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company serves as a vehicle for Lewis's critique of American business ethics in the 1920s. The Glen Oriole arrangement — where the nominal developer is a front man for the real owners — satirizes the routine corruption beneath Zenith's boosterish rhetoric about "progress" and "development" (004-028). The firm's promotional language, with its mechanically cheerful exhortations and empty slogans, mirrors Babbitt's own hollow rhetoric as a public speaker.

The realty company functions as more than Babbitt's profession — it is the framework through which he understands his place in the world. At dinner parties he introduces himself as "George Babbitt of the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company" (008-018), the title conferring the identity he most values. His business card bears the same inscription, and his self-respect is inseparable from the company's standing. When his social standing crumbles during his brief rebellion, the business consequences are immediate: clients withdraw, rumors circulate, and his professional network — painstakingly built over years of boosterism — begins to dissolve.

The firm's integration into Babbitt's identity is so complete that his midlife crisis plays out through the office. His dissatisfaction with Miss McGoun's carbon-paper expenditures (025-032) precedes his wider dissatisfaction with his life; her departure (032-030) coincides with his social ostracism; and Jake Offutt's report that the Traction Bunch want to return (034-029) signals his reintegration into the Zenith establishment. The office is not merely where Babbitt works — it is where his status is created, maintained, and threatened.

Lewis's satire works through accumulation rather than exaggeration. Each detail — the nude-cherub cigar-lighter, the "most expensive water-cooler," the catechism of form-letters — builds the portrait of a man whose personality has been shaped by, and become indistinguishable from, his profession. The firm's letterhead reads "BABBITT-THOMPSON REALTY COMPANY, Reeves Building, zinith" [sic] — a minor detail that captures the gap between Zenith's self-image and its reality: the city that calls itself Zenith cannot spell its own name on its own business stationery.

Most tellingly, the firm demonstrates that in Zenith, a man _is_ what he sells — and what he sells is himself. Babbitt's product is not houses or lots or development opportunities; it is George F. Babbitt, the man you can trust, the booster who believes in Zenith, the solid citizen who belongs to every right-thinking club. When the town stops believing in this performance, the business collapses. When the town decides Babbitt is still one of them after all, the business revives. The commodity traded at the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company is ultimately Babbitt's own conformity.
