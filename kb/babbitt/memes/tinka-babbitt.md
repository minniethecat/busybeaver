# Tinka Babbitt

Katherine Babbitt — always called Tinka — is the youngest child of George and [[myra-babbitt|Myra Babbitt]]. At ten years old, she is "still a baby" to her family, a perpetual child whose innocence and perceptiveness offer fleeting relief from the novel's satirical treatment of adult [[zenith|Zenith]]. [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s tenderness toward Tinka is one of his few genuinely attractive traits (001-030, 002-011).

## Role in the Novel

Tinka serves as a register of the family's emotional temperature. When [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is at his most human, it is often in his interactions with her — calling her "kittiedoolie," sending her post[[automobile|car]]ds from business trips, worrying that she isn't sleeping. Lewis uses Tinka sparingly but effectively: she is the one member of the [[family|household]] who neither lectures nor competes, and her presence reminds the reader that [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] is not merely a type but a father (002-012, 013-032).

## Key Scenes

- **The morning bathroom** — Tinka is one of the five [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]s competing for the single bathroom in the morning, a domestic comedy that establishes the crowded intimacy of the [[family|household]] (001-023).
- **"Well, kittiedoolie!"** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s morning greeting to Tinka is an eruption of pure affection, uncalculated and unperformed — rare moments of authenticity in a life otherwise given over to role-playing (002-012).
- **Breakfast negotiations** — Tinka wails and complains alongside her older siblings at the breakfast table. [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s admonition — "Careful, Tinka, your sleeve is in the butter" — captures the mundane chaos of family life (002-015, 002-016).
- **The movies** — Tinka is taken to the movies with her parents, a weekly ritual of middle-class [[vacation|leisure]]. Her preference for the pictures is noted alongside the family's other consumption habits (012-013, 012-016).
- **[[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s tenderness** — On a business trip, [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] sends Tinka a post[[automobile|car]]d: "Papa wishes you were here." The gesture is small but significant — it is in these unguarded moments with his youngest child that [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] most nearly resembles a whole person (013-032).

## Character Traits

Tinka is energetic, demanding, and completely unaffected — a child in a novel about adults who have forgotten how to be genuine. She does not represent hope or redemption in any programmatic sense; Lewis is too canny for that. But she does represent the existence of a private, domestic sphere that the public world of business and [[community|boosterism]] cannot fully colonize. [[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]] tells [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]], "You know less about morality than Tinka," and the remark lands because it is true (020-013).

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt]]** — Tinka's father; [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] dotes on her with an uncharacteristic tenderness
- **[[myra-babbitt]]** — Tinka's mother, who manages Tinka's daily [[automobile|car]]e
- **[[ted-babbitt]]** — Tinka's older brother; Ted alternates between ignoring and teasing her
