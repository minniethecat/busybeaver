# Maine

The destination of Babbitt's annual vacation with Paul Riesling — the one sanctioned escape from the pressures of Zenith. In the Maine woods, Babbitt can be "natural" in ways impossible in Floral Heights. But as he discovers, "Vacation doesn't seem to have done me a bit of good" (011-024) — the problem is not Zenith but the man who carries it within him.

## Role in the Novel

Maine serves as the novel's pastoral counterpoint to urban Zenith — a place of natural beauty and authentic connection, particularly between Babbitt and Paul. But the vacation ultimately fails to provide lasting transformation. Babbitt returns to Zenith with his "desire for rebellion partly satisfied" (013-080) but not resolved. Maine is a safety valve, not an escape route. After three weeks, he is "crankier and nervouser than when I came up here" (011-024).
