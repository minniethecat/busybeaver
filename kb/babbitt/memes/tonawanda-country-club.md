# Tonawanda Country Club

Zenith's most exclusive country club, membership in which marks the boundary between the city's commercial aristocracy and its aspiring middle class.

## Description

The Tonawanda Country Club was the more exclusive of Zenith's two country clubs, contrasted with the Golf and Country Club used by Babbitt's circle. It took its name from "the orchard-dappled Tonawanda Hills to the North" (003-031), a landscape of "fat dairy land and big barns and comfortable herds" that embodied the settled, old-money character of its membership. Its members included Charles McKelvey, Horace Updike, "and the other rich men who lunched not at the Athletic but at the Union Club" (012-011).

## The Two-Tier Country Club System

Zenith's country club system reflected the city's class structure. In Zenith, "it was as necessary for a Successful Man to belong to a country club as it was to wear a linen collar" (012-011). Babbitt himself "hustled out to his country club and hustled through nine holes of golf as a rest after the week's hustle" every Saturday (012-010). But the country club he belonged to was not the Tonawanda — it was the less exclusive Golf and Country Club. The Tonawanda occupied a higher tier entirely.

Critically, the distinction was not simply financial. Babbitt was prosperous, yet he and Myra were "not asked to join the Tonawanda Country Club nor invited to the dances at the Union" (015-004). The exclusion reflected a qualitative difference in social standing: McKelvey's inherited wealth and Ivy League connections versus Babbitt's self-made commercial success and State University pedigree.

## Social Function

The club served as a marker of social distinction in Zenith. While George Babbitt belonged to the Athletic Club and the Boosters' Club, the Tonawanda remained beyond his reach. This exclusion defines the ceiling of Babbitt's social aspirations — he can rise within the commercial middle class but cannot penetrate the inherited wealth and social prestige represented by the Tonawanda set.

The Tonawanda Hills themselves, visible from Zenith, serve as a geographical symbol of this inaccessible elite world. The Zeeco test car "slid up the Tonawanda hill on high" (009-038) — a literal climb that mirrors Babbitt's frustrated social climbing.

## Satirical Significance

The Tonawanda Country Club represents the genuine social hierarchy that Babbitt's booster ideology pretends does not exist. Despite Zenith's rhetoric of democratic opportunity and the Boosters' Club's claims of fellowship, the Tonawanda barrier demonstrates that money alone — at least Babbitt's kind of money — cannot buy entry into the highest social tier. Sinclair Lewis underscores this quietly devastating fact in a single sentence fragment: "They were not asked to join the Tonawanda Country Club nor invited to the dances at the Union" (015-004). The passive construction — they were "not asked" — emphasizes that the decision is made by others, beyond Babbitt's power to influence or buy his way in.
