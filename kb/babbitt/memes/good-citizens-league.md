# Good Citizens' League

## Description

The Good Citizens' League (GCL) is a conservative civic organization that emerges in the novel's final chapters as the enforcement mechanism for political and social orthodoxy in Zenith. The League "had spread through the country, but nowhere was it so effective and well esteemed as in cities of the type of Zenith" (034-002), functioning as a kind of civic immune system that attacks any deviation from the business-class consensus. It is organized by Vergil Gunch and supported by the city's establishment — Colonel Rutherford Snow, Charles McKelvey, and the other pillars of Zenith respectability.

## Social Function

The League enforces conformity through the threat of social and economic ostracism. Its methods are not violent but are nonetheless coercive: businessmen who refuse to join find their customers drifting away, their invitations drying up, their reputations whispered into disrepute. The League's genius is that it makes persecution look like civic responsibility — "the League was not merely sensational; it was sacred" (034-018). By wrapping its enforcement of orthodoxy in the language of patriotism and good citizenship, the League transforms bullying into virtue.

The League's targets are the usual suspects of 1920s reaction: labor organizers, socialists, pacifists, and anyone who questions the established order. But its real target is independence of mind — the mere willingness to think differently from one's neighbors. Babbitt's brief rebellion, during which he socializes with liberals and defends strikers, makes him a natural target for the League's attention.

## Key Scenes

- **Formation (Chapter XXIX)**: Vergil Gunch tells Babbitt about the newly organized Good Citizens' League, describing its purpose as combating the "reds" and agitators (029-062). Babbitt's hesitation to join immediately — "I'll have to think it over" (029-064) — marks the beginning of his social isolation.
- **The Committee Visit (Chapter XXXII)**: Colonel Snow and another League member visit Babbitt's office to pressure him to join. "We've come from the Good Citizens' League. We've decided we want you to join" (032-016) — the language of invitation barely concealing the threat beneath.
- **Capitulation (Chapter XXXII)**: Babbitt "determined to join the Good Citizens' League the next time he was asked" (032-029). The decision is presented as voluntary but is in fact the result of systematic social pressure that has made non-membership unbearable.
- **Final Absorption (Chapter XXXIV)**: By the novel's end, Babbitt has been fully reabsorbed into the League, its meetings providing the same backslapping camaraderie as the Boosters' Club, with the added frisson of ideological righteousness.

## Satirical Significance

The Good Citizens' League is Lewis's most devastating portrait of American conformism in its organized form. The League does not persecute dissenters because it is evil but because it is righteous — its members genuinely believe they are protecting their community from dangerous ideas. The irony is that the "danger" they combat is nothing more than the mildest forms of liberal dissent: Babbitt's brief friendships with Seneca Doane and Dr. Kurt Yavitch are treated as sedition. The League represents the endpoint of the booster ideology: when "good citizenship" becomes compulsory, citizenship itself becomes meaningless.
