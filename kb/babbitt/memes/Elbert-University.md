# Elbert University

Elbert University is an institution of higher education in Nebraska, mentioned in *Babbitt* as the source of the Reverend John Jennison Drew's Master of Arts and Doctor of Divinity degrees. Along with Waterbury College of Oklahoma — which granted Drew his LL.D. — Elbert represents the network of obscure credentialing institutions that furnish Zenith's professional class with the academic titles that authorize their authority (016-011).

## Role in the Novel

Elbert University functions as a single detail in Lewis's portrait of the Reverend Dr. John Jennison Drew, but its significance extends well beyond its brief mention. The university's location in Nebraska — geographically remote from Zenith, institutionally unverifiable — is the point: Drew's credentials derive their force not from any community's knowledge of Elbert but from the sheer accumulation of post-nominal letters. The novel's treatment of academic credentials anticipates a later American tradition of satire, from *The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance* ("when the legend becomes fact, print the legend") to contemporary critiques of diploma-mill culture (016-011).

## Credentialing Context

Drew's academic pedigree — Elbert for M.A. and D.D., Waterbury for LL.D. — is a list of places no reader has heard of, and the obscurity is intentional. Lewis could have sent Drew to Princeton or Yale; instead he invented Elbert and Waterbury to emphasize that Drew's authority rests on credentials no one can examine. The degrees serve the same function in Drew's career that booster slogans serve in Babbitt's: they are verbal tokens of competence that substitute for any demonstration of it (016-011).

## Key Scenes

- **Drew's introduction** — When the narrator introduces the Reverend Dr. Drew, his degrees are listed before any personal quality: "the Reverend John Jennison Drew, M.A., D.D., LL.D." The string of post-nominal letters is itself a kind of character description — and a damning one. The degrees, from Elbert and Waterbury, are presented as academic facts whose very obscurity exposes the mechanism of credentialism (016-011).

## Satirical Significance

Elbert University belongs to Lewis's larger satire of institutional authority in American life. Every institution in *Babbitt* — the [[boosters-club]], the [[chatham-road-presbyterian-church]], the [[zenith-real-estate-board]] — derives its power not from demonstrated competence but from titles, memberships, and certificates. Elbert is the academic expression of this pattern: a place whose only function in the novel is to supply the letters that follow Drew's name. That the university exists at all is a credential; that it is in Nebraska is a credential; that no one in Zenith has ever visited it is, paradoxically, also a credential — because credentials work best when they cannot be examined.
