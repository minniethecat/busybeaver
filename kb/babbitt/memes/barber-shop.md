# Barber Shop

The barber shop located in the Reeves Building, where George Babbitt receives his daily professional shave. A minor setting that carries disproportionate symbolic weight: it is the stage for one of Babbitt's most mundane yet telling rituals of middle-class masculinity.

## Description

The barber shop occupies a ground-floor space in the Reeves Building, convenient to Babbitt's real-estate office. It serves the building's professional tenants, offering the daily shave that, in 1920s Zenith, is a marker of white-collar respectability. The shop is identified by its location — "the Reeves Building Barber Shop" (024-017) — and by the barber's professional relationship with his regular customers.

## Social Function

The daily shave functions as a secular ritual: it confirms Babbitt's membership in the class of men who are shaved by others rather than shaving themselves. It is a small luxury that has become a necessity, part of the rhythm of commercial life.

The barber shop also serves as a site of masculine sociability — the kind of place where men exchange casual conversation in a space free of women. When Babbitt later refuses to patronize it, he is disrupting not just a grooming habit but a social ritual. He feels guilty about "deserting his neighbor, the Reeves Building Barber Shop" (024-017), the word "neighbor" framing the barber as a member of his community.

## Key Scenes

- **Daily shave** — Babbitt's morning visit to the barber shop is mentioned as part of his routine, a ritual as regular as his office hours (003-037)
- **The rebellion** — In the midst of his midlife crisis, Babbitt stops going to the Reeves Building Barber Shop (024-017). He tells himself "I don't own the Reeves Building! These barbers got nothing on me!" (024-018), the defiant language revealing that even this small change feels like a declaration of independence

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the barber shop to illustrate the pettiness of Babbitt's rebellion. The most dramatic act of nonconformity available to him within his daily routine is switching barbers. That this registers as a genuine transgression in Babbitt's world — that he must psych himself up for it with assertions of independence — is a measure of how thoroughly his life is governed by habit and social expectation.

The barber shop also speaks to the novel's treatment of masculinity. The professional shave is a male ritual, and abandoning it is a small crisis of identity. Babbitt's world of offices, clubs, and barber shops is entirely male; the barber shop is one node in this homosocial network.
