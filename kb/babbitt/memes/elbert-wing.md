# Elbert Wing

Elbert Wing is a Zenith realtor, one of the five official delegates to the State Association of Real Estate Boards convention in Monarch. Known to his fellow delegates as "El Wing," he appears more vividly than most of the convention cast: Lewis gives him a distinctive professional anecdote and a cameo in the hotel-room drinking scene that captures the convention's ritualized masculine release.

## Role in the Novel

Wing is introduced among the five official delegates — "Babbitt, Rountree, W. A. Rogers, Alvin Thayer, and Elbert Wing" (013-018) — and, unlike Thayer, receives two moments of individuation. On the train to Monarch, Wing tells a story about his marketing strategy for a suburban development: "I hired this shop-window for a week, and put up a big sign, 'Toy Town for Tiny Tots,' and stuck in a lot of doll houses and some dinky little trees, and then down at the bottom, 'Baby Likes This Dollydale, but Papa and Mama Will Prefer Our Beautiful Bungalows,' and you know, that certainly got folks talking, and first week we sold—" (013-027). The anecdote is interrupted by the noise of passing factories, but its content is revealing: Wing's business is a form of theater, marketing suburban houses with the same techniques used to sell toys in shop windows.

Later, at the Hotel Sedgwick in Monarch, Wing's wife has conveniently "gone on to Pittsburg," leaving him available for the convention's real business: drinking, story-telling, and male bonding in the hotel room. "At half-past seven they sat in their room, with Elbert Wing and two up-state delegates. Their coats were off, their vests open, their faces red, their voices emphatic. They were finishing a bottle of corrosive bootlegged whisky" (013-059). The scene — men "in a happy state of nature" — is the convention stripped of its official pretense. Wing is part of this ritual of masculine release, the temporary suspension of domestic constraint that the convention licenses.

## Key Scenes

- **The train to Monarch** — Wing tells his Toy Town marketing anecdote as the train passes through the factory district. The juxtaposition of Wing's marketing patter ("Baby Likes This Dollydale") with the industrial landscape of "furnaces spurting flame" is characteristic Lewis: the booster's world of cheerful commercial fantasy set against the grim physical reality of industrial production (013-027).

- **The hotel-room drinking scene** — Wing, his wife conveniently absent, joins Babbitt, Rogers, and two up-state delegates for an evening of bootleg whisky, cigar-smoking, and story-telling. "They were, in fact, males in a happy state of nature" (013-059). The phrase is Lewis at his most satirically anthropological: the convention men, stripped of wives and offices, revert to a pre-civilized condition.

- **W. A. Rogers's performance** — During the dinner scene, Wing admires Rogers's comic bullying of the Italian waiter: "Gee, you certainly did have that poor Dago going, W. A. He couldn't make you out at all!" (013-065). Wing's response reflects the convention's fraternal ethic: the humor that binds the group together is humor at the expense of outsiders.

## Character Traits

Wing is a practical businessman — his Toy Town story reveals a marketing imagination more playful than Babbitt's earnest booster prose — and a reliable participant in the convention's rituals of masculine camaraderie. The nickname "El Wing" suggests easy familiarity within the group. His wife's absence from the convention ("El Wing's wife has gone on to Pittsburg") hints at a conjugal arrangement that accommodates, or at least does not obstruct, the convention's licensed male revelry.
