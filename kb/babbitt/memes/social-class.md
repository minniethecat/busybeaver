# Social Class

The unspoken but rigidly enforced hierarchy of [[zenith]] society. Babbitt's class prides itself on American egalitarianism while maintaining an intricate and punishing class system — a system that determines where you live, whom you marry, which clubs admit you, and what dignity you are permitted. The novel meticulously maps the gradations of status while its characters loudly insist that class does not exist in America. This contradiction — between the ideology of classlessness and the reality of hierarchy — is one of Lewis's sharpest satirical insights.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The McKelveys as aristocracy** — The McKelveys represent the upper tier of Zenith society that Babbitt can never quite reach. Their dinner parties are the ultimate social prize; Babbitt's exclusion from them is a wound that never fully heals. The McKelveys embody the paradox of American aristocracy: they have no title, no lineage, no official status — only money and the social power it confers.

- **The Eathorne Mansion** — The mansion, "reminiscent of London, Back Bay, Rittenhouse Square" (017-006), embodies old money and inherited status in a city that claims to value only hustle and achievement. Its very architecture is a rebuke to the booster myth of self-making.

- **Floral Heights as middle-class achievement** — Babbitt's neighborhood represents the successful middle tier: comfortable, respectable, standard, and anxiously aware of those above and below. The Babbitts' house is "standard, advertised wares" (001-032) — class status expressed through consumer goods.

- **Ed Overbrook as class anxiety** — Babbitt's relationship with the struggling Overbrook family reveals his own class insecurity. He avoids them because they remind him of the economic precarity that lies just beneath his own respectability.

- **The strikers as invisible class** — The working-class strikers of Chapter XXVII appear in the novel only as a threat to social order, never as individuals with their own lives and claims. Their invisibility is Lewis's indictment of his own protagonist's moral blind spot.

## Key Passages

> Most of the castles of the testy Victorian tetrarchs are gone now or decayed into boarding-houses, but the Eathorne Mansion remains virtuous and aloof, reminiscent of London, Back Bay, Rittenhouse Square. (017-006)

## Satirical Function

Lewis's treatment of social class is among his most penetrating satire. The Babbitt class's insistence that America is classless, even as they police every boundary of status with anxious precision, exposes the central hypocrisy of the American meritocratic myth. The McKelveys are aristocrats in a country that has abolished aristocracy; the workers are invisible in a democracy that claims to represent them. Lewis suggests that class in America is more insidious than in Europe precisely because it is denied — a hierarchy that dare not speak its name, and therefore cannot be questioned. Babbitt's brief sympathy for the strikers is the novel's one gesture toward class consciousness — and it is swiftly punished by the very class system whose existence everyone denies.
