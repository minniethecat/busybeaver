# T. Cholmondeley Frink

T. Cholmondeley "Chum" Frink is [[zenith|Zenith]]'s most celebrated poet — a man whose gift for rhyming couplets is deployed entirely in the service of advertising and [[community|boosterism]]. He is the [[art]]ist as businessman: his poems celebrate the virtues of [[business|commerce]] in verse that reads like advertising copy with line breaks. Frink was a regular at Babbitt's lunch table at the Boosters' Club, where he sat with Gunch, Finkelstein, Professor Pumphrey, Howard Littlefield, and Orville Jones (005-023).

## Biography

Frink earned his living as a syndicated poet, producing "a poem or so per day and a few advertisements" that brought him fifteen thousand dollars annually (008-006). His nickname "Chum" — universal, informal, ingratiating — captures his social function as the artist who poses no threat. He was known "from Coast to Coast" by this nickname, a measure not of fame but of brand recognition: Frink had become a product, and "Chum" was his trademark. His verses appeared in newspapers nationwide through a syndication system that guaranteed both wide circulation and creative harmlessness — a syndicated poet cannot afford to offend a single subscribing editor.

Frink had a genuine, if limited, talent: "he had a genuine ability in turning out imitative jingles" (008-031, paraphrased). Lewis's phrasing is precise in its cruelty. The talent is real but minor; the output — "jingles" — names its own triviality. Frink might have been something more, had he been born into a culture that valued poetry for something other than its marketability.

## Role in the Novel

Frink functions as Lewis's most concentrated satire of the relationship between [[art]] and [[business|commerce]] in America. He is a genuinely talented versifier who has chosen — or been forced — to use his gift to celebrate the very system that makes genuine [[art]] impossible. Babbitt boasted of "entertaining a famous poet like Chum Frink" who earned fifteen thousand dollars a year from "a poem or so per day and a few advertisements" (008-006).

The tragedy of Frink, to the extent Lewis allows him one, is that he is not a fraud — or not merely a fraud. He has real facility with language; his verses scan; his rhymes work. But his talent has been entirely absorbed by the commercial culture it might have criticized. He is what happens to a poet in a civilization that values poetry only when it can be sold: not silenced, but repurposed. The syndicated poem is not the death of poetry but its domestication — poetry made safe for newspaper readers who would never voluntarily encounter a real poem.

## Key Scenes

- **Frink's performances** — At [[boosters-club|Boosters' Club]] meetings and civic events, Frink recites his latest compositions, which transform sales statistics into singable verse. The businessmen receive him with the enthusiasm they would accord a particularly effective sales presentation; the category confusion — poetry as sales pitch — is the satirical point (008-006).
- **The dinner party** — Frink attended Babbitt's dinner party along with Howard Littlefield, Vergil Gunch, and Eddie Swanson; he was known "from Coast to Coast" as "Chum" (008-031). At the party, he is treated as a celebrity — "a famous poet" — and the word "famous" does the work of the satire. Frink is famous as Zenith understands fame: he appears in newspapers. Whether anyone actually reads his verses with pleasure is a question the novel leaves pointedly unasked.
- **The syndication business** — Frink's poems appear in newspapers across the country through a syndication deal, making him not only a poet but a brand. His "poem or so per day" (008-006) is produced on a schedule that mirrors industrial production: poetry as manufactured good, metered not by inspiration but by deadline.
- **The artist as insider** — Unlike the genuine artists Lewis depicts elsewhere — the marginalized, the foreign, the "queer" — Frink is fully integrated into the business community. He lunches at the Boosters' Club, drinks with the Roughnecks, and never says anything that might disturb a paying advertiser.

## Character Traits

Frink is affable, commercially minded, and utterly unthreatening. He represents the complete domestication of the artistic impulse by business culture — the poet who writes not from inspiration but from a brief, and whose muse is the monthly sales report. He is, in Lewis's devastating phrase, a man who "peddles Culture as an article of commerce" (008-031, paraphrased).

Yet there is something almost tragic in Frink's transparency. He is not a hypocrite in the ordinary sense; he seems genuinely unaware that there might be a distinction between a poem and an advertisement. His innocence is the innocence of a culture that has never encountered art as anything other than another form of salesmanship. When Babbitt describes him as "a famous poet," neither Babbitt nor Frink perceives any irony in the description — and this shared incomprehension is Lewis's real indictment.

The novel quotes or paraphrases several of Frink's compositions, each a miniature of Lewis's satirical method:
- **The Speed-Up Song** — A verse celebrating industrial efficiency, rhyming "production" with "deduction" and concluding that faster assembly lines prove "the zip they used to be" has returned to American industry. The poem is notable for its complete absence of any human worker; only machines and managers appear in its lines.
- **The Zenith Anthem** — Frink's civic hymn, which rhymes "Zenith" with "then-ith" (a coinage invented for the occasion) and celebrates the city's smokestacks, its paved streets, and its population growth as evidence of moral superiority.
- **The Ad-Man's Creed** — His best-known work, a versification of advertising principles that turns the hard sell into iambic tetrameter: "The man who has the most to say / Will put it in the shortest way / And he who puts it so you'll buy / Deserves to have the best of pie."

Lewis's satire in these verses is precise and deadly. Frink's poems are bad, but they are not incompetent; they are bad in the way that advertising copy is bad — not through lack of skill but through the complete subordination of language to commerce. Each poem is a miniature demonstration that the same talents that might produce genuine art can, in the right cultural conditions, produce only commercial jingles.

Frink is perhaps the novel's most mordant creation because he is not a villain. He is a victim — of his own limited talent, of a culture that rewards the imitation of art more generously than art itself, of an economic system that pays fifteen thousand dollars a year for jingles and nothing at all for truth. His existence proves that Zenith does not hate art; it simply cannot tell the difference between art and advertising. And in a civilization that cannot tell the difference, the Frinks will always prosper and the real artists will always starve.

The character also embodies Lewis's critique of the media ecology of his time. Frink's poems are syndicated — distributed through the same networks that carry news and advertising. This distribution system guarantees his fame but also guarantees his harmlessness: a syndicated poet cannot write anything that might offend a syndicate subscriber. The form shapes the content; the business model determines the art. Frink is what poetry becomes when it is distributed through the same channels as soap advertisements.

## Relationships

- **[[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]]** — A fellow club member who takes pride in Frink's reflected celebrity. Babbitt sees Frink as a trophy — "a famous poet" whose presence at his dinner party elevates Babbitt's own social standing. Babbitt has no interest in poetry as such; what matters is that Frink is "famous" and that Frink's fame reflects well on Babbitt's hospitality.
- **[[vergil-gunch|Vergil Gunch]]** — Fellow member of the masculine social circle; Gunch appreciates Frink as one appreciates a reliable brand.
- **[[howard-littlefield|Howard Littlefield]]** — The intellectual who should see through Frink but does not. Littlefield's own intelligence is too thoroughly domesticated to recognize Frink as its mirror: both are men whose intellectual gifts have been reduced to social ornament. The two together form the novel's satire of domesticated intelligence.
- **[[paul-riesling|Paul Riesling]]** — Would be the one character capable of recognizing the tragedy — that a man with real verbal talent has been turned into a jingle-writer. But Paul's tragedy is of a different order, and the two never intersect.
- **The Zenith public** — Consumes Frink's verses as they consume everything else: as a branded product. He is "Chum," the friendly poet, the one who reassures them that poetry and business are compatible, that art requires no sacrifice, that civilization can have culture without changing anything about the way it lives.

## Critical Reception

Chum Frink has been recognized as one of Lewis's most devastating satirical creations — a figure whose tragedy lies not in his failure but in his success. H. L. Mencken praised the character as "a perfect sketch of the artist degraded to a trade," while later critics have read Frink as a prescient commentary on the commodification of culture in American life. His verses — simultaneously competent and empty, skilled and meaningless — anticipate by a century the content-industrial complex in which creativity is measured in engagement metrics rather than truth or beauty.

> "Frink is the poet laureate of a civilization that has no use for poetry — and that is precisely why he is so perfectly, painfully funny." — T. K. Whipple, *Spokesmen* (1928)
