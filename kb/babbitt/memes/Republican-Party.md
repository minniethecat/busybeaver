# Republican Party

The Republican Party is the political organization that dominates the worldview of George Babbitt and his fellow Zenith businessmen. In Babbitt's America, the party functions not as a forum for political deliberation but as a fixed institution that dictates opinions on national issues—from disarmament to tariff policy to foreign relations. The party represents the political dimension of the all-encompassing conformity that Lewis critiques throughout the novel.

## Description

The Republican Party is listed among the "eternal" verities of Babbitt's universe, placed alongside baseball as a fundamental, unquestionable institution (PN 001-040). This casual equivalence captures the unthinking reverence with which Babbitt regards the party—it is part of the natural order, not a choice open to debate. The pairing with baseball is significant: both are treated as fixtures of American identity that a "regular fellow" simply accepts, not subjects for intellectual examination.

The party's actual operation is far removed from democratic participation. Party senators "in little smoky rooms in Washington" decide what Babbitt and his class should think about disarmament, tariff, and Germany (PN 007-025). The imagery of back-room dealings suggests a political process that is opaque, undemocratic, and controlled by insiders rather than constituents. The specific issues listed—disarmament, tariff policy, and Germany—reflect the post-World War I political landscape of the early 1920s, when debates over international engagement, protectionism, and treatment of the defeated Central Powers dominated Republican politics.

## Political Positions (as received by Babbitt)

The novel presents party positions not as Babbitt's reasoned conclusions but as received opinions handed down from party leadership. The senators in their "little smoky rooms" determine the correct stance on:

- **Disarmament** — reflecting post-WWI debates about military reduction and international treaties
- **Tariff** — the protectionist trade policy favored by business-aligned Republicans
- **Germany** — the question of how to treat the defeated power, entangled with both foreign policy and domestic political considerations (PN 007-025)

In each case, Babbitt's opinion is not his own but is transmitted to him through party channels and reinforced by his social environment. He does not arrive at political views through study or deliberation; he absorbs them as part of the package of respectable middle-class identity.

## Social Function

The Republican Party serves as one component of the interlocking system of institutions that determine Babbitt's identity. Lewis places it in a chain of determiners: the Elks, the Boosters' Club, the Chamber of Commerce, the Presbyterian Church, the Republican Party, and national advertisers—each shaping a different facet of Babbitt's beliefs and behavior from without, leaving nothing to his supposed "individuality" (PN 007-025).

This institutional chain is carefully ordered by Lewis. The fraternal order (Elks) provides belonging; the business club (Boosters) provides networking; the Chamber of Commerce provides economic ideology; the church provides moral sanction; the Republican Party provides political identity; and national advertisers provide desire itself. Together they constitute a complete system for manufacturing the standardized American businessman, leaving no dimension of life—social, economic, moral, political, or psychological—to individual choice.

## Key Scenes

The Republican Party is invoked at two critical moments in Babbitt's characterization. In the novel's opening chapter, it appears in the list of "eternal" things that Babbitt holds sacred but cannot articulate—alongside his family, his business, and baseball (PN 001-040). In Chapter 7, during a more politically charged passage, Lewis connects the party directly to the mechanism of manufactured opinion: senators in smoky rooms determine what the "regular fellow" thinks (PN 007-025). The party never appears as an active institution with meetings or debates; it is present only as a received identity, a pre-fabricated set of opinions that Babbitt wears like a ready-made suit.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis's treatment of the Republican Party is a pointed critique of how political allegiance in middle-class America operates as a form of social conformity rather than genuine political engagement. The party is not something Babbitt chooses—it is something that shapes him. The "little smoky rooms in Washington" evoke Progressive-era critiques of machine politics and the influence of big business on government. The juxtaposition of political authority with commercial advertising in the same sentence underscores Lewis's argument that Babbitt's entire consciousness is manufactured by external forces.

The satirical force of the passage lies in the absence of any mention of policy substance. The party's positions on disarmament, tariff, and Germany are not presented as arguments but as edicts. Babbitt does not support the Republican Party because he has evaluated its platform; he supports it because a "regular fellow" does, just as a "regular fellow" roots for the home team. Political identity in Babbitt's world is not a conclusion but a premise.
