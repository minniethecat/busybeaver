# Max Kruger

Max Kruger is a Zenith banker and a member of the exclusive social set centered on [[charles-mckelvey|Charles McKelvey]]. Along with McKelvey and [[irving-tate|Irving Tate]] the tool-manufacturer, Kruger belongs to the tiny circle of Zenith men whose social position places them above even the most prosperous Boosters. He appears at the McKelvey dinner party that represents Babbitt's closest approach to — and most humiliating exclusion from — the city's true elite.

## Role in the Novel

Kruger functions as a marker of the social boundary that Babbitt cannot cross. He is listed among "such social leaders as Charles McKelvey the millionaire contractor, Max Kruger the banker, Irving Tate the tool-manufacturer, and Adelbert Dobson the fashionable interior decorator" (015-004) — men whose names define the limit of Babbitt's social universe. At the McKelvey dinner, Kruger's conversation with McKelvey about hosting Sir Gerald Doak and the "jungle dance" for which Mona Dodsworth had made costumes marks an inner circle from which Babbitt, despite being physically present at the same dinner, remains excluded.

## Key Scenes

- **The McKelvey dinner party** — Kruger is among the select guests at McKelvey's dinner for Sir Gerald Doak. Babbitt listens as McKelvey says to Kruger, "Yes, we'll put up Sir Gerald Doak" (015-015) — a display of casual intimacy with visiting British nobility that both thrills and excludes Babbitt, whose "democratic love for titles became a rich relish." Later, Kruger and McKelvey discuss a "jungle dance" for which Mona Dodsworth had designed costumes (015-017), their conversation a closed circuit of elite social planning.

## Character Traits

Lewis sketches Kruger with minimal detail — a name and an occupation — because his function in the novel requires no further elaboration. He is "the banker," as McKelvey is "the contractor" and Tate is "the tool-manufacturer." The occupations are metonymic: each man's identity is inseparable from his economic power. Kruger's presence in a scene signals that serious money — not merely comfortable prosperity — is in the room.

## The Banker as Social Type

Kruger belongs to a recognizable type in the social taxonomy of Babbitt: the banker as social arbiter. In the economic hierarchy of Zenith, the banker occupies a position above the manufacturer, the contractor, and (certainly) the realtor. Where Babbitt's prosperity depends on the transaction-by-transaction labor of selling property, Kruger's wealth is self-perpetuating — money generating money, independent of the grubby details of individual deals. This structural difference in economic position translates directly into social rank: Kruger belongs to the McKelvey set not because he has earned his way in through effort but because his economic position places him there automatically.

The "jungle dance" conversation between Kruger and McKelvey (015-017) is a revealing moment. The two men discuss an event at which Mona Dodsworth — wife of the "unusually sensitive" architect Adelbert Dobson — had created costumes. The conversation is notable not for its content but for its structure: it is a closed circuit, an exchange between two men who share assumptions, experiences, and a social vocabulary that excludes Babbitt entirely. Kruger does not need to explain himself to McKelvey because McKelvey already understands; Babbitt, listening, understands nothing and belongs nowhere.

## Satirical Significance

Kruger's function in the novel is primarily structural: he is a marker on the social map that Babbitt can read but cannot navigate. Lewis deploys him as a sign — a sign that there exists a social territory beyond the Boosters' Club, beyond the Athletic Club, beyond every institution in which Babbitt feels himself a leader. The fact that Kruger is a banker rather than, say, a manufacturer or merchant is important: banking is the most abstract form of wealth, the furthest removed from the material production that occupies most of Zenith's citizens. Kruger's money has no smell of the factory floor; it is clean, paper, and infinitely renewable — the pure form of the social power that Babbitt can only approximate.
