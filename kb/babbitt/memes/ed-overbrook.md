# Ed Overbrook

Ed Overbrook is George Babbitt's college classmate whose business has failed. He had a large family and a feeble insurance business out in the suburb of Dorchester (015-049). When Overbrook invites the Babbitts to dinner, Babbitt makes excuses rather than associate with failure. Overbrook represents the dark side of the success cult — the man who did not make it, and whose very existence threatens the narrative of meritocratic triumph.

## Biography

Ed Overbrook is Babbitt's college classmate of the class of 1896, now living in Dorchester with a large family and running a feeble insurance business (015-049). He is the negative of Babbitt's self-portrait — the man Babbitt could have been, the man Babbitt fears he still might become. In a novel where address is identity — Floral Heights, Royal Ridge, the Athletic Club on Vernon Street — to live in Dorchester is to have already failed the only test that Zenith recognizes.

## Role in the Novel

Overbrook appears in only a handful of pages, but his presence haunts the novel's second half. He is one of a series of figures — along with Paul Riesling, Seneca Doane, and the briefly-glimpsed working-class characters — who represent alternatives to the booster life. Unlike Paul, who is Babbitt's intimate, or Doane, who is his political antagonist, Overbrook is simply a mirror: a man of Babbitt's own generation, his own background, his own profession, who took the same risks and made the same bets and lost. His failure is the argument against the system that Babbitt cannot afford to hear.

The Overbrook episode also introduces a theme that Lewis develops more fully in Babbitt's later rebellion: the discovery that the booster creed offers no resources for genuine human connection. Overbrook reaches out to Babbitt in the language of friendship — "I hate to think of how we been drifting apart" — and Babbitt can only respond in the language of social obligation. The failure is not Overbrook's but Babbitt's: he has become a man incapable of receiving an honest gesture (015-051).

## Key Scenes

- **The Class Reunion** — Babbitt saw Overbrook in a shiny blue serge business-suit, being diffident in a corner with three other failures. He went over and was cordial out of obligation (015-050). The reunion scene is a taxonomy of success and failure, a living graph of who rose and who sank in the twenty years since graduation. Overbrook's "shiny blue serge" suit — the fabric polished by wear into a telltale gleam — is a visual indictment more damning than any balance sheet. The three other failures with whom Overbrook shares the corner are never named; they are a silent chorus, a reminder that for every Babbitt who rose there were several who sank.

- **The Dinner Invitation** — Overbrook embarrassed Babbitt by blurting, "Say, Georgie, I hate to think of how we been drifting apart. I wish you and Mrs. Babbitt would come to dinner some night" (015-051). Babbitt reluctantly accepted but groaned to his wife, "Might as well go and get it over," amazed that Overbrook didn't know "the first thing about social etiquette" — phoning instead of having his wife write a proper invitation (015-052). The invitation scene is structured as a double failure of communication: Overbrook, speaking from genuine loneliness and affection, transgresses the social code; Babbitt, speaking from the code he has internalized as morality, cannot hear the affection for the breach.

## Character Traits

Overbrook functions as a brief but piercing reminder of what Babbitt fears most: failure. Babbitt's refusal to dine with the Overbrooks exposes the cruelty beneath the booster ideology — in a culture that measures worth by success, failure is contagious. To sit at Overbrook's table would be to risk infection by association. The booster creed has no category for the honest failure, the unlucky man, the man who tried and fell short (015-049).

The contagion metaphor is operative at every level of Babbitt's response. He does not merely pity Overbrook; he fears him. "Poor old Ed" — the adjective is automatic, the diminutive name-form a reflex of condescension — represents the terrifying possibility that the difference between Floral Heights and Dorchester is thinner than the booster creed allows. Babbitt's own business depends on connections, on confidence, on the belief of other solid citizens that he is one of them.

Babbitt briefly feels shame at his treatment of Overbrook, but the shame is not strong enough to change his behavior. The conscience flickers and dies, smothered by the far more powerful instinct for social self-preservation. Babbitt does not rationalize his behavior — he simply stops thinking about it. The guilt is not argued away but allowed to subside, like a physical discomfort that fades when you stop attending to it. This is perhaps the most damning detail in Lewis's portrait: Babbitt's conscience is not overridden by competing principles but by the sheer inertia of his social habits.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis's insight is that in a culture organized around the worship of success, failure is not merely unfortunate — it is morally suspect. The failed man must have done something wrong, must bear some hidden defect of character or judgment, because the alternative — that the system is arbitrary, that success and virtue are uncorrelated — would unravel the entire ideology. Overbrook's existence is thus not merely sad but threatening: he is walking evidence that the booster creed is a lie.

Dorchester, where Overbrook lives, is the geography of this failure — a suburb that the novel mentions only in connection with him (015-049). It is the shadow of Floral Heights, where the lawns are smaller, the houses shabbier, the commutes longer. The geography of the novel is a map of moral judgment, and Dorchester is where they send the failures. It is also the destination of the downwardly mobile: the Street Traction Company planned to build its car-repair shops there (019-002), an industrial encroachment that would never have been proposed for Floral Heights or Royal Ridge.

The Overbrook episode illuminates Babbitt's relationship with his wife as well. He groans to Myra about the invitation, expecting her to share his annoyance, and she does — not because she is cruel but because she has been trained to share her husband's social judgments. The Babbitts are a unit in their refusal of the Overbrooks, the marriage functioning as it so often does in the novel: as a conspiracy of mutual reinforcement against any value that threatens the established order.
