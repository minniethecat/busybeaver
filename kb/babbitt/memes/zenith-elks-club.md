# Zenith Elks Club

The Zenith chapter of the Benevolent and Protective Order of Elks, a fraternal organization whose membership functions as a barometer of civic standing in Zenith — belonging to the Elks signals that one is a "Regular Fellow," while exclusion marks one as an outsider.

## Description

The Elks are one of the prominent fraternal organizations in Zenith, referenced throughout the novel as a key institution in the city's social architecture. Vergil Gunch, the coal-dealer, serves as Esteemed Leading Knight and is rumored to be a candidate for Exalted Ruler (005-016). The organization is mentioned alongside other lodges — the Moose, Red Men, Knights of Columbus, Masons, Oddfellows — as part of the dense network of fraternal societies that define "decent" middle-class masculinity in Zenith (014-034).

Babbitt's relationship with the Elks tracks his arc through the novel. In his conformist phase, he "went more regularly to the Elks" and saw himself "as a Prominent Citizen" (016-002). During his rebellion, he "still made pretense of going to the Elks and to committee-meetings but less and less did he trouble to have his excuses interesting" (030-006). By the novel's end, he returns to "the pleasures of his home and of the Athletic Club, the Boosters, the Elks" (034-018).

## Social Function

The Elks serve as both social venue and status marker. Membership — or its absence — defines social boundaries: "Neither Angus nor Maxwell belonged to the Elks or to the Athletic Club; neither of them had ever called Babbitt 'brother' or asked his opinions on carburetors" (015-026). The "brother" address encapsulates the Elks' conflation of fraternal ritual with business camaraderie.

The Elks also serve as a cover story for Babbitt's affair — "on evenings when he was believed to be at the Elks" (029-011) he visited Tanis Judique. This instrumental use of the organization as alibi captures Lewis's irony: the fraternal lodge supposedly devoted to "brotherly" virtue becomes the shield for adultery (030-006).

At the state real-estate convention, the Pioneer delegates carry an enormous banner reading "B. P. O. E.--Best People on Earth--Boost Pioneer, Oh Eddie" (013-039), revealing how fraternal identity merges with boosterism — the Elks and civic promotion are practically the same religion.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's evening return** — "When he returned from the Elks he peered coyly into the living-room" (018-004), a moment that captures the Elks as the excuse-giving institution of masculine evening absences
- **The Pioneer delegation banner** — At the state convention, the Pioneer Elks unfurl a banner declaring "B. P. O. E.--Best People on Earth" (013-039), satirizing fraternal boosterism as competitive civic display

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Elks to expose the conflation of fraternal ritual, business networking, and moral self-congratulation in American middle-class life. The "brotherly" rhetoric masks a system of commercial favor-trading and social gatekeeping. The organization's name — invoking the noble elk while serving the mundane purposes of lunch-club boosterism — embodies the novel's satire of pretension. Babbitt's use of the Elks as an alibi for adultery (029-011)(030-006) completes the irony: the fraternal order of "protection" becomes the cover for moral transgression.
