# Real Estate

Real estate in *Babbitt* is far more than a profession — it is the novel's master metaphor for American civilization itself. Lewis, who worked briefly as a newspaper reporter in Zenith-like cities and whose father was a doctor in a small Midwestern town, understood that the realtor was the archetypal figure of 1920s America: part salesman, part visionary, part con man, and wholly a believer in the gospel of growth.

## Definition in Context

For George Babbitt, real estate is not a job but an identity. The office is his "pirate ship" (003-002), and the deal is his theater of masculine competence. Lewis uses the real-estate transaction as a lens through which to examine the moral universe of American capitalism: a world in which "Vision" means bullying a partner into buying a lot above appraisal value (004-034), and in which a "good prospect" is someone vulnerable enough to be sold something they do not need at a price they cannot afford. Babbitt is "conventionally honest" (004-021) — honest enough to keep good records, not honest enough to refuse twice the value of a house from a foolish buyer.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The office as identity** — Babbitt's sense of self is inseparable from his professional role. "To George F. Babbitt, as to most prosperous citizens of Zenith, his motor-car was poetry and tragedy, love and heroism. The office was his pirate ship but the car his shore" (003-002). The office is where he feels powerful and competent, in contrast to the domestic space where he is perpetually inadequate.
- **The Glen Oriole development** — Babbitt's sales pitch for this "high-class restricted development" (004-005) reveals the intersection of real estate and social aspiration: houses are sold not for their physical qualities but for the social status they confer through exclusion
- **The Lyte-Purdy squeeze** — The novel's most detailed business episode, in which Babbitt and Conrad Lyte conspire to force Archibald Purdy to pay an inflated price for a piece of property (004-034). Babbitt frames this as shrewd business; Lewis frames it as legalized predation
- **The form-letter as literature** — Babbitt's fortnightly form-letter to "prospects" (003-055) is his creative output, the purple prose through which he transforms transactions into destiny. It is simultaneously absurd and poignant: this is the closest Babbitt comes to art
- **The National Real Estate Board convention** — Chapters XIII–XIV follow Babbitt to the annual convention in Monarch, where real estate becomes indistinguishable from religion, patriotism, and masculine bonding — a civic liturgy complete with speeches, banquets, and ritualized deal-making

## Key Passages

> To George F. Babbitt, as to most prosperous citizens of Zenith, his motor-car was poetry and tragedy, love and heroism. The office was his pirate ship but the car his shore. (003-002)

> He was conventionally honest, he kept his records of buyers and sellers complete, he had experience with leases and titles and an excellent memory for prices. (004-021)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses real estate to expose the moral architecture of American capitalism: a system in which being "conventionally honest" — following the letter of the law while ignoring its spirit — is considered virtuous. Babbitt is not a villain; he is a man who has never been asked to examine the ethics of his profession because his entire society is organized to prevent such examination. The novel suggests that real estate is the quintessential American business because it sells not buildings but dreams — and the dream, in Zenith, is always of the next deal, the next development, the next rung on a ladder that never stops moving upward.
