# The Good Citizens' League Formation

The formation of the Good Citizens' League in [[zenith]] — the novel's most explicit portrait of booster ideology transformed into an apparatus of political enforcement. Ostensibly organized to promote "good citizenship," the League in practice polices the boundaries of acceptable opinion among Zenith's business class, targeting "radicals," "soreheads," and anyone whose views deviate from the booster orthodoxy. It is the institutional form of the "belligerent conformity" (034-029) that defines Zenith's public life.

## Background

The League emerges in the novel's later chapters, as the political and economic context of the early 1920s — strikes, labor unrest, the Red Scare — provides a pretext for the organization of business-class anxiety into formal political power. Babbitt, initially skeptical, is eventually pressured into joining, and his brief period of resistance to the League's demands becomes one of the triggers for his social ostracism.

## Key Events

- **The League's founding** — Organized by Zenith's business leadership as a response to perceived threats to the social order.
- **Recruitment** — Members of the [[boosters-club]] and other business organizations are pressured to join; membership becomes a test of loyalty.
- **Babbitt's resistance** — During his [[babbitts-rebellion|rebellion]], Babbitt initially refuses to join, criticizing the League as a "bunch of bullies."
- **Babbitt's capitulation** — Under the combined pressure of business decline and social ostracism, Babbitt eventually joins the League — the final act of his retreat from rebellion.

## The League as Enforcement Mechanism

The Good Citizens' League is Lewis's portrayal of how civic boosterism, stripped of its genial surface, functions as social control. The League does not need to use force — it uses membership. To be outside the League is to be outside the community of respectable business opinion, and that exclusion carries economic as well as social consequences. The League is the logical endpoint of the booster ideology: community reduced to conformity, citizenship reduced to compliance.

## Consequences

The League's formation marks the novel's turn from satire of booster culture to critique of its political function. What begins as fellowship ends as enforcement. Babbitt's eventual membership in the League — his surrender to the demand for conformity — is the final, definitive end of his rebellion. "The last scar of his rebellion was healed" (034-029) when he accepts what the League represents: that in Zenith, belonging requires submission.
