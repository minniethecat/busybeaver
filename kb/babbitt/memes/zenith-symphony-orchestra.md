# Zenith Symphony Orchestra

A proposed symphony orchestra for the city of Zenith, advocated by George Babbitt as a marker of civic culture and urban prestige comparable to Minneapolis and Cincinnati. The proposal, though never realized within the narrative, serves as a concentrated expression of the booster spirit that defines Babbitt's public life.

## Description

The Zenith Symphony Orchestra was proposed by George Babbitt during a business meeting as a civic cultural initiative. Babbitt framed the orchestra not primarily as an artistic endeavor but as a competitive asset — a way for Zenith to gain the kind of cultural credit that other Midwestern cities already enjoyed. He urged his colleagues "to O.K. the proposition of a Symphony Orchestra for Zenith" (021-017), presenting the matter as a routine item of civic business, no different in kind from approving a new sewer bond or a street-paving contract.

Babbitt's rhetorical strategy is entirely instrumental. He argued that supporting a symphony would demonstrate that Zenith was not merely a commercial center but a city of culture and refinement, explicitly citing Minneapolis and Cincinnati as cities that benefited from having "first-class musickers and a swell conductor" (021-020). The phrasing is revealing: "musickers" — a Babbitt coinage that reduces professional musicians to a vaguely comic category — and "swell conductor," a term borrowed from the vocabulary of social approbation rather than musical discernment. Babbitt's language betrays him; he cannot describe the orchestra in aesthetic terms because he has no aesthetic vocabulary.

His peroration distills the booster ethos to its essence: "whoop it up for Culture and a World-beating Symphony Orchestra!" (021-021). "Culture" with a capital C — an abstraction to be advertised, not experienced. "World-beating" — the competitive frame that reduces every human activity to a contest Zenith must win. The businessmen applaud, not because they care about music but because they recognize the rhetoric of civic promotion and respond to it as they would to a sales pitch.

## Social Function

The proposal functions in the novel as a quintessential example of Zenith boosterism — the conflation of culture with civic promotion. Babbitt's appeal is couched entirely in the language of competitive status. The businessmen applaud not because they care about music but because the orchestra represents another metric by which Zenith can claim superiority over its rivals. The scene satirizes the reduction of art to a promotional slogan.

The deeper function of the proposal is to reveal the poverty of Babbitt's imaginative life. He can conceive of a symphony orchestra — he knows that cities have them, that they are markers of metropolitan status — but he cannot conceive of why anyone would actually want to listen to one. The gap between knowing that culture is important and understanding what culture _is_ constitutes the central tragedy of Babbitt's character, and the symphony proposal crystallizes it in a single, brief scene.

The proposal also illuminates the economics of civic culture in Zenith. The orchestra requires "the proposition" to be "O.K.'d" (021-017) — a vote of approval that presumably carries a financial commitment. Culture, in Babbitt's world, is something you fund, not something you make. The businessmen at the meeting will never play in the orchestra, never attend its concerts with genuine attention, never allow the music to disturb their settled convictions. They will simply pay for it and claim credit for having it, as they would for a new factory or a larger convention hall.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the symphony orchestra proposal to expose the hollow instrumentalism of Zenith's civic culture. In Babbitt's rhetoric, a symphony is indistinguishable from a new factory or a larger convention hall — merely another piece of evidence that Zenith is "world-beating." The satire lies in the absolute absence of any genuine interest in music; culture is valued solely for its promotional utility (021-017) (021-021).

The scene also functions as a character study in miniature. Babbitt's inability to speak about music in musical terms — his reliance on the vocabulary of salesmanship and civic rivalry — reveals the limits of his inner life more efficiently than any extended psychological analysis could. He is not a hypocrite in the usual sense; he genuinely believes that supporting a symphony orchestra is a good thing to do. He simply cannot say why, because the category of "good in itself" does not exist in his mental framework. Everything must be good _for_ something — for beating other cities, for attracting business, for enhancing Zenith's reputation.

This is the satirical point that Lewis drives home: Babbitt's world has no room for intrinsic value. The symphony orchestra will never be heard; it will only be promoted. And in that promotion, the music itself — the thing that might actually enrich a human life — is lost before it begins.
