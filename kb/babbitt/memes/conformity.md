# Conformity

The invisible social physics of Zenith — a force so pervasive it requires no enforcement because it has been internalized as morality itself. Lewis anatomizes conformity not as a political condition but as a spiritual one: the slow surrender of individual desire to the comfortable gravity of the herd.

## Definition in Context

In *Babbitt*, conformity is less a choice than an atmosphere — something breathed rather than debated. Babbitt does not consciously decide to conform; he simply inhabits a world where deviation is unthinkable. When he momentarily rebels in the novel's final third, the "belligerent conformity" of his earlier life is revealed less as cowardice than as a kind of gravity from which escape requires enormous energy (034-029). Lewis's insight is that conformity in Zenith is enforced not by laws or violence but by the far more efficient mechanisms of golf games, dinner parties, and the dread of being thought "queer."

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The standardized morning** — Babbitt's alarm clock, his bathroom routine, his identical breakfasts — the novel opens with a portrait of a man whose every gesture has been pre-scripted by convention. Even his rebellion begins with the smallest deviation: sleeping ten minutes late.
- **The language of "regular fellow"** — Throughout the novel, the highest praise is to be a "regular fellow," the worst condemnation to be "highbrow" or "queer." These terms do the work of conformity without requiring any specific doctrinal content — they are pure social enforcement.
- **Babbitt's belligerent orthodoxy** — Before his rebellion, Babbitt is an enforcer of conformity: he demands his son Ted "conform to decent standards" (029-063), he polices the respectability of his friends, he serves as an unpaid deputy sheriff of the conventional.
- **The Good Citizens' League** — The novel's surveillance apparatus for conformity, organized to crush dissent during the Zenith labor strikes. Its power lies not in violence but in the economic and social consequences it can impose on non-members.
- **The flight back to security** — After his rebellion collapses, Babbitt flees "back to the security of conformity" (032-032), suggesting that conformity is ultimately less about belief than about safety — the relief of being unremarkable.

## Key Passages

> He had, doubtless, sat in the same seat for a year, and the arm of the chair was worn where he laid his hand. He was not of the type that experiments with new brands of cigars. (paraphrase of 032-032 with original: "he perceived that his late freedom had been only a sham, and that he was again caught in the mesh of respectability. He desired to flee back to the security of conformity, provided there was a decent excuse.")

> He discovered that he actually liked the Boosters' Club and the Athletic Club; that he missed the routine of the office and the pleasant and regular meals at home. He who had been a revolutionist became a reformer, and from a reformer he shrank to a tired business man who wanted only to be allowed to sit in peace in his own den. (034-029)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses conformity to expose the central paradox of American individualism: a society that celebrates the self-made man while punishing any self that is actually made differently from its neighbors. Babbitt's tragedy is not that he is oppressed by conformity but that he consents to it — indeed, he becomes its most enthusiastic salesman. The novel's satire targets not conformity itself but the bad faith with which Zenith pretends that its conformists are rugged individualists. When Babbitt's brief rebellion collapses, Lewis refuses either to condemn or to sentimentalize the surrender: the "tired business man" who wants only peace is both a figure of pathos and an indictment of a civilization that makes peace and integrity incompatible.
