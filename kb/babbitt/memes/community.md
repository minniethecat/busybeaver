# Community

In Zenith, "community" is a talismanic word — spoken with reverence at every Boosters' Club lunch, inscribed on every church bulletin, invoked to justify every act of exclusion. Lewis peels back the rhetoric to reveal community as the velvet glove on the iron fist of conformity.

## Definition in Context

The community that Babbitt serves — orator at the Boosters' Club, deacon at Chatham Road Presbyterian, "responsible member of the community" (006-014) — is less a geographical or social reality than a rhetorical construct. It is the audience before whom respectability is performed, the jury that judges deviations, the collective whose approval is the only currency that matters. Lewis's insight is that "community" in Zenith functions not to include but to standardize: to be in the community is to have surrendered whatever is individual in oneself.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The Boosters' Club as community theater** — The weekly lunches are rituals of community membership where businessmen perform their belonging through speeches, handshakes, and the collective chanting of "Pep!" To miss a meeting is to place oneself outside the community's grace.
- **The church as community center** — Reverend John Jennison Drew transforms Chatham Road Presbyterian into a "true community center" (016-011) complete with gymnasium, movie nights, and business seminars — community as comprehensive programming rather than spiritual fellowship.
- **"An influence in the community"** — The phrase recurs throughout the novel as the highest accolade (017-051), bestowed on bankers, ministers, and real-estate men alike. Community influence is the secular equivalent of salvation — the evidence that one's life has mattered.
- **The community garage** — In one of the novel's characteristic deflations, Babbitt's fantasy of a "community garage for his acreage development" (001-019) translates the rhetoric of civic spirit into the language of real-estate profit — community as business opportunity.
- **The community as enforcer** — When Babbitt rebels, the community closes ranks with terrifying efficiency: the country club cuts him, his neighbors whisper, his business suffers. Community is revealed as an informal police force.

## Key Passages

> He had made his church a true community center. It contained everything but a swimming pool. (016-011)

> "The moral springs of the community's life lie deep in its Sunday schools." (016-029 — from a sermon quotation)

> He was a responsible member of the community — or at least he was considered one, which in Zenith amounted to the same thing. (paraphrase of the novel's attitude)

## Satirical Function

Lewis dissects "community" as the master term of Zenith's self-deception. Every exclusion — of socialists, of union organizers, of anyone who reads "queer" books — is performed in the name of community values. Every act of conformity is justified as community spirit. By the novel's end, "community" has been revealed as a synonym for whatever the business class wants it to mean — a floating signifier that can justify both a church gymnasium and a blacklist. The satire is not that community is a lie but that Zenith has made it one: a word that conceals more than it reveals about who belongs and who does not.
