# Miss McGoun

Miss McGoun is [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s stenographer, one of nine staff members in his real-estate office alongside Stanley Graff the outside salesman, old Mat Penniman the general utility man, and the partners Henry Thompson and Babbitt himself (003-042). Babbitt acknowledged her competence — "McGoun's a good stenog., smart's a whip" — while simultaneously mourning the mediocrity of his other employees (003-043).

## The Invisible Labor of Business

Miss McGoun represents the invisible female labor that sustains the masculine world of business. She takes dictation, types letters, and manages the office's documentary life, yet she remains a function rather than a character — a voice called for by a shouted surname. Her presence reveals the gender hierarchy of the business world: men make deals, women type the correspondence. Without Miss McGoun, Babbitt's office would grind to a halt — every contract, every letter, every commission check passes through her hands — but the novel never gives her a first name.

## The Shouted Surname

Babbitt's habitual call — "Msgoun," which meant "Miss McGoun" — is the novel's most compressed statement about gender and labor (003-047). The name is truncated, mangled, stripped of its honorific dignity. She is not "Miss McGoun" in Babbitt's daily practice; she is a barked monosyllable, a human intercom button. The contraction is efficient, affectionate in its way, and utterly dehumanizing.

## Key Scenes

- **The morning dictation** — Babbitt sighed, read through his mail, and shouted "Msgoun," which meant "Miss McGoun," to begin dictating his correspondence (003-047). The morning dictation was the ritual that launched Babbitt's business day; Miss McGoun was the vessel through which his commercial thoughts became typed reality.

- **The Gribble letter** — Babbitt dictated a characteristic business letter through Miss McGoun: "Omar Gribble, send it to his office, Miss McGoun, yours of twentieth to hand and in reply would say look here, Gribble, I'm awfully afraid if we go on shilly-shallying like this we'll just naturally lose out" (003-048). The letter continued with a property assessment matter, Babbitt instructing her to "get busy" about the deal (003-049). The Gribble letter is a perfect specimen of Babbitt's epistolary style — the blend of business jargon ("yours of twentieth to hand"), colloquial urgency ("shilly-shallying"), and salesman's pressure ("we'll just naturally lose out"). Miss McGoun had to translate this oral performance into typed prose, smoothing the rough edges while preserving the thrust.

## Competence Without Recognition

Babbitt's rare acknowledgment of Miss McGoun's competence — "smart's a whip" — is immediately followed by complaints about the rest of the staff (003-043). Her excellence is noted only as a contrast to others' mediocrity. She is praised not for what she does but for not being a problem. In the office ecology, she is the reliable appliance, the human typewriter that never malfunctions.

## Gender and the Office

The Babbitt-Thompson Realty office was a microcosm of 1920s gender relations in the American workplace. Men held the titles (partner, salesman) and made the money; women held the support roles (stenographer, file clerk) and made the men efficient. The stenographer was a new figure in the early twentieth century — a product of the commercial colleges that trained young women in typing and shorthand, creating a female clerical workforce that transformed the white-collar office. Miss McGoun is a beneficiary of this transformation and a prisoner of its limits.
