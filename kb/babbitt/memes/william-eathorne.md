# William Eathorne

The embodiment of inherited [[prosperity|wealth]] in a city that claims to value only hustle — a banker whose "thin lips" and patrician reserve mark the class boundary that [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] can never cross.

## Role in the Novel

Eathorne represents old money in [[zenith|Zenith]], a category that the city's booster ideology pretends does not exist. His mansion, "reminiscent of London, Back Bay, Rittenhouse Square" (017-006), stands as a rebuke to the myth of American classlessness. When [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] meets him, he is struck dumb by the social gulf between them: "It was indecent to think of using the 'How's tricks, ole socks?' which gratified [[vergil-gunch|Vergil Gunch]]" (017-009).

## Key Scenes

- **The meeting** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s encounter with Eathorne reveals the class stratification that [[zenith|Zenith]]'s rhetoric denies. [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s usual he[[art]]y manner fails him; he "sat politely, and politely did Eathorne observe, opening his thin lips just wide enough to dismiss the words" (017-009).
- **The mansion** — The Eathorne Mansion, with its marble steps scrubbed daily and brass plate reverently polished, is a physical monument to inherited status in a city of self-made men.

## Character Traits

Eathorne is defined by reserve, refinement, and the quiet confidence of inherited position. His "softly paternal" inquiries are exercises in social condescension — polite, distant, and utterly uninterested in [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s world of hustle and [[progress|pep]]. He is what [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] can never become: secure in status he did not have to earn.
