# Standard Oil Company

The Standard Oil Company, referenced in Ted Babbitt's frustrated outburst about his future, functions in the novel not as a depicted institution but as a symbol of escape — the fantasy of a corporate career in China that offers an alternative to the middle-class conformity his father embodies (006-033).

## Description

Standard Oil enters the novel solely through Ted's imagination. Complaining about his high-school curriculum, Ted declares, "I'd like to be one of these fellows that the Standard Oil Company sends out to China, and you live in a compound and don't have to do any work, and you get to see the world and pagodas and the ocean and everything!" (006-033). The company is never shown operating in Zenith or interacting with any character; it exists only as a mental destination — a place in the imagination where the constraints of Geometry, Cicero, and Babbitt's pragmatic lectures about Shakespeare's college-entrance utility do not apply. Ted interprets Standard Oil not as a business but as a visa: the corporate employer that will transport him beyond Zenith's horizons to pagodas and ocean crossings (006-033).

Ted's fantasy stands in pointed contrast to the correspondence-course advertisements he has clipped from magazines, which promise "POWER AND PROSPERITY IN PUBLIC SPEAKING" with an educational symbol that is "no antiquated lamp or torch or owl of Minerva, but a row of dollar signs" (006-033). The Standard Oil reverie belongs to the same commercialized imagination that produces these ads: it is escape, but escape purchased through a corporation, a life lived in "a compound" (006-033) — a word that suggests both the colonial enclave and the containment that the word implies.

Later in the same conversation, Ted returns to the China theme, asking his father: "Wouldn't it be a good idea if I could go off to China or some peppy place, and study engineering or something by mail?" (006-066). The question merges the Standard Oil fantasy with the correspondence-course ethos: escape to China, but study by mail — adventure and credentialing, exotic distance and domestic certification, in a single wish.

## Social Function

Ted's invocation of Standard Oil registers generational difference. Where Babbitt sees business as the means to acquire a "solid" house, a respectable church membership, and standing in the Boosters' Club, Ted sees it as the means to escape all those things. Standard Oil represents the multinational scale that Zenith's local boosters — with their lodge banquets and real-estate conventions — can never reach.

The irony is that Standard Oil is no less a corporate bureaucracy than the real-estate firms of Zenith. Ted's fantasy of living in a compound and doing no work (006-033) is a boy's romance, not a critique. Lewis presents it without editorializing, letting the reader notice that Ted's rebellion leads not to freedom but to a different corporation — the exchange of one "solid" institution for another, imagined as more glamorous because it is farther away. His desire to "see the world and pagodas and the ocean" (006-033) is genuine in its yearning and naive in its assumption that Standard Oil will deliver it.

## Key Scenes

- **Ted's Curriculum Rebellion**: In the Babbitt living-room, surrounded by Home Study assignments in plain geometry, Cicero, and Milton's _Comus_, Ted declares his Standard Oil fantasy as a protest against the curriculum his father defends on purely pragmatic grounds (006-033). Babbitt, defending the high-school curriculum, insists that Ted study Shakespeare "because they're required for college entrance, and that's all there is to it!" and recommends instead that Ted take "Business English, and learn how to write an ad, or letters that would pull" (006-032). Ted's reply — a cascade of alternative ambitions (aviator, garage owner, Standard Oil expatriate) — is a protest against his father's instrumental view of learning.

- **Babbitt's Gentleman-Class Lecture**: Babbitt counters Ted's China-and-correspondence-course plan with a defense of the B.A. as a social credential, invoking his own hard-won climb into the "gentlemen class." "I've found out it's a mighty nice thing to be able to say you're a B.A. Some client that doesn't know what you are and thinks you're just a plug business man, he gets to shooting off his mouth about economics or literature or foreign trade conditions, and you just ease in something like, 'When I was in college — course I got my B.A. in sociology and all that junk —' Oh, it puts an awful crimp in their style!" (006-066). The college degree is not about learning but about conversational leverage — a credential that allows the businessman to silence a client who presumes intellectual superiority. Babbitt connects this directly to class status: "My dad was a pretty good old coot, but he never had much style to him, and I had to work darn hard to earn my way through college. Well, it's been worth it, to be able to associate with the finest gentlemen in Zenith, at the clubs and so on, and I wouldn't want you to drop out of the gentlemen class" (006-066). The "gentlemen class" — a Babbitt coinage that conflates manners, money, and membership — is what Standard Oil threatens to bypass.

## Satirical Significance

The Standard Oil reference functions as Lewis's quiet exposure of the limits of Babbitt's world-view. Babbitt can only defend Shakespeare in terms of college-entrance requirements and business-letter writing (006-032)(006-033); Ted can only imagine escape in terms of another corporation. The Standard Oil fantasy is the inverse of Zenith — international where Zenith is provincial, exotic where Zenith is mundane — but still corporate, still administered, still a compound with walls.

The correspondence-course advertisements Ted brandishes — with their dollar-sign educational symbol and their promise of "POWER AND PROSPERITY IN PUBLIC SPEAKING" (006-033) — form a triptych with the Standard Oil dream and Babbitt's B.A.-as-social-weapon logic. All three treat education as a transaction, a means to status or escape rather than an end. Lewis suggests that the younger generation's revolt is as circumscribed as the older generation's conformity: both imagine the good life as one administered by a company, whether Babbitt-Thompson Realty or Standard Oil.

This scene ends on a note of tenderness that complicates the satire. After Ted dashes off to chorus rehearsal without finishing his homework, Babbitt does not enforce his usual rule — "Six times in the past sixty days Babbitt had stormed, 'You will not do it first thing in the morning! You'll do it right now!' but to-night he said, 'Well, better hustle,' and his smile was the rare shy radiance he kept for Paul Riesling" (006-067). The Standard Oil argument, for all its generational impasse, ends not in discipline but in an uncharacteristic softening — Babbitt's private tenderness surfacing through the cracks in his booster carapace.
