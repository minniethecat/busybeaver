# Prosperity

In Zenith, prosperity is not merely an economic condition — it is a theology. To be prosperous is to be righteous; to question prosperity is to commit blasphemy. Lewis anatomizes a civilization that has elevated material success from a happy circumstance to a moral imperative.

## Definition in Context

The Zenith that Babbitt gazes upon from his sleeping-porch is a city defined by prosperity — "shining new houses" on the hills, towers that "aspired above the morning mist," a landscape that is less geography than an argument for the rightness of commerce. Prosperity in *Babbitt* functions as both fact and ideology: the city really is prosperous, and this prosperity is constantly cited as proof that Zenith's way of life is the correct one. To be poor in Zenith is not merely unfortunate; it is suspect — evidence of moral failure in a city where "rational prosperity" (014-033) is the official religion.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The visible city** — Zenith's architecture is the physical evidence of prosperity: the "shining new houses," the Second National Tower, the "innumerous lodges and prosperity-boosting lunch-clubs" (016-004). The city wears its wealth as a badge of honor.
- **Prosperity as moral argument** — When discussing politics, business, or religion, Babbitt and his peers invariably return to prosperity as the clinching premise: whatever produces prosperity must be good. The syllogism is Prosperity → Virtue, and it admits no counterexamples.
- **"Rational prosperity"** — Babbitt's campaign speeches celebrate "rational prosperity" (014-033) — prosperity tamed, made respectable, distinguished from mere greed. The adjective "rational" does the work of laundering material self-interest into civic philosophy.
- **The price of prosperity** — Lewis quietly notes the labor that undergirds Zenith's wealth: the "cruelty" that insures "the prosperity of their cubs" (007-052), the workers whose song is heard at dawn but whose faces are never seen.
- **Prosperity and conformity** — In Zenith, prosperity is the reward for conformity, and poverty the punishment for deviation. When Babbitt rebels, his business suffers — the prosperity that was the proof of his virtue is withdrawn.

## Key Passages

> The towers of Zenith aspired above the morning mist; the song of labor was in the air — in a city built, it seemed, for giants. (001-005, paraphrased)

> "I tell you, Zenith and her sister-cities are producing a new type of civilization. There are men who say that we're just money-grubbers. But every city needs men who will sacrifice their time and energy to rational prosperity." (014-033, paraphrased)

> The worst thing about these fellows is that they've standardized all the beauty out of life. (007-048, paraphrased — the counter-argument prosperity's boosters refuse to hear)

## Satirical Function

Lewis's treatment of prosperity walks a tightrope between admiration and indictment. He grants Zenith its genuine achievement — the city really is a marvel of productivity — while exposing the spiritual poverty that underwrites material abundance. The novel's deepest satire is not that prosperity is bad but that it is insufficient: Babbitt has everything the American Dream promises and is still unhappy. The prosperity ideology collapses under the weight of its own success — it delivers the goods but cannot deliver the meaning it promised.
