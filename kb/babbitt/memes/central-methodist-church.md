# Central Methodist Church

A Methodist congregation in Zenith, notable primarily as the competitive rival to the [[chatham-road-presbyterian-church]]'s Sunday School, and as the cultural benchmark for Lewis's satire of artistic mediocrity in the "Age of Pep."

## Description

The Central Methodist Church operated the largest Sunday School in Zenith, maintaining its lead over Chatham Road Presbyterian's school through aggressive competitive tactics. The rivalry between the two churches' educational programs reflects the commercialization of religion in Zenith, where Sunday Schools were run with the competitive zeal of business enterprises.

## The Sunday School Competition

### The Rivalry

The competition between Central Methodist and Chatham Road Presbyterian was a microcosm of Zenith's booster culture, with church growth measured in the same quantitative terms as business expansion. Chatham Road Presbyterian, under [[John-Jennison-Drew|Dr. Drew]]'s leadership, "climbed from fourth place to second" in Sunday School enrollment — but "it did not become the largest school in Zenith" because "the Central Methodist Church kept ahead of it" (017-033).

> The Chatham Road Presbyterian Sunday School had climbed from fourth place to second. It did not become the largest school in Zenith — the Central Methodist Church kept ahead of it by methods which Dr. Drew scored as "unfair, undignified, un-American, ungentlemanly, and unchristian." (017-033)

The result at Chatham Road was framed in commercial terms: "there was rejoicing in heaven, or at least in that portion of heaven included in the parsonage of Dr. Drew" (017-033). The parenthetical qualification — that heaven's rejoicing was confined to Dr. Drew's parsonage — is classic Lewis irony, reducing divine approval to institutional self-congratulation.

### Dr. Drew's Accusations

Dr. Drew's string of indignant adjectives — "unfair, undignified, un-American, ungentlemanly, and unchristian" (017-033) — is itself satirical. The five-part condemnation mixes moral categories (unfair, unchristian) with social ones (undignified, ungentlemanly) and patriotic ones (un-American), revealing the way booster rhetoric collapsed all values into a single competitive frame. What exactly Central Methodist's "methods" were is left unspecified — a deliberate omission that suggests the rivalry mattered more than any actual principle at stake.

## The Easter Cantata as Cultural Benchmark

The Central Methodist Church's Easter cantata appears in the novel as a satirical yardstick of artistic incompetence. Describing Eunice Littlefield's ambition to be a cinema actress and her consumption of motion-picture magazines, Lewis writes:

> These stripy young starlets could not have acted in the Easter cantata of the Central Methodist Church. (018-006)

The implication is that the cantata represents the lowest conceivable rung of performative competence — a level these magazine-featured manicure-girls-turned-actresses could not even reach. This passing reference embeds the Central Methodist Church in Lewis's broader satire of the "Age of Pep," where cinema magazines, church pageants, and booster culture all participate in the same economy of manufactured enthusiasm.

## Social Function

The Central Methodist Church serves as a narrative foil to Chatham Road Presbyterian. Its competitive tactics highlight the way religious institutions in Babbitt's Zenith operated by the same metrics of size, growth, and market dominance as the city's commercial enterprises. The church is not depicted as a spiritual community but as an organization competing for market share in the Sunday School industry — a satirical reduction that is central to Lewis's critique of American religious life. In this respect, it mirrors the competitive dynamics of secular institutions like the [[boosters-club]] and the [[zenith-chamber-of-commerce]], where numbers and rankings matter more than purpose.

## Satirical Significance

The brief but pointed references to Central Methodist Church encapsulate one of Lewis's central critiques: that in a culture where everything is measured by commercial standards, even churches compete for market share. The Sunday School rivalry is a miniature of the competitive boosterism that pervades every institution in Zenith. The Easter cantata reference extends the satire to the arts, suggesting a continuum of mediocrity from church pageant to Hollywood. Together, the two mentions paint a portrait of an institution entirely defined by competition — not by faith, community, or worship, but by its capacity to out-hustle the Presbyterians.
