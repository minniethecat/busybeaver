# The Boosters' Club Annual Election

The annual election of officers at the Zenith Boosters' Club — a ritual in which four hundred businessmen gather in the O'Hearn House ballroom wearing celluloid nickname buttons, subject to a ten-cent fine for addressing one another by anything but their nicknames, to perform an elaborate pageant of democratic procedure that masks the complete identity of interests among Zenith's business elite.

## Background

The International Organization of Boosters' Clubs, Lewis notes with mock-reverence, "has become a world-force for optimism, manly pleasantry, and good business" (021-003). Chapters exist in thirty countries, though "nine hundred and twenty of the thousand chapters, however, are in the United States" (021-003) — a statistic that locates boosterism as a distinctively American export. "None of these is more ardent than the Zenith Boosters' Club" (021-003).

The second March lunch is designated "the most important of the year, as it was to be followed by the annual election of officers" (021-003). The word "agitation" describes the atmosphere: "There was agitation abroad" (021-003) — a term normally reserved for political unrest, here applied to the selection of club officers, collapsing the distinction between democratic ferment and institutional routine.

## Key Elements

- **The ritual of enforced intimacy** — As each of the four hundred Boosters enters, he takes from a wallboard "a huge celluloid button announcing his name, his nickname, and his business" (021-003). A fine of ten cents is levied for calling a Fellow Booster by anything but his nickname. The air is "radiant with shouts of 'Hello, Chet!' and 'How're you, Shorty!' and 'Top o' the mornin', Mac!'" (021-003). The nicknames perform intimacy while the buttons ensure it cannot fail — sociability engineered to be foolproof.

- **The seating lottery** — Boosters sit at "friendly tables for eight, choosing places by lot" (021-003). This random seating, with its pretense of democratic mixing, ensures that "only two persons from each department of business were permitted to join, so that you at once encountered the Ideals of other occupations, and realized the metaphysical oneness of all occupations — plumbing and portrait-painting, medicine and the manufacture of chewing-gum" (021-003). The phrase "metaphysical oneness" is Lewis's distillation of booster philosophy: all occupations are one because all businessmen are interchangeable.

- **Babbitt's table performance** — At Babbitt's table sit Albert Boos the merchant tailor, Hector Seybolt of the Little Sweetheart Condensed Milk Company, Emil Wengert the jeweler, Professor Pumphrey of the Riteway Business College, Dr. Walter Gorbutt, Roy Teegarten the photographer, and Ben Berkey the photo-engraver. The table is "particularly happy" because Professor Pumphrey has had a birthday. Babbitt wins the day's approval with: "Don't talk about pumps to that guy! The only pump he knows is a bottle! Honest, they tell me he's starting a class in home-brewing at the ole college!" (021-003).

- **The election itself** — After the lunch and festivities, the chair announces: "Gentlemen, we will now proceed to the annual election of officers" (021-022). For each office, a single nominee is presented and ratified by acclamation. The election is a foregone conclusion; the real political work has occurred in the weeks of informal negotiation that precede the lunch.

## Satirical Significance

The Boosters' Club election is Lewis's most concentrated satire of American civic ritual. The entire apparatus — nickname buttons, seating lotteries, fines for using real names — exists to produce an experience of democratic participation that is the opposite of democracy. No offices are contested; the "agitation" that precedes the election is social, not political. The Boosters have perfected a form of institutional life in which the performance of equality (everyone has a nickname, everyone sits by lot) coexists with the reality of hierarchy (everyone knows who the officers will be before the meeting begins).

The booklet listing members by occupation, with its advertisements and the admonition "There's no rule that you have to trade with your Fellow Boosters, but get wise, boy — what's the use of letting all this good money get outside of our happy fambly?" (021-003), makes explicit what the ritual obscures: the Boosters' Club is an instrument of commercial solidarity. The "metaphysical oneness of all occupations" is merely the philosophical cover for a business cartel.

Lewis's deadpan catalog of Babbitt's table companions — from condensed milk to chewing-gum — insists on the mundane materiality beneath the booster rhetoric of "world-force" and "optimism." These are not captains of industry shaping civilization; they are tradesmen eating lunch.
