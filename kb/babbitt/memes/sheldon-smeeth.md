# Sheldon Smeeth

Sheldon Smeeth is the educational director of the [[zenith-ymca|Y.M.C.A.]] and the leader of the church choir at [[chatham-road-presbyterian-church|Chatham Road Presbyterian Church]]. A pale but strenuous young man with curly hair and a smile, "Sheldy" embodies the professionalized, relentlessly cheerful religiosity that [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] finds both inescapable and repellent.

## Role in the Novel

Smeeth functions as an agent of the social-conformity machinery that Babbitt alternately submits to and rebels against. Where [[john-jennison-drew|Dr. Drew]] represents the intellectual pretensions of liberal Protestantism and [[william-washington-eathorne|Eathorne]] represents old-money church patronage, Smeeth represents its emotional manipulation — the smile that never falters, the hand that never lets go. He appears at key moments of Babbitt's attempted return to orthodoxy, trailing whispers of disapproval.

## Key Scenes

- **The December church service** — Smeeth conducts an impressive musical program and sings the offertory at a packed service. Babbitt admires [[john-jennison-drew|Dr. Drew's]] sermon but resents that "some misguided person had taught young Mr. Smeeth to smile, smile, smile while he was singing." (016-014)
- **Teaching the boys' class** — Babbitt observes Smeeth teaching a class of sixteen-year-old boys in Sunday School: "a pale but strenuous young man with curly hair and a smile." The experience disconcerts Babbitt. (016-024)
- **The prayer meeting for Babbitt** — When Babbitt is pressured into a private prayer meeting with Dr. Drew, Smeeth comes "frolicking" into the study, smirks forgivingly, and kneels beside Babbitt with an arm around him, moaning "Yes, Lord! Help our brother, Lord!" while Dr. Drew prays. When Drew asks if the deputation has arrived, Smeeth answers with equal businesslike liveliness. [034-011 through 034-015]
- **The church encounter during Babbitt's rebellion** — Smeeth accosts Babbitt on the street, imprisoning his hand in a damp clasp and chanting, "Brother Babbitt, we haven't seen you at church very often lately." Babbitt shakes off the affectionate clasp — "Sheldy liked to hold hands for a long time" — and snarls a reply, but afterward winces at the thought that "that white worm" represents the church's surveillance of his orthodoxy. (032-038)

## Character Traits

Smeeth is defined by his relentless, performative cheerfulness. His smile is both his weapon and his shield — it reassures the orthodox and disarms the hesitant. His physical mannerisms — the damp hands, the prolonged handclasps, the frolicking entrance — make his piety feel invasive rather than comforting. Lewis deploys him as a satirical portrait of the professional Christian worker: efficient, smiling, interchangeable, and fundamentally hollow. His instant pivot from prayerful moans to businesslike efficiency ("Yep, right outside") captures the commodification of religious sentiment that the novel critiques.
