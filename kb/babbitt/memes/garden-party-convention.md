# The Convention Garden Party

The garden party at the S.A.R.E.B. convention in Monarch — an event that [[george-babbitt]] endures rather than enjoys, reflecting that his wife "would have enjoyed all this social agony" (013-043), and one that illuminates a fault line in Babbitt's character: his comfort with the quantitative certainties of business boosterism versus his unease with the qualitative demands of social performance.

## Background

The garden party is one of several social events arranged by the Monarch Chamber of Commerce as part of the annual convention of the State Association of Real Estate Boards (013-004). Monarch, "Zenith's chief rival among the cities of the state" (013-004), is hosting the convention, and the social program is designed to showcase the city's hospitality and sophistication — a competitive display of civic virtue not unlike the booster rituals Babbitt knows from Zenith.

The convention is Babbitt's first experience as an official delegate representing Zenith on the state stage. He has arrived with a paper to deliver — an argument that realtors deserve recognition as "professional men" — and the garden party represents the informal dimension of convention life: the networking, social maneuvering, and status negotiation that occur between the formal sessions.

## Key Elements

- **"Social agony"** — Babbitt's private diagnosis of the garden party is characteristically blunt: "Myra would have enjoyed all this social agony" (013-043). The phrase "social agony" captures Babbitt's ambivalent relationship to the rituals of middle-class sociability. He participates in them — he is, after all, a Booster, an Elk, a precinct leader — but he experiences them as ordeals rather than pleasures. His wife Myra, who does not attend the convention, is imagined as someone who would have appreciated the event's nuances in a way Babbitt cannot.

- **The motor tours as escape** — Babbitt "cared less for the garden party than for the motor tours which the Monarch Chamber of Commerce had arranged" (013-043). The tours take delegates to "water-reservoirs, suburban trolley-stations, and tanneries" — the infrastructure of industrial civilization rather than its social surface. Babbitt's preference for water-reservoirs over garden parties is an important character note: he is more comfortable measuring and comparing than conversing and charming. "Indefatigably he viewed" these sites, "devouring the statistics which were given to him" (013-043). Statistics are Babbitt's native language; garden-party conversation is a foreign tongue.

- **The lumber statistic** — Babbitt's response to the motor tours crystallizes his booster mentality. He marvels to his roommate, W. A. Rogers: "Of course this town isn't a patch on Zenith; it hasn't got our outlook and natural resources; but did you know — I nev' did till to-day — that they manufactured seven hundred and sixty-three million feet of lumber last year? What d' you think of that!" (013-043). The remark is a perfect specimen of booster thinking: Monarch must be simultaneously acknowledged for its achievements and subordinated to Zenith's superiority. The specific figure — 763 million feet — is the kind of detail that gives Babbitt genuine pleasure, a pleasure the garden party cannot provide.

- **The speech that follows** — The garden party and motor tours precede Babbitt's convention speech, and the contrast between his social unease and his platform confidence is instructive. On the platform, despite initial nervousness, he finds his voice: talking to an audience about professional identity is a form of performance Babbitt understands; mingling at a garden party is not.

## Satirical Significance

The garden party scene is brief but diagnostically rich. It exposes a distinction within booster culture that the novel's broader satire sometimes obscures: the difference between the boosterism of infrastructure — water-reservoirs, lumber statistics, trolley-stations — and the boosterism of sociability — garden parties, nicknames, club lunches. Babbitt is fluent in the first and awkward in the second.

This awkwardness humanizes Babbitt. He is not simply a conformist; he is a conformist who finds conformity uncomfortable, who diagnoses social events as "agony" even as he attends them. His preference for "water-reservoirs, suburban trolley-stations, and tanneries" (013-043) over garden parties suggests a genuine affinity for the material world of production and measurement that exists alongside his performative booster identity.

The detail that Babbitt reflects on Myra's absence — "Myra would have enjoyed all this social agony" (013-043) — is also significant. Throughout the novel, Babbitt's relationship with his wife is characterized by condescension and inattention. Yet here, alone at a convention in a rival city, he thinks of her with something like respect: she possesses a social competence he lacks. The moment is fleeting but reveals a dimension of the Babbitts' marriage that Babbitt's usual domestic complaints obscure.
