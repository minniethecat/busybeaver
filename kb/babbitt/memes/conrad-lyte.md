# Conrad Lyte

Conrad Lyte is a real-estate speculator in [[zenith|Zenith]] — a "merry miser" who represents the pure profit motive at [[labor|work]] in the city's land economy. Unlike [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]], who wraps his transactions in civic rhetoric, Lyte is unapologetically in business for the money (004-031, 032-027).

## Role in the Novel

Lyte serves as a foil to [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s self-image as an honest businessman. While [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] tells himself he provides a service to the [[neighborhood|community]], Lyte's deals make visible the extraction at the he[[art]] of [[zenith|Zenith]]'s real-estate economy. When [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]] advises Lyte to purchase a lot and then the [[neighborhood|community]] pays Lyte for having employed foresight, Lewis lays bare the mechanism by which speculation is rebranded as public service (004-034, 004-035).

- **The morning conference** — [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]], Lyte, and Archibald Purdy meet at eleven-thirty to discuss real-estate matters. The scene establishes Lyte as a peer in [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s commercial net[[labor|work]] (004-029).

- **Lyte's Troubles** — Like other [[zenith|Zenith]] businessmen, Lyte "also had Troubles." The capitalized noun, presented without irony, hints at the domestic or financial difficulties that the business [[art|culture]] cannot acknowledge directly (031-009).

## Character Traits

Lyte is described as the "merry miser" — a man who enjoys his [[prosperity|wealth]] without pretense. He lacks [[george-babbitt|Babbitt]]'s need to moralize his transactions, and in this he is both more honest and more alarming. Lyte embodies the speculative energy that drives [[zenith|Zenith]]'s [[progress|growth]], untroubled by the question of whether that [[progress|growth]] serves any purpose beyond enrichment.
