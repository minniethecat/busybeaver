# Royal Drug Store

A Zenith drug store known for its ornate interior — described as possessing "mosaic splendor" (018-032) — where the younger generation gathers to consume soda-fountain treats and discuss the inadequacies of their parents' generation.

## Description

The Royal Drug Store is a commercial establishment in Zenith that serves as a gathering place for the city's youth. Its interior is characterized by "mosaic splendor" (018-032), a phrase that captures both its decorative ambition and the ironic contrast between its ornate appearance and the mundane consumption it houses. It is one of many "drug-stores to supply the more immediate needs of Greator Suburban Estates," an early real-estate development whose "tracts of absolute residence territory" are surrounded by "groceries and laundries and drug-stores" [003-029 context].

The drug store features a soda fountain where patrons consume "hot chocolate, lumps of nougat, and an assortment of glacé nuts" (018-032) — the standard indulgences of 1920s American youth culture.

## Key Scenes

- **Ted and Eunice at the soda fountain** — In Chapter XVIII, Ted Babbitt and Eunice Littlefield sit at the Royal Drug Store consuming hot chocolate and nougat while Ted complains about his father: "it gets me why Dad doesn't just pass out from being so poky. Every evening he sits there, about half-asleep, and if Rone or I say, 'Oh, come on, let's do something,' he doesn't even take the trouble to think about it. He just yawns and says, 'Naw, this suits me right here.'" (018-032). The scene is rich with dramatic irony: Ted's complaint — that Babbitt is too dull to seek amusement — mirrors almost precisely Babbitt's own later complaint to his wife about Verona and Kenneth Escott: "Sitting there — sitting there — night after night — not wanting to do anything" (018-035).

## Social Function

The Royal Drug Store functions as a neutral ground where the younger generation can gather away from parental supervision. Its soda fountain represents a democratized luxury — "mosaic splendor" available for the price of a hot chocolate. The scene stages the generational conflict that pervades the novel: Ted's complaint about his father's "pokiness" (018-032) is the adolescent mirror of Babbitt's own midlife restlessness, each generation finding the other's contentment incomprehensible.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis deploys the Royal Drug Store scene to dramatize the novel's central irony about generational judgment. Ted's dismissal of his father as hopelessly dull (018-032) is undercut when, just paragraphs later, Babbitt uses almost identical language to complain about Verona: "Sitting there — sitting there — night after night — not wanting to do anything" (018-035). Neither generation recognizes itself in the mirror the novel holds up. The drug store, with its "mosaic splendor," provides a setting where youth can feel sophisticated while consuming the same mass-produced pleasures their parents' generation manufactured.
