# Irving Tate

Irving Tate is a Zenith tool-manufacturer and a member of the exclusive social circle centered on [[charles-mckelvey|Charles McKelvey]], the millionaire contractor. Alongside McKelvey and [[max-kruger|Max Kruger]] the banker, Tate belongs to the tiny set of Zenith men who have ascended beyond boosterism into genuine social leadership — a realm that [[george-babbitt|George Babbitt]] can observe but not enter.

## Role in the Novel

Tate functions as a social boundary-marker, one of the names that defines the ceiling of Babbitt's aspirations. He is listed among "such social leaders as Charles McKelvey the millionaire contractor, Max Kruger the banker, Irving Tate the tool-manufacturer, and Adelbert Dobson the fashionable interior decorator" — men whom Babbitt theoretically knows from college, who still call him "Georgie," but who never invite him to dinner at their houses on [[Royal-Ridge]] (015-004).

Tate's occupation as "tool-manufacturer" is significant in Lewis's taxonomy of Zenith. A manufacturer — not a retailer, not a realtor, not a salesman — stands on firmer economic ground than the booster businessmen who dominate Babbitt's social circle. The tool-manufacturer makes things; the realtor only moves them. This distinction, unspoken but real, helps explain why Tate and his set can afford to ignore the frantic networking that consumes Babbitt's life.

## Character Traits

Lewis gives Tate no dialogue, no described appearance, no anecdote. He is a name and an occupation — and this minimalism is precisely the point. The McKelvey set is defined for Babbitt by its inaccessibility; the less he knows about Tate, the more Tate represents a world from which Babbitt is excluded. Lewis's refusal to fill in the character mirrors Babbitt's exclusion from the character's dinner table.
