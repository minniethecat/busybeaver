# Evening Advocate

A Zenith newspaper, the afternoon counterpart to the morning Advocate-Times. It is the source of Babbitt's limited intellectual engagement — comic strips and booster editorials — and a representative institution of the city's media landscape, which exists to celebrate rather than scrutinize.

## Description

The Evening Advocate is the afternoon newspaper serving Zenith. Its comic strips "composed his favorite literature" (006-031) — the phrasing is doubly satirical: Babbitt's "favorite literature" comes from the funny pages, and even this is mediated by the booster press. The paper's Saturday edition carries editorials on topics like "The" (016-011), suggesting a conventionality so complete it barely needs a subject.

The paper exists alongside the Advocate-Times, Zenith's morning paper; together they form a media duopoly. When both papers "attributed this valuable" (034-008) something-or-other, the coordinated coverage suggests that Zenith's newspapers function as a single booster voice.

## Social Function

The Evening Advocate serves as an instrument of booster ideology. Its editorials reinforce the values of the commercial class; its comic strips provide a safe, unchallenging form of entertainment. For a man like Babbitt, whose intellectual life extends no further than the newspaper, the Advocate defines the boundaries of acceptable thought.

The paper also exemplifies the booster media's role in suppressing dissent. No radical opinions appear in its pages; no investigative reporting disturbs Zenith's commercial peace. The Evening Advocate, like the city's clubs and churches, exists to affirm what its readers already believe.

## Satirical Significance

Lewis's treatment of the Evening Advocate is one of the novel's sharper media critiques. That Babbitt's "favorite literature" is the comic strips is not merely a joke about his philistinism — it is an indictment of a media environment that offers nothing more challenging. The newspaper, which should inform and provoke, instead tranquilizes.

The elided editorial title — "The" (016-011) — is a masterstroke of satirical compression. Whatever the editorial was about, its subject was so conventional that Lewis can leave it unnamed, confident that readers will understand: Zenith journalism has no content, only booster sentiment.
