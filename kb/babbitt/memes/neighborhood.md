# Neighborhood

The physical and social geography of Zenith's middle-class life, where the "bright roofs and immaculate turf and amazing comfort" (003-017) of Floral Heights serve as both achievement and prison for the novel's characters.

## Definition in Context

Floral Heights, Babbitt's neighborhood, is the spatial expression of the Zenith ethos. Built by "solid citizens" who transformed "a wilderness of rank second-growth elms and oaks and maples" (003-017) into a suburban paradise, the neighborhood embodies the booster faith in progress and improvement. But its "precise streets" (003-017) also represent the conformity that defines — and constrains — the lives of its residents.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **Floral Heights as achievement** — The neighborhood is the physical proof of Babbitt's success: "Twenty years before, the hill on which Floral Heights was spread... had been a wilderness" (003-017). The transformation of wilderness into suburb mirrors the narrative of progress that Babbitt's class tells about itself.
- **The yard as self** — Babbitt's yard is "the neat yard of a successful business man of Zenith, that is, it was perfection, and made him also perfect" (001-018), revealing how neighborhood aesthetics substitute for moral worth.
- **Social stratification** — The contrast between Floral Heights and the Eathorne Mansion — "reminiscent of London, Back Bay, Rittenhouse Square" (017-006) — marks the class distinctions within Zenith's ostensibly classless society.
- **Neighborhood as conformity** — The "precise streets" and "immaculate turf" of Floral Heights enforce a regime of visible respectability. Every lawn, every garage, every sleeping-porch is subject to the judgment of neighbors.

## Key Passages

> They had labored, these solid citizens. Twenty years before, the hill on which Floral Heights was spread, with its bright roofs and immaculate turf and amazing comfort, had been a wilderness of rank second-growth elms and oaks and maples. Along the precise streets were still a few wooded vacant lots. (003-017)

## Satirical Function

Lewis uses Floral Heights as a spatial metaphor for the Zenith worldview: orderly, prosperous, and stifling. The neighborhood's transformation from wilderness to "immaculate turf" parallels Babbitt's own taming by the forces of conformity. The "precise streets" suggest not only planning but control — a grid of expectations from which deviation is visible and punished. Lewis implies that the suburbs, far from being a retreat from the pressures of business, are themselves an extension of the business ethos into domestic life: competitive, standardized, and obsessed with appearances.
