# Marriage

In Zenith, marriage is a business merger with bedroom privileges — the foundational unit of the respectable life, yet one that Lewis depicts as an emotional vacuum maintained through routine, mutual irritation, and the shared project of keeping up appearances.

## Definition in Context

Babbitt's marriage to Myra is neither a tragedy nor a romance but something more corrosive to the spirit: a habit. The novel opens not with love but with routine — Babbitt's irritation at his wife's "detestably cheerful" morning greeting (001-016), his resentment at the soda she has placed in the bathroom, the wordless negotiations of the breakfast table. Marriage in *Babbitt* is the institution that domesticates male desire into suburban respectability, transforming passion into real-estate ambition and rebellion into middle-aged crisis.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The morning ritual as marital portrait** — Chapter I's breakfast scene is a miniature of the Babbitt marriage: irritation disguised as affection, conversation that communicates nothing, a wife who has become furniture and a husband who resents the furniture for being familiar.
- **The "inspiring wife" performance** — Babbitt publicly praises Myra as "inspiring" (005-026) while privately finding her tedious — the public/private split that defines Zenith marriages. The performance of marital admiration is itself a social obligation.
- **The separate vacation** — Babbitt's trip to Maine without Myra (005-037) is an act of quiet desertion rationalized as rest. Neither spouse acknowledges the relief of separation — it is the novel's most honest marital scene.
- **Paul Riesling's marriage as warning** — Paul's miserable marriage to Zilla is the nightmare version of Babbitt's tolerable discontent — what happens when the suppressed tensions of Zenith marriage erupt into open warfare.
- **The wife as social manager** — Myra's role is to manage Babbitt's social calendar, his dinner parties, his respectability — she is less a partner than a producer of the Babbitt family brand.

## Key Passages

> He was sufficiently roused by his wife to look at her. (001-027)

> The evening before his marriage was an agony, and the morning after was a dull misery. He had never been in love with Myra. He had married her because she was a "good sensible girl" and because his mother had told him to. (paraphrase from Chapter VI)

> If a man is bored by his wife, do you seriously mean he has a right to chuck her? (005-037 — from a conversation about marriage and divorce between Babbitt and Paul)

## Satirical Function

Lewis deploys marriage as his primary exhibit in the case against respectability. The Babbitt marriage is functional: it produces children, manages a household, maintains social standing. What it does not produce is intimacy, passion, or even genuine companionship. Lewis's satire cuts both ways — Babbitt is not a victim of marriage so much as a co-author of its emptiness, a man who chose a "good sensible girl" over love and has been paying the emotional mortgage ever since. The novel suggests that Zenith marriages survive not through love but through the sheer inertia of convention and the terror of being thought irregular.
