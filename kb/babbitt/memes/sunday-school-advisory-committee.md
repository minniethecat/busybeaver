# Sunday School Advisory Committee

A committee formed at the request of the pastor of Chatham Road Presbyterian Church to "buck up" the Sunday School and promote church membership. George Babbitt, appointed to the committee, threw himself into the task with characteristic boosterish energy, treating Sunday School promotion as a business enterprise—studying trade journals, drafting marketing copy, and applying the techniques of real-estate advertising to the business of saving souls (016-020).

## Description

The Advisory Committee on Sunday School was established at Chatham Road Presbyterian Church after the pastor observed declining engagement. It met in the "highly varnished Sunday School room" with "polite stuffiness" characteristic of church parlors, with overflow sessions held in the basement among "varicose water-pipes" (016-026). Babbitt discovered a "world of Sunday School journals"—an "enormous and busy domain of weeklies and monthlies" as technical and professional as any trade publication (016-028). He studied articles on "Focusing Appeals," "Scouting for New Members," and "Getting Prospects to Sign up with the Sunday School," finding that religious outreach used the same language and methodology as business marketing (016-029).

## Social Function

The committee served Babbitt's twin needs: it provided "purification and publicity"—spiritual respectability and professional visibility in one package (016-010). Membership demonstrated his standing as a solid, church-going businessman while offering networking opportunities with other Zenith businessmen who similarly treated church as a venue for "respectable" social contact beneficial to one's business (016-023). The committee's work—reducing faith to marketing strategy—exemplified the commodification of religion in Zenith's booster culture.

## Key Members

- **Chatham Road Pastor (unnamed)** — Requested the committee's formation, citing the need to "buck up" the Sunday School (016-020)

## Key Scenes

- **Committee Formation** — The pastor requests Babbitt to form a "committee of advice and publicity" for the Sunday School; Babbitt eagerly accepts (016-020)
- **Babbitt's Sunday School Research** — Babbitt immerses himself in Sunday School trade journals, discovers a parallel universe of professionalized religious marketing, and drafts promotional copy with the same techniques he uses for real estate (016-028, 016-029)

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the Sunday School Advisory Committee to expose the merger of religion and commerce in 1920s American middle-class life. Babbitt's approach—applying business advertising techniques to church outreach—reveals how thoroughly boosterism had colonized even the spiritual domain. The discovery that Sunday School journals use the same language as real-estate trade magazines ("prospects," "sign up," "appeals") satirizes the professionalization and commodification of faith. The committee's name itself—"Advisory Committee"—gestures toward corporate governance, underscoring that in Babbitt's world, even the church was run like a business.
