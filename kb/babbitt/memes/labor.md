# Labor

The great unmentionable of Zenith's booster literature — the working people whose sweat built the towers but whose demands for a share of the prosperity are treated as a foreign contagion. Lewis's treatment of labor reveals the class fault line beneath the city's rhetoric of universal progress.

## Definition in Context

Labor appears in *Babbitt* primarily as a problem — something to be managed, suppressed, or rhetorically neutralized. The "song of labor" that opens the novel (001-005) is quickly drowned out by the noise of business transactions and booster speeches. When labor organizes and makes demands, it transforms in the business-class imagination from "solid citizens" (003-017) into a menace requiring the Good Citizens' League to combat. Lewis anatomizes this transformation without editorializing — he simply shows the businessmen talking, and their fear does the satiric work.

## Manifestations in the Novel

- **The invisible builders** — The novel opens with a reference to the "song of labor in a city built" (001-005), but the laborers themselves are almost entirely absent from the narrative. Their invisibility is the novel's formal acknowledgment of their social position.
- **Labor unions as threat** — Babbitt's attitude toward unions is a reflex of his class position: he takes pride "in putting it over on the labor unions" in a real-estate deal (005-032), treating labor not as a partner in prosperity but as an adversary to be outmaneuvered.
- **The "respectable labor-leaders"** — The distinction between "respectable" and unrespectable labor leaders (007-058) marks the boundary of acceptable dissent in Zenith: labor may exist, but only in domesticated form.
- **The Good Citizens' League** — Formed during a labor strike, the League is the business class's extra-governmental enforcement mechanism, a reminder that Zenith's commitment to law and order is contingent on labor knowing its place.
- **The absent working class** — The novel's narrative perspective remains resolutely middle-class; working people appear only as servants, clerks, and comic relief. This structural exclusion is Lewis's sharpest comment on the class system.

## Key Passages

> The towers of Zenith aspired above the morning mist; the song of labor was in the air — in a city built, it seemed, for giants. (001-005, paraphrased)

> A good labor union is of value because it keeps the working-men from being exploited, but a labor union that tries to run the factory is a menace to American institutions. (004-026, paraphrased from Babbitt's views)

> You and me and all the nice respectable labor-leaders think you and me are the bosses, but it ain't so. (007-058 — from a labor figure's speech)

## Satirical Function

Lewis's treatment of labor is a study in perspective: by restricting the narrative almost entirely to the business class's point of view, he reproduces the blindness that is the privilege of that class. Babbitt never sees a worker as a full human being with a family, aspirations, and grievances — and because the novel rarely departs from Babbitt's consciousness, neither does the reader. The satire is structural: the absence of labor from a novel about an industrial city is itself the indictment. When labor does appear, it is either as a threat to be suppressed or a tool to be used — never as a voice with its own claim to dignity.
