# First Congregational Church of Catawba

## Description

The First Congregational Church of Catawba was the church of George Babbitt's boyhood in the village of Catawba, where he was born and raised before rising to prominence in Zenith. The church represented the rural Protestant respectability of Babbitt's origins—a world of Sunday School classrooms, "polite stuffiness to be found only in church parlors," and "drab Sunday School books: 'Hetty, a Humble Heroine' and 'Josephus, a Lad of Palestine'"(016-026).

> "What Babbitt saw, however, was the First Congregational Church of Catawba. He was back in the Sunday School of his boyhood. He smelled again that polite stuffiness to be found only in church parlors; he recalled the case of drab Sunday School books." (016-026)

## Role in Babbitt's Life

The church served as an emotional touchstone for Babbitt, a symbol of the simpler virtues of his Catawba youth that he had abandoned for the more sophisticated—and more hollow—life of a Zenith businessman. Visiting the Sunday School of Chatham Road Presbyterian Church in Zenith triggered a wave of nostalgia: "What Babbitt saw, however, was the First Congregational Church of Catawba. He was back in the Sunday School of his boyhood"(016-026).

Babbitt's relationship with his Catawba church was characteristic of his larger relationship with religion: sentimental, rooted in childhood habit rather than adult conviction, and complicated by the social ambitions that had carried him from "the village of Catawba"(008-003) to the prosperity of Floral Heights. He recalled attending Sunday School "every chance I got"(016-030), but the memory was tinged with the knowledge that he had left that world behind—along with the earnest faith it represented.

## Key Scenes

**Sunday School Nostalgia (Chapter XVI)** — While attending a church function at the Chatham Road Presbyterian Church in Zenith, Babbitt was transported back to his Catawba Sunday School. He "smelled again that polite stuffiness to be found only in church parlors; he recalled the case of drab Sunday School books"(016-026) and "thumbed once more the high-colored text-cards which no boy wanted but no boy liked to throw away, because they were somehow sacred"(016-026). The memory was both comforting and disquieting—a reminder of a faith he had outgrown without replacing.

## Satirical Significance

The First Congregational Church of Catawba stands in ironic contrast to the commodified religion of Zenith—the "bright cheerful" services of Chatham Road Presbyterian, where the pastor "is especially keen on good congregational singing"(017-045). Babbitt's Catawba church was a place of genuine, if provincial, piety; his Zenith church was a social institution where business connections were made and respectability was performed. The distance between the two measured the distance between Babbitt's authentic self and the standardized businessman he had become.
