# Outing Golf and Country Club

The Outing Golf and Country Club is the country club where George Babbitt golfs and socializes on weekends, located on a cliff above Lake Kennepoose and serving the upper-middle tier of Zenith's business class (012-011).

## Description

The Outing Golf and Country Club is "a pleasant gray-shingled building with a broad porch, on a daisy-starred cliff above Lake Kennepoose" (012-011). The club's architecture — gray shingles, broad porch — suggests comfort and respectability rather than ostentation. The location above Lake Kennepoose, with its daisy-starred surroundings, places the club in Zenith's recreational periphery, where successful businessmen escape the city each Saturday.

Babbitt's weekend routine revolves around the club: "Every Saturday afternoon he hustled out to his country club and hustled through nine holes of golf as a rest after the week's hustle" (012-010). The repetition of "hustle" — applied equally to work and to leisure — suggests that golf at the Outing is less a genuine rest than another form of obligatory activity for the successful businessman.

## Social Function

The country club is presented as a mandatory institution for Zenith's business class: "In Zenith it was as necessary for a Successful Man to belong to a country club as it was to wear a linen collar" (012-011). The country club functions simultaneously as recreation, status display, and social sorting mechanism.

The Outing occupies a specific position in Zenith's social hierarchy. It is not the most elite club — that distinction belongs to the Tonawanda Country Club, to which "belonged Charles McKelvey, Horace Updike, and the other rich men who lunched not at the Athletic but at the Union Club" (012-011). Babbitt's defensiveness about this pecking order is revealing: "You couldn't hire me to join the Tonawanda, even if I did have a hundred and eighty bucks to throw away on the initiation fee," he insists with suspicious frequency (012-011). He contrasts the Outing's "real human fellows" and "finest lot of little women in town" with the Tonawanda's "would-be's in New York suits" (012-011). The protest is too vigorous — Babbitt is rationalizing his exclusion from the class above him.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt's defense of the Outing** — Babbitt explains at length why he wouldn't join the Tonawanda club even "on a bet," praising the Outing's genuine fellowship versus the Tonawanda's pretension (012-011), in a passage that reveals more about his social anxieties than about the clubs themselves
- **Saturday golf routine** — Babbitt's weekly rush to the club for nine holes of golf is described as his primary relaxation, though the language of "hustle" suggests it is less a leisure activity than another obligation (012-010)

## Satirical Significance

The Outing Golf and Country Club is a key site for Lewis's satire of class distinctions in Zenith. The country club system functions as a mechanism for ranking and sorting — the Outing is good enough for successful realtors, but not for the truly rich. Babbitt's loud insistence that he prefers the Outing to the Tonawanda is a classic example of sour grapes, and Lewis lets the reader see that the distinction matters intensely to Babbitt precisely because he cannot cross it. The country club, supposedly a place of leisure and escape, is actually one more arena where status must be anxiously maintained.
