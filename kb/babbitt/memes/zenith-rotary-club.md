# Zenith Rotary Club

A rival service organization to the Zenith Boosters' Club, part of the international Rotary movement. Its visiting secretary attended the Boosters' Club annual election luncheon, demonstrating the competitive yet collegial relationship among Zenith's business networking organizations.

## Description

The Zenith Rotary Club was one of several service organizations — alongside the Boosters, the Kiwanis, and others — that structured the social and business life of Zenith's commercial class. Its visiting secretary attended the Boosters' Club's March luncheon to address the members, an occasion that highlighted both the rivalry and the fraternal bonds among the city's business organizations (PN 021-016). The secretary's claim to distinction — "State Motor Car License Number 5" — reveals the status markers valued in this world (PN 021-016–021-017).

## Social Function

The Rotary Club served as a parallel network to the Boosters' Club, offering overlapping but distinct opportunities for business contact and social recognition. The secretary's speech, in which he called for "a cheer for the Boosters and Rotarians and the Kiwanis all together," underscores the interconnected nature of Zenith's civic organizations — they were rivals for members and prestige yet united in promoting the ethos of boosterism (PN 021-017). Babbitt admired the secretary's low license number as a mark of status, reflecting his own preoccupation with visible signs of importance (PN 021-017).

## Key Members

- The visiting Rotary secretary — owner of State Motor Car License Number 5, described as "a rival organization" representative who addressed the Boosters at their election luncheon (PN 021-016–021-017)

## Key Scenes

- **The Boosters' election luncheon** — The Rotary secretary addresses the Boosters at their most important meeting of the year, exchanging friendly gibes about the competition between organizations (PN 021-016–021-017)
- **Babbitt's real estate board speech** — Babbitt references Rotarians as exemplars of the "Regular Guy" booster spirit, alongside Kiwanis Club members (PN 014-023, 014-034)

## Satirical Significance

The Rotary Club exemplifies Lewis's critique of the standardization of American civic life. The organization is virtually indistinguishable from the Boosters or Kiwanis — all serve the same function of business networking disguised as "service," all employ the same rhetoric of pep and good-fellowship. That the visiting secretary's most prized possession is a low motor-vehicle license number — a purely arbitrary status marker — reveals the emptiness of the values these organizations promote. The Rotary is one more interchangeable component in the machinery of Zenith's booster culture.
