# The Local Election Campaign

The mayoral campaign that pitted [[seneca-doane]], a labor-backed lawyer and State University graduate, against Lucas Prout, a mattress-manufacturer "with a perfect record for sanity" (014-002), in which [[george-babbitt]] discovered his gifts as a campaign orator — and the limits of his own political imagination.

## Background

The autumn of the campaign saw Mr. W. G. Harding appointed President of the United States, "but Zenith was less interested in the national campaign than in the local election" (014-002). This hierarchy of interest — local booster politics mattering more than national governance — is characteristic of Zenith's inward-facing civic culture.

Seneca Doane, "though he was a lawyer and a graduate of the State University, was candidate for mayor of Zenith on an alarming labor ticket" (014-002). The concessive "though" encapsulates the establishment's bewilderment: education and professional standing are supposed to immunize a man against labor sympathies. To oppose Doane, "the Democrats and Republicans united on Lucas Prout, a mattress-manufacturer with a perfect record for sanity" (014-002). The fusion ticket — two parties that ostensibly compete for power combining to defeat a common threat — exposes the genuine political divide in Zenith: not Democrat versus Republican but booster versus dissenter.

Mr. Prout's support base is revealing: "the banks, the Chamber of Commerce, all the decent newspapers, and George F. Babbitt" (014-002). The list moves from institutions to a specific name without transition, as though Babbitt's endorsement carries the same institutional weight.

## Key Elements

- **Babbitt's precinct leadership** — Babbitt serves as precinct-leader on Floral Heights, but "his district was safe and he longed for stouter battling" (014-002). His convention paper having given him "the beginning of a reputation for oratory," the Republican-Democratic Central Committee sends him to the Seventh Ward and South Zenith to address "small audiences of workmen and clerks, and wives uneasy with their new votes" (014-002). The detail about "wives uneasy with their new votes" is historically significant: the Nineteenth Amendment had recently granted women suffrage, and the political establishment was uncertain how to address this new constituency.

- **Babbitt's campaign faith** — Lewis depicts Babbitt's political convictions with devastating economy: "He had faith; he was certain that if Lincoln were alive, he would be electioneering for Mr. W. G. Harding — unless he came to Zenith and electioneered for Lucas Prout" (014-003). The conditional clause — Lincoln might support Prout if he came to Zenith — captures the solipsism of local booster thinking: even Abraham Lincoln would recognize that Zenith's mattress-manufacturer deserves the presidency of a city. Babbitt "did not confuse audiences by silly subtleties; Prout represented honest industry, Seneca Doane represented whining laziness, and you could take your choice" (014-003).

- **The rewards of campaigning** — Babbitt's efforts earn him local fame: "He acquired a fame enduring for weeks" (014-002). The temporal qualifier "enduring for weeks" is characteristically acerbic. Headlines indicate that "George F. Babbitt had addressed Cheering Throng" and "Distinguished Man of Affairs had pointed out the Fallacies of Doane" (014-002). A photograph appears in the Sunday rotogravure with the caption "Leaders of Zenith Finance and Commerce Who Back Prout" (014-002).

## Satirical Significance

The campaign's central satirical target is the emptiness of booster political discourse. Babbitt's certainty that Lincoln would campaign for Harding — a man whose administration would become synonymous with corruption — is offered without irony. His campaign method consists of reducing all political complexity to a binary: "Prout represented honest industry, Seneca Doane represented whining laziness, and you could take your choice" (014-003).

Lewis's parenthetical observation that Babbitt "almost liked common workmen. He wanted them to be well paid, and able to afford high rents — though, naturally, they must not interfere with the reasonable profits of stockholders" (014-003) distills the economic logic of booster progressivism: workers deserve prosperity, but only within parameters that preserve existing distributions of wealth and power.

The unified Democratic-Republican ticket is perhaps the most damning detail. In Zenith, the two-party system collapses into a single establishment party whenever genuinely redistributive politics appears. Doane's "alarming labor ticket" (014-002) represents the one thing the system cannot assimilate: the possibility that working people might govern themselves.

## Consequences

Babbitt's campaign experience transforms his self-conception. The photograph in the rotogravure and the headlines about "Cheering Throngs" give him a taste of public recognition that his real-estate career has never provided. His discovery that he is "a natural orator" (014-003) feeds ambitions that will later surface in his convention address and his brief, abortive rebellion.

The campaign also establishes Seneca Doane as a persistent figure in Babbitt's moral landscape — the dissenter whose very existence calls the booster consensus into question. When Babbitt later defends Doane at the Athletic Club, the roots of that defiance lie in this campaign, where he first encountered the radical lawyer as a political reality rather than an abstraction.
