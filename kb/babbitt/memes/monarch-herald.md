# Monarch Herald

A newspaper published in Monarch, Zenith's chief commercial rival among midwestern cities. Babbitt encountered the *Monarch Herald* during the State Association of Real Estate Boards convention, using it to find entertainment for the evening.

## Description

The *Monarch Herald* appears in the novel as the local newspaper of Monarch, a city of comparable size and ambition to Zenith. During the S.A.R.E.B. convention, Babbitt and his fellow delegates had dinner in the Moorish Grillroom of the Hotel Sedgwick. After a round of teasing the Italian waiter, "In the _Monarch Herald_, Babbitt found an advertisement which he read aloud, to applause and laughter," for a burlesque show at the Old Colony Theatre featuring the "Wrollicking Wrens" [^013-065].

The advertisement, reproduced at length in the novel, promises "the bonniest bevy of beauteous bathing babes in burlesque" and promises that attendees "will get 111% on your kale in this fun-fest." Babbitt's dramatic reading of this advertisement to his companions represents one of the high points of their evening's entertainment [^013-065][^013-069].

## Social Function

The *Monarch Herald* functions in the novel primarily as a vehicle for burlesque advertising — a print artifact of Monarch's commercial culture. That Babbitt turns to the newspaper not for news but for amusement listings underscores Lewis's portrait of the businessman's relationship to print media: the newspaper is a consumer guide, not a source of civic information. The paper's name, combining "Monarch" (a royal title) with "Herald" (a messenger), echoes the boosterish naming conventions of the era's provincial press.

## Key Scenes

- **Dinner at the Hotel Sedgwick** — After a boisterous dinner, Babbitt discovers the Old Colony Theatre advertisement in the _Monarch Herald_ and reads it aloud to the amusement of his fellow delegates. The moment exemplifies the convention's atmosphere of forced jollity [^013-065].

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the *Monarch Herald* to satirize the cultural barrenness of middle-American booster cities. The newspaper's name suggests grandeur, but its pages yield burlesque advertisements. The extended quotation of the ad — with its mangled syntax ("Shake the Old Dogs to the WROLLICKING WRENS") and crude sales pitch — is a miniature of the commercial culture Lewis saw dominating cities like Zenith and Monarch. The businessmen's delight in the ad reveals that their sensibility is no more elevated than the copy they mock.
