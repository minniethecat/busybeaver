# California Building

An office building opposite Babbitt's workplace in the Reeves Building, whose three-story height — one floor taller than Babbitt's own — becomes an emblem of his chronic dissatisfaction with his professional circumstances.

## Description

The California Building is introduced through Babbitt's habitual observation of it from his office window. "As always he noted that the California Building across the way was three stories, in contrast with the Reeves Building and its two" (005-008). This daily ritual of comparison — his building against the California Building — transforms commercial architecture into a measure of personal inadequacy. The California Building is not described in any further architectural detail; its significance is purely relative, as the taller neighbor that diminishes Babbitt's own professional station by comparison.

The phrase "as always he noted" (005-008) is telling: this is not a fresh observation but a recurring one, suggesting that Babbitt has measured himself against the California Building countless times. The building's name — evoking the distant, glamorous West — adds a layer of aspirational geography to the architectural comparison.

## Social Function

The California Building functions in the novel as a spatial correlative of Babbitt's perpetual sense of insufficiency. It is not that the building is remarkable — it is merely three stories — but that Babbitt's building is two. The one-story difference materializes the gap between what Babbitt is and what he feels he should be. This is the architecture of envy in a city where every structure is also a status claim.

The California Building also represents the random distribution of prestige in Zenith's commercial geography: Babbitt's building could as easily have been three stories and the California two, but the arbitrary fact of height becomes freighted with meaning in a competitive business culture. The building is a blank surface onto which Babbitt projects his anxieties.

## Key Scenes

- **Babbitt at his office window** — "As always," Babbitt notes the California Building's three-story superiority to his own two-story Reeves Building (005-008), a moment that crystallizes his ambient professional dissatisfaction

## Satirical Significance

Lewis uses the California Building as an almost absurdly compressed symbol of Babbitt's spiritual condition. A one-story height differential between two undistinguished commercial buildings becomes a daily meditation on failure. The joke is not that Babbitt works in a two-story building — an entirely respectable arrangement — but that his mind automatically converts a neutral fact of the built environment into evidence of his own inadequacy. The California Building, by simply existing across the street, indicts him. This is boosterism's psychological cost: in a culture where everything is ranked, everything becomes a ranking.
