## Overview

The First Christian Church of Monarch appears during the annual convention of the state real-estate association, held that year in Monarch (013-004). Its pastor — "a large man with a long damp frontal lock" — delivers the convention's opening invocation, a prayer that conflates commerce with Christianity and treats the gathering of real-estate men as a matter of divine interest.

## The Convention Prayer

At the opening session of the real-estate convention, following a welcome by the mayor of Monarch, the pastor of the First Christian Church takes the podium. His prayer is remarkable for its content:

> The pastor of the First Christian Church of Monarch, a large man with a long damp frontal lock, informed God that the real-estate men were here now. (013-036)

The verb "informed" is Lewis's satirical masterstroke — the pastor does not pray to God in supplication; he notifies God, as though the Almighty were a bureau chief who needed to be kept apprised of convention schedules. The prayer's implicit theology is one of partnership between church and commerce. The pastor does not bless the real-estate men in the name of a higher calling; he simply acknowledges their presence as an event of cosmic significance. Religion, in this formulation, exists to sanctify business, not to critique it.

## The Pastor

The pastor is described as "a large man with a long damp frontal lock" (013-036) — a physical description that reduces a spiritual leader to a caricature. The "damp frontal lock" suggests physical exertion, perhaps nerves, or the heat of the room, and undermines the dignity of his office. Like [[John-Jennison-Drew|Dr. John Jennison Drew]] of [[chatham-road-presbyterian-church]], the Monarch pastor is less a shepherd of souls than a functionary in the civic-religious apparatus that authenticates the proceedings of the business class. Both men serve the same function: to lend a veneer of piety to gatherings whose true purpose is commercial.

## Context: The Monarch Convention

The state real-estate convention in Monarch is a significant event in [[George-Babbitt|Babbitt]]'s professional life. It opens with the mayor's welcome and the pastor's prayer, followed by a banquet from the Monarch Chamber of Commerce and an afternoon reception by the Manufacturers' Association (013-036, 013-039).

The convention is held in Monarch, "Zenith's chief rival among the cities of the state" (013-004), and the church's participation reflects Monarch's civic pride in hosting the event. The city mobilizes its institutions — government, church, commerce — to impress the visiting delegates, a coordinated performance of boosterism in which the pastor is one performer among many. Babbitt and his fellow delegates arrive at Monarch aboard the midnight train (013-019), and the opening ceremonies represent the city of Monarch staging its best face for the visitors — the mayor, the pastor, the banquet, the reception, all lined up like floats in a civic parade.

The delegates receive gifts from the host city: leather notebooks for the men, chrysanthemums for the ladies (013-039). The pastor's prayer fits seamlessly into this economy of reciprocal gestures — it is a gift of spiritual authentication in exchange for the delegates' presence.

## Satirical Significance

The First Christian Church of Monarch represents, in miniature, Lewis's critique of the alliance between organized religion and commercial culture in the American city. The pastor's prayer does not challenge the real-estate men to consider the ethics of their profession; it does not invoke justice, charity, or the teachings of Christ. It simply registers their presence for the divine record, as though God were a notary public. This is religion stripped of prophetic content — a ceremonial accessory to business, as interchangeable as the chrysanthemums presented to the ladies. The church's role is to bless what is, not to imagine what might be.
