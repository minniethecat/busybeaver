# Elks Club

One of the fraternal organizations — along with the Boosters, the Athletic Club, the Moose, the Red Men, and the Knights of Columbus — that structure masculine social life in Zenith. The Elks represent the ritual dimension of business-class camaraderie: initiation rites, secret handshakes, and the performance of brotherhood that substitutes for genuine intimacy.

## Leadership and Power

Vergil Gunch, the coal-dealer, was equally powerful in the Elks and in the Boosters' Club, embodying the overlapping leadership of Zenith's fraternal organizations (008-031). This concentration of power was typical: the same dozen men chaired the committees, led the initiations, and set the tone for every major fraternal body in the city. The Elks lodge was not separate from the business establishment — it was the business establishment in ceremonial dress.

## Ritual and Social Function

The weekly Elks meeting followed a predictable rhythm: a ritual opening with secret passwords and grips, a report from the secretary, a perfunctory discussion of lodge business, and then the real business of the evening — cigars, poker, and the exchange of commercial tips disguised as friendship. The initiation ceremony, with its mock-solemn pledges of brotherly love, was a performance that allowed businessmen to feel noble while doing exactly what they would have done anyway: networking.

## The Boosters' Convention

The Elks lodge hosted the annual Boosters' Club convention events, where out-of-town delegates were entertained with the ritual bonhomie that characterized Zenith's civic life (013-039). The lodge hall, decorated with elk heads and American flags, became a theater of masculine belonging: handshakes that were also credit checks, toasts that were also sales pitches, fellowship that was also market research.

## Babbitt's Membership

Babbitt himself was a member of the Elks as part of the expected roster of club affiliations for a solid citizen of Zenith (001-040). He attended meetings with the dutiful regularity of a man ticking boxes on the checklist of respectability. The Elks, like the Boosters and the Athletic Club, demanded nothing of him but his presence and his dues — and in return, they confirmed his identity as a Regular Fellow, a Good Egg, a man who Belonged.

## The Fraternal Network

The overlapping membership of the Elks, Moose, Red Men, and Knights of Columbus created what Babbitt's world called "good-fellowship" — a network of mutual obligation that greased the wheels of commerce. A man who met you at the Elks on Wednesday would remember you at the bank on Thursday. The lodges were the infrastructure of trust in a city where business was done on handshakes, and every handshake was rehearsed in a lodge hall.
