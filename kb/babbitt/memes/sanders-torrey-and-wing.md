# Sanders, Torrey and Wing

A rival real estate firm in Zenith that poses a competitive threat to the Babbitt-Thompson Realty Company, embodying the business rivalries, professional anxieties, and transactional nature of commercial relationships that underlie George Babbitt's public confidence.

## Description

Sanders, Torrey and Wing is one of the real estate firms operating in Zenith's competitive property market. The firm's three-name partnership follows the conventional format for professional firms of the era and suggests an established, multi-partner operation with substantial resources. The firm competes directly with Babbitt-Thompson for the most lucrative development opportunities in the city, particularly those involving the politically connected Street Traction Company (032-027).

## Competition with Babbitt-Thompson

The firm first becomes a source of anxiety for Babbitt when his business partner, Henry Thompson, informs him that the Street Traction Company officials—long-standing Babbitt-Thompson clients—are planning a new real-estate deal and have selected Sanders, Torrey and Wing to handle the transaction instead of Babbitt-Thompson (032-027). Thompson's warning is explicit: "One little rumor about your being a crank would do more to ruin this business than all the plots and stuff that these fools write about" (032-027). The loss of the Traction Company's business represents a significant professional blow, one that Thompson attributes directly to Babbitt's growing reputation as a political nonconformist.

The novel suggests that Babbitt's independent thinking during his brief rebellion has made him untrustworthy in the eyes of Zenith's business establishment, and Sanders, Torrey and Wing is the immediate beneficiary of his diminished standing. The Street Traction executives' willingness to switch firms underscores how business "loyalty" in Zenith is purely transactional — governed by the latest deal and the safest political alignment rather than lasting relationships.

## Miss McGoun's Defection

A week after Miss McGoun, Babbitt's long-time secretary, left his employ, he learned that she had gone to work for Sanders, Torrey and Wing (032-030). The defection is doubly wounding: it deprives Babbitt of an employee who understood his business methods, and it provides his rivals with inside knowledge of his operations. Miss McGoun's departure — from a firm whose fortunes were declining to one on the ascent — mirrors the larger pattern of Zenith business culture, where professional relationships follow the trajectory of power. The episode underscores how thoroughly Babbitt's business standing is unraveling as his social conformity weakens.

## Business Recovery

Even as Babbitt's fortunes decline, the competitive landscape shifts again. Late in the novel, Jake Offutt — the political fixer who served as nominal developer of the Glen Oriole project — reports that the "Traction Bunch" are dissatisfied with how Sanders, Torrey and Wing handled their last deal and are willing to negotiate with Babbitt-Thompson once more (034-029). Offutt's language — "It is Needham's comfortable little idea to sneak in on them" (034-031) — describes the Street Traction's purchasing agent approaching Babbitt for a "secret purchase" of land for a car barn. This reversal confirms the loyalty-free, deal-by-deal nature of Zenith business culture, where firms rise and fall based on the latest transaction rather than lasting relationships. The day after Offutt's report, Babbitt "went to see the purchasing-agent of the Street Traction Company and they made plans for the secret purchase" (034-031), demonstrating that even after his social punishment, Babbitt's commercial skills remained valued.

## Key Scenes

- **The Street Traction deal lost** — Thompson warns Babbitt that Sanders, Torrey and Wing have been chosen for the Street Traction deal, attributing the loss to rumors about Babbitt's political nonconformity (032-027)
- **Miss McGoun's defection** — Babbitt learns that his former secretary has joined Sanders, Torrey and Wing, compounding his business losses (032-030)
- **The reversal** — Jake Offutt reports the Traction Bunch are dissatisfied with Sanders's work, opening the door for Babbitt-Thompson to reclaim the business (034-029, 034-031)

## Key Members

- **[Partners unnamed]** — Sanders, Torrey, and Wing themselves are never characterized individually; their collective name on the firm's letterhead is their entire identity in the novel, suggesting the impersonal, institutional nature of competition in Zenith's business world

## Satirical Significance

Sanders, Torrey and Wing functions in the novel primarily as a narrative device — the rival firm that crystallizes Babbitt's professional anxieties. The partners are never characterized individually; they exist only as a corporate name, a faceless competitor whose success at Babbitt-Thompson's expense illustrates the impersonal, institutional nature of business competition in Zenith. The firm's victory in securing the Street Traction deal — and its subsequent failure to satisfy that client — suggests that success in Zenith's business culture is less about competence than about alignment with the prevailing political winds. The partners' anonymity is itself satirical: in a novel full of named, vividly drawn businessmen, the rivals at Sanders, Torrey and Wing are defined entirely by their competitive relationship to the protagonist, not by any individual qualities.
