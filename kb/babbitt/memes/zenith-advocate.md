# Zenith Advocate-Times

## Description

The *Advocate-Times* is Zenith's dominant newspaper, delivered to Babbitt's Floral Heights door each morning as part of the ritual of respectable citizenship. With its morning edition, evening companion (*Evening Advocate*), and Sunday gravure section, the paper blankets the city with a unified message of commercial optimism. The *Advocate-Times* is not merely a news organ but an ideological apparatus: it prints the statements that the business class wants printed, suppresses dissent, and employs society editors and managing editors who serve as unofficial publicity agents for the booster establishment.

## Social Function

The *Advocate-Times* serves as Zenith's information gatekeeper and consensus-manufacturing engine. Babbitt himself relies on the paper as a source of ready-made opinions: he "could not have produced an original opinion" (004-023) on the labor disputes covered by the *Advocate-Times*, yet he reads its headlines as an "exhilarating drug" (002-017) that confirms his worldview. The paper functions as what Lewis implicitly diagnoses as a propaganda machine for the commercial class — publishing booster rhetoric, providing favorable coverage to Babbitt's real-estate speeches, and employing figures like Kenneth Escott to place "insinuating items about neighborliness and the Bible" (017-032) that advance business interests under the guise of news.

## Key Scenes

- **Morning Ritual (Chapter I)**: Babbitt's day begins with "the rolled-up *Advocate*" thumping against the front door (001-011), the paper as regular as a heartbeat in the Floral Heights routine.
- **The Bulletin-Board (Chapter XII)**: Babbitt "compromised on watching the *Advocate-Times* bulletin-board" (012-006) — the public display of headlines serving as a kind of civic altar where citizens gather to receive the authorized version of events.
- **Babbitt's Speech Coverage (Chapter XIII)**: The managing editor agrees to cover Babbitt's annual address to the real-estate board, and the speech appears on the third page — a placement that Babbitt finds gratifying (013-045). The coverage itself, reported "with unabridged and understanding reports" (014-011), functions as boosterism by another name.
- **The Frink Lecture Coverage (Chapter XIV)**: The *Advocate-Times* "reported the lecture so fully" (014-038) on T. Cholmondeley Frink's appearance, treating the poet-advertising-man's banalities as significant cultural events.
- **Gravure Section**: The Sunday edition's rotogravure section features photographs of Zenith's leading citizens, including Babbitt himself (014-003), confirming status through the medium of newsprint.
- **Comic Strips**: The *Evening Advocate* provides Babbitt's "favorite literature and art" — the comic strips (006-031) — suggesting that even the newspaper's entertainment function serves to pacify rather than to provoke thought.

## Satirical Significance

The *Advocate-Times* embodies the complete fusion of journalism and boosterism. The paper does not report on Zenith's business establishment from the outside; it is part of that establishment, its editors and society writers moving in the same social circles as the businessmen they cover. When the paper reports Babbitt's speech "with unabridged and understanding reports" (014-011), the word "understanding" carries ironic weight — the coverage is sympathetic because the paper shares the speaker's assumptions. Lewis anticipates mid-20th-century critiques of media concentration and manufactured consent: the *Advocate-Times* is a one-newspaper town's sole source of authorized reality, and it uses that monopoly to enforce conformity.
