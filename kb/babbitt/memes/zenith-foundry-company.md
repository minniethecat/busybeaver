# Zenith Foundry Company

## Description

The Zenith Foundry Company was a manufacturing firm in Zenith, one of the many industrial concerns that formed the city's commercial backbone. The company's secretary was consulted by George Babbitt and his father-in-law, Henry Thompson, regarding "an interesting artistic project—a cast-iron fence for Linden Lane Cemetery"(006-006). The brief mention of the foundry is characteristic of how Lewis uses business transactions to sketch the dense commercial web of Zenith: even a cemetery fence becomes an occasion for negotiation, discount-seeking, and the collision of old and new business styles.

> "They talked to the secretary of the Zenith Foundry Company about an interesting artistic project—a cast-iron fence for Linden Lane Cemetery." (006-006)

## Key Reference

The sole direct reference to the Zenith Foundry Company occurs during a morning of business calls undertaken by Babbitt and Thompson (Chapter VI). The visit to the foundry is sandwiched between negotiations with the Zeeco Motor Company and a real-estate deal—a typical Babbitt morning in which every encounter, however mundane, is charged with the rituals of boostering and commercial reciprocity.

The foundry itself may be the source of the "belching glare of a distant foundry, wiping out the sharp-edged stars"(017-024) that Babbitt observes one evening—suggesting that the company's industrial operations are part of the nocturnal landscape of Zenith, their furnace glow a counterpoint to the domestic tranquility of Floral Heights.

## Satirical Significance

The Zenith Foundry Company episode is a small but telling illustration of Lewis's satire of American business culture. The project—a cast-iron fence for a cemetery—is described by its promoters as an "artistic project"(006-006), a phrase that conflates commercial manufacturing with aesthetic aspiration in the characteristic fashion of Zenith boosterism. The irony is deepened by the fact that Babbitt and Thompson, two men wholly indifferent to art, are the ones pursuing this "artistic" endeavor—motivated not by beauty but by the prospect of a transaction.
