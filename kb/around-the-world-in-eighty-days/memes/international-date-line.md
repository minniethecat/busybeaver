# International Date Line

The imaginary line running roughly along the 180th meridian, where travelers crossing eastward subtract one day from the calendar and those crossing westward add one — the geographical fact that transforms the climax of [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]'s journey from defeat into triumph.

## The Mechanism

When a traveler moves eastward around the globe — "in the face of the sun" — each day is fractionally shorter than twenty-four hours. Over the full 360 degrees of longitude, the cumulative effect is exactly one full day gained. Conversely, westward travel loses a day. The International Date Line is the formal boundary where this adjustment is applied, though in Verne's time it was not yet universally standardized.

## Role in the Novel

Fogg does not plan to use the date line to his advantage. He follows the fastest available connections eastward without calculating the effect on the calendar. As a result, he arrives in London believing himself to be five minutes late and defeated — only to discover, when [[jean-passepartout|Passepartout]] is sent to summon a clergyman for the wedding, that it is Saturday, not Sunday. Fogg has gained a full day.

The novel's conclusion records that Fogg "had accomplished the journey round the world in eighty days" (PN: 031-136). Without the eastward gain of one day, the journey would have taken eighty-one days by the calendar — and the wager would have been lost.

## Historical Context

In 1872, the International Date Line was not yet formally established by international agreement (that would come with the 1884 International Meridian Conference). However, the practical phenomenon was well understood by navigators. Ships crossing the Pacific had long observed the convention of adjusting their calendar at the 180th meridian. Verne exploited this known but not universally appreciated fact to engineer one of literature's most celebrated plot twists.
