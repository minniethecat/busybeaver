# Brigham Young

Brigham Young (1801–1877) was the second president of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints and the founder of Salt Lake City. In *Around the World in Eighty Days*, he is referenced during the train passage through Utah, where the Mormon settlement he established provides a spectacle of exotic Americana for the travelling party.

## Role in the Novel

Brigham Young does not appear in person in the novel. He is referenced during Section 22–23, when the train carrying Fogg and his companions crosses Utah Territory and the passengers observe the Mormon settlements. Young is invoked as the leader and prophet of the Latter-day Saints — a figure of both admiration and controversy in the 1870s American imagination. ([023-031], [023-039])

## Biography / Background

Brigham Young succeeded Joseph Smith as leader of the Mormon church after Smith's martyrdom, leading the Latter-day Saints on their epic migration westward to the Salt Lake Valley in 1847. Under his leadership, Salt Lake City grew from a desert encampment into a thriving settlement. By the time Jules Verne was writing *Around the World in Eighty Days* (published 1872), Young was a figure of international notoriety — admired for his organizational genius and controversial for the Mormon practice of polygamy. ([023-031])

## Key Actions (as referenced in novel)

- **Founding Salt Lake City**: The novel describes the Mormon capital that Young established, noting its orderly streets and prosperous appearance — evidence of his organizational capability. ([023-031])
- **Facing legal challenge**: The novel references the U.S. government's attempt to "imprison Brigham Young on a charge of rebellion and polygamy," reflecting the real-world legal conflicts between the Mormon leadership and federal authorities in the 1870s. ([022-180])

## Character Significance

Brigham Young appears in the novel not as a character but as a symbol — part of Verne's panoramic tour of American peculiarities. The Mormon settlement at Salt Lake City is one of several spectacles (alongside the buffalo herds, the Rocky Mountains, and the Pacific Railroad itself) that make the crossing of America an education in the continent's diversity. Young represents the intersection of religion, politics, and frontier ambition that defined the American West. Verne's treatment is characteristically even-handed: the novel neither endorses nor condemns Mormonism, presenting it instead as one more remarkable fact about the New World.

> Joseph Smith, the inspired prophet, Brigham Young, his successor, had founded the new Zion in the Utah territory. ([023-031])
