[028-001] [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]] learned all this in consulting his “[[bradshaws-guide|Bradshaw]],” which gave him

[028-002] the daily movements of the transatlantic steamers.

[028-003] [[passepartout|Passepartout]] was crushed; it overwhelmed him to lose the boat by

[028-004] three-quarters of an hour. It was his fault, for, instead of helping

[028-005] his master, he had not ceased putting obstacles in his path! And when

[028-006] he recalled all the incidents of the tour, when he counted up the sums

[028-007] expended in pure loss and on his own account, when he thought that the

[028-008] immense stake, added to the heavy charges of this useless journey,

[028-009] would completely ruin Mr. Fogg, he overwhelmed himself with bitter

[028-010] self-accusations. Mr. Fogg, however, did not reproach him; and, on

[028-011] leaving the Cunard pier, only said: “We will consult about what is best

[028-012] to-morrow. Come.”

[028-013] The party crossed the Hudson in the Jersey City ferryboat, and drove in

[028-014] a carriage to the St. Nicholas Hotel, on Broadway. Rooms were engaged,

[028-015] and the night passed, briefly to Phileas Fogg, who slept profoundly,

[028-016] but very long to [[aouda|Aouda]] and the others, whose agitation did not permit

[028-017] them to rest.

[028-018] The next day was the 12th of December. From seven in the morning of the

[028-019] 12th to a quarter before nine in the evening of the 21st there were

[028-020] nine days, thirteen hours, and forty-five minutes. If Phileas Fogg had

[028-021] left in the “China,” one of the fastest steamers on the Atlantic, he

[028-022] would have reached [[liverpool|Liverpool]], and then [[london|London]], within the period agreed

[028-023] upon.

[028-024] Mr. Fogg left the hotel alone, after giving Passepartout instructions

[028-025] to await his return, and inform Aouda to be ready at an instant’s

[028-026] notice. He proceeded to the banks of the Hudson, and looked about among

[028-027] the vessels moored or anchored in the river, for any that were about to

[028-028] depart. Several had departure signals, and were preparing to put to sea

[028-029] at morning tide; for in this immense and admirable port there is not

[028-030] one day in a hundred that vessels do not set out for every quarter of

[028-031] the globe. But they were mostly sailing vessels, of which, of course,

[028-032] Phileas Fogg could make no use.

[028-033] He seemed about to give up all hope, when he espied, anchored at the

[028-034] Battery, a [[the-telegraph|cable]]’s length off at most, a trading vessel, with a screw,

[028-035] well-shaped, whose funnel, puffing a cloud of smoke, indicated that she

[028-036] was getting ready for departure.

[028-037] Phileas Fogg hailed a boat, got into it, and soon found himself on

[028-038] board the “Henrietta,” iron-hulled, wood-built above. He ascended to

[028-039] the deck, and asked for [[captain-speedy|the captain]], who forthwith presented himself.

[028-040] He was a man of fifty, a sort of sea-wolf, with big eyes, a complexion

[028-041] of oxidised copper, red hair and thick neck, and a growling voice.

[028-042] “The captain?” asked Mr. Fogg.

[028-043] “I am the captain.”

[028-044] “I am Phileas Fogg, of London.”

[028-045] “And I am Andrew Speedy, of Cardiff.”

[028-046] “You are going to put to sea?”

[028-047] “In an hour.”

[028-048] “You are bound for—”

[028-049] “Bordeaux.”

[028-050] “And your cargo?”

[028-051] “No freight. Going in ballast.”

[028-052] “Have you any passengers?”

[028-053] “No passengers. Never have passengers. Too much in the way.”

[028-054] “Is your vessel a swift one?”

[028-055] “Between eleven and twelve knots. The “Henrietta,” well known.”

[028-056] “Will you carry me and three other persons to Liverpool?”

[028-057] “To Liverpool? Why not to China?”

[028-058] “I said Liverpool.”

[028-059] “No!”

[028-060] “No?”

[028-061] “No. I am setting out for Bordeaux, and shall go to Bordeaux.”

[028-062] “Money is no object?”

[028-063] “None.”

[028-064] The captain spoke in a tone which did not admit of a reply.

[028-065] “But the owners of the ‘Henrietta’—” resumed Phileas Fogg.

[028-066] “The owners are myself,” replied the captain. “The vessel belongs to

[028-067] me.”

[028-068] “I will freight it for you.”

[028-069] “No.”

[028-070] “I will buy it of you.”

[028-071] “No.”

[028-072] Phileas Fogg did not betray the least disappointment; but the situation

[028-073] was a grave one. It was not at [[new-york|New York]] as at [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], nor with the

[028-074] captain of the “Henrietta” as with the captain of the “Tankadere.” Up

[028-075] to this time money had smoothed away every obstacle. Now money failed.

[028-076] Still, some means must be found to cross the Atlantic on a boat, unless

[028-077] by balloon—which would have been venturesome, besides not being capable

[028-078] of being put in practice. It seemed that Phileas Fogg had an idea, for

[028-079] he said to the captain, “Well, will you carry me to Bordeaux?”

[028-080] “No, not if you paid me two hundred dollars.”

[028-081] “I offer you two thousand.”

[028-082] “Apiece?”

[028-083] “Apiece.”

[028-084] “And there are four of you?”

[028-085] “Four.”

[028-086] Captain Speedy began to scratch his head. There were eight thousand

[028-087] dollars to gain, without changing his [[around-the-world-route|route]]; for which it was well

[028-088] worth conquering the repugnance he had for all kinds of passengers.

[028-089] Besides, passengers at two thousand dollars are no longer passengers,

[028-090] but valuable merchandise. “I start at nine o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]],” said Captain

[028-091] Speedy, simply. “Are you and your party ready?”

[028-092] “We will be on board at nine o’clock,” replied, no less simply, Mr.

[028-093] Fogg.

[028-094] It was half-past eight. To disembark from the “Henrietta,” jump into a

[028-095] hack, hurry to the St. Nicholas, and return with Aouda, Passepartout,

[028-096] and even the inseparable [[detective-fix|Fix]] was the work of a brief time, and was

[028-097] performed by Mr. Fogg with the coolness which never abandoned him. They

[028-098] were on board when the “Henrietta” made ready to weigh anchor.

[028-099] When Passepartout heard what this last voyage was going to cost, he

[028-100] uttered a prolonged “Oh!” which extended throughout his vocal gamut.

[028-101] As for [[fix-detective|Fix]], he said to himself that the Bank of England would certainly

[028-102] not come out of this affair well indemnified. When they reached

[028-103] England, even if Mr. Fogg did not throw some handfuls of bank-bills

[028-104] into the sea, more than seven thousand pounds would have been spent!

[028-105] CHAPTER XXXIII.

[028-106] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG SHOWS HIMSELF EQUAL TO THE OCCASION

[028-107] An hour after, the “Henrietta” passed the lighthouse which marks the

[028-108] entrance of the Hudson, turned the point of Sandy Hook, and put to sea.

[028-109] During the day she skirted Long Island, passed Fire Island, and

[028-110] directed her course rapidly eastward.

[028-111] At noon the next day, a man mounted the bridge to ascertain the

[028-112] vessel’s position. It might be thought that this was Captain Speedy.

[028-113] Not the least in the world. It was Phileas Fogg, Esquire. As for

[028-114] Captain Speedy, he was shut up in his cabin under lock and key, and was

[028-115] uttering loud cries, which signified an anger at once pardonable and

[028-116] excessive.

[028-117] What had happened was very simple. Phileas Fogg wished to go to

[028-118] Liverpool, but the captain would not carry him there. Then Phileas Fogg

[028-119] had taken passage for Bordeaux, and, during the thirty hours he had

[028-120] been on board, had so shrewdly managed with his banknotes that the

[028-121] sailors and stokers, who were only an occasional crew, and were not on

[028-122] the best terms with the captain, went over to him in a body. This was

[028-123] why Phileas Fogg was in command instead of Captain Speedy; why the

[028-124] captain was a prisoner in his cabin; and why, in short, the “Henrietta”

[028-125] was directing her course towards Liverpool. It was very clear, to see

[028-126] Mr. Fogg manage the craft, that he had been a sailor.

[028-127] How the [[spirit-of-adventure|adventure]] ended will be seen anon. Aouda was anxious, though

[028-128] she said nothing. As for Passepartout, he thought Mr. Fogg’s manœuvre

[028-129] simply glorious. The captain had said “between eleven and twelve

[028-130] knots,” and the “Henrietta” confirmed his prediction.

[028-131] If, then—for there were “ifs” still—the sea did not become too

[028-132] boisterous, if the wind did not veer round to the east, if no accident

[028-133] happened to the boat or its machinery, the “Henrietta” might cross the

[028-134] three thousand miles from New York to Liverpool in the nine days,

[028-135] between the 12th and the 21st of December. It is true that, once

[028-136] arrived, the affair on board the “Henrietta,” added to that of the Bank

[028-137] of England, might create more difficulties for Mr. Fogg than he

[028-138] imagined or could desire.

[028-139] During the first days, they went along smoothly enough. The sea was not

[028-140] very unpropitious, the wind seemed stationary in the north-east, the

[028-141] sails were hoisted, and the “Henrietta” ploughed across the waves like

[028-142] a real transatlantic [[steamer-travel|steamer]].

[028-143] Passepartout was delighted. His master’s last exploit, the consequences

[028-144] of which he ignored, enchanted him. Never had the crew seen so jolly

[028-145] and dexterous a fellow. He formed warm friendships with the sailors,

[028-146] and amazed them with his acrobatic feats. He thought they managed the

[028-147] vessel like gentlemen, and that the stokers fired up like heroes. His

[028-148] loquacious good-humour infected everyone. He had forgotten the past,

[028-149] its vexations and delays. He only thought of the end, so nearly

[028-150] accomplished; and sometimes he boiled over with impatience, as if

[028-151] heated by the furnaces of the “Henrietta.” Often, also, the worthy

[028-152] fellow revolved around Fix, looking at him with a keen, distrustful

[028-153] eye; but he did not speak to him, for their old intimacy no longer

[028-154] existed.

[028-155] Fix, it must be confessed, understood nothing of what was going on. The

[028-156] conquest of the “Henrietta,” the bribery of the crew, Fogg managing the

[028-157] boat like a skilled seaman, amazed and confused him. He did not know

[028-158] what to think. For, after all, a man who began by stealing fifty-five

[028-159] thousand pounds might end by stealing a vessel; and Fix was not

[028-160] unnaturally inclined to conclude that the “Henrietta” under Fogg’s

[028-161] command, was not going to Liverpool at all, but to some part of the

[028-162] world where the robber, turned into a pirate, would quietly put himself

[028-163] in safety. The conjecture was at least a plausible one, and the

[028-164] detective began to seriously regret that he had embarked on the affair.

[028-165] As for Captain Speedy, he continued to howl and growl in his cabin; and

[028-166] Passepartout, whose duty it was to carry him his meals, courageous as

[028-167] he was, took the greatest precautions. Mr. Fogg did not seem even to

[028-168] know that there was a captain on board.

[028-169] On the 13th they passed the edge of the Banks of Newfoundland, a

[028-170] dangerous locality; during the winter, especially, there are frequent

[028-171] fogs and heavy gales of wind. Ever since the evening before the

[028-172] barometer, suddenly falling, had indicated an approaching change in the

[028-173] atmosphere; and during the night the temperature varied, the cold

[028-174] became sharper, and the wind veered to the south-east.

[028-175] This was a misfortune. Mr. Fogg, in order not to deviate from his

[028-176] course, furled his sails and increased the force of the steam; but the

[028-177] vessel’s speed slackened, owing to the state of the sea, the long waves

[028-178] of which broke against the stern. She pitched violently, and this

[028-179] retarded her progress. The breeze little by little swelled into a

[028-180] tempest, and it was to be feared that the “Henrietta” might not be able

[028-181] to maintain herself upright on the waves.

[028-182] Passepartout’s visage darkened with the skies, and for two days the

[028-183] poor fellow experienced constant fright. But Phileas Fogg was a bold

[028-184] mariner, and knew how to maintain headway against the sea; and he kept

[028-185] on his course, without even decreasing his steam. The “Henrietta,” when

[028-186] she could not rise upon the waves, crossed them, swamping her deck, but

[028-187] passing safely. Sometimes the screw rose out of the water, beating its

[028-188] protruding end, when a mountain of water raised the stern above the

[028-189] waves; but the craft always kept straight ahead.

[028-190] The wind, however, did not grow as boisterous as might have been

[028-191] feared; it was not one of those tempests which burst, and rush on with

[028-192] a speed of ninety miles an hour. It continued fresh, but, unhappily, it

[028-193] remained obstinately in the south-east, rendering the sails useless.

[028-194] The 16th of December was the seventy-fifth day since Phileas Fogg’s

[028-195] departure from London, and the “Henrietta” had not yet been seriously

[028-196] delayed. Half of the voyage was almost accomplished, and the worst

[028-197] localities had been passed. In summer, success would have been

[028-198] well-nigh certain. In winter, they were at the mercy of the bad season.

[028-199] Passepartout said nothing; but he cherished hope in secret, and

[028-200] comforted himself with the reflection that, if the wind failed them,

[028-201] they might still count on the steam.
