# Phileas Fogg

The central figure of Jules Verne's novel and the engine that drives the entire [[around-the-world-route|circumnavigation]]: a gentleman of exact habits, impenetrable reserve, and absolute integrity who undertakes the most audacious journey of the nineteenth century not out of wanderlust but because he has given his word at the [[reform-club|Reform Club]]. His [[the-wager|twenty-thousand-pound wager]] sets in motion a chain of events that touches every continent and every major character, from his devoted servant [[passepartout]] to the woman he rescues from death, [[aouda]].

## Role in the Novel

Phileas Fogg is the protagonist and narrative center of _Around the World in Eighty Days_. He first appears as a mysterious resident of No. 7 Savile Row, [[london|London]], a man of such precise habits that his neighbors know him only by his comings and goings at exactly prescribed hours. When he wagers twenty thousand pounds at the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] that he can circumnavigate the globe in eighty days, he transforms from a figure of quiet routine into the subject of international fascination. (PN: 001-158)

Fogg functions as a study in contrasts: outwardly cold and mechanical, inwardly capable of profound loyalty and courage. His journey is both a race against time and a gradual revelation of the depths concealed beneath his impassive exterior. As [[detective-fix]] learns across three continents, Fogg's apparent indifference masks a moral compass of unshakeable precision.

> "Mr. Fogg was certainly the most exact and settled man that ever existed. He was never seen to be moved by anything." (001-158)

## Biography

Fogg is a man of unknown origins — "nothing was known of his past" — who appears to have no family, no profession, and no visible means of support beyond a substantial bank account. He lives alone at No. 7 Savile Row, attended by a single servant, and divides his time between the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] (where he reads the papers and plays whist) and his precisely-managed household. (PN: 001-061)

His past is deliberately left opaque. Rumors suggest he may have traveled widely in his youth, but the narrator offers no confirmation. This opacity is central to his character: Fogg is defined entirely by his present actions, not by his history. His nationality is English, his class is gentleman, and his religion — if any — is punctuality. His membership in the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] and his dealings with the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] are the only institutional anchors in an otherwise untethered existence.

> "Was Phileas Fogg rich? Undoubtedly. But those who knew him best could not imagine how he had made his fortune." (001-075)

## Character Significance

Fogg represents the Victorian ideal of the [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] taken to its logical extreme — and subtly humanized. He begins the novel as what [[passepartout]] calls "a real machine," but the journey reveals that his mechanical exterior houses genuine moral depth. His precision is not coldness but a form of [[duty|discipline]] that enables [[courage]]; his reserve is not indifference but the self-control of a man who feels deeply but expresses sparingly.

The novel's great structural joke — that Fogg's very precision causes him to forget the date line and thus win his [[the-wager|wager]] — is also its thematic resolution. Fogg the machine is saved by the fact that even machines cannot fully account for the curvature of the earth. The human world, the novel suggests, is larger and stranger than any timetable can measure.

> "He was so exact that he was never in a hurry, and was always ready." (001-061)
