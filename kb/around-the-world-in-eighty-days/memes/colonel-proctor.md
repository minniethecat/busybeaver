# Colonel Stamp W. Proctor

The loud-mouthed American veteran whose chance encounter with Phileas Fogg on the transcontinental railway escalates from political argument to a duel — only to be interrupted by the sudden violence of a Sioux war party.

## Role in the Novel

Colonel Proctor appears during the railway journey from San Francisco to New York, in the section between the Great Salt Lake and the Sierra Nevada. A large, red-faced man with the bearing of a military officer, he dominates the train car with his loud opinions on politics, war, and the superiority of American institutions. (PN: 025-088)

When Proctor's bluster collides with Fogg's impassivity, the result is an escalating confrontation. Proctor challenges Fogg to a duel, which is arranged for the next station stop. But before the duel can proceed, a band of Sioux warriors attacks the train, abruptly transforming the social drama into a life-or-death struggle. Proctor vanishes from the narrative after the attack, his role as catalyst fulfilled.

## Key Actions

- Engages in loud political debate in the train car, drawing Fogg's silent disapproval (025-088)
- Challenges Fogg to a duel after a perceived slight (026-042)
- Prepares to fight at the next station, only for the Sioux attack to interrupt the proceedings (026-175)
- Fights alongside the other passengers during the Sioux raid (026-190)
- Disappears from the narrative after the attack — fate unresolved

## Character Significance

Proctor functions as a type — the boorish American — but also as a plot device. His interruption of the train journey with a duel creates a situation in which Fogg's composure is tested not by the impersonal obstacles of weather and distance but by the unpredictable aggression of another human being. The Sioux attack that interrupts the duel is one of Verne's most effective surprises, transforming a comic confrontation into a genuine crisis.

> "He was one of those Americans who are to be met with everywhere." (025-088)
