# Timetable

The railway and steamer timetable was the foundational document of nineteenth-century travel. Unlike earlier eras, when journeys were measured in "a few days" or "about a week," the age of steam reduced travel to precise hours and minutes. A traveller consulting Bradshaw's Guide could know, to the quarter-hour, when a steamer would depart Brindisi and when the connecting train would arrive in Calcutta.

## Definition

A timetable in the context of the novel is a published schedule of arrivals and departures for railways, steamers, and connecting conveyances. The most famous of these was *Bradshaw's Continental Railway Steam Transit and General Guide*, the red-bound volume that Fogg carries under his arm as he departs London. It listed every railway and steamer connection across Europe, Asia, and beyond — a compendium of industrial civilization's mastery over distance. (PN: 003-024)

## Role in the Novel

Fogg's entire wager is a bet on the reliability of timetables. His itinerary — published as a thought-experiment in the *Daily Telegraph* — assumes that every connection will be made exactly as scheduled. The novel relentlessly tests this assumption: a missed steamer in Bombay, an incomplete railway in India, a delayed departure in Hong Kong. Each disruption is a crack in the edifice of the timetable, and Fogg's genius lies in his ability to paper over those cracks with money, improvisation, and sheer will.

Passepartout initially marvels at the precision of it all, then grows bewildered as the gaps between the timetable and reality widen. The timetable represents a worldview — that the world is orderly, predictable, and conquerable by planning — and the novel's plot is a sustained interrogation of whether that worldview can survive contact with the actual world. (PN: 004-130)

## The Timetable as a Symbol

The timetable in Verne's novel is more than a travel document. It is a symbol of the Victorian faith in systems: the belief that if you could plan carefully enough, nothing could go wrong. Fogg, the ultimate systems-thinker, treats Bradshaw as scripture. When the railway across India proves incomplete, Fogg does not lament — he buys an elephant. The timetable may fail, but the mindset it represents does not.

This tension between system and reality runs through the entire novel. The telegraph — the other great information system of the age — transmits Fix's suspicions faster than Fogg can travel. The railway timetable may not be perfect, but the telegraphic network is close to it, and it nearly destroys Fogg at Liverpool. Together, the timetable and the telegraph represent the two faces of the nineteenth-century information revolution: one promising to move bodies predictably across space, the other promising to move information instantaneously. In the novel, the latter threatens to undo what the former makes possible.

> "It's all very well to plan a journey round the world, but it is after you have started that the difficulties begin." — Detective Fix (PN: 010-010)

## Historical Context

In 1872, the year of Fogg's journey, *Bradshaw's Guide* had been published monthly for over thirty years. It was the standard reference for any traveller in Europe and the imperial world. The precision of its timetables reflected genuine improvements in transportation infrastructure: the completion of the Suez Canal (1869), the Pacific Railroad (1869), and the expansion of the Indian railway network. For the first time in history, a person could plan a journey around the world and have a reasonable expectation of completing it within a fixed number of days.
