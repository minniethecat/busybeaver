# Suez

The Egyptian port at the southern end of the Suez Canal where Detective Fix first identifies Phileas Fogg as the suspected Bank of England robber and dispatches the fateful telegraph to Scotland Yard. Suez is the first non-European stop on Fogg's itinerary and the site where the chase narrative of the novel is decisively launched.

## Role in the Novel

Suez is far more than a coaling stop. It is the place where the novel's central narrative engine -- the pursuit of Fogg by Fix -- becomes operational. Fix has been dispatched to Suez in the aftermath of the Bank of England robbery, one of many detectives sent to ports around the world to intercept the suspected thief (002-172). He waits for the arrival of the steamer *Mongolia*, betting that the robber will pass through Egypt en route to Bombay and the Dutch or French colonies in Asia (004-183).

Fix positions himself on the quay with the British consul as the *Mongolia* arrives at eleven o'clock on the morning of October 9th (004-105, 004-200). The bustling port scene -- sailors, merchants, ship-brokers, porters, and fellahs crowding the jetty (004-164/004-166) -- provides the backdrop for Fix's predator-like scrutiny of every passenger who disembarks. His instincts tell him the robber is on board (004-192).

The crucial identification occurs not on the quay but at the British consulate. When Passepartout arrives to have Fogg's passport visaed, Fix -- who has insinuated himself at the consul's office -- intercepts the document. The description on the passport matches exactly the description of the bank robber supplied by Scotland Yard (005-008/005-009). Fix's involuntary start nearly betrays him; the passport is identical to the dispatch he carries.

Fix persuades the consul to delay the visa process, hoping to detain Fogg until a warrant from London can arrive (005-044). But Fogg himself appears at the consulate, placidly presenting his passport for the visa he knows is legally unnecessary but wishes to obtain as proof of his passage through Suez (005-065/005-066). The consul, finding no grounds for refusal, signs and dates the document (005-068), while Fix "devoured" the stranger with his eyes (005-052), now certain of his man.

The section concludes with Fix's decisive action: returning to the telegraph office, he sends the notorious dispatch to Scotland Yard: "I've found the bank robber, Phileas Fogg. Send without delay warrant of arrest to Bombay" (004-085/004-088). This telegraph, relayed as *Suez to London* (004-084), sets in motion the transcontinental pursuit that defines the novel's plot.

## Historical and Cultural Context

In 1872, Suez was a thriving port at the southern terminus of the Suez Canal, which had opened only three years earlier in 1869. The canal, designed by Ferdinand de Lesseps, had transformed global maritime trade by eliminating the need to round the Cape of Good Hope, saving approximately 4,300 nautical miles on the Europe-Asia route. Verne's inclusion of Suez as the first foreign stop reflects his fascination with contemporary technological infrastructure and its compression of global space -- a theme explicitly debated at the Reform Club in Section 3.

The British consulate at Suez was part of the extensive diplomatic network that underpinned British imperial presence along the route to India. The consul's office served not only diplomatic functions but also as an informal intelligence-gathering post, as Fix's use of it demonstrates.

## Journey Context

Suez is the third line of the *Daily Telegraph*'s famous eighty-day timetable (003-036), reached after a seven-day journey from London via Mont Cenis and Brindisi. Fogg is precisely on schedule: the *Mongolia* was due at Suez on October 9th and arrives punctually at eleven in the morning. The steamer stops for four hours at Suez to take on coal (004-178), during which the events at the consulate unfold. From Suez, the next leg is the thirteen-day crossing to Bombay (003-038), spanning the entire Red Sea and Arabian Sea.
