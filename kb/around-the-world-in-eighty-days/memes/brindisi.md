# Brindisi

The Italian port where Phileas Fogg boards the Peninsular and Oriental Company's steamer *Mongolia*, departing Europe for the Orient. Brindisi is, in the novel's itinerary, the last European waypoint -- the threshold between the familiar world of railway timetables and the more unpredictable realm of steamer crossings, canal passages, and non-European landscapes.

## Role in the Novel

Brindisi appears in the novel primarily as a line in the *Daily Telegraph*'s famous timetable: "From London to Suez via Mont Cenis and Brindisi, by rail and steamboats ... 7 days" (003-036). This is the itinerary Fogg follows, departing London on the evening of October 2nd and arriving at Brindisi on Saturday, October 5th, at 4 p.m. (005-091). The *Mongolia* sails from Brindisi at 5 p.m. the same day (005-092), giving Fogg exactly enough time to transfer from the railway to the waiting steamer.

Brindisi is significant for two structural reasons. First, it represents the end of the European railway network and the beginning of the maritime phase of the journey. From this point, Fogg's progress depends on steamer schedules, coaling stops, and weather conditions rather than the precise punctuality of European trains. Second, Brindisi is included in Fogg's meticulous travel log -- a notebook he maintains with the columns Date, Place, and Time (005-091 through 005-104) -- which Passepartout discovers at the consulate in Suez and which later provides Fix with evidence of Fogg's full itinerary.

The *Mongolia* takes on passengers and the Indian mail at Brindisi (004-140), making it a critical junction in the P&O's imperial communications network. The steamer, built of iron and displacing 2,800 tons (004-102/004-103), plies the regular route between Brindisi and Bombay via the Suez Canal (004-106), maintaining speeds of ten knots an hour (004-108). The greater part of the passengers who board at Brindisi are bound for India -- officials, military officers, merchants, and their families (006-009).

## Journey Context

The journey to Brindisi takes Fogg from London across France via the newly-completed Mont Cenis Tunnel, which opened in 1871 and dramatically shortened the Paris-Turin rail connection. This was the fastest route to the Mediterranean at the time, eliminating the need for the longer sea passage around Spain. Brindisi itself was a relatively new addition to the P&O route, having been established as a mail and passenger port after Italian unification (1861) and the opening of the Suez Canal (1869) made the eastern Mediterranean route the preferred path to India.

Fogg's arrival and departure at Brindisi underscores his character: he spends no time sightseeing, takes no interest in the Italian city, and proceeds directly from train to steamer with the mechanical precision of a transit passenger.

## Historical and Cultural Context

In 1872, Brindisi was one of the most important ports in the eastern Mediterranean. As the Italian terminal of the "Overland Route" to India -- a combination of rail across France and Italy and steamer through the Suez Canal -- it served as the gateway between European rail networks and P&O steamer services. The route was designed to deliver passengers and mail from London to Bombay in less than three weeks, a speed that would have been unthinkable before the canal's opening.

Verne's inclusion of Brindisi in the novel's itinerary reflects his journalistic attention to contemporary transportation infrastructure. The *Daily Telegraph* timetable that launches the wager (003-036 through 003-046) reads like a shipping gazette and railway guide, and Brindisi's place in it -- exactly on the seven-day mark of the eighty-day schedule -- confirms the compression of distance that steam technology had achieved by the 1870s.
