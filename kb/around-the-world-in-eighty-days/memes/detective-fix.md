# Detective Fix

## Role in the Novel

Detective Fix is the antagonist of *Around the World in Eighty Days*, a Scotland Yard detective who pursues Phileas Fogg across three continents in the mistaken belief that Fogg is the perpetrator of the Bank of England robbery. Fix's pursuit creates the novel's central dramatic tension, and his gradual transformation from pursuer to reluctant ally mirrors the reader's own growing conviction of Fogg's innocence.

Fix first appears at Suez, where he has been dispatched after the bank robbery to intercept the suspected thief. He boards the Mongolia and, observing Fogg's "phlegmatic" demeanour (005-075), becomes convinced that Fogg matches the description of the robber: "a well-dressed gentleman of polished manners" (002-053). From this point, Fix's entire existence becomes defined by the chase: he follows Fogg through Bombay (007-094), across India, to Calcutta (012-060), Singapore, and Hong Kong (013-006), waiting at each stop for the arrest warrant that never catches up with his quarry.

## Biography

Fix is a detective of what Verne calls "the new school" -- a professional agent of Scotland Yard, distinguished from the older generation of amateur or aristocratic investigators. He is described as intelligent, tenacious, and increasingly desperate as the pursuit unfolds. His motivation is partly professional duty, partly the two-thousand-pound reward offered by the Bank of England (015-084, 015-151).

Fix's moral trajectory is complex. He begins as a competent officer acting on reasonable suspicion, but as the warrant fails to arrive and Fogg leaves British territory at Hong Kong, Fix crosses an ethical line. He drugs Passepartout with opium in a Hong Kong tavern (015-166--015-169), preventing him from informing Fogg of the Carnatic's early departure (015-019). This act -- keeping Fogg "detained at Hong Kong" (015-114) -- is the novel's clearest instance of Fix abandoning legal process for expediency.

In America, Fix's role shifts. He becomes an uneasy travelling companion, then an outright ally when the Sioux attack the transcontinental train (025-088). By Liverpool, Fix believes he can finally make his arrest on British soil -- and he does, detaining Fogg at the Custom House (030-015). But the revelation that the real robber, James Strand, was arrested three days earlier (030-199--030-203) transforms Fix from triumphant detective to humiliated pursuer. Fogg knocks him down "with the precision of a machine" (030-015), and Fix's journey ends in disgrace.

## Character Significance

Fix is the novel's most psychologically complex character. He is neither a villain nor a mere comic foil. His pursuit of Fogg is, by the standards of his profession, entirely reasonable: a bank robbery of fifty-five thousand pounds has occurred (015-128--015-131), a suspect matching Fogg's description has been identified, and the suspect has abruptly left London on a journey that requires extraordinary sums of ready cash. Fix's error is not in suspecting Fogg, but in allowing suspicion to become certainty, and in abandoning lawful process when it proves inconvenient.

The drugging of Passepartout is the novel's most troubling act. Here Fix ceases to be a detective and becomes something closer to a criminal himself: he uses the infrastructure of the opium trade -- Verne's careful description of the den situates it within British colonial commerce (015-039--015-045) -- to achieve his ends. The irony is sharp: Fix, the guardian of British law, exploits the most notorious vice of British imperialism to detain an innocent man.

Yet Fix is not a static figure. His journey from pursuer to prisoner-of-circumstances mirrors the novel's broader argument about the limits of rational deduction. The world, Verne suggests, is not reducible to detective work; it is too strange, too contingent, too full of elephants and typhoons and Sioux attacks to be captured by a Scotland Yard brief. Fix's ultimate humiliation -- arrested in his own mind by the truth -- is the novel's final verdict on the detective's art.
