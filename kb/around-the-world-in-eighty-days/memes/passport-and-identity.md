# Passport and Identity

In a novel about crossing borders at speed, the passport is both an instrument of movement and a tool of control — the document that certifies identity, authorizes passage, and, in Fix's hands, becomes a weapon.

## Definition

The passport in this novel is the nineteenth-century precursor to the modern travel document: a letter of introduction and identification issued by one's government, vised by consular officials at each port of call. Fogg carries a passport describing his appearance and certifying his British nationality. He presents it willingly at Suez, where the British consul stamps it — and where Fix first confirms his quarry's identity. (PN: 005-005, 005-006)

The chapter title itself — "WHICH ONCE MORE DEMONSTRATES THE USELESSNESS OF PASSPORTS AS AIDS TO DETECTIVES" — encapsulates Verne's ironic treatment of the document. (PN: 005-025) The passport is "identical" with the description of the bank robber, giving Fix his first confirmation. (PN: 005-008) Yet Fogg's insistence on getting the passport vised at every port — despite there being no legal requirement — is what makes him traceable. As the consul observes: "Passports are only good for annoying honest folks, and aiding in the flight of rogues." (PN: 005-040)

The passport operates as a symbol of the tension between mobility and surveillance. It enables Fogg's journey — without proper papers, he could not board steamers or cross frontiers — but it also makes him legible to the authorities. Fix's pursuit depends entirely on the documentary infrastructure of empire: passports, arrest warrants, telegraphic dispatches. Fogg's freedom is contingent on the very bureaucracy that threatens to entrap him.

## Role in the Novel

The passport is central to the novel's detective plot. Fix's strategy depends on confirming Fogg's identity at each port where documentation is required. The passport vised at Suez (PN: 005-068) is the first link in a chain of documentary evidence that Fix hopes will culminate in arrest on British soil. At Aden, Fogg and Passepartout go ashore solely to have the passport vised again, and Fix follows them unobserved. (PN: 006-116)

Fogg's rationale for the passport vises is telling: he wants documented proof of his passage through each point on the route. "When I get my passport vised on my return," he tells his whist partners, "you will be able to judge whether I have made the tour correctly." (PN: 003-203) The passport is thus not merely a travel document but a ledger of achievement — a paper trail that will vindicate his claim.

Before departing London, Fogg visits the passport office. (PN: 007-007, 007-015) At San Francisco, he repeats the ritual at the English consulate. (PN: 021-068) The pattern of visa-seeking at every major stop becomes almost compulsive — a ritual of documentation that mirrors Fogg's obsession with precision.

More subtly, the passport raises questions about identity itself. Fogg is, in Fix's eyes, not who his passport declares him to be — he is a bank robber, not a gentleman. The novel plays with this doubleness: Fogg's identity is simultaneously certified by the state and contested by its agent. The resolution — Fogg is exactly who his passport says — is a vindication of documentary truth over paranoid suspicion.

## Historical and Cultural Context

The modern passport system was in its infancy in 1872. Britain did not require passports for travel until the First World War; what Fogg carries is more akin to a letter of introduction, vised by consuls as a courtesy. The elaborate visa requirements, border controls, and biometric identification of the twenty-first century would have been unimaginable to Verne's readers.

Yet the novel anticipates the modern anxiety about identity and documentation. Fix's pursuit of Fogg through papers and telegrams prefigures the surveillance state; Aouda's lack of papers (she is a rescued woman with no official identity) prefigures the predicament of the stateless person. The novel, in its light-hearted way, raises questions that would become urgent in the century to follow.

The consul's remark that "passports are only good for annoying honest folks" (PN: 005-040) captures a nineteenth-century skepticism about state documentation that would seem naive to modern readers. Fogg, in his insistence on getting every visa, is a transitional figure: the modern bureaucratic traveler before the bureaucracy that would define modern travel had fully cohered.
