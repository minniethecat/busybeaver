# The Illustrated London News

The illustrated weekly newspaper that published [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]'s portrait, copied from a photograph hanging in the [[reform-club|Reform Club]]. This single act of reportage transformed Fogg from an obscure gentleman into a celebrity — the man whose [[the-wager|eighty-day wager]] became a matter of national interest.

## Role in the Novel

The _Illustrated London News_ appears at a pivotal moment in the public phase of Fogg's story. After the [[daily-telegraph|Daily Telegraph]] published Fogg's itinerary and the other newspapers had weighed in, the _Illustrated London News_ took the coverage a step further by giving Fogg a face:

> "The _Illustrated London News_ came out with his portrait, copied from a photograph in the Reform Club." (004-033)

This was no small thing. In an era before mass photography, an engraved portrait in the _Illustrated London News_ meant recognition — Fogg was now a personage whose appearance readers would know, whose progress they could follow, and whose success or failure would be a matter of public record.

## Late-Story Reappearance

The _Illustrated London News_ reappears near the novel's end, alongside [[the-times-newspaper|the Times]], as one of the publications that had chronicled Fogg's journey. By then, Fogg had completed his circuit of the globe — and the papers that had once mocked him had a very different story to tell. (PN: 015-191)

## From Skepticism to Celebration

The _Illustrated London News_ played a role in the broader arc of public opinion that Verne traces across the novel — a trajectory that begins with derision and ends with vindication. When the newspaper first published Fogg's portrait, it lent his cause a measure of visual credibility, but the wager was still widely regarded as folly (004-033). By the time Fogg was seen reading the _Illustrated London News_ in his Hong Kong hotel, the public debate was still unresolved — the paper remained part of his evening ritual alongside the _Times_, but no verdict had yet been rendered on his enterprise (015-191).

That verdict arrived in the novel's final chapters, where the media narrative pivoted dramatically. When the real Bank of England robber, James Strand, was arrested in Edinburgh on December 17th, the entire public perception of Fogg reversed:

> "It is time to relate what a change took place in English public opinion when it transpired that the real bankrobber, a certain James Strand, had been arrested... Three days before, Phileas Fogg had been a criminal, who was being desperately followed up by the police; now he was an honourable gentleman, mathematically pursuing his eccentric journey round the world." (030-198–204)

With Fogg's innocence established, the newspapers — including, by implication, the _Illustrated London News_ — resumed their coverage with renewed enthusiasm. "The papers resumed their discussion about the wager; all those who had laid bets, for or against him, revived their interest, as if by magic; the 'Phileas Fogg bonds' again became negotiable, and many new wagers were made" (031-001–004). The man who had been a suspected criminal was now a celebrity whose return was a matter of national anticipation: "The anxiety in which, for three days, London society existed, cannot be described" (031-014).

The shift from skepticism to celebration reflects a deeper theme in the novel: the fickleness of Victorian public opinion and the power of reputation in London society. The _Illustrated London News_ — by publishing the portrait, by covering the story, and by being part of the media apparatus that Fogg himself consumed — stands as a symbol of this dynamic. It had helped create Fogg's public image, and when the evidence turned in his favour, it helped broadcast his vindication. The great crowd that gathered in Pall Mall on the final Saturday evening (031-023) was in part a testament to the work that illustrated weeklies like the _Illustrated London News_ had done in making an eccentric gentleman's private wager into a matter of public record and national conversation.

## Media Ecosystem

The publication of Fogg's portrait illustrates the interconnected Victorian media ecosystem that Verne carefully depicts. The Reform Club photograph existed; the _Illustrated London News_ engraved it; the [[daily-telegraph|Daily Telegraph]] wrote about it; and Fogg, who had never sought publicity, found himself the subject of a national conversation — all before he had crossed the Channel.
