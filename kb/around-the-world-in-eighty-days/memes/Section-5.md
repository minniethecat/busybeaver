[005-001] [[detective-fix|Fix]] took up a position, and carefully examined each face and figure

[005-002] which made its appearance. Presently one of the passengers, after

[005-003] vigorously pushing his way through the importunate crowd of porters,

[005-004] came up to him and politely asked if he could point out the English

[005-005] consulate, at the same time showing a [[passport-and-identity|passport]] which he wished to have

[005-006] _visaed_. [[fix-detective|Fix]] instinctively took the passport, and with a rapid glance

[005-007] read the description of its bearer. An involuntary motion of surprise

[005-008] nearly escaped him, for the description in the passport was identical

[005-009] with that of the bank robber which he had received from [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]].

[005-010] “Is this your passport?” asked he.

[005-011] “No, it’s my master’s.”

[005-012] “And your master is—”

[005-013] “He stayed on board.”

[005-014] “But he must go to [[british-consular-service|the consul]]’s in person, so as to establish his

[005-015] identity.”

[005-016] “Oh, is that necessary?”

[005-017] “Quite indispensable.”

[005-018] “And where is the consulate?”

[005-019] “There, on the corner of the square,” said Fix, pointing to a house two

[005-020] hundred steps off.

[005-021] “I’ll go and fetch my master, who won’t be much pleased, however, to be

[005-022] disturbed.”

[005-023] The passenger bowed to Fix, and returned to the [[steamer-travel|steamer]].

[005-024] CHAPTER VII.

[005-025] WHICH ONCE MORE DEMONSTRATES THE USELESSNESS OF PASSPORTS AS AIDS TO

[005-026] DETECTIVES

[005-027] The detective passed down the quay, and rapidly made his way to the

[005-028] consul’s office, where he was at once admitted to the presence of that

[005-029] official.

[005-030] “Consul,” said he, without preamble, “I have strong reasons for

[005-031] believing that my man is a passenger on the ‘Mongolia.’” And he

[005-032] narrated what had just passed concerning the passport.

[005-033] “Well, Mr. Fix,” replied the consul, “I shall not be sorry to see the

[005-034] rascal’s face; but perhaps he won’t come here—that is, if he is the

[005-035] person you suppose him to be. A robber doesn’t quite like to leave

[005-036] traces of his flight behind him; and, besides, he is not obliged to

[005-037] have his passport countersigned.”

[005-038] “If he is as shrewd as I think he is, consul, he will come.”

[005-039] “To have his passport _visaed?_”

[005-040] “Yes. Passports are only good for annoying honest folks, and aiding in

[005-041] the flight of rogues. I assure you it will be quite the thing for him

[005-042] to do; but I hope you will not _visa_ the passport.”

[005-043] “Why not? If the passport is genuine I have no right to refuse.”

[005-044] “Still, I must keep this man here until I can get a warrant to arrest

[005-045] him from [[london|London]].”

[005-046] “Ah, that’s your look-out. But I cannot—”

[005-047] The consul did not finish his sentence, for as he spoke a knock was

[005-048] heard at the door, and two strangers entered, one of whom was the

[005-049] servant whom Fix had met on the quay. The other, who was his master,

[005-050] held out his passport with the request that the consul would do him the

[005-051] favour to _visa_ it. The consul took the document and carefully read

[005-052] it, whilst Fix observed, or rather devoured, the stranger with his eyes

[005-053] from a corner of the room.

[005-054] “You are Mr. [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]?” said the consul, after reading the

[005-055] passport.

[005-056] “I am.”

[005-057] “And this man is your servant?”

[005-058] “He is: a Frenchman, named [[passepartout|Passepartout]].”

[005-059] “You are from London?”

[005-060] “Yes.”

[005-061] “And you are going—”

[005-062] “To [[bombay|Bombay]].”

[005-063] “Very good, sir. You know that a _visa_ is useless, and that no

[005-064] passport is required?”

[005-065] “I know it, sir,” replied Phileas Fogg; “but I wish to prove, by your

[005-066] _visa_, that I came by [[suez|Suez]].”

[005-067] “Very well, sir.”

[005-068] The consul proceeded to sign and date the passport, after which he

[005-069] added his official seal. Mr. Fogg paid the customary fee, coldly bowed,

[005-070] and went out, followed by his servant.

[005-071] “Well?” queried the detective.

[005-072] “Well, he looks and acts like a perfectly honest man,” replied the

[005-073] consul.

[005-074] “Possibly; but that is not the question. Do you think, consul, that

[005-075] this phlegmatic [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] resembles, feature by feature, the robber

[005-076] whose description I have received?”

[005-077] “I concede that; but then, you know, all descriptions—”

[005-078] “I’ll make certain of it,” interrupted Fix. “The servant seems to me

[005-079] less mysterious than the master; besides, he’s a Frenchman, and can’t

[005-080] help talking. Excuse me for a little while, consul.”

[005-081] Fix started off in search of Passepartout.

[005-082] Meanwhile Mr. Fogg, after leaving the consulate, repaired to the quay,

[005-083] gave some orders to Passepartout, went off to the “Mongolia” in a boat,

[005-084] and descended to his cabin. He took up his note-book, which contained

[005-085] the following memoranda:

[005-086] “Left London, Wednesday, October 2nd, at 8.45 p.m.

[005-087] “Reached Paris, Thursday, October 3rd, at 7.20 a.m.

[005-088] “Left Paris, Thursday, at 8.40 a.m.

[005-089] “Reached Turin by Mont Cenis, Friday, October 4th, at 6.35 a.m.

[005-090] “Left Turin, Friday, at 7.20 a.m.

[005-091] “Arrived at [[brindisi|Brindisi]], Saturday, October 5th, at 4 p.m.

[005-092] “Sailed on the ‘Mongolia,’ Saturday, at 5 p.m.

[005-093] “Reached [[the-suez-canal|Suez]], Wednesday, October 9th, at 11 a.m.

[005-094] “Total of hours spent, 158½; or, in days, six days and a half.”

[005-095] These dates were inscribed in an [[around-the-world-route|itinerary]] divided into columns,

[005-096] indicating the month, the day of the month, and the day for the

[005-097] stipulated and actual arrivals at each principal point Paris, Brindisi,

[005-098] Suez, Bombay, [[calcutta|Calcutta]], Singapore, [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], [[yokohama|Yokohama]], [[san-francisco|San Francisco]],

[005-099] [[new-york|New York]], and London—from the 2nd of October to the 21st of December;

[005-100] and giving a space for setting down the gain made or the loss suffered

[005-101] on arrival at each locality. This methodical record thus contained an

[005-102] account of everything needed, and Mr. Fogg always knew whether he was

[005-103] behind-hand or in advance of his time. On this Friday, October 9th, he

[005-104] noted his arrival at Suez, and observed that he had as yet neither

[005-105] gained nor lost. He sat down quietly to breakfast in his cabin, never

[005-106] once thinking of inspecting the town, being one of those Englishmen who

[005-107] are wont to see foreign countries through the eyes of their domestics.

[005-108] CHAPTER VIII.

[005-109] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT TALKS RATHER MORE, PERHAPS, THAN IS PRUDENT

[005-110] Fix soon rejoined Passepartout, who was lounging and looking about on

[005-111] the quay, as if he did not feel that he, at least, was obliged not to

[005-112] see anything.

[005-113] “Well, my friend,” said the detective, coming up with him, “is your

[005-114] passport _visaed?_”

[005-115] “Ah, it’s you, is it, monsieur?” responded Passepartout. “Thanks, yes,

[005-116] the passport is all right.”

[005-117] “And you are looking about you?”

[005-118] “Yes; but we travel so fast that I seem to be journeying in a dream. So

[005-119] this is Suez?”

[005-120] “Yes.”

[005-121] “In Egypt?”

[005-122] “Certainly, in Egypt.”

[005-123] “And in Africa?”

[005-124] “In Africa.”

[005-125] “In Africa!” repeated Passepartout. “Just think, monsieur, I had no

[005-126] idea that we should go farther than Paris; and all that I saw of Paris

[005-127] was between twenty minutes past seven and twenty minutes before nine in

[005-128] the morning, between the Northern and the Lyons stations, through the

[005-129] windows of a car, and in a driving rain! How I regret not having seen

[005-130] once more Père la Chaise and the circus in the Champs Elysées!”

[005-131] “You are in a great hurry, then?”

[005-132] “I am not, but my master is. By the way, I must buy some shoes and

[005-133] shirts. We came away without trunks, only with a carpet-bag.”

[005-134] “I will show you an excellent shop for getting what you want.”

[005-135] “Really, monsieur, you are very kind.”

[005-136] And they walked off together, Passepartout chatting volubly as they

[005-137] went along.

[005-138] “Above all,” said he; “don’t let me lose the steamer.”

[005-139] “You have plenty of time; it’s only twelve o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]].”

[005-140] Passepartout pulled out his big watch. “Twelve!” he exclaimed; “why,

[005-141] it’s only eight minutes before ten.”

[005-142] “Your watch is slow.”

[005-143] “My watch? A family watch, monsieur, which has come down from my

[005-144] great-grandfather! It doesn’t vary five minutes in the year. It’s a

[005-145] perfect chronometer, look you.”

[005-146] “I see how it is,” said Fix. “You have kept London time, which is two

[005-147] hours behind that of Suez. You ought to regulate your watch at noon in

[005-148] each country.”

[005-149] “I regulate my watch? Never!”

[005-150] “Well, then, it will not agree with the sun.”

[005-151] “So much the worse for the sun, monsieur. The sun will be wrong, then!”

[005-152] And the worthy fellow returned the watch to its fob with a defiant

[005-153] gesture. After a few minutes silence, Fix resumed: “You left London

[005-154] hastily, then?”

[005-155] “I rather think so! Last Friday at eight o’clock in the evening,

[005-156] Monsieur Fogg came home from his club, and three-quarters of an hour

[005-157] afterwards we were off.”

[005-158] “But where is your master going?”

[005-159] “Always straight ahead. He is going round the world.”

[005-160] “Round the world?” cried Fix.

[005-161] “Yes, and in eighty days! He says it is on a [[the-wager|wager]]; but, between us, I

[005-162] don’t believe a word of it. That wouldn’t be common sense. There’s

[005-163] something else in the wind.”

[005-164] “Ah! Mr. Fogg is a character, is he?”

[005-165] “I should say he was.”

[005-166] “Is he rich?”

[005-167] “No doubt, for he is carrying an enormous sum in brand new banknotes

[005-168] with him. And he doesn’t spare the money on the way, either: he has

[005-169] offered a large reward to [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]] of the ‘Mongolia’ if he gets us

[005-170] to Bombay well in advance of time.”

[005-171] “And you have known your master a long time?”

[005-172] “Why, no; I entered his service the very day we left London.”

[005-173] The effect of these replies upon the already suspicious and excited

[005-174] detective may be imagined. The hasty departure from London soon after

[005-175] the robbery; the large sum carried by Mr. Fogg; his eagerness to reach

[005-176] distant countries; the pretext of an eccentric and foolhardy bet—all

[005-177] confirmed Fix in his theory. He continued to pump poor Passepartout,

[005-178] and learned that he really knew little or nothing of his master, who

[005-179] lived a solitary existence in London, was said to be rich, though no

[005-180] one knew whence came his riches, and was mysterious and impenetrable in

[005-181] his affairs and habits. Fix felt sure that Phileas Fogg would not land

[005-182] at Suez, but was really going on to Bombay.

[005-183] “Is Bombay far from here?” asked Passepartout.

[005-184] “Pretty far. It is a ten days’ voyage by sea.”

[005-185] “And in what country is Bombay?”

[005-186] “India.”

[005-187] “In Asia?”

[005-188] “Certainly.”

[005-189] “The deuce! I was going to tell you there’s one thing that worries

[005-190] me—my burner!”

[005-191] “What burner?”

[005-192] “My gas-burner, which I forgot to turn off, and which is at this moment

[005-193] burning at my expense. I have calculated, monsieur, that I lose two

[005-194] shillings every four and twenty hours, exactly sixpence more than I

[005-195] earn; and you will understand that the longer our journey—”

[005-196] Did Fix pay any attention to Passepartout’s trouble about the gas? It

[005-197] is not probable. He was not listening, but was cogitating a project.

[005-198] Passepartout and he had now reached the shop, where Fix left his

[005-199] companion to make his purchases, after recommending him not to miss the

[005-200] steamer, and hurried back to the consulate. Now that he was fully

[005-201] convinced, Fix had quite recovered his equanimity.

[005-202] “Consul,” said he, “I have no longer any doubt. I have spotted my man.

[005-203] He passes himself off as an odd stick who is going round the world in

[005-204] eighty days.”

[005-205] “Then he’s a sharp fellow,” returned the consul, “and counts on

[005-206] returning to London after putting the police of the two countries off

[005-207] his track.”

[005-208] “We’ll see about that,” replied Fix.

[005-209] “But are you not mistaken?”

[005-210] “I am not mistaken.”

[005-211] “Why was this robber so anxious to prove, by the _visa_, that he had

[005-212] passed through Suez?”

[005-213] “Why? I have no idea; but listen to me.”

[005-214] He reported in a few words the most important parts of his conversation

[005-215] with Passepartout.

[005-216] “In short,” said the consul, “appearances are wholly against this man.

[005-217] And what are you going to do?”

[005-218] “Send a dispatch to London for a warrant of arrest to be dispatched

[005-219] instantly to Bombay, take passage on board the ‘Mongolia,’ follow my

[005-220] rogue to India, and there, on English ground, arrest him politely, with

[005-221] my warrant in my hand, and my hand on his shoulder.”

[005-222] Having uttered these words with a cool, careless air, the detective

[005-223] took leave of the consul, and repaired to the [[the-telegraph|telegraph]] office, whence

[005-224] he sent the dispatch which we have seen to the London police office. A

[005-225] quarter of an hour later found Fix, with a small bag in his hand,

[005-226] proceeding on board the “Mongolia;” and, ere many moments longer, the

[005-227] noble steamer rode out at full steam upon the waters of the Red Sea.
