# Cultural Encounters in Around the World

A novel about circumnavigating the globe should, one might expect, be rich in encounters between the traveler and the cultures through which he passes. *Around the World in Eighty Days* is — and is not — such a novel. Fogg's journey takes him through Egypt, India, Singapore, Hong Kong, Japan, and the United States, yet the novel's treatment of cultural difference is shaped less by curiosity about those places than by the ideological assumptions of its era. This article examines the pattern of cultural encounters in the novel and what they reveal about the Victorian travel imagination.

## The Window and the Mirror

Fogg's mode of travel — railway carriage, steamer deck, consular office — defines the quality of his encounters. He sees the world through windows. India is experienced as a blur of temples, elephants, and railway stations; Japan is glimpsed through the spectacle of an acrobat troupe; America is a series of railway compartments and frontier settlements observed at speed.

This is not merely a limitation of Fogg's character but a structural feature of the novel's form. Verne's narrative economy requires that places be evoked rather than explored, that cultures be summarized rather than investigated. The result is a world rendered in vivid but shallow strokes: the "tropical vegetation" of India, the "strange costumes" of Japan, the "energetic chaos" of San Francisco. Each place receives a paragraph, not a chapter.

The notable exceptions — the suttee episode in India and the political rally in San Francisco — are exceptions that prove the rule: they are defined by action (rescue, confrontation) rather than observation. Fogg engages with the world not by understanding it but by intervening in it.

## Orientalism in Motion

Edward Said's concept of Orientalism — the Western representation of the "Orient" as exotic, irrational, and in need of Western intervention — finds rich illustration in the novel. India is the land of suttee and tiger hunts, of "half-savage" country traversed by a British-engineered railway. (PN: 007-174) Aouda is described in terms that emphasize her exotic beauty and her difference from European women; her Parsi identity is presented as a marker of her distinctiveness from the "common" Hindus around her.

The rescue-of-Aouda episode is the novel's most explicit orientalist set piece. The suttee is presented as a barbaric custom from which Fogg must rescue the virtuous victim. The Parsee guide, the drugged priests, the rising-from-the-pyre deception — all are rendered in the vocabulary of colonial adventure. Aouda's gratitude and eventual love for Fogg complete the orientalist narrative: the non-European woman is saved by the European gentleman and rewards him with devotion. (PN: 012-045, 012-056, 034-075)

Yet the novel complicates this reading. Aouda is not passive — she proposes marriage to Fogg, an act of remarkable agency. And her "Eastern" qualities are presented not as inferior to European culture but as complementary to Fogg's mechanical precision. The novel's resolution suggests that Fogg needs Aouda's human warmth as much as she needs his protection. The orientalist frame remains, but it is stretched to accommodate a more equal relationship.

## Empire as Infrastructure, Infrastructure as Empire

The cultural encounters in the novel are mediated by imperial infrastructure. Fogg does not meet "India" — he meets the British Indian railway, the British consular network, the P&O steamer lines. The telegraph wires that carry Fix's dispatches run alongside the railway tracks; the arrest warrant that Fix awaits in Hong Kong travels by the same ships and trains as Fogg himself.

This infrastructure is inherently cultural. It imposes British time (the railway timetable), British law (the warrant Fix carries), and British social norms (the Reform Club wager) on non-European space. Fogg's journey is possible because the world has been made legible to British travelers — mapped, scheduled, administered. The cultural difference that remains is the difference that survives this infrastructural homogenization: costume, cuisine, religious practice, landscape. It is, in other words, the difference that can be consumed as spectacle without threatening the infrastructure that enables the journey.

## America: The Mirror Cracked

The American leg of the journey complicates the novel's cultural pattern. Unlike India or Japan, America is not represented as exotic — it is represented as dangerously familiar. The San Francisco political rally (PN: 021-004) is a riot of democratic energy that contrasts sharply with the ordered calm of the Reform Club. The Mormon sermon on the train, the buffalo herd, the Sioux attack — each represents a frontier reality that resists the civilizational narrative Fogg has taken for granted.

Fix's presence in America also changes. In the colonial spaces of Suez, Bombay, and Hong Kong, Fix operates with relative freedom — the imperial infrastructure is his infrastructure. In America, he is a foreign detective on foreign soil, his authority contingent rather than assumed. The dynamic shifts: Fix must cooperate with Fogg rather than manipulate him. The cultural encounter here is not between European and non-European but between two versions of the West — one ordered, imperial, and gentlemanly; the other democratic, violent, and improvisational.

## Conclusion: The World as Backdrop

*Around the World in Eighty Days* is not a novel of cultural understanding — it is a novel of cultural traversal. The world through which Fogg travels is a backdrop for the drama of character and time, not a subject of sustained attention. This is both a limitation of the novel's form (the adventure genre requires movement, not dwelling) and a feature of its ideological assumptions (the world exists to be crossed, not understood).

Yet the novel is not monolithic. Aouda's agency, Fix's transformation, and the destabilizing energy of the American episodes suggest that Verne was aware of the limits of the perspective his novel assumes. The cultural encounters in *Eighty Days* are shallow by modern standards, but they are not empty — they contain, in compressed form, the contradictions and possibilities of a world being knit together by steam and telegraph while still riven by profound cultural difference.
