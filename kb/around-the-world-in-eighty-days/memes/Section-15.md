[015-001] On reaching the quay where they were to embark on the “Carnatic,” he

[015-002] was not astonished to find [[detective-fix|Fix]] walking up and down. The detective

[015-003] seemed very much disturbed and disappointed.

[015-004] “This is bad,” muttered [[passepartout|Passepartout]], “for the gentlemen of the Reform

[015-005] Club!” He accosted [[fix-detective|Fix]] with a merry smile, as if he had not perceived

[015-006] that [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]’s chagrin. The detective had, indeed, good reasons to

[015-007] inveigh against the bad luck which pursued him. The warrant had not

[015-008] come! It was certainly on the way, but as certainly it could not now

[015-009] reach [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] for several days; and, this being the last English

[015-010] territory on [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]]’s [[around-the-world-route|route]], the robber would escape, unless he could

[015-011] manage to detain him.

[015-012] “Well, Monsieur Fix,” said Passepartout, “have you decided to go with

[015-013] us so far as America?”

[015-014] “Yes,” returned Fix, through his set teeth.

[015-015] “Good!” exclaimed Passepartout, laughing heartily. “I knew you could

[015-016] not persuade yourself to separate from us. Come and engage your berth.”

[015-017] They entered the [[steamer-travel|steamer]] office and secured cabins for four persons.

[015-018] The clerk, as he gave them the tickets, informed them that, the repairs

[015-019] on the “Carnatic” having been completed, the steamer would leave that

[015-020] very evening, and not next morning, as had been announced.

[015-021] “That will suit my master all the better,” said Passepartout. “I will

[015-022] go and let him know.”

[015-023] Fix now decided to make a bold move; he resolved to tell Passepartout

[015-024] all. It seemed to be the only possible means of keeping Phileas Fogg

[015-025] several days longer at Hong Kong. He accordingly invited his companion

[015-026] into a tavern which caught his eye on the quay. On entering, they found

[015-027] themselves in a large room handsomely decorated, at the end of which

[015-028] was a large camp-bed furnished with cushions. Several persons lay upon

[015-029] this bed in a deep sleep. At the small tables which were arranged about

[015-030] the room some thirty customers were drinking English beer, porter, gin,

[015-031] and brandy; smoking, the while, long red clay pipes stuffed with little

[015-032] balls of opium mingled with essence of rose. From time to time one of

[015-033] the smokers, overcome with the narcotic, would slip under the table,

[015-034] whereupon the waiters, taking him by the head and feet, carried and

[015-035] laid him upon the bed. The bed already supported twenty of these

[015-036] stupefied sots.

[015-037] Fix and Passepartout saw that they were in a smoking-house haunted by

[015-038] those wretched, cadaverous, idiotic creatures to whom the English

[015-039] merchants sell every year the miserable drug called opium, to the

[015-040] amount of one million four hundred thousand pounds—thousands devoted to

[015-041] one of the most despicable vices which afflict humanity! The Chinese

[015-042] government has in vain attempted to deal with the evil by stringent

[015-043] laws. It passed gradually from the rich, to whom it was at first

[015-044] exclusively reserved, to the lower classes, and then its ravages could

[015-045] not be arrested. Opium is smoked everywhere, at all times, by men and

[015-046] women, in the Celestial Empire; and, once accustomed to it, the victims

[015-047] cannot dispense with it, except by suffering horrible bodily

[015-048] contortions and agonies. A great smoker can smoke as many as eight

[015-049] pipes a day; but he dies in five years. It was in one of these dens

[015-050] that Fix and Passepartout, in search of a friendly glass, found

[015-051] themselves. Passepartout had no money, but willingly accepted Fix’s

[015-052] invitation in the hope of returning the [[duty|obligation]] at some future time.

[015-053] They ordered two bottles of port, to which the Frenchman did ample

[015-054] justice, whilst Fix observed him with close attention. They chatted

[015-055] about the journey, and Passepartout was especially merry at the idea

[015-056] that Fix was going to continue it with them. When the bottles were

[015-057] empty, however, he rose to go and tell his master of the change in the

[015-058] time of the sailing of the “Carnatic.”

[015-059] Fix caught him by the arm, and said, “Wait a moment.”

[015-060] “What for, Mr. Fix?”

[015-061] “I want to have a serious talk with you.”

[015-062] “A serious talk!” cried Passepartout, drinking up the little wine that

[015-063] was left in the bottom of his glass. “Well, we’ll talk about it

[015-064] to-morrow; I haven’t time now.”

[015-065] “Stay! What I have to say concerns your master.”

[015-066] Passepartout, at this, looked attentively at his companion. Fix’s face

[015-067] seemed to have a singular expression. He resumed his seat.

[015-068] “What is it that you have to say?”

[015-069] Fix placed his hand upon Passepartout’s arm, and, lowering his voice,

[015-070] said, “You have guessed who I am?”

[015-071] “Parbleu!” said Passepartout, smiling.

[015-072] “Then I’m going to tell you everything—”

[015-073] “Now that I know everything, my friend! Ah! that’s very good. But go

[015-074] on, go on. First, though, let me tell you that those gentlemen have put

[015-075] themselves to a useless expense.”

[015-076] “Useless!” said Fix. “You speak confidently. It’s clear that you don’t

[015-077] know how large the sum is.”

[015-078] “Of course I do,” returned Passepartout. “Twenty thousand pounds.”

[015-079] “Fifty-five thousand!” answered Fix, pressing his companion’s hand.

[015-080] “What!” cried the Frenchman. “Has Monsieur Fogg dared—fifty-five

[015-081] thousand pounds! Well, there’s all the more reason for not losing an

[015-082] instant,” he continued, getting up hastily.

[015-083] Fix pushed Passepartout back in his chair, and resumed: “Fifty-five

[015-084] thousand pounds; and if I succeed, I get two thousand pounds. If you’ll

[015-085] help me, I’ll let you have five hundred of them.”

[015-086] “Help you?” cried Passepartout, whose eyes were standing wide open.

[015-087] “Yes; help me keep Mr. Fogg here for two or three days.”

[015-088] “Why, what are you saying? Those gentlemen are not satisfied with

[015-089] following my master and suspecting his honour, but they must try to put

[015-090] obstacles in his way! I blush for them!”

[015-091] “What do you mean?”

[015-092] “I mean that it is a piece of shameful trickery. They might as well

[015-093] waylay Mr. Fogg and put his money in their pockets!”

[015-094] “That’s just what we count on doing.”

[015-095] “It’s a conspiracy, then,” cried Passepartout, who became more and more

[015-096] excited as the liquor mounted in his head, for he drank without

[015-097] perceiving it. “A real conspiracy! And gentlemen, too. Bah!”

[015-098] Fix began to be puzzled.

[015-099] “Members of the [[london|Reform Club]]!” continued Passepartout. “You must know,

[015-100] Monsieur Fix, that my master is an honest man, and that, when he makes

[015-101] a [[the-wager|wager]], he tries to win it fairly!”

[015-102] “But who do you think I am?” asked Fix, looking at him intently.

[015-103] “Parbleu! An agent of the members of the [[reform-club|Reform Club]], sent out here to

[015-104] interrupt my master’s journey. But, though I found you out some time

[015-105] ago, I’ve taken good care to say nothing about it to Mr. Fogg.”

[015-106] “He knows nothing, then?”

[015-107] “Nothing,” replied Passepartout, again emptying his glass.

[015-108] The detective passed his hand across his forehead, hesitating before he

[015-109] spoke again. What should he do? Passepartout’s mistake seemed sincere,

[015-110] but it made his design more difficult. It was evident that the servant

[015-111] was not the master’s accomplice, as Fix had been inclined to suspect.

[015-112] “Well,” said the detective to himself, “as he is not an accomplice, he

[015-113] will help me.”

[015-114] He had no time to lose: Fogg must be detained at Hong Kong, so he

[015-115] resolved to make a clean breast of it.

[015-116] “Listen to me,” said Fix abruptly. “I am not, as you think, an agent of

[015-117] the members of the Reform Club—”

[015-118] “Bah!” retorted Passepartout, with an air of raillery.

[015-119] “I am a police detective, sent out here by the London office.”

[015-120] “You, a detective?”

[015-121] “I will prove it. Here is my commission.”

[015-122] Passepartout was speechless with astonishment when Fix displayed this

[015-123] document, the genuineness of which could not be doubted.

[015-124] “Mr. Fogg’s wager,” resumed Fix, “is only a pretext, of which you and

[015-125] the gentlemen of the Reform are dupes. He had a motive for securing

[015-126] your innocent complicity.”

[015-127] “But why?”

[015-128] “Listen. On the 28th of last September a robbery of fifty-five thousand

[015-129] pounds was committed at the Bank of England by a person whose

[015-130] description was fortunately secured. Here is his description; it

[015-131] answers exactly to that of Mr. Phileas Fogg.”

[015-132] “What nonsense!” cried Passepartout, striking the table with his fist.

[015-133] “My master is the most honourable of men!”

[015-134] “How can you tell? You know scarcely anything about him. You went into

[015-135] his service the day he came away; and he came away on a foolish

[015-136] pretext, without trunks, and carrying a large amount in banknotes. And

[015-137] yet you are bold enough to assert that he is an honest man!”

[015-138] “Yes, yes,” repeated the poor fellow, mechanically.

[015-139] “Would you like to be arrested as his accomplice?”

[015-140] Passepartout, overcome by what he had heard, held his head between his

[015-141] hands, and did not dare to look at the detective. Phileas Fogg, the

[015-142] saviour of [[aouda|Aouda]], that brave and generous man, a robber! And yet how

[015-143] many presumptions there were against him! Passepartout essayed to

[015-144] reject the suspicions which forced themselves upon his mind; he did not

[015-145] wish to believe that his master was guilty.

[015-146] “Well, what do you want of me?” said he, at last, with an effort.

[015-147] “See here,” replied Fix; “I have tracked Mr. Fogg to this place, but as

[015-148] yet I have failed to receive the warrant of arrest for which I sent to

[015-149] London. You must help me to keep him here in Hong Kong—”

[015-150] “I! But I—”

[015-151] “I will share with you the two thousand pounds reward offered by the

[015-152] Bank of England.”

[015-153] “Never!” replied Passepartout, who tried to rise, but fell back,

[015-154] exhausted in mind and body.

[015-155] “Mr. Fix,” he stammered, “even should what you say be true—if my master

[015-156] is really the robber you are seeking for—which I deny—I have been, am,

[015-157] in his service; I have seen his generosity and goodness; and I will

[015-158] never betray him—not for all the gold in the world. I come from a

[015-159] village where they don’t eat that kind of bread!”

[015-160] “You refuse?”

[015-161] “I refuse.”

[015-162] “Consider that I’ve said nothing,” said Fix; “and let us drink.”

[015-163] “Yes; let us drink!”

[015-164] Passepartout felt himself yielding more and more to the effects of the

[015-165] liquor. Fix, seeing that he must, at all hazards, be separated from his

[015-166] master, wished to entirely overcome him. Some pipes full of opium lay

[015-167] upon the table. Fix slipped one into Passepartout’s hand. He took it,

[015-168] put it between his lips, lit it, drew several puffs, and his head,

[015-169] becoming heavy under the influence of the narcotic, fell upon the

[015-170] table.

[015-171] “At last!” said Fix, seeing Passepartout unconscious. “Mr. Fogg will

[015-172] not be informed of the ‘Carnatic’s’ departure; and, if he is, he will

[015-173] have to go without this cursed Frenchman!”

[015-174] And, after paying his bill, Fix left the tavern.

[015-175] CHAPTER XX.

[015-176] IN WHICH FIX COMES FACE TO FACE WITH PHILEAS FOGG

[015-177] While these events were passing at the opium-house, Mr. Fogg,

[015-178] unconscious of the danger he was in of losing the steamer, was quietly

[015-179] escorting Aouda about the streets of the English quarter, making the

[015-180] necessary purchases for the long voyage before them. It was all very

[015-181] well for an Englishman like Mr. Fogg to make the tour of the world with

[015-182] a carpet-bag; a lady could not be expected to travel comfortably under

[015-183] such conditions. He acquitted his task with characteristic serenity,

[015-184] and invariably replied to the remonstrances of his fair companion, who

[015-185] was confused by his patience and generosity:

[015-186] “It is in the interest of my journey—a part of my programme.”

[015-187] The purchases made, they returned to the hotel, where they dined at a

[015-188] sumptuously served _table-d’hôte;_ after which Aouda, shaking hands

[015-189] with her protector after the English fashion, retired to her room for

[015-190] rest. Mr. Fogg absorbed himself throughout the evening in the perusal

[015-191] of the _[[the-times-newspaper|Times]]_ and _[[illustrated-london-news|Illustrated London News]]_.

[015-192] Had he been capable of being astonished at anything, it would have been

[015-193] not to see his servant return at bedtime. But, knowing that the steamer

[015-194] was not to leave for [[yokohama|Yokohama]] until the next morning, he did not

[015-195] disturb himself about the matter. When Passepartout did not appear the

[015-196] next morning to answer his master’s bell, Mr. Fogg, not betraying the

[015-197] least vexation, contented himself with taking his carpet-bag, calling

[015-198] Aouda, and sending for a palanquin.
