# Andrew Stuart

The Reform Club member whose casual skepticism — voiced during a game of whist — launches the most famous journey in Victorian fiction. Without Andrew Stuart's doubt, there would be no wager; without the wager, no journey; without the journey, no novel.

## Role in the Novel

Stuart is introduced in Chapter 2 as one of "Mr. Fogg's usual partners at whist: Andrew Stuart, an engineer; John Sullivan and Samuel Fallentin, bankers; Thomas Flanagan, a brewer." (PN: 002-131)

In the pivotal third chapter, Stuart and his partners are playing whist when the conversation turns to a recent newspaper article asserting that an eighty-day circumnavigation is now theoretically possible. Stuart, the engineer, dismisses the claim — citing the unpredictability of weather, breakdowns, and the myriad obstacles that no timetable can account for. (PN: 003-009) He is, in Verne's phrase, "the incredulous Stuart" who is "not convinced" by the newspaper's calculations. (PN: 003-027)

Fogg, characteristically, does not argue. He simply states that eighty days is sufficient. Stuart, growing increasingly irritated — "It's absurd!" — challenges Fogg directly. (PN: 003-071) When Fogg stakes twenty thousand pounds, Stuart is the first to accept, uttering the line that seals the novel's plot: "When I say I'll wager, I mean it." (PN: 003-079, 003-094)

The memorandum of the wager is drawn up on the spot, signed by Stuart along with Fallentin, Sullivan, Flanagan, and Ralph. (PN: 003-098) Fogg announces he will depart "this very evening." (PN: 003-102)

Stuart reappears at the novel's climax, in Chapter 31, waiting at the Reform Club with the other four members of the wager as the deadline approaches. He grows increasingly nervous as the final minutes tick away: "Five minutes more." (PN: 031-094) "I wouldn't give up my four thousand of the bet," he declares, clinging to hope in the final seconds. (PN: 031-104)

When Fogg appears at the fifty-seventh second of the final minute, Stuart and his partners are both relieved and astonished. (PN: 031-098, 031-123)

## Key Actions

- Dismisses the possibility of an eighty-day circumnavigation during a whist game at the Reform Club (PN: 003-009)
- Argues against the timetable, listing obstacles: "Suppose the Hindoos or Indians pull up the rails" (PN: 003-052)
- Accepts Fogg's wager, staking his share of the twenty thousand pounds (PN: 003-079)
- Signs the memorandum of the wager alongside four other club members (PN: 003-098)
- Asks Fogg at departure: "You do not forget when you are due in London again?" (PN: 003-207)
- At the novel's climax, counts down the final minutes at the Reform Club as the deadline approaches (PN: 031-069, 031-094)
- Declares the wager lost in the final seconds — "He has lost, gentlemen" — before Fogg's miraculous arrival (PN: 031-086)

## Character Significance

Stuart is the embodiment of reasonable skepticism — the voice of common sense that Fogg's entire journey is designed to refute. His arguments against the eighty-day timetable are entirely rational: weather, breakdowns, border delays, the sheer improbability of perfect connections across twenty thousand miles. As an engineer, he thinks in terms of tolerances and failure modes; the newspaper's tidy timetable must be wrong because real systems always break.

Fogg's counter-argument is not a rebuttal of these points but a demonstration: he simply goes and does it. The engineer's skepticism is refuted not by logic but by action — which is perhaps the novel's deepest comment on the relationship between theory and practice, between the armchair critic and the man who acts.

Stuart's role in the climax completes his arc. He is not a villain but a sportsman: he bets against Fogg, but he respects the bet. His nervousness in the final moments — checking the clock, counting the minutes, hoping against hope that Fogg will appear — suggests that he has come to admire his opponent even while opposing him.

> "When I say I'll wager," returned Stuart, "I mean it." (PN: 003-079)
