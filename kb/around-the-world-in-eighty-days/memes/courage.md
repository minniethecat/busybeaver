# Courage

Phileas Fogg is not a conventional hero. He carries no weapon, seeks no conflict, and displays no bravado. Yet his journey is an extended act of courage — the quiet, methodical courage of a man who refuses to acknowledge the possibility of failure.

## Definition

Courage in this novel takes a distinctly Victorian form: not the swashbuckling bravado of a Dumas hero, but the steady nerve of a man who has calculated the risks and decided to proceed. Fogg's courage is inseparable from his precision — he faces danger not by charging at it but by incorporating it into his calculations.

Passepartout provides the novel's counterpoint: a more conventional, physical courage that manifests in daring rescues and quick improvisations. Both forms are validated, but the novel's deeper admiration is reserved for Fogg's quiet variety — the courage that does not recognize itself as such because it is too busy executing the plan.

## Role in the Novel

Courage operates as the necessary complement to duty. Duty tells Fogg what he must do; courage enables him to do it. When Sir Francis Cromarty calls the wager a "useless eccentricity," Fogg does not argue — he simply continues. (PN: 007-136). When the Sioux attack, he does not panic — he organizes a defense. When the Henrietta runs out of coal, he does not despair — he buys the ship and burns its superstructure.

The novel's most striking display of courage is Fogg's willingness to lose. At every turn, he risks not merely his fortune but his reputation and, in the case of the Sioux attack, his life. He does so without heroics, and this absence of drama is precisely what makes his courage impressive — it is courage refined into habit.

## Historical & Cultural Context

Victorian ideals of courage were shaped by a century of imperial warfare, from Waterloo to the Indian Mutiny. Military courage — the willingness to face death in battle — was the dominant model. But the Victorian period also saw the emergence of alternative forms of courage: the explorer's courage in facing the unknown, the engineer's courage in attempting the impossible, and the moral courage of reformers who challenged established norms.

Fogg represents a peculiarly modern form of courage: the courage of the ordinary man in extraordinary circumstances. He is not a soldier or an explorer; he is a gentleman who made a bet. His courage is democratized bravery — available to anyone with sufficient nerve and a Bradshaw's guide.
