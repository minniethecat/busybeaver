# Precision and Timekeeping

## Definition

Precision and timekeeping in *Around the World in Eighty Days* constitute both the defining quality of Phileas Fogg's character and the structural principle of the novel's plot. Fogg is introduced not as a man who is merely punctual, but as a man who has internalised time to the point of identity: his body is "as exactly regulated as a Leroy chronometer" (002-021), his meals are eaten "at hours mathematically fixed" (001-107), and his daily movements follow a schedule of "unbroken regularity" (002-063). Time for Fogg is not an external constraint but a moral and existential framework -- to deviate from it is not merely inconvenient but a failure of character.

## Role in the Novel

Precision operates in the novel on three levels: structural, dramatic, and ironic.

### Structural Precision

The novel itself is a clock. Its thirty-seven chapters map onto the days of Fogg's journey with the precision of a timetable. The Morning Chronicle's itinerary (003-038--003-040) functions as the novel's master schedule, and each subsequent segment either conforms to or deviates from it. Fogg keeps a journal in which he records time gained and lost: "According to his journal, he was due at Calcutta on the 25th of October, and that was the exact date of his actual arrival. He was therefore neither behind-hand nor ahead of time" (011-132--011-134). The journal is the narrative's own accounting system, reducing the chaos of global travel to columns of numbers.

### Dramatic Precision

Time pressure creates the novel's primary dramatic engine. The wager's eighty-day limit means that every delay has catastrophic implications. When the railway stops at Kholby, Fogg "consulted his watch" and coolly calculates the remaining time (008-029). When the Rangoon is delayed by a storm, the lost twenty-four hours (014-099) seem to doom the enterprise. The precision of Fogg's timekeeping makes these delays legible as crises: without the watch, there is no wager; without the wager, there is no novel.

### Ironic Precision

The novel's deepest irony concerns timekeeping itself. Fogg keeps his watch set to London time throughout the journey, faithfully adding time as he travels eastward. His servant Passepartout, who refuses to adjust his own family heirloom watch, is mocked for provincial stubbornness -- yet the servant's watch accidentally tracks sun-time, which would have revealed the longitude gain. Fogg's discovery aboard the General Grant that "his watch exactly agreed with the ship's chronometers" (020-101) only confirms his blindness: the ship's chronometers also track Greenwich Mean Time. The culmination of this irony is the novel's famous resolution. When Fogg arrives in London believing he is five minutes late and has lost the wager, he has in fact gained a full day by travelling eastward: "sixty-eight days divided by the seventy-two degrees, multiplied by four minutes, gives precisely twenty-four hours" (031-152). Precision, the very quality that seemed to have failed Fogg, is what saves him -- but only because it exceeds his own understanding of it.

## Related Concepts

- **Gentlemanly Conduct**: Precision is the temporal expression of the gentlemanly code. Fogg's exactitude is a form of moral discipline.
- **The Wager**: The eighty-day limit converts precision from a character trait into a plot mechanism.
- **The Timetable**: Bradshaw's Continental Railway Steam Transit and General Guide, together with the steamers' sailing schedules, provides the external framework that Fogg's internal precision must navigate.
- **Railway Travel**: The railway schedule is the most demanding form of temporal precision -- a train leaves at exactly eight (007-002) and waits for no one.
- **International Date Line**: The novel's final joke on precision -- Fogg's methodical timekeeping is so precise that it overlooks the one temporal fact (longitude gain) that makes him a winner.

## Historical and Cultural Context

The Victorian era was the age of standardised time. Before the railway network, towns kept their own local solar time; the Great Western Railway adopted a unified "railway time" in 1840, and by 1880 Greenwich Mean Time was the legal standard throughout Britain. The International Meridian Conference of 1884 established the global system of time zones and the International Date Line -- two years after Verne's novel was published. The novel thus captures a transitional moment: time had been standardised enough for an eighty-day circumnavigation to be scheduled, but not yet standardised enough for Fogg to realise he could gain a day.

Fogg's "Leroy chronometer" (002-021) reflects the prestige of French precision engineering -- Julien Le Roy was the preeminent French clockmaker of the 18th century -- while Bradshaw's railway guide, first published in 1839, had become the indispensable companion of the Victorian traveller.
