# Gentlemanly Conduct

## Definition

Gentlemanly conduct in *Around the World in Eighty Days* refers to the Victorian ideal of masculine behaviour as embodied by Phileas Fogg: punctuality, emotional restraint, financial integrity, and an unshakeable adherence to one's word. This code is not merely decorative -- it drives every major decision Fogg makes, from the acceptance of the wager itself through the rescue of Aouda to the final racing sequence from Liverpool to London. The novel systematically tests whether the code of the gentleman can survive contact with the chaotic realities of global travel, and its answer is an emphatic yes -- but only through the transformation of the gentleman from a passive observer of rules into an active agent of his own fate.

## Role in the Novel

Fogg is introduced as "the most exact and settled gentleman in the United Kingdom" (001-158), a description that serves as the novel's thesis statement about his character. His life at the Reform Club is governed by precise rules: he breakfasts and dines "at hours mathematically fixed" (001-107), walks "with a regular step" (001-113), and retires to bed at exactly midnight (002-086). Passepartout, on his first day of service, pronounces his verdict: "A domestic and regular gentleman! A real machine; well, I don't mind serving a machine" (002-103).

The wager itself is an expression of gentlemanly conduct. Fogg stakes his fortune -- twenty thousand pounds, half his total wealth -- on his word as a gentleman (003-206). His opponents accept this verbal bond because it is guaranteed by the code: a gentleman's word is his credit. Throughout the journey, Fogg's conduct is observed and commented upon by other characters: Sir Francis Cromarty calls the wager "a useless eccentricity" (007-137), but concedes the integrity of the man who made it; Fix, even while suspecting Fogg of robbery, cannot help but note his "phlegmatic" composure (005-075); and Aouda, the novel's moral touchstone, gradually comes to see in Fogg's restraint not coldness but the highest form of care.

The novel's key test of gentlemanly conduct occurs at Hong Kong. When Passepartout is offered five hundred pounds to betray Fogg, he refuses: "I come from a village where they don't eat that kind of bread!" (015-158--015-159). Passepartout's loyalty, despite his comic blunders, is the novel's most sincere endorsement of the gentlemanly code -- not as a matter of social class, but as a matter of personal integrity that transcends class altogether.

## Related Concepts

- **The Wager**: The practical expression of gentlemanly conduct. Fogg's wager is a bet, but it is also a pledge of honour that cannot be abandoned.
- **Duty**: Fogg's sense of obligation extends beyond the wager to Aouda (rescuing her at Pillaji), to Passepartout (posting bail at Calcutta), and ultimately to the Reform Club members who trusted his word.
- **Precision and Timekeeping**: The temporal dimension of gentlemanly conduct. Fogg's exactitude is not obsession but discipline -- the outward form of an inward commitment.
- **Reform Club**: The institutional home of gentlemanly conduct, where the code is reinforced by the daily rituals of whist, dining, and the reading of newspapers.

## Historical and Cultural Context

The Victorian gentleman was a social category as much as a moral one. The Reform Club, founded in 1836, was the quintessential institution of the liberal gentleman -- a place where Whig politicians, bankers, and men of commerce gathered to dine, debate, and play whist. Fogg's membership in this club marks him as a man of substance whose word can be trusted.

But Verne's treatment of the gentleman is not uncritical. The novel repeatedly shows the limits of the code: Fogg's emotional restraint can verge on coldness; his financial generosity can seem impersonal; his refusal to explain himself can lead to unnecessary conflict. The dramatic arc of the novel is, in part, a process of humanising the gentleman -- of showing that the "machine" Passepartout describes can learn to love, to grieve, and to act with spontaneous generosity. By the end, Fogg is still a gentleman, but he is no longer a machine.
