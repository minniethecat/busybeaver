[011-001] [[passepartout|Passepartout]] made it a point, as he made his purchases, to take a good

[011-002] look at the city. It was formerly defended by a noble fort, which has

[011-003] since become a state prison; its commerce has dwindled away, and

[011-004] Passepartout in vain looked about him for such a bazaar as he used to

[011-005] frequent in Regent Street. At last he came upon an elderly, crusty Jew,

[011-006] who sold second-hand articles, and from whom he purchased a dress of

[011-007] Scotch stuff, a large mantle, and a fine otter-skin pelisse, for which

[011-008] he did not hesitate to pay seventy-five pounds. He then returned

[011-009] triumphantly to the station.

[011-010] The influence to which the priests of Pillaji had subjected [[aouda|Aouda]] began

[011-011] gradually to yield, and she became more herself, so that her fine eyes

[011-012] resumed all their soft Indian expression.

[011-013] When the poet-king, Ucaf Uddaul, celebrates the charms of the queen of

[011-014] Ahmehnagara, he speaks thus:

[011-015] “Her shining tresses, divided in two parts, encircle the harmonious

[011-016] contour of her white and delicate cheeks, brilliant in their glow and

[011-017] freshness. Her ebony brows have the form and charm of the bow of Kama,

[011-018] the god of love, and beneath her long silken lashes the purest

[011-019] reflections and a celestial light swim, as in the sacred lakes of

[011-020] Himalaya, in the black pupils of her great clear eyes. Her teeth, fine,

[011-021] equal, and white, glitter between her smiling lips like dewdrops in a

[011-022] passion-flower’s half-enveloped breast. Her delicately formed ears, her

[011-023] vermilion hands, her little feet, curved and tender as the lotus-bud,

[011-024] glitter with the brilliancy of the loveliest pearls of Ceylon, the most

[011-025] dazzling diamonds of Golconda. Her narrow and supple waist, which a

[011-026] hand may clasp around, sets forth the outline of her rounded figure and

[011-027] the beauty of her bosom, where youth in its flower displays the wealth

[011-028] of its treasures; and beneath the silken folds of her tunic she seems

[011-029] to have been modelled in pure silver by the godlike hand of

[011-030] Vicvarcarma, the immortal sculptor.”

[011-031] It is enough to say, without applying this poetical rhapsody to Aouda,

[011-032] that she was a charming woman, in all the European acceptation of the

[011-033] phrase. She spoke English with great purity, and [[the-parsee-guide|the guide]] had not

[011-034] exaggerated in saying that the young Parsee had been transformed by her

[011-035] bringing up.

[011-036] The [[railway-travel|train]] was about to start from [[allahabad|Allahabad]], and [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]] proceeded to

[011-037] pay the guide the price agreed upon for his service, and not a farthing

[011-038] more; which astonished Passepartout, who remembered all that his master

[011-039] owed to the guide’s devotion. He had, indeed, risked his life in the

[011-040] [[spirit-of-adventure|adventure]] at Pillaji, and, if he should be caught afterwards by the

[011-041] Indians, he would with difficulty escape their vengeance. Kiouni, also,

[011-042] must be disposed of. What should be done with the elephant, which had

[011-043] been so dearly purchased? Phileas Fogg had already determined this

[011-044] question.

[011-045] “Parsee,” said he to the guide, “you have been serviceable and devoted.

[011-046] I have paid for your service, but not for your devotion. Would you like

[011-047] to have this elephant? He is yours.”

[011-048] The guide’s eyes glistened.

[011-049] “Your honour is giving me a fortune!” cried he.

[011-050] “Take him, guide,” returned Mr. Fogg, “and I shall still be your

[011-051] debtor.”

[011-052] “Good!” exclaimed Passepartout. “Take him, friend. Kiouni is a brave

[011-053] and faithful beast.” And, going up to the elephant, he gave him several

[011-054] lumps of sugar, saying, “Here, Kiouni, here, here.”

[011-055] The elephant grunted out his satisfaction, and, clasping Passepartout

[011-056] around the waist with his trunk, lifted him as high as his head.

[011-057] Passepartout, not in the least alarmed, caressed the animal, which

[011-058] replaced him gently on the ground.

[011-059] Soon after, Phileas Fogg, [[sir-francis-cromarty|Sir Francis Cromarty]], and Passepartout,

[011-060] installed in a carriage with Aouda, who had the best seat, were

[011-061] whirling at full speed towards Benares. It was a run of eighty miles,

[011-062] and was accomplished in two hours. During the journey, the young woman

[011-063] fully recovered her senses. What was her astonishment to find herself

[011-064] in this carriage, on the railway, dressed in European habiliments, and

[011-065] with travellers who were quite strangers to her! Her companions first

[011-066] set about fully reviving her with a little liquor, and then Sir Francis

[011-067] narrated to her what had passed, dwelling upon the courage with which

[011-068] Phileas Fogg had not hesitated to risk his life to save her, and

[011-069] recounting the happy sequel of the venture, the result of

[011-070] Passepartout’s rash idea. Mr. Fogg said nothing; while Passepartout,

[011-071] abashed, kept repeating that “it wasn’t worth telling.”

[011-072] Aouda pathetically thanked her deliverers, rather with tears than

[011-073] words; her fine eyes interpreted her gratitude better than her lips.

[011-074] Then, as her thoughts strayed back to the scene of the sacrifice, and

[011-075] recalled the dangers which still menaced her, she shuddered with

[011-076] terror.

[011-077] Phileas Fogg understood what was passing in Aouda’s mind, and offered,

[011-078] in order to reassure her, to escort her to [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], where she might

[011-079] remain safely until the affair was hushed up—an offer which she eagerly

[011-080] and gratefully accepted. She had, it seems, a Parsee relation, who was

[011-081] one of the principal merchants of Hong Kong, which is wholly an English

[011-082] city, though on an island on the Chinese coast.

[011-083] At half-past twelve the train stopped at Benares. The Brahmin legends

[011-084] assert that this city is built on the site of the ancient Casi, which,

[011-085] like Mahomet’s tomb, was once suspended between heaven and earth;

[011-086] though the Benares of to-day, which the Orientalists call the Athens of

[011-087] India, stands quite unpoetically on the solid earth, Passepartout

[011-088] caught glimpses of its brick houses and clay huts, giving an aspect of

[011-089] desolation to the place, as the train entered it.

[011-090] Benares was Sir Francis Cromarty’s destination, the troops he was

[011-091] rejoining being encamped some miles northward of the city. He bade

[011-092] adieu to Phileas Fogg, wishing him all success, and expressing the hope

[011-093] that he would come that way again in a less original but more

[011-094] profitable fashion. Mr. Fogg lightly pressed him by the hand. The

[011-095] parting of Aouda, who did not forget what she owed to Sir Francis,

[011-096] betrayed more warmth; and, as for Passepartout, he received a hearty

[011-097] shake of the hand from the gallant general.

[011-098] The railway, on leaving Benares, passed for a while along the valley of

[011-099] the Ganges. Through the windows of their carriage the travellers had

[011-100] glimpses of the diversified landscape of Behar, with its mountains

[011-101] clothed in verdure, its fields of barley, wheat, and corn, its jungles

[011-102] peopled with green alligators, its neat villages, and its still

[011-103] thickly-leaved forests. Elephants were bathing in the waters of the

[011-104] sacred river, and groups of Indians, despite the advanced season and

[011-105] chilly air, were performing solemnly their pious ablutions. These were

[011-106] fervent Brahmins, the bitterest foes of Buddhism, their deities being

[011-107] Vishnu, the solar god, Shiva, the divine impersonation of natural

[011-108] forces, and Brahma, the supreme ruler of priests and legislators. What

[011-109] would these divinities think of India, anglicised as it is to-day, with

[011-110] steamers whistling and scudding along the Ganges, frightening the gulls

[011-111] which float upon its surface, the turtles swarming along its banks, and

[011-112] the faithful dwelling upon its borders?

[011-113] The panorama passed before their eyes like a flash, save when the steam

[011-114] concealed it fitfully from the view; the travellers could scarcely

[011-115] discern the fort of Chupenie, twenty miles south-westward from Benares,

[011-116] the ancient stronghold of the rajahs of Behar; or Ghazipur and its

[011-117] famous rose-water factories; or the tomb of Lord Cornwallis, rising on

[011-118] the left bank of the Ganges; the fortified town of Buxar, or Patna, a

[011-119] large manufacturing and trading-place, where is held the principal

[011-120] opium market of India; or Monghir, a more than European town, for it is

[011-121] as English as Manchester or Birmingham, with its iron foundries,

[011-122] edgetool factories, and high chimneys puffing clouds of black smoke

[011-123] heavenward.

[011-124] Night came on; the train passed on at full speed, in the midst of the

[011-125] roaring of the tigers, bears, and wolves which fled before the

[011-126] locomotive; and the marvels of Bengal, Golconda ruined Gour,

[011-127] Murshedabad, the ancient capital, Burdwan, Hugly, and the French town

[011-128] of Chandernagor, where Passepartout would have been proud to see his

[011-129] country’s flag flying, were hidden from their view in the darkness.

[011-130] [[calcutta|Calcutta]] was reached at seven in the morning, and the packet left for

[011-131] Hong Kong at noon; so that Phileas Fogg had five hours before him.

[011-132] According to his journal, he was due at Calcutta on the 25th of

[011-133] October, and that was the exact date of his actual arrival. He was

[011-134] therefore neither behind-hand nor ahead of time. The two days gained

[011-135] between [[london|London]] and [[bombay|Bombay]] had been lost, as has been seen, in the

[011-136] journey across India. But it is not to be supposed that Phileas Fogg

[011-137] regretted them.

[011-138] CHAPTER XV.

[011-139] IN WHICH THE BAG OF BANKNOTES DISGORGES SOME THOUSANDS OF POUNDS MORE

[011-140] The train entered the station, and Passepartout jumping out first, was

[011-141] followed by Mr. Fogg, who assisted his fair companion to descend.

[011-142] Phileas Fogg intended to proceed at once to the Hong Kong [[steamer-travel|steamer]], in

[011-143] order to get Aouda comfortably settled for the voyage. He was unwilling

[011-144] to leave her while they were still on dangerous ground.

[011-145] Just as he was leaving the station a policeman came up to him, and

[011-146] said, “Mr. Phileas Fogg?”

[011-147] “I am he.”

[011-148] “Is this man your servant?” added the policeman, pointing to

[011-149] Passepartout.

[011-150] “Yes.”

[011-151] “Be so good, both of you, as to follow me.”

[011-152] Mr. Fogg betrayed no surprise whatever. The policeman was a

[011-153] representative of the law, and law is sacred to an Englishman.

[011-154] Passepartout tried to reason about the matter, but the policeman tapped

[011-155] him with his stick, and Mr. Fogg made him a signal to obey.

[011-156] “May this young lady go with us?” asked he.

[011-157] “She may,” replied the policeman.

[011-158] Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout were conducted to a palkigahri, a

[011-159] sort of four-wheeled carriage, drawn by two horses, in which they took

[011-160] their places and were driven away. No one spoke during the twenty

[011-161] minutes which elapsed before they reached their destination. They first

[011-162] passed through the “black town,” with its narrow streets, its

[011-163] miserable, dirty huts, and squalid population; then through the

[011-164] “European town,” which presented a relief in its bright brick mansions,

[011-165] shaded by coconut-trees and bristling with masts, where, although it

[011-166] was early morning, elegantly dressed horsemen and handsome equipages

[011-167] were passing back and forth.

[011-168] The carriage stopped before a modest-looking house, which, however, did

[011-169] not have the appearance of a private mansion. The policeman having

[011-170] requested his prisoners—for so, truly, they might be called—to descend,

[011-171] conducted them into a room with barred windows, and said: “You will

[011-172] appear before Judge Obadiah at half-past eight.”

[011-173] He then retired, and closed the door.

[011-174] “Why, we are prisoners!” exclaimed Passepartout, falling into a chair.

[011-175] Aouda, with an emotion she tried to conceal, said to Mr. Fogg: “Sir,

[011-176] you must leave me to my fate! It is on my account that you receive this

[011-177] treatment, it is for having saved me!”

[011-178] Phileas Fogg contented himself with saying that it was impossible. It

[011-179] was quite unlikely that he should be arrested for preventing a [[the-rescue-of-aouda|suttee]].

[011-180] The complainants would not dare present themselves with such a charge.

[011-181] There was some mistake. Moreover, he would not, in any event, abandon

[011-182] Aouda, but would escort her to Hong Kong.

[011-183] “But the steamer leaves at noon!” observed Passepartout, nervously.

[011-184] “We shall be on board by noon,” replied his master, placidly.

[011-185] It was said so positively that Passepartout could not help muttering to

[011-186] himself, “Parbleu that’s certain! Before noon we shall be on board.”

[011-187] But he was by no means reassured.

[011-188] At half-past eight the door opened, the policeman appeared, and,

[011-189] requesting them to follow him, led the way to an adjoining hall. It was

[011-190] evidently a court-room, and a crowd of Europeans and natives already

[011-191] occupied the rear of the apartment.

[011-192] Mr. Fogg and his two companions took their places on a bench opposite

[011-193] the desks of the magistrate and his clerk. Immediately after, Judge

[011-194] Obadiah, a fat, round man, followed by the clerk, entered. He proceeded

[011-195] to take down a wig which was hanging on a nail, and put it hurriedly on

[011-196] his head.
