# San Francisco

San Francisco is Fogg's portal to the New World — the city where the Pacific crossing ends and the American continent begins. It is also the setting for one of the novel's most memorable set pieces: a chaotic political rally that triggers a chain of events leading to combat on the transcontinental railroad.

## Role in the Novel

Fogg arrives at San Francisco on December 3rd, having crossed the Pacific from Yokohama in nine days aboard the Pacific Mail steamer *General Grant*. (PN: 020-074) Passepartout, in his eagerness to set foot on American soil, executes "a perilous vault" and plunges through worm-eaten planks — a comic entrance into the New World. (PN: 021-004)

With a full day before the six o'clock train to New York, Fogg and Aouda take a carriage to the International Hotel on Montgomery Street. (PN: 021-013) Verne paints a vivid picture of a city transformed: no longer the lawless Gold Rush camp of 1849, but "a great commercial emporium" with wide streets, Anglo-Saxon Gothic churches, palatial warehouses, and a population of "nervously active, gentlemanly-looking men." (PN: 021-019, 021-037)

While strolling through Montgomery Street, Fogg, Aouda, and Fix (whom they encounter by "the greatest chance in the world") walk straight into a political rally for the election of a justice of the peace. (PN: 021-046, 021-094) The crowd erupts into factional violence between supporters of Camerfield and Mandiboy. Fogg, defending Aouda, is about to be struck by "a big brawny fellow with a red beard" when Fix intercepts the blow. (PN: 021-120) The assailant is Colonel Stamp Proctor — and Fogg calmly vows to "come back to America to find him." (PN: 021-173)

The encounter sets the stage for the rematch on the Pacific Railroad, where Proctor and Fogg, revolvers in hand, are interrupted by a Sioux attack. (PN: 025-128)

## Journey Context

San Francisco marks the transition from the maritime to the continental phase of Fogg's journey. The Pacific crossing is complete; the American land crossing, by rail from ocean to ocean, is about to begin. The city is both a waystation and a proving ground — it is here that Fogg first encounters the violence of the American frontier, foreshadowed by the political brawl and the revolvers Passepartout purchases.

The railway connection — the Central Pacific from San Francisco to Ogden, and the Union Pacific from Ogden to Omaha — represents the technological marvel that makes Fogg's schedule possible. Verne treats the transcontinental railroad, completed only three years before the novel's events, with the same wonder he reserves for the Suez Canal: it is "an uninterrupted metal ribbon" measuring 3,786 miles. (PN: 021-189)

## Historical and Cultural Context

In 1872, San Francisco was barely a quarter-century removed from the Gold Rush of 1849. The city's population had swelled from a few hundred to over 150,000, making it the largest city west of the Mississippi. The transcontinental railroad, completed in May 1869 at Promontory Summit, Utah, had transformed San Francisco from an isolated outpost into a hub of global commerce — hence Verne's description of it as "no longer the legendary city of 1849." (PN: 021-019)

The political rally that Fogg stumbles into reflects the rough-and-tumble character of American municipal politics in the post-Civil War era. The "election of a justice of the peace" that devolves into a brawl with canes, fists, and revolvers is Verne's satirical commentary on American democracy — a system so energetic that it cannot help but become violent.
