# Passepartout

If [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] is the novel's clock, Passepartout is its pulse — a French valet of irrepressible good humor whose appetite for [[spirit-of-adventure|adventure]], loyalty to his master, and talent for getting into (and out of) trouble make him one of literature's most beloved servants. His journey from restless wanderer to devoted companion forms the emotional spine of the [[around-the-world-route|circumnavigation]], counterbalancing [[phileas-fogg|Fogg's]] mechanical precision with the [[courage|warmth of human connection]].

## Role in the Novel

Passepartout is hired by [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]] on the very morning [[the-wager|the wager]] is made — October 2, 1872 — and becomes the journey's emotional center. Where [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]] is silent, Passepartout is voluble; where Fogg is calculating, Passepartout is impulsive; where Fogg measures time, Passepartout experiences it. Together they form one of fiction's great complementary pairs.

His role extends beyond comic relief. Passepartout's physical daring — he is a former acrobat — repeatedly saves the expedition: his impersonation of the dead rajah enables [[aouda|Aouda's]] rescue in [[the-rescue-from-suttee|the suttee episode]], his agility on the transcontinental train saves lives during [[the-sioux-attack|the Sioux attack]], and his resourcefulness in [[yokohama]] keeps him alive after being separated from Fogg.

> "Faith, I've seen some strange things in my time, but nothing like this." (002-007)

## Biography

Jean Passepartout is a Frenchman of about thirty who has pursued an extraordinarily varied career: "he had been an itinerant singer, a circus-rider, a professor of gymnastics, and a sergeant in the fire-brigade." His restless life has left him yearning for repose, and he takes the position with [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]] precisely because he believes it will offer the quiet domesticity he craves. (PN: 002-056)

The irony is immediate and complete. Instead of a settled life at Savile Row, [[london]], Passepartout finds himself hurtling around the world at breakneck speed. But his adaptability, honed by years of varied employment, proves invaluable. He can ride an elephant through the Indian jungle, navigate the opium dens of [[hong-kong]], and perform acrobatics in a [[yokohama]] circus — all with the same cheerful competence.

> "His last situation had been at the house of a country gentleman, where he had been coachman and general servant, and from which he had gone away, as he said, on the look-out for adventure." (002-056)

## Character Significance

Passepartout embodies the French popular spirit that Verne, though a Frenchman writing in French, displaced onto an English protagonist. If [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]] represents the stiff-upper-lip ideal of [[gentlemanly-conduct|Victorian gentlemanliness]], Passepartout represents the warmth, wit, and adaptability that the Victorian English admired (and envied) in their cross-Channel neighbors.

Structurally, Passepartout serves as the reader's surrogate. He experiences the journey with the wonder and alarm that [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]] never displays, and his misadventures provide the comic counterpoint to Fogg's deadpan progress. His loyalty, once earned, is absolute — and his joy at Fogg's ultimate success in [[the-wager|the wager]] is the reader's joy as well. His [[spirit-of-adventure|restless energy]] and [[courage|physical bravery]] make him far more than a servant: he is the human face of the [[around-the-world-route|great journey]].
