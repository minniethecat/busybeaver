# Allahabad

Allahabad (now Prayagraj) is an Indian city in the Gangetic plain, situated at the sacred confluence of the [[ganges|Ganges]] and Yamuna rivers. During [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg's]] eighty-day circumnavigation, Allahabad was the critical railway junction where the interrupted [[great-indian-peninsula-railway|Great Indian Peninsula Railway]] line from [[bombay|Bombay]] to [[calcutta|Calcutta]] resumed — the destination that Fogg and his companions raced to reach after the famous [[the-elephant-journey|elephant journey]] across the Kholby gap.

## A Railway Junction of Critical Importance

The Great Indian Peninsula Railway, extending from Bombay toward Calcutta, had an incomplete fifty-mile section between Kholby and Allahabad. As the [[daily-telegraph|newspapers]] reported before Fogg's departure, "the section between Rothal and Allahabad, on the Great Indian Peninsula Railway, is still unfinished" (PN_003-033).

This gap became the first major test of Fogg's resolve. Upon learning at Kholby that the railway ended and passengers would have to find their own way onward, Fogg's servant [[passepartout|Passepartout]] was dismayed, but Fogg himself remained calm. The party acquired an elephant named Kiouni and, guided by [[the-parsee-guide|a Parsee guide]], pressed eastward through jungle and across difficult terrain toward Allahabad.

## The Journey to Allahabad

The overland leg from Kholby to Allahabad took approximately fifteen hours of elephant travel across the territory of Bundelcund. The guide estimated that "supposing it took the elephant fifteen hours to reach Allahabad, they would arrive in time" (PN_008-120). The journey was conducted at night and into the following morning, with the guide leading Kiouni through forests and across open country, avoiding villages where possible.

A secondary crisis arose when the party encountered the procession of a suttee — a widow about to be burned on her husband's funeral pyre. Sir Francis Cromarty recognized the ceremony, and the Parsee guide confirmed that the victim was the widow of an independent rajah of Bundelcund. Fogg, declaring that he would "have yet twelve hours to spare" and could "devote them to the rescue of this woman," decided to intervene. This decision led to the dramatic rescue of [[aouda|Aouda]], who would become Fogg's companion for the remainder of the journey.

## Arrival and Continuation

The station at Allahabad was reached about ten o'clock in the morning following the rescue. The interrupted railway line resumed there, and Fogg, Aouda, Passepartout, and Sir Francis Cromarty boarded the train heading eastward to Calcutta. Upon arrival, the travelers found themselves in "the streets of Allahabad, that is, the City of God" (PN_010-186) — one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in India and a site of religious pilgrimage for Hindus.

## Historical and Cultural Significance

Allahabad's name derives from "Ilahabad" — the City of God — bestowed by the Mughal emperor Akbar in the late 16th century. Its location at the Triveni Sangam (the triple confluence of the Ganges, Yamuna, and the mythical Saraswati rivers) made it a major pilgrimage site long before the railway age. By 1872, when Fogg passed through, Allahabad had become a strategic node in British India's railway network, linking the Bombay presidency with Bengal.
