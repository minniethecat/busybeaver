# The Reform Club

The Pall Mall gentlemen's club that serves as the novel's geographical and social anchor — the place where [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] plays whist, stakes his fortune, and must return to claim victory. The [[reform-club|Reform Club]] is both the alpha and omega of the [[around-the-world-route|circumnavigation]]: Fogg departs from its doors and, eighty days later (give or take a meridian), walks back through them.

## Role in the Novel

The [[reform-club|Reform Club]] is more than setting — it is the institutional embodiment of the Victorian world order that makes the [[the-wager|wager]] possible. Its members include men of politics, finance, and empire; its card rooms host the conversations that become international incidents; its clocks measure the time by which a gentleman's honor is judged.

Fogg's membership in the Club establishes his social standing without revealing his history. We learn that he was admitted "on the recommendation of Messrs. Baring Brothers," the eminent bankers — a detail that conveys both respectability and opacity. He is known at the Club only by his habits: punctual arrival, silent dining, methodical whist. (PN: 001-069)

> "Phileas Fogg was a member of the Reform Club, and that was all that was known of him." (001-069)

The Club's role in the narrative spans the entire novel:

| Narrative Function | Section | Description |
|-------------------|---------|-------------|
| Introduction | 1 | Fogg is introduced through his Club routine — the daily walk from Savile Row to Pall Mall |
| Incitement | 3 | [[the-wager-at-reform-club|The wager]] is proposed and accepted during a whist game |
| Deadline | 1–33 | The Club's clock at 8:45 PM on December 21 is the finish line |
| Climax | 34 | Fogg walks into the Club at the last possible moment — or so he believes |

## The Whist Game

Whist, a forerunner of bridge, is Fogg's sole recreation and the context for [[the-wager-at-reform-club|the fateful wager]]. The card game is not incidental: whist rewards calculation, memory, and emotional control — the very qualities that define Fogg. His fellow players — [[andrew-stuart|Andrew Stuart]], John Sullivan, Samuel Fallentin, and Thomas Flanagan — serve as the chorus of skepticism against which Fogg's conviction is measured.

> "During the progress of the rubber, the conversation turned upon a newspaper paragraph." (003-062)

## Institutional Significance

The real Reform Club, founded in 1836 at 104 Pall Mall, [[london|London]], was the political headquarters of the Liberal Party and a symbol of progressive Victorian values. Its membership included statesmen, writers, and industrialists — the architects of the British Empire whose railways, steamships, and telegraphs made the [[around-the-world-route|eighty-day journey]] thinkable.

Verne's choice of the Reform Club (rather than the more conservative Carlton or the more aristocratic White's) is thematically significant: the Reform Club represented the forward-looking, technologically confident wing of the British establishment. A wager on global circumnavigation belongs in a club whose very name promises reform and progress.
