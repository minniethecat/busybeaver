# Bradshaw's Guide

The railway and steamship timetable guide that [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] carried in his carpet-bag throughout his eighty-day journey. Published monthly in [[london|London]], Bradshaw's _Continental Railway Steam Transit and General Guide_ was the essential reference for any Victorian traveler — a compendium of schedules, connections, and routes that covered the ever-expanding networks of rail and steamship lines across the globe.

## Role in the Novel

Bradshaw's appears at two critical moments in the narrative. When Fogg departs London with his servant [[passepartout|Passepartout]], the guide is among the few items he packs:

> "He took the carpet-bag, opened it, and slipped into it a goodly roll of Bank of England notes, which would pass wherever he might go. Passepartout, seeing the red-bound copy of Bradshaw's Continental Railway Steam Transit and General Guide, thought to himself that his master must be more eccentric than he had supposed." (003-172)

The guide reappears in America, when Fogg consults it to find an alternative route after missing a connection:

> "Fogg learned all this in consulting his 'Bradshaw,' which gave him all the information he required concerning the means of transit across the American continent." (028-001)

## Symbolic Function

Bradshaw's is more than a practical tool — it is a symbol of the rational, calculable world that Fogg inhabits. The guide reduces the planet to a network of timetables, connections, and transit times. If the [[around-the-world-route|eighty-day journey]] was possible, it was because Bradshaw's had already mapped it. [[the-wager|The wager]] was, in a sense, a bet on the accuracy of Bradshaw's.

## Limitations of the Guide

For all its authority, Bradshaw's could not account for the friction of real travel. The novel systematically exposes the gap between the printed timetable and the unpredictable reality of nineteenth-century transit, testing Fogg's faith in the guide at every turn.

The most dramatic failure of Bradshaw's predictive power occurs in India. The guide listed the Great Indian Peninsula Railway as a complete route from Bombay to Calcutta — and newspapers had indeed "announced the opening of the railway throughout" (008-060). But when the train reached the hamlet of Kholby, the conductor delivered a blunt verdict:

> "Certainly. The railway isn't finished. No. There's still a matter of fifty miles to be laid from here to Allahabad, where the line begins again." (008-058)

No timetable could have warned of this gap. The passengers were told they "must provide means of transportation for themselves from Kholby to Allahabad" (008-065). Fogg, characteristically, was unsurprised:

> "Not at all; but I knew that some obstacle or other would sooner or later arise on my route. Nothing, therefore, is lost. I have two days, which I have already gained, to sacrifice." (008-074)

The Kholby gap consumed Fogg's entire time buffer. The narrator later confirms that "the two days gained between London and Bombay had been lost, as has been seen, in the journey across India" (011-135). Bradshaw's could not predict unfinished railways — and the papers could not be trusted to report them accurately.

Weather, too, lay beyond the guide's purview. On the voyage from Singapore to Hong Kong, a November gale forced the Rangoon "to proceed slowly, and the captain estimated that she would reach Hong Kong twenty hours behind time, and more if the storm lasted" (014-056). No timetable accounts for headwinds. Bradshaw's could tell Fogg when a steamer was scheduled to depart; it could not tell him whether the steamer would actually arrive on time.

The most painful demonstration of the guide's limits comes in America. After Fogg misses the Liverpool-bound China by "three-quarters of an hour" (027-177), he consults Bradshaw's — as he always does (028-001) — only to learn that no other steamer "could not cross the Atlantic in time to save the wager." The guide that had made his journey calculable now confirmed its failure. Every schedule it contained was irrelevant if the traveler was not there to meet it.

In this sense, Bradshaw's embodies the central tension of the novel: the desire for precision against the chaos of contingency. Fogg carries the guide across three continents, but he succeeds not because it was infallible — it was demonstrably not — but because he was willing to improvise where its pages ran blank.

## Historical Context

The real Bradshaw's Railway Guide, founded by George Bradshaw in 1839, was the world's first compilation of railway timetables. By the 1870s — the setting of Verne's novel — Bradshaw's had expanded to cover continental Europe and beyond, becoming synonymous with railway travel itself. The guide ceased publication in 1961, a casualty of the age of automobiles and airlines.
