[001-042] CHAPTER I.

[001-043] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG AND PASSEPARTOUT ACCEPT EACH OTHER, THE ONE AS

[001-044] MASTER, THE OTHER AS MAN

[001-045] Mr. [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] lived, in 1872, at No. 7, Saville Row, Burlington

[001-046] Gardens, the house in which Sheridan died in 1814. He was one of the

[001-047] most noticeable members of the [[london|Reform Club]], though he seemed always to

[001-048] avoid attracting attention; an enigmatical personage, about whom little

[001-049] was known, except that he was a polished man of the world. People said

[001-050] that he resembled Byron—at least that his head was Byronic; but he was

[001-051] a bearded, tranquil Byron, who might live on a thousand years without

[001-052] growing old.

[001-053] Certainly an Englishman, it was more doubtful whether Phileas Fogg was

[001-054] a Londoner. He was never seen on ’Change, nor at the Bank, nor in the

[001-055] counting-rooms of the “City”; no ships ever came into London docks of

[001-056] which he was the owner; he had no public employment; he had never been

[001-057] entered at any of the Inns of Court, either at the Temple, or Lincoln’s

[001-058] Inn, or Gray’s Inn; nor had his voice ever resounded in the Court of

[001-059] Chancery, or in the Exchequer, or the Queen’s Bench, or the

[001-060] Ecclesiastical Courts. He certainly was not a manufacturer; nor was he

[001-061] a merchant or a [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] farmer. His name was strange to the

[001-062] scientific and learned societies, and he never was known to take part

[001-063] in the sage deliberations of the Royal Institution or the London

[001-064] Institution, the Artisan’s Association, or the Institution of Arts and

[001-065] Sciences. He belonged, in fact, to none of the numerous societies which

[001-066] swarm in the English capital, from the Harmonic to that of the

[001-067] Entomologists, founded mainly for the purpose of abolishing pernicious

[001-068] insects.

[001-069] Phileas Fogg was a member of the Reform, and that was all.

[001-070] The way in which he got admission to this exclusive club was simple

[001-071] enough.

[001-072] He was recommended by the Barings, with whom he had an open credit. His

[001-073] cheques were regularly paid at sight from his account current, which

[001-074] was always flush.

[001-075] Was Phileas Fogg rich? Undoubtedly. But those who knew him best could

[001-076] not imagine how he had made his fortune, and Mr. Fogg was the last

[001-077] person to whom to apply for the information. He was not lavish, nor, on

[001-078] the contrary, avaricious; for, whenever he knew that money was needed

[001-079] for a noble, useful, or benevolent purpose, he supplied it quietly and

[001-080] sometimes anonymously. He was, in short, the least communicative of

[001-081] men. He talked very little, and seemed all the more mysterious for his

[001-082] taciturn manner. His daily habits were quite open to observation; but

[001-083] whatever he did was so exactly the same thing that he had always done

[001-084] before, that the wits of the curious were fairly puzzled.

[001-085] Had he travelled? It was likely, for no one seemed to know the world

[001-086] more familiarly; there was no spot so secluded that he did not appear

[001-087] to have an intimate acquaintance with it. He often corrected, with a

[001-088] few clear words, the thousand conjectures advanced by members of the

[001-089] club as to lost and unheard-of travellers, pointing out the true

[001-090] probabilities, and seeming as if gifted with a sort of second sight, so

[001-091] often did events justify his predictions. He must have travelled

[001-092] everywhere, at least in the spirit.

[001-093] It was at least certain that Phileas Fogg had not absented himself from

[001-094] London for many years. Those who were honoured by a better acquaintance

[001-095] with him than the rest, declared that nobody could pretend to have ever

[001-096] seen him anywhere else. His sole pastimes were reading the [[passport-and-identity|papers]] and

[001-097] playing whist. He often won at this game, which, as a silent one,

[001-098] harmonised with his nature; but his winnings never went into his purse,

[001-099] being reserved as a fund for his charities. Mr. Fogg played, not to

[001-100] win, but for the sake of playing. The game was in his eyes a contest, a

[001-101] struggle with a difficulty, yet a motionless, unwearying struggle,

[001-102] congenial to his tastes.

[001-103] Phileas Fogg was not known to have either wife or children, which may

[001-104] happen to the most honest people; either relatives or near friends,

[001-105] which is certainly more unusual. He lived alone in his house in Saville

[001-106] Row, whither none penetrated. A single domestic sufficed to serve him.

[001-107] He breakfasted and dined at the club, at hours mathematically fixed, in

[001-108] the same room, at the same table, never taking his meals with other

[001-109] members, much less bringing a guest with him; and went home at exactly

[001-110] midnight, only to retire at once to bed. He never used the cosy

[001-111] chambers which the Reform provides for its favoured members. He passed

[001-112] ten hours out of the twenty-four in Saville Row, either in sleeping or

[001-113] making his toilet. When he chose to take a walk it was with a regular

[001-114] step in the entrance hall with its mosaic flooring, or in the circular

[001-115] gallery with its dome supported by twenty red porphyry Ionic columns,

[001-116] and illumined by blue painted windows. When he breakfasted or dined all

[001-117] the resources of the club—its kitchens and pantries, its buttery and

[001-118] dairy—aided to crowd his table with their most succulent stores; he was

[001-119] served by the gravest waiters, in dress coats, and shoes with swan-skin

[001-120] soles, who proffered the viands in special porcelain, and on the finest

[001-121] linen; club decanters, of a lost mould, contained his sherry, his port,

[001-122] and his cinnamon-spiced claret; while his beverages were refreshingly

[001-123] cooled with ice, brought at great cost from the American lakes.

[001-124] If to live in this style is to be eccentric, it must be confessed that

[001-125] there is something good in eccentricity.

[001-126] The mansion in Saville Row, though not sumptuous, was exceedingly

[001-127] comfortable. The habits of its occupant were such as to demand but

[001-128] little from the sole domestic, but Phileas Fogg required him to be

[001-129] almost superhumanly prompt and regular. On this very 2nd of October he

[001-130] had dismissed [[james-forster|James Forster]], because that luckless youth had brought

[001-131] him shaving-water at eighty-four degrees Fahrenheit instead of

[001-132] eighty-six; and he was awaiting his successor, who was due at the house

[001-133] between eleven and half-past.

[001-134] Phileas Fogg was seated squarely in his armchair, his feet close

[001-135] together like those of a grenadier on parade, his hands resting on his

[001-136] knees, his body straight, his head erect; he was steadily watching a

[001-137] complicated [[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] which indicated the hours, the minutes, the seconds,

[001-138] the days, the months, and the years. At exactly half-past eleven Mr.

[001-139] Fogg would, according to his daily habit, quit Saville Row, and repair

[001-140] to the Reform.

[001-141] A rap at this moment sounded on the door of the cosy apartment where

[001-142] Phileas Fogg was seated, and James Forster, the dismissed servant,

[001-143] appeared.

[001-144] “The new servant,” said he.

[001-145] A young man of thirty advanced and bowed.

[001-146] “You are a Frenchman, I believe,” asked Phileas Fogg, “and your name is

[001-147] John?”

[001-148] “Jean, if monsieur pleases,” replied the newcomer, “[[passepartout|Jean Passepartout]],

[001-149] a surname which has clung to me because I have a natural aptness for

[001-150] going out of one business into another. I believe I’m honest, monsieur,

[001-151] but, to be outspoken, I’ve had several trades. I’ve been an itinerant

[001-152] singer, a circus-rider, when I used to vault like Leotard, and dance on

[001-153] a rope like Blondin. Then I got to be a professor of gymnastics, so as

[001-154] to make better use of my talents; and then I was a sergeant fireman at

[001-155] Paris, and assisted at many a big fire. But I quitted France five years

[001-156] ago, and, wishing to taste the sweets of domestic life, took service as

[001-157] a valet here in England. Finding myself out of place, and hearing that

[001-158] Monsieur Phileas Fogg was the most exact and settled gentleman in the

[001-159] United Kingdom, I have come to monsieur in the hope of living with him

[001-160] a tranquil life, and forgetting even the name of Passepartout.”

[001-161] “Passepartout suits me,” responded Mr. Fogg. “You are well recommended

[001-162] to me; I hear a good report of you. You know my conditions?”

[001-163] “Yes, monsieur.”

[001-164] “Good! What time is it?”

[001-165] “Twenty-two minutes after eleven,” returned Passepartout, drawing an

[001-166] enormous silver watch from the depths of his pocket.

[001-167] “You are too slow,” said Mr. Fogg.

[001-168] “Pardon me, monsieur, it is impossible—”

[001-169] “You are four minutes too slow. No matter; it’s enough to mention the

[001-170] error. Now from this moment, twenty-nine minutes after eleven, a.m.,

[001-171] this Wednesday, 2nd October, you are in my service.”

[001-172] Phileas Fogg got up, took his hat in his left hand, put it on his head

[001-173] with an automatic motion, and went off without a word.
