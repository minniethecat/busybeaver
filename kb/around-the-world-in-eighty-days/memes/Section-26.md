[026-001] [[aouda|Aouda]] was safe; and [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]], who had been in the thickest of the

[026-002] fight, had not received a scratch. [[detective-fix|Fix]] was slightly wounded in the arm.

[026-003] But [[passepartout|Passepartout]] was not to be found, and tears coursed down Aouda’s

[026-004] cheeks.

[026-005] All the passengers had got out of the [[railway-travel|train]], the wheels of which were

[026-006] stained with blood. From the tyres and spokes hung ragged pieces of

[026-007] flesh. As far as the eye could reach on the white plain behind, red

[026-008] trails were visible. The last Sioux were disappearing in the south,

[026-009] along the banks of Republican River.

[026-010] Mr. Fogg, with folded arms, remained motionless. He had a serious

[026-011] decision to make. Aouda, standing near him, looked at him without

[026-012] speaking, and he understood her look. If his servant was a prisoner,

[026-013] ought he not to risk everything to [[the-rescue-of-aouda|rescue]] him from the Indians? “I will

[026-014] find him, living or dead,” said he quietly to Aouda.

[026-015] “Ah, Mr.—Mr. Fogg!” cried she, clasping his hands and covering them

[026-016] with tears.

[026-017] “Living,” added Mr. Fogg, “if we do not lose a moment.”

[026-018] Phileas Fogg, by this resolution, inevitably sacrificed himself; he

[026-019] pronounced his own doom. The delay of a single day would make him lose

[026-020] the [[steamer-travel|steamer]] at [[new-york|New York]], and his [[the-wager|bet]] would be certainly lost. But as he

[026-021] thought, “It is my duty,” he did not hesitate.

[026-022] The commanding officer of Fort Kearney was there. A hundred of his

[026-023] soldiers had placed themselves in a position to defend the station,

[026-024] should the Sioux attack it.

[026-025] “Sir,” said Mr. Fogg to [[captain-speedy|the captain]], “three passengers have

[026-026] disappeared.”

[026-027] “Dead?” asked the captain.

[026-028] “Dead or prisoners; that is the uncertainty which must be solved. Do

[026-029] you propose to pursue the Sioux?”

[026-030] “That’s a serious thing to do, sir,” returned the captain. “These

[026-031] Indians may retreat beyond the Arkansas, and I cannot leave the fort

[026-032] unprotected.”

[026-033] “The lives of three men are in question, sir,” said Phileas Fogg.

[026-034] “Doubtless; but can I risk the lives of fifty men to save three?”

[026-035] “I don’t know whether you can, sir; but you ought to do so.”

[026-036] “Nobody here,” returned the other, “has a right to teach me my duty.”

[026-037] “Very well,” said Mr. Fogg, coldly. “I will go alone.”

[026-038] “You, sir!” cried [[fix-detective|Fix]], coming up; “you go alone in pursuit of the

[026-039] Indians?”

[026-040] “Would you have me leave this poor fellow to perish—him to whom every

[026-041] one present owes his life? I shall go.”

[026-042] “No, sir, you shall not go alone,” cried the captain, touched in spite

[026-043] of himself. “No! you are a brave man. Thirty volunteers!” he added,

[026-044] turning to the soldiers.

[026-045] The whole company started forward at once. The captain had only to pick

[026-046] his men. Thirty were chosen, and an old sergeant placed at their head.

[026-047] “Thanks, captain,” said Mr. Fogg.

[026-048] “Will you let me go with you?” asked Fix.

[026-049] “Do as you please, sir. But if you wish to do me a favour, you will

[026-050] remain with Aouda. In case anything should happen to me—”

[026-051] A sudden pallor overspread the detective’s face. Separate himself from

[026-052] the man whom he had so persistently followed step by step! Leave him to

[026-053] wander about in this desert! Fix gazed attentively at Mr. Fogg, and,

[026-054] despite his suspicions and of the struggle which was going on within

[026-055] him, he lowered his eyes before that calm and frank look.

[026-056] “I will stay,” said he.

[026-057] A few moments after, Mr. Fogg pressed the young woman’s hand, and,

[026-058] having confided to her his precious carpet-bag, went off with the

[026-059] sergeant and his little squad. But, before going, he had said to the

[026-060] soldiers, “My friends, I will divide five thousand dollars among you,

[026-061] if we save the prisoners.”

[026-062] It was then a little past noon.

[026-063] Aouda retired to a waiting-room, and there she waited alone, thinking

[026-064] of the simple and noble generosity, the tranquil courage of Phileas

[026-065] Fogg. He had sacrificed his fortune, and was now risking his life, all

[026-066] without hesitation, from duty, in silence.

[026-067] Fix did not have the same thoughts, and could scarcely conceal his

[026-068] agitation. He walked feverishly up and down the platform, but soon

[026-069] resumed his outward composure. He now saw the folly of which he had

[026-070] been guilty in letting Fogg go alone. What! This man, whom he had just

[026-071] followed around the world, was permitted now to separate himself from

[026-072] him! He began to accuse and abuse himself, and, as if he were director

[026-073] of police, administered to himself a sound lecture for his greenness.

[026-074] “I have been an idiot!” he thought, “and this man will see it. He has

[026-075] gone, and won’t come back! But how is it that I, Fix, who have in my

[026-076] pocket a warrant for his arrest, have been so fascinated by him?

[026-077] Decidedly, I am nothing but an ass!”

[026-078] So reasoned the detective, while the hours crept by all too slowly. He

[026-079] did not know what to do. Sometimes he was tempted to tell Aouda all;

[026-080] but he could not doubt how the young woman would receive his

[026-081] confidences. What course should he take? He thought of pursuing Fogg

[026-082] across the vast white plains; it did not seem impossible that he might

[026-083] overtake him. Footsteps were easily printed on the snow! But soon,

[026-084] under a new sheet, every imprint would be effaced.

[026-085] Fix became discouraged. He felt a sort of insurmountable longing to

[026-086] abandon the game altogether. He could now leave Fort Kearney station,

[026-087] and pursue his journey homeward in peace.

[026-088] Towards two o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] in the afternoon, while it was snowing hard, long

[026-089] whistles were heard approaching from the east. A great shadow, preceded

[026-090] by a wild light, slowly advanced, appearing still larger through the

[026-091] mist, which gave it a fantastic aspect. No train was expected from the

[026-092] east, neither had there been time for the succour asked for by

[026-093] [[the-telegraph|telegraph]] to arrive; the train from Omaha to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] was not due

[026-094] till the next day. The mystery was soon explained.

[026-095] The locomotive, which was slowly approaching with deafening whistles,

[026-096] was that which, having been detached from the train, had continued its

[026-097] [[around-the-world-route|route]] with such terrific rapidity, carrying off the unconscious

[026-098] engineer and stoker. It had run several miles, when, the fire becoming

[026-099] low for want of fuel, the steam had slackened; and it had finally

[026-100] stopped an hour after, some twenty miles beyond Fort Kearney. Neither

[026-101] [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]] nor the stoker was dead, and, after remaining for some

[026-102] time in their swoon, had come to themselves. The train had then

[026-103] stopped. The engineer, when he found himself in the desert, and the

[026-104] locomotive without cars, understood what had happened. He could not

[026-105] imagine how the locomotive had become separated from the train; but he

[026-106] did not doubt that the train left behind was in distress.

[026-107] He did not hesitate what to do. It would be prudent to continue on to

[026-108] Omaha, for it would be dangerous to return to the train, which the

[026-109] Indians might still be engaged in pillaging. Nevertheless, he began to

[026-110] rebuild the fire in the furnace; the pressure again mounted, and the

[026-111] locomotive returned, running backwards to Fort Kearney. This it was

[026-112] which was whistling in the mist.

[026-113] The travellers were glad to see the locomotive resume its place at the

[026-114] head of the train. They could now continue the journey so terribly

[026-115] interrupted.

[026-116] Aouda, on seeing the locomotive come up, hurried out of the station,

[026-117] and asked the conductor, “Are you going to start?”

[026-118] “At once, madam.”

[026-119] “But the prisoners, our unfortunate fellow-travellers—”

[026-120] “I cannot interrupt the trip,” replied the conductor. “We are already

[026-121] three hours behind time.”

[026-122] “And when will another train pass here from San Francisco?”

[026-123] “To-morrow evening, madam.”

[026-124] “To-morrow evening! But then it will be too late! We must wait—”

[026-125] “It is impossible,” responded the conductor. “If you wish to go, please

[026-126] get in.”

[026-127] “I will not go,” said Aouda.

[026-128] Fix had heard this conversation. A little while before, when there was

[026-129] no prospect of proceeding on the journey, he had made up his mind to

[026-130] leave Fort Kearney; but now that the train was there, ready to start,

[026-131] and he had only to take his seat in the car, an irresistible influence

[026-132] held him back. The station platform burned his feet, and he could not

[026-133] stir. The conflict in his mind again began; anger and failure stifled

[026-134] him. He wished to struggle on to the end.

[026-135] Meanwhile the passengers and some of the wounded, among them Colonel

[026-136] [[colonel-proctor|Proctor]], whose injuries were serious, had taken their places in the

[026-137] train. The buzzing of the over-heated boiler was heard, and the steam

[026-138] was escaping from the valves. The engineer whistled, the train started,

[026-139] and soon disappeared, mingling its white smoke with the eddies of the

[026-140] densely falling snow.

[026-141] The detective had remained behind.

[026-142] Several hours passed. The weather was dismal, and it was very cold. Fix

[026-143] sat motionless on a bench in the station; he might have been thought

[026-144] asleep. Aouda, despite the storm, kept coming out of the waiting-room,

[026-145] going to the end of the platform, and peering through the tempest of

[026-146] snow, as if to pierce the mist which narrowed the horizon around her,

[026-147] and to hear, if possible, some welcome sound. She heard and saw

[026-148] nothing. Then she would return, chilled through, to issue out again

[026-149] after the lapse of a few moments, but always in vain.

[026-150] Evening came, and the little band had not returned. Where could they

[026-151] be? Had they found the Indians, and were they having a conflict with

[026-152] them, or were they still wandering amid the mist? The commander of the

[026-153] fort was anxious, though he tried to conceal his apprehensions. As

[026-154] night approached, the snow fell less plentifully, but it became

[026-155] intensely cold. Absolute silence rested on the plains. Neither flight

[026-156] of bird nor passing of beast troubled the perfect calm.

[026-157] Throughout the night Aouda, full of sad forebodings, her heart stifled

[026-158] with anguish, wandered about on the verge of the plains. Her

[026-159] imagination carried her far off, and showed her innumerable dangers.

[026-160] What she suffered through the long hours it would be impossible to

[026-161] describe.

[026-162] Fix remained stationary in the same place, but did not sleep. Once a

[026-163] man approached and spoke to him, and the detective merely replied by

[026-164] shaking his head.

[026-165] Thus the night passed. At dawn, the half-extinguished disc of the sun

[026-166] rose above a misty horizon; but it was now possible to recognise

[026-167] objects two miles off. Phileas Fogg and the squad had gone southward;

[026-168] in the south all was still vacancy. It was then seven o’clock.

[026-169] The captain, who was really alarmed, did not know what course to take.

[026-170] Should he send another detachment to the rescue of the first? Should he

[026-171] sacrifice more men, with so few chances of saving those already

[026-172] sacrificed? His hesitation did not last long, however. Calling one of

[026-173] his lieutenants, he was on the point of ordering a reconnaissance, when

[026-174] gunshots were heard. Was it a signal? The soldiers rushed out of the

[026-175] fort, and half a mile off they perceived a little band returning in

[026-176] good order.

[026-177] Mr. Fogg was marching at their head, and just behind him were

[026-178] Passepartout and the other two travellers, rescued from the Sioux.

[026-179] They had met and fought the Indians ten miles south of Fort Kearney.

[026-180] Shortly before the detachment arrived, Passepartout and his companions

[026-181] had begun to struggle with their captors, three of whom the Frenchman

[026-182] had felled with his fists, when his master and the soldiers hastened up

[026-183] to their relief.

[026-184] All were welcomed with joyful cries. Phileas Fogg distributed the

[026-185] reward he had promised to the soldiers, while Passepartout, not without

[026-186] reason, muttered to himself, “It must certainly be confessed that I

[026-187] cost my master dear!”

[026-188] Fix, without saying a word, looked at Mr. Fogg, and it would have been

[026-189] difficult to analyse the thoughts which struggled within him. As for

[026-190] Aouda, she took her protector’s hand and pressed it in her own, too

[026-191] much moved to speak.
