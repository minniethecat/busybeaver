[027-001] Meanwhile, [[passepartout|Passepartout]] was looking about for the [[railway-travel|train]]; he thought he

[027-002] should find it there, ready to start for Omaha, and he hoped that the

[027-003] time lost might be regained.

[027-004] “The train! the train!” cried he.

[027-005] “Gone,” replied [[detective-fix|Fix]].

[027-006] “And when does the next train pass here?” said [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]].

[027-007] “Not till this evening.”

[027-008] “Ah!” returned the impassible [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] quietly.

[027-009] CHAPTER XXXI.

[027-010] IN WHICH FIX, THE DETECTIVE, CONSIDERABLY FURTHERS THE INTERESTS OF

[027-011] PHILEAS FOGG

[027-012] Phileas Fogg found himself twenty hours behind time. Passepartout, the

[027-013] involuntary cause of this delay, was desperate. He had ruined his

[027-014] master!

[027-015] At this moment the detective approached Mr. Fogg, and, looking him

[027-016] intently in the face, said:

[027-017] “Seriously, sir, are you in great haste?”

[027-018] “Quite seriously.”

[027-019] “I have a purpose in asking,” resumed [[fix-detective|Fix]]. “Is it absolutely necessary

[027-020] that you should be in [[new-york|New York]] on the 11th, before nine o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] in the

[027-021] evening, the time that the [[steamer-travel|steamer]] leaves for [[liverpool|Liverpool]]?”

[027-022] “It is absolutely necessary.”

[027-023] “And, if your journey had not been interrupted by these Indians, you

[027-024] would have reached New York on the morning of the 11th?”

[027-025] “Yes; with eleven hours to spare before the steamer left.”

[027-026] “Good! you are therefore twenty hours behind. Twelve from twenty leaves

[027-027] eight. You must regain eight hours. Do you wish to try to do so?”

[027-028] “On foot?” asked Mr. Fogg.

[027-029] “No; on a sledge,” replied Fix. “On a sledge with sails. A man has

[027-030] proposed such a method to me.”

[027-031] It was the man who had spoken to Fix during the night, and whose offer

[027-032] he had refused.

[027-033] Phileas Fogg did not reply at once; but Fix, having pointed out the

[027-034] man, who was walking up and down in front of the station, Mr. Fogg went

[027-035] up to him. An instant after, Mr. Fogg and the American, whose name was

[027-036] Mudge, entered a hut built just below the fort.

[027-037] There Mr. Fogg examined a curious vehicle, a kind of frame on two long

[027-038] beams, a little raised in front like the runners of a sledge, and upon

[027-039] which there was room for five or six persons. A high mast was fixed on

[027-040] the frame, held firmly by metallic lashings, to which was attached a

[027-041] large brigantine sail. This mast held an iron stay upon which to hoist

[027-042] a jib-sail. Behind, a sort of rudder served to guide the vehicle. It

[027-043] was, in short, a sledge rigged like a sloop. During the winter, when

[027-044] the trains are blocked up by the snow, these sledges make extremely

[027-045] rapid journeys across the frozen plains from one station to another.

[027-046] Provided with more sails than a cutter, and with the wind behind them,

[027-047] they slip over the surface of the prairies with a speed equal if not

[027-048] superior to that of the express trains.

[027-049] Mr. Fogg readily made a bargain with the owner of this land-craft. The

[027-050] wind was favourable, being fresh, and blowing from the west. The snow

[027-051] had hardened, and Mudge was very confident of being able to transport

[027-052] Mr. Fogg in a few hours to Omaha. Thence the trains eastward run

[027-053] frequently to Chicago and New York. It was not impossible that the lost

[027-054] time might yet be recovered; and such an opportunity was not to be

[027-055] rejected.

[027-056] Not wishing to expose [[aouda|Aouda]] to the discomforts of travelling in the

[027-057] open air, Mr. Fogg proposed to leave her with Passepartout at Fort

[027-058] Kearney, the servant taking upon himself to escort her to Europe by a

[027-059] better [[around-the-world-route|route]] and under more favourable conditions. But Aouda refused to

[027-060] separate from Mr. Fogg, and Passepartout was delighted with her

[027-061] decision; for nothing could induce him to leave his master while Fix

[027-062] was with him.

[027-063] It would be difficult to guess the detective’s thoughts. Was this

[027-064] conviction shaken by Phileas Fogg’s return, or did he still regard him

[027-065] as an exceedingly shrewd rascal, who, his journey round the world

[027-066] completed, would think himself absolutely safe in England? Perhaps

[027-067] Fix’s opinion of Phileas Fogg was somewhat modified; but he was

[027-068] nevertheless resolved to do his duty, and to hasten the return of the

[027-069] whole party to England as much as possible.

[027-070] At eight o’clock the sledge was ready to start. The passengers took

[027-071] their places on it, and wrapped themselves up closely in their

[027-072] travelling-cloaks. The two great sails were hoisted, and under the

[027-073] pressure of the wind the sledge slid over the hardened snow with a

[027-074] velocity of forty miles an hour.

[027-075] The distance between Fort Kearney and Omaha, as the birds fly, is at

[027-076] most two hundred miles. If the wind held good, the distance might be

[027-077] traversed in five hours; if no accident happened the sledge might reach

[027-078] Omaha by one o’clock.

[027-079] What a journey! The travellers, huddled close together, could not speak

[027-080] for the cold, intensified by the rapidity at which they were going. The

[027-081] sledge sped on as lightly as a boat over the waves. When the breeze

[027-082] came skimming the earth the sledge seemed to be lifted off the ground

[027-083] by its sails. Mudge, who was at the rudder, kept in a straight line,

[027-084] and by a turn of his hand checked the lurches which the vehicle had a

[027-085] tendency to make. All the sails were up, and the jib was so arranged as

[027-086] not to screen the brigantine. A top-mast was hoisted, and another jib,

[027-087] held out to the wind, added its force to the other sails. Although the

[027-088] speed could not be exactly estimated, the sledge could not be going at

[027-089] less than forty miles an hour.

[027-090] “If nothing breaks,” said Mudge, “we shall get there!”

[027-091] Mr. Fogg had made it for Mudge’s interest to reach Omaha within the

[027-092] time agreed on, by the offer of a handsome reward.

[027-093] The prairie, across which the sledge was moving in a straight line, was

[027-094] as flat as a sea. It seemed like a vast frozen lake. The railroad which

[027-095] ran through this section ascended from the south-west to the north-west

[027-096] by Great Island, Columbus, an important Nebraska town, Schuyler, and

[027-097] Fremont, to Omaha. It followed throughout the right bank of the Platte

[027-098] River. The sledge, shortening this route, took a chord of the arc

[027-099] described by the railway. Mudge was not afraid of being stopped by the

[027-100] Platte River, because it was frozen. The road, then, was quite clear of

[027-101] obstacles, and Phileas Fogg had but two things to fear—an accident to

[027-102] the sledge, and a change or calm in the wind.

[027-103] But the breeze, far from lessening its force, blew as if to bend the

[027-104] mast, which, however, the metallic lashings held firmly. These

[027-105] lashings, like the chords of a stringed instrument, resounded as if

[027-106] vibrated by a violin bow. The sledge slid along in the midst of a

[027-107] plaintively intense melody.

[027-108] “Those chords give the fifth and the octave,” said Mr. Fogg.

[027-109] These were the only words he uttered during the journey. Aouda, cosily

[027-110] packed in furs and cloaks, was sheltered as much as possible from the

[027-111] attacks of the freezing wind. As for Passepartout, his face was as red

[027-112] as the sun’s disc when it sets in the mist, and he laboriously inhaled

[027-113] the biting air. With his natural buoyancy of spirits, he began to hope

[027-114] again. They would reach New York on the evening, if not on the morning,

[027-115] of the 11th, and there were still some chances that it would be before

[027-116] the steamer sailed for Liverpool.

[027-117] Passepartout even felt a strong desire to grasp his ally, Fix, by the

[027-118] hand. He remembered that it was the detective who procured the sledge,

[027-119] the only means of reaching Omaha in time; but, checked by some

[027-120] presentiment, he kept his usual reserve. One thing, however,

[027-121] Passepartout would never forget, and that was the sacrifice which Mr.

[027-122] Fogg had made, without hesitation, to [[the-rescue-of-aouda|rescue]] him from the Sioux. Mr.

[027-123] Fogg had risked his fortune and his life. No! His servant would never

[027-124] forget that!

[027-125] While each of the party was absorbed in reflections so different, the

[027-126] sledge flew past over the vast carpet of snow. The creeks it passed

[027-127] over were not perceived. Fields and streams disappeared under the

[027-128] uniform whiteness. The plain was absolutely deserted. Between the Union

[027-129] [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]] road and the branch which unites Kearney with Saint Joseph it

[027-130] formed a great uninhabited island. Neither village, station, nor fort

[027-131] appeared. From time to time they sped by some phantom-like tree, whose

[027-132] white skeleton twisted and rattled in the wind. Sometimes flocks of

[027-133] wild birds rose, or bands of gaunt, famished, ferocious prairie-wolves

[027-134] ran howling after the sledge. Passepartout, revolver in hand, held

[027-135] himself ready to fire on those which came too near. Had an accident

[027-136] then happened to the sledge, the travellers, attacked by these beasts,

[027-137] would have been in the most terrible danger; but it held on its even

[027-138] course, soon gained on the wolves, and ere long left the howling band

[027-139] at a safe distance behind.

[027-140] [[About]] noon Mudge perceived by certain landmarks that he was crossing

[027-141] the Platte River. He said nothing, but he felt certain that he was now

[027-142] within twenty miles of Omaha. In less than an hour he left the rudder

[027-143] and furled his sails, whilst the sledge, carried forward by the great

[027-144] impetus the wind had given it, went on half a mile further with its

[027-145] sails unspread.

[027-146] It stopped at last, and Mudge, pointing to a mass of roofs white with

[027-147] snow, said: “We have got there!”

[027-148] Arrived! Arrived at the station which is in daily communication, by

[027-149] numerous trains, with the Atlantic seaboard!

[027-150] Passepartout and Fix jumped off, stretched their stiffened limbs, and

[027-151] aided Mr. Fogg and the young woman to descend from the sledge. Phileas

[027-152] Fogg generously rewarded Mudge, whose hand Passepartout warmly grasped,

[027-153] and the party directed their steps to the Omaha railway station.

[027-154] The Pacific Railroad proper finds its terminus at this important

[027-155] Nebraska town. Omaha is connected with Chicago by the Chicago and Rock

[027-156] Island Railroad, which runs directly east, and passes fifty stations.

[027-157] A train was ready to start when Mr. Fogg and his party reached the

[027-158] station, and they only had time to get into the cars. They had seen

[027-159] nothing of Omaha; but Passepartout confessed to himself that this was

[027-160] not to be regretted, as they were not travelling to see the sights.

[027-161] The train passed rapidly across the State of Iowa, by Council Bluffs,

[027-162] Des Moines, and Iowa City. During the night it crossed the Mississippi

[027-163] at Davenport, and by Rock Island entered Illinois. The next day, which

[027-164] was the 10th, at four o’clock in the evening, it reached Chicago,

[027-165] already risen from its ruins, and more proudly seated than ever on the

[027-166] borders of its beautiful Lake Michigan.

[027-167] Nine hundred miles separated Chicago from New York; but trains are not

[027-168] wanting at Chicago. Mr. Fogg passed at once from one to the other, and

[027-169] the locomotive of the Pittsburgh, Fort Wayne, and Chicago Railway left

[027-170] at full speed, as if it fully comprehended that that gentleman had no

[027-171] time to lose. It traversed Indiana, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and New Jersey

[027-172] like a flash, rushing through towns with antique names, some of which

[027-173] had streets and car-tracks, but as yet no houses. At last the Hudson

[027-174] came into view; and, at a quarter-past eleven in the evening of the

[027-175] 11th, the train stopped in the station on the right bank of the river,

[027-176] before the very pier of the Cunard line.

[027-177] The “China,” for Liverpool, had started three-quarters of an hour

[027-178] before!

[027-179] CHAPTER XXXII.

[027-180] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG ENGAGES IN A DIRECT STRUGGLE WITH BAD FORTUNE

[027-181] The “China,” in leaving, seemed to have carried off Phileas Fogg’s last

[027-182] hope. None of the other steamers were able to serve his projects. The

[027-183] “Pereire,” of the French Transatlantic Company, whose admirable

[027-184] steamers are equal to any in speed and comfort, did not leave until the

[027-185] 14th; the Hamburg boats did not go directly to Liverpool or [[london|London]], but

[027-186] to Havre; and the additional trip from Havre to Southampton would

[027-187] render Phileas Fogg’s last efforts of no avail. The Inman steamer did

[027-188] not depart till the next day, and could not cross the Atlantic in time

[027-189] to save the [[the-wager|wager]].
