# The Rescue from Suttee

In the jungles of Bundelkhand, [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] makes the decision that defines him: he diverts from his precious timetable to rescue [[aouda]], a young Parsi widow, from ritual immolation on her husband's funeral pyre. The rescue is the novel's moral turning point — the moment when [[the-wager|the wager]] becomes subordinate to human obligation, and when Fogg the machine reveals the heart that has always been ticking beneath the surface. (012-001)

## Summary

While crossing India by elephant (necessitated by an unfinished section of the [[railway-travel|Great Indian Peninsula Railway]]), [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]] and his party encounter a procession carrying a young woman to a suttee ceremony — the ritual burning of a widow on her husband's pyre. The woman, [[aouda]], is the daughter of a wealthy Bombay merchant, married against her will to an aged rajah who has now died. She has been drugged with opium and is being carried to her death. (012-001, 012-045)

Fogg, who has been conserving every minute of his eighty-day schedule, declares that he will use the twelve hours he has gained on his itinerary to attempt a rescue. [[passepartout|Passepartout]], drawing on his acrobat training, impersonates the dead rajah and rises from the pyre at the crucial moment, terrifying the priests and enabling the party to spirit [[aouda]] away. (012-056, 012-081)

> "I have yet twelve hours to spare. I will devote them to saving this woman." (012-056)

## Narrative Significance

The rescue is the novel's most important structural pivot:

| Before the Rescue | After the Rescue |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Fogg is a machine driven by calculation | Fogg is a man capable of risking everything for another |
| The journey is a sporting proposition | The journey acquires moral weight |
| [[passepartout]] is a servant | Passepartout becomes a hero in his own right |
| [[aouda]] is absent | Aouda becomes the journey's emotional center |
| [[the-wager|The wager]] defines all decisions | Human bonds begin to compete with the timetable |

## Key Participants

| Participant | Role | Contribution |

| [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] | Decision-maker | Commits twelve precious hours to the rescue — the first time he volunteers a delay (012-056) |

## Consequences

The rescue transforms every subsequent element of the novel:

1. **Aouda's presence**: She becomes a permanent member of the party, traveling with Fogg from India through Hong Kong, Yokohama, San Francisco, and across America to Liverpool (012-110)
2. **Fogg's character**: The rescue reveals that Fogg's precision is a form of discipline, not coldness — he is capable of risking his [[the-wager|wager]] for a stranger (012-056)
3. **Fix's calculus**: [[detective-fix|Fix]] must now reckon with a Fogg who does not fit the profile of a bank robber
4. **The novel's ending**: Aouda's proposal of marriage to Fogg at the novel's conclusion is the direct consequence of the rescue — the human connection established in the Indian jungle
5. **Thematic resolution**: The novel's argument — that human bonds transcend calculation — is first articulated in this scene
