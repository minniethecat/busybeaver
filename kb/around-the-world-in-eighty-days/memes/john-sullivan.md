# John Sullivan

John Sullivan is a banker and member of the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] who appears in the novel's opening chapters as one of the whist players present when [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] makes his famous wager to circumnavigate the globe in eighty days.

## Role in the Novel

Sullivan is one of five Reform Club members who gather daily for a game of whist. Along with Samuel Fallentin (banker), Andrew Stuart (engineer), Gauthier Ralph (director of the Bank of England), and Thomas Flanagan (brewer), Sullivan forms the audience before whom Fogg declares his intention — and stakes his fortune. (PN: 003-032)

When conversation turns to a newspaper article calculating the theoretical minimum time for a round-the-world journey, Sullivan is among those who argue that unforeseen delays make the schedule impossible. His skepticism, shared by his fellow whist players, goads Fogg into the wager that sets the entire plot in motion. (PN: 031-031)

> "That is true, gentlemen," added John Sullivan. "Only eighty days, now that the section between Rothal and Allahabad, on the Great Indian Peninsula Railway, is opened." (003-032)

## The Wager Scene

Sullivan's role is most prominent during the wager scene itself. As Fogg calculates the precise schedule — London to Suez, Suez to Bombay, across India, to Hong Kong, Yokohama, San Francisco, New York, and back to London — Sullivan and Fallentin serve as the financial voices of the club, calculating odds and raising practical objections. (PN: 031-054)

When the moment of decision arrives and Fogg places £20,000 on the table, Sullivan is among the five who accept the wager. His voice marks the final countdown before Fogg departs: "Sixteen minutes to nine!" — the precise moment Fogg must leave to begin his journey on schedule. (PN: 031-084)

## Significance

As a representative of England's financial establishment, Sullivan embodies the rational skepticism that Fogg's wager defies. His presence at the whist table grounds the novel's opening in the real world of London's gentlemen's clubs, where vast sums could indeed change hands over a game of cards and a debate about railway timetables.
