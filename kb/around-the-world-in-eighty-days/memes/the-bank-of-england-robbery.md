# The Bank of England Robbery

On 29 September 1872, three days before Phileas Fogg's departure from London, a gentleman walked out of the Bank of England with fifty-five thousand pounds in banknotes — stolen from the chief cashier's desk in broad daylight. The theft triggered one of the largest manhunts in British history, and its most consequential consequence was the pursuit of an entirely innocent man around the world. (PN: 002-029)

## The Crime

The robbery was unusually brazen. A well-dressed gentleman — described by witnesses as having polished manners and "the air of a perfectly well-bred man" — entered the Bank on the morning of 29 September, approached the chief cashier's counter, and walked away with a bundle of notes valued at fifty-five thousand pounds. The method was simple to the point of audacity: the gentleman appeared so respectable that no one thought to challenge him. (PN: 002-053)

News of the theft spread rapidly through London's clubs and newspapers. The Bank offered a reward of two thousand pounds for information leading to the robber's arrest, plus a percentage of the recovered funds. "Picked detectives" were dispatched to all the principal ports of England and the Continent — Liverpool, Southampton, Brindisi, Suez — on the theory that the thief would attempt to flee the country. (PN: 002-059)

## The Case Against Fogg

Phileas Fogg fell under suspicion for three reasons. First, his physical description — a gentleman of regular habits, about forty, well-dressed — closely matched the witness accounts of the robber. Second, Fogg departed London on the very evening the robbery was discovered, travelling toward Brindisi. Third, Fogg's behaviour — his mathematical precision, his silence, his apparent indifference to scenery — struck Fix as exactly the sort of cold-blooded calculation one would expect from a criminal mastermind.

The irony, of course, is that Fogg is entirely innocent. He is not fleeing justice; he is racing toward it. The £20,000 he carries for the journey is his own money, drawn methodically from his account at Baring Brothers. The fifty-five thousand pounds Fogg spends during the journey — on elephants, steamers, bribes, and coal — comes from his own fortune, not the Bank of England's. Fix's entire pursuit is built on a mistake, and the novel's tension derives from the reader's knowledge that Fix is wrong while Fogg has no idea he is being pursued. (PN: 004-015)

## Fix's Investigation

Detective Fix of Scotland Yard is assigned to Suez as part of the dragnet. When the steamer *Mongolia* arrives carrying a passenger who matches the robber's description — and whose servant asks the consul to stamp his passport without his master having to appear in person — Fix is convinced he has found his man. He telegraphs London: "I've found the bank robber, Phileas Fogg. Send without delay a warrant of arrest to Bombay." (PN: 004-040)

The warrant, however, can only be served on British soil. Fix spends the remainder of the novel following Fogg across India, through Hong Kong, across the Pacific, and across America — waiting for the moment when he can make the arrest. When that moment finally arrives in Liverpool, Fogg has already spent his entire fortune and won Aouda's love. The arrest is the novel's darkest moment, and it is made possible entirely by the robbery that set the whole plot in motion.

## Narrative Significance

The Bank robbery is the engine that drives the novel's secondary plot. Without it, Fix has no reason to pursue Fogg; without Fix, the journey lacks its antagonist. The robbery also serves a thematic function: it introduces the theme of mistaken identity that runs through the entire narrative. Fogg, the most predictable man in London, is mistaken for the most audacious thief in England. Aouda, the suttee widow, is mistaken for a willing sacrifice. Passepartout, the loyal servant, is mistaken for Fix's accomplice. The novel repeatedly asks: can you ever really know a person by their surface?

> "In the first place, he is no robber at all," returned Ralph. (PN: 002-084)
