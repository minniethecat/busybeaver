# Thomas Flanagan

Thomas Flanagan was a brewer and member of the [[reform-club|Reform Club]], one of the five gentlemen who gathered daily for a game of whist with [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]. He was present on the evening of October 2, 1872, when conversation about the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] robbery and a newspaper article on global travel times led Fogg to wager his fortune on circumnavigating the globe in eighty days. (PN: 003-025)

## Role in the Wager

Flanagan was among the group of Reform Club members — alongside [[andrew-stuart|Andrew Stuart]], [[john-sullivan|John Sullivan]], Samuel Fallentin, and [[gauthier-ralph|Gauthier Ralph]] — who formed Fogg's usual whist table. All were rich and highly respectable figures, even by the standards of a club that included the princes of English trade and finance. When Stuart challenged Fogg's optimistic interpretation of the eighty-day timetable, Flanagan and the others provided the skeptical chorus that drove the stakes higher. (PN: 003-028)

During the discussion, Fogg handed the deck of cards to Flanagan and calmly said "Cut, sir," before continuing to make his case — a gesture of perfect composure that underlined the unshakeable character for which Fogg was known. (PN: 003-034)

## The Final Day

On the eightieth day, Flanagan joined his fellow club members in the anxious wait for Fogg's arrival. When the clock passed twenty minutes past eight, he voiced the growing consensus: Fogg's project had been absurdly foolish, and whatever his punctuality, he could not prevent the delays that had accumulated against him. His judgement, like that of the other whist players, was overturned when Fogg appeared at the club moments before the deadline and won the wager. (PN: 030-139)
