# James Forster

The servant whose two-degree error costs him his position — and whose dismissal on the morning of October 2, 1872 creates the vacancy that Passepartout fills, setting the entire adventure in motion.

## Role in the Novel

James Forster never appears in the novel. He exists only as a cautionary reference: the servant dismissed by Fogg for the unforgivable sin of serving shaving water at eighty-four degrees rather than the prescribed eighty-six. Passepartout, learning of his predecessor's fate, resolves to avoid any such error — a resolution that sharpens the irony when he himself becomes the agent of delay and complication. (PN: 002-088)

Forster's ghost haunts the novel's early chapters. Passepartout's anxiety about his new master's exacting standards is given concrete form by Forster's example. The fact that Fogg dismissed a man over two degrees of water temperature establishes, more economically than any description could, the precise nature of the machine-like gentleman Passepartout has agreed to serve.

## Key Actions

- Dismissed by Fogg for a two-degree error in shaving water temperature — an event that occurs before the novel begins (002-088)

## Character Significance

Forster is a masterclass in economical characterization. He never speaks, never appears, and exists only as a reference — yet his shadow shapes Passepartout's behavior throughout the early chapters. He is the before-photograph against which Passepartout's own service will be measured, and his fate is the worst-case scenario that Passepartout's misadventures threaten to realize.

> "The fact is, that his predecessor, having brought him shaving-water at eighty-four degrees instead of eighty-six, had been dismissed." (002-088)
