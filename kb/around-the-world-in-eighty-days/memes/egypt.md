# Egypt

Egypt, and specifically the Isthmus of Suez, is the first foreign land that [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] sets foot upon after departing London — and the place where [[fix-detective|Detective Fix]] first identifies him as the suspected Bank of England robber. Though Fogg never leaves the port, the Suez passage represents the first gate of his global circuit and the origin of the parallel journey that will shadow him to the novel's end.

## The Suez Canal

The Suez Canal, opened in 1869 — just three years before Fogg's journey — had transformed global travel by eliminating the long passage around the Cape of Good Hope. The *Mongolia*, belonging to the Peninsular and Oriental Company, made the passage from Brindisi to Suez in four days, and from Suez to Bombay in thirteen. The canal was "one of the fastest" routes in the company's service, and the P&O steamers maintained a regular schedule between Brindisi, Suez, and Bombay. (PN: 004-106)

The canal was also an emblem of imperial engineering. Conceived and financed by French interests but vital to British imperial communication with India, it represented the entanglement of technology, empire, and commerce that made Fogg's eighty-day circuit conceivable. Without the canal, the journey to Bombay would have required the long passage around Africa — adding weeks to the itinerary and making the wager impossible. (PN: 004-108)

## Fix at Suez

The Suez stop is narrated primarily from the perspective of Fix, the Scotland Yard detective who has been stationed at the port to intercept the suspected bank robber. Fix paces the quay, watching the approaching steamer with the anxiety of a hunter who fears his prey has taken another route. When the *Mongolia* arrives and a passenger matching the description presents his passport for visa — "an indispensable formality" in Fogg's own words — Fix is certain he has found his man. (PN: 004-113)

The passport visa, which Fogg obtains with characteristic precision, becomes the first documentary evidence of his passage. Fix, examining the passport, notes that it bears "the description of the bearer" — height, complexion, hair color — all of which match the suspect. Yet Fix cannot act: to arrest Fogg in Egypt he would need a warrant from London, and the telegraphic dispatch requesting one has only just been sent. He must wait, and follow. (PN: 004-125)

## A Liminal Space

Egypt in the novel is a liminal space — not a destination but a threshold. Fogg never leaves the steamer's vicinity. The port is a zone of transit, populated by British consuls, P&O agents, and the motley commerce of empire. Passepartout, more curious than his master, observes the scene: "the town of Suez, built at the extremity of the gulf, a collection of houses standing out against the sandy shore." But Fogg, absorbed in his cards and his calculations, registers nothing. (PN: 005-001)

The contrast is deliberate. Fogg traverses the most storied landscapes of the world — the Red Sea, the Indian subcontinent, the Pacific — with the detachment of a man reading a railway timetable. Egypt, the land of the Pharaohs, passes without remark. It is not that Fogg fails to see it; it is that he has defined "seeing" out of the purpose of his journey.

> "How long will she stop at Suez?" — Fogg's sole question upon arrival (PN: 004-177)
