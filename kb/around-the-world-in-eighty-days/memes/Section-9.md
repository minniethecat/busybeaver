[009-001] In the midst of his gaiety, however, one thought troubled the worthy

[009-002] servant. What would [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]] do with the elephant when he got to

[009-003] [[allahabad|Allahabad]]? Would he carry him on with him? Impossible! The cost of

[009-004] transporting him would make him ruinously expensive. Would he sell him,

[009-005] or set him free? The estimable beast certainly deserved some

[009-006] consideration. Should Mr. Fogg choose to make him, [[passepartout|Passepartout]], a

[009-007] present of Kiouni, he would be very much embarrassed; and these

[009-008] thoughts did not cease worrying him for a long time.

[009-009] The principal chain of the Vindhias was crossed by eight in the

[009-010] evening, and another halt was made on the northern slope, in a ruined

[009-011] bungalow. They had gone nearly twenty-five miles that day, and an equal

[009-012] distance still separated them from the station of Allahabad.

[009-013] The night was cold. The Parsee lit a fire in the bungalow with a few

[009-014] dry branches, and the warmth was very grateful, provisions purchased at

[009-015] Kholby sufficed for supper, and the travellers ate ravenously. The

[009-016] conversation, beginning with a few disconnected phrases, soon gave

[009-017] place to loud and steady snores. The guide watched Kiouni, who slept

[009-018] standing, bolstering himself against the trunk of a large tree. Nothing

[009-019] occurred during the night to disturb the slumberers, although

[009-020] occasional growls from panthers and chatterings of monkeys broke the

[009-021] silence; the more formidable beasts made no cries or hostile

[009-022] demonstration against the occupants of the bungalow. [[sir-francis-cromarty|Sir Francis]] slept

[009-023] heavily, like an honest soldier overcome with fatigue. Passepartout was

[009-024] wrapped in uneasy dreams of the bouncing of the day before. As for Mr.

[009-025] Fogg, he slumbered as peacefully as if he had been in his serene

[009-026] mansion in Saville Row.

[009-027] The journey was resumed at six in the morning; [[the-parsee-guide|the guide]] hoped to reach

[009-028] Allahabad by evening. In that case, Mr. Fogg would only lose a part of

[009-029] the forty-eight hours saved since the beginning of the tour. Kiouni,

[009-030] resuming his rapid gait, soon descended the lower spurs of the

[009-031] Vindhias, and towards noon they passed by the village of Kallenger, on

[009-032] the Cani, one of the branches of the Ganges. The guide avoided

[009-033] inhabited places, thinking it safer to keep the open country, which

[009-034] lies along the first depressions of the basin of the great river.

[009-035] Allahabad was now only twelve miles to the north-east. They stopped

[009-036] under a clump of bananas, the fruit of which, as healthy as bread and

[009-037] as succulent as cream, was amply partaken of and appreciated.

[009-038] At two o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] the guide entered a thick forest which extended several

[009-039] miles; he preferred to travel under cover of the woods. They had not as

[009-040] yet had any unpleasant encounters, and the journey seemed on the point

[009-041] of being successfully accomplished, when the elephant, becoming

[009-042] restless, suddenly stopped.

[009-043] It was then four o’clock.

[009-044] “What’s the matter?” asked Sir Francis, putting out his head.

[009-045] “I don’t know, officer,” replied the Parsee, listening attentively to a

[009-046] confused murmur which came through the thick branches.

[009-047] The murmur soon became more distinct; it now seemed like a distant

[009-048] concert of human voices accompanied by brass instruments. Passepartout

[009-049] was all eyes and ears. Mr. Fogg patiently waited without a word. The

[009-050] Parsee jumped to the ground, fastened the elephant to a tree, and

[009-051] plunged into the thicket. He soon returned, saying:

[009-052] “A procession of Brahmins is coming this way. We must prevent their

[009-053] seeing us, if possible.”

[009-054] The guide unloosed the elephant and led him into a thicket, at the same

[009-055] time asking the travellers not to stir. He held himself ready to

[009-056] bestride the animal at a moment’s notice, should flight become

[009-057] necessary; but he evidently thought that the procession of the faithful

[009-058] would pass without perceiving them amid the thick foliage, in which

[009-059] they were wholly concealed.

[009-060] The discordant tones of the voices and instruments drew nearer, and now

[009-061] droning songs mingled with the sound of the tambourines and cymbals.

[009-062] The head of the procession soon appeared beneath the trees, a hundred

[009-063] paces away; and the strange figures who performed the religious

[009-064] ceremony were easily distinguished through the branches. First came the

[009-065] priests, with mitres on their heads, and clothed in long lace robes.

[009-066] They were surrounded by men, women, and children, who sang a kind of

[009-067] lugubrious psalm, interrupted at regular intervals by the tambourines

[009-068] and cymbals; while behind them was drawn a car with large wheels, the

[009-069] spokes of which represented serpents entwined with each other. Upon the

[009-070] car, which was drawn by four richly caparisoned zebus, stood a hideous

[009-071] statue with four arms, the body coloured a dull red, with haggard eyes,

[009-072] dishevelled hair, protruding tongue, and lips tinted with betel. It

[009-073] stood upright upon the figure of a prostrate and headless giant.

[009-074] Sir Francis, recognising the statue, whispered, “The goddess Kali; the

[009-075] goddess of love and death.”

[009-076] “Of death, perhaps,” muttered back Passepartout, “but of love—that ugly

[009-077] old hag? Never!”

[009-078] The Parsee made a motion to keep silence.

[009-079] A group of old fakirs were capering and making a wild ado round the

[009-080] statue; these were striped with ochre, and covered with cuts whence

[009-081] their blood issued drop by drop—stupid fanatics, who, in the great

[009-082] Indian ceremonies, still throw themselves under the wheels of

[009-083] Juggernaut. Some Brahmins, clad in all the sumptuousness of Oriental

[009-084] apparel, and leading a woman who faltered at every step, followed. This

[009-085] woman was young, and as fair as a European. Her head and neck,

[009-086] shoulders, ears, arms, hands, and toes were loaded down with jewels and

[009-087] gems with bracelets, earrings, and rings; while a tunic bordered with

[009-088] gold, and covered with a light muslin robe, betrayed the outline of her

[009-089] form.

[009-090] The guards who followed the young woman presented a violent contrast to

[009-091] her, armed as they were with naked sabres hung at their waists, and

[009-092] long damascened pistols, and bearing a corpse on a palanquin. It was

[009-093] the body of an old man, gorgeously arrayed in the habiliments of a

[009-094] rajah, wearing, as in life, a turban embroidered with pearls, a robe of

[009-095] tissue of silk and gold, a scarf of cashmere sewed with diamonds, and

[009-096] the magnificent weapons of a Hindoo prince. Next came the musicians and

[009-097] a rearguard of capering fakirs, whose cries sometimes drowned the noise

[009-098] of the instruments; these closed the procession.

[009-099] Sir Francis watched the procession with a sad countenance, and, turning

[009-100] to the guide, said, “A [[the-rescue-of-aouda|suttee]].”

[009-101] The Parsee nodded, and put his finger to his lips. The procession

[009-102] slowly wound under the trees, and soon its last ranks disappeared in

[009-103] the depths of the wood. The songs gradually died away; occasionally

[009-104] cries were heard in the distance, until at last all was silence again.

[009-105] Phileas Fogg had heard what Sir Francis said, and, as soon as the

[009-106] procession had disappeared, asked: “What is a suttee?”

[009-107] “A suttee,” returned the general, “is a human sacrifice, but a

[009-108] voluntary one. The woman you have just seen will be burned to-morrow at

[009-109] the dawn of day.”

[009-110] “Oh, the scoundrels!” cried Passepartout, who could not repress his

[009-111] indignation.

[009-112] “And the corpse?” asked Mr. Fogg.

[009-113] “Is that of the prince, her husband,” said the guide; “an independent

[009-114] rajah of Bundelcund.”

[009-115] “Is it possible,” resumed Phileas Fogg, his voice betraying not the

[009-116] least emotion, “that these barbarous customs still exist in India, and

[009-117] that the English have been unable to put a stop to them?”

[009-118] “These sacrifices do not occur in the larger portion of India,” replied

[009-119] Sir Francis; “but we have no power over these savage territories, and

[009-120] especially here in Bundelcund. The whole district north of the Vindhias

[009-121] is the theatre of incessant murders and pillage.”

[009-122] “The poor wretch!” exclaimed Passepartout, “to be burned alive!”

[009-123] “Yes,” returned Sir Francis, “burned alive. And, if she were not, you

[009-124] cannot conceive what treatment she would be obliged to submit to from

[009-125] her relatives. They would shave off her hair, feed her on a scanty

[009-126] allowance of rice, treat her with contempt; she would be looked upon as

[009-127] an unclean creature, and would die in some corner, like a scurvy dog.

[009-128] The prospect of so frightful an existence drives these poor creatures

[009-129] to the sacrifice much more than love or religious fanaticism.

[009-130] Sometimes, however, the sacrifice is really voluntary, and it requires

[009-131] the active interference of the Government to prevent it. Several years

[009-132] ago, when I was living at [[bombay|Bombay]], a young widow asked permission of the

[009-133] governor to be burned along with her husband’s body; but, as you may

[009-134] imagine, he refused. The woman left the town, took refuge with an

[009-135] independent rajah, and there carried out her self-devoted purpose.”

[009-136] While Sir Francis was speaking, the guide shook his head several times,

[009-137] and now said: “The sacrifice which will take place to-morrow at dawn is

[009-138] not a voluntary one.”

[009-139] “How do you know?”

[009-140] “Everybody knows about this affair in Bundelcund.”

[009-141] “But the wretched creature did not seem to be making any resistance,”

[009-142] observed Sir Francis.

[009-143] “That was because they had intoxicated her with fumes of hemp and

[009-144] opium.”

[009-145] “But where are they taking her?”

[009-146] “To the pagoda of Pillaji, two miles from here; she will pass the night

[009-147] there.”

[009-148] “And the sacrifice will take place—”

[009-149] “To-morrow, at the first light of dawn.”

[009-150] The guide now led the elephant out of the thicket, and leaped upon his

[009-151] neck. Just at the moment that he was about to urge Kiouni forward with

[009-152] a peculiar whistle, Mr. Fogg stopped him, and, turning to Sir Francis

[009-153] Cromarty, said, “Suppose we save this woman.”

[009-154] “Save the woman, Mr. Fogg!”

[009-155] “I have yet twelve hours to spare; I can devote them to that.”

[009-156] “Why, you are a man of heart!”

[009-157] “Sometimes,” replied Phileas Fogg, quietly; “when I have the time.”

[009-158] CHAPTER XIII.

[009-159] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT RECEIVES A NEW PROOF THAT FORTUNE FAVORS THE

[009-160] BRAVE

[009-161] The project was a bold one, full of difficulty, perhaps impracticable.

[009-162] Mr. Fogg was going to risk life, or at least liberty, and therefore the

[009-163] success of his tour. But he did not hesitate, and he found in Sir

[009-164] Francis Cromarty an enthusiastic ally.

[009-165] As for Passepartout, he was ready for anything that might be proposed.

[009-166] His master’s idea charmed him; he perceived a heart, a soul, under that

[009-167] icy exterior. He began to love Phileas Fogg.

[009-168] There remained the guide: what course would he adopt? Would he not take

[009-169] part with the Indians? In default of his assistance, it was necessary

[009-170] to be assured of his neutrality.

[009-171] Sir Francis frankly put the question to him.

[009-172] “Officers,” replied the guide, “I am a Parsee, and this woman is a

[009-173] Parsee. Command me as you will.”

[009-174] “Excellent!” said Mr. Fogg.

[009-175] “However,” resumed the guide, “it is certain, not only that we shall

[009-176] risk our lives, but horrible tortures, if we are taken.”

[009-177] “That is foreseen,” replied Mr. Fogg. “I think we must wait till night

[009-178] before acting.”

[009-179] “I think so,” said the guide.

[009-180] The worthy Indian then gave some account of the victim, who, he said,

[009-181] was a celebrated beauty of the Parsee race, and the daughter of a

[009-182] wealthy Bombay merchant. She had received a thoroughly English

[009-183] education in that city, and, from her manners and intelligence, would

[009-184] be thought an European. Her name was [[aouda|Aouda]]. Left an orphan, she was

[009-185] married against her will to the old rajah of Bundelcund; and, knowing

[009-186] the fate that awaited her, she escaped, was retaken, and devoted by the

[009-187] rajah’s relatives, who had an interest in her death, to the sacrifice

[009-188] from which it seemed she could not escape.

[009-189] The Parsee’s narrative only confirmed Mr. Fogg and his companions in

[009-190] their generous design. It was decided that the guide should direct the

[009-191] elephant towards the pagoda of Pillaji, which he accordingly approached

[009-192] as quickly as possible. They halted, half an hour afterwards, in a

[009-193] copse, some five hundred feet from the pagoda, where they were well

[009-194] concealed; but they could hear the groans and cries of the fakirs

[009-195] distinctly.

[009-196] They then discussed the means of getting at the victim. The guide was

[009-197] familiar with the pagoda of Pillaji, in which, as he declared, the

[009-198] young woman was imprisoned. Could they enter any of its doors while the

[009-199] whole party of Indians was plunged in a drunken sleep, or was it safer

[009-200] to attempt to make a hole in the walls? This could only be determined

[009-201] at the moment and the place themselves; but it was certain that the

[009-202] abduction must be made that night, and not when, at break of day, the

[009-203] victim was led to her funeral pyre. Then no human intervention could

[009-204] save her.
