[010-001] As soon as night fell, about six o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]], they decided to make a

[010-002] reconnaissance around the pagoda. The cries of the fakirs were just

[010-003] ceasing; the Indians were in the act of plunging themselves into the

[010-004] drunkenness caused by liquid opium mingled with hemp, and it might be

[010-005] possible to slip between them to the temple itself.

[010-006] The Parsee, leading the others, noiselessly crept through the wood, and

[010-007] in ten minutes they found themselves on the banks of a small stream,

[010-008] whence, by the light of the rosin torches, they perceived a pyre of

[010-009] wood, on the top of which lay the embalmed body of the rajah, which was

[010-010] to be burned with his wife. The pagoda, whose minarets loomed above the

[010-011] trees in the deepening dusk, stood a hundred steps away.

[010-012] “Come!” whispered [[the-parsee-guide|the guide]].

[010-013] He slipped more cautiously than ever through the brush, followed by his

[010-014] companions; the silence around was only broken by the low murmuring of

[010-015] the wind among the branches.

[010-016] Soon the Parsee stopped on the borders of the glade, which was lit up

[010-017] by the torches. The ground was covered by groups of the Indians,

[010-018] motionless in their drunken sleep; it seemed a battlefield strewn with

[010-019] the dead. Men, women, and children lay together.

[010-020] In the background, among the trees, the pagoda of Pillaji loomed

[010-021] distinctly. Much to the guide’s disappointment, the guards of the

[010-022] rajah, lighted by torches, were watching at the doors and marching to

[010-023] and fro with naked sabres; probably the priests, too, were watching

[010-024] within.

[010-025] The Parsee, now convinced that it was impossible to force an entrance

[010-026] to the temple, advanced no farther, but led his companions back again.

[010-027] [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] and [[sir-francis-cromarty|Sir Francis Cromarty]] also saw that nothing could be

[010-028] attempted in that direction. They stopped, and engaged in a whispered

[010-029] colloquy.

[010-030] “It is only eight now,” said the brigadier, “and these guards may also

[010-031] go to sleep.”

[010-032] “It is not impossible,” returned the Parsee.

[010-033] They lay down at the foot of a tree, and waited.

[010-034] The time seemed long; the guide ever and anon left them to take an

[010-035] observation on the edge of the wood, but the guards watched steadily by

[010-036] the glare of the torches, and a dim light crept through the windows of

[010-037] the pagoda.

[010-038] They waited till midnight; but no change took place among the guards,

[010-039] and it became apparent that their yielding to sleep could not be

[010-040] counted on. The other plan must be carried out; an opening in the walls

[010-041] of the pagoda must be made. It remained to ascertain whether the

[010-042] priests were watching by the side of their victim as assiduously as

[010-043] were the soldiers at the door.

[010-044] After a last consultation, the guide announced that he was ready for

[010-045] the attempt, and advanced, followed by the others. They took a

[010-046] roundabout way, so as to get at the pagoda on the rear. They reached

[010-047] the walls about half-past twelve, without having met anyone; here there

[010-048] was no guard, nor were there either windows or doors.

[010-049] The night was dark. The moon, on the wane, scarcely left the horizon,

[010-050] and was covered with heavy clouds; the height of the trees deepened the

[010-051] darkness.

[010-052] It was not enough to reach the walls; an opening in them must be

[010-053] accomplished, and to attain this purpose the party only had their

[010-054] pocket-knives. Happily the temple walls were built of brick and wood,

[010-055] which could be penetrated with little difficulty; after one brick had

[010-056] been taken out, the rest would yield easily.

[010-057] They set noiselessly to work, and the Parsee on one side and

[010-058] [[passepartout|Passepartout]] on the other began to loosen the bricks so as to make an

[010-059] aperture two feet wide. They were getting on rapidly, when suddenly a

[010-060] cry was heard in the interior of the temple, followed almost instantly

[010-061] by other cries replying from the outside. Passepartout and the guide

[010-062] stopped. Had they been heard? Was the alarm being given? Common

[010-063] prudence urged them to retire, and they did so, followed by Phileas

[010-064] Fogg and Sir Francis. They again hid themselves in the wood, and waited

[010-065] till the disturbance, whatever it might be, ceased, holding themselves

[010-066] ready to resume their attempt without delay. But, awkwardly enough, the

[010-067] guards now appeared at the rear of the temple, and there installed

[010-068] themselves, in readiness to prevent a surprise.

[010-069] It would be difficult to describe the disappointment of the party, thus

[010-070] interrupted in their work. They could not now reach the victim; how,

[010-071] then, could they save her? Sir Francis shook his fists, Passepartout

[010-072] was beside himself, and the guide gnashed his teeth with rage. The

[010-073] tranquil Fogg waited, without betraying any emotion.

[010-074] “We have nothing to do but to go away,” whispered Sir Francis.

[010-075] “Nothing but to go away,” echoed the guide.

[010-076] “Stop,” said Fogg. “I am only due at [[allahabad|Allahabad]] tomorrow before noon.”

[010-077] “But what can you hope to do?” asked Sir Francis. “In a few hours it

[010-078] will be daylight, and—”

[010-079] “The chance which now seems lost may present itself at the last

[010-080] moment.”

[010-081] Sir Francis would have liked to read Phileas Fogg’s eyes. What was this

[010-082] cool Englishman thinking of? Was he planning to make a rush for the

[010-083] young woman at the very moment of the sacrifice, and boldly snatch her

[010-084] from her executioners?

[010-085] This would be utter folly, and it was hard to admit that Fogg was such

[010-086] a fool. Sir Francis consented, however, to remain to the end of this

[010-087] terrible drama. The guide led them to the rear of the glade, where they

[010-088] were able to observe the sleeping groups.

[010-089] Meanwhile Passepartout, who had perched himself on the lower branches

[010-090] of a tree, was resolving an idea which had at first struck him like a

[010-091] flash, and which was now firmly lodged in his brain.

[010-092] He had commenced by saying to himself, “What folly!” and then he

[010-093] repeated, “Why not, after all? It’s a chance,—perhaps the only one; and

[010-094] with such sots!” Thinking thus, he slipped, with the suppleness of a

[010-095] serpent, to the lowest branches, the ends of which bent almost to the

[010-096] ground.

[010-097] The hours passed, and the lighter shades now announced the approach of

[010-098] day, though it was not yet light. This was the moment. The slumbering

[010-099] multitude became animated, the tambourines sounded, songs and cries

[010-100] arose; the hour of the sacrifice had come. The doors of the pagoda

[010-101] swung open, and a bright light escaped from its interior, in the midst

[010-102] of which Mr. Fogg and Sir Francis espied the victim. She seemed, having

[010-103] shaken off the stupor of intoxication, to be striving to escape from

[010-104] her executioner. Sir Francis’s heart throbbed; and, convulsively

[010-105] seizing Mr. Fogg’s hand, found in it an open knife. Just at this moment

[010-106] the crowd began to move. The young woman had again fallen into a stupor

[010-107] caused by the fumes of hemp, and passed among the fakirs, who escorted

[010-108] her with their wild, religious cries.

[010-109] Phileas Fogg and his companions, mingling in the rear ranks of the

[010-110] crowd, followed; and in two minutes they reached the banks of the

[010-111] stream, and stopped fifty paces from the pyre, upon which still lay the

[010-112] rajah’s corpse. In the semi-obscurity they saw the victim, quite

[010-113] senseless, stretched out beside her husband’s body. Then a torch was

[010-114] brought, and the wood, heavily soaked with oil, instantly took fire.

[010-115] At this moment Sir Francis and the guide seized Phileas Fogg, who, in

[010-116] an instant of mad generosity, was about to rush upon the pyre. But he

[010-117] had quickly pushed them aside, when the whole scene suddenly changed. A

[010-118] cry of terror arose. The whole multitude prostrated themselves,

[010-119] terror-stricken, on the ground.

[010-120] The old rajah was not dead, then, since he rose of a sudden, like a

[010-121] spectre, took up his wife in his arms, and descended from the pyre in

[010-122] the midst of the clouds of smoke, which only heightened his ghostly

[010-123] appearance.

[010-124] Fakirs and soldiers and priests, seized with instant terror, lay there,

[010-125] with their faces on the ground, not daring to lift their eyes and

[010-126] behold such a prodigy.

[010-127] The inanimate victim was borne along by the vigorous arms which

[010-128] supported her, and which she did not seem in the least to burden. Mr.

[010-129] Fogg and Sir Francis stood erect, the Parsee bowed his head, and

[010-130] Passepartout was, no doubt, scarcely less stupefied.

[010-131] The resuscitated rajah approached Sir Francis and Mr. Fogg, and, in an

[010-132] abrupt tone, said, “Let us be off!”

[010-133] It was Passepartout himself, who had slipped upon the pyre in the midst

[010-134] of the smoke and, profiting by the still overhanging darkness, had

[010-135] delivered the young woman from death! It was Passepartout who, playing

[010-136] his part with a happy audacity, had passed through the crowd amid the

[010-137] general terror.

[010-138] A moment after all four of the party had disappeared in the woods, and

[010-139] the elephant was bearing them away at a rapid pace. But the cries and

[010-140] noise, and a ball which whizzed through Phileas Fogg’s hat, apprised

[010-141] them that the trick had been discovered.

[010-142] The old rajah’s body, indeed, now appeared upon the burning pyre; and

[010-143] the priests, recovered from their terror, perceived that an abduction

[010-144] had taken place. They hastened into the forest, followed by the

[010-145] soldiers, who fired a volley after the fugitives; but the latter

[010-146] rapidly increased the distance between them, and ere long found

[010-147] themselves beyond the reach of the bullets and arrows.

[010-148] CHAPTER XIV.

[010-149] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG DESCENDS THE WHOLE LENGTH OF THE BEAUTIFUL VALLEY

[010-150] OF THE GANGES WITHOUT EVER THINKING OF SEEING IT

[010-151] The rash exploit had been accomplished; and for an hour Passepartout

[010-152] laughed gaily at his success. Sir Francis pressed the worthy fellow’s

[010-153] hand, and his master said, “Well done!” which, from him, was high

[010-154] commendation; to which Passepartout replied that all the credit of the

[010-155] affair belonged to Mr. Fogg. As for him, he had only been struck with a

[010-156] “queer” idea; and he laughed to think that for a few moments he,

[010-157] Passepartout, the ex-gymnast, ex-sergeant fireman, had been the spouse

[010-158] of a charming woman, a venerable, embalmed rajah! As for the young

[010-159] Indian woman, she had been unconscious throughout of what was passing,

[010-160] and now, wrapped up in a travelling-blanket, was reposing in one of the

[010-161] howdahs.

[010-162] The elephant, thanks to the skilful guidance of the Parsee, was

[010-163] advancing rapidly through the still darksome forest, and, an hour after

[010-164] leaving the pagoda, had crossed a vast plain. They made a halt at seven

[010-165] o’clock, the young woman being still in a state of complete

[010-166] prostration. The guide made her drink a little brandy and water, but

[010-167] the drowsiness which stupefied her could not yet be shaken off. Sir

[010-168] Francis, who was familiar with the effects of the intoxication produced

[010-169] by the fumes of hemp, reassured his companions on her account. But he

[010-170] was more disturbed at the prospect of her future fate. He told Phileas

[010-171] Fogg that, should [[aouda|Aouda]] remain in India, she would inevitably fall

[010-172] again into the hands of her executioners. These fanatics were scattered

[010-173] throughout the county, and would, despite the English police, recover

[010-174] their victim at Madras, [[bombay|Bombay]], or [[calcutta|Calcutta]]. She would only be safe by

[010-175] quitting India for ever.

[010-176] Phileas Fogg replied that he would reflect upon the matter.

[010-177] The station at Allahabad was reached about ten o’clock, and, the

[010-178] interrupted line of [[railway-travel|railway]] being resumed, would enable them to reach

[010-179] Calcutta in less than twenty-four hours. Phileas Fogg would thus be

[010-180] able to arrive in time to take the [[steamer-travel|steamer]] which left Calcutta the next

[010-181] day, October 25th, at noon, for [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]].

[010-182] The young woman was placed in one of the waiting-rooms of the station,

[010-183] whilst Passepartout was charged with purchasing for her various

[010-184] articles of toilet, a dress, shawl, and some furs; for which his master

[010-185] gave him unlimited credit. Passepartout started off forthwith, and

[010-186] found himself in the streets of Allahabad, that is, the City of God,

[010-187] one of the most venerated in India, being built at the junction of the

[010-188] two sacred rivers, Ganges and Jumna, the waters of which attract

[010-189] pilgrims from every part of the peninsula. The Ganges, according to the

[010-190] legends of the Ramayana, rises in heaven, whence, owing to Brahma’s

[010-191] agency, it descends to the earth.
