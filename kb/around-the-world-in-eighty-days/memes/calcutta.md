# Calcutta

## Role in the Novel

Calcutta is the capital of British India at the time of the novel (1872) and a critical juncture in Phileas Fogg's journey, where the railway from Bombay terminates and the Pacific steamer network begins. It is the site of the novel's most overtly comic sequence: the arrest and trial of Fogg and Passepartout before Judge Obadiah for the desecration of the Malabar Hill pagoda in Bombay.

Fogg arrives at Calcutta at seven in the morning on October 25th, precisely on schedule according to his journal (011-130, 011-132). The steamer Rangoon for Hong Kong departs at noon, giving him a mere five hours of buffer (011-131). Yet this narrow window is immediately threatened: as Fogg leaves Calcutta railway station, a policeman approaches, addresses him by name, and arrests both master and servant (011-145, 011-151). Passepartout's reckless visit to the Bombay temple -- which Fix had instigated and documented -- has finally caught up with them.

The court scene that follows (Section 12) is a masterful blend of legal farce and dramatic tension. Judge Obadiah, a rotund figure who begins proceedings with the wrong wig on his head (012-001--012-005), presides over a proceeding brought by the three priests of Malabar Hill. Fogg calmly admits the facts (012-031) but turns the tables by demanding the priests "admit what they were going to do at the pagoda of Pillaji" (012-033--012-034) -- the suttee from which he rescued Aouda. The judge sentences Passepartout to fifteen days' imprisonment and a fine of three hundred pounds, and Fogg to a week's imprisonment and one hundred and fifty pounds, on the legal principle that a master is responsible for his paid servant's acts (012-079--012-088).

Fix, observing from a corner of the courtroom, "rubbed his hands softly with satisfaction": if Fogg could be "detained in Calcutta a week, it would be more than time for the warrant to arrive" (012-089--012-090). But Fogg's financial resources prove decisive. He offers bail (012-097), and when the judge sets it at one thousand pounds per prisoner, Fogg "takes a roll of bank-bills from the carpet-bag" and places them on the clerk's desk without hesitation (012-102--012-104). "Meanwhile, you are liberated on bail," pronounces the judge (012-106). Fogg's single word -- "Come!" (012-107) -- and the party departs for the harbour.

## Journey Context

Calcutta represents the completion of the Indian crossing. The original timetable allocated three days for the Bombay-to-Calcutta rail journey (003-039), followed by thirteen days by steamer from Calcutta to Hong Kong (003-040). The interruption of the railway at Kholby, where the line was still under construction (008-062), forced Fogg to purchase an elephant and cross the remaining fifty miles overland. Despite this detour, he arrives in Calcutta exactly on schedule (011-133--011-134) -- the two days gained between London and Bombay having been "lost in the journey across India" (011-135--011-136).

Departure from Calcutta aboard the Rangoon marks the transition from Indian railways to Pacific steamers, and from British-ruled territory to the Chinese seas. The entire Calcutta-to-Hong-Kong leg comprises "some three thousand five hundred" miles by sea (012-142).

## Historical and Cultural Context

Calcutta in 1872 was the seat of the Governor-General of India (006-158) and the administrative hub of the British Raj. The Great Indian Peninsula Railway, whose main line terminated at Calcutta (006-191), was a symbol of British engineering ambition -- the line that "traverses the peninsula from Bombay to Calcutta in three days" (006-179). Verne's depiction of the court blends authentic colonial legal procedure with burlesque: the wig-exchange comedy of Judge Obadiah and the clerk, the three Indian priests as plaintiffs, and the exorbitant bail amount all serve to underscore the absurdity of British legal formalism applied to a distant colony.

The courtroom also reveals Verne's satirical treatment of imperial law. The judge's ruling -- "the English law protects equally and sternly the religions of the Indian people" (012-076--012-077) -- rings hollow when set against the novel's earlier depiction of the suttee ritual at Pillaji and the colonial administration's inability to prevent it. Law in Calcutta, for Fogg, is an obstacle to be bought off rather than a principle to be respected.
