# Captain Andrew Speedy

The captain of the steamer *Henrietta*, bound from New York to Bordeaux, who finds his ship commandeered, his authority appropriated, and his vessel burned for fuel by a gentleman in a hurry -- and lives to tell the tale with a profit. One of the novel's most memorable comic characters.

## Role in the Novel

Captain Speedy appears late in the novel, in Sections 28 and 29, when Fogg, having missed the Liverpool steamer at New York, approaches the *Henrietta* -- a small, fast, iron-hulled vessel known for making "between eleven and twelve knots" (028-045) -- and negotiates passage. Speedy is from Cardiff (028-043) and a master of the merchant marine; his ship is bound for Bordeaux, and he takes professional pride in holding his course.

Fogg's proposal is simple: take us to Liverpool. Speedy's refusal is equally simple: "No" (028-058). The confrontation escalates quickly. Fogg offers money -- first for passage, then for the ship itself. When Speedy refuses to divert from Bordeaux, Fogg buys the *Henrietta* outright, and "It might be thought that this was Captain Speedy," the narrator wryly observes, "Captain Speedy, he was shut up in his cabin under lock and key" (028-065). For the remainder of the Atlantic crossing, while Fogg commands the vessel, Speedy "continued to howl and growl in his cabin" (028-138).

The comic sequence deepens in Section 29 when the *Henrietta* runs out of coal. Fogg sends Passepartout to "go for Captain Speedy" (029-037), who emerges from confinement like an explosion: "The bomb was Captain Speedy" (029-041). When Fogg informs him they must burn the ship's wooden superstructure for fuel, Speedy erupts: "Burn my vessel!" (029-053). Fogg's response -- a wad of banknotes representing the full value of the ship, paid in advance -- has "a prodigious effect on Andrew Speedy" (029-057), who seizes the notes, counts them, and instantly transforms from indignant captain to cooperative passenger.

The Henrietta is reduced to a bare iron hulk, arriving at Queenstown, Ireland (029-095) -- close enough to Liverpool for Fogg to complete the journey by rail. Captain Speedy, now "deeply interested in Mr. Fogg's project" (029-119), bids farewell cordially at the quay. The man who started as an obstacle to Fogg's progress ends as a profiting bystander to it, his fury melted by the same force that drives the entire novel: money.

- Watches in horror as Fogg proposes to burn the *Henrietta* for fuel (029-053)
- Is bought off by banknotes, with "a prodigious effect on Andrew Speedy" (029-057/029-058)

- Becomes "deeply interested in Mr. Fogg's project" after the transaction (029-119)

## Character Significance

Captain Speedy is a minor character who illustrates a major theme: the triumph of money and will over convention and routine. He is a competent, rule-following professional whose ship runs on schedule -- and he is utterly helpless against a man who treats money as a tool, obstacles as fuel, and other people's property as negotiable. His name is Verne's joke: Speedy is anything but.

But Speedy is also more than a one-note joke. His transformation from indignant opponent to cooperative profiteer is complete and genuine -- once the money is in his hands, he becomes "deeply interested in Mr. Fogg's project." There is no residual resentment, no wounded professional pride. Speedy is a pure creature of the market: his ship, his authority, and his indignation are all commodities, and they all have their price. In this he mirrors the novel's deeper argument: that in the modern world, everything -- ships, schedules, national boundaries, even human loyalties -- is fungible given sufficient capital.

The *Henrietta* episode is also the novel's most vivid illustration of Fogg's improvisational genius. Throughout the journey, Fogg has been a passenger -- a man who boards other people's ships and rides other people's railways. But when the Liverpool steamer departs without him, Fogg transforms from consumer of transport to producer of it. Captain Speedy is the necessary sacrifice: his ship, his fuel, and his dignity are all consumed in Fogg's determination to reach Liverpool on time.

> "But I am in a hurry," said Fogg. "So am I," replied Speedy. (028-015)
