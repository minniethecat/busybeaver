# Elder William Hitch

A Mormon elder traveling on the transcontinental railway through Utah whose earnest sermon on the history and doctrines of Mormonism gives Verne an occasion for one of the novel's most extended digressions.

## Role in the Novel

Elder Hitch appears during the railway passage through Utah, between Salt Lake City and the Sierra Nevada. Finding a captive audience in the train car, he delivers a lengthy lecture on Mormon history — the migration to Utah under Brigham Young, the founding of Salt Lake City, and the doctrines of the faith. (PN: 025-042)

The episode is part travelogue, part satire, and part cultural commentary. Verne treats Mormonism with a mixture of fascination and condescension typical of the period, but Hitch himself is portrayed sympathetically — sincere in his beliefs, harmless in his zeal.

## Key Actions

- Delivers an extended lecture on Mormon history and doctrine to the captive train audience (025-042)
- Defends the practice of polygamy with theological argument rather than bluster (025-060)
- Departs the train at his station, his sermon complete (025-095)

## Character Significance

Elder Hitch serves two functions. He provides local color — the Mormon presence in Utah was one of the most distinctive features of the American West for European readers. And he provides an occasion for Verne to exercise his gift for documentary description, using the elder's lecture to educate French readers about a religious movement that fascinated and appalled European opinion in equal measure.

> "He was an elder in the Mormon Church, and was going to preach somewhere in the neighborhood." (025-042)
