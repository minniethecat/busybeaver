# Jules Verne's Worldview in Eighty Days

*Around the World in Eighty Days* is simultaneously a celebration of technological progress, a document of nineteenth-century imperial consciousness, and — in its quieter moments — a critique of the very values it appears to champion. This article examines the three pillars of Verne's worldview as expressed in the novel: techno-optimism, imperial ambivalence, and the complex treatment of cultural difference.

## Techno-Optimism: The World as Machine

The novel's most visible ideological commitment is to technology as the enabler of human ambition. Verne's narrator describes Fogg as "the most exact and settled gentleman in the United Kingdom" (PN: 001-158), and this exactness is mirrored in the infrastructure Fogg depends upon: railways that run to published timetables, steamers that cross oceans on schedule, and the recently-opened Suez Canal that makes the India route possible. The locomotive "guided by an English engineer" across India (PN: 007-174) is celebrated without irony as the instrument of civilization.

Yet Verne's techno-optimism is qualified by the novel's dramatic structure. Every major crisis in the journey arises from the failure of technology: the incomplete Indian railway at Kholby, the missed Carnatic at Hong Kong, the coal shortage on the Atlantic. Fogg's triumph is not the triumph of machines but of human improvisation over machines. The burning of the Henrietta's superstructure for fuel (PN: 029-011) is the novel's emblematic image: technology, when it fails, can be consumed and transcended.

This ambivalence distinguishes Verne from mere boosterism. His admiration for steam, telegraph, and railway is genuine, but his deeper interest is in how human character responds when technology reaches its limits. Fogg's mechanical precision is admirable; Fogg's capacity to abandon precision when the timetable collapses is heroic.

## Imperial Infrastructure, Imperial Consciousness

The novel's geography is entirely determined by the British Empire. Fogg travels from one colonial possession or sphere of influence to another: Egypt (under British occupation), Aden, Bombay, Calcutta, Hong Kong. The infrastructure that enables his journey — the P&O steamers, the Indian railways, the telegraph network — is imperial infrastructure. Verne treats this as a background fact, neither celebrating nor questioning it. Sir Francis Cromarty, the British Indian Army officer, is presented as simply a knowledgeable traveling companion; his presence in India as a colonial military officer is not examined. (PN: 007-028)

This normalization of empire is itself a political position. The novel presents the world as a network of British-administered junctions — Suez, Bombay, Calcutta, Hong Kong — connected by British-managed transport. Non-European peoples appear as scenery, obstacles, or victims in need of rescue. The suttee episode (PN: 012-056) is the most explicit instance: Fogg's rescue of Aouda is framed as the intervention of civilized morality against barbaric custom, reinforcing the imperial justification that Verne neither invented nor questioned.

At the same time, the novel offers glimpses of a more critical perspective. The American leg of the journey — the political rally in San Francisco, the buffalo herd that halts the train, the Sioux attack — presents a different kind of violence: the violence of democracy, of settlement, of the frontier. Verne's America is more chaotic and less orderly than his India, but it is also more alive. The implicit comparison is not flattering to British imperial administration.

## Cultural Difference: Stereotype and Subversion

Verne's treatment of cultural difference follows the conventions of his era. Passepartout's French enthusiasm is contrasted with Fogg's English phlegm; Aouda's "Oriental grace" is praised; the Japanese acrobat troupe is rendered as exotic spectacle. The novel does not transcend the racial and cultural stereotypes of 1872 — it exemplifies them.

But there are moments of subversion. Passepartout, the servant, is the novel's moral center — it is his daring that enables the rescue of Aouda, his loyalty that anchors the journey, his humanity that Fogg gradually learns to emulate. The novel's most radical move is to make a French valet the agent of an English gentleman's moral education. And Aouda, despite being introduced as a passive victim, ends the novel by proposing marriage to Fogg — an act of agency that reverses the expected Victorian romantic script. (PN: 034-075)

Fix, too, complicates the novel's apparent certainties. He is a detective of the "new school" (PN: 002-180) — modern, rational, persistent — and he is wrong about everything. His error is not ignorance but the systematic application of logic to false premises. The novel's implicit critique of detective methodology — of the confidence that evidence and reason can reliably produce truth — is one of its most modern elements.

## Conclusion: Between Celebration and Critique

*Around the World in Eighty Days* occupies an unstable position between the celebration of modernity and its critique. It presents technological infrastructure as the condition of human freedom while dramatizing that infrastructure's repeated failure. It normalizes imperial geography while allowing non-European characters moments of genuine agency. It deploys cultural stereotypes while subverting the hierarchy those stereotypes were meant to enforce.

This instability is not a flaw but the source of the novel's enduring interest. Verne was not a philosopher or a political theorist; he was a storyteller whose ideological commitments were complex, inconsistent, and productively unresolved. The novel's worldview is best understood not as a doctrine but as a field of tensions — between precision and improvisation, between empire and adventure, between the machine and the human being who must learn to transcend it.
