# The Daily Telegraph

A London newspaper that played a distinctive role in the public reception of [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]'s [[the-wager|eighty-day wager]]. Where other newspapers dismissed the project as madness, the _Daily Telegraph_ alone gave it hesitant support and published the detailed itinerary that showed the journey was, on paper at least, possible.

## Role in the Novel

The _Daily Telegraph_ enters the story during the whist-game conversation at the [[reform-club|Reform Club]]. When the gentlemen discuss the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] robbery, it is the _Telegraph_ that first identifies Fogg as "a gentleman" — a seemingly casual remark that carries weight in the Club's social calculus.

> "The _Daily Telegraph_ says that he is a gentleman." (002-149)

Later, when Fogg announces his plan to circumnavigate the globe in eighty days, the press reaction is almost uniformly hostile:

> "The _Times, Standard, Morning Post_, and _Daily News_, and twenty other highly respectable newspapers scouted Mr. Fogg's project as madness; the _Daily Telegraph_ alone hesitatingly supported him." (004-024)

## The Itinerary

The _Telegraph_'s most concrete contribution was publishing the estimated travel times that made Fogg's wager appear feasible — a table of steamer and railway connections from London to Suez, Bombay, Calcutta, Hong Kong, Yokohama, San Francisco, New York, and back to London, totaling exactly eighty days. Fogg carried this clipping in his pocket throughout the journey. (PN: 003-035)

## Public Opinion Shift

The _Telegraph_'s hesitant endorsement did more than provide Fogg with a travel schedule — it created a seed audience of believers. After other newspapers had uniformly attacked the project, the public's appetite for the story grew, and the columns devoted to Fogg's venture were eagerly devoured by all classes of readers.

The turning point came when the [[illustrated-london-news|Illustrated London News]] published Fogg's portrait, copied from a photograph in the Reform Club. Rather than continuing to dismiss the project, a segment of the _Telegraph_'s readership began to voice cautious optimism:

> "A few readers of the _Daily Telegraph_ even dared to say, 'Why not, after all? Stranger things have come to pass.'" (004-034)

This line is remarkable for what it implies. The _Telegraph_'s readers were not merely consuming news passively — they were actively debating the merits of Fogg's scheme and, in at least some cases, coming down on his side. The phrase "even dared to say" underscores the social risk of publicly endorsing Fogg: after the near-unanimous condemnation by the respectable press, voicing support required a certain boldness.

The _Telegraph_ thus functioned as a counterweight to the skepticism of [[the-times-newspaper|The Times]] and the other London dailies. By publishing the itinerary and offering hesitant support, it gave its readers permission to consider Fogg's project seriously. This early constituency of believers — however small — was the first crack in the wall of public ridicule, and it foreshadowed the eventual shift in sentiment that would greet Fogg upon his triumphant return.

## Significance

The _Telegraph_'s editorial stance serves two narrative functions. First, it establishes that Fogg's project was not entirely irrational — a respected metropolitan newspaper treated it as plausible. Second, the paper's later publication of Fogg's portrait (alongside the [[illustrated-london-news|Illustrated London News]]) transforms Fogg from an obscure clubman into a public figure, raising the stakes of the wager beyond the card room at Pall Mall.
