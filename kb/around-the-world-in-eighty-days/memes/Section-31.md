[031-001] The [[passport-and-identity|papers]] resumed their discussion about the [[the-wager|wager]]; all those who had

[031-002] laid bets, for or against him, revived their interest, as if by magic;

[031-003] the “[[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] bonds” again became negotiable, and many new wagers

[031-004] were made. Phileas Fogg’s name was once more at a premium on ’Change.

[031-005] His five friends of the [[london|Reform Club]] passed these three days in a state

[031-006] of feverish suspense. Would Phileas Fogg, whom they had forgotten,

[031-007] reappear before their eyes! Where was he at this moment? The 17th of

[031-008] December, the day of James Strand’s arrest, was the seventy-sixth since

[031-009] Phileas Fogg’s departure, and no news of him had been received. Was he

[031-010] dead? Had he abandoned the effort, or was he continuing his journey

[031-011] along the [[around-the-world-route|route]] agreed upon? And would he appear on Saturday, the 21st

[031-012] of December, at a quarter before nine in the evening, on the threshold

[031-013] of the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] saloon?

[031-014] The anxiety in which, for three days, London society existed, cannot be

[031-015] described. Telegrams were sent to America and Asia for news of Phileas

[031-016] Fogg. Messengers were dispatched to the house in Saville Row morning

[031-017] and evening. No news. The police were ignorant what had become of the

[031-018] detective, [[detective-fix|Fix]], who had so unfortunately followed up a false scent.

[031-019] Bets increased, nevertheless, in number and value. Phileas Fogg, like a

[031-020] racehorse, was drawing near his last turning-point. The bonds were

[031-021] quoted, no longer at a hundred below par, but at twenty, at ten, and at

[031-022] five; and paralytic old Lord Albemarle bet even in his favour.

[031-023] A great crowd was collected in Pall Mall and the neighbouring streets

[031-024] on Saturday evening; it seemed like a multitude of brokers permanently

[031-025] established around the Reform Club. Circulation was impeded, and

[031-026] everywhere disputes, discussions, and financial transactions were going

[031-027] on. The police had great difficulty in keeping back the crowd, and as

[031-028] the hour when Phileas Fogg was due approached, the excitement rose to

[031-029] its highest pitch.

[031-030] The five antagonists of Phileas Fogg had met in the great saloon of the

[031-031] club. John Sullivan and Samuel Fallentin, the bankers, [[andrew-stuart|Andrew Stuart]],

[031-032] the engineer, Gauthier Ralph, the director of the Bank of England, and

[031-033] Thomas Flanagan, the brewer, one and all waited anxiously.

[031-034] When the [[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] indicated twenty minutes past eight, Andrew Stuart got

[031-035] up, saying, “Gentlemen, in twenty minutes the time agreed upon between

[031-036] Mr. Fogg and ourselves will have expired.”

[031-037] “What time did the last [[railway-travel|train]] arrive from [[liverpool|Liverpool]]?” asked Thomas

[031-038] Flanagan.

[031-039] “At twenty-three minutes past seven,” replied Gauthier Ralph; “and the

[031-040] next does not arrive till ten minutes after twelve.”

[031-041] “Well, gentlemen,” resumed Andrew Stuart, “if Phileas Fogg had come in

[031-042] the 7:23 train, he would have got here by this time. We can, therefore,

[031-043] regard the bet as won.”

[031-044] “Wait; don’t let us be too hasty,” replied Samuel Fallentin. “You know

[031-045] that Mr. Fogg is very eccentric. His punctuality is well known; he

[031-046] never arrives too soon, or too late; and I should not be surprised if

[031-047] he appeared before us at the last minute.”

[031-048] “Why,” said Andrew Stuart nervously, “if I should see him, I should not

[031-049] believe it was he.”

[031-050] “The fact is,” resumed Thomas Flanagan, “Mr. Fogg’s project was

[031-051] absurdly foolish. Whatever his punctuality, he could not prevent the

[031-052] delays which were certain to occur; and a delay of only two or three

[031-053] days would be fatal to his tour.”

[031-054] “Observe, too,” added John Sullivan, “that we have received no

[031-055] intelligence from him, though there are telegraphic lines all along his

[031-056] route.”

[031-057] “He has lost, [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]],” said Andrew Stuart, “he has a hundred times

[031-058] lost! You know, besides, that the ‘China’—the only [[steamer-travel|steamer]] he could

[031-059] have taken from [[new-york|New York]] to get here in time arrived yesterday. I have

[031-060] seen a list of the passengers, and the name of Phileas Fogg is not

[031-061] among them. Even if we admit that fortune has favoured him, he can

[031-062] scarcely have reached America. I think he will be at least twenty days

[031-063] behind-hand, and that Lord Albemarle will lose a cool five thousand.”

[031-064] “It is clear,” replied Gauthier Ralph; “and we have nothing to do but

[031-065] to present Mr. Fogg’s cheque at Barings to-morrow.”

[031-066] At this moment, the hands of the club clock pointed to twenty minutes

[031-067] to nine.

[031-068] “Five minutes more,” said Andrew Stuart.

[031-069] The five gentlemen looked at each other. Their anxiety was becoming

[031-070] intense; but, not wishing to betray it, they readily assented to Mr.

[031-071] Fallentin’s proposal of a rubber.

[031-072] “I wouldn’t give up my four thousand of the bet,” said Andrew Stuart,

[031-073] as he took his seat, “for three thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine.”

[031-074] The clock indicated eighteen minutes to nine.

[031-075] The players took up their cards, but could not keep their eyes off the

[031-076] clock. Certainly, however secure they felt, minutes had never seemed so

[031-077] long to them!

[031-078] “Seventeen minutes to nine,” said Thomas Flanagan, as he cut the cards

[031-079] which Ralph handed to him.

[031-080] Then there was a moment of silence. The great saloon was perfectly

[031-081] quiet; but the murmurs of the crowd outside were heard, with now and

[031-082] then a shrill cry. The pendulum beat the seconds, which each player

[031-083] eagerly counted, as he listened, with mathematical regularity.

[031-084] “Sixteen minutes to nine!” said John Sullivan, in a voice which

[031-085] betrayed his emotion.

[031-086] One minute more, and the wager would be won. Andrew Stuart and his

[031-087] partners suspended their game. They left their cards, and counted the

[031-088] seconds.

[031-089] At the fortieth second, nothing. At the fiftieth, still nothing.

[031-090] At the fifty-fifth, a loud cry was heard in the street, followed by

[031-091] applause, hurrahs, and some fierce growls.

[031-092] The players rose from their seats.

[031-093] At the fifty-seventh second the door of the saloon opened; and the

[031-094] pendulum had not beat the sixtieth second when Phileas Fogg appeared,

[031-095] followed by an excited crowd who had forced their way through the club

[031-096] doors, and in his calm voice, said, “Here I am, gentlemen!”

[031-097] CHAPTER XXXVII.

[031-098] IN WHICH IT IS SHOWN THAT PHILEAS FOGG GAINED NOTHING BY HIS TOUR

[031-099] AROUND THE WORLD, UNLESS IT WERE HAPPINESS

[031-100] Yes; Phileas Fogg in person.

[031-101] The reader will remember that at five minutes past eight in the

[031-102] evening—about five and twenty hours after the arrival of the travellers

[031-103] in London—[[passepartout|Passepartout]] had been sent by his master to engage the

[031-104] services of the [[reverend-wilson|Reverend Samuel Wilson]] in a certain marriage ceremony,

[031-105] which was to take place the next day.

[031-106] Passepartout went on his errand enchanted. He soon reached the

[031-107] clergyman’s house, but found him not at home. Passepartout waited a

[031-108] good twenty minutes, and when he left the reverend gentleman, it was

[031-109] thirty-five minutes past eight. But in what a state he was! With his

[031-110] hair in disorder, and without his hat, he ran along the street as never

[031-111] man was seen to run before, overturning passers-by, rushing over the

[031-112] sidewalk like a waterspout.

[031-113] In three minutes he was in Saville Row again, and staggered back into

[031-114] Mr. Fogg’s room.

[031-115] He could not speak.

[031-116] “What is the matter?” asked Mr. Fogg.

[031-117] “My master!” gasped Passepartout—“marriage—impossible—”

[031-118] “Impossible?”

[031-119] “Impossible—for to-morrow.”

[031-120] “Why so?”

[031-121] “Because to-morrow—is Sunday!”

[031-122] “Monday,” replied Mr. Fogg.

[031-123] “No—to-day is Saturday.”

[031-124] “Saturday? Impossible!”

[031-125] “Yes, yes, yes, yes!” cried Passepartout. “You have made a mistake of

[031-126] one day! We arrived twenty-four hours ahead of time; but there are only

[031-127] ten minutes left!”

[031-128] Passepartout had seized his master by the collar, and was dragging him

[031-129] along with irresistible force.

[031-130] Phileas Fogg, thus kidnapped, without having time to think, left his

[031-131] house, jumped into a cab, promised a hundred pounds to the cabman, and,

[031-132] having run over two dogs and overturned five carriages, reached the

[031-133] Reform Club.

[031-134] The clock indicated a quarter before nine when he appeared in the great

[031-135] saloon.

[031-136] Phileas Fogg had accomplished the journey round the world in eighty

[031-137] days!

[031-138] Phileas Fogg had won his wager of twenty thousand pounds!

[031-139] How was it that a man so exact and fastidious could have made this

[031-140] error of a day? How came he to think that he had arrived in London on

[031-141] Saturday, the twenty-first day of December, when it was really Friday,

[031-142] the twentieth, the seventy-ninth day only from his departure?

[031-143] The cause of the error is very simple.

[031-144] Phileas Fogg had, without suspecting it, gained one day on his journey,

[031-145] and this merely because he had travelled constantly _eastward;_ he

[031-146] would, on the contrary, have lost a day had he gone in the opposite

[031-147] direction, that is, _westward_.

[031-148] In journeying eastward he had gone towards the sun, and the days

[031-149] therefore diminished for him as many times four minutes as he crossed

[031-150] degrees in this direction. There are three hundred and sixty degrees on

[031-151] the circumference of the earth; and these three hundred and sixty

[031-152] degrees, multiplied by four minutes, gives precisely twenty-four

[031-153] hours—that is, the day unconsciously gained. In other words, while

[031-154] Phileas Fogg, going eastward, saw the sun pass the meridian _eighty_

[031-155] times, his friends in London only saw it pass the meridian

[031-156] _seventy-nine_ times. This is why they awaited him at the Reform Club

[031-157] on Saturday, and not Sunday, as Mr. Fogg thought.

[031-158] And Passepartout’s famous family watch, which had always kept London

[031-159] time, would have betrayed this fact, if it had marked the days as well

[031-160] as the hours and the minutes!

[031-161] Phileas Fogg, then, had won the twenty thousand pounds; but, as he had

[031-162] spent nearly nineteen thousand on the way, the pecuniary gain was

[031-163] small. His object was, however, to be victorious, and not to win money.

[031-164] He divided the one thousand pounds that remained between Passepartout

[031-165] and the unfortunate [[fix-detective|Fix]], against whom he cherished no grudge. He

[031-166] deducted, however, from Passepartout’s share the cost of the gas which

[031-167] had burned in his room for nineteen hundred and twenty hours, for the

[031-168] sake of regularity.

[031-169] That evening, Mr. Fogg, as tranquil and phlegmatic as ever, said to

[031-170] [[aouda|Aouda]]: “Is our marriage still agreeable to you?”

[031-171] “Mr. Fogg,” replied she, “it is for me to ask that question. You were

[031-172] ruined, but now you are rich again.”

[031-173] “Pardon me, madam; my fortune belongs to you. If you had not suggested

[031-174] our marriage, my servant would not have gone to the Reverend Samuel

[031-175] Wilson’s, I should not have been apprised of my error, and—”

[031-176] “Dear Mr. Fogg!” said the young woman.

[031-177] “Dear Aouda!” replied Phileas Fogg.

[031-178] It need not be said that the marriage took place forty-eight hours

[031-179] after, and that Passepartout, glowing and dazzling, gave the bride

[031-180] away. Had he not saved her, and was he not entitled to this honour?

[031-181] The next day, as soon as it was light, Passepartout rapped vigorously

[031-182] at his master’s door. Mr. Fogg opened it, and asked, “What’s the

[031-183] matter, Passepartout?”

[031-184] “What is it, sir? Why, I’ve just this instant found out—”

[031-185] “What?”

[031-186] “That we might have made the tour of the world in only seventy-eight

[031-187] days.”

[031-188] “No doubt,” returned Mr. Fogg, “by not crossing India. But if I had not

[031-189] crossed India, I should not have saved Aouda; she would not have been

[031-190] my wife, and—”

[031-191] Mr. Fogg quietly shut the door.

[031-192] Phileas Fogg had won his wager, and had made his journey around the

[031-193] world in eighty days. To do this he had employed every means of

[031-194] conveyance—steamers, railways, carriages, yachts, trading-vessels,

[031-195] sledges, elephants. The eccentric gentleman had throughout displayed

[031-196] all his marvellous qualities of coolness and exactitude. But what then?

[031-197] What had he really gained by all this trouble? What had he brought back

[031-198] from this long and weary journey?

[031-199] Nothing, say you? Perhaps so; nothing but a charming woman, who,

[031-200] strange as it may appear, made him the happiest of men!

[031-201] Truly, would you not for less than that make the tour around the world?
