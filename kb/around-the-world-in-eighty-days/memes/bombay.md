# Bombay

Bombay (now Mumbai) in 1872 is the western gateway to British India — a thriving colonial port where the Arabia, China, and Mongolia steamers of the [[p-and-o-company|P&O line]] discharge their passengers, mail, and cargo from [[suez]]. For [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]], it is the threshold of the Orient and the first critical transfer point of the [[around-the-world-route|circumnavigation]]. The city's [[railway-travel|railway connection]] to [[calcutta]] represents the imperial infrastructure that makes the eighty-day [[the-wager|wager]] plausible. (005-015)

## Role in the Novel

Fogg arrives at Bombay aboard the Mongolia and immediately boards the Great Indian Peninsula Railway for [[calcutta]], wasting no time on sightseeing. But Bombay is where [[detective-fix|Fix]] first realizes his quarry has escaped him — the arrest warrant from London has not arrived — and where [[passepartout|Passepartout's]] misadventure at the Malabar Hill pagoda sets up Fix's later machinations. (006-011)

The city represents both the efficiency of empire (the railway connection is immediate) and its friction (Passepartout's violation of temple rules creates a legal liability that Fix exploits). Bombay is thus simultaneously a triumph of imperial connectivity and a site of cultural collision. (006-045)

> "The Mongolia had on this very day crossed the very meridian which was to mark the beginning of Fogg's journey." (005-015)

## Journey Context

Fogg transits Bombay without truly experiencing it — the city is a junction, not a destination. This pattern repeats throughout the journey: Fogg sees the world's great cities only through railway station windows and harbor approaches. The novel is a circumnavigation without tourism. The [[around-the-world-route|route]] treats Bombay as a mathematical waypoint, not a human place — a perspective that both enables and limits Fogg's relationship with the world. (006-011)

The railway from Bombay to [[calcutta]] was a recent achievement (completed 1870), and its existence was central to the plausibility of the eighty-day [[the-wager|wager]]. Without the Great Indian Peninsula Railway, Fogg would have needed weeks to cross the subcontinent by road. (006-011)
