# Mudge

Mudge is an American frontiersman hired by Phileas Fogg at Fort Kearney to guide a sail-powered sledge across the snow-covered Nebraska plains. His expertise and confidence prove crucial to the party's race to reach Omaha before the connecting train to Chicago departs.

## Role in the Novel

Mudge appears in Section 27 during the crossing of America, when the transcontinental railway is blocked by snow between Fort Kearney and Omaha. Fogg, unwilling to accept a delay, hires Mudge to pilot an improvised sail-sledge across the frozen landscape. Mudge's role is that of the capable frontier guide — confident, resourceful, and motivated by the substantial reward Fogg offers. ([027-036])

## Biography / Background

Mudge is stationed at a hut near Fort Kearney, where he has developed expertise in crossing the Nebraska plains by sledge. The narrator notes that his hands have "hardened" from the work and that he knows the terrain intimately, able to read landmarks invisible to outsiders. His confidence is grounded in practical experience: he has made the crossing many times and understands both the physical challenges and the time required. ([027-036], [027-051])

## Key Actions

- **Accepting the commission**: Mudge agrees to pilot the sledge from Fort Kearney to Omaha, motivated by Fogg's promise of a generous reward. ([027-036])
- **Sailing across the Nebraska plains**: For the duration of the crossing, Mudge stands at the rudder, expertly navigating the sail-sledge while the passengers huddle against the cold. ([027-083])
- **Reassuring the passengers**: When questioned about the viability of reaching Omaha in time, Mudge replies with frontier confidence: "If nothing breaks, we shall get there!" ([027-090])
- **Reading the landscape**: Around noon, Mudge identifies landmarks that confirm their position and progress — a skill born of deep familiarity with the terrain. ([027-140])
- **Arriving at Omaha**: Mudge successfully navigates the sledge to Omaha, pointing out the city's rooftops to his passengers as they approach. He is generously rewarded by Fogg and warmly thanked by Passepartout. ([027-146], [027-152])

## Character Significance

Mudge represents the American frontier type that Verne admired: the practical, self-reliant individual who masters nature through ingenuity rather than brute force. The sail-sledge itself — a fusion of sailing technology with overland travel — is a quintessentially American innovation in Verne's imagination, and Mudge is its ideal operator. His confidence ("If nothing breaks, we shall get there!") contrasts with the anxious calculations of the Reform Club gentlemen: where they deal in probabilities, Mudge deals in certainties grounded in direct experience.

> Mudge was very confident of being able to cross the snow-covered plains, and his hands had hardened from the work. ([027-051])
