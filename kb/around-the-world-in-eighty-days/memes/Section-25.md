[025-001] It was here that the Union [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]] Railroad was inaugurated on the 23rd

[025-002] of October, 1867, by the chief engineer, General Dodge. Two powerful

[025-003] locomotives, carrying nine cars of invited guests, amongst whom was

[025-004] Thomas C. Durant, vice-president of the road, stopped at this point;

[025-005] cheers were given, the Sioux and Pawnees performed an imitation Indian

[025-006] battle, fireworks were let off, and the first number of the _Railway

[025-007] Pioneer_ was printed by a press brought on the [[railway-travel|train]]. Thus was

[025-008] celebrated the inauguration of this great railroad, a mighty instrument

[025-009] of progress and civilisation, thrown across the desert, and destined to

[025-010] link together cities and towns which do not yet exist. The whistle of

[025-011] the locomotive, more powerful than Amphion’s lyre, was about to bid

[025-012] them rise from American soil.

[025-013] Fort McPherson was left behind at eight in the morning, and three

[025-014] hundred and fifty-seven miles had yet to be traversed before reaching

[025-015] Omaha. The road followed the capricious windings of the southern branch

[025-016] of the Platte River, on its left bank. At nine the train stopped at the

[025-017] important town of North Platte, built between the two arms of the

[025-018] river, which rejoin each other around it and form a single artery, a

[025-019] large tributary, whose waters empty into the Missouri a little above

[025-020] Omaha.

[025-021] The one hundred and first meridian was passed.

[025-022] [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]] and his partners had resumed their game; no one—not even the

[025-023] dummy—complained of the length of the trip. [[detective-fix|Fix]] had begun by winning

[025-024] several guineas, which he seemed likely to lose; but he showed himself

[025-025] a not less eager whist-player than Mr. Fogg. During the morning, chance

[025-026] distinctly favoured that [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]. Trumps and honours were showered

[025-027] upon his hands.

[025-028] Once, having resolved on a bold stroke, he was on the point of playing

[025-029] a spade, when a voice behind him said, “I should play a diamond.”

[025-030] Mr. Fogg, [[aouda|Aouda]], and [[fix-detective|Fix]] raised their heads, and beheld Colonel

[025-031] [[colonel-proctor|Proctor]].

[025-032] Stamp Proctor and Phileas Fogg recognised each other at once.

[025-033] “Ah! it’s you, is it, Englishman?” cried the colonel; “it’s you who are

[025-034] going to play a spade!”

[025-035] “And who plays it,” replied Phileas Fogg coolly, throwing down the ten

[025-036] of spades.

[025-037] “Well, it pleases me to have it diamonds,” replied Colonel Proctor, in

[025-038] an insolent tone.

[025-039] He made a movement as if to seize the card which had just been played,

[025-040] adding, “You don’t understand anything about whist.”

[025-041] “Perhaps I do, as well as another,” said Phileas Fogg, rising.

[025-042] “You have only to try, son of John Bull,” replied the colonel.

[025-043] Aouda turned pale, and her blood ran cold. She seized Mr. Fogg’s arm

[025-044] and gently pulled him back. [[passepartout|Passepartout]] was ready to pounce upon the

[025-045] American, who was staring insolently at his opponent. But Fix got up,

[025-046] and, going to Colonel Proctor said, “You forget that it is I with whom

[025-047] you have to deal, sir; for it was I whom you not only insulted, but

[025-048] struck!”

[025-049] “Mr. Fix,” said Mr. Fogg, “pardon me, but this affair is mine, and mine

[025-050] only. The colonel has again insulted me, by insisting that I should not

[025-051] play a spade, and he shall give me satisfaction for it.”

[025-052] “When and where you will,” replied the American, “and with whatever

[025-053] weapon you choose.”

[025-054] Aouda in vain attempted to retain Mr. Fogg; as vainly did the detective

[025-055] endeavour to make the quarrel his. Passepartout wished to throw the

[025-056] colonel out of the window, but a sign from his master checked him.

[025-057] Phileas Fogg left the car, and the American followed him upon the

[025-058] platform. “Sir,” said Mr. Fogg to his adversary, “I am in a great hurry

[025-059] to get back to Europe, and any delay whatever will be greatly to my

[025-060] disadvantage.”

[025-061] “Well, what’s that to me?” replied Colonel Proctor.

[025-062] “Sir,” said Mr. Fogg, very politely, “after our meeting at San

[025-063] Francisco, I determined to return to America and find you as soon as I

[025-064] had completed the business which called me to England.”

[025-065] “Really!”

[025-066] “Will you appoint a meeting for six months hence?”

[025-067] “Why not ten years hence?”

[025-068] “I say six months,” returned Phileas Fogg; “and I shall be at the place

[025-069] of meeting promptly.”

[025-070] “All this is an evasion,” cried Stamp Proctor. “Now or never!”

[025-071] “Very good. You are going to [[new-york|New York]]?”

[025-072] “No.”

[025-073] “To Chicago?”

[025-074] “No.”

[025-075] “To Omaha?”

[025-076] “What difference is it to you? Do you know Plum Creek?”

[025-077] “No,” replied Mr. Fogg.

[025-078] “It’s the next station. The train will be there in an hour, and will

[025-079] stop there ten minutes. In ten minutes several revolver-shots could be

[025-080] exchanged.”

[025-081] “Very well,” said Mr. Fogg. “I will stop at Plum Creek.”

[025-082] “And I guess you’ll stay there too,” added the American insolently.

[025-083] “Who knows?” replied Mr. Fogg, returning to the car as coolly as usual.

[025-084] He began to reassure Aouda, telling her that blusterers were never to

[025-085] be feared, and begged Fix to be his second at the approaching duel, a

[025-086] request which the detective could not refuse. Mr. Fogg resumed the

[025-087] interrupted game with perfect calmness.

[025-088] At eleven o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] the locomotive’s whistle announced that they were

[025-089] approaching Plum Creek station. Mr. Fogg rose, and, followed by Fix,

[025-090] went out upon the platform. Passepartout accompanied him, carrying a

[025-091] pair of revolvers. Aouda remained in the car, as pale as death.

[025-092] The door of the next car opened, and Colonel Proctor appeared on the

[025-093] platform, attended by a Yankee of his own stamp as his second. But just

[025-094] as the combatants were about to step from the train, the conductor

[025-095] hurried up, and shouted, “You can’t get off, gentlemen!”

[025-096] “Why not?” asked the colonel.

[025-097] “We are twenty minutes late, and we shall not stop.”

[025-098] “But I am going to fight a duel with this gentleman.”

[025-099] “I am sorry,” said the conductor; “but we shall be off at once. There’s

[025-100] the bell ringing now.”

[025-101] The train started.

[025-102] “I’m really very sorry, gentlemen,” said the conductor. “Under any

[025-103] other circumstances I should have been happy to oblige you. But, after

[025-104] all, as you have not had time to fight here, why not fight as we go

[025-105] along?”

[025-106] “That wouldn’t be convenient, perhaps, for this gentleman,” said the

[025-107] colonel, in a jeering tone.

[025-108] “It would be perfectly so,” replied Phileas Fogg.

[025-109] “Well, we are really in America,” thought Passepartout, “and the

[025-110] conductor is a gentleman of the first order!”

[025-111] So muttering, he followed his master.

[025-112] The two combatants, their seconds, and the conductor passed through the

[025-113] cars to the rear of the train. The last car was only occupied by a

[025-114] dozen passengers, whom the conductor politely asked if they would not

[025-115] be so kind as to leave it vacant for a few moments, as two gentlemen

[025-116] had an affair of honour to settle. The passengers granted the request

[025-117] with alacrity, and straightway disappeared on the platform.

[025-118] The car, which was some fifty feet long, was very convenient for their

[025-119] purpose. The adversaries might march on each other in the aisle, and

[025-120] fire at their ease. Never was duel more easily arranged. Mr. Fogg and

[025-121] Colonel Proctor, each provided with two six-barrelled revolvers,

[025-122] entered the car. The seconds, remaining outside, shut them in. They

[025-123] were to begin firing at the first whistle of the locomotive. After an

[025-124] interval of two minutes, what remained of the two gentlemen would be

[025-125] taken from the car.

[025-126] Nothing could be more simple. Indeed, it was all so simple that Fix and

[025-127] Passepartout felt their hearts beating as if they would crack. They

[025-128] were listening for the whistle agreed upon, when suddenly savage cries

[025-129] resounded in the air, accompanied by reports which certainly did not

[025-130] issue from the car where the duellists were. The reports continued in

[025-131] front and the whole length of the train. Cries of terror proceeded from

[025-132] the interior of the cars.

[025-133] Colonel Proctor and Mr. Fogg, revolvers in hand, hastily quitted their

[025-134] prison, and rushed forward where the noise was most clamorous. They

[025-135] then perceived that the train was attacked by a band of Sioux.

[025-136] This was not the first attempt of these daring Indians, for more than

[025-137] once they had waylaid trains on the road. A hundred of them had,

[025-138] according to their habit, jumped upon the steps without stopping the

[025-139] train, with the ease of a clown mounting a horse at full gallop.

[025-140] The Sioux were armed with guns, from which came the reports, to which

[025-141] the passengers, who were almost all armed, responded by revolver-shots.

[025-142] The Indians had first mounted the engine, and half stunned [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]]

[025-143] and stoker with blows from their muskets. A Sioux chief, wishing to

[025-144] stop the train, but not knowing how to work the regulator, had opened

[025-145] wide instead of closing the steam-valve, and the locomotive was

[025-146] plunging forward with terrific velocity.

[025-147] The Sioux had at the same time invaded the cars, skipping like enraged

[025-148] monkeys over the roofs, thrusting open the doors, and fighting hand to

[025-149] hand with the passengers. Penetrating the baggage-car, they pillaged

[025-150] it, throwing the trunks out of the train. The cries and shots were

[025-151] constant. The travellers defended themselves bravely; some of the cars

[025-152] were barricaded, and sustained a siege, like moving forts, carried

[025-153] along at a speed of a hundred miles an hour.

[025-154] Aouda behaved courageously from the first. She defended herself like a

[025-155] true heroine with a revolver, which she shot through the broken windows

[025-156] whenever a savage made his appearance. Twenty Sioux had fallen mortally

[025-157] wounded to the ground, and the wheels crushed those who fell upon the

[025-158] rails as if they had been worms. Several passengers, shot or stunned,

[025-159] lay on the seats.

[025-160] It was necessary to put an end to the struggle, which had lasted for

[025-161] ten minutes, and which would result in the triumph of the Sioux if the

[025-162] train was not stopped. Fort Kearney station, where there was a

[025-163] garrison, was only two miles distant; but, that once passed, the Sioux

[025-164] would be masters of the train between Fort Kearney and the station

[025-165] beyond.

[025-166] The conductor was fighting beside Mr. Fogg, when he was shot and fell.

[025-167] At the same moment he cried, “Unless the train is stopped in five

[025-168] minutes, we are lost!”

[025-169] “It shall be stopped,” said Phileas Fogg, preparing to rush from the

[025-170] car.

[025-171] “Stay, monsieur,” cried Passepartout; “I will go.”

[025-172] Mr. Fogg had not time to stop the brave fellow, who, opening a door

[025-173] unperceived by the Indians, succeeded in slipping under the car; and

[025-174] while the struggle continued and the balls whizzed across each other

[025-175] over his head, he made use of his old acrobatic experience, and with

[025-176] amazing agility worked his way under the cars, holding on to the

[025-177] chains, aiding himself by the brakes and edges of the sashes, creeping

[025-178] from one car to another with marvellous skill, and thus gaining the

[025-179] forward end of the train.

[025-180] There, suspended by one hand between the baggage-car and the tender,

[025-181] with the other he loosened the safety chains; but, owing to the

[025-182] traction, he would never have succeeded in unscrewing the yoking-bar,

[025-183] had not a violent concussion jolted this bar out. The train, now

[025-184] detached from the engine, remained a little behind, whilst the

[025-185] locomotive rushed forward with increased speed.

[025-186] Carried on by the force already acquired, the train still moved for

[025-187] several minutes; but the brakes were worked and at last they stopped,

[025-188] less than a hundred feet from Kearney station.

[025-189] The soldiers of the fort, attracted by the shots, hurried up; the Sioux

[025-190] had not expected them, and decamped in a body before the train entirely

[025-191] stopped.

[025-192] But when the passengers counted each other on the station platform

[025-193] several were found missing; among others the courageous Frenchman,

[025-194] whose devotion had just saved them.

[025-195] CHAPTER XXX.

[025-196] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG SIMPLY DOES HIS DUTY

[025-197] Three passengers including Passepartout had disappeared. Had they been

[025-198] killed in the struggle? Were they taken prisoners by the Sioux? It was

[025-199] impossible to tell.

[025-200] There were many wounded, but none mortally. Colonel Proctor was one of

[025-201] the most seriously hurt; he had fought bravely, and a ball had entered

[025-202] his groin. He was carried into the station with the other wounded

[025-203] passengers, to receive such attention as could be of avail.
