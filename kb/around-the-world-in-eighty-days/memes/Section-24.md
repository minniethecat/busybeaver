[024-001] “[[detective-fix|Mr. Fix]],” resumed [[aouda|Aouda]], “[[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]] will allow no one to avenge him. He

[024-002] said that he would come back to America to find this man. Should he

[024-003] perceive [[colonel-proctor|Colonel Proctor]], we could not prevent a collision which might

[024-004] have terrible results. He must not see him.”

[024-005] “You are right, madam,” replied [[fix-detective|Fix]]; “a meeting between them might ruin

[024-006] all. Whether he were victorious or beaten, Mr. Fogg would be delayed,

[024-007] and—”

[024-008] “And,” added [[passepartout|Passepartout]], “that would play the game of the gentlemen

[024-009] of the [[london|Reform Club]]. In four days we shall be in [[new-york|New York]]. Well, if my

[024-010] master does not leave this car during those four days, we may hope that

[024-011] chance will not bring him face to face with this confounded American.

[024-012] We must, if possible, prevent his stirring out of it.”

[024-013] The conversation dropped. Mr. Fogg had just woke up, and was looking

[024-014] out of the window. Soon after Passepartout, without being heard by his

[024-015] master or Aouda, whispered to the detective, “Would you really fight

[024-016] for him?”

[024-017] “I would do anything,” replied Fix, in a tone which betrayed determined

[024-018] will, “to get him back living to Europe!”

[024-019] Passepartout felt something like a shudder shoot through his frame, but

[024-020] his confidence in his master remained unbroken.

[024-021] Was there any means of detaining Mr. Fogg in the car, to avoid a

[024-022] meeting between him and the colonel? It ought not to be a difficult

[024-023] task, since that [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] was naturally sedentary and little curious.

[024-024] The detective, at least, seemed to have found a way; for, after a few

[024-025] moments, he said to Mr. Fogg, “These are long and slow hours, sir, that

[024-026] we are passing on the [[railway-travel|railway]].”

[024-027] “Yes,” replied Mr. Fogg; “but they pass.”

[024-028] “You were in the habit of playing whist,” resumed Fix, “on the

[024-029] steamers.”

[024-030] “Yes; but it would be difficult to do so here. I have neither cards nor

[024-031] partners.”

[024-032] “Oh, but we can easily buy some cards, for they are sold on all the

[024-033] American trains. And as for partners, if madam plays—”

[024-034] “Certainly, sir,” Aouda quickly replied; “I understand whist. It is

[024-035] part of an English education.”

[024-036] “I myself have some pretensions to playing a good game. Well, here are

[024-037] three of us, and a dummy—”

[024-038] “As you please, sir,” replied Phileas Fogg, heartily glad to resume his

[024-039] favourite pastime even on the railway.

[024-040] Passepartout was dispatched in search of the steward, and soon returned

[024-041] with two packs of cards, some pins, counters, and a shelf covered with

[024-042] cloth.

[024-043] The game commenced. Aouda understood whist sufficiently well, and even

[024-044] received some compliments on her playing from Mr. Fogg. As for the

[024-045] detective, he was simply an adept, and worthy of being matched against

[024-046] his present opponent.

[024-047] “Now,” thought Passepartout, “we’ve got him. He won’t budge.”

[024-048] At eleven in the morning the train had reached the dividing ridge of

[024-049] the waters at Bridger Pass, seven thousand five hundred and twenty-four

[024-050] feet above the level of the sea, one of the highest points attained by

[024-051] the track in crossing the Rocky Mountains. After going about two

[024-052] hundred miles, the travellers at last found themselves on one of those

[024-053] vast plains which extend to the Atlantic, and which nature has made so

[024-054] propitious for laying the iron road.

[024-055] On the declivity of the Atlantic basin the first streams, branches of

[024-056] the North Platte River, already appeared. The whole northern and

[024-057] eastern horizon was bounded by the immense semi-circular curtain which

[024-058] is formed by the southern portion of the Rocky Mountains, the highest

[024-059] being Laramie Peak. Between this and the railway extended vast plains,

[024-060] plentifully irrigated. On the right rose the lower spurs of the

[024-061] mountainous mass which extends southward to the sources of the Arkansas

[024-062] River, one of the great tributaries of the Missouri.

[024-063] At half-past twelve the travellers caught sight for an instant of Fort

[024-064] Halleck, which commands that section; and in a few more hours the Rocky

[024-065] Mountains were crossed. There was reason to hope, then, that no

[024-066] accident would mark the journey through this difficult country. The

[024-067] snow had ceased falling, and the air became crisp and cold. Large

[024-068] birds, frightened by the locomotive, rose and flew off in the distance.

[024-069] No wild beast appeared on the plain. It was a desert in its vast

[024-070] nakedness.

[024-071] After a comfortable breakfast, served in the car, Mr. Fogg and his

[024-072] partners had just resumed whist, when a violent whistling was heard,

[024-073] and the train stopped. Passepartout put his head out of the door, but

[024-074] saw nothing to cause the delay; no station was in view.

[024-075] Aouda and Fix feared that Mr. Fogg might take it into his head to get

[024-076] out; but that gentleman contented himself with saying to his servant,

[024-077] “See what is the matter.”

[024-078] Passepartout rushed out of the car. Thirty or forty passengers had

[024-079] already descended, amongst them Colonel Stamp Proctor.

[024-080] The train had stopped before a red signal which blocked the way. The

[024-081] engineer and conductor were talking excitedly with a signal-man, whom

[024-082] the station-master at Medicine Bow, the next stopping place, had sent

[024-083] on before. The passengers drew around and took part in the discussion,

[024-084] in which Colonel Proctor, with his insolent manner, was conspicuous.

[024-085] Passepartout, joining the group, heard the signal-man say, “No! you

[024-086] can’t pass. The bridge at Medicine Bow is shaky, and would not bear the

[024-087] weight of the train.”

[024-088] This was a suspension-bridge thrown over some rapids, about a mile from

[024-089] the place where they now were. According to the signal-man, it was in a

[024-090] ruinous condition, several of the iron wires being broken; and it was

[024-091] impossible to risk the passage. He did not in any way exaggerate the

[024-092] condition of the bridge. It may be taken for granted that, rash as the

[024-093] Americans usually are, when they are prudent there is good reason for

[024-094] it.

[024-095] Passepartout, not daring to apprise his master of what he heard,

[024-096] listened with set teeth, immovable as a statue.

[024-097] “Hum!” cried Colonel Proctor; “but we are not going to stay here, I

[024-098] imagine, and take root in the snow?”

[024-099] “Colonel,” replied the conductor, “we have telegraphed to Omaha for a

[024-100] train, but it is not likely that it will reach Medicine Bow in less

[024-101] than six hours.”

[024-102] “Six hours!” cried Passepartout.

[024-103] “Certainly,” returned the conductor, “besides, it will take us as long

[024-104] as that to reach Medicine Bow on foot.”

[024-105] “But it is only a mile from here,” said one of the passengers.

[024-106] “Yes, but it’s on the other side of the river.”

[024-107] “And can’t we cross that in a boat?” asked the colonel.

[024-108] “That’s impossible. The creek is swelled by the rains. It is a rapid,

[024-109] and we shall have to make a circuit of ten miles to the north to find a

[024-110] ford.”

[024-111] The colonel launched a volley of oaths, denouncing the railway company

[024-112] and the conductor; and Passepartout, who was furious, was not

[024-113] disinclined to make common cause with him. Here was an obstacle,

[024-114] indeed, which all his master’s banknotes could not remove.

[024-115] There was a general disappointment among the passengers, who, without

[024-116] reckoning the delay, saw themselves compelled to trudge fifteen miles

[024-117] over a plain covered with snow. They grumbled and protested, and would

[024-118] certainly have thus attracted Phileas Fogg’s attention if he had not

[024-119] been completely absorbed in his game.

[024-120] Passepartout found that he could not avoid telling his master what had

[024-121] occurred, and, with hanging head, he was turning towards the car, when

[024-122] [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]], a true Yankee, named [[james-forster|Forster]] called out, “Gentlemen,

[024-123] perhaps there is a way, after all, to get over.”

[024-124] “On the bridge?” asked a passenger.

[024-125] “On the bridge.”

[024-126] “With our train?”

[024-127] “With our train.”

[024-128] Passepartout stopped short, and eagerly listened to the engineer.

[024-129] “But the bridge is unsafe,” urged the conductor.

[024-130] “No matter,” replied Forster; “I think that by putting on the very

[024-131] highest speed we might have a chance of getting over.”

[024-132] “The devil!” muttered Passepartout.

[024-133] But a number of the passengers were at once attracted by the engineer’s

[024-134] proposal, and Colonel Proctor was especially delighted, and found the

[024-135] plan a very feasible one. He told stories about engineers leaping their

[024-136] trains over rivers without bridges, by putting on full steam; and many

[024-137] of those present avowed themselves of the engineer’s mind.

[024-138] “We have fifty chances out of a hundred of getting over,” said one.

[024-139] “Eighty! ninety!”

[024-140] Passepartout was astounded, and, though ready to attempt anything to

[024-141] get over Medicine Creek, thought the experiment proposed a little too

[024-142] American. “Besides,” thought he, “there’s a still more simple way, and

[024-143] it does not even occur to any of these people! Sir,” said he aloud to

[024-144] one of the passengers, “the engineer’s plan seems to me a little

[024-145] dangerous, but—”

[024-146] “Eighty chances!” replied the passenger, turning his back on him.

[024-147] “I know it,” said Passepartout, turning to another passenger, “but a

[024-148] simple idea—”

[024-149] “Ideas are no use,” returned the American, shrugging his shoulders, “as

[024-150] the engineer assures us that we can pass.”

[024-151] “Doubtless,” urged Passepartout, “we can pass, but perhaps it would be

[024-152] more prudent—”

[024-153] “What! Prudent!” cried Colonel Proctor, whom this word seemed to excite

[024-154] prodigiously. “At full speed, don’t you see, at full speed!”

[024-155] “I know—I see,” repeated Passepartout; “but it would be, if not more

[024-156] prudent, since that word displeases you, at least more natural—”

[024-157] “Who! What! What’s the matter with this fellow?” cried several.

[024-158] The poor fellow did not know to whom to address himself.

[024-159] “Are you afraid?” asked Colonel Proctor.

[024-160] “I afraid? Very well; I will show these people that a Frenchman can be

[024-161] as American as they!”

[024-162] “All aboard!” cried the conductor.

[024-163] “Yes, all aboard!” repeated Passepartout, and immediately. “But they

[024-164] can’t prevent me from thinking that it would be more natural for us to

[024-165] cross the bridge on foot, and let the train come after!”

[024-166] But no one heard this sage reflection, nor would anyone have

[024-167] acknowledged its justice. The passengers resumed their places in the

[024-168] cars. Passepartout took his seat without telling what had passed. The

[024-169] whist-players were quite absorbed in their game.

[024-170] The locomotive whistled vigorously; the engineer, reversing the steam,

[024-171] backed the train for nearly a mile—retiring, like a jumper, in order to

[024-172] take a longer leap. Then, with another whistle, he began to move

[024-173] forward; the train increased its speed, and soon its rapidity became

[024-174] frightful; a prolonged screech issued from the locomotive; the piston

[024-175] worked up and down twenty strokes to the second. They perceived that

[024-176] the whole train, rushing on at the rate of a hundred miles an hour,

[024-177] hardly bore upon the rails at all.

[024-178] And they passed over! It was like a flash. No one saw the bridge. The

[024-179] train leaped, so to speak, from one bank to the other, and the engineer

[024-180] could not stop it until it had gone five miles beyond the station. But

[024-181] scarcely had the train passed the river, when the bridge, completely

[024-182] ruined, fell with a crash into the rapids of Medicine Bow.

[024-186] The train pursued its course, that evening, without interruption,

[024-187] passing Fort Saunders, crossing Cheyne Pass, and reaching Evans Pass.

[024-188] The road here attained the highest elevation of the journey, eight

[024-189] thousand and ninety-two feet above the level of the sea. The travellers

[024-190] had now only to descend to the Atlantic by limitless plains, levelled

[024-191] by nature. A branch of the “grand trunk” led off southward to Denver,

[024-192] the capital of Colorado. The country round about is rich in gold and

[024-193] silver, and more than fifty thousand inhabitants are already settled

[024-194] there.

[024-195] Thirteen hundred and eighty-two miles had been passed over from San

[024-196] Francisco, in three days and three nights; four days and nights more

[024-197] would probably bring them to New York. Phileas Fogg was not as yet

[024-198] behind-hand.

[024-199] During the night Camp Walbach was passed on the left; Lodge Pole Creek

[024-200] ran parallel with the road, marking the boundary between the

[024-201] territories of Wyoming and Colorado. They entered Nebraska at eleven,

[024-202] passed near Sedgwick, and touched at Julesburg, on the southern branch

[024-203] of the Platte River.
