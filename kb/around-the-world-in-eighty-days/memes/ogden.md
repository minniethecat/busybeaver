# Ogden

Ogden was a small but strategically vital railroad junction in the Utah Territory, where the transcontinental line met the branch railway to Salt Lake City. When Phileas Fogg's train reached Ogden at two o'clock on the afternoon of 5 December 1872, it rested for six hours — long enough for Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout to take the branch line to visit the Mormon capital. (PN: 023-001)

## Geography and Infrastructure

Ogden sits at the western foot of the Wasatch Range, where the Weber River emerges from the mountains. By 1872, it had become a major junction on the Pacific Railroad: the point where trains from the east divided, some continuing westward across the Great Basin toward California, others turning south on the branch line to Salt Lake City. The city's importance derived entirely from the railroad — it was a creation of steam and steel, not of natural geography.

## Fogg's Visit

Fogg's party arrived at Ogden on schedule. With six hours to wait, they boarded the branch train for the short journey to Salt Lake City, where they spent two hours touring the strangely ordered streets of the Mormon capital. Passepartout, ever curious, was eager to see the city. Fogg, characteristically, visited with the same methodical calm with which he did everything else. (PN: 023-071)

The visit to Salt Lake City was the only purely touristic detour of Fogg's entire journey — a rare moment when the timetable allowed a gap, and Fogg chose to fill it with sightseeing rather than waiting at the station. It was made possible entirely by Ogden's role as a junction: without the six-hour stop, Fogg would never have seen the Mormon capital.

After returning to Ogden, the train departed northeast at six o'clock, following the Weber River for an hour before heading into the mountains. The party had completed nearly nine hundred miles since leaving San Francisco. (PN: 023-130)

## Historical Significance

Ogden's importance in the novel reflects its real historical role. The completion of the transcontinental railroad at Promontory Summit (about fifty miles northwest of Ogden) in 1869 transformed Ogden from a small Mormon settlement into a major transportation hub. Trains from the east and west met here, and the branch line to Salt Lake City made Ogden the gateway to the Mormon heartland.

By including Ogden — an obscure railroad town — in his narrative, Verne demonstrated his meticulous research into the real infrastructure of global travel. The novel's geography is not fictional; it is anchored in the actual railway timetables and junction points of 1872.
