[021-001] [[passepartout|Passepartout]], in his joy on reaching at last the American continent,

[021-002] thought he would manifest it by executing a perilous vault in fine

[021-003] style; but, tumbling upon some worm-eaten planks, he fell through them.

[021-004] Put out of countenance by the manner in which he thus “set foot” upon

[021-005] the New World, he uttered a loud cry, which so frightened the

[021-006] innumerable cormorants and pelicans that are always perched upon these

[021-007] movable quays, that they flew noisily away.

[021-008] [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]], on reaching shore, proceeded to find out at what hour the

[021-009] first [[railway-travel|train]] left for [[new-york|New York]], and learned that this was at six o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]]

[021-010] p.m.; he had, therefore, an entire day to spend in the Californian

[021-011] capital. Taking a carriage at a charge of three dollars, he and [[aouda|Aouda]]

[021-012] entered it, while Passepartout mounted the box beside the driver, and

[021-013] they set out for the International Hotel.

[021-014] From his exalted position Passepartout observed with much curiosity the

[021-015] wide streets, the low, evenly ranged houses, the Anglo-Saxon Gothic

[021-016] churches, the great docks, the palatial wooden and brick warehouses,

[021-017] the numerous conveyances, omnibuses, horse-cars, and upon the

[021-018] side-walks, not only Americans and Europeans, but Chinese and Indians.

[021-019] Passepartout was surprised at all he saw. [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] was no longer

[021-020] the legendary city of 1849—a city of banditti, assassins, and

[021-021] incendiaries, who had flocked hither in crowds in pursuit of plunder; a

[021-022] paradise of outlaws, where they gambled with gold-dust, a revolver in

[021-023] one hand and a bowie-knife in the other: it was now a great commercial

[021-024] emporium.

[021-025] The lofty tower of its City Hall overlooked the whole panorama of the

[021-026] streets and avenues, which cut each other at right-angles, and in the

[021-027] midst of which appeared pleasant, verdant squares, while beyond

[021-028] appeared the Chinese quarter, seemingly imported from the Celestial

[021-029] Empire in a toy-box. Sombreros and red shirts and plumed Indians were

[021-030] rarely to be seen; but there were silk hats and black coats everywhere

[021-031] worn by a multitude of nervously active, gentlemanly-looking men. Some

[021-032] of the streets—especially Montgomery Street, which is to San Francisco

[021-033] what Regent Street is to [[london|London]], the Boulevard des Italiens to Paris,

[021-034] and Broadway to New York—were lined with splendid and spacious stores,

[021-035] which exposed in their windows the products of the entire world.

[021-036] When Passepartout reached the International Hotel, it did not seem to

[021-037] him as if he had left England at all.

[021-038] The ground floor of the hotel was occupied by a large bar, a sort of

[021-039] restaurant freely open to all passers-by, who might partake of dried

[021-040] beef, oyster soup, biscuits, and cheese, without taking out their

[021-041] purses. Payment was made only for the ale, porter, or sherry which was

[021-042] drunk. This seemed “very American” to Passepartout. The hotel

[021-043] refreshment-rooms were comfortable, and Mr. Fogg and Aouda, installing

[021-044] themselves at a table, were abundantly served on diminutive plates by

[021-045] negroes of darkest hue.

[021-046] After breakfast, Mr. Fogg, accompanied by Aouda, started for the

[021-047] English consulate to have his [[passport-and-identity|passport]] _visaed_. As he was going out,

[021-048] he met Passepartout, who asked him if it would not be well, before

[021-049] taking the train, to purchase some dozens of Enfield rifles and Colt’s

[021-050] revolvers. He had been listening to stories of attacks upon the trains

[021-051] by the Sioux and Pawnees. Mr. Fogg thought it a useless precaution, but

[021-052] told him to do as he thought best, and went on to [[british-consular-service|the consulate]].

[021-053] He had not proceeded two hundred steps, however, when, “by the greatest

[021-054] chance in the world,” he met [[detective-fix|Fix]]. The detective seemed wholly taken by

[021-055] surprise. What! Had Mr. Fogg and himself crossed the [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]] together,

[021-056] and not met on the [[steamer-travel|steamer]]! At least [[fix-detective|Fix]] felt honoured to behold once

[021-057] more the [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] to whom he owed so much, and, as his business

[021-058] recalled him to Europe, he should be delighted to continue the journey

[021-059] in such pleasant company.

[021-060] Mr. Fogg replied that the honour would be his; and the detective—who

[021-061] was determined not to lose sight of him—begged permission to accompany

[021-062] them in their walk about San Francisco—a request which Mr. Fogg readily

[021-063] granted.

[021-064] They soon found themselves in Montgomery Street, where a great crowd

[021-065] was collected; the side-walks, street, horsecar rails, the shop-doors,

[021-066] the windows of the houses, and even the roofs, were full of people. Men

[021-067] were going about carrying large posters, and flags and streamers were

[021-068] floating in the wind; while loud cries were heard on every hand.

[021-069] “Hurrah for Camerfield!”

[021-070] “Hurrah for Mandiboy!”

[021-071] It was a political meeting; at least so Fix conjectured, who said to

[021-072] Mr. Fogg, “Perhaps we had better not mingle with the crowd. There may

[021-073] be danger in it.”

[021-074] “Yes,” returned Mr. Fogg; “and blows, even if they are political, are

[021-075] still blows.”

[021-076] Fix smiled at this remark; and, in order to be able to see without

[021-077] being jostled about, the party took up a position on the top of a

[021-078] flight of steps situated at the upper end of Montgomery Street.

[021-079] Opposite them, on the other side of the street, between a coal wharf

[021-080] and a petroleum warehouse, a large platform had been erected in the

[021-081] open air, towards which the current of the crowd seemed to be directed.

[021-082] For what purpose was this meeting? What was the occasion of this

[021-083] excited assemblage? Phileas Fogg could not imagine. Was it to nominate

[021-084] some high official—a governor or member of Congress? It was not

[021-085] improbable, so agitated was the multitude before them.

[021-086] Just at this moment there was an unusual stir in the human mass. All

[021-087] the hands were raised in the air. Some, tightly closed, seemed to

[021-088] disappear suddenly in the midst of the cries—an energetic way, no

[021-089] doubt, of casting a vote. The crowd swayed back, the banners and flags

[021-090] wavered, disappeared an instant, then reappeared in tatters. The

[021-091] undulations of the human surge reached the steps, while all the heads

[021-092] floundered on the surface like a sea agitated by a squall. Many of the

[021-093] black hats disappeared, and the greater part of the crowd seemed to

[021-094] have diminished in height.

[021-095] “It is evidently a meeting,” said Fix, “and its object must be an

[021-096] exciting one. I should not wonder if it were about the ‘Alabama,’

[021-097] despite the fact that that question is settled.”

[021-098] “Perhaps,” replied Mr. Fogg, simply.

[021-099] “At least, there are two champions in presence of each other, the

[021-100] Honourable Mr. Camerfield and the Honourable Mr. Mandiboy.”

[021-101] Aouda, leaning upon Mr. Fogg’s arm, observed the tumultuous scene with

[021-102] surprise, while Fix asked a man near him what the cause of it all was.

[021-103] Before the man could reply, a fresh agitation arose; hurrahs and

[021-104] excited shouts were heard; the staffs of the banners began to be used

[021-105] as offensive weapons; and fists flew about in every direction. Thumps

[021-106] were exchanged from the tops of the carriages and omnibuses which had

[021-107] been blocked up in the crowd. Boots and shoes went whirling through the

[021-108] air, and Mr. Fogg thought he even heard the crack of revolvers mingling

[021-109] in the din, the rout approached the stairway, and flowed over the lower

[021-110] step. One of the parties had evidently been repulsed; but the mere

[021-111] lookers-on could not tell whether Mandiboy or Camerfield had gained the

[021-112] upper hand.

[021-113] “It would be prudent for us to retire,” said Fix, who was anxious that

[021-114] Mr. Fogg should not receive any injury, at least until they got back to

[021-115] London. “If there is any question about England in all this, and we

[021-116] were recognised, I fear it would go hard with us.”

[021-117] “An English subject—” began Mr. Fogg.

[021-118] He did not finish his sentence; for a terrific hubbub now arose on the

[021-119] terrace behind the flight of steps where they stood, and there were

[021-120] frantic shouts of, “Hurrah for Mandiboy! Hip, hip, hurrah!”

[021-121] It was a band of voters coming to the [[the-rescue-of-aouda|rescue]] of their allies, and

[021-122] taking the Camerfield forces in flank. Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Fix found

[021-123] themselves between two fires; it was too late to escape. The torrent of

[021-124] men, armed with loaded canes and sticks, was irresistible. Phileas Fogg

[021-125] and Fix were roughly hustled in their attempts to protect their fair

[021-126] companion; the former, as cool as ever, tried to defend himself with

[021-127] the weapons which nature has placed at the end of every Englishman’s

[021-128] arm, but in vain. A big brawny fellow with a red beard, flushed face,

[021-129] and broad shoulders, who seemed to be the chief of the band, raised his

[021-130] clenched fist to strike Mr. Fogg, whom he would have given a crushing

[021-131] blow, had not Fix rushed in and received it in his stead. An enormous

[021-132] bruise immediately made its appearance under the detective’s silk hat,

[021-133] which was completely smashed in.

[021-134] “Yankee!” exclaimed Mr. Fogg, darting a contemptuous look at the

[021-135] ruffian.

[021-136] “Englishman!” returned the other. “We will meet again!”

[021-137] “When you please.”

[021-138] “What is your name?”

[021-139] “Phileas Fogg. And yours?”

[021-140] “Colonel Stamp [[colonel-proctor|Proctor]].”

[021-141] The human tide now swept by, after overturning Fix, who speedily got

[021-142] upon his feet again, though with tattered clothes. Happily, he was not

[021-143] seriously hurt. His travelling overcoat was divided into two unequal

[021-144] parts, and his trousers resembled those of certain Indians, which fit

[021-145] less compactly than they are easy to put on. Aouda had escaped

[021-146] unharmed, and Fix alone bore marks of the fray in his black and blue

[021-147] bruise.

[021-148] “Thanks,” said Mr. Fogg to the detective, as soon as they were out of

[021-149] the crowd.

[021-150] “No thanks are necessary,” replied Fix; “but let us go.”

[021-151] “Where?”

[021-152] “To a tailor’s.”

[021-153] Such a visit was, indeed, opportune. The clothing of both Mr. Fogg and

[021-154] Fix was in rags, as if they had themselves been actively engaged in the

[021-155] contest between Camerfield and Mandiboy. An hour after, they were once

[021-156] more suitably attired, and with Aouda returned to the International

[021-157] Hotel.

[021-158] Passepartout was waiting for his master, armed with half a dozen

[021-159] six-barrelled revolvers. When he perceived Fix, he knit his brows; but

[021-160] Aouda having, in a few words, told him of their [[spirit-of-adventure|adventure]], his

[021-161] countenance resumed its placid expression. Fix evidently was no longer

[021-162] an enemy, but an ally; he was faithfully keeping his word.

[021-163] Dinner over, the coach which was to convey the passengers and their

[021-164] luggage to the station drew up to the door. As he was getting in, Mr.

[021-165] Fogg said to Fix, “You have not seen this Colonel Proctor again?”

[021-166] “No.”

[021-167] “I will come back to America to find him,” said Phileas Fogg calmly.

[021-168] “It would not be right for an Englishman to permit himself to be

[021-169] treated in that way, without retaliating.”

[021-170] The detective smiled, but did not reply. It was clear that Mr. Fogg was

[021-171] one of those Englishmen who, while they do not tolerate duelling at

[021-172] home, fight abroad when their honour is attacked.

[021-173] At a quarter before six the travellers reached the station, and found

[021-174] the train ready to depart. As he was about to enter it, Mr. Fogg called

[021-175] a porter, and said to him: “My friend, was there not some trouble

[021-176] to-day in San Francisco?”

[021-177] “It was a political meeting, sir,” replied the porter.

[021-178] “But I thought there was a great deal of disturbance in the streets.”

[021-179] “It was only a meeting assembled for an election.”

[021-180] “The election of a general-in-chief, no doubt?” asked Mr. Fogg.

[021-181] “No, sir; of a justice of the peace.”

[021-182] Phileas Fogg got into the train, which started off at full speed.

[021-183] CHAPTER XXVI.

[021-184] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG AND PARTY TRAVEL BY THE PACIFIC RAILROAD

[021-185] “From ocean to ocean”—so say the Americans; and these four words

[021-186] compose the general designation of the “great trunk line” which crosses

[021-187] the entire width of the United States. The Pacific Railroad is,

[021-188] however, really divided into two distinct lines: the Central Pacific,

[021-189] between San Francisco and Ogden, and the Union Pacific, between Ogden

[021-190] and Omaha. Five main lines connect Omaha with New York.

[021-191] New York and San Francisco are thus united by an uninterrupted metal

[021-192] ribbon, which measures no less than three thousand seven hundred and

[021-193] eighty-six miles. Between Omaha and the Pacific the railway crosses a

[021-194] territory which is still infested by Indians and wild beasts, and a

[021-195] large tract which the Mormons, after they were driven from Illinois in

[021-196] 1845, began to colonise.
