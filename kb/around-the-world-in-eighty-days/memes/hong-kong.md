# Hong Kong

Hong Kong in 1872 was Britain's newest and most strategically vital colonial acquisition in East Asia — a free port on the South China coast that served as the gateway between the Indian Ocean steamer routes and the Pacific. For Phileas Fogg, it is the city where the detective plot and the travel plot collide with maximum force.

## Role in the Novel

Hong Kong is the setting for some of the novel's most consequential narrative developments. Fogg arrives aboard the Rangoon, expecting to transfer directly to the Carnatic for the Pacific crossing to Yokohama. But the Carnatic, her repairs completed ahead of schedule, has already sailed — leaving Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout stranded. (PN: 016-005)

The missed connection is no accident. Detective Fix, desperate to keep Fogg on British soil until the arrest warrant arrives from London, has been working behind the scenes. His most aggressive tactic unfolds in Hong Kong: he encounters Passepartout in a tavern, reveals his identity as a Scotland Yard detective, and attempts to recruit the servant as an informant. When Passepartout refuses, Fix drugs him in an opium den — a calculated act that separates servant from master at the most critical moment of the journey. (PN: 018-071)

The result is that Fogg boards the pilot-boat Tankadere for Shanghai without Passepartout, while Passepartout, drugged and disoriented, is carried aboard the Carnatic by the opium-den waiters — unconscious but en route to Yokohama. The Hong Kong episode thus splits the party and transforms the journey's second half into a race to reunite.

## Journey Context

Hong Kong represents the point of maximum vulnerability in Fogg's journey. Until now, his timetable has held — minor delays have been absorbed, and the wager has seemed achievable. But the missed Carnatic and Passepartout's disappearance represent a dual crisis: a lost connection and a lost companion. Fogg's response — chartering a twenty-ton pilot-boat for a storm-tossed voyage of eight hundred miles to Shanghai — demonstrates for the first time that he is willing to abandon the scheduled infrastructure entirely and improvise. The Hong Kong crisis is the crucible in which Fogg's methodical character is tested and proved equal to chaos.

## Historical & Cultural Context

Hong Kong Island was ceded to Britain in 1842 under the Treaty of Nanking, and the Kowloon Peninsula was added in 1860. By 1872, Victoria (now Central) was a thriving entrepôt — opium, tea, silk, and coolie labor flowed through its harbour. Verne's portrayal is colored by Victorian Orientalism: the opium den is exoticised as a site of oriental vice, and the colonial police station is represented as an outpost of British order. Yet the novel's geography is accurate: Hong Kong was indeed the critical transfer point between the P&O's India-China service and the Pacific Mail's transpacific route to San Francisco.
