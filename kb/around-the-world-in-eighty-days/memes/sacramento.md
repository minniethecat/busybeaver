# Sacramento

Sacramento, the capital of California, was the eastern gateway of the American transcontinental railroad journey that [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] undertook during his eighty-day circumnavigation. The [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]–Sacramento segment formed the first leg of the Pacific-to-Atlantic railway crossing, with Sacramento serving as the starting point of the Central Pacific line.

## Role in Fogg's Journey

Fogg passed through Sacramento largely unnoticed. The travelers, exhausted from their Pacific crossing and the excitement of reaching American soil, "while fast asleep, passed through Sacramento" (PN_028-001) at midnight. The train "sped on across the State of California," the country between San Francisco and Sacramento described as "not very hilly" — a flat agricultural plain through the Central Valley.

The Central Pacific Railroad, "taking Sacramento for its starting-point," extended eastward across the Sierra Nevada toward the Great Basin and the Rocky Mountains (PN_029-001). This line, completed in 1869, was the western half of the transcontinental railroad that Fogg relied upon — a modern engineering marvel that traversed "the Sierra Nevada, and descends, _viâ_ Sacramento, to the Pacific" (PN_028-001).

## The Central Pacific Railroad Hub

Sacramento's role as the Central Pacific's headquarters and eastern terminus made it one of the most strategically important railway cities in the American West. From Sacramento, the line climbed from near sea level to over seven thousand feet through the Sierra Nevada passes — Donner Pass among them — before descending into the Nevada desert. The fact that Fogg and his party slept through the city was characteristic of Verne's narrative technique: important places flash past, mentioned as waypoints rather than destinations, emphasizing the sheer speed and scale of the journey.

## Historical Context

By 1872, when Fogg made his journey, Sacramento had been transformed by two decades of explosive growth following the California Gold Rush of 1849. The city's position at the confluence of the Sacramento and American Rivers made it a natural transportation hub — first for riverboats carrying prospectors and supplies to the gold fields, and later for the railway that ended the isolation of the Pacific coast. The completion of the transcontinental railroad in 1869 had made it possible for a traveler like Fogg to cross from the Pacific to the Atlantic in seven days — a journey that, a generation earlier, would have taken months by wagon or weeks by ship around Cape Horn.
