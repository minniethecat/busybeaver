# New York

New York is the last waystation of Phileas Fogg's global itinerary — the city where disaster and improvisation converge in the novel's most audacious act of nautical piracy. After a three-thousand-mile rail journey across America, Fogg arrives to find his Atlantic steamer has sailed without him, and responds by purchasing a merchant vessel outright.

## Role in the Novel

Fogg and his party arrive at the Hudson River pier on the night of December 11th, only to discover that the Cunard steamer *China*, bound for Liverpool, has departed three-quarters of an hour earlier. (PN: 027-173) The loss is crushing: Fogg must now cross the Atlantic in nine days with no scheduled steamer at his disposal. Passepartout is "crushed" by guilt, believing his actions throughout the journey are responsible for the delay. (PN: 028-006)

The next morning, Fogg searches the Hudson waterfront and spots a screw steamer, the *Henrietta*, preparing to depart for Bordeaux. Its captain, Andrew Speedy of Cardiff, refuses to alter his course. Fogg first offers two thousand dollars per passenger for passage to Bordeaux; Captain Speedy accepts. (PN: 028-083) Once at sea, however, Fogg bribes the crew to mutiny, locks Captain Speedy in his cabin, and assumes command of the vessel — redirecting it toward Liverpool. (PN: 028-114, 028-123)

The *Henrietta* makes excellent speed across the Atlantic. When her coal runs out short of Liverpool, Fogg purchases the ship itself from Captain Speedy and burns her wooden upper works as fuel — every spare plank, spar, and fitting — until nothing remains but the iron hull. (PN: 029-040, 029-053, 029-061) The *Henrietta* enters Queenstown Harbour on December 21st, and Fogg transfers to a mail steamer for the final leg to Liverpool. (PN: 029-124)

## Key Events

- Fogg's party crosses the Hudson by Jersey City ferryboat and takes rooms at the St. Nicholas Hotel on Broadway (PN: 028-013)
- The *China*, for Liverpool, is discovered to have sailed three-quarters of an hour earlier (PN: 027-173)
- Fogg searches the Hudson waterfront alone at dawn and spies the *Henrietta* at anchor off the Battery (PN: 028-026)
- Fogg negotiates passage to Bordeaux at two thousand dollars per person — eight thousand dollars total (PN: 028-080)
- At sea, Fogg bribes the crew, imprisons Captain Speedy, and redirects the *Henrietta* toward Liverpool (PN: 028-114)
- The *Henrietta* runs out of coal; Fogg buys the vessel and burns it for fuel to reach Queenstown (PN: 029-053, 029-061)
- The *Henrietta* enters Queenstown Harbour at one o'clock; Fogg shakes hands with Speedy and departs by express train (PN: 029-124)

## Journey Context

New York represents the final improvisation in Fogg's journey. Every preceding obstacle — the Indian railway gap, the missed Carnatic in Hong Kong, the Sioux attack in Nebraska — has been met with calm resourcefulness. But in New York, Fogg's resources are pushed to their limit. The *Henrietta* gambit is his most brazen act: purchasing passage, bribing a crew, imprisoning a captain, and ultimately burning the vessel for fuel. That Fogg manages all this while remaining the novel's most imperturbable character is part of Verne's comic genius.

The city itself barely registers as a place — Fogg experiences it as a pier, a hotel, and a waterfront. Verne is more interested in the *idea* of New York as the Atlantic gateway than in its streets or citizens. The party's brief stay underscores the novel's central tension between the global infrastructure of steam and the human improvisation required when that infrastructure fails.

## Historical and Cultural Context

In 1872, New York was already the principal American port and the busiest harbor in the Western Hemisphere. The transatlantic steamer lines — Cunard, Inman, French Line, Hamburg-Amerika — operated from piers along the Hudson and East Rivers. The railway terminus on the New Jersey side (Jersey City) connected by ferry to Manhattan, as Fogg's party experiences. The St. Nicholas Hotel on Broadway, where Fogg stays, was one of the city's finest, famous for its white marble facade and opulent interiors.

Fogg's purchase of the *Henrietta* and his subsequent command of the vessel reflects a period when merchant shipping was still a matter of individual enterprise. Captain Speedy, as owner-captain, could negotiate passage on his own terms — and could be bought out, ship and all, for the right price.
