# Duty

Beyond the wager and the timetable, Phileas Fogg is driven by a deeper force: duty. It is duty that makes him rescue a stranger in India, duty that compels him to pursue Sioux captors across the American plains, and duty that enables him to accept apparent defeat with equanimity.

## Definition

Duty in this novel is the unwritten obligation that a gentleman owes to others — not because the law requires it, but because character demands it. Fogg never explains his moral philosophy; he simply acts. When told of the suttee, he says only, "I have yet twelve hours to spare; I can devote them to her." (PN: 012-056). The calculus is temporal — twelve spare hours — but the imperative is moral.

Duty in Verne's world is hierarchical: first to one's word (the wager), then to those one has taken under protection (Aouda, Passepartout), and only then to oneself. Fogg consistently subordinates his own safety and fortune to these obligations, and the novel rewards him for it.

## Role in the Novel

Duty provides the moral architecture of the plot. Every major decision Fogg makes can be understood as a choice between competing duties: to his timetable or to Aouda's safety, to his wager or to Passepartout's rescue. Fogg never hesitates in these choices — he always chooses the duty to others over the duty to his schedule — and this consistency is what transforms him from a "machine" into a hero.

The novel's happy ending is a vindication of duty. By rescuing Aouda, Fogg gains a wife; by pursuing Passepartout's captors, he retains a loyal servant; by accepting defeat with dignity, he earns the respect of all. Duty, the novel suggests, is not merely right — it is, in the long run, prudent.

## Historical & Cultural Context

The Victorian concept of duty was shaped by a confluence of religious, philosophical, and social forces. Evangelical Christianity emphasized moral obligation to others; Kantian philosophy elevated duty to a universal principle; and the cult of the gentleman translated abstract duty into a social code.

Fogg embodies an idealized version of this duty: it is secular but absolute, personal but universalizable. Unlike the religious missionary or the imperial administrator, Fogg has no ideology — he simply acts because it is right. This moral minimalism is, in its way, more appealing to modern readers than the self-justifying rhetoric of empire that accompanied much Victorian moral action.
