# Ganges River

The Ganges is the great sacred river of India, flowing from the Himalayas to the Bay of Bengal through the heart of the subcontinent. In *Around the World in Eighty Days*, [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] travels the length of the Ganges valley by rail — yet, as the narrator notes with gentle irony, he "descends the whole length of the beautiful valley of the Ganges without ever thinking of seeing it" ([PN=014-005]). The river serves as both backdrop and symbol: of the ancient culture through which Fogg passes with mathematical indifference, of the spiritual landscape that his clockwork journey cannot accommodate, and of India itself — vast, sacred, and irreducible to timetables.

## In the Novel

Fogg's rail journey from [[allahabad|Allahabad]] to [[calcutta|Calcutta]] follows the valley of the Ganges, passing through some of the most storied landscapes of India. Through the carriage windows, the travelers glimpse the sacred waters of the river, the temples that line its banks at Benares, and the pilgrims who travel from every corner of the peninsula to bathe in its currents ([PN=014-005]).

The narrator describes the scene with a richness that contrasts pointedly with Fogg's indifference:

> "Through the windows of their carriage the travellers had glimpses of the diversified landscape of Behar, with its green mountains, its fields of barley and corn, and its jungles peopled with alligators." ([PN=014-019])

This gap between landscape and observer — between the Ganges valley's beauty and Fogg's absorbed whist game — is one of the novel's most telling moments. The Ganges reveals what Fogg's method misses: the world he travels through but never truly sees.

## The Sacred River

The Ganges, along with the Jumna, is described as one of India's sacred rivers. According to Hindu tradition, the Ganges descends from paradise; bathing in its waters is believed to purify the soul. At Benares — the ancient city of temples and ghats — the devotions are most visible: the faithful immerse themselves in the river at dawn, while funeral pyres burn along the banks, sending the dead to their final rest ([PN=014-010]).

The novel treats this sacred geography with a mixture of respect and distance characteristic of Verne's narrator. The ritual life of the river is described in detail, yet always from the perspective of the passing train — observed, never entered.

## Transportation and Trade

The Ganges is not only a sacred landscape but a practical artery. Steamboats ply its waters, carrying passengers and goods along the waterway. Its junction with the Jumna at [[allahabad|Allahabad]] forms a critical node in the Indian transportation network — a convergence of water, rail, and road that makes Fogg's rapid passage through northern India possible ([PN=014-033]).

The river's role in the novel is thus doubled: it is both the spiritual heart of India and a piece of the logistical puzzle that Fogg must solve. This tension — between sacred meaning and practical function — runs through all of the novel's Indian chapters.
