# Great Indian Peninsula Railway

The railway company whose line across the Indian subcontinent was supposed to carry [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] and his party from [[bombay|Bombay]] to [[calcutta|Calcutta]]. That the line was incomplete — a fifty-mile gap between Kholby and Allahabad — became the first major disruption to Fogg's carefully calculated [[around-the-world-route|eighty-day schedule]].

## Role in the Novel

The railway appears in the story at a moment of apparent confidence. Fogg, having crossed the Arabian Sea aboard the _Mongolia_ of the [[p-and-o-company|P&O Company]], boards the train at Bombay believing the rails run unbroken to Calcutta. Verne presents the route with cartographic precision:

> "The general route of the Great Indian Peninsula Railway is as follows: leaving Bombay, it passes through Salcette, crossing to the mainland opposite Tannah, goes over the chain of the Western Ghauts, runs thence north-east as far as Burhampoor, skirts the nearly independent territory of Bundelcund, ascends to Allahabad, turns thence eastwardly, meets the Ganges at Benares, then departs from the river a little, and, descending south-eastward by Burdivan and the French town of Chandernagore, has its terminus at Calcutta." (006-184)

## The Kholby Gap

At Kholby, the party discovers that the railway is not yet finished. The fifty-mile section between Kholby and [[allahabad|Allahabad]] is still under construction. Passengers must find their own means of crossing the gap — a disruption that would be fatal to any tight schedule.

Fogg's response is characteristically decisive: he purchases an elephant and hires a Parsee guide, turning a railway failure into one of the most memorable episodes of the journey — the overland crossing that leads to [[the-rescue-from-suttee|the rescue of Aouda]]. (PN: 008-080)

## Sir Francis Cromarty

Phileas Fogg did not travel alone on the GIPR train. Sharing his carriage was [[sir-francis-cromarty|Sir Francis Cromarty]], the brigadier-general who had been one of Fogg's whist partners aboard the _Mongolia_ and who was now on his way to rejoin his corps at Benares. (007-113) Sir Francis was a man who knew India deeply: he had distinguished himself in the last Sepoy revolt, made India his home, and was "almost as familiar as a native with the customs, history, and character of India and its people." (007-118)

Cromarty provided knowledgeable commentary throughout the journey. As the train climbed into the Western Ghauts, he reminded Fogg that "some years ago" the railway had stopped at the base of those very mountains — passengers were then obliged to cross the pass in palanquins or on ponies. (007-147) The railway, he noted, had already been extended once; neither man suspected it was still incomplete.

That is why Cromarty's reaction to the halt at Kholby is so telling. When the conductor announced "Passengers will get out here!", Fogg "looked at Sir Francis Cromarty for an explanation; but the general could not tell what meant a halt in the midst of this forest of dates and acacias." (008-045) This was not a naive tourist surprised by India — this was a seasoned Anglo-Indian officer who had made the subcontinent his home for decades. Even he was caught off guard.

The confrontation with the conductor only deepened his incredulity. When told "The railway isn't finished," Cromarty fired back: "But the papers announced the opening of the railway throughout." (008-060) He pressed the point — "Yet you sell tickets from Bombay to Calcutta" — and the narrative notes he was "growing warm," soon becoming "furious." (008-062, 008-067)

Cromarty's astonishment serves an important narrative function. If even a brigadier-general who had lived in India for years did not know the line was broken, then Phileas Fogg — who had planned every stage of his journey from the Reform Club in London — could hardly be blamed for the oversight. The gap was not a failure of Fogg's planning but a deception perpetuated by newspapers and railway companies alike.

## Historical Context

The real Great Indian Peninsula Railway was India's first passenger railway, founded in 1849. Its line from Bombay to Calcutta was indeed opened in stages, and in 1872 (the year of Fogg's fictional journey) the gap Verne describes was historically accurate. The GIPR was central to British imperial infrastructure, collapsing the travel time across India from weeks to days.
