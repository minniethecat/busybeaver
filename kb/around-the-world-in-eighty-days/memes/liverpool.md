# Liverpool

The port where Phileas Fogg is arrested by Detective Fix at the very moment he believes his journey is over -- and the place where the novel's greatest crisis unfolds. Liverpool is the gateway back to England, but it becomes the site of Fogg's imprisonment, his apparent loss of the wager, and the dramatic revelation that ultimately saves him: the real bank robber, James Strand, has already been caught.

## Role in the Novel

Liverpool is the novel's crisis point. Fogg and his party arrive aboard the *Henrietta*, having burned the ship's upper works as fuel after exhausting the coal supply (029-052). They disembark on the Liverpool quay at twenty minutes before twelve on the 21st of December (029-137/029-171) -- an extraordinary feat of improvisation that has kept the eighty-day schedule just barely alive.

But the moment Fogg sets foot on English soil, Fix acts. Throughout the entire journey, Fix has been unable to arrest Fogg because the warrant was only valid within British jurisdiction. Now, at last, the detective steps forward, shows his warrant, and declares: "I arrest you in the Queen's name!" (029-141/029-143). This is the culmination of Fix's transcontinental pursuit -- the detective has shadowed Fogg across India, Hong Kong, Yokohama, San Francisco, and the Atlantic, given him passage to Liverpool (030-092), all for this single moment of arrest on British territory (029-163).

Passepartout, witnessing his master's arrest, nearly attacks Fix but is restrained by policemen (029-148). Fogg himself, "honest and courageous," is arrested as a common robber (029-152). The arrest, as the narrator observes, was "fatal" (029-170): Fogg is taken into custody at the Custom House, and by the time the real robber James Strand is revealed to have been arrested in Edinburgh three days earlier (030-014/030-200), it appears too late. The express train to London -- the last one that could have delivered Fogg to the Reform Club by the 8:45 p.m. deadline -- has departed thirty-five minutes earlier (030-027).

The journey from Liverpool to London was six hours (029-173). Fogg, now free but deprived of the regular express, charters a special train. He arrives at the Reform Club at thirty-five minutes past eight (031-109) -- apparently five minutes after the 8:45 deadline. But here the novel's most famous twist intervenes: Passepartout, dispatched to notify the Reverend Samuel Wilson of the upcoming marriage, discovers it is actually Saturday, not Sunday (030-188). Fogg has gained one day by traveling eastward around the globe -- he has crossed the International Date Line without realizing it. The real time is 8:40 p.m., not 8:40 a.m. the next day. He dashes to the Reform Club and arrives precisely at the fifty-seventh second before twenty minutes to nine, winning the wager (031-153 through 031-201).

## Journey Context

Liverpool was the natural Atlantic terminus for the P&O steamer lines that connected Britain to its empire. In the novel's eighty-day timetable (003-043/003-044), the leg from New York to London by steamer and rail was allocated nine days. Fogg actually arrives in Liverpool after a far more harrowing transatlantic crossing aboard the barely-floating *Henrietta*, having commandeered the vessel from Captain Speedy at New York. The journey from Liverpool to London by express train normally took six hours, making Liverpool the critical last transfer point before the final dash to the Reform Club.
