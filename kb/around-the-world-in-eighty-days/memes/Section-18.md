[018-001] The “Carnatic,” setting sail from [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] at half-past six on the 7th

[018-002] of November, directed her course at full steam towards Japan. She

[018-003] carried a large cargo and a well-filled cabin of passengers. Two

[018-004] state-rooms in the rear were, however, unoccupied—those which had been

[018-005] engaged by [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]].

[018-006] The next day a passenger with a half-stupefied eye, staggering gait,

[018-007] and disordered hair, was seen to emerge from the second cabin, and to

[018-008] totter to a seat on deck.

[018-009] It was [[passepartout|Passepartout]]; and what had happened to him was as follows:

[018-010] Shortly after [[detective-fix|Fix]] left the opium den, two waiters had lifted the

[018-011] unconscious Passepartout, and had carried him to the bed reserved for

[018-012] the smokers. Three hours later, pursued even in his dreams by a fixed

[018-013] idea, the poor fellow awoke, and struggled against the stupefying

[018-014] influence of the narcotic. The thought of a duty unfulfilled shook off

[018-015] his torpor, and he hurried from the abode of drunkenness. Staggering

[018-016] and holding himself up by keeping against the walls, falling down and

[018-017] creeping up again, and irresistibly impelled by a kind of instinct, he

[018-018] kept crying out, “The ‘Carnatic!’ the ‘Carnatic!’”

[018-019] The [[steamer-travel|steamer]] lay puffing alongside the quay, on the point of starting.

[018-020] Passepartout had but few steps to go; and, rushing upon the plank, he

[018-021] crossed it, and fell unconscious on the deck, just as the “Carnatic”

[018-022] was moving off. Several sailors, who were evidently accustomed to this

[018-023] sort of scene, carried the poor Frenchman down into the second cabin,

[018-024] and Passepartout did not wake until they were one hundred and fifty

[018-025] miles away from China. Thus he found himself the next morning on the

[018-026] deck of the “Carnatic,” and eagerly inhaling the exhilarating

[018-027] sea-breeze. The pure air sobered him. He began to collect his sense,

[018-028] which he found a difficult task; but at last he recalled the events of

[018-029] the evening before, [[fix-detective|Fix]]’s revelation, and the opium-house.

[018-030] “It is evident,” said he to himself, “that I have been abominably

[018-031] drunk! What will Mr. Fogg say? At least I have not missed the steamer,

[018-032] which is the most important thing.”

[018-033] Then, as Fix occurred to him: “As for that rascal, I hope we are well

[018-034] rid of him, and that he has not dared, as he proposed, to follow us on

[018-035] board the “Carnatic.” A detective on the track of Mr. Fogg, accused of

[018-036] robbing the Bank of England! Pshaw! Mr. Fogg is no more a robber than I

[018-037] am a murderer.”

[018-038] Should he divulge Fix’s real errand to his master? Would it do to tell

[018-039] the part the detective was playing? Would it not be better to wait

[018-040] until Mr. Fogg reached [[london|London]] again, and then impart to him that an

[018-041] agent of the metropolitan police had been following him round the

[018-042] world, and have a good laugh over it? No doubt; at least, it was worth

[018-043] considering. The first thing to do was to find Mr. Fogg, and apologise

[018-044] for his singular behaviour.

[018-045] Passepartout got up and proceeded, as well as he could with the rolling

[018-046] of the steamer, to the after-deck. He saw no one who resembled either

[018-047] his master or [[aouda|Aouda]]. “Good!” muttered he; “Aouda has not got up yet,

[018-048] and Mr. Fogg has probably found some partners at whist.”

[018-049] He descended to the saloon. Mr. Fogg was not there. Passepartout had

[018-050] only, however, to ask the purser the number of his master’s state-room.

[018-051] The purser replied that he did not know any passenger by the name of

[018-052] Fogg.

[018-053] “I beg your pardon,” said Passepartout persistently. “He is a tall

[018-054] [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]], quiet, and not very talkative, and has with him a young

[018-055] lady—”

[018-056] “There is no young lady on board,” interrupted the purser. “Here is a

[018-057] list of the passengers; you may see for yourself.”

[018-058] Passepartout scanned the list, but his master’s name was not upon it.

[018-059] All at once an idea struck him.

[018-060] “Ah! am I on the ‘Carnatic?’”

[018-061] “Yes.”

[018-062] “On the way to [[yokohama|Yokohama]]?”

[018-063] “Certainly.”

[018-064] Passepartout had for an instant feared that he was on the wrong boat;

[018-065] but, though he was really on the “Carnatic,” his master was not there.

[018-066] He fell thunderstruck on a seat. He saw it all now. He remembered that

[018-067] the time of sailing had been changed, that he should have informed his

[018-068] master of that fact, and that he had not done so. It was his fault,

[018-069] then, that Mr. Fogg and Aouda had missed the steamer. Yes, but it was

[018-070] still more the fault of the traitor who, in order to separate him from

[018-071] his master, and detain the latter at Hong Kong, had inveigled him into

[018-072] getting drunk! He now saw the detective’s trick; and at this moment Mr.

[018-073] Fogg was certainly ruined, his [[the-wager|bet]] was lost, and he himself perhaps

[018-074] arrested and imprisoned! At this thought Passepartout tore his hair.

[018-075] Ah, if Fix ever came within his reach, what a settling of accounts

[018-076] there would be!

[018-077] After his first depression, Passepartout became calmer, and began to

[018-078] study his situation. It was certainly not an enviable one. He found

[018-079] himself on the way to Japan, and what should he do when he got there?

[018-080] His pocket was empty; he had not a solitary shilling, not so much as a

[018-081] penny. His passage had fortunately been paid for in advance; and he had

[018-082] five or six days in which to decide upon his future course. He fell to

[018-083] at meals with an appetite, and ate for Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and himself. He

[018-084] helped himself as generously as if Japan were a desert, where nothing

[018-085] to eat was to be looked for.

[018-086] At dawn on the 13th the “Carnatic” entered the port of Yokohama. This

[018-087] is an important port of call in the [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]], where all the

[018-088] mail-steamers, and those carrying travellers between North America,

[018-089] China, Japan, and the Oriental islands put in. It is situated in the

[018-090] bay of Yeddo, and at but a short distance from that second capital of

[018-091] the Japanese Empire, and the residence of the Tycoon, the civil

[018-092] Emperor, before the Mikado, the spiritual Emperor, absorbed his office

[018-093] in his own. The “Carnatic” anchored at the quay near the custom-house,

[018-094] in the midst of a crowd of ships bearing the flags of all nations.

[018-095] Passepartout went timidly ashore on this so curious territory of the

[018-096] Sons of the Sun. He had nothing better to do than, taking chance for

[018-097] his guide, to wander aimlessly through the streets of Yokohama. He

[018-098] found himself at first in a thoroughly European quarter, the houses

[018-099] having low fronts, and being adorned with verandas, beneath which he

[018-100] caught glimpses of neat peristyles. This quarter occupied, with its

[018-101] streets, squares, docks, and warehouses, all the space between the

[018-102] “promontory of the Treaty” and the river. Here, as at Hong Kong and

[018-103] [[calcutta|Calcutta]], were mixed crowds of all races, Americans and English,

[018-104] Chinamen and Dutchmen, mostly merchants ready to buy or sell anything.

[018-105] The Frenchman felt himself as much alone among them as if he had

[018-106] dropped down in the midst of Hottentots.

[018-107] He had, at least, one resource,—to call on the French and English

[018-108] consuls at Yokohama for assistance. But he shrank from telling the

[018-109] story of his adventures, intimately connected as it was with that of

[018-110] his master; and, before doing so, he determined to exhaust all other

[018-111] means of aid. As chance did not favour him in the European quarter, he

[018-112] penetrated that inhabited by the native Japanese, determined, if

[018-113] necessary, to push on to Yeddo.

[018-114] The Japanese quarter of Yokohama is called Benten, after the goddess of

[018-115] the sea, who is worshipped on the islands round about. There

[018-116] Passepartout beheld beautiful fir and cedar groves, sacred gates of a

[018-117] singular architecture, bridges half hid in the midst of bamboos and

[018-118] reeds, temples shaded by immense cedar-trees, holy retreats where were

[018-119] sheltered Buddhist priests and sectaries of Confucius, and interminable

[018-120] streets, where a perfect harvest of rose-tinted and red-cheeked

[018-121] children, who looked as if they had been cut out of Japanese screens,

[018-122] and who were playing in the midst of short-legged poodles and yellowish

[018-123] cats, might have been gathered.

[018-124] The streets were crowded with people. Priests were passing in

[018-125] processions, beating their dreary tambourines; police and custom-house

[018-126] officers with pointed hats encrusted with lac and carrying two sabres

[018-127] hung to their waists; soldiers, clad in blue cotton with white stripes,

[018-128] and bearing guns; the Mikado’s guards, enveloped in silken doubles,

[018-129] hauberks and coats of mail; and numbers of military folk of all

[018-130] ranks—for the military profession is as much respected in Japan as it

[018-131] is despised in China—went hither and thither in groups and pairs.

[018-132] Passepartout saw, too, begging friars, long-robed pilgrims, and simple

[018-133] civilians, with their warped and jet-black hair, big heads, long busts,

[018-134] slender legs, short stature, and complexions varying from copper-colour

[018-135] to a dead white, but never yellow, like the Chinese, from whom the

[018-136] Japanese widely differ. He did not fail to observe the curious

[018-137] equipages—carriages and palanquins, barrows supplied with sails, and

[018-138] litters made of bamboo; nor the women—whom he thought not especially

[018-139] handsome—who took little steps with their little feet, whereon they

[018-140] wore canvas shoes, straw sandals, and clogs of worked wood, and who

[018-141] displayed tight-looking eyes, flat chests, teeth fashionably blackened,

[018-142] and gowns crossed with silken scarfs, tied in an enormous knot behind

[018-143] an ornament which the modern Parisian ladies seem to have borrowed from

[018-144] the dames of Japan.

[018-145] Passepartout wandered for several hours in the midst of this motley

[018-146] crowd, looking in at the windows of the rich and curious shops, the

[018-147] jewellery establishments glittering with quaint Japanese ornaments, the

[018-148] restaurants decked with streamers and banners, the tea-houses, where

[018-149] the odorous beverage was being drunk with “saki,” a liquor concocted

[018-150] from the fermentation of rice, and the comfortable smoking-houses,

[018-151] where they were puffing, not opium, which is almost unknown in Japan,

[018-152] but a very fine, stringy tobacco. He went on till he found himself in

[018-153] the fields, in the midst of vast rice plantations. There he saw

[018-154] dazzling camellias expanding themselves, with flowers which were giving

[018-155] forth their last colours and perfumes, not on bushes, but on trees, and

[018-156] within bamboo enclosures, cherry, plum, and apple trees, which the

[018-157] Japanese cultivate rather for their blossoms than their fruit, and

[018-158] which queerly-fashioned, grinning scarecrows protected from the

[018-159] sparrows, pigeons, ravens, and other voracious birds. On the branches

[018-160] of the cedars were perched large eagles; amid the foliage of the

[018-161] weeping willows were herons, solemnly standing on one leg; and on every

[018-162] hand were crows, ducks, hawks, wild birds, and a multitude of cranes,

[018-163] which the Japanese consider sacred, and which to their minds symbolise

[018-164] long life and prosperity.

[018-165] As he was strolling along, Passepartout espied some violets among the

[018-166] shrubs.

[018-167] “Good!” said he; “I’ll have some supper.”

[018-168] But, on smelling them, he found that they were odourless.

[018-169] “No chance there,” thought he.

[018-170] The worthy fellow had certainly taken good care to eat as hearty a

[018-171] breakfast as possible before leaving the “Carnatic;” but, as he had

[018-172] been walking about all day, the demands of hunger were becoming

[018-173] importunate. He observed that the butchers stalls contained neither

[018-174] mutton, goat, nor pork; and, knowing also that it is a sacrilege to

[018-175] kill cattle, which are preserved solely for farming, he made up his

[018-176] mind that meat was far from plentiful in Yokohama—nor was he mistaken;

[018-177] and, in default of butcher’s meat, he could have wished for a quarter

[018-178] of wild boar or deer, a partridge, or some quails, some game or fish,

[018-179] which, with rice, the Japanese eat almost exclusively. But he found it

[018-180] necessary to keep up a stout heart, and to postpone the meal he craved

[018-181] till the following morning. Night came, and Passepartout re-entered the

[018-182] native quarter, where he wandered through the streets, lit by

[018-183] vari-coloured lanterns, looking on at the dancers, who were executing

[018-184] skilful steps and boundings, and the astrologers who stood in the open

[018-185] air with their telescopes. Then he came to the harbour, which was lit

[018-186] up by the resin torches of the fishermen, who were fishing from their

[018-187] boats.
