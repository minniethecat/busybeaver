[014-001] [[detective-fix|Fix]] looked intently at his companion, whose countenance was as serene

[014-002] as possible, and laughed with him. But [[passepartout|Passepartout]] persisted in

[014-003] chaffing him by asking him if he made much by his present occupation.

[014-004] “Yes, and no,” returned [[fix-detective|Fix]]; “there is good and bad luck in such

[014-005] things. But you must understand that I don’t travel at my own expense.”

[014-006] “Oh, I am quite sure of that!” cried Passepartout, laughing heartily.

[014-007] Fix, fairly puzzled, descended to his cabin and gave himself up to his

[014-008] reflections. He was evidently suspected; somehow or other the Frenchman

[014-009] had found out that he was a detective. But had he told his master? What

[014-010] part was he playing in all this: was he an accomplice or not? Was the

[014-011] game, then, up? Fix spent several hours turning these things over in

[014-012] his mind, sometimes thinking that all was lost, then persuading himself

[014-013] that [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]] was ignorant of his presence, and then undecided what course

[014-014] it was best to take.

[014-015] Nevertheless, he preserved his coolness of mind, and at last resolved

[014-016] to deal plainly with Passepartout. If he did not find it practicable to

[014-017] arrest Fogg at [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], and if Fogg made preparations to leave that

[014-018] last foothold of English territory, he, Fix, would tell Passepartout

[014-019] all. Either the servant was the accomplice of his master, and in this

[014-020] case the master knew of his operations, and he should fail; or else the

[014-021] servant knew nothing about the robbery, and then his interest would be

[014-022] to abandon the robber.

[014-023] Such was the situation between Fix and Passepartout. Meanwhile Phileas

[014-024] Fogg moved about above them in the most majestic and unconscious

[014-025] indifference. He was passing methodically in his orbit around the

[014-026] world, regardless of the lesser stars which gravitated around him. Yet

[014-027] there was near by what the astronomers would call a disturbing star,

[014-028] which might have produced an agitation in this [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]’s heart. But

[014-029] no! the charms of [[aouda|Aouda]] failed to act, to Passepartout’s great

[014-030] surprise; and the disturbances, if they existed, would have been more

[014-031] difficult to calculate than those of Uranus which led to the discovery

[014-032] of Neptune.

[014-033] It was every day an increasing wonder to Passepartout, who read in

[014-034] Aouda’s eyes the depths of her gratitude to his master. Phileas Fogg,

[014-035] though brave and gallant, must be, he thought, quite heartless. As to

[014-036] the sentiment which this journey might have awakened in him, there was

[014-037] clearly no trace of such a thing; while poor Passepartout existed in

[014-038] perpetual reveries.

[014-039] One day he was leaning on the railing of the engine-room, and was

[014-040] observing the engine, when a sudden pitch of the [[steamer-travel|steamer]] threw the

[014-041] screw out of the water. The steam came hissing out of the valves; and

[014-042] this made Passepartout indignant.

[014-043] “The valves are not sufficiently charged!” he exclaimed. “We are not

[014-044] going. Oh, these English! If this was an American craft, we should blow

[014-045] up, perhaps, but we should at all events go faster!”

[014-046] CHAPTER XVIII.

[014-047] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG, PASSEPARTOUT, AND FIX GO EACH ABOUT HIS BUSINESS

[014-048] The weather was bad during the latter days of the voyage. The wind,

[014-049] obstinately remaining in the north-west, blew a gale, and retarded the

[014-050] steamer. The “Rangoon” rolled heavily and the passengers became

[014-051] impatient of the long, monstrous waves which the wind raised before

[014-052] their path. A sort of tempest arose on the 3rd of November, the squall

[014-053] knocking the vessel about with fury, and the waves running high. The

[014-054] “Rangoon” reefed all her sails, and even the rigging proved too much,

[014-055] whistling and shaking amid the squall. The steamer was forced to

[014-056] proceed slowly, and [[captain-speedy|the captain]] estimated that she would reach Hong

[014-057] Kong twenty hours behind time, and more if the storm lasted.

[014-058] Phileas Fogg gazed at the tempestuous sea, which seemed to be

[014-059] struggling especially to delay him, with his habitual tranquillity. He

[014-060] never changed countenance for an instant, though a delay of twenty

[014-061] hours, by making him too late for the [[yokohama|Yokohama]] boat, would almost

[014-062] inevitably cause the loss of the [[the-wager|wager]]. But this man of [[courage|nerve]]

[014-063] manifested neither impatience nor annoyance; it seemed as if the storm

[014-064] were a part of his programme, and had been foreseen. Aouda was amazed

[014-065] to find him as calm as he had been from the first time she saw him.

[014-066] Fix did not look at the state of things in the same light. The storm

[014-067] greatly pleased him. His satisfaction would have been complete had the

[014-068] “Rangoon” been forced to retreat before the violence of wind and waves.

[014-069] Each delay filled him with hope, for it became more and more probable

[014-070] that Fogg would be obliged to remain some days at Hong Kong; and now

[014-071] the heavens themselves became his allies, with the gusts and squalls.

[014-072] It mattered not that they made him sea-sick—he made no account of this

[014-073] inconvenience; and, whilst his body was writhing under their effects,

[014-074] his spirit bounded with hopeful exultation.

[014-075] Passepartout was enraged beyond expression by the unpropitious weather.

[014-076] Everything had gone so well till now! Earth and sea had seemed to be at

[014-077] his master’s service; steamers and railways obeyed him; wind and steam

[014-078] united to speed his journey. Had the hour of adversity come?

[014-079] Passepartout was as much excited as if the twenty thousand pounds were

[014-080] to come from his own pocket. The storm exasperated him, the gale made

[014-081] him furious, and he longed to lash the obstinate sea into obedience.

[014-082] Poor fellow! Fix carefully concealed from him his own satisfaction,

[014-083] for, had he betrayed it, Passepartout could scarcely have restrained

[014-084] himself from personal violence.

[014-085] Passepartout remained on deck as long as the tempest lasted, being

[014-086] unable to remain quiet below, and taking it into his head to aid the

[014-087] progress of the ship by lending a hand with the crew. He overwhelmed

[014-088] the captain, officers, and sailors, who could not help laughing at his

[014-089] impatience, with all sorts of questions. He wanted to know exactly how

[014-090] long the storm was going to last; whereupon he was referred to the

[014-091] barometer, which seemed to have no intention of rising. Passepartout

[014-092] shook it, but with no perceptible effect; for neither shaking nor

[014-093] maledictions could prevail upon it to change its mind.

[014-094] On the 4th, however, the sea became more calm, and the storm lessened

[014-095] its violence; the wind veered southward, and was once more favourable.

[014-096] Passepartout cleared up with the weather. Some of the sails were

[014-097] unfurled, and the “Rangoon” resumed its most rapid speed. The time lost

[014-098] could not, however, be regained. Land was not signalled until five

[014-099] o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] on the morning of the 6th; the steamer was due on the 5th.

[014-100] Phileas Fogg was twenty-four hours behind-hand, and the Yokohama

[014-101] steamer would, of course, be missed.

[014-102] The pilot went on board at six, and took his place on the bridge, to

[014-103] guide the “Rangoon” through the channels to the port of Hong Kong.

[014-104] Passepartout longed to ask him if the steamer had left for Yokohama;

[014-105] but he dared not, for he wished to preserve the spark of hope, which

[014-106] still remained till the last moment. He had confided his anxiety to Fix

[014-107] who—the sly rascal!—tried to console him by saying that Mr. Fogg would

[014-108] be in time if he took the next boat; but this only put Passepartout in

[014-109] a passion.

[014-110] Mr. Fogg, bolder than his servant, did not hesitate to approach the

[014-111] pilot, and tranquilly ask him if he knew when a steamer would leave

[014-112] Hong Kong for Yokohama.

[014-113] “At high tide to-morrow morning,” answered the pilot.

[014-114] “Ah!” said Mr. Fogg, without betraying any astonishment.

[014-115] Passepartout, who heard what passed, would willingly have embraced the

[014-116] pilot, while Fix would have been glad to twist his neck.

[014-117] “What is the steamer’s name?” asked Mr. Fogg.

[014-118] “The ‘Carnatic.’”

[014-119] “Ought she not to have gone yesterday?”

[014-120] “Yes, sir; but they had to repair one of her boilers, and so her

[014-121] departure was postponed till to-morrow.”

[014-122] “Thank you,” returned Mr. Fogg, descending mathematically to the

[014-123] saloon.

[014-124] Passepartout clasped the pilot’s hand and shook it heartily in his

[014-125] delight, exclaiming, “Pilot, you are the best of good fellows!”

[014-126] The pilot probably does not know to this day why his responses won him

[014-127] this enthusiastic greeting. He remounted the bridge, and guided the

[014-128] steamer through the flotilla of junks, tankas, and fishing boats which

[014-129] crowd the harbour of Hong Kong.

[014-130] At one o’clock the “Rangoon” was at the quay, and the passengers were

[014-131] going ashore.

[014-132] Chance had strangely favoured Phileas Fogg, for had not the “Carnatic”

[014-133] been forced to lie over for repairing her boilers, she would have left

[014-134] on the 6th of November, and the passengers for Japan would have been

[014-135] obliged to await for a week the sailing of the next steamer. Mr. Fogg

[014-136] was, it is true, twenty-four hours behind his time; but this could not

[014-137] seriously imperil the remainder of his tour.

[014-138] The steamer which crossed the [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]] from Yokohama to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]

[014-139] made a direct connection with that from Hong Kong, and it could not

[014-140] sail until the latter reached Yokohama; and if Mr. Fogg was twenty-four

[014-141] hours late on reaching Yokohama, this time would no doubt be easily

[014-142] regained in the voyage of twenty-two days across the Pacific. He found

[014-143] himself, then, about twenty-four hours behind-hand, thirty-five days

[014-144] after leaving [[london|London]].

[014-145] The “Carnatic” was announced to leave Hong Kong at five the next

[014-146] morning. Mr. Fogg had sixteen hours in which to attend to his business

[014-147] there, which was to deposit Aouda safely with her wealthy relative.

[014-148] On landing, he conducted her to a palanquin, in which they repaired to

[014-149] [[reform-club|the Club]] Hotel. A room was engaged for the young woman, and Mr. Fogg,

[014-150] after seeing that she wanted for nothing, set out in search of her

[014-151] cousin Jeejeeh. He instructed Passepartout to remain at the hotel until

[014-152] his return, that Aouda might not be left entirely alone.

[014-153] Mr. Fogg repaired to the Exchange, where, he did not doubt, every one

[014-154] would know so wealthy and considerable a personage as the Parsee

[014-155] merchant. Meeting a broker, he made the inquiry, to learn that Jeejeeh

[014-156] had left China two years before, and, retiring from business with an

[014-157] immense fortune, had taken up his residence in Europe—in Holland the

[014-158] broker thought, with the merchants of which country he had principally

[014-159] traded. Phileas Fogg returned to the hotel, begged a moment’s

[014-160] conversation with Aouda, and without more ado, apprised her that

[014-161] Jeejeeh was no longer at Hong Kong, but probably in Holland.

[014-162] Aouda at first said nothing. She passed her hand across her forehead,

[014-163] and reflected a few moments. Then, in her sweet, soft voice, she said:

[014-164] “What ought I to do, Mr. Fogg?”

[014-165] “It is very simple,” responded the gentleman. “Go on to Europe.”

[014-166] “But I cannot intrude—”

[014-167] “You do not intrude, nor do you in the least embarrass my project.

[014-168] Passepartout!”

[014-169] “Monsieur.”

[014-170] “Go to the ‘Carnatic,’ and engage three cabins.”

[014-171] Passepartout, delighted that the young woman, who was very gracious to

[014-172] him, was going to continue the journey with them, went off at a brisk

[014-173] gait to obey his master’s order.

[014-174] CHAPTER XIX.

[014-175] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT TAKES A TOO GREAT INTEREST IN HIS MASTER, AND

[014-176] WHAT COMES OF IT

[014-177] Hong Kong is an island which came into the possession of the English by

[014-178] the Treaty of Nankin, after the war of 1842; and the colonising genius

[014-179] of the English has created upon it an important city and an excellent

[014-180] port. The island is situated at the mouth of the Canton River, and is

[014-181] separated by about sixty miles from the Portuguese town of Macao, on

[014-182] the opposite coast. Hong Kong has beaten Macao in the struggle for the

[014-183] Chinese trade, and now the greater part of the transportation of

[014-184] Chinese goods finds its depot at the former place. Docks, hospitals,

[014-185] wharves, a Gothic cathedral, a government house, macadamised streets,

[014-186] give to Hong Kong the appearance of a town in Kent or Surrey

[014-187] transferred by some strange magic to the antipodes.

[014-188] Passepartout wandered, with his hands in his pockets, towards the

[014-189] Victoria port, gazing as he went at the curious palanquins and other

[014-190] modes of conveyance, and the groups of Chinese, Japanese, and Europeans

[014-191] who passed to and fro in the streets. Hong Kong seemed to him not

[014-192] unlike [[bombay|Bombay]], [[calcutta|Calcutta]], and Singapore, since, like them, it betrayed

[014-193] everywhere the evidence of English supremacy. At the Victoria port he

[014-194] found a confused mass of ships of all nations: English, French,

[014-195] American, and Dutch, men-of-war and trading vessels, Japanese and

[014-196] Chinese junks, sempas, tankas, and flower-boats, which formed so many

[014-197] floating parterres. Passepartout noticed in the crowd a number of the

[014-198] natives who seemed very old and were dressed in yellow. On going into a

[014-199] barber’s to get shaved he learned that these ancient men were all at

[014-200] least eighty years old, at which age they are permitted to wear yellow,

[014-201] which is the Imperial colour. Passepartout, without exactly knowing

[014-202] why, thought this very funny.
