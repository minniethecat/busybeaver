# The Rescue of Aouda

When Phileas Fogg diverts his journey to rescue a young Parsi woman from ritual immolation in India, he performs the single act that transforms the novel's meaning — from a wager about speed into a testament about human obligation.

## Definition

The rescue of Aouda refers to the episode in which Fogg and Passepartout, traveling through the Indian jungle, encounter a procession preparing to perform suttee — the ritual burning of a widow on her husband's funeral pyre. Although the practice had been outlawed by the British administration, it persisted in remote regions. The young woman had been drugged with opium and hemp by the priests of Pillaji to ensure her compliance. (PN: 011-010)

Fogg decides to intervene despite the risk to his timetable, declaring that the delay "caused by the rescue" of Aouda was an acceptable sacrifice, as he had "twelve hours to spare." (PN: 012-052, 012-056)

The rescue succeeds through Passepartout's extraordinary daring. Under cover of darkness and the smoke of the pyre, he slips onto the funeral platform, disguises himself in the rajah's garments, and rises from the flames like a spectre — terrifying the gathered crowd and allowing Aouda to be spirited away by Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty. (PN: 010-120, 010-121, 010-133) The act is both a thrilling adventure and a profound moral statement. Aouda afterward "pathetically thanked her deliverers, rather with tears than words." (PN: 011-072)

## Role in the Novel

The rescue marks the novel's moral turning point. Before this episode, Fogg's journey is purely mechanical — a gentleman honoring a wager. After rescuing Aouda, the journey acquires a human dimension. Fogg has taken responsibility for another person's fate, and this responsibility complicates every subsequent decision. Fogg offers to escort Aouda to Hong Kong, where she has a wealthy Parsi relative. (PN: 011-182)

Aouda herself becomes the novel's emotional center. Her gratitude evolves into love, and her presence transforms Fogg from a "machine" into a man capable of feeling. The rescue that costs Fogg twelve hours ultimately saves his wager — through the International Date Line windfall — and earns him a wife. Fogg's own retrospective assessment is conclusive: "If I had not crossed India, I should not have saved Aouda; she would not have been my wife." (PN: 031-189)

## Historical and Cultural Context

Suttee (sati) was a funeral practice in some Hindu communities where a widow would immolate herself on her husband's pyre. The British East India Company banned the practice in 1829 under Governor-General Lord William Bentinck, following decades of campaigning by Indian reformers like Ram Mohan Roy. However, enforcement was uneven, and isolated instances continued in remote areas — especially where, as in the novel, the widow was drugged rather than a willing participant.

Verne's inclusion of suttee reflects both Victorian horror at the practice and the imperial justification that British rule was morally necessary to suppress "barbaric" customs. The rescue narrative thus operates on multiple levels: as adventure, as moral drama, and as colonial discourse about civilization versus savagery.

A crucial detail is that Aouda is Parsi, not Hindu — the daughter of a Bombay merchant married into a princely family. The suttee is thus doubly transgressive: the practice belonged to Hindu tradition, not the Parsi community from which Aouda came. Her victimization is the result of both gender oppression and cultural exploitation, making Fogg's intervention all the more necessary.
