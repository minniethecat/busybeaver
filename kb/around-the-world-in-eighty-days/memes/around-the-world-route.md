# The Route

The eighty-day itinerary traced by [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] represents a snapshot of global connectivity in 1872 — a route made possible by the [[the-suez-canal|Suez Canal]], the Indian [[railway-travel|railways]], and the American transcontinental railroad, each a recent triumph of nineteenth-century engineering. The [[around-the-world-route|route]] is not merely geography: it is a clock, a bet, and the skeleton around which the entire novel is constructed. (004-117)

## Definition

The route is the geographical path of [[phileas-fogg|Fogg's]] journey: [[london|London]] → Paris → Turin → Brindisi (rail), Brindisi → [[suez|Suez]] → [[bombay|Bombay]] (steamer via the [[the-suez-canal|Suez Canal]]), Bombay → [[calcutta|Calcutta]] (rail, with an elephant detour), Calcutta → [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] → [[yokohama|Yokohama]] → [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] (steamer across the Pacific), San Francisco → [[new-york|New York]] (rail across America), and New York → [[liverpool|Liverpool]] → [[london|London]] (steamer across the Atlantic). (004-117)

The route exploits the prevailing infrastructure of empire and commerce. Nearly every segment follows routes established for imperial administration or trade: the [[p-and-o-company|P&O]] steamer lines, the Great Indian Peninsula Railway, the Pacific Mail Steamship Company, and the transatlantic packet service. (008-197)

> "The nearest route thither, now that a railway connected Bombay with Calcutta, lay across the Indian peninsula." (006-011)

## Role in the Novel

The route is more than geography — it is a clock. Each segment has a scheduled duration, and each deviation threatens [[the-wager|the wager]]. The novel's structure mirrors the route: chapters correspond to geographical segments, and the reader, like Fogg, is always aware of time gained or lost. (004-117)

The route also exposes Fogg to the world beyond England. It takes him through the recently-opened [[the-suez-canal|Suez Canal]] (completed 1869), across an India still recovering from the 1857 rebellion, through colonial [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], to a [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] energized by the Gold Rush, and back through the industrial heartland of America. Each stop challenges Fogg's assumptions about the predictability of the world. (006-011)

## Historical & Cultural Context

The route was a real possibility in 1872, not a fantasy. Thomas Cook had begun offering around-the-world tours in 1872, and the _Bradshaw's Continental Railway Guide_ provided the precise timetables Fogg consults. The [[the-suez-canal|Suez Canal]] (1869), the American transcontinental railroad (1869), and the Indian railway network (substantially complete by 1870) had just closed the last gaps in global circumnavigation. (006-011)

Verne was a voracious consumer of geographical literature and travel journalism. The route in the novel is so carefully researched that contemporary readers could, in principle, have followed it themselves — a fact that contributed enormously to the novel's popularity and plausibility. (004-117)
