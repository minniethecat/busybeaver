[029-001] On this day [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]] came on deck, went up to [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]], and began

[029-002] to speak earnestly with him. Without knowing why it was a presentiment,

[029-003] perhaps [[passepartout|Passepartout]] became vaguely uneasy. He would have given one of

[029-004] his ears to hear with the other what the engineer was saying. He

[029-005] finally managed to catch a few words, and was sure he heard his master

[029-006] say, “You are certain of what you tell me?”

[029-007] “Certain, sir,” replied the engineer. “You must remember that, since we

[029-008] started, we have kept up hot fires in all our furnaces, and, though we

[029-009] had coal enough to go on short steam from [[new-york|New York]] to Bordeaux, we

[029-010] haven’t enough to go with all steam from New York to [[liverpool|Liverpool]].” “I

[029-011] will consider,” replied Mr. Fogg.

[029-012] Passepartout understood it all; he was seized with mortal anxiety. The

[029-013] coal was giving out! “Ah, if my master can get over that,” muttered he,

[029-014] “he’ll be a famous man!” He could not help imparting to [[detective-fix|Fix]] what he had

[029-015] overheard.

[029-016] “Then you believe that we really are going to Liverpool?”

[029-017] “Of course.”

[029-018] “Ass!” replied the detective, shrugging his shoulders and turning on

[029-019] his heel.

[029-020] Passepartout was on the point of vigorously resenting the epithet, the

[029-021] reason of which he could not for the life of him comprehend; but he

[029-022] reflected that the unfortunate [[fix-detective|Fix]] was probably very much disappointed

[029-023] and humiliated in his self-esteem, after having so awkwardly followed a

[029-024] false scent around the world, and refrained.

[029-025] And now what course would Phileas Fogg adopt? It was difficult to

[029-026] imagine. Nevertheless he seemed to have decided upon one, for that

[029-027] evening he sent for the engineer, and said to him, “Feed all the fires

[029-028] until the coal is exhausted.”

[029-029] A few moments after, the funnel of the “Henrietta” vomited forth

[029-030] torrents of smoke. The vessel continued to proceed with all steam on;

[029-031] but on the 18th, the engineer, as he had predicted, announced that the

[029-032] coal would give out in the course of the day.

[029-033] “Do not let the fires go down,” replied Mr. Fogg. “Keep them up to the

[029-034] last. Let the valves be filled.”

[029-035] Towards noon Phileas Fogg, having ascertained their position, called

[029-036] Passepartout, and ordered him to go for [[captain-speedy|Captain Speedy]]. It was as if

[029-037] the honest fellow had been commanded to unchain a tiger. He went to the

[029-038] poop, saying to himself, “He will be like a madman!”

[029-039] In a few moments, with cries and oaths, a bomb appeared on the

[029-040] poop-deck. The bomb was Captain Speedy. It was clear that he was on the

[029-041] point of bursting. “Where are we?” were the first words his anger

[029-042] permitted him to utter. Had the poor man been an apoplectic, he could

[029-043] never have recovered from his paroxysm of wrath.

[029-044] “Where are we?” he repeated, with purple face.

[029-045] “Seven hundred and seven miles from Liverpool,” replied Mr. Fogg, with

[029-046] imperturbable calmness.

[029-047] “Pirate!” cried Captain Speedy.

[029-048] “I have sent for you, sir—”

[029-049] “Pickaroon!”

[029-050] “—sir,” continued Mr. Fogg, “to ask you to sell me your vessel.”

[029-051] “No! By all the devils, no!”

[029-052] “But I shall be obliged to burn her.”

[029-053] “Burn the ‘Henrietta’!”

[029-054] “Yes; at least the upper part of her. The coal has given out.”

[029-055] “Burn my vessel!” cried Captain Speedy, who could scarcely pronounce

[029-056] the words. “A vessel worth fifty thousand dollars!”

[029-057] “Here are sixty thousand,” replied Phileas Fogg, handing the captain a

[029-058] roll of bank-bills. This had a prodigious effect on Andrew Speedy. An

[029-059] American can scarcely remain unmoved at the sight of sixty thousand

[029-060] dollars. The captain forgot in an instant his anger, his imprisonment,

[029-061] and all his grudges against his passenger. The “Henrietta” was twenty

[029-062] years old; it was a great bargain. The bomb would not go off after all.

[029-063] Mr. Fogg had taken away the match.

[029-064] “And I shall still have the iron hull,” said the captain in a softer

[029-065] tone.

[029-066] “The iron hull and the engine. Is it agreed?”

[029-067] “Agreed.”

[029-068] And Andrew Speedy, seizing the banknotes, counted them and consigned

[029-069] them to his pocket.

[029-070] During this colloquy, Passepartout was as white as a sheet, and Fix

[029-071] seemed on the point of having an apoplectic fit. Nearly twenty thousand

[029-072] pounds had been expended, and Fogg left the hull and engine to the

[029-073] captain, that is, near the whole value of the craft! It was true,

[029-074] however, that fifty-five thousand pounds had been stolen from the Bank.

[029-075] When Andrew Speedy had pocketed the money, Mr. Fogg said to him, “Don’t

[029-076] let this astonish you, sir. You must know that I shall lose twenty

[029-077] thousand pounds, unless I arrive in [[london|London]] by a quarter before nine on

[029-078] the evening of the 21st of December. I missed the [[steamer-travel|steamer]] at New York,

[029-079] and as you refused to take me to Liverpool—”

[029-080] “And I did well!” cried Andrew Speedy; “for I have gained at least

[029-081] forty thousand dollars by it!” He added, more sedately, “Do you know

[029-082] one thing, Captain—”

[029-083] “Fogg.”

[029-084] “Captain Fogg, you’ve got something of the Yankee about you.”

[029-085] And, having paid his passenger what he considered a high compliment, he

[029-086] was going away, when Mr. Fogg said, “The vessel now belongs to me?”

[029-087] “Certainly, from the keel to the truck of the masts—all the wood, that

[029-088] is.”

[029-089] “Very well. Have the interior seats, bunks, and frames pulled down, and

[029-090] burn them.”

[029-091] It was necessary to have dry wood to keep the steam up to the adequate

[029-092] pressure, and on that day the poop, cabins, bunks, and the spare deck

[029-093] were sacrificed. On the next day, the 19th of December, the masts,

[029-094] rafts, and spars were burned; the crew worked lustily, keeping up the

[029-095] fires. Passepartout hewed, cut, and sawed away with all his might.

[029-096] There was a perfect rage for demolition.

[029-097] The railings, fittings, the greater part of the deck, and top sides

[029-098] disappeared on the 20th, and the “Henrietta” was now only a flat hulk.

[029-099] But on this day they sighted the Irish coast and Fastnet Light. By ten

[029-100] in the evening they were passing Queenstown. Phileas Fogg had only

[029-101] twenty-four hours more in which to get to London; that length of time

[029-102] was necessary to reach Liverpool, with all steam on. And the steam was

[029-103] about to give out altogether!

[029-104] “Sir,” said Captain Speedy, who was now deeply interested in Mr. Fogg’s

[029-105] project, “I really commiserate you. Everything is against you. We are

[029-106] only opposite Queenstown.”

[029-107] “Ah,” said Mr. Fogg, “is that place where we see the lights

[029-108] Queenstown?”

[029-109] “Yes.”

[029-110] “Can we enter the harbour?”

[029-111] “Not under three hours. Only at high tide.”

[029-112] “Stay,” replied Mr. Fogg calmly, without betraying in his features that

[029-113] by a supreme inspiration he was about to attempt once more to conquer

[029-114] ill-fortune.

[029-115] Queenstown is the Irish port at which the transatlantic steamers stop

[029-116] to put off the mails. These mails are carried to Dublin by express

[029-117] trains always held in readiness to start; from Dublin they are sent on

[029-118] to Liverpool by the most rapid boats, and thus gain twelve hours on the

[029-119] Atlantic steamers.

[029-120] Phileas Fogg counted on gaining twelve hours in the same way. Instead

[029-121] of arriving at Liverpool the next evening by the “Henrietta,” he would

[029-122] be there by noon, and would therefore have time to reach London before

[029-123] a quarter before nine in the evening.

[029-124] The “Henrietta” entered Queenstown Harbour at one o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] in the

[029-125] morning, it then being high tide; and Phileas Fogg, after being grasped

[029-126] heartily by the hand by Captain Speedy, left that [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] on the

[029-127] levelled hulk of his craft, which was still worth half what he had sold

[029-128] it for.

[029-129] The party went on shore at once. Fix was greatly tempted to arrest Mr.

[029-130] Fogg on the spot; but he did not. Why? What struggle was going on

[029-131] within him? Had he changed his mind about “his man”? Did he understand

[029-132] that he had made a grave mistake? He did not, however, abandon Mr.

[029-133] Fogg. They all got upon the [[railway-travel|train]], which was just ready to start, at

[029-134] half-past one; at dawn of day they were in Dublin; and they lost no

[029-135] time in embarking on a steamer which, disdaining to rise upon the

[029-136] waves, invariably cut through them.

[029-137] Phileas Fogg at last disembarked on the Liverpool quay, at twenty

[029-138] minutes before twelve, 21st December. He was only six hours distant

[029-139] from London.

[029-140] But at this moment Fix came up, put his hand upon Mr. Fogg’s shoulder,

[029-141] and, showing his warrant, said, “You are really Phileas Fogg?”

[029-142] “I am.”

[029-143] “I arrest you in the Queen’s name!”

[029-144] CHAPTER XXXIV.

[029-145] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG AT LAST REACHES LONDON

[029-146] Phileas Fogg was in prison. He had been shut up in the Custom House,

[029-147] and he was to be transferred to London the next day.

[029-148] Passepartout, when he saw his master arrested, would have fallen upon

[029-149] Fix had he not been held back by some policemen. [[aouda|Aouda]] was

[029-150] thunderstruck at the suddenness of an event which she could not

[029-151] understand. Passepartout explained to her how it was that the honest

[029-152] and courageous Fogg was arrested as a robber. The young woman’s heart

[029-153] revolted against so heinous a charge, and when she saw that she could

[029-154] attempt to do nothing to save her protector, she wept bitterly.

[029-155] As for Fix, he had arrested Mr. Fogg because it was his duty, whether

[029-156] Mr. Fogg were guilty or not.

[029-157] The thought then struck Passepartout, that he was the cause of this new

[029-158] misfortune! Had he not concealed Fix’s errand from his master? When Fix

[029-159] revealed his true character and purpose, why had he not told Mr. Fogg?

[029-160] If the latter had been warned, he would no doubt have given Fix proof

[029-161] of his innocence, and satisfied him of his mistake; at least, Fix would

[029-162] not have continued his journey at the expense and on the heels of his

[029-163] master, only to arrest him the moment he set foot on English soil.

[029-164] Passepartout wept till he was blind, and felt like blowing his brains

[029-165] out.

[029-166] Aouda and he had remained, despite the cold, under the portico of the

[029-167] Custom House. Neither wished to leave the place; both were anxious to

[029-168] see Mr. Fogg again.

[029-169] That gentleman was really ruined, and that at the moment when he was

[029-170] about to attain his end. This arrest was fatal. Having arrived at

[029-171] Liverpool at twenty minutes before twelve on the 21st of December, he

[029-172] had till a quarter before nine that evening to reach the [[reform-club|Reform Club]],

[029-173] that is, nine hours and a quarter; the journey from Liverpool to London

[029-174] was six hours.

[029-175] If anyone, at this moment, had entered the Custom House, he would have

[029-176] found Mr. Fogg seated, motionless, calm, and without apparent anger,

[029-177] upon a wooden bench. He was not, it is true, resigned; but this last

[029-178] blow failed to force him into an outward betrayal of any emotion. Was

[029-179] he being devoured by one of those secret rages, all the more terrible

[029-180] because contained, and which only burst forth, with an irresistible

[029-181] force, at the last moment? No one could tell. There he sat, calmly

[029-182] waiting—for what? Did he still cherish hope? Did he still believe, now

[029-183] that the door of this prison was closed upon him, that he would

[029-184] succeed?

[029-185] However that may have been, Mr. Fogg carefully put his watch upon the

[029-186] table, and observed its advancing hands. Not a word escaped his lips,

[029-187] but his look was singularly set and stern. The situation, in any event,

[029-188] was a terrible one, and might be thus stated: if Phileas Fogg was

[029-189] honest he was ruined; if he was a knave, he was caught.

[029-190] Did escape occur to him? Did he examine to see if there were any

[029-191] practicable outlet from his prison? Did he think of escaping from it?

[029-192] Possibly; for once he walked slowly around the room. But the door was

[029-193] locked, and the window heavily barred with iron rods. He sat down

[029-194] again, and drew his journal from his pocket. On the line where these

[029-195] words were written, “21st December, Saturday, Liverpool,” he added,

[029-196] “80th day, 11.40 a.m.,” and waited.
