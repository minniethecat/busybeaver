# Spirit of Adventure

While Phileas Fogg circles the globe out of obligation to a wager, his servant Passepartout embarks on the same journey out of a restless hunger for novelty — embodying the Victorian era's ambivalent relationship with adventure: half romantic ideal, half dangerous frivolity.

## Definition

The spirit of adventure in this novel is represented primarily through Passepartout, who has spent his life seeking "repose" after a career of restless movement — acrobat, fireman, soldier, valet. He takes the position with Fogg precisely because he wants a settled life, yet the wager plunges him back into the very uncertainty he had hoped to escape. (PN: 002-056)

The novel distinguishes between two kinds of adventure: Fogg's methodical journey (adventure as problem-solving) and Passepartout's openness to the unexpected (adventure as experience). Both are validated, but the novel's sympathies subtly favor Passepartout's approach — he sees India, rescues Aouda, and experiences the journey as life, not merely as a problem to be solved.

## Role in the Novel

Adventure serves as the novel's emotional engine. The plot may be driven by Fogg's timetable, but the reader's engagement depends on the unpredictable incidents that timetable cannot account for: the elephant purchase, the opium-den rescue, the Sioux attack, the burning steamer. Each is a miniature adventure within the larger framework.

The spirit of adventure also provides the moral education of the novel. Fogg begins as a man who treats the world as a mathematical equation; through the adventures forced upon him, he learns that the world cannot be reduced to a timetable. The novel thus argues, implicitly, that adventure — openness to the unexpected — is essential to being fully human.

## Historical & Cultural Context

The Victorian era was the golden age of adventure literature. H. Rider Haggard's _King Solomon's Mines_, Robert Louis Stevenson's _Treasure Island_, and Rudyard Kipling's Indian stories all appeared within two decades of _Around the World in Eighty Days_. Adventure was both entertainment and ideology — stories of European travelers overcoming exotic dangers reinforced imperial self-confidence.

Verne occupies an interesting position within this tradition. His adventures are technological rather than martial — Fogg's weapons are timetables and banknotes, not rifles. The novel's adventures arise from the friction between mechanical precision and organic reality, making it a uniquely modern form of adventure narrative.
