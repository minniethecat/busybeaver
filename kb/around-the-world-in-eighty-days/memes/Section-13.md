[013-001] [[detective-fix|Fix]] thought over these probabilities during the long hours which he

[013-002] spent in his cabin, and kept repeating to himself, “Now, either the

[013-003] warrant will be at [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], in which case I shall arrest my man, or

[013-004] it will not be there; and this time it is absolutely necessary that I

[013-005] should delay his departure. I have failed at [[bombay|Bombay]], and I have failed

[013-006] at [[calcutta|Calcutta]]; if I fail at Hong Kong, my reputation is lost: Cost what

[013-007] it may, I _must_ succeed! But how shall I prevent his departure, if

[013-008] that should turn out to be my last resource?”

[013-009] [[fix-detective|Fix]] made up his mind that, if worst came to worst, he would make a

[013-010] confidant of [[passepartout|Passepartout]], and tell him what kind of a fellow his

[013-011] master really was. That Passepartout was not [[phileas-fogg|Fogg]]’s accomplice, he was

[013-012] very certain. The servant, enlightened by his disclosure, and afraid of

[013-013] being himself implicated in the crime, would doubtless become an ally

[013-014] of the detective. But this method was a dangerous one, only to be

[013-015] employed when everything else had failed. A word from Passepartout to

[013-016] his master would ruin all. The detective was therefore in a sore

[013-017] strait. But suddenly a new idea struck him. The presence of [[aouda|Aouda]] on

[013-018] the “Rangoon,” in company with Phileas Fogg, gave him new material for

[013-019] reflection.

[013-020] Who was this woman? What combination of events had made her Fogg’s

[013-021] travelling companion? They had evidently met somewhere between Bombay

[013-022] and Calcutta; but where? Had they met accidentally, or had Fogg gone

[013-023] into the interior purposely in quest of this charming damsel? Fix was

[013-024] fairly puzzled. He asked himself whether there had not been a wicked

[013-025] elopement; and this idea so impressed itself upon his mind that he

[013-026] determined to make use of the supposed intrigue. Whether the young

[013-027] woman were married or not, he would be able to create such difficulties

[013-028] for Mr. Fogg at Hong Kong that he could not escape by paying any amount

[013-029] of money.

[013-030] But could he even wait till they reached Hong Kong? Fogg had an

[013-031] abominable way of jumping from one boat to another, and, before

[013-032] anything could be effected, might get full under way again for

[013-033] [[yokohama|Yokohama]].

[013-034] Fix decided that he must warn the English authorities, and signal the

[013-035] “Rangoon” before her arrival. This was easy to do, since the [[steamer-travel|steamer]]

[013-036] stopped at Singapore, whence there is a telegraphic wire to Hong Kong.

[013-037] He finally resolved, moreover, before acting more positively, to

[013-038] question Passepartout. It would not be difficult to make him talk; and,

[013-039] as there was no time to lose, Fix prepared to make himself known.

[013-040] It was now the 30th of October, and on the following day the “Rangoon”

[013-041] was due at Singapore.

[013-042] Fix emerged from his cabin and went on deck. Passepartout was

[013-043] promenading up and down in the forward part of the steamer. The

[013-044] detective rushed forward with every appearance of extreme surprise, and

[013-045] exclaimed, “You here, on the ‘Rangoon’?”

[013-046] “What, Monsieur Fix, are you on board?” returned the really astonished

[013-047] Passepartout, recognising his crony of the “Mongolia.” “Why, I left you

[013-048] at Bombay, and here you are, on the way to Hong Kong! Are you going

[013-049] round the world too?”

[013-050] “No, no,” replied Fix; “I shall stop at Hong Kong—at least for some

[013-051] days.”

[013-052] “Hum!” said Passepartout, who seemed for an instant perplexed. “But how

[013-053] is it I have not seen you on board since we left Calcutta?”

[013-054] “Oh, a trifle of sea-sickness—I’ve been staying in my berth. The Gulf

[013-055] of Bengal does not agree with me as well as the Indian Ocean. And how

[013-056] is Mr. Fogg?”

[013-057] “As well and as punctual as ever, not a day behind time! But, Monsieur

[013-058] Fix, you don’t know that we have a young lady with us.”

[013-059] “A young lady?” replied the detective, not seeming to comprehend what

[013-060] was said.

[013-061] Passepartout thereupon recounted Aouda’s history, the affair at the

[013-062] Bombay pagoda, the purchase of the elephant for two thousand pounds,

[013-063] the [[the-rescue-of-aouda|rescue]], the arrest, and sentence of the Calcutta court, and the

[013-064] restoration of Mr. Fogg and himself to liberty on bail. Fix, who was

[013-065] familiar with the last events, seemed to be equally ignorant of all

[013-066] that Passepartout related; and the later was charmed to find so

[013-067] interested a listener.

[013-068] “But does your master propose to carry this young woman to Europe?”

[013-069] “Not at all. We are simply going to place her under the protection of

[013-070] one of her relatives, a rich merchant at Hong Kong.”

[013-071] “Nothing to be done there,” said Fix to himself, concealing his

[013-072] disappointment. “A glass of gin, Mr. Passepartout?”

[013-073] “Willingly, Monsieur Fix. We must at least have a friendly glass on

[013-074] board the ‘Rangoon.’”

[013-075] CHAPTER XVII.

[013-076] SHOWING WHAT HAPPENED ON THE VOYAGE FROM SINGAPORE TO HONG KONG

[013-077] The detective and Passepartout met often on deck after this interview,

[013-078] though Fix was reserved, and did not attempt to induce his companion to

[013-079] divulge any more facts concerning Mr. Fogg. He caught a glimpse of that

[013-080] mysterious [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] once or twice; but Mr. Fogg usually confined

[013-081] himself to the cabin, where he kept Aouda company, or, according to his

[013-082] inveterate habit, took a hand at whist.

[013-083] Passepartout began very seriously to conjecture what strange chance

[013-084] kept Fix still on the [[around-the-world-route|route]] that his master was pursuing. It was really

[013-085] worth considering why this certainly very amiable and complacent

[013-086] person, whom he had first met at [[suez|Suez]], had then encountered on board

[013-087] the “Mongolia,” who disembarked at Bombay, which he announced as his

[013-088] destination, and now turned up so unexpectedly on the “Rangoon,” was

[013-089] following Mr. Fogg’s tracks step by step. What was Fix’s object?

[013-090] Passepartout was ready to [[the-wager|wager]] his Indian shoes—which he religiously

[013-091] preserved—that Fix would also leave Hong Kong at the same time with

[013-092] them, and probably on the same steamer.

[013-093] Passepartout might have cudgelled his brain for a century without

[013-094] hitting upon the real object which the detective had in view. He never

[013-095] could have imagined that Phileas Fogg was being tracked as a robber

[013-096] around the globe. But, as it is in human nature to attempt the solution

[013-097] of every mystery, Passepartout suddenly discovered an explanation of

[013-098] Fix’s movements, which was in truth far from unreasonable. Fix, he

[013-099] thought, could only be an agent of Mr. Fogg’s friends at the Reform

[013-100] Club, sent to follow him up, and to ascertain that he really went round

[013-101] the world as had been agreed upon.

[013-102] “It’s clear!” repeated the worthy servant to himself, proud of his

[013-103] shrewdness. “He’s a spy sent to keep us in view! That isn’t quite the

[013-104] thing, either, to be spying Mr. Fogg, who is so honourable a man! Ah,

[013-105] gentlemen of the Reform, this shall cost you dear!”

[013-106] Passepartout, enchanted with his discovery, resolved to say nothing to

[013-107] his master, lest he should be justly offended at this mistrust on the

[013-108] part of his adversaries. But he determined to chaff Fix, when he had

[013-109] the chance, with mysterious allusions, which, however, need not betray

[013-110] his real suspicions.

[013-111] During the afternoon of Wednesday, 30th October, the “Rangoon” entered

[013-112] the Strait of Malacca, which separates the peninsula of that name from

[013-113] Sumatra. The mountainous and craggy islets intercepted the beauties of

[013-114] this noble island from the view of the travellers. The “Rangoon”

[013-115] weighed anchor at Singapore the next day at four a.m., to receive coal,

[013-116] having gained half a day on the prescribed time of her arrival. Phileas

[013-117] Fogg noted this gain in his journal, and then, accompanied by Aouda,

[013-118] who betrayed a desire for a walk on shore, disembarked.

[013-119] Fix, who suspected Mr. Fogg’s every movement, followed them cautiously,

[013-120] without being himself perceived; while Passepartout, laughing in his

[013-121] sleeve at Fix’s manœuvres, went about his usual errands.

[013-122] The island of Singapore is not imposing in aspect, for there are no

[013-123] mountains; yet its appearance is not without attractions. It is a park

[013-124] checkered by pleasant highways and avenues. A handsome carriage, drawn

[013-125] by a sleek pair of New Holland horses, carried Phileas Fogg and Aouda

[013-126] into the midst of rows of palms with brilliant foliage, and of

[013-127] clove-trees, whereof the cloves form the heart of a half-open flower.

[013-128] Pepper plants replaced the prickly hedges of European fields;

[013-129] sago-bushes, large ferns with gorgeous branches, varied the aspect of

[013-130] this tropical clime; while nutmeg-trees in full foliage filled the air

[013-131] with a penetrating perfume. Agile and grinning bands of monkeys skipped

[013-132] about in the trees, nor were tigers wanting in the jungles.

[013-133] After a drive of two hours through the country, Aouda and Mr. Fogg

[013-134] returned to the town, which is a vast collection of heavy-looking,

[013-135] irregular houses, surrounded by charming gardens rich in tropical

[013-136] fruits and plants; and at ten o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] they re-embarked, closely

[013-137] followed by the detective, who had kept them constantly in sight.

[013-138] Passepartout, who had been purchasing several dozen mangoes—a fruit as

[013-139] large as good-sized apples, of a dark-brown colour outside and a bright

[013-140] red within, and whose white pulp, melting in the mouth, affords

[013-141] gourmands a delicious sensation—was waiting for them on deck. He was

[013-142] only too glad to offer some mangoes to Aouda, who thanked him very

[013-143] gracefully for them.

[013-144] At eleven o’clock the “Rangoon” rode out of Singapore harbour, and in a

[013-145] few hours the high mountains of Malacca, with their forests, inhabited

[013-146] by the most beautifully-furred tigers in the world, were lost to view.

[013-147] Singapore is distant some thirteen hundred miles from the island of

[013-148] Hong Kong, which is a little English colony near the Chinese coast.

[013-149] Phileas Fogg hoped to accomplish the journey in six days, so as to be

[013-150] in time for the steamer which would leave on the 6th of November for

[013-151] Yokohama, the principal Japanese port.

[013-152] The “Rangoon” had a large quota of passengers, many of whom disembarked

[013-153] at Singapore, among them a number of Indians, Ceylonese, Chinamen,

[013-154] Malays, and Portuguese, mostly second-class travellers.

[013-155] The weather, which had hitherto been fine, changed with the last

[013-156] quarter of the moon. The sea rolled heavily, and the wind at intervals

[013-157] rose almost to a storm, but happily blew from the south-west, and thus

[013-158] aided the steamer’s progress. The captain as often as possible put up

[013-159] his sails, and under the double action of steam and sail the vessel

[013-160] made rapid progress along the coasts of Anam and Cochin China. Owing to

[013-161] the defective construction of the “Rangoon,” however, unusual

[013-162] precautions became necessary in unfavourable weather; but the loss of

[013-163] time which resulted from this cause, while it nearly drove Passepartout

[013-164] out of his senses, did not seem to affect his master in the least.

[013-165] Passepartout blamed [[captain-speedy|the captain]], [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]], and the crew, and

[013-166] consigned all who were connected with the ship to the land where the

[013-167] pepper grows. Perhaps the thought of the gas, which was remorselessly

[013-168] burning at his expense in Saville Row, had something to do with his hot

[013-169] impatience.

[013-170] “You are in a great hurry, then,” said Fix to him one day, “to reach

[013-171] Hong Kong?”

[013-172] “A very great hurry!”

[013-173] “Mr. Fogg, I suppose, is anxious to catch the steamer for Yokohama?”

[013-174] “Terribly anxious.”

[013-175] “You believe in this journey around the world, then?”

[013-176] “Absolutely. Don’t you, Mr. Fix?”

[013-177] “I? I don’t believe a word of it.”

[013-178] “You’re a sly dog!” said Passepartout, winking at him.

[013-179] This expression rather disturbed Fix, without his knowing why. Had the

[013-180] Frenchman guessed his real purpose? He knew not what to think. But how

[013-181] could Passepartout have discovered that he was a detective? Yet, in

[013-182] speaking as he did, the man evidently meant more than he expressed.

[013-183] Passepartout went still further the next day; he could not hold his

[013-184] tongue.

[013-185] “Mr. Fix,” said he, in a bantering tone, “shall we be so unfortunate as

[013-186] to lose you when we get to Hong Kong?”

[013-187] “Why,” responded Fix, a little embarrassed, “I don’t know; perhaps—”

[013-188] “Ah, if you would only go on with us! An agent of the Peninsular

[013-189] Company, you know, can’t stop on the way! You were only going to

[013-190] Bombay, and here you are in China. America is not far off, and from

[013-191] America to Europe is only a step.”
