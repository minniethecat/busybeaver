# The Crossing of the Pacific

The segment of Phileas Fogg's eighty-day journey across the Pacific Ocean — from [[yokohama|Yokohama]] to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] — aboard the steamship General Grant. It is the longest maritime leg of the journey, spanning approximately four thousand five hundred miles of open ocean, yet it passes with remarkable calm — a quiet interlude between the chaos of Asia and the drama of the American crossing.

## Summary

After [[passepartout|Passepartout]] rejoins Fogg and [[aouda]] in Yokohama (following his separation from the party in Hong Kong and his adventures in a Japanese circus, covered at [[passepartout-in-yokohama]]), the reunited party boards the General Grant, an American steamer bound for San Francisco. (PN: 020-001–020-030)

The voyage itself is uneventful — a marked contrast to the tumultuous legs that precede and follow it. The General Grant crosses the Pacific in approximately twenty-one days, arriving at San Francisco on December 3, exactly on schedule. The passengers pass the time in the calm of the ship's saloon, with Fogg resuming his habit of playing whist. [[detective-fix|Detective Fix]], who has also boarded the General Grant, maintains his surveillance but makes no move to arrest Fogg on the open sea. (PN: 020-031–021-050)

## Thematic Significance

The Pacific crossing is the novel's breathing space — a long maritime interlude in which the narrative momentum pauses and the relationships among the characters deepen in the enforced proximity of a steamship. It is also a demonstration of the novel's structural rhythm: between every major dramatic episode (the Indian jungle, the American frontier) there is a passage of deliberate calm, mirroring the rhythm of travel itself.

The crossing also exemplifies the global maritime network that makes Fogg's journey possible. The General Grant is one link in a chain of steamers that connect every major port on the globe — a chain that, in 1872, was newly complete and still remarkable to Verne's readers.
