[022-001] The journey from [[new-york|New York]] to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] consumed, formerly, under

[022-002] the most favourable conditions, at least six months. It is now

[022-003] accomplished in seven days.

[022-004] It was in 1862 that, in spite of the Southern Members of Congress, who

[022-005] wished a more southerly [[around-the-world-route|route]], it was decided to lay the road between

[022-006] the forty-first and forty-second parallels. President Lincoln himself

[022-007] fixed the end of the line at Omaha, in Nebraska. The work was at once

[022-008] commenced, and pursued with true American energy; nor did the rapidity

[022-009] with which it went on injuriously affect its good execution. The road

[022-010] grew, on the prairies, a mile and a half a day. A [[railway-travel|locomotive]], running

[022-011] on the rails laid down the evening before, brought the rails to be laid

[022-012] on the morrow, and advanced upon them as fast as they were put in

[022-013] position.

[022-014] The [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]] Railroad is joined by several branches in Iowa, Kansas,

[022-015] Colorado, and Oregon. On leaving Omaha, it passes along the left bank

[022-016] of the Platte River as far as the junction of its northern branch,

[022-017] follows its southern branch, crosses the Laramie territory and the

[022-018] Wahsatch Mountains, turns the Great Salt Lake, and reaches Salt Lake

[022-019] City, the Mormon capital, plunges into the Tuilla Valley, across the

[022-020] American Desert, Cedar and Humboldt Mountains, the Sierra Nevada, and

[022-021] descends, _viâ_ Sacramento, to the Pacific—its grade, even on the Rocky

[022-022] Mountains, never exceeding one hundred and twelve feet to the mile.

[022-023] Such was the road to be traversed in seven days, which would enable

[022-024] [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]—at least, so he hoped—to take the Atlantic [[steamer-travel|steamer]] at New

[022-025] York on the 11th for [[liverpool|Liverpool]].

[022-026] The car which he occupied was a sort of long omnibus on eight wheels,

[022-027] and with no compartments in the interior. It was supplied with two rows

[022-028] of seats, perpendicular to the direction of the train on either side of

[022-029] an aisle which conducted to the front and rear platforms. These

[022-030] platforms were found throughout the train, and the passengers were able

[022-031] to pass from one end of the train to the other. It was supplied with

[022-032] saloon cars, balcony cars, restaurants, and smoking-cars; theatre cars

[022-033] alone were wanting, and they will have these some day.

[022-034] Book and news dealers, sellers of edibles, drinkables, and cigars, who

[022-035] seemed to have plenty of customers, were continually circulating in the

[022-036] aisles.

[022-037] The train left Oakland station at six o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]]. It was already night,

[022-038] cold and cheerless, the heavens being overcast with clouds which seemed

[022-039] to threaten snow. The train did not proceed rapidly; counting the

[022-040] stoppages, it did not run more than twenty miles an hour, which was a

[022-041] sufficient speed, however, to enable it to reach Omaha within its

[022-042] designated time.

[022-043] There was but little conversation in the car, and soon many of the

[022-044] passengers were overcome with sleep. [[passepartout|Passepartout]] found himself beside

[022-045] [[detective-fix|the detective]]; but he did not talk to him. After recent events, their

[022-046] relations with each other had grown somewhat cold; there could no

[022-047] longer be mutual sympathy or intimacy between them. [[fix-detective|Fix]]’s manner had

[022-048] not changed; but Passepartout was very reserved, and ready to strangle

[022-049] his former friend on the slightest provocation.

[022-050] Snow began to fall an hour after they started, a fine snow, however,

[022-051] which happily could not obstruct the train; nothing could be seen from

[022-052] the windows but a vast, white sheet, against which the smoke of the

[022-053] locomotive had a greyish aspect.

[022-054] At eight o’clock a steward entered the car and announced that the time

[022-055] for going to bed had arrived; and in a few minutes the car was

[022-056] transformed into a dormitory. The backs of the seats were thrown back,

[022-057] bedsteads carefully packed were rolled out by an ingenious system,

[022-058] berths were suddenly improvised, and each traveller had soon at his

[022-059] disposition a comfortable bed, protected from curious eyes by thick

[022-060] curtains. The sheets were clean and the pillows soft. It only remained

[022-061] to go to bed and sleep which everybody did—while the train sped on

[022-062] across the State of California.

[022-063] The country between San Francisco and Sacramento is not very hilly. The

[022-064] Central Pacific, taking Sacramento for its starting-point, extends

[022-065] eastward to meet the road from Omaha. The line from San Francisco to

[022-066] Sacramento runs in a north-easterly direction, along the American

[022-067] River, which empties into San Pablo Bay. The one hundred and twenty

[022-068] miles between these cities were accomplished in six hours, and towards

[022-069] midnight, while fast asleep, the travellers passed through Sacramento;

[022-070] so that they saw nothing of that important place, the seat of the State

[022-071] government, with its fine quays, its broad streets, its noble hotels,

[022-072] squares, and churches.

[022-073] The train, on leaving Sacramento, and passing the junction, Roclin,

[022-074] Auburn, and Colfax, entered the range of the Sierra Nevada. ’Cisco was

[022-075] reached at seven in the morning; and an hour later the dormitory was

[022-076] transformed into an ordinary car, and the travellers could observe the

[022-077] picturesque beauties of the mountain region through which they were

[022-078] steaming. The railway track wound in and out among the passes, now

[022-079] approaching the mountain-sides, now suspended over precipices, avoiding

[022-080] abrupt angles by bold curves, plunging into narrow defiles, which

[022-081] seemed to have no outlet. The locomotive, its great funnel emitting a

[022-082] weird light, with its sharp bell, and its cow-catcher extended like a

[022-083] spur, mingled its shrieks and bellowings with the noise of torrents and

[022-084] cascades, and twined its smoke among the branches of the gigantic

[022-085] pines.

[022-086] There were few or no bridges or tunnels on the route. The railway

[022-087] turned around the sides of the mountains, and did not attempt to

[022-088] violate nature by taking the shortest cut from one point to another.

[022-089] The train entered the State of Nevada through the Carson Valley about

[022-090] nine o’clock, going always northeasterly; and at midday reached Reno,

[022-091] where there was a delay of twenty minutes for breakfast.

[022-092] From this point the road, running along Humboldt River, passed

[022-093] northward for several miles by its banks; then it turned eastward, and

[022-094] kept by the river until it reached the Humboldt Range, nearly at the

[022-095] extreme eastern limit of Nevada.

[022-096] Having breakfasted, Mr. Fogg and his companions resumed their places in

[022-097] the car, and observed the varied landscape which unfolded itself as

[022-098] they passed along the vast prairies, the mountains lining the horizon,

[022-099] and the creeks, with their frothy, foaming streams. Sometimes a great

[022-100] herd of buffaloes, massing together in the distance, seemed like a

[022-101] moveable dam. These innumerable multitudes of ruminating beasts often

[022-102] form an insurmountable obstacle to the passage of the trains; thousands

[022-103] of them have been seen passing over the track for hours together, in

[022-104] compact ranks. The locomotive is then forced to stop and wait till the

[022-105] road is once more clear.

[022-106] This happened, indeed, to the train in which Mr. Fogg was travelling.

[022-107] [[About]] twelve o’clock a troop of ten or twelve thousand head of buffalo

[022-108] encumbered the track. The locomotive, slackening its speed, tried to

[022-109] clear the way with its cow-catcher; but the mass of animals was too

[022-110] great. The buffaloes marched along with a tranquil gait, uttering now

[022-111] and then deafening bellowings. There was no use of interrupting them,

[022-112] for, having taken a particular direction, nothing can moderate and

[022-113] change their course; it is a torrent of living flesh which no dam could

[022-114] contain.

[022-115] The travellers gazed on this curious spectacle from the platforms; but

[022-116] Phileas Fogg, who had the most reason of all to be in a hurry, remained

[022-117] in his seat, and waited philosophically until it should please the

[022-118] buffaloes to get out of the way.

[022-119] Passepartout was furious at the delay they occasioned, and longed to

[022-120] discharge his arsenal of revolvers upon them.

[022-121] “What a country!” cried he. “Mere cattle stop the trains, and go by in

[022-122] a procession, just as if they were not impeding travel! Parbleu! I

[022-123] should like to know if Mr. Fogg foresaw _this_ mishap in his programme!

[022-124] And here’s an engineer who doesn’t dare to run the locomotive into this

[022-125] herd of beasts!”

[022-126] The engineer did not try to overcome the obstacle, and he was wise. He

[022-127] would have crushed the first buffaloes, no doubt, with the cow-catcher;

[022-128] but the locomotive, however powerful, would soon have been checked, the

[022-129] train would inevitably have been thrown off the track, and would then

[022-130] have been helpless.

[022-131] The best course was to wait patiently, and regain the lost time by

[022-132] greater speed when the obstacle was removed. The procession of

[022-133] buffaloes lasted three full hours, and it was night before the track

[022-134] was clear. The last ranks of the herd were now passing over the rails,

[022-135] while the first had already disappeared below the southern horizon.

[022-136] It was eight o’clock when the train passed through the defiles of the

[022-137] Humboldt Range, and half-past nine when it penetrated Utah, the region

[022-138] of the Great Salt Lake, the singular colony of the Mormons.

[022-139] CHAPTER XXVII.

[022-140] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT UNDERGOES, AT A SPEED OF TWENTY MILES AN HOUR, A

[022-141] COURSE OF MORMON HISTORY

[022-142] During the night of the 5th of December, the train ran south-easterly

[022-143] for about fifty miles; then rose an equal distance in a north-easterly

[022-144] direction, towards the Great Salt Lake.

[022-145] Passepartout, about nine o’clock, went out upon the platform to take

[022-146] the air. The weather was cold, the heavens grey, but it was not

[022-147] snowing. The sun’s disc, enlarged by the mist, seemed an enormous ring

[022-148] of gold, and Passepartout was amusing himself by calculating its value

[022-149] in pounds sterling, when he was diverted from this interesting study by

[022-150] a strange-looking personage who made his appearance on the platform.

[022-151] This personage, who had taken the train at Elko, was tall and dark,

[022-152] with black moustache, black stockings, a black silk hat, a black

[022-153] waistcoat, black trousers, a white cravat, and dogskin gloves. He might

[022-154] have been taken for a clergyman. He went from one end of the train to

[022-155] the other, and affixed to the door of each car a notice written in

[022-156] manuscript.

[022-157] Passepartout approached and read one of these notices, which stated

[022-158] that [[elder-hitch|Elder William Hitch]], Mormon missionary, taking advantage of his

[022-159] presence on train No. 48, would deliver a lecture on Mormonism in car

[022-160] No. 117, from eleven to twelve o’clock; and that he invited all who

[022-161] were desirous of being instructed concerning the mysteries of the

[022-162] religion of the “Latter Day Saints” to attend.

[022-163] “I’ll go,” said Passepartout to himself. He knew nothing of Mormonism

[022-164] except the custom of polygamy, which is its foundation.

[022-165] The news quickly spread through the train, which contained about one

[022-166] hundred passengers, thirty of whom, at most, attracted by the notice,

[022-167] ensconced themselves in car No. 117. Passepartout took one of the front

[022-168] seats. Neither Mr. Fogg nor Fix cared to attend.

[022-169] At the appointed hour Elder William Hitch rose, and, in an irritated

[022-170] voice, as if he had already been contradicted, said, “I tell you that

[022-171] Joe Smith is a martyr, that his brother Hiram is a martyr, and that the

[022-172] persecutions of the United States Government against the prophets will

[022-173] also make a martyr of Brigham Young. Who dares to say the contrary?”

[022-174] No one ventured to gainsay [[reverend-wilson|the missionary]], whose excited tone

[022-175] contrasted curiously with his naturally calm visage. No doubt his anger

[022-176] arose from the hardships to which the Mormons were actually subjected.

[022-177] The government had just succeeded, with some difficulty, in reducing

[022-178] these independent fanatics to its rule. It had made itself master of

[022-179] Utah, and subjected that territory to the laws of the Union, after

[022-180] imprisoning Brigham Young on a charge of rebellion and polygamy. The

[022-181] disciples of the prophet had since redoubled their efforts, and

[022-182] resisted, by words at least, the authority of Congress. Elder Hitch, as

[022-183] is seen, was trying to make proselytes on the very railway trains.

[022-184] Then, emphasising his words with his loud voice and frequent gestures,

[022-185] he related the history of the Mormons from Biblical times: how that, in

[022-186] Israel, a Mormon prophet of the tribe of Joseph published the annals of

[022-187] the new religion, and bequeathed them to his son Mormon; how, many

[022-188] centuries later, a translation of this precious book, which was written

[022-189] in Egyptian, was made by Joseph Smith, junior, a Vermont farmer, who

[022-190] revealed himself as a mystical prophet in 1825; and how, in short, the

[022-191] celestial messenger appeared to him in an illuminated forest, and gave

[022-192] him the annals of the Lord.
