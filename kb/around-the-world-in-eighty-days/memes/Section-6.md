[006-001] CHAPTER IX.

[006-002] IN WHICH THE RED SEA AND THE INDIAN OCEAN PROVE PROPITIOUS TO THE

[006-003] DESIGNS OF PHILEAS FOGG

[006-004] The distance between [[suez|Suez]] and Aden is precisely thirteen hundred and

[006-005] ten miles, and the regulations of the company allow the steamers one

[006-006] hundred and thirty-eight hours in which to traverse it. The “Mongolia,”

[006-007] thanks to the vigorous exertions of [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]], seemed likely, so

[006-008] rapid was her speed, to reach her destination considerably within that

[006-009] time. The greater part of the passengers from [[brindisi|Brindisi]] were bound for

[006-010] India some for [[bombay|Bombay]], others for [[calcutta|Calcutta]] by way of Bombay, the

[006-011] nearest [[around-the-world-route|route]] thither, now that a [[railway-travel|railway]] crosses the Indian peninsula.

[006-012] Among the passengers was a number of officials and military officers of

[006-013] various grades, the latter being either attached to the regular British

[006-014] forces or commanding the Sepoy troops, and receiving high salaries ever

[006-015] since the central government has assumed the powers of the East India

[006-016] Company: for the sub-lieutenants get £280, brigadiers, £2,400, and

[006-017] generals of divisions, £4,000. What with the military men, a number of

[006-018] rich young Englishmen on their travels, and the hospitable efforts of

[006-019] the purser, the time passed quickly on the “Mongolia.” The best of fare

[006-020] was spread upon the cabin tables at breakfast, lunch, dinner, and the

[006-021] eight o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] supper, and the ladies scrupulously changed their toilets

[006-022] twice a day; and the hours were whirled away, when the sea was

[006-023] tranquil, with music, dancing, and games.

[006-024] But the Red Sea is full of caprice, and often boisterous, like most

[006-025] long and narrow gulfs. When the wind came from the African or Asian

[006-026] coast the “Mongolia,” with her long hull, rolled fearfully. Then the

[006-027] ladies speedily disappeared below; the pianos were silent; singing and

[006-028] dancing suddenly ceased. Yet the good ship ploughed straight on,

[006-029] unretarded by wind or wave, towards the straits of Bab-el-Mandeb. What

[006-030] was [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] doing all this time? It might be thought that, in his

[006-031] anxiety, he would be constantly watching the changes of the wind, the

[006-032] disorderly raging of the billows—every chance, in short, which might

[006-033] force the “Mongolia” to slacken her speed, and thus interrupt his

[006-034] journey. But, if he thought of these possibilities, he did not betray

[006-035] the fact by any outward sign.

[006-036] Always the same impassible member of the [[london|Reform Club]], whom no incident

[006-037] could surprise, as unvarying as the ship’s chronometers, and seldom

[006-038] having the curiosity even to go upon the deck, he passed through the

[006-039] memorable scenes of the Red Sea with cold indifference; did not care to

[006-040] recognise the historic towns and villages which, along its borders,

[006-041] raised their picturesque outlines against the sky; and betrayed no fear

[006-042] of the dangers of the Arabic Gulf, which the old historians always

[006-043] spoke of with horror, and upon which the ancient navigators never

[006-044] ventured without propitiating the gods by ample sacrifices. How did

[006-045] this eccentric personage pass his time on the “Mongolia”? He made his

[006-046] four hearty meals every day, regardless of the most persistent rolling

[006-047] and pitching on the part of the [[steamer-travel|steamer]]; and he played whist

[006-048] indefatigably, for he had found partners as enthusiastic in the game as

[006-049] himself. A tax-collector, on the way to his post at Goa; the Rev.

[006-050] Decimus Smith, returning to his parish at Bombay; and a

[006-051] brigadier-general of the English army, who was about to rejoin his

[006-052] brigade at Benares, made up the party, and, with Mr. Fogg, played whist

[006-053] by the hour together in absorbing silence.

[006-054] As for [[passepartout|Passepartout]], he, too, had escaped sea-sickness, and took his

[006-055] meals conscientiously in the forward cabin. He rather enjoyed the

[006-056] voyage, for he was well fed and well lodged, took a great interest in

[006-057] the scenes through which they were passing, and consoled himself with

[006-058] the delusion that his master’s whim would end at Bombay. He was

[006-059] pleased, on the day after leaving [[the-suez-canal|Suez]], to find on deck the obliging

[006-060] person with whom he had walked and chatted on the quays.

[006-061] “If I am not mistaken,” said he, approaching this person, with his most

[006-062] amiable smile, “you are the [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] who so kindly volunteered to

[006-063] guide me at Suez?”

[006-064] “Ah! I quite recognise you. You are the servant of the strange

[006-065] Englishman—”

[006-066] “Just so, monsieur—”

[006-067] “[[detective-fix|Fix]].”

[006-068] “Monsieur [[fix-detective|Fix]],” resumed Passepartout, “I’m charmed to find you on

[006-069] board. Where are you bound?”

[006-070] “Like you, to Bombay.”

[006-071] “That’s capital! Have you made this trip before?”

[006-072] “Several times. I am one of the agents of the Peninsular Company.”

[006-073] “Then you know India?”

[006-074] “Why yes,” replied Fix, who spoke cautiously.

[006-075] “A curious place, this India?”

[006-076] “Oh, very curious. Mosques, minarets, temples, fakirs, pagodas, tigers,

[006-077] snakes, elephants! I hope you will have ample time to see the sights.”

[006-078] “I hope so, Monsieur Fix. You see, a man of sound sense ought not to

[006-079] spend his life jumping from a steamer upon a railway train, and from a

[006-080] railway train upon a steamer again, pretending to make the tour of the

[006-081] world in eighty days! No; all these gymnastics, you may be sure, will

[006-082] cease at Bombay.”

[006-083] “And Mr. Fogg is getting on well?” asked Fix, in the most natural tone

[006-084] in the world.

[006-085] “Quite well, and I too. I eat like a famished ogre; it’s the sea air.”

[006-086] “But I never see your master on deck.”

[006-087] “Never; he hasn’t the least curiosity.”

[006-088] “Do you know, Mr. Passepartout, that this pretended tour in eighty days

[006-089] may conceal some secret errand—perhaps a diplomatic mission?”

[006-090] “Faith, Monsieur Fix, I assure you I know nothing about it, nor would I

[006-091] give half a crown to find out.”

[006-092] After this meeting, Passepartout and Fix got into the habit of chatting

[006-093] together, the latter making it a point to gain the worthy man’s

[006-094] confidence. He frequently offered him a glass of whiskey or pale ale in

[006-095] the steamer bar-room, which Passepartout never failed to accept with

[006-096] graceful alacrity, mentally pronouncing Fix the best of good fellows.

[006-097] Meanwhile the “Mongolia” was pushing forward rapidly; on the 13th,

[006-098] Mocha, surrounded by its ruined walls whereon date-trees were growing,

[006-099] was sighted, and on the mountains beyond were espied vast

[006-100] coffee-fields. Passepartout was ravished to behold this celebrated

[006-101] place, and thought that, with its circular walls and dismantled fort,

[006-102] it looked like an immense coffee-cup and saucer. The following night

[006-103] they passed through the Strait of Bab-el-Mandeb, which means in Arabic

[006-104] “The Bridge of Tears,” and the next day they put in at Steamer Point,

[006-105] north-west of Aden harbour, to take in coal. This matter of fuelling

[006-106] steamers is a serious one at such distances from the coal-mines; it

[006-107] costs the Peninsular Company some eight hundred thousand pounds a year.

[006-108] In these distant seas, coal is worth three or four pounds sterling a

[006-109] ton.

[006-110] The “Mongolia” had still sixteen hundred and fifty miles to traverse

[006-111] before reaching Bombay, and was obliged to remain four hours at Steamer

[006-112] Point to coal up. But this delay, as it was foreseen, did not affect

[006-113] Phileas Fogg’s programme; besides, the “Mongolia,” instead of reaching

[006-114] Aden on the morning of the 15th, when she was due, arrived there on the

[006-115] evening of the 14th, a gain of fifteen hours.

[006-116] Mr. Fogg and his servant went ashore at Aden to have the [[passport-and-identity|passport]] again

[006-117] _visaed;_ Fix, unobserved, followed them. The _visa_ procured, Mr. Fogg

[006-118] returned on board to resume his former habits; while Passepartout,

[006-119] according to custom, sauntered about among the mixed population of

[006-120] Somalis, Banyans, Parsees, Jews, Arabs, and Europeans who comprise the

[006-121] twenty-five thousand inhabitants of Aden. He gazed with wonder upon the

[006-122] fortifications which make this place the Gibraltar of the Indian Ocean,

[006-123] and the vast cisterns where the English engineers were still at work,

[006-124] two thousand years after the engineers of Solomon.

[006-125] “Very curious, _very_ curious,” said Passepartout to himself, on

[006-126] returning to the steamer. “I see that it is by no means useless to

[006-127] travel, if a man wants to see something new.” At six p.m. the

[006-128] “Mongolia” slowly moved out of the roadstead, and was soon once more on

[006-129] the Indian Ocean. She had a hundred and sixty-eight hours in which to

[006-130] reach Bombay, and the sea was favourable, the wind being in the

[006-131] north-west, and all sails aiding the engine. The steamer rolled but

[006-132] little, the ladies, in fresh toilets, reappeared on deck, and the

[006-133] singing and dancing were resumed. The trip was being accomplished most

[006-134] successfully, and Passepartout was enchanted with the congenial

[006-135] companion which chance had secured him in the person of the delightful

[006-136] Fix. On Sunday, October 20th, towards noon, they came in sight of the

[006-137] Indian coast: two hours later the pilot came on board. A range of hills

[006-138] lay against the sky in the horizon, and soon the rows of palms which

[006-139] adorn Bombay came distinctly into view. The steamer entered the road

[006-140] formed by the islands in the bay, and at half-past four she hauled up

[006-141] at the quays of Bombay.

[006-142] Phileas Fogg was in the act of finishing the thirty-third rubber of the

[006-143] voyage, and his partner and himself having, by a bold stroke, captured

[006-144] all thirteen of the tricks, concluded this fine campaign with a

[006-145] brilliant victory.

[006-146] The “Mongolia” was due at Bombay on the 22nd; she arrived on the 20th.

[006-147] This was a gain to Phileas Fogg of two days since his departure from

[006-148] London, and he calmly entered the fact in the itinerary, in the column

[006-149] of gains.

[006-150] CHAPTER X.

[006-151] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT IS ONLY TOO GLAD TO GET OFF WITH THE LOSS OF HIS

[006-152] SHOES

[006-153] Everybody knows that the great reversed triangle of land, with its base

[006-154] in the north and its apex in the south, which is called India, embraces

[006-155] fourteen hundred thousand square miles, upon which is spread unequally

[006-156] a population of one hundred and eighty millions of souls. The British

[006-157] Crown exercises a real and despotic dominion over the larger portion of

[006-158] this vast country, and has a governor-general stationed at Calcutta,

[006-159] governors at Madras, Bombay, and in Bengal, and a lieutenant-governor

[006-160] at Agra.

[006-161] But British India, properly so called, only embraces seven hundred

[006-162] thousand square miles, and a population of from one hundred to one

[006-163] hundred and ten millions of inhabitants. A considerable portion of

[006-164] India is still free from British authority; and there are certain

[006-165] ferocious rajahs in the interior who are absolutely independent. The

[006-166] celebrated East India Company was all-powerful from 1756, when the

[006-167] English first gained a foothold on the spot where now stands the city

[006-168] of Madras, down to the time of the great Sepoy insurrection. It

[006-169] gradually annexed province after province, purchasing them of the

[006-170] native chiefs, whom it seldom paid, and appointed the governor-general

[006-171] and his subordinates, civil and military. But the East India Company

[006-172] has now passed away, leaving the British possessions in India directly

[006-173] under the control of the Crown. The aspect of the country, as well as

[006-174] the manners and distinctions of race, is daily changing.

[006-175] Formerly one was obliged to travel in India by the old cumbrous methods

[006-176] of going on foot or on horseback, in palanquins or unwieldy coaches;

[006-177] now fast steamboats ply on the Indus and the Ganges, and a great

[006-178] railway, with branch lines joining the main line at many points on its

[006-179] route, traverses the peninsula from Bombay to Calcutta in three days.

[006-180] This railway does not run in a direct line across India. The distance

[006-181] between Bombay and Calcutta, as the bird flies, is only from one

[006-182] thousand to eleven hundred miles; but the deflections of the road

[006-183] increase this distance by more than a third.

[006-184] The general route of the [[great-indian-peninsula-railway|Great Indian Peninsula Railway]] is as follows:

[006-185] Leaving Bombay, it passes through Salcette, crossing to the continent

[006-186] opposite Tannah, goes over the chain of the Western Ghauts, runs thence

[006-187] north-east as far as Burhampoor, skirts the nearly independent

[006-188] territory of Bundelcund, ascends to [[allahabad|Allahabad]], turns thence eastwardly,

[006-189] meeting the Ganges at Benares, then departs from the river a little,

[006-190] and, descending south-eastward by Burdivan and the French town of

[006-191] Chandernagor, has its terminus at Calcutta.
