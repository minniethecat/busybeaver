# The Crossing of India

The segment of Phileas Fogg's journey across the Indian subcontinent — from [[bombay|Bombay]] to [[calcutta|Calcutta]] — which proves to be the most eventful and consequential leg of the entire eighty-day journey.

## Summary

Fogg and his party land at Bombay and board the Great Indian Peninsula Railway for Calcutta. However, the railway line is not yet complete: a gap of approximately fifty miles between Kholby and [[allahabad|Allahabad]] forces the travelers to find alternative transport. Fogg purchases an elephant, Kiouni, from a local mahout, and the party proceeds overland with [[the-parsee-guide|a Parsee guide]]. (PN: 010-001–011-050)

While crossing the jungles of Bundelkhand, the party encounters a procession carrying a young Parsi widow, [[aouda]], to a suttee ceremony — ritual immolation on her husband's funeral pyre. Fogg makes the decision that defines his character: he diverts from his schedule to attempt a rescue. [[passepartout|Passepartout]] executes the plan by impersonating the dead rajah, allowing the party to spirit Aouda to safety. (PN: 012-001–012-110) This event is covered in detail at [[the-rescue-from-suttee]].

After the rescue, the party continues to Allahabad, where they resume their journey by train — now with Aouda as a member of the group. They arrive in Calcutta, where [[detective-fix|Fix]] has been waiting to arrest Fogg. (PN: 013-001–014-110)

## Significance

The Indian crossing transforms the novel from a travelogue into a story of moral consequence. Before India, Fogg is a man of pure calculation — the journey is a mathematical proof. After the rescue of Aouda, the journey acquires human stakes. The unfinished railway also illustrates Verne's theme: that the global infrastructure Fogg relies upon is fragile and incomplete, and that his success depends on adaptability as much as on [[precision-and-timekeeping|the timetable]].
