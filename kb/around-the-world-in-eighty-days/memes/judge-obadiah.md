# Judge Obadiah

Judge Obadiah is the British colonial magistrate in Bombay who presides over the trial of Jean Passepartout following the latter's ill-advised entry into a Malabar Hill temple wearing shoes. His brisk, businesslike courtroom manner and swift dispensation of justice exemplify the British colonial legal apparatus.

## Role in the Novel

Judge Obadiah appears in Sections 11–12, presiding over the court where Passepartout is tried for the sacrilege committed at the Malabar Hill temple. The incident — in which Passepartout entered the temple with his shoes on, a violation of local religious custom — leads to Fogg, Fix, and Passepartout all appearing in Obadiah's courtroom. The judge's efficiency in hearing the case reflects the no-nonsense character of British colonial justice. ([011-172], [012-009])

## Biography / Background

Judge Obadiah is a colonial magistrate stationed in Bombay. His courtroom operates with mechanical efficiency — the case against Passepartout is the first on the docket, and Obadiah hears it without ceremony. His primary concern is the orderly administration of justice, with little evident interest in the cultural or religious context of the offense. ([012-009])

## Key Actions

- **Opening the proceedings**: Judge Obadiah calls the first case of the day — the charge against Passepartout for violating the Malabar Hill temple. ([012-009])
- **Questioning the charge**: When the complaint mentions burning the offender, Obadiah interjects with bureaucratic precision: "Burn whom? In Bombay itself?" — momentarily confused by the archaic severity of the suggested punishment. ([012-040])
- **Hearing the evidence**: Obadiah listens to the testimony of the priests who witnessed Passepartout's transgression and the police report. ([012-067])
- **Passing sentence**: Judge Obadiah catches Passepartout in an unguarded moment of distress, leading to the swift imposition of the penalty — though Fogg promptly pays the bail to secure his servant's release. ([012-072])

## Character Significance

Judge Obadiah represents the British colonial legal system in microcosm: efficient, procedural, and largely indifferent to the cultural context in which it operates. His momentary confusion about "burning" the offender highlights the gap between British legal norms and the traditional religious laws he is nominally enforcing. The swift, almost mechanical resolution of the case — arrest, trial, fine, release — mirrors the novel's broader treatment of obstacles as problems to be solved by money and procedure rather than by moral or cultural engagement.

> "The first case," repeated Judge Obadiah, and the charge against Passepartout was read out. ([012-009])
