# Sir Francis Cromarty

The brigadier-general of the British Indian Army who becomes Fogg's traveling companion across India -- and an enthusiastic ally in one of the novel's most dramatic episodes: the rescue of Aouda from the suttee pyre at Pillaji.

## Role in the Novel

Sir Francis Cromarty boards the train at Bombay and shares the journey with Fogg, Passepartout, and Fix across the Indian subcontinent from Bombay to Calcutta (007-113/007-114). A tall, fair man of about fifty who has served in India since his youth (007-113), he provides local knowledge and perspective that Fogg's Bradshaw's guide cannot supply.

Cromarty serves as a narrative foil and chorus. Where Fogg is silent, Cromarty is voluble; where Fogg calculates, Cromarty reflects. His initial commentary on the wager -- he calls it "a useless eccentricity" (007-136) -- gives voice to the skepticism of the practical military man. But his relationship to Fogg evolves dramatically over the course of the Indian journey. At the Kholby interruption, where the railway abruptly terminates (the section between Kholby and Allahabad not yet completed, contrary to what the ticket sellers claimed), Cromarty shares Fogg's indignation: "Yet you sell tickets from Bombay to Calcutta!" (008-029). Together they search the village for alternative transport, and it is Cromarty who finds the elephant Kiouni that will carry them forward (008-079).

The turning point in Cromarty's character arc comes during the suttee episode at Pillaji. Awakened by the sound of a procession, Cromarty recognizes the statue of Kali and immediately understands what is happening (009-085). He watches with "a sad countenance" (009-092) and explains that the woman is being led to "burned alive" (009-119). When Fogg declares "Suppose we save this woman" (009-128), Cromarty becomes "an enthusiastic ally" (009-128). The brigadier, who began as a detached observer of Fogg's eccentricity, now joins him in a perilous rescue attempt deep in territory where "we have no power" (009-116). During the rescue itself, Cromarty's heart throbs as he watches the victim on the pyre (010-112), and after the rescue succeeds, he presses Passepartout's hand in shared emotion (010-152). 

Cromarty departs the narrative at his destination of Benares (011-059), but not before Aouda -- now revived and traveling with the party -- expresses her gratitude to him for his role in saving her life (011-095).

## Biography

Like many officers of the British Indian Army, Cromarty has spent his adult life in India. He knows the railways, the telegraph lines, the cantonments, and the political geography of the subcontinent with an intimacy that only long service can provide. His presence on the train is itself a consequence of empire: officers routinely traveled between Bombay, Calcutta, and the interior, maintaining the circulatory system of colonial administration.

Cromarty's Indian experience makes him an invaluable intermediary between the novel's European readers and the Indian landscape. He explains the geography, comments on the political situation in the regions through which they travel (007-088), and provides the imperial perspective that contextualizes Fogg's journey through British territory.

- Discusses the time difference with Passepartout, whose family watch still shows London time (008-028)
- Reacts with fury when the railway terminates at Kholby despite tickets being sold through to Calcutta (008-029)
- Acquires the elephant Kiouni for the overland journey to Allahabad (008-079)
- Recognizes the suttee procession at Pillaji and explains it to Fogg (009-085)
- Becomes an "enthusiastic ally" when Fogg proposes to rescue the victim (009-128)

- Departs at Benares, his destination, bidding farewell to the party (011-059)

## Character Significance

Cromarty represents the empire as a matter-of-fact reality -- not a political project or a moral crusade, but simply the world as it is. His presence in India, his knowledge of its geography, and his unquestioning assumption of British authority are all treated by Verne as background facts, no more remarkable than the railway itself.

But Cromarty also undergoes a character development that enriches his role beyond mere exposition. His transformation from skeptical observer to enthusiastic rescuer mirrors the novel's own progression from rational travelogue to romantic adventure. When Cromarty joins Fogg in the rescue attempt -- entering territory where "we have no power" -- he steps beyond his role as an imperial functionary and acts as a moral agent, risking his safety for a woman he has never met. This is the empire at its most idealistic: using its power not to dominate but to intervene against what it perceives as barbarism.

> "The general only saw in the wager a useless eccentricity." (007-136)
