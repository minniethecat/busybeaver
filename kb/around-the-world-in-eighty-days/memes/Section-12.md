[012-001] “The first case,” said he. Then, putting his hand to his head, he

[012-002] exclaimed, “Heh! This is not my wig!”

[012-003] “No, your worship,” returned the clerk, “it is mine.”

[012-004] “My dear Mr. Oysterpuff, how can a judge give a wise sentence in a

[012-005] clerk’s wig?”

[012-006] The wigs were exchanged.

[012-007] [[passepartout|Passepartout]] was getting nervous, for the hands on the face of the big

[012-008] [[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] over the judge seemed to go around with terrible rapidity.

[012-009] “The first case,” repeated Judge Obadiah.

[012-010] “[[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]?” demanded Oysterpuff.

[012-011] “I am here,” replied Mr. Fogg.

[012-012] “Passepartout?”

[012-013] “Present,” responded Passepartout.

[012-014] “Good,” said the judge. “You have been looked for, prisoners, for two

[012-015] days on the trains from [[bombay|Bombay]].”

[012-016] “But of what are we accused?” asked Passepartout, impatiently.

[012-017] “You are about to be informed.”

[012-018] “I am an English subject, sir,” said Mr. Fogg, “and I have the right—”

[012-019] “Have you been ill-treated?”

[012-020] “Not at all.”

[012-021] “Very well; let the complainants come in.”

[012-022] A door was swung open by order of the judge, and three Indian priests

[012-023] entered.

[012-024] “That’s it,” muttered Passepartout; “these are the rogues who were

[012-025] going to burn our young lady.”

[012-026] The priests took their places in front of the judge, and the clerk

[012-027] proceeded to read in a loud voice a complaint of sacrilege against

[012-028] Phileas Fogg and his servant, who were accused of having violated a

[012-029] place held consecrated by the Brahmin religion.

[012-030] “You hear the charge?” asked the judge.

[012-031] “Yes, sir,” replied Mr. Fogg, consulting his watch, “and I admit it.”

[012-032] “You admit it?”

[012-033] “I admit it, and I wish to hear these priests admit, in their turn,

[012-034] what they were going to do at the pagoda of Pillaji.”

[012-035] The priests looked at each other; they did not seem to understand what

[012-036] was said.

[012-037] “Yes,” cried Passepartout, warmly; “at the pagoda of Pillaji, where

[012-038] they were on the point of burning their victim.”

[012-039] The judge stared with astonishment, and the priests were stupefied.

[012-040] “What victim?” said Judge Obadiah. “Burn whom? In Bombay itself?”

[012-041] “Bombay?” cried Passepartout.

[012-042] “Certainly. We are not talking of the pagoda of Pillaji, but of the

[012-043] pagoda of Malabar Hill, at Bombay.”

[012-044] “And as a proof,” added the clerk, “here are the desecrator’s very

[012-045] shoes, which he left behind him.”

[012-046] Whereupon he placed a pair of shoes on his desk.

[012-047] “My shoes!” cried Passepartout, in his surprise permitting this

[012-048] imprudent exclamation to escape him.

[012-049] The confusion of master and man, who had quite forgotten the affair at

[012-050] Bombay, for which they were now detained at [[calcutta|Calcutta]], may be imagined.

[012-051] [[detective-fix|Fix]] the detective, had foreseen the advantage which Passepartout’s

[012-052] escapade gave him, and, delaying his departure for twelve hours, had

[012-053] consulted the priests of Malabar Hill. Knowing that the English

[012-054] authorities dealt very severely with this kind of misdemeanour, he

[012-055] promised them a goodly sum in damages, and sent them forward to

[012-056] Calcutta by the next [[railway-travel|train]]. Owing to the delay caused by the [[the-rescue-of-aouda|rescue]] of

[012-057] the young widow, [[fix-detective|Fix]] and the priests reached the Indian capital before

[012-058] Mr. Fogg and his servant, the magistrates having been already warned by

[012-059] a dispatch to arrest them should they arrive. Fix’s disappointment when

[012-060] he learned that Phileas Fogg had not made his appearance in Calcutta

[012-061] may be imagined. He made up his mind that the robber had stopped

[012-062] somewhere on the [[around-the-world-route|route]] and taken refuge in the southern provinces. For

[012-063] twenty-four hours Fix watched the station with feverish anxiety; at

[012-064] last he was rewarded by seeing Mr. Fogg and Passepartout arrive,

[012-065] accompanied by a young woman, whose presence he was wholly at a loss to

[012-066] explain. He hastened for a policeman; and this was how the party came

[012-067] to be arrested and brought before Judge Obadiah.

[012-068] Had Passepartout been a little less preoccupied, he would have espied

[012-069] the detective ensconced in a corner of the court-room, watching the

[012-070] proceedings with an interest easily understood; for the warrant had

[012-071] failed to reach him at Calcutta, as it had done at Bombay and [[suez|Suez]].

[012-072] Judge Obadiah had unfortunately caught Passepartout’s rash exclamation,

[012-073] which the poor fellow would have given the world to recall.

[012-074] “The facts are admitted?” asked the judge.

[012-075] “Admitted,” replied Mr. Fogg, coldly.

[012-076] “Inasmuch,” resumed the judge, “as the English law protects equally and

[012-077] sternly the religions of the Indian people, and as the man Passepartout

[012-078] has admitted that he violated the sacred pagoda of Malabar Hill, at

[012-079] Bombay, on the 20th of October, I condemn the said Passepartout to

[012-080] imprisonment for fifteen days and a fine of three hundred pounds.”

[012-081] “Three hundred pounds!” cried Passepartout, startled at the largeness

[012-082] of the sum.

[012-083] “Silence!” shouted the constable.

[012-084] “And inasmuch,” continued the judge, “as it is not proved that the act

[012-085] was not done by the connivance of the master with the servant, and as

[012-086] the master in any case must be held responsible for the acts of his

[012-087] paid servant, I condemn Phileas Fogg to a week’s imprisonment and a

[012-088] fine of one hundred and fifty pounds.”

[012-089] Fix rubbed his hands softly with satisfaction; if Phileas Fogg could be

[012-090] detained in Calcutta a week, it would be more than time for the warrant

[012-091] to arrive. Passepartout was stupefied. This sentence ruined his master.

[012-092] A [[the-wager|wager]] of twenty thousand pounds lost, because he, like a precious

[012-093] fool, had gone into that abominable pagoda!

[012-094] Phileas Fogg, as self-composed as if the judgment did not in the least

[012-095] concern him, did not even lift his eyebrows while it was being

[012-096] pronounced. Just as the clerk was calling the next case, he rose, and

[012-097] said, “I offer bail.”

[012-098] “You have that right,” returned the judge.

[012-099] Fix’s blood ran cold, but he resumed his composure when he heard the

[012-100] judge announce that the bail required for each prisoner would be one

[012-101] thousand pounds.

[012-102] “I will pay it at once,” said Mr. Fogg, taking a roll of bank-bills

[012-103] from the carpet-bag, which Passepartout had by him, and placing them on

[012-104] the clerk’s desk.

[012-105] “This sum will be restored to you upon your release from prison,” said

[012-106] the judge. “Meanwhile, you are liberated on bail.”

[012-107] “Come!” said Phileas Fogg to his servant.

[012-108] “But let them at least give me back my shoes!” cried Passepartout

[012-109] angrily.

[012-110] “Ah, these are pretty dear shoes!” he muttered, as they were handed to

[012-111] him. “More than a thousand pounds apiece; besides, they pinch my feet.”

[012-112] Mr. Fogg, offering his arm to [[aouda|Aouda]], then departed, followed by the

[012-113] crestfallen Passepartout. Fix still nourished hopes that the robber

[012-114] would not, after all, leave the two thousand pounds behind him, but

[012-115] would decide to serve out his week in jail, and issued forth on Mr.

[012-116] Fogg’s traces. That [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] took a carriage, and the party were soon

[012-117] landed on one of the quays.

[012-118] The “Rangoon” was moored half a mile off in the harbour, its signal of

[012-119] departure hoisted at the mast-head. Eleven o’clock was striking; Mr.

[012-120] Fogg was an hour in advance of time. Fix saw them leave the carriage

[012-121] and push off in a boat for the [[steamer-travel|steamer]], and stamped his feet with

[012-122] disappointment.

[012-123] “The rascal is off, after all!” he exclaimed. “Two thousand pounds

[012-124] sacrificed! He’s as prodigal as a thief! I’ll follow him to the end of

[012-125] the world if necessary; but, at the rate he is going on, the stolen

[012-126] money will soon be exhausted.”

[012-127] The detective was not far wrong in making this conjecture. Since

[012-128] leaving [[london|London]], what with travelling expenses, bribes, the purchase of

[012-129] the elephant, bails, and fines, Mr. Fogg had already spent more than

[012-130] five thousand pounds on the way, and the percentage of the sum

[012-131] recovered from the bank robber promised to the detectives, was rapidly

[012-132] diminishing.

[012-133] CHAPTER XVI.

[012-134] IN WHICH FIX DOES NOT SEEM TO UNDERSTAND IN THE LEAST WHAT IS SAID TO

[012-135] HIM

[012-136] The “Rangoon”—one of the [[p-and-o-company|Peninsular and Oriental]] Company’s boats plying

[012-137] in the Chinese and Japanese seas—was a screw steamer, built of iron,

[012-138] weighing about seventeen hundred and seventy tons, and with engines of

[012-139] four hundred horse-power. She was as fast, but not as well fitted up,

[012-140] as the “Mongolia,” and Aouda was not as comfortably provided for on

[012-141] board of her as Phileas Fogg could have wished. However, the trip from

[012-142] Calcutta to [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] only comprised some three thousand five hundred

[012-143] miles, occupying from ten to twelve days, and the young woman was not

[012-144] difficult to please.

[012-145] During the first days of the journey Aouda became better acquainted

[012-146] with her protector, and constantly gave evidence of her deep gratitude

[012-147] for what he had done. The phlegmatic gentleman listened to her,

[012-148] apparently at least, with coldness, neither his voice nor his manner

[012-149] betraying the slightest emotion; but he seemed to be always on the

[012-150] watch that nothing should be wanting to Aouda’s comfort. He visited her

[012-151] regularly each day at certain hours, not so much to talk himself, as to

[012-152] sit and hear her talk. He treated her with the strictest politeness,

[012-153] but with the precision of an automaton, the movements of which had been

[012-154] arranged for this purpose. Aouda did not quite know what to make of

[012-155] him, though Passepartout had given her some hints of his master’s

[012-156] eccentricity, and made her smile by telling her of the wager which was

[012-157] sending him round the world. After all, she owed Phileas Fogg her life,

[012-158] and she always regarded him through the exalting medium of her

[012-159] gratitude.

[012-160] Aouda confirmed the [[the-parsee-guide|Parsee guide]]’s narrative of her touching history.

[012-161] She did, indeed, belong to the highest of the native races of India.

[012-162] Many of the Parsee merchants have made great fortunes there by dealing

[012-163] in cotton; and one of them, Sir Jametsee Jeejeebhoy, was made a baronet

[012-164] by the English government. Aouda was a relative of this great man, and

[012-165] it was his cousin, Jeejeeh, whom she hoped to join at Hong Kong.

[012-166] Whether she would find a protector in him she could not tell; but Mr.

[012-167] Fogg essayed to calm her anxieties, and to assure her that everything

[012-168] would be mathematically—he used the very word—arranged. Aouda fastened

[012-169] her great eyes, “clear as the sacred lakes of the Himalaya,” upon him;

[012-170] but the intractable Fogg, as reserved as ever, did not seem at all

[012-171] inclined to throw himself into this lake.

[012-172] The first few days of the voyage passed prosperously, amid favourable

[012-173] weather and propitious winds, and they soon came in sight of the great

[012-174] Andaman, the principal of the islands in the Bay of Bengal, with its

[012-175] picturesque Saddle Peak, two thousand four hundred feet high, looming

[012-176] above the waters. The steamer passed along near the shores, but the

[012-177] savage Papuans, who are in the lowest scale of humanity, but are not,

[012-178] as has been asserted, cannibals, did not make their appearance.

[012-179] The panorama of the islands, as they steamed by them, was superb. Vast

[012-180] forests of palms, arecs, bamboo, teakwood, of the gigantic mimosa, and

[012-181] tree-like ferns covered the foreground, while behind, the graceful

[012-182] outlines of the mountains were traced against the sky; and along the

[012-183] coasts swarmed by thousands the precious swallows whose nests furnish a

[012-184] luxurious dish to the tables of the Celestial Empire. The varied

[012-185] landscape afforded by the Andaman Islands was soon passed, however, and

[012-186] the “Rangoon” rapidly approached the Straits of Malacca, which gave

[012-187] access to the China seas.

[012-188] What was detective Fix, so unluckily drawn on from country to country,

[012-189] doing all this while? He had managed to embark on the “Rangoon” at

[012-190] Calcutta without being seen by Passepartout, after leaving orders that,

[012-191] if the warrant should arrive, it should be forwarded to him at Hong

[012-192] Kong; and he hoped to conceal his presence to the end of the voyage. It

[012-193] would have been difficult to explain why he was on board without

[012-194] awakening Passepartout’s suspicions, who thought him still at Bombay.

[012-195] But necessity impelled him, nevertheless, to renew his acquaintance

[012-196] with the worthy servant, as will be seen.

[012-197] All the detective’s hopes and wishes were now centred on Hong Kong; for

[012-198] the steamer’s stay at Singapore would be too brief to enable him to

[012-199] take any steps there. The arrest must be made at Hong Kong, or the

[012-200] robber would probably escape him for ever. Hong Kong was the last

[012-201] English ground on which he would set foot; beyond, China, Japan,

[012-202] America offered to Fogg an almost certain refuge. If the warrant should

[012-203] at last make its appearance at Hong Kong, Fix could arrest him and give

[012-204] him into the hands of the local police, and there would be no further

[012-205] trouble. But beyond Hong Kong, a simple warrant would be of no avail;

[012-206] an extradition warrant would be necessary, and that would result in

[012-207] delays and obstacles, of which the rascal would take advantage to elude

[012-208] justice.
