# Detective Fix

Detective Fix is the [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] detective who pursues [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] around the world, mistakenly believing him to be the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] robber. A central antagonist of the novel, Fix's pursuit drives much of the story's tension — yet his actions paradoxically advance Fogg's journey at every turn, making him an unwitting ally as much as an adversary.

## The Assignment

When the Bank of England was robbed of fifty-five thousand pounds, picked detectives were dispatched "to all the principal ports of America and Europe" to watch for the thief. Among them was Detective Fix — "one of the detectives who had been sent out in consequence of the bank robbery" (PN: 004-122). A man of "intelligent and restless eyes," Fix embodied the professional doggedness of Scotland Yard. His assignment took him to Suez, where he waited for the steamer *Mongolia*, suspecting that the robber — whose description matched Fogg — might attempt to flee via the newly opened Suez Canal route.

Fix's first sighting came when he met the British consul at Suez. "No, Mr. Fix," replied the consul when pressed about the steamer's expected arrival, "she was bespoken yesterday at Port Said" (PN: 004-134). The exchange establishes Fix's restless impatience and the consul's bureaucratic calm — a dynamic that will recur throughout Fix's pursuit. After interviewing [[passepartout|Passepartout]], who had come ashore to have Fogg's passport stamped, Fix became convinced his quarry was aboard:

> "Good!" said Fix. "If the robber is on board he will no doubt get off at Suez." (PN: 004-183)

The consul's assessment captures Fix's professional character: "Mr. Fix," said the consul, "I like your way of acting" (PN: 004-154). And the narrator concurs: "Mr. Fix evidently was not wanting in intelligence" (PN: 004-163).

## A Pursuit Around the World

Fix's pursuit takes him from Suez to Bombay, across India to Calcutta, to Hong Kong, Yokohama, San Francisco, and across America to New York and Liverpool. At each stage, he is hindered by a fundamental constraint: he cannot arrest Fogg on foreign territory until a warrant arrives from London. This limitation transforms Fix from a straightforward policeman into an increasingly desperate figure — sabotaging Fogg where he can (detaining Passepartout in Hong Kong, maneuvering Fogg away from the Carnatic steamer), yet forced to remain dangerously close to his quarry at all times.

After arriving at Bombay, "Fix, according to habit, scrutinized the passengers" (PN: 004-172). His methodical, almost mechanical approach to detection is both his strength and his limitation. He can observe and track with relentless precision, but he cannot question the premise on which his entire pursuit rests.

The irony of Fix's pursuit is stated plainly in the chapter headings: Fix's actions "considerably further the interests of Phileas Fogg" (PN: 001-035). At every turn, his interference — paying for transportation, clearing bureaucratic obstacles, arranging passage — ensures that Fogg stays on schedule. The detective becomes, in effect, Fogg's unwilling travel agent.

## The Warrant and the Revelation

Fix's warrant finally arrives when the travelers reach Liverpool — on British soil at last. He arrests Fogg immediately, holding him at the Custom House. But the delay proves costly: the real robber, a man named James Strand, had been arrested three days earlier in Edinburgh. Fogg, released, arrives at the Reform Club just in time — and Fix is left with nothing but his professional embarrassment. The moment completes the novel's inversion of pursuer and pursued: Fix, who has spent eighty days stalking his quarry, discovers that the entire effort was built on a misidentification.

## Character Analysis

Detective Fix occupies a unique position in the novel's moral architecture. He is not a villain in the conventional sense; he is a professional doing his job with imperfect information. His error is not malice but a failure of imagination — he cannot conceive that a man might travel around the world simply to win a wager. His narrow, police-procedural worldview cannot accommodate Fogg's eccentric grandeur.

What distinguishes Fix from a mere obstacle is that his presence allows Verne to explore the collision between law enforcement and individual freedom. Fix's warrant — a piece of paper issued in a London office — has no force across most of the globe. In the unregulated spaces of international travel, the detective is as powerless as any ordinary passenger. The novel dramatizes a world where national law has not yet caught up with global mobility. Fix is not merely chasing a suspect; he is chasing the limits of jurisdiction in an age of steam-powered borders.

Fix also serves as a foil to Fogg. Where Fogg is imperturbable, Fix is anxious and reactive. Where Fogg moves with mechanical precision, Fix lurches from one scheme to the next. The detective's increasing desperation — culminating in the Hong Kong opium den where he drugs Passepartout — charts a moral decline that Fogg, for all his coldness, never approaches. The contrast is instructive: Fogg's eccentricity is nobler than Fix's official rectitude.
