# The Wager at the Reform Club

The moment that transforms a quiet evening of whist into a global sensation: [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]], provoked by a newspaper article and egged on by his whist partner [[andrew-stuart|Andrew Stuart]], stakes twenty thousand pounds on the proposition that a man can travel [[around-the-world-route|around the world]] in eighty days. The wager is not merely the novel's inciting incident — it is the mathematical and moral framework within which every subsequent event is measured.

## Summary

On the evening of October 2, 1872, at the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] in Pall Mall, [[london]], [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] engages in a dispute with his fellow whist players over a _Morning Chronicle_ article calculating that the opening of a new railway section in India reduces the minimum circumnavigation time to eighty days. [[andrew-stuart|Andrew Stuart]] dismisses the calculation as theoretical, citing the "unforeseen accidents" that plague travel. Fogg, characteristically, responds with action rather than argument: he offers to undertake the journey himself, staking twenty thousand pounds — half his fortune — on the outcome. (PN: 003-062–003-110)

The wager is accepted by five fellow club members: [[andrew-stuart|Stuart]], Lord Albemarle, John Sullivan, Samuel Fallentin, and Thomas Flanagan. Fogg departs that very evening, accompanied by his newly-hired servant [[passepartout]], setting in motion a chain of events that will cross three oceans, four continents, and the International Date Line.

> "I will bet twenty thousand pounds against anyone who wishes, that I will make the tour of the world in eighty days or less — in nineteen hundred and twenty hours, or a hundred and fifteen thousand two hundred minutes. Will you?" (003-062)

## Narrative Significance

The wager scene establishes every major theme of the novel in a single dramatic encounter:

| Theme | How Established |
|-------|----------------|
| Precision | Fogg calculates the journey not in days but in minutes — 115,200 of them |
| Character | Fogg's refusal to debate versus the club members' incredulity defines his nature |
| Imperial confidence | The very possibility of an eighty-day circuit depends on British imperial infrastructure |
| Stakes | Half a fortune wagered on pure principle — Fogg stands to gain nothing but the satisfaction of being right |

The wager also sets the novel's temporal and geographic parameters. Every subsequent event — every delay, every rescue, every race against the clock — is measured against the eighty-day deadline established in this scene.

## Consequences

The wager triggers every major plot thread:

1. **The pursuit**: News of the wager reaches [[detective-fix|Detective Fix]], who connects Fogg's sudden departure with the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] robbery and begins his transcontinental pursuit
2. **The rescue**: Fogg's refusal to deviate from his timetable is tested — and broken — when he rescues [[aouda]] from [[the-rescue-from-suttee|suttee]] in India
3. **The complications**: Each leg of the [[around-the-world-route|journey]] introduces obstacles (arrests, attacks, accidents) that Fogg must overcome without exceeding his eighty-day allowance
4. **The victory**: Fogg returns to the [[reform-club]] apparently five minutes late, only to discover that eastward travel has gained him a day — he has won after all
