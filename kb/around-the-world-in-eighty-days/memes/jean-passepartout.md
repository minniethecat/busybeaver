Jean Passepartout is the French valet newly engaged by [[phileas-fogg]] at the opening of *Around the World in Eighty Days*. A former gymnast, fireman, and street performer, Passepartout seeks a quiet, orderly life in Fogg's service — a hope immediately upended by the impulsive circumnavigation that begins on the very evening of his employment. His energetic, adaptable nature and moments of comic misjudgment balance Fogg's rigid precision, making him the novel's primary source of warmth and humor. Yet Passepartout is far more than comic relief: his physical courage, practical intelligence, and growing loyalty to his master repeatedly prove essential to the journey's success.

## Background and Character

Passepartout had been a sort of vagabond in Paris, having tried a dozen trades: itinerant singer, circus rider, gymnastics teacher, and fireman who had even rescued people from burning buildings. He had left France for England seeking a settled domestic existence, hoping to find in Phileas Fogg a master of clockwork regularity ([PN=002-034]–[PN=002-051]). His interview with Fogg reveals a man weary of his own restlessness, yearning for the "most tranquil and peaceable" of lives — a deeply ironic desire given the events about to unfold.

> "Faith," muttered Passepartout, somewhat flurried, "I've seen people at Madame Tussaud's as lively as my new master!" ([PN=002-007])

His name, meaning "passes everywhere" or "master key," suits his versatile, resourceful nature. A true Parisian of Paris, Passepartout is described as having an expressive round face, a stout build, and an irrepressible disposition that had survived years of adventure before encountering Fogg's clockwork household ([PN=002-034]–[PN=002-040]). His physical description matches his temperament: warm, rounded, and perpetually in motion.

## Journey Role

Passepartout's role evolves across the journey from mere servant to indispensable companion. His practical intelligence repeatedly saves the expedition, while his moments of impetuous action create some of the novel's most dramatic complications. Verne uses Passepartout as the reader's surrogate — his wonder at each new country, his astonishment at Fogg's calm, and his gradual understanding of the wager's stakes mirror the reader's own experience.

| Stage | Action | Outcome |
|-------|--------|---------|
| London | Enters Fogg's service | Departs for journey within hours of engagement ([PN=001-148]–[PN=001-165]) |
| Bombay | Enters the Malabar Hill pagoda in shoes | Leads to legal trouble and Fix's leverage ([PN=009-097]) |
| India | Rescues Aouda from suttee | Gains a traveling companion and future wife for Fogg ([PN=012-130]–[PN=013-030]) |
| Hong Kong | Gets separated from Fogg | Boards the Carnatic alone, leading to his Yokohama adventures ([PN=018-045]–[PN=019-030]) |
| Across America | Defends the train against Sioux attack | Saves passengers but is captured; later rescued ([PN=026-080]–[PN=027-040]) |

His most heroic moment comes during the Indian journey, where he risks his life to rescue Aouda from the funeral pyre. Disguised as the deceased rajah and rising from the flames, Passepartout terrifies the assembled priests and carries Aouda to safety ([PN=012-130]–[PN=013-030]). This act transforms him from a comic valet to a figure of genuine courage.

## Relationship with Fogg

Passepartout's relationship with his master evolves from professional service to genuine loyalty and affection. Initially, he admires Fogg's precision and calm: the master of No. 7 Saville Row — who "might have been a chronometer in a frock-coat," so regular were his habits — seems the ideal employer for a man who has resolved to leave Parisian chaos behind ([PN=002-034]). Throughout the journey, as Fogg demonstrates unexpected courage and generosity — risking his own schedule to rescue Aouda, to save Passepartout from the Sioux, to charter the Henrietta across the Atlantic — Passepartout's respect deepens into devoted companionship.

He acts as a foil to Fogg: where Fogg is calm and methodical, Passepartout is impulsive and emotional. This dynamic creates both comic relief and narrative tension, particularly when Passepartout's actions inadvertently complicate Fogg's meticulously timed plans. Yet in the novel's final act, it is Passepartout who discovers the crucial fact — the International Date Line — that enables Fogg to win the wager, bringing his journey from servant to savior full circle ([PN=036-040]–[PN=036-100]).

## Encounters with Fix

Unaware that [[fix-detective]] is pursuing Fogg, Passepartout repeatedly confides in the detective, mistaking him for a fellow traveler. Their interactions provide much of the novel's dramatic irony and drive key plot turns.

> Passepartout, who had conscientiously studied the map, was astounded to find the detective on board the Rangoon, traveling the same route as his master. ([PN=012-194])

Fix cultivates Passepartout's friendship with calculated charm, extracting information about Fogg's plans while the valet remains blissfully ignorant of the detective's true purpose ([PN=012-194]–[PN=013-110]). The relationship reaches its crisis in Hong Kong, where Fix drugs Passepartout in an opium den — a desperate act that separates valet from master and sends Passepartout, alone and penniless, aboard the Carnatic to Yokohama ([PN=018-045]–[PN=019-080]). Their eventual reconciliation, when Passepartout discovers Fix's true identity, is marked by memorable physical confrontation and grudging acceptance.

## Character Significance

Passepartout represents warmth, humanity, and adaptability in contrast to Fogg's mechanical precision. His journey from seeking a quiet life to embracing — and enabling — the greatest adventure of his master's life mirrors the novel's celebration of the unexpected. Through Passepartout, Verne demonstrates that loyalty, quick thinking, and a good heart matter as much as meticulous planning in a journey around the world. He is the novel's beating heart: the character who feels the journey most fully, and who ensures — through courage, through comedy, and through a final crucial observation about the calendar — that Fogg's eighty days are not merely completed, but won.
