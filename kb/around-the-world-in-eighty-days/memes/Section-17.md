[017-001] [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] was also thinking of [[passepartout|Passepartout]], who had so strangely

[017-002] disappeared. Looking at the matter from every point of view, it did not

[017-003] seem to him impossible that, by some mistake, the man might have

[017-004] embarked on the “Carnatic” at the last moment; and this was also

[017-005] [[aouda|Aouda]]’s opinion, who regretted very much the loss of the worthy fellow

[017-006] to whom she owed so much. They might then find him at [[yokohama|Yokohama]]; for, if

[017-007] the “Carnatic” was carrying him thither, it would be easy to ascertain

[017-008] if he had been on board.

[017-009] A brisk breeze arose about ten o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]]; but, though it might have been

[017-010] prudent to take in a reef, the pilot, after carefully examining the

[017-011] heavens, let the craft remain rigged as before. The “Tankadere” bore

[017-012] sail admirably, as she drew a great deal of water, and everything was

[017-013] prepared for high speed in case of a gale.

[017-014] Mr. Fogg and Aouda descended into the cabin at midnight, having been

[017-015] already preceded by [[detective-fix|Fix]], who had lain down on one of the cots. The

[017-016] pilot and crew remained on deck all night.

[017-017] At sunrise the next day, which was 8th November, the boat had made more

[017-018] than one hundred miles. The log indicated a mean speed of between eight

[017-019] and nine miles. The “Tankadere” still carried all sail, and was

[017-020] accomplishing her greatest capacity of speed. If the wind held as it

[017-021] was, the chances would be in her favour. During the day she kept along

[017-022] the coast, where the currents were favourable; the coast, irregular in

[017-023] profile, and visible sometimes across the clearings, was at most five

[017-024] miles distant. The sea was less boisterous, since the wind came off

[017-025] land—a fortunate circumstance for the boat, which would suffer, owing

[017-026] to its small tonnage, by a heavy surge on the sea.

[017-027] The breeze subsided a little towards noon, and set in from the

[017-028] south-west. The pilot put up his poles, but took them down again within

[017-029] two hours, as the wind freshened up anew.

[017-030] Mr. Fogg and Aouda, happily unaffected by the roughness of the sea, ate

[017-031] with a good appetite, [[fix-detective|Fix]] being invited to share their repast, which he

[017-032] accepted with secret chagrin. To travel at this man’s expense and live

[017-033] upon his provisions was not palatable to him. Still, he was obliged to

[017-034] eat, and so he ate.

[017-035] When the meal was over, he took Mr. Fogg apart, and said, “sir”—this

[017-036] “sir” scorched his lips, and he had to control himself to avoid

[017-037] collaring this “[[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]]”—“sir, you have been very kind to give me a

[017-038] passage on this boat. But, though my means will not admit of my

[017-039] expending them as freely as you, I must ask to pay my share—”

[017-040] “Let us not speak of that, sir,” replied Mr. Fogg.

[017-041] “But, if I insist—”

[017-042] “No, sir,” repeated Mr. Fogg, in a tone which did not admit of a reply.

[017-043] “This enters into my general expenses.”

[017-044] Fix, as he bowed, had a stifled feeling, and, going forward, where he

[017-045] ensconced himself, did not open his mouth for the rest of the day.

[017-046] Meanwhile they were progressing famously, and John Bunsby was in high

[017-047] hope. He several times assured Mr. Fogg that they would reach Shanghai

[017-048] in time; to which that gentleman responded that he counted upon it. The

[017-049] crew set to work in good earnest, inspired by the reward to be gained.

[017-050] There was not a sheet which was not tightened, not a sail which was not

[017-051] vigorously hoisted; not a lurch could be charged to the man at the

[017-052] helm. They worked as desperately as if they were contesting in a Royal

[017-053] yacht regatta.

[017-054] By evening, the log showed that two hundred and twenty miles had been

[017-055] accomplished from [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], and Mr. Fogg might hope that he would be

[017-056] able to reach Yokohama without recording any delay in his journal; in

[017-057] which case, the many misadventures which had overtaken him since he

[017-058] left [[london|London]] would not seriously affect his journey.

[017-059] The “Tankadere” entered the Straits of Fo-Kien, which separate the

[017-060] island of Formosa from the Chinese coast, in the small hours of the

[017-061] night, and crossed the Tropic of Cancer. The sea was very rough in the

[017-062] straits, full of eddies formed by the counter-currents, and the

[017-063] chopping waves broke her course, whilst it became very difficult to

[017-064] stand on deck.

[017-065] At daybreak the wind began to blow hard again, and the heavens seemed

[017-066] to predict a gale. The barometer announced a speedy change, the mercury

[017-067] rising and falling capriciously; the sea also, in the south-east,

[017-068] raised long surges which indicated a tempest. The sun had set the

[017-069] evening before in a red mist, in the midst of the phosphorescent

[017-070] scintillations of the ocean.

[017-071] John Bunsby long examined the threatening aspect of the heavens,

[017-072] muttering indistinctly between his teeth. At last he said in a low

[017-073] voice to Mr. Fogg, “Shall I speak out to your honour?”

[017-074] “Of course.”

[017-075] “Well, we are going to have a squall.”

[017-076] “Is the wind north or south?” asked Mr. Fogg quietly.

[017-077] “South. Look! a typhoon is coming up.”

[017-078] “Glad it’s a typhoon from the south, for it will carry us forward.”

[017-079] “Oh, if you take it that way,” said John Bunsby, “I’ve nothing more to

[017-080] say.” John Bunsby’s suspicions were confirmed. At a less advanced

[017-081] season of the year the typhoon, according to a famous meteorologist,

[017-082] would have passed away like a luminous cascade of electric flame; but

[017-083] in the winter equinox it was to be feared that it would burst upon them

[017-084] with great violence.

[017-085] The pilot took his precautions in advance. He reefed all sail, the

[017-086] pole-masts were dispensed with; all hands went forward to the bows. A

[017-087] single triangular sail, of strong canvas, was hoisted as a storm-jib,

[017-088] so as to hold the wind from behind. Then they waited.

[017-089] John Bunsby had requested his passengers to go below; but this

[017-090] imprisonment in so narrow a space, with little air, and the boat

[017-091] bouncing in the gale, was far from pleasant. Neither Mr. Fogg, Fix, nor

[017-092] Aouda consented to leave the deck.

[017-093] The storm of rain and wind descended upon them towards eight o’clock.

[017-094] With but its bit of sail, the “Tankadere” was lifted like a feather by

[017-095] a wind, an idea of whose violence can scarcely be given. To compare her

[017-096] speed to four times that of a [[railway-travel|locomotive]] going on full steam would be

[017-097] below the truth.

[017-098] The boat scudded thus northward during the whole day, borne on by

[017-099] monstrous waves, preserving always, fortunately, a speed equal to

[017-100] theirs. Twenty times she seemed almost to be submerged by these

[017-101] mountains of water which rose behind her; but the adroit management of

[017-102] the pilot saved her. The passengers were often bathed in spray, but

[017-103] they submitted to it philosophically. Fix cursed it, no doubt; but

[017-104] Aouda, with her eyes fastened upon her protector, whose coolness amazed

[017-105] her, showed herself worthy of him, and bravely weathered the storm. As

[017-106] for Phileas Fogg, it seemed just as if the typhoon were a part of his

[017-107] programme.

[017-108] Up to this time the “Tankadere” had always held her course to the

[017-109] north; but towards evening the wind, veering three quarters, bore down

[017-110] from the north-west. The boat, now lying in the trough of the waves,

[017-111] shook and rolled terribly; the sea struck her with fearful violence. At

[017-112] night the tempest increased in violence. John Bunsby saw the approach

[017-113] of darkness and the rising of the storm with dark misgivings. He

[017-114] thought awhile, and then asked his crew if it was not time to slacken

[017-115] speed. After a consultation he approached Mr. Fogg, and said, “I think,

[017-116] your honour, that we should do well to make for one of the ports on the

[017-117] coast.”

[017-118] “I think so too.”

[017-119] “Ah!” said the pilot. “But which one?”

[017-120] “I know of but one,” returned Mr. Fogg tranquilly.

[017-121] “And that is—”

[017-122] “Shanghai.”

[017-123] The pilot, at first, did not seem to comprehend; he could scarcely

[017-124] realise so much determination and tenacity. Then he cried, “Well—yes!

[017-125] Your honour is right. To Shanghai!”

[017-126] So the “Tankadere” kept steadily on her northward track.

[017-127] The night was really terrible; it would be a miracle if the craft did

[017-128] not founder. Twice it could have been all over with her if the crew had

[017-129] not been constantly on the watch. Aouda was exhausted, but did not

[017-130] utter a complaint. More than once Mr. Fogg rushed to protect her from

[017-131] the violence of the waves.

[017-132] Day reappeared. The tempest still raged with undiminished fury; but the

[017-133] wind now returned to the south-east. It was a favourable change, and

[017-134] the “Tankadere” again bounded forward on this mountainous sea, though

[017-135] the waves crossed each other, and imparted shocks and counter-shocks

[017-136] which would have crushed a craft less solidly built. From time to time

[017-137] the coast was visible through the broken mist, but no vessel was in

[017-138] sight. The “Tankadere” was alone upon the sea.

[017-139] There were some signs of a calm at noon, and these became more distinct

[017-140] as the sun descended toward the horizon. The tempest had been as brief

[017-141] as terrific. The passengers, thoroughly exhausted, could now eat a

[017-142] little, and take some repose.

[017-143] The night was comparatively quiet. Some of the sails were again

[017-144] hoisted, and the speed of the boat was very good. The next morning at

[017-145] dawn they espied the coast, and John Bunsby was able to assert that

[017-146] they were not one hundred miles from Shanghai. A hundred miles, and

[017-147] only one day to traverse them! That very evening Mr. Fogg was due at

[017-148] Shanghai, if he did not wish to miss the [[steamer-travel|steamer]] to Yokohama. Had there

[017-149] been no storm, during which several hours were lost, they would be at

[017-150] this moment within thirty miles of their destination.

[017-151] The wind grew decidedly calmer, and happily the sea fell with it. All

[017-152] sails were now hoisted, and at noon the “Tankadere” was within

[017-153] forty-five miles of Shanghai. There remained yet six hours in which to

[017-154] accomplish that distance. All on board feared that it could not be

[017-155] done, and every one—Phileas Fogg, no doubt, excepted—felt his heart

[017-156] beat with impatience. The boat must keep up an average of nine miles an

[017-157] hour, and the wind was becoming calmer every moment! It was a

[017-158] capricious breeze, coming from the coast, and after it passed the sea

[017-159] became smooth. Still, the “Tankadere” was so light, and her fine sails

[017-160] caught the fickle zephyrs so well, that, with the aid of the currents

[017-161] John Bunsby found himself at six o’clock not more than ten miles from

[017-162] the mouth of Shanghai River. Shanghai itself is situated at least

[017-163] twelve miles up the stream. At seven they were still three miles from

[017-164] Shanghai. The pilot swore an angry oath; the reward of two hundred

[017-165] pounds was evidently on the point of escaping him. He looked at Mr.

[017-166] Fogg. Mr. Fogg was perfectly tranquil; and yet his whole fortune was at

[017-167] this moment at stake.

[017-168] At this moment, also, a long black funnel, crowned with wreaths of

[017-169] smoke, appeared on the edge of the waters. It was the American steamer,

[017-170] leaving for Yokohama at the appointed time.

[017-171] “Confound her!” cried John Bunsby, pushing back the rudder with a

[017-172] desperate jerk.

[017-173] “Signal her!” said Phileas Fogg quietly.

[017-174] A small brass cannon stood on the forward deck of the “Tankadere,” for

[017-175] making signals in the fogs. It was loaded to the muzzle; but just as

[017-176] the pilot was about to apply a red-hot coal to the touchhole, Mr. Fogg

[017-177] said, “Hoist your flag!”

[017-178] The flag was run up at half-mast, and, this being the signal of

[017-179] distress, it was hoped that the American steamer, perceiving it, would

[017-180] change her course a little, so as to succour the pilot-boat.

[017-181] “Fire!” said Mr. Fogg. And the booming of the little cannon resounded

[017-182] in the air.
