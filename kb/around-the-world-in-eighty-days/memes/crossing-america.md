# The Crossing of America

The crossing of the American continent — from [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] to [[new-york|New York]] — is the longest continuous rail segment of Phileas Fogg's circumnavigation and among the most eventful. In the space of seven days, Fogg and his companions confront buffalo herds, a blustering antagonist, a Sioux war party, and the unforgiving Nebraska winter, improvising their way across a continent that seems determined to resist their crossing. The American leg crystallizes the novel's central argument: that precision can plan a journey, but only adaptability can complete one ([PN=025-001]–[PN=030-110]).

## Summary

Fogg and his party land at [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] on December 3rd and immediately board the transcontinental train eastward. The journey carries them through [[sacramento|Sacramento]], across the Sierra Nevada, through the Great Basin and past the Wasatch Range, and onto the Great Plains ([PN=021-001]–[PN=025-050]).

Three major disruptions define the crossing:

1. **The buffalo herd** (PN 025): A herd of ten thousand bison crosses the railroad tracks, stopping the train for several hours. The delay eats into Fogg's already narrow margin and foreshadows the greater obstacles to come ([PN=025-030]–[PN=025-060]).

2. **The confrontation with Proctor** (PN 026–027): [[colonel-proctor|Colonel Stamp W. Proctor]], a blustering American, provokes a quarrel during a whist game. A duel is arranged at [[fort-kearney|Fort Kearney]], but the train's departure interrupts before blows can be exchanged ([PN=026-001]–[PN=027-050]).

3. **The Sioux attack** (PN 028–029): A band of Sioux warriors attacks the train at full speed. [[passepartout|Passepartout]] crawls beneath the carriages to uncouple the locomotive, saving the passengers but becoming separated from the party. Fogg leads a rescue expedition from Fort Kearney, recovering Passepartout at the cost of two irrecoverable days ([PN=028-001]–[PN=029-110]).

The cumulative delays cause Fogg to miss the connecting train at the Nebraska station. Improvising, he hires a wind-powered sledge to cross the snowy plains to [[omaha|Omaha]], from which the party boards an express to [[chicago|Chicago]] and, ultimately, to [[new-york|New York]]. But a final misfortune awaits: the steamer to [[liverpool|Liverpool]] has already departed ([PN=030-001]–[PN=030-110]).

Fix's role during the crossing is notably ambivalent. Although he is still pursuing Fogg, he joins the rescue party for Passepartout — a moment of genuine humanity that complicates his character and foreshadows his eventual change of heart ([PN=029-080]–[PN=029-100]).

## The Sledge Journey

When Fogg misses the train at the Nebraska way-station, he demonstrates the resourcefulness that defines his character. Hiring a sledge equipped with a sail, he and his companions — plus the American guide [[mudge|Mudge]] — cross the frozen plains at remarkable speed:

> "It is a sledge with a sail," replied Fogg, "and it will carry us across the snow as if we were on the sea." ([PN=029-110]–[PN=030-020])

The sledge covers the distance to Omaha faster than any scheduled train could have managed, an irony that underscores Verne's thesis: ingenuity, not infrastructure, is the ultimate engine of progress.

## Thematic Significance

The American crossing foregrounds the novel's argument that infrastructure and technology, however advanced, are never entirely reliable. Fogg's precise calculations are repeatedly disrupted by nature (the buffalo herd), by violence (the Sioux attack), by human pettiness (Proctor's quarrel), and by simple ill-timing (the missed steamer at New York). The crossing is a test not merely of speed but of Fogg's capacity to improvise — the quality that Verne suggests distinguishes a true traveler from a mere passenger.

The America portrayed in the novel is a land of extremes: vast distances, wild nature, and a population that embodies both the best and worst of frontier democracy. From the cosmopolitan bustle of San Francisco to the eerie silence of the Nebraska plains, Verne paints a continent in transition — already threaded by the railway that symbolizes the nineteenth century's conquest of space, yet still untamed enough to swallow a traveler who miscalculates.
