# Transportation Modes

Phileas Fogg's journey around the world in eighty days was only possible because of the remarkable transportation infrastructure of the late nineteenth century: a global network of steamers, railways, and sailing vessels that had compressed the world into a navigable circuit.

## The Steam Revolution

The 1870s marked the high point of the steam age. Steamers crossing the oceans and railways spanning continents had radically shortened travel times. Fogg's itinerary — published in the *Daily Telegraph* — assumed seamless connections between these modes: from London to Suez by rail and steamer, from Suez to Bombay by steamer, across India by rail, and onward across the Pacific and the Atlantic. (PN: 003-035)

The Peninsular and Oriental Company's steamers, including the *Mongolia*, maintained regular schedules across the imperial routes. These vessels made "more than ten knots an hour" and connected the far-flung ports of the British Empire — Brindisi, Suez, Bombay, Singapore, Hong Kong — with a regularity that made Fogg's precise calculations possible. (PN: 004-100)

The timetables of steamships and railways were the calculus upon which Fogg's entire wager depended. Passepartout, bewildered by his master's obsession with schedules, observed that Fogg would spend his life "jumping from a steamer upon a railway train, and from a railway train upon a steamer again." (PN: 004-130)

## The Hierarchy of Conveyances

The novel presents a hierarchy of transportation, descending from the reliable to the desperate:

| Mode | Examples in the Novel | Reliability |
|------|----------------------|-------------|
| Railway | Great Indian Peninsula Railway, Pacific Railroad | High — but incomplete (the Allahabad gap) |
| Ocean Steamer | *Mongolia*, *Rangoon*, *General Grant* | High — barring weather and coal shortages |
| Sailing Vessel | *Tankadere*, *Henrietta* | Low — dependent on wind; used only in extremis |
| Elephant | Kiouni, the elephant purchased at Kholby | Improvised — the most unconventional conveyance |
| Sledge | Ice sledge across Nebraska | Improvised — the most desperate measure |

Each descent in this hierarchy marks a crisis in Fogg's journey. When the railway across India proves incomplete — the track between Kholby and Allahabad not yet laid — Fogg is forced to purchase an elephant and traverse the jungle. When the *Carnatic* departs without him, he charters the pilot-boat *Tankadere*. When coal runs out on the Atlantic crossing, he buys the *Henrietta* itself and burns its wooden fittings as fuel. The arc of the novel is, in part, the progressive abandonment of scheduled transport for desperate improvisation.

## Imperial Infrastructure

The transportation network that Fogg traverses was an infrastructure of empire. The railways crossing India linked Bombay to Calcutta, enabling the movement of goods, troops, and administrators. The Suez Canal, opened in 1869, had eliminated the long passage around the Cape of Good Hope, cutting the journey from Brindisi to Bombay to thirteen days. The Pacific Railroad, completed in 1869, had linked the American continent from coast to coast. Each of these engineering achievements was a precondition for Fogg's eighty-day circuit. (PN: 004-108)

Yet the infrastructure was also fragile. The railway across India, advertised as complete, was missing a fifty-mile stretch. The steamer schedules were thrown into chaos by storms and headwinds. The telegraph — the nervous system of empire — transmitted both the dispatch that launched Fix's pursuit and the warrant that nearly doomed Fogg in Liverpool. Transportation and communication were intertwined: the telegraph made the journey possible to track, and nearly impossible to complete.

> "It's all very well to plan a journey round the world, but it is after you have started that the difficulties begin." — Fix, reflecting on the fragility of timetabled travel (PN: 010-010)
