# The Route

Phileas Fogg's eighty-day route was first published as a thought-experiment in the *Morning Chronicle* (later referenced in the *Daily Telegraph*): a chain of railways and steamers that would, on paper, carry a traveller around the world in precisely eighty days. When Fogg wagered £20,000 that he could actually complete this journey, the route ceased to be a speculation and became a test of will, luck, and the reliability of Victorian transportation. (PN: 003-035)

## The Planned Itinerary

The route, as published, was as follows:

| From | To | Mode | Duration |
|------|----|------|----------|
| London | Brindisi | Railway and steamer | — |
| Brindisi | Bombay | Steamer (*Mongolia*) | 13 days |
| Bombay | Calcutta | Railway (Great Indian Peninsula) | 3 days |
| Calcutta | Hong Kong | Steamer (*Rangoon*) | 13 days |
| Hong Kong | Yokohama | Steamer (*Carnatic*) | 6 days |
| Yokohama | San Francisco | Steamer (*General Grant*) | 22 days |
| San Francisco | New York | Railway (Pacific Railroad) | 7 days |
| New York | London | Steamer (*China*) | 9 days |

Total: eighty days, assuming every connection is made precisely on schedule. (PN: 003-035)

## Deviations from the Plan

The actual journey diverged from the published route at nearly every stage. The railway across India was incomplete between Kholby and Allahabad, forcing Fogg to purchase an elephant and traverse fifty miles of jungle. The *Carnatic* departed Hong Kong without Fogg aboard, compelling him to charter the pilot-boat *Tankadere* to Shanghai. The transcontinental train was attacked by Sioux in Nebraska. The *China* was unavailable in New York, so Fogg commandeered the *Henrietta* — a cargo steamer — and burned its wooden fittings as fuel when the coal ran out.

The gap between the planned route and the actual journey is the novel's central dramatic tension. The route represents order; the journey represents chaos. Fogg's genius is that he navigates the chaos without ever abandoning the premise that the route is achievable.

## The Missing Day

The route's most famous feature is not a segment but a consequence: by travelling eastward, Fogg gains a day relative to London. Each time zone crossed eastward advances his clock by roughly an hour, and after crossing the International Date Line (though the term did not yet exist in 1872), he has accumulated a full twenty-four hours. Fogg, counting days by the sun he has seen, believes he has lost the wager when he arrives in London at what he thinks is 8:50 p.m. on Saturday, 21 December — five minutes late. Passepartout discovers the error: it is actually Friday, 20 December. The route, in a final irony, has given Fogg a day he did not know he possessed. (PN: 037-020)

## The Route as a Concept

The route is more than a list of places. It is a map of the nineteenth-century world: a world connected by British steamship lines, Indian railways, American transcontinental railroads, and the Suez Canal. Every segment of the route follows the infrastructure of empire. Fogg's journey demonstrates that the world had been made navigable — but only for those with the money, passports, and cultural fluency to navigate it.

> Fogg "had made a wager of twenty thousand pounds that he would accomplish the tour of the world in eighty days or less." (PN: 003-010)
