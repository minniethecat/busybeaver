[007-001] The passengers of the “Mongolia” went ashore at half-past four p.m.; at

[007-002] exactly eight the [[railway-travel|train]] would start for [[calcutta|Calcutta]].

[007-003] [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]], after bidding good-bye to his whist partners, left the

[007-004] [[steamer-travel|steamer]], gave his servant several errands to do, urged it upon him to

[007-005] be at the station promptly at eight, and, with his regular step, which

[007-006] beat to the second, like an astronomical [[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]], directed his steps to

[007-007] the [[passport-and-identity|passport]] office. As for the wonders of [[bombay|Bombay]]—its famous city hall,

[007-008] its splendid library, its forts and docks, its bazaars, mosques,

[007-009] synagogues, its Armenian churches, and the noble pagoda on Malabar

[007-010] Hill, with its two polygonal towers—he cared not a straw to see them.

[007-011] He would not deign to examine even the masterpieces of Elephanta, or

[007-012] the mysterious hypogea, concealed south-east from the docks, or those

[007-013] fine remains of Buddhist architecture, the Kanherian grottoes of the

[007-014] island of Salcette.

[007-015] Having transacted his business at the passport office, Phileas Fogg

[007-016] repaired quietly to the railway station, where he ordered dinner. Among

[007-017] the dishes served up to him, the landlord especially recommended a

[007-018] certain giblet of “native rabbit,” on which he prided himself.

[007-019] Mr. Fogg accordingly tasted the dish, but, despite its spiced sauce,

[007-020] found it far from palatable. He rang for the landlord, and, on his

[007-021] appearance, said, fixing his clear eyes upon him, “Is this rabbit,

[007-022] sir?”

[007-023] “Yes, my lord,” the rogue boldly replied, “rabbit from the jungles.”

[007-024] “And this rabbit did not mew when he was killed?”

[007-025] “Mew, my lord! What, a rabbit mew! I swear to you—”

[007-026] “Be so good, landlord, as not to swear, but remember this: cats were

[007-027] formerly considered, in India, as sacred animals. That was a good

[007-028] time.”

[007-029] “For the cats, my lord?”

[007-030] “Perhaps for the travellers as well!”

[007-031] After which Mr. Fogg quietly continued his dinner. [[detective-fix|Fix]] had gone on

[007-032] shore shortly after Mr. Fogg, and his first destination was the

[007-033] headquarters of the Bombay police. He made himself known as a [[london|London]]

[007-034] detective, told his business at Bombay, and the position of affairs

[007-035] relative to the supposed robber, and nervously asked if a warrant had

[007-036] arrived from London. It had not reached the office; indeed, there had

[007-037] not yet been time for it to arrive. [[fix-detective|Fix]] was sorely disappointed, and

[007-038] tried to obtain an order of arrest from the director of the Bombay

[007-039] police. This the director refused, as the matter concerned the London

[007-040] office, which alone could legally deliver the warrant. Fix did not

[007-041] insist, and was fain to resign himself to await the arrival of the

[007-042] important document; but he was determined not to lose sight of the

[007-043] mysterious rogue as long as he stayed in Bombay. He did not doubt for a

[007-044] moment, any more than [[passepartout|Passepartout]], that Phileas Fogg would remain

[007-045] there, at least until it was time for the warrant to arrive.

[007-046] Passepartout, however, had no sooner heard his master’s orders on

[007-047] leaving the “Mongolia” than he saw at once that they were to leave

[007-048] Bombay as they had done [[suez|Suez]] and Paris, and that the journey would be

[007-049] extended at least as far as Calcutta, and perhaps beyond that place. He

[007-050] began to ask himself if this [[the-wager|bet]] that Mr. Fogg talked about was not

[007-051] really in good earnest, and whether his fate was not in truth forcing

[007-052] him, despite his love of repose, around the world in eighty days!

[007-053] Having purchased the usual quota of shirts and shoes, he took a

[007-054] leisurely promenade about the streets, where crowds of people of many

[007-055] nationalities—Europeans, Persians with pointed caps, Banyas with round

[007-056] turbans, Sindes with square bonnets, Parsees with black mitres, and

[007-057] long-robed Armenians—were collected. It happened to be the day of a

[007-058] Parsee festival. These descendants of the sect of Zoroaster—the most

[007-059] thrifty, civilised, intelligent, and austere of the East Indians, among

[007-060] whom are counted the richest native merchants of Bombay—were

[007-061] celebrating a sort of religious carnival, with processions and shows,

[007-062] in the midst of which Indian dancing-girls, clothed in rose-coloured

[007-063] gauze, looped up with gold and silver, danced airily, but with perfect

[007-064] modesty, to the sound of viols and the clanging of tambourines. It is

[007-065] needless to say that Passepartout watched these curious ceremonies with

[007-066] staring eyes and gaping mouth, and that his countenance was that of the

[007-067] greenest booby imaginable.

[007-068] Unhappily for his master, as well as himself, his curiosity drew him

[007-069] unconsciously farther off than he intended to go. At last, having seen

[007-070] the Parsee carnival wind away in the distance, he was turning his steps

[007-071] towards the station, when he happened to espy the splendid pagoda on

[007-072] Malabar Hill, and was seized with an irresistible desire to see its

[007-073] interior. He was quite ignorant that it is forbidden to Christians to

[007-074] enter certain Indian temples, and that even the faithful must not go in

[007-075] without first leaving their shoes outside the door. It may be said here

[007-076] that the wise policy of the British Government severely punishes a

[007-077] disregard of the practices of the native religions.

[007-078] Passepartout, however, thinking no harm, went in like a simple tourist,

[007-079] and was soon lost in admiration of the splendid Brahmin ornamentation

[007-080] which everywhere met his eyes, when of a sudden he found himself

[007-081] sprawling on the sacred flagging. He looked up to behold three enraged

[007-082] priests, who forthwith fell upon him; tore off his shoes, and began to

[007-083] beat him with loud, savage exclamations. The agile Frenchman was soon

[007-084] upon his feet again, and lost no time in knocking down two of his

[007-085] long-gowned adversaries with his fists and a vigorous application of

[007-086] his toes; then, rushing out of the pagoda as fast as his legs could

[007-087] carry him, he soon escaped the third priest by mingling with the crowd

[007-088] in the streets.

[007-089] At five minutes before eight, Passepartout, hatless, shoeless, and

[007-090] having in the squabble lost his package of shirts and shoes, rushed

[007-091] breathlessly into the station.

[007-092] Fix, who had followed Mr. Fogg to the station, and saw that he was

[007-093] really going to leave Bombay, was there, upon the platform. He had

[007-094] resolved to follow the supposed robber to Calcutta, and farther, if

[007-095] necessary. Passepartout did not observe the detective, who stood in an

[007-096] obscure corner; but Fix heard him relate his adventures in a few words

[007-097] to Mr. Fogg.

[007-098] “I hope that this will not happen again,” said Phileas Fogg coldly, as

[007-099] he got into the train. Poor Passepartout, quite crestfallen, followed

[007-100] his master without a word. Fix was on the point of entering another

[007-101] carriage, when an idea struck him which induced him to alter his plan.

[007-102] “No, I’ll stay,” muttered he. “An offence has been committed on Indian

[007-103] soil. I’ve got my man.”

[007-104] Just then the locomotive gave a sharp screech, and the train passed out

[007-105] into the darkness of the night.

[007-106] CHAPTER XI.

[007-107] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG SECURES A CURIOUS MEANS OF CONVEYANCE AT A

[007-108] FABULOUS PRICE

[007-109] The train had started punctually. Among the passengers were a number of

[007-110] officers, Government officials, and opium and indigo merchants, whose

[007-111] business called them to the eastern coast. Passepartout rode in the

[007-112] same carriage with his master, and a third passenger occupied a seat

[007-113] opposite to them. This was [[sir-francis-cromarty|Sir Francis Cromarty]], one of Mr. Fogg’s

[007-114] whist partners on the “Mongolia,” now on his way to join his corps at

[007-115] Benares. Sir Francis was a tall, fair man of fifty, who had greatly

[007-116] distinguished himself in the last Sepoy revolt. He made India his home,

[007-117] only paying brief visits to England at rare intervals; and was almost

[007-118] as familiar as a native with the customs, history, and character of

[007-119] India and its people. But Phileas Fogg, who was not travelling, but

[007-120] only describing a circumference, took no pains to inquire into these

[007-121] subjects; he was a solid body, traversing an orbit around the

[007-122] terrestrial globe, according to the laws of rational mechanics. He was

[007-123] at this moment calculating in his mind the number of hours spent since

[007-124] his departure from London, and, had it been in his nature to make a

[007-125] useless demonstration, would have rubbed his hands for satisfaction.

[007-126] Sir Francis Cromarty had observed the oddity of his travelling

[007-127] companion—although the only opportunity he had for studying him had

[007-128] been while he was dealing the cards, and between two rubbers—and

[007-129] questioned himself whether a human heart really beat beneath this cold

[007-130] exterior, and whether Phileas Fogg had any sense of the beauties of

[007-131] nature. The brigadier-general was free to mentally confess that, of all

[007-132] the eccentric persons he had ever met, none was comparable to this

[007-133] product of the exact sciences.

[007-134] Phileas Fogg had not concealed from Sir Francis his design of going

[007-135] round the world, nor the circumstances under which he set out; and the

[007-136] general only saw in the wager a useless eccentricity and a lack of

[007-137] sound common sense. In the way this strange [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] was going on, he

[007-138] would leave the world without having done any good to himself or

[007-139] anybody else.

[007-140] An hour after leaving Bombay the train had passed the viaducts and the

[007-141] Island of Salcette, and had got into the open country. At Callyan they

[007-142] reached the junction of the branch line which descends towards

[007-143] south-eastern India by Kandallah and Pounah; and, passing Pauwell, they

[007-144] entered the defiles of the mountains, with their basalt bases, and

[007-145] their summits crowned with thick and verdant forests. Phileas Fogg and

[007-146] Sir Francis Cromarty exchanged a few words from time to time, and now

[007-147] Sir Francis, reviving the conversation, observed, “Some years ago, Mr.

[007-148] Fogg, you would have met with a delay at this point which would

[007-149] probably have lost you your wager.”

[007-150] “How so, Sir Francis?”

[007-151] “Because the railway stopped at the base of these mountains, which the

[007-152] passengers were obliged to cross in palanquins or on ponies to

[007-153] Kandallah, on the other side.”

[007-154] “Such a delay would not have deranged my plans in the least,” said Mr.

[007-155] Fogg. “I have constantly foreseen the likelihood of certain obstacles.”

[007-156] “But, Mr. Fogg,” pursued Sir Francis, “you run the risk of having some

[007-157] difficulty about this worthy fellow’s [[spirit-of-adventure|adventure]] at the pagoda.”

[007-158] Passepartout, his feet comfortably wrapped in his travelling-blanket,

[007-159] was sound asleep and did not dream that anybody was talking about him.

[007-160] “The Government is very severe upon that kind of offence. It takes

[007-161] particular care that the religious customs of the Indians should be

[007-162] respected, and if your servant were caught—”

[007-163] “Very well, Sir Francis,” replied Mr. Fogg; “if he had been caught he

[007-164] would have been condemned and punished, and then would have quietly

[007-165] returned to Europe. I don’t see how this affair could have delayed his

[007-166] master.”

[007-167] The conversation fell again. During the night the train left the

[007-168] mountains behind, and passed Nassik, and the next day proceeded over

[007-169] the flat, well-cultivated country of the Khandeish, with its straggling

[007-170] villages, above which rose the minarets of the pagodas. This fertile

[007-171] territory is watered by numerous small rivers and limpid streams,

[007-172] mostly tributaries of the Godavery.

[007-173] Passepartout, on waking and looking out, could not realise that he was

[007-174] actually crossing India in a railway train. The locomotive, guided by

[007-175] an English engineer and fed with English coal, threw out its smoke upon

[007-176] cotton, coffee, nutmeg, clove, and pepper plantations, while the steam

[007-177] curled in spirals around groups of palm-trees, in the midst of which

[007-178] were seen picturesque bungalows, viharis (sort of abandoned

[007-179] monasteries), and marvellous temples enriched by the exhaustless

[007-180] ornamentation of Indian architecture. Then they came upon vast tracts

[007-181] extending to the horizon, with jungles inhabited by snakes and tigers,

[007-182] which fled at the noise of the train; succeeded by forests penetrated

[007-183] by the railway, and still haunted by elephants which, with pensive

[007-184] eyes, gazed at the train as it passed. The travellers crossed, beyond

[007-185] Milligaum, the fatal country so often stained with blood by the

[007-186] sectaries of the goddess Kali. Not far off rose Ellora, with its

[007-187] graceful pagodas, and the famous Aurungabad, capital of the ferocious

[007-188] Aureng-Zeb, now the chief town of one of the detached provinces of the

[007-189] kingdom of the Nizam. It was thereabouts that Feringhea, the Thuggee

[007-190] chief, king of the stranglers, held his sway. These ruffians, united by

[007-191] a secret bond, strangled victims of every age in honour of the goddess

[007-192] Death, without ever shedding blood; there was a period when this part

[007-193] of the country could scarcely be travelled over without corpses being

[007-194] found in every direction. The English Government has succeeded in

[007-195] greatly diminishing these murders, though the Thuggees still exist, and

[007-196] pursue the exercise of their horrible rites.
