# The Fictional Geography of Eighty Days

Phileas Fogg's itinerary is often celebrated as a triumph of nineteenth-century transportation infrastructure — a journey made possible by railways, steamers, and the recently-opened Suez Canal. But a closer examination reveals that Verne's geography is as much fictional construction as documentary report. The route Fogg follows was not quite possible in 1872; its very impossibility is part of the novel's meaning.

## The Published Route and Its Problems

The *Daily Telegraph* article that triggers the wager lays out a plausible itinerary: London to Suez via Mont Cenis (rail and steamer), Suez to Bombay (steamer), Bombay to Calcutta (rail), Calcutta to Hong Kong (steamer), Hong Kong to Yokohama (steamer), Yokohama to San Francisco (steamer), San Francisco to New York (rail), New York to London (steamer and rail). On paper, the math works: eighty days, give or take. (PN: 003-027, 003-036)

But the published timetable assumed conditions that did not exist in 1872. The Indian railway from Bombay to Calcutta, though largely complete, had a critical gap between Kholby and Allahabad — rails laid but not yet connected. The transcontinental railroad across America, completed at Promontory Summit in 1869, was operational but still vulnerable to weather, mechanical failure, and — as the novel dramatizes — buffalo herds on the tracks. (PN: 022-010)

## The Gaps Verne Chose

Verne was not ignorant of these gaps. He built the novel around them.

The Kholby-Allahabad gap is the first and most explicit rupture in Fogg's timetable. Informed that the railway is incomplete, Fogg does not hesitate: he purchases an elephant, hires a Parsee guide, and traverses the jungle. The elephant, named Kiouni, becomes both a practical solution and a symbolic rejection of technological dependence. (PN: 007-174) The gap in the railway is the gap through which adventure enters the novel.

The second major departure is the Atlantic crossing. Fogg misses the Liverpool steamer at New York — a failure of the scheduled infrastructure — and responds by purchasing the Henrietta, a small merchant vessel bound for Bordeaux. When coal runs short, he buys the ship itself and burns its wooden superstructure as fuel. (PN: 029-011) This episode has no historical basis; no merchant captain in 1872 would have allowed his ship to be disassembled mid-ocean for fuel. It is pure fantasy — and the novel's most exhilarating sequence.

## What the Route Reveals

Verne's geographical choices reveal his narrative priorities. The route favors British imperial infrastructure (P&O steamers, Indian railways) over alternatives that existed in 1872. Fogg could have crossed Russia via the Trans-Siberian telegraph route; he could have taken French shipping lines through Indochina. Neither option is mentioned. The world of the novel is the world as seen from London — a network of British-administered junctions connected by British-managed transport.

The American leg is the exception that proves the rule. Once Fogg enters the United States, the infrastructure is recognizably modern (the transcontinental railroad) but the environment is more chaotic: political brawls in San Francisco, a Mormon sermon in Utah, a buffalo herd that stops the train. (PN: 020-015, 022-010) America represents both the triumph of technology and its entanglement with the violent energies of the frontier. It is the least Victorian segment of the journey and, perhaps for that reason, the most vividly rendered.

## The Date Line as Geographical Surprise

The novel's most famous geographical device — the International Date Line — was not an established concept in 1872. Time zones were not internationally standardized until 1884, and the Date Line itself remained informal for decades after. Fogg's "discovery" that he has gained a day by traveling east (PN: 031-035) was scientifically correct but not widely understood by Verne's readers. The novel functions as a popular-science demonstration: a thought experiment about what happens when personal timekeeping collides with planetary scale.

The Date Line twist also serves a narrative purpose. It transforms Fogg's apparent failure into triumph without requiring him to do anything differently. The rescue of Aouda, which cost twelve hours, is vindicated by an astronomical accident. The novel's moral logic — doing the right thing pays unexpected dividends — is underwritten by celestial mechanics.
