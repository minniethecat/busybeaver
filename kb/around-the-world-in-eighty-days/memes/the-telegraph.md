# The Telegraph

While Fogg races around the world at the speed of steam, information travels at the speed of electricity. The telegraph is the invisible antagonist of the novel — the technology that allows Detective Fix to pursue his quarry across continents, always one step ahead of the physical journey.

## Definition

The electric telegraph, developed in the 1830s and 1840s, enabled near-instantaneous communication across vast distances through a network of wires, cables, and relay stations. By 1872, telegraph lines spanned Europe, crossed the Atlantic via submarine cable (completed 1866), and reached into India, China, and parts of the Americas.

In the novel, the telegraph functions as an instrument of surveillance and control. The Bank of England telegraphs Fix's arrest warrant to Bombay; Fix telegraphs his superiors from Suez; news of Fogg's progress is telegraphed to newspapers in London. Information, in Verne's world, moves faster than any train or steamer — a fact that makes Fogg's predicament more precarious with every mile.

## Role in the Novel

The telegraph creates the central dramatic irony of Fix's pursuit. Fix can communicate ahead of Fogg's arrival, preparing to intercept him at each port, but he cannot arrest Fogg until they are on British soil where the warrant is valid. The telegraph thus keeps Fix perpetually one step ahead but perpetually powerless — a pursuer who knows exactly where his quarry will be but cannot act on that knowledge.

The telegraph also connects the journey to the wider world. London follows Fogg's progress through telegraphed dispatches; the wager becomes a matter of public speculation; and Fogg's apparent success or failure is known in England almost as soon as it occurs. The world shrinks not only through transportation but through communication.

> "The _Daily Telegraph_ says that he is a gentleman." (002-144)

## Historical & Cultural Context

The first successful transatlantic telegraph cable was laid in 1866, just six years before Fogg's journey. It was one of the great engineering achievements of the age — a project championed by Cyrus Field and built by the Great Eastern, the largest ship ever constructed at the time. The cable reduced communication time between London and New York from ten days (by ship) to a few minutes.

Verne understood that the telegraph represented a revolution as profound as the railway or steamer. In making the telegraph central to Fix's pursuit, he dramatizes a truth about the modern world: the ability to know and the ability to act operate at different speeds. Fix knows where Fogg is, but he cannot reach him in time to act on that knowledge — a predicament that remains familiar in the age of the internet.
