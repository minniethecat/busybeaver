# Transcontinental Railway

The railroad spanning the North American continent from the Pacific coast to the Atlantic — completed in 1869, only three years before [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]'s journey — that provided the crucial American link in his eighty-day circumnavigation.

## The Route

Fogg boards the transcontinental train at [[san-francisco|San Francisco]], bound for [[new-york|New York]] via [[omaha|Omaha]] and [[chicago|Chicago]]. Verne describes the journey in detail, noting the vast distances involved: between Omaha and the Pacific the railway crosses a still-wild country of nearly nine hundred miles. The track winds through mountain passes, across plains, and alongside rivers, with the narrator observing that there were "few or no bridges or tunnels on the route."

## Completion and Historical Context

The First Transcontinental Railroad was completed on May 10, 1869, when the Union Pacific and Central Pacific lines met at Promontory Summit, Utah Territory. Its completion was one of the defining technological achievements of the nineteenth century, shrinking a journey that had previously taken months by wagon or ship (around Cape Horn) to a matter of days by rail.

For Verne, writing in 1872, the railroad was so new that its inclusion in the novel was almost journalistic — a report on the latest advance in global connectivity. Fogg's ability to include an American rail crossing in his itinerary was a direct consequence of a technological feat that had been accomplished within living memory.

## Obstacles on the Line

The American leg of Fogg's journey is not without incident. The train is attacked by Sioux warriors, who derail part of the train and temporarily halt Fogg's progress. This episode dramatizes the tension between technological infrastructure and the unsettled territories through which it passed. The railroad may have connected the coasts, but the land it crossed remained contested.

After the Sioux attack, Fogg is forced to complete the journey using an improvised sledge with sails — a striking juxtaposition of the most modern transport (the railway) being replaced by the most elemental (wind power across snow). This detour nearly costs him the wager.

## Significance

The transcontinental railway was, alongside the Suez Canal and the Indian railways, one of the three great infrastructure projects that made eighty-day circumnavigation thinkable. Together, they represented the closing of the world — the transformation of geography into timetable.
