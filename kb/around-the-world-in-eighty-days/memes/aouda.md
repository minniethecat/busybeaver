# Aouda

Aouda enters the novel as a victim to be rescued and leaves it as the agent of Fogg's emotional awakening — the young Parsi widow whose journey from the funeral pyre of Bundelkhand to the parlors of Savile Row traces one of literature's most improbable and satisfying arcs.

## Role in the Novel

Aouda is introduced in the Indian jungle, where a procession is preparing to consign her to the flames of her deceased husband's funeral pyre — the outlawed practice of suttee. Fogg, informed by Passepartout, declares that he has "twelve hours to spare" and diverts the expedition to rescue her. (PN: 012-052, 012-056)

Her role evolves from rescued stranger to essential companion. As the journey continues, Aouda's intelligence, grace, and quiet courage win the respect of all — including, eventually, the affections of the man who rescued her. By the novel's end, it is Aouda who proposes marriage to Fogg, reversing the conventional romantic script and revealing that the true journey has been Fogg's emotional awakening. (PN: 030-175, 031-170)

## Biography

Aouda is the daughter of a wealthy Parsi merchant of Bombay, educated in English and accustomed to European society. She is a relative of the renowned Parsi philanthropist Sir Jamsetjee Jejeebhoy. (PN: 012-160, 012-164) Her marriage to an aged rajah of Bundelkhand was arranged — a common practice among Indian princely families seeking alliance with Bombay's commercial elite. When the rajah died, his relatives sought to dispose of his young widow through suttee, both to avoid the obligation of her maintenance and to claim her inheritance. She was drugged with opium and hemp by the priests of Pillaji. (PN: 011-010)

After her rescue, Aouda accompanies Fogg to Hong Kong, Yokohama, San Francisco, and across America and the Atlantic to England. Her presence transforms the journey from a wager into a human story, and her eventual marriage to Fogg provides the novel with its emotional and structural completion.

## Key Actions

- Faces death by suttee in the Indian jungle, accepting her fate with drugged composure (PN: 012-045, 011-010)
- Travels with Fogg's party across three continents, adapting with grace to each new circumstance (PN: 012-112, 012-145)
- Learns of the wager and smiles — the first open acknowledgment of the absurdity at the heart of Fogg's enterprise (PN: 012-156)
- Offers to stay behind in Hong Kong rather than compromise Fogg's journey — a gesture of selflessness (PN: 014-162)
- Endures the Sioux attack on the transcontinental railroad with "courage from the first" (PN: 025-154)
- Grows "pale as death" during Fogg's confrontation with Colonel Proctor on the train (PN: 025-091)
- Proposes marriage to Phileas Fogg at the novel's conclusion, transforming the relationship from obligation to love (PN: 030-175, 030-181)
- Receives Fogg's simple declaration — "I love you!" — one of the novel's most emotionally direct moments (PN: 030-182)

## Character Significance

Aouda is often dismissed as a passive figure — the rescued damsel of Victorian adventure fiction. But this reading underestimates her agency. It is Aouda who first smiles at the absurdity of the wager, puncturing Fogg's solemnity; it is Aouda who offers to sacrifice her own safety for Fogg's schedule; it is Aouda who, at the novel's end, proposes marriage — an act of remarkable initiative for a Victorian heroine. (PN: 012-156, 014-162, 030-175)

She represents the human reward that Fogg's mechanical precision cannot account for. He calculates time to the minute; she cannot be calculated. The novel's ultimate wisdom is that Fogg, who plans everything, did not plan for Aouda — and is the richer for it. As Fogg himself reflects at the conclusion, "If I had not crossed India, I should not have saved Aouda; she would not have been my wife." (PN: 031-189)

> "At the random mercy of encountering some one who would understand her, she told her story in brief words." (PN: 012-115)
