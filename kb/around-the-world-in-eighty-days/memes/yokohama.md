# Yokohama

Yokohama is the most improbable setting in Fogg's journey — the city where his French servant becomes a Japanese acrobat, and where the novel's comic and sentimental registers achieve their fullest expression in a single, ridiculous moment on a theatre stage.

## Role in the Novel

Yokohama enters the narrative as a destination of necessity. After Passepartout, drugged by Fix in Hong Kong, misses the sailing of the *Carnatic*, he awakens to find himself alone and penniless in a foreign city. (PN: 019-001) Selling his European clothes for a Japanese costume, he wanders the streets in desperate search of passage to America.

A placard advertising "The Honourable William Batulcar's" troupe — acrobats, jugglers, and gymnasts bound for the United States — catches his eye. (PN: 019-069) Batulcar, recognizing Passepartout as a Frenchman, engages him as a clown and acrobat: "You can sing standing on your head, with a top spinning on your left foot, and a sabre balanced on your right?" (PN: 019-104)

The climactic performance — a "human pyramid" of Long Noses representing the Car of Juggernaut — is Yokohama's unforgettable set piece. Fifty performers, their faces adorned with bamboo noses up to ten feet long, stack themselves in tiers on one another's prosthetic appendages. (PN: 019-147) Passepartout, wearing a six-foot false nose, forms the base of the pyramid. As the orchestra builds to a climax, he spots Fogg and Aouda in the audience. Abandoning his position, he topples the entire structure, scrambles over the footlights, and lands at his master's feet, crying, "Ah, my master! my master!" (PN: 019-181)

Fogg's reply is characteristically terse: "Very well; then let us go to the steamer, young man!" (PN: 019-183) The Honourable Mr. Batulcar, "furious with rage," is placated with a handful of banknotes. (PN: 019-186) At half-past six, Fogg, Aouda, and a Passepartout still wearing his wings and six-foot nose board the American steamer *General Grant* bound for San Francisco. (PN: 019-189)

## Journey Context

Yokohama arrives at the novel's midpoint, when Fogg's fortunes seem darkest. Passepartout is lost; the wager appears doomed. Yet Yokohama proves to be the setting for one of the book's happiest reversals — the reunion of master and servant in circumstances of pure farce.

The juxtaposition is quintessential Verne: Fogg's mechanical precision meets Passepartout's chaotic humanity, and the result is a collapsed pyramid, a furious impresario, and a servant who crosses the footlights still wearing his wings. That this scene is also deeply moving — Passepartout's cry of recognition is one of the novel's emotional peaks — demonstrates Verne's mastery of tone. Yokohama is at once the novel's most comic and its most heartfelt location.

## Historical and Cultural Context

In 1872, Yokohama had been open to foreign trade for only thirteen years, since the Treaty of Amity and Commerce of 1858 effectively ended Japan's two centuries of sakoku (isolation). The city was the principal treaty port and the gateway through which Western influence entered Japan. Verne's depiction of Yokohama as a city of "jugglers, clowns, acrobats" and exotic performances reflects the European fascination with Japanese culture — Japonisme — that swept through France in the 1860s and 1870s.

The Long Noses, with their bamboo prosthetics up to ten feet in length, are likely Verne's invention, but they draw on European stereotypes of Japanese theatre and the fascination with the grotesque that characterized much Western representation of Japan at the time. The god Tingou is also a fabrication, a playful nod to the Western tendency to exoticize Eastern religious practice.
