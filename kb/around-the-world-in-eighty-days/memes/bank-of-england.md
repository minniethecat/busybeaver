# The Bank of England

The institution whose robbery of fifty-five thousand pounds sets the entire plot in motion. A package of banknotes taken from the Bank's counters -- and a description that happens to match Phileas Fogg -- triggers Detective Fix's transcontinental pursuit, which becomes the novel's central narrative engine.

## Role in the Novel

The Bank of England robbery is the precipitating event of the entire story. On the day of the Reform Club wager discussion, the gentlemen -- several of them bank officials (002-187) -- are preoccupied with a sensational crime. Three days earlier, a package of banknotes worth fifty-five thousand pounds had been taken from the Bank's counter (002-153/002-154). The Daily Telegraph reports that the thief is "a well-dressed gentleman of polished manners" (002-180) -- precisely the profile that would later be projected onto Fogg.

The robbery reveals the Bank's institutional culture and the particular vulnerabilities of Victorian banking. The Bank of England in this period "reposes a touching confidence in the honesty of the public," as Verne sardonically notes: "gold, silver, banknotes are freely exposed, at the mercy of the first comer" (002-158/002-160). There are no guards, no clerks demanding receipts; anyone could walk up to a counter, take a package of notes, and walk out. This culture of trust -- a hallmark of gentlemanly capitalism -- is what makes the fifty-five thousand pound theft possible.

The Bank's response is swift and global. "As soon as the robbery was discovered, picked detectives hastened off to Liverpool, Glasgow, Havre, Suez, Brindisi, New York, and other ports" (002-171/002-172), motivated by a reward of two thousand pounds and five percent of any recovered sum (002-173). Fix is one of these picked detectives, dispatched to Suez because India is seen as a logical destination for a fugitive seeking to escape English justice (004-183).

The ironic resolution arrives in Section 30: the real bank robber, a man named James Strand, was arrested in Edinburgh on December 17th -- three days before Fogg reaches Liverpool (030-200). Strand's arrest vindicates Fogg entirely, though it arrives so late in the narrative that Fogg has already been imprisoned at the Liverpool Custom House and apparently lost the wager.

## Historical Context

The real Bank of England, founded in 1694, was by Verne's time the central bank of the world's largest empire and the anchor of the international gold standard. Its Threadneedle Street headquarters, known as "The Old Lady of Threadneedle Street," symbolized British financial stability and global commercial power. The novel's plot depends on the Bank's institutional reach: a robbery in London triggers a manhunt that extends to Egypt, India, Hong Kong, Japan, and America. This global enforcement capability was a genuine feature of British imperial power, supported by the telegraph network and the consular system that Verne depicts.

The sum of fifty-five thousand pounds in 1872 was enormous -- equivalent to several million pounds in modern currency. That such a sum could simply be lifted from a counter, and that the thief's departure from London could be so rapid that detectives were immediately dispatched globally, reflects contemporary anxieties about the vulnerability of gentlemanly institutions to imposture and fraud.

One of the Reform Club's whist players, Gauthier Ralph, is himself "one of the Directors of the Bank of England" (002-133), providing a direct link between the robbery investigation and the club where Fogg's wager is debated. This detail underscores the novel's tight integration of finance, empire, and the gentlemanly social world.
