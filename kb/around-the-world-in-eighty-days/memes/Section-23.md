[023-001] Several of the audience, not being much interested in [[reverend-wilson|the missionary]]’s

[023-002] narrative, here left the car; but [[elder-hitch|Elder Hitch]], continuing his lecture,

[023-003] related how Smith, junior, with his father, two brothers, and a few

[023-004] disciples, founded the church of the “Latter Day Saints,” which,

[023-005] adopted not only in America, but in England, Norway and Sweden, and

[023-006] Germany, counts many artisans, as well as men engaged in the liberal

[023-007] professions, among its members; how a colony was established in Ohio, a

[023-008] temple erected there at a cost of two hundred thousand dollars, and a

[023-009] town built at Kirkland; how Smith became an enterprising banker, and

[023-010] received from a simple mummy showman a papyrus scroll written by

[023-011] Abraham and several famous Egyptians.

[023-012] The Elder’s story became somewhat wearisome, and his audience grew

[023-013] gradually less, until it was reduced to twenty passengers. But this did

[023-014] not disconcert the enthusiast, who proceeded with the story of Joseph

[023-015] Smith’s bankruptcy in 1837, and how his ruined creditors gave him a

[023-016] coat of tar and feathers; his reappearance some years afterwards, more

[023-017] honourable and honoured than ever, at Independence, Missouri, the chief

[023-018] of a flourishing colony of three thousand disciples, and his pursuit

[023-019] thence by outraged Gentiles, and retirement into the Far West.

[023-020] Ten hearers only were now left, among them honest [[passepartout|Passepartout]], who was

[023-021] listening with all his ears. Thus he learned that, after long

[023-022] persecutions, Smith reappeared in Illinois, and in 1839 founded a

[023-023] community at Nauvoo, on the Mississippi, numbering twenty-five thousand

[023-024] souls, of which he became mayor, chief justice, and general-in-chief;

[023-025] that he announced himself, in 1843, as a candidate for the Presidency

[023-026] of the United States; and that finally, being drawn into ambuscade at

[023-027] Carthage, he was thrown into prison, and assassinated by a band of men

[023-028] disguised in masks.

[023-029] Passepartout was now the only person left in the car, and the Elder,

[023-030] looking him full in the face, reminded him that, two years after the

[023-031] assassination of Joseph Smith, the inspired prophet, Brigham Young, his

[023-032] successor, left Nauvoo for the banks of the Great Salt Lake, where, in

[023-033] the midst of that fertile region, directly on the [[around-the-world-route|route]] of the

[023-034] emigrants who crossed Utah on their way to California, the new colony,

[023-035] thanks to the polygamy practised by the Mormons, had flourished beyond

[023-036] expectations.

[023-037] “And this,” added Elder William Hitch, “this is why the jealousy of

[023-038] Congress has been aroused against us! Why have the soldiers of the

[023-039] Union invaded the soil of Utah? Why has Brigham Young, our chief, been

[023-040] imprisoned, in contempt of all justice? Shall we yield to force? Never!

[023-041] Driven from Vermont, driven from Illinois, driven from Ohio, driven

[023-042] from Missouri, driven from Utah, we shall yet find some independent

[023-043] territory on which to plant our tents. And you, my brother,” continued

[023-044] the Elder, fixing his angry eyes upon his single auditor, “will you not

[023-045] plant yours there, too, under the shadow of our flag?”

[023-046] “No!” replied Passepartout courageously, in his turn retiring from the

[023-047] car, and leaving the Elder to preach to vacancy.

[023-048] During the lecture the [[railway-travel|train]] had been making good progress, and towards

[023-049] half-past twelve it reached the northwest border of the Great Salt

[023-050] Lake. Thence the passengers could observe the vast extent of this

[023-051] interior sea, which is also called the Dead Sea, and into which flows

[023-052] an American Jordan. It is a picturesque expanse, framed in lofty crags

[023-053] in large strata, encrusted with white salt—a superb sheet of water,

[023-054] which was formerly of larger extent than now, its shores having

[023-055] encroached with the lapse of time, and thus at once reduced its breadth

[023-056] and increased its depth.

[023-057] The Salt Lake, seventy miles long and thirty-five wide, is situated

[023-058] three miles eight hundred feet above the sea. Quite different from Lake

[023-059] Asphaltite, whose depression is twelve hundred feet below the sea, it

[023-060] contains considerable salt, and one quarter of the weight of its water

[023-061] is solid matter, its specific weight being 1,170, and, after being

[023-062] distilled, 1,000. Fishes are, of course, unable to live in it, and

[023-063] those which descend through the Jordan, the Weber, and other streams

[023-064] soon perish.

[023-065] The country around the lake was well cultivated, for the Mormons are

[023-066] mostly farmers; while ranches and pens for domesticated animals, fields

[023-067] of wheat, corn, and other cereals, luxuriant prairies, hedges of wild

[023-068] rose, clumps of acacias and milk-wort, would have been seen six months

[023-069] later. Now the ground was covered with a thin powdering of snow.

[023-070] The train reached Ogden at two o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]], where it rested for six hours,

[023-071] [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]] and his party had time to pay a visit to Salt Lake City,

[023-072] connected with Ogden by a branch road; and they spent two hours in this

[023-073] strikingly American town, built on the pattern of other cities of the

[023-074] Union, like a checker-board, “with the sombre sadness of right-angles,”

[023-075] as Victor Hugo expresses it. The founder of the City of the Saints

[023-076] could not escape from the taste for symmetry which distinguishes the

[023-077] Anglo-Saxons. In this strange country, where the people are certainly

[023-078] not up to the level of their institutions, everything is done

[023-079] “squarely”—cities, houses, and follies.

[023-080] The travellers, then, were promenading, at three o’clock, about the

[023-081] streets of the town built between the banks of the Jordan and the spurs

[023-082] of the Wahsatch Range. They saw few or no churches, but the prophet’s

[023-083] mansion, the court-house, and the arsenal, blue-brick houses with

[023-084] verandas and porches, surrounded by gardens bordered with acacias,

[023-085] palms, and locusts. A clay and pebble wall, built in 1853, surrounded

[023-086] the town; and in the principal street were the market and several

[023-087] hotels adorned with pavilions. The place did not seem thickly

[023-088] populated. The streets were almost deserted, except in the vicinity of

[023-089] the temple, which they only reached after having traversed several

[023-090] quarters surrounded by palisades. There were many women, which was

[023-091] easily accounted for by the “peculiar institution” of the Mormons; but

[023-092] it must not be supposed that all the Mormons are polygamists. They are

[023-093] free to marry or not, as they please; but it is worth noting that it is

[023-094] mainly the female citizens of Utah who are anxious to marry, as,

[023-095] according to the Mormon religion, maiden ladies are not admitted to the

[023-096] possession of its highest joys. These poor creatures seemed to be

[023-097] neither well off nor happy. Some—the more well-to-do, no doubt—wore

[023-098] short, open, black silk dresses, under a hood or modest shawl; others

[023-099] were habited in Indian fashion.

[023-100] Passepartout could not behold without a certain fright these women,

[023-101] charged, in groups, with conferring happiness on a single Mormon. His

[023-102] common sense pitied, above all, the husband. It seemed to him a

[023-103] terrible thing to have to guide so many wives at once across the

[023-104] vicissitudes of life, and to conduct them, as it were, in a body to the

[023-105] Mormon paradise with the prospect of seeing them in the company of the

[023-106] glorious Smith, who doubtless was the chief ornament of that delightful

[023-107] place, to all eternity. He felt decidedly repelled from such a

[023-108] vocation, and he imagined—perhaps he was mistaken—that the fair ones of

[023-109] Salt Lake City cast rather alarming glances on his person. Happily, his

[023-110] stay there was but brief. At four the party found themselves again at

[023-111] the station, took their places in the train, and the whistle sounded

[023-112] for starting. Just at the moment, however, that the locomotive wheels

[023-113] began to move, cries of “Stop! stop!” were heard.

[023-114] Trains, like time and tide, stop for no one. The [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] who uttered

[023-115] the cries was evidently a belated Mormon. He was breathless with

[023-116] running. Happily for him, the station had neither gates nor barriers.

[023-117] He rushed along the track, jumped on the rear platform of the train,

[023-118] and fell, exhausted, into one of the seats.

[023-119] Passepartout, who had been anxiously watching this amateur gymnast,

[023-120] approached him with lively interest, and learned that he had taken

[023-121] flight after an unpleasant domestic scene.

[023-122] When the Mormon had recovered his breath, Passepartout ventured to ask

[023-123] him politely how many wives he had; for, from the manner in which he

[023-124] had decamped, it might be thought that he had twenty at least.

[023-125] “One, sir,” replied the Mormon, raising his arms heavenward —“one, and

[023-126] that was enough!”

[023-127] CHAPTER XXVIII.

[023-128] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT DOES NOT SUCCEED IN MAKING ANYBODY LISTEN TO

[023-129] REASON

[023-130] The train, on leaving Great Salt Lake at Ogden, passed northward for an

[023-131] hour as far as Weber River, having completed nearly nine hundred miles

[023-132] from [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. From this point it took an easterly direction

[023-133] towards the jagged Wahsatch Mountains. It was in the section included

[023-134] between this range and the Rocky Mountains that the American engineers

[023-135] found the most formidable difficulties in laying the road, and that the

[023-136] government granted a subsidy of forty-eight thousand dollars per mile,

[023-137] instead of sixteen thousand allowed for the work done on the plains.

[023-138] But the engineers, instead of violating nature, avoided its

[023-139] difficulties by winding around, instead of penetrating the rocks. One

[023-140] tunnel only, fourteen thousand feet in length, was pierced in order to

[023-141] arrive at the great basin.

[023-142] The track up to this time had reached its highest elevation at the

[023-143] Great Salt Lake. From this point it described a long curve, descending

[023-144] towards Bitter Creek Valley, to rise again to the dividing ridge of the

[023-145] waters between the Atlantic and the [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]]. There were many creeks in

[023-146] this mountainous region, and it was necessary to cross Muddy Creek,

[023-147] Green Creek, and others, upon culverts.

[023-148] Passepartout grew more and more impatient as they went on, while [[detective-fix|Fix]]

[023-149] longed to get out of this difficult region, and was more anxious than

[023-150] Phileas Fogg himself to be beyond the danger of delays and accidents,

[023-151] and set foot on English soil.

[023-152] At ten o’clock at night the train stopped at Fort Bridger station, and

[023-153] twenty minutes later entered Wyoming Territory, following the valley of

[023-154] Bitter Creek throughout. The next day, 7th December, they stopped for a

[023-155] quarter of an hour at Green River station. Snow had fallen abundantly

[023-156] during the night, but, being mixed with rain, it had half melted, and

[023-157] did not interrupt their progress. The bad weather, however, annoyed

[023-158] Passepartout; for the accumulation of snow, by blocking the wheels of

[023-159] the cars, would certainly have been fatal to Mr. Fogg’s tour.

[023-160] “What an idea!” he said to himself. “Why did my master make this

[023-161] journey in winter? Couldn’t he have waited for the good season to

[023-162] increase his chances?”

[023-163] While the worthy Frenchman was absorbed in the state of the sky and the

[023-164] depression of the temperature, [[aouda|Aouda]] was experiencing fears from a

[023-165] totally different cause.

[023-166] Several passengers had got off at Green River, and were walking up and

[023-167] down the platforms; and among these Aouda recognised Colonel Stamp

[023-168] [[colonel-proctor|Proctor]], the same who had so grossly insulted Phileas Fogg at the San

[023-169] Francisco meeting. Not wishing to be recognised, the young woman drew

[023-170] back from the window, feeling much alarm at her discovery. She was

[023-171] attached to the man who, however coldly, gave her daily evidences of

[023-172] the most absolute devotion. She did not comprehend, perhaps, the depth

[023-173] of the sentiment with which her protector inspired her, which she

[023-174] called gratitude, but which, though she was unconscious of it, was

[023-175] really more than that. Her heart sank within her when she recognised

[023-176] the man whom Mr. Fogg desired, sooner or later, to call to account for

[023-177] his conduct. Chance alone, it was clear, had brought Colonel Proctor on

[023-178] this train; but there he was, and it was necessary, at all hazards,

[023-179] that Phileas Fogg should not perceive his adversary.

[023-180] Aouda seized a moment when Mr. Fogg was asleep to tell [[fix-detective|Fix]] and

[023-181] Passepartout whom she had seen.

[023-182] “That Proctor on this train!” cried Fix. “Well, reassure yourself,

[023-183] madam; before he settles with Mr. Fogg; he has got to deal with me! It

[023-184] seems to me that I was the more insulted of the two.”

[023-185] “And, besides,” added Passepartout, “I’ll take charge of him, colonel

[023-186] as he is.”
