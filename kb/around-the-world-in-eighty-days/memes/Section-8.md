[008-001] At half-past twelve the [[railway-travel|train]] stopped at Burhampoor where [[passepartout|Passepartout]]

[008-002] was able to purchase some Indian slippers, ornamented with false

[008-003] pearls, in which, with evident vanity, he proceeded to encase his feet.

[008-004] The travellers made a hasty breakfast and started off for Assurghur,

[008-005] after skirting for a little the banks of the small river Tapty, which

[008-006] empties into the Gulf of Cambray, near Surat.

[008-007] Passepartout was now plunged into absorbing reverie. Up to his arrival

[008-008] at [[bombay|Bombay]], he had entertained hopes that their journey would end there;

[008-009] but, now that they were plainly whirling across India at full speed, a

[008-010] sudden change had come over the spirit of his dreams. His old vagabond

[008-011] nature returned to him; the fantastic ideas of his youth once more took

[008-012] possession of him. He came to regard his master’s project as intended

[008-013] in good earnest, believed in the reality of the [[the-wager|bet]], and therefore in

[008-014] the tour of the world and the necessity of making it without fail

[008-015] within the designated period. Already he began to worry about possible

[008-016] delays, and accidents which might happen on the way. He recognised

[008-017] himself as being personally interested in the wager, and trembled at

[008-018] the thought that he might have been the means of losing it by his

[008-019] unpardonable folly of the night before. Being much less cool-headed

[008-020] than [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]], he was much more restless, counting and recounting the

[008-021] days passed over, uttering maledictions when the train stopped, and

[008-022] accusing it of sluggishness, and mentally blaming Mr. Fogg for not

[008-023] having bribed [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]]. The worthy fellow was ignorant that, while

[008-024] it was possible by such means to hasten the rate of a [[steamer-travel|steamer]], it could

[008-025] not be done on the railway.

[008-026] The train entered the defiles of the Sutpour Mountains, which separate

[008-027] the Khandeish from Bundelcund, towards evening. The next day Sir

[008-028] Francis [[sir-francis-cromarty|Cromarty]] asked Passepartout what time it was; to which, on

[008-029] consulting his [[precision-and-timekeeping|watch]], he replied that it was three in the morning. This

[008-030] famous timepiece, always regulated on the Greenwich meridian, which was

[008-031] now some seventy-seven degrees westward, was at least four hours slow.

[008-032] Sir Francis corrected Passepartout’s time, whereupon the latter made

[008-033] the same remark that he had done to [[detective-fix|Fix]]; and upon the general insisting

[008-034] that the watch should be regulated in each new meridian, since he was

[008-035] constantly going eastward, that is in the face of the sun, and

[008-036] therefore the days were shorter by four minutes for each degree gone

[008-037] over, Passepartout obstinately refused to alter his watch, which he

[008-038] kept at [[london|London]] time. It was an innocent delusion which could harm no

[008-039] one.

[008-040] The train stopped, at eight o’clock, in the midst of a glade some

[008-041] fifteen miles beyond Rothal, where there were several bungalows, and

[008-042] workmen’s cabins. The conductor, passing along the carriages, shouted,

[008-043] “Passengers will get out here!”

[008-044] Phileas Fogg looked at Sir Francis Cromarty for an explanation; but the

[008-045] general could not tell what meant a halt in the midst of this forest of

[008-046] dates and acacias.

[008-047] Passepartout, not less surprised, rushed out and speedily returned,

[008-048] crying: “Monsieur, no more railway!”

[008-049] “What do you mean?” asked Sir Francis.

[008-050] “I mean to say that the train isn’t going on.”

[008-051] The general at once stepped out, while Phileas Fogg calmly followed

[008-052] him, and they proceeded together to the conductor.

[008-053] “Where are we?” asked Sir Francis.

[008-054] “At the hamlet of Kholby.”

[008-055] “Do we stop here?”

[008-056] “Certainly. The railway isn’t finished.”

[008-057] “What! not finished?”

[008-058] “No. There’s still a matter of fifty miles to be laid from here to

[008-059] [[allahabad|Allahabad]], where the line begins again.”

[008-060] “But the [[passport-and-identity|papers]] announced the opening of the railway throughout.”

[008-061] “What would you have, officer? The papers were mistaken.”

[008-062] “Yet you sell tickets from Bombay to [[calcutta|Calcutta]],” retorted Sir Francis,

[008-063] who was growing warm.

[008-064] “No doubt,” replied the conductor; “but the passengers know that they

[008-065] must provide means of transportation for themselves from Kholby to

[008-066] Allahabad.”

[008-067] Sir Francis was furious. Passepartout would willingly have knocked the

[008-068] conductor down, and did not dare to look at his master.

[008-069] “Sir Francis,” said Mr. Fogg quietly, “we will, if you please, look

[008-070] about for some means of conveyance to Allahabad.”

[008-071] “Mr. Fogg, this is a delay greatly to your disadvantage.”

[008-072] “No, Sir Francis; it was foreseen.”

[008-073] “What! You knew that the way—”

[008-074] “Not at all; but I knew that some obstacle or other would sooner or

[008-075] later arise on my [[around-the-world-route|route]]. Nothing, therefore, is lost. I have two days,

[008-076] which I have already gained, to sacrifice. A steamer leaves Calcutta

[008-077] for [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] at noon, on the 25th. This is the 22nd, and we shall

[008-078] reach Calcutta in time.”

[008-079] There was nothing to say to so confident a response.

[008-080] It was but too true that the railway came to a termination at this

[008-081] point. The papers were like some watches, which have a way of getting

[008-082] too fast, and had been premature in their announcement of the

[008-083] completion of the line. The greater part of the travellers were aware

[008-084] of this interruption, and, leaving the train, they began to engage such

[008-085] vehicles as the village could provide four-wheeled palkigharis, waggons

[008-086] drawn by zebus, carriages that looked like perambulating pagodas,

[008-087] palanquins, ponies, and what not.

[008-088] Mr. Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty, after searching the village from end

[008-089] to end, came back without having found anything.

[008-090] “I shall go afoot,” said Phileas Fogg.

[008-091] Passepartout, who had now rejoined his master, made a wry grimace, as

[008-092] he thought of his magnificent, but too frail Indian shoes. Happily he

[008-093] too had been looking about him, and, after a moment’s hesitation, said,

[008-094] “Monsieur, I think I have found a means of conveyance.”

[008-095] “What?”

[008-096] “An elephant! An elephant that belongs to an Indian who lives but a

[008-097] hundred steps from here.”

[008-098] “Let’s go and see the elephant,” replied Mr. Fogg.

[008-099] They soon reached a small hut, near which, enclosed within some high

[008-100] palings, was the animal in question. An Indian came out of the hut,

[008-101] and, at their request, conducted them within the enclosure. The

[008-102] elephant, which its owner had reared, not for a beast of burden, but

[008-103] for warlike purposes, was half domesticated. The Indian had begun

[008-104] already, by often irritating him, and feeding him every three months on

[008-105] sugar and butter, to impart to him a ferocity not in his nature, this

[008-106] method being often employed by those who train the Indian elephants for

[008-107] battle. Happily, however, for Mr. Fogg, the animal’s instruction in

[008-108] this direction had not gone far, and the elephant still preserved his

[008-109] natural gentleness. Kiouni—this was the name of the beast—could

[008-110] doubtless travel rapidly for a long time, and, in default of any other

[008-111] means of conveyance, Mr. Fogg resolved to hire him. But elephants are

[008-112] far from cheap in India, where they are becoming scarce, the males,

[008-113] which alone are suitable for circus shows, are much sought, especially

[008-114] as but few of them are domesticated. When therefore Mr. Fogg proposed

[008-115] to the Indian to hire Kiouni, he refused point-blank. Mr. Fogg

[008-116] persisted, offering the excessive sum of ten pounds an hour for the

[008-117] loan of the beast to Allahabad. Refused. Twenty pounds? Refused also.

[008-118] Forty pounds? Still refused. Passepartout jumped at each advance; but

[008-119] the Indian declined to be tempted. Yet the offer was an alluring one,

[008-120] for, supposing it took the elephant fifteen hours to reach Allahabad,

[008-121] his owner would receive no less than six hundred pounds sterling.

[008-122] Phileas Fogg, without getting in the least flurried, then proposed to

[008-123] purchase the animal outright, and at first offered a thousand pounds

[008-124] for him. The Indian, perhaps thinking he was going to make a great

[008-125] bargain, still refused.

[008-126] Sir Francis Cromarty took Mr. Fogg aside, and begged him to reflect

[008-127] before he went any further; to which that [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] replied that he was

[008-128] not in the habit of acting rashly, that a bet of twenty thousand pounds

[008-129] was at stake, that the elephant was absolutely necessary to him, and

[008-130] that he would secure him if he had to pay twenty times his value.

[008-131] Returning to the Indian, whose small, sharp eyes, glistening with

[008-132] avarice, betrayed that with him it was only a question of how great a

[008-133] price he could obtain. Mr. Fogg offered first twelve hundred, then

[008-134] fifteen hundred, eighteen hundred, two thousand pounds. Passepartout,

[008-135] usually so rubicund, was fairly white with suspense.

[008-136] At two thousand pounds the Indian yielded.

[008-137] “What a price, good heavens!” cried Passepartout, “for an elephant.”

[008-138] It only remained now to find a guide, which was comparatively easy. A

[008-139] young Parsee, with an intelligent face, offered his services, which Mr.

[008-140] Fogg accepted, promising so generous a reward as to materially

[008-141] stimulate his zeal. The elephant was led out and equipped. The Parsee,

[008-142] who was an accomplished elephant driver, covered his back with a sort

[008-143] of saddle-cloth, and attached to each of his flanks some curiously

[008-144] uncomfortable howdahs. Phileas Fogg paid the Indian with some banknotes

[008-145] which he extracted from the famous carpet-bag, a proceeding that seemed

[008-146] to deprive poor Passepartout of his vitals. Then he offered to carry

[008-147] Sir Francis to Allahabad, which the brigadier gratefully accepted, as

[008-148] one traveller the more would not be likely to fatigue the gigantic

[008-149] beast. Provisions were purchased at Kholby, and, while Sir Francis and

[008-150] Mr. Fogg took the howdahs on either side, Passepartout got astride the

[008-151] saddle-cloth between them. The Parsee perched himself on the elephant’s

[008-152] neck, and at nine o’clock they set out from the village, the animal

[008-153] marching off through the dense forest of palms by the shortest cut.

[008-154] CHAPTER XII.

[008-155] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG AND HIS COMPANIONS VENTURE ACROSS THE INDIAN

[008-156] FORESTS, AND WHAT ENSUED

[008-157] In order to shorten the journey, [[the-parsee-guide|the guide]] passed to the left of the

[008-158] line where the railway was still in process of being built. This line,

[008-159] owing to the capricious turnings of the Vindhia Mountains, did not

[008-160] pursue a straight course. The Parsee, who was quite familiar with the

[008-161] roads and paths in the district, declared that they would gain twenty

[008-162] miles by striking directly through the forest.

[008-163] Phileas Fogg and Sir Francis Cromarty, plunged to the neck in the

[008-164] peculiar howdahs provided for them, were horribly jostled by the swift

[008-165] trotting of the elephant, spurred on as he was by the skilful Parsee;

[008-166] but they endured the discomfort with true British phlegm, talking

[008-167] little, and scarcely able to catch a glimpse of each other. As for

[008-168] Passepartout, who was mounted on the beast’s back, and received the

[008-169] direct force of each concussion as he trod along, he was very careful,

[008-170] in accordance with his master’s advice, to keep his tongue from between

[008-171] his teeth, as it would otherwise have been bitten off short. The worthy

[008-172] fellow bounced from the elephant’s neck to his rump, and vaulted like a

[008-173] clown on a spring-board; yet he laughed in the midst of his bouncing,

[008-174] and from time to time took a piece of sugar out of his pocket, and

[008-175] inserted it in Kiouni’s trunk, who received it without in the least

[008-176] slackening his regular trot.

[008-177] After two hours the guide stopped the elephant, and gave him an hour

[008-178] for rest, during which Kiouni, after quenching his thirst at a

[008-179] neighbouring spring, set to devouring the branches and shrubs round

[008-180] about him. Neither Sir Francis nor Mr. Fogg regretted the delay, and

[008-181] both descended with a feeling of relief. “Why, he’s made of iron!”

[008-182] exclaimed the general, gazing admiringly on Kiouni.

[008-183] “Of forged iron,” replied Passepartout, as he set about preparing a

[008-184] hasty breakfast.

[008-185] At noon the Parsee gave the signal of departure. The country soon

[008-186] presented a very savage aspect. Copses of dates and dwarf-palms

[008-187] succeeded the dense forests; then vast, dry plains, dotted with scanty

[008-188] shrubs, and sown with great blocks of syenite. All this portion of

[008-189] Bundelcund, which is little frequented by travellers, is inhabited by a

[008-190] fanatical population, hardened in the most horrible practices of the

[008-191] Hindoo faith. The English have not been able to secure complete

[008-192] dominion over this territory, which is subjected to the influence of

[008-193] rajahs, whom it is almost impossible to reach in their inaccessible

[008-194] mountain fastnesses. The travellers several times saw bands of

[008-195] ferocious Indians, who, when they perceived the elephant striding

[008-196] across-country, made angry and threatening motions. The Parsee avoided

[008-197] them as much as possible. Few animals were observed on the route; even

[008-198] the monkeys hurried from their path with contortions and grimaces which

[008-199] convulsed Passepartout with laughter.
