# Steam Technology

The steam engine was the defining technology of the nineteenth century. By the time of Phileas Fogg's journey in 1872, steam power had transformed every mode of transportation: locomotives thundered across continents, steamers crossed oceans on fixed schedules, and a traveller could move from London to Bombay in less than two weeks — a journey that, a generation earlier, would have taken months under sail.

## Definition

Steam technology converts the thermal energy of boiling water into mechanical motion through a piston-driven engine. In transportation, the steam locomotive and the marine steam engine were the two principal applications. The locomotive drove wheels on iron rails, achieving speeds of up to sixty miles per hour on level track. The marine steam engine turned paddle-wheels or screw propellers, enabling ships to maintain consistent speeds regardless of wind conditions — typically ten to twelve knots for the vessels of the Peninsular and Oriental Company. (PN: 004-100)

## Role in the Novel

Without steam technology, Fogg's journey is inconceivable. Every conveyance he boards — the *Mongolia*, the Great Indian Peninsula Railway, the *Rangoon*, the Pacific Railroad, the *Henrietta* — is powered by steam. The novel is, in part, a celebration of the steam age: human beings, for the first time, could outrun the wind and the horse.

Verne, however, does not treat steam as infallible. Each steam-powered conveyance is vulnerable to the constraints of fuel and machinery. The *Henrietta* runs out of coal crossing the Atlantic, forcing Fogg to purchase the ship and burn its wooden fittings. The locomotive crossing the American West must stop for fuel and water at remote stations. Steam conquers distance, but it does so at a cost — and that cost can threaten the journey when resources run thin. (PN: 030-010)

## The Steam Engine in Victorian Culture

The steam engine represented everything the Victorian age admired: power, precision, and the mastery of nature through engineering. Verne's narrator describes the locomotive in India as "guided by an English engineer and fed with English coal," casting steam technology as an instrument of imperial civilization (PN: 007-174). The steamer *Mongolia*, with its iron hull and engines of two thousand eight hundred tons burden, is described in detail — a mechanical marvel that makes the world smaller with each revolution of its screw.

Yet the novel also gestures at the fragility of this technological infrastructure. The railway across India is incomplete. The steamer schedules are disrupted by weather. Coal is consumed faster than anticipated. Steam technology is powerful but not invincible, and Fogg's journey is a demonstration both of what it can do and of what happens when it fails.
