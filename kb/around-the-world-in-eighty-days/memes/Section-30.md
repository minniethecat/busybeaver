[030-001] The Custom House [[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] struck one. [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]] observed that his watch was

[030-002] two hours too fast.

[030-003] Two hours! Admitting that he was at this moment taking an express

[030-004] [[railway-travel|train]], he could reach [[london|London]] and the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] by a quarter before

[030-005] nine, p.m. His forehead slightly wrinkled.

[030-006] At thirty-three minutes past two he heard a singular noise outside,

[030-007] then a hasty opening of doors. [[passepartout|Passepartout]]’s voice was audible, and

[030-008] immediately after that of [[detective-fix|Fix]]. Phileas Fogg’s eyes brightened for an

[030-009] instant.

[030-010] The door swung open, and he saw Passepartout, [[aouda|Aouda]], and [[fix-detective|Fix]], who

[030-011] hurried towards him.

[030-012] Fix was out of breath, and his hair was in disorder. He could not

[030-013] speak. “Sir,” he stammered, “sir—forgive me—most—unfortunate

[030-014] resemblance—robber arrested three days ago—you are free!”

[030-015] Phileas Fogg was free! He walked to the detective, looked him steadily

[030-016] in the face, and with the only rapid motion he had ever made in his

[030-017] life, or which he ever would make, drew back his arms, and with the

[030-018] precision of a machine knocked Fix down.

[030-019] “Well hit!” cried Passepartout, “Parbleu! that’s what you might call a

[030-020] good application of English fists!”

[030-021] Fix, who found himself on the floor, did not utter a word. He had only

[030-022] received his deserts. Mr. Fogg, Aouda, and Passepartout left the Custom

[030-023] House without delay, got into a cab, and in a few moments descended at

[030-024] the station.

[030-025] Phileas Fogg asked if there was an express train about to leave for

[030-026] London. It was forty minutes past two. The express train had left

[030-027] thirty-five minutes before. Phileas Fogg then ordered a special train.

[030-028] There were several rapid locomotives on hand; but the railway

[030-029] arrangements did not permit the special train to leave until three

[030-030] o’clock.

[030-031] At that hour Phileas Fogg, having stimulated [[andrew-stuart|the engineer]] by the offer

[030-032] of a generous reward, at last set out towards London with Aouda and his

[030-033] faithful servant.

[030-034] It was necessary to make the journey in five hours and a half; and this

[030-035] would have been easy on a clear road throughout. But there were forced

[030-036] delays, and when Mr. Fogg stepped from the train at the terminus, all

[030-037] the clocks in London were striking ten minutes before nine.[1]

[030-038] Having made the tour of the world, he was behind-hand five minutes. He

[030-039] had lost the [[the-wager|wager]]!

[030-040] [1] A somewhat remarkable eccentricity on the part of the London

[030-041] clocks!—TRANSLATOR.

[030-042] CHAPTER XXXV.

[030-043] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG DOES NOT HAVE TO REPEAT HIS ORDERS TO

[030-044] PASSEPARTOUT TWICE

[030-045] The dwellers in Saville Row would have been surprised the next day, if

[030-046] they had been told that Phileas Fogg had returned home. His doors and

[030-047] windows were still closed, no appearance of change was visible.

[030-048] After leaving the station, Mr. Fogg gave Passepartout instructions to

[030-049] purchase some provisions, and quietly went to his domicile.

[030-050] He bore his misfortune with his habitual tranquillity. Ruined! And by

[030-051] the blundering of the detective! After having steadily traversed that

[030-052] long journey, overcome a hundred obstacles, braved many dangers, and

[030-053] still found time to do some good on his way, to fail near the goal by a

[030-054] sudden event which he could not have foreseen, and against which he was

[030-055] unarmed; it was terrible! But a few pounds were left of the large sum

[030-056] he had carried with him. There only remained of his fortune the twenty

[030-057] thousand pounds deposited at Barings, and this amount he owed to his

[030-058] friends of the Reform Club. So great had been the expense of his tour

[030-059] that, even had he won, it would not have enriched him; and it is

[030-060] probable that he had not sought to enrich himself, being a man who

[030-061] rather laid wagers for honour’s sake than for the stake proposed. But

[030-062] this wager totally ruined him.

[030-063] Mr. Fogg’s course, however, was fully decided upon; he knew what

[030-064] remained for him to do.

[030-065] A room in the house in Saville Row was set apart for Aouda, who was

[030-066] overwhelmed with grief at her protector’s misfortune. From the words

[030-067] which Mr. Fogg dropped, she saw that he was meditating some serious

[030-068] project.

[030-069] Knowing that Englishmen governed by a fixed idea sometimes resort to

[030-070] the desperate expedient of suicide, Passepartout kept a narrow watch

[030-071] upon his master, though he carefully concealed the appearance of so

[030-072] doing.

[030-073] First of all, the worthy fellow had gone up to his room, and had

[030-074] extinguished the gas burner, which had been burning for eighty days. He

[030-075] had found in the letter-box a bill from the gas company, and he thought

[030-076] it more than time to put a stop to this expense, which he had been

[030-077] doomed to bear.

[030-078] The night passed. Mr. Fogg went to bed, but did he sleep? Aouda did not

[030-079] once close her eyes. Passepartout watched all night, like a faithful

[030-080] dog, at his master’s door.

[030-081] Mr. Fogg called him in the morning, and told him to get Aouda’s

[030-082] breakfast, and a cup of tea and a chop for himself. He desired Aouda to

[030-083] excuse him from breakfast and dinner, as his time would be absorbed all

[030-084] day in putting his affairs to rights. In the evening he would ask

[030-085] permission to have a few moment’s conversation with the young lady.

[030-086] Passepartout, having received his orders, had nothing to do but obey

[030-087] them. He looked at his imperturbable master, and could scarcely bring

[030-088] his mind to leave him. His heart was full, and his conscience tortured

[030-089] by remorse; for he accused himself more bitterly than ever of being the

[030-090] cause of the irretrievable disaster. Yes! if he had warned Mr. Fogg,

[030-091] and had betrayed Fix’s projects to him, his master would certainly not

[030-092] have given the detective passage to [[liverpool|Liverpool]], and then—

[030-093] Passepartout could hold in no longer.

[030-094] “My master! Mr. Fogg!” he cried, “why do you not curse me? It was my

[030-095] fault that—”

[030-096] “I blame no one,” returned Phileas Fogg, with perfect calmness. “Go!”

[030-097] Passepartout left the room, and went to find Aouda, to whom he

[030-098] delivered his master’s message.

[030-099] “Madam,” he added, “I can do nothing myself—nothing! I have no

[030-100] influence over my master; but you, perhaps—”

[030-101] “What influence could I have?” replied Aouda. “Mr. Fogg is influenced

[030-102] by no one. Has he ever understood that my gratitude to him is

[030-103] overflowing? Has he ever read my heart? My friend, he must not be left

[030-104] alone an instant! You say he is going to speak with me this evening?”

[030-105] “Yes, madam; probably to arrange for your protection and comfort in

[030-106] England.”

[030-107] “We shall see,” replied Aouda, becoming suddenly pensive.

[030-108] Throughout this day (Sunday) the house in Saville Row was as if

[030-109] uninhabited, and Phileas Fogg, for the first time since he had lived in

[030-110] that house, did not set out for his club when Westminster clock struck

[030-111] half-past eleven.

[030-112] Why should he present himself at the Reform? His friends no longer

[030-113] expected him there. As Phileas Fogg had not appeared in the saloon on

[030-114] the evening before (Saturday, the 21st of December, at a quarter before

[030-115] nine), he had lost his wager. It was not even necessary that he should

[030-116] go to his bankers for the twenty thousand pounds; for his antagonists

[030-117] already had his cheque in their hands, and they had only to fill it out

[030-118] and send it to the Barings to have the amount transferred to their

[030-119] credit.

[030-120] Mr. Fogg, therefore, had no reason for going out, and so he remained at

[030-121] home. He shut himself up in his room, and busied himself putting his

[030-122] affairs in order. Passepartout continually ascended and descended the

[030-123] stairs. The hours were long for him. He listened at his master’s door,

[030-124] and looked through the keyhole, as if he had a perfect right so to do,

[030-125] and as if he feared that something terrible might happen at any moment.

[030-126] Sometimes he thought of Fix, but no longer in anger. Fix, like all the

[030-127] world, had been mistaken in Phileas Fogg, and had only done his duty in

[030-128] tracking and arresting him; while he, Passepartout. . . . This thought

[030-129] haunted him, and he never ceased cursing his miserable folly.

[030-130] Finding himself too wretched to remain alone, he knocked at Aouda’s

[030-131] door, went into her room, seated himself, without speaking, in a

[030-132] corner, and looked ruefully at the young woman. Aouda was still

[030-133] pensive.

[030-134] [[About]] half-past seven in the evening Mr. Fogg sent to know if Aouda

[030-135] would receive him, and in a few moments he found himself alone with

[030-136] her.

[030-137] Phileas Fogg took a chair, and sat down near the fireplace, opposite

[030-138] Aouda. No emotion was visible on his face. Fogg returned was exactly

[030-139] the Fogg who had gone away; there was the same calm, the same

[030-140] impassibility.

[030-141] He sat several minutes without speaking; then, bending his eyes on

[030-142] Aouda, “Madam,” said he, “will you pardon me for bringing you to

[030-143] England?”

[030-144] “I, Mr. Fogg!” replied Aouda, checking the pulsations of her heart.

[030-145] “Please let me finish,” returned Mr. Fogg. “When I decided to bring you

[030-146] far away from the country which was so unsafe for you, I was rich, and

[030-147] counted on putting a portion of my fortune at your disposal; then your

[030-148] existence would have been free and happy. But now I am ruined.”

[030-149] “I know it, Mr. Fogg,” replied Aouda; “and I ask you in my turn, will

[030-150] you forgive me for having followed you, and—who knows?—for having,

[030-151] perhaps, delayed you, and thus contributed to your ruin?”

[030-152] “Madam, you could not remain in India, and your safety could only be

[030-153] assured by bringing you to such a distance that your persecutors could

[030-154] not take you.”

[030-155] “So, Mr. Fogg,” resumed Aouda, “not content with rescuing me from a

[030-156] terrible death, you thought yourself bound to secure my comfort in a

[030-157] foreign land?”

[030-158] “Yes, madam; but circumstances have been against me. Still, I beg to

[030-159] place the little I have left at your service.”

[030-160] “But what will become of you, Mr. Fogg?”

[030-161] “As for me, madam,” replied the [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]], coldly, “I have need of

[030-162] nothing.”

[030-163] “But how do you look upon the fate, sir, which awaits you?”

[030-164] “As I am in the habit of doing.”

[030-165] “At least,” said Aouda, “want should not overtake a man like you. Your

[030-166] friends—”

[030-167] “I have no friends, madam.”

[030-168] “Your relatives—”

[030-169] “I have no longer any relatives.”

[030-170] “I pity you, then, Mr. Fogg, for solitude is a sad thing, with no heart

[030-171] to which to confide your griefs. They say, though, that misery itself,

[030-172] shared by two sympathetic souls, may be borne with patience.”

[030-173] “They say so, madam.”

[030-174] “Mr. Fogg,” said Aouda, rising and seizing his hand, “do you wish at

[030-175] once a kinswoman and friend? Will you have me for your wife?”

[030-176] Mr. Fogg, at this, rose in his turn. There was an unwonted light in his

[030-177] eyes, and a slight trembling of his lips. Aouda looked into his face.

[030-178] The sincerity, rectitude, firmness, and sweetness of this soft glance

[030-179] of a noble woman, who could dare all to save him to whom she owed all,

[030-180] at first astonished, then penetrated him. He shut his eyes for an

[030-181] instant, as if to avoid her look. When he opened them again, “I love

[030-182] you!” he said, simply. “Yes, by all that is holiest, I love you, and I

[030-183] am entirely yours!”

[030-184] “Ah!” cried Aouda, pressing his hand to her heart.

[030-185] Passepartout was summoned and appeared immediately. Mr. Fogg still held

[030-186] Aouda’s hand in his own; Passepartout understood, and his big, round

[030-187] face became as radiant as the tropical sun at its zenith.

[030-188] Mr. Fogg asked him if it was not too late to notify the Reverend Samuel

[030-189] [[reverend-wilson|Wilson]], of Marylebone parish, that evening.

[030-190] Passepartout smiled his most genial smile, and said, “Never too late.”

[030-191] It was five minutes past eight.

[030-192] “Will it be for to-morrow, Monday?”

[030-193] “For to-morrow, Monday,” said Mr. Fogg, turning to Aouda.

[030-194] “Yes; for to-morrow, Monday,” she replied.

[030-195] Passepartout hurried off as fast as his legs could carry him.

[030-196] CHAPTER XXXVI.

[030-197] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG’S NAME IS ONCE MORE AT A PREMIUM ON ’CHANGE

[030-198] It is time to relate what a change took place in English public opinion

[030-199] when it transpired that the real bankrobber, a certain James Strand,

[030-200] had been arrested, on the 17th day of December, at Edinburgh. Three

[030-201] days before, Phileas Fogg had been a criminal, who was being

[030-202] desperately followed up by the police; now he was an honourable

[030-203] gentleman, mathematically pursuing his eccentric journey round the

[030-204] world.
