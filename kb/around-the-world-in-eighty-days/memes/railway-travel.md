# Railway Travel

## Definition

Railway travel in *Around the World in Eighty Days* refers to the network of steam-powered railway lines that constitute the land-based component of Phileas Fogg's circumnavigation. Together with ocean-going steamers, the railways are what make the eighty-day wager conceivable: the Morning Chronicle timetable assigns three days for the Bombay-to-Calcutta rail crossing (003-039) and seven days for the San-Francisco-to-New-York transcontinental crossing. The railway is presented as the defining technology of the Victorian age -- a triumph of engineering that has collapsed distances, unified nations, and made the modern global timetable possible.

## Role in the Novel

Railway travel serves three principal narrative functions. First, it provides the temporal discipline against which Fogg's personal precision is measured. The train "would start for Calcutta" at "exactly eight" (007-002), and its "punctual" departure (007-110) affirms the Victorian faith in mechanical regularity. Second, the railway becomes a stage for social observation and narrative incident: the rescue of Aouda is set in motion during the Indian rail journey (011-064), Colonel Proctor's entrance and the interrupted duel occur aboard the transcontinental train (025-088, 025-094), and the Sioux attack turns a passenger car into a battlefield (025-139). Third, the interruption of the railway -- most dramatically at Kholby, where "the railway came to a termination at this point" (008-080) -- forces Fogg to improvise, revealing that the Victorian faith in technology is always contingent on geography, politics, and the unfinished nature of empire.

## Key Railway Segments

### The London-to-Dover Line (Chapter 003)

Fogg's journey begins on the railway: "The train leaves for Dover at a quarter before nine" (003-100). The scene establishes the rhythm -- steamer to railway, railway to steamer -- that defines the entire journey.

### The Great Indian Peninsula Railway (Chapters 007-011)

The GIPR is the novel's first extended railway sequence. The line, which "traverses the peninsula from Bombay to Calcutta in three days" (006-179), crosses a landscape that Verne depicts as a collision of technology and wilderness. The locomotive, "guided by an English engineer, carried its passengers across a country still half savage" (007-174). Passepartout marvels at the passing panorama of "pagodas, ruined temples, and picturesque villages" (011-098), while the train itself becomes a microcosm of imperial order: English officers, government officials, and opium merchants share the carriages (007-109--007-110).

The Kholby interruption is the Indian railway's dramatic climax. Passepartout's cry -- "Monsieur, no more railway!" (008-050) -- announces that the line, despite newspaper reports of its completion (008-060), is still under construction. The fifty-mile gap between Kholby and Allahabad rehearses the novel's central theme: technology is never as finished as the timetables promise. Fogg's response -- purchasing an elephant, Kiouni, and crossing the gap overland -- demonstrates that true mastery consists not in relying on systems but in adapting when they fail.

### The American Transcontinental Railway (Chapters 022-027)

The Pacific Railroad, completed in 1869 just three years before the novel's setting, is the technological marvel that makes the American crossing possible. The train leaves Oakland station at six o'clock (022-037), and Verne describes its construction in terms that evoke the conquest of nature: the rails were laid "a mile and a half a day" across the prairies, with "a locomotive, running on the rails laid down the evening before" bringing more rails for the next day's advance (022-010).

The American railway sequence is the novel's most action-packed segment. Events include the Mormon lecture by Elder Hitch (025-042), the whist game interrupted by Colonel Proctor (025-088), the interrupted duel (025-094), the Sioux attack in which the train conductor is wounded and the locomotive uncoupled (025-139, 026-104), and the dramatic bridge crossing at Medicine Bow where "the whole train, rushing on at the rate of a hundred miles an hour, hardly bore upon the rails at all" -- the bridge collapsing into the rapids moments after the last car passes (024-176--024-181).

After the Sioux attack and the loss of the train at Kearney, Fogg switches to a wind-powered sledge for the leg to Omaha (027-029), where the Chicago and Rock Island Railroad provides the connection eastward (027-155). A "train was ready to start when Mr. Fogg and his party reached the station" at Omaha (027-157), and the final rail segment from Chicago to New York is described with the urgency of a chase: "The locomotive left at full speed, as if it fully comprehended that the gentleman had no time to lose" (027-167).

### The Dublin-Liverpool Express (Chapter 029)

The final railway segment is the Dublin-to-Liverpool express, which Fogg boards after crossing from Queenstown (029-133). This train brings him to Liverpool -- and to Fix's waiting handcuffs. The railway, which has been Fogg's ally throughout the journey, delivers him directly into custody.

## Related Concepts

- **Steamer Travel**: The complementary maritime technology. Fogg's journey alternates between rail and steamer, and Fix imagines him "jumping from a steamer upon a railway train, and from a railway train upon a steamer again" (006-079).
- **The Timetable / Bradshaw's Guide**: Bradshaw's Continental Railway Steam Transit and General Guide (003-174) is the bible of the railway traveller, containing the schedules that make the wager's precise timing possible.
- **Around the World Route**: The integrated network of railways and steamers defined by the Morning Chronicle itinerary.
- **Time Pressure**: Railway schedules create absolute deadlines: miss a train and the entire connection chain collapses. "Trains, like time and tide, stop for no one" (023-114).
- **Coal Logistics**: As with steamers, the railway's dependence on coal supplies creates a vulnerability that the novel dramatises.

## Historical and Cultural Context

The Great Indian Peninsula Railway, chartered in 1849, was one of the first railway companies in India and a flagship of British imperial infrastructure. By 1872, the year of Fogg's journey, the line connected Bombay with Calcutta via Allahabad -- but, as the novel accurately notes, the final section was not yet complete in all locations. The Kholby gap reflects the historical reality of railway construction in India, where terrain, climate, and political resistance slowed progress.

The American Pacific Railroad, completed with the driving of the Golden Spike at Promontory Summit, Utah, on May 10, 1869, had shrunk the transcontinental journey from months to days. Verne's description of the line -- "there were few or no bridges or tunnels on the route" (022-086) -- reflects the engineering philosophy of American railroad construction, which favoured speed of construction over the elaborate tunnelling and bridging typical of European lines.
