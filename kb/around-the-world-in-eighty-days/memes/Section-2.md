[002-001] [[passepartout|Passepartout]] heard the street door shut once; it was his new master

[002-002] going out. He heard it shut again; it was his predecessor, James

[002-003] [[james-forster|Forster]], departing in his turn. Passepartout remained alone in the

[002-004] house in Saville Row.

[002-005] CHAPTER II.

[002-006] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT IS CONVINCED THAT HE HAS AT LAST FOUND HIS IDEAL

[002-007] “Faith,” muttered Passepartout, somewhat flurried, “I’ve seen people at

[002-008] Madame Tussaud’s as lively as my new master!”

[002-009] Madame Tussaud’s “people,” let it be said, are of wax, and are much

[002-010] visited in [[london|London]]; speech is all that is wanting to make them human.

[002-011] During his brief interview with [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]], Passepartout had been

[002-012] carefully observing him. He appeared to be a man about forty years of

[002-013] age, with fine, handsome features, and a tall, well-shaped figure; his

[002-014] hair and whiskers were light, his forehead compact and unwrinkled, his

[002-015] face rather pale, his teeth magnificent. His countenance possessed in

[002-016] the highest degree what physiognomists call “repose in action,” a

[002-017] quality of those who act rather than talk. Calm and phlegmatic, with a

[002-018] clear eye, Mr. Fogg seemed a perfect type of that English composure

[002-019] which Angelica Kauffmann has so skilfully represented on canvas. Seen

[002-020] in the various phases of his daily life, he gave the idea of being

[002-021] perfectly well-balanced, as exactly regulated as a Leroy chronometer.

[002-022] Phileas Fogg was, indeed, exactitude personified, and this was betrayed

[002-023] even in the expression of his very hands and feet; for in men, as well

[002-024] as in animals, the limbs themselves are expressive of the passions.

[002-025] He was so exact that he was never in a hurry, was always ready, and was

[002-026] economical alike of his steps and his motions. He never took one step

[002-027] too many, and always went to his destination by the shortest cut; he

[002-028] made no superfluous gestures, and was never seen to be moved or

[002-029] agitated. He was the most deliberate person in the world, yet always

[002-030] reached his destination at the exact moment.

[002-031] He lived alone, and, so to speak, outside of every social relation; and

[002-032] as he knew that in this world account must be taken of friction, and

[002-033] that friction retards, he never rubbed against anybody.

[002-034] As for Passepartout, he was a true Parisian of Paris. Since he had

[002-035] abandoned his own country for England, taking service as a valet, he

[002-036] had in vain searched for a master after his own heart. Passepartout was

[002-037] by no means one of those pert dunces depicted by Molière with a bold

[002-038] gaze and a nose held high in the air; he was an honest fellow, with a

[002-039] pleasant face, lips a trifle protruding, soft-mannered and serviceable,

[002-040] with a good round head, such as one likes to see on the shoulders of a

[002-041] friend. His eyes were blue, his complexion rubicund, his figure almost

[002-042] portly and well-built, his body muscular, and his physical powers fully

[002-043] developed by the exercises of his younger days. His brown hair was

[002-044] somewhat tumbled; for, while the ancient sculptors are said to have

[002-045] known eighteen methods of arranging Minerva’s tresses, Passepartout was

[002-046] familiar with but one of dressing his own: three strokes of a

[002-047] large-tooth comb completed his toilet.

[002-048] It would be rash to predict how Passepartout’s lively nature would

[002-049] agree with Mr. Fogg. It was impossible to tell whether the new servant

[002-050] would turn out as absolutely methodical as his master required;

[002-051] experience alone could solve the question. Passepartout had been a sort

[002-052] of vagrant in his early years, and now yearned for repose; but so far

[002-053] he had failed to find it, though he had already served in ten English

[002-054] houses. But he could not take root in any of these; with chagrin, he

[002-055] found his masters invariably whimsical and irregular, constantly

[002-056] running about the country, or on the look-out for [[spirit-of-adventure|adventure]]. His last

[002-057] master, young Lord Longferry, Member of Parliament, after passing his

[002-058] nights in the Haymarket taverns, was too often brought home in the

[002-059] morning on policemen’s shoulders. Passepartout, desirous of respecting

[002-060] the [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] whom he served, ventured a mild remonstrance on such

[002-061] conduct; which, being ill-received, he took his leave. Hearing that Mr.

[002-062] Phileas Fogg was looking for a servant, and that his life was one of

[002-063] unbroken regularity, that he neither travelled nor stayed from home

[002-064] overnight, he felt sure that this would be the place he was after. He

[002-065] presented himself, and was accepted, as has been seen.

[002-066] At half-past eleven, then, Passepartout found himself alone in the

[002-067] house in Saville Row. He began its inspection without delay, scouring

[002-068] it from cellar to garret. So clean, well-arranged, solemn a mansion

[002-069] pleased him; it seemed to him like a snail’s shell, lighted and warmed

[002-070] by gas, which sufficed for both these purposes. When Passepartout

[002-071] reached the second story he recognised at once the room which he was to

[002-072] inhabit, and he was well satisfied with it. Electric bells and

[002-073] speaking-tubes afforded communication with the lower stories; while on

[002-074] the mantel stood an electric [[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]], precisely like that in Mr. Fogg’s

[002-075] bedchamber, both beating the same second at the same instant. “That’s

[002-076] good, that’ll do,” said Passepartout to himself.

[002-077] He suddenly observed, hung over the clock, a card which, upon

[002-078] inspection, proved to be a programme of the daily routine of the house.

[002-079] It comprised all that was required of the servant, from eight in the

[002-080] morning, exactly at which hour Phileas Fogg rose, till half-past

[002-081] eleven, when he left the house for the [[reform-club|Reform Club]]—all the details of

[002-082] service, the tea and toast at twenty-three minutes past eight, the

[002-083] shaving-water at thirty-seven minutes past nine, and the toilet at

[002-084] twenty minutes before ten. Everything was regulated and foreseen that

[002-085] was to be done from half-past eleven a.m. till midnight, the hour at

[002-086] which the methodical gentleman retired.

[002-087] Mr. Fogg’s wardrobe was amply supplied and in the best taste. Each pair

[002-088] of trousers, coat, and vest bore a number, indicating the time of year

[002-089] and season at which they were in turn to be laid out for wearing; and

[002-090] the same system was applied to the master’s shoes. In short, the house

[002-091] in Saville Row, which must have been a very temple of disorder and

[002-092] unrest under the illustrious but dissipated Sheridan, was cosiness,

[002-093] comfort, and method idealised. There was no study, nor were there

[002-094] books, which would have been quite useless to Mr. Fogg; for at the

[002-095] Reform two libraries, one of general literature and the other of law

[002-096] and politics, were at his service. A moderate-sized safe stood in his

[002-097] bedroom, constructed so as to defy fire as well as burglars; but

[002-098] Passepartout found neither arms nor hunting weapons anywhere;

[002-099] everything betrayed the most tranquil and peaceable habits.

[002-100] Having scrutinised the house from top to bottom, he rubbed his hands, a

[002-101] broad smile overspread his features, and he said joyfully, “This is

[002-102] just what I wanted! Ah, we shall get on together, Mr. Fogg and I! What

[002-103] a domestic and regular gentleman! A real machine; well, I don’t mind

[002-104] serving a machine.”

[002-105] CHAPTER III.

[002-106] IN WHICH A CONVERSATION TAKES PLACE WHICH SEEMS LIKELY TO COST PHILEAS

[002-107] FOGG DEAR

[002-108] Phileas Fogg, having shut the door of his house at half-past eleven,

[002-109] and having put his right foot before his left five hundred and

[002-110] seventy-five times, and his left foot before his right five hundred and

[002-111] seventy-six times, reached the Reform Club, an imposing edifice in Pall

[002-112] Mall, which could not have cost less than three millions. He repaired

[002-113] at once to the dining-room, the nine windows of which open upon a

[002-114] tasteful garden, where the trees were already gilded with an autumn

[002-115] colouring; and took his place at the habitual table, the cover of which

[002-116] had already been laid for him. His breakfast consisted of a side-dish,

[002-117] a broiled fish with Reading sauce, a scarlet slice of roast beef

[002-118] garnished with mushrooms, a rhubarb and gooseberry tart, and a morsel

[002-119] of Cheshire cheese, the whole being washed down with several cups of

[002-120] tea, for which the Reform is famous. He rose at thirteen minutes to

[002-121] one, and directed his steps towards the large hall, a sumptuous

[002-122] apartment adorned with lavishly-framed paintings. A flunkey handed him

[002-123] an uncut _[[the-times-newspaper|Times]]_, which he proceeded to cut with a skill which betrayed

[002-124] familiarity with this delicate operation. The perusal of this paper

[002-125] absorbed Phileas Fogg until a quarter before four, whilst the

[002-126] _Standard_, his next task, occupied him till the dinner hour. Dinner

[002-127] passed as breakfast had done, and Mr. Fogg re-appeared in the

[002-128] reading-room and sat down to the _Pall Mall_ at twenty minutes before

[002-129] six. Half an hour later several members of the Reform came in and drew

[002-130] up to the fireplace, where a coal fire was steadily burning. They were

[002-131] Mr. Fogg’s usual partners at whist: [[andrew-stuart|Andrew Stuart]], an engineer; John

[002-132] Sullivan and Samuel Fallentin, bankers; Thomas Flanagan, a brewer; and

[002-133] Gauthier Ralph, one of the Directors of the Bank of England—all rich

[002-134] and highly respectable personages, even in a club which comprises the

[002-135] princes of English trade and finance.

[002-136] “Well, Ralph,” said Thomas Flanagan, “what about that robbery?”

[002-137] “Oh,” replied Stuart, “the Bank will lose the money.”

[002-138] “On the contrary,” broke in Ralph, “I hope we may put our hands on the

[002-139] robber. Skilful detectives have been sent to all the principal ports of

[002-140] America and the Continent, and he’ll be a clever fellow if he slips

[002-141] through their fingers.”

[002-142] “But have you got the robber’s description?” asked Stuart.

[002-143] “In the first place, he is no robber at all,” returned Ralph,

[002-144] positively.

[002-145] “What! a fellow who makes off with fifty-five thousand pounds, no

[002-146] robber?”

[002-147] “No.”

[002-148] “Perhaps he’s a manufacturer, then.”

[002-149] “The _[[daily-telegraph|Daily Telegraph]]_ says that he is a gentleman.”

[002-150] It was Phileas Fogg, whose head now emerged from behind his newspapers,

[002-151] who made this remark. He bowed to his friends, and entered into the

[002-152] conversation. The affair which formed its subject, and which was town

[002-153] talk, had occurred three days before at the Bank of England. A package

[002-154] of banknotes, to the value of fifty-five thousand pounds, had been

[002-155] taken from the principal cashier’s table, that functionary being at the

[002-156] moment engaged in registering the receipt of three shillings and

[002-157] sixpence. Of course, he could not have his eyes everywhere. Let it be

[002-158] observed that the Bank of England reposes a touching confidence in the

[002-159] honesty of the public. There are neither guards nor gratings to protect

[002-160] its treasures; gold, silver, banknotes are freely exposed, at the mercy

[002-161] of the first comer. A keen observer of English customs relates that,

[002-162] being in one of the rooms of the Bank one day, he had the curiosity to

[002-163] examine a gold ingot weighing some seven or eight pounds. He took it

[002-164] up, scrutinised it, passed it to his neighbour, he to the next man, and

[002-165] so on until the ingot, going from hand to hand, was transferred to the

[002-166] end of a dark entry; nor did it return to its place for half an hour.

[002-167] Meanwhile, the cashier had not so much as raised his head. But in the

[002-168] present instance things had not gone so smoothly. The package of notes

[002-169] not being found when five o’clock sounded from the ponderous clock in

[002-170] the “drawing office,” the amount was passed to the account of profit

[002-171] and loss. As soon as the robbery was discovered, picked detectives

[002-172] hastened off to [[liverpool|Liverpool]], Glasgow, Havre, [[suez|Suez]], [[brindisi|Brindisi]], [[new-york|New York]],

[002-173] and other ports, inspired by the proffered reward of two thousand

[002-174] pounds, and five per cent. on the sum that might be recovered.

[002-175] Detectives were also charged with narrowly watching those who arrived

[002-176] at or left London by rail, and a judicial examination was at once

[002-177] entered upon.

[002-178] There were real grounds for supposing, as the _Daily Telegraph_ said,

[002-179] that the thief did not belong to a professional band. On the day of the

[002-180] robbery a well-dressed gentleman of polished manners, and with a

[002-181] well-to-do air, had been observed going to and fro in the paying room

[002-182] where the crime was committed. A description of him was easily procured

[002-183] and sent to the detectives; and some hopeful spirits, of whom Ralph was

[002-184] one, did not despair of his apprehension. The [[passport-and-identity|papers]] and clubs were

[002-185] full of the affair, and everywhere people were discussing the

[002-186] probabilities of a successful pursuit; and the Reform Club was

[002-187] especially agitated, several of its members being Bank officials.
