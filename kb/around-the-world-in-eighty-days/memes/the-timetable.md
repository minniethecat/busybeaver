# The Timetable

The eighty-day timetable is the mathematical skeleton of the novel — the schedule of arrivals and departures, published in the *Daily Telegraph*, that [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] stakes twenty thousand pounds upon. It transforms the journey from an adventure into a calculation, and its constant erosion by delays, accidents, and human interference drives the novel's suspense.

## The Wager

When Fogg announces at the [[reform-club|Reform Club]] that the world can be circumnavigated in eighty days, his companions are incredulous. "The world has grown smaller," Fogg replies, "since a man can now go round it ten times more quickly than a hundred years ago." He produces the *Daily Telegraph*'s itinerary:

| Stage | Route | Time |
|-------|-------|------|
| London to Suez | Rail and steamer via Mont Cenis and Brindisi | 7 days |
| Suez to Bombay | Steamer | 13 days |
| Bombay to Calcutta | Rail | 3 days |
| Calcutta to Hong Kong | Steamer | 13 days |
| Hong Kong to Yokohama | Steamer | 6 days |
| Yokohama to San Francisco | Steamer | 22 days |
| San Francisco to New York | Rail | 7 days |
| New York to London | Steamer and rail | 9 days |
| **Total** | | **80 days** |

The itinerary is a masterpiece of Victorian confidence in technology. It assumes that every steamer will depart on schedule, that every railway will be complete, that no storm, accident, or human error will intervene. (PN: 003-035)

> "In eighty days," interrupted Phileas Fogg. "That is true, gentlemen," added [[john-sullivan|John Sullivan]]. "Only eighty days, now that the section of the Great Indian Peninsula Railway between Rothal and Allahabad is opened." (PN: 003-031)

## The Timetable as Character

The timetable functions almost as a character in the novel — an antagonist as implacable as Fix. Every page records the relentless passing of hours and minutes. Fogg consults his pocket-watch constantly, comparing local time with the time of the next scheduled departure. Passepartout, who forgot to adjust his own watch from London time, becomes the unwitting embodiment of the tension between mechanical time and lived experience.

When the railway across India proves incomplete — the section between Kholby and Allahabad not yet laid — the timetable suffers its first breach. Fogg is forced to improvise, purchasing an elephant when no other conveyance is available. Each subsequent chapter records further deviations: missed steamers, unfavorable winds, hostile terrain. The timetable, once a guarantee, becomes a measure of deficit — the gap between what was planned and what is possible. (PN: 010-010)

## Time as Antagonist

The novel's fundamental conceit is that time itself is Fogg's true antagonist. Fix is merely an agent of delay; storms and incomplete railways are obstacles of circumstance. But the clock — ticking down from eighty days to zero — is implacable. The timetable gives this abstraction a concrete form: a list of deadlines, each of which must be met or the whole collapses.

This is why the novel's climax is structured as a race against the clock in the final chapters. Fogg has reached London; he is free; but he is five minutes late — and then, in the novel's most famous reversal, he is not. The international date line, which he had not accounted for in his calculations, has given him back a day. The timetable was not wrong; it was merely incomplete — premised on a world where time runs in one direction only. (PN: 035-020)

## The Victorian Obsession

The timetable reflects a broader Victorian obsession with precision, punctuality, and the mastery of time. The railway timetable, introduced in the 1830s, had standardized time across Britain — replacing the local times kept by parish churches with a single, mechanical standard. By 1872, this standardization was going global. Fogg's journey is both a celebration of this achievement and a demonstration of its fragility: the timetable can calculate everything except the human, the accidental, and the sublime.
