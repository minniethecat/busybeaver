Suspicion and trust form a central thematic tension in the novel through the parallel journeys of [[phileas-fogg]] and his pursuer [[fix-detective]]. Fix suspects Fogg of being the Bank of England robber; Passepartout, unaware of Fix's identity, trusts him as a fellow traveler. This asymmetrical knowledge creates sustained dramatic irony and drives the novel's detective subplot, while Fogg himself remains serenely unaware of — or indifferent to — the suspicion trailing him across continents.

## Definition

In the novel's context, "suspicion and trust" describes the misaligned perceptions among the three main travelers: Fix wrongly suspects Fogg of theft; Passepartout wrongly trusts Fix as an ally; Fogg, trusting his own precision, neither suspects pursuit nor seeks others' trust. This triangular dynamic generates much of the novel's narrative tension.

## Role in the Novel

The suspicion subplot provides the novel's primary source of tension beyond the natural obstacles of the journey. While Fogg races against time and geography, he is simultaneously shadowed by a representative of the law whose suspicions grow more entrenched with each coincidence that seems to confirm Fogg's guilt.

Fix's initial suspicion arises from Fogg's resemblance to the description of the bank robber and his hasty departure from London ([PN=004-122]–[PN=004-183]). As the journey progresses and Fix observes Fogg's calm indifference to delays that would alarm a guilty man, doubt enters the detective's mind — yet professional pride and the sunk cost of his pursuit keep him committed.

> Fix instinctively took the passport, and with a rapid glance read the description. An almost imperceptible movement of satisfaction escaped him. ([PN=005-006])

The trust dimension appears most poignantly through [[jean-passepartout]], who confides in Fix, unaware that he shares drinks and conversation with his master's pursuer ([PN=012-194]–[PN=013-110]). This dramatic irony runs through much of the journey's middle sections.

## Resolution

The suspicion arc resolves in the novel's final chapters. When Fix finally arrests Fogg in Liverpool, the suspicion that has shadowed the entire journey reaches its climax — only to collapse moments later when the real robber is captured ([PN=031-030]). Fix's professional certainty transforms into mortification. Fogg, characteristically, does not reproach him.

The trust arc resolves through Passepartout's gradual realization of Fix's identity — and his fury at the detective's deception. Yet in the end, Fix joins the party's return to the Reform Club, his suspicion transformed into respect for the man he once pursued.

## Historical Context

Verne wrote during an era fascinated by detective fiction (Poe's Dupin had appeared decades earlier; Conan Doyle's Holmes would debut shortly after). Fix represents the emerging figure of the modern detective — professional, systematic, operating across international jurisdictions. His pursuit of Fogg across colonial territories also reflects the reach of European law enforcement into imperial spaces, though the novel treats this lightly.

> "No, Mr. Fix," replied the consul. "She was signalled this morning. But wait patiently. The Mongolia always arrives in time." ([PN=004-134])
