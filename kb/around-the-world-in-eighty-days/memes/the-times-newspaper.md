# The Times

_The Times_ of London was the newspaper that [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] read each morning at the [[reform-club|Reform Club]], cutting its pages with a practiced hand. As the most authoritative voice of the Victorian press establishment, the _Times_ led the chorus of newspapers that dismissed Fogg's eighty-day wager as impossible — a position it maintained until his triumphant return proved the entire press corps wrong.

## Fogg's Daily Ritual

The _Times_ was part of the precise morning routine that defined Fogg's life before the wager. Each day at the Reform Club, he would take "an uncut _Times_, which he proceeded to cut with a skill which betrayed long practice" (PN_002-123). Along with the _Standard_, _Morning Post_, and _Daily News_, the paper was among Fogg's "sole pastimes" beyond reading and playing whist (PN_001-096).

This detail — the careful cutting of the pages — reveals something essential about Fogg's character: his mechanical exactness, his preference for routine, and his engagement with the wider world through the filter of printed information rather than personal experience. The man who would race around the globe began each day by methodically reading about it.

## The Press Verdict on the Wager

When news of Fogg's wager broke, the _Times_ led the dismissal:

> "The _Times_, _Standard_, _Morning Post_, and _Daily News_, and twenty other highly respectable newspapers scouted Mr. Fogg's project as madness" (PN_004-022).

Only the [[daily-telegraph|Daily Telegraph]] offered hesitant support. The _Times_ argued, in essence, that such a journey could not be accomplished — that unforeseen delays, missed connections, and the sheer physical impossibility of the schedule would doom the attempt. The newspaper's position was the voice of institutional reason: the world was too large, too unpredictable, to be compressed into eighty days.

## The Paper That Was Wrong

The novel's conclusion delivered a stinging rebuke to the _Times_ and its fellow skeptics. Fogg's success was not merely a personal triumph but a demonstration that the press establishment had misjudged what the modern transportation network — railways, steamships, the Suez Canal — had made possible. The newspaper that prided itself on being the most informed voice in London had failed to grasp the central technological fact of its age: the world had grown smaller, and a man could now "go round it ten times more quickly than a hundred years ago" (PN_003-022).

This irony was not lost on Verne's readers. The _Times_ — the paper of record — had been wrong about the most newsworthy event of the season. The novel thus offers a gentle satire of the press: even the most authoritative sources can miss the story unfolding before them.

## Relationship to Other Newspapers

The press ecosystem in the novel reflects the competitive London newspaper market of the 1870s. The _Times_ stood at the apex, the voice of the establishment, while the [[daily-telegraph|Daily Telegraph]] occupied a more iconoclastic position — younger, more willing to entertain the improbable. The [[illustrated-london-news|Illustrated London News]], meanwhile, represented the visual press, with engravings that brought distant events to life for London readers. Together, these papers documented Fogg's journey — first in disbelief, eventually in astonishment.
