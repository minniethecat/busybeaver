[004-001] Just as the [[railway-travel|train]] was whirling through Sydenham, [[passepartout|Passepartout]] suddenly

[004-002] uttered a cry of despair.

[004-003] “What’s the matter?” asked [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]].

[004-004] “Alas! In my hurry—I—I forgot—”

[004-005] “What?”

[004-006] “To turn off the gas in my room!”

[004-007] “Very well, young man,” returned Mr. Fogg, coolly; “it will burn—at

[004-008] your expense.”

[004-009] CHAPTER V.

[004-010] IN WHICH A NEW SPECIES OF FUNDS, UNKNOWN TO THE MONEYED MEN, APPEARS ON

[004-011] ’CHANGE

[004-012] Phileas Fogg rightly suspected that his departure from [[london|London]] would

[004-013] create a lively sensation at the West End. The news of the [[the-wager|bet]] spread

[004-014] through the [[reform-club|Reform Club]], and afforded an exciting topic of conversation

[004-015] to its members. From the club it soon got into the [[passport-and-identity|papers]] throughout

[004-016] England. The boasted “tour of the world” was talked about, disputed,

[004-017] argued with as much warmth as if the subject were another Alabama

[004-018] claim. Some took sides with Phileas Fogg, but the large majority shook

[004-019] their heads and declared against him; it was absurd, impossible, they

[004-020] declared, that the tour of the world could be made, except

[004-021] theoretically and on paper, in this minimum of time, and with the

[004-022] existing means of travelling. The _[[the-times-newspaper|Times]], Standard, Morning Post_, and

[004-023] _Daily News_, and twenty other highly respectable newspapers scouted

[004-024] Mr. Fogg’s project as madness; the _[[daily-telegraph|Daily Telegraph]]_ alone hesitatingly

[004-025] supported him. People in general thought him a lunatic, and blamed his

[004-026] Reform Club friends for having accepted a wager which betrayed the

[004-027] mental aberration of its proposer.

[004-028] Articles no less passionate than logical appeared on the question, for

[004-029] geography is one of the pet subjects of the English; and the columns

[004-030] devoted to Phileas Fogg’s venture were eagerly devoured by all classes

[004-031] of readers. At first some rash individuals, principally of the gentler

[004-032] sex, espoused his cause, which became still more popular when the

[004-033] _[[illustrated-london-news|Illustrated London News]]_ came out with his portrait, copied from a

[004-034] photograph in the Reform Club. A few readers of the _Daily Telegraph_

[004-035] even dared to say, “Why not, after all? Stranger things have come to

[004-036] pass.”

[004-037] At last a long article appeared, on the 7th of October, in the bulletin

[004-038] of the Royal Geographical Society, which treated the question from

[004-039] every point of view, and demonstrated the utter folly of the

[004-040] enterprise.

[004-041] Everything, it said, was against the travellers, every obstacle imposed

[004-042] alike by man and by nature. A miraculous agreement of the times of

[004-043] departure and arrival, which was impossible, was absolutely necessary

[004-044] to his success. He might, perhaps, reckon on the arrival of trains at

[004-045] the designated hours, in Europe, where the distances were relatively

[004-046] moderate; but when he calculated upon crossing India in three days, and

[004-047] the United States in seven, could he rely beyond misgiving upon

[004-048] accomplishing his task? There were accidents to machinery, the

[004-049] liability of trains to run off the line, collisions, bad weather, the

[004-050] blocking up by snow—were not all these against Phileas Fogg? Would he

[004-051] not find himself, when travelling by [[steamer-travel|steamer]] in winter, at the mercy of

[004-052] the winds and fogs? Is it uncommon for the best ocean steamers to be

[004-053] two or three days behind time? But a single delay would suffice to

[004-054] fatally break the chain of communication; should Phileas Fogg once

[004-055] miss, even by an hour; a steamer, he would have to wait for the next,

[004-056] and that would irrevocably render his attempt vain.

[004-057] This article made a great deal of noise, and, being copied into all the

[004-058] papers, seriously depressed the advocates of the rash tourist.

[004-059] Everybody knows that England is the world of betting men, who are of a

[004-060] higher class than mere gamblers; to bet is in the English temperament.

[004-061] Not only the members of the Reform, but the general public, made heavy

[004-062] wagers for or against Phileas Fogg, who was set down in the betting

[004-063] books as if he were a race-horse. Bonds were issued, and made their

[004-064] appearance on ’Change; “Phileas Fogg bonds” were offered at par or at a

[004-065] premium, and a great business was done in them. But five days after the

[004-066] article in the bulletin of the Geographical Society appeared, the

[004-067] demand began to subside: “Phileas Fogg” declined. They were offered by

[004-068] packages, at first of five, then of ten, until at last nobody would

[004-069] take less than twenty, fifty, a hundred!

[004-070] Lord Albemarle, an elderly paralytic [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]], was now the only

[004-071] advocate of Phileas Fogg left. This noble lord, who was fastened to his

[004-072] chair, would have given his fortune to be able to make the tour of the

[004-073] world, if it took ten years; and he bet five thousand pounds on Phileas

[004-074] Fogg. When the folly as well as the uselessness of the [[spirit-of-adventure|adventure]] was

[004-075] pointed out to him, he contented himself with replying, “If the thing

[004-076] is feasible, the first to do it ought to be an Englishman.”

[004-077] The Fogg party dwindled more and more, everybody was going against him,

[004-078] and the bets stood a hundred and fifty and two hundred to one; and a

[004-079] week after his departure an incident occurred which deprived him of

[004-080] backers at any price.

[004-081] The commissioner of police was sitting in his office at nine o’[[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]]

[004-082] one evening, when the following telegraphic dispatch was put into his

[004-083] hands:

[004-084] _[[suez|Suez]] to London._

[004-085] ROWAN, COMMISSIONER OF POLICE, SCOTLAND YARD:

[004-086] I’ve found the bank robber, Phileas Fogg. Send without delay

[004-087] warrant of arrest to [[bombay|Bombay]].

[004-088] FIX, _Detective_.

[004-089] The effect of this dispatch was instantaneous. The polished gentleman

[004-090] disappeared to give place to the bank robber. His photograph, which was

[004-091] hung with those of the rest of the members at the Reform Club, was

[004-092] minutely examined, and it betrayed, feature by feature, the description

[004-093] of the robber which had been provided to the police. The mysterious

[004-094] habits of Phileas Fogg were recalled; his solitary ways, his sudden

[004-095] departure; and it seemed clear that, in undertaking a tour round the

[004-096] world on the pretext of a wager, he had had no other end in view than

[004-097] to elude the detectives, and throw them off his track.

[004-098] CHAPTER VI.

[004-099] IN WHICH FIX, THE DETECTIVE, BETRAYS A VERY NATURAL IMPATIENCE

[004-100] The circumstances under which this telegraphic dispatch about Phileas

[004-101] Fogg was sent were as follows:

[004-102] The steamer “Mongolia,” belonging to the [[p-and-o-company|Peninsular and Oriental]]

[004-103] Company, built of iron, of two thousand eight hundred tons burden, and

[004-104] five hundred horse-power, was due at eleven o’clock a.m. on Wednesday,

[004-105] the 9th of October, at [[the-suez-canal|Suez]]. The “Mongolia” plied regularly between

[004-106] [[brindisi|Brindisi]] and Bombay _viâ_ the Suez Canal, and was one of the fastest

[004-107] steamers belonging to the company, always making more than ten knots an

[004-108] hour between Brindisi and Suez, and nine and a half between Suez and

[004-109] Bombay.

[004-110] Two men were promenading up and down the wharves, among the crowd of

[004-111] natives and strangers who were sojourning at this once straggling

[004-112] village—now, thanks to the enterprise of M. Lesseps, a fast-growing

[004-113] town. One was the British consul at Suez, who, despite the prophecies

[004-114] of the English Government, and the unfavourable predictions of

[004-115] Stephenson, was in the habit of seeing, from his office window, English

[004-116] ships daily passing to and fro on the great canal, by which the old

[004-117] roundabout [[around-the-world-route|route]] from England to India by the Cape of Good Hope was

[004-118] abridged by at least a half. The other was a small, slight-built

[004-119] personage, with a nervous, intelligent face, and bright eyes peering

[004-120] out from under eyebrows which he was incessantly twitching. He was just

[004-121] now manifesting unmistakable signs of impatience, nervously pacing up

[004-122] and down, and unable to stand still for a moment. This was [[detective-fix|Fix]], one of

[004-123] the detectives who had been dispatched from England in search of the

[004-124] bank robber; it was his task to narrowly watch every passenger who

[004-125] arrived at Suez, and to follow up all who seemed to be suspicious

[004-126] characters, or bore a resemblance to the description of the criminal,

[004-127] which he had received two days before from the police headquarters at

[004-128] London. The detective was evidently inspired by the hope of obtaining

[004-129] the splendid reward which would be the prize of success, and awaited

[004-130] with a feverish impatience, easy to understand, the arrival of the

[004-131] steamer “Mongolia.”

[004-132] “So you say, consul,” asked he for the twentieth time, “that this

[004-133] steamer is never behind time?”

[004-134] “No, [[fix-detective|Mr. Fix]],” replied [[british-consular-service|the consul]]. “She was bespoken yesterday at Port

[004-135] Said, and the rest of the way is of no account to such a craft. I

[004-136] repeat that the ‘Mongolia’ has been in advance of the time required by

[004-137] the company’s regulations, and gained the prize awarded for excess of

[004-138] speed.”

[004-139] “Does she come directly from Brindisi?”

[004-140] “Directly from Brindisi; she takes on the Indian mails there, and she

[004-141] left there Saturday at five p.m. Have patience, Mr. Fix; she will not

[004-142] be late. But really, I don’t see how, from the description you have,

[004-143] you will be able to recognise your man, even if he is on board the

[004-144] ‘Mongolia.’”

[004-145] “A man rather feels the presence of these fellows, consul, than

[004-146] recognises them. You must have a scent for them, and a scent is like a

[004-147] sixth sense which combines hearing, seeing, and smelling. I’ve arrested

[004-148] more than one of these gentlemen in my time, and, if my thief is on

[004-149] board, I’ll answer for it; he’ll not slip through my fingers.”

[004-150] “I hope so, Mr. Fix, for it was a heavy robbery.”

[004-151] “A magnificent robbery, consul; fifty-five thousand pounds! We don’t

[004-152] often have such windfalls. Burglars are getting to be so contemptible

[004-153] nowadays! A fellow gets hung for a handful of shillings!”

[004-154] “Mr. Fix,” said the consul, “I like your way of talking, and hope

[004-155] you’ll succeed; but I fear you will find it far from easy. Don’t you

[004-156] see, the description which you have there has a singular resemblance to

[004-157] an honest man?”

[004-158] “Consul,” remarked the detective, dogmatically, “great robbers always

[004-159] resemble honest folks. Fellows who have rascally faces have only one

[004-160] course to take, and that is to remain honest; otherwise they would be

[004-161] arrested off-hand. The artistic thing is, to unmask honest

[004-162] countenances; it’s no light task, I admit, but a real art.”

[004-163] Mr. Fix evidently was not wanting in a tinge of self-conceit.

[004-164] Little by little the scene on the quay became more animated; sailors of

[004-165] various nations, merchants, ship-brokers, porters, fellahs, bustled to

[004-166] and fro as if the steamer were immediately expected. The weather was

[004-167] clear, and slightly chilly. The minarets of the town loomed above the

[004-168] houses in the pale rays of the sun. A jetty pier, some two thousand

[004-169] yards along, extended into the roadstead. A number of fishing-smacks

[004-170] and coasting boats, some retaining the fantastic fashion of ancient

[004-171] galleys, were discernible on the Red Sea.

[004-172] As he passed among the busy crowd, Fix, according to habit, scrutinised

[004-173] the passers-by with a keen, rapid glance.

[004-174] It was now half-past ten.

[004-175] “The steamer doesn’t come!” he exclaimed, as the port clock struck.

[004-176] “She can’t be far off now,” returned his companion.

[004-177] “How long will she stop at Suez?”

[004-178] “Four hours; long enough to get in her coal. It is thirteen hundred and

[004-179] ten miles from Suez to Aden, at the other end of the Red Sea, and she

[004-180] has to take in a fresh coal supply.”

[004-181] “And does she go from Suez directly to Bombay?”

[004-182] “Without putting in anywhere.”

[004-183] “Good!” said Fix. “If the robber is on board he will no doubt get off

[004-184] at Suez, so as to reach the Dutch or French colonies in Asia by some

[004-185] other route. He ought to know that he would not be safe an hour in

[004-186] India, which is English soil.”

[004-187] “Unless,” objected the consul, “he is exceptionally shrewd. An English

[004-188] criminal, you know, is always better concealed in London than anywhere

[004-189] else.”

[004-190] This observation furnished the detective food for thought, and

[004-191] meanwhile the consul went away to his office. Fix, left alone, was more

[004-192] impatient than ever, having a presentiment that the robber was on board

[004-193] the “Mongolia.” If he had indeed left London intending to reach the New

[004-194] World, he would naturally take the route _viâ_ India, which was less

[004-195] watched and more difficult to watch than that of the Atlantic. But

[004-196] Fix’s reflections were soon interrupted by a succession of sharp

[004-197] whistles, which announced the arrival of the “Mongolia.” The porters

[004-198] and fellahs rushed down the quay, and a dozen boats pushed off from the

[004-199] shore to go and meet the steamer. Soon her gigantic hull appeared

[004-200] passing along between the banks, and eleven o’clock struck as she

[004-201] anchored in the road. She brought an unusual number of passengers, some

[004-202] of whom remained on deck to scan the picturesque panorama of the town,

[004-203] while the greater part disembarked in the boats, and landed on the

[004-204] quay.
