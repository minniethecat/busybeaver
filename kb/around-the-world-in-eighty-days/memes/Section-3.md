[003-001] Ralph would not concede that the work of the detectives was likely to

[003-002] be in vain, for he thought that the prize offered would greatly

[003-003] stimulate their zeal and activity. But [[andrew-stuart|Stuart]] was far from sharing this

[003-004] confidence; and, as they placed themselves at the whist-table, they

[003-005] continued to argue the matter. Stuart and Flanagan played together,

[003-006] while [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]] had Fallentin for his partner. As the game proceeded

[003-007] the conversation ceased, excepting between the rubbers, when it revived

[003-008] again.

[003-009] “I maintain,” said Stuart, “that the chances are in favour of the

[003-010] thief, who must be a shrewd fellow.”

[003-011] “Well, but where can he fly to?” asked Ralph. “No country is safe for

[003-012] him.”

[003-013] “Pshaw!”

[003-014] “Where could he go, then?”

[003-015] “Oh, I don’t know that. The world is big enough.”

[003-016] “It was once,” said Phileas Fogg, in a low tone. “Cut, sir,” he added,

[003-017] handing the cards to Thomas Flanagan.

[003-018] The discussion fell during the rubber, after which Stuart took up its

[003-019] thread.

[003-020] “What do you mean by ‘once’? Has the world grown smaller?”

[003-021] “Certainly,” returned Ralph. “I agree with Mr. Fogg. The world has

[003-022] grown smaller, since a man can now go round it ten times more quickly

[003-023] than a hundred years ago. And that is why the search for this thief

[003-024] will be more likely to succeed.”

[003-025] “And also why the thief can get away more easily.”

[003-026] “Be so good as to play, Mr. Stuart,” said Phileas Fogg.

[003-027] But the incredulous Stuart was not convinced, and when the hand was

[003-028] finished, said eagerly: “You have a strange way, Ralph, of proving that

[003-029] the world has grown smaller. So, because you can go round it in three

[003-030] months—”

[003-031] “In eighty days,” interrupted Phileas Fogg.

[003-032] “That is true, gentlemen,” added John Sullivan. “Only eighty days, now

[003-033] that the section between Rothal and [[allahabad|Allahabad]], on the Great Indian

[003-034] Peninsula Railway, has been opened. Here is the estimate made by the

[003-035] _[[daily-telegraph|Daily Telegraph]]:_—

[003-036] From [[london|London]] to [[suez|Suez]] _viâ_ Mont Cenis and [[brindisi|Brindisi]], by rail and

[003-037] steamboats ................. 7 days

[003-038] From [[the-suez-canal|Suez]] to [[bombay|Bombay]], by [[steamer-travel|steamer]] .................... 13 ”

[003-039] From Bombay to [[calcutta|Calcutta]], by rail ................... 3 ”

[003-040] From Calcutta to [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]], by steamer ............. 13 ”

[003-041] From Hong Kong to [[yokohama|Yokohama]] (Japan), by steamer ..... 6 ”

[003-042] From Yokohama to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]], by steamer ......... 22 ”

[003-043] From San Francisco to [[new-york|New York]], by rail ............. 7 ”

[003-044] From New York to London, by steamer and rail ........ 9 ”

[003-045] -------

[003-046] Total ............................................ 80 days.”

[003-047] “Yes, in eighty days!” exclaimed Stuart, who in his excitement made a

[003-048] false deal. “But that doesn’t take into account bad weather, contrary

[003-049] winds, shipwrecks, [[railway-travel|railway]] accidents, and so on.”

[003-050] “All included,” returned Phileas Fogg, continuing to play despite the

[003-051] discussion.

[003-052] “But suppose the Hindoos or Indians pull up the rails,” replied Stuart;

[003-053] “suppose they stop the trains, pillage the luggage-vans, and scalp the

[003-054] passengers!”

[003-055] “All included,” calmly retorted Fogg; adding, as he threw down the

[003-056] cards, “Two trumps.”

[003-057] Stuart, whose turn it was to deal, gathered them up, and went on: “You

[003-058] are right, theoretically, Mr. Fogg, but practically—”

[003-059] “Practically also, Mr. Stuart.”

[003-060] “I’d like to see you do it in eighty days.”

[003-061] “It depends on you. Shall we go?”

[003-062] “Heaven preserve me! But I would [[the-wager|wager]] four thousand pounds that such a

[003-063] journey, made under these conditions, is impossible.”

[003-064] “Quite possible, on the contrary,” returned Mr. Fogg.

[003-065] “Well, make it, then!”

[003-066] “The journey round the world in eighty days?”

[003-067] “Yes.”

[003-068] “I should like nothing better.”

[003-069] “When?”

[003-070] “At once. Only I warn you that I shall do it at your expense.”

[003-071] “It’s absurd!” cried Stuart, who was beginning to be annoyed at the

[003-072] persistency of his friend. “Come, let’s go on with the game.”

[003-073] “Deal over again, then,” said Phileas Fogg. “There’s a false deal.”

[003-074] Stuart took up the pack with a feverish hand; then suddenly put them

[003-075] down again.

[003-076] “Well, Mr. Fogg,” said he, “it shall be so: I will wager the four

[003-077] thousand on it.”

[003-078] “Calm yourself, my dear Stuart,” said Fallentin. “It’s only a joke.”

[003-079] “When I say I’ll wager,” returned Stuart, “I mean it.”

[003-080] “All right,” said Mr. Fogg; and, turning to the others, he continued:

[003-081] “I have a deposit of twenty thousand at Baring’s which I will willingly

[003-082] risk upon it.”

[003-083] “Twenty thousand pounds!” cried Sullivan. “Twenty thousand pounds,

[003-084] which you would lose by a single accidental delay!”

[003-085] “The unforeseen does not exist,” quietly replied Phileas Fogg.

[003-086] “But, Mr. Fogg, eighty days are only the estimate of the least possible

[003-087] time in which the journey can be made.”

[003-088] “A well-used minimum suffices for everything.”

[003-089] “But, in order not to exceed it, you must jump mathematically from the

[003-090] trains upon the steamers, and from the steamers upon the trains again.”

[003-091] “I will jump—mathematically.”

[003-092] “You are joking.”

[003-093] “A true Englishman doesn’t joke when he is talking about so serious a

[003-094] thing as a wager,” replied Phileas Fogg, solemnly. “I will bet twenty

[003-095] thousand pounds against anyone who wishes that I will make the tour of

[003-096] the world in eighty days or less; in nineteen hundred and twenty hours,

[003-097] or a hundred and fifteen thousand two hundred minutes. Do you accept?”

[003-098] “We accept,” replied Messrs. Stuart, Fallentin, Sullivan, Flanagan, and

[003-099] Ralph, after consulting each other.

[003-100] “Good,” said Mr. Fogg. “The train leaves for Dover at a quarter before

[003-101] nine. I will take it.”

[003-102] “This very evening?” asked Stuart.

[003-103] “This very evening,” returned Phileas Fogg. He took out and consulted a

[003-104] pocket almanac, and added, “As today is Wednesday, the 2nd of October,

[003-105] I shall be due in London in this very room of the [[reform-club|Reform Club]], on

[003-106] Saturday, the 21st of December, at a quarter before nine p.m.; or else

[003-107] the twenty thousand pounds, now deposited in my name at Baring’s, will

[003-108] belong to you, in fact and in right, gentlemen. Here is a cheque for

[003-109] the amount.”

[003-110] A memorandum of the wager was at once drawn up and signed by the six

[003-111] parties, during which Phileas Fogg preserved a stoical composure. He

[003-112] certainly did not bet to win, and had only staked the twenty thousand

[003-113] pounds, half of his fortune, because he foresaw that he might have to

[003-114] expend the other half to carry out this difficult, not to say

[003-115] unattainable, project. As for his antagonists, they seemed much

[003-116] agitated; not so much by the value of their stake, as because they had

[003-117] some scruples about betting under conditions so difficult to their

[003-118] friend.

[003-119] The [[precision-and-timekeeping|clock]] struck seven, and the party offered to suspend the game so

[003-120] that Mr. Fogg might make his preparations for departure.

[003-121] “I am quite ready now,” was his tranquil response. “Diamonds are

[003-122] trumps: be so good as to play, gentlemen.”

[003-123] CHAPTER IV.

[003-124] IN WHICH PHILEAS FOGG ASTOUNDS PASSEPARTOUT, HIS SERVANT

[003-125] Having won twenty guineas at whist, and taken leave of his friends,

[003-126] Phileas Fogg, at twenty-five minutes past seven, left the Reform Club.

[003-127] [[passepartout|Passepartout]], who had conscientiously studied the programme of his

[003-128] duties, was more than surprised to see his master guilty of the

[003-129] inexactness of appearing at this unaccustomed hour; for, according to

[003-130] rule, he was not due in Saville Row until precisely midnight.

[003-131] Mr. Fogg repaired to his bedroom, and called out, “Passepartout!”

[003-132] Passepartout did not reply. It could not be he who was called; it was

[003-133] not the right hour.

[003-134] “Passepartout!” repeated Mr. Fogg, without raising his voice.

[003-135] Passepartout made his appearance.

[003-136] “I’ve called you twice,” observed his master.

[003-137] “But it is not midnight,” responded the other, showing his watch.

[003-138] “I know it; I don’t blame you. We start for Dover and Calais in ten

[003-139] minutes.”

[003-140] A puzzled grin overspread Passepartout’s round face; clearly he had not

[003-141] comprehended his master.

[003-142] “Monsieur is going to leave home?”

[003-143] “Yes,” returned Phileas Fogg. “We are going round the world.”

[003-144] Passepartout opened wide his eyes, raised his eyebrows, held up his

[003-145] hands, and seemed about to collapse, so overcome was he with stupefied

[003-146] astonishment.

[003-147] “Round the world!” he murmured.

[003-148] “In eighty days,” responded Mr. Fogg. “So we haven’t a moment to lose.”

[003-149] “But the trunks?” gasped Passepartout, unconsciously swaying his head

[003-150] from right to left.

[003-151] “We’ll have no trunks; only a carpet-bag, with two shirts and three

[003-152] pairs of stockings for me, and the same for you. We’ll buy our clothes

[003-153] on the way. Bring down my mackintosh and traveling-cloak, and some

[003-154] stout shoes, though we shall do little walking. Make haste!”

[003-155] Passepartout tried to reply, but could not. He went out, mounted to his

[003-156] own room, fell into a chair, and muttered: “That’s good, that is! And

[003-157] I, who wanted to remain quiet!”

[003-158] He mechanically set about making the preparations for departure. Around

[003-159] the world in eighty days! Was his master a fool? No. Was this a joke,

[003-160] then? They were going to Dover; good! To Calais; good again! After all,

[003-161] Passepartout, who had been away from France five years, would not be

[003-162] sorry to set foot on his native soil again. Perhaps they would go as

[003-163] far as Paris, and it would do his eyes good to see Paris once more. But

[003-164] surely a [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] so chary of his steps would stop there; no

[003-165] doubt—but, then, it was none the less true that he was going away, this

[003-166] so domestic person hitherto!

[003-167] By eight o’clock Passepartout had packed the modest carpet-bag,

[003-168] containing the wardrobes of his master and himself; then, still

[003-169] troubled in mind, he carefully shut the door of his room, and descended

[003-170] to Mr. Fogg.

[003-171] Mr. Fogg was quite ready. Under his arm might have been observed a

[003-172] red-bound copy of [[bradshaws-guide|Bradshaw]]’s Continental Railway Steam Transit and

[003-173] General Guide, with its timetables showing the arrival and departure of

[003-174] steamers and railways. He took the carpet-bag, opened it, and slipped

[003-175] into it a goodly roll of Bank of England notes, which would pass

[003-176] wherever he might go.

[003-177] “You have forgotten nothing?” asked he.

[003-178] “Nothing, monsieur.”

[003-179] “My mackintosh and cloak?”

[003-180] “Here they are.”

[003-181] “Good! Take this carpet-bag,” handing it to Passepartout. “Take good

[003-182] care of it, for there are twenty thousand pounds in it.”

[003-183] Passepartout nearly dropped the bag, as if the twenty thousand pounds

[003-184] were in gold, and weighed him down.

[003-185] Master and man then descended, the street-door was double-locked, and

[003-186] at the end of Saville Row they took a cab and drove rapidly to Charing

[003-187] Cross. The cab stopped before the railway station at twenty minutes

[003-188] past eight. Passepartout jumped off the box and followed his master,

[003-189] who, after paying the cabman, was about to enter the station, when a

[003-190] poor beggar-woman, with a child in her arms, her naked feet smeared

[003-191] with mud, her head covered with a wretched bonnet, from which hung a

[003-192] tattered feather, and her shoulders shrouded in a ragged shawl,

[003-193] approached, and mournfully asked for alms.

[003-194] Mr. Fogg took out the twenty guineas he had just won at whist, and

[003-195] handed them to the beggar, saying, “Here, my good woman. I’m glad that

[003-196] I met you;” and passed on.

[003-197] Passepartout had a moist sensation about the eyes; his master’s action

[003-198] touched his susceptible heart.

[003-199] Two first-class tickets for Paris having been speedily purchased, Mr.

[003-200] Fogg was crossing the station to the train, when he perceived his five

[003-201] friends of the Reform.

[003-202] “Well, gentlemen,” said he, “I’m off, you see; and, if you will examine

[003-203] my [[passport-and-identity|passport]] when I get back, you will be able to judge whether I have

[003-204] accomplished the journey agreed upon.”

[003-205] “Oh, that would be quite unnecessary, Mr. Fogg,” said Ralph politely.

[003-206] “We will trust your word, as a gentleman of honour.”

[003-207] “You do not forget when you are due in London again?” asked Stuart.

[003-208] “In eighty days; on Saturday, the 21st of December, 1872, at a quarter

[003-209] before nine p.m. Good-bye, gentlemen.”

[003-210] Phileas Fogg and his servant seated themselves in a first-class

[003-211] carriage at twenty minutes before nine; five minutes later the whistle

[003-212] screamed, and the train slowly glided out of the station.

[003-213] The night was dark, and a fine, steady rain was falling. Phileas Fogg,

[003-214] snugly ensconced in his corner, did not open his lips. Passepartout,

[003-215] not yet recovered from his stupefaction, clung mechanically to the

[003-216] carpet-bag, with its enormous treasure.
