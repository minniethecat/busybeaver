# Circumnavigation

The act of traveling around the entire circumference of the Earth — the central organizing premise of [[phileas-fogg|Phileas Fogg]]'s journey and the novel's framework for exploring the increasingly interconnected world of the late nineteenth century.

## The Wager

Fogg's circumnavigation is driven by [[the-wager|a £20,000 wager]] at the [[reform-club|Reform Club]]: that he can travel around the world in no more than eighty days. The wager originates from a *Daily Telegraph* article calculating that, with the latest railway and steamship connections, such a journey is mathematically possible:

> "the world in eighty days or less; in nineteen hundred and twenty hours, or a hundred and fifteen thousand two hundred minutes" (PN: 003-096)

Fogg's fellow club members are incredulous. "The journey round the world in eighty days?" they ask; "I'd like to see you do it in eighty days" (PN: 003-066, 003-060). Fogg's response is characteristically precise: "In eighty days; on Saturday, the 21st of December, 1872, at a quarter before nine p.m." (PN: 003-208).

## The Route

The journey follows an eastward course: [[london|London]] → [[suez|Suez]] → [[bombay|Bombay]] → [[calcutta|Calcutta]] → [[hong-kong|Hong Kong]] → [[yokohama|Yokohama]] → [[san-francisco|San Francisco]] → [[new-york|New York]] → [[london|London]]. This direction proves crucial at the climax: traveling eastward means Fogg "gains" a day by crossing the International Date Line — a fact he does not realize until the final moments. As the chapter summary notes, Fogg "had accomplished the journey round the world in eighty days" (PN: 031-136).

The route was not a leisurely grand tour. As Passepartout himself reflected: to travel "around the world in eighty days! Was his master a fool? No. Was he in jest? No" (PN: 003-159). The journey was a calculated demonstration that the globe had shrunk to the scale of a timetable.

## Technological Context

Fogg's circumnavigation in eighty days was, in 1872, at the very edge of technological possibility. It required the coordinated use of:

- **Railways**: The Great Indian Peninsula Railway, the transcontinental American railroad, and European trunk lines
- **Steamers**: The Mongolia, Carnatic, Rangoon, and Henrietta — each connecting one continent to the next
- **Telegraph**: The global telegraphic network that allows [[fix-detective|Detective Fix]] to track Fogg and newspapers to follow his progress
- **The Suez Canal**: Opened in 1869, the canal was the crucial link that made an eighty-day schedule possible, cutting thousands of miles from the traditional route around the Cape of Good Hope

The novel was published in 1872, the same year as the events it depicts — making it a near-future extrapolation of existing technology rather than science fiction. Verne's readers could verify the railway timetables and steamer schedules for themselves. The *Mongolia*, the *Carnatic*, and the other vessels Fogg boards were real ships on real routes, and Verne took care to ground his fiction in the verifiable facts of contemporary transportation.

## The Eastward Advantage

Fogg's choice of an eastward route was not arbitrary. Heading east — "in the face of the sun" — each day of travel is fractionally shorter (PN: 008-035). Over the full 360 degrees of longitude, the cumulative effect is exactly twenty-four hours. Fogg did not plan for this; he simply followed the fastest available connections. But the eastward direction transforms the novel's denouement: Fogg arrives in London believing himself five minutes late, only to discover he has a full day in hand.

The novel's conclusion records that Fogg "had employed every means of conveyance — steamers, railways, carriages, yachts, trading-vessels, sledges, an elephant" to complete the circuit (PN: 031-193). The circumnavigation was not merely a journey — it was a systematic demonstration that every known mode of transport could be linked into a single, unbroken chain around the globe.

## Thematic Significance

Circumnavigation in the novel is more than a travel itinerary. It is a meditation on the closing of the world: the era when steam, steel, and the [[telegraph|electric telegraph]] had knitted the continents together so tightly that a man could race the sun itself and, for £20,000, have a chance of winning. Fogg's journey also demonstrates that mastery of the global timetable — [[precision-and-timekeeping|precision and timekeeping]] — is a form of power.

The circumnavigation is not merely Fogg's personal challenge but a marker of a historical threshold. In 1872, the world had become small enough to be crossed in eighty days but large enough that the attempt was still audacious. Verne captured a moment when the planet's geography was fully known but its connectivity was still being proven — when a man could, for the first time in human history, buy a through-ticket around the world.
