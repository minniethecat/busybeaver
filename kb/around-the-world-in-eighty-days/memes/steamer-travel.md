# Steamer Travel

## Definition

Steamer travel in *Around the World in Eighty Days* refers to the network of steam-powered ocean liners -- primarily operated by the Peninsular and Oriental Company (P&O) and the Pacific Mail Steamship Company -- that constitute the maritime backbone of Phileas Fogg's circumnavigation. Steam navigation, combined with the Suez Canal and the transcontinental railways, is what makes an eighty-day journey around the world conceivable: Fogg's original itinerary allocates twenty-six days to rail and no fewer than forty-four days to steamers.

## Role in the Novel

Steamers serve three intertwined functions in the novel. First, they are the structural framework of the journey: the timetable published in the Morning Chronicle (003-038--003-040) lists each steamer connection -- Suez to Bombay (13 days), Calcutta to Hong Kong (13 days), Hong Kong to Yokohama (6 days), Yokohama to San Francisco (22 days) -- and the narrative's chapters map onto these maritime segments. Second, they function as a floating microcosm of Victorian society, where Fogg, Aouda, Passepartout, and Fix share meals, play whist, and negotiate their relationships against the backdrop of the open sea. Third, they embody the tension between technological precision and natural contingency: every steamer is "due" at a certain hour (014-099), but storms, boiler repairs (014-132), and fuel shortages (029-011) constantly threaten the schedule.

Fogg's relationship with steamers evolves across the journey. He begins as a passive passenger who expects the Mongolia to behave "like some vast clock" (005-075). By the end, he has commandeered the Henrietta, imprisoned its captain in his own cabin (028-065), and burned the ship's wooden superstructure as fuel when the coal runs out (029-011). The trajectory from passenger to commander mirrors Fogg's broader transformation from a creature of timetables to an improvisational adventurer.

## Key Steamers

|---------|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Mongolia** | Brindisi to Bombay via Suez | The first steamer. Fogg boards at Brindisi; Fix boards at Suez. The P&O flagship, "a screw steamer of two thousand eight hundred tons burden... of iron, with a lifting screw" | **004-100**, **005-015** |
| **Rangoon** | Calcutta to Hong Kong via Singapore | The P&O steamer that carries Fogg from India to China. Described as "a screw steamer, built of iron, of about seventeen hundred and seventy-seven tons, with four hundred horse-power" | **012-136**, **012-142** |
| **Carnatic** | Hong Kong to Yokohama | The steamer Fogg misses due to Fix's scheming. Its early departure (boiler repairs completed sooner than expected) creates the crisis at Hong Kong | **014-145**, **016-023** |
| **Tankadere** | Hong Kong to Shanghai | The pilot-boat Fogg charters after missing the Carnatic. A sailing vessel, not a steamer, it represents Fogg's shift to improvisation | **016-102**, **017-148** |
| **General Grant** | Yokohama to San Francisco | The Pacific Mail steamer that carries Fogg, Aouda, Passepartout, and Fix across the Pacific | **019-040**, **020-014** |

## Related Concepts

- **Railway Travel**: The complementary land-based technology. Fogg's journey is a rhythm of "jumping from a steamer upon a railway train, and from a railway train upon a steamer again" (006-079).
- **The Suez Canal**: Opened in 1869, the canal enables direct steamer passage from the Mediterranean to the Red Sea, reducing the London-to-Bombay journey by thousands of miles.
- **Around the World Route**: The integrated network of steamer and railway connections that makes the wager possible.
- **Coal Logistics**: The practical constraint on steamer travel. Coal "is a serious matter at such distances from the coal-mines" (006-106), and Fogg's burning of the Henrietta's superstructure dramatises the fragility of steam-powered mobility.
- **Time Pressure**: The steamer schedule creates the novel's temporal architecture. Missing a steamer means waiting "a week for the sailing of the next" (014-134--014-135).

## Historical and Cultural Context

The P&O Company, founded in 1837, was the dominant maritime power on the Suez-Bombay-Calcutta-Hong Kong route at the time of the novel's setting (1872). Its steamers, combined with the Suez Canal (opened 1869) and the Great Indian Peninsula Railway, had compressed the London-to-Calcutta journey from months to weeks. The Pacific Mail Steamship Company, founded in 1848, provided the Yokohama-to-San Francisco connection that completes Fogg's transpacific crossing.

Verne's enthusiasm for steam technology is evident throughout the novel. The steamers are described with the precision of a technical manual -- tonnage, horsepower, construction materials -- but they also serve as vehicles for wonder. The Mongolia "ploughed across the waters of the Red Sea" (005-075), the Rangoon's screw propeller churns through the Chinese seas, and even the improvised Tankadere becomes, in Verne's hands, a vessel of romance and daring. Steam travel in the novel is both a solved engineering problem and a continuing source of narrative possibility.
