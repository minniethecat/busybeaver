# Omaha

Omaha, Nebraska, is the city where the Union Pacific Railroad meets the eastern rail network, making it the gateway to the American West — and, for Phileas Fogg, the gateway to the Pacific coast. It is one of the most strategically significant stops on Fogg's global itinerary.

## Role in the Novel

Omaha marks the transition point between the established railway network of the eastern United States and the transcontinental line that crosses the Great Plains and the Rocky Mountains to the Pacific. Five main lines connect Omaha with [[new-york|New York]], while the Union Pacific continues westward for 1,386 miles to [[san-francisco|San Francisco]]. (PN: 021-190)

> Five main lines connect Omaha with New York. Between Omaha and the Pacific the railway crosses a territory still infested with Indians and wild beasts — a vast tract which the Mormons began to colonise about 1845, after they had been driven out of Illinois. (021-193)

The construction of the Union Pacific had fixed the end of the line at Omaha, in Nebraska. From Omaha, the railway passes along the left bank of the Platte River before ascending into the Rocky Mountains — a route that Fogg and his companions traverse under the constant threat of delay from bison herds, broken bridges, and the ever-present [[detective-fix|Detective Fix]]. (PN: 022-007)

## The Crossing

Fogg's train reaches Omaha on the evening schedule, having survived a close encounter with a herd of ten thousand bison that brought the locomotive to a standstill. The delay is made up, and the train proceeds eastward toward [[chicago|Chicago]] — but not before telegraphic communications alert Fix to the progress of his quarry. (PN: 025-015)

On the westward journey from Omaha, the railway passes through a landscape that Verne describes in vivid detail — the Platte River valley, the Laramie Mountains, and the vast emptiness of the Great Basin. The isolation of this route makes it both dangerous and essential for Fogg's timetable.

## Strategic Significance

In the geography of Fogg's journey, Omaha is the linchpin of the American crossing. Without the Union Pacific completed to Omaha, and without the five eastern trunk lines radiating from it, the transcontinental transit that Fogg relies upon would be impossible. The city thus exemplifies the novel's broader theme: that the world of 1872 had been knitted together by steam and steel to an extent that made an eighty-day circumnavigation not merely conceivable, but achievable.
