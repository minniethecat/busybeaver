[019-001] The streets at last became quiet, and the patrol, the officers of

[019-002] which, in their splendid costumes, and surrounded by their suites,

[019-003] [[passepartout|Passepartout]] thought seemed like ambassadors, succeeded the bustling

[019-004] crowd. Each time a company passed, Passepartout chuckled, and said to

[019-005] himself: “Good! another Japanese embassy departing for Europe!”

[019-006] CHAPTER XXIII.

[019-007] IN WHICH PASSEPARTOUT’S NOSE BECOMES OUTRAGEOUSLY LONG

[019-008] The next morning poor, jaded, famished Passepartout said to himself

[019-009] that he must get something to eat at all hazards, and the sooner he did

[019-010] so the better. He might, indeed, sell his [[precision-and-timekeeping|watch]]; but he would have

[019-011] starved first. Now or never he must use the strong, if not melodious

[019-012] voice which nature had bestowed upon him. He knew several French and

[019-013] English songs, and resolved to try them upon the Japanese, who must be

[019-014] lovers of music, since they were for ever pounding on their cymbals,

[019-015] tam-tams, and tambourines, and could not but appreciate European

[019-016] talent.

[019-017] It was, perhaps, rather early in the morning to get up a concert, and

[019-018] the audience prematurely aroused from their slumbers, might not

[019-019] possibly pay their entertainer with coin bearing the Mikado’s features.

[019-020] Passepartout therefore decided to wait several hours; and, as he was

[019-021] sauntering along, it occurred to him that he would seem rather too well

[019-022] dressed for a wandering artist. The idea struck him to change his

[019-023] garments for clothes more in harmony with his project; by which he

[019-024] might also get a little money to satisfy the immediate cravings of

[019-025] hunger. The resolution taken, it remained to carry it out.

[019-026] It was only after a long search that Passepartout discovered a native

[019-027] dealer in old clothes, to whom he applied for an exchange. The man

[019-028] liked the European costume, and ere long Passepartout issued from his

[019-029] shop accoutred in an old Japanese coat, and a sort of one-sided turban,

[019-030] faded with long use. A few small pieces of silver, moreover, jingled in

[019-031] his pocket.

[019-032] “Good!” thought he. “I will imagine I am at the Carnival!”

[019-033] His first care, after being thus “Japanesed,” was to enter a tea-house

[019-034] of modest appearance, and, upon half a bird and a little rice, to

[019-035] breakfast like a man for whom dinner was as yet a problem to be solved.

[019-036] “Now,” thought he, when he had eaten heartily, “I mustn’t lose my head.

[019-037] I can’t sell this costume again for one still more Japanese. I must

[019-038] consider how to leave this country of the Sun, of which I shall not

[019-039] retain the most delightful of memories, as quickly as possible.”

[019-040] It occurred to him to visit the steamers which were about to leave for

[019-041] America. He would offer himself as a cook or servant, in payment of his

[019-042] passage and meals. Once at [[san-francisco|San Francisco]], he would find some means of

[019-043] going on. The difficulty was, how to traverse the four thousand seven

[019-044] hundred miles of the [[the-pacific-crossing|Pacific]] which lay between Japan and the New World.

[019-045] Passepartout was not the man to let an idea go begging, and directed

[019-046] his steps towards the docks. But, as he approached them, his project,

[019-047] which at first had seemed so simple, began to grow more and more

[019-048] formidable to his mind. What need would they have of a cook or servant

[019-049] on an American [[steamer-travel|steamer]], and what confidence would they put in him,

[019-050] dressed as he was? What references could he give?

[019-051] As he was reflecting in this wise, his eyes fell upon an immense

[019-052] placard which a sort of clown was carrying through the streets. This

[019-053] placard, which was in English, read as follows:

[019-054] ACROBATIC JAPANESE TROUPE,

[019-055] HONOURABLE WILLIAM BATULCAR, PROPRIETOR,

[019-056] LAST REPRESENTATIONS,

[019-057] PRIOR TO THEIR DEPARTURE TO THE UNITED STATES,

[019-058] OF THE

[019-059] LONG NOSES! LONG NOSES!

[019-060] UNDER THE DIRECT PATRONAGE OF THE GOD TINGOU!

[019-061] GREAT ATTRACTION!

[019-062] “The United States!” said Passepartout; “that’s just what I want!”

[019-063] He followed the clown, and soon found himself once more in the Japanese

[019-064] quarter. A quarter of an hour later he stopped before a large cabin,

[019-065] adorned with several clusters of streamers, the exterior walls of which

[019-066] were designed to represent, in violent colours and without perspective,

[019-067] a company of jugglers.

[019-068] This was the Honourable William Batulcar’s establishment. That

[019-069] [[gentlemanly-conduct|gentleman]] was a sort of Barnum, the director of a troupe of

[019-070] mountebanks, jugglers, clowns, acrobats, equilibrists, and gymnasts,

[019-071] who, according to the placard, was giving his last performances before

[019-072] leaving the Empire of the Sun for the States of the Union.

[019-073] Passepartout entered and asked for Mr. Batulcar, who straightway

[019-074] appeared in person.

[019-075] “What do you want?” said he to Passepartout, whom he at first took for

[019-076] a native.

[019-077] “Would you like a servant, sir?” asked Passepartout.

[019-078] “A servant!” cried Mr. Batulcar, caressing the thick grey beard which

[019-079] hung from his chin. “I already have two who are obedient and faithful,

[019-080] have never left me, and serve me for their nourishment and here they

[019-081] are,” added he, holding out his two robust arms, furrowed with veins as

[019-082] large as the strings of a bass-viol.

[019-083] “So I can be of no use to you?”

[019-084] “None.”

[019-085] “The devil! I should so like to cross the Pacific with you!”

[019-086] “Ah!” said the Honourable Mr. Batulcar. “You are no more a Japanese

[019-087] than I am a monkey! Who are you dressed up in that way?”

[019-088] “A man dresses as he can.”

[019-089] “That’s true. You are a Frenchman, aren’t you?”

[019-090] “Yes; a Parisian of Paris.”

[019-091] “Then you ought to know how to make grimaces?”

[019-092] “Why,” replied Passepartout, a little vexed that his nationality should

[019-093] cause this question, “we Frenchmen know how to make grimaces, it is

[019-094] true but not any better than the Americans do.”

[019-095] “True. Well, if I can’t take you as a servant, I can as a clown. You

[019-096] see, my friend, in France they exhibit foreign clowns, and in foreign

[019-097] parts French clowns.”

[019-098] “Ah!”

[019-099] “You are pretty strong, eh?”

[019-100] “Especially after a good meal.”

[019-101] “And you can sing?”

[019-102] “Yes,” returned Passepartout, who had formerly been wont to sing in the

[019-103] streets.

[019-104] “But can you sing standing on your head, with a top spinning on your

[019-105] left foot, and a sabre balanced on your right?”

[019-106] “Humph! I think so,” replied Passepartout, recalling the exercises of

[019-107] his younger days.

[019-108] “Well, that’s enough,” said the Honourable William Batulcar.

[019-109] The engagement was concluded there and then.

[019-110] Passepartout had at last found something to do. He was engaged to act

[019-111] in the celebrated Japanese troupe. It was not a very dignified

[019-112] position, but within a week he would be on his way to San Francisco.

[019-113] The performance, so noisily announced by the Honourable Mr. Batulcar,

[019-114] was to commence at three o’clock, and soon the deafening instruments of

[019-115] a Japanese orchestra resounded at the door. Passepartout, though he had

[019-116] not been able to study or rehearse a part, was designated to lend the

[019-117] aid of his sturdy shoulders in the great exhibition of the “human

[019-118] pyramid,” executed by the Long Noses of the god Tingou. This “great

[019-119] attraction” was to close the performance.

[019-120] Before three o’clock the large shed was invaded by the spectators,

[019-121] comprising Europeans and natives, Chinese and Japanese, men, women and

[019-122] children, who precipitated themselves upon the narrow benches and into

[019-123] the boxes opposite the stage. The musicians took up a position inside,

[019-124] and were vigorously performing on their gongs, tam-tams, flutes, bones,

[019-125] tambourines, and immense drums.

[019-126] The performance was much like all acrobatic displays; but it must be

[019-127] confessed that the Japanese are the first equilibrists in the world.

[019-128] One, with a fan and some bits of paper, performed the graceful trick of

[019-129] the butterflies and the flowers; another traced in the air, with the

[019-130] odorous smoke of his pipe, a series of blue words, which composed a

[019-131] compliment to the audience; while a third juggled with some lighted

[019-132] candles, which he extinguished successively as they passed his lips,

[019-133] and relit again without interrupting for an instant his juggling.

[019-134] Another reproduced the most singular combinations with a spinning-top;

[019-135] in his hands the revolving tops seemed to be animated with a life of

[019-136] their own in their interminable whirling; they ran over pipe-stems, the

[019-137] edges of sabres, wires and even hairs stretched across the stage; they

[019-138] turned around on the edges of large glasses, crossed bamboo ladders,

[019-139] dispersed into all the corners, and produced strange musical effects by

[019-140] the combination of their various pitches of tone. The jugglers tossed

[019-141] them in the air, threw them like shuttlecocks with wooden battledores,

[019-142] and yet they kept on spinning; they put them into their pockets, and

[019-143] took them out still whirling as before.

[019-144] It is useless to describe the astonishing performances of the acrobats

[019-145] and gymnasts. The turning on ladders, poles, balls, barrels, &c., was

[019-146] executed with wonderful precision.

[019-147] But the principal attraction was the exhibition of the Long Noses, a

[019-148] show to which Europe is as yet a stranger.

[019-149] The Long Noses form a peculiar company, under the direct patronage of

[019-150] the god Tingou. Attired after the fashion of the Middle Ages, they bore

[019-151] upon their shoulders a splendid pair of wings; but what especially

[019-152] distinguished them was the long noses which were fastened to their

[019-153] faces, and the uses which they made of them. These noses were made of

[019-154] bamboo, and were five, six, and even ten feet long, some straight,

[019-155] others curved, some ribboned, and some having imitation warts upon

[019-156] them. It was upon these appendages, fixed tightly on their real noses,

[019-157] that they performed their gymnastic exercises. A dozen of these

[019-158] sectaries of Tingou lay flat upon their backs, while others, dressed to

[019-159] represent lightning-rods, came and frolicked on their noses, jumping

[019-160] from one to another, and performing the most skilful leapings and

[019-161] somersaults.

[019-162] As a last scene, a “human pyramid” had been announced, in which fifty

[019-163] Long Noses were to represent the Car of Juggernaut. But, instead of

[019-164] forming a pyramid by mounting each other’s shoulders, the artists were

[019-165] to group themselves on top of the noses. It happened that the performer

[019-166] who had hitherto formed the base of the Car had quitted the troupe, and

[019-167] as, to fill this part, only strength and adroitness were necessary,

[019-168] Passepartout had been chosen to take his place.

[019-169] The poor fellow really felt sad when—melancholy reminiscence of his

[019-170] youth!—he donned his costume, adorned with vari-coloured wings, and

[019-171] fastened to his natural feature a false nose six feet long. But he

[019-172] cheered up when he thought that this nose was winning him something to

[019-173] eat.

[019-174] He went upon the stage, and took his place beside the rest who were to

[019-175] compose the base of the Car of Juggernaut. They all stretched

[019-176] themselves on the floor, their noses pointing to the ceiling. A second

[019-177] group of artists disposed themselves on these long appendages, then a

[019-178] third above these, then a fourth, until a human monument reaching to

[019-179] the very cornices of the theatre soon arose on top of the noses. This

[019-180] elicited loud applause, in the midst of which the orchestra was just

[019-181] striking up a deafening air, when the pyramid tottered, the balance was

[019-182] lost, one of the lower noses vanished from the pyramid, and the human

[019-183] monument was shattered like a castle built of cards!

[019-184] It was Passepartout’s fault. Abandoning his position, clearing the

[019-185] footlights without the aid of his wings, and, clambering up to the

[019-186] right-hand gallery, he fell at the feet of one of the spectators,

[019-187] crying, “Ah, my master! my master!”

[019-188] “You here?”

[019-189] “Myself.”

[019-190] “Very well; then let us go to the steamer, young man!”

[019-191] [[phileas-fogg|Mr. Fogg]], [[aouda|Aouda]], and Passepartout passed through the lobby of the

[019-192] theatre to the outside, where they encountered the Honourable Mr.

[019-193] Batulcar, furious with rage. He demanded damages for the “breakage” of

[019-194] the pyramid; and Phileas Fogg appeased him by giving him a handful of

[019-195] banknotes.

[019-196] At half-past six, the very hour of departure, Mr. Fogg and Aouda,

[019-197] followed by Passepartout, who in his hurry had retained his wings, and

[019-198] nose six feet long, stepped upon the American steamer.
