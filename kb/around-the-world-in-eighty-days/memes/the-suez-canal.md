# The Suez Canal

Opened in 1869, just three years before Fogg's journey, the Suez Canal represents the final piece of infrastructure that makes an eighty-day circumnavigation possible -- transforming a route that once required sailing around Africa into a direct passage from the Mediterranean to the Indian Ocean.

## Definition

The Suez Canal is a 120-mile artificial waterway connecting the Mediterranean Sea at Port Said to the Red Sea at Suez, cutting through the Isthmus of Suez in Egypt. Before its construction, ships traveling from Europe to India had to navigate around the Cape of Good Hope -- a detour of approximately 4,300 miles and several weeks' sailing time.

In the novel, the canal is the first major waypoint after Fogg's departure from London. He passes through it aboard the *Mongolia*, the P&O steamer that carries him from Brindisi to Bombay. The passage is described with an engineer's appreciation for the audacity of the project and a tourist's fascination with the exotic landscape through which the canal cuts.

## Role in the Novel

The Suez Canal serves as both a geographical and symbolic threshold. It marks Fogg's entry into the non-European world -- the point at which the journey ceases to be a European railway trip and becomes a global adventure. The *Mongolia* plies regularly between Brindisi and Bombay via the Suez Canal (004-106), a route only possible since the canal's opening three years earlier.

It is also where Detective Fix first encounters Fogg. Waiting at Suez -- the canal's southern terminus -- Fix has been dispatched as one of the picked detectives sent to ports around the world following the Bank of England robbery (002-171/002-172). His reasoning is strategic: a fugitive heading for the Dutch or French colonies in Asia would naturally pass through the canal, as the Atlantic route was more heavily watched (004-193/004-195).

The canal is woven into the itinerary's very language. When Fogg has his passport visaed at the British consulate in Suez, he explains his purpose: "I wish to prove, by your visa, that I came by Suez" (005-065/005-066). The canal is not just a waterway; it is a proof of passage, a line on the itinerary that confirms forward progress.

Fogg's travel log, maintained with pathological precision, records the passage (005-097), and the canal reappears in retrospective discussion of the completed route (031-194 through 031-195). The canal is one element in the chain of infrastructure -- railways, steamers, telegraphs, and canals -- that compresses the globe and makes the eighty-day schedule possible.

The canal represents the novel's central paradox: the journey that seems impossibly audacious is, in fact, made possible by rational engineering. Verne takes pleasure in showing how canals, railways, and steamers -- the infrastructure of empire and commerce -- enable the romantic adventure of circumnavigation.

## Historical and Cultural Context

The Suez Canal was one of the great engineering projects of the nineteenth century, designed by Ferdinand de Lesseps and built between 1859 and 1869. It was inaugurated with enormous fanfare -- Verdi's opera *Aida* was commissioned for the occasion, though it premiered two years late. The construction relied heavily on forced Egyptian labor under the corvee system, making it a project of colonial extraction as well as engineering triumph.

The canal transformed global trade and geopolitics. It made India vastly more accessible to Britain, intensifying the imperial connection, and it shifted the strategic balance of the Mediterranean. For Verne, writing in 1872, the canal was astonishingly recent -- it had been open barely three years. His inclusion of it reflects his characteristic attention to contemporary technological developments; his novels function as a kind of journalism about the built environment of the modern world.

Verne's detailed attention to speeds and distances is on display in the canal passages. The *Mongolia* maintains ten knots an hour between Brindisi and Suez and nine and a half between Suez and Bombay (004-108) -- a level of precision that would have resonated with readers accustomed to shipping gazettes and railway timetables. The canal, in Verne's rendering, is not a wonder to be marveled at but a piece of infrastructure to be scheduled -- a characteristically modern attitude toward what was still, in 1872, a very new addition to the global landscape.
