## Definition

Odin, called the All-Father (Old Norse Alföðr), the God of Hosts, and the Whisperer in the Wind, is the chief deity of the Norse pantheon as depicted throughout the novel. He presides over [[valhalla|Valhall]]a, where the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] — warriors chosen from the battle-slain by his shield-maidens the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] — [[hall-feast|feast]] and fight until Ragnarok. In the novel's theodicy, Odin is the sovereign arbiter of fate in battle: kings swear [[oath|oaths]] at his altar, death in combat is understood as his personal choosing, and the souls of heroes are delivered before his high seat by the [[valkyries|Valkyries]].

The novel presents Odin not as an abstract mythological reference but as an active, immanent divine presence. [[king-eric|King Eric]] swears a binding oath at Odin's altar in the [[upsala|Upsala]] temple on the eve of battle (015-041), and the entire final chapter is set within Odin's celestial hall, where he personally receives Styrbiorn's soul and declares his favour. That declaration — "I chose him first I loved the best" (016-008) — forms the novel's culminating theological statement.


> The King laid hand upon the ring and prayed. Nigh an hour he stood there a-praying, and he sware oath unto the All-Father that if He should vouchsafe to him victory in this battle, then would he after ten years give himself unto Odin. And the King prayed, and made new his oath, and looked for a sign.
>
> (015-041)

## Occurrences in the Novel

Odin's presence in the novel intensifies as the narrative moves toward its climax. The first reference comes in the Spae-Wife's Lay, recited by Biorn during the Yule [[hall-feast|feast]] in Denmark, where the line "And Odin muttereth with Mimir's head" (005-099) [[all-places|places]] Odin at the heart of the eschatological prophecy that darkens the [[hall-feast|feast]] and unsettles [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]].

During the [[hall-feast|banquet]] in [[upsala|Upsala]], a skald recites the Hell-ride of Brynhild, a poem from the Volsung cycle. In the song, Brynhild's fate is attributed directly to Odin's will: "Over wroth waxed Odin with me for that" (009-057). As Styrbiorn listens, he reflects that "it was Odin set that fire about her, and that weird upon her" (009-087), and in his mind's eye he perceives [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]] as a Valkyrie — "Hild the Helm'd, the Valkyrie, Odin's shield-may" (009-088). This passage establishes a motif that culminates in the final chapter: the earthly queen mirrors the divine shield-maidens who serve Odin.

The most dramatic invocation of Odin in the mortal realm occurs on [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]]'s eve. [[king-eric|King Eric]], outmatched by Styrbiorn's forces, rises at midnight and goes "to the inner shrine where was the altar and holy place of Odin, and the ring and the twigs and the blood-bowls" (015-041). There he swears a terrible oath: if Odin grants him victory, Eric will "after ten years give himself unto Odin" — a bargain whose terms are unmistakably those of human sacrifice. The king waits for a sign but receives none, only "the blackness and the silence and the smell of blood" (015-041).

The final chapter, "[[valhalla|Valhall]]a" (016), opens with Odin gazing upon the battlefield from his high seat (016-001). After the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] and [[valkyries|Valkyries]] assemble, the Valkyrie Skogul presents Styrbiorn before the All-Father, questioning the wisdom of taking so young and promising a lord from the living world. Odin's reply — "Frontward are his wounds, and death availed but to tighten his grip on the sword-hilt. Be still and question not: I chose him first I loved the best" (016-008) — closes the novel's arc and reaffirms Odin's absolute sovereignty over life and death in battle.

## Historical/Mythological Context

In Old Norse religion, Odin (Old Norse Óðinn) is the chief of the Æsir, associated with wisdom, war, death, poetry, and the runes. He sacrificed an eye at Mimir's well for wisdom, hung himself on Yggdrasill for nine nights to learn the runes, and presides over [[valhalla|Valhall]]a. His attributes include the spear Gungnir, the eight-legged horse Sleipnir, and his two ravens Huginn and Muninn (Thought and Memory). He is called by many names — the Prose Edda lists over 170 — and the novel draws on several of these: All-Father (Alföðr), Father of Hosts (Herfaðr), and God of Hosts (Herjans).

The myth of Odin's election of the slain, central to the novel's final chapter, is grounded in the Eddic understanding that death in combat is not random but a divine selection. Grímnismál 8-10 describes [[valhalla|Valhall]]a and Odin's choice of the [[einheriar|Einheriar]]. The human sacrifice motif in [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s oath reflects the historical practice of blót, where kings and chieftains offered sacrifices — including, in extremis, themselves — to Odin for victory. Adam of Bremen's account of the temple at [[upsala|Uppsala]] (11th century) describes a temple with idols of Odin, Thor, and Freyr, with Odin as the central figure — a setting closely akin to the "altar and holy place of Odin" that [[king-eric|King Eric]] visits in the novel (015-041).
