## Definition

*Scat* (Old Norse *skattr*, cognate with Old English *sceatt* and Gothic *skatts*) was the tribute or tax paid by a subordinate ruler — typically an earl or petty king — to his overlord, acknowledging the overlord's sovereignty and the tributary's vassalage. Unlike a casual gift or one-time ransom, scat was an ongoing obligation: a recurring payment that bound the tributary in a political-economic relationship of subordination. In the Norse world of the novel, the payment of scat is the chief visible sign of political submission; conversely, the refusal to pay scat is tantamount to a declaration of independence. The concept bridges the economic, legal, and political domains, embodying the hierarchical structure of Norse kingship where authority rested not on territorial borders but on networks of personal obligation cemented by tribute.


> But as soon as Earl Hakon was gotten with his ships out of the land, men say that he went ashore on an isle that is there and made blood-offering unto the Gods and so went again ashipboard and so home to Hladir. And ruleth Norway from that day forth as he were very King, and never no more payeth no scat, and counteth not a flea's worth neither christen-faith nor the King of the Danes, whose Earl he was aforetime.

## Occurrences in the Novel

Scat appears most prominently in Biorn's account of [[earl-hakon|Earl Hakon of Norway]], whose political defiance is defined by his cessation of tribute to [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]. After being forcibly baptised by Keisar Otto and [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] Bluetooth, Earl Hakon renounces both Christianity and his former allegiance. As Biorn tells Styrbiorn, Earl Hakon "ruleth Norway from that day forth as he were very King, and never no more payeth no scat, and counteth not a flea's worth neither christen-faith nor the [[harald-bluetooth|King of the Danes]], whose Earl he was aforetime" (005-009). The triple negative ("never no more payeth no scat") underscores the finality of Hakon's breach: the refusal of scat is the refusal of subordination itself. Styrbiorn's laconic response — "That was well done" (005-010) — signals his approval of this act of political self-assertion, foreshadowing his own ambition to rule without paying tribute to any overlord.

The reverse side of the scat relationship — receiving tribute — appears in the description of Styrbiorn's treasure at the betrothal [[hall-feast|banquet]] in [[upsala|Upsala]]. The hall hangings and golden vessels on display are "plunder of war or gifts of peace and tribute made to Styrbiorn by kings and lords that he had brought under him" (009-022). This passage reveals that Styrbiorn's own empire, like that of [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]], is built upon the extraction of tribute from conquered and subordinate rulers. The accumulation of scat becomes a visible measure of his power: the magnificence of the [[hall-feast|banquet]], which astonishes the Swedish court with treasures "not one tenth the number nor goodliness" ever before seen in the realm, flows directly from the tribute system that Styrbiorn has imposed on the Wendish and Baltic lands.

The mechanism of tribute is further exemplified in Styrbiorn's dealings with [[burisleif|King Burisleif]] of the Wends. After defeating the Wendish host in battle, Styrbiorn imposes peace terms that include formal acknowledgement of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]'s independence; and "[[burisleif|King Burisleif]] gave Styrbiorn great store of gifts and treasures, and bound himself to fetch Styrbiorn ships and men to go a-warring with him whensoever he should call for them" (012-026). Although the term "scat" is not used here, the transaction functions identically: a defeated ruler delivers treasure and pledges military service to his conqueror, establishing the same overlord-tributary relationship that defines the scat system.

## Historical Context

The Old Norse *skattr* was a fundamental institution of Viking Age kingship. Unlike modern taxation, which applies universally to a defined territory, scat was a personal obligation between lords — a tribute paid by one ruler to another in recognition of superior rank and protection. The system is well-documented in [[skaldic-lore|skaldic poetry]] and the Icelandic sagas, particularly *Heimskringla*, where the payment and withholding of *skattr* marks the political fortunes of Norwegian kings. The English cognate *sceatt* also denoted both a coin and a tribute payment in Anglo-Saxon England, pointing to the deep Germanic roots of the concept. In the novel, scat appears in exactly this traditional sense: as a political-economic bond between unequals, where payment signifies subordination and non-payment signifies rebellion.
