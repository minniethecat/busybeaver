# The Thing at Upsala

## Summary

The Thing at Upsala was the lawful assembly that [[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious summoned in the middle term of the great [[hall-feast|feast]] celebrating Styrbiorn's return and betrothal. Eric's declared purpose was "to make over and give unto Styrbiorn, his brother's son, with all lawful ceremonies and before the face of the Swedes in lawful [[thing-stead|Thing]] assembled, that half share of the kingly power in Sweden which [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]] had held aforetime" (009-001). This was to be the constitutional moment that resolved the saga's central conflict: Styrbiorn, denied his inheritance since childhood, would at last receive his due before the assembled freemen of the realm.

The [[thing-stead|Thing]] was proclaimed simultaneously with the [[hall-feast|feast]] and the betrothal — the triple event designed to put all of Sweden's political business in order at a stroke. The betrothal bound Styrbiorn to Denmark; the [[thing-stead|Thing]] was to bind Styrbiorn to Sweden. The wedding itself was to follow the [[thing-stead|Thing]], once Styrbiorn had formally taken up kingdom (009-002).

The Thing at Upsala, however, is never depicted on the page. The [[all-events|events]] that unfold in the aftermath of the betrothal [[hall-feast|banquet]] — Sigrid's nocturnal visit to Styrbiorn's chamber (Chapter 10), the Queen's confession of her feelings and Styrbiorn's reckless response — overtake Eric's careful plan. The constitutional moment that was meant to seal the saga's peace instead vanishes into the story's gathering darkness.


> Whereupon, great uproar among the bonders, who in fine carried it with so high a hand that they feared not to choose and proclaim one Lambi the White, a man from Stocksound in Tenthland, King in Styrbiorn's despite of that half of the kingdom claimed by him; and therewith the Thing broken up in a tumult, so that it had wanted but a little to have befallen a battle betwixt the King's men and the bonders.
>
> (002-004)

## Context

The Thing at Upsala was Eric's answer to the unlawful [[thing-stead|Thing]] of Chapter 2, where bonders hostile to Styrbiorn had proclaimed [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]] as a rival king in the King's absence (002-004). That earlier assembly had been "summoned against all law, the King being not there" (002-004) and had broken up in tumult. By contrast, Eric's [[thing-stead|Thing]] was to be everything the earlier assembly was not: lawful, properly summoned, presided over by the King and the aged lawman Thorgnyr, and designed to accomplish the orderly transfer of power.

The [[thing-stead|Thing]] was also Eric's attempt to settle the Danish question on terms favourable to Sweden. By granting Styrbiorn his half of the kingdom publicly and lawfully, Eric would have bound his nephew to the Swedish realm with ties of gratitude and obligation that would have made Danish influence harmless. Styrbiorn, Duke of the half-kingdom, would have had no need of Danish allies — and his betrothal to [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] would have been a dynastic ornament rather than a political threat.

The [[thing-stead|Thing]]'s failure to materialise is one of the novel's great ironies. Eric, the shrewd old king, had planned everything perfectly — except for the human heart. The Queen's passion and Styrbiorn's susceptibility to it undid what centuries of Swedish law and custom had been designed to secure.

## Key Participants

**[[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious** — The convenor of the [[thing-stead|Thing]], who intended to use it to grant Styrbiorn the half-kingdom and thereby neutralise the threat his nephew posed (009-001).

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — The intended beneficiary of the [[thing-stead|Thing]], who was to receive his inheritance at last before the assembled Swedes (009-001).

**[[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]]** — The aged law-speaker of Sweden, whose presence at the [[thing-stead|Thing]] would have given it the force of immemorial tradition. Thorgnyr had served the Swedish kings for forty years (010-023). Though his role at this particular [[thing-stead|Thing]] is never realised on the page, his earlier confrontation with [[king-eric|King Eric]] in Chapter 2 had made clear that even a king could not act against the law if the lawman opposed him (002-025, 002-044).

**Thyri** — Styrbiorn's betrothed, whose marriage was to follow the [[thing-stead|Thing]] "when Styrbiorn should have taken kingdom" (009-002). She was to be the new king's queen.

## Consequences

The [[thing-stead|Thing]]'s failure to occur is the turning point of the novel's political plot. Eric had staked everything on this assembly as the mechanism for resolving the succession crisis. When the betrothal [[hall-feast|feast]] collapsed into the "broken meats" that followed, the [[thing-stead|Thing]] — and with it, the peaceful transfer of power — became impossible. Styrbiorn would soon flee Sweden, Eric would crown his infant son Olaf as joint king to secure his own line, and the path to [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] was set.

The novel thus presents a world in which political institutions — the [[thing-stead|Thing]], the law, the covenant of king and bonders — are real and powerful, but ultimately fragile. They cannot contain the human passions that drive the [[all-persons|characters]]: the love and hate between Styrbiorn and Sigrid, the old king's desperate need for an heir, the bonders' distrust of the young prince. The [[thing-stead|Thing]] was meant to be the novel's resolution; instead, it became its ghost.

## Historical Basis

The [[thing-stead|Thing]] (Old Norse "thing") was the central political institution of Norse and Germanic societies. The [[upsala|Uppsala]] [[thing-stead|Thing]] was one of the most important in Sweden, where kings were acclaimed, laws were spoken, and disputes were settled. The requirement that a king receive the consent of the [[thing-stead|Thing]] before taking power was a real constitutional check on royal authority. In the novel, Eric's insistence on giving Styrbiorn his kingdom "with all lawful ceremonies and before the face of the Swedes in lawful [[thing-stead|Thing]] assembled" (009-001) reflects the genuine Norse political culture in which a grant of power without the [[thing-stead|Thing]]'s approval would have been legally worthless. The novel's tragedy is that all the law in Sweden could not prevent the catastrophe that followed.
