# Jomsvikings

## Description

The Jomsvikings (also called Jomsburgers) were the warrior brotherhood that inhabited the fortress of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] on the [[wendland|Wendland]] coast. They were "held for the greatest champions and men of war, and scarcely was any man thought their match in that time" (004-005). Every member, from the highest lord to the least among the fellowship, was bound by a sworn oath to uphold the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] Law — a comprehensive code governing admission, conduct, plunder-sharing, and the absolute prohibition of fear (004-003).

The brotherhood was not a tribe or a nation but a voluntary association of warriors drawn from many lands. Its lords included men of Denmark, Sweden, Norway, and the Baltic coasts. What united them was not blood or land but the covenant they had sworn to [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]], and through [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] to one another. They fared out each summer a-viking, bringing home treasure that was divided equally "to the pole," and wintered in their own [[all-places|places]] across the North — all except one captain chosen by lot to hold [[jomsburg|the burg]] through winter (005-003).

The Jomsviking ideal was the warrior who needed nothing but his brothers, his ship, and his sword. Women were forbidden in [[jomsburg|the burg]]. No man might speak a craven word, "howsoever hopeless a turn their affair might take" (004-004). The oath included the obligation of mutual vengeance: every man was sworn to avenge another's slaying as though the slain man were his own brother. This web of sworn obligations created a fighting force whose discipline and ferocity were legendary.

## Membership

**[[palnatoki|Palnatoki of Fion]]** — The founder and captain of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], who built the fortress, established the law, and ruled the brotherhood with absolute authority. His word settled all disputes and his judgement was final in every matter within [[jomsburg|the burg]] (004-001, 004-004).

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — Admitted to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] after defeating [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] in single combat — the only man besides [[vagn-akison|Vagn Akison]] ever admitted under the age requirement (004-061). Rose to be called captain in [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s absence (005-001). His bond with the brotherhood would carry them all to [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]].

**[[bui-digri|Bui the Stout]]** — Lord from [[borgundholm|Borgundholm]], a fierce and hot-headed warrior who taunted Styrbiorn at his arrival but became one of his most bloodthirsty allies. Led the right wing at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] (004-016, 015-011).

**[[sigvaldi|Sigvaldi Jarl]]** — Son of [[strut-harald|Earl Strut-Harald]] of [[skaney|Skane]]y, "crooked of counsel" and a lover of deceit. Opposed Styrbiorn's admission and later abandoned the fleet under cover of darkness on the eve of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] (004-033, 015-011).

**Biorn Broadwicker** — An Icelander of the Broadwickers' line, Styrbiorn's sworn foster-brother. Famous as a skald and a champion "in wooing of women as in holding of his own against men" (005-002).

**[[heming|Heming]] and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell the High]]** — Sons of Strut-Harald, brothers of Sigvaldi. Commanded the left wing at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] along with the Danish contingent after Sigvaldi's desertion (015-011).

**[[sigurd-cape|Sigurd Cape]]** — Bui's brother, another of the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] lords, who fought alongside Bui on the right wing at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] (004-002, 015-011).

**[[biorn-the-brisk|Biorn the Brisk]]** — One of the lords of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] present when Styrbiorn arrived (004-002).

## Role in the Story

The Jomsvikings are the engine of the saga's second half. Styrbiorn's admission to the brotherhood transforms him from an exiled prince into the captain of the most feared fighting force in the North. The Jomsburgers provide him with ships, warriors, and the legitimacy of their fearsome reputation. Without them, Styrbiorn would have remained a youth with a grievance; with them, he became a threat to kingdoms.

The brotherhood's internal dynamics drive much of the novel's tension. Sigvaldi's duplicity, Bui's bloodlust, [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s measured authority, and Biorn's steadfast loyalty create a microcosm of the larger political forces at play. The great council on the seawall in Chapter 12, where [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] pledges unconditional support while Sigvaldi mutters objections, is one of the novel's key political scenes (012-002).

The Jomsvikings' final act in the saga is their march to [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]]. They followed Styrbiorn to Sweden and fought with him for three days against overwhelming odds. The battle broke them: Styrbiorn fell, and the greatest warrior brotherhood of the age was shattered on the fields by [[fyriswater|Fyriswater]]. Yet they had kept their oath to the end — never a craven word, even in the face of extinction.

## Rules/Customs

The [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] Law is detailed in full in Chapter 4. Its key provisions were: no man under eighteen or over fifty; no man who would flee from an equal foe; mutual vengeance for all slain brothers; no slander; equal plunder-sharing; no women in [[jomsburg|the burg]]; no absence over three nights without [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s leave; all tidings to be told by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] alone; and absolute prohibition of "any craven word, howsoever hopeless a turn their affair might take" (004-004). The captain's authority was absolute — [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] "alone must there rule all things according to his will" (004-004).

## Historical Basis

The Jomsvikings appear in the thirteenth-century *Jomsvikinga saga*, which describes them as an elite warrior brotherhood based at the fortress of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] on the Baltic coast. They feature prominently in several Norse sagas, including *Heimskringla* and *Olafs saga Tryggvasonar*, where they participate in major [[all-events|battles]] of the late tenth century. Historians debate the brotherhood's historicity: some see them as a literary invention modelled on real warrior guilds; others argue for a historical core, with the fortress possibly located at Wolin in modern Poland. The novel follows saga tradition in presenting them as the supreme warriors of the age, bound by a code that made them greater than any single king's household force.
