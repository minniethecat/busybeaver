## Definition

Foster-brotherhood (Old Norse fóstbrœðralag) is a formalised bond of mutual loyalty between two men, ritually established and carrying obligations comparable to those of blood kinship. Unlike ordinary friendship, foster-brotherhood is a sworn covenant: each party pledges to avenge the other's death, to share fortunes, and to stand by the other in all conflicts. In the novel, the relationship between Styrbiorn and Biorn Asbrandson is explicitly defined as foster-brotherhood, and it forms the most enduring and consequential personal bond in the narrative.

The term "foster-brother" appears at key moments to reaffirm the depth of the bond. When [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] asks Biorn whether he is Styrbiorn's friend, Biorn corrects her: "Not friend only, but very foster-brother" (006-010). The qualifier "very" underscores that foster-brotherhood is a different order of relationship from mere friendship — it is a formal, sworn, and binding tie.

## Occurrences in the Novel

The foster-brother bond between Styrbiorn and Biorn is first named in Chapter VI, when Thyri [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]'s daughter interrogates Biorn about his relationship to Styrbiorn. Biorn's reply — "Not friend only, but very foster-brother" (006-010) — establishes the special nature of the bond early, before the reader has seen it tested in battle.

The bond is repeatedly invoked in moments of intimacy and crisis. During the tense waiting before the fleet's sailing, Biorn asks Styrbiorn, "Is that true, foster-brother, thinkest thou?" (011-014), the address itself a reassurance of their shared understanding. In [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], the narrator identifies Biorn as "Biorn his foster-brother" (012-004) when Biorn raises a difficult subject that only he could safely broach. When Styrbiorn must send word to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] to summon the fleet, he trusts no one else: "Styrbiorn sent Biorn Asbrandson his foster-brother to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] to let [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] know" (013-002). The mission is one of supreme trust — Biorn alone can speak for Styrbiorn in his absence.

On the eve of battle at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], the bond receives its final and most poignant expression. Styrbiorn, who has been largely silent about the coming fight, turns to Biorn: "Styrbiorn now said to Biorn his foster-brother, 'I was never a talker, and now, beyond most times, my lips are sealed'" (015-012). The address — his foster-brother — signals that even in this moment of foreboding, the bond holds. The intimacy of that silence, shared between sworn brothers who need no words, is the novel's final portrait of the relationship before the battle consumes them both.

## Historical/Mythological Context

Foster-brotherhood (fóstbrœðralag) was a recognised institution in Old Norse society, attested in multiple saga sources including Gísla saga, Fóstbrœðra saga, and the legendary Hrólfs saga kraka. The ritual typically involved mingling blood — sometimes by letting it flow into the earth beneath a strip of turf raised on spears — and swearing mutual obligations. The ceremony created a kinship bond that was understood to be as binding as biological brotherhood.

In the novel, the foster-brother bond between Styrbiorn and Biorn echoes the relationship between historical figures such as the sworn brothers of Jómsvíkinga saga. The novel's treatment differs from some saga conventions in that the bond is never betrayed: Biorn remains loyal to the end, and Styrbiorn's trust in him is absolute. This idealised portrayal makes the foster-brotherhood the moral anchor of the narrative — the one relationship that war, ambition, and death cannot sever.

Biorn Asbrandson corrects Thyri on the nature of his bond with Styrbiorn:

> "Not friend only, but very foster-brother," answered he.
