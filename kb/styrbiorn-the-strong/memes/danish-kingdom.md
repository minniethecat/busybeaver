# Danish Kingdom

## Description

The Danish Kingdom (the Dane-realm) under [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]], called Bluetooth, was a realm in the grip of transformation. Harald had been forced to accept Christian baptism by Emperor Otto of Germany, and his kingdom now bore the marks of that conversion: a priest in a long-coloured gown who hallowed the board before every meal, a kirk near the King's house where mass was said, and a King who attended Christian services while his pagan past haunted the edges of his hall (005-009).

The kingdom's seat was at [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]] in Sealand, where Harald's great hall stood "up not far from the seaside on a little eminence overlooking the gray land and the gray sea" (005-007). It was a place of wealth and power, where the King could summon his mighty men for great [[hall-feast|feast]]s and where gifts of gold rings and Earl's titles could be offered to those he wished to bind to his service (005-011, 005-046).

> He was burly of build and quack-bellied and thick and short of neck and with great chuff cheeks, and his mouth was ugly, and a monstrous dead tooth lay out of it over his lip of one side, that had been there these many years; and it was thence came that by-name by which men called him through all the Northlands, of Harald Blue-tooth.
>
> (005-008)

But the Dane-realm was also a kingdom at odds with itself. The forced conversion had created a fault line between the King's outward Christian observance and the old ways that still held sway in the hearts of many [[danes|Danes]] — and, more threateningly, in the hearts of visitors from the still-pagan North. When Biorn recited the Spae-Wife's Lay in Harald's hall, the Christian facade cracked: the priest spouted blasphemy, and the King "with his great flat face corpse-livid even in the friendly firelight, gazed on Styrbiorn as if he beheld in him the very presence of Surt with flaming Sword" (005-122). The kingdom was Christian on the surface, pagan underneath — and the tension between the two would shape its dealings with Styrbiorn and his [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]].

Harald's realm also extended over [[earl-hakon|Earl Hakon of Norway]], at least in theory. Biorn explained that Hakon had paid [[scat-tribute|scat]] to Harald before the forced conversion, but after accepting baptism under duress on his way home from the Emperor's war, the Earl had "gone ashore on an isle and made blood-offering unto the Gods," reverting to paganism and ruling Norway "as he were very King" thereafter (005-009). The Dane-realm's authority over its dependencies was thus as fragile as its Christian faith.

## Membership

**[[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] (Bluetooth)** — The [[harald-bluetooth|King of the Danes]], a burly man "quack-bellied and thick and short of neck and with great chuff cheeks," whose mouth was marred by a dead tooth that gave him his famous by-name (005-008). He had accepted Christianity under force but ruled a kingdom still pagan at heart.

**[[queen-thyri|Thyri]]** — Harald's daughter, fourteen winters old at the time of Styrbiorn's first visit, "of few words but with a merry eye" and hair "black and of a dainty curling growth" (005-012). Her betrothal to Styrbiorn bound the Danish and Swedish houses.

**Svein** — Harald's only son, nine winters old during Styrbiorn's visit, who was being fostered with [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] (005-012). He would grow to become Svein Forkbeard, the future king of Denmark and conqueror of England.

**The Christian Priest** — A figure of comic absurdity in the novel, who wore a long-coloured gown "like as women use to wear" with the top of his head "shaven bald and smooth" (005-009). His reaction to Biorn's pagan recital — spewing "all manner of foul speech and blasphemy" — reduced him to a figure of fun (005-122).

## Role in the Story

The Danish Kingdom functions in the novel as the counterweight to Sweden — the other great power of the North, whose alliance could tip the balance between Styrbiorn and Eric. Harald's initial welcome of Styrbiorn and Biorn at the Yule [[hall-feast|feast]] (Chapter 5) promised a Danish-[[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] alliance that would make Styrbiorn unbeatable. But the relationship soured: Harald's attempts to convert Styrbiorn and make him an Earl were refused, and Styrbiorn's mockery of the King's Christian faith created a breach that would never fully heal (005-011, 005-122).

When Styrbiorn later returned to Denmark seeking aid against Eric, Harald was coerced into joining the expedition — brought "willy-nilly" into the campaign, guarded by a shield-burg of Styrbiorn's men to ensure he did not flee (015-011). The Danish Kingdom thus moved from potential ally to reluctant vassal, and the Danish contingent at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] fought under Styrbiorn's banners rather than their own king's.

Harald's kingdom also represents the novel's commentary on the Christianisation of the North. The forced conversion is portrayed as a source of weakness, not strength: the King who had been made Christian against his will could not command the loyalty of men who still honoured the old gods. The Spae-Wife's Lay in Harald's hall is the novel's most powerful statement of the pagan world's enduring hold on the Northern imagination.

## Historical Basis

[[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] Bluetooth (Haraldr Gormsson, c. 958-986 CE) was the historical king who unified Denmark and converted the [[danes|Danes]] to Christianity around 965 CE. His reign is documented on the Jelling Stone, which bears the inscription: "Harald king of the [[danes|Danes]], who won all Denmark and Norway and made the [[danes|Danes]] Christian." His forced conversion by Otto II (not Otto I, as the novel has it) is historically plausible, though the details vary in different sources. His son Svein Forkbeard would indeed succeed him and go on to conquer England. The novel's portrait of Harald as a reluctant Christian ruling a kingdom divided between old and new faith is consistent with the historical picture of the conversion period.
