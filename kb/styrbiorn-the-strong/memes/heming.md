## Biography

Heming was the second son of Strut-Harald, a famous Earl in [[skaney|Skane]]y, and was accounted among the lords of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] as a "doughty man of his hands and a great champion" (004-002). Along with his brothers Sigvaldi and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell the High]], Heming was part of the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fellowship from the time of [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn Olafson]]'s first arrival at [[jomsburg|the burg]].

One winter, by the custom of the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] lords, the lot fell upon Heming to remain as captain of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] through the cold months. The lords of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] were wont, after the summer's harryings were done and the booty safely bestowed, to disperse each to his own place, leaving only one of their chiefest men to hold [[jomsburg|the burg]] through winter. "Nor was any eager to have it," the saga notes, but they took the duty turn and turn about (005-003). Heming's winter captaincy, during the Yule season when Styrbiorn and his men were at [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s court in Denmark, marks him as a reliable and trusted member of the brotherhood (005-003).

Like his brother [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]], Heming spent the winter at their father's hall in [[skaney|Skane]]y when Styrbiorn arrived there after fleeing Sweden (008-003). When spring came, he sailed with Styrbiorn from [[skaney|Skane]]y to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] (008-008). His father's judgment of him, delivered to Styrbiorn at their parting, was mixed: "Heming can steer a ship and can make good play with sword and spear, but methinks he will ever have that nature to follow still where another leadeth" (008-004).

At the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], Heming's defining moment came after his eldest brother Sigvaldi deserted the host under cover of darkness. Heming and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell the High]] took command of the left wing, and Sigvaldi's men — to the wonder of all — refused to follow their captain in desertion and instead "went all under Heming and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]]" (015-011). Together they led the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] left wing alongside the Danish contingent under [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s captains (015-011).

## Role in the Story

Heming represents the ideal of the dependable warrior: capable with ship and sword, loyal to his [[oath|sworn oaths]], and content to follow worthy leaders rather than scheme for his own advancement. His father's assessment — that Heming will "ever have that nature to follow still where another leadeth" — is offered as a limitation, yet the saga quietly vindicates this nature at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], where the follower proves more honourable than the cunning leader who deserted.

Heming's winter captaincy by lot illustrates the democratic-communal dimension of the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] law: even an unglamorous duty like winter garrison command was shared equally among the lords. His acceptance of this duty without complaint contrasts with the political maneuvering of his elder brother Sigvaldi and establishes Heming as a man of quiet reliability rather than ambition.

The absorption of Sigvaldi's men under Heming and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]]'s command at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] is one of the saga's most telling moral moments. It suggests that the rank and file of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]], whatever their own flaws, could recognize and reject treachery when they saw it, and that they trusted the straightforward Heming and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]] more than the cunning Sigvaldi.

## Historical Basis

Heming (*Hemingr*) appears in the *Jómsvíkinga saga* as the second son of [[strut-harald|Earl Strut-Harald]] and brother of Sigvaldi and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell the High]]. Unlike his brothers, Heming does not feature prominently in Anglo-Saxon or other independent historical sources, which may suggest he is a more purely saga-derived figure. The saga tradition presents him consistently as the loyal, dependable middle brother — a narrative archetype that Eddison preserves and deepens through Strut-Harald's character analysis. The practice of leaving a winter garrison at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] under a rotating captain is attested in the saga sources and reflects the seasonal rhythm of Viking warfare, in which raiding was a summer activity and winter was spent in hall.
