## Definition

In the Norse world of the novel, an oath (Old Norse eiðr) is a solemn and binding vow, typically sworn upon a god — most often Thor, for the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] company — or upon a sacred object such as a temple ring or altar. An oath is not merely a promise but a religious act: to break one is to forfeit both honour and the protection of the gods, and in the harsh law of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]], it carries the penalty of death.

The oath functions as the foundational bond of the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fellowship. When Styrbiorn joins the company, the procedure is explicit: "must swear a strong oath to hold by the lay of the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] vikings" (004-003). The oath creates a mutual obligation: the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] pledge loyalty to Styrbiorn, and Styrbiorn in return pledges to lead them and share spoils equally. The binding nature of oaths is further dramatised when [[king-eric|King Eric]], facing defeat, visits Odin's altar and "sware oath unto the [[odin|All-Father]]" (015-041), pledging himself as a human [[blood-offering|sacrifice]] in return for victory.


> So now was a ram slain and oaths taken betwixt them of either side, calling ancient witnesses to hear their vow: by the southing sun, and by the bright lightning-stream, and by the rock of Victory-Tyr, and by the ring of Ulli. And therewith must each man say, "If I do not according to this oath, then let the ship that saileth under me sail not, though a fair wind blow; and let the sword I draw bite not, save when it shall sing about mine own head."
>
> (004-036)

## Occurrences in the Novel

The oath ceremony is first described in Chapter IV, "[[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]," when Styrbiorn is inducted into the fellowship. The procedure has a precise ritual form: "So now was a ram slain and oaths taken betwixt them of either hand, full sure, unto Thor" (004-036). The ram-sacrifice and the invocation of Thor — the god most associated with oaths in Norse practice — lends the ceremony religious gravity. Biorn, speaking of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]]' earlier vows to Styrbiorn, states that they were "made fast betwixt us by strong oaths unto Thor, in whom most of all us vikings put our trust" (004-032).

That the oath is no empty formality is underscored later in the same chapter, when [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] reminds Styrbiorn of the penalty for oath-breaking: "Stands it not in the Law that he that breaketh oath with us, be he captain or common man, shall take death for his reward?" (004-031). Styrbiorn himself later contemplates having Biorn slain "despite all oaths" (004-042), revealing the tension between political expediency and the iron law of the fellowship.

The oath reappears at the narrative's climax. [[king-eric|King Eric]], desperate on the eve of the third day at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], goes to the [[upsala|Upsala]] temple and "sware oath unto the [[odin|All-Father]] that if He should vouchsafe to him victory in this battle, then would he after ten years give himself unto Odin" (015-041). This oath — a promise of self-sacrifice — mirrors and intensifies the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] ram-oath: where the vikings offered an animal to Thor, the king offers his own life to Odin.

## Historical/Mythological Context

In Old Norse society, the oath (eiðr) was a fundamental legal and religious institution. Oaths were sworn on a temple ring (stallahringr) or on sacrificial animals, and the gods — particularly Thor and Ullr — were invoked as witnesses and avengers of perjury. The Icelandic sagas and law codes (Grágás) attest to the central role of oath-taking in legal procedures, marriage contracts, and political alliances.

The [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]]' oath-ceremony in the novel, with its ram-sacrifice to Thor, corresponds to the historical practice of blót, where animals were sacrificed and their blood was used to hallow the oath-ring. Jómsvíkinga saga describes a strict code of conduct for the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]], including provisions against fleeing battle and speaking words of fear — laws which the novel obliquely references through [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s reminders of the penalty for oath-breaking. [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s self-sacrificial oath to Odin reflects the motif of the king who offers himself to the gods for the good of his [[all-persons|people]], a pattern attested in saga accounts of rulers such as Aun the Old (Ynglinga saga).
