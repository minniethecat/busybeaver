# Fetch (Fylgja)

## Definition

The fylgja (plural *fylgjur*), rendered in the saga as "fetch," is a supernatural attendant spirit in Norse belief — an externalised soul or guardian that accompanies every person from birth and whose fate is inseparable from the person to whom it is attached. The word *fylgja* derives from the Old Norse verb "to follow," and the spirit follows each individual throughout life, appearing most often at moments of crisis or on the eve of death. To those gifted with second sight — known in Old Norse as *ofreskr* — the fylgja could manifest visibly, most commonly in animal form (a bear, [[earl-wolf|wolf]], or bird, reflecting the person's character) or as a woman. The appearance of a person's fylgja to another was held to be a death-omen: to see a man's fetch was to know the manner and imminence of his [[norse-fate|doom]].

In the world of *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]*, the fylgja is no distant mythological abstraction but a living presence, apprehended by [[queen-sigrid|Sigrid the Queen]] through her gift of second sight at two critical moments that bracket the saga — the first on Olaf's [[howe]] before the story's action begins, and the second on the same howe as the battle of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] reaches its climax.

## Occurrences in the Novel

### Sigrid's First Vision on Olaf's Howe (Chapter 1)

The saga introduces Sigrid's supernatural sight early, though obliquely. In the novel's opening chapter, the young Sigrid stands with Styrbiorn atop [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]]'s Howe, watching him wrestle with his musk-ox [[moldi|Moldi]]. As they speak, she is suddenly seized by a vision: "Sigrid seemed as if she heard him not. She was gazing with an altered countenance south over [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]]. Styrbiorn looked up and saw her so stare, wide-eyed, as if in fear: as if in those silent water-meadows she saw some strange matter, hidden until now" (001-056). When Styrbiorn presses her, she warns him away: "I am three years older than thou, and can see things thou canst not see. The dead be in this place. Come away" (001-066). Her eyes are "fixed on him, but as if she saw him not" and she looks "ghastly" (001-068).

This scene is the first intimation of Sigrid's second sight — a power she herself acknowledges as a function of her age and nature, and one that the young Styrbiorn, with his warrior's scepticism, dismisses as womanish fear: "Women are ever afeared of bugs and bogles" (001-067). But the retrospective narration in Chapter 3 confirms the supernatural character of the event when it refers to "that Sigrid who was with Styrbiorn on [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]]'s howe, and saw visions there as is aforesaid" (003-001).

### Sigrid's Vision of the Fetches at Fyrisfield (Chapter 15)

The saga's most explicit fylgja manifestation occurs in Chapter 15, as the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]] rages. Four years after her first vision, Sigrid again climbs [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]]'s Howe — now as Queen of Sweden — to watch for signs of the battle's outcome. The narrative draws an explicit parallel to the earlier scene: "She climbed the howe and stood there gazing southaway, even as she had gazed four years ago when she had stood there and looked on Styrbiorn trying his strength with [[moldi|Moldi]]" (015-023).

What follows is the definitive utterance on the fylgja in the novel. Sigrid speaks to herself: "I saw fetches then... I saw him as if read and battle-slain in the mead yonder. And I thought not this day should come when such a sight should be meat and drink to me" (015-023). The phrase "read" here carries the Old English sense of *raed* — counsel, decree, fate — while "battle-slain" names the vision's content with terrible clarity. Four years earlier, on the same howe, Sigrid saw Styrbiorn's fylgja — his attendant spirit — and in seeing it, saw his fated death at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], "in the mead yonder." The vision foretold precisely what now comes to pass: Styrbiorn falls that very day, his army destroyed by the scree-fall on [[windbergsfell|Windbergsfell]] and the rout that follows (015-051—015-053). By day's end, Thorgnyr reports that Styrbiorn and twenty men "were stood together back to back on a knoll of grass in the midst o' the field" with their standard-shafts struck into the earth, surrounded on all sides (015-053). The Queen waits in the dark until [[king-eric|King Eric]] returns and the question she was ready to ask "froze in her throat" (015-067) — for the vision had already shown her what this day would bring.

### Sigrid's Second Sight as a Character Trait

The fylgja vision is not an isolated supernatural intrusion but part of Sigrid's established character. Her ability to "see things thou canst not see" (001-066) marks her as a woman of *ofreskr* — second sight — a quality associated in Norse tradition with certain individuals, often women, who could perceive the hidden world of spirits and fates. This gift sets her apart from the saga's warrior men, who trust in strength and steel. Styrbiorn's dismissive "bugs and bogles" (001-067) contrasts with Sigrid's genuine terror at what she sees, a terror the more striking for her otherwise proud and haughty bearing. The fylgja vision thus serves as a window into Sigrid's inner life: beneath the ambition and the queenly poise is a woman burdened with knowledge she did not seek, who has seen the doom of the man she once scorned and now watches come to ruin.

## Historical Context

In historical Norse belief, the fylgja (plural *fylgjur*, also rendered "fetch") was an attendant spirit or external soul bound to an individual or family from birth to death. The word derives from the Old Norse verb *fylgja* ("to follow, accompany"), and the fylgja was conceived as accompanying each person throughout life — unseen by most, but visible to those with second sight, particularly in dreams or at the approach of death.

The fylgja could take several forms. Most commonly, it appeared as an animal — a bear, wolf, boar, fox, or bird — whose nature reflected the character and destiny of the person to whom it was attached. In other traditions, the fylgja manifested as a woman, often clad in white or blue, who acted as a guardian. The fylgja was not a soul in the Christian sense but a semi-independent spirit-being, a personification of fate or luck (*hamingja*) that could sometimes be transferred from a dying person to a descendant. To see one's own fylgja when awake was a known omen of imminent death; to see another's fylgja foretold that person's fate.

The concept appears across the Old Norse literary corpus. In *Njal's Saga*, a man dreams of a goat that lies in a hollow bleeding, interpreted as the fylgja of his enemy about to be killed. In the *Volsunga Saga*, [[sigurd-cape|Sigurd]]'s death is foreshadowed by visions and dreams. The *Poetic Edda* references fylgjur attending a child at birth. Across these sources, the fylgja functions as a bridge between the world of the living and the foreknowledge of fate — exactly the role it plays in *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]*, where Sigrid's vision of Styrbiorn's "fetches" on Olaf's Howe reveals his doom years before it is accomplished.

The notion of an "external" soul or guardian spirit has parallels across Indo-European cultures: the Greek *daimon*, the Roman *genius*, the Christian guardian angel, and the Irish fetch or *taisch*. E.R. Eddison's rendering "fetches" in the English text follows a long tradition in English folklore (from Old English *faecc*, meaning "apparition, double"), where a "fetch" was a wraith or double of a living person whose appearance presaged death. Eddison's choice of "fetches" is philologically precise: it captures both the literal Norse concept of a following spirit and the English folkloric association with doom and foreknowledge.
