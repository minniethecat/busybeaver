## Biography

Thorir was one of three men — along with [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]] and [[thorgisl|Thorgisl]] — held in good esteem by [[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious, and widely believed to be the King's bastard sons (007-003). His parentage was never formally acknowledged, but the King's favour toward the three was noted by all at court.

Thorir is distinguished among the trio by his cynical wit. When [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]] suggested Styrbiorn would be a threat upon his return from exile, Thorir coined the mocking epithet "lap-king" — "one that should sit quiet in his mother's lap, and leave us to follow our sport" (007-025). This sneering dismissal of the young Styrbiorn captures Thorir's characteristic blend of contempt and pragmatism.

At the violent [[ball-game-kysingtarn|ball-game at Kysingtarn]], Thorir was struck hard in the belly by a ball thrown by Biorn of the opposing side — a blow that "knocked all the breath out of his body" (007-075). [[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf]] and [[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]] looked on as the game degenerated into open violence between Styrbiorn's [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] and the King's men.

Thorir was the one who proposed taking their suspicions to [[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]], reasoning that "this should be the hopefullest way, if thou wouldst be willing to talk to the King" (007-085). When Thorgnyr rebuffed them, it was Thorir who pragmatically assessed the situation: "He will not heed us" (007-095).

At the [[upsala|Upsala]] [[hall-feast|banquet]], Thorir shared in the wine and mockery with his companions. When [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]] complained of Styrbiorn's growing influence, Thorir offered his signature counsel of indifference: "Well, so the ale runneth trill-lill down the throat, what need grieve us?" (009-020). He also took pleasure in teasing [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]] about his attraction to the King's dark-haired bondmaid, leaning close to whisper in his ear (009-025).

Thorir last appears among the court assemblies before [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], standing with "sneering looks and the swagger of court bravery" (014-008). His fate after the battle is not recorded, but he is presumed to have fallen with [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s host.

## Role in the Story

Thorir completes the triadic antagonist group of the King's bastards. Where [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]] is the aggressive schemer and [[thorgisl|Thorgisl]] the cautious observer, Thorir is the cynical wit — quick with a mocking phrase and quicker to accept the status quo when resistance seems futile. His "lap-king" epithet is the most memorable contribution of the three to the novel's dialogue, encapsulating their contempt for the young Styrbiorn's claim to power.

The trio's shifting dynamic — from active scheming to resigned drinking — mirrors the arc of Styrbiorn's inexorable rise, as even his most dedicated court enemies gradually concede the futility of opposing him.

## Relationships

- **[[helgi-ericson|Helgi]]** (companion): The eldest and most aggressive of the three; Thorir acts as his sardonic foil (007-025)
- **[[thorgisl|Thorgisl]]** (companion): Fellow bastard son; the three drink, scheme, and mock together at court (009-008)
- **[[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious** (presumed father): Showed favour to the three bastards without formal acknowledgment (007-003)
- **[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn Olafson]]** (rival): The target of Thorir's "lap-king" mockery and the object of the bastards' resentment (007-025)
- **[[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]]** (acquaintance): Thorir proposed seeking the lawman's counsel, but was rebuffed (007-085)

## Historical Basis

Thorir (Þórir) is a common Old Norse name meaning "Thor's warrior." Like his companions [[thorgisl|Thorgisl]] and [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]], Thorir appears to be a fictional creation of E.R. Eddison, not based on a specific historical figure from the Styrbiorn legend. The trio of bastard sons serves a dramatic function as court antagonists, a common motif in Norse saga literature.
