# Slaying of Aki

The **Slaying of Aki** is the inciting event of *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]*, occurring in Chapter I when the fifteen-year-old [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn Olafson]] kills [[aki|Aki]], a man of the [[king-eric-bodyguard|King's Bodyguard]], at a [[hall-feast|feast]] in [[upsala|Upsala]]. This act of violence triggers Styrbiorn's three-year exile and sets the entire narrative in motion.

## Summary

During a [[hall-feast|feast]] at [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s hall, [[aki|Aki]] — "a man of good kin among the bonder-folk" — speaks rashly to the young Styrbiorn, mocking his demand for half the [[swedish-kingdom|Swedish kingdom]] as his inheritance (001-027). Styrbiorn, who had come before the King to claim his birthright as the son of the late [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]], responds with sudden and lethal violence.

The narrator records that Styrbiorn, "bethinking him not at all that he spake in the King's hall," seizes a drinking-horn and strikes [[aki|Aki]] a mortal blow (001-028). The killing is swift and decisive — [[aki|Aki]] dies on the spot, and Styrbiorn declares: "And I'll pay no boot for his slaying" (001-053), refusing the customary weregild that Norse law would demand for such a killing.

The killing sends shockwaves through the Swedish court. The bonder-folk, [[aki|Aki]]'s kinsmen, are outraged, and "much murmuring against Styrbiorn" spreads through the land (002-001). [[king-eric|King Eric]] must navigate between justice for his subject and his obligation to his nephew.

## Context

### Styrbiorn's Inheritance Claim

The slaying occurs in the context of Styrbiorn's demand for his inheritance. As the son of the late [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]] Biornson — Eric's brother and co-king — Styrbiorn believes he is entitled to half the Swedish realm. He makes this demand at the age of fifteen, the traditional Norse age of majority, coming before [[king-eric|King Eric]] with a retinue of supporters (001-002).

### The Feast Setting

The killing takes place in the King's hall during a [[hall-feast|feast]], a setting where the laws of hospitality (*grith*) should protect all present. By killing [[aki|Aki]] within the hall, Styrbiorn violates not only the general peace but the specific sanctity of the King's table — a double breach of Norse legal custom.

> Aki of the King's bodyguard (bethinking him not at all that he spake in the King's hall, and before the King) answered him with scornful words (001-027).

This detail is significant: [[aki|Aki]] speaks carelessly "before the King," exploiting the protection of the hall to taunt the young prince. His miscalculation costs him his life.

## Consequences

### Styrbiorn's Exile

The immediate consequence is Styrbiorn's exile. [[king-eric|King Eric]], guided by [[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]]'s counsel, decrees that Styrbiorn must leave Sweden for three years (002-001). This judgment is carefully calibrated: harsh enough to appease the bonder-folk's demand for justice, but lenient enough to spare the King's nephew from the full penalty of outlawry.

### Political Fallout

The killing sharpens existing political divisions. The bonder-folk, already suspicious of Styrbiorn's ambitions, are "dug up anew" in their grief (002-004). The incident creates a lasting enmity between Styrbiorn and the King's household — particularly with other members of the [[king-eric-bodyguard|King's bodyguard]], who will remain his enemies throughout the novel.

### Narrative Engine

The exile drives the entire plot: Styrbiorn's journey abroad leads him to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], where he joins the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]], meets [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]], and eventually returns to challenge [[king-eric|King Eric]] at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]]. Without the slaying, there is no exile; without the exile, there is no story.

## Historical Basis

The slaying of [[aki|Aki]] and Styrbiorn's subsequent exile are fictional inventions by E.R. Eddison, though they draw on authentic Norse literary conventions. The historical [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]] (*Styrbjörn Sterki*) is known from a fragmentary tradition preserved in the *Hervarar saga* and several Icelandic þættir, but the details of his falling-out with [[king-eric|King Eric]] are not recorded in surviving sources. Eddison constructs this scene following the saga convention of a [[hall-feast|feast]]-hall killing (*borðvíg*) that precipitates a feud.
