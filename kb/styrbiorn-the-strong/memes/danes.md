# Danes

The **Danes** are the [[all-persons|people]] of Denmark under [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] ([[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]]), portrayed in *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]* as a proud and powerful Viking-age nation whose political and military fortunes are deeply entangled with [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn]]'s quest for the Swedish throne.

## Description

The Danes appear throughout the novel as a distinct political and cultural entity, centered on [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s court. They are characterized by their maritime power, their complex relationship with their Scandinavian neighbors, and their role as both host and battlefield ally to Styrbiorn.

Eddison portrays the Danes with a distinct cultural identity. The narrator notes that [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] "was a man of the greatest largesse and magnificence in all things outward," and his court at [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]] is a center of wealth, poetry, and political intrigue (005-008). The Danes drink competitively ("drank cup and cup against the Danes" (005-008)) and maintain the custom of skalds reciting lays in the hall each night (005-009).

A significant cultural detail is that [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] had "bade the Danes take christening" (005-009), marking the historical transition of Denmark from Norse paganism to Christianity under [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]]'s reign. This religious dimension adds tension to their interactions with the staunchly pagan Swedes and [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]].

### Key Lords

The sons of [[strut-harald|Strut-Harald the Earl]] bridge the Danish world and the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fellowship:

- **[[sigvaldi|Sigvaldi Jarl]]** — eldest son, future [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] captain
- **[[heming|Heming]]** — second son, captain of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] during one winter
- **[[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell the High]]** — third son, prominent warrior

These men of [[skaney|Skane]]y (then Danish territory) move fluidly between [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]'s court and the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fortress, embodying the interconnected nature of Scandinavian power politics.

## Role in the Story

### Hosting Styrbiorn

The Danes first become significant when Styrbiorn, seeking a winter haven during his exile, is persuaded by [[biorn-broadwicker|Biorn Broadwicker]] to "guest" with [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] in Denmark (005-005). The [[yule-feast-denmark|Yule Feast in Denmark]] becomes one of the novel's most extended set-pieces, with Styrbiorn navigating the dangerous waters of a foreign court where skalds praise him and rivals seek to discredit him.

> King Harald was a man of the greatest largesse and magnificence in all things outward. His hall was the richest in all the North, and every night was a skald bidden to sing a lay before the King and his house-carles. (005-008)

### The Marriage Alliance

The Danish alliance is sealed when Styrbiorn marries [[queen-thyri|Thyri]], [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]'s daughter. The betrothal is negotiated by [[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf]] acting as Styrbiorn's ambassador (008-031). This marriage gives Styrbiorn a political anchor — a claim on Danish support — that strengthens his position against [[king-eric|King Eric]].

Queen Thyri's own perspective on the Danes provides insight into the cultural tensions at play. She tells Styrbiorn that "we Danes love them not" when speaking of the Swedish kings, explaining that "kings have reason to fear them" (006-029). Her Danish identity remains a significant element of her character and her role as mediator between Styrbiorn and her father.

### The Cowing of the Dane-King

The Danish relationship reaches a crisis when the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]], including Styrbiorn and the sons of Strut-Harald, challenge [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] directly. The episode known as the [[cowing-of-the-dane-king|Cowing of the Dane-King]] demonstrates the limits of Danish royal authority when confronted with the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fighting force (012-031). Harald is forced to make peace on unfavorable terms.

### The Fleet and Fyrisfield

Despite the earlier humiliation, the Danes contribute significantly to Styrbiorn's invasion fleet. The expedition "sailed for the north" with "the most part of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] and the Danes" (013-042). At the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], the Danes under [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]'s following form the left wing of Styrbiorn's battle array (015-011).

However, some Danes who remained behind in [[jutland|Jutland]] engage in political maneuvers of their own: "upon these things did the Danes that were left behind in [[jutland|Jutland]]" pursue their own interests (013-044), highlighting the factional nature of Danish politics.

## Historical Basis

The Danes of *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]* correspond to the historical Danish [[all-persons|people]] of the late 10th century, during the reign of [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]] (c. 958–986). This was a transformative period in Danish history: Harald had unified Denmark under a single crown, converted the nation to Christianity (as commemorated on the famous Jelling Stone), and extended Danish influence into Norway.

Eddison's portrayal is broadly faithful to the historical record. The Danes of the novel are a formidable naval power, culturally sophisticated (with their skalds and hall-customs), and politically fragmented — all consistent with what is known of late Viking-age Denmark. The poet-king dynamic at Harald's court reflects genuine Norse court culture, where skalds served as both entertainers and political propagandists.

The tension between the Danes and the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] — Danish subjects who operate as an autonomous military power — reflects the real political complexity of the period, where [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]'s semi-independent status created recurrent conflicts with the Danish crown.
