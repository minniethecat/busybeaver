# Helgi Ericson

**Helgi Ericson** is one of the three illegitimate sons of [[king-eric|King Eric the Victorious]] who serve as antagonists to [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]] throughout the latter half of the novel. Alongside his half-brothers [[thorgisl]] and [[thorir]], Helgi is a constant thorn in Styrbiorn's side — mocking, scheming, and ultimately leading the King's bodyguard against him at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]].

## Biography

Helgi first appears in Chapter VII, "Eric and Styrbiorn," where he is introduced as one of three men whom [[king-eric|King Eric]] "held in good esteem" at his court in [[upsala|Upsala]] (007-001). The narrator notes that these three were "not good friends with the throng of [[all-persons|people]], and many thought it as near a bit to call them ill-doers as call them men of valour" (007-001).

Though their parentage is never explicitly confirmed, the narrator observes: "None knew the father or kindred of any one of them, but most folk thought they must be [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s bastards, and that for this sake his eye rested kindly on them" (007-001). This ambiguous status — royal blood without legitimate standing — drives much of their hostility toward Styrbiorn, who represents a legitimate rival claimant to the Swedish throne.

Throughout the novel, Helgi demonstrates a sharp tongue and a talent for provocation. When [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]] passes by carrying the infant Prince Olaf, Helgi remarks darkly that Styrbiorn might send the child "to a cold lodging," quoting the proverb "a foe's child is a wolf to cherish" (007-004). He is the first to raise suspicions about Styrbiorn and [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]]'s relationship, claiming he has "smelt matters betwixt them now and then" (007-008) — a rumor that would have dangerous consequences.

## Role in the Story

### The Ball-Game at Kysingtarn

Helgi's animosity toward Styrbiorn erupts into open violence at the [[ball-game-kysingtarn|ball-game at Kysingtarn]]. When Styrbiorn arrives at the frozen tarn to join the play, Helgi and his faction set themselves against him. The narrator records that "sometimes would those companions, Helgi and his, be keeking and girding" at Styrbiorn (007-022), and that Styrbiorn "played most against Helgi, and Helgi gat ever the worst of it" (007-023).

The ball-game escalates into a brawl when Helgi, after one of Styrbiorn's tackles, calls for his brothers to join the fray. [[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf]] later asks pointedly about their conduct: "What say Helgi and his? Methinks they have been somewhat busy of late" (007-034). The confrontation at [[kysingtarn|Kysingtarn]] marks the point where the rivalry between Styrbiorn and the bastard brothers turns from verbal sparring to physical hostility.

### The Banquet at Upsala

At the [[thing-at-upsala|banquet in Upsala]] celebrating Styrbiorn's betrothal to [[queen-thyri|Thyri Haraldsdottir]], Helgi and his brothers sit "cheek by cheek" with barely concealed resentment (009-004). When wine flows freely, Helgi's tongue loosens further. He complains openly about Styrbiorn's favored position: "it dislikes me he should sit there so quiet" (009-006), and makes insinuations about the betrothal.

> "He thinketh on the Swede realm, which needs must be his when he hath done away the King hereafter, and will have this bond with the Dane-King to strengthen him in the more." (009-007)

Helgi's provocations at the [[hall-feast|feast]] contribute to the rising tension that eventually spills over in the "Broken Meats" incident, where Styrbiorn's men clash physically with the King's household (010-020).

### The Battle of Fyrisfield

Helgi's narrative arc culminates at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], where he serves as "captain of the King's bodyguard" (015-017). He leads the shield-wall surrounding [[king-eric|King Eric]] during the battle's final phase, personally engaging in combat with the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]].

During the battle, Helgi is severely wounded by one of Styrbiorn's champions. The text records that he was carried into the King's hall with grievous injuries: "They bare Helgi into the King's hall. He was bleeding heavily from his wound" (015-033). His fate after the battle is left ambiguous, though his wounds are described as life-threatening.

## Relationships

- **[[king-eric|King Eric the Victorious]]** — Presumed father. Eric favors Helgi and his half-brothers, granting them positions in his household despite their dubious reputation.
- **[[thorgisl]]** — Half-brother and fellow antagonist. [[thorgisl|Thorgisl]] often plays the more cautious foil to Helgi's provocations.
- **[[thorir]]** — Half-brother. [[thorir|Thorir]] participates in the scheming against Styrbiorn.
- **[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — Archenemy. Helgi views Styrbiorn as a threat to his own prospects and the stability of the "lap-king" arrangement under Eric.
- **[[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]]** — Helgi spreads rumors about Sigrid's relationship with Styrbiorn, making her a target of his political machinations.

## Historical Basis

The figure of Helgi Ericson does not appear in the historical record. E.R. Eddison appears to have invented Helgi and his brothers as dramatic foils for Styrbiorn, drawing on the well-established saga convention of royal bastards who scheme against legitimate heirs. The historical [[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious is known to have had children, but the names of any illegitimate sons are not recorded in surviving sources such as the *Hervarar saga* or Adam of Bremen's *Gesta Hammaburgensis*.
