## Biography

Olaf Biornson was the son of King [[biorn-the-old|Biorn the Old]] and joint King of Sweden alongside his brother [[king-eric|Eric the Victorious]]. The two brothers reigned together for forty years "in good brotherly love and friendship" (001-005). Olaf married [[ingibiorg|Ingibiorg]], daughter of Earl [[thrand-of-sula|Thrand of Sula]], and they had four daughters — Thora, Thurid, Asdis, and Aud — before the long-awaited birth of their son Styrbiorn (001-005).

Olaf's reign was marked by his frustration at having no male heir. The saga notes that "ungainly it seemed to King Olaf that he should get none but girl-children," but in the end [[ingibiorg|Queen Ingibiorg]] bore him a son (001-006). This son, Styrbiorn, would become the protagonist of the saga — though Olaf did not live to see his son's rise, his exile, or his eventual fall at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]].

Olaf died before the main [[all-events|events]] of the novel, and his burial [[howe]] (mound) at [[upsala|Upsala]] became a site of symbolic significance. It was upon this howe that the young Styrbiorn sat when he demanded his inheritance from [[king-eric|King Eric]] — a scene that sets the entire narrative in motion (001-002). A king's bodyguard reported to Eric: "So it is, Lord, that we found him sitting on his father's howe, King Olaf Biornson's" (001-008).

## Role in the Story

Though Olaf Biornson is deceased before the novel's opening, his legacy looms large over the narrative. His joint kingship with Eric establishes the legitimacy of Styrbiorn's claim to half the Swedish realm. Chapter I is titled "On King Olaf's Howe," and the howe serves as a physical and symbolic locus — the place where Styrbiorn asserts his birthright, and where the conflict with his uncle Eric is first kindled.

Styrbiorn's identity is repeatedly tied to his father. When challenged at sea, he declares himself as "Styrbiorn, whom men call the [[styrbiorn|Swedes' Champion]], son of Olaf the Swede-King" (013-015). The paternal lineage is central to Styrbiorn's claim and his self-conception throughout the saga.

## Historical Basis

The historical Olaf Biornson (Óláfr Bjǫrnsson) is a semi-legendary figure known from Norse sources, including the *Heimskringla* and *Styrbjarnar þáttr Svíakappa*. He is said to have ruled jointly with his brother [[king-eric|Eric the Victorious]] in the late 10th century. According to the *Heimskringla*, Olaf died of poisoning (though Eddison's novel does not specify his cause of death), leaving his son Styrbiorn under Eric's guardianship — a situation that led to the legendary conflict dramatized in the novel.

Eddison follows the broad outlines of the Norse tradition while expanding the character of Olaf primarily through the symbolic weight of his howe and the theme of paternal legacy. The historical Olaf's reign is placed in the context of the consolidation of the [[swedish-kingdom|Swedish kingdom]] at [[upsala|Uppsala]].
