# Betrothal Feast of Styrbiorn and Thyri

## Summary

The betrothal [[hall-feast|feast]] of Styrbiorn and [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] was the central [[hall-feast|banquet]] of a great multi-day [[hall-feast|feast]] summoned by [[king-eric|King Eric]] at [[upsala|Upsala]]. The occasion served a dual purpose: it celebrated the troth-plighting of Styrbiorn, the King's nephew, to [[thyri|Thyri]], daughter of [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] of Denmark, and it preceded the lawful [[thing-stead|Thing]] at which Eric intended to grant Styrbiorn his half-share of the kingdom (009-001). [[thyri|Thyri]] had come from Denmark with "a great and honourable company to bring her home to [[upsala|Upsala]]," and the Swedes "deemed well of her and praised her beauty" (009-001).

The [[hall-feast|banquet]], held on the third night of the [[hall-feast|feast]], was a spectacle of unprecedented magnificence. The King's hall was hung with "outlandish hangings, curtains of rich crimson stuff and cloth of gold and broidered work brought out of [[garthrealm|Micklegarth]] or Garthrealm or the Western Isles" (009-022). The tables blazed with plates and goblets "rough with jewels and beautifully enchased," treasures that Styrbiorn had brought from his eastern harrying — "so many and rich and goodly as had not heretofore been seen, not one tenth the number nor goodliness, in the realm of Sweden" (009-022). The greatness of the company matched the grandeur of the setting: "never was so great a throng of folk gathered together in the King's house" (009-003).

The [[hall-feast|feast]]'s dramatic centre was the skald's performance of the Hell-ride of Brynhild, the old poem of doomed love and betrayal. As the skald spoke the lay, Styrbiorn's gaze fell upon [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]], and he beheld her "with bodily eye" but saw her in his mind as Brynhild the Valkyrie (009-088). When their eyes finally met — "he looked up: met, for the first time, Sigrid's glance, and became on the instant like a man drunk" (009-093). This silent exchange, witnessed by no one but the two of them, set in motion the [[all-events|events]] that would unravel the betrothal and reshape the saga's central relationships.

The saga captures the splendour of the occasion in lavish detail:

> The hall was hung with outlandish hangings, curtains of rich crimson stuff and cloth of gold and broidered work brought out of Micklegarth or Garthrealm or the Western Isles, plunder of war or gifts of peace and tribute made to Styrbiorn by kings and lords that he had brought under him; and these treasures he had given now to the King his uncle, so many and rich and goodly as had not heretofore been seen, not one tenth the number nor goodliness, in the realm of Sweden.
>
> (009-022)


## Context

[[king-eric|King Eric]] had summoned the [[hall-feast|feast]] and the [[thing-stead|Thing]] as part of his effort to settle the Danish question and Styrbiorn's claim to the throne in a single stroke. The betrothal to [[thyri|Thyri]] bound Styrbiorn to the Danish royal house — a powerful alliance — while the promised grant of half the kingdom would satisfy Styrbiorn's demand for his inheritance. The celebration, Eric hoped, would close the chapter of Styrbiorn's exile and secure the [[swedish-kingdom|Swede-realm]]'s peace.

The [[hall-feast|feast]] was also the setting for the simmering resentments of the Swedish court. [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]], [[thorgisl|Thorgisl]], and [[thorir|Thorir]] — three of Eric's courtiers who had never accepted Styrbiorn's return — sat cheek by jowl at the [[hall-feast|banquet]], grousing about Styrbiorn's pride and speculating darkly about the King's wandering eye (009-005 to 009-021). Their muttered discontent, set against the splendour of the [[hall-feast|feast]], foreshadowed the "broken meats" that would follow in its aftermath.

## Key Participants

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — The betrothed prince, sat in the high seat on the lower bench, merry of heart "and at ease and at peace, as for that while, with all the world" (009-022). His composure shattered the moment his eyes met Sigrid's.

**[[thyri|Thyri]]** — Styrbiorn's affianced bride, "white-skinned and with night-dark hair, quiet, with eyes and ears only for him" (009-022). She sat beside Styrbiorn, unaware of the silent exchange that passed between him and the Queen.

**[[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious** — Host of the [[hall-feast|feast]], seated in his high seat with the Queen at his right hand. He showed "his youth come back upon him, eating and drinking and making merry, oftenest with his eye on Styrbiorn" (009-022).

**[[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]] the Haughty** — Sat at the King's right hand, quiet and composed in her purple silk gown and darkly sparkling jewels. Her exchange of glances with Styrbiorn during the Brynhild lay was the [[hall-feast|feast]]'s hidden climax (009-093).

**The Skald** — Performed the Hell-ride of Brynhild, a poem of doomed love that became the mirror in which Styrbiorn and Sigrid saw their own fates reflected (009-023 to 009-082).

## Consequences

The betrothal [[hall-feast|feast]] should have sealed a political alliance and ushered in an era of peace. Instead, the moment between Styrbiorn and Sigrid — that single sustained look across the King's table while the Brynhild lay filled the hall — planted the seed of destruction. In the very next chapter, Sigrid came to Styrbiorn's chamber in the night, and the "broken meats" of the [[hall-feast|feast]]'s aftermath tore apart the arrangement the [[hall-feast|banquet]] had been meant to celebrate. Styrbiorn, who had sat at ease and at peace, would within days be plotting flight from Sweden.

## Historical Basis

The troth-plighting (festarmal) was a formal Norse betrothal ceremony that preceded the wedding, often held at a [[hall-feast|feast]] before witnesses. The exchange of gifts and the public declaration before the assembled company were essential elements. The novel follows this pattern: the betrothal was "performed with due ceremony" at the [[hall-feast|banquet]], but the wedding proper was deferred until after the [[thing-stead|Thing]], when Styrbiorn was to have taken up his kingdom (009-002). The [[hall-feast|banquet]]'s magnificence, with its treasures from [[garthrealm|Micklegarth]] and the Eastern trade routes, reflects the wealth that Norse chieftains amassed through raiding and [[scat-tribute|tribute]] during the Viking Age.
