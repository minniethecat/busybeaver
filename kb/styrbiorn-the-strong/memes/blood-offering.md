## Definition

The blood-offering, known in Old Norse as *blót*, was the central sacrificial ritual of Norse paganism. It involved the ritual slaughter of animals (and in earlier times, possibly humans), with the blood being collected and sprinkled upon altars, temple walls, and participants as a means of hallowing and communion with the gods. The term *blót* is cognate with the Old English *blōtan* and Gothic *blōtan*, both meaning "to worship with sacrifice." In the context of the novel, blood-offering functions as the primary expression of fidelity to the old Norse gods, performed at the great temple at [[upsala|Upsala]] ([[upsala|Uppsala]]) and at sacred sites across the Norse world.

## Occurrences in the Novel

Blood-offering appears at two critical junctures in the story, serving symbolic and political purposes.

The first occurrence is in Chapter 1, during the annual autumn sacrifice at the temple at [[upsala|Upsala]]. [[king-eric|King Eric]], following ancient custom, "made great blood-offering in the temple at [[upsala|Upsala]] for the goodness of the year, as was his wont and the wont of his fathers before him from time immemorial" (001-003). Lords and great men gathered from across the land for the rite, while Styrbiorn, aged fifteen, refused to join the [[hall-feast|feast]], instead sitting on his father's howe — a gesture of defiance that marks his coming of age. The King refers to this ceremony as "every autumn [[hall-feast|feast]] of sacrifice" and notes that Styrbiorn's protest has now occurred for three consecutive years (001-004).

The second occurrence is recounted in Chapter 5, in a story told by Biorn to Styrbiorn aboard [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]'s ship. [[earl-hakon|Earl Hakon of Norway]], after being forcibly baptized into Christianity by Keisar Otto and [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]] Harald, renounced his forced conversion the moment he was free. As Biorn relates: "as soon as Earl Hakon was gotten with his ships out of the land, men say that he went ashore on an isle that is there and made blood-offering unto the Gods and so went again ashipboard and so home to Hladir" (005-009). By performing the blood-offering, Hakon ritually repudiated the Christian faith and restored his allegiance to the old gods — an act that the narrator treats as the restoration of Hakon's independence and honour.

## Historical Context

The *blót* was the most important public ritual in pre-Christian Scandinavia. Historical and literary sources — including Adam of Bremen's *Gesta Hammaburgensis*, the Icelandic sagas, and Snorri Sturluson's *Heimskringla* — describe large-scale sacrifices held at major cult centres such as Old [[upsala|Uppsala]] in Sweden and Hlaðir (Lade) in Norway. These gatherings typically occurred at fixed seasonal points: mid-winter (Yule), spring (for victory), and autumn (for the harvest and the goodness of the year). The autumn blót, as depicted in the novel, was a communal [[hall-feast|feast]] (*veizla*) combined with sacrifice, drinking, and political assembly — a social institution as much as a religious one.

The contrast drawn in the novel between the blood-offering and Christian worship reflects the historical reality of the Norse conversion period (c. 800–1100 CE). For rulers like Earl Hakon, the performance of the blót was not only an act of personal faith but a declaration of political autonomy against the pressure of Christian kings and emperors who sought to impose the new religion as a tool of centralised authority.

The blood-offering at Upsala was the central ritual of Norse kingship:

> Eric the King made great blood-offering in the temple at Upsala for the goodness of the year, as was his wont and the wont of his fathers before him from time immemorial.
>
> (001-003)
