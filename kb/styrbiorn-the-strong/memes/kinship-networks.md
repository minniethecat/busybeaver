# Kinship Networks of the Saga

## Thesis

The political conflicts of *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]* are structured by four interconnected kin-[[all-organizations|groups]] — the Yngling dynasty, [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]]'s house, the Strut-Harald network, and [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]]'s Danish house — whose marriage alliances, blood feuds, and succession disputes form the lattice upon which the saga's narrative is built. Every major event in the novel can be traced to the intersection of at least two of these networks.

## Evidence
The Yngling kin traced their bloodline to the gods themselves:

> Eric the Victorious was in that time King in Upsala, the son of Biorn the Old, the son of Eric, the son of Emund, and had dominion over the Up-Swedes and over the folk of Nether-realm and Southmanland and East and West Gautland and over all countries and kingdoms eastaway from the Elf to the main sea. King Eric was as now in his old years, and was held for a man of mickle might and worship, sitting in that state and stead whereas his forefathers aforetime sat from the days of Ragnar Hairy-breeks; and in their veins was the blood of the kin of the Ynglings, even from that far time when the Gods came first from Asgarth of ancient days, and Yngvi-Frey dwelt among mankind in kingdom in Upsala.
>
> (001-001)


### The Yngling Dynasty: The Claim to Sweden

The Yngling kin trace their descent from [[yngling-kin|Yngvi-Frey]], the divine ancestor of the Swedish royal line (014-022). At the time of the novel, the dynasty is represented by two brothers: [[olaf-biornson|Olaf Biornson]], who ruled as king until his death on Olaf's Howe, and [[king-eric|Eric the Victorious]], who succeeded him (001-001). Olaf's son is [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn]] — the rightful heir by primogeniture, but denied the kingship by the bonders' vote in favour of his uncle Eric (001-039).

This core dynastic tension — uncle versus nephew, elected king versus hereditary prince — is the engine of the entire saga. Eric's own children are illegitimate ([[helgi-ericson|Helgi]], [[thorgisl|Thorgisl]], [[thorir|Thorir]]), complicating the succession and making Styrbiorn's claim both stronger in blood and weaker in law (007-015).

The Yngling network extends laterally through [[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf of West Gautland]], brother of Styrbiorn's mother [[ingibiorg|Queen Ingibiorg]], who serves as Eric's closest counsellor while remaining Styrbiorn's uncle — embodying the divided loyalties that kinship creates (007-025).

### Skogul-Tosti's House: The Marriage That Made a Queen

[[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]] was a wealthy landholder in [[west-gautland|West Gautland]] whose daughter [[queen-sigrid|Sigrid]] married [[king-eric|King Eric]] — a union that transformed a provincial noble house into the centre of Swedish royal power (008-005). Sigrid's sister [[gudrid|Gudrid]] provides a secondary connection to the landed gentry of [[arland|Arland]].

Sigrid herself is the most politically consequential figure produced by this network. Her marriage to Eric legitimises his kingship after Olaf's death; her presence as an observer-seeress — on Olaf's Howe in Chapter 1 (001-001), at the betrothal [[hall-feast|feast]] in Chapter 9 (009-001), on [[windbergsfell|Windbergsfell]] during the battle in Chapter 15 (015-035) — makes her the novel's moral witness. She is bound by marriage to the Ynglings but by blood to [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]]'s allies among the bonders, placing her at the intersection of the very [[all-organizations|factions]] that will destroy her nephew Styrbiorn.

### The Strut-Harald Network: Jomsburg's Royal Family

[[strut-harald|Strut-Harald]] was an earl in [[skaney|Skaney]] whose children became the dominant power bloc within [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]. His sons — [[sigvaldi|Sigvaldi]], [[heming|Heming]], [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell the High]] — were [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] lords, and his daughter [[tofa-strut-haraldsdottir|Tofa]] married [[sigurd-cape|Sigurd Cape]], another [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] chieftain (004-010). Their stronghold on [[borgundholm|Borgundholm]] ([[borgundholm|Bornholm]]) gave them an independent power base in the Baltic.

The Strut-Harald network represents an alternative model of power to the Yngling dynasty: not hereditary kingship sanctioned by divine descent, but earned authority within a warrior-republic. When Styrbiorn arrives at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], he must win the respect of this network on its own terms — through demonstrated prowess, not blood-right. His eventual acceptance by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] and the Strut-Harald sons is what transforms him from a lone exile into the commander of the largest military force in the North.

### Harald Bluetooth's House: The Danish Dimension

[[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]], King of Denmark, presides over the fourth kin-group. His daughter [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] becomes the object of Styrbiorn's desire — and the catalyst for the [[cowing-of-the-dane-king|confrontation at Alaburg]] that extracts one hundred Danish ships for Styrbiorn's invasion fleet (012-045). Harald's son Svein is fostered by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] (005-006), creating a pre-existing link between the Danish royal house and the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] warrior elite.

The Danish network is distinguished from the other three by its Christianisation. Harald was baptised "willy-nilly" by the Emperor Otto (005-009), and his court observes Christian ritual alongside Norse drinking customs. This religious hybridity marks the Danish house as transitional — caught between the old world of pagan kinship and the new world of Christian kingship — and partially explains why Harald is the weakest of the saga's kings, unable to resist Styrbiorn's demands.

### The Intersections: How the Networks Drive the Plot

The novel's conflicts arise precisely where these networks intersect:

- **Yngling × [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]]**: Eric's marriage to Sigrid creates the royal couple whose stability the bonders prefer over Styrbiorn's claim. The marriage is both the cause of Styrbiorn's disinheritance and the obstacle to his return.
- **Yngling × Strut-Harald**: Styrbiorn's integration into [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]'s warrior elite gives him the military capacity to challenge Eric. Without the Strut-Harald network, Styrbiorn's exile would have ended in obscurity.
- **Yngling × [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]]**: Styrbiorn's marriage to Thyri creates a dynastic link between the Swedish claimant and the Danish crown — a link Harald never wanted, and which Styrbiorn extracts by force.
- **Strut-Harald × [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]]**: The fostering of Svein by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] (005-006) creates a pre-existing channel between [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] and the Danish court that Styrbiorn exploits when he arrives at [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]].

## Conclusion

Eddison's novel is not merely a biography of Styrbiorn but a study in how kinship structures political action in the Viking world. The four kin-[[all-organizations|groups]] are not static background — they are the active framework within which every character operates, constraining choices and generating conflicts. Styrbiorn's tragedy is that he is caught between two incompatible principles of kinship: the hereditary blood-right that makes him the Yngling heir, and the elective authority that the bonders and the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] warrior code recognise. He spends the novel trying to reconcile them — and dies because he cannot.
