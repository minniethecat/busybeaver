## Definition

The war-arrow (Old Norse herör) is a token of mobilisation in Norse warfare: a wooden arrow, often fire-blackened or notched, sent from district to district to summon the free men to arms. It functions as both a practical signal and a legal instrument — those who fail to answer the summons are judged outlaws or cowards. In the novel, [[king-eric|King Eric]] deploys the war-arrow as part of his defence preparations against Styrbiorn's invading fleet, pairing it with the proclamation of an all-folk hosting (almenningr).

The war-arrow is the material symbol of the king's prerogative to call the [[all-persons|people]] to war. Unlike a written proclamation, it travels by hand from settlement to settlement, each bearer passing it on to the next, creating a chain of obligation that extends from the royal hall to the remotest farmstead. The novel's single reference — "let shear up the war-arrow, and call an all-folk hosting" (014-006) — captures both the speed and the gravity of the summons.


> "Let call a Thing straightway in Upsala. There, what griefs soever the bonders have, right them out of hand... And therewithal let shear up the war-arrow, and call an all-folk hosting. And that were good rede, too, to stick the seaways that leadeth to Upsala, so as their ships may find no fairway to come up at us through the Low."
>
> (014-006)

## Occurrences in the Novel

The war-arrow appears in Chapter XIV, "[[king-eric|King Eric]]'s Hosting," when [[king-eric|King Eric]], having learned of Styrbiorn's approach, begins to muster his defence. The narrator reports that Eric "let shear up the war-arrow, and call an all-folk hosting out of the land of the Swedes" (014-006). The verb "shear up" suggests the physical preparation of the arrow — sharpening, notching, or otherwise marking it — a detail that grounds the ritual in material practice.

The war-arrow's deployment is paired with the summoning of the [[thing-stead|Thing]], the public assembly where the levy is formally declared and organised: "he summoned a [[thing-stead|Thing]] to be holden ... at which [[thing-stead|Thing]] he was minded to make proclamation of an all-folk hosting" (014-005). The combination of war-arrow (executive summons) and [[thing-stead|Thing]] (legislative assembly) reflects the dual foundations of Norse kingship: the king commands, but he commands through law and custom, not by fiat alone. The war-arrow thus embodies the constitutional limits of royal power even as it activates the king's ultimate prerogative — the call to war.

## Historical/Mythological Context

In medieval Scandinavian law and custom, the war-arrow (herör, also called boð) was the standard method of mobilising the levy (leiðangr). The practice is attested in multiple provincial laws, including the Frostathing Law and the Gulathing Law of Norway, and in saga accounts such as Heimskringla. The arrow was typically of wood, sometimes charred, and carried by a recognised messenger (boðmaðr) from district to district. Each household that received it was obliged to pass it on to the next within a prescribed time, on pain of fine or outlawry.

The "all-folk hosting" (almenningr) that the war-arrow summons in the novel is the levy in its fullest form — every free man capable of bearing arms, as opposed to a selective levy (hálf almenningr, half-hosting) where only every other man was called. [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s choice of a full hosting underscores the existential scale of the threat: Styrbiorn's [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fleet is not a raiding party but an invasion force, and the kingdom's entire military resources must be mobilised to meet it.
