## Definition

The Einheriar (Old Norse *einherjar*, singular *einheri*, "those who fight alone" or "those who are the sole champions") are the warriors who die bravely in battle and are chosen to dwell in Odin's hall of [[valhalla|Valhall]]a. In Norse mythology, half of those slain in combat are selected by the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] -- Odin's shield-maidens -- and brought to [[valhalla|Valhall]]a, where they form the [[odin|All-Father]]'s host. Their existence is both a reward for martial valour and a strategic preparation: they train through daily combat and nightly [[hall-feast|feast]]ing, mustering over countless ages as Odin's army for the final cataclysm of Ragnarok, when they will fight alongside the gods against the forces of chaos.

In the novel, the Einheriar appear exclusively in the final chapter, "[[valhalla|Valhall]]a" (016), which depicts the aftermath of the battle of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] from a divine perspective. Styrbiorn himself is received into their company at the chapter's climax, when Odin declares him pre-eminent among the chosen dead.

## Occurrences in the Novel

The Einheriar make their sole but climactic appearance in Chapter 16, "[[valhalla|Valhall]]a." The chapter opens with Odin seated upon his high seat, his immortal eyes fixed upon the battlefield of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] (016-001). Into this celestial scene the host of slain warriors enters:

> "Now thronged the Einheriar into Valhalla, smoking from the fight, innumerable as the multitudinous clouds in a mackerel sky at eve, heroes of bliss, of many lands, chosen from many generations of men; and the voice of their talk and deep-echoing laughter was like the sounding of the sea, and they were like unto Gods in stature and seeming, and their weapons and rich apparel like to a sunset glory in a summer garden after rain." (016-003)

This passage establishes the key attributes of the Einheriar: their multitudinous number, collected across all ages and nations; their transformed, godlike stature; and their joy in the afterlife, expressed in "deep-echoing laughter like the sounding of the sea."

When the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] approach [[valhalla|Valhall]]a with the newly slain -- including Styrbiorn -- the Einheriar respond with martial acclamation:

> "Then lightnings streamed in rivers of molten steel and silver from the roof-beams of that hall, which is lofty as the tent of night, and the Einheriar clashed their weapons together and shouted with a shout that was heard above the deafening thunder: 'Hail to the choosers! the storm raisers! Hail to the shield-mays of the Lord of Spears, the Father of Ages, the Loving One! Hail to the lords of earth whom they bring to join our fellowship!'" (016-004)

The Einheriar's salute -- clashing their weapons together -- demonstrates their warriors' instinct transformed into ritual celebration. Their shout identifies the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] as "choosers" and "storm raisers," acknowledging the divine mechanism by which their fellowship is perpetually replenished.

The [[valkyries|Valkyries]] then circle "nine times ... high in air above the tables of the blest in [[valhalla|Valhall]]a" (016-006) before presenting their chosen warriors. The "tables of the blest" are the Einheriar's [[hall-feast|feast]]ing tables, and the ritual of presentation underscores that each newly arrived warrior is delivered to "those whose craft it is to mend that which is broken, and put out the arrow, and close up the wound, and wake the great soul to receive again its proper body, now forever fair, forever desirable and strong" (016-006). This passage describes the transformation that makes a slain mortal into one of the Einheriar: the healing of battle wounds and the restoration of the body to an immortal, perfected state.

The chapter culminates when the Valkyrie Skogul presents Styrbiorn before Odin. Though she does so "with some sickness of heart," lamenting that "earth goeth destitute for the need of such" (016-007), Odin's reply confirms Styrbiorn's worthiness: "Frontward are his wounds, and death availed but to tighten his grip on the sword-hilt. Be still and question not: I chose him first I loved the best" (016-008). With these words, Styrbiorn is formally inducted into the Einheriar -- not merely as one among many, but as Odin's most favoured champion.

## Historical Context

The concept of the Einheriar is attested in both the Poetic Edda and the Prose Edda, the primary sources for Norse mythology. In *Grimnismal* (The Sayings of Grimnir), Odin describes [[valhalla|Valhall]]a's five hundred and forty doors, through which eight hundred Einheriar will march abreast to face the [[earl-wolf|Wolf]] at Ragnarok. In Snorri Sturluson's *Gylfaginning*, the daily cycle of the Einheriar is elaborated: each morning they don their armour and go out into the courtyard to fight one another in practice combat; each evening their wounds are healed, and they [[hall-feast|feast]] on the flesh of the ever-renewing boar Saehrimnir, drinking mead from the udder of the goat Heidrun. This cycle of combat and [[hall-feast|feast]] is not mindless repetition but purposeful training: the Einheriar are Odin's army-in-waiting, being prepared for the eschatological battle.

The name *einherjar* has been variously interpreted. The most widely accepted etymology derives it from *ein-* ("one, alone") and *herjar* ("warriors, fighters"), possibly meaning "those who fight alone" -- a reference to their excellence as single combatants -- or "those who constitute the one army," the unified host of the dead. This duality of meaning is thematically appropriate: each Einheri is a champion in his own right, yet all are gathered into a single fellowship under Odin.

The Einheriar are not the only inhabitants of the Norse afterlife: those who die of sickness or old age go to Hel, while those who drown are taken by Ran. But the Einheriar occupy the highest tier of the warrior's imagination -- an eternity of honourable combat and fellowship in the presence of the chief god. This reflects the Viking-age valorisation of death in battle as the supreme male virtue.

The novel's depiction of the Einheriar draws directly on this Eddaic tradition. The "innumerable" host "of many lands, chosen from many generations of men" (016-003) echoes the Eddas' portrayal of Odin's ongoing recruitment across all ages of the world. The "rich apparel like to a sunset glory" (016-003) suggests the golden-shielded hall of the Eddas. And the [[valkyries|Valkyries]]' ritual of healing and restoring the slain warriors (016-006) corresponds to the Eddaic account of the Einheriar's daily regeneration.

## Related Concepts

The Einheriar are inseparably linked to several other Norse [[all-concepts|concepts]] that appear in the novel:

- **[[valhalla|Valhalla]]**: The hall of Odin, which is the dwelling-place and [[hall-feast|feast]]ing-hall of the Einheriar. The hall is described in Chapter 16 with its "roof-beams ... lofty as the tent of night" (016-004). The novel's [[valhalla|Valhall]]a chapter is the single narrative locus for the Einheriar's appearance.
- **[[valkyries|Valkyries]]**: The "choosers of the slain" who select warriors for the Einheriar and deliver them to [[valhalla|Valhall]]a. Their arrival in Chapter 16 is greeted by the Einheriar's acclamation (016-004).
- **[[odin|Odin]]**: The [[odin|All-Father]], lord of [[valhalla|Valhall]]a and ultimate commander of the Einheriar. His pronouncement in 016-008 confirms Styrbiorn's place among the chosen dead.
- **[[norse-fate|Norse Fate]]**: The Einheriar's purpose is fundamentally bound to the Norse eschatological framework: they are being mustered for Ragnarok, the fated doom of the gods.
- **[[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]]**: The battle from which the newly slain -- including Styrbiorn -- are gathered by the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] and delivered to join the Einheriar in Chapter 16.
- **[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]**: The novel's protagonist, whose death at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] results in his induction into the Einheriar as Odin's personally chosen champion.
