# Howe (Burial Mound)

## Definition

A **howe** (from Old Norse *haugr*) is a burial mound or barrow, a raised earthen structure built over the grave of a person of high status in the Norse world. In the saga, the howe serves as more than a tomb — it is a place of memory, a landmark visible across the landscape, and a locus where the boundary between the living and the dead grows thin. A howe is "like a high house in bigness" and dominates its surroundings, affirming the enduring presence of the dead among the living (001-009).

The howe is intimately bound with the cult of ancestors. When a great king or warrior dies, he is not simply buried but "laid in howe" — a phrase used throughout the saga to mark the passage of generations and signal the transition of power (001-002). The howe stands as a monument that the living visit, sit upon, and even receive visions upon, making it a bridge across time and mortality.

## Occurrences in the Novel

### King Olaf's Howe at Upsala

The most prominent howe in the saga is [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]] Biornson's howe at [[upsala|Upsala]], which appears across multiple chapters. After [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]]'s sudden death — men whispered of venom in his cup — he was laid in howe at [[upsala|Upsala]] (001-002). His son Styrbiorn makes this howe his refuge: whenever he is troubled or wishes to assert his claim, he sits atop his father's howe rather than attending the King's hall (001-003).

The howe becomes Styrbiorn's place of solitude and self-definition. He tells [[king-eric|King Eric]], "I would not come to your board today, but abode by my father's howe" — a gesture that simultaneously honors his dead father and stakes his claim to manhood (001-019). When at ease with nothing to do, Styrbiorn returns to the howe, for "him-seemed there should be no place where he should be more at ease" (001-030).

### A Witness to Fate

The howe bears witness to critical moments. It is on the howe's top that Styrbiorn wrestles with his musk-ox [[moldi|Moldi]], the struggle observed by Sigrid [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]]'s daughter, who watches "the might of Styrbiorn while he struggled with [[moldi|Moldi]] on his father's howe" (001-035). This scene at the howe — the boy and his ox, the watching girl — is the first of many threads that will bind Styrbiorn and Sigrid.

Four years later, during the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]] returns to the same howe as a vantage point. She climbs [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]]'s howe to watch the battle, "even as she had gazed four years ago when she had stood there and looked on Styrbiorn trying his strength with [[moldi|Moldi]]" (015-023). The howe thus frames the story: it is present at its beginning and its catastrophic end.

### Howes in Jomsburg

After the sea-battle at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] and his men bring their slain ashore "and laid them in howe by the seashore a little east of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]" (004-061). These howes beneath the seashore mark the Jomsviking fallen and become part of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]'s landscape, reminders of the warrior brotherhood's cost.

### Thorgnyr's Reflection

In his late years, [[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]] contemplates mortality before [[king-eric|King Eric]]: "Thou and I wax old. And when we are laid in howe the ordering of these things shall lie in other men's hands" (002-040). Here the howe symbolises the inevitable end, the point at which all worldly authority yields to [[norse-fate|the Fates]].

## Historical and Mythological Context

In historical Norse culture, burial mounds (*haugar*) were constructed for individuals of high status throughout the Viking Age and earlier. The largest, such as those at Gamla [[upsala|Uppsala]] in Sweden, were royal burial monuments. The practice of sitting on a howe — as Styrbiorn does — appears in Norse literature as a ritual act associated with claiming kingship and communing with the ancestral dead. In Ynglinga saga, a king is said to "sit on the howe" as part of asserting sovereignty.

The saga's howe at [[upsala|Upsala]] draws on the real landscape of Old [[upsala|Uppsala]], where three great royal mounds — the "Kungshögarna" — still stand visible for miles. The author, E.R. Eddison, wove this real [[all-places|geography]] into his imagined world, giving the howe a presence as vivid as any character.
