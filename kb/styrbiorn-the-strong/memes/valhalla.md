## Definition

Valhalla (Old Norse Valhöll, "hall of the slain") is the great hall of Odin in Asgard, where half of those who die in battle are taken by the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] after death. The chosen warriors, called the [[einheriar|Einheriar]], [[hall-feast|feast]] and fight by day in preparation for Ragnarok, the final battle at the end of the world. Valhalla represents the highest honour attainable by a Norse warrior: a glorious afterlife in the presence of the [[odin|All-Father]] himself.

In the novel, Valhalla serves as both a mythological reference in the Spae-Wife's Lay recited by Biorn at the Yule [[hall-feast|feast]], and as the climactic setting of the final chapter, which is titled "Valhalla" (016). The hall is depicted as a vast, storm-wreathed celestial [[hall-feast|feast]]ing-place where the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] gather, the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] deliver the slain, and Odin presides from his high seat.


> Now thronged the Einheriar into Valhalla, smoking from the fight, innumerable as the multitudinous clouds in a mackerel sky at eve, heroes of bliss, of many lands, chosen from many generations of men; and the voice of their talk and deep-echoing laughter was like the sounding of the sea.
>
> (016-003)

## Occurrences in the Novel

Valhalla is invoked at two key moments in the narrative. The first is during the Yule [[hall-feast|feast]] in Denmark (005), when Biorn recites the Spae-Wife's Lay -- a poem foretelling Ragnarok, the Twilight of the Gods. In its closing stanzas, Surt is described "from the south cometh, switch-bane in hand" (005-116), and after Biorn finishes, [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] gazes upon Styrbiorn "as if he beheld in him the very presence of Surt with flaming Sword, captaining the fiends in their onset on Valhalla" (005-122). This moment links Styrbiorn himself to the eschatological forces that will one day assail Odin's hall.

The second and most sustained treatment comes in the final chapter, "Valhalla" (016), which depicts the aftermath of the battle of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] from a divine perspective. The chapter opens with the [[odin|All-Father]] gazing upon the battlefield from his high seat "carved out of coppery-louring thunderclouds" (016-001). The [[einheriar|Einheriar]] then "thronged ... into Valhalla, smoking from the fight, innumerable as the multitudinous clouds in a mackerel sky at eve" (016-003). The [[valkyries|Valkyries]], "those Maidens of Victory," arrive on the storm-wind bearing the corpses of slain heroes: "their flying steeds swooping and balancing on the gale like seagulls in wild weather ... rode up the night into Valhalla" (016-005). They circle nine times "high in air above the tables of the blest in Valhalla" (016-006) before presenting their chosen warriors to Odin. The chapter culminates with the Valkyrie Skogul presenting Styrbiorn before the [[odin|All-Father]], who declares: "Frontward are his wounds, and death availed but to tighten his grip on the sword-hilt. Be still and question not: I chose him first I loved the best" (016-008).

## Historical/Mythological Context

In Old Norse religion, Valhalla (Valhöll) is one of the afterlife destinations for the dead, specifically reserved for warriors slain in battle. According to the Poetic Edda (Grimnismal) and the Prose Edda (Gylfaginning), the hall has five hundred and forty doors, through which eight hundred warriors can march abreast. Its roof is thatched with golden shields, and its rafters are spears. Each day the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] don their armour and fight one another, and each evening their wounds are healed and they [[hall-feast|feast]] on the flesh of the boar Saehrimnir, drinking mead that flows from the udder of the goat Heidrun.

Valhalla is fundamentally tied to Ragnarok: the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] are Odin's army, mustered over countless generations to fight alongside the gods when the forces of chaos -- Surt, Loki, the Fenris-[[earl-wolf|Wolf]], and the legions of Muspell -- make their final assault. This eschatological dimension is central to the novel's use of Valhalla: the Spae-Wife's Lay explicitly prophesies Surt's "onset on Valhalla" (005-122), and the final chapter shows the hall in its full functioning glory as the reception point for the battle-slain.
