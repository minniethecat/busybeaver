# Styrbiorn's Journey: The Arc of Exile and Return

## Thesis

Styrbiorn's geographic exile traces a ring from [[upsala|Uppsala]] eastward through [[holmgarth|Holmgarth]] and [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], then back to Sweden for the fatal battle at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] — a physical journey that mirrors his psychological transformation from exiled boy-prince to feared warlord, and ultimately to tragic hero received in [[valhalla|Valhall]]a. Eddison structures the novel as a geographic cycle: Styrbiorn is cast out from the centre of power ([[upsala|Uppsala]]), builds his strength on the periphery ([[holmgarth|Holmgarth]], [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], Denmark), and returns to challenge the centre — only to be destroyed at the moment of his greatest might.

## Evidence
Styrbiorn's journey began on the howe, where he sat unmoved against the wind:

> Styrbiorn sat on the top of the howe, unmoved as it, face to the wind. The Earl came up to him, fain to steady himself with a hand at whiles, what with the sudden squalls and the slippery grass. When he was up, "This is ill doing," he said, shouting in his ear.
>
> (001-010)


### The Expulsion: Uppsala (Chapters 1–2)

Styrbiorn's journey begins with violence at the heart of the [[swedish-kingdom|Swedish kingdom]]. At the [[thing-at-upsala|Great Assembly in Uppsala]], the bonders deny Styrbiorn the kingship that his father Olaf held before him, preferring his uncle [[king-eric|Eric]] as king (001-039). The tension erupts at [[ball-game-kysingtarn|Kysingtarn]], where Styrbiorn, in a rage after an on-field quarrel, kills [[aki|Aki of the King's Bodyguard]] with a ball-stroke to the head (001-052). Eric's judgment is exile: Styrbiorn must depart Sweden before the spring thaw, taking only "one [[long-ship|long-ship]]" and sixty men (001-056).

This expulsion sets the pattern for the entire arc: Styrbiorn's violent nature both creates his opportunities and forecloses his options. He leaves Sweden not as a prince but as an [[outlawry|outlaw]] — a status that will define his relationship to legitimate power for the rest of the novel.

### The Eastern Circuit: Holmgarth and Biarmaland (Chapters 4–5)

Styrbiorn sails eastward into the Baltic, wintering at [[holmgarth|Holmgarth]] ([[holmgarth|Novgorod]]), the great eastern trading city ruled by King Valdimar (004-002). From this base he raids north into [[biarmaland|Biarmaland]] on the White Sea coast, enriching himself and his followers with plunder (004-003). This eastern phase serves a crucial narrative function: it removes Styrbiorn from the Scandinavian political theatre while he matures from boy to man, and it provides him with the wealth and reputation necessary to enter [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] as something more than a supplicant.

### The Recruitment Ground: Jomsburg (Chapters 4, 11, 13)

[[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] is the pivot-point of Styrbiorn's journey. It is here that he finds his most loyal followers — [[biorn-broadwicker|Biorn Broadwicker]], the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] — and here that he builds the military machine that will carry him back to Sweden. His acceptance by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] and the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] brotherhood transforms him from lone exile to commander of a warrior elite (004-025).

But [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] is also where Styrbiorn's ambition first overreaches law. He brings his wife [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] into the stronghold against the explicit prohibition of [[jomsburg-law|Jomsburg law]], which forbids women within its walls (011-010). This violation — born of his insistence that his will transcends any constraint — foreshadows the hubris that will destroy him at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]].

### The Danish Detour: Roiskeld and Alaburg (Chapters 5–6, 12)

Denmark represents Styrbiorn's diplomatic phase. His winter at [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Bluetooth]]'s court in [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]] is initially cordial but turns hostile when Styrbiorn's desire for Harald's daughter Thyri clashes with Harald's plans to marry her to [[burisleif|King Burisleif]] of the Wends (006-023). The quarrel at the [[hall-feast|Yule-drinking]] (006-044) — exacerbated by the pagan [[spae-wifes-lay|Spae-Wife's Lay]] that Biorn chants at Styrbiorn's whispered request (005-050) — ends with Styrbiorn departing Denmark as an enemy.

The return to Denmark is Styrbiorn at his most formidable. He wins three sea-fights against the [[danes|Danes]] and corners Harald at [[alaburg|Alaburg]] on the Limfirth (012-032). The [[cowing-of-the-dane-king|cowing of Harald]] is Styrbiorn's greatest political victory: he extorts one hundred ships and Harald's daughter in marriage (012-045). The exiled boy has become the dominant power in the Baltic. Yet even here, Eddison signals the fragility of Styrbiorn's position — Harald capitulates with a "dead fish" handshake (012-064) and warns that Styrbiorn's strength "shall one day overreach itself" (012-063).

### The Return and the Fall: Fyrisfield (Chapters 14–15)

The invasion of Sweden is the climax of the geographic arc. Styrbiorn assembles the largest fleet seen in the North — three hundred long-ships gathered at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], augmented by Harald's hundred (013-015). He sails up the Swedish coast and lands at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], the plain before [[upsala|Uppsala]].

The [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]] (015-001) is the collapse of everything Styrbiorn built. [[king-eric|King Eric]] deploys the cunning tactic of tying together the oxen and cattle to break the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] shield-wall (015-030). Outnumbered and out-generaled, Styrbiorn's army is destroyed. The man who cowed the King of Denmark cannot overcome the King of Sweden — the centre of power he was cast out from at the novel's beginning.

### Valhalla: The Terminal Point (Chapter 16)

The final geographic movement is not horizontal but vertical. Styrbiorn dies on [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] and is received into [[valhalla|Valhalla]] by [[odin|Odin]] himself, who sends the [[valkyries|Valkyries]] to "choose him out from the press of the battle" (016-008). The exiled outlaw becomes an [[einheriar|Einheri]], one of Odin's chosen warriors. The geographic ring closes: cast out from the centre of earthly power, Styrbiorn is received into the centre of cosmic power.

## Conclusion

Styrbiorn's journey maps the [[all-places|geography]] of Norse power in Eddison's novel: the Swedish centre at [[upsala|Uppsala]], the Baltic trading network anchored by [[holmgarth|Holmgarth]], the independent warrior-republic of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], the Christianised [[danish-kingdom|Danish kingdom]], and the wild eastern frontiers of [[biarmaland|Biarmaland]] and [[kirialaland|Kirialaland]]. More than a travelogue, this arc is a structural device that organises the novel's [[all-concepts|themes]]: exile and belonging, legitimate and illegitimate authority, the relationship between violence and rule. Styrbiorn spends the entire novel trying to return to the place he was expelled from — and when he finally arrives, at the head of the greatest army the North has ever seen, the place destroys him. His true home, Eddison suggests, is not [[upsala|Uppsala]] but [[valhalla|Valhall]]a: the destination that was waiting for him from the beginning.
