# Earl Hakon of Norway

## Biography

Earl Hakon, known as Hakon the Mighty (or by his detractors as Hakon the Ill), was the ruler of Norway in the late tenth century. Though formally an earl under the Danish crown, he governed as a sovereign lord from his seat at Hladir (Lade) in Trondelag. The saga's afterword records that at the time of Styrbiorn's fall in 983, "the great Earl Hakon, called by some the Mighty but by some the Ill, ruled like a King in Norway" (017-001). His reign marked a period of resistance against the encroaching tide of Christian conversion that was sweeping across the Norse world under the pressure of the Holy Roman Empire.

Hakon's defining moment in the saga -- though he never appears directly on stage -- is recounted in a story told by [[biorn-broadwicker|Biorn the Broadwickers' Champion]] to Styrbiorn aboard [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]'s ship. When Keisar Otto and [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] of Denmark forced baptism upon him, Hakon outwardly submitted, but the moment his ships were free of Danish waters, he went ashore on an isle, performed the blood-offering to the old gods, and returned to Norway as a pagan ruler, paying no further [[scat-tribute|tribute]] to the Danish king and "counteth not a flea's worth neither christen-faith nor the [[harald-bluetooth|King of the Danes]], whose Earl he was aforetime" (005-009).

Earl Hakon's death is mentioned in passing during the rumours swirling around the [[thing-stead|Thing]] at [[upsala|Upsala]], where one of the many tales of Styrbiorn's movements claimed that he "was gotten into Norway and had slain Earl Hakon there in a pitched battle" (014-008) -- though this was merely one of the "likely and unlikely" rumours of a panicked assembly.

## Role in the Saga

Although Earl Hakon never appears in the novel's present action, his story serves a vital thematic function. His tale is told in Chapter 5, during the tense winter that Styrbiorn and his [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] spend at [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s court in [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]]. Surrounded by Christian worship -- the King's priest in his "long-coloured gown like as women use to wear" hallowing the board each night, the kirk beside the King's house where mass is said at proper seasons -- the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] feel profoundly alienated (005-009). Styrbiorn asks Biorn how the [[danes|Danes]] came to abandon the old gods, and Biorn's answer is the story of Keisar Otto's conquest, the forced baptism of [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]], and Earl Hakon's resistance.

Hakon's story provides the model for the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]]' own defiance. When Biorn concludes that Hakon renounced his forced baptism and "made blood-offering unto the Gods," Styrbiorn's response is immediate and approving: "That was well done" (005-010). The Earl of Norway becomes, within the narrative, a symbol of the possibility of resistance -- that a forced conversion can be undone, that a Norse lord can throw off both the foreign faith and the foreign king in a single act of sacrificial restoration. The story also illuminates the political dimension of Christianisation: for Hakon, the blood-offering is simultaneously a religious act and a declaration of independence from Danish overlordship.

## Key Relationships

**Keisar Otto (Otto II)** -- The Holy Roman Emperor who forced baptism on the [[danes|Danes]] and, through [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]], on Earl Hakon. Otto represents the external pressure of Christian empire that drives the religious conflict of the saga.

**[[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] of Denmark** -- Hakon's nominal overlord, who summoned him to help defend the Dane-work against Keisar Otto, but then collaborated in forcing Christianity upon him. After the blood-offering, Hakon repudiates Harald's authority entirely, paying no more scat (tribute) and counting "not a flea's worth" his suzerainty (005-009).

**[[eystein-the-fox|Eystein the Fox]]** -- A Norseman from Halogaland who fled Norway "because of Earl Hakon, for he had slain one of the Earl's men" (004-038). Eystein's exile and later service under Styrbiorn demonstrates Hakon's reach and the consequences of crossing him within Norway.

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn Olafson]]** -- Though they never meet in the saga, Styrbiorn hears Hakon's story and admires his defiance. The rumour that Styrbiorn had slain Hakon in battle (014-008) -- false though it proves -- links the two men in the imagination of the saga's world: both are figures of Norse independence against overwhelming forces.

## Historical Basis

Earl Hakon Sigurdarson (c. 937-995) is one of the most vividly documented figures of late Viking Age Norway. He appears extensively in the kings' sagas, particularly Snorri Sturluson's Heimskringla (the Saga of Olaf Tryggvason), the saga of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]], and Saxo Grammaticus's Gesta Danorum. Historically, Hakon was the de facto ruler of Norway from approximately 975 to 995, governing from Hladir as the last great pagan earl before the Christianisation of Norway under Olaf Tryggvason. His forced baptism by Otto II and [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]] at the Dane-work (Danevirke) and his subsequent apostasy are attested in multiple saga sources, though the details vary. The novel's version -- the immediate blood-offering on an isle, the renunciation of both Christian faith and Danish overlordship -- draws on the saga tradition's treatment of Hakon as the archetypal pagan holdout against Christian conversion. His death (c. 995), killed by his own thrall while hiding in a pigsty, is recorded in the sagas and lies beyond the novel's timeframe.

Earl Hakon's apostasy became the model of pagan defiance for the Jomsburgers:

> But as soon as Earl Hakon was gotten with his ships out of the land, men say that he went ashore on an isle that is there and made blood-offering unto the Gods and so went again ashipboard and so home to Hladir. And ruleth Norway from that day forth as he were very King, and never no more payeth no scat, and counteth not a flea's worth neither christen-faith nor the King of the Danes, whose Earl he was aforetime.
>
> (005-009)
