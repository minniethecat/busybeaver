# Yngling-kin (Divine Lineage)

## Definition

The Yngling-kin (*Ynglinga aett*) is the legendary divine dynasty of Swedish kings, descended from the gods themselves and ruling from the seat at [[upsala|Upsala]]. The name derives from **Yngvi-Frey** — the god Freyr, who in Norse mythology is said to have been the first of the line to dwell among mankind as a king. According to the saga's opening passage, the blood of this line flows from "that far time when the Gods came first from Asgarth of ancient days, and Yngvi-Frey dwelt among mankind in kingdom in [[upsala|Upsala]]" (001-001).

This divine ancestry is not mere decoration: it is the living foundation of royal legitimacy in the saga's world. The Yngling blood in [[king-eric|King Eric]] and Styrbiorn's veins marks them as more than warlords or chieftains — they are descendants of gods, part of a chain of authority that stretches back to the mythic dawn of the world. This concept underpins the entire political structure of the novel: the [[upsala|Upsala]] kings do not rule by conquest alone but by sacred inheritance.

The Yngling dynasty is one of the great legendary royal lines of the Germanic North. Its members are known as the *Ynglingar*, and their story is preserved in the *Ynglinga saga*, the first book of Snorri Sturluson's *Heimskringla*, which traces the descent of Norwegian kings back through the Swedish Ynglings to the gods of Asgarth.

## Occurrences in the Novel

### The Blood of the Ynglings

The concept is established in the saga's very first paragraph, which traces [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s lineage and situates him within the divine dynastic tradition:

> Eric the Victorious was in that time King in Upsala ... sitting in that state and stead whereas his forefathers aforetime sat from the days of Ragnar Hairy-breeks; and in their veins was the blood of the kin of the Ynglings, even from that far time when the Gods came first from Asgarth of ancient days, and Yngvi-Frey dwelt among mankind in kingdom in Upsala (001-001).

This single sentence compresses an entire cosmology: gods descending to earth, a divine king ruling among men, and an unbroken line of descent passing through generations of legendary kings down to Eric and, by extension, his nephew Styrbiorn.

### Kinship and Priestly Lineages

The theme of divine or sacral lineage reappears in connection with the priesthood of Frey. In [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], Biorn recounts the lineage of a priest in his Icelandic homeland:

> "Snorri Thorgrimson priest in my time; and he had the priesthood from his father before him, Thorgrim the Priest of Frey, and it was in their kin and line since the time of Thorolf Most-beard that came first of them out to Iceland" (005-032).

This passage demonstrates how the cult of Frey — and by extension the Yngling-kin's divine patron — reached from Sweden to Iceland, carried in the bloodlines of priestly families. It is a parallel strand to the royal line, showing how the sacred genealogies knit together the Norse world.

### The Twilight of the Gods

The Yngling ancestry gains its full weight only when set against the saga's cosmic backdrop. At the Yule [[hall-feast|feast]], Styrbiorn's skald sings "of Ragnarok and the Twilight of the Gods" (005-078). The end of the gods is the end of everything the Yngling blood represents — the kings of [[upsala|Upsala]] are bound to the fate of the Aesir themselves. When the Queen's women later cry out at the battle of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] "that here was the world's end come upon them and the beginning of Ragnarok" (015-045), the horror is not abstract: it is the fear that the divine order from which the Yngling kings derive their legitimacy is crumbling into ruin.

### The Upsala Seat

The seat at [[upsala|Upsala]] is repeatedly referred to as ancient, inherited — a "state and stead" occupied by successive generations of Yngling kings. The preface to the saga underscores this continuity, describing "the [[upsala|Upsala]] kings" as a power of "greater might" than the kings of Denmark (P02-002). This enduring royal centre at [[upsala|Upsala]] — where Yngvi-Frey himself once ruled — is the physical anchor of the Yngling claim to authority.

## Historical and Mythological Context

The historical Ynglings are known primarily through the *Ynglinga saga*, where Snorri Sturluson (c. 1230) traced the descent of Harald Fairhair of Norway through a line of Swedish kings back to the gods. The name "Yngling" derives from Yngvi, an older name for the god Freyr. In the mythological framework, Freyr was given lordship over the Swedes and founded the temple at [[upsala|Uppsala]] ([[upsala|Upsala]]), where the royal dynasty continued to perform sacrifices and maintain the sacred kingship.

Archaeological evidence supports the centrality of Old [[upsala|Uppsala]] as a royal and religious centre. The three great mounds at Gamla [[upsala|Uppsala]] (*Kungshögarna*) are believed to be royal burials dating to the 6th century, precisely the era when the later Yngling kings would have ruled. The temple at [[upsala|Uppsala]], described by Adam of Bremen in the 11th century, was a major cult centre where sacrifices were offered to Thor, Odin, and Freyr.

The concept of divine kingship — where the ruler's authority flows from descent from the gods — is a recurring theme across Indo-European cultures. In the Norse context, it is expressed most explicitly in the Yngling tradition. The king is not merely a political leader but a sacred figure whose blood carries the god's presence into the human world. This is the framework E.R. Eddison draws upon in *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]*, making the Yngling-kin the invisible architecture of the entire saga's political universe.
