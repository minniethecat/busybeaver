# Spae-Wife's Lay (Völuspá)

## Definition

The Spae-Wife's Lay — Völuspá in Old Norse, meaning "the prophecy of the seeress" (*völva* = seeress, *spá* = prophecy) — is the first and most celebrated poem of the Poetic Edda (the Elder Edda). It is the primary repository of Norse myth, recounting the entire span of cosmic history from the mouth of a seeress raised from the dead by Odin.

The *völva* begins at the Ginnungagap, the yawning void before creation. She proceeds through the making of the world, the fashioning of the first humans, and the strife among the Gods. The poem culminates in the cataclysm of Ragnarok, the Twilight of the Gods, in which the old world is consumed by fire and flood and a renewed one rises from the sea.

In the saga of [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]], the Spae-Wife's Lay is the most powerful poetic performance in the novel. [[biorn-broadwicker|Biorn the Broadwickers' Champion]] recites it in [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s hall to praise the old Gods and shame the Christian-tending [[danes|Danes]]. The effect is overwhelming: the company is held motionless, as if the darkness itself has grown thick with unseen presences.

The lay is described as possessing "something houseless" (005-072) — a quality that transcends mere words and reaches into the cosmic order that the poem describes. In its historical context, the Völuspá is one of the most important surviving documents of Norse religion. Composed probably in the tenth century in Iceland, it synthesises pagan cosmology with a haunting awareness of impending [[norse-fate|doom]] that may reflect the approaching Christian conversion.


> This was the beginning of the lay that Biorn the Broadwickers' Champion spake in King Harald's hall: not his own, but the old holy Spae-Wife's Lay which telleth of the beginning of all that is, foretelling also the end thereof, and the ways of the Gods with men.
>
> (005-050)

## Occurrences in the Novel

### Biorn Recites the Spae-Wife's Lay

The Spae-Wife's Lay is performed in Chapter 5, during the [[hall-feast|Yule-drinking]] in [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s hall in Denmark. The setting is charged with religious and political tension: [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] has suppressed the old blood-[[blood-offering|sacrifice]] and embraced a Christian-tending court. Styrbiorn, seated opposite the King in the lower high-seat, observes: "This is somewhat unholy, this [[hall-feast|Yule-drinking]], with neither blood-sacrifice nor praise of the Gods. Canst thou not speak somewhat in praise of the Gods, Biorn, so that they be not angry with us? and shame these [[danes|Danes]] which regard 'em not?" (005-048). Biorn agrees willingly, "the willinger, because 'tis thou that askest it" (005-049).

Biorn stands forth before the assembled warriors, thralls, and nobles. The novel sets the scene with vivid sensory detail: "Thralls had piled fuel fresh on the fires, and the tongues of fire licked upward, and the reek hung about the black roof-beams and the lofty pillars of the hall; and the faces of men were red with ale and [[hall-feast|feast]]ing and the fires' heat and glare, and the bright light of the fires sparkled back from their eyes and from their rings and collars of gold" (005-050). The narrator identifies what Biorn is about to perform in precise terms: "not his own, but the old holy Spae-Wife's Lay which telleth of the beginning of all that is, foretelling also the end thereof, and the ways of the Gods with men" (005-050).

The lay begins with the ancient formula: "Hearing I crave of all Holy Beings, / Of high and low, Heimdall's children" (005-051–052), and proceeds through the creation myth — the Ginnungagap: "In the beginning, of yore, Nothing was: / Nor sand there was nor sea nor the surges cold: / Earth was not at all, neither upper Heaven: / Only a Gap was there Gaping: of grass not any" (005-059–062). It recounts the fashioning of the world by the sons of Bor (005-063–064) and the first springing of green life when the Sun shone from the south (005-065–066).

### The Power of the Recitation

The effect of Biorn's recitation is extraordinary. The narrator describes a collective trance: "Stone still sat every man of them while Biorn spake the Spae-Wife's Lay. Only as the song went on men seemed to draw nearer together with a gradual motion not to be seen" (005-072). The lay seems to thin the boundary between the living and the unseen world: "as though there were in that song something houseless, that made them huggle together for warmth and light and right flesh and blood. And it was as if the murk and sable night huddled and stirred on the smoky confines of the firelight behind and round and above the [[hall-feast|feast]]ers; as if in the murk of it were a myriad watchers, unbeholden yet close, waiting and watching, while Biorn stood forth in the brightness and spoke his Lay" (005-072).

The recitation has different effects on the three principal figures present. [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] is disturbed and frightened: "Harald the King sat back in his high seat hunched up in his beard. With one great hairy fist before him on the table he grasped the drinking horn, stock still. His eyes were cast down for the first while; then he raised them and fixed a dark and troubled stare on Styrbiorn" (005-072). Styrbiorn, by contrast, is exalted: "his nostrils widened, as if in the surging wash and rhythm of the great Lay he heard the sea surge beneath his keel, his surf-deer, sweeping him on to where surf and cliff break together on some unimagined shore" (005-072).

### The Ragnarok Prophecy

As the lay progresses, Biorn reaches the most fearsome portion — the prophecy of Ragnarok, the Twilight of the Gods. The novel renders the poem's apocalyptic stanzas in full: "Brother shall fight with brother, and to bane be turned: / Sisters' offspring shall spill the bands of kin. / Hard 'tis with the world: of whoredom mickle: / An axe age, a sword age: shields shall be cloven; / A wind age, a wolf age, ere the world's age founder" (005-091–095). The cosmic catastrophe is summoned, with Surt coming from the south with his flaming sword and the heavens riven open, while the refrain tolls like a funeral bell: "Garm bayeth ghastful at Gnipa's cave: / The fast must be loos'd and the [[earl-wolf|Wolf]] fare free" (005-102–103).

### Aftermath: The Christian Priest's Outburst and the King's Fear

When Biorn finishes, the aftermath is immediate and dramatic: "For a minute after Biorn had ended there was dead quiet. Then that priest leapt up, proffering from his lips, higgledy-piggledy like water guggling out of a bottleneck, all manner of foul speech and blasphemy; out of which this much was apparent, that he would have the [[danes|Danes]] do some mischief to Biorn" (005-122). One of the King's men removes the priest from the hall, but the damage is done: "But the rest sat silent all, shamefaced, ill at ease, and after a little fell a-drinking again, yet with little jollity" (005-122).

The lay's deepest effect is on the King himself, whose fear of Styrbiorn is now fully crystallised: "But the King, with his great flat face corpse-livid even in the friendly firelight, gazed yet on Styrbiorn as if he beheld in him the very presence of Surt with flaming Sword, captaining the fiends in their onset on [[valhalla|Valhall]]a" (005-122). The Spae-Wife's Lay has achieved exactly what Styrbiorn intended — it has awakened in the King a primal dread of the old Gods and their champion, and in doing so it has altered the political balance between the two men. As the narrator confirms in Chapter 6, "he knew in his bones that it was the look of a man that seeth and knoweth his master, and feareth him and hateth him for his mastery and hateth his own little strength" (006-044).

The next day, [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] confronts Biorn: "Why didst thou speak that bad song before the King yester-night? ... the King my father was put in a mighty taking and hath slept not a wink the whole night through" (006-001, 006-003). Biorn's reply captures the ancient Norse understanding of the spoken word's power: "'Yet 'tis oftener that which goeth in at the mouth hath suchlike force, not songs, which goeth in at the ear'" (006-004).

## Historical Context

The Völuspá is preserved in two medieval Icelandic manuscripts: the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270) and the Hauksbók (AM 544 4to, early 14th century). Between them the two versions run to approximately 66 stanzas in the *fornyrðislag* ("old story metre"), the characteristic alliterative measure of Eddic poetry. The poem is spoken through the mouth of a *völva* — a wandering seeress, one of the most mysterious figures in Norse religious practice — who addresses her prophecy to both the human assembly ("Hearing I crave of all Holy Beings") and to Odin himself ("Wilt thou, Father of the Slain, that forth I tell thee").

The Völuspá's vision is cyclopean in scale. It opens with the void (Ginnungagap) and the primordial giant Ymir; proceeds through the creation of the world, the establishment of the heavenly bodies, the golden age of the Gods, and their first strife; descends into the present age of war, oath-breaking, and moral decay; and culminates in Ragnarok — the doom of the Gods — in which Fenrir the [[earl-wolf|Wolf]] breaks his bonds, the World-Serpent rises from the sea, and the fire-giant Surt advances from the south with his flaming sword. The sun turns black, the stars fall, and the earth sinks into the sea. Yet the poem does not end in total annihilation: a new, green world rises from the waves, and a new generation of Gods inherits the golden chess-pieces of their predecessors.

The saga's note at the end of the volume states: "The verses are translations. Those in Chapters V and IX are rendered nearly line for line and word for word, and in an alliterative measure somewhat resembling the original, out of the Elder Edda" (017-003). This confirms that Eddison was working directly from the Old Norse text of the Völuspá and the Helreið Brynhildar (the Hell-ride of Brynhild, recited in Chapter 9), and took pains to reproduce not only the content but the formal poetic qualities of the original.

In the context of the novel, the choice of the Völuspá is thematically rich. The poem's vision of cosmic doom — "A wind age, a wolf age, ere the world's age founder" — mirrors Styrbiorn's own trajectory as a doomed hero, a champion of the old order whose fate is to be consumed by the forces he unleashes. The lay also functions as a symbol of the pagan worldview in its last, most potent flowering, set against the grey encroachment of Christianity represented by the blaspheming priest and the uneasy, half-converted King.
