# Arland

## Description

Arland is the estate of [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]], a mighty nobleman of Sweden, situated in the south of the country. The hall at Arland was a place of wealth and hospitality, large enough to receive the King himself when he travelled from [[upsala|Upsala]] with his company for a [[hall-feast|feast]] lasting nine nights (003-001). The house stood as the seat of one of the realm's great landed men, whose daughter Sigrid would become Queen of Sweden.

The estate is described indirectly through the [[all-events|events]] that unfold within its walls. The hall was large enough to accommodate a royal retinue, with an upper bench and lower bench in the traditional Norse fashion, and bedchambers private enough for [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]] and his wife [[gudrid|Gudrid]] to discuss the King's intentions in whispers (003-009 to 003-011). The household bustled with preparations when the King's coming was announced: [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]] "let make ready his house and his household to receive the King with all worship" (003-002).

> King Eric came south to Arland to guest with Skogul-Tosti, the father of that Sigrid who was with Styrbiorn on King Olaf's howe, and saw visions there as is aforesaid. Tosti was a great friend of the King's, and made him a nine-night feast with a great company.
>
> (003-001)
## Role in the Story

Arland serves as the stage for one of the novel's pivotal turning points: the King's courtship of Sigrid. [[king-eric|Eric the Victorious]] rode south to guest with [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]] for nine nights, and on the ninth night of the [[hall-feast|feast]] asked Sigrid, [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]]'s daughter, to bear him the drinking horn — a gesture heavy with implication (003-003 to 003-005). It was at Arland that the King took [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]] aside to speak of his daughter's future, a conversation that would reshape the emotional and political landscape of the entire saga (003-025).

The estate is also the place where [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]] and [[gudrid|Gudrid]]'s marriage comes into clearest view. Their bedroom conversation after the [[hall-feast|feast]] — [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]] teasing his wife while removing his shoes, confident that the King intended Sigrid for Styrbiorn, and [[gudrid|Gudrid]] more cautious in her reading of [[all-events|events]] — provides one of the rare glimpses of domestic warmth and private affection in the novel (003-009 to 003-024).

Arland's significance extends beyond the marriage plot. When the war-arrow was sent out across Sweden summoning all men to the King's hosting against Styrbiorn's invasion, it was from Arland that [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]] rode two days and a night without sleep to be "earliest come of all the great men" to [[upsala|Upsala]], proving that the loyalty rooted in the bond formed at that nine-night [[hall-feast|feast]] was absolute (014-007).

## Associated Characters

**[[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]]** — The master of Arland, a mighty nobleman whose seat was a place of honour and hospitality (003-001).

**[[gudrid|Gudrid]]** — [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]]'s wife and mistress of the hall at Arland, a shrewd woman who sized up the King's intentions more accurately than her husband (003-011).

**[[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]] the Haughty** — [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]]'s daughter, who at Arland was called upon to bear the King the drinking horn, the moment that set her on the path to becoming Queen of Sweden (003-003 to 003-005).

**[[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious** — Came to Arland as [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]]'s guest and left having chosen his future Queen (003-001, 003-025).

## Historical Context

Arland in the novel corresponds to the estate of [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]] in Norse saga tradition. The historical [[skogul-tosti|Tosti]]s (or Skoglar-[[skogul-tosti|Tosti]]) was a Swedish nobleman of the 10th century, mentioned in *Heimskringla* as the father of [[queen-sigrid|Sigrid the Haughty]]. The location of his estate is not precisely known, though saga sources place it in the south of Sweden, consistent with the novel's [[all-places|geography]] that has the King travelling south from [[upsala|Upsala]] to reach it. The nine-night [[hall-feast|feast]] with its drinking ceremonies and political negotiations reflects authentic Norse customs of hospitality and alliance-building among the nobility.
