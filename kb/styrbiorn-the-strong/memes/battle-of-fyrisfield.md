# Battle of Fyrisfield

## Summary

The Battle of Fyrisfield was the climactic engagement of the saga, fought over three days on the water-meadows south of [[upsala|Upsala]] between the Jomsviking host of [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]] and the army of his uncle, [[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious. It was, by the novel's account, the greatest battle ever seen in the Northlands: "so great were the armies of Styrbiorn and of the King that no man that was there had ever seen so great a war-gathering in all the Northlands" (015-010).

The battle opened with a tactic devised by [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]]: the King's army drove baggage animals — horses and oxen with fire set to their tails — into the Jomsviking ranks, causing chaos and forcing Styrbiorn's men to waste their strength on "butchers' work and the hewing down of naked thralls" while the King's forces waited fresh (015-014). Despite this, the men of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fought through the day without blenching. By nightfall on the first day, neither side could claim victory (015-019).

The second day saw the King's army steadily worn down. [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]] arrived with fresh levies from [[helsingland|Helsingland]] around midnight, giving Eric hope (015-020). But by the end of the second day's fighting, Styrbiorn had gained the advantage: "had there been but another hour of daylight it was like enough there had been an end, and the King's whole army broken and done away" (015-033). That night, Styrbiorn prayed to Thor for victory, his voice carrying from wing to wing of the camp — and men heard it in the King's camp and even in [[upsala|Upsala]] (015-039). [[king-eric|King Eric]], sleepless, went to the temple at [[upsala|Upsala]] and swore an [[oath]] to Odin: if he were granted victory, he would give himself to the [[odin|All-Father]] after ten years (015-041).

On the third day, the King's army was beaten back step by step, the battle pressing up the narrowing valley between [[windbergsfell|Windbergsfell]] and the [[fyriswater|Fyriswater]] until those watching from Olaf's [[howe]] could see the raven-wings of Styrbiorn's helm riding the battle like an eagle on the storm (015-024). Yet the King's host held together, and in the final hours, the tide turned. Eric won the victory. Styrbiorn fell. The King returned to [[upsala|Upsala]] in the dark night and was met by the Queen — a victor walking among the ghosts of the fallen (015-067).

Before the armies clashed, Biorn Broadwicker delivered Styrbiorn's battle-speech to the host — a moment that fused leader and followers into a single instrument of war:

> There was not a man in that army that lost a word of this, hearing indeed the voice of Biorn but gazing the while on their lord out of whose shadow he spoke: on the great stature of Styrbiorn towering above them, and on the great raven-wings shadowing like death. As Biorn ended, Styrbiorn reared his head yet higher and shouted in a great voice. "Your watchword for egging on one another to battle: Forth, forth, Styrbiorn's men!"
>
> (015-013)


## Context

The battle was the inevitable culmination of the conflict seeded in the novel's opening chapters. Styrbiorn, denied his inheritance of half the kingdom by his uncle, had sought power through the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] brotherhood. Returning to Sweden with a fleet and host, he refused Thorgnyr's offer of peace on the eve of battle (015-008). Eric's crowning of his infant son Olaf as joint king — an act of dynastic consolidation — had made reconciliation impossible (013-007). The three-day battle on [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] was the resolution of a struggle that had been brewing since Styrbiorn first sat on his father's howe and demanded his birthright.

## Key Participants

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — Commanded the Jomsviking host. Fought with superhuman ferocity, slaying Earl Aunund, Kalf of Kalmar, and Karl Heriolfson. Faced An the Black and his five [[berserk-gang|berserk]] brothers alone and slew them all (015-016, 015-018). Refused peace and prayed to Thor for victory (015-008, 015-039).

**[[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious** — Commanded the Swedish army. Sought Odin's favour in the temple at night, swearing to give himself to the god after ten years (015-041). Won the victory but at terrible cost.

**[[bui-digri|Bui the Stout]]** — Led the right wing of Styrbiorn's host with his brother [[sigurd-cape|Sigurd Cape]] and the Wendish levies (015-011).

**[[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]]** — Devised the tactic of the baggage animals (015-014). Watched the battle from Olaf's howe, seeing her visions from four years before come to pass (015-023).

**[[helgi-ericson|Helgi]]** — Captain of the King's bodyguard, who wounded Styrbiorn in the thigh and was himself mortally wounded, dying in the Queen's arms (015-032, 015-033).

**[[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf]]** — Brought Thorgnyr's peace offer to Styrbiorn before the battle. Commanded the Swedes who had taken Styrbiorn's side (015-007, 015-011).

## Consequences

The most immediate consequence was the death of [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]] and the destruction of the Jomsviking host as a political force in the North. Eric retained his kingdom, earning the by-name "the Victorious" from this triumph. The [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] brotherhood, deprived of its greatest champion, would never again pose the same threat to the established kingdoms.

Yet the novel's final chapter, "[[valhalla|Valhall]]a," suggests a different kind of consequence: Styrbiorn's entry into Odin's hall, received as a hero. The battle was a military defeat for the novel's protagonist, but in the saga's moral universe — the world of the old gods, of Thor and Odin, of the heroic code — it was also a kind of victory. Eric won the kingdom; Styrbiorn won [[valhalla|Valhall]]a.

## Historical Basis

The Battle of [[fyriswater|Fyris]]vellir ([[fyrisfield|Fyrisvall]]arna) is attested in several Norse sources, including *Heimskringla* (specifically the Saga of Olaf Tryggvason), the *Styrbjarnar thattr Sviakappa* (the Tale of Styrbiorn the Swedish Champion), and Saxo Grammaticus's *Gesta Danorum*. The battle is traditionally dated to around 984-986 CE. According to these sources, Styrbiorn, nephew of [[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious, led a fleet of [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] against Sweden. Eric won the battle through a combination of tactical ingenuity — including the use of cattle against the Jomsviking shield-wall — and sacrifices to Odin. The novel follows this tradition closely, while expanding the battle from a single engagement into a three-day epic.
