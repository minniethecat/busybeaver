## Definition

The long-ship (Old Norse *langskip*) was the most advanced warship of the Viking Age: a long, narrow, clinker-built wooden vessel with a shallow draft, propelled by both a square sail and oars. Its defining features included a symmetrical stem and stern (allowing it to reverse direction without turning), a single mast, and often a carved dragon-head prow that gave the largest vessels their name — *dreki*, the dragon-ship. In the world of the novel, the long-ship is the indispensable instrument of power: the means by which Styrbiorn wins his fleet, the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] enforce their law upon the sea, and armies are transported to the final battle on the [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]].

## Occurrences in the Novel

### Jomsburg Harbour

The [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] stronghold is built around its capacity to shelter long-ships on a massive scale. [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] has constructed a sea-gate in the harbour-mouth, "like to the gate of a walled town, so as a three hundred long-ships might enter and ride in the harbour within the sea-gate" (004-001). The harbour is the heart of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]'s power, enabling the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] to equip their ships and "so fare forth again on the Viking path" (004-006). The sheer volume of vessels gathered for Styrbiorn's campaign is later likened to leaves covering a pool: "until [[jomsburg|the burg]] was filled with men and the harbour thick with long-ships, like a stagnant pool under sallows in the season of the year when the leaves do fall, and the leaves of the sallows lie so many on the face of the pool that hard it is to tell whether it be water there or firm land hid with the leaves" (008-008).

### Styrbiorn's Fleet

The long-ship is inseparable from Styrbiorn's bid for power. When he first petitions [[king-eric|King Eric]], he asks modestly for "twenty ships" with which to go harrying (002-057). The King, seeing the princely ambition in him, grants "not twenty but sixty ships shalt thou have, and a full tale of men thereto" (002-059), sending Styrbiorn abroad for three winters to learn "the trade of kings." Later, when Styrbiorn challenges [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] for entry into the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] Lay, he proposes single ship-combat: "let them row out a long-ship against his with an even number of men" (004-032), and the ensuing ship-to-ship duel proves his worth. By the time of the great invasion of Sweden, Styrbiorn's fleet "needed wide sea-room to sail, as with foaming wake they stood out from the land, and it was long between the first ship and the last, and between that which steered outermost and that which steered nearest to landward" (013-043).

### The Sailing of the Fleet

Chapter 13, titled "The Sailing of the Fleet," is the novel's central naval set-piece. The fleet departs [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] with [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] watching from the seawall: "Thyri stood there looking him farewell from the seawall of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] as the fleet steered west, and she was as a flower that is seen afar lonely on the sheer rock face of some wind-grieved mountain" (013-033). The navigation of the fleet through the Limfirth and into the open sea — "row[ing] out from the haven and down the Limfirth, and so set sail and hove out into the open sea" (013-043) — charts the course for war. [[king-eric|King Eric]], on the eve of battle, rides down to River-oyce "to see for sure that Styrbiorn's fleet had not sailed up into the firth despite the sticking of the channel at [[sigtun|Sigtun]]" (014-050), demonstrating how the movement of the fleet is the strategic centre of gravity for both sides.

### Ships as Metaphor

Throughout the saga, the ship serves as a figure of speech for human beauty, power, and fate. Styrbiorn's mantle in the wind "puffed and flapped like a ship's sail in a storm when the rudder is broken" (001-011). Thyri's carriage is described as "like the dragonhead of a ship of war treading turbulent seas" (001-034), and [[queen-sigrid|Sigrid the Haughty]] rising to serve mead is compared to "a ship's mast, half laid over by a gust of wind, rises erect from the trough of the wave" (003-006). Sigrid herself deploys ship imagery as a proverb: "So far, good. But the ship's not beached yet" (003-010), meaning the matter is not concluded. Biorn's Spae-Wife's Lay even conjures the sensory experience of "the sea surge beneath his keel, his surf-deer, sweeping him on" (005-072), casting the ship as a living creature of fate.
