## Definition

Fate, known in Old English as *wyrd* and in Old Norse as *urðr*, is the central organising principle of Norse cosmology and narrative. It is the belief that the course of every life — and of the cosmos itself — is predetermined, woven by the three Norns (Urðr, Verðandi, and Skuld) who sit at the Well of Urðr beneath the world-tree Yggdrasill.

The word *wyrd* (cognate with Modern English *weird*) originally meant "that which has become" or "that which has turned out," carrying the sense of past [[all-events|events]] shaping the present and future as an unalterable chain. In the Norse worldview, fate is not a blind mechanical force but a pattern woven by conscious beings — the Norns, who "shaped the lives of men" and whose decrees could not be escaped, only met with courage.

This fatalism is the philosophical backbone of the saga, giving shape to the narrative's tragic arc from Styrbiorn's first defiance on his father's howe to his final stand at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]].

## Occurrences in the Novel

The concept of fate pervades the entire narrative, appearing in speech, action, and supernatural vision.

The lawman Thorgnyr delivers the novel's most explicit statement of Norse fatalism in his counsel to [[king-eric|King Eric]]. Speaking of the inevitable conflict with Styrbiorn, the King muses that perhaps a wise man would "let alone: what must be must be" — and Thorgnyr offers him a governing philosophy: "There is but one way for a man, and that is to remember that none may avoid his fate. This is to a man as the due ballast to the ship, which maketh the vessel indeed loom somewhat deeper, but keepeth it from tossing too lightly upon the uncertain waters" (006-040). Fate, in this view, is not a curse but a stabilising weight.

The Fates themselves are invoked at key moments. Thorgnyr tells [[king-eric|King Eric]] that after they are laid in their howes, others "will order them as the Fates shall ordain" (002-040). Later, when Styrbiorn stands alone with [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]] in her chamber, the narrator invokes "those shadowy-visaged and iron-handed Fates that lay men at their length" (010-009), and Styrbiorn's movement towards her is likened to "some motion of those grey Fates, or of things drawn by them blindly" (010-010). The unseen powers "which weave the web of destiny" (010-012) are said to throw the shuttle yet again after the consummation — the narrator rendering the affair itself as a thread in a cosmic loom.

Styrbiorn's character embodies the Norse ideal of meeting fate with defiance. When Strut-Harald bids him farewell, the Earl declares that "unto few men is it fated to be great, and of fair fame, and long-lived" and prophesies Styrbiorn shall have the first two but a short life (008-004). On the eve of battle, Styrbiorn proclaims before his army: "if it be my fate to overlive this battle, then will it lie in my hand to do you good" (015-012) — accepting his fate while resolving to shape what lies within his power. [[king-eric|King Eric]], on the other hand, faces the coming conflict as "a man that hath put behind him both good and ill, and goeth in some deep resolve unto his fate" (014-006).

The supernatural dimension of fate appears in Sigrid's vision. Standing on Olaf's Howe, she sees "[[fetch-fylgja|fetches]]" (Old Norse *fylgja*, fate-spirits attached to each person) and beholds Styrbiorn "as if read and battle-slain" (015-023) — a foreknowledge of his doom that she had glimpsed years earlier. Biorn, too, warns that Styrbiorn is "fey" (011-020), the Norse term for one marked by fate for imminent death. Sigvaldi likewise says men would call Styrbiorn "fey" to bear raven's wings on his helm, "for well I think they shall bring us ill luck" (013-026).

The Spae-Wife's Lay, performed by Biorn at Yule in Denmark, is itself an extended prophecy of cosmic fate — reaching from the creation of the nine worlds to Ragnarok, the doom of the gods, and the [[earl-wolf|wolf]] who "feedeth on the lives of fey men death-doom'd" (005-074).

## Historical/Mythological Context

In historical Norse belief, the Norns (*nornir*) were three female beings who ruled the destiny of gods and men. Snorri Sturluson's *Prose Edda* names them Urðr ("What Once Was," i.e., Fate), Verðandi ("What Is Becoming"), and Skuld ("What Shall Be"), and [[all-places|places]] them at the Well of Urðr (Urðarbrunnr) beneath Yggdrasill. They are depicted as spinning or weaving the threads of fate — an image that appears in the novel as the "web of destiny" (010-012). In some sources, the Norns are multiple and diverse, with some being of the Aesir, some of the elves, and some of the dwarves.

The Anglo-Saxon concept of *wyrd* is closely related. In Old English poetry such as *The Wanderer* and *Beowulf*, *wyrd* is a pervasive force governing the rise and fall of men and kingdoms. The famous maxim *"Wyrd bið ful aræd"* ("Fate is fully determined") captures the same fatalistic spirit that animates [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]. The Norse and Old English traditions share the notion that a man's proper response to fate is not to resist or complain but to meet it with steadfast courage — what the Norse called *drengskapr*, the quality of a man who does what must be done regardless of outcome.

The concept of the *fylgja* (plural *fylgjur*), translated as "fetch" in the novel, is a supernatural attendant spirit tied to an individual's fate. The *fylgja* often appears in dreams or visions as a woman or animal, and seeing one's own *fylgja* is a portent of death. Sigrid's vision of Styrbiorn's "fetches" (015-023) draws on this tradition, marking her as one who can perceive the workings of fate.
