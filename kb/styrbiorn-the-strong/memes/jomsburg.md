# Jomsburg

## Description

Jomsburg was the fortress-home of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]], built by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki of Fion]] on the coast of [[wendland|Wendland]]. The sagas describe it as a place of almost mythical impregnability: "a marvellous sure place had he made it, builded with stone walls about and about" (004-001). At the harbour-mouth, [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] had constructed a sea-gate like the gate of a walled town, so massive that "a three hundred long-ships might enter and ride in the harbour within the sea-gate" (004-001).

The burg itself contained everything a warrior brotherhood required for self-sufficiency and power. There was "good houseroom for every man of them that was in the [[jomsburg-law|Lay of the Jomsburgers]], and treasure chambers enow to keep safe the good treasure they brought home thither from their harrying, and store chambers filled with corn and meal and kippered fish and all manner of victual and stores" (004-001). A well of fresh water flowed perpetually within the walls. Both landward and seaward, Jomsburg was "a place not to be fought against, girt about with thick high walls inexpugnable and stone cliffs washed with the sea" (004-001).

The seawall that ringed the harbour was a place of assembly, where [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] walked at dawn and where the lords of Jomsburg gathered to hold their councils (004-006, 012-002). On evenings, men walked there in the light of the westering sun to talk of war and purpose (012-004). Below the wall, the sea churned endlessly: in calm weather there were "shags fishing and diving and a whirl of white sea-mews screaming and gaffling in the air above them" (004-006); in heavy seas, the waves flung themselves against the wall with thunderous violence (011-003).

East of the burg, by the seashore, the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] buried their dead in howes on the coast, laying to rest those who had fallen in battle — a shoreline graveyard for the warrior dead (004-061).

The Jomsburgers' way of life, as described in the saga, was one of disciplined peace within the burg and fierce enterprise beyond its walls:

> So they sat in the burg in good peace, and kept well their laws. They fared abroad every summer a-harrying in this land and in that and gat them good fee and good renown. And they were held for the greatest champions and men of war, and scarcely was any man thought their match in that time.
>
> (004-005)


## Role in the Story

Jomsburg is the middle world of the saga, the crucible in which Styrbiorn is transformed from an exiled prince into a warrior-king. His arrival at Jomsburg's seawall, the sea-battle that follows, and his single combat with [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] all take place within the shadow of its walls. It is here that he finds, as he told [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] on that first day, "friendship" — and more than friendship, the brotherhood of warriors whose allegiance would shape the [[norse-fate|destiny]] of the North (004-017).

The burg is also the theatre of the saga's most charged political moments: the great council of Chapter 12 where [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] pledged unconditional aid, Sigvaldi whispered his objections, and Bui demanded war (012-014, 012-019); the stormy night of Chapter 11 when Styrbiorn, returned from Sweden in disgrace, stood on the seawall watching the surges and spoke of his betrayal (011-001, 011-012); and the unprecedented moment in Chapter 13 when Styrbiorn brought [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] into the burg against the ancient law forbidding women within Jomsburg's walls — an act so extraordinary that [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]], the law's enforcer, broke his own rule for the first and only time (013-019, 013-023).

The burg's walls are the boundary between the known world of Danish and Swedish politics and the untamed life of the viking. Within them, the Jomsburg Law holds absolute sway, enforced by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] alone; beyond them lies the sea, the summer harrying, and the freedom of the warrior's life.

## Associated Characters

**[[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]** — The founder and captain of Jomsburg, who built its walls, installed its sea-gate, and enforced its laws with absolute authority (004-001, 004-003).

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — Came to Jomsburg seeking admission, fought [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] in single combat on its waters, and rose to be called captain in [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s absence (004-017, 005-001).

**[[bui-digri|Bui the Stout]]** — One of the lords of Jomsburg who taunted Styrbiorn from the seawall and later demanded to burn [[upsala|Upsala]] (004-016, 012-019).

**[[sigvaldi|Sigvaldi Jarl]]** — A Jomsburg lord, son of [[strut-harald|Earl Strut-Harald]], who counselled caution at the council on the seawall (012-014).

**Biorn Broadwicker** — Styrbiorn's foster-brother who walked the seawall with him and swore never to leave him (011-007, 011-022).

## Historical Context

Jomsburg (Old Norse: Jomsborg) is a semi-legendary Viking stronghold traditionally located on the southern shore of the Baltic Sea, on the coast of [[wendland|Wendland]] (modern-day Poland/Germany). The stronghold appears prominently in the *Jomsvikinga saga*, which describes it as a fortified settlement with a harbour capable of holding a great fleet. The exact location of Jomsburg has never been archaeologically confirmed, though several sites — including Wolin in Poland — have been proposed. The novel follows saga tradition in making Jomsburg an almost mythic place, a fortress whose impregnability and strict warrior code define the men who dwell within it.
