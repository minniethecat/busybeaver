# Fyriswater

## Description

The Fyriswater is the river that flows southward from below the city of [[upsala|Upsala]], winding through the flat water-meadows that stretch toward [[sigtun|Sigtun]] and the sea. From the summit of [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]]'s howe, the river could be seen "winding southward from beneath [[upsala|Upsala]] through the flats" (001-031). It was this river that gave its name to [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], the stretch of meadowland along its banks — and, after the battle, to [[fyrisfield|Fyrisvall]], the field of the Fyris.

The river forms a natural boundary and orientation point in the landscape around [[upsala|Upsala]]. On the western side of its course rise the far mountains of Upland; on the eastern side, the scree-slopes of [[windbergsfell|Windbergsfell]]. The river defines the [[all-places|geography]] of the saga's climax, as the battle on [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] was fought in the ings — the low-lying water-meadows — of the Fyriswater (015-023). When the fighting pressed northward up the valley between the river and [[windbergsfell|Windbergsfell]], the Queen on Olaf's howe could trace the battle's progress by watching the valley floor between the river and the fell (015-023).

## Role in the Story

The Fyriswater is not a character in the saga, but it is the silent witness to its greatest [[all-events|events]]. The river's ings form the field where Styrbiorn and Eric fought their three-day battle, and its name became synonymous with the conflict itself. The field beside the river became [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]]; the legendary battle fought there became known as the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisvall]] (015-001).

The river also figures in Sigrid's second sight. In the opening chapters, standing on the howe, Sigrid gazes south over the Fyriswater and its water-meadows and sees something there that others cannot — a vision that prefigures the field's bloody [[norse-fate|destiny]] (003-058). The Fyriswater thus connects the novel's beginning with its end, flowing through the landscape and through the story from the first pages to the last.

On the third day of the battle, the fighting pressed into the higher valley "betwixt the fell and the river," and the Queen could see the black raven-wings of Styrbiorn's helm moving where the battle ebbed and flowed along the ings of Fyriswater (015-023). The river defined the battlefield — and the battlefield defined the saga.

## Associated Characters

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — Fought his final battle on the fields beside the Fyriswater (015-010).

**[[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious** — Defended his kingdom along the Fyriswater's meadows (015-010).

**[[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]]** — Watched the battle on the Fyriswater's ings from Olaf's howe, as she had once gazed south over the same river in vision (003-058, 015-023).

## Historical Context

The river Fyris (Fyrisan in modern Swedish) flows through [[upsala|Uppsala]], and the plains beside it — the [[fyrisfield|Fyrisvall]]arna — are the traditional site of the semi-legendary Battle of Fyrisvellir described in Norse sources. The modern district of Fyrislund in [[upsala|Uppsala]] preserves the name in local [[all-places|geography]]. The river itself was a major feature of the Old [[upsala|Uppsala]] landscape, winding through the fertile plains below the royal seat and temple complex.

The view from King Olaf's Howe, where the Fyriswater defines the landscape:

> Southaway from beneath their feet wound Fyriswater amid the flats below Upsala, and there lay spread the far-stretching low country toward Sigtun and the sea, and westward the far mountains of Upland shadowy-gray in the light of morning.
>
> (001-031)
