# Yule Feast in Denmark

## Summary

The Yule [[hall-feast|feast]] at [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s hall in [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]] was a great winter gathering where Styrbiorn and his [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] company came as guests during the third winter of Styrbiorn's exile. [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]], "a man of the greatest largesse," welcomed them with good fare and strong drink (005-007). The [[hall-feast|feast]] became a stage for the collision of the old Norse world of the gods and the new Christian world that Harald had been forced to adopt.

The centrepiece of the [[hall-feast|feast]] was Biorn Broadwicker's recital of the Spae-Wife's Lay ([[spae-wifes-lay|Voluspa]]), the great Norse poem of creation and [[norse-fate|doom]]. Biorn performed it at Styrbiorn's request, who felt the [[hall-feast|Yule-drinking]] was "somewhat unholy" without "blood-[[blood-offering|sacrifice]] nor praise of the Gods" and wished to "shame these [[danes|Danes]] which regard 'em not" (005-048). The recital held the hall in spellbound silence. When it ended, the Christian priest leapt up spewing blasphemy, and the King sat "with his great flat face corpse-livid even in the friendly firelight, gazing on Styrbiorn as if he beheld in him the very presence of Surt with flaming Sword" (005-122).

The [[hall-feast|feast]] marked the turning point in the relationship between Styrbiorn and [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]. What had begun as hopeful friendship had already grown strained over Harald's attempts to convert Styrbiorn and make him his man. After the recital, the breach widened beyond repair.


> Many would have had Styrbiorn go home with them and spend Yuletide as their guest, in so much that there was like to be very mischief amongst them out of argument which of them should have him. Until in the end, Biorn said in his ear, "There is good guesting, Styrbiorn, with King Harald Gormson in Denmark."
>
> (005-005)

## Context

Styrbiorn and Biorn had come to Denmark at Biorn's suggestion, who had guested with Harald before and knew the King had a marriageable daughter (005-005). The [[hall-feast|feast]] occurred against the backdrop of Harald's forced conversion by Emperor Otto — Biorn had recounted how "the Keisar won the Dane-work, and let do christen the King willy-nilly, him and all his men" (005-009). [[earl-hakon|Earl Hakon of Norway]] had accepted Christian baptism under duress but reverted to the old gods the moment he was out of the Emperor's reach — an example that clearly influenced Styrbiorn's attitude toward Harald's faith (005-009).

Styrbiorn had already made himself unpopular with the King through his jests about replacing Harald with another king and his refusal to take baptism or become Harald's earl (005-011). The Yule [[hall-feast|feast]] brought these tensions to a head in a single, unforgettable night.

## Key Participants

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — The [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] guest who challenged the Christian court by having the old heathen lay recited. His growing bond with [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] began during this winter (005-013).

**Biorn Broadwicker** — Recited the Spae-Wife's Lay in [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]'s hall, a performance of such power that it silenced the entire company and terrified the King (005-050, 005-072).

**[[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] (Bluetooth)** — The Dane-King whose forced Christianity sat uneasily with his pagan past. The [[hall-feast|feast]] exposed the fragility of his conversion (005-008, 005-122).

**Thyri** — [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]'s daughter, fourteen winters old, who began her quiet but significant conversations with Styrbiorn during this winter (005-012, 005-013).

## Consequences

The Yule [[hall-feast|feast]] crystallised the hostility between Styrbiorn and [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]. Though Harald made no immediate move against his guests, the Spae-Wife's Lay had shown him the gulf between his court and Styrbiorn's world. The [[hall-feast|feast]] also brought Styrbiorn and Thyri into closer contact, laying the foundation for their later betrothal. And the recital itself stands as one of the most memorable moments in the novel — a pagan prophecy of Ragnarok spoken in a Christian king's hall, the old world rising up to challenge the new.

## Historical Basis

The Yule (Jol) [[hall-feast|feast]] was the most important celebration in the Norse calendar, marking the winter solstice. It involved ritual drinking (the "[[hall-feast|Yule-drinking]]" or *drekka jol*) dedicated to the gods, particularly Odin and Freyr. In the novel, the [[hall-feast|feast]] at Harald's hall represents the tension of the conversion period, when Christian kings continued to hold the traditional Yule gatherings but reshaped them to exclude the old rituals of sacrifice and god-praise. [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]]'s historical conversion around 965 CE is well-attested, including on the famous Jelling Stone, which declares that Harald "made the [[danes|Danes]] Christian."
