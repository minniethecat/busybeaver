# King Eric's Bodyguard

**King Eric's Bodyguard** is the personal retinue of household warriors serving [[king-eric|King Eric the Victorious]] of Sweden. Distinct from the broader Swedish army (*ledung*), the bodyguard represents the King's inner circle of trusted fighting men — his house-carles (*húskarlar*) who attend him in peace and form his shield-wall in war.

## Description

The King's bodyguard appears throughout the novel as the instrument and symbol of royal authority. Its members ride with the King on progress through the kingdom (002-002), attend him in his hall at [[upsala|Upsala]], and serve as his personal battle-guard in war. The narrator describes them as "men of his bodyguard" who are "arrayed" around the King on the battlefield (015-011).

The bodyguard's composition reflects the political realities of the Swedish court. Its members include both legitimate household warriors and the King's illegitimate sons — [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]], [[thorgisl]], and [[thorir]] — whose ambiguous status grants them proximity to power without formal standing. This dual nature, combining professional warriors with political favorites, makes the bodyguard both a military asset and a locus of court intrigue.

The bodyguard's culture is one of personal loyalty to the King above all. A member is expected to "lout before the King" and execute his commands without question (002-055). This loyalty is tested when the bodyguard must face Styrbiorn — the King's own nephew — in battle.

### Structure

The bodyguard appears to be organized as a traditional Norse *hirð* (household retinue):
- **Captain**: [[helgi-ericson|Helgi Ericson]], commanding the guard in battle (015-017)
- **Core warriors**: Named figures who appear throughout the narrative
- **Rank and file**: Unnamed house-carles who form the bulk of the fighting force

## Role in the Story

### The Aki Incident

The bodyguard first becomes significant through the [[slaying-of-aki|slaying of Aki]], one of its members, by [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn]] (001-028). [[aki|Aki]]'s death creates lasting enmity between the bodyguard and Styrbiorn. The narrator notes that "the grief of [[aki|Aki]]'s slaying" is "dug up anew" whenever the bodyguard encounters Styrbiorn or his supporters (002-004).

This incident establishes the bodyguard as Styrbiorn's natural enemies within the Swedish court. [[aki|Aki]]'s fellow guardsmen view Styrbiorn not as the King's nephew but as a killer of their comrade — a murderer who refused even to pay the customary weregild (001-053).

### The Broken Meats Incident

At the betrothal [[hall-feast|feast]] for Styrbiorn and [[queen-thyri|Thyri]], tensions between the bodyguard and Styrbiorn's men erupt again. A man of the King's bodyguard enters the hall to announce something to the King (002-055), and the strained atmosphere between the two [[all-organizations|factions]] is palpable throughout the [[hall-feast|banquet]] scenes in Chapters IX and X.

### The Battle of Fyrisfield

The bodyguard's narrative purpose culminates at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], where they form the King's personal shield-wall:

> Eric the King had his bodyguard about him, and they made a shield-burg about the King. Helgi was captain of the King's bodyguard (015-017).

This is the bodyguard's supreme test: facing the elite [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] in the battle's climactic phase. [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]], as captain, engages in personal combat with one of Styrbiorn's champions:

> Helgi was there in the door, harnessed for battle. (015-026)

The fight is brutal. [[helgi-ericson|Helgi]] is severely wounded: "He hath come too near me" (015-032), and is borne into the King's hall bleeding heavily (015-033). The bodyguard's losses at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] are severe, with many of its members falling in the desperate final stand.

### After the Battle

In the battle's aftermath, a survivor of the King's bodyguard composes a stave (short poem) commemorating the fallen (015-061) — a traditional Norse practice that underscores the bodyguard's role as the keeper of the King's martial honor even in defeat.

## Historical Basis

The royal bodyguard (*hirð*) was a standard institution in Viking-age Scandinavian courts. Historical sources, including [[skaldic-lore|skaldic poetry]] and later saga accounts, describe these retinues as the King's most trusted warriors — men who ate at his table, received gifts from his hand, and were expected to fight and die at his side.

The *Jomsvikinga saga* and *Heimskringla* provide numerous examples of royal bodyguards in action. The most famous is probably the *húskarlar* of King Cnut the Great, who later formalized the institution under a written code of laws (*Vederlag*). Eric's bodyguard is clearly modeled on this historical institution, though its inclusion of the King's illegitimate sons in leadership roles is a fictional dramatic device.
