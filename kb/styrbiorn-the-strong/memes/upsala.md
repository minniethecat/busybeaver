# Upsala

## Description

Upsala is the ancient seat of the Swedish kings, the political and religious heart of the [[swedish-kingdom|Swede-realm]] where [[king-eric|Eric the Victorious]] held his court. The city is home to the King's great hall, where [[hall-feast|feast]]s and councils were held by firelight and lamplight amid "bench and board and the many-coloured raiment of that great company" (001-017). Beyond the King's garth lay the temple where the autumn blood-offering was made "for the goodness of the year, as was his wont and the wont of his fathers before him from time immemorial" (001-003), and the [[thing-stead|Thing-stead]] where the freemen of Sweden met to speak law and pass judgment.

The most conspicuous landmark of Upsala was [[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]]'s howe, a [[howe|barrow]] "like a high house in bigness" reared against the sky, "all overgrown with rank grass" whose tufts ducked and paled and rose again in the ceaseless wind (001-009). This howe — the burial mound of Styrbiorn's father — dominated the landscape, visible from the King's hall, the temple, and the open fields beyond.

Southwards from Upsala wound the [[fyriswater|Fyriswater]] amid the flats below the city, and beyond it lay "the far-stretching low country toward [[sigtun|Sigtun]] and the sea." To the east rose the scree-slopes of [[windbergsfell|Windbergsfell]], and westward the far mountains of Upland stretched "shadowy-gray in the light of morning" (001-031). The city commanded a view of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], the water-meadows where the final battle of the saga would be fought (015-001).

## Role in the Story

Upsala is the opening and closing ground of the saga — where Styrbiorn's story begins on his father's howe and where his fate is sealed by [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s authority. The city serves as the backdrop for some of the most pivotal scenes: the slaying of [[aki|Aki]] in the King's hall (001-027), Sigrid's vision on the howe (001-035), the autumn [[blood-offering|sacrifice]] (001-003), the grand [[hall-feast|banquet]] of betrothal where Styrbiorn and [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] were troth-plighted (009-002), and the "broken meats" chapter that follows the [[hall-feast|feast]]'s dark aftermath (010-001).

During the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], the city becomes a vantage point from which those who remain behind watch the fate of armies. [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]] climbed Olaf's howe to observe the battle, "even as she had gazed four years ago when she had stood there and looked on Styrbiorn trying his strength with [[moldi|Moldi]]" (015-023). Bui would have burned Upsala to the ground: he asked no guerdon but to "lay in the first brand at the burning of Upsala" — a wish Styrbiorn refused him (012-019).

Upsala in the novel is not a neutral setting but a charged landscape where every location — the howe, the hall, the temple, the [[thing-stead|Thing-stead]] — carries the weight of ancestral custom and royal power. The city embodies the established order that Styrbiorn must either inherit or overthrow.

## Associated Characters

**[[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious** — Reigned in Upsala as sole King of Sweden, holding his court in the King's hall and making sacrifice at the temple (001-001, 001-003).

**[[olaf-biornson|King Olaf]] Biornson** — Laid in howe at Upsala after his sudden death (001-002). His howe became Styrbiorn's refuge.

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — The heir who refused the King's board and instead sat upon his father's howe, claiming his inheritance from the dead rather than the living (001-003, 001-019).

**[[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]]** — Queen in Upsala, who sat at Eric's right hand at the [[hall-feast|feast]] and later climbed the howe to watch the battle (009-003, 015-023).

**[[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]]** — The aged lawman who served at Upsala, advising kings and passing judgment at the [[thing-stead|Thing-stead]] (002-014).

## Historical Context

Old Uppsala (Gamla Uppsala) was the pre-Christian religious and political centre of the Swedes, located in modern-day Uppland. The site featured a renowned pagan temple described by Adam of Bremen in the 11th century, royal burial mounds (the Kungshogarna) still visible today, and a thing assembly site. The novel draws on this rich historical [[all-places|geography]], giving the howe, the temple, and the [[thing-stead|Thing-stead]] a presence as vivid as any character.
