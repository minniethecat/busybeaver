## Definition

Outlawry was the most severe legal penalty in Norse society. A man declared outlaw — known in Old Norse as *skógarmaðr* ("forest-man") or *útlagi* ("outlaw," literally "one laid outside the law") — was stripped of all legal protection, banished from the community, and forfeited all property. He could be killed by anyone without the killer incurring blood-guilt or paying wergild. Outlawry was pronounced by the [[thing-stead|Thing]] assembly, and it represented a form of civic death: the outlaw ceased to exist in the eyes of the law, living in the wilderness beyond the reach of kinship, hospitality, and avenging kin.

The concept was fundamental to Norse justice, which operated not through imprisonment or state punishment but through the community's power to exclude.

In the novel, outlawry is both a background legal institution and a dramatic force that shapes the central conflict. The term itself appears explicitly in connection with [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]], whom [[king-eric|King Eric]] has declared outlaw. Styrbiorn later seeks leave to kill this outlaw — thereby asserting his rights under the law.

Beyond this explicit instance, the institution of outlawry provides the implicit legal framework for the breakdown between Styrbiorn and [[king-eric|King Eric]], culminating in Styrbiorn's effective banishment from Sweden and return with an army.

## Legal Framework in Norse Society

The [[thing-stead|Thing]] assembly — the gathering of all free landholding men — held the sole authority to declare a man outlaw. This judgement could be either *fjorbaugsgardr* ("lesser outlawry," a three-year exile with possibility of return after paying compensation) or *skóggangr* ("full outlawry," permanent banishment, loss of all property, and the status of *skógarmaðr* — a man of the woods, beyond all law). The latter meant that the outlaw's life stood forfeit: anyone might kill him with impunity. He could not be fed, sheltered, or aided in any way — to do so was itself an offence. The outlaw was, in the most literal sense, a wolf's head: a creature hunted.

This institution rested on the Norse understanding that law and community were coextensive. To be inside the law was to belong to the web of kinship, obligation, and mutual protection that defined civilised life. To be *útlagi* — outside the law — was to be severed from that web. The phrase "forest-man" captures this vividly: the outlaw was exiled to the untamed wilderness, the realm of beasts and outcasts, no longer part of the human social order.

This was a punishment more feared than death, for a Norseman's identity, security, and honour were inseparable from his place within the kin-group and the assembly of free men.

## Occurrences in the Novel

### Lambi the White: The Novel's Explicit Outlaw

The most direct reference to outlawry in the novel concerns [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]], a sea-king who has been declared outlaw by [[king-eric|King Eric]]. When Styrbiorn comes before the King in Chapter 2, he explicitly invokes his right to deal with this outlaw: "give me leave too to slay your outlaw [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]], which these shagamuffin bonders and that old man did openly name for king. And that was the biggest shame ever heard tell of" (002-057).

This passage reveals two crucial facts: Lambi has been declared outlaw by the King (the legal fact), and the bonders at an unlawful [[thing-stead|Thing]] have nevertheless named him king (the political defiance).

Styrbiorn does carry out this killing. In Chapter 4, recounting his deeds to [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], he states plainly: "afore that, I slew mine uncle's outlaw, [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]], that was a sea-king of great note. That was off [[skaney|Skane]]y-side last summer" (004-020).

By slaying Lambi, Styrbiorn enforces the law of outlawry — the killing is lawful because Lambi, as outlaw, is outside the law's protection. Yet this act carries political weight beyond the law, for Lambi was the bonders' rival candidate for kingship. The killing of the outlaw thus doubles as the elimination of a political rival, achieved within the frame of legal right.

### The Unlawful Thing and the Bonders' Defiance

The proclamation of [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]] as king at an unlawful [[thing-stead|Thing]] represents a direct challenge to [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s authority and to the legal order itself. The [[thing-stead|Thing]] that proclaimed Lambi was held while the King was absent — "when the [[thing-stead|Thing]] was broken up according to law and I gone otherwhere for a season" (002-030) — and lacked lawful authority. At this assembly, the bonders not only named a rival king but drove [[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf]] and Styrbiorn from the [[thing-stead|Thing-stead]] with stones and muck (002-011), a public humiliation of the King's kinsman and his chief earl.

The bonders' grievance was rooted in Styrbiorn's slaying of [[aki|Aki]], a man of good kin among them, for which no compensation (boot) was initially paid: "there was mighty discontent among the bonder-folk because of this slaying of [[aki|Aki]], and much murmuring against Styrbiorn and his lawless and unbridled vein who should so slay the man and pay no boot therefor" (002-001).

The King ultimately paid the boot himself — a settlement that quieted the immediate unrest but did not extinguish the bonders' deeper hostility. The incident illustrates the close connection between the payment of compensation and the maintenance of social peace: failure to pay boot was a step toward outlawry, and the bonders' own resort to an unlawful assembly and a rival king shows how fragile the legal order could become when the King's own kinsman defied its norms.

### Styrbiorn's Effective Outlawry

Though the novel never uses the word "outlaw" for Styrbiorn himself, his trajectory follows the pattern of outlawry in its social logic. After the confrontation in [[upsala|Upsala]] — Styrbiorn's night visit to [[queen-sigrid|Queen Sigrid]]'s chamber and the ensuing breach with [[king-eric|King Eric]] — Styrbiorn flees Sweden for [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]. [[king-eric|King Eric]] sends Thorgnyr to tell Styrbiorn that "If I should see him, that should be his death" (010-025). Though no formal [[thing-stead|Thing]] pronounces Styrbiorn an outlaw, the effect is identical: he is banished from the kingdom, his life forfeit should the King find him, and his inheritance — half the kingdom — is stripped away.

Styrbiorn's response is to gather the entire power of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] and the [[danes|Danes]] and sail north to take Sweden by force: "All things else that are mine I stake on this throw, to fare north now into Sweden and take it by force" (013-020).

This is the classic pattern of the outlawed prince who returns with an army — a figure known from historical Norse politics and saga literature alike. The legal framework of outlawry, with its logic of exclusion and the forfeiture of rights, provides the deep structure of the novel's central conflict.

## Historical Context

Outlawry was a fundamental institution across the Germanic world from the earliest recorded period through the late Middle Ages. In Iceland — whose legal records are the most detailed surviving — the *Grágás* laws provide elaborate procedures for outlawry, distinguishing between lesser outlawry (three years) and full outlawry (permanent). The Icelandic system, in the absence of a king, vested all authority in the [[thing-stead|Thing]] and the *godar* (chieftain-priests). In mainland Scandinavia, kings could also declare outlawry, though typically in conjunction with or through the [[thing-stead|Thing]] assembly.

The status of the *skógarmaðr* features prominently in the Icelandic family sagas. Gisli Sursson of *Gisla saga* is perhaps the most famous literary outlaw: declared *skógarmaðr* for a killing, he survives for years in the Icelandic wilderness, aided by his loyal wife, until finally hunted down and killed. The saga conveys the terror and loneliness of the outlaw's existence — constantly hiding, dependent on secret allies whose own lives are endangered by their assistance, cut off from the community that once gave life its meaning.

In a royal context, outlawry was a potent political weapon. Kings could use the declaration of outlawry to eliminate rivals, confiscate property, and crush opposition. The historical [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]] — known from the Icelandic *Styrbjarnar attr Sviakappa* and references in *Knytlinga saga* and other sources — was said to have been driven from Sweden after a conflict with his uncle [[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious. The saga tradition records that he raised a great fleet and returned to fight at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]] ([[fyriswater|Fyris]]vellir) near [[upsala|Uppsala]], where he was defeated and killed. The novel's narrative closely follows this historical outline, which is itself a near-perfect illustration of the outlawry pattern in royal Norse politics.
