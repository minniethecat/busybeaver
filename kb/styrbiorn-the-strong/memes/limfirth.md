## Description

The Limfirth (modern Limfjord) was a broad firth cutting across northern [[jutland|Jutland]], connecting the North Sea to the Kattegat. In the Viking Age it was a major waterway providing sheltered passage for ships and access to the Danish interior. [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] Bluetooth's stronghold of [[alaburg|Alaburg]] stood on its shores, making the firth a center of royal authority and a natural gathering place for fleets.


> It was wonderful stormy weather that night, and it so befell that there was sailing by the Jutland coast a little north of the east mouth of the Limfirth a man of Iceland bound south out of the Swede-realm on a cheaping voyage. The wind and the sea drave his ship aland and she was broken in pieces in the shallows, but the skipper and his ship's company saved themselves and came ashore.
>
> (013-004)

## Role in the Story

### Approach to Alaburg

When Styrbiorn sailed to confront [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]], he entered "through the [[jutland|Jutland]] sea into the Limfirth and found there [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]" (012-032). The firth served as the maritime gateway to Harald's seat of power. Vessels from across the northern seas navigated the Limfirth to reach the Danish court.

### The Naval Standoff

During the crisis between Styrbiorn and Harald, the Limfirth became the stage for naval maneuvering. Harald's ships "were already there in the Limfirth over against [[alaburg|Alaburg]], and some seven score of the King's ships" (013-034), creating a blockade. The King's taunting challenge — "Wilt thou bridge the Limfirth with thy ships, Styrbiorn?" (013-035) — underscored the firth's role as both barrier and battleground.

### Departure

Styrbiorn's eventual departure saw his fleet sailing "from the haven and down the Limfirth, and so set sail and hove out to sea" (013-043). The firth marked the boundary between Harald's authority and the open sea where Styrbiorn could again act as a free sea-king.
