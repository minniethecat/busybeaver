# Harald Bluetooth

## Biography

[[harald-bluetooth|Harald Gormson]], known throughout the Northlands as Harald Bluetooth, was King of Denmark during the time of [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]. He was the son of Gorm the Old and maintained his court at [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]] in Sealand, in a great hall overlooking the gray land and the gray sea, and also held a fair house at [[alaburg|Alaburg]] on the Limfirth (005-007) (012-032). His rule marked a period of profound transition for the [[danish-kingdom|Danish kingdom]], caught between the old Norse religion and the advance of Christianity from the south.

Physically, Harald was a striking and unhandsome figure. Eddison describes him as "burly of build and quack-bellied and thick and short of neck and with great chuff cheeks, and his mouth was ugly, and a monstrous dead tooth lay out of it over his lip of one side, that had been there these many years" (005-008). It was from this disfigurement that he gained the by-name "Blue-tooth" by which he was known across the Northlands -- a name that would prove more enduring than his political legacy (005-008).

Despite his physical shortcomings, Harald presented himself with royal magnificence. The novel depicts him as "a man of the greatest largesse" who set good fare and strong drink before his guests (005-008). His hospitality was generous but also prodigal: every night his hall became a scene of excess, with all the "Dane-folk swine-drunken, so that they lay where they slid and snouked and snored till morning" (005-008). Harald himself outdrank his folk yet was "overcome at length with his quaffings and lay drunk in his chair" (005-008). This pattern of drunken collapse would recur throughout the narrative, painting Harald as a king unable to govern even his own appetites.

### The Christian Conversion

The defining event of Harald's reign took place before the novel's main action, when "Keisar Otto came up against the Dane-work and bade the [[danes|Danes]] take christening" (005-009). After a long war, "the Keisar won the Dane-work, and let do christen the King willy-nilly, him and all his men" (005-009). The Emperor also compelled Harald to baptise his ally [[earl-hakon|Earl Hakon of Norway]], though Hakon renounced both Christianity and Harald's overlordship immediately upon reaching open sea. As Biorn relates: "as soon as Earl Hakon was gotten with his ships out of the land, men say that he went ashore on an isle that is there and made blood-offering unto the Gods and so went again ashipboard and so home to Hladir" (005-009).

Under Harald, Denmark was the only Scandinavian kingdom where any real foothold for Christianity had been established: "Only in Denmark, under [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Gormson]], had Christianity as yet any sure foothold in the North" (017-001). The Christian ritual sat uneasily upon the Danish court. Every night before meat, a priest "in a long-coloured gown like as women use to wear, and had the top of his head shaven bald and smooth" would hallow the board (005-009). The King attended mass in his kirk with his followers, yet his comportment -- the nightly drunkenness, his volatile moods, his deep disturbance at hearing the pagan Spae-Wife's Lay -- revealed a man whose conversion was more political submission than spiritual transformation.

### The Yule at Roiskeld

Styrbiorn and Biorn arrived at Harald's court in Sealand during the winter season, and the King "gave them good welcome, and bade them be with him over Yuletide" (005-007). At first Harald sought to attach Styrbiorn to his own service, offering "both goods and land thereto and an Earl's name, if he would leave [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] and come to him" (005-011). When Styrbiorn refused, and refused as well to take christening, their friendship ceased to grow and "bide where it was, like a thorn set in poor soil on a windy headland" (005-011).

The crisis erupted at the great [[hall-feast|Yule-drinking]]. At Styrbiorn's whispered urging, Biorn stood forth and recited the [[spae-wifes-lay|Voluspa]] -- the old holy Spae-Wife's Lay "which telleth of the beginning of all that is, foretelling also the end thereof, and the ways of the Gods with men" (005-050). The recitation of the pagan prophecy profoundly disturbed Harald. The chapter notes that "Harald the King sat back in his high seat hunched up in his beard. With one great hairy fist before him on the table he grasped the drinking horn, stock still" (005-072). His eyes were "cast down for the first while; then he raised them and fixed a dark and troubled stare on Styrbiorn" (005-072). The King's reaction was so violent that [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] later reported to Biorn: "the King my father was put in a mighty taking and hath slept not a wink the whole night through" (006-003).

The following day brought the final breach. The King pressed Styrbiorn to leave Denmark for Sweden, and Styrbiorn, in a fury, departed with threats: "When I come again, I will show thee and thy [[danes|Danes]] if I be a man slow to take mine own or not" (006-048). The [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] sailed away in midwinter storm, though Biorn and [[bessi-thorlakson|Bessi Thorlakson]] managed to smooth over the quarrel so that they parted with formal gifts rather than outright war (006-056).

### The Cowing at Alaburg

When Styrbiorn returned with the assembled power of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], the balance of power shifted decisively. After winning three sea-fights against the [[danes|Danes]], Styrbiorn sailed through the [[jutland|Jutland]] sea into the Limfirth, where King Harald "deemed it not hopeful to fight with Styrbiorn, and they made peace there" (012-032). The King offered hospitality at his house in [[alaburg|Alaburg]], and they put off until morning all discussion of terms.

The next day, Styrbiorn and Harald met alone upon a bluff overlooking the strand. Styrbiorn demanded "an hundred ships full-manned to follow and fight for him in three great [[all-events|battles]], and that he besides should have to wife Thyri, Harald's daughter" (012-045). Harald refused, and their parley escalated through acrimony and recrimination. The King taunted Styrbiorn with his prior failure in Sweden: "I scarce think he'll back thee again, though" (012-055). He brought up the promise of Thyri to [[burisleif|King Burisleif]] of the Wends (012-056).

Styrbiorn then delivered his ultimatum in one of the novel's most dramatic confrontations. Standing over Harald "like as one fighting dog stands a-bristling over another," Styrbiorn gave him this choice: "either to stretch out thine hand and betrothe to me thy daughter, and swear friendship to me, and these hundred ships too; or if not, I will flit thee ashipboard this very night, over seas to [[kirialaland|Kirialaland]] or the Finn-heaths of Smaland or otherwhere, and deliver thee up to savage men shall shear off thy nose and thine ears and draw out thy tripes for their dogs to eat" (012-061).

The description of Harald's reaction is one of the most vivid in the novel:

> The King looked up in his eyes without blenching, a deadly look. A dirty greyish pallor was on the King's cheek and brow: his lips were drawn back, showing in a set grin his teeth and gums and that ugly great tooth of his that jutted forth like a wild swine's tusk blue and monstrous in the left-hand corner of his mouth. His right hand was clenched like iron on his sword-hilt, but his sword abode safe home in the scabbard. (012-062)

Harald capitulated. "So it is, Styrbiorn," he said, "that thou art a strong man. But I think thy strength shall one day overreach itself. I' the mean season, thou must have thy way, here as in what else besides" (012-063). When Styrbiorn took his hand in agreement, the King's grip "was like a dead fish" (012-064). Styrbiorn made him swear first by Christ, then by Thor, binding him doubly (012-065) (012-067).

Thus was Harald "cowed" -- the Preface directly quotes Heimskringla: "Styrbiorn, our kinsman, cowed him, so that Harald became his man" [P02-001]. The title of Chapter XII -- "The Cowing of the Dane-King" -- encapsulates the episode.

### The Final Humiliation

When Styrbiorn returned from [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] with his full fleet and announced his intention to sail for Sweden, Harald saw his last chance to assert himself. He sought to limit his contribution to token support, offering "two score ships well found and manned" (013-038). Styrbiorn was unyielding. He demanded two hundred ships and chose Harald himself as the man he would take with him, declaring "And that shall be Harald the King" (013-040).

> The King would have fared aboard of his own ship, but Styrbiorn made him go with him in *Ironbeak*. The King had learnt now the wisdom of that saying, "Never strive to match the sea," and he would contend no more against Styrbiorn. Styrbiorn said to Biorn, "I will trust my father-in-law very discreetly. Till this fight be over, I will keep him by me like a doll. That will be good for him and good for his following. For they will guess well that if they keep not faith with me I will slay him out of hand." (013-042)

The [[danes|Danes]] left behind in [[jutland|Jutland]] sang a mocking stave that captured Harald's reduced state: "Now must the Dane-lord / Follow that hosting: / Land-lorn and folk-lorn / Drees he his [[norse-fate|weird]] now" [013-045 to 013-052]. He was present at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], where the [[danes|Danes]] fought on the left flank as "King Harald Gormson's following" under their captain Bui (015-011).

## Role in the Story

[[harald-bluetooth|Harald Gormson]] functions as a foil to [[king-eric|King Eric]] the Victorious and as a dramatic illustration of the power hierarchy among the Norse kingdoms. Where Eric is the formidable, untouchable king whom Styrbiorn dares not challenge directly, Harald is the king whom Styrbiorn can dominate, humiliate, and extrude for resources -- ships, men, and a daughter. The acknowledgment that [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s authority simply cannot be challenged, while Harald's can, establishes the principle that the Swedish crown at [[upsala|Upsala]] stands above the Danish monarchy in the saga's world.

Harald also embodies the conflicted state of late tenth-century Norse kingship. He is a Christian king by force, not conviction -- baptised "willy-nilly" by the Emperor, attending mass while drinking himself unconscious each night, and reacting to pagan poetry with primordial terror (005-009) (005-072). His court is a visual symbol of this contradiction: the priest in his gown and the kirk beside the hall sit alongside [[hall-feast|feast]]s that descend into pagan-tinged excess. Eddison depicts Harald not as a wicked man but as a diminished one -- a king whose power is hollow, whose dignity is routinely punctured, and whose reign is defined by submission to stronger forces, whether the Holy Roman Emperor to his south or the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] to his east.

Thematically, Harald represents a world in decline from the heroic ideal. His drunkenness, his corpulence, his dead tooth, and his cowed submission to Styrbiorn all mark him as a figure of decay -- a contrast to the fierce vitality of Styrbiorn and the sacred authority of Eric. He is the king who has lost the mandate of the old gods without fully gaining the sanction of the new one, a ruler caught between worlds and adequate to neither.

## Relationships

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** -- The central dynamic of Harald's role in the novel. Harald first welcomed Styrbiorn as a guest at [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]] for the Yule season (005-007), sought to make him his man (005-011), and then sought to rid himself of him when the relationship soured (006-044). Their conflict culminated in Harald's humiliation at [[alaburg|Alaburg]], where Styrbiorn cowed him into surrendering ships and his daughter [012-045 to 012-067]. Harald then accompanied Styrbiorn's fleet to Sweden, kept "like a doll" as a hostage for his followers' good behaviour (013-042).

**Thyri** -- Harald's daughter, described as fourteen winters old with "black hair and of a dainty curling growth," of few words but with a merry eye (005-012). Harald loved her but had betrothed her to [[burisleif|King Burisleif]] of the Wends, a match she did not desire [012-038 to 012-039]. His thwarting of Thyri's own wishes, and Styrbiorn's interest in her, formed the catalyst for their first rupture at [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]]. Styrbiorn's demand for Thyri as part of the peace terms reversed Harald's matrimonial diplomacy at a stroke (012-045). When Styrbiorn later insisted on taking Thyri to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] -- violating [[jomsburg|the burg]]'s ancient law against admitting women -- Harald was "very ill content" but could do nothing to prevent it (013-017).

**Svein** -- Harald's only son, "now at fostering with [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]" in [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], aged nine winters (005-012). This fostering arrangement was a sign of the complex and interdependent relationship between the Danish crown and the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] establishment that predated Styrbiorn's arrival. The irony that [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] fostered Harald's son while [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s own sworn men would later humiliate Harald was not lost on the saga's [[all-persons|characters]] (005-006).

**Keisar Otto (Otto II)** -- The Holy Roman Emperor who forced Harald and his [[danes|Danes]] to accept Christianity at the Dane-work. This military and spiritual defeat defined Harald's reign and left him a diminished figure among his pagan neighbours (005-009). The Emperor's victory over Harald stands in the background as the original wound from which Harald's diminished kingship never recovered.

**[[earl-hakon|Earl Hakon of Norway]]** -- Summoned by Harald to help defend the Dane-work against the Emperor, Hakon was also forced into Christianity alongside Harald. He subsequently renounced both the faith and Harald's overlordship, refusing to pay [[scat-tribute|tribute]] and ruling Norway as an independent king (005-009). Hakon's successful defiance contrasts with Harald's continued submission and underscores the weakness of Harald's authority beyond Denmark's borders.

**[[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]** -- The captain of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], a Dane by kin, who fostered Harald's son Svein (005-012) (012-028). [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s decision to follow Styrbiorn into Denmark, despite being "content to be fosterer of his son," represented a bitter betrayal for Harald -- the man he had entrusted with his heir now sailing against him under Styrbiorn's banner [012-028 to 012-031].

**[[burisleif|King Burisleif]] of the Wends** -- Harald's original choice of husband for Thyri (006-023). When Styrbiorn learned of this arrangement and that Thyri did not consent to it, it fuelled his determination to claim her for himself (012-038). The breaking of this betrothal by Harald's surrender to Styrbiorn's demands was a diplomatic humiliation that the novel notes directly: "men wondered much, thinking that here was a new strong wind set in, to blow so suddenly away that old standing bridal pact made with [[burisleif|King Burisleif]]" (013-001).

## Historical Basis

[[harald-bluetooth|Harald Gormson]], known to history as Harald Bluetooth (Old Norse: Haraldr Gormsson; Danish: Harald Blatand), ruled Denmark from approximately 958 until his death around 986. He is one of the best-attested Viking Age kings, most famously commemorated on the larger Jelling stone, where the runic inscription declares that "Harald king won for himself all of Denmark and Norway and made the [[danes|Danes]] Christian" -- a claim that succinctly captures the two [[all-concepts|themes]] of his reign: unification and conversion.

Historical sources disagree on the details of Harald's conversion. Widukind of Corvey's Res Gestae Saxonicae attributes it to a clerical missionary named Poppo, who carried hot iron to demonstrate the power of Christ. Other chronicles, like Adam of Bremen's Gesta Hammaburgensis, credit political pressure from the Ottonian emperors. Eddison follows the tradition emphasizing imperial compulsion, having Harald baptised "willy-nilly" by Keisar Otto after the fall of the Dane-work (005-009).

The Jomsviking saga tradition records Harald's interactions with [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] and the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]], including the fostering of his son Svein (the future Svein Forkbeard, who would himself become King of Denmark and England) at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]. While the sagas disagree on the details of Harald's submission to Styrbiorn -- some present the relationship as an alliance rather than a cowing -- Eddison's novel follows the Heimskringla tradition in which Styrbiorn compels Harald's submission. The novel's Preface explicitly ties this to the saga source: "Styrbiorn, our kinsman, cowed him, so that Harald became his man" [P02-001].

[[harald-bluetooth|Harald Gormson]] died around 986, possibly in a rebellion led by his own son Svein Forkbeard. His reign represents a pivotal moment in Scandinavian history: the last generation of kings who could claim the old ways while presiding over the region's irrevocable turn toward Christian Europe.
