# Cowing of the Dane King

The **Cowing of the Dane King** is the political and military subjugation of King [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Gormson]] of Denmark by [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn Olafson]], unfolding across two distinct phases in *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]*. The first phase occurs during Styrbiorn's winter stay at Harald's court in [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]] (Chapters V–VI), where Styrbiorn's personal defiance turns Harald's attempt at expulsion into a diplomatic embarrassment. The second and decisive phase occurs in Chapter XII — which bears the event's name as its title — when Styrbiorn returns at the head of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburg]] fleet, wins three sea-fights in Danish waters, and corners Harald at [[alaburg|Alaburg]] (Aalborg), forcing the Dane-king to sue for peace. The preface summarizes the outcome: Styrbiorn "cowed him, so that Harald became his man" [P02-002].

## Summary

### First Phase: The Winter at Roiskeld (Chapters V–VI)

During the first winter of his exile, Styrbiorn and his retinue stay as guests at [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]]'s court in [[roiskeld|Roiskeld]]. Styrbiorn quickly perceives the weakness of Harald's household: the Dane-folk are habitually "swine-drunken" by nightfall, leaving the king's hall virtually defenseless (005-008). His growing intimacy with Harald's daughter [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] — "oft would Styrbiorn and [[thyri|Thyri]] be a-talking together" (005-013) — further complicates his position.

When Harald, increasingly uncomfortable with Styrbiorn's presence, attempts to shamefully expel his guests (006-046), Styrbiorn responds with open defiance: "I will show thee and thy [[danes|Danes]] if I be a man slow to take my revenge" (006-048). Through the mediation of [[biorn-broadwicker|Biorn Asbrandson]], who counsels against parting "unfriends with [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]" (006-050), relations are temporarily smoothed (006-056). But the underlying dynamic has been established: Harald has backed down before Styrbiorn's menace.

### Second Phase: The Military Campaign (Chapter XII)

Years later, now commanding the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] fleet, Styrbiorn sails west-viking into Denmark with the explicit aim of bringing Harald to heel (012-027). [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]], though a Dane himself, ultimately honors his [[oath]] to aid Styrbiorn (012-030). Styrbiorn wins three consecutive sea-fights in Danish waters, establishing naval supremacy, then sails through the [[jutland|Jutland]] sea into the Limfirth where he finds Harald and his host (012-032).

> The King deemed it not hopeful to fight with Styrbiorn, and they made peace there (012-032).

Harald, outmatched at sea and cornered in his own realm, handsels peace to Styrbiorn before all the assembled folk. The terms are deferred to the following day (012-032), but the substance is already clear: Harald has capitulated. During the subsequent entertainment at Harald's fair house at [[alaburg|Alaburg]], Styrbiorn re-encounters [[thyri|Thyri]] — their meeting, charged with unspoken feeling, sets in motion the [[betrothal-feast|betrothal]] that will follow [012-033–012-040].

## Context

### Styrbiorn's Trajectory

The two-phase cowing bookends Styrbiorn's transformation from an exiled prince dependent on a foreign king's hospitality (Phase I) to a warlord commanding the most feared fighting force in the Baltic (Phase II). The first visit establishes the personal animosity; the second resolves it through overwhelming military force. Between them lies Styrbiorn's winning of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], his eastern campaigns, and his subjugation of [[burisleif|King Burisleif]] of the Wends [012-025–012-026].

### Harald's Strategic Weakness

[[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] is depicted as a ruler of wealth and largesse but limited martial capacity. His court's nightly drunkenness (005-008) symbolizes a deeper vulnerability: the Danish military cannot match the hardened [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] in sea-fighting. Harald's decision not to contest Styrbiorn's fleet — "the King deemed it not hopeful to fight" (012-032) — acknowledges this asymmetry.

### Palnatoki's Dilemma

[[palnatoki|Palnatoki]], the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] chieftain, is himself a Dane from Fion and fostered Harald's son Svein (012-028). He initially resists Styrbiorn's plan to sail against Denmark, but ultimately yields: "I will not be behindhand with thee in friendship, nor use thee so unhandsomely, seeing I swear oath to aid thee" (012-030). He delegates command to Styrbiorn while remaining ashore, preserving a thin diplomatic fiction.

## Consequences

### Diplomatic Subjugation

The cowing transforms the power relationship between Styrbiorn and the Danish crown. Where Styrbiorn had once been a guest dependent on Harald's hospitality, he is now the dominant partner, dictating terms from a position of military strength. Harald's formal "handselling of peace" before the assembled folk (012-032) is a public acknowledgment of his reduced status.

### The Thyri Alliance

The reunion with [[thyri|Thyri]] at [[alaburg|Alaburg]] (012-033) proves decisive. Her admission that her betrothal to [[burisleif|King Burisleif]] is not of her own will (012-039) opens the path to the [[betrothal-feast|betrothal]] and eventual marriage that gives Styrbiorn a political foothold in Denmark — and through it, a staging ground for his invasion of Sweden.

### Road to Fyrisfield

The cowing secures Styrbiorn's western flank and provides Danish ships and men for the final campaign. [[danes|Harald's Danes]] fight alongside Styrbiorn at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]] (015-011), a direct consequence of the submission exacted at [[alaburg|Alaburg]].

## Historical Basis

The historical [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]] (Harald Gormsson, r. c. 958–986) was among the most successful Danish kings of the Viking Age, unifying Denmark and conquering Norway — a far more formidable figure than Eddison's fictionalized Harald. There is no historical record of Styrbiorn intimidating or subjugating Harald; the episode is Eddison's invention, serving to establish Styrbiorn's heroic stature and to introduce the [[thyri|Thyri]] marriage plot. The real [[harald-bluetooth|Harald Bluetooth]] was deposed by his son Svein Forkbeard, who appears in the novel as a boy of nine (005-012).
