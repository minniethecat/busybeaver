## Definition

The Valkyries (Old Norse *Valkyrjur*, singular *Valkyrja*, from *valr* "the slain on the battlefield" and *kjósa* "to choose") are a company of supernatural shield-maidens who serve Odin, the [[odin|All-Father]]. Their primary office is twofold: upon the field of battle they select which warriors shall fall, and thereafter they bear the souls of the chosen slain — the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] — to [[valhalla|Valhall]]a, the hall of the slain, where they minister to the fallen heroes at the eternal [[hall-feast|feast]]. In the novel they are styled *choosers* and *storm raisers* (016-004) and *shield-mays of the [[odin|Lord of Spears]]* (016-004), epithets that capture both their elective function and their warlike nature.

Within the novel's mythic economy the Valkyries operate at the intersection of fate, divine will, and martial glory. They are not independent agents but instruments of Odin's sovereign purpose, executing his choice in the disposition of battle-slain warriors (016-007). Their role bridges the earthly theatre of war — [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], the novel's climactic battle — and the transcendent realm of [[valhalla|Valhall]]a, where the novel's apocalyptic vision culminates. The Valkyrie figure thus serves as the narrative's central symbol of the Norse heroic ideal: death chosen, not suffered; defeat transformed into eternal victory through Odin's electing grace.

## Occurrences in the Novel

The Valkyries appear at two structurally significant moments: a lyrical, retrospective invocation in Chapter 9 during the Yule [[hall-feast|banquet]], and a dramatic, visionary manifestation in Chapter 16 at the novel's climax.

### Chapter 9: The Lay of Hild the Helm'd

During the Yule [[hall-feast|feast]] aboard [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]'s ship, Biorn Broadwicker recites a verse in which he recalls the figure of Hild the Helm'd, the Valkyrie. The context is a poetic reflection on love, blame, and divine agency. In the verses, Odin himself is named as the agent who "set that fire about her" (009-087), and Hild is remembered in "her free and glorious time" as "the Valkyrie, Odin's shield-may" (009-088). The association is striking: the Valkyrie is not merely a chooser of the slain but also a figure of radiant, unconstrained beauty, linked here to the poet's memory of a beloved woman:

> "Hild the Helm'd," the Valkyrie, Odin's shield-may. Like as in a trance he watched her, such as she was in her free and glorious time. (009-088)

The same figure is introduced earlier in the lay with the line "Hild the Helm'd, whoso knew me" (009-053), establishing her as a figure of recognition and transformation. The epithet "Helm'd" suggests armour — the Valkyrie is at once beautiful and martial, a shield-maiden and a symbol of lost glory.

### Chapter 16: The Vision at Valhalla

Chapter 16 constitutes the novel's eschatological climax. Following the slaughter at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]], the narrative shifts from the earthly battlefield to the supernatural realm of [[valhalla|Valhall]]a. The Valkyries appear here in their fully realized mythic function as the escorts and servitors of the battle-slain.

The [[einheriar|Einheriar]], the fallen warriors, "thronged... into [[valhalla|Valhall]]a, smoking from the fight, innumerable as the sand upon the seashore" (016-003), where Odin himself receives them. With a lifted hand, Odin silences the host, and the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] clash their weapons in deafening thunder, crying:

> "Hail to the choosers! the storm raisers! Hail to the shield-mays of the Lord of Spears, the Father of Hosts!" (016-004)

This passage is the novel's most explicit theological statement regarding the Valkyries. The three epithets — *choosers*, *storm raisers*, *shield-mays* — delineate their tripartite office: elective (they choose), elemental (they raise the storm of battle), and martial (they bear arms for Odin). "Victory rode up the night into [[valhalla|Valhall]]a" (016-005), and the Valkyries then descend to serve the blest at their tables (016-006).

A specific Valkyrie, Skogul, is named in this climactic scene: "Before the [[odin|All-Father]]'s face the Valkyrie Skogul" (016-007). Her presence is described with a sensory intensity that surpasses the visual — it is "like the brandishing of spears" (016-007), a simile that fuses the Valkyrie's own being with the instruments of war. She cries aloud to Odin, addressing him as "O [[odin|God of Hosts]]," and presents something that "His shield-may cherished against her bosom" (016-008). The intimate bodily image — the shield-may holding something precious to her bosom — humanises the Valkyrie even as it elevates her to the status of Odin's trusted minister.

### The Name Skogul

The name *Skogul* (Old Norse *Skögul*, meaning "shaker" or "high-towering") appears twice in the novel in distinct referents. The Valkyrie Skogul manifests in the [[valhalla|Valhall]]a vision (016-007), but the name also belongs to a mortal character: [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]], the father of [[queen-sigrid|Sigrid the Haughty]], a portly thane of [[arland|Arland]] [003-001, 003-002]. This dual usage — the mortal bearing the Valkyrie's name — is unlikely to be accidental in a novel so attentive to the mythic resonance of names. [[skogul-tosti|Skogul-Tosti]]'s daughter Sigrid is, in the novel's logic, the earthly catalyst of the conflict that fills [[valhalla|Valhall]]a with slain warriors; the mortal Skogul-namesake thus unwittingly provisions the harvest that the Valkyrie Skogul reaps.

## Historical Context

In the Old Norse mythological corpus, the Valkyries are richly attested across both the Poetic Edda and the Prose Edda of Snorri Sturluson. *[[spae-wifes-lay|Völuspá]]* (The [[spae-wifes-lay|Seeress's Prophecy]]) names several — Skögul, Gunnr, Hildr, Göndul, and Geirskögul among them — and presents them as maidens who "ride over the earth" to choose the slain. *Grímnismál* describes their service in [[valhalla|Valhall]]a: they pour ale for the [[einheriar|Einheriar]] and tend the everlasting [[hall-feast|feast]]. *Völundarkviða* names three Valkyries — Hlaðguðr svanhvít, Hervör alvitr, and Ölrún — who shed their swan-shirts to become the wives of mortal men, a motif that underlies the complex relationship between the divine and the human in Norse myth.

Snorri Sturluson's *Gylfaginning* (ch. 36) provides the most systematic account: "Odin sends them to every battle. They choose men destined to die and they decide victory. Gunnr and Róta and the youngest Norn, called Skuld, always ride to choose the slain and decide the issue of battle." This passage explicitly links the Valkyries to the Norns, the weavers of fate, situating the choosers of the slain within a broader Norse theology of predestination — a theology the novel actively appropriates.

In the novel's Victorian-archaic idiom, E. R. Eddison and his narrator render "Valkyrjur" as *Valkyrie*, "choosers," and "shield-mays" — a translation strategy that domesticates the Old Norse into a deliberately archaic English. Eddison's Valkyries owe as much to Richard Wagner's *Die Walküre* and to the Victorian romantic revival of Norse antiquity (notably William Morris and George Webbe Dasent) as they do to the medieval sources. The novel transforms the mythological Valkyries from narrative agents in the Eddas into symbolic instruments of Odin's sovereignty and of the heroic fatalism that pervades the work.

## Related Concepts

- **[[valhalla|Valhall]]a** — The hall of the slain, over which the Valkyries minister. The novel depicts [[valhalla|Valhall]]a as both a mythic destination and the narrative's eschatological endpoint (Chapter 16).
- **Odin** — The [[odin|All-Father]], [[odin|Lord of Spears]], for whom the Valkyries serve as shield-maidens and executors of his will [016-004, 016-007].
- **[[einheriar|Einheriar]]** — The chosen battle-slain who are conducted to [[valhalla|Valhall]]a by the Valkyries and who hail them as "choosers" and "storm raisers" [016-003, 016-004].
- **Norse Fate** — The Valkyries' function as "choosers" implicates them in the Norse concept of predestined death (*orlög*), linking the divine choosing of the slain with the broader web of fate.
- **Blood-offering** — The ritual [[blood-offering|sacrifice]] (*blót*) performed by Earl Hakon (005-009) and [[king-eric|King Eric]] (001-003) that restores allegiance to the old gods, the same gods for whom the Valkyries labour.
