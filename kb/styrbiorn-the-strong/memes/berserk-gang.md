## Definition

The berserk-gang (Old Norse berserksgangr, literally "berserk-going") is a state of frenzied, trance-like battle-rage that seizes Norse warriors, rendering them impervious to pain and fear while driving them to fight with extraordinary violence. The novel presents the berserk-gang as a quasi-supernatural possession: those overtaken by it bellow like beasts, lose awareness of their surroundings, and fight until either victory or death. In the novel's martial economy, the berserk is the most feared instrument on the battlefield — a human weapon loosed when ordinary tactics fail.

The phenomenon is described in physiological terms: Styrbiorn at one point is said to appear "like to a man with the berserk-gang upon him" (013-009), and [[king-eric|King Eric]]'s berserks "had now the berserk-gang upon them: their bellowing was heard high above the clash of steel and that undersound of groaning and screaming" (015-018). The bellowing — an animal roar that cuts through the din of battle — is the auditory signature of the berserk state.

The most vivid portrait of the berserk-gang occurs not on the battlefield but in King Harald's hall at Alaburg, when Styrbiorn learns of his uncle's betrayal:

> He was leaned forth over the table on his elbows, with nostrils wide and in his eyes a light like as is in a lynx's eyes about to spring. He had in his right hand in that instant an ale-horn, and so mightily his hand shut on the horn that the horn cracked and burst under his handgrip and the ale was wasted on the board. The chapman was afeared beholding him, and all men beheld him with some dread, for he seemed like to a man with the berserk-gang upon him, and well they deemed that that should be the bane of many a man there if Styrbiorn should break forth in that kind amongst them. So for a full minute's space not a man stirred nor spake in that hall, gazing all on Styrbiorn.
>
> (013-009)


## Occurrences in the Novel

The berserk-gang is introduced in Chapter XIII, "The Sailing of the Fleet," in connection with Styrbiorn himself. As he contemplates the coming war with his uncle, he "seemed like to a man with the berserk-gang upon him, and well they deemed it that knew him" (013-009). This description links the battle-fury to Styrbiorn's volatile temperament: when provoked, he becomes something other than the measured leader, a force of nature rather than a strategist.

The most extended depiction comes during the battle of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] (Chapter XV). On the third day of fighting, when the battle is at its most desperate, "the King sent forth his berserks, An the Black and his kinsmen" (015-017). These are named warriors — specialists deployed at the critical moment. Their entry transforms the battlefield: "An the Black and his fellows had now the berserk-gang upon them: their bellowing was heard high above the clash of steel and that undersound of groaning and screaming, and they urged on the King's folk to follow them up in that onrush" (015-018). Their role is both tactical (breaking enemy lines) and psychological (rallying their own side, terrifying the enemy).

The novel's treatment of the berserk-gang aligns it with the broader theme of battle as a sacred, ecstatic experience — akin to Odin's own dominion over frenzy and inspiration. That the berserks serve [[king-eric|King Eric]], and that they are deployed at the battle's decisive moment, subtly reinforces the divine favour that the King has purchased with his oath.

## Historical/Mythological Context

In Old Norse literature, berserkir are warriors who fight in a trance-like fury. The term appears in [[skaldic-lore|skaldic poetry]] from the 9th century onward and is described in the Ynglinga saga (ch. 6): Odin's men "went without armour, mad as dogs or wolves, bit their shields, and were strong as bears or bulls; they killed men but neither fire nor iron could hurt them." The berserksgangr is often associated with shapeshifting traditions — the warrior taking on the nature of a bear (ber-serkr, "bear-shirt") or [[earl-wolf|wolf]] (ulfhéðinn).

The berserkir were both feared and distrusted in Norse society. While valuable in battle — as the novel's deployment of An the Black and his kinsmen demonstrates — they were also seen as socially dangerous. Several sagas (Egil's Saga, Grettir's Saga) portray berserks as bullies and troublemakers in peacetime. The novel follows this ambivalent tradition: the berserks are unleashed as a last resort, their fury useful but barely controllable, and they operate at the margins of the ordered battle-line, closer to forces of nature than to disciplined soldiers.
