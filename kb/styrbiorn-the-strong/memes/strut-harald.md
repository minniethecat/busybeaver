## Biography

Strut-Harald was a famous Earl in [[skaney|Skane]]y (Skåne), the Danish earldom lying east across the Sound from Sealand (004-002). By the time [[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn Olafson]] came to know him, Strut-Harald was already a very old man, past his fighting days, yet he maintained a household of extraordinary magnificence. His word "went as a king's" throughout [[skaney|Skane]]y-side, and his great house "was scant to be matched in all the [[danish-kingdom|kingdom of Denmark]]" (008-002).

In the deep winter, Styrbiorn and his men sailed south and put in at [[skaney|Skane]]y, where Strut-Harald gave them "noble welcome and kept them with him till winter's end" (008-001). The Earl sat in state "like unto a king," with guests perpetually coming and going, clad always in "exceeding costly attire" with jewelled ornaments and weapons of rare workmanship. Most striking was his hat "covered with round plates of gold of the bigness of the palm of a woman's hand," a headpiece that many thought "had more gold about it than many a king's crown" (008-002).

Despite his age, Strut-Harald was no figure of decay. He remained "straight-shouldered and broad-chested and with a proud and high carriage of the head," a big man and tall, great-boned and lean (008-002). His authority was upheld by his powerful sons and sons-in-law and the young men of his blood who honoured him.

When Styrbiorn came to bid him farewell, Strut-Harald held him long by both hands and delivered a remarkable judgment of his three sons: [[heming|Heming]] he praised as a ship-steerer and swordsman but a natural follower; [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]] he called "a man of war, but he hath little wit"; Sigvaldi he named "a fox" who would win lordship, wealth, fame, and long life but would never be praised by the best men. He concluded by telling Styrbiorn that greatness and fair fame would be his, but that his life would be short (008-004). He gave Styrbiorn "a great axe inlaid with silver and gold" as a parting gift (008-004).

## Role in the Story

Strut-Harald provides the saga with one of its most fully realized portraits of old age and worldly wisdom. Unlike the active warrior-lords who dominate the narrative, Strut-Harald has passed beyond the age of harryings and contentions, yet his authority remains undiminished. His great house in [[skaney|Skane]]y serves as a winter refuge for Styrbiorn and his men, a place of rest and gathering before the violent campaigns to come.

His farewell judgment of his three sons is among the novel's most psychologically acute passages. It reveals Strut-Harald as a man of penetrating insight who loves his sons but sees their limitations with unsparing clarity. His prediction about Styrbiorn — greatness, fair fame, and a short life — functions as an almost prophetic summary of the saga's tragic arc.

Through Strut-Harald's three sons — Sigvaldi the fox, [[heming|Heming]] the follower, and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]] the witless warrior — the saga populates its [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] scenes with a dynastic network whose internal tensions drive key plot developments, particularly Sigvaldi's desertion at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] and the loyalty of [[heming|Heming]] and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]] who remain to command the left wing (015-011).

## Quotes

> "I would thou wert my son. For my sons, good though they be, I deem not good enough." — Strut-Harald to Styrbiorn (008-004)

> "I think, Styrbiorn, that the first two of these will be thine. But I think thy life will be short." — Strut-Harald's prophecy (008-004)

## Historical Basis

Strut-Harald (Old Norse: *Strút-Haraldr*) appears in the *Jómsvíkinga saga* and related sources as a powerful Danish earl and the father of Sigvaldi, the Jomsviking leader. "Strut" is an Old Norse word for a conical headdress or pointed cap, and the saga tradition explains this nickname by reference to the ornate hat he wore — a detail that Eddison incorporates and elaborates upon in his description of the hat covered with gold plates. The historical Strut-Harald is associated with Skåne ([[skaney|Skane]]y) in the late 10th century, and his sons Sigvaldi and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell]] the Tall (Þorkell inn hávi) are well-attested figures in Scandinavian and Anglo-Saxon sources.
