# Sigvaldi Jarl

## Biography

Sigvaldi was one of the three sons of [[strut-harald|Earl Strut-Harald]], a famous earl in [[skaney|Skane]]y, and brother to [[heming|Heming]] and [[thorkell-the-high|Thorkell the High]] (004-002). He was among the lords present in [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] when Styrbiorn arrived with his fleet of sixty ships seeking admission to the warrior brotherhood. Unlike the hot-headed Bui, Sigvaldi was ever the practical man: when the confrontation on the seawall threatened to escalate, it was Sigvaldi who urged immediate action, saying, "The ships be manned, Toki. 'Twere well an we rowed out forthright: take these conies, ere they take fright and up scut and away" (004-024).

At the great council on [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] seawall in Chapter 12, when Styrbiorn prepared to press his claim to the Swedish throne, Sigvaldi alone among the lords showed reluctance. As [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] pledged unconditional support — "we shall give Styrbiorn aid not here nor there, nor for this or that, but freely and for what [[thing-stead|thing]] soever he will take in hand" — Sigvaldi leaned closer to [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] and whispered some objection. [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] dismissed him flatly: "I care not for that. I will have no conditions." Sigvaldi bit his lip but kept silence, rising with the rest to clash swords on shields in pledge of aid (012-014, 012-015).

When the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] debated the wisdom of attacking [[king-eric|King Eric]] in [[upsala|Upsala]], Sigvaldi spoke against the enterprise, warning that many men would find it hard to keep such an [[oath]], "to egg him on to so unhopeful and bad an enterprise as that should be, to go up against the King o' the Swedes in [[upsala|Upsala]]. For many a man would find it a hard thing and an ill to keep to his oath, were it to draw us into suchlike folly" (012-021). His caution, while framed as pragmatism, was read by his comrades as a kind of cowardice — an unwillingness to risk everything for Styrbiorn's cause.

Sigvaldi's most vivid exchange with Styrbiorn came as the fleet prepared to sail, when Styrbiorn bound raven's wings on his helm. Sigvaldi warned him that bearing raven's wings befitted no man, "nor yet the lesser Gods neither, but the [[odin|All-Father]] alone," and counselled him to leave them off, "For well I think they shall bring us ill luck." When Styrbiorn brushed this aside, Sigvaldi pressed further, "I counsel thee out of my love and loyalty. A man might say, to look on thee, thou hadst now clapt up thy good luck in a cage of gold. But I feared thee less in thy mad melancholy discontents last winter" (013-026, 013-028).

> We have promised Styrbiorn this, to follow and uphold him this summer. And methinks there is little wisdom in this, to egg him on to so unhopeful and bad an enterprise as that should be, to go up against the King o' the Swedes in Upsala. For many a man would find it a hard thing and an ill to keep to his oath, were it to draw us into suchlike folly.
>
> (012-021)

## Role in the Story

Sigvaldi serves as the voice of caution and self-preservation within the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] brotherhood, a counterweight to the fiery enthusiasm of [[bui-digri|Bui the Stout]] and the unconditional loyalty of [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]. His character embodies the political calculation that the warrior code is supposed to suppress: he calculates risk, questions the wisdom of hopeless enterprises, and [[all-places|places]] self-interest before honour. In this respect, he functions as a foil to the novel's idealised warrior values, reminding the reader that even among the legendary [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]], human nature admits of cunning and self-preservation.

## Key Relationships

**[[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]** — Sigvaldi sat among [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s councillors and, unlike the others, was not afraid to voice dissent. His whispered objection at the council earned him a public rebuke from [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]], who overruled him without discussion (012-014).

**[[bui-digri|Bui the Stout]]** — Where Sigvaldi counselled caution, Bui demanded war. Bui's hot-headed demand to "lay in the first brand at the burning of [[upsala|Upsala]]" (012-019) stood in direct contrast to Sigvaldi's warning against an unhopeful enterprise (012-021).

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — Sigvaldi's relationship with Styrbiorn was marked by ambivalence. He pledged aid alongside the other lords but consistently doubted the wisdom of Styrbiorn's cause and advised against his most daring acts, including the wearing of raven's wings on his helm (013-026).
