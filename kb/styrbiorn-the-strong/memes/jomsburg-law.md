# Jomsburg Law (Jomsviking Warrior Code)

## Definition

The **[[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] Law** (also called the *lay of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]]*) was the sworn warrior code that governed the elite brotherhood of vikings at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], the great sea-fortress captained by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki of Fion]]. Every man admitted to the fellowship, whether of high birth or low, was required to swear a binding oath to uphold its terms — and any who broke them was driven out (004-003).

The code was not a legal code in the civil sense; it was a personal covenant, a set of rules that transformed a collection of individual warriors into a disciplined brotherhood bound by mutual obligation and shared fate.

The law was "covenanted" among the members and administered by the captain, [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]], who held sole authority to settle disputes, admit new members, and pronounce judgements. The code's provisions covered every aspect of the warrior's life: who could join, how plunder was shared, what conduct was forbidden, and above all the absolute prohibition against fear or cowardice, no matter how desperate the circumstances (004-004).

## Occurrences in the Novel

### The Law Covenanted at Jomsburg

The [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] Law is introduced in full in Chapter IV, where its provisions are recited as the framework of the fortress's society. The code is presented as having been established by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] and binding upon "every man of them, were he of great estate or were he least among their fellowship" (004-003). The terms are set out in a single concentrated passage that constitutes the constitution of the Jomsviking warband.

The law's scope is comprehensive. It governs admission — no man under eighteen winters or over fifty may join — and sets the moral tone of the fellowship by forbidding slander, requiring mutual vengeance, and demanding that all plunder be brought to the common pole for equal division (004-004).

The captain's authority is absolute: [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] alone decides all disputes, and no man may even speak news without his leave. The prohibition against bringing women into the fortress and the rule that no man may be abroad more than three nights without permission further reinforce the totalising character of the brotherhood's discipline.

### Styrbiorn's Challenge to the Law

The law's age restriction becomes the pivot of the chapter's central conflict. Styrbiorn arrives at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] still a youth, and [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] rebuffs him precisely on the grounds of the code: "Thou art a boy. Come back in a year or twain, with a beard grown" (004-027). When Styrbiorn refuses to leave, he proposes a trial by combat — "if they thought him over young to be had into their law and fellowship, then let them try it out, since by handy-stripes at equal odds is a man best known" (004-032).

This challenge tests the law's fundamental tension between its written rules and the warrior ethos of proven worth. [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] resolves it by personal decision: he fights Styrbiorn ship to ship, and when he finds in him a warrior of extraordinary mettle, he suspends the age rule. The saga records that "never before was any man had into [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] that was less than eighteen winters old, nor, after Styrbiorn, was any man else had in by them under age save only [[vagn-akison|Vagn Akison]] alone" (004-061). The law, in other words, was not a rigid code but a living covenant — capable of yielding before proven greatness at the captain's discretion.

### The Laws in Practice: Honour and Obedience

After Styrbiorn's admission, the saga notes that the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] "sat in [[jomsburg|the burg]] in good peace, and kept well their laws" (004-005). Their outward lives — summer harrying expeditions, the gathering of "good fee and good renown" — were the direct expression of the code's discipline. The [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] were "held for the greatest champions and men of war, and scarcely was any man thought their match in that time" (004-005). The law created the conditions for that pre-eminence: by eliminating internal conflict, ensuring fair division of spoils, and demanding absolute courage, it forged a fighting force greater than the sum of its individual members.

The law's requirement of personal loyalty also shaped the relationships within [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]. When [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] later sailed from [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] and never returned, he "had bounden Biorn to him with [[oath|oaths]] of brotherhood, and had they been brothers born of one womb they could not have been more bounden in love, nor, indeed, had ever two brothers such true love" (005-002). The [[oath|oaths]] sworn under the law created bonds as strong as blood — a central purpose of the covenant.

### Sigvaldi and the Crooked Counsellor

Not all [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] lived equally by the law's spirit. Sigvaldi, one of the three sons of Strut-Harald, is introduced as a man "by nature from his youth up crooked of counsel" who "loved not direct and open dealings, but was as ready for all deceits and flimflams as an oyster for a fresh tide" (004-033). His presence within the brotherhood — and his later assumption of the captaincy after [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s departure — demonstrates the tension between the law's ideals and the flawed men who swore to uphold it. Sigvaldi's career in the saga becomes a study in how the law could be bent by those who operated within its letter while violating its spirit.


> First, that there should no man be received in that fellowship older than fifty nor any younger than eighteen winters. Nor yet any that would run from another of like strength and like-weaponed with himself. And thirdly, that every man of them should be sworn to avenge another's slaying, as though he were his own brother. Fourthly, that none should kindle slander among men there.
>
> (004-004)

## Historical Context

The historical [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] are known primarily from the *Jomsvikinga saga*, a thirteenth-century Icelandic saga that narrates the founding of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] by [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] (or [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]) and the exploits of its warrior brotherhood. The saga's account of the Jomsviking laws closely parallels the code described in *[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]*, including the age restrictions (no one under eighteen or over fifty), the prohibition on bringing women into the fortress, the requirement to avenge fallen comrades, and the absolute ban on words of fear.

Historians debate whether [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] was a historical place or a literary invention. The fortress is traditionally located on the southern coast of the Baltic Sea, possibly at modern Wolin in Poland — a site that archaeological evidence suggests was indeed a major Viking-age trading centre and stronghold. The semi-legendary nature of the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] [[all-places|places]] them alongside other warrior elites of the Viking Age such as the [[berserk-gang|berserk]]ers and the ulfhednar, though the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] are distinguished by their institutionalised code rather than by ecstatic battle-trance.

The Jomsviking code bears comparison with other warband [[oath|oaths]] known from early medieval Europe. The *comitatus* described by Tacitus in *Germania* — a lord's retinue bound by [[oath|oaths]] of loyalty and shame at surviving their leader in battle — shares structural features with the Jomsburg covenant. Similarly, the Varangian Guard of Byzantium, composed largely of Norse warriors, operated under codes of discipline and mutual obligation. The [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] Law can be understood as a literary idealisation of the warband ethic that governed Viking raiding parties, given institutional form and fixed in a written-like code.

The requirement that all plunder be brought "to the pole" for equal division reflects genuine Norse practice. Runestone inscriptions and saga accounts attest to the practice of *felag* — a fellowship based on shared property and equal division of spoils, from which the modern English word "fellow" derives. The [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] Law elevated this practical arrangement into a sacred covenant, enforced by the ultimate sanction of expulsion — to be "put away and out of their law" (004-004).
