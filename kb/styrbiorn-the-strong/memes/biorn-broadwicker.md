# Biorn the Broadwickers' Champion

## Biography

Biorn Asbrandson of Coombe was an Icelander who came to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] shortly before Styrbiorn himself arrived and was known by the by-name "the Broadwickers' Champion." He was in his twenty-ninth year when he first appears in the saga, a man "of most goodly countenance and pleasant of speech," and by reputation "as big a champion in wooing of women as in holding of his own against men" (005-002). He and Styrbiorn formed an immediate bond, bound by [[oath|oaths]] of brotherhood such that "had they been brothers born there could scarce closer love and friendship have been betwixt them" (005-002).

When Styrbiorn's three years of exile were near their end and he needed a place to pass the winter, Biorn proposed that they guest with [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] in Denmark, where Biorn himself had been well received when he first came east from Iceland. Biorn also pointed out that [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]] had "a daughter young and meet for wedlock" (005-005), nudging Styrbiorn toward a marriage alliance. Styrbiorn accepted the plan, jesting that [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] had fostered [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]'s son, so [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]] should foster them in turn (005-006).

At [[yule-feast-denmark|King Harald's Yule-drinking]], Biorn proved himself a skald of rare talent. He first performed a drapa of twenty stanzas in praise of [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]], which so pleased the King that he gave Biorn "a gold ring weighed twelve ounces" (005-044, 005-046). At Styrbiorn's request, Biorn then recited the Spae-Wife's Lay (the [[spae-wifes-lay|Voluspa]]), an ancient and sacred poem recounting the creation of the world, the wars of the Gods, and the [[norse-fate|doom]] of Ragnarok (005-048 to 005-122). The song's grim prophecy -- particularly the verse about the [[earl-wolf|Wolf]] who "feedeth on the lives of fey men death-doomed" and the coming of the Twilight of the Gods -- disturbed [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] so deeply that he could not sleep the whole night through (006-003).

The morning after the song, [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] the King's daughter sought Biorn out as a trusted intermediary. She asked him to persuade Styrbiorn to leave Denmark, fearing her father's anger and his plans to marry her to the Wend-[[burisleif|king Burisleif]]. Biorn promised to "keep counsel" and to do her bidding, even at the cost of keeping secrets from his foster-brother (006-009 to 006-012). When Thyri later revealed a prophetic dream warning against Styrbiorn's return to Sweden -- "North 'tis, and North 'tis, and ne'er may we linger" (006-037) -- Biorn counselled stoic acceptance of fate, telling her that "none may avoid his fate" and comparing this knowledge to "the due ballast to the ship, which maketh the vessel indeed loom somewhat deeper, but keepeth it from tossing too lightly upon the uncertain waters" (006-040).

Biorn remained by Styrbiorn's side through the difficult winter at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] after the disastrous return from Sweden, following his foster-brother on his long solitary walks and ensuring that the Wendish raiders kept their distance (012-003). He accompanied Styrbiorn on the campaign that cowed [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]] and witnessed the reunion of Styrbiorn and Thyri at [[alaburg|Alaburg]] (012-032).

## Role in the Saga

Biorn serves multiple narrative functions: he is Styrbiorn's closest friend and the emotional anchor of the [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] sections, a bridge between the Icelandic saga tradition and the Swedish-Danish political drama, and a voice of pagan resolution in a court that has abandoned the old gods. His recitation of the Spae-Wife's Lay in Chapter 5 is one of the novel's most thematically loaded passages, casting the entire saga's [[all-events|events]] against the cosmic backdrop of Ragnarok and establishing the doom-laden atmosphere that hangs over Styrbiorn's fate.

His position as trusted confidant to both Styrbiorn and Thyri [[all-places|places]] him at the centre of the novel's romantic and political entanglements, though unlike the other major [[all-persons|characters]] he seems untouched by romantic passion himself -- a man of friendship rather than desire, whose loyalty is given without reserve.

## Key Relationships

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** -- Bound by [[oath|oaths]] of brotherhood from nearly the day Styrbiorn entered [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], Biorn is Styrbiorn's closest friend and constant companion. He counsels, protects, and follows his foster-brother through every crisis (005-002, 012-003).

**Thyri Haraldsdaughter** -- The Dane-King's daughter who trusts Biorn as her intermediary with Styrbiorn. She confides in him her fears of her father's plans and her prophetic dreams, and Biorn's gentleness and discretion earn her confidence (006-001 to 006-042).

**[[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]** -- Biorn won [[harald-bluetooth|the Dane-King]]'s favour with his skaldic art, but his recitation of the pagan [[spae-wifes-lay|Voluspa]] -- at Styrbiorn's request -- deeply unsettled the Christian king and strained the relationship between the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] and the Danish court (005-046, 006-003).

**[[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]** -- Biorn came to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] before Styrbiorn and won his place among the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]]. He sits among the lords at [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s councils (012-012).

## Historical Basis

The character of Biorn Asbrandson of Coombe is based on the figure of Biorn Asbrandsson from the *Eyrbyggja Saga* (chapters XXVIII--XXIX), who is recorded as having voyaged to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] during the time of [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]] and then to Sweden with the [[jomsvikings|Jomsburgers]] for the battle of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] where Styrbiorn fell. The historical Biorn was known as "Biorn the Broadwickers' Champion" (Breidavikurkappi), a man of the Broadwickers' family in Iceland. The saga records that he later voyaged to Vinland and was never heard from again. The novel draws on this saga tradition, making him a more central figure and endowing him with skaldic gifts that bring the great mythological poetry of the Edda into the narrative.
