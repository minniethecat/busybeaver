# Skaldic Lore (Norse Poetry)

## Definition

The skalds were the poets and storytellers of the Norse world — the living vessels of history, religion, and fame. In a culture without widespread literacy, the skald's art was the sole medium by which the deeds of kings and heroes were preserved, the genealogies of great families were remembered, and the sacred lays of the gods were transmitted from generation to generation. The skald was more than an entertainer; he was the keeper of collective memory and the bestower of the sole form of immortality the Norse world recognised: the undying fame that outlasts a man's earthly life.

Skaldic verse took two primary forms. The **drapa** was a formal praise-poem composed for a king or chieftain, elaborate in its metre and rich with kennings — the compressed metaphorical compounds (such as "surf-deer" for ship) that were the hallmark of the skaldic style. The **lay** (*ljod*) was a narrative poem, often of great length, recounting myths, heroic legends, or prophecies.

Both forms were performed aloud in the hall before an assembled company, and a skald's reputation rose or fell on the power of his delivery. As the saga demonstrates, a great lay could hold an entire hall in thrall — kings, warriors, and queens alike — and change the emotional and political weather of a gathering.

In the novel, skaldic lore appears at critical moments, shaping [[norse-fate|the fates]] of the principal [[all-persons|characters]] and serving as a vehicle for the tension between the old Norse religion and the encroaching tide of Christianity. Poetry in this world is not ornament — it is a force that acts upon men and [[all-events|events]].

## Occurrences in the Novel

### Biorn Recites the Spae-Wife's Lay in King Harald's Hall

The most sustained portrayal of skaldic art in the saga occurs in Chapter 5, when [[biorn-broadwicker|Biorn the Broadwickers' Champion]] rises before [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald Gormson]]'s [[hall-feast|Yule-drinking]] in Denmark. [[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]], having heard "that there be good skalds in Iceland," asks Biorn directly: "What hast thou to say to that, Biorn?" (005-041) Biorn answers with pride, declaring that "there shall never be better skalds found than in Iceland, though dale meet knoll" (005-042). The King, intrigued, says: "I think thou must be a skald, Biorn. And that would please me more than aught else, if thou wouldst sing a stave or speak forth some ditty or song of thine" (005-043).

Biorn chooses to recite not a poem of his own making but "the old holy Spae-Wife's Lay which telleth of the beginning of all that is, foretelling also the end thereof, and the ways of the Gods with men" (005-050). The setting is charged with symbolic weight: the hall ablaze with fires rekindled, "the faces of men red with ale and [[hall-feast|feast]]ing and the fires' heat and glare, and the bright light of the fires sparkled back from their eyes and from their rings and collars of gold" (005-050). Styrbiorn has urged Biorn to speak in praise of the old Gods, to "shame these [[danes|Danes]] which regard 'em not" (005-048), in a hall where Christian influence has already suppressed the old rites.

The power of Biorn's recitation is described in mythic terms. The entire company falls into a trancelike stillness: "Stone still sat every man of them while Biorn spake the Spae-Wife's Lay. Only as the song went on men seemed to draw nearer together with a gradual motion not to be seen" (005-072). The lay conjures a sense of supernatural presence, "as if in the murk of it were a myriad watchers, unbeholden yet close, waiting and watching, while Biorn stood forth in the brightness and spoke his Lay" (005-072).

[[harald-bluetooth|King Harald]] sits "hunched up in his beard," gripping his drinking-horn "stock still," his eyes eventually rising to fix "a dark and troubled stare on Styrbiorn" (005-072). Styrbiorn himself is transported: "his nostrils widened, as if in the surging wash and rhythm of the great Lay he heard the sea surge beneath his keel, his surf-deer, sweeping him on to where surf and cliff break together on some unimagined shore" (005-072).

When Biorn finishes, he follows his sacred lay with "his drapa that he had made in praise of Harald the King, and when it was done all thought Biorn was as good a skald as had been known in the [[danish-kingdom|Dane-realm]] these many years as far back as men might remember" (005-046). The King rewards him with "a gold ring weighed twelve ounces" (005-046) — the traditional gift by which a patron acknowledged a skald's excellence.

### The Skald at Styrbiorn's Betrothal Feast

Skaldic performance again takes centre stage in Chapter 9, at the magnificent betrothal [[hall-feast|banquet]] for Styrbiorn and [[queen-thyri|Thyri]] at [[upsala|Upsala]]. As the night grows late and men fall to story-telling and man-matching, [[king-eric|King Eric]] forbids the competitive game as too dangerous for a company drawn from multiple lands and allegiances. He commands instead that "a skald of his sing them somewhat, whether some lay or drapa. And best of all, some old love-song, sith this is a betrothal [[hall-feast|feast]] tonight" (009-023).

The skald obeys and recites the Hell-ride of Brynhild — the tragic love-legend of the Volsung cycle. The performance is rendered with powerful effect: "all were silent listening while the skald spake that lay, for he spake it well and in a manner to touch men's hearts" (009-082). Styrbiorn sits "still, harkening attentively," but his gaze is fixed on [[queen-sigrid|Sigrid the Queen]], who sits opposite him at the King's right hand, "looking down, so that her eyes were hid under their long lazy lashes" (009-082).

The song works its own magic on Styrbiorn: "held with the music of that song, and his thoughts within him were on the sadness of the song, so that, looking on the Queen, he saw not her, but in imagination that Queen of old time, Brynhild" (009-082). In this charged moment, the ancient lay becomes a mirror in which the living drama of the [[hall-feast|feast]] — the unspoken tensions between Styrbiorn, Sigrid, and Thyri — is refracted and intensified.

## Historical Context

In historical Norse culture, the skald occupied a position of high honour and considerable influence. The etymology of the word is uncertain — possibly related to the Old High German *skellan* ("to sound, to ring") or Proto-Germanic *skeldan* ("to scold, to rebuke") — but the role is well attested from the ninth century onward. Skalds were typically attached to the courts of kings and chieftains, where they served as poets, chroniclers, and occasionally diplomats. A skald's praise-poem could cement a king's reputation for generations; equally, a skald's satirical verse (*nid*) was feared as a weapon that could destroy a man's honour.

The skaldic art was distinguished by its extraordinary formal complexity. Unlike the simpler Eddic verse (the alliterative metre of the mythological and heroic lays preserved in the Poetic Edda), skaldic poetry employed intricate *drottkvaett* ("court metre") — an eight-line stanza form with internal rhyme (*hending*), strict syllable counts, and dense kennings that demanded a learned audience to unravel. A kenning for "sea," for example, might be "whale-road" or "sail-steed's path"; for "warrior," "feeder of ravens." This complexity served a dual purpose: it elevated the subject beyond the mundane and it protected the poem from being altered in oral transmission, since any change would break the metrical and alliterative structure.

The two major poems performed in the saga are drawn directly from the Norse poetic corpus. The [[spae-wifes-lay|Voluspa]] ("Spae-Wife's Lay") is the first and most famous poem of the Poetic Edda, recounting the creation and destruction of the cosmos from the lips of a seeress raised from the dead by Odin. The Hell-ride of Brynhild (*Helreid Brynhildar*) is another Eddic poem, in which the dead Valkyrie Brynhild recounts her tragic love story as she rides the road to Hel. Both poems belong to the older Eddic tradition rather than to the technically intricate skaldic style per se, but the saga presents their performance as the work of skalds, reflecting the popular understanding of the skald as the bearer of all poetic tradition.

By the time of the saga's setting (the late tenth century), the skaldic tradition was at its height. Icelandic skalds were especially prized at Scandinavian courts, as Biorn's boast affirms (005-042). The tradition would survive the conversion to Christianity, though its subject matter shifted from pagan mythology to Christian [[all-concepts|themes]] and chivalric romance. The last great skald of the classical period was Snorri Sturluson (1179–1241), whose *Prose Edda* preserved the rules of skaldic composition for posterity and whose *Heimskringla* incorporated skaldic verse as historical evidence.
