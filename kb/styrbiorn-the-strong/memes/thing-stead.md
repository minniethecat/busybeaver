## Definition

The Thing (Old Norse "þing") was the governing assembly in Norse and Germanic societies, where free men gathered to debate matters of law, settle disputes, elect or acclaim kings, and render judgments. The Thing-stead was the physical location where such assemblies were held — a sacred, hallowed space set apart from ordinary dwellings. By Norse custom, the Thing-stead was held to be under the protection of the gods, and violence or law-breaking within its bounds was considered sacrilege. In the novel, the Thing-stead at [[upsala|Upsala]] forms part of a sacred precinct alongside the temple and the King's garth (royal enclosure).


> Earl Wolf stood up and went betwixt the benches and the fires and forth of the main door into the King's garth, and forth of the garth past the houses of men and the Thing-stead and the temple till he was come to the open field. It was murk and wild weather, and evening drawing in. Like a high house in bigness, Olaf's howe reared against the fading light.
>
> (001-009)

## Occurrences in the Novel

The Thing-stead at [[upsala|Upsala]] is first mentioned in Chapter 1 as [[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf]] departs the King's garth. The Earl passes "past the houses of men and the Thing-stead and the temple" on his way to Olaf's howe (001-009), establishing the Thing-stead as one of the three key landmarks of the [[upsala|Upsala]] sacred complex. The spatial sequence — houses of men, Thing-stead, temple — suggests the Thing-stead sat between the secular dwellings and the holy temple precinct, a liminal space where the political and sacred realms intersected.

The Thing-stead appears most prominently in connection with two assemblies summoned by [[king-eric|King Eric]]. The first, described in Chapter 2, was called unlawfully in the King's absence by bonders hostile to Styrbiorn. At this assembly, "great uproar among the bonders" led to the proclamation of [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]] as a rival king and the Thing was "broken up in a tumult" (002-004). [[earl-wolf|Earl Wolf]] later recounts being driven from the assembly with stones and muck (002-011), and [[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]] confirms the Thing "broke up in an uproar" (002-046). The King was outraged that such an assembly was held without his authority (002-030).

The second assembly was lawfully summoned by [[king-eric|King Eric]] in Chapter 9, where he intended "with all lawful ceremonies and before the face of the Swedes in lawful Thing assembled" to grant Styrbiorn half the kingdom (009-001). Later, as Styrbiorn's invasion threatened, the King again "let hallow the holy Thing" and gathered "the throng of the bonders and the common sort and the King's men and the following of the Earls and lords" in the Thing-mead before [[upsala|Upsala]] to prepare the realm's defense (014-007).

## Historical Context

In historical Norse society, the Thing was the central institution of governance. Each district had its own Thing, and higher-level Things existed for larger regions — the [[upsala|Uppsala]] Thing was among the most important in Sweden. The assembly was presided over by a law-speaker (Old Norse "logsogumadr"), a role embodied in the novel by [[thorgnyr-lawman|Thorgnyr the Lawman]], who had served the Swedish kings for forty years (010-023). Free landholding men (bonders) had the right to attend, speak, and vote. Kings could not rule without the consent of the Thing, and the bonders' power to acclaim or reject a king was a real political force — as demonstrated when they proclaimed [[lambi-the-white|Lambi the White]] as a rival to Styrbiorn (002-004).

The physical Thing-stead was commonly marked by natural features or standing stones and was considered a site of sacred truce. The proximity of the Thing-stead to the temple at [[upsala|Upsala]] reflects the deeply intertwined nature of law and religion in Norse culture: the Thing was hallowed, and its proceedings carried both legal and ritual weight.
