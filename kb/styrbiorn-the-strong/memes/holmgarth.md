# Holmgarth

## Description

Holmgarth — the Old Norse *Holmgarðr*, the historical Novgorod — is the great Varangian trading city of the eastern route, the furthest reach of the Viking world into the lands of the Rus. In Eddison's telling it is a place of wealth and princely power, a city where exiled princes find hosts, where princesses give gifts of gold and silks, and from which young warriors ride west to join the fellowship of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]. It is the eastern pole of Styrbiorn's world, the counterweight to [[upsala|Upsala]] in the west — a city known to every viking who raided the coasts of [[biarmaland|Biarmaland]] and Balagarth-Side during the summer months (004-022).

The novel does not linger on physical description of Holmgarth's streets or halls; it is evoked instead through its [[all-persons|people]] and its place in the [[all-places|geography]] of the saga. It is "east overseas" from Sweden, reached by crossing the Keel — the mountainous spine of Scandinavia — and then taking ship eastward across the Baltic and beyond (004-038). It belongs to the "[[garthrealm|Garth realm]]" that men spoke of alongside [[biarmaland|Biarmaland]] and [[wendland|Wendland]] and [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], a world of eastern principalities and river-roads known to the Norse traders and raiders who plied the Austrvegr (007-041).

The city's character emerges through the quality of its inhabitants: it is a seat of princes, not of sea-kings. The great Prince's son Valdimar comes to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] not as a viking but as a lord, and the princess of Holmgarth dispenses gifts of "cramoisy silk and gold" — treasures of the eastern trade routes rather than the plunder of the western seas (011-001). Holmgarth represents a different kind of power: settled, princely, connected to the overland trade that brought silk from Byzantium and silver from the Caliphate into the North.

## Role

Holmgarth enters the saga as the place of Styrbiorn's exile — a refuge eastward when the west had closed its doors. Before his coming to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]], Styrbiorn had been "guesting east in Holmgarth," and from there he and his company had "harried summer-long in [[biarmaland|Biarmaland]] and Balagarth-Side" (004-022). This eastern sojourn is the shadow-period of Styrbiorn's life, the years between his father's death and his emergence as a Jomsviking captain, and Holmgarth is its centre.

The city's significance deepens through its representatives at [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]. Valdimar, "the great Prince's son of Holmgarth," appears among the lords and princes of the east who have joined [[palnatoki|Palnatoki]]'s brotherhood (008-011). Alongside him stands Ere-Skeggi, "young brother to an Earl of the east country," who was likewise from Holmgarth (008-011). These eastern lords bring to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] a different temper from the Danish and Swedish vikings: they are princes by birth, not sea-raiders by trade, and their presence testifies to the magnetic pull of Styrbiorn's cause across the whole Norse world.

[[thorir|Thorir]]'s teasing question to Biorn Broadwicker — "How? Is not princesses in Holmgarth enough for him?" — reveals that Styrbiorn's eastern exile was more than a military venture (007-009). There was a princess in Holmgarth, and she had given him gifts: "cramoisy silk and gold which the princess of Holmgarth had given him in days gone by" (011-001). The relationship is never fully explicated — Eddison works by indirection — but it is clear enough that the men of [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] knew of it and could jest about it.

The eastern connection reaches its culmination at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]]. Valdimar of Holmgarth marches with Styrbiorn's host into the final battle and falls "with a spear-wound in the flank" (015-018). Ere-Skeggi stands among the named warriors who "lay that day on the field of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]]," alongside Bessi, Gunnstein, and Valdimar himself (015-011). The men of Holmgarth, who had travelled from the furthest edge of the Norse world to fight for Styrbiorn, gave their lives for his cause on the water-meadows below [[upsala|Upsala]].

Throughout the saga, Holmgarth serves as a measure of Styrbiorn's reach and reputation. As the [[jomsvikings|Jomsvikings]] tell it: "In Holmgarth and the east he hath overborne all. [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]] he holdeth in his hand" (008-033). The city is both the starting-point of his Viking career and proof of the breadth of his dominion. When Styrbiorn's men speak of his power, Holmgarth is the word they use first.

## Associated Characters

**Valdimar of Holmgarth** — The great Prince's son of Holmgarth, one of the eastern lords who joined the Jomsviking fellowship and fought for Styrbiorn. He fell at the [[battle-of-fyrisfield|Battle of Fyrisfield]], struck by a spear in the flank (008-011, 015-018).

**Ere-Skeggi** — Young brother to an Earl of the east country, from Holmgarth. He was among the named warriors who lay dead on the field of [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] (008-011, 015-011).

**[[styrbiorn|Styrbiorn the Strong]]** — The exiled Swedish prince who found refuge in Holmgarth before coming to [[jomsburg|Jomsburg]]. His eastern sojourn with the Holmgarth company marked the first stage of his transformation from landless heir to viking captain (004-022, 004-038).

**The Princess of Holmgarth** — Never named in the text, she gave Styrbiorn gifts of cramoisy silk and gold during his time in the east. [[thorir|Thorir]]'s jest suggests she was something more than a casual acquaintance (007-009, 011-001).

**Biorn Broadwicker** — Styrbiorn's foster-brother, who accompanied him on the eastern journey to Holmgarth and remained at his side through all that followed (004-022, 004-038).

## Historical Context

Holmgarth is the Old Norse name for Novgorod (from *holmr* "island" + *garðr* "enclosure, fortified settlement"), one of the most significant urban centres of early Rus and a crucial node in the Varangian trade network that connected Scandinavia to Byzantium and the Islamic world. Situated on the Volkhov River near Lake Ilmen, Novgorod was founded — according to the *Primary Chronicle* — in the mid-9th century and quickly became a major commercial and political centre under the Rurikid dynasty.

The term "Holmgarth" appears widely in Norse sources, including runic inscriptions and the sagas, reflecting the well-established Scandinavian presence in the region during the Viking Age. Norse traders, mercenaries, and adventurers — the Varangians — used the river systems of what is now Russia and Ukraine as highways to the Black Sea and the Caspian, and Novgorod was the gateway to this eastern world. The "[[garthrealm|Garth realm]]" mentioned in the novel reflects the Norse conception of *[[garthrealm|Garðaríki]]*, the "realm of towns," their name for the territories of the Rus.

Eddison's portrait of Holmgarth as a seat of princes rather than sea-kings is historically apt. Novgorod was governed by a prince (*knyaz*) who ruled in concert with the city's veche (assembly), and its wealth derived from trade and [[scat-tribute|tribute]] rather than raiding. The presence of a "princess" dispensing silk and gold accords with the archaeological record: Novgorod has yielded rich finds of Byzantine silks, Islamic silver dirhams, and goods from across the known world, attesting to its role as a great emporium of the east-west trade.

Valdimar of Holmgarth's name is suggestive. *Valdimar* (Old Norse *Valdimarr*) is the Norse form of the Slavic name Vladimir, borne by several rulers of the Rus — most famously Vladimir the Great (Vladimir I of Kiev, r. 980–1015), who was roughly contemporary with the [[all-events|events]] of the saga. Whether Eddison intended a direct historical reference or simply borrowed the name for verisimilitude, the echo gives Valdimar of Holmgarth a resonance with the real rulers of the eastern Norse world.

Eddison's geographical imagination, which ranges from the Elf River in the west to the [[garthrealm|Garth realm]] in the east, encompasses the full extent of the Viking world. Holmgarth marks the eastern limit of that world, and the fact that men travel from there to fight and die for Styrbiorn at [[fyrisfield|Fyrisfield]] is a measure of how far the saga's reach extends.
