# I to Hercules

*I to Hercules* is a novel by [[john-munting-statement|John Munting]] that became an unexpected bestseller in 1929. The book's publication and subsequent success form a significant subplot within the documentary record, illuminating [[john-munting|Munting]]'s literary career and the social circles that intersected with the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] case.

The novel's title and content — touching on science, religion, and the Victorian worldview — reflect the intellectual preoccupations of [[john-munting|Munting]]'s circle. Its success brought [[john-munting|Munting]] public recognition that would later colour press coverage of his involvement in the investigation.

## Publication and Success

[[john-munting|Munting]] announced the forthcoming publication in a letter: "*I to Hercules* comes out next month. I hope old Merritt won't be let down over it. He continues to be enthusiastic" [PN=028-012]. The reference to Merritt — presumably his publisher — suggests cautious optimism rather than expectation of the phenomenon to come.

The book's reception exceeded all predictions. In a subsequent letter, [[john-munting|Munting]] described the extraordinary response:

> "First edition sold out. Presses rolling out new printings day and night — Merritt nearly off his head and saying, 'I told you so.'" [PN=030-005]

The tone of [[john-munting|Munting]]'s account is one of bewildered delight: "what have I done that I should be a bestseller? Is thy servant a tripe-hound that he should do this thing?" [PN=030-005].

Publishers who had previously rejected the manuscript — "publishers who turned *Hercules* down" — now scrambled to secure [[john-munting|Munting]]'s next contract, a reversal he noted with sardonic satisfaction [PN=030-005].

## Content and Themes

The novel engaged with the intellectual debates of its time. [[john-munting|Munting]]'s research for the book involved consultation with a mathematically trained clergyman about "Victorian materialism" and the relationship between science and religion [PN around 704]. Topics under discussion included Einstein's theories and the philosophical implications of modern physics.

The blend of scientific and religious themes — "Glands or God — Which?" as one press inquiry put it — captured the public imagination [PN=030-005]. [[john-munting|Munting]]'s parson contact observed that "scientists would at last allow him to believe what the Church taught," a reconciliation [[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|the novel]] appears to have dramatised [PN=030-005].

## Public Identity

The novel's success attached itself permanently to [[john-munting|Munting]]'s public identity. When the [[morning-express|Morning Express]] reported on the [[the-inquest|Harrison inquest]], it identified the witness as "Mr. [[john-munting|John Munting]], author of *I to Hercules*" [PN=048-006]. The epithet followed him through the case, framing his involvement for the reading public.

The success also brought media attention that extended beyond literary circles. [[john-munting|Munting]] found himself "besieged in his charming Bayswater flat" by press agencies, literary hostesses, and an "American lion-tamer" — a comic exaggeration that captures the absurdity of sudden celebrity [PN=030-005].

## Portrait Connection

The bestselling author's portrait was painted by [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]], described as "that brilliant young artist" who completed the work "in a fit of boredom one afternoon when the model hadn't turned up" [PN=030-005]. The portrait — and [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s casual approach to it — underscores the overlapping social world of artists and writers in which both [[john-munting|Munting]] and [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] moved.
