# Professor Hoskyns

## Overview

Professor Hoskyns is a distinguished physicist and an old Oxford contemporary of [[mr-perry|Mr. Perry]]. He appears only in the final chapter, at a dinner gathering at Perry's home, where he participates in a wide-ranging philosophical discussion about science, religion, and the nature of causality.

## The Dinner at Perry's

In the final chapter, [[mr-perry|Perry]] invites [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]] to dinner to meet Hoskyns: "An old college friend of his, the extremely celebrated Professor Hoskyns, was coming over to spend the evening with him. Hoskyns is, of course, a very brilliant physicist, and Perry thought it would interest me to meet him" [PN=053-022].

Hoskyns is described as "long, thin, bald, and much more human-looking than his Press photographs" [PN=053-023]. He is installed in a "broken-springed leather armchair" and calls Perry "Jim" — indicating the familiarity of old college friends [PN=053-023].

## The Discussion

The dinner conversation ranges across science, religion, and determinism. Also present are [[professor-matthews|Professor Matthews]] (a biologist), Waters (a chemist), and the new curate. The discussion explores the tension between scientific and religious worldviews, with Perry arguing that modern science reinforces rather than undermines religious truths.

Hoskyns serves as a representative of the scientific establishment — eminent, recognized, and comfortable enough in his views to banter with his old theological friend. His presence gives weight to the intellectual circle that Paul Harrison moves within in the aftermath of the case.
