# The Circumstantial Case

The case against [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]] for the murder of [[george-harrison|George Harrison]] was built entirely on circumstantial evidence. There was no eyewitness to the poisoning, no confession, and no direct proof of the act — only a chain of inferences drawn from documents, chemical analysis, and the logic of motive and opportunity.

## The Evidence Chain

The prosecution's case rested on several linked inferences. The poison was identified as [[synthetic-muscarine|synthetic muscarine]] — produced in a laboratory, not extracted from a mushroom [PN=052-152]. Lathom had the knowledge to obtain or produce such a substance. He had the opportunity to administer it. And he had the motive: his relationship with [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]] was blocked by George Harrison's existence, and [[divorce-question|divorce was not a viable option]].

## The Documentary Foundation

Unusually for a murder case, much of the evidence was documentary. Letters, statements, and notes formed the primary record. [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]]'s [[the-dossier|dossier]] compiled this material and demonstrated how the documents themselves — their dates, their handwriting, their tone — could serve as evidence [PN=049-153].

## The Gap in the Chain

The circumstantial case had one notable gap: the means by which Lathom obtained synthetic muscarine. The link between the chemical knowledge demonstrated at the dinner party and the actual procurement of the poison was never directly established in the documentary record. The conviction rested on the cumulative weight of inference rather than a single decisive piece of proof.

## Legal and Literary Significance

The Harrison case exemplifies both the power and the limitation of circumstantial evidence. The chain of inference was strong enough to convict, but the absence of direct proof left permanent uncertainty about the exact mechanism of the crime — a ambiguity that is central to the novel's enduring fascination.
