# Jack Munting

## Overview

Jack Munting is [[john-munting|John Munting]]'s brother, mentioned in passing in Munting's letters to [[elizabeth-drake|Elizabeth Drake]]. He appears indirectly as the addressee of Munting's self-admonitions and as a point of contrast in the investigation into the Harrison affair.

## Mentions in the Text

John Munting twice addresses himself with the phrase "Pull yourself together, Jack Munting!" — once in a moment of emotional confession about jealousy [PN=026-001], and again when he accuses himself of becoming "hysterical" with his "glands functioning madly in the wrong places" [PN=026-003]. These self-directed exhortations reveal Munting's habit of using his own name as a form of self-discipline.

Later, when [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]] questions [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]] about an alleged intimacy between Munting and [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]], Lathom firmly denies it, saying: "On my word of honour, there was not the very slightest attachment or understanding of any kind between Mrs. Harrison and Jack Munting. They rather disliked each other, if anything. Jack was married last Easter to a very charming woman, with whom he is much in love" [PN=049-253].

## Significance

Jack Munting's name is used here by Lathom to emphasize personal knowledge — calling him "Jack" rather than "John" or "Munting" signals the closeness of their friendship. The information that Jack "was married last Easter" provides a timeline detail and confirms that Munting had no romantic entanglement with Margaret Harrison.
