# Dr. Hughes

Dr. Hughes was the physician of [[bovey-tracey|Bovey Tracey]], [[manaton|Devon]], who served as the primary medical examiner in the [[george-harrison|George Harrison]] murder case. He performed the autopsy on [[george-harrison|Harrison]]'s body and provided key medical evidence at [[the-inquest|the inquest]].

Described as "a brisk and confident young man," Dr. Hughes represented the modern, science-minded medical profession that was transforming forensic investigation in the late 1920s [PN=045-139]. His findings formed the medical foundation of the case.

## Role in the Investigation

Dr. Hughes first appears in the documentary record when [[john-munting-statement|John Munting]] arrived in [[bovey-tracey|Bovey Tracey]]. [[john-munting|Munting]] "communicated at once with Dr. Hughes, and proceeded in the doctor's company" to examine the circumstances of [[death-of-george-harrison|Harrison's death]] [PN=047-044].

The doctor had already taken charge of the body: "he had had the body removed to [[bovey-tracey|Bovey Tracey]] for the purpose of an autopsy" [PN=047-049]. His preliminary examination established that death was not from natural causes, setting the investigation in motion.

> "Dr. Hughes of Bovey Tracey, who performed the autopsy, stated that death was due to heart failure following upon acute congestion of the lungs." [PN=048-023]

## Medical Evidence

Dr. Hughes's autopsy findings were detailed and precise. He "gave evidence that he had examined the body externally and internally" and "added that there were no external marks of violence" [PN=047-049, PN=047-053]. This eliminated physical trauma as the cause of death and pointed toward poisoning.

His observations about the condition of the organs — particularly the congestion of the lungs — provided the clinical picture that, combined with [[chemical-analysis-report|the chemical analysis]] by [[james-lubbock|Sir James Lubbock]], established [[muscarine-poisoning|muscarine poisoning]] as the definitive cause of death.

## Personality and Manner

[[john-munting|Munting]]'s account captures something of Dr. Hughes's character. When [[john-munting|Munting]] made a casual remark about the case, the doctor responded "with a sort of grim smile" — suggesting a man accustomed to the darker realities of medical practice [PN=045-144].

His brisk confidence and professional competence made him a reliable witness. The documentary record contains no challenge to his findings or his handling of the autopsy — unusual in a case where so much other evidence was contested.
