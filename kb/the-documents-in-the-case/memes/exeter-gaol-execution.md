# The Execution at Exeter Gaol

The execution at Exeter Gaol was the final event in the Harrison poisoning case. [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]], convicted of the murder of [[george-harrison|George Harrison]], was hanged at 8 a.m. at the Devon prison [PN=053-013].

## The Sentence

Following the trial and [[the-conviction|conviction]], Lathom was sentenced to death. Despite the possibility of an appeal, the sentence was carried out. The execution represented the final chapter in a case that had begun with a mushroom dinner and unfolded through a complex web of correspondence, forensic analysis, and legal proceedings.

## Location

Exeter Gaol, the county prison for Devon, was the site of the execution. Its use reflected the Devon jurisdiction of the crime — both the poisoning at [[the-shack-manaton|The Shack]] and the subsequent inquest at [[bovey-tracey|Bovey Tracey]] fell within the Devon authorities' purview.

## Narrative Function

The execution note — a brief newspaper-style announcement appended to the final chapter of [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]]'s dossier — serves as the stark conclusion to the documentary narrative. After hundreds of pages of correspondence, speculation, and investigation, the case is reduced to a single paragraph: time, place, and the carrying out of the sentence.

The execution marks the end of the legal process but leaves unresolved the moral questions raised by the case: the nature of [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]]'s involvement, the reliability of documentary evidence, and the limits of justice in cases built on circumstantial proof and chemical analysis rather than direct testimony.
