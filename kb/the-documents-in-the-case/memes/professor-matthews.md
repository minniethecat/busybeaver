# Professor Matthews

## Overview

Professor Matthews, nicknamed "Stingo," is a biologist who has "done so much work on heredity" [PN=053-023]. He is an Oxford contemporary of [[mr-perry|Mr. Perry]] and [[professor-hoskyns|Professor Hoskyns]], and appears at the dinner gathering in the final chapter.

## The Dinner at Perry's

Matthews is described as "a swarthy little man in spectacles" [PN=053-023]. His nickname "Stingo" is used affectionately by both Hoskyns and Perry, indicating their long friendship dating back to Oxford days [PN=053-023].

He brings with him Waters, "the coming man in chemistry," with whom he is on "terms of the heartiest friendship and disagreement" — a phrase that captures the collegial friction of academic life [PN=053-023].

## In the Discussion

During the dinner-table debate, Perry uses Matthews as an example of how science echoes ancient religious teachings: "Look at Stingo here. He tells them that if two unfit people marry, their unfitness will be visited on their children unto the third and fourth generation, after which they will probably die out through mere degeneration. We've been telling them that for three or four thousand years, and Matthews has only just caught up to us" [PN=053-026].

This places Matthews's work on heredity in direct conversation with biblical concepts of generational consequences — a key theme in a case concerned with how the sins of one generation reverberate through the lives of others.

Later in the discussion, Perry argues that all life is interconnected: "Can you separate the child from the father, the man from the brute, or even the man from the vegetable cell, Stingo?" [PN=053-030] — directly addressing Matthews by his nickname.
