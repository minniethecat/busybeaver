# Mushroom Identification

Mushroom identification — the practice of distinguishing edible from poisonous fungi — lies at the heart of the Harrison case. [[george-harrison|George Harrison]]'s expertise as an amateur mycologist was both his passion and, potentially, the instrument of his death.

## Harrison's Expertise

George Harrison was an experienced fungi forager who was writing a book on the subject, *[[neglected-edible-treasures|Neglected Edible Treasures]]*, illustrated with his own [[george-harrison-watercolours|watercolours]] [PN=020-008]. He regularly gathered wild mushrooms and was familiar with the distinguishing characteristics of local species.

His expertise was sufficient that [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]] noted his obsession, complaining that he talked "interminably about his eternal fungi" [PN=018-006]. He roamed the Devon countryside on his annual "camp," collecting specimens and painting watercolour "bits" [PN=045-002].

## The Deadly Mistake Hypothesis

The official verdict — accidental death — rested on the assumption that Harrison had misidentified a poisonous mushroom as an edible one. The [[amanita-muscaria|Amanita muscaria]] (fly agaric), with its distinctive red cap with white spots, contains muscarine — the alkaloid identified in Harrison's remains by [[james-lubbock|Sir James Lubbock]] [PN=048-018].

For an experienced mycologist to make such an error would be surprising. Fly agaric is one of the most recognisable fungi in the European countryside. The implausibility of the misidentification was one of the first hints that the death might not be accidental.

## The Synthetic Alternative

The alternative explanation — developed through the dinner-party conversation in the final chapter — was that Harrison had not misidentified a mushroom at all. The muscarine that killed him was [[synthetic-muscarine|synthetic]], produced in a laboratory and administered deliberately [PN=052-183]. Under this theory, Harrison's expertise was irrelevant — he was the victim of a calculated poisoning disguised as a foraging accident.
