# Margaret Harrison's Letters to Lathom

## Summary

Between May and September 1929, [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]] wrote a series of passionate, intimate letters to [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]], the young artist with whom she was having an adulterous affair. The letters — preserved in the case file as chapters 38 through 44 and chapter 46 — reveal the depth of their relationship and Margaret's growing desperation. She addresses Lathom as "Petra" (a private nickname) and signs herself "Lolo." The letters form a crucial thread in the documentary evidence, exposing the emotional landscape behind [[george-harrison|George Harrison]]'s death.

## Key Letters

### Letter of May 4, 1929 (Chapter 38)

In her first preserved letter, Margaret describes her reunion with Lathom "under the Gorgon's eye" — her private name for her husband George Harrison [PN=038-001]. She speaks of feeling "dead" during the months of their separation: "I had been dead all through those dreadful months. When you went away, I felt as if the big frost had got right into my heart" [PN=038-003].

She appeals to Lathom not to leave her alone again: "We can't go on like this, can we? I couldn't go through those long, long weeks again without seeing or hearing you, not so much as your dear untidy writing on an envelope" [PN=038-004].

### Letter of June 6, 1929 (Chapter 39)

Margaret responds to Lathom's suggestion that she leave her husband: "You think in your dear, impetuous way that it would all be so easy, but it wouldn't really, darling" [PN=039-002]. She reflects on the irony of her situation — if she hadn't married Harrison, she would never have met Lathom: "If I hadn't married him, I should never have lived here, never met you, and oh, darling, what could make up for that?" [PN=039-004].

### Subsequent Letters (Chapters 40–44, 46)

The letters continue through the summer of 1929, with Margaret's tone shifting between passionate devotion and despair. By chapter 46 (her final letter), she is writing in the aftermath of George Harrison's death, and the correspondence reveals the emotional pressure that had been building throughout the affair.

## Evidentiary Significance

These letters are significant for several reasons:

- **Motive**: They establish that Margaret Harrison had a powerful emotional motive to be free of her husband. She repeatedly describes him as a "Gorgon" — cold, stony, and oppressive.
- **State of mind**: The letters provide direct insight into Margaret's psychological state in the months leading up to Harrison's death.
- **Relationship with Lathom**: They document the intensity and physical nature of the affair between Margaret and Lathom.
- **Paul Harrison's investigation**: [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]] later cites one of these letters as evidence that "shortly before the time when he told you the affair was over, Margaret Harrison had written him a letter clearly indicating that she believed herself to be about to have a child by him" [PN=049-273].

The letters are preserved in full within the case file, constituting a remarkable documentary record of the emotional dynamics underlying the crime.
