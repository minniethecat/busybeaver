# Epistolary Form

The epistolary form — storytelling through letters, documents, and other written communications — is the defining narrative technique of *[[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|The Documents in the Case]]*. The entire novel is constructed as a dossier of correspondence, statements, and press extracts, with no omniscient narrator.

## The Narrative Structure

The novel unfolds entirely through primary documents: letters from [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]] to [[olive-farebrother|Olive Farebrother]], letters from [[john-munting|John Munting]] to [[elizabeth-drake|Elizabeth Drake]], letters from [[george-harrison|George Harrison]] to [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]], statements by Paul Harrison and John Munting, and extracts from the [[morning-express|Morning Express]].

This structure means the reader experiences the story as its participants do — through fragments of information arriving at different times, from different perspectives, with different degrees of reliability. No single voice controls the narrative.

## Advantages of the Form

The epistolary form is particularly suited to a story about evidence and interpretation. The reader, like [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]], must sift through documents, compare accounts, and construct a coherent narrative from partial and sometimes contradictory sources [PN=049-153].

The form also creates dramatic irony: the reader can see connections between correspondences that the individual letter-writers cannot, particularly the parallel observations of tenants at [[15-whittington-terrace|15 Whittington Terrace]].

## Innovation in the Harrison Case

Dorothy L. Sayers and [[robert-eustace|Robert Eustace]] used the epistolary form to create a detective story in which the reader must actively participate in the detection. The clues are embedded in the documents themselves — the handwriting, the dates, the tone of voice, and the gaps between letters — rather than being discovered by a detective character.
