# Sir Maurice Berkeley

## Overview

Sir Maurice Berkeley is a distinguished engineer and [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]]'s professional superior. During the events of the case, Paul Harrison is working under Sir Maurice on a bridge construction project in Central Africa. George Harrison expresses great pride in his son's position, calling Sir Maurice "the greatest engineer of his day" [PN=015-005].

## Relationship to the Harrisons

[[george-harrison|George Harrison]] wrote to his son in a letter dated December 1928, expressing his pleasure at Paul's work: "Yours is a great opportunity. I know how proud and happy I should have been at your age to have the advantage of working under so distinguished a man as Sir Maurice" [PN=015-005].

George Harrison later refers to Sir Maurice again in October 1929, approving Paul's handling of a disciplinary matter: "I entirely approve your decision, and so, I am sure, must Sir Maurice, if the matter comes to his notice" [PN=015-005]. This suggests Sir Maurice's judgment carries decisive weight in both professional and personal matters.

## As a Measure of Trust

Paul Harrison invokes Sir Maurice's name as a standard of reliability when defending his father's expertise: "I would as cheerfully stake my life on the wholesomeness of a dish prepared by my father as on the stability of a girder-stress calculated by my chief, Sir Maurice Berkeley" [PN=049-248]. This comparison — equating his father's mycological knowledge to an engineer's structural calculations — reflects Paul's professional mindset and his deep trust in expert knowledge over personal testimony.
