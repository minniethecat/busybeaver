# The Divorce Question

The divorce question haunted the relationship between [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]] and [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]]. While their [[the-affair|affair]] was known or suspected by multiple correspondents, the prospect of a legal divorce — and remarriage — was foreclosed by social convention and practical impossibility.

## Margaret's Calculation

Margaret Harrison acknowledged the reality directly in her letters to Lathom. She knew of a woman who had obtained a divorce, but noted that "her husband took the blame" — implying that Lathom would not or could not do the same [PN=039-005].

Her letters reveal a woman trapped between desire and social ruination: "If I love you I ought to let him divorce me. Darling, do think how horrible it would be" [PN=040-004]. The "stain of the divorce courts" and the prospect of seeing her name in the papers was unbearable to her [PN=040-004].

## The Time Problem

Even if a divorce could be obtained, it would not come quickly enough. "Even if we got a divorce, it wouldn't be in time" [PN=044-005] — Margaret's words suggest that external pressures or Lathom's circumstances made delay fatal to their plans.

## The Murder Implication

The impossibility of a timely, socially acceptable divorce is one of the strongest circumstantial indicators of motive in the Harrison case. If [[george-harrison|George Harrison]] could not be divorced, the only way to free Margaret for remarriage was his death.

This logic, while never stated explicitly in the correspondence, hangs over the later chapters of the narrative. The divorce question transforms what might have been a conventional romantic entanglement into a potential motive for murder.
