# George Harrison's Watercolours

George Harrison's watercolour painting was a central personal pursuit mentioned throughout the correspondence in *The Documents in the Case*. His botanical illustrations of plants and fungi in their natural habitat reveal his character as a meticulous observer and an eccentric enthusiast.

## The Artistic Practice

Harrison "dabbled" in watercolours and produced illustrations of plants and fungi [PN=005-007]. His work was described as showing a careful attention to colour accuracy — he was distressed when a picture of fungi "came out too green by three-quarters of a tone" [PN=027-005].

The watercolours were intended to illustrate a book he was writing on edible fungi, which would eventually be published as *[[neglected-edible-treasures|Neglected Edible Treasures]]* [PN=020-008]. His illustrations of plants and fungi were noted by observers to be "beautifully accurate in line" [PN=027-006].

## Character Revealed

Harrison's watercolours served as a constant point of reference for those around him. [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]] observed that he "goes in for dabbling in watercolours" [PN=005-007]. [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]] later reflected that his watercolours and his cookery were his "one means of self-expression" [PN=037-016].

[[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]], who shared an interest in art, initially connected with Harrison through the watercolours — Harrison brought them out for Lathom to examine on his first visit [PN=003-007]. This artistic common ground set the stage for Lathom's deepening involvement with the Harrison household.

## The "Prim" Criticism

Harrison's watercolours, though technically accurate, were also the subject of criticism. They were described as "too prim considered as works of art" [PN=027-006] — competent in draftsmanship but lacking the expressive quality of a true artist. This criticism echoed broader assessments of Harrison's character: precise, methodical, and somewhat constrained.

## The Annual Camp

Harrison took an annual "camp" to sketch watercolour "bits" and gather natural specimens in the Devon countryside [PN=045-002]. These expeditions combined his interests in painting, fungi hunting, and outdoor rambling — the full expression of his eccentric passions.

## After His Death

After Harrison's death, [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]] found a number of his father's delicate watercolours pinned up in his study [PN=049-365]. These surviving works stood as a testament to the gentle, absorbed side of a man whose death would become the subject of a criminal investigation.
