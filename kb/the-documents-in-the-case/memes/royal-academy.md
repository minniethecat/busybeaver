# Royal Academy

## Overview

The Royal Academy of Arts in London is where [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]] exhibited his portrait of [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]] in the spring of 1929. The exhibition of this portrait plays a key role in the social dynamics of the case.

## The Portrait Exhibition

Lathom testified at [[the-inquest|the inquest]] that he "had painted a portrait of Mrs. Harrison, which had been exhibited in the spring of 1929 at the Royal Academy" [PN=047-060]. This confirms the formal, public nature of the portrait — it was not a private work but one submitted to the most prestigious exhibition venue in Britain.

The portrait had been the subject of earlier tension between [[george-harrison|George Harrison]] and his wife. George Harrison initially objected to the portrait being shown: "I did not care about the project. It is the kind of picture to attract a good deal of comment of one sort and another, and these young people do not quite see the amount of undesirable publicity it might involve" [PN=022-011].

Despite these objections, the portrait was exhibited. [[john-munting|John Munting]] later mentions seeing Lathom at the Royal Academy after the events of the case: "I've only seen him once since he was married, and that was at the Royal Academy" [PN=049-270].

## Significance

The Royal Academy exhibition places the personal drama of the Harrison household in a public, institutional context. Lathom's ambition as a professional artist — seeking Academy recognition — is part of what makes his relationship with Margaret Harrison both possible and dangerous. The portrait is at once a work of art, evidence of intimacy, and a career stepping-stone.
