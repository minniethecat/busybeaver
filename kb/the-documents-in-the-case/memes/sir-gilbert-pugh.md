# Sir Gilbert Pugh

## Overview

Sir Gilbert Pugh is the Director of Public Prosecutions who reviews [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]]'s manuscript account of [[death-of-george-harrison|his father's death]]. He appears only at the very end of the case documents, reading through the evidence that Paul Harrison has assembled.

## Role in the Case

Sir Gilbert does not appear in the events of the case itself. Rather, his role is as the final reader — the external legal authority to whom Paul Harrison's account is addressed. The letter from Paul Harrison to Sir Gilbert forms the outermost frame of the documentary evidence: "Paul Harrison to Sir Gilbert Pugh" [PN=001-001].

At the conclusion, we see Sir Gilbert finishing the manuscript: "Sir Gilbert Pugh, Director of Public Prosecutions, turned the last page of the manuscript, and sat for a few minutes in silence" [PN=053-001]. He mentally anticipates the difficulty of presenting expert scientific testimony to a jury: "Mentally he watched his expert witnesses displaying an asymmetric molecule to a jury of honest tradesmen, under a withering fire of commentary by the counsel for the defence" [PN=053-001].

## Significance

Sir Gilbert's presence at the end of the narrative serves as a structural frame. The case file — all the letters, statements, and inquest reports — has been assembled by Paul Harrison for Sir Gilbert's review. We are, in effect, reading over Sir Gilbert's shoulder. His silent contemplation at the end leaves the question of prosecution hanging, and the title *The Documents in the Case* takes on its full meaning: these are the documents submitted to the Director of Public Prosecutions.
