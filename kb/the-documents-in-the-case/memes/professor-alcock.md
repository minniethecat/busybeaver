# Professor Alcock

## Overview

Professor Alcock is mentioned in passing as an old friend of [[george-harrison|George Harrison]], whom Harrison invokes as an authority during a domestic argument about Einstein and modern science.

## Mentions in the Text

Professor Alcock appears only in [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]]'s early letters. During a dinner-table dispute about Einstein, Harrison dismisses the physicist as "a charlatan who was pulling people's legs with his theories" [PN=003-004]. When challenged, he appeals to authority: "Just you ask my old friend, Professor Alcock, if you won't believe me" [PN=003-004].

In a later letter, Munting recalls Harrison making the same appeal: "'Why do you want to believe it?' said Harrison. 'It's all words. When it comes to doing anything practical you have to come back to common sense. My friend Professor Alcock—'" [PN=007-003], at which point the quotation breaks off.

## Significance

Professor Alcock never appears in person. He functions as a rhetorical device — Harrison's appeal to an absent authority to shut down arguments he cannot or will not engage with on their merits. The name-dropping reveals Harrison's intellectual insecurity: unable to debate Einstein on substance, he retreats to his connection with a professor whose credentials presumably validate Harrison's contempt for theoretical physics.

The fact that neither Margaret Harrison nor Agatha Milsom has ever met or seen Professor Alcock ("Mrs. Harrison said she couldn't ask Professor Alcock, because she had never seen him" [PN=003-004]) adds to the impression that Harrison's invocation of his academic friend may be as much about asserting status as about seeking truth.
