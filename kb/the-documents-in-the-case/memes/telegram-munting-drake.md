# Telegram: Munting to Drake (February 26, 1929)

## Summary

The telegram from [[john-munting|John Munting]] to [[elizabeth-drake|Elizabeth Drake]], dated February 26, 1929 [PN=035-001], is a brief but critical document in [[death-of-george-harrison|the murder]] timeline. Its terse message — "A little mad and quite mistaken" — captures the emotional intensity of the period immediately before [[george-harrison|George Harrison]]'s death.

## Full Text

The telegram consists of a single line:

> "A little mad and quite mistaken."

This cryptic message, sent to [[elizabeth-drake|Elizabeth Drake]] ("[[elizabeth-drake|Bungie]]"), communicates [[john-munting|Munting]]'s emotional state and his reassessment of a situation — likely related to his suspicions about [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] and [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]].

## Analysis

The telegram's significance is primarily chronological. Dated February 26, 1929, it falls within the critical window: the month when [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] and [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]]'s affair was developing [PN=029-003], and less than a month before [[death-of-george-harrison|Harrison's death]] at [[the-shack-manaton|The Shack]] in late March.

The phrase "quite mistaken" suggests [[john-munting|Munting]] had initially suspected something that he now doubts — or had denied something he now accepts. The ambiguity is deliberate; the telegram represents a moment of realization or reversal whose full meaning only becomes clear in retrospect.

The brevity of the telegram (six words) contrasts with the lengthy letters that dominate the narrative. It is [[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|the novel]]'s most compressed document — a moment of urgent communication that marks a turning point.
