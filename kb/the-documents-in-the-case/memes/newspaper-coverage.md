# Newspaper Coverage of the Harrison Case

Newspaper reports — specifically the *[[morning-express|Morning Express]]* coverage — provide the public-facing record of the Harrison poisoning case within the narrative. Three extracts from the newspaper are reproduced in [[the-dossier|Paul Harrison's dossier]], offering the only external, institutional perspective on events otherwise known through private correspondence.

## The Inquest Report

The *Morning Express* of Tuesday, October 21st, 1929, reported on the opening of [[the-inquest|the inquest]] before [[dr-pringle|Dr. Pringle]], the coroner [PN=047-001]. The report noted that the court was crowded — an indication of the public interest the case had already generated [PN=047-005].

The newspaper conveyed key testimony, including the evidence about [[george-harrison|George Harrison]]'s "mania for cooking unconventional dishes of hedgehogs, mushrooms and other natural products" [PN=048-006]. This characterisation — the eccentric amateur naturalist undone by his own hobby — became the dominant public narrative of the case.

## The Adjourned Hearing

The *Morning Express* of Wednesday, November 5th, 1929, covered the resumed inquest, at which [[james-lubbock|Sir James Lubbock]], the Home Office Analyst, gave his crucial evidence about identifying muscarine in the remains [PN=048-001]. The newspaper reported his testimony that he had "not the slightest doubt" about the identification [PN=048-018].

## The Execution

The *Morning Express* of November 30th, 1930, carried the brief notice of the execution at Exeter Gaol [PN=053-011] — three terse lines that concluded the public narrative of the case. The contrast between this minimal notice and the hundreds of pages of private correspondence that constituted the true record of events is striking.

## Narrative Function

The newspaper extracts serve as the "official" version of events — brief, public, and superficial — against which the rich, contradictory, and deeply personal documents of the correspondence are measured. They represent the gap between what the world was told and what actually happened.
