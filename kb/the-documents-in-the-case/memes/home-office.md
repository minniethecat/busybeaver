# Home Office

## Overview

The Home Office is the British government department responsible for domestic affairs, including forensic analysis in criminal investigations. In the case of [[death-of-george-harrison|George Harrison's death]], Sir James Lubbock, the Home Office Analyst, provided crucial expert testimony at the inquest, establishing that Harrison died of muscarine poisoning.

## Role in the Case

The Home Office enters the case through the testimony of [[james-lubbock|Sir James Lubbock]], who was called as the first expert witness at the resumed inquest [PN=048-008]. As the Home Office Analyst, Lubbock conducted chemical analysis of the deceased's stomach contents, vomited matter, and the remains of the mushroom dish found at [[the-shack-manaton|The Shack]] [PN=048-008].

The analysis confirmed the presence of muscarine — the poisonous principle of *Amanita muscaria* (Fly Agaric) [PN=048-009]. This forensic evidence was central to establishing that Harrison's death was caused by poisoning rather than natural causes.

## Key Testimony

Sir James Lubbock's testimony established several critical points:

- He obtained "a considerable quantity of a substance known as 'muscarine'" from the stomach contents, vomited matter, and the unconsumed mushroom dish [PN=048-009]
- He estimated that the deceased had consumed "a very large quantity of the poison" [PN=048-010]
- He described the symptoms of muscarine poisoning: acute sickness, violent vomiting, diarrhoea, suffocation, dizziness, and possible blindness [PN=048-011]
- Death would result from respiratory paralysis — the victim would "be unable to breathe and would die of suffocation" [PN=048-012]
- He noted that the Fly Agaric (*Amanita muscaria*) is "liable to be eaten in mistake" for the edible Warty Caps (*Amanita rubescens*) [PN=048-014]

## Institutional Context

The Home Office's involvement underscores the seriousness with which the authorities treated Harrison's death. The deployment of a senior Home Office Analyst reflects the suspicious circumstances surrounding the death and the need for authoritative forensic evidence. The case file also references the Home Office in connection with the arsenic case mentioned by Lubbock: "working late tonight, sir, at the Home Office. I think it's the arsenic case" [PN=052-094].
