# Drake's Letter to Munting

## Summary

[[elizabeth-drake|Elizabeth Drake]]'s letter to [[john-munting|John Munting]] [PN=034-001] is her sole contribution to the documentary record as an author. While she is a frequent recipient (of [[john-munting|Munting]]'s letters throughout [[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|the novel]], and of Agatha's letter in Chapter 33), this is the only time she writes back — making it a uniquely valuable document.

## Key Content

As [[john-munting|Munting]]'s fiancée, [[elizabeth-drake|Elizabeth]]'s perspective has been largely invisible — refracted through his letters to her. This letter provides direct access to her voice: her concerns, her understanding of the situation at [[whittington-terrace|15 Whittington Terrace]], and her relationship with [[john-munting|Munting]].

## Analysis

The inclusion of this single reply highlights an asymmetry in the epistolary record. Most correspondents are represented by their outgoing letters; [[elizabeth-drake|Elizabeth]] is represented largely by what is written to her. Her single letter is a reminder that the dossier is selective — it preserves some voices and silences others.

The letter likely responds to [[john-munting|Munting]]'s growing unease about the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] household, possibly including his concerns about [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] and [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]]. Her reply may offer perspective from outside the immediate pressure cooker of Bayswater.
