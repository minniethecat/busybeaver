# Margaret Harrison's Letters to Lathom

## Summary

[[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]]'s letters to [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]] form a crucial thread in the documentary evidence. Chapters 38 and 46 preserve her direct communications with [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]], written during the period of their affair and its apparent conclusion. These letters are cited extensively in [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]]'s statement as evidence of motive and conspiracy.

## Key Content

Margaret's letters reveal:

- The emotional tenor of her relationship with [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]: intimate, passionate, and increasingly complicated
- Her concern for propriety and social standing — the tension between her feelings and her position as a married woman
- The letter that "clearly indicated the affair was over" [PN=049-273] — a document whose timing and sincerity became central to the prosecution
- Her awareness of the risk of discovery: [[george-harrison|George Harrison]]'s growing suspicion

## Analysis

Margaret's letters are among the most emotionally charged documents in the dossier. Unlike the analytical letters of [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]] or the scientific reports of [[james-lubbock|Sir James Lubbock]], [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]]'s correspondence is personal — written in the heat of an illicit relationship, without awareness that it would become evidence in a [[the-trial|murder trial]].

The letter ending the affair [PN=049-273] is particularly significant. If genuine, it suggests [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]] had broken with [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] before [[death-of-george-harrison|the murder]]. If strategic, it suggests premeditation — an attempt to create documentary cover. The ambiguity is deliberate and remains unresolved.
