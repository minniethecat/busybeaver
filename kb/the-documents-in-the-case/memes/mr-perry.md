# Mr. Perry

## Overview

Mr. Perry is the vicar mentioned in passing in [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]]'s early letters to [[olive-farebrother|Olive Farebrother]]. He is a minor figure in Agatha's Norfolk social circle — a man who admires her needlework and represents the conventional, respectable world that Agatha inhabits before becoming entangled with the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] household.

## Role in the Case

Mr. Perry has no direct connection to [[death-of-george-harrison|the murder]] case. His significance is as an emblem of Agatha's pre-Bayswater life — the orderly, provincial, clerical world she has left behind. He appears in her first letter: "Mr. Perry is the vicar — I'm sure I have told you about him" [PN=002-011].

He admires Agatha's needlework: "Mr. Perry saw it the other day when he came to call" [PN=001-009] and is "tremendously impressed" with it [PN=004-005]. These domestic details establish Agatha's character before the darker narrative of the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] household unfolds.

## Character

Mr. Perry is sketched in minimal strokes — he is a vicar, he makes pastoral calls, he appreciates traditional feminine accomplishments. He belongs to the world of Norfolk tea-parties and parish visits — a world Agatha is increasingly distant from as she becomes absorbed in the Bayswater drama.

His presence in the early letters serves a structural function: it establishes normalcy before the disruption. He is the "before" against which the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] affair is the "after."
