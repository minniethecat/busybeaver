# Psychoanalysis

The system of psychological theory and therapy developed by Sigmund Freud, which provides an implicit framework for understanding character motivations in the case documents. While never named directly in the correspondence, psychoanalytic concepts — repression, the unconscious, the significance of seemingly trivial actions — permeate the narrative's approach to human behavior.

## Relevance to the Case

Several characters display patterns of behavior that invite psychoanalytic interpretation. [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]]'s keen observation of domestic detail and her interest in what lies beneath the surface of ordinary life mirrors the psychoanalytic method of reading symptoms. Her letters to [[olive-farebrother|Olive Farebrother]] frequently probe the hidden dynamics of the Harrison household.

[[dr-trevor|Dr. Trevor]], as a medical professional, would have been acquainted with psychoanalytic ideas, which were widely discussed in British medical circles by the late 1920s.

## In the Literary Context

[[dorothy-l-sayers|Dorothy L. Sayers]] was writing during a period when Freudian ideas were entering mainstream British intellectual culture. The novel's epistolary form — where characters reveal themselves through their own words, often inadvertently — can be read as a literary parallel to the psychoanalytic process of free association and the interpretation of verbal clues.

## Source References

While Freud is never cited by name in the correspondence, the psychological framing of character motivations — particularly in [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]]'s observations about [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]]'s behavior and the analysis of marital dynamics between [[george-harrison|George]] and [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]] — draws on psychoanalytic concepts of repression and unconscious motivation that were widely current in 1920s literary culture.
