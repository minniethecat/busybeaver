# Robert Eustace

Robert Eustace was the pen name of **Eustace Robert Barton** (1868–1943), an English medical doctor who collaborated with [[dorothy-l-sayers|Dorothy L. Sayers]] on _[[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|The Documents in the Case]]_ (1930).

## Collaboration with Dorothy L. Sayers

Eustace approached Sayers with the idea for a detective novel in which the murder method would involve a rare poison — an area where his medical training provided authentic technical detail. Sayers wrote the literary and character-driven portions of the narrative, while Eustace contributed the scientific and toxicological framework, particularly the chemistry of [[muscarine-poisoning|muscarine]] and the forensic distinction between natural and synthetic alkaloids.

This collaboration was unusual for Sayers, who typically wrote alone. The result was a novel distinct from her Lord Peter Wimsey series — an epistolary narrative that foregrounds forensic chemistry alongside psychological observation.

## Medical Background

As a qualified physician, Eustace brought clinical authority to the [[chemical-analysis-report|chemical analysis report]] and the toxicological arguments that form the evidentiary core of the novel. His knowledge of laboratory procedure and the pharmacology of mushroom alkaloids grounded the story's scientific claims in real-world forensic practice of the late 1920s.

## Source References

Eustace's contribution is most visible in the forensic and toxicological sections of the correspondence, particularly the [[chemical-analysis-report|chemical analysis]] of the mushroom samples and the expert testimony regarding synthetic [[muscarine-poisoning|muscarine]].
