# George Harrison

George Harrison is [[death-of-george-harrison|the murder]] victim at the center of *[[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|The Documents in the Case]]* — a 56-year-old Head of the Accountants' Department whose death by [[muscarine-poisoning|synthetic muscarine poisoning]] at [[the-shack-weekend|The Shack]] in [[manaton|Manaton]], [[manaton|Devon]], triggers the investigation that culminates in [[lathom-arrest|the arrest]] and conviction of [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]]. Through the letters he writes to his son [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]], George emerges as a man of conventional values and domestic affections whose household at [[whittington-terrace|15 Whittington Terrace, Bayswater]], becomes the stage for a tragedy of adultery, jealousy, and premeditated murder.

## Domestic Life at Whittington Terrace

George Harrison presides over a household of intersecting lives at [[whittington-terrace|15 Whittington Terrace, Bayswater]]. He lives with his wife [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]] and, during the period leading to his death, shares the building with two lodgers: the young artist [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]] in the ground-floor maisonnette and the chemist [[john-munting|John Munting]] in the flat above.

His letters to [[paul-harrison|Paul]] — who is working abroad as a civil engineer — provide an intimate chronicle of domestic life. He writes warmly about mushrooming expeditions with [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]], dinner parties, and the small pleasures of suburban routine [PN=015-008]. Yet beneath the surface of genial domesticity, tensions are accumulating. Harrison is aware of [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]]'s growing attachment to [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]], and his letters trace the arc of his unease from mild concern to genuine alarm.

> "I am sorry you are not here to go a-mushrooming with me. Margaret, of course, is not much of a walker." [PN=015-008]

## Character and Values

Harrison emerges from his correspondence as a man of his class and generation: conservative in values, proud of his professional standing as Head of the Accountants' Department, and devoted to his son. His letters to [[paul-harrison|Paul]] are affectionate and confiding — "George Harrison to [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]]" in chapters 15, 20, 24, and 36 [PN=015-001] [PN=020-001] [PN=024-001] [PN=036-001] — revealing a father who takes genuine pleasure in his son's achievements and concerns.

He is described by [[john-munting|Munting]] as someone who was "never jealous of [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]], as he was of every other man who came near [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]]" [PN=037-020]. This observation is crucial: Harrison's unusual tolerance of [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] — the one man toward whom he did not exhibit the possessiveness that characterized his other responses to male attention around his wife — suggests a blindness that proved fatal.

## Relationship with Margaret Harrison

George Harrison's marriage to [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]] forms the emotional core of the tragedy. The evidence suggests a marriage of long standing that had settled into comfortable routine before [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s arrival disrupted its equilibrium. Harrison's awareness of his wife's discontent — and her growing fascination with the young artist — is documented with painful clarity.

The irony of Harrison's position is acute: he tolerates [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] because he fails to perceive him as a threat, even as the affair between [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] and [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]] develops under his own roof. His concern about "[[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s influence" [PN=036-009] comes too late — by the time he articulates his suspicions to [[paul-harrison|Paul]], the emotional and physical triangle is already fully formed.

## Knowledge of Mushrooms

Harrison shared with his potential murderer an enthusiasm for mycology. His letters reference mushrooming expeditions — "mushrooming with me" [PN=015-008] — and his interest in identifying and collecting wild fungi. This shared interest becomes tragically significant: it explains why Harrison would have accepted and consumed a mushroom dish prepared by [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] without suspicion, and it provides the context in which [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s expertise could be deployed to lethal effect.

[[john-munting|Munting]] observed that Harrison, though an enthusiast, lacked the scientific rigor that characterized [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s approach to fungi [PN=042-006]. Harrison collected mushrooms as a hobby; [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] studied them as a science. The gap between amateur enthusiasm and expert knowledge became, in the end, the gap between life and death.

## Death at The Shack

Harrison died at [[the-shack-manaton|The Shack]] in [[manaton|Manaton]], [[manaton|Devon]], during a weekend excursion in March 1929 [PN=053-013]. The party included Harrison, [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]], [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]], [[john-munting|Munting]], and others. The meal that killed him — a dish of cooked fungus — was prepared from mushrooms gathered during the weekend.

## Letters as Evidence

[[george-harrison-letters|Harrison's letters]] serve a dual function in the documentary record: they are both intimate personal communications and crucial forensic evidence. His correspondence establishes:

- **Timeline**: The sequence of events at [[whittington-terrace|Whittington Terrace]] from [[christmas-1928-whittington-terrace|Christmas 1928]] through early 1929
- **Motive**: His growing concern about [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s influence on [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]] [PN=036-009]
- **Opportunity**: The domestic arrangements that gave [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] access to the kitchen and to [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]]
- **Character**: The portrait of a man whose conventional virtues — trust, tolerance, domestic devotion — rendered him vulnerable

## Posthumous Significance

George Harrison's death transforms him from a suburban accountant into the absent center of a murder investigation. Every character in the documentary record is defined, directly or indirectly, by their relationship to him: [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]] as the unfaithful widow, [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] as the accused murderer, [[paul-harrison|Paul]] as the grieving son seeking justice, [[john-munting|Munting]] as the scientific witness whose expertise bridges the gap between suspicion and proof.

The tragedy of George Harrison is not merely that he was murdered, but that the qualities that defined his life — his trust in those around him, his tolerance of [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]], his devotion to domestic routine — were precisely the qualities that his murderer exploited.
