# Neglected Edible Treasures

## Overview

*Neglected Edible Treasures* is [[george-harrison|George Harrison]]'s book about edible wild foods — fungi, plants, and other "foodstuffs to be obtained from our native woods and hedgerows" [PN=048-002]. The book was published by Messrs. Hopkin & Bigelow and was referenced extensively at [[the-inquest|the inquest]] into Harrison's death.

## Content and Character

Harrison described the book in a letter to [[paul-harrison|his son]] as "the little book I am writing on *Neglected Edible Treasures*" [PN=015-006]. He was preparing it during his holidays at [[the-shack-manaton|The Shack]], where he tested recipes involving unconventional ingredients.

The book's subject matter reflected Harrison's eccentric personality. He was an enthusiast for wild food — nettles, fungi, hedgehogs — and saw himself as something of a culinary pioneer. [[john-munting|John Munting]] described the book's concept with characteristic irony: "telling people how to live on nettles and toadstools and that sort of thing, and how in case of another Great War we could support the entire nation on boiled hedgehogs or some such nastiness" [PN=022-008].

## At the Inquest

At the inquest, the book was described as "an interesting and highly original volume" [PN=048-002]. The coroner's court consulted it alongside the Government publication *Edible and Poisonous Fungi* to compare the illustrations of edible and poisonous species — specifically Warty Caps and Fly Agaric [PN=048-014].

The irony is sharp: Harrison's own book on edible treasures, which celebrated his expertise, became an exhibit in the investigation of his death by a poisonous "treasure."

## Publication Details

Messrs. Hopkin & Bigelow were "interested in my 'operculum'" — Harrison's term for the book — and "rather think they mean to publish it" [PN=015-006]. The book appears to have been published by the time of the inquest, as copies were available for reference.
