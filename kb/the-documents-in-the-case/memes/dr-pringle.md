# Dr. Pringle

Dr. Pringle was the coroner for the district who presided over the inquest into the death of [[george-harrison|George Harrison]].

## Role in the Inquest

Dr. Pringle opened the inquest on a Tuesday in October 1929 at a crowded court [PN=047-005]. He presided over the proceedings, hearing evidence from witnesses including [[dr-hughes|Dr. Hughes]] of [[bovey-tracey|Bovey Tracey]], who had performed the autopsy [PN=047-049].

The coroner adjourned the inquest after the initial hearing to allow for further investigation [PN=047-062]. At the resumed sitting, the coroner's jury heard additional evidence, including testimony from [[james-lubbock|Sir James Lubbock]], the Home Office Analyst [PN=048-006].

## Summing Up

In his summing up to the jury, Dr. Pringle laid stress on the danger of eating unconventional foods without proper identification [PN=048-027]. He guided the jury through the evidence concerning the distinction between natural and synthetic poisons — a central point in establishing the cause of death.

The jury, after a few minutes' consultation, returned a verdict of Accidental Death, due to poisoning by muscarine [PN=048-028].

## Significance

Dr. Pringle's conduct of the inquest illustrates the limitations of the coronial process in cases involving sophisticated chemical knowledge. The distinction between natural and synthetic muscarine — which later proved crucial to the understanding of the case — was not fully explored in the inquest proceedings. The average coroner's jury, as [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]] later reflected, was ill-equipped to grasp the chemical complexities at issue [PN=049-003].
