# Margaret Harrison

Margaret Harrison was the wife of [[george-harrison|George Harrison]] and a central figure in the documentary record. Her letters to [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]] — intimate, desperate, and revelatory — provide the most direct window into the emotional dynamics of the Harrison household and the events leading to her husband's death.

## The Marriage

Margaret Harrison's marriage to George Harrison appears, from her letters, to have been a source of profound unhappiness. She described a husband absorbed in his hobbies — painting [[george-harrison-watercolours|watercolours]], foraging for fungi, cooking [[hedgehog-cookery|hedgehogs]] — and emotionally unavailable to her.

The marriage was described by observers as strained. [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]] noted the tension in the household, and [[john-munting|John Munting]] observed the dynamics between the Harrisons at close quarters.

## The Affair

Margaret's relationship with [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]] is documented in her series of letters to him, spanning chapters 38 through 46 of the correspondence. These letters reveal a woman in love and in despair — caught between passion and the social impossibility of [[divorce-question|divorce]].

She wrote candidly of her feelings: of knowing a woman who obtained a divorce [PN=039-005], of the horror of the "stain of the divorce courts" [PN=040-004], and of the impossibility of a timely resolution — "even if we got a divorce, it wouldn't be in time" [PN=044-005].

## The Question of Guilt

Margaret Harrison's role in her husband's death remains the central ambiguity of the case. Her letters provide [[motive-in-the-harrison-case|motive]] — an intolerable marriage from which only death could provide escape without social ruin. But the documentary record does not establish her direct involvement in the poisoning.

[[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]]'s investigation uncovered documents in his stepmother's handwriting that had been misrepresented [PN=049-213], suggesting a pattern of deception extending beyond the immediate circumstances of George Harrison's death.
