# Olive Farebrother

## Overview

Olive Farebrother is the primary recipient of [[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]]'s letters and, by extension, the intended reader of [[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|the novel]]'s principal narrative thread. Though she never appears directly in the story and her replies are not included, her role as confidante is essential to the epistolary form — it is to Olive that Agatha unburdens herself of observations, suspicions, and psychological analyses of the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] household.

## Role in the Case

Olive Farebrother is the silent partner in Agatha's epistolary relationship. Agatha addresses her in multiple chapters: "[[agatha-milsom|Agatha Milsom]] to Olive Farebrother" appears as the heading for Chapters 1, 10, 16, 18, and 21 — and implicitly throughout the first half of [[the-documents-in-the-case-novel|the novel]] [PN=001-001] [PN=010-001] [PN=016-001] [PN=018-001] [PN=021-001].

The one-sided nature of the correspondence (we never see Olive's replies) is a deliberate narrative device. It positions the reader as Olive — receiving Agatha's letters, forming opinions, waiting for the next installment. Olive's silence is the space into which the reader enters.

## Character

What we know of Olive Farebrother is inferred entirely from Agatha's tone and content. She is clearly an intimate friend — someone Agatha trusts with her most pointed observations and psychological speculations. Agatha shares [[dr-trevor|Dr. Trevor]]'s advice with Olive [PN=001-005] [PN=001-006], discusses [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s character [PN=010-008], and confides her growing unease about the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] household.

Olive is presumably of similar social standing to Agatha — educated, perceptive, and interested in human psychology. Her patience with Agatha's lengthy, analytical letters suggests a deep friendship.
