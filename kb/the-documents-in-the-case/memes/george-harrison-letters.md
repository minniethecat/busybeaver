# George Harrison's Letters to Paul

## Summary

[[george-harrison|George Harrison]]'s letters to his son [[paul-harrison|Paul]] — preserved in Chapters 15, 20, 24, and 36 — are [[george-harrison|the victim]]'s own voice in the documentary record. They provide the most direct window into the pre-murder atmosphere at [[whittington-terrace|15 Whittington Terrace]] from the perspective of the man who would die there.

## Key Content

Harrison's letters cover domestic life with a warmth that makes his subsequent death more poignant:
- Mushroom-hunting expeditions: "I am hoping that young [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] will come out with me sometime" [PN=015-008]
- The [[paris-trip-december-1928|Paris trip]] with [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]]: December 15-28, 1928 [PN=025-002]
- Growing concern about the household dynamics: "our friendship with young [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]], whom we all like so much, should not..." [PN=036-009]
- [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s distress: "[[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] is extremely distressed, as you may imagine" [PN=036-010]

## Analysis

Harrison's letters are tragically ironic. He writes affectionately of [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] throughout, unaware that the young artist is having an affair with his wife and will eventually be convicted of his murder. The letter mentioning mushroom-hunting [PN=015-008] is particularly chilling — the very activity that would provide [[death-of-george-harrison|the murder]] weapon.

The warmth of [[george-harrison|Harrison]]'s paternal voice — proud of [[paul-harrison|Paul]], affectionate toward [[margaret-harrison|Margaret]], tolerant of [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]] — makes him more than just "[[george-harrison|the victim]]." He becomes a fully realized character whose death matters.
