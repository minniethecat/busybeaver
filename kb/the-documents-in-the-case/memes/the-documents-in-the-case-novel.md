# The Documents in the Case (Novel)

*The Documents in the Case* is a collaborative detective novel by [[dorothy-l-sayers|Dorothy L. Sayers]] and Robert Eustace, published in 1930 by Ernest Benn. Unusual in the [[dorothy-l-sayers|Sayers]] canon, it departs from the Lord Peter Wimsey series to present a standalone mystery told entirely through documents: letters, statements, newspaper extracts, and a [[the-inquest|coroner's inquest]] report. The novel's epistolary form — a dossier of primary sources compiled by [[george-harrison|the victim]]'s son [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]] — creates a distinctive reading experience in which the detective work is performed by the reader, sifting evidence alongside the characters.

## Publication and Authorship

The novel was published in 1930, during the golden age of detective fiction. [[dorothy-l-sayers|Sayers]], already established as the creator of Lord Peter Wimsey, collaborated with Eustace — a pen name for Dr. Eustace Robert Barton, a physician with expertise in forensic medicine and toxicology. Eustace's medical knowledge provided the scientific foundation for the novel's central forensic puzzle: the detection and analysis of synthetic [[muscarine-poisoning|muscarine]] poisoning.

The collaboration is visible in the novel's texture. The psychological acuity of the character portraits — [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison's]] complex motivations, [[harwood-lathom|Lathom's]] charismatic menace, [[john-munting|Munting's]] scientific ambivalence — bears [[dorothy-l-sayers|Sayers]]'s literary signature. The detailed toxicology, the chemical analysis procedures, and the mycological precision reflect Eustace's professional expertise.

## Epistolary Form

The novel is constructed as a dossier of documents assembled by [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]], a civil engineer working abroad, who receives letters from his parents and their household at [[whittington-terrace|15 Whittington Terrace, Bayswater]]. Following his father's death, [[paul-harrison|Paul]] compiles the correspondence — along with witness statements, inquest testimony, and newspaper reports — into a documentary record that constitutes the novel itself.

This form serves multiple functions:

| Function | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Multiple perspectives | Each character speaks in their own voice, unmediated by a narrator |
| Reader as detective | The reader must weigh conflicting accounts and interpret silences |
| Documentary realism | The letters and reports create the texture of an authentic case file |
| Dramatic irony | Letters written before [[death-of-george-harrison|the murder]] are read with retrospective knowledge of what follows |

The epistolary structure means that crucial information is often conveyed indirectly — through what a character chooses to write, or not write, in a letter — rather than through explicit narration. The reader, like [[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]], must read between the lines.

## The Central Mystery

The novel's mystery concerns the death of [[george-harrison|George Harrison]], a 56-year-old accountant who dies at [[the-shack-manaton|The Shack]] in [[manaton|Manaton]], [[manaton|Devon]], after consuming a dish of wild mushrooms. Initially attributed to natural causes, the death is re-examined following a [[the-inquest|coroner's inquest]], where chemical analysis reveals the presence of synthetic [[muscarine-poisoning|muscarine]] — a toxic alkaloid extracted from the *[[amanita-muscaria|Amanita muscaria]]* mushroom.

The investigation focuses on [[harwood-lathom|Harwood Lathom]], a young artist and expert amateur mycologist who boards at the [[whittington-terrace|Harrison home]] and is conducting an affair with [[george-harrison|the victim]]'s wife. The convergence of [[harwood-lathom|Lathom]]'s mycological expertise, his access to the [[george-harrison|Harrison]] kitchen, and his romantic involvement with [[margaret-harrison|Margaret Harrison]] forms the circumstantial case against him.

## Literary Significance

*The Documents in the Case* occupies a distinctive position in the [[dorothy-l-sayers|Sayers]] canon. Its departure from the Wimsey series — no aristocratic detective, no London clubland, no witty banter between Lord Peter and Bunter — disappointed some contemporary readers but demonstrated [[dorothy-l-sayers|Sayers]]'s willingness to experiment with form. The novel's documentary structure anticipated later experiments in crime fiction, and its integration of forensic science into the narrative fabric reflected the growing sophistication of the detective genre in the interwar period.

The novel is also notable for its treatment of class and domesticity. The [[george-harrison|Harrison]]s' Bayswater terrace, with its subdivided flats and lodgers, presents a more socially heterogeneous world than the country houses of traditional detective fiction. The crime emerges from the tensions of shared domestic space — proximity, jealousy, class aspiration — rather than from the intrigues of inherited wealth.
