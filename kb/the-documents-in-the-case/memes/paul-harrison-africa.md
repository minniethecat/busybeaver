# Paul Harrison in Central Africa

[[paul-harrison|Paul Harrison]] was working as an engineer building a bridge in [[central-africa|Central Africa]] when his father [[george-harrison|George Harrison]] died. His absence during the critical events — and his return to investigate — frames the entire documentary narrative.

## The Engineering Work

Paul Harrison was "building a bridge in Central Africa and doing rather well" [PN=005-008]. His work as an engineer placed him at a vast physical remove from the domestic drama unfolding at [[15-whittington-terrace|Whittington Terrace]] and [[the-shack-manaton|The Shack]].

His professional competence as an engineer — systematic, analytical, methodical — would later inform his approach to investigating his father's death. The same qualities that served him in bridge-building served him in reconstructing events from documents.

## Absence and Return

Paul Harrison was "in Africa when the news of my father's death reached me" [PN=049-002]. By the time he learned of the tragedy, the inquest had already returned its verdict of accidental death and the case appeared closed.

His return to England transformed the narrative. As an outsider with engineering training and no emotional stake in the household dynamics, he brought fresh eyes to the documentary evidence. He took over the work in Central Africa from a colleague, "thus leaving him to the undisturbed pursuit of his private investigations" into his father's death [PN=049-154].

## The Investigator's Method

Paul Harrison's approach to the case reflected his engineering background. He compiled [[the-dossier|a dossier]] of documents, systematically gathered evidence, and subjected each claim to verification. His method — compare accounts, identify inconsistencies, follow the documentary trail — was the approach of an engineer troubleshooting a complex system.
