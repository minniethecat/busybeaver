# René Descartes (1596–1650)

**René Descartes** (31 March 1596 – 11 February 1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist.

*The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]* (*[[the-passions-of-the-soul|Les Passions de l'âme]]*, 1649) was his final published work, written for Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia.

It represents his most developed treatment of ethics and moral psychology, synthesizing his [[soul|mind-body dualism]], his physiology of the [[animal-spirits]], and his doctrine of [[free-will]] into a unified theory of the [[passion|passions]].

## Intellectual Context

### Breaking with the Ancients

Descartes positioned his treatment of the [[passion|passions]] as an explicit rejection of the [[aristotle|Aristotelian]]-scholastic tradition:

> The most glaring defect in the sciences we have from the ancients is what they wrote about the passions. The teachings of the ancients about the passions are so skimpy and mostly so implausible that I can't hope to approach the truth except by leaving the paths they have followed. So I'll have to write as though I were considering a topic that no-one had dealt with before me. [[001-001]]

### The Physicist's Approach

Descartes treated the [[passion|passions]] as natural phenomena, not as moral failings.

His approach was that of a physicist (*en physicien*) — explaining the [[passion|passions]] through the mechanical interactions of the body, specifically the [[animal-spirits]], the nerves, and the [[pineal-gland]].

## The Passions of the Soul

### Structure

The work is divided into three parts, tracing a trajectory from general principles to particular applications:

| Part | Articles | Content | Methodological Role |
|------|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Part I | 1–50 | The [[passion|Passion]]s in General | Metaphysical and physiological foundations |
| Part II | 51–148 | The Number and Order of the [[passion|Passion]]s | Taxonomy and mechanism of each passion |
| Part III | 149–212 | Particular [[passion|Passion]]s | Application to ethics and conduct of life |

### The Six Primitive Passions

Descartes identifies six fundamental [[passion|passions]]: [[wonder]], [[love]], [[hatred]], [[desire]], [[joy]], and [[sadness]].

All other [[passion|passions]] are either composed from these six or are species of them. [[069-002]][[069-003]][[069-004]][[069-005]][[069-006]][[069-007]]

## Key Philosophical Contributions

### Action and Passion as One

Descartes dissolves the traditional dichotomy.

> An action and passion are always a single thing that has these two names because of the two different subjects to which it may be related. [[001-002]]

### Free Will and Generosity

Descartes' ethical thought culminates in [[generosity]] (*[[generosity|générosité]]*).

> The generous person knows that nothing truly belongs to him except this free control of his volitions, and feels within himself a firm and constant resolution to use his freedom well. [[153-001]]

### The Unitary Soul

Against the [[aristotle|Aristotelian]] tradition, Descartes insists on the soul's unity.

> Each of us has only one soul, which doesn't have different parts; it is at once sensitive and rational too, and all its appetites are volitions. [[047-007]]

### The Mastery of the Passions

The work's final teaching concerns wisdom and the [[passion|passions]].

> The chief use of wisdom lies in its teaching us to master and control them so skillfully that the evils that they cause are quite bearable, and even become a source of joy. [[212-001]]
