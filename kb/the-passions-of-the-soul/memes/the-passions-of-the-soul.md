# The Passions of the Soul (Les Passions de l'âme, 1649)

**The Passions of the Soul** (*Les Passions de l'âme*) is René [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' final published philosophical work, completed in 1649.

It provides a comprehensive mechanistic account of human emotions grounded in the [[soul|mind-body dualism]], while developing an ethical framework centered on the virtue of [[generosity]].

## Structure

The work is organized into three parts of increasing specificity.

### Part I: The Passions in General (Articles 1–50)

Establishes the metaphysical and physiological foundations: the nature of the [[soul]], the function of the [[animal-spirits]], and the [[soul|mind]]-body relationship.

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] opens by declaring his methodological break with the [[aristotle|ancients]]:

> The most glaring defect in the sciences we have from the ancients is what they wrote about the passions. This topic has been strenuously explored ... and yet the teachings of the ancients about the passions are so skimpy and mostly so implausible. [[001-001]]

He then defines a passion as: "perceptions, sensations or commotions of the soul which we relate particularly to the soul, and are caused, maintained and strengthened by some movement of the [[animal-spirits|spirits]]." [[027-002]][[027-003]]

### Part II: The Number and Order of the Passions (Articles 51–148)

Identifies the six primitive [[passion|passions]] and their derivatives.

> But there aren't many simple and basic passions. Look over my list and you'll easily see that there are only six: wonder, love, hatred, desire, joy, sadness. All the others are either composed from some of these six or they are species of them. [[069-002]][[069-007]][[069-008]]

| Primitive [[passion|Passion]] | Articles | Function |
|-------------------|----------|----------|
| [[wonder]] | 70–78 | Attention to novel objects |
| [[love]] | 79–85 | Inclination toward a good |
| [[hatred]] | 79–80, 84–85 | Aversion from an evil |
| [[desire]] | 86–90 | Pursuit of a future good |
| [[joy]] | 91, 93–95 | Enjoyment of a present good |
| [[sadness]] | 92–95 | Suffering of a present evil |

### Part III: Particular Passions (Articles 149–212)

Examines specific [[passion|passions]] in detail, culminating in the doctrine of [[generosity]] and the general remedy for excessive [[passion|passions]]. [[211-001]]

## Central Doctrines

### The Passions as Bodily Phenomena

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] treats the [[passion|passions]] as perceptions caused by the movement of [[animal-spirits]].

> What the passions do for us consists solely in this: they dispose our soul to want the things that nature decides are useful to us, and to persist in this volition; and the agitation of the spirits that normally causes the passions also disposes the body to move in ways that help to bring about those useful things. [[052-002]][[052-003]]

### The Conflict Within

The apparent conflict between reason and passion is a mechanical opposition, not a war between parts of the soul.

> Each of us has only one soul, which doesn't have different parts; it is at once sensitive and rational too, and all its appetites are volitions. [[047-007]]

### Generosity as the Sovereign Remedy

The work's ethical culmination is [[generosity]] (*[[generosity|générosité]]*): the knowledge of one's [[free-will]] combined with the firm resolution to use it well. [[153-001]]

## Historical Significance

*The Passions of the Soul* is significant as:

1. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' final and most mature philosophical work
2. The most systematic 17th-century treatment of the emotions
3. A foundational text for later theories of emotion by Spinoza and Hume
4. A methodological bridge between mechanistic physiology and moral philosophy

## The Final Teaching

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] ends with the work's deepest insight:

> The soul can have pleasures of its own. But the pleasures that it shares with the body depend entirely on the passions, so that persons whom the passions can move most deeply are capable of enjoying the sweetest pleasures of this life. The chief use of wisdom lies in its teaching us to master and control them so skillfully that the evils that they cause are quite bearable, and even become a source of joy. [[212-001]]
