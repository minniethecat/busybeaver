# Love (L'Amour)

**Love** (*l'amour*) is one of the six primitive [[passion|passions]] in [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]*. It is defined as a commotion of the soul that joins us in will to an object we judge to be good for us — the fundamental passion of **approach** and **union**, d[[anger|ire]]ctly opposed to [[hatred]].

## 定义

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] provides his formal definition in Article 79:

> Love is a commotion of the soul caused by the movement of the animal spirits, which incites the soul to join itself in will to objects that appear to be beneficial to it. [[079-001]]

This definition highlights three essential features: (1) love is a bodily-caused passion, not a pure act of will; (2) it is d[[anger|ire]]cted at *apparent* goods, which may or may not be truly beneficial; and (3) it involves a *volitional joining* — the soul wills to consider itself united with the loved object.

Crucially, love and hatred are distinguished by their **object**, not by their felt quality:

> When we think of something as good and as something that is beneficial to us, this makes us have love for it; and when we think of something as bad or harmful, this arouses hatred in us. [[056-001]]

## 笛卡尔的论述

### 两种爱

In Articles 81–82, [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] distinguishes two primary kinds of love:

> A distinction is commonly made between two sorts of love: (i) **benevolent love** for x, which prompts us to wish x well; (ii) **concupiscent love** of x, which makes us want to possess x. But I think this is just a matter of two different effects of love, not two different kinds. [[081-001]][[081-002]][[081-003]][[081-004]]

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] instead proposes a more nuanced classification based on the **comparative esteem** we hold for the loved object:

> If we want to mark off as many kinds of love as there are kinds of thing that we can love, we must consider how greatly we esteem the object of our love compared to ourselves. [[082-001]]

This yields a spectrum: love for things lower than ourselves (objects, [[soul|anima]]ls) → love for equals (friends) → love for superiors (parents, rulers) → the highest love (God). All share the same essential structure — volitional joining — but differ in the comparative valuation involved.

### 生理表现

Love has a distinctive physiological signature. Unlike [[wonder]] (which has no cardiac component), love affects the pulse and digestion:

> When love is present, the pulse is full, even and regular, accompanied by a gentle heat in the chest; and the digestion of food is speeded up. [[083-001]] (paraphrase of Descartes' physiological account)

This physiological pattern reflects love's fundamental orientation: it is an **expansive**, **incorporative** passion — the body prepares to receive and join with the good object.

### 与欲望的区别

Love is often conflated with [[desire]], but [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] carefully distinguishes them:

- **Love** joins us to a present good.
- **Des[[anger|ire]]** agitates us toward a future good.

> Love makes us want to be joined with the loved thing, whereas desire agitates the soul and makes it want to obtain the thing desired. [[079-001]]

## 与其他概念的关系

- **[[hatred]]** — Love's d[[anger|ire]]ct contrary; where love joins, hatred separates (Articles 79–85).

- **[[desire]]** — Love is the ground of des[[anger|ire]]; we des[[anger|ire]] only what we love. Every des[[anger|ire]] presupposes love of its object.

- **[[joy]]** — Joy is the [[joy|enjoyment]] of a possessed good; love is the joining to a good whether possessed or not.

- **[[sadness]]** — Love coexists with sadness when the loved object is absent or threatened by loss.

- **[[wonder]]** — Wonder precedes love temporally: we must first notice a novel object before we can love it.

- **[[generosity]]** — True generosity is grounded in proper love of oneself, extending to benevolent love of others.

## 后世影响与解释

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' analysis of love as "volitional joining" — rather than mere feeling — influenced Spinoza's definition of love as "joy accompanied by the idea of an external cause" and Malebranche's account of love as the soul's motion toward God.

The distinction between benevolent and concupiscent love, though [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] partially dissolves it, remains a central topic in philosophical discussions of love (e.g., Nygren's *Agape and Eros*, C.S. Lewis's *The Four Loves*).
