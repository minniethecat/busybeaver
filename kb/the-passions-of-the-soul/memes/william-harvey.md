# William Harvey (1578–1657)

**William Harvey** (1578–1657) appears in *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]* in one explicit reference (Article 7), where [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] invokes his discovery of blood circulation as a paradigm of empirical science liberated from ancient authority. This endorsement is significant: Harvey's circulatory model is the physiological foundation upon which [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] builds his mechanistic account of the [[passion|passions]].

## 思想史定位

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] cites Harvey in the context of establishing the mechanistic framework of the body, which is the necessary preliminary to the ent[[anger|ire]] theory of the [[passion|passions]]:

> Likewise anyone who hasn't been completely blinded by the authority of the ancients, and has been willing to open his eyes to examine Harvey's view about the circulation of the blood, will be sure that the body's veins and arteries are like streams through which the blood constantly rushes. [[007-001]]

This passage performs double work in [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' argument:

1. **Physiological**: Harvey's discovery that blood circulates continuously through the body (passing from the heart through arteries and returning through veins) provides the anatomical basis for [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' account of how [[animal-spirits|animal spirit]]s are produced and distributed. In [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' adaptation, the finest particles of blood are filtered at the base of the brain to become [[animal-spirits|animal spirit]]s, which then flow through the nerves to move the muscles and produce the bodily signs of the [[passion|passions]].

2. **Methodological**: The phrase "hasn't been completely blinded by the authority of the ancients" aligns Harvey's empirical discovery with [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' own methodological declaration in Article 1 — leaving the paths of the ancients to treat the [[passion|passions]] "as a physicist" (*en physicien*). Harvey's circulatory model exemplifies the new science: observation-based, mechanical, and indifferent to textual authority.

> **Historical note**: [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] accepted Harvey's discovery of circulation but rejected Harvey's account of the heart's motive power (Harvey attributed it to muscular contraction; Descartes attributed it to the expansion of blood by heat — the "fire without light" in the heart). This disagreement, though not recorded in *The Passions of the Soul*, is documented in Descartes' *Description of the Human Body* (1648) and his correspondence.
