# Passion

In *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]* (1649), [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] redefines **passion** (*passion de l'[[soul|âme]]*) through a radical methodological shift: treating the passions not as moral vices to be extirpated, but as natural phenomena to be explained "as a physicist" (*en physicien*). This physiological approach — grounding the passions in the mechanical movements of the [[animal-spirits]] and the [[pineal-gland]] — was unprecedented and marked a complete departure from the [[ancient-philosophy|ancients']] treatment.

## Definition

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] provides his canonical definition in Article 27:

> We can define them generally as perceptions, sensations or commotions of the soul which we relate particularly to the soul, and are caused, maintained and strengthened by some movement of the spirits. [[027-002]][[027-003]]

This definition has three critical components, each unpacked in the following articles:

1. **Perceptions or commotions** — The passions are passive states of the soul, not its active volitions. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] prefers the term "commotions" because "the passions agitate and disturb the soul more forcefully than any other kinds of thought the soul may have." [[028-001]]

2. **Related particularly to the soul** — This distinguishes passions from sensations that refer to external objects (smells, sounds, colours) or to the body (hunger, thirst, pain). We feel a passion *in* the soul itself, not in any external locus. [[029-001]]

3. **Caused by movement of the [[animal-spirits|spirits]]** — The "last and most immediate cause" of any passion is the agitation of the [[animal-spirits|animal spirit]]s moving the pineal gland. This distinguishes passions from volitions, which are caused by the soul itself. [[029-001]]

## Descartes' Framework

### Action and Passion as a Single Thing

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] begins by dissolving the traditional dichotomy:

> Although the agent and patient — the maker and the undergoer — are often quite different, an action and passion are always a single thing that has these two names because of the two different subjects to which it may be related. [[001-002]]

What is "action" in the body (the movement of [[animal-spirits|animal spirit]]s) is simultaneously "passion" in the soul (the felt emotion). This dual-aspect monism is the metaphysical foundation of [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' ent[[anger|ire]] theory.

### The Soul's Functions

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] partitions all mental activity into two exhaustive categories:

| Category | Nature | Examples |
|----------|--------|----------|
| **Actions** (volitions) | Come d[[anger|ire]]ctly from the soul; depend on free will | Deciding to think, willing to move |
| **Passions** (perceptions) | Received by the soul; caused by the body | Wonder, [[love]], hatred, des[[anger|ire]], joy, sadness |

> There is nothing in us that we must attribute to our soul except our thoughts. There are two main kinds of thoughts — actions of the soul and passions of the soul. [[017-001]]

### Three Primary Causes

The passions can arise from three sources, with the third being the most common:

1. **The soul's own activity** — When the soul deliberately conceives an object, it can stir the corresponding passion. [[051-002]]
2. **The body's overall state** — Bodily dispositions or brain impressions can produce passions without any identifiable external cause. [[051-004]]
3. **Objects stimulating the senses** — These are "their principal and most common causes." [[051-006]][[051-007]]

## The Six Primitive Passions

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] reduces the great multitude of passions to six fundamental or "primitive" passions, from which all others derive:

> Look over my list and you'll easily see that there are only six: wonder, love, hatred, desire, joy, sadness. All the others are either composed from some of these six or they are species of them. [[069-002]][[069-003]][[069-004]][[069-005]][[069-006]][[069-007]]

| Primitive Passion | Articles | Nature | Object |
|-------------------|----------|--------|--------|
| [[wonder]] | 70–78 | Sudden surprise at something novel | The unusual object itself |
| [[love]] | 79–85 | Joining oneself in will to a beneficial object | A good for oneself |
| [[hatred]] | 79–80, 84–85 | Separating oneself from a harmful object | An evil for oneself |
| [[desire]] | 86–90 | Agitation to obtain a future good or avoid evil | The future |
| [[joy]] | 91, 93–95 | Enjoyment of a present good | A present good |
| [[sadness]] | 92–95 | Suffering from a present evil | A present evil |

The six primitive passions form the structural backbone of Part II of *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]* (Articles 51–148), with each passion analyzed in terms of its physiological mechanism, its object, and the specific passions that branch from it.

## Function and Purpose

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] rejects the Stoic and Augustinian view that passions are disorders of the soul to be suppressed. Instead, he attributes to them a positive natural function:

> What the passions do for us consists solely in this: they dispose our soul to want the things that nature decides are useful to us, and to persist in this volition; and the agitation of the spirits that normally causes the passions also disposes the body to move in ways that help to bring about those useful things. [[052-002]][[052-003]]

The passions are therefore **biologically adaptive**: they orient the whole psychophysical organism toward benefit and away from harm. Love draws us toward nourishing objects; fear pulls us back from danger; des[[anger|ire]] propels us toward future goods.

## Mastery and Regulation

While the passions are intrinsically good, they requ[[anger|ire]] regulation:

> Now that we have met up with all the passions, we have much less reason for anxiety about them than we had before. We see that they are all intrinsically good, and that all we have to avoid is their misuse or their excess. [[211-001]]

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' "most general remedy" against excessive passion combines physiological insight with cognitive discipline: when feeling the blood agitated, one should recognize that "everything presented to the imagination tends to mislead the soul" — making reasons for pursuing the passion's object appear stronger than they are. The will should delay judgment until the bodily commotion subsides. [[211-001]]

The ultimate goal is stated in the work's final article:

> The soul can have pleasures of its own. But the pleasures that it shares with the body depend entirely on the passions, so that persons whom the passions can move most deeply are capable of enjoying the sweetest pleasures of this life. ... The chief use of wisdom lies in its teaching us to master and control them so skillfully that the evils that they cause are quite bearable, and even become a source of joy. [[212-001]]
