# Free Will (Libre Arbitre)

**Free will** (*libre arbitre*) is the axis around which [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' ent[[anger|ire]] ethical philosophy turns.

It is the one thing in human beings that cannot be constrained, the sole ground of self-esteem, and the foundation of [[generosity]] — the sovereign virtue that masters all the [[passion|passions]].

## The Nature of Free Will

### The Soul's Absolute Power

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] defines volitions — acts of the will — as one of two fundamental kinds of thought (alongside [[passion|passions]]/perceptions):

> There is nothing in us that we must attribute to our soul except our thoughts. There are two main kinds of thoughts — actions of the soul and passions of the soul. The ones I call 'actions' are all our volitions, i.e. acts of the will, because we experience them as coming directly from our soul with, apparently, no input from anything else. [[017-001]]

The will's freedom is absolute and unconstrained:

> The will is by its nature so free that it can't ever be constrained. [[041-001]]

### Actions vs. Passions

While the body can influence the soul's [[passion|passions]], it cannot touch its volitions:

| Domain | Nature | Relationship to Body |
|--------|--------|---------------------|
| Volitions (actions) | Absolutely within the soul's power | Can't be changed by the body except ind[[anger|ire]]ctly [[041-004]] |
| [[passion|Passion]]s (perceptions) | Absolutely dependent on bodily events | Can't be changed by the soul except ind[[anger|ire]]ctly [[041-004]] |

## The Source of Human Dignity

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] locates the unique worth of human beings ent[[anger|ire]]ly in free will:

> I see only one thing in us that could entitle us to esteem ourselves, namely the exercise of our free will and our command of our volitions. For we can be rightly praised or blamed only for actions that depend on this free will; it makes us like God in a way, by making us masters of ourselves. [[152-001]]

This is a striking claim: free will is what makes humans resemble God — not reason, not knowledge, not virtue, but the simple capacity for free choice. Every human being possesses it equally, which grounds a radical equality in moral worth.

## Free Will and Desire

The most common error concerning the [[passion|passions]] is failing to distinguish what depends on free will from what does not:

> It seems to me that the most common error concerning desires is the failure to distinguish adequately the things that do depend wholly on us from the ones that don't. Regarding those that depend only on us — i.e. on our free will — our knowledge of their goodness ensures that there's no such thing as desiring them too ardently, because that's what virtue is — doing the good things that depend on us. [[144-001]]

The practical implication is that properly d[[anger|ire]]cted des[[anger|ire]] — des[[anger|ire]] for what free will alone can achieve — can never be excessive. Virtue itself consists in willing well, and one cannot will well too ardently.

## Free Will and Generosity

Free will is the metaphysical foundation of [[generosity]], as [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] explains:

> True generosity ... consists only in his knowing that nothing truly belongs to him except this free control of his volitions, and his feeling within himself a firm and constant resolution to use his freedom well. [[153-001]]

The generous person's self-esteem is grounded ent[[anger|ire]]ly in free will — not in wealth, reputation, intelligence, or any external advantage. This makes generosity a universally accessible virtue.

## Universal Equality

Because free will is equally present in all:

> Since others have free will just as we do, they may use it just as well. [[155-001]]

This recognition leads the generous person to humility: no one can rate themselves above others because everyone possesses the same free will. The only relevant difference is how well one uses it — and even that difference is attributed to lack of knowledge rather than lack of virtuous will. [[154-001]]
