# Aristotle (384–322 BCE)

**Aristotle** (384–322 BCE) appears in *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]* not as a d[[anger|ire]]ct interlocutor but as the unnamed architect of the philosophical tradition against which [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] defines his ent[[anger|ire]] project. When [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] opens the work by declaring the ancients' teachings on the [[passion|passions]] "so skimpy and mostly so implausible that I can't hope to approach the truth except by leaving the paths they have followed" [[001-001]], he is above all rejecting the Aristotelian framework that had dominated European philosophy for four centuries.

## Role in The Passions of the Soul

### The Rejection of the Ancients

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' opening methodological declaration positions his work as a complete rupture with the Aristotelian-scholastic tradition:

> The most glaring defect in the sciences we have from the ancients is what they wrote about the passions. This topic has been strenuously explored, and doesn't seem to be especially hard to investigate because we all feel passions in ourselves ... and yet the teachings of the ancients about the passions are so skimpy and mostly so implausible that I can't hope to approach the truth except by leaving the paths they have followed. So I'll have to write as though I were considering a topic that no-one had dealt with before me. [[001-001]]

This is a methodological manifesto: [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] presents his physiology of the [[passion|passions]] not as a refinement of the Aristotelian tradition but as an ent[[anger|ire]]ly new science — treating the [[passion|passions]] "not as an orator, nor even as a moral philosopher, but as a physicist" (*en physicien*).

### The Schools

The institutional embodiment of the Aristotelian framework is explicitly named:

> I am well aware that in the Schools [= 'the philosophy departments dominated by Aristotle and Aquinas'] the passion that makes one bold ... [[087-001]]

The "Schools" (*les Écoles*) were the university philosophy departments of [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' time, where the synthesis of Aristotle and Thomas Aquinas constituted the official curriculum. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' rejection of this framework was not merely theoretical — it carried institutional risk, and his rhetorical care in presenting his views reflects this.

### The Authority of the Ancients

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] frames his challenge to Aristotelianism as a liberation from blind authority:

> It sometimes happens that ... a person hasn't been completely blinded by the authority of the ancients, and has been willing to open his eyes to examine Harvey's view about the circulation of the blood. [[007-001]]

This passage is instructive: [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] aligns himself with William [[william-harvey|Harvey]]'s empirical discovery (the circulation of blood, 1628) against the authority of ancient texts. The implication is clear — the Aristotelian framework discourages empirical investigation in favor of textual authority.

## Key Philosophical Departures

| Aristotelian View | [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' Departure |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| The soul has parts (rational, sensitive, appetitive) | "Each of us has only one soul, which doesn't have different parts" [[047-007]] — conflicts attributed to soul parts are really body vs. will |
| [[passion|Passion]]s are moral disorders to be moderated by reason | [[passion|Passion]]s are "all intrinsically good" [[211-001]] — they are biological functions, not moral failures |
| Explanation proceeds from final causes | Explanation proceeds from mechanical causes ([[animal-spirits|animal spirit]]s, nerves, the pineal gland) |
| The heart is the seat of the [[passion|passions]] (common belief) | The pineal gland is the functional seat where soul and body interact [[031-001]] |

## The Enduring Tension

Despite the radical rejection, [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' relationship to Aristotle is not simple negation. The very categories he critiques — the [[passion|passions]] as a distinct domain of inquiry, the relationship between body and soul, the taxonomy of affective states — are inherited from the Aristotelian tradition. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' innovation lies not in discarding the questions but in replacing the method: from a metaphysics of the soul's faculties to a physics of the body's mechanisms.

This methodological shift would prove enormously influential. The modern scientific study of emotion — from William James' physiological theory to contemporary affective neuroscience — operates within the Cartesian paradigm of treating affective states as bodily phenomena to be explained mechanistically before being evaluated morally.
