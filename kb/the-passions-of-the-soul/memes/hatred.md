# Hatred (La Haine)

**Hatred** (*la haine*) is one of the six primitive [[passion|passions]] in [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]*. It is the passion of **separation** and **[[desire|aversion]]** — a commotion of the soul that causes it to consider itself apart from an object it judges to be harmful, standing in d[[anger|ire]]ct opposition to [[love]].

## 定义

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] defines hatred as the structural mirror-image of [[love]]:

> Hatred is a commotion of the soul caused by the animal spirits, which incites the soul to want to be separated from objects that present themselves to it as harmful. [[079-001]]

The judgment of harmfulness — not the felt intensity — is the defining feature:

> When we think of something as bad or harmful, this arouses hatred in us. [[056-001]]

Crucially, hatred is not distinguished into kinds as [[love]] is:

> Although hatred is directly opposed to love, we don't distinguish it into as many kinds, because we don't pay as much attention to the differences between the evils from which we are separated by an act of will as we do to the differences between the goods to which we join ourselves. [[084-001]]

## 笛卡尔的论述

### 生理表现

Hatred has a distinctive and unpleasant physiological signature, opposite to that of [[love]]:

> In hatred, on the other hand, I observe that the pulse is uneven, weaker, and often quicker; that we feel chills alternating with a kind of burning heat in the chest; that the stomach stops doing its job and tends to vomit and reject the food we have eaten. [[098-001]]

This physiological pattern reflects hatred's essential orientation: it is a **contractive**, **expulsive** passion — the body prepares to reject and expel the harmful object, in contrast to [[love]]'s expansive, incorporative warmth.

### 与悲伤的复合

Hatred rarely occurs alone. It frequently combines with [[sadness]]:

> The first heat [of hatred] that wasn't joined to sadness made the same impression on the soul as a simple aversion. It also brought it about that the animal spirits ... were driven towards the muscles that serve to push away and repel the object of hatred. [[110-001]]

This compounding makes hatred particularly dangerous to the soul — it adds the suffering of sadness to the agitation of [[desire|aversion]]. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] observes that :

> Sadness doesn't diminish except when hatred, which is often joined to sadness, is removed. [[105-001]]

### 正当与不正当的恨

Not all hatred is pathological. In Article 100, [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] notes the possibility of righteous hatred:

> It is sometimes useful to hate things that are truly harmful — provided the hatred stays within its proper bounds, and the soul does its duty, provided there is no hatred mixed with the sadness. [[100-001]]

The key therapeutic insight: hatred is to be limited to genuinely harmful objects and not allowed to spill over into diffuse animosity or self-destructive sadness.

### 与欲望的关系

Like [[love]], hatred generates [[desire]]:

> The same desire is accompanied by hatred, anxiety and sadness when we judge it to be contrary to some other desire. [[087-003]]

When we hate something we lack the power to avoid immediately, hatred transforms into anxious des[[anger|ire]] — the agitation to escape a future evil.

## 与其他概念的关系

- **[[love]]** — Hatred's d[[anger|ire]]ct contrary; the two are the fundamental binary of approach/avoidance that structures all other [[passion|passions]] (Articles 79–85).

- **[[desire]]** — Hatred of a future evil generates des[[anger|ire]] to avoid it; hatred of a present evil generates des[[anger|ire]] to escape it.

- **[[sadness]]** — Hatred and sadness are frequently compounded; pure hatred (without sadness) is rare but possible as "simple [[desire|aversion]]."

- **[[joy]]** — Joy can co-occur with hatred when we hate something and rejoice in its absence or destruction.

- **[[wonder]]** — Like [[love]], hatred is often preceded by wonder at the novel object, though wonder itself has no evaluative valence.

- **[[generosity]]** — The generous person feels hatred only toward genuine evils, and even then without the compounding excess of sadness.

## 后世影响与解释

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' treatment of hatred as the structural opposite of [[love]] — both defined by their intentional objects (good/harmful) rather than their felt qualities — was a significant break from the [[aristotle|Aristotelian]] tradition, which classified [[passion|passions]] primarily by their qualitative feel.

This intentionalist approach influenced Spinoza's *Ethics* (where hatred is "sadness accompanied by the idea of an external cause") and continues to inform contemporary philosophy of emotion, particularly cognitive theories that emphasise the role of appraisal and judgment in emotional experience.
