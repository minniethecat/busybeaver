# Article 198: The function of indignation

[198-001] Indignation shows up much more in people who want to appear virtuous than in ones who really are. Those who love virtue can’t see the vices of others without some aversion, but they become impassioned only about the greatest and most extraordinary vices.

[198-002] •To be highly indignant about trivial matters is to be difficult and peevish;

[198-003] •To be indignant about matters for which no-one can be blamed is to be unjust;

[198-004] •To direct one’s indignation not only to the actions of human beings but also to the works of God or of nature is impertinent and absurd.

[198-005] The last of those is what is done by those who—constantly discontented with their condition and their fortune—venture to find fault with how the world is governed and with the secrets of Providence.
