# Article 91: The definition of joy

[091-001] Joy is a pleasant commotion in the soul—it is

[091-002] the soul’s enjoyment of a good that •impressions in the brain represent to it as its own.

[091-003] I say that this commotion is the soul’s enjoyment of a good, for in fact that’s the only benefit the soul gets from all the goods it possesses; any good from which it doesn’t get joy might as well be one that it doesn’t possess. I add that the good is one that impressions in the brain represent as the soul’s own, so as not to confuse this joy, which is a passion, with the purely intellectual joy that arises in the soul through an action of the soul alone. The latter may be said to be a pleasant commotion that the soul arouses in itself whenever

[091-004] it enjoys a good that •its understanding represents to it as its own.

[091-005] While the soul is joined to the body, of course, this intellectual joy can hardly fail to be accompanied by the joy that is a passion. For as soon as our understanding perceives that we possess some good—even one that is so unlike anything pertaining to the body that we can’t imagine it—the imagination nevertheless immediately forms in the brain an impression from which there ensues the movement of the spirits that arouses the passion of joy.
