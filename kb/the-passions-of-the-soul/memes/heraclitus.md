# Heraclitus (c. 535–475 BCE)

**Heraclitus** (c. 535–475 BCE) appears in *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]* in a single brief reference (Article 196) as the paradigmatic "weeping philosopher." [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] invokes both Heraclitus and [[democritus|Democritus]] as a pair to illustrate how individual temperament shapes the expression of the [[passion|passions]].

## 思想史定位

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' reference to Heraclitus occurs in Part III, Article 196, which analyzes why different people react to the same wrongdoing with different [[passion|passions]] — some with [[indignation]] mixed with [[pity]], others with [[indignation]] mixed with [[derision]]:

> To do evil is also in some way to receive evil; and therefore some people combine pity to their indignation, and others derision, depending on whether they bear good-will or ill-will towards those whom they see acting wrongly. So the laughter of Democritus and the tears of Heraclitus could have come from the same cause. [[196-001]]

> It was said in ancient times that Democritus laughed at human folly, which reduced Heraclitus to tears. [[196-002]]

The point is physiological and psychological rather than moral: two people confronting the same object — human folly — generate different [[passion|passions]] (amusement vs. [[sadness|grief]]) because of differences in their bodily temperament and habitual associations. The same "cause" — witnessing human wrongdoing — produces contrary [[passion|passions]] in different individuals, a phenomenon [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] explains not through moral evaluation but through the mechanics of brain traces and [[animal-spirits|animal spirit]] flows.

Heraclitus is not engaged as a philosophical authority in his own right. His doctrines of universal flux (*panta rhei*), the *logos*, and f[[anger|ire]] as the primordial substance are neither endorsed nor refuted in *The [[the-passions-of-the-soul|Passions of the Soul]]*. He functions solely as a recognizable exemplar of a melancholic temperament, preserved from antiquity as a contrast to [[democritus|Democritus]]' sanguine disposition.
