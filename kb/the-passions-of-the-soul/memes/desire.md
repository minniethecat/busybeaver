# Desire (Le Désir)

**Des[[anger|ire]]** (*le désir*) is one of the six primitive [[passion|passions]] in [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' taxonomy. It is unique among them in being inherently **future-d[[anger|ire]]cted**: whereas [[love]], [[hatred]], [[joy]], and [[sadness]] are oriented toward present objects, des[[anger|ire]] is an agitation that reaches forward in time, impelling the soul to obtain an absent good or avoid a threatening evil.

## 定义

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]] defines des[[anger|ire]] through its temporal orientation in Article 86:

> The passion of desire is an agitation of the soul caused by the animal spirits, which disposes the soul to want — for the future — the things it represents to itself as suitable. [[086-001]]

This definition isolates three essential features: (1) des[[anger|ire]] is bodily-caused (by the [[animal-spirits|animal spirit]]s), (2) it is future-d[[anger|ire]]cted ("for the future"), and (3) it is d[[anger|ire]]cted at what the soul *represents to itself* as suitable — not necessarily what is truly good.

Des[[anger|ire]] must be carefully distinguished from volition (an action of the soul) and [[love]] (a joining to a present object):

> I use the phrase *de volonté* to talk about desire, which is a completely different thing from the passion of desire. [[080-001]]

## 笛卡尔的论述

### 欲望与嫌恶

The traditional distinction between des[[anger|ire]] (for good) and aversion (from evil) is, [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] argues, a single passion:

> They call the former 'desire' and the latter 'aversion'. But because there is no good whose privation is not an evil, and no evil whose privation is not a good, the same passion is at work whether we pursue a good or flee an opposite evil. [[087-001]]

This unification is characteristic of [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' method: reduce the [[passion|passions]] to their essential structure rather than multiplying kinds based on surface differences.

### 欲望的种类

Although [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] resists multiplying kinds, he acknowledges that des[[anger|ire]] varies enormously:

> There are as many kinds of desire as there are of love or hatred, and the strongest and most important are those that are born from love and hatred respectively. [[088-001]]

A des[[anger|ire]] born from [[love]] is the eager reaching toward a be[[love]]d good; a des[[anger|ire]] born from hatred is the anxious fleeing from a hated evil. The former is expansive and hopeful; the latter is contractive and often accompanied by [[sadness]]:

> The same desire is accompanied by hatred, anxiety and sadness when we judge it to be contrary to some other desire. [[087-003]]

### 欲望与时间

Des[[anger|ire]]'s defining characteristic is its future orientation:

> All desire looks forward — not to the present, but to some future time. [[057-001]]

This makes des[[anger|ire]] fundamentally different from the other primitive [[passion|passions]]. Love and joy are about what is present; des[[anger|ire]] is about what is absent and pursued. This temporal structure explains why des[[anger|ire]] is the passion most susceptible to excess: its object is always not-yet-attained, so it can never be fully satisfied by what is present.

### 生理表现

Des[[anger|ire]] shares physiological features with both [[love]] and hatred, depending on its origin:

- Des[[anger|ire]] born from [[love]]: accelerated pulse, warmth in the chest
- Des[[anger|ire]] born from hatred: agitation, irregular pulse, tension

In all cases, des[[anger|ire]] produces an extraordinary alertness in the sense organs:

> Desire ... makes all the senses more acute, and makes all the parts of the body more mobile. [[086-001]] (paraphrase)

## 与其他概念的关系

- **[[love]]** — Des[[anger|ire]] is the future-d[[anger|ire]]cted motion that [[love]] generates; we des[[anger|ire]] what we [[love]] but do not yet possess.

- **[[hatred]]** — Hatred generates the des[[anger|ire]] to flee or destroy; des[[anger|ire]] is the temporal extension of both [[love]] and hatred.

- **[[joy]]** — Joy is the [[joy|enjoyment]] of a present good; des[[anger|ire]] is the pursuit of an absent one. They are complementary across time.

- **[[sadness]]** — Des[[anger|ire]] compounded with sadness becomes anxious longing; des[[anger|ire]] compounded with joy becomes hopeful anticipation.

- **[[hope]]** — Hope is a species of des[[anger|ire]] d[[anger|ire]]cted at a good judged to be attainable (not treated in the primitive six but derived from des[[anger|ire]]).

- **[[free-will]]** — Volition (an action of the soul) is categorically distinct from the passion of des[[anger|ire]]; confusing them obscures the action/passion distinction foundational to [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' theory.

## 后世影响与解释

[[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' analysis of des[[anger|ire]] as a future-d[[anger|ire]]cted passion distinct from both [[love]] and volition influenced Spinoza's *conatus* doctrine (the striving to persevere in being, which Spinoza identified with des[[anger|ire]] as the essence of man) and later existentialist accounts of des[[anger|ire]] as constitutive of human temporality (Heidegger's *Sorge*, Sartre's *désir*).

The distinction between "des[[anger|ire]]-as-lack" (Platonic) and "des[[anger|ire]]-as-productive" (Deleuzian) can be traced in part to the ambiguities in [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' own treatment.
