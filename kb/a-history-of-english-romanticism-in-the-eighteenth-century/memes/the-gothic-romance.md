# The Gothic Romance

## Definition

The Gothic romance is, in Beers's history, the class of prose fiction that "starts with '[[the-castle-of-otranto|The Castle of Otranto]]'" — the haunted-castle tale that carried the medieval revival into the novel. (006-001)

Where [[gothicism]] names the antiquarian *taste* for medieval manners and architecture, the Gothic romance is the narrative *form* that taste produced: [[horace-walpole|Walpole]]'s "Castle of Otranto, A Gothic Story" was published in 1765 as a deliberate experiment in fiction. (007-032)

Beers treats it as a distinct "class of novel" — the "prototype" of a lineage "afterward imitated by [[anne-radcliffe|Mrs. Radcliffe]] and perfected by [[walter-scott|Sir Walter Scott]]." (007-020)

## Origins & Development

On Beers's account the form was "a casual outgrowth of [Walpole's] architectural amusements": his toy castle at Strawberry Hill "begat 'The Castle of Otranto.'" (007-032)

Walpole himself called the book "an attempt to blend the two kinds of romance, the ancient and the modern," and his "claim to having created a new species of romance has been generally allowed." (007-033)

Its "novel feature," Beers writes, "was its Gothic setting; the 'wind whistling through the battlements'; the secret trap-door, with iron ring, by which Isabella sought to make her escape." (007-038)

Beers notes an even earlier specimen in Thomas Leland's "Longsword, Earl of Salisbury, An Historical Romance" (1762), "probably the first fiction of the kind in English literature," though in it "the Gothic properties are few." (007-050)

## Key Figures & Works

Walpole supplied the prototype; his imitators fixed the genre. The first was [[clara-reeve|Clara Reeve]]'s "Champion of Virtue" (1777), likewise "styled on its title-page 'A Gothic Story,'" and reprinted as "[[the-old-english-baron|The Old English Baron]]." (007-041)

Reeve owned her book a "literary descendant of 'Otranto,'" and Scott, contributing her memoir, "defended Walpole's frank use of the supernatural against her criticisms" and "gave the preference to Walpole's method." (007-051)

The genre's most popular hand was [[anne-radcliffe|Mrs. Radcliffe]], whose fictions add "a tone that is absent from Walpole's: [[romanticism]] plus [[sentimentalism]]." (007-056)

Her romances are "not usually mediaeval in subject," yet "her machinery is prevailingly Gothic and the real hero of the story is commonly, as in Walpole, some haunted building." (007-058)

The apparatus reached its extravagant limit in [[matthew-lewis|Lewis]]'s "[[the-monk|Monk]]" (1795), "a three-volume romance of the Gothic type, and a lineal descendant of Walpole and Mrs. Radcliffe." (011-062)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

For Beers the Gothic romance answered a hunger the age's poetry had left unfed — "the longing of the time for freedom, adventure, strong action, and emotion." (011-049)

Its vogue, he argues, "infected not merely the department of the chivalry play and the Gothic romance, but prose fiction in general," spreading into "morbid and fantastic creations like Beckford's 'Vathek,' Godwin's 'St. Leon' and 'Caleb Williams.'" (011-049)

Eastlake's catalogue of the type — "the feudal tyrant, the venerable ecclesiastic, the forlorn but virtuous damsel, the castle itself with its moats and drawbridge, its gloomy dungeons and solemn corridors" — became the standing scenery of "the romantic school." (007-020)

Through Radcliffe and Scott, Beers makes it the channel by which the [[medievalism|medieval]] and [[chivalry|chivalric]] past re-entered popular narrative, feeding directly into the historical romance of the next century. (007-033)

## Contrasts & Debates

The genre's great vulnerability was its own machinery. Reviewing Otranto, Reeve objected that "the machinery is so violent that it destroys the effect it is intended to excite," and set out "to compose a work upon the same plan, wherein these defects might be avoided." (007-041)

Critics divided over the supernatural itself: Radcliffe explained her ghosts away by natural causes, while Lewis "used, and abused, the now familiar apparatus of Gothic romance" — "magic mirrors, enchanted wands, midnight incantations, sorcerers, ghosts, demons." (011-062)

Beers reads the whole vogue with amused detachment, "laughable as many of them seem today, with their improbable plots and exaggerated characters," yet granting that they "met a need" the Augustan wits and the elegiac poets alike had missed. (011-049)
