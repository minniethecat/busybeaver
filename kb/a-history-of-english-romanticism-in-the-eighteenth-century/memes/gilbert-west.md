## Overview

Gilbert West was an English poet and classical translator whose Spenserian imitations placed him among the earliest forerunners of the romantic revival. Best known for his translations of Pindar and for Spenserian poems such as "Education" (1751) and "On the Abuse of Travelling" (1739), West contributed to the eighteenth-century rediscovery of [[edmund-spenser]] as a model for English poetry. His work also tapped the vein of Druidic and primitive imagery that would become a hallmark of the [[gothicism|Gothic]] and romantic sensibility.

## Life

West was educated at Eton and subsequently at Oxford, though Dr. Johnson records that he was "seduced to a more airy mode of life by a commission in a troop of horse, procured him by his uncle." (003-072) There was no family relationship between Gilbert West and [[thomas-gray]]'s Eton friend Richard West, though Gray and [[horace-walpole]] read Gilbert West's early Spenserian imitation with enthusiasm. Gray wrote to Richard West from Florence in 1740: "Mr. Walpole and I have frequently wondered you should never mention a certain imitation of Spenser, published last year by a namesake of yours, with which we are all enraptured and enmarvelled." (003-072)

West later settled at Wickham, where he was visited by [[william-shenstone]] and other literary figures of the Spenserian circle. (004-098)

## Works & Contribution

West published his first Spenserian imitation, "On the Abuse of Travelling: A Canto in Imitation of Spenser," in 1739. This was followed by "Education" in 1751, another Spenserian poem that contained, among other things, an attack in six stanzas upon the formal geometric garden — a theme closely aligned with the emerging romantic preference for natural wildness over artificial order. (004-096)

Beers judges West to have been "a very tame poet, and the only quality of Spenser's which he succeeded in catching was his prolixity." West used "the allegorical machinery of the 'Faërie Queene' for moral and mildly satirical ends." (003-037) Despite this lukewarm assessment, West's choice of Spenser as a model placed him within the vanguard of poets who, from the 1730s onward, turned away from the heroic couplet toward older English forms.

West's most substantial contribution to the romantic stock of imagery appears in his "Institution of the Order of the Garter," which contains a striking passage describing the ghosts of Druids hovering about the ruined altars at Stonehenge. [[joseph-warton]] quoted these lines in his "Essay on Pope" as an illustration of the poetic capabilities of druidical themes, alongside a stanza from Gray's "Bard." (006-010)

West was also the translator of Pindar, a labor that connected him to the cult of the ode and the sublime that ran parallel to the Spenserian revival. (003-037)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

West occupies a modest but instructive place in the early history of English romanticism. His Spenserian imitations, published in the 1730s and 1750s, predate the full flourishing of the Gothic revival and belong to what Beers calls the "Spenserian" phase of the eighteenth-century romantic movement. (003-037)

The romantic elements in West's poetry are of two kinds. First, his use of the Spenserian stanza and allegorical machinery aligned him with the general movement away from Augustan classicism toward older English models. Second, his Druidic imagery — those "mysterious rows of rude enormous obelisks" at Stonehenge, "by the pale moon discerned on Sarum's plain" — anticipated the romantic fascination with the primitive, the ancient, and the mysteriously sublime. (006-010)

His attack on the geometric garden in "Education" likewise placed him on the side of nature against artifice, a central romantic polarity. Beers quotes from this poem in the context of [[landscape-gardening]] and the transition from formal to natural garden design. (004-096)

## Reception & Influence

West's reputation never approached that of his contemporaries Gray or [[william-collins]], and his poetry has been largely forgotten. Yet his early adoption of Spenser as a model, at a time when Pope and the heroic couplet still dominated English verse, makes him a significant figure in the genealogy of the romantic revival. The favorable attention his work received from Gray and Walpole confirms that the Spenserian impulse was felt, even in the 1730s, as a fresh and promising direction for English poetry. (003-072)
