# The Spenserian Stanza

## Definition

The Spenserian stanza is the verse form of [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]]'s [[the-faerie-queene|"Faërie Queene"]], and in Beers's account its eighteenth-century revival was above all a revolt of form. "The name inscribed in large letters on the standard of the new school was not Shakspere but Spenser" (003-017).

Its virtue, for the age, lay in "the amplitude of Spencer's stanza, 'the full strong sail of his great verse,'" a "serene, leisurely manner, copious Homeric similes, and lingering detail" that offered relief from the couplet (003-017). Beers quotes John Philips's earlier praise: "How much more stately and majestic in epic poems, especially of heroic argument, Spenser's stanza... is above the way either of couplet or alternation of four verses only" (003-070).

## Origins & Development

Beers dates the modern series of imitations from "a most unlikely person, Matthew Prior, whose 'Ode to the Queen,' 1706, was in a ten-lined modification of Spenser's stanza and employed a few archaisms" (003-029). Samuel Croxall followed with cantos "in the stanza of the 'Faërie Queene'" (003-029).

The form served both mock-heroic and serious ends. Cambridge's "Archimage," Beers notes, was "written in the strict Spenserian stanza," illustrating "the frequent employment of this form in occasional pieces of a humorous intention" (003-032). More broadly the revival belonged to a "general change in the forms of verse," a "readiness to lay aside [[the-heroic-couplet|the heroic couplet]] in favor of other kinds" (004-003).

## Key Figures & Works

Beers draws a sharp line between echoers and originators: "just as [[james-thomson|Thomson]] and [[william-shenstone|Shenstone]] got original effects from Spenser's stanza, while West and Cambridge and Lloyd were nothing but echoes" (005-008). Thomson's [[the-castle-of-indolence|"Castle of Indolence"]] and Shenstone's [[the-schoolmistress|"Schoolmistress"]] stand among the successful adaptations of the form (004-003).

A late and composite example is [[james-beattie|Beattie]]'s [[the-minstrel|"The Minstrel"]] (1771–74): "It was in the Spenserian stanza, was tinged with the enthusiastic melancholy of the Wartons, followed the landscape manner of Thomson" (008-087). Beattie justified the choice on the ground that the stanza "seems, from its Gothic structure and original, to bear some relation to the subject and spirit of the poem" (008-091).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers judges the true legacy of the minor Spenserians to lie not in their own verse but in the form they preserved. Their "real service was done in reviving a taste for a better kind of poetry," and "particularly in restoring to English verse a stanza form, which became so noble an instrument in the hands of later poets" (003-056).

Those later poets, he adds, "used it with as much freedom and vigor as if they had never seen the 'Faërie Queene'": "One is seldom reminded of Spenser while reading 'Childe Harold' or 'Adonais' or 'The Eve of Saint Agnes'" (003-056). The stanza thus carried a Spenserian inheritance forward into the full Romantic movement while shedding the archaism that marked its revival.

## Contrasts & Debates

The Spenserian stanza is best understood against the metre it displaced. Beers frames the whole revival as an "escape" from a "generation surfeited with Pope's rhetorical devices," from the couplet's "narrow imprisonment of the sense" into Spenser's "copious" amplitude (003-017). Where [[spenserianism|Spenserian imitation]] as a movement embraced diction, archaism, and allegory, the stanza is its purely metrical bequest, set apart from both [[the-heroic-couplet|the couplet]] and [[blank-verse|blank verse]] as the age's alternatives.
