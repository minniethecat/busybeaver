# Sentimentalism

## Definition

Sentimentalism is one of those critical words that Beers counts among the terms which "connote so much" that no single sentence can define them — set beside renaissance, philistine, and pre-Raphaelite as a mark of identification rather than a fixed idea (001-001). At its core it names an appeal to the emotions by the method of suggestion, the faculty Beers pairs against reason as "passion and sentiment" (001-023). He treats it as native to the romantic temper: the greater spirituality of romantic literature carries with it "its deeper emotion, its more passionate tenderness," but "hence likewise its sentimentality, its melancholy and, in particular, the morbid fascination which the thought of death has had for the Gothic mind" (001-029). The medieval work romanticism looked back to was itself "marked by an excess of sentiment," a strong sense of color, and a feeble sense of form (001-006).

## Origins & Development

Beers frames sentimentalism as one current in a wider return "of warmth and color--that is, of emotion and imagination--into English life and thought," a revolt against "the coldness and spiritual deadness of the first half of the eighteenth century" that also produced the Methodist and Evangelical revival (002-012). Following Leslie Stephen, he links three currents of tendency — sentimentalism, romanticism, and naturalism — noting that the first English sentimentalists, Richardson and Sterne, "were anything but romantic," and that the shared "return to nature" expressed "emotions which have been repressed by existing conventions" (002-013). The taste ripened through the landscape poets: Thomson's rhapsodies on domestic love, his pity for caged birds and hunted hares, "foretoken the sentimentalism of Sterne and Goldsmith," and that "half affected itch of simplicity which titillated the sensibilities of a corrupt and artificial society" in Rousseau and Bernardin de St. Pierre (004-017). By mid-century the reaction against Queen Anne gayety had made the elegiac mood dominant, so that "the imaginative literature of the years 1740-60 was largely the literature of low spirits" (005-033).

## Key Figures & Works

The Miltonic group gave sentimentalism its purest lyric voice. Thomas Warton's "The Pleasures of Melancholy" (1747) versified the cult of "cheerless shades" and "twilight cells" where "thoughtful Melancholy loves to muse" (005-026). Collins introduced a personified figure of Melancholy in his ode "The Passions" and filled Dodsley's miscellanies with odes to Evening, Solitude, and Melancholy (005-040); he was himself "afflicted with a melancholia which finally developed into madness," while Gray "suffered from inherited gout and a lasting depression of spirits" (005-041). Young's "Night Thoughts" (1742-44) and Blair's "Grave" (1743) supplied the funereal extreme, with their "rich note of romantic despair" (005-035). This "pensive, elegiac strain" — the mossy cave, the hermit's cell, the drip of spring water — belonged, Beers writes, to "Gray, Collins, and the Wartons" (004-069). Its masterpiece was Gray's "Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard" (1751), in which "this elegiac feeling found its most perfect expression" (005-037) and whose moral "is suffused with emotion and expressed concretely" (005-056). In Collins, Beers finds "a union, as in Keats, of Attic form with romantic sensibility" (005-046).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

For Beers sentimentalism is not merely a mood but a preparatory phase of the [[romanticism|romantic]] movement. The elegiac, melodramatic view of the grave "as an inspirer of pleasing gloom" was, in Perry's phrase that Beers endorses, "preparing readers for the romantic outbreak" (005-036), and the mossy grot and hermit's cell were "a note of the coming romanticism" (004-069). Sentimentalism was thus "one phase of that revolt against the coldness and spiritual deadness" of the age, running parallel to Rousseau's "Nouvelle Héloise" and Goethe's "Sorrows of Werther" on the Continent (002-012), and knit by Leslie Stephen into the same historical fabric as romanticism and naturalism (002-013). See the chapters [[the-landscape-poets|The Landscape Poets]] and [[the-miltonic-group|The Miltonic Group]] for its fullest development.

## Contrasts & Debates

Beers is candid about the affectation that shadowed the cult of feeling. The bachelor poets of the Miltonic group, he observes, were mostly cheerful men, and "the melancholy which these good fellows affected was manifestly a mere literary fashion" — they were sad "only for wantonness" (005-045). The same suspicion attaches to the "half affected itch of simplicity" that titillated a corrupt and artificial society (004-017). A further distinction, drawn from Leslie Stephen, keeps the two movements apart at their origin: the first English sentimentalists, Richardson and Sterne, "were anything but romantic," so that tender feeling and medieval revival, though later intertwined, began as separate impulses (002-013).
