# James Macpherson

## Overview

James Macpherson is the "young clergyman in the Highlands" whose Ossianic poems became one of the great literary sensations — and scandals — of the eighteenth century. Beers records Gray's early excitement over "'my old Scotch (rather Irish) poetry ... said to be translations (literal and in prose) from the *Erse* tongue, done by one MacPherson, a young clergyman in the Highlands'" (009-002). His work stands at the centre of the age's traffic in forged antiquity.

## Life

Beers follows Macpherson through the making of the Ossian poems. Encouraged by "Blair and Mr. Home, MacPherson was prevailed upon to make further translations from the materials in his hands," and these "were published in the 'Fragments' ... with a preface of eight pages by Blair" (009-007). Their success in Edinburgh was such "that a subscription was started, to send the compiler through the Highlands in search of more Gaelic poetry" (009-007), the researches issuing in his epics.

## Works & Contribution

Macpherson's principal works are the two "ancient epics." "The result of the researches was 'Fingal, an Ancient Epic Poem in Six Books ... Translated from the Gaelic language by James MacPherson'" (London, 1762), followed by "'Temora, an Ancient Epic Poem in Eight Books'" (1763) (009-008, 009-009). He "asserted that he had made his versions from Gaelic poems ascribed to Ossian or Oisin, the son of Fingal" (009-009). Beers's summary of the scholarly verdict is that "The most damaging feature of MacPherson's case was his refusal or neglect to produce his originals," though he plainly "had materials of some kind" which he "manipulated ... by piecing fragments together" (009-028). See [[fingal]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Macpherson gave the revival a body of misty, primitive, Northern epic that Augustan poetry could not supply. Beers ranks his "Ossian" among the works that gave "an impulse to romantic literature" (002-002), and its melancholy and wild landscape fed exactly the romantic taste. He is, with Chatterton, the great instance of the age's characteristic mode — the forgery that becomes influential literature (010-015). See [[literary-forgery]] and [[ossian]].

## Reception & Influence

The Ossian poems provoked the fiercest of the century's authenticity debates. Johnson "not only disputed the antiquity of MacPherson's 'Ossian,' but he denied it any poetic merit," calling *Fingal* "'as gross an imposition as ever the world was troubled with'" (009-015). Wordsworth too rejected the imagery as "spurious" (009-022). Yet the poems' European influence was immense, and Beers grants that behind the fraud lay real Gaelic "materials" that Macpherson had reworked (009-028).
