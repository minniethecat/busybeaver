# Horace Walpole

## Overview

Horace Walpole is, for Beers, the man in whom the antiquarian "virtuosity" of the age crossed most conspicuously into literature. "The most notable instance that we encounter of virtuosity invading the neighboring realm of literature is in the case of Strawberry Hill and 'The Castle of Otranto'" (007-014). He was, Beers writes, "the son of the great prime minister, Robert Walpole ... a person of varied accomplishments and undoubted cleverness" (007-014).

## Life

Beers presents Walpole as "a man of fashion, a man of taste, and a man of letters; though, in the first of these characters, he entertained or affected a contempt for the last" (007-014). The dominant fact of his career, for this history, is his medievalizing bent: Eastlake finds it "impossible ... to peruse either the letters or the romances of this remarkable man, without being struck by the unmistakable evidence which they contain of his medieval predilections" (007-020). His villa at Strawberry Hill, the great monument of Gothic "virtuosity," is inseparable from his one famous romance (007-014).

## Works & Contribution

Walpole's chief contribution to Beers's story is *The Castle of Otranto*. Eastlake's verdict, which Beers adopts, is that "His 'Castle of Otranto' was perhaps the first modern work of fiction which depended for its interest on the incidents of a chivalrous age, and it thus became the prototype of that class of novel which was afterward imitated by Mrs. Radcliffe and perfected by Sir Walter Scott" (007-020). Walpole framed the book with a scholarly hoax and defended, in his second preface, "'an attempt to blend the two kinds of romance, the ancient and the modern'" (007-033). He also lent his Strawberry Hill press to Gray, printing "the two 'sister odes'" there in 1757 (005-053). See [[the-castle-of-otranto]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Walpole is a hinge of the Gothic revival. Through Strawberry Hill and *Otranto* he turned antiquarian collecting into imaginative literature (007-014), and his romance became "the prototype" of the whole Gothic-novel tradition running to Radcliffe and Scott (007-020). His "medieval predilections" (007-020) made him a leading agent of the return to the Middle Ages that the revival advanced. See [[gothicism]] and [[the-gothic-revival]].

## Reception & Influence

Walpole's influence, in Beers's account, is measured by the school he founded: the Gothic novel "afterward imitated by Mrs. Radcliffe and perfected by Sir Walter Scott" (007-020). His skeptical acuteness also made him an early exposer of fraud — "lately bitten in the Ossian business," he "had begun to suspect imposture" in Chatterton's Rowley poems (010-025). Beers treats him as at once a maker of romantic fiction and a wary man of the world.
