# Robert Blair

## Overview

Robert Blair is, for Beers, the poet of *The Grave*, one of the two great voices — with Young — of the mid-century "funereal sable" (005-035). Yet Beers is careful to qualify his romanticism: Blair and Young "are scarcely to be reckoned among the romanticists" (005-038), being rather forerunners of the graveyard mood than romantics proper.

## Life

Beers records Blair almost entirely through his one poem. "'The Grave,' by Robert Blair" (005-074) was published in 1743 (005-035), the year before Young's *Night Thoughts* was completed. Beyond the poem and its date Beers keeps to Blair's literary place, grouping him with Young as one of the "heavy didactic-moral poets" of the age (005-038).

## Works & Contribution

Blair's contribution is *The Grave* (1743), a blank-verse meditation on death whose "mortuary verse has a certain impressiveness, in its gloomy monotony, not unlike that of Quarles' 'Divine Emblems'" (005-039). Beers quotes its characteristic imagery — the wind that "'howls,'" the "'night's foul bird ... Rocked in the spire,'" the "'gloomy aisles'" and "'the low vaults, / The mansions of the dead'" (005-039). Like the *Emblems*, "'The Grave' has been kept from oblivion" by that concentrated gloom (005-039). See [[the-grave]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Blair's place in the revival is that of a forerunner rather than a full participant. Beers ranks him among the "heavy didactic-moral poets," who nonetheless "touched the string which, in the Gothic imagination, vibrates" (005-038) — the note of death, night, and the tomb. *The Grave* thus belongs to the sentimental prehistory of romanticism, one of the "shades of somberness" from which the mature elegiac poetry would emerge (005-035). See [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]] and [[blank-verse]].

## Reception & Influence

Beers's estimate places Blair below the true romantics — "scarcely to be reckoned among the romanticists" (005-038) — while granting his poem lasting "impressiveness, in its gloomy monotony" (005-039). *The Grave* survives, with *Night Thoughts*, as the "funereal sable" extreme (005-035) against which Beers measures the more delicate melancholy of Gray and the Wartons.
