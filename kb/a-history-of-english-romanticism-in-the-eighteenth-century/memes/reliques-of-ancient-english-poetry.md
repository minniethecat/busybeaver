# Reliques of Ancient English Poetry

## Overview

The *Reliques of Ancient English Poetry* is [[thomas-percy|Thomas Percy]]'s three-volume collection of old ballads and early verse, and the pivotal book through which the [[the-ballad-revival|ballad revival]] flowed into English [[romanticism]] and [[medievalism]].

Beers calls it the decisive book of its decade: "The decade from 1760 to 1770 is important in the history of English romanticism, and its most important title is Thomas Percy's 'Reliques of Ancient English Poetry'" (008-003).

It was "a revivifying contact with one department, at least, of early verse literature, which did much to clear the way for Scott and Coleridge and Keats" (008-003). Percy's book is treated at length in the chapter [[percy-and-the-ballads|Percy and the Ballads]].

## Author & Date

The book's "most important title," in full "'Reliques of Ancient English Poetry: Consisting of Old Heroic Ballads, Songs, and Other Pieces of our Earlier Poets,'" was "published in three volumes in 1765" by Thomas Percy (008-003).

Percy was also the man who Englished Mallet's Norse antiquities — "Thomas Percy, the editor of the 'Reliques,'" who issued the translation "under the title, '[[northern-antiquities|Northern Antiquities]]'" (006-004).

The *Reliques* followed close on *[[the-castle-of-otranto|The Castle of Otranto]]*, which "appeared in December, 1764, and the 'Reliques,' only in the year following" (010-015).

Percy himself was a country parson: "Thomas Percy, the compiler of the 'Reliques,' was a parish clergyman, settled at the retired hamlet of Easton Maudit, Northamptonshire" who "for years . . . had amused his leisure by collecting ballads" (008-051).

The plan was not wholly his own; it "was the last"—the poet Shenstone—"who suggested the plan of the 'Reliques'" (008-051), and Percy's neighbour at the Leasowes had "encouraged Percy to publish his 'Reliques'" (004-073).

## Composition & Sources

The materials of the collection were gathered from the great antiquarian repositories:

> The materials for the "Reliques" were drawn partly from the Pepys collection at Magdalen College, Cambridge; from Anthony Wood's, made in 1676, in the Ashmolean Museum at Oxford; from manuscript and printed ballads in the Bodleian, the British Museum, the archives of the Antiquarian Society, and private collections. (008-060)

The editor "acknowledged obligations to [[thomas-warton|Thomas Warton]] and many others" (008-060). But the heart of the book was a single relic — "a certain folio manuscript in a handwriting of Charles I.'s time, containing 191 songs and ballads, which Percy had begged, then still very young, from his friend Humphrey Pitt, of Prior's-Lee in Shropshire" (008-060).

Its rescue became a founding legend of the ballad revival: when Percy first saw it, the manuscript was "torn, unbound, and mutilated, 'lying dirty on the floor under a bureau in the parlor, being used by the maids to light the fire'" (008-060). From this famous "folio manuscript"

Percy "professed to have taken 'the greater part' of the pieces in the 'Reliques,'" though "in truth he took only 45 of the 176 poems in his first edition from this source" (008-060).

## Content & Form

As its subtitle declares, the collection gathered "Old Heroic Ballads, Songs, and Other Pieces of our Earlier Poets" (008-003) — the popular and minstrel poetry that Augustan taste had let lapse.

Beers treats it as the recovery of a whole neglected "department ... of early verse literature" (008-003), set beside such other "symptom[s] of the return of" the old poetry as Warton's work (006-080).

The *Reliques* thereby restored to literary currency the ballad measure and the plain narrative manner of "our Earlier Poets" (008-003) — the very qualities the polished couplet had suppressed.

The book mingled genuine folk-poetry with romance-derived pieces and modern lyric. Among the historical and border pieces stood "The Hunting of the Cheviot"—"the elder version of 'Chevy Chase'"—which "was mentioned by Philip Sidney" (008-022); the later "Chevy Chase" was the ballad Addison "praised . . . selecting for special mention" in the *Spectator* (008-044).

To temper the older material, "'to atone for the rudeness of the more obsolete poems,' and 'to take off from the tediousness of the longer narratives,' Percy interspersed a few modern ballads and a large number of 'little elegant pieces of the lyric kind' by Skelton, Hawes, Gascoigne, Raleigh, Marlowe, Shakspere, Jonson . . . and other well-known poets" (008-078).

The first edition also "included one acknowledged child of Percy's muse, 'The Friar of Orders Grey,'" while "later editions afforded his longer poem, 'The Hermit of Warkworth,' first published independently in 1771" (008-079).

## Significance to Romanticism

Beers rates the *Reliques* above even [[ossian|Ossian]] for lasting effect: "It made a less immediate and exciting impression upon contemporary Europe than MacPherson's 'Poems of Ossian,' but it was more fruitful in enduring results" (008-003).

With *The Castle of Otranto* it forms the twin landmark of its decade (006-059), and "with Percy's 'Reliques,' it had promoted, beyond any" other single force the medieval revival (006-044). Above all it "did much to clear the way for Scott and Coleridge and Keats" (008-003) — the direct line into the great age of romantic poetry.

Beers underscores its epochal standing:

> With all its imperfections--perhaps partly in consequence of its imperfections--the "Reliques" was an epoch-making book. (008-080)

Its fertility is measured by what it set in motion. "Percy not only rescued, himself, a number of ballads from forgetfulness; . . . his book prompted others to hunt out and publish similar relics before it was too late," being "the occasion of collections like Herd's (1769), Scott's (1802-03), and Motherwell's (1827), and many more" (008-082).

Percy's *Reliques* "prompted the 'Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border'" (011-052). Beers goes so far as to say that "without the 'Reliques,' 'The Ancient Mariner,' 'The Lady of the Lake,' 'La Belle Dame sans Merci,' 'Stratton Water,' and 'The Haystack in the Floods' might never have been" (008-082).

Its ballad strain also fed the forgeries of Chatterton in the [[the-rowley-poems|Rowley Poems]], which give "the ballad strain of the 'Reliques,' but *plus* something of" more (010-068).

## Reception & Controversy

The book's influence worked through the next generation of poets.

Wordsworth, in the *Lyrical Ballads*, testified that "I do not think there is a writer in verse of the present day who would not be proud to acknowledge his obligations to the 'Reliques'" (008-082), and rated Percy's genius so high that he quoted "two stanzas from 'The Child of Elle' in the 'Reliques'" against Bürger's German version (008-056).

Yet Percy's editorial method drew fire. He "made no secret of the fact that he filled *lacunae* in his originals with stanzas, and, in some cases, with nearly entire poems of his own composition," and was "furiously assailed by Joseph Ritson for manipulating his texts," until "Mr. Furnivall finally got leave to have the folio manuscript copied and printed" (008-061).

Modern scholars agreed: of "The Child of Elle," Hales complained that "in the 'Reliques' it is buried in a heap of 'polished' verses composed by Percy" (008-057).

Abroad, the collection was decisive:

> The "Reliques" worked powerfully in Germany, too. It was received in Lessing's circle with universal enthusiasm, and fell in with that newly aroused interest in "Volkslieder" which prompted Herder's "Stimmen der Völker" (1778-79). (008-084)

Bürger "may be said to have been made by the English ballad literature," publishing "five translations from Percy" (008-084), and Herder's own preface "testifies that the 'Reliques' was the starting-point and the kernel of his whole undertaking" (008-097).

Closer to home, the young Walpole "had fed on Percy's 'Reliques' in boyhood" (006-001), and the collection stands, with Ossian and the Norse revival, among the forces that "promoted, beyond any" other the return to the Middle Ages (006-044).
