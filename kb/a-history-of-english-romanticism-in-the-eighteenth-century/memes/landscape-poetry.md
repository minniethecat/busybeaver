# Landscape Poetry

## Definition

Landscape poetry is Beers's name for the eighteenth-century verse "that concerns itself with rural life or natural scenery," which he is careful not to equate outright with romance: "There is nothing necessarily romantic in literature that concerns itself with rural life or natural scenery" (004-001). Yet he accepts, "with some qualification," Professor McClintock's statement that "the 'beginning and presence of a creative, romantic movement is almost always shown by the love, study, and interpretation of physical nature'" (004-001). The connection is not accidental: quoting Ruskin and Leslie Stephen, Beers holds that "naturalism and romanticism had a common root: the desire, namely, to escape into the fresh air and into freer conditions, from a literature which dealt, in a strictly regulated way, with the indoor life of a highly artificial society" (004-001).

## Origins & Development

The pastoral had exhausted itself — "Professing to chant the praises of innocence and simplicity, it had become itself utterly unreal and conventional, in the hands of cockneys like Philips and Pope" — and "When the romantic spirit took possession of the poetry of nature, it manifested itself in a passion for wildness, grandeur, solitude" (004-001). Of this passion there was "as yet comparatively little even in the verse of Thomson, Shenstone, Akenside, and Dyer," yet "the work of these pioneers in the 'return to nature' represents the transition, and must be taken into account in any complete history of the romantic movement" (004-001, 004-002). An outward sign of the change was formal: "the general change in the forms of verse ... was an outward sign of the revolution in poetic feeling," as poets showed "a readiness to lay aside the heroic couplet in favor of other kinds" — blank verse for "The Seasons," "Night Thoughts," and "The Grave"; Spenserian stanza for "The Castle of Indolence" and "The Schoolmistress"; octosyllabics for "Grongar Hill" (004-003).

## Key Figures & Works

The founding text was James Thomson's "The Seasons," begun with "Winter" in 1726, "no sooner read than universally admired" (004-005). Beers stresses "the stronger contrast ... between Thomson and Pope, not alone in subject and feeling, but in diction and verse": "Thomson's style is florid and luxuriant, his numbers flowing and diffuse, while Pope had wonted the English ear to the extreme of compression" (004-005). Thomson "was something of a naturalist, who wrote lovingly and with his 'eye upon the object,'" so that "His images of external nature are never false and seldom vague, like Pope's" (004-007). Coleridge's contrast serves Beers well: "The love of nature seems to have led Thomson to a cheerful religion, and a gloomy religion to have led Cowper to a love of nature" (004-007). Among the other "pioneers," Beers notes that Dyer "was a landscape painter, as well as a poet; and Shenstone was one of the best of landscape gardeners" (004-002); Thomson's manner spread so widely that "'There was hardly one verse writer of any eminence,' says Gosse, 'from 1725-50, who was not in some manner guided or biased by Thomson'" (004-005).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Landscape poetry matters to Beers as one of the tributaries of the romantic revival — the naturalist strand that ran, at root, with the romantic desire "to escape into the fresh air and into freer conditions" (004-001). He is careful about its limits: he will not "pursue the history of nature poetry into its later developments," passing over "Cowper and Crabbe ... neither of whom was romantic in any sense — or even of Wordsworth, the spirit of whose art, as a whole, was far from romantic" (004-002). "But it is the beginnings that are important" (004-002). Thomson's descriptions of "the first spring showers, the thunderstorm in summer, the trout-fishing, the sheep-washing, and the terrors of the winter night" were "not only strange to the public of that day, but were new in English poetry" — the revelation of "a fresh world of beauty" (004-006). See [[romanticism]] for the movement this naturalism fed, and [[the-picturesque]] for the parallel aesthetic of scenery.

## Contrasts & Debates

Beers repeatedly measures Thomson's "simple descriptive or pictorial method" against later poets, conceding "we are perhaps too apt to undervalue" it — "Compared with Wordsworth's mysticism, with Shelley's passionate pantheism, with Byron's romantic gloom ... it seems to us unimaginative" (004-006). Thomson has been "likened, as a colorist, to Rubens," his best passages sharing that painter's "glow ... breadth, and ... vital energy" (004-006). The geologist Hugh Miller, visiting the Hagley landscape Thomson had painted in "Spring," explained the poet's "enumerative style" — "in dealing with very extended prospects, he rather enumerates than describes" — as the honest record of a real prospect of "at least fifty miles" whose "amazing, overpowering multiplicity ... neither the pen nor the pencil can adequately express," so that "description, in even the hands of a master, sinks into mere enumeration" (004-009).
