## Overview

The Lake Country — the fells and waters of Cumberland and Westmoreland — is, for Beers, ground that "has since become classic" but which the eighteenth century had to discover. [[thomas-gray]]'s tour of it in 1769, recorded in his "Journal in the Lakes," is the book's chief early witness to the modern feeling for mountain scenery, anticipating Wordsworth by a full generation (005-064).

## Description & Location

Beers calls Gray's Lakes tour "the most significant of Gray's 'Lilliputian travels'" (005-064). "The lover of Wordsworth encounters with a singular interest, in Gray's 'Journal in the Lakes,' written nearly thirty years before the 'Lyrical Ballads,' names like Grasmere, Winander, Skiddaw, Helvellyn, Derwentwater, Borrowdale, and Lodore" (005-064). The region's growing fame bred its own literature — a "'Descriptive Poem,' on the Lake Country, in octosyllabic couplets," and prose surveys such as "The Mountains and Lakes of Cumberland and Westmoreland" (005-079). The same north-country of "Westmoreland, and Cumberland" was also part of "the ballad land" of the older minstrelsy (008-005).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

What sets Gray's journal apart, Beers argues, is not the itinerary but the tone: "a certain intimacy of comprehension, a depth of tone which makes them seem like nineteenth-century writing" (005-064). In this the Lakes tour prefigures the central achievement of English romanticism — the marriage of exact natural observation with deep feeling that Wordsworth would complete. The place belongs with the other landscapes that taught the age to look at nature freshly, alongside the gardens of [[the-leasowes|the Leasowes]] and the sublimity of [[the-alps|the Alps]]; it is a key waypoint in the rise of [[landscape-poetry]] and [[the-picturesque]]. The region drew the Gothic novelist Ann Radcliffe too, who "accompanied her husband in excursions to the Lakes and other parts of England," and whose diary sketches of such scenery were, Beers thought, "much superior in the truthfulness and local color of their nature sketching to anything in her novels" (007-064).
