# Letters on Chivalry and Romance

## Overview

*Letters on Chivalry and Romance* is Richard Hurd's set of critical letters (1762), one of the founding documents of romantic criticism. Beers credits them directly with the movement's beginnings: Hurd's "'Letters on Chivalry and Romance' (1762) helped to initiate the romantic movement" (007-001). Their purpose was frankly revisionist — "to prove 'the pre-eminence of the Gothic manners and fictions, as adapted to the ends of poetry, above the classic'" (007-002).

## Author & Date

The *Letters* are the work of Richard Hurd, "afterward Bishop of Lichfield and Coventry, and later of Worcester," "a friend of Gray and Mason" (007-001). They appeared in 1762 and "perhaps owed their inspiration, in part, to Sainte Palaye's 'Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie,'" the standard French authority on the subject (007-001).

## Summary / Content

Hurd argues that the greatest poets drew their power from romance, not from classical learning: "'The greatest geniuses ... such as Ariosto and Tasso in Italy, and Spenser and Milton in England, were seduced by these barbarities of their forefathers; were even charmed by the Gothic romances'" (007-002). He presses the case through Shakespeare — who "'is greater when he uses Gothic manners and machinery, than when he employs classical'" — and even through Tasso, whose classical imitations he finds "'faint and cold and almost insipid, when compared with his original fictions'" of "'Gothic invention'" (007-007). The "Gothic manners of chivalry," he explains, sprang "'out of the feudal system'" (007-006).

## Form & Style

The work is criticism in epistolary form — a set of letters, learned but urbane. Beers ranks Hurd among the "mild clerical essayists ... who amused their leisure with general speculations about the origin of romance" (007-049), the argumentative manner being that of the polite eighteenth-century essay rather than the antiquarian's collection.

## Significance to Romanticism

The *Letters* supplied the revival with its medievalizing theory. By arguing "the pre-eminence of the Gothic manners and fictions ... above the classic" for poetry (007-002), Hurd gave critical warrant to everything the Spenserian and Gothic revivals were attempting in practice. Beers's placing of the book at the head of the movement — it "helped to initiate the romantic movement" (007-001) — marks its importance as the revival's aesthetic manifesto in prose. See [[chivalry]] and [[gothicism]].

## Reception & Controversy

Beers weighs the *Letters* soberly against the antiquarian collectors: "Scholars like Percy, Tyrwhitt, and Ritson, who ... rescued the fragments of ancient minstrelsy ... performed a more useful service than mild clerical essayists, such as Beattie and Hurd, who amused their leisure with general speculations about the origin of romance" (007-049). Even so, the *Letters* proved seminal, their argument closely followed by later writers on romance (007-048).
