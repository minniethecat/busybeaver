# Richard Hurd

## Overview

Richard Hurd is, for Beers, the critic whose *Letters on Chivalry and Romance* gave the medieval revival its theory. Hurd's "'Letters on Chivalry and Romance' (1762) helped to initiate the romantic movement" (007-001), arguing "the pre-eminence of the Gothic manners and fictions, as adapted to the ends of poetry, above the classic" (007-002).

## Life

Beers records the outline of Hurd's career: he was "afterward Bishop of Lichfield and Coventry, and later of Worcester," and "a friend of Gray and Mason" (007-001). He belonged to the class of "mild clerical essayists, such as Beattie and Hurd, who amused their leisure with general speculations about the origin of romance" (007-049). Beyond this literary and ecclesiastical placing, Beers keeps to Hurd's writings rather than his private life.

## Works & Contribution

Hurd's contribution is the *Letters on Chivalry and Romance* (1762), which "perhaps owed their inspiration, in part, to Sainte Palaye's 'Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie'" (007-001). Their argument is that romance-matter is the true source of poetic power: the great poets "'were seduced by these barbarities of their forefathers; were even charmed by the Gothic romances'" (007-002), and "Shakspere 'is greater when he uses Gothic manners and machinery, than when he employs classical'" (007-007). See [[letters-on-chivalry-and-romance]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Hurd supplied the revival with its critical warrant in the medieval key. By arguing "the pre-eminence of the Gothic manners and fictions ... above the classic" for poetry (007-002), he gave theoretical form to what the Spenserians and the Gothic revival were doing in practice. Beers's placing of his *Letters* at the head of the movement — they "helped to initiate the romantic movement" (007-001) — marks Hurd as one of the revival's founding critics. See [[chivalry]] and [[gothicism]].

## Reception & Influence

Hurd's argument proved seminal, "closely" followed by later writers on romance such as Clara Reeve and Beattie (007-048). Beers weighs him soberly against the collectors, judging that scholars "like Percy, Tyrwhitt, and Ritson ... performed a more useful service than mild clerical essayists, such as Beattie and Hurd" (007-049) — yet the *Letters* remained, for the history of criticism, a founding document of the romantic turn (007-001).
