# The Faërie Queene

## Overview

*The Faërie Queene* is Edmund Spenser's great Elizabethan romance-epic, the poem that the eighteenth-century revival raised as its banner. Beers calls it "the typical work of the English Renaissance" (002-018), and makes it the emblem of the new taste: "The name inscribed in large letters on the standard of the new school was not Shakspere but Spenser," for "If there is any poet who is *par excellence* the poet of romance, whose art is the antithesis of Pope's, it is the poet of the 'Faërie Queene'" (003-017).

## Author & Date

The poem is the work of Edmund Spenser, the Elizabethan whom the Augustan revaluation had to restore against Pope; Beers notes "the then paradoxical thesis that Spenser was as great a man as Pope" (002-013). Its central appeal for the mid-century was formal and imaginative — a release from the couplet into "the amplitude of Spencer's stanza, 'the full strong sail of his great verse'" (003-017). The poem had reached the eighteenth century imperfectly served by scholarship: a "third folio of the 'Faërie Queene'" appeared "in 1679, but no critical ed[ition]" for long after (003-027).

## Summary / Content

*The Faërie Queene* is a vast allegorical romance in Spenser's own nine-line stanza. Beers's contemporaries recognized both its power and its difficulty: one critic "says that the 'Faërie Queene' has no uniformity: the language is not so obsolete as is commonly supposed, and is intelligible after some practice; but the choice of stanza is unfortunate," and yet "Spenser's verse is more melodious than any other English poet's except Mr. Waller's" (003-023). Its allegory and archaic diction were the very features the revival came to prize (003-028).

## Form & Style

The poem's medium is the Spenserian stanza, whose "amplitude" Beers contrasts with "the tinkle of the couplet, with its monotonously recurring rhyme, its inevitable caesura, its narrow imprisonment of the sense" (003-017). To a "generation surfeited" with that couplet, Spenser's "great verse" came as a relief (003-017). The critics compared its structure to the medieval: the poem was likened to "an ancient, majestic piece of Gothic architecture" against "a neat, modern" building (003-012).

## Significance to Romanticism

*The Faërie Queene* is the fountainhead of the whole Spenserian strain of the revival. As "the standard of the new school" (003-017), it authorized the return to allegory, archaism, and the ample stanza that the imitators — Thomson, Shenstone, and the rest — pursued. Its status as the poem "whose art is the antithesis of Pope's" (003-017) made it the natural rallying-point for every poet weary of Augustan correctness. See [[spenserianism]] and [[the-spenserians]].

## Reception & Controversy

The eighteenth century's estimate of the poem was itself the battleground. To rank "Spenser as ... as great a man as Pope" was a "paradoxical thesis" (002-013); critics disputed the stanza's wisdom and the diction's obscurity (003-023) even as they conceded Spenser's supreme melody. The revaluation of *The Faërie Queene* — from a difficult antique to "the standard of the new school" (003-017) — is Beers's measure of how far the romantic tide had turned.
