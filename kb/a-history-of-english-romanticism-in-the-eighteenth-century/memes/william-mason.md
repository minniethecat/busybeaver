## Overview

William Mason (1724–1797) was a poet, dramatist, and the friend and editor of [[thomas-gray|Gray]], whom Beers dismisses as "a small poet and a somewhat absurd person." (004-042)(005-008)

He belonged, with Gray and [[horace-walpole|Walpole]], to the innermost circle of the mid-century revival, and his "The English Garden," "Caractacus," and Runic experiments touch several of its characteristic enthusiasms.

## Life

Beers records few personal details. Mason was a beneficed clergyman who, like [[john-dyer|Dyer]] and [[joseph-warton|Joseph Warton]], "took unto themselves wives"—the melancholy "these good fellows affected" being, Beers judges, "manifestly a mere literary fashion." (005-045)

He moved in the closest literary company of his day: "Gray, Walpole, and Mason constitute a group, encouraging each other's studies in their correspondence and occasional meetings." (011-090)

His statements about his own work, Beers warns, "are not always to be relied upon." (005-017)

## Works & Contribution

Mason's longest poem, "The English Garden" (1757), Beers treats as a specimen of "that brood of didactic blank-poems, begotten of Phillips' 'Cyder' and Thomson's 'Seasons.'" (004-047)

Its blank verse, "closely imitative of Thomson's," shows "the influence of Thomson's inflated diction... at its worst." (004-047)

In emulation of "[[the-seasons|The Seasons]]" he wove "a sentimental love story—Alcander and Nerina—into his third book," recommending gardeners to trace the natural curves that "the scudding hare / Draws to her dew-sprent seat o'er thymy heaths." (004-052)

Earlier he had aped Milton. His "Musaeus," "Il Bellicoso," and "Il Pacifico" (written 1744) "follow copy in every particular." (005-017)

Beers ranks him among "a new group of lyrical poets: Collins, Gray, Mason, and the brothers Joseph and Thomas Warton," for whom "Milton was master." (005-007)

But his originality was slight: "He aped, first Milton and afterward Gray, so closely that his work often seems like parody." (005-008)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Mason's contribution lay in the revival's antiquarian and Celtic-Norse enthusiasms. His "Caractacus" (1759) was "a dramatic poem on the Greek model, with a chorus of British bards, and a principal Druid for choragus," its scene "the sacred grove in Mona." (006-016)

The scholarship was shaky. While "Caractacus" was still in the works, Gray "takes him to task for mixing the Gothic and Celtic mythologies," instructing him that Woden's Valhalla "belong[s] to the doctrine of the Scalds, not of the Bards." (006-018)

He also tried his hand at [[northern-antiquities|Norse matter]]: his "Song of Harold the Valiant" was "a rather insipid versification" of a saga passage, "designed... for insertion in the introduction to Gray's abortive history of English poetry." (006-019)

## Reception & Influence

Mason's surest place in the record is as an arbiter of authenticity. When Walpole received [[chatterton|Chatterton's]] "Rowley" manuscripts, he showed them to "his friends Gray and Mason, who promptly pronounced them modern fabrications and recommended him to return them without further notice." (010-026)

For all Beers's scorn of his verse, Mason belonged to the small, self-aware nucleus of the movement, whose "interchange of views" and mutual encouragement gave the scattered revival what little "solidarity" it possessed. (011-090)
