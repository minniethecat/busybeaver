# The Rowley Poems

## Overview

*The Rowley Poems* are the pseudo-antique verses that the boy [[thomas-chatterton]] composed and attributed to a fictitious fifteenth-century [[bristol]] monk, Thomas Rowley.

They stand with *Ossian* as the century's supreme example of [[literary-forgery]] passing over into literature, bearing directly on Beers's account of [[medievalism]] and the [[gothicism|Gothic revival]] within English [[romanticism]].

Beers makes them central to his whole argument, "for, aside from its dramatic interest, and leaving out of question the absolute value of the Rowley poems," Chatterton's story "is most instructive as to the conditions which brought about the romantic revival. It shows by what process antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001).

## Composition & Publication

Their author was Thomas Chatterton, "born in 1752, the posthumous son of a dissipated schoolmaster, whose ancestors for a hundred and fifty years had been, in unbroken succession, sextons to the church of St. Mary Redcliffe" (010-002).

Beers half-seriously traces the Rowley invention to that inheritance — "the bent which Chatterton's genius took spontaneously and almost from infancy" (010-002). The poems belong to the years around 1768–70, when "many of the Rowley poems were in print" (006-059).

The imposture grew out of a wholly self-directed education. A "dreamy, silent, solitary child," Chatterton would not learn his letters until "his eye was caught by the illuminated capitals in an old music folio," after which "he learned to read from a black-letter Bible" (010-004).

Brooding on the antiquities of his native city, "he gradually constructed for himself a picture of fifteenth-century Bristol, including a group of figures, partly historical and partly fabulous, all centering about Master William Canynge," the merchant who "founded or restored [[st-mary-redcliffe|St. Mary Redcliffe's]]" (010-008).

According to a schoolfellow, Thistlethwaite, Chatterton claimed as early as the summer of 1764 to possess "old manuscripts, found in a chest in Redcliffe Church" (010-015).

In 1768–69 he produced for George Catcott "the long tragical interlude 'Aella,' 'The Bristowe Tragedie,' and other shorter pieces, all of which he declared to be transcripts from manuscripts in Canynge's chest, and the work of Thomas Rowley, a secular priest of Bristol, who flourished about 1460" (010-019).

In December 1768 he opened a correspondence with the London publisher James Dodsley, enclosing "a specimen of 'Aella'" (010-020). His stated motive frames the whole enterprise as a defence of the medieval past:

> The motive that actuates me to do this . . . is to convince the world that the monks (of whom some have so despicable an opinion) were not such blockheads as generally thought, and that good poetry might be wrote in the dark days of superstition, as well as in these more enlightened ages. (010-020)

Only "the eclogue of 'Elinoure and Juga'" appeared in his lifetime (010-028). In April 1770 "he left Bristol for London, and cast himself upon the hazardous fortunes of a literary career," and "on August 24 he took poison" (010-028).

Not until "1777, seven years after Chatterton's death, [were] his Rowley poems . . . first collected and published by Thomas Tyrwhitt, the Chaucerian editor, who gave, in an appendix, his reasons for believing that Chatterton was their real author, and Rowley a myth" (010-035).

## Content & Form

The poems purport to be the work of "Thomas Rowley, parish priest" of fifteenth-century Bristol (010-008), and include such pieces as "'The Bristowe Tragedie'" (010-015). They "were born of St. Mary's Church" — of Chatterton's brooding on the antiquities of Redcliffe (010-002) — and were passed off with the aid of forged vellum documents.

Beers treats them as the poetic distillation of local antiquarian fascination into original verse (010-001).

The Rowley canon is varied. Beers notes that it includes "a number of dramatic or quasi-dramatic pieces, 'Goddwyn,' 'The Tournament,' 'The Parliament of Sprites'; the narrative poem of 'The Battle of Hastings,' and a collection of 'eclogues,'" mostly "in the ten-lined stanza," with "'English Metamorphosis' . . . an imitation of a passage in 'The Faërie Queene'" (010-052).

"The longest of the Rowley poems is 'Aella,' 'a tragycal enterlude or discoorseynge tragedie' in 147 stanzas" (010-048).

"Perhaps the most engaging of the Rowley poems are 'An Excelente Balade of Charitie,' written in the rhyme royal; and 'The Bristowe Tragedie,' in the common ballad stanza, and said by Tyrwhitt to be founded on an historical fact: the excecution at Bristol, in 1461, of Sir Baldwin Fulford" (010-056).

The Rowley poems affect a fifteenth-century English — archaic spelling and diction meant to pass for medieval. Their exposure turned on precisely this counterfeit: Skeat "cannot find the slightest indication that Chatterton had ever seen a MS. of early date," the boy being "singularly addicted to the use of capitals, which in old MSS. are rather scarce" (010-033).

The forged manuscripts were "smeared with glue or brown varnish, or stained with ochre, to give them an appearance of age" (010-033).

## Significance to Romanticism

*The Rowley Poems* are, with Ossian, the great instance of the age's characteristic mode: the forgery that becomes literature. Through them "antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001).

Chatterton's tragic story stands at the very head of Beers's history: "The history of English romanticism has its tragedy: the life and death of Thomas Chatterton, 'The marvelous boy'" (010-001). They gave the medieval revival original poetry under counterfeit warrant. See [[literary-forgery]] and [[medievalism]].

## Reception & Controversy

The Rowley poems provoked one of the century's great authenticity disputes. Horace Walpole, "lately bitten in the Ossian business,"

"had begun to suspect imposture" (010-025); "Thomas Warton had seen one of them, and pronounced it a clumsy forgery; the script not of the fifteenth century, but unmistakably modern" (010-033), and devoted part of his *History of English Poetry* to reviewing them (010-039).

Tyrwhitt's verdict that Chatterton was the true author was "shared at the time by all competent authorities — Gray, Thomas Warton, and Malone, the editor of the *variorum* Shakspere, among others" (010-035).

Beers thought the poems' partisans overrated them, recording that "Malone . . . pronounced Chatterton the greatest genius that England had produced since Shakspere," while endorsing Gosse's cooler estimate that "our estimate of the complete originality of the Rowley poems must be tempered by a recollection" of their derivative art (010-046). His own critical measure is severe:

> tried by an absolute standard, they are markedly inferior not only to true mediaeval work like Chaucer's poems and the English and Scottish ballads, but also to the best modern work conceived in the same spirit: to "Christabel" and "The Eve of St. Agnes." (010-048)

Yet Beers's final verdict cuts past the fraud to the poetry: their real interest lies in showing "by what process antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001).
