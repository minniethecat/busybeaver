# The Ballad Revival

## Definition

The Ballad Revival is Beers's name for the 18th-century recovery of old English and Scottish popular ballads. He treats it as the decisive turn in the period: the regeneration of English poetic style at the close of the last century "came from an unexpected quarter," and what the imitators of Spenser and Milton had failed to achieve was "much more effectually done by Percy and the ballad collectors"—a recall of British poetry to imagination and to older and better models than Dryden and Pope (008-001). The material was what the Germans call *Volkspoesie*, or popular poetry, as opposed to literary *Kunstpoesie* (008-003)—a body of narrative ballads handed down chiefly by oral transmission, still living "in the memories and upon the lips of the common people" (008-004). Beers is careful to restrict the term to the genuine folk-song, the traditional minstrel ballad of the Middle Ages, and to exclude the street ballads that were made *for* the people rather than *by* them and had "no literary character whatever" (008-020, 008-021).

## Origins & Development

In their origin these songs were sung to the harp by wandering minstrels, and in later times chanted by ballad-singers at fairs and ale-houses, surviving in the tenacious memory of the people (008-006). A few favorites such as "Chevy Chase" and "Johnnie Armstrong" had long been familiar, yet were "hardly regarded as literature," being looked on as nursery tales, though antiquaries like Pepys and Selden had quietly collected them (008-041). Addison praised the naturalness and simplicity of the popular ballads in the *Spectator* (008-044)—old ballads that Johnson, by contrast, "thought mean and foolish" (002-038). Professor Child counts nine ballad collections before Percy, the chief being the volumes ascribed to Ambrose Philips and Allan Ramsay's "Tea Table Miscellany" (008-042). Ballad forgery, too, "had begun early," with Lady Wardlaw's "Hardyknut" (1719); yet before Percy "no concerted or intelligent effort" had been made toward collecting and editing the corpus of English minstrelsy (008-050). The decisive decade was 1760 to 1770, crowned by Percy's "Reliques" in 1765 (008-003).

## Key Figures & Works

Thomas Percy, a parish clergyman at Easton Maudit, had long amused his leisure by collecting ballads; the plan of the "Reliques" was suggested by Shenstone (008-051). Its nucleus was a folio manuscript that Percy found "lying dirty on the floor under a bureau," used by the maids to light the fire (008-060). Percy freely filled gaps with stanzas—and sometimes whole poems—of his own composition (008-061), for which Joseph Ritson furiously assailed him, even denying the folio's existence in what was "an age of forgeries" (008-062). Walter Scott gathered his "Minstrelsy of the Border" from the oral recitation of pipers, shepherds, and old women (008-007), and James Beattie's popular poem "The Minstrel" took its title and theme from Percy's "Essay on the Ancient Minstrels" (008-087).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers calls the "Reliques" "an epoch-making book" (008-080). Beyond the ballads it rescued, it "prompted others to hunt out and publish similar relics"—Herd's collection (1769), Scott's (1802-03), Motherwell's (1827)—and inspired original genius: Wordsworth said English poetry had been "absolutely redeemed" by it, and without it "The Ancient Mariner," "The Lady of the Lake," and "La Belle Dame sans Merci" might never have been (008-082). Scott recalled reading the volumes as a boy until he "forgot the hour of dinner," entranced in his "intellectual banquet" (008-083). The book worked powerfully in Germany too, feeding Herder's "Stimmen der Völker" (008-084). Its force lay in contrast: the old ballads "were everything which the eighteenth century was not," rough and wild where that age was smooth and tame (008-063). Even continental romanticism linked the "mania for ballads" with the impulse "to dig up the Middle Ages" (001-038).

## Contrasts & Debates

The revival opened a lasting quarrel over editorial method: Percy dressed his ballads "with modern sauces" and interpolated his own verse, while Ritson, "a thoroughly critical, conscientious student," demanded a literal transcript of the text as found (008-058, 008-061). Beers defends Percy on historical grounds—his readers "did not want torsos and scraps" (008-058)—while insisting on the distinction between the genuine traditional folk-song and the worthless broadside street ballad (008-020). Both disputes unfolded against the period's anxiety over authenticity, "an age of forgeries" in which Percy's own imitations could be suspected alongside Chatterton and MacPherson (008-062).
