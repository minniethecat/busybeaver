# The Sublime

## Definition

The sublime is, for the reformers Beers follows, one of the two great sources of poetic power. Joseph Warton makes it a first principle: "The sublime and the pathetic are the two chief nerves of all genuine poesy" — asking pointedly, "What is there transcendently sublime or pathetic in Pope?" (006-063). Beers takes the term in the sense the century gave it after Addison: naming "greatness and novelty, _i.e._, the sublime and the wonderful, as, equally with beauty, the chief sources of imaginative pleasure" (004-080). It is thus the aesthetic of grandeur and awe set beside — and often above — the merely beautiful.

## Origins & Development

The sublime enters Beers's history as the critical lever by which the Warton school pried poetry loose from Augustan correctness. Warton's charge against Pope is precisely a deficiency of sublimity: Pope "early left the more poetical provinces of his art, to become a moral, didactic, and satiric poet," and Warton borrows Voltaire's verdict — that he was "'Incapable peut-être du sublime qui élève l'áme, et du sentiment qui l'attendrit'" — the poet of Reason, not of the soul (006-064). Against this, "A clear head and acute understanding are not sufficient alone to make a poet"; what is wanted is "a creative and glowing imagination" (006-064). The classical age, by contrast, had degraded the term — rising "up to a climax of the false sublime" (002-050) — and Beers notes that "Satire, that bane of the sublime, was imported from France" (003-063).

## Key Figures & Works

The taste for the sublime turns the poets outward to wild nature. Gray, visiting the Scotch Highlands in 1765, gives it its most famous expression: "Since I saw the Alps, I have seen nothing sublime till now. ... the mountains are ecstatic ... None but those monstrous creatures of God know how to join so much beauty with so much horror" (005-063). Akenside, "Following Addison," built his "Pleasures of Imagination" around "greatness and novelty ... the sublime and the wonderful," though Beers finds the execution "obscure, metaphysical, and, as a consequence, frigid" (004-080). The Wartons themselves, Thomas and Joseph, are for Beers the theorists who put "the sublime and the pathetic" at the center of criticism against the reigning "poet of Reason" (006-063, 006-064).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

The sublime is one of the aesthetic engines of the romantic revival: by exalting "greatness," "novelty," and "the wonderful" over correctness and good sense, it licensed exactly the wildness, grandeur, and solitude that Augustan taste had proscribed (004-080, 006-063). Gray's ecstasy before mountains that "join so much beauty with so much horror" (005-063) is the sensibility that will carry through to the romantic gloom "in presence of the mountains and the sea." Warton's demand for "a creative and glowing imagination" (006-064) is, in effect, a manifesto for the imaginative poetry the revival sought to restore. ## Contrasts & Debates

The central debate the sublime frames is Warton's arraignment of Pope, and behind him the whole classical school: whether wit and "a clear head" can make a poet, or whether "the sublime and the pathetic" are indispensable (006-063, 006-064). Beers is alert, too, to the counterfeit article — the classical rhetoric that mounts "to a climax of the false sublime, as an orator" straining for effect (002-050) — and to the way satire, "that bane of the sublime," worked against it (003-063). The quarrel is thus not only sublime-versus-beautiful but genuine sublimity versus its Augustan imitation.
