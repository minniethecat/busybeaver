## Overview

The Scottish Highlands are, for Beers, one of the great imaginative territories of the revival: the country of [[ossian]], the setting of Collins's Highland ode, and the landscape whose "beauty" and "horror" awakened the taste for [[the-sublime]]. Beers lists "Collins' 'Ode on the Superstitions of the Scottish Highlands'" among the contributions to romantic literature that reach back to a wild, pre-medieval past (002-002).

## Description & Location

When [[thomas-gray]] "visited the Scotch Highlands" in 1765 he sent back rapturous letters: "Since I saw the Alps, I have seen nothing sublime till now.... the mountains are ecstatic, and ought to be visited in pilgrimage once a year. None but those monstrous creatures of God know how to join so much beauty with so much horror" (005-063). The same north-eastern coast furnishes the scene of Ann Radcliffe's "Castles of Athlin and Dunbayne," set "in the most romantic part of the Highlands," where a castle stands "on the summit of a rock whose base was in the sea" — "a fine place for storms" (007-066).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

The Highlands supplied the revival with a native store of "false themes." [[william-collins]]'s "Ode on the Popular Superstitions of the Highlands of Scotland," written in 1749 and inscribed to John Home, set out "to recommend to him the Scottish fairy lore as a fit subject for poetry," justifying the choice by the example of Spenser and Shakespeare (005-047). Above all the region is the ground of [[james-macpherson]]'s Ossian: the misty glens and storm-beaten coasts of the north gave the epic [[fingal]] its scenery and its melancholy. Beers treats this Celtic material as a strand of romanticism distinct from, yet allied to, the Gothic and medieval revivals (002-002).
