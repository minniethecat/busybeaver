# Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard

## Overview

The *Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard* is Thomas Gray's meditative poem on death and obscurity, the crowning work of the mid-century elegiac and "graveyard" mood. Beers judges it categorically: "it was the most admired and influential poem of the generation" (005-057), and "Gray's popular fame rests, and will always rest, upon his immortal 'Elegy'" (005-053).

## Author & Date

"The 'Elegy' was published in 1751" (005-053), a few years after Gray's "simpler odes, the 'Ode on the Spring,' the 'Hymn to Adversity' and the Eton College ode ... printed in Dodsley's collection in 1748" (005-053). Its author himself was ambivalent about its fame: "He himself denied somewhat impatiently that it was his best poem, and thought that its popularity was owing to its subject" (005-053).

## Summary / Content

The poem replaces abstract moralizing with a scene: "Instead of general reflections upon the shortness of life, the vanity of ambition, the leveling power of death, and similar commonplaces, we have the picture of the solitary poet, lingering among the graves at twilight ... till the place and the hour conspire to work their effect upon the mind and prepare it for the strain of meditation that follows" (005-057). It is this dramatized, particular setting — the churchyard at dusk — that gives the poem its distinctive power.

## Form & Style

The *Elegy* belongs to the elegiac-quatrain manner that the age set beside its Pindaric odes and Spenserian imitations (005-032). Its style achieves what Beers calls "the perfection of its style," which "with the universal appeal of its subject" has "made the 'Elegy' known by heart to more readers than any other poem in the language" (005-057).

## Significance to Romanticism

The poem is the summit of the poetry of melancholy that Beers traces from "Il Penseroso" through the mid-century (005-033). Its example was formative: the "young poet to try his hand at a churchyard piece" followed where Gray led (005-057). As "the most admired and influential poem of the generation" (005-057), it carried the elegiac, twilight, death-haunted sensibility — one of the sentimental tributaries of the revival — to its widest audience. See [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]] and [[romanticism]].

## Reception & Controversy

The *Elegy*'s fame was such that "Parody is one proof of celebrity," and "the 'sister odes' were presently parodied" (005-057); but the *Elegy* "was more than celebrated and more than popular" (005-057). Beers records the divided critical estimate — that "There are not wanting critics of authority, such as Lowell and Matthew Arnold," who weighed Gray's rank — while affirming that his "popular fame ... will always rest, upon his immortal 'Elegy'" (005-053).
