# Primitivism

## Definition

Primitivism, in Beers's account, is the romantic age's growing attraction to the rude, the simple, and the spontaneous, prized as more genuine than the polish of civilization. He locates it precisely as "that attraction toward the savage, the awful, the mysterious, the primitive, which marks the romantic mood in [[naturalism|naturalistic]] poetry" (004-022).

Its aesthetic premise is that the earliest verse is the truest. "The song," Beers writes, "is the most primitive and spontaneous kind of poetry, and the most direct utterance of personal feeling" (002-051). The whole tendency is a turning-back toward "the primitive and popular character" of poetry, what Herder and Goethe termed its _Ursprünglichkeit_ and _Volksthümlichkeit_ (011-022).

## Origins & Development

Beers presents primitivism as a reaction against an over-refined age. The [[classicism|classical]] period, for all its brilliance, "could not sing," being poor in exactly the lyric that is poetry's most primitive form (002-051). The seventeenth century, he adds, was likewise unfitted "to feel and understand the spirit of primitive antiquity," so that to appreciate Homer "it was thought necessary to civilize the barbarian" (002-062).

The mood grew as the century tired of its own manners. By the era of Ossian "the age was tired of polish, of wit, of over-civilization; it was groping toward the rude, the primitive, the heroic" (009-040). Late in the book Beers still reads the vogue of German fiction as satisfying "the demand for something wild and primitive, for the return into poetry" of what refinement had banished (011-049).

## Key Figures & Works

The most powerful vehicle of primitivism was the recovered folk-song. Beers defines the traditional ballad as the "product of the people... in rude and primitive neighborhoods, like the Scottish border," where mediaeval conditions survived (008-021). Its "naïve, primitive quality cannot be acquired: the secret is lost," he insists, a quality no lettered poet could counterfeit (008-029).

[[james-macpherson|MacPherson]]'s [[ossian|"Ossian"]] answered this appetite directly, its "wild paraphrases" received with "extraordinary enthusiasm" by an age craving "a tale of the times of old" (009-040). [[thomas-percy|Percy]]'s ballad collections and the new reading of Homer as "essentially a minstrel or ballad-maker" (011-022) belong to the same current.

## Role in the Romantic Revival

For Beers primitivism is one of the deep undercurrents feeding the Romantic revival: the reawakened relish for "the savage, the awful, the mysterious, the primitive" that stirs beneath naturalistic poetry (004-022). It reframed literary value, teaching the age to prize the spontaneous folk quality it could no longer produce (008-029).

Even the doubts about Ossian's authenticity turned on this ideal. Beers refuses to concede "that the songs of primitive, heroic ages are always insensible to the sublime in nature," defending the primitive against the charge of crudeness (009-027). The appetite for the primitive thus underwrote the whole return to ballad, epic, and the poetry of "the times of old."

## Contrasts & Debates

Primitivism stands as the direct antithesis of [[classicism|classical]] polish. Where the Augustan ideal civilized and corrected, primitivism "was tired of polish, of wit, of over-civilization" and sought the unlettered instead (009-040). It overlaps with, but is broader than, [[the-ballad-revival|the ballad revival]] and [[naturalism|naturalism]]: the ballad revival is its concrete literary programme, while primitivism names the underlying valuation of the rude and spontaneous over the refined.
