## Overview

Edinburgh is, in Beers's account, the intellectual capital of the Scottish revival — the city of the literati where the Ossianic poems were launched and debated. It was here that Dr. Hugh Blair, "Professor of Rhetoric in the University of Edinburgh," took up [[james-macpherson]]'s translations and set in motion the whole [[ossian|Ossian]] enterprise (009-006).

From this one learned circle radiated the two Celtic currents that most stirred the age — the epic of [[fingal|Fingal]] carried down from [[the-highlands|the Highlands]], and the old ballads of the Lowlands — while later the same city became a gateway for German romance.

It supplied both the sensation and the scholarship: the Advocates' Library preserved the manuscripts, the university furnished the sponsors, and men such as [[thomas-gray|Gray]] and David Hume followed its productions with keen attention.

## Setting & history

The city functioned as a clearing-house for the new antiquarian and balladic interests. When John Home first obtained Macpherson's Gaelic fragments, "he sent or showed copies to Dr. Hugh Blair" at the university (009-006); the resulting "Fragments," issued "with a preface of eight pages by Blair,"

"attracted so much attention in Edinburgh that a subscription was started, to send the compiler through the Highlands in search of more Gaelic poetry" (009-007). Edinburgh was also a theatrical and scholarly centre: Home's tragedy "Douglas" was "first played at Edinburgh in 1756," and Gray prized the old Scotch ballad on which it was founded (008-028).

As a scholarly capital, Edinburgh was also the great repository of the manuscripts that the controversy turned upon. Allan Ramsay's ballads were drawn in part from George Bannatyne's collection "of some eight hundred pages, made... about 1570 and still preserved in the Advocates' Library at Edinburgh" (008-043).

The same library held the Gaelic evidence that ultimately answered Dr. Johnson's charge that no old Gaelic manuscripts existed:

> As to Gaelic manuscripts, there are over sixty in the Advocates' Library at Edinburgh, varying in age from three hundred to five hundred years. (009-016)

## Associations (persons & works)

The city drew, or produced, many of the figures central to Beers's northern chapters. Its university offered James [[james-beattie|Beattie]] "the chair of Moral Philosophy," though he stuck faithfully to Aberdeen (008-088).

Its most famous philosopher, David Hume, "who afterward became skeptical as to their authenticity," first "wrote to Gray, assuring him that these poems were in everybody's mouth in the Highlands, and had been handed down from father to son, from an age beyond all memory and tradition" (009-003).

And it was the setting of Walter Scott's apprenticeship: "In 1792 Walter Scott was a young lawyer at Edinburgh and had just attained his majority" (011-023).

## Cultural significance

Edinburgh gave the revival both its great Celtic sensation and its fiercest test of authenticity.

The [[ossian|Ossian]] controversy — turning on "the authenticity of the Ossianic poems" and "also on their literary value" — began in the city's learned circles the moment the poems appeared, with Blair and Home as sponsors and Macpherson as the traveller they financed through [[the-highlands|the Highlands]] (009-006, 009-007).

At the same time the Scottish capital and its Lowland hinterland were the heart of "the ballad land" whose songs fed [[the-ballad-revival|the ballad revival]] (008-005). The city thus stands at the junction of the two most powerful northern currents — Ossianic and balladic — that flowed into English [[romanticism]].

Beyond the Celtic sensation, Edinburgh later became a channel for [[the-german-tributary|the German tributary]]. In the winter of 1792 "a class of six or seven young men was formed at Edinburgh for the study of German, and Scott joined it," an interest that Scott traced to a paper "read before the Royal Society of Edinburgh in April, 1788, by Henry Mackenzie" (011-024).

Beers quotes Scott's recollection of that awakening:

> The literary persons of Edinburgh were then first made aware of the existence of works of genius in a language cognate with the English, and possessed of the same manly force of expressions. (011-024)
