# The Sonnet Revival

## Definition

The sonnet revival is Beers's name for the mid-century recovery of a verse form that Augustan taste had let die. Under the reign of Pope's [[the-heroic-couplet|heroic couplet]], he writes, "the sonnet, for instance, disappeared entirely." (002-042)

Its return he dates to "about the middle of the eighteenth century," when the form was "revived by [[thomas-gray|Gray]], Stillingfleet, Edwards, and [[thomas-warton|Thomas Warton]]." (002-042)

Like the recovered [[the-ode|ode]] and the revival of [[blank-verse|blank verse]], the sonnet's reappearance marks a widening of metrical range against the couplet's century-long monopoly. (002-042)

## Origins & Development

Beers ties the revival directly to the cult of Milton's minor poems: "To the increasing popularity of Milton's minor poetry is due the revival of the sonnet." (005-032)

Its founding instance was private and posthumous — "Gray's solitary sonnet, on the death of his friend Richard West, was composed in 1742 but not printed till 1775, after the author's death." (005-032)

That single poem had a long afterlife: Wordsworth "selected [it] to illustrate his strictures on the spurious [[poetic-diction|poetic diction]] of the eighteenth century" in the preface to "Lyrical Ballads." (005-032)

Alongside Gray, Beers records Thomas Edwards, "himself a Spenser lover, whose 'sugared sonnets among his private friends' begin about 1750 and reach the number of fifty." (003-073)

## Key Figures & Works

[[thomas-warton|Thomas Warton]] made the sonnet a vehicle for his antiquarian enthusiasms, composing sonnets "Written at Stonehenge," "To Mr. Gray," and "On King Arthur's Round Table." (006-035)

His best-known, "Written in a Blank Leaf of Dugdale's Monasticon" — "a favorite with Charles Lamb" — closes on lines that "might have been written by Longfellow": "Nor rough nor barren are the winding ways / Of hoar Antiquity, but strewn with flowers." (006-026)

Warton also addressed a sonnet to Richard Hurd, the friend of Gray and Mason whose "Letters on Chivalry and Romance" "helped to initiate the romantic movement." (007-001)

The subjects of these poems — Stonehenge, Arthur's Round Table, an old monastic folio — show how far the revived sonnet had travelled from Augustan wit toward antiquity and reverie. (006-026)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

The sonnet revival belongs, for Beers, to the larger Miltonic and medievalising current of the mid-century — the same taste that produced the ode and the antiquarian poem. (005-032)

Its practitioners were the men of the new school: [[the-poetry-of-melancholy|melancholy]] and antiquity, not Augustan satire, are its matter, as in Warton's sonnets on Stonehenge and "hoar Antiquity." (006-035)

That the recovered form flowed on to Bowles and Wordsworth makes it, in Beers's telling, one of the quieter tributaries of the [[romanticism|romantic]] renewal of English verse. (005-032)

## Contrasts & Debates

The revived sonnet drew the fire of the classical camp. Johnson lumped Warton's antiquarian pieces — "Ode and elegy and sonnet" — into a famous parody of the whole medievalising manner. (006-049)

The form itself had been a casualty of the couplet's dominance, which "so prevailed in our poetry for a century or more that one almost loses sight of the fact that any other form was employed." (002-042)

Its recovery thus enacted in miniature the age's larger quarrel between the [[classicism|classical]] and the [[romanticism|romantic]] — the correct couplet against the older, freer forms of Milton and the Elizabethans. (002-042)
