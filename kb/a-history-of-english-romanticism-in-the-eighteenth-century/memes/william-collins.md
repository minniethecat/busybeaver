# William Collins

## Overview

William Collins is, for Beers, the finest of the mid-century lyric poets — "Collins is among the choicest of English lyrical poets" (005-046). His odes carry "a flute-like music" that Beers ranks above Gray's, and his work marks, "romantically speaking," an "advance upon that of the descriptive and elegiac poets" (005-046).

## Life

Beers keeps to Collins's poetry rather than his life. He places him among "these Miltonic lyrists" of the mid-century (005-046), the generation of Gray and the Wartons. His "Ode on the Superstitions of the Scottish Highlands" is named among the "Contributions to romantic literature" of the age (002-002), and his "Ode on the Death of Mr. Thomson" and "Dirge in Cymbeline" among "his most heartfelt lyrics" (005-038).

## Works & Contribution

Collins's contribution is his odes. Beers singles out "the one 'To Evening,' and the one written in 1746 — 'How sleep the brave,' which are sweeter, more natural, and more spontaneous than Gray's" (005-046). Swinburne's epigram serves as Beers's estimate of his gift: "'The Muse gave birth to Collins,' says Swinburne; 'she did but give suck to Gray'" (005-046). Collins also "endeavored to give a novel tu[rn]" to poetry in the romantic direction (002-026), and had "'more hearse-like airs than carols,'" a melancholy strain running through his lyrics (005-038).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Collins advances the revival on its lyric and imaginative side. Beers presents "the work of these Miltonic lyrists" as a "romantic" advance beyond mere description and elegy (005-046), and Collins is their choicest voice — "a solitary song-bird among many more or less excellent pipers and pianists" (005-046). His "Ode on the Superstitions of the Scottish Highlands" reaches toward the primitive and supernatural matter the revival prized (002-002). See [[the-ode]] and [[romanticism]].

## Reception & Influence

Beers's judgment of Collins is unusually warm — his odes "sweeter, more natural, and more spontaneous than Gray's" (005-046), and Swinburne's contrast of the two poets (005-046) becomes his summary. Yet Beers also notes the melancholy that pervades the lyrics (005-038), and Collins's place among the poets who gave the age its "novel" romantic turn (002-026).
