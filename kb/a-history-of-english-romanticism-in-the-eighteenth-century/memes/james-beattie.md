# James Beattie

## Overview

James Beattie is, for Beers, a late and composite figure of the revival — author of *The Minstrel*, "a poem once widely popular, in which several strands of romantic influence are seen twisted together" (008-087), and of a critical *Dissertation on Fable and Romance*. He belongs among the "mild clerical essayists" who theorized romance in their leisure (007-049).

## Life

Beers treats Beattie through his two works. He was "the author of 'The Minstrel'" and of "'Dr. Beattie's Dissertation on Fable and Romance'" (007-048), grouped with Hurd among the "mild clerical essayists" of the antiquarian revival (007-049). *The Minstrel*'s two books "published in 1771" and "1774" mark his moment; "the work was never completed" (008-087).

## Works & Contribution

Beattie's contribution is *The Minstrel, or the Progress of Genius* and the critical *Dissertation*. Of the poem, "The design of 'The Minstrel' is to 'trace the progress of a Poetical Genius, born in a rude age'" (008-089), though its Gothic frame is more atmosphere than substance (008-089). Beers finds it derivative — it "abounds in insipid morality, the commonplaces of denunciation against luxury and ambition" (008-096). His *Dissertation* "follows pretty closely the lines of Hurd's 'Letters on Chivalry and Romance'" (007-048). See [[the-minstrel]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beattie's importance is exactly his composite character. In *The Minstrel*, Beers writes, "several strands of romantic influence are seen twisted together": "It was in the Spenserian stanza, was tinged with the enthusiastic melancholy of the Wartons, followed the landscape manner of Thomson, had elegiac echoes of Gray, and was perhaps not unaffected, in its love of mountain scenery, by MacPherson" (008-087). He thus gathers the Spenserian, melancholy, landscape, elegiac, and Ossianic currents into one late poem. See [[spenserianism]] and [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]].

## Reception & Influence

*The Minstrel* was "once widely popular" (008-087), and Beattie's *Dissertation* was read alongside Hurd and Warton by later writers on romance such as Clara Reeve (007-048). Beers's critical estimate is cool — the poem "abounds in insipid morality, the commonplaces of denunciation against luxury and ambition" (008-096) — but its value as the poem "in which several strands of romantic influence are seen twisted together" is, for the historian, considerable (008-087).
