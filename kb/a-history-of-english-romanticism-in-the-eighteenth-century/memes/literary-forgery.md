# Literary Forgery

## Definition

Literary forgery, in Beers's history, is the fabrication of "antique" poetry passed off as genuine remains — the age's characteristic mode of counterfeit antiquity. Chatterton's era was, in Beers's phrase, "the day of literary forgery; the Ossian controversy was raging" (010-015). The practice was not new — "Ballad forgery had begun early" (008-050) — but in Macpherson's Ossian and Chatterton's Rowley it produced texts of extraordinary consequence. Beers frames the Chatterton story as "most instructive as to the conditions which brought about the romantic revival": "It shows by what process antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001).

## Origins & Development

The two great impostures fed on the antiquarian appetite of the mid-century. James Macpherson published his Ossianic "translations" from the Gaelic, and "The most damaging feature of MacPherson's case was his refusal or neglect to produce his originals," though testimony left "little doubt that he had materials of some kind" — "old Gaelic manuscripts" and "transcriptions taken down in Gaelic from the recitation of aged persons in the Highlands" — pieced, lopped, and "manipulated" to an uncertain degree (009-028). Chatterton, in Bristol, invented the fifteenth-century monk-poet Thomas Rowley, tracing verses "in pale and yellow writing, as if faded with age," on vellum "found in a chest in Redcliffe Church" (010-015). The Rowley "scraps of vellum" were "smeared with glue or brown varnish, or stained with ochre, to give them an appearance of age" (010-033).

## Key Figures & Works

Macpherson's Ossian and Chatterton's Rowley are the twin monuments. Ossian drew doubters early — Beers records that skeptics "became skeptical as to their authenticity" and pressed Macpherson to produce his sources (009-003, 009-028). Chatterton's fabrications were exposed by the antiquaries: Horace Walpole, "lately bitten in the Ossian business," "had begun to suspect imposture" (010-025), and "Thomas Warton had seen one of them, and pronounced it a clumsy forgery; the script not of the fifteenth century, but unmistakably modern" (010-033). Skeat later found "not the slightest indication that Chatterton had ever seen a MS. of early date," the boy being "singularly addicted to the use of capitals, which in old MSS. are rather scarce" (010-033). Even Percy's balladry belongs to the story's fringe, for "Ballad forgery had begun early" (008-050).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers's paradox is that the forgeries, whatever their fraud, were engines of the revival: through Chatterton "antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001), and his tragedy is set at the head of the movement — "The history of English romanticism has its tragedy: the life and death of Thomas Chatterton — 'The marvelous boy'" (010-001). Ossian and Rowley gave the age precisely the misty, ancient, Northern imaginative matter that Augustan poetry lacked, and Beers ranks Macpherson's "Ossian" among the works that gave "an impulse to romantic literature" (002-002). The forgeries thus did the revival's work in the revival's despite — supplying, under counterfeit warrant, the medieval and primitive imagination it craved. ## Contrasts & Debates

The forgeries generated the century's fiercest authenticity debates. On Ossian, critics split: Professor Sullivan noted that genuine old Irish poetry had "very little in descriptions of outward nature," while Principal Shairp took that same "sadness of tone in describing nature" as "a strong proof of authenticity" (009-019). Wordsworth, "born and reared in a mountainous country," rejected Ossian on the evidence of his own eyes — "I knew that the imagery was spurious," everything "defined, insulated, dislocated, deadened, yet nothing distinct" (009-022). On Rowley, the philological case was decisive: forged script, modern habits of capitalization, the tell-tale vellum (010-033). The debates thus turned on the very question the revival raised — what is genuine antiquity, and does it matter to the poetry?
