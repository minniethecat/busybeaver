# Edward Young

## Overview

Edward Young is, for Beers, "the author of 'Night Thoughts'" and of the *Conjectures on Original Composition* — a poet who bridges the Augustan age and the graveyard school, and a critic who helped awaken the European interest in original genius. Goss called him "a 'connecting link between this group of poets and their predecessors of the Augustan age'" (005-035).

## Life

Beers treats Young chiefly through two works and their dates. His *Night Thoughts* appeared across "(1742-44)," at the "funereal sable" extreme of the age's somberness (005-035); his critical essay, the "'Conjectures on Original Composition,' was published in 1759" (011-020). Beyond these landmarks Beers records little of the private life, keeping to Young's public place in the literary history.

## Works & Contribution

Young's two contributions are the poem and the essay. *Night Thoughts* is his blank-verse meditation on death, grouped with "the 'Essay on Man,' and ... 'The Grave'" as "a poem of the moral-didactic order" (005-056), and set at the darkest pole of the graveyard verse (005-035). The *Conjectures on Original Composition* (1759) made him, for the German literary historians, one of the "three English critics ... as predecessors of Herder in awakening interest in popular poetry" (011-020). See [[night-thoughts]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Young matters to the revival on both a sentimental and a critical axis. *Night Thoughts* carried the death-haunted, "funereal" strain of the poetry of melancholy to its extreme (005-035), part of the wider return of feeling against Augustan gayety. And the *Conjectures* — a plea for original genius over imitation — helped supply the theory of originality that the revival, at home and abroad, was reaching toward (011-020). See [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]] and [[blank-verse]].

## Reception & Influence

Young's European influence was very large. Beers notes "the translations of Young's 'Night Thoughts'" on the Continent (011-087), and credits his *Conjectures* with a role, recognized by German historians, in "awakening interest in popular poetry" ahead of Herder (011-020). At home, Beers ranks his poem below Gray's *Elegy* — the "masterpiece of this whole 'Il Penseroso' school" — as more purely "moral-didactic" (005-056).
