## Overview

Matthew Arnold was a Victorian poet and critic whose pronouncements on poetry, Homer, and the Celtic genius serve Beers throughout "A History of English Romanticism in the Eighteenth Century" as authoritative reference points. Though Arnold wrote in the nineteenth century, well after the romantic movement that Beers chronicles, his critical judgments — on the superiority of "genuine poetry" over Augustan verse, on Gray's odes, on Ossian, and on the Celtic contribution to English literature — are woven throughout the narrative as a later critic's confirmation of romantic values.

## Life

Matthew Arnold was born in 1822 at Laleham, Middlesex, the son of Dr. Thomas Arnold, the celebrated headmaster of Rugby School. He was educated at Rugby, Winchester, and Balliol College, Oxford, and served as Professor of Poetry at Oxford from 1857 to 1867. For most of his working life he was an inspector of schools, travelling widely across England and the Continent. His literary output included some of the most influential Victorian poetry — "Dover Beach," "The Scholar Gypsy," "Sohrab and Rustum" — as well as seminal works of cultural and literary criticism. He died in 1888.

## Works & Contribution

Arnold's critical writings are quoted by Beers across five different chapters, revealing the range of Arnold's engagement with the romantic tradition. In the chapter on the Augustans, Beers invokes Arnold's famous distinction: "The difference between genuine poetry and the poetry of Dryden, Pope, and all their school is briefly this: their poetry is conceived and composed in their wits, genuine poetry is conceived and composed in the soul." (002-027) This judgment — that Augustan poetry is poetry of wit rather than soul — is the very premise of Beers's narrative of romantic revival.

In the context of Shakspere criticism, Beers notes that Arnold "wanted an English Academy," but that "the academic vaccine, after so long a transmission, worked but mildly" — Arnold being among those critics who recognized Shakspere's greatness while still measuring him by classical standards. (003-012)

On Gray, Beers records that "there are not wanting critics of authority, such as Lowell and Matthew Arnold, who have pronounced Gray's odes higher poetry than his 'Elegy'" — a judgment that Beers respectfully disputes, siding with popular instinct and Dr. Johnson. (005-053)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Arnold does not participate directly in the eighteenth-century romantic revival but rather serves as its belated critical vindicator. His defense of [[ossian]] is particularly significant. Beers quotes a long passage from Arnold's "Study of Celtic Literature" (1867) in which Arnold argues that, however much of Macpherson's Ossian may be forged, "there will still be left in the book a residue with the very soul of the Celtic genius in it." (009-020)

This endorsement from Arnold — the apostle of culture and critical discrimination — carries weight in Beers's own assessment of Ossian. Arnold's claim that Macpherson's book "has the proud distinction of having brought this soul of the Celtic genius into contact with the nations of modern Europe, and enriched all our poetry by it" provides a counterweight to the relentless debunking of Ossian's authenticity. (009-020)

In the chapter on the German tributary, Beers invokes Arnold's insistence on Homer's "nobility" and "grand style" as a corrective to the nineteenth-century tendency — exemplified by Professor Newman's translations — to reduce Homer to a primitive ballad-maker. (011-022)

Arnold's own poem "The Scholar Gypsy" is mentioned by Beers as having been suggested by a passage in one of the German ballads translated by [[matthew-lewis]]. (011-096) This connection, though brief, illustrates the web of influence linking German romanticism, English translation, and Victorian poetry.

## Reception & Influence

Arnold represents, in Beers's narrative, the critical consensus of the later nineteenth century — a position that has absorbed the romantic revolution but retains the classical commitment to standards of judgment. His willingness to defend Ossian's Celtic "residue" while rejecting its forged elements, to praise Gray's odes while recognizing the Elegy's popular supremacy, and to balance the claims of primitive vigor and classical nobility makes him an ideal witness for Beers's own balanced assessments.
