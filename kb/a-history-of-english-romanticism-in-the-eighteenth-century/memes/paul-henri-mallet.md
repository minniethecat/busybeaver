## Overview

Paul-Henri Mallet was a Swiss scholar from Geneva whose work on Norse mythology and early Scandinavian literature provided essential source material for the eighteenth-century romantic revival. His "Histoire de Dannemarc," translated into English as "Northern Antiquities" (1770) by Bishop [[thomas-percy]], became the standard reference on Runic mythology throughout Europe. Mallet's scholarship nourished the Gothic taste for northern antiquity in both England and Germany, influencing figures from [[thomas-gray]] to Percy and shaping the romantic conception of the primitive sublime.

## Life

Mallet was born in Geneva, where he received his education before becoming professor of French literature at the Academy of Copenhagen. His residence in Denmark gave him access to the Old Norse manuscripts and Eddaic materials that became the foundation of his scholarly reputation. He later served as tutor to the Crown Prince of Denmark and travelled widely in northern Europe. Mallet returned to Geneva in his later years, where he continued his literary and historical work until his death in 1807. (006-004)

## Works & Contribution

Mallet's principal work was his "Histoire de Dannemarc" (History of Denmark), first published in 1755. The book included extensive translations and discussions of the Old Norse Eddas and Sagas, making this body of literature accessible to a European readership for the first time. Beers records that it bears the same relation to the literature of Runic mythology that [[sainte-palaye]]'s "Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie" bears to the study of medieval chivalry — the standard authority for all eighteenth-century writing on the subject. (007-001)

Thomas Percy, already at work on his "[[reliques-of-ancient-english-poetry]]," undertook the English translation, which appeared in 1770 under the title "Northern Antiquities; or a Description of the Manners, Customs, Religion, and Laws of the Ancient Danes." The translation included Percy's own preface, defending the study of northern antiquities against classical prejudice. (006-005)

Through Mallet, the Eddaic literature made a deep impression in Germany as well as England. Gerstenberg's "Gedicht eines Skalden" (1766), one of the earliest literary fruits of the German translation of Mallet's work, predated the publication of Gray's Norse odes by two years. (011-006)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Mallet's importance to the romantic movement lies in his role as a transmitter of the "Gothic" northern past. Beers positions his "Histoire de Dannemarc" as a scholarly counterpart to Sainte Palaye's work on medieval chivalry — together, these two continental scholars supplied the documentary foundation upon which English writers like [[richard-hurd]], Percy, and Gray built their imaginative reconstructions of the medieval and primitive worlds. (011-001)

The Runic and Norse material that Mallet introduced fed directly into the cult of the primitive and the sublime that was central to pre-romantic aesthetics. Gray's two Norse odes, "The Fatal Sisters" and "The Descent of Odin," drew inspiration from Mallet's presentation of the Edda, though the vogue for "Runic" poetry was not without its critics. Beers notes that the antiquary [[joseph-ritson]] ridiculed "Mallet's and Gray's Runic experiments" as unfounded. (007-031)

However, Beers also observes that Mallet's understanding of his materials was imperfect: "Mallet himself had a very confused notion" of the relationship between the Celtic and Gothic races, conflating the ancient Britons with the Scandinavian invaders. (006-018) This confusion reflects the broader scholarly limitations of the period, yet his work remained the indispensable gateway to northern antiquity for two generations of romantic writers.

## Reception & Influence

The influence of Mallet's "Northern Antiquities" extended well beyond England. In Germany, where the romantic movement was developing along parallel lines, Mallet's presentation of Norse mythology reinforced the growing enthusiasm for the Teutonic past and the literature of the Volk. (011-006) The Eddaic strain in German romanticism, from Gerstenberg through the Schlegels, can be traced in part to Mallet's pioneering scholarship.

In England, though Mallet's name gradually faded behind the more famous literary works his scholarship enabled, his role as a conduit between continental antiquarian learning and British literary practice was foundational. Percy's "Reliques" and the ballad revival, Gray's Norse experiments, and the broader eighteenth-century fascination with the primitive sublime all rest, to some degree, on the documentary groundwork that Mallet laid. (007-001)
