## Overview

Stonehenge, the great prehistoric circle on Salisbury Plain, appears in Beers's history as an image of remote, mysterious antiquity — "the supposed habitation of Druids, bards and wizards" (006-010). For the poets of [[the-school-of-warton|the Warton school]] it was exactly the kind of "place remarkably romantic" whose associations stirred the imagination more than any classical allusion could.

## Description & Location

The monument enters English verse as a figure of moss-grown, solitary age. Beers cites [[john-dyer]]'s "The Fleece," with its picture "of 'solitary Stonehenge gray with moss'" among the ruins and mountains that drew the landscape poet's romantic eye (004-092). Thomas Warton made the site the subject of a sonnet, "Written at Stonehenge," one of a group of poems "touching upon his favorite studies" alongside "On King Arthur's Round Table" (006-035). The stones' appeal lies in their vast, unexplained antiquity — "mysterious rows / Of rude enormous obelisks," in the passage Beers connects with the cult of "ruined altars" (006-010).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

Stonehenge belongs to the Druidic strain of the revival. Joseph Warton, arguing against Pope, held that "the mention of places remarkably romantic, the supposed habitation of Druids, bards and wizards, is far more pleasing to the imagination, than the obvious introduction of Cam and Isis," and "frequently wondered that our modern writers have made so little use of the druidical times and the traditions of the old bards" (006-010). Behind him stood Milton, whose "old bards, the famous Druids" on "the shaggy top of Mona high" furnished the model image (006-009). Stonehenge thus became a touchstone for [[medievalism|the taste for antiquity]] and [[the-sublime]] that the Wartons opposed to Augustan polish.
