# Poetic Diction

## Definition

Poetic diction, in Beers's account, is the stock of Latinized vocabulary, periphrasis, and personified abstraction that the eighteenth-century classical school held to be the proper language of verse. Beers treats it as "an outward sign of the classical preference for the general over the concrete." (002-054)

The vocabulary was Latinized, he explains, "because, in English, the _mot propre_ is commonly a Saxon word, while its Latin synonym has a convenient indefiniteness that keeps the subject at arm's length." (002-054)

Its favorite ornament was personification, "which gave a false air of life to abstractions by the easy process of spelling them with a capital letter." (002-054)

Beers fixes the term by the target [[william-wordsworth|Wordsworth]] set for attack — "the spurious poetic diction of the eighteenth century" that the [[romanticism|romantic]] reformers set out to purge. (005-032)

## Origins & Development

The manner belonged to the reign of "correctness," and its rhetorical figures — "personifications, periphrasis, antithesis, and climax" — were, Beers writes, "so dear to the Augustan heart." (008-063)

Its defenders thought a rich, elevated diction essential to poetry. Beers quotes Gray's dictum that "the language of the age is never the language of poetry, except among the French, whose verse . . . differs in nothing from prose." (006-070)

He reads Gray's letter as "an anticipatory defense of himself against Wordsworth's strictures in the preface to the 'Lyrical Ballads,'" observing that "the romanticists loved a rich diction" no less than the Augustans did. (006-070)

The style hardened into a set of conventional signs — the perpetual "gild" of descriptive verse and the "feeble mythology" of capitalized passions that survived through the whole period. (002-068) (005-046)

## Key Figures & Works

[[william-collins|Collins]]' ode "The Passions," Beers writes, "abounds in those personifications which . . . constituted, in eighteenth century poetry, a sort of feeble mythology: 'wan Despair,' 'dejected Pity,' 'brown Exercise.'" (005-046)

He traces the machinery back to Milton, whose allegorical figures in "L'Allegro" and "Il Penseroso" "gave a new lease of life to this obsolescent machinery which the romanticists ought to have abandoned to the Augustan schools." (005-046)

The classic case of protest is [[thomas-gray|Gray]]'s sonnet on the death of Richard West, "selected by Wordsworth, to illustrate his strictures on the spurious poetic diction of the eighteenth century, in the appendix to the preface to the second edition of 'Lyrical Ballads.'" (005-032)

Against the manner Beers sets the [[reliques-of-ancient-english-poetry|Reliques]] of [[thomas-percy|Percy]], whose old ballads had "none of the conventional figures of speech or rhetorical artifices like personifications, periphrasis, antithesis, and climax so dear to the Augustan heart." (008-063)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers makes the old ballads the antidote to the disease of diction. The [[the-ballad-revival|ballad revival]], he writes, was "a striking object lesson . . . of the plethora from which English poetic diction was suffering, and of the sanative value of a book like the 'Reliques.'" (008-078)

Yet the malady infected even the antiquaries: Percy's own restorations of fragmentary pieces "are full of [[sentimentalism|sentimentalism]], affectation, and the spurious poetic diction of his age." (008-058)

The reform was completed only when Wordsworth, in the Appendix to the "Lyrical Ballads," arraigned a century of verse that "does not contain a single new image of external nature." (002-068)

Beers thus presents the purging of poetic diction as one face of the larger return from the general to the concrete — from [[classicism|classical]] abstraction to romantic particularity. (002-054)

## Contrasts & Debates

The debate turned on whether a special, elevated language was proper to poetry at all. Gray held that it was; Wordsworth denied it, and the quarrel over diction became a defining line between [[classicism|classicism]] and [[romanticism|romanticism]]. (006-070) (005-032)

Beers is careful about the paradox that the romanticists themselves "loved a rich diction," so that the objection was less to ornament than to its stereotyped, abstract, and insincere Augustan form. (006-070) (008-063)
