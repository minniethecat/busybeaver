# The Schoolmistress

## Overview

*The Schoolmistress* is William Shenstone's Spenserian poem, which Beers calls "One of the pleasantest outcomes of this literary fashion" (003-041). Gray's verdict fixes its rank: he "pronounced 'The Schoolmistress' a masterpiece in its kind" (004-071). With Thomson's *Castle of Indolence* it is named among the standing "specimens of this class of literature" (003-031).

## Author & Date

The poem is William Shenstone's, "published in an unfinished shape in 1737 and, as finally completed, in 1742" (003-041). Shenstone was himself, Beers notes elsewhere, "one of the best of landscape gardeners" (004-002); Gray's tart portrait of the man — "always wishing for money, for fame and other distinctions," his "whole philosophy" consisting "in living, against his will, in retirement" — accompanies his praise of the poem (004-071).

## Summary / Content

*The Schoolmistress* is "an affectionate half-humorous description of the little dame-school of Shenstone's — and of everybody's — native village, and has the true idyllic touch" (003-041). Its tenderness is concrete: Beers instances "the incident of the hen with her brood of chickens, entering the open door of the schoolhouse in search of crumbs, and of the grief of the little sister" (003-042). Goldsmith remembered it "when he drew the picture of the school in his 'Deserted Village'" (003-042).

## Form & Style

The poem applies "Spenser's stately verse and grave, ancient words" to "so humble a theme," which "gives a very quaint effect" (003-042). Beers distinguishes its comedy from mere parody: "The humor of 'The Schoolmistress' is genuine, not dependent on the more burlesque, as in Pope's and Cambridge's experiments; and it is warmed with a certain tenderness" (003-042). Written in the Spenserian stanza, it belongs with the "new type" of poems that abandoned the heroic couplet (004-003).

## Significance to Romanticism

*The Schoolmistress* shows the Spenserian revival turning the old stately stanza to fresh, tender, domestic use — evidence that the imitation of Spenser "lay, at least, a future, a development" (003-057). Its genuine humor and idyllic feeling mark the recovery of a warmth and particularity the Augustan mode lacked; Gray's high estimate (004-071) certifies its standing among the discerning. See [[spenserianism]] and [[the-faerie-queene]].

## Reception & Controversy

The poem's reception was warm and its influence real — praised by Gray, imitated by Goldsmith (003-042, 004-071). Beers pairs it repeatedly with Thomson's *Castle of Indolence* as the two enduring "specimens" of the Spenserian fashion (003-031), and notes its currency in the general recognition of "the popularity" of the mid-century revival texts (010-046).
