# Medievalism

## Definition

Medievalism is, for Beers, all but coextensive with romanticism itself. In the working definition that opens the book, [[Romanticism]] "means the reproduction in modern art or literature of the life and thought of the Middle Ages" (001-002). The critical sense of the word *romantic* points the same way: it is defined as "pertaining or appropriate to the style of the Christian and popular literature of the Middle Ages, as opposed to the classical antique," and *romance* itself descends from the tales of chivalrous adventure composed in the medieval vernacular tongues (001-009). Beers grounds the term in a plain "unlikeness" between "the thought and art of the nations of pagan antiquity and the thought and art of the peoples of Christian, feudal Europe" — the cathedral of Chartres and the walls of Nuremberg standing on the medieval, romantic side of the line (001-003). Even so, he treats medievalism as one leading strand rather than the whole: it is "the side" of the romantic reaction "with which the present work will mainly deal," alongside "other elements besides medievalism which enter into the concept of the term 'romantic'" (002-001).

## Origins & Development

Beers frames the medieval revival as "only an incident--though a leading incident--of" the larger European reaction against the eighteenth century, one that "did not reach importance until the seventh decade of the century" (002-001). A decisive theoretical impulse came from Richard Hurd, whose "Letters on Chivalry and Romance" (1762) "helped to initiate the romantic movement," drawing in part on Sainte Palaye's "Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie" (007-001). The taste then spread outward from scholarship: Beers records "the antiquarian spirit... spreading from architecture and numismatics into literature," accompanied by satire on the antiquaries who strove to "bid every long-lost Gothic art revive" (007-012). By the close of the century the vogue was entrenched enough that "the taste for medievalism... has survived" successive changes of fashion (001-038).

## Key Figures & Works

Hurd's letters argued outright for "the pre-eminence of the Gothic manners and fictions, as adapted to the ends of poetry, above the classic" (007-002). The most conspicuous embodiment of the revival was Horace Walpole, who from about 1750 turned his villa at Strawberry Hill "into a miniature Gothic castle" and "may be regarded as having given a real impetus to the revival of pointed architecture" (007-018). Out of these architectural amusements grew "The Castle of Otranto" (1765), "perhaps the first modern work of fiction which depended for its interest on the incidents of a chivalrous age," and thus "the prototype of that class of novel which was afterward imitated by Mrs. Radcliffe and perfected by Sir Walter Scott" (007-020). Walpole, in Leslie Stephen's phrase, "discovered that a most charming toy might be made of medievalism" (007-027). His success bred imitators, among them Clara Reeve, whose "Champion of Virtue" (1777) was reprinted as "The Old English Baron" and styled on its title-page "A Gothic Story" (007-041).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

The revival of the Middle Ages is, in Beers' account, one of the taproots of English romanticism: "the Gothic revival went hand in hand with the romantic movement in literature, if indeed it did not give it its original impulse" (007-019). The change of taste can be measured in a single word, as "the despised 'Gothick' of Addison... was fast becoming the admired 'Gothic' of Scott" (007-005). Yet Beers is candid that the early medievalism was superficial: "Walpole's medievalism was very thin," for its author "knew little about the Middle Ages and was not in touch with their spirit" (007-039)(007-040). The genuine recovery of medieval feeling would come only with later hands.

## Contrasts & Debates

Medievalism is by nature a "referential" idea: *romantic* "implies its opposite, the classic," so the return to the Middle Ages defines itself against pagan antiquity (001-003). During the classical period the very term "Gothic," as used in criticism, "was synonymous with barbarous, lawless, and tawdry" (007-009). Beers also notes the dissenting view of critics such as Dr. Hedge, for whom "medievalism... is merely an accident of romance" rather than its essence (001-030) — a reminder that, even within the book, the identification of romanticism with the medieval remains provisional.
