# The Ode

## Definition

The ode, in Beers's history, is the lyric form whose mid-century revival marks the return of "music" and imagination to English verse after the reign of the couplet. He treats it partly as a symptom of restlessness under the classical yoke: "When the poets wished to be daring and irregular, they were apt to give vent in that species of pseudo-Pindaric ode which Cowley had introduced" — a form Dr. Johnson deplored as "a literary disease" (002-042). In its finished Miltonic-lyric form, however, the ode becomes for Beers the vehicle of the age's choicest poetry, the medium of "the Miltonic lyrists" whose work "marks an advance upon that of the descriptive and elegiac poets" (005-046).

## Origins & Development

The ode's revival belongs to the general recovery of pre-Augustan lyric forms. Beers sets it beside the return of the sonnet, which "disappeared entirely, until revived by Gray, Stillingfleet, Edwards, and Thomas Warton, about the middle of the eighteenth century" (002-042). At first the impulse produced excess: Dodsley's collection shows "Pindaric odes *ad nauseam*, with imitations of Spenser and Milton," the "minor poetry of an age" faithfully keeping "the ... impressure of a passing literary fashion" (005-032). Beers is severe on the crude article — "The pseudo-Pindaric ode is, in itself, an exotic," an "abstraction" (005-046) — but the form matured in abler hands into the true lyric of Collins and Gray.

## Key Figures & Works

The ode's triumph is Collins's. "Romantically speaking, the work of these Miltonic lyrists marks an advance," and "Collins is among the choicest of English lyrical poets": "There is a flute-like music in his best odes — such as the one 'To Evening,' and the one written in 1746 — 'How sleep the brave,' which are sweeter, more natural, and more spontaneous than Gray's" (005-046). Swinburne's verdict serves Beers as summary: "'The Muse gave birth to Collins ... she did but give suck to Gray'" — Collins "a solitary song-bird among many more or less excellent pipers and pianists" (005-046). Gray's own odes belong here too, alongside the ode "written for music, and performed" among "the latest of his poems" (005-031). Behind them stands Cowley, whose "pseudo-Pindaric ode" opened, for good and ill, the whole vein (002-042).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

The ode is one of the formal engines of the romantic revival: by reviving an irregular, musical, imaginative lyric against the "correctness" of the couplet, it gave the returning romantic feeling a native shape. Beers reads the very wish "to be daring and irregular" (002-042) as the restlessness out of which the revival grew, and ranks the Miltonic lyric ode as a "romantic" advance beyond mere description and elegy (005-046). In Collins's "flute-like music" (005-046) the age recovered a spontaneity and song that Augustan verse had disciplined away. See [[romanticism]] for the movement and [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]] for the elegiac mood the odes so often carried.

## Contrasts & Debates

The ode's history frames a debate about form and value: whether the "pseudo-Pindaric" irregularity was "a literary disease" (002-042) and "an exotic" (005-046), or the seed of a genuine lyric revival. Beers holds both truths at once — condemning the "*ad nauseam*" imitations (005-032) while exalting Collins's finished odes (005-046). Behind the quarrel lies the larger contest of rhyme against irregular measure, and of "wit" against "music": Beers's curious note that "rhyme, a mediaeval invention," had become "the classical school['s]" badge, while the freer lyric measures became "a shibboleth of romanticizing poets" (002-039).
