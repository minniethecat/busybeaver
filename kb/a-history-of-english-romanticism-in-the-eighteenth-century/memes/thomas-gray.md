# Thomas Gray

## Overview

Thomas Gray is, for Beers, one of the central figures of the mid-century revival — the poet whose "immortal 'Elegy'" summed up [[the-poetry-of-melancholy|the poetry of melancholy]] and whose antiquarian and scenic enthusiasms pointed straight toward [[romanticism]].

"Gray's popular fame rests, and will always rest, upon his immortal 'Elegy'" (005-053), yet Beers is at least as interested in his Norse studies and his feeling for wild nature.

Placed by Beers among the Miltonic lyrists of [[the-miltonic-group]] and again among the scholars of [[the-school-of-warton]], Gray joins the [[elegy-written-in-a-country-churchyard|churchyard Elegy]] to the [[northern-antiquities|Norse revival]], so that a single career runs from the poetry of the tomb to the mythology of Odin.

"Thomas Gray is a much more important figure than Collins in the intellectual history of his generations; but this superior importance does not rest entirely upon his verse" (005-051) — Beers ranks him, with fellow scholar-poet [[thomas-warton]], among the learned poets whose taste and reading opened the way for the romantic age.

## Life

Beers records Gray chiefly through his poems and his letters. The *Elegy* "was published in 1751"; his earlier odes had been "printed in Dodsley's collection in 1748"; and "the two 'sister odes,' 'The Progress of Poesy' and 'The Bard,' were struck off from Horace Walpole's private press at Strawberry Hill in 1757" (005-053).

Beers stresses the scholarly cast of the man: "One of the greatest scholars among English poets, his taste was equal to his acquisitions," and his "letters, journals, and other prose remains, posthumously published, first showed how long an arc his mind had subtended on the circle of art and thought" (005-051).

His travels began early: "In 1739 he went to France and Italy with [[horace-walpole|Horace Walpole]]," was "abroad three years," and "in 1741 he quarreled with Walpole at Florence, separated from him and made his way home alone in a leisurely manner" (005-057).

His later years were given to travel among ruins and mountains: in 1765 he visited the Scotch Highlands (005-063), and "in 1770, the year before his death, he spent six weeks on a ramble through the western counties ... visiting ... Hagley and the Leasowes, the Malvern Hills and Tintern Abby" (005-064).

"The most significant of Gray's 'Lilliputian travels' was his tour of the Lake Country in 1769" (005-064).

## Works & Contribution

Gray's contribution runs on two lines. The first is the elegiac: the *Elegy*, "the most admired and influential poem of the generation" (005-057), which replaced abstract moralizing with "the picture of the solitary poet, lingering among the graves at twilight" (005-057). Beers sets it at the head of its school:

> But the "Elegy" is the masterpiece of this whole "Il Penseroso" school, and has summed up for all English readers, for all time, the poetry of the tomb. (005-056)

Its effect was formal as well as emotional, consecrating the ten-syllabled quatrain to elegiac uses so that "it became almost _de rigueur_ for a young poet to try his hand at a churchyard piece" (005-057).

The second line is the antiquarian-Northern: "Gray's studies in Runic literature took shape in 'The Fatal Sisters' and 'The Descent of Odin,' written in 1761, published in 1768," paraphrased from Bartholin (006-006).

He was a careful scholar of the material, correcting Mason for "mixing the Gothic and Celtic mythologies" and insisting that "Woden and his Valhalla belong to 'the doctrine of the Scalds, not of the Bards'" (006-018).

Beers stresses that Gray was early on this ground: "Gray did not wait for the English translation of Mallet's book" (006-005), the "Introduction à l'Histoire de Dannemarc" (1755) that "first aroused, in modern Europe, an interest in [[northern-antiquities|Norse mythology]]" (006-004). ## Role in the Romantic Revival

Gray prefigures romanticism in three ways at once. His *Elegy* crowned the poetry of melancholy (005-057); his Runic paraphrases opened Norse mythology to English verse (006-006); and his response to wild scenery anticipated Wordsworth.

Before the [[the-alps|Alps]], he wrote, "'Since I saw the Alps, I have seen nothing sublime till now ... the mountains are ecstatic'" (005-063), and his account of Netley Abbey — best "'visited only in the dusk of the evening'" — is, Beers says, "a prophecy of Scott" (005-062).

His Lake Country tour set him "on ground that has since become classic," where "the lover of Wordsworth encounters with a singular interest" his notes (005-064).

Beers reads Gray's whole correspondence as a barometer of the changing taste. Gray "set out with classical prepossessions — forming his verse, as he declared, after Dryden — and ended with translations from Welsh and Norse hero-legends, and with an admiration for [[ossian|Ossian]] and Scotch ballads" (005-057).

He was, moreover, "one of the first of modern travelers to speak appreciatively of [[gothicism|Gothic]] architecture, and of the scenery of the Alps, and to note those strange and characteristic aspects of foreign life which we now call [[the-picturesque|picturesque]]" (005-057).

Where Beers credits [[percy-and-the-ballads|Percy]] with the ballad and the [[the-german-tributary|German tributary]] with the Sturm-und-Drang, he credits Gray with the Northern mythology.

His "thrilling Ode on the 'Descent of Odin'" (006-074) drew on Mallet, whose credit it was "of first exciting that interest in Scandinavian antiquity which has enriched the prose and poetry not only of England but of Europe in general" (006-005).

## Reception & Influence

Gray's fame was immense and durable — the *Elegy* "known by heart to more readers than any other poem in the language" (005-057). Beers records the divided estimate of his rank among "critics of authority, such as Lowell and Matthew Arnold" (005-053), and notes Gray's own impatience with the *Elegy*'s popularity, which "he thought ... was owing to its subject" (005-053).

Contemporary opinion was likewise split. Johnson praised the *Elegy* while distrusting the odes:

> In the character of his 'Elegy,' I rejoice to concur with the common reader; for by the common sense of readers uncorrupted with literary prejudices ... must be finally decided all claims to poetical honors. The 'Churchyard' abounds with images which find a mirror in every mind. (005-054)

Beers himself half-agrees with the complaint against the more elaborate odes: "There are noble lines in Gray's more elaborate odes, but they ... make ... that mechanical, artificial impression of which Johnson complains" (005-055).

Against Gray he sets his friend [[william-collins|Collins]], whose best odes are "sweeter, more natural, and more spontaneous than Gray's," so that Swinburne could say, "The Muse gave birth to Collins ... she did but give suck to Gray" (005-046).

Yet in the long view Beers gives Gray the larger place, and his Norse work he credits with helping open the vein that runs "to William Morris' 'Sigurd the Volsung'" (006-005) — his poems from the Norse preceding, "though not the composition," the wider German and English enthusiasm for the Eddaic literature (011-006).
