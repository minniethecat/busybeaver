## Overview

Joseph Addison (1672–1719) was the essayist of the "Spectator" and the arbiter who, as [[richard-hurd|Hurd]] put it, "gave the law in taste here." (007-008)

His authority made him the classical century's taste-maker; yet the same papers that "endeavored... to banish this Gothic taste" also praised the old ballads and mapped the "pleasures of the imagination," opening doors the [[romanticism|Romantic revival]] would later walk through. (007-010)

## Life

Beers gives little external biography, drawing Addison from his essays and travels. His learning, he notes, was almost wholly ancient: "His favorite models were Latin. His favorite critics were French." (002-035)

On his continental tour Addison was, in Beers's phrase, "quite blind" to mountain scenery. He wrote of "a very troublesome journey over the Alps... you can't imagine how much I am pleased with the sight of a plain." (005-057)

His tragedy "Cato" was pronounced by [[samuel-johnson|Johnson]] "unquestionably the noblest production of Addison's genius." (002-038)

## Works & Contribution

Beers places Addison second in the classical line of "high authority in three successive generations—Dryden, Addison, and Johnson." (002-035)

His "Spectator" criticism applied the ancient rules. He "tries 'Paradise Lost' by Aristotle's rules for the composition of an epic," asking only whether it observes the recipes of Homer and Vergil. (002-047)

He looked at Milton's poem, Beers observes, "as a manufactured article to be tested by comparing it with standard fabrics by recognized makers, like the authors of the Iliad and Aeneid." (002-048)

His most fruitful papers were those on the "pleasures of the imagination" (Spectator Nos. 411–421). "Following Addison," [[mark-akenside]] would name "greatness and novelty, i.e., the sublime and the wonderful," as sources of imaginative pleasure. (004-074)(004-080)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Addison is the great paradox of Beers's account. As classical arbiter he declared "the taste of most of our English poets... is extremely Gothic," boasting, "I have endeavored... to banish this [[gothicism|Gothic taste]] which has taken possession among us." (007-010)

Yet Beers judges the label a misnomer. "Addison was a writer of pure taste, but the coldness and timidity of his imagination... made him mistake for spurious decoration the efflorescence of that warm, creative fancy which ran riot in Gothic art." (007-011)

In the same essays he did the revival unintended service. In Spectator Nos. 70, 74, and 85 he "praised the naturalness and simplicity of the popular ballads," singling out "Chevy Chase" and "The Two Children in the Wood." (008-044)

He also "guardedly commends 'the fairy way of writing,'" a romantic license that [[samuel-johnson|Johnson]] "despised." (002-038)

## Reception & Influence

Addison's ballad-criticism drew ridicule. He "was severely ridiculed by his contemporaries for these concessions," and Johnson wrote that he "descended now and then to lower disquisitions." (008-045)

But Beers grants the significance: "it was much that Addison, whose own verse was so artificial, should have had a taste for the wild graces of folk-song." (008-045)

His indifference to [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]] was equally telling. His verses on "the Greatest English Poets" (1694) consigned the poet to "a barbarous age"—and Addison "acknowledged to Spence that, when he wrote this passage, he had never read Spenser!" (003-024)(003-027)

## References

Beers cites Addison across the book: as classical law-giver in chapters II and VII, as unwitting friend of the ballads in chapter VIII, and as the source of Akenside's imagination-theory in chapter IV. The "Spectator" is a recurring source.
