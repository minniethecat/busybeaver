## Overview

Matthew Gregory Lewis (1775–1818), "commonly nicknamed 'Monk' Lewis, from the title of his famous romance," was the novelist and balladeer who funneled German terror into English literature and briefly mentored the young [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]]. (011-053)

His "Ambrosio, or the Monk" (1795) carried the [[the-gothic-romance|Gothic romance]] to a sensational extreme—"a lineal descendant of [[horace-walpole|Walpole]] and [[anne-radcliffe|Mrs. Radcliffe]]." (011-062)

## Life

Beers introduces Lewis as "a singular figure"—a "cheerful, foppish, round-faced little man," in Scott's description, who made "an odd contrast to the grewsome horrors of his books." (011-055)

"His 'Monk' had been published in 1795, when the author was only twenty." (011-054)

He began the romance at Oxford in 1792, then "went to Germany and took up his residence at Weimar, where he was introduced to Goethe" and to "the bizarre productions of the Sturm- und Drangperiode." (011-062)

His end was as theatrical as his fiction: after visiting his Jamaica estates, "Lewis died of yellow fever and was buried at sea." (011-057)

## Works & Contribution

Lewis's "first and most celebrated publication was 'Ambrosio, or the Monk' (1795), a three-volume romance of the Gothic type." (011-062)

He described it to his mother as "a romance in the style of '[[the-castle-of-otranto|The Castle of Otranto]],'" finishing it in ten weeks after reading Radcliffe's "Mysteries of Udolpho." (011-062)

"The Monk" heaped up "the now familiar apparatus of Gothic romance"—"magic mirrors, enchanted wands, midnight incantations, sorcerers, ghosts, demons; haunted chambers." (011-062)

Its "voluptuous" descriptions provoked such an outcry that "the Attorney General" moved to "enjoin the sale of the book," and Lewis suppressed the offending passages in later editions. (011-067)

For the stage he wrote the melodrama "The Castle Spectre," "first performed December 14, 1797, at Drury Lane," where it "ran sixty nights." (011-068)

His most famous ballad, "Alonzo the Brave and the Fair Imogene," was "original with Lewis, though evidently suggested by 'Lenore.'" (011-072)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Lewis's importance to Beers lies in his role as conduit for the German supernatural. "For years Lewis was one of the most active intermediaries between the German purveyors of the terrible and the English literary market." (011-062)

His anthology "Tales of Wonder" gathered "translations by Lewis from Goethe's 'Fisher' and 'Erl-King,' and from German versions of Runic ballads in Herder's 'Stimmen der Völker.'" (011-078)

It was through this project that he drew in Scott. Collecting for "Tales of Wonder," Lewis "begged that Scott would contribute," and Scott "felt highly flattered by the Monk's request." (011-055)

## Reception & Influence

Beers judges Lewis a lightweight: "It is no more possible to take Monk Lewis seriously than to take Horace Walpole seriously. They are both like children telling ghost-stories in the dark." (011-081)

Yet his influence on greater men was real. Scott "respectfully deferred to his advice" on versification, however comic Beers finds the pupil's superiority to the master. (011-078)

Byron "had always a kindly feeling for Lewis, though he laughed at him in 'English Bards and Scotch Reviewers.'" (011-056)

In 1816 Lewis "sojourned for a space with Byron and Shelley in their Swiss retreat and set the whole company composing goblin stories," the "most remarkable outcome" of which "was Mrs. Shelley's abnormal romance, 'Frankenstein.'" (011-057)
