# Edmund Spenser

## Overview

Edmund Spenser is, for Beers, the Elizabethan master whom the eighteenth-century revival raised as its banner — "the poet of the 'Faërie Queene,'" whose "art is the antithesis of Pope's" (003-017). "The name inscribed in large letters on the standard of the new school was not Shakspere but Spenser" (003-017), and Spenser is for Beers the poet "who is *par excellence* the poet of romance" (003-017).

## Life

Beers approaches Spenser through his reputation rather than his biography. In his own age his standing was supreme: "the complimentary verses by many hands published with the 'Faërie Queene' and the numerous references to Spenser ... leave no doubt as to the fact that his contemporaries accorded him the foremost place among Eng[lish poets]" (003-021). But "Spenser had not fared so well as Shakspere under the change which came over public taste after the Restoration" (003-021), so that the Augustan age had to be argued back to "the then paradoxical thesis that Spenser was as great a man as Pope" (002-013).

## Works & Contribution

Spenser's contribution to this history is *The Faërie Queene*, "the typical work of the English Renaissance" (002-018) and the model of the whole Spenserian revival. Its appeal for the mid-century lay in "the amplitude of Spencer's stanza, 'the full strong sail of his great verse'" (003-017), a release from the couplet. His authority reached even his greatest successor: "Milton confessed to Dryden that Spenser was his 'poetical father'" (003-021). See [[the-faerie-queene]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Though an Elizabethan, Spenser is the presiding genius of the eighteenth-century revival. As the poet "whose art is the antithesis of Pope's" (003-017), he supplied the imitators — Thomson, Shenstone, Beattie — with stanza, diction, and allegory. Beers makes the choice of Spenser over Pope the very emblem of the turning tide: "To go from Pope to Spenser was to exchange platitudes ... for" imaginative amplitude (003-017), and "In the imitation of Spenser lay, at least, a future, a development" (003-057). See [[spenserianism]] and [[romanticism]].

## Reception & Influence

Spenser's eighteenth-century fortunes were themselves the measure of the revival. Neglected relatively after the Restoration (003-021), he was restored by the Spenserians to "the standard of the new school" (003-017). Beers records the difficulty of that revaluation — the "paradoxical thesis that Spenser was as great a man as Pope" (002-013) — and its fertility, the imitation of Spenser opening "a future" where the imitation of Pope led only to decline (003-057).
