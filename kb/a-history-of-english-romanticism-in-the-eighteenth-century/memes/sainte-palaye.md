## Overview

Jean-Baptiste de La Curne de Sainte-Palaye was a French scholar of the Middle Ages whose monumental works on chivalry and the troubadours provided essential antiquarian foundations for the eighteenth-century Gothic revival. Beers pairs him with [[paul-henri-mallet]] as one of the "continental scholars" whose learning nourished the English romantic movement before the German tributary began to flow. His "Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie" (1759–1781) was a standard authority that inspired English writers from [[richard-hurd]] to [[thomas-percy]].

## Life

Sainte-Palaye was born in 1697 at Auxerre, France, into an ancient Burgundian family. He was a scholar of remarkably wide learning, versed not only in the history of medieval institutions but also in old French and Provençal dialects. To familiarize himself with Provençal, he travelled to the south of France, where he collected a large library of Provençal books and manuscripts. His twin interests — in the institutions of chivalry and in the poetry of the troubadours — made him the foremost medievalist of his generation in France. He died in 1781, the year that saw the publication of the third and concluding volume of his "Mémoires." (007-001)

## Works & Contribution

Sainte-Palaye's magnum opus was the "Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie" (Memoirs of Ancient Chivalry), published in three volumes between 1759 and 1781. Beers describes this as "a monumental work" that, as a standard authority, "bears much the same relation to the literature of its subject that Mallet's 'Histoire de Dannemarc' bears to all the writing on Runic mythology that was done in Europe during the eighteenth century." (007-001)

In 1774 he published his "Histoire de Troubadours" (History of the Troubadours), drawing on the Provençal manuscripts he had collected. Among his other works were a "Dictionary of French Antiquities," a glossary of Old French, and an edition of the medieval romance "Aucassin et Nicolete." (007-001)

Sainte-Palaye's works reached an English audience through the translations of Mrs. Susannah Dobson, who rendered the "History of the Troubadours" into English in 1779 and the "Memoirs of Ancient Chivalry" in 1784. (007-001)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Sainte-Palaye's influence on the English romantic revival operated primarily through two channels. First, his "Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie" was one of the inspirations for Richard Hurd's "Letters on Chivalry and Romance" (1762), the critical manifesto that argued for "the pre-eminence of the Gothic manners and fictions, as adapted to the ends of poetry, above the classic." Hurd's daring preference for Gothic over classical machinery — a landmark moment in the rise of romantic taste — perhaps "owed their inspiration, in part, to Sainte Palaye's 'Mémoires.'" (007-001)

Second, Sainte-Palaye's recovery and documentation of Provençal poetry contributed to the broader European rediscovery of medieval vernacular literature. Alongside Mallet's work on the Norse Eddas, Sainte-Palaye's scholarship on the troubadours helped to establish that the Middle Ages had possessed a literature of sophistication and beauty, challenging the Enlightenment prejudice that dismissed the entire period as barbarous.

Beers returns to Sainte-Palaye in the final chapter, identifying him — together with Mallet — as the sum total of foreign influence on the English romantic movement before the German tributary began to flow in the 1790s. "Up to the last decade of the eighteenth century," Beers writes, "the romantic movement in Great Britain had been self-developed and independent of foreign influence, except for such stimulus as it had found, once and again, in the writings of continental scholars like Sainte Palaye and Mallet." (011-001)

The English antiquary [[george-ellis]], a friend and correspondent of [[walter-scott]], was "sometimes called 'the Sainte Palaye of England'" for his work in recovering early English poetry — a testimony to Sainte-Palaye's standing as the European archetype of the medievalist scholar. (008-084)

## Reception & Influence

Sainte-Palaye's reputation in England was that of a foundational scholar whose work made the imaginative reconstructions of the Gothic revival possible. His "Mémoires" stood, through the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, as the authoritative treatment of chivalric institutions, and his recovery of troubadour poetry opened a new chapter in the history of European lyric. Though his name is now known chiefly to specialists, his influence on the romantic revaluation of the Middle Ages was profound and lasting. (011-001)
