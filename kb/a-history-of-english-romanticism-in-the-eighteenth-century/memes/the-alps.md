## Overview

The Alps stand in Beers's book as the archetype of natural sublimity — the measure against which even the Scottish mountains were judged. It was of the Alps that [[thomas-gray]] was thinking when, amid the [[the-highlands|Highlands]] in 1765, he wrote that "since I saw the Alps, I have seen nothing sublime till now" (005-063). The mountains belong to the same discovery of grandeur and terror in wild scenery that underlies [[the-sublime]].

## Description & Location

The Alps enter English poetry as the great continental spectacle of the Grand Tour. Gray's ranking of them above every later prospect — the standard by which the ecstatic, "monstrous" beauty of the Highlands is finally praised — fixes their place as the summit of sublime landscape (005-063). Their fascination outlasts the eighteenth century: Beers notes that Byron's early love of Highland scenery "had prepared him to love the Alps and 'blue Friuli's mountains,'" binding the northern and Alpine sublime together in the Romantic imagination (009-047).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

The taste for Alpine scenery is part of the larger revolution in feeling the book traces. Even minor verse registered it: Beers lists "'The Alps,' in heavy Thomsonian blank verse," among the descriptive poems of the mid-century, showing how quickly the mountains became a set subject in the manner of Thomson's nature-painting (005-079). The same imaginative landscape would later be, in Beers's phrase, "the Venice and the Alps of Ruskin" — proof that the sublime scenery first relished by Gray and his contemporaries had become permanent property of the Romantic and Victorian imagination (007-063).
