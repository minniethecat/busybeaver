## Overview

Hagley was the country seat of Lord Lyttelton, a Worcestershire park that Beers ranks among the great early examples of the new, natural style of landscape gardening. It is bound up with the poetry of nature: the prospect from its hill was the very view [[james-thomson]] "had painted in 'Spring'," and the park later enshrined a temple to his memory (004-008, 004-070).

## Description & Location

Horace Walpole, surveying the history of English gardening, "names Theobalds and Nonsuch as famous examples of the old formal style of garden; Stourhead, Hagley, and Stowe... of the new" (004-041). The geologist Hugh Miller, who "visited Lyttelton's country seat at Hagley in 1845," describes its "famous landscape" beneath the lines from Thomson's "Spring" — "the bursting prospect spreads immense around" (004-008). "At Hagley, halfway up the hillside," Miller "saw a semi-octagonal temple dedicated to the genius of Thomson," standing "in a grassy hollow which commanded a vast, open prospect and was a favorite resting place of the poet of 'The Seasons'"; in a secluded ravine stood also an urn Lyttelton had inscribed to the memory of Shenstone (004-070).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

Hagley belongs to the same movement as the neighbouring [[the-leasowes|Leasowes]] — the rejection of the formal, geometric garden for an ideal of cultivated wildness that Beers reads as the outdoor counterpart of [[landscape-poetry]] and [[the-picturesque]]. Its "big park" set the fashion so strongly that even visitors to Hagley would go on to ask after [[william-shenstone|Shenstone]]'s smaller creation nearby (004-053, 004-062). By memorialising Thomson on its hillside, the park makes literal the link between the cult of natural scenery and the verse of the [[the-landscape-poets|landscape poets]]; Beers returns to "Hagley and the Leasowes" as twin monuments of that taste (005-064).
