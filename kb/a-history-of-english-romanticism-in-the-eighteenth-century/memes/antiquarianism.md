# Antiquarianism

## Definition

Antiquarianism, in Beers's history, is the learned study of the material and textual remains of the past — "the manners, the buildings, the records, and the coins of the ages that preceded us" — that supplied the [[medievalism|medieval revival]] with its raw materials. (006-060)

In its narrow professional form it was the work of "antiquaries like Gough, Hearne, Bentham, Perry, Grose," whose researches produced "only local histories, surveys of cathedrals and of sepulchral monuments, books about Druidic remains, Roman walls and coins." (006-001)

Beers marks the moment the spirit widened, quoting a contemporary essayist that "the antiquarian spirit . . . has now extended itself to those poetical compositions which were popular among our forefathers, but which have gradually sunk into oblivion." (006-060)

By that extension the old ballad, once "thought worthy the attention of children only," was "rescued from the hands of the vulgar, to obtain a place in the collection of the man of taste." (006-060)

## Origins & Development

Beers traces the growth of antiquarianism to "the progress made in historical inquiries during the last half of the eighteenth century," citing Leslie Stephen's account of the awakening "interest in the minuter details of the past." (002-013)

At first, he notes, the new learning bred "an unreasonable contempt for the past," so that "Gothic . . . became a simple term of contempt." (002-013)

But the temper reversed itself: "an antiquarian is naturally a conservative, and men soon began to love the times whose peculiarities they were so diligently studying." (002-013)

Beers dates the decisive turn to about 1760, when "writers began to gravitate decidedly toward the Middle Ages" and the scholarship at last began to feed the imagination. (006-001)

## Key Figures & Works

Beers names "antiquarian scholars like Joseph and [[thomas-warton|Thomas Warton]]" among the founders of the taste, "eager students of old English poetry, Gothic architecture, and British antiquities." (002-013) (006-025)

Thomas Warton gave the movement its great monument, [[the-history-of-english-poetry|the "History of English Poetry"]] — the design Gray had "vaguely designed" and Warton "executed with great industry." (002-013)

Warton's antiquarian humor even surfaces in his verse — a mock "epistle . . . to Thomas Hearne, the antiquary," cursing those who would print old prayers "in modern type instead of black letter." (006-035)

Among the ballad-editors Beers singles out Joseph Ritson as "a thoroughly critical, conscientious student of poetic antiquities" who "held the right theory of an editor's functions," against [[thomas-percy|Percy]]'s freer hand. (008-061)

The novelists drew on the same well: for her feudal scenes Mrs. Radcliffe "consulted Leland's 'Collectanea,' Warton's 'History of English Poetry,' . . . the publications of the Society of Antiquaries and similar authorities." (007-070)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers makes antiquarianism the indispensable engine of the revival: "A literary movement which reverts to the past for its inspiration is necessarily also a learned movement. Antiquarian scholarship must lead the way." (006-001)

So long as the knowledge stayed with the professional antiquaries, he writes, "it bears no fruit in creative literature"; it was "only when men of imagination and of elegant tastes were enlisted in such pursuits that the dry stick of antiquarianism put forth blossoms." (006-001)

The poets therefore had "to decipher manuscripts, learn Old English, visit ruins, collect ballads and ancient armor," until scholar and poet together "reconstructed an image of medieval society." (006-001)

The type is Scott, whose "vast apparatus of notes and introductory matter . . . shows how necessary it was for the romantic poet to be his own antiquary" — an heir, through Coleridge, of [[chatterton|Chatterton]]. (006-001)

## Contrasts & Debates

The antiquary long wrote on the defensive. Beers notes "the apologetic air which antiquarian scholars assumed, when venturing to recommend their favorite studies to the attention of a classically minded public." (003-059)

The taste itself drew the line of the age's great quarrel: the essayist Beers quotes divides readers into "the lovers and imitators of Spenser and Milton" and those "of Dryden, Boileau, and Pope" — "in modern phrase, the [[romanticism|romanticists]] and the [[classicism|classicists]]." (006-060)

Later critics doubted the whole enterprise: Ruskin charged that Scott's "romance and antiquarianism, his knighthood and monkery, are all false, and he knows them to be false." (002-060)
