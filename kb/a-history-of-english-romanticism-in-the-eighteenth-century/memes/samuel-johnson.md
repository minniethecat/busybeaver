## Overview

Samuel Johnson (1709–1784) was the pre-eminent critic of the English Augustan age, whose name Beers joins to [[alexander-pope|Pope's]] as shorthand for the classical century itself—"the times of Pope and Johnson in England." (001-012)

A "sturdy disciple of Dryden and Pope," he stood, in Beers's image, as "a sort of classical watch-dog" who "prowled up and down before the temple of the British Muses." (002-038)

## Life

Beers gives few external facts, presenting Johnson chiefly through the anecdotes of Boswell's "Life." He appears as a member, with [[thomas-warton]] and [[joseph-warton|Joseph Warton]], of the Literary Club and a contributor to the "Idler" and the "Adventurer." (006-048)

His travels enter the narrative as tests of authenticity: an insensibility "to the wild beauties of Highland scenery" on his 1773 tour of the Western Islands with Boswell. (009-013)

In April 1776 he went "down to Bristol," Boswell records, "where I was entertained with seeing him inquire upon the spot into the authenticity of Rowley's poetry." (010-034)

## Works & Contribution

Beers ranks Johnson third in a line of "high authority in three successive generations—Dryden, Addison, and Johnson"—who together "consolidated a body of literary opinion which may be described, in the main, as classical." (002-035)

His criticism enforced the classical rules. He was blank verse's "persistent foe, and regarded it as little short of immoral," while praising Pope for employing "but sparingly the triplets and alexandrines" that varied the couplet. (002-039)

His "Lives of the Poets" and editions fixed the canon. He called Dryden "the father of English criticism," the writer "who first taught us to determine, upon principles, the merit of composition." (003-008)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Johnson is the great resister in Beers's story—the critic against whom nearly every romantic tendency ran. When "Spenserian had become so much the fashion," it "provoke[d] a rebuke from Dr. Johnson." (003-053)

He met the Miltonic revival with contempt. As late as 1779 he spoke of Milton's minor poems "with an absence of appreciation that now seems utterly astounding." (005-006)

Of Milton's uneven achievement he told Hannah More: "Milton, madam, was a genius that could cut a colossus from a rock, but could not carve heads upon cherry stones." (005-006)

He disliked [[thomas-gray]] and spoke of the odes with disdain: "there is a kind of cumbrous splendor which we wish away." (005-054)

Yet even Johnson bowed to the "Elegy," "rejoic[ing] to concur with the common reader" that it "abounds with images which find a mirror in every mind." (005-054)

## Reception & Influence

On the two great forgery controversies Johnson was the chief skeptic. "Among the most truculent of the disbelievers" of [[james-macpherson|Macpherson's]] Ossian, he "disputed the antiquity" of the poems and "denied it any poetic merit." (009-013)

He pronounced "Fingal" "as gross an imposition as ever the world was troubled with," arguing that a "barbarous people" could not have made such verse. (009-015)

Beers records the verdict of hindsight: "It is now quite well established that Dr. Johnson was wrong on all these points." (009-016)

He was warmer toward [[chatterton|Chatterton]], whose Rowley poems he investigated at Bristol; of the boy he said, "This is the most extraordinary young man that has encountered my knowledge." (010-034)

## References
