## Overview

Wales enters Beers's history on three fronts at once: as the landscape of [[john-dyer]]'s painter's eye, as the source of the bardic poetry [[thomas-gray]] translated, and as a country of ruined castles reclaimed by the Gothic taste. It is a working example of how a single region could feed the naturalistic, the Celtic-antiquarian, and the medievalising currents of the revival together.

## Description & Location

The Welsh hills are, first, painter's country. Dyer "was a landscape painter... and spent some time wandering about the mountains of Wales in the practice of his art"; his [[grongar-hill]] "is, in fact, a pictorial poem, a sketch of the landscape seen from the top of his favorite summit in South Wales" (004-087). The land is also studded with the medieval fabric that the antiquarian revival prized: Beers notes how Walpole and his followers helped "arrest the process of dilapidation" of many a ruin, instancing "Rhyddlan Castle, in North Wales," saved when it "fell into the possession of Dr. Shipley" (007-025).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

Wales gave the revival a native bardic past. In 1764 Gray, "incited by the publication of Dr. Evans' 'Specimens,'" made "a few translations from the Welsh," chief among them "The Triumphs of Owen," celebrating a victory won "about 1160 by a prince of North Wales, Owen Ap Griffin" (006-017) — the same Welsh matter that stands behind his Pindaric "The Bard." Welsh scenery served the Gothic stage as well: the scene of Lewis's drama is "Conway Castle in Wales, where abides Earl Osmond, a feudal tyrant of the '[[the-castle-of-otranto|Otranto]]' type" (011-070). Landscape, bardic antiquity, and Gothic melodrama thus converge on the one country.
