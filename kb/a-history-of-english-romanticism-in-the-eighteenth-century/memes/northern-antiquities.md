# Northern Antiquities

## Definition

Northern antiquities, in Beers's account, is the eighteenth-century recovery of Norse and Runic literature and mythology — the "Scandinavian antiquity which has enriched" later English poetry (006-005). Beers dates its modern origin precisely, and with some irony: "It is not a little odd that the book which first aroused, in modern Europe, an interest in Norse mythology should have been written by a Frenchman" — Paul Henri Mallet's "Introduction à l'Histoire de Dannemarc" (1755), which included "a translation of the first part of the Younger Edda ... and versions of several Runic poems," and was Englished in 1770 by Percy (006-004).

## Origins & Development

The transmission ran from Copenhagen scholarship through French mediation to English poetry. Mallet's work "was translated into English, in 1770, by Thomas Percy, the editor of the 'Reliques'" (006-004), but the English poets did not wait for it: "Gray did not wait for the English translation of Mallet's book," writing to Mason in 1758 that he was "'pleased with the Gothic Elysium'" and had "'seen it all in Mallet's Introduction to the History of Denmark'" (006-005). Behind Mallet lay the older antiquaries — Bartholin's "De Causis Contemnendae Mortis" (Copenhagen, 1689), whence Gray drew his paraphrases (006-006), and the Runic ode "preserved by Olaus Wormius" in "the Latin of Hickes' 'Thesaurus'" (006-011). Beers marks the long arc of influence: "It is a far cry from Mallet's 'System of Runic Mythology' to William Morris' 'Sigurd the Volsung' (1877), but to Mallet belongs the credit of first exciting that interest" (006-005).

## Key Figures & Works

Gray is the poet of this recovery. "Gray's studies in Runic literature took shape in 'The Fatal Sisters' and 'The Descent of Odin,' written in 1761, published in 1768" — paraphrases from Bartholin, the first describing "the Valkyrie weaving the fates of the Danish and Irish warriors in the battle of Clontarf," the second narrating "the descent of Odin to Niflheimer, to inquire of Hela concerning the doom of" Balder (006-006). Mallet supplied the sources and Percy the English channel (006-004). Gray was also the age's most careful reader of the material, correcting Mason for "mixing the Gothic and Celtic mythologies" and insisting that "Woden and his Valhalla belong to 'the doctrine of the Scalds, not of the Bards'" (006-018).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Northern antiquities opened to the romantic revival a whole pre-classical imaginative world — the "Norse hero-legends" and Runic mythology that lay outside the Greco-Roman inheritance (005-057). Beers ranks "Gray's translations from the Welsh and the Norse" among the "Contributions to romantic literature" that reach back to periods antedating even Christian chivalry (002-002). By naturalizing Odin, the Valkyries, and the Edda in English verse, the Norse revival extended the medievalizing and primitivist impulse of the age northward and backward, feeding its appetite for the sublime, the barbaric, and the remote. See [[medievalism]] for the antiquarian turn, [[the-sublime]] for the taste it served, and [[romanticism]] for the movement.

## Contrasts & Debates

The recovery was dogged by scholarly confusion, which Beers is careful to record: "The scholarship of the day was not always accurate in discriminating between ancient systems of religion," and Gray had to instruct Mason against "mixing the Gothic and Celtic mythologies," conceding that "in that scarcity of Celtic ideas we labor under," a poet might "borrow from the Edda, 'dropping, however, all mention of Woden and his Valkyrian virgins'" (006-018). The debate — Gothic versus Celtic, Scald versus Bard — shows the antiquarian enthusiasm outrunning exact knowledge, even as it supplied poetry with its powerful new matter (006-011, 006-018).
