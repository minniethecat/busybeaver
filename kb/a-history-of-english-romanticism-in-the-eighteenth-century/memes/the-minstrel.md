# The Minstrel

## Overview

*The Minstrel* is James Beattie's Spenserian poem, valuable to Beers less for its own merit than as a confluence of the century's romantic tendencies: it is "a poem once widely popular, in which several strands of romantic influence are seen twisted together" (008-087). Its full title, "'The Minstrel, or the Progress of Genius,'" declares its theme (008-087).

## Author & Date

The poem is the work of James Beattie, also author of a "'Dissertation on Fable and Romance'" (007-048). "The first book was published in 1771, the second in 1774, and the work was never completed" (008-087). Beattie belonged to the "mild clerical essayists" of the antiquarian revival (007-049), and his *Minstrel* was among the most popular poems of its moment.

## Summary / Content

"The design of 'The Minstrel' is to 'trace the progress of a Poetical Genius, born in a rude age,'" a youthful shepherd who "'lived in Gothic days'" (008-089). Beers notes the irony that "nothing less truly Gothic or medieval could easily be imagined than the actual process of this young poet's education" (008-089) — the Gothic frame being more atmosphere than substance.

## Form & Style

The poem is "in the Spenserian stanza" (008-087), and its manner is frankly derivative. "'The Minstrel,' like 'The Seasons,' abounds in insipid morality, the commonplaces of denunciation against luxury and ambition, and the praise of simplicity and innocence," and "The influence of Thomson is clearly perceptible in these stanzas" (008-096). Its picturesque set-pieces — the "long-robed minstrels" who "wake the warbling wire" — show the descriptive Thomsonian legacy (008-096).

## Significance to Romanticism

*The Minstrel*'s importance is exactly its composite character. In it, Beers writes, "several strands of romantic influence are seen twisted together": "It was in the Spenserian stanza, was tinged with the enthusiastic melancholy of the Wartons, followed the landscape manner of Thomson, had elegiac echoes of Gray, and was perhaps not unaffected, in its love of mountain scenery, by MacPherson" (008-087). It thus gathers into one poem the Spenserian, melancholy, landscape, elegiac, and Ossianic currents of the whole revival. See [[spenserianism]] and [[romanticism]].

## Reception & Controversy

The poem was "once widely popular" (008-087), but Beers's interest in it is historical rather than aesthetic. Its critical weakness he states plainly — it "abounds in insipid morality, the commonplaces of denunciation against luxury and ambition" (008-096) — so that its value lies in its usefulness as the poem "in which several strands of romantic influence are seen twisted together" (008-087).
