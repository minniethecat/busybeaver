# Romanticism

## Overview

Romanticism is the organizing idea of Beers's whole volume: as he employs the word, following Heine, it "means the reproduction in modern art or literature of the life and thought of the Middle Ages" (001-002).

Beers treats the term as inseparable from its opposite, [[classicism]] — *romantic* is "not an independent but a referential word," one that "implies its opposite, the classic" (001-003).

His narrative follows this romantic impulse as it swells from antiquarian curiosity into an imaginative love of the past, a current he frames as part of "the general European reaction against the spirit of the eighteenth century" (002-001).

The narrower medieval strand of that reaction — the vein running through [[medievalism]], [[spenserianism]], and [[the-gothic-revival]] — is the "incident" his book chiefly treats (002-001), while the definitional groundwork is laid in the opening chapter [[the-subject-defined]], where he cautions that a rigid definition would only anticipate the substance of the volume (001-001).

## Definition

Beers opens by warning that a rigid, dictionary-style definition of *romanticism* would only anticipate the substance of his whole volume; the word connotes so much of the history of the human mind that any short formula serves merely as "a convenient mark of identification" (001-001).

As a working definition he adopts Heine's: romanticism "means the reproduction in modern art or literature of the life and thought of the Middle Ages" (001-002). He stresses that *romantic* is not an independent but a referential word — it implies its opposite, the classic, so that to conceive the romantic one must also conceive the classic (001-003).

The etymology reinforces this: the vernacular *romans* gave its name to the medieval tale of chivalrous adventure, and the later adjective *romantic* was "marked from birth" with a shade of disapproval, becoming a synonym for the sentimental, wild, and extravagant (001-009).

> [Romanticism] means the reproduction in modern art or literature of the life and thought of the Middle Ages. (001-002)

## Origins & Development

The abstract noun *romanticism* was coined late — the movement "was not enough developed to call for a name until the opening of the nineteenth century," and in England it "was never so compact, conscious, and definite a movement" as in Germany and France, its baptism coming from abroad (001-010).

Beers frames the phenomenon historically as part of "the general European reaction against the spirit of the eighteenth century," which in England began early but "was at first gradual, timid, and unconscious," not reaching importance until the seventh decade and culminating only in the early nineteenth century (002-001).

The eighteenth-century critics had dismissed the medieval past with epithets of "Gothic ignorance, rudeness, and barbarism" (002-008); but gradually "the despised ages of monkery, feudalism, and superstition began to reassert their claims upon the imagination," as ruined castles, illuminated missals, black-letter ballads, and old tapestries "acquired a new value" (002-009).

Beers reads this recovery as a return of "warmth and color — that is, of emotion and imagination — into English life and thought," one phase of a wider revolt against "the coldness and spiritual deadness of the first half of the eighteenth century" (002-012).

> For it was, in part, a return of warmth and color into English letters. (002-012)

## Key Figures & Works

For Beers, romanticism "was something more, then, than a new literary mode; a taste cultivated by dilettante virtuosos, like Horace Walpole, college recluses like Gray, and antiquarian scholars like Joseph and Thomas Warton" (002-013).

The antiquarian temper mattered: Joseph Warton "ventured to uphold the then paradoxical thesis that Spenser was as great a man as Pope," while Thomas Warton executed the history of English poetry Gray had only designed (002-013).

Older poets furnished models — Beers names Spenser and Milton together as "platonizing poets" of the earlier tradition (001-044) — and he treats the influence of Milton as a distinct current feeding the romantic revival of the eighteenth century (005-001).

As modern exemplars of the romantic inspiration Beers points to Scott's *Ivanhoe* and, on the Continent, to Victor Hugo's *Notre Dame de Paris* (001-004), the medieval subject being the natural home of "unworked sources of romantic effect."

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Because "the romantic revival was a protest against this reigning mode," Beers argues, one must understand the classical Augustan age it opposed (002-014). The medieval revival was "only an incident — though a leading incident — of this movement," and it is precisely that side which his book chiefly treats (002-001).

In its deeper significance the revival was not merely aesthetic but "a reaching out of the human spirit after a more ideal type of religion and ethics" than the formal churchmanship of the age afforded (002-013), running parallel to the Methodist and Evangelical awakening and to the sentimentalism of Richardson and Sterne (002-012).

It is this continuity — antiquarian curiosity ripening into imaginative love of the past — that Beers traces through the Spenserians, the Miltonic group, the school of Warton, and the Gothic revival, until the movement "culminated only in the early years of the nineteenth century" (002-001).

## Contrasts & Debates

The romantic/classic antithesis is, for Beers, the axis of the whole subject. Pater is quoted to the effect that the romantic temper consists in "the addition of strangeness to beauty," curiosity joined to the desire of beauty (001-012), while Dr. Hedge locates the essence of romance in mystery and, elsewhere, declares that "the essence of romanticism is aspiration" (001-020).

Beers illustrates the confusion the terms bred through Musset's satire, in which two provincials pursue for years an ever-shifting definition of *romantisme* (001-033).

He closes by noting that romanticism "is a word which faces in two directions": once opposed to classicism, in modern times it stands opposed to realism, its idealism and mysticism contrasting with the realist's adherence to fact (001-040).

> It remains to add that romanticism is a word which faces in two directions. It is now opposed to realism, as it was once opposed to classicism. (001-040)

Much of the definitional chapter is devoted to sorting out the paired terms as critics have used them (001-011): where the classic looks to Greek and Roman antiquity, the romantic looks to the popular literature of the Middle Ages "as opposed to the classical antique" (001-009).

The eighteenth century itself Beers describes "in the main, as classical," marked above all by its "respect for authority" (002-029, 002-035). The following contrasts summarize the oppositions Beers and the critics he cites draw between the two tempers.

| Aspect | Classical | Romantic |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Model / era | The antique — Greek and Roman (001-020) | The Middle Ages and its romances (001-009) |
| Governing standard | Respect for rules, models, form (001-040) | "The addition of strangeness to beauty" (001-012) |
| Formal quality | Statuesque (001-025) | Picturesque (001-025) |
| Faculty appealed to | The subject through the feelings (002-060) | Romanticism through the imagination (002-060) |
| Eighteenth-century stance | Respect for authority (002-029) | A protest against the reigning mode (002-014) |
| Modern antithesis | Once opposed to romanticism (001-040) | Now faces realism (001-040) |
