# Thomas Chatterton

## Overview

Thomas Chatterton is, for Beers, the tragic figure at the centre of the whole story: "The history of English romanticism has its tragedy: the life and death of Thomas Chatterton — 'The marvelous boy, / The sleepless soul that perished in his pride'" (010-001). His invention of the fifteenth-century monk-poet Rowley is "most instructive as to the conditions which brought about the romantic revival," showing "by what process antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001).

## Life

Beers sets the story in Bristol — "old Saxon *Bricgestowe*, 'place of the bridge'" — where "Chatterton was born in 1752, the posthumous son of a dissipated schoolmaster, whose ancestors for a hundred and fifty years had been, in unbroken succession, sextons to the church of St. Mary Redcliffe" (010-002). Beers half-seriously attributes "to heredity the bent which Chatterton's genius took spontaneously and almost from infancy" (010-002). His precocity was extraordinary — his are "precocious verses" of the kind found "in youthful artists" (005-009) — and by around 1768 "many of the Rowley poems were in print" (006-059).

## Works & Contribution

Chatterton's work is the body of pseudo-medieval verse fathered on "Thomas Rowley, parish priest" of fifteenth-century Bristol (010-008), including "'The Bristowe Tragedie'" (010-015). The poems "were born of St. Mary's Church" — of his brooding on the antiquities of Redcliffe (010-002) — and were supported by forged documents on aged vellum (010-033). Beers's point is that the forgery was genuine poetry: through Chatterton "antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001). See [[the-rowley-poems]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Chatterton stands at the head of Beers's history because he embodies its central process. He shows "by what process antiquarianism became poetry" (010-001) — how the antiquarian fascination of the age crystallized into original imaginative work. His influence ran directly into the great romantics: "Coleridge, his verse derives from Chatterton; and the line of Gothic roman[ce]" traces back through him (006-001). His tragic death made him the very emblem of romantic genius unrecognized — "The marvelous boy, / The sleepless soul that perished in his pride" (010-001).

## Reception & Influence

Beers treats Chatterton's fate as archetypal — the young genius destroyed, whose story "has been often told" and demands retelling for its "dramatic interest" (010-001). The Rowley poems provoked a great authenticity controversy: Walpole "had begun to suspect imposture" (010-025), and "Thomas Warton had seen one of them, and pronounced it a clumsy forgery" (010-033), later reviewing them in his *History of English Poetry* (010-039). Yet his imaginative influence on Coleridge and the Gothic line (006-001) outlived the exposure of the fraud.
