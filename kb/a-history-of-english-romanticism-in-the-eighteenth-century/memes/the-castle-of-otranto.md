# The Castle of Otranto

## Overview

*The Castle of Otranto* is Horace Walpole's Gothic romance, the leading literary product of the same medievalizing taste that raised his villa at Strawberry Hill: "The most notable instance that we encounter of virtuosity invading the neighboring realm of literature is in the case of Strawberry Hill and 'The Castle of Otranto'" (007-014). Beers treats it as the fountainhead of a whole genre — the book "with which starts" the modern Gothic romance (006-001).

## Author & Date

Its author was "Horace Walpole, the son of the great prime minister, Robert Walpole ... a person of varied accomplishments and undoubted cleverness," at once "a man of fashion, a man of taste, and a man of letters" (007-014). Eastlake finds in all his work "the unmistakable evidence ... of his medieval predilections" (007-020). The romance belongs to the 1760s, appearing a few years before "Percy's 'Reliques' and 'The Castle of Otranto'" are grouped as landmarks of the decade (007-005), and by the 1770s it had, with the *Reliques*, "been out more than ten years" (006-059).

## Summary / Content

The tale is built from the apparatus of feudal romance. Eastlake catalogues its furniture: "The feudal tyrant, the venerable ecclesiastic, the forlorn but virtuous damsel, the castle itself with its moats and drawbridge," and the machinery of supernatural portents (007-020) — among them "the prodigious helmet in the Castle of Otranto, and the gigantic arm" (006-073). Walpole framed the book with an elaborate mystification: the preface "pretended that the book had been printed at Naples in black-letter in 1529, and was found in the library of an old Catholic family in the north of England" (007-033).

## Form & Style

Walpole described his method, in the preface to the second edition, as "an attempt to blend the two kinds of romance, the ancient and the modern" (007-033). He defended his mixing of registers — the "humorous dialogues among the servants of the castle" — by appeal to high precedent, declaring "he had taken nature and Shakspere for his models," and "fell foul of Voltaire for censuring the mixture of buffoonery and solemnity in Shakspere's tragedies" (007-033).

## Significance to Romanticism

For Beers the book is foundational to the Gothic strain of the romantic revival. Eastlake's verdict, which Beers endorses, is that "His 'Castle of Otranto' was perhaps the first modern work of fiction which depended for its interest on the incidents of a chivalrous age, and it thus became the prototype of that class of novel which was afterward imitated by Mrs. Radcliffe and perfected by Sir Walter Scott" (007-020). It is, in Beers's account, the point at which the antiquarian "virtuosity" of Strawberry Hill crossed over into imaginative literature (007-014), giving the medieval revival its first sustained work of prose fiction. See [[gothicism]] and [[the-gothic-revival]].

## Reception & Controversy

The romance's blend of "buffoonery and solemnity" drew the same objections that Walpole had anticipated in defending Shakespeare (007-033), and its fictitious black-letter provenance was part of a period fashion for antique disguises (007-033). Beers sets it beside Percy's *Reliques* as the twin landmark of the decade 1760–1770 (007-005, 006-059), and its influence — "imitated by Mrs. Radcliffe and perfected by Sir Walter Scott" — is Beers's measure of its enduring consequence (007-020).
