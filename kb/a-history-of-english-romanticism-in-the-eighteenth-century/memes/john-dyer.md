# John Dyer

## Overview

John Dyer is, for Beers, a painter-poet of the "return to nature," author of *Grongar Hill* and one "among the earliest Spenserians" (004-002). "Dyer was a landscape painter, as well as a poet" (004-002), and his best work brings a painter's eye to English scenery.

## Life

Beers gives Dyer's biography in brief, painterly strokes: he was "a young Welshman" (004-086), "a landscape painter who had been educated at Westminster school, studied under Richardson at London, and spent some time wandering about the mountains of Wales in the practice of his art" (004-087). He belongs, with Thomson and Shenstone, to the little group of "pioneers in the 'return to nature'" (004-002).

## Works & Contribution

Dyer's contribution is chiefly *Grongar Hill* (1726), "a pictorial poem, a sketch of the landscape seen from the top of his favorite summit in South Wales" (004-087), written "in the octosyllabic couplet of Milton's 'L'Allegro' and 'Il Penseroso'" (004-086). Beers finds it "a slight piece of work, careless and even slovenly in execution, but with an ease and lightness of touch that contrast pleasantly with Thomson's and Akenside's ponderosity" (004-087). His later blank-verse "'Fleece' (1757)" belongs to the didactic-Georgic vein (004-047). See [[grongar-hill]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Dyer anticipates the romantic love of mountains and wild scenery. "Wordsworth was attracted by Dyer's love of 'mountain turf' and 'spacious airy downs' and 'naked Snowdon's wide, aerial waste'" — "The 'power of hills' was on him" (004-089). "Like Wordsworth, too, he moralized his song," the "ruined tower" of *Grongar Hill* suggesting "the transience of human life" (004-089). As painter and Spenserian both, he joins two of the revival's currents. See [[landscape-poetry]] and [[the-picturesque]].

## Reception & Influence

Beers's estimate is affectionate but measured: *Grongar Hill* is "slight" and "slovenly," yet full of "ease and lightness of touch" (004-087). Its lasting interest is Wordsworthian — Dyer's feeling for "mountain turf" and the "power of hills" (004-089) marks him an early, genuine voice of the nature-poetry the revival would carry to its height.
