## Overview

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832) is the sovereign figure of German literature, present in nearly every chapter of Beers's book as a touchstone: his "Götz von Berlichingen" (1773) gave the *Sturm und Drang* its founding drama, his "Sorrows of Werther" spread sentimentalism across Europe, and his definition of the classic–romantic antithesis—as "healthy and morbid"—became one of the framing ideas of the whole debate. (001-018)

Beers treats him not as a subject of the English romantic revival but as its great German parallel and comparative measure, the figure against whom the English movement reveals its own character.

## Life

Beers does not narrate Goethe's life but notes its pivotal moments: his student encounter with Shakspere at Leipzig "through the detached passages given in 'Dodd's Beauties of Shakspere'"; his Shakespeare-feast at Frankfort in 1771, where "healths were drunk to the 'Will of all Wills'"; and the mature turn toward classicism after his *Italienische Reise*. (011-009, 011-011, 011-017)

In 1781–82 the young [[william-taylor|William Taylor of Norwich]] "call[ed] upon Goethe at Weimar, with a letter of introduction," and [[matthew-lewis|Lewis]] "went to Germany and took up his residence at Weimar, where he was introduced to Goethe" in 1792. (011-044, 011-062)

## Works & Contribution

Goethe's "Götz von Berlichingen" (1773) was one of "two plays [that] were remotely responsible" for the entire wave of German melodramas and romances that flooded English theaters and circulating libraries, the other being Schiller's "Die Räuber." (011-047)

The play, with "its robber knights, secret tribunal, imperialist troops, gipsies, the Bishop of Bamberg, vengeance, conflagration, and the domestic virtues," furnished the machinery that English Gothic would adopt wholesale. (011-047)

His "Sorrows of Werther" (1774) was a European sensation that "fell in with that current of feeling which Carlyle called 'Wertherism'"—the cult of sensibility that washed through Richardson, Rousseau, and the young Goethe alike. (009-047, 007-056)

His ballad art, especially "Der Fischer" and "Erl-King," was translated by Lewis for "Tales of Wonder," and Scott paraphrased a romance-fragment from Goethe's opera "Claudina von Villa Bella" as "Frederick and Alice." (011-078)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Goethe's role in Beers's story is comparative and definitional. His career enacted the classic–romantic oscillation that Beers traces through English poetry: "In his youth," Goethe was "altogether romantic," but after Italy there ensued "a reversion to the classic; not the exploded pseudo-classic of the eighteenth-century brand, but the true Hellenic spirit." (011-017)

Yet even in his classical phase, "Goethe and Schiller, even in the last years of the century, vied with one another in the composition of romantic ballads." (011-018)

Beers uses Goethe to frame his largest argument: that "the leaders of German poetry, Goethe and Schiller, both attained the summit of their art in the imitation of classical models"—a judgment that throws into relief the English romantic preference for medieval over classical antiquity. (011-018)

Goethe's comment that [[james-macpherson|Ossian]] had drawn him to "the Acroceraunian mountains of old name" confirms the European reach of the Celtic revival. (009-047)

## Reception & Influence

Goethe's influence on English romanticism was real but indirect. Scott found in "Götz" a model for medieval historical romance: "as Percy's 'Reliques' prompted the 'Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border,' so 'Götz' prompted the 'Lay of the Last Minstrel' and 'Marmion.'" (011-052)

Beers notes, however, that Scott ultimately "found better work for his hands to do than translating German ballads and melodramas," and that "the tide of German romance had begun to ebb before the close of the century." (011-082, 011-083)

Goethe's distinction between the classic ("healthy") and the romantic ("morbid"), reported by Beers, became one of the most quoted formulations of the antithesis, even as Beers shows that the English romanticists refused the dichotomy. (001-018)
