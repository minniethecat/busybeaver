## Overview

Gottfried August Bürger (1747–1794) was a German poet "who may be said to have been made by the English ballad literature, of which he was an ardent student." (008-084)

His ghastly ballad "Lenore" crossed the Channel in multiple competing English translations and set Walter Scott on his earliest literary path. Beers treats him as the incarnation of the German tributary that flowed back into English romanticism after Percy's "Reliques" had first crossed into Germany.

## Life

Beers gives no biography of Bürger beyond his death date. He was born in Molmerswende in 1747, studied at Göttingen, and became a central figure of the Göttinger Hain circle—particulars the book assumes rather than relates.

What mattered to Beers was his work: the ballad voice that rivalled Percy's. "Even Bürger had not Percy's fine sensibility," yet he had a rude energy that electrified English readers. (008-056)

## Works & Contribution

Bürger's "Lenore" (1773) "remains Bürger's masterpiece, and in its various English versions—Taylor's, Scott's, Beresford's, Spencer's—took manifold possession of England." (011-027)

The poem draws upon common ballad motives: the dead lover returns at midnight and carries off his bride on a spectral ride to the grave. "The story is managed, indeed, with much of that subtle art which Coleridge used in 'The Ancient Mariner' and 'Christabel.'" (011-027)

His second most popular ballad was "Des Pfarrer's Tochter von Taubenhain," printed in the *Monthly Magazine* for April 1796 under the title "The Lass of Fair Wone." (011-042)

With Goethe, Herder, and others, Bürger joined the *Sturm und Drang* enthusiasm for folk-song. He "wrote Minnesongs, in which [he] imitated the old German lyric poets" and hailed the Nibelungenlied as the German Iliad. (011-003)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Bürger's place in Beers's story is as the return conduit. Percy's "Reliques" "worked powerfully in Germany," received in Lessing's circle with "universal enthusiasm" and feeding Herder's "Stimmen der Völker." (008-084)

From that seed, Bürger's ballad art grew, and it was a translation of "Lenore" that, in the autumn of 1794, Miss Aikin read aloud at Dugald Stewart's. Scott was not present, but "his friend Mr. Cranstoun described the performance to him; and he was so much impressed by it" that he immediately set about his own translation. (011-025)

Thus the English ballad revival, exported to Germany, returned to England through Bürger and sparked Scott's first literary effort—a perfect instance of the "German tributary" that is the subject of Beers's final chapter.

## Reception & Influence

Scott's friends assured him his version was superior to Taylor's, and Taylor himself wrote generously: "The ghost nowhere makes his appearance so well as with you, or his exit so well as with Mr. Spencer." (011-028)

Yet "Lewis was right in preferring Taylor's version, which has a wildness and quaintness not found in Scott's more literal and more polished rendering." (011-029)

Scott later reprinted his "Wild Huntsman," from Bürger, in Lewis's "Tales of Wonder." (011-078)

Beyond Scott, Bürger's spectral balladry influenced the whole Gothic ballad tradition: Lewis's "Alonzo the Brave and the Fair Imogene" was "evidently suggested by 'Lenore.'" (011-072)

## References
