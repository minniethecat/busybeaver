## Overview

Thomas Tyrwhitt (1730–1786) was the Chaucerian editor and classical scholar whom Beers credits with two distinct services to the romantic revival: his editions made Chaucer and the medieval poets accessible, and his linguistic analysis definitively exposed [[chatterton|Chatterton's]] Rowley forgery.

Beers classes him with [[thomas-percy|Percy]] and [[joseph-ritson|Ritson]] among the scholars "who, as collectors and editors, rescued the fragments of ancient ministrelsy and gave the public access to concrete specimens of mediaeval poetry." (007-049)

## Life

Beers gives little biography of Tyrwhitt. He was educated at Eton and Oxford, became a fellow of Merton College, and served as Clerk of the House of Commons.

His scholarly career was defined by two great editorial projects: an edition of Chaucer's "Canterbury Tales" (1775–78, listed in Beers's bibliography as "Tyrwhitt's ed., Oxford, 1798, 2 vols., 2d ed.") and his 1777 edition of the Rowley poems, in which he appended his reasons for believing Chatterton their true author. (011-135, 010-035)

## Works & Contribution

Tyrwhitt's Chaucerian scholarship was foundational. He established the principles of Middle English editing at a time when the language of the fourteenth century was little understood, and his edition is the direct ancestor of all modern Chaucerian textual criticism.

His most dramatic intervention in literary history was the Rowley controversy. When Chatterton's pseudo-medieval poems were posthumously collected, it was Tyrwhitt who "first collected and published" them in 1777, appending "his reasons for believing that Chatterton was their real author, and Rowley a myth." (010-035)

Those "reasons are convincing to any modern scholar," Beers writes, and Tyrwhitt's opinion "was shared at the time by all competent authorities—Gray, Thomas Warton, Dr. Johnson, Goldsmith, Garrick, Sir Joshua Reynolds, Sheridan." (010-035)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Tyrwhitt's linguistic case against the Rowley poems was precise and devastating. He "pointed out that the Rowleian dialect was not English of the fifteenth century, nor of any century, but a grotesque jumble of archaic words of very different periods and dialects." (010-038)

He noted that "the orthography and grammatical forms were such as occurred in no old English poet known to the student of literature," and that Rowley's use of the possessive *itts*, instead of *his*, was "an error which constantly appears in Chatterton's acknowledged writings." (010-038)

Through this forensic demonstration, Tyrwhitt not only settled the Rowley controversy—"unlike the Ossian puzzle, which was a harder nut to crack, this Rowley controversy was really settled from the start"—but also modeled the scholarly method that would be applied to the Ossian question. (010-037)

His edition of Chaucer likewise served the romantic cause: by making genuine medieval poetry accessible, he helped the reading public distinguish between authentic antiquity and modern imposture.

## Reception & Influence

Tyrwhitt's edition of the Rowley poems remains foundational. Skeat, the great nineteenth-century Chaucerian, built on Tyrwhitt's conclusions, and later scholarship has only confirmed them.

Beers notes that Tyrwhitt's opinion was immediately accepted by "all competent authorities," though a few antiquaries—notably Jacob Bryant and Jeremiah Milles—persisted in defending Rowley's authenticity, "relying upon the imagination of Chatterton" for the whole case. (010-035, 010-037)

"Nothing is so extinct as an extinct controversy," Beers remarks, and Tyrwhitt's demonstration that the Rowleian dialect was "a grotesque jumble" closed the question within a generation. (010-037, 010-038)
