# Classicism

## Definition

For Beers, classicism is the necessary foil to romanticism: *romantic* is "not an independent, but a referential word. It implies its opposite, the classic," so that to conceive the one requires conceiving the other (001-003). The classical qualities he abstracts from Greek and Roman genius are "clearness, simplicity, restraint, unity of design, subordination of the part to the whole" — an impression of "noble plainness and severity, of harmony in construction, economy of means and clear, definite outline" (001-005). In its historical English sense the term names a period: "The classic, or pseudo-classic, period of English literature lasted from the middle of the seventeenth till the end of the eighteenth century" (002-013), the age of Queen Anne and the first two Georges being "our Augustan or classical age" (002-014). Following Pater, Beers identifies the classical temper with "the respect for authority, the love of order and decorum, the disposition to follow rules and models," predominating in "the times of Pope and Johnson in England" (001-012).

## Origins & Development

This English classicism was, Beers insists, "of a special type ... more Latin than Greek, and more French than Latin," a "classicism in red heels and a periwig" (002-015). Its immediate source was the French reform: "Malherbe and Boileau insisted upon the need of discarding tawdry ornaments of style and cultivating simplicity, clearness, propriety, decorum, moderation; above all, good sense," backed by the new Academy and the rules of Aristotle as read through Horace and Longinus (002-019). The prestige of the *siècle de Louis Quatorze* and the return of the exiled Stuart court in 1660 imposed this aesthetic code upon England for over a century (002-019). Beers reads the change of style as symptomatic of the century's mission — "to assert the universality of law and, at the same time, the sufficiency of the reason to discover the laws" — with a consequent "dislike of irregularity in art and irrationality in religion" (002-022).

## Key Figures & Works

The reigning spirit was academic: the age sought "to discover a formula of correctness in all the arts, to set up a tribunal of taste and establish canons of composition," with Horace, as interpreted by Boileau, for its favorite master (002-029). Pope's *Essay on Criticism* codified the creed — follow Nature, "observe the rules, which are only 'Nature methodized,'" and imitate the ancients (002-030). William Walsh had advised the youthful Pope that the one way left open to excel was "by correctness" (002-036), and "three writers of high authority in three successive generations — Dryden, Addison, and Johnson — consolidated a body of literary opinion" that made it "a writer's duty to be 'correct'" (002-035). The characteristic products were satire, burlesque, and travesty — the "Dunciad" and "The Rape of the Lock" (002-028). Matthew Arnold's verdict serves Beers as summary: the poetry of "Dryden, Pope, and all their school ... is conceived and composed in their wits," not "in the soul" (002-027).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Classicism matters to Beers chiefly as the thing romanticism reacted against: "the romantic revival was a protest against this reigning mode" (002-014). Queen Anne literature was classical precisely "in its lack of those elements of mystery and aspiration which ... [are] of the essence of romanticism" — "a literature of this world," of "perfect clearness, but ... a clearness without subtlety or depth" (002-024). It was unromantic too "in its self-restraint, its objectivity, and its lack of curiosity"; caring "little for the country or outward nature," it "disliked 'strangeness added to beauty' ... disapproved of anything original, exotic, tropical, bizarre" and of "mountains and Gothic architecture" (002-025). The revival was thus a reaction against this "Augustan formalism" (002-060). As Beers puts it, romanticism's "freedom and lawlessness, its love of novelty" contrast with "the classical respect for rules, models, formulae, precedents, conventions" (001-040). ## Contrasts & Debates

Critics disputed where the classic/romantic line fell. Sidney Colvin held the distinction to be "much less of subject than of treatment": in classical writing "every idea is called up to the mind as nakedly as possible ... exhibited in white light," the writer's temper "one of self-possession"; in romantic writing objects appear "through a colored and iridescent atmosphere" (001-031). De Stendhal made classicism stand "for conservatism, authority, imitation, the spirit of the past" (001-015). Beers finds the classical bias most visible in its diction, where "circumstances or periphrasis was the capital means by which the Augustan poet avoided precision and attained nobility of style," calling a woman "a 'nymph'" or fishes "the scaly tribe" (002-056).
