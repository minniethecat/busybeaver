# The Grave

## Overview

*The Grave* is Robert Blair's blank-verse meditation on death, one of the darkest works of the mid-century graveyard school. Beers sets it, with Young's *Night Thoughts*, at the sable extreme of the age's somberness — "the funereal sable to Young's 'Night Thoughts' (1742-44) and Blair's 'Grave' (1743)" (005-035).

## Author & Date

The poem, "'The Grave,' by Robert Blair" (005-074), was published in 1743 (005-035), the year before the completion of Young's *Night Thoughts*. Beers groups the two poets constantly as the twin voices of the mortuary verse of their decade (005-035, 005-056).

## Summary / Content

*The Grave* is a poem of the tomb, of night, decay, and the terrors of the churchyard. Beers quotes its characteristic manner — the wind that "'howls,'" the "'night's foul bird, / Rocked in the spire,'" the "'gloomy aisles, / Black-plastered and hung round with shreds of scutcheons / And tattered coats-of-arms,'" and "'the low vaults, / The mansions of the dead'" (005-039). It is imagery of ruin and mortality pressed to its gloomiest pitch.

## Form & Style

The poem is written in blank verse — "'The Seasons,' 'Night Thoughts' and 'The Grave' are written in blank verse" (004-003). Beers finds that "Blair's mortuary verse has a certain impressiveness, in its gloomy monotony, not unlike that of Quarles' 'Divine Emblems'" (005-039). Like the *Emblems*, "'The Grave' has been kept from oblivion" by that very concentrated gloom (005-039).

## Significance to Romanticism

*The Grave* carried the sentimental, death-haunted current of the revival to one of its extremes, giving the "poetry of the tomb" (005-056) its starkest images. With *Night Thoughts* it forms the "funereal sable" pole against which Beers measures "the delicate gray of the Wartons" (005-035) — the range of somber feeling out of which the mature elegiac poetry, crowned by Gray's *Elegy*, would come. See [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]] and [[blank-verse]].

## Reception & Controversy

Beers ranks *The Grave* below Gray's *Elegy* — "the masterpiece of this whole 'Il Penseroso' school" — placing it, with "'Night Thoughts,'" among poems "of the moral-didactic order" whose moral is less "suffused with emotion and expressed concretely" (005-056). Yet he grants its lasting "impressiveness, in its gloomy monotony" (005-039), and its survival where more polished verse has been forgotten.
