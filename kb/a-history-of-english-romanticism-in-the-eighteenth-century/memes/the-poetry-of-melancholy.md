# The Poetry of Melancholy

## Definition

The poetry of melancholy is the pensive, elegiac temper that Beers finds spreading through mid-century verse — a literary mood built on "the indulgence of the atrabilious humor" (005-033). For Beers it belongs to the deeper spirituality of the romantic mind: romantic literature is marked by "its sentimentality, its melancholy and, in particular, the morbid fascination which the thought of death has had for the Gothic mind," where "The classic nations concentrated their attention on life and light, and spent few thoughts upon darkness and the tomb" (001-029). No Greek, he adds, "could have conceived such a book as the 'Hydriotaphia' or the 'Anatomy of Melancholy'" (001-029).

## Origins & Development

The fountainhead was Milton: "The poem of Milton which made the deepest impression upon the new school of poets was 'Il Penseroso'" — "This little masterpiece, which sums up in imagery of 'Attic choice' the pleasures that Burton and Fletcher and many others had found in the indulgence of the atrabilious humor, fell in with a current of tendency" (005-033). That tendency was generational: "Pope had died in 1744, Swift in 1745, the last important survivors of the Queen Anne wits; and already the reaction against gayety had set in, in the deliberate and exaggerated solemnity which took possession of all departments of verse" (005-033). "That elegiac mood, that love of retirement and seclusion," which Beers had already remarked in Shenstone, "became now the dominant note in English" poetry (005-033). Its currency reached even "The Castle of Indolence," where a novel music "lulled the pensive melancholy mind" (005-075).

## Key Figures & Works

The mood ran through the whole Miltonic and Warton group. Joseph Warton praised in Spenser "a certain pleasing melancholy in his sentiments, the constant companion of an elegant taste," and, reviewing Pope's "Eloisa to Abelard," admired how "the effect and influence of Melancholy, who is beautifully personified ... cannot be too much applauded" — for "That temper of mind casts a gloom on all things" (005-073). Collins's ode addresses the melancholy muse itself: "O thou whom wandering Warton saw ... As by the pale moon's glimmering gleam / He mused his melancholy theme" (005-070). Yet Beers is skeptical of its sincerity: Dyer, Mason, and Joseph Warton "were beneficed clergymen," and "The melancholy which these good fellows affected was manifestly a mere literary fashion. They were sad 'only for wantonness'" — the cheerful, "even convivial" Wartons being no true melancholics at all (005-045).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

For Beers the poetry of melancholy is one of the sentimental tributaries of the romantic revival — the strand by which the "greater spirituality of romantic literature, its deeper emotion, its more passionate tenderness" found a native English idiom (001-029). It is the same "Gothic mind" that dwells on "darkness and the tomb" which will, in the graveyard poets and the elegists, feed the wider return of feeling against Augustan gayety (001-029). The mood's spread — from "Il Penseroso" through Shenstone's "love of retirement and seclusion" to its becoming "the dominant note" — traces the century's turn from classical light toward romantic shadow (005-033). ## Contrasts & Debates

Beers keeps the sincere and the fashionable melancholy in view at once. Against the genuine "temper of mind" that "casts a gloom on all things" (005-073) he sets the affected sort — the clergyman-poets "sad 'only for wantonness,'" a "mere literary fashion" (005-045). Gray, tartly refusing Nicholls's garden pleasures — "are you not ashamed of yourself? ... nor ever shall be either dirty or amused as long as I live" — figures the pose Beers distrusts, and "Gray never was" amused, "but the Wartons were easily amused" (005-045). The debate is thus whether the age's melancholy was a real deepening of feeling or "a mere literary fashion" — Beers granting it was, at least in the clergymen, largely the latter (005-045).
