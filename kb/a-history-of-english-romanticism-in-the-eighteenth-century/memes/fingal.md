# Fingal

## Overview

*Fingal* is the "ancient epic" that James Macpherson published as a translation from the Gaelic of the bard Ossian — the most ambitious and most contested of the Ossianic poems. The full title announces the claim: "'Fingal, an Ancient Epic Poem in Six Books: Together with several other poems, composed by Ossian the son of Fingal. Translated from the Gaelic language by James MacPherson'" (009-008). It stands at the centre of the whole controversy over literary forgery.

## Author & Date

*Fingal* was published in "London, 1762; together with 'Temora, an Ancient Epic Poem in Eight Books,' etc., etc., London, 1763" (009-008, 009-009). Macpherson "asserted that he had made his versions from Gaelic poems ascribed to Ossian or Oisin, the son of Fingal or Finn MacCumhail, a chief renowned in Irish and Scottish song and popular legend" (009-009).

## Summary / Content

The poem's world is the heroic Highlands of legend. "Fingal was the king of Morven, a district of the western Highlands, and head of the ancient warlike clan or race of the Feinne or Fenians"; "Tradition placed him in the third century," and "His son, Ossian, the warrior-bard, survived all his kindred," blind and old (009-009). Beers quotes its epic manner — the second "'Duan,' or book, of 'Fingal'" with its battle narrative, "'An arrow found his manly breast'" and the bard's lament for "the illustrious son of Fingal" (009-034, 009-048).

## Form & Style

Macpherson's original was a rhythmic, image-laden prose. Its distinctive "landscape features" became a mark of the whole Ossianic corpus, shared with "Temora" (009-023). Its manner was thought so essential that later hands tried to recast it: John Wodrow "attempted" several pieces "in heroic couplets, in 1769; and 'Fingal' in 1771," and "'Fingal' — which seems to have been the favorite — was again turned into heroic couplets by Ewen Cameron, in 1776" (009-055).

## Significance to Romanticism

Whatever its authenticity, *Fingal* gave the age a body of misty, primitive, Northern epic that Augustan poetry could not supply, and it belongs with the works that gave "an impulse to romantic literature" (002-002). Its melancholy, its wild landscape, and its bardic antiquity fed exactly the taste the revival was cultivating. Beers treats it as the great specimen of the era's characteristic mode of counterfeit antiquity — "the day of literary forgery; the Ossian controversy was raging" (010-015). See [[literary-forgery]] and [[ossian]].

## Reception & Controversy

*Fingal* provoked the fiercest of the authenticity debates. "The most damaging feature of MacPherson's case was his refusal or neglect to produce his originals" (009-028), and Johnson "not only disputed the antiquity of MacPherson's 'Ossian,' but he denied it any poetic merit," pronouncing "'I look upon MacPherson's Fingal to be as gross an imposition as ever the world was troubled with'" (009-015). Its defenders answered in kind: Wodrow "maintained that there was no reasonable doubt of the antiquity and authenticity," and Cameron prefaced his version "by the attestations of a number of Highland gentlemen to the genuineness of the originals," quoting Blair's claim that Ossian equalled "Homer and Vergil" (009-055).
