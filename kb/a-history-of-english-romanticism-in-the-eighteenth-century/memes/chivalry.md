# Chivalry

## Definition

Chivalry, in Beers's history, means the feudal-knightly manners and the romance-matter that grew from them — the imaginative world of "the era of Christian chivalry and feudalism, extending roughly from the eleventh century to the fifteenth" (002-002). Hurd defines its distinctiveness socially: "the Gothic manners of chivalry, as springing out of the feudal system, were as singular as that system" itself (007-006). Its recovery is bound up with a single book: Hurd's "'Letters on Chivalry and Romance' (1762) helped to initiate the romantic movement" (007-001).

## Origins & Development

The antiquarian study of chivalry was a mid-century enterprise with French roots. Hurd's *Letters* "perhaps owed their inspiration, in part, to Sainte Palaye's 'Mémoires sur l'ancienne Chevalerie,' the first volume of which was issued in 1759," a "monumental work" standing to its subject "much the same relation ... that Mallet's" history bore to Norse antiquity (007-001). The turn toward the Middle Ages was datable and gradual: "It was not until about 1760 that writers began to gravitate decidedly toward the Middle Ages," and only then did "the literature of the knight" suffer "resurrection" (006-001). The recovery embraced the whole feudal apparatus — "the forms of heraldry, architecture, chivalry, ecclesiology and feudal law" (006-001).

## Key Figures & Works

Hurd is the presiding figure. His argument is that romance-matter, not classical culture, gave the Renaissance poets their power: "the Renaissance poets, Ariosto and Spenser, owe their finest effects not to their tinge of classical culture but to their romantic materials," and "Shakspere 'is greater when he uses Gothic manners and machinery, than when he employs classical'" (007-007). Even Tasso's classicizing was a loss — his "imitations of the ancients" being "'faint and cold and almost insipid, when compared with his original fictions,'" those "'lies ... of Gothic invention'" (007-007). Behind the theorists lay the old texts: Chaucer had used "the mediaeval machinery of allegory and dreams" and carried "romantic love" into "Troilus and Cresseide" (002-006), while the "chivalry romances" themselves furnished the matter that Cervantes would burlesque (002-006).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Chivalry is one of the great imaginative reservoirs of the romantic revival. Beers dates the revival's decisive medieval turn to about 1760, when "the literature of the knight" was resurrected (006-001), and credits Hurd's *Letters* with helping "to initiate the romantic movement" (007-001). Hurd's claim that poetry's "finest effects" spring from "romantic materials" rather than "classical culture" (007-007) is, in the chivalric key, a charter for the revival: it authorized poets to draw on feudal manners, knightly romance, and "Gothic invention" (007-007). See [[gothicism]] for the aesthetic that framed it, [[medievalism]] for the wider antiquarian turn, and [[romanticism]] for the movement.

## Contrasts & Debates

The debate chivalry provoked was Hurd's contest of "the Gothic manners and fictions ... above the classic" for poetry (007-002), argued specifically on the ground of romance: whether Ariosto, Spenser, and Shakespeare were the greater for their "romantic materials" or the worse for their "barbarities" (007-007). Beers notes the countervailing realist strain — Chaucer's "'good sense'" and "insistent realism" in the "Canterbury Tales" (002-006) — and the fact that the chivalric romances had long been objects of "burlesque" (002-006). The recovery of chivalry thus meant reversing a long verdict, dignifying as poetry what the classical age had treated as "caprice and absurdity" (007-002).
