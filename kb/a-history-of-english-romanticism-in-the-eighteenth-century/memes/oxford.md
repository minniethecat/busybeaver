## Overview

Oxford is the academic home of the revival's scholar-poets. It was the city of [[thomas-warton]] — who filled the very chair of Professor of Poetry that his father had held (006-022) — and its libraries made it the base from which the [[the-school-of-warton|Warton school]] mined the old English and Gothic past.

More than a backdrop, Oxford supplied the antiquarian materials and the temper that the revival needed.

Thomas Warton "was always identified with Oxford, where he resided for forty-seven years" (006-023), and it was out of that long residence in college cloisters and libraries that his [[the-history-of-english-poetry|History of English Poetry]] grew — the project [[thomas-gray]] had schemed and then "relinquished the design to Warton" (006-045).

In Beers's account Oxford is where antiquarian enthusiasm was disciplined into scholarship, and so where the study-side of [[romanticism]] took shape.

## Setting & history

The Wartons were an Oxford family by inheritance and habit. Their father, "vicar of Basingstoke," "had been a fellow of Magdalen and Professor of Poetry at Oxford, which latter position was afterward filled by the younger of his two sons" (006-022).

Thomas Warton was "the life of a common room at Oxford," and both brothers were "eager students of old English poetry, Gothic architecture, and British antiquities" (006-025).

The city's collections were his workshop: "the Bodleian Library was one of his haunts," and he pored over vellum manuscripts "in the Ashmolean Museum at Oxford... written on vellum, with illuminations" (006-040). Joseph Warton, too, "then an Oxford undergraduate," wrote his early blank verse there (005-009).

Warton's whole career was bound to the university. He was educated with his brother at Oxford (006-023), and in later life:

> "Thomas was always identified with Oxford, where he resided for forty-seven years. He was appointed, in 1785, Camden Professor of History in the university, but gave no lectures." (006-023)

The colleges themselves were part of his romance of place. His days were spent among the libraries and gateways of the town:

> "The Bodleian Library was one of his haunts, and he was frequently seen 'surveying with quiet and rapt earnestness the ancient gateway of Magdalen College.' He delighted in illuminated manuscripts and black-letter folios." (006-040)

Even his indolence was collegiate: he "waxed fat and indolent in college cloisters, until Johnson compared him to a turkey cock" (006-024).

Oxford was, besides, a place where the old and the new taste jostled — Sir Joshua Reynolds "had supplied a painted window of classical design for New College, Oxford," and Warton answered with complimentary verses confessing those "portraitures of Attic art" had won him back to the true taste (006-037).

## Cultural significance

Oxford's importance is that it joined enthusiasm to scholarship. The Wartons brought to the revival "fine critical taste, and elegant scholarship" and an "odd passion" for antiquity — Thomas "a believer in ghosts" who "used secretly to attend hangings" — yet their learning in Spenser, Milton, and medieval verse gave the movement its scholarly ballast (006-025).

It was in the Bodleian and Ashmolean that antiquarian romance became a discipline, feeding Warton's "History of English Poetry" and the taste for [[spenserianism|Spenserian]] and [[medievalism|medieval]] matter. Oxford is thus the study-side counterpart to the ruins and gardens elsewhere in this book.

The line from the university ran outward into the wider revival. Warton's Oxford learning issued in his "History of English Poetry from the Twelfth to the Close of the Sixteenth Century," published in three volumes in 1774, 1777, and 1781 (006-044) — the very design [[thomas-gray]] had begun and handed on to him (006-045).

Even the university's official chairs carried the new interests: Robert Lowth, Bishop of Oxford and Professor of Poetry, delivered there in 1753 the Hebrew-poetry lectures that fed the period's fresh sense of primitive and original genius (011-020).

Against this Oxford of scholars stands the town's own memory of self-taught tastes as well — for even as an undergraduate Shenstone "had signalized his natural tastes by wearing his own hair instead of the wig" then universally the fashion (004-061).
