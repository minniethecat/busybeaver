# Naturalism

## Definition

Naturalism, in the sense Beers borrows from Leslie Stephen, is one of "the three currents of tendency known as sentimentalism, [[romanticism|romanticism]], and naturalism" that ran together beneath eighteenth-century letters. (002-013)

Its animating impulse is the "return to nature" — "a sentiment which underlies . . . both the sentimental and romantic movements." (002-013)

Beers distinguishes it from romanticism by its method. Where the romanticist widens "the intellectual horizon" across "a greater number of ages and countries," the naturalist tries "to fall back upon the thoughts and emotions common to all races," "basing our philosophy on deeper principles." (002-013)

So defined, naturalism names less a school than a direction — the turn from "cramping conventions" back toward the "liberty of nature." (002-016)

## Origins & Development

Beers roots naturalism in reaction against the urban temper of Augustan verse, a literature that "cared little for the country or outward nature, and nothing for the life of remote times and places." (002-025)

The change showed first as a fresh attention to scenery. Beers grants Professor McClintock's rule that "the beginning and presence of a creative, romantic movement is almost always shown by the love, study, and interpretation of physical nature." (004-001)

Its earliest carriers were the descriptive poets: Beers counts [[james-thomson|Thomson]], [[william-shenstone|Shenstone]], Akenside, and [[john-dyer|Dyer]] as "pioneers in the 'return to nature'" whose work "represents the transition." (004-001) (004-002)

Yet the naturalism of these pioneers was still mild; of "wildness, grandeur, solitude," Beers writes, "there was as yet comparatively little even in [their] verse." (004-001)

## Key Figures & Works

The pioneers were practical lovers of the ground as well as poets of it: Dyer "was a landscape painter, as well as a poet," and Shenstone "was one of the best of landscape gardeners." (004-002)

Their [[landscape-poetry|nature poetry]] — Thomson's [[the-seasons|The Seasons]], Dyer's [[grongar-hill|Grongar Hill]] — Beers treats as the seed-bed of the movement, though he cautions that "it is the beginnings that are important." (004-002)

He is careful to exclude later nature-poets from the romantic line: neither Cowper nor Crabbe "was romantic in any sense," nor "even . . . Wordsworth, the spirit of whose art, as a whole, was far from romantic." (004-002)

When at last "the romantic spirit took possession of the poetry of nature," Beers writes, "it manifested itself in a passion for wildness, grandeur, solitude." (004-001)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers makes naturalism and romanticism twin growths of one stock. Ruskin and Leslie Stephen are cited "as witnesses to the fact that naturalism and romanticism had a common root: the desire . . . to escape into the fresh air and into freer conditions, from a literature which dealt . . . with the indoor life of a highly artificial society." (004-001)

The common element, Beers adds from Ruskin, was "a desire to escape from the Augustan formalism." (002-060)

Ruskin's own image supplies the motto: "men steal out to the fields and mountains; and, finding among these color and liberty and variety and power, rejoice in all the wildest shattering of the mountain side, as an opposition to Gower Street." (002-061)

In dress no less than verse the age read the sign: the open Byronic collar "seemed to assist the liberty of nature against cramping conventions." (002-016)

## Contrasts & Debates

Beers keeps naturalism and romanticism distinct even while pairing them: the one seeks truth by "increasing our knowledge," the other by "basing our philosophy on deeper principles." (002-013)

He also sets the English case against the German. In England "romanticism was but one among many contemporary tendencies: sentimentalism, naturalism, realism," whereas in Germany the movement was "simply an incident of the _sturm- und Drangperiode_." (011-016)
