# Night Thoughts

## Overview

*Night Thoughts* is Edward Young's long blank-verse meditation on death, mortality, and immortality — the darkest note of the mid-century graveyard mood. Beers places it at the extreme of the age's somberness: "There were various shades of somberness, from the delicate gray of the Wartons to the funereal sable to Young's 'Night Thoughts' (1742-44) and Blair's 'Grave' (1743)" (005-035).

## Author & Date

The poem, formally "'The Complaint; or, Night Thoughts,'" is the work of Edward Young (011-294, 011-020), published across 1742–44 (005-035). Beers records Young's larger place in the age's self-understanding: he was also "the author of ... 'Conjectures on Original'" composition (011-020), a document of the era's turn toward originality against imitation.

## Summary / Content

*Night Thoughts* belongs, with the *Elegy* and *The Grave*, to "the poetry of the tomb" (005-056) — the mid-century verse of night, death, and the churchyard. Beers groups it among the blank-verse meditations that gave "the funereal sable" its fullest expression (005-035), a poem sustained on the great commonplaces "of the moral-didactic order" (005-056).

## Form & Style

The poem is written in blank verse — "'The Seasons,' 'Night Thoughts' and 'The Grave' are written in blank verse" (004-003), among the "new type" of poems that laid aside the heroic couplet. Beers classes it, with Pope's "Essay on Man" and Blair's "Grave," as "a poem of the moral-didactic order" (005-056), though without the concrete emotional suffusion he praises in Gray's *Elegy*.

## Significance to Romanticism

*Night Thoughts* carried the sentimental, death-haunted strain of the revival to its darkest pitch and its widest audience. Its European reach was large: Beers notes "the translations of Young's 'Night Thoughts'" abroad (011-087), part of the poem's role as a channel of English melancholy into Continental literature. Goss even called Young "a 'connecting link between this group of poets and their predecessors of the Augustan age'" (005-035). See [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]] and [[blank-verse]].

## Reception & Controversy

Beers's estimate is comparative and qualified: he ranks the *Elegy* as "the masterpiece of this whole 'Il Penseroso' school," and measures *Night Thoughts* against it as a poem "of the moral-didactic order" whose moral is less "suffused with emotion and expressed concretely" (005-056). Yet its contemporary and Continental fame was immense, its translations spreading the graveyard mood across Europe (011-087).
