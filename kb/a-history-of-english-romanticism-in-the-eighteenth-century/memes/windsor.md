## Overview

Windsor — the royal forest and castle on the Thames — occupies a double place in Beers's argument. As the subject of Pope's "Windsor Forest" it stands for the old, conventional treatment of nature that the revival reacted against; as the site of a boyhood visit that fired [[thomas-warton]]'s imagination, it also marks a first stirring of the new antiquarian feeling.

## Description & Location

Pope's "Windsor Forest" is Beers's chief example of Augustan landscape as mere ornament. In it, "in Pope's 'Pastorals' and 'Windsor Forest,' the imagery, when not actually false, is vague and conventional, and the language abounds in classical insipidities" — "blushing Flora," "gay gilded scenes," "balmy zephyrs" (002-053). Thomas Warton later pressed the same charge: Pope's "'Windsor Forest'" has "no local color," whereas "the scenes of Thomson are frequently as wild and romantic as those of Salvator Rosa" (006-066). Against this, the real fabric of Windsor Castle told on a susceptible boy: "Warton's biographers track his passion for antiquity to the impression left upon his mind by a visit to Windsor Castle, when he was a boy" (006-040).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

Windsor thus figures the very hinge of the change Beers traces. The same royal precinct that Pope had smoothed into "generalities at second hand" (002-053) became, for the next generation, a doorway into the medieval past — for it was among such vaulted domes and "wreathëd windows... with hues romantic tinged" that Warton learned to love "the vaulted dome" of Gothic art (006-040). The place measures the distance between [[the-augustans|Augustan]] convention and the antiquarian romance of [[the-school-of-warton|the Warton school]]; Warton's own application of the word "romantic" to medieval glass-staining is, Beers notes, "significant" (006-040). Even Wordsworth, looking back, could allow only "a passage or two in the 'Windsor Forest' of Pope" as containing "a single new image of external nature" in the long interval "between the publication of the 'Paradise Lost' and the 'Seasons'" (002-068).
