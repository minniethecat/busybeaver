# The Elegy

## Definition

As Beers treats it, the elegy is less a fixed metrical scheme than a mood and a species of poem. Johnson's dictum, quoted with approval, fixes its temper: "the character of the elegy is gentleness and tenuity" (004-071).

Its matter is the pensive, elegiac strain of retirement and meditation, "a note of the coming romanticism" that Beers hears already stirring at mid-century (004-069). By the years 1740–60 "that elegiac mood, that love of retirement and seclusion" had "became now the dominant note in English poetry," so that the verse of the period was "largely the literature of low spirits" (005-033).

## Origins & Development

Beers roots the eighteenth-century elegy in the vogue for the ten-syllabled quatrain. [[william-shenstone|Shenstone]]'s "Elegies" antedate [[thomas-gray|Gray]]'s poem and supplied its stanza, which Shenstone had himself borrowed from the love elegies of James Hammond (004-071).

Johnson wondered why Hammond and others "have thought the quatrain of ten syllables elegiac," a measure Dryden had pronounced "the most magnificent of all the measures which our language affords" (004-071). Through Dodsley's collection the fashion spread as "elegiac quatrains on the model of Shenstone and Gray" (005-032). It was Gray's poem, Beers notes, whose "universal currency" finally "associated it for many years almost exclusively with elegiac poetry" (004-098).

## Key Figures & Works

Beers ranks Gray at the summit of the form: it was "in Gray's 'Elegy Written in a Country Churchyard' (1751), that this elegiac feeling found its most perfect expression" (005-037). Gray's popular fame, he adds, "rests, and will always rest, upon his immortal 'Elegy'" (005-053).

The wider school Beers names as "the descriptive and elegiac poets, Thomson, Akenside, Dyer, and Shenstone," linking [[james-thomson|Thomson]], [[john-dyer|Dyer]] and Shenstone to the strain (005-046). He sets [[william-collins|Collins]] beside Gray among the Miltonic lyrists whose work "marks an advance" upon theirs (005-046). The pensive strain, he observes, runs through "Gray, Collins, and the Wartons" (004-069).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

The elegiac vein Beers reads as the muse's "withdrawal from the world's high places into the cool sequestered vale of life" (004-069). Gray's success made the churchyard elegy a school: his poem served "to consecrate the ten-syllabled quatrain to elegiac uses," and Mason re-titled his "Isis" once Gray's stanza "has generally obtained" (005-057).

"It became almost de rigueur for a young poet to try his hand at a churchyard piece," Beers writes, citing Cumberland's undergraduate imitation and the renewed vogue for Parnell's "Night Piece on Death" (005-057). From this generation the later poets inherited "their elegiac refinement, and their unimpassioned" temper (011-049).

## Contrasts & Debates

Beers records the standing quarrel over whether the "Elegy" or Gray's Pindaric odes were the higher work. Lowell and Arnold "pronounced Gray's odes higher poetry than his 'Elegy,'" Lowell adding that "'The Progress of Poesy'... overflies all other English lyrics like an eagle" (005-053).

Against them Beers "venture[s] to think that the popular instinct on this point is right," siding with the "Elegy" and even with Johnson, who allowed it "a happy selection of images" while dismissing the odes as "forced plants, raised in a hot-house" (005-053). The elegy is thus set apart from [[the-ode|the ode]] as the plainer, more universally felt form.
