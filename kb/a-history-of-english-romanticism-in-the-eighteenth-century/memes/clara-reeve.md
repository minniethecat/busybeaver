## Overview

Clara Reeve (1725–1803) was an English novelist and critic whose "Champion of Virtue" (1777), later retitled "The Old English Baron," was one of the first and most successful imitations of [[horace-walpole|Walpole's]] "[[the-castle-of-otranto|Castle of Otranto]]." Her "Progress of Romance" (1785) was an early attempt at a systematic theory of prose fiction. (007-041)

## Life

Clara Reeve was the daughter of a clergyman. She lived and died at Ipswich (1725–1803), leading the quiet provincial life common to many of the women writers who sustained the Gothic revival.

(007-051) Her first publication was "The Phoenix" (1772), a translation of "Argenis," a Latin romance by John Barclay. (007-041) [[walter-scott|Walter Scott]] later contributed a memoir of her to "Ballantyne's Novelists' Library," acknowledging her place in the genealogy of the Gothic novel. (007-051)

## Works & Contribution

Reeve's major fictional work appeared in 1777 as "The Champion of Virtue," styled on its title-page "A Gothic Story." Reprinted the following year as "The Old English Baron," it went on to pass through at least thirteen editions.

(007-041) Like Walpole, she employed the device of a discovered manuscript, professing to have transcribed the story from an original in the Old English language—a convention already becoming "somewhat threadbare" by the 1770s. (007-042)

Where Walpole had embraced the full supernatural apparatus of giant helmets and bleeding statues, Reeve undertook to admit "only a rather mild dose of the marvelous."

(007-042) She criticized Walpole's excesses, and Walpole, "evidently nettled by her strictures," described "The Old English Baron"

dismissively. (007-051) Scott, in his memoir, defended Walpole's frank use of the supernatural against Reeve's more cautious approach, giving the preference to Walpole's method.

(007-051) Yet Beers notes that despite its subtitle, Reeve's fiction is "much less 'Gothic' than its model"—its marvels restrained, its tone closer to domestic realism than to the lurid atmosphere of Otranto. (007-042)

"The Progress of Romance" (1785), issued in two volumes, was Reeve's critical contribution to the debate over the nature of prose fiction.

She gave a list, with conjectural dates, of medieval romances in French and English, but devoted the greater part of the book to contemporary fiction—the novels of Richardson, Fielding, Smollett, Crébillon, Marivaux, and Rousseau.

(007-047) She commended Thomas Leland's "Longsword, Earl of Salisbury" (1762) as "a romance in reality, and not a novel:—a story like those of the Middle Ages, composed of chivalry, love, and religion." (007-047)

Reeve's critical pages quote frequently from [[thomas-percy|Percy's]] "Essay on the Ancient Minstrels," mention [[james-macpherson|Ossian]] and [[chatterton|Chatterton]], and refer to [[richard-hurd|Hurd]], the [[thomas-warton|Wartons]], and other authorities of the romantic revival.

(007-048) She acknowledged that she had not read [[james-beattie|Beattie's]] "Dissertation on Fable and Romance" or Warton's "History of English Poetry" until after completing her own design. (007-048)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Reeve occupies a distinctive position in Beers's genealogy of the Gothic. Where Walpole provided the primal scene—Otranto—and [[anne-radcliffe|Mrs. Radcliffe]] perfected its machinery, Reeve represents a more conservative, rationalizing impulse within the Gothic tradition.

Her insistence on restraining the supernatural anticipated Radcliffe's "explained supernatural," and her critical writings helped establish a vocabulary for discussing the difference between "romance" and "novel."

[[thomas-chatterton|Chatterton's]] medieval fabrications belong to the same current of antiquarian fiction as Reeve's more modest productions. Beers notes that Chatterton's imagined Middle Ages were a "poor, faint simulacrum, compared with Scott's"—and the same could be said of Reeve's domesticated Gothic—but both contributed to the gradual construction of a usable medieval past. (010-013)

In the retrospective survey at the gate of the new century, Beers groups Reeve among those who never formed a coherent movement but whose individual efforts, taken together, prepared the way for the full Romantic achievement. (011-090)

## Reception & Influence

"The Old English Baron" proved remarkably durable, passing through thirteen editions and remaining in print into the 1880s.

(007-041) Scott's memoir testifies to her recognized place in the tradition, even as it registers the critical preference for Walpole's bolder supernaturalism.

(007-051) By the close of the nineteenth century, Reeve had settled into her role as a minor but instructive figure in the prehistory of the English novel—a writer who helped bridge the gap between the medieval romance and the modern psychological fiction that would succeed it.

## References
