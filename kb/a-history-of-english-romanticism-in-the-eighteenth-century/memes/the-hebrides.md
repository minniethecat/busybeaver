## Overview

The Hebrides — the "Western Islands" off the Scottish coast — recur through Beers's book less as a described geography than as an image of the wild, storm-beaten North that fired the romantic imagination. Beers traces the whole cluster of allusions to a single source: "Here too, as in the phrase about 'the stormy Hebrides,' 'Lycidas' seems to have furnished the spark that kindled the imaginations of the poets" (006-007). Through Milton the islands became a standing emblem of [[the-sublime]] and of the Celtic antiquity that "shared with Gothic in this newly aroused interest" (006-007).

## Description & Location

The islands appear as "the naked, melancholy isles / Of farthest Thule," where "the Atlantic surge / Pours in among the stormy Hebrides" — the description [[james-thomson]] gives in "The Seasons" (004-023). Their remotest reaches carry the graves of "the Scottish, Irish, and Norwegian kings in Icolmkill" and the ruined chapel of St. Flannan, "round the moist marge of each cold Hebrid isle," in the stanza Beers singles out from Collins's ode (005-048). It was this same tract that Johnson and Boswell crossed "in their tour to the Hebrides or Western Islands" (003-046).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

For the revival the Hebrides were charged ground. Thomas Warton, arguing for "the reintroduction of the preternatural into poetry," quotes "the famous stanza about the Hebrides in 'The Castle of Indolence'" as evidence that "these _Gothic_ charms are in truth more striking to the imagination than the classical" (006-073). [[william-collins]], in his ode on the popular superstitions of the Highlands, sends the muse "round the moist marge of each cold Hebrid isle" among a "pygmy folk" and second-sighted seers (005-048). And the same northern seascape is the country of [[ossian]], whose desolate halls and "land of strangers" belong to the same imaginative geography (009-013). The islands thus knit together the Miltonic, the Celtic, and the Gothic strands of the movement.
