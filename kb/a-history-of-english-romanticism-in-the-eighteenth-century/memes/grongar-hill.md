# Grongar Hill

## Overview

*Grongar Hill* is John Dyer's short landscape poem, published in the same year as Thomson's *Winter* and belonging to the same dawning taste for external nature. Beers calls it "a pictorial poem, a sketch of the landscape seen from the top of his favorite summit in South Wales" (004-087). It stands among the earliest of the "little descriptive pieces" by which the poetry of nature began (004-086).

## Author & Date

The poem is John Dyer's, "a young Welshman," and was published in 1726 "in two poetical miscellanies," paired with "The Country Walk" (004-086). Dyer "was a landscape painter who had been educated at Westminster school, studied under Richardson at London, and spent some time wandering about the mountains of Wales in the practice of his art" (004-087) — a painter's eye behind the poet's pen.

## Summary / Content

*Grongar Hill* is the sketch of a prospect: the view "from the top of his favorite summit in South Wales" (004-087). Dyer moralizes the scene in the manner of the age — "In 'Grongar Hill,' the ruined tower suggests the transience of human life: the rivers running down to the sea are likened to man's career from birth to death" (004-089). Campbell's famous couplet, "'Tis distance lends enchantment to the view / And robes the mountain in its azure hue," "is thought to owe something to Dyer's" lines (004-089).

## Form & Style

The poem was written "in the octosyllabic couplet of Milton's 'L'Allegro' and 'Il Penseroso'" — though "as first printed" it "was a sort of irregular ode with alternate rhyming," later "rewritten throughout in couplets" (004-086). Beers finds it "a slight piece of work, careless and even slovenly in execution, but with an ease and lightness of touch that contrast pleasantly with Thomson's and Akenside's ponderosity" (004-087).

## Significance to Romanticism

*Grongar Hill* anticipates the romantic love of mountains and wild scenery. "Wordsworth was attracted by Dyer's love of 'mountain turf' and 'spacious airy downs' and 'naked Snowdon's wide, aerial waste,'" for "The 'power of hills' was on him," and "Like Wordsworth, too, he moralized his song" (004-089). Its "moldering Gothic tower" and its glances at "solitary Stonehenge" (004-092) mark the mingling of landscape and antiquarian feeling that the revival would develop. See [[landscape-poetry]] and [[the-picturesque]].

## Reception & Controversy

Beers's estimate balances the poem's carelessness against its charm — "slight" and "slovenly," yet full of "ease and lightness of touch" (004-087). Its lasting interest is that "Wordsworth was attracted by Dyer's love of 'mountain turf' and 'spacious airy downs' and 'naked Snowdon's wide, aerial waste'" — the "power of hills" that "was on him" too (004-089).
