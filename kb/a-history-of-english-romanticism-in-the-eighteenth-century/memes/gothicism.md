# Gothicism

## Overview

Gothicism is Beers's term for the eighteenth-century return of taste toward "the Gothic" — the medieval manners, architecture, and romance that the [[the-augustans|Augustan]] age had lumped under "the epithets of Gothic ignorance, rudeness, and barbarism" (002-008).

For Beers the contrast is architectural at its root: on his reading "a Gothic cathedral expresses aspiration, and a Greek temple satisfied completeness" (001-020), and it is on that side of aspiration that the [[romanticism|romantic]] temper falls.

The taste ran through [[medievalism|medieval]] antiquarianism, the recovered fashion of [[chivalry]], and the architectural play of [[strawberry-hill|Strawberry Hill]], and it culminated in the [[the-gothic-revival|Gothic revival]] and its first novel, [[the-castle-of-otranto|"The Castle of Otranto"]], "styled on its title-page 'A Gothic Story'" (007-041).

> It has often been pointed out, _e.g._, that a Gothic cathedral expresses aspiration, and a Greek temple satisfied completeness. (001-020)

## Definition

Gothicism is Beers's name for the reviving taste for "the Gothic" — the medieval manners, architecture, and romance that Augustan classicism had scorned.

Its status is defined by its enemies: the classical temper "disliked 'strangeness added to beauty' ... disapproved of anything original, exotic, tropical, bizarre," and above all "disapproved of mountains and Gothic architecture" (002-025).

The whole polemical case for Gothicism was put by Bishop Hurd, whose *Letters on Chivalry and Romance* set out "to prove 'the pre-eminence of the Gothic manners and fictions, as adapted to the ends of poetry, above the classic'" (007-002).

## Historical development

Beers traces the revival across a sequence of stages, from Augustan contempt through the antiquarian recovery to Walpole's "Gothic Story" (002-008)(007-041).

### Origins & development

The recovery of the Gothic began as a defense of the great poets who had used it.

Hurd pointed out that "The greatest geniuses ... such as Ariosto and Tasso in Italy, and Spenser and Milton in England, were seduced by these barbarities of their forefathers; were even charmed by the Gothic romances," and asked "may there not be something in the Gothic Romance peculiarly suited to the view of a genius and to the ends of poetry?" (007-002).

He explained the Gothic's low repute historically: unlike the Greek "system of heroic manners," which "masterpieces of composition ... so fixed the credit of it," the Gothic had been "disgraced in their infancy by bad writers," so that "they could never be brought into vogue by the attempts of later poets" (007-006).

Its distinctive matter was social: "the Gothic manners of chivalry, as springing out of the feudal system, were as singular as that system" itself (007-006).

### Key figures & works

The rehabilitation of Gothic architecture is a leading index of the change.

Where the classical eye had recoiled from it (002-025), the antiquarian eye grew fond: critics came "to speak appreciatively of Gothic architecture, and of the scenery of the Alps" (005-057), and the students of the new taste were "students of old English poetry, Gothic architecture, and British antiquities" (006-025).

The Spenserians made the analogy explicit, comparing Spenser's poem to "an ancient, majestic piece of Gothic architecture ... compared with a neat, modern" building (003-012), and one critic apologized for the author by noting the likeness "between Spenser's work and Gothic architecture" (003-028).

Hurd's *Letters* supplied the theory (007-002); Thomas Warton, who "is known to have begun a work on Gothic architecture" (006-040), the antiquarian practice.

The taste became a physical fashion in Horace Walpole's toy castle at [[strawberry-hill|Strawberry Hill]], from which, Beers argues, the first Gothic novel directly sprang:

> Accordingly we find, on turning to "The Castle of Otranto," that, just as Walpole's Gothicism was an accidental "sport" from his general virtuosity; so his romanticism was a casual outgrowth of his architectural amusements. (007-032)

For Beers the villa was more stage-set than scholarship — "Strawberry Hill, with all its gimcracks, its pasteboard battlements and stained-paper carvings, was the lineal ancestor of the new law-courts" (007-028) — yet from that "accidental 'sport'" the [[the-castle-of-otranto|"Castle of Otranto"]], "A Gothic Story," was published in 1765 (007-041)(007-032).

## Relation to neighbouring concepts

### Role in the romantic revival

Gothicism is, for Beers, among the central channels of the romantic revival: it names the taste the revival restored against Augustan classicism, which had "disapproved of ... Gothic architecture" as of everything "exotic ... bizarre" (002-025).

Hurd's contention that "the Gothic manners and fictions" were, "as adapted to the ends of poetry, above the classic" (007-002) is in effect the revival's aesthetic manifesto in the medieval key.

By dignifying "the Gothic Romance" as "peculiarly suited to ... the ends of poetry" (007-002), Gothicism reopened for English verse the whole imaginative world of chivalry, feudal manners, and medieval architecture that the Enlightenment had closed. See [[medievalism]] for the broader antiquarian turn, [[chivalry]] for its social matter, and [[romanticism]] for the movement.

### Contrasts & debates

Gothicism is defined by its opposition to the classic, and Hurd frames the debate as a straight contest of value: whether "the Gothic manners and fictions" stand "above the classic" for poetry (007-002). The classical side had settled the question by taste, lumping Gothic architecture with mountains among the objects of Augustan distaste (002-025).

Hurd answers on aesthetic and historical grounds — the Gothic failed not in itself but in its unlucky "infancy," "disgraced ... by bad writers" (007-006) — while conceding that "the philosophic moderns" might have "gone too far" in dismissing it (007-002).

The Spenserian critics' architectural analogy — the "majestic piece of Gothic architecture" against the "neat, modern" building (003-012) — captures the whole quarrel of the picturesque-irregular against the correct-regular.
