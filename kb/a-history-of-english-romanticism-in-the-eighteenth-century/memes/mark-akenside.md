## Overview

Mark Akenside (1721–1770) was an English poet and physician whose didactic blank-verse poem "The Pleasures of Imagination" (1744) stood among the most ambitious productions of the mid-eighteenth century. He ranks among the earlier Spenserians by virtue of his youthful poem "The Virtuoso" (1737), and his odes helped carry the Miltonic mode into the 1740s and 1750s. (004-085)

## Life

Akenside was a north country man, born in Newcastle upon Tyne, and educated partly in Scotland. He studied medicine and became a physician of distinction, settling eventually at Northampton and later in London. (004-074)

His poem "The Pleasures of Imagination" appeared when he was only twenty-three, and its precocious ambition earned him immediate literary fame. (004-074) Samuel Johnson, who disliked Akenside's Whig politics, represented him at Northampton as "having deafened the place with clamors for liberty."

(004-076) Smollett caricatured him in "Peregrine Pickle" under the name of "the Doctor," portraying him as harsh, formal, and dogmatic. (004-075)

Akenside never married, sharing that distinction with several of his fellow poets—Thomson, Shenstone, Collins, Gray, and Thomas Warton. (005-044)

## Works & Contribution

Akenside's major work was "The Pleasures of Imagination," a didactic blank-verse poem in three books, later rewritten as "The Pleasures of the Imagination"—a revision that, in Beers's judgment, spoiled it.

(004-074) The title and something of the course of thought were taken from [[joseph-addison|Addison's]] celebrated series of papers on the same subject in the *Spectator* (Nos. 411–421). (004-074)

The poem belongs to a distinct brood of eighteenth-century didactic blank-verse productions begotten of Philips's "Cyder" and Thomson's "Seasons," alongside Mallet's "Excursion" (1728), Somerville's "Chase" (1734), Armstrong's "Art of Preserving Health" (1744), Dyer's "Fleece" (1757), and Grainger's "Sugar Cane" (1764). (004-047)

Akenside also ranks among the earlier Spenserians by virtue of "The Virtuoso" (1737), a Spenserian imitation that satirized the antiquarian spirit long before it had become a recognized fashion.

(007-012) A collection of his "Odes" appeared in 1745—the same year as Collins' and Joseph Warton's—and a second in 1760. (004-085) Beers judges them "of little value," but notes that they show "traces of Milton's minor poetry and that elegiac sentiment, common to the lyrical school of the mid-century." (004-085)

One of Akenside's best short pieces is his "Inscription for a Grotto," which Beers declares "not unworthy of Landor." (004-069)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers places Akenside within the landscape-poet tradition that descends from Thomson, noting that his verse was among the first to treat natural description as a serious poetic subject rather than decorative background. (004-001) But the assessment is mixed: Akenside's poetry is "too abstract," and "in place of images, he presents the reader with dissertations." (004-078)

Gray complained of the poem's obscurity and said it was published nine years too early, though he admitted that it occasionally rose "even to the best, particularly in description." (004-075) Johnson, who could not read it, nevertheless spoke of Akenside with "a certain cautious respect, which seems rather a concession to contemporary opinion than an appreciation of his real merits." (004-076)

The fundamental problem, in Beers's analysis, is that Akenside "might have chosen for his motto Milton's lines: 'How charming is divine philosophy!'" But he forgot "what Milton said about the duty of poetry to be simple, sensuous, and passionate." (004-080) Akenside's poetry is instead "obscure, metaphysical, and, as a consequence, frigid." (004-080)

Despite these limitations, Akenside's influence was widely felt. Thomas Warton's "Pleasures of Melancholy" (1747) was made up "in nearly equal parts, of Milton and Akenside." (005-026) Throughout the century, his name appears alongside Thomson's as a benchmark for descriptive and elegiac verse.

(005-046) Looking back from the threshold of the nineteenth century, Beers groups Akenside among the "forerunners of Scott, Coleridge, and Keats"—writers of a distinctly inferior order whose collective labor nevertheless prepared the ground for the Romantic achievement. (011-015)

## Reception & Influence

Akenside's poem enjoyed a popularity in its own day "now rather hard to account for," in Beers's phrase. (004-075) Johnson's grudging respect and Smollett's satire both testify to his prominence in the mid-century literary landscape.

Later criticism has largely confirmed Gray's verdict: that Akenside's ambition exceeded his poetic gift, and that his abstract, philosophical manner left his verse wanting in the sensuous particularity that gives the best Romantic poetry its life.

His "Pleasures of Imagination" influenced the structure and aspirations of Thomas Warton's early poetry and stood as a reference point for the Miltonic-Blank-Verse school throughout the second half of the century.

(005-026) His Spenserian experiments in "The Virtuoso" and his ten-lined Spenserian odes make him a transitional figure between the Augustan Spenserians and the full Romantic rediscovery of Elizabethan poetry. (004-085)
