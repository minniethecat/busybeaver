# Spenserianism

## Definition

Spenserianism is the eighteenth-century vogue for imitating Edmund Spenser — his stanza, his archaic diction, and his allegory. Beers records that "By the middle of the century Spenserian had become so much the fashion as to provoke a rebuke from Dr. Johnson, who prowled up and down before the temple of the British Muses like a sort of classical watch-dog" (003-053). Johnson's own *Rambler* conceded its momentum: "The imitation of Spenser ... by the influence of some men of learning and genius, seems likely to gain upon the age" (003-053). The movement is bound up above all with a verse form — "the strict Spenserian stanza" — whose revival Beers treats as an outward token of the change in taste (003-032).

## Origins & Development

The recovery of Spenser ran against the grain of a classicism that ranked Pope supreme; the "paradoxical thesis that Spenser was as great a man as Pope" (002-013) marks how far the revaluation had to go. The imitations multiplied until Johnson's mid-century rebuke, and his objections define the classical resistance: of West's imitations he wrote that "Such compositions are not to be reckoned among the great achievements of intellect, because their effect is local and temporary ... An imitation of Spenser is nothing to a reader, however acute, by whom Spenser has never been perused" (003-055). Yet even Johnson granted "that allegory is perhaps one of the most pleasing vehicles of instruction," so that "To imitate the fictions and sentiments of Spenser can incur no reproach" (003-053). The stanza's champions pressed its dignity — Philips urging "How much more stately and majestic in epic poems ... Spenser's stanza ... is above the way either of couplet or alternation of four verses only ... were it revived, would soon be acknowledged" (003-070).

## Key Figures & Works

The Spenserian stanza reappeared in poems grave and comic alike. Cambridge's "Archimage," "written in the strict Spenserian stanza, illustrates the frequent employment of this form in occasional pieces of a humorous intention," describing "a domestic boating party on the Thames" (003-032). More consequentially, Beers notes elsewhere that "'The Castle of Indolence' and 'The Schoolmistress'" were written "in Spenserian stanza," among the "new type" of poems that abandoned the heroic couplet (004-003). William West's imitations drew Johnson's censure (003-055), and the diction of "platonizing poets, like Spenser and Milton" supplied a model against Augustan smoothness (001-044). The movement thus gathered Thomson, Shenstone, and their fellows under the sign of the revived stanza (004-003).

## Role in the Romantic Revival

For Beers, Spenserianism matters less as pastiche than as an opening: "In the imitation of Spenser lay, at least, a future, a development; while the imitation of Pope was conducting steadily toward Darwin's 'Botanic Garden'" (003-057). The abandonment of the heroic couplet for the Spenserian stanza was "an outward sign of the revolution in poetic feeling," "The imitation of Spenser ... only one instance of a readiness to lay aside the heroic couplet in favor of other kinds" (004-003). By restoring an older, richer, more pictorial and allegorical manner, the Spenserian revival helped reopen the imaginative and medievalizing channels that the romantic revival would widen. See [[romanticism]] for the movement and [[medievalism]] for the antiquarian taste Spenser's archaism served.

## Contrasts & Debates

The great debate Spenserianism provoked was Johnson's: whether imitating Spenser was a "great achievement of intellect" or merely "local and temporary," appealing "not to reason or passion, but to memory," and lost on any reader "by whom Spenser has never been perused" (003-055). Beers weighs Johnson's stricture against the movement's fertility and finds for the imitators — "it was better to imitate Spenser than Pope" (003-057). The quarrel is thus a proxy for the larger contest between classical correctness and the reviving taste for allegory, archaism, and "stately and majestic" verse (003-070).
