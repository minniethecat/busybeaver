# Thomas Warton

## Overview

Thomas Warton is, for Beers, the scholar-poet who gave the medieval revival its learning. "The greatest service ... that Thomas Warton rendered to the studies that he loved was his 'History of English Poetry'" (006-044).

With his brother Joseph he belongs to the [[the-school-of-warton|"School of Warton"]] that carried the taste for old English poetry, Gothic architecture, and British antiquities into the mid-century.

Beers is emphatic about the pair's place in the story: "The true pioneers of the mediaeval revival were the Warton brothers" (006-020), and it was as a group that "'the school of Warton' was a term employed" by contemporaries (006-021).

His learning in old verse and his relish for the [[gothicism|Gothic]] made him a central figure in [[romanticism|the Romantic revival]] and in the wider [[the-gothic-revival|Gothic revival]] that Beers traces, standing beside [[thomas-gray]] and Percy's [[reliques-of-ancient-english-poetry|Reliques]] as a founder of the taste. See [[the-history-of-english-poetry]].

## Life

Joseph and Thomas Warton "were the sons of Thomas Warton, vicar of Basingstoke, who had been a fellow of Magdalen and Professor of Poetry at [[oxford|Oxford]]" (006-022); both brothers were themselves at Oxford, and "Joseph afterward became headmaster of Winchester" (006-023). Beers paints the younger Warton in vivid, humanizing strokes:

> He was the life of a common room at Oxford, romped with the schoolboys when he visited Dr. Warton at Winchester, and was said to have a hankering after pipes and ale and the broad mirth of the taproom. (006-025)

"Both Wartons had an odd passion for military parades; and Thomas — who was a believer in ghosts — used secretly to attend hangings" (006-025). The brothers were "remarkably harmonious in their tastes and intellectual pursuits, eager students of old English poetry, Gothic architecture, and British antiquities" (006-025).

As a poet of standing he was long attached to the court, turning out "his annual laureate odes, 'On his Majesty's Birthday'" (006-028).

## Works & Contribution

Warton's great work is the *History of English Poetry from the Twelfth to the Close of the Sixteenth Century*, published "in three volumes ... respectively in 1774, 1777, and 1781" (006-044):

> The greatest service ... that Thomas Warton rendered to the studies that he loved was his "History of English Poetry." (006-044)

Beside the *History* he was an active critic and editor of the older poets: his "Observations on the Faerie Queen" pursued an "illustrative method" of scholarship (006-061), and he later edited Milton's minor poems, the edition "published in 1785, by Thomas Warton, whose annotations have been" prized (005-006).

He also planned a study of Gothic architecture — he "is known to have begun a work on Gothic architecture" (006-040).

He wrote verse steeped in the medieval, "the most purely romantic of Thomas Warton's poems" being "'The Crusade' and 'The Grave of King Arthur'" (006-031), together with the "Ode Written at Vale-Royal Abbey" (006-041) and "the sonnets of Thomas Warton, nine in number and all Miltonic" (005-032).

See [[the-history-of-english-poetry]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Warton advanced the revival on the scholarly front. His *History* made the neglected medieval and Tudor past of English verse accessible; paired "with Percy's 'Reliques,' it had promoted, beyond any other work," the return to the Middle Ages (006-044). His own poetry helped naturalize the very word "romantic" in its medieval-architectural sense (006-040):

> Superstition, with capricious hand ... With hues romantic tinged the gorgeous pane. (006-040)

Beers marks his early recognition of the change of taste — it was "remarked by Thomas Warton that, at the first revival" the old forms returned (002-017). The *History* itself became a channel for the new medievalism, in which "Chatterton and Percy are both" involved (006-059). See [[medievalism]] and [[gothicism]].

## Reception & Influence

The lasting authority of the *History* is shown by its repeated re-editing across the nineteenth century (006-044), and Beers ranks it, with Tyrwhitt's *Chaucer*, as work of "real importance" (011-091). Its currency in Beers's own day is registered by the standard reference to "Warton, Thomas, Jr. 'History of English Poetry.' Ed.

Hazlitt" (011-283), and its authority extended to the antiquarian romance, as when it is cited alongside a "'History ... and Romance' or Mr. Warton's 'History of English Poetry'" (007-048).

Warton also engaged the controversies of his day, devoting part of the *History* to a review of Chatterton's Rowley poems and pronouncing one forged manuscript "a clumsy forgery" (010-033, 010-039).
