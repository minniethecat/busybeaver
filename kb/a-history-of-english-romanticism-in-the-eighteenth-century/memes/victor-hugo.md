## Overview

Victor Hugo was the towering figure of French Romanticism, whose poetry, novels, and critical manifestos defined the movement across Europe. Though he belongs to the nineteenth century rather than the eighteenth, Beers invokes Hugo repeatedly throughout "A History of English Romanticism in the Eighteenth Century" as a comparative reference point — the definitive romantic against whom the earlier English pioneers are measured. Hugo's "Notre Dame de Paris," his preface to "Cromwell" (1827), and his pronouncements on the nature of romantic art serve throughout Beers's study as touchstones for defining what romanticism is and what it is not.

## Life

Victor Marie Hugo was born in Besançon, France, in 1802, the son of a Napoleonic general. He emerged as the leader of the French Romantic school in the 1820s, publishing volumes of poetry, historical novels, and plays that broke decisively with French classical conventions. His dramatic manifesto, the preface to his unacted play "Cromwell" (1827), became the defining statement of French Romantic doctrine. Hugo's long career spanned most of the nineteenth century; he died in Paris in 1885 as a national icon.

Though Hugo appears throughout Beers's book only as a point of reference, his presence is pervasive. Beers opens his preface by imagining a composite of Byron and De Quincey as the English equivalent of Heine and Gautier — "as Gautier was of Victor Hugo" — establishing Hugo immediately as the archetype of the romantic master around whom a school forms. (P01-006)

## Works & Contribution

Hugo's works furnish Beers with a recurring set of contrasts between classical and romantic art. "Notre Dame de Paris" (1831) is cited as an exemplary romantic work, set beside Scott's "Ivanhoe" and Fouqué's "Der Zauberring," in opposition to the classical models of Flaxman's Homer drawings, Alfieri's tragedies, and David's paintings. (001-004)

The preface to "Cromwell" (1827) serves Beers as the locus classicus of romantic theory. In this manifesto, Hugo rejected the classical unities and called for a drama that mingled the sublime and the grotesque, reflecting the full range of human experience. Beers footnotes this document as a key statement of the romantic position. (001-044)

Hugo's satirical characterization of eighteenth-century French verse — "cette poésie fardée, mouchetée, poudrée, du dix-huitième siècle, cette littérature à paniers, à pompons et à falbalas" — is quoted by Beers to illustrate the artificiality of the classical school against which the romantics rebelled. The costumes of Watteau, Beers adds, contrast with the simple folds of Greek drapery as much as "The Rape of the Lock" contrasts with the Iliad. (002-015)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Hugo functions in Beers's argument primarily as a standard of comparison — the fully realized romantic whose example illuminates, by contrast, the tentative and incomplete romanticism of the eighteenth-century English pioneers. When Beers speaks of the "melancholy which Victor Hugo pronounces a distinguishing badge of romantic art," he is tracing the same quality backward into [[james-thomson]]'s "The Seasons" and the landscape poets of the 1740s. (004-025)

The discovery of Shakspere, Beers argues, "begot Schiller and Victor Hugo" on the Continent, while in eighteenth-century England it "begot only Ireland's forgeries" — a pointed contrast between the Continental romanticism that achieved full expression and the English movement that remained fitful and incomplete until the nineteenth century. (003-016)

Hugo's influence is also felt in the sociology of the romantic movement. Beers notes that the French romanticists rallied to the support of Hugo's melodramas at the Théâtre Français "nearly a century later" than the English claque that [[oliver-goldsmith]] described defending the restored Shakspere on the London stage — a parallel that underscores the recurring dynamics of literary revolution. (006-054)

## Reception & Influence

Hugo's impact on the European romantic movement was immense and immediate. His plays, novels, and critical writings established the framework within which romanticism was understood throughout the nineteenth century. For Beers, writing in 1898, Hugo is close enough to be a living memory — "Die Romantische Schule" and "Histoire du Romantisme" are recent books — yet distant enough to serve as a historical benchmark. (P01-006)

The English romantic revival that Beers traces, from the Spenserians through the Gothic revival to the ballad collectors and the German tributary, culminates not in an English Hugo but in the diffusion of romantic energies across multiple figures and genres. Hugo thus represents, in Beers's narrative, the unified romantic achievement that England approached but never quite attained in the eighteenth century.
