# The Castle of Indolence

## Overview

*The Castle of Indolence* is James Thomson's Spenserian poem of 1748, which Beers singles out as the finest fruit of the whole Spenserian revival: "the only one among the professed scholars of Spenser who caught the glow and splendor of the master was James Thomson" (003-043). It has kept its readers where drier imitations failed — "'The Castle of Indolence' has never wanted admirers" through "all changes of taste" (003-014).

## Author & Date

The poem is Thomson's, published in 1748 (003-043, 003-071) — the same Thomson whose *Seasons* had founded the poetry of nature. Beers treats it as the proof that genius transforms even imitation: "It is the privilege of genius to be original even in its imitations. Thomson took shape and hue from Spenser, but added something of his own, and the result has a value quite independent of its success as a reproduction" (003-043).

## Summary / Content

"'The Castle of Indolence,' 1748, is a fine poem; at least the first part of it is, for the second book is tiresomely allegorical, and somewhat involved in plot" (003-043). Its power lies in description — "There is a magic art in the description of the" enchanted, drowsy realm, as in the stanza of the "glittering streamlets" whose "lulling murmur made" the poem's characteristic music (003-044). Beers quotes it among the passages that gave the age "romantic imagery" (003-077).

## Form & Style

Written in "the stanza of the 'Faërie Queene'" (003-029), the poem achieves what Beers calls "the romantic iridescence, the 'atmosphere' which is lacking to the sharp contours of Augustan verse" — "an effect which cannot be wholly accounted for by what the poet says; an effect which is wrought by subtle sensations awakened by the sound and indefinite associations evoked by the words" (003-044). This atmospheric suggestiveness is precisely what the couplet could not supply.

## Significance to Romanticism

For Beers the poem is the moment the Spenserian revival stopped being pastiche and became poetry: alone among Spenser's scholars Thomson "caught the glow and splendor of the master" (003-043). Its "romantic iridescence" and "atmosphere" (003-044) are exactly the qualities the revival sought to recover against "the sharp contours of Augustan verse." See [[spenserianism]] and [[the-faerie-queene]].

## Reception & Controversy

The poem's standing has been remarkably durable — it "has never wanted admirers, through all changes of taste" (003-014). Beers grants the second book's allegorical tedium (003-043) but rests its fame on the first book's descriptive magic. He ranks it among the works of genuine "romantic imagery" produced by the Spenserian fashion (003-077).
