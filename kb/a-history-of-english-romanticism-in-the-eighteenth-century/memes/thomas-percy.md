# Thomas Percy

## Overview

Thomas Percy is, for Beers, the editor whose *Reliques of Ancient English Poetry* did more than any scholar's imitations to recall English poetry to the walks of imagination. "What scholars and professional men of letters had sought to do by their imitations of Spenser and Milton ... was much more effectually done by Percy and the ballad collectors" (008-001).

## Life

Beers treats Percy through his editorial work rather than his biography. He was "the editor of the 'Reliques'" (006-004), and later a bishop; the *Reliques* was his monument. His labors also extended to Norse antiquity: he Englished Mallet's book "in 1770 ... under the title, 'Northern Antiquities'" (006-004), making the Edda and the Runic poems available to English readers.

## Works & Contribution

Percy's great work is the *Reliques of Ancient English Poetry* (1765), which Beers calls the decade's "most important title" (008-003). By collecting "Old Heroic Ballads, Songs, and Other Pieces of our Earlier Poets" (008-003), Percy "did much to clear the way for Scott and Coleridge and Keats" (008-003). His translation of Mallet as *Northern Antiquities* (006-004) opened a second front, the Norse. Beers's summary of his achievement is that where the imitators had merely tried "to recall British poetry to the walks of imagina[tion]," Percy actually did it (008-001). See [[reliques-of-ancient-english-poetry]] and [[northern-antiquities]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Percy is a hinge of the revival on its popular-antiquarian side. His *Reliques*, "more fruitful in enduring results" than even Ossian (008-003), channelled the ballad tradition into the mainstream of English poetry and, with Warton's *History*, "promoted, beyond any" other force the medieval revival (006-044). Through him the old ballads reached the poets who would make the great age of romanticism. See [[the-ballad-revival]] and [[medievalism]].

## Reception & Influence

Percy's editorial method drew fierce attack from the purist Joseph Ritson — "an ill-tempered and eccentric man" who "attacked Warton as well as Percy" and "took issue with the theory maintained in Percy's introductory 'Essay on the Ancient Minstrels'" (008-062). Percy "was so abashed by the ridicule" that the controversy left its mark (008-056). Yet Beers's verdict is that his service — bringing "concrete specimens of mediaeval poetry" to the public — outweighed all the essayists' "general speculations" (007-049).
