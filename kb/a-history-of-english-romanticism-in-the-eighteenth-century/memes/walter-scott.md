## Overview

Sir Walter Scott (1771–1832) appears throughout Beers's narrative not as a subject of the eighteenth-century revival but as its capstone figure—the writer in whom the scattered impulses of Spenserianism, ballad collecting, Gothic medievalism, and German Romanticism converged into the historical novel and the romantic verse tale.

His career forms the natural terminus of the movement that Beers traces from Thomson to the "Lyrical Ballads." (007-020)

## Life

Walter Scott was born in Edinburgh in 1771 and educated for the law. In 1792, while a young lawyer, he first encountered the German Romantic poetry that would shape his early literary career.

(011-023) His translations of Bürger's ballads "Lenore" and "The Wild Huntsman," published anonymously in 1796, were his first book. (011-025) Shortly afterward he translated Goethe's "Götz von Berlichingen" (1799), the drama of the medieval knight-errant. (011-162)

Scott's subsequent career as a poet—"The Lay of the Last Minstrel" (1805), "Marmion" (1808), "The Lady of the Lake" (1810)—established him as the most popular narrative poet of his age. His turn to the novel with "Waverley" (1814) inaugurated a new literary form and made him the most widely read author in Europe.

## Works & Contribution

Scott's first literary enterprise was not original composition but the editing and collecting of traditional ballads. Beers notes that Scott "got much of the material for his 'Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border' from oral tradition," following in the path blazed by [[thomas-percy|Percy's]] "Reliques of Ancient English Poetry"

(1765). (008-007) Scott was "steeped to the lips in ballad lore," and his editorial methods combined scholarly antiquarianism with a poet's instinct for the living tradition. (008-029)

His translations from the German—Bürger's "Lenore" and "The Wild Huntsman" (1796), Goethe's "Götz von Berlichingen" (1799)—were the first major conduits through which German Romanticism entered English literature.

(011-045) Beers finds it remarkable "that so healthy a genius as Walter Scott should have made his début in literature under the spell of a taste so morbid" as the German Sturm und Drang.

(011-045) Yet the German influence was a passing phase: Scott's robust nature soon asserted itself, and his mature work channels the Gothic and the romantic into forms that are fundamentally sane and humane.

As a young man, Scott also made acquaintance with the Ossianic poems of [[james-macpherson|Macpherson]], and Beers records the hope—expressed to Scott's face—that he would one day "feel moved to cast Ossian's mantle" and produce a modern national epic. (009-052) This hope was in a sense fulfilled in the Waverley novels, which did for Scottish history what Ossian had attempted in mythic mode.

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers treats Scott as the figure in whom all the threads of the eighteenth-century revival are gathered and brought to completion. The feudal tyrant, the venerable castle, and the persecuted maiden—the machinery of the Gothic novel inherited from [[horace-walpole|Walpole]] through [[anne-radcliffe|Mrs. Radcliffe]]—were "perfected by Sir Walter Scott" in the historical novel. (007-020)

Scott's antiquarian imagination was not always historically accurate—Beers remarks that "Walter Scott's archaeology is not always correct" and that his reconstructions could be "infinitely tedious"—but the sheer imaginative energy of his medievalism carried all before it.

(007-070) He "united to the fire of a poet the minute learning of an antiquary," a combination that gave his historical fictions their distinctive authority. (008-081)

In the larger argument of the book, Scott exemplifies the transformation of the Romantic revival from a fringe antiquarian enthusiasm into a dominant literary mode.

Beers's narrative of the eighteenth century is organized around the gradual triumph of romance over classicism; Scott represents that triumph's final, irreversible phase.

The "forerunners of Scott, Coleridge, and Keats"—Akenside, Shenstone, Dyer, the Wartons, Percy, Walpole, Radcliffe, Lewis, Chatterton—are "writers of a distinctly inferior order," but Scott stands at the threshold where collective preparation becomes individual achievement. (011-015)

## Reception & Influence

Scott's influence on the nineteenth century was immense and pan-European. His translations helped establish the taste for German Romanticism in Britain, and his own poetry and novels were translated, imitated, and adapted across the continent. Beers quotes a verse that captures the contemporary sense of Scott's ascendancy: "To crown with honor thee and Walter Scott!" (011-056)

Lockhart, Scott's biographer, believed that the translation of "Götz" should have been published ten years sooner to have had its full effect, since the English public had already become sated with the melodramas and romances of Kotzebue and the German *Kraftmänner* by the time Scott's version appeared.

(011-049) This judgment, which Beers appears to endorse, suggests that Scott's German phase was as much a matter of fortunate timing as of literary discernment.

## References

Beers invokes Scott throughout the book as the figure in whom the Romantic revival culminates, but his most concentrated discussions occur in chapter VII ("The Gothic Revival"), chapter VIII ("Percy and the Ballads"), chapter IX ("Ossian"), and chapter XI ("The German Tributary"). Scott is not a subject of the eighteenth-century story as such; he is the horizon toward which it tends.
