# The Picturesque

## Definition

The picturesque, in Beers's account, is the taste that prized what could be composed like a painted landscape: freedom, variety, irregularity, and naturalness set against rule, monotony, uniformity, and artifice (004-056). Its very name looks to the painter's canvas — Shenstone held that a "landscape should contain variety enough to form a picture upon canvas," and that "the landscape painter is the gardener's best designer" (004-055). Beers aligns the quality with the romantic rather than the classical temper: he recalls Schlegel's saying that the genius of the antique drama was statuesque, "and that of the romantic drama picturesque," the one correct, symmetric, and sharp in outline, the other lingering, shadowed, and incomplete (001-025).

## Origins & Development

The application of such terms to scenery came comparatively late, after the literary revolution in taste had begun (001-010). The style it displaced was the old formal Italian, French, or Dutch garden, inherited from the seventeenth century, with its clipped hedges, straight alleys, and topiary (004-034); Beers even draws an analogy between the French garden's "trim regularity and artificial smoothness" and the couplets of Pope (004-038). Against this rose "the new or natural school of landscape gardening," which Gray in 1763 called a "purely English art" born scarcely forty years before, and of which Beers makes Thomson in large measure the father (004-031). Its principle was to imitate nature — to let trees keep their shapes, to substitute winding walks for straight alleys and natural waterfalls for fountains (004-054). The reform was so identified with England that the Continent adopted the names *Englische Garten* and *jardin Anglais* for grounds laid out in the natural taste (004-032). Later practitioners such as "the ruthless Capability Brown" carried the fashion to the point of laying waste many a prim old garden (004-046).

## Key Figures & Works

The movement's pioneers doubled as painters and gardeners. Dyer "was a landscape painter, as well as a poet," and Shenstone "one of the best of landscape gardeners" (004-002); Dyer's "Grongar Hill" is "in fact, a pictorial poem," a sketch of the landscape seen from a Welsh summit (004-087). Shenstone set down his principles in his "Unconnected Thoughts on Gardening" (1764), objecting to straight lines and insisting that the shape of ground, trees, and water "must be sacred to nature" (004-055). William Kent, the architect and landscape painter, worked on the ruling principle "that nature abhors a straight line," and famously "leaped the fence and saw that all nature was a garden" (004-060). Beers treats these figures within [[the-landscape-poets]], the chapter richest in the picturesque impulse.

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers argues that naturalism and [[Romanticism]] had a common root — the desire to escape into fresh air and freer conditions, manifesting itself in "a passion for wildness, grandeur, solitude" (004-001). He draws an explicit "correspondence between this new taste for picturesque gardening" and the new taste in literature that "left the world of society for the solitudes of nature," and ultimately turned to the Gothic ages and to Norse and Celtic antiquity (004-056). The picturesque tourist, who "came out of curiosity" to view famous grounds like the Leasowes, is a symptom of the same mood (004-064). Where naturalistic poetry felt "that attraction toward the savage, the awful, the mysterious, the primitive," Beers finds the properly romantic note (004-022); and the increasing prominence of the mossy cave and hermit's cell in verse and gardening he calls "a note of the coming romanticism" (004-069). Even the word *romantic* applied to scenery — the woody dell, the leafy glen, the forest path — carries this charge, as against the plain public highway (001-018).

## Contrasts & Debates

The picturesque defines itself by opposition. Beers sets the statuesque completeness of the classic — the Greek temple "correct, symmetric, sharp in outline, in the clear light of day" — against the shadow, color, and perspective of romantic art (001-025). Schopenhauer's contrast of the English and old French gardens sharpens the point: the English garden brings "the will of Nature" to purest expression, while the French reflects "only the will of the owner who has subdued Nature" (004-032). The picturesque was thus not merely a fashion in grounds but a philosophy of nature over artifice.
