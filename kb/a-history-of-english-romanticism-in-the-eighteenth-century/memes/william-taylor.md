## Overview

William Taylor of Norwich (1765–1836) was the translator and critic who "did more than any man of his generation, by his translations and critical papers in the *Monthly Magazine* and *Monthly Review*, to spread a knowledge of the new German literature in England." (011-043)

His translation of [[gottfried-burger|Bürger's]] "Lenore" was the spark that lit Walter Scott's earliest literary ambition, and his wide-ranging critical writing made him, in Beers's account, the indispensable conduit of the German tributary.

## Life

Taylor's German connection began early. "When a lad of sixteen he had been sent to study at Detmold, Westphalia, and had spent more than a year (1781–82) in Germany, calling upon [[johann-goethe|Goethe]] at Weimar, with a letter of introduction, on his way home to England." (011-044)

His linguistic attainment was unusual for his time. Lucy Aikin wrote of him: "When his acquaintance with this literature began, there was probably no English translation of any German author but through the medium of the French, and he is very likely to have been the first Englishman of letters to read Goethe, Wieland, Lessing, and Bürger in the originals." (011-045)

## Works & Contribution

Taylor's most famous single work was his translation of Bürger's "Lenore." In the autumn of 1794, "Miss Aikin, afterward Mrs. Barbauld, entertained a party at Dugald Stewart's by reading a translation of Bürger's ghastly ballad 'Lenore.'" (011-025)

The translation had not yet been published and Miss Aikin read from manuscript. Scott was not present but his friend described the performance to him, and Scott "was so much impressed by it that he immediately translated the poem himself." (011-025)

Taylor followed "Lenore" with translations of Bürger's "Des Pfarrer's Tochter von Taubenhain" (printed as "The Lass of Fair Wone"), a version of Goethe's "Iphigenie auf Tauris," and Lessing's "Nathan der Weise." (011-042, 011-045)

His magnum opus was the "Historical Survey of German Poetry" (1830, 3 vols.), a comprehensive critical history that introduced English readers to the whole course of German literature. (011-274)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Taylor's importance to the romantic revival was as the chief English intermediary of German literature. His "Lenore" translation set off the chain reaction that led to Scott's first efforts, Lewis's "Tales of Wonder," and the whole fashion for German balladry.

In translating "Lenore," Taylor "nationalized and individualized the theme by making his William a knight of Richard the Lion Heart's, who had fallen in Holy Land"—a liberty from Bürger's Prague setting that Scott followed in his own version. (011-041)

His version was preferred by [[matthew-lewis|Lewis]] over Scott's as "the most effective," possessing "a wildness and quaintness not found in Scott's more literal and more polished rendering." (011-029)

Scott's friends assured him his version was superior, and "Taylor himself wrote to him: 'The ghost nowhere makes his appearance so well as with you, or his exit so well as with Mr. Spencer'"; yet Beers agrees with Lewis that Taylor's version better caught "the *Grobheit*, the rude, rough manner of popular poetry." (011-028, 011-029)

## Reception & Influence

Taylor's critical writing shaped English perceptions of German literature for a generation. His papers in the *Monthly Magazine* and *Monthly Review* were, for many English readers, their first encounter with the names of Goethe, Schiller, and the *Sturm und Drang*.

His "Historical Survey of German Poetry" remained a standard reference through the mid-nineteenth century, and Beers's own chapter XI draws upon Taylor's framework for understanding the German tributary to English romanticism.

Southey wrote to Taylor in 1798, lamenting that Coleridge's "Ancient Mariner" was "the clumsiest attempt at German sublimity I ever saw"—a judgment that reveals how thoroughly Taylor had set the terms of the German-English literary conversation. (011-083)

## References

Beers discusses Taylor almost exclusively in chapter XI (The German Tributary), where he serves as the primary English agent of German literary influence. A two-volume "Memoir of Taylor of Norwich" by J. W. Robberds (1843) is listed in the bibliography. (011-250)
