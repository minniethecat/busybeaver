## Overview

Joseph Ritson (1752–1803) was "an ill-tempered and eccentric man: a vegetarian, a free-thinker, a spelling reformer, and latterly a Jacobin"—and, for all that, one of the most exacting antiquarian editors of the ballad revival. (008-062)

He was the gadfly who exposed Percy's heavy editorial hand in the "Reliques" and helped force the publication of the folio manuscript that Percy had so long guarded. Beers classes him with [[thomas-percy|Percy]] and [[thomas-tyrwhitt|Tyrwhitt]] among the scholars "who, as collectors and editors, rescued the fragments of ancient ministrelsy." (007-049)

## Life

Beers sketches Ritson's personality more sharply than his biography. He "used to describe any clerical antagonist as a 'stinking priest'" and "attacked [[thomas-warton|Warton]] as well as Percy." (008-062)

His quarrel with Percy turned on the theory maintained in Percy's introductory "Essay on the Ancient Minstrels," that minstrels were not only performers but authors of the ballads they sang—a position Ritson "took issue with" fiercely. (008-062)

He died insane in 1803. (008-062)

## Works & Contribution

Ritson's editorial output was formidable. His bibliography in Beers's appendix lists "Ancient English Metrical Romances" (1802, 3 vols.), "Ancient Songs" (1792), "English Anthology" (1792–94, 3 vols.), "Pieces of Ancient Popular Poetry" (1833, 2d ed.), and "Robin Hood" (1832, 2d ed., 2 vols.). (011-245, 011-246, 011-247, 011-248, 011-249)

His importance lay in his textual rigor. Where Percy freely "filled *lacunae* in his originals with stanzas, and, in some cases, with nearly entire poems of his own composition," Ritson demanded fidelity to the manuscript source. (008-061)

It was Ritson's relentless pressure, together with that of Furnivall and Hales, that eventually forced open access to the Percy folio manuscript: "Mr. Furnivall finally got leave to have the folio manuscript copied and printed," vindicating Ritson's long campaign. (008-061)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Ritson's role in the romantic revival is paradoxical. A man of the Enlightenment in his rationalism and his anti-clericalism, he was yet a crucial enabler of the return to medieval poetry: his editions made genuine early English verse available to a reading public that had known it only through modernized adaptations.

Beers judges that scholars like Ritson and Tyrwhitt "performed a more useful service than mild clerical essayists, such as [[james-beattie|Beattie]] and [[richard-hurd|Hurd]], who amused their leisure with general speculations about the origin of poetry." (007-049)

His quarrel with Percy exposed the central tension of the ballad revival—the conflict between antiquarian fidelity and poetic adaptation—and pushed the field toward the modern editorial standards that would eventually produce Child's "English and Scottish Popular Ballads."

## Reception & Influence

Ritson's immediate influence was that of a troublesome corrector. "He attacked Warton as well as Percy," and his pedantic asperity—Beers notes he was "ill-tempered"—limited his direct following. (008-062)

Yet the broader effect of his scholarship was lasting. By insisting that Percy produce the folio manuscript, Ritson helped establish the principle that ballad editing required documentary evidence, not poetic fancy.

The editions he produced remained standard reference works through the nineteenth century, and his "Robin Hood" collection was the foundation of all subsequent study of the outlaw ballads.

## References

Beers mentions Ritson in chapters VI (The School of Warton), VII (The Gothic Revival), and VIII (Percy and the Ballads), always in the company of Percy and Tyrwhitt as the triad of antiquarian scholars who made medieval poetry accessible.
