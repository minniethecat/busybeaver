# The Seasons

## Overview

*The Seasons* is James Thomson's long descriptive poem of external nature, the founding text of the eighteenth-century "return to nature." Beers ranks it among "these pioneers in the 'return to nature'" whose work "represents the transition, and must be taken into account in any complete history of the romantic movement" (004-002). To its first readers "'The Seasons' must have come as the revelation of a fresh world of beauty" (004-006).

## Author & Date

Thomson, "a young Scotchman," "came to London to push his literary fortunes" in 1725, and through his countryman Mallet "helped him to bring out 'Winter,' the first installment of 'The Seasons,' which was published in 1726" (004-005). "'Winter' was followed by 'Summer' in 1727, 'Spring' in 1728, and the completed 'Seasons' in 1730" (004-005). Thomson kept revising: "The original 'Seasons' contained only 3902 lines ... while the author's final revision of 1746 gave 5413" (004-005).

## Summary / Content

The poem describes the natural year with a fresh, particularizing eye. Its novelties were real: "Such passages as those which describe the first spring showers, the thunderstorm in summer, the trout-fishing, the sheep-washing, and the terrors of the winter night, were not only strange to the public of that day, but were new in English poetry" (004-006). Thomson "was something of a naturalist, who wrote lovingly and with his 'eye upon the object,'" so that "His images of external nature are never false and seldom vague, like Pope's" (004-007). His method could tend to catalogue: dealing with "very extended prospects, he rather enumerates than describes," until "The picture becomes a catalogue" (004-009).

## Form & Style

*The Seasons* is written in blank verse, one of the "new type" of poems by which the age laid aside the heroic couplet (004-003). Beers marks "the stronger contrast ... between Thomson and Pope, not alone in subject and feeling, but in diction and verse": "Thomson's style is florid and luxuriant, his numbers flowing and diffuse, while Pope had wonted the English ear to the extreme of compression" (004-005). Coleridge's comparison stands: "In chastity of diction and the harmony of blank verse, Cowper leaves Thomson immeasurably below him; yet I still feel the latter to have been the born poet" (004-007).

## Significance to Romanticism

The poem's importance is that it reopened external nature to English verse against the "indoor life of a highly artificial society" (004-001). Its fertility is Beers's proof of its originality: "One proof that 'The Seasons' was the work of a fresh and independent genius is afforded by the many imitations to which it soon gave birth" (004-005) — in Germany, France, and at home, where "'There was hardly one verse writer of any eminence,' says Gosse, 'from 1725-50, who was not in some manner guided or biased by Thomson'" (004-005). ## Reception & Controversy

The poem's popularity was immediate and lasting — "no sooner read than universally admired" (004-005) — save among those "who had not been used to feel, or to look for anything in poetry beyond a *point* of satirical or epigrammatic wit" (004-005). Later taste found it "unimaginative" beside Wordsworth or Shelley, and Beers concedes "we are perhaps too apt to undervalue Thomson's simple descriptive or pictorial method," likening him "as a colorist, to Rubens" (004-006).
