## Overview

Eton College, on the Thames near Windsor, is the school of [[thomas-gray]] and the subject of his "Ode on a Distant Prospect of Eton College." Beers uses the ode as a touchstone of the new sensibility: "When Gray published his exquisite ode on Eton College... little notice was taken of it," yet Thomas Warton could later place it "far above Pope's 'Pastorals'" (006-066). The place is also the cradle of a friendship — Gray, [[horace-walpole]], and Richard West — that runs through the early history of the revival.

## Description & Location

Eton was the scene of Gray's boyhood and of the schoolfellowship that shaped him. Beers records that Gray, Walpole, and Richard West were together "at Eton," and that Gray's "solitary sonnet" belonged to that early circle (003-073). Walpole, "as we have seen, had been an Eton friend of Gray," and the two later travelled abroad together (007-015). Looking back on the school across the water, the poet turns its playing-fields elegiac: "When Gray sees the Eton schoolboys at their sports, he is sadly reminded" of "The ministers of human fate / And black Misfortune's baleful train" (005-044).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

The Eton ode belongs with the poetry of melancholy and retrospection that Beers gathers in the Miltonic group; its "Hymn to Adversity" and "the Eton College ode" were written in the same meditative vein (005-053). What makes the place matter is less its architecture than its use as a vantage of feeling — the "distant prospect" from which youth is seen as already shadowed by fate. That elegiac gaze is continuous with the mood of Gray's [[elegy-written-in-a-country-churchyard]], and marks the shift Warton praised away from Augustan wit toward [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]].
