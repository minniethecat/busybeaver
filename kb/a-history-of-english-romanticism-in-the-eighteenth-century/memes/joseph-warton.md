## Overview

Joseph Warton (1722–1800) was an English poet, critic, and clergyman whose [[essay-on-pope|Essay on Pope]] (1756) became a landmark document in the shift away from Augustan literary values toward the Romantic revival.

With his younger brother [[thomas-warton]], he formed the nucleus of what contemporaries called the "School of Warton," pioneering the appreciation of Spenser, Milton's minor poetry, and medieval antiquity. (006-020)

## Life

Joseph Warton was the elder son of Thomas Warton Sr., vicar of Basingstoke, who had been a fellow of Magdalen College and Professor of Poetry at Oxford.

(006-022) In 1740, as an Oxford undergraduate of eighteen, he wrote his blank-verse poem "The Enthusiast, or the Lover of Nature," which displayed an instinct for the direction of the literary current that Beers commends as "not uncommon in youthful artists." (005-009)

Warton took holy orders and became a beneficed clergyman, marrying and raising a family—unlike several of his literary contemporaries. (005-045) He later served as headmaster of Winchester College, where his convivial temperament and fondness for military parades made him a beloved, if sometimes eccentric, figure in the life of the school. (006-025)

## Works & Contribution

Warton's first major publication was a volume of fourteen odes issued in 1746, appearing simultaneously with the odes of [[william-collins]]. (005-022) Gray, writing to Thomas Wharton, remarked that "each is the half of a considerable man, and one the counterpart of the other"—Warton having "but little invention, very poetical choice of expression, and a good ear." (005-022)

The odes most closely resembling Milton in manner are "To Fancy," "To Solitude," and "To the Nightingale," all written in the eight-syllabled couplet. (005-023) Warton also wrote an "Ode to Evening," contributing to the crepuscular mode that characterized the Miltonic group. (005-040)

His most consequential prose work was the "Essay on the Genius and Writings of Pope" (1756, with a second volume in 1782). In it, Warton argued that the highest reaches of poetry were the sublime, the pathetic, and the imaginative—qualities he found in Spenser, Shakespeare, and Milton rather than in Pope's correctness.

(006-046) He testified that [[james-thomson|Thomson's]] "Seasons" had been "very instrumental in diffusing a taste for the beauties of nature and landscape." (004-031)

Beers notes that Warton's pretensions as a poet are "much less than his younger brother's," acknowledging that Joseph's true significance lay in his critical advocacy rather than his verse. (006-027) His "Essay on Pope" was an "attempt to dethrone the monarch of English poetry," substituting a more romantic canon for the Augustan orthodoxy. (006-061)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Joseph Warton occupies a pivotal position in Beers's narrative of the Romantic revival. As early as 1756, writing after quoting copiously from Milton's "Nativity Ode," he declared that poem "not sufficiently read nor admired"—a judgment that helped redirect taste toward the Miltonic sublime. (005-004)

The Warton brothers together were, in Beers's formulation, "the true pioneers of the mediaeval revival." (006-020) The term "School of Warton" was used by unsympathetic critics to disparage the new taste for antique minstrelsy and Gothic subjects, but it captures the brothers' role as the intellectual center of the emerging romantic sensibility. (006-021)

Warton's influence extended into the canon of descriptive and landscape poetry. He praised West's "Monody on the Death of Queen Caroline" and commended the chorus of British bards in Mason's "Caractacus"—works that blended the Spenserian and Miltonic inheritances.

(005-067) His advocacy for the picturesque and for natural description helped shape the expectations of the reading public that would later embrace the full Romantic achievement.

When [[thomas-percy|Percy's]] "Reliques of Ancient English Poetry" was issued, the now venerable Joseph Warton was so entranced that he could hardly speak of anything else—a fitting emblem of the continuity between the mid-century pioneers and the ballad revival of the 1760s. (007-074)

## Reception & Influence

Warton's "Essay on Pope" exercised a decisive influence on the next generation of critics and poets.

Beers cites the judgment that "Warton's 'Essay on the Genius and Writings of Pope' was an attempt to dethrone the monarch of English poetry," and while the essay did not succeed in diminishing Pope's reputation permanently, it opened a space in critical discourse for values outside the Augustan framework. (006-061)

The romantic leanings of the Warton brothers appear to have been an inheritance: their father's volume of verse, published posthumously, revealed tastes for the antique and the fanciful that anticipated his sons' more systematic advocacy. (006-022)
