## Overview

Bristol — "old Saxon _Bricgestowe_, 'place of the bridge'" over the Avon — is the city of [[chatterton]], and through him a city of the imagination. Beers calls Chatterton "the child of Redcliffe Church" and makes his "love of place" the mainspring of his art: "Bristol was his world" (010-002, 010-012). The medieval Bristol of the [[the-rowley-poems]] is Chatterton's own invention, projected back onto the real streets and church he knew.

## Description & Location

The city lies on the Avon "not far above its confluence with the Severn," and there "Chatterton was born in 1752, the posthumous son of a dissipated schoolmaster, whose ancestors for a hundred and fifty years had been... sextons to the church of [[st-mary-redcliffe|St. Mary Redcliffe]]" (010-002). Beers peoples his account with the ordinary figures of "the Bristol _bourgeoisie_" — church organists and tradesmen among whom the boy grew up (010-004). It was to citizens of this town — Burgum "a worthy pewterer of Bristol," and the surgeon-antiquary William Barrett, "engaged in writing a history of Bristol" — that Chatterton addressed his fabricated pedigrees and documents (010-017, 010-018).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

Beers reads Chatterton's Bristol beside Scott's Border country: quoting Wordsworth's phrase "localized romance," he finds in Chatterton the same "absorption in the past" that "derived its intensity from his love of place" (010-012). Into "The Battle of Hastings" Chatterton "did not forget to introduce a Bristowan contingent" performing "prodigies of valor upon the Normans" (010-012). The whole Rowley cycle is anchored to a supposed fifteenth-century Bristol and its merchant-patron Canynge, and to the monk "Thomas Rowley, a secular priest of Bristol, who flourished about 1460" (010-019). The city thus becomes a laboratory of romantic [[medievalism]], where an invented local past does the imaginative work later done by antiquarian scholarship.
