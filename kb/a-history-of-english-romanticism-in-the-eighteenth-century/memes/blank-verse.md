# Blank Verse

## Definition

Blank verse is the unrhymed measure whose fortunes Beers reads as a barometer of the classic–romantic contest. The Restoration critics had condemned it: "the Restoration critics were at one in judging blank verse too 'low' for a poem of heroic dimensions," and though "Addison gave it the preference in epic poetry, Johnson was its persistent foe, and regarded it as little short of immoral" (002-039). Beers draws the paradox sharply: "rhyme, a mediaeval invention, should have been associated in the last century with the classical school of poetry; while blank verse, the nearest English equivalent of the language of Attic tragedy, was a shibboleth of romanticizing poets, like Thomson" (002-039).

## Origins & Development

The measure's authority was Milton's, and its eighteenth-century revival ran with the "return to nature." Among "the new type" of mid-century poems that abandoned the heroic couplet, "'The Seasons,' 'Night Thoughts' and 'The Grave' are written in blank verse" (004-003). Its champions had to fight Augustan prejudice: Adam Smith "had given the preference to rhyme over blank verse," and Johnson, hearing it, "exclaimed, 'Sir, ... had I known that he loved rhyme as much as you tell me he does, I should have hugged him'" (004-004). Even sympathetic critics set limits — "Gray could endure no blank verse outside of Milton" (002-039) — but the form spread through the descriptive poets as the natural vehicle of a freer, more expansive manner.

## Key Figures & Works

Thomson is blank verse's great eighteenth-century practitioner. Beers weighs its music against Cowper's: "In chastity of diction and the harmony of blank verse, Cowper leaves Thomson immeasurably below him; yet I still feel the latter to have been the born poet" (004-007). John Philips had earlier carried Milton's measure into mock-heroic and Georgic — "the poet of 'L'Allegro' and 'Il Penseroso' ... had spoken of rhyme as 'the invention of a barbarous age'" — and Philips' "The Splendid Shilling," "Blenheim," and "Cyder" "were all avowed imitation of Milton" (004-003). Thomson honored him as "'Philips, Pomona's bard, the second thou / Who nobly durst, in rime-unfettered verse, / With British freedom sing the British song'" (004-004). The measure thus links Milton, Philips, Thomson, and the whole school of nature poetry.

## Role in the Romantic Revival

For Beers, blank verse is one of the formal signatures of the romantic revival: its adoption was, with the Spenserian stanza, "an outward sign of the revolution in poetic feeling," the readiness "to lay aside the heroic couplet in favor of other kinds" (004-003). That the "romanticizing poets" made unrhymed Miltonic measure their "shibboleth" against the couplet of "the classical school" (002-039) makes the choice of verse itself a romantic declaration. Thomson's "rime-unfettered verse," sung "With British freedom" (004-004), embodies the liberation from Augustan discipline that the revival sought. See [[romanticism]] for the movement and [[spenserianism]] for the parallel stanzaic revolt.

## Contrasts & Debates

The whole life of blank verse in the century is a debate: rhyme versus no rhyme, "correct" heroic couplet versus Miltonic freedom. Beers stages it through its partisans — Johnson "its persistent foe," ready to "hug" a lover of rhyme (002-039, 004-004); Addison its epic advocate; Gray its cautious friend "outside of Milton" (002-039). The classical objection that blank verse was "too 'low'" for "heroic dimensions" (002-039) meets the romantic counter-claim of "British freedom" (004-004). Beers's irony — that "mediaeval" rhyme served the classicists while the "Attic" unrhymed line served the romantics — makes blank verse the neatest emblem of the age's crossed allegiances (002-039).
