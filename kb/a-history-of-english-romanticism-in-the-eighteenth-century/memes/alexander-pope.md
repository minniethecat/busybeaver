## Overview

Alexander Pope (1688–1744) was the presiding poet of the English Augustan age, whose name Beers couples with [[samuel-johnson|Johnson's]] to name the whole classical temper of the century—"the times of Pope and Johnson in England." (001-012)

His mastery of the [[the-heroic-couplet|heroic couplet]] fixed the single "standard of excellence in poetry" for a hundred years, making him the reigning authority against which the [[romanticism|Romantic revival]] measured itself. (002-044)

## Life

Beers treats Pope less as a life to narrate than as the emblem of an age, dating the classical high-water mark to "the time of Pope's death (1744)." (002-017)

He records the poet's formative counsel from William Walsh, "the muses' judge and friend," who told the youthful Pope that the one way still open to excel his predecessors "was by correctness." (002-036)

Pope was grateful for the advice, and named Walsh in his "Essay on Criticism" as the friend who had "taught his muse to sing, Prescribed her heights and pruned her tender wing." (002-037)

On religion, Beers notes that "Dryden and Pope were professed Catholics," though he finds nothing to set their sacred verse apart from that of their Protestant contemporaries. (002-023)

The deaths of Pope in 1744 and Swift in 1745 marked, for Beers, the passing of "the last important survivors of the Queen Anne wits." (005-033)

## Works & Contribution

Pope brought the heroic couplet "to perfection," giving it "all the energy and variety of which it was capable," so that for a century English poetry almost lost sight of any other form. (002-042)

Beers traces the Augustan "standard of excellence" to "the smooth distichs of heroic verse which Pope was the first to perfect," imitated by "hundreds of later rhymers who lacked his nobler powers." (002-044)

His "Essay on Criticism" laid down the age's creed: "follow Nature, and in order that you may follow nature, observe the rules, which are only 'Nature methodized,' and also imitate the ancients." (002-030)

So complete was his commitment to rhyme that, "among his many thousand rhymed couplets," Pope "left no blank verse except the few lines contributed to [[the-seasons|Thomson's 'Seasons']]." (002-039)

Beers finds the classical mode powerless before deep feeling:

> Pope's "Epistle of Eloisa to Abelard" rings hollow: it is rhetoric, not poetry. (002-050)

Pope, he concludes, "is always 'heard,' never 'overheard.'" (002-050)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Pope stands in Beers's history as the pole opposite to romance. The poet "whose art is the antithesis of Pope's," he writes, is "the poet of the '[[the-faerie-queene|Faërie Queene]].'" (003-017)

He frames the whole tradition as a swing between two poles: "English poetry has oscillated between the poles of [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]] and Pope." (003-019)

The new school raised Spenser's name against his; a generation "surfeited with Pope's rhetorical devices" turned with relief to the amplitude of the [[the-spenserian-stanza|Spenserian stanza]]. (003-017)

Beers is careful to add that Pope himself "had read the 'Faërie Queene' with delight when he was a boy, and re-read it with equal pleasure in his last years." (003-022)

The decisive assault came from the critics. Lowell called [[joseph-warton|Joseph Warton's]] "Essay on the Genius and Writings of Pope" (1756) "the earliest public official declaration of war against the reigning mode." (006-046)

Warton placed Pope only "in the second rank of poets, below Spenser, Shakspere, and Milton"—an estimate Beers calls revolutionary for a book issued just twelve years after Pope's death. (006-062)

## Reception & Influence

Warton predicted that "Pope's fame as a poet will ultimately rest on his 'Windsor Forest,' his 'Epistle of Eloisa to Abelard,' and 'The Rape of the Lock'"—a prophecy Beers says time has "in part" already belied. (006-075)

Yet Pope's authority long outlived him. [[horace-walpole|Horace Walpole]], for all his Gothic dabbling, "said that poetry died with Pope, whose measure and manner he employed in his own verses." (007-031)

Beers resists caricature, warning that "it would be a mistake to suppose that the men of Pope's generation, including Pope himself, were altogether wanting in romantic feeling." (002-058)

## References
