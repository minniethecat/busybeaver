# William Shenstone

## Overview

William Shenstone is, for Beers, at once a Spenserian poet and one of the age's most characteristic men of taste — author of *The Schoolmistress* and maker of the celebrated landscape garden at the Leasowes. Beers names him among the poets in whom "That elegiac mood, that love of retirement and seclusion" first appears (005-033), and calls him "one of the best of landscape gardeners" (004-002).

## Life

Beers records Shenstone's temperament and circumstances with affectionate precision. "At Oxford, Shenstone had signalized his natural tastes by wearing his own hair instead of the wig then (1732) universally the fashion" (004-061). "On coming of age, he inherited a Shropshire farm, called the Leasowes ... and an annuity of some three hundred pounds," and "was of an indolent, retiring, and somewhat melancholy temperament; and, instead of pursuing a professional career, he settled down" to adorn his grounds (004-061). Gray's tart portrait — a man "always wishing for money, for fame and other distinctions," whose "whole philosophy consisted in living, against his will, in retirement" — Beers quotes in full (004-071).

## Works & Contribution

Shenstone's contribution to this history is *The Schoolmistress*, "One of the pleasantest outcomes of this literary fashion" (003-041), "an affectionate half-humorous description of the little dame-school of Shenstone's — and of everybody's — native village" (003-041). Gray "pronounced 'The Schoolmistress' a masterpiece in its kind" (004-071). Its application of "Spenser's stately verse and grave, ancient words" to "so humble a theme" gives its "quaint effect," its humor "warmed with a certain tenderness" (003-042). See [[the-schoolmistress]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Shenstone advances the revival on two of its sentimental fronts. His poem belongs to the Spenserian revival that Beers saw as holding "a future, a development" (003-057); and his temperament — "indolent, retiring, and somewhat melancholy" (004-061), given to "love of retirement and seclusion" (005-033) — makes him an early register of the elegiac sensibility that runs through the mid-century. His landscape gardening at the Leasowes links him to the parallel taste for scenery (004-002). See [[the-poetry-of-melancholy]] and [[spenserianism]].

## Reception & Influence

*The Schoolmistress* was warmly received and imitated — Goldsmith "evidently had it in memory when he drew the picture of the school in his 'Deserted Village'" (003-042). Beers pairs it with Thomson's *Castle of Indolence* as the two lasting "specimens of this class of literature" (003-031). Gray's mixed estimate — praising the poem while slighting the man (004-071) — is the sharpest of the contemporary notices Beers preserves.
