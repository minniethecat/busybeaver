## Overview

Strawberry Hill was the villa of [[horace-walpole]] — son of the prime minister Robert Walpole and "a man of fashion, a man of taste, and a man of letters" (007-014) — which he remodelled into a miniature Gothic castle over some twenty years. For Beers it is "the most notable instance that we encounter of virtuosity invading the neighboring realm of literature," the point where antiquarian collecting passed directly into fiction (007-014). The house is inseparable from the book it produced: "Strawberry Hill begat 'The Castle of Otranto'" (007-032).

## Description & Location

Walpole bought the villa about 1750, "on the Thames near Windsor," where it had formerly belonged to Mrs. Chenevix, a fashionable London toy-woman (007-017). He then "began to turn his house into a miniature Gothic castle, in which he is said to have 'outlived three sets of his own battlements'" (007-018). The fabric was, in Beers's account, "an odd blend of ecclesiastical and castellated Gothic applied to domestic uses" — a cloister, a chapel, a round tower, a gallery, a "refectory," a stair-turret with Gothic balustrade, stained windows, mural scutcheons, and Gothic paper-hangings (007-018). Among the curiosities he gathered there were suits of armor and other medieval bric-à-brac (007-031). In 1757 Walpole also set up a private press at Strawberry Hill (005-053).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

Walpole "may be regarded as having given a real impetus to the revival of pointed architecture" (007-018), and the villa became a place of pilgrimage whose ground plans were later catalogued in detail (007-021). Its deeper significance is literary. Beers stresses that "just as Walpole's Gothicism was an accidental 'sport' from his general virtuosity, so his romanticism was a casual outgrowth of his architectural amusements" (007-032). The taste for [[gothicism]] embodied in the house — see the chapter [[the-gothic-revival|The Gothic Revival]] — thus fed directly into the [[medievalism]] of the early Romantic movement, even as Walpole's "mock-gothic became something of a laughing-stock, after the true principles of medieval architecture were better understood" (007-018).

## Notable Episodes

The origin of [[the-castle-of-otranto]] was a dream at Strawberry Hill. Walpole told the Rev. William Cole that he "waked one morning... from a dream, of which all I could recover was, that I had thought myself in an ancient castle... and that, on the uppermost banister of a great staircase, I saw a gigantic hand in armor" (007-032). In the evening he sat down and began to write.
