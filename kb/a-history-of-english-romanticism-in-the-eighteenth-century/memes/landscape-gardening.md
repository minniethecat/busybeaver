# Landscape Gardening

## Definition

Landscape gardening, in Beers's history, is "the new or natural school" of garden design that rose in England beside the [[landscape-poetry|descriptive poetry]] of the early eighteenth century — an art that "preferred freedom, variety, irregularity, and naturalness to rule, monotony, uniformity, and artifice." (004-031) (004-056)

Its guiding maxim Beers takes from [[horace-walpole|Walpole]]'s account of William Kent, whose "ruling principle was that nature abhors a straight line." (004-060)

Kent, Walpole wrote, "leaped the fence and saw that all nature was a garden," so that "the gentle stream was taught to serpentine seemingly at its pleasure" in place of "canals, circular basins, and cascades." (004-060)

Beers presents it as "a purely English art," so identified with the nation that the Continent still calls such grounds "_Englische Garten_" and "_jardin Anglais_." (004-031) (004-032)

## Origins & Development

Beers dates the art's birth to the 1720s, quoting [[thomas-gray|Gray]], who wrote in 1763 that "it is not forty years since the art was born among us; and it is sure that there was nothing in Europe like it." (004-031)

Because Gray's "not forty years" reaches back to [[the-seasons|The Seasons]] (1726–30), Beers is willing "to acknowledge [[james-thomson|Thomson]] as, in a great measure, the father of the national school of landscape gardening." (004-031)

The style it displaced was the formal garden — "the old-fashioned Italian or French or Dutch garden" that the Augustan age "simply inherited . . . from the seventeenth century," the kind "Le Notre realized so brilliantly at Versailles." (004-034)

William Kent, "the architect and landscape painter," led the reform and "is said to have acknowledged that he caught his taste in gardening from the descriptive passages in [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]]." (004-057)

The movement grew intolerant of what it replaced: "the ruthless Capability Brown and his myrmidons laid waste many a prim but lovely old garden, with its avenues, terraces, and sun dials." (004-046)

## Key Figures & Works

The most interesting amateur, for Beers, is [[william-shenstone|Shenstone]], maker of the [[the-leasowes|Leasowes]], of whom Hugh Miller said that England "never produced a greater landscape gardener." (004-060)

Shenstone practised an "artificial-natural" gardening to match his verse, closing "every vista" with "a rustic bench, a root-house, a Gothic alcove, a grotto, a hermitage, a memorial urn." (004-067)

Kent, by contrast, carried imitation so far that Walpole reports he "went so far in his imitation of natural scenery as to plant _dead_ trees in Kensington Garden." (004-067)

The new taste was codified in prose and verse: [[horace-walpole|Walpole]]'s essay on gardening and Mason's poem were "quoted with approval" in Joseph Warton's "Essay on Pope," in a digression "on the new school of landscape gardening" that also names the Leasowes. (006-061)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers makes the garden a parable of the whole change in taste. There is, he writes, "a correspondence between this new taste for [[the-picturesque|picturesque]] gardening . . . and that new taste in literature which discarded the couplet for [[blank-verse|blank verse]]." (004-056)

The same impulse that freed the garden, he continues, "left the world of society for the solitudes of nature, and ultimately went, in search of fresh stimulus, to the remains of [[gothicism|the Gothic]] ages and the rude fragments of Norse and Celtic antiquity." (004-056)

Even the poets registered the analogy: in the young [[thomas-warton|Warton]]'s verse the challenge "Can Kent design like Nature?" stands beside the praise of "Shakspere's warblings wild." (005-010)

Beers thus reads the natural garden as one of the earliest and most visible signs of the [[romanticism|romantic]] return to nature. (004-031)

## Contrasts & Debates

The central contrast was national and philosophical — the English "objective" garden against the French "subjective" one. Beers quotes Schopenhauer that in the English garden "the will of Nature . . . is brought to the purest possible expression." (004-032)

The fashion had its scoffers. Dr. Johnson "was . . . at no pains to conceal his contempt for [Shenstone's] horticultural pursuits," doubting whether to "make water run where it will be seen . . . demands any great powers of mind." (004-062)

Even the reformers had begun in satire: Addison in the _Spectator_ and Pope in the _Guardian_ "ridiculed the excesses of the reigning mode" before the natural school displaced it. (004-034)
