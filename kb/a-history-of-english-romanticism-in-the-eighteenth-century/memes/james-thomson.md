# James Thomson

## Overview

James Thomson is, for Beers, the founder of the poetry of nature and one of "these pioneers in the 'return to nature'" whose work "must be taken into account in any complete history of the romantic movement" (004-002). His *Seasons* opened external nature to English verse, and his *Castle of Indolence* was the finest fruit of the Spenserian revival.

## Life

Beers records Thomson's beginnings in London. "In 1725 James Thomson, a young Scotchman, came to London to push his literary fortunes," and through his countryman Mallet "procured Thomson introductions into titled society, and helped him to bring out 'Winter,' the first installment of 'The Seasons,' which was published in 1726" (004-005). He continued to revise his great poem through his life, the final version of 1746 much enlarged (004-005).

## Works & Contribution

Thomson's two contributions to this history are *The Seasons* and *The Castle of Indolence*. Of the first, Beers stresses the contrast with Pope — "Thomson's style is florid and luxuriant, his numbers flowing and diffuse" (004-005) — and Thomson's naturalist's eye, writing "lovingly and with his 'eye upon the object'" so that "His images of external nature are never false and seldom vague, like Pope's" (004-007). Of the second, that "the only one among the professed scholars of Spenser who caught the glow and splendor of the master was James Thomson," whose *Castle of Indolence* has "the romantic iridescence ... which is lacking to the sharp contours of Augustan verse" (003-043, 003-044). See [[the-seasons]] and [[the-castle-of-indolence]].

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Thomson prefigures romanticism as the poet of the "return to nature." His *Seasons* embodied the desire "to escape into the fresh air and into freer conditions" (004-001), and its novelties — the spring showers, the summer thunderstorm, the winter night — "were new in English poetry" (004-006). In the *Castle of Indolence* he achieved the "atmosphere" and suggestive music the revival prized (003-044). His enormous fertility of influence — hardly a poet "from 1725-50 ... who was not in some manner guided or biased by Thomson" (004-005) — made him the age's great model. See [[landscape-poetry]] and [[blank-verse]].

## Reception & Influence

*The Seasons* was "no sooner read than universally admired" (004-005), and its "many imitations," at home and in Germany and France, are Beers's proof "that 'The Seasons' was the work of a fresh and independent genius" (004-005). Later taste found Thomson "unimaginative" beside Wordsworth, and Beers concedes "we are perhaps too apt to undervalue" his "simple descriptive or pictorial method," likening him "as a colorist, to Rubens" (004-006).
