# The History of English Poetry

## Overview

*The History of English Poetry* is Thomas Warton's great work of literary scholarship, which Beers calls "The greatest service ... that Thomas Warton rendered to the studies that he loved" (006-044). Its full title — "'History of English Poetry from the Twelfth to the Close of the Sixteenth Century'" (006-044) — announces its scope: the recovery, for the first time on a large scale, of the neglected medieval and Tudor past of English verse.

## Author & Date

The *History* is the work of Thomas Warton, published "in three volumes ... respectively in 1774, 1777, and 1781," with "The fragment of a fourth volume ... issued in 1790" (006-044). Its scholarly importance was such that it was repeatedly re-edited — "A revised edition in four volumes ... in 1824 ... corrected, augmented, and annotated by Ritson, Douce, Park," and again in 1871 (006-044). Beers pairs it with Tyrwhitt's *Chaucer* as the age's most consequential antiquarian labor: "Tyrwhitt's 'Chaucer' and Warton's 'History of English Poetry' had a real importance" (011-091).

## Summary / Content

The *History* surveyed English poetry "from the Twelfth to the Close of the Sixteenth Century" (006-044), drawing on the whole apparatus of manuscript and antiquarian scholarship — set by Beers among such source-collections as "Warton's 'History of English Poetry,' the 'Household Book of Edward'" and the rest (007-070). Its later volumes engaged the living controversies of the day: Warton devoted part "of the second volume of his 'History of English Poetry' (1778) to a review of the R[owley]" poems (010-039).

## Form & Style

The work is scholarship, not belles-lettres: a systematic literary history built on primary sources. Beers treats it as the mature form of the antiquarian impulse that elsewhere produced mere "general speculations," Warton here doing the harder work of survey and documentation (007-049, 006-044).

## Significance to Romanticism

The *History* gave the medieval revival its scholarly foundation. Beers records that with Percy's *Reliques* it "had promoted, beyond any" other force the turn to the Middle Ages (006-044), making accessible the poetry of "the Twelfth to the Close of the Sixteenth Century" that Augustan taste had ignored. By reading even Chatterton's Rowley poems within a scholarly frame (010-039), Warton's *History* helped make antiquarian learning the servant of the new poetic taste. See [[medievalism]] and [[the-school-of-warton]].

## Reception & Controversy

The *History*'s authority is measured by its long editorial afterlife — successive revisions by Price, Ritson, and others across the nineteenth century (006-044). Beers ranks it, with Tyrwhitt's *Chaucer*, as work of "real importance" (011-091), and notes its direct engagement with the Rowley controversy through Warton's review of the forged poems (010-039).
