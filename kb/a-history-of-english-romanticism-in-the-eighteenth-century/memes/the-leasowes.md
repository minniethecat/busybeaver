## Overview

The Leasowes was the small Shropshire estate of the poet [[william-shenstone]], which he transformed into one of the most celebrated ornamental farms of the age. For Beers it stands with Hagley and Stowe as a landmark in the eighteenth-century turn toward natural, irregular gardening — the taste that runs parallel to the rise of [[landscape-poetry]] and [[the-picturesque]].

## Description & Location

Shenstone "inherited a Shropshire farm, called the Leasowes, in the parish of Hales Owen and an annuity of some three hundred pounds" on coming of age (004-061). "Of an indolent, retiring, and somewhat melancholy temperament," he gave up any professional career and "settled down upon his property and, about the year 1745, began to turn it into a _fermé ornée_" (004-061), an ornamented farm of some three hundred acres (004-053). Beers notes the "miniature wilderness at the Leasowes" as a distinctly modern kind of contrived naturalness (005-009).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

"The fame of the Leasowes attracted visitors from all parts of the country" — "literary men like Spence, Home, and Dodsley; picturesque tourists, who came out of curiosity; and titled persons, who came, or sent their gardeners, to obtain hints for laying out their grounds" (004-064). Its reputation was such that visitors to neighbouring [[hagley]] would ask instead to see Shenstone's place (004-062). Dodsley's "A Description of the Leasowes" became a set text of the new garden fashion (004-098). In Beers's argument this reshaping of ground — the rejection of formal, geometric layout for winding walks and cultivated wildness — belongs to the same sensibility that produced the natural feeling of the [[the-landscape-poets|landscape poets]], among whom Shenstone, author of [[the-schoolmistress]], is himself counted.
