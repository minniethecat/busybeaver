## Overview

St. Mary Redcliffe is the great parish church of Bristol under whose shadow [[chatterton]] was born and from which his forgeries sprang. Beers frames its role in an exact parallel with Walpole's villa: "Just as truly as 'The Castle of Otranto' was sprung from [[strawberry-hill|Strawberry Hill]], the Rowley poems were born of St. Mary's Church" (010-002). It is the physical cradle of [[the-rowley-poems]] and, with them, of one of the eighteenth century's most influential experiments in [[medievalism]].

## Description & Location

The church stands on Redcliffe Hill in Bristol. Beers describes it — quoting Chatterton's own phrase, "the pride of Bristowe and the western land" — as "one of the finest parish churches in England," a rich specimen of late Gothic or "decorated" style, its building or restoration dating from the middle of the fifteenth century (010-003). Chatterton's father was sub-chanter in Bristol Cathedral, and the family's house and school in Pile Street "were only a few yards from Redcliffe Church"; there Chatterton "was born, under the eaves almost of the sanctuary" (010-003). His uncle by marriage, Richard Phillips, had become sexton in 1748, so "the boy had the run of the aisles and transepts" among the stone effigies of knights (010-003). The church had been founded or restored by the merchant William Canynge, several times mayor of Bristol (010-008).

## Association with the Romantic Revival

The decisive detail is the muniment room "over the north porch of St. Mary Redcliffe's," where "several old chests filled with parchments" — church-wardens' accounts, inventories, and similar documents — had long lain neglected (010-014). One, "Master Canynge's coffer," had been broken open, and Chatterton's father had carried a number of the parchments home "and used them to cover copy-books"; the boy, his eye "attracted by these yellow sheep-skins, with their antique script," appropriated and hoarded them (010-014). From this hoard grew the fiction of the monk "Rowley," whose supposed documents Chatterton later supplied to the antiquary William Barrett for his "History of Bristol" (010-018). The church thus became the seedbed of a romantic [[literary-forgery]] that fed the age's hunger for a medieval past.
