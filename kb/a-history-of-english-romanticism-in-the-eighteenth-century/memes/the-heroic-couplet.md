# The Heroic Couplet

## Definition

The heroic couplet is the rhymed, decasyllabic distich that Beers treats as the classical school's standard verse — "the smooth distichs of heroic verse which Pope was the first to perfect." (002-044)

In its finished form the couplet was closed: Beers cites Dryden's praise of Waller as the poet who "first showed us how to conclude the sense most commonly in distichs," whereas in earlier verse the sense "runs on for so many lines together, that the reader is out of breath to overtake it." (002-039)

Its masters gave it an epigrammatic, antithetical finish; Beers speaks of "Pope's antithetical trick" and of a style whose "unrelaxing brilliancy and compression" wearied later readers. (004-015) (003-017)

Within Augustan poetics rhyme "meant constraint where blank verse meant freedom," so the couplet became the emblem of a disciplined, "correct" poetry. (002-039)

## Origins & Development

Beers dates the couplet's rise to the mid-seventeenth century: Gosse "pointed out couplets of Waller, written as early as 1623, which have the formal precision of Pope's," while Denham's Thames passage in "Cooper's Hill" (1642) "anticipates the best performance of Augustan verse." (002-019)

The classical tradition held that its "excellence and dignity" were, in Dryden's words, "never fully known till Mr. Waller taught it," making Waller the first correct versifier. (002-039)

Pope's own triplet traces the descent — "Waller was smooth, but Dryden taught to join / The varying verse, the full-resounding line" — assigning to Dryden the couplet's "long majestic march and energy divine." (006-067)

Pope carried the form to its height and was praised by Johnson for sparingly using "the triplets and alexandrines by which Dryden sought to vary the monotony of the couplet." (002-039)

By the second quarter of the eighteenth century its supremacy broke: from Thomson's "Winter" (1726) to Gray's "Elegy" (1751), Beers observes, the leading poems were of a new type, with "not one composed in the heroic couplet hitherto so vigorously imposed on serious verse." (004-003)

## Key Figures & Works

Pope stands as the couplet's supreme master, so identified with the form that in Thomas Warton's monody "Musaeus" the shade of Pope answers "in heroic couplets" while the other mourners speak blank verse. (005-020)

Beers roots the form's authority in Waller and Dryden — "the smooth distichs of heroic verse which Pope was the first to perfect." (002-044)

The counter-roll is made of poets who laid the couplet aside: [[the-seasons|The Seasons]] and [[night-thoughts|Night Thoughts]] were "written in blank verse," [[the-castle-of-indolence|The Castle of Indolence]] and [[the-schoolmistress|The Schoolmistress]] "in Spenserian stanza," and [[grongar-hill|Grongar Hill]] "in octosyllabics," while the early odes of [[thomas-gray|Gray]] and [[william-collins|Collins]] used "a great variety of simple but novel lyric measures." (004-003)

Even in abandonment its discipline lingered: [[james-thomson|Thomson]]'s blank verse in The Seasons, Beers writes, "is a blank verse which has been passed through the strainer of the heroic couplet." (004-015)

## Role in the Romantic Revival

Beers makes the abandonment of the couplet a symptom of the deeper [[romanticism|romantic]] change. The general shift in verse forms, he writes, "was an outward sign of the revolution in poetic feeling," the imitation of Spenser being "only one instance of a readiness to lay aside the heroic couplet." (004-003)

He frames the reversal as a paradox of the age:

> ...that curiously topsy-turvy condition of things in which rhyme was a mark of the classic, and blank verse of the romantic. (005-001)

Because rhyme signified the classic and [[blank-verse|blank verse]] the romantic, Milton's example told: "Paradise Lost" "did much to keep alive the tradition of English blank verse through a period remarkable for its bigoted devotion to rhyme, and especially to the heroic couplet." (005-002)

Beers ties the shift to a broader movement of taste, finding a correspondence between the new picturesque gardening and "that new taste in literature which discarded the couplet for blank verse, or for various stanza forms." (004-056)

## Contrasts & Debates

The couplet's chief rivals were blank verse and the [[spenserianism|Spenserian stanza]]. To ears trained on "the tinkle of the couplet, with its monotonously recurring rhyme, its inevitable caesura, its narrow imprisonment of the sense," Beers writes, the amplitude of [[edmund-spenser|Spenser]]'s stanza in [[the-faerie-queene|The Faërie Queene]] came "as a relief." (003-017)

The couplet embodied the [[classicism|classical]] cult of "correctness": the age sought "a formula of correctness in all the arts," and William Walsh advised the youthful Pope to excel his predecessors "by correctness." (002-029) (002-036)

Beers even draws an analogy between the couplet and the formal French garden, seeing a kinship between its "trim regularity and artificial smoothness, and the couplets which Pope wrote." (004-038)

Against this "correctness" the romanticizing critics pressed for freedom; Joseph Warton, glossing Pope's Waller-and-Dryden triplet, "expresses a preference for blank verse over rhyme, in long poems on subjects of a dignified kind." (006-067)
