[008-002] 91. _Q._--Will you describe the construction of the boilers used in steam

[008-003] vessels?

[008-004] _A._--These are of two classes, flue boilers and tubular boilers, but the

[008-005] latter are now most used. In the flue boiler the furnaces are set within

[008-006] the boiler, and the flues proceeding from them wind backwards and forwards

[008-007] within the boiler until finally they meet and enter the chimney. Figs. 6,

[008-008] 7, and 8 are different views of the flue boilers of the steamer Forth.

[008-009] There are 4 boilers (as shown in plan, Fig. 6), with 3 furnaces in each, or

[008-010] 12 furnaces in all. Fig. 7 is an elevation of 2 boilers, the one to the

[008-011] right being the front view, and that to the left a transverse section. Fig.

[008-012] 8 is a longitudinal section through 2 boilers. The direction of the arrows

[008-013] in plan and longitudinal section, will explain the direction of the smoke

[008-014] current.

[008-017] 92. _Q._--Is this arrangement different from that obtaining in tubular

[008-018] boilers?

[008-019] _A._--In tubular boilers, the smoke after leaving the furnace just passes

[008-020] once through a number of small tubes and then enters the chimney. These

[008-021] tubes are sometimes of brass, and they are usually about 3 inches in

[008-022] diameter, and 6 or 7 feet long.

[008-026] Figs. 9, 10, and 11 represent a marine tubular boiler; fig. 9 being a

[008-027] vertical longitudinal section, fig. 10 half a front elevation and half a

[008-028] transverse section, and fig. 11 half a back elevation and half a transverse

[008-029] section near the end. There is a projecting part on the top of the boiler

[008-030] called the "steam chest," of which the purpose is to retain for the use of

[008-031] the cylinder a certain supply of steam in a quiescent state, in order that

[008-032] it may have time to clear itself of foam or spray. A steam chest is a usual

[008-033] part of all marine boilers. In fig. 9 A is the furnace, B the steam chest,

[008-034] and C the smoke box which opens into the chimney. The front of the smoke

[008-035] box is usually closed by doors which may be opened when necessary to sweep

[008-036] the soot out of the tubes.

[008-056] THE ENGINE.

[008-057] 93. _Q._--The steam passes from the boiler through, the steam pipe into the

[008-058] cylinder of the engine?

[008-059] _A._--And presses up and down the piston alternately, being admitted

[008-060] alternately above and below the piston by suitable valves provided for that

[008-061] purpose.

[008-062] 94. _Q._--This reciprocating motion is all that is required in a pumping

[008-063] engine?

[008-064] _A._--The prevailing form of the pumping engine consists of a great beam

[008-065] vibrating on a centre like the beam of a pair of scales, and the cylinder

[008-066] is in connection with one end of the beam and the pump stands at the other

[008-067] end. The pump end of the beam is usually loaded, so as to cause it to

[008-068] preponderate when the engine is at rest; and the whole effort of the steam

[008-069] is employed in overcoming this preponderance until a stroke is performed,

[008-070] when, the steam being shut off, the heavy end of the beam again falls and

[008-071] the operation is repeated.

[008-072] 95. _Q._--in the double-acting engine the piston is pushed by the steam

[008-073] both ways, whereas in the single-acting engine it is only pushed one way?

[008-074] _A._--The structure and action of a double-acting land engine of the kind

[008-075] introduced by Mr. Watt, will be understood by a reference to the annexed

[008-076] figure (fig. 20), where an engine of this kind is shown in section. A is

[008-077] the cylinder in which a movable piston, T, is forced alternately up and

[008-078] down by the alternate admission, to each side, of the steam from the

[008-079] boiler. The piston, by means of a rod called the piston rod, gives motion

[008-080] to the beam V W, which by means of a heavy bar, P, called the connecting

[008-081] rod, moves the crank, Q, and with it the fly wheel, X, from which the

[008-082] machinery to be driven derives its motion.

[008-083] 96. _Q._--Where does the steam enter from the boiler?

[008-084] [Illustration: Fig. 20.]

[008-085] _A._--At the steam pipe, B. The throttle valve in that pipe is an

[008-086] elliptical plate of metal swivelling on a spindle passing through its edge

[008-087] from side to side, and by turning which more or less the opening through

[008-088] the pipe will be more or less closed. The extent to which this valve is

[008-089] opened or closed is determined by the governor, D, the balls of which, as

[008-090] they collapse or expand, move up or down a collar on the governor spindle,

[008-091] which motion is communicated to the throttle valve by suitable rods and

[008-092] bell-cranks. The governor, it will be seen, consists substantially of two

[008-093] heavy balls attached to arms fixed upon an upright shaft, which is kept in

[008-094] revolution by means of a cord driven by a pulley on the fly wheel shaft.

[008-095] The velocity with which the balls of the governor revolve being

[008-096] proportional to that of the fly wheel, it will follow, that if by reason of

[008-097] too rapid a supply of steam, an undue speed be given to the fly wheel, and

[008-098] therefore to the balls, a divergence of the balls will take place to an

[008-099] extent corresponding to the excess of velocity, and this movement being

[008-100] communicated to the throttle valve it will be partly closed (see fig. 1),

[008-101] the supply of steam to the engine will be diminished, and the velocity of

[008-102] its motion will be reduced. If, on the other hand, the motion of the engine

[008-103] is slower than is requisite, owing to a deficient supply of steam through

[008-104] B, then the balls, not being sufficiently affected by centrifugal force,

[008-105] will fall towards the vertical spindle, and the throttle valve, C, will be

[008-106] more fully opened, whereby a more ample supply of steam will be admitted to

[008-107] the cylinder, and the speed of the engine will be increased to the

[008-108] requisite extent.

[008-109] 97. _Q._--The piston must be made to fit the cylinder accurately so as to

[008-110] prevent the passage of steam?

[008-111] _A._--The piston is accurately fitted to the cylinder, and made to move in

[008-112] it steam tight by a packing of hemp driven tightly into a groove or recess

[008-113] round the edge of the piston, and which is squeezed down by an iron ring

[008-114] held by screws. The piston divides the cylinder into two compartments,

[008-115] between which there is no communication by which steam or any other elastic

[008-116] fluid can pass. A casing set beside the cylinder contains the valves, by

[008-117] means of which the steam which impels the piston is admitted and withdrawn,

[008-118] as the piston commences its motion in each direction. The upper steam box

[008-119] B, is divided into three compartments by two valves. Above the upper steam

[008-120] valve V, is a compartment communicating with the steam pipe B. Below the

[008-121] lower valve E is another compartment communicating with a pipe called the

[008-122] eduction pipe, which leads downwards from the cylinder to the condenser, in

[008-123] which vessel the steam is condensed by a jet of cold water. By the valve V,

[008-124] a communication may be opened or closed between the boiler and the top of

[008-125] the cylinder, so as to permit or prevent a supply of steam from the one to

[008-126] pass to the other. By the valve E a communication may be open or closed

[008-127] between the top of the cylinder and the condenser, so that the steam in the

[008-128] top compartment of the cylinder may either be permitted to escape into the

[008-129] condenser, or may be confined to the cylinder. The continuation of the

[008-130] steam pipe leads to the lower steam box B', which, like the upper, is

[008-131] divided into three compartments by two valves V' and E', and the action of

[008-132] the lower valves is in all respects the same as that of the upper.

[008-133] 98. _Q._--Are all these valves connected together so that they act

[008-134] simultaneously?

[008-135] _A._--The four valves V, E, V', E' are connected by rods to a single handle

[008-136] H, which handle is moved alternately up and down by means of pins or

[008-137] tappets, placed on the rod which works the air pump. When the handle H is

[008-138] pressed down, the levers in connexion with it open the upper exhausting

[008-139] valve E, and the lower steam valve V', and close the upper steam valve V

[008-140] and the lower exhausting valve E'. On the other hand, when the handle H is

[008-141] pressed up it opens the upper steam valve V and the lower exhausting valve

[008-142] E', and at the same time closes the upper exhausting valve E, and the lower

[008-143] steam valve V'.

[008-144] 99. _Q._--Where is the condenser situated?

[008-145] _A._--The condenser K is immerged in a cistern of cold water. At its side

[008-146] there is a tube I, for the admission of water to condense the steam, and

[008-147] which is governed by a cock, by opening which to any required extent, a jet

[008-148] of cold water may be made to play in the condenser. From the bottom of the

[008-149] condenser a short pipe leads to the air pump J, and in this pipe there is a

[008-150] flap valve, called the foot valve, opening towards the air pump. The air

[008-151] pump is a pump set in the same cistern of cold water that holds the

[008-152] condenser, and it is fitted with a piston or bucket worked by the rod L,

[008-153] attached to the great beam, and fitted with a valve opening upwards in the

[008-154] manner of a common sucking pump. The upper part of the air pump

[008-155] communicates with a small cistern S, called the hot well, through a valve

[008-156] opening outwards and called the delivery valve. A pump M, called the hot

[008-157] water pump, lifts hot water out of the hot well to feed the boiler, and

[008-158] another pump N lifts cold water from a well or other source of supply, to

[008-159] maintain the supply of water to the cold water cistern, in which the

[008-160] condenser and air pump are placed.

[008-161] 100. Q.--Will you explain now the manner in which the engine acts?

[008-162] A.--The piston being supposed to be at the top of the cylinder, the handle

[008-163] H will be raised by the lower pin or tappet on the air pump rod, and the

[008-164] valves V and E' will be opened, and at the same time the other pair of

[008-165] valves V' and E will be closed. Steam will therefore be admitted above the

[008-166] piston and the steam or air which had previously filled the cylinder below

[008-167] the piston will be drawn off to the condenser. It will there encounter the

[008-168] jet of cold water, which is kept constantly playing there by keeping the

[008-169] cock I sufficiently open. It will thus be immediately condensed or reduced

[008-170] to water, and the cylinder below the piston will have a vacuum in it. The

[008-171] steam therefore admitted from the steam pipe through the open valve V to

[008-172] the top of the cylinder, not being resisted by pressure below, will press

[008-173] the piston to the bottom of the cylinder. As it approaches that position,

[008-174] the handle H will be struck down by the upper pin or tappet on the air pump

[008-175] rod, and the valves V and E', previously open, will be closed, while the

[008-176] valves V' and E, previously closed, will be opened. The steam which has

[008-177] just pressed down the piston, and which now fills the cylinder above the

[008-178] piston, will then flow off, through the open valve E, to the condenser,

[008-179] where it will be immediately condensed by the jet of cold water; and steam

[008-180] from the boiler, admitted through the open valve V', will fill the cylinder

[008-181] below the piston, and press the piston upwards. When the piston has reached

[008-182] the top of the cylinder, the lower pin on the air pump rod will have struck

[008-183] the handle upwards, and will thereby have closed the valves V' and E, and

[008-184] opened the valves V and E'. The piston will then be in the same situation

[008-185] as in the commencement, and will again descend, and so will continue to be

[008-186] driven up and down by the steam.
