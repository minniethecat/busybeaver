[042-001] 487. _Q._--How is the outer bearing of the paddle wheels supplied with

[042-002] tallow?

[042-003] _A._--The bearing at the outer end of the paddle shaft is sometimes

[042-004] supplied with tallow, forced into a hole in the plummer block cover, as in

[042-005] the case of water wheels; but for vessels intended to perform long voyages,

[042-006] it is preferable to have a pipe leading down to the oil cup above the

[042-007] journal from the top of the paddle box, through which pipe oil may at any

[042-008] time be supplied.

[042-009] 488. _Q._--Will you explain the method of putting engines into a steam

[042-010] vessel?

[042-011] _A._--As an illustration of this operation it may be advisable to take the

[042-012] case of a side lever engine, and the method of proceeding is as follows:--

[042-013] First measure across from the inside of paddle bearers to the centre of the

[042-014] ship, to make sure that the central line, running in a fore and aft

[042-015] direction on the deck or beams, usually drawn by the carpenter, is really

[042-016] in the centre. Stretch a line across between the paddle bearers in the

[042-017] direction of the shaft; to this line, in the centre of the ship where the

[042-018] fore and aft mark has been made, apply a square with arms six or eight feet

[042-019] long, and bring a line stretched perpendicularly from the deck to the

[042-020] keelson, accurately to the edge of the square: the lower point of the line

[042-021] where it touches the keelson will be immediately beneath the marks made

[042-022] upon the deck. If this point does not come in the centre of the keelson, it

[042-023] will be better to shift it a little, so as to bring it to the centre,

[042-024] altering the mark upon the deck correspondingly, provided either paddle

[042-025] shaft will admit of this being done--one of the paddle brackets being

[042-026] packed behind with wood, to give it an additional projection from the side

[042-027] of the paddle bearer. Continue the line fore and aft upon the keelson as

[042-028] nearly as can be judged in the centre of the ship; stretch another line

[042-029] fore and aft through the mark upon the deck, and look it out of winding

[042-030] with the line upon the keelson. Fix upon any two points equally distant

[042-031] from the centre, in the line stretched transversely in the direction of the

[042-032] shaft; and from those points, as centres, and with any convenient radius,

[042-033] sweep across the fore and aft line to see that the two are at right angles;

[042-034] and, if not, shift the transverse line a little to make them so. From the

[042-035] transverse line next let fall a line upon each outside keelson, bringing

[042-036] the edge of the square to the line, the other edge resting on the keelson.

[042-037] A point will thus be got on each outside keelson, perpendicularly beneath

[042-038] the transverse line running in the direction of the shaft, and a line drawn

[042-039] between those two points will be directly below the shaft. To this line the

[042-040] line of the shaft marked on the sole plate has to be brought, care being

[042-041] taken, at the same time, that the right distance is preserved between the

[042-042] fore and aft line upon the sole plate, and the fore and aft line upon the

[042-043] central keelson.

[042-044] 489. _Q._--Of course the keelsons have first to be properly prepared?

[042-045] _A._--In a wooden vessel, before any part of the machinery is put in, the

[042-046] keelsons should be dubbed fair and straight, and be looked out of winding

[042-047] by means of two straight edges. The art of placing engines in a ship is

[042-048] more a piece of plain common sense than any other feat in engineering, and

[042-049] every man of intelligence may easily settle a method of procedure for

[042-050] himself. Plumb lines and spirit levels, it is obvious, cannot be employed

[042-051] on board a vessel, and the problem consists in so placing the sole plates,

[042-052] without these aids, that the paddle shaft will not stand awry across the

[042-053] vessel, nor be carried forward beyond its place by the framing shouldering

[042-054] up more than was expected. As a plumb line cannot be used, recourse must be

[042-055] had to a square; and it will signify nothing at what angle with the deck

[042-056] the keelsons run, so long as the line of the shaft across the keelsons is

[042-057] square down from the shaft centre. The sole plates being fixed, there is no

[042-058] difficulty in setting the other parts of the engine in their proper places

[042-059] upon them. The paddle wheels must be hung from the top of the paddle box to

[042-060] enable the shaft to be rove through them, and the cross stays between the

[042-061] engines should be fixed in when the vessel is afloat. To try whether the

[042-062] shafts are in a line, turn the paddle wheels, and try if the distance

[042-063] between the cranks is the same at the upper and under, and the two

[042-064] horizontal centres; if not, move the end of the paddle shaft up or down,

[042-065] backward or forward, until the distance between the cranks at all the four

[042-066] centres is the same.

[042-067] 490. _Q._--In what manner are the engines of a steam vessel secured to the

[042-068] hull?

[042-069] _A._--The engines of a steamer are secured to the hull by means of bolts

[042-070] called holding down bolts, and in wooden vessels a good deal of trouble is

[042-071] caused by these bolts, which are generally made of iron. Sometimes they go

[042-072] through the bottom of the ship, and at other times they merely go through

[042-073] the keelson,--a recess being made in the floor or timbers to admit of the

[042-074] introduction of a nut. The iron, however, wears rapidly away in both cases,

[042-075] even though the bolts are tinned; and it has been found the preferable

[042-076] method to make such of the bolts as pass through the bottom, or enter the

[042-077] bilge, of Muntz's metal, or of copper. In a side lever engine, four Muntz's

[042-078] metal bolts may be put through the bottom at the crank end of the framing

[042-079] of each engine, four more at the main centre, and four more at the

[042-080] cylinder, making twelve through bolts to each engine; and it is more

[042-081] convenient to make these bolts with a nut at each end, as in that case the

[042-082] bolts may be dropped down from the inside, and the necessity is obviated of

[042-083] putting the vessel on very high blocks in the dock, in order to give room

[042-084] to put the bolts up from the bottom. The remainder of the holding down

[042-085] bolts may be of iron, and may, by means of a square neck, be screwed into

[042-086] the timber of the keelsons as wood screws--the upper part being furnished

[042-087] with a nut which may be screwed down upon the sole plate, so soon as the

[042-088] wood screw portion is in its place. If the cylinder be a fixed one it

[042-089] should be bolted down to the sole plate by as many bolts as are employed to

[042-090] attach the cylinder cover, and they should be of copper or brass, in any

[042-091] situation that is not easily accessible.

[042-092] 491. _Q._--If the engines become loose, how do you refix them?

[042-093] _A._--It is difficult to fix engines effectually which have once begun to

[042-094] work in the ship, for in time the surface of the keelsons on which the

[042-095] engines bear becomes worn uneven, and the engines necessarily rock upon it.

[042-096] As a general rule, the bolts attaching the engines to the keelsons are too

[042-097] few and of too large a diameter: it would be preferable to have smaller

[042-098] bolts, and a greater number of them. In addition to the bolts going through

[042-099] the keelsons or the vessel's bottom, there should be a large number of wood

[042-100] screws securing the sole plate to the keelson, and a large number of bolts

[042-101] securing the various parts of the engine to the sole plate. In iron

[042-102] vessels, holding down bolts passing through the bottom are not expedient;

[042-103] and there the engine has merely to be secured to the iron plate of the

[042-104] keelsons, which are made hollow to admit of a more effectual attachment.

[042-105] 492. _Q._--What are the proper proportions of bolts?

[042-106] _A._--In well formed bolts, the spiral groove penetrates about one twelfth

[042-107] of the diameter of the cylinder round which it winds, so that the diameter

[042-108] of the solid cylinder which remains is five sixths of the diameter over the

[042-109] thread. If the strain to which iron may be safely subjected in machinery is

[042-110] one fifteenth of its utmost strength, or 4,000 lbs. on the square inch,

[042-111] then 2,180 lbs. may be sustained by a screw an inch in diameter, at the

[042-112] outside of the threads. The strength of the holding down bolts may easily

[042-113] be computed, when the elevating force of the piston or main centre is

[042-114] known; but it is expedient very much to exceed this strength in practice,

[042-115] on account of the elasticity of the keelsons, the liability to corrosion,

[042-116] and other causes.

[042-117] THE LOCOMOTIVE ENGINE.

[042-118] 493. _Q._--What is the amount of tractive force requisite to draw carriages

[042-119] on railways?

[042-120] _A._--Upon well formed railways with carriages of good construction, the

[042-121] average tractive force required for low speeds is about 7-1/2 lbs. per ton,

[042-122] or 1/300th of the load, though in some experimental cases, where particular

[042-123] care was taken to obtain a favorable result, the tractive force has been

[042-124] reduced as low as 1/500th of the load. At low speeds the whole of the

[042-125] tractive force is expended in overcoming the friction, which is made up

[042-126] partly of the friction of attrition in the axles, and partly of the rolling

[042-127] friction, or the obstruction to the rolling of the wheels upon the rail.

[042-128] The rolling friction is very small when the surfaces are smooth, and in the

[042-129] case of railway carriages does not exceed 1/1000th. of the load; whereas

[042-130] the draught on common roads of good construction, which is chiefly made up

[042-131] of the rolling friction, is as much as 1/36th of the load.

[042-132] 494._Q._--In reference to friction you have already stated that the

[042-133] friction of iron sliding upon brass, which has been oiled and then wiped

[042-134] dry, so that no film of oil is interposed, is about 1/11th of the pressure,

[042-135] but that in machines in actual operation, where there is a film of oil

[042-136] between the rubbing surfaces, the friction is only about one third of this

[042-137] amount, or 1/33d of the weight.  How then can the tractive resistance of

[042-138] locomotives at low speeds, which you say is entirely made up of friction,

[042-139] be so little as 1/500th. of the weight?

[042-140] _A._--I did not state that the resistance to traction was 1/500th of the

[042-141] weight upon an average--to which condition the answer given to a previous

[042-142] question must be understood to apply--but I stated that the average

[042-143] traction was about 1/300th of the load, which nearly agrees with my former

[042-144] statement. If the total friction be 1/300th of the load, and the rolling

[042-145] friction be 1/1000th of the load, then the friction of attrition must be

[042-146] 1/429th of the load; and if the diameter of the wheels be 36 in., and the

[042-147] diameter of the axles be 3 in., which are common proportions, the friction

[042-148] of attrition must be increased in the proportion of 36 to 3, or 12 times,

[042-149] to represent the friction of the rubbing surface when moving with the

[042-150] velocity of the carriage, 12/429ths are about 1/35th of the load, which

[042-151] does not differ much from the proportion of 1/33d as previously determined.

[042-152] 495. _Q._--What is the amount of adhesion of the wheels upon the rails?

[042-153] _A._--The adhesion of the wheels upon the rails is about 1/5th of the

[042-154] weight when the rails are clean, or either perfectly wet or perfectly dry;

[042-155] but when the rails are half wet or greasy, the adhesion is not more than

[042-156] 1/10th or 1/12th of the weight or pressure upon the wheels. The weight of a

[042-157] locomotive of modern construction varies from 20 to 25 tons.
