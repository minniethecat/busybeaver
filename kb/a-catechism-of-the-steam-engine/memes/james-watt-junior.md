# James Watt Jr.

**James Watt Jr.** (1769–1848) was the son of [[james-watt|James Watt]] and partner in the firm of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]]. He managed the Soho Foundry and was instrumental in developing the firm's [[marine-engine|marine engine]] business during the transition from land-based pumping engines to marine steam propulsion.

## Career and Contributions

Watt Jr. took over the day-to-day management of the Soho Foundry from his father, overseeing the construction of engines that adhered to the [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] design standards — wagon boilers, [[condensation|condensing engine]]s, and the distinctive parallel-motion valve gear. The firm's rules for dimensioning boilers and fly-wheels, developed from decades of practical experience, remained authoritative references throughout the nineteenth century and are cited by [[john-bourne|Bourne]] (003-024, 003-026).

Under his direction, [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] expanded into [[marine-engine|marine engine]] construction, competing with newer firms such as [[maudslay-sons-and-field|Maudslay Sons and Field]] and [[john-penn|John Penn & Son]]. The firm's engines were installed in numerous naval and commercial vessels.

## Legacy

James Watt Jr.'s stewardship ensured that the [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] engineering tradition continued well into the nineteenth century. The design standards and manufacturing practices established at Soho under his management influenced engine construction long after the original [[james-watt|Watt]] patents had expired.
