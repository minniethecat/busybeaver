# Blow-Off

**Blow-off** is the operation of discharging a portion of the water from a [[Boiler|boiler]] while it is under steam, to expel accumulated sediment, brine, and loose scale. (065-142) (065-143) It is performed through a blow-off cock — a valve fitted at the lowest part of the boiler, typically at the bottom of the [[furnace|fire box]] or shell. (034-111) (034-112)

## Purpose

Water continuously evaporated in a boiler leaves behind dissolved salts and solid impurities. If not periodically removed, these form a hard incrustation or scale on the heating surfaces, which reduces heat transfer, causes overheating of the metal plates, and can lead to dangerous bulging or collapse of the flues. Regular blow-off prevents the concentration of these impurities from reaching harmful levels. (065-147) (065-148)

## Blow-Off Frequency

| [[boiler|Boiler]] Type | Recommended Blow-Off Interval |
|-------------|------------------------------|
| [[flue|Flue]] boilers | Twice per watch (every 4 hours) (065-144) (065-145) |
| Tubular boilers | Once every 20 minutes (065-146) |

Tubular boilers, with their smaller water spaces and higher evaporation rates, concentrate impurities much faster than flue boilers and therefore demand far more frequent blow-off. (065-145) (065-146) The amount of blow-off should in every case be sufficient to effectually prevent any injurious amount of incrustation. (065-147) (065-148)

## Determining the Blow-Off Point

In [[Marine-Engine|marine]] boilers operating with salt water feed, a practical method was used to determine the safe concentration limit. A weighted phial was floated in fresh water and its floatation line marked; then it was floated in salt water and the second floatation line marked; an equal height above the salt water mark indicated the blow-off point — the brine density at which the boiler must be blown off to prevent salt precipitation and scale formation. (065-138) (065-141)

## Emergency Use

The blow-off cock also served as an emergency safety device. If the [[Safety-Valve|safety valves]] became stuck and steam pressure rose uncontrollably — indicated at once by the [[Steam-Gauge|steam gauge]] — the blow-off cocks of the boiler should be opened immediately, along with the blow-through valves of the engine, and the fires raked out. (025-112) (025-113) (025-114)

> "If the flues be already red hot, on no account must cold water be suffered to enter the boiler, but the heat should be maintained in the furnaces, and the blow off cocks be opened, or the mud hole doors loosened, so as to let all the water escape." (025-128) (025-130)

This emergency procedure allowed the boiler to be depressurised without the catastrophic steam generation that would result from cold water touching red-hot metal.

## Practical Considerations

The blow-off cock must be positioned so that the engine driver can reach it while the engine is at work. (034-113) It must not discharge the blown-off water among the machinery, as the water carries abrasive sand and scale particles that would damage bearings and sliding surfaces. (034-114) (034-115) In marine practice, the blow-off was typically directed overboard.
