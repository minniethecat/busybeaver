[063-001] 702. _Q._--Will you explain the next operation which a cylinder undergoes?

[063-002] _A._--The next stage is the boring; and in boring cylinders of 74 inches

[063-003] diameter, the boring bar must move so as to make one revolution in about

[063-004] 4-1/2 minutes, at which speed the cutters will move at the rate of about

[063-005] 5 feet per minute. In boring brass, the speed must be slower; the common

[063-006] rate at which the tool moves in boring brass air pumps is about 3 feet per

[063-007] minute. If this speed be materially exceeded the tool will be spoiled, and

[063-008] the pump made taper. The speed proper for boring a cylinder will answer for

[063-009] boring the brass air pump of the same engine. A brass air pump of 36-1/2

[063-010] inches diameter requires the bar to make one turn in about three minutes,

[063-011] which is also the speed proper for a cylinder 60 inches in diameter. To

[063-012] bore a brass air pump 36-1/2 inches in diameter requires a week, an iron

[063-013] one requires 48 hours, and a copper one 24 hours. In turning a malleable

[063-014] iron shaft 12-3/4 inches in diameter, the shaft should make about five

[063-015] turns per minute, which is equivalent to a speed in the tool of about 16

[063-016] feet per minute; but this speed may be exceeded if soap and water be

[063-017] plentifully run on the point of the tool. A boring mill, of which the speed

[063-018] may be varied from one turn in six minutes to twenty-five turns in one

[063-019] minute, will be suitable for all ordinary wants that can occur in practice.

[063-020] 703. _Q._--Are there any precautions necessary to be observed in order that

[063-021] the boring may be truly effected?

[063-022] _A._--In fixing a cylinder into the boring mill, great care must be taken

[063-023] that it is not screwed down unequally; and indeed it will be impossible to

[063-024] bore a large cylinder in a horizontal mill without being oval, unless the

[063-025] cylinder be carefully gauged when standing on end, and be set up by screws

[063-026] when laid in the mill until it again assumes its original form. A large

[063-027] cylinder will inevitably become oval if laid upon its side; and if while

[063-028] under the tension due to its own weight it be bored round, it will become

[063-029] oval again when set upon end. If the bottom be cast in, the cylinder will

[063-030] be probably found to be round at one end and oval at the other, unless a

[063-031] vertical boring mill be employed, or the precautions here suggested be

[063-032] adopted.

[063-033] 704. _Q._--Does the boring tool make the cylinder sufficiently smooth for

[063-034] the reception of the piston?

[063-035] _A._--Many engine makers give no other finish to their cylinders; but

[063-036] Messrs. Penn grind their cylinders after they are bored, by laying them on

[063-037] their side, and rubbing a piece of lead, with a cross iron handle like that

[063-038] of a rolling stone, and smeared with emery and oil, backward and forward--

[063-039] the cylinder being gradually turned round so as to subject every part

[063-040] successively to the operation. The lead by which this grinding is

[063-041] accomplished is cast in the Cylinder, whereby it is formed of the right

[063-042] curve; but the part of the cylinder in which it is cast should be

[063-043] previously heated by a hot iron, else the metal may be cracked by the

[063-044] sudden heat.

[063-045] 705. _Q._--How are the parts of a piston fitted together so as to be

[063-046] perfectly steam tight?

[063-047] _A._--The old practice was to depend chiefly upon grinding as the means of

[063-048] making the rings tight upon the piston or upon one another; but scraping is

[063-049] now chiefly relied on. Some makers, however, finish their steam surfaces by

[063-050] grinding them with powdered Turkey stone and oil. A slight grinding, or

[063-051] polishing, with powdered Turkey stone and oil, appears to be expedient in

[063-052] ordinary cases, and may be conveniently accomplished by setting the piston

[063-053] on a revolving table, and holding the ring stationary by a cross piece of

[063-054] wood while the table turns round. Pieces of wood may be interposed between

[063-055] the ring and the body of the piston, to keep the ring nearly in its right

[063-056] position; but these pieces of wood should be fitted so loosely as to give

[063-057] some side play, else the disposition would arise to wear the flange of the

[063-058] piston into a groove.

[063-059] 706. _Q._--What kind of tool is used for finishing surfaces by scraping?

[063-060] _A._--A flat file bent, and sharpened at the end, makes an eligible scraper

[063-061] for the first stages; or a flat file sharpened at the end and used like a

[063-062] chisel for wood. A three-cornered file, sharpened at all the corners, is

[063-063] the best instrument for finishing the operation. The scraping tool should

[063-064] be of the best steel, and should be carefully sharpened at short intervals

[063-065] on a Turkey stone, so as to maintain a fine edge.

[063-066] 707. _Q._--Will you explain the method of fitting together the valve and

[063-067] cylinder faces?

[063-068] _A._--Both faces must first be planed, then filed according to the

[063-069] indications of a metallic straight edge, and subsequently of a thick

[063-070] metallic face plate, and finally scraped very carefully until the face

[063-071] plate bears equally all over the surface. In planing any surface, the

[063-072] catches which retain the surface on the planing machine should be relaxed

[063-073] previously to the last cut, to obviate distortion from springing. To

[063-074] ascertain, whether the face plate bears equally, smear it over with a

[063-075] little red ochre and oil, and move the face plate slightly, which will fix

[063-076] the color upon the prominent points. This operation is to be repeated

[063-077] frequently; and as the work advances, the quantity of coloring matter is to

[063-078] be diminished, until finally it is spread over the face plate in a thin

[063-079] film, which only dims the brightness of the plate. The surfaces at this

[063-080] stage must be rubbed firmly together to make the points of contact visible,

[063-081] and the higher points will become slightly clouded, while the other parts

[063-082] are left more or less in shade. If too small a quantity of coloring matter

[063-083] be used at first, it will be difficult to form a just conception of the

[063-084] general state of the surface, as the prominent points will alone be

[063-085] indicated, whereas the use of a large quantity of coloring matter in the

[063-086] latter stages would destroy the delicacy of the test the face plate

[063-087] affords. The number of bearing points which it is desirable to establish on

[063-088] the surface of the work, depends on the use to which the surface is to be

[063-089] applied; but whether it is to be finished with great elaboration, or

[063-090] otherwise, the bearing points should be distributed equally over the

[063-091] surface. Face plates, or planometers, as they are sometimes termed, are

[063-092] supplied by most of the makers of engineering tools. Every factory should

[063-093] be abundantly supplied with them, and also with steel straight edges; and

[063-094] there should be a master face plate, and a master straight edge, for the

[063-095] sole purpose of testing, from time to time, the accuracy of those in use.

[063-096] 708. _Q._--Is the operation of surfacing, which you have described,

[063-097] necessary in the case of all slide valves?

[063-098] _A._--Yes; and in fitting the faces of a D valve, great care must, in

[063-099] addition, be taken that the valve is not made conical; for unless the back

[063-100] be exactly parallel with the face, it will be impossible to keep the

[063-101] packing from being rapidly cut away. When the valve is laid upon the face

[063-102] plate, the back must be made quite fair along the whole length, by draw

[063-103] filing, according to the indications of a straight edge; and the distance

[063-104] from the face to the extreme height of the back must be made identical at

[063-105] each extremity.

[063-106] 709. _Q._--When you described the operation of boring the cylinder, you

[063-107] stated that the cylinder, when laid upon its side, became oval; will not

[063-108] this change of figure distort the cylinder face?

[063-109] _A._--It is not only in the boring of the cylinder that it is necessary to

[063-110] be careful that there is no change of figure, for it will be impossible to

[063-111] face the valves truly in the case of large cylinders, unless the cylinder

[063-112] be placed on end, or internal props be introduced to prevent the collapse

[063-113] due to the cylinder's weight. It may be added, that the change of figure is

[063-114] not instantaneous, but becomes greater after some continuance of the strain

[063-115] than it was at first, so that in gauging a cylinder to ascertain the

[063-116] difference of diameter when it is placed on its side, it should have lain

[063-117] some days upon its side to ensure the accuracy of the operation.

[063-118] 710. _Q._--How is any flaw in the valve or cylinder face remedied?

[063-119] _A._--Should a hole occur either in the valve, in the cylinder, or any

[063-120] other part where the surface requires to be smooth, it may be plugged up

[063-121] with a piece of cast iron, as nearly as possible of the same texture. Bore

[063-122] out the faulty part, and afterward widen the hole with an eccentric drill,

[063-123] so that it will be of the least diameter at the mouth. The hole may go more

[063-124] than half through the iron: fit then a plug of cast iron roughly by filing,

[063-125] and hammer it into the hole, whereby the plug will become riveted in it,

[063-126] and its surface may then be filed smooth. Square pieces may be let in after

[063-127] the same fashion, the hole being made dovetailed, and the pieces thus

[063-128] fitted will never come out.

[063-129] 711. _Q._--When cylinders are faced with brass, how is the face attached to

[063-130] the cylinder?

[063-131] _A._--Brass faces are put upon valves or cylinders by means of small brass

[063-132] screws tapped into the iron, with conical necks for the retention of the

[063-133] brass: they are screwed by means of a square head, which, when the screw is

[063-134] in its place, is cut off and filed smooth. In some cases the face is made

[063-135] of extra thickness, and a rim not so thick runs round it, forming a step or

[063-136] recess for the reception of brass rivets, the heads of which are clear of

[063-137] the face.

[063-138] 712. _Q._--What is the best material for valve faces?

[063-139] _A._--Much trouble is experienced with every modification of valve face;

[063-140] but cast iron working upon cast iron is, perhaps, the best combination yet

[063-141] introduced. A usual practice is to pin brass faces on the cylinder,

[063-142] allowing the valve to retain its cast iron face. Some makers employ brass

[063-143] valves, and others pin brass on the valves, leaving the cylinder with a

[063-144] cast iron face. If brass valves are used, it is advisable to plane out two

[063-145] grooves across the face, and to fill them up with hard cast iron to prevent

[063-146] rutting. Speculum metal and steel have been tried for the cylinder faces,

[063-147] but only with moderate success. In some cases the brass gets into ruts; but

[063-148] the most prevalent affection is a degradation of the iron, owing to the

[063-149] action of the steam, and the face assuming a granular appearance, something

[063-150] like loaf sugar. This action shows itself only at particular spots, and

[063-151] chiefly about the angles of the port or valve face. At first the action is

[063-152] slow; but when once the steam has worked a passage for itself, the cutting

[063-153] away becomes very rapid, and, in a short time, it will be impossible to

[063-154] prevent the engine from heating when stopped, owing to the leakage of steam

[063-155] through the valve into the condenser. Copper steam pipes seem to have some

[063-156] galvanic action on valve faces, and malleable iron pipes have sometimes

[063-157] been substituted; but they are speedily worn out by oxidation, and the

[063-158] scales of rust which are carried on by the steam scratch the valves and

[063-159] cylinders, so that the use of copper pipes is the least evil.

[063-160] 713. _Q._--Will you explain in what manner the joints of an engine are

[063-161] made?

[063-162] _A._--Rust joints are not now much used in engines of any kind, yet it is

[063-163] necessary that the engineer should be acquainted with the manner of their

[063-164] formation. One ounce of sal-ammoniac in powder is mingled with 18 ounces or

[063-165] a pound of borings of cast iron, and a sufficiency of water is added to wet

[063-166] the mixture thoroughly, which should be done some hours before it is wanted

[063-167] for use. Some persons add about half an ounce of flowers of brimstone to

[063-168] the above proportions, and a little sludge from the grindstone trough. This

[063-169] cement is caulked into the joints with a caulking iron, about three

[063-170] quarters of an inch wide and one quarter of an inch thick, and after the

[063-171] caulking is finished the bolts of the joints may be tried to see if they

[063-172] cannot be further tightened. The skin of the iron must, in all cases, be

[063-173] broken where a rust joint is to be made; and, if the place be greasy, the

[063-174] surface must be well rubbed over with nitric acid, and then washed with

[063-175] water, till no grease remains. The oil about engines has a tendency to

[063-176] damage rust joints by recovering the oxide. Coppersmiths staunch the edges

[063-177] of their plates and rivets by means of a cement formed of pounded

[063-178] quicklime, with serum of blood, or white of egg; and in copper boilers such

[063-179] a substance may be useful in stopping the impalpable leaks which sometimes

[063-180] occur, though Roman, cement appears to be nearly as effectual.
