# Eccentric

An **eccentric** is a circular disc or wheel fixed on a rotating shaft with its centre displaced from the shaft axis. As the shaft revolves, the eccentric imparts a reciprocating motion to a rod or strap encircling it, thereby converting rotary into linear motion. In the [[steam|steam engine]], eccentrics are the standard mechanism for driving the [[slide-valve]]s that control the admission and [[eduction|exhaust]] of steam to and from the cylinder (009-115, 010-016).

## Operation

The eccentric is mounted on the [[crank|crank shaft]] or [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] shaft. As the shaft rotates, the eccentric rod — attached to a strap around the eccentric — moves back and forth, transmitting motion to the [[slide-valve|slide valve]]. The rod drives the valve to open and close the steam ports at precise moments in the piston stroke (010-016, 010-020).

The eccentric rod is commonly fitted with a disengaging notch: when it is desired to stop the engine, the connection between the eccentric and the [[slide-valve|slide valve]] can be broken by lifting the eccentric rod out of the notch that engages the pin on the valve lever. To restart, the rod is lowered to re-engage (010-025, 010-041, 010-042).

## Double Eccentrics

In engines that require reversal — particularly [[locomotive]]s and [[marine-engine]]s — double eccentrics are fixed on the shaft. These consist of two eccentrics set at different angular positions, corresponding to forward and backward motion. By engaging the appropriate eccentric rod, the engine can be made to run in either direction (010-078, 016-076, 016-080).

The [[link-motion]], developed by [[George Stephenson]], connects both eccentric rods through a slotted link. By raising or lowering the link relative to the valve rod, the motion of either eccentric can be selected, enabling not only reversal but also continuously variable expansion as the link takes an intermediate position between the two eccentric rods (016-089, 016-091).

## Use in Engines

In [[locomotive]] engines, the eccentrics are fixed on the [[crank-shaft|crank axle]] so that the valve moves in precise relation to the piston position. By moving the eccentric laterally against a stop, the eccentric can be partially turned around the shaft, thereby adjusting the timing of steam admission — a method known as adjusting the lead (016-103, 016-104).

In [[marine-engine]]s, the eccentric is similarly mounted on the main shaft and drives the [[slide-valve|slide valve]] through a rod. For large [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s, the eccentric must be made sufficiently wide to distribute the operating force without overheating (036-139, 036-140).
