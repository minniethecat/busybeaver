# Steam

**Steam** is water in its gaseous phase, formed by the application of heat. It is the primary working fluid in [[steam-engine|steam engines]], where its expansive force is converted into mechanical work.

## Properties

Steam possesses several key properties that make it suitable as a working fluid:

- **Expansive force**: When water is converted to steam, it expands to approximately 1,700 times its original volume at atmospheric pressure. (001-011)
- **Latent heat**: The heat required to convert water to steam without raising its temperature is known as latent heat. (001-015)
- **Elasticity**: Steam behaves as an elastic fluid, with pressure varying inversely with volume according to Boyle's law. (002-023)

## Generation

Steam is generated in a [[boiler|boiler]], where water is heated by the combustion of fuel. The rate of steam generation depends on the heating surface area, the temperature difference, and the circulation of water. (010-005)

Key factors in steam generation include:
- **Heating surface**: The area of boiler plate exposed to fire on one side and water on the other
- **Draught**: The flow of air through the furnace, which affects combustion efficiency
- **Feed water**: Water supplied to the boiler, which should be as pure as possible to prevent scale formation

## Use in Engines

In a steam engine, high-pressure steam is admitted into a cylinder where it expands against a piston. The expansion converts the thermal energy of the steam into mechanical work. After expansion, the steam may be [[eduction|exhaust]]ed to the atmosphere (non-[[condensation|condensing engine]]s) or condensed back to water ([[condensation|condensing engine]]s) to create a vacuum that assists the return stroke. (003-012)
