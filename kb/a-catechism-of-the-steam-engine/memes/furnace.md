# Furnace

The **furnace** in a [[boiler|steam boiler]] is the enclosed chamber where fuel (coal, coke, or wood) is burned, generating the heat required to convert water into steam. The furnace is the primary heat source of the boiler and its design fundamentally affects boiler efficiency, fuel consumption, and smokelessness (007-089, 012-091).

## Configuration

In land boilers, the furnace is commonly set in brickwork external to the boiler shell, with the boiler placed above or beside it. The heated gases from the furnace pass through flues that traverse the boiler (007-090). In the Cornish and Lancashire boiler designs, the furnace is placed within a large internal cylindrical flue running through the boiler, surrounded by water on all sides for maximum heat absorption (007-161, 007-164).

In [[locomotive]] boilers, the furnace takes the form of a [[fire-box]] at one end of the boiler, enclosed by a water-jacket on the sides and top (011-042). The [[fire-box]] connects to the smoke-box through multiple small [[flue|fire-tube]]s that carry the hot gases through the water space.

The draught through the furnace is regulated by a damper in the chimney. In land boilers this draught is produced by the height of the chimney; in locomotives the [[blast pipe]] creates an artificial forced draught (007-144, 011-039).

## Combustion and Firing

The best method of firing ordinary furnaces is to spread the coal thinly and evenly over the grate, throwing it well toward the back of the furnace rather than piling it at the front (012-091, 012-103). The fire should always be spread thin, and the coal broken into small pieces to promote thorough combustion. A smokeless furnace requires a sufficiently large capacity to allow the combustible gases time to burn completely before entering the flues (012-123, 012-126).

The furnace bars must be placed closer together for coal than for wood fuel, as coal requires a sharper draught through a thinner fire (012-108). Where heat in the furnace is very intense, the combustion may be too rapid to be complete, resulting in smoke and wasted fuel; a larger proportion of furnace room relative to the heating surface is therefore desirable (012-132, 012-139).

## Large-Scale Applications

In large marine and mill boilers, multiple furnaces are employed. Some flue boilers are constructed with three furnaces in each of multiple parallel flues, giving twelve furnaces in total for the largest installations (008-009, 008-010). This arrangement permits individual firing control and ensures uniform heating of the boiler water.
