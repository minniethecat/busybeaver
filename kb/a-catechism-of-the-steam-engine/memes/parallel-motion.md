The **parallel motion** is a mechanical linkage invented by [[james-watt|James Watt]] in 1784 that guides the [[piston]] rod of a beam engine to move in a nearly straight vertical line, despite being connected to the end of a rocking beam that travels in an arc. It was one of [[james-watt|Watt]]'s most celebrated inventions and was essential to the successful operation of double-acting beam engines.

## Principle of operation

> [009-047] The parallel motion is an arrangement of jointed rods, so connected to the piston rod and the beam that the piston rod is maintained in a vertical position throughout its stroke, notwithstanding the circumstance that the end of the beam to which it is attached moves in the arc of a circle.

The linkage consists of a system of bars connecting the piston rod, the beam end, and a fixed radius rod anchored to the engine framing. Together, these constrain the piston rod's path to a sufficiently straight line for practical engine operation.

## Watt's invention

> [009-045] This mechanism is called the parallel motion.

[[james-watt|Watt]] considered the parallel motion among his proudest mechanical achievements, noting that it elegantly solved the problem of connecting a reciprocating piston to a rocking beam without the piston rod binding or leaking at the cylinder's top seal. It superseded the earlier chain-and-arch-head arrangement, which could only pull (not push) and was therefore limited to [[double-acting|single-acting]] engines.

## Adjustment and accuracy

The geometry of the parallel motion requires precise setup, and [[john-bourne|Bourne]] provides methods for determining the correct centres:

> [064-149] To fix the centre for the parallel motion shaft, when the parallel bars are not more than two-thirds of the length of the radius rods, drop a perpendicular from the end of the side rod; this gives the centre of parallel motion shaft when the side rod is in its middle position.

> [065-002] The parallel motion when put in its place should be tested by raising and lowering the piston rod by hand, and noting whether it deviates from the vertical in any part of the stroke.

The accuracy of the parallel motion directly affects the performance of the [[stuffing-box]] seal and the smoothness of engine operation:

> [037-121] This species of packing answers very well when the parallel motion is true.

## Limitations and applications

The parallel motion is specific to beam engines, where the cylinder is vertical and the piston rod must be guided to a rocking beam above. In [[locomotive|locomotives]] and direct-acting engines, the cross-head and slide bars performed the equivalent straight-line guidance function. The [[connecting-rod]] then converts the linear motion into rotation at the [[crank]].

Despite its specificity to beam engines, the parallel motion remained in use throughout the nineteenth century in stationary pumping and mill engines, where the beam engine configuration persisted for its reliability and ease of maintenance.
