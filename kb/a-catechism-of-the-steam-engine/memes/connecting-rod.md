# Connecting Rod

The **connecting rod** is the rigid bar or link that connects the cross-head (or the piston rod directly) to the [[Crank|crank]], transmitting the linear reciprocating force of the [[Piston|piston]] and converting it into rotary motion at the [[crank|crank shaft]]. (009-171) (011-033)

## Construction and Materials

In land engines the connecting rod is usually of cast iron with a cruciform cross-section for stiffness. (028-063) It is preferable, however, to make the connecting rod of malleable iron, which provides greater resistance to the alternating tensile and compressive stresses it undergoes. (028-068)

> "In land engines the connecting rod is usually of cast iron with a cruciform section: but it is preferable, however, to make the connecting rod of malleable iron, and this is now generally the practice." (028-063) (028-068)

In [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s early engines, some connecting rods were made of wood, while later ones were of iron. (028-071) The transition from wood to iron paralleled the general improvement in engine pressures and speeds.

## Dimensions and Proportioning

The diameter of the connecting rod at the ends is calculated from the pressure on the piston and the stroke length. (029-024) The rod must be stout enough to resist buckling under compression during the inward stroke while remaining as light as practical to minimise inertia forces.

| Engine Type | Typical Material | Cross-Section |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Land (stationary) | Cast iron | Cruciform |
| Marine | Malleable iron | Rectangular or round |
| [[locomotive|Locomotive]] | Forged iron/steel | Round with flattened ends |

## Connection to the Cross-Head and Crank

At one end the connecting rod is attached to a pin fixed in the cross-head, which slides on guide bars and receives the piston rod. (010-169) At the other end it engages the crank pin on the [[crank|crank shaft]]. (009-171) The rod passes through [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]]es where it enters enclosed spaces, though in many configurations both ends work in the open air. (010-169)

In some engine designs, particularly beam engines, the connecting rod extends downwards from the beam end to give motion to the [[crank|crank shaft]]. (010-115)

## Role in the Engine Cycle

The connecting rod performs a critical kinematic function: during the steam admission phase, it transfers the full force of steam pressure from the piston to the crank. (011-033) As the crank rotates, the angular relationship between the connecting rod and the crank changes continuously, producing a varying mechanical advantage throughout the stroke.

The rod also transmits the return force during the [[eduction|exhaust]] stroke, pulling the piston back when the crank passes the dead centre and the [[Eccentric|eccentric]] shifts the [[Valve|valve]] to admit steam on the opposite side.
