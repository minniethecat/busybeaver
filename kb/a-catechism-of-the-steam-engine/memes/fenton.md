# Fenton

**Fenton** (fl. early 19th century) was a British engineer and metallurgist known for two distinct contributions to [[steam|steam engine]]ering: an expansion valve mechanism for varying the cut-off point in [[steam|steam engine]]s, and Fenton's metal, a proprietary bearing alloy used in locomotive axle boxes.

## Career and Contributions

Fenton's [[expansion-valve|expansion gear]] sought to accomplish [[expansion-valve|expansive working]] by introducing a spiral feather on the [[crank-shaft|crank axle]]. By moving the eccentric laterally against this spiral feather, the eccentric was partially turned round so as to cut off the steam at a different part of the stroke (016-102). This mechanical approach was one of several contemporaneous designs — alongside those of [[cabrey|Cabrey]], [[dodds|Dodds]], [[farcot|Farcot]], [[edwards|Edwards]], and [[lavagrian|Lavagrian]] — that [[john-bourne|Bourne]] enumerated as "too numerous for description" in full detail (016-102).

Beyond [[expansion-valve|expansion gear]], Fenton developed a bearing metal that saw practical application in locomotive construction. [[john-bourne|Bourne]]'s specification for a standard locomotive design records that the axle boxes were "of cast iron with bushes of Fenton's metal," with the leading axle having four bearings (057-154). The springs in this design were formed of steel plates 3 feet long, 4 inches broad, and half an inch thick.

## Legacy

Fenton's dual contributions — in both valve gear design and bearing metallurgy — illustrate the multidisciplinary nature of early [[locomotive|locomotive engine]]ering. While his expansion mechanism was one of many competing designs, Fenton's metal found direct industrial application in the running gear of main-line locomotives.
