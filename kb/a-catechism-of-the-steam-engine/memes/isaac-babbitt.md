# Isaac Babbitt (1799–1862)

Isaac Babbitt was an American metallurgist and inventor best known for creating Babbitt metal, a soft bearing alloy that revolutionized the durability and maintainability of rotating machinery in the steam age.

## Career and Contributions

Babbitt's key invention was a patent lining metal for bearing bushes, composed of a specific alloy of copper, antimony, and tin. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] records the formulation: "Babbitt's patent lining metal for bushes has been largely employed in the bushes of locomotive axles and other machinery: it is composed of 1 lb. of copper, 1 lb. regulus of antimony, and 10 lbs. of tin." (064-053)

The alloy's properties — soft enough to conform to the shaft journal yet hard enough to resist wear, with embedded hard particles to carry the load — made it ideal for [[locomotive]] axle bearings and [[steam|steam engine]] main bearings. Unlike the brass and bronze bearings it replaced, Babbitt metal could be re-poured and scraped to fit, greatly simplifying maintenance.

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] includes instructions on "Babbitt's metal, how to compound" (068-088) in the index, underlining the importance of the material to working engineers of the period. The alloy became a standard in [[steam|steam engine]] manufacturing, from stationary mill engines to [[marine-engine|marine engines]] and railway locomotives.

## Legacy

Babbitt metal remains in widespread use today (often spelled "babbit" generically), and the term "Babbitt bearing" is standard engineering terminology for any white-metal-lined plain bearing. His 1839 patent marked the beginning of engineered bearing materials, replacing ad-hoc brass formulations with a systematically developed alloy whose proportions were optimized for the specific demands of high-speed rotating machinery.
