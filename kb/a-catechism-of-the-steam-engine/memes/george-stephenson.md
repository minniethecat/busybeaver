# George Stephenson

**George Stephenson** (1781–1848) was an English civil and mechanical engineer, renowned as the "Father of Railways" for his pioneering work in steam [[Locomotive|locomotive]] design and railway construction. His [[Stephenson Link Motion|link motion]] arrangement for variable [[expansion-valve|expansion valve gear]] became a standard feature of [[locomotive|locomotive engine]]s throughout the nineteenth century. (016-074)

## Career and Contributions

Stephenson began his career as an engine-wright at collieries in Northumberland, where he gained practical experience with stationary and winding engines. His early locomotives, built for the Killingworth Colliery, demonstrated the feasibility of steam traction on iron rails.

Stephenson's most celebrated achievement was the establishment of the Liverpool and Manchester Railway (1830), the first public railway to rely exclusively on steam traction and to carry both passengers and freight. His locomotive *Rocket*, designed with his son [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]], won the Rainhill Trials of 1829 and set the pattern for locomotive design for decades.

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] repeatedly cites Stephenson's engineering practice as a benchmark in locomotive design. Stephenson's long-tubed boiler, his arrangement of the [[furnace|fire box]], and his variable [[expansion-valve|expansion valve gear]] are discussed throughout the *Catechism* as exemplars of contemporary practice. (022-173)

## Key Inventions

### The Stephenson Link Motion

Stephenson's most enduring technical contribution was the **link motion** for reversing and variable expansion. He connected the ends of the forward and backward eccentric rods by a link with a curved slot in which a pin upon the end of the valve rod works. (016-076) By moving this link to bring the forward eccentric rod in line with the valve rod, the valve receives the motion due to that eccentric; whereas if the backward eccentric rod is brought in line, the valve receives the motion proper for reversing. Intermediate positions of the link produce varying degrees of expansion, allowing the driver to control the [[cut-off|steam cut-off]].

> "Stephenson connects the ends of the forward and backward eccentric rods by a link with a curved slot in which a pin upon the end of the valve rod works. By moving this link so as to bring the forward eccentric rod in the same line with the valve rod, the valve receives the motion due to that eccentric; whereas if the backward eccentric rod is brought in a line with the valve rod, the valve gets the motion proper for reversing." (016-076)

The [[link-motion|Stephenson link motion]] was used for many years and remained the standard [[link-motion|reversing gear]] for locomotives well into the later nineteenth century, being simpler and more robust than competing arrangements.

### Locomotive Boiler Design

Stephenson's locomotive boilers were distinguished by their long tubes, which increased the heating surface relative to the fire-grate area. In Stephenson's boiler, the tubes above the [[furnace|fire box]] were bent down to connect with the chimney, and the cylinders were placed beneath the smoke box. (022-158) [[john-bourne|Bourne]] notes that Stephenson's long-tubed boilers produced a somewhat greater expenditure of power upon the blast compared to shorter-tubed designs, but their superior steaming capacity made them the preferred choice for express locomotives. (022-177)

## Workshop Practice

Stephenson established rigorous standards in his workshops. His pistons employed [[screw-propeller|screw]]s threaded into the piston rod with careful attention to preventing slack — though [[john-bourne|Bourne]] notes these were at one time liable to work loose until improved by subsequent engineers. (044-099) Stephenson used cast iron axle boxes with brasses and grease lubrication by means of siphon wicks. (045-022)

For valve gear, Stephenson employed a **gridiron [[slide-valve|slide valve]]** consisting of two plates with corresponding grooves that opened their ports simultaneously, providing a large steam passage with a small amount of travel. (035-020) This design reduced wear and improved the precision of steam distribution.

## Legacy

Stephenson's influence extended far beyond his own engines. His engineering practice became a reference standard for subsequent locomotive builders: [[john-bourne|Bourne]] compares the proportions of cylinders, boilers, [[furnace|fire box]]es, and [[safety-valve|safety valve]]s to Stephenson's designs throughout the *Catechism*. (023-017) (044-026) The link motion bearing his name was employed on locomotives worldwide, and his approach to railway construction — standard gauge track, careful grading, and stone block sleepers — shaped the global railway network.
