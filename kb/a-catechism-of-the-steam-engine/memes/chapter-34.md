[034-001] 413. _Q._--What are the usual dimensions of the barrel?

[034-002] _A._--The thickness of the plates composing the barrel of the boiler varies

[034-003] generally from 5/16ths to 3/8ths of an inch, and the plates should run in

[034-004] the direction of the circumference, so that the fibres of the iron may be

[034-005] in the direction of the strain. The diameter of the barrel commonly varies

[034-006] from 3 ft. to 3 ft. 6 inches; the diameter of the rivets should be from

[034-007] 11/16ths to 3/4ths of an inch, and the pitch of the rivets or distance

[034-008] between their centres should be from 17/8th to 2 inches.

[034-009] 414. _Q._--How are the fire boxes of a locomotive constructed?

[034-010] _A._--The space between the external and internal fire boxes forms a water

[034-011] space, which must be stayed every 4-1/2 or 5 inches by means of copper or

[034-012] iron stay bolts, screwed through the outer fire box into the metal of the

[034-013] inner fire box, and securely riveted within it: iron stay bolts are as

[034-014] durable as copper, and their superior tenacity gives them an advantage.

[034-015] Sometimes tubes are employed as stays. The internal and external fire boxes

[034-016] are joined together at the bottom by a N-shaped iron, and round the fire

[034-017] door they are connected by means of a copper ring 1-1/4 in. thick, and 2

[034-018] in. broad,--the inner fire box being dished sufficiently outward at that

[034-019] point, and the outer fire box sufficiently inward, to enable a circle of

[034-020] rivets 3/4 of an inch in diameter passing through the copper ring and the

[034-021] two thicknesses of iron, to make a water-tight joint. The thickness of the

[034-022] plates composing the external fire box is in general 3/8ths of an inch if

[034-023] the fire box is circular, and from 3/8ths to 1/2 inch if the fire box is

[034-024] square; and the thickness of the internal fire box is in most cases 7/16ths

[034-025] if copper, and from 3/8ths to 7/16ths of an inch if of iron. Circular

[034-026] internal fire boxes, if made of iron, should be welded rather than riveted,

[034-027] as the rivet heads are liable to be burnt away by the action of the fire;

[034-028] and when the fire boxes are square each side should consist of a single

[034-029] plate, turned over at the edges with a radius of 3 inches, for the

[034-030] introduction of the rivets.

[034-031] 415. _Q._--Is there any provision for stiffening the crown of the furnace

[034-032] in a locomotive?

[034-033] _A._--The roof of the internal fire box, whether flat as in Stephenson's

[034-034] engines, or dome shaped as in Bury's, requires to be stiffened with cross

[034-035] stay bars, but the bars require to be stronger and more numerous when

[034-036] applied to a flat surface. The ends of these stay bars rest above the

[034-037] vertical sides of the fire box; and to the stay bars thus extending across

[034-038] the crown, the crown is attached at intervals by means of stay bolts. There

[034-039] are projecting bosses upon the stay bars encircling the bolts at every

[034-040] point where a bolt goes through, but in the other parts they are kept clear

[034-041] of the fire box crown so as to permit the access of water to the metal;

[034-042] and, with the view of facilitating the ascent of the steam, the bottom of

[034-043] each stay bar should be sharpened away in those parts where it does not

[034-044] touch the boiler.

[034-045] 416. _Q._--Is any inconvenience experienced from the intense heat in a

[034-046] locomotive furnace?

[034-047] _A._--The fire bars in locomotives have always been a source of trouble, as

[034-048] from the intensity of the heat in the furnace they become so hot as to

[034-049] throw off a scale, and to bend under the weight of the fuel. The best

[034-050] alleviation of these evils lies in making the bars deep and thin: 4 or 5

[034-051] inches deep by five eighths of an inch thick on the upper side, and three

[034-052] eighths of an inch on the under side, are found in practice to be good

[034-053] dimensions. In some locomotives a frame carrying a number of fire bars is

[034-054] made so that it may be dropped suddenly by loosening a catch; but it is

[034-055] found that any such mechanism can rarely be long kept in working order, as

[034-056] the molten clinker by running down between the frame and the boiler will

[034-057] generally glue the frame into its place. It is therefore found preferable

[034-058] to fix the frame, and to lift up the bars by the dart used by the stoker,

[034-059] when any cause requires the fire to be withdrawn. The furnace bars of

[034-060] locomotives are always made of malleable iron, and indeed for every species

[034-061] of boiler malleable iron bars are to be preferred to bars of cast iron, as

[034-062] they are more durable, and may if thin be set closer together, whereby the

[034-063] small coal or coke is saved that would otherwise fall into the ash pit. The

[034-064] ash box of locomotives is made of plate iron, a quarter thick: it should

[034-065] not be less than 10 in. deep, and its bottom should be about 9 in. above

[034-066] the level of the rails. The chimney of a locomotive is made of plate iron

[034-067] one eighth of an inch thick: it is usually of the same diameter as the

[034-068] cylinder, but is better smaller, and must not stand more than 14 ft. high

[034-069] above the level of the rails.

[034-070] 417. _Q._--Are locomotive boilers provided with a steam chest?

[034-071] _A._--The upper portion of the external fire box is usually formed into a

[034-072] steam chest, which is sometimes dome shaped, sometimes semicircular, and

[034-073] sometimes of a pyramidical form, and from this steam chest the steam is

[034-074] conducted away by an internal pipe to the cylinders; but in other cases an

[034-075] independent steam chest is set upon the barrel of the boiler, consisting of

[034-076] a plate iron cylinder, 20 inches in diameter, 2 feet high, and three

[034-077] eighths of an inch thick, with a dome shaped top, and with the seam welded

[034-078] and the edge turned over to form a flange of attachment to the boiler. The

[034-079] pyramidical dome, of the form employed in Stephenson's locomotives,

[034-080] presents a considerable extent of flat surface to the pressure of the

[034-081] steam, and this flat surface requires to be very strongly stayed with angle

[034-082] irons and tension rods; whereas the semiglobular dome of the kind employed

[034-083] in Bury's engines requires no staying whatever. Latterly, however, these

[034-084] domes over the fire box have been either much reduced in size or abandoned

[034-085] altogether.

[034-086] 418. _Q._--Is any beneficial use made of the surplus steam of a locomotive?

[034-087] _A._--To save the steam which is formed when the engine is stationary, a

[034-088] pipe is usually fitted to the boiler, which on a cock being turned conducts

[034-089] the steam into the water in the tender, whereby the feed water is heated,

[034-090] and less fuel is subsequently required. This method of disposing of the

[034-091] surplus steam may be adopted when the locomotive is descending inclines, or

[034-092] on any occasion where more steam is produced than the engine can consume.

[034-093] 419._Q._--What means are provided to facilitate the inspection and cleaning

[034-094] of locomotive boilers?

[034-095] _A._--The man hole, or entrance into the boiler, consists of a circular or

[034-096] oval aperture of about 15 in. diameter, placed in Bury's locomotive at the

[034-097] apex of the dome, and in Stephenson's upon the front of the boiler, a few

[034-098] inches below the level of the rounded part; and the cover of the man hole

[034-099] in Bury's engine contains the safety valve seats. In whatever situation

[034-100] this man hole is placed, the surfaces of the ring encircling the hole, and

[034-101] of the internal part of the door or cover, should be accurately fitted

[034-102] together by scraping or grinding, so that they need only the interposition

[034-103] of a little red lead to make them quite tight when screwed together. Lead

[034-104] or canvas joints, if of any considerable thickness, will not long withstand

[034-105] the action of high pressure steam; and the whole of the joints about a

[034-106] locomotive should be such that they require nothing more than a little

[034-107] paint or putty, or a ring of wire gauze smeared with white or red lead to

[034-108] make them perfectly tight. There must be a mud hole opposite the edge of

[034-109] each water space, if the fire box be square, to enable the boiler to be

[034-110] easily cleaned out, and these holes are most conveniently closed by screwed

[034-111] plugs made slightly taper. A cock for emptying the boiler is usually fixed

[034-112] at the bottom of the fire box, and it should be so placed as to be

[034-113] accessible when the engine is at work, in order that the engine driver may

[034-114] blow off some water if necessary; but it must not be in such a position as

[034-115] to send the water blown off among the machinery, as it might carry sand or

[034-116] grit into the bearings, to their manifest injury.

[034-117] 420. _Q._--Will you state the dimensions of the tube plate, and the means

[034-118] of securing the tubes in it?

[034-119] _A._--The tube plates are generally made from five eighths to three fourths

[034-120] of an inch thick, but seven eighths of an inch thick appears to be

[034-121] preferable, as when the plate is thick the holes will not be so liable to

[034-122] change their figure during the process of feruling the tubes: the distance

[034-123] between the tubes should never be made less than three fourths of an inch,

[034-124] and the holes should be slightly tapered so as to enable the tubes to hold

[034-125] the tube plates together. The tubes are secured in the tube plates by means

[034-126] of taper ferules driven into the ends of the tubes. The ferules are for the

[034-127] most part made of steel at the fire box end, and of wrought iron at the

[034-128] smoke box end, though ferules of malleable cast iron have in some cases

[034-129] been used with advantage: malleable cast iron ferules are almost as easily

[034-130] expanded when hammered cold upon a mandrel, as the common wrought iron ones

[034-131] are at a working heat. Spring steel, rolled with a feather edge, to

[034-132] facilitate its conversion into ferules, is supplied by some of the

[034-133] steel-makers of Sheffield, and it appears expedient to make use of steel

[034-134] thus prepared when steel ferules are employed. In cases where ferules are

[034-135] not employed, it may be advisable to set out the tube behind the tube plate

[034-136] by means of an expanding mandrel. There are various forms of this

[034-137] instrument. One form is that known as Prosser's expanding mandrel, in which

[034-138] there are six or eight segments, which are forced out by means of a

[034-139] hexagonal or octagonal wedge, which is forced forward by a screw. When the

[034-140] wedge is withdrawn, the segments collapse sufficiently to enable them to

[034-141] enter the tube, and there is an annular protuberance on the exterior circle

[034-142] of the segments, which protuberance, when the mandrel is put into the tube,

[034-143] just comes behind the inner edge of the tube plate. When the wedge is

[034-144] tightened up by the screw, the protuberance on the exterior of the segments

[034-145] composing the mandrel causes a corresponding bulge to take place in the

[034-146] tube, at the back of the tube plate, and the tube is thereby brought into

[034-147] more intimate contact with the tube plate than would otherwise be the case.

[034-148] There is a steel ring indented into the segments of Prosser's mandrel, to

[034-149] contract the segments when the central wedge is withdrawn. A more

[034-150] convenient form of the instrument, however, is obtained by placing the

[034-151] segments in a circular box, with one end projecting; and supporting each

[034-152] segment in the box by a tenon, which fits into a mortise in the cylindrical

[034-153] box. To expand the segments, a round tapered piece of steel, like a drift,

[034-154] is forced into a central hole, round which the segments are arranged. A

[034-155] piece of steel tube, also slit up to enable a central drift to expand it,

[034-156] answers very well; but the thickness of that part of the tube in which

[034-157] there requires to be spring enough to let the mandrel expand, requires to

[034-158] be sufficiently reduced to prevent the pieces from cracking when the

[034-159] central drift is driven in by a hammer. The drift is better when made with

[034-160] a globular head, so that it may be struck back by the hammer, as well as be

[034-161] driven in. An expanding mandrel, with a central drift, is more rapid in its

[034-162] operation than when the expansion is produced by means of a screw.
