# Dodds

Dodds was a British engineer who contributed to the development of the [[expansion-valve|expansion valve]] in the 1840s, an essential mechanism for improving the efficiency of [[steam|steam engine]]s by cutting off steam admission before the end of the piston stroke.

## Career and Contributions

Dodds's approach to variable expansion employed a mechanical arrangement on the [[crank-shaft|crank axle]]. As [[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes: "Dodds seeks to attain the same end by corresponding mechanical arrangements" (016-105), using "a spiral feather on the [[crank-shaft|crank axle]], by moving the eccentric laterally against which the eccentric is partially turned round so as to cut off the steam at a different part of the stroke."

This design was one of several competing expansion valve mechanisms developed during the 1840s, alongside those of [[Cabrey]], [[Fenton]], [[Farcot]], [[Edwards]], [[Lavagrian]], [[Bodmer]], [[Meyer]], [[Hawthorn]], and [[Gonzenbach]]. Dodds's specific contribution was the spiral feather mechanism on the [[crank-shaft|crank axle]] — a purely mechanical solution that avoided the complexity of separate valve gear.

Dodds's expansion valve is indexed in the *Catechism* under "expansion valves, by [[cabrey|Cabrey]], [[fenton|Fenton]], Dodds, [[farcot|Farcot]], [[edwards|Edwards]], [[lavagrian|Lavagrian]], [[rudolf-bodmer|Bodmer]], [[meyer|Meyer]], [[robert-hawthorn|Hawthorn]], [[gonzenbach|Gonzenbach]], and [[john-bourne|Bourne]]" (069-055) and receives its own entry: "Dodds, expansion valve by" (068-339).

## Legacy

Dodds's expansion valve represents one variant in the burst of inventive activity around [[cut-off|variable cut-off]] mechanisms in the 1840s. While the specific spiral feather design did not become the dominant standard, the collective work of Dodds and his contemporaries established the principle of automatic variable expansion that would be refined in later [[Corliss engine|Corliss]] and high-speed engine designs.
