# Oscillating Engine

The **oscillating engine** is a type of [[marine-engine|marine steam engine]] in which the [[Cylinder|cylinders]] are mounted on trunnions and oscillate to follow the motion of the [[Crank|crank]], eliminating the need for a separate [[Connecting-Rod|connecting rod]]. (009-134) (036-124)

This design was widely adopted for [[paddle-wheel|paddle]]-wheel steamers, and together with the side-lever engine it was one of the principal forms of [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] engine in the mid-nineteenth century. (009-138)

## How It Works

In the oscillating engine, the cylinder is set immediately above the [[crank|crank shaft]], and the piston rod attaches directly to the crank pin. (010-058) As the crank rotates, the entire cylinder pivots on its trunnions, which serve both as the mounting bearings and as passages for steam admission and [[eduction|exhaust]].

The trunnions are hollow, carrying steam to and from the cylinder through the bearings themselves. This arrangement eliminates the cross-head, the [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]], and much of the external steam piping required by a fixed-cylinder engine. (028-034)

> "There is the side lever engine, and the oscillating engine, which are the only kinds of paddle engine employed for the propulsion of steam vessels of a large size." (009-134) (009-138)

## Penn's Oscillating Engines

The most notable builder of oscillating engines was [[john-penn|John Penn]] of Greenwich, whose firm constructed oscillating engines for many naval and mercantile vessels. (010-069) Penn's oscillating engines employed a distinctive piston with a single packing ring and a tongue piece packed behind with hemp. (038-020)

| Feature | Penn's Oscillating Engine |
|---------|--------------------------|
| [[cylinder|Cylinder]] mounting | On trunnions, direct to crank |
| [[piston|Piston]] design | Single packing ring with tongue piece |
| Air pump drive | By eccentric on main shaft (036-136) |
| Stuffing boxes | [[tallow|Tallow]]-based packing introduced (037-094) |
| Application | Royal Navy and packet steamers |

The parts of Penn's oscillating engine generally resemble those of other engines except that the cylinder oscillates. (037-023)

## Advantages and Objections

The oscillating engine offers compactness and simplicity: fewer moving parts than a side-lever engine, lighter weight, and lower cost of construction. It is particularly suitable for vessels with limited engine-room height.

However, objections were raised against the oscillating engine in its early days. (036-141) There was a tendency for the steam pressure in the trunnions to force the cylinder against one side, causing uneven wear, and the oscillating motion challenged the durability of the steam joints at the trunnion faces. (036-158) These objections were largely overcome by careful workmanship and the use of [[packing|metallic packing]], and the type proved highly successful in service.

> "In common with every other improvement, the oscillating engine, at the time of its introduction, encountered a good deal of opposition." (036-142)

## Geared Variants

In some installations, the oscillating engines were geared to the [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] shaft, allowing the engines to run at a higher speed than the [[paddle-wheel|paddle]]s, which improved efficiency. (010-130) The geared oscillating engine is similar in most particulars to the direct-acting [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] oscillating engine. (010-130)

## Vertical Oscillating Engines

In addition to the horizontal form, vertical oscillating engines were also employed, particularly where deck height was constrained. (010-127) In these engines the cylinder oscillated in a vertical plane, and the [[crank|crank shaft]] was positioned below.
