[055-001] 632. _Q._--How are the trunnion plummer blocks made?

[055-002] _A._--The trunnion plummer blocks are formed in the same manner as the

[055-003] crank shaft plummer blocks; the nuts are kept from turning back by means of

[055-004] a pinching screw passing through a stationary washer. It is not expedient

[055-005] to cast the trunnion plummer blocks upon the lower frame, as is sometimes

[055-006] done; for the cylinders, being pressed from the steam trunnions by the

[055-007] steam, and drawn in the direction of the condenser by the vacuum, have a

[055-008] continual tendency to approach one another; and as they wear slightly

[055-009] toward midships, there would be no power of readjustment unless the plummer

[055-010] blocks were movable. The flanges of the trunnions should always fit tight

[055-011] against the plummer block sides, but there should be a little play sideways

[055-012] at the necks of the trunnions, so that the cylinder may be enabled to

[055-013] expand when heated, without throwing an undue strain upon the trunnion

[055-014] supports.

[055-015] 633. _Q._--What kind of paddle wheel is supplied with these oscillating

[055-016] engines?

[055-017] _A._--The wheels are of the feathering kind, 9 feet 8 inches in diameter,

[055-018] measuring to the edges of the floats; and there are 10 floats upon each

[055-019] wheel, measuring 4 feet 6 inches long each, and 18-1/2 inches broad. There

[055-020] are two sets of arms to the wheel, which converge to a cast iron centre,

[055-021] formed like a short pipe with large flanges, to which the arms are affixed.

[055-022] The diameter of the shaft, where the centre is put on, is 4-1/2 inches, the

[055-023] external diameter of the pipe is 8 inches, and the diameter of the flanges

[055-024] is 20 inches, and their thickness 1-1/4 inch. The flanges are 12 inches

[055-025] asunder at the outer edge, and they partake of the converging direction of

[055-026] the arms. The arms are 2-1/4 inches broad and half an inch thick; the heads

[055-027] are made conical, and each is secured into a recess upon the side of the

[055-028] flange by means of three bolts. The ring which connects together the arms,

[055-029] runs round at a distance of 3 feet 6 inches from the centre, and the

[055-030] projecting ends of the arms are bent backward the length of the lever which

[055-031] moves the floats, and are made very wide and strong at the point where they

[055-032] cross the ring, to which they are attached by four rivets. The feathering

[055-033] action of the floats is accomplished by means of a pin fixed to the

[055-034] interior of the paddle box, set 3 inches in advance of the centre of the

[055-035] shaft, and in the same horizontal line. This pin is encircled by a cast

[055-036] iron collar, to which rods are attached 1-3/8 inch diameter in the centre,

[055-037] proceeding to the levers, 7 inches long, fixed on the back of the floats in

[055-038] the line of the outer arms. One of these rods, however, is formed of nearly

[055-039] the same dimensions as one of the arms of the wheel, and is called the

[055-040] driving arm, as it causes the cast iron collar to turn round with the

[055-041] revolution of the wheel, and this collar, by means of its attachments to

[055-042] the floats, accomplishes the feathering action. The eccentricity in this

[055-043] wheel is not sufficient to keep the floats in the vertical position, but in

[055-044] the position between the vertical and the radial. The diameter of the pins

[055-045] upon which the floats turn is 1-3/8 inch, and between the pins and paddle

[055-046] ring two stud rods are set between each of the projecting ends of the arms,

[055-047] so as to prevent the two sets of arms from being forced nearer or further

[055-048] apart; and thus prevent the ends of the arms from hindering the action of

[055-049] the floats, by being accidentally jammed upon the sides of the joints.

[055-050] Stays, crossing one another, proceed from the inner flange of the centre to

[055-051] the outer ring of the wheel, and from the outer flange of the centre to the

[055-052] inner ring of the wheel, with the view of obtaining greater stiffness. The

[055-053] floats are formed of plate iron, and the whole of the joints and joint pins

[055-054] are steeled, or formed of steel. For sea-going vessels the most approved

[055-055] practice is to make the joint pins of brass, and also to bush the eyes of

[055-056] the joints with brass; and the surface should be large to diminish wear.

[055-057] 634. _Q._--Can you give the dimensions of any other oscillating engines?

[055-058] _A._--In Messrs. Penn's 50 horse power oscillating engine, the diameter of

[055-059] the cylinder is 3 feet 4 inches, and the length of the stroke 3 feet. The

[055-060] thickness of the metal of the cylinder is 1 inch, and the thickness of the

[055-061] cylinder bottom is 1-3/4 inch, crossed with feathers, to give it additional

[055-062] stiffness. The diameter of the trunnion bearings is 1 foot 2 inches, and

[055-063] the breadth of the trunnion bearings 5-1/2 inches. Messrs. Penn, in their

[055-064] larger engines, generally make the area of the steam trunnion less than

[055-065] that of the eduction trunnion, in the proportion of 32 to 37; and the

[055-066] diameter of the eduction trunnion is regulated by the internal diameter of

[055-067] the eduction pipe, which is about 1/5th of the diameter of the cylinder.

[055-068] But a somewhat larger proportion than this appears to be expedient: Messrs.

[055-069] Rennie make the area of their eduction pipes, in oscillating engines, 1/22d

[055-070] of the area of the cylinder. In the oscillating engines of the Oberon, by

[055-071] Messrs. Rennie, the cylinder is 61 inches diameter, and 1-1/2 inch thick

[055-072] above and below the belt, but in the wake of the belt it is 1-1/4 inch

[055-073] thick, which is also the thickness of metal of the belt itself. The

[055-074] internal depth of the belt is 2 feet 6 inches, and its internal breadth is

[055-075] 4 inches. The piston rod is 6-3/4 inches in diameter, and the total depth

[055-076] of the cylinder stuffing box is 2 feet 4 inches, of which 18 inches

[055-077] consists of a brass bush--this depth of bearing being employed to prevent

[055-078] the stuffing box or cylinder from wearing oval.

[055-079] 635. _Q._--Can you give any other examples?

[055-080] _A._--The diameter of cylinder of the oscillating engines of the steamers

[055-081] Pottinger, Ripon, and Indus, by Miller & Ravenhill, is 76 inches, and the

[055-082] length of the stroke 7 feet. The thickness of the metal of the cylinder is

[055-083] 1-11/16 inch; diameter of the piston rod 8-3/4 inches; total depth of

[055-084] cylinder stuffing box 3 feet; depth of bush in stuffing box 4 inches; the

[055-085] rest of the depth, with the exception of the space for packing, being

[055-086] occupied with a very deep gland, bushed with brass. The internal diameter

[055-087] of the steam pipe is 13 inches; diameter of steam trunnion journal 25

[055-088] inches; diameter of eduction trunnion journal 25 inches; thickness of metal

[055-089] of trunnions 2-1/4 inches; length of trunnion bearings 11 inches;

[055-090] projection of cylinder jacket, 8 inches; depth of packing space in

[055-091] trunnions, 10 inches; width of packing space in trunnions, or space round

[055-092] the pipes, 1-1/2 inch; diameter of crank pin 10-1/4 inches; length of

[055-093] bearing of crank pin 15-1/2, inches. There are six boilers on the tubular

[055-094] plan in each of these vessels; the length of each boiler is 10 feet 6

[055-095] inches, and the breadth 8 feet; and each boiler contains 62 tubes 3 inches

[055-096] in diameter, and 6 feet 6 inches long, and two furnaces 6 feet 4-1/2 inches

[055-097] long, and 3 feet 1-1/2 inch broad.

[055-098] 636. _Q._--Is it the invariable practice to make the piston rod cap of

[055-099] brass in the way you have described?

[055-100] _A._--In all oscillating engines of any considerable size, the cover of the

[055-101] connecting brass, which attaches the crank pin to the connecting rod, is

[055-102] formed of malleable iron; and the socket also, which is cuttered to the end

[055-103] of the piston rod, is of malleable iron, and is formed with a T head,

[055-104] through which bolts pass up through the brass, to keep the cover of the

[055-105] brass in its place.

[055-106] 637. _Q._--Is the piston of an oscillating engine made deeper than in

[055-107] common engines?

[055-108] _A._--It is expedient, in oscillating engines, to form the piston with a

[055-109] projecting rim round the edge above and below, and a corresponding recess

[055-110] in the cylinder cover and cylinder bottom, whereby the breadth of bearing

[055-111] of the solid part of the metal will be increased, and in many engines this

[055-112] is now done.

[055-113] 638. _Q._--Would any difficulty be experienced in keeping the trunnions

[055-114] tight in a high pressure oscillating engine?

[055-115] _A._--It is very doubtful whether the steam trunnions of a high pressure

[055-116] oscillating engine will continue long tight if the packing consists of

[055-117] hemp; and it appears preferable to introduce a brass ring, to embrace the

[055-118] pipe, cut spirally, with an overlap piece to cover the cut, and packed

[055-119] behind with hemp.

[055-120] 639. _Q._--How is the packing of the trunnions usually effected?

[055-121] _A._--The packing of the trunnions, after being plaited as hard as

[055-122] possible, and cut to the length to form one turn round the pipe, is dipped

[055-123] into boiling tallow, and is then compressed in a mould, consisting of two

[055-124] concentric cylinders, with a gland forced down into the annular space by

[055-125] three to six screws in the case of large diameters, and one central screw

[055-126] in the case of small diameters. Unless the trunnion packings be well

[055-127] compressed, they will be likely to leak air, and it is, therefore,

[055-128] necessary to pay particular attention to this condition. It is also very

[055-129] important that the trunnions be accurately fitted into their brasses by

[055-130] scraping, so that there may not be the smallest amount of play left upon

[055-131] them; for if any upward motion is permitted, it will be impossible to

[055-132] prevent the trunnion packings from leaking.

[055-133] DIRECT ACTING SCREW ENGINE.

[055-134] 640. _Q._--Will you describe the configuration and construction of a direct

[055-135] acting screw engine?

[055-136] _A._--I will take as an example of this species of engine, the engine

[055-137] constructed by Messrs. John Bourne & Co., for the screw steamer Alma, a

[055-138] vessel of 500 tons burden. This engine is a single steeple engine laid on

[055-139] its side, and in its general features it resembles the engines of the

[055-140] Amphion already described, only that there is one cylinder instead of two.

[055-141] The cylinder is of 42 inches diameter and 42 inches stroke, and the vessel

[055-142] has been propelled by this single engine at the rate of fourteen miles an

[055-143] hour.

[055-144] 641. _Q._--Is not a single engine liable to stick upon the centre so that

[055-145] it cannot be started or reversed with facility?

[055-146] _A._--A single engine is no doubt more liable to stick upon the centre than

[055-147] two engines, the cranks of which are set at right angles with one another;

[055-148] but numerous paddle vessels are plying successfully that are propelled by a

[055-149] single engine, and the screw offers still greater facility than paddles for

[055-150] such a mode of construction. In the screw engine referred to, as the

[055-151] cylinder is laid upon its side, there is no unbalanced weight to be lifted

[055-152] up every stroke, and the crank, whereby the screw shaft is turned round,

[055-153] consists of two discs with a heavy side intended to balance the momentum of

[055-154] the piston and its connections; but these counter-weights by their

[055-155] gravitation also prevent the connecting rod and crank from continuing in

[055-156] the same line when the engine is stopped, and in fact they place the crank

[055-157] in the most advantageous position for starting again when it has to be set

[055-158] on.

[055-159] 642. _Q._--Will you explain the general arrangement of the parts of this

[055-160] engine?

[055-161] _A._--The cylinder lies on its side near one side of the vessel, and from

[055-162] the end of the cylinder two piston rods extend to a cross head sliding

[055-163] athwartships, in guides, near the other side of the vessel. To this cross

[055-164] head the connecting rod is attached, and one end of it partakes of the

[055-165] motion of the cross head or piston, while the other end is free to follow

[055-166] the revolution of the crank on the screw shaft.
