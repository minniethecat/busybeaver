# Vacuum Gauge

The **vacuum gauge** is an instrument applied to the [[Condenser|condenser]] of a [[steam|steam engine]] to indicate the degree of perfection attained by the vacuum. (002-072) (002-073) It is essentially a barometer adapted for [[condensation|condenser]] use — the higher the vacuum, the greater the efficiency gained from the condensing principle.

## Operating Principle

The vacuum gauge measures the difference between atmospheric pressure and the pressure inside the [[condensation|condenser]]. In a [[Condensation|condensing]] engine, the [[eduction|exhaust]] steam is cooled and condensed, creating a partial vacuum behind the [[Piston|piston]]. The vacuum gauge allows the engine-man to verify that this vacuum is being maintained at the expected level.

> "Yes; and it is called the vacuum gauge, because it shows the degree of perfection the vacuum has attained." (002-073)

When the air has been fully drawn out of the [[Cylinder|cylinder]] and [[condensation|condenser]] by the [[Air-Pump|air pump]], the vacuum gauge should show a vacuum equivalent to about three inches of mercury. (035-128) (035-129) The engineer shuts all valves and observes the gauge; any loss of vacuum indicates air leakage into the [[condensation|condenser]].

## Types

### Mercurial Gauge

The earliest and most common form of steam and vacuum gauge in [[john-bourne|Bourne]]'s time consisted of a glass tube containing mercury. (018-085) [[steam|Steam]] pressure forces the mercury up the tube, while vacuum draws it upward from the opposite end, the column height being read against a marked scale. (018-086) (018-087) A float riding on the mercury surface in the open leg of the U-tube could point to graduated marks on a scale, providing a direct reading. (018-100)

### Bourdon's Gauge

By the mid-nineteenth century, Bourdon's gauges had come into widespread use for both steam pressure and vacuum measurement. (018-101) (018-102) The Bourdon gauge employs a curved, flattened metal tube that tends to straighten under internal pressure or coil more tightly under vacuum; this motion is transmitted through a linkage to a pointer on a dial. Bourdon's steam and vacuum gauges are recorded among the standard fittings of marine boilers. (068-197)

## Practical Use

The vacuum gauge is essential during engine start-up to confirm that the [[air-pump|air pump]] has evacuated the [[condensation|condenser]]. It also serves as a continuous monitor during operation: a falling vacuum warns of air leaks, [[condensation|condenser]] fouling, or [[air-pump|air pump]] malfunction — all of which reduce engine efficiency.
