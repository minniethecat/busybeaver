The **valve chest** is a chamber or housing in a steam engine that encloses the valves
controlling the admission of steam to the cylinder and the exhaust of spent steam.
It forms an integral part of the engine's steam distribution system, sitting atop or
alongside the cylinder and containing the slide valves, gridiron valves, or other
valve gear that regulate steam flow.

## Construction and Arrangement

In Stephenson's locomotive engines, the regulator consists of a slide valve covering
a port on the top of the valve chests, with a rod passing from this valve through
the smoke box below the boiler, brought up to the engineer's reach by a lever
parallel to the starting lever. (035-016) In engines with variable expansion, a
single cock for draining purposes is located on the bottom of the valve chest.
(044-092)

In the Corliss engine, the cylinder and valve chests are arranged with two distinct
sets of valves — steam valves and exhaust valves — operated independently of each
other, with separate steam and exhaust connections. (059-130)
