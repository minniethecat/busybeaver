The **[[valve|throttle valve]]** is a regulating [[valve]] placed in the steam pipe between the [[boiler]] and the [[cylinder]]. By varying its opening, the engine driver or [[governor]] controls the quantity of steam admitted to the engine, thereby regulating engine speed and power output. It is one of the fundamental control elements of any [[steam|steam engine]] installation.

## Position and construction

> [008-085] At the steam pipe, B. The throttle valve in that pipe is an elliptical plate, which turns upon a hinge, and is made to fit the pipe accurately by grinding.

> [008-091] Motion is communicated to the throttle valve by suitable rods and levers connecting it with the governor, which regulates the speed of the engine.

The valve is typically a disc or butterfly-type plate mounted on a spindle, rotating within the steam pipe to open or close the passage:

> [008-105] The throttle valve, C, will be opened or closed as the governor spindle rises or falls, thereby maintaining a uniform speed.

## Governor control

In stationary engines, the [[valve|throttle valve]] is automatically adjusted by the centrifugal [[governor]]:

> [002-105] In some cases, partially to close the throttle valve in the steam pipe, whereby the speed of the engine is regulated to a nearly uniform rate, notwithstanding variations in the work being performed.

When the engine speed increases beyond the desired rate, the governor raises its spindle, which through levers partially closes the [[valve|throttle valve]], reducing steam flow and bringing the speed back down. Conversely, a drop in speed opens the throttle wider.

## Manual operation

In locomotives and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s, where power demand varies continuously, the [[valve|throttle valve]] is operated manually by the driver via a lever or handle. The friction of the valve against its seat can be significant at high steam pressures:

> [004-086] The friction of the throttle valve which shuts off the steam, and of the valve gearing which operates the valves, must be overcome before the engine can start.

## Expansion control

In some engine designs, particularly those without a separate [[expansion-valve]], the [[valve|throttle valve]] works in conjunction with the [[link-motion|link motion]] to achieve [[expansion-valve|expansive working]]:

> [016-129] Most of the modern engines trust for their expansive working to the link motion and the throttle valve.

By throttling the steam at admission (operating with the throttle partially closed), the initial steam pressure in the cylinder is reduced, producing an effect similar to early cut-off. However, throttling is thermodynamically less efficient than true [[expansion-valve|expansive working]] with a [[cut-off|variable cut-off]], as it introduces irreversible losses.
