[032-001] INCRUSTATION AND CORROSION OF BOILERS.

[032-002] 389. _Q._--What is the cause of the formation of scale in marine boilers?

[032-003] _A._--Scale is formed in all boilers which contain earthy or saline

[032-004] matters, just in the way in which a scaly deposit, or rock, as it is

[032-005] sometimes termed, is formed in a tea kettle. In sea water the chief

[032-006] ingredient is common salt, which exists in solution: the water admitted to

[032-007] the boiler is taken away in the shape of steam, and the saline matter which

[032-008] is not vaporizable accumulates in process of time in the boiler, until its

[032-009] amount is so great that the water is saturated, or unable to hold any more

[032-010] in solution; the salt is then precipitated and forms a deposit which

[032-011] hardens by heat. The formation of scale, therefore, is similar to the

[032-012] process of making salt from sea water by evaporation, the boiler being, in

[032-013] fact, a large salt pan.

[032-014] 390. _Q._--But is the scale soluble in fresh water like the salt in a salt

[032-015] pan?

[032-016] _A._--No, it is not; or if soluble at all, is only so to a very limited

[032-017] extent. The several ingredients in sea water begin to be precipitated from

[032-018] solution at different degrees of concentration; and the sulphate and

[032-019] carbonate of lime, which begin to be precipitated when a certain state of

[032-020] concentration is reached, enter largely into the composition of scale, and

[032-021] give it its insoluble character. Pieces of waste or other similar objects

[032-022] left within a marine boiler appear, when taken out, as if they had been

[032-023] petrified; and the scale deposited upon the flues of a marine boiler

[032-024] resembles layers of stone.

[032-025] 391. _Q/_--Is much inconvenience experienced in marine boilers from these

[032-026] incrustations upon the flues?

[032-027] _A._--Incrustation in boilers at one time caused much more perplexity than

[032-028] it does at present, as it was supposed that in some seas it was impossible

[032-029] to prevent the boilers of a steamer from becoming salted up; but it has now

[032-030] been satisfactorily ascertained that there is very little difference in the

[032-031] saltness of different seas, and that however salt the water may be, the

[032-032] boiler will be preserved from any injurious amount of incrustation by

[032-033] blowing off, as it is called, very frequently, or by permitting a

[032-034] considerable portion of the supersalted water to escape at short intervals

[032-035] into the sea. If blowing off be sufficiently practised, the scale upon the

[032-036] flues will never be much thicker than a sheet of writing paper, and _no

[032-037] excuse_ should be accepted from engineers for permitting a boiler to be

[032-038] damaged by the accumulation of calcareous deposit.

[032-039] 392. _Q._--What is the temperature at which sea water boils in a steam

[032-040] boiler?

[032-041] _A._--Sea water contains about 1/33rd its weight of salt, and in the open

[032-042] air it boils at the temperature of 213.2°; if the proportion of salt be

[032-043] increased to 2/33rds of the weight of the water, the boiling point will

[032-044] rise to 214.4°; with 3/33rds of salt the boiling point will be 215.5°;

[032-045] 4/33rds, 216.7°; 5/33rds, 217.9°; 6/33rds, 219°; 7/33rds, 220.2°; 8/33rds,

[032-046] 221.4°; 9/33rds, 222.5°; 10/33rds, 223.7°; 11/33rds, 224.9°; and 12/33rds,

[032-047] which is the point of saturation, 226°. In a steam boiler the boiling

[032-048] points of water containing these proportions of salt must be higher, as the

[032-049] elevation of temperature due to the pressure of the steam has to be added

[032-050] to that due to the saltness of the water; the temperature of steam at the

[032-051] atmospheric pressure being 212°, its temperature, at a pressure of 15 lbs.

[032-052] per square inch above the atmosphere, will be 250°, and adding to this 4.7°

[032-053] as the increased temperature due to the saltness of the water when it

[032-054] contains 4/33rds of salt, we have 254.7° as the temperature of the water in

[032-055] the boiler, when it contains 4/33rds of salt and the pressure of the steam

[032-056] is 15 lbs. on the square inch.

[032-057] 393. _Q._--What degree of concentration of the salt water may be safely

[032-058] permitted in a boiler?

[032-059] _A._--It is found by experience that when the concentration of the salt

[032-060] water in a boiler is prevented from exceeding that point at which it

[032-061] contains 2/33rds its weight of salt, no injurious incrustation will take

[032-062] place, and as sea water contains only 1/33rd of its weight of salt, it is

[032-063] clear that it must be reduced by evaporation to one half of its bulk before

[032-064] it can contain 2/33rds of salt; or, in other words, a boiler must blow out

[032-065] into the sea one half of the water it receives as feed, in order to prevent

[032-066] the water from rising above 2/33rds of concentration, or 8 ounces of salt

[032-067] to the gallon.

[032-068] 394. _Q._--How do you determine 8 ounces to the gallon to be equivalent to

[032-069] twice the density of salt water, or "two salt waters" as it is sometimes

[032-070] called?

[032-071] _A._--The density of the water of different seas varies somewhat. A gallon

[032-072] of fresh water weighs 10 lbs.; a gallon of salt water from the Baltic

[032-073] weighs 10.15 lbs.; a gallon of salt water from the Irish Channel weighs

[032-074] 10.28 lbs.; and a gallon of salt water from the Mediterranean 10.29 lbs. If

[032-075] we take an average saltness represented by a weight of 10.25 lbs., then a

[032-076] gallon of water concentrated to twice this saltness will weigh 10.5 lbs.,

[032-077] or the salt in it will weigh .5 lbs or 8 oz., which is the proportion of 8

[032-078] oz. to the gallon. However, the proportion of 2/33rds gives a greater

[032-079] proportion than 8 oz. to the gallon, for 2/33 = 1/16 nearly, and 1/16 of 10

[032-080] lbs. = 10 oz. By keeping the density of the water in a marine boiler at the

[032-081] proportion of 8 or 10 oz. to the gallon, no inconvenient amount of scale

[032-082] will be deposited on the flues or tubes. The bulk of water, it may be

[032-083] remarked, is not increased by putting salt in it up to the point of

[032-084] saturation, but only its density is increased.

[032-085] 395. _Q._--Is there not a great loss of heat by blowing off so large a

[032-086] proportion of the heated water from the boiler?

[032-087] _A._--The loss is not very great. Boilers are sometimes worked at a

[032-088] saltness of 4/33rds, and taking this saltness and supposing the latent heat

[032-089] of steam to be at 1000° at the temperature of 212°, and reckoning the sum

[032-090] of the latent and sensible heats as forming a constant quantity, the latent

[032-091] heat of steam at the temperature of 250° will be 962°, and the total heat

[032-092] of the steam will be 1212° in the case of fresh water; but as the feed

[032-093] water is sent into the boiler at the temperature of 100°, the accession of

[032-094] heat it receives from the fuel will be 1112° in the case of fresh water, or

[032-095] 1112° increased by 3.98° in the case of water containing 4/33ds of salt--

[032-096] the 3.98° being the 4.7° increase of temperature due to the presence of

[032-097] 4/33rds of salt, multiplied by 0.847 the specific heat of steam. This makes

[032-098] the total accession of heat received by the steam in the boiler equal to

[032-099] 1115.98°, or say 1116°, which multiplied by 3, as 3 parts of the water are

[032-100] raised into steam, gives us 3348° for the heat in the steam, while the

[032-101] accession of heat received in the boiler by the 1 part of residual brine

[032-102] will be 154.7°, multiplied by 0.85, the specific heat of the brine, or

[032-103] 130.495°; and 3348° divided by 130.495° is about 1/26th. It appears,

[032-104] therefore, that by blowing off the boiler to such an extent that the

[032-105] saltness shall not rise above what answers to 4/33rds of salt, about 1/25th

[032-106] of the heat is blown into the sea; this is but a small proportion, and as

[032-107] there will be a greater waste of heat, if from the existence of scale upon

[032-108] the flues the heat can be only imperfectly transmitted to the water, there

[032-109] cannot be even an economy of fuel in niggard blowing off, while it involves

[032-110] the introduction of other evils. The proportion of 4/33rds of saltness,

[032-111] however, or 16 oz. to the gallon, is larger than is advisable, especially

[032-112] as it is difficult to keep the saltness at a perfectly uniform point, and

[032-113] the working point should, therefore, be 2/33rds as before prescribed.

[032-114] 396. _Q._--Have no means been devised for turning to account the heat

[032-115] contained in the brine which is expelled from the boiler?

[032-116] _A._--To save a part of the heat lost by the operation of blowing off, the

[032-117] hot brine is sometimes passed through a number of small tubes surrounded by

[032-118] the feed water; but there is no very great gain from the use of such

[032-119] apparatus, and the tubes are apt to become choked up, whereby the safety of

[032-120] the boiler may be endangered by the injurious concentration of its

[032-121] contents. Pumps, worked by the engine for the extraction of the brine, are

[032-122] generally used in connection with the small tubes for the extraction of the

[032-123] heat from the supersalted water; and if the tubes become choked the pumps

[032-124] will cease to eject the water, while the engineer may consider them to be

[032-125] all the while in operation.

[032-126] 397._Q._--What is the usual mode of blowing off the supersalted water from

[032-127] the boiler?

[032-128] _A._--The general mode of blowing off the boiler is to allow the water to

[032-129] rise gradually for an hour or two above the lowest Working level, and then

[032-130] to open the cock communicating with the sea, and keep it open until the

[032-131] surface of the water within the boiler has fallen several inches; but in

[032-132] some cases a cock of smaller size is allowed to run water continuously, and

[032-133] in other cases brine pumps are used as already mentioned. In every case in

[032-134] which the supersalted water is discharged from the boiler in a continuous

[032-135] stream, a hydrometer or salt gauge of some convenient construction should

[032-136] be applied to the boiler, so that the density of the water may at all times

[032-137] be visible, and immediate notice be given of any interruption of the

[032-138] operation. Various contrivances have been devised for this purpose, the

[032-139] most of which operate on the principle of a hydrometer; but perhaps a more

[032-140] satisfactory principle would be that of a differential steam gauge, which

[032-141] would indicate the difference of pressure between the steam in the boiler

[032-142] and the steam of a small quantity of fresh water enclosed in a suitable

[032-143] vessel, and immerged in the water of the boiler.

[032-144] 398. _Q._--What is the advantage of blowing off from the surface of the

[032-145] water in the boiler?

[032-146] _A._--Blowing off from a point near the surface of the water is more

[032-147] beneficial than blowing off from the bottom of the boiler. Solid particles

[032-148] of any kind, it is well known, if introduced into boiling water, will lower

[032-149] the boiling point in a slight degree, and the steam will chiefly be

[032-150] generated on the surface of the particles, and indeed will have the

[032-151] appearance of coming out of them; if the particles be small the steam

[032-152] generated beneath and around them will balloon them to the surface of the

[032-153] water, where the steam will be liberated and the particles will descend;

[032-154] and the impalpable particles in a marine boiler, which by their subsidence

[032-155] upon the flues concrete into scale, are carried in the first instance to

[032-156] the surface of the water, so that if they be caught there and ejected from

[032-157] the boiler, the formation of scale will be prevented.

[032-158] 399. _Q._--Are there any plans in operation for taking advantage of this

[032-159] property of particles rising to the surface?

[032-160] _A._--Advantage is taken of this property in Lamb's Scale Preventer, which

[032-161] is substantially a contrivance for blowing off from the surface of the

[032-162] water that in practice is found to be very effectual; but a float in

[032-163] connection with a valve at the mouth of the discharging pipe is there

[032-164] introduced, so as to regulate the quantity of water blown out by the height

[032-165] of the water level, or by the extent of opening given to the feed cock. The

[032-166] operation, however, of the contrivance would be much the same if the float

[032-167] were dispensed with; but the float acts advantageously in hindering the

[032-168] water from rising too high in the boiler, should too much feed be admitted,

[032-169] and thereby obviates the risk of the water running over into the cylinder.

[032-170] In some boilers sheet iron vessels, called sediment collectors, are

[032-171] employed, which collect into them the impalpable matter, which in Lamb's

[032-172] apparatus is ejected from the boiler at once. One of these vessels, of

[032-173] about the size and shape of a loaf of sugar, is put into each boiler with

[032-174] the apex of the cone turned downwards into a pipe leading overboard, for

[032-175] conducting the sediment away from the boiler. The base of the cone stands

[032-176] some distance above the water line, and in its sides conical slits are cut,

[032-177] so as to establish a free communication between the water within the

[032-178] conical vessel and the water outside it. The particles of stony matter

[032-179] which are ballooned to the surface by the steam in every other part of the

[032-180] boiler, subside within the cone, where, no steam being generated, the water

[032-181] is consequently tranquil; and the deposit is discharged overboard by means

[032-182] of a pipe communicating with the sea. By blowing off from the surface of

[032-183] the water, the requisite cleansing action is obtained with less waste of

[032-184] heat; and where the water is muddy, the foam upon the surface of the water

[032-185] is ejected from the boiler--thereby removing one of the chief causes of

[032-186] priming.
