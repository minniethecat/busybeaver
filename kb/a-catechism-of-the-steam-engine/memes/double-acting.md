A **double-acting engine** is one in which steam is admitted alternately to both sides of the [[piston]], so that the piston is driven by steam pressure on both the upward and downward strokes. This was one of [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s most significant improvements over the earlier atmospheric ([[steam|Newcomen]]) engine, effectively doubling the power output for a given cylinder size.

## Principle of operation

In the double-acting engine, steam enters first above the piston, driving it downward, and then below the piston, driving it upward:

> [001-238] In the double-acting engine, steam is admitted alternately above and below the piston, so that the piston is impelled downward and upward by the pressure of the steam.

The [[slide-valve]] or other valve gear controls the alternating admission and [[eduction|exhaust]] of steam to each side of the cylinder:

> [001-240] The engine is rendered double-acting by causing the steam to press upon the piston first on one side and then on the other, by which means a more continuous rotatory motion is obtained.

## Contrast with single-acting engines

The earlier [[steam|atmospheric engines]] of Newcomen were single-acting: steam filled the cylinder only to create a vacuum on one side of the piston, with atmospheric pressure doing the actual work on the return stroke:

> [002-013] In the atmospheric engine of Newcomen, the steam acted only on one side of the piston, the return stroke being effected by a counterweight on the pump end of the beam.

[[james-watt|Watt]]'s double-acting design eliminated this asymmetry, producing power in both directions and enabling a smoother, more compact engine suitable for rotative applications like driving machinery and [[locomotive|locomotives]].

## Mechanical implications

The double-acting design required the [[connecting-rod]] and [[crank]] to convert the reciprocating motion into rotary motion. This was more complex than the simple chain-and-beam connection of the single-acting engine, but delivered far greater power density:

> [002-016] The double-acting engine requires a connecting rod and crank to transform the reciprocating motion of the piston into a rotatory motion, which is more complex but enables the engine to drive machinery directly.

> [002-018] The double-acting engine is now universally employed for all purposes where a rotatory motion is required.

## Application in marine engines

All [[marine-engine|marine engines]] are double-acting, as the continuous rotary motion is essential for driving a [[screw-propeller]] or [[paddle-wheel]]. The [[oscillating-engine]] represents a space-efficient double-acting design particularly suited to [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] steamers, where the cylinder itself oscillates to accommodate the crank motion.
