[015-001] 183. _Q._--Is this amount of benefit actually realized in practice?

[015-002] _A._--Only in some cases. It appears to be indispensable to the realization

[015-003] of any large amount of benefit by expansion, that the cylinder should be

[015-004] enclosed in a steam jacket, or should in some other way be effectually

[015-005] protected from refrigeration. In some engines not so protected, it has been

[015-006] found experimentally that less benefit was obtained from the fuel by

[015-007] working expansively than by working without expansion--the whole benefit

[015-008] due to expansion being more than counteracted by the increased

[015-009] refrigeration due to the larger surface of the cylinder required to develop

[015-010] the power. In locomotive engines, with outside cylinders, this condition of

[015-011] the advantageous use of expansion has been made very conspicuous, as has

[015-012] also been the case in screw steamers with four cylinders, and in which the

[015-013] refrigerating surface of the cylinders was consequently large.

[015-014] 184. _Q._--The steam is admitted to and from the cylinder by means of a

[015-015] slide or sluice valve?

[015-016] [Illustration: Fig. 33.]

[015-017] _A._--Yes; and of the slide valve there are many varieties; but the kinds

[015-018] most in use are the D valve,--so called from its resemblance to a half

[015-019] cylinder or D in its cross section--and the three ported valve, shown in

[015-020] fig. 33, which consists of a brass or iron box set over the two ports or

[015-021] openings into the cylinder, and a central port which conducts away the

[015-022] steam to the atmosphere or condenser; but the length of the box is so

[015-023] adjusted that it can only cover one of the cylinder ports and the central

[015-024] or eduction port at the same time. The effect, therefore, of moving the

[015-025] valve up and down, as is done by the eccentric, is to establish a

[015-026] connection alternately between each cylinder port and the central passage

[015-027] whereby the steam escapes; and while the steam is escaping from beneath the

[015-028] piston, the position of the valve is such, that a free communication exists

[015-029] between the space above the piston and the steam in the boiler. The piston

[015-030] is thus urged alternately up and down--the valve so changing its position

[015-031] before the piston arrives at the end of the stroke, that the pressure is by

[015-032] that time thrown on the reverse side of the piston, so as to urge it into

[015-033] motion in the opposite direction.

[015-034] 185. _Q._--Is the motion of the valve, then, the reverse of that of the

[015-035] piston?

[015-036] _A._--No. The valve does not move down when the piston moves down, nor does

[015-037] it move down when the piston moves up; but it moves from its mid position,

[015-038] to the extremity of its throw, and back again to its mid position, while

[015-039] the piston makes an upward or downward movement, so that the motion is as

[015-040] it were at right angles to the motion of the piston; or it is the same

[015-041] motion that the piston of another engine, the crank of which is set at

[015-042] right angles with that of the first engine, would acquire.

[015-043] 186. _Q._--Then in a steam vessel the valve of one engine may be worked

[015-044] from the piston of the other?

[015-045] _A._--Yes, it may; or it may be worked from its own connecting rod; and in

[015-046] the case of locomotive engines, this has sometimes been done.

[015-047] 187. _Q._--What is meant by the lead of the valve?

[015-048] _A._--The amount of opening which the valve presents for the admission of

[015-049] the steam, when the piston is just beginning its stroke. It is found

[015-050] expedient that the valve should have opened a little to admit steam on the

[015-051] reverse side of the piston before the stroke terminates; and the amount of

[015-052] this opening, which is given by turning the eccentric more or less round

[015-053] upon the shaft, is what is termed the lead.

[015-054] 188. _Q._--And what is meant by the lap of the valve?

[015-055] _A._--It is an elongation of the valve face to a certain extent over the

[015-056] port, whereby the port is closed sooner than would otherwise be the case.

[015-057] This extension is chiefly effected at that part of the valve where the

[015-058] steam is admitted, or upon the _steam side_ of the valve, as the technical

[015-059] phrase is; and the intent of the extension is to close the steam passage

[015-060] before the end of the stroke, whereby the engine is made to operate to a

[015-061] certain extent expansively. In some cases, however, there is also a certain

[015-062] amount of lap given to the escape or eduction side, to prevent the eduction

[015-063] from being performed too soon when the lead is great; but in all cases

[015-064] there is far less lap on the eduction than on the steam side, very often

[015-065] there is none, and sometimes less than none, so that the valve is incapable

[015-066] of covering both the ports at once.

[015-067] 189. _Q._--What is the usual proportional length of stroke of the valve?

[015-068] _A._--The common stroke of the valve in rotative engines is twice the

[015-069] breadth or depth of the port, and the length of the valve face will then be

[015-070] just the breadth of the port when there is lap on neither the steam nor

[015-071] eduction side. Whatever lap is given, therefore, makes the valve face just

[015-072] so much longer. In some engines, however, the stroke of the valve is a good

[015-073] deal more than twice the breadth of the port; and it is to the stroke of

[015-074] the valve that the amount of lap should properly be referred.

[015-075] 190. _Q._--Can you tell what amount of lap will accomplish any given amount

[015-076] of expansion?

[015-077] _A._--Yes, when the stroke of the valve is known. From the length of the

[015-078] stroke of the piston subtract that part of the stroke which is intended to

[015-079] be accomplished before the steam is cut off; divide the remainder by the

[015-080] length of the stroke of the piston, and extract the square root of the

[015-081] quotient, which multiply by half the stroke of the valve, and from the

[015-082] product take half the lead; the remainder will be the lap required.

[015-083] 191. _Q._--Can you state how we may discover at what point of the stroke

[015-084] the eduction passage will be closed?

[015-085] _A._--To find how much before the end of the stroke the eduction passage

[015-086] will be closed:--to the lap on the steam side add the lead, and divide the

[015-087] sum by half the stroke of the valve; find the arc whose sine is equal to

[015-088] the quotient, and add 90° to it.; divide the lap on the eduction side by

[015-089] half the stroke of the valve, and find the arc whose cosine is equal to the

[015-090] quotient; subtract this arc from the one last obtained, and find the cosine

[015-091] of the remainder; subtract this cosine from 2, and multiply the remainder

[015-092] by half the stroke of the piston; the product is the distance of the piston

[015-093] from the end of the stroke when the eduction passage is closed.

[015-094] 192. _Q._--Can you explain how we may determine the distance of the piston

[015-095] from the end of the stroke, before the steam urging it onward is allowed to

[015-096] escape?

[015-097] _A._--To find how far the piston is from the end of its stroke when the

[015-098] steam that is propelling it by expansion is allowed to escape to the

[015-099] atmosphere or condenser--to the lap on the steam side add the lead; divide

[015-100] the sum by half the stroke of the valve, and find the arc whose sine is

[015-101] equal to the quotient; find the arc whose sine is equal to the lap on the

[015-102] eduction side, divided by half the stroke of the valve; add these two arcs

[015-103] together and subtract 90°; find the cosine of the residue, subtract it from

[015-104] 1, and multiply the remainder by half the stroke of the piston; the product

[015-105] is the distance of the piston from the end of its stroke when the steam

[015-106] that is propelling it is allowed to escape into the atmosphere or

[015-107] condenser. In using these rules, all the dimensions are to be taken in

[015-108] inches, and the answers will be found in inches also.

[015-109] 193. _Q._--Is it a benefit or a detriment to open the eduction passage

[015-110] before the end of the stroke?

[015-111] _A._--In engines working at a high rate of speed, such as locomotive

[015-112] engines, it is very important to open the exhaust passage for the escape of

[015-113] the steam before the end of the stroke, as an injurious amount of back

[015-114] pressure is thus prevented. In the earlier locomotives a great loss of

[015-115] effect was produced from inattention to this condition; and when lap was

[015-116] applied to the valves to enable the steam to be worked expansively, it was

[015-117] found that a still greater benefit was collaterally obtained by the earlier

[015-118] escape of the steam from the eduction passages, and which was incidental to

[015-119] the application of lap to the valves. The average consumption of coke per

[015-120] mile was reduced by Mr. Woods from 40 lbs. per mile to 15 lbs. per mile,

[015-121] chiefly by giving a free outlet to the escaping steam.

[015-122] 194. _Q._--To what extent can expansion be carried beneficially by means of

[015-123] lap upon the valve?

[015-124] _A._--To about one-third of the stroke; that is, the valve may be made with

[015-125] so much lap, that the steam will be cut off when two thirds of the stroke

[015-126] have been performed, leaving the residue to be accomplished by the agency

[015-127] of the expanding steam; but if more lap be put on than answers to this

[015-128] amount of expansion, a very distorted action of the valve will be produced,

[015-129] which may impair the efficiency of the engine. If a further amount of

[015-130] expansion than this is wanted, it may be accomplished by wire drawing the

[015-131] steam, or by so contracting the steam passage that the pressure within the

[015-132] cylinder must decline when the speed of the piston is accelerated, as it is

[015-133] about the middle of the stroke.

[015-134] 195. _Q._--Will you explain how this result ensues?

[015-135] _A._--If the valve be so made as to shut off the steam by the time two

[015-136] thirds of the stroke have been performed, and the steam be at the same time

[015-137] throttled in the steam pipe, the full pressure of the steam within the

[015-138] cylinder cannot be maintained except near the beginning of the stroke where

[015-139] the piston travels slowly; for, as the speed of the piston increases, the

[015-140] pressure necessarily subsides, until the piston approaches the other end of

[015-141] the cylinder, where the pressure would rise again but that the operation of

[015-142] the lap on the valve by this time has had the effect of closing the

[015-143] communication between the cylinder and steam pipe, so as to prevent more

[015-144] steam from entering. By throttling the steam, therefore, in the manner here

[015-145] indicated, the amount of expansion due to the lap may be doubled, so that

[015-146] an engine with lap enough upon the valve to cut off the steam at two-thirds

[015-147] of the stroke, may, by the aid of wire drawing, be virtually rendered

[015-148] capable of cutting off the steam at one-third of the stroke.

[015-149] 196. _Q._--Is this the usual way of cutting off the steam?

[015-150] _A._--No; the usual way of cutting off the steam is by means of a separate

[015-151] valve, termed an expansion valve; but such a device appears to be hardly

[015-152] necessary in ordinary engines. In the Cornish engines, where the steam is

[015-153] cut off in some cases at one-twelfth of the stroke, a separate valve for

[015-154] the admission of steam, other than that which permits its escape, is of

[015-155] course indispensable; but in common rotative engines, which may realize

[015-156] expansive efficacy by throttling, a separate expansion valve does not

[015-157] appear to be required.

[015-158] 197. _Q._--That is, where much expansion is required, an expansion valve is

[015-159] a proper appendage, but where not much is required, a separate expansion

[015-160] valve may be dispensed with?

[015-161] _A._--Precisely so. The wire drawing of the steam causes a loss of part of

[015-162] its power, and the result will not be quite so advantageous by throttling

[015-163] as by cutting off. But for moderate amounts of expansion it will suffice,

[015-164] provided there be lap upon the slide valve.
