# Stop Valve

The **stop valve** is a valve placed in the steam passage between a [[boiler]] and the main steam pipe, or between two boilers connected to a common steam main. Its purpose is to cut off the connection, enabling one boiler to be taken out of service for inspection, cleaning, or repair while others remain in operation (025-102, 068-176).

## Operation

The stop valve is designed so that the steam pressure from the boiler presses the valve against its seat, assisting in maintaining a tight closure. In some arrangements, mechanical safeguards are provided to prevent the stop valve from being closed inadvertently when the engine is at work — a situation that would leave the boiler without any escape for steam except through the [[safety-valve]] (025-104).

In [[marine-engine]] installations, boilers are now generally supplied with stop valves, whereby any one boiler may be disconnected from the remainder of the steam system without affecting the operation of the others (033-093). This is essential for naval and merchant vessels where reliability mandates multiple boilers and the ability to isolate a damaged or leaking unit at sea.

## Construction

Marine stop valves are usually spindle valves of large size, operated by a hand wheel and [[screw-propeller|screw]] spindle that raises or lowers the valve disc (033-095). On the discharge side of a [[feed-pump|feed pump]], a stop valve — sometimes called the discharge valve — is provided to prevent backflow when the pump is not operating (040-116).

Stop valves must be constructed to withstand full boiler pressure and to operate reliably even after long periods without use. The valve body is typically of cast iron or gunmetal, the spindle of brass or bronze, and the seating faces are ground to ensure a steam-tight closure. In modern practice, stop valves are often supplemented by non-return or check valves that close automatically if the pressure in the steam main exceeds boiler pressure.

## Use in Boiler Systems

Stop valves are essential components in any steam plant with multiple boilers feeding a common steam range. By closing the stop valve on a particular boiler, that unit is effectively removed from the system — its fires may be drawn, the boiler cooled, and maintenance performed — while the remaining boilers continue to supply steam to the engines. On shore installations, stop valves also permit the boiler to be isolated from the engine for overnight shutdowns or seasonal lay-ups.
