[026-003] suitable for all kinds of engines, whether high or low pressure:--multiply

[026-004] the square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches by the speed of the

[026-008] evaporating surface has been properly proportioned to the engine power.

[026-009] 313. _Q._--Is this rule applicable to locomotives?

[026-010] _A._--It is applicable to high pressure engines of every kind. The

[026-011] dimensions of safety valves, however, in practice are very variable, being

[026-012] in some cases greater, and in some cases less, than what the rule gives,

[026-013] the consideration being apparently as often what proportions will best

[026-014] prevent the valve from sticking in its seat, as what proportions will

[026-015] enable the steam to escape freely. In Bury's locomotives, the safety valve

[026-016] was generally 2-1/2 inches diameter for all sizes of boiler, and the valve

[026-017] was kept down by a lever formed in the proportion of 5 to 1, fitted at one

[026-018] end with a Salter's balance. As the area of the valve was 5 square inches,

[026-019] the number of pounds shown on the spring balance denoted the number of

[026-020] pounds pressure on each square inch of the boiler.

[026-021] 314. _Q._--Is there only one safety valve in a locomotive boiler?

[026-022] _A._--There are always two.

[026-023] 315. _Q._--And are they always pressed down by a spring balance, and never

[026-024] by weights?

[026-025] _A._--They are never pressed down by weights; in fact, weights would not

[026-026] answer on a locomotive at all, as they would jump up and down with the

[026-027] jerks or jolts of the train, and cause much of the steam to escape. In land

[026-028] and marine boilers, however, the safety valve is always kept down by

[026-029] weights; but in steam vessels a good deal of steam is lost in stormy

[026-030] weather by the opening of the valve, owing to the inertia of the weights

[026-031] when the ship sinks suddenly in the deep recess between the waves.

[026-032] 316. _Q._--What other sizes of safety valves are used in locomotives?

[026-033] _A._--Some are as large as 4 inches diameter, giving 12 square inches of

[026-034] area; and others are as small as 1-3/16 inch diameter, giving 1 square inch

[026-035] of area.

[026-036] 317. _Q._--And are these valves all pressed down by a Salter's spring

[026-037] balance?

[026-038] _A._--In the great majority of cases they are so, and the lever by which

[026-039] they are pressed down is generally graduated in the proportion of the area

[026-040] of the valve to unity; that is, in the case of a valve of 12 inches area,

[026-041] the long end of the lever to which the spring balance is attached is 12

[026-042] times the length of the short end, so that the weight or pressure on the

[026-043] balance shows the pressure per square inch on the boiler. In some cases,

[026-044] however, a spiral spring, and in other cases a pile of elliptical springs,

[026-045] is placed directly upon the top of the valve, and it appears desirable that

[026-046] one of the valves at least should be loaded in this manner. It is difficult

[026-047] when the lever is divided in such a proportion as 12 to 1, to get

[026-048] sufficient lift of the valve without a large increase of pressure on the

[026-049] spring; and it appears expedient, therefore, to employ a shorter lever,

[026-050] which involves either a reduction in the area of the valve, or an increased

[026-051] strength in the spring.

[026-052] 318. _Q._--What are the proper dimensions of the steam passages?

[026-053] _A._--In slow working engines the common size of the cylinder passages is

[026-054] one twenty-fifth of the area of the cylinder, or one fifth of the diameter

[026-055] of the cylinder, which is the same thing. This proportion corresponds very

[026-056] nearly with one square inch per horse power when the length of the cylinder

[026-057] is about equal to its diameter; and one square inch of area per horse power

[026-058] for the cylinder ports and eduction passages answers very well in the case

[026-059] of engines working at the ordinary speed of 220 feet per minute. The area

[026-060] of the steam pipe is usually made less than the area of the eduction pipe,

[026-061] especially when the engine is worked expansively, and with a considerable

[026-062] pressure of steam. In the case of ordinary condensing engines, however,

[026-063] working with the usual pressure of from 4 to 8 lbs. above the atmosphere,

[026-064] the area of the steam pipe is not less than a circular inch per horse

[026-065] power. In such engines the diameter of the steam pipe may be found by the

[026-066] following rule: divide the number of nominal horse power by 0.8 and extract

[026-067] the square root of the quotient, which will be the internal diameter of the

[026-068] steam pipe.

[026-069] 319. _Q._--Will you explain by what process of computation these

[026-070] proportions are arrived at?

[026-071] _A._--The size of the steam pipe is so regulated that there will be no

[026-072] material disparity of pressure between the cylinder and boiler; and in

[026-073] fixing the size of the eduction passage the same object is kept in view.

[026-074] When the diameter of the cylinder and the velocity with which the piston

[026-075] travels are known, it is easy to tell what the velocity of the steam in the

[026-076] steam pipe will be; for if the area of the cylinder be 25 times greater

[026-077] than that of the steam pipe, the steam in the steam pipe must travel 25

[026-078] times faster than the piston, and the difference of pressure requisite to

[026-079] produce this velocity of the steam can easily be ascertained, by finding

[026-080] what height a column of steam must be to give that velocity, and what the

[026-081] weight or pressure is of such a column. In practice, however, this

[026-082] proportion is always exceeded from the condensation of steam in the pipe.

[026-083] 320. _Q._--If the relation you have mentioned subsist between the area of

[026-084] the steam passages and the velocity of the piston, then the passages must

[026-085] be larger when the piston travels very rapidly?

[026-086] _A._--And they are so made. The area of the ports of locomotive engines is

[026-087] usually so proportioned as to be from 1/10th to 1/8th the area of the

[026-088] cylinder--in some cases even as much as 1/6th; and in all high speed

[026-089] engines the ports should be very large, and the valve should have a good

[026-090] deal of travel so as to open the port very quickly. The area of port which

[026-091] it appears advisable to give to modern engines of every description, is

[026-092] expressed by the following rule:--multiply the area of the cylinder in

[026-093] square inches by the speed of the piston in feet per minute, and divide the

[026-094] product by 4,000; the quotient is the area of each cylinder port in square

[026-095] inches. This rule gives rather more than a square inch of port per nominal

[026-096] horse power to condensing engines working at the ordinary speed; but the

[026-097] excess is but small, and is upon the right side. For engines travelling

[026-098] very fast it gives a good deal more area than the common proportion, which

[026-099] is too small in nearly every case. In locomotive engines the eduction pipe

[026-100] passes into the chimney and the force of the issuing steam has the effect

[026-101] of maintaining a rapid draught through the furnace as before explained. The

[026-102] orifice of the waste steam pipe, or the blast pipe as it is termed, is much

[026-103] contracted in some engines with the view of producing a fiercer draught,

[026-104] and an area of 1/22d of the cylinder is a common proportion; but this is as

[026-105] much contraction as should be allowed, and is greater than is advisable.

[026-106] 321. _Q._--In engines moving at a high rate of speed, you have stated that

[026-107] it is important to give the valve lead, or in other words to allow the

[026-108] steam to escape before the end of the stroke?

[026-109] _A._--Yes, this is very important, else the piston will have to force out

[026-110] the steam from the cylinder, and will be much resisted. Near the end of the

[026-111] stroke the piston begins to travel slowly, and if the steam be then

[026-112] permitted to escape, very little of the effective stroke is lost, and time

[026-113] is afforded to the steam, before the motion of the piston is again

[026-114] accelerated, to make its escape by the port. In some locomotives, from

[026-115] inattention to this adjustment, and from a contracted area of tube section,

[026-116] which involved a strong blast, about half the power of the engine has been

[026-117] lost; but in more recent engines, by using enlarged ports and by giving

[026-118] sufficient lead, this loss has been greatly diminished.

[026-119] 322. _Q._--What do you call sufficient lead?

[026-120] _A._--In fast going engines I would call it sufficient lead, when the

[026-121] eduction port was nearly open at the end of the stroke.

[026-122] 323. _Q._--Can you give any example of the benefit of increasing the lead?

[026-123] _A._--The early locomotives were made with very little lead, and the

[026-124] proportions were in fact very much the same as those previously existing in

[026-125] land engines. About 1832, the benefits of lap upon the valve, which had

[026-126] been employed by Boulton and Watt more than twenty years before, were

[026-127] beginning to be pretty generally apprehended; and, in the following year,

[026-128] this expedient of economy was applied to the steamer Manchester, in the

[026-129] Clyde, and to some other vessels, with very marked success. Shortly after

[026-130] this time, lap began to be applied to the valves of locomotives, and it was

[026-131] found that not only was there a benefit from the operation of expansion,

[026-132] but that there was a still greater benefit from the superior facility of

[026-133] escape given to the steam, inasmuch as the application of lap involved the

[026-134] necessity of turning the eccentric round upon the shaft, which caused the

[026-135] eduction to take place before the end of the stroke. In 1840, one of the

[026-136] engines of the Liverpool and Manchester Railway was altered so as to have 1

[026-137] inch lap on the valve, and 1 inch opening on the eduction side at the end

[026-138] of the stroke, the valve having a total travel of 4-1/4 inches. The

[026-139] consumption of fuel per mile fell from 36.3 lbs. to 28.6 lbs, or about 25

[026-140] per cent., and a softer blast sufficed. By using larger exhaust passages,

[026-141] larger tubes, and closer fire bars, the consumption was subsequently

[026-142] brought down to 15 lbs. per mile.

[026-143] AIR PUMP, CONDENSER, AND HOT AND COLD WATER PUMPS.

[026-144] 324. _Q._--Will you state the proper dimensions of the air pump and

[026-145] condenser in laud and marine engines?

[026-146] _A_--Mr. Watt made the air pump of his engine half the diameter of the

[026-147] cylinder and half the stroke, or one eighth of the capacity, and the

[026-148] condenser was usually made about the same size as the air pump; but as the

[026-149] pressure of the steam has been increased in all modern engines, it is

[026-150] better to make the air pump a little larger than this proportion. 0.6 of

[026-151] the diameter of the cylinder and half the stroke answers very well, and the

[026-152] condenser may be made as large as it can be got with convenience, though

[026-153] the same size as the air pump will suffice.

[026-154] 325. _Q._--Are air pumps now sometimes made double acting?

[026-155] _A._--Most of the recent direct acting marine engines for driving the screw

[026-156] are fitted with a double acting air pump, and when the air pump is double

[026-157] acting, it need only be about half the size that is necessary when it is

[026-158] single acting. It is single acting in nearly every case, except the case of

[026-159] direct acting screw engines of recent construction.

[026-160] 326. _Q._--What is the difference between a single and a double acting air

[026-161] pump?

[026-162] _A._--The single acting air pump expels the air and water from the

[026-163] condenser only in the upward stroke of the pump, whereas a double acting

[026-164] air pump expels the air and water both in the upward and downward stroke.

[026-165] It has, therefore, to be provided with inlet and outlet valves at both

[026-166] ends, whereas the single acting pump has only to be provided with an inlet

[026-167] or foot valve, as it is termed, at the bottom, and with an outlet or

[026-168] delivery valve, as it is termed, at the top. The single acting air pump

[026-169] requires to be provided with a valve or valves in the piston or bucket of

[026-170] the pump, to enable the air and water lying below the bucket when it begins

[026-171] to descend, and which have entered from the condenser during the upward

[026-172] stroke, to pass through the bucket into the space above it during the

[026-173] downward stroke, from whence they are expelled into the atmosphere on the

[026-174] upward stroke succeeding. But in the double acting air pump no valve is

[026-175] required in the piston or bucket of the pump, and all that is necessary is

[026-176] an inlet and outlet valve at each end.
