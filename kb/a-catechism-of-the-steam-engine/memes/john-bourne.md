# John Bourne

**John Bourne** (fl. 1840s–1860s) was an English civil engineer and technical author. He wrote *A Catechism of the [[steam|Steam]] Engine* (first published 1847), which became a standard textbook for [[marine-engine|marine engine]]ers throughout the Victorian era. He was also a partner in the engine-building firm John Bourne & Co.

## Career and Contributions

Bourne's principal contribution to [[steam|steam engine]]ering was his writing. *A Catechism of the [[steam|Steam]] Engine* distilled the practical and theoretical knowledge of [[steam|steam engine]]ering into an accessible question-and-answer format, covering boilers, engines, [[screw-propeller|propeller]]s, and auxiliary machinery. The work drew on the latest experimental data and the practices of leading engine builders of the period.

As an engineer, Bourne also published on the [[screw-propeller|screw]] [[screw-propeller|propeller]], contributing to the technical debate over [[screw-propeller|screw]] versus [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] propulsion that dominated mid-nineteenth-century naval architecture (051-053).

## Engineering Works

Through his firm John Bourne & Co., Bourne constructed [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s including those for the [[screw-propeller|screw]] steamer *Alma*. The firm's engines were noted for their compact direct-acting design, suitable for naval and commercial vessels (055-137, 068-076, 068-318).

## Legacy

Bourne's *Catechism* served as the standard textbook for a generation of [[marine-engine|marine engine]]ers. Its methodical approach and comprehensive treatment of steam technology made it an essential reference in engine works, drawing offices, and naval academies. The book remains a valuable historical source for understanding nineteenth-century [[steam|steam engine]]ering practice.
