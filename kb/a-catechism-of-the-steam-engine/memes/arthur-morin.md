# Arthur Morin (1795–1880)

Arthur Jules Morin was a French physicist and military engineer who conducted some of the most complete early experiments on mechanical friction. His findings were widely cited by [[steam|steam engine]] designers for the proper sizing of bearings and determination of frictional losses.

## Career and Contributions

Morin served in the French army as an engineer and later became director of the Conservatoire des Arts et Métiers in Paris. His most influential work was on the friction of surfaces sliding against one another — a topic of direct practical importance to [[steam|steam engine]] builders who needed to minimize power losses in [[cylinder]] walls, [[piston]] rods, and shaft [[bearing]]s.

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] introduces Morin's work with the question: "Has not M. Morin, in France, made some very complete experiments to determine the friction of surfaces of different kinds sliding upon one another?" (005-094) The answer acknowledges Morin's thoroughness but notes that "the result does not differ materially from what is stated" by earlier British engineers.

Nevertheless, Morin's precise measurements provided valuable data. "Upon the whole, M. Morin found the resistance due to friction to be somewhat greater than it has been found to be by various other engineers." (005-098) When surfaces were merely wiped with a greasy cloth — without a proper film of lubricating material — Morin found the friction of brass upon cast iron to be 0.107, or about one-tenth of the load. This finding underscored the importance of proper [[lubrication]] in [[steam|steam engine]] maintenance.

Morin also contributed rules for determining the proper surface area of bearings based on load and speed, and studied the friction of rough surfaces. His work complemented that of [[John Southern|Southern]] in En[[stuffing-box|gland]], together forming the empirical basis for mechanical efficiency calculations in [[steam|steam engine]] design.

## Legacy

Morin's friction experiments were a standard reference for mechanical engineers throughout the nineteenth century. His data tables — covering different material pairings, lubrication conditions, and surface finishes — enabled engineers to calculate bearing dimensions and power losses with greater confidence than had previously been possible.
