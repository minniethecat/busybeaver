The **screw propeller** is a rotary propulsion device used in [[marine-engine|marine engines]] to drive vessels through water. It consists of two or more twisted blades (arms) mounted on a shaft, forming what is essentially a short segment of a helical screw. The screw propeller replaced the [[paddle-wheel]] as the dominant means of steam vessel propulsion during the mid-nineteenth century.

## Configuration and mode of action

The screw propeller transforms rotary motion from the engine shaft into forward thrust. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes its basic geometry:

> [010-143] A screw propeller may be supposed to be a short piece cut off a screw of large diameter like a spiral stair, and the pitch of a spiral stair is the vertical height from any given step to the step immediately overhead.

> [049-072] The ordinary form of screw propeller is represented in figs. 46 and 47; fig. 46 being a perspective view, and fig. 47 an end view, or view such as is seen when looking upon the end of the shaft. The screw here represented is one with two arms or blades.

The number of arms corresponds to the number of helical threads:

> [049-109] The threads, in the case of a screw propeller, answer to the arms, so that in every screw propeller the number of threads entering into the composition of the screw will be the same as the number of arms. An ordinary screw with two blades is a short piece of a screw of two threads.

## Historical development

Early screw propellers used multiple complete turns of the helix, but practical experience led to significant shortening:

> [049-088] The original screw propellers were made with several convolutions of screw, but it was found advantageous to shorten them, until they are now only made one-sixth of a convolution in length.

This shortening improved efficiency by reducing frictional drag while maintaining effective thrust. The [[great-exhibition-1851|Great Exhibition of 1851]] featured several screw propeller designs alongside other marine innovations.

## Slip and efficiency

A key performance metric for screw propellers is *slip* — the difference between the theoretical advance (based on pitch and rotation speed) and the actual forward progress of the vessel:

> [049-127] In well formed screw propellers what is the amount of slip found to be? If the screw be properly proportioned to the resistance that the vessel has to overcome, the slip will not be more than 10 per cent., but in some cases it amounts to 30 per cent., or even more than this.

## Comparison with paddle wheels

The relative merits of screw versus [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] propulsion were actively debated in the mid-nineteenth century, and [[john-bourne|Bourne]] offers a balanced assessment:

> [049-149] Is the screw propeller as effectual an instrument of propulsion as the radial or feathering paddle? In all cases of deep immersion it appears to be quite as effectual as the radial paddle, indeed, more so; but it is scarcely as effectual as the feathering paddle, with any amount of immersion, and scarcely as effectual as the common paddle in the case of light immersions.

The screw was found particularly advantageous for vessels with deep draft, contributing to its eventual dominance in ocean-going [[steam]] navigation.

## Direct drive arrangements

In many screw engines, the propeller was driven directly from the engine [[crank-shaft|crankshaft]] without intermediate gearing:

> [017-100] employed to drive the screw propeller without intermediate gearing, India rubber discs, resting on a perforated metal plate, are now generally adopted.

This direct-drive approach simplified the machinery and reduced transmission losses, though it required the engine to operate at the propeller's optimal rotational speed.
