[046-001] 524. _Q._--At what part of the boiler is the feed water admitted?

[046-002] _A._--The feed pipe of most locomotive engines enters the boiler near the

[046-003] bottom and about the middle of its length. In Stephenson's engine the water

[046-004] is let in at the smoke box end of the boiler, a little below the water

[046-005] level; by this means the heat is more fully extracted from the escaping

[046-006] smoke, but the arrangement is of questionable applicability to engines of

[046-007] which the steam dome and steam pipe are at the smoke box end, as in that

[046-008] case the entering cold water would condense the steam.

[046-009] 525. _Q._--How are the pipes connecting the tender and locomotive

[046-010] constructed, so as to allow of play between the engine and tender without

[046-011] leakage?

[046-012] _A._--The pipes connecting the tender with the pumps should allow access to

[046-013] the valves and free motion to the engine and tender. This end is attained

[046-014] by the use of ball and socket joints; and, to allow some end play, one

[046-015] piece of the pipe slides into the other like a telescope, and is kept tight

[046-016] by means of a stuffing box. Any pipe joint between the engine and tender

[046-017] must be made in this fashion.

[046-018] 526. _Q._--Have you any suggestion to make respecting the arrangement of

[046-019] the feed pump?

[046-020] _A._--It would be a material improvement if a feed pump was to be set in

[046-021] the tender and worked by means of a small engine, such as that now used in

[046-022] steam vessels for feeding the boilers. The present action of the feed pumps

[046-023] of locomotives is precarious, as, if the valves leak in the slightest

[046-024] degree, the steam or boiling water from the boiler will prevent the pumps

[046-025] from drawing. It appears expedient, therefore, that at least one pump

[046-026] should be far from the boiler and should be set among the feed water, so

[046-027] that it will only have to force. If a pump was arranged in the manner

[046-028] suggested, the boiler could still be fed regularly, though the locomotive

[046-029] was standing still; but it would be prudent to have the existing pumps

[046-030] still wrought in the usual way by the engine, in case of derangement of the

[046-031] other, or in case the pump in the tender might freeze.

[046-032] 527. _Q._--Will you explain the construction of locomotive wheels?

[046-033] _A._--The wheels of a locomotive are always made of malleable iron. The

[046-034] driving wheels are made larger to increase the speed; the bearing wheels

[046-035] also are easier on the road when large. In the goods engines the driving

[046-036] wheels are smaller than in the passenger engines, and are generally coupled

[046-037] together. Wheels are made with much variety in their constructive details:

[046-038] sometimes they are made with cast iron naves, with the spokes and rim of

[046-039] wrought iron; but in the best modern wheels the nave is formed of the ends

[046-040] of the spokes welded together at the centre. When cast iron naves are

[046-041] adopted, the spokes are forged out of flat bars with T-formed heads, and

[046-042] are arranged radially in the founder's mould, the cast iron, when fluid,

[046-043] being poured among them. The ends of the T heads are then welded together

[046-044] to constitute the periphery of the wheel or inner tire; and little

[046-045] wedge-form pieces are inserted where there is any deficiency of iron. In

[046-046] some cases the arms are hollow, though of wrought iron; the tire of wrought

[046-047] iron, and the nave of cast iron; and the spokes are turned where they are

[046-048] fitted into the nave, and are secured in their sockets by means of cutters.

[046-049] Hawthorn makes his wheels with cast iron naves and wrought iron rims and

[046-050] arms; but instead of welding the arms together, he makes palms on their

[046-051] outer end, which are attached by rivets to the rim. These rivets, however,

[046-052] unless very carefully formed, are apt to work loose; and it would probably

[046-053] be found an improvement if the palms were to be slightly indented into the

[046-054] rim, in cases in which the palms do not meet each other at the ends. When

[046-055] the rim is turned it is ready for the tire, which is now made of steel.

[046-056] 528. _Q._--How do you find the length of bar necessary for forming a tire?

[046-057] _A._--To find the proper length of bar requisite for the formation of a

[046-058] hoop of any given diameter, add the thickness of the bar to the required

[046-059] diameter, and the corresponding circumference in the table of

[046-060] circumferences of circles is the length of the bar. If the iron be bent

[046-061] edgewise the breadth of the bar must be added to the diameter, for it is

[046-062] the thickness of the bar measured radially that is to be taken into

[046-063] consideration. In the tires of railway wheels, which have a flange on one

[046-064] edge, it is necessary to add not only the thickness of the tire, but also

[046-065] two thirds of the depth of the flange; generally, however, the tire bars

[046-066] are sent from the forge so curved that the plain edge of the tire is

[046-067] concave, and the flange edge convex, while the side which is afterward to

[046-068] be bent into contact with the cylindrical surface of the wheel is a plane.

[046-069] In this case the addition of the diameter of two thirds of the depth of the

[046-070] flange is unnecessary, for the curving of the flange edge has the effect of

[046-071] increasing the real length of the bar. When the tire is thus curved, it is

[046-072] only necessary to add the thickness of the hoop to the diameter, and then

[046-073] to find the circumference from a table; or the same result will be obtained

[046-074] by multiplying the diameter thus increased by the thickness of the hoop by

[046-075] 3.1416.

[046-076] 529. _Q._--How are the tires attached to the wheels?

[046-077] _A._--The materials for wheel tires are first swaged separately, and then

[046-078] welded together under the heavy hammer at the steel works; after which they

[046-079] are bent to the circle, welded, and turned to certain gauges. The tire is

[046-080] now heated to redness in a circular furnace; during the time it is getting

[046-081] hot, the iron wheel, turned to the right diameter, is bolted down upon a

[046-082] face plate or surface; the tire expands with the heat, and when at a cherry

[046-083] red, it is dropped over the wheel, for which it was previously too small,

[046-084] and it is also hastily bolted down to the surface plate; the whole mass is

[046-085] then quickly immersed by a swing crane in a tank of water five feet deep,

[046-086] and hauled up and down till nearly cold; the tires are not afterward

[046-087] tempered. The tire is attached to the rim with rivets having countersunk

[046-088] heads, and the wheel is then fixed on its axle.

[046-089] 530. _Q._--Is it necessary to have the whole tire of steel?

[046-090] _A._--It is not indispensable that the whole tire should be of steel; but a

[046-091] dovetail groove, turned out of the tire at the place where it bears most on

[046-092] the rail, and fitted with a band of steel, will suffice. This band may be

[046-093] put in in pieces, and the expedient appears to be the best way of repairing

[046-094] a worn tire; but particular care must be taken to attach these pieces very

[046-095] securely to the tire by rivets, else in the rapid revolution of the wheel

[046-096] the steel may be thrown out by the centrifugal force. In aid of such

[046-097] attachment the steel, after being introduced, is well hammered, which

[046-098] expands it sideways until it fills the dovetail groove.

[046-099] 531. _Q._--Is any arrangement adopted to facilitate the passage of the

[046-100] locomotive round curves?

[046-101] _A._--The tire is turned somewhat conical, to facilitate the passage of the

[046-102] engine round curves--the diameter of the outer wheel being virtually

[046-103] increased by the centrifugal force of the engine, and that of the inner

[046-104] wheel being correspondingly diminished, whereby the curve is passed without

[046-105] the resistance which would otherwise arise from the inequality of the

[046-106] spaces passed over by wheels of the same diameter fixed upon the same axle.

[046-107] The rails, moreover, are not set quite upright, but are slightly inclined

[046-108] inward, in consequence of which the wheels must be either conical or

[046-109] slightly dished, to bear fairly upon the rails. One benefit of inclining

[046-110] the rails in this way, and coning the tires, is that the flange of the

[046-111] wheels is less liable to bear against the sides of the rail, and with the

[046-112] same view the flanges of all the wheels are made with large fillets in the

[046-113] corners. Wheels have been placed loose upon the axle, but they have less

[046-114] stability, and are not now much used. Nevertheless this plan appears to be

[046-115] a good one if properly worked out.

[046-116] 532. _Q._--Are any precautions taken to prevent engines from being thrown

[046-117] off the rails by obstructions left upon the line?

[046-118] _A_.--In most engines a bar is strongly attached to the front of the

[046-119] carriage on each side, and projects perpendicularly downward to within a

[046-120] short distance of the rail, to clear away stones or other obstructions that

[046-121] might occasion accidents if the engine ran over them.

[046-125] RESISTANCE OF VESSELS IN WATER.

[046-126] 533. _Q._--How do you determine the resistance encountered by a vessel

[046-127] moving in water?

[046-128] _A._--The resistance experienced by vessels moving in water varies as the

[046-129] square of the velocity of their motion, or nearly so; and the power

[046-130] necessary to impart an increased velocity varies nearly as the cube of such

[046-131] increased velocity. To double the velocity of a steam vessel, therefore,

[046-132] will require four times the amount of tractive force, and as that

[046-133] quadrupled force must act through twice the distance in the same time, an

[046-134] engine capable of exerting eight times the original power will be

[046-135] required.[1]

[046-136] 534. _Q._--In the case of a board moving in water in the manner of a paddle

[046-137] float, or in the case of moving water impinging on a stationary board, what

[046-138] will be the pressure produced by the impact?

[046-139] _A._--The pressure produced upon a flat board, by striking water at right

[046-140] angles to the surface of the board, will be equal to the weight of a column

[046-141] of water having the surface struck as a base, and for its altitude twice

[046-142] the height due to the velocity with which the board moves through the

[046-143] water. If the board strike the water obliquely, the resistance will be

[046-144] less, but no very reliable law has yet been discovered to determine its

[046-145] amount.

[046-146] 535. _Q._--Will not the resistance of a vessel in moving through the water

[046-147] be much less than that of a flat board of the area of the cross section?

[046-148] _A._--It will be very much less, as is manifest from the comparatively

[046-149] small area of paddle board, and the small area of the circle described by

[046-150] the screw, relatively with the area of the immersed midship section of the

[046-151] vessel. The absolute speed of a vessel, with any given amount of power,

[046-152] will depend very much upon her shape.

[046-153] 536. _Q._--In what way is it that the shape of a vessel influences her

[046-154] speed, since the vessels of the same sectional area must manifestly put in

[046-155] motion a column of water of the same magnitude, and with the same velocity?

[046-156] _A._--A vessel will not strike the water with the same velocity when the

[046-157] bow lines are sharp as when they are otherwise; for a very sharp bow has

[046-158] the effect of enabling the vessel to move through a great distance, while

[046-159] the particles of water are moved aside but a small distance, or in other

[046-160] words, it causes the velocity with which the water is moved to be very

[046-161] small relatively with the velocity of the vessel; and as the resistance

[046-162] increases as the square of the velocity with which the water is moved, it

[046-163] is conceivable enough in what way a sharp bow may diminish the resistance.
