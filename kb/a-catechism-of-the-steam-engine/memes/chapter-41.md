[041-001] 475. _Q._--What is the best mode of making pipes tight where they penetrate

[041-002] the ship's side?

[041-003] _A._--In wooden vessels the pipes where they pierce the ship's side, should

[041-004] be made tight, as follows:--the hole being cut, a short piece of lead pipe,

[041-005] with a broad flange at one end, should be fitted into it, the place having

[041-006] been previously smeared with white lead, and the pipe should then be beaten

[041-007] on the inside, until it comes into close contact all around with the wood.

[041-008] A loose flange should next be slipped over the projecting end of the lead

[041-009] pipe, to which it should be soldered, and the flanges should both be nailed

[041-010] to the timber with scupper nails, white lead having been previously spread

[041-011] underneath. This method of procedure, it is clear, prevents the possibility

[041-012] of leakage down through the timbers; and all, therefore, that has to be

[041-013] guarded against after this precaution, is to prevent leakage into the ship.

[041-014] To accomplish this object, let the pipe which it is desired to attach be

[041-015] put through the leaden hause, and let the space between the pipe and the

[041-016] lead be packed with gasket and white lead, to which a little olive oil has

[041-017] been added. The pipe must have a flange upon it to close the hole in the

[041-018] ship's side; the packing must then be driven in from the outside, and be

[041-019] kept in by means of a gland secured with bolts passing through the ship's

[041-020] side. If the pipe is below the water line the gland must be of brass, but

[041-021] for the waste water pipe a cast iron gland will answer. This method of

[041-022] securing pipes penetrating the side, however, though the best for wooden

[041-023] vessels, will, it is clear, fail to apply to iron ones. In the case of iron

[041-024] vessels, it appears to be the best practice to attach a short iron nozzle,

[041-025] projecting inward from the skin, for the attachment of every pipe below the

[041-026] water line, as the copper or brass would waste the iron of the skin if the

[041-027] attachment were made in the usual way.

[041-028] DETAILS OF THE SCREW AND SCREW SHAFT.

[041-029] 476. _Q._--What is the best method of fixing the screw upon the shaft?

[041-030] _A._--The best way is to cut two large grooves in the shaft coming up to a

[041-031] square end, and two corresponding grooves or key seats in the screw boss

[041-032] opposite the arms. Fit into the grooves on the shaft keys with heads, the

[041-033] length of which is equal to half the depth of the boss, and with the ends

[041-034] of the keys bearing against the ends of the grooves in the shaft. Then ship

[041-035] on the propeller, and drive other keys of an equal length from the other

[041-036] side of the boss, so that the points of the keys will nearly meet in the

[041-037] middle; next burr up the edge of the grooves upon the heads of the keys, to

[041-038] prevent them from working back; and finally tap a bolt into the side of the

[041-039] boss to penetrate the shaft. Propellers so fitted will never get slack.

[041-040] 477. _Q._--What is the best way of fitting in the screw pipe at the stern?

[041-041] _A._--It should have projecting rings, which should be turned; and cast

[041-042] iron pieces with holes in them, bored out to the sizes of these rings,

[041-043] should be secured to the stern frames, and the pipe be then shipped through

[041-044] all. Before this is done, however, the stern post must be bored out by a

[041-045] template to fit the pipe, and the pipe is to be secured at the end to the

[041-046] stern post either by a great external nut of cast iron, or by bolts passing

[041-047] through the stern post and through lugs on the pipe. The pipe should be

[041-048] bored throughout its entire length, and the shaft should be turned so as to

[041-049] afford a very long bearing which will prevent rapid wear.

[041-050] 478. _Q._--How is the hole formed in the deadwood of the ship in which the

[041-051] screw works?

[041-052] _A._--A great frame of malleable iron, the size of the hole, is first set

[041-053] up, and the plating of the ship is brought to the edge of this hole, and is

[041-054] riveted through the frame. It is important to secure this frame very firmly

[041-055] to the rest of the ship, with which view it is advisable to form a great

[041-056] palm, like the palm of a vice, on its inner superior corner, which,

[041-057] projecting into the ship, may be secured by breast-hook plates to the

[041-058] sides, whereby the strain which the screw causes will be distributed over

[041-059] the stern, instead of being concentrated on the rivets of the frame.

[041-060] 479. _Q._--Are there several lengths of screw shaft?

[041-061] _A._--There are.

[041-062] 480. _Q._--How then are these secured to one another?

[041-063] _A._--The best mode of securing the several lengths of shaft together is by

[041-064] forging the shafts with flanges at the ends, which are connected together

[041-065] by bolts, say six strong bolts in each, accurately fitted to the holes.

[041-068] 481. _Q._--How is the thrust of the shaft usually received?

[041-069] _A._--In some cases it is received on a number of metal discs set in a box

[041-070] containing oil; and should one of these discs stick fast from friction, the

[041-071] others will be free to revolve. This arrangement, which is represented in

[041-072] fig. 44, is used pretty extensively and answers the purpose perfectly. It

[041-073] is of course necessary that the box in which the discs A are set, shall be

[041-074] strong enough to withstand the thrust which the screw occasions. Another

[041-075] arrangement still more generally used, is that represented in figs. 55 and

[041-076] 56, p. 331. It is a good practice to make the thrust plummer block with a

[041-077] very long sole in the direction of the shaft, so as to obviate any risk of

[041-078] canting or springing forward when the strain is applied, as such a

[041-079] circumstance, if occurring even to a slight extent, would be very likely to

[041-080] cause the bearing to heat.

[041-081] 482. _Q._--Are there not arrangements existing in some vessels for enabling

[041-082] the screw to be lifted out of the water while the vessel is at sea?

[041-083] _A._--There are; but such arrangements are not usual in merchant vessels.

[041-084] In one form of apparatus the screw is set on a short shaft in the middle of

[041-085] a sliding frame, which can be raised or lowered in grooves like a window

[041-086] and the screw shaft within the ship can be protruded or withdrawn by

[041-087] appropriate mechanism, so as to engage or leave free this short shaft as

[041-088] may be required. When the screw has to be lifted, the screw shaft is drawn

[041-089] into the vessel, leaving the short shaft free to be raised up by the

[041-090] sliding frame, and the frame is raised by long screws turned round by a

[041-091] winch purchase on deck. A chain or rope, however, is better for the purpose

[041-092] of raising this frame, than long screws; but the frame should in such case

[041-093] be provided with pall catches like those of a windlass, which, if the rope

[041-094] should break, will prevent the screw from falling.

[041-095] DETAILS OF THE PADDLES AND PADDLE SHAFT.

[041-096] 483. _Q._--What are the most important details of the construction of

[041-097] paddle wheels?

[041-098] _A._--The structure of the feathering wheel will be hereafter described in

[041-099] connection with an account of the oscillating engine; and it will be

[041-100] expedient now to restrict any account of the details to the common radial

[041-101] paddle, as applied to ocean steamers. The best plan of making the paddle

[041-102] centres is with square eyes, and each centre should be secured in its place

[041-103] by means of eight thick keys. The shaft should be burred up against the

[041-104] head of these keys with a chisel, so as to prevent the keys from coming

[041-105] back of their own accord. If the keys are wanted to be driven back, this

[041-106] burr must be cut off, and if made thick, and of the right taper, they may

[041-107] then be started without difficulty. The shaft must of course be forged with

[041-108] square projections on it, so as to be suitable for the application of

[041-109] centres with square eyes. Messrs. Maudslay & Co. bore out their paddle

[041-110] centres, and turn a seat for them on the shaft, afterward fixing them on

[041-111] the shaft with a single key. This plan is objectionable for the two

[041-112] reasons, that it is insecure when new, and when old is irremovable. The

[041-113] general practice among the London engineers is to fix the paddle arms at

[041-114] the centre to a plate by means of bolts, a projection being placed upon the

[041-115] plates on each side of the arm, to prevent lateral motion; but this method

[041-116] is inferior in durability to that adopted in the Clyde, in which each arm

[041-117] is fitted into a socket by means of a cutter--a small hole being left

[041-118] opposite to the end of each arm, whereby the arm may be forced back by a

[041-119] drift.

[041-120] 484. _Q._--How are the arms attached to the outside rings?

[041-121] _A._--Some engineers join the paddle arms to the outer ring by means of

[041-122] bolts; but unless very carefully fitted, those bolts after a time become

[041-123] slack sideways, and a constant working of the parts of the wheel goes on in

[041-124] consequence. Sometimes the part of the other ring opposite the arm is

[041-125] formed into a mortise, and the arms are wedged tight in these holes by

[041-126] wedges driven in on each side; but the plan is an expensive one, and not

[041-127] satisfactory, as the wedges work loose even though riveted over at the

[041-128] point. The best mode of making a secure attachment of the arms to the ring,

[041-129] consists in making the arms with long T heads, and riveting the cross piece

[041-130] to the outer ring with a number of rivets, not of the largest size, which

[041-131] would weaken the outer ring too much. The best way of securing the inner

[041-132] rings to the arms is by means of lugs welded on the arms, and to which the

[041-133] rings are riveted.

[041-134] 485. _Q._--What are the scantlings of the paddle floats?

[041-135] _A._--The paddle floats are usually made either of elm or pine; if of the

[041-136] former, the common thickness for large sea-going vessels is about 2-1/2

[041-137] inches; if of the latter, 3 inches. The floats should have plates on both

[041-138] sides, else the paddle arms will be very liable to cut into the wood, and

[041-139] the iron of the arms will be very rapidly wasted. When the floats have been

[041-140] fresh put on they must be screwed up several times before they come to a

[041-141] bearing. If this be not done, the bolts will be sure to get slack at sea,

[041-142] and all the floats on the weather side may be washed off. The bolts for

[041-143] holding on the paddle floats are made extra strong, on account of the

[041-144] corrosion to which they are subject; and the nuts should be made large, and

[041-145] should be square, so that they may be effectually tightened up, even though

[041-146] their corners be worn away by corrosion. It is a good plan to give the

[041-147] thread of the paddle bolts a nick with a chisel, after the nut has been

[041-148] screwed up, which will prevent the nut from turning back. Paddle floats,

[041-149] when consisting of more than one board, should be bolted together edgeways,

[041-150] by means of bolts running through their whole breadth. The floats should

[041-151] not be notched to allow of their projection beyond the outer ring, as, if

[041-152] the sides of the notch be in contact with the outer ring, the ring is soon

[041-153] eaten away in that part, and the projecting part of the float, being

[041-154] unsupported, is liable to be broken off.

[041-155] 486. _Q._--Do not the wheels jolt sideways when the vessel rolls?

[041-156] _A._--It is usual to put a steel plate at each end of the paddle shafts

[041-157] tightened with a key, to prevent end play when the vessel rolls, but the

[041-158] arrangement is precarious and insufficient. Messrs. Maudslay make their

[041-159] paddle shaft bearings with very large fillets in the corner, with the view

[041-160] of diminishing the evil; but it would be preferable to make the bearings of

[041-161] the crank shafts spheroidal; and, indeed, it would probably be an

[041-162] improvement if most of the bearings about the engine were to be made in the

[041-163] same fashion. The loose end of the crank pin should be made not spheroidal,

[041-164] but consisting of a portion of a sphere; and a brass bush might then be

[041-165] fitted into the crank eye, that would completely encase the ball of the

[041-166] pin, and yet permit the outer end of the paddle shaft to fall without

[041-167] straining the pin, the bush being at the same time susceptible of a slight

[041-168] end motion. The paddle shaft, where it passes through the vessel's side, is

[041-169] usually surrounded by a lead stuffing box, which will yield if the end of

[041-170] the shaft falls; this stuffing box prevents leakage into the ship from the

[041-171] paddle wheels: but it is expedient, as a further precaution, to have a

[041-172] small tank on the ship's side immediately beneath the stuffing box, with a

[041-173] pipe leading down to the bilge to catch and conduct away any water that may

[041-174] enter around the shaft.
