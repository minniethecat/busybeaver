[009-001] 101. Q.--But what becomes of the cold water which is let into the condenser

[009-002] to condense the steam?

[009-003] A.--It is pumped out by the air pump in the shape of hot water, its

[009-004] temperature having been raised considerably by the admixture of the steam

[009-005] in it. When the air pump piston ascends it leaves behind it a vacuum; and

[009-006] the foot valve being relieved from all pressure, the weight of the water in

[009-007] the condenser forces it open, and the warm water flows from the condenser

[009-008] into the lower part of the air pump, from which its return to the condenser

[009-009] is prevented by the intervening valve. When the air pump piston descends,

[009-010] its pressure on the liquid under it will force open the valve in it,

[009-011] through which the hot water will ascend; and when the bucket descends to

[009-012] the bottom of the pump barrel, the warm water which was below it will all

[009-013] have passed above it, and cannot return. When the bucket next ascends, the

[009-014] water above it, not being able to return through the bucket valve, will be

[009-015] forced into the hot well through the delivery valve S. The hot water pump

[009-016] M, pumps a small quantity of this hot water into the boiler, to compensate

[009-017] for the abstraction of the water that has passed off in the form of steam.

[009-018] The residue of the hot water runs to waste.

[009-019] 102. _Q._--By what expedient is the piston rod enabled to pass through the

[009-020] cylinder cover without leaking steam out of the cylinder or air into it?

[009-021] _A._--The hole in the cylinder lid, through which the piston rod passes, is

[009-022] furnished with a recess called a stuffing box, into which a stuffing or

[009-023] packing of plaited hemp is forced, which, pressing on the one side against

[009-024] the interior of the stuffing box, and on the other side against the piston

[009-025] rod, which is smooth and polished, prevents any leakage in this situation.

[009-026] The packing of this stuffing box is forced down by a ring of metal

[009-027] tightened by screws. This ring, which accurately fits the piston rod, has a

[009-028] projecting flange, through which bolts pass for tightening the ring down

[009-029] upon the packing; and a similar expedient is employed in nearly every case

[009-030] in which packing is employed.

[009-031] 103. _Q._--In what way is the piston rod connected to the great beam?

[009-032] _A._--The piston rod is connected to the great beam by means of two links,

[009-033] one at each side of the beam shown at _f g_, (fig. 21.) These links are

[009-034] usually made of the same length as the crank, and their purpose is to

[009-035] enable the end of the great beam to move in the arc of a circle while the

[009-036] piston rod maintains the vertical position. The point of junction,

[009-037] therefore, of the links and the piston rod is of the form of a knuckle or

[009-038] bend at some parts of the stroke.

[009-039] 104. _Q._--But what compels the top of the piston rod to maintain the

[009-040] vertical position?

[009-041] _A._--Some engines have guide rods set on each side of the piston rod, and

[009-042] eyes on the top of the piston rod engage these guide rods, and maintain the

[009-043] piston rod in a vertical position in every part of the stroke. More

[009-044] commonly, however, the desired end is attained by means of a contrivance

[009-045] called the parallel motion.

[009-046] 105. _Q._--What is the parallel motion?

[009-047] _A._--The parallel motion is an arrangement of jointed rods, so connected

[009-048] together that the divergence from the vertical line at any point in the arc

[009-049] described by the beam is corrected by an equal and opposite divergence due

[009-050] to the arc performed by the jointed rods during the stroke; and as these

[009-051] opposite deviations mutually correct one another, the result is that the

[009-052] piston rod moves in a vertical direction.

[009-053] 106. _Q._--Will you explain the action more in detail?

[009-054] _A._--The pin, fig 21, which passes through the end of the beam at _f_ has

[009-055] a link _f g_ hung on each side of the beam, and a short cross bar, called a

[009-056] cross head, extends from the bottom of one of these links to the bottom of

[009-057] the other, which cross head is perforated with a hole in the middle for the

[009-058] reception of the piston rod. There are similar links _b d_ at the point of

[009-059] the main beam, where the air pump rod is attached. There are two rods _d g_

[009-060] connecting the links _b d_ with the links _f g_, and these rods, as they

[009-061] always continue parallel to the main beam throughout the stroke, are called

[009-062] _parallel bars_. Attached to the end of these two rods at _d_ are two other

[009-063] rods _c d_, of which the ends at _c_ are attached to stationary pins, while

[009-064] the ends at _d_ follow the motion of the lower ends of the links _b d_.

[009-065] These rods are called the _radius bars_. Now it is obvious that the arc

[009-066] described by the point _d_, with _c_ as a centre, is opposite to the arc

[009-067] described by the point _g_ with _d_ as a centre. The rod _d g_ is,

[009-068] therefore, drawn back horizontally by the arc described at _d_ to an extent

[009-069] equal to the versed sine of the arc described at _g_, or, in other words,

[009-070] the line described by the point _g_ becomes a straight line instead of a

[009-071] curve.

[009-073] 107. _Q._--Does the air pump rod move vertically as well as the piston rod?

[009-074] _A._--It does. The air pump rod is suspended from a cross head, passing

[009-075] from the centre of one of the links _b d_ to the centre of the other link,

[009-076] on the opposite side of the beam. Now, as the distance from the central

[009-077] axis of the great beam to the point _b_ is equal to the length of the rod

[009-078] _c d_, it will follow that the upper end of the link will follow one arc,

[009-079] and the lower end an equal and opposite arc. A point in the centre of the

[009-080] link, therefore, where these opposite motions meet, will follow no arc at

[009-081] all, but will move up and down vertically in a straight line.

[009-082] 108. _Q._--The use of the crank is to obtain a circular motion from a

[009-083] reciprocating motion?

[009-084] _A._--That is the object of it, and it accomplishes its object in a very

[009-085] perfect manner, as it gradually arrests the velocity of the piston towards

[009-086] the end of the stroke, and thus obviates what would otherwise be an

[009-087] injurious shock upon the machine. When the crank approaches the lowest part

[009-088] of its throw, and at the same time the piston is approaching the top of the

[009-089] cylinder, the motion of the crank becomes nearly horizontal, or, in other

[009-090] words, the piston is only advanced through a very short distance, for any

[009-091] given distance measured on the circle described by the crank pin. Since,

[009-092] then, the velocity of rotation of the crank is nearly uniform, it will

[009-093] follow that the piston will move very slowly as it approaches the end of

[009-094] the stroke; and the piston is brought to a state of rest by this gradually

[009-095] retarded motion, both at the top and the bottom of the stroke.

[009-096] 109. _Q._--What causes the crank to revolve at a uniform velocity?

[009-097] _A._--The momentum of the machinery moved by the piston, but more

[009-098] especially of the fly wheel, which by its operation redresses the unequal

[009-099] pressures communicated by the crank, and compels the crank shaft to revolve

[009-100] at a nearly uniform velocity. Everyone knows that a heavy wheel if put into

[009-101] rapid rotation cannot be immediately stopped. At the beginning and end of

[009-102] the stroke when the crank is vertical, no force of torsion can be exerted

[009-103] on the crank shaft by the crank, but this force is at its maximum when the

[009-104] crank is horizontal. From the vertical point, where this force is nothing,

[009-105] to the horizontal point, where it is at its maximum, the force of torsion

[009-106] exerted on the crank shaft is constantly varying; and the fly wheel by its

[009-107] momentum redresses these irregularities, and carries the crank through that

[009-108] "dead point," as it is termed, where the piston cannot impart any rotative

[009-109] force.

[009-110] 110. _Q._--Are the configuration and structure of the steam engine, as it

[009-111] left the hand of Watt, materially different from those of modern engines?

[009-112] _A._--There is not much difference. In modern rotative land engines, the

[009-113] valves for admitting the steam to the cylinder or condenser, instead of

[009-114] being clack or pot-lid valves moved by tappets on the air pump rod, are

[009-115] usually sluice or sliding valves, moved by an eccentric wheel on the crank

[009-116] shaft. Sometimes the beam is discarded altogether, and malleable iron is

[009-117] more largely used in the construction of engines instead of the cast iron,

[009-118] which formerly so largely prevailed. But upon the whole the steam engine of

[009-119] the present day is substantially the engine of Watt; and he who perfectly

[009-120] understands the operation of Watt's engine, will have no difficulty in

[009-121] understanding the operation of any of the numerous varieties of engines

[009-122] since introduced.

[009-123] THE MARINE ENGINE.

[009-124] 111. _Q._--Will you describe the principal features of the kind of steam

[009-125] engine employed for the propulsion of vessels?

[009-126] _A._--Marine engines are of two kinds,--paddle engines and screw engines.

[009-127] In the one case the propelling instrument is paddle wheels kept in rotation

[009-128] at each side of the ship: in the other case, the propelling instrument is a

[009-129] screw, consisting of two or more twisted vanes, revolving beneath the water

[009-130] at the stern. Of each class of engines there are many distinct varieties.

[009-131] 112. _Q._--What are the principal varieties of the paddle engine?

[009-134] _A._--There is the side lever engine (fig. 26), and the oscillating engine

[009-135] (fig. 27), besides numerous other forms of engine which are less known or

[009-136] employed, such as the trunk (fig. 22), double cylinder (fig. 23), annular,

[009-137] Gorgon (fig. 24), steeple (fig. 25), and many others. The side lever

[009-138] engine, however, and the oscillating engine, are the only kinds of paddle

[009-139] engines which have been received with wide or general favor.

[009-141] 113. _Q._--Will you explain the main distinctive features of the side lever

[009-142] engine?

[009-143] _A._--In all paddle vessels, whatever be their subordinate characteristics,

[009-144] a great shaft of wrought iron, s, turned round by the engine, has to be

[009-145] carried from side to side of the vessel, on which shaft are fixed the

[009-146] paddle wheels. The paddle wheels may either be formed with fixed float

[009-147] boards for engaging the water, like the boards of a common undershot water

[009-148] wheel, or they may be formed with _feathering_ float boards as they are

[009-149] termed, which is float boards movable on a centre, and so governed by

[009-150] appropriate mechanism that they enter and leave the water in a nearly

[009-151] vertical position. The common fixed or radial floats, however, are the kind

[009-152] most widely employed, and they are attached to the arms of two or more

[009-153] rings of malleable iron which are fixed by appropriate centres on the

[009-154] paddle shaft. It is usual in steam vessels to employ two engines, the

[009-155] cranks of which are set at right angles with one another. When the paddle

[009-156] wheels are turned by the engines, the float boards engaging the water cause

[009-157] a forward thrust to be imparted to the shaft, which propels forward the

[009-158] vessel on the same principle that a boat is propelled by the action of

[009-159] oars.

[009-161] 114. _Q._--These remarks apply to all paddle vessels?

[009-162] _A._--They do. With respect to the side lever engine, it may be described

[009-163] to be such a modification of the land beam engine already described, as

[009-164] will enable it to be got below the deck of a vessel. With this view,

[009-165] instead of a single beam being placed overhead, two beams are used, one of

[009-166] which is set on each side of the engine as low down as possible. The cross

[009-167] head which engages the piston rod is made somewhat longer than the diameter

[009-168] of the cylinder, and two great links or rods proceed one from each end of

[009-169] the cross head to one of the side levers or beams. A similar cross bar at

[009-170] the other end of the beams serves to connect them together and to the

[009-171] connecting rod which, proceeding from thence upwards, engages the crank,

[009-172] and thereby turns round the paddle wheels.
