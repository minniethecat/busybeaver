# Thomas Crampton (1816–1888)

Thomas Russell Crampton was a British [[locomotive|locomotive engine]]er best known for the Crampton locomotive, a distinctive express passenger engine design that placed the driving axle behind the [[fire-box|firebox]] to allow very large driving wheels for high-speed running.

## Career and Contributions

The Crampton locomotive was designed for speed. By placing the single driving axle behind the [[fire-box|firebox]] — rather than ahead of it, as in conventional designs — Crampton freed the driving wheels from the constraints of the [[fire-box|firebox]] width and could use wheels of eight feet in diameter. This large diameter reduced piston speed for a given track speed, improving mechanical efficiency and reducing wear.

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] illustrates "Crampton's express engine, adapted for the ordinary or narrow gauge railways" (011-060) alongside [[daniel-gooch|Gooch]]'s wide-gauge express engine for comparison. The Crampton design featured cylinders 24 inches in diameter with an 18-inch stroke, eight-foot driving wheels, and a total loaded weight of 50 tons including tender.

"In one of Crampton's locomotives, the Liverpool," (011-072) the engine achieved notable performance figures. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] indexes "Crampton's locomotive for the narrow gauge" (069-240), confirming the engine's recognized place in locomotive history. The Crampton type was built both in Britain and by French railways, where the design enjoyed particular success on the long, straight routes between Paris and provincial cities.

## Legacy

The Crampton locomotive's aesthetics — long boiler, low center of gravity, and enormous driving wheels — made it one of the most visually distinctive express engines of the 1840s and 1850s. While the design was ultimately superseded by engines with more coupled axles for adhesion, Crampton's willingness to challenge conventional locomotive layout influenced subsequent designers. French railways operated Crampton locomotives into the 1870s, and preserved examples survive in French railway museums.
