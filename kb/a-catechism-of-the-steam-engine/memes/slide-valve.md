# Slide Valve

The **slide valve** is the most common form of steam distribution valve, consisting of a flat or box-shaped sliding element that moves over a ported face on the side or bottom of the [[Cylinder|cylinder]], alternately admitting steam to and [[eduction|exhaust]]ing it from each end. (015-017) It is driven by an [[Eccentric|eccentric]] mounted on the [[crank|crank shaft]], with which it is connected by a rod or link. (010-016)

## Principle of Operation

The slide valve moves back and forth across a flat surface — the valve face — in which three ports are cut: two outer ports leading to the two ends of the cylinder, and a central port connected to the [[eduction|exhaust]]. (010-035) The valve itself is a hollow box, typically shaped like an inverted D (hence the name **D-valve**), with a cavity that connects the steam port to the [[eduction|exhaust]] port at the appropriate moment. (010-035)

> "The eccentric which works the slide valve is placed upon the crank shaft." (010-016)

As the eccentric rotates with the [[crank|crank shaft]], it imparts a reciprocating motion to the slide valve. (011-033) The eccentric is set ahead of the crank by approximately 90°, so that the valve opens each steam port just before the piston reaches the end of its stroke. The connection between the eccentric and the valve is made through a rod, and a disengaging mechanism allows the connection to be broken by lifting the end of the eccentric rod clear of its pin. (010-025)

## Varieties

There are many varieties of slide valve, differing principally in the length of the valve face and the arrangement of the steam passages. (015-017) The most common are:

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| D-valve (simple slide) | A single hollow box with one cavity; used on low-pressure engines |
| Three-ported valve | An extended valve with separate admission and cut-off edges |
| Double-ported valve | Two admission ports per end for reduced travel |
| Grid-iron valve | Multiple narrow ports to reduce face friction |

An additional slide valve is sometimes mounted on the back of the main valve, serving as an expansion valve to cut off the steam supply at an earlier point in the stroke than is possible with the main valve alone. (016-064) (016-116) This arrangement, known as a **[[expansion-valve|cut-off valve]] on the back of the slide**, provides independent control of the expansion ratio without altering the main valve's timing. (016-117)

## Lap and Expansion

The **lap** of the slide valve — the amount by which the valve face extends beyond the port edges when the valve is at mid-travel — determines the point in the stroke at which steam admission is cut off. (015-164) By increasing the lap, the steam is cut off earlier, giving a greater degree of [[Lap|expansion]] and improving fuel economy.

The slide valve is sometimes placed on the side or at the bottom of the cylinder rather than on the top, depending on the engine configuration and the convenience of access for maintenance. (016-066)

## Regulation of Motion

The throw of the slide valve — the total distance it travels — is determined by the eccentric's throw, and the timing is set by the eccentric's angular position on the shaft. (016-073) The purpose of the eccentric and rod mechanism is to regulate the distribution of steam to the cylinder in precise coordination with the position of the [[Piston|piston]]. (010-079)

In engines where the slide valve is used for both admission and [[eduction|exhaust]], the [[eduction|exhaust]] passage is made as direct as possible to minimise back-pressure, which would otherwise reduce the effective power of the engine. (018-058)
