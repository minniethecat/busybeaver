**Horsepower** is a unit of power measurement that originated with [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s need to compare the output of his [[steam|steam engine]]s to the familiar work of horses. In [[steam|steam engine]]ering practice, it evolved into several distinct measures—most importantly **nominal horsepower** and **indicated horsepower**—used for rating, taxation, and performance assessment of engines.

## Origins with Watt

[[james-watt|James Watt]] established the original equivalence through experiment:

> [003-019] Watt ascertained that a horse could raise 33,000 lbs. one foot high per minute, and this has been adopted as the standard of horsepower.

This definition allowed engine purchasers to understand engine capacity in familiar terms, and became a cornerstone of [[steam|steam engine]] marketing and engineering.

## Nominal horsepower

Nominal horsepower is a calculated (not measured) figure based on the dimensions of the cylinder and a conventional working pressure. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes its application in [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s:

> [010-151] The nominal horsepower of a marine engine is a conventional quantity, and is computed from the area of the piston and the velocity of the piston, assuming a certain pressure of steam in the cylinder.

This measure was used for admiralty ratings and commercial classification but did not reflect actual engine output, which varied with boiler pressure and mechanical condition.

## Indicated horsepower

Indicated horsepower is derived from actual measurements taken by the [[indicator]], an instrument that traces a [[indicator-diagram|pressure-volume diagram]] of the steam in the cylinder:

> [013-036] The indicator is an instrument for tracing the actual pressure of the steam in the cylinder at every point of the stroke, and from this diagram the actual horsepower exerted by the engine may be computed.

> [016-154] The actual horsepower of an engine is the number of times 33,000 lbs. it will raise one foot high per minute, and is determined by multiplying the area of the piston in square inches by the pressure of the steam in lbs. per square inch, and by the velocity of the piston in feet per minute, and dividing by 33,000.

## Comparison and practical significance

The difference between nominal and indicated horsepower could be substantial. An engine rated at 200 nominal horsepower might develop 600 or more indicated horsepower under favorable conditions. This gap reflected improvements in boiler design, expansion use, and [[valve]] timing — factors not captured by the nominal formula.

The [[indicator]] became essential for accurate power measurement:

> [016-163] The indicator diagram shows at a glance whether the engine is in proper adjustment, and whether the valves are properly set.
