[004-011] 38. _Q._--If the motions of a pendulum be dependent on the speed with which

[004-012] a body falls, then a certain ratio must subsist between the distance

[004-013] through which a body falls in a second, and the length of the second's

[004-014] pendulum?

[004-015] _A._--And so there is; the length of the second's pendulum at the level of

[004-016] the sea in London, is 39.1393 inches, and it is from the length of the

[004-017] second's pendulum that the space through which a body falls in a second has

[004-018] been determined. As the time in which a pendulum vibrates is to the time in

[004-019] which a heavy body falls through half the length of the pendulum, as the

[004-020] circumference of a circle is to its diameter, and as the height through

[004-021] which a body falls is as the square of the time of falling, it is clear

[004-022] that the height through which a body will fall, during the vibration of a

[004-023] pendulum, is to half the length of the pendulum as the square of the

[004-024] circumference of a circle is to the square of its diameter; namely, as

[004-025] 9.8696 is to 1, or it is to the whole length of the pendulum as the half of

[004-026] this, namely, 4.9348 is to 1; and 4.9348 times 39.1393 in. is 16-1/12 ft.

[004-027] very nearly, which is the space through which a body falls by gravity in a

[004-028] second.

[004-029] 39. _Q._--Are the motions of the conical pendulum or governor reducible to

[004-030] the same laws which apply to the common pendulum?

[004-031] _A._--Yes; the motion of the conical pendulum may be supposed to be

[004-032] compounded of the motions of two common pendulums, vibrating at right

[004-033] angles to one another, and one revolution of a conical pendulum will be

[004-034] performed in the same time as two vibrations of a common pendulum, of which

[004-035] the length is equal to the vertical height of the point of suspension above

[004-036] the plane of revolution of the balls.

[004-037] 40. _Q._--Is not the conical pendulum or governor of a steam engine driven

[004-038] by the engine?

[004-039] _A._--Yes.

[004-040] 41. _Q._--Then will it not be driven round as any other mechanism would be

[004-041] at a speed proportional to that of the engine?

[004-042] _A._--It will.

[004-043] 42. _Q._--Then how can the length of the arms affect the time of

[004-044] revolution?

[004-045] [Illustration: Fig. 1.]

[004-046] _A._--By flying out until they assume a vertical height answering to the

[004-047] velocity with which they rotate round the central axis. As the speed is

[004-048] increased the balls expand, and the height of the cone described by the

[004-049] arms is diminished, until its vertical height is such that a pendulum of

[004-050] that length would perform two vibrations for every revolution of the

[004-051] governor. By the outward motion of the arms, they partially shut off the

[004-052] steam from the engine. If, therefore, a certain expansion of the balls be

[004-053] desired, and a certain length be fixed upon for the arms, so that the

[004-054] vertical height of the cone is fixed, then the speed of the governor must

[004-055] be such, that it will make half the number of revolutions in a given time

[004-056] that a pendulum equal in length to the height of the cone would make of

[004-057] vibrations. The rule is, multiply the square root of the height of the cone

[004-058] in inches by 0.31986, and the product will be the right time of revolution

[004-059] in seconds. If the number of revolutions and the length of the arms be

[004-060] fixed, and it is wanted to know what is the diameter of the circle

[004-061] described by the balls, you must divide the constant number 187.58 by the

[004-062] number of revolutions per minute, and the square of the quotient will be

[004-063] the vertical height in inches of the centre of suspension above the plane

[004-064] of the balls' revolution. Deduct the square of the vertical height in

[004-065] inches from the square of the length of the arm in inches, and twice the

[004-066] square root of the remainder is the diameter of the circle in which the

[004-067] centres of the balls revolve.

[004-068] 43. _Q._ Cannot the operation of a governor be deduced merely from the

[004-069] consideration of centrifugal and centripetal forces?

[004-070] _A._--It can; and by a very simple process. The horizontal distance of the

[004-071] arm from the spindle divided by the vertical height, will give the amount

[004-072] of centripetal force, and the velocity of revolution requisite to produce

[004-073] an equivalent centrifugal force may be found by multiplying the centripetal

[004-074] force of the ball in terms of its own weight by 70,440, and dividing the

[004-075] product by the diameter of the circle made by the centre of the ball in

[004-076] inches; the square root of the quotient is the number of revolutions per

[004-077] minute. By this rule you fix the length of the arms, and the diameter of

[004-078] the base of the cone, or, what is the same thing, the angle at which it is

[004-079] desired the arms shall revolve, and you then make the speed or number of

[004-080] revolutions such, that the centrifugal force will keep the balls in the

[004-081] desired position.

[004-082] 44. _Q._--Does not the weight of the balls affect the question?

[004-083] _A._--Not in the least; each ball may be supposed to be made up of a number

[004-084] of small balls or particles, and each particle of matter will act for

[004-085] itself. Heavy balls attached to a governor are only requisite to overcome

[004-086] the friction of the throttle valve which shuts off the steam, and of the

[004-087] connections leading thereto. Though the weight of a ball increases its

[004-088] centripetal force, it increases its centrifugal force in the same

[004-089] proportion.

[004-090] THE MECHANICAL POWERS.

[004-091] 45. _Q._--What do you understand by the mechanical powers?

[004-092] _A._--The mechanical powers are certain contrivances, such as the wedge,

[004-093] the screw, the inclined plane, and other elementary machines, which convert

[004-094] a small force acting through a great space into a great force acting

[004-095] through a small space. In the school treatises on mechanics, a certain

[004-096] number of these devices are set forth as the mechanical powers, and each

[004-097] separate device is treated as if it involved a separate principle; but not

[004-098] a tithe of the contrivances which accomplish the stipulated end are

[004-099] represented in these learned works, and there is no very obvious necessity

[004-100] for considering the principle of each contrivance separately when the

[004-101] principles of all are one and the same. Every pressure acting with a

[004-102] certain velocity, or through a certain space, is convertible into a greater

[004-103] pressure acting with a less velocity, or through a smaller space; but the

[004-104] quantity of mechanical force remains unchanged by its transformation, and

[004-105] all that the implements called mechanical powers accomplish is to effect

[004-106] this transformation.

[004-107] 46. _Q._--Is there no power gained by the lever?

[004-108] _A._--Not any: the power is merely put into another shape, just as the

[004-109] contents of a hogshead of porter are the same, whether they be let off by

[004-110] an inch tap or by a hole a foot in diameter. There is a greater gush in the

[004-111] one case than the other, but it will last a shorter time; when a lever is

[004-112] used there is a greater force exerted, but it acts through a shorter

[004-113] distance. It requires just the same expenditure of mechanical power to lift

[004-114] 1 lb. through 100 ft., as to lift 100 lbs. through 1 foot. A cylinder of a

[004-115] given cubical capacity will exert the same power by each stroke, whether

[004-116] the cylinder be made tall and narrow, or short and wide; but in the one

[004-117] case it will raise a small weight through a great height, and in the other

[004-118] case, a great weight through a small height.

[004-119] 47. _Q._--Is there no loss of power by the use of the crank?

[004-120] _A._--Not any. Many persons have supposed that there was a loss of power by

[004-121] the use of the crank, because at the top and bottom centres it is capable

[004-122] of exerting little or no power; but at those times there is little or no

[004-123] steam consumed, so that no waste of power is occasioned by the peculiarity.

[004-124] Those who imagine that there is a loss of power caused by the crank perplex

[004-125] themselves by confounding the vertical with the circumferential velocity.

[004-126] If the circle of the crank be divided by any number of equidistant

[004-127] horizontal lines, it will be obvious that there must be the same steam

[004-128] consumed, and the same power expended, when the crank pin passes from the

[004-129] level of one line to the level of the other, in whatever part of the circle

[004-130] it may be, those lines being indicative of equal ascents or descents of the

[004-131] piston. But it will be seen that the circumferential velocity is greater

[004-132] with the same expenditure of steam when the crank pin approaches the top

[004-133] and bottom centres; and this increased velocity exactly compensates for the

[004-134] diminished leverage, so that there is the same power given out by the crank

[004-135] in each of the divisions.

[004-136] 48. _Q._--Have no plans been projected for gaining power by means of a

[004-137] lever?

[004-138] _A._--Yes, many plans,--some of them displaying much ingenuity, but all

[004-139] displaying a complete ignorance of the first principles of mechanics, which

[004-140] teach that power cannot be gained by any multiplication of levers and

[004-141] wheels. I have occasionally heard persons say: "You gain a great deal of

[004-142] power by the use of a capstan; why not apply the same resource in the case

[004-143] of a steam vessel, and increase the power of your engine by placing a

[004-144] capstan motion between the engine and paddle wheels?" Others I have heard

[004-145] say: "By the hydraulic press you can obtain unlimited power; why not then

[004-146] interpose a hydraulic press between the engines and the paddles?" To these

[004-147] questions the reply is sufficiently obvious. Whatever you gain in force you

[004-148] lose in velocity; and it would benefit you little to make the paddles

[004-149] revolve with ten times the force, if you at the same time caused them to

[004-150] make only a tenth of the number of revolutions. You cannot, by any

[004-151] combination of mechanism, get increased force and increased speed at the

[004-152] same time, or increased force without diminished speed; and it is from the

[004-153] ignorance of this inexorable condition, that such myriads of schemes for

[004-154] the realization of perpetual motion, by combinations of levers, weights,

[004-155] wheels, quicksilver, cranks, and other mere pieces of inert matter, have

[004-156] been propounded.

[004-157] 49. _Q._--Then a force once called into existence cannot be destroyed?

[004-158] _A._--No; force is eternal, if by force you mean power, or in other words

[004-159] pressure acting though space. But if by force you mean mere pressure, then

[004-160] it furnishes no measure of power. Power is not measurable by force but by

[004-161] force and velocity combined.

[004-162] 50. _Q._--Is not power lost when two moving bodies strike one other and

[004-163] come to a state of rest?

[004-164] _A._--No, not even then. The bodies if elastic will rebound from one

[004-165] another with their original velocity; if not elastic they will sustain an

[004-166] alteration of form, and heat or electricity will be generated of equivalent

[004-167] value to the power which has disappeared.
