# Locomotive

A **locomotive** is a self-propelled [[steam|steam engine]] designed to run on rails and draw carriages or wagons. It represents one of the three principal applications of the [[steam|steam engine]], alongside mill work and marine navigation (002-008). The locomotive transforms the expansive power of steam into rotary motion applied directly to the driving wheels, propelling itself and its train forward on the rails by the mere friction between the wheels and the iron track (011-015).

## Operation

The locomotive employs high-pressure steam that, after performing its work in the cylinders, escapes directly into the atmosphere through the [[blast-pipe|blast pipe]] — making it a non-condensing, high-pressure engine (011-018, 011-022). Each locomotive contains two engines driving cranks set at right angles to each other, ensuring that when one crank is at a dead point the other is in an effective position to continue the rotation (011-011, 011-043).

A key feature of the locomotive is the [[blast pipe]], a contracted orifice through which the [[eduction|exhaust]] steam rushes into the chimney. The blast creates a partial vacuum that draws air through the furnace and [[flue|fire-tube]]s, intensifying the combustion proportionate to the quantity of steam being expended (011-039). The tender, a separate carriage attached behind the locomotive, carries coke for feeding the furnace and water for replenishing the boiler (011-050, 011-051).

## Types

Modern locomotives from the mid-19th century employ a horizontal tubular boiler with a [[fire-box]] at one end and a smoke-box at the other, connected by numerous small [[flue|fire-tube]]s that efficiently transmit heat to the surrounding water (011-056). [[thomas-crampton|Crampton]]'s locomotives represent a notable variant with low boiler placement and large driving wheels behind the [[fire-box]], enabling high speeds on express passenger trains (011-072).

Locomotive boilers operate under conditions requiring thin tubes to transmit heat quickly, with a rapid circulation of water maintained through the boiler by the intense evaporation rate demanded by the engine (012-095, 012-132).

## Use in Engines

The locomotive engine differs fundamentally from stationary and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s in its compactness, the necessity of a forced draught, and the direct application of power to the driving wheels without intermediate gearing. The [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] transmits the piston's reciprocating motion directly to the crank on the driving axle (011-033). In outside-cylinder locomotives the cylinders are placed outside the frames, with the piston rod driving onto a crank pin in the driving wheel directly (015-010).
