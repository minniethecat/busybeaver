[060-001] Fig. 71 represents, partly in section and partly in plan, the cylinder,

[060-002] steam chests, valves, &c., of one of the Woodruff & Beach high pressure

[060-003] Engines, Wright's patent.

[060-004] Fig. 72 represents, in elevation, the cam shaft, to the upper end of which,

[060-005] not shown in the drawing, is attached the ordinary centrifugal governor.

[060-006] The cylinder, steam chests, valves, &c., being similar to those of other

[060-007] engines, need no special notice; but the cam for opening and closing the

[060-008] steam valves, fig. 72, requires particular attention, as it embodies a

[060-009] beautiful and simple device for cutting off the steam with certainty at any

[060-010] part of the stroke, the motion being produced automatically by the action

[060-011] of the governor on this cam, throwing it more or less out of centre with

[060-012] the spindle of the governor, as the rotation of the balls is less or more

[060-013] rapid, the eccentricity of the cam determining the amount of steam admitted

[060-014] to the working cylinder of the engine. To produce this effect the cam is

[060-015] made as follows:

[060-016] _C_ is a hollow cylinder or shell, with a part of one end formed into a cam

[060-017] proper. Throughout the whole length of this piece, upon the inside, there

[060-018] is a spiral groove cut to receive one end of a feather, by which its pitch

[060-019] or eccentricity is regulated. _C'_ is also a hollow cylinder or shell, of

[060-020] the same length and diameter as _C_, with a similar spiral groove cut on

[060-021] the inside, the outside being perfectly smooth and plain, upon which the

[060-022] toe (_t_) for closing the valves is fastened. The inside piece consists of

[060-023] two hubs _D, D'_, eccentric with each other, and made in one piece, _D_

[060-024] being turned to exactly fit the inside of the shell _C_, and _D'_ to fit

[060-025] the shell _C'_, the hub _D'_ having a socket (_c_) into which the spindle

[060-026] (_s_) of the governor is screwed; the end (_d_) of the hub _D_ forming a

[060-027] journal or bearing, with a bevel wheel on its extremity to convey motion

[060-028] from the crank-shaft gearing to the governor and cut-off. There is a hole

[060-029] throughout the length of the inside hubs _D_ and _D'_, which is continued

[060-030] through the spindle of the governor, and contains the rod (_r_) that

[060-031] connects the cam with the governor. This hole is eccentric to the outside

[060-032] surface of the hub _D_, as well as to the shell _C_, and concentric with

[060-033] the hub _D'_ and shell _C'_, and with the governor rod (_r_).

[060-034] The shell _C_ and hub _D_, and shell _C'_ and hub _D'_, are connected

[060-035] together by feathers; one piece of each feather is of a spiral form, and

[060-036] the other a straight or rectangular piece, the two being connected together

[060-037] by a stub on the rectangular piece, which fits into a hole or bearing in

[060-038] the other or spiral piece, so that the latter can turn on the stub and

[060-039] accommodate itself to the groove in which it has to work. The spiral part

[060-040] of each feather works in the spiral groove on the inside of its

[060-041] corresponding shell _C_ and _C'_ respectively, and the rectangular pieces

[060-042] work in a straight groove cut in the hubs _D_ and _D'_, the inner parts of

[060-043] the rectangular pieces being fastened to the governor rod (_r_), so that

[060-044] the feathers are permanently connected with the governor.

[060-045] The shell _C'_ revolves inside of two yokes (_y_) and (_y'_), one attached

[060-046] to each steam-valve toe, (_a_) and (_a'_) respectively.

[060-047] On the inside of each yoke, and opposite to its valve-toe, is a raised

[060-048] piece, against which the closing piece (_t_) on the shell (_C'_) acts to

[060-049] close the valves.

[060-050] This shell (_C'_), as before noticed, has a spiral groove on its inside,

[060-051] similar in all respects to that in the cam-shell (_C_); and being acted

[060-052] upon in the same manner and through the same rod by the governor, it is

[060-053] evident that the closing piece (_t_) on its outside will always hold the

[060-054] same relation to the opening toe on the lower or cam-shell (_C_); and

[060-055] whatever alteration is made in the one, a corresponding alteration takes

[060-056] place in the other, thereby insuring the closing of the valves at the

[060-057] proper time at every point of the variation of the cut-off.

[060-058] When the several pieces above described are put together, the apparatus for

[060-059] opening and closing the valves and producing the cut-off is complete, as

[060-060] shown in fig. 72, and it operates as follows:

[060-063] Motion is communicated by gearing from the crank-shaft to the bevel wheel

[060-064] on the piece (_d_) on the end of the hub _D_, and is communicated to the

[060-065] spindle of the governor, which is screwed into the socket on _D'_. As the

[060-066] balls rise or fall, through change of centrifugal force due to the

[060-067] variation in the speed of rotation, they raise or depress the governor-rod,

[060-068] which passes through the spindle and the hubs _D'_ and _D_, and is attached

[060-069] to the feathers, thereby raising or depressing the feathers, which, acting

[060-070] on their respective spiral grooves, instantly alters the lift of the cam on

[060-071] the shell (_C_), and brings the closing toe (_t_) on the shell (_C'_) into

[060-072] proper position for closing, and so regulates the amount of steam admitted

[060-073] to the cylinder.

[060-074] [Illustration: Fig. 71.]

[060-075] Consequently, any speed may be selected at which the load of the engine is

[060-076] to move, and any variation from that will be instantly felt by the

[060-077] governor, and corrected by this simple and beautiful device. There is no

[060-078] jar in the working of the parts; the feathers move noiselessly in their

[060-079] grooves; the governor rod moves up and down through the spindle and the

[060-080] hubs _D_ and _D'_, and can be regulated by hand to give any required

[060-081] opening of the steam ports to suit the work to be done. Any change in the

[060-082] amount of work will then alter the speed of the engine, and so affect the

[060-083] governor and cam, as before said.

[060-084] It is unnecessary to insist on the great economy attained by using steam

[060-085] with a well-regulated cut-off, for practical men know now that the

[060-086] essential points of excellence in the steam engine are a good boiler, which

[060-087] generates the greatest quantity of steam for the least consumption of fuel;

[060-088] and, secondly, a reliable cut-off, which uses the steam to the best

[060-089] advantage, by admitting the proper quantity for the work required.

[060-090] STEAM FIRE ENGINES.--Portable engines for the extinguishment of fires, are

[060-091] an American invention, and to Messrs. A.B. & E. Latta, of Cincinnati,

[060-092] working on the right principles, is due the credit which they claim in

[060-093] their circular, as follows:

[060-094] "We claim to be the _original_ and first _projectors_ of the _first

[060-095] successful steam fire engine_ in the world's history. There have been many

[060-096] attempts at making a machine of such construction as would answer to

[060-097] extinguish fires; but none of them proved to be available in a sufficiently

[060-098] short space of time to warrant their use as a fire apparatus. We hold that

[060-099] a steam fire engine should be of such nature as to be brought into

[060-100] requisition in as short a space of time as is necessary to get the machine

[060-101] on the ground, and the hose laid and ready to work: that is, supposing the

[060-102] fire to be within one square of the place where the steamer is located. The

[060-103] object in locating a machine at any point is to protect that immediate

[060-104] vicinity; and it is therefore absolutely necessary to have it available in

[060-105] the shortest space of time, and that with unerring certainty. We think that

[060-106] reliability is of the greatest importance to the protection of a city from

[060-107] fire, as everything is dependent on the _working_ of such apparatus in

[060-108] time; and for this reason no expense should be spared on this kind of

[060-109] machinery."

[060-110] Fig. 73 is a representation of one of the Messrs. Latta's fire engines, of

[060-111] which there are many of different classes, according to the requirements;

[060-112] they say that they can furnish engines as low as $1,000, and have made some

[060-113] for $10,000.

[060-114] The first peculiar feature of this engine is the boiler; it differs

[060-115] entirely from all boilers now in use.

[060-116] [Illustration: Fig. 73.]

[060-117] The fire box or furnace is simply a square box or furnace of any required

[060-118] dimensions; it is nothing more than a water space surrounding the fire,

[060-119] stay-bolted as all water spaces are. It is made of boiler plate in the

[060-120] usual manner. The water space extends only 2/3 of the height, the balance

[060-121] being a single sheet. The bottom of this fire box is crossed by grate bars

[060-122] to support the fuel; in its rear side are fire doors, inserted for firing.

[060-123] The internal arrangements of the boiler are composed of a large number of

[060-124] tubes, lying across in a horizontal position, put together in sections with

[060-125] return bends resembling the coils for heating buildings. These coils are of

[060-126] small pipe (say one inch in diameter), and as numerous as may be necessary.

[060-127] They give the required amount of steam. They are secured to wrought-iron

[060-128] plates at each end by rivets. These plates lie close to the box, and are

[060-129] secured to it, top and bottom. These tubes are wrought iron, firmly screwed

[060-130] into the bends, so as to prevent any possible breaking.

[060-131] The box has a hole through both sheets, in the same manner as a hollow

[060-132] stay-bolt, through which the coil pipe passes, having no connection with

[060-133] the box. After passing into the box it divides into two pipes, then

[060-134] subdivides into four, and so on, until its numbers equal the number of

[060-135] coils in the box, and to which each limb is attached. The upper ends of

[060-136] these coils are the same in number, and are carried through at the top or

[060-137] nearly the top of the box. They then run down outside to the steam chamber,

[060-138] or rather water space, as the box is both steam chamber and water space.

[060-139] These pipes empty their contents into the box, steam and water, as it may

[060-140] come, all together. It will be observed that these coils of tube are

[060-141] sufficiently separated to allow the fire to pass between them freely, and

[060-142] cover their whole surface.

[060-143] The mode of operation of this boiler is this: The fire box is filled 2/3

[060-144] full of water. The coils are dry at starting; the space for fuel being

[060-145] filled with good wood, the fire is lighted, and in a few moments the

[060-146] engineer moves his hand pump, which takes its water from the box to which

[060-147] it is attached, and forces it through the coils. By this means steam is

[060-148] generated in from 3 to 5 minutes, so as to start the engine.

[060-149] It will be seen that the water performs a complete circuit; it is taken

[060-150] from the box and passed through the coils; what is steam remains in the

[060-151] steam chamber, and what is not (if any) drops back into the box from where

[060-152] it started. Hence it will be seen that a large surface is exposed to a

[060-153] small quantity of water, and in a way that it is entirely controllable. All

[060-154] the engineer has to do to surcharge his steam, is to reduce the speed of

[060-155] the pump (which is independent of the main engine). By raising the heat and

[060-156] quantity of water, any degree of elasticity can be given to the steam, and

[060-157] that, too, with the least amount of waste heat in giving a natural draft.

[060-158] Hence the great economy of this boiler.

[060-159] The next feature of this engine is, it has no wood work about it to perish

[060-160] with the heat and roughness of the streets. All the wheels are wrought

[060-161] iron; and, as yet, these are the only ones that have stood a steam fire

[060-162] engine. The frame is wrought iron; truck, on which the front wheel is hung,

[060-163] wrought iron. The axles are cast steel. The engine and pump is a

[060-164] double-acting piston pump direct, without any rotary motion; with a

[060-165] perfect balance valve, it is balanced at all times, and hence the engine

[060-166] remains quiet without blocking, when at work. The engine is mounted on

[060-167] three wheels, which enables it to be turned in a very short space.
