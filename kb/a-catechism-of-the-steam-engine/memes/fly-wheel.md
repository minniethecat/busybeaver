The **fly wheel** is a heavy revolving wheel attached to the [[crank]] shaft of a [[steam]] engine. Its function is to store kinetic energy during the power stroke and release it during the idle portions of the cycle, thereby equalizing the rotational speed and preventing the jerky motion that would otherwise result from the intermittent nature of the piston's driving force. Together with the [[crank]] and [[connecting-rod]], the fly wheel is a fundamental component of the rotative engine.

## Function

> [009-106] The force exerted on the crank shaft is constantly varying; and the fly wheel by its operation redresses the unequal pressures, and carries the crank through the dead points at which the power is evanescent.

The fly wheel acts as a mechanical energy reservoir. When the [[piston]] delivers surplus torque during the middle of its stroke, the fly wheel accelerates slightly, storing the excess as rotational kinetic energy. When torque falls off near the ends of the stroke (the dead centers), the fly wheel decelerates slightly, returning that stored energy to the shaft and keeping it turning smoothly:

> [009-098] The fly wheel, which by its operation redresses the unequal pressures and carries the crank through those parts of the revolution where the power is insufficient.

## Sizing considerations

The weight and diameter of a fly wheel are determined by how much speed variation is acceptable for the engine's intended duty:

> [028-001] By what considerations do you determine the dimensions of the fly wheel of an engine?
>
> [028-003] By a reference to the power generated, each half stroke of the engine, and the number of half strokes that are necessary to give to the fly wheel its standard velocity, supposing the whole power devoted to that object. In practice the power resident in the fly varies from 2-1/2 to 6 times that generated each half stroke.

> [028-011] If a very equable motion is required, a heavier or swifter fly wheel must be employed.

## Boulton and Watt's rule

[[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] provided an empirical formula for calculating the required cross-sectional area of a cast-iron fly wheel rim:

> [028-013] What is Boulton and Watt's rule for fly wheels?
>
> [028-015] Multiply 44,000 times the square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches by the length of the stroke in feet, and divide this product by the product of the square of the number of revolutions of the fly wheel per minute, multiplied by the cube of its diameter in feet. The quotient is the area of section of the fly wheel rim in square inches.

## High-speed engines

Engines running at higher rotational speeds require lighter fly wheels for the same degree of speed regulation:

> [017-141] The high speed engine does not require so heavy a fly wheel as the low speed engine.
>
> [017-143] No; the fly wheel will be lighter, both by virtue of its greater velocity of rotation, and by the greater uniformity of pressure exerted by the steam.

The fly wheel rim is typically driven by a pulley arrangement from the [[crank|crank shaft]]:

> [008-094] A cord driven by a pulley on the fly wheel shaft.
