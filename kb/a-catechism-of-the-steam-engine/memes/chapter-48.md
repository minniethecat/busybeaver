[048-001] 550. _Q._--Will you further illustrate this doctrine by an example?

[048-002] _A._--The screw steamer Fairy, if enlarged to three times the size while

[048-003] retaining the same form, would have twenty-seven times the capacity, nine

[048-004] times the sectional area, and nine times the power. The length of such a

[048-005] vessel would be 434 feet; her breadth 63 feet 4-1/2 inches; her draught of

[048-006] water 16-1/2 feet; her area of immersed section 729 square feet; and her

[048-007] nominal power 1080 horses. Now as the lengths of the Fairy and of the new

[048-008] vessel are in the proportion of 1 to 3, the speeds will be in the

[048-009] proportion of the square root of 1 to the square root of 3; or, in other

[048-010] words, the speed of the large vessel will be 1.73 times greater than the

[048-011] speed of the small vessel. If therefore the speed of the Fairy be 13 knots,

[048-012] the speed of the new vessel will be 22.49 knots, although the proportion of

[048-013] power to sectional area, which is supposed to be the measure of the

[048-014] resistance, is in both cases precisely the same. If the speed of the Fairy

[048-015] herself had to be increased to 22.29 knots, the power would have to be

[048-016] increased in the proportion of the cube of 13 to the cube of 22.49, or 5.2

[048-017] times, which makes the power necessary to propel the Fairy at that speed

[048-018] equal to 624 nominal horses power.

[048-019] STRUCTURE AND OPERATION OF PADDLE WHEELS.

[048-020] 551. _Q._--Will you describe the configuration and mode of action of the

[048-021] paddle wheels in general use?

[048-022] _A._--There are two kinds of paddle wheels in extensive use, the one being

[048-023] the ordinary radial wheel, in which the floats are fixed on arms radiating

[048-024] from the centre; and the other the feathering wheel, in which each float is

[048-025] hung upon a centre, and is so governed by suitable mechanism as to be

[048-026] always kept in nearly the vertical position. In the radial wheel there is

[048-027] some loss of power from oblique action, whereas in the feathering wheel

[048-028] there is little or no loss from this cause; but in every kind of paddle

[048-029] there is a loss of power from the recession of the water from the float

[048-030] boards, or the _slip_ as it is commonly called; and this loss is the

[048-031] necessary condition of the resistance for the propulsion of the vessel

[048-032] being created in a fluid. The slip is expressed by the difference between

[048-033] the speed of the wheel and the speed of the vessel, and the larger this

[048-034] difference is the greater the loss of power from slip must be--the

[048-035] consumption of steam in the engine being proportionate to the velocity of

[048-036] the wheel, and the useful effect being proportionate to the speed of the

[048-037] ship.

[048-038] 552. _Q._--The resistance necessary for propulsion will not be situated at

[048-039] the circumference of the wheel?

[048-040] _A._--In the feathering wheel, where every part of any one immerged float

[048-041] moves forward with the same horizontal velocity, the pressure or resistance

[048-042] may be supposed to be concentrated in the centre of the float; whereas, in

[048-043] the common radial wheel this cannot be the case, for as the outer edge of

[048-044] the float moves more rapidly than the edge nearest the centre of the wheel,

[048-045] the outer part of the float is the most effectual in propulsion. The point

[048-046] at which the outer and inner portions of the float just balance one another

[048-047] in propelling effect, is called the _centre of pressure_; and if all the

[048-048] resistances were concentrated in this point, they would have the same

[048-049] effect as before in resisting the rotation of the wheel. The resistance

[048-050] upon any one moving float board totally immersed in the water will, when

[048-051] the vessel is at rest, obviously vary as the square of its distance from

[048-052] the centre of motion--the resistance of a fluid varying with the square of

[048-053] the velocity; but, except when the wheel is sunk to the axle or altogether

[048-054] immersed in the water, it is impossible, under ordinary circumstances, for

[048-055] one float to be totally immersed without others being immersed partially,

[048-056] whereby the arc described by the extremity of the paddle arm will become

[048-057] greater than the arc described by the inner edge of the float; and

[048-058] consequently the resistance upon any part of the float will increase in a

[048-059] higher ratio than the square of its distance from the centre of motion--the

[048-060] position of the centre of pressure being at the same time correspondingly

[048-061] affected. In the feathering wheel the position of the centre of pressure of

[048-062] the entering and emerging floats is continually changing from the lower

[048-063] edge of the float--where it is when the float is entering or leaving the

[048-064] water--to the centre of the float, which is its position when the float is

[048-065] wholly immerged; but in the radial wheel the centre of pressure can never

[048-066] rise so high as the centre of the float.

[048-067] 553. _Q._--All this relates to the action of the paddle when the vessel is

[048-068] at rest: will you explain its action when the vessel is in motion?

[048-069] _A._--When the wheel of a coach rolls along the ground, any point of its

[048-070] periphery describes in the air a curve which is termed a cycloid; any point

[048-071] within the periphery traces a prolate or protracted cycloid, and any point

[048-072] exterior to the periphery traces a curtate or contracted cycloid--the

[048-073] prolate cycloid partaking more of the nature of a straight line, and the

[048-074] curtate cycloid more of the nature of a circle. The action of a paddle

[048-075] wheel in the water resembles in this respect that of the wheel of a

[048-076] carriage running along the ground: that point in the radius of the paddle

[048-077] of which the rotative speed is just equal to the velocity of the vessel

[048-078] will describe a cycloid; points nearer the centre, prolate cycloids, and

[048-079] points further from the centre, curtate cycloids. The circle described by

[048-080] the point whose velocity equals the velocity of the ship, is called the

[048-081] _rolling circle_, and the resistance due to the difference of velocity of

[048-082] the rolling circle and centre of pressure is that which operates in the

[048-083] propulsion of the vessel. The resistance upon any part of the float,

[048-084] therefore, will vary as the square of its distance from the rolling circle,

[048-085] supposing the float to be totally immerged; but, taking into account the

[048-086] greater length of time during which the extremity of the paddle acts,

[048-087] whereby the resistance will be made greater, we shall not err far in

[048-088] estimating the resistance upon any point at the third power of its distance

[048-089] from the rolling circle in the case of light immersions, and the 2.5 power

[048-090] in the case of deep immersions.

[048-091] 554. _Q._--How is the position of the centre of pressure to be determined?

[048-092] _A._--With the foregoing assumption, which accords sufficiently with

[048-093] experiment to justify its acceptation, the position of the centre of

[048-094] pressure may be found by the following rule:--from the radius of the wheel

[048-095] substract the radius of the rolling circle; to the remainder add the depth

[048-096] of the paddle board, and divide the fourth power of the sum by four times

[048-097] the depth; from the cube root of the quotient subtract the difference

[048-098] between the radii of the wheel and rolling circle, and the remainder will

[048-099] be the distance of the centre of pressure from the upper edge of the

[048-100] paddle.

[048-101] 555. _Q._--How do you find the diameter of the rolling circle?

[048-102] _A._--The diameter of the rolling circle is very easily found, for we have

[048-103] only to divide 5,280 times the number of miles per hour, by 60 times the

[048-104] number of strokes per minute, to get an expression for the circumference of

[048-105] the rolling circle, or the following rule may be adopted:--divide 88 times

[048-106] the speed of the vessel in statute miles per hour, by 3.1416 times the

[048-107] number of strokes per minute; the quotient will be the diameter in feet of

[048-108] the rolling circle. The diameter of the circle in which the centre of

[048-109] pressure moves or the effective diameter of the wheel being known, and also

[048-110] the diameter of the rolling circle, we at once find the excess of the

[048-111] velocity of the wheel over the vessel.

[048-112] 556. _Q._--Will you illustrate these rules by an example?

[048-113] _A._--A steam vessel of moderately good shape, and with engines of 200

[048-114] horses power, realises, with 22 strokes per minute, a speed of 10.62 miles

[048-115] per hour. To find the diameter of the rolling circle, we have 88 times

[048-116] 10.62, equal to 934.66, and 22 times 3.1416, equal to 69.1152; then 934.66

[048-117] divided by 69.1152 is equal to 13.52 feet, which is the diameter of the

[048-118] rolling circle. The diameter of the wheel is 19 ft. 4 in., so that the

[048-119] diameter of the rolling circle is about 2/3ds of the diameter of the wheel,

[048-120] and this is a frequent proportion. The depth of the paddle board is 2 feet,

[048-121] and the difference between the diameters of the wheel and rolling circle

[048-122] will be 5.8133, which will make the difference of their radii 2.9067; and

[048-123] adding to this the depth of the paddle board, we have 4.9067, the fourth

[048-124] power of which is 579.64, which, divided by four times the depth of the

[048-125] paddle board, gives us 72.455, the cube root of which is 4.1689, which,

[048-126] diminished by the difference of the radii of the wheel and rolling circle,

[048-127] leaves 1.2622 feet for the distance of the centre of pressure from the

[048-128] upper edge of the paddle board in the case of light immersions. The radius

[048-129] of the wheel being 9.6667, the distance from the centre of the wheel to the

[048-130] upper edge of the float is 7.6667, and adding to this 1.2622, we get 8.9299

[048-131] feet as the radius, or 17.8598 feet as the diameter of the circle in which

[048-132] the centre of pressure revolves. With 22 strokes per minute, the velocity

[048-133] of the centre of pressure will be 20.573 feet per second, and with 10.62

[048-134] miles per hour for the speed of the vessel, the velocity of the rolling

[048-135] circle will be 15.576 feet per second. The effective velocity will be the

[048-136] difference between these quantities, or 4.997 feet per second. Now the

[048-137] height from which a body must fall by gravity, to acquire a velocity of

[048-138] 4.997 feet per second, is about .62 feet; and twice this height, or 1.24

[048-139] feet, multiplied by 62-1/2, which is the number of Lbs. weight in a cubic

[048-140] foot of water, gives 77-1/2 Lbs. as the pressure on each square foot of the

[048-141] vertical paddle boards. As each board is of 20 square feet of area, and

[048-142] there is a vertical board on each side of the ship, the total pressure on

[048-143] the vertical paddle boards will be 2900 Lbs.

[048-144] 557. _Q._--What pressure is this equivalent to on each square inch of the

[048-145] pistons?

[048-146] _A._--A vessel of 200 horses power will have two cylinders, each 50 inches

[048-147] diameter, and 5 feet stroke, or thereabout. The area of a piston of 50

[048-148] inches diameter is 1963.5 square inches, so that the area of the two

[048-149] pistons is 3927 square inches, and the piston will move through 10 feet

[048-150] every revolution; and with 22 strokes per minute this will be 220 feet per

[048-151] minute, or 3.66 feet per second. Now, if the effective velocity of the

[048-152] centre of pressure and the velocity of the pistons had been the same, then

[048-153] a pressure of 2900 Lbs. upon the vertical paddles would have been balanced

[048-154] by an equal pressure on the pistons, which would have been in this case

[048-155] about .75 Lbs. per square inch; but as the effective velocity of the centre

[048-156] of pressure is 4.997 feet per second, while that of the pistons is only

[048-157] 3.66 feet per second, the pressure must be increased in the proportion of

[048-158] 4.997 to 3.66 to establish an equilibrium of pressure, or, in other words,

[048-159] it must be 1.02 Lbs. per square inch. It follows from this investigation,

[048-160] that, in radial wheels, the greater part of the engine power is distributed

[048-161] among the oblique floats.
