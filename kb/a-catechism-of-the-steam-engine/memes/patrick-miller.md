# Patrick Miller

**Patrick Miller** (1731–1815) was a Scottish banker and inventor. He financed and conducted some of the earliest experiments with steam-powered navigation in Britain, building [[paddle-wheel|paddle]]-wheel vessels that operated on the Forth and Clyde Canal in the late 1780s.

## Career and Contributions

Miller's steam vessel experiments predated the commercial success of steam navigation by several decades. Working with engineer William Symington, he fitted a [[steam|steam engine]] to a twin-hulled [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] boat and achieved successful trials on Dalswinton Loch in 1788. The vessel used a pair of [[paddle-wheel|paddle wheels]] mounted between the hulls, driven by a small atmospheric engine.

While Miller's vessels did not achieve commercial viability, they demonstrated the fundamental feasibility of steam navigation. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] references the engines built by Miller, Ravenhill and Co. as part of the lineage of [[marine-engine|marine engine]] development, noting their cylinder dimensions and performance (028-046).

## Legacy

Patrick Miller is recognised as one of the pioneers who bridged the gap between theoretical speculation and practical steam navigation. His experiments directly influenced later developers including Symington and [[henry-bell|Henry Bell]], whose *Comet* of 1812 became Europe's first commercially successful steamboat.
