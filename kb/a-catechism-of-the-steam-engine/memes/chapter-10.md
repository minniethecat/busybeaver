[010-001] 115. _Q._--Will you further illustrate this general description by an

[010-002] example?

[010-003] [Illustration: Fig. 26.]

[010-004] _Q._--Fig. 26 is a side elevation of a side lever engine; x x represent the

[010-005] beams or keelsons to which the engines are attached, and on which the

[010-006] boilers rest. The engines are tied down by strong bolts passing through the

[010-007] bottom of the vessel, but the boiler keeps its position by its weight

[010-008] alone. The condenser and air pump are worked off the side levers by means

[010-009] of side rods and a cross head. A strong gudgeon, called the _main centre_,

[010-010] passes through the condenser at K, the projecting ends of which serve to

[010-011] support the side levers or beams. L is the piston rod, which, by means of

[010-012] the cross head and side rods, is connected to the side levers or beams, one

[010-013] of which is shown at H H. The line M represents the connecting rod, to

[010-014] which motion is imparted by the beams, through the medium of the cross tail

[010-015] extending between the beams, and which by means of the crank turns the

[010-016] paddle shaft S. The eccentric which works the slide valve is placed upon

[010-017] the paddle shaft. It consists of a disc of metal encircled by a hoop, to

[010-018] which a rod is attached, and the disc is perforated with a hole for the

[010-019] shaft, not in the centre, but near one edge. When, therefore, the shaft

[010-020] revolves, carrying the eccentric with it, the rod attached to the

[010-021] encircling hoop receives a reciprocating motion, just as it would do if

[010-022] attached to a crank in the shaft.

[010-023] 116. _Q._--Will you describe the mode of starting the engine?

[010-024] _A._--I may first mention that when the engine is at rest, the connection

[010-025] between the eccentric and the slide valve is broken, by lifting the end of

[010-026] the eccentric rod out of a notch which engages a pin on the valve shaft,

[010-027] and the valve is at such times free to be moved by hand by a bar of iron,

[010-028] applied to a proper part of the valve gear for that purpose. This being so,

[010-029] the engineer, when he wishes to start the engine, first opens a small valve

[010-030] called the _blow through valve_, which permits steam from the boiler to

[010-031] enter the engine both above and below the piston, and also to fill the

[010-032] condenser and air pump. This steam expels the air from the interior of the

[010-033] engine, and also any water which may have accumulated there; and when this

[010-034] has been done, the blow through valve is shut, and a vacuum very soon forms

[010-035] within the engine, by the condensation of the steam. If now the slide valve

[010-036] be moved by hand, the steam from the boiler will be admitted on one side of

[010-037] the piston, while there is a vacuum on the other side, and the piston will,

[010-038] therefore, be moved in the desired direction. When the piston reaches the

[010-039] end of the stroke, the valve has to be moved in the reverse direction, when

[010-040] the piston will return, and after being moved thus by hand, once or twice,

[010-041] the connection of the valve with the eccentric is to be restored by

[010-042] allowing the notch on the end of the eccentric rod to engage the pin on the

[010-043] valve lever, when the valve will be thereafter moved by the engine in the

[010-044] proper manner. It will, of course, be necessary, when the engine begins to

[010-045] move, to open the injection cock a little, to enable water to enter for the

[010-046] condensation of the steam. In the most recent marine engines, a somewhat

[010-047] different mechanism from this is used for giving motion to the valves, but

[010-048] that mechanism will be afterwards described.

[010-049] 117. _Q._--Are all marine engines condensing engines?

[010-050] _A._--Nearly all of them are so; but recently a number of gunboats have

[010-051] been constructed, with high pressure engines. In general, however, marine

[010-052] engines are low pressure or condensing engines.

[010-053] 118. _Q._--Will you now describe the chief features of the oscillating

[010-054] paddle marine engine?

[010-055] _A._--In the oscillating paddle marine engine, the arrangement of the

[010-056] paddle shaft and paddle wheels is the same as in the case already

[010-057] described, but the whole of the side levers, side rods, cross head, cross

[010-058] tail, and connecting rod are discarded. The cylinder is set immediately

[010-059] under the crank; the top of the piston rod is connected immediately to the

[010-060] crank pin; and, to enable the piston rod to accommodate itself to the

[010-061] movement of the crank, the cylinder is so constructed as to be susceptible

[010-062] of vibrating or oscillating upon two external axes or trunnions. These

[010-063] trunnions are generally placed about half way up on the sides of the

[010-064] cylinder; and through one of them steam is received from the boiler, while

[010-065] through the other the steam escapes to the condenser. The air pump is

[010-066] usually worked by means of a crank in the shaft, which crank moves the air

[010-067] pump bucket up and down as the shaft revolves.

[010-068] 119. _Q._--Will you give an example of a paddle oscillating engine?

[010-069] _A._--I will take as an example the oscillating engines constructed by

[010-070] Messrs. Ravenhill & Salked, for the Holyhead Packets. Fig. 27 is a

[010-071] longitudinal section of this vessel, showing an engine and boiler; and fig.

[010-072] 28 is a transverse section of one of the engines, showing also one of the

[010-073] wheels. There are two cylinders in this vessel, and one air pump, which

[010-074] lies in an inclined position, and is worked by a crank in the shaft which

[010-075] stretches between the cylinders, and which is called the _intermediate

[010-076] shaft_. A A, is one of the cylinders, B B the piston rod, and C C the

[010-077] crank. D is the crank in the intermediate shaft, which works the air pump

[010-078] E. There are double eccentrics fixed on the shaft, whereby the movement of

[010-079] the slide valves is regulated. The purpose of the double eccentrics is to

[010-080] enable an improved arrangement of valve gear to be employed, which is

[010-081] denominated the _link motion_, and which will be described hereafter. I I

[010-082] are the steam pipes leading to the steam trunnions K K, on which, and on

[010-083] the eduction trunnions connected with the pipe M, the cylinders oscillate.

[010-084] 120. _Q._--By what species of mechanism are the positions of the paddle

[010-085] floats of feathering wheels governed?

[010-086] _A._--The floats are supported by spurs projecting from the rim of the

[010-087] wheel, and they may be moved upon the points of the spurs, to which they

[010-088] are attached by pins, by means of short levers proceeding from the backs of

[010-089] the floats, and connected to rods which proceed towards the centre of the

[010-090] wheel. The centre, however, to which these rods proceed is not concentric

[010-091] with the wheel, and the rods, therefore, are moved in and out as the wheel

[010-092] revolves, and impart a corresponding motion to the floats. In some

[010-093] feathering wheels the proper motion is given to the rods by means of an

[010-094] eccentric on the ship's side. The action of paddle wheels, whether radial

[010-095] or feathering, will be more fully described in the chapter on Steam

[010-096] Navigation.

[010-097] SCREW ENGINES.

[010-098] 121. _Q._--What are the principal varieties of screw engines?

[010-099] [Illustration: Fig. 27.]

[010-100] [Illustration: Fig. 28.]

[010-101] _A._--The engines employed for the propulsion of screw vessels are divided

[010-102] into two great classes,--geared engines and direct acting engines; and each

[010-103] of these classes again has many varieties. In screw vessels, the shaft on

[010-104] which the screw is set requires to revolve at a much greater velocity than

[010-105] is required in the case of the paddle shaft of a paddle vessel; and in

[010-106] geared engines this necessary velocity of rotation is obtained by the

[010-107] intervention of toothed wheels,--the engines themselves moving with the

[010-108] usual velocity of paddle engines; whereas in direct acting engines the

[010-109] required velocity of rotation is obtained by accelerating the speed of the

[010-110] engines, and which are connected immediately to the screw shaft.

[010-111] 122. _Q._--Will you describe some of the principal varieties of geared

[010-112] engines?

[010-113] _A._--A good many of the geared engines for screw vessels are made in the

[010-114] same manner as land engines, with a beam overhead, which by means of a

[010-115] connecting rod extending downwards, gives motion to the crank shaft, on

[010-116] which are set the cog wheels which give motion to pinions on the screw

[010-117] shaft,--the teeth of the wheels being generally of wood and the teeth of

[010-118] the pinions of iron. There are usually several wheels on the crank shaft

[010-119] and several pinions on the screw shaft; but the teeth of each do not run in

[010-120] the same line, but are set a little in advance of one another, so as to

[010-121] divide the thickness of the tooth into as many parts as there are

[010-122] independent wheels or pinions. By this arrangement the wheels work more

[010-123] smoothly than they would otherwise do.

[010-124] 123. _Q._--What other forms are there of geared screw engines?

[010-125] _A._--In some cases the cylinders lie on their sides in the manner of the

[010-126] cylinders of a locomotive engine. In other cases vertical trunk engines are

[010-127] employed; and in other cases vertical oscillating engines.

[010-128] 124. _Q._--Will you give an example of a geared vertical oscillating

[010-129] engine?

[010-130] _A._--The engines of a geared oscillating engine are similar to the paddle

[010-131] wheel engines (figs. 27 and 28), but the engines are placed lengthways of

[010-132] the ship, and instead of a paddle wheel on the main shaft, there is a

[010-133] geared wheel which connects with a pinion on the screw shaft. The engines

[010-134] of the Great Britain are made off the same patterns as the paddle engines

[010-135] constructed by Messrs. John Penn & Son, for H.M.S. Sphinx. The diameter of

[010-136] each cylinder is 82-1/2 inches, the length of travel or stroke of the

[010-137] piston is 6 feet, and the nominal power is 500 horses. The Great Britain is

[010-138] of 3,500 tons burden, and her displacement at 16 feet draught of water is

[010-139] 2,970 tons. The diameter of the screw is 15-1/2 feet, length of screw in

[010-140] the line of the shaft, 3 feet 2 inches, and the pitch of the screw, 19

[010-141] feet.

[010-142] 125. _Q._--What do you mean by the pitch of the screw?

[010-143] _A._--A screw propeller may be supposed to be a short piece cut off a screw

[010-144] of large diameter like a spiral stair, and the pitch of a spiral stair is

[010-145] the vertical height from any given step to the step immediately overhead.

[010-146] 126. _Q._--What is the usual number of arms?

[010-147] _A._--Generally a screw has two arms, but sometimes it has three or more.

[010-148] The Great Britain had three arms or twisted blades resembling the vanes of

[010-149] a windmill. The multiple of the gearing in the Great Britain is 3 to 1, and

[010-150] there are 17-1/2 square feet of heating surface in the boiler for each

[010-151] nominal horse power. The crank shaft being put into motion by the engine,

[010-152] carries round with it the great cog wheel, or aggregation of cog wheels,

[010-153] affixed to its extremity; and these wheels acting on suitable pinions on

[010-154] the screw shaft, cause the screw to make three revolutions for every

[010-155] revolution made by the engine.

[010-156] 127. _Q._--What are the principal varieties of direct acting screw engines?

[010-157] _A._--In some cases four engines have been employed instead of two, and the

[010-158] cylinders have been laid on their sides on each side of the screw shaft.

[010-159] This multiplication of engines, however, introduces needless complication,

[010-160] and is now but little used. In other cases two inverted cylinders are set

[010-161] above the screw shaft on appropriate framing; and connecting rods attached

[010-162] to the ends of the piston rods turn round cranks in the screw shaft.

[010-163] 128. _Q._--What is the kind of direct acting screw engine employed by

[010-164] Messrs. Penn.

[010-165] _A._--It is a horizontal trunk engine. In this engine a round pipe called a

[010-166] trunk penetrates the piston, to which it is fixed, being in fact cast in

[010-167] one piece with it; and the trunk also penetrates the top and bottom of the

[010-168] cylinder, through which it moves, and is made tight therein by means of

[010-169] stuffing boxes. The connecting rod is attached at one end to a pin fixed in

[010-170] the middle of the trunk, while the other end engages the crank in the usual

[010-171] manner. The air pump is set within the condenser, and is wrought by a rod

[010-172] which is fixed to the piston and derives its motion therefrom. The air pump

[010-173] is of that species which is called double-acting. The piston or bucket is

[010-174] formed without valves in it, but an inlet and outlet valve is fixed to each

[010-175] end of the pump, through the one of which the water is drawn into the pump

[010-176] barrel, and through the other of which it is expelled into the hot well.
