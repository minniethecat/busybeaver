[062-005] 695. _Q._--Can you give any other examples of the power necessary for

[062-006] grinding corn?

[062-007] _A._--An engine exerting 23-1/3 horses power by the indicator works two

[062-008] pairs of flour stones of 4 feet 8 inches diameter, two pairs of stones

[062-009] grinding oatmeal of 4 feet 8 inches diameter, one dressing machine, one

[062-010] pair of fanners, one dust screen, and one sifting machine. One of the flour

[062-011] stones makes 85, and the other 90 revolutions in the minute. One of the

[062-012] oatmeal stones makes 120, and the other 140 revolutions in the minute. To

[062-013] take another case:--An engine exerting 26-1/2 indicator horses power works

[062-014] two pairs of flour stones, one dressing machine, two pairs of stones

[062-015] grinding oatmeal, and one pair of shelling stones. The flour stones, one

[062-016] pair of the oatmeal stones, and shelling stones, are 4 feet 8 inches

[062-017] diameter. The diameter of the other pair of oatmeal stones is 3 feet 8

[062-018] inches. The length of the cylinder of the dressing machine is 7 feet 6

[062-019] inches. The flour stones make 87 revolutions in the minute, and the larger

[062-020] oatmeal stone 111 revolutions, but the smaller oatmeal stone and the

[062-021] shelling stone revolve faster than this. At the time the indicator diagram

[062-022] was taken, each pair of flour stones was grinding at the rate of 5 bushels

[062-023] an hour; each pair of oatmeal stones about 24 bushels an hour; and the

[062-024] shelling stones were shelling at the rate of about 54 bushels an hour. The

[062-025] fanners and screen were also in operation.

[062-026] 696. _Q._--Have you any other case to enumerate?

[062-027] _A._--I may mention one in which the power of the same engine was increased

[062-028] by giving it a larger supply of steam. The engine when working with 8.65

[062-029] horses power, gives motion to one pair of oatmeal stones of 4 feet 6 inches

[062-030] diameter, and one pair of flour stones 4 feet 8 inches diameter. The

[062-031] oatmeal stone makes 100 revolutions in the minute, and the flour stone 89.

[062-032] The oatmeal stones grind about 36 bushels in the hour, and the flour stones

[062-033] 5 bushels in the hour. The engine when working to 12 horses power drives

[062-034] one pair of flour stones, 4 feet 8 inches diameter, at 89 revolutions per

[062-035] minute and one pair of stones of the same diameter at 105 revolutions,

[062-036] grinding beans for cattle. The flour mill stones with this proportion of

[062-037] power, being more largely fed, ground 6 bushels per hour, and the other

[062-038] stones also ground 6 bushels per hour. When the power was increased to 18

[062-039] horses, and the engine was burdened in addition with a dressing machine

[062-040] having a cylinder of 19 inches diameter, the speed of the flour stone fell

[062-041] to 85, and of the beans stone to 100 revolutions per minute, and the yield

[062-042] was also reduced. The dressing machine dressed 24 bushels per hour.

[062-043] 697. _Q._--What is the power necessary to work a sugar mill such as is used

[062-044] to press the juice from canes in the West Indies?

[062-045] _A._--Twenty horses power will work a sugar mill having rollers about 5

[062-046] feet long and 28 inches diameter; the rollers making 2-1/3 turns in a

[062-047] minute. If the rollers be 26 inches diameter and 4-1/2 feet long, 18 horses

[062-048] power will suffice to work them at the same speed, and 16 horses power if

[062-049] the length be reduced to 3 feet 8 inches. 12 horses power will be required

[062-050] to work a sugar mill with rollers 24 inches diameter and 4 feet 2 inches

[062-051] long; and 10 horses power will suffice if the rollers be 3 feet 10 inches

[062-052] long and 23 inches diameter. The speed of the surface of sugar mill rollers

[062-053] should not be greater than 16 feet per minute, to allow time for the canes

[062-054] to part with their juice. In the old mills the speed was invariably too

[062-055] great. The quantity of juice expressed will not be increased by increasing

[062-056] the speed of the rollers, but more of the juice will pass away in the

[062-057] begass or woody refuse of the cane.

[062-058] 698. _Q._--What is the amount of power necessary to drive cotton mills?

[062-059] _A._--An indicator or actual horse power will drive 305 hand mule spindles,

[062-060] with proportion of preparing machinery for the same; or 230 self-acting

[062-061] mule spindles with preparation; or 104 throstle spindles with preparation;

[062-062] or 10-1/2 power looms with common sizing. The throstles referred to are the

[062-063] common throstles spinning 34's twist for power loom weaving, and the

[062-064] spindles make 4000 turns per minute. The self-acting mules are Robert's,

[062-065] about one half spinning 36's weft, and spindles revolving 4800 turns per

[062-066] minute; and the other half spinning 36's twist, with the spindles revolving

[062-067] 5200 times per minute. Half the hand mules were spinning 36's weft, at 4700

[062-068] revolutions, and the other half 36's twist at 5000 revolutions per minute.

[062-069] The average breadth of the looms was 37 inches, weaving 37 inch cloth,

[062-070] making 123 picks per minute,--all common calicoes about 60 reed, Stockport

[062-071] count, and 68 picks to the inch. To take another example in the case of a

[062-072] mill for twisting cotton yarn into thread:--In this mill there are 27

[062-073] frames with 96 common throstle spindles in each, making in all 2592

[062-074] spindles. The spindles turn 2200 times in a minute; the bobbins are 1-7/8

[062-075] inches diameter, and the part which holds the thread is 2-3/16 inches long.

[062-076] In addition to the twisting frames the steam engine works 4 turning lathes,

[062-077] 3 polishing lathes, 2 American machines for turning small bobbins, two

[062-078] circular saws, one of 22 and the other of 14 inches diameter, and 24 bobbin

[062-079] heads or machines for filling the bobbins with finished thread. The power

[062-080] required to drive the whole of this machinery is 28-1/2 horses. When all

[062-081] the machinery except the spindles is thrown off, the power required is 21

[062-082] horses, so that 2592, the total number of spindles, divided by 21, the

[062-083] total power, is the number of twisting spindles worked by each actual horse

[062-084] power. The number is 122.84.

[062-120] CHAPTER XII.

[062-121] MANUFACTURE AND MANAGEMENT OF STEAM ENGINES.

[062-122] CONSTRUCTION OF ENGINES.

[062-123] 700. _Q._--What are the qualities which should be possessed by the iron of

[062-124] which the cylinder of steam engines are made?

[062-125] _A._--The general ambition in making cylinders is to make them sound and

[062-126] hard; but it is expedient also to make them tough, so as to approach as

[062-127] nearly as possible to the state of malleable iron. This may be done by

[062-128] mixing in the furnace as many different kinds of iron as possible; and it

[062-129] may be set down as a general rule in iron founding, that the greater the

[062-130] number of the kinds of metal entering into the composition of any casting,

[062-131] the denser and tougher it will be. The constituent atoms of the different

[062-132] kinds of iron appear to be of different sizes, and the mixture of different

[062-133] kinds maintains the toughness, while it adds to the density and cohesive

[062-134] power. Hot blast iron was at one time generally believed to be weaker than

[062-135] cold blast iron, but it is now questioned whether it is not the stronger of

[062-136] the two. The cohesive strength of unmixed iron is not in proportion to its

[062-137] specific gravity, and its elasticity and power to resist shocks appear to

[062-138] become greater as the specific gravity becomes less. Nos. 3 and 4 are the

[062-139] strongest irons. In most cases, iron melted in a cupola is not so strong as

[062-140] when remelted in an air furnace, and when run into green sand it is not

[062-141] reckoned so strong as when run into dry sand, or loam. The quality of the

[062-142] fuel, and even the state of the weather, exerts an influence on the quality

[062-143] of the iron: smelting furnaces, on the cold blast principle, have long been

[062-144] known to yield better iron in winter than in summer, probably from the

[062-145] existence of less moisture in the air; and it would probably be found to

[062-146] accomplish an improvement in the quality of the iron if the blast were made

[062-147] to pass through a vessel containing muriate of lime, by which the moisture

[062-148] of the air would be extracted. The expense of such a preparation would not

[062-149] be considerable, as, by subsequent evaporation, the salt might be used over

[062-150] and over again for the same purpose.

[062-151] 701. _Q._--Will you explain the process of casting cylinders?

[062-152] _A._--The mould into which the metal is poured is built up of bricks and

[062-153] loam, the loam being clay and sand ground together in a mill, with the

[062-154] addition of a little horse-dung to give it a fibrous structure and prevent

[062-155] cracks. The loam board, by which the circle of the cylinder is to be swept,

[062-156] is attached to an upright iron bar, at the distance of the radius of the

[062-157] cylinder, and a cylindrical shell of brick is built up, which is plastered

[062-158] on the inside with loam, and made quite smooth by traversing the

[062-159] perpendicular loam board round it. A core is then formed in a similar

[062-160] manner, but so much smaller as to leave a space between the shell and the

[062-161] core equal to the thickness of the cylinder, and into this space the melted

[062-162] metal is poured. Whatever nozzles or projections are required upon the

[062-163] cylinder, must be formed by means of wooden patterns, which are built into

[062-164] the shell, and subsequently withdrawn; but where a number of cylinders of

[062-165] the same kind are required, it is advisable to make these patterns of iron,

[062-166] which will not be liable to warp or twist while the loam is being dried.

[062-167] Before the iron is cast into the mould, the interior of the mould must be

[062-168] covered with finely powdered charcoal--or blackening, as it is technically

[062-169] termed; and the secret of making finely skinned castings lies in using

[062-170] plenty of blackening. In loam and dry sand castings the charcoal should be

[062-171] mixed with thick clay water, and applied until it is an eighth of an inch

[062-172] thick, or more; the surface should be then very carefully smoothed or

[062-173] sleeked, and if the metal has been judiciously mixed, and the mould

[062-174] thoroughly dried, the casting is sure to be a fine one. Dry sand and loam

[062-175] castings should be, as much as possible, made in boxes; the moulds may

[062-176] thereby be more rapidly and more effectually dried, and better castings

[062-177] will be got with a less expense.
