# Marine Engine

The **marine engine** is a [[steam|steam engine]] specifically designed and adapted for propelling ships and boats. It is distinguished from land engines by its compact vertical arrangement, the use of surface condensation for recovering feed water at sea, and its ability to operate reliably under the rolling and pitching motion of a vessel. (007-073) (010-055)

## Essential Characteristics

All marine engines are [[condensation|condensing engine]]s, because the supply of fresh water at sea is limited and the [[eduction|exhaust]] steam must be recovered for re-use in the [[Boiler|boiler]]. (010-049) The [[condensation|condenser]] is therefore an indispensable component, and in marine practice the [[condenser|surface condenser]] — where steam is condensed by contact with cold tubes rather than by direct injection — is universally adopted to preserve the purity of the feed water. (026-145)

Marine engines are built with a low centre of gravity to improve the vessel's stability, and their working parts are arranged to operate within the confined height of the engine room. (010-055) The engine must also be capable of being reversed quickly and reliably, as manoeuvring in harbour or avoiding collision at sea depends on prompt reversal of the [[Propeller|propeller]] or [[Paddle Wheel|paddle wheels]].

## Types of Marine Engine

| Type | Arrangement | Application |
|------|------------|-------------|
| Side-lever | Beam pivoted low, [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] upwards | Early [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] steamers |
| Oscillating | [[cylinder|Cylinder]]s swing on trunnions; direct connection to crank | Paddle steamers, compact (010-055) |
| Direct-acting [[screw-propeller|screw]] | [[cylinder|Cylinder]]s inverted, direct connection to [[screw-propeller|propeller]] shaft | Screw-propelled vessels (026-155) |
| Trunk | Trunk piston eliminates piston rod, very compact | Warships, low-headroom installations |

In the [[Oscillating Engine|oscillating paddle marine engine]], the cylinders are mounted on trunnions and swing as they follow the crank, which eliminates the need for a [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] and cross-head and allows the engine to fit within a very low profile. (010-055)

## Direct-Acting Screw Engines

The most recent direct-acting marine engines for driving the [[Screw Propeller|screw propeller]] employ a pair of inverted cylinders placed directly over the [[crank|crank shaft]], with the piston rods connected to the crank by a single [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] on each side. (026-155) This arrangement minimises the number of working parts and reduces the overall height of the machinery.

> "In the most recent direct acting marine engines for driving the screw propeller, the cylinders are inverted and placed directly over the crank shaft." (026-155)

## Power and Nominal Ratings

In marine engines the [[horsepower|nominal horsepower]] — a figure based on the dimensions of the [[Piston|piston]] and its speed — considerably exceeds the actual power delivered, and calculation by indicator gives a far more reliable measure. (020-064) In ordering a marine engine, the purchaser specifies the [[horsepower|nominal horsepower]], but the builder proportions the actual cylinder dimensions to deliver the required power under the expected conditions of steam pressure and vacuum.

The proper proportions for marine engine components — including the diameters of shafts, thickness of cylinder walls, and bearing areas — follow rules established by experience. (028-069) (029-016)

## Slow-Speed Operation

Marine engines of the mid-nineteenth century typically operated at low rotational speeds — in the range of 15 to 40 revolutions per minute for [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] engines — because the [[screw-propeller|propeller]] or [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] wheel must turn slowly to maintain efficient thrust. (016-015) The [[Slide Valve|slide valve]] gear used in these slow-speed engines was the variety suited to low speeds, generally the simple [[slide-valve|D-valve]] with moderate lap. (016-015)
