An **expansion valve** is a mechanism in a [[steam|steam engine]] that cuts off the admission of steam to the cylinder before the [[piston]] has completed its full stroke. By stopping the inflow early, the steam already in the cylinder expands and continues to push the piston using its own elastic force, rather than drawing fresh steam from the [[boiler]] for the entire stroke. This principle — called **expansive working** — is one of the most important means of improving the fuel efficiency of [[steam|steam engine]]s.

## Principle of expansive working

When steam is admitted to the cylinder for only a portion of the stroke and then cut off, the steam expands as the piston continues to move, its pressure gradually falling:

> [015-151] In expansive working, the steam is cut off before the end of the stroke, and the piston is then impelled by the expansion of the steam already in the cylinder.

> [015-156] The expansion of steam is productive of a great saving of fuel, as the steam which has been generated in the boiler performs an additional amount of work by its elastic force after the communication with the boiler has been closed.

## Types of expansion valves

Several mechanisms achieve [[cut-off|variable cut-off]]:

> [015-158] The expansion valve may be a separate valve placed upon the back of the ordinary slide valve, and worked by a separate eccentric.

> [016-014] The expansion valve is usually a gridiron valve, consisting of a number of narrow bars, and is placed between the steam pipe and the ordinary slide valve.

> [016-025] The expansion valve gear may be constructed so as to vary the point of cut-off while the engine is in motion, by shifting the position of the eccentric or by the use of a link motion.

The [[link-motion]] ([[link-motion|Stephenson link motion]]) was the most successful variable expansion gear, allowing the engineer to adjust the cut-off point while the engine ran, optimizing efficiency for different loads and speeds.

## Practical benefits

The fuel savings from expansive working were substantial. Early engines using [[steam]] at full boiler pressure throughout the stroke consumed far more coal than engines that employed cut-off. Expansion became standard practice on [[marine-engine|marine engines]], where coal consumption directly affected voyage range:

> [016-025] The link motion enables the engineer to set the eccentric at any desired point, regulating the expansion to suit the work the engine has to perform.

The [[indicator]] played a key role in verifying proper expansion valve setting, as its [[indicator-diagram|pressure-volume diagram]] revealed exactly where cut-off occurred and whether the expansion curve was efficient.
