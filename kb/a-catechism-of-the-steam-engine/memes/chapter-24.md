[024-001] 295. _Q._--What are the causes of priming?

[024-002] _A._--The causes of priming are an insufficient amount of steam room, an

[024-003] inadequate area of water level, an insufficient width between the flues or

[024-004] tubes for the ascent of the steam and the descent of water to supply the

[024-005] vacuity the steam occasions, and the use of dirty water in the boiler. New

[024-006] boilers prime more than old boilers, and steamers entering rivers from the

[024-007] sea are more addicted to priming than if sea or river water had alone been

[024-008] used in the boilers--probably from the boiling point of salt water being

[024-009] higher than that of fresh, whereby the salt water acts like so much molten

[024-010] metal in raising the fresh water into steam. Opening the safety valve

[024-011] suddenly may make a boiler prime, and if the safety valve be situated near

[024-012] the mouth of the steam pipe, the spray or foam thus created may be mingled

[024-013] with the steam passing into the engine, and materially diminish its

[024-014] effective power; but if the safety valve be situated at a distance from the

[024-015] mouth of the steam pipe, the quantity of foam or spray passing into the

[024-016] engine may be diminished by opening the safety valve; and in locomotives,

[024-017] therefore, it is found beneficial to have a safety valve on the barrel of

[024-018] the boiler at a point remote from the steam chest, by partially opening

[024-019] which, any priming in that part of the boiler adjacent to the steam chest

[024-020] is checked, and a purer steam than before pusses to the engine.

[024-021] 296. _Q._--What is the proper remedy for priming?

[024-022] _A._--When a boiler primes, the engineer generally closes the throttle

[024-023] valve partially, turns off the injection water, and opens the furnace

[024-024] doors, whereby the generation of steam is checked, and a less violent

[024-025] ebullition in the boiler suffices. Where the priming arises from an

[024-026] insufficient amount of steam room, it may be mitigated by putting a higher

[024-027] pressure upon the boiler and working more expansively, or by the

[024-028] interposition of a perforated plate between the boiler and the steam chest,

[024-029] which breaks the ascending water and liberates the steam. In some cases,

[024-030] however, it may be necessary to set a second steam chest on the top of the

[024-031] existing one, and it will be preferable to establish a communication with

[024-032] this new chamber by means of a number of small holes, bored through the

[024-033] iron plate of the boiler, rather than by a single large orifice. Where

[024-034] priming arises from the existence of dirty water in the boiler, the evil

[024-035] may be remedied by the use of collecting vessels, or by blowing off largely

[024-036] from the surface; and where it arises from an insufficient area of water

[024-037] level, or an insufficient width between the flues for the free ascent of

[024-038] the steam and the descent of the superincumbent water, the evil may be

[024-039] abated by the addition of circulating pipes in some part of the boiler,

[024-040] which will allow the water to descend freely to the place from whence the

[024-041] steam rises, the width of the water spaces being virtually increased by

[024-042] restricting their function to the transmission of a current of steam and

[024-043] water to the surface. It is desirable to arrange the heating surface in

[024-044] such a way that the feed water entering the boiler at its lowest point is

[024-045] heated gradually as it ascends, until toward the superior part of the flues

[024-046] it is raised gradually into steam; but in all cases there will be currents

[024-047] in the boiler for which it is proper to provide. The steam pipe proceeding

[024-048] to the engine should obviously be attached to the highest point of the

[024-049] steam chest, in boilers of every construction.

[024-050] 297. _Q._--Having now stated the proportions proper to be adopted for

[024-051] evaporating any given quantity of water in steam boilers, will you proceed

[024-052] to show how you would proportion a boiler to do a given amount of work? say

[024-053] a locomotive boiler which will propel a train of 100 tons weight at a speed

[024-054] of 50 miles an hour.

[024-055] _A._--According to experiments on the resistance of railway trains at

[024-056] various rates of speed, made by Mr. Gooch, of the Great Western Railway, it

[024-057] appears that a train weighing, with locomotive, tender, and carriages,

[024-058] about 100 tons, experiences, at a speed of 50 miles an hour, a resistance

[024-059] of about 3,000 lbs., or about 30 lbs. per ton; which resistance includes

[024-060] the resistance of the engine as well as that of the train. This, therefore,

[024-061] is the force which must be imparted at the circumference of the driving

[024-062] wheels, except that small part intercepted by the engine itself, and the

[024-063] force exerted by the pistons must be greater than that at the circumference

[024-064] of the driving wheel, in the proportion of their slower motion, or in the

[024-065] proportion of the circumference of the driving wheel to the length of a

[024-066] double stroke of the engine. If the diameter of the driving wheel be 5-1/2

[024-067] feet, its circumference will be 17.278 feet, and if the length of the

[024-068] stroke be 18 inches, the length of a double stroke will be 3 feet. The

[024-069] pressure on the pistons must therefore be greater than the traction at the

[024-070] circumference of the driving wheel, in the proportion of 17.278 to 3, or,

[024-071] in other words, the mean pressure on the pistons must be 17,278 lbs.; and

[024-072] the area of cylinders, and pressure of steam, must be such as to produce

[024-073] conjointly this total pressure. It thus becomes easy to tell the volume and

[024-074] pressure of steam required, which steam in its turn represents its

[024-075] equivalent of water which is to be evaporated from the boiler, and the

[024-076] boiler must be so proportioned, by the rules already given, as to evaporate

[024-077] this water freely. In the case of a steam vessel, the mode of procedure is

[024-078] the same, and when the resistance and speed are known, it is easy to tell

[024-079] the equivalent value of steam.

[024-080] STRENGTH OF BOILERS.

[024-081] 298. _Q._--What strain should the iron of boilers be subjected to in

[024-082] working?

[024-083] _A._--The iron of boilers, like the iron of machines or structures, is

[024-084] capable of withstanding a tensile strain of from 50,000 to 60,000 lbs. upon

[024-085] every square inch of section; but it will only bear a third of this strain

[024-086] without permanent derangement of structure, and it does not appear

[024-087] expedient in any boiler to let the strain exceed 4,000 lbs. upon the square

[024-088] inch of sectional area of metal, especially if it is liable to be weakened

[024-089] by corrosion.

[024-090] 299._Q._--Have any experiments been made to determine the strength of

[024-091] boilers?

[024-092] _A._--The question of the strength of boilers was investigated very

[024-093] elaborately a few years ago by a committee of the Franklin Institute, in

[024-094] America, and it was found that the tenacity of boiler plate increased with

[024-095] the temperature up to 550°, at which point the tenacity began to diminish.

[024-096] At 32°, the cohesive force of a square inch of section was 56,000 lbs.; at

[024-097] 570°, it was 66,500 lbs.; at 720°, 55,000 lbs.; at 1,050°, 32,000 lbs.; at

[024-098] 1,240°, 22,000 lbs.; and at 1,317°, 9,000 lbs. Copper follows a different

[024-099] law, and appears to be diminished in strength by every addition to the

[024-100] temperature. At 32° the cohesion of copper was found to be 32,800 lbs. per

[024-101] square inch of section, which exceeds the cohesive force at any higher

[024-102] temperature, and the square of the diminution of strength seems to keep

[024-103] pace with the cube of the increased temperature. Strips of iron cut in the

[024-104] direction of the fibre were found to be about 6 per cent. stronger than

[024-105] when cut across the grain. Repeated piling and welding was found to

[024-106] increase the tenacity of the iron, but the result of welding together

[024-107] different kinds of iron was not found to be favorable. The accidental

[024-108] overheating of a boiler was found to reduce the ultimate or maximum

[024-109] strength of the plates from 65,000 to 45,000 lbs. per square inch of

[024-110] section, and riveting the plates was found to occasion a diminution in

[024-111] their strength to the extent of one third. These results, however, are not

[024-112] precisely the same as those obtained by Mr. Fairbairn.

[024-113] 300. _Q._--What were the results obtained by him?

[024-114] _A._--He found that boiler plate bore a tensile strain of 23 tons per

[024-115] square inch before rupture, which was reduced to 16 tons per square inch

[024-116] when joined together by a double row of rivets, and 13 tons, or about

[024-117] 30,000, when joined together by a single row of rivets. A circular boiler,

[024-118] therefore, with the ends of its plates double riveted, will bear at the

[024-119] utmost about 36,000 lbs. per square inch of section, or about 12,000 lbs.

[024-120] per square inch of section without permanent derangement of structure.

[024-121] 301. _Q._--What pressure do cylindrical boilers sustain in practice?

[024-122] _A._--In some locomotive boilers, which are worked with a pressure of 80

[024-123] lbs. upon the square inch, the thickness of the plates is only 5/16ths of

[024-124] an inch, while the barrel of the boiler is 39 inches in diameter. It will

[024-125] require a length of 3.2 inches of the boiler when the plates are 5/16ths

[024-126] thick to make up a sectional area of one square inch, and the separating

[024-127] force will be 39 times 3.2 multiplied by 80, which makes the separating

[024-128] force 9,984 lbs., sustained by two square inches of sectional area--one on

[024-129] each side; or the strain is 4,992 lbs. per square inch of sectional area,

[024-130] which is quite as great strain as is advisable. The accession of strength

[024-131] derived from the boiler ends is not here taken into account, but neither is

[024-132] the weakening effect counted that is caused by the rivet holes. Some

[024-133] locomotives of 4 feet diameter of barrel and of 3/8ths iron have been

[024-134] worked to as high a pressure as 200 lbs. on the inch; but such feats of

[024-135] daring are neither to be imitated nor commended.

[024-136] 302._Q._--Can you give a rule for the proper thickness of cylindrical

[024-137] boilers?

[024-138] _A._--The thickness proper for cylindrical boilers of wrought iron, exposed

[024-139] to an internal pressure, may be found by the following rule:--multiply 2.54

[024-140] times the internal diameter of the cylinder in inches by the greatest

[024-141] pressure within the cylinder per circular inch, and divide by 17,800; the

[024-142] result is the thickness in inches. If we apply this rule to the example of

[024-143] the locomotive boiler just given, we have 39 x 2.54 x 62.832 (the pressure

[024-144] per circular inch corresponding to 80 lbs. per square inch) = 6224.1379,

[024-145] and this, divided by 17,800, gives 0.349 as the thickness in inches,

[024-146] instead of 0.3125, or 5/16ths, the actual thickness. If we take the

[024-147] pressure per square inch instead of per circular inch, we obtain the

[024-148] following rule, which is somewhat simpler:--multiply the internal diameter

[024-149] of the cylinder in inches by the pressure in pounds per square inch, and

[024-150] divide the product by 8,900; the result is the thickness in inches. Both

[024-151] these rules give the strain about one fourth of the elastic force, or 4,450

[024-152] lbs. per square inch of sectional area of the iron; but 3,000 lbs. is

[024-153] enough when the flame impinges directly on the iron, as in some of the

[024-154] ordinary cylindrical boilers, and the rule may be adapted for that strain

[024-155] by taking 6,000 as a divisor instead of 8,900.

[024-156] 303. _Q._--In marine and wagon boilers, which are not of a cylindrical

[024-157] form, how do you procure the requisite strength?

[024-158] _A._--Where the sides of the boiler are flat, instead of being cylindrical,

[024-159] a sufficient number of stays must be introduced to withstand the pressure;

[024-160] and it is expedient not to let the strain upon these stays be more than

[024-161] 3,000 lbs. per square inch of section, as the strength of internal stays in

[024-162] boilers is generally soon diminished by corrosion. Indeed, a strain at all

[024-163] approaching that upon locomotive boilers would be very unsafe in the case

[024-164] of marine boilers, on account of the corrosion, both internal and external,

[024-165] to which marine boilers are subject. The stays should be small and numerous

[024-166] rather than large and few in number, as, when large stays are employed, it

[024-167] is difficult to keep them tight at the ends, and oxidation of the shell

[024-168] follows from leakage at the ends of the stays. All boilers should be

[024-169] proved, when new, to twice or three times the pressure they are intended to

[024-170] bear, and they should be proved occasionally by the hand pump when in use,

[024-171] to detect any weakness which corrosion may have occasioned.
