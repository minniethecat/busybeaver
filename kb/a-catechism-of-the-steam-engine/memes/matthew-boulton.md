# Matthew Boulton

**Matthew Boulton** (1728–1809) was an English manufacturer, entrepreneur, and business partner of [[james-watt|James Watt]]. Through the Soho Manufactory near Birmingham, Boulton provided the precision manufacturing capability and commercial vision that brought [[james-watt|Watt]]'s engine improvements to the world.

## Career and Contributions

Boulton established the Soho Manufactory in 1761, one of the largest and most advanced factories of its time. The works produced metal goods including buttons, buckles, and silver plate, employing water power and later [[steam|steam power]] for machinery.

In 1774, Boulton entered into partnership with [[james-watt|James Watt]], recognizing the commercial potential of [[james-watt|Watt]]'s improved engine design. Boulton's key contributions included:

- **Manufacturing precision**: Boulton's works employed skilled craftsmen and advanced tooling capable of boring cylinders to the tight tolerances [[james-watt|Watt]]'s design required.
- **Commercial advocacy**: Boulton promoted the engine to mine owners, particularly in Cornwall, overcoming initial skepticism about the new technology. (003-024)
- **Financial backing**: Boulton financed the development work and defended [[james-watt|Watt]]'s patents against infringement.

## The Boulton and Watt Firm

The partnership of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] became the leading [[steam|steam engine]] builder of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. Their engines powered mines, mills, and waterworks across Britain and abroad. Their design standards — for boilers, fly-wheels, and engine proportions — became industry benchmarks, frequently cited by [[john-bourne|Bourne]] in his Catechism. (007-081)
