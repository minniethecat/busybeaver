[011-001] THE LOCOMOTIVE ENGINE.

[011-002] 129. _Q._--Will you describe the more important features of the locomotive

[011-003] engine?

[011-004] _A._--The locomotive employed to draw carriages upon railways, consists of

[011-005] a cylindrical boiler filled with brass tubes, through which the hot air

[011-006] passes on its progress from the furnace to the chimney, and attached to the

[011-007] boiler are two horizontal cylinders fitted with pistons, valves, connecting

[011-008] rods, and other necessary apparatus to enable the power exerted by the

[011-009] pistons to turn round the cranked axle to which the driving wheels are

[011-010] attached. There are, therefore, two independent engines entering into the

[011-011] composition of a locomotive, the cranks of which are set at right angles

[011-012] with one another, so that when one crank is at its dead point, the other

[011-013] crank is in a position to act with its maximum efficacy. The driving

[011-014] wheels, which are fixed on the crank shaft and turn round with it, propel

[011-015] the locomotive forward on the rails by the mere adhesion of friction, and

[011-016] this is found sufficient not merely to move the locomotive, but to draw a

[011-017] long train of carriages behind it.

[011-018] 130. _Q._--Are locomotive engines condensing or high pressure engines.

[011-019] _A._--They are invariably high pressure engines, and it would be impossible

[011-020] or at least highly inconvenient, to carry the water necessary for the

[011-021] purpose of condensation. The steam, therefore, after it has urged the

[011-022] piston to the end of the stroke, escapes into the atmosphere. In locomotive

[011-023] engines the waste steam is always discharged into the chimney through a

[011-024] vertical pipe, and by its rapid passage it greatly increases the intensity

[011-025] of the draught in the chimney, whereby a smaller fire grate suffices for

[011-026] the combustion of the fuel, and the evaporative power of the boiler is much

[011-027] increased.

[011-028] 131. _Q._--Can you give an example of a good locomotive engine of the usual

[011-029] form?

[011-030] _A._--To do this I will take the example of one of Hawthorn's locomotive

[011-031] engines with six wheels represented in fig. 29; not one of the most modern

[011-032] construction now in use, nor yet one of the most antiquated. M is the

[011-033] cylinder, R the connecting rod, C C the eccentrics by which the slide valve

[011-034] is moved; J J is the steam pipe by which the steam is conducted from the

[011-035] steam dome of the boiler to the cylinder. Near the smoke stack end of this

[011-036] pipe is a valve K or regulator moved by a handle _p_ at the front of the

[011-037] boiler, and of which the purpose is to regulate the admission of the steam

[011-038] to the cylinder; _f_ is a safety valve kept closed by springs; N is the

[011-039] eduction pipe, or, as it is commonly termed in locomotives, the _blast

[011-040] pipe_, by which the steam, escaping from the cylinder after the stroke has

[011-041] been performed, is projected up the chimney H. The water in the boiler of

[011-042] course covers the tubes and also the top of the furnace or fire box. It

[011-043] will be understood that there are two engines in each locomotive, though,

[011-044] from the figure being given in section, only one engine can be shown. The

[011-045] cylinders of this engine are each 14 inches diameter; the length of the

[011-046] stroke of the piston is 21 inches. There are two sets of driving wheels, 5

[011-047] feet diameter, with outside connections.

[011-048] [Illustration: Fig. 29.]

[011-049] 132. _Q._--What is the tender of a locomotive?

[011-050] _A._--It is a carriage attached to the locomotive, of which the purpose is

[011-051] to contain coke for feeding the furnace, and water for replenishing the

[011-052] boiler.

[011-053] 133. _Q._--Can you give examples of modern locomotives?

[011-054] [Illustration: Fig. 30.]

[011-055] [Illustration: Fig. 31.]

[011-056] _A._--The most recent locomotives resemble in their material features the

[011-057] locomotive represented in fig. 29. I can, however, give examples of some of

[011-058] the most powerful engines of recent construction. Fig. 30 represents

[011-059] Gooch's express engine, adapted for the wide gauge of the Great Western

[011-060] Railway; and fig. 31 represents Crampton's express engine, adapted for the

[011-061] ordinary or narrow gauge railways. The cylinders of Gooch's engine are each

[011-062] 18 inches diameter, and 24 inches stroke; the driving wheels are 8 feet in

[011-063] diameter; the fire grate contains 21 square feet of area; and the heating

[011-064] surface of the fire box is 153 square feet. There are in all 305 tubes in

[011-065] the boiler, each of 2 inches diameter, giving a heating surface in the

[011-066] tubes of 1799 square feet. The total heating surface, therefore, is 1952

[011-067] square feet. Mr. Gooch states that an engine of this class will evaporate

[011-068] from 300 to 360 cubic feet of water in the hour, and will convey a load of

[011-069] 236 tons at a speed of 40 miles an hour, or a load of 181 tons at a speed

[011-070] of 60 miles an hour. The weight of this engine empty is 31 tons; of the

[011-071] tender 8-1/2 tons; and the total weight of the engine when loaded is 50

[011-072] tons. In one of Crampton's locomotives, the Liverpool, with one set more of

[011-073] carrying wheels than the fig., the cylinders are of 24 inches diameter and

[011-074] 18 inches stroke; the driving wheels are 8 feet in diameter; the fire grate

[011-075] contains 21-1/2 square feet of area; and the heating surface of the fire

[011-076] box is 154 square feet. There are in all 300 tubes in the boiler of 2-3/16

[011-077] inches external diameter, giving a surface in the tubes of 2136 square

[011-078] feet, and a total heating surface of 2290 square feet. The weight of this

[011-079] engine is stated to be 35 tons when ready to proceed on a journey. Both

[011-080] engines were displayed at the Great Exhibition in 1851, as examples of the

[011-081] most powerful locomotive engines then made. The weight of such engines is

[011-082] very injurious to the railway; bending, crushing, and disturbing the rails,

[011-083] and trying very severely the whole of the railway works. No doubt the

[011-084] weight may be distributed upon a greater number of wheels, but if the

[011-085] weight resting on the driving wheels be much reduced, they will not have

[011-086] sufficient bite upon the rails to propel the train without slipping. This,

[011-087] however, is only one of the evils which the demand for high rates of speed

[011-088] has produced. The width of the railway, or, as it is termed, the _gauge_ of

[011-089] the rails, being in most of the railways in this kingdom limited to 4 feet

[011-090] 8-1/2 inches, a corresponding limitation is imposed on the diameter of the

[011-091] boiler; which in its turn restricts the number of the tubes which can be

[011-092] employed. As, however, the attainment of a high rate of speed requires much

[011-093] power, and consequently much heating surface in the boiler, and as the

[011-094] number of tubes cannot be increased without reducing their diameter, it has

[011-095] become necessary, in the case of powerful engines, to employ tubes of a

[011-096] small diameter, and of a great length, to obtain the necessary quantity of

[011-097] heating surface; and such tubes require a very strong draught in the

[011-098] chimney to make them effective. With a draught of the usual intensity the

[011-099] whole of the heat will be absorbed in the portion of the tube nearest the

[011-100] fire box, leaving that portion nearest the smoke box nothing to do but to

[011-101] transmit the smoke; and with long tubes of small diameter, therefore, a

[011-102] very strong draught is indispensable. To obtain such a draught in

[011-103] locomotives, it is necessary to contract the mouth of the blast pipe,

[011-104] whereby the waste steam will be projected into the chimney with greater

[011-105] force; but this contraction involves an increase of the pressure on the

[011-106] eduction side of the piston, and consequently causes a diminution in the

[011-107] power of the engine. Locomotives with small and long tubes, therefore, will

[011-108] require more coke to do the same work than locomotives in which larger and

[011-109] shorter tubes may be employed.

[011-110] CHAPTER II.

[011-111] HEAT, COMBUSTION, AND STEAM.

[011-112] HEAT.

[011-113] 134. _Q._--What is meant by latent heat?

[011-114] _A._--By latent heat is meant the heat existing in bodies which is not

[011-115] discoverable by the touch or by the thermometer, but which manifests its

[011-116] existence by producing a change of state. Heat is absorbed in the

[011-117] liquefaction of ice, and in the vaporization of water, yet the temperature

[011-118] does not rise during either process, and the heat absorbed is therefore

[011-119] said to become latent. The term is somewhat objectionable, as the effect

[011-120] proper to the absorption of heat has in each case been made visible; and it

[011-121] would be as reasonable to call hot water latent steam. Latent heat, in the

[011-122] present acceptation of the term, means sensible liquefaction or

[011-123] vaporization; but to produce these changes heat is as necessary as to

[011-124] produce the expansion of mercury in a thermometer tube, which is taken as

[011-125] the measure of temperature; and it is hard to see on what ground heat can

[011-126] be said to be latent when its presence is made manifest by changes which

[011-127] only heat can effect. It is the _temperature_ only that is latent, and

[011-128] latent temperature means sensible vaporization or liquefaction.

[011-129] 135. _Q._--But when you talk of the latent heat of steam, what do you mean

[011-130] to express?

[011-131] _A._--I mean to express the heat consumed in accomplishing the vaporization

[011-132] compared with that necessary for producing the temperature. The latent heat

[011-133] of steam is usually reckoned at about 1000 degrees, by which it is meant

[011-134] that there is as much heat in any given weight of steam as would raise its

[011-135] constituent water 1000 degrees if the expansion of the water could be

[011-136] prevented, or as would raise 1000 times that quantity of water one degree.

[011-137] The boiling point of water, being 212 degrees, is 180 degrees above the

[011-138] freezing point of water--the freezing point being 32 degrees; so that it

[011-139] requires 1180 times as much heat to raise 1 lb. of water into steam, as to

[011-140] raise 1180 lbs. of water one degree; or it requires about as much heat to

[011-141] raise a pound of boiling water into steam, as would raise 5-1/2 lbs. of

[011-142] water from the freezing to the boiling point; 5-1/2 multiplied by 180 being

[011-143] 990 or 1000 nearly.

[011-144] 136. _Q._--When it is stated that the latent heat of steam is 1000 degrees,

[011-145] it is only meant that this is a rough approximation to the truth?

[011-146] _A._--Precisely so. The latent heat, in point of fact, is not uniform at

[011-147] all temperatures, neither is the total amount of heat the same at all

[011-148] temperatures. M. Regnault has shown, by a very elaborate series of

[011-149] experiments on steam, which he has lately concluded, that the total heat in

[011-150] steam increases somewhat with the pressure, and that the latent heat

[011-151] diminishes somewhat with the pressure. This will be made obvious by the

[011-152] following numbers:

[011-153] Pressure.          Temperature.             Total Heat.      Latent Heat.

[011-154] 15 lbs.            213.1°                  1178.9°           965.8°

[011-155] 50                 281.0                   1199.6            918.6

[011-156] 100                 327.8                   1213.9            886.1

[011-157] If, then, steam of 100 lbs. be expanded down to steam of 15 lbs., it will

[011-158] have 35 degrees of heat over that which is required for the maintenance of

[011-159] the vaporous state, or, in other words, it will be surcharged with heat.

[011-160] 137. _Q._--What do you understand by specific heat?

[011-161] _A._--By specific heat, I understand the relative quantities of heat in

[011-162] bodies at the same temperature, just as by specific gravity I understand

[011-163] the relative quantities of matter in bodies of the same bulk. Equal weights

[011-164] of quicksilver and water at the same temperature do not contain the same

[011-165] quantities of heat, any more than equal bulks of those liquids contain the

[011-166] same quantity of matter. The absolute quantity of heat in any body is not

[011-167] known; but the relative heat of bodies at the same temperature, or in other

[011-168] words their specific heats, have been ascertained and arranged in tables,--

[011-169] the specific heat of water being taken as unity.

[011-170] 138. _Q._--In what way does the specific heat of a body enable the quantity

[011-171] of heat in it to be determined?

[011-172] _A._--If any body has only half the specific heat of water, then a pound of

[011-173] that body will, at any given temperature, have only half the heat in it

[011-174] that is in a pound of water at the same temperature. The specific heat of

[011-175] air is .2669, that of water being 1; or it is 3.75 times less than that of

[011-176] water. An amount of heat, therefore, which would raise a pound of water 1

[011-177] degree would raise a pound of air 3.75 degrees.
