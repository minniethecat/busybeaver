# Crank Shaft

The **[[crank|crank shaft]]** is the principal rotating shaft of a [[steam|steam engine]], upon which the [[crank]] is formed or fixed. It receives the rotary force communicated by the crank from the reciprocating piston, and transmits this power to the driven machinery — [[paddle-wheel|paddle wheels]] in [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s, driving wheels in [[locomotive]]s, or [[fly-wheel|flywheel]]s and belt pulleys in stationary mill engines (010-115, 010-118, 011-014).

## Operation

The pressures communicated by the crank compel the [[crank|crank shaft]] to revolve (009-099). The force exerted on the [[crank|crank shaft]] by the crank is not uniform throughout a revolution — it is constantly varying, being greatest when the [[crank-pin]] is in a horizontal or near-horizontal position and reducing to zero at the top and bottom dead centres (009-103, 009-106). To smooth this variation, there are usually several wheels — [[fly-wheel]]s or gear wheels — fixed on the [[crank|crank shaft]], which absorb energy during the power stroke and release it during the idle portion of the cycle (010-118).

When the [[crank|crank shaft]] is put into motion by the engine, the [[fly-wheel|flywheel]] mounted upon it stores angular momentum and returns energy to the shaft during those parts of the revolution when the crank contributes little (010-151).

## Arrangement in Engines

In a double-acting [[marine-engine]], two piston rods may connect to a single [[crank|crank shaft]]: one passes above the [[crank|crank shaft]] and the other below it, the [[cross-head]] lying in the same horizontal plane as the shaft centre (037-043, 037-044). In locomotives, the [[crank|crank shaft]] is the driving axle itself, with the crank formed directly upon it (037-057).

## Dimensions

The necks or [[gudgeon|bearing journal]]s of the [[crank|crank shaft]] must be proportioned to withstand the bending and torsional loads applied during operation. [[james-watt|James Watt]] established empirical rules for determining the diameter of his [[crank|crank shaft]] necks based on the cylinder diameter and steam pressure (028-143). The shaft journals run in heavy brass or white-metal bearings, lubricated to reduce friction and wear. In large [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s, the [[crank|crank shaft]] may be forged in sections and coupled together by flanged joints.
