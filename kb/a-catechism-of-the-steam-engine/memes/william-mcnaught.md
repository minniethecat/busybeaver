# William McNaught

**William McNaught** (fl. mid 19th century) was a British engineer and instrument maker, best known for inventing McNaught's indicator — the common form of [[indicator|steam engine indicator]] used throughout nineteenth-century British engineering practice.

## Career and Contributions

The [[indicator|steam engine indicator]] was an instrument used to ascertain the actual power exerted by an engine. It consisted of a small cylinder with a piston moving against a spring, which compressed to a degree proportional to the steam pressure within the main [[cylinder|engine cylinder]]. By recording the pressure throughout the stroke on a paper-wound barrel, the indicator produced a diagram from which the engine's [[horsepower|horsepower]] could be calculated (018-118).

McNaught's design became the standard form of this instrument in British practice. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] described the common indicator by direct reference to McNaught's model: "The structure of the common form of indicator will be most readily apprehended by a reference to fig. 36, which is a McNaught's indicator" (018-124). The instrument featured a movable barrel upon which paper was wound and secured by brass clamps, with a watch spring coiled at the bottom of the barrel to provide the restoring force.

The index of the volume records "McNaught's indicator" and its structure and mode of use as reference entries (069-197, 069-297), confirming the instrument's recognized standing in [[steam|steam engine]]ering literature.

## Legacy

McNaught's indicator became an indispensable tool for [[steam|steam engine]] testing and optimization. The [[indicator-diagram|indicator diagram]] it produced remained the primary diagnostic instrument for [[steam|steam engine]] performance throughout the nineteenth century and beyond. McNaught's name became synonymous with the instrument itself — a mark of his lasting contribution to practical [[steam|steam engine]]ering.
