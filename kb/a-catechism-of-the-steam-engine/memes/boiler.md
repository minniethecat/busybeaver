# Boiler

A **boiler** is a closed vessel in which water is heated by the combustion of fuel to generate [[steam|steam]] under pressure. The boiler is the primary energy conversion component of a [[steam|steam engine]], transforming the chemical energy of fuel into the thermal energy of steam. (007-172)

## Types of Boilers

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes several boiler types used in [[steam|steam engine]] practice:

- **Wagon boiler**: A rectangular boiler with an internal [[flue|fire-tube]], commonly used for land engines. [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]]'s standard design used this form. (007-081)
- **Marine boiler**: Designed for ships, typically of the flue or tubular type, built to withstand the motion of the vessel. (013-090)
- **[[locomotive|Locomotive]] boiler**: A compact [[flue|fire-tube]] boiler designed for railway use, combining high steam-raising capacity with light weight. (022-158)

## Key Design Factors

The performance of a boiler depends on several design parameters:

- **Heating surface**: The area of boiler plate exposed to hot gases on one side and water on the other. More heating surface allows faster steam generation. (020-056)
- **[[grate|Grate]] area**: The area of the [[grate|fire grate]] determines how much fuel can be burned per hour.
- **Draught**: The flow of air through the furnace, either natural (chimney) or forced ([[blast-pipe|blast pipe]]), affects combustion intensity. (007-136)
- **Water circulation**: Good circulation prevents local overheating and ensures even steam generation.
