[012-001] COMBUSTION.

[012-002] 139. _Q._--What is the nature of combustion?

[012-003] _A._--Combustion is nothing more than an energetic chemical combination,

[012-004] or, in other words, it is the mutual neutralization of opposing

[012-005] electricities. When coal is brought to a high temperature it acquires a

[012-006] strong affinity for oxygen, and combination with oxygen will produce more

[012-007] than sufficient heat to maintain the original temperature; so that part of

[012-008] the heat is rendered applicable to other purposes.

[012-009] 140. _Q._--Does air consist of oxygen?

[012-010] _A._--Air consists of oxygen and nitrogen mixed together in the proportion

[012-011] of 3.29 lbs. of nitrogen to 1 lb. of oxygen. Every pound of coal requires

[012-012] about 2.66 lbs. of oxygen for its saturation, and therefore for every pound

[012-013] of coal burned, 8.75 pounds of nitrogen must pass through the fire,

[012-014] supposing all the oxygen to enter into combination. In practice, however,

[012-015] this perfection of combination does not exist; from one-third to one-half

[012-016] of the oxygen will pass through the fire without entering into combination

[012-017] at all; so that from 16 to 18 lbs. of air are required for every pound of

[012-018] coal burned. 18 lbs. of air are about 240 cubic feet, which may be taken as

[012-019] the quantity of air required for the combustion of a pound of coal in

[012-020] practice.

[012-021] 141. _Q._--What are the constituents of coal?

[012-022] _A._--The chief constituent of coal is carbon or pure charcoal, which is

[012-023] associated in various proportions with volatile and earthy matters. English

[012-024] coal contains 80 to 90 per cent. of carbon, and from 8 to 18 per cent. of

[012-025] volatile and earthy matters, but sometimes more than this. The volatile

[012-026] matters are hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, and sulphur.

[012-027] 142. _Q._--What is the difference between anthracite and bituminous coal?

[012-028] _A._--Anthracite consists almost entirely of carbon, having 91 per cent. of

[012-029] carbon, with about 7 per cent. of volatile matter and 2 per cent. of ashes.

[012-030] Newcastle coal contains about 83 per cent. of carbon, 14 per cent. of

[012-031] volatile matter, and 3 per cent. of ashes.

[012-032] 143. _Q._--Will you recapitulate the steps by which you determine the

[012-033] quantity of air required for the combustion of coal?

[012-034] _A._--Looking to the quantity of oxygen required to unite chemically with

[012-035] the various constituents of the coal, we find for example that in 100 lbs.

[012-036] of anthracite coal, consisting of 91.44 lbs. of carbon, and 3.46 lbs. of

[012-037] hydrogen, we shall for the 91.44 lbs. of carbon require 243.84 lbs. of

[012-038] oxygen--since to saturate a pound of carbon by the formation of carbonic

[012-039] acid, requires 2-2/3 lbs. of oxygen. To saturate a pound of hydrogen in the

[012-040] formation of water, requires 8 lbs. of oxygen; hence 3.46 Fibs. of hydrogen

[012-041] will take 27.68 lbs. of oxygen for its saturation. If then we add 243.84

[012-042] lbs. to 27.68 lbs. we have 271.52 lbs. of oxygen required for the

[012-043] combustion of 100 lbs. of coal. A given weight of air contains nearly 23.32

[012-044] per cent of oxygen; hence to obtain 271.52 lbs. of oxygen, we must have

[012-045] about four times that quantity of atmospheric air, or more accurately, 1164

[012-046] lbs. of air for the combustion of 100 lbs. of coal. A cubic foot of air at

[012-047] ordinary temperature weighs about .075 lbs.; so that 100 lbs. of coal

[012-048] require 15,524 cubic feet of air, or 1 lb. of coal requires about 155 cubic

[012-049] feet of air, supposing every atom of the oxygen to enter into combination.

[012-050] If, then, from one-third to one-half of the air passes unconsumed through

[012-051] the fire, an allowance of 240 cubic feet of air for each pound of coal will

[012-052] be a small enough allowance to answer the requirements of practice, and in

[012-053] some cases as much as 300 cubic feet will be required,--the difference

[012-054] depending mainly on the peculiar configuration of the furnace.

[012-055] 144. _Q._--Can you state the evaporative efficacy of a pound of coal?

[012-056] _A._--The evaporative efficacy of a pound of carbon has been found

[012-057] experimentally to be equivalent to that necessary to raise 14,000 lbs. of

[012-058] water through 1 degree, or 14 lbs. of water through 1000 degrees, supposing

[012-059] the whole heat generated to be absorbed by the water. Now, if the water be

[012-060] raised into steam from a temperature of 60°, then 1118.9° of heat will have

[012-061] to be imparted to it to convert it into steam of 15 lbs. pressure per

[012-062] square inch. 14,000 / 1118.9 = 12.512 Lbs. will be the number of pounds of

[012-063] water, therefore, which a pound of carbon can raise into steam of 15 lbs.

[012-064] pressure from a temperature of 60°. This, however, is a considerably larger

[012-065] result than can be expected in practice.

[012-066] 145. _Q._--Then what is the result that may be expected in practice?

[012-067] _A._--The evaporative powers of different coals appear to be nearly

[012-068] proportional to the quantity of carbon in them; and bituminous coal is,

[012-069] therefore, less efficacious than coal consisting chiefly of pure carbon. A

[012-070] pound of the best Welsh or anthracite coal is capable of raising from 9-1/2

[012-071] to 10 lbs. of water from 212° into steam, whereas a pound of the best

[012-072] Newcastle is not capable of raising more than about 8-1/2 lbs. of water

[012-073] from 212° into steam; and inferior coals will not raise more than 6-1/2

[012-074] lbs. of water into steam. In America it has been found that 1 lb. of the

[012-075] best coal is equal to 2-1/2 lbs. of pine wood, or, in some cases to 3 lbs.;

[012-076] and a pound of pine wood will not usually evaporate more than about 2 1/2

[012-077] lbs. of water, though, by careful management, it may be made to evaporate 4

[012-078] 1/2 lbs. Turf will generate rather more steam than wood. Coke is equal or

[012-079] somewhat superior to the best coal in evaporative effect.

[012-080] 146. _Q._--How much water will a pound of coal raise into steam in ordinary

[012-081] boilers?

[012-082] _A._--From 6 to 8 lbs. of water in the generality of land boilers of medium

[012-083] quality, the difference depending on the kind of boiler, the kind of coal,

[012-084] and other circumstances. Mr. Watt reckoned his boilers as capable of

[012-085] evaporating 10.08 cubic feet of water with a bushel or 84 lbs. of Newcastle

[012-086] coal, which is equivalent to 7 1/2 lbs. of water evaporated by 1 lb. of

[012-087] coal, and this may be taken as the performance of common land boilers at

[012-088] the present time. In some of the Cornish boilers, however, a pound of coal

[012-089] raises 11.8 lbs. of boiling water into steam, or a cwt. of coal evaporates

[012-090] about 21 cubic feet of water from 212°.

[012-091] 147. _Q._--What method of firing ordinary furnaces is the best?

[012-092] _A._--The coals should be broken up into small pieces, and sprinkled thinly

[012-093] and evenly over the fire a little at a time. The thickness of the stratum

[012-094] of coal upon the grate should depend upon the intensity of the draught: in

[012-095] ordinary land or marine boilers it should be thin, whereas in locomotive

[012-096] boilers it requires to be much thicker. If the stratum of coal be thick

[012-097] while the draught is sluggish, the carbonic acid resulting from combustion

[012-098] combines with an additional atom of carbon in passing through the fire, and

[012-099] is converted into carbonic oxide, which may be defined to be invisible

[012-100] smoke, as it carries off a portion of the fuel: if, on the contrary, the

[012-101] stratum of coal be thin while the draught is very rapid, an injurious

[012-102] refrigeration is occasioned by the excess of air passing through the

[012-103] furnace. The fire should always be spread of uniform thickness over the

[012-104] bars of the grate, and should be without any holes or uncovered places,

[012-105] which greatly diminish the effect of the fuel by the refrigeratory action

[012-106] of the stream of cold air which enters thereby. A wood fire requires to be

[012-107] about 6 inches thicker than a coal one, and a turf fire requires to be 3 or

[012-108] 4 inches thicker than a wood one, so that the furnace bars must be placed

[012-109] lower where wood or turf is burned, to enable the surface of the fire to be

[012-110] at the same distance from the bottom of the boiler.

[012-111] 148. _Q._--Is a slow or a rapid combustion the most beneficial?

[012-112] _A._--A slow combustion is found by experiment to give the best results as

[012-113] regards economy of fuel, and theory tells us that the largest advantage

[012-114] will necessarily be obtained where adequate time has been afforded for a

[012-115] complete combination of the constituent atoms of the combustible, and the

[012-116] supporter of combustion. In many of the cases, however, which occur in

[012-117] practice, a slow combustion is not attainable; but the tendencies of slow

[012-118] combustion are both to save the fuel, and to burn the smoke.

[012-119] 149. _Q._--Is not the combustion in the furnaces of the Cornish boilers

[012-120] very slow?

[012-121] A.--Yes, very slow; and there is in consequence very little smoke evolved.

[012-122] The coal used in Cornwall is Welsh coal, which evolves but little smoke,

[012-123] and is therefore more favorable for the success of a smokeless furnace; but

[012-124] in the manufacturing districts, where the coal is more bituminous, it is

[012-125] found that smoke may be almost wholly prevented by careful firing and by

[012-126] the use of a large capacity of furnace.

[012-127] 150. _Q._--Do you consider slow combustion to be an advisable thing to

[012-128] practise in steam vessels?

[012-129] _A._--No, I do not. When the combustion is slow, the heat in the furnaces

[012-130] and flues is less intense, and a larger amount of heating surface

[012-131] consequently becomes necessary to absorb the heat. In locomotives, where

[012-132] the heat of the furnace is very intense, there will be the same economy of

[012-133] fuel with an allowance of 5 or 6 square feet of surface to evaporate a

[012-134] cubic foot of water as in common marine boilers with 10 or 12.

[012-135] 151. _Q._--What is the method of consuming smoke pursued in the

[012-136] manufacturing districts?

[012-137] _A._--In Manchester, where some stringent regulations for the prevention of

[012-138] smoke have for some time been in force, it is found that the readiest way

[012-139] of burning the smoke is to have a very large proportion of furnace room,

[012-140] whereby slow combustion may be carried on. In some cases, too, a favourable

[012-141] result is arrived at by raising a ridge of coal across the furnace lying

[012-142] against the bridge, and of the same height: this ridge speedily becomes a

[012-143] mass of incandescent coke, which promotes the combustion of the smoke

[012-144] passing over it.

[012-145] 152. _Q._--Is the method of admitting a stream of air into the flues to

[012-146] burn the smoke regarded favorably?

[012-147] _A._--No; it is found to be productive of injury to the boiler by the

[012-148] violent alternations of temperature it occasions, as at some times cold air

[012-149] impinges on the iron of the boiler, and at other times flame,--just as

[012-150] there happens to be smoke or no smoke emitted by the furnace. Boilers,

[012-151] therefore, operating upon this principle, speedily become leaky, and are

[012-152] much worn by oxidation, so that, if the pressure is considerable, they are

[012-153] liable to explode. It is very difficult to apportion the quantity of air

[012-154] admitted, to the varying wants of the fire; and as air may at some times be

[012-155] rushing in when there is no smoke to consume, a loss of heat, and an

[012-156] increased consumption of fuel may be the result of the arrangement; and,

[012-157] indeed, such is the result in practice, though a carefully performed

[012-158] experiment usually demonstrates a saving in fuel of 10 or 12 per cent.

[012-159] 153. _Q._--What other plans have been contrived for obviating the nuisance

[012-160] of smoke?

[012-161] _A._--They are too various for enumeration, but most of them either operate

[012-162] upon the principle of admitting air into the flues to accomplish the

[012-163] combustion of the uninflammable parts of the smoke, or seek to attain the

[012-164] same object by passing the smoke over or through the fire or other

[012-165] incandescent material. Some of the plans, indeed, profess to burn the

[012-166] inflammable gases as they are evolved from the coal, without permitting the

[012-167] admixture of any of the uninflammable products of combustion which enter

[012-168] into the composition of smoke; but this object has been very imperfectly

[012-169] fulfilled in any of the contrivances yet brought under the notice of the

[012-170] public, and in some cases these contrivances have been found to create

[012-171] weightier evils than they professed to relieve.
