The **flue** is a passage or conduit within a [[boiler]] that carries hot gases from the [[furnace]] through the water space to the [[chimney]]. Flues are the principal means by which heat from combustion is transferred to the boiler water; their design — length, cross-sectional area, and path — directly determines the boiler's steaming efficiency.

## Flue boilers

In early and many stationary boilers the flues are large brickwork or iron passages that wind back and forth through the water space:

> [008-004] These are of two classes, flue boilers and tubular boilers, of which the latter are now most used. In the flue boiler the furnaces are set within the boiler, and the flues proceeding from them wind back and forth within the water space.

The furnace itself is often set within one of the flues:

> [007-161] In this boiler there are two internal flues in which the furnace is placed. The furnace is surrounded by water, so that no heat is lost by radiation.

The combustion gases typically pass first through a central flue, then return along side flues before entering the chimney:

> [007-170] The smoke, after passing through the central flue, circulates round the sides and beneath the bottom of the boiler before its final escape into the chimney.

## Tubular boilers

The tubular boiler replaces large flues with many small-diameter tubes — greatly increasing the heating surface:

> [008-020] The boiler is filled with a number of small tubes, through which the smoke passes from the furnace to the smoke box, and then enters the chimney. These boilers, with their internal tubes, have greatly less weight and bulk than the flue boilers of the same power.

This development made the [[locomotive]] boiler practical, as the large heating surface in a compact volume could generate steam at the high rates demanded by locomotive cylinders.

## Heating surface

The effectiveness of a flue depends on the area of metal exposed to hot gases on one side and water on the other — the heating surface:

| [[boiler|Boiler]] type | Typical heating surface per HP | Relative weight |
|------------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| Flue boiler (land) | 10–15 sq ft | High |
| Tubular boiler (locomotive) | 5–8 sq ft | Very low |
| Marine flue boiler | 12–18 sq ft | High |

> [012-130] The intensity of the heat in the furnace and flues is less intense in a marine boiler than in a locomotive boiler, and a large amount of heating surface is requisite to extract the necessary heat from the gases.

## Flue gas temperature

The temperature of the gases progressively falls as they travel through the flues, and it is important to extract as much heat as possible before they enter the chimney:

> [020-017] The flues must be made of a certain length to enable the heat to be sufficiently extracted from the smoke before it enters the chimney. The flues, moreover, must have a certain area to give the requisite draught.

If the gases exit too hot, fuel is wasted; if they cool too much, the [[damper|draft]] weakens and combustion suffers. Good boiler design balances these competing requirements.
