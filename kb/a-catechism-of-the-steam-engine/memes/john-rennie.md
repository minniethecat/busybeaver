# John Rennie

**John Rennie** (1761–1821), and his son **George Rennie** (1791–1866), were Scottish engineers whose experimental work on friction provided important reference data for [[steam|steam engine]] builders. G. Rennie's friction measurements are cited by [[john-bourne|Bourne]] in discussions of shaft design and bearing materials (005-065).

## Career and Contributions

The Rennies conducted systematic experiments to measure the coefficient of friction between various materials under different conditions. Their work established baseline values for the frictional resistance of iron shafts rotating in brass bearings — data essential for calculating the power losses in engine shafts and for dimensioning bearings correctly.

George Rennie found that with a bearing pressure of approximately 15 psi, the friction of wrought-iron shafts in brass bearings amounted to roughly 8 per cent of the transmitted load. This figure became a standard reference for [[marine-engine|marine engine]] designers, allowing them to estimate shaft power losses and select appropriate bearing dimensions (005-065).

## Significance

The Rennie friction data exemplifies the shift from rule-of-thumb engineering to quantitative, experimentally-informed design that characterised nineteenth-century [[steam|steam engine]]ering progress. By providing reliable coefficients, the Rennies enabled engineers to move beyond guesswork when sizing shafts and bearings for increasingly powerful marine and stationary engines.
