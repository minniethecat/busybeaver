[023-001] 286. _Q._--Will you illustrate the relation between the length and diameter

[023-002] of locomotive tubes by a comparison with the proportion of flues in flue

[023-003] boilers?

[023-004] _A._--In most locomotives the velocity of the draught is such that it would

[023-005] require very long tubes to extract the heat from the products of

[023-006] combustion, if the heat were transmitted through the metal of the tubes

[023-007] with only the same facility as through the iron of ordinary flue boilers.

[023-008] The Nile steamer, with engines of 110 nominal horses power each, and with

[023-009] two boilers having two independent flues in each, of such dimensions as to

[023-010] make each flue equivalent to 55 nominal horses power, works at 62 per cent.

[023-011] above the nominal power, so that the actual evaporative efficacy of each

[023-012] flue would be equivalent to 89 actual horses power, supposing the engines

[023-013] to operate without expansion; but as the mean pressure in the cylinder is

[023-014] somewhat less than the initial pressure, the evaporative efficacy of each

[023-015] flue may be reckoned equivalent to 80 actual horses power. With this

[023-016] evaporative power there is a calorimeter of 990 square inches, or 12.3

[023-017] square inches per actual horse power; whereas in Stephenson's locomotive

[023-018] with 150 tubes, if the evaporative power be taken at 200 cubic feet of

[023-019] water in the hour, which is a large supposition, the engine will be equal

[023-020] to 200 actual horses power. If the internal diameter of the tubes be taken

[023-021] at thirteen eighths of an inch, the calorimeter per actual horse power will

[023-022] only be 1.1136 square inches, or in other words the calorimeter in the

[023-023] locomotive boiler will be 11.11 times less than in the flue boiler for the

[023-024] same power, so that the draught in the locomotive must be 11.11 times

[023-025] stronger, and the ratio of the length of the tube to its diameter 11.11

[023-026] times greater than in the flue boiler, supposing the heat to be transmitted

[023-027] with only the same facility. The flue of the Nile would require to be 35-

[023-028] 1/2 inches in diameter if made of the cylindrical form, and 47-3/4 feet

[023-029] long; the tubes of a locomotive if 1-3/8ths inch diameter would only

[023-030] require to be 22.19 inches long with the same velocity of draught; but as

[023-031] the draught is 11.11 times faster than in a flue boiler, the tubes ought to

[023-032] be 246.558 inches, or about 20-1/2 feet long according to this proportion.

[023-033] In practice, however, they are one third less than this, which reduces the

[023-034] heating surface from 9 to 6 square feet per actual horse power, and this

[023-035] length even is found to be inconvenient. It is greatly preferable therefore

[023-036] to increase the calorimeter, and diminish the intensity of the draught.

[023-037] BOILER CHIMNEYS.

[023-038] 287. _Q._--By what process do you ascertain the dimensions of the chimney

[023-039] of a land boiler?

[023-040] _A._--By a reference to the volume of air it is necessary in a given time

[023-041] to supply to the burning fuel, and to the velocity of motion produced by

[023-042] the rarefaction in the chimney; for the area of the chimney requires to be

[023-043] such, that with the velocity due to that rarefaction, the quantity of air

[023-044] requisite for the combustion of the fuel shall pass through the furnace in

[023-045] the specified time. Thus if 200 cubic feet of air of the atmospheric

[023-046] density are required for the combustion of a pound of coal,--though 250

[023-047] lbs. is nearer the quantity generally required,--and 10 lbs. of coal per

[023-048] horse power per hour are consumed by an engine, then 2000 cubic feet of air

[023-049] must be supplied to the furnace per horse power per hour, and the area of

[023-050] the chimney must be such as to deliver this quantity at the increased bulk

[023-051] due to the high temperature of the chimney when moving with the velocity

[023-052] the rarefaction within the chimney occasions, and which, in small chimneys,

[023-053] is usually such as to support a column of half an inch of water. The

[023-054] velocity with which a denser fluid flows into a rarer one is equal to the

[023-055] velocity a heavy body acquires in falling through a height equal to the

[023-056] difference of altitude of two columns of the heavier fluid of such heights

[023-057] as will produce the respective pressures; and, therefore, when the

[023-058] difference of pressure or amount of rarefaction in the chimney is known, it

[023-059] is easy to tell the velocity of motion which ought to be produced by it. In

[023-060] practice, however, these theoretical results are not to be trusted, until

[023-061] they have received such modifications as will make them representative of

[023-062] the practice of the most experienced constructors.

[023-063] 288. _Q._--What then is the rule followed by the most experienced

[023-064] constructors?

[023-065] _A._--Boulton and Watt's rule for the dimensions of the chimney of a land

[023-066] engine is as follows:--multiply the number of pounds of coal consumed under

[023-067] the boiler per hour by 12, and divide the product by the square root of the

[023-068] height of the chimney in feet; the quotient is the area of the chimney in

[023-069] square inches in the smallest part. A factory chimney suitable for a 20

[023-070] horse boiler is commonly made about 20 in. square inside, and 80 ft. high;

[023-071] and these dimensions are those which answer to a consumption of 15 lbs. of

[023-072] coal per horse power per hour, which is a very common consumption in

[023-073] factory engines. If 15 lbs. of coal be consumed per horse power per hour,

[023-074] the total consumption per hour in a 20 horse boiler will be 300 lbs., and

[023-075] 300 multiplied by 12 = 3600, and divided by 9 (the square root of the

[023-076] height) = 400, which is the area of the chimney in square inches. It will

[023-077] not answer well to increase the height of a chimney of this area to more

[023-078] than 40 or 50 yards, without also increasing the area, nor will it be of

[023-079] utility to increase the area much without also increasing the height. The

[023-080] quantity of coal consumed per hour in pounds, multiplied by 5, and divided

[023-081] by the square root of the height of the chimney, is the proper collective

[023-082] area of the openings between the bars of the grate for the admission of air

[023-083] to the fire.

[023-084] 289. _Q._--Is this rule applicable to the chimneys of steam vessels?

[023-085] _A._--In steam vessels Boulton and Watt have heretofore been in the habit

[023-086] of allowing 8-1/2 square inches of area of chimney per horse power, but

[023-087] they now allow 6 square inches to 7 square inches. In some steam vessels a

[023-088] steam blast like that of a locomotive, but of a smaller volume, is used in

[023-089] the chimney, and many of the evils of a boiler deficient in draught may be

[023-090] remedied by this expedient, but a steam blast in a low pressure engine

[023-091] occasions an obvious waste of steam; it also makes an unpleasant noise, and

[023-092] in steam vessels it frequently produces the inconvenience of carrying the

[023-093] smaller parts of the coal up the chimney, and scattering it over the deck

[023-094] among the passengers. It is advisable, therefore, to give a sufficient

[023-095] calorimeter in all low pressure boilers, and a sufficient height of chimney

[023-096] to enable the chimney to operate without a steam jet; but it is useful to

[023-097] know that a steam jet is a resource in the case of a defective boiler, or

[023-098] where the boiler has to be urged beyond its power.

[023-099] STEAM ROOM AND PRIMING.

[023-100] 290. _Q._--What is the capacity of steam room allowed in boilers per horse

[023-101] power?

[023-102] _A._--The capacity of steam room allowed by Boulton and Watt in their land

[023-103] wagon boilers is 8-3/4 cubic feet per horse power in the two horse power

[023-104] boiler, and 5-3/4 cubic feet in the 20 horse power boiler; and in the

[023-105] larger class of boilers, such as those suitable for 30 and 45 horse power

[023-106] engines, the capacity of the steam room does not fall below this amount,

[023-107] and, indeed, is nearer 6 than 5-3/4 cubic feet per horse power. The content

[023-108] of water is 18-1/2 cubic feet per horse power in the two horse power

[023-109] boiler, and 15 cubic feet per horse power in the 20 horse power boiler.

[023-110] 291. _Q._--Is this the proportion Boulton and Watt allow in their marine

[023-111] boilers?

[023-112] _A._--Boulton and Watt in their early steam vessels were in the habit of

[023-113] allowing for the capacity of the steam, space in marine boilers 16 times

[023-114] the content of the cylinder; but as there were two cylinders, this was

[023-115] equivalent to 8 times the content of both cylinders, which is the

[023-116] proportion commonly followed in land engines, and which agrees very nearly

[023-117] with the proportion of between 5 and 6 cubic feet of steam room per horse

[023-118] power already referred to. Taking for example an engine with 23 inches

[023-119] diameter of cylinder and 4 feet stroke, which will be 18.4 horse power--the

[023-120] area of the cylinder will be 415.476 square inches, which, multiplied by

[023-121] 48, the number of inches in the stroke, will give 19942.848 for the

[023-122] capacity of the cylinder in cubic inches; 8 times this is 159542.784 cubic

[023-123] inches, or 92.3 cubic feet; 92.3 divided by 18.4 is rather more than 5

[023-124] cubic feet per horse power.

[023-125] 292. _Q._--Is the production of the steam in the boiler uniform throughout

[023-126] the stroke of the engine?

[023-127] _A._--It varies with the slight variations in the pressure within the

[023-128] boiler throughout the stroke. Usually the larger part of the steam is

[023-129] produced during the first part of the stroke of the engine, for there is

[023-130] then the largest demand for steam, as the steam being commonly cut off

[023-131] somewhat before the end of the stroke, the pressure rises somewhat in the

[023-132] boiler during that period, and little steam is then produced. There is less

[023-133] necessity that the steam space should be large when the flow of steam from

[023-134] the boiler is very uniform, as it will be where there are two engines

[023-135] attached to the boiler at right angles with one another, or where the

[023-136] engines work at a great speed, as in the case of locomotive engines. A high

[023-137] steam chest too, by rendering boiling over into the steam pipes, or priming

[023-138] as it is called, more difficult, obviates the necessity for so large a

[023-139] steam space; as does also a perforated steam pipe stretching through the

[023-140] length of the boiler, so as not to take the steam from one place. The use

[023-141] of steam of a high pressure, worked expansively, has the same operation; so

[023-142] that in modern marine boilers, of the tubular construction, where the whole

[023-143] or most of these modifying circumstances exist, there is no necessity for

[023-144] so large a proportion of steam room as 5 or 6 cubic feet per nominal horse

[023-145] power, and about one, 1-1/2, or 2 cubic feet of steam room per cubic foot

[023-146] of water evaporated, more nearly represents the general practice.

[023-147] 293. _Q._--Is this the proportion of steam room adopted in locomotive

[023-148] boilers?

[023-149] _A._--No; in locomotive boilers the proportion of steam room per cubic foot

[023-150] of water evaporated is considerably less even than this. It does not

[023-151] usually exceed 1/5 of a cubic foot per cubic foot of water evaporated; and

[023-152] with clean water, with a steam dome a few feet high set on the barrel of

[023-153] the boiler, or with a perforated pipe stretching from end to end of the

[023-154] barrel, and with the steam room divided about equally between the barrel

[023-155] and the fire box, very little priming is found to occur even with this

[023-156] small proportion of total steam room. About 3/4 the depth of the barrel is

[023-157] usually filled with water, and 1/4 with steam.

[023-158] 294. _Q._--What is priming?

[023-159] _A._--Priming is a violent agitation of the water within the boiler, in

[023-160] consequence of which a large quantity of water passes off with the steam in

[023-161] the shape of froth or spray. Such a result is injurious, both as regards

[023-162] the efficacy of the engine, and the safety of the engine and boiler; for

[023-163] the large volume of hot water carried by the steam into the condenser

[023-164] impairs the vacuum, and throws a great load upon the air pump, which

[023-165] diminishes the speed and available power of the engine; and the existence

[023-166] of water within the cylinder, unless there be safety valves upon the

[023-167] cylinder to permit its escape, will very probably cause some part of the

[023-168] machinery to break, by suddenly arresting the motion of the piston when it

[023-169] meets the surface of the water,--the slide valve being closed to the

[023-170] condenser before the termination of the stroke, in all engines with lap

[023-171] upon the valves, so that the water within the cylinder is prevented from

[023-172] escaping in that direction. At the same time the boiler is emptied of its

[023-173] water too rapidly for the feed pump to be able to maintain the supply, and

[023-174] the flues are in danger of being burnt from a deficiency of water above

[023-175] them.
