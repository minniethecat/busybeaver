# Crank Pin

The **crank pin** is the cylindrical journal or bearing surface at the extremity of the [[crank]] to which the [[connecting-rod]] is coupled. It serves as the physical point through which the reciprocating force of the piston is transmitted to the crank, converting linear motion into rotary motion of the [[crank-shaft]] (009-091, 010-060).

## Operation

As the crank revolves, the crank pin travels in a circular path. The velocity of the crank pin is nearly uniform, so the piston moves slowly as it approaches the end of its stroke; the leverage exerted upon the crank pin, however, varies continuously throughout the revolution. When the crank pin passes from the level of one line to the level of another, equal arcs correspond to equal ascents or descents of the piston, but the power expended by the steam is greatest when the crank pin approaches the top and bottom centres (004-128, 004-132).

In [[locomotive]] engines, the crank pin is formed directly on the driving axle. In marine and mill engines, the loose end of the crank pin is made not spheroidal but flat, enabling the [[connecting-rod]] to accommodate the motion of the crank (037-016, 041-163).

## Dimensions

The diameter of the crank pin is proportional to the load it bears. In practice, the diameter in inches is found by multiplying 0.02836 times the square root of the pressure on the piston in pounds per square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the [[cylinder]] in inches (029-096). The thickness of the crank web at the crank pin centre is determined by adding 0.02521 times the square root of the pressure on the piston, multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder, to the pin diameter (029-057).

The crank pin is one of the most heavily stressed components in a [[steam|steam engine]], requiring careful proportioning for the necessary strength (029-168). In large engines, the crank pin may be fixed immovably to the [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] rather than to the crank itself, particularly where the axles are made straight and the crank pins are inserted into forged bosses (044-136, 044-157).

## Construction

[[crank|Crank]] pins must be finished to a high degree of smoothness and accuracy. In wrought-iron cranks, the pin is forged integrally with the crank web; in built-up cranks, it is inserted and secured by a key or shrink fit. The surface of the pin is case-hardened or fitted with a brass bushing to resist wear from the oscillating motion of the [[connecting-rod]] bearing.
