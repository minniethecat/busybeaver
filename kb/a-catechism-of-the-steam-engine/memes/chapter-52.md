[052-001] 601. _Q._--Is it not important to make the stern of screw vessels very

[052-002] fine, with the view of diminishing the slip, and increasing the speed?

[052-003] _A._--It is most important. The Rifleman, a vessel of 486 tons, had

[052-004] originally engines of 200 horses power, which propelled her at a speed of 8

[052-005] knots an hour. The Teazer, a vessel of 296 tons, had originally engines of

[052-006] 100 horses power, which propelled her at a speed of 6-1/2 knots an hour.

[052-007] The engines of the Teazer were subsequently transferred to the Rifleman,

[052-008] and new engines of 40 horse power were put into the Teazer. Both vessels

[052-009] were simultaneously sharpened at the stern, and the result was, that the

[052-010] 100 horse engines drove the Rifleman, when sharpened, as fast as she had

[052-011] previously been driven by the 200 horse engines; and the 40 horse engines

[052-012] drove the Teazer, when sharpened, a knot an hour faster than she had

[052-013] previously been driven by the 100 horse engines. The immersion of both

[052-014] vessels was kept unchanged in each case; and the 100 horse engines of the

[052-015] Teazer, when transferred to the Rifleman, drove that vessel, after she had

[052-016] been sharpened, 2 knots an hour faster than they had previously driven a

[052-017] vessel not much more than half the size. These are important facts for

[052-018] every one to be acquainted with who is interested in the success of screw

[052-019] vessels, and who seeks to obtain the maximum of efficiency with the minimum

[052-020] of expense.[1]

[052-021] [1] See Treatise on the Screw Propeller, by John Bourne, C. E.

[052-022] PROPORTIONS OF SCREWS.

[052-023] 602. _Q._--In fixing upon the proportions of a screw proper to propel any

[052-024] given vessel, how would you proceed?

[052-025] _A._--I would first compute the probable resistance of the vessel, and I

[052-026] would be able to find the relative resistances of the screw and hull, and

[052-027] in every case it is advisable to make the screw as large in diameter as

[052-028] possible. The larger the screw is, the greater will be the efficiency of

[052-029] the engine in propelling the vessel; the larger will be the ratio of the

[052-030] pitch to the diameter, which produces a maximum effect; and the smaller

[052-031] will be the length of the screw or the fraction of a convolution to produce

[052-032] a maximum effect.

[052-033] 603. _Q._--Will you illustrate this doctrine by a practical example?

[052-034] _A._--The French screw steamer Pelican was fitted successively with two

[052-035] screws of four blades, but the diameter of the first screw was 98.42

[052-036] inches, and the diameter of the second 54 inches. If the efficiency of the

[052-037] first screw by represented by 1, that of the second screw will be

[052-038] represented by .823, or, in other words, if the first screw would give a

[052-039] speed of 10 knots, the second would give little more than 8. The most

[052-040] advantageous ratio of pitch to diameter was found to be 2.2 in the case of

[052-041] the large screw, and 1.384 in the case of the small. The fraction of a

[052-042] convolution which was found to be most advantageous was .281 in the case of

[052-043] the large screw, and .450 in the case of the small screw.

[052-044] 604. _Q_--Were screws of four blades found to be more efficient than screws

[052-045] with two?

[052-046] _A_--They were found to have less slip, but not to be more

[052-047] efficient, the increased slip in those of two blades being balanced by the

[052-048] increased friction in those of four. Screws of two blades, to secure a

[052-049] maximum efficiency, must have a finer pitch than screws of four.

[052-050] 605. _Q._--Are the proportions found to be most suitable in the case of the

[052-051] Pelican applicable to the screws of other vessels?

[052-052] _A._--Only to those which have the same relative resistance of screw and

[052-053] hull. Taking the relative resistance to be the area of immersed midship

[052-054] section, divided by the square of the screw's diameter, it will in the case

[052-055] of the Rattler be 380/100 or 3.8. From the experiments made by MM. Bourgois

[052-056] and Moll on the screw steamer Pelican, they have deduced the proportions of

[052-057] screws proper for all other classes of vessels, whether the screws are of

[052-058] two, four, or six blades.

[052-059] 606. _Q._--Will you specify the nature of their deductions?

[052-060] _A._--I will first enumerate those which bear upon screws with two blades.

[052-061] When the relative resistance is 5.5 the ratio of pitch to diameter should

[052-062] be 1.006, and the fraction of the pitch or proportion of one entire

[052-063] convolution should be 0.454. When the relative resistance is 5, the ratio

[052-064] of pitch to diameter should be 1.069, and fraction of pitch 0.428; relative

[052-065] resistance 4.5, pitch 1.135, fraction 0.402; relative resistance 4, pitch

[052-066] 1.205, fraction 0.378; relative resistance 3.5, pitch 1.279, fraction

[052-067] 0.355; relative resistance 3, pitch 1.357, fraction 0.334; relative

[052-068] resistance 2.5, pitch 1.450, fraction 0.313; relative resistance 2, pitch

[052-069] 1.560, fraction 0.294; relative resistance 1.5, pitch 1.682, fraction

[052-070] 0.275. The relative resistance of 4 is that which is usual in an auxiliary

[052-071] line of battle ship, 3.5 in an auxiliary frigate, 3 in a high speed line of

[052-072] battle ship, 2.5 in a high speed frigate, 2 in a high speed corvette, and

[052-073] 1.5 in a high speed despatch boat.

[052-074] 607. _Q._--What are the corresponding proportions of screws of four blades?

[052-075] _A._--The ratios of the pitches to the diameter being for each of the

[052-076] relative resistances enumerated above, 1.342, 1.425, 1.513, 1.607, 1.705,

[052-077] 1.810, 1.933, 2.080, and 2.243, the respective fractions of pitch or

[052-078] fractions of a whole convolution will be 0.455, 0.428, 0.402, 0.378, 0.355,

[052-079] 0.334, 0.313, 0.294, and 0.275.

[052-080] 608. _Q._--And what are the corresponding proportions proper for screws of

[052-081] six blades?

[052-082] _A._--Beginning with the relative resistance of 5.5 as before, the proper

[052-083] ratio of pitch to diameter for that and each of the successive resistances

[052-084] in the case of screws with six blades, will be 1.677, 1.771, 1.891, 1.2009,

[052-085] 2.131, 2.262, 2.416, 2.600, 2.804; and the respective fractions of pitch

[052-086] will be 0.794, 0.749, 0.703, 0.661, 0.621, 0.585, 0.548, 0.515, and 0.481.

[052-087] These are the proportions which will give a maximum performance in every

[052-088] case.[1]

[052-089] [1] In my Treatise on the Screw Propeller I have gone into these various

[052-090] questions more fully than would consort with the limits of this

[052-091] publication.

[052-092] SCREW VESSELS WITH FULL AND AUXILIARY POWER.

[052-093] 609. _Q._--Do you consider that the screw propeller is best adapted for

[052-094] vessels of full power, or for vessels with auxiliary power?

[052-095] _A._--It is, in my opinion, best adapted for vessels with auxiliary power,

[052-096] and it is a worse propeller than paddle wheels for vessels which have

[052-097] habitually to encounter strong head winds. Screw vessels are but ill

[052-098] calculated--at least as constructed heretofore--to encounter head winds,

[052-099] and the legitimate sphere of the screw is in propelling vessels with

[052-100] auxiliary power.

[052-101] 610. _Q._--Does the screw act well in conjunction with sails?

[052-102] _A._--I cannot say it acts better than paddles, except in so far as it is

[052-103] less in the way and is less affected by the listing or heeling over of the

[052-104] ship. A small steam power, however, acts very advantageously in aid of

[052-105] sails, for not only does the operation of the sails in reducing the

[052-106] resistance of the hull virtually increase the screw's diameter, but the

[052-107] screw, by reducing the resistance which has to be overcome by the sails and

[052-108] by increasing the speed of the vessel, enables the sails to act with

[052-109] greater efficiency, as the wind will not rebound from them with as great a

[052-110] velocity as it would otherwise do, and a larger proportion of the power of

[052-111] the wind will also be used up. In the case of beam winds, moreover, the

[052-112] action of the screw, by the larger advance it gives to the vessel will

[052-113] enable the sails to intercept a larger column of wind in a given time. It

[052-114] appears, therefore, that the sails add to the efficiency of the screw, and

[052-115] that the screw also adds to the efficiency of the sails.

[052-116] 611. _Q._--What is the comparative cost of transporting merchandise in

[052-117] paddle steamers of full power, in screw steamers of auxiliary power, and in

[052-118] sailing ships?

[052-119] _A._--That will depend very much upon the locality where the comparison is

[052-120] made. In the case of vessels performing distant ocean voyages, in which

[052-121] they may reckon upon the aid of uniform and constant winds, such as the

[052-122] trade winds or the monsoon, sailing ships of large size will be able to

[052-123] carry more cheaply than any other species of vessel. But where the winds

[052-124] are irregular and there is not much sea room, or for such circumstances as

[052-125] exist in the Channel or Mediterranean trades, screw vessels with auxiliary

[052-126] power will constitute the cheapest instrument of conveyance.

[052-127] 612. _Q._--Are there any facts recorded illustrative of the accuracy of

[052-128] this conclusion?

[052-129] _A._--A full paddle vessel of 1000 tons burden and 350 horses power, will

[052-130] carry about 400 tons of cargo, besides coal for a voyage of 500 miles, and

[052-131] the expense of such a voyage, including wear and tear, depreciation, &c.,

[052-132] will be about 190_l_. The duration of the voyage will be about 45-1/2

[052-133] hours. A screw vessel of 400 tons burden and 100 horses power, will carry

[052-134] the same amount of cargo, besides her coals, on the same voyage, and the

[052-135] expense of the voyage, including wear and tear, depreciation, &c., will be

[052-136] not much more than 60_l_. An auxiliary screw vessel, therefore, can carry

[052-137] merchandise at one third of the cost of a full-powered paddle vessel. By

[052-138] similar comparisons made between the expense of conveying merchandise in

[052-139] auxiliary screw steamers and sailing ships on coasting voyages, it appears

[052-140] that the cost in screw steamers is about one third less than in the sailing

[052-141] ships; the greater expedition of the screw steamers much more than

[052-142] compensating for the expense which the maintenance of the machinery

[052-143] involves.

[052-144] SCREW AND PADDLES COMBINED.

[052-145] 613. _Q._--Would not a screw combined with paddles act in a similarly

[052-146] advantageous way as a screw or paddles when aided by the wind?

[052-147] _A._--If in any given paddle vessel a supplementary screw be added to

[052-148] increase her power and speed, the screw will act in a more beneficial

[052-149] manner than if it had the whole vessel to propel itself, and for a like

[052-150] reason the paddles will act in a more beneficial manner. There will be less

[052-151] slip both upon the paddles and upon the screw than if either had been

[052-152] employed alone; but the same object would be attained by giving the vessel

[052-153] larger paddles or a larger screw.

[052-154] 614. _Q._--Have any vessels been constructed with combined screw and

[052-155] paddles?

[052-156] _A._--Not any that I know of, except the great vessel built under the

[052-157] direction of Mr. Brunel. The Bee many years since was fitted with both

[052-158] screw and paddles, but this was for the purpose of ascertaining the

[052-159] relative efficiency of the two modes of propulsion, and not for the purpose

[052-160] of using both together.

[052-161] 615. _Q._--What would be the best means of accelerating the speed of a

[052-162] paddle vessel by the introduction of a supplementary screw?

[052-163] _A._--If the vessel requires new boilers, the best course of procedure

[052-164] would be to work a single engine giving motion to the screw with high

[052-165] pressure steam, and to let the waste steam from the high pressure engine

[052-166] work the paddle engines. In this way the power might be doubled without any

[052-167] increased expenditure of fuel per hour, and there would be a diminished

[052-168] expenditure per voyage in the proportion of the increased speed.

[052-169] 616. _Q._--What would the increased speed be by doubling the power?

[052-170] _A._--The increase would be in the proportion of the cube root of 1 to the

[052-171] cube root of 2, or it would be 1.25 times greater. If, therefore, the

[052-172] existing speed were 10 miles, it would be increased to 12-1/2 miles by

[052-173] doubling the power, and the vessel would ply with about a fourth less coals

[052-174] by increasing the power in the manner suggested.

[052-175] 617. _Q._--Is not high pressure steam dangerous in steam vessels?

[052-176] _A._--Not necessarily so, and it has now been introduced into a good number

[052-177] of steam vessels with satisfactory results. In the case of locomotive

[052-178] engines, where it is used so widely, very few accidents have occurred; and

[052-179] in steam vessels the only additional source of danger is the salting of the

[052-180] boiler. This may be prevented either by the use of fresh water in the

[052-181] boiler, or by practising a larger amount of blowing off, to insure which it

[052-182] should be impossible to diminish the amount of water sent into the boiler

[052-183] by the feed pump, and the excess should be discharged overboard through a

[052-184] valve near the water level of the boiler, which valve is governed by a

[052-185] float that will rise or fall with the fluctuating level of the water. If

[052-186] the float be a copper ball, a little water should be introduced into it

[052-187] before it is soldered or brazed up, which will insure an equality of

[052-188] pressure within and without the ball, and a leakage of water into it will

[052-189] then be less likely to take place. A stone float, however, is cheaper, and

[052-190] if properly balanced will be equally effective. All steam vessels should

[052-191] have a large excess of boiling feed water constantly flowing into the

[052-192] boiler, and a large quantity of water constantly blowing off through the

[052-193] surface valves, which being governed by floats will open and let the

[052-194] superfluous water escape whenever the water level rises too high. In this

[052-195] way the boiler will be kept from salting, and priming will be much less

[052-196] likely to occur. The great problem of steam navigation is the economy of

[052-197] fuel, since the quantity of fuel consumed by a vessel will very much

[052-198] determine whether she is profitable or otherwise. Notwithstanding the

[052-199] momentous nature of this condition, however, the consumption of fuel in

[052-200] steam vessels is a point to which very little attention has been paid, and

[052-201] no efficient means have yet been adopted in steam vessels to insure that

[052-202] measure of economy which is known to be attainable, and which has been

[052-203] attained already in other departments of engineering in which the benefits

[052-204] of such economy are of less weighty import. It needs nothing more than the

[052-205] establishment of an efficient system of registration in steam vessels, to

[052-206] insure a large and rapid economy in the consumption of fuel, as this

[052-207] quality would then become the test of an engineer's proficiency, and would

[052-208] determine the measure of his fame. In the case of the Cornish engines, a

[052-209] saving of more than half the fuel was speedily effected by the introduction

[052-210] of the simple expedient of registration. In agricultural engines a like

[052-211] economy has speedily followed from a like arrangement; yet in both of these

[052-212] cases the benefits of a large saving are less eminent than they would be in

[052-213] the case of steam navigation; and it is to be hoped that this expedient of

[052-214] improvement will now be speedily adopted.
