The **blow through valve** is a small valve in a steam engine used during the
starting procedure to admit steam from the boiler into the engine — both above
and below the piston, and into the condenser and air pump — in order to expel
air and accumulated water from the interior of the engine before commencing
normal operation.

## Starting Procedure

When the engineer wishes to start the engine, the blow through valve is first
opened. Steam from the boiler enters the engine on both sides of the piston and
fills the condenser and air pump, driving out air and any water that may have
accumulated. Once this purging is complete, the blow through valve is shut, and
a vacuum soon forms within the engine through the condensation of the steam.
The slide valve can then be moved by hand to admit steam to one side of the
piston, initiating motion. (010-030) After one or two manual strokes, the valve
gear connection to the eccentric is restored for automatic operation. (010-034)

## Safety Function

In the event of safety valve failure — indicated by the steam gauge — the blow
through valves of the engine and the blow off cocks of the boiler should be
opened at once, and the fires raked out to prevent overpressure. (025-113)
