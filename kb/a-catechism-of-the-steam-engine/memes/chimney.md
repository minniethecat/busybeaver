The **chimney** (also called a smokestack or funnel in marine contexts) is the vertical conduit through which the products of combustion from a [[boiler]] furnace escape to the atmosphere. In a [[steam]] engine installation, the chimney serves two essential functions: it carries away smoke and hot gases, and — more importantly — it creates the draft or current of air that draws fresh combustion air through the [[furnace]] [[grate|fire bars]] and [[flue|flues]].

## Principle of the chimney draft

The chimney produces a draft by the difference in density between the column of hot gas within it and the surrounding column of cooler atmospheric air:

> [011-025] The force of the draught in the chimney, whereby a smaller fire grate suffices for the combustion of the fuel, is due to the difference in specific gravity between the column of hot air within the chimney and a corresponding column of cold atmospheric air.

The taller the chimney, the greater the weight difference between the two columns, and the stronger the draft. This is why marine boilers — which could only have short funnels — often required the assistance of the [[blast-pipe]] to create adequate draft:

> [011-098] In locomotives, the steam, after performing its duty in the cylinders, is always discharged into the chimney through a blast pipe, to increase the intensity of the draught. Without this expedient, the chimney of a locomotive would require to be made of a very inconvenient height to make them effective.

## Construction

The chimney is typically constructed of riveted iron plate. In locomotives it rises directly from the smoke box at the front of the boiler:

> [008-034] The front of the smoke box opens into the chimney. These boilers, with their internal tubes, have greatly less weight and bulk than the flue boilers of the same power.

In [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s the funnel (as the chimney is called at sea) is often made telescopic or hinged so it can be lowered when the vessel passes under bridges. In stationary land engines the chimney may be built of brick and can be of considerable height — factory chimneys of the mid-19th century commonly reached 200 feet or more.

## Damper control

The chimney draft is regulated by a [[damper]] — a pivoted metal plate placed in the chimney or uptake:

> [007-145] A plate of metal or a damper, as it is called, which slides up and down in the flue, and this damper is closed more or less when the pressure of the steam is too high, to check the draught and moderate the fire.

> [058-109] There is a balanced damper, c, in the chimney. The weight, d, is attached to a lever, e, which is moved by a rod connected with a piston in a small cylinder.

## Chimney proportions

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] gives precise proportions for the chimney area relative to the [[grate|fire grate]] area:

> [020-116] At the point where it enters the chimney, 1/11th of the area of the fire grate bars should be taken as the area of the chimney.

> [022-029] 118 square inches sectional area of chimney, 14 square inches area over fire grate.

The chimney area narrows toward the top because the gases cool and contract as they ascend:

> [020-108] It is also expedient to make the area of the chimney less at the top than at the bottom, in the proportion of about 1 to 2, as the gases cool in their ascent and contract in volume.
