[050-001] 573. _Q._--But perhaps under such circumstances the speed of the screw

[050-002] vessel will be the greater of the two?

[050-003] _A._--No; the speed of the two vessels will be the same, unless the

[050-004] strength of the head wind be so great as to bring the vessels nearly to a

[050-005] state of rest, and on that supposition the screw vessel will have the

[050-006] advantage. Such cases occur very rarely in practice; and in the case of the

[050-007] ordinary resistances imposed by head winds, the speed of the screw and

[050-008] paddle vessel will be the same, but the screw vessel will consume most

[050-009] coals.

[050-010] 574. _Q._--What is the cause of this peculiarity?

[050-011] _A._--The cause is, that when the screw is so proportioned in its length as

[050-012] to be most suitable for propelling vessels in calms, it is too short to be

[050-013] suitable for propelling vessels which encounter a very heavy resistance. It

[050-014] follows, therefore, that if it is prevented from pursuing its spiral course

[050-015] in the water, it will displace the water to a certain extent laterally, in

[050-016] the manner it does if the engine be set on when the vessel is at anchor;

[050-017] and a part of the engine power is thus wasted in producing a useless

[050-018] disturbance of the water, which in paddle vessels is not expended at all.

[050-019] 575. _Q._--If a screw and paddle vessel of the same mould and power be tied

[050-020] stern to stern, will not the screw vessel preponderate and tow the paddle

[050-021] vessel astern against the whole force of her engines?

[050-022] _A._--Yes, that will be so.

[050-023] 576. _Q._--And seeing that the vessels are of the same mould and power, so

[050-024] that neither can derive an advantage from a variation in that condition,

[050-025] does not the preponderance of the screw vessel show that the screw must be

[050-026] the most powerful propeller?

[050-027] _A._---No, it does not.

[050-028] 577. _Q._--Seeing that the vessels are the same in all respects except as

[050-029] regards the propellers, and that one of them exhibits a superiority, does

[050-030] not this circumstance show that one propeller must be more powerful than

[050-031] the other?

[050-032] _A._--That does not follow necessarily, nor is it the fact in this

[050-033] particular case. All steam vessels when set into motion, will force

[050-034] themselves forward with an amount of thrust which, setting aside the loss

[050-035] from friction and from other causes, will just balance the pressure on the

[050-036] pistons. In a paddle vessel, as has already been explained, it is easy to

[050-037] tell the tractive force exerted at the centre of pressure of the paddle

[050-038] wheels, when the pressure urging the pistons, the dimensions of the wheels

[050-039] and the speed of the vessel are known; and that force, whatever be its

[050-040] amount, must always continue the same with any constant pressure on the

[050-041] pistons. In a screw vessel the same law applies, so that with any given

[050-042] pressure on the pistons and discarding the consideration of friction, it

[050-043] will follow that whatever be the thrust exerted by a paddle or a screw

[050-044] vessel, it must remain uniform whether the vessel is in motion or at rest,

[050-045] and whether moving at a high or a low velocity through the water. Now to

[050-046] achieve an equal speed during calms in two vessels of the same model, there

[050-047] must be the same amount of propelling thrust in each; and this thrust,

[050-048] whatever be its amount, cannot afterward vary if a uniform pressure of

[050-049] steam be maintained. The thrusts, therefore, caused by their respective

[050-050] propelling instruments, when a screw and paddle vessel are tied stern to

[050-051] stern, must be the same as at other times; and as at other times those

[050-052] thrusts are equal, so must they be when the vessels are set in the

[050-053] antagonism supposed.

[050-054] 578. _Q._--How comes it then that the screw vessel preponderates?

[050-055] _A._--Not by virtue of a larger thrust exerted by the screw in pressing

[050-056] forward the shaft and with it the vessel, but by the gravitation against

[050-057] the stern of the wave of water which the screw raises by its rapid

[050-058] rotation. This wave will only be raised very high when the progress of the

[050-059] vessel through the water is nearly arrested, at which time the centrifugal

[050-060] action of the screw is very great; and the vessel under such circumstances

[050-061] is forced forward partly by the thrust of the screw, and partly by the

[050-062] hydrostatic pressure of the protuberance of water which the centrifugal

[050-063] action of the screw raises up at the stern.

[050-064] 579. _Q._--Can you state any facts in corroboration of this view?

[050-065] _A._--The screw vessel will not preponderate if a screw and paddle vessel

[050-066] be tied bow to bow and the engines of each be then reversed. In, some screw

[050-067] vessels the amount of thrust actually exerted by the screw under all its

[050-068] varying circumstances, has been ascertained by the application of a

[050-069] dynamometer to the end of the shaft. By this instrument--which is formed by

[050-070] a combination of levers like a weighing machine for carts--a thrust or

[050-071] pressure of several tons can be measured by the application of a small

[050-072] weight; and it has been found, by repeated experiment with the dynamometer,

[050-073] that the thrust of the screw in a screw vessel when towing a paddle vessel

[050-074] against the whole force of her engines, is just the same as it is when the

[050-075] two vessels are maintaining an equal speed in calms. The preponderance of

[050-076] the screw vessel must, therefore, be imputable to some other agency than to

[050-077] a superior thrust of the screw, which is found by experiment not to exist.

[050-078] 580. _Q._--Has the dynamometer been applied to paddle vessels?

[050-079] _A._--It has not been applied to the vessels themselves, as in the case of

[050-080] screw vessels, but it has been employed on shore to ascertain the amount of

[050-081] tractive force that a paddle vessel can exert on a rope.

[050-082] 581. _Q._--Have any experiments been made to determine the comparative

[050-083] performances of screw and paddle vessels at sea?

[050-084] _A._--Yes, numerous experiments; of which the best known are probably those

[050-085] made on the screw steamer Rattler and the paddle steamer Alecto, each

[050-086] vessel of the same model, size, and power,--each vessel being of about 800

[050-087] tons burden and 200 horses power. Subsequently another set of experiments

[050-088] with the same object was made with the Niger screw steamer and the Basilisk

[050-089] paddle steamer, both vessels being of about 1000 tons burden and 400 horses

[050-090] power. The general results which were obtained in the course of these

[050-091] experiments are those which have been already recited.

[050-092] 582. _Q._--Will you recapitulate some of the main incidents of these

[050-093] trials?

[050-094] _A._--I may first state some of the chief dimensions of the vessels. The

[050-095] Rattler is 176 feet 6 inches long, 32 feet 8-1/2 inches broad, 888 tons

[050-096] burden, 200 horses power, and has an area of immersed midship section of

[050-097] 380 square feet at a draught of water of 11 feet 5-1/2 inches. The Alecto

[050-098] is of the same dimensions in every respect, except that she is only of 800

[050-099] tons burden, the difference in this particular being wholly owing to the

[050-100] Rattler having been drawn out about 15 feet at the stern, to leave abundant

[050-101] room for the application of the screw. The Rattler was fitted with a

[050-102] dynamometer, which enabled the actual propelling thrust of the screw shaft

[050-103] to be measured; and the amount of this thrust, multiplied by the distance

[050-104] through which the vessel passed in a given time, would determine the amount

[050-105] of power actually utilized in propelling the ship. Both vessels were fitted

[050-106] with indicators applied to the cylinders, so as to determine the amount of

[050-107] power exerted by the engines.

[050-108] 583. _Q._--How many trials of the vessels were made on this occasion?

[050-109] _A._--Twelve trials in all; but I need not refer to those in which similar

[050-110] or identical results were only repeated. The first trial was made under

[050-111] steam only, the weather was calm and the water smooth. At 54 minutes past 4

[050-112] in the morning both vessels left the Nore, and at 30-1/2 minutes past 2 the

[050-113] Rattler stopped her engines in Yarmouth Roads, where in 20-1/2 minutes

[050-114] afterward she was joined by the Alecto. The mean speed achieved by the

[050-115] Rattler during this trial was 9.2 knots per hour; the mean speed of the

[050-116] Alecto was 8.8 knots per hour. The slip of the screw was 10.2 per cent. The

[050-117] actual power exerted by the engines, as shown by the indicator, was in the

[050-118] case of the Rattler 334.6 horses, and in the case of the Alecto 281.2

[050-119] horses; being a difference of 53.4 horses in favor of the Rattler. The

[050-120] forward thrust upon the screw shaft was 3 tons, 17 cwt., 3 qrs., and 14

[050-121] lbs. The horse power of the shaft--or power actually utilized--ascertained

[050-122] by multiplying the thrust in pounds by the space passed through by the

[050-123] vessel in feet per minute, and dividing by 33,000, was 247.8 horses power.

[050-124] This makes the ratio of the shaft to the engine power as 1 to 1.3, or, in

[050-125] other words, it shows that the amount of engine power utilized in

[050-126] propulsion was 77 per cent. In a subsequent trial made with the vessels

[050-127] running before the wind, but with no sails set and the masts struck, the

[050-128] speed realized by the Rattler was 10 knots per hour. The slip of the screw

[050-129] was 11.2 per cent. The actual power exerted by the engines of the Rattler

[050-130] was 368.8 horses. The actual power exerted by the engines of the Alecto was

[050-131] 291.7 horses. The thrust of the shaft was equal to a weight of 4 tons, 4

[050-132] cwt., 1 qr., 1 lb. The horse power of the shaft was 290.2 horses, and the

[050-133] ratio of the shaft to the engine power was 1 to 1.2. Here, therefore, the

[050-134] amount of the engine power utilized was 84 per cent.

[050-135] 584. _Q._--If in any screw vessel the power of the engine be diminished by

[050-136] shutting off the steam or otherwise, you will then have a larger screw

[050-137] relatively with the power of the engine than before?

[050-138] _A._--Yes.

[050-139] 585. _Q._--Was any experiment made to ascertain the effect of this

[050-140] modification?

[050-141] _A._--There was; but the result was not found to be better than before. The

[050-142] experiment was made by shutting off the steam from the engines of the

[050-143] Rattler until the number of strokes was reduced to 17 in the minute. The

[050-144] actual power was then 126.7 horses; thrust upon the shaft 2 tons, 2 cwt., 3

[050-145] qrs., 14 lbs; horse power of shaft 88.4 horses; ratio of shaft to engine

[050-146] power 1 to 1.4; slip of the screw 18.7 per cent. In this experiment the

[050-147] power utilized was 71 per cent.

[050-148] 586. _Q._--Was any experiment made to determine the relative performances

[050-149] in head winds?

[050-150] _A._--The trial in which this relation was best determined lasted for seven

[050-151] hours, and was made against a strong head wind and heavy head sea. The

[050-152] speed of the Rattler by patent log was 4.2 knots; and at the conclusion of

[050-153] the trial the Alecto had the advantage by about half a mile. Owing to an

[050-154] accidental injury to the indicator, the power exerted by the engines of the

[050-155] Rattler in this trial could not be ascertained; but judging from the power

[050-156] exerted in other experiments with the same number of revolutions, it

[050-157] appears probable that the power actually exerted by the Rattler was about

[050-158] 300 horses. The number of strokes per minute made by the engines of the

[050-159] Rattler was 22, whereas in the Alecto the number of strokes per minute was

[050-160] only 12; so that while the engines of the Alecto were reduced, by the

[050-161] resistance occasioned by a strong head wind, to nearly half their usual

[050-162] speed, the engines of the Rattler were only lessened about one twelfth of

[050-163] their usual speed. The mean thrust upon the screw shaft during this

[050-164] experiment, was 4 tons, 7 cwt., 0 qr., 16 lbs. The horse power of the shaft

[050-165] was 125.9 horses, and the slip of the screw was 56 per cent. Taking the

[050-166] power actually exerted by the Rattler at 300 horses, the power utilized in

[050-167] this experiment is only 42 per cent.
