[006-001] 65. _Q._--What is the cohesive strength of steel?

[006-002] _A._--The ultimate tensile strength of good cast or blistered steel is

[006-003] about twice as great as that of wrought iron, being about 130,000 lbs. per

[006-004] square inch of section. The tensile strength of gun metal, such as is used

[006-005] in engines, is about 36,000 lbs. per square inch of section; of wrought

[006-006] copper about 33,000 lbs.; and of cast copper about 19,000 lbs. per square

[006-007] Inch of section.

[006-008] 66. _Q._--Is the crushing strength of steel greater or less than its

[006-009] tensile strength?

[006-010] _A._--It is about twice greater. A good steel punch will punch through a

[006-011] plate of wrought iron of a thickness equal to the diameter of the punch. A

[006-012] punch therefore of an inch diameter will pierce a plate an inch thick. Now

[006-013] it is well known, that the strain required to punch a piece of metal out of

[006-014] a plate, is just the same as that required to tear asunder a bar of iron of

[006-015] the same area of cross section as the area of the surface cut. The area of

[006-016] the surface cut in this case will be the circumference of the punch, 3.1416

[006-017] inches, multiplied by the thickness of the plate, 1 inch, which makes the

[006-018] area of the cut surface 3.1416 square inches. The area of the point of the

[006-019] punch subjected to the pressure is .7854 square inches, so that the area

[006-020] cut to the area crushed is as four to one. In other words, it will require

[006-021] four times the strain to crush steel that is required to tear asunder

[006-022] malleable iron, or it will take about twice the strain to crush steel that

[006-023] it will require to break it by extension.

[006-024] 67. _Q._--What strain may be applied to malleable iron in practice?

[006-025] _A._--A bar of wrought iron to which a tensile or compressing strain is

[006-026] applied, is elongated or contracted like a very stiff spiral spring, nearly

[006-027] in the proportion of the amount of strain applied up to the limit at which

[006-028] the strength begins to give way, and within this limit it will recover its

[006-029] original dimensions when the strain is removed. If, however, the strain be

[006-030] carried beyond this limit, the bar will not recover its original

[006-031] dimensions, but will be permanently pulled out or pushed in, just as would

[006-032] happen to a spring to which an undue strain had been applied. This limit is

[006-033] what is called the limit of elasticity; and whenever it is exceeded, the

[006-034] bar, though it may not break immediately, will undergo a progressive

[006-035] deterioration, and will break in the course of time. The limit of

[006-036] elasticity of malleable iron when extended, or, in other words, the tensile

[006-037] strain to which a bar of malleable iron an inch square may be subjected

[006-038] without permanently deranging its structure, is usually taken at 17,800

[006-039] lbs., or from that to 10 tons, depending on the quality of the iron. It has

[006-040] also been found that malleable iron is extended about one ten-thousandth

[006-041] part of its length for every ton of direct strain applied to it.

[006-042] 68. _Q._--What is the limit of elasticity of cast iron?

[006-043] _A._--It is commonly taken at 15,300 lbs. per square inch of section; but

[006-044] this is certainly much too high, as it exceeds the tensile strength of

[006-045] irons of medium quality. A bar of cast iron if compressed by weights will

[006-046] be contracted in length twice as much as a bar of malleable iron under

[006-047] similar circumstances; but malleable iron, when subjected to a greater

[006-048] strain than 12 tons per square inch of section, gradually crumples up by

[006-049] the mere continuance of the weight. A cast-iron bar one inch square and ten

[006-050] feet long, is shortened about one tenth of an inch by a compressing force

[006-051] of 10,000 lbs., whereas a malleable iron bar of the same dimensions would

[006-052] require to shorten it equally a compressing force of 20,000 lbs. As the

[006-053] load, however, approaches 12 tons, the compressions become nearly equal,

[006-054] and above that point the rate of the compression of the malleable iron

[006-055] rapidly increases. A bar of cast iron, when at its breaking point by the

[006-056] application of a tensile strain, is stretched about one six-hundredth part

[006-057] of its length; and an equal strain employed to compress it, would shorten

[006-058] it about one eight-hundredth part of its length.

[006-059] 69. _Q._--But to what strain may the iron used in the construction of

[006-060] engines be safely subjected?

[006-061] _A._--The most of the working parts of modern engines are made of malleable

[006-062] iron, and the utmost strain to which wrought iron should be subjected in

[006-063] machinery is 4000 lbs. per square inch of section. Cast iron should not be

[006-064] subjected to more than half of this. In locomotive boilers the strain of

[006-065] 4000 lbs. per square inch of section is sometimes exceeded by nearly one

[006-066] half; but such an excess of strain approaches the limits of danger.

[006-067] 70. _Q._--Will you explain in what way the various strains subsisting in a

[006-068] steam engine may be resolved into tensile and crushing strains; also in

[006-069] what way the magnitude of those strains may be determined?

[006-070] _A._--To take the case of a beam subjected to a transverse strain, such as

[006-071] the great beam of an engine, it is clear, if we suppose the beam broken

[006-072] through the middle, that the amount of strain at the upper and lower edges

[006-073] of the beam, where the whole strain may be supposed to be collected, will,

[006-074] with any given pressure on the piston, depend upon the proportion of the

[006-075] length to the depth of the beam. One edge of the beam breaks by extension,

[006-076] and the other edge by compression; and the upper and lower edges may be

[006-077] regarded as pillars, one of which is extended by the strain, and the other

[006-078] is compressed. If, to make an extreme supposition, the depth of the beam is

[006-079] taken as equal to its length, then the pillars answering to the edges of

[006-080] the beam will be compressed, and extended by what is virtually a bellcrank

[006-081] lever with equal arms; the horizontal distance from the main centre to the

[006-082] end of the beam being one of the arms, and the vertical height from the

[006-083] main centre to the top edge of the beam being the other arm. The distance,

[006-084] therefore, passed through by the fractured edge of the beam during a stroke

[006-085] of the engine, will be equal to the length of the stroke; and the strain it

[006-086] will have to sustain will consequently be equal to the pressure on the

[006-087] piston. If its motion were only half that of the piston, as would be the

[006-088] case if its depth were made one half less, the strain the beam would have

[006-089] to bear would be twice as great; and it may be set down as an axiom, that

[006-090] the strain upon any part of a steam engine or other machine is inversely

[006-091] equal to the strain produced by the prime mover, multiplied by the

[006-092] comparative velocity with which the part in question moves. If any part of

[006-093] an engine moves with a less velocity than the piston, it will have a

[006-094] greater strain on it, if resisted, than is thrown upon the piston. If it

[006-095] moves with a greater velocity than the piston, it will have a less strain

[006-096] upon it, and the difference of strain will in every case be in the inverse

[006-097] proportion of the difference of the velocity.

[006-098] 71. _Q._--Then, in computing the amount of metal necessary to give due

[006-099] strength to a beam, the first point is to determine the velocity with which

[006-100] the edge of the beam moves at that point were the strain is greatest?

[006-101] _A._--The web of a cast-iron beam or girder serves merely to connect the

[006-102] upper and lower edges or flanges rigidly together, so as to enable the

[006-103] extending and compressing strains to be counteracted in an effectual manner

[006-104] by the metal of those flanges. It is only necessary, therefore, to make the

[006-105] flanges of sufficient strength to resist effectually the crushing and

[006-106] tensile strains to which they are exposed, and to make the web of the beam

[006-107] of sufficient strength to prevent a distortion of its shape from taking

[006-108] place.

[006-109] 72. _Q._--Is the strain greater from being movable or intermittent than if

[006-110] it was stationary?

[006-111] _A._--Yes it is nearly twice as great from being movable. Engineers are in

[006-112] the habit of making girders intended to sustain a stationary load, about

[006-113] three times stronger than the breaking weight; but if the load be a movable

[006-114] one, as is the case in the girders of railway bridges, they make the

[006-115] strength equal to six times the breaking weight.

[006-116] 73. _Q._--Then the strain is increased by the suddenness with which it is

[006-117] applied?

[006-118] _A._--If a weight be placed on a long and slender beam propped up in the

[006-119] middle, and the prop be suddenly withdrawn, so as to allow deflection to

[006-120] take place, it is clear that the deflection must be greater than if the

[006-121] load had been gradually applied. The momentum of the weight and also of the

[006-122] beam itself falling through the space through which it has been deflected,

[006-123] has necessarily to be counteracted by the elasticity of the beam; and the

[006-124] beam will, therefore, be momentarily bent to a greater extent than what is

[006-125] due to the load, and after a few vibrations up and down it will finally

[006-126] settle at that point of deflection which the load properly occasions. It is

[006-127] obvious that a beam must be strong enough, not merely to sustain the

[006-128] pressure due to the load, but also that accession of pressure due to the

[006-129] counteracted momentum of the weight and of the beam itself. Although in

[006-130] steam engines the beam is not loaded by a weight, but by the pressure of

[006-131] the steam, yet the momentum of the beam itself must in every case be

[006-132] counteracted, and the momentum will be considerable in every case in which

[006-133] a large and rapid deflection takes place. A rapid deflection increases the

[006-134] amount of the deflection as well as the amount of the strain, as is seen in

[006-135] the cylinder cover of a Cornish pumping engine, into which the steam is

[006-136] suddenly admitted, and in which the momentum of the particles of the metal

[006-137] put into motion increases the deflection to an extent such as the mere

[006-138] pressure of the steam could not produce.

[006-139] 74. _Q._--What will be the amount of increased strain consequent upon

[006-140] deflection?

[006-141] _A._--The momentum of any moving body being proportional to the square of

[006-142] its velocity, it follows that the strain will be proportional to the square

[006-143] of the amount of deflection produced in a specified time.

[006-144] 75. _Q._--But will not the inertia of a beam resist deflection, as well as

[006-145] the momentum increase deflection?

[006-146] _A._--No doubt that will be so; but whether in practical cases increase of

[006-147] mass without reference to strength or load will, upon the whole, increase

[006-148] or diminish deflection, will depend very much upon the magnitude of the

[006-149] mass relatively with the magnitude of the deflecting pressure, and the

[006-150] rapidity with which that pressure is applied and removed. Thus if a force

[006-151] or weight be very suddenly applied to the middle of a ponderous beam, and

[006-152] be as suddenly withdrawn, the inertia of the beam will, as in the case of

[006-153] the collision of bodies, tend to resist the force, and thus obviate

[006-154] deflection to a considerable extent; but if the pressure be so long

[006-155] continued as to produce the amount of deflection due to the pressure, the

[006-156] effect of the inertia in that case will be to increase the deflection.

[006-157] 76. _Q._--Will the pressure given to the beam of an engine in different

[006-158] directions facilitate its fracture?

[006-159] _A._--Iron beams bent alternately in opposite directions, or alternately

[006-160] deflected and released, will be broken in the course of time with a much

[006-161] less strain than is necessary to produce immediate fracture. It has been

[006-162] found, experimentally, that a cast-iron bar, deflected by a revolving cam

[006-163] to only half the extent due to its breaking weight, will in no case

[006-164] withstand 900 successive deflections; but, if bent by the cam to only one

[006-165] third of its ultimate deflection, it will withstand 100,000 deflections

[006-166] without visible injury. Looking, however, to the jolts and vibrations to

[006-167] which engines are subject, and the sudden strains sometimes thrown upon

[006-168] them, either from water getting into the cylinder or otherwise, it does not

[006-169] appear that a strength answering to six times the breaking weight will give

[006-170] sufficient margin for safety in the case of cast-iron beams.
