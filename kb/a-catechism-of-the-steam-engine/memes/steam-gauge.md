The **steam gauge** is an instrument that measures and displays the pressure of [[steam]] within a [[boiler]]. It is an essential piece of engine-room instrumentation, enabling the engineer to monitor boiler conditions and detect unsafe pressure build-up before the [[safety-valve|safety valve]] lifts. By the mid-nineteenth century, several forms of steam gauge had been developed.

## Types and construction

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes a recently introduced design that used thermal expansion rather than direct mechanical measurement:

> [018-113] A form of steam gauge like a thermometer, with a flattened bulb; and the pressure of the steam, by compressing the bulb, causes the mercury to rise to a point proportional to the pressure applied.

This type operated on the principle that steam pressure compresses a hollow bulb, displacing mercury in a connected tube — a direct-reading instrument that required no gears or levers.

## Safety function

The steam gauge serves a critical safety role by providing continuous visibility of boiler pressure:

> [025-098] The application of a steam gauge to each boiler, which will make the existence of any undue pressure in any of the boilers immediately visible.

If a [[safety-valve|safety valve]] sticks or becomes inoperative — a not uncommon occurrence — the steam gauge provides the first indication of danger:

> [025-112] The inaction of the safety valves is at once indicated by the steam gauge, and when discovered, the blow through valves of the engine and blow off cocks of the boiler should at once be opened, and the fires raked out.

## Differential gauge

For comparing pressures between different parts of the engine, a differential steam gauge was sometimes employed:

> [032-140] The satisfactory principle would be that of a differential steam gauge.

A differential gauge measures the pressure difference between two points — for example, between the boiler and the steam chest, or between the two sides of a piston — rather than the absolute pressure relative to atmosphere.

## Operational importance

The steam gauge, together with the [[vacuum-gauge]] (for [[condensation|condenser]] vacuum) and the water level gauge, formed the essential instrumentation suite of any well-equipped engine room. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] recommends that every boiler be fitted with its own independent gauge, and that the gauge connection should be independent of any stop valve, so that an inadvertently closed valve cannot isolate the gauge from the pressure it is meant to monitor.
