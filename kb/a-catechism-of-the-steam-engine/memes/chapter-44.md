[044-001] 505. _Q._--What is the difference between inside and outside cylinder

[044-002] engines?

[044-003] _A._--Outside cylinders are so designated when placed upon the outside of

[044-004] the framing, with their connecting rods operating upon pins in the driving

[044-005] wheels; while the inside cylinders are situated within the framing, and the

[044-006] connecting rods attach themselves to cranks in the driving axle.

[044-007] 506. _Q._--Whether are inside or outside cylinder engines to be preferred?

[044-008] _A._--A diversity of opinion obtains as to the relative merits of outside

[044-009] and inside cylinders. The chief objection to outside cylinders is, that

[044-010] they occasion a sinuous motion in the engine which is apt to send the train

[044-011] off the rails; but this action may be made less perceptible or be remedied

[044-012] altogether, by placing a weight upon one side of the wheels, the momentum

[044-013] of which will just balance the momentum of the piston and its connections.

[044-014] The sinuous or rocking motion of locomotives is traceable to the arrested

[044-015] momentum of the piston and its attachments at every stroke of the engine,

[044-016] and the effect of the pressure thus created will be more operative in

[044-017] inducing oscillation the farther it is exerted from the central line of the

[044-018] engine. If both cylinders were set at right angles in the centre of the

[044-019] carriage, and the pistons were both attached to a central crank, there

[044-020] would be no oscillation produced; or the same effect would be realized by

[044-021] placing one cylinder in the centre of the carriage, and two at the sides--

[044-022] the pistons of the side cylinders moving simultaneously: but it is

[044-023] impossible to couple the piston of an upright cylinder direct to the axle

[044-024] of a locomotive, without causing the springs to work up and down with every

[044-025] stroke of the engine: and the use of three cylinders, though adopted in

[044-026] some of Stephenson's engines, involves too much complication to be a

[044-027] beneficial innovation.

[044-028] 507. _Q._--Whether are four-wheeled or six-wheeled engines preferable?

[044-029] _A._--Much controversial ingenuity has been expended upon the question of

[044-030] the relative merits of the four and six-wheeled engines; one party

[044-031] maintaining that four-wheeled engines are most unsafe, and the other that

[044-032] six-wheeled engines are unmechanical, and are more likely to occasion

[044-033] accidents. The four-wheeled engines, however, appear to have been charged

[044-034] with faults that do not really attach to them when properly constructed;

[044-035] for it by no means follows that if the axle of a four-wheeled engine

[044-036] breaks, or even altogether comes away, that the engine must fall down or

[044-037] run off the line; inasmuch as, if the engine be properly coupled with the

[044-038] tender, it has the tender to sustain it. It is obvious enough, that such a

[044-039] connection may be made between the tender and the engine, that either the

[044-040] fore or hind axle of the engine may be taken away, and yet the engine will

[044-041] not fall down, but will be kept up by the support which the tender affords;

[044-042] and the arguments hitherto paraded against the four-wheeled engines are, so

[044-043] far as regards the question of safety, nothing more than arguments against

[044-044] the existence of the suggested connection. It is no doubt the fact, that

[044-045] locomotive engines are now becoming too heavy to be capable of being borne

[044-046] on four wheels at high speeds without injury to the rails; but the

[044-047] objection of damage to the rails applies with at least equal force to most

[044-048] of the six-wheeled engines hitherto constructed, as in those engines the

[044-049] engineer has the power of putting nearly all the weight upon the driving

[044-050] wheels; and if the rail be wet or greasy, there is a great temptation to

[044-051] increase the bite of those wheels by screwing them down more firmly upon

[044-052] the rails. A greater strain is thus thrown upon the rail than can exist in

[044-053] the case of any equally heavy four-wheeled engine; and the engine is made

[044-054] very unsafe, as a pitching motion will inevitably be induced at high

[044-055] speeds, when an engine is thus poised upon the central driving wheels, and

[044-056] there will also be more of the rocking or sinuous motion. Locomotives,

[044-057] however, intended to achieve high speeds or to draw heavy loads, are now

[044-058] generally made with eight wheels, and in some cases the driving wheels are

[044-059] placed at the end of the engine instead of in the middle.

[044-060] 508. _Q._--As the question of the locomotive boiler has been already

[044-061] disposed of in discussing the question of boilers in general, it now only

[044-062] remains to inquire into the subject of the engine, and we may commence with

[044-063] the cylinders. Will you state the arrangement and construction of the

[044-064] cylinders of a locomotive and their connections?

[044-065] _A._--The cylinders are placed in the same horizontal plane as the axle of

[044-066] the driving wheels, and the connecting rod which is attached to the piston

[044-067] rod engages either a crank in the driving axle or a pin in the driving

[044-068] wheel, according as the cylinders are inside or outside of the framework.

[044-069] The cylinders are generally made an inch longer than the stroke, or there

[044-070] is half an inch of clearance at each end of the cylinder, to permit the

[044-071] springs of the vehicle to act without causing the piston to strike the top

[044-072] or bottom of the cylinder. The thickness of metal of the cylinder ends is

[044-073] usually about a third more than the thickness of the cylinder itself, and

[044-074] both ends are generally made removable. The priming of the boiler, when it

[044-075] occurs, is very injurious to the cylinders and valves of locomotives,

[044-076] especially if the water be sandy, as the grit carried over by the steam

[044-077] wears the rubbing surfaces rapidly away. The face of the cylinder on which

[044-078] the valve works is raised a little above the metal around it, both to

[044-079] facilitate the operation of forming the face and with the view of enabling

[044-080] any foreign substance deposited on the face to be pushed aside by the valve

[044-081] into the less elevated part, where it may lie without occasioning any

[044-082] further disturbance. The valve casing is sometimes cast upon the cylinder,

[044-083] and it is generally covered with a door which may be removed to permit the

[044-084] inspection of the faces. In some valve casings the top as well as the back

[044-085] is removable, which admits of the valve and valve bridle being removed with

[044-086] greater facility. A cock is placed at each end of locomotive cylinders, to

[044-087] allow the water to be discharged which accumulates in the cylinder from

[044-088] priming or condensation; and the four cocks of the two cylinders are

[044-089] usually connected together, so that by turning a handle the whole are

[044-090] opened at once. In Stephenson's engines, however, with variable expansion,

[044-091] there is but one cock provided for this purpose, which is on the bottom of

[044-092] the valve chest.

[044-093] 509. _Q._--What kind of piston is used in locomotives?

[044-094] _A._--The variety of pistons employed in locomotives is very great, and

[044-095] sometimes even the more complicated kinds are found to work very

[044-096] satisfactorily; but, in general, those pistons which consist of a single

[044-097] ring and tongue piece, or of two single rings set one above the other, so

[044-098] as to break joint, are preferable to those which consist of many pieces. In

[044-099] Stephenson's pistons the screws were at one time liable to work slack, and

[044-100] the springs to break.

[044-101] 510. _Q._--Will you explain the connection of the piston rod with the

[044-102] connecting rod?

[044-103] _A._--The piston rods of all engines are now generally either case hardened

[044-104] very deeply, or are made of steel; and in locomotive engines the diameter

[044-105] of the piston rod is about one seventh of the diameter of the cylinder, and

[044-106] it is formed of tilted steel. The cone of the piston rod, by which it is

[044-107] attached to the piston, is turned the reverse way to that which is adopted

[044-108] in common engines, with the view of making the cutter more accessible from

[044-109] the bottom of the cylinder, which is made to come off like a door. The top

[044-110] of the piston rod is secured with a cutter into a socket with jaws, through

[044-111] the holes of which a cross head passes, which is embraced between the jaws

[044-112] by the small end of the connecting rod, while the ends of the cross head

[044-113] move in guides. Between the piston rod clutch and the guide blocks, the

[044-114] feed pump rod joins the cross head in some engines.

[044-115] 511. _Q._--What kind of guides is employed for the end of the piston rod?

[044-116] _A._--The guides are formed of steel plates attached to the framing,

[044-117] between which work the guide blocks, fixed on the ends of the cross head,

[044-118] which have flanges bearing against the inner edges of the guides. Steel or

[044-119] brass guides are better than iron ones: Stephenson and Hawthorn attach

[044-120] their guides at one end to a cross stay, at the other to lugs on the

[044-121] cylinder cover; and they are made stronger in the middle than at the ends.

[044-122] Stout guide rods of steel, encircled by stuffing boxes on the ends of the

[044-123] cross head, would probably be found superior to any other arrangement. The

[044-124] stuffing boxes might contain conical bushes, cut spirally, in addition to

[044-125] the packing, and a ring, cut spirally, might be sprung upon the rod and

[044-126] fixed in advance of the stuffing box, with lateral play to wipe the rod

[044-127] before entering the stuffing box, to prevent it from being scratched by the

[044-128] adhesion of dust.

[044-129] 512. _Q._--Is any provision made for keeping the connecting rod always of

[044-130] the same length?

[044-131] _A._--In every kind of locomotive it is very desirable that the length of

[044-132] the connecting rod should remain invariable, in spite of the wear of the

[044-133] brasses, for there is a danger of the piston striking against the cover of

[044-134] the cylinder if it be shortened, as the clearance is left as small as

[044-135] possible in order to economize steam. In some engines the strap encircling

[044-136] the crank pin is fixed immovably to the connecting rod by dovetailed keys,

[044-137] and a bolt passes through the keys, rod, and strap, to prevent the

[044-138] dovetailed keys from working out. The brass is tightened by a gib and

[044-139] cutter, which is kept from working loose by three pinching screws and a

[044-140] cross pin or cutter through the point. The effect of this arrangement is to

[044-141] lengthen the rod, but at the cross head end of the rod the elongation is

[044-142] neutralized by making the strap loose, so that in tightening the brass the

[044-143] rod is shortened by an amount equal to its elongation at the crank pin end.

[044-144] The tightening here is also effected by a gib and cutter, which is kept

[044-145] from working loose by two pinching screws pressing on the side of the

[044-146] cutter. Both journals of the connecting rod are furnished with oil cups,

[044-147] having a small tube in the centre with siphon wicks. The connecting rod is

[044-148] a thick flat bar, with its edges rounded.

[044-149] 513. _Q._--How is the cranked axle of locomotives constructed?

[044-150] _A._--The cranked axle of locomotives is always made of wrought iron, with

[044-151] two cranks forged upon it toward the middle of its length, at a distance

[044-152] from each other answerable to the distance between the cylinders. Bosses

[044-153] are made on the axle for the wheels to be keyed upon, and bearings for the

[044-154] support of the framing. The axle is usually forged in two pieces, which are

[044-155] afterward welded together. Sometimes the pieces for the cranks are put on

[044-156] separately, but the cranks so made are liable to give way. In engines with

[044-157] outside cylinders the axles are made straight-the crank pins being inserted

[044-158] in the naves of the wheels. The bearings to which the connecting rods are

[044-159] attached are made with very large fillets in the corners, so as to

[044-160] strengthen the axle in that part, and to obviate side play in the

[044-161] connecting rod. In engines which, have been in use for some time, however,

[044-162] there is generally a good deal of end play in the bearings of the axles

[044-163] themselves, and this slackness contributes to make the oscillation of the

[044-164] engine more violent; but this evil may be remedied by making the bearings

[044-165] spheroidal, whereby end play becomes impossible.
