# Air Pump

The **air pump** is a vital component of condensing [[steam|steam engine]]s. Its primary function is to remove from the [[condenser]] the water into which the [[eduction|exhaust]] steam has been condensed, together with any air that enters with the steam or through leakage. By continuously evacuating the [[condensation|condenser]], the air pump maintains the vacuum necessary for efficient engine operation (002-050, 009-003).

## Operation

The air pump is worked off the main engine — in beam engines from a cross-head on the main beam; in side-lever [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s from the side lever; and in direct-acting engines from the cross-head or an intermediate shaft (009-059, 010-008, 010-077). Two tappets on the air pump rod operate the suction and delivery valves: the lower tappet opens the foot valve at the bottom of the stroke, and the upper tappet actuates the delivery valve at the top (008-163, 008-174, 009-114).

When the air pump piston ascends, it leaves behind a partial vacuum, which causes the foot valve to open and admit hot water and air from the [[condensation|condenser]] into the lower part of the air pump (009-005, 009-008). When the piston descends, the foot valve closes and the delivery valve opens, forcing the water up into the hot well, from which the [[feed-pump]] returns it to the boiler (009-009).

## Configuration

In most [[condensation|condensing engine]]s, the air pump is set within the [[condensation|condenser]] itself, receiving its motion directly from the engine. A short pipe leads from the bottom of the [[condensation|condenser]] into the air pump, in which a foot valve opens toward the air pump to prevent backflow (008-149, 008-150, 010-171).

In [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s, the air pump delivers water overboard through a delivery valve and side pipe, rather than returning it to the boiler, as [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s use salt water for condensation (008-170, 008-235).

## Efficiency Considerations

The air pump consumes a measurable fraction of the engine's power. The work required to pump out the condensed water against atmospheric pressure, combined with the friction of the pump and valves, is deducted from the engine's net output (017-014). In engines of the highest efficiency, such as Cornish pumping engines, careful attention is given to air pump design to minimize this loss.

Some later designs proposed performing part of the condensation within the air pump itself, mitigating the shock of the valves and improving the smoothness of operation (017-111, 017-113).
