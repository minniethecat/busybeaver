[020-001] 251. _Q._--You have now shown that the performance of an engine is

[020-002] determinable by the indicator; but how do you determine the power of the

[020-003] boiler?

[020-004] _A._--By the quantity of water it evaporates. There is, however, no very

[020-005] convenient instrument for determining the quantity of water supplied to a

[020-006] boiler, and the consequence is that this element is seldom ascertained.

[020-007] CHAPTER V.

[020-008] PROPORTION OF BOILERS.

[020-009] HEATING AND FIRE GRATE SURFACE.

[020-010] 252. _Q._--What are the considerations which must chiefly be attended to in

[020-011] settling the proportions of boilers?

[020-012] _A._--In the first place there must be sufficient grate surface to enable

[020-013] the quantity of coal requisite for the production of the steam to be

[020-014] conveniently burnt, taking into account the intensity of the draught; and

[020-015] in the next place there must be a sufficient flue surface readily to absorb

[020-016] the heat thus produced, so that there may be no needless waste of heat by

[020-017] the chimney. The flues, moreover, must have such an area, and the chimney

[020-018] must be of such dimensions, as will enable a suitable draught through the

[020-019] fire to be maintained; and finally the boiler must be made capable of

[020-020] containing such supplies of water and steam as will obviate inconvenient

[020-021] fluctuations in the water level, and abate the risk of water being carried

[020-022] over into the engine with the steam. With all these conditions the boiler

[020-023] must be as light and compact as possible, and must be so contrived as to be

[020-024] capable of being cleaned and repaired with facility.

[020-025] 253. _Q._--Supposing, then, that you had to proportion a boiler, which

[020-026] should be capable of supplying steam sufficient to propel a steam vessel or

[020-027] railway train at a given speed, or to perform any other given work, how

[020-028] would you proceed?

[020-029] _A._--I would first ascertain the resistance which had to be overcome, and

[020-030] the velocity with which it was necessary to overcome it. I should then be

[020-031] in a position to know what pressure and volume of steam were required to

[020-032] overcome the resistance at the prescribed rate of motion; and, finally, I

[020-033] should allow a sufficient heating and fire grate surface in the boiler

[020-034] according to the kind of boiler it was, to furnish the requisite quantity

[020-035] of steam, or, in other words, to evaporate the requisite quantity of water.

[020-036] 254. _Q._--will you state the amount of heating surface and grate surface

[020-037] necessary to evaporate a given quantity of water?

[020-038] _A._--The number of square feet of heating or flue surface, required to

[020-039] evaporate a cubic foot of water per hour, is about 70 square feet in

[020-040] Cornish boilers, 8 to 11 square feet in land and marine boilers, and 5 or 6

[020-041] square feet in locomotive boilers. The number of square feet of heating

[020-042] surface per square foot of fire grate, is from 13 to 15 square feet in

[020-043] wagon boilers; about 40 square feet in Cornish boilers; and from 50 to 90

[020-044] square feet in locomotive boilers. About 80 square feet in locomotives is a

[020-045] very good proportion.

[020-046] 255. _Q._--What is the heating surface of boilers per horse power?

[020-047] _A._--About 9 square feet of flue and furnace surface per horse power is

[020-048] the usual proportion in wagon boilers, reckoning the total surface as

[020-049] effective surface, if the boilers be of a considerable size; but in the

[020-050] case of small boilers the proportion is larger. The total heating surface

[020-051] of a two horse power wagon boiler is, according to Boulton and Watt's

[020-052] proportions, 30 square feet, or 15 ft. per horse power; whereas, in the

[020-053] case of a 45 horse power boiler the total heating surface is 438 square

[020-054] feet, or 9.6 ft. per horse power. In marine boilers nearly the same

[020-055] proportions obtain. The original boilers of the Great Western steamer, by

[020-056] Messrs. Maudslay, were proportioned with about 10 square feet of flue and

[020-057] furnace surface per horse power, reckoning the total amount as effective;

[020-058] but in the boilers of the Retribution, by the same makers, but of larger

[020-059] size, a somewhat smaller proportion of heating surface was adopted. Boulton

[020-060] and Watt have found that in their marine flue boilers, 9 square feet of

[020-061] flue and furnace surface are requisite to boil off a cubic foot of water

[020-062] per hour, which is the proportion of heating surface that is allowed in

[020-063] their land boilers per horse power; but inasmuch as in most modern engines,

[020-064] and especially in marine engines, the nominal considerably exceeds the

[020-065] actual power, they allow 11 or 12 square feet of heating surface per

[020-066] nominal horse power in their marine boilers, and they reckon as effective

[020-067] heating surface the tops of the flues, and the whole of the sides of the

[020-068] flues, but hot the bottoms. For their land engines they still retain Mr.

[020-069] Watt's standard of power, which makes the actual and the nominal power

[020-070] identical; and an actual horse power is the equivalent of a cubic foot of

[020-071] water raised into steam every hour.

[020-072] 256. _Q._--What is the proper proportion of fire grate per horse power?

[020-073] _A._--Boulton and Watt allow 0.64 of a square foot area of grate bars per

[020-074] nominal horse power in their marine boilers, and a good effect arises from

[020-075] this proportion; but sometimes so large an area of fire grate cannot be

[020-076] conveniently got, and the proportion of half a square foot per horse power,

[020-077] which is the proportion adopted in the original boiler of the Great

[020-078] Western, seems to answer very well in engines working with a moderate

[020-079] pressure, and with some expansion; and this proportion is now very widely

[020-080] adopted. With this allowance, there will be 22 to 24 square feet of heating

[020-081] surface per square foot of fire grate; and if the consumption of fuel be

[020-082] taken at 6 lbs. per nominal horse power per hour, there will be about 12

[020-083] lbs. of coal consumed per hour on each square foot of grate. The furnaces

[020-084] should not be more than 6 ft. long, as, if much longer than this, it will

[020-085] be impossible to work them properly for any considerable length of time, as

[020-086] they will become choked with clinker at the back ends.

[020-087] 257. _Q._--What quantity of fuel is usually consumed per hour on each

[020-088] square foot of fire grate?

[020-089] _A._--The quantity of fuel burned on each square foot of fire grate per

[020-090] hour, varies very much in different boilers; in wagon boilers it is from 10

[020-091] to 13 lbs.; in Cornish boilers from 3-1/2 to 4 lbs.; and in locomotive

[020-092] boilers from 80 to 150 lbs.; but about 1 cwt. per hour is a good proportion

[020-093] in locomotives, as has been already explained.

[020-094] CALORIMETER AND VENT.

[020-095] 258. _Q._--In what manner are the proper sectional area and the proper

[020-096] capacity of the flue of a boiler determined?

[020-097] _A._--The proper collective area for the escape of the smoke and flame over

[020-098] the furnace bridges in marine boilers is 19 square inches per nominal horse

[020-099] power, according to Boulton and Watt's practice, and for the sectional area

[020-100] of the flue they allow 18 square inches per horse power. The sectional area

[020-101] of the flue in square inches is what is termed the _calorimeter_ of the

[020-102] boiler, and the calorimeter divided by the length of the flue in feet is

[020-103] what is termed the _vent_. In marine flue boilers of good construction the

[020-104] vent varies between the limits of 20 and 25, according to the size of the

[020-105] boiler and other circumstances--the largest boilers having generally the

[020-106] largest vents; and the calorimeter divided by the vent will give the length

[020-107] of the flue in feet. The flues of all flue boilers diminish in their

[020-108] calorimeter as they approach the chimney, as the smoke contracts in its

[020-109] volume in proportion as it parts with its heat.

[020-110] 259. _Q._--Is the method of determining the dimensions of a boiler flue, by

[020-111] a reference to its vent and calorimeter, the method generally pursued?

[020-112] _A._--It is Boulton and Watt's method; but some very satisfactory boilers

[020-113] have been made by allowing a proportion of 0.6 of a square foot of fire

[020-114] grate per nominal horse power, and making the sectional area of the flue at

[020-115] the largest part 1/7th of the area of fire grate, and at the smallest part,

[020-116] where it enters the chimney, 1/11th of the area of the fire grate. These

[020-117] proportions are retained whether the boiler is flue or tubular, and from 14

[020-118] to 16 square feet of tube surface is allowed per nominal horse power.

[020-119] 260. _Q._--Are the proportions of vent and calorimeter, taken by Boulton

[020-120] and Watt for marine flue boilers, applicable also to wagon and tubular

[020-121] boilers?

[020-122] _A._--No. In wagon and tubular boilers very different proportions prevail,

[020-123] yet the proportions of every kind of boiler are determinable on the same

[020-124] general principle. In wagon boilers the proportion of the perimeter of the

[020-125] flue which is effective as heating surface, is to the total perimeter as 1

[020-126] to 3, or, in some cases as 1 to 2.5; and with any given area of flue,

[020-127] therefore, the length of the flue must be from 3 to 2.5 times greater than

[020-128] would be necessary if the total surface were effective, else the requisite

[020-129] quantity of heating surface will not be obtained. If, then, the vent be the

[020-130] calorimeter, divided by the length, and the length be made 3 or 2.5 times

[020-131] greater, the vent must become 3 or 2.5 times less; and in wagon boilers

[020-132] accordingly, the vent varies from 8 to 11 instead of from 21 to 25, as in

[020-133] the case of marine flue boilers. In tubular marine boilers the calorimeter

[020-134] is usually made only about half the amount allowed by Boulton and Watt for

[020-135] marine flue boilers, or, in other words, the collective sectional area of

[020-136] the tubes, for the transmission of the smoke, is from 8 to 9 square inches

[020-137] per nominal horse power. It is better, however, to make the sectional area

[020-138] larger than this, and to work the boiler with the damper sufficiently

[020-139] closed to prevent the smoke and flame from rushing exclusively through a

[020-140] few of the tubes.

[020-141] 261. _Q._--What are the ordinary dimensions of the flue in wagon boilers?

[020-142] _A._--In Boulton and Watt's 45 horse wagon boiler the area of flue is 18

[020-143] square inches per horse power, but the area per horse power increases very

[020-144] rapidly as the size of the boiler becomes less, and amounts to about 80

[020-145] square inches per horse power in a boiler of 2 horse power. Some such

[020-146] increase is obviously inevitable, if a similar form of flue be retained in

[020-147] the larger and smaller powers, and at the same time the elongation of the

[020-148] flue in the same proportion as the increase of any other dimension is

[020-149] prevented; but in the smaller class of wagon boilers the consideration of

[020-150] facility of cleaning the flues is also operative in inducing a large

[020-151] proportion of sectional area. Boulton and Watt's 2 horse power wagon boiler

[020-152] has 30 square feet of surface, and the flue is 18 inches high above the

[020-153] level of the boiler bottom, by 9 inches wide; while their 12 horse wagon

[020-154] boiler has 118 square feet of heating surface, and the dimensions of the

[020-155] flue similarly measured are 36 inches by 13 inches. The width of the

[020-156] smaller flue, if similarly proportioned to the larger one, would be 6-1/2

[020-157] inches, instead of 9 inches, and, by assuming this dimension, we should

[020-158] have the same proportion of sectional area per square foot of heating

[020-159] surface in both boilers. The length of flue in the 2 horse boiler is 19.5

[020-160] ft., and in the 12 horse boiler 39 ft., so that the length and height of

[020-161] the flue are increased in the same proportion.

[020-162] 262. _Q._--Will you give an example of the proportions of a flue, in the

[020-163] case of a marine boiler?

[020-164] _A._--The Nile steamer, with engines of 110 horse power by Boulton and

[020-165] Watt, is supplied with steam by two boilers, which are, therefore, of 55

[020-166] horses power each. The height of the flue winding within the boiler is 60

[020-167] inches, and its mean width 16-1/2 inches, making a sectional area or

[020-168] calorimeter of 990 square inches, or 18 square inches per horse power of

[020-169] the boiler. The length of the flue is 39 ft., making the vent 25, which is

[020-170] the vent proper for large boilers. In the Dee and Solway steamers, by Scott

[020-171] and Sinclair, the calorimeter is only 9.72 square inches per horse power;

[020-172] in the Eagle, by Caird, 11.9; in the Thames and Medway, by Maudslay, 11.34,

[020-173] and in a great number of other cases it does not rise above 12 square

[020-174] inches per horse power; but the engines of most of these vessels are

[020-175] intended to operate to a certain extent expansively, and the boilers are

[020-176] less powerful in evaporating efficacy on that account.
