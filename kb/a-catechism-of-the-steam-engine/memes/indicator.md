# Indicator

The **indicator** is an instrument that draws a diagram representing the changing pressure of steam inside the engine [[Cylinder|cylinder]] throughout a complete stroke. (017-009) From this diagram the mean effective pressure, the [[Horsepower|indicated horsepower]], and the behaviour of the [[Slide Valve|valve]] gear can be accurately determined. (018-013) (018-118)

## Construction

The indicator is in effect a miniature [[steam|steam engine]] cylinder of small bore, fitted with a piston that moves against a calibrated spring. (017-009) The body of the indicator contains a cylinder, a piston, and a spring whose compression is proportional to the steam pressure. (018-127) A pencil or stylus attached to the piston rod records the piston's movement on a paper-wrapped drum.

The indicator cylinder is connected to the [[cylinder|engine cylinder]] by a cock, which when opened admits steam pressure to the underside of the indicator piston. (018-143) The drum, which holds the paper, is caused to rotate back and forth by a cord connected to the engine's cross-head or some other reciprocating part, so that its rotation corresponds to the motion of the [[piston|engine piston]].

> "An instrument called the indicator, which consists of a small cylinder, a piston, a spring, and a pencil, records the pressure of the steam at every point of the stroke." (018-118)

The common form of indicator in nineteenth-century British practice was **[[william-mcnaught|McNaught]]'s indicator**, in which the spring is placed above the piston and the pencil motion is transmitted through a [[parallel-motion|parallel linkage]] to ensure [[parallel-motion|straight-line motion]] of the stylus. (018-124)

## The Indicator Diagram

The diagram drawn by the indicator — known as the **[[indicator-diagram|indicator diagram]]** — is a closed curve whose horizontal axis represents the stroke of the piston and whose vertical axis represents the steam pressure at each point. (018-155) When the diagram is a complete rectangle of full boiler pressure extending over the whole stroke, the engine is said to be operating at full power without expansion. (018-171)

A typical [[indicator-diagram|indicator diagram]] from a low-pressure [[condensation|condensing engine]] shows (019-023):
- The **admission line** — pressure rises as steam enters
- The **steam line** — pressure maintained during admission
- The **expansion curve** — pressure falls as the steam expands after cut-off
- The **[[eduction|exhaust]] line** — pressure drops to near vacuum
- The **compression curve** — slight pressure rise just before the next admission

| Phase | What It Shows | Diagnostic Value |
|-------|--------------|-----------------|
| Admission | [[steam|Steam]] entry pressure | [[boiler|Boiler]] pressure adequacy |
| [[steam|Steam]] line | Pressure maintained | Port size and passage efficiency |
| Expansion | Pressure decay after cut-off | Correctness of [[Lap|lap]] setting |
| Exhaust | Back pressure | [[condenser|Condenser]] effectiveness |
| Compression | Cushioning at stroke end | [[valve|Valve]] timing precision |

## Taking an Indicator Diagram

In proceeding to take an [[indicator-diagram|indicator diagram]], the first step is to ensure that the paper is properly fixed on the drum and that the pencil bears lightly on the paper. (019-011) The cock connecting the indicator to the cylinder is then opened and the instrument is allowed to record one or two complete cycles. The diagram thus obtained provides a permanent record of the engine's internal steam distribution.

To calculate the mean effective pressure from the diagram, the area of the closed figure is measured — usually with a planimeter — and divided by the length of the diagram. (017-019) This mean pressure, multiplied by the piston area and the piston speed, gives the [[horsepower|indicated horsepower]].

> "The power of an engine is readily ascertained by the indicator, by measuring the area of the diagram." (017-059)

## Practical Value

The indicator reveals defects in the valve setting that are invisible from outside the engine. A diagram with a rounded admission corner indicates wire-drawing — steam throttling through a partially-opened port. A sloping steam line reveals undersized passages. A high [[eduction|exhaust]] line indicates poor vacuum or restricted [[eduction|exhaust]] ports. (019-030)

In [[Marine Engine|marine engines]], where the engine room exposes machinery to the motion of the vessel, the indicator is essential for verifying that the engine performs equally well in both directions of rotation, as is required for manoeuvring. (018-013)
