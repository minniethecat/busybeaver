# William Fairbairn (1789–1874)

Sir William Fairbairn was a Scottish structural engineer and shipbuilder whose experiments on the strength of materials were widely cited in nineteenth-century boiler design. His testing of boiler plate tensile strength provided empirical data that complemented theoretical analysis.

## Career and Contributions

Fairbairn established a successful engineering works in Manchester, where he conducted systematic experiments on the strength of iron plates. His findings on boiler plate strength became a standard reference for engineers designing [[boiler]] shells and determining safe working pressures.

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] references Fairbairn's experiments when discussing the diminution of strength caused by riveting and punching boiler plates: the results obtained in one set of tests were "not precisely the same as those obtained by Mr. Fairbairn" (024-112). The Catechism then proceeds to detail Fairbairn's findings: "He found that boiler plate bore a tensile strain of 23 tons per square inch before rupture, which was reduced to 16 tons per square inch" when the plate was punched and riveted (024-114).

Fairbairn's work established that the process of punching holes for [[rivet]]s significantly weakened the surrounding metal, a finding that influenced boiler construction practices throughout the nineteenth century.

## Legacy

Fairbairn's experimental methods set an important precedent for the empirical testing of materials in [[steam|steam engine]] and boiler construction. His data on tensile strength reduction due to punching and riveting informed safety standards well beyond his time, and his name appears in the index of the Catechism as an authoritative reference on boiler strength (068-138).
