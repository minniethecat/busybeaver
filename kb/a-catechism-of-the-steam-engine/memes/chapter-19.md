[019-001] 237. _Q._--And how is this area ascertained?

[019-002] _A._--It may be ascertained in various ways; but the usual mode is to take

[019-003] the vertical height of the diagram at a number of equidistant points on a

[019-004] base line, and then to take the mean of these several heights as

[019-005] representative of the mean pressure actually urging the piston. Now if you

[019-006] have the pressure on the piston per square inch, and if you know the number

[019-007] of square inches in its area, and the velocity with which it moves in feet

[019-008] per minute, you have obviously the dynamical effort of the engine, or, in

[019-009] other words, its actual power.

[019-010] 238. _Q._--How is the base line you have referred to obtained?

[019-011] _A._--In proceeding to take an indicator diagram, the first thing to be

[019-012] done is to allow the barrel to make two or three reciprocations with the

[019-013] pencil resting against it, before opening the cock attached to the

[019-014] cylinder. There will thus be traced a horizontal line, which is called the

[019-015] _atmospheric line_, and in condensing engines, a part of the diagram will

[019-016] be above and a part of it below this line; whereas, in high pressure

[019-017] engines the whole of the diagram will be above this line. Upon this line

[019-018] the vertical ordinates may be set off at equal distances, or upon any base

[019-019] line parallel to it; but the usual course is to erect the ordinates on the

[019-020] atmospheric line.

[019-021] 239. _Q._--Will you give an example of an indicator diagram?

[019-022] [Illustration: Fig. 37]

[019-023] _A._--Fig. 37 is an indicator diagram taken from a low pressure engine, and

[019-024] the waving line _a b c_, forming a sort of irregular parallelogram, is that

[019-025] which is described by the pencil. The atmospheric line is represented by

[019-026] the line o o. The scale at the side shows the pressure of the steam, which

[019-027] in this engine rose to about 9 lbs. per square inch, and the vacuum fell to

[019-028] 11 lbs. The steam begins to be cut off when, about one-fourth of the stroke

[019-029] has been performed, and the pressure consequently falls.

[019-030] 240. _Q._--Is this species of indicator which you have just described

[019-031] applicable to locomotive engines?

[019-032] _A._--It is no doubt applicable under suitable conditions; but another

[019-033] species of indicator has been applied by Mr. Gooch to locomotive engines,

[019-034] which presents several features of superiority for such a purpose.

[019-035] This indicator has its cylinder placed horizontally; and its piston

[019-036] compresses two elliptical springs; a slide valve is substituted for a cock,

[019-037] to open or close the communication with the engine. The top of the piston

[019-038] rod of this indicator is connected to the short arm of a smaller lever, to

[019-039] the longer arm of which the pencil is attached, and the pencil has thus a

[019-040] considerably larger amount of motion than the piston; but it moves in the

[019-041] arc of a circle instead of in a straight line. The pencil marks on a web of

[019-042] paper, which is unwound from one drum and wound on to another, so that a

[019-043] succession of diagrams are taken without the necessity of any intermediate

[019-044] manipulation.

[019-045] 241. _Q._--These diagrams being taken with a pencil moving in an arc, will

[019-046] be of a distorted form?

[019-047] _A._--They will not be of the usual form, but they may be easily translated

[019-048] into the usual form. It is undoubtedly preferable that the indicator should

[019-049] act immediately in the production of the final form of diagram.

[019-050] DYNAMOMETER, GAUGES, AND CATARACT.

[019-051] 242. _Q._--What other gauges or instruments are there for telling the

[019-052] state, or regulating the power of an engine?

[019-053] _A._--There is the counter for telling the number of strokes the engine

[019-054] makes, and the dynamometer for ascertaining the tractive power of steam

[019-055] vessels or locomotives; then there are the gauge cocks, and glass tubes, or

[019-056] floats, for telling the height of water in the boiler; and in pumping

[019-057] engines there is the cataract for regulating the speed of the engine.

[019-058] 243. _Q._--Will you describe the mechanism of the counter?

[019-059] _A._--The counter consists of a train of wheel work, so contrived that by

[019-060] every stroke of the engine an index hand is moved forward a certain space,

[019-061] whereby the number of strokes made by the engine in any given time is

[019-062] accurately recorded. In most cases the motion is communicated by means of a

[019-063] detent,--attached to some reciprocating part of the engine,--to a ratchet

[019-064] wheel which gives motion to the other wheels in its slow revolution; but it

[019-065] is preferable to derive the motion from some revolving part of the engine

[019-066] by means of an endless screw, as where the ratchet is used the detent will

[019-067] sometimes fail to carry it round the proper distance. In the counter

[019-068] contrived by Mr. Adie, an endless screw works into the rim of two small

[019-069] wheels situated on the same axis, but one wheel having a tooth more than

[019-070] the other, whereby a differential motion is obtained; and the difference in

[019-071] the velocity of the two wheels, or their motion upon one another, expresses

[019-072] the number of strokes performed. The endless screw is attached to some

[019-073] revolving part of the engine, whereby a rotatory motion is imparted to it;

[019-074] and the wheels into which the screws work hang down from it like a

[019-075] pendulum, and are kept stationary by the action of gravity.

[019-076] 244. _Q._--What is the nature of the dynamometer?

[019-077] _A._--The dynamometer employed for ascertaining the traction upon railways

[019-078] consists of two flat springs joined together at the ends by links, and the

[019-079] amount of separation of the springs at the centre indicates, by means of a

[019-080] suitable hand and dial, the force of traction. A cylinder of oil, with a

[019-081] small hole through its piston, is sometimes added to this instrument to

[019-082] prevent sudden fluctuations. In screw vessels the forward thrust of the

[019-083] screw is measured by a dynamometer constructed on the principle of a

[019-084] weighing machine, in which a small spring pressure at the index will

[019-085] balance a very great pressure where the thrust is employed; and in each

[019-086] case the variations of pressure are recorded by a pencil on a sheet of

[019-087] paper, carried forward by suitable mechanism, whereby the mean thrust is

[019-088] easily ascertained. The tractive force of paddle wheel steamers is

[019-089] ascertained by a dynamometer fixed on shore, to which the floating vessel

[019-090] is attached by a rope. Sometimes the power of an engine is ascertained by a

[019-091] friction break dynamometer applied to the shaft.

[019-092] 345. _Q._--What will determine the amount of thrust shown by the

[019-093] dynamometer?

[019-094] _A._--In locomotives and in paddle steamers it will be determined by the

[019-095] force turning the wheels, and by the smallness of the diameter of the

[019-096] wheels; for with small wheels the thrust will be greater than with large

[019-097] wheels. In screw vessels the thrust will be determined by the force turning

[019-098] round the screw, and by the smallness of the screw's pitch; for with any

[019-099] given force of torsion a fine pitch of screw will give a greater thrust

[019-100] than a coarse pitch of screw, just as is the case when a screw works in a

[019-101] solid nut.

[019-102] 246. _Q._--Will you explain the use of the glass gauges affixed to the

[019-103] boiler?

[019-104] _A._--The glass gauges are tubes affixed to the fronts of boilers, by the

[019-105] aid of which the height of the water within the boilers is readily

[019-106] ascertainable, for the water will stand at the same height in the tube as

[019-107] in the boiler, with which there is a communication maintained both at the

[019-108] top and bottom of the tube by suitable stopcocks. The cocks connecting the

[019-109] glass tube with the boiler should always be so constructed that the tube

[019-110] may be blown through with the steam, to clear it of any internal concretion

[019-111] that may impair its transparency; and the construction of the sockets in

[019-112] which the tube is inserted should be such, that, even when there is steam

[019-113] in the boiler, a broken tube may be replaced with facility.

[019-114] 247. _Q._--What then are the gauge cocks?

[019-115] _A._--The gauge cocks are cocks penetrating the boiler at different

[019-116] heights, and which, when opened, tell whether it is water or steam that

[019-117] exists at the level at which they are respectively inserted. It is unsafe

[019-118] to trust to the glass gauges altogether as a means of ascertaining the

[019-119] water level, as sometimes they become choked, and it is necessary,

[019-120] therefore, to have gauge cocks in addition; but if the boiler be short of

[019-121] steam, and a partial vacuum be produced within it, the glass gauges become

[019-122] of essential service, as the gauge cocks will not operate in such a case,

[019-123] for though opened, instead of steam and water escaping from them, the air

[019-124] will rush into the boiler. It is expedient to carry a pipe from the lower

[019-125] end of the glass tube downward into the water of the boiler, and a pipe

[019-126] from the upper end upward into the steam in the boiler, so as to prevent

[019-127] the water from boiling down through the tube, as it might otherwise do, and

[019-128] prevent the level of the water from being ascertainable. The average level

[019-129] of water in the boiler should be above the centre of the tube; and the

[019-130] lowest of the gauge cocks should always run water, and the highest should

[019-131] always blow steam.

[019-132] 248. _Q._--Is not a float sometimes employed to indicate the level of the

[019-133] water in the boiler?

[019-134] _A._--A float for telling the height of water in the boiler is employed

[019-135] only in the case of land boilers, and its action is like that of a buoy

[019-136] floating on the surface, which, by means of a light rod passing vertically

[019-137] through the boiler, shows at what height the water stands. The float is

[019-138] usually formed of stone or iron, and is so counterbalanced as to make its

[019-139] operation the same as if it were a buoy of timber; and it is generally put

[019-140] in connection with the feed valve, so that in proportion as the float

[019-141] rises, the supply of feed water is diminished. The feed water in land

[019-142] boilers is admitted from a small open cistern, situated at the top of an

[019-143] upright or stand pipe set upon the boiler, and in which there is a column

[019-144] of water sufficiently high to balance the pressure of the steam.

[019-145] 249. _Q._--What is the cataract which is employed to regulate the speed of

[019-146] pumping engines?

[019-147] [Illustration: Fig. 38. ]

[019-148] _A._--The cataract consists of a small pump-plunger _b_ and barrel, set in

[019-149] a cistern of water, the barrel being furnished on the one side with a

[019-150] valve, _c_, opening inwards, through which the water obtains admission to

[019-151] the pump chamber from the cistern, and on the other by a plug, _d_, through

[019-152] which, if the plunger be forced down, the water must pass out of the pump

[019-153] chamber. The engine in the upward stroke of the piston, which is

[019-154] accomplished by the preponderance of weight at the pump end of the beam,

[019-155] raises up the plunger of the cataract by means of a small rod,--the water

[019-156] entering readily through the valve already referred to; and when the engine

[019-157] reaches the top of the stroke, it liberates the rod by which the plunger

[019-158] has been drawn up, and the plunger then descends by gravity, forcing out

[019-159] the water through the cock, the orifice of which has previously been

[019-160] adjusted, and the plunger in its descent opens the injection valve, which

[019-161] causes the engine to make a stroke.

[019-162] 250. _Q._--Suppose the cock of the cataract be shut?

[019-163] _A._--If the cock of the cataract be shut, it is clear that the plunger

[019-164] cannot descend at all, and as in that case the injection valve cannot be

[019-165] opened, the engine must stand still; but if the cock be slightly opened,

[019-166] the plunger will descend slowly, the injection valve will slowly open, and

[019-167] the engine will make a gradual stroke as it obtains the water necessary for

[019-168] condensation. The extent to which the cock is open, therefore, will

[019-169] regulate the speed with which the engine works; so that, by the use of the

[019-170] cataract, the speed of the engine may be varied to suit the variations in

[019-171] the quantity of water requiring to be lifted from the mine. In some cases

[019-172] an air cylinder, and in other cases an oil cylinder, is employed instead of

[019-173] the apparatus just described; but the principle on which the whole of these

[019-174] contrivances operate is identical, and the only difference is in the

[019-175] detail.
