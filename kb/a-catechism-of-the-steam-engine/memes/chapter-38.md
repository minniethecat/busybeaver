[038-001] 446. _Q._--The rings should be carefully fitted to one another laterally?

[038-002] _A._--The rings are to be fitted laterally to the piston, and to one

[038-003] another, by scraping--a steady pin being fixed upon the flange of the

[038-004] piston, and fitting into a corresponding hole in the lower ring, to keep

[038-005] the lower ring from turning round; and a similar pin being fixed into the

[038-006] top edge of the lower ring to prevent the upper ring from turning round;

[038-007] but the holes into which these pins fit must be made oblong, to enable the

[038-008] rings to press outward as the rubbing surfaces wear. In most cases it will

[038-009] be expedient to press the packing rings out with springs where they are not

[038-010] packed behind with hemp, and the springs should be made very strong, as the

[038-011] prevailing fault of springs is their weakness. Sometimes short bent

[038-012] springs, set round at regular intervals between the packing rings and body

[038-013] of the piston, are employed, the centre of each spring being secured by a

[038-014] steady pin or bolt screwed into the side of the piston; but it will not

[038-015] signify much what kind of springs is used, provided they have sufficient

[038-016] tension. When pistons are made of a single ring, or of a succession of

[038-017] single rings, the strength of each ring should be tested previously to its

[038-018] introduction into the piston, by means of a lever loaded by a heavy weight.

[038-019] 447. _Q._--What kind of piston is employed by Messrs. Penn?

[038-020] _A._--Messrs. Penn's piston for oscillating engines has a single packing

[038-021] ring, with a tongue piece, or mortice end, made in the manner already

[038-022] described. The ring is packed behind with hemp packing, and the piece of

[038-023] metal which covers the joint is a piece of thick sheet copper or brass, and

[038-024] is indented into the iron of the ring, so as to offer no obstruction to the

[038-025] application of the hemp. The ring is fitted to the piston only on the under

[038-026] edge; the top edge is rounded to a point from the inside, and the junk ring

[038-027] does not bear upon it, but the junk ring squeezes down the hemp packing

[038-028] between the packing ring and the body of the piston.

[038-029] 448. _Q._--How should the piston rod be secured to the piston?

[038-030] _A._--The piston rod, where it fits into the piston, should have a good

[038-031] deal of taper; for if the taper be too small the rod will be drawn through

[038-032] the hole, and the piston will be split asunder. Small grooves are sometimes

[038-033] turned out of the piston rod above and below the cutter hole, and hemp is

[038-034] introduced in order to make the piston eye tight. Most piston rods are

[038-035] fixed to the piston by means of a gib and cutter, but in some cases the

[038-036] upper portion of the rod within the eye is screwed, and it is fixed into

[038-037] the piston by means of an indented nut. This nut is in some cases

[038-038] hexagonal, and in other cases the exterior forms a portion of a cone which

[038-039] completely fills a corresponding recess in the piston; but nuts made in

[038-040] this way become rusted into their seat after some time, and cannot be

[038-041] started again without much difficulty. Messrs. Miller, Ravenhill & Co. fix

[038-042] in their piston rods by means of an indented hexagonal nut, which may be

[038-043] started by means of an open box key. The thread of the screw is made flat

[038-044] upon the one side and much slanted on the other, whereby a greater strength

[038-045] is secured, without creating any disposition to split the nut. In side

[038-046] lever engines it is a judicious practice to add a nut to the top of the

[038-047] piston rod, in addition to the cutter for securing the piston rod to the

[038-048] cross head. In a good example of an engine thus provided, the piston rod is

[038-049] 7 in. in diameter, and the screw 5 in.; the part of the rod which fits into

[038-050] the cross head eye is 1 ft. 5-1/2 in. long, and tapers from 6-1/2 in. to

[038-051] 6-13/16 in. diameter. This proportion of taper is a good one; if the taper

[038-052] be less, or if a portion of the piston rod within the cross head eye be

[038-053] left untapered, as is sometimes the case, it is very difficult to detach

[038-054] the parts from one another.

[038-055] 449. _Q._--Which is the most beneficial construction of slide valve?

[038-056] _A._--The best construction of slide valve appears to be that adopted by

[038-057] Messrs. Penn for their larger engines, and which consists of a three ported

[038-058] valve, to the back of which a ring is applied of an area equal to that of

[038-059] exhaustion port, and which, by bearing steam tight against the back of the

[038-060] casing, so that a vacuum may be maintained within the ring, puts the valve

[038-061] in equilibrium, so that it may be moved with an inconsiderable exercise of

[038-062] force. The back of the valve casing is put on like a door, and its internal

[038-063] surface is made very true by scraping. There is a hole through the valve so

[038-064] as to conduct away any steam which may enter within the ring by leakage,

[038-065] and the ring is kept tight against the back of the casing by means of a

[038-066] ring situated beneath the bearing ring, provided with four lugs, through

[038-067] which bolts pass tapped into bosses on the back of the valve; and, by

[038-068] unscrewing these bolts,--which may be done by means of a box key which

[038-069] passes through holes in the casing closed with screwed plugs,--the lower

[038-070] ring is raised upwards, carrying the bearing ring before it. The rings must

[038-071] obviously be fitted over a boss upon the back of the valve; and between the

[038-072] rings, which are of brass, a gasket ring is interposed to compensate by its

[038-073] compressibility for any irregularity of pressure, and each of the bolts is

[038-074] provided with a ratchet collar to prevent it from turning back, so that the

[038-075] engineer, in tightening these bolts, will have no difficulty in tightening

[038-076] them equally, if he counts the number of clicks made by the ratchet. Where

[038-077] this species of valve is used, it is indispensable that large escape valves

[038-078] be applied to the cylinder, as a valve on this construction is unable to

[038-079] leave the face. In locomotive engines, the valve universally employed is

[038-080] the common three ported valve.

[038-081] 450. _Q._--Might not an equilibrium valve be so constructed by the

[038-082] interposition of springs, as to enable it to leave the cylinder face when

[038-083] an internal force is applied?

[038-084] _A._--That can no doubt be done, and in some engines has been done. In the

[038-085] screw steamer Azof, the valve is of the equilibrium construction, but the

[038-086] plate which carries the packing on which the top ring rests, is an octagon,

[038-087] and fits into an octagonal recess on the back of the valve. Below each side

[038-088] of the octagon there is a bent flat spring, which lifts up the octagonal

[038-089] plate, and with it the packing ring against the back of the valve casing;

[038-090] and should water get into the cylinder, it escapes by lifting the valve,

[038-091] which is rendered possible by the compressibility of the springs. An

[038-092] equivalent arrangement is shown in figs. 39 and 40, where the ring is

[038-093] lifted by spiral springs.

[038-096] 451. _Q._--What species of valve is that shown in figs. 39 and 40?

[038-097] [Illustration: Fig. 40. EQUILIBRIUM GRIDIRON SLIDE VALVE. Back View with

[038-098] Ring removed. Scale 3/4 inch = 1 foot.]

[038-099] _A._--It is an equilibrium gridiron valve; so called because it lets the

[038-100] steam in and out by more than one port.  A A are the ordinary steam

[038-101] passages to the top and bottom of the cylinder; B B is the ring which rubs

[038-102] against the back of the valve casing, and D is the eduction passage, S S S

[038-103] S shows the limits of the steam space, for the steam penetrates to the

[038-104] central chamber S S by the sides of the valve. When the valve is opened

[038-105] upon the steam side, the cylinder receives steam through both ports at that

[038-106] end of the cylinder, and both ports at the other end of the cylinder are at

[038-107] the same time open to the eduction. The benefit of this species of valve

[038-108] is, that it gives the same opening of the valve that is given in ordinary

[038-109] engines, with half the amount of travel; or if three ports were made

[038-110] instead of two, then it would give the same area of opening that is given

[038-111] in common engines with one third the amount of travel. For direct acting

[038-112] screw engines this species of valve is now extensively used.

[038-113] 452. _Q._--Will you describe the configuration and mode of attachment of

[038-114] the eccentric by which the valve is moved?

[038-115] _A._--In marine engines, whether paddle or screw, if moving at a slow rate

[038-116] of speed, the eccentric is generally loose upon the shaft, for the purpose

[038-117] of backing, and is furnished with a back balance and catches, so that it

[038-118] may stand either in the position for going ahead, or in that for going

[038-119] astern. The body of the eccentric is of cast iron, and it is put on the

[038-120] shaft in two pieces. The halves are put together with rebated joints to

[038-121] keep them from separating laterally, and they are prevented from sliding

[038-122] out by round steel pins, each ground into both halves; square keys would

[038-123] probably be preferable to round pins in this arrangement, as the pins tend

[038-124] to wedge the jaws of the eccentric asunder. In some cases the halves of the

[038-125] eccentric are bolted together by means of flanges, which is, perhaps, the

[038-126] preferable practice. The eccentric hoop in marine and land engines is

[038-127] generally of brass; it is expedient to cast an oil cup on the eccentric

[038-128] hoop, and, where practicable, a pan should be placed beneath the eccentric

[038-129] for the reception of the oil droppings. The notch of the eccentric rod for

[038-130] the reception of the pin of the valve shaft is usually steeled, to prevent

[038-131] inconvenient wear; for when the sides of the notch wear, the valve movement

[038-132] is not only disturbed, but it is very difficult to throw the eccentric rod

[038-133] out of gear. It is found to be preferable, however, to fit this notch with

[038-134] a brass bush, for the wear is then less rapid, and it is an easy thing to

[038-135] replace this bush with another when it becomes worn. The eccentric catches

[038-136] of the kind usually employed in marine engines, sometimes break off at the

[038-137] first bolt hole, and it is preferable to have a bolt in advance of the

[038-138] catch face, or to have a hoop encircling the shaft with the catches welded

[038-139] on it, the hoop itself being fixed by bolts or a key. This hoop may either

[038-140] be put on before the cranks in one piece or afterwards in two pieces.

[038-141] 453. _Q._--Are such eccentrics used in direct acting screw engines?

[038-142] _A._--No; direct acting screw engines are usually fitted with the link

[038-143] motion and two fixed eccentrics.

[038-144] AIR PUMP AND CONDENSER.

[038-145] 454. _Q._--What are the details of the air pump?

[038-146] _A._--The air pump bucket and valves are all of brass in modern marine

[038-147] engines, and the chamber of the pump is lined with copper, or made wholly

[038-148] of brass, whereby a single boring suffices. When a copper lining is used,

[038-149] the pump is first bored out, and a bent sheet of copper is introduced,

[038-150] which is made accurately to fill the place, by hammering the copper on the

[038-151] inside. Air pump rods of Muntz's metal or copper are much used. Iron rods

[038-152] covered with brass are generally wasted away where the bottom cone fits

[038-153] into the bucket eye, and if the casing be at all porous, the water will

[038-154] insinuate itself between the casing and the rod and eat away the iron. If

[038-155] iron rods covered with brass be used, the brass casing should come some

[038-156] distance into the bucket eye; the cutter should be of brass, and a brass

[038-157] washer should cover the under side of the eye, so as to defend the end of

[038-158] the rod from the salt water. Rods of Muntz's metal are probably on the

[038-159] whole to be preferred. It is a good practice to put a nut on the top of the

[038-160] rod, to secure it more firmly in the cross head eye, where that plan can be

[038-161] conveniently adopted. The part of the rod which fits into the cross head

[038-162] eye should have more taper when made of copper or brass, than when made of

[038-163] iron; as, if the taper be small, the rod may get staved into the eye,

[038-164] whereby its detachment will be difficult.
