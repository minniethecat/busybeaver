[001-013] PREFACE

[001-014] TO THE FOURTH EDITION.

[001-015] For some years past a new edition of this work has been called for, but I

[001-016] was unwilling to allow a new edition to go forth with all the original

[001-017] faults of the work upon its head, and I have been too much engaged in the

[001-018] practical construction of steam ships and steam engines to find time for

[001-019] the thorough revision which I knew the work required. At length, however, I

[001-020] have sufficiently disengaged myself from these onerous pursuits to

[001-021] accomplish this necessary revision; and I now offer the work to the public,

[001-022] with the confidence that it will be found better deserving of the favorable

[001-023] acceptation and high praise it has already received. There are very few

[001-024] errors, either of fact or of inference, in the early editions, which I have

[001-025] had to correct; but there are many omissions which I have had to supply,

[001-026] and faults of arrangement and classification which I have had to rectify. I

[001-027] have also had to bring the information, which the work professes to afford,

[001-028] up to the present time, so as to comprehend the latest improvements.

[001-029] For the sake of greater distinctness the work is now divided into chapters.

[001-030] Some of these chapters are altogether new, and the rest have received such

[001-031] extensive additions and improvements as to make the book almost a new one.

[001-032] One purpose of my emendations has been to render my remarks intelligible to

[001-033] a tyro, as well as instructive to an advanced student. With this view, I

[001-034] have devoted the first chapter to a popular description of the Steam

[001-035] Engine--which all may understand who can understand anything--and in the

[001-036] subsequent gradations of progress I have been careful to set no object

[001-037] before the reader for the first time, of which the nature and functions are

[001-038] not simultaneously explained. The design I have proposed to myself, in the

[001-039] composition of this work, is to take a young lad who knows nothing of steam

[001-040] engines, and to lead him by easy advances up to the highest point of

[001-041] information I have myself attained; and it has been a pleasing duty to me

[001-042] to smooth for others the path which I myself found so rugged, and to

[001-043] impart, for the general good of mankind, the secrets which others have

[001-044] guarded with so much jealousy. I believe I am the first author who has

[001-045] communicated that practical information respecting the steam engine, which

[001-046] persons proposing to follow the business of an engineer desire to possess.

[001-047] My business has, therefore, been the rough business of a pioneer; and while

[001-048] hewing a road through the trackless forest, along which all might hereafter

[001-049] travel with ease, I had no time to attend to those minute graces of

[001-050] composition and petty perfection of arrangement and collocation, which are

[001-051] the attribute of the academic grove, or the literary parterre. I am,

[001-052] nevertheless, not insensible to the advantages of method and clear

[001-053] arrangement in any work professing to instruct mankind in the principles

[001-054] and practice of any art; and many of the changes introduced into the

[001-055] present edition of this work are designed to render it less exceptionable

[001-056] in this respect. The woodcuts now introduced into the work for the first

[001-057] time will, I believe, much increase its interest and utility; and upon the

[001-058] whole I am content to dismiss it into circulation, in the belief that those

[001-059] who peruse it attentively will obtain a more rapid and more practical

[001-060] acquaintance with the steam engine in its various applications, than they

[001-061] would be likely otherwise to acquire.

[001-062] I have only to add that I have prepared a sequel to the present work, in

[001-063] the shape of a Hand-Book of the Steam Engine, containing the whole of the

[001-064] rules given in the present work, illustrated by examples worked out at

[001-065] length, and also containing such useful tables and other data, as the

[001-066] engineer requires to refer to constantly in the course of his practice.

[001-067] This work may be bound up with the "Catechism," if desired, to which it is

[001-068] in fact a Key.

[001-069] I shall thankfully receive from engineers, either abroad or at home,

[001-070] accounts of any engines or other machinery, with which they may become

[001-071] familiar in their several localities; and I shall be happy, in my turn, to

[001-072] answer any inquiries on engineering subjects which fall within the compass

[001-073] of my information. If young engineers meet with any difficulty in their

[001-074] studies, I shall be happy to resolve it if I can; and they may communicate

[001-075] with me upon any such point without hesitation, in whatever quarter of the

[001-076] world they may happen to be.

[001-077] JOHN BOURNE.

[001-078] 9 BILLITER STREET,

[001-079] LONDON,

[001-080] _March 1st, 1856_.

[001-081] PREFACE

[001-082] TO THE FIFTH EDITION.

[001-083] The last edition of the present work, consisting of 3,500 copies, having

[001-084] been all sold off in about ten months, I now issue another edition, the

[001-085] demand for the work being still unabated. It affords, certainly, some

[001-086] presumption that a work in some measure supplies an ascertained want, when,

[001-087] though addressing only a limited circle--discoursing only of technical

[001-088] questions, and without any accident to stimulate it into notoriety,--it

[001-089] attains so large a circulation as the present work has reached. Besides

[001-090] being reprinted in America, it has been translated into German, French,

[001-091] Dutch, and I believe, into some other languages, so that there is, perhaps,

[001-092] not too much vanity in the inference that it has been found serviceable to

[001-093] those perusing it. I can with truth say, that the hope of rendering some

[001-094] service to mankind, in my day and generation, has been my chief inducement

[001-095] in writing it, and if this end is fulfilled, I have nothing further to

[001-096] desire.

[001-097] I regret that circumstances have prevented me from yet issuing the

[001-098] "Hand-Book" which I have had for some time in preparation, and to which, in

[001-099] my Preface of the last year, I referred. I hope to have sufficient leisure

[001-100] shortly, to give that and some other of my literary designs the necessary

[001-101] attention. Whatever may have been the other impediments to a more prolific

[001-102] authorship, certainly one of them has not been the coldness of the

[001-103] approbation with which my efforts have been received, since my past

[001-104] performances seem to me to have met with an appreciation far exceeding

[001-105] their deserts.

[001-106] JOHN BOURNE.

[001-107] _February 2d, 1857_.

[001-108] PUBLISHERS' NOTICE.

[001-109] In offering to the American public a reprint of a work on the Steam Engine

[001-110] so deservedly successful, and so long considered standard, the publishers

[001-111] have not thought it necessary that it should be an exact copy of the

[001-112] English edition; there were some details in which they thought it could be

[001-113] improved, and better adapted to the use of American engineers. On this

[001-114] account, the size of the page has been increased to a full 12mo, to admit

[001-115] of larger illustrations, which in the English edition are often on too

[001-116] small a scale; and some of the illustrations themselves have been supplied

[001-117] by others equally applicable, more recent, and to us more familiar

[001-118] examples. The first part of Chapter XI, devoted in the English edition to

[001-119] English portable and fixed agricultural engines, in this edition gives

[001-120] place entirely to illustrations from American practice, of steam engines as

[001-121] applied to different purposes, and of appliances and machines necessary to

[001-122] them. But with the exception of some of the illustrations and the

[001-123] description of them, and the correction of a few typographical errors, this

[001-124] edition is a faithful transcript of the latest English edition.

[001-209] MECHANICAL PRINCIPLES OF THE STEAM ENGINE.

[001-210] CLASSIFICATION OF ENGINES.

[001-211] 1. _Q._--What is meant by a vacuum?

[001-212] _A._--A vacuum means an empty space; a space in which there is neither

[001-213] water nor air, nor anything else that we know of.

[001-214] 2. _Q._--Wherein does a high pressure differ from a low pressure engine?

[001-215] _A._--In a high pressure engine the steam, after having pushed the piston

[001-216] to the end of the stroke, escapes into the atmosphere, and the impelling

[001-217] force is therefore that due to the difference between the pressure of the

[001-218] steam and the pressure of the atmosphere. In the condensing engine the

[001-219] steam, after having pressed the piston to the end of the stroke, passes

[001-220] into the condenser, in which a vacuum is maintained, and the impelling

[001-221] force is that due to the difference between the pressure of the steam above

[001-222] the piston, and the pressure of the vacuum beneath it, which is nothing;

[001-223] or, in other words, you have then the whole pressure of the steam urging

[001-224] the piston, consisting of the pressure shown by the safety-valve on the

[001-225] boiler, and the pressure of the atmosphere besides.

[001-226] 3. _Q._--In what way would you class the various kinds of condensing

[001-227] engines?

[001-228] _A._--Into single acting, rotative, and rotatory engines. Single acting

[001-229] engines are engines without a crank, such as are used for pumping water.

[001-230] Rotative engines are engines provided with a crank, by means of which a

[001-231] rotative motion is produced; and in this important class stand marine and

[001-232] mill engines, and all engines, indeed, in which the rectilinear motion of

[001-233] the piston is changed into a circular motion. In rotatory engines the steam

[001-234] acts at once in the production of circular motion, either upon a revolving

[001-235] piston or otherwise, but without the use of any intermediate mechanism,

[001-236] such as the crank, for deriving a circular from a rectilinear motion.

[001-237] Rotatory engines have not hitherto been very successful, so that only the

[001-238] single acting or pumping engine, and the double acting or rotative engine

[001-239] can be said to be in actual use. For some purposes, such, for example, as

[001-240] forcing air into furnaces for smelting iron, double acting engines are

[001-241] employed, which are nevertheless unfurnished with a crank; but engines of

[001-242] this kind are not sufficiently numerous to justify their classification as

[001-243] a distinct species, and, in general, those engines may be considered to be

[001-244] single acting, by which no rotatory motion is imparted.
