[005-011] FRICTION.

[005-012] 52. _Q._--What is friction?

[005-013] _A._--Friction is the resistance experienced when one body is rubbed upon

[005-014] another body, and is supposed to be the result of the natural attraction

[005-015] which bodies have for one another, and of the interlocking of the

[005-016] impalpable asperities upon the surfaces of all bodies, however smooth.

[005-017] There is, no doubt, some electrical action involved in its production, not

[005-018] yet recognized, nor understood; and it is perhaps traceable to the

[005-019] disturbance of the electrical equilibrium of the particles of the body

[005-020] owing to the condensation or change of figure which all bodies must

[005-021] experience when subjected to a strain. When motion in opposite directions

[005-022] is given to smooth surfaces, the minute asperities of one surface must

[005-023] mount upon those of the other, and both will be abraded and worn away, in

[005-024] which act power must be expended. The friction of smooth rubbing substances

[005-025] is less when the composition of those substances is different, than when it

[005-026] is the same, the particles being supposed to interlock less when the

[005-027] opposite prominences or asperities are not coincident.

[005-028] 53. _Q._--Does friction increase with the extent of rubbing surface?

[005-029] _A._--No; the friction, so long as there is no violent heating or abrasion,

[005-030] is simply in the proportion of the pressure keeping the surfaces together,

[005-031] or nearly so. It is, therefore, an obvious advantage to have the bearing

[005-032] surfaces of steam engines as large as possible, as there is no increase of

[005-033] friction by extending the surface, while there is a great increase in the

[005-034] durability. When the bearings of an engine are made too small, they very

[005-035] soon wear out.

[005-036] 54. _Q._--Does friction increase in the same ratio as velocity?

[005-037] _A._--No; friction does not increase with the velocity at all, if the

[005-038] friction over a given amount of surface be considered; but it increases as

[005-039] the velocity, if the comparison be made with the time during which the

[005-040] friction acts. Thus the friction of each stroke of a piston is the same,

[005-041] whether it makes 20 strokes in the minute, or 40: in the latter case,

[005-042] however, there are twice the number of strokes made, so that, though the

[005-043] friction per stroke is the same, the friction per minute is doubled. The

[005-044] friction, therefore, of any machine per hour varies as the velocity, though

[005-045] the friction per revolution remains, at all ordinary velocities, the same.

[005-046] Of excessive velocities we have not sufficient experience to enable us to

[005-047] state with confidence whether the same law continues to operate among them.

[005-048] 55. _Q._--Can you give any approximate statement of the force expended in

[005-049] overcoming friction?

[005-050] _A._--It varies with the nature of the rubbing bodies. The friction of iron

[005-051] sliding upon iron, has generally been taken at about one tenth of the

[005-052] pressure, when the surfaces are oiled and then wiped again, so that no film

[005-053] of oil is interposed. The friction of iron rubbing upon brass has generally

[005-054] been taken at about one eleventh of the pressure under the same

[005-055] circumstances; but in machines in actual operation, where a film of some

[005-056] lubricating material is interposed between the rubbing surfaces, it is not

[005-057] more than one third of this amount or 1/33d of the weight. While this,

[005-058] however, is the average result, the friction is a good deal less in some

[005-059] cases. Mr. Southern, in some experiments upon the friction of the axle of a

[005-060] grindstone--an account of which may be found in the 65th volume of the

[005-061] Philosophical Transactions--found the friction to amount to less than

[005-062] 1/40th of the weight; and Mr. Wood, in some experiments upon the friction

[005-063] of locomotive axles, found that by ample lubrication the friction may be

[005-064] made as little as 1/60th of the weight. In some experiments upon the

[005-065] friction of shafts by Mr. G. Rennie, he found that with a pressure of from

[005-066] 1 to 5 cwt. the friction did not exceed 1/39th of the pressure when tallow

[005-067] was the unguent employed; with soft soap it became 1/34th. The fact appears

[005-068] to be that the amount of the resistance denominated friction depends, in a

[005-069] great measure, upon the nature of the unguent employed, and in certain

[005-070] cases the viscidity of the unguent may occasion a greater retardation than

[005-071] the resistance caused by the attrition. In watchwork therefore, and other

[005-072] fine mechanism, it is necessary both to keep the bearing surfaces small,

[005-073] and to employ a thin and limpid oil for the purpose of lubrication, for the

[005-074] resistance caused by the viscidity of the unguent increases with the amount

[005-075] of surface, and the amount of surface is relatively greater in the smaller

[005-076] class of works.

[005-077] 56. _Q._--Is a very thin unguent preferable also for the larger class of

[005-078] bearings?

[005-079] _A._--The nature of the unguent, proper for different bearings, appears to

[005-080] depend in a great measure upon the amount of the pressure to which the

[005-081] bearings are subjected,--the hardest unguents being best where the pressure

[005-082] is greatest. The function of lubricating substances is to prevent the

[005-083] rubbing surfaces from coming into contact, whereby abrasion would be

[005-084] produced, and unguents are effectual in this respect in the proportion of

[005-085] their viscidity; but if the viscidity of the unguent be greater than what

[005-086] suffices to keep the surfaces asunder, an additional resistance will be

[005-087] occasioned; and the nature of the unguent selected should always have

[005-088] reference, therefore, to the size of the rubbing surfaces, or to the

[005-089] pressure per square inch upon them. With oil the friction appears to be a

[005-090] minimum when the pressure on the surface of a bearing is about 90 lbs. per

[005-091] square inch. The friction from too small a surface increases twice as

[005-092] rapidly as the friction from too large a surface, added to which, the

[005-093] bearing, when the surface is too small, wears rapidly away.

[005-094] 57. _Q._--Has not M. Morin, in France, made some very complete experiments

[005-095] to determine the friction of surfaces of different kinds sliding upon one

[005-096] another?

[005-097] _A._--He has; but the result does not differ materially from what is stated

[005-098] above, though, upon the whole, M. Morin, found the resistance due to

[005-099] friction to be somewhat greater than it has been found to be by various

[005-100] other engineers. When the surfaces were merely wiped with a greasy cloth,

[005-101] but had no film of lubricating material interposed, the friction of brass

[005-102] upon cast iron he found to be .107, or about 1/10th of the load, which was

[005-103] also the friction of cast iron upon oak. But when a film of lubricating

[005-104] material was interposed, he found that the friction was the same whether

[005-105] the surfaces were wood on metal, wood on wood, metal on wood, or metal on

[005-106] metal; and the amount of the friction in such case depended chiefly on the

[005-107] nature of the unguent. With a mixture of hog's lard and olive oil

[005-108] interposed between the surfaces, the friction was usually from 1/12th to

[005-109] 1/14th of the load, but in some cases it was only 1/20th of the load.

[005-110] 58. _Q._--May water be made to serve for purposes of lubrication?

[005-111] _A._--Yes, water will answer very well if the surface be very large

[005-112] relatively with the pressure; and in screw vessels where the propeller

[005-113] shaft passes through a long pipe at the stern, the stuffing box is

[005-114] purposely made a little leaky. The small leakage of water into the vessel

[005-115] which is thus occasioned, keeps the screw shaft in this situation always

[005-116] wet, and this is all the lubrication which this bearing requires or

[005-117] obtains.

[005-118] 59. _Q._--What is the utmost pressure which may be employed without heating

[005-119] when oil is the lubricating material?

[005-120] _A._--That will depend upon the velocity. When the pressure exceeds 800

[005-121] lbs. per square inch, however, upon the section of the bearing in a

[005-122] direction parallel with the axis, then the oil will be forced out and the

[005-123] bearing will necessarily heat.

[005-124] 60. _Q._--But, with, a given velocity, can you tell the limit of pressure

[005-125] which will be safe in practice; or with a given pressure, can you tell the

[005-126] limit of velocity?

[005-127] _A._--Yes; that may be done by the following empirical rule, which has been

[005-128] derived from observations made upon bearings of different sizes and moving

[005-129] with different velocities. Divide the number 70,000 by the velocity of the

[005-130] surface of the bearing in feet per minute. The quotient will be the number

[005-131] of pounds per square inch of section in the line of the axis that may be

[005-132] put upon the bearing. Or, if we divide 70,000 by the number of pounds per

[005-133] square inch of section, then the quotient will be the velocity in feet per

[005-134] minute at which the circumference of the bearing may work.

[005-135] 61. _Q._--The number of square inches upon which the pressure is reckoned,

[005-136] is not the circumference of the bearing multiplied by its length, but the

[005-137] diameter of the bearing multiplied by its length?

[005-138] _A._--Precisely so, it will be the diameter multiplied by the length of the

[005-139] bearing.

[005-169] STRENGTH OF MATERIALS AND STRAINS SUBSISTING IN MACHINES.

[005-170] 63. _Q._--In what way are the strengths of the different parts of a steam

[005-171] engine determined?

[005-172] _A._--By reference to the amount of the strain or pressure to which they

[005-173] are subjected, and to the cohesive strength of the iron or other material

[005-174] of which they are composed. The strains subsisting in engines are usually

[005-175] characterized as tensile, crushing, twisting, breaking, and shearing

[005-176] strains; but they may be all resolved into strains of extension and strains

[005-177] of compression; and by the power of the materials to resist these two

[005-178] strains, will their practical strength be measurable.

[005-179] 64. _Q._--What are the ultimate strengths of the malleable and cast iron,

[005-180] brass, and other materials employed in the construction of engines?

[005-181] _A._--The tensile and crushing strengths of any given material are by no

[005-182] means the same. The tensile strength, or strength when extended, of good

[005-183] bar iron is about 60,000 lbs., or nearly 27 tons per square inch of

[005-184] section; and the tensile strength of cast iron is about 15,000 lbs., or say

[005-185] 6 3/4 to 7 tons per square inch of section. These are the weights which are

[005-186] required to break them. The crushing strain of cast iron, however, is about

[005-187] 100,000 lbs., or 44 1/2 tons; whereas the crushing strength of malleable

[005-188] iron is not more than 27,000 lbs., or 12 tons, per square inch of section,

[005-189] and indeed it is generally less than this. The ultimate tensile strength,

[005-190] therefore, of malleable iron is four times greater than that of cast iron,

[005-191] but the crushing strength of cast iron is between three and four times

[005-192] greater than that of wrought iron. It may be stated, in round numbers, that

[005-193] the tensile strength of malleable iron is twice greater than its crushing

[005-194] strength; or, in other words, that it will take twice the strain to break a

[005-195] bar of malleable iron by drawing it asunder endways, than will cripple it

[005-196] by forcing it together endways like a pillar; whereas a bar of cast iron

[005-197] will be drawn asunder with one sixth of the force that will be required to

[005-198] break or cripple it when forced together endways like a pillar.
