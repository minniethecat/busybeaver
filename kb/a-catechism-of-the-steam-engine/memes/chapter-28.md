[028-001] 340. _Q._--By what considerations do you determine the dimensions of the

[028-002] fly wheel of an engine?

[028-003] _A._--By a reference to the power generated, each half stroke of the

[028-004] engine, and the number of half strokes that are necessary to give to the

[028-005] fly wheel its standard velocity, supposing the whole power devoted to that

[028-006] object. In practice the power resident in the fly varies from 2-1/2 to 6

[028-007] times that generated each half stroke; and if the weight of the wheel be

[028-008] equal to the pressure on the piston, its velocity must be such as it would

[028-009] acquire by falling through a height equal to from 2-1/2 to 6 times the

[028-010] stroke, according to the purpose for which the engine is intended. If a

[028-011] very equable motion is required, a heavier or swifter fly wheel must be

[028-012] employed.

[028-013] 341. _Q._--What is Boulton and Watt's rule for fly wheels?

[028-014] _A._--Their rule is one which under any given circumstances fixes the

[028-015] sectional area of the fly wheel rim, and it is as follows:--multiply 44,000

[028-016] times the square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches by the length of

[028-017] the stroke in feet, and divide this product by the product of the square of

[028-018] the number of revolutions of the fly wheel per minute, multiplied by the

[028-019] cube of its diameter in feet. The quotient is the area of section of the

[028-020] fly wheel rim in square inches.

[028-021] STRENGTHS OF LAND ENGINES.

[028-022] 342. _Q._--Can you give a rule for telling the proper thickness of the

[028-023] cylinders of steam engines?

[028-024] _A._--In low pressure engines the thickness of metal of the cylinder, in

[028-025] engines of a medium size, should be about 1/40th of the diameter of the

[028-026] cylinder, which, with a pressure of steam of 20 lbs. above the atmosphere,

[028-027] will occasion a strain of only 400 Lbs. per square inch of section of the

[028-028] metal; the thickness of the metal of the trunnion bearings of oscillating

[028-029] engines should be 1/32d of the diameter of the cylinder, and the breadth of

[028-030] the bearing should be about half its diameter. In high pressure engines the

[028-031] thickness of the cylinder should be about 1/16th its diameter, which, with

[028-032] a pressure of steam of 80 lbs. upon the square inch, will occasion a strain

[028-033] of 640 lbs. upon the square inch of section of the metal; and the thickness

[028-034] of the metal of the trunnion bearings of high pressure oscillating engines

[028-035] should be 1/13th of the diameter of the cylinder. The strength, however, is

[028-036] not the sole consideration in proportioning cylinders, for they must be

[028-037] made of a certain thickness, however small the pressure is within them,

[028-038] that they may not be too fragile, and will stand boring. While, also, an

[028-039] engine of 40 inches diameter would be about one inch thick, the thickness

[028-040] would not be quite two inches in an 80 inch cylinder. In fact there will be

[028-041] a small constant added to the thickness for all diameters, which will be

[028-042] relatively larger the smaller the cylinders become. In the cylinders of

[028-043] Penn's 12 horse power engines, the diameter of cylinder being 21-1/2

[028-044] inches, the thickness of the metal is 9/16ths: in Penn's 40 inch cylinders,

[028-045] the thickness is 1 inch, and in the engines of the Ripon, Pottinger, and

[028-046] Indus, by Messrs. Miller, Ravenhill and Co., with cylinders 76 inches

[028-047] diameter, the thickness of the metal is 1-11/16. These are all oscillating

[028-048] engines.

[028-049] 343. _Q._--What is the proportion of the piston rod?

[028-050] _A._--The diameter of the piston rod is usually made 1/10th of the diameter

[028-051] of the cylinder, or the sectional area of the piston rod is 1/100th of the

[028-052] area of the cylinder. This proportion, however, is not applicable to

[028-053] locomotive, or even fast moving marine engines. In locomotive engines the

[028-054] piston rod is made 1/7th of the diameter of the cylinder, and it is obvious

[028-055] that where the pressure on the piston is great, the piston rod must be

[028-056] larger than when the pressure on the piston is small.

[028-057] 344. _Q._--What are the proper dimensions of the main links of a land beam

[028-058] engine?

[028-059] _A._--The sectional area of the main links in land beam engines is 1/113th

[028-060] of the area of the cylinder, and the length of the main links is usually

[028-061] half the length of the stroke.

[028-062] 345. _Q._--What are the dimensions of the connecting rod of a land engine?

[028-063] _A._--In land engines the connecting rod is usually of cast iron with a

[028-064] cruciform section: the breadth across the arms of the cross is about 1/20th

[028-065] of the length of the rod, the sectional area at the centre 1/28th of the

[028-066] area of the cylinder, and at the ends 1/35th of the area of the cylinder:

[028-067] the length of the rod is usually 3-1/2 times the length of the stroke. It

[028-068] is preferable, however, to make the connecting rod of malleable iron, and

[028-069] then the dimensions will be those proper for marine engines.

[028-070] 346. _Q._--What was Mr. Watt's rule for the connecting rod?

[028-071] _A._--Some of his connecting rods were of iron and some of wood. To

[028-072] determine the thickness when of wood, multiply the square of the diameter

[028-073] of the cylinder in inches by the length of the stroke in feet, and divide

[028-074] the product by 24. Extract the fourth root of the quotient, which is the

[028-075] thickness in inches. For iron the rule is the same, only the divisor was

[028-076] 57.6 instead of 24.

[028-077] 347. _Q._--What are the dimensions of the end studs of a land engine beam?

[028-078] _A._--In low pressure engines the diameter of the end studs of the engine

[028-079] beam are usually made 1/9th of the diameter of the cylinder when of cast

[028-080] iron, and 1/10th when of wrought iron, which gives a load with low steam of

[028-081] about 500 lbs. per circular inch of transverse section; but a larger size

[028-082] is preferable, as with large bearings the brasses do not wear so rapidly

[028-083] and the straps are not so likely to be burst by the bearings becoming oval.

[028-084] These sizes, as also those which immediately follow, suppose the pressure

[028-085] on the piston to be 18 lbs. per circular inch.

[028-086] 348. _Q._--How is the strength of a cast iron gudgeon computed?

[028-087] _A._--To find the proper size of a cast iron gudgeon adapted to sustain any

[028-088] given weight:--multiply the weight in lbs. by the intended length of

[028-089] bearing expressed in terms of the diameter; divide the product by 500, and

[028-090] extract the square root of the quotient, which is the diameter in inches.

[028-091] 349. _Q._--What was Mr. Watt's rule for the strength of gudgeons?

[028-092] _A._--Supposing the gudgeon to be square, then, to ascertain the thickness,

[028-093] multiply the weight resting on the gudgeon by the distance between the

[028-094] trunnions, and divide the product by 333. Extract the cube root of the

[028-095] quotient, which is the thickness in inches.

[028-096] 350. _Q._--How do you find the proper strength for the cast iron beam of a

[028-097] land engine?

[028-098] _A._--If the force acting at the end of an engine beam be taken at 18 lbs.

[028-099] per circular inch of the piston, then the force acting at the middle will

[028-100] be 36 lbs. per circular inch of the piston, and the proper strength of the

[028-101] beam at the centre will be found by the following rule:--divide the weight

[028-102] in lbs. acting at the centre by 250, and multiply the quotient by the

[028-103] distance between the extreme centres. To find the depth, the breadth being

[028-104] given:--divide this product by the breadth in inches, and extract the

[028-105] square root of the quotient, which is the depth. The depth of a land engine

[028-106] beam at the ends is usually made one third of the depth at the centre (the

[028-107] depth at the centre being equal to the diameter of the cylinder in the case

[028-108] of low pressure engines), while the length is made equal to three times the

[028-109] length of the stroke, and the mean thickness 1/108th of the length--the

[028-110] width of the edge bead being about three times the thickness of the web. In

[028-111] many modern engines the force acting at the end of the beam is more than 18

[028-112] lbs. per circular inch of the piston, but the above rules are still

[028-113] applicable by taking an imaginary cylinder with an area larger in the

[028-114] proportion of the larger pressure.

[028-115] 351. _Q._--What was Mr. Watt's rule for the main beams of his engines?

[028-116] _A._--Some of those beams were of wood and some of cast iron. The wood

[028-117] beams were so proportioned that the thickness was 1/58th of the

[028-118] circumference, and the depth 1/375. The side of the beam, supposing it

[028-119] square, was found by multiplying the diameter of the cylinder by the length

[028-120] of the stroke, and extracting the cube root of the quotient, which will be

[028-121] the depth or thickness of the beam. This rule allows a beam 16 feet long to

[028-122] bend 1/8th of an inch, and a beam 32 feet long to bend 1/4 of an inch. For

[028-123] cast iron beams the square of the diameter of the cylinder, multiplied by

[028-124] the length between the centres, is equal to the square of the depth,

[028-125] multiplied by the thickness.

[028-126] 352. _Q._--What law does the strength of beams and shafts follow?

[028-127] _A._--In the case of beams subjected to a breaking force, the strength with

[028-128] any given cohesion of the material will be proportional to the breadth,

[028-129] multiplied by the square of the depth; and in the case of revolving shafts

[028-130] exposed to a twisting strain, the strength with any given cohesive power of

[028-131] the material will be as the cube of the diameter.

[028-132] 353. _Q._--How is the strength of a cast iron shaft to resist torsion

[028-133] determined?

[028-134] _A._--Experiments upon the force requisite to twist off cast iron necks

[028-135] show that if the cube of the diameter of neck in inches be multiplied by

[028-136] 880, the product will be the force of torsion which will twist them off

[028-137] when acting at 6 inches radius; on this fact the following rule is founded:

[028-138] To find the diameter of a cast iron fly wheel shaft:--multiply the square

[028-139] of the diameter of the cylinder in inches, by the length of the crank in

[028-140] inches, and extract the cube root of the product, which multiply by 0.3025,

[028-141] and the result will be the proper diameter of the shaft in inches at the

[028-142] smallest part, when of cast iron.

[028-143] 354. _Q._--What was Mr. Watt's rule for the necks of his crank shafts?

[028-144] _A._--Taking the pressure on the piston at 12 lbs. pressure on the square

[028-145] inch, and supposing this force to be applied at one foot radius, divide the

[028-146] total pressure of the piston reduced to 1 foot of radius by 31.4, and

[028-147] extract the cube root of the quotient, which is the diameter of the shaft:

[028-148] or extract the cube root of 13.7 times the number of cubic feet of steam

[028-149] required to make one revolution, which is also the diameter of the shaft.

[028-150] 355. _Q._--Can you give any rule for the strength of the teeth of wheels?

[028-151] _A._--To find the proper dimensions for the teeth of a cast iron wheel:--

[028-152] multiply the diameter of the pitch circle in feet by the number of

[028-153] revolutions to be made per minute, and reserve the product for a divisor;

[028-154] multiply the number of _actual_ horses power to be transmitted by 240, and

[028-155] divide the product by the above divisor, which will give the strength. If

[028-156] the pitch be given to find the breadth, divide the above strength by the

[028-157] square of the pitch in inches; or if the breadth be given, then to find the

[028-158] pitch divide the strength by the breadth in inches, and extract the square

[028-159] root of the quotient, which is the proper pitch in inches. The length of

[028-160] the teeth is usually about 5/8ths of the pitch. Pinions to work

[028-161] satisfactorily should not have less than 30 or 40 teeth, and where the

[028-162] speed exceeds 220 feet in the minute, the teeth of the larger wheel should

[028-163] be of wood, made a little thicker, to keep the strength unimpaired.
