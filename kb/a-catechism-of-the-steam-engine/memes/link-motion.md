The **link motion** is a [[valve]] gear mechanism that controls steam admission to the [[cylinder]] by combining the motion of two [[eccentric|eccentrics]] through a slotted link. Invented by William Williams and improved by Robert [[george-stephenson|Stephenson]], it enables the engine driver to vary the point of [[cut-off|steam cut-off]], reverse the direction of rotation, and achieve [[expansion-valve|expansive working]] — all from a single control lever. It became the most widely used valve gear on locomotives and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s of the nineteenth century.

## Principle of operation

The link motion interposes a curved slotted link between the eccentric rods and the valve rod:

> [016-084] What is now employed extensively, is what is termed "the link motion." It is represented in the annexed figure, fig. 35, where _e_ is the valve rod, which is attached by a pin to an open curved link susceptible of being moved up and down by the bell-crank lever.

Two eccentrics, set at opposite positions on the [[crank-shaft|crankshaft]], impart motion to each end of the link. By raising or lowering the link relative to the valve rod, the effective motion transmitted to the [[slide-valve]] can be varied continuously — from full forward gear, through mid-gear (where the valve barely moves), to full reverse.

## Expansive working

One of the link motion's key advantages is the ability to achieve variable expansion without a separate [[expansion-valve]]:

> [016-125] When they are fitted with the link motion, as they nearly all are, a very good expansive action can be obtained by giving a suitable adjustment to it, without employing an expansion valve at all.

> [016-129] Most of the modern engines trust for their expansive working to the link motion and the [[throttle-valve|throttle valve]].

By operating in a shorter cut-off position (achieved by moving the link toward mid-gear), steam is admitted to the cylinder for only a portion of the piston stroke, allowing the steam to expand and do additional work before [[eduction|exhaust]].

## Historical significance

Before the link motion, reversing and [[expansion-valve|expansive working]] required separate mechanisms:

> [045-045] In the engines in use before the introduction of the link motion, the forks of the eccentric rod were of steel, and the length of the eccentric rod was the distance between the centre of the crank axle and the centre of the valve shaft; but in modern engines the use of the link motion is universal.

The link motion consolidated control of the engine into a single reversing lever, greatly simplifying operation. It was adopted first on locomotives and later on [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s and stationary engines.

## Construction

> [058-015] The link of the link motion is formed of wrought iron. It is hung by a link from a pin attached to the framing; and instead of being susceptible of upward and downward motion, as in the case of the link represented in fig. 57, a rod connecting the valve rod with the movable block in the link, is susceptible of this motion, whereby the same result is arrived at as if the link were moved and the block was stationary.

Two variants existed: one in which the link itself was raised and lowered, and another in which the link remained stationary while the die block moved within it. Both achieved the same functional result of varying the valve travel and cut-off point.
