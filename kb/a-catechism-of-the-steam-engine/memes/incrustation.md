**Incrustation** (commonly called boiler scale or furring) is the hard deposit that forms on the internal heating surfaces of a [[boiler]] when dissolved mineral salts — principally from evaporated seawater — precipitate out of the boiler water. As scale accumulates on the [[flue|flues]] and furnace plates, it acts as an insulator, reducing heat transfer efficiency and causing the metal to overheat, which can lead to dangerous plate failure.

## Formation of incrustation

Incrustation forms when boiler water becomes concentrated with dissolved salts through continuous evaporation. In marine boilers fed with seawater this problem is particularly acute:

> [025-150] If the water in the boiler be suffered to become too salt, an incrustation of salt will take place upon the furnace plates and flues, which will cause them to become red hot, and may burn them.

> [032-026] What is the cause of incrustations upon the flues? It arises from the precipitation of the salt and other impurities contained in the water upon the heating surface, where they are left in the condition of a solid incrustation.

## Prevention — brining

The standard preventive measure in marine boilers is "brining" — periodically blowing down a portion of the concentrated boiler water and replacing it with fresh feedwater:

> [032-032] How may incrustation be prevented? By the process of what is termed _brining_ — that is, by blowing out from the boiler a certain portion of the concentrated water which holds the salt in solution, and supplying its place with fresh feed water.

The [[salinometer]] is used to measure the salt concentration and determine when brining is necessary:

> [032-061] If the water be blown out of the boiler in the proportion of about one-third of the quantity evaporated, and be replaced by fresh sea water, no injurious incrustation will take place.

## Consequences of incrustation

The insulating effect of scale is severe. Even a thin layer can cause dangerous overheating:

> [065-148] A scale of no very great thickness will cause a very material reduction in the evaporative power of a boiler, and will occasion a considerable waste of fuel to maintain the same amount of evaporation.

> [066-013] In a boiler in which the brining process has been neglected, an incrustation five-eighths of an inch thick has been known to form, causing the furnace plates to become red hot.

> [066-024] Soft incrustations being, of course, the most destructive to the boiler plates, as they offer the greatest obstruction to the transmission of the heat.

## Scale removal

| Method | Application | Effectiveness |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Brining (preventive) | Marine boilers, continuous | Prevents buildup if done regularly |
| Mechanical scraping | All boilers | Effective for hard scale |
| Tube brushing | Tubular boilers | Essential routine maintenance |
| Chemical cleaning | Land boilers | Dissolves scale without dismantling |
| Chipping and hammering | Severe cases | Risk of damaging plates |

> [066-025] Drawing out the tubes removes the incrustation; but the tubes should still be examined and the scale removed more completely after withdrawal.
