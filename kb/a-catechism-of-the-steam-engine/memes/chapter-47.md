[047-006] 538. _Q._--Does the resistance produced by this friction increase with the

[047-007] velocity?

[047-008] _A._--It increases nearly as the square of the velocity. At two nautical

[047-009] miles per hour, the thrust necessary to overcome the friction varies as the

[047-010] 1.823 power of the velocity; and at eight nautical miles per hour, the

[047-011] thrust necessary to overcome the friction varies as the 1.713 power of the

[047-012] velocity. It is hardly proper, perhaps, to call this resistance by the name

[047-013] of friction; it is partly, perhaps mainly, due to the viscidity or adhesion

[047-014] of the water.

[047-015] 539. _Q._--Perhaps at high velocities this resistance may become less?

[047-016] _A_.--That appears very probable. It may happen that at high velocities the

[047-017] adhesion is overcome, so that the water is dragged off the vessel, and the

[047-018] friction thereafter follows the law which obtains in the case of solid

[047-019] bodies. But any such conclusion is mere speculation, since no experiments

[047-020] illustrative of this question have yet been made.

[047-021] 540. _Q._--Will a vessel experience more resistance in moving in salt water

[047-022] than in moving in fresh?

[047-023] _A._--If the immersion be the same in both cases a vessel will experience

[047-024] more resistance in moving in salt water than in moving in fresh, on account

[047-025] of the greater density of salt water; but as the notation is proportionably

[047-026] greater in the salt water the resistance will be the same with the same

[047-027] weight carried.

[047-028] 541. _Q._--Discarding for the present the subject of friction, and looking

[047-029] merely to the question of bow and stern resistance, in what manner should

[047-030] the hull of a vessel be formed so as to make these resistances a minimum?

[047-031] _A._--The hull should be so formed that the water, instead of being away

[047-032] driven forcibly from the bow, is opened gradually, so that every particle

[047-033] of water may be moved aside slowly at first, and then faster, like the ball

[047-034] of a pendulum, until it reaches the position of the midship frame, at which

[047-035] point it will have come to a state of rest, and then again, like a

[047-036] returning pendulum, vibrate back in the same way, until it comes to rest at

[047-037] the stern. It is not difficult to describe mechanically the line which the

[047-038] water should pursue. If an endless web of paper be put into uniform motion,

[047-039] and a pendulum carrying a pencil or brush be hung in front of it, then such

[047-040] pendulum will trace on the paper the proper water line of the ship, or the

[047-041] line which the water should pursue in order that no power may be lost

[047-042] except that which is lost in friction. It is found, however, in practice,

[047-043] that vessels formed with water lines on this principle are not much

[047-044] superior to ordinary vessels in the facility with which they pass through

[047-045] the water: and this points to the conclusion that in ordinary vessels of

[047-046] good form, the amount of power consumed in overcoming the resistance due to

[047-047] the wave at the bow and the partial vacuity at the stern is not so great as

[047-048] has heretofore been supposed, and that, in fact, the main resistance is

[047-049] that due to the friction.

[047-050] [1] This statement supposes that there is no difference of level between

[047-051] the water at the bow and the water at the stern. In the experiments on the

[047-052] steamer Pelican, the resistance was found to vary, as the 2.28th power of

[047-053] the velocity, but the deviation from the recognized law was imputed to a

[047-054] difference in the level of the water at the bow and stern.

[047-055] EXPERIMENTS ON THE RESISTANCE OF VESSELS.

[047-056] 542. _Q._--Have experiments been made to determine the resistance which

[047-057] steam vessels experience in moving through the waters?

[047-058] _A._--Experiments have been made both to determine the relative resistance

[047-059] of different classes of vessels, and also the absolute resistance in pounds

[047-060] or tons. The first experiments made upon this subject were conducted by

[047-061] Messrs. Boulton and Watt, and they have been numerous, long continued, and

[047-062] carefully performed. These experiments were made upon paddle vessels.

[047-063] 543. _Q._--Will you recount the chief results of these experiments?

[047-064] _A._--The purpose of the experiments was to establish a coefficient of

[047-065] performance, which with any given class of vessel would enable the speed,

[047-066] which would be obtained with any given power, to be readily predicted. This

[047-067] coefficient was obtained by multiplying the cube of the velocity of the

[047-068] vessels experimented upon, in miles per hour, by the sectional area of the

[047-069] immersed midship section in square feet, and dividing by the numbers of

[047-070] nominal horses power, and this coefficient will be large in the proportion

[047-071] of the goodness of the shape of the vessel.

[047-072] 544. _Q._--How many experiments were made altogether?

[047-073] _A._--There were five different sets of experiments on five different

[047-074] classes of vessels. The first set of experiments was made in 1828, upon the

[047-075] vessels Caledonia, Diana, Eclipse, Kingshead, Moordyke, and Eagle-vessels

[047-076] of a similar form and all with square bilges and flat floors; and the

[047-077] result was to establish the number 925 as the coefficient of performance of

[047-078] such vessels. The second set of experiments was made upon the superior

[047-079] vessels Venus, Swiftsure, Dasher, Arrow, Spitfire, Fury, Albion, Queen,

[047-080] Dart, Hawk, Margaret, and Hero-all vessels having flat floors and round

[047-081] bilges, where the coefficient became 1160. The third set of experiments was

[047-082] made upon the vessels Lightning, Meteor, James Watt, Cinderella, Navy

[047-083] Meteor, Crocodile, Watersprite, Thetis, Dolphin, Wizard, Escape, and

[047-084] Dragon-all vessels with rising floors and round bilges, and the coefficient

[047-085] of performance was found to be 1430. The fourth set of experiments was made

[047-086] in 1834, upon the vessels Magnet, Dart, Eclipse, Flamer, Firefly, Ferret,

[047-087] and Monarch, when the coefficient of performance was found to be 1580. The

[047-088] fifth set of experiments was made upon the Red Rover, City of Canterbury,

[047-089] Herne, Queen, and Prince of Wales, and in the case of those vessels the

[047-090] coefficient rose to 2550. The velocity of any of these vessels, with any

[047-091] power or sectional area, may be ascertained by multiplying the coefficient

[047-092] of its class by the nominal horse power, dividing by the sectional area in

[047-093] square feet, and extracting the cube root of the quotient, which will be

[047-094] the velocity in miles per hour; or the number of nominal horse power

[047-095] requisite for the accomplishment of any required speed may be ascertained

[047-096] by multiplying the cube of the required velocity in miles per hour, by the

[047-097] sectional area in square feet, and dividing by the coefficient: the

[047-098] quotient is the number of nominal horse power requisite to realize the

[047-099] speed.

[047-100] 545. _Q._--Seeing, however, that the nominal power does not represent an

[047-101] invariable amount of dynamical efficiency, would it not be better to make

[047-102] the comparison with reference to the actual power?

[047-103] _A._--In the whole of the experiments recited, except in the case of one or

[047-104] two of the last, the pressure of steam in the boiler varied between 2-3/4

[047-105] lbs. and 4 lbs. per square inch, and the effective pressure on the piston

[047-106] varied between 11 lbs. and 13 lbs. per square inch, so that the average

[047-107] ratio of the nominal to the actual power may be easily computed; but it

[047-108] will be preferable to state the nominal power of some of the vessels, and

[047-109] their actual power as ascertained by experiment.

[047-110] 546. _Q._--Then state this.

[047-111] _A._--Of the Eclipse, the nominal power was 76, and the actual power 144.4

[047-112] horses; of the Arrow, the nominal power was 60, and the actual 119.5;

[047-113] Spitfire, nominal 40, actual 64; Fury, nominal 40, actual 65.6; Albion,

[047-114] nominal 80, actual 135.4; Dart, nominal 100, actual 152.4; Hawk, nominal

[047-115] 40, actual 73; Hero, nominal 100, actual 171.4; Meteor, nominal 100, actual

[047-116] 160; James Watt, nominal 120, actual 204; Watersprite, nominal 76, actual

[047-117] 157.6; Dolphin, nominal 140, actual 238; Dragon, nominal 80, actual 131;

[047-118] Magnet, nominal 140, actual 238; Dart, nominal 120, actual 237; Flamer,

[047-119] nominal 120, actual 234; Firefly, nominal 52, actual 86.6; Ferret, nominal

[047-120] 52, actual 88; Monarch, nominal 200, actual 378. In the case of swift

[047-121] vessels of modern construction, such as the Red Rover, Herne, Queen, and

[047-122] Prince of Wales, the coefficient appears to be about 2550; but in these

[047-123] vessels there is a still greater excess of the actual over the nominal

[047-124] power than in the case of the vessels previously enumerated, and the

[047-125] increase in the coefficient is consequent upon the increased pressure of

[047-126] the steam in the boiler, as well as the superior form of the ship. The

[047-127] nominal power of the Red Rover, Herne, and City of Canterbury is, in each

[047-128] case, 120 horses, but the actual power of the Red Rover is 294, of the

[047-129] Herne 354, and of the City of Canterbury 306, and in some vessels the

[047-130] excess is still greater; so that with such variations it becomes necessary

[047-131] to adopt a coefficient derived from the introduction of the actual instead

[047-132] of the nominal power.

[047-133] 547. _Q._--What will be the average difference between the nominal and

[047-134] actual powers in the several classes of vessels you have mentioned and the

[047-135] respective coefficients when corrected for the actual power?

[047-136] _A._--In the first class of vessels experimented upon, the actual power

[047-137] was about 1.6 times greater

[047-138] than the nominal power; in the second class, 1.67 times greater; in the

[047-139] third class, 1.7 times

[047-140] greater; and in the fourth, 1.96 times greater; while in such vessels as

[047-141] the Red Rover and City of

[047-142] Canterbury, it is 2.65 times greater; so that if we adopt the actual

[047-143] instead of the nominal power in

[047-144] fixing the coefficients, we shall have 554 as the first coefficient, 694

[047-145] as the second, 832 for the

[047-146] third, and 806 for the fourth, instead of 925, 1160, 1430, and 1580 as

[047-147] previously specified; while

[047-148] for such vessels as the Red Rover, Herne, Queen, and Prince of Wales, we

[047-149] shall have 962 instead of

[047-150] 2550. These smaller coefficients, then, express the relative merits of

[047-151] the different vessels without

[047-152] reference to any difference of efficacy in the engines, and it appears

[047-153] preferable, with such a

[047-154] variable excess of the actual over the nominal power, to employ them

[047-155] instead of those first referred

[047-156] to. From the circumstance of the third of the new coefficients being

[047-157] greater than the fourth, it

[047-158] appears that the superior result in the fourth set of experiments arose

[047-159] altogether from a greater

[047-160] excess of the actual over the nominal power.

[047-161] 548. _Q._--These experiments, you have already stated, were all made on

[047-162] paddle vessels. Have similar coefficients of performance been obtained in

[047-163] the case of screw vessels?

[047-164] _A._--The coefficients of a greater number of screw vessels have been

[047-165] obtained and recorded, but it would occupy too much time to enumerate them

[047-166] here. The coefficient of performance of the Fairy is 464.8; of the Rattler

[047-167] 676.8; and of the Frankfort 792.3. This coefficient, however, refers to

[047-168] nautical and not to statute miles. If reduced to statute miles for the

[047-169] purpose of comparison with the previous experiments, the coefficients will

[047-170] respectively become 703, 1033, and 1212; which indicate that the

[047-171] performance of screw vessels is equal to the performance of paddle vessels,

[047-172] but some of the superiority of the result may be imputed to the superior

[047-173] size of the screw vessels.

[047-174] INFLUENCE OF THE SIZE OF VESSELS UPON THEIR SPEED.

[047-175] 549. _Q._--Will large vessels attain a greater speed than small, supposing

[047-176] each to be furnished with the same proportionate power?

[047-177] _A._--It is well known that large vessels furnished with the same

[047-178] proportionate power, will attain a greater speed than small vessels, as

[047-179] appears from the rule usual in yacht races of allowing a certain part of

[047-180] the distance to be run to vessels which are of inferior size. The velocity

[047-181] attained by a large vessel will be greater than the velocity attained by a

[047-182] small vessel of the same mould and the same proportionate power, in the

[047-183] proportion of the square roots of the linear dimensions of the vessels. A

[047-184] vessel therefore with four times the sectional area and four times the

[047-185] power of a smaller symmetrical vessel, and consequently of twice the

[047-186] length, will have its speed increased in the proportion of the square root

[047-187] of 1 to the square root of 2, or 1.4 times.
