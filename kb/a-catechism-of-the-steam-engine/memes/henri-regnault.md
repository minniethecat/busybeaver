# Henri Regnault (1810–1878)

Henri Victor Regnault was a French physicist and chemist who conducted some of the most elaborate and carefully controlled experiments on the thermal properties of steam in the nineteenth century.

## Career and Contributions

Regnault's most significant contribution to [[steam|steam engine]] science was his extensive series of experiments on the relationship between heat, temperature, and pressure in steam. His work demonstrated that the total heat in steam increases somewhat with pressure, and that the latent heat of vaporization varies with temperature — findings that refined the practical understanding of [[steam]] engine thermodynamics. (013-134)

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] notes that Regnault "has shown, by a very elaborate series of experiments on steam, which he has lately concluded, that the total heat in steam increases somewhat with the pressure, and that the latent heat diminishes as the sensible heat increases." (011-148) A table containing the results arrived at by Regnault was included in the Key to the Catechism. (014-071)

Regnault showed that the total amount of heat existing in a given weight of steam increases slightly with pressure, so that "the sum of the latent and sensible heats do not form a constant quantity." (013-140) This finding had practical implications for engineers calculating the efficiency of [[boiler]]s and [[condenser]]s.

## Legacy

Regnault's experimental data became a standard reference for [[steam|steam engine]]ers throughout the latter half of the nineteenth century. His experiments on heat were cited alongside those of [[Southern]] and the French Academy as authoritative sources on steam properties. The precision of his experimental method set a new standard for thermodynamic research, and his tables of steam properties were used by engineers designing [[marine-engine|marine engines]] and stationary power plants.
