[049-001] 558. _Q._--How comes this to be the case?

[049-002] _A._--To understand how it happens that more power is expended upon the

[049-003] oblique than upon the vertical floats, it is necessary to remember that the

[049-004] only resistance upon the vertical paddle is that due to the difference of

[049-005] velocity of the wheel and the ship; but if the wheel be supposed to be

[049-006] immersed to its axle, so that the entering float strikes the water

[049-007] horizontally, it is clear that the resistance on such float is that due to

[049-008] the whole velocity of rotation; and that the resistance to the entering

[049-009] float will be the same whether the vessel is in motion or not. The

[049-010] resistance opposed to the rotation of any float increases from the position

[049-011] of the vertical float-where the resistance is that due to the difference of

[049-012] velocity of the wheel and vessel--until it reaches the plane of the axis,

[049-013] supposing the wheel to be immersed so far, where the resistance is that due

[049-014] to the whole velocity of rotation; and although in any oblique float the

[049-015] total resistance cannot be considered operative in a horizontal direction,

[049-016] yet the total resistance increases so rapidly on each side of the vertical

[049-017] float, that the portion of it which is operative in the horizontal

[049-018] direction, is in all ordinary cases of immersion very considerable. In the

[049-019] feathering wheel, where there is little of this oblique action, the

[049-020] resistance will be in the proportion of the square of the horizontal

[049-021] velocities of the several floats, which may be represented by the

[049-022] horizontal distances between them; and in the feathering wheel, the

[049-023] vertical float having the greatest horizontal velocity will have the

[049-024] greatest propelling effect.

[049-025] 559. _Q._--Should the floats in feathering wheels enter and leave the water

[049-026] vertically?

[049-027] _A._--The floats should be so governed by the central crank or eccentric,

[049-028] that the entering and emerging floats have a direction intermediate between

[049-029] a radius and a vertical line.

[049-030] 560. _Q._--Can you give any practical rules for proportioning paddle

[049-031] wheels?

[049-032] _A._--A common rule for the pitch of the floats is to allow one float for

[049-033] every foot of diameter of the wheel; but in the case of fast vessels a

[049-034] pitch of 2-1/2 feet, or even less, appears preferable, as a close pitch

[049-035] occasions less vibration. If the floats be put too close, however, the

[049-036] water will not escape freely from between them, and if set too far apart

[049-037] the stroke of the entering paddle will occasion an inconvenient amount of

[049-038] vibratory motion, and there will also be some loss of power. To find the

[049-039] proper area of a single float:--divide the number of actual horses power of

[049-040] both engines by the diameter of the wheel in feet; the quotient is the area

[049-041] of one paddle board in square feet proper for sea going vessels, and the

[049-042] area multiplied by 0.6 will give the length of the float in feet. In very

[049-043] sharp vessels, which offer less resistance in passing through the water,

[049-044] the area of paddle board is usually one-fourth less than the above

[049-045] proportion, and the proper length of the float may in such case be found by

[049-046] multiplying the area by 0.7. In sea going vessels about four floats are

[049-047] usually immersed, and in river steamers only one or two floats. There is

[049-048] more slip in the latter case, but there is also more engine power exerted

[049-049] in the propulsion of the ship, from the greater speed of engine thus

[049-050] rendered possible.

[049-051] 561. _Q._--Then is it beneficial to use small floats?

[049-052] _A._--Quite the contrary. If to permit a greater speed of the engine the

[049-053] floats be diminished in area instead of being raised out of the water, no

[049-054] appreciable accession to the speed of the vessel will be obtained; whereas

[049-055] there will be an increased speed of vessel if the accelerated speed of the

[049-056] engine be caused by diminishing the diameter of the wheels. In vessels

[049-057] intended to be fast, therefore, it is expedient to make the wheels small,

[049-058] so as to enable the engine to work with a high velocity; and it is

[049-059] expedient to make such wheels of the feathering kind, to obviate loss of

[049-060] power from oblique action. In no wheel must the rolling circle fall below

[049-061] the water line, else the entering and emerging floats will carry masses of

[049-062] water before them. The slip is usually equal to about one-fourth of the

[049-063] velocity of the centre of pressure in well proportioned wheels; but it is

[049-064] desirable to have the slip as small as is possible consistently with the

[049-065] observance of other necessary conditions. The speed of the engine and also

[049-066] the speed of the vessel being fixed, the diameter of the rolling circle

[049-067] becomes at once ascertainable, and adding to this the slip, we have the

[049-068] diameter of the wheel.

[049-069] CONFIGURATION AND ACTION OF THE SCREW.

[049-070] 562. _Q._--Will you describe more in detail than you have yet done, the

[049-071] configuration and mode of action of the screw propeller?

[049-072] _A._--The ordinary form of screw propeller is represented in figs. 46 and

[049-073] 47; fig. 46 being a perspective view, and fig. 47 an end view, or view such

[049-074] as is seen when looking upon the end of the shaft. The screw here

[049-075] represented is one with two arms or blades. Some screws have three arms,

[049-076] some four and some six; but the screw with two arms is the most usual, and

[049-077] screws with more than three arms are not now much employed in this country.

[049-078] The screw on being put into revolution by the engine, preserves a spiral

[049-079] path in the water, in which it draws itself forward in the same way as a

[049-080] screw nail does when turned round in a piece of wood, whereas the paddle

[049-081] wheel more resembles the action of a cog wheel working in a rack.

[049-082] [Illustration: Fig. 46. Fig. 47. ORDINARY FORM OF SCREW PROPELLER.]

[049-083] 563. _Q._--But the screw of a steam vessel has no resemblance to a screw

[049-084] nail?

[049-085] _A._--It has in fact a very close resemblance if you suppose only a very

[049-086] short piece of the screw nail to be employed, and if you suppose, moreover,

[049-087] the thread of the screw to be cut nearly into the centre to prevent the

[049-088] wood from stripping. The original screw propellers were made with several

[049-089] convolutions of screw, but it was found advantageous to shorten them, until

[049-090] they are now only made one-sixth of a convolution in length.

[049-091] 564. _Q._--And the pitch you have already explained to be the distance in

[049-092] the line of the shaft from one convolution to the next, supposing the screw

[049-093] to consist of two or more convolutions?

[049-094] _A._--Yes, that is what is meant by the pitch. If a thread be wound upon a

[049-095] cylinder with an equal distance between the convolutions, it will trace a

[049-096] screw of a uniform pitch; and if the thread be wound upon the cylinder with

[049-097] an increasing distance between each convolution, it will trace a screw of

[049-098] an increasing pitch. But two or more threads may be wound upon the cylinder

[049-099] at the same time, instead of a single thread. If two threads be wound upon

[049-100] it they will trace a double-threaded screw; if three threads be wound upon

[049-101] it they will trace a treble-threaded screw; and so of any other number. Now

[049-102] if the thread be supposed to be raised up into a very deep and thin spiral

[049-103] feather, and the cylinder be supposed to become very small, like the newel

[049-104] of a spiral stair, then a screw will be obtained of the kind proper for

[049-105] propelling vessels, except that only a very short piece of such screw must

[049-106] be employed. Whatever be the number of threads wound upon a cylinder, if

[049-107] the cylinder be cut across all the threads will be cut. A slice cut out of

[049-108] the cylinder will therefore contain a piece of each thread. But the

[049-109] threads, in the case of a screw propeller, answer to the arms, so that in

[049-110] every screw propeller the number of threads entering into the composition

[049-111] of the screw will be the same as the number of arms. An ordinary screw with

[049-112] two blades is a short piece of a screw of two threads.

[049-113] 565. _Q._--In what part of the ship is the screw usually placed?

[049-114] [Illustration: Fig. 48]

[049-115] _A._--In that part of the run of the ship called the dead wood, which is a

[049-116] thin and unused part of the vessel just in advance of the rudder. The usual

[049-117] arrangement is shown in fig. 48, which represents the application to a

[049-118] vessel of a species of screw which has the arms bent backwards, to

[049-119] counteract the centrifugal motion given to the water when there is a

[049-120] considerable amount of slip.

[049-121] 566. _Q._--How is the slip in a screw vessel determined?

[049-122] _A._--By comparing the actual speed of the vessel with the speed due to the

[049-123] pitch and number of revolutions of the screw, or, what is the same thing,

[049-124] the speed which the vessel would attain if the screw worked in a solid nut.

[049-125] The difference between the actual speed and this hypothetical speed, is the

[049-126] slip.

[049-127] 567. _Q._--In well formed screw propellers what is the amount of slip found

[049-128] to be?

[049-129] _A._--If the screw be properly proportioned to the resistance that the

[049-130] vessel has to overcome, the slip will not be more than 10 per cent., but in

[049-131] some cases it amounts to 30 per cent., or even more than this. In other

[049-132] cases, however, the slip is nothing at all, and even less than nothing; or,

[049-133] in other words the vessel passes through the water with a greater velocity

[049-134] than if the screw were working in a solid nut.

[049-135] 568. _Q._--Then it must be by the aid of the wind or some other extraneous

[049-136] force?

[049-137] _A._--No; by the action of the screw alone.

[049-138] 569. _Q._--But how is such a result possible?

[049-139] _A._--It appears to be mainly owing to the centrifugal action of the screw,

[049-140] which interposes a film or wedge of water between the screw itself and the

[049-141] water on which the screw reacts. This negative slip, as it is called,

[049-142] chiefly occurs when the pitch of the screw is less than its diameter, and

[049-143] when, consequently, the velocity of rotation is greater than if a coarser

[049-144] pitch had been employed. There is, moreover, in all vessels passing through

[049-145] the water with any considerable velocity, a current of water following the

[049-146] vessel, in which current, in the case of a screw vessel, the screw will

[049-147] revolve; and in certain cases the phenomenon of negative slip may be

[049-148] imputable in part to the existence of this current.

[049-149] 570. _Q._--Is the screw propeller as effectual an instrument of propulsion

[049-150] as the radial or feathering paddle?

[049-151] _A._--In all cases of deep immersion it appears to be quite as effectual as

[049-152] the radial paddle, indeed, more so; but it is scarcely as effectual as the

[049-153] feathering paddle, with any amount of immersion, and scarcely as effectual

[049-154] as the common paddle in the case of light immersions.

[049-155] COMPARATIVE ADVANTAGES OF PADDLE AND SCREW VESSELS.

[049-156] 571. _Q._--Whether do you consider paddle or screw vessels to be on the

[049-157] whole the most advantageous?

[049-158] _A._--That is a large question, and can only receive a qualified answer. In

[049-159] some cases the use of paddles is indispensable, as, for example, in the

[049-160] case of river vessels of a limited draught of water, where it would not be

[049-161] possible to get sufficient depth below the water surface to enable a screw

[049-162] of a proper diameter to be got in.

[049-163] 572. _Q._--But how does the matter stand in the case of ocean vessels?

[049-164] _A._--In the case of ocean vessels, it is found that paddle vessels fitted

[049-165] with the ordinary radial wheels, and screw vessels fitted with the ordinary

[049-166] screw, are about equally efficient in calms and in fair or beam winds with

[049-167] light and medium immersions. If the vessels are loaded deeply, however, as

[049-168] vessels starting on a long voyage and carrying much coal must almost

[049-169] necessarily be, then the screw has an advantage, since the screw acts in

[049-170] its best manner when deeply immersed, and the paddles in their worst. When

[049-171] a screw and paddle vessel, however, of the same model and power are set to

[049-172] encounter head winds, the paddle vessel it is found has in all cases an

[049-173] advantage, not in speed, but in economy of fuel. For whereas in a paddle

[049-174] vessel, when her progress is resisted, the speed of the engine diminishes

[049-175] nearly in the proportion of the diminished speed of ship, it happens that

[049-176] in a screw vessel this is not so,--at least to an equal extent,--but the

[049-177] engines work with nearly the same rate of speed as if no increase of

[049-178] resistance had been encountered by the ship. It follows from this

[049-179] circumstance, that whereas in paddle vessels the consumption of steam, and

[049-180] therefore of fuel, per hour is materially diminished when head winds occur,

[049-181] in screw vessels a similar diminution in the consumption of steam and fuel

[049-182] does not take place.
