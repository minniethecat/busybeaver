The **[[furnace|fire box]]** (or firebox) is the furnace chamber of a locomotive or marine [[boiler]] in which the fuel — typically coal or coke — is burned on a grate. It is surrounded on all sides (except the bottom) by water spaces, so that the heat of the fire is transferred directly to the boiler water through the walls of the [[furnace|fire box]]. The hot gases produced by combustion pass from the [[furnace|fire box]] through a set of horizontal flue tubes into the smoke box at the opposite end of the boiler:

> [033-166] The boiler consists of three main parts: the barrel containing the tubes, the fire box, and the smoke box. (033-167)

## Construction and materials

The [[furnace|fire box]] is a double-walled structure. The inner [[furnace|fire box]], which is directly exposed to the fire, was traditionally made of copper for its superior thermal conductivity and resistance to corrosion. The outer shell (external [[furnace|fire box]]) was made of iron:

> [033-167] The barrel, smoke box, and external fire box are always of iron, but the internal fire box is generally made of copper, though sometimes also it is made of iron. (033-169)

The space between the inner and outer shells is filled with boiler water, connected to the main boiler barrel. Stay-bolts or stays tie the inner and outer shells together, resisting the pressure that tends to collapse the inner box inward. The bottom of the [[furnace|fire box]] is open to the grate and ashpan below.

## Heating surface and dimensions

The [[furnace|fire box]] contributes substantially to the total heating surface of the boiler, as its walls are in direct contact with both the fire and the water. The crown sheet (top of the inner [[furnace|fire box]]) is particularly effective, being directly over the hottest part of the fire:

> [011-064] The heating surface of the fire box is 153 square feet. There are in all 305 tubes in the boiler, each of 2 inches diameter, giving a heating surface in the tubes of 852 square feet. (011-065)

Typical [[furnace|fire box]] dimensions for locomotives of the mid-nineteenth century varied by class:

> [022-053] Inside diameter of fire box 53 inches; inside width of fire box 63 inches; height of fire box above bars 63 inches — these dimensions varying with the size and duty of the engine. (022-055)

## Draft and the blast pipe

The [[furnace|fire box]] is maintained under a slight vacuum during operation by the action of the [[blast-pipe]] in the smoke box. Exhaust steam from the cylinders is discharged up the chimney through the [[blast-pipe|blast pipe]], creating a partial vacuum that draws air through the fire bed and hot gases through the tubes:

> [022-126] Is the exhaustion produced by the blast as great in the fire box as in the smoke box? — The exhaustion in the smoke box was generally equivalent to 12 inches of water, while in the fire box it was only about 3 inches. (022-135)

The pressure differential between [[furnace|fire box]] and smoke box drives the flow of combustion gases through the tubes, and the intensity of the blast directly affects the rate of combustion and steam generation.

## Temperature and fuel economy

A high [[furnace|fire box]] temperature was recognized as conducive to efficient combustion:

> [018-063] A high temperature of the fire box is found to be conducive to economy of fuel; and this condition, in its turn, involves a small area of grate bars. (018-064)

A smaller grate area concentrated the fire, producing higher temperatures and more complete combustion, though it required more frequent stoking and careful fire management.

## Safety: the fusible plug

A critical safety device was incorporated into the [[furnace|fire box]] crown. If the water level in the boiler fell too low, exposing the crown sheet to the fire without water cooling on the other side, the metal would rapidly overheat and could collapse, causing a catastrophic boiler explosion. To prevent this, a fusible plug was [[screw-propeller|screw]]ed into the crown:

> [025-144] A fusible plug is inserted with advantage in the crown of the fire box, which is melted out if the water becomes too low, and thus gives notice of the danger. (025-145)

When the plug melted, steam and water would jet into the [[furnace|fire box]], extinguishing the fire and alerting the engine crew to the low-water condition.

## Marine boiler fire boxes

In marine boilers, the [[furnace|fire box]] served the same function but was often arranged differently — in return-flue and multitubular boilers, the furnace might be an internal cylindrical flue rather than a rectangular box. In [[marine-engine|marine engines]] with [[condenser|jet condenser]]s, priming (water [[priming|carry-over]]) was less of a concern around the [[furnace|fire box]] than in locomotives, though the [[furnace|fire box]] was particularly susceptible to damage if salt scale accumulated on the water-side surfaces (023-155).
