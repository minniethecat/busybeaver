The **paddle wheel** is a marine propulsion device in which rotating wheels, fitted with boards or floats, are mounted on each side of a steam vessel. As the wheels rotate, the floats engage the water and propel the vessel forward. Before the widespread adoption of the [[screw-propeller]], the paddle wheel was the dominant means of steam vessel propulsion throughout the early nineteenth century.

## Types of paddle wheels

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] identifies two principal varieties:

> [048-022] There are two kinds of paddle wheels in extensive use, the one being the ordinary radial wheel, in which the floats are fixed on arms radiating from the centre; and the other the feathering wheel, in which each float is hung upon a centre, and is so governed by suitable mechanism as to be always kept in nearly the vertical position.

In the radial wheel, there is a disadvantage: the floats enter and leave the water at an angle, producing some vertical splash and loss of propulsive efficiency. The feathering wheel addresses this by keeping each float nearly vertical throughout its immersion.

## Float configuration

> [009-146] The paddle wheels may either be formed with fixed float boards for engaging the water, like the boards of a common undershot water wheel, or they may be formed with _feathering_ float boards as they are termed, which is float boards movable on a centre, and so governed by appropriate mechanism that they enter and leave the water in a nearly vertical position.

The feathering mechanism, as applied in [[oscillating-engine|oscillating engines]], uses an [[eccentric]] to control the angle of each float throughout its rotation. This produces smoother engagement with the water and higher propulsive efficiency than the simpler radial float.

## Propulsive principle

> [009-127] In the one case the propelling instrument is paddle wheels kept in rotation at each side of the ship: in the other case, the propelling instrument is a screw, consisting of two or more twisted vanes, revolving beneath the water at the stern.

The tractive force of paddle steamers was measured practically:

> [019-088] The tractive force of paddle wheel steamers is ascertained by a dynamometer fixed on shore, to which the floating vessel is attached by a rope.

## Mechanical arrangement

The paddle wheels are mounted on a paddle shaft that extends across the vessel and is driven by the engine. The outer bearing requires special attention:

> [042-001] How is the outer bearing of the paddle wheels supplied with tallow? The bearing is a brass bush, in which a recess is made to contain the tallow, and the tallow is forced up to the journal by a weighted piston.

The wheels must be carefully hung to ensure proper alignment:

> [042-059] The paddle wheels must be hung from the top of the paddle box to enable them to be adjusted vertically, and the distance between the shafts must be accurately set.

## Decline and legacy

The paddle wheel was gradually superseded by the [[screw-propeller]] for ocean-going vessels, particularly after the success of [[screw-propeller|screw]] steamers like the Great Britain. However, paddle wheels remained in use for river steamers and shallow-draft vessels well into the late nineteenth century, where their ability to operate in restricted water depths was advantageous.
