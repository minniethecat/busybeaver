[045-001] 514. _Q._--How are the bearings of the axles arranged?

[045-002] _A._--The axles bear only against the top of the axle boxes, which are

[045-003] generally of brass; but a plate extends underneath the bearing, to prevent

[045-004] sand from being thrown upon it. The upper part of the box in most engines

[045-005] has a reservoir of oil, which is supplied to the journal by tubes with

[045-006] siphon wicks. Stephenson uses cast iron axle boxes with brasses, and grease

[045-007] instead of oil; and the grease is fed upon the journal by the heat of the

[045-008] bearing melting it, whereby it is made to flow down through a hole in the

[045-009] brass. Any engines constructed with outside bearings have inside bearings

[045-010] also, which are supported by longitudinal bars, which serve also in some

[045-011] cases to support the piston guides; these bearings are sometimes made so as

[045-012] not to touch the shafts unless they break.

[045-013] 515. _Q._--How are the eccentrics of a locomotive constructed?

[045-014] _A._--In locomotives the body of the eccentric is of cast iron, in inside

[045-015] cylinder engines the eccentrics are set on the axle between the cranks, and

[045-016] they are put on in two pieces held together by bolts; but in straight axle

[045-017] engines the eccentrics are cast in a piece, and are secured on the shaft by

[045-018] means of a key. The eccentric, when in two pieces, is retained at its

[045-019] proper angle on the shaft by a pinching screw, which is provided with a jam

[045-020] nut to prevent it from working loose. A piece is left out of the eccentric

[045-021] in casting it to allow of the screw being inserted, and the void is

[045-022] afterward filled by inserting a dovetailed piece of metal. Stephenson and

[045-023] Hawthorn leave holes in their eccentrics on each side of the central arm,

[045-024] and they apply pinching screws in each of these holes. The method of fixing

[045-025] the eccentric to the shaft by a pinching screw is scarcely sufficiently

[045-026] substantial; and cases are perpetually occurring, when this method of

[045-027] attachment is adopted, of eccentrics shifting from their place. In the

[045-028] modern engines the eccentrics are forged on the axles.

[045-029] 516. _Q._--How are the eccentric straps constructed?

[045-030] _A._--The eccentric hoops are generally of wrought iron, as brass hoops are

[045-031] found liable to break. When formed of malleable iron, one half of the strap

[045-032] is forged with the rod, the other half being secured to it by bolts, nuts,

[045-033] and jam nuts. Pieces of brass are, in some cases, pinned within the

[045-034] malleable iron hoop; but it appears to be preferable to put brasses within

[045-035] the hoop to encircle the eccentric, as in the case of any other bearing.

[045-036] When the brass straps are used, the lugs have generally nuts on both sides,

[045-037] so that the length of the eccentric rod may be adjusted by their means to

[045-038] the proper length; but it is better for the lugs of the hoops to abut

[045-039] against the necks of the screws, and, if any adjustment be necessary from

[045-040] the wear of the straps, washers can be interposed. In some engines the

[045-041] adjustment is effected by screwing the valve rod, and the cross head

[045-042] through which it passes has a nut on either side of it, by which its

[045-043] position upon the valve rod is determined.

[045-044] 517. _Q._--Will you describe the eccentric rod and valve levers?

[045-045] _A._--In the engines in use before the introduction of the link motion, the

[045-046] forks of the eccentric rod were of steel, and the length of the eccentric

[045-047] rod was the distance between the centre of the crank axle and the centre of

[045-048] the valve shaft; but in modern engines the use of the link motion is

[045-049] universal. The valve lever in locomotives is usually longer than the

[045-050] eccentric lever, to increase the travel of the valve, if levers are

[045-051] employed; but it is better to connect the valve rod to the link of the link

[045-052] motion without the intervention of levers. The pins of the eccentric lever

[045-053] in the old engines used to wear quickly; Stephenson used to put a ferule of

[045-054] brass on these pins, which being loose, and acting like a roller,

[045-055] facilitated the throwing in and out of gear, and when worn could easily be

[045-056] replaced, so that there was no material derangement of the motion of the

[045-057] valve from play in this situation.

[045-058] 518. _Q._--What is the arrangement of a starting lever?

[045-059] _A._--The starting lever travels between two iron segments, and can be

[045-060] fixed in any desired position. This is done by a small catch or bell crank,

[045-061] jointed to the bottom of the handle at the end of the lever, and coming up

[045-062] by the side of the handle, but pressed out from it by a spring. The smaller

[045-063] arm of this bell crank is jointed to a bolt, which shoots into notches,

[045-064] made in one of the segments between which the lever moves. By pressing the

[045-065] bell crank against the handle of the lever the bolt is withdrawn, and the

[045-066] lever may be shifted to any other point, when, the spring being released,

[045-067] the bolt flies into the nearest notch.

[045-068] 519. _Q._--In what way does the starting handle act on the machinery of the

[045-069] engine to set it in motion?

[045-070] _A._--Its whole action lies in raising or depressing the link of the link

[045-071] motion relatively with the valve rod. If the valve rod be attached to the

[045-072] middle of the link, the valve will derive no motion from, it at all, and

[045-073] the engine will stop. If the attachment be slipped to one end of the link

[045-074] the engine will go ahead, and if slipped to the other end it will go

[045-075] astern. The starting handle merely achieves this change of position.

[045-076] 520. _Q._--Will you explain the operation of setting the valve of a

[045-077] locomotive?

[045-078] _A._--In setting the valves of locomotives, place the crank in the position

[045-079] answerable to the end of the stroke of the piston, and draw a straight

[045-080] line, representing the centre line of the cylinder, through the centres of

[045-081] the crank shaft and crank pin. From the centre of the shaft describe a

[045-082] circle with the diameter equal to the throw of the valve; another circle to

[045-083] represent the crank shaft; and a third circle to represent the path of the

[045-084] crank pin. From the centre of the crank shaft, draw a line perpendicular to

[045-085] the centre line of the cylinder and crank shaft, and draw another

[045-086] perpendicular at a distance from the first equal to the amount of the lap

[045-087] and the lead of the valve: the points in which this line intersects the

[045-088] circle of the eccentric are the points in which the centre of the eccentric

[045-089] should be placed for the forward and reverse motions. When the eccentric

[045-090] rod is attached directly to the valve, the radius of the eccentric, which

[045-091] precedes the crank in its revolution, forms with the crank an obtuse angle;

[045-092] but when, by the intervention of levers, the valve has a motion, opposed to

[045-093] that of the eccentric rod, the angle contained by the crank and the radius

[045-094] of the eccentric must be acute, and the eccentric must follow the crank: in

[045-095] other words, with a direct attachment to the valve the eccentric is set

[045-096] _more_ than one fourth of a revolution in advance of the crank, and with an

[045-097] indirect attachment the eccentric is set _less_ than one fourth of a circle

[045-098] behind the crank. If the valve were without lead or lap the eccentric would

[045-099] be exactly one fourth of a circle in advance of the crank or behind the

[045-100] crank, according to the nature of the valve connection; but as the valve

[045-101] would thus cover the port by the amount of the lap and lead, the eccentric

[045-102] must be set forward so as to open the port to the extent of the lap and

[045-103] lead, and this is effected by the plan just described.

[045-104] 521. _Q._--In the event of the eccentrics slipping round upon the shaft,

[045-105] which you stated sometimes happens, is it necessary to perform the

[045-106] operation of setting the valve as you have just described it?

[045-107] _A._--If the eccentrics shift upon the shaft, they may be easily refixed by

[045-108] setting the valve open the amount of the lead, setting the crank at the end

[045-109] of the stroke, and bringing round the eccentric upon the shaft till the

[045-110] eccentric rod gears with the valve. It would often be troublesome in

[045-111] practice to get access to the valve for the purpose of setting it, and this

[045-112] may be dispensed with if the amount of lap on the valve and the length of

[045-113] the eccentric rod be known. To this end draw upon a board two straight

[045-114] lines at right angles to one another, and from their point of intersection

[045-115] as a centre describe two circles, one representing the circle of the

[045-116] eccentric, the other the crank shaft; draw a straight line parallel to one

[045-117] of the diameters, and distant from it the amount of the lap and the lead:

[045-118] the points in which his parallel intersects the circle of the eccentric are

[045-119] the positions of the forward and backward eccentrics. Through these points

[045-120] draw straight lines from the centre of the circle, and mark the

[045-121] intersection of these lines with the circle of the crank shaft; measure

[045-122] with a pair of compasses the chord of the arc intercepted between either of

[045-123] these points, and the diameter which is at right angles with the crank, and

[045-124] the diameters being first marked on the shaft itself, then by transferring

[045-125] with the compasses the distance found in the diagram, and marking the

[045-126] point, the eccentric may at any time be adjusted without difficulty.

[045-128] 522. _Q._--Will you describe the structure and arrangement of the feed

[045-129] pumps of locomotive engines?

[045-130] _A._--The feed pumps of locomotives are generally made of brass, but the

[045-131] plungers are sometimes made of iron, and are generally attached to the

[045-132] piston, cross head, though in Stephenson's engines they are worked by rods

[045-133] attached to eyes on the eccentric hoops. There is a ball valve, fig. 45,

[045-134] between the pump and the tender, and two usually in the pipe leading from

[045-135] the pump to the boiler, besides a cock close to the boiler, by which the

[045-136] pump may be shut off from the boiler in case of any accident to the valves.

[045-137] The ball valves are guided by four branches, which rise vertically, and

[045-138] join together at the top in a hemispherical form. The shocks of the ball

[045-139] against this cap have in some cases broken it after one week's work, from

[045-140] the top of the cage having been flat, and the branches not having had their

[045-141] junction at the top properly filleted. These valve guards are attached in

[045-142] different ways to the pipes; when one occurs at the junction of two pieces

[045-143] of pipe it has a flange, which along with the flanges of the pipes and that

[045-144] of the valve seat are held together by a union joint. It is sometimes

[045-145] formed with a thread at the under end, and screwed into the pipe. The balls

[045-146] are cast hollow to lessen the shock, as well as to save the metal. In some

[045-147] cases where the feed pump plunger has been attached to the cross head, the

[045-148] piston rod has been bent by the strain; and that must in all cases occur,

[045-149] if the communication between the pump and boiler be closed when the engine

[045-150] is started, and there be no escape valve for the water.

[045-151] 523. _Q._--Are none but ball valves used in the feed pump?

[045-152] _A._--Spindle valves have in some cases been used instead of ball valves,

[045-153] but they are more subject to derangement; but piston valves, so contrived

[045-154] as to shut a portion of water in the cage when about to close, might be

[045-155] adopted with a great diminution of the shock. Slide valves might be

[045-156] applied, and would probably be found preferable to any of the expedients at

[045-157] present in use. In all spindle valves opened and shut rapidly, it is

[045-158] advisable to have the lower surface conical, to take off the shock of the

[045-159] water; and a large lift of the valve should be prevented, else much of the

[045-160] water during the return stroke of the pump will flow out before the valve

[045-161] shuts.
