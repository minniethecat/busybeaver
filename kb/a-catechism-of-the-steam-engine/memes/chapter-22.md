[022-001] _A._--In the Albion Mill engines when working without expansion, it was

[022-002] found that 1 lb. of water in the shape of steam raised 28,489 lbs. 1 foot

[022-003] high. A cubic foot of water, therefore, or 62-1/2 lbs., if consumed in the

[022-004] hour, would raise 1780562.5 lbs. one foot high in the hour, or would raise

[022-005] 29,676 lbs. one foot high in a minute; and if to this we add one tenth for

[022-006] waste at the ends of the cylinder, a waste which hardly exists in modern

[022-007] engines, we have 32,643 lbs. raised one foot high in the minute, or a horse

[022-008] power very nearly. In some cases the approximation appears still nearer.

[022-009] Thus, in a 40 horse engine working without expansion, Watt found that .674

[022-010] feet of water were evaporated from the boiler per minute, which is just a

[022-011] cubic foot per horse power per hour; but it is not certain in this case

[022-012] that the nominal and actual power were precisely identical. It will be

[022-013] quite safe, however, to reckon an actual horse power as producible by the

[022-014] evaporation of a cubic foot of water in the hour in the case of engines

[022-015] working without expansion; and for boiling off this quantity in flue or

[022-016] wagon boilers, about 8 lbs. of coal will be required and 9 square feet of

[022-017] flue surface.

[022-018] MODERN MARINE AND LOCOMOTIVE BOILERS.

[022-019] 275. _Q._--These proportions appear chiefly to refer to old boilers. I wish

[022-020] you to state what are the proportions of modern flue and tubular marine

[022-021] boilers.

[022-022] _A._--In modern marine boilers the area of fire grate is less than in Mr.

[022-023] Watt's original boilers, where it was one square foot to nine square feet

[022-024] of heating surface. The heat in the furnace is consequently more intense,

[022-025] and a somewhat less amount of surface suffices to evaporate a cubic foot of

[022-026] water. In Boulton and Watt's modern flue boilers they allow for the

[022-027] evaporation of a cubic foot of water 8 square feet of heating surface, 70

[022-028] square inches of fire grate, 13 square inches sectional area of flues, 6

[022-029] square inches sectional area of chimney, 14 square inches area over furnace

[022-030] bridges, ratio of area of flue to area of fire grate 1 to 5.4. To evaporate

[022-031] a cubic foot of water per hour in tubular boilers, the proportions are--

[022-032] heating surface 9 square feet, fire grate 70 square inches, sectional area

[022-033] of tubes 10 square inches, sectional area of back uptake 12 square inches,

[022-034] sectional area of front uptake 10 square inches, sectional area of chimney

[022-035] 7 square inches, ratio of diameter of tube to length of tube 1/28th to

[022-036] 1/30th, cubical content of boiler exclusive of steam chest 6.5 cubic feet,

[022-037] cubical content of steam chest 1.5 cubic feet.

[022-038] 276. _Q._--These proportions do not apply to locomotive boilers?

[022-039] _A._--Not at all. In locomotive boilers the draught is maintained by the

[022-040] projection of the waste steam which escapes from the cylinders up the

[022-041] chimney, and the draught is much more powerful and the combustion much more

[022-042] rapid than in cases in which the combustion is maintained by the natural

[022-043] draught of a chimney, except indeed the chimney be of very unusual

[022-044] temperature and height. The proportions proper for locomotive boilers will

[022-045] be seen by the dimensions of a few locomotives of approved construction,

[022-046] which have been found to give satisfactory results in practice, and which

[022-081] THE BLAST IN LOCOMOTIVES.

[022-082] 277. _Q._--What is the amount of draught produced in locomotive boilers in

[022-083] comparison with that existing in other boilers?

[022-084] _A._--A good chimney of a land engine will produce a degree of exhaustion

[022-085] equal to from 1-1/2 to 2-1/2 inches of water. In locomotive boilers the

[022-086] exhaustion is in some cases equal to 12 or 13 inches of water, but from 3

[022-087] to 6 inches is a more common proportion.

[022-088] 278. _Q._--And what force of blast is necessary to produce this exhaustion?

[022-089] _A._--The amount varies in different engines, depending on the sectional

[022-090] area of the tubes and other circumstances. But on the average, it may be

[022-091] asserted that such a pressure of blast as will support an inch of mercury,

[022-092] will maintain sufficient exhaustion in the smoke box to support an inch of

[022-093] water; and this ratio holds whether the exhaustion is little or great. To

[022-094] produce an exhaustion in the smoke box, therefore, of 6 inches of water,

[022-095] the waste steam would require to be of sufficient pressure to support a

[022-096] column of 6 inches of mercury, which is equivalent to a pressure of 3 lbs.

[022-097] on the square inch.

[022-098] 279. _Q._--How is the force of the blast determined?

[022-099] _A._--By the amount of contraction given to the mouth of the blast pipe,

[022-100] which is a pipe which conducts the waste steam from the cylinders and

[022-101] debouches at the foot of the chimney. If a strong blast be required, the

[022-102] mouth of this pipe requires to be correspondingly contracted, but such

[022-103] contraction throws a back pressure on the piston, and it is desirable to

[022-104] obtain the necessary draught with as little contraction of the blast pipe

[022-105] as possible. The blast pipe is generally a breeches pipe of which the legs

[022-106] join just before reaching the chimney; but it is better to join the two

[022-107] cylinders below, and to let a single pipe ascend to within 12 or 18 inches

[022-108] of the foot of the chimney. If made with too short a piece of pipe above

[022-109] the joining, the steam will be projected against each side of the chimney

[022-110] alternately, and the draught will be damaged and the chimney worn. The

[022-111] blast pipe should not be regularly tapered, but should be large in the body

[022-112] and gathered in at the mouth.

[022-113] 280. _Q._--Is a large and high chimney conducive to strength of draught in

[022-114] locomotives?

[022-115] _A._--It has not been found to be so. A chimney of three or four times its

[022-116] own diameter in height appears to answer fully as well as a longer one; and

[022-117] it was found that when in an engine with 17 inch cylinders a chimney of

[022-118] 15-1/4 inches was substituted for a chimney of 17-1/2 inches, a superior

[022-119] performance was the result. The chimney of a locomotive should have half

[022-120] the area of the tubes at the ferules, which is the most contracted part,

[022-121] and the blast orifice should have 1/10th of the area of the chimney. The

[022-122] sectional area of the tubes through the ferules should be as large as

[022-123] possible. Tubes without ferules it is found pass one fourth more air, and

[022-124] tubes with ferules only at the smoke box end pass one tenth more air than

[022-125] when there are ferules at both ends.

[022-126] 281. _Q._--Is the exhaustion produced by the blast as great in the fire box

[022-127] as in the smoke box?

[022-128] _A._--Experiments have been made to determine this, and in few cases has it

[022-129] been found to be more than about half as great as ordinary speeds; but much

[022-130] depends on the amount of contraction in the tubes. In an experiment made

[022-131] with an engine having 147 tubes of 1-3/4 inches external diameter, and 13

[022-132] feet 10 inches long, and with a fire grate having an area of 9-1/2 square

[022-133] feet, the exhaustion at all speeds was found to be three times greater in

[022-134] the smoke box than in the fire box. The exhaustion in the smoke box was

[022-135] generally equivalent to 12 inches of water, while in the fire box it was

[022-136] equivalent to only 4 inches of water; showing that 4 inches were required

[022-137] to draw the air through the grate and 8 inches through the tubes.

[022-138] 282. _Q._--What will be the increase of evaporation in a locomotive from a

[022-139] given increase of exhaustion?

[022-140] _A._--The rate of evaporation in a locomotive or any other boiler will vary

[022-141] as the quantity of air passing through the fire, and the quantity of air

[022-142] passing through the fire will vary nearly as the square root of the

[022-143] exhaustion. With four times the exhaustion, therefore, there will be about

[022-144] twice the evaporation, and experiment shows that this theoretical law holds

[022-145] with tolerable accuracy in practice.

[022-146] 283. _Q._--But the same exhaustion will not be produced by a given strength

[022-147] of blast in all engines?

[022-148] _A._--No; engines with contracted fire grates and an inadequate sectional

[022-149] area of tubes, will require a stronger blast than engines of better

[022-150] proportions; but in any given engine the relations between the blast

[022-151] exhaustion and evaporation, hold which have been already defined.

[022-152] 284. _Q._--Is the intensity of the draught under easy regulation?

[022-153] _A._--The intensity of the draught may easily be diminished by partially

[022-154] closing the damper in the chimney, and it may be increased by contracting

[022-155] the orifice of the blast. A variable blast pipe, the orifice of which may

[022-156] be enlarged or contracted at pleasure, has been much used. There are

[022-157] various devices for this purpose, but the best appears to be that adopted

[022-158] in Stephenson's engine, where a conical nozzle is moved up or down within

[022-159] the blast pipe, which is made somewhat larger in diameter than the base of

[022-160] the cone, but with a ring projecting internally, against which the base of

[022-161] the cone abuts when the nozzle is pushed up. When the nozzle stands at the

[022-162] top of the pipe the whole of the steam has to pass through it, and the

[022-163] intensity of the blast is increased by the increased velocity thus given to

[022-164] the steam; whereas when the nozzle is moved downward the steam escapes

[022-165] through the annular opening left between the nozzle and the pipe, as well

[022-166] as through the nozzle itself, and the intensity of the blast is diminished

[022-167] by the enlargement of the opening for the escape of the steam thus made

[022-168] available.

[022-169] 285._Q._--What is the best diameter for the tubes of locomotive boilers?

[022-170] _A._--Bury's locomotive with 14 inch cylinders contains 92 tubes of 2-1/8th

[022-171] inches external diameter, and 10 feet 6 inches long; whereas Stephenson's

[022-172] locomotive with 15 inch cylinders contains 150 tubes of 1-5/8ths external

[022-173] diameter, 13 feet 6 inches long. In Stephenson's boiler, in order that the

[022-174] part of the tubes next the chimney may be of any avail for the generation

[022-175] of steam, the draught has to be very intense, which in its turn involves a

[022-176] considerable expenditure of power; and it is questionable whether the

[022-177] increased expenditure of power upon the blast, in Stephenson's long tubed

[022-178] locomotives, is compensated by the increased generation of steam consequent

[022-179] upon the extension of the heating surface. When the tubes are small in

[022-180] diameter they are apt to become partially choked with pieces of coke; but

[022-181] an internal diameter of 1-5/8ths may be employed without inconvenience if

[022-182] the draught be of medium intensity.
