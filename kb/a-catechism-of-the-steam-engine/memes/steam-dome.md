# Steam Dome

The **steam dome** is a raised chamber mounted on the top of the [[Boiler|boiler]] barrel, from which steam is drawn through the steam pipe to the [[Cylinder|cylinder]]. (011-034) (011-035) Its purpose is to provide a steam space well above the water surface, so that the steam taken from the boiler is as dry as possible and free from entrained water droplets — a condition known as [[Priming|priming]].

## Construction and Position

The steam dome is typically a cylindrical or domed casting bolted to the top of the boiler barrel, rising a few feet above the boiler shell. (023-152) The steam pipe enters the dome near its crown, where the steam is driest. In [[Locomotive|locomotive]] engines, the dome is usually positioned towards the middle or forward end of the boiler barrel.

> "J J is the steam pipe by which the steam is conducted from the steam dome of the boiler to the cylinder. Near the smoke stack end of this pipe is a valve K or regulator moved by a handle at the front of the boiler." (011-034) (011-036)

In some boiler designs the steam dome and steam pipe are located at the smoke box end of the boiler. (046-007) This arrangement placed the steam take-off at the hottest part of the boiler, though there was a risk that entering cold feed water might condense steam in the dome if the feed inlet was nearby. (046-008)

## Role in Steam Quality

With clean water and a steam dome several feet high on the boiler barrel, the water carried over with the steam is substantially reduced. (023-152) The dome acts as a disengagement space: as steam rises from the water surface, any droplets entrained in the flow fall back by gravity before reaching the steam pipe inlet. This is particularly important in locomotives and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s where the motion of the vessel or vehicle can cause water surge and aggravate priming.

## Vertical Engine Arrangements

In some [[Marine-Engine|vertical marine engines]], the [[cylinder|steam cylinder]]s are fastened directly to the boiler, with the [[cylinder|steam cylinder]]s attached directly to the steam dome. (061-025) This arrangement obviates the need for separate steam piping between boiler and engine, making the installation compact and reducing heat loss — a particular advantage in steamship machinery spaces where every inch of space was contested.

## Historical Note

By [[john-bourne|Bourne]]'s time, the steam dome was already being described as a feature "used only in old engines" (068-183), suggesting that alternative methods of steam separation and drying — such as internal perforated dry pipes running the length of the boiler — were superseding the external dome in newer designs.
