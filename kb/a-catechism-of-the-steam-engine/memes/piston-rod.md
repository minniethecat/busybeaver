The **piston rod** is the rod that connects the [[piston]] inside the [[cylinder]] to the external mechanism of the [[steam|steam engine]]. It transmits the force generated by steam pressure on the piston face to the beam, [[cross-head]], or other working parts, and must withstand both tensile and compressive loads as the piston reciprocates:

> [008-079] The piston, by means of a rod called the piston rod, gives motion to the beam. (008-080)

The piston rod passes through the [[cylinder-cover|cylinder cover]] via a [[stuffing-box]], which must seal against high-pressure steam while allowing free linear motion of the rod.

## Connection to the beam

In beam engines, the piston rod connects to the great beam through a pair of parallel links forming a knuckle joint:

> [009-031] The piston rod is connected to the great beam by means of two links, one at each side of the beam. (009-033)

The junction of the links and the piston rod forms a knuckle or bend at some parts of the stroke, and this articulation was a focus of careful mechanical design. [[james-watt|Watt]]'s [[parallel-motion]] linkage was introduced specifically to constrain the top of the piston rod to move in a straight vertical line despite the arcuate motion of the beam end.

## Guidance and alignment

Maintaining the vertical alignment of the piston rod was essential to prevent binding in the [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]] and uneven wear of the piston within the cylinder. Two methods were employed:

- **Parallel motion linkage**: [[james-watt|Watt]]'s elegant geometric linkage guided the piston rod head in a near-perfect straight line without sliding contact. This was standard on beam engines.
- **Guide rods**: In direct-acting engines, rigid guide rods were set on each side of the piston rod.

> [009-041] Some engines have guide rods set on each side of the piston rod, and eyes on the top of the piston rod engage these guide rods, and maintain the piston rod in a vertical position in every part of the stroke. (009-043)

The guide-rod method was simpler and more robust but introduced additional sliding friction and required continuous lubrication.

## Stuffing box seal

The piston rod must pass through the cylinder cover while preventing steam leakage. This is achieved by the [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]]:

> [009-021] The hole in the cylinder lid, through which the piston rod passes, is furnished with a recess called a stuffing box, into which a stuffing or packing of hemp is placed, and compressed by a gland or brass bush. (009-022)

The packing material — typically plaited hemp saturated with tallow — was compressed around the rod by tightening the [[stuffing-box|gland]] bolts. A brass bush or ring, accurately fitting the piston rod, ensured even compression:

> [009-027] This ring, which accurately fits the piston rod, has a projecting flange, through which bolts pass for tightening the ring down upon the packing. (009-028)

Excessive tightening caused overheating and scoring of the rod surface; too little allowed steam to escape. The correct adjustment was a matter of practical skill for the engine tender.

## Materials and construction

[[piston|Piston]] rods were typically forged from wrought iron or, in later practice, mild steel. They were turned to a smooth cylindrical finish where they passed through the [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]], and often had a slightly larger diameter at the piston end to accommodate the piston attachment. In vertical engines, the weight of the piston and rod assembly was supported either by the beam (in beam engines) or by the cross-head guides (in direct-acting engines).
