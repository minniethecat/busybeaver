# Safety Valve

The **safety valve** is a device fitted to every steam [[Boiler|boiler]] that automatically opens to discharge steam when the internal pressure rises above a predetermined safe limit, thereby preventing rupture or explosion of the boiler shell. (025-100) It is the most critical safety component in any steam-generating plant.

## Principle of Operation

The valve is held closed against its seat by a spring or a weighted lever, the force of which is set to correspond to the maximum allowable working pressure of the boiler. (011-038) When the steam pressure inside the boiler exceeds this set force, the valve lifts from its seat, allowing steam to escape. (007-125) As pressure falls back within limits, the spring or weight re-seats the valve.

> "Every boiler should have a safety valve of sufficient size to allow the steam to escape as fast as it is generated." (025-100)

The safety valve spring should be made so that its action is gradual and not sudden, ensuring that the valve opens progressively rather than with a violent pop that could damage the seating surfaces. (025-103)

## Location and Arrangement

The position of the safety valve on the boiler shell significantly affects its effectiveness. If the valve is placed near the water surface, the agitation caused by opening it may cause the boiler to prime — carrying water over with the steam into the cylinders. (024-011) Locating the safety valve at a distance from the water surface reduces this risk. (024-014)

On [[Locomotive|locomotives]], it is found beneficial to have a safety valve mounted directly on the barrel of the boiler, where the steam space is largest and furthest from the water surface. (024-017)

## Capacity Requirements

A safety valve must be of adequate size to discharge steam faster than the boiler can generate it. If the generation rate — under forced firing or accidental overheating — exceeds the discharge capacity, pressure may continue to rise even with the valve fully open, with dangerous consequences. (025-066)

> "The steam may be too rapid for the safety valve to discharge it with sufficient facility, and the pressure may in consequence rise beyond what is safe." (025-066)

The required valve area is calculated from the grate area or heating surface, increasing with boiler size. The advent of higher-pressure boilers in the nineteenth century made adequate safety-valve sizing increasingly critical.

## Accidental Locking and Maintenance

If a safety valve is accidentally jammed shut — by corrosion, scale, or careless assembly — the boiler may be heated to a dangerous condition without any outward sign until it is too late. (025-056) For this reason, the safety valve should be periodically tested by lifting it manually when the boiler is under steam. (025-106)

The action of safety valves should be checked regularly and the valve and seat kept free of scale and corrosion. (025-106) A waste steam pipe connected to the safety valve discharge conveys the escaping steam safely outside the engine room. (025-117)

## Early History and Regulation

Early boilers often lacked adequate safety valves, and boiler explosions were a frequent cause of fatalities in the first half of the nineteenth century. (025-056) The widespread adoption of mandatory safety-valve requirements — two independent valves per boiler, each of sufficient size — was one of the earliest examples of safety regulation in mechanical engineering. (023-166)
