# Lap

The **lap** of a [[Valve|valve]] is the elongation of the valve face over the steam port, which causes the port to close before the end of the piston stroke, thereby enabling [[expansion-valve|expansive working]]. (015-055) (015-056)

The lap is the primary means by which a fixed cut-off is achieved in [[slide-valve]] engines, and its magnitude directly determines the degree of expansion obtained from the steam.

## Steam Side and Eduction Side Lap

The extension of the valve face is chiefly applied on the *steam side* of the valve — the side through which steam enters the cylinder. (015-058) By closing the steam passage before the stroke is complete, the engine is made to operate expansively for a portion of each stroke. (015-060)

In some cases, lap is also given to the eduction or escape side. (015-062) This prevents the [[eduction|eduction passage]] from opening too early, which would otherwise occur when the lead is large. However, there is generally far less lap on the [[eduction|eduction side]] than on the steam side, and sometimes none at all. (015-064)

> "It is an elongation of the valve face to a certain extent over the port, whereby the port is closed sooner than would otherwise be the case. This extension is chiefly effected at that part of the valve where the steam is admitted, or upon the steam side of the valve, as the technical phrase is; and the intent of the extension is to close the steam passage before the end of the stroke, whereby the engine is made to operate to a certain extent expansively." (015-055) (015-060)

## Relationship with Valve Stroke

The common stroke of the valve in rotative engines is twice the breadth of the port. (015-068) When there is no lap on either the steam or [[eduction|eduction side]], the valve face is just the width of the port. (015-070) Any lap given to the valve therefore makes the valve face correspondingly longer, and the lap should properly be measured in relation to the stroke of the valve rather than the port width alone. (015-071) (015-074)

In some engines the valve stroke considerably exceeds twice the port breadth, which allows a greater range of lap and hence a greater degree of expansion. (015-072)

## Calculating Lap for a Given Expansion

The amount of lap required to achieve a particular degree of expansion can be calculated when the valve stroke is known. (015-075)

> "From the length of the stroke of the piston subtract that part of the stroke which is intended to be accomplished before the steam is cut off; divide the remainder by the length of the stroke of the piston, and extract the square root of the quotient, which multiply by half the stroke of the valve, and from the product take half the lead; the remainder will be the lap required." (015-078) (015-082)

## Lap and Lead

The lap works in concert with the [[lead]] of the valve. Lead is the amount by which the steam port is already open when the piston is at the end of its stroke. (015-086) When calculating lap, half the lead is deducted from the product, meaning that increased lead reduces the effective lap for a given expansion ratio. (015-082)

To determine when the [[eduction|eduction passage]] will close relative to the end of the stroke, the lap on the steam side, the lead, and the lap on the [[eduction|eduction side]] must all be considered together. (015-086) (015-088)

## Practical Significance

| Factor | Effect of Increased Lap |
|--------|------------------------|
| [[cut-off|Cut-off]] point | Earlier cut-off, more expansion |
| [[valve|Valve]] face length | Longer face required |
| [[steam|Steam]] consumption | Reduced per stroke |
| Power per stroke | Reduced (offset by economy gain) |
| [[eduction|Eduction]] timing | Earlier closure of [[eduction|exhaust]] |

In locomotives and [[marine-engine|marine engines]] where high ratios of expansion are desired, the lap is correspondingly larger. In pumping engines where a full stroke is required, lap may be minimal or absent.
