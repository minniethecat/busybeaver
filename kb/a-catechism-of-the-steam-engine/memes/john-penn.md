# John Penn

**John Penn** (1805–1878) was an English marine [[Steam|steam]] engine manufacturer whose firm, **John Penn & Son**, became one of the principal suppliers of engines for the Royal Navy and early transatlantic steamships. His [[oscillating-engine|oscillating engine]]s and [[trunk-engine|trunk engines]] are extensively described by [[john-bourne|Bourne]] as exemplars of mid-nineteenth-century [[marine-engine|marine engine]]ering practice. (010-135) (054-042)

## Career and Contributions

Penn established his engineering works at Greenwich, London, where he specialized in [[marine-engine|marine steam engine]]s. By the 1840s, his firm was producing engines for some of the most important vessels of the era. The engines of the *Great Britain* — Isambard Kingdom Brunel's revolutionary iron-hulled [[screw-propeller|screw]] steamer — were made off the same patterns as the [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] engines constructed by Messrs. John Penn & Son for H.M.S. Sphinx. (010-135) The *Great Britain*'s engines had cylinders of 82½ inches diameter and a stroke of 6 feet, developing a nominal power of 500 horses. (010-135)

Penn's engines were noted for their compactness, reliability, and the precision of their manufacture. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] draws upon Penn's practice throughout the *Catechism* to illustrate best practice in cylinder design, piston construction, valve gear, and shaft proportioning.

## Key Inventions and Designs

### The Oscillating Engine

Penn was particularly associated with the **[[oscillating-engine|oscillating engine]]**, in which the cylinder itself oscillated on trunnions, eliminating the need for a [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] and cross-head. The piston rod connected directly to the crank, and the steam passages passed through the trunnions. In Messrs. Penn's 50 [[horsepower|horse power]] [[oscillating-engine|oscillating engine]], the diameter of the cylinder was 25½ inches and the length of stroke was 22 inches. (055-058) The trunnion bearings were proportioned at 5½ inches breadth for this size of engine. (055-063)

The parts of Penn's [[oscillating-engine|oscillating engine]] were distinguished by their simplicity: the cylinder pivoted on hollow trunnions that served simultaneously as steam inlet and [[eduction|exhaust]] passages, reducing the number of moving parts compared to conventional side-lever engines. (037-023)

### The Trunk Engine

Penn also built **[[trunk-engine|trunk engines]]** — a form of direct-acting engine in which the [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] passed through a hollow [[trunk-engine|trunk]] in the center of the piston. The figure of Penn's [[trunk-engine|trunk]] engine is described by [[john-bourne|Bourne]] in detail, showing the arrangement of the cylinders, [[trunk-engine|trunk]]s, and cross-head guides. (039-058) The [[trunk-engine|trunk]] engine was particularly suited to naval applications where vertical space was limited, as it eliminated the tall side-levers of conventional [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s.

> "Messrs. Penn's piston for oscillating engines has a single packing ring, with a tongue piece, or mortice end, made in the manner already described. The ring is packed behind with hemp packing, and the piece of metal which covers the joint is a piece of thick sheet copper or brass, and is indented into the iron of the ring." (038-020)

### Valve and Cylinder Design

Penn's valve gear employed a **three-ported valve** for their larger engines, with separate steam and [[eduction|exhaust]] passages that improved the efficiency of steam distribution. (039-064) The valve guard used by Penn was noted by [[john-bourne|Bourne]] to be "rather small" compared to other manufacturers' practice, suggesting a design philosophy that favored compactness. (039-079)

Penn ground his cylinders after boring, laying them on their sides and using a rotating grinding tool — a practice that produced a finer surface finish than boring alone and reduced piston friction. (063-036)

## Workshop Practice

Penn's smaller engines, rated at 12 [[horsepower|horse power]], employed cylinders of 21½ inches diameter with metal thickness of 9/16ths of an inch. (028-043) In his larger 40-inch cylinders, the metal thickness was proportionally increased according to the pressure to be contained. (028-044)

In Penn's smaller engines, the framing was arranged so that the cranks were placed beneath, making the engine compact and self-contained — an arrangement well suited to [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] steamers with limited machinery space. (053-012)

## Legacy

John Penn & Son became one of the largest [[marine-engine|marine engine]] builders of the mid-nineteenth century, supplying engines for vessels of the Royal Navy, the Cunard Line, and other prominent steamship companies. [[john-bourne|Bourne]]'s repeated references to Penn's practice — across cylinder design, piston construction, valve gear, eccentric rods, shaft proportioning, and grinding methods — attest to Penn's standing as a benchmark manufacturer whose designs were studied and emulated throughout the engineering profession.
