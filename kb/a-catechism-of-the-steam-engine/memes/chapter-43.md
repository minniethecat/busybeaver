[043-001] _A._--The cost of a common narrow gauge locomotive, of average power,

[043-002] varies from £1,900 to £2,200; it will run on an average 130 miles per day,

[043-003] at a cost for repairs of 2-1/2d. per mile; and the cost of locomotive

[043-004] power, including repairs, wages, oil, and coke, does not much exceed 6d.

[043-005] per mile run, on economically managed railways. This does not include a

[043-006] sinking fund for the renewal of the engines when worn out, which may be

[043-007] taken as equivalent to 10 per cent. on their original cost.

[043-008] 497. _Q._--Does the expense of traction increase much with an increased

[043-009] speed?

[043-010] _A._--Yes; it increases very rapidly, partly from the undulation of the

[043-011] earth when a heavy train passes over it at a high velocity, but chiefly

[043-012] from the resistance of the atmosphere and blast pipe, which constitute the

[043-013] greatest of the impediments to motion at high speeds. At a speed of 30

[043-014] miles an hour, the atmospheric resistance has been found in some cases to

[043-015] amount to about 12 lbs. a ton; and in side winds the resistance even

[043-016] exceeds this amount, partly in consequence of the additional friction

[043-017] caused from the flanges of the wheels being forced against the rails, and

[043-018] partly because the wind catches to a certain extent the front of every

[043-019] carriage, whereby the efficient breadth of each carriage, in giving motion

[043-020] to the air in the direction of the train, is very much increased. At a

[043-021] speed of 30 miles an hour, an engine evaporating 200 cubic feet of water in

[043-022] the hour, and therefore exerting about 200 horses power, will draw a load

[043-023] of 110 tons. Taking the friction of the train at 7-1/2 lbs. per ton, or 825

[043-024] lbs. operating at the circumference of the driving wheel--which, with 5 ft.

[043-025] 6 in. wheels, and 18 in. stroke, is equivalent to 4,757 lbs. upon the

[043-026] piston--and taking the resistance of the blast pipe at 6 lbs. per square

[043-027] inch of the pistons, and the friction of the engine unloaded at 1 lb. per

[043-028] square inch, which, with pistons 12 in. in diameter, amount together to

[043-029] 1,582 lbs., and reckoning the increased friction of the engine due to the

[043-030] load at 1/7th of the load, as in some cases it has been found

[043-031] experimentally to be, though a much less proportion than this would

[043-032] probably be a nearer average, we have 7018.4 lbs. for the total load upon

[043-033] the pistons. At 30 miles an hour the speed of the pistons will be 457.8

[043-034] feet per minute, and 7018.4 lbs. multiplied by 457.8 ft. per minute, are

[043-035] equal to 3213023.5 lbs. raised one foot high in the minute, which, divided

[043-036] by 33,000, gives 97.3 horses power as the power which would draw 110 tons

[043-037] upon a railway at a speed of 30 miles an hour, if there were no atmospheric

[043-038] resistance. The atmospheric resistance is at the rate of 12 lbs. a ton,

[043-039] with a load of 110 tons, equal to 1,320 lbs., moving at a speed of 30 miles

[043-040] an hour, which, when reduced, becomes 105.8 horses power, and this, added

[043-041] to 97.3, makes 203.1, instead of 200 horses power, as ascertained by a

[043-042] reference to the evaporative power of the boiler. This amount of

[043-043] atmospheric resistance, however, exceeds the average, and in some of the

[043-044] experiments for ascertaining the atmospheric resistance, a part of the

[043-045] resistance due to the curves and irregularities of the line has been

[043-046] counted as part of the atmospheric resistance.

[043-047] 498. _Q._--Is the resistance per ton of the engine the same as the

[043-048] resistance per ton of the train?

[043-049] _A._--No; it is more, since the engine has not merely the resistance of the

[043-050] atmosphere and of the wheels to encounter, but the resistance of the

[043-051] machinery besides. According to Mr. Gooch's experiments upon a train

[043-052] weighing 100 tons, the resistance of the engine and tender at 13.1 miles

[043-053] per hour was found by the indicator to be 12.38 lbs.; the resistance per

[043-054] ton of the train, as ascertained by the dynamometer, was at the same speed

[043-055] 7.58 lbs., and the average resistance of locomotive and train was 9.04 lbs.

[043-056] At 20.2 miles per hour these resistances respectively became 19.0, 8.19,

[043-057] and 12.2 lbs. At 441 miles per hour the resistances became 34.0, 21.10, and

[043-058] 25.5 lbs., and at 57.4 miles an hour they became 35.5, 17.81, and 23.8 lbs.

[043-059] 499. _Q._--Is it not maintained that the resistance of the atmosphere to

[043-060] the progress of railway trains increases as the square of the velocity?

[043-061] _A._--The atmospheric resistance, no doubt, increases as the square of the

[043-062] velocity, and the power, therefore, necessary to overcome it will increase

[043-063] as the cube of the velocity, since in doubling the speed four times, the

[043-064] power must be expended in overcoming the atmospheric resistance in half the

[043-065] time. At low speeds, the resistance does not increase very rapidly; but at

[043-066] high speeds, as the rapid increase in the atmospheric resistance causes the

[043-067] main resistance to be that arising from the atmosphere, the total

[043-068] resistance will vary nearly as the square of the velocity. Thus the

[043-069] resistance of a train, including locomotive and tender, will, at 15 miles

[043-070] an hour, be about 9.3 lbs. per ton; at 30 miles an hour it will be 13.2

[043-071] lbs. per ton; and at 60 miles an hour, 29 lbs. per ton. If we suppose the

[043-072] same law of progression to continue up to 120 miles an hour, the resistance

[043-073] at that speed will be 92.2 lbs. per ton, and at 240 miles an hour the

[043-074] resistance will be 344.8 lbs. per ton. Thus, in doubling the speed from 60

[043-075] to 120 miles per hour, the resistance does not fall much short of being

[043-076] increased fourfold, and the same remark applies to the increase of the

[043-077] speed from 120 to 240 miles an hour. These deductions and other deductions

[043-078] from Mr. Gooch's experiments on the resistance of railway trains, are fully

[043-079] discussed by Mr. Clark, in his Treatise on railway machinery, who gives the

[043-080] following rule for ascertaining the resistance of a train, supposing the

[043-081] line to be in good order, and free from curves:--To find the total

[043-082] resistance of the engine, tender, and train in pounds per ton, at any given

[043-083] speed. Square the speed in miles per hour; divide it by 171, and add 8 to

[043-084] the quotient. The result is the total resistance at the rails in lbs. per

[043-085] ton.

[043-086] 500._Q._--How comes it, that the resistance of fluids increases as the

[043-087] square of the velocity, instead of the velocity simply?

[043-088] _A._--Because the height necessary to generate the velocity with which the

[043-089] moving object strikes the fluid, or the fluid strikes the object, increases

[043-090] as the _square_ of the velocity, and the resistance or the weight of a

[043-091] column of any fluid varies as the height. A falling body, as has been

[043-092] already explained, to have acquired twice the velocity, must have fallen

[043-093] through four times the height; the velocity generated by a column of any

[043-094] fluid is equal to that acquired by a body falling through the height of the

[043-095] column; and it is therefore clear, that the pressure due to any given

[043-096] velocity must be as the square of that velocity, the pressure being in

[043-097] every case as twice the altitude of the column. The work done, however, by

[043-098] a stream of air or other fluid in a given time, will vary as the cube of

[043-099] the velocity; for if the velocity of a stream of air be doubled, there will

[043-100] not only be four times the pressure exerted per square foot, but twice the

[043-101] quantity of air will be employed; and in windmills, accordingly, it is

[043-102] found, that the work done varies nearly as the cube of the velocity of the

[043-103] wind. If, however, the work done by _a given quantity_ of air moving at

[043-104] different speeds be considered, it will vary as the squares of the speeds.

[043-105] 501. _Q._--But in a case where there is no work done, and the resistance

[043-106] varies as the square of the speed, should not the power requisite to

[043-107] overcome that resistance vary as the square of the speed?

[043-108] _A._--It should if you consider the resistance over a given distance, and

[043-109] not the resistance during a given time. Supposing the resistance of a

[043-110] railway train to increase as the square of the speed, it would take four

[043-111] times the power, so far as atmospheric resistance is concerned, to

[043-112] accomplish a mile at the rate of 60 miles an hour, that it would take to

[043-113] accomplish a mile at 30 miles an hour; but in the former case there would

[043-114] be twice the number of miles accomplished in the same time, so that when

[043-115] the velocity of the train was doubled, we should require an engine that was

[043-116] capable of overcoming four times the resistance at twice the speed, or in

[043-117] other words, that was capable of exerting eight times the power, so far as

[043-118] regards the element of atmospheric resistance. We know by experience,

[043-119] however, that it is easier to attain high speeds on railways than in steam

[043-120] vessels, where the resistance does increase nearly as the square of the

[043-121] speed.

[043-122] 502. _Q._--Will you describe generally the arrangement of a locomotive

[043-123] engine?

[043-124] _A._--The boiler and engine are hung upon a framework set on wheels, and,

[043-125] together with this frame or carriage, constitute what is commonly called

[043-126] the locomotive. Behind the locomotive runs another carriage, called the

[043-127] tender, for holding coke and water. A common mode of connecting the engine

[043-128] and tender is by means of a rigid bar, with an eye at each end through

[043-129] which pins are passed. Between the engine and tender, however, buffers

[043-130] should always be interposed, as their pressure contributes greatly to

[043-131] prevent oscillation and other irregular motions of the engine.

[043-132] 503. _Q._--How is the framing of a locomotive usually constructed?

[043-133] _A._--All locomotives are now made with the framing which supports the

[043-134] machinery situated within the wheels; but for some years a vehement

[043-135] controversy was maintained respecting the relative merits of outside and

[043-136] inside framing, which has terminated, however, in the universal adoption of

[043-137] the inside framing. It is difficult, in engines intended for the narrow

[043-138] gauge, to get cylinders within the framing of sufficient diameter to meet

[043-139] the exigencies of railway locomotion; by casting both cylinders in a piece,

[043-140] however, a considerable amount of room may be made available to increase

[043-141] their diameters. It is very desirable that the cylinders of locomotives

[043-142] should be as large as possible, so that expansion may be adopted to a large

[043-143] extent; and with any given speed of piston, the power of an engine either

[043-144] to draw heavy loads, or achieve high velocities, will be increased with

[043-145] every increase of the dimensions of the cylinder. The framing of

[043-146] locomotives, to which the boiler and machinery are attached, and which

[043-147] rests upon the springs situated above the axles, is formed generally of

[043-148] malleable iron, but in some engines the side frames consist of oak with

[043-149] iron plates riveted on each side. The guard plates are in these cases

[043-150] generally of equal length, the frames being curved upward to pass over the

[043-151] driving axle. Hard cast iron blocks are riveted between the guard plates to

[043-152] serve as guides for the axle bushes. The side frames are connected across

[043-153] the ends, and cross stays are introduced beneath the boiler to stiffen the

[043-154] frame sideways, and prevent the ends of the connecting or eccentric rods

[043-155] from falling down if they should be broken.

[043-156] 504. _Q._--What is the nature and arrangement of the springs of

[043-157] locomotives?

[043-158] _A._--The springs are of the ordinary carriage kind, with plates connected

[043-159] at the centre, and allowed to slide on each other at their ends. The upper

[043-160] plate terminates in two eyes, through each of which passes a pin, which

[043-161] also passes through the jaws of the bridle, connected by a double threaded

[043-162] screw to another bridle, which is jointed to the framing; the centre of the

[043-163] spring rests upon the axle box. Sometimes the springs are placed between

[043-164] the guard plates, and below the framing which rests upon their extremities.

[043-165] One species of springs which has gained a considerable introduction,

[043-166] consists of a number of flat steel plates with a piece of metal or other

[043-167] substance interposed between them at the centre, leaving the ends standing

[043-168] apart. It would be preferable, perhaps, to make the plates of a common

[043-169] spring with different curves, so that the leaves, though in contact at the

[043-170] centre, would not be in contact with the ends with light loads, but would

[043-171] be brought into contact gradually, as the strain conies on: a spring would

[043-172] thus be obtained that was suitable for all loads.
