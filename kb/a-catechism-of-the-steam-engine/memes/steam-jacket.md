The **steam jacket** is a hollow casing that surrounds the [[cylinder]] of a [[steam|steam engine]]. Live steam from the [[boiler]] is circulated through this annular space to maintain the cylinder walls at a high temperature, thereby reducing the condensation of steam upon the relatively cooler metal surfaces during admission. The steam jacket was an important innovation for improving the [[horsepower|fuel economy]] of [[steam|steam engine]]s.

## Principle of operation

When steam enters a cold or cooling cylinder, a portion condenses on the metal walls, reducing the effective pressure and wasting heat. The steam jacket counteracts this by surrounding the cylinder with steam at full boiler temperature:

> [015-004] The cylinder should be enclosed in a steam jacket, or should in some other way be effectually protected from cooling by the atmosphere.

By maintaining the cylinder at a temperature close to that of the incoming steam, the jacket minimises initial condensation — one of the principal sources of thermodynamic loss identified by [[james-watt|James Watt]].

## Watt's experience

[[james-watt|Watt]] himself experimented with the steam jacket and confirmed its practical benefit through direct measurement of fuel consumption:

> [037-074] Mr. Watt, in his early practice, discarded the steam jacket for a time, but resumed it again, as he found its discontinuance occasioned a perceptible waste of fuel; and in modern engines it has been found that where a jacket is used less coal is consumed than where the use of a jacket is rejected.

This empirical confirmation — that the jacket saves more fuel than the additional heat lost through its larger radiating surface — established the steam jacket as standard practice for stationary and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s:

> [037-072] The engines are made with steam jackets, and in some cases a flue winds spirally round the cylinder, for keeping the steam hot.

## Construction and insulation

> [068-301] Cylinders should have a steam jacket, and be felted and planted.

The exterior of the jacket is typically covered with several layers of felt or other non-conducting material, and then encased in timber or sheet metal lagging. This insulation reduces heat loss from the jacket's outer surface, ensuring that the jacket steam primarily heats the cylinder rather than the engine room.

## Practical considerations

While the steam jacket adds cost and complexity to the cylinder casting, its fuel-saving benefit was considered well established by [[john-bourne|Bourne]]'s time. The jacket is supplied with steam directly from the boiler, sometimes through a separate branch from the main steam pipe. In some designs, a spiral flue wound around the cylinder served a similar function, though the full jacket was generally more effective.
