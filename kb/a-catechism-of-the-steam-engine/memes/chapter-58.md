[058-001] 661. _Q._--Will you specify the dimensions of the principal parts of the

[058-002] engine?

[058-003] _A._--Each of the cylinders which is 14-1/4 inches diameter, has the valve

[058-004] casing cast upon it. The steam ports are 13 inches long and 1-5/8 inches

[058-005] broad, and the exhaust port is 2-1/2 inches broad. The travel of the valve

[058-006] is 4-1/8 inches, the lap 1 inch, and the lead 1/4 inch. The piston is 4

[058-007] inches thick: its body is formed of brass with a cover of cast iron, and

[058-008] between the body and the cover two flanges, forged on the piston rod, are

[058-009] introduced to communicate the push and pull of the piston to the rod. The

[058-010] piston rod is of iron, 2-1/2 inches diameter. The guide bars for guiding

[058-011] the top of the piston rod are of steel, 4 inches broad, fixed to rib iron

[058-012] bearers, with hard wood 1/4 of an inch thick, interposed. The connecting

[058-013] rod is 6 feet long between the centres, and is fitted with bushes of white

[058-014] metal. The eccentrics are formed of wrought iron, and have 4-1/8 inches of

[058-015] throw. The link of the link motion is formed of wrought iron. It is hung by

[058-016] a link from a pin attached to the framing; and instead of being susceptible

[058-017] of upward and downward motion, as in the case of the link represented in

[058-018] fig. 57 a rod connecting the valve rod with the movable block in the link,

[058-019] is susceptible of this motion, whereby the same result is arrived at as if

[058-020] the link were moved and the block was stationary. One or the other

[058-021] expedient is preferable, according to the general nature of the

[058-022] arrangements adopted. The slide valve is of brass, and the regulator

[058-023] consists of two brass slide valves worked over ports in a chest in the

[058-024] steam pipe, set in the smoke box. The steam pipe is of brass, No. 14. wire

[058-025] gauge, perforated within the boiler barrel with holes 1/12th of an inch in

[058-026] diameter along its upper side. The blast pipe, which is of copper, has an

[058-027] orifice of 4-1/4 inches diameter. There is a damper, formed like a Venetian

[058-028] blind, with the plates running athwartships at the end of the tubes.

[058-030] 662. _Q._--Of what construction is the safety valve?

[058-031] _A._--There are two safety valves, consisting of pistons 1-3/16 inch in

[058-032] diameter, and which are kept down by spiral springs placed immediately over

[058-033] them. A section of this valve is given in fig. 58.

[058-034] 663. _Q._--What are the dimensions of the feed pumps?

[058-035] _A._--The feed pumps are of brass, with plungers 4 inches diameter and

[058-036] 3-1/4 inches stroke. The feed pipe is of copper, 2 inches diameter. A good

[058-037] deal of trouble has been experienced in locomotives from the defective

[058-038] action of the feed pump, partly caused by the leakage of steam into the

[058-039] pumps, which prevented the water from entering them, and partly from the

[058-040] return of a large part of the water through the valves at the return stroke

[058-041] of the pump, in consequence of the valve lifting too high. The pet cock--a

[058-042] small cock communicating with the interior of the pump--will allow any

[058-043] steam to escape which gains admission, and the air which enters by the cock

[058-044] cools down the barrel of the pump, so that in a short time it will be in a

[058-045] condition to draw. The most ordinary species of valve in the feed pumps of

[058-046] locomotives, is the ball valve.

[058-047] Notwithstanding the excellent performance of the best examples of

[058-048] locomotive engines, it is quite certain that there is still much room for

[058-049] improvement; and indeed various sources of economy are at present visible,

[058-050] which, if properly developed, would materially reduce the expense of the

[058-051] locomotive power. In all engines the great source of expense is the fuel;

[058-052] and although the consumption of fuel has been greatly reduced within the

[058-053] last ten or fifteen years, it is capable of being still further reduced by

[058-054] certain easy expedients of improvement, which therefore it is important

[058-055] should be universally applied. One of these expedients consists in heating

[058-056] the feed water by the waste steam; and the feed water should in every case

[058-057] be sent into the boiler _boiling hot_, instead of being quite cold, as is

[058-058] at present generally the case. The ports of the cylinders should be as

[058-059] large as possible; the expansion of the steam should be carried to a

[058-060] greater extent; and in the case of engines with outside cylinders, the

[058-061] waste steam should circulate entirely round the cylinders before escaping

[058-062] by the blast pipe. The escape of heat from the boiler should be more

[058-063] carefully prevented; and the engine should be balanced by weights on the

[058-064] wheels to obviate a waste of power by yawing on the rails. The most

[058-065] important expedient of all, however, lies in the establishment of a system

[058-066] of registering the performance of all new engines, in order that

[058-067] competition may stimulate the different constructors to the attainment of

[058-068] the utmost possible economy; and under the stimulus of comparison and

[058-069] notoriety, a large measure of improvement would speedily ensue. The

[058-070] benefits consequent on public competition are abundantly illustrated by the

[058-071] rapid diminution of the consumption of fuel in the case of agricultural

[058-072] engines, when this stimulus was presented.

[058-073] CHAPTER XI

[058-074] OF VARIOUS FORMS, APPLICATIONS, AND APPLIANCES OF THE STEAM ENGINE.

[058-075] In the English edition of this work, the first part of this chapter is

[058-076] devoted to examples of Portable and fixed Agricultural engines, of

[058-077] different makers and styles of workmanship, but not in sufficient detail,

[058-078] nor illustrated on large enough scale to be of practical value as models,

[058-079] forming rather in fact an illustrated catalogue of the manufacturer, than a

[058-080] study for the mechanic. On this account, they have been entirely omitted,

[058-081] and their place supplied by a few illustrations from American workmanship,

[058-082] not only of Steam Engines, of various forms and applications, but also of

[058-083] various machines, or appliances, connected with the working of engines, as

[058-084] for the determination, or regulation of pressure, of the boilers; for the

[058-085] supply or feed of the boilers, the regulation of the speed of the engine,

[058-086] and the like.

[058-087] The Gauges used in this country to show the pressures of steam in boilers

[058-088] are of various constructions, but perhaps the most common is the Bourdon,

[058-089] or, as it is known here, the Ashcroft gauge, from the party introducing it,

[058-090] and holding the patent. Fig. 59 represents its interior construction. It

[058-091] consists of a thin metallic tube, _a_, bent into nearly a complete circle

[058-092] closed at one end, the steam being introduced at the other, at _b_. The

[058-093] effect of the pressure of the steam on the interior of the tube is to

[058-094] expand the circle, more or less according to the pressure, the elasticity

[058-095] of the metal returning the circle to its original position, when the

[058-096] pressure is removed. The free or closed end of the tube is connected by a

[058-097] link _c_ with a lever _d_, at the opposite end of which is segmental gear,

[058-098] in gear with a pinion, on which is a hand, which marks the pressure on a

[058-099] dial. The dial and hand are not shown on the cut, but are on the exterior

[058-100] case removed to show the construction.

[058-103] Fig. 60 is an elevation of a boiler with Clark's Patent Steam and Fire

[058-104] Regulator attached, for the control of the draft of the chimney by the

[058-105] pressure of steam in the boiler. It consists of a chamber, _a_, with a

[058-106] flexible diaphragm or cover on top, in communication with the boiler. On

[058-107] this diaphragm rests a plunger or piston, which is held down like a safety

[058-108] valve, by a lever and weight, _b_. The end of the lever is connected with a

[058-109] balanced damper, _c_, in the chimney. The weight, _b_, is placed at any

[058-110] required position on the lever, and when the pressure of steam in the

[058-111] boiler, exerted on the diaphragm, becomes sufficient to raise the weight,

[058-112] the lever rises, and the damper begins to close, and to check the draft in

[058-113] the chimney. When properly adjusted, the machine works on a variation of

[058-114] from, one to two pounds between the extremes of motion. When the dampers

[058-115] are very large, say 3 feet or over, they should be set on rollers, like

[058-116] common grindstone rollers; the regulator should be attached directly to the

[058-117] damper, the length of the pipe connecting the regulator with the boiler

[058-118] being of no account.

[058-119] [Illustration: Fig. 61.]

[058-120] Porter's Patent Governor, fig. 61, is a modification of the ordinary

[058-121] centrifugal governor. Very small balls are employed, from 2-1/4 to 2-5/8

[058-122] inches in diameter. These swing from a single joint at the axis of the

[058-123] spindle, which is the most sensitive arrangement, and make from 300 to 350

[058-124] revolutions per minute, at which speed their centrifugal force lifts the

[058-125] counterpoise. The lower arms are jointed to the upper ones at the centres

[058-126] of the balls, and connect with the slide by joints about two inches apart.

[058-127] The counterpoise may be attached to the slide in any manner; for the sake

[058-128] of elegance, it is put in the form of a vase rising between the arms, its

[058-129] stem forming the slide. The vase is hollow and filled with lead, and weighs

[058-130] from 60 lbs. to 175 lbs. It moves freely on the spindle, through nearly

[058-131] twice the vertical distances traversed by the balls, and is capable of

[058-132] rising from 2-1/2 to 3 inches, before its rim will touch the arms. It is

[058-133] represented in the figure as lifted through about one half of its range of

[058-134] action.

[058-135] The standard is bored out of the solid, forming a long and perfect bearing

[058-136] for the spindle; the arms and balls are of gun metal, the joint pins of

[058-137] steel; every part of the governor is finished bright, except the bracket

[058-138] carrying the lever, and the square base of the standard, which are painted.

[058-139] The pulley is from 3 to 10 inches in diameter, and makes in the larger

[058-140] sizes about 125 revolutions, and in the smaller 230 revolutions per minute;

[058-141] the higher speed of the governor being got up by gearing.

[058-142] Mr. Porter warrants the following action in this governor, operating any

[058-143] regulating valve or cut-off which is in reasonably good order. The engine

[058-144] should be run with the stop-valve wide open, and, except the usual oiling,

[058-145] will require no attention from the engineer, under any circumstances, after

[058-146] it is started, until it is to be stopped. No increase in the pressure of

[058-147] steam will affect its motion perceptibly. The extreme possible variation in

[058-148] the speed, between that at which the regulating valve will be held wide

[058-149] open, and that at which it will be closed, is from 3 to 5 per cent., being

[058-150] least in the largest governors. This is less than 1/6 of the variation

[058-151] required by the average of ordinary governors, and is with difficulty

[058-152] detected by the senses. The entire load which the engine is capable of

[058-153] driving may be thrown on or off at once, and one watching the revolutions

[058-154] cannot tell when it is done. The governor will be sensibly affected by a

[058-155] variation in the motion of the engine of 1 revolution in 800.

[058-156] Notwithstanding this extreme sensitiveness, or rather by reason of it, it

[058-157] will not oscillate, but when the load is uniform will stand quite, or

[058-158] nearly, motionless.

[058-159] For the supply of the water to the boiler, in many positions, it is very

[058-160] convenient to have a pump unconnected with the engine. On this account it

[058-161] is very usual in this country to have what are called donkey pumps or

[058-162] engines independent of the main engines, which can be used to feed the

[058-163] boilers, or for supplying water for many other purposes.

[058-164] Fig. 62 is a longitudinal section of the Worthington Steam Pump, the first

[058-165] of its kind, and for many years in successful operation.
