**Eduction** is the phase of the [[steam]] engine cycle in which the steam, having performed its work on the [[piston]], is permitted to escape from the [[cylinder]]. The spent steam passes through the eduction port — uncovered by the [[slide-valve]] at the appropriate moment — and travels via the eduction pipe either to the [[condenser]] (in [[condensation|condensing engine]]s) or directly to the atmosphere (in high-pressure or non-[[condensation|condensing engine]]s).

## The eduction stroke

In a [[double-acting|double-acting engine]], eduction occurs on one side of the piston while steam is being admitted on the other:

> [011-106] In a double-acting engine, at the same time that the steam is acting upon the upper side of the piston, the steam beneath the piston, which has already performed its office, is passing to the condenser through the eduction side of the piston.

The eduction pipe is the conduit from the cylinder to the [[condensation|condenser]] or [[blast-pipe]]:

> [011-039] An eduction pipe, or, as it is commonly termed, an equation pipe in the vulgar parlance of the engine room, leads from the eduction side of the cylinder to the condenser.

> [008-122] The eduction pipe leads downwards from the cylinder into the condenser K, which is immerged in a cistern of cold water.

## Eduction lap

The exhaust opening is controlled by lap on the eduction side of the valve, analogous to [[lap|steam lap]] on the admission side but typically much smaller:

> [015-062] There is far less lap on the eduction than on the steam side, very often there is none at all.

> [015-064] The lap on the eduction side is given to prevent the eduction passage from being opened too early before the end of the stroke.

The timing of eduction port closure determines the point at which compression begins — the trapped exhaust steam is compressed by the returning piston, cushioning the piston at the end of its stroke:

> [015-084] To determine at what point of the stroke the eduction passage will be closed — divide the lap on the eduction side by half the stroke of the valve.

## Eduction in non-condensing engines

In high-pressure engines without a [[condensation|condenser]], the eduction steam is discharged directly to the atmosphere. In [[locomotive|locomotives]] this exhaust is directed through the [[blast-pipe]] into the [[chimney]] to boost the draft. The characteristic "chuff" sound of a steam locomotive is the rhythmic discharge of eduction steam.

| Engine type | Eduction destination | Back pressure |
|------------|---------------------|---------------|
| Condensing (land/marine) | [[condenser|Condenser]] vacuum | ~3–5 psi absolute |
| Non-condensing (locomotive) | Atmosphere via [[blast-pipe]] | ~15–18 psi absolute |
| High-pressure (portable) | Atmosphere directly | ~15 psi absolute |

> [017-066] If we make a deduction of a pound and a half from the effective pressure for the friction of the engine, and for the resistance due to the eduction pipe and passages, we shall have a closer approximation to the actual useful pressure.
