# Cylinder

The **cylinder** is the central working chamber of a [[steam|steam engine]], in which steam expands to drive a [[Piston]] alternately up and down (008-058). It is the core component that converts the expansive force of steam into mechanical motion, and its dimensions, proportions, and construction largely determine the power and efficiency of the engine.

## Design and Construction

The cylinder must be accurately bored and finished to provide a smooth working surface for the piston. It is typically made of cast iron, and its internal surface is turned and polished to reduce friction (008-111). The cylinder cover, or [[cylinder-cover|cylinder head]], seals the top of the cylinder and contains a [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]] through which the piston rod passes; it is secured by a great number of bolts and should be of copper to resist corrosion (031-162) (042-090).

| Dimension | Relationship |
|-----------|-------------|
| Diameter | Determines piston area and thus total force |
| Stroke length | Together with diameter determines cylinder volume |
| Thickness | Must withstand steam pressure without distortion |
| Jacket clearance | [[steam|Steam]] space around cylinder for temperature maintenance |

The [[steam-jacket|cylinder jacket]] — a surrounding casing through which steam is circulated — maintains the cylinder at working temperature, preventing condensation losses (035-119). On large marine and stationary engines the cylinder is often enclosed in a steam jacket to reduce wasteful condensation of the entering steam.

## Operation

In a [[double-acting|double-acting engine]], steam is admitted alternately above and below the [[Piston]] by suitable valves (008-059) (008-060). The piston divides the cylinder into two compartments — above and below — and steam enters each compartment in turn (008-114) (008-118). When steam enters the top compartment, it presses the piston downward; at the bottom of the stroke, the valve mechanism admits steam below the piston while opening the top to the [[condensation|condenser]], and the cycle reverses.

> "The piston is accurately fitted to the cylinder, and made to move in it steam tight, by means of a packing of hemp or soft metal, which is placed round the edge of the piston, and which is squeezed down by screws. The piston divides the cylinder into two compartments, between which there is no communication by which steam can pass." (008-111) (008-114)

The speed of the engine is regulated by controlling the rate at which steam enters the cylinder (008-107). In [[condensation|condensing engine]]s, a passage leads downward from the cylinder to the [[condensation|condenser]], where [[eduction|exhaust]] steam is collapsed into water, creating a vacuum on the [[eduction|exhaust side]] of the piston (008-122).

## Types of Cylinders

[[john-bourne|Bourne]]'s catechism describes several cylinder configurations used across different engine types:

- **Single-acting cylinder**: [[steam|Steam]] admitted on one side of the piston only, used in atmospheric and pumping engines. The piston is pushed down by steam and lifted by a counterweight (008-162).
- **Double-acting cylinder**: [[steam|Steam]] admitted alternately on both sides of the piston, used in rotative engines for mills, marine propulsion, and locomotives (008-072).
- **Oscillating cylinder**: The cylinder itself pivots on trunnions, permitting a direct connection to the crank without a [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]]; used in some marine [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] engines (014-115).

## Role in Engine Performance

The power of a [[steam|steam engine]] is directly proportional to the area of its cylinder. Engine performance is calculated by multiplying the square of the cylinder diameter in inches by the effective pressure and the piston speed (003-028). A cylinder that is tall and narrow produces a different mechanical effect than one that is short and wide, even with the same steam pressure (004-116).

The cylinder of a Cornish pumping engine differs considerably from that of a locomotive: the Cornish cylinder is tall, [[double-acting|single-acting]], and enclosed in a steam jacket, while the locomotive cylinder is compact, double-acting, and exposed to the atmosphere (006-135). On locomotive cylinders, four plugs are [[screw-propeller|screw]]ed into the cylinder cover, which when removed permit inspection of the interior without disassembly (054-004).

> "The power of an engine is determinable by multiplying the square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches by the effective pressure in pounds per square inch, and by the speed of the piston in feet per minute, and dividing the product by 33,000." (003-028)
