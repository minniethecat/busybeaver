# Piston

The **piston** is the movable disk or plug that fits closely within the [[Cylinder]] of a [[steam|steam engine]], receiving the pressure of the steam and transmitting that force through the piston rod to the crank or beam. (001-215) (008-111)

## Construction and Fitting

The piston must be accurately fitted to the cylinder so that steam cannot pass between the piston edge and the cylinder wall, yet it must move freely. The piston is made to move steam tight by means of a packing of hemp or soft metal placed round the edge and squeezed down by [[screw-propeller|screw]]s. (008-111)

The piston divides the cylinder into two compartments between which there is no communication. (008-114) This division is essential to the [[double-acting|double-acting engine]]: steam admitted to one side while the other opens to the [[Condensation|condenser]] creates the driving pressure differential.

> "The piston is accurately fitted to the cylinder, and made to move in it steam tight, by means of a packing of hemp or soft metal, which is placed round the edge of the piston, and which is squeezed down by screws. The piston divides the cylinder into two compartments, between which there is no communication by which steam can pass." (008-111) (008-114)

## Operation in the Engine Cycle

In a [[double-acting|double-acting engine]], steam is admitted alternately above and below the piston by suitable [[Valve|valves]] provided for that purpose. (008-059) (008-060)

| Phase | Upper Compartment | Lower Compartment | Piston Motion |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| [[steam|Steam]] admission (down) | [[steam|Steam]] in | Exhaust to [[condensation|condenser]] | Downward |
| [[steam|Steam]] admission (up) | Exhaust to [[condensation|condenser]] | [[steam|Steam]] in | Upward |
| [[cut-off|Cut-off]] | Expansion | Exhaust/expansion | Continuing |

The piston, by means of a rod called the piston rod, is connected to the cross-head and thence to the [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]]. (008-079) This mechanism changes the reciprocating motion into circular motion at the crank. (001-233)

## Piston Speed and Engine Power

The power of an engine is calculable by multiplying the number of strokes the piston makes per minute by the pressure on the piston. (003-020)

> "The power of an engine is determinable by multiplying the square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches by the effective pressure in pounds per square inch, and by the speed of the piston in feet per minute, and dividing the product by 33,000." (003-028)

The friction of each stroke of a piston is the same, regardless of speed. (005-040) At higher speeds the piston velocity imposes greater strain on connecting components, requiring careful proportioning.

## Piston Types

| Type | Description | Application |
|------|------------|-------------|
| Solid piston | Single metal disk with packing rings | Small stationary engines |
| Bucket piston | Hollow piston with valve for water lift | Pumping engines (008-152) |
| Metallic piston | All-metal construction with spring-loaded rings | High-pressure and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s |
| Trunk piston | Elongated piston with [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] inside | Trunk engines (039-058) |

In pumping engines, the piston is sometimes called a "bucket" and is fitted with a valve allowing water to pass through during the return stroke. (008-152)

In [[marine-engine|Marine Engine]] designs, the piston is typically all-metal, as [[packing|hemp packing]] deteriorates rapidly under high temperatures. [[john-penn|John Penn]]'s piston for [[oscillating-engine|oscillating engine]]s used a single packing ring with a tongue piece, packed behind with hemp — a design [[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes in detail. (038-020)

[[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]]'s locomotive pistons employed [[screw-propeller|screw]]s threaded into the piston rod, though these were at one time liable to work slack until the design was improved. (044-099)
