[017-001] 211. _Q._--Then, each nominal horse power of a modern engine may raise much

[017-002] more than 33,000 lbs. one foot high in a minute?

[017-003] _A._--Yes; some raise 52,000 lbs., others 60,000 lbs., and others 66,000

[017-004] lbs., one foot high in a minute by each nominal horse power. Some engines

[017-005] indeed work as high as five times above the nominal power, and therefore no

[017-006] comparison can be made between the performances of different engines,

[017-007] unless the power actually exerted be first discovered.

[017-008] 212. _Q._--How is the power actually exerted by engines ascertained?

[017-009] _A._--By means of an instrument called the indicator, which is a miniature

[017-010] cylinder and piston attached to the cylinder cover of the main engine, and

[017-011] which indicates, by the pressure exerted on a spring, the amount of

[017-012] pressure or vacuum existing within the cylinder. From this pressure,

[017-013] expressed in pounds per square inch, deduct a pound and a half of pressure

[017-014] for friction, the loss of power in working the air pump, &c.; multiply the

[017-015] area of the piston in square inches by this residual pressure, and by the

[017-016] motion of the piston, in feet per minute, and divide by 33,000; the

[017-017] quotient is the actual number of horses power of the engine. The same

[017-018] result is attained by squaring the diameter of the cylinder, multiplying by

[017-019] the pressure per square inch, as shown by the indicator, less a pound and a

[017-020] half, and by the motion of the piston, in feet per minute, and dividing by

[017-021] 42,017.

[017-022] 213. _Q._ How is the nominal power of an engine ascertained?

[017-023] _A._--Since the nominal power is a mere conventional expression, it is

[017-024] clear that it must be determined by a merely conventional process. The

[017-025] nominal power of ordinary condensing engines may be ascertained by the

[017-026] following rule: multiply the square of the diameter of the cylinder in

[017-027] inches, by the velocity of the piston in feet per minute, and divide the

[017-028] product by 6,000; the quotient is the number of nominal horses power. In

[017-029] using this rule, however, it is necessary to adopt the speed of piston

[017-030] prescribed by Mr. Watt, which varies with the length of the stroke. The

[017-031] speed of piston with a 2 feet stroke is, according to his system, 160 per

[017-032] minute; with a 2 ft. 6 in. stroke, 170; 3 ft., 180; 3 ft. 6 in., 189; 4

[017-033] ft., 200; 5 ft., 215; 6 ft., 228; 7 ft., 245; 8 ft., 256 ft.

[017-034] 214. _Q._--Does not the speed of the piston increase with the length of the

[017-035] stroke?

[017-036] _A._--It does: the speed of the piston varies nearly as the cube root of

[017-037] the length of the stroke.

[017-038] 215. _Q._--And may not therefore some multiple of the cube root of the

[017-039] length of the stroke be substituted for the velocity of the piston in

[017-040] determining the nominal power?

[017-041] _A._--The substitution is quite practicable, and will accomplish some

[017-042] simplification, as the speed of piston proper for the different lengths of

[017-043] stroke cannot always be remembered. The rule for the nominal power of

[017-044] condensing engines when thus arranged, will be as follows: multiply the

[017-045] square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches by the cube root of the

[017-046] stroke in feet, and divide the product by 47; the quotient is the number of

[017-047] nominal horses power of the engine, supposing it to be of the ordinary

[017-048] condensing description. This rule assumes the existence of a uniform

[017-049] effective pressure upon the piston of 7 lbs. per square inch; Mr. Watt

[017-050] estimated the effective pressure upon the piston of his 4 horse power

[017-051] engines at 6-8 lbs. per square inch, and the pressure increased slightly

[017-052] with the power, and became 6.94 lbs. per square inch in engines of 100

[017-053] horse power; but it appears to be more convenient to take a uniform

[017-054] pressure of 7 lbs. for all powers. Small engines, indeed, are somewhat less

[017-055] effective in proportion than large ones, but the difference can be made up

[017-056] by slightly increasing the pressure in the boiler; and small boilers will

[017-057] bear such an increase without inconvenience.

[017-058] 216. _Q._--How do you ascertain the power of high pressure engines?

[017-059] _A._--The actual power is readily ascertained by the indicator, by the same

[017-060] process by which the actual power of low pressure engines is ascertained.

[017-061] The friction of a locomotive engine when unloaded is found by experiment to

[017-062] be about 1 lb. per square inch on the surface of the pistons, and the

[017-063] additional friction caused by any additional resistance is estimated at

[017-064] about .14 of that resistance; but it will be a sufficiently near

[017-065] approximation to the power consumed by friction in high pressure engines,

[017-066] if we make a deduction of a pound and a half from the pressure on that

[017-067] account, as in the case of low pressure engines. High pressure engines, it

[017-068] is true, have no air pump to work; but the deduction of a pound and a half

[017-069] of pressure is relatively a much smaller one where the pressure is high,

[017-070] than where it does not much exceed the pressure of the atmosphere. The

[017-071] rule, therefore, for the actual horse power of a high pressure engine will

[017-072] stand thus: square the diameter of the cylinder in inches, multiply by the

[017-073] pressure of the steam in the cylinder per square inch less 1-1/2 lb., and

[017-074] by the speed of the piston in feet per minute, and divide by 42,017; the

[017-075] quotient is the actual horse power.

[017-076] 217. _Q._--But how do you ascertain the nominal horse power of high

[017-077] pressure engines?

[017-078] _A._--The nominal horse power of a high pressure engine has never been

[017-079] defined; but it should obviously hold the same relation to the actual power

[017-080] as that which obtains in the case of condensing engines, so that an engine

[017-081] of a given nominal power may be capable of performing the same work,

[017-082] whether high pressure or condensing. This relation is maintained in the

[017-083] following rule, which expresses the nominal horse power of high pressure

[017-084] engines: multiply the square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches by

[017-085] the cube root of the length of stroke in feet, and divide the product by

[017-086] 15.6. This rule gives the nominal power of a high pressure engine three

[017-087] times greater than that of a low pressure engine of the same dimensions;

[017-088] the average effective pressure being taken at 21 lbs. per square inch

[017-089] instead of 7 lbs., and the speed of the piston in feet per minute being in

[017-090] both rules 128 times the cube root of the length of stroke.[1]

[017-091] 218. _Q._--Is 128 times the cube root of the stroke in feet per minute the

[017-092] ordinary speed of all engines?

[017-093] _A._--Locomotive engines travel at a quicker speed--an innovation brought

[017-094] about not by any process of scientific deduction, but by the accidents and

[017-095] exigencies of railway transit. Most other engines, however, travel at about

[017-096] the speed of 128 times the cube root of the stroke in feet; but some marine

[017-097] condensing engines of recent construction travel at as high a rate as 700

[017-098] feet per minute. To mitigate the shock of the air pump valves in cases in

[017-099] which a high speed has been desirable, as in the case of marine engines

[017-100] employed to drive the screw propeller without intermediate gearing, India

[017-101] rubber discs, resting on a perforated metal plate, are now generally

[017-102] adopted; but the India rubber should be very thick, and the guards employed

[017-103] to keep the discs down should be of the same diameter as the discs

[017-104] themselves.

[017-105] 219. _Q._--Can you suggest any eligible method of enabling condensing

[017-106] engines to work satisfactorily at a high rate of speed?

[017-107] _A._--The most feasible way of enabling condensing engines to work

[017-108] satisfactorily at a high speed, appears to lie in the application of

[017-109] balance weights to the engine, so as to balance the momentum of its moving

[017-110] parts, and the engine must also be made very strong and rigid. It appears

[017-111] to be advisable to perform the condensation partly in the air pump, instead

[017-112] of altogether in the condenser, as a better vacuum and a superior action of

[017-113] the air pump valves will thus be obtained. Engines constructed upon this

[017-114] plan may be driven at four times the speed of common engines, whereby an

[017-115] engine of large power may be purchased for a very moderate price, and be

[017-116] capable of being put into a very small compass; while the motion, from

[017-117] being more equable, will be better adapted for most purposes for which a

[017-118] rotary motion is required. Even for pumping mines and blowing iron

[017-119] furnaces, engines of this kind appear likely to come into use, for they are

[017-120] more suitable than other engines for driving the centrifugal pump, which in

[017-121] many cases appears likely to supersede other kinds of pumps for lifting

[017-122] water; and they are also conveniently applicable to the driving of fans,

[017-123] which, when so arranged that the air condensed by one fan is employed to

[017-124] feed another, and so on through a series of 4 or 5, have succeeded in

[017-125] forcing air into a furnace with a pressure of 2-1/2 lbs. on the square

[017-126] inch, and with a far steadier flow than can be obtained by a blast engine

[017-127] with any conceivable kind of compensating apparatus. They are equally

[017-128] applicable if blast cylinders be employed.

[017-129] 220. _Q._--Then, if by this modification of the engine you enable it to

[017-130] work at four times the speed, you also enable it to exert four times the

[017-131] power?

[017-132] _A._--Yes; always supposing it to be fully supplied with steam. The nominal

[017-133] power of this new species of engine can readily be ascertained by taking

[017-134] into account the speed of the piston, and this is taken into account by the

[017-135] Admiralty rule for power.

[017-136] 221. _Q._--What is the Admiralty rule for determining the power of an

[017-137] engine?

[017-138] _A._--Square the diameter of the cylinder in inches, which multiply by the

[017-139] speed of the piston in feet per minute, and divide by 6,000; the quotient

[017-140] is the power of the engine by the Admiralty rule.[2]

[017-141] 222. _Q._--The high speed engine does not require so heavy a fly wheel as

[017-142] common engines?

[017-143] _A._--No; the fly wheel will be lighter, both by virtue of its greater

[017-144] velocity of rotation, and because the impulse communicated by the piston is

[017-145] less in amount and more frequently repeated, so as to approach more nearly

[017-146] to the condition of a uniform pressure.

[017-147] 223. _Q._--Can nominal be transformed into actual horse power?

[017-148] _A._--No; that is not possible in the case of common condensing engines.

[017-149] The actual power exerted by an engine cannot be deduced from its nominal

[017-150] power, neither can the nominal power be deduced from the power actually

[017-151] exerted, or from anything else than the dimensions of the cylinder. The

[017-152] actual horse power being a dynamical unit, and the nominal horse power a

[017-153] measure of capacity of the cylinder, are obviously incomparable things.

[017-154] 224. _Q._--That is, the _nominal_ power is a commercial unit by which

[017-155] engines are bought and sold, and the _actual_ power a scientific unit by

[017-156] which the quality of their performance is determined?

[017-157] _A._--Yes; the nominal power is as much a commercial measure as a yard or a

[017-158] bushel, and is not a thing to be ascertained by any process of science, but

[017-159] to be fixed by authority in the same manner as other measures. The actual

[017-160] power, on the contrary, is a mechanical force or dynamical effort capable

[017-161] of raising a given weight through a given distance in a given time, and of

[017-162] which the amount is ascertainable by scientific investigation.

[017-163] 225. _Q._--Is there any other measure of an actual horse power than 33,000

[017-164] lbs. raised one foot high in the minute?

[017-165] _A._--There cannot be any _different_ measure, but there are several

[017-166] equivalent measures. Thus the evaporation of a cubic foot of water in the

[017-167] hour, or the expenditure of 33 cubic feet of low pressure steam per minute,

[017-168] is reckoned equivalent to an actual horse power, or 528 cubic feet of water

[017-169] raised one foot high in the minute involves the same result.
