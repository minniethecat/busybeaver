[064-001] 714. _Q._--Will you explain the method of case hardening the parts of

[064-002] engines?

[064-003] _A._--The most common plan for case hardening consists in the insertion of

[064-004] the articles to be operated upon among horn or leather cuttings, hone dust,

[064-005] or animal charcoal, in an iron box provided with a tight lid, which is then

[064-006] put into a furnace for a period answerable to the depth of steel required.

[064-007] In some cases the plan pursued by the gunsmiths may be employed with

[064-008] convenience. The article is inserted in a sheet iron case amid bone dust,

[064-009] often not burned; the lid of the box is tied on with wire, and the joint

[064-010] luted with clay; the box is heated to redness as quickly as possible and

[064-011] kept half an hour at a uniform heat: its contents are then suddenly

[064-012] immersed in cold water. The more unwieldy portions of an engine may be case

[064-013] hardened by prussiate of potash--a salt made from animal substances,

[064-014] composed of two atoms of carbon and one of nitrogen, and which operates on

[064-015] the same principle as the charcoal. The iron is heated in the fire to a

[064-016] dull red heat, and the salt is either sprinkled upon it or rubbed on in a

[064-017] lump, or the iron is rubbed in the salt in powder. The iron is then

[064-018] returned to the fire for a few minutes, and finally immersed in water. By

[064-019] some persons the salt is supposed to act unequally, as if there were greasy

[064-020] spots upon the iron which the salt refused to touch, and the effect under

[064-021] any circumstances is exceedingly superficial; nevertheless, upon all parts

[064-022] not exposed to wear, a sufficient coating of steel may be obtained by this

[064-023] process.

[064-024] 715. _Q._--What kind of iron is most suitable for the working parts of an

[064-025] engine?

[064-026] _A._--In the malleable iron work of engines scrap iron has long been used,

[064-027] and considered preferable to other kinds; but if the parts are to be case

[064-028] hardened, as is now the usual practice, the use of scrap iron is to be

[064-029] reprehended, as it is almost sure to make the parts twist in the case

[064-030] hardening process. In case hardening, iron absorbs carbon, which causes it

[064-031] to swell; and as some kinds of iron have a greater capacity for carbon than

[064-032] other kinds, in case hardening they will swell more, and any such unequal

[064-033] enlargement in the constituent portions of a piece of iron will cause it to

[064-034] change its figure. In some cases, case hardening has caused such a twisting

[064-035] of the parts of an engine, that they could not afterward be fitted

[064-036] together; it is preferable, therefore, to make such parts as are to be case

[064-037] hardened to any considerable depth of Lowmoor, Bowling, or Indian iron,

[064-038] which being homogeneous will absorb carbon equally, and will not twist.

[064-039] 716. _Q._--What is the composition of the brass used for engine bearings?

[064-040] _A._--The brass bearings of an engine are composed principally of copper

[064-041] and tin. A very good brass for steam engine bearings consists of old copper

[064-042] 112 lbs., tin 12-1/2 lbs., zinc 2 or 3 oz.; and if new tile copper be used,

[064-043] there should be 13 lbs. of tin instead of 12-1/2 lbs. A tough brass for

[064-044] engine work consists of 1-1/2 lb. tin, 1-1/2 lb. zinc, and 10 lbs. copper;

[064-045] a brass for heavy bearings, 2-1/2 oz. tin, 1/2 oz. zinc, and 1 lb. copper.

[064-046] There is a great difference in the length of time brasses wear, as made by

[064-047] different manufacturers; but the difference arises as much from a different

[064-048] quantity of surface, as from a varying composition of the metal. Brasses

[064-049] should always be made strong and thick, as when thin they collapse upon the

[064-050] bearing and increase the friction and the wear.

[064-051] 717. _Q._--How is Babbitt's metal for lining the bushes of machinery

[064-052] compounded?

[064-053] _A._--Babbitt's patent lining metal for bushes has been largely employed in

[064-054] the bushes of locomotive axles and other machinery: it is composed of 1 lb.

[064-055] of copper, 1 lb. regulus of antimony, and 10 lbs. of tin, or other similar

[064-056] proportions, the presence of tin being the only material condition. The

[064-057] copper is first melted, then the antimony is added, with a small proportion

[064-058] of tin-charcoal being strewed over the surface of the metal in the crucible

[064-059] to prevent oxidation. The bush or article to be lined, having been cast

[064-060] with a recess for the soft metal, is to be fitted to an iron mould, formed

[064-061] of the shape and size of the bearing or journal, allowing a little in size

[064-062] for the shrinkage. Drill a hole for the reception of the soft metal, say

[064-063] 1/2 to 3/4 inch diameter, wash the parts not to be tinned with a clay wash

[064-064] to prevent the adhesion of the tin, wet the part to be tinned with alcohol,

[064-065] and sprinkle fine sal-ammoniac upon it; heat the article until fumes arise

[064-066] from the ammonia, and immerse it in a kettle of Banca tin, care being taken

[064-067] to prevent oxidation. When sufficiently tinned, the bush should be soaked

[064-068] in water, to take off any particles of ammonia that may remain upon it, as

[064-069] the ammonia would cause the metal to blow. Wash with pipe clay, and dry;

[064-070] then heat the bush to the melting point of tin, wipe it clean, and pour in

[064-071] the metal, giving it sufficient head as it cools; the bush should then be

[064-072] scoured with fine sand, to take off any dirt that may remain upon it, and

[064-073] it is then fit for use. This metal wears for a longer time than ordinary

[064-074] gun metal, and its use is attended with very little friction. If the

[064-075] bearing heats, however, from the stopping of the oil hole or otherwise, the

[064-076] metal will be melted out. A metallic grease, containing particles of tin in

[064-077] the state of an impalpable powder, would probably be preferable to the

[064-078] lining of metal just described.

[064-079] 718. _Q._--Can you state the composition of any other alloys that are used

[064-080] in engine work?

[064-081] _A._--The ordinary range of good yellow brass that files and turns well, is

[064-082] about 4-1/2 to 9 ounces of zinc to the pound of copper. Flanges to stand

[064-083] brazing may be made of copper 1 lb., zinc 1/2 oz., lead 3/8 oz. Brazing

[064-084] solders when stated in the order of their hardness are:-three parts copper

[064-085] and one part zinc (very hard), eight parts brass and one part zinc (hard),

[064-086] six parts brass, one part tin, and one part zinc (soft); a very common

[064-087] solder for iron, copper, and brass, consists of nearly equal parts of

[064-088] copper and zinc. Muntz's metal consists of forty parts zinc and sixty of

[064-089] copper; any proportions between the extremes of fifty parts of zinc and

[064-090] fifty parts copper, and thirty-seven zinc and sixty-three copper, will roll

[064-091] and work at a red heat, but forty zinc to sixty copper are the proportions

[064-092] preferred. Bell metal, such as is used for large bells, consists of 4-1/2

[064-093] ounces to 5 ounces of tin to the pound of copper; speculum metal consists

[064-094] of from 7-1/2 ounces to 8-1/2 ounces of tin to the pound of copper.

[064-095] ERECTION OF ENGINES.

[064-096] 719. _Q._--Will you explain the operation of erecting a pair of side lever

[064-097] engines in the workshop?

[064-098] A.--In beginning the erection of side lever marine engines in the workshop,

[064-099] the first step is to level the bed plate lengthways and across, and strike

[064-100] a line up the centre, as near as possible in the middle, which indent with

[064-101] a chisel in various places, so that it may at any time be easily found

[064-102] again. Strike another line at right angles with this, either at the

[064-103] cylinder or crank centre, by drawing a perpendicular in the usual manner.

[064-104] Lay the other sole plate alongside at the right distance, and strike a line

[064-105] at the cylinder or crank centre of it also, shifting either sole plate a

[064-106] little endways until these two transverse lines come into the same line,

[064-107] which may be ascertained by applying a straight edge across the two sole

[064-108] plates. Strike the rest of the centres across, and drive a pin into each

[064-109] corner of each sole plate, which file down level, so as to serve for points

[064-110] of reference at any future stage; next, try the cylinder, or plumb it on

[064-111] the inside roughly, and see how it is for height, in order to ascertain

[064-112] whether much will be required to be chipped off the bottom, or whether more

[064-113] requires to be chipped off the one side than the other. Chip the cylinder

[064-114] bottom fair; set it in its place, plumb the cylinder very carefully with a

[064-115] straight edge and silk thread, and scribe it so as to bring the cylinder

[064-116] mouth to the right height, then chip the sole plate to suit that height.

[064-117] The cylinder must then be tried on again, and the parts filed wherever they

[064-118] bear hard, until the whole surface is well fitted. Next, chip the place for

[064-119] the framing; set up the framing, and scribe the horizontal part of the jaw

[064-120] with the scriber used for the bottom of the cylinder, the upright part

[064-121] being set to suit the shaft centres, and the angular flange of cylinder,

[064-122] where the stay is attached, having been previously chipped plumb and level.

[064-123] The stake wedges with which the framing is set up preparatorily to the

[064-124] operation of scribing, must be set so as to support equally the

[064-125] superincumbent weight, else the framing will spring from resting unequally,

[064-126] and it will be altogether impossible to fit it well. These directions

[064-127] obviously refer exclusively to the old description of side lever engine

[064-128] with cast iron framing; but there is more art in erecting an engine of that

[064-129] kind with accuracy, than in erecting one of the direct action engines,

[064-130] where it is chiefly turned or bored surfaces that have to be dealt with.

[064-131] 720. _Q._--How do you lay out the positions of the centres of a side lever

[064-132] engine?

[064-133] _A._--In fixing the positions of the centres in side lever engines, it

[064-134] appears to be the most convenient way to begin with the main centre. The

[064-135] height of the centre of the cross head at half stroke above the plane of

[064-136] the main centre is fixed by the drawing of the engine, which gives the

[064-137] distance from the centre of cross head at half stroke to the flange of the

[064-138] cylinder; and from thence it is easy to find the perpendicular distance

[064-139] from the cylinder flange to the plane of the main centre, merely by putting

[064-140] a straight edge along level, from the position of the main centre to the

[064-141] cylinder, and measuring from the cylinder flauge down to it, raising or

[064-142] lowering the straight edge until it rests at the proper measurement. The

[064-143] main centre is in that plane, and the fore and aft position is to be found

[064-144] by plumbing up from the centre line on the sole plate. To find the paddle

[064-145] shaft centre, plumb up from the centre line marked on the edge of the sole

[064-146] plate, and on this line lay off from the plane of the main centre the

[064-147] length of the connecting rod, if that length be already fixed, or otherwise

[064-148] the height fixed in the drawing of the paddle shaft above the main centre.

[064-149] To fix the centre for the parallel motion shaft, when the parallel bars are

[064-150] connected with the cross head, lay off from the plane of main centre the

[064-151] length of the parallel bar from the centre of the cylinder, deduct the

[064-152] length of the radius crank, and plumb up the central line of motion shaft;

[064-153] lay off on this line, measuring from the plane of main centre, the length

[064-154] of the side rod; this gives the centre of parallel motion shaft when the

[064-155] radius bars join the cross head, as is the preferable practice where

[064-156] parallel motions are used. The length of the connecting rod is the distance

[064-157] from the centre of the beam when level, or the plane of the main centre, to

[064-158] the centre of the paddle shaft. The length of the side rods is the distance

[064-159] from the centre line of the beam when level, to the centre of the cross

[064-160] head when the piston is at half stroke. The length of the radius rods of

[064-161] the parallel motion is the distance from the point of attachment on the

[064-162] cross head or side rod, when the piston is at half stroke, to the extremity

[064-163] of the radius crank when the crank is horizontal; or in engines with the

[064-164] parallel motion attached to the cross head, it is the distance from the

[064-165] centre of the pin of the radius crank when horizontal to the centre of the

[064-166] cylinder. Having fixed the centre of the parallel motion shaft in the

[064-167] manner just described, it only remains to put the parts together when the

[064-168] motion is attached to the cross head; but when the motion is attached to

[064-169] the side rod, the end of the parallel bar must not move in a perpendicular

[064-170] line, but in an arc, the versed sine of which bears the same ratio to that

[064-171] of the side lever, that the distance from the top of the side rod to the

[064-172] point of attachment bears to the total length of the side rod.
