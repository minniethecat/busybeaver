The **blast pipe** is a key component of the [[locomotive]] boiler, in which the [[eduction|exhaust]] steam from the cylinders is discharged upward through the chimney ([[chimney|smokestack]]). This blast of steam creates a powerful partial vacuum in the smokebox, pulling hot combustion gases from the [[fire-box|firebox]] through the boiler tubes, and drawing fresh air through the [[grate|fire grate]]. The blast pipe was the invention that made the high-speed steam locomotive practical.

## Principle of operation

The [[eduction|exhaust]] steam, after performing its work in the cylinders, still carries considerable velocity. By directing it upward through a narrowed orifice in the chimney, it induces a strong upward current:

> [011-103] The exhaust steam from the cylinders is discharged into the chimney through a pipe called the blast pipe, and the momentum of the steam rushing up the chimney creates a partial vacuum in the smoke box, which draws the air through the fire and the tubes of the boiler.

> [022-099] The blast pipe is a pipe which conducts the exhaust steam from the cylinders into the chimney, for the purpose of increasing the draught.

## Effect on boiler performance

The blast pipe directly couples engine power to boiler output: the harder the engine works, the more [[eduction|exhaust]] steam is produced, and the stronger the draught becomes. This self-reinforcing characteristic enables the compact boiler of a locomotive to generate steam far more rapidly than a stationary boiler of equivalent size:

> [022-104] The blast pipe greatly increases the intensity of the draught, and enables the locomotive boiler to generate a large quantity of steam from a small boiler.

> [022-105] The blast pipe is the instrument by which the locomotive is enabled to travel at a high velocity; for without some such artificial means of draught, the boiler would be unable to generate steam fast enough for the demands of the engine.

## Design considerations

The blast pipe must balance two competing requirements:

1. A narrow orifice creates a stronger blast and better draught, but increases back-pressure on the pistons, reducing net power.
2. A wide orifice reduces back-pressure but produces weaker draught, limiting boiler output.

> [022-111] The blast pipe must be proportioned so as to produce an adequate draught without imposing an undue back pressure upon the pistons.

Variable blast pipes, with adjustable orifice areas, were developed to optimize this trade-off across different running conditions.

## Historical significance

The blast pipe was perfected by [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] and contributed decisively to the success of the [[locomotive]] at the Rainhill Trials (1829). Without it, locomotives could not sustain the high speeds and power outputs needed for railway service. The principle remains fundamental to steam locomotive design.
