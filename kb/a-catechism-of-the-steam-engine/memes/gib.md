A **gib** is a tapered metal wedge used in [[steam|steam engine]] construction to secure and adjust the connection between a reciprocating rod — such as the [[piston]] rod or [[connecting-rod]] — and its attachment point, typically the [[cross-head]] or a bearing brass. The gib works in conjunction with a cutter (a parallel-sided key) to form a tight, adjustable joint that can be taken up as wear occurs.

## Gib and cutter

The standard fastening method for connecting the piston rod to the [[cross-head|crosshead]] employs a gib and cutter:

> [038-035] The piston rod is attached to the piston by means of a gib and cutter, but in some cases the rod is riveted over, or secured by a nut.

The gib is the tapered member; the cutter (or cotter) is the parallel member driven alongside it. Driving the gib deeper into the slot forces the two parts of the joint together, eliminating play:

> [029-138] What are the proper dimensions of the gibs and cutters of an engine? The depth of gibs and cutters for attaching the piston rod to the crosshead may be 0.45 times the diameter of the cylinder, and the thickness 0.007 times the diameter.

## Gib dimensions

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] gives precise proportions:

> [029-140] The depth of gibs and cutters for attaching the piston rod to the crosshead is 0.45 times the diameter of the cylinder; and the thickness of the gibs and cutters is 0.007 times the diameter.

This is a surprisingly large proportion — for a 50-inch cylinder, the gib would be 22.5 inches deep (along the rod axis) and 0.35 inches thick.

## Applications in the steam engine

| Location | Purpose | Adjustment method |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| [[piston|Piston]] rod to [[cross-head|crosshead]] | Primary attachment | Drive gib deeper |
| Connecting rod brass | Bearing clearance adjustment | Gib tightens brass halves |
| Crosshead brasses | Guide shoe adjustment | Wedge-driven take-up |
| [[valve|Valve]] gear linkage | Rod length fine adjustment | Gib and cotter joint |

> [044-138] The brass is tightened by a gib and cutter, which is kept from turning by a small pin.

> [044-144] The tightening here is also effected by a gib and cutter, which is kept in place by a small set screw.

## Advantages over bolted connections

The gib and cutter offers several advantages over bolted joints in [[steam|steam engine]] service:
- **Take-up**: Wear can be compensated by driving the gib deeper, without replacing parts
- **Positive lock**: The wedge action is self-locking under vibration
- **Quick disassembly**: Removing the cutter allows the joint to be separated rapidly for maintenance
- **No threads**: Eliminates the risk of seized or stripped threads in high-heat locations
