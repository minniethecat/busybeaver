[031-001] 380. _Q._--What is the benefit of bridges?

[031-002] _A._--Bridges are found in practice to have a very sensible operation in

[031-003] increasing the production of steam, and in some boilers in which the brick

[031-004] bridges have been accidentally knocked down by the firemen, a very

[031-005] considerable diminution in the supply of steam has been experienced. Their

[031-006] chief operation seems to lie in concentrating the heat within the furnace

[031-007] to a higher temperature, whereby the heat is more rapidly transmitted from

[031-008] the furnace to the water, and less heat has consequently to be absorbed by

[031-009] the flues. In this way the bridges render the heating surface of a boiler

[031-010] more effective, or enable a smaller amount of heating surface to suffice.

[031-011] 381. _Q._--Are the bridges behind the furnaces the only bridges used in

[031-012] steam boilers?

[031-013] _A._--It is not an uncommon practice to place a hanging bridge, consisting

[031-014] of a plate of iron descending a certain distance into the flue, at that

[031-015] part of the flue where it enters the chimney, whereby the stratum of hot

[031-016] air which occupies the highest part of the flue is kept in protracted

[031-017] contact with the boiler, and the cooler air occupying the lower part of the

[031-018] flue is that which alone escapes. The practice of introducing a hanging

[031-019] bridge is a beneficial one in the case of some boilers, but is not

[031-020] applicable universally, as boilers with a small calorimeter cannot be

[031-021] further contracted in the flue without a diminution in their evaporating

[031-022] power. In tubular boilers a hanging bridge is not applicable, but in some

[031-023] cases a perforated plate is placed against the ends of the tubes, which by

[031-024] suitable connections is made to operate as a sliding damper which partially

[031-025] or totally closes up the end of every tube, and at other times a damper

[031-026] constructed in the manner of a venetian blind is employed in the same

[031-027] situation. These varieties of damper, however, have only yet been used in

[031-028] locomotive boilers, though applicable to tubular boilers of every

[031-029] description.

[031-030] 382. _Q._--Is it a benefit to keep the flues or tubes appertaining to each

[031-031] furnace distinct?

[031-032] _A._--In a flue boiler this cannot be done, but in a tubular boiler it is

[031-033] an advantage that there should be a division between the tubes pertaining

[031-034] to each furnace, so that the smoke of each furnace may be kept apart from

[031-035] the smoke of the furnace adjoining it until the smoke of both enters the

[031-036] chimney, as by this arrangement a furnace only will be rendered inoperative

[031-037] in cleaning the fires instead of a boiler, and the tubes belonging to one

[031-038] furnace may be swept if necessary at sea without interfering injuriously

[031-039] with the action of the rest. In a steam vessel it is necessary at intervals

[031-040] to empty out one or more furnaces every watch to get rid of the clinkers

[031-041] which would otherwise accumulate in them; and it is advisable that the

[031-042] connection between the furnaces should be such that this operation, when

[031-043] being performed on one furnace, shall injure the action of the rest as

[031-044] little as possible.

[031-045] 383. _Q._--Can any constructive precautions be taken to prevent the

[031-046] furnaces and tube plates of the boiler from being burned by the intensity

[031-047] of the heat?

[031-048] _A._--The sides of the internal furnaces or flues in all boilers should be

[031-049] so constructed that the steam may readily escape from their surfaces, with

[031-050] which view it is expedient to make the bottom of the flue somewhat wider

[031-051] than the top, or slightly conical in the cross section; and the upper

[031-052] plates should always be overlapped by the plates beneath, so that the steam

[031-053] cannot be retained in the overlap, but will escape as soon as it is

[031-054] generated. If the sides of the furnace be made high and perfectly vertical,

[031-055] they will speedily be buckled and cracked by the heat, as a film of steam

[031-056] in such a case will remain in contact with the iron which will prevent the

[031-057] access of the water, and the iron of the boiler will be injured by the high

[031-058] temperature it must in that case acquire. To moderate the intensity of the

[031-059] heat acting upon the furnace sides, it is expedient to bring the outside

[031-060] fire bars into close contact with the sides of the furnace, so as to

[031-061] prevent the entrance of air through the fire in that situation, by which

[031-062] the intensity of the heat would be increased. The tube plate nearest the

[031-063] furnace in tubular boilers should also be so inclined as to facilitate the

[031-064] escape of the steam; and the short bent plate or flange of the tube plate,

[031-065] connecting the tube plate with the top of the furnace, should be made with

[031-066] a gradual bend, as, if the bend be sudden, the iron will be apt to crack or

[031-067] burn away from the concretion of salt. Where the furnace mouths are

[031-068] contracted by bending in the sides and top of the furnace, as is the

[031-069] general practice, the bends should be gradual, as salt is apt to accumulate

[031-070] in the pockets made by a sudden bend, and the plates will then burn into

[031-071] holes.

[031-072] 384. _Q._--In what manner is the tubing of boilers performed?

[031-073] _A._--The tubes of marine boilers are generally iron tubes, three inches in

[031-074] diameter, and between six and seven feet long; but sometimes brass tubes of

[031-075] similar dimensions are employed. When brass tubes are employed, the use of

[031-076] ferules driven into the ends of the tubes is sometimes employed to keep

[031-077] them tight; but when the tubes are of malleable iron, of the thickness of

[031-078] Russell's boiler tubes, they may be made tight merely by firmly driving

[031-079] them into the tube plates, and the same may be done with thick brass tubes.

[031-080] The holes in the tube plate next the front of the boiler are just sensibly

[031-081] larger in diameter than the holes in the other tube plate, and the holes

[031-082] upon the outer surfaces of both tube plates are very slightly countersunk.

[031-083] The whole of the tubes are driven through both tube plates from the front

[031-084] of the boiler,--the precaution, however, being taken to drive them in

[031-085] gently at first with a light hand hammer, until the whole of the tubes have

[031-086] been inserted to an equal depth, and then they may be driven up by degrees

[031-087] with a heavy hammer, whereby any distortion of the holes from unequal

[031-088] driving will be prevented. Finally, the ends of the tubes should be riveted

[031-089] up so as to fill the countersink; the tubes should be left a little longer

[031-090] than the distance between the outer surfaces of the tube plates, so that

[031-091] the countersink at the ends may be filled by staving up the end of the tube

[031-092] rather than by riveting it over; and the staving will be best accomplished

[031-093] by means of a mandril with a collar upon it, which is driven into the tube

[031-094] so that the collar rests upon the end of the tube to be riveted; or a tool

[031-095] like a blunt chisel with a recess in its point may be used, as is the more

[031-096] usual practice.

[031-097] 385. _Q._--Should not stays be introduced in substitution of some of the

[031-098] tubes?

[031-099] _A._--It appears expedient in all cases that some of the tubes should be

[031-100] screwed at the ends, so as to serve as stays if the riveting at the tube

[031-101] ends happens to be burned away, and also to act as abutments to the riveted

[031-102] tube--or else to introduce very strong rods of about the same diameter as a

[031-103] tube, in substitution of some of the tubes; and these stays should have

[031-104] nuts at each end both within and without the tube plates, which nuts should

[031-105] be screwed up, with white lead interposed, before the tubes are inserted.

[031-106] If the tubes are long, their expansion when the boiler is being blown off

[031-107] will be apt to start them at the ends, unless very securely fixed; and it

[031-108] is difficult to prevent brass tubes of large diameter and proportionate

[031-109] length from being started at the ends, even when secured by ferules; but

[031-110] the brass tubes commonly employed are so small as to be susceptible of

[031-111] sufficient compression endways by the adhesion due to the ferules to

[031-112] compensate for the expansion, whereby they are prevented from starting at

[031-113] the ends. In some, of the early marine boilers fitted with brass tubes, a

[031-114] galvanic action at the ends of the tubes was found to take place, and the

[031-115] iron of the tube plates was wasted away in consequence, with rapidity; but

[031-116] further experience proved the injury to be attributable chiefly to

[031-117] imperfect fitting, whereby a leakage was caused that induced oxidation, and

[031-118] when, the tubes were well fitted any injurious action at the ends of the

[031-119] tubes was found to cease.

[031-120] 386. _Q._--What is the best mode of constructing the chimney and the parts

[031-121] in connection therewith?

[031-122] _A._--In sea-going steamers the funnel plates are usually about nine feet

[031-123] long and 3/16ths thick; and where different flues or boilers have their

[031-124] debouch in the same chimney, it is expedient to run division plates up the

[031-125] chimney for a considerable distance, to keep the draughts distinct. The

[031-126] dampers should not be in the chimney but at the end of the boiler flue, so

[031-127] that they may be available for use if the funnel by accident be carried

[031-128] away. The waste steam pipe should be of the same height as the funnel, so

[031-129] as to carry the waste steam clear of it, for if the waste steam strikes the

[031-130] funnel it will wear the iron into holes; and the waste steam pipes should

[031-131] be made at the bottom with a faucet joint, to prevent the working of the

[031-132] funnel, when the vessel rolls, from breaking the pipe at the neck. There

[031-133] should be two hoops round the funnel, for the attachment of the funnel

[031-134] shrouds, instead of one, so that the funnel may not be carried overboard if

[031-135] one hoop breaks, or if the funnel breaks at the upper hoop from the

[031-136] corrosive action of the waste steam, as sometimes happens. The deck over

[031-137] the steam chest should be formed of an iron plate supported by angle iron

[031-138] beams, and there should be a high angle iron cooming round the hole in the

[031-139] deck through which the chimney ascends, to prevent any water upon the deck

[031-140] from leaking down upon the boiler. Around the lower part of the funnel

[031-141] there should be a sheet iron casing to prevent any inconvenient dispersion

[031-142] of heat in that situation, and another short piece of casing, of a somewhat

[031-143] larger diameter, and riveted to the chimney, should descend over the first

[031-144] casing, so as to prevent the rain or spray which may beat against the

[031-145] chimney from being poured down within the casing upon the top of the

[031-146] boiler. The pipe for conducting away the waste water from the top of the

[031-147] safety valve should lead overboard, and not into the bilge of the ship, as

[031-148] inconvenience arises from the steam occasionally passing through it, if it

[031-149] has its termination in the engine room.

[031-150] 387. _Q._--Are not the chimneys of some vessels made so that they may be

[031-151] lowered when required?

[031-152] _A._--The chimneys of small river vessels which have to pass under bridges

[031-153] are generally formed with a hinge, so that they may be lowered backward

[031-154] when passing under a bridge; and the chimneys of some screw vessels are

[031-155] made so as to shut up like a spyglass when the fires are put out and the

[031-156] vessel is navigated under sails. In smaller vessels, however, two lengths

[031-157] of chimney suffice; and in that case there is a standing piece on deck,

[031-158] which, however, does not project above the bulwarks.

[031-159] 388. _Q._--Will you explain any further details in the construction of

[031-160] marine boilers which occur to you as important?

[031-161] _A._--The man-hole and mud-hole doors, unless put on from the outside, like

[031-162] a cylinder cover, with a great number of bolts, should be put on from the

[031-163] inside with cross bars on the outside, and the bolts should be strong, and

[031-164] have coarse threads and square nuts, so that the threads may not be

[031-165] overrun, nor the nuts become round, by the unskilful manipulations of the

[031-166] firemen, by whom these doors are removed or replaced. It is very expedient

[031-167] that sufficient space should be left between the furnace and the tubes in

[031-168] all tubular boilers to permit a boy to go in to clear away any scale that

[031-169] may have formed, and to hold on the rivets in the event of repair being

[031-170] wanted; and it is also expedient that a vertical row of tubes should be

[031-171] left out opposite to each water space to allow the ascent of the steam and

[031-172] descent of the water, as it has been found that the removal of the tubes in

[031-173] that position, even in a boiler with deficient heating surface, has

[031-174] increased the production of steam, and diminished the consumption of fuel.

[031-175] The tubes should all be kept in the same vertical line, so as to permit the

[031-176] introduction of an instrument to scrape them; but they may be zig-zagged in

[031-177] the horizontal line, whereby a greater strength of metal will be obtained

[031-178] around the holes in the tube plates, and the tubes should not be placed too

[031-179] close together, else their heating efficacy will be impaired.
