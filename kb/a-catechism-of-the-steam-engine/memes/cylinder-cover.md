The **cylinder cover** (also called the cylinder lid or cylinder head) is the end plate that closes the [[cylinder]] of a [[steam|steam engine]], forming a pressure-tight seal against the high-pressure steam within. It is secured to the cylinder flange by a ring of bolts and is pierced at its centre by a [[stuffing-box]], through which the [[piston-rod|piston rod]] passes while maintaining steam-tightness. The cylinder cover is one of the most highly stressed components in the engine, subject to both the static pressure of the steam and the dynamic forces of the piston's reciprocating motion.

## Construction and bolting

The cylinder cover must withstand the full boiler pressure acting over the entire area of the cylinder bore. Proper bolting is therefore essential:

> [031-162] A cylinder cover, with a great number of bolts, should be put on from the inside with cross bars on the outside, and the bolts should be strong. (031-163)

In [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s, [[john-bourne|Bourne]] recommends copper or brass bolts to resist corrosion from the damp engine-room atmosphere:

> [042-090] The bolts used to attach the cylinder cover, and they should be of copper or brass, in any situation where they are likely to be exposed to moisture. (042-090)

The practice of putting the cover on from the inside created a cleaner external appearance, as no bolt heads projected beyond the face of the cylinder, though it made disassembly for maintenance more involved.

## Piston rod passage

The most critical feature of the cylinder cover is the central opening for the piston rod. A [[stuffing-box]] is fitted into the cover to permit the rod to slide freely while preventing steam leakage:

> [009-020] The hole in the cylinder lid, through which the piston rod passes, is furnished with a stuffing box to prevent the escape of steam. (009-021)

The [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]] contains packing material — traditionally hemp or cotton soaked in tallow — compressed by a [[stuffing-box|gland]], or more commonly a brass bush, to form a seal around the reciprocating rod.

## Access plugs and relief devices

[[cylinder|Cylinder]] covers typically incorporated threaded plugs that could be removed for inspection, drainage, or to introduce lubricating oil:

> [054-004] There are four plugs screwed into the cylinder cover, which, when removed, permit the interior of the cylinder to be examined, or oil to be introduced. (054-004)

These plugs also served the important safety function of providing a controlled weak point: if water accumulated in the cylinder (from priming or condensation), the plugs could be blown out before the cylinder itself ruptured.

## Cornish engine practice

In Cornish pumping engines, where steam was admitted suddenly at full boiler pressure, the cylinder cover was subjected to particularly severe thermal and mechanical shocks:

> [006-135] The cylinder cover of a Cornish pumping engine, into which the steam is suddenly admitted, and in which the momentum of the particles of the metal is suddenly arrested, is very liable to fracture. (006-136)

To resist these shocks, Cornish engine covers were often made thicker and reinforced with internal ribs. Some designs incorporated a separate nozzle cast into the cover for the steam inlet, distributing the incoming steam over a wider area to reduce the impact on any single point.

## Considerations for expansion and contraction

In engines fitted with [[steam-jacket|steam jackets]], the cylinder cover was exposed to live steam on both its inner and outer faces. This reduced thermal gradients and the associated risk of casting distortion. The cover on the bottom (crank-end) of vertical cylinders bore the additional load of draining condensation water, and its bolts were often made slightly stronger than those on the top cover (044-121).
