[066-001] 729. _Q._--Should the steam be let out of the boiler, after it has blown

[066-002] out the water, when the engine is stopped?

[066-003] _A._--No; it is better to retain the steam in the boiler, as the heat and

[066-004] moisture it occasions soften any scale adhering to the boiler, and cause it

[066-005] to peel off. Care must, however, be taken not to form a vacuum in the

[066-006] boiler; and the gauge cocks, if opened, will prevent this.

[066-007] 730. _Q._--Are tubular boilers liable to the formation of scale in certain

[066-008] places, though generally free from it?

[066-009] _A._--In tubular boilers a good deal of care is required to prevent the

[066-010] ends of the tubes next the furnace from becoming coated with scale. Even

[066-011] when the boiler is tolerably clean in other places the scale will collect

[066-012] here; and in many cases where the amount of blowing off previously found to

[066-013] suffice for flue boilers has been adopted, an incrustation five eighths of

[066-014] an inch in thickness has formed in twelve months round the furnace ends of

[066-015] the tubes, and the stony husks enveloping them have actually grown together

[066-016] in some parts so as totally to exclude the water.

[066-017] 731. _Q._--When a tubular boiler gets incrusted in the manner you have

[066-018] described, what is the best course to be adopted for the removal of the

[066-019] scale?

[066-020] _A._--When a boiler gets into this state the whole of the tubes must be

[066-021] pulled out, which may be done by a Spanish windlass combined with a pair of

[066-022] blocks; and three men, when thus provided, will be able to draw out from 50

[066-023] to 70 tubes per day,--those tubes with the thickest and firmest

[066-024] incrustations being, of course, the most difficult to remove. The act of

[066-025] drawing out the tubes removes the incrustation; but the tubes should

[066-026] afterward be scraped by drawing them backward and forward between the old

[066-027] files, fixed in a vice, in the form of the letter V. The ends of the tube

[066-028] should then be heated and dressed with the hammer, and plunged while at a

[066-029] blood heat into a bed of sawdust to make them cool soft, so that they may

[066-030] be riveted again with facility. A few of the tubes will be so far damaged

[066-031] at the ends by the act of drawing them out, as to be too short for

[066-032] reinsertion: this result might be to a considerable extent obviated by

[066-033] setting the tube plates at different angles, so that the several horizontal

[066-034] rows of tubes would not be originally of the same length, and the damaged

[066-035] tubes of the long rows would serve to replace the short ones; but the

[066-036] practice would be attended with other inconveniences.

[066-037] 732. _Q._--Is there no other means of keeping boilers free from scale than

[066-038] by blowing off?

[066-039] _A._--Muriatic acid, or muriate of ammonia, commonly called sal-ammoniac,

[066-040] introduced into a boiler, prevents scale to a great extent; but it is

[066-041] liable to corrode the boiler internally, and also to damage the engine, by

[066-042] being carried over with the steam; and the use of such intermixtures does

[066-043] not appear to be necessary, if blowing off from the surface of the water is

[066-044] largely practised. In old boilers, however, already incrusted with scale,

[066-045] the use of muriate of ammonia may sometimes be advantageous.

[066-046] 733. _Q._--Are not the tubes of tubular boilers liable to be choked up by

[066-047] deposits of soot?

[066-048] _A._--The soot which collects in the inside of the tubes of tubular boilers

[066-049] is removed by means of a brush, like a large bottle brush; and the

[066-050] carbonaceous scale, which remains adhering to the interior of the tubes, is

[066-051] removed by a circular scraper. Ferules in the tubes interfere with the

[066-052] action of this scraper, and in the case of iron tubes ferules are now

[066-053] generally discarded; but it will sometimes be necessary to use ferules for

[066-054] iron tubes, where the tubes have been drawn and reinserted, as it may be

[066-055] difficult to refix the tubes without such an auxiliary. Tubes one tenth of

[066-056] an inch in thickness are too thin: one eighth of an inch is a better

[066-057] thickness, and such tubes will better dispense with the use of ferules, and

[066-058] will not so soon wear into holes.

[066-059] 734. _Q._--If the furnace or flue of a boiler be injured, how do you

[066-060] proceed to repair it?

[066-061] _A._--If from any imperfection in the roof of a furnace or flue a patch

[066-062] requires to be put upon it, it will be better to let the patch be applied

[066-063] upon the upper, rather than upon the lower, surface of the plate; as if

[066-064] applied within the furnace a recess will be formed for the lodgment of

[066-065] deposit, which will prevent the rapid transmission of the heat in that

[066-066] part; and the iron will be very liable to be again burned away. A crack in

[066-067] a plate may be closed by boring holes in the direction of the crack, and

[066-068] inserting rivets with large heads, so as to cover up the imperfection. If

[066-069] the top of the furnace be bent down, from the boiler having been

[066-070] accidentally allowed to get short of water, it may be set up again by a

[066-071] screw jack,--a fire of wood having been previously made beneath the injured

[066-072] plate; but it will in general be nearly as expeditious a course to remove

[066-073] the plate and introduce a new one, and the result will be more

[066-074] satisfactory.

[066-075] 735. _Q._--In the case of the chimney being carried away by shot or

[066-076] otherwise, what course would you pursue?

[066-077] _A._--In some cases of collision, the funnel is carried away and lost

[066-078] overboard, and such cases are among the most difficult for which a remedy

[066-079] can be sought. If flame come out of the chimney when the funnel is knocked

[066-080] away, so as to incur the risk of setting the ship on fire, the uptake of

[066-081] the boiler must be covered over with an iron plate, or be sufficiently

[066-082] covered to prevent such injury. A temporary chimney must then be made of

[066-083] such materials as are on board the ship. If there are bricks and clay or

[066-084] lime on board, a square chimney may be built with them, or, if there be

[066-085] sheet iron plates on board, a square chimney may be constructed of them. In

[066-086] the absence of such materials, the awning stanchions may be set up round

[066-087] the chimney, and chain rove in through among them in the manner of wicker

[066-088] work, so as to make an iron wicker chimney, which may then be plastered

[066-089] outside with wet ashes mixed with clay, flour, or any other material that

[066-090] will give the ashes cohesion. War steamers should carry short spare

[066-091] funnels, which may easily be set up should the original funnel be shot

[066-092] away; and if a jet of steam be let into the chimney, a very short and small

[066-093] funnel will suffice for the purpose of draught.

[066-094] MANAGEMENT OF MARINE ENGINES.

[066-095] 736. _Q._--What are the most important of the points which suggest

[066-096] themselves to you in connection with the management of marine engines?

[066-097] _A._--The attendants upon engines should prepare themselves for any

[066-098] casualty that may arise, by considering possible cases of derangement, and

[066-099] deciding In what way they would act should certain accidents occur. The

[066-100] course to be pursued must have reference to particular engines, and no

[066-101] general rules can therefore be given; but every marine engineer should be

[066-102] prepared with the measures to be pursued in the emergencies in which he may

[066-103] be called upon to act, and where everything may depend upon his energy and

[066-104] decision.

[066-105] 737. _Q._--What is the first point of a marine engineer's duty?

[066-106] _A._--The safe custody of the boiler. He must see that the feed is

[066-107] maintained, being neither too high nor too low, and that blowing out the

[066-108] supersalted water is practised sufficiently. The saltness of the water at

[066-109] every half hour should be entered in the log book, together with the

[066-110] pressure of steam, number of revolutions of the engine, and any other

[066-111] particulars which have to be recorded. The economical use of the fuel is

[066-112] another matter which should receive particular attention. If the coal is

[066-113] very small, it should be wetted before being put on the fire. Next to the

[066-114] safety of the boiler, the bearings of the engine are the most important

[066-115] consideration. These points, indeed, constitute the main parts of the duty

[066-116] of an engineer, supposing no accident to the machinery to have taken place.

[066-117] 738. _Q._--If the eccentric catches or hoops were disabled, how would you

[066-118] work the valve?

[066-119] _A._--If the eccentric catches or hoops break or come off, and the damage

[066-120] cannot readily be repaired, the valve may be worked by attaching the end of

[066-121] the starting handle to any convenient part of the other engine, or to some

[066-122] part in connection with the connecting rod of the same engine. In side

[066-123] lever engines, with the starting bar hanging from the top of the diagonal

[066-124] stay, as is a very common arrangement, the valve might be wrought by

[066-125] leading a rope from the side lever of the other engine through blocks so as

[066-126] to give a horizontal pull to the hanging starting bar, and the bar could be

[066-127] brought back by a weight. Another plan would be, to lash a piece of wood to

[066-128] the cross tail butt of the damaged engine, so as to obtain a sufficient

[066-129] throw for working the valve, and then to lead a piece of wood or iron, from

[066-130] a suitable point in the piece of wood attached to the cross tail, to the

[066-131] starting handle, whereby the valve would receive its proper motion. In

[066-132] oscillating engines it is easy to give the required motion to the valve, by

[066-133] deriving it from the oscillation of the cylinder.

[066-134] 739. _Q._--What would you do if a crank pin broke?

[066-135] _A._--If the crank pin breaks in a paddle vessel with two engines, the

[066-136] other engine must be made to work one wheel. In a screw vessel the same

[066-137] course may be pursued, provided the broken crank is not the one through

[066-138] which the force of the other engine is communicated to the screw. In such a

[066-139] case the vessel will be as much disabled as if she broke the screw shaft or

[066-140] screw.

[066-141] 740. _Q._--Will the unbroken engine, in the case of disarrangement of one

[066-142] of the two engines of a screw or paddle vessel, be able of itself to turn

[066-143] the centre?

[066-144] _A._--It will sometimes happen, when there is much lead upon the slide

[066-145] valve, that the single engine, on being started, cannot be got to turn the

[066-146] centre if there be a strong opposing wind and sea; the piston going up to

[066-147] near the end of the stroke, and then coming down again without the crank

[066-148] being able to turn the centre. In such cases, it will be necessary to turn

[066-149] the vessel's head sufficiently from the wind to enable some sail to be set;

[066-150] and if once there is weigh got upon the vessel the engine will begin to

[066-151] work properly, and will continue to do so though the vessel be put head to

[066-152] wind as before.

[066-153] 741. _Q._--What should be done if a crack shows itself in any of the shafts

[066-154] or cranks?

[066-155] _A._--If the shafts or cranks crack, the engine may nevertheless be worked

[066-156] with moderate pressure to bring the vessel into port; but if the crack be

[066-157] very bad, it will be expedient to fit strong blocks of wood under the ends

[066-158] of the side levers, or other suitable part, to prevent the cylinder bottom

[066-159] or cover from being knocked out, should the damaged part give way. The same

[066-160] remark is applicable when flaws are discovered in any of the main parts of

[066-161] the engine, whether they be malleable or cast iron; but they must be

[066-162] carefully watched, so that the engines may be stopped if the crack is

[066-163] extending further. Should fracture occur, the first thing obviously to be

[066-164] done is to throw the engines out of gear; and should there be much weigh on

[066-165] the vessel, the steam should at once be thrown on the reverse side of the

[066-166] piston, so as to counteract the pressure of the paddle wheel.
