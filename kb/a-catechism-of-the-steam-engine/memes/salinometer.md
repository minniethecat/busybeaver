# Salinometer

The **salinometer** is an instrument for determining the saltness or specific gravity of the water within a marine [[boiler]]. By measuring the concentration of dissolved salts, the engineer can determine when the water has become too saline and must be partially expelled by [[blow-off|blowing off]], in order to prevent salting and [[priming]] (065-128).

## Operation

In a marine boiler, water is continuously evaporated into steam, leaving behind the salts originally dissolved in the feed water. Over time, the concentration of salt in the remaining water increases — a condition known as salting — which is a potential source of danger, as it can lead to priming and overheating of the boiler plates (052-179). To prevent this, boilers are periodically blown off, discharging a portion of the concentrated brine and replacing it with fresher feed water (052-195).

To determine when [[blow-off|blowing off]] is necessary, a sample of water is drawn from the boiler from time to time, and the salinometer is immersed in it after it has been cooled (065-131). By the graduations of the salinometer the saltness of the water is ascertained, and [[blow-off|blowing off]] is continued until the desired state of freshness is attained (065-132, 065-136). Should the salinometer be accidentally broken, a spare instrument must be kept on board, as the safe operation of the boilers depends upon accurate saltness measurement (065-136).

## Construction

The salinometer is typically a hydrometer-type instrument: a weighted glass float with a graduated stem. When placed in a sample of boiler water, it sinks to a depth proportional to the density of the liquid. The higher the salt concentration, the higher the instrument floats, and the reading on the graduated scale indicates the saltness directly. Modern salinometers may also employ electrical conductivity cells for continuous monitoring, but the hydrometric principle remained standard throughout the steam era.

## Use in Marine Boilers

The salinometer is specific to marine practice, where boilers are fed with sea water. Land boilers, fed with fresh water from rivers or reservoirs, have far less concern with salt accumulation. Marine boilers prevented from salting by [[blow-off|blowing off]] rely on the salinometer as the primary indicator of when this operation is required (068-141). Without it, the engineer would be operating blind, risking both [[priming]] (water carried over into the [[cylinder]]) and scale deposition on the heating surfaces.
