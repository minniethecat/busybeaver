The **trunk engine** is a type of direct-acting [[steam]] engine in which a large hollow pipe called a *trunk* passes through the [[piston]] and the cylinder covers. The [[connecting-rod]] is attached to a pin fixed inside the trunk, enabling it to act directly upon the [[crank]] without the need for side rods or a beam. This compact design was used primarily in marine [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] vessels during the mid-nineteenth century.

## Construction and principle

The trunk engine eliminates the side rods and cross-head by passing the [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] through the interior of a hollow piston extension:

> [010-165] It is a horizontal trunk engine. In this engine a round pipe called a trunk penetrates the piston, to which it is fixed, being in fact cast in one piece with it; and the trunk also penetrates the top and bottom of the cylinder, through which it moves, and is made tight therein by means of stuffing boxes. The connecting rod is attached at one end to a pin fixed in the middle of the trunk, while the other end engages the crank in the usual manner.

This arrangement allows the engine to be built more compactly than a side-lever engine, making it suitable for marine applications where space is constrained. The [[air-pump|air pump]] is typically driven directly from the piston:

> [037-021] A very elegant and efficient arrangement of trunk engine suitable for paddle vessels has latterly been employed by Messrs. Rennie, of which all the parts resemble those of Penn's oscillating engine except that the cylinders are stationary instead of being movable.

## Disadvantages

Despite its compactness, the trunk engine has an inherent thermal inefficiency:

> [037-027] But the vice of all trunk engines is that they are necessarily more wasteful of steam, as the large mass of metal entering into the composition of the trunk, moving as it does alternately into the atmosphere and the steam, must cool and condense a part of the steam.

> [037-032] The radiation of heat from the interior of the trunk will have the same operation, though in vertical trunk engines the loss from this cause might probably be reduced by filling the trunk with oil, so far as this could be done without the oil being spilt over the edge.

This alternating exposure of the trunk to hot steam and cooler external air causes condensation losses, making the trunk engine inherently less efficient than an equivalent [[oscillating-engine]] or steeple engine.

## Comparison with oscillating engines

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] compares the trunk engine with its chief rival for [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] vessels:

> [037-020] Is the trunk engine inferior to the oscillating? A very elegant and efficient arrangement of trunk engine suitable for paddle vessels has latterly been employed by Messrs. Rennie.

However, [[john-bourne|Bourne]] ultimately expresses a preference for the horizontal steeple engine for [[screw-propeller|screw]] propulsion, noting that the trunk engine's steam-wasting tendency remained a persistent limitation despite its mechanical elegance.

## Applications and legacy

The trunk engine found its primary application in [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] steamers where low headroom or compact machinery space was required. The design was championed by engineers including [[john-rennie|Rennie]], but it was gradually superseded by the more efficient [[oscillating-engine]] for [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] vessels and by horizontal engines for [[screw-propeller|screw]] propulsion. The concept of passing a [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] through a hollow piston trunk nonetheless influenced later compact engine designs.
