[061-001] Many engines have been constructed by the Messrs. Latta for the fire

[061-002] companies, of different cities, and have been in successful competition

[061-003] with other engines; the farthest throw ever made by one of their

[061-004] first-class engines was 310 feet from a 1-5/8 inch nozzle; steaming time,

[061-005] starting from cold water, 3-1/2 minutes.

[061-006] [Illustration: Fig. 74 AMOSKEAG STEAM FIRE ENGINE.]

[061-007] Fig. 74 is a representation of one class of steam fire engine, as built by

[061-008] the Amoskeag Manufacturing Company, at Manchester, N.H. The boiler is an

[061-009] upright tubular boiler, of a peculiar construction, the patent right to

[061-010] which is vested in the Amoskeag Manufacturing Company. This boiler is very

[061-011] simple in its combination, and for safety, strength, durability, and

[061-012] capacity for generating steam is unsurpassed. No fan or artificial blower

[061-013] is ever used or needed, the natural draft of the boiler being always

[061-014] sufficient. Starting with cold water in the boiler, a working head of steam

[061-015] can be generated in _less than five minutes_ from the time of kindling the

[061-016] fire. The engine "Amoskeag," owned by the city of Manchester, has played

[061-017] two streams in _three minutes and forty seconds_ after touching the match,

[061-018] at the same time drawing her own water. The boilers are made and proved so

[061-019] as to be safely run at a steam pressure of 140 to 150 lbs. to the square

[061-020] inch; but the engines are constructed so as to give the best streams at a

[061-021] pressure of about 100 lbs. to the square inch, and for service at fires a

[061-022] steam pressure of about 60 lbs. to the square inch is all that is required.

[061-023] The various styles of engine are all _vertical_ in their action, and in all

[061-024] the pumps and steam cylinders are firmly and directly fastened to the

[061-025] boiler, the steam cylinders being attached directly to the steam dome. This

[061-026] arrangement obviates the necessity of carrying steam to the cylinders

[061-027] through pipes of considerable length, and the machine has very little

[061-028] vibratory motion when in operation--so little that it is not necessary to

[061-029] block its wheels to keep it in its place, or to take the weight off the

[061-030] springs before commencing work.

[061-031] The pumps are placed on the engines as near the ground as they can be with

[061-032] safety, and are arranged so as to attach the suction and leading hose to

[061-033] either or both sides of the machine, as may be most convenient or

[061-034] desirable, so that less difficulty will be found in placing an engine for

[061-035] work, and when required to draw its own water, it has only to draw it the

[061-036] shortest possible distance.

[061-037] Each engine has two "feed pumps" for supplying the boiler, and also a

[061-038] connection between the main forcing pumps and the boiler, so that it can be

[061-039] supplied from that source if desirable. The tank which carries the water

[061-040] for supplying the boiler is so placed that the water in it is always above

[061-041] the "feed pumps," an advantage that insures the almost certain working of

[061-042] these pumps. These pumps are of brass, the best locomotive pattern, and one

[061-043] of them running with the engine, when at work, furnishes an ample supply of

[061-044] water to the boiler.

[061-045] [Illustration: Fig. 75.]

[061-046] The engines are exceedingly portable; they can be turned about or placed

[061-047] for service in as contracted a space as any hand engine, and two good

[061-048] horses will draw a first-class engine with the greatest ease, carrying at

[061-049] the same time water for the boiler, a supply of fuel sufficient to run the

[061-050] engine two hours, the driver, the engineer, and the fireman.

[061-051] Fig. 75 is a representation of the class of steam fire engine built by

[061-052] Silsbee, Mynderse & Co., Seneca Falls, N. Y. under Holly's patent.

[061-053] The boiler is vertical, with vertical water tubes passing directly through

[061-054] the fire. These tubes are closed at the bottom and open at the top, where

[061-055] they pass through a water-tight plate, and communicate with the water in

[061-056] the boiler. The arrangement of the tubes causes a constant current, the

[061-057] water rising on the outside of the tubes as they are heated, and its place

[061-058] being supplied by a current flowing downward through the tube to the

[061-059] boiler. The smoke and flame pass among the tubes up through flues.

[061-060] Both engine and pump are rotary, and of the same type. They consist

[061-061] essentially of two elliptical rotary pistons, cogged and working into one

[061-062] another in an air-tight case. The pistons fit close to the inside of the

[061-063] case, and gear into each on the line of their conjugate diameters. The

[061-064] action is somewhat similar to the old-fashioned rotary pump, consisting of

[061-065] two cog wheels in gear with, each other, the spaces at the side of the case

[061-066] being filled with water, which at the centre are occupied by the teeth in

[061-067] gear. In Holly's pump, instead of uniform teeth, and depending on the fit

[061-068] of the teeth with the side of the case and with each other for the packing,

[061-069] there are two large teeth in each piston opposite each other, which have

[061-070] slide pistons, and intermediate with these large teeth are small cogs,

[061-071] which continue the motion of the rotary pistons. The machine works very

[061-072] smoothly, and performs the work necessary, in ordinary service, under a

[061-073] pressure of 50 to 60 lbs.

[061-074] There are many other makers of fire engines in this country; but sufficient

[061-075] examples are given to illustrate the class; so successful have they been,

[061-076] that they are fast superseding hand engines, even in the smaller cities.

[061-126] By this it will be seen, that in a city like Manchester, with from twenty

[061-127] to twenty-five thousand inhabitants, a first-class steam fire engine can be

[061-128] run at an expense not to exceed that of a first-class hand engine, while in

[061-129] service it will do at least _four times_ the work. The cost of repairs is

[061-130] found by experience to be no greater on the steam fire engines than on hand

[061-131] engines.

[061-132] The Excavator, fig. 76, is the invention of the late Mr. Otis, an

[061-133] application of the spoon dredging machine of the docks to railway purposes,

[061-134] with very important modifications. The machine consists of a strong truck,

[061-135] _A_, _A_, mounted on railway wheels, on which is placed the boiler _C_, the

[061-136] crane _E_, and the requisite gearing. The excavator or shovel, _D_, is a

[061-137] box of wrought iron, with strong points in front to act as picks in

[061-138] loosening the earth, and its bottom hung by a hinge at _d_, so that, by

[061-139] detaching a catch, it may fly open and discharge the material raised. To

[061-140] operate the machine, suppose the shovel _D_ to be in the position shown in

[061-141] the cut; it is lowered by the chains _o_, _o_, and thrown forward or

[061-142] backward, if necessary, by the drum _B_, and handle _S_, till the picks in

[061-143] the front of the shovel are brought in proper contact with the face of the

[061-144] cut; motion forward is now given to the shovel by the drum _B_ and handle

[061-145] _S_, and at the same time it is raised by the chains _o_, _o_. These two

[061-146] motions can be so adjusted to each other, as to give movement to the shovel

[061-147] to enable it to loosen and scrape up a shovelful of earth. The handle _S_

[061-148] is now left free, and the shovel _D_ is raised vertically by the chains

[061-149] _o_, _o_. The crane is now turned round, till the shovel comes over a rail

[061-150] car on a side track; the bottom of the shovel is opened, and the dirt

[061-151] deposited in the car. All these motions are performed by the aid of a steam

[061-152] engine, and are controlled by a man who stands on a platform at _f_.

[061-154] 692. _Q._--Having now described the most usual and approved forms of

[061-155] engines applicable to numerous miscellaneous purposes for which a moderate

[061-156] amount of steam power is required, will you briefly recapitulate what

[061-157] amount of work of different kinds an engine of a given power will perform,

[061-158] so that any one desiring to employ an engine to perform a given amount of

[061-159] work, will be able to tell what the power of such engine should be?

[061-160] _A._--It will of course be impossible to recapitulate all the purposes to

[061-161] which engines are applicable, or to specify for every case the amount of

[061-162] power necessary for the accomplishment of a given amount of work; but some

[061-163] examples may be given which will be applicable to the bulk of the cases

[061-164] occurring in practice.

[061-165] 693. _Q._--Beginning, then, with the power necessary for threshing,--a 4

[061-166] horse power engine, with cylinder 6 inches diameter, pressure of steam 45

[061-167] lbs., per square inch, and making 140 revolutions per minute, will thresh

[061-168] out 40 quarters of wheat in 10 hours with a consumption of 3 cwt. of coals.

[061-169] _A._--Although this may be done, it is probably too much to say that it can

[061-170] be done on an average, and about three fourths of a quarter of wheat per

[061-171] horse power would probably be a nearer average. The amount of power

[061-172] consumed varies with the yield.
