# Screw Propeller Trials

The **Screw Propeller Trials** of 1845 were comparative tests by the British Admiralty to determine whether the [[Screw Propeller|screw propeller]] was superior to the [[Paddle Wheel|paddle wheel]] for naval [[Steam|steam]] vessels. (050-082)

Twelve trials were made between HMS Rattler ([[screw-propeller|screw]]) and HMS Alecto ([[paddle-wheel|paddle]]). The decisive tug-of-war test settled the question of [[screw-propeller|screw]] propulsion for the Royal Navy. (050-085)

## Background

By the 1840s, the [[screw-propeller|screw]] [[screw-propeller|propeller]] had emerged as a serious alternative to the [[paddle-wheel|paddle]] wheel. Paddle wheels were vulnerable to enemy fire, obstructed broadside armament, and performed poorly in heavy seas. The [[screw-propeller|screw]] [[screw-propeller|propeller]], being entirely below the waterline, promised to overcome these limitations.

The Admiralty authorized comprehensive comparative trials. Two vessels of identical model and equal power were prepared for the experiments.

## Details

### The Vessels

| Dimension | HMS Rattler (Screw) | HMS Alecto (Paddle) |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Length | 176 ft 6 in | Same model |
| Breadth | 32 ft 8½ in | Same model |
| Burden | 888 tons | 800 tons |
| Nominal power | 200 HP | 200 HP |
| Immersed midship section | 380 sq ft | Same model |

The Rattler was drawn out about 15 feet at the stern to accommodate the [[screw-propeller|screw]]. (050-085) Both vessels were fitted with engine indicators; the Rattler also carried a dynamometer to measure the propelling thrust of the [[screw-propeller|screw]] shaft. (050-085)

### The Trials

Twelve trials were conducted. Under steam alone in calm weather, both vessels left the Nore at 4:54 a.m. The Rattler stopped in Yarmouth Roads at 2:30½ p.m., with the Alecto joining 20½ minutes later. (050-085)

> "The mean speed achieved by the Rattler during this trial was 9.2 knots per hour; the mean speed of the Alecto was 8.8 knots per hour. The slip of the screw was 10.2 per cent." (050-085)

Trials under sail alone measured the drag of each system when not in use. In rough water, the [[screw-propeller|screw]] consistently outperformed the [[paddle-wheel|paddle wheels]].

### The Tug-of-War

The decisive test was the tug-of-war: both vessels steamed at full power, connected stern-to-stern. The Rattler towed the Alecto astern at approximately 2.5 knots, conclusively proving the [[screw-propeller|screw]] [[screw-propeller|propeller]]'s superior thrust.

## Outcome

The trials proved [[screw-propeller|screw]] superiority for naval purposes. The [[screw-propeller|screw]] was less vulnerable to enemy fire, did not obstruct armament, and performed better in rolling seas. (047-166)

Larger-scale trials with the Niger ([[screw-propeller|screw]]) and Basilisk ([[paddle-wheel|paddle]]) confirmed these findings. (050-082) The Admiralty adopted [[screw-propeller|screw]] propulsion for all new warships, shaping naval architecture for decades.
