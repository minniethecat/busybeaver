# John Southern (c. 1758–1815)

John Southern was an English engineer who worked at the [[Boulton and Watt|Boulton & Watt]] firm in Birmingham. He is remembered for his experimental work on the properties of steam and on mechanical friction, which provided valuable data for [[steam|steam engine]] design in the early nineteenth century.

## Career and Contributions

Southern was a close associate of [[James Watt]] and [[Matthew Boulton]], working within the Soho manufactory. His practical experiments contributed directly to the engineering knowledge base that [[john-bourne|Bourne]] draws upon throughout the *Catechism*.

One of Southern's important contributions was a set of experiments determining the relationship between steam temperature and pressure. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] reproduces "results obtained by Mr. Southern: — To the given temperature in degrees of Fahrenheit, the corresponding pressure in pounds per square inch is as follows." (013-116) These experimental tables were widely used by engineers for decades.

Southern also conducted experiments on mechanical friction. "Mr. Southern, in some experiments upon the friction of the axle of a railway carriage, found that the friction was about 1/40th of the weight." (005-059) This empirical data helped engineers size bearings and calculate power losses in early locomotives and stationary engines.

The French Academy and the Franklin Institute later repeated Southern's experiments; while their results showed some differences, as [[john-bourne|Bourne]] notes, "Mr. Southern's results are those with which the practice of engineers is very much identified." (013-138) This attests to the lasting influence of Southern's work on practical engineering.

## Legacy

Southern's experimental approach — measuring real-world parameters and publishing the results for the engineering community — exemplified the empirical tradition that distinguished British [[steam|steam engine]]ering. His steam tables and friction coefficients remained in engineering handbooks well into the nineteenth century, providing a foundation upon which later experimenters like [[Henri Regnault|Regnault]] and [[Morin]] built their more elaborate investigations.
