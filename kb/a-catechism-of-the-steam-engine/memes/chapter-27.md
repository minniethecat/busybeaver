[027-001] 337. _Q_--What are the dimensions of the foot and discharge valves of the

[027-002] air pump?

[027-003] _A._--The area through the foot and discharge valves is usually made equal

[027-004] to one fourth of the area of the air pump, and the diameter of the waste

[027-005] water pipe is made one fourth of the diameter of the cylinder, which gives

[027-006] an area somewhat less than that of the foot and discharge valve passages.

[027-007] But this proportion only applies in slow engines. In fast engines, with the

[027-008] air pump bucket moving as fast as the piston, the area through the foot and

[027-009] discharge valves should be equal to the area of the pump itself, and the

[027-010] waste water pipe should be of about the same dimensions.

[027-011] 328. _Q._--You have stated that double acting air pumps need only be of

[027-012] half the size of single acting ones. Does that relation hold at all speeds?

[027-013] _A._--It holds at all speeds if the velocity of the pump buckets are in

[027-014] each case the same; but it does not hold if the engine with the single

[027-015] acting pump works slowly, and the engine with the double acting pump moves

[027-016] rapidly, as in the case of direct acting screw engines. All pumps moving at

[027-017] a high rate of speed lose part of their efficiency, and such pumps should

[027-018] therefore be of extra size.

[027-019] 329. _Q._--How do you estimate the quantity of water requisite for

[027-020] condensation?

[027-021] _A._--Mr. Watt found that the most beneficial temperature of the hot well

[027-022] of his engines was 100 degrees. If, therefore, the temperature of the steam

[027-023] be 212°, and the latent heat 1,000°, then 1,212° may be taken to represent

[027-024] the heat contained in the steam, or 1,112° if we deduct the temperature of

[027-025] the hot well. If the temperature of the injection water be 50°, then 50

[027-026] degrees of cold are available for the abstraction of heat; and as the total

[027-027] quantity of heat to be abstracted is that requisite to raise the quantity

[027-028] of water in the steam 1,112 degrees, or 1,112 times that quantity one

[027-029] degree, it would raise one fiftieth of this, or 22.24 times the quantity of

[027-030] water in the steam, 50 degrees. A cubic inch of water therefore raised into

[027-031] steam will require 22.24 cubic inches of water at 50 degrees for its

[027-032] condensation, and will form therewith 23.24 cubic inches of hot water at

[027-033] 100 degrees. Mr. Watt's practice was to allow about a wine pint (28.9 cubic

[027-034] inches) of injection water, for every cubic inch of water evaporated from

[027-035] the boiler.

[027-036] 330. _Q._--Is not a good vacuum in an engine conducive to increased power?

[027-037] _A._--It is.

[027-038] 331. _Q._--And is not the vacuum good in the proportion in which the

[027-039] temperature is low, supposing there to be no air leaks?

[027-040] _A._--Yes.

[027-041] 332. _Q._--Then how could Mr. Watt find a temperature of 100° in the water

[027-042] drawn from the condenser, to be more beneficial than a temperature of 70°

[027-043] or 80°, supposing there to be an abundant supply of cold water?

[027-044] 333. _A._--Because the superior vacuum due to a temperature of 70° or 80°

[027-045] involves the admission of so much cold water into the condenser, which has

[027-046] afterward to be pumped out in opposition to the pressure of the atmosphere,

[027-047] that the gain in the vacuum does not equal the loss of power occasioned by

[027-048] the additional load upon the pump, and there is therefore a clear loss by

[027-049] the reduction of the temperature below 100°, if such reduction be caused by

[027-050] the admission of an additional quantity of water. If the reduction of

[027-051] temperature, however, be caused by the use of colder water, there is a gain

[027-052] produced by it, though the gain will within certain limits be greater if

[027-053] advantage be taken of the lowness of the temperature to diminish the

[027-054] quantity of injection.

[027-055] 334. _Q._--How do you determine the proper area of the injection orifice?

[027-056] _A._--The area of the injection orifice proper for any engine can easily be

[027-057] told when the quantity of water requisite to condense the steam is known,

[027-058] and the pressure is specified under which the water enters the condenser.

[027-059] The vacuum in the condenser may be taken at 26 inches of mercury, which is

[027-060] equivalent to a column of water 29.4 ft. high, and the square root of 29.4

[027-061] multiplied by 8.021 is 43.15, which is the velocity in feet per second that

[027-062] a heavy body would acquire in falling 29.4 ft., or with which the water

[027-063] would enter the condenser. Now, if a cubic foot of water evaporated per

[027-064] hour be equivalent to an actual horse power, and 28.9 cubic inches of water

[027-065] be requisite for the condensation of a cubic inch of water in the form of

[027-066] steam, 28.9 cubic feet of condensing water per horse power per hour, or

[027-067] 13.905 cubic inches per second, will be necessary for the engine, and the

[027-068] size of the injection orifice must be such that this quantity of water

[027-069] flowing with the velocity of 43.15 ft. per second, or 517.8 inches per

[027-070] second, will gain admission to the condenser. Dividing, therefore, 13.905,

[027-071] the number of cubic inches to be injected, by 517.8, the velocity of influx

[027-072] in inches per second, we get 0.02685 for the area of the orifice in square

[027-073] inches; but inasmuch as it has been found by experiment that the actual

[027-074] discharge of water through a hole in a thin plate is only six tenths of the

[027-075] theoretical discharge on account of the contracted vein, the area of the

[027-076] orifice must be increased in the proportion of such diminution of effect,

[027-077] or be made 0.04475, or 1/22d of a square inch per horse power. This, it

[027-078] will be remarked, is the theoretical area required per actual horse power;

[027-079] but as the friction and contractions in the pipe further reduce the

[027-080] discharge, the area is made 1/15th of a square inch per actual horse power,

[027-081] or rather per cubic foot of water evaporated from the boiler.

[027-082] 335. _Q._--Cannot the condensation of the steam be accomplished by any

[027-083] other means than by the admission of cold water into the condenser?

[027-084] _A._--It may be accomplished by the method of external cold, as it is

[027-085] called, which consists in the application of a large number of thin

[027-086] metallic surfaces to the condenser, on the one side of which the steam

[027-087] circulates, while on the other side there is a constant current of cold

[027-088] water, and the steam is condensed by coming into contact with the cold

[027-089] surfaces, without mingling with the water used for the purpose of

[027-090] refrigeration. The first kind of condenser employed by Mr. Watt was

[027-091] constructed after this fashion, but he found it in practice to be

[027-092] inconvenient from its size, and to become furred up or incrusted when the

[027-093] water was bad, whereby the conducting power of the metal was impaired. He

[027-094] therefore reverted to the use of the jet of cold water, as being upon the

[027-095] whole preferable. The jet entered the condenser instead of the cylinder as

[027-096] was the previous practice, and this method is now the one in common use.

[027-097] Some few years ago, a good number of steam vessels were fitted with Hall's

[027-098] condensers, which operated on the principle of external cold, and which

[027-099] consisted of a faggot of small copper tubes surrounded by water; but the

[027-100] use of those condensers has not been persisted in, and most of the vessels

[027-101] fitted with them have returned to the ordinary plan.

[027-102] 336. _Q._--You stated that the capacity of the feed pump was 1/240th of the

[027-103] capacity of the cylinder in the case of condensing engines,--the engine

[027-104] being double acting and the pump single acting,--and that in high pressure

[027-105] engines the capacity of the pump should be greater in proportion to the

[027-106] pressure of the steam. Can you give any rule that will express the proper

[027-107] capacity for the feed pump at all pressures?

[027-108] _A._--That will not be difficult. In low pressure engines the pressure in

[027-109] the boiler may be taken at 5 lbs. above the atmospheric pressure, or 20

[027-110] lbs. altogether; and as high pressure steam is merely low pressure steam

[027-111] compressed into a smaller compass, the size of the feed pump in relation to

[027-112] the size of the cylinder must obviously vary in the direct proportion of

[027-113] the pressure; and if it be 1/240th of the capacity of the cylinder when the

[027-114] total pressure of the steam is 20 lbs., it must be 1/120th of the capacity

[027-115] of the cylinder when the pressure is 40 lbs. per square inch, or 25 lbs.

[027-116] per square inch above the atmospheric pressure. This law of variation is

[027-117] expressed by the following rule:--multiply the capacity of the cylinder in

[027-118] cubic inches by the total pressure of the steam in lbs. per square inch, or

[027-119] the pressure per square inch on the safety valve plus 15, and divide the

[027-120] product by 4,800; the quotient is the capacity of the feed pump in cubic

[027-121] inches, when the feed pump is single acting and the engine double acting.

[027-122] If the feed pump be double acting, or the engine single acting, the

[027-123] capacity of the pump must just be one half of what is given by this rule.

[027-124] 337. _Q._--But should not some addition be made to the size of pump thus

[027-125] obtained if the pump works at a high rate of speed?

[027-126] _A._--No; this rule makes allowance for defective action. All pumps lift

[027-127] much less water than is due to the size of their barrels and the number of

[027-128] their strokes. Moderately good pumps lose 50 per cent. of their theoretical

[027-129] effect, and bad pumps 80 per cent.

[027-130] 338. _Q._--To what is this loss of effect to be chiefly ascribed?

[027-131] _A._--Mainly to the inertia of the water, which, if the pump piston be

[027-132] drawn up very rapidly, cannot follow it with sufficient rapidity; so that

[027-133] there may be a vacant space between the piston and the water; and at the

[027-134] return stroke the momentum of the water in the pipe expends itself in

[027-135] giving a reverse motion to the column of water approaching the pump.

[027-136] Messrs. Kirchweger and Prusman, of Hanover, have investigated this subject

[027-137] by applying a revolving cock at the end of a pipe leading from an elevated

[027-138] cistern containing water, and the water escaped at every revolution of the

[027-139] cock in the same manner as if a pump were drawing it. With a column of

[027-140] water of 17 feet, they found that at 80 revolutions of the cock per minute,

[027-141] the water delivered per minute by the cock was 9.45 gallons; but with 140

[027-142] revolutions of the cock per minute, the water delivered per minute by the

[027-143] cock was only 5.42 gallons. They subsequently applied an air vessel to the

[027-144] pipe beside the cock, when the discharge rose to 12.9 gallons per minute

[027-145] with 80 revolutions, and 18.28 gallons with 140 revolutions. Air vessels

[027-146] should therefore be applied to the suction side of fast moving pumps, and

[027-147] this is now done with good results.

[027-148] 339. _Q._--What are the usual dimensions of the cold water pump of land

[027-149] engines?

[027-150] _A._--If to condense a cubic inch of water raised into steam 28.9 cubic

[027-151] inches of condensing water are required, then the cold water pump ought to

[027-152] be 28.9 times larger than the feed pump, supposing that its losses were

[027-153] equally great. The feed pump, however, is made sufficiently large to

[027-154] compensate for leaks in the boiler and loss of steam through the safety

[027-155] valve, so that it will be sufficient if the cold water pump be 24 times

[027-156] larger than the feed pump. This ratio is preserved by the following rule:--

[027-157] multiply the capacity of the cylinder in cubic inches by the total pressure

[027-158] of the steam per square inch, or the pressure on the safety valve plus 15,

[027-159] and divide the product by 200. The quotient is the proper capacity of the

[027-160] cold water pump in cubic inches when the engine is double acting, and the

[027-161] pump single acting.
