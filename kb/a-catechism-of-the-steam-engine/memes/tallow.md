**Tallow** — rendered animal fat, typically from beef or mutton — was the principal lubricant used in early [[steam]] engines for lubricating [[cylinder]] bores, [[piston]] rods, and [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]] [[packing]]. Because tallow is insoluble in water and maintains its lubricating properties at the high temperatures found in [[cylinder|steam cylinder]]s, it remained the cylinder lubricant of choice well into the mid-19th century, long after mineral oils had become available for colder bearing applications.

## Use in cylinder lubrication

Tallow was introduced into the cylinder either by pouring it directly into the steam supply or through dedicated tallow cups:

> [005-066] With tallow as a lubricant, the friction of the piston was found to be about 1/39th of the pressure upon it.

> [042-002] How is the cylinder supplied with tallow? It is forced into a hole in the cylinder by means of a tallow cock, being previously melted so that it may flow easily.

The tallow cock is a small valve through which melted tallow is injected into the steam space, where it coats the cylinder walls and piston:

> [042-004] The tallow cock consists of a small receptacle for the melted tallow, which is forced into the cylinder by the pressure of the steam acting upon it when the cock is opened.

## Packing and rod lubrication

Tallow is also essential for lubricating and preserving the [[packing|hemp packing]] in [[stuffing-box|stuffing box]]es:

> [035-108] Abundance of tallow should be allowed, especially at first, to diminish the friction of the packing against the rod and prevent the rod from being scored.

The tallow saturates the hemp fibers, reducing friction and preventing the packing from burning or hardening against the hot reciprocating rod:

> [037-123] In some cases a piece of sheet India rubber is interposed between the coils of the packing, which effects a great saving of tallow. It has been found that packing arranged in this manner is more easily kept, and requires less tallow to maintain it in good condition.

## Tallow-soaked packings

A common workshop practice was to impregnate packing material with tallow before installation:

> [055-123] The packing is first immersed in boiling tallow, and is then compressed in a mould to the desired shape. By this means the packing is thoroughly saturated with the lubricating material.

## Properties and limitations

| Property | Advantage | Limitation |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| High melting point | Remains viscous in hot cylinders | Solidifies in cold pipes |
| Water insolubility | Not washed away by wet steam | — |
| Animal fat base | Readily available in the 19th century | Could turn rancid in storage |
| Low friction with iron | ~1/39 of load | Higher friction than modern oils |
| Natural origin | Non-toxic, traditional | Varied quality between batches |

> [068-046] The engineer's stores for a long voyage should include a liberal supply of tallow, and a small box of waste for wiping the machinery.

By the late 19th century, mineral-based cylinder oils gradually replaced tallow, though tallow continued in use on some older engines and in situations where its specific properties were valued.
