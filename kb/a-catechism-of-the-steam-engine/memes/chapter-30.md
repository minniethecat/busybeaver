[030-001] CHAPTER VII.

[030-002] CONSTRUCTIVE DETAILS OF BOILERS.

[030-003] *       *       *       *       *

[030-004] LAND AND MARINE BOILERS.

[030-005] 369. _Q._--Will you explain the course of procedure in the construction and

[030-006] setting of wagon boilers?

[030-007] _A._--Most boilers are made of plates three eighths of an inch thick, and

[030-008] the rivets are from three eighths to three fourths of an inch in diameter.

[030-009] In the bottom and sides of a wagon boiler the heads of the rivets, or the

[030-010] ends formed on the rivets before they are inserted, should be large and

[030-011] placed next the fire, or on the outside; whereas on the top of the boiler

[030-012] the heads should be on the inside. The rivets should be placed about two

[030-013] inches distant from centre to centre, and the centre of the row of rivets

[030-014] should be about one inch from the edge of the plate. The edges of the

[030-015] plates should be truly cut, both inside and outside, and after the parts of

[030-016] the boiler have been riveted together, the edges of the plates should be

[030-017] set up or caulked with a blunt chisel about a quarter of an inch thick in

[030-018] the point, and struck by a hammer of about three or four pounds weight, one

[030-019] man holding the caulking tool while another strikes.

[030-020] 370. _Q._--Is this the usual mode of caulking?

[030-021] _A._--No, it is not the usual mode; but it is the best mode, and is the

[030-022] mode adopted by Mr. Watt. The usual mode now is for one man to caulk the

[030-023] seams with a hammer in one hand and a caulking chisel in the other, and in

[030-024] some of the difficult corners of marine flue boilers it is not easy for two

[030-025] men to get in. A good deal of the caulking has also sometimes to be done

[030-026] with the left hand.

[030-027] 371. _Q._--Should the boiler be proved after caulking?

[030-028] _A._--The boiler should be filled with water and caulked afresh in any

[030-029] leaky part. When emptied again, all the joints should be painted with a

[030-030] solution of sal ammoniac in urine, and so soon as the seams are well rusted

[030-031] they should be dried with a gentle fire, and then be painted over with a

[030-032] thin putty formed of whiting and linseed oil, the heat being continued

[030-033] until the putty becomes so hard that it cannot be readily scratched with

[030-034] the nail, and care must be taken neither to burn the putty nor to

[030-035] discontinue the fire until it has become quite dry.

[030-036] 372. _Q._--How should the brickwork setting of a wagon boiler be built?

[030-037] _A._--In building the brickwork for the setting of the boiler, the part

[030-038] upon which the heat acts with most intensity is to be built with clay

[030-039] instead of mortar, but mortar is to be used on the outside of the work. Old

[030-040] bars of flat iron may be laid under the boiler chime to prevent that part

[030-041] of the boiler from being burned out, and bars of iron should also run

[030-042] through the brickwork to prevent it from splitting. The top of the boiler

[030-043] is to be covered with brickwork laid in the best lime, and if the lime be

[030-044] not of the hydraulic kind, it should be mixed with Dutch terrass, to make

[030-045] it impenetrable to water. The top of the boiler should be well plastered

[030-046] with this lime, which will greatly conduce to the tightness of the seams.

[030-047] Openings into the flues must be left in convenient situations to enable the

[030-048] flues to be swept out when required, and these openings may be closed with

[030-049] cast iron doors jointed with clay or mortar, which may be easily removed

[030-050] when required. Adjacent to the chimney a slit must be left in the top of

[030-051] the flue with a groove in the brickwork to enable the sliding door or

[030-052] damper to be fixed in that situation, which by being lowered into the flue

[030-053] will obstruct the passage of the smoke and moderate the draught, whereby

[030-054] the chimney will be prevented from drawing the flame into it before the

[030-055] heat has acted sufficiently upon the boiler.

[030-056] 373. _Q._--Are marine constructed in the same way as land boilers?

[030-057] _A._--There is very little difference in the two cases: the whole of the

[030-058] shells of marine boilers, however, should be double riveted with rivets

[030-059] 11/16ths of an inch in diameter, and 2-3/8th inches from centre to centre,

[030-060] the weakening effect of double riveting being much less than that of single

[030-061] riveting. The furnaces above the line of bars should be of the best

[030-062] Lowmoor, Bowling, or Staffordshire scrap plates, and the portion of each

[030-063] furnace above the bars should consist only of three plates, one for the top

[030-064] and one for each side, the lower seam of the side plates being situated

[030-065] beneath the level of the bars, so as not to be exposed to the heat of the

[030-066] furnace. The tube plates of tubular boilers should be of the best Lowmoor,

[030-067] or Bowling iron, seven eighths to one inch thick: the shells should be of

[030-068] the best Staffordshire, or Thornycroft S crown iron, 7/16ths of an inch

[030-069] thick.

[030-070] 374. _Q._--Of what kind of iron should the angle iron or corner iron be

[030-071] composed?

[030-072] _A._--Angle iron should not be used in the construction of boilers, as in

[030-073] the manufacture it becomes reedy, and is apt to split up in the direction

[030-074] of its length: it is much the safer practice to bend the plates at the

[030-075] corners of the boiler; but this must be carefully done, without introducing

[030-076] any more sharp bends than can be avoided, and plates which require to be

[030-077] bent much should be of Lowmoor iron. It will usually be found expedient to

[030-078] introduce a ring of angle iron around the furnace mouths, though it is

[030-079] discarded in the other parts of the boiler; but it should be used as

[030-080] sparingly as possible, and any that is used should be of the best quality.

[030-081] 375. _Q._--Is it not important to have the holes in the plates opposite to

[030-082] one another?

[030-083] _A._--The whole of the plates of a boiler should have the holes for the

[030-084] rivets punched, and the edges cut straight, by means of self-acting

[030-085] machinery, in which a travelling table carries forward the plate with an

[030-086] equal progression every stroke of the punch or shears; and machinery of

[030-087] this kind is now extensively employed. The practice of forcing the parts of

[030-088] boilers together with violence, by means of screw-jacks, and drifts through

[030-089] the holes, should not be permitted; as a great strain may thus be thrown

[030-090] upon the rivets, even when there is no steam in the boiler. All rivets

[030-091] should be of the best Lowmoor iron. The work should be caulked both within

[030-092] and without wherever it is accessible, but in the more confined situations

[030-093] within the flues the caulking will in many cases have to be done with the

[030-094] hand or chipping hammer, instead of the heavy hammer previously prescribed.

[030-095] 376. _Q._--How is the setting of marine boilers with internal furnaces

[030-096] effected?

[030-097] _A._--In the setting of marine boilers care must be taken that no copper

[030-098] bolts or nails project above the wooden platform upon which they rest, and

[030-099] also that no projecting copper bolts in the sides of the ship touch the

[030-100] boiler, as the galvanic action in such a case would probably soon wear the

[030-101] points of contact into holes. The platform may consist of three inch

[030-102] planking laid across the keelsons nailed with iron, nails, the heads of

[030-103] which are well punched down, and caulked and puttied like a deck. The

[030-104] surface may then be painted over with thin putty, and fore and aft boards

[030-105] of half the thickness may then be laid down and nailed securely with iron

[030-106] nails, having the heads well punched down. This platform must then be

[030-107] covered thinly and evenly with mastic cement and the boiler be set down

[030-108] upon it, and the cement must be caulked beneath the boiler by means of

[030-109] wooden caulking tools, so as completely to fill every vacuity. Coomings of

[030-110] wood sloped on the top must next be set round the boiler, and the space

[030-111] between the coomings and the boiler must be caulked full of cement, and be

[030-112] smoothed off on the top to the slope of the coomings, so as to throw off

[030-113] any water that might be disposed to enter between the coomings and the

[030-114] boiler.

[030-115] 377. _Q._--How is the cement used for setting marine boilers compounded?

[030-116] _A._--Mastic cement proper for the setting of boilers is sold in many

[030-117] places ready made. Hamelin's mastic is compounded as follows:--to any given

[030-118] weight of sand or pulverized earthenware add two thirds such given weight

[030-119] of powdered Bath, Portland, or other similar stone, and to every 560 lbs.

[030-120] weight of the mixture add 40 lbs. weight of litharge, 2 lbs. of powdered

[030-121] glass or flint, 1 lb. of minium, and 2 lbs. of gray oxide of lead; pass the

[030-122] mixture through a sieve, and keep it in a powder for use. When wanted for

[030-123] use, a sufficient quantity of the powder is mixed with some vegetable oil

[030-124] upon a board or in a trough in the manner of mortar, in the proportion of

[030-125] 605 lbs. of the powder to 5 gallons of linseed, walnut, or pink oil, and

[030-126] the mixture is stirred and trodden upon until it assumes the appearance of

[030-127] moistened sand, when it is ready for use. The cement should be used on the

[030-128] same day as the oil is added, else it will be set into a solid mass.

[030-129] 378. _Q._--What is the best length of the furnaces of marine boilers?

[030-130] _A._--It has already been stated that furnace bars should not much exceed

[030-131] six feet in length, as it is difficult to manage long furnaces; but it is a

[030-132] frequent practice to make the furnaces long and narrow, the consequence of

[030-133] which is, that it is impossible to fire them effectually at the after end,

[030-134] especially upon long voyages and in stormy weather, and air escapes into

[030-135] the flues at the after end of the bars, whereby the efficacy of the boiler

[030-136] is diminished. Where the bars are very long it will generally be found that

[030-137] an increased supply of steam and a diminished consumption of coal will be

[030-138] the consequence of shortening them, and the bars should always lie with a

[030-139] considerable inclination to facilitate the distribution of the fuel over

[030-140] the after part of the furnace. When there are two lengths of bars in the

[030-141] furnace, it is expedient to make the central cross bar for bearing up the

[030-142] ends double, and to leave a space between the ends of the bars so that the

[030-143] ashes may fall through between them. The space thus left enables the bars

[030-144] to expand without injury on the application of heat, whereas without some

[030-145] such provision the bars are very liable to get burned out by bending up in

[030-146] the centre, or at the ends, as they must do if the elongation of the bars

[030-147] on the application of heat be prevented; and this must be the effect of

[030-148] permitting the spaces at the ends of the bars to be filled up with ashes.

[030-149] At each end of each bed of bars it is expedient to leave a space which the

[030-150] ashes cannot fill up so as to cause the bars to jam; and care must be taken

[030-151] that the heels of the bars do not come against any of the furnace bearers,

[030-152] whereby the room left at the end of the bars to permit the expansion would

[030-153] be rendered of no avail.

[030-154] 379. _Q._--Have you any remarks to offer respecting the construction and

[030-155] arrangement of the furnace bridges and dampers of marine boilers?

[030-156] _A._--The furnace bridges of marine boilers are walls or partitions built

[030-157] up at the ends of the furnaces to narrow the opening for the escape of heat

[030-158] into the flues. They are either made of fire brick or of plate iron

[030-159] containing water: in the case of water bridges, the top part of the bridge

[030-160] should be made with a large amount of slant so as to enable the steam to

[030-161] escape freely, but notwithstanding this precaution the plates of water

[030-162] bridges are apt to crack at the bend, so that fire brick bridges appear on

[030-163] the whole to be preferable. In shallow furnaces the bridges often come too

[030-164] near the furnace top to enable a man to pass over them; and it will save

[030-165] expense if in such bridges the upper portion is constructed of two or three

[030-166] fire blocks, which may be lifted off where a person requires to enter the

[030-167] flues to sweep or repair them, whereby the perpetual demolition and

[030-168] reconstruction of the upper part of the bridge will be prevented.
