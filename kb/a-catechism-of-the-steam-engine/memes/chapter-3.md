[003-001] 21. _Q._--By what considerations is the _vis viva_ or mechanical energy

[003-002] proper for the fly-wheel of an engine determined?

[003-003] _A._--By a reference to the power produced every half-stroke of the engine,

[003-004] joined to the consideration of what relation the energy of the fly-wheel

[003-005] rim must have thereto, to keep the irregularities of motion within the

[003-006] limits which are admissible. It is found in practice, that when the power

[003-007] resident in the fly-wheel rim, when the engine moves at its average speed,

[003-008] is from two and a half to four times greater than the power generated by

[003-009] the engine in one half-stroke--the variation, depending on the energy

[003-010] inherent in the machinery the engine has to drive and the equability of

[003-011] motion required--the engine will work with sufficient regularity for most

[003-012] ordinary purposes, but where great equability of motion is required, it

[003-013] will be advisable to make the power resident in the fly-wheel equal to six

[003-014] times the power generated by the engine in one half-stroke.

[003-015] 22. _Q._---Can you give a practical rule for determining the proper

[003-016] quantity of cast iron for the rim of a fly-wheel in ordinary land engines?

[003-017] _A._--One rule frequently adopted is as follows:--Multiply the mean

[003-018] diameter of the rim by the number of its revolutions per minute, and square

[003-019] the product for a divisor; divide the number of actual horse power of the

[003-020] engine by the number of strokes the piston makes per minute, multiply the

[003-021] quotient by the constant number 2,760,000, and divide the product by the

[003-022] divisor found as above; the quotient is the requisite quantity of cast iron

[003-023] in cubic feet to form the fly-wheel rim.

[003-024] 23. _Q._--What is Boulton and Watt's rule for finding the dimensions of the

[003-025] fly-wheel?

[003-026] _A._--Boulton and Watt's rule for finding the dimensions of the fly-wheel

[003-027] is as follows:--Multiply 44,000 times the length of the stroke in feet by

[003-028] the square of the diameter of the cylinder in inches, and divide the

[003-029] product by the square of the number of revolutions per minute multiplied by

[003-030] the cube of the diameter of the fly-wheel in feet. The resulting number

[003-031] will be the sectional area of the rim of the fly-wheel in square inches.

[003-032] CENTRAL FORCES.

[003-033] 24. _Q._--What do you understand by centrifugal and centripetal forces?

[003-034] _A._--By centrifugal force, I understand the force with which a revolving

[003-035] body tends to fly from the centre; and by centripetal force, I understand

[003-036] any force which draws it to the centre, or counteracts the centrifugal

[003-037] tendency. In the conical pendulum, or steam engine governor, which consists

[003-038] of two metal balls suspended on rods hung from the end of a vertical

[003-039] revolving shaft, the centrifugal force is manifested by the divergence of

[003-040] the balls, when the shaft is put into revolution; and the centripetal

[003-041] force, which in this instance is gravity, predominates so soon as the

[003-042] velocity is arrested; for the arms then collapse and hang by the side of

[003-043] the shaft.

[003-044] 25. _Q._--What measures are there of the centrifugal force of bodies

[003-045] revolving in a circle?

[003-046] _A._--The centrifugal force of bodies revolving in a circle increases as

[003-047] the diameter of the circle, if the number of revolutions remain the same.

[003-048] If there be two fly-wheels of the same weight, and making the same number

[003-049] of revolutions per minute, but the diameter of one be double that of the

[003-050] other, the larger will have double the amount of centrifugal force. The

[003-051] centrifugal force of the _same wheel_, however, increases as the square of

[003-052] the velocity; so that if the velocity of a fly-wheel be doubled, it will

[003-053] have four times the amount of centrifugal force.

[003-054] 26. _Q._--Can you give a rule for determining the centrifugal force of a

[003-055] body of a given weight moving with a given velocity in a circle of a given

[003-056] diameter?

[003-057] _A._--Yes. If the velocity in feet per second be divided by 4.01, the

[003-058] square of the quotient will be four times the height in feet from which a

[003-059] body must have fallen to have acquired that velocity. Divide this quadruple

[003-060] height by the diameter of the circle, and the quotient is the centrifugal

[003-061] force in terms of the weight of the body, so that, multiplying the quotient

[003-062] by the actual weight of the body, we have the centrifugal force in pounds

[003-063] or tons. Another rule is to multiply the square of the number of

[003-064] revolutions per minute by the diameter of the circle in feet, and to divide

[003-065] the product by 5,870. The quotient is the centrifugal force in terms of the

[003-066] weight of the body.

[003-067] 27. _Q._--How do you find the velocity of the body when its centrifugal

[003-068] force and the diameter of the circle in which it moves are given?

[003-069] _A._--Multiply the centrifugal force in terms of the weight of the body by

[003-070] the diameter of the circle in feet, and multiply the square root of the

[003-071] product by 4.01; the result will be the velocity of the body in feet per

[003-072] second.

[003-073] 28. _Q._--Will you illustrate this by finding the velocity at which the

[003-074] cast iron rim of a fly-wheel 10 feet in diameter would burst asunder by its

[003-075] centrifugal force?

[003-076] _A._--If we take the tensile strength of cast iron at 15,000 lbs. per

[003-077] square inch, a fly-wheel rim of one square inch of sectional area would

[003-078] sustain 30,000 lbs. If we suppose one half of the rim to be so fixed to the

[003-079] shaft as to be incapable of detachment, then the centrifugal force of the

[003-080] other half of the rim at the moment of rupture must be equal to 30,000 lbs.

[003-081] Now 30,000 lbs. divided by 49.48 (the weight of the half rim) is equal to

[003-082] 606.3, which is the centrifugal force in terms of the weight. Then by the

[003-083] rule given in the last answer 606.3 x 10 = 6063, the square root of which

[003-084] is 78 nearly, and 78 x 4.01 = 312.78, the velocity of the rim in feet per

[003-085] second at the moment of rupture.

[003-086] 29. _Q._--What is the greatest velocity at which it is safe to drive a cast

[003-087] iron fly-wheel?

[003-088] _A._--If we take 2,000 lbs. as the utmost strain per square inch to which

[003-089] cast iron can be permanently subjected with safety; then, by a similar

[003-090] process to that just explained, we have 4,000 lbs./49.48 = 80.8 which

[003-091] multiplied by 10 = 808, the square root of which is 28.4, and 28.4 x 4.01 =

[003-092] 113.884, the velocity of the rim in feet per second, which may be

[003-093] considered as the highest consistent with safety. Indeed, this limit should

[003-094] not be approached in practice on account of the risks of fracture from

[003-095] weakness or imperfections in the metal.

[003-096] 30. _Q._--What is the velocity at which the wheels of railway trains may

[003-097] run if we take 4,000 lbs. per square inch as the greatest strain to which

[003-098] malleable iron should be subjected?

[003-099] _A._--The weight of a malleable iron rim of one square inch sectional area

[003-100] and 7 feet diameter is 21.991 feet x 3.4 lbs. = 74.76, one half of which is

[003-101] 37.4 lbs. Then by the same process as before, 8,000/37.4 = 213.9, the

[003-102] centrifugal force in terms of the weight: 213.9 x 7, the diameter of the

[003-103] wheel = 1497.3, the square root of which, 38.3 x 4.01 = 155.187 feet per

[003-104] second, the highest velocity of the rims of railway carriage wheels that is

[003-105] consistent with safety. 155.187 feet per second is equivalent to 105.8

[003-106] miles an hour. As 4,000 lbs. per square inch of sectional area is the

[003-107] utmost strain to which iron should be exposed in machinery, railway wheels

[003-108] can scarcely be considered safe at speed even considerably under 100 miles

[003-109] an hour, unless so constructed that the centrifugal force of the rim will

[003-110] be counteracted, to a material extent, by the centripetal action of the

[003-111] arms. Hooped wheels are very unsafe, unless the hoops are, by some process

[003-112] or other, firmly attached to the arms. It is of no use to increase the

[003-113] dimensions of the rim of a wheel with the view of giving increased strength

[003-114] to counteract the centrifugal force, as every increase in the weight of the

[003-115] rim will increase the centrifugal force in the same proportion.

[003-116] CENTRES OF GRAVITY, GYRATION, AND OSCILLATION.

[003-117] 31. _Q._--What do you understand by the centre of gravity of a body?

[003-118] _A._--That point within it, in which the whole of the weight may be

[003-119] supposed to be concentrated, and which continually endeavors to gain the

[003-120] lowest possible position. A body hung in the centre of gravity will remain

[003-121] at rest in any position.

[003-122] 32. _Q._--What is meant by the centre of gyration?

[003-123] _A._--The centre of gyration is that point in a revolving body in which the

[003-124] whole momentum may be conceived to be concentrated, or in which the whole

[003-125] effect of the momentum resides. If the ball of a governor were to be moved

[003-126] in a straight line, the momentum might be said to be concentrated at the

[003-127] centre of gravity of the ball; but inasmuch as, by its revolution round an

[003-128] axis, the part of the ball furthest removed from the axis moves more

[003-129] quickly than the part nearest to it, the momentum cannot be supposed to be

[003-130] concentrated at the centre of gravity, but at a point further removed from

[003-131] the central shaft, and that point is what is called the centre of gyration.

[003-132] 33. _Q._--What is the centre of oscillation?

[003-133] _A._--The centre of oscillation is a point in a pendulum or any swinging

[003-134] body, such, that if all the matter of the body were to be collected into

[003-135] that point, the velocity of its vibration would remain unaffected. It is in

[003-136] fact the mean distance from the centre of suspension of every atom, in a

[003-137] ratio which happens not to be an arithmetical one. The centre of

[003-138] oscillation is always in a line passing through the centre of suspension

[003-139] and the centre of gravity.

[003-140] THE PENDULUM AND GOVERNOR.

[003-141] 34. _Q._--By what circumstance is the velocity of vibration of a pendulous

[003-142] body determined?

[003-143] _A._--By the length of the suspending rod only, or, more correctly, by the

[003-144] distance between the centre of suspension and the centre of oscillation.

[003-145] The length of the arc described does not signify, as the times of vibration

[003-146] will be the same, whether the arc be the fourth or the four hundredth of a

[003-147] circle, or at least they will be nearly so, and would be so exactly, if the

[003-148] curve described were a portion of a cycloid. In the pendulum of clocks,

[003-149] therefore, a small arc is preferred, as there is, in that case, no sensible

[003-150] deviation from the cycloidal curve, but in other respects the size of the

[003-151] arc does not signify.

[003-152] 35. _Q._--If then the length of a pendulum be given, can the number of

[003-153] vibrations in a given time be determined?

[003-154] _A._--Yes; the time of vibration bears the same relation to the time in

[003-155] which a body would fall through a space equal to half the length of the

[003-156] pendulum, that the circumference of a circle bears to its diameter. The

[003-157] number of vibrations made in a given time by pendulums of different

[003-158] lengths, is inversely as the square roots of their lengths.

[003-159] 36. _Q._--Then when the length of the second's pendulum is known the proper

[003-160] length of a pendulum to make any given number of vibrations in the minute

[003-161] can readily be computed?

[003-162] _A._--Yes; the length of the second's pendulum being known, the length of

[003-163] another pendulum, required to perform any given number of vibrations in the

[003-164] minute, may be obtained by the following rule: multiply the square root of

[003-165] the given length by 60, and divide the product by the given number of

[003-166] vibrations per minute; the square of the quotient is the length of pendulum

[003-167] required. Thus if the length of a pendulum were required that would make 70

[003-168] vibrations per minute in the latitude of London, then SQRT(39.1393) x 60/70

[003-169] = (5.363)^2 = 28.75 in. which is the length required.
