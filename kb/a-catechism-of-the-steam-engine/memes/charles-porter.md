# Charles Porter

**Charles Porter** (fl. 1850s–1880s) was an American engineer best known for his invention of the isochronous [[governor|centrifugal governor]]. His design improved upon [[james-watt|Watt]]'s earlier governor by enabling more precise and responsive speed control, making it suitable for the high-speed engines that were becoming increasingly common in the later nineteenth century.

## Career and Contributions

Porter's principal contribution was the weighted [[governor|centrifugal governor]]. Unlike [[james-watt|Watt]]'s governor, which relied on the mass of the fly-balls alone, Porter's design added a central weight that increased the sensitivity and power of the governor's response. This allowed the governor to maintain nearly constant engine speed under varying loads — a critical requirement for textile mills, electric generators, and other applications demanding steady rotational speed.

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes Porter's patent governor as a significant refinement, noting its compact design and reliable operation (058-120). The Porter governor became widely adopted in both stationary and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s.

## Legacy

The Porter governor represented a major advance in automatic engine control. Its principles influenced subsequent governor designs and contributed to the development of precise speed regulation in steam and later internal combustion engines.
