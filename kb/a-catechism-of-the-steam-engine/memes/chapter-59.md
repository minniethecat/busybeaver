[059-001] The general arrangement is that of a Steam Cylinder, the piston rod of

[059-002] which, carried through into the water cylinder and attached directly to the

[059-003] water plunger, works back and forth without rotary motion, and of course

[059-004] without using either crank or fly wheel.

[059-005] [Illustration: Fig. 62.]

[059-006] In the figures, _a_ is the Steam Cylinder--_b_, the Steam Chest--_d_, a

[059-007] handle for regulating the steam valve--_f_, the starting bar _g, g_,

[059-008] tappets attached to the valve rod, which is moved by the contact of the arm

[059-009] _e_, on the piston rod with said tappets--_h_, the double-acting water

[059-010] plunger working through a packing ring--_o, o_, force valves--_o', o'_,

[059-011] suction valves. The pump piston is represented as moving from right to

[059-012] left, the arrows indicating the course of the water through the passages.

[059-013] The suction valves _o'_, on the right side, and the force valves _o_, on

[059-014] the left side, are show open; _x_, is an air chamber made of copper; _s_,

[059-015] the suction pipe terminating in a vacuum chamber; made by prolonging the

[059-016] suction pipe, and closing it perfectly tight at the top, the connection

[059-017] being made to the pump by a branch as shown; _m, m_, are hand-hole plates,

[059-018] affording easy access to the water valves; _n, n_, small holes through the

[059-019] plunger, which relieve the pressure near the end of the stroke, to give

[059-020] momentum to throw the valves when working at slow speed.

[059-021] [Illustration: Fig. 63.]

[059-022] Fig. 63 is a perspective view of H.R. Worthington's Duplex Steam Pump. The

[059-023] prominent peculiarity of this pump is its valve motion. As seen in the cut,

[059-024] two steam pumps are placed side by side (or end to end, if desired). Each

[059-025] pump, by a rock shaft connected with its piston rod, gives a constant and

[059-026] easy motion to the steam valve of the other. Each pump therefore gives

[059-027] steam to and starts its neighbor, and then finishes its own stroke, pausing

[059-028] an instant till its own steam valve, being opened by the other pump, allows

[059-029] it to make the return stroke.

[059-030] This combined action produces a perfectly positive valve motion without

[059-031] dead points, great regularity and ease of motion, and entire absence of

[059-032] noise or shock of any kind. Both kinds of pumps are made by Mr.

[059-033] Worthington, of various size according to the requirements, the duplex

[059-034] being used for boiler feed and for the supply of cities with water.

[059-035] Fig. 64 is a side elevation of the Woodward Steam Pump. The pump is direct

[059-036] acting. The steam and water piston being on the same rod, but momentum is

[059-037] obtained to throw the valves by means of a fly wheel, placed beyond the

[059-038] pump, and connected with the piston rod by a cross head and a yoke. The

[059-039] machine is simple in its construction and action, and is extensively used.

[059-040] Giffard's Injector, both in Europe and this country, is quite extensively

[059-041] used to supply the place of a pump, as independent feed for all classes of

[059-042] boilers. It is represented in elevation and section, figs. 65 and 66.

[059-046] _A_, steam pipe leading from the boiler. _B_, a perforated tube or

[059-047] cylinder, through which the steam passes into the space _b_. _C_ screwed

[059-048] rod for regulating the passage of steam through the annular conical space

[059-049] _c_, and worked by the handle _d/_. _E_, suction pipe, leading from the

[059-050] tank or hot well to small chamber _m_. _F_, annular conical opening or

[059-051] discharge pipe, the size of which is regulated by the movement of the tube

[059-052] or cylinder _B_. _G_, hand wheel for actuating the cylinder _B_. _H_,

[059-053] opening, in connection with the atmosphere, intervening between discharge

[059-054] pipe _F_ and the receiving pipe through which the water is forced. _I_,

[059-055] tube through which the water passes to the boiler. _K_, valve for

[059-056] preventing the return of the water from the boiler when the injector is not

[059-057] working. _L_, waste or overflow pipe. _M_, nut to tighten the packing rings

[059-058] _g_ and upper packing _i_ in cylinder _B_. _N_, lock nut to hold _M_.

[059-059] The pipe _A_ is connected with the steam space of the boiler at its highest

[059-060] part, to obtain as dry steam as possible. The passage of the steam into _A_

[059-061] is controlled by a cock, as is also the feed pipe to the boiler. In

[059-062] working, both are opened, the steam passes through _A_ into the space _b_,

[059-063] and issuing through the nozzle _c_ with the pressure due to its head, and a

[059-064] partial vacuum by its contact with the feed water, it drives this water in

[059-065] connection with the jet through the pipe _F_ into the pipe _I_ in

[059-066] connection with the water space of the boiler.

[059-067] _Method of Working._--Turn the wheel so as to permit a small quantity of

[059-068] water to flow to the instrument. Open the steam cock connecting the

[059-069] apparatus with the boiler. Turn slightly the handle, which will admit a

[059-070] small quantity of steam to the apparatus; a partial vacuum is thus

[059-071] produced, causing the water to enter through the supply pipe. As soon as

[059-072] this happens, which can be observed at the overflow pipe, the supply of

[059-073] steam or water may be increased as required, up to the capacity of the

[059-074] instrument, regulating either by means of the wheel and handle, so as to

[059-075] prevent any overflow. The quantity of water delivered into the boiler, may

[059-076] be varied by means of the stop cocks on the steam and water pipes, without

[059-077] altering the handles on the injector; a graduated cock on the water supply

[059-078] pipe is very convenient for this purpose.

[059-079] The machines are manufactured by Wm. Sellers & Co. Philadelphia.

[059-080] As an example of Portable Steam Engines, of which there are large numbers

[059-081] in this country of different manufacturers, we give the representation

[059-082] (fig. 67) of one made by J.C. Hoadley, of Lawrence, Mass.

[059-083] [Illustration: Fig. 67.]

[059-084] In these machines, the rules and proportions of the locomotive engine are

[059-085] adapted to the requirements of stationary power, for all purposes under

[059-086] forty horse power. The leading ideas are: high velocity, high pressure,

[059-087] good valve motion, large fire-box, numerous and short flues, and steam

[059-088] blast. The characteristic features are: great strength of boiler, fully

[059-089] adequate to bear with safety 200. lbs. pressure per sq. in., great

[059-090] compactness and simplicity, large and adjustable wearing surfaces, and the

[059-091] entire absence of all finish, or polish, for mere show.

[059-092] The cylinder is placed over the centre of the boiler, at the fire-box end,

[059-093] so that the strain due to the engine is central to the boiler (which serves

[059-094] as bed plate); the starting valve is under the hand of the engineer when at

[059-095] the fire door; and both ends of the crank shaft are available for driving

[059-096] pulleys.

[059-097] For the sake of compactness, the cylinders are set low, by means of a

[059-098] depression in the boiler between the stands of the crank shaft, to admit of

[059-099] the play of the crank and connecting rod. All the parts are attached to the

[059-100] boiler, which is made of sufficient strength to bear all extra strain due

[059-101] to the working of the engine.

[059-102] They have feed water heater, force pumps, Jackson's governor and valve,

[059-103] belt for governor, belt pulley, turned on the face, steam gauge;

[059-104] everything, in short, necessary to the convenient working of a steam

[059-105] engine. All engines are fired up and tried before they leave the shop, and

[059-106] they are warranted tight, safe, and complete.

[059-107] A strong and convenient running gear, so arranged as to be easily attached

[059-108] and detached at pleasure, is furnished, if desired; forming, when separate,

[059-109] a useful wagon.

[059-110] [Illustration: Fig. 68.]

[059-111] Fig. 68 is a compact vertical engine, as built by R. Hoe & Co., of this

[059-112] city. It is intended to drive printing presses, but is adapted to any kind

[059-113] of work, and is especially suited to such places as require economy of

[059-114] space. Although the value of expansion has been called in question by some

[059-115] of the engineers of the United States Navy, and under an appropriation from

[059-116] Congress is now to be made the subject of experiment; yet, in almost all

[059-117] the manufactories and workshops of the United States, no matter what the

[059-118] form of steam engine, or the purposes to which it is applied, whether

[059-119] stationary, locomotive, or marine, some form of cut-off, by which expansion

[059-120] of the steam can be availed of, is considered indispensable. Many varieties

[059-121] are in use, but those engines are most popular in which the cut-off is

[059-122] applied directly to the valves on the cylinder, opening them quickly and

[059-123] shutting off almost instantly, avoiding all wire drawing of the steam at

[059-124] the ports, and regulating the speed of the engine promptly. Of this class

[059-125] of engines, those manufactured by the Corliss Steam Engine Company, of

[059-126] Providence, R.I., are perhaps the widest known, not only for their

[059-127] extensive introduction, but also from having, by a long and successful

[059-128] litigation, established the claims of the patentee, Mr. George H. Corliss.

[059-129] [Illustration: Fig. 70.]

[059-130] Fig. 70 is a section of the cylinder and valve chests of a horizontal

[059-131] Corliss engine. _S_ is the steam connection, and _E_ the exhaust; there are

[059-132] two distinct sets of valves, the steam _s, s'_, and the exhaust _e, e'_,

[059-133] operated independently of each other. In their construction the valves may

[059-134] be considered cylindrical plugs, of which portions near the ports are cut

[059-135] away to admit the steam and reduce the bearing surface; the valves are

[059-136] fitted on the lathe and the seats by boring. The motion given to the valves

[059-137] is rocking, but it will be observed that the valves are not firmly

[059-138] connected to the rocking shaft or cylinder; in the figure the valves are

[059-139] shown shade lined, and the shaft or stem plain; in this way the valves are

[059-140] not affected by the packing of the valve stem, but always rest upon the

[059-141] face of the ports. In the figure the piston is just about to commence its

[059-142] outstroke, the movement of the steam is supposed to be represented by the

[059-143] arrows; the inner steam valve _s_, and the outer exhaust _e'_, are just

[059-144] beginning to open. It will be observed that the outer steam _s'_ is fully

[059-145] closed, whilst the inner exhaust valve _e_ is but barely so, showing that

[059-146] there has been a cut-off on the steam valve, but no lead to the exhaust,

[059-147] that it was left fully open till the completion of the stroke.

[059-148] [Illustration: Fig. 71.]

[059-149] Fig. 71 is a side elevation of the cylinder, with the valve connections

[059-150] with the governor. _S_ is the steam pipe; _s, s'_ handles to the steam

[059-151] valves, and _e, e'_ to the exhaust valves, shown in dotted line in fig. 70.

[059-152] The handles to the exhaust valves are connected directly to a rocking plate

[059-153] _R_, to which motion is given by a connection _x_, with an eccentric on the

[059-154] engine shaft. When once set, therefore the movement of the exhaust valves

[059-155] is constant, and they will always be opened and closed at the same point of

[059-156] the stroke. Connected with the rocking plate _R_, and on opposite sides of

[059-157] its centre, the same as the exhaust valve connections, there are two

[059-158] levers, vibrating on a centre _c_, of which one only is shown, as it covers

[059-159] the other; to the upper ends of these levers pawls are attached, one end of

[059-160] which rests on the stems or rods connected with the handles _s, s'_, of the

[059-161] steam valves; on these stems there are notches against which the pawls

[059-162] strike, and as the levers vibrate inward they push back the stems and

[059-163] thereby open the valves, and this continues for the whole length of the

[059-164] inward motion of the levers, or till the outer extremities of the pawls

[059-165] come in contact with the end of the short lever _l_, which, pushing down

[059-166] the outer end of the pawls, relieves the stems at the other ends, and the

[059-167] valve stem returns to its place through the force of springs attached to

[059-168] the outer extremities of the valve stems _a_, are cylindrical guides to the

[059-169] valve stems, at the inner extremities of which are air cushions. The lever

[059-170] _l_ is connected directly with the governor. As the balls rise, they

[059-171] depress the extremity, which comes in contact with the pawls sooner, and

[059-172] thereby shut the valves earlier; and on the contrary when the balls are

[059-173] depressed, the valves remain open longer; as the pawls come in contact with

[059-174] the stems always at one point, the steam valves open constantly, but are

[059-175] closed at any point by the relief of the pawls, according to the speed of

[059-176] the governor.
