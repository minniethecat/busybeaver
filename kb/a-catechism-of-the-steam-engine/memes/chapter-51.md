[051-001] 587. _Q._--What are the dimensions of the screw in the Rattler?

[051-002] _A._--Diameter 10 feet, length 1 foot 3 inches, pitch 11 feet. The

[051-003] foregoing experiments show that with a larger screw a better average

[051-004] performance would be obtained. The best result arrived at, was when the

[051-005] vessel was somewhat assisted by the wind, which is equivalent to a

[051-006] reduction of the resistance of the hull, or to a smaller hull, which is

[051-007] only another expression for a larger proportionate screw.

[051-008] 588. _Q._--When you speak of a larger screw, what increase of dimension do

[051-009] you mean to express?

[051-010] _A._--An increase of the diameter. The amount of reacting power of the

[051-011] screw upon the water is hot measured by the number of square feet of

[051-012] surface of the arms, but by the area of the disc or circle in which the

[051-013] screw revolves. The diameter of the screw of the Rattler being 10 feet, the

[051-014] area of its disc is 78.5 square feet; and with the amount of thrust already

[051-015] mentioned as existing in the first experiment, viz. 8722 lbs., the reacting

[051-016] pressure on each square foot of the screw's disc will be 108-1/2 lbs. The

[051-017] immersed midship section being 380 square feet, this is equivalent to 23

[051-018] lbs. per square foot of immersed midship section at a speed of 9.2 knots

[051-019] per hour.

[051-020] 589. _Q._--In smaller vessels of similar form, will the resistance per

[051-021] square foot of midship section be more than this?

[051-022] _A._--It will be considerably more. In the Pelican, a vessel of 109-3/4

[051-023] square feet of midship section, I estimate the resistance per square foot

[051-024] of midship section at 30 lbs., when the speed of the vessel is 9.7 knots

[051-025] per hour. In the Minx with an immersed midship section of 82 square feet,

[051-026] the resistance per square foot of immersed midship section was found by the

[051-027] dynamometer to be 41 lbs. at a speed of 8-1/2 knots; and in the Dwarf, a

[051-028] vessel with 60 square feet of midship section, I estimate the resistance

[051-029] per square foot of midship section at 46 lbs. at a speed of 9 knots per

[051-030] hour, which is just double the resistance per square foot of the Rattler.

[051-031] The diameter of the screw of the Minx is 4-1/2 feet, so that the area of

[051-032] its disc is 15.9 square feet, and the area of immersed midship section is

[051-033] about 5 times greater than that of the screw's disc. The diameter of the

[051-034] screw of the Dwarf is 5 feet 8 inches, so that the area of its disc is

[051-035] 25.22 square feet, and the area of immersed midship section is 2.4 times

[051-036] greater than that of the screw's disc. The pressure per square foot of the

[051-037] screw's disc is 214 lbs. in the case of the Minx, and 109-1/2 lbs. in the

[051-038] case of the Dwarf.

[051-039] 590. _Q._--From the greater proportionate resistance of small vessels, will

[051-040] not they require larger proportionate screws than large vessels?

[051-041] _A._--They will.

[051-042] 591. _Q._--Is there any ready means of predicting what the amount of thrust

[051-043] of a screw will be?

[051-044] _A._--When we know the amount of pressure on the pistons, and the velocity

[051-045] of their motion relatively with the velocity of advance made by the screw,

[051-046] supposing it to work in a solid nut, it is easy to tell what the thrust of

[051-047] the screw would be if it were cleared of the effects of friction and other

[051-048] irregular sources of disturbance. The thrust, in fact, would be at once

[051-049] found by the principle of virtual velocities; and if we take this

[051-050] theoretical thrust and diminish it by one fourth to compensate for friction

[051-051] and lateral slip, we shall have a near approximation to the amount of

[051-052] thrust that will be actually exerted.[1]

[051-053] [1] See Treatise on the Screw Propeller, by J. Bourne, C. E.

[051-054] COMPARATIVE ADVANTAGES OF DIFFERENT SCREWS.

[051-055] 592. _Q._--What species of screw do you consider the best?

[051-056] _A._--In cases in which a large diameter of screw can be employed, the

[051-057] ordinary screw or helix with two blades seems to be as effective as any

[051-058] other, and it is the most easily constructed. If, however, the screw is

[051-059] restricted in diameter, or if the vessel is required to tow, or will have

[051-060] to encounter habitually strong head winds, it will be preferable to employ

[051-061] a screw with an increasing pitch, and also of such other configuration that

[051-062] it will recover from the water some portion of the power that has been

[051-063] expended in slip.

[051-064] 593. _Q._--How can this be done?

[051-065] _A._--There are screws which are intended to accomplish, this object

[051-066] already in actual use. When there is much slip a centrifugal velocity is

[051-067] given to the water, and the screw, indeed, if the engine be set on when the

[051-068] vessel is at rest, acts very much as a centrifugal fan would do if placed

[051-069] in the same situation. The water projected outward by the centrifugal force

[051-070] escapes in the line of least resistance, which is to the surface; and if

[051-071] there be a high column of water over the screw, or, in other words, if the

[051-072] screw is deeply immersed, then the centrifugal action is resisted to a

[051-073] greater extent, and there will be less slip produced. The easiest

[051-074] expedient, therefore, for obviating loss by slip is to sink the screw

[051-075] deeply in the water; but as there are obvious limits to the application of

[051-076] this remedy, the next best device is to recover and render available for

[051-077] propulsion some part of the power which has been expended in giving motion

[051-078] to the water. One device for doing this consists in placing the screw well

[051-079] forward in the dead wood, so that it shall be overhung by the stern of the

[051-080] ship. The water forced upward by the centrifugal action of the screw will,

[051-081] by impinging on the overhanging stern, press the vessel forward in the

[051-082] water, just in the same way as is done by the wind when acting on an

[051-083] oblique sail. I believe, the two revolving vanes without any twist or

[051-084] obliquity on them at all, would propel a vessel if set well forward in the

[051-085] dead wood or beneath the bottom, merely by the ascent of the water up the

[051-086] inclined plane of the vessel's run; and, at all events, a screw so placed

[051-087] would, in my judgment, aid materially in propelling the vessel when her

[051-088] progress was resisted by head winds.

[051-089] 594. _Q._--But you said there are some kinds of screws which profess to

[051-090] accomplish this?

[051-091] [Illustration: Fig. 49. THE EARL OF DUNDONALD'S PROPELLER.]

[051-092] _A._--There are screws which profess to counteract the centrifugal velocity

[051-093] given to the water by imparting to it an equal centripetal force, the

[051-094] consequence of which will be, that the water projected backward by the

[051-095] screw, instead of taking the form of the frustum of a cone, with its small

[051-096] end next the screw, will take the form of a cylinder. One of these forms of

[051-097] screw is that patented by the Earl of Dundonald in 1843, and which is

[051-098] represented in fig. 49. Another is the form of screw already represented in

[051-099] fig. 48, and which was patented by Mr. Hodgson in 1844. Mr. Hodgson bends

[051-100] the arms of his propellers backward, not into the form of a triangle, but

[051-101] into the form of a parabola, to the end that the impact of the screw on the

[051-102] particles of the water may cause them to converge to a focus, as the rays

[051-103] of light would do in a parabolic reflector. But this particular

[051-104] configuration is not important, seeing that the same convergence which is

[051-105] given to the particles of the water, with a screw of uniform pitch bent

[051-106] back into the form of a parabola, will be given with a screw bent back into

[051-107] the form of a triangle, if the pitch be suitably varied between the centre

[051-108] and the circumference.

[051-109] 595. _Q._--Then the pitch may be varied in two ways?

[051-110] _A._--Yes: a screw may have a pitch increasing in the direction of the

[051-111] length, as would happen in the case of a spiral stair, if every successive

[051-112] step in the ascent was thicker than the one below it; or it may increase

[051-113] from the centre to the circumference, as would happen in the case of a

[051-114] spiral stair, if every step were thinner at the centre of the lower than at

[051-115] its outer wall. When the pitch of a screw increases in the direction of its

[051-116] length, the leading edge of the screw enters the water without shock or

[051-117] impact, as the advance of the leading edge per revolution will not be

[051-118] greater than the advance of the vessel. When the pitch of a screw increases

[051-119] in the direction of its diameter, the central part of the screw will

[051-120] advance with only the same velocity as the water, so that it cannot

[051-121] communicate any centrifugal velocity to the water; and the whole slip, as

[051-122] well as the whole propelling pressure, will occur at the outer part of the

[051-123] screw blades.

[051-124] 596. _Q._--Is there any advantage derived from these forms of screws?

[051-125] _A._--There is a slight advantage, but it is so slight as hardly to balance

[051-126] the increased trouble of manufacture, and, consequently, they are not

[051-127] generally or widely adopted.

[051-128] 597. _Q._--What other kinds of screw are there proposing to themselves the

[051-129] same or similar objects?

[051-130] _A._--There is the corrugated screw, the arms of which are corrugated, so

[051-131] as it were to gear with the water during its revolution, and thereby

[051-132] prevent it from acquiring a centrifugal velocity. Then there is Griffith's

[051-133] screw, which has a large ball at its centre, which, by the suction it

[051-134] creates at its hinder part, in passing through the water, produces a

[051-135] converging force, which partly counteracts the divergent action of the

[051-136] arms. Finally, there is Holm's screw, which has now been applied to a good

[051-137] number of vessels with success.

[051-138] 598. _Q._--Will you describe the configuration and action of Holm's screw?

[051-139] _A._--First, then, the screw increases in the direction of its length, and

[051-140] this increase is very rapid at the following edge, so that, in fact, the

[051-141] following edge stands in the plane of the shaft, or in the vertical

[051-142] longitudinal plane of the vessel. Then the ends of the arms are bent over

[051-143] into a curved flange, the edge of which points astern, and the point where

[051-144] this curved flange joins the following edge of the screw is formed, not

[051-145] into an angle, but into a portion of a sphere, so that this corner

[051-146] resembles the bowl of a spoon. When the screw is put into revolution, the

[051-147] water is encountered by the leading edge of the screw without shock, as its

[051-148] advance is only equal to the advance of the vessel, and before the screw

[051-149] leaves the water it is projected directly astern. At the same time, the

[051-150] curved flange at the rim of the screw prevents the dispersion of the water

[051-151] in a radial direction, and it consequently assumes the form of a column or

[051-152] cylinder of water, projected backward from the ship.

[051-153] 599. _Q._--What is the nature of Beattle's screw?

[051-154] _A._--Beattie's screw is an arrangement of the screw propeller whereby it

[051-155] is projected beyond the rudder, and the main object of the arrangement is

[051-156] to take away the vibratory motion at the stern,--an intention which it

[051-157] accomplishes in practice. There is an oval eye in the rudder, to permit the

[051-158] screw shaft to pass through it.

[051-159] 600. _Q._--When the diameter of the cylinder of water projected backward by

[051-160] a screw, and the force urging it into motion are known, may not the

[051-161] velocity it will acquire be approximately determined?

[051-162] _A._--That will not be very difficult; and I will take for illustration the

[051-163] case of the Minx, already referred to, which will show how such a

[051-164] computation is to be conducted. The speed of this vessel, in one of the

[051-165] experiments made with her, was 8.445 knots; the number of revolutions of

[051-166] the screw per minute, 231.32; and the pressure on each square foot of area

[051-167] of the screw's disc, 214 lbs. If a knot be taken to be 6075.6 feet, then

[051-168] the distance advanced by the vessel, when the speed is 8.445 knots, will be

[051-169] 3.7 feet per revolution, and this advance will be made in about .26 of a

[051-170] second of time. Now the distance which a body will fall by gravity, in .26

[051-171] of a second, is 1.087 feet; and a weight of 214 lbs. put into motion by

[051-172] gravity, or by a pressure of 214 lbs., would, therefore, acquire a velocity

[051-173] of 1.087 feet during the time one revolution of the screw is being

[051-174] performed. The weight to be moved, however, is 3.7 cubic feet of water,

[051-175] that being the new water seized by the screw each revolution for every

[051-176] square foot of surface in the screw's disc; and 3.7 cubic feet of water

[051-177] weigh 231.5 lbs., so that the urging force of 214 lbs. is somewhat less

[051-178] than the force of gravity, and the velocity of motion communicated to the

[051-179] water will be somewhat under 1.087 feet per revolution, or we may say it

[051-180] will be in round numbers 1 foot per revolution. This, added to the progress

[051-181] of the vessel, will make the distance advanced by the screw through the

[051-182] water 4.7 feet per revolution, leaving the difference between this and the

[051-183] pitch, namely 1.13 feet, to be accounted for on the supposition that the

[051-184] screw blades had broken laterally through the water to that extent. It

[051-185] would be proper to apply some correction to this computation, which would

[051-186] represent the increased resistance due to the immersion of the screw in the

[051-187] water; for a column of water cannot be moved in the direction of its axis

[051-188] beneath the surface, without giving motion to the superincumbent water, and

[051-189] the inertia of this superincumbent water must, therefore, be taken into the

[051-190] account. In the experiment upon the Minx, the depth of this superincumbent

[051-191] column was but small. The total amount of the slip was 36.53 per cent.; and

[051-192] there will not be much error in setting down about one half of this as due

[051-193] to the recession of the water in the direction of the vessel's track, and

[051-194] the other half as due to the lateral penetration of the screw blades.
