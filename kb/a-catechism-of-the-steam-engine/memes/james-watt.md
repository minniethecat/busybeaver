# James Watt

**James Watt** (1736–1819) was a Scottish engineer, instrument maker, and inventor. His improvements to the [[steam|steam engine]] — particularly the separate [[condensation|condenser]] — transformed it from a crude pumping device into the prime mover of the Industrial Revolution.

## Career and Contributions

Watt's principal contributions to [[steam|steam engine]] development include:

- **The separate [[condensation|condenser]]** (patented 1769): By condensing steam in a separate vessel rather than in the cylinder, Watt eliminated the wasteful cooling and reheating of the cylinder at each stroke. This innovation approximately halved fuel consumption. (035-037)

- **The [[double-acting|double-acting engine]]**: Watt devised a mechanism that admitted steam alternately to each side of the piston, making the engine capable of producing rotary motion for powering machinery.

- **The sun-and-planet gear**: To convert reciprocating piston motion to rotation without infringing existing crank patents.

- **The [[governor|centrifugal governor]]**: An automatic device for regulating engine speed by controlling steam admission.

## Collaboration with Boulton

In 1774, Watt entered into partnership with [[matthew-boulton|Matthew Boulton]], whose Soho Manufactory near Birmingham provided the manufacturing capability to build engines to Watt's precise specifications. The firm of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] became the dominant [[steam|steam engine]] builder of the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. (003-024)

## Legacy

[[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]]'s design principles — embodied in their standard wagon boiler and [[condensation|condensing engine]] — continued to influence engine construction throughout the nineteenth century. Their rules for dimensioning boilers, fly-wheels, and other components are frequently cited by [[john-bourne|Bourne]]. (003-026)
