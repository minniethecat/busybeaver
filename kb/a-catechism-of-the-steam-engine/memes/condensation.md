# Condensation

**Condensation** is the process by which steam returns to its liquid state (water), releasing its latent heat. In a [[steam|steam engine]], condensation is used to create a vacuum in the condenser, which greatly improves engine efficiency by reducing back-pressure on the piston. (001-211)

## The Condenser

The **condenser** is a vessel into which steam is [[eduction|exhaust]]ed from the cylinder after performing work. A jet of cold water is injected, causing the steam to collapse into a much smaller volume of water. This rapid condensation creates a vacuum, which assists the return stroke of the piston. (001-220)

The condenser is typically maintained at a temperature of about 100°F (38°C), which corresponds to a pressure of about 1 psi absolute. The vacuum is maintained by the [[air-pump|air pump]], which removes the condensed water, air, and any uncondensed vapor. (001-164)

## Condensing vs. Non-Condensing Engines

Engines are classified by whether they use condensation:

- **Condensing engines**: [[steam|Steam]] [[eduction|exhaust]]s into a condenser, creating a vacuum. These achieve higher efficiency and were the standard for stationary and [[marine-engine|marine engine]]s. (002-043)
- **Non-condensing engines**: [[steam|Steam]] [[eduction|exhaust]]s directly to the atmosphere. These are simpler but less efficient. Most [[locomotive|locomotive]] engines are non-condensing due to the practical difficulty of carrying a condenser. (010-035)

## Efficiency Impact

Condensation approximately doubles the power output of an engine for the same steam consumption, because the vacuum created means that atmospheric pressure assists the piston on its return stroke. In [[james-watt|Watt]]'s early engines, the separate condenser was the key innovation that made the [[steam|steam engine]] practical for widespread use. (005-020)
