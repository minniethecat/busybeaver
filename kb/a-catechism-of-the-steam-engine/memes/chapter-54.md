[054-001] 625. _Q._--What description of piston is used?

[054-002] _A._--The piston is packed with hemp, but the junk ring is made of

[054-003] malleable iron, as cast iron junk rings have been found liable to break:

[054-004] there are four plugs screwed into the cylinder cover, which, when removed,

[054-005] permit a box key to be introduced, to screw down the piston packing. The

[054-006] screws in the junk ring are each provided with a small ratchet, cut in a

[054-007] washer fixed upon the head, to prevent the screw from turning back; and the

[054-008] number of clicks given by these ratchets, in tightening up the bolts,

[054-009] enables the engineer to know when they have all been tightened equally. In

[054-010] more recent engines, and especially in those of large size, Messrs. Penn

[054-011] employ for the piston packing a single metallic ring with tongue piece and

[054-012] indented plate behind the joint; and this ring is packed behind with hemp

[054-013] squeezed by the junk ring as in ordinary hemp-packed pistons.

[054-014] 626. _Q._--Will you describe the construction of the cap for connecting the

[054-015] piston rod with the crank pin?

[054-016] _A._--The cap for attaching the piston rod to the crank pin, is formed

[054-017] altogether of brass, which brass serves to form the bearing of the crank

[054-018] pin. The external diameter of the socket by which this cap is attached to

[054-019] the piston rod is 3-5/16 inches. The diameter of the crank pin is 3 inches,

[054-020] and the length of the crank pin bearing 3-7/8 inches. The thickness of the

[054-021] brass around the crank pin bearing is 1 inch, and the upper portion of the

[054-022] brass is secured to the lower portion, by means of lugs, which are of such

[054-023] a depth that the perpendicular section through the centre of the bearing

[054-024] has a square outline measuring 7 inches in the horizontal direction, 3-7/8

[054-025] inches from the centre of the pin to the level of the top of the lugs, and

[054-026] 2-1/2 inches from the centre of the pin to the level of the bottom of the

[054-027] lugs. The width of the lugs is 2 inches, and the bolts passing through them

[054-028] are 1-1/4 inch in diameter. The bolts are tapped into the lower portion of

[054-029] the cap, and are fitted very accurately by scraping where they pass through

[054-030] the upper portion, so as to act as steady pins in preventing the cover of

[054-031] the crank pin bearing from being worked sideways by the alternate thrust on

[054-032] each side. The distance between the centres of the bolts is 5 inches, and

[054-033] in the centre of the cover, where the lugs, continued in the form of a web,

[054-034] meet one another, an oil cup 1-5/8 inch in diameter, 1-1/8 inch high, and

[054-035] provided with an internal pipe, is cast upon the cover, to contain oil for

[054-036] the lubrication of the crank pin bearing. The depth of the cutter for

[054-037] attaching the cap to the piston rod is 1-1/4 inch and its thickness is

[054-038] 3/8ths of an inch.

[054-039] 627. _Q._--Will you describe the means by which the air pump rod is

[054-040] connected with the crank which works the air pump?

[054-041] [Illustration: Fig. 50. AIR PUMP CONNECTING ROD AND CROSS HEAD.  Messrs.

[054-042] Penn.]

[054-043] _A._--A similar cap to that of the piston rod attaches the air pump crank

[054-044] to the connecting rod by which the air pump rod is moved, but in this

[054-045] instance the diameter of the bearing is 5 inches, and the length of the

[054-046] bearing is about 3 inches. The air pump connecting rod and cross head are

[054-047] shown in perspective in fig. 50. The thickness of the brass encircling the

[054-048] bearing of the shaft is three fourths of an inch upon the edge, and 1-1/8

[054-049] inch in the centre, the back being slightly rounded; the width of the lugs

[054-050] is 1-5/8 inch, and the depth of the lugs is 2 inches upon the upper brass,

[054-051] and 2 inches upon the lower brass, making a total depth of 4 inches. The

[054-052] diameter of the bolts passing through the lugs is 1 inch, and the bolts are

[054-053] tapped into the lower brass, and accurately fitted into the upper one, so

[054-054] as to act as steady pins, as in the previous instance. The lower eye of the

[054-055] connecting rod is forked, so as to admit the eye of the air pump rod; and

[054-056] the pin which connects the two together is prolonged into a cross head, as

[054-057] shown in fig. 50. The ends of this cross head move in guides. The forked

[054-058] end of the connecting rod is fixed upon the cross head by means of a

[054-059] feather, so that the cross head partakes of the motion of the connecting

[054-060] rod, and a cap, similar to that attached to the piston rod, is attached to

[054-061] the air pump rod, for connecting it with the cross head. The diameter of

[054-062] the air pump rod is 1-1/2 inch, the external diameter of the socket

[054-063] encircling the rod is 2-1/8 inches, and the depth of the socket 4-1/2

[054-064] inches from the centre of the cross head. The depth of the cutter for

[054-065] attaching the socket to the rod is 1 inch, and its thickness 5/16 inch. The

[054-066] breadth of the lugs is 1-3/8 inch, the depth 1-1/4 inch, making a total

[054-067] depth of 2-1/2 inches; and the diameter of the bolts seven eighths of an

[054-068] inch. The diameter of the cross head at the centre is 2 inches, the

[054-069] thickness of each jaw around the bearing 1 inch, and the breadth of each

[054-070] 9/16 inch.

[054-071] 628. _Q._--What are the dimensions of the crank shaft and cranks?

[054-072] _A._--The diameter of the intermediate shaft journal is 4-3/16 inches, and

[054-073] of the paddle shaft journal 4-3/8 inches; the length of the journal in each

[054-074] case is 5 inches. The diameter of the large eye of the crank is 7 inches,

[054-075] and the diameter of the hole through it is 4-3/8 inches; the diameter of

[054-076] the small eye of the crank is 5-1/4 inches, the diameter of the hole

[054-077] through it being 3 inches. The depth of the large eye is 4-1/4 inches, and

[054-078] of the small eye 3-3/4 inches; the breadth of the web is 4 inches at the

[054-079] shaft end, and 3 inches at the pin end, and the thickness of the web is

[054-080] 2-5/8 inches. The width of the notch forming the crank in the intermediate

[054-081] shaft for working the air pump is 3-1/2 inches, and the width of each of

[054-082] the arms of this crank is 3-15/16 inches. Both the outer and inner corners

[054-083] of the crank are chamfered away, until the square part of the crank meets

[054-084] the round of the shaft. The method of securing the cranks pins into the

[054-085] crank eyes of the intermediate shaft consists in the application of a nut

[054-086] to the end of each pin, where it passes through the eye, the projecting end

[054-087] of the pin being formed with a thread upon which the nut is screwed.

[054-088] 629. _Q._--Will you describe the eccentric and eccentric rod?

[054-089] [Illustration: Fig. 51. ECCENTRIC AND ROD. Messrs. Penn.]

[054-090] _A._--The eccentric and eccentric rod are shown in fig. 51. The eccentric

[054-091] is put on the crank shaft in two halves, joined in the diameter of largest

[054-092] eccentricity by means of a single bolt passing through lugs on the central

[054-093] eye, and the back balance is made in a separate piece five eighths of an

[054-094] inch thick, and is attached by means of two bolts, which also help to bind

[054-095] the halves of the eccentric together. The eccentric strap is half an inch

[054-096] thick, and 1-1/4 inch broad, and the flanges of the eccentric, within which

[054-097] the strap works, are each three eighths of an inch thick. The eccentric rod

[054-098] is attached to the eccentric hoop by means of two bolts passing through

[054-099] lugs upon the rod, and tapped into a square boss upon the hoop; and pieces

[054-100] of iron, of a greater or less thickness, are interposed between the

[054-101] surfaces in setting the valve, to make the eccentric rod of the right

[054-102] length. The eccentric rod is kept in gear by the push of a small horizontal

[054-103] rod, attached to a vertical blade spring, and it is thrown out of gear by

[054-104] means of the ordinary disengaging apparatus, which acts in opposition to

[054-105] the spring, as, in cases where the eccentric rod is not vertical, it acts

[054-106] in opposition to the gravity of the rod.

[054-107] 630. _Q._--Will you explain in detail the construction of the valve

[054-108] gearing, or such parts of it as are peculiar to the oscillating engine?

[054-109] _A._--The eccentric rod is attached by a pin, 1 inch in diameter, to an

[054-110] open curved link or sector with a tail projecting upward and passing

[054-111] through an eye to guide the link in a vertical motion. The link is formed

[054-112] of iron case-hardened, and is 2-3/4 inches deep at the middle, and 2-3/8

[054-113] inches deep at the ends, and 1 inch broad. The opening in the link, which

[054-114] extends nearly its entire length, is 1-5/16 inch broad; and into this

[054-115] opening a brass block 2 inches long is truly fitted, there being a hole

[054-116] through the block 3/4 inch diameter, for the reception of the pin of the

[054-117] valve shaft lever. The valve shaft is 1-3/4 inch diameter at the end next

[054-118] the link or segment, and diminishes regularly to the other end, but its

[054-119] cross section assumes the form of an octagon in its passage round the

[054-120] cylinder, measuring mid-way 1-1/4 inch deep, by about 3/4 inch thick, and

[054-121] the greatest depth of the finger for moving the valve is about 1 inch. The

[054-122] depth of the lever for moving the valve shaft is 2 inches at the broad, and

[054-123] 1-1/4 inch at the narrow end. The internal breadth of the mortice in which

[054-124] the valve finger moves is 5/16 inch, and its external depth is 1-3/4 inch,

[054-125] which leaves three eighths of an inch as the thickness of metal round the

[054-126] hole; and the breadth, measuring in the direction of the hole, is 1-1/2

[054-127] inch. The valve rod is three fourths of an inch in diameter, and the

[054-128] mortice is connected to the valve rod by a socket 1 inch long, and 1-1/8

[054-129] inch diameter, through which a small cutter passes. A continuation of the

[054-130] rod, eleven sixteenths of an inch diameter, passes upward from the mortice,

[054-131] and works through an eye, which serves the purpose of a guide. In addition

[054-132] to the guide afforded to the segment by the ascending tail, it is guided at

[054-133] the ends upon the columns of the framing by means of thin semicircular

[054-134] brasses, 4 inches deep, passing round the columns, and attached to the

[054-135] segment by two 3/8 inch bolts at each end, passing through projecting

[054-136] feathers upon the brasses and segment, three eighths of an inch in

[054-137] thickness. The curvature of the segment is such as to correspond with the

[054-138] arc swept from the centre of the trunnion to the centre of the valve lever

[054-139] pin when the valve is at half stroke as a radius; and the operation of the

[054-140] segment is to prevent the valve from being affected by the oscillation of

[054-141] the cylinder, but the same action, would be obtained by the employment of a

[054-142] smaller eccentric with more lead. In some engines the segment is not formed

[054-143] in a single piece, but of two curved blades, with blocks interposed at the

[054-144] ends, which may be filed down a little, to enable the sides of the slot to

[054-145] be brought nearer, as the metal wears away.

[054-146] 631. _Q._--What kind of plummer blocks are used for the paddle shaft

[054-147] bearings?

[054-148] _A._--The paddle shaft plummer blocks are altogether of brass, and are

[054-149] formed in much the same manner as the cap of the piston rod, only that the

[054-150] sole is flat, as in ordinary plummer blocks, and is fitted between

[054-151] projecting lugs of the framing, to prevent side motion. In the bearings

[054-152] fitted on this plan, however, the upper brass will generally acquire a good

[054-153] deal of play after some amount of wear. The bolts are worked slack in the

[054-154] holes, though accurately fitted at first; and it appears expedient,

[054-155] therefore, either to make the bolts very large, and the sockets through

[054-156] which they pass very deep, or to let one brass fit into the other.
