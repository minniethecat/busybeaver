[029-001] 356. _Q._--What was Mr. Watt's rule for the pitch of wheels?

[029-002] _A._--Multiply five times the diameter of the larger wheel by the diameter

[029-003] of the smaller, and extract the fourth root of the product, which is the

[029-004] pitch.

[029-005] STRENGTH OF MARINE AND LOCOMOTIVE ENGINES.

[029-006] 357. _Q._--Cannot you give some rules of strength which will be applicable

[029-007] whatever pressure may be employed?

[029-008] _A._--In the rules already given, the effective pressure may be reckoned at

[029-009] from 18 to 20 lbs. upon every square inch of the piston, as is usual in

[029-010] land engines; and if the pressure upon every square inch of the piston be

[029-011] made twice greater, the dimensions must just be those proper for an engine

[029-012] of twice the area of piston. It will not be difficult, however, to

[029-013] introduce the pressure into the rules as an element of the computation,

[029-014] whereby the result will be applicable both to high and low pressure

[029-015] engines.

[029-016] 358. _Q._--Will you apply this mode of computation to a marine engine, and

[029-017] first find the diameter of the piston rod?

[029-018] _A._--The diameter of the piston rod may be found by multiplying the

[029-019] diameter of the cylinder in inches, by the square root of the pressure on

[029-020] the piston in lbs. per square inch, and dividing by 50, which makes the

[029-021] strain 1/7th of the elastic force.

[029-022] 359. _Q._--What will be the rule for the connecting rod, supposing it to be

[029-023] of malleable iron?

[029-024] _A._--The diameter of the connecting rod at the ends, may be found by

[029-025] multiplying 0.019 times the square root of the pressure on the piston in

[029-026] lbs. per square inch by the diameter of the cylinder in inches; and the

[029-027] diameter of the connecting rod in the middle may be found by the following

[029-028] rule:--to 0.0035 times the length of the connecting rod in inches, add 1,

[029-029] and multiply the sum by 0.019 times the square root of the pressure on the

[029-030] piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder

[029-031] in inches. The strain is equal to 1/6th of the elastic force.

[029-032] 360. _Q._--How will you find the diameter of the cylinder side rods of a

[029-033] marine engine?

[029-034] _A._--The diameter of the cylinder side rods at the ends may be found by

[029-035] multiplying 0.0129 times the square root of the pressure on the piston in

[029-036] lbs. per square inch by the diameter of the cylinder; and the diameter of

[029-037] the cylinder side rods at the middle is found by the following rule:--to

[029-038] 0.0035 times the length of the rod in inches, add 1, and multiply the sum

[029-039] by 0.0129 times the square root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per

[029-040] square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder in inches; the

[029-041] product is the diameter of each side rod at the centre in inches. The

[029-042] strain upon the side rods is by these rules equal to 1/6th of the elastic

[029-043] force.

[029-044] 361. _Q._--How do you determine the dimensions of the crank?

[029-045] _A._--To find the exterior diameter of the large eye of the crank when of

[029-046] malleable iron:--to 1.561 times the pressure of the steam upon the piston

[029-047] in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the square of the length of the

[029-048] crank in inches, add 0.00494 times the square of the diameter of the

[029-049] cylinder in inches, multiplied by the square of the number of lbs. pressure

[029-050] per square inch on the piston; extract the square root of this quantity;

[029-051] divide the result by 75.59 times the square root of the length of the crank

[029-052] in inches, and multiply the quotient by the diameter of the cylinder in

[029-053] inches; square the product and extract the cube root of the square, to

[029-054] which add the diameter of the hole for the reception of the shaft, and the

[029-055] result will be the exterior diameter of the large eye of the crank when of

[029-056] malleable iron. The diameter of the small eye of the crank may be found by

[029-057] adding to the diameter of the crank pin 0.02521 times the square root of

[029-058] the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the

[029-059] diameter of the cylinder in inches.

[029-060] 362. _Q._--What will be the thickness of the crank web?

[029-061] _A._--The thickness of the web of the crank, supposing it to be continued

[029-062] to the centre of the shaft, would at that point be represented by the

[029-063] following rule:--to 1.561 times the square of the length of the crank in

[029-064] inches, add 0.00494 times the square of the diameter of the cylinder in

[029-065] inches, multiplied by the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch;

[029-066] extract the square root of the sum, which multiply by the diameter of the

[029-067] cylinder squared in inches, and by the pressure on the piston in lbs. per

[029-068] square inch; divide the product by 9,000, and extract the cube root of the

[029-069] quotient, which will be the proper thickness of the web of the crank when

[029-070] of malleable iron, supposing the web to be continued to the centre of the

[029-071] shaft. The thickness of the web at the crank pin centre, supposing it to be

[029-072] continued thither, would be 0.022 times the square root of the pressure on

[029-073] the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the

[029-074] cylinder. The breadth of the web of the crank at the shaft centre should be

[029-075] twice the thickness, and at the pin centre 1-1/2 times the thickness of the

[029-076] web; the length of the large eye of the crank would be equal to the

[029-077] diameter of the shaft, and of the small eye 0.0375 times the square root of

[029-078] the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the

[029-079] diameter of the cylinder.

[029-080] 363. _Q._--Will you apply the same method of computation to find the

[029-081] dimensions of a malleable iron paddle shaft?

[029-082] _A._--The method of computation will be as follows:--to find the dimensions

[029-083] of a malleable iron paddle shaft, so that the strain shall not exceed

[029-084] 5/6ths of the elastic force, or 5/6ths of the force iron is capable of

[029-085] withstanding without permanent derangement of structure, which in tensile

[029-086] strains is taken at 17,800 lbs. per square inch: multiply the pressure in

[029-087] lbs. per square inch on the piston by the square of the diameter of the

[029-088] cylinder in inches, and the length of the crank in inches, and extract the

[029-089] cube root of the product, which, multiplied by 0.08264, will be the

[029-090] diameter of the paddle shaft journal in inches when of malleable iron,

[029-091] whatever the pressure of the steam may be. The length of the paddle shaft

[029-092] journal should be 1-1/4 times the diameter; and the diameter of the part

[029-093] where the crank is put on is often made equal to the diameter over the

[029-094] collars of the journal or bearing.

[029-095] 364. _Q._--How do you find the diameter of the crank pin?

[029-096] _A._--The diameter of the crank pin in inches may be found by multiplying

[029-097] 0.02836 times the square root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per

[029-098] square inch, by the diameter of the cylinder in inches. The length of the

[029-099] pin is usually about 9/8th times its diameter, and the strain if all thrown

[029-100] upon the end of the pin will be equal to the elastic force; but in ordinary

[029-101] working, the strain will only be equal to 1/3d of the elastic force.

[029-102] 365. _Q._--What are the dimensions of the cross head?

[029-103] _A._--If the length of the cross head be taken at 1.4 times the diameter of

[029-104] the cylinder, the dimensions of the cross head will be as follows:--the

[029-105] exterior diameter of the eye in the cross head for the reception of the

[029-106] piston rod, will be equal to the diameter of the hole, plus 0.02827 times

[029-107] the cube root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch,

[029-108] multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder in inches; and the depth of the

[029-109] eye will be 0.0979 times the cube root of the pressure on the piston in

[029-110] lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder in inches.

[029-111] The diameter of each cross head journal will be 0.01716 times the square

[029-112] root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by

[029-113] the diameter of the cylinder in inches--the length of the journal being

[029-114] 9/8ths its diameter. The thickness of the web at centre will be 0.0245

[029-115] times the cube root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch,

[029-116] multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder in inches; and the depth of web

[029-117] at centre will be 0.09178 times the cube root of the pressure on the piston

[029-118] in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder in

[029-119] inches. The thickness of the web at journal will be 0.0122 times the square

[029-120] root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by

[029-121] the diameter of the cylinder in inches; and the depth of the web at journal

[029-122] will be 0.0203 times the square root of the pressure upon the piston in

[029-123] lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder in inches.

[029-124] In these rules for the cross head, the strain upon the web is 1/2.225 times

[029-125] the elastic force; the strain upon the journal in ordinary working is

[029-126] 1/2.33 times the elastic force; and if the outer ends of the journals are

[029-127] the only bearing points, the strain is 1/1.165 times the elastic force,

[029-128] which is very little in excess of the elastic force.

[029-129] 366. _Q._--How do you find the diameter of the main centre when

[029-130] proportioned according to this rule?

[029-131] _A._--The diameter of the main centre may be found by multiplying 0.0367

[029-132] times the square root of the pressure upon the piston in lbs. per square

[029-133] inch, by the diameter of the cylinder in inches, which will give the

[029-134] diameter of the main centre journal in inches when of malleable iron, and

[029-135] the length of the main centre journal should be 1-1/2 times its diameter;

[029-136] the strain upon the main centre journal in ordinary working will be about

[029-137] 1/2 the elastic force.

[029-138] 367. _Q._--What are the proper dimensions of the gibs and cutters of an

[029-139] engine?

[029-140] _A._--The depth of gibs and cutters for attaching the piston rod to the

[029-141] cross head, is 0.0358 times the cube root of the pressure of the steam on

[029-142] the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the

[029-143] cylinder; and the thickness of the gibs and cutters is 0.007 times the cube

[029-144] root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by

[029-145] the diameter of its cylinder. The depth of the cutter through the piston is

[029-146] 0.017 times the square root of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per

[029-147] square inch, multiplied by the diameter of the cylinder in inches; and the

[029-148] thickness of the cutter through the piston is 0.007 times the square root

[029-149] of the pressure on the piston in lbs. per square inch, multiplied by the

[029-150] diameter of the cylinder.

[029-151] 368. _Q._--Are not some of the parts of an engine constructed according to

[029-152] these rules too weak, when compared with the other parts?

[029-153] _A._--It is obvious, from the varying proportions subsisting in the

[029-154] different parts of the engine between the strain and the elastic force,

[029-155] that in engines proportioned by these rules--which represent nevertheless

[029-156] the average practice of the best constructors--some of the parts must

[029-157] possess a considerable excess of strength over other parts, and it appears

[029-158] expedient that this disparity should be diminished, which may best be done

[029-159] by increasing the strength of the parts which are weakest; inasmuch as the

[029-160] frequent fracture of some of the parts shows that the dimensions at present

[029-161] adopted for those parts are scarcely sufficient, unless the iron of which

[029-162] they are made is of the best quality. At the same time it is quite certain,

[029-163] that engines proportioned by these rules will work satisfactorily where

[029-164] good materials are employed; but it is important to know in what parts good

[029-165] materials and larger dimensions are the most indispensable. In many of the

[029-166] parts, moreover, it is necessary that the dimensions should be proportioned

[029-167] to meet the wear and the tendency to heat, instead of being merely

[029-168] proportioned to obtain the necessary strength; and the crank pin is one of

[029-169] the parts which requires to be large in diameter, and as long as possible

[029-170] in the bearing, so as to distribute the pressure, and prevent the

[029-171] disposition to heat which would otherwise exist. The cross head journals

[029-172] also should be long and large; for as the tops of the side rods have little

[029-173] travel, the oil is less drawn into the bearings than if the travel was

[029-174] greater, and is being constantly pressed out by the punching strain. This

[029-175] strain should therefore be reduced as far as possible by its distribution

[029-176] over a large surface. In the rules which are contained in the answers to

[029-177] the ten preceding questions (358 to 367) the pressure on the piston in lbs.

[029-178] per square inch is taken as the sum of the pressure of steam in the boiler

[029-179] and of the vacuum; the latter being assumed to be 15 lbs. per square inch.
