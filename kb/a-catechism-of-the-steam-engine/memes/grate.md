The **grate** (or fire grate) is the framework of cast-iron bars forming the floor of a [[boiler]] [[furnace]]. It supports the bed of burning coal while permitting combustion air to pass upward from the ash pit through the spaces between the bars. The design of the grate — its area, bar spacing, and slope — directly determines the rate and efficiency of combustion.

## Construction

The grate bars are set with a downward slope toward the back of the furnace, so that fresh coal thrown onto the front of the grate gradually works its way rearward as it burns:

> [007-094] The furnace bars, or the grate or fire bars, which slope downward toward the back of the grate, so that the fuel which is thrown in at the front door of the furnace gradually moves toward the back of the grate.

Air enters from below through the ash pit:

> [007-096] The necessary supply of air enters from the ash pit through the grate bars, and the smoke ascending from the fire passes along the flue F along one side to the front of the boiler.

## Grate area and engine power

The area of the fire grate is one of the fundamental design parameters of any [[boiler|steam boiler]], as it limits the maximum rate of coal combustion:

> [011-063] The fire grate contains 21 square feet of area in a boiler of 6 feet diameter.

> [011-074] In a boiler 8 feet in diameter the fire grate contains 37 square feet of area.

The relationship between grate area and engine power is well established:

> [018-064] A locomotive boiler, which is required to raise a large quantity of steam in a short time, involves a small area of grate bars relative to the heating surface, but a high rate of combustion per square foot.

> [018-066] In ordinary land boilers about 12 lbs of coal are consumed per hour for each square foot of grate bars, and upon each foot of grate about 1/2 a cubic foot of water per hour is evaporated.

## Rate of combustion

| [[boiler|Boiler]] type | Coal consumption per sq ft of grate | Evaporation per sq ft of grate |
|------------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Land boiler (ordinary) | ~12 lbs/hr | ~0.5 cu ft water/hr |
| Marine boiler | ~16–20 lbs/hr | ~0.6–0.8 cu ft water/hr |
| [[locomotive|Locomotive]] boiler | ~60–100 lbs/hr | ~3–4 cu ft water/hr |

The thickness of the coal bed on the grate is also important:

> [012-094] The thickness of the coal upon the grate should depend upon the intensity of the draught. Where the draught is strong the fire may be thick, but where the draught is weak the fire must be thin.

## Revolving grate

To address the problem of smoke (unburned coal particles), various mechanical grates were developed:

> [013-042] Mr. Brunton of Birmingham, I believe, first instituted the plan of a revolving grate for consuming the smoke, and thereby adding to the heating power of the fuel.

> [013-046] The fire grate is made like a round table capable of turning round horizontally. The coal is precipitated upon the grate by a hopper, and in the revolution of the grate before it can have reached the flue the coal is thoroughly coked and the combustible gases are burnt.

The revolving grate ensured that fresh coal was continuously brought into the hottest zone, promoting complete combustion and reducing smoke.
