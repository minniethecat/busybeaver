# John Kingston

John Kingston was a British [[marine-engine|marine engine]]er who invented Kingston's valves — external hull-mounted valves that provided a critical safety redundancy for steamship sea connections.

## Career and Contributions

Kingston's valves addressed a specific safety problem in steam vessels: the [[blow-off]] cocks and [[injection-cock|sea injection cock]]s that penetrated the ship's hull were vulnerable to failure of their internal piping. If an internal pipe or cock broke, the vessel could flood. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes the solution: "in modern steam vessels Kingston's valves are also used, which consist of a spindle or plate valve, fitted to the exterior of the ship, so that if the internal pipe or cock breaks, the external valve will still be operative." (040-099)

This external placement was particularly important for [[blow-off]] cocks, which required occasional regrinding to maintain a tight seal. "The sea cocks cannot be re-ground without putting the vessel into dock, except by the use of Kingston's valves, or some equivalent expedient." (040-104) By placing the primary sealing surface on the exterior of the hull, Kingston's design allowed maintenance to be performed without dry-docking the vessel — a significant operational advantage.

The valves were adopted on "modern steam vessels" and became standard fittings on [[marine-engine|marine]] boilers, where reliable sea-water injection for [[condenser]]s was essential. They were also used on [[blow-off]] connections that discharged boiler water overboard.

## Legacy

Kingston valves remain a standard component in [[marine-engine|marine engine]]ering, and the term "Kingston valve" persists in nautical and naval engineering vocabulary. The principle of redundant external sealing that Kingston introduced — a backup closure on the hull side of any through-hull connection — became fundamental to ship safety design.
