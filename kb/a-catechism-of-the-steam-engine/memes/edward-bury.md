# Edward Bury

**Edward Bury** (1794–1858) was an English locomotive manufacturer. His firm, Bury, Curtis and Kennedy of Liverpool, produced over 400 locomotives for railways in Britain and abroad. His distinctive bar-frame design became one of the standard locomotive configurations of the early railway era.

## Career and Contributions

Bury's locomotives were characterised by their bar-frame construction, in which wrought-iron bars replaced the heavy plate frames then common. This design reduced weight without sacrificing strength, making Bury's engines well-suited to the lightly built track of early railways.

His standard locomotive used 14-inch diameter cylinders and a [[fire-box|firebox]] with a domed top, producing sufficient power for passenger and goods service. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] describes Bury's locomotive cylinder proportions as a representative example of sound practice (022-170).

## Key Design Features

The Bury bar-frame differed from contemporary plate-frame designs in several respects:

- **Wrought-iron bar frames** replaced heavy cast or riveted plate frames, reducing total engine weight by approximately 15–20 per cent
- **Inside cylinders** kept the reciprocating masses close to the centre line, reducing lateral oscillation at speed
- **Dome-topped [[fire-box|firebox]]** provided an enlarged steam space, reducing priming in service

These features made Bury's locomotives particularly influential in the 1830s and 1840s, before heavier rail and more powerful engines became the norm.

## Legacy

Bury's bar-frame design was adopted by numerous railways and influenced locomotive construction in Europe and North America. His firm's output of over 400 engines made it one of the largest locomotive manufacturers of its day. The bar-frame principle continued to be used in locomotive design well into the twentieth century.
