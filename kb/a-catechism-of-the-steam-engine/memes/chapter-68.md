[068-001] 753. _Q._--What should be done if a tube bursts in the boiler?

[068-002] _A._--When a tube bursts, a wooden or iron plug must be driven into each

[068-003] end of it, and if the water or steam be rushing out so fiercely that the

[068-004] exact position of the imperfection cannot be discovered, it will be

[068-005] advisable to diminish the pressure by increasing the supply of feed water.

[068-006] Should the leak be so great that the level of the water in the boiler

[068-007] cannot be maintained, it will be expedient to drop the bars and quench the

[068-008] fire, so as to preserve the tubes and fire box from injury.

[068-009] 754. _Q._--If any of the working parts of a locomotive break or become

[068-010] deranged, what should be done?

[068-011] _A._--Should the piston rod or connecting rod break, or the cutters fall

[068-012] out or be clipped off--as sometimes happens to the piston cutter when the

[068-013] engine is suddenly reversed upon a heavy train--the parts should be

[068-014] disconnected, if the connection cannot be restored, so as to enable one

[068-015] engine to work; and of course the valve of the faulty engine must be kept

[068-016] closed. If one engine has not power enough to enable the train to proceed

[068-017] with the blast pipe full open, the engine may perhaps be able to take on a

[068-018] part of the carriages, or it may run on by itself to fetch assistance. The

[068-019] same course must be pursued if any of the valve gearing becomes deranged,

[068-020] and the defects cannot be rectified upon the spot.

[068-021] 755. _Q._--What are the most usual causes of railway collisions?

[068-022] _A._--Probably fogs and inexactness in the time kept by the trains.

[068-023] Collisions have sometimes occurred from carriages having been blown from a

[068-024] siding on to the rails by a high wind; and the slippery state of the rails,

[068-025] or the fracture of a break, has sometimes occasioned collisions at terminal

[068-026] stations. Collision has also repeatedly taken place from one engine having

[068-027] overtaken another, from the failure of a tube in the first engine, or from

[068-028] some other slight disarrangement; and collision has also taken place from

[068-029] the switches having been accidentally so left as to direct the train into a

[068-030] siding, instead of continuing it on the main line. Every train now carries

[068-031] fog signals, which are detonating packets, which are fixed upon the rails

[068-032] in advance or in the rear of a train which, whether from getting off the

[068-033] rails or otherwise, is stopped upon the line, and which are exploded by the

[068-034] wheels of any approaching train.

[068-035] 756. _Q._--What other duties of an engine-driver are there deserving

[068-036] attention?

[068-037] _A._--They are too various to be all enumerated here, and they also vary

[068-038] somewhat with the nature of the service. One rule, however, of universal

[068-039] application, is for the driver to look after matters himself, and not

[068-040] delegate to the stoker the duties which the person in charge of the engine

[068-041] should properly perform. Before leaving a station, the engine-driver should

[068-042] assure himself that he has the requisite supply of coke and water. Besides

[068-043] the firing tools and rakes for clearing the tubes, he should have with him

[068-044] in the tender a set of signal lamps and, torches, for tunnels and for

[068-045] night, detonating signals, screw keys, a small tank of oil, a small cask of

[068-046] tallow, and a small box of waste, a coal hammer, a chipping hammer, some

[068-047] wooden and iron plugs for the tubes, and an iron tube holder for inserting

[068-048] them, one or two buckets, a screw jack, wooden and iron wedges, split wire

[068-049] for pins, spare cutters, some chisels and files, a pinch bar, oil cans and

[068-050] an oil syringe, a chain, some spare bolts, and some cord, spun yarn, and

[068-051] rope.
