# George Corliss

**George Corliss** (1817–1888) was an American engineer and inventor. His Corliss [[steam|steam engine]], patented in 1849, was one of the most efficient stationary [[steam|steam engine]] designs of the nineteenth century. It used separate inlet and [[eduction|exhaust]] valves with a distinctive trip-gear mechanism that cut off steam precisely regardless of load.

## Career and Contributions

The Corliss engine achieved its remarkable fuel economy through several innovations:

- **Separate valves** for admission and [[eduction|exhaust]] at each end of the cylinder, reducing the heat losses that occurred in conventional [[slide-valve]] engines where the same passage served both functions
- **Trip-gear valve actuation**: the inlet valves were opened by a cam and then released (tripped) to close rapidly under spring action, providing a sharp and precise cut-off point independent of governor motion
- **Wrist-plate mechanism**: a rocking plate on the cylinder side distributed motion to all four valves, providing mechanical simplicity and reliable timing

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] references engines manufactured by the Corliss [[steam|Steam]] Engine Company as examples of high-efficiency stationary power, noting their widespread adoption in mills and factories (059-125).

## Legacy

The Corliss engine set the standard for stationary [[steam|steam engine]] efficiency from the 1850s through the early twentieth century. Thousands were installed worldwide, and the Corliss valve gear influenced subsequent engine designs. The company's engines were particularly valued in the textile industry, where steady speed and low coal consumption were essential.
