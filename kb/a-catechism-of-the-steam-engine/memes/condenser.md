The **[[condensation|condenser]]** is a vessel in a [[steam]] engine into which [[eduction|exhaust]] steam is discharged and condensed by contact with cold water. By collapsing the steam back into liquid water, the [[condensation|condenser]] creates and maintains a partial vacuum on the [[eduction|exhaust side]] of the [[piston]], greatly increasing the engine's power output and efficiency. It was one of [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s most important inventions, distinguishing his engine fundamentally from the earlier atmospheric engines of Newcomen.

## Principle of operation

The [[condensation|condenser]] works by injecting a spray of cold water into the [[eduction|exhaust]] steam:

> [002-045] The steam, after having performed its office in the cylinder, is permitted to pass into a vessel called the condenser, where a shower of cold water is discharged upon it. The steam is condensed by the cold water, and falls in the form of hot water to the bottom of the condenser.

The dramatic reduction in volume — steam occupies roughly 1,600 times the volume of the same mass of liquid water — creates the vacuum. The accumulated hot water and any entrained air are continuously removed by the [[air-pump]]:

> [002-048] The water, which would else be accumulated in the condenser, is continually being pumped out by a pump worked by the engine. This pump is called the air pump, because it also discharges any air which may have entered with the water.

## Quality of the vacuum

The vacuum achieved is not perfect. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] explains why:

> [002-056] Is the vacuum in the condenser a perfect vacuum? Not quite perfect; for the cold water entering for the purpose of condensation is heated by the steam, and emits a vapor of a tension represented by about three inches of mercury; that is, when the common barometer stands at 30 inches, a barometer with the space above the mercury communicating with the condenser, will stand at about 27 inches.

> [002-063] Is this imperfection of the vacuum wholly attributable to the vapor in the condenser? No; it is partly attributable to the presence of a small quantity of air which enters with the water, and which would accumulate until it destroyed the vacuum altogether but for the action of the air pump, which expels it with the water.

Thus a practical [[condensation|condenser]] vacuum of about 27 inches of mercury (roughly 90% of a perfect vacuum) was typical of well-maintained engines.

## Construction and situation

> [008-145] The condenser K is immerged in a cistern of cold water. At its side there is a tube I, for the admission of water to condense the steam, and which is governed by a cock, by opening which to any required extent, a jet of cold water may be made to play in the condenser.

The [[condensation|condenser]] is submerged in cold water to keep its walls cool and to assist in condensing any steam that reaches the vessel walls. The injection cock controlling the cold water jet is one of the key adjustments available to the engine operator:

> [010-045] To open the injection cock a little, to enable water to enter and condense the steam.

## Vacuum gauge

The degree of vacuum is monitored by an instrument:

> [002-071] Is a barometer sometimes applied to the condensers of steam engines? Yes; and it is called the vacuum gauge, because it shows the degree of vacuum which has been attained.

Together with the [[steam-gauge]] on the boiler, the [[vacuum-gauge|vacuum gauge]] formed the essential instrumentation pair for monitoring engine performance.

## Historical significance

[[james-watt|Watt]]'s separate [[condensation|condenser]] (patented in 1769) was the breakthrough that made the [[steam|steam engine]] a practical prime mover. By moving the condensation process out of the working cylinder and into a separate vessel, [[james-watt|Watt]] eliminated the wasteful cooling and reheating of the cylinder that had made Newcomen engines so inefficient. The [[condensation|condenser]] remains a feature of all condensing [[steam|steam engine]]s to the present day.
