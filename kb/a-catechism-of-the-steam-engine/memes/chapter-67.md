[067-001] 742. _Q._--Have you any information to offer relative to the lubrication of

[067-002] engine bearings?

[067-003] _A._--A very useful species of oil cup is now employed in a number of steam

[067-004] vessels, and which, it is said, accomplishes a considerable saving of oil,

[067-005] at the same time that it more effectually lubricates the bearings. A

[067-006] ratchet wheel is fixed upon a little shaft which passes through the side of

[067-007] the oil cup, and is put into slow revolution by a pendulum attached to its

[067-008] outside and in revolving it lifts up little buckets of oil and empties them

[067-009] down a funnel upon the centre of the bearing. Instead of buckets a few

[067-010] short pieces of wire are sometimes hung on the internal revolving wheel,

[067-011] the drops of oil which adhere on rising from the liquid being deposited.

[067-012] upon a high part set upon the funnel, and which, in their revolution, the

[067-013] hanging wires touch. By this plan, however, the oil is not well supplied at

[067-014] slow speeds, as the drops fall before the wires are in proper position for

[067-015] feeding the journal. Another lubricator consists of a cock or plug inserted

[067-016] in the neck of the oil cup, and set in revolution by a pendulum and ratchet

[067-017] wheel, or any other means. There is a small cavity in one side of the plug,

[067-018] which is filled with oil when that side is uppermost, and delivers the oil

[067-019] through the bottom pipe when it comes opposite to it.

[067-020] 743. _Q._--What are the prevailing causes of the heating of bearings?

[067-021] _A._--Bad fitting, deficient surface, and too tight screwing down.

[067-022] Sometimes the oil hole will choke, or the syphon wick for conducting the

[067-023] oil from the oil cup into the central pipe leading to the bearing will

[067-024] become clogged with mucilage from the oil. In some cases bearings heat from

[067-025] the existence of a cruciform groove on the top brass for the distribution

[067-026] of the oil, the effect of which is to leave the top of the bearings dry. In

[067-027] the case of revolving journals the plan for cutting a cruciform channel for

[067-028] the distribution of the oil does not do much damage; but in other cases, as

[067-029] in beam journals, for instance, it is most injurious, and the brasses

[067-030] cannot wear well wherever the plan is pursued. The right way is to make a

[067-031] horizontal groove along the brass where it meets the upper surface of the

[067-032] bearing, so that the oil may be all deposited on the highest point of the

[067-033] journal, leaving the force of gravity to send it downward. This channel

[067-034] should, of course, stop short a small distance from each flange of the

[067-035] brass, otherwise the oil would run out at the ends.

[067-036] 744. _Q._--If a bearing heats, what is to be done?

[067-037] _A._--The first thing is to relax the screws, slow or stop the engine, and

[067-038] cool the bearing with water, and if it is very hot, then hot water may be

[067-039] first employed to cool it, and then cold. Oil with sulphur intermingled is

[067-040] then to be administered, and as the parts cool down, the screws may be

[067-041] again cautiously tightened, so as to take any jump off the engine from the

[067-042] bearing being too slack. The bearings of direct acting screw engines

[067-043] require constant watching, as, if there be any disposition to heat

[067-044] manifested by them, they will probably heat with great rapidity from the

[067-045] high velocity at which the engines work. Every bearing of a direct acting

[067-046] screw engine should have a cock of water laid on to it, which may be

[067-047] immediately opened wide should heating occur; and it is advisable to work

[067-048] the engine constantly, partly with water, and partly with oil applied to

[067-049] the bearings. The water and oil are mixed by the friction into a species of

[067-050] soap which both cools and lubricates, and less oil moreover is used than if

[067-051] water were not employed. It is proper to turn off the water some time

[067-052] before the engine is stopped, so as to prevent the rusting of the bearings.

[067-053] MANAGEMENT OF LOCOMOTIVES.

[067-054] 745. _Q._--What are the chief duties of the engine driver of a locomotive?

[067-055] _A._--His first duties are those which concern the safety of the train; his

[067-056] next those which concern the safety and right management of the engine and

[067-057] boiler. The engine driver's first solicitude should be relative to the

[067-058] observation and right interpretation of the signals; and it is only after

[067-059] these demands upon his attention have been satisfied, that he can look to

[067-060] the state of his engine.

[067-061] 746. _Q._--As regards the engine and boiler, what should his main duties

[067-062] be?

[067-063] _A._--The engineer of a locomotive should constantly be upon the foot board

[067-064] of the engine, so that the regulator, the whistle or the reversing handle

[067-065] may be used instantly, if necessary; he must see that the level of the

[067-066] water in the boiler is duly maintained, and that the steam is kept at a

[067-067] uniform pressure. In feeding the boilers with water, and the furnaces with

[067-068] fuel, a good deal of care and some tact are necessary, as irregularity in

[067-069] the production of steam will often occasion priming, even though the water

[067-070] be maintained at a uniform level; and an excess of water will of itself

[067-071] occasion priming, while a deficiency is a source of obvious danger. The

[067-072] engine is generally furnished with three gauge cocks, and water should

[067-073] always come out of the second gauge cock, and steam out of the top one when

[067-074] the engine is running: but when the engine is at rest, the water in the

[067-075] boiler is lower than when in motion, so that when the engine is at rest,

[067-076] the water will be high enough if it just reaches to the middle gauge cock.

[067-077] In all boilers which generate steam rapidly, the volume of the water is

[067-078] increased by the mingled steam, and in feeding with cold water the level at

[067-079] first falls; but it rises on opening the safety valve, which causes the

[067-080] steam in the water to swell to a larger volume. In locomotive boilers, the

[067-081] rise of the water level due to the rapid generation of steam is termed

[067-082] "false water." To economize fuel, the variable expansion gear, if the

[067-083] engine has one, should be adjusted to the load, and the blast pipe should

[067-084] be worked with the least possible contraction; and at stations the damper

[067-085] should be closed to prevent the dissipation of heat.

[067-086] 747. _Q._--In starting from a station, what precautions should be observed

[067-087] with respect to the feed?

[067-088] _A._--In starting from a station, and also in ascending inclined planes,

[067-089] the feed water is generally shut off; and therefore before stopping or

[067-090] ascending inclined planes, the boiler should be well filled up with water.

[067-091] In descending inclined planes an extra supply of water may be introduced

[067-092] into the boiler, and the fire may be fed, as there, is at such times a

[067-093] superfluity of steam. In descending inclined planes the regulator must be

[067-094] partially closed, and it should be entirely closed if the plane be very

[067-095] steep. The same precaution should be observed in the case of curves, or

[067-096] rough places on the line, and in passing over points or crossings.

[067-097] 748. _Q._--In approaching a station, how should the supply of water and

[067-098] fuel be regulated?

[067-099] _A._--The boiler should be well filled with water on approaching a station,

[067-100] as there is then steam to spare, and additional water cannot be

[067-101] conveniently supplied when the engine is stationary. The furnace should be

[067-102] fed with small quantities of fuel at a time, and the feed should be turned

[067-103] off just before a fresh supply of fuel is introduced. The regulator may, at

[067-104] the same time, be partially closed; and if the blast pipe be a variable

[067-105] one, it will be expedient to open it widely while the fuel is being

[067-106] introduced, to check the rush of air in through the furnace door, and then

[067-107] to contract it very much so soon as the furnace door is closed, in order to

[067-108] recover the fire quickly. The proper thickness of coke upon the grate

[067-109] depends upon the intensity of the draught; but in heavily loaded engines it

[067-110] is usually kept up to the bottom of the fire door. Care, however, must be

[067-111] taken that the coke does not reach up to the bottom row of tubes so as to

[067-112] choke them up. The fuel is usually disposed on the grate like a vault; and

[067-113] if the fire box be a square one, it is heaped high in the corners, the

[067-114] better to maintain the combustion.

[067-115] 749. _Q._--How can you tell whether the feed pumps are operating properly?

[067-116] _A._--To ascertain whether the pumps are acting well, the pet cock must be

[067-117] turned, and if any of the valves stick they will sometimes be induced to

[067-118] act again by working with the pet cock open, or alternately open and shut.

[067-119] Should the defect arise from a leakage of steam into the pump, which

[067-120] prevents the pump from drawing, the pet cock remedies the evil by

[067-121] permitting the steam to escape.

[067-122] 750. _Q._--What precautions should be taken against priming in locomotives?

[067-123] _A._--Should priming occur from the water in the boiler being dirty, a

[067-124] portion of it may be blown out; and should there be much boiling down

[067-125] through the glass gauge tube, the stop cock may be partially closed. The

[067-126] water should be wholly blown out of locomotive boilers three times a week,

[067-127] and at those times two mud-hole doors at opposite corners of the boiler

[067-128] should be opened, and the boiler be washed internally by means of a hose.

[067-129] If the boiler be habitually fed with dirty water, the priming will be a

[067-130] constant source of trouble.

[067-131] 751. _Q._--What measures should the locomotive engineer take, to check the

[067-132] velocity of the train, on approaching a station where he has to stop?

[067-133] _A._--On approaching a station the regulator should be gradually closed,

[067-134] and it should be completely shut about half a mile from the station if the

[067-135] train be a very heavy one: the train may then be brought to rest by means

[067-136] of the breaks. Too much reliance, however, must not be put upon the breaks,

[067-137] as they sometimes give way, and in frosty weather are nearly inoperative.

[067-138] In cases of urgency the steam may be thrown upon the reverse side of the

[067-139] piston, but it is desirable to obviate this necessity as far as possible.

[067-140] At terminal stations the steam should be shut off earlier than at roadside

[067-141] stations, as a collision will take place at terminal stations if the train

[067-142] overshoots the place where it ought to stop. There should always be a good

[067-143] supply of water when the engine stops, but the fire may be suffered

[067-144] gradually to burn low toward the conclusion of the journey.

[067-145] 752. _Q._--What is the duty of an engine man on arriving at the end of his

[067-146] journey?

[067-147] _A._--So soon as the engine stops it should be wiped down, and be then

[067-148] carefully examined: the brasses should be tried, to see whether they are

[067-149] slack or have been heating; and, by the application of a gauge, it should

[067-150] be ascertained occasionally whether the wheels are square on their axles,

[067-151] and whether the axles have end play, which should be prevented. The

[067-152] stuffing boxes must be tightened, and the valve gear examined, and the

[067-153] eccentrics be occasionally looked at to see that they have not shifted on

[067-154] their axles, though this defect will be generally intimated by the

[067-155] irregular beating of the engines. The tubes should also be examined and

[067-156] cleaned out, and the ashes emptied out of the smoke box through the small

[067-157] ash door at the end. If the engine be a six-wheeled one, with the driving

[067-158] wheels in the middle, it will be liable to pitch, and oscillate if too much

[067-159] weight be thrown upon the driving wheels; and where such faults are found

[067-160] to exist, the weight upon the drivings wheels should be diminished. The

[067-161] practice of blowing off the boiler by the steam, as is always done in

[067-162] marine boilers, should not be permitted as a general rule in locomotive

[067-163] boilers, when the tubes are of brass and the fire box of copper; but when

[067-164] the tubes and fire boxes are of iron, there will not be an equal risk of

[067-165] injury. Before starting on a journey, the engine man should take a summary

[067-166] glance beneath the engine--but before doing so he ought to assure himself

[067-167] that no other engine is coming up at the time. The regulator, when the

[067-168] engine is standing, should be closed and locked, and the eccentric rod be

[067-169] fixed out of gear, and the tender break screwed down; the cocks of the oil

[067-170] vessels should at the same time be shut, but should all be opened a short

[067-171] time before the train starts.
