[035-001] 421. _Q._--Will you explain the means that are adopted to regulate the

[035-002] admission of steam to the cylinders?

[035-003] _A._--In locomotives, the admission of the steam from the boiler to the

[035-004] cylinders is regulated by a valve called the regulator, which is generally

[035-005] placed immediately above the internal fire box, and is connected with two

[035-006] copper pipes;--one conducting steam from the highest point of the dome down

[035-007] to it, and the other conducting the steam that has passed through it along

[035-008] the boiler to the upper part of the smoke box. Regulators may be divided

[035-009] into two sorts, viz., those with, sliding valves and steam ports, and those

[035-010] with conical valves and seats, of which the latter kind are the best. The

[035-011] former kind have for the most part consisted of a circular valve and face,

[035-012] with radial apertures, the valve resembling the outstretched wings of a

[035-013] butterfly, and being made to revolve on its central pivot by connecting

[035-014] links between its outer edges, or by its central spindle. In some of

[035-015] Stephenson's engines the regulator consists of a slide valve covering a

[035-016] port on the top of the valve chests. A rod passes from this valve through

[035-017] the smoke box below the boiler, and by means of a lever parallel to the

[035-018] starting lever, is brought up to the engineer's reach. Cocks were at first

[035-019] used as regulators, but were given up, as they were found liable to stick

[035-020] fast. A gridiron slide valve has been used by Stephenson, which consists of

[035-021] a perforated square moving upon a face with an equal number of holes. This

[035-022] plan of a valve gives, with a small movement, a large area of opening. In

[035-023] Bury's engines a sort of conical plug is used, which is withdrawn by

[035-024] turning the handle in front of the fire box: a spiral grove of a very large

[035-025] pitch is made in the valve spindle, in which fits a pin fixed to the

[035-026] boiler, and by turning the spindle an end motion is given to it, which

[035-027] either shuts or opens the steam passage according to the direction in which

[035-028] it is turned. The best regulator would probably be a valve of the

[035-029] equilibrium description, such as is used in the Cornish engine: there would

[035-030] be no friction in such a regulator, and it could be opened or shut with a

[035-031] small amount of force. Such valves, indeed, are now sometimes employed for

[035-032] regulators in locomotives.

[035-033] CHAPTER VIII.

[035-034] CONSTRUCTIVE DETAILS OF ENGINES.

[035-035] PUMPING ENGINES.

[035-036] 422. _Q._--Will you explain the course of procedure in the erection of a

[035-037] pumping engine, such as Boulton and Watt introduced into Cornwall?

[035-038] _A._--The best instructions on this subject are those of Mr. Watt himself,

[035-039] which are as follows:--Having fixed on the proper situation of the pump in

[035-040] the pit, from its centre measure out the distance to the centre of the

[035-041] cylinder, from which set off all the other dimensions of the house,

[035-042] including the thickness of the walls, and dig out the whole of the included

[035-043] ground to the depth of the bottom of the cellar, so that the bottom of the

[035-044] cylinder may stand on a level with the natural ground of the place, or

[035-045] lower, if convenient, for the less the height of the house above the

[035-046] ground, the firmer it will be. The foundations of the walls must be laid at

[035-047] least two feet lower than the bottom of the cellar, unless the foundation

[035-048] be firm rock; and care must be taken to leave a small drain into the pit

[035-049] quite through the lowest part of the foundation of the lever wall, to let

[035-050] off any water that may be spilt in the engine house, or may naturally come

[035-051] into the cellar. If the foundation at that depth does not prove good, you

[035-052] must either go down to a better if in your reach, or make it good by a

[035-053] platform of wood or piles, or both.

[035-054] 423. _Q._--These directions refer to the foundations?

[035-055] _A._.--Yes; but I will now proceed to the other parts. Within the house,

[035-056] low walls must be built to carry the cylinder beams, so as to leave

[035-057] sufficient room to come at the holding down bolts, and the ends of these

[035-058] beams must also be lodged in the wall The lever wall must be built in the

[035-059] firmest manner, and run solid, course by course, with thin lime mortar,

[035-060] care being taken that the lime has not been long slaked. If the house be

[035-061] built of stone, let the stones be large and long, and let many headers be

[035-062] laid through the wall: it should also be a rule, that every stone be laid

[035-063] on the broadest bed it has, and never set on its edge. A course or two

[035-064] above the lintel of the door that leads to the condenser, build into the

[035-065] wall two parallel flat thin bars of iron equally distant from each other,

[035-066] and from the outside and inside of the wall, and reaching the whole breadth

[035-067] of the lever wall. About a foot higher in the wall, lay at every four feet

[035-068] of the breadth of the front, other bars of the same kind at right angles to

[035-069] the former course, and reaching quite through the thickness of the wall;

[035-070] and at each front corner lay a long bar in the middle of the side walls,

[035-071] and reaching quite through the front wall; if these bars are 10 feet or 12

[035-072] feet long it will be sufficient. When the house is built up nearly to the

[035-073] bottom of the opening under the great beam another double course of bars is

[035-074] to be built in, as has been directed. At the level of the upper cylinder

[035-075] beams, holes must be left in the walls for their ends, with room to move

[035-076] them laterally, so that the cylinder may be got in; and smaller holes must

[035-077] be left quite through the walls for the introduction of iron bars, which

[035-078] being firmly fastened to the cylinder beams at one end, and screwed at the

[035-079] other or outer end, will serve, by their going through both the front and

[035-080] back walls, to bind the house more firmly together. The spring beams or

[035-081] iron bars fastened to them must reach quite through the back wall, and be

[035-082] keyed or screwed up tight; and they must be firmly fastened to the lever

[035-083] wall on each side, either by iron bars, firm pieces of wood, or long strong

[035-084] stones, reaching far back into the wall. They must also be bedded solidly,

[035-085] and the residue of the opening must be built up in the firmest manner.

[035-086] 424. _Q._--If there be a deficiency of water for the purpose of

[035-087] condensation, what course should be pursued?

[035-088] _A._--If there be no water in the neighborhood that can be employed for the

[035-089] purpose of condensation, it will be necessary to make a pond, dug in the

[035-090] earth, for the reception of the water delivered by the air pump, to the end

[035-091] that it may be cooled and used again for the engine. The pond may be three

[035-092] or four feet deep, and lined with turf, puddled, or otherwise made water

[035-093] tight. Throwing up the water into the air in the form of a jet to cool it,

[035-094] has been found detrimental; as the water is then charged with air which

[035-095] vitiates the vacuum.

[035-096] 425. _Q._--How is the piston of a pumping engine packed?

[035-097] _A._--To pack the piston, take sixty common-sized white or untarred

[035-098] rope-yarns, and with them plait a gasket or flat rope as close and firm as

[035-099] possible, tapering for eighteen inches at each end, and long enough to go

[035-100] round the piston, and overlapped for that length; coil this rope the thin

[035-101] way as hard as possible, and beat it with a sledge hammer until its breadth

[035-102] answers the place; put it in and beat it down with a wooden drift and a

[035-103] hand mallet, pour some melted tallow all around, then pack in a layer of

[035-104] white oakum half an inch thick, so that the whole packing may have the

[035-105] depth of five to six inches, depending on the size of the engine; finally,

[035-106] screw down the junk ring. The packing should be beat solid, but not too

[035-107] hard, otherwise it will create so great a friction as to prevent the easy

[035-108] going of the engine. Abundance of tallow should be allowed, especially at

[035-109] first; the quantity required will be less as the cylinder grows smooth. In

[035-110] some of the more modern pumping engines, the piston is provided with

[035-111] metallic packing, consisting for the most part of a single ring with a

[035-112] tongue piece to break the joint, and packed behind with hemp. The upper

[035-113] edge of the metallic ring is sharpened away from the inside so as to permit

[035-114] more conveniently the application of hemp packing behind it; and the junk

[035-115] ring is made much the same as if no metallic packing were employed.

[035-116] 426. _Q._--Will you explain the mode of putting the engine into operation?

[035-117] _A._--To set the engine going, the steam must be raised until the pressure

[035-118] in the steam pipe is at least equal to three pounds on the square inch; and

[035-119] when the cylinder jacket is fully warmed, and steam issues freely from the

[035-120] jacket cock, open all the valves or regulators; the steam will then

[035-121] forcibly blow out the air or water contained in the eduction pipe, and to

[035-122] get rid of the air in the cylinder, shut the steam valve after having blown

[035-123] through the engine for a few minutes. The cold water round the condenser

[035-124] will condense some of the steam contained in the eduction pipe, and its

[035-125] place will be supplied by some of the air from the cylinder. The steam

[035-126] valve must again be opened to blow out that air, and the operation is to be

[035-127] repeated until the air is all drawn out of the cylinder. When that is the

[035-128] case shut all the valves, and observe if the vacuum gauge shows a vacuum in

[035-129] the condenser; when there is a vacuum equivalent to three inches of

[035-130] mercury, open the injection a very little, and shut it again immediately;

[035-131] and if this produces any considerable vacuum, open the exhausting valve a

[035-132] very little way, and the injection at the same time. If the engine does not

[035-133] now commence its motion, it must be blown through again until it moves. If

[035-134] the engine be lightly loaded, or if there be no water in the pumps, the

[035-135] throttle valve must be kept nearly closed, and the top and exhaustion

[035-136] regulators must be opened only a very little way, else the engine will make

[035-137] its stroke with violence, and perhaps do mischief. If there is much

[035-138] unbalanced weight on the pump end, the plug which opens the steam valve

[035-139] must be so regulated, that the valve will only be opened very slightly; and

[035-140] if after a few strokes it is found that the engine goes out too slowly, the

[035-141] valve may be then so adjusted as to open wider. The engine should always be

[035-142] made to work full stroke, that is, until the catch pins be made to come

[035-143] within half an inch of the springs at each end, and the piston should stand

[035-144] high enough in the cylinder when the engine is at rest, to spill over into

[035-145] the perpendicular steam pipe any water which may be condensed above it; for

[035-146] if water remain upon the piston, it will increase the consumption of steam.

[035-147] When the engine is to be stopped, shut the injection valve and secure it,

[035-148] and adjust the tappets so as to prevent the exhausting valve from opening

[035-149] and to allow the steam valve to open and remain open, otherwise a partial

[035-150] vacuum may arise in the cylinder, and it may be filled with water from the

[035-151] injection or from leaks. A single acting engine, when it is in good order,

[035-152] ought to be capable of going as slow as one stroke in ten minutes, and as

[035-153] fast as ten strokes in one minute; and if it does not fulfil these

[035-154] conditions, there is some fault which should be ascertained and remedied.
