[039-001] 455. _Q._--What species of packing is used in air pumps?

[039-002] _A._--Metallic packing has in some instances been employed in air pump

[039-003] buckets, but its success has not been such as to lead to its further

[039-004] adoption. The packing commonly employed is hemp. A deep solid block of

[039-005] metal, however, without any packing, is often employed with a satisfactory

[039-006] result; but this block should have circular grooves cut round its edge to

[039-007] hold water. Where ordinary packing is employed, the bucket should always be

[039-008] made with a junk ring, whereby the packing may be easily screwed down at

[039-009] any time with facility. In slow moving engines the bucket valve is

[039-010] generally of the spindle or pot-lid kind, but butterfly valves are

[039-011] sometimes used. The foot and delivery valves are for the most part of the

[039-012] flap or hanging kind. These valves all make a considerable noise in

[039-013] working, and are objectionable in many ways. Valves on Belidor's

[039-014] construction, which is in effect that of a throttle valve hung off the

[039-015] centre, were some years ago proposed for the delivery and foot valves; and

[039-016] it appears probable that their operation would be more satisfactory than

[039-017] that of the valves usually employed.

[039-018] 456. _Q._--Where is the delivery valve usually situated?

[039-019] _A._--Some delivery valve seats are bolted into the mouth of the air pump,

[039-020] whereby access to the pump bucket is rendered difficult: but more commonly

[039-021] the delivery valve is a flap valve exterior to the pump. If delivery valve

[039-022] seats be put in the mouth of the air pump at all, the best mode of fixing

[039-023] them appears to be that adopted by Messrs. Maudslay. The top of the pump

[039-024] barrel is made quite fair across, and upon this flat surface a plate

[039-025] containing the delivery valve is set, there being a small ledge all round

[039-026] to keep it steady. Between the bottom of the stuffing box of the pump cover

[039-027] and the eye of the valve seat a short pipe extends encircling the pump rod,

[039-028] its lower end checked into the eye of the valve seat, and its upper end

[039-029] widening out to form the bottom of the stuffing box of the pump cover. Upon

[039-030] the top of this pipe some screws press, which are accessible from the top

[039-031] of the stuffing box gland, and the packing also aids in keeping down the

[039-032] pipe, the function of which is to retain the valve seat in its place. When

[039-033] the pump bucket has to be examined the valve seat may be slung with the

[039-034] cover, so as to come up with the same purchase. For the bucket valves of

[039-035] such pumps Messrs. Maudslay employ two or more concentric ring valves with

[039-036] a small lift. These valves have given a good deal of trouble in some cases,

[039-037] in consequence of the frequent fracture of the bolts which guide and

[039-038] confine the rings; but this is only a fault of detail which is easily

[039-039] remedied, and the principle appears to be superior to that of any of the

[039-040] other metallic air pump valves at present in common use.

[039-042] 457. _Q._--Are not air pump valves now very generally made of india rubber?

[039-043] _A._--They are almost invariably so made if the engines are travelling

[039-044] fast, as in the case of direct acting screw engines, and they are very

[039-045] often made of large discs or rings of india rubber, even when the engines

[039-046] travel slowly. A very usual and eligible arrangement for many purposes is

[039-047] that shown in fig. 41, where both foot and delivery valves are situated in

[039-048] the ends of the pump, and they, as well as the valve in the bucket are made

[039-049] of india rubber rings closing on a grating. The trunk in the air pump

[039-050] enables guide rods to be dispensed with.

[039-054] 458. _Q._--The air pump, when double acting, has of course inlet and outlet

[039-055] valves at each end?

[039-056] _A._--Yes; and the general arrangement of the valves of double acting air

[039-057] pumps, such as are usual in direct acting screw engines, is that

[039-058] represented in the figure of Penn's trunk engine already described in

[039-059] Chapter I. Each inlet and outlet valve consists of a number of india rubber

[039-060] discs set over a perforated brass plate, and each disc is bound down by a

[039-061] bolt in the middle, which bolt also secures a brass guard set above the

[039-062] disc to prevent it from rising too high. The usual configuration of those

[039-063] valves is that represented in figs. 42, 43, and 44; figs. 42 and 43 being a

[039-064] section and ground plan of the species of valve used by Messrs. Penn, and

[039-065] fig. 44 being a section of that used by Messrs. Maudslay. It is important

[039-066] in these valves to have the india rubber thick,--say about an inch thick

[039-067] for valves eight inches in diameter. It is also advisable to make the

[039-068] central bolts with a nut above and a nut below, and to form the bolt with a

[039-069] counter sunk neck, so that it will not fall down when the top nut is

[039-070] removed. The lower point of the bolt should be riveted over on the nut to

[039-071] prevent it from unscrewing, and the top end should have a split pin through

[039-072] the point for the same purpose. The hole through which the bolt passes

[039-073] should be tapped, though the bolt is not screwed into it, so that if a bolt

[039-074] breaks, a temporary stud may be screwed into the hole without the necessity

[039-075] of taking out the whole plate. The guard should be large, else the disc may

[039-076] stretch in the central hole until it comes over it; but the guard should

[039-077] not permit too much lift of the valve, else a good deal of the water and

[039-078] air will return into the pump at the return stroke before the valve shuts.

[039-079] Penn's guard is rather small, and Maudslay's permits too much lift.

[039-080] 459. _Q._--What is the proper area through the valve gratings?

[039-081] _A._--The collective area should be at least equal to the area of the pump

[039-082] piston, and the lower edges of the perforations should be rounded off to

[039-083] afford more free ingress or egress to the water.

[039-084] 460. _Q._--Is there much strain thrown on the plates in which the valves

[039-085] are set?

[039-086] _A._--A good deal of strain; and in the earlier direct acting screw engines

[039-087] these plates were nearly in every case made too light. They should be made

[039-088] thick, have strong feathers upon them, and be very securely bolted down

[039-089] with split pins at the points of the bolts, to prevent them from

[039-090] unscrewing. The plate will be very apt to be broken should some of the

[039-091] bolts become loose. Of course all the bolts and split pins, as well as the

[039-092] plates and guards, must be of brass.

[039-093] 461. _Q._--How are the plates to be taken out should that become necessary?

[039-094] _A._--They are usually taken out through a door in the top of the hot well

[039-095] provided for that purpose, which door should be as large as the plates

[039-096] themselves; and it is a good precaution to cast upon this door--which will

[039-097] be of cast iron--six or eight stout projecting feet which will press upon

[039-098] the top of the outlet or delivery valve plate when the door is screwed

[039-099] down. The upper or delivery valve plate and the lower or foot valve plate

[039-100] should have similar feet. A large part of the strain will thus be

[039-101] transferred from the plates to the door, which can easily be made strong

[039-102] enough to sustain it. It is advisable that the plates should lie at an

[039-103] angle so that the shock of the water may not come upon the whole surface at

[039-104] once.

[039-105] 462. _Q._--Does the double acting air pump usual in direct acting screw

[039-106] engines, produce as good a vacuum as the single acting air pump usual in

[039-107] paddle engines?

[039-108] _A._--It will do so if properly constructed; but I do not know of any case

[039-109] of a double acting air pump, with india rubber valves, which has been

[039-110] properly constructed.

[039-111] 463. _Q._--What is the fault of such pumps?

[039-112] _A._--The pump frequently works by starts, as if at times it did not draw

[039-113] at all, and then again on a sudden gorged itself with water, so as to throw

[039-114] a great strain upon the working parts. The vacuum, moreover, is by no means

[039-115] so good as it should be, and it is a universal vice of direct acting screw

[039-116] engines that the vacuum is defective. I have been at some pains to

[039-117] investigate the causes of this imperfection; and in a sugar house engine

[039-118] fitted with pumps like those of a direct acting screw engine to maintain a

[039-119] vacuum in the pans, I found that a better vacuum was produced when the

[039-120] engine was going slowly than when it was going fast; which is quite the

[039-121] reverse of what was to have been expected, as the hot water which had to be

[039-122] removed by the condensation of the steam proceeding from the pan, was a

[039-123] constant quantity. In this engine, too, which was a high pressure one, the

[039-124] irregularities of the engine consequent upon the fitful catching of the

[039-125] water by the pump, was more conspicuous, as the working of this vacuum pump

[039-126] was the only work that the engine had to perform.

[039-127] 464. _Q._--And were you able to discover the cause of these irregularities?

[039-128] _A._--The main cause of them I found to be the largeness of the space left

[039-129] between the valve plates in this class of pumps, and out of which there is

[039-130] nothing to press the air or water which may be lying there. It consequently

[039-131] happens, that if there be the slightest leakage of air into the pump, this

[039-132] air is merely compressed, and not expelled, by the advance of the air pump

[039-133] piston. It expands again to its former bulk on the return of the pump

[039-134] piston, and prevents the water from entering until there is such an

[039-135] accumulation of pressure in the condenser as forces the water into the

[039-136] pump, when the air being expelled by the water, causes a good vacuum to be

[039-137] momentarily formed in the pump when it gorges itself by taking a sudden

[039-138] gulp of water. So soon, however, as the pressure falls in the condenser and

[039-139] some more air leaks into the pump, the former imperfect action recurs and

[039-140] is again redressed in the same violent manner.

[039-141] 465. _Q._--Is this irregular action of the pump the cause of the imperfect

[039-142] vacuum?

[039-143] _A._--It is one cause. Sometimes one end of the pump will alone draw and

[039-144] the other end will be inoperative, although it is equally open to the

[039-145] condenser, and this will chiefly take place at the stuffing box end, where

[039-146] a leakage of air is more likely to occur. I find, however, that even when

[039-147] both ends of the pump are acting equally and there is no leakage of air at

[039-148] all, the vacuum maintained by a double acting horizontal pump with india

[039-149] rubber valves, is not so good as that maintained by a single acting pump of

[039-150] the kind usual in old engines.

[039-151] 466. _Q._--Will you specify more precisely what were the results you

[039-152] obtained?

[039-153] _A._--When the vacuum pan was exhausted by the pumps without any boiling

[039-154] being carried on in the pan, but only a little cold water being let into

[039-155] it, and also into the pumps to enable them to act in their best manner, it

[039-156] was found that whereas with the old pump a vacuum of 114 on the sugar

[039-157] boiler's gauge could be readily obtained, equal to about 29-1/2 inches of

[039-158] mercury, the lowest that could possibly be got with the new horizontal pump

[039-159] was 122 degrees of the sugar boiler's gauge, or 29 inches of mercury, and

[039-160] to get that the engine must not go faster than 10 or 12 strokes per minute.

[039-161] The proper speed of the engine was 75 strokes per minute, but if allowed to

[039-162] go at that speed the vacuum fell to 130 of the sugar maker's gauge, or

[039-163] 28-1/2 inches of mercury. When the steam was let into the worms of the pan

[039-164] so as to boil the water in it, the vacuum was 134 at 75 revolutions of the

[039-165] engine, and went down to 132 at 40 revolutions, but rose again to 135,

[039-166] equal to about 28-1/4 inches of mercury, at 20 revolutions.
