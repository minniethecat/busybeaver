The **cataract** is a hydraulic timing device employed on Cornish pumping engines to regulate the interval between successive strokes, and thereby control the engine's operating speed. Unlike the centrifugal [[governor]], which continuously modulates steam admission, the cataract imposes a deliberate pause between strokes — the engine waits until the cataract completes its cycle before initiating the next power stroke:

> [019-057] In pumping engines there is the cataract for regulating the speed of the engine. (019-057)

The cataract was essential in mine pumping applications, where the quantity of water to be lifted varied and the engine's speed needed to be adjusted accordingly.

## Construction and operation

The cataract is a simple hydraulic device consisting of a small plunger working in a barrel submerged in a cistern of water:

> [019-148] The cataract consists of a small pump-plunger and barrel, set in a cistern of water, the barrel being furnished on the one side with a valve, opening inwards, through which the water obtains admission to the pump chamber from the cistern, and on the other by a plug, through which, if the plunger be forced down, the water must pass out of the pump chamber. (019-152)

The operating cycle proceeds as follows: During the engine's upward (return) stroke — accomplished by the preponderance of weight at the pump end of the beam — the plunger of the cataract is lifted, and water enters freely through the inlet valve:

> [019-155] The engine in the upward stroke of the piston raises up the plunger of the cataract by means of a small rod — the water entering readily through the valve already referred to. (019-157)

When the engine reaches the top of its stroke, the lifting rod is liberated. The plunger now descends under its own weight, but its descent is resisted by the water which must escape through a restricted outlet:

> [019-157] When the engine reaches the top of the stroke, it liberates the rod by which the plunger has been drawn up, and the plunger then descends by gravity, forcing out the water through the cock, the orifice of which has previously been adjusted, and the plunger in its descent opens the injection valve, which causes the engine to make a stroke. (019-161)

Only when the plunger reaches the bottom of its travel and opens the [[injection-cock|injection valve]] does the next power stroke commence. The time taken for the plunger to descend constitutes the pause between strokes.

## Speed regulation

The engine's speed is adjusted by varying the opening of the cataract cock:

> [019-163] If the cock of the cataract be shut, it is clear that the plunger cannot descend at all, and as in that case the injection valve cannot be opened, the engine must stand still; but if the cock be slightly opened, the plunger will descend slowly, the injection valve will slowly open, and the engine will make a gradual stroke. (019-168)

The extent to which the cock is open regulates the speed with which the engine works. A small opening yields a slow descent and a long pause between strokes; a larger opening permits rapid descent and a faster engine:

> [019-170] By the use of the cataract, the speed of the engine may be varied to suit the variations in the quantity of water requiring to be lifted from the mine. (019-171)

The mine engineer — or "captain" — could adjust the cataract cock to match the engine's pace to the inflow of water into the mine sump. In wet seasons the cock was opened further for faster pumping; in drier conditions a slower pace economized coal.

## Variants

While the water cataract was most common, alternative fluids were sometimes used:

> [019-172] In some cases an air cylinder, and in other cases an oil cylinder, is employed instead of the apparatus just described; but the principle on which the whole of these contrivances operate is identical. (019-174)

An air cataract used a piston compressing air through a small orifice; an oil cataract substituted oil for water. Both offered more consistent viscosity than water across temperature ranges, but were more expensive to construct and maintain.

## Role in the Cornish cycle

The cataract was a defining feature of the Cornish pumping engine's operating cycle. Unlike a rotative engine, which runs continuously, the Cornish engine made discrete single strokes separated by pauses. The cataract governed these pauses. Combined with the use of high-pressure steam and early [[cut-off]] (sometimes as early as one-twelfth of the stroke), the cataract enabled the Cornish engine to achieve remarkable fuel economy — a critical advantage in remote mining districts where coal was expensive to transport.
