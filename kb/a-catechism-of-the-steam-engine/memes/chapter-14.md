[014-001] 171. _Q._--And is not high pressure steam hotter than low pressure steam?

[014-002] _A._--Yes, the temperature of steam rises with the pressure.

[014-003] 172. _Q._--How then comes it, that there is the same quantity of heat in

[014-004] the same weight of high and low pressure steam, when the high pressure

[014-005] steam has the highest temperature?

[014-006] _A._--Because although the temperature or sensible heat rises with the

[014-007] pressure, the latent heat becomes less in about the same proportion. And as

[014-008] has been already explained, the latent and sensible heats taken together

[014-009] make up nearly the same amount at all temperatures; but the amount is

[014-010] somewhat greater at the higher temperatures. As a damp sponge becomes wet

[014-011] when subjected to pressure, so warm vapor becomes hot when forced into less

[014-012] bulk, but in neither case does the quantity of moisture or the quantity of

[014-013] heat sustain any alteration. Common air becomes so hot by compression that

[014-014] tinder may be inflamed by it, as is seen in the instrument for producing

[014-015] instantaneous light by suddenly forcing air into a syringe.

[014-016] 173. _Q._--What law is followed by surcharged steam on the application of

[014-017] heat?

[014-018] _A._--The same as that followed by air, in which the increments in volume

[014-019] are very nearly in the same proportion as the increments in temperature;

[014-020] and the increment in volume for each degree of increased temperature is

[014-021] 1/490th part of the volume at 32°. A volume of air which, at the

[014-022] temperature of 32°, occupies 100 cubic feet, will at 212° fill a space of

[014-023] 136.73 cubic feet. The volume which air or steam--out of contact with

[014-024] water--of a given temperature acquires by being heated to a higher

[014-025] temperature, the pressure remaining the same, may be found by the following

[014-026] rule:--To each of the temperatures before and after expansion, add the

[014-027] constant number 458: divide the greater sum by the less, and multiply the

[014-028] quotient by the volume at the lower temperature; the product will give the

[014-029] expanded volume.

[014-030] 174. _Q._--If the relative volumes of steam and water are known, is it

[014-031] possible to tell the quantity of water which should be supplied to a

[014-032] boiler, when the quantity of steam expended is specified?

[014-033] _A._--Yes; at the atmospheric pressure, about a cubic inch of water has to

[014-034] be supplied to the boiler for every cubic foot of steam abstracted; at

[014-035] other pressures, the relative bulk of water and steam may be determined as

[014-036] follows:--To the temperature of steam in degrees of Fahrenheit, add the

[014-037] constant number 458, multiply the sum by 37.3, and divide the product by

[014-038] the elastic force of the steam in pounds per square inch; the quotient will

[014-039] give the volume required.

[014-040] 175. _Q._--Will this rule give the proper dimensions of the pump for

[014-041] feeding the boiler with water?

[014-042] _A._--No; it is necessary in practice that

[014-043] the feed pump should be able to supply the boiler with a much larger

[014-044] quantity of water than what is indicated by these proportions, from the

[014-045] risk of leaks, priming, or other disarrangements, and the feed pump is

[014-046] usually made capable of raising 3-1/2 times the water evaporated by the

[014-047] boiler. About 1/240th of the capacity of the cylinder answers very well for

[014-048] the capacity of the feed pump in the case of low pressure engines,

[014-049] supposing the cylinder to be double acting, and the pump single acting; but

[014-050] it is better to exceed this size.

[014-051] 176. _Q._--Is this rule for the size of the feed pump applicable to the

[014-052] case of high pressure engines?

[014-053] _A._--Clearly not; for since a cylinder full of high pressure steam,

[014-054] contains more water than the same cylinder full of low pressure steam, the

[014-055] size of the feed must vary in the same proportion as the density of the

[014-056] steam. In all pumps a good deal of the effect is lost from the imperfect

[014-057] action of the valves; and in engines travelling at a high rate of speed, in

[014-058] particular, a large part of the water is apt to return, through the suction

[014-059] valve of the pump, especially if much lift be permitted to that valve. In

[014-060] steam vessels moreover, where the boiler is fed with salt water, and where

[014-061] a certain quantity of supersalted water has to be blown out of the boiler

[014-062] from time to time, to prevent the water from reaching too high a degree of

[014-063] concentration, the feed pump requires to be of additional size to supply

[014-064] the extra quantity of water thus rendered necessary. When the feed water is

[014-065] boiling or very hot, as in some engines is the case, the feed pump will not

[014-066] draw from a depth, and will altogether act less efficiently, so that an

[014-067] extra size of pump has to be provided in consequence. These and other

[014-068] considerations which might be mentioned, show the propriety of making the

[014-069] feed pump very much larger than theory requires. The proper proportions of

[014-070] pumps, however, forms part of a subsequent chapter.

[014-073] CHAPTER III.

[014-074] EXPANSION OF STEAM AND ACTION OF THE VALVES.

[014-075] 177. _Q._--What is meant by working engines expansively?

[014-076] _A._--Adjusting the valves, so that the steam is shut off from the cylinder

[014-077] before the end of the stroke, whereby the residue of the stroke is left to

[014-078] be completed by the expanding steam.

[014-079] 178. _Q._--And what is the benefit of that practice?

[014-080] _A._--It accomplishes an important saving of steam, or, what is the same

[014-081] thing, of fuel; but it diminishes the power of the engine, while increasing

[014-082] the power of the steam. A larger engine will be required to do the same

[014-083] work, but the work will be done with a smaller consumption of fuel. If, for

[014-084] example, the steam be shut off when only half the stroke is completed,

[014-085] there will only be half the quantity of steam used. But there will be more

[014-086] than half the power exerted; for although the pressure of the steam

[014-087] decreases after the supply entering from the boiler is shut off, yet it

[014-088] imparts, during its expansion, _some_ power, and that power, it is clear,

[014-089] is obtained without any expenditure of steam or fuel whatever.

[014-090] 179. _Q._--What will be the pressure of the steam, under such

[014-091] circumstances, at the end of the stroke?

[014-092] _A._--If the steam be shut off at half stroke, the pressure of the steam,

[014-093] reckoning the total pressure both below and above the atmosphere, will just

[014-094] be one-half of what it was at the beginning of the stroke. It is a well

[014-095] known law of pneumatics, that the pressure of elastic fluids varies

[014-096] inversely as the spaces into which they are expanded or compressed. For

[014-097] example, if a cubic foot of air of the atmospheric density be compressed

[014-098] into the compass of half a cubic foot, its elasticity will be increased

[014-099] from 15 lbs. on the square inch to 30 lbs. on the square inch; whereas, if

[014-100] its volume be enlarged to two cubic feet, its elasticity will be reduced to

[014-101] 7-1/2 lbs. on the square inch, being just half its original pressure. The

[014-102] same law holds in all other proportions, and with all other gases and

[014-103] vapors, provided their temperature remains unchanged; and if the steam

[014-104] valve of an engine be closed, when the piston has descended through

[014-105] one- fourth of the stroke, the steam within the cylinder will, at the end

[014-106] of the stroke, just exert one-fourth of its initial pressure.

[014-107] 180. _Q._--Then by computing the varying pressure at a number of stages,

[014-108] the average or mean pressure throughout the stroke may be approximately

[014-109] determined?

[014-110] [Illustration: Fig. 32. Diagram showing law of expansion of steam in a

[014-111] cylinder.]

[014-112] _A._--Precisely so. Thus in the accompanying figure, (fig. 32), let E be a

[014-113] cylinder, J the piston, _a_ the steam pipe, _c_ the upper port, _f_ the

[014-114] lower port, _d_ the steam pipe, prolonged to _e_ the equilibrium valve, _g_

[014-115] the eduction valve, M the steam jacket, N the cylinder cover, O stuffing

[014-116] box, _n_ piston rod, P cylinder bottom; let the cylinder be supposed to be

[014-117] divided in the direction of its length into any number of equal parts, say

[014-118] twenty, and let the diameter of the cylinder represent the pressure of the

[014-119] steam, which, for the sake of simplicity, we may take at 10 lbs., so that

[014-120] we may divide the cylinder, in the direction of its diameter, into ten

[014-121] equal parts. If now the piston be supposed to descend through five of the

[014-122] divisions, and the steam valve then be shut, the pressure at each

[014-123] subsequent position of the piston will be represented by a series, computed

[014-124] according to the laws of pneumatics, and which, if the initial pressure be

[014-125] represented by 1, will give a pressure of .5 at the middle of the stroke,

[014-126] and .25 at the end of it.

[014-127] If this series be set off on the horizontal lines, it will mark out a

[014-128] hyperbolic curve--the area of the part exterior to which represents the

[014-129] total efficacy of the stroke, and the interior area, therefore, represents

[014-130] the diminution in the power of a stroke, when the steam is cut off at

[014-131] one- fourth of the descent. If the squares above the point, where the steam

[014-132] is cut off, be counted, they will be found to amount to 50; and if those

[014-133] beneath that point be counted or estimated, they will be found to amount to

[014-134] about 69. These squares are representative of the power exerted; so that

[014-135] while an amount of power represented by 50 has been obtained by the

[014-136] expenditure of a quarter of a cylinder full of steam, we get an amount of

[014-137] power represented by 69, without any expenditure of steam at all, merely by

[014-138] permitting the steam first used to expand into four times its original

[014-139] volume.

[014-140] 181. _Q._--Then by working an engine expansively, the power of the steam is

[014-141] increased, but the power of the engine is diminished?

[014-142] _A._--Yes. The efficacy of a given quantity of steam is more than doubled

[014-143] by expanding the steam four times, while the efficacy of each stroke is

[014-144] made nearly one-half less. And, therefore, to carry out the expansive

[014-145] principle in practice, the cylinder requires to be larger than usual, or

[014-146] the piston faster than usual, in the proportion in which the expansion is

[014-147] carried out. Every one who is acquainted with simple arithmetic, can

[014-148] compute the terminal pressure of steam in a cylinder, when he knows the

[014-149] initial pressure and the point at which the steam is cut off; and he can

[014-150] also find, by the same process, any pressure intermediate between the first

[014-151] and the last. By setting down these pressures in a table, and taking their

[014-152] mean, he can determine the effect, with tolerable accuracy, of any

[014-153] particular measure of expansion. It is necessary to remark, that it is the

[014-154] total pressure of the steam that he must take; not the pressure above the

[014-155] atmosphere, but the pressure above a perfect vacuum.

[014-156] 182. _Q._--Can you give any rule for ascertaining at one operation the

[014-157] amount of benefit derivable from expansion?

[014-158] _A._--Divide the length of stroke through which the steam expands, by the

[014-159] length of stroke performed with full pressure, which last call 1; the

[014-160] hyperbolic logarithm of the quotient is the increase of efficiency due to

[014-161] expansion. According to this rule it will be found, that if a given

[014-162] quantity of steam, the power of which working at full pressure is

[014-163] represented by 1, be admitted into a cylinder of such a size that its

[014-164] ingress is concluded when one-half the stroke has been performed, its

[014-165] efficacy will be raised by expansion to 1.69; if the admission of the steam

[014-166] be stopped at one-third of the stroke, the efficacy will be 2.10; at

[014-167] one- fourth, 2.39; at one-fifth, 2.61; at one-sixth, 2.79; at one-seventh,

[014-168] 2.95; at one-eighth, 3.08. The expansion, however, cannot be carried

[014-169] beneficially so far as one-eighth, unless the pressure of the steam in the

[014-170] boiler be very considerable, on account of the inconvenient size of

[014-171] cylinder or speed of piston which would require to be adopted, the

[014-172] friction of the engine, and the resistance of vapor in the condenser, which

[014-173] all become relatively greater with a smaller urging force.
