# D. K. Clark (d. 1878)

Daniel Kinnear Clark was a British [[locomotive|railway engine]]er and technical author whose *Treatise on Railway Machinery* became a standard reference for locomotive designers. He also invented a patent steam and fire [[throttle-valve|regulator]] for [[boiler]] draft control.

## Career and Contributions

Clark's most enduring contribution was his *Treatise on Railway Machinery*, a comprehensive work covering locomotive design, train resistance, and boiler construction. [[john-bourne|Bourne]] references Clark's work in the *Catechism* when discussing train resistance: "discussed by Mr. Clark, in his Treatise on railway machinery, who gives the following rule for ascertaining the resistance of a train." (043-079) This empirical rule was valuable to [[locomotive|railway engine]]ers calculating the power required for given loads and gradients.

In addition to his theoretical contributions, Clark was a practical inventor. He developed "Clark's Patent [[steam|Steam]] and Fire Regulator" (058-103), a device for controlling the draft of the [[chimney]] by regulating the admission of air above the fire. This [[throttle-valve|regulator]] improved combustion efficiency and allowed finer control of steam generation than the simple [[damper]] arrangements previously used. The device was sufficiently significant to be indexed as "Clark's patent steam fire [[throttle-valve|regulator]]" (068-245) and cross-referenced under "Regulator, Clark's, patent steam and fire" in the Catechism's index.

## Legacy

Clark's treatise bridged the gap between practical [[locomotive|locomotive engine]]ering and systematic technical documentation. His train resistance formulas and [[throttle-valve|regulator]] design represented the maturation of [[locomotive|railway engine]]ering from empirical craft to analytical science in the mid-nineteenth century.
