[018-001] [1] Tables of the horse power of both high and low pressure

[018-002] engines are given in the Key.

[018-003] [2] Example.--What is the power of an engine of 42 inches

[018-004] diameter, 3-1/2 feet stroke, and making 85 strokes per minute? The

[018-005] speed of the piston will be 7 (the length of a double stroke) x 85 =

[018-006] 595 feet per minute. Now 42 x 42 = 1,764 x 595 = 1,049,580 / 6,000 =

[018-007] 175 horses power.

[018-008] DUTY OF ENGINES AND BOILERS.

[018-009] 226. _Q._--What is meant by the duty of a engine?

[018-010] _A._--The work done in relation to the fuel consumed.

[018-011] 227. _Q._--And how is the duty ascertained?

[018-012] _A._--In ordinary mill or marine engines it can only be ascertained by the

[018-013] indicator, as the load upon such engines is variable, and cannot readily be

[018-014] determined; but in the case of engines pumping water, where the load is

[018-015] constant, the number of strokes performed by the engine will represent the

[018-016] work done, and the amount of work done by a given quantity of coal

[018-017] represents the duty. In Cornwall the duty of an engine is expressed by the

[018-018] number of millions of pounds raised one foot high by a bushel, or 94 lbs.

[018-019] of Welsh coal. A bushel of Newcastle coal will only weigh 84 Lbs.; and in

[018-020] comparing the duty of a Cornish engine with the performance of an engine in

[018-021] some locality where a different kind of coal is used, it is necessary to

[018-022] pay regard to such variations.

[018-023] 228. _Q._--Can you tell the duty of an engine when you know its consumption

[018-024] of coal per horse power per hour?

[018-025] _A._--Yes, if the power given be the actual, and not the nominal, power.

[018-026] Divide 166.32 by the number of pounds of coal consumed per actual horse

[018-027] power per hour; the quotient is the duty in millions of pounds. If you

[018-028] already have the duty in millions of pounds, and wish to know the

[018-029] equivalent consumption in pounds per actual horse power per hour, divide

[018-030] 166.32 by the duty in millions of pounds; the quotient is the consumption

[018-031] per actual horse power per hour. The duty of a locomotive engine is

[018-032] expressed by the weight of coke it consumes in transporting a ton through

[018-033] the distance of one mile upon a railway; but this is a very imperfect

[018-034] method of representing the duty, as the tractive efficacy of a pound of

[018-035] coke becomes less as the speed of the locomotive becomes greater; and the

[018-036] law of variation is not accurately known.

[018-037] 229. _Q._--What amount of power is generated in good engines of the

[018-038] ordinary kind by a given weight of coal?

[018-039] _A._--The duty of different kinds of engines varies very much, and there

[018-040] are also great differences in the performance of different engines of the

[018-041] same class. In ordinary rotative condensing engines of good construction,

[018-042] 10 lbs. of coal per nominal horse power per hour is a common consumption;

[018-043] but such engines exert nearly twice their nominal power, so that the

[018-044] consumption per actual horse power per hour may be taken at from 5 to 6

[018-045] lbs. Engines working very expansively, however, attain an economy much

[018-046] superior to this. The average duty of the pumping engines in Cornwall is

[018-047] about 60,000,000 lbs. raised 1 ft. high by a bushel of Welsh coals, which

[018-048] weighs 94 lbs. This is equivalent to a consumption of 3.1 lbs. of coal per

[018-049] actual horse power per hour; but some engines reach a duty of above

[018-050] 100,000,000, or 1.74 lbs. of coal per actual horse power per hour.

[018-051] Locomotives consume from 8 to 10 lbs. of coke in evaporating a cubic foot

[018-052] of water, and the evaporation of a cubic foot of water per hour may be set

[018-053] down as representing an actual horse power in locomotives as well as in

[018-054] condensing engines, if expansion be not employed. When the locomotive is

[018-055] worked expansively, however, there is of course a less consumption of water

[018-056] and fuel per horse power, or per ton per mile, than when the full pressure

[018-057] is used throughout the stroke; and most locomotives now operate with as

[018-058] much expansion as can be conveniently given by the slide valves.

[018-059] 230. _Q._--But is not the evaporative power of locomotives affected

[018-060] materially by the proportions of the boiler?

[018-061] _A._--Yes, but this may be said of all boilers; but in locomotive boilers,

[018-062] perhaps, the effect of any misproportion becomes more speedily manifest. A

[018-063] high temperature of the fire box is found to be conducive to economy of

[018-064] fuel; and this condition, in its turn, involves a small area of grate bars.

[018-065] The heating surface of locomotive boilers should be about 80 square feet

[018-066] for each square foot of grate bars, and upon each foot of grate bars about

[018-067] 1 cwt. of coke should be burnt in the hour.

[018-068] 231. _Q._--Probably the heat is more rapidly absorbed when the temperature

[018-069] of the furnace is high?

[018-070] _A._--That seems to be the explanation. The rapidity with which a hot body

[018-071] imparts heat to a colder, varies as the square of the difference of

[018-072] temperature; so that if the temperature of the furnace be very high, the

[018-073] larger part of the heat passes into the water at the furnace, thereby

[018-074] leaving little to be transmitted by the tubes. If, on the contrary, the

[018-075] temperature of the furnace be low, a large part of the heat will pass into

[018-076] the tubes, and more tube surface will be required to absorb it. About 16

[018-077] cubic feet of water should be evaporated by a locomotive boiler for each,

[018-078] square foot of fire grate, which, with the proportion of heating surface

[018-079] already mentioned, leaves 5 square feet of heating surface to evaporate a

[018-080] cubic foot of water in the hour. This is only about half the amount of

[018-081] surface usual in land and marine boilers per cubic foot evaporated, and its

[018-082] small amount is due altogether to the high temperature of the furnace,

[018-083] which, by the rapidity of transmission it causes, is tantamount to an

[018-084] additional amount of heating surface.

[018-085] 232. _Q._--You have stated that the steam and vacuum gauges are generally

[018-086] glass tubes, up which mercury is forced by the steam or sucked by the

[018-087] vacuum?

[018-088] _A._--Vacuum gauges are very often of this construction, but steam

[018-089] gauges more frequently consist of a small iron tube, bent like the letter

[018-090] U, and into which mercury is poured. The one end of this tube communicates

[018-091] with the boiler, and the other end with the atmosphere; and when the

[018-092] pressure of the steam rises in the boiler, the mercury is forced down in

[018-093] the leg communicating with the boiler and rises in the other leg, and the

[018-094] difference of level in the legs denotes the pressure of the steam. In this

[018-095] gauge a rise of the mercury one inch in the one leg involves a difference

[018-096] of the level between the two legs of two inches, and an inch of rise is,

[018-097] therefore, equivalent to two inches of mercury, or a pound of pressure. A

[018-098] small float of wood is placed in the open leg to show the rise or fall of

[018-099] the mercury, and this leg is surmounted by a brass scale, graduated in

[018-100] inches, to the marks of which the float points.

[018-101] 233. _Q._--What other kinds of steam and vacuum gauges are there?

[018-102] _A._--There are many other kinds; but probably Bourdon's gauges are now in

[018-103] more extended use than, any other, and their operation has been found to be

[018-104] satisfactory in practice. The principle of their action may be explained to

[018-105] be, that a thin elliptical metal tube, if bent into a ring, will seek to

[018-106] coil or uncoil itself if subjected to external or internal pressure, and to

[018-107] an extent proportional to the pressure applied. The end of the tube is

[018-108] sharpened into an index, and moves to an extent corresponding to the

[018-109] pressure applied to the tube; but in the more recent forms of this

[018-110] apparatus, a dial and a hand, like those of a clock, are employed, and the

[018-111] hand is moved round by a toothed sector connected to the tube, and which

[018-112] sector acts on a pinion attached to the hand. Mr. Shank, of Paisley, has

[018-113] lately introduced a form of steam gauge like a thermometer, with a

[018-114] flattened bulb; and the pressure of the steam, by compressing the bulb,

[018-115] causes the mercury to rise to a point proportional to the pressure applied.

[018-116] THE INDICATOR.

[018-117] 234. _Q._--You have already stated that the actual power of an engine is

[018-118] ascertained by an instrument called the indicator, which consists of a

[018-119] small cylinder with a piston moving against a spring, and compressing it to

[018-120] an extent answerable to the pressure of the steam. Will you explain further

[018-121] the structure and mode of using that instrument?

[018-122] [Illustration: Fig. 36]

[018-123] _A._--The structure of the common form of indicator will be most readily

[018-124] apprehended by a reference to fig. 36, which is a McNaught's indicator.

[018-125] Upon a movable barrel A, a piece of paper is wound, the ends of which are

[018-126] secured by the slight brass clamps shown in the drawing. The barrel is

[018-127] supported by the bracket _b_, proceeding from the body of the indicator,

[018-128] and at the bottom of the barrel a watch spring is coiled with one end

[018-129] attached to the barrel and the other end to the bracket, so that when the

[018-130] barrel is drawn round by a string wound upon its lower end like a roller

[018-131] blind, the spring returns the barrel to its original position, when the

[018-132] string is relaxed. The string is attached to some suitable part of the

[018-133] engine, and at every stroke the string is drawn out, turning round the

[018-134] barrel, and the barrel is returned again by the spring on the return

[018-135] stroke.

[018-136] 235. _Q_--But in what way can these reciprocations of the barrel determine

[018-137] the power of the engine?

[018-138] _A._--They do not determine it of themselves, but are only part of the

[018-139] operation. In the inside of the cylinder _c_ there is a small piston moving

[018-140] steam tight in a cylinder of which _d_ is the piston rod, and _e_ a spiral

[018-141] spring of steel, which the piston, when forced upwards by the steam or

[018-142] sucked downwards by the vacuum, either compresses or extends; _f_ is a cock

[018-143] attached to the cylinder of the indicator, and which is screwed into the

[018-144] cylinder cover. It is obvious that, so soon as this cock is opened, the

[018-145] piston will be forced up when the space above the piston of the engine is

[018-146] opened to the boiler, and sucked down when that space is opened to the

[018-147] condenser--in each case to an extent proportionate to the pressure of the

[018-148] steam or the perfection of the vacuum, the top of the piston _c_ being open

[018-149] to the atmosphere. A pencil, _p_, with a knife hinge, is inserted into the

[018-150] piston rod, at _e_, and the point of the pencil bears upon the surface of

[018-151] the paper wound upon the drum A. If the drum A did not revolve, this pencil

[018-152] would merely trace on the paper a vertical line; but as the drum A moves

[018-153] round and back again every stroke of the engine, and as the pencil moves up

[018-154] and down again every stroke of the engine, the combined movements trace

[018-155] upon the paper a species of rectangle, which is called an indicator

[018-156] diagram; and the nature of this diagram determines the nature of the

[018-157] engine's performance.

[018-158] 236. _Q._--How does it do this?

[018-159] _A._--It is clear that if the pencil was moved up instantaneously to the

[018-160] top of its stroke, and was also moved down instantaneously to the bottom of

[018-161] its stroke, and if it remained without fluctuation while at the top and

[018-162] bottom, the figure described by the pencil would be a perfect rectangle, of

[018-163] which the vertical height would represent the total pressure of the steam

[018-164] and vacuum, and therefore the total pressure urging the piston of the

[018-165] engine. But in practice the pencil will neither rise nor fall

[018-166] instantaneously, nor will it remain at a uniform height throughout the

[018-167] stroke. If the steam be worked expansively the pressure will begin to fall

[018-168] so soon as the steam is cut off; and at the end of the stroke, when the

[018-169] steam comes to be discharged, the subsidence of pressure will not be

[018-170] instantaneous, but will occupy an appreciable time. It is clear, therefore,

[018-171] that in no engine can the diagram described by an indicator be a complete

[018-172] rectangle; but the more nearly it approaches to a rectangle, the larger

[018-173] will be the power produced at every stroke with any given pressure, and the

[018-174] area of the space included within the diagram will in every case accurately

[018-175] represent the power exerted by the engine during that stroke.
