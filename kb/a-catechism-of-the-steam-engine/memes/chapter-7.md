[007-001] 77. _Q._--Does the same law hold in the case of the deflection of malleable

[007-002] iron bars?

[007-003] _A._--In the case of malleable iron bars it has been found that no very

[007-004] perceptible damage was caused by 10,000 deflections, each deflection being

[007-005] such as was due to half the load that produced a large permanent

[007-006] deflection.

[007-007] 78. _Q._--The power of a rod or pillar to resist compression becomes very

[007-008] little when the diameter is small and the length great?

[007-009] _A._--The power of a rod or pillar to resist compression, varies nearly as

[007-010] the fourth power of the diameter divided by the square of the length. In

[007-011] the case of hollow cylindrical columns of cast iron, it has been found,

[007-012] experimentally, that the 3.55th power of the internal diameter, subtracted

[007-013] from the 3.55th power of the external diameter, and divided by the 1.7th

[007-014] power of the length, will represent the strength very nearly. In the case

[007-015] of hollow cylindrical columns of malleable iron, experiment shows that the

[007-016] 3.59th power of the internal diameter, subtracted from the 3.59th power of

[007-017] the external diameter, and divided by the square of the length, gives a

[007-018] proper expression for the strength; but this rule only holds where the

[007-019] strain does not exceed 8 or 9 tons on the square inch of section. Beyond 12

[007-020] or 13 tons per square inch of section, the metal cannot be depended upon to

[007-021] withstand the strain, though hollow pillars will sometimes bear 15 or 16

[007-022] tons per square inch of section.

[007-023] 79. _Q._--Does not the thickness of the metal of the pillars or tubes

[007-024] affect the question?

[007-025] _A._--It manifestly does; for a tube of very thin metal, such as gold leaf

[007-026] or tin foil, would not stand on end at all, being crushed down by its own

[007-027] weight. It is found, experimentally, that in malleable iron tubes of the

[007-028] respective thicknesses of .525, .272, and .124 inches, the resistances per

[007-029] square inch of section are 19.17, 14.47, and 7.47 tons respectively. The

[007-030] power of plates to resist compression varies nearly as the cube, or more

[007-031] nearly as the 2.878th power of their thickness; but this law only holds so

[007-032] long as the pressure applied does not exceed from 9 to 12 tons per square

[007-033] inch of section. When the pressure is greater than this the metal is

[007-034] crushed, and a new law supervenes, according to which it is necessary to

[007-035] employ plates of twice or three times the thickness, to obtain twice the

[007-036] resisting power.

[007-037] 80. _Q._--In a riveted tube, will the riveting be much, damaged by heavy

[007-038] strains?

[007-039] _A._--It will be most affected by percussion. Long-continued impact on the

[007-040] side of a tube, producing a deflection of only one fifth of that which

[007-041] would be required to injure it by pressure, is found to be destructive of

[007-042] the riveting; but in large riveted structures, such as a ship or a railway

[007-043] bridge, the inertia of the mass will, by resisting the effect of impact,

[007-044] prevent any injurious action from this cause from taking place.

[007-045] 81. _Q._--Will the power of iron to resist shocks be in all cases

[007-046] proportional to its power to resist strains?

[007-047] _A._--By no means. Some cast iron is very hard and brittle; and although it

[007-048] will in this state resist compression very strongly, it, will be easily

[007-049] broken by a blow. Iron which has been remelted many times generally falls

[007-050] into this category, as it will also do if run into very small castings. It

[007-051] has been found, by experiment, that iron of which the crushing weight per

[007-052] square inch is about 42 tons, will, if remelted twelve times, bear a

[007-053] crushing weight of 70 tons, and if remelted eighteen times it will bear a

[007-054] crushing weight of 83 tons; but taking its power to resist impact in its

[007-055] first state at 706, this power will be raised at the twelfth remelting to

[007-056] 1153, and will be sunk at the eighteenth remelting to 149.

[007-057] 82. _Q._--From all this it appears that a combination of cast iron and

[007-058] malleable iron is the best for the beams of engines?

[007-059] _A._--Yes, and for all beams. Engine beams should be made deeper at the

[007-060] middle than they are now made; the web should be lightened by holes pierced

[007-061] in it, and round the edge of the beam there should be a malleable iron hoop

[007-062] or strap securely attached to the flanges by riveting or otherwise. The

[007-063] flanges at the edges of engine beams are invariably made too small. It is

[007-064] in them that the strength of the beam chiefly resides.

[007-068] THE BOILER.

[007-069] 83. _Q._--What are the chief varieties of the steam engine in actual

[007-070] practical use?

[007-071] _A._--There is first the single-acting engine, which is used for pumping

[007-072] water; the rotative land engine, which is employed to drive mills and

[007-073] manufactories; the rotative marine engine, which is used to propel steam

[007-074] vessels; and the locomotive engine, which is employed on railways. The last

[007-075] is always a high-pressure engine; the others are, for the most part,

[007-076] condensing engines.

[007-077] 84. _Q._--Will you explain the construction and action of the single-acting

[007-078] engine, used for draining mines?

[007-079] _A._--Permit me then to begin with the boiler, which is common and

[007-080] necessary to all engines; and I will take the example of a wagon boiler,

[007-081] such as was employed by Boulton and Watt universally in their early

[007-082] engines, and which is still in extensive use. This boiler is a long

[007-083] rectangular vessel, with a rounded top, like that of a carrier's wagon,

[007-084] from its resemblance to which it derives its name. A fire is set beneath

[007-085] it, and flues constructed of brickwork encircle it, so as to keep the flame

[007-086] and smoke in contact with the boiler for a sufficient time to absorb the

[007-087] heat.

[007-089] 85. _Q._--This species of boiler has not an internal furnace, but is set in

[007-090] brickwork, in which the furnace is formed?

[007-091] _A._--Precisely so. The general arrangement and configuration will be at

[007-092] once understood by a reference to the annexed figure (fig. 3), which is a

[007-093] transverse section of a wagon boiler. The line b represents the top of the

[007-094] grate or fire bars, which slope downward from the front at an angle of

[007-095] about 25°, giving the fuel a tendency to move toward the back of the grate.

[007-096] The supply of air ascends from the ash pit through the grate bars, and the

[007-097] flame passes over a low wall or bridge, and traverses the bottom of the

[007-098] boiler. The smoke rises up at the back of the boiler, and proceeds through

[007-099] the flue F along one side to the front, and returns along the other side of

[007-100] the boiler, and then ascends the chimney. The performance of this course by

[007-101] the smoke is what is termed a wheel draught, as the smoke wheels once round

[007-102] the boiler, and then ascends the chimney.

[007-103] 86. _Q._--Is the performance of this course by the smoke universal in wagon

[007-104] boilers?

[007-105] _A._--No; such boilers sometimes have what is termed a split draught. The

[007-106] smoke and flame, when they reach the end of the boiler, pass in this case

[007-107] through an iron flue or tube, reaching from end to end of the boiler; and

[007-108] on arriving at the front of the boiler, the smoke splits or separates--one

[007-109] half passing through a flue on the one side of the boiler, and the other

[007-110] half passing through a flue on the other side of the boiler--both of these

[007-111] flues having their debouch in the chimney.

[007-112] 87. _Q._--What are the appliances usually connected with a wagon boiler?

[007-113] _A._--On the top of the boiler, near the front, is a short cylinder, with a

[007-114] lid secured by bolts. This is the manhole door, the purpose of which is to

[007-115] enable a man to get into the inside of the boiler when necessary for

[007-116] inspection and repair. On the top of this door is a small valve opening

[007-117] downward, called the atmospheric valve. The intention of this valve is to

[007-118] prevent a vacuum from being formed accidentally in the boiler, which might

[007-119] collapse it; for if the pressure in the boiler subsides to a point

[007-120] materially below the pressure of the atmosphere, the valve will open and

[007-121] allow air to get in. A bent pipe, which rises up from the top of the

[007-122] boiler, immediately behind the position of the manhole, is the steam pipe

[007-123] for conducting the steam to the engine; and a bent pipe which ascends from

[007-124] the top of the boiler, at the back end, is the waste-steam pipe for

[007-125] conducting away the steam, which escapes through the safety valve. This

[007-126] valve is set in a chest, standing on the top of the boiler, at the foot of

[007-127] the waste-steam pipe, and it is loaded with iron or leaden weights to a

[007-128] point answerable to the intended pressure of the steam.

[007-129] 88. _Q._--How is the proper level of the water in the boiler maintained?

[007-130] _A._--By means of a balanced buoy or float. This float is attached to a

[007-131] rod, which in its turn is attached to a lever set on the top of a large

[007-132] upright pipe. The upper part of the pipe is widened out into a small

[007-133] cistern, through a short pipe in the middle of which a chain passes to the

[007-134] damper; but any water emptied into this small cistern cannot pass into the

[007-135] pipe, except through a small valve fixed to the lever to which the rod is

[007-136] attached. The water for replenishing the boiler is pumped into the small

[007-137] cistern on the top of the pipe; and it follows from these arrangements that

[007-138] when the buoy falls, the rod opens the small valve and allows the feed

[007-139] water to enter the pipe, which communicates with the water in the boiler;

[007-140] whereas, when the buoy rises, the feed cannot enter the pipe, and it has,

[007-141] therefore, to run to waste through an overflow pipe provided for the

[007-142] purpose.

[007-143] 89. _Q._--How is the strength of the fire regulated?

[007-144] _A._--The draught through the furnaces of land boilers is regulated by a

[007-145] plate of metal or a damper, as it is called, which slides like a sluice up

[007-146] and down in the flue, and this damper is closed more or less when the

[007-147] intensity of the fire has to be moderated. In wagon boilers this is

[007-148] generally accomplished by self-acting mechanism. In the small cistern pipe,

[007-149] which is called a stand pipe, the water rises up to a height proportional

[007-150] to the pressure of the steam, and the surface of the water in this pipe

[007-151] will rise or fall with the fluctuations in the pressure of the steam. In

[007-152] this pipe a float is placed, which communicates by means of a chain with

[007-153] the damper. If the pressure of the steam rises, the float will be raised

[007-154] and the damper closed, whereas, if the pressure in the boiler falls, the

[007-155] reverse of this action will take place.

[007-158] 90. _Q._--Are all land boilers of the same construction as that which you

[007-159] have just described?

[007-160] _A._--No; many land boilers are now made of a cylindrical form, with one or

[007-161] two internal flues in which the furnace is placed. A boiler of this kind is

[007-162] represented in Figs. 4 and 5, and which is the species of boiler

[007-163] principally used in Cornwall. In this boiler a large internal cylinder or

[007-164] flue runs from end to end. In the fore part of this cylinder the furnace is

[007-165] placed, and behind the furnace a large tube filled with water extends to

[007-166] the end of the boiler. This internal tube is connected to the bottom part

[007-167] of the boiler by a copper pipe standing vertically immediately behind the

[007-168] furnace bridge, and to the top part of the boiler by a bent copper pipe

[007-169] which stands in a vertical position near the end of the boiler. The smoke,

[007-170] after passing through the central flue, circulates round the sides and

[007-171] beneath the bottom of the boiler before its final escape into the chimney.

[007-172] The boiler is carefully covered over to prevent the dispersion of the heat.
