[021-001] 263. _Q._--Then the chief difference in the proportions established by

[021-002] Boulton and Watt, and those followed by the other manufacturers you have

[021-003] mentioned is, that Boulton and Watt set a more powerful boiler to do the

[021-004] same work?

[021-005] _A._--That is the main difference. The proportion which one part of the

[021-006] boiler bears to another part is very similar in the cases cited, but the

[021-007] proportion of boiler relatively to the size of the engine varies very

[021-008] materially. Thus the calorimeter _of each boiler_ of the Dee and Solway is

[021-009] 1296 square inches; of the Eagle, 1548 square inches; and of the Thames and

[021-010] Medway, 1134 square inches; and the length of flue is 57, 60, and 52 ft. in

[021-011] the boilers respectively, which makes the respective vents 22-1/2, 25, and

[021-012] 21. Taking then the boiler of the Eagle for comparison with the boiler of

[021-013] the Nile, as it has the same vent, it will be seen that the proportions of

[021-014] the two are almost identical, for 990 is to 1548 as 39 is to 60, nearly;

[021-015] but Messrs. Boulton and Watt would not have set a boiler like that of the

[021-016] Eagle to do so much work.

[021-017] 264. _Q._--Then the evaporating power of the boiler varies as the sectional

[021-018] area of the flue?

[021-019] _A._--The evaporating power varies as the square root of the area of the

[021-020] flue, if the length of the flue remain the same; but it varies as the area

[021-021] simply, if the length of the flue be increased in the same proportion as

[021-022] its other dimensions. The evaporating power of a boiler is referable to the

[021-023] amount of its heating surface, and the amount of heating surface in any

[021-024] flue or tube is proportional to the product of the length of the tube and

[021-025] the square root of its sectional area, multiplied by a certain quantity

[021-026] that is constant for each particular form. But in similar tubes the length

[021-027] is proportional to the square root of the sectional area; therefore, in

[021-028] similar tubes, the amount of heating surface is proportional to the

[021-029] sectional area. On this area also depends the quantity of hot air passing

[021-030] through the flue, supposing the intensity of the draught to remain

[021-031] unaffected, and the quantity of hot air or smoke passing through the flue

[021-032] should vary in the same ratio as the quantity of surface.

[021-033] 265. _Q._--A boiler, therefore, to exert four times the power, should have

[021-034] four times the extent of heating surface, and four times the sectional area

[021-035] of flue for the transmission of the smoke?

[021-036] _A._--Yes; and if the same form of flue is to be retained, it should be of

[021-037] twice the diameter and twice the length; or twice the height and width if

[021-038] rectangular, and twice the length. As then the diameter or square root of

[021-039] the area increases in the same ratio as the length, the square root of the

[021-040] area divided by the length ought to be a constant quantity in each type of

[021-041] boiler, in order that the same proportions of flue may be retained; and in

[021-042] wagon boilers without an internal flue, the height in inches of the flue

[021-043] encircling the boiler divided by the length of the flue in feet will be 1

[021-044] very nearly. Instead of the square root of the area, the effective

[021-045] perimeter, or outline of that part of the cross section of the flue which

[021-046] is effective in generating steam, may be taken; and the effective perimeter

[021-047] divided by the length ought to be a constant quantity in similar forms of

[021-048] flues and with the same velocity of draught, whatever the size of the flue

[021-049] may be.

[021-050] 266. _Q._--Will this proportion alter if the form of the flue be changed?

[021-051] _A._--It is clear, that with any given area of flue, to increase the

[021-052] perimeter by adopting a different shape is tantamount to a diminution of

[021-053] the length of the flue; and, if the perimeter be diminished, the length of

[021-054] the flue must at the same time be increased, else it will be impossible to

[021-055] obtain the necessary amount of heating surface. In Boulton and Watt's wagon

[021-056] boilers, the sectional area of the flue in square inches per square foot of

[021-057] heating surface is 5.4 in the two horse boiler; in the three horse it is

[021-058] 4.74; in the four horse, 4.35; six horse, 3.75; eight horse, 4.33; ten

[021-059] horse, 3.96; twelve horse, 3.63; eighteen horse, 3.17; thirty horse, 2.52;

[021-060] and in the forty-five horse boiler, 2.05 square inches. Taking the amount

[021-061] of heating surface in the 45 horse boiler at 9 square feet per horse power,

[021-062] we obtain 18 square inches of sectional area of flue per horse power, which

[021-063] is also Boulton and Watt's proportion of sectional area for marine boilers

[021-064] with internal flues.

[021-065] 267. _Q._--If to increase the perimeter of a flue is virtually to diminish

[021-066] the length, then a tubular boiler where the perimeter is in effect greatly

[021-067] extended ought to have but a short length of tube?

[021-068] _A._--The flue of the Nile steamer if reduced to the cylindrical form would

[021-069] be 35-1/2 inches in diameter to have the same area; but it would then

[021-070] require to be made 47-3/4 feet long, to have the same amount of heating

[021-071] surface, excluding the bottom as non-effective. Supposing that with these

[021-072] proportions the heat is sufficiently extracted from the smoke, then every

[021-073] tube of a tubular boiler in which the same draught existed ought to have

[021-074] very nearly the same proportions.

[021-075] 268. _Q._--But what are the best proportions of the parts of tubular

[021-076] boilers relatively with one another?

[021-077] _A._--The proper relative proportions of the parts of tubular boilers may

[021-078] easily be ascertained by a reference to the settled proportions of flue

[021-079] boilers; for the same general principles are operative in both cases. In

[021-080] the Nile steamer each boiler of 55 horse power has about 497 square feet of

[021-081] flue surface or 9 square feet per horse power, reckoning the total surface

[021-082] as effective. The area of the flue, which is rectangular is 990 square

[021-083] inches, therefore the area is equal to that of a tube 35-1/2 inches in

[021-084] diameter; and such a tube, to have a heating surface of 497 square feet,

[021-085] must be 53.4 feet or 640.8 inches in length. The length, therefore, of the

[021-086] tube, will be about 18 times its diameter, and with the same velocity of

[021-087] draught these proportions must obtain, whatever the absolute dimensions of

[021-088] the tube may be. With a calorimeter, therefore, of 18 square inches per

[021-089] horse power, the length of a tube 3 inches diameter must not exceed 4 feet

[021-090] 6 inches, since the heat will be sufficiently extracted from the smoke in

[021-091] this length, if the smoke only travels at the velocity due to a calorimeter

[021-092] of 18 square inches per horse power.

[021-093] 269. _Q._--Is this, then, the maximum length of flue which can be used in

[021-094] tubular boilers with advantage?

[021-095] _A._--By no means. The tubes of tubular boilers are almost always more than

[021-096] 4 feet 6 inches long, but then the calorimeter is almost always less than

[021-097] 18 square inches per horse power--generally about two thirds of this.

[021-098] Indeed, tubular boilers with a large calorimeter are not found to be so

[021-099] satisfactory as where the calorimeter is small, partly from the propensity

[021-100] of the smoke in such cases to pass through a few of the tubes instead of

[021-101] the whole of them, and partly from the deposit of soot which takes place

[021-102] when the draught is sluggish. It is a very confusing practice, however, to

[021-103] speak of nominal horse power in connection with boilers, since that is a

[021-104] quantity quite indeterminate.

[021-105] EVAPORATIVE POWER OF BOILERS.

[021-106] 270. _Q._--The main thing after all in boilers is their evaporative powers?

[021-107] _A._--The proportions of tubular boilers, as of all boilers, should

[021-108] obviously have reference to the evaporation required, whereas the demand

[021-109] upon the boiler for steam is very often reckoned contingent upon the

[021-110] nominal horse power of the engine; and as the nominal power of an engine is

[021-111] a conventional quantity by no means in uniform proportion to the actual

[021-112] quantity of steam consumed, perplexing complications as to the proper

[021-113] proportions of boilers have in consequence sprung up, to which most of the

[021-114] failures in that department of engineering may be imputed. It is highly

[021-115] expedient, therefore, in planning boilers for any particular engine, to

[021-116] consider exclusively the actual power required to be produced, and to

[021-117] apportion the capabilities of the boiler accordingly.

[021-118] 271. _Q._--In other words you would recommend the inquiry to be restricted

[021-119] to the mode of evaporating a given number of cubic feet of water in the

[021-120] hour, instead of embracing the problem how an engine of a given nominal

[021-121] power was to be supplied with steam?

[021-122] _A._--I would first, as I have already stated, consider the actual power

[021-123] required to be produced, and then fix the amount of expansion to be

[021-124] adopted. If the engine had to work up to three times its nominal power, as

[021-125] is now common in marine engines, I should either increase correspondingly

[021-126] the quantity of evaporating surface in the boiler, or adopt such an amount

[021-127] of expansion as would increase threefold the efficacy of the steam, or

[021-128] combine in a modified manner both of these arrangements. Reckoning the

[021-129] evaporation of a cubic foot of water in the hour as equivalent to an actual

[021-130] horse power, and allowing a square yard or 9 square feet as the proper

[021-131] proportion of flue surface to evaporate a cubic foot of water in the hour,

[021-132] it is clear that I must either give 27 square feet of heating surface in

[021-133] the boiler to have a trebled power without expansion, or I must cut off the

[021-134] steam at one seventh of the stroke to obtain a three-fold power without

[021-135] increasing the quantity of heating surface. By cutting off the steam,

[021-136] however, at one third of the stroke, a heating surface of 13-1/2 square

[021-137] feet will give a threefold power, and it will usually be the most judicious

[021-138] course to carry the expansion as far as possible, and then to add the

[021-139] proportion of heating surface necessary to make good the deficiency still

[021-140] found to exist.

[021-141] 272. _Q._--But is it certain that a cubic foot of water evaporated in the

[021-142] hour is equivalent to an actual horse power?

[021-143] _A._--An actual horse power as fixed by Watt is 33,000 lbs. raised one foot

[021-144] high in the minute; and in Watt's 40 horse power engine, with a 31-1/2 inch

[021-145] cylinder, 7 feet stroke, and making 17-1/2 strokes a minute, the effective

[021-146] pressure is 6.92 lbs. on the square inch clear of all deductions. Now, as a

[021-147] horse power is 33,000 lbs. raised one foot high, and as there are 6.92 lbs,

[021-148] on the square inch, it is clear that 33,000 divided by 6.92, on 4768 square

[021-149] inches with 6.92 lbs. on each if lifted 1 foot or 12 inches high, will also

[021-150] be equal to a horse power. But 4768 square inches multiplied by 12 inches

[021-151] in height is 57224.4 cubic inches, or 33.1 cubic feet, and this is the

[021-152] quantity of steam which must be expended per minute to produce an actual

[021-153] horse power.

[021-154] 273. _Q._--But are 33 cubic feet of steam expended per minute equivalent to

[021-155] a cubic foot of water expended in the hour?

[021-156] _A._.--Not precisely, but nearly so. A cubic foot of water produces 1669

[021-157] cubic feet of steam of the atmospheric density of 15 lbs. per square inch,

[021-158] whereas a consumption of 33 cubic feet of steam in the minute is 1980 cubic

[021-159] feet in the hour. In Watt's engines about one tenth was reckoned as loss in

[021-160] filling the waste spaces at the top and bottom of the cylinder, making 1872

[021-161] cubic feet as the quantity consumed per hour without this waste; and in

[021-162] modern engines the waste at the ends of the cylinder is inconsiderable.
