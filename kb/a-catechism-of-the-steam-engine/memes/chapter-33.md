[033-001] 400. _Q._--What is the cause of the rapid corrosion of marine boilers?

[033-002] _A._--Marine boilers are corroded externally in the region of the steam

[033-003] chest by the dripping of water from the deck; the bottom of the boiler is

[033-004] corroded by the action of the bilge water, and the ash pits by the practice

[033-005] of quenching the ashes with, salt water. These sources of injury, however,

[033-006] admit of easy remedy; the top of the boiler may be preserved from external

[033-007] corrosion by covering it with felt upon which is laid sheet lead soldered

[033-008] at every joint so as to be impenetrable to water; the ash pits may be

[033-009] shielded by guard plates which are plates fitting into the ash pits and

[033-010] attached to the boiler by a few bolts, so that when worn they may be

[033-011] removed and new ones substituted, whereby any wear upon the boiler in that

[033-012] part will be prevented; and there will be very little wear upon the bottom

[033-013] of a boiler if it be imbedded in mastic cement laid upon a suitable

[033-014] platform.

[033-015] 401. _Q._--Are not marine boilers subject to internal corrosion?

[033-016] _A._--Yes; the greatest part of the corrosion of a boiler takes place in

[033-017] the inside of the steam chest, and the origin of this corrosion is one of

[033-018] the obscurest subjects in the whole range of engineering. It cannot be from

[033-019] the chemical action of the salt water upon the iron, for the flues and

[033-020] other parts of the boiler beneath the water suffer very little from

[033-021] corrosion, and in steam vessels provided with Hall's condensers, which

[033-022] supply the boiler with fresh water, not much increased durability of the

[033-023] boiler has been experienced. Nevertheless, marine boilers seldom last more

[033-024] than for 5 or 6 years, whereas land boilers made of the same quality of

[033-025] iron often last 18 or 20 years, and it does not appear probable that land

[033-026] boilers would last a very much shorter time if salt water were used in

[033-027] them. The thin film of scale spread over the parts of a marine boiler

[033-028] situated beneath the water, effectually protect them from corrosion; and

[033-029] when the other parts are completely worn out the flues generally remain so

[033-030] perfect, that the hammer marks upon them are as conspicuous as at their

[033-031] first formation. The operation of the steam in corroding the interior of

[033-032] the boiler is most capricious--the parts which are most rapidly worn away

[033-033] in one boiler being untouched in another; and in some cases one side of a

[033-034] steam chest will be very much wasted away while the opposite side remains

[033-035] uninjured. Sometimes the iron exfoliates in the shape of a black oxide

[033-036] which comes away in flakes like the leaves of a book, while in other cases

[033-037] the iron appears as if eaten away by a strong acid which had a solvent

[033-038] action upon it. The application of felt to the outside of a boiler, has in

[033-039] several cases been found to accelerate sensibly its internal corrosion;

[033-040] boilers in which there is a large accumulation of scale appear to be more

[033-041] corroded than where there is no such deposit; and where the funnel passes

[033-042] through the steam chest the iron of the steam chest is invariably much more

[033-043] corroded than where the funnel does not pass through it.

[033-044] 402. _Q._--Can you suggest no reason for the rapid internal corrosion of

[033-045] marine boilers?

[033-046] _A._--The facts which I have enumerated appear to indicate that the

[033-047] internal corrosion of marine boilers is attributable chiefly to the

[033-048] existence of surcharged steam within them, which is steam to which an

[033-049] additional quantity of heat has been communicated subsequently to its

[033-050] generation, so that its temperature is greater than is due to its elastic

[033-051] force; and on this hypothesis the observed facts relative to corrosion

[033-052] become to some extent explicable. Felt, applied to the outside of a boiler,

[033-053] may accelerate its internal corrosion by keeping the steam in a surcharged

[033-054] state, when by the dispersion of a part of the heat it would cease to be in

[033-055] that state; boilers in which there is a large accumulation of scale must

[033-056] have worked with the water very salt, which necessarily produces surcharged

[033-057] steam; for the temperature of steam cannot be less than that of the water

[033-058] from which it is generated, and inasmuch as the boiling point of water,

[033-059] under any given pressure, rises with the saltness of the water, the

[033-060] temperature of the steam must rise with the saltness of the water, the

[033-061] pressure remaining the same; or, in other words, the steam must have a

[033-062] higher temperature than is due to its elastic force, or be in the state of

[033-063] surcharged steam. The circumstance of the chimney flue passing through the

[033-064] steam will manifestly surcharge the steam with heat, so that all the

[033-065] circumstances which are found to accelerate corrosion, are it appears such

[033-066] as would also induce the formation of surcharged steam.

[033-067] 403. _Q._--Is it the natural effect of surcharged steam to waste away iron?

[033-068] _A._--It is the natural effect of surcharged steam to oxidate the iron with

[033-069] which it is in contact, as is illustrated by the familiar process for

[033-070] making hydrogen gas by sending steam through a red hot tube filled with

[033-071] pieces of iron; and although the action of the surcharged steam in a boiler

[033-072] is necessarily very much weaker than where the iron is red hot, it

[033-073] manifestly must have _some_ oxidizing effect, and the amount of corrosion

[033-074] produced may be very material where the action is perpetual. Boilers with a

[033-075] large extent of heating surface, or with descending flues circulating

[033-076] through the cooler water in the bottom of the boiler before ascending the

[033-077] chimney, will be less corroded internally than boilers in which a large

[033-078] quantity of the heat passes away in the smoke; and the corrosion of the

[033-079] boiler will be diminished if the interior of any flue passing through the

[033-080] steam be coated with fire brick, so as to present the transmission of the

[033-081] heat in that situation. The best practice, however, appears to consist in

[033-082] the transmission of the smoke through a suitable passage on the outside of

[033-083] the boiler, so as to supersede the necessity of carrying any flue through

[033-084] the steam at all; or a column of water may be carried round the chimney,

[033-085] into which as much of the feed water may be introduced as the heat of the

[033-086] chimney is capable of raising to the boiling point, as under this

[033-087] limitation the presence of feed water around the chimney in the steam chest

[033-088] will fail to condense the steam.

[033-089] 404. _Q._--In steam vessels there are usually several boilers?

[033-090] _A._--Yes, in steam vessels of considerable power and size.

[033-091] 405. _Q._--Are these boilers generally so constructed, that any one of them

[033-092] may be thrown out of use?

[033-093] _A._--Marine boilers are now generally supplied with stop valves, whereby

[033-094] one boiler may be thrown out of use without impairing the efficacy of the

[033-095] remainder. These stop valves are usually spindle valves of large size, and

[033-096] they are for the most part set in a pipe which runs across the steam

[033-097] chests, connecting the several boilers together. The spindles of these

[033-098] valves should project through stuffing boxes in the covers of the valve

[033-099] chests, and they should be balanced by a weighted lever, and kept in

[033-100] continual action by the steam. If the valves be lifted up, and be suffered

[033-101] to remain up, as is the usual practice, they will become fixed by corrosion

[033-102] in that position, and it will be impossible after some time to shut them on

[033-103] an emergency. These valves should always be easily accessible from the

[033-104] engine room; and it ought not to be necessary for the coal boxes to be

[033-105] empty to gain access to them.

[033-106] 406. _Q._--Should each boiler have at least one safety valve for itself?

[033-107] _A._--Yes; it would be quite unsafe without this provision, as the stop

[033-108] valve might possibly jam. Sometimes valves jam from a distortion in the

[033-109] shape of the boiler when a considerable pressure is put upon it.

[033-110] 407. _Q._--How is the admission of the water into the boiler regulated?

[033-111] _A._--The admission of feed water into the boiler is regulated by hand by

[033-112] the engineer by means of cocks, and sometimes by spindle valves raised and

[033-113] lowered by a screw. Cocks appear to be the preferable expedient, as they

[033-114] are less liable to accident or derangement than screw valves, and in modern

[033-115] steam vessels they are generally employed.

[033-116] 408. _Q._--At what point of the boiler is the feed introduced?

[033-117] _A._--The feed water is usually conducted from the feed cock to a point

[033-118] near the bottom of the boiler by means of an internal pipe, the object of

[033-119] this arrangement being to prevent the rising steam from being condensed by

[033-120] the entering water. By being introduced near the bottom of the boiler, the

[033-121] water comes into contact in the first place with the bottoms of the

[033-122] furnaces and flues, and extracts heat from them which could not be

[033-123] extracted by water of a higher temperature, whereby a saving of fuel is

[033-124] accomplished. In some cases the feed water is introduced into a casing

[033-125] around the chimney, from whence it descends into the boiler. This plan

[033-126] appears to be an expedient one when the boiler is short of heating surface,

[033-127] and more than a usual quantity of heat ascends the chimney; but in well

[033-128] proportioned boilers a water casing round the chimney is superfluous. When

[033-129] a water casing is used the boiler is generally fed by a head of water, the

[033-130] feed water being forced up into a small tank, from whence it descends into

[033-131] the boiler by the force of gravity, while the surplus runs to waste, as in

[033-132] the feeding apparatus of land engines.

[033-133] 409. _Q._--Suppose that the engineer should shut off the feed water from

[033-134] the boilers while the engine was working, what would be the result?

[033-135] _A._--The result would be to burst the feed pipes, except for a safety

[033-136] valve placed on the feed pipe between the engine and the boilers, which

[033-137] safety valve opens when any undue pressure comes upon the pipes, and allows

[033-138] the water to escape. There is, however, generally a cock on the suction

[033-139] side of the feed pump, which regulates the quantity of water drawn into the

[033-140] pump. But there must be cocks on the boilers also to determine into which

[033-141] boiler the water shall be chiefly discharged, and these cocks are sometimes

[033-142] all shut accidentally at the same time.

[033-143] 410. _Q._--Is there no expedient in use in steam vessels for enabling the

[033-144] position of the water level in the boiler to determine the quantity of feed

[033-145] water admitted?

[033-146] _A._--In some steam vessels floats have been introduced to regulate the

[033-147] feed, but their action cannot be depended on in agitated water, if applied

[033-148] after the common fashion. Floats would probably answer if placed in a

[033-149] cylinder which communicates with the water in the boiler by means of small

[033-150] holes; and a disc of metal might be attached to the end of a rod extending

[033-151] beneath the water level, so as to resist irregular movements from the

[033-152] motion of the ship at sea, which would otherwise impair the action of the

[033-153] apparatus.

[033-154] 411. _Q._--How is the proper level of the water in the boiler of a steam

[033-155] vessel maintained when, the engine is stopped for some time, and the boiler

[033-156] is blowing off steam?

[033-157] _A._--By means of a separate pump worked sometimes by hand, but usually by

[033-158] a small separate engine called the Donkey engine. This pump, by the aid of

[033-159] suitable cocks, will pump from the sea into the boiler; from the sea upon

[033-160] deck either to wash decks or to extinguish fire; and from the bilge

[033-161] overboard, through a suitable orifice in the side of the ship.

[033-162] LOCOMOTIVE BOILERS.

[033-163] 412. _Q._--Will you recapitulate the general features of locomotive

[033-164] boilers?

[033-165] _A._--Locomotive boilers consist of three portions (see fig. 29): the

[033-166] barrel E, E, containing the tubes, the fire box B, and the smoke box F; of

[033-167] which the barrel smoke box, and external fire box are always of iron, but

[033-168] the internal fire box is generally made of copper, though sometimes also it

[033-169] is made of iron. The tubes are sometimes of iron, but generally of brass

[033-170] fixed in by ferules. The whole of the iron plates of a locomotive boiler

[033-171] Which are subjected to the pressure of steam, should be Lowmoor or Bowling

[033-172] plates of the best quality; and the copper should be coarse grained, rather

[033-173] than rich or soft, and be perfectly free from irregularities of structure

[033-174] and lamination.
