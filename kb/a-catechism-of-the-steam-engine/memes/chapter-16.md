[016-001] 198. _Q._--Will you explain the structure or configuration of expansion

[016-002] apparatus of the usual construction?

[016-003] [Illustration: Fig 34.]

[016-004] _A._--The structure of expansion apparatus is very various; but all the

[016-005] kinds operate either on the principle of giving such a motion to the slide

[016-006] valve as will enable it to cut off the steam, at the desired point, or on

[016-007] the principle of shutting off the steam by a separate valve in the steam

[016-008] pipe or valve casing. The first class of apparatus has not been found so

[016-009] manageable, and is not in extensive use, except in that form known as the

[016-010] link motion. Of the second class, the most simple probably is the

[016-011] application of a cam giving motion to the throttle valve, or to a valve of

[016-012] the same construction, which either accurately fits the steam pipe, or

[016-013] which comes round to a face, which, however, it is restrained from touching

[016-014] by a suitable construction of the cam. A kind of expansion valve, often

[016-015] employed in marine engines of low speed, is the kind used in the Cornish

[016-016] engines, and known as the equilibrium valve. This valve is represented in

[016-017] fig. 34. It consists substantially of an annulus or bulging cylinder of

[016-018] brass, with a steam-tight face both at its upper and lower edges, at which

[016-019] points it fits accurately upon a stationary seat. This annulus may be

[016-020] raised or lowered without being resisted by the pressure of the steam, and

[016-021] in rotative engines it is usually worked by a cam on the shaft. The

[016-022] expansion cam is put on the shaft in two pieces, which are fastened to each

[016-023] other by means of four bolts passing through lugs, and is fixed to the

[016-024] shaft by keys. A roller at one end of a bell-crank lever, which is

[016-025] connected with the expansion valve, presses against the cam, so that the

[016-026] motion of the lever will work the valve. The roller is kept against the cam

[016-027] by a weight on a lever attached to the same shaft, but a spring is

[016-028] necessary for high speeds. If the cam were concentric with the shaft, the

[016-029] lever which presses upon it would remain stationary, and also the expansion

[016-030] valve; but by the projection of the cam, the end of the lever receives a

[016-031] reciprocating motion, which is communicated to the valve.

[016-032] 199. _Q._--The cam then works the valve?

[016-033] _A._--Yes. The position of the projection of the cam determines the point

[016-034] in relation to the stroke at which the valve is opened, and its

[016-035] circumferential length determines the length of the time during which the

[016-036] valve continues open. The time at which the valve should begin to open is

[016-037] the same under all circumstances, but the duration of its opening varies

[016-038] with the amount of expansion desired. In order to obtain this variable

[016-039] extent of expansion, there are several projections made upon the cam, each

[016-040] of which gives a different degree, or _grade_ as it is usually called, of

[016-041] expansion. These grades all begin at the same point on the cam, but are of

[016-042] different lengths, so that they begin to move the lever at the same time,

[016-043] but differ in the time of returning it to its original position.

[016-044] 200. _Q._--How is the degree of expansion changed?

[016-045] _A._--The change of expansion is effected by moving the roller on to the

[016-046] desired grade; which is done by slipping the lever carrying the roller

[016-047] endways on the shaft or pin sustaining it.

[016-048] 201. _Q._--Are such cams applicable in all cases?

[016-049] _A._--In engines moving at a high rate of speed the roller will be thrown

[016-050] back from the cam by its momentum, unless it be kept against it by means of

[016-051] springs. In some cases I have employed a spring formed of a great number of

[016-052] discs of India rubber to keep the roller against the cam, but a few brass

[016-053] discs require to be interposed to prevent the India rubber discs from being

[016-054] worn in the central hole.

[016-055] 202. _Q._--May not the percussion incident to the action of a cam at a high

[016-056] speed, when the roller is not kept up to the face by springs, be obviated

[016-057] by giving a suitable configuration to the cam itself?

[016-058] _A._--It may at all events be reduced. The outline of the cam should be a

[016-059] parabola, so that the valve may be set in motion precisely as a falling

[016-060] body would be; but it will, nevertheless, be necessary that the roller on

[016-061] which the cam presses should be forced upward by a spring rather than by a

[016-062] counterweight, as there will thus be less inertia or momentum in the mass

[016-063] that has to be moved.

[016-064] 203. _Q._--An additional slide valve is sometimes used for cutting off the

[016-065] steam?

[016-066] _A._--Yes, very frequently; and the slide valve is sometimes on the side or

[016-067] back of the valve casing, and sometimes on the back of the main or

[016-068] distributing valve, and moving with it.

[016-069] 204. _Q._--Are cams used in locomotive engines?

[016-070] _A._--In locomotive engines the use of cams is inadmissible, and other

[016-071] expedients are employed, of which those contrived by Stephenson and by

[016-072] Cabrey operate on the principle of accomplishing the requisite variations

[016-073] of expansion by altering the throw of the slide valve.

[016-074] 205. _Q._--What is Stephenson's arrangement?

[016-075] [Illustration: Fig. 35.]

[016-076] _A._--Stephenson connects the ends of the forward and backward eccentric

[016-077] rods by a link with a curved slot in which a pin upon the end of the valve

[016-078] rod works. By moving this link so as to bring the forward eccentric rod in

[016-079] the same line with the valve rod, the valve receives the motion due to that

[016-080] eccentric; whereas if the backward eccentric rod is brought in a line with

[016-081] the valve rod, the valve gets the motion proper for reversing, and if the

[016-082] link be so placed that the valve rod is midway between the two eccentric

[016-083] rods, the valve will remain nearly stationary. This arrangement, which is

[016-084] now employed extensively, is what is termed "the link motion." It is

[016-085] represented in the annexed figure, fig. 35, where _e_ is the valve rod,

[016-086] which is attached by a pin to an open curved link susceptible of being

[016-087] moved up and down by the bell-crank lever _f''_ _f''_, supported on the

[016-088] centre _g_, and acting on the links _f_, while the valve rod _e_ remains in

[016-089] the same horizontal plane; _d d'_ are the eccentric rods, and the link is

[016-090] represented in its lowest position. The dotted lines _h' h''_ show the

[016-091] position of the eccentric rods when the link is in its highest position,

[016-092] and _l l'_ when in mid position.

[016-093] 206. _Q._--What is Cabrey's arrangement?

[016-094] _A._--Mr. Cabrey makes his eccentric rod terminate in a pin which works

[016-095] into a straight slotted lever, furnished with jaws similar to the jaws on

[016-096] the eccentric rods of locomotives. By raising the pin of the eccentric rod

[016-097] in this slot, the travel of the valve will be varied, and expansive action

[016-098] will be the result.

[016-099] 207. _Q._--What other forms of apparatus are there for working steam

[016-100] expansively?

[016-101] _A._--They are too numerous for description here, but a few of them may be

[016-102] enumerated. Fenton seeks to accomplish the desired object by introducing a

[016-103] spiral feather on the crank axle, by moving the eccentric laterally against

[016-104] which the eccentric is partially turned round so as to cut off the steam at

[016-105] a different part of the stroke. Dodds seeks to attain the same end by

[016-106] corresponding mechanical arrangements. Farcot, Edwards, and Lavagrian cut

[016-107] off the steam by the application of a supplementary valve at the back of

[016-108] the ordinary valve, which supplementary valve is moved by tappets fixed to

[016-109] the valve casing. Bodmer, in 1841, and Meyer, in 1842, employed two slides

[016-110] or blocks fitted over apertures in the ordinary slide valve, and which

[016-111] blocks were approximated or set apart by a right and left handed screw

[016-112] passing through both.[1] Hawthorn, in 1843, employed as an expansion valve

[016-113] a species of frame lying on the ordinary cylinder face upon the outside of

[016-114] the valve, and working up against the steam side of the valve at each end

[016-115] so as to cut off the steam. In the same year Gonzenbach patented an

[016-116] arrangement which consists of an additional slide valve and valve casing

[016-117] placed on the back of the ordinary slide valve casing, and through this

[016-118] supplementary valve the steam must first pass. This supplementary valve is

[016-119] worked by a double ended lever, slotted at one end for the reception of a

[016-120] pin on the valve link, the position of which in the slot determines the

[016-121] throw of the supplementary valve, and the consequent degree of expansion.

[016-122] 208. _Q._--What is the arrangement of expansion valve used in the most

[016-123] approved modern engines?

[016-124] _A._--In modern engines, either marine or locomotive, it is found that if

[016-125] they are fitted with the link motion, as they nearly all are, a very good

[016-126] expansive action can be obtained by giving a suitable adjustment to it,

[016-127] without employing an expansion valve at all. Diagrams taken from engines

[016-128] worked in this manner show a very excellent result, and most of the modern

[016-129] engines trust for their expansive working to the link motion and the

[016-130] throttle valve.

[016-131] [1] In 1838 I patented an arrangement of expansion valve, consisting of two

[016-132] movable plates set upon the ordinary slide valve, and which might be drawn

[016-133] together or asunder by means of a right and left handed screw passing

[016-134] through both plates. The valve spindle was hollow, and a prolongation of

[016-135] the screw passed up through it, and was armed on the top with a small

[016-136] wheel, by means of which the plates might be adjusted while the engine was

[016-137] at work. In 1839 I fitted an expansion valve in a steam vessel, consisting

[016-138] of two plates, connected by a rod, and moved by tappets up against the

[016-139] steam edges of the valve. In another steam vessel I fitted the same species

[016-140] of valve, but the motion was not derived from tappets, but from a moving

[016-141] part of the engine, though at the moderate speed at which these engines

[016-142] worked I found tappets to operate well and make little noise. In 1837 I

[016-143] employed, as an expansion valve, a rectangular throttle valve, accurately

[016-144] fitting a bored out seat, in which it might be made to revolve, though it

[016-145] did not revolve in working. This valve was moved by a pin in a pinion,

[016-146] making two revolutions for every revolution of the engine, and the

[016-147] configuration of the seat determined the amount of the expansion. In 1855 I

[016-148] have again used expansion valves of this construction in engines making one

[016-149] hundred revolutions per minute, and with perfectly satisfactory results.--

[016-150] J.B.

[016-151] CHAPTER IV.

[016-152] MODES OF ESTIMATING THE POWER AND PERFORMANCE OF ENGINES AND BOILERS.

[016-153] HORSES POWER.

[016-154] 209. _Q._--What do you understand by a horse power?

[016-155] _A._--An amount of mechanical force that will raise 33,000 lbs. one foot

[016-156] high in a minute. This standard was adopted by Mr. Watt, as the average

[016-157] force exerted by the strongest London horses; the object of his

[016-158] investigation being to enable him to determine the relation between the

[016-159] power of a certain size of engine and the power of a horse, so that when it

[016-160] was desired to supersede the use of horses by the erection of an engine, he

[016-161] might, from the number of horses employed, determine the size of engine

[016-162] that would be suitable for the work.

[016-163] 210. _Q._--Then when we talk of an engine of 200 horse power, it is meant

[016-164] that the impelling efficacy is equal to that of 200 horses, each lifting

[016-165] 33,000 lbs. one foot high in a minute?

[016-166] _A._--No, not now; such was the case in Watt's engines, but the capacity of

[016-167] cylinder answerable to a horse power has been increased by most engineers

[016-168] since his time, and the pressure on the piston has been increased also, so

[016-169] that what is now called a 200 horse power engine exerts, almost in every

[016-170] case, a greater power than was exerted in Watt's time, and a horse power,

[016-171] in the popular sense of the term, has become a mere conventional unit for

[016-172] expressing a certain size of engine, without reference to the power

[016-173] exerted.
