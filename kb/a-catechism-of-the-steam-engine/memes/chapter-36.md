[036-001] 427. _Q._--Your explanation has reference to the pumping engine as

[036-002] introduced into Cornwall by Watt: have any modifications been since made

[036-003] upon it?

[036-004] _A._--In the modern Cornish engines the steam is used very expansively, and

[036-005] a high pressure of steam is employed. In some cases a double cylinder

[036-006] engine is used, in which the steam, after having given motion to a small

[036-007] piston on the principle of a high pressure engine, passes into a larger

[036-008] cylinder, where it operates on the principle of a condensing engine; but

[036-009] there is no superior effect gained by the use of two cylinders, and there

[036-010] is greater complexity in the apparatus. Instead of the lever walls, cast

[036-011] iron columns are now frequently used for supporting the main beam in

[036-012] pumping engines, and the cylinder end of the main beam is generally made

[036-013] longer than the pump end in engines made in Cornwall, so as to enable the

[036-014] cylinder to have a long stroke, and the piston to move quickly, without

[036-015] communicating such a velocity to the pump buckets as will make them work

[036-016] with such a shock as to wear themselves out quickly. A high pressure of

[036-017] steam, too, can be employed where the stroke is long, without involving the

[036-018] necessity of making the working parts of such large dimensions as would

[036-019] otherwise be necessary; for the strength of the parts of a single acting

[036-020] engine will require to be much the same, whatever the length of the stroke

[036-021] may be.

[036-022] 428. _Q._--What kind of pump is mostly used in draining deep mines?

[036-023] _A._--The pump now universally preferred is the plunger pump, which admits

[036-024] of being packed or tightened while the engine is at work; but the lowest

[036-025] lift of a mine is generally supplied with a pump on the suction principle,

[036-026] both with the view of enabling the lowest pipe to follow the water with

[036-027] facility as the shaft is sunk deeper, and to obviate the inconvenience of

[036-028] the valves of the pump being rendered inaccessible by any flooding in the

[036-029] mine. The pump valves of deep mines are a perpetual source of expense and

[036-030] trouble, as from the pressure of water upon them it is difficult to prevent

[036-031] them from closing with violence; and many expedients have been contrived to

[036-032] mitigate the evil, of which the valve known as Harvey and West's valve has

[036-033] perhaps gained the widest acceptation.

[036-034] 429. _Q._--Will you describe Harvey and West's pump valve?

[036-035] _A._--This valve is a compromise between the equilibrium valve, of the kind

[036-036] employed for admitting the steam to and from the cylinder in single acting

[036-037] engines, and the common spindle valve formerly used for that purpose; and

[036-038] to comprehend its action, it is necessary that the action of the

[036-039] equilibrium valve, which has been already represented fig. 34, should first

[036-040] be understood. This valve consists substantially of a cylinder open at both

[036-041] ends, and capable of sliding upon a stationary piston fixed upon a rod the

[036-042] length of the cylinder, which proceeds from the centre of the orifice the

[036-043] valve is intended to close. It is clear, that when the cylinder is pressed

[036-044] down until its edge rests upon the bottom of the box containing it, the

[036-045] orifice of the pipe must be closed, as the steam can neither escape past

[036-046] the edge of the cylinder nor between the cylinder and the piston; and it is

[036-047] equally clear, that as the pressure upon the cylinder is equal all around

[036-048] it, and the whole of the downward pressure is maintained by the stationary

[036-049] piston, the cylinder can be raised or lowered without any further exertion

[036-050] of force than is necessary to overcome the friction of the piston and of

[036-051] the rod by which the cylinder is raised. Instead of the rubbing surface of

[036-052] a piston, however, a conical valve face between the cylinder and piston is

[036-053] employed, which is tight only when the cylinder is in its lowest position;

[036-054] and there is a similar face between, the edge of the cylinder and the

[036-055] bottom of the box in which it is placed. The moving part of the valve, too,

[036-056] instead of being a perfect cylinder, is bulged outward in the middle, so as

[036-057] to permit the steam to escape past the stationary piston when the

[036-058] cylindrical part of the valve is raised. It is clear, that if such a valve

[036-059] were applied to a pump, no pressure of water within the pump would suffice

[036-060] to open it, neither would any pressure of water above the valve cause it to

[036-061] shut with violence; and if an equilibrium valve, therefore, be used as a

[036-062] pump valve at all, it must be opened and shut by mechanical means. In

[036-063] Harvey and West's valves, however, the equilibrium principle is only

[036-064] partially adopted; the lower face is considerably larger in diameter than

[036-065] the upper face, and the difference constitutes an annulus of pressure,

[036-066] which will cause the valve to open or shut with the same force as a spindle

[036-067] valve of the area of the annulus. To deaden the shock still more

[036-068] effectually, the lower face of the valve is made to strike upon end wood

[036-069] driven into an annular recess in the pump bucket; and valves thus

[036-070] constructed work with very little noise or tremor; but it is found in

[036-071] practice, that the use of Harvey and West's valve, or any contrivance of a

[036-072] similar kind, adds materially to the load upon the pump, especially in low

[036-073] lifts where the addition of a load, to the valve makes a material addition

[036-074] to the total resistance which the engine has to overcome. Instead of end

[036-075] wood driven into a recess for the valve to strike upon, a mixture of tin

[036-076] and lead cast in a recess is now frequently used, and is found to be

[036-077] preferable to the wood.

[036-078] 430. _Q._--Is there any other kind of pump valve which is free from the

[036-079] shocks incidental to the working of common valves?

[036-080] _A._--In some cases canvass valves are used for pumps, with the effect of

[036-081] materially mitigating the shock; but they require frequent renewal, and are

[036-082] of inferior eligibility in their action to the slide valve, which might in

[036-083] many cases be applied to pumps without inconvenience.

[036-084] 431. _Q._--Could not a form of pump be devised capable of working without

[036-085] valves at all?

[036-086] _A._.--It appears probable, that by working a common reciprocating pump at

[036-087] a high speed, a continuous flow of water might be maintained through the

[036-088] pipes in such a way as to render the existence of any valves superfluous

[036-089] after once the action was begun, the momentum of the moving water acting in

[036-090] fact as valves. The centrifugal pump, however, threatens to supersede pumps

[036-091] of every other kind; and if the centrifugal pump be employed there will be

[036-092] no necessity for pump valves at all. There is less loss of effect by the

[036-093] centrifugal pump than by the common pump.

[036-094] 432. _Q._--What is the best form of the centrifugal pump?

[036-095] _A._--There are two forms in which the centrifugal pump may be applied to

[036-096] mines;--that in which the arms diverge from the bottom, like the letter V;

[036-097] and that in which revolving arms are set in a tight case near the bottom of

[036-098] the mine, and are turned by a shaft from the surface. Such pumps both draw

[036-099] and force; and either by arranging them in a succession of lifts in the

[036-100] shaft of the mine, or otherwise, the water may be drawn without

[036-101] inconvenience from any depth. The introduction of the centrifugal pump

[036-102] would obviously extinguish the single acting engine, as rotative engines

[036-103] working at a high speed would be the most appropriate form of engine where

[036-104] the centrifugal pump was employed.

[036-105] 433. _Q._--This would not be a heavy deprivation?

[036-106] _A._--The single acting engine is a remnant of engineering barbarism which

[036-107] must now be superseded by more compendious contrivances. The Cornish

[036-108] engines, though rudely manufactured, are very expensive in production, as a

[036-109] large engine does but little work; whereas by employing a smaller engine,

[036-110] moving with a high speed, the dimensions may be so far diminished that the

[036-111] most refined machinery may be obtained at less than the present cost.

[036-112] 434. _Q._--Are not the Cornish engines more economical in fuel than other

[036-113] engines?

[036-114] _A._--It is a mistake to suppose that there is any peculiar virtue in the

[036-115] existing form of Cornish engine to make it economical in fuel, or that a

[036-116] less lethargic engine would necessarily be less efficient. The large duty

[036-117] of the engines in Cornwall is traceable to the large employment of the

[036-118] principle of expansion, and to a few other causes which may be made of

[036-119] quite as decisive efficacy in smaller engines working with a quicker speed;

[036-120] and there is therefore no argument in the performance of the present

[036-121] engines against the proposed substitution.

[036-122] VARIOUS FORMS OF MARINE ENGINES.

[036-123] 435. _Q._--What species of paddle engine do you consider to be the best?

[036-124] _A._--The oscillating engine.

[036-125] 436. _Q._--Will you explain the grounds of that preference?

[036-126] _A._--The engine occupies little space, consists of few parts, is easily

[036-127] accessible for repairs, and may be both light and strong at the same time.

[036-128] In the case of large engines the crank in the intermediate shaft is a

[036-129] disadvantage, as it is difficult to obtain such a forging quite sound. But

[036-130] by forging it in three cranked flat bars, which are then laid together and

[036-131] welded into a square shaft, a sound forging will be more probable, and the

[036-132] bars should be rounded a little on the sides which are welded to allow the

[036-133] scoriae to escape during that operation. It is important in so large a

[036-134] forging not to let the fire be too fierce, else the surface of the iron

[036-135] will be burnt before the heart is brought to a welding heat. In some cases

[036-136] in oscillating engines the air pump has been wrought by an eccentric, and

[036-137] that may at any time be done where doubt of obtaining a sound intermediate

[036-138] shaft is entertained; but the precaution must be taken to make the

[036-139] eccentric very wide so as to distribute the pressure over a large surface,

[036-140] else the eccentric will be apt to heat.

[036-141] 437. _Q._--Have not objections been brought against the oscillating engine?

[036-142] _A._--In common with every other improvement, the oscillating engine, at

[036-143] the time of its introduction, encountered much opposition. The cylinder, it

[036-144] was said, would become oval, the trunnion bearings would be liable to heat

[036-145] and the trunnion joints to leak, the strain upon the trunnions would be apt

[036-146] to bend in or bend out the sides of the cylinder; and the circumstance of

[036-147] the cylinder being fixed across its centre, while the shaft requires to

[036-148] accommodate itself to the working of the ship, might, it was thought, be

[036-149] the occasion of such a strain upon the trunnions as would either break them

[036-150] or bend the piston rod. It is a sufficient reply to these objections to say

[036-151] that they are all hypothetical, and that none of them in practice have been

[036-152] found to exist--to such an extent at least as to occasion any

[036-153] inconvenience; but it is not difficult to show that they are altogether

[036-154] unsubstantial, even without a recourse to the disproofs afforded by

[036-155] experience.

[036-156] 438. _Q._--Is there not a liability in the cylinder to become oval from the

[036-157] strain thrown on it by the piston?

[036-158] _A._--There is, no doubt, a tendency in oscillating engines for the

[036-159] cylinder and the stuffing box to become oval, but after a number of years'

[036-160] wear it is found that the amount of ellipticity is less than that which is

[036-161] found to exist in the cylinders of side lever engines after a similar

[036-162] trial. The resistance opposed by friction to the oscillation of the

[036-163] cylinder is so small, that a man is capable of moving a large cylinder with

[036-164] one hand; whereas in the side lever engine, if the parallel motion be in

[036-165] the least untrue, which is, at some time or other, an almost inevitable

[036-166] condition, the piston is pushed with great force against the side of the

[036-167] cylinder, whereby a large amount of wear and friction is occasioned. The

[036-168] trunnion bearings, instead of being liable to heat like other journals, are

[036-169] kept down to the temperature of the steam by the flow of steam passing

[036-170] through them; and the trunnion packings are not liable to leak when the

[036-171] packings, before being introduced, are squeezed in a cylindrical mould.
