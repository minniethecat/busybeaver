[013-001] 154. _Q._--You refer, I suppose, to Mr. Charles Wye Williams' Argand

[013-002] furnace?

[013-003] _A._--I chiefly refer to it, though I also comprehend all other schemes in

[013-004] which there is a continuous admission of air into the flues, with an

[013-005] intermittent generation of smoke.

[013-006] 155. _Q._--This is not so in Prideaux's furnace?

[013-007] _A._--No; in that furnace the air is admitted only during a certain

[013-008] interval, or for so long, in fact, as there is smoke to be consumed.

[013-009] 156. _Q._--Will you explain the chief peculiarities of that furnace?

[013-010] _A._--The whole peculiarity is in the furnace door. The front of the door

[013-011] consists of metal Venetians, which are opened when the top lever is lifted

[013-012] up, and shut when that lever descends to its lowest position. When the

[013-013] furnace door is opened to replenish the fire with coals, the top lever is

[013-014] raised up, and with it the piston of the small cylinder attached to the

[013-015] side of the furnace. The Venetians are thereby opened, and a stream of air

[013-016] enters the furnace, which, being heated in its passage among the numerous

[013-017] heated plates attached to the back of the furnace door, is in a favorable

[013-018] condition for effecting the combustion of the inflammable parts of the

[013-019] smoke. The piston in the small cylinder gradually subsides and closes the

[013-020] Venetians; and the rate of the subsidence of the piston may obviously be

[013-021] regulated by a cock, or, as in this case, a small screw valve, so that the

[013-022] Venetians shall just close when there is no more smoke to be consumed;--the

[013-023] air or other fluid within the cylinder being forced out by the piston in

[013-024] its descent.

[013-025] 157. _Q._--Had Mr. Watt any method of consuming smoke?

[013-026] _A._--He tried various methods, but eventually fixed upon the method of

[013-027] coking the coal on a dead plate at the furnace door, before pushing it into

[013-028] the fire. That method is perfectly effectual where the combustion is so

[013-029] slow that the requisite time for coking is allowed, and it is much

[013-030] preferable to any of the methods of admitting air at the bridge or

[013-031] elsewhere, to accomplish the combustion of the inflammable parts of the

[013-032] smoke.

[013-033] 158. _Q._--What are the details of Mr. Watt's arrangement as now employed?

[013-034] _A._--The fire bars and the dead plate are both set at a considerable

[013-035] inclination, to facilitate the advance of the fuel into the furnace. In

[013-036] Boulton and Watt's 30 horse power land boiler, the dead plate and the

[013-037] furnace bars are both about 4 feet long, and they are set at the angle of

[013-038] 30 degrees with the horizon.

[013-039] 159. _Q._--Is the use of the dead plate universally adopted in Boulton and

[013-040] Watt's land boilers?

[013-041] _A._--It is generally adopted, but in some cases Boulton and Watt have

[013-042] substituted the plan of a revolving grate for consuming the smoke, and the

[013-043] dead plate then becomes both superfluous and inapplicable. In this

[013-044] contrivance the fire is replenished with coals by a self-acting mechanism.

[013-045] 160. _Q._--Will you explain the arrangement of the revolving grate?

[013-046] _A._--The fire grate is made like a round table capable of turning

[013-047] horizontally upon a centre; a shower of coal is precipitated upon the grate

[013-048] through a slit in the boiler near the furnace mouth, and the smoke evolved

[013-049] from the coal dropped at the front part of the fire is consumed by passing

[013-050] over the incandescent fuel at the back part, from which all the smoke must

[013-051] have been expelled in the revolution of the grate before it can have

[013-052] reached that position.

[013-053] 161. _Q._--Is a furnace with a revolving grate applicable to a steam

[013-054] vessel?

[013-055] _A._--I see nothing to prevent its application. But the arrangement of the

[013-056] boiler would perhaps require to be changed, and it might be preferable to

[013-057] combine its use with the employment of vertical tubes, for the transmission

[013-058] of the smoke. The introduction of any effectual automatic contrivance for

[013-059] feeding the fire in steam vessels, would bring about an important economy,

[013-060] at the same time that it would give the assurance of the work being better

[013-061] done. It is very difficult to fire furnaces by hand effectually at sea,

[013-062] especially in rough weather and in tropical climates; whereas machinery

[013-063] would be unaffected by any such disturbing causes, and would perform with

[013-064] little expense the work of many men.

[013-065] 162. _Q._--The introduction of some mechanical method of feeding the fire

[013-066] with coals would enable a double tier of furnaces to be adopted in steam

[013-067] vessels without inconvenience?

[013-068] _A._--Yes, it would have at least that tendency; and as the space available

[013-069] for area of grate is limited in a steam vessel by the width of the vessel,

[013-070] it would be a great convenience if a double tier of furnaces could be

[013-071] employed without a diminished effect. It appears to me, however, that the

[013-072] objection would still remain of the steam raised by the lower furnace being

[013-073] cooled and deadened by the air entering the ash-pit of the upper fire, for

[013-074] it would strike upon the metal of the ash-pit bottom.

[013-075] 163. _Q._--Have any other plans been devised for feeding the fire by

[013-076] self-acting means besides that of a revolving grate?

[013-077] _A._--Yes, many plans, but none of them, perhaps, are free from an

[013-078] objectionable complication. In some arrangements the bars are made like

[013-079] screws, which being turned round slowly, gradually carry forward the coal;

[013-080] while in other arrangements the same object is sought to be attained by

[013-081] alternately lifting and depressing every second bar at the end nearest the

[013-082] mouth of the furnace. In Juckes' furnace, the fire bars are arranged in the

[013-083] manner of rows of endless chains working over a roller at the mouth of the

[013-084] furnace, and another roller at the farther end of the furnace. These

[013-085] rollers are put into slow revolution, and the coal which is deposited at

[013-086] the mouth of the furnace is gradually carried forward by the motion of the

[013-087] chains, which act like an endless web. The clinkers and ashes left after

[013-088] the combustion of the coal, are precipitated into the ash-pit, where the

[013-089] chain turns down over the roller at the extremity of the furnace. In

[013-090] Messrs. Maudslays' plan of a self-feeding furnace the fire bars are formed

[013-091] of round tubes, and are placed transversely across the furnace. The ends of

[013-092] the bars gear into endless screws running the whole length of the furnace,

[013-093] whereby motion is given to the bars, and the coal is thus carried gradually

[013-094] forward. It is very doubtful whether any of these contrivances satisfy all

[013-095] the conditions required in a plan for feeding furnaces of the ordinary form

[013-096] by self-acting means, but the problem of providing a suitable contrivance,

[013-097] does not seem difficult of accomplishment, and will no doubt be effected

[013-098] under adequate temptation.

[013-099] 164. _Q._--Have not many plans been already contrived which consume the

[013-100] smoke of furnaces very effectually?

[013-101] _A._--Yes, many plans; and besides those already mentioned there are

[013-102] Hall's, Coupland's, Godson's, Robinson's, Stevens's, Hazeldine's, Indie's,

[013-103] Bristow and Attwood's, and a great number of others. One plan, which

[013-104] promises well, consists in making the flame descend through the fire bars,

[013-105] and the fire bars are formed of tubes set on an incline and filled with

[013-106] water, which water will circulate with a rapidity proportionate to the

[013-107] intensity of the heat. After all, however, the best remedy for smoke

[013-108] appears to consist in removing from it those portions which form the smoke

[013-109] before the coal is brought into use. Many valuable products may be got from

[013-110] the coal by subjecting it to this treatment; and the residuum will be more

[013-111] valuable than before for the production of steam.

[013-112] STEAM.

[013-113] 165. _Q._--Have experiments been made to determine the elasticity of steam

[013-114] at different temperatures?

[013-115] _A._--Yes; very careful experiments. The following rule expresses the

[013-116] results obtained by Mr. Southern:--To the given temperature in degrees of

[013-117] Fahrenheit add 51.3 degrees; from the logarithm of the sum, subtract the

[013-118] logarithm of 135.767, which is 2.1327940; multiply the remainder by 5.13,

[013-119] and to the natural number answering to the sum, add the constant

[013-120] fraction .1, which will give the elastic force in inches of mercury. If the

[013-121] elastic force be known, and it is wanted to determine the corresponding

[013-122] temperature, the rule must be modified thus:--From the elastic force, in

[013-123] inches of mercury, subtract the decimal .1, divide the logarithm of the

[013-124] remainder by 5.13, and to the quotient add the logarithm 2.1327940; find

[013-125] the natural number answering to the sum, and subtract therefrom the

[013-126] constant 51.3; the remainder will be the temperature sought. The French

[013-127] Academy, and the Franklin Institute, have repeated Mr. Southern's

[013-128] experiments on a larger scale; the results obtained by them are not widely

[013-129] different, and are perhaps nearer the truth, but Mr. Southern's results are

[013-130] generally adopted by engineers, as sufficiently accurate for practical

[013-131] purposes.

[013-132] 166. _Q._--Have not some superior experiments upon this subject been lately

[013-133] made in France?

[013-134] _A._--Yes, the experiments of M. Regnault upon this subject have been very

[013-135] elaborate and very carefully conducted, and the results are probably more

[013-136] accurate than have been heretofore obtained. Nevertheless, it is

[013-137] questionable how far it is advisable to disturb the rules of Watt and

[013-138] Southern, with which the practice of engineers is very much identified, for

[013-139] the sake of emendations which are not of such magnitude as to influence

[013-140] materially the practical result. M. Regnault has shown that the total

[013-141] amount of heat, existing in a given weight of steam, increases slightly

[013-142] with the pressure, so that the sum of the latent and sensible heats do not

[013-143] form a constant quantity. Thus, in steam of the atmospheric pressure, or

[013-144] with 14.7 Lbs. upon the square inch, the sensible heat of the steam is 212

[013-145] degrees, the latent heat 966.6 degrees, and the sum of the latent and

[013-146] sensible heats 1178.6 degrees; whereas in steam of 90 pounds upon the

[013-147] square inch the sensible heat is 320.2 degrees, the latent heat 891.4

[013-148] degrees, and the sum of the latent and sensible heats 1211.0 degrees. There

[013-149] is, therefore, 33 degrees less of heat in any given weight of water, raised

[013-150] into steam of the atmospheric pressure, than if raised into steam of 90

[013-151] Lbs.[1] pressure.

[013-152] 167. _Q._--What expansion does water undergo in its conversion into steam?

[013-153] _A._--A cubic inch of water makes about a cubic foot of steam of the

[013-154] atmospheric pressure.

[013-155] 168. _Q._--And how much at a higher pressure?

[013-156] _A._--That depends upon what the pressure is. But the proportion is easily

[013-157] ascertained, for the pressure and the bulk of a given quantity of steam, as

[013-158] of air or any other elastic fluid, are always inversely proportional to one

[013-159] another. Thus if a cubic inch of water makes a cubic foot of steam, with

[013-160] the pressure of one atmosphere, it will make half a cubic foot with the

[013-161] pressure of two atmospheres, a third of a cubic foot with the pressure of

[013-162] three atmospheres, and so on in all other proportions. High pressure steam

[013-163] indeed is just low pressure steam forced into a less space, and the

[013-164] pressure will always be great in the proportion in which the space is

[013-165] contracted.

[013-166] 169. _Q._--If this be so, the quantity of heat in a given weight of steam

[013-167] must be nearly the same, whether the steam is high or low pressure?

[013-168] _A._--Yes; the heat in steam is nearly a constant quantity, at all

[013-169] pressures, but not so precisely. Steam to which an additional quantity of

[013-170] heat has been imparted after leaving the boiler, or as it is called

[013-171] "surcharged steam," comes under a different law, for the elasticity of such

[013-172] steam may be increased without any addition being made to its weight; but

[013-173] surcharged steam is not at present employed for working engines, and it may

[013-174] therefore be considered in practice that a pound of steam contains very

[013-175] nearly the same quantity of heat at all pressures.

[013-176] 170. _Q._--Does not the quantity of heat in any body vary with the

[013-177] temperature?

[013-178] _A._--Other circumstances remaining the same the quantity of heat in a body

[013-179] increases with the temperatures.
