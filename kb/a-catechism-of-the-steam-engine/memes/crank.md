# Crank

The **crank** is a lever attached to a rotating shaft by which the reciprocating motion of a [[steam|steam engine]]'s piston is converted into circular rotary motion, enabling the engine to drive machinery, [[paddle-wheel|paddle wheels]], or locomotive driving wheels (001-230, 009-082). An engine provided with a crank is termed a rotative engine; those without — used exclusively for pumping — are non-rotative (001-229).

## Operation

The crank receives the force transmitted through the [[connecting-rod]] from the cross-head of the piston. As the crank revolves, the leverage varies continuously: when the crank pin is at the top or bottom of its circle (the dead points), the piston exerts no torsional force on the crank shaft — the pressure merely pushes against the bearings (004-121, 009-103). Between these points, as the crank descends, the effective leverage increases and the motion of the crank becomes nearly horizontal or tangential to its circle, producing the useful turning effort (009-089).

To address the issue of dead points, two cranks are typically set at right angles on the same shaft, so that when one is at the dead point the other is in an effective position (002-006).

## Efficiency

There is a certain loss of power inherent in the use of a crank because of the varying leverage — at the top and bottom centres the crank contributes nothing to the rotary motion (004-119). If the circle of the crank be divided into equal arcs, the power transmitted through each arc is not identical to the power expended by the steam during the same interval, causing a velocity variation that the [[fly-wheel|flywheel]] is employed to regulate (009-091, 009-096).

The loss has been historically controversial: while some engineers proposed crank substitutes (quicksilver engines, bell-crank arrangements, and other contrivances), the crank's simplicity, durability, and the compensating effect of the [[fly-wheel|flywheel]] have ensured its near-universal adoption in rotative [[steam|steam engine]]s (006-080, 004-155).

## Use in Engines

In [[marine-engine]]s, the crank shaft carries [[paddle-wheel|paddle wheels]] or a [[screw-propeller|screw]] [[screw-propeller|propeller]] shaft. In [[locomotive]] engines, the crank is formed directly on the driving axle, with the [[connecting-rod|connecting rod]] driving from the cross-head to the crank pin. In mill engines, the crank drives a [[fly-wheel|flywheel]] and belt system. In all cases, the crank serves the same fundamental purpose — obtaining continuous circular motion from the reciprocating stroke (009-082, 009-034).
