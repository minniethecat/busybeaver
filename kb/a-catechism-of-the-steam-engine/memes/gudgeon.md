A **gudgeon** is a cylindrical journal, pin, or bearing surface used as a pivot in [[steam|steam engine]] mechanisms. Gudgeons serve as the bearing points for beams, [[connecting-rod|connecting rods]], side levers, and cross-heads, carrying substantial loads while allowing free rotational movement. The correct proportioning of gudgeons to withstand applied forces was a key practical concern for engine builders.

## Function in the engine

In beam and side-lever engines, the main gudgeon carries the weight of the beams and transmits forces throughout the engine frame:

> [010-009] A strong gudgeon, called the _main centre_, passes through the condenser at K, the projecting ends of which serve to support the side levers or beams.

Gudgeons are subject to both bending and shearing forces, and must be sized to resist deformation under the heaviest loads encountered during engine operation. They function as plain bearings, typically running in brass or bronze bushes with continuous lubrication.

## Strength calculation

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] provides practical rules for computing the necessary dimensions of cast iron gudgeons:

> [028-087] To find the proper size of a cast iron gudgeon adapted to sustain any given weight:—multiply the weight in lbs. by the intended length of bearing expressed in terms of the diameter; divide the product by 500, and extract the square root of the quotient, which is the diameter in inches.

This empirical formula incorporates both the magnitude of the load and the length of the bearing surface, reflecting the fact that a longer bearing distributes the load over a greater area.

## Watt's rule

[[james-watt|James Watt]] developed his own method for sizing gudgeons:

> [028-092] Supposing the gudgeon to be square, then, to ascertain the thickness, multiply the weight resting on the gudgeon by the distance between the trunnions, and divide the product by 333. Extract the cube root of the quotient, which is the thickness in inches.

[[james-watt|Watt]]'s rule treats the gudgeon as a beam in bending, with the load multiplied by the distance between supports to give the bending moment. The cube root relationship reflects the fact that the strength of a rectangular section increases with the cube of its depth.

## Materials and wear

Cast iron was the standard material for stationary engine gudgeons, valued for its compressive strength and wear resistance when running against lubricated brass bearings. In high-speed or highly stressed applications, wrought iron or steel gudgeons were sometimes substituted. Regular lubrication with tallow or oil was essential to prevent scoring and overheating of the bearing surfaces.
