# Indicator Diagram

The **indicator diagram** is a graphical record traced by the [[Indicator|steam engine indicator]], showing the variation of steam pressure within the [[Cylinder|cylinder]] throughout a complete stroke of the [[Piston|piston]]. The area of the diagram represents the work done by the steam on the piston, and from it the indicated [[Horsepower|horsepower]] of the engine can be calculated. (019-002) (019-003) (019-005)

## Taking the Diagram

To obtain an indicator diagram, the first step is to allow the paper-wrapped barrel of the indicator to make two or three reciprocations with the pencil resting against it, before opening the cock that connects the indicator to the cylinder. (019-011) (019-012) (019-013)

> "There will thus be traced a horizontal line, which is called the atmospheric line, and in condensing engines, a part of the diagram will be above and a part of it below this line; whereas, in high pressure engines the whole of the diagram will be above this line." (019-014) (019-017)

This atmospheric line serves as the base reference. Vertical ordinates are then erected at equal intervals along this line, and the mean of their heights represents the mean effective pressure acting on the piston. (019-018) (019-020) (019-003) (019-004)

## Interpreting the Diagram

| Region | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **Above atmospheric line** | [[steam|Steam]] pressure driving the piston forward |
| **Below atmospheric line** ([[condensation|condensing engine]]s only) | Vacuum assisting the return stroke |
| **Area enclosed** | Total work per cycle; proportional to indicated power |

When the indicator cock is opened, the pencil traces a waving line forming a sort of irregular parallelogram — rising as steam enters, falling as the steam expands after [[Cut-Off|cut-off]], dropping sharply at [[eduction|exhaust]], and rising again as the piston returns. (019-023) (019-024) The scale marked at the side of the diagram registers the steam pressure in pounds per square inch; in one recorded example, the steam pressure rose to about 9 lbs. per square inch and the vacuum fell to 11 lbs. (019-026) (019-028)

## Calculation of Power

The mean vertical height of the diagram, multiplied by the area of the piston and the piston's velocity in feet per minute, yields the dynamical effort of the engine — its actual indicated power. (019-005) (019-008) This measure, known as [[horsepower|indicated horsepower]], was the standard method of rating [[steam|steam engine]]s throughout the nineteenth century and remains fundamental to engine thermodynamics.

## Gooch's Locomotive Indicator

A variant of the indicator was devised for [[Locomotive|locomotive]] engines by [[daniel-gooch|Daniel Gooch]], which presented several features of superiority for railway service. (019-033) (019-034) In [[daniel-gooch|Gooch]]'s instrument the indicator cylinder was placed horizontally and compressed two elliptical springs rather than a single coil spring. A [[slide-valve|slide valve]] replaced the usual cock for opening and closing communication with the [[cylinder|engine cylinder]]. (019-035) (019-036) Most significantly, the pencil marked on a continuous web of paper unwound from one drum and wound onto another, enabling a succession of diagrams to be taken automatically without intermediate manipulation — an essential advantage when testing an engine at speed. (019-042) (019-044)

Although the diagrams from [[daniel-gooch|Gooch]]'s indicator were slightly distorted because the pencil moved in an arc rather than a straight line, they could easily be translated into the usual rectangular form. (019-045) (019-047)
