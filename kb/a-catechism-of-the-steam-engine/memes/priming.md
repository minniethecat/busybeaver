**Priming** is the phenomenon in which water from a [[boiler]] is carried over into the steam pipes along with the steam, instead of pure dry steam alone being delivered to the [[cylinder]]. It is a serious operational fault that can cause water hammer, loss of power, and in extreme cases, damage to the engine from incompressible water entering the cylinder. The term derives from the idea that the boiler is "priming" the steam pipe with water.

## Definition

> [023-158] What is priming?

Priming occurs when the surface of the boiling water in the boiler becomes violently agitated, and water droplets or even slugs of liquid water are entrained in the steam flow and carried out of the boiler through the steam pipe.

## Causes

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] identifies four principal causes:

> [024-001] What are the causes of priming?
>
> [024-002] The causes of priming are an insufficient amount of steam room, an inadequate area of water level, an insufficient width between the flues or tubes for the ascent of the steam and the descent of water to supply the vacuity the steam occasions, and the use of dirty water in the boiler.

Additionally, new boilers are more prone to priming than old ones, and a change in water chemistry can trigger it:

> [024-006] New boilers prime more than old boilers, and steamers entering rivers from the sea are more addicted to priming than if sea or river water had alone been used.

## Remedies

The immediate operational response to priming is to reduce the rate of steam generation:

> [024-021] What is the proper remedy for priming?
>
> [024-022] When a boiler primes, the engineer generally closes the throttle valve partially, turns off the injection water, and opens the furnace doors, whereby the generation of steam is checked, and a less violent ebullition in the boiler suffices.

Longer-term remedies address the underlying causes:

> [024-025] Where the priming arises from an insufficient amount of steam room, it may be mitigated by putting a higher pressure on the boiler.

> [024-034] Where priming arises from the existence of dirty water in the boiler, the evil may be remedied by the use of collecting vessels, or by blowing off largely.

## Dangers

Excessive priming can be dangerous, particularly if it proceeds unchecked:

> [025-119] May not danger arise from excessive priming?
>
> [025-120] If the water be carried out of the boiler so rapidly by priming that the level of the water cannot be maintained, and the flues or furnaces are uncovered, the boiler may be damaged by overheating.

## Prevention by design

Modern boiler design (already well understood in [[john-bourne|Bourne]]'s time for locomotive-type boilers) minimizes priming by providing adequate steam space above the water line and by separating the steam offtake from the most turbulent regions of the water surface:

> [023-155] In locomotive engines the steam is taken from the top of the steam chest, which is situated above the tubes and the fire box, and very little priming is found to occur even with this arrangement.
