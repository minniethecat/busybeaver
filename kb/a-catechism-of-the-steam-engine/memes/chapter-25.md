[025-001] 304._ Q._--Will you describe the disposition of the stays in a marine

[025-002] boiler?

[025-003] _A._--If the pressure of steam be 20 lbs. on the square inch, which is a

[025-004] very common pressure in tubular boilers, there will be a pressure of 2,880

[025-005] lbs. on every square foot of flat surface; so that if the strain upon the

[025-006] stays is not to exceed 3,000 lbs. on the square inch of section, there must

[025-007] be nearly a square inch of sectional area of stay for every square foot of

[025-008] flat surface on the top and bottom, sides, and ends of the boiler. This

[025-009] very much exceeds the proportion usually adopted; and in scarcely any

[025-010] instance are boilers stayed sufficiently to be safe when the shell is

[025-011] composed of flat surfaces. The furnaces should be stayed together with

[025-012] bolts of the best scrap iron, 1-1/4 inch in diameter, tapped through both

[025-013] plates of the water space with thin nuts in each furnace; and it is

[025-014] expedient to make the row of stays, running horizontally near the level of

[025-015] the bars, sufficiently low to come beneath the top of the bars, so as to be

[025-016] shielded from the action of the fire, with which view they should follow

[025-017] the inclination of the bars. The row of stays between the level of the bars

[025-018] and the top of the furnace should be as near the top of the furnace as will

[025-019] consist with the functions they have to perform, so as to be removed as far

[025-020] as possible from the action of the heat; and to support the furnace top,

[025-021] cross bars may either be adopted, to which the top is secured with bolts,

[025-022] as in the case of locomotives, or stays tapped into the furnace top, with a

[025-023] thin nut beneath, may be carried to the top of the boiler; but very little

[025-024] dependence can be put in such stays as stays for keeping down the top of

[025-025] the boiler; and the top of the boiler must, therefore, be stayed nearly as

[025-026] much as if the stays connecting it with the furnace crowns did not exist.

[025-027] The large rivets passing through thimbles, sometimes used as stays for

[025-028] water spaces or boiler shells, are objectionable; as, from the great amount

[025-029] of hammering such rivets have to receive to form the heads, the iron

[025-030] becomes crystalline, so that the heads are liable to come off, and, indeed,

[025-031] sometimes fly off in the act of being formed. If such a fracture occurs

[025-032] between the boilers after they are seated in their place, or in any

[025-033] position not accessible from the outside, it will in general be necessary

[025-034] to empty the faulty boiler, and repair the defect from the inside.

[025-035] 305. _Q._--What should be the pitch or numerical distribution of the stays?

[025-036] _A._--The stays, where the sides of the boiler are flat, and the pressure

[025-037] of the steam is from 20 to 30 lbs., should be pitched about a foot or 18

[025-038] inches asunder; and in the wake of the tubes, where stays cannot be carried

[025-039] across to connect the boiler sides, angle iron ribs, like the ribs of a

[025-040] ship, should be riveted to the interior of the boiler, and stays of greater

[025-041] strength than the rest should pass across, above, and below the tubes, to

[025-042] which the angle irons would communicate the strain. The whole of the long

[025-043] stays within a boiler should be firmly riveted to the shell, as if built

[025-044] with and forming a part of it; as, by the common method of fixing them in

[025-045] by means of cutters, the decay or accidental detachment of a pin or cutter

[025-046] may endanger the safety of the boiler. Wherever a large perforation in the

[025-047] shell of any circular boiler occurs, a sufficient number of stays should be

[025-048] put across it to maintain the original strength; and where stays are

[025-049] intercepted by the root of the funnel, short stays in continuation of them

[025-050] should be placed inside.

[025-051] BOILER EXPLOSIONS.

[025-052] 306. _Q._--What is the chief cause of boiler explosions?

[025-053] _A._--The chief cause of boiler explosions is, undoubtedly, too great a

[025-054] pressure of steam, or an insufficient strength of boiler; but many

[025-055] explosions have also arisen from the flues having been suffered to become

[025-056] red hot. If the safety valve of a boiler be accidentally jammed, or if the

[025-057] plates or stays be much worn by corrosion, while a high pressure of steam

[025-058] is nevertheless maintained, the boiler necessarily bursts; and if, from an

[025-059] insufficiency of water in the boiler, or from any other cause, the flues

[025-060] become highly heated, they may be forced down by the pressure of the steam,

[025-061] and a partial explosion may be the result. The worst explosion is where the

[025-062] shell of the boiler bursts; but the collapse of a furnace or flue is also

[025-063] very disastrous generally to the persons in the engine room; and sometimes

[025-064] the shell bursts and the flues collapse at the same time; for if the flues

[025-065] get red hot, and water be thrown upon them either by the feed pump or

[025-066] otherwise, the generation of steam may be too rapid for the safety valve to

[025-067] permit its escape with sufficient facility, and the shell of the boiler

[025-068] may, in consequence, be rent asunder. Sometimes the iron of the flues

[025-069] becomes highly heated in consequence of the improper configuration of the

[025-070] parts, which, by retaining the steam in contact with the metal, prevents

[025-071] the access of the water: the bottoms of large flues, upon which the flame

[025-072] beats down, are very liable to injury from this cause; and the iron of

[025-073] flues thus acted upon may be so softened that the flues will collapse

[025-074] upward with the pressure of the steam. The flues of boilers may also become

[025-075] red hot in some parts from the attachment of scale, which, from its

[025-076] imperfect conducting power, will cause the iron to be unduly heated; and if

[025-077] the scale be accidentally detached, a partial explosion may occur in

[025-078] consequence.

[025-079] 307. _Q._--Does the contact of water with heated metal occasion an

[025-080] instantaneous generation of steam?

[025-081] _A._--It is found that a sudden disengagement of steam does not immediately

[025-082] follow the contact of water with the hot metal, for water thrown upon red

[025-083] hot iron is not immediately converted into steam, but assumes the

[025-084] spheroidal form and rolls about in globules over the surface. These

[025-085] globules, however high the temperature of the metal may be on which they

[025-086] are placed, never rise above the temperature of 205°, and give off but very

[025-087] little steam; but if the temperature of the metal be lowered, the water

[025-088] ceases to retain the spheroidal form, and comes into intimate contact with

[025-089] the metal, whereby a rapid disengagement of steam takes place. If water be

[025-090] poured into a very hot copper flask, the flask may be corked up, as there

[025-091] will be scarce any steam produced so long as the high temperature is

[025-092] maintained; but so soon as the temperature is suffered to fall below 350°

[025-093] or 400°, the spheroidal condition being no longer maintainable, steam is

[025-094] generated with rapidity, and the cork will be projected from the mouth of

[025-095] the flask with great force.

[025-096] 308. _Q._--What precautions can be taken to prevent boiler explosions?

[025-097] _A._--One useful precaution against the explosion of boilers from too great

[025-098] an internal pressure, consists in the application of a steam gauge to each

[025-099] boiler, which will make the existence of any undue pressure in any of the

[025-100] boilers immediately visible; and every boiler should have a safety valve of

[025-101] its own, the passage leading to which should have no connection with the

[025-102] passage leading to any of the stop valves used to cut off the connection

[025-103] between the boilers; so that the action of the safety valve may be made

[025-104] independent of the action of the stop valve. In some cases stop valves have

[025-105] jammed, or have been carried from their seats into the mouth of the pipe

[025-106] communicating between them, and the action of the safety valves should be

[025-107] rendered independent of all such accidents. Safety valves, themselves,

[025-108] sometimes stick fast from corrosion, from the spindles becoming bent, from

[025-109] a distortion of the boiler top with a high pressure, in consequence of

[025-110] which the spindles become jammed in the guides, and from various other

[025-111] causes which it would be tedious to enumerate; but the inaction of the

[025-112] safety valves is at once indicated by the steam gauge, and when discovered,

[025-113] the blow through valves of the engine and blow off cocks of the boiler

[025-114] should at once be opened, and the fires raked out. A cone in the ball of

[025-115] the waste steam pipe to send back the water carried upward by the steam,

[025-116] should never be inserted; as in some cases this cone has become loose, and

[025-117] closed up the mouth of the waste steam pipe, whereby the safety valves

[025-118] being rendered inoperative, the boiler was in danger of bursting.

[025-119] 309. _Q._--May not danger arise from excessive priming?

[025-120] _A._--If the water be carried out of the boiler so rapidly by priming that

[025-121] the level of the water cannot be maintained, and the flues or furnaces are

[025-122] in danger of becoming red hot, the best plan is to open every furnace door

[025-123] and throw in a few buckets full of water upon the fire, taking care to

[025-124] stand sufficiently to the one side to avoid being scalded by the rush of

[025-125] steam from the furnace. There is no time to begin drawing the fires in such

[025-126] an emergency, and by this treatment the fires, though not altogether

[025-127] extinguished, will be rendered incapable of doing harm. If the flues be

[025-128] already red hot, on no account must cold water be suffered to enter the

[025-129] boiler, but the heat should be maintained in the furnaces, and the blow off

[025-130] cocks be opened, or the mud hole doors loosened, so as to let all the water

[025-131] escape; but at the same time the pressure must be kept quite low in the

[025-132] boiler, so that there will be no danger of the hot flues collapsing with

[025-133] the pressure of the steam.

[025-134] 310. _Q._--Are plugs of fusible metal useful in preventing explosions?

[025-135] _A._--Plugs of fusible metal were at one time in much repute as a

[025-136] precaution against explosion, the metal being so compounded that it melted

[025-137] with the heat of high pressure steam; but the device, though ingenious, has

[025-138] not been found of any utility in practice. The basis of fusible metal is

[025-139] mercury, and it is found that the compound is not homogeneous, and that the

[025-140] mercury is forced by the pressure of the steam out of the interstices of

[025-141] the metal combined with it, leaving a porous metal which is not easily

[025-142] fusible, and which is, therefore, unable to perform its intended function.

[025-143] In locomotives, however, and also in some other boilers, a lead rivet is

[025-144] inserted with advantage in the crown of the fire box, which is melted out

[025-145] if the water becomes too low, and thus gives notice of the danger.

[025-146] 311. _Q._--May not explosion occur in marine boilers from the accumulation

[025-147] of salt on the flues?

[025-148] _A._--Yes, in marine boilers this is a constant source of danger, which is

[025-149] only to be met by attention on the part of the engineer. If the water in

[025-150] the boiler be suffered to become too salt, an incrustation of salt will

[025-151] take place on the furnaces, which may cause them to become red hot, and

[025-152] they may then be collapsed even by their own weight aided by a moderate

[025-153] pressure of steam. The expedients which should be adopted for preventing

[025-154] such an accumulation of salt from taking place within the boiler as will be

[025-155] injurious to it, properly fall under the head of the management of steam

[025-156] boilers, and will be explained in a subsequent chapter.
