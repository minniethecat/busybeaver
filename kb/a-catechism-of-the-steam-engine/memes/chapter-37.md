[037-001] 439. _Q._--Might not the eduction trunnions be immersed in water?

[037-002] _A._--In some cases a hollow, or lantern brass, about one third or one

[037-003] fourth the length of the packing space, and supplied with steam or water by

[037-004] a pipe, is introduced in the middle of the packing, so that if there be any

[037-005] leakage through the trunnion, it will be a leakage of steam or water, which

[037-006] will not vitiate the vacuum; but in ordinary cases this device will not be

[037-007] necessary, and it is not commonly employed. It is clear that there can be

[037-008] no buckling of the sides of the cylinder by the strain upon the trunnions,

[037-009] if the cylinder be made strong enough, and in cylinders of the ordinary

[037-010] thickness such an action has never been experienced; nor is it the fact,

[037-011] that the intermediate shaft of steam vessels, to which part alone the

[037-012] motion is communicated by the engine, requires to adapt itself to the

[037-013] altering forms of the vessel, as the engine and intermediate shaft are

[037-014] rigidly connected, although the paddle shaft requires to be capable of such

[037-015] an adaptation. Even if this objection existed, however, it could easily be

[037-016] met by making the crank pin of the ball and socket fashion, which would

[037-017] permit the position of the intermediate shaft, relatively with that of the

[037-018] cylinder, to be slightly changed, without throwing an undue strain upon any

[037-019] of the working parts.

[037-020] 440. _Q._--Is the trunk engine inferior to the oscillating?

[037-021] _A._--A very elegant and efficient arrangement of trunk engine suitable for

[037-022] paddle vessels has latterly been employed by Messrs. Rennie, of which all

[037-023] the parts resemble those of Penn's oscillating engine except that the

[037-024] cylinders are stationary instead of being movable; and a round trunk or

[037-025] pipe set upon the piston, and moving steam tight through the cylinder

[037-026] cover, enables the connecting rod which is fixed to the piston to vibrate

[037-027] within it to the requisite extent. But the vice of all trunk engines is

[037-028] that they are necessarily more wasteful of steam, as the large mass of

[037-029] metal entering into the composition of the trunk, moving as it does

[037-030] alternately into the atmosphere and the steam, must cool and condense a

[037-031] part of the steam. The radiation of heat from the interior of the trunk

[037-032] will have the same operation, though in vertical trunk engines the loss

[037-033] from this cause might probably be reduced by filling the trunk with oil, so

[037-034] far as this could be done without the oil being spilt over the edge.

[037-035] 441. _Q._--What species of screw engine do you consider the best?

[037-036] _A._--I am inclined to give the preference to a variety of the horizontal

[037-037] steeple engine, such as was first used in H.M.S. Amphion. In this engine

[037-038] the cylinders lie on their sides, and they are placed near the side of the

[037-039] vessel with their mouths pointing to the keel. From each cylinder two long

[037-040] piston rods proceed across the vessel to a cross head working in guides;

[037-041] and from this cross head a connecting rod returns back to the centre of the

[037-042] vessel and gives motion to the crank. The piston rods are so placed in the

[037-043] piston that one of them passes above the crank shaft, and the other below

[037-044] the crank shaft. The cross head lies in the same horizontal plane as the

[037-045] centre of the cylinder, and a lug projects upwards from the cross head to

[037-046] engage one piston rod, and downwards from the cross head to engage the

[037-047] other piston rod. The air pump is double acting, and its piston or bucket

[037-048] has the same stroke as the piston of the engine. The air pump bucket

[037-049] derives its motion from an arm on the cross head, and a similar arm is

[037-050] usually employed in engines of this class to work the feed and bilge pumps.

[037-051] 442. _Q._--Is not inconvenience experienced in direct acting screw engines

[037-052] from the great velocity of their motion?

[037-053] _A._--Not if they are properly constructed; but they require to be much

[037-054] stronger, to be fitted with more care, and to have the bearing surfaces

[037-055] much larger than is necessary in engines moving slowly. The momentum of the

[037-056] reciprocating parts should also be balanced by a weight applied to the

[037-057] crank or crank shaft, as is done in locomotives. A very convenient

[037-058] arrangement for obtaining surface is to form the crank of each engine of

[037-059] two cast iron discs cast with heavy sides, the excess of weight upon the

[037-060] heavy sides being nearly equal to that of the piston and its connections.

[037-061] When the piston is travelling in one direction the weights are travelling

[037-062] in the opposite; and the momentum of the piston and its attachments, which

[037-063] is arrested at each reciprocation, is just balanced by the equal and

[037-064] opposite momentum of the weights. One advantage of the horizontal engine

[037-065] is, that a single engine may be employed, whereby greater simplicity of the

[037-066] machinery and greater economy of fuel will be obtained, since there will be

[037-067] less radiating surface in one cylinder than in two.

[037-069] 443. _Q._--Is it a beneficial practice to make cylinders with steam

[037-070] jackets?

[037-071] _A._--In Cornwall, where great attention is paid to economy of fuel, all

[037-072] the engines are made with steam jackets, and in some cases a flue winds

[037-073] spirally round the cylinder, for keeping the steam hot. Mr. Watt, in his

[037-074] early practice, discarded the steam jacket for a time, but resumed it

[037-075] again, as he found its discontinuance occasioned a perceptible waste of

[037-076] fuel; and in modern engines it has been found that where a jacket is used

[037-077] less coal is consumed than where the use of a jacket is rejected. The cause

[037-078] of this diminished effect is not of very easy perception, for the jacket

[037-079] exposes a larger radiating surface for the escape of the heat than the

[037-080] cylinder; nevertheless, the fact has been established beyond doubt by

[037-081] repeated trials, that engines provided with a jacket are more economical

[037-082] than engines without one. The exterior of the cylinder, or jacket, should

[037-083] be covered with several plies of felt, and then be cased in timber, which

[037-084] must be very narrow, the boards being first dried in a stove, and then

[037-085] bound round the cylinder with hoops, like the staves of a cask. In many of

[037-086] the Cornish engines the steam is let into casings formed in the cylinder

[037-087] cover and cylinder bottom, for the further economisation of the heat, and

[037-088] the cylinder stuffing box is made very deep, and a lantern or hollow brass

[037-089] is introduced into the centre of the packing, into which brass the steam

[037-090] gains admission by a pipe provided for the purpose; so that in the event of

[037-091] the packing becoming leaky, it will be steam that will be leaked into the

[037-092] cylinder instead of air, which, being incondensable, would impair the

[037-093] efficiency of the engine. A lantern brass, of a similar kind, is sometimes

[037-094] introduced into the stuffing boxes of oscillating engines, but its use

[037-095] there is to receive the lateral pressure of the piston rod, and thus take

[037-096] any strain off the packing.

[037-097] 444. _Q._--Will you explain the proper course to pursue in the production

[037-098] of cylinders?

[037-099] _A._--In all engines the valve casing, if made in a separate piece from the

[037-100] cylinder, should be attached by means of a metallic joint, as such a

[037-101] barbarism as a rust joint in such situations is no longer permissible. In

[037-102] the case of large engines with valve casings suitable for long slides, an

[037-103] expansion joint in the valve casing should invariably be inserted,

[037-104] otherwise the steam, by gaining admission to the valve casing before it can

[037-105] enter the cylinder, expands the casing while the cylinder remains unaltered

[037-106] in its dimensions, and the joints are damaged, and in some cases the

[037-107] cylinder is cracked by the great strain thus introduced. The chest of the

[037-108] blow-through valve is very commonly cast upon the valve casing; and in

[037-109] engines where the cylinders are stationary this is the most convenient

[037-110] practice. All engines, where the valve is not of such a construction as to

[037-111] leave the face when a pressure exceeding that of the steam is created in

[037-112] the cylinder by priming or otherwise, should be provided with an escape

[037-113] valve to let out the water, and such valve should be so constructed that

[037-114] the water cannot fly out with violence over the attendants; but it should

[037-115] be conducted away by a suitable pipe, to a place where its discharge can

[037-116] occasion no inconvenience. The stuffing boxes of all engines which cannot

[037-117] be stopped frequently to be repacked, should be made very deep; metallic

[037-118] packing in the stuffing box has been used in some engines, consisting in

[037-119] most instances of one or more rings, cut, sprung, and slipped upon the

[037-120] piston rod before the cross head is put on, and packed with hemp behind.

[037-121] This species of packing answers very well when the parallel motion is true,

[037-122] and the piston rod free from scratches, and it accomplishes a material

[037-123] saving of tallow. In some cases a piece of sheet brass, packed behind with

[037-124] hemp, has been introduced with good effect, a flange being turned over on

[037-125] the under edge of the brass to prevent it from slipping up or down with the

[037-126] motion of the rod. The sheet brass speedily puts an excellent polish upon

[037-127] the rod, and such a packing is more easily kept, and requires less tallow

[037-128] than where hemp alone is employed. In side lever marine engines the

[037-129] attachments of the cylinder to the diagonal stay are generally made of too

[037-130] small an area, and the flanges are made too thick. A very thick flange cast

[037-131] on any part of a cylinder endangers the soundness of the cylinder, by

[037-132] inducing an unequal contraction of the metal; and it is a preferable course

[037-133] to make the flange for the attachment or the framing thin, and the surface

[037-134] large--the bolts being turned bolts and nicely fitted. If from malformation

[037-135] in this part the framing works to an inconvenient extent, the best

[037-136] expedient appears to be the introduction of a number of steel tapered

[037-137] bolts, the holes having been previously bored out; and if the flanges be

[037-138] thick enough, square keys may also be introduced, half into one flange and

[037-139] half into the other, so as to receive the strain. If the jaw cracks or

[037-140] breaks away, however, it will be best to apply a malleable iron hoop around

[037-141] the cylinder to take the strain, and this will in all cases be the

[037-142] preferable expedient, where from any peculiarities of structure there is a

[037-143] difficulty in introducing bolts and keys of sufficient strength.

[037-144] 445. _Q._--Which is the most eligible species of piston?

[037-145] _A._--For large engines, pistons with a metallic packing, consisting of a

[037-146] single ring, with the ends morticed into one another, and a piece of metal

[037-147] let in flush over the joint and riveted to one end of the ring, appears to

[037-148] be the best species of piston; and if the cylinder be oscillating, it will

[037-149] be expedient to chamfer off the upper edge of the ring on the inner side,

[037-150] and to pack it at the back with hemp. If the cylinder be a stationary one,

[037-151] springs may be substituted for the hemp packing, but in any case it will be

[037-152] expedient to make the vertical joints of the ends of the ring run a little

[037-153] obliquely, so as to prevent the joint forming a ridge in the cylinder. For

[037-154] small pistons two rings may be employed, made somewhat eccentric internally

[037-155] to give a greater thickness of metal in the centre of the ring; these rings

[037-156] must be set one above the other in the cylinder, and the joints, which are

[037-157] oblique, must be set at right angles with one another, so as to obviate any

[037-158] disposition of the rings, in their expansion, to wear the cylinder oval.

[037-159] The rings must first be turned a little larger than the diameter of the

[037-160] cylinder, and a piece is then to be cut out, so that when the ends are

[037-161] brought together the ring will just enter within the cylinder. The ring,

[037-162] while retained in a state of compression, is then to be put in the lathe

[037-163] and turned very truly, and finally it is to be hammered on the inside with

[037-164] the small end of the hammer, to expand the metal, and thus increase the

[037-165] elasticity.
