[056-001] 643. _Q._--What is the advantage of two discs entering into the composition

[056-002] of the crank instead of one?

[056-003] _A._--A double crank, such as two discs form with the crank pin, is a much

[056-004] steadier combination than would result if only one disc were employed with

[056-005] an over-hung pin. Then the friction on the neck of the shaft is made one

[056-006] half less by being divided between the two bearings, and the short

[056-007] prolongation of the shaft beyond the journal is convenient for the

[056-008] attachment of the eccentrics to work the valves.

[056-009] 644. _Q._--Will you enumerate some of the principal dimensions of this

[056-010] engine?

[056-011] _A._--The bottom frame, on which also the condenser is cast, forms the base

[056-012] of the engine: on one end of it the cylinder is set; on the other end are

[056-013] the guides for the cross head, and in the middle are the bearings for the

[056-014] crank shaft. The part where the cylinder stands is two feet high above the

[056-015] engine platform, and the elevation to the centre of the guides or the

[056-016] centre of the shaft is 10 inches higher than this. The metal both of the

[056-017] side frames and bottom flange is 1-1/4 inch thick. The cylinder has flanges

[056-018] cast on its sides, upon which it rests on the bottom frame, and it is sunk

[056-019] between the sides of the frame so as to bring the centre of the cylinder in

[056-020] the same plane as the centre of the screw shaft. The opening left at the

[056-021] guides for the reception of the guide blocks is 6 inches deep, and the

[056-022] breadth of the bearing surface is 11 inches. The cover of the guides is 8

[056-023] inches deep at the middle, and about half the depth at the ends, and holes

[056-024] are cored through the central web for two oil cups on each guide. The brass

[056-025] for each of the crank shaft bearings is cut into four pieces so that it may

[056-026] be tightened in the up and down direction by the bolts, which secure the

[056-027] plummer block cap, and tightened in the athwartship direction, which is the

[056-028] direction of the strain, by screwing up a wedge-formed plate against the

[056-029] side of the brass, a parallel plate being applied to the other side of the

[056-030] brass, which may be withdrawn to get out the wedge piece when the shaft

[056-031] requires to be lifted out of its place. The air pump is bolted to one side

[056-032] of the bottom frame, and a passage is cast on it conducting from the

[056-033] condenser to the air pump. In this passage the inlet and outlet valves at

[056-034] each end of the air pump are situated, and appropriate doors are formed

[056-035] above them to make them easily accessible. The outlet passage leading from

[056-036] the air pump communicates with the waste water pipe, through which the

[056-037] water expelled by the air pump is discharged overboard.

[056-038] 645. _Q._--Is the cylinder of the usual strength and configuration?

[056-039] _A._--The cylinder is formed of cast iron in the usual way, and is 1-1/8

[056-040] inch thick in the barrel. The ends are of the same thickness, but are each

[056-041] stiffened with six strong feathers. The piston is cast open. The bottom of

[056-042] it is 5/8ths of an inch thick, and it is stiffened by six feathers 3/4 of

[056-043] an inch thick; but the feather connecting the piston rod eyes is 1-1/4 inch

[056-044] thick, and the metal round the eyes is 2 inches thick. The piston is closed

[056-045] by a disc or cover 5/8ths of an inch thick, secured by 15 bolts, and this

[056-046] cover answers also the purpose of a junk ring. The piston packing consists

[056-047] of a single cast iron ring 3-1/2 inches broad, and 1/2 inch thick, packed

[056-048] behind with hemp. This ring is formed with a tongue piece, with an indented

[056-049] plate behind the cut; and the cut is oblique to prevent a ridge forming in

[056-050] the cylinder. The total thickness of the piston is 5-1/2 inches. The piston

[056-051] rods are formed with conical ends for fitting into the piston, but are

[056-052] coned the reverse way as in locomotives, and are secured in the piston by

[056-053] nuts on the ends of the rods, these nuts being provided with ratchets to

[056-054] prevent them from unscrewing accidentally.

[056-055] 646. _Q._--What species of slide valve is employed?

[056-056] _A._--The ordinary three ported valve, and it is set on the top of the

[056-057] cylinder. The cylinder ports are 4-1/2 inches broad by 24 inches long; and

[056-058] to relieve the valve from the great friction due to the pressure on so

[056-059] large a surface, a balance piston is placed over the back of the valve, to

[056-060] which it is connected by a strong link; and the upward pressure on this

[056-061] piston being nearly the same as the downward pressure on the valve, it

[056-062] follows that the friction is extinguished, and the valve can be moved with

[056-063] great case with one hand. The balance piston is 21 inches in diameter. In

[056-064] the original construction of this balance piston two faults were committed.

[056-065] The passage communicating between the condenser and the top of the balance

[056-066] piston was too small, and the pins at the ends of the link connecting the

[056-067] valve and balance piston were formed with an inadequate amount of bearing

[056-068] surface. It followed from this misproportion that the balance piston, being

[056-069] adjusted to take off nearly the whole of the pressure, lifted the valve off

[056-070] the face at the beginning of each stroke. For the escape of the steam into

[056-071] the eduction passage momentarily impaired the vacuum subsisting there, and

[056-072] owing to the smallness of the passage leading to the space above the

[056-073] balance piston, the vacuum subsisting in that space could not be impaired

[056-074] with equal rapidity. The balance piston, therefore, rose by the upward

[056-075] pressure upon it momentarily predominating over the downward pressure on

[056-076] the valve; but this fault was corrected by enlarging the communicating

[056-077] passage between the top of the balance piston and the eduction pipe. The

[056-078] smallness of the pins at the ends of the link connecting the valve and

[056-079] balance piston, caused the surfaces to cut into one another, and to wear

[056-080] very rapidly, and the pins and eyes in this situation should be large in

[056-081] diameter, and as long as they can be got, as they are not so easily

[056-082] lubricated as the other bearings about the engine, and are moreover kept at

[056-083] a high temperature by the steam. The balance piston is packed in the same

[056-084] way as the main piston of the engine. Its cylinder, which is only a few

[056-085] inches in length, is set on the top of the valve casing, and a trunk

[056-086] projects upwards from its centre to enable the connecting link to rise up

[056-087] in it to attain the necessary length.

[056-089] 647. _Q._--What is the diameter of the piston rods and connecting rod?

[056-090] _A._--The piston rods, which are two in number, are 3 inches diameter, and

[056-091] 12 feet 10 inches long over all. They were, however, found to be rather

[056-092] small, and have since been made half an inch thicker. The connecting rod

[056-093] consists of two rods, which are prolongations of the bolts that connect the

[056-094] sides of the brass bushes which encircle the crank pin and cross head. The

[056-095] connecting rod is shown in perspective in fig. 52. The rods composing it

[056-096] are each 2-3/4 inches in diameter.

[056-097] 648. _Q._--Will you describe the configuration of the cross head.

[056-098] _A._--The cross head, exhibited in fig. 53, is a round piece of iron like a

[056-099] short shaft, with two unequal arms keyed upon it, the longer of which _b_

[056-100] works the air pump, and the shorter _c_ works the feed pump. The piston

[056-101] rods enter these arms at _a A._ The cross head is 8 inches diameter where

[056-102] it is embraced by the connecting rod at _e_, and 7 inches diameter where

[056-103] the air pump and feed pump arms are fixed on. The ends of the cross head _d

[056-104] d_, for a length of 12 inches, are reduced to 3 inches diameter where they

[056-105] fit into round holes in the centre of the guide blocks. Those blocks are of

[056-106] cast iron 6 inches deep, 11 inches wide, and 14 inches long, and they are

[056-107] formed with flanges 1 inch thick on the inner sides of the blocks. The

[056-108] projection of the air pump lever from the centre of the cross head is 1

[056-109] foot 9 inches, and it is bent 5-3/4 inches to one side to enable it to

[056-110] engage the air pump rod. The eye of this arm is 6 inches broad and about 2

[056-111] inches thick. At the part where one of the piston rods passes through it,

[056-112] the arm is 8 inches deep and 6 inches wide; but the width thereafter

[056-113] narrows to 3 inches, and finally to 2 inches; and the depth of the web of

[056-114] the arm reduces from 8 inches at the piston rod, to 4 inches at the eye,

[056-115] which receives the end of the air pump rod. The feed pump arm is only 3

[056-116] inches thick, and has 9 inches of projection from the centre of the cross

[056-117] head; but the eye attached to it on the opposite side of the cross head for

[056-118] the reception of the other piston rod is of the same length as that part of

[056-119] the air pump arm which one of the piston rods passes through. The piston

[056-120] rods have strong nuts on each side of each of these arms to attach them to

[056-121] the arms, and also to enable the length of the piston rods to be suitably

[056-122] adjusted, to leave equal clearance between the piston and each end of the

[056-123] cylinder at the termination of the stroke.

[056-126] 649. _Q._--Will you recapitulate the main particulars of the air pump?

[056-127] _A._--The air pump is made of brass 12-1/2 inches diameter and 42 inches

[056-128] stroke, and the metal of the barrel is 9/16ths of an inch thick. The air

[056-129] pump bucket is a solid piston of brass, 6-1/2 inches deep at the edge, and

[056-130] 7 inches deep at the eye; and in the edge three grooves are turned to hold

[056-131] water which answers the purpose of packing. The inlet and outlet valves of

[056-132] the air pump consist of brass plates 1/2 inch with strong feathers across

[056-133] them, and in each plate there are six grated perforations covered by india

[056-134] rubber discs 7 inches in diameter. These six perforations afford

[056-135] collectively an area for the passage of the water equal to the area of the

[056-136] pump. The air pump rod is of brass, 2-1/2 inches diameter.

[056-137] 650. _Q._--What are the constructive peculiarities of the discs and crank

[056-138] pin?

[056-139] _A._--The discs, which are 64 inches diameter, are formed of cast iron, and

[056-140] are 2-1/2 inches thick in the body, and 5 inches broad at the rim. The

[056-141] crank shaft is 8-1/2 inches diameter, and the central boss of the disc

[056-142] which receives the shaft measures 10 inches through the eye, and the metal

[056-143] of the eye is 3 inches thick. In the part of the disc opposite to the crank

[056-144] pin, the web is thickened to 10 inches for nearly the whole semicircle,

[056-145] with the view of making that side of the disc heavier than the other side;

[056-146] and when the engine is stopped, the gravitation of this heavy side raises

[056-147] the crank pin to the highest point it can attain, whereby it is placed in

[056-148] mid stroke, and cannot rest with the piston rods and connecting rod in a

[056-149] horizontal line. The crank pin is 8-1/2 inches diameter, and the length of

[056-150] the bearing or rubbing part of it is 16 inches. It is secured at the ends

[056-151] to the discs by flanges 18 inches diameter, and 2 inches thick. These

[056-152] flanges are indented into thickened parts of the discs, and are each

[056-153] attached to its corresponding disc by six bolts 2 inches diameter,

[056-154] countersunk in the back of the disc, and tapped into the malleable iron

[056-155] flange. Besides this attachment, each end of the pin, reduced to 4-1/2

[056-156] inches diameter, passes through a hole in its corresponding disc, and the

[056-157] ends of the pin are then riveted over. The crank pin is perforated through

[056-158] the centre by a small hole about 3/4 of an inch in diameter, and three

[056-159] perforations proceed from this central hole to the surface of the pin. Each

[056-160] crank shaft bearing is similarly perforated, and pipes are cast in the

[056-161] discs connecting these perforations together. The result of this

[056-162] arrangement is, that a large part of the oil or water fed into the bearings

[056-163] of the shaft is driven by the centrifugal action of the discs to the

[056-164] surface of the crank pin, and in this way the crank pin may be oiled or

[056-165] cooled with water in a very effectual manner. To intercept the water or oil

[056-166] which the discs thus drive out by their centrifugal action, a light paddle

[056-167] box or splash board of thin sheet brass is made to cover the upper part of

[056-168] each of the discs, and an oil cup with depending wick is supported by the

[056-169] tops of these paddle boxes, which wick is touched at each revolution of the

[056-170] crank by a bridge standing in the middle of an oil cup attached to the

[056-171] crank pin. The oil is wiped from the wick by the projecting bridge at each

[056-172] revolution, and subsides into the cup from whence it proceeds to lubricate

[056-173] the crank pin bearing. This is the expedient commonly employed to oil the

[056-174] crank pins of direct acting engines; but in the engine now described, there

[056-175] are over and above this expedient, the communicating passages from the

[056-176] shaft bearings to the surface of the pin, by which means any amount of

[056-177] cooling or lubrication can be administered to the crank pin bearing,

[056-178] without the necessity of stopping or slowing the engine.
