The **equilibrium valve** is a form of [[valve|steam valve]] in which the steam pressure on the two sides is balanced, so that the valve may be moved easily without the friction that would arise from the pressure of steam on one side. It was developed for use in large [[marine-engine|marine engines]] and stationary engines where the force required to move an unbalanced valve against full steam pressure would be prohibitive. The equilibrium valve is closely related to the [[slide-valve]] but differs in being pressure-balanced.

## Construction

> [016-016] This valve is represented in fig. 34. It consists substantially of an annulus or bulging cylinder of brass, with a steam-tight face both at its upper and lower edges, at which points it fits accurately upon a stationary seat. This annulus may be raised or lowered without getting off the seat.

The valve body is essentially a hollow cylinder or ring (annulus) that slides over a fixed internal piston or seat. [[steam|Steam]] can pass around and through the valve body, but because equal areas are exposed to steam pressure on opposite faces, the net force pushing the valve against its seat is nearly zero:

> [036-039] This valve consists substantially of a cylinder open at both ends, and capable of sliding upon a stationary piston fixed upon a rod the length of the cylinder, which proceeds from the centre of the orifice the valve controls.

## Principle of equilibrium

The name derives from the balance (equilibrium) of forces. In an ordinary valve, full steam pressure on one side forces the valve against its seating surface, creating friction proportional to the pressure. In the equilibrium valve, the steam is admitted to both sides in such a way that the pressure forces cancel, leaving only the modest effort needed to overcome the valve's own inertia and the friction of its spindle packing.

## Usage as a throttle or stop valve

The equilibrium valve found its primary application as a [[valve|throttle valve]] or stop valve, where it had to be opened and closed against full boiler pressure:

> [038-081] Might not an equilibrium valve be so constructed by the interposition of springs, as to enable it to leave the cylinder face when required, yet press sufficiently to maintain it in contact with the seat?

## Compromise designs

For [[double-acting|double-acting engine]]s, a compromise design known as the **double-ported valve** was developed, combining features of the equilibrium valve with the common [[stop-valve|spindle valve]]:

> [036-035] This valve is a compromise between the equilibrium valve, of the kind employed for admitting the steam to and from the cylinder in single acting engines, and the common spindle valve formerly used for that purpose.

## Pump valve application

[[john-bourne|Bourne]] cautioned that the equilibrium valve requires mechanical actuation when used as a pump valve, since pressure balance means no self-closing force:

> [036-061] If an equilibrium valve, therefore, be used as a pump valve at all, it must be opened and shut by mechanical means.
