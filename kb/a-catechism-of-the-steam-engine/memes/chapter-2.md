[002-001] 4. _Q._--Is not the circular motion derived from a cylinder engine very

[002-002] irregular, in consequence of the unequal leverage of the crank at the

[002-003] different parts of its revolution?

[002-004] _A._--No; rotative engines are generally provided with a fly-wheel to

[002-005] correct such irregularities by its momentum; but where two engines with

[002-006] their respective cranks set at right angles are employed, the irregularity

[002-007] of one engine corrects that of the other with sufficient exactitude for

[002-008] many purposes. In the case of marine and locomotive engines, a fly-wheel is

[002-009] not employed; but for cotton spinning, and other purposes requiring great

[002-010] regularity of motion, its use with common engines is indispensable, though

[002-011] it is not impossible to supersede the necessity by new contrivances.

[002-012] 5. _Q._--You implied that there is some other difference between single

[002-013] acting and double acting engines, than that which lies in the use or

[002-014] exclusion of the crank?

[002-015] _A._--Yes; single acting engines act only in one way by the force of the

[002-016] steam, and are returned by a counter-weight; whereas double acting engines

[002-017] are urged by the steam in both directions. Engines, as I have already said,

[002-018] are sometimes made double acting, though unprovided with a crank; and there

[002-019] would be no difficulty in so arranging the valves of all ordinary pumping

[002-020] engines, as to admit of this action; for the pumps might be contrived to

[002-021] raise water both by the upward and downward stroke, as indeed in some mines

[002-022] is already done. But engines without a crank are almost always made single

[002-023] acting, perhaps from the effect of custom, as much as from any other

[002-024] reason, and are usually spoken of as such, though it is necessary to know

[002-025] that there are some deviations from the usual practice.

[002-026] NATURE AND USES OF A VACUUM.

[002-027] 6. _Q._--The pressure of a vacuum you have stated is nothing; but how can

[002-028] the pressure of a vacuum be said to be nothing, when a vacuum occasions a

[002-029] pressure of 15 lbs. on the square inch?

[002-030] _A._--Because it is not the vacuum which exerts this pressure, but the

[002-031] atmosphere, which, like a head of water, presses on everything immerged

[002-032] beneath it. A head of water, however, would not press down a piston, if the

[002-033] water were admitted on both of its sides; for an equilibrium would then be

[002-034] established, just as in the case of a balance which retains its equilibrium

[002-035] when an equal weight is added to each scale; but take the weight out of one

[002-036] scale, or empty the water from one side of the piston, and motion or

[002-037] pressure is produced; and in like manner pressure is produced on a piston

[002-038] by admitting steam or air upon the one side, and withdrawing the steam or

[002-039] air from the other side. It is not, therefore, to a vacuum, but rather to

[002-040] the existence of an unbalanced plenum, that the pressure made manifest by

[002-041] exhaustion is due, and it is obvious therefore that a vacuum of itself

[002-042] would not work an engine.

[002-043] 7. _Q._--How is the vacuum maintained in a condensing engine?

[002-044] _A._--The steam, after having performed its office in the cylinder, is

[002-045] permitted to pass into a vessel called the condenser, where a shower of

[002-046] cold water is discharged upon it. The steam is condensed by the cold water,

[002-047] and falls in the form of hot water to the bottom of the condenser. The

[002-048] water, which would else be accumulated in the condenser, is continually

[002-049] being pumped out by a pump worked by the engine. This pump is called the

[002-050] air pump, because it also discharges any air which may have entered with

[002-051] the water.

[002-052] 8. _Q._--If a vacuum be an empty space, and there be water in the

[002-053] condenser, how can there be a vacuum there?

[002-054] _A._--There is a vacuum above the water, the water being only like so much

[002-055] iron or lead lying at the bottom.

[002-056] 9. _Q._--Is the vacuum in the condenser a perfect vacuum?

[002-057] _A._--Not quite perfect; for the cold water entering for the purpose of

[002-058] condensation is heated by the steam, and emits a vapor of a tension

[002-059] represented by about three inches of mercury; that is, when the common

[002-060] barometer stands at 30 inches, a barometer with the space above the mercury

[002-061] communicating with the condenser, will stand at about 27 inches.

[002-062] 10. _Q._--Is this imperfection of the vacuum wholly attributable to the

[002-063] vapor in the condenser?

[002-064] _A._--No; it is partly attributable to the presence of a small quantity of

[002-065] air which enters with the water, and which would accumulate until it

[002-066] destroyed the vacuum altogether but for the action of the air pump, which

[002-067] expels it with the water, as already explained. All common water contains a

[002-068] certain quantity of air in solution, and this air recovers its elasticity

[002-069] when the pressure of the atmosphere is taken off, just as the gas in soda

[002-070] water flies up so soon as the cork of the bottle is withdrawn.

[002-071] 11. _Q._--Is a barometer sometimes applied to the condensers of steam

[002-072] engines?

[002-073] _A._--Yes; and it is called the vacuum gauge, because it shows the degree

[002-074] of perfection the vacuum has attained. Another gauge, called the steam

[002-075] gauge, is applied to the boiler, which indicates the pressure of the steam

[002-076] by the height to which the steam forces mercury up a tube. Gauges are also

[002-077] applied to the boiler to indicate the height of the water within it so that

[002-078] it may not be burned out by the water becoming accidentally too low. In

[002-079] some cases a succession of cocks placed a short distance above one another

[002-080] are employed for this purpose, and in other cases a glass tube is placed

[002-081] perpendicularly in the front of the boiler and communicating at each end

[002-082] with its interior. The water rises in this tube to the same height as in

[002-083] the boiler itself, and thus shows the actual water level. In most of the

[002-084] modern boilers both of these contrivances are adopted.

[002-085] 12. _Q._--Can a condensing engine be worked with a pressure less than that

[002-086] of the atmosphere?

[002-087] _A._--Yes, if once it be started; but it will be a difficult thing to start

[002-088] an engine, if the pressure of the steam be not greater than that of the

[002-089] atmosphere. Before an engine can be started, it has to be blown through

[002-090] with steam to displace the air within it, and this cannot be effectually

[002-091] done if the pressure of the steam be very low. After the engine is started,

[002-092] however, the pressure in the boiler may be lowered, if the engine be

[002-093] lightly loaded, until there is a partial vacuum in the boiler. Such a

[002-094] practice, however, is not to be commended, as the gauge cocks become

[002-095] useless when there is a partial vacuum in the boiler; inasmuch as, when

[002-096] they are opened, the water will not rush out, but air will rush in. It is

[002-097] impossible, also, under such circumstances, to blow out any of the sediment

[002-098] collected within the boiler, which, in the case of the boilers of steam

[002-099] vessels, requires to be done every two hours or oftener. This is

[002-100] accomplished by opening a large cock which permits some of the supersalted

[002-101] water to be forced overboard by the pressure of the steam. In some cases,

[002-102] in which the boiler applied to an engine is of inadequate size, the

[002-103] pressure within the boiler will fall spontaneously to a point considerably

[002-104] beneath the pressure of the atmosphere; but it is preferable, in such

[002-105] cases, partially to close the throttle valve in the steam pipe, whereby the

[002-106] issue of steam to the engine is diminished; and the pressure in the boiler

[002-107] is thus maintained, while the cylinder receives its former supply.

[002-108] 13. _Q._--If a hole be opened into a condenser of a steam engine, will air

[002-109] rush into it?

[002-110] _A._--If the hole communicates with the atmosphere, the air will be drawn

[002-111] in.

[002-112] 14. _Q._--With what Velocity does air rush into a vacuum?

[002-113] _A._--With the velocity which a body would acquire by falling from the

[002-114] height of a homogeneous atmosphere, which is an atmosphere of the same

[002-115] density throughout as at the earth's surface; and although such an

[002-116] atmosphere does not exist in nature, its existence is supposed, in order to

[002-117] facilitate the computation. It is well known that the velocity with which

[002-118] water issues from a cistern is the same that would be acquired by a body

[002-119] falling from the level of the head to the level of the issuing point; which

[002-120] indeed is an obvious law, since every particle of water descends and issues

[002-121] by virtue of its gravity, and is in its descent subject to the ordinary

[002-122] laws of falling bodies. Air rushing into a vacuum is only another example

[002-123] of the same general principle: the velocity of each particle will be that

[002-124] due to the height of the column of air which would produce the pressure

[002-125] sustained; and the weight of air being known, as well as the pressure it

[002-126] exerts on the earth's surface, it becomes easy to tell what height a column

[002-127] of air, an inch square, and of the atmospheric density, would require to

[002-128] be, to weigh 15 lbs. The height would be 27,818 feet, and the velocity

[002-129] which the fall of a body from such a height produces would be 1,338 feet

[002-130] per second.

[002-131] VELOCITY OF FALLING BODIES AND MOMENTUM OF MOVING BODIES.

[002-132] 15. _Q._--How do you determine the velocity of falling bodies of different

[002-133] kinds?

[002-134] _A._--All bodies fall with the same velocity, when there is no resistance

[002-135] from the atmosphere, as is shown by the experiment of letting fall, from

[002-136] the top of a tall exhausted receiver, a feather and a guinea, which reach

[002-137] the bottom at the same time. The velocity of falling bodies is one that is

[002-138] accelerated uniformly, according to a known law. When the height from which

[002-139] a body falls is given, the velocity acquired at the end of the descent can

[002-140] be easily computed. It has been found by experiment that the square root of

[002-141] the height in feet multiplied by 8.021 will give the velocity.

[002-142] 16. _Q._--But the velocity in what terms?

[002-143] _A._--In feet per second. The distance through which a body falls by

[002-144] gravity in one second is 16-1/12 feet; in two seconds, 64-4/12 feet; in

[002-145] three seconds, 144-9/12 feet; in four seconds, 257-4/12 feet, and so on. If

[002-146] the number of feet fallen through in one second be taken as unity, then the

[002-147] relation of the times to the spaces will be as follows:--

[002-148] Number of seconds               | 1| 2| 3| 4| 5| 6|

[002-149] Units of space passed through   | 1| 4| 9|16|25|36| &c.

[002-150] so that it appears that the spaces passed through by a falling body are as

[002-151] the squares of the times of falling.

[002-152] 17. _Q._--Is not the urging force which causes bodies to fall the force of

[002-153] gravity?

[002-154] _A._--Yes; the force of gravity or the attraction of the earth.

[002-155] 18. _Q._--And is not that a uniform force, or a force acting with a uniform

[002-156] pressure?

[002-157] _A._--It is.

[002-158] 19. _Q._--Therefore during the first second of falling as much impelling

[002-159] power will be given by the force of gravity as during every succeeding

[002-160] second?

[002-161] _A._--Undoubtedly.

[002-162] 20. _Q._--How comes it, then, that while the body falls 64-4/12 feet in two

[002-163] seconds, it falls only 16-1/12 feet in one second; or why, since it falls

[002-164] only 16-1/12 feet in one second, should it fall more than twice 16-1/12

[002-165] feet in two?

[002-166] _A._--Because 16-1/12 feet is the average and not the maximum velocity

[002-167] during the first second. The velocity acquired _at the end_ of the 1st

[002-168] second is not 16-1/12, but 32-1/6 feet per second, and at the end of the 2d

[002-169] second a velocity of 32-1/6 feet has to be added; so that the total

[002-170] velocity at the end of the 2d second becomes 64-2/6 feet; at the end of the

[002-171] 3d, the velocity becomes 96-3/6 feet, at the end of the 4th, 128-4/6 feet,

[002-172] and so on. These numbers proceed in the progression 1, 2, 3, 4, &c., so

[002-173] that it appears that the velocities acquired by a falling body at different

[002-174] points, are simply as the times of falling. But if the velocities be as the

[002-175] times, and the total space passed through be as the squares of the times,

[002-176] then the total space passed through must be as the squares of the velocity;

[002-177] and as the _vis viva_ or mechanical power inherent in a falling body, of

[002-178] any given weight, is measurable by the height through which it descends, it

[002-179] follows that the _vis viva_ is proportionate to the square of the velocity.

[002-180] Of two balls therefore, of equal weight, but one moving twice as fast as

[002-181] the other, the faster ball has four times the energy or mechanical force

[002-182] accumulated in it that the slower ball has. If the speed of a fly-wheel be

[002-183] doubled, it has four times the _vis viva_ it possessed before--_vis viva_

[002-184] being measurable by a reference to the height through which a body must

[002-185] have fallen, to acquire the velocity given.
