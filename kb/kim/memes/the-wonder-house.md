# The Wonder House

The Wonder House — the Ajaib-Gher in the native tongue — is the Lahore Museum, a grand building of colonial [[lahore|Lahore]] that houses one of the finest collections of Gandharan Buddhist art in the world. It is here, on its steps beside the great gun [[zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]], that the novel's two central figures — [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] — first meet, setting the entire narrative in motion.

> There were scores of such collections in the Wonder House. Then, too, there were things in cases that the Curator had collected himself — models of temples, and little plaster casts of the holy images of all the world.
>
> (001-095)

## In *Kim*

The Wonder House appears in the novel's opening scene and echoes throughout the narrative as a touchstone of [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s spiritual quest. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] arrives in [[lahore|Lahore]] having travelled from [[tibet|Tibet]], drawn by the reputation of the museum's Buddhist antiquities (001-002). He stands before the museum, marvelling: "The Ajaib-Gher, the Wonder House!" (001-024), and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], the street boy who knows every inch of [[lahore|Lahore]], directs him inside.

Inside the museum, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] encounters the [[the-curator|Curator]], a scholar of Buddhist art who speaks to him in his own tongue and shows him the sacred images. "The Sahib in the Wonder House has talked to him like a brother," [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] observes (001-120). This encounter emboldens [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] — the museum becomes for him a kind of shrine where knowledge is preserved and [[pilgrimage|pilgrim]]s are welcomed.

The Wonder House recurs in [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s memory as a place of wisdom and kindness. He speaks of "the Keeper of the Images in the Wonder House" (011-070) with reverence, crediting [[the-curator|the Curator]] with giving him the gift of written characters so he could map his [[pilgrimage]]. In the novel's final chapters, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] recalls "the Fountain of Wisdom in the Wonder House" (014-097), and reflects that the Keeper of the Images "was in past life a very wise man" (015-020). The museum has become, in [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s Buddhist worldview, one of the stations on his path toward enlightenment.

## Description

The Wonder House stands opposite [[zam-zammah|the gun Zam-Zammah]] in the [[lahore|Lahore]] bazaar (001-002). It is "the Wonder House, as the natives call the Lahore Museum" (001-002) — a name that captures both the awe it inspires and its function as a cabinet of curiosities for the colonial public. The building itself is imposing, housing galleries of sculpture, painting, and ethnographic displays.

The museum's Buddhist gallery is its spiritual heart in the novel. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] is drawn to the "many images in the Wonder House of [[lahore|Lahore]]" (001-039), particularly a Bodhisattva that he believes to be a sacred relic. [[the-curator|The Curator]]'s office — a quiet scholarly space within the museum — becomes the site of the first meaningful conversation between East and West in the novel, conducted in Urdu and marked by mutual respect.

## Historical Context

The Lahore Museum opened in 1864 and was significantly expanded in 1894 with a new building designed by Sir Ganga Ram. Its collection of Gandharan Buddhist sculpture — dating from the 1st to 5th centuries CE — was among the finest in the British Empire, drawing scholars and [[pilgrimage|pilgrim]]s alike.

John Lockwood Kipling, Rudyard Kipling's father, served as the museum's first curator from 1875 to 1893. He was a scholar of Indian art and architecture who deeply influenced his son's appreciation of Indian culture. [[the-curator|The Curator]] in *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* is widely understood to be a portrait of Lockwood Kipling himself — a scholarly, empathetic Englishman who bridges the cultural divide between British India and the ancient traditions of the subcontinent.
