# The Old Soldier's Mutiny Tale

On the Grand Trunk Road, Kim and the lama encountered a grizzled old soldier — a retired Risaldar-major of a cavalry regiment — who had served the British Raj "in the days of the Mutiny as a native officer in a newly raised cavalry regiment" (003-064). Seated cross-legged, he "told tales of the Mutiny and young captains thirty years in their graves" (003-113). His recounting of the great rebellion of 1857 was not merely reminiscence but a vivid oral history, delivered with the authority of one who had lived through it and emerged loyal to the Crown.

## Context

Kim had been travelling with the lama along the Grand Trunk Road toward Umballa. After Kim surreptitiously delivered Mahbub Ali's secret message to the British officer at Umballa — his first act in the Great Game — he and the lama continued their journey eastward. It was on this road that they encountered the old soldier, a figure who had long ago retired from active service and now occupied his days with the dignity of his pension and the weight of his memories.

The soldier was a man of considerable standing: a Risaldar-major, the highest rank a native officer could achieve in the Indian cavalry, who proudly declared: "I have no less than three Rissaldar-majors — all in the regiments" (003-155), referring to the military careers of his sons. The soldier embodied a generation of Indians who had loyally served the British during the Mutiny and whose identity was bound up in that service.

The old soldier was immediately drawn to Kim, whom he called "Friend of all the World" (003-075) after Kim had correctly predicted an impending war — a piece of intelligence the boy had gleaned from his mission at Umballa. Kim's remark that "a war is toward" (004-018) confirmed the soldier's belief in the boy's prophetic gifts, and he declared: "For once in all my days I have met a true prophet — who was not in the Army" (004-023).

## The Event

### The Encounter

The meeting occurred along the Grand Trunk Road where the old soldier habitually sat. Kim initially approached and spoke with him, and the soldier "bobbled up" (003-106) to inquire about the boy and his saffron-robed companion. Kim, ever resourceful, parlayed his insider knowledge — that a war was brewing in the north — into a claim of second sight, attributing his intelligence to astrological knowledge imparted by the lama.

The soldier tested the boy: "Give me a sign," he thundered. "If there were war..." (003-075). Kim answered with such conviction that the old soldier was impressed. Having seen active service himself, the soldier had a keen instinct for genuine military intelligence, even when it came wrapped in the guise of prophecy.

### The Story Unfolds

The old soldier's recounting of the Mutiny was interwoven with his interactions with Kim and the lama. He told his tales of "the Mutiny and young captains thirty years in their graves" (003-113) as Kim listened. The soldier's narrative was not a single linear account but a series of vignettes: stories of courage, of loyalty tested, of the chaos that engulfed northern India in 1857, and of the British officers he had served under and who had fallen.

He also spoke of his sons, who had followed him into military service: "Three Rissaldar-majors in three regiments" (003-157). The old soldier's pride in his lineage of service was tempered by the melancholy of aging — he ambled "up the village street" (003-120) on his scrawny pony, a diminished figure but one whose authority was still recognized.

The lama, ever detached from worldly matters, observed the exchange with his characteristic gentleness, while Kim — whose own father had been a sergeant in the Mavericks — felt a kinship with the old soldier that went unspoken. The old soldier's tales bridged the gap between Kim's inherited British identity and his Indian upbringing, mirroring the dual loyalties that would define Kim's life in the Great Game.

## Consequences

- **Kim's prophetic reputation is established.** The old soldier's public endorsement of Kim as "a true prophet" (004-023) spreads along the Road, contributing to the boy's already growing mystique.

- **The lama gains a respected ally.** The old soldier takes a benevolent interest in the holy man, endorsing his spiritual authority and offering informal protection on the dangerous road.

- **The Mutiny's living memory is preserved.** Through the soldier's oral narrative, the events of 1857 are transmitted to a new generation — Kim, who will later serve the same imperial power that the old soldier defended, hears firsthand what loyalty meant in a time of crisis.

- **A template of service is set before Kim.** The old soldier represents one model of Indian-British relations: loyalty through martial service, rewarded with rank, pension, and respect — a path that stands in contrast to the espionage and disguise that will define Kim's own contribution.
