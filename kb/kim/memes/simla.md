# Simla

Simla is the British summer capital perched in the Himalayan foothills, serving as the command centre of the [[the-great-game|Great Game]] in *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. It is where [[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]] receives intelligence, where [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]] trains [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] in espionage tradecraft, and where the coded wires of the British Indian [[the-chain-men|intelligence network]] converge.

## In *Kim*

Simla first appears in connection with [[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]], who "at Simla was advised from [[lucknow|Lucknow]] by wire that young O'Hara had arrived" (007-113). The city is the unseen hand directing [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s transformation — [[colonel-creighton|Creighton]], [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]], and their network operate from Simla's bazar and bungalows.

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]] himself arrives in Simla in Chapter 8, travelling with the [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]]d [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]]: "A sudden spate in [[gugger-river|the Gugger River]] swept away the main road into the lower Simla bazar" (008-165). He lodges at [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]]'s shop, "among the European shops. All Simla knows it" (008-174), where he receives his crucial training in [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]], [[secrecy-and-surveillance|observation]], and the jewel game.

Chapter 9 shows Simla's social world — the Town Hall, the European quarter, the elaborate protocols of colonial society. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], now schooled, navigates this world with the same adaptability he showed in the [[lahore|Lahore]] bazar. The city also functions as an intelligence hub: "a code-wire reporting the arrival of the Russians was then being handed in at Simla" (012-043).

> About this time Colonel Creighton at Simla was advised from Lucknow by wire that young O'Hara had disappeared. Mahbub Ali was in town selling horses, and to him the Colonel confided the affair one morning cantering round Annandale racecourse.
>
> (007-113)

## Description

Simla is depicted as a city of contrasts: the "broad road under Simla Town Hall" (009-003), the European shops, and the rambling bazar where cultures mix. It is a city built on Himalayan ridges, connected to the plains by precarious roads subject to spates and landslides. The climate is cooler than the plains — a relief for the British — but the political atmosphere is charged with intelligence operations.

## Historical Context

Simla became the official summer capital of British India in 1864, hosting the Viceroy and the Imperial government from April to October each year. Its location in the Himalayan foothills provided escape from the Indian summer heat. By the 1890s, Simla was the nerve centre of British Indian political and military intelligence, with the Foreign Department's secret services operating from its bungalows and clubs.

Kipling himself visited Simla as a journalist for the *Civil and Military Gazette*, and his intimate knowledge of its social and political geography enriches the novel's portrayal.

## Travel Routes

Simla is connected to the plains by road via [[gugger-river|the Gugger River]] valley. The journey from [[lucknow|Lucknow]] or [[umballa|Umballa]] involves a train to the foothills, then a road ascent into the mountains.
