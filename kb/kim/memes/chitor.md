# Chitor

**Chitor** (modern Chittorgarh) is an ancient fortified city in Rajasthan, referenced in the Great Game narrative of Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. It serves as a waypoint in the clandestine journey of the spy E23, who uses the city as a hiding place and a temporary cache for sensitive documents.

## In *Kim*

Chitor appears in the confession of E23, the spy whom Kim encounters on the train to Delhi. Fleeing pursuit from enemy agents in the South, E23 recounts that he "lay in old Chitor city a week, a penitent in a temple" (011-145). During this time, unable to safely transport the secret letter entrusted to him, he buries it "under the Queen's Stone, at Chitor, in the place known to us all" (011-145). Later, when E23 reaches safety, he plans to return to Chitor to recover the buried document (011-150). The city is also referenced in chapter 12, when a character mentions having to "go down to Chitor to find that beastly letter" (012-128).

> "Neither love spies, and there is no justice. I was hunted like a wet jackal; but I broke through at Bandakui, where I heard there was a charge against me of murder in the city I had left---of the murder of a boy."
>
> (011-148)

## Description

The novel describes Chitor as an ancient city in "Kings' country" — a region of princely states beyond the direct jurisdiction of British law (011-147). E23 notes that the surrounding territories of Kotah, Jaipur, and Gwalior "neither love spies, and there is no justice" (011-148), making the area both a refuge and a trap for those involved in the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]. The city's temple provides sanctuary for the fugitive spy, who poses as a religious penitent.

## Historical Context

Chittorgarh (Chitor) is one of the most historically significant fortresses in Rajasthan. In the late 19th century, when *Kim* is set, it lay within the princely state of Mewar under British suzerainty. The region of Rajputana was a patchwork of princely states — territories ruled by Indian maharajas under treaty relationships with the British Crown. This patchwork jurisdiction, where British law did not directly apply, made the region ideal for spies and fugitives seeking to evade colonial authorities. The Queen's Stone referenced by E23 likely alludes to a landmark within the fort complex.
