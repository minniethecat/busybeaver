# The Prophecy of the Red Bull

Kimball O'Hara, the Irish colour-sergeant who died in poverty in Lahore, left his infant son three things: a birth certificate, a discharge paper, and a prophecy. The prophecy — that a Red Bull on a green field would one day come for little Kim, attended by the Colonel on his tall horse and nine hundred devils — was a dying soldier's fantasy, born of opium and loneliness. Yet it came true, in every particular, when the boy wandered into the camp of his father's old regiment. The prophecy's fulfilment marks the moment when Kim's two worlds — the Indian street and the British military — collide irreversibly.

## Context

Kim's father, Kimball O'Hara, had been a colour-sergeant of the Mavericks, an Irish regiment. After his discharge, he worked as a gang-foreman on the Ferozepore railway line, married a nursemaid, and sank into opium and despair. On his deathbed, he delivered the words that would shape his son's life:

> "Nine hundred first-class devils, whose God was a Red Bull on a green field, would attend to Kim, if they had not forgotten O'Hara — poor O'Hara that was gang-foreman on the Ferozepore line" (001-004).

The half-caste woman who raised Kim "sewed parchment, paper, and birth-certificate into a leather amulet-case which she strung round Kim's neck" (001-004). And she repeated the prophecy, in her own broken English: "There will come for you a great Red Bull on a green field, and the Colonel riding on his tall horse, yes, and nine hundred devils" (001-005).

Kim, boylike, half-believed it: "I shall remember. A Red Bull and a Colonel on a horse will come, but first, my father said, will come the two men making ready the ground for these matters" (001-006).

## The Event

### The prophecy travels

As Kim and the lama journeyed along the Grand Trunk Road, the prophecy travelled with them. When the old soldier recounted the story to a cultivator's wife, the lama listened with gentle curiosity: "It was a bull — a Red Bull that shall come and help thee and carry thee — whither? I have forgotten. A Red Bull on a green field, was it not?" (002-116).

The cultivator's wife was fascinated: "Do ye both dream dreams? A Red Bull on a green field, that shall carry thee to the heavens or what? Was it a vision? Did one make a prophecy?" (002-118). But Kim, embarrassed by the attention, deflected: "Nay, it is but a tale I told thee" (002-117).

Nevertheless, the lama — drawing on the Buddhist logic of karma — offered an interpretation: "In a former life it may be thou hast acquired merit, and the Bull will come to reward thee" (002-121). The prophecy was acquiring weight, layer by layer, as it passed from teller to teller.

### The Bull appears

Near Umballa, the band of the Mavericks marched past on the road, "playing the regiment to camp; for the men were all Twelve hundred, and they swung across the plain in column of companies" (005-047). Kim saw the regimental flag snap-snapping in the evening breeze:

> "It was no more than an ordinary camp marking-flag; but the regiment, always punctilious in matters of millinery, had charged it with the regimental device, the Red Bull, which is the crest of the Mavericks — the great Red Bull on a background of Irish green" (005-031).

The lama, squinting at the flag, recognized it: "Certainly it is thy Bull. Certainly" (005-032). And as the sun broke through the trees, Kim felt it "was the crown of the Umballa Brahmin's prophecy" (005-038). The celestial signs had aligned.

### Fulfilment

Kim crept into the camp that night, drawn by an irresistible curiosity. Inside the Mess tent, he saw the regiment's golden mascot — a red-gold bull with lowered head, ramping upon a field of Irish green, looted long ago from the Summer Palace at Pekin. The officers raised their glasses to it. Kim was discovered by the chaplain, seized, and identified.

When Father Victor questioned him, Kim laid out the whole improbable story: "I saw the Red Bull on the green field, and my father he said: 'Nine hundred pukka devils and the Colonel riding on a horse will look after you when you find the Red Bull!' I did not know what to do when I saw the Bull, but I went away and I came again when it was dark" (005-107).

The Reverend Bennett, who had wanted the boy flogged as a thief, was silenced. Father Victor saw Providence: "Here is Kimball O'Hara's son — and the Mavericks are his father's regiment. This is not chance" (005-082). Every element of the dying sergeant's prophecy had been fulfilled: the Bull (the regimental crest), the Colonel (Creighton), the nine hundred devils (the regiment's twelve hundred men), and even "the two men making ready the ground" — Father Victor and Bennett themselves.

## Consequences

- **Kim's identity is exposed.** The amulet that had hung round his neck all his life — through the Lahore bazaars, through the Grand Trunk Road journey — has at last been opened. Kim is no longer a "little Friend of all the World"; he is Kimball O'Hara's son, a white boy, and a ward of the regiment.

- **The lama loses his chela — temporarily.** The holy man, who had come to rely on Kim as his disciple and guide, watches helplessly as the white men claim the boy. The lama withdraws to wait, trusting that the Wheel will turn.

- **Kim's formal education begins.** The prophecy's fulfilment leads directly to St. Xavier's. The street boy's training in the Great Game has begun.

- **The supernatural and the rational converge.** The novel never resolves whether the prophecy was genuine foresight or coincidence — Kipling leaves the question open. The lama sees karma; Father Victor sees Providence; Kim, pragmatic as ever, simply takes the next step.
