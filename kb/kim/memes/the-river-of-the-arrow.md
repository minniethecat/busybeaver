# The River of the Arrow

**The River of the Arrow** is the spiritual goal of the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]]'s [[pilgrimage]] in Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. According to Buddhist legend, the Buddha once shot an arrow into the air, and where it landed, a river sprang forth whose waters could cleanse all sin and liberate the bather from the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Life]].

## In the Novel

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s search for [[the-search-for-the-river|the River of the Arrow]] is the spiritual engine of the novel. He first describes his quest at the [[lahore|Lahore]] museum, explaining that a dream told him to seek the River. When asked where it is, he replies simply: "If I knew, think you I would not cry it aloud?" (001-075).

The River is never precisely located during the novel — a fact that is central to its meaning. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] believes it lies somewhere along the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]], and his journey with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] becomes a [[pilgrimage]] in search of it. The River represents an elusive spiritual truth that cannot be found through maps or logic but only through devotion and surrender.

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s ultimate encounter with the River occurs in the final chapters, though the novel handles this moment with characteristic ambiguity, leaving room for both literal and metaphorical interpretations.

## Key Passages

> "Where, then, is the River? My dream told me to find it. So I came. I am here. But where is the River?" (001-075)
> — The lama, upon arriving in Lahore

> "The River — the River of the Arrow — I have found it!" (015-058)
> — The lama, at his moment of supposed spiritual breakthrough

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] describes the River as being fed by a spring that appeared when the Bodhisattva's arrow struck the ground, making it a site of direct divine intervention in the physical world. His faith in its existence never wavers, despite the skepticism of those he meets.

## Related Characters

## Symbolism

The River of the Arrow functions on multiple symbolic levels:
- **Faith**: The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s unwavering belief despite the absence of empirical evidence
- **Salvation**: The cleansing of sin and karma through divine grace
- **The Unattainable**: The River's ambiguous location mirrors the elusiveness of enlightenment itself
- **Convergence**: In the novel's climax, [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s worldly triumph and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s spiritual attainment converge
