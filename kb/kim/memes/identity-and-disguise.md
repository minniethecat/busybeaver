# Identity and Disguise

**Identity and Disguise** is one of the most pervasive themes in Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. The novel's protagonist, [[kimball-ohara|Kimball O'Hara]], is an Irish orphan raised as a native boy in the [[lahore|Lahore]] bazaar, who can shift between identities — white Sahib and Indian street urchin — with a change of clothing, language, and manner. This fluid identity is both his greatest asset in the [[the-great-game|Great Game]] and the source of the novel's deepest questions about who he truly is.

## In the Novel

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s identity is multiple and layered from the novel's first pages. He is "[[kimball-ohara|Friend of All the World]]," a nickname earned by his ability to get along with everyone regardless of caste or creed. He is also "[[kimball-ohara|Kimball O'Hara]], boy," the son of an Irish soldier, though he has never known any life but the bazaar.

Throughout the novel, [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] adopts and discards identities as the situation demands. He passes as a Hindu boy, a Muslim, a Buddhist chela, a Sahib schoolboy, and eventually a spy. Each identity is a costume, but each also leaves its mark on him. The novel's central tension is whether [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] will ultimately choose one identity over the others, or remain forever suspended between worlds.

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] provides the novel's spiritual answer to this question of identity. To [[teshoo-lama|the lama]], all worldly identities are [[illusion-and-reality|illusion]]s — "there is neither black nor white, Hind nor [[tibet|Bhotiyal]]" (012-052) — and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s true self lies beyond any of the roles he plays.

## Key Passages

> "Who is Kim — Kim — Kim?" (015-290)
> — Kim's moment of existential crisis, questioning his own identity at the novel's climax

> "I am not a Sahib. I am thy chela." (011-055)
> — Kim choosing his identity as the lama's disciple over his European heritage

The novel explores identity not as a fixed essence but as a performance — something that can be put on like clothing — while simultaneously suggesting that [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s true self lies deeper than any performance.

## Symbolism

Identity in *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* is connected to the novel's deeper philosophical concerns:
- **The Buddhist perspective**: All identity is [[illusion-and-reality|maya]] ([[illusion-and-reality|illusion]]); the self is a construct
- **The colonial perspective**: Identity is imposed by race and empire; [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] defies these categories
- **The spy's perspective**: Identity is a tool to be deployed and discarded as needed
- **[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s synthesis**: True identity emerges not from choosing between worlds but from embracing all of them
