# Gugger River

The Gugger River is a seasonal watercourse in [[punjab|the Punjab]] plains that appears in Chapter 8 of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* as the scene of a near-disaster during the journey from [[umballa|Umballa]] to [[simla|Simla]]. A sudden flood in the river sweeps away one of [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]]'s horses and nearly takes [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] with it.

## In *Kim*

The Gugger River episode occurs during what the narrator describes as a journey "[[kimball-ohara|Kim]] will remember till he dies" (008-160). [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]], travelling with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and a string of horses from [[umballa|Umballa]] to [[simla|Simla]], has been enjoying the wandering road through the foothills. The narrative catalogues the pleasures of the journey — the morning light on the distant snows, the chattering monkeys, the solemn deodars — before abruptly pivoting to the river:

"A sudden spate in the Gugger River swept down one horse (the most valuable, be sure), and nearly drowned [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] among the dancing boulders" (008-160).

The parenthetical "the most valuable, be sure" carries Kipling's characteristic irony — of course it would be the best horse that the river takes. The "dancing boulders" evoke the violence of the flash flood, and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s narrow escape adds a note of real danger to what had been an idyllic travelogue.

The journey continues through further mishaps — a Government elephant stampedes the horses, Mahbub spends eight hours haggling over two unsaleable screws — but the narrator insists "it was all pure delight" (008-160). The Gugger River spate is the most dramatic of these incidents, a reminder that travel in Kipling's India carries genuine peril.

## Description

The Gugger (modern Ghaggar) is a seasonal river that rises in the Siwalik Hills of Himachal Pradesh and flows through the plains of [[punjab|Punjab]] and Haryana. During the dry season it is often a broad, sandy bed with little or no flowing water. But during the monsoon or after sudden cloudbursts in the hills, it can rise rapidly into a violent torrent — the "sudden spate" that Kipling describes. The river lies on the route between [[kalka|Kalka]] and the Pinjore Gardens, on the approach to the hill station of [[simla|Simla]].

## Historical Context

The Ghaggar River has a [[identity-and-disguise|dual identity]]: historically, it is identified by some scholars with the ancient Sarasvati River of the Rig Veda, though modern geological consensus holds that the Sarasvati was a different, now-extinct river system. In Kipling's time, the Ghaggar was simply one of the seasonal rivers that travellers on the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]] and the [[simla|Simla]] road had to contend with. The danger of flash floods was real and well-known; many travellers' accounts from the period describe similar narrow escapes. The [[simla|Simla]] road — the route by which the Viceroy and the entire Government of India migrated to the hills each summer — was well-maintained, but nature could still catch the unwary.
