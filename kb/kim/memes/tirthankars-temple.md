# Tirthankars' Temple

The **Tirthankars' Temple** (also called the Temple of the Tirthankars) is a Jain temple located about a mile outside the city of [[benares|Benares]] (011-018). It serves as a crucial fixed point in the novel: the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] uses it as his base of operations while [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] attends [[st-xaviers-school|St. Xavier's School]] in [[lucknow|Lucknow]], receiving letters and money there and engaging in spiritual debates with the resident Jain priests.

The temple represents a practical intersection of the lama's Buddhist spiritual quest with the lived religious landscape of India, where Jain, Hindu, Buddhist, and Muslim traditions coexist and overlap.

## In *Kim*

The lama first mentions the temple in his earliest conversation with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] at the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]], when he speaks of a fellow Seeker: "one of the pure faith in a Jain temple of that city" (001-082). This unnamed contact becomes the lama's anchor in [[benares|Benares]] throughout the novel.

During Kim's schooling years, the temple functions as the lama's permanent address. Letters from [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] are directed "care of Tirthankars' Temple, Benares" (006-078), and the lama receives correspondence and money through a native banker associated with the temple (006-093). When [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] explains the lama's situation to [[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]], he notes that "he explained it all at the Tirthankars' Temple at Benares" (006-157).

When sending [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] off to school at the end of Chapter VII, the lama tells him:

> "To the Temple of the Tirthankars at Benares. That is the place I have chosen till I find my River. Do not weep; for, look you, all Desire is Illusion and a new binding upon the Wheel."
>
> (007-081)

The temple hosts spiritual and philosophical discussions. The lama recounts "much debate in the Temple of the Tirthankars on this matter; some saying..." (007-069), indicating that the Jain priests engage with him in theological conversation. The priests are portrayed as "kindly" (009-148) and tolerant, with the lama acknowledging that "the Jains officially recognize all the Gods of the Hindus" (011-092).

Later in the narrative, after the lama's arduous journey into the hills, he returns to the temple. The priests receive him back without judgment: "the kindly Jain priests made haste to be silent" when they realized the lama's state (009-148), and he eventually makes his way back there to recover after his spiritual crisis.

## Description

The temple is situated "about a mile outside the city" of Benares (011-018). An [[kimball-ohara|Oswal banker]] from [[ajmir|Ajmir]] is associated with it, handling the lama's financial affairs (011-032). The temple compound includes courtyards where "now and again, a Jain priest crossed the court, with some small offering to the images" (011-082), and where [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] interacts with the resident priests.

The atmosphere is described as quiet and scholarly, in contrast to the bustling streets of Benares. It provides the lama with a space where he can sit "upon the lowest step of the temple" (011-043) in contemplation, watching the world pass.

## Historical Context

Jainism has deep roots in the Benares region, with the city being an important center for both Digambara and Svetambara traditions. The Tirthankars are the twenty-four enlightened teachers of Jainism, and temples dedicated to them are common throughout India.

A Jain temple in late 19th-century Benares would have been a functioning religious institution, and it is historically plausible that it would host visiting seekers and provide services such as receiving mail — functions that [[rudyard-kipling|Kipling]] draws on to give the lama a practical anchor within the novel's geography.
