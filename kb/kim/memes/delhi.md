# Delhi

Delhi, the ancient Mughal capital and a key administrative centre of British India, appears throughout *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* as a city of dual character: a place of luxury and power, but also a place of danger for those who play the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]. It is a city where warrants are issued, lawyers scheme, and agents move in the shadows.

## In *Kim*

Delhi enters the novel early as a point on the map of intrigue. [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] wires to his "sub-partner at Delhi" (001-204) with coded messages about [[lutuf-ullah|Lutuf Ullah]], demonstrating the city's role as a node in the [[the-chain-men|intelligence network]]. A "sleek young gentleman from Delhi" (001-212) meets Mahbub at [[the-serai|the serai gate]] — a fellow agent with keys that unlock secrets.

The city looms as a threat for some characters. The [[the-old-soldier|old soldier]] warns that warrants will be issued "against me at Delhi" (011-158) if he is captured, describing how "every tear and rag upon my body would be sworn to" in the city's courts. For the poor or the fugitive, Delhi is dangerous — a place where the machinery of law grinds men down.

Yet Delhi is also a city of sophistication and culture. It "shares with [[lucknow|Lucknow]] the claim to talk the only pure Urdu" (007-054), a marker of its status as a centre of Mughal refinement. It is a place some characters have travelled to and through: "one of these I went as far as Delhi to the southward" (007-133), the [[the-old-soldier|old soldier]] recalls of his military campaigns.

Delhi's railway station serves as a gateway: "they slid into Delhi about lamp-lighting time" (011-211), and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is warned not to "bang about as though Delhi station belonged to you" (012-020) — reminders that the city is a place of watchful eyes and strict hierarchies.

> It is a far cry to Delhi, and farther to Benares. If I go to Delhi and come back, it is a matter of three weeks. And, God knows, something may happen in the meantime.
>
> (001-083)

## Description

Delhi is depicted more through its associations than through physical description in the novel. It is the seat of government, a place of "lawyers" and "warrants" (011-156), suggesting the bureaucratic machinery of the Raj. Its station is a hub of the railway network that connects [[punjab|the Punjab]] to the Gangetic plain. The old soldier recalls the siege of Delhi during the Mutiny of 1857 — "I saw Delhi shake at least" (003-143) — a memory that colours the city with the violence of living history.

## Historical Context

In Kipling's time, Delhi was a city of two identities. The old Mughal capital — Shahjahanabad — was a dense, walled city of mosques, bazaars, and havelis, still recovering from the devastation of the 1857 Mutiny. The new British capital — the Civil Lines and later (from 1911) New Delhi — was a world of bungalows, barracks, and administrative buildings.

Delhi would become the capital of British India in 1911, a decade after *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* was published. At the time of the novel, Calcutta was still the official capital, but Delhi's symbolic importance — as the seat of the Mughal emperors whom the British had displaced — was already immense.
