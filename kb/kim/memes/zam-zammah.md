# Zam-Zammah

Zam-Zammah is the great green-bronze cannon that stands outside the [[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]] in [[lahore|Lahore]], serving as the novel's iconic opening image. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is introduced to the reader seated astride this cannon — "he sat, in defiance of municipal orders, astride the gun Zam-Zammah" (001-001) — a posture that declares his independence from caste, creed, and colonial authority in a single image.

## In *Kim*

The cannon is the throne from which [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] surveys his kingdom. He "drummed his heels against Zam-Zammah" (001-008) while [[secrecy-and-surveillance|watching]] the traffic of the [[lahore|Lahore]] bazaar, and his rival Abdullah "climbed up Zam-Zammah's wheel" (001-009) to challenge his right to sit there. The Hindu and Muslim boys below argue over who holds the gun, to which [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] retorts that neither does — "'All Mussalmans fell off Zam-Zammah long ago!'" (001-011), and "'The Hindus fell off Zam-Zammah too'" (001-014). [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], the white orphan raised as a native, occupies a space that belongs to no community, just as he belongs to no single [[identity-and-disguise|identity]].

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] first pauses by the cannon on his arrival in [[lahore|Lahore]]: "The old man halted by Zam-Zammah and looked round" (001-103), [[kim-meets-the-lama|the first meeting]] point of the novel's two central figures. Later, when [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] reunites with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]], [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] recalls this moment: "'I remember when I first spoke to thee under Zam-Zammah the great gun'" (005-150). The cannon has become a landmark not only of [[lahore|Lahore]] but of their shared history.

The cannon's shadow lengthens over the bazaar as the day progresses — "the shadow of Zam-Zammah grew long" (001-136) — marking the passage of time in the novel's unhurried opening.

> The old man halted by Zam-Zammah and looked round till his eye fell on Kim. The inspiration of his pilgrimage had left him for awhile, and he felt old, forlorn, and very empty.
>
> (001-103)

## Description

Zam-Zammah is a massive cannon of green-bronze, cast with intricate designs and mounted on a stone plinth. Its name is onomatopoeic — "Zam-Zammah" echoes the thunder of its firing. By the time of the novel, the cannon is long silent, serving as a children's climbing frame and a landmark in the bazaar.

It stands directly opposite the entrance to the [[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]] ([[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]]), creating a striking juxtaposition: on one side, the repository of ancient Buddhist wisdom; on the other, the symbol of Mughal and British military might.

## Historical Context

The actual Zam-Zammah cannon was cast in 1757 in [[lahore|Lahore]] on the orders of Shah Wali Khan, a minister of Ahmad Shah Durrani, the founder of [[mahbub-ali|the Afghan]] Durrani Empire. It earned the nickname "[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s Gun" after Kipling's novel made it famous. The cannon saw action in several battles before being captured by the British and eventually placed outside the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]], where it remains to this day.

John Lockwood Kipling, Rudyard Kipling's father and the first curator of the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]], wrote about the cannon in his scholarly work, and his son transformed it into one of the most memorable opening images in English literature.
