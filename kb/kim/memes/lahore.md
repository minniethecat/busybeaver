# Lahore

Lahore is the primary urban setting of the opening chapters of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*, serving as the boy's native city and the place where the novel's two central journeys begin. It is a crowded, cosmopolitan walled city in [[punjab|the Punjab]] region of British India, home to the [[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]] ([[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]]), the roaring Motee Bazar, and the great gun [[zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]] upon which [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is first introduced.

> He knew the wonderful walled city of Lahore from the Delhi Gate to the outer Fort Ditch; was hand in glove with men who led lives stranger than anything Haroun al Raschid dreamed of.
>
> (001-007)

## In *Kim*

Lahore is [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s childhood home and the stage for the novel's opening. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] grows up on its streets, knowing "the wonderful walled city of Lahore from the [[delhi|Delhi]] Gate to the outer Fort Ditch" (001-007), befriending [[mahbub-ali|horse-trader]]s, spies, and priests. It is in Lahore that [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] encounters the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] at the [[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]], forming the relationship that drives the entire narrative. The city also serves as a node in the [[the-great-game|Great Game]] — [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] operates from Lahore, sending coded messages and horses through its serais and gates.

Lahore appears in later chapters as a point of reference: characters measure their journey's distance from Lahore ("an hundred kos from Lahore already," 002-136), and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s Lahore experiences — his knowledge of castes, customs, and the art of invisibility — form the foundation of his value as a spy.

## Description

The novel depicts Lahore as a dense urban fabric where every lane and bazar has its character. The Motee Bazar roars with commerce (001-015), [[the-serai|the Kashmir Serai]] houses travellers and horse-dealers (001-162), and the [[the-wonder-house|Ajaib-Gher]] — the [[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]] — stands as a repository of Buddhist and Hindu antiquities that draw [[pilgrimage|pilgrim]]s from across India. The city is "wonderful" and "walled" (001-007), its streets crowded with "all the population of the city" on festival days (001-102).

The city's railway station serves as a departure point for the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]] journey (001-202), marking Lahore as both origin and threshold. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s intimate knowledge of its lanes, its people's dialects, and its hidden corners is described as his greatest asset.

## Historical Context

In the late 19th century, Lahore was a major city of [[punjab|the Punjab]] under British rule, annexed in 1849 after the Second Anglo-Sikh War. It was a key administrative and military centre, with railways connecting it to [[delhi|Delhi]], Calcutta, and the North-West Frontier. The [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]] (opened 1864) housed one of the finest collections of Gandharan Buddhist art in the world — a fact that Kipling, whose father John Lockwood Kipling served as the museum's first curator, drew upon directly for the novel's opening scene.

Lahore's cosmopolitan character — Sikhs, Muslims, Hindus, British, and Afghans trading and living in close quarters — reflected [[punjab|Punjab]]'s position as India's crossroads, a theme Kipling weaves throughout the narrative.

## Travel Routes

Lahore stands at the western terminus of the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]]'s [[punjab|Punjab]]i section. From Lahore, travellers can go:
- East to [[umballa|Umballa]] and [[delhi|Delhi]] via the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]]
- North to the Passes and Afghanistan
- South-west to [[karachi|Karachi]] and the sea
