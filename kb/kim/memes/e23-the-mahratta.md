**E.23 the Mahratta** is a British Indian intelligence operative in Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*, codenamed E.23. A product of [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]]'s training, he appears as a field agent of [[the-great-game|the Great Game]] — tracked, wounded, and fleeing pursuers across northern India.

His chance encounter with Kim on a train from Somna Road to Delhi provides Kim with his first real field test as a spy-in-training.

## Background and Training

Lurgan Sahib, the Simla jewel-merchant and spy-trainer, shaped E.23 from unpromising raw material.

Kim overhears Colonel Creighton and Lurgan discussing the agent: "Lurgan Sahib had made E.23 what E.23 was, out of a bewildered, impertinent, lying, little North-West Province man." (010-070) Like Kim himself, E.23 was tutored in the skills of memory, disguise, and observation that Lurgan prized in his operatives.

E.23's work took him into the dangerous Southern States, where he inherited a mission from a predecessor who had been murdered.

"One letter indeed had been procured, but the finder was later found dead by the roadside in the habit of an Arab trader, as E.23, taking up the work, duly reported." (010-042) The mission involved intercepting incendiary correspondence between a Moslem religious authority and a disaffected young prince — exactly the kind of political intelligence that the Great Game depended upon.

## The Escape from the South

E.23's account of his flight, whispered to Kim on the train, reveals the desperate conditions of a hunted spy. Having procured the compromising letter he was sent to find, he escaped the city and fled to Mhow — but was betrayed. "At Mhow a woman brought charge against me of theft of jewellery in that city which I had left.

Then I saw the cry was out against me." (011-145) He bribed the police and took refuge in the ancient city of Chitor, hiding as a penitent in a temple for a week.

There, at a prearranged dead-drop, he concealed the precious letter: "I buried it under the Queen's Stone, at Chitor, in the place known to us all." (011-145) Moving north, he attempted to disguise himself as a Mahratta — hence his codename — and hoped to double back to recover the document. But the pursuit closed in.

His enemies, he told Kim, would fabricate a murder charge: "Twenty — a hundred, if need be — will have seen me slay that boy. And thou art useless!" (011-159) Reflecting on the life of a covert operative, he observed bleakly: "We of the Game are beyond protection. If we die, we die. Our names are blotted from the book. That is all." (011-150)

## The Train Encounter

At Somna Road station, a wounded and terrified man tumbled into Kim's railway compartment. Kim immediately recognized the signs of a fellow operative: "If, as he asserted, he had been rolled over and over on the earth, there should have been signs of gravel-rash on the skin.

But all his injuries seemed clean cuts." (011-126) The recognition signal — an amulet strung on square-plaited copper wire with black enamel on silver — confirmed his identity as a Son of the Charm, a brother in the intelligence service.

Kim and E.23 exchanged coded phrases: the talk of *tarkeean* curry, the formulaic question "Of whose service art thou?", and Kim's whispered acknowledgement of "a Son of the Charm." (011-134)(011-137) Kim, with the lama and the Kamboh farmer as unwitting cover, set about transforming E.23's appearance.

He stripped the agent, smeared him with ash and charcoal, marked his forehead with caste-signs, and fed him opium to redden his eyes — remaking the hunted Mahratta into a half-naked, ash-smeared Saddhu. "Art thou only a beginner?" E.23 gasped as Kim worked, "labouring literally for the dear life." (011-184)

## The Delhi Escape

At Delhi station, E.23's disguise held. Police searched the train carriage by carriage, the young English officer reading from a description: "See the young Sahib reading from a paper. My description is in his hand." (012-011) But Kim's handiwork passed inspection — E.23, counting his beads with the steady jerk of a Saddhu's wrist, drew no suspicion.

The policeman dismissed the compartment's occupants as "nothing here but a parcel of holy-bolies." (012-013)

E.23's next challenge was communicating the letter's hiding place. "The trouble now lies in sending a wire as to the place where I hid that letter I was sent to find. I cannot go to the *tar*-office in this guise." (012-014) But his escape was secured when he spotted a police officer on the platform: "Strickland Sahib has pulled me to land. We may work together at the Game yet.

Farewell!" (012-032) That evening, a code-wire reached Simla reporting E.23's arrival at Delhi and the whereabouts of the hidden letter. (012-043) Meanwhile, an over-zealous policeman arrested an innocent cotton-broker in E.23's place, "while E23 was paddling through byways into the locked heart of Delhi city." (012-044)

## Kim's First Field Test

The E.23 episode marks a watershed in Kim's development as a spy. The encounter at Somna Road was improvisational field-work — no instructions, no backup, just the boy's training and instinct.

As Kim bent over the Mahratta's neck, "his heart nearly choking him; for this was the Great Game with a vengeance." (011-144)

Kim reflected later, in Hindustani, on the reach of the intelligence network: "From the South — God knows how far — came up the Mahratta, playing the Great Game in fear of his life." (012-168)

The successful rescue proved that Kim's Lurgan-taught skills — disguise, quick thinking, coded recognition — could function under real pressure.

E.23's survival validated Kim's readiness for field operations, setting the stage for his own journey into the northern hills.
