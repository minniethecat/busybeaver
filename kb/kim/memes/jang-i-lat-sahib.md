## Role in the Narrative

The Jang-i-Lat Sahib (Commander-in-Chief) appears as the guest of honour at Colonel Creighton's dinner in Umballa. (002-167) Kim, hiding in the garden, watches the tall, black-haired man arrive in a landau with four native troopers, "erect as an arrow." (002-150) Creighton presents Mahbub Ali's intelligence to him, and the Commander-in-Chief makes the decision: "It isn't a question of weeks. It is a question of days — hours almost." (002-155)

> "Very good. I saw the Jang-i-Lat Sahib [the Commander-in-Chief] come to a big dinner. I saw him in Creighton Sahib's office. I saw the two read the white stallion's pedigree. I heard the very orders given for the opening of a great war."
>
> (008-040)

## The Decision for War

The Commander-in-Chief authorises immediate action based on Mahbub Ali's message, ordering warnings to "the Pindi and Peshawar brigades" and estimating that "eight thousand should be enough." (002-157) When his subordinate asks "Then it means war?", the Commander-in-Chief corrects him: "No. Punishment." (002-160-161) This distinction — punitive expedition rather than declared war — reflects the political calculus of the Raj.

## Later Reference

The Jang-i-Lat Sahib reappears in Chapter 8, where an officer remarks "I saw the Jang-i-Lat Sahib" in confirming a sighting. (008-040) These brief appearances make him more symbol than character — the embodiment of imperial military authority that Kim's espionage serves.
