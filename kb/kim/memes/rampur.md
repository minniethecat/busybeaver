# Rampur

Rampur is a Himalayan princely state that appears in *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* primarily through the cover story adopted by [[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]]. When [[the-babu|the Babu]] needs a plausible [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] to approach the Russian agents in the hills, he presents himself as an agent of the Rajah of Rampur — a ruse that gives him access while concealing his true role in the [[the-great-game|Great Game]].

> Then by Rampur into Chini. That is the only way, gentlemen and ladies say.
>
> (012-168)

## In *Kim*

Rampur enters the novel in Chapter 12 when [[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]] outlines his route to [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]: "I shall go to [[mussoorie|Mussoorie]] ... Then by Rampur into [[chini|Chini]]. That is the only way they can come" (012-168). [[the-babu|The Babu]] maps Rampur as the intermediate point between the British hill station of [[mussoorie|Mussoorie]] and the remote [[chini|Chini Valley]] — a necessary staging post on the only feasible route.

In Chapter 13, Rampur becomes central to [[the-babu|the Babu]]'s [[illusion-and-reality|deception]]. Arriving at the Russian agents' camp, he introduces himself as "agent for His Royal Highness, the Rajah of Rampur, gentlemen. What can I do for you, please?" (013-033). The ruse succeeds — the Russians accept his cover, and [[the-babu|the Babu]] explains that "his master at Rampur held the very same opinion" as himself regarding the beneficence of the British Government (013-047).

The local hillmen take the Rampur connection seriously. One coolie, "whose hut lay within a few miles of the Rajah's rickety palace," warns his companions: "It is well to be brave when one does not live in Rampur. If we get a bad name among the Sahibs, none will employ us as *shikarris* any more" (013-118). Another is identified simply as "the nervous Rampur man" (013-126), his proximity to the Rajah's authority making him more cautious than his fellows.

## Description

Rampur in *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* is depicted as a typical Himalayan hill state — a small princely domain ruled by a Rajah whose "rickety palace" suggests modest wealth and limited influence. The state sits on the route between [[mussoorie|Mussoorie]] and [[chini|Chini]], making it strategically positioned on the approaches to the higher Himalaya. Its Rajah is described as holding pro-British views, or at least views that [[the-babu|the Babu]] can plausibly attribute to him, making the cover [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] credible to Europeans travelling in the region.

## Historical Context

Rampur Bushahr (modern Rampur in Himachal Pradesh) was a historic town on the Sutlej River that served as the winter capital of the princely state of Bushahr. The Rajah's "rickety palace" described in the novel reflects the [[illusion-and-reality|reality]] of many small Himalayan princely states — politically loyal to the British but economically modest. The town lay on the old Hindustan-[[tibet|Tibet]] trade route, making it a natural waypoint for travellers heading into the high mountains. The Rajah of Bushahr in Kipling's time was a British ally, lending credibility to [[the-babu|the Babu]]'s cover story.

## Travel Routes

Rampur lies on the Sutlej River road between [[mussoorie|Mussoorie]] and [[chini|Chini]] Valley. The route runs through the hill country of Bushahr, following the river gorge northward toward the [[tibet|Tibet]]an frontier. For travellers heading from British India into the high Himalaya, Rampur was a natural provisioning stop — the last town of any size before the road climbed into the remoter valleys.
