# The Curator

**The Curator** of the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]] — the "[[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]]" — is the English scholar who provides the novel's first example of respectful cross-cultural encounter. His meeting with the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] in Chapter I establishes the museum as a space where Eastern and Western knowledge traditions can meet as equals (001-046).

## Background

The curator is an Englishman employed by the British Indian administration to manage the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]] and its collection of Buddhist and Hindu antiquities. He is a scholar as much as an administrator, and his knowledge of Buddhist art and iconography allows him to recognize [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s learning immediately.

## Role in the Narrative

When [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] enters the museum, the curator welcomes him with the words "Welcome, then, O [[teshoo-lama|lama from Tibet]]. Here be the images" (001-046). This moment of recognition — one scholar greeting another across vast cultural distance — sets the tone for the novel's treatment of cross-cultural encounter at its best.

The curator provides [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] with access to the museum's Buddhist collection, and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] responds with profound emotion to the images of the Buddha. The curator also gives [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] practical advice about his [[pilgrimage]], suggesting he travel the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]] and test each stream for [[the-river-of-the-arrow|the River]]'s virtue (003-048).

## Relationships

- **[[Teshoo Lama]]** — The curator recognizes [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] as a fellow scholar and treats him with genuine respect
- **[[Kimball O'Hara]]** — The curator meets [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], who has brought [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] to the museum
