# Training with Lurgan Sahib

At Simla, in the cluttered shop of Lurgan Sahib, Kim receives his most critical training for the Great Game. Under the tutelage of the enigmatic gem-trader and healer, Kim learns the jewel game — a brutal exercise in memory and observation — and is tested against hypnotic suggestion. The training transforms Kim from a gifted street boy into a disciplined operative capable of disguise, memorization, and resistance to psychological manipulation.

## Context

After Father Victor's death and his release from the Mavericks, Kim was sent to Lurgan Sahib at Simla on Mahbub Ali's instruction. "Thou art to lodge in Lurgan Sahib's house till it is time to go again to school," Mahbub told him, adding with uncharacteristic reverence: "Lurgan Sahib himself asked for thee" (008-170). Kim did not yet understand the honour. Lurgan Sahib was one of the most skilled operatives in Colonel Creighton's intelligence network — a man who healed the sick and, in the same rooms, forged gemstones and trained spies.

## The Event

### The jewel game

Lurgan Sahib's training began with a test. He scattered fifteen jewels on a tray — rubies, turquoises, sapphires, flawed diamonds — and gave Kim one minute to study them (009-041). Then he covered the tray.

Kim, who had spent his childhood observing every detail of the Lahore bazaars, passed the first trial easily. But Lurgan increased the difficulty: more stones, shorter times, subtle flaws that only a trained eye could detect. The jewel game was not merely about memory — it was about learning to see what others missed. "Sometimes very fine jewels will crack of themselves if you set them to work, and there is no knowing beforehand" (009-064). The same was true of men.

### The test of will

The final test came not with jewels but with mind. Lurgan Sahib, who had studied hypnosis among the mystics of the East, attempted to break Kim's will through suggestion. A cracked jar fell and shattered — Lurgan insisted Kim had seen it whole. The boy wavered, but his amulet and his own fierce sense of self held. Kim refused the false memory.

Lurgan Sahib's assistant, a Hindu boy who had failed this same test years before, watched with fear. Kim passed. He was not merely suggestible — he could resist. In the Great Game, this distinction meant survival.

### Disguise and craft

Beyond the jewel game, Lurgan taught Kim the practical arts of espionage: how to alter his skin colour with walnut juice and lamp-black, how to dress and walk as a Hindu, a Mohammedan, or a Buddhist novice. Kim, who had been practicing such changes since childhood, excelled. But Lurgan formalized the craft, adding techniques Kim had never imagined (010-048).

## Consequences

- **Kim became a trained operative.** The jewel game sharpened his natural gifts into professional skills. He could now memorize documents at a glance, identify persons in disguise, and resist interrogation.

- **The Great Game gained a valuable agent.** Colonel Creighton and Mahbub Ali had identified Kim's raw talent. Lurgan Sahib refined it. From this point forward, Kim was no longer a runner of errands but a spy in training.

- **Kim's dual identity strengthened.** The training at Simla — between worlds, between races, between roles — deepened Kim's ability to move through different identities. It also planted the seeds of the identity crisis that would surface later: "Who is Kim?" (007-032).

## Key Passages
