# Pilgrimage

**Pilgrimage** is the organizing physical and spiritual framework of Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. The novel is structured as a pilgrimage narrative, following the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]]'s sacred journey across India in search of [[the-search-for-the-river|the River of the Arrow]] — but it is also an anti-pilgrimage, as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] simultaneously pursues worldly knowledge and power through the [[the-great-game|Great Game]].

## In the Novel

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s pilgrimage defines the geography and pace of the novel. His journey from [[lahore|Lahore]] to the southern plains and back to the Himalayas traces a sacred circuit across the Indian subcontinent. Along the way, the pilgrimage framework brings [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] into contact with the full spectrum of Indian society — priests, farmers, soldiers, merchants, and spies — each encountered in the context of sacred travel.

India itself is portrayed as a land of pilgrims. [[the-grand-trunk-road|The Grand Trunk Road]] is crowded with "holy men of all creeds" (003-188), and the novel depicts pilgrimage as one of the few social activities that transcends caste and religious boundaries. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s pilgrimage, though Buddhist, is respected by Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs alike, reflecting the syncretic religious culture of pre-modern India.

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s relationship to [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s pilgrimage is complex. He serves as [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s chela (disciple), guiding him along the road and begging for their food — the traditional role of a pilgrim's attendant. But [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is also using the pilgrimage as cover for his own activities, making the sacred journey serve secular purposes (011-055).

## Key Passages

> "I came to find the River. So I came. I am here. But where is the River?" (001-075)
> — The lama's pilgrimage quest, stated at the novel's beginning

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s pilgrimage is described as following "the Blessed Feet in all their wanderings" (001-099), connecting his journey to the tradition of Buddhist sacred travel that had crossed Asia for centuries.

## Historical Background

Pilgrimage has been central to Indian religious practice for millennia, with networks of sacred sites spanning the subcontinent. The Buddhist tradition of pilgrimage, established by the Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE, created routes that Kipling's [[teshoo-lama|lama]] would have recognized. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s journey from [[tibet|Tibet]] to India follows historical pilgrimage routes used by [[tibet|Tibet]]an Buddhists traveling to the sacred sites of the Buddha's life.
