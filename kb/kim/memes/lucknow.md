# Lucknow

Lucknow is the city where [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is sent to receive his formal Western education at [[st-xaviers-school|St Xavier's]] School. It represents the British institutional world that [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] must learn to navigate — a sharp contrast to the street life of [[lahore|Lahore]] and the open road of the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]].

## In *Kim*

Lucknow enters the narrative in Chapter 5, when [[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]] and [[father-victor|Father Victor]] decide [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] must be educated: "[[st-xaviers-school|St Xavier's]] in Partibus at Lucknow" (005-187). [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] resists at first, but [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] persuades him: "In seven days thou wilt go with me to Lucknow, seeing and hearing new things" (006-171).

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]] spends three years at [[st-xaviers-school|St Xavier's]] in Lucknow, learning mathematics, map-reading, and the behaviour of a Sahib — skills that later serve him in the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]. When [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] arrives at "the crowded Lucknow station" (007-049), the driver proudly declares the city "beautiful in her garish style" (007-054). The school is his home for three years, a pivotal transformation from street boy to educated operative.

> "...the best schooling a boy can get in India is, of course, at St Xavier's in Partibus at Lucknow." This took some time to interpret, for Bennett wished to cut it short.
>
> (005-187)

## Description

The novel presents Lucknow through the eyes of a newcomer: the railway station is crowded, the city's architecture rich in decorative detail. It is a city of institutions — [[st-xaviers-school|St Xavier's]] School, the railway junction, the European cantonments — rather than the organic street life of [[lahore|Lahore]]. Lucknow stands as the intermediary zone between native India and the colonial order.

## Historical Context

Lucknow was the capital of Oudh (Awadh), annexed by the British in 1856. A major site of the 1857 Rebellion, by the 1890s it was a key administrative and railway hub. [[st-xaviers-school|St Xavier's]] in the novel is modelled on La Martinière College, Lucknow — an institution Kipling knew through his own experience of colonial boarding schools.
