# The Lama's Enlightenment

The quest that opens the novel — the Tibetan lama's search for the River of the Arrow, the stream shot by the Lord Buddha that washes away all sin — reaches its culmination in the final chapter. Wounded, exhausted, and returned from the Hills to the Plains, the lama experiences the enlightenment he has sought across all of India. The River is found: not in the remote Himalayas, but in the quiet, familiar landscape of the Plains, within reach of the Kulu woman's house where Kim recovers from his own ordeal.

## Context

From the moment the lama entered the Lahore Museum in the novel's opening pages, his purpose was singular. The Curator had listened as the old man spoke of "the River of the Arrow" — "a certain River" whose waters could wash away the sins of a lifetime (001-069). The lama had followed the traces of the Buddha's footsteps throughout India, from Tuticorin to Bombay, from Benares to the northern passes. He had told Kim on their first meeting: "I follow the places of His life till I come to the River of the Arrow" (001-088).

Throughout the years of wandering, the lama had never lost faith. Even while Kim was at St. Xavier's, the old man continued his pilgrimage, occasionally visiting the Temple of the Tirthankars in Benares, "a little thinner and a shade yellower, if that were possible, but gentle and untainted as ever" (009-148). Yet the River eluded him. It was shown to him in dreams that the quest could not succeed "unless that seeker had with him the one chela appointed to bring the event to a happy issue" (009-148).

The reunion with Kim after St. Xavier's restored the pair. Together they travelled north into the Hills, where the confrontation with the Russian spies changed everything.

## The Event

### The sign of the Arrow

After the Russian struck him, the lama lay wounded under the pines. The blow became a spiritual sign. He whispered to Kim: "I have come near to great evil, chela" (013-110). And then, with sudden clarity, he declared: "The Arrow fell in the Plains — not in the Hills. Therefore, what make we here?" (014-107).

The lama had spent years searching the Himalayas — the natural home, one would think, of a Buddhist holy man's enlightenment. But the Arrow, he now understood, had fallen elsewhere. The Hills were not his destination; they were a detour born of desire.

### The descent

Kim, weakened by his ordeal and the psychological toll of his dual identity, needed care. The Woman of Shamlegh sheltered them, and later they descended to the Plains, to the house of the Kulu woman — the same sharp-tongued hill-woman who had travelled with them earlier. There, Kim fell into an exhausted sleep that lasted for days, his body and mind recovering from the strain of the Great Game.

During Kim's long sleep, the lama sat beside him, meditating and watching. The old man's physical strength, paradoxically, improved in the good air of the Plains. Kim noted: "But thou art ten times the stronger man in this good air" (015-013). The lama agreed: "Here, or hereabouts, fell the Arrow, yes. We will go very softly, perhaps, a kos a day, for the Search is sure" (015-013).

### The River found

In the quiet of the Plains, beside a small stream near the Kulu woman's house, the lama achieved what he had sought for so long. The text does not describe the River as a dramatic, thundering cascade; it is a quiet, ordinary stream — the kind of water the lama might have passed a hundred times without noticing. But now, with his chela beside him and the stain of the Hills washed from his spirit, he saw it for what it was.

The lama raises a hand toward the rampart of the Himalayas. "Not with you, O blessed among all hills, fell the Arrow of Our Lord! And never shall I breathe your airs again!" (015-012). The search is over. The lama has found the River of the Arrow, and with it, freedom from the Wheel of Things.

### Enlightenment

The lama's enlightenment is not described in terms of supernatural vision but of spiritual release. He has shed his last attachment — even his attachment to the Hills, to the romantic quest for a hidden stream in the high places. He has accepted that the Arrow fell in the ordinary, human landscape of the Plains. His soul, as he told Kim in their first conversation, has been freed from the stain of sin.

The lama's final words convey a profound serenity. He has achieved what he set out to do. The Wheel has turned, and he is free.

## Consequences

- **The lama achieves liberation.** The quest that drove the entire narrative — the search for the River of the Arrow — is fulfilled. The lama's spiritual journey is complete.

- **Kim returns to himself.** While the lama achieves enlightenment, Kim recovers from his identity crisis. The lama's serenity helps Kim resolve his own divided self: "I am Kim. This is the great world, and I am only Kim. Who is Kim?" (007-031).

- **The novel's two quests converge.** Kim's Great Game mission (the document theft, the Russian confrontation) and the lama's spiritual quest (the River) reach their respective resolutions simultaneously, in the same place, under the same roof. The spiritual and the worldly, which have run parallel throughout the novel, meet at last.
