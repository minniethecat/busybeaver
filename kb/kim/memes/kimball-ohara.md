# Kimball O'Hara

**Kimball "Kim" O'Hara** is the protagonist of Rudyard Kipling's *Kim*. An Irish orphan raised as a native boy in the [[lahore|Lahore]] bazaar, Kim navigates between two worlds — the spiritual quest of the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] and the political intrigue of the [[the-great-game|Great Game]] — embodying the novel's central exploration of [[identity-and-disguise|identity]], [[loyalty-and-betrayal|loyalty]], and belonging.

## Background

Kim is the son of Kimball O'Hara Sr., an Irish soldier in [[the-mavericks|the Mavericks regiment]] who died in poverty in [[lahore|Lahore]]. His mother died of cholera when he was an infant. Raised by a half-caste woman in the bazaar, Kim grows up speaking Urdu, [[punjab|Punjab]]i, and English, moving freely through [[lahore|Lahore]]'s diverse social world. His father left him three documents and a prophecy of a [[the-red-bull-prophecy|Red Bull on a green field]] (001-035).

## Role in the Narrative

Kim's journey begins when he encounters the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] outside the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]] and volunteers to become his chela (disciple). As they travel along the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]], Kim is simultaneously drawn into the [[the-great-game|Great Game]] — the British [[the-chain-men|intelligence network]] operating across India. His ability to pass as a native Indian makes him uniquely valuable as a spy (007-033).

The novel traces Kim's dual education: spiritual teaching from [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] and espionage training from [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]], [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]], and [[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]]. Sent to [[st-xaviers-school|St. Xavier's School]] for a European education, Kim acquires the mathematical and [[secrecy-and-surveillance|observation]]al skills needed for Survey work while maintaining his connections to the [[the-chain-men|intelligence network]].

Kim's ultimate test comes when he helps thwart Russian agents in the Himalayas while simultaneously aiding [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] in his final spiritual breakthrough. At the novel's end, Kim has proven himself in both worlds but faces the unresolved question of which [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] — Sahib or native, spy or disciple — he will ultimately claim.

## Relationships

- **[[Teshoo Lama]]** — Kim's spiritual teacher, whom he serves as chela and guides across India
- **[[Mahbub Ali]]** — The Pathan [[mahbub-ali|horse-trader]] who recruits Kim into the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]
- **[[Lurgan Sahib]]** — The [[simla|Simla]] spymaster who trains Kim in [[secrecy-and-surveillance|observation]] and [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]]
- **[[Colonel Creighton]]** — The British officer who oversees Kim's education and intelligence career
- **[[Hurree Babu]]** — Fellow intelligence agent and occasional rival

## Key Passages

> "Who is Kim — Kim — Kim?" (015-290)
> — Kim's existential crisis as he confronts the question of his own identity

> "I am not a Sahib. I am thy chela." (011-055)
> — Kim choosing his identity as the lama's disciple over his European heritage

## Identity and Disguise

Kim's defining characteristic is his ability to change [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] at will. He can dress, speak, and behave as a Hindu boy, a Muslim servant, a Buddhist chela, or an English schoolboy. This fluidity is both a gift and a burden — it makes him a perfect spy but leaves him uncertain of who he truly is.
