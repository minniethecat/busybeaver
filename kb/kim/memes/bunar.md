Bunár is a princely state in the Himalayan high passes, ruled by a Rajah who possesses guns and commands armed men. Along with its neighbor [[hilas|Hilás]], Bunár features in the final chapters of *Kim* as a place where Great Game intrigues unfold. (012-145)

## The Bunár Men

The Bunár men carry a reputation for violence that gives even seasoned travelers pause. Hurree Babu confesses his fear: "I was afraid these Bunár men would cut my throat every instant on the road." (012-147) Despite this, the Babu navigates their territory successfully, demonstrating the blend of courage and cunning that defines his character.

> I was afraid these Bunár men would cut my throat every instant on the road.
>
> (012-147)

## In the Great Game

Like Hilás, Bunár is a destination for the Russian and French spies seeking influence among the hill states. The two Rajahs "undertook to preserve peace upon pain of dethronement" — a precarious balancing act between rival imperial powers. (012-145)

The Babu reports that evidence against the foreign agents would be found "in a King's letter that either Hilás or Bunár has written. They guard it most carefully." (013-069)

## In the Final Journey

Bunár stands alongside Hilás in the sweeping geography of the novel's conclusion: "Hilás, Bunár, and four hundred miles of hill roads" form the terrain across which Kim and the lama travel. (013-169) The repeated pairing of the two names throughout the text underscores their intertwined role in the Himalayan political landscape.

## The Two Rajahs

The Rajahs of Hilás and Bunár jointly held the strategic mountain passes that the Great Game's rival powers sought to control. The two princes "undertook to preserve peace upon pain of dethronement" (012-145), a threat that reflected British determination to prevent any independent diplomacy between the hill states and Russian agents.

Despite this pressure — or perhaps because of it — the Rajahs played both sides. The foreign agents were "well received by Hilás and Bunár" (012-151), their hosts making "great promises" while extracting gifts.

The Rajahs' canny diplomacy, balancing between empires and guarding incriminating royal letters, reveals the complexity of frontier politics: small states surviving by manipulating the rivalries of great powers. "They guard it most carefully," the Babu noted of the King's letter, (013-069) recognizing that in the high passes, information was as valuable as territory.
