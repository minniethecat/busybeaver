## Overview

The Pirzai Kotal was a mountain pass on the North-West Frontier of British India, near the Afghan border. It was the site of a border action in which Sikh and Dogra companies of the British Indian Army engaged Afridi tribesmen — one of the many small frontier skirmishes that characterized the "Great Game" era. (002-048)

## The Battle

The young Dogra soldier traveling on the train with Kim and the lama recounted the action with pride. Two companies of Dogras from the Ludhiana Sikhs regiment came to the aid of their Sikh comrades at the Pirzai Kotal, facing eight Afridi standards on the ridge. The fight had occurred approximately three months before the events of Chapter 2. The soldier noted that a Mohammedan regiment had also been stationed nearby at the pass, and one of their *naiks* (corporals) — a priest — had spoken prophecies during the fighting. (002-048)(002-090)

## Significance

The Pirzai Kotal action exemplifies the constant low-intensity warfare along India's North-West Frontier during the British Raj. These border skirmishes, fought in the mountain passes against independent Pashtun tribes, were the backdrop against which the Great Game of imperial rivalry between Britain and Russia was played. For Kim, the news of such military actions — passed through bazaar talk and railway gossip — became part of his education in the realities of imperial power. (002-048)(002-090)

A Dogra soldier recounts the frontier action:

> "Thy Sikhs thought so when our two companies came to help them at the Pirzai Kotal in the face of eight Afridi standards on the ridge not three months gone."
>
> (002-048)
