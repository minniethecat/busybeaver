# Spiti

Spiti is a high-altitude Himalayan region that appears in the mountain chapters of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. The novel does not describe Spiti directly as a place, but its men — the Spiti coolies — play a notable role in the dramatic confrontation between [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] and the Russian agents in Chapter 13, and the region serves as a geographical reference point on the journey northward.

> All the Spiti valley was naked to the keen air, and Kim could see the lama''s dragomen and the Hurree Babu''s coolies---some of whom were Spiti men and, by their lights, good Buddhists.
>
> (013-082)

## In *Kim*

Spiti first enters the narrative as a landmark visible from the high passes. In Chapter 13, as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] press deeper into the hills, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] pauses "in the neck of some uplifted pass that gave on Spiti and [[kulu|Kulu]]" and stretches "out his hands yearningly towards the high snows of the horizon" (013-009). The mention of Spiti alongside [[kulu|Kulu]] places it among the great Himalayan landscapes that draw [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] forward.

The Spiti men appear as the coolies hired by the Russian agents to carry their baggage. When the Russian snatches and tears [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s drawing of the Wheel, "a low murmur of horror went up from the coolies — some of whom were Spiti men and, by their lights, good Buddhists" (013-082). Their Buddhist faith aligns them with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]], and their reaction signals the moral breach the Russian has committed.

The Spiti men continue to speak through the aftermath. One warns the Russian: "Do not curse us — do not curse him. It was but his zeal, [[teshoo-lama|Holy One]]!" (013-107). Another offers a hillman's wisdom: "After a blow, it is best to sleep" (013-122). And a third draws a comparison from hunting: "I remember an old ibex, out Ladakh-way, that Dupont Sahib missed on a shoulder-shot, seven seasons back, standing up just like him" (013-129).

In Chapter 14, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] speaks of the spiritual aftermath, recounting how "a lust to let the Spiti men kill" arose in him after the blow (014-015). He describes his struggle to overcome that impulse through Buddhist practice, making the Spiti men's presence a catalyst for one of [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s most significant spiritual reflections. Later, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] "would point out the road to Spiti and north across the Parungla" (014-072), using the region as a navigational reference.

## Description

Spiti is a cold desert mountain valley in the Trans-Himalayan region, lying north of the Sutlej River gorge and east of [[kulu|Kulu]]. In Kipling's time, it was a remote, sparsely populated area inhabited by Buddhist communities with cultural ties to [[tibet|Tibet]]. The Spiti men in the novel are portrayed as hill-people — trackers and *shikarris* familiar with the terrain, devout in their Buddhist faith, and experienced in serving European Sahibs on hunting and survey expeditions.

## Historical Context

The Spiti Valley was part of the princely state of Bushahr in the nineteenth century, under British suzerainty. It lay close to the [[tibet|Tibet]]an frontier and was culturally [[tibet|Tibet]]an Buddhist. The region's men frequently worked as porters and guides for British expeditions — a practice reflected in the novel's Spiti coolies, who had clearly worked for European sportsmen before. The valley sat on one of the traditional routes between India and [[tibet|Tibet]], making it strategically relevant to the [[the-great-game|Great Game]] geography.

## Travel Routes

Spiti lies north of [[simla|Simla]] and the Sutlej Valley, accessible through high mountain passes. The route from [[chini|Chini]] Valley continues north toward Spiti, and from Spiti the road leads toward Ladakh and the [[tibet|Tibet]]an plateau. The Parungla pass, mentioned by [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] (014-072), connects Spiti to the routes heading north.
