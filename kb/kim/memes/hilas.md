Hilás is a princely state in the Himalayan high passes, ruled by a Rajah who possesses guns. Along with its neighbor [[bunar|Bunár]], Hilás appears in the latter chapters of *Kim* as a node in the Great Game's frontier politics — a place where foreign agents seek audience and where intelligence reports must be sent back. (012-145)(013-041)

## As a Great Game Waypoint

The Russian and French agents operating in the Himalayas are "well received by Hilás and Bunár." (012-151) The two Rajahs make "great promises" to the foreigners, exploiting their position between empires to extract advantage. Intelligence reports from the region are "sent back from Hilás, or even Leh." (013-041)

The Babu notes that evidence of the Russians' activities would be found "in a King's letter that either Hilás or Bunár has written." (013-069)

> They are well received by Hilás and Bunár. They make great promises; they speak as the mouthpiece of a Kaisar with gifts.
>
> (012-151)

## In the Final Journey

Hilás marks a waypoint on Kim and the lama's final journey through the hills. The narrative sweep encompasses "Hilás, Bunár, and four hundred miles of hill roads" as the stage across which the novel's climactic events unfold. (013-169) The paired names of Hilás and Bunár serve as a refrain, evoking the complex political geography of the Himalayan frontier.

## Paired Destinies

The persistent pairing of Hilás and Bunár throughout the text — "Hilás and Bunár," "either Hilás or Bunár," "Hilás, Bunár, and four hundred miles" — is not merely a convenient geographic shorthand. Kipling uses the twin principalities to illustrate a broader pattern of the frontier: rival states locked in shared dependence, each mirroring the other's precarious sovereignty.

When the Babu reports that the Russians' evidence resides "in a King's letter that either Hilás or Bunár has written" (013-069), the ambiguity is telling — in the Great Game, the two Rajahs function as a single political unit, their fates intertwined. The paired names become a rhythmic refrain through the final chapters, a reminder that in the high passes, no state stands alone.
