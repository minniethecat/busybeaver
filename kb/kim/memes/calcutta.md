## Gateway of the Grand Trunk

Calcutta is the distant terminus of the Grand Trunk Road, which the village priest describes to the lama as "the great road to Calcutta" running westward. (003-046) For the pilgrims and travellers on the Road, Calcutta represents the far end of the subcontinent's great artery — a world away from the Punjab villages where Kim and the lama walk.

> "Three kos (six miles) to the westward runs the great road to Calcutta."
>
> (003-046)

## A Centre of Education

Calcutta University, one of India's premier institutions, produces the English-educated Bengali class that populates Kipling's India. Lurgan Sahib's assistant, the Babu, is described as "an M A of Calcutta University" (009-143), and the city's educational prestige is noted with both admiration and gentle mockery: to be "Calcutta-taught" (012-094) marks a Bengali as erudite and voluble. The "great school at Calcutta" (012-105) draws ambitious students from across India who "bear away prizes at rich Calcutta." (012-106)

## In the Great Game

Calcutta also serves as a backdrop for intelligence work. Hurree Chunder Mookerjee mentions "Chandernagore being so close to Calcutta" (013-046) in the context of tracking the Russian spies, and boys sent to study there are groomed for roles in the Survey of India — Kim's own future path. The "lordly halls of Calcutta" (012-182) represent the institutional power and privilege that the Great Game both serves and exploits.
