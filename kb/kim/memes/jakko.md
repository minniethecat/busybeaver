## Location and Character

Jakko is the prominent hill that rises above [[simla|Simla]], the summer capital of British India. Crowned with deodar trees, it catches "the breath of a cool wind" that flows through the city below. (009-007) The hill is "shouldering the stars," a phrase that captures both its elevation and the sense of mystery that surrounds Lurgan Sahib's operations there. (009-007)

> Together they set off through the mysterious dusk, full of the noises of a city below the hillside, and the breath of a cool wind in deodar-crowned Jakko, shouldering the stars.
>
> (009-007)

## Lurgan Sahib's House

On Jakko Road sits the shop and residence of [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]], the healer of sick pearls who is also one of the Great Game's master trainers. It is here that [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is sent during his school holidays to receive training in espionage tradecraft. (009-004)

The house itself is a place of mystery and testing. Within its walls, Kim encounters devil-dance masks from Tibet, a Japanese warrior's armor, and—most memorably—a phonograph that plays disturbing messages to test his nerve. The boy is unimpressed by the supernatural atmosphere: "This place," he says aloud, "is like a Wonder House." (009-020)

## Winter Setting

During the Christmas holidays, Jakko's character transforms dramatically. "Jakko road was four feet deep in snow that year," and Kim spends his time "in front of a roaring wood-fire" at Lurgan's house, learning chapters of the Koran, native drugs and their administering runes, and the art of writing charms on parchment. (010-008) The snowbound isolation provides ideal conditions for intensive training—the outside world sealed away while Kim absorbs the crafts of deception.

## Symbolic Resonance

Jakko appears once more at the novel's end, in a poetic image that frames Kim's journey: the lama had "vanished like a dawn-cloud on Jakko." (015-005) This simile connects the mountain with impermanence and spiritual transcendence—the lama's presence, like a morning mist on the hill, seems to dissolve into enlightenment. The mountain thus bridges Kim's two worlds: the covert world of Lurgan Sahib's training below its slopes, and the lama's spiritual quest that rises above them.
